In the rural areas, ignorance and tradition prevail, while the town of Issoudun suffers from social stagnation. Families focus on survival amid declining fortunes, lacking the class distinctions that enrich social life. The absence of nobility, eliminated by historical upheavals, has left Issoudun devoid of the social dynamics that once invigorated it. The town's refusal to host a garrison, justified by the desire for cheap provisions, has further isolated it from contemporary society and economic benefits. A notable judicial drama in the past led to the permanent loss of military presence, which had previously contributed to the town's vibrancy.

Bourges faces similar decline, with a decreasing population and a lack of vitality in its communities. The government bears responsibility for this stagnation, failing to address the underlying issues or send energetic leaders to revitalize these regions. Ambitious individuals who attempt to bring change often succumb to the inertia of provincial life. By 1822, Issoudun was governed by local men, leading to ineffective administration. The procureur du roi was closely related to the community, and the judge was known for a notorious remark that defined his career. The police commissioner, entrenched since the Restoration, had deep local ties, while religious influence was negligible, and even the curate lacked respect.
