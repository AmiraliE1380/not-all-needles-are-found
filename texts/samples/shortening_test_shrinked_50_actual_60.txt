In the rural areas, ignorance and tradition prevail, while the town of Issoudun suffers from social stagnation. Families focus on their own survival amid declining fortunes, lacking the class distinctions that enrich social life. The absence of nobility, eliminated by historical upheavals like the Revolution, has left Issoudun devoid of the social dynamics that once invigorated it. The town has consistently rejected military garrisons, citing the cost of provisions, which has further isolated it from contemporary society and economic benefits. A notable judicial incident in the past led to the permanent loss of its garrison, and the brief presence of troops during the civil war did little to endear the military to the locals.

Bourges, similarly, faces a decline in population and vitality, reflecting a broader malaise in these communities. The government bears responsibility for this stagnation, as it fails to address the underlying issues or send energetic leaders to revitalize the regions. Ambitious individuals who attempt to bring change often succumb to the inertia of provincial life. By 1822, Issoudun was governed by local men, leading to ineffective administration, with power often reduced to a farce. The procureur du roi, Monsieur Mouilleron, was closely related to the community, and the judge was known for a notorious remark that defined his career. The long-serving police commissary had extensive local ties, while religious influence was negligible, and even the curate lacked respect.
