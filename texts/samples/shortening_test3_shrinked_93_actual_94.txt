Doctor Rouget, who had a troubled relationship with his sons, financed Max’s education until 1805, leaving his paternity ambiguous. After Rouget's death in 1806, Max, a spirited and athletic youth, engaged in reckless behavior, including stealing fruit and hunting. At seventeen, he accidentally caused a pregnant woman's death while stealing, prompting him to flee to Bourges and enlist in the military.

Max quickly rose through the ranks during three campaigns, becoming a captain. Captured by the English in Portugal, he spent four years in the brutal Spanish prison at Cabrera, where he became demoralized but also a leader among fellow prisoners. Upon returning to Issoudun after the war, he found his parents dead and sought recognition for his service, but faced rejection from military authorities.

Despite his lack of social standing, Max became a prominent figure in Issoudun, admired by the common people but scorned by the bourgeoisie. He led a group called the Knights of Idleness, engaging in mischievous pranks that unsettled the town. His reputation as a hero solidified after a violent confrontation with royalist officers, further establishing his influence and notoriety in the community.
