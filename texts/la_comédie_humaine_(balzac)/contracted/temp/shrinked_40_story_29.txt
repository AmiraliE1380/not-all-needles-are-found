AN OLD MAID

CHAPTER I. ONE OF MANY CHEVALIERS DE VALOIS

France boasted many Chevaliers de Valois; one resided in Alençon, scarcely knowing his namesakes elsewhere. He claimed descent from old Valois lines, though the male branches had mostly vanished. The Alençon chevalier matched the stereotype: an elderly, tall, thin, nearly penniless gentleman who had spent his youth in Paris, reached thirty amid conquests before the Revolution, and was accepted provincially as a genuine Valois. His standing rested on manner, not wealth: he dined out, played cards nightly, and told polished stories of Louis XV’s court and revolutionary days. He never repeated his bon mots, avoided discussing love affairs, neglected mass yet remained staunchly royalist—an attitude forgiven because of his charm.

He cultivated the little theatricalities of the ancien régime: an old gold snuff-box bearing the Princess Goritza’s portrait, a taste for refined apparel, and other fading graces. Though fifty-eight, he insisted he was fifty; careful dress and hair gave him a preserved air. A single enormous nose dominated his pale face—one side red, the other white—suggesting a “hot liver” that physicians jokingly linked to a prodigal heart; his gallantries confirmed the diagnosis. Pleasant laugh-lines and a courtly bearing testified to a life of amusement.

His neat but slightly worn linen, maroon coat with gilt buttons, close-crimped cravat, square-buckled shoes, and twin watch-chains created an effect of elegant thrift. He wore cotton in his ears and two tiny diamond ear-rings shaped as little blackamoors’ heads—oddities tolerated because they fitted his role. He played the old-marquis part with skill, an art rarer since the loss of figures like Mole.

Private details remained mysterious. He lodged modestly on Madame Lardot’s rue du Cours, two rooms for a hundred francs a year, returning there only to sleep and breakfast lightly. He made almost no fire, walked between eleven and four, read, and paid quarterly visits to his man of business. He professed a small annuity—six hundred francs—though a banker paid him one hundred and fifty francs quarterly, remitted by Monsieur Bordin of Paris, at the chevalier’s insistence on secrecy.

Welcomed in Alençon’s best houses, he enlivened salons with card skill, witty stories, and a kindly approving grimace. Young women coveted his compliments; his old-fashioned pet names—“my heart,” “my jewel”—were still charming. At cards he praised opponents, tolerated poor players, and in a small gesture or snuff-box movement could quiet a room. To working girls, he was paternal and flattering, giving trifles—chocolates, ribbons—that bought their secret loyalty and discretion. Despite small winnings, he remained the town’s darling.

But his means were larger than his declared annuity. After the July days, townsfolk learned his card winnings averaged about one hundred and fifty francs every quarter, and that he had the pluck to have his annuity paid to himself quietly. Over time he had amassed about ten thousand francs. The Bourbon Restoration brought the Marquis de Pombreton repaying twelve hundred pistoles, enabling investments on the Grand Livre. Through influential acquaintances he obtained a three-hundred-franc pension from the king’s privy purse and the cross of Saint-Louis, allowing him to style himself a retired colonel for services in the Catholic armies of the West. Thus, besides his theatrical poverty, he enjoyed an honest income of roughly one thousand francs. He changed little: whist, boston, backgammon, piquet, graceful snuffing, measured walks. Yet he harbored a steady ambition: to marry the wealthy old maid Mademoiselle Cormon, using her fortune to reclaim a place at court. This hope explained his royal bearing and decision to live in Alençon.

CHAPTER II. SUSANNAH AND THE ELDERS

One mid-spring Wednesday the chevalier, dressing in a green-flowered damask gown, heard the light step of a young wash-girl. Three taps and she slipped into his room.

“Ah! is it you, Suzanne?” he said, stropping his razor. “What have you come for, my dear little jewel of mischief?”

“I have come to tell you something which may perhaps give you as much pleasure as pain,” she answered.

Suzanne worked for Madame Lardot; the wash-rooms served the town’s best linen. The chevalier, self-appointed judge of love as revealed by linen, was discreet and generous with trifles. He frequented the work-room, bestowing candies and small attentions on his “little ducks,” who adored his gallant protection—an echo of seigneurial ways. He no longer ruined himself for mistresses; he gave toffees and compliments and enjoyed their gossip as a household feuilleton.

Suzanne, clever, ambitious, handsome—reminiscent of Sophie Arnould in a Norman, somewhat rubenesque form—was a favorite. She confided to him her plan: to go to Paris and secure a protector, preferably an old bachelor who’d finance her trip. The chevalier listened with paternal indulgence, draping her before him in a gesture of faded gallantry, then advised: go to Monsieur du Bousquier. “Don’t take linen to anyone else,” he warned; du Bousquier, an old bachelor, was rich and ambitious, fearful of scandal and thus easily managed. He urged her to twist the man round her finger, but warned not to involve him—he had no money to help her.

Du Bousquier was of an old Alençon family who made his fortune as commissary of provisions to the armies from 1793 to 1799. He lived magnificently, mingled with Barras and Fouché, and had once supported hopes of ministerial rank, but miscalculation at Marengo and Napoleon’s disfavour ruined him. Returning to Alençon with only twelve hundred francs a year on the Grand Livre, he was received among royalists but distrusted by the nobility. He had once courted Mademoiselle Armande and been refused. Middle-aged, plump, broad-chested, with a voice like cutting moist wood, he favored the liberal faction, manipulating elections and nursing patient resentment against the Restoration.

The chevalier had recommended Suzanne to du Bousquier because both he and du Bousquier secretly hoped to marry Mademoiselle Cormon, the wealthy old maid. The chevalier never asked her; du Bousquier had and been refused. Their rivalry was courteous in one and less restrained in the other, but both frequented the same evening circles.

Suzanne sped to du Bousquier’s rue du Cygne house, a once-stately grey Jura-stone dwelling now neglectfully splendid. He lived with a single Breton servant, René, a simple, greedy fellow trained to polish boots and floors and to be a confidant.

René grunted that there was no linen to wash; Suzanne went upstairs and found du Bousquier in bed, swinging curtain-rings. She spoke gravely: she could be useful to him; she offered her hand and proposed that if he attached himself to her he might profit—Paris was changing, governments remade, misfortune could prove opportunity. She asked whether he now worked for anybody; his answer was “for myself,” blunt and proud.

She reproached him for never becoming a father; he had long toyed with the idea of marrying the respectable and wealthy Mademoiselle Cormon to advance socially and politically. Suzanne’s offer therefore presented a different choice: a bold, quick-minded wife who might help him in the present. He hesitated, flattered and suspicious, priding himself on never being duped by women.

Suzanne added urgency: she could not stay in Alençon—her mother would beat her, Madame Lardot would dismiss her. She needed money to reach Paris and for what Paris required. Du Bousquier, pressed by debts—payments on his house, decorators’ bills—offered three hundred francs. She feigned to go; alarmed and unwilling to be shown up, he softened. When she mentioned seeking help from Madame Granson of the Maternity Society, he yielded further, giving her a key and telling her to take from his secretary a bag containing about six hundred francs—“it is all I possess.” He warned her against deception.

Suzanne received the sack with impertinence: had he not given more if she had only asked? He kissed her, she cursed his stinginess on leaving, and tucked the bag into an osier gamebag. Already swift in mischief, she planned to ask Madame Granson and Mademoiselle Cormon’s Maternity Society to complete the thousand francs needed. In revenge for du Bousquier’s parsimony, she even contemplated using his complaisance to embroil him in a scandal. Thus, beneath playful dialogue and provincial charm, desires, rivalries, and schemes for social ascension intersected—an old chevalier plotting marriage to regain court favour, a ruined contractor nursing political grievances, and a clever wash-girl turning both men into instruments for her Parisian ambitions.

Madame Granson, widow of a lieutenant-colonel killed at Jena, lived on a meagre pension of nine hundred francs plus three hundred from a small property; her savings had been spent on educating a son. Their lodging in the rue du Bercail was a humble ground‑floor apartment in a provincial town: a street door above three steep steps, a passage to an interior courtyard, a gallery staircase, a dining‑room and kitchen on one side, and a multifunctional salon that served as the widow’s bedroom on the other. A portrait of the lieutenant‑colonel presided over the room.

Athanase Granson, twenty‑three, slept in an attic and added six hundred francs to the household from a post in the mayor’s office, obtained through his relation Mademoiselle Cormon; he was keeper of the archives. Athanase was thin, pale, of medium height, with a hollow face and dark eyes that suggested repressed talent. Irregular features and a melancholy cast revealed an imprisoned spirit: contemplative, timid, discouraged by provincial stagnation. Poverty bred in him a fierce pride; he was more thinker than actor, liable to sudden resolves that others might call madness. His library work went unnoticed; lacking encouragement, his abilities languished.

Beneath this lay a secret passion for his distant cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon, also pursued by the Chevalier de Valois and du Bousquier. His love began as calculation—Cormon was thought wealthy—and he hoped marriage would provide the stability to free him for fame and secure his mother’s comfort. That practical motive initially made him ashamed of his feeling, and he feared ridicule for a young man loving an “old maid” of forty. Yet in a provincial setting where manners are fixed and opportunities few, marriage for material security is common; Athanase’s prudence gradually yielded to real passion as habit and constant attention made him see only her perfections. Isolated, she became for him the single figure of desire, and his secret feeling deepened while his timidity grew.

Madame Granson, plain and composed beneath the portrait, suspected her son’s attachment and calculated candidly. She saw advantages: a talented husband would honor the family and neighborhood. She weighed obstacles—his poverty and Cormon’s age—but believed patience could overcome them. Like other provincials, she watched for favorable circumstances and plotted quietly. Distrusting du Bousquier, she concealed her tactics from Athanase.

Suzanne’s lie—confessed to Madame Granson—became a powerful weapon in the widow’s hands. As treasurer of the Maternity Society, Madame Granson could spread scandal under the guise of charity, rallying support for “the chaste Suzanne” and using sympathy to wound du Bousquier. The Chevalier de Valois anticipated possible outcomes but could not foresee the full storm.

At breakfast—coffee, bread and butter, radishes—mother and son kept their simple routine. Athanase, leaning on his elbow, twirled his spoon and took in the poor room—red brick floor, straw chairs, a painted buffet, checked curtains. His pallid face, framed by black hair and lit by the window, was the first thing Suzanne saw when she entered. Suzanne, a grisette familiar with hardship, felt an instantaneous electric attraction—an inexplicable conviction: “It is he!” The flash dispelled her usual cynicism. What had seemed a jest the night before now felt like a grave misdeed; she recoiled at her own readiness to deceive. Practical considerations—the young man’s poverty, her hopes of betterment, perhaps destiny—checked her scruples.

She asked for a private interview with Madame Granson and was led to the bedchamber. When Suzanne rejoined Athanase, tears came to her eyes. Madame Granson, radiant, had at last a terrible weapon against du Bousquier and promised the seduced girl the active support of charitable ladies, foreseeing a scandal over the guilty man’s head.

“My dear child,” Madame Granson told her son, “we dine to‑night with Mademoiselle Cormon; do take pains with your appearance. Put on that handsome frilled shirt and your green coat of Elbeuf cloth. I have my reasons.” She urged him to study the Chevalier’s easy manners and to soften his stiff air. Athanase listened, surprised but obedient; he rose, took his cap, and went to the mayor’s office, wondering whether his mother suspected his secret. Passing the rue du Val‑Noble—Cormon’s street—he indulged the common daydream of the devoted provincial: how little she guessed that a young man loved her, would be faithful, let her manage her fortune, yet could not speak.

Suzanne returned to her mother’s thinking of Athanase, and like many who long to help an adored man beyond their means, contemplated using her body as a stepping‑stone for his advancement. Her falsehood had served its purpose: a few days later she left town with money and fine clothes, including a green reps dress and a green bonnet lined with pink given by Monsieur de Valois—a gift she preferred even to money—and set off for Paris. Alençon lamented her misfortunes as the ladies of the Charity and Maternity Societies showed lively sympathy. Though Suzanne belonged to the Parisian‑bound class of gallantry, she remained in a fairly decent stratum; the Chevalier’s patronage and a distinguished Restoration journalist’s protection offered her prospects, perhaps even marriage.

In nearly all the smaller French prefectures there exists a salon that gathers the respectable, well‑considered people of the community who are not at the very top of society. The master and mistress of such an establishment are among the town’s leaders, received wherever they choose; no public fête or formal dinner occurs without them. But the château people—the heads of great estates, the highest personages—do not go to their houses; social intercourse between them and the salon is kept to cards and the occasional reciprocal dinner.

This salon, where the lesser nobility, clergy, and magistracy meet, exercises great influence. Judgment and mind of the region reside in that solid, unostentatious society, where each man knows his neighbor’s resources and where indifference to luxury and dress prevails—trifles beside concerns like securing ten or twelve acres of pasture. Immoveable in its prejudices, this circle follows a beaten track, accepting nothing from Paris without long trial; it rejects cashmeres and speculative investments alike, scoffs at fashions and novelties, reads little, and is indifferent to science, literature, or industrial inventions. It insists on removing an incompatible prefect; if administration resists, it isolates him, like bees walling up a snail in wax. Gossip in this society often becomes a solemn verdict. Young women seldom appear there; when they do it is to seek approbation of their conduct. The supremacy of one house wounds other natives, who console themselves by calculating the profit from its entertainments. If no fortune large enough exists to keep an open house, the leading people choose a neutral meeting‑place, as at Alençon, where the public salon convenes in the residence of an inoffensive old maid whose settled life offends no one.

For years the upper classes of Alençon met at the house of Mademoiselle Rose‑Marie‑Victoire Cormon. Her fortune, unknown to her, was coveted by Madame Granson, her second cousin, and by two old bachelors: the Chevalier de Valois and the Chevalier du Bousquier. Rose lived with her maternal uncle, the venerable Abbé de Sponde, once her guardian and now her heir. The Cormon family had been among the province’s most considerable bourgeois families, allied with nobility: stewards to the Duc d’Alençon, magistrates, and bishops among their members. Her maternal grandfather, Monsieur de Sponde, had been elected by the Nobility to the States‑General; her father, Monsieur Cormon, by the Tiers‑État, though neither accepted the mission. For a century daughters of the family had married provincial nobles, and the Cormons appeared in many genealogies; no bourgeois family seemed so near nobility.

The house where Mademoiselle Cormon lived had belonged to the family since Henry IV’s time when Pierre Cormon, steward to the last Duc d’Alençon, built it. Though it produced little revenue and was costly to maintain, such an aristocratic private dwelling in a provincial town attracted envy and temptation. The solid, ancient mansion in rue du Val‑Noble—granite with diamond‑cut blocks, one clear story, steep roof and projecting oak‑framed windows—conveyed an appearance almost peerly to visitors who thought the mayor ought to live there. Stables, coach‑house, kitchens, and laundry flanked the courtyard.

A side of the porte‑cochère opened to a modest courtyard garden—roses, pinks, lilies, Spanish broom, potted pomegranate and myrtle in season—kept with a scrupulous, almost Dutch neatness that betrayed the life of an old maid who, lacking other occupation, introduced meticulous order into a region proud of professing indifference to comfort. A glass door from the portico led into an antechamber—a tiled, wainscoted gallery housing family portraits, cloaks, clogs, overshoes, umbrellas, and pelisses of guests; benches lined the walls for servants and a stove countered the north wind. The house was divided into two halves. Toward the courtyard were the staircase well, a large dining‑room overlooking the garden, and a pantry communicating with the kitchen. On the other side was the salon with four windows and two smaller rooms—a boudoir looking on the garden, and an office looking on the courtyard. The upper floor contained a family apartment and a suite where the Abbé lived. Garrets housed rats and mice whose nocturnal restlessness Mademoiselle Cormon lamented with comic surprise. The garden, about half an acre, bordered the shallow Brillante river, near dyehouses and trades; trimmed linden hedges and pottery vases completed the modest, tranquil setting: nothing pretentious, but nothing transitory.

The ground floor, wholly devoted to reception, embodied the provincial spirit. The great square salon had four windows modestly cased in gray woodwork. An oblong mirror above the fireplace and draped green damask curtains were among the few ornaments. Furniture covered with tapestry, painted woodwork of an earlier century, chairs with La Fontaine scenes, some mended tapestries—everything showed preservation rather than novelty. A huge beam divided the ceiling; an old rock‑crystal chandelier hung swathed in green gauze. On the mantel were Sèvres‑blue vases, old girandoles, and a clock whose bronze‑gilt group illustrated Sedaine’s Le Déserteur: soldiers bringing a deserter and a fainting young woman, a moralized decoration typical of provincial taste. Portraits by Rigaud and pastels by Latour hung on the walls. Four card tables, a backgammon board, and a piquet table occupied the room—the only ceiled chamber in the house.

The dining‑room, paved in black‑and‑white stone with painted beams, contained a large marble‑topped sideboard necessary for the provincial war on the stomach. Frescoed walls, varnished cane seats, and plain wood doors gave it a patriarchal air. The provincial genius preserves everything: nothing is new or decrepit; a cold precision pervades the house. This Cormon house typified burgher houses in Normandy, Brittany, Maine, and Anjou and helps explain manners and customs. Life within was calm and monotonously regular. A library below river level housed well‑bound, dusty books chosen from regional presses; transport costs limited purchases to desirable, well‑selected works.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s society numbered about one hundred and fifty persons. Some visited country estates or traveled, but many came every night unless elsewhere invited; most were mature, few had traveled, and several had taken part in the chouannerie and were beginning to speak openly of that war now that rewards were being distributed to its defenders. Monsieur de Valois, a mover in the last uprising, had recently related stratagems used against Hulot, an old republican commander stationed at Alençon.

Women in this society took little trouble with dress except on Wednesdays, when Mademoiselle Cormon gave a dinner and guests returned the “visit of digestion.” Wednesdays were gala days; the assembly was numerous; visitors appeared in ribbons; some women brought sewing or knitting; young girls made patterns for Alençon point lace to pay their expenses. Certain husbands attended out of policy; young men were few; whispers were impossible without attracting attention, so clandestine love‑making was rare.

Every evening at six the antechamber filled. Each habitué brought cane, cloak, and lantern. Habits were patriarchal: if the Abbé lay down, or Mademoiselle retired, servants did not interrupt; the first comer received the next, and whist, piquet, or boston began when company sufficed, without awaiting the hosts. If dark, servants lit candles at the first bell; seeing the salon lighted, the abbé would slowly descend. Backgammon and piquet, three boston tables, and the whist table filled—twenty‑five or thirty persons; occasionally forty appeared. Between eight and nine servants accompanied their masters home; short of a revolution no one remained after ten. Guests departed in groups, discoursing on the game or continuing conversations on land, wills, quarrels among heirs, or aristocratic haughtiness—much like Parisians leaving a theatre. Du Bousquier, the society’s born statistician, estimated the salon’s habitués commanded 131 votes in the electoral college and mustered about eighteen thousand francs a year from nearby landed estates.

Alençon’s society was not entirely represented by this salon; higher aristocracy had its own circles, and the receiver‑general’s house acted like a government inn where society danced, plotted, flirted, and supped. These salons communicated through mixed individuals with Mademoiselle Cormon’s house, which nevertheless sat in judgment on the others, criticizing their dinners’ luxury, their ices at balls, and the women’s behavior and fashions.

As sovereign of this coterie, Mademoiselle Cormon naturally attracted suitors. To marry her was to reign in Alençon: election as deputy for a bourgeois, or official advancement for a noble. Two bachelors had long hopes: the Chevalier de Valois and the Chevalier du Bousquier. Athanase Granson, another suitor, loved her for herself as well as for her fortune. But the drama of their rivalries circled a woman who never guessed they existed, despite her eager desire to be married.

Why, despite fortune and suitors, did she remain unmarried? Several reasons combined. She always wished to marry a nobleman; from 1788 to 1798 political circumstances were hostile and despite the Consulate and Empire the numbers of suitable bachelors were altered. She feared the Revolutionary tribunal and consequently hesitated. She refused old men and would not marry very young ones; provincial families commonly married sons early to avoid conscription, and she obstinately refused soldiers—she would not give a man up to the Emperor; she wanted him for herself. Thus, from 1804 to 1815 she found no suitable match among young men. Besides preferring nobility, she had a strong desire to be loved for herself. She set tests for suitors so subtle that unlucky aspirants failed without realizing it: an unguarded word, a joke, or a tone might condemn a man—one had no heart, another told lies, a third was irreligious, a fourth sought money. She demanded moral and social perfection, wanting to be married for imaginary defects that she intended to redeem by revealing virtues. Nobles met common souls who prioritized interest and lacked her finer calculations.

As she approached a “second youth” her distrust grew. She presented herself unfavorably and behaved so that last wooers hesitated to bind themselves to a woman whose virtuous, scrutinizing nature required penetration men—who wanted virtue ready‑made—did not possess. Fear of marrying for her money made her suspicious beyond reason. After each disappointment she grew to despise mankind, adopting a secret misanthropy which tinged her conversation with bitterness and her eyes with severity. Celibacy hardened her manners; she strove to sanctify herself in despair: noble vengeance, she cut for God the rough diamond rejected by man. Public opinion judged her accordingly; society assumed her rejections had secret causes—deformity or hidden faults—but she was as pure as a saint, healthy, and full of kindness; Nature had intended her for motherhood.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s person was not classically beautiful but had the rustic, buxom freshness called la beauté du diable. Broad feet, sinewy legs, a stout waist, and plump arms gave a Norman outline. Undecided eyes gave a sheepish simplicity; an aquiline nose contrasted with a narrow forehead; thick red lips showed kindliness while her forehead suggested a lack of ideas. Chestnut hair of extraordinary length added vigor. In a ball‑dress she might have seemed handsome; officers admired her robust figure. But as years passed plumpness filled her; no corset could restore youthful proportions. Her bosom’s amplitude overhung when she stooped; her chin doubled, shortening her neck and hindering carriage. No wrinkles but folds of flesh; jests said she powdered her skin like an infant’s.

This ample person attracted Athanase. Many indebted elegants would have welcomed making Mademoiselle Cormon happy. But at forty she had fortified virtue by stern religious practices. For three years a confessor directed maceration and advised scourging—much that modern medicine would find absurd. These practices cast a monastic tint over Rose, who took on a yellow pallor; a down darkened her upper lip and her temples grew shiny. Rumors said she suffered rushes of blood to the head. She confided her ills to the Chevalier de Valois, who would pull out his snuff‑box and say: “The right composing draught, my dear lady, is a good and kind husband.”

The violence of hope made Rose afraid to look a man in the face lest he perceive her feelings. By will—perhaps from tests—she treated those she felt attracted to ungraciously, fearful of seeming foolish. Society took her behavior for revenge for a refusal and made fun of her abortive marriages. By 1815 she was forty‑two; desire for marriage bordered on monomania because she saw chances of children slipping away. She loved wholesomely and simply—Catholic, chaste, incapable of coquetry.

The Abbé de Sponde could not aid matrimonial manoeuvres. Now seventy, he attributed the Revolution’s disasters to Providence punishing a dissolute Church and adopted an ascetic life, hiding charity and penances. Detached from earthly interests, his mind turned to sufferers and the Church’s needs; he left property management to his niece, who gave him its income while he spent surpluses in secret alms. The abbé loved his niece as a daughter but was abstracted and could not conceive the flesh’s agitations; accustomed to reverence him, Rose dared not tell him of her desires. His silence was affable and benevolent; tall, spare, grave, solemn, his presence carried sacred authority. He was fond of the Voltairean chevalier; relics of nobility and clergy recognized each other by generous traits.

Some might imagine Mademoiselle Cormon employed arts of coquetry to secure a husband—but she did not. She was immovable in her high‑necked chemisette; her gowns were made by Alençon’s two hump‑backed sisters, who had taste. She refused airy deceits of elegance, choosing substance in all things. Piety allowed no subtleties: she trod the path of salvation, preferring prolonged virginity’s sorrows to trickery. In a woman armed with a scourge, virtue will not compromise; consequently love and self‑interest must seek her resolutely. Devotion can produce moral myopia: pious persons turn so to celestial things they become blind to many earthly matters. The Chevalier de Valois found it hard to decide whether stupidity makes one pious or piety makes one stupid. But whether stupidity or sanctity, their sorrows deserve pity like those of genius.

Thus Rose was judged virginally ignorant. She failed to read Athanase Granson’s eyes—shy herself, she never suspected shyness in others; she did not recognize quavering tones as unuttered sentiment. It is unsurprising that a devoted soul may be blind to a noble one: heart qualities differ from intellectual ones as genius differs from nobility of soul. This good creature, who grieved at making yearly preserves for only her uncle and herself, was becoming almost ridiculous. Sympathy and mockery mingled: some pitied her goodness, others derided her defects. People speculated on who would inherit her property and her uncle’s savings. For a while she was suspected of being an “original”—dangerous in the provinces, where originality, meaning ideas not understood by others, is not permissible; provinces demand equality of mind and manner.

After 1804 the phrase “married like Mademoiselle Cormon” became proverbial in Alençon for ridiculous failures. Parisian sarcasm found it odd that so excellent a woman should excite laughter. She received the whole society at her house, was charitable, pious, incapable of malice, and embodied the spirit and habits of the place; she had never left them and loved their prejudices and interests.

Despite an income of eighteen thousand francs from land—a considerable provincial fortune—she lived simply. At her country place at Prébaudet she rode in an old wicker carriolle hung on two white leather straps, drawn by a wheezy mare and hardly protected by rusty leather curtains. Jacquelin cared for it as if it were the finest coupé in Paris. Mademoiselle valued it, having used it for twelve years—a fact she noted with triumphant thrift. Most inhabitants were grateful she did not humiliate them by ostentation; had she imported a calèche from Paris they would have gossiped more about it than her matrimonial failures. In the provinces people look to results and care little about the elegance of means so long as they function.

To complete the picture of the household’s internal habits, the circle around Mademoiselle Cormon and the Abbé de Sponde must include Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette, who provided for the uncle and niece’s comfort.

Jacquelin, about forty, short, fat, ruddy, with a Breton-sailor face, had served them twenty-two years. He waited at table, groomed the mare, tended the garden, blacked the abbé’s boots, ran errands, chopped wood, drove the carriole, fetched oats and hay from Prébaudet, and dozed in the antechamber in the evenings. He loved Josette, a thirty-year-old, short, plump woman whose face—“like a dirty apricot”—betrayed sense and character; town gossip held that she managed her mistress. Mademoiselle would have dismissed Josette if she had married Jacquelin, so the two resigned themselves to saving their wages and loving in silence, awaiting some distant change. Mariette, cook of fifteen years, knew how to prepare Alençon’s most esteemed dishes.

The old Norman mare Pénélope, drawn to Prébaudet by Mademoiselle, had served the family eighteen years and was tended with as much devotion as any human servant. Mademoiselle and Jacquelin hoped to use her for ten more years. The household—four dependents and a beast—moved each day with mechanical regularity; as they said, “they had eaten their white bread first.” Mademoiselle Cormon, driven by a fixed idea, became hard to please, petty, and nagging—less by temperament than to occupy her active mind. Without husband or children, she busied herself with minutiae: napkins marked “Z” before the “O’s,” whether Pénélope had her oats at two o’clock, a neglected dust-layer, a poorly made toast, Josette’s delay in closing blinds that faded furniture. Small things became grounds for serious quarrels. Once Josette handed her the wrong devotional text and Mademoiselle left church in embarrassment; the town heard of the calamity. She would admonish Josette softly—“Such a thing must never happen again”—and the little quarrels served as catharses, secretly steadying her. Josette and Jacquelin accepted her moods like weather: “It is fine to-day,” or “It rains,” without blaming the heavens. Each morning they wondered in what humor she would rise, as farmers wonder at dawn mists.

She had narrowed, quite naturally, to the infinite pettinesses of life: herself and God, her confessor and the weekly wash, her preserves and church services, and her uncle to care for, occupied her feeble intellect. Her perfect health magnified the slightest digestive trouble; she submitted to quarterly medicinal doses strong enough, it was said, to have killed Pénélope but seeming to rejuvenate her mistress. A small pimple could occasion inquiries for a week until Josette triumphantly blamed a hare or Mariette’s seasoning: “No doubt, no doubt, it was the hare!”

Four times a year she spent days at her estate, Prébaudet. In mid-May she liked to see whether her apple-trees had “snowed” (fallen blossoms promising an abundant cider harvest), supervise winter repairs, and order flower-beds and the vegetable garden that supplied her table. Every season had business. Before departing she always gave a farewell dinner to intimate friends, though she expected to see them again within weeks. Her departure was town news: shopkeepers repeated it from door to door—“So Mademoiselle Cormon is going to Prébaudet!”—accompanied by provincial commentary.

The Wednesday Suzanne chose to reveal her scandal was such a farewell Wednesday, when Mademoiselle was driving and Josette distractedly packed. Madame Granson had spent the morning visiting houses, and the malicious Chevalier de Valois was playing piquet with Mademoiselle Armande, who governed aristocratic salons. The chevalier and Madame Granson wanted to see how Mademoiselle Cormon, as both marriageable woman and president of the Maternity Society, would receive the news. Du Bousquier, the innocent suitor, walked on the promenade, beginning to suspect Suzanne had tricked him.

On gala days Mademoiselle Cormon’s table was laid about half-past three; the fashionable dined at four. One of her chief pleasures was seeing herself dressed as mistress of the house to receive guests. When equipped, a ray of hope would glide into her heart; she felt that nature had not provided in vain and that some brave man might present himself. She reveled in inspecting the dining-room—an oblong table set with a snow-white cloth, twenty covers, a squadron of bottles with honorable labels, and little paper name cards in the trembling hand of the abbé—the only duty he assumed. After these preliminaries she would go in her finery to her uncle, who took his walk on the terrace overlooking the Brillante and the coppice. In waiting she never joined the Abbé de Sponde without asking some ridiculous question to rouse him from his meditative silence; she regarded it as her duty to talk and make herself agreeable, a practice advised by her spiritual director, the Abbé Couturier. She perspired in her corset thinking up ideas; her efforts produced absurdities such as: “No one can be in two places at once—unless it is a little bird,” which somehow provoked a debate on the apostles’ ubiquity. Such talk earned her the polite label “that good Mademoiselle Cormon”—a way of saying she was rather a fool; many peers, however, took her sincerity at face value and praised her.

When conversation flagged the Chevalier de Valois, who loved to assist women, would transform her sayings into wit by sustaining them paradoxically; once when she could not distinguish an ox from a bull, he claimed the only difference was like that between sheep and lamb. He often covered her retreats so deftly that she seemed never to have said anything silly. She took his help as evidence of her adequacy and grew complacent in her ignorance, acquiring a self-possession that gave her speeches a solemnity akin to patriotic absurdities.

One day she tried to draw her uncle into a matrimonial discussion. Leaning on his arm she reasoned: if everything below happens according to God’s will, then if she remains unmarried God wills it. But if she could marry tomorrow, does not her will destroy God’s? The Abbé de Sponde, a former prior of the Sorbonne, gently showed the childishness of the logic, pointing out she had put in an “if.” When she argued that God must have meant women to marry or not uniformly, the abbé replied that she blamed the Church, which declared celibacy better. “You have too much mind, Rose; you don’t need so much to be happy,” he said, a remark that pleased and confirmed her self-opinion.

Guests arrived and the familiarities between servants and company began: Mariette telling Monsieur du Ronceret she had cooked cauliflowers au gratin “expressly for you,” Josette informing Madame Granson that “Mademoiselle has thought of you, and there’s fish for dinner,” the chevalier in his easy condescending tone asking Mariette if there was some dainty he should reserve. Du Bousquier was not invited. Mademoiselle Cormon, faithful to her ambivalence, treated the fifty-year-old suitor ill though she felt inexplicable sentiments toward him. She had refused him yet sometimes repented; a presentiment haunted her that she might yet marry him, and terror prevented her desiring it. Madame Granson and the chevalier, observing her naive glances toward du Bousquier, resolved to ruin his hopes.

Two tardy guests—Monsieur du Coudrai, recorder of mortgages, and Monsieur Choisnel, the notary—kept dinner waiting. As they arrived, Jacquelin announced “They are all in the garden.” The company strolled the terrace above the river, admiring water-plants, mosaics of currents, and houses across the Sarthe with old wooden galleries and little gardens where clothes dried—a charming provincial landscape.

The chevalier scanned faces; he had introduced his firebrand into the chief houses. The scandal about Suzanne and du Bousquier had not yet been openly discussed: provincials distill gossip, waiting the right moment. Whispers circulated: “You have heard?” “Yes.” “Du Bousquier?” “And that handsome Suzanne.” “Does Mademoiselle Cormon know of it?” “No.” “Ha!”

The chevalier, noting Madame Granson’s triumphant look, suspected not only her joy but self-interest. He watched Athanase and saw, in a furtive look at Mademoiselle Cormon, a flash that set his plans awry. He offered his arm to Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he revered. She, secretly wishing for alliance, admired the name de Valois as a social object, though his seeming purity and indifference worried her; she wanted a husband a little dissipated—someone with experience to purify. Observers have remarked pious women’s leaning toward scamps; Mademoiselle’s wish showed this: she would have liked a man to combine the chevalier’s nobility with du Bousquier’s vigor.

Two matters agitated the town and linked du Bousquier to civic tensions. One concerned the rector of Alençon who had once taken the constitutional oath but was winning Catholics by virtue; middle-class supporters rallied to him through du Bousquier, while Mademoiselle Cormon and the Abbé de Sponde, of the uncompromising “little Church,” refused to receive him. The second was du Bousquier’s idea of building a theatre in Alençon; Athanase Granson and other young clerks championed the project. The chevalier urged Mademoiselle Cormon to save Athanase from this influence. “You have so much sense and judgment in social proprieties and influence with his mother; ought you not to use it?” he said, implying that lycée-bred lads marched about in uniform and that Athanase’s shyness and pale looks foretold a sad end; he warned that such associations might cost him his place in the mayor’s office. Madame Granson, who overheard part of the talk, followed Mademoiselle hoping to learn the outcome. Jacquelin announced dinner, and the recorder cut off the chevalier, allowing him instead to address Madame Granson directly; she gratefully promised to enlighten her son. The chevalier had long seen Athanase’s unyielding republican convictions—rooted in artistic sorrows and a bitter social view—and knew he would persist. To remain faithful to the Left in Alençon was to earn Mademoiselle Cormon’s aversion.

Thus this outwardly peaceful society was internally agitated by craft and passion like any diplomatic circle. The guests ate with healthy provincial appetites. Toward the end of the first course Mademoiselle Cormon made the celebrated remark that would be repeated for two years: in the debate against du Bousquier—whom the chevalier and others depicted as a pernicious intriguer—she had innocently said, “As for me, I thought that Monsieur du Bousquier cared chiefly for childish things.” The phrase had enormous success: it undermined the Princess Goritza’s dignity and astonished the chevalier into silent admiration; he applauded discreetly, as at the Opera.

Prompted by the chevalier, du Bousquier was caricatured as a monster, a Pere Gigogne who had supplied the Foundling Hospital for fifteen years; his immoral habits were made to explain Parisian revels. The chevalier speculated that a du Bousquier might marry a “Suzette,” invoking Madame du Barry and the Emperor Joseph; Mademoiselle corrected him with naïve historicism. The Abbé de Sponde, touched with charitable solemnity, urged succor for the poor girl now unlikely to marry. The judge, Madame Granson, and others volleyed puns and jests until the abbé’s grave “Nor can he be a foster-father” quieted them.

Usually a man is ignorant of town rumors; du Bousquier remained superbly unaware of Suzanne’s revelations and was jaunty at coffee. Mademoiselle Cormon, embarrassed, lectured Athanase with platitudes about royalist politics and morality. Athanase listened with a stupid air, gazing at her enormous bust and swelling in his love, which transformed her shrill voice into a soft murmur and her flat remarks into witticisms. Love, the narrator notes, is a maker of false coin, turning copper into gold.

“Will you promise me?” she asked Athanase, and the words pierced his absorbed mind. Had he spoken then, he might have revealed his passion; but his shy absorption ruined him. When his mother demanded what he had been saying to Mademoiselle, he replied “Nothing.” Madame Granson, who believed du Bousquier forever lost in Mademoiselle’s esteem after that evening, thought little more of the remark.

The salon later filled: tables for piquet and backgammon, groups by the fire in evening finery, each repeating their farewell: “So you are going tomorrow to Prébaudet?” “Yes, I really must.” Mademoiselle Cormon appeared preoccupied; Madame Granson noticed she was thinking. “What are you thinking of, cousin?” she asked. “I am thinking,” Mademoiselle replied, “of that poor girl. As president of the Maternity Society, I will give you fifty francs for her.” “Fifty francs!” cried Madame Granson in astonishment. Mademoiselle never gave so much, but her sudden generosity showed du Bousquier had risen in her estimation.

“Du Bousquier is not only a monster, he is a villain,” said Madame Granson. “When a man has done a wrong like that, he ought to pay the indemnity. Isn’t it his place rather than ours to look after the girl?—who, to tell you the truth, seems rather questionable.” Mademoiselle, who could not quite grasp how hearts work, said innocently that perhaps one could be calumniated for loving Athanase, yet be loved truly. Madame Granson tried to explain the physical side: “It is impossible for Suzanne to love du Bousquier. And if the heart counts for nothing—” “But, cousin, what do people love with if not their hearts?” inquired the old maid, widening her eyes in honest puzzlement.

Madame Granson advised a show of displeasure: not receiving du Bousquier in her house until he married would manifest exemplary morals. Mademoiselle promised to consult her uncle and the Abbé Couturier upon her return from Prébaudet. The lights, the women in best clothes, and the solemn assembly made Mademoiselle proud: to many it was the best of Parisian society. Du Bousquier, whistling a pastoral air, was the centre of curiosity: some admired him, others saw danger. The chevalier, going home, planned to spread a rumor of his marriage to Mademoiselle Cormon to secure attention and advantage—hoping to use connections to become mayor, then deputy, dreaming of peerage and power, and imagining how a marriage would serve his ambitions.

When Madame Granson reached home she fiercely argued with her son Athanase, who could not see the link between his political opinions and his love—a quarrel the household had never known before.

The next morning Mademoiselle Cormon, packed into the old carriole with Josette and a pyramid of parcels, set off for Prebaudet; fate was about to thrust a sudden chance at marriage on her.

The day after her arrival, while she breakfasted and heard reports from her keeper and gardener, Jacquelin burst in breathless. “Monsieur l’abbé sends an express—the son of Mère Grosmort—with a letter. The lad left Alençon before daylight. Can’t I give him a glass of wine?” Alarmed, she read that Monsieur de Troisville, grandson of one of her uncle’s old friends and lately in the Russian service during the Emigration, intended to retire to Alençon and had asked the Abbe de Sponde for hospitality. The absent-minded abbé begged his niece to return at once to help receive this guest; the viscount’s delayed letter suggested he might arrive that very night.

Her reaction was instantaneous. She ordered Penelope harnessed, demanded leave-taking in half an hour, and despite rain and warnings about the mare’s oats drove back to town at a gallop, pushing Jacquelin and Josette to hurry. Absorbed in visions of a trousseau and wedding-day schemes, she chattered about fitting Prebaudet for a vicomte and resolved, with her uncle’s help, not to let him leave as he entered.

Her sudden return—Penelope lathered and at full gallop, parcels half-packed, the early hour, and her visible agitation—stirred Alençon. The great green gate opening and closing, the sight of a frantic old maid, and gossip sent ripples through the town. Josette guessed the expected man: between forty and forty-two, a bachelor of middling fortune. Mademoiselle already called herself Vicomtesse de Troisville.

She scolded the abbé in absentia for his neglect and set the household into feverish motion. Mariette was sent to market for the best provisions with orders to spare no expense; Moreau the upholsterer was summoned to contrive a bed in Mademoiselle’s boudoir so the viscount would not be lodged too near her uncle’s bare room. She fretted about her appearance, the house’s furnishings, and whether the viscount’s smile would reflect her years. Everything comfortable now seemed rusty and absurd to the ardent spinster willing to spend half her savings to refit Prebaudet overnight.

Josette, Jacquelin, Mariette, Moreau and their agents set to work like ants—scrubbing, polishing, bringing out ceremonial china and damask, preparing the best liqueurs, arranging clothes, and dressing themselves in full. The garden was raked, the table laid, and Mademoiselle rehearsed her role as hostess of a brilliant society.

At about four the postilion’s click-clack announced arrival. A single post-chaise drew up; boys and men followed it to the gate. The abbé greeted the unknown gentleman; the chaise was emptied and rolled into the coach-house. The vicomte alighted with the carriage of a man who had seen Europe: blue eyes, black hair, olive skin, dignified, nearer forty-six than forty, with a manner that spoke diplomacy and travel.

Mademoiselle, trembling with hope, presented herself when Josette opened the boudoir. The abbé introduced them: “Niece, this is Monsieur le Vicomte de Troisville; Monsieur, my niece, Mademoiselle Cormon.” She thought her uncle had done wonders. The abbé and his niece left the vicomte to arrange his lodgings while they walked by the river; she was already imagining matrimonial inevitabilities.

Back inside, she declared the viscount’s manners, voice, and looks proved him suitable. The abbé, absent-minded as ever, observed only that the vicomte had written he wanted to buy a house; if he were married he would not have come alone, he added, not thinking that his niece might interpret differently. Mademoiselle, for whom forty seemed youth to a septuagenarian priest, continued to flatter hope. The vicomte admired the Brillante, the house’s harmony; she praised her trees, nightingales, and ancestral continuity, adding that she would change nothing unless she married—when everything must suit the husband. The vicomte complimented her principles and household arrangements with courtly ease.

Dinner gave Mademoiselle a chance to shine. The vicomte spoke lightly of interiors and land; she took every look as encouragement, poured every cup with trembling eagerness, and assumed the bearing of a queen of Alençon. At dessert the sound of expected friends arriving heightened her excitement; she ordered coffee and Madame Amphoux’s rare liqueurs brought into the salon—a conspicuous sign, noted by all and destined to fuel gossip.

Rumors spread fast. The Abbe de Sponde, asked about the newcomer, had said a vicomte who had served in Russia was expected. Between two and five a labial telegraphy ran: Mademoiselle Cormon had found a husband by letter and would marry the Vicomte de Troisville. Speculation varied—was he a poor younger son or a wealthy Russian?—and all her acquaintances determined to visit that evening.

When the company assembled, du Bousquier muttered that the liqueurs’ presence meant something planned, while the chief-justice surmised that the marriage had been arranged long ago with letters from Odessa. The Chevalier de Valois, usually witty and composed, paled at the left cheek and sought to hide his agitation by commenting on the cold. Mademoiselle, flushed and triumphant from her morning’s emotion, presented the viscount to everyone with pride.

Monsieur de Troisville and the Chevalier, both aristocrats by nature, recognized each other and struck an immediate accord, forming a circle by the fire. Conversation turned to houses and lodging. Then, in a moment that unmade all Mademoiselle’s hopes, the Chevalier asked whether the vicomte was married.

“Yes,” Monsieur de Troisville replied. “For the last sixteen years, to a daughter of the Princess Scherbellof.”

Mademoiselle Cormon fainted. Du Bousquier sprang forward and supported her to her bedroom; Josette cut her corset bindings, and du Bousquier splashed water to revive her. When she opened her eyes she saw du Bousquier and gave a modest cry—an intimate, involuntary sight of her disarray now observed by a man. He retired, and six women, led by Madame Granson, tended her.

The company, amused and scandalized, discussed the comedy. The abbé had known the vicomte’s errand but, absent-minded, had not supposed his niece could be interested; the vicomte, preoccupied with house-hunting and indifferent to talk of his wife, had never announced she existed and naturally assumed Mademoiselle understood from his coming that he was single. Jacquelin, who had dreamed of his mistress’s match, felt the sting: Madame Amphoux’s liqueurs destined for a bachelor would not be served to a married man.

Next morning, bled and recovered, Mademoiselle Cormon could have shown dignity and Christian resignation; instead she offered, with admirable composure, to lend her house temporarily to the viscount until he found a place. He told her lightly he had two sons and two daughters and would inconvenience her; the abbé reminded her that he had already offered the same in a previous letter she had not received. Shocked, she realized then that her uncle had known all along.

Left alone and conscious that prompt marriage was the only cure for scandal, she considered her options. Josette urged her to accept du Bousquier. Two bachelors—du Bousquier and the Chevalier de Valois—sensed opportunity; each resolved to press his suit that morning. Du Bousquier, coarse but sincere, arrived first. The Chevalier, polishing his look with rouge for once, delayed and so lost his chance.

Du Bousquier entered with rough earnestness. Mademoiselle, remembering how he had borne her when she fainted, saw his devotion in a new light. He declared he had never changed his feelings, that her first refusal had not discouraged him, and that in spite of her severity he loved her. He praised her beauty with a simplicity that struck her. Touched, she asked him to promise to live a Christian life with her, to respect her religious practices and confessors. He agreed. Then, with tender pragmatism, she asked one more thing: she begged him to declare—a lie she felt monstrous—that their marriage had been agreed upon for six months, so as to silence gossip. Du Bousquier, ardent and eager, accepted the sacrifice gladly.

At that moment the Chevalier arrived. He found the pair, and du Bousquier, in triumph, confessed to him—ever eager to wound—that he had secretly kept an engagement and intended to marry Mademoiselle in a few days; the house in the rue du Cygne was therefore for sale, and the abbé had taken the vicomte to view it. The Chevalier believed the falsehood; after a cutting compliment about their happiness he noticed du Bousquier’s false front hanging askew and was provoked to amusement by the sight of a man affecting dignity with his wig awry. Mademoiselle blushed; du Bousquier was mortified; the old grudge between aristocrat and purveyor simmered.

Seeing everything decided, Mademoiselle, anxious to keep the peace, proposed a simple game of piquet. Du Bousquier fetched the table, and the Chevalier, stunned by his loss, allowed himself to be led to the game. The abbé and the vicomte returned from viewing houses; the vicomte’s interest in Mademoiselle’s house confirmed the plausibility of du Bousquier’s fabrications. Mademoiselle took her uncle aside and begged that he mention to the viscount the rue du Cygne house was for sale—a plausible explanation for the visit—and concealed the lie from the absent-minded priest.

The town took the story to heart. By evening the rumor of a marriage spread and entertained Alençon as much as the anxieties of 1814–1815 once had. Some laughed; others accepted it as fact. The middle classes rejoiced at seeing the proud Cormons humbled into marriage with a purveyor. The Chevalier offered his usual biting commentary among friends: “The Cormons end as they began; there’s only a hand’s breadth between a steward and a purveyor.”

The news that Mademoiselle Cormon had married struck Athanase Granson like a stab to the heart, though he betrayed nothing outwardly. He learned it at the du Roncerets’ while his mother played boston; she thought his pallor was merely nerves, unaware that rumor had been eroding his hopes all day. Mademoiselle Cormon had been the one card on which Athanase had staked his life; her marriage felt to him like catastrophe. Hardened by a fatalistic education under the Empire, his imagination, already fed on dread, magnified the blow until hope and self-belief gave way. He sat beside his mother, eyes fixed on the cards in a stupor she mistook for indifference—his coldness explained his earlier refusal to make customary sacrifices for the match, whose “liberal” political overtones complicated private affairs.

After that evening, Athanase fled to the Sarthe countryside—windmills, river meadows, lonely poplars—seeking the solitude he loved. Provincials lacked sensitivity for such views; he wandered vacant under a poplar while neighbors told his mother, “There is something the matter with your son.” She assumed he brooded on some great work. He abandoned political activity, sometimes adopting a satirical gaiety that was really personal scorn; he had few friends and no woman to console him. Had Suzanne appeared earlier, his miseries might have been exchanged for love; as it was, she crossed his path only later.

Suzanne’s ambitions were stoked by tales of the Marquis de Montauran’s adventure with Marie de Verneuil at the More tavern. Enamored of beauty and seduction’s romance, she wanted to make life into a stage for exerting power over men. Months after leaving Alençon in haste, she returned with an artist to see Fougères and the scene of the Marquis’s story, imagining a disguised arrival and hoping to send money delicately to her mother and perhaps to Athanase.

For a month the town argued over Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage. Some denied it, some affirmed it; du Bousquier’s sale of his house to the Marquis de Troisville seemed to contradict the story until the twentieth day, when Monsieur Lepressoir, the liberals’ notary, went to her house and the contract was signed. The marriage was the first of many sacrifices Mademoiselle Cormon would make to her husband. Du Bousquier bore a long antipathy to Choisnel, the usual notary; Mademoiselle Cormon received anonymous letters and, to her surprise, learned from them that Suzanne had never been a heroine of any such exploit—the seducer with a false toupet was fiction. Suzanne confessed the trick, sent a thousand francs to a maternity society, and tried to punish the old purveyor; the society thereafter resolved to assist only real misfortunes.

Despite gossip, the banns were published. The bride retired to Prébaudet, visited daily by du Bousquier with lavish bouquets. On a dull rainy June morning, Mademoiselle Cormon and Sieur du Bousquier were married at noon in the parish church before the whole town. They left the mayor’s office for the church in an ostentatious open calèche from Paris—an affront to provincials wary of Parisian luxury, rising rents, and foreign furniture. The loss of the old carriole, the new horses, and carriage display prompted gossip about outsiders taking local money. Pépéno had died six weeks before; Madame du Bousquier entered the church with her left foot—an omen, because “Left” had begun to carry political meaning—and the priest, by chance, opened his book at the De Profundis. No wedding party followed; the pair departed immediately for Prébaudet. People murmured that Parisian customs would conquer provincial ways. Jacquelin and Josette’s later gay wedding offered one cheerful refutation of dire predictions.

Du Bousquier used proceeds from his former house to modernize the hôtel Cormon, decided to remain two seasons at Prébaudet, and took the Abbé de Sponde with them. The town feared “comfort.” Soon the bridegroom drove into town in a fine tilbury, deposited his wife’s savings at the Grand-Livre, and—by manipulating for a rise—had made himself a personal fortune almost as large as his wife’s.

A fate worse than modern furniture was linked to the marriage. On the evening of the ceremony, Athanase sat with his mother by a small fire. Madame Granson proposed a visit to the du Roncerets’; Athanase, in a childish voice, asked her to stay longer, calling her “my dear mamma.” She consented, preferring to listen to his plans rather than play boston. He clung to her with desperate tenderness, kissed her eyes and hair, and, while trying to conceal a deadly resolve, told her he wished to leave Alençon and suffer away from her. She refused to be parted; he persisted. She offered money hidden in her petticoat for his outfit.

After a brief outing to du Ronceret’s, Athanase went to the Sarthe, settled beneath his chosen poplar on two stones, reviewed a future he no longer expected, and in a deliberate, nearly soundless act slipped into the deep pool where he had long intended to end his life. Madame Granson returned at half-past nine to find a short note: “My good mother, I have departed; don’t be angry with me.” She thought he had gone off cleverly and went to bed in peace.

The Sarthe had risen that morning and brought eels; a fisherman drew his net where Athanase had thrown himself and recovered the body at dawn. Friends prepared Madame Granson for the remains. The suicide shocked Alençon: the vain young man of genius had died with no protector the night before, and a thousand voices cried, “I would have helped him.” The Chevalier de Valois explained the suicide as an act of revenge for love, revealing Athanase’s sincere devotion to Mademoiselle Cormon. Guided by the chevalier’s rendering, Madame Granson recalled details that confirmed him; the story became touching and many women wept. Her grief was silent, concentrated, and not fully understood. The narrative distinguishes two sorts of maternal mourning: the public grief for a son admired in society, and the deeper private mourning of a mother who shared a child’s inner life.

Before the new Madame du Bousquier returned to town, Madame du Ronceret drove to Prébaudet to fling Athanase’s corpse upon the roses of the bride’s joy and sober her bridal month. On the road back to Alençon, Madame du Bousquier met Madame Granson at the corner of rue Val-Noble; the mother’s dying look—filled with malediction and reproach—struck the bride to the heart.

The evening after the catastrophe, Madame Granson sought out the town’s rector, though she had been hostile to him. After she shrouded her son with her own hands, she found the modest priest putting away flax and yarn to help the poor. He took her into his plain dining room; she noticed his frugality. She implored him; he professed to know why she had come and to trust her and Madame du Bousquier’s relation to pacify the bishop at Sées. He agreed to pray and say masses for the unhappy child, but insisted that scandal be avoided. He alone, without other clergy, would bury the body at night to prevent scandal.

Toward midnight a coffin was secretly borne to the parish church by four young men, Athanase’s comrades. A few friends, veiled women, and half a dozen intimates attended a discreet mortuary mass said by the rector and a choirboy. The body was carried to a corner of the cemetery and marked by a plain black cross without inscription. Athanase lived and died in shadow. Neither the rector nor the bishop was blamed; the mother’s piety redeemed the impiety of her son’s final act.

Months later, half beside herself with grief, Madame Granson went to the pool where her son had drowned, perhaps hoping to recover thoughts of him beneath that poplar or to see what his last eyes had seen. There she met Suzanne, who had come to Alençon the same morning she had heard the tragedy and who had planned—had Athanase lived—to send him several thousand francs by a false pretext. Seeing Madame Granson, Suzanne passed her weeping and whispered, “I loved him!” Before leaving town, Suzanne changed the bride’s orange blossoms to rue and declared that Madame du Bousquier would always be Mademoiselle Cormon, revenging Athanase and the chevalier with one cutting tongue.

Alençon also witnessed a slower suicide: the Chevalier de Valois died in life over fourteen years. Three months after the du Bousquier marriage, his linen was frayed, hair unkempt; the elegance that had masked grief collapsed. The cavalier who had sustained appearances now showed the soul’s power over the body. Hope deserted him and he aged overnight—pallid, gaunt, his nose’s delicate grace lost under tobacco stains; his clever sayings grew rarer though he still took snuff and alarming dinners. His intercourse with the Princess Goritza lessened. One day he appeared at Mademoiselle Armande’s salon with his calf of leg on his shin-bone—an image that horrified the town. The chevalier, who for ten years had awaited Mademoiselle Cormon and missed his game, frightened society by this decay. A mortified grisette revealed his mornings’ details; he now passed for a libertine and bore blames the liberals sometimes ascribed to du Bousquier. The faubourg Saint-Germain, pitying him, drew him closer and restored a few smiles; but enmity against du Bousquier hardened. Eleven persons deserted the Cormon salon for the d’Esgrignons.

The old maid’s marriage divided Alençon into parties. The salon d’Esgrignon represented the old aristocracy (the returning Troisvilles attached themselves to it); the Cormon salon under du Bousquier represented a class of opinion neither truly liberal nor resolutely royalist, the class that had cast votes like the famous 221. The salon du Ronceret, secretly allied to Cormon, was boldly liberal.

The Abbé de Sponde, after returning from Prébaudet, felt inward pain and told no word to his niece; to Mademoiselle Armande he admitted he would have preferred the chevalier over Monsieur du Bousquier. The abbé mourned the loss of his little grove and lindens—symbols of home and prayer—cut down to make an English garden. Du Bousquier, with Abbé Couterier’s authorization, made alterations to please his wife that destroyed the venerable dignity of the old house: mahogany and blue satin, stucco columns and Greek moldings produced a mélange of out-of-season magnificence. At first the town gabbled, but months later took pride in the luxury. Manufacturers restored houses, modern furniture arrived, and astral lamps appeared. Yet the Abbé de Sponde perceived private unhappiness: noble simplicity had governed their lives; that character was lost the first winter when du Bousquier gave two balls every month—the abbé prayed on his knees during the revels. Political tone in the salon was perverted. The abbé saw du Bousquier’s imperiousness and his niece losing control of her property—left only the linen and table to manage—while Jacquelin became coachman, René groom, and a Parisian chef took charge. He saw in du Bousquier not merely ambition but a willingness to reconcile and seduce the clergy to consolidate power: du Bousquier arranged a reconciliation between the abbé’s friend, the minister of Saint-Léonard, and the parish rector. The abbé considered this an apostasy; he died as orthodoxy seemed to expire in the diocese.

In 1819 the abbé’s bequest increased Madame du Bousquier’s income to twenty-five thousand francs, not counting Prébaudet or Val-Noble. Du Bousquier returned her capital and used it to buy lands contiguous to Prébaudet, enlarging the domain. He passed for one of the department’s richest men, a frequent liberal candidate who narrowly missed victory and then ostentatiously repudiated them by seeking royalist office. This rancorous republican, mad with ambition, resolved to rival and ruin Alençon’s aristocracy when it regained power. He strengthened himself with the Church by a feint of piety—accompanying his wife to mass, giving to convents, aiding congregations—secretly supported by liberals and protected by clerical approval. Calling himself a constitutional royalist, he kept beside the aristocracy to undermine it, and he succeeded.

He subsidized point d’Alençon manufacture, renewed the linen trade, helped found a factory; Alençon gained industrial life. He posed as a banker and promoter of enterprises—insurance, public conveyances, petitions for roads and bridges—troubling the government until it yielded to community interest. Du Bousquier embittered provincial nobility against court nobility and the peerage, bringing constitutional royalists to side with the “Journal des Débats” and M. de Chateaubriand against the throne—a harbinger of journalism’s triumph in 1830.

After 1830 he became receiver-general, relying on relations with the Duc d’Orléans and Monsieur de Folmon, receiving about eighty thousand francs a year. In public he appeared respectable, steady and obliging. Alençon owed him its industrial ties and the sudden proliferation of private carriages. Du Ronceret’s remark, “Du Bousquier is a very strong man,” became the countryside’s verdict.

But to his wife the saying had a double meaning. The public politician was a domestic despot. This great citizen, generous to the world, was cold and without conjugal affection at home: astute, hypocritical, sly—a Cromwell in velvet. His wife bore no children; Suzanne’s and the chevalier’s insinuations seemed vindicated. The liberal bourgeoisie and the church party blamed Madame du Bousquier, saying she was too old at marriage and that pregnancy would have been dangerous. Friends praised du Bousquier, saying she was lucky to have him. Misjudged by society, the pious woman displayed her virtues at home. She lived in tears but presented a placid face; she told herself she had loved the chevalier yet had married du Bousquier, and Athanase’s death pursed her like remorse. Her uncle’s death added to her sorrow; grief soaked into her life.

The chevalier contrived to expose du Bousquier’s baseness to her by eliciting, in the intimacy of the d’Esgrignon salon, the secrets of her house—the inability to have children and the deceptions of her marriage—thereby rendering du Bousquier for a time ridiculous and odious. Ridicule faded, gossip subsided, but the incident intensified du Bousquier’s hatred of Esgrignon. He took public revenge by inserting a spiteful notice in his own Courrier de l’Orne, deriding the existence of some Monsieur de Pombreton.

Though her marriage was essentially negative, Madame du Bousquier found advantages: a remarkable man about her was preferable to solitude. Du Bousquier was preferable to a dog. He showed for his wife a sentiment more real and less selfish than that felt by servants or hopeful heirs. Later she regarded him as an instrument of divine chastisement: she had brought sorrow on Madame Granson and hastened her uncle’s death; obedient to religion, she kissed the rod and publicly praised him while weeping in prayer.

Bruised and immolated, compelled to make her husband happy and attached to him by habit and duty, her life became perpetual contradiction. She had married a man she hated but whom she nevertheless tended. Often she walked in spiritual consolation while he ate her preserves with satisfaction. She watched to satisfy his slightest wish and read proofs of his dutiful oblivion in small attentions. When he kissed her forehead and called her “a good woman,” tears came to her eyes. He likely made concessions enough to earn her respect; Catholic virtue did not demand total dissimulation. She withdrew into severe religious practices and ceased thinking of vanity. She presented to the world a union of Christian virtue; du Bousquier was one of the luckiest men in France.

The narrative notes the coincidence of the Chevalier de Valois’s death with Suzanne’s mother’s death: the chevalier died, broken by the fall of the monarchy, in August 1830. He had joined Charles X’s cortege and returned weakened, carrying money he intended for the king’s disposal; he died the day Charles X set foot on foreign soil. At the chevalier’s sale, Suzanne bought a snuff-box portrait for a thousand francs; later it passed to a collector. If the dead could see after them, the chevalier would have blushed for many things.

The story closes with two reflective remarks. First, if it prompts possessors of relics to bequeath them to loved hands, it will please the romantic. Second, it contains a higher moral: the need for a new kind of education—anthropology chairs—because modern myths, like ancient ones, are misunderstood. Had Mademoiselle Cormon been more cultivated, had Alençon had a professor of anthropology, or had she read Ariosto, much of the conjugal tragedy might have been averted; she might have understood why mythic figures choose one lover over another. The narrator attributes this opinion to a pupil of M. Ballanche without claiming full responsibility.

No information is given about the fate of the negroes’ heads in diamonds. Madame du Val-Noble may be seen at the Opéra; thanks to the chevalier’s guidance she appears nearly well-bred. Madame du Bousquier still lives, and after sixty confided to Madame du Coudrai that she could not endure the thought of dying an old maid.
