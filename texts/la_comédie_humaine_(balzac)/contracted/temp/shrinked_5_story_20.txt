Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman gentleman who became a Gascon by marrying a wealthy heiress and selling his estate, served as major of the Gate Guards until his death in 1813. He avoided the Revolution by going to Martinique. After his wife’s death in 1810 he grew miserly, depriving his son Paul of resources.

Paul returned from college in 1810 and lived under his father’s strict control for three years, which stunted his emotional development and left him timid. Confined to his father’s mansion and short of money, he could not socialize and felt out of place at royalist gatherings. He coped with exercise and novels, but his intellectual and social life remained limited.

When his father died Paul inherited a fortune but soon grew disillusioned with Bordeaux. He spent six years traveling Europe, quickly depleting his capital and relying increasingly on the estate’s revenues. Realizing he needed to settle, he returned to Bordeaux confident his noble title would attract a suitable match.

Paul confided in his friend de Marsay about his wish to marry and live as a country gentleman. De Marsay counseled against marriage, warning it would cost Paul his freedom and happiness and advising him to enjoy bachelorhood. Paul, however, longed for family, companionship, and stability, refusing to accept de Marsay’s cynicism.

In winter 1821 the newly titled Comte de Manerville set about restoring the family mansion and reestablishing his reputation. His charm gained favor among the local elite, but he believed marriage essential to a fulfilled life, especially with Mademoiselle Evangelista, the celebrated beauty of Bordeaux.

Mademoiselle Evangelista, Natalie, was the daughter of a wealthy Spanish merchant and had been raised in luxury after her father’s death. Rumors about her extravagant upbringing made some suitors wary. When Paul and Natalie met, they were immediately attracted; Paul dismissed concerns about finances, confident he could manage their situation. He idealized Natalie and overlooked potential faults, while the social excitement surrounding the match heightened its appeal. Yet Natalie’s refined upbringing and her mother’s ambitions foreshadowed complications.

In winter 1822 Paul pressed to marry Natalie with the aid of his great-aunt, the Baronne de Maulincour. After consulting Madame Evangelista, the baroness warned Paul to be cautious about the dowry and advised he consult their notary, Maître Mathias, to safeguard his interests.

At the notarial meeting, Solonet proposed terms favoring Madame Evangelista, while Mathias insisted on protections for Paul. Their debate revealed a clash of approaches—Mathias’s traditional prudence versus Solonet’s opportunism. They reached a compromise that secured Natalie’s inheritance and Madame Evangelista’s financial stability, but the uneasy concessions hinted at future tensions.

The following day jeweler Elie Magus appraised Madame Evangelista's diamonds for Comte Paul and Mademoiselle Natalie's marriage contract, valuing the jewelry at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” diamond at 100,000 francs. Yet he offered only 75,000 for the diamond and 160,000 for the lot because of depreciation. The discrepancy sparked rumors about the marriage’s stability, suggesting Madame Evangelista was selling her house and that the wedding might be canceled. Notaries denied these claims, but public skepticism persisted, fed by jealousy of the family's wealth.

To counter the gossip, Madame Evangelista staged an extravagant “fete of the camellias” to display her riches. Preparations took over a month while the marriage contract was finalized, stirring community interest. On signing day she grasped the contract’s implications and feared for her daughter’s fortune, seeking clarification of the terms.

The courtyard became a lavish tent filled with camellias, creating an opulent atmosphere. Guests arrived excited, but the contract signing felt ominous. As the entailment clause was explained, Madame Evangelista recognized the financial risk if Paul died first, realizing her prior maneuvers had jeopardized Natalie’s future.

When the contract was finalized, she confronted the notaries in frustration. Solonet suggested postponing the signing, but she believed delay would worsen their reputation. She ultimately decided to proceed with the marriage, vowing to protect her daughter’s interests and planning to invest wisely for Natalie's security.

The signing marked a turning point: Madame Evangelista resolved to navigate her new reality. As festivities began she felt a mix of triumph and resentment, aware that her daughter’s happiness was tangled with her ambitions and fears. The night unfolded with celebration, but underlying tensions hinted at future conflict.

Meanwhile Paul harbored doubts, particularly about Madame Evangelista. He reassured himself about financial arrangements but remained cautious, mindful of Mathias’s warnings. Unaware, Madame Evangelista contemplated withdrawing her influence, struggling with her complex Creole identity.

The next day Madame Evangelista and Natalie reminisced about their harmonious life. She lamented Natalie’s marriage, warning that love could fade if not managed carefully, and advised her daughter to establish authority as a wife while maintaining allure through social engagements.

As the wedding approached, Madame Evangelista proposed selling her jewelry to support Natalie; Paul accepted, oblivious to deeper tensions. Determined to step back, she believed her absence would let Natalie and Paul bond. Paul felt relieved, sensing newfound freedom.

Natalie, anxious about life without her mother, resolved to be a good wife. The ceremony took place at midnight, an hour often associated with ominous omens. Afterward Madame Evangelista feigned despair to extract promises from Paul, ensuring his commitment to Natalie. As they departed for Paris, Natalie signaled to her mother that Paul had won the initial battle, but she was beginning to assert influence.

Five years later Comte Paul de Manerville visited his old friend Monsieur Mathias in Bordeaux. Mathias noticed Paul’s changed demeanor and lamented his financial troubles. Paul revealed he had left Natalie without her knowledge and planned to sail to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune. He confessed massive debts—over a million and a half francs—from lavish spending.

Mathias suggested Paul could live off Natalie's income while recovering, but Paul refused, wanting to earn his fortune independently. The next day Mathias received a bill of exchange for 150,000 francs from de Marsay, a friend of Paul’s. Despite concerns, Mathias accepted Paul’s determination to go to India. At the quay they faced gossip about Paul’s downfall. As Paul prepared to board, Mathias signaled him with important letters, but Paul pocketed them unread.

At sea, Paul reflected on his choices and, seized by seasickness, spent days in his cabin before opening the letters. Natalie's letter expressed anguish over his departure and her desire to support him, revealing she might be pregnant. De Marsay’s letter outlined Paul’s financial ruin and warned of a conspiracy against him, urging him to return to Paris. Confronted with these harsh realities, Paul felt anger and despair but resolved to face his challenges and restore his honor.
