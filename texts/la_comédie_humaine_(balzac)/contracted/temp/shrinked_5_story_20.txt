Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman gentleman, married a wealthy heiress from Bordeaux and became a Gascon, drawn by the allure of her estate, the chateau de Lanstrac. He served as major of the Gate Guards until his death in 1813, having avoided the Revolution by going to Martinique in 1790. On his return he found Lanstrac well managed by Mathias, a notary’s clerk who embraced new ideas. After Madame de Manerville's death in 1810, the old man grew miserly, depriving his only son, Paul, of resources.

Paul de Manerville returned from college in 1810 and endured three years of strict paternal control that stifled his character. Physically courageous, he lacked the will to oppose his father and thus lost moral courage. Repressed feelings festered; he became timid in society, trapped by the old mansion and unable to engage with peers. His father took him to royalist gatherings where Paul felt out of place among the remnants of nobility, bored and unaware of potential social connections.

To cope with monotony, Paul practiced activities his father deemed essential for a gentleman—fencing, horseback riding—and spent his free time reading novels. His father’s death liberated him from routine, leaving him a substantial inheritance and well-maintained estates. Yet he harbored a deep aversion to Bordeaux and Lanstrac, where he had spent summers hunting.

Eager for a new life, Paul invested in consols and travelled across Europe, serving at various embassies. Years of indulgence and poor management eroded much of his fortune; he then decided to return to Bordeaux to manage his affairs and settle down as a country gentleman. Conscious of his rank, he felt compelled to marry well, believing his foreign experience had given him the knowledge and elegance for a successful match.

Despite extravagance, Paul had harmed no woman and kept a reputation for integrity. He maintained a refined household with well-dressed servants and a fashionable bachelor establishment. One day he confided in his friend de Marsay about his desire to marry and settle. De Marsay, however, cautioned him, arguing marriage would invite ridicule and responsibility that would diminish his happiness.

De Marsay criticized marriage as mainly for those seeking financial stability or heirs. He questioned Paul’s motives, warning that marriage would not assure love or affection from children, who might resent parental strictness. He suggested marriage would confine Paul to mediocrity, stripping him of freedoms and potential that bachelorhood offered. In de Marsay’s view, parents’ authority provokes children’s hatred, and marriage often ends in compromise and anti-climax.

Paul, however, expressed a sincere desire for a close, affectionate relationship with a wife—emotional support and companionship rather than a mere social position. He rejected the idea that he would become a mere social academician, insisting he was ready to embrace family responsibilities. De Marsay remained skeptical, advising Paul to wait until older and more established before marrying, doubting a young wife would share Paul’s ideals or disdain for social expectations.

Their conversation highlighted a clash between de Marsay’s cynicism and Paul’s earnest longing for connection. De Marsay’s view reduced marriage to a trap that mortifies individuality and breeds resentment; Paul saw it as the best prospect for meaning and domestic happiness. Despite warnings, Paul remained resolute: he believed marriage could bring the fulfillment he sought, accepting the risks and challenges ahead.

Despite the impact of earlier conversations, the Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux in the winter of 1821, determined to carry out his plans. Restoring and furnishing the family mansion sustained his reputation for elegance. Reintroduced to Bordeaux's royalist society, he quickly rose to fashionable prominence, bringing Parisian manners and savoir-faire. An old marquise called him “the pink of fashion,” a label taken up by the liberal clique as satire and by royalists in earnest.

Paul de Manerville embraced the title and flourished in attention. He combined charm and wit, was respectful yet engaging with women, and balanced foppery with a genuine care for his appearance. Medium height, a healthy complexion, and graceful movements enhanced his appeal. Paul sought more to receive happiness than to bestow it; he wanted understanding and encouragement, qualities that made him well suited for marriage.

Conscious that provincial life required marriage, Paul—still young and averse to mundane pursuits—wanted the variety a household could provide. His inclination toward marriage was intensified by a secret love for the beauty of Bordeaux, Mademoiselle Evangelista.

Around the turn of the century a wealthy Spaniard, Evangelista, settled in Bordeaux and gained admittance to noble salons by fortune and connections. His wife, a Creole from an illustrious Spanish family, maintained their status with extravagant tastes, indulging whims without heed to expense, supported by her devoted husband. After Evangelista's death in 1813, Madame Evangelista, now a widow with a fortune and a beautiful daughter, continued to live lavishly even as her wealth diminished.

By the time Paul returned, Natalie was a striking young woman, yet no proposals had been made—largely because her mother's extravagance deterred suitors. The rumor spread that only a prince could afford to marry her, a notion fomented by jealous mothers and rival young women in Bordeaux society. Natalie, accustomed to luxury and adoration, remained blissfully unaware of life beyond her privilege.

Six months after Paul’s arrival they met at a high-society event and recognized each other's charm. Madame Evangelista perceived his interest and hoped he would become her son-in-law; Paul imagined Natalie as his future wife. He had known of the Evangelista fortune since childhood, which smoothed his approach to marriage.

Warnings about the Evangelistas' extravagance were dismissed by Paul with disdain, setting the tone for his social interactions. His Parisian experience let him navigate provincial society without being deterred by gossip. Madame Evangelista began inviting him to formal dinners, and Paul found himself increasingly drawn into the prospect of marriage.

As he spent time with the Evangelistas he felt pride in their company. Society began to see him as Natalie’s suitor, and he enjoyed the attention and admiration that followed. Royalist circles flattered him, suggesting a marriage to Natalie would be prudent given her beauty and her mother's intelligence and connections.

Paul frequently visited their home and was captivated by Madame Evangelista’s beauty and charm. At forty she retained striking presence, and her reputation for virtue enhanced her allure. Their relationship had a mutual understanding: Madame viewed Paul as a means to raise her social standing in Paris.

Paul was enchanted by the mother-daughter dynamic, which blended friendship with familial affection. Yet Madame Evangelista had ambitions of her own and hoped to use Paul to further them. Paul, flattered, increased his visits.

A year after his return Paul’s attentions to Natalie led society to conclude he was courting her, though neither mother nor daughter seemed actively to pursue marriage. Natalie maintained a charming reserve that intrigued Paul; he valued her ability to engage without familiarity. This restraint allowed him to imagine a future with her and to see marriage as desirable.

Paul rationalized that marriage would bring him happiness, especially given his financial means. He believed Natalie’s upbringing in luxury would suit his lifestyle and thought their union would be advantageous. He brushed aside concerns about potential difficulties, focusing on the benefits.

Contemplating their future, Paul saw Natalie’s beauty and standing as enhancements to his own. He felt marrying her would secure his position and provide a partner who would elevate his status in society. He remained unaware of the complexities ahead, idealizing the relationship without grasping the challenges of marriage.

Natalie, sheltered and naive, was attracted to Paul’s charm but lacked experience of married life. Paul, though enamored, was cautious about possible pitfalls and whether he had the strength to manage a relationship with a woman who might not fully appreciate him.

The narrative then widens to the broader theme of marriage in French society, notably the negotiations over marriage contracts. Often ignored in literature, these contracts play a crucial role in shaping couples’ lives by intertwining personal desires with societal expectations. The impending discussions about the marriage contract would ultimately influence Paul and Natalie’s future, foreshadowing the complexities to come.

At the beginning of winter 1822, Paul de Manerville formally requested the hand of Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista through his great-aunt, the Baronne de Maulincour. The baroness lingered in Medoc to assist Paul. After discussing the dowry with Madame Evangelista, she told Paul the marriage was likely: Natalie’s dowry was her patrimony, and her mother was contributing nothing of her own. The baroness advised Paul to consult their notary, Maitre Mathias, to ensure the marriage contract properly protected his interests.

Anxious and uncertain, Paul visited Madame Evangelista to discuss the contract. She suggested both families’ notaries meet to negotiate terms. Privately, Madame Evangelista fretted over her finances: she owed her daughter a significant sum from her late husband’s estate and could not pay. Trapped by this debt, she feared that if Paul withdrew his proposal, Natalie’s prospects would be ruined.

The next day Madame Evangelista summoned her notary, Maitre Solonet, and revealed her troubles. Solonet assured her they could manage the situation, recommending a contract that favored their interests while concealing the financial difficulties. He was confident they could negotiate without exposing her debt.

That evening both notaries met. Maitre Mathias, representing Paul, was cautious and focused on protecting his client. Solonet was more modern and opportunistic. They embodied different systems: Mathias the traditional, Solonet the newer, more flexible approach.

During dinner Paul and Natalie shared tender moments while the notaries considered the marriage’s financial implications. Mathias warned of the imbalance: Natalie’s family had squandered wealth, and marrying into such a household risked financial ruin for Paul.

Madame Evangelista and Solonet plotted to secure favorable terms. They proposed she sell her house and use the proceeds to pay debts, improving Natalie’s dowry. Mathias doubted this plan, fearing it would expose Paul to vulnerability.

Tension rose as the notaries debated contract terms. Mathias insisted on safeguards for Paul; Solonet pushed for advantages for Madame Evangelista. Their discussion grew heated as both argued the marriage’s financial consequences.

Ultimately, they reached a compromise. Madame Evangelista agreed to create an entail on her property to secure her daughter’s future while protecting Paul’s interests. This arrangement allowed the marriage to proceed without immediate threat to either party’s financial stability.

As the evening ended, both families felt relief. Paul was optimistic about his future with Natalie, and Madame Evangelista was pleased with the negotiation’s outcome. Yet beneath the relief, tensions remained and the potential for conflict lingered as they prepared for the marriage.

The first day of negotiations revealed the complexities of love, ambition, and money, setting the stage for the challenges ahead.

The following day Elie Magus, then in Bordeaux, answered Madame Evangelista’s summons, thinking it concerned jewels for the bride, Comte Paul’s fiancée Mademoiselle Natalie. Instead he was asked to appraise the mother-in-law’s property, including her diamonds, for the marriage contract. Aware of differences between Asian and Brazilian diamonds, Magus valued Madame Evangelista’s necklace and earrings at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” at 100,000 francs. When she asked about selling them, he explained the marked loss she would incur.

That conversation sparked staff rumors and speculation about the marriage’s stability. Society became absorbed in the drama: gossip claimed Madame Evangelista was selling her house and that the marriage was in jeopardy. Notaries denied the claims, but public opinion stayed skeptical, fed by envy of the Evangelistas’ wealth and rank.

Paul organizing a hunting party only intensified the gossip. Madame de Gyas, keen to confirm the story, visited Natalie and her mother under a pretense of concern and passed on the circulating tales. The two women dismissed the gossip, recognizing its motives—especially the desperation of mothers pushing their daughters into advantageous matches.

Preparing for the wedding, Madame Evangelista planned a grand fête to display her wealth and counter the rumors. The fete of the camellias required lavish decorations and the finalization of the marriage contract. As the day approached she had a moment of doubt about the contract’s implications, suspecting that her daughter’s fortune might be at risk.

On the day of signing the atmosphere was festive; guests arrived for the celebration. Natalie, radiant in bridal attire, and Paul, equally enamored, paid little heed to the legal discussions. Madame Evangelista concentrated on the contract, which spelled out financial arrangements and entailment of property.

When the notaries explained the entail, Madame Evangelista grasped that her daughter’s financial security was compromised. They clarified that if Paul died first, Natalie would inherit only a fraction of her expected fortune. The revelation struck her deeply; she felt her daughter was being ruined.

Although she had once intended to manipulate the arrangement, Madame Evangelista found herself constrained by the public nature of the wedding preparations and the expectations they created. She resolved to ensure her daughter’s happiness, even if it meant sacrificing some of her own interests.

As the contract was finalized, Madame Evangelista’s emotions surged. She vowed to protect her daughter’s future, even while nursing resentment toward Paul. The signing marked a turning point, cementing the marriage and the financial ties that bound the families.

After the festivities, Madame Evangelista reflected on a new determination to reclaim her daughter’s fortune. She resolved to invest wisely and regain control of her finances, driven by a desire for vengeance against Paul. This shift transformed her from a careless spender into a shrewd strategist, setting the stage for future conflicts as she plotted to influence Paul’s life and secure Natalie’s interests.

CHAPTER V. THE MARRIAGE CONTRACT--THIRD DAY

The excitement of the fête had temporarily distracted Paul from his anxious thoughts, but they returned to haunt him at night. He wondered whether his mother-in-law, Madame Evangelista, might have deceived him without Mathias's intervention. Despite his worries, he reassured himself that in two days Natalie would be his wife and their financial arrangements were clear. Yet he resolved to remain vigilant, questioning whether Mathias's concerns were valid.

Unbeknownst to Paul, his future had shifted. The cleverest of the two women he was marrying had become his enemy, contemplating withdrawing her interests from their shared life. Paul failed to recognize the cunning of his Creole mother-in-law, who combined European intellect, tropical passion, and Eastern indifference. Dormant during her happy marriage, this nature was now ignited by the impending loss of her daughter. With only one day left before Natalie’s marriage, Madame Evangelista saw she could influence the couple’s life: Natalie trusted her implicitly, and a single conversation could alter their future, revealing the vulnerability of social laws to the power of words.

The next morning Madame Evangelista sat on the edge of Natalie’s bed, reminiscing about their harmonious life together and lamenting the thought of Natalie belonging to a man she must obey. Natalie dismissed obedience, believing love would prevail. Madame Evangelista cautioned that conjugal love could quickly fade if not nurtured, stressing the need for Natalie to establish authority from the start.

She warned that her presence could complicate Natalie’s new role and suggested she should leave to avoid conflict. She advised Natalie to appear to obey Paul while subtly establishing dominance. Their conversation revealed the complexities of marital dynamics: Madame Evangelista insisted on maintaining a social life to keep Paul interested and engaged.

Feeling overwhelmed, Natalie sought guidance on navigating her new life. Madame Evangelista explained that success in marriage lay in balancing affection and independence. She warned against constant proximity, which breeds indifference, and urged Natalie to cultivate her allure and keep Paul entertained so their relationship would remain vibrant.

As they discussed marital intricacies, Natalie confessed fears about being alone with Paul. Madame Evangelista reassured her they would stay in touch and that she would visit regularly, but insisted on the necessity of separation for Natalie’s happiness and future.

When Paul arrived, he tried to mask unease, which Madame Evangelista noticed. She proposed selling her jewelry to support the couple, emphasizing Natalie’s happiness and vanity. Paul accepted, moved by his future mother-in-law’s sacrifice.

Solonet, the notary, arrived with news of potential buyers for Madame Evangelista’s property, which could provide a significant financial boost. Natalie agreed to the sale, recognizing the need to manage their finances wisely.

Determined to ensure her daughter’s happiness, Madame Evangelista decided to step back from their lives in Paris, believing her presence might hinder the marriage. She expressed a desire to support them from a distance, emphasizing the importance of allowing Natalie and Paul to establish their own dynamic.

As the wedding day approached, the couple prepared for their midnight ceremony, which carried an air of foreboding. Guests speculated about Madame Evangelista’s absence and its implications. Despite the somber atmosphere, Natalie felt the weight of her mother’s advice and the uncertainty of her new life.

After the ceremony, Madame Evangelista assumed the role of a grieving mother, extracting promises from Paul that ensured her ongoing influence. She sent the newlyweds off to Paris, satisfied that her plans were unfolding as intended. Natalie, now alone with Paul, began to assert her authority, setting the stage for the complex dynamics of their marriage.

CHAPTER VI. CONCLUSION

Five years later, in November, Comte Paul de Manerville, cloaked and somber, arrived at the home of his old friend Monsieur Mathias in Bordeaux. The retired notary, now too old for business, had sold his practice and lived quietly. Mathias was absent when Paul came; his housekeeper led him to the room of the late Madame Mathias, who had died a year before. Exhausted from travel, Paul slept until evening. When Mathias returned, he found Paul asleep and, noting it was the anniversary of his wife's death, expressed concern for him.

Mathias lamented the public notices of Paul's financial troubles, feeling personal shame at seeing the seizure announcements. He reflected that since Paul's marriage to Natalie Evangelista, things had gone badly, and he blamed that union for Paul's ruin. Despite his own difficulties, Mathias was relieved to have saved the titles to Paul's estates, though the usufruct had been lost.

When Paul awoke, he appeared calm despite his altered look. They shared a meal, and Mathias voiced sorrow over the seizure of Paul's properties. Paul revealed he had left Natalie without her knowledge and was sailing to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune. Mathias was shocked that Paul, a count, would enter commerce, but Paul insisted he was no longer a count and was determined to succeed under another name.

Paul outlined his financial situation, admitting massive debts incurred through lavish spending and gifts to Natalie. Mathias was incredulous at the scale of the liabilities, which included large sums owed to usurers. Paul acknowledged he had been forced to sell assets and take loans to maintain their lifestyle, culminating in the current catastrophe.

Despite Mathias's concerns, Paul was resolute about seeking fortune abroad. He believed he could return wealthy by forty. Mathias doubted the feasibility of the plan and the reliability of de Marsay, who was supposed to send money.

The next day Mathias received a bill of exchange from de Marsay, confirming that funds would arrive. Paul, however, remained determined to leave for India, convinced it was his only chance to recover. As he prepared to depart, Mathias lamented losing his friend, and Paul boarded the ship, waving goodbye.

Onboard, Paul was consumed by thoughts of his past and his love for Natalie. He felt a mix of regret and determination as he faced an uncertain future. Overcome by seasickness, he spent days in his cabin, reflecting on his choices and on the affection binding him to his wife.

Once better, Paul opened letters from Natalie and de Marsay. Natalie's letter revealed anguish at his departure and resolve to support him financially—she pledged her jewels to pay his debts and vowed to live at Lanstrac with her mother and economize to help him. She expressed love and commitment.

Paul was moved by her words, torn between love and despair. He recognized the depth of their bond yet was haunted by doubts about their future. De Marsay's letter laid out the extent of Paul's ruin and the machinations of his mother-in-law, who had conspired against him. It painted a grim picture: Paul's wife and her mother profited from his downfall.

As Paul processed betrayal and reality, he felt anger and helplessness. He realized he had been naive in trust and love; the weight of his decisions pressed on him. In the end, Paul was left to grapple with the consequences of his choices, the love he had for Natalie, and the uncertain path ahead as he sailed away from France, contemplating what the future might bring.
