**EUGENIE GRANDET**

**I**

In certain provincial towns, some houses evoke a profound melancholy, reminiscent of cloisters or ruins. One such house in Saumur, at the end of a steep street leading to the chateau, embodies this sadness. The street, now seldom traveled, is characterized by its narrowness and the silence of its ancient homes, which are over three centuries old. These houses, with their carved oaken beams and worn window-sills, tell the history of France through their architecture.

Next to a dilapidated artisan's home stands the mansion of Monsieur Grandet, a country gentleman whose door bears the remnants of armorial bearings. The ground-floors of merchants in this area are not shops but dark, deep rooms with no glass, where goods are displayed in a simple manner. The inhabitants of Saumur, deeply connected to the land, are acutely aware of the weather's impact on their livelihoods, with conversations revolving around the state of the harvest and the market.

On Saturdays, merchants leave for their vineyards, leaving the streets empty. Life unfolds outdoors, with neighbors observing each other closely. The old town of Saumur, once home to the local nobility, houses the Grandet family, whose story is central to this narrative.

Monsieur Grandet, known as le Pere Grandet, was a master cooper who became wealthy during the Revolution by purchasing church properties. With his wife’s dowry, he acquired prime vineyards and farms, becoming a respected figure in Saumur. His wealth grew through shrewd investments and a keen understanding of the wine trade, making him a significant player in the local economy.

By 1816, Grandet's estate was estimated at nearly four million francs, with many believing he had hidden treasures. His reputation was built on his financial acumen and his ability to manipulate the market, earning him both admiration and fear. He lived frugally, relying on his land for sustenance and employing only a few trusted individuals.

Grandet's demeanor was simple and unassuming, yet he commanded respect. He rarely spoke, preferring to communicate through short, decisive phrases. His stutter, often mistaken for a lack of education, was a calculated facade. He was a man of routine, dressed consistently in practical clothing, and his life revolved around his daughter, Eugenie, whom he intended to marry off advantageously.

The social dynamics of Saumur were influenced by the competition for Eugenie's hand in marriage. Two families, the Cruchots and the des Grassins, vied for her favor, each believing they could secure a more advantageous match. The Cruchots, led by the ambitious C. de Bonfons, sought to align with the Grandets through marriage, while the des Grassins, represented by the charming Adolphe, aimed to win Eugenie’s heart.

As the story unfolds, the tension between these families escalates, with the stakes raised by the potential inheritance of Eugenie’s fortune. The arrival of the estate of Froidfond for sale creates further intrigue, as both factions see it as a means to elevate their status and secure Eugenie’s future.

Monsieur Grandet, seizing the opportunity, purchases the estate, further solidifying his wealth and influence. This acquisition sends ripples through Saumur, as the townspeople speculate on the implications for the Grandet family and the ongoing competition for Eugenie’s hand.

In this world of ambition, avarice, and social maneuvering, the fate of Eugenie Grandet hangs in the balance, reflecting the complexities of love, wealth, and familial duty in a changing society.

**Summary:**

The house of Monsieur Grandet is a cold, silent structure made of tufa stone, resembling a jail with its weathered archway and crumbling bas-relief of the four seasons. The entrance features a solid oak door with a rusty grating, leading to a dark vault and a damp garden enclosed by thick walls. The main room, a large hall, serves multiple purposes—an antechamber, salon, office, and dining room—where local townsfolk conduct business with Grandet. The hall is adorned with gray wooden panels, an old brass clock, and faded tapestries, reflecting the family's modest lifestyle.

Madame Grandet and their daughter, Eugenie, live under strict conditions, with Grandet controlling their resources tightly. They manage household tasks diligently, often working late into the night. La Grande Nanon, their servant, has been with the family for thirty-five years, earning a meager wage but saving enough to secure her future. Despite her rough appearance, she is devoted to Grandet, who treats her with a mix of disdain and grudging affection.

As the story unfolds, it is November 1819, and the family prepares to celebrate Eugenie's birthday. Grandet presents her with a gold coin, while Madame Grandet gives her a new dress. The family dinner is interrupted by the arrival of local notables, including the Cruchots and the des Grassins, who bring gifts for Eugenie. The des Grassins' son, Adolphe, offers her a workbox, which delights her, while Grandet remains focused on his wealth and the potential marriage prospects for Eugenie.

The evening progresses with card games, revealing the underlying tensions and ambitions of the guests, all vying for Grandet's favor and wealth. The atmosphere is a mix of familial warmth and the cold calculations of social status and financial gain. Grandet's miserly nature dominates the scene, overshadowing genuine emotions, as the women in the household remain blissfully unaware of the true extent of his wealth and the machinations surrounding them.

As the night continues, the sound of a loud knock at the door interrupts the festivities, signaling an unexpected visitor and heightening the tension in the household.

Nanon lit a candle and opened the door, followed by her master, Monsieur Grandet. His wife, alarmed, called out to him, while the players exchanged worried glances. Monsieur des Grassins suggested they all go, sensing something ominous. When a young man arrived with a porter carrying trunks, Grandet brusquely told his wife to return to the game, leaving the players uneasy.

The newcomer was Charles Grandet, the son of Grandet from Paris. He was handsome and dressed in the latest fashion, which contrasted sharply with the provincial attire of the others. As he entered, he greeted the company with grace, but Grandet's stern demeanor overshadowed the warm welcome. Madame Grandet offered him hospitality, but Grandet interrupted, insisting he needed no refreshment.

Charles, having been sent to Saumur for the first time, aimed to impress with his Parisian sophistication. He brought an extravagant collection of clothing and accessories, intending to showcase his status. However, he was unaware of the modesty of his uncle's home and the provincial lifestyle that awaited him.

The provincial guests, including the Cruchots and des Grassins, were taken aback by Charles's elegance. They scrutinized him with a mix of admiration and disdain, feeling both intrigued and threatened by his presence. Eugenie Grandet, Charles's cousin, was particularly captivated by him, feeling a longing for the refinement he represented, contrasting sharply with her own mundane life.

As the game of loto concluded, Nanon returned to announce preparations for Charles's room. Eugenie, eager to impress her cousin, rushed to help with the arrangements, suggesting improvements to make his stay more comfortable. She even proposed buying a wax candle, a luxury her father would typically deny.

Meanwhile, Madame des Grassins attempted to charm Charles, hinting at social gatherings that could alleviate his boredom in Saumur. She subtly criticized Grandet's miserly nature and suggested that Charles would find more enjoyment outside his uncle's home. The conversation hinted at potential romantic interests, with Madame des Grassins positioning herself as a matchmaker.

As the evening progressed, Grandet received a letter revealing a family tragedy: his brother was bankrupt and dying, leaving his son Charles in a precarious situation. The letter implored Grandet to care for Charles, emphasizing the urgency of the request. Grandet's reaction was one of mixed emotions, as he concealed his thoughts while contemplating the implications of his brother's demise.

After the guests departed, Grandet's demeanor shifted, revealing a man grappling with familial duty and the weight of financial responsibility. The evening had set the stage for a complex interplay of relationships, ambitions, and the looming specter of wealth and poverty that would shape the lives of all involved. 

In summary, the arrival of Charles Grandet in Saumur disrupts the provincial status quo, igniting curiosity and ambition among the local elite, while also unveiling the burdens of family legacy and the expectations of wealth.

**Summary of the Text**

Monsieur Grandet, a miserly man, instructs his nephew Charles about the household routine upon his arrival. He emphasizes his hard work and lack of wealth, despite rumors of his riches. Charles, feeling out of place in his uncle's shabby home, is shown to his room by Grandet, who warns him about the ferocious dog. Charles is disheartened by the dilapidated state of the house and questions why his father sent him there.

As Charles settles in, he reflects on his situation, wondering about the purpose of his visit. Meanwhile, Eugenie, Grandet's daughter, experiences a newfound awareness of her feelings as she prepares for the day. She admires her appearance and feels a sense of hope and excitement, particularly regarding her cousin Charles. However, she also struggles with self-doubt, believing she is not beautiful enough for him.

Eugenie's morning is filled with thoughts of Charles, and she seeks to impress him by asking Nanon, the servant, to prepare a special breakfast. Grandet, however, is stingy with resources and engages in a comical argument with Nanon over provisions. Despite his frugality, he begrudgingly allows Nanon to make a galette for the young ones.

As the day progresses, Grandet takes Eugenie for a walk, during which they encounter Cruchot, the notary. Their conversation reveals Grandet's disdain for the idea of marrying Eugenie to Charles, stating he would rather throw her into the Loire than allow such a union. This declaration devastates Eugenie, who had begun to develop feelings for Charles.

Later, Grandet receives news of his brother's suicide, which shocks the family. Eugenie is deeply affected by the news, feeling compassion for Charles, who remains unaware of his father's fate. Grandet insists that Eugenie should not inform Charles until he returns, revealing his cold-heartedness.

Eugenie, overwhelmed by her emotions, confides in her mother, who understands her daughter's feelings but urges her to respect her father's wishes. Despite the tension, Eugenie expresses her affection for Charles and desires to prepare a special breakfast for him, showcasing her growing attachment.

As the narrative unfolds, the contrast between Grandet's miserly nature and the warmth of Eugenie's budding love for Charles becomes evident. The story captures the complexities of familial relationships, societal expectations, and the innocence of first love against a backdrop of financial hardship and emotional turmoil.

After two hours of anxious preparation, Eugenie managed to create an unusual breakfast for her cousin Charles, which deviated from their family's customary standing meal of bread, fruit, and wine. She set the table with care, fearing her father's reaction if he returned to find such a spread. Madame Grandet reassured her, promising to take the blame if necessary. When Charles finally arrived, he was dressed flamboyantly and greeted them cheerfully, despite the recent destruction of his family estate.

As they sat down to eat, Charles expressed his hunger and desire for more extravagant food, which made Eugenie wish she could provide him with a partridge. Their conversation revealed the stark contrast between Charles's Parisian lifestyle and Eugenie's simple country life. Charles complimented Eugenie, making her blush, and they shared a moment of connection. However, the atmosphere shifted when Nanon brought in coffee, prompting Charles to comment on their outdated brewing method.

The mood darkened as they heard the familiar knock of Monsieur Grandet, Eugenie's father. The women panicked, quickly hiding evidence of the breakfast. Grandet entered, scrutinizing the scene and immediately sensing something was amiss. He chastised them for the lavish meal, unaware of its significance to Charles. As he prepared his own meager breakfast, he noticed the sugar and questioned its source, leading to a tense moment of silence among the women.

Charles, oblivious to the underlying tension, asked about his father's health, only to be met with the devastating news of his father's death by suicide. The revelation shattered him, and he wept openly, prompting sympathy from Eugenie and her mother. Grandet, however, remained detached, focusing on the financial implications of Charles's loss rather than the emotional devastation.

Eugenie, moved by Charles's grief, felt a growing affection for him. She and her mother decided to comfort him, but Charles was consumed by sorrow, lamenting his father's struggles. Eugenie encouraged him to think of his honor, trying to redirect his focus amidst his despair. The two women returned to their sewing, reflecting on the gravity of the situation and the stark contrast between their quiet lives and the turmoil surrounding them.

As they sewed in silence, Eugenie contemplated wearing mourning for her uncle, indicating her deepening connection to Charles and the shared grief that now bound them together.

At around four o'clock, a sharp knock at the door startled Madame Grandet. She worried about her husband, but Grandet entered joyfully, revealing that he had sold his wine to a Belgian for a good price. He gleefully shared that he had made a significant profit, but when his daughter Eugenie suggested helping her cousin Charles, who was grieving for his father, Grandet's mood soured. He scolded Eugenie for even thinking of spending money on Charles, threatening to send her away if she mentioned it again.

Grandet, consumed by thoughts of investments and profits, retreated to his secret den, calculating his wealth, which had grown significantly. Meanwhile, Charles was mourning in his room, refusing to eat. Grandet dismissed the idea of mourning attire, insisting that true mourning was internal, not external. The family dinner was tense and silent, with Madame Grandet suggesting they should wear mourning, but Grandet scoffed at the expense.

Eugenie, feeling a surge of compassion for Charles, secretly visited him, offering comfort. She admired his handsome face, now marked by grief, and felt a deep connection to him. This encounter stirred feelings within her, awakening a desire for love and connection that she had never experienced before. 

The next morning, Grandet's demeanor shifted; he seemed more thoughtful and less harsh. He planned to manipulate the situation to his advantage, intending to help Charles while maintaining his own financial interests. He devised a scheme to impress the townspeople without spending his own money, using his brother's failure as a means to gain respect.

Eugenie busied herself with caring for Charles, bringing him breakfast and attending to his needs. Their bond deepened as they shared moments of tenderness amidst their respective sorrows. Charles, feeling grateful for their kindness, began to see Eugenie in a new light, appreciating her innocence and compassion.

As the day progressed, Grandet prepared to host a dinner for the Cruchots, signaling a rare moment of generosity. The household buzzed with activity as they set the table, and Charles, though still grieving, joined them, his presence marked by a quiet dignity. The atmosphere was charged with unspoken emotions, as Eugenie and Charles exchanged glances filled with understanding and hope, bridging the gap between their worlds through shared suffering and budding affection.

At this moment, the town of Saumur was more intrigued by Grandet's dinner for the Cruchots than by the previous night's controversial wine sale. Grandet, a shrewd miser, paid little attention to public opinion, believing himself superior to the community he often deceived.

The des Grassins family soon learned of Guillaume Grandet's death and decided to visit Grandet that evening to express their condolences and to understand his motives for inviting the Cruchots. At dinner, the atmosphere was somber, with Grandet grave, Charles silent, and Eugenie and Madame Grandet saying little. After dinner, Charles excused himself to write letters, allowing Grandet to speak privately with the Cruchots.

Grandet, known for his cunning, employed his stutter and feigned deafness to manipulate the conversation, avoiding responsibility for his actions. He engaged in a discussion about bankruptcy with Monsieur C. de Bonfons, who attempted to guide him through the complexities of insolvency and liquidation. Grandet, while pretending to be confused, was actually assessing how to navigate his brother's financial troubles without tarnishing his own reputation.

The Cruchots suggested that Grandet could liquidate his brother's affairs to preserve his family's honor. Grandet, however, expressed his reluctance to engage in matters outside his expertise, insisting he was a simple wine-grower focused on his vines. The conversation turned to the possibility of buying up his brother's debts at a reduced rate, which Grandet found intriguing but complicated.

As the discussion progressed, the notary, concerned about the legality of the proposed actions, interrupted to caution against compromising their integrity. Just then, the des Grassins arrived, expressing their condolences for the Grandet family's misfortunes. The notary seized the opportunity to assert that Grandet intended to liquidate his brother's debts honorably, surprising the des Grassins, who had expected Grandet to act selfishly.

The des Grassins, impressed by Grandet's supposed nobility, offered to help manage the financial situation, but Grandet preferred to work with them rather than the Cruchots. He confided in the banker about his interest in investing in government bonds, indicating his desire to profit from the situation while maintaining a façade of familial duty.

After the des Grassins left, Grandet instructed his servant Nanon to prepare for a secretive journey. He planned to sell his gold in Angers, taking advantage of rising prices due to military preparations. Grandet's meticulousness ensured that no one in the neighborhood suspected his true intentions as he departed under the cover of night.

In summary, Grandet's dinner served as a backdrop for his cunning manipulation of those around him, as he navigated familial obligations and financial opportunities while maintaining his reputation as a respectable member of the community. His actions reflected a complex interplay of greed, deception, and the desire to uphold a facade of honor amidst personal gain.

Her heart sank as she spotted a letter addressed to "My dear Annette," igniting feelings of jealousy and despair.

Eugenie then found another letter addressed to Alphonse, where Charles detailed his financial ruin and requested assistance in settling his affairs. Moved by his situation, Eugenie decided to offer him her savings, which she had carefully accumulated. She retrieved a purse filled with gold coins, worth nearly six thousand francs, and approached Charles, who was initially hesitant to accept her gift.

Eugenie insisted, kneeling before him and imploring him to take the money. Overcome with emotion, Charles eventually agreed, recognizing the depth of their bond. He then revealed a precious family heirloom, a dressing case containing portraits of his parents, asking her to keep it safe. As they shared this intimate moment, both felt a shift in their relationship. Charles, despite his misfortunes, began to see hope in their bond, while Eugenie vowed to support him. Eugenie's father, Grandet, returned home with news of his financial dealings, having profited from the sale of gold.
