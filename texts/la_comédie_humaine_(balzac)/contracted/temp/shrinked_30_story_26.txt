Bianchon, a notable Parisian physician, trained under the troubled Desplein, who grappled with his legacy and embraced atheism, feeling isolated in his beliefs. Desplein, who denied the existence of a soul, faced mixed judgments about his eccentricity.

Bianchon, who experienced poverty during his medical studies, was known for his integrity. Desplein recognized his potential and mentored him in medical practice and Parisian society. Bianchon was intrigued when he saw Desplein enter a church, given his atheism. At dinner, Desplein dismissed the significance of mass, criticizing its historical implications.

When questioned about his church visit, Desplein offered a vague excuse. Bianchon later discovered that Desplein had founded the service he attended, adding to the mystery of his participation in rituals contrary to his beliefs.

After the 1830 Revolution, Bianchon joined Desplein at Saint-Sulpice for mass, seeking an explanation. Desplein recounted his early years of extreme poverty, living in a dilapidated building and struggling to finance his education. Despite these hardships, he was determined to succeed, feeling misunderstood by those who had never faced true poverty. His neighbor Bourgeat, a humble water-carrier, supported Desplein's education, and their bond deepened. Desplein cared for Bourgeat until his death, honoring his faith by arranging masses for his soul.
