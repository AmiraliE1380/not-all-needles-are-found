Trained by Desplein, he became a physiologist. Desplein’s skill and atheism—hardened by dissections—proved paradoxical; critics noted eccentricity and scorn. When Bianchon mentioned a destitute water‑carrier, Desplein helped; Bianchon later learned he attended Mass, complicating the portrait.

Some time passed before Dr. Bianchon, despite his friendship with Desplein, found a chance to ask about a pivotal episode in the surgeon’s life. Their meetings rarely allowed private talk, but seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, Bianchon saw Desplein entering Saint-Sulpice; they attended mass together.

“Can you explain your sudden piety?” Bianchon asked as they left. Desplein first deflected, observing that many display outward religion while doubting inwardly, and criticized politicians; but pressed about why he had founded this mass, he answered plainly: he was nearing the end of his life and could trace its beginnings. Standing in the Rue des Quatre‑Vents, he pointed to the house where he had spent two desperate years and recalled hunger, poverty, isolation, and an irritable temper that kept friends away. With no money for books or medical training, he had struggled alone from destitution.

He spoke of meager meals, tireless labor, and a lack of sympathy; those born to wealth could not imagine his plight and treated debt as moral failing. Early hardships shaped his view of humanity: he had seen selfishness and envy, and expected mediocrity to try to pull down those who strove for excellence.

A turning point came at the depth of his poverty, when he contemplated enlisting. He had hoped for a box of linen from a relative, but when the porter demanded too much to release it, despair set in. Then his neighbor Bourgeat—a humble water‑carrier—appeared and changed his fate.

Though poor himself, Bourgeat paid Desplein’s debts and secured new lodgings. He devoted his savings to Desplein’s future, understood his mission, and supported him selflessly. Desplein described Bourgeat as nurturing, almost maternal, proud of his protégé yet seeking no recognition. When Desplein became a house surgeon, leaving Bourgeat pained him; Bourgeat remained proud nevertheless.

Later Desplein repaid him by buying him a horse and a barrel—fulfilling a long‑held dream. Bourgeat was at once angry that Desplein had spent his money on him and delighted; he never claimed credit for Desplein’s rise, though Desplein felt he owed him everything.

When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein tended him devotedly; Bourgeat died in his arms, leaving him all he had. A man of strong faith, Bourgeat’s wishes were honored with masses after his death. Desplein explained that the mass he founded was a tribute to Bourgeat’s faith; he hoped, if there is a paradise, that Bourgeat had found it. He admitted he would give anything for Bourgeat’s conviction.

Bianchon, who had witnessed Desplein’s last days, could not say whether the great surgeon died an atheist. He wondered whether Bourgeat might have opened Heaven’s gates for Desplein just as he had opened the doors to the earthly temple they had shared.

PARIS, January 1836.

ADDENDUM: Bianchon and Desplein appear across Balzac’s Human Comedy—Bianchon in Father Goriot, Lost Illusions, Cousin Betty, and others; Desplein in Cousin Pons, Lost Illusions, The Thirteen, Pierrette, A Bachelor’s Establishment, Modeste Mignon, and related pieces.
