Desplein trained Horace Bianchon as a physiologist. He paired surgical brilliance with atheism hardened by corpse dissection and drew criticism for odd dress, moodiness, contempt. Bianchon earned his mentorship; Desplein’s care for a destitute water‑carrier revealed unexpected compassion.

Some time passed before Dr. Bianchon, despite their friendship, found an opening to ask Desplein about a pivotal episode. Their meetings rarely allowed private talk, but seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, Bianchon saw Desplein entering Saint‑Sulpice and knelt beside him; they attended mass together.

“Can you explain your sudden piety?” Bianchon asked as they left. Desplein at first deflected, noting outward devotion that masks skepticism and criticizing politicians; pressed, he replied plainly: nearing the end of his life, he could trace its beginnings. Standing in the Rue des Quatre‑Vents, he pointed to the house where he had lived two desperate years and recalled hunger, poverty, isolation, and an irritable temper that made friendship difficult. He had no money for books or medical training and struggled alone to rise from destitution.

He spoke of meager meals and tireless labor without sympathy or support. The privileged could not imagine his plight and treated debt as a choice. Early hardships made him expect selfishness and envy, and to see mediocrity trying to pull down those who strove for excellence.

A turning point came when, at the depth of his poverty, he contemplated enlisting. He had hoped for a box of linen from a relative, but when the porter demanded too much to release it, despair set in. Then his neighbor Bourgeat—a humble water‑carrier—appeared and changed his fate.

Bourgeat, though poor himself, paid Desplein’s debts and secured new lodgings. He devoted his savings to Desplein’s future, understood his mission, and supported him selflessly. Desplein described Bourgeat as nurturing, almost maternal, proud of his protégé yet seeking no recognition. When Desplein became a house surgeon, leaving Bourgeat pained him; Bourgeat was proud nonetheless.

Later Desplein repaid him by buying a horse and a barrel, fulfilling a long‑held dream. Bourgeat was angry that Desplein had spent his money on him, yet delighted; he never claimed credit for Desplein’s rise, though Desplein felt he owed him everything. When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein tended him devotedly; Bourgeat died in his arms, leaving him all he had. He had strong faith, and Desplein honored his wishes by having masses said for him after his death. The mass Desplein founded was a tribute to Bourgeat’s faith; Desplein hoped, if there is a paradise, that Bourgeat had found peace there and admitted he would give anything for such faith.

Bianchon, who had witnessed Desplein’s last days, could not say whether the great surgeon died an atheist. He wondered whether Bourgeat might have opened the gates of Heaven for Desplein, just as he had opened the doors to the earthly temple they had shared.

PARIS, January 1836.
