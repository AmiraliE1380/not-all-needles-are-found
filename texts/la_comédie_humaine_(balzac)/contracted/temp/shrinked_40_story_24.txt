To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

Dear Child,—You, the joy of the household, whose pink or white pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of Wierzschovnia, followed by the tender eyes of your father and mother,—how can I dedicate to you a melancholy story? And yet sorrows should be spoken of to a young girl idolized as you are, for the day may come when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them. It is difficult, Anna, to find in our manners and morals a subject worthy of your eyes; no choice has been left me; but perhaps you will feel how fortunate your fate is when you read the story sent to you by
                                                    Your old friend,
                                                         De Balzac.

I. THE LORRAINS

At dawn one October day in 1827 a sixteen-year-old youth in humble clothes stood in a small square of Lower Provins. The river mills already whirred; the echo from the Upper Town made the place so still that even a distant diligence could be heard. Two long sides of the square, divided by an avenue of lindens, were built in a plain bourgeois style. Few trades opened onto it; fronts were shaded by vines or roses climbing to second-story windows. One end opened toward the main street and river gardens; the other led to a quieter parallel street.

At that quieter end the youth recognized the house he sought: a white stone front grooved like courses, closed gray blinds, slender iron balconies with yellow-painted rosettes. Three dormer windows pierced a slate roof; a new weather-vane of a hunter shooting a hare topped the center. Three stone steps led to the door. A leaden pipe discharged sink-water into a gutter at one side, showing the kitchen; two shuttered windows with heart-shaped openings indicated the dining-room. Vent-holes to the cellar had painted iron shutters cut in open-work. Everything looked recently repaired, suggesting the small taste and self-satisfaction of a retired petty shopkeeper.

The young man inspected these homely details with pleasure tinged by sadness; his eyes traveled from kitchen to roof. A calico curtain in one garret window caught the rosy light of the rising sun. He stepped back, leaned against a linden, and sang in the drawling tone of western France a Breton ditty often sung to newly married couples:

“We’ve come to wish you happiness in marriage,
  To m’sieur your husband
  As well as to you:
You have just been bound, madam’ la mariée,
  With bonds of gold
  That only death unbinds:
You will go no more to balls or gay assemblies;
  You must stay at home
  While we shall go.
Have you thought how you are pledged to be
  True to your spouse,
  And love him like yourself?
Receive these flowers our hands do now present you;—
  Alas! your fleeting honors
  Will fade as they.”

This tender, melancholy air would awaken a Breton maiden’s memories. As he finished the first couplet the singer, never taking his eyes from the attic curtain, saw no sign of life. While he sang the second couplet the curtain stirred. At “Receive these flowers,” a face appeared; a white hand cautiously opened the casement, and a girl made a sign with her head as he closed with the melancholy thought, “Alas! your fleeting honors will fade as they.”

To her the young workman suddenly produced a yellow flower, common in Brittany—the furze, or broom.

“Is it really you, Brigaut?” she said in a low voice.

“Yes, Pierrette, yes. I am in Paris. I have started to make my way; but I’m ready to settle here, near you.”

A first-floor window beneath Pierrette’s attic creaked. The girl showed terror and whispered: “Run away!”

The lad leaped like a frightened frog to a bend in the street where a mill projected as the square opens into the main thoroughfare; but despite his agility his hob-nailed shoes echoed on the stones, easily distinguished from the mill’s music, and no doubt heard by the person who had opened the window.

That person was an old maid. No man would have torn himself from a morning nap to listen to a minstrel in a jacket; only a maid wakes to songs of love. She opened her blinds with the furtive motion of a bat, looked about, saw nothing, and only heard the flying footfalls. In her neglected state—no false front or collarette, a little black silk bag covering a bony skull, pushed-aside night-cap—she had a harsh, angular face with deep wrinkles; the sight of her at a window was abrupt and forbidding. She owned the house with her brother, who slept so soundly that nothing disturbed him.

The old maid stretched her neck from the window, raised cold pale-blue eyes toward the attic, tried to see Pierrette, and, finding it useless, drew back like a tortoise hiding its head. The blinds were then closed, and the street resumed its silence, broken only by peasants coming in or other early risers.

When there is an old maid in a house, watch-dogs are unnecessary; not the slightest event escapes her observation or comment. This trifling incident was therefore destined to excite grave suppositions and to open the way for one of those obscure family dramas—none the less terrible because secret.

Pierrette did not go back to bed. Brigaut’s arrival was an immense event to her. During the night—Eden of the wretched—she escaped the fault-findings that weighed on her by day. Like the hero of a ballad, her sleep seemed her only happy life; waking hours were a bad dream. She had her only pleasurable waking in three years. The memories of her childhood sang their melodious ditties in her soul. The first couplet had come in a dream; the second made her spring from bed; at the third she doubted her ears; but when the fourth was sung, standing in her night-gown with bare feet at the window, she recognized Brigaut, the companion of her childhood.

Yes—there was the well-known square jacket with bobtails, the printed cotton waistcoat, the linen shirt fastened by a gold heart, the large collar, the earrings, stout shoes, and trousers of blue-gray drilling unevenly colored—the humble durable things of Breton peasantry. The big white-horn buttons that fastened the jacket made the girl’s heart beat. When she saw the bunch of broom her eyes filled with tears; then a dreadful fear drove back the budding happiness. She thought her cousin sleeping in the room below might have heard the noise she made in jumping to the window. The fear was just; the old maid had appeared, and Pierrette made Brigaut the terrified sign which he obeyed without understanding it. Such instinctive submission to a girl’s bidding reveals one of those innocent and absolute affections that blossom now and then in life. Whoever had seen the lad as he ran away would have loved the ingenuous chivalry of his feeling.

Jacques Brigaut was worthy of Pierrette Lorrain, who was just fifteen. Two children! Pierrette could not help crying as she watched his flight in the terror her gesture had conveyed. Then she sat in a shabby armchair before a small table topped by a mirror. Resting her elbows, head in hands, she thought for an hour, calling to memory the Marais, the village of Pen-Hoel, perilous pond voyages in a boat untied from an old willow by little Jacques; the faces of her grandfather and grandmother, her mother’s sufferings, Major Brigaut’s handsome face—in short her careless childhood. It was a dream, a luminous joy on the gloomy present.

Her beautiful chestnut hair escaped in disorder from her cap, rumpled in sleep—a cambric cap she had made. Little ringlets escaped from gray curl-papers at each side of her forehead. From the back hung a heavy braid half unplaited. Her whiteness betrayed the malady of girlhood, chlorosis, which deprives the body of color, destroys appetite, and disorders the organism. Waxy tones marked her flesh. The neck and shoulders betrayed wasted arms, flung forward and crossed upon the table. Her feet seemed enervated, the night-gown barely reached her knees, showing flaccid muscles, blue veins, and impoverished legs. The cold, which she heeded not, turned her lips violet; a sad smile drew up the corners of a sensitive mouth that showed small, ivory-white teeth. Her ears were dainty, and despite the roundness of her face she was lovely. All animation was in the eyes: the iris brown like Spanish tobacco with black flecks and golden reflections round brilliant pupils. Pierrette was made to be gay but was sad. Her lost gaiety still showed in the vivacious eye, the ingenuous grace of her brow, the smooth curve of her chin. The long eyelashes lay upon cheek-bones made prominent by suffering. Paleness rendered the lines and details purely distinct. The ear alone was a little masterpiece of modeling.

Perhaps you would like to know her history, and this is it.

Pierrette’s mother was a Demoiselle Auffray of Provins, half-sister by the father to Madame Rogron, mother of the present owners of the house.

Monsieur Auffray had married at eighteen and again near sixty-nine. By his first marriage he had an only, plain daughter, who married at sixteen to innkeeper Rogron; by his second he had a charming daughter, so that Madame Rogron was fifty when the half-sister was born and already had two grown children.

The youngest daughter married at eighteen a man of her choice, a Breton officer named Lorrain, captain in the Imperial Guard. The captain, anxious to rise to a colonelcy, exchanged into a line regiment. While he, then a major, and his wife enjoyed themselves in Paris on the allowance of Monsieur and Madame Auffray, or scoured Germany, old Auffray (formerly a grocer) died at eighty-eight without making a will. His property was administered by his daughter, Madame Rogron, and her husband so completely in their interests that nothing remained for the widow beyond the house she lived in on the little square and a few acres. This widow, the mother of Madame Lorrain, was thirty-eight at her husband’s death. Like many widows, she unwisely remarried. She sold the house and land to her step-daughter, Madame Rogron, and married a young physician named Neraud, who wasted her fortune. She died two years later of grief and misery.

Thus the share of her father’s property that should have come to Madame Lorrain almost entirely disappeared, reduced to a small sum of eight thousand francs. Major Lorrain was killed at the battle of Montereau, leaving his wife, then twenty-one, with a little daughter of fourteen months and only a pension and an eventual inheritance from his parents, Monsieur and Madame Lorrain, retail shopkeepers at Pen-Hoel in the Marais. These Lorrains, Pierrette’s grandparents, sold wood, slates, tiles, pipes. Their business, from incapacity or ill-luck, did poorly and gave them scarcely enough. The failure of Collinet at Nantes, caused by 1814 events and a fall in colonial products, deprived them of twenty-four thousand francs they had deposited with that house.

The arrival of their daughter-in-law was welcome. Her pension of eight hundred francs was a handsome income at Pen-Hoel. The eight thousand francs which the widow’s half-brother and sister Rogron sent her from her father’s estate were placed in the Lorrains’ business; they gave her a mortgage on a little house they owned in Nantes, let for three hundred francs and barely worth ten thousand.

Madame Lorrain the younger, Pierrette’s mother, died in 1819. The child was small, delicate, and weakly; the damp climate of the Marais did not agree with her. But the husband’s family persuaded her, to keep her with them, that in no other place could she find a healthier region. She was so petted that her death brought nothing but honor to the old Lorrains.

Some declared that Brigaut, an old Vendeen who had served under Charette and other leaders in the wars against the Republic, counted for much in the younger Madame Lorrain’s willingness to remain in the Marais. All Pen-Hoel saw him—called respectfully Major Brigaut—spending his days and evenings in the Lorrains’ parlor beside the window of the imperial major. Toward the last, the curate of Pen-Hoel urged old Madame Lorrain to have her daughter-in-law marry Brigaut, promising to have the major appointed justice of the peace through the Vicomte de Kergarouet’s influence. The young woman’s death ended the matter.

Pierrette was left with her grandparents, who owed her four hundred francs a year, interest on the little property placed in their hands. This small sum supported her. Growing less fit, they soon faced an active competitor who ruined them. With his connivance, the Lorrains borrowed on notes they could not meet, driving them into bankruptcy. Pierrette’s claim on the house in Nantes was superseded by her grandmother’s legal rights, enforced to secure daily bread. The house sold for nine thousand five hundred francs, of which one thousand five hundred went for costs. The remaining eight thousand came to Madame Lorrain, who lived on their income in a humble place at Nantes called Saint-Jacques, where the two old people had bed and board for a small payment.

As it was impossible to keep Pierrette with them, the old Lorrains wrote to her uncle and aunt Rogron in Provins. These Rogrons were dead. The letter might easily have been lost, but the post persistently traced it to Monsieur Rogron, son of the deceased, a mercer in the rue Saint-Denis in Paris; curiosity about a possible inheritance ensured its transmission, and the Treasury its fee.

These Rogrons, toward whom the old Lorrains, though dreading to part with their dear granddaughter, stretched their supplicating hands, became unexpectedly the masters of Pierrette’s destiny. It is therefore indispensable to explain both their antecedents and their character.

Pere Rogron, the innkeeper of Provins who had married old Auffray’s daughter by his first wife, was a coarse, gaudy figure: inflamed face, veiny nose, cheeks stained by Bacchic redness, short, fat, pot-bellied, with stout legs and thick hands. He had the shrewdness of a provincial innkeeper and liked good living and being waited on by pretty girls. Neither he nor his wife was handsome; both suited one another. Rogron belonged to the blunt, egotistical sort whose vices were displayed without concealment. Grasping, selfish, indecent, and given to gratifying whims, he squandered earnings until his teeth failed; what remained was a miserly selfishness. In old age he sold his inn, gathered what he could of his late father-in-law Auffray’s property, and moved into the shabby house on the square of Provins bought cheaply from the widow of old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother.

The Rogrons’ income included about two thousand francs a year from twenty-seven local lots and twenty thousand francs from selling the inn. The Auffray house, though dilapidated, became their residence. Rogron turned to horticulture, expanding a garden to the river’s edge, building a stone embankment and cultivating aquatic flora.

Early in their marriage the Rogrons had a son and a daughter, both unattractive—victims, like many, of a coarse upbringing. Sent cheaply to nurses who worked in the fields, the children returned from a rough provincial infancy with coarsened features and harsh voices. Their mother’s vanity was mortified, her sternness producing no effect; the father’s severity made that sternness seem mild by comparison. The children were often left to run loose in stables, courtyards, or streets, sometimes whipped, sometimes sent to their dour grandfather Auffray. The Rogrons defended their appropriation of most of Auffray’s property by accusing him of injustice to his grandchildren, yet they themselves had been harsh.

Rogron ensured the children had basic training: he sent his son to school, bought a cartman to save him from conscription, and apprenticed his daughter Sylvie in Paris at thirteen with six hundred francs for board. Two years later their son Jérôme-Denis followed. When asked what he meant to do with them, Rogron brashly declared his intention to kick them into independence once they understood life, boasting that his father had kicked him three times whereas he would give only one kick and more money—an avowal of cutting parental responsibility to its financial minimum.

Sylvie, apprenticed in the rue Saint-Denis, rose quickly. By nineteen she was earning four hundred francs; at twenty she was second demoiselle at the Maison Julliard, wholesale silk dealers; by twenty-one she was forewoman at a thousand francs. Jérôme began in the Maison Guepin; at eighteen he was head-clerk earning twelve hundred francs. The siblings socialized cheaply on Sundays and holidays. Around the end of 1815 they pooled savings of about twenty thousand francs to buy the property and good-will of the “Family Sister,” a large retail establishment in the quarter. Sylvie kept the books and correspondence, Jérôme managed the shop. By 1821 fierce competition had consumed much of their capital; it cost them everything to maintain the business and its reputation.

Though scarcely forty, Sylvie’s natural ugliness, relentless work, and a severe expression aged her. Jérôme, looking silliest to customers, had a retreating forehead, close-cut grizzled hair, a dull, lifeless expression, and bluish eyes lacking spark. Short and fat like his father, he retained a flabby pallor typical of those confined to back-shops and counting-rooms, forever tying bundles, making change, snarling at clerks, and repeating phrases to customers.

Their limited intellect had been entirely absorbed in keeping their business going and learning the trade: thread, needles, ribbons, pins, buttons, and haberdashery took up everything. Outside the shop they knew nothing of Paris beyond the Rue Saint-Denis; the shop was their universe. They were adept at scolding clerks and young women and assigning blame. Their pleasure was the hum of a busy counter; when Parisians strolled unconcerned with buying, the day was “bad weather for sales.”

Rogron’s mechanical skill at tying parcels and keeping an eye on the shop made him admired by apprentices; he could fold and tie and spot everything that passed. Yet without his sister he would have been helpless. Sylvie possessed common sense and a trade genius; she advised purchases, dispatched him to remote French towns to save a trifle, and drove the business’s speculation. Necessity to support the business animated their relentless activity. Rogron understood only his clerkly tasks; he had no grasp of the business as a whole and often recoiled when Sylvie ordered goods sold below cost to clear worn fashions. He neither reasoned well nor ill, simply accepted his sister’s authority—partly because she was older—and the isolation of a life devoted to mere needs had left him clownish and vacuous. Sylvie had discouraged his marrying, preferring to keep him dependent and avoid the expense and disturbance a wife might bring.

Silliness in such men is talkative. Rogron chattered constantly to clerks with trivial shop jokes and had developed a meaningless shop phraseology, explaining manufacturing details to customers who knew nothing, attaining a petty superiority through this babble. The Rogrons, baptized by mistake into shopkeeping, lacked heart-feelings; their natures were shriveled and harsh from toil, privation, and long apprenticeships. They were not implacable but impractical and unsympathetic toward misfortune. To them, virtue, honor, and loyalty reduced to the payment of bills. Irritable, stingy, and given to petty economies, they bore a dreadful reputation among rue Saint-Denis merchants. Their connection to Provins—returning three or four times a year and occasionally closing the shop—kept them afloat by attracting apprentices from the provinces, whom Rogron sent, praising his son’s success, to families hoping to start children in Paris. When clerks or young women escaped, rejoicing at freedom increased the Rogrons’ bad name; new victims were supplied yearly by the indefatigable father.

From fifteen, Sylvie had two faces: the amiable, wheedling seller who smiled and charmed customers and the sour spinster peering from half-open blinds whose real expression was stern and forbidding. It was while they were in mourning for their father that the Lorrains’ letter reached them. Their father’s death left them the house in Provins—acquired virtually by their father from Pierrette’s grandmother—and other lands and moneys gained through usurious loans and mortgages on peasantry. The yearly taking of stock was over; the “Family Sister” had been paid for. The Rogrons owned about sixty thousand francs of merchandise, forty thousand in bank or cash, and the goodwill and value of their business. Sitting behind their private counter they debated what to do next.

Like many retail shopkeepers, they aspired to become bourgeois. Selling the good-will could yield over a hundred and fifty thousand francs; placing remaining assets in public funds would bring each about four thousand francs a year, and proceeds could repair and improve their Provins house. They could then live comfortably in Provins. They eyed their forewoman—the daughter of a rich farmer at Donnemarie who had lost seven of nine children over five years—as a possible successor; Rogron had tried to make her his wife but failed. Sylvie opposed such a match but intended to make the woman their successor in the shop.

The internal life of such shopkeepers is opaque: they live on hope—dreams of municipal honors, country-houses with plaster statues and trick fountains, distinctions in the National Guard. Provins nourished such fanatical longings in the Rogrons. Provins, described with affectionate exaggeration, is a sudden town appearing on a hill after a gray wheat plain, watered by two rivers, with a charming valley, ramparts, mills, inns, shops, retired merchants, diligences, and a sentiment of rooted attachment among those who leave to make fortunes. The Rogrons’ imaginations were captivated. As Jérôme piled buttons or rolled ribbons he pictured the upper town, ravines, rivers, and the little square below—the house he would renovate with new front, bedrooms, salon, billiard-room, dining-room, kitchen garden turned English pleasure-ground with lawns, grottos, fountains, and statuary. He coveted upholsterers’ windows and draperies for his imagined Provins house. Though their Paris rooms were sparsely furnished, in fancy they already owned better furniture. Sylvie, pen in hand, envisioned receiving the bourgeoisie of Provins, admiring herself in salon mirrors, and selecting caps. The rue Saint-Denis atmosphere and market mud made them long for Provins roses; the promised valley became a monomania, intensified by the grind of mercer’s business.

It was amid these meditations that the Lorrains’ letter came. The Rogrons knew little of their cousin Pierrette Lorrain; their father had acquired the Auffray property after they left home and had spoken little about the family. A brief genealogical discussion revealed Aunt Lorrain to be the younger sister of their own mother by their grandfather Auffray’s second marriage—a second marriage the Rogrons believed had wronged them by dividing Auffray’s property. With this suspicion, they considered the Lorrains’ request to take in the orphan Pierrette through a self-interested lens. Would taking charge of a cousin commit them to obligations? Might she become a legal heir if she remained unmarried? Could they send her away if displeased? Would they be forced to marry her into their family? If Jérôme found a wife among Provins heiresses, property should be preserved for his children; Sylvie’s ideal “yoke-mate” was a stupid, rich, ugly girl who could be governed. They decided to refuse taking Pierrette in. Sylvie volunteered to write the answer but delayed, busy with urgent business; when the forewoman offered to buy their stock and good-will—a sale the Rogrons accepted—the matter dropped from the old maid’s mind.

Sylvie and her brother left for Provins four years before Brigaut’s arrival that would touch Pierrette’s life; their subsequent doings there, born of the same commercial, narrow antecedents, are essential to the story. Provins, with its charm and the Rogrons’ bourgeois ambitions, proved destined to be no less fateful for Pierrette than the Rogrons’ Parisian commercial upbringing had prepared them to be.

When the petty shopkeeper returns from Paris to the provinces, he brings back echoes of the capital that soon fade, leaving only superficial marks where Paris briefly touched the town. The Rogrons, brother and sister, were seized by such an impulse: having saved, they spent on remodeling their old house into something they imagined charming.

Their fixed idea, endlessly discussed with the contractor, produced the façade and interior Brigaut examined: elaborate plans, handsome furnishings, and a parade of choicer objects meant to rival Provins’ notables. “If Monsieur Garceland has it, put it in,” Mademoiselle Rogron pronounced; they fretted over details, balked at extra costs yet surrendered to comparisons—“The Lesourds had theirs painted last year”—aiming to match the town’s leading families.

For a year they watched workmen, answered townspeople’s questions, and tried to insinuate themselves into Provins society. In Paris they had known no one; in Provins their former masters had returned to prominence: Monsieur and Madame Julliard of the “Chinese Worm,” the Guepin clan now keeping the “Three Distaffs,” and Madame Guenee, from whom the Rogrons had bought the “Family Sister.” These families intermarried and dominated municipal life—Garceland the mayor a Guepin son-in-law; Abbé Peroux, the curate, brother to Madame Julliard; Monsieur Tiphaine junior, a judge, brother to Madame Guenee.

The queen of Provins was the beautiful, clever, ambitious Madame Mélanie Tiphaine junior, who, removed from a convent to marry, had become the social manager of the town. She hosted twice-weekly salons, cultivated every vanity to forward her husband’s career, and aimed to have him elected deputy and later a judge in Paris. Her circle—joined by Madame Guenee, the Julliards, and others—formed the town’s ruling court. Julliard junior, secretly enamored of her, launched the “Bee-hive,” a journal of family contributions and local notices. The few aristocrats held a separate salon at the Comtesse de Breautey’s.

The Rogrons were received, after careful study, first by Madame Julliard the elder and Madame Garceland, then—after difficulty—by Madame Tiphaine. Society winnows by wealth, education, manners, accomplishments, and character; the Guepins, Guenees, and Julliards stood higher than the Rogrons, whose father had been held in contempt over the Auffray property—a fact familiar to notary Auffray.

At Madame Tiphaine’s salon everyone understood one another; the Rogrons hoped admission by the mayor would suffice. Sylvie practiced boston; Rogron, unable to play, twirled his thumbs and spoke only of his new house. He choked on words and made a comic show of embarrassment. Sylvie betrayed herself at cards—sharp, irritable, whining when she lost, insolent when she won; nagging and quarrelsome, she quickly became a social scourge. Yet they had a silly, open ambition: having spent thirty thousand francs on their restoration and possessing at least ten thousand francs a year, they believed themselves wealthy and sought to impose on Provins, which promptly measured their vulgarity, ignorance, and envy.

Madame Tiphaine had already noticed them and told Julliard junior she would not admit such people to her select salon. “I wish my society to be select; and it certainly will not be if I admit those Rogrons,” she said. Monsieur Tiphaine sneered at their shopkeeper origins; Julliard called them impudent, wanting to crush other houses though “they have hardly enough to live on.” They judged the sister’s harsh voice and thick hands intolerable. Madame Tiphaine’s remark—“my salon is not an inn”—was much admired and circulated. A month passed before she returned Sylvie’s visit; provincial insolence and measured neglect stung.

Sylvie had an awkward evening at Madame Tiphaine’s, provoking a scene with old Madame Julliard over boston. Afterwards the card-tables were set before Sylvie arrived so she would be shunted to an onlooker’s role; at Madame Julliard senior’s they played whist, which Sylvie did not know. Invitations stopped. Satirical townspeople teased the Rogrons about the eggs in their cornice and their wine cellar; the pair tried to parry by giving sumptuous dinners to return civilities and display luxury.

Their first dinner for town leaders became a test. Madame Tiphaine, who had introduced the Parisian custom of leaving after coffee, was anxious to go; the Rogrons accompanied her to the door in provincial form, but when she departed without staying for the full evening, the rest quickly imitated her, leaving the Rogrons disconcerted. They had hoped to astonish guests with their “palace,” but Madame Tiphaine later described the house to her salon in terms that ridiculed their taste: a long corridor and portico, a painted statue, a black-marble painted staircase, white calico curtains with red borders fastened by brass lion-paws, lamps like restaurant lights—novelties everywhere. The dining-room had five cheap engraved heroics in gilt frames; the salon was all red—curtains, furniture, mantelpiece—heavy common candelabra, a clock on Roman standards, a chandelier wrapped in green muslin, walls papered in red flock to imitate velvet, chromo-lithographs in stucco frames, uniform cashmere and elm furniture; an Aubusson carpet in a vulgar pattern completed a room that looked as if no one lived there: no books, knick-knacks, or flowers. Madame Tiphaine preferred Madame Julliard’s pastels and old provincial silver; “The provinces are provinces; they are only ridiculous when they mimic Paris,” she concluded. Her sketch revealed the absurdities a clever builder had foisted on the Rogrons—expensive novelties that gratified bourgeois taste but exposed their vulgarity.

Their plans failed. Invitations were refused—“already engaged” to Madame Garceland and others. The Rogrons had supposed a few dinners would secure position; only young men who would mock them and habitués who dined everywhere accepted. Frightened by the loss of forty thousand francs on her “dear house,” Sylvie economized: she dismissed the cook, hired a country girl, and took to cooking “for pleasure.”

Fourteen months after returning, they had fallen into solitude and idleness. Banished from society, Sylvie developed a strong hatred for the Tiphaines, Julliards, and the rest of the “clique” and sought a rival circle. But the lesser bourgeoisie comprised only small shopkeepers free only on Sundays and men like Vinet and the Bonapartist Baron Gouraud, whom Rogron carelessly befriended despite warnings.

The siblings sat by the stove, recalling former customers and old resentments, weighed down by ennui. Days lengthened into lazy habits: lingering in bed, shaving as an event, quarreling about hot water, wandering to inspect garden shrubs or a sick hen, tapping the barometer like a friend. They read the “Bee-hive” and the Constitutionnel, the cost shared by Vinet and Gouraud; Rogron delivered the paper to Gouraud and enjoyed long yarns and puzzling warnings about the clique—warnings he foolishly repeated. His walks yielded scraps of gossip—archaeological projects, talks about Provins’ catacombs and springs—that became topics at home. Evenings were desert-like; they creaked at one another like rusty wheels. Rogron spoke of marrying, which frightened him; Sylvie sought a third person and remembered a distant cousin, Pierrette Lorrain, scarcely inquired about and perhaps presumed dead.

Sylvie, too careful to lose opportunity, pretended to have found a Lorrain letter to raise the prospect of Pierrette’s arrival. She answered Madame Lorrain’s letter half-affectionately, half commercially, blaming delay on the change of abode and hinting Pierrette might inherit twelve thousand francs a year if Jérôme did not marry. The Rogrons awaited the cousin like caged beasts. Sylvie even went to Madame Tiphaine to mention Pierrette, deploring the girl’s misfortune and posing as eager to assist—only to be met by sarcasm. During cards, Madame Garceland recalled the Rogrons’ shameful conduct over the Auffray property; the notary explained events. Monsieur Tiphaine asked where the little girl was.

“In Brittany,” said Rogron.

“Brittany is a large place,” Monsieur Lesourd remarked.

Sylvie clumsily answered questions about when they had replied and where the girl might be sent. Rogron blundered, claiming to have written to the Institution of Saint-Jacques, only to be told that was an almshouse for the elderly. Desfondrilles recalled a Nantes house sold under court order swallowing up Mademoiselle Lorrain’s claim, leaving the girl unprovided for. The Rogrons retreated, blaming malicious gossip. Sylvie saw that her ostensible benevolence had made no impression; she had lost ground. Thereafter the Rogrons no longer concealed their hatred of that class.

Into this resentment stepped two disreputable allies. Colonel Gouraud, a retired officer—short, fat, decorated, with a red, battle-scarred face and the Legion of Honor—lived modestly on a small pension and jolly lawlessness; he loved opportunity and plunder. The lawyer Vinet, tall, thin, pale, and spiteful, nursed liberal opinions in place of talent. Disfavour with the court, a marriage that had enraged a family, and poverty made him eager for opposition triumphs to elevate him. His quick, rasping speech and readiness to exploit legal avenues made him dangerous.

Both watched the Rogrons with curiosity. Vinet aimed to found a newspaper and, seeing the brother’s money and house, concluded Rogron could be made useful. When Gouraud told him the Rogrons had quarreled with the Upper-town bourgeois aristocracy, Vinet proposed a plan.

“One woman or another—handsome or ugly—you don’t care; marry Mademoiselle Rogron and we can organize something at once,” he said. Gouraud had thought of opportunistically exploiting Pierrette Lorrain; Vinet suggested securing wills in their favor. “You’d get a very comfortable house,” he said. As for the little girl—Gouraud leered, promising to assess how far he could profit.

Thus the Rogrons, rejected by the elite they sought to join, became the center of a new, unscrupulous circle: a veteran with a taste for plunder and a lawyer eager to exploit both politics and private advantage. Their vanity and hatred of the clique, paired with the colonel’s coarse designs and Vinet’s legal opportunism, set in motion schemes that would determine Pierrette’s fate and the Rogrons’ future.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After her grandparents were taken to the hospital where they were expected to die, young Pierrette—proud and unused to charity—felt a private relief on learning she had well‑to‑do relations. Brigaut, the son of her mother’s friend and her childhood companion, then an apprentice cabinet‑maker in Nantes, offered the sixty francs of his apprentice tips to send her to Paris; she accepted with the indifference of true affection. Brigaut spent Sundays consoling Pierrette at Saint‑Jacques, delighting in protecting the girl he loved and promising to make a fortune while she waited. In October 1824, just after her eleventh birthday, the sorrowful grandparents and Brigaut entrusted her to the Nantes–Paris diligence conductor, begging him to see her safely onto the Provins coach. Brigaut ran after the coach until out of breath; both cried as she disappeared.

Unfamiliar with city life and effectively penniless, Pierrette arrived in Paris dependent on the conductor’s kindness. Told of her “rich cousins,” he paid hotel expenses and insisted the Provins conductor care for the child and obtain payment from the family. Four days later, about nine on a Monday night, a kindly conductor of the Messageries‑royales led Pierrette—with only a small bundle of two dresses, two chemises, and two pairs of stockings—to Mademoiselle Rogron’s, announced, “I’ve brought you a cousin,” and demanded forty‑seven francs for expenses. The Rogrons, astonished, paid. The conductor refused thanks, saying they had cared for the child as if she were their own.

Sylvie, Rogron, and their maid Adèle received Pierrette with stolid surprise; she was thrown to them like a parcel, with no soft transition from the wretched chamber at Saint‑Jacques to their dining room, which seemed a palace to her. Shy and speechless in her coarse Breton clothes—blue petticoat, cambric apron, thick shoes, blue stockings, white kerchief, red worsted mittens, and a washed Breton cap haloing her rosy face—Pierrette might have charmed others. But the Rogrons viewed her through the lens of cost and decorum. They inspected her bundle, decided she must sleep, and tucked her into a modest room with threadbare furnishings. Pierrette, amazed at cotton sheets and the warmth of the house, quietly praised it while Sylvie and Rogron fretted about future expenses. “You won’t get off under three hundred francs,” Rogron declared; Sylvie lay awake shocked.

Reared on affection and freedom at Pen‑Hoel and petted by everyone, Pierrette had known hardship at Saint‑Jacques but had lived on her grandparents’ love. Now the cold greeting of her cousins baffled and wounded a heart delicate as a fawn’s. Still tired, she slept.

Next morning she rose early, explored the staircase and garden, marvelled at the kiosk and river, and was punished at breakfast for noise and dirt. “You must be very good and quiet,” Sylvie scolded; Rogron called her dirty and ignorant; Adèle sniffed at her coarse chemises. Their remarks hurt Pierrette though she scarcely understood why. The Rogrons, proud of their house and furniture, had turned care for possessions into a worship. Sylvie kept a closet of dusting tools and took petty delight in maintaining household goods; her ardor for cleanliness hardened into principle and pleasure.

Determined to remake Pierrette to Provins standards, they set about sewing and fittings the first week: underclothes, boots, a corset, a blue reps dress, a silk‑lined cape, and a blue velvet bonnet—fashionable trappings Sylvie hoped would stand up to local scrutiny. She engaged Mademoiselle Borain, the town’s best seamstress, scolding the child for ignorance when Pierrette confessed she had only played in Brittany and been loved by her grandparents. Pierrette preferred the warm, watching company of the sewing women to her relations’ pedantry; the seamstresses liked her, while Sylvie fretted the expense—“That child will make us pay through the nose!”—yet pushed on, buying quality clothes to rival other mothers.

Transformed, Pierrette became Provins’s most enchanting child; Sunday after Sunday, the ladies embraced her. Their admiration inflated Sylvie’s self‑regard—she believed the praise reflected her benevolence—yet she grew resentful when invitations for Pierrette did not bring callers to her salon. The child’s success, which might have delighted a maternal heart, only enraged the Rogrons’ selfishness.

Fashion, however, exposed fragility: used to running and rough play, Pierrette wore out shoes and tore delicate garments. A mother might have corrected gently; the Rogrons calculated loss. Constant admonitions about propriety, economy, and decency all flowed from one theme—“Pierrette will ruin us.” In fault‑finding lectures they reverted to petty, shopkeeping habits they'd ostensibly left behind; deprived of larger spheres to govern, they exercised despotism on the helpless child. Narrow natures expand by persecuting: for them self‑interest fused with a thirst for control and vindication.

Pierrette became central to their lives: first as an expense, then as a presence to be governed and displayed. Sylvie’s initial softness hardened into the cold “Pierrette.” Reprimands sharpened; the cousins sank into habitual severity they convinced themselves was beneficial. Too sensitive and straightforward for such hardness, the child dreaded scoldings; tears rose at the slightest reproof. She learned to repress her natural sprightliness, showing it only in friends’ homes. Within a month she had acquired passive compliance; when Rogron asked if she were ill, she fled to the garden and wept by the river.

One day, despite careful measures, she tore her best reps frock at Madame Tiphaine’s, where she’d been happy. Terrified of home punishment, she confided in friends; Tiphaine happened to have matching material and discreetly repaired the frock. When Sylvie discovered this “trick,” she forbade Pierrette further visits to Tiphaine’s, cutting off one of the few joyful outlets she had. This refusal, and the beginning of formal education, marked the end of the first three months in Provins—the only period that had held genuine pleasure.

Meanwhile the colonel and Vinet watched the Rogrons with foxlike caution, making calls and infiltrating their confidence with polite reserve. On the very night Sylvie forbade Tiphaine’s visits, the colonel and a lawyer friend—using gossip and partisan spite to inflame Sylvie—suggested the Rogrons form an opposition salon against Provins’s “ministerialists.” Vinet exploited slander to rouse Sylvie; the lawyer painted Tiphaine’s speech as an insult, provoking the Rogrons’ vanity. Vinet then introduced his mild, well‑bred wife to Sylvie, whose meek deference pleased and flattered Sylvie’s self‑importance. These new allies encouraged the Rogrons’ snug social ambitions and further isolated Pierrette.

Under their watchful, acquisitive eyes the child’s life narrowed. Shut indoors and permitted out only under strict supervision, she lived beneath an incessant “Don’t touch that, child, let that alone!” She was drilled in carriage and behavior; Sylvie, rigid and proud, ordered her erect and sometimes enforced compliance with slaps. The free, joyous Breton girl learned, little by little, to curb every liveliness and to become, as best she could, an automaton—quiet, obedient, and constrained to match the Rogrons’ appetite for domination and display.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
One evening early in Pierrette’s second stay with her cousins, the unseen child came to kiss the guests good-night. Sylvie turned away coldly and Pierrette burst into tears. Vinet and Sylvie mocked her; Sylvie contrasted the grandmother who took Pierrette’s money with a cousin who would leave her hers. “I would rather be robbed and loved,” Pierrette said and was threatened with being sent back. Humiliated, she went to Rogron, who, with Sylvie, accused her of mischief—opening a watch, breaking a mainspring—and of heedlessness. Ashamed, she left the room.

Rogron resolved to subdue the child. Madame Vinet suggested school; Vinet and the colonel had other plans. Vinet proposed using the managing editor of an opposition journal as Pierrette’s teacher if Rogron would back the paper financially. Sylvie called Pierrette a “rough diamond” needing polishing, and the plan to hire a master and launch a paper advanced while Pierrette dissolved in tears. Blaming her cousin, she resolved to be faultless; unaware she became the subject of further schemes.

Pierrette did get a writing-master to teach reading, writing, and arithmetic—an arrival greeted at the Rogrons’ as a harbinger of disorder: ink-spots, scattered sand, torn books. She was told to earn her keep and not be a burden; to weep was treated as an affront to their generosity. Rogron adopted the tone he used with clerks: scolding her, summoning her from play to drill lessons, growing almost savage. Sylvie, teaching what little she knew of women’s work, showed no tenderness. Their narrow pleasure in worrying the child hardened into severity, justified by alleged “self-will.” Unable to adapt instruction to Pierrette’s slow learning, they called her stupid and clumsy for trifles. Constantly abused by words and looks, she took on a sheep-like submission, sheltering her mind in passive obedience. Her bright colors faded; when she complained of feeling ill “everywhere,” they dismissed it as nonsense or feigned attention-seeking.

Only Adèle, the fat servant, softened toward the child—warming her bed secretly after Sylvie’s rebukes. But the Rogrons treated Pierrette’s affection as dissimulation; any gentle feeling met suspicion and accusations of ulterior motives. Colonel Gouraud, anxious to please Sylvie, approved her actions; Vinet attributed the child’s behavior to Breton obstinacy and encouraged the Rogrons. Flattered, Rogron agreed to be security for the Courrier de Provins; Sylvie invested five thousand francs.

The colonel and Vinet then organized a wider financial and political campaign. They sold shares in the paper to farmers, independents, and buyers of national-domain land, exploited local fears, and divided judicial advertisements between the existing Bee-hive and the new Courrier. The paper’s first number contained a pompous eulogy of Rogron, likening him to Laffitte and arousing expectations of contested elections. The Courrier attacked local figures like the Tiphaine family; Madame Tiphaine feared losing social position.

As the Courrier spread, Vinet adopted liberal dress and airs; his wife took a servant and comported herself like the wife of a prominent man. Vinet and his ally Cournant, a liberal notary, worked to increase the Rogrons’ rentals: pursuing suits over planting and trees, collecting fines, and ejecting peasants who had defaulted on debts secured by the elder Rogron. Using the Rogrons’ savings—who had been setting aside a large sum annually—they invested in improved lands, dividing holdings into small farms rented to safe tenants. By 1826 these investments yielded about five thousand francs a year, with tenants bearing many repair costs.

By the end of Pierrette’s second stay, life under the Rogrons had hardened into cold indifference, petty fault-finding, and the absence of affection; she felt a grave chill closing in. She contemplated running back to her grandparents in Brittany, but two events prevented escape: old Lorrain died, and Rogron was appointed her guardian. Had the grandmother died first, Rogron—advised by Vinet—would have claimed Pierrette’s eight thousand francs and left the old man destitute. Prompted by Vinet’s remark that one never knew who might live or die, Rogron forced Madame Lorrain to secure for Pierrette the reversion of the eight thousand francs at her death.

Preparing for her first communion, Pierrette abandoned thoughts of flight and began to change in another way. Sylvie insisted that the local vicar, Monsieur Habert—a zealous, austere priest thought to belong to a Congregation—should instruct her. Habert and his sister, a thin, unmarried schoolmistress, took an interest in the child. Rooted in Breton Catholic practice and naturally inclined toward devotion, Pierrette responded fully to the priest’s teaching. Suffering predisposed her to mysticism; the priest taught her to see Jesus as a heavenly bridegroom and to interpret her trials as part of divine providence. Faith gave meaning to her suffering; she abandoned escape and took refuge in prayer, hope, and charity. Surprised, Sylvie also adopted more piety under the priest’s influence, while Rogron—bound to Colonel Gouraud, Vinet, and liberal ambitions—remained unmoved.

Mademoiselle Rogron befriended Mademoiselle Habert; the sister offered to take Pierrette to her school to spare Sylvie the trouble, but the Rogrons decided the house would be too lonely without the child. Their attachment seemed excessive to the rival camp.

Gouraud and Vinet, however, feared the Haberts’ influence: they suspected the priest aimed to marry his sister advantageously, as the colonel hoped to marry Sylvie. They saw the priest and his sister as rivals as formidable as themselves. Both pairs—the liberals led by Vinet and the Haberts with their clerical circle—visited the Rogrons frequently, turning the salon into a battlefield where each faction sought the household’s allegiance.

The liberals struck back. Vinet brought Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf and her mother from Troyes—Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, noble yet impoverished—persuaded that by combining her means with Vinet’s she could live in Provins and marry the gullible Rogron. Their arrival strengthened the liberal party and scandalized Provins’ aristocrats. Bathilde, at twenty-five, was dazzling: fair, well-formed, with the carriage of a high-bred woman. Her simple but careful dress and light courtship overwhelmed Rogron, who masked excitement as indifference. Sylvie and Céleste Habert were deceived by his manner; Vinet was not.

The colonel, courting Sylvie, took pains each evening to appear the party leader: clean shirt, velvet, a new overcoat, a rosette, foregoing smoking. Vinet and the colonel also spread insinuations that Monsieur Habert was sympathetic to liberal ideas; the bishop, hearing this, summoned Habert and told him to stop visiting the Rogrons—but his sister persisted. The Rogron salon thus became firmly established as a liberal rendezvous.

Politics and matrimonial schemes mingled: the Villele ministry fell in 1826; Vinet, who had borrowed to buy land to qualify as a candidate, nearly beat Tiphaine by two votes. The Rogrons’ salon served as Liberal headquarters, frequented by notary Cournant and Doctor Neraud. The Rogrons themselves scarcely understood the extent of their triumph.

Bathilde, instructed by Vinet to view Pierrette as an enemy, treated the girl with disdain. Madame Vinet, constrained by her husband’s will and later estranged by the Chargeboeufs’ arrival, could do little for Pierrette. The child often clung to Madame Vinet, begging to be taught embroidery; gently treated, Pierrette could learn and shine. But with the Chargeboeufs installed and Sylvie contemplating marriage, Pierrette was increasingly seen as an obstacle.

Nearly fourteen, pallid from neglected illness, Pierrette’s existence struck Sylvie as a burden. The salon habitués advised turning her into a servant: dismiss Adèle and have Pierrette keep house and cook. Pierrette, generous and self-sacrificing, offered willingly to learn the bitter bread of service. Adèle was sent away, depriving Pierrette of her only protector.

Reduced to the status of a servant, she was scolded for the smallest speck of dust. Objects she once admired became odious; no matter how she strove, her cousins found cause to reproach her. In two years she received no praise or kindness; the best she could hope for was simply not being scolded. She bore the morose ill-humor of two celibates—people unfamiliar with tenderness—with angelic patience, daily reminded of her dependence.

Such pressure wore on her body as well as her spirit. Secret internal pains undermined her strength and halted her development. Slowly, through hidden sufferings and exhaustion, the child declined toward the fragile, silent state in which her childhood companion later found her—the pale figure beneath the morning song.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut’s arrival provoked in the Rogron household, it helps to explain how he came to Provins; he plays a quiet but pivotal role.

When Brigaut fled the house he had not merely been frightened by Pierrette’s gesture; he was appalled by the change in her. The voice, eyes, and gestures he had known—lively and tender—had been replaced by something he scarcely recognized. From a distance his legs trembled; he felt a chill down his back. He had seen only Pierrette’s shadow. Climbing to the Upper town, he found a vantage where he could see the square and her house; he stared at it mournfully and discovered the depth of his attachment to his adopted sister.

Passion between children is rare, yet Pierrette and Brigaut had often protected each other. He had given her money for a journey once and nearly killed himself chasing the departing diligence; she knew nothing of this. For him such memories warmed his hard life. For her sake he had struggled to improve himself: he learned a trade, went to Paris to make his fortune, and after a fortnight could not resist returning to Provins, walking from Saturday night to Monday morning. He had meant to go back to Paris, but seeing Pierrette kept him; an unconscious magnetism held him. Tears from his eyes and from hers showed him how deep his feeling was. To her he was a Breton boy of childhood; to him she was life itself.

At sixteen Brigaut could not yet do fine joinery—he had not learned to draw or model a cornice—but during his apprenticeship he earned four or five francs a day by piece-work, enough to live in Provins near Pierrette. He chose the best cabinet-maker in town, intending to learn by work while keeping watch over her. His mind was made up: he returned to Paris for certificate, tools, and baggage, and three days later became a journeyman in Monsieur Frappier’s establishment, the best cabinet-maker in Provins. Active, steady workmen were prized; by the end of the month he was foreman and was fed and lodged by Frappier, who taught him arithmetic and line drawing. The shop and house were in the Grand’Rue, not a hundred feet from Pierrette’s square.

Brigaut suppressed his passion and behaved prudently. He made Madame Frappier tell him what she knew about the Rogrons, including how their father had grabbed old Auffray’s property. He collected other family details. He met Pierrette at market with her cousin and shuddered to see the heavy basket she carried; on Sundays he went to church in his best clothes to look for her. There, for the first time, he learned Pierrette was Mademoiselle Lorrain. Pierrette saw him and discreetly signaled him to keep away. That tiny gesture contained a world for him, as had the earlier signal from her window that told him to run away. Oh, what fortune he must make to marry her—he was told she would inherit the Rogron house, a hundred acres, and twelve thousand francs a year, besides savings.

The persevering Breton therefore resolved to educate himself thoroughly in his trade in Provins rather than in Paris, to remain near Pierrette and later explain his plans and the protection he could offer. He wanted to know why she looked pale and why debility had dulled her eyes; he wanted to discover what made her seem on the brink of death. The two signs she had given—gestures admitting friendship but pleading caution—alarmed him. She evidently asked him to wait, for otherwise there was danger. As she left the church she gave him a tearful look; but he could no more have guessed the events inside the Rogron house during his fortnight in town than he could have squared the circle.

Pierrette herself had reason to be fearful the morning after Brigaut invaded her dreams. She thought Sylvie must have heard the song; she did not know the powerful reasons the old maid had to be alert. For the last eight days strange events and bitter feelings had agitated the Rogron salon’s principal figures. These hidden matters, carefully concealed, were destined to fall like an avalanche on Pierrette. Such secret schemes and calculations underlie many revolutions, whether political, social, or domestic; their subtle significance must be traced to understand what followed.

Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste) and Mademoiselle Sylvie Rogron both desired to marry Rogron; Céleste was ten years older and hoped her children would inherit all the Rogron property. Sylvie, at forty-two, faced a risky age for marriage. Céleste, instigated by her vindictive brother the priest, magnified Sylvie’s fears. Sylvie trembled; celibates are haunted by fear of death, and with a change of ministry the Liberals had come to power in town. The colonel would likely be made mayor; the thought appealed to Sylvie. She resolved to consult a doctor, though the step would expose her.

Consulting Monsieur Neraud, the Liberal physician and rival of Martener, would be a political mistake; so Céleste proposed to hide Sylvie in her dressing-room while she herself consulted Monsieur Martener about the delicate matter. Whether Martener was Céleste’s accomplice is unclear; at any rate he told Céleste that even at thirty the danger, though slight, existed. “With your constitution you need fear nothing,” he added. But pressed about a woman over forty—a middle-aged unmarried woman—Martener grew clinical: elasticity declines with age, especially in sedentary women; childbirth after forty is rare and risky for an unmarried woman. His grim prognosis: an unmarried woman would make a great mistake marrying after forty; marriage then would be more a union of interests than real conjugal vitality.

Sylvie left the doctor's more frightened than before. Céleste urged patience: “wait,” she counseled, Jesuitically aware that time might resolve her rival’s suit. Sylvie’s renewed devotion to the church made her conscience troublesome; the priest explained the Church sees marriage primarily for propagation and rebukes second marriages and passion without social purpose. This moral peril gave extraordinary force to her passion; the very prohibition added attraction. Mademoiselle Rogron’s perturbation did not escape the lawyer Vinet.

One evening after a game, Vinet drew Sylvie aside. He perceived she was troubled and, by letting others leave, elicited the truth. A shrewd man of the world and the editor of the Courrier, he privately counseled Sylvie to delay marriage for ten years. Inwardly he wondered how to secure the Rogron fortune for his own family—he planned it should go to Bathilde—and he immediately schemed to drive the colonel from the field. The priest’s influence and Vinet’s financial cunning balanced each other; Rogron himself had little piety, and the black-robed pair were fairly matched in manipulating him.

When Céleste’s victory over Sylvie seemed assured, Vinet saw his chance to arrange Rogron’s marriage to Bathilde; he knew Jérôme (Rogron) was already inwardly susceptible to Bathilde and guessed that once alone they would be married. Vinet, who had learned of Sylvie’s secret love, realized how such an obsession in an old maid gripped by religion could be exploited: it could be used to ruin Pierrette and the colonel alike, each becoming the cause of the other’s downfall.

The next day Vinet met the colonel while Rogron and the colonel strolled their daily round. Whenever the three men were seen together the town gossiped; the triumvirate symbolized local Liberal pride: Vinet the editor, the colonel the arm, Rogron the purse. Vinet stopped the colonel and confided: Pierrette is charming; you can do better than marry Sylvie; if you play it well you can marry Pierrette in two years. He then explained the Jesuit’s maneuver and its effect on Sylvie. The colonel, who had long thought about Pierrette but concealed his feelings, was stirred. His roughness to the child was a mask; though he often treated her harshly in public, when alone he would be affectionate. After Vinet’s talk he began to seek opportunities to find Pierrette alone, and his behavior softened; he praised her father and recounted his bravery.

A few days before Brigaut’s coming, Sylvie had suddenly seen Gouraud and Pierrette conversing and jealousy seized her with monastic violence. Jealousy, credulous and suspicious, enlarges fancy without adding intelligence; it filled her with fantastic ideas. When she heard Brigaut’s ditty a few mornings later, she leaped to a conclusion: the man who had sung “Madam’ le mariée,” addressing Pierrette, must be the colonel. She had noticed a change in the colonel’s manners for a week; he was the only man who had paid her attention in her solitary life. So she watched him with all her mind and eyes; by hope and despair she magnified everything into a mirage. By dint of staring she saw nothing real. Alternately she repelled, admitted, and conquered the thought of rivalry. She compared herself—forty-two with gray hair—with Pierrette’s delicate fairness and thought of men of fifty who might love such girls. Before the colonel came to the house, she had heard odd stories at the Tiphaines’ salon about his life. Old maids hold exaggerated Platonic sentiments and fixed doctrines. The mere thought of being jilted by the colonel was torture; she lay awake rehearsing desires, Pierrette’s conduct, and the strange song that had awakened her with the word “marriage.” Foolishly she opened her window—without reflecting that Pierrette would hear her. If she had spied instead, she would have seen Brigaut and the fatal scene might not have begun.

Pierrette, fragile, unwell, had her duties: she unbarred and opened kitchen shutters and the garden door, fetched brooms and utensils with meticulous care, and labored to please her cold-eyed cousin. She hated reproof and sought calm; happiness was seeing her cousin’s glance be calm rather than satisfied. Her skin was damp with labor when she returned to put the kitchen in order and light the stove to carry hot water to her two cousins and to prepare the dining-room fire. For this she fetched wood from the cellar, moving from warm rooms into damp air—sudden transitions that worsened her condition. She did not know she was ill; strange cravings came, she ate raw vegetables secretly, and she was unaware how serious her state was. If Neraud, the Rogrons’ physician, had warned her before Brigaut’s arrival she would only have smiled; life was so bitter she could smile at death. But Brigaut’s song and presence revived her like rain on parched soil; unconsciously she wanted to live and thought perhaps she did not suffer after all.

That morning Pierrette slipped into her cousin’s bedroom to make the fire, left hot water, roused Rogron, then went to take in milk, bread, and provisions. She lingered on the doorstep hoping Brigaut would come; he was by then already on his way to Paris. Finishing the dining-room, she worked in the kitchen when she heard Sylvie coming down. Mademoiselle Rogron appeared in a brown silk dressing-gown with her cap awry and a night-gown showing; she came straight to Pierrette.

“Ha! here you are, lovesick young lady!” Sylvie mocked.

“What is it, cousin?” Pierrette asked.

“You came into my room like a sly cat and crept out the same way, though you knew I had something to say to you.”

“To me?”

“You had a serenade this morning, as if you were a princess.”

“A serenade!” Pierrette repeated, bewildered.

“A serenade,” Sylvie mocked. “And you’ve a lover, too.”

“What is a lover, cousin?” Pierrette inquired honestly.

Sylvie evaded the definition. “Do you dare tell me a man did not come under your window and talk to you of marriage?”

Pierrette, schooled by persecution, answered cautiously: “I don’t know what you mean—”

“Who means?—your dog?” said Sylvie sharply.

“I should have said ‘cousin,’” Pierrette corrected humbly.

“And didn’t you get up and go in your bare feet to the window?—which will give you an illness; and perhaps you didn’t talk to your lover, either?”

“No, cousin.”

“You have many faults, but I did not think you told lies. You will have to explain this to your cousin and to me, or I shall take severe measures.”

Sylvie, exasperated and curious, meant to frighten the girl. Pierrette, who endured more than she had strength for, kept silence. Silence is sometimes a slave’s weapon; it baffles envy’s accusations. Sylvie watched her, saw the girl’s cheeks color in burning patches—signs a mother would have noticed and soothed, but an old maid without maternal feeling drew only suspicion from them.

“She blushes; she is guilty!” thought Sylvie.

Pierrette’s silence was turned against her. “Pierrette,” Sylvie continued, “before your cousin comes down we must talk together. Come,” she softened, “shut the street door; if anyone comes they will ring and we shall hear them.”

Despite the mist rising from the river, Sylvie took Pierrette across the lawn to the rock terrace—an ornamental quay with iris and water plants. She changed tactics, thinking firmness might catch Pierrette tripping; the hyena became a cat.

“Pierrette,” she said, “you are no longer a child; you are nearly fifteen; it’s not surprising you should have a lover.”

“But, cousin,” said Pierrette, raising angelic eyes, “what is a lover?”

Sylvie could not define it decently; instead she answered bluntly, “A lover is a man who loves us and wishes to marry us.”

“In Brittany we call the young man a suitor,” Pierrette answered simply.

“Well, remember that in owning your feelings for a man you do no wrong; the wrong is in hiding them. Have you pleased some of the men who visit here?”

“No.”

“Do you love any of them?”

“No.”

“Certain?”

“Quite certain.”

“Look at me, Pierrette.”

The girl looked.

“A man called to you this morning in the square.”

Pierrette lowered her eyes and said nothing.

“You went to your window, you opened it, and you spoke to him.”

“No cousin, I went to look out and I saw a peasant.”

“Pierrette, you have much improved since your first communion; you have become pious and obedient; I am satisfied with you. I don’t say this to puff you up with pride.”

In the old maid’s mind Pierrette’s meekness and silence had been misread for hypocrisy. Pierrette raised grateful eyes at the praise, but the praise shifted to threat.

“But if it is all hypocrisy, if I find you a serpent that I have warmed in my bosom, you will be a wicked girl, an infamous creature!”

“I think I have nothing to reproach myself with,” Pierrette answered, painfully shocked.

“You know that to lie is a mortal sin?”

“Yes, cousin.”

“Well, you are now under the eye of God,” said Sylvie, pointing upward; “swear to me that you did not know that peasant.”

“I will not swear,” said Pierrette.

“Ha! he was no peasant, you little viper.”

Pierrette rushed away like a frightened fawn. Sylvie called her in a dreadful voice.

“The bell is ringing,” Pierrette answered.

“Artful wretch!” thought Sylvie. “She is depraved in mind; now I am certain the little adder has wound herself round the colonel. She has heard us say he was a baron. To be a baroness! little fool! Ah! I’ll get rid of her; I’ll apprentice her out, and soon too!”

Sylvie was so lost in thought she did not notice her brother Rogron coming down the path lamenting frost on his dahlias. “Sylvie! what are you thinking about? I thought you were looking at the fish; sometimes they jump,” he said. “How did you sleep? Don’t you think my skin is getting tabid?” Rogron had been uneasy about his health since his failed suit with Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf.

Pierrette called that breakfast was ready. At sight of her Sylvie’s face went green and yellow; bile in commotion, she looked at the corridor floor and ordered Pierrette to rub it.

“I will rub it now if you wish,” Pierrette said, unaware the additional labor might harm her.

Through breakfast Sylvie pretended she wanted this and that just to make Pierrette rise repeatedly. Pierrette, painfully conscious, brought milk mixed with cream in a large silver goblet after heating it in a bain-marie. The cousins poured coffee. When Sylvie prepared hers she saw an atom of coffee-grounds floating on the surface. There the storm broke.

“What is the matter?” Rogron asked.

“The matter is that mademoiselle has put dust in my milk. Do you suppose I am going to drink coffee with ashes in it? Well, I am not surprised; no one can do two things at once. She wasn’t thinking of the milk! a blackbird might have flown through the kitchen today and she wouldn’t have seen it! and then it was my coffee, ha! that didn’t signify!”

She lifted aside the grounds that had passed through the filter.

“But, cousin, that is coffee,” Pierrette said.

“Oh! then it is I who tell lies, is it?” Sylvie cried, blasting the girl with a fearful look.

Emotional strain often sharpens clarity; Sylvie’s anger had the cold clearness she often used in her shop to inspire fear. “You had better dare to give me the lie!” she continued. “You deserve to be sent from the table to eat by yourself in the kitchen.”

“What’s the matter with you two?” asked Rogron. “You are as cross as bears this morning.”

“Mademoiselle knows what I have against her,” said Sylvie. “I leave her to make up her mind before speaking to you; for I mean to show her more kindness than she deserves.”

Pierrette looked out the window to avoid Sylvie’s frightening eyes. “Look at her! she pays no attention to what I am saying than if I were that sugar-basin! And yet mademoiselle has a sharp ear; she can hear and answer from the top of the house when someone talks to her from below. She is perversity itself,—perversity, I say; and you needn’t expect any good of her; do you hear me, Jérôme?”

“What has she done wrong?” Rogron asked.

“At her age, too! to begin so young!” screamed Sylvie.

Pierrette rose to clear the table and give herself something to do; the scene was nearly unbearable. Though such language was not new, she had never grown used to it. Her cousin’s rage seemed to accuse her of crime. She imagined what fury would be if they knew about Brigaut; perhaps they would send him away and she would lose him. All a slave’s rapid thoughts came; she resolved to keep absolute silence about an incident her conscience told her was innocent. But the cruel words and wounding suspicion so shocked her that as she reached the kitchen she was seized with a convulsion of the stomach and felt deadly sick. She dared not complain; she was not sure anyone would help her. Returning to the dining-room she was white as a sheet, said she was unwell, and went to bed, dragging herself by the baluster and thinking she would die. “Poor Brigaut!” she thought.

“The girl is ill,” Rogron observed.

“She ill! That’s only shamming,” Sylvie replied loudly so Pierrette might hear. “She was well enough this morning, I can tell you.”

This last blow prostrated Pierrette; she went to bed weeping and prayed to God to take her from this world.

For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the “Constitutionnel” to Gouraud; nevertheless the colonel came punctually to fetch his paper, to gossip, and, weather permitting, to take Rogron for a walk. Sylvie dressed with as much coquetry as she could—hoping to see the colonel and question him. She fancied herself handsome in a green gown, yellow shawl with red border, and a white bonnet with gray feathers. About the hour the colonel usually arrived she took her place in the salon with her brother, keeping him at home in dressing-gown and slippers.

When Gouraud entered, Rogron begged him to wait, leaving Sylvie alone with the colonel. He noted a certain solemnity on the pock-marked face of the old maid and paid her compliments. Sylvie said she had wanted to go out, but Pierrette, her little cousin, was ill. Gouraud’s distrust of Vinet was constant: Vinet controlled the paper, took the revenues, and reigned as master while the colonel, the responsible editor, earned little. Vinet and his associate Cournant had done the Rogrons many services; Gouraud was only a powerless half-pay officer. Vinet would be the deputy consulted by the liberals. The colonel also knew Rogron harbored an ardent, concealed passion for Bathilde de Chargeboeuf; when Gouraud had hinted he might propose to Bathilde, Rogron had turned cold and hostile.

Thus Vinet reigned in the Rogron household; the colonel was marginalized, holding only the precarious tie of a tender, if somewhat deceitful, affection for Sylvie. A lawyer’s suggestion that Gouraud break with Sylvie and marry Pierrette had flattered the colonel’s vanity; yet he suspected Vinet intended to separate him from Sylvie and, by exploiting her fears, throw the Rogron property into Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf’s hands.

Left alone with Sylvie, the colonel detected her uneasiness; she had planned to see him privately. Suspecting Vinet, he went on guard. He never believed a woman’s word; when Sylvie said Pierrette had gone to bed before midday, he suspected secrecy. He tried to sound casual.

“You are dressed divinely,” Gouraud said, and Sylvie shyly accepted the compliment. He suggested sending Pierrette to Paris to be placed in a milliner’s shop; Sylvie, uneasy, saw in this the mark of Vinet’s meddling. Alarmed, Gouraud deliberately spoke disparagingly of Bathilde and of beauty as a poor foundation for marriage; at forty-eight he preferred comfort and a companion, not children or a young wife whose admirers might humiliate him. When Sylvie asked bluntly, “Then you don’t love Pierrette?” he laughed and denied it.

Reassured, Sylvie pretended shrewdness, attributing the marriage plan to her brother. The colonel, seeing an opening, declared his real wish: he had long chosen Sylvie for her good qualities and desired no wife but her. He took her hand and kissed it. Sylvie tried one more test: “But if I want you to marry Pierrette? If I leave her my fortune?” The colonel refused, fearing a younger wife’s competition, preferring to avoid future humiliations. Sylvie smiled but remained grim inside.

When Rogron returned, the colonel bowed to Sylvie in a lover-like way and, on their walk, told Rogron he had been joking about Bathilde: he confessed his wishes, declaring he longed to be Rogron’s brother-in-law. “If nothing is wanting but my consent you have it,” cried Rogron, delighted to be rid of Bathilde as a rival. Sylvie then spent the morning imagining household arrangements—an upper floor for her brother and the rest furnished for her and her husband—and resolved, true to old-maid suspicion, to test the colonel and prevent any private intercourse between him and Pierrette.

Pierrette came down before dinner to lay the table. Sylvie, forced to cook, seized any pretext to show suspicion: she swore at Pierrette for a grease spot on her gown—proof, she declared, that Pierrette hadn’t done her work herself. “You are like the dog of the marshal who woke up as soon as the saucepans rattled,” Sylvie snapped, accusing the girl of feigning illness and lying generally.

After dinner Sylvie sent Pierrette to dress in her best clothes; suspicion made Sylvie invent reasons to keep Pierrette close, to judge the colonel’s truthfulness.

That evening the Chargeboeuf ladies arrived first. On Vinet’s advice Bathilde dressed elaborately—blue velveteen, transparent fichu, garnet pendants, ringlets—displaying queenly manners and girlish coquetry aimed at Rogron. Her mother carried an aristocratic insolence. Bathilde had intelligence; during a two-month stay at Vinet’s house he had discovered and fed her mind, inflaming her contempt for a world that prized money over beauty. Vinet told her marriage could be used to gain position and influence: marry Rogron, make him deputy, and through political maneuvering secure promotion—then, if fortunes changed, marry up later. He spoke of women as instruments to advance men like post-horses.

Vinet, who visited nightly with Madame and Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, had transformed himself into a man of consequence—gold spectacles, silk waistcoat, white cravat—shedding former bitterness for a sly, agreeable perfidy. Madame de Chargeboeuf bewitched the salon with condescension; Bathilde staged coquettish displays for Rogron, stepping before him, adjusting her bonnet, placing her foot on the fender for his admiration. She teased him, and he, pacified, murmured, “You are too beautiful... too dangerous.”

Pierrette’s meek civility contrasted painfully with Bathilde’s disdain: Bathilde pushed past her; Pierrette took her hat as if she were a servant. Vinet feigned admiration, fixating on Pierrette to insult her indirectly. “Fine!” the poor child answered, saying the compliment was for Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf. The scene made Pierrette painfully aware of her dependence and smallness.

Céleste Habert entered with the colonel. Mademoiselle Habert, now the Rogrons’ enemy, resembled a stern schoolmistress—caps, grim mouth, coarse hairs on a mole; she took snuff and sat with a posture of habitual stiffness. Her presence completed the company, and everyone showed civility despite undermining one another.

They took to a game of boston. Mademoiselle Habert sat opposite Sylvie; the colonel beside her; Bathilde near her mother and Rogron; Pierrette floated between Sylvie and the colonel. Sylvie watched the colonel closely and plotted. When she undertook a grande misere in hearts, the pool rose high. Pierrette, keeping her embroidery, watched the colonel. At a moment when the colonel’s play seemed to follow a hint, Sylvie cried that Pierrette had seen her hand and then accused the girl outright. Céleste and others insisted the colonel had simply played his game; Desfondrilles, the deputy-judge, swore Pierrette had seen only the colonel’s hand; notary Cournant echoed the verdict.

But Gouraud flattered the idea that “little girls know how to slide their eyes into everything,” and persisted that Pierrette had peeked. Under pressure, Sylvie cried, “You are a story-teller and a little fool,” and the truthful Breton denied it, then fled in fear without a light, ran to her room, and struck her head on the open passage door. Sylvie, hearing the blow, cried, “Good! I’m glad of it.” Desfondrilles and Mademoiselle Habert observed the injury; Sylvie justified it by blaming Pierrette for disobedience. Madame de Chargeboeuf, laughing, suggested Sylvie pay her misere first, hinting at Sylvie’s notorious bad faith at cards. Sylvie sat and forgot Pierrette; her indifference surprised no one. When the game ended around half past nine, she flung herself into an easy chair by the fire and did not rise as guests departed. The colonel’s behavior tormented her; she did not know what to think. “Men are so false!” she cried as she went to bed.

Pierrette had struck a blow on the head just above the ear; the next day there was a swelling. At breakfast Sylvie said, “God has punished you. You disobeyed me; you treated me with disrespect... you got what you deserved.” Rogron, more sensible, suggested she ought to put on a compress of salt and water. Pierrette, meek and dependent, accepted even such remarks as proofs of kindness.

The week continued in torment. Sylvie increased her cruelty; Pierrette, proud and stubborn, complained not, bearing vague and sharp pains while thinking constantly of Brigaut — fearing for him if discovered, comforted that he was nearby in Provins. The single glimpse she had of him lingered like an exile’s look at his country.

Brigaut, moved by the same hunger to communicate, devised a simple plan: Pierrette should lower letters on a string from her window at a set hour. One market morning he slipped a note to her while pretending to buy fruit; she hid it in her apron, cheeks aflame. These two children, scarcely aware of the depth of their feelings, carried more emotion between them than many adult lovers. Sylvie, who did not notice Brigaut’s Breton accent, paid him no heed.

That night Pierrette read Brigaut’s letter. He begged her to let down a string at midnight so they could exchange notes, called her the victim of “monsters,” invoked their Breton past, vowed to earn enough to take her home, said he had 150 francs and could send money by the string, and begged her to pray with him in church. The letter uplifted Pierrette; it was balm in her misery.

Risking discovery, she stole pen, paper, and ink and wrote back: a simple outpouring naming Brigaut and her grandmother as the only people she loved; describing brutal treatment — forced to scrub pots, do rough work that marred her hands, be scolded and used like a dog — and begging rescue back to Pen-Hoël, to memories of sliding on the pond. She confessed pains in head and body, hid her tears, and asked if he would love her always. She weighted the note with a crust of bread and lowered it at midnight. Brigaut broke the string to take it and lingered beneath her window; they signaled to one another by candlelight for over an hour before parting.

Next day Pierrette rose transformed — light, joyful — a change Sylvie noticed with the sharp jealousy of an old maid. At dinner Sylvie accused her of feigning illness or having a lover, ordered her to sit in the dining-room with a candle, and watched her closely. Pierrette endured; Brigaut’s promised nightly visits kept her alive. One midnight he left a new note explaining an owl-cry signal his father had taught him and said he would return in a few days with good news, hinting at measures to liberate her. He also wrote secretly to Madame Lorrain in Nantes, begging her to come for her granddaughter and to justify herself against rumours that she had taken Pierrette’s money; he feared the grandmother might be dead.

Pierrette clung to Brigaut’s assurances as a desert traveler clings to a palm-tree. But Sylvie had grown suspicious. She had seen a figure under the window and then, alarmed, discovered Pierrette’s cord and began spying as relentlessly as any customs officer. Pierrette, foreseeing danger, sewed the letters into her corset and covered them. That seemed to end the lovers’ nightly meetings.

Sylvie spent three nights awake, then sent Pierrette to confession and searched the garret, finding nothing; her resentment hardened into cruel watchfulness. Pierrette’s pains worsened; she developed a regular fever. One Sunday evening, amid a salon game, Pierrette fainted. The colonel caught and carried her to a sofa. Sylvie sneered it was a trick, and in front of guests forced Pierrette to unfasten her gown; when Sylvie felt papers in the corset she pretended outrage, calling the girl a hypocrite. Afterwards she detained Vinet, a lawyer and ally, and revealed through him her decision never to marry the colonel. Vinet, pragmatic and selfish, advised Sylvie to secure power by other means: find a rich wife for Rogron, or forge bargains that would leave Pierrette with nothing. He hinted the colonel was a gambler whose recklessness would ruin him unless he married a fortune. This planted schemes of revenge and deprivation in Sylvie’s mind.

That night three owl-like cries echoed in the square. Pierrette, feverish, opened her window at the signal and lowered a ball of silk. Sylvie, awake and frantic, heard the noise, raced upstairs, and surprised Pierrette unfastening the cord. A violent struggle followed. Pierrette clenched the letter in her hand; Sylvie grabbed and twisted the child’s arm, dug her nails in, and — in frenzy — even bit the wrist, trying to force the hand open. Pierrette screamed, “Help! help! they are murdering me!” The grotesque contest between stoic innocence and savage jealousy left Pierrette exhausted and bleeding.

At that instant loud knocks sounded at the door. Rogron opened; Brigaut rushed in, followed by a tall gray woman — Pierrette’s grandmother — whose sudden appearance changed everything. Seeing the torn corset and the bloody hand, the old woman carried Pierrette away in her arms to Frappier’s cabinet-maker’s house nearby, where Brigaut and the Frappiers improvised a bed. Pierrette fainted; the letter fell from her loosened fingers.

Madame Lorrain’s presence cowed Sylvie. She had come from Pen-Hoël after receiving Brigaut’s urgent letter. The old Breton woman — solemn, spare-faced with glittering eyes — grasped in an instant what Pierrette had endured. She swore, knelt upon the bricks, and demanded Brigaut fetch the best doctor in Paris. Producing banknotes — forty-two thousand francs recently given her by a repentant creditor, Monsieur Collinet, to reimburse past losses — she offered funds for immediate care. Brigaut rode off by diligence for Bianchon in Paris, as Martener, the local doctor, advised.

Martener examined Pierrette and found an abscess above the left ear; the bruised hand could not have been self-inflicted. He urged full disclosure of the maltreatment and awaited the Paris physician’s arrival while prescribing a quieting draught: Pierrette needed sleep. Left to her grandmother, the child told everything — the endless scolding, the heavy labor, the insults, the scalded and injured hands, the hunger for the Breton home — details that would later serve in a judicial inquiry. The grandmother’s knowledge of Pierrette’s suffering and her promises of security soothed the girl.

Meanwhile Sylvie, stunned by the grandmother’s sudden might and by Brigaut’s letters she had read after ripping open the corset, slept in uneasy bewilderment, not foreseeing where her spite would lead. The colonel, intercepted by Vinet’s schemes and threatened by political and marital entanglements, found himself at odds with those around him. Vinet and Sylvie plotted to secure property and influence; the colonel, warned and enraged, demanded a rich wife before elections or threatened consequences.

At Frappier’s Pierrette lay half-dead, her fingers unbent and bloodied, while those who loved her — Brigaut, Madame Lorrain, the Frappiers — stood silent. The grandmother, who had fetched the money that made rescue possible, clasped her hands and resolved to complain to the authorities and secure proper care. Martener, convinced this was no mere youthful pretence, prepared to consult with the best surgeon available. Brigaut had already left for Paris to fetch Bianchon.

Thus the little drama — love, spying, cruelty, and rescue — reached a decisive moment. Pierrette, exhausted by the physical consequences of her courage, sank into a sleep made safe by her grandmother’s presence and a soothing draught, while the others, stunned and ashamed, anticipated the legal and moral reckonings to come.

At nine that morning Monsieur Martener told Monsieur Tiphaine about Pierrette’s condition: the quarrel with Sylvie, the physical and moral abuses by the Rogrons, and two serious illnesses those cruelties had produced. Tiphaine summoned Auffray, a notary and Pierrette’s maternal relative. The affair ignited a factional feud: Rogron supporters had already spread slanders about Madame Tiphaine’s mother and du Tillet, and Tiphaine saw Pierrette’s case as a chance to humiliate the Rogrons, who hosted a salon tied to anti‑monarchist activity and an opposition journal. The public prosecutor was alerted; Auffray was chosen as guardian so he could call a Family Council and, backed by testimony from three doctors, demand Pierrette’s release. Lesourd, the procureur, would steer the matter toward a criminal inquiry.

By midday Provins was aflutter. Pierrette’s faint cries from the previous night became public horror when a crowd gathered at Frappier’s shop; Frappier reported her arrival with broken fingers and a bloody hand. Doctor Bianchon’s post‑chaise arrived about one; Martener and the hospital surgeon were summoned. The Rogrons were accused of deliberate ill‑treatment that had nearly killed their cousin.

At the law courts Vinet hurried to the Rogrons. Under questioning Sylvie at first evaded, then admitted jealousy and, reluctantly, that her suspicions were baseless. Vinet, exploiting their fear, insisted on a plausible public motive for his defense. They contrived Rogron’s immediate marriage to Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf—announced that day and to have banns posted Sunday—and Sylvie agreed to appear in the marriage contract as assigning her capital while retaining income. Vinet urged antedating the contract to bind the Chargeboeufs publicly and preserve their visits, promising to defend them if they signed. By noon Vinet had authority to draw the contract and represent the Rogrons.

At twelve a petition accused Brigaut and the widow Lorrain of abducting the minor Pierrette from her guardian’s house—turning the defenders into complainants. The hearing was postponed until four; Tiphaine wanted Auffray present with the doctors’ report. The marriage announcement and Sylvie’s concessions alienated two supporters, Mademoiselle Habert and Colonel Gouraud, who publicly stayed with the Rogrons but intended to harm them. Martener and others recounted that Pierrette had earlier struck herself the evening she was forced to leave the salon, citing Mademoiselle Rogron’s heartless remarks. Vinet, having secured the Chargeboeuf alliance, planned to use it to dominate Provins socially and politically.

From midday to four the Tiphaine circle watched over Pierrette. Unaware of the tumult, she found consolation with her grandmother and Brigaut. Bianchon protested the barbarity and insisted all town physicians examine the case; Dr. Neraud, a Rogron friend, attended. The unanimous medical report explained Pierrette’s malady—a sadly common but horrifying cranial condition—and attributed it to ill‑treatment.

At four President Tiphaine presided. Madame Lorrain, Auffray, Brigaut and others entered the court; Vinet alone represented the Rogrons. He claimed two strangers had forced entry into the Rogron house and abducted the minor. Auffray, as surrogate guardian, presented the physicians’ report—signed by a Paris authority and all Provins doctors—asserting that Pierrette’s near‑fatal condition resulted from ill‑treatment by Rogron and his sister. Auffray asked that Pierrette not be returned to the guardian’s household but remain with a family member and proposed convening a Family Council to consider deposing the guardian.

Vinet objected that he had not seen the report and could not rebut it. The judge, noting the report might already have been submitted to the procureur du roi, heard the case anyway. Tiphaine forbade returning Pierrette to her guardian until the Family Council met, commissioned the attending physician and the hospital surgeon to inspect her, and stipulated that if her injuries were alarming the criminal courts would intervene, without prejudice to Auffray’s civil suit.

Vinet scoffed, calling it fuss over a girl supposedly involved with an apprentice, and left to rally his faction. He framed the Tiphaines’ actions as partisan persecution and Sylvie’s intervention as natural for a jealous sister seizing letters.

Frappier’s house was soon judged unsuitable; Pierrette was moved to Auffray’s with conspicuous care. Laid on a stretcher and accompanied by a Gray Sister and her grandmother, she passed under many windows, her pallor strengthening Auffray’s case. At Auffray’s she was installed on the second floor with Martener and the hospital surgeon attending; she was tenderly cared for.

That evening the dispute polarized public opinion. The Rogron salon filled as Vinet rallied Liberals; the Chargeboeuf ladies dined there to sign the contract. Vinet minimized the Pierrette episode, confident royal courts would vindicate them; some accepted the Chargeboeuf alliance as wiping the Rogrons’ slate clean and depicting Pierrette as depraved. In Madame Tiphaine’s salon, vengeance for past slanders was turned on the Rogrons, now cast as monsters. Journalists offered opposing accounts: one claimed no case existed; the Rogron paper advised patience for the law.

The Family Council, chosen by the juge de paix, included Rogron and the two Auffrays as nearest relatives, Monsieur Ciprey (a maternal nephew), Mademoiselle Habert, Pierrette’s confessor, and Colonel Gouraud. The judge’s choice seemed impartial—Habert and Gouraud had been seen as Rogron allies. Rogron requested Vinet for the council, postponing it to late December when Tiphaine would be in Paris—a delay tactic by Vinet.

Vinet spoke for three hours before the council, trying to prove an intrigue between Pierrette and Brigaut to justify Sylvie’s severity and portraying the guardian’s delegation of duties as natural. Despite his efforts, the council unanimously removed Rogron’s guardianship; Auffray became guardian and Ciprey surrogate. Testimony included Adele the servant against her former masters, Mademoiselle Habert recounting Mademoiselle Rogron’s cruel remarks, and Brigaut’s letter from Pierrette describing her ill‑treatment. The council concluded Pierrette’s illness resulted from neglect by the guardian, and the case would proceed publicly. Rogron, advised by Vinet, opposed the council’s report being accepted by the court. Authorities intervened as Pierrette’s condition worsened; the trial, placed on the docket, was postponed until March 1828.

Meanwhile Rogron married Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf; Sylvie moved to the second floor with her, and Madame Rogron supplanted Madame Tiphaine socially. Tiphaine, supported by Nucingen and du Tillet, advanced to civil judge and moved to Paris; his son inherited and sold the Provins house—evidence, in Vinet’s telling, of Tiphaine’s contempt for the town. The promotion yielded little for Vinet’s faction, but Vinet exploited the events politically. Thus Pierrette’s brutal martyrdom—culminating in trepanning performed under Bianchon’s and Martener’s advice—was mired in legal delay while she languished in pain.

Martener and the Auffrays grew fond of Pierrette and her grandmother. Martener, who had begun his career in Paris but returned to provincial practice, devoted himself to curing her, treating her with reverent care. The Auffrays’ household fell quiet in her presence; their house, on a terrace beneath chateau ruins, became her refuge, and her room gave her rare views of a landscape she had seldom seen.

Brigaut, unable to work, visited three times daily, faithful and sorrowful. The grandmother, concealing her despair to comfort Pierrette, made her a Breton cap recalling better times. Pierrette’s pale, serious features embodied melancholy; all served her devotedly. Madame Martener sent a piano that transfixed her; her calm resignation impressed clergy and lay visitors alike.

Martener corresponded with Bianchon and sought to drain the abscess; periodic improvements cheered them, but the cranial disease remained grave. Bianchon brought the celebrated Desplein from Paris, who warned only a miracle could save the child: caries of the bone had begun and, at her age, bones were fragile. The operation was performed in early March 1828 by Desplein, who cautioned the odds. Martener had proposed an even bolder procedure—rejected by Desplein. Returning from Paris, Martener announced that science could do no more; recovery was left to God. They responded with masses and vows.

While Pierrette lay dying, Vinet continued to malign her in court. The judge accepted the Family Council’s report; Vinet appealed. The procureur du roi required further evidence, including testimony from Pierrette. When Desfondrilles came to take it, Pierrette was dying with her confessor near for extreme unction. She begged forgiveness for her cousins, saying judgment belonged to God; she told her grandmother to leave all to Brigaut and to give Adele a thousand francs, insisting that had Adele stayed with the Rogrons she would not be dying.

On a bright Tuesday of Easter week at three in the afternoon, Pierrette died. Her grandmother kept vigil and sewed her shroud. Brigaut went to Frappier’s to make the coffin; Frappier watched him plane ribbons of wood wet with tears and lined the coffin with lead. By chance the lead cost exactly what Pierrette had given Brigaut for her journey—a coincidence that overwhelmed him. He burned his tools, settled accounts, and bid farewell, his grief part of the awful finale of the Rogrons’ tyranny.

At the Auffray house the room where Pierrette lay was crowded. Rogron’s emissaries stood near the body, and a surgeon in a dissecting apron prepared instruments; Madame Lorrain cried out, fearing they intended to open the body. Brigaut, who had just brought the coffin, seized a chisel, threatened the men, and swore he would kill anyone who touched her. Auffray and the curate restrained him; the lawyers and doctors left. The plumber soldered the lead to make the coffin harder to open. With Pierrette dead, the guardianship suit collapsed; Rogron did not dare attend her funeral.

Brigaut left Provins, went to Paris, joined the Royal Guard, and afterward the Algerian expedition, rising to major and later an officer; he inherited forty‑six thousand francs from Madame Lorrain, who died in Paris in 1829. Vinet prospered politically—elected deputy in 1830 and later procureur‑general—while Rogron became receiver‑general and Tiphaine, after aligning with the July dynasty, became president of the Royal court. Madame Tiphaine and Madame Rogron lived amicably; Vinet remained close to Tiphaine. Rogron declined in health and sense; Sylvie managed his property and lived frugally. Desfondrilles, aided by Vinet, became president of the Provins court.

Locals gradually softened the scandal into gossip about weak constitutions or imprudent liaisons; many involved felt no remorse. Only two men remembered Pierrette daily—Doctor Martener and Major Brigaut; they alone knew the hideous truth. To grasp the full scale of that truth one thinks of Beatrice Cenci—another young woman ruined by intrigues—reminding us that legality often serves scoundrelism unless one believes in a higher justice.

ADDENDUM: Many characters recur in Balzac’s Human Comedy—Bianchon, Brigaut, Desplein, General Gouraud, Matifat, Nucingen, du Tillet, Madame Tiphaine, Vinet, and others appear in works like Father Goriot, Lost Illusions, César Birotteau, The Chouans, Cousin Pons, The Vendetta, The Member for Arcis, and The Firm of Nucingen.
