Have you felt unease and sadness in the crowded galleries since the revolution of 1830? Balzac argues that the true Salon has vanished beneath an overwhelming number of artists. Where once a strict selection showcased only the finest works and provoked passionate debate—helping form reputations like Delacroix and Ingres—now exhibitions are numbed by quantity. Before 1817 only a select number of paintings were shown; today galleries overflow, individual works lose impact, exhibitions close quickly, and the Salon’s role as arbiter of taste has been reduced to a tumultuous bazaar. Great artists are obscured by mediocrity; paradoxically, talk of unrecognized genius increases when the Academy’s discerning judgment is absent.

Amid this swelling catalogue stands Pierre Grassou, nicknamed “Fougeres.” In 1832 he lives in a cramped rue de Navarin apartment with a modest studio overlooking Montmartre. Tidy and careful, Grassou is a genre painter who avoids the burdens of historical subjects, focusing on easel painting suited to his modest means.

One December morning, the picture dealer Elie Magus visits. Magus, who habitually bought Grassou’s works cheaply, proposes a business arrangement: Grassou should paint portraits of the bourgeois Vervelle family, who are eager to enter the art world. Their daughter has a substantial dowry, and Magus hints that marriage might secure Grassou’s financial future. Though startled by talk of marriage, Grassou sees in the commission a chance to establish himself as a portraitist and possibly improve his status.

To understand Grassou’s position, Balzac traces his past. He studied under masters like Servin and Schinner but never fully mastered their techniques; his education was marked by lack of recognition and ridicule for mediocrity. Gentle and diligent, he nonetheless failed to stand out beside flashier peers. His first Salon attempt—a village-wedding scene in 1819—was rejected. Schinner bluntly advised him to give up painting. Persisting, Grassou refined the piece and sold it to Magus for a meager sum, launching a long career of perseverance.

Grassou’s story embodies the struggles of many contemporaries: ambition tempered by limited talent, the hope for recognition, and the harsh reality that circumstance and market forces often overshadow true artistic merit.

For several days Pierre Fougeres waited near Elie Magus's shop hoping his painting would attract notice, but it was ignored. After a week it disappeared; when Fougeres asked, Elie said he had sold it and would buy more if Fougeres produced Flemish interiors, anatomy lessons, and landscapes. Overjoyed, Fougeres felt validated and missed the ironic tone a more cynical artist might have noticed.

He ran into his friend Joseph Bridau but, consumed with painting, could not enjoy the evening. Back home he sketched, bought supplies, hired models, and devoted himself to his craft. After two months he produced four paintings. Schinner and Bridau called them derivative and urged literature, but Fougeres retouched the canvases and sold them to Elie for twenty-five francs apiece, barely breaking even. He noticed a strange mist over his paintings in the shop but dismissed it.

Over seven years he developed competent, if unremarkable, work. Elie sold the pieces, and Fougeres earned a modest living. At the 1829 Exhibition, with friends' help, he entered a striking piece—a young man in prison inspired by Gerard Dou—which drew attention even from Charles X. Madame bought the painting and commissioned more.

Fougeres’s success rested more on imitation than innovation. Despite criticism, his modesty and perseverance won supporters and a reputation for dedication. He furnished a studio and completed commissions punctually, a rarity among artists. By thirty-seven he had produced around two hundred paintings, achieving a style that appealed to the bourgeoisie. Friends often preferred his character to his art and regretted his career choice, yet he offered sound advice and maintained a keen appreciation for beauty.

After the July Revolution he continued submitting to the Salon, lived frugally, and managed finances with his notary Cardot. He dreamed of a comfortable artistic life and aspired to join the Institute. One day the wealthy Vervelle family visited for portraits. The father, once a bottle-dealer, admired Fougeres’ Legion of Honor cross. The family liked his work, Pierre was charmed by their daughter Virginie, and the Vervelles began to see him as a suitable match, intertwining personal hopes with professional ambitions.

When Fougeres began painting Mademoiselle Virginie, Grassou had effectively become part of the Vervelle household, drawn to his neat studio as a second home. Near the end of the sitting, Joseph Bridau burst in, disheveled, begged five hundred francs to meet creditors, and criticized Grassou’s style, urging acceptance of nature’s imperfections. Bridau left; Madame Vervelle feared he had spoiled the portrait, but Grassou assured her he had improved it.

Flattered by their admiration, Grassou accepted an invitation to the Vervelles’ country house at Ville d’Avray. They received him with lavish hospitality—admiring grounds, a grand dinner, and fawning praise as if he were a celebrated artist—then proudly showed their gallery, convinced their collection rivaled the king’s. Examining the canvases, Grassou was shocked to find many were his own works, long sold to the dealer Elie Magus. Vervelle boasted the collection’s value at three hundred thousand francs; Grassou, with some irony, whispered that he had sold those same pictures for far less. Vervelle promised to double his daughter’s dowry if Grassou could prove the claim. The revelation raised Grassou’s standing; when he later completed their portraits he gifted them, securing their favor.

Today Pierre Grassou is regarded as a competent portrait painter, earning about twenty thousand francs a year. He lives comfortably with his wife’s six-thousand-franc dowry, paints bourgeois portraits for five hundred francs each, and even received the Legion of Honor. Yet he remains insecure—convinced other artists mock him and critics ignore him—still craving Academy recognition and secretly swapping weaker works in the Ville d’Avray gallery for those by celebrated painters. His mediocrity, tempered by kindness and generosity, renders him complex and oddly sympathetic.
