PIERRE GRASSOU

Since the revolution of 1830 the Salon has lost its former force. Once a selective exhibition that honored a small number of works and provoked lively debate—helping to create names like Delacroix and Ingres—it has become overwhelmed by sheer numbers. The public is numbed; exhibitions close quickly, individual pieces lose impact, and the excitement around art fades. Before 1817 only a limited number of paintings were shown; now galleries overflow, diluting quality and attention.

The Salon’s essence is dissipated in this chaos. What once was refined selection is now a tumultuous bazaar where great artists are obscured. Decamps’ notable works, for example, would have stood out more amid fewer high-quality pieces than they do amid mediocrity. Ironically, as the number of artists grows, talk of unrecognized genius increases. When the Academy judged strictly, true talent was acknowledged; without that judgment, art suffers and the Salon risks perishing.

Amid the swelling catalogue stands Pierre Grassou, nicknamed “Fougeres.” In 1832 he lived in a cramped rue de Navarin apartment with a studio overlooking Montmartre. Modest and tidy, his studio reflected his careful nature and limited means. Avoiding the burdens of history painting, he focused on easel and genre work.

One December morning Grassou, preparing to work, was visited by Elie Magus, a picture dealer. Magus habitually bought Grassou’s paintings cheaply and proposed a business arrangement: Grassou would paint portraits of the bourgeois Vervelle family, who wished to enter the art world. Their daughter had a substantial dowry, and Magus hinted that marriage could benefit Grassou financially.

Grassou, living simply and solitary, was startled by talk of marriage. The idea of painting three family portraits, however, appealed as a chance to establish himself as a portraitist. Magus left to find the Vervelles, leaving Grassou to weigh the opportunity’s potential.

To understand Grassou’s situation, we look back. He studied under various masters—Servin and Schinner among them—but never fully grasped their techniques. His education was marked by a lack of recognition; he was often mocked for mediocrity. Gentle and diligent, he nevertheless failed to stand out among flashier or more eccentric peers.

His first Salon attempt, a village-wedding scene in 1819, was rejected. Rather than despair, he sought Schinner’s critique, who bluntly advised him to give up painting. Still determined, Grassou labored to refine the rejected piece, only to sell it to Magus for a meager sum. That sale began a long, difficult career of perseverance against discouragement.

As Grassou navigated his art life he remained committed to his craft, embodying the struggles and hopes of many contemporaries. His story reflects broader themes of ambition, recognition, and the harsh realities of an art world where circumstance often overshadows talent.

For several days Pierre Fougeres lingered near Elie's shop hoping his painting would attract notice, but it went unnoticed. After a week it vanished; when he casually asked, Elie Magus said he had sold it and would buy more if Fougeres produced Flemish interiors, anatomy lessons, and landscapes. Overjoyed, Fougeres felt validated.

He missed the irony in Elie's manner that a more cynical artist might have seen. He met his friend Joseph Bridau but, consumed with painting, could not enjoy the evening. Back home he sketched furiously, inspired by newfound confidence, bought supplies, hired models, and devoted himself to his craft.

After two months he produced four paintings. Schinner and Bridau dismissed them as derivative and suggested he try literature; undeterred, Fougeres retouched the canvases and sold them to Elie for twenty-five francs apiece, barely breaking even. He noticed a strange mist over his paintings in the shop but dismissed it as imagination.

Over seven years he honed his skills, producing competent but not exceptional work. Elie sold the paintings, letting Fougeres earn a modest living. At the 1829 Exhibition, with friends' help, he entered a powerful piece—a young man in prison inspired by Gerard Douw—which drew significant attention, even from Charles X. Madame purchased the painting and commissioned more.

Fougeres' success came from his ability to imitate rather than innovate, a lesson he learned well. Despite criticism, his modesty and perseverance won him supporters, and he gained a reputation for dedication. With his earnings he furnished a studio and completed commissions punctually, a rarity among artists.

By thirty-seven he had produced around two hundred paintings, achieving a style that appealed to the bourgeoisie. His friends often preferred his character to his art and regretted his career choice, yet he still gave sound advice and maintained a keen appreciation for beauty.

After the July Revolution he continued submitting work to the Salon, living frugally and managing finances with his notary Cardot. He dreamed of a comfortable life as an artist, aspiring to join the Institute and gain recognition.

One day the wealthy Vervelle family visited his studio for portraits. The father, once a bottle-dealer, was impressed by Fougeres' Legion of Honor cross. The family admired his work, and Pierre was charmed by their daughter Virginie. On subsequent sittings they grew familiar, discussing their art collection and financial stability.

During sessions Pierre flattered the family and endeared himself to them. The father boasted of their valuable collection, while the notary praised Fougeres' character and financial prudence, stressing his ability to provide for a family. This reputation strengthened the Vervelles' interest, and they began to see him as a suitable match for Virginie.

As sittings progressed Pierre's feelings for Virginie deepened and he began to envision a future with her, despite initially focusing on his art. The Vervelles' admiration and talk of his success further fueled his aspirations, intertwining personal hopes with professional ambitions.

The day Fougeres began painting Mademoiselle Virginie, he was already practically part of the Vervelle family. They liked the tidy studio, which felt like a second home. Near the end of the sitting, Joseph Bridau burst in, disheveled and startling them; he scanned the room, approached Grassou, and struggled with his coat. "Wood is dear," he remarked, and asked for five hundred francs to fend off creditors. Grassou agreed to write a note. Bridau then criticized his painting style and urged him to accept nature's imperfections.

As Bridau left, Madame Vervelle worried about his influence on Virginie's portrait, but Grassou reassured her he had only improved it. Intrigued by the artist’s supposed genius, the Vervelles invited Fougeres to their country house the following Sunday, hoping to flatter him with hospitality and their paintings.

Flattered, Grassou went to Ville d'Avray and was warmly received. They showed their well-kept grounds, gave a lavish dinner, and treated him like a celebrated artist. After the meal they led him to their picture gallery, eager for his opinion on a collection they believed rivaled King Louis-Philippe's.

As Grassou examined the canvases, he was shocked to recognize many as his own works long ago sold to the dealer Elie Magus. Vervelle boasted the collection was worth three hundred thousand francs; Grassou, aware of the irony, whispered that he had sold those pictures for far less. Impressed, Vervelle promised to double his daughter's dowry if Grassou could substantiate his claim. The revelation raised Grassou’s standing with the family, who continued to regard him as a great artist. When he completed their portraits he presented them as gifts, further cementing their bond.

Today Pierre Grassou is known as a competent portrait painter, earning about twenty thousand francs a year. He lives comfortably with his wife, whose dowry was six thousand francs, and paints family portraits for five hundred francs each. His financial success has enhanced his reputation, and he even received the Legion of Honor for his conduct during civil unrest.

Despite these achievements, Grassou is insecure, convinced other artists mock him and critics overlook him. He strives for recognition—hoping for the Academy—while secretly replacing his poorer works with those of more celebrated artists in the Ville d'Avray collection. His mediocrity, tempered by kindness and generosity, makes him a complex and sympathetic figure in the artistic community.
