Have you felt unease and sadness in the crowded galleries since the revolution of 1830? Balzac argues the true Salon has been buried under an excess of artists. Where strict selection once showcased the finest works and provoked debate—building reputations like Delacroix and Ingres—exhibitions now overwhelm viewers. Limited displays have become floods: individual works lose impact, shows close quickly, and the Salon’s role as arbiter of taste has degenerated into a tumultuous bazaar. Great artists are obscured by mediocrity, and talk of unrecognized genius grows as the Academy’s judgment wanes.

Amid this swelling catalogue stands Pierre Grassou, nicknamed “Fougeres.” In 1832 he lives in a cramped rue de Navarin apartment with a small studio near Montmartre. Tidy and careful, Grassou is a genre painter who avoids historical subjects, producing easel paintings suited to his modest means.

One December morning the dealer Elie Magus, who habitually bought Grassou’s works cheaply, proposes a commission: portraits of the bourgeois Vervelle family, eager to enter the art world. Magus hints the daughter’s dowry—and even marriage—could secure Grassou’s future. Startled, Grassou nonetheless sees a chance to establish himself.

Balzac traces his past: trained under Servin and Schinner, mocked for mediocrity, rejected from the 1819 Salon and advised to quit, yet he persisted, selling a refined piece to Magus for little. Grassou’s tale embodies many contemporaries’ struggle: ambition constrained by limited talent, hopeful striving, and the market’s power over artistic merit.

For days Pierre Fougeres waited near Elie Magus’s shop hoping his painting would attract attention; when it disappeared after a week Elie claimed he had sold it and asked Fougeres to produce Flemish interiors, anatomy lessons, and landscapes. Overjoyed, Fougeres felt validated, missing the ironic skepticism a more cynical artist might have shown.

He ran into his friend Joseph Bridau but, absorbed in painting, could not enjoy the evening. At home he sketched, bought supplies, hired models, and after two months produced four canvases. Schinner and Bridau called them derivative; Fougeres retouched them and sold each to Elie for twenty-five francs, barely breaking even. He noticed a strange mist over the paintings in the shop but dismissed it.

Over seven years he developed competent, unremarkable work that brought modest income. At the 1829 Exhibition a prison scene, inspired by Gerard Dou, attracted notice—even from Charles X—and Madame bought it and commissioned more. His success rested more on imitation than innovation, but his modesty, perseverance, punctuality, and steady output (about two hundred paintings by thirty-seven) won bourgeois buyers and supporters.

After the July Revolution he lived frugally, managed money with notary Cardot, and aspired to the Institute. A commission from the wealthy Vervelle family introduced him to their daughter Virginie; the family admired his Legion of Honor cross and began to view him as a suitable match, blending personal and professional hopes.

When Fougeres began painting Mademoiselle Virginie, Grassou had already joined the Vervelle household and frequented his neat studio. Near the sitting’s end Joseph Bridau burst in, disheveled, begged five hundred francs for creditors, and criticized Grassou’s style; Madame Vervelle feared the portrait ruined, but Grassou reassured her.

He accepted an invitation to the Vervelles’ Ville d’Avray country house, where lavish hospitality and fawning praise treated him like a celebrated artist. In their gallery Vervelle boasted a three-hundred-thousand-franc collection—many canvases Grassou recognized as his, long sold to dealer Elie Magus. Vervelle hinted he’d bought them for far less and offered to double his daughter’s dowry if Grassou could prove it. The revelation raised Grassou’s standing; he later finished and gifted their portraits.

Grassou now earns about twenty thousand francs, aided by his wife’s six-thousand-franc dowry, charges five hundred francs per portrait, and holds the Legion of Honor. Yet he remains insecure, craving Academy recognition and secretly swapping weaker works for famous ones; his mediocrity, softened by generosity, makes him oddly sympathetic.
