**Pierre Grassou**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*

**Dedication**  
To The Lieutenant-Colonel of Artillery, Periollas, As a Testimony of the Affectionate Esteem of the Author, De Balzac

---

Following the 1830 revolution, the art exhibition scene has transformed, creating unease among viewers. The once-respected Salon has become a chaotic bazaar, losing its ability to showcase the finest art. Overwhelmed by the volume of submissions, the public struggles to engage with the works, resulting in a decline in passionate discourse that once surrounded celebrated artists like Delacroix and Ingres. The Salon's expansion has diluted its quality, making it difficult for great artists to stand out.

The current art world is rife with complaints about unrecognized genius, even as many mediocre works gain acceptance. The lack of a discerning jury has led to declining artistic standards, putting the future of art at risk.

Among the lesser-known artists is Pierre Grassou, a painter from Fougeres, who embodies the struggles of many in the art community. In 1832, Grassou lived in a modest Paris studio, characterized by simple furnishings. As a genre painter, he was content with limited ambitions and lacked the desire to pursue high art.

One December morning, while preparing to paint, Grassou was visited by Elie Magus, a shrewd picture dealer. Magus proposed that Grassou paint portraits of the bourgeois Vervelle family, who were eager to enter the art world. The family included a daughter with a substantial dowry, which intrigued Grassou, despite his initial reluctance to marry.

Grassou's background reveals a diligent but unremarkable artist. He studied under various masters, including Servin and Schinner, but never distinguished himself. His gentle demeanor and mediocrity earned him the nickname "Fougeres," which he accepted with humility. After moving to Paris, he struggled to establish himself, facing rejection at his first exhibition in 1819 with a painting that failed to impress the jury.

Instead of succumbing to despair, Grassou sought feedback from Schinner, who candidly advised him to abandon painting due to his lack of talent. Undeterred, Grassou revised his work but ultimately sold it to Magus for a meager sum. This transaction marked the beginning of his career, albeit one filled with challenges and modest success.

Grassou's life reflects the broader struggles of artists in a changing cultural landscape, where the value of art is increasingly questioned. His journey illustrates the tension between ambition and reality as he navigates the complexities of the art world while remaining true to his humble origins.

################################################
For several days, Pierre Fougeres lingered near Elie's shop, hoping for recognition of his painting, which ultimately vanished. When he inquired, Elie informed him it had sold and offered to buy more of his work. Overjoyed, Fougeres felt validated in his talent despite others' skepticism. He returned to his studio, inspired, and worked tirelessly on new pieces, eventually producing four paintings that were critiqued as imitative.

Despite the criticism, Elie bought the paintings for a modest price, allowing Fougeres to live frugally while continuing to create art. After years of hard work, he gained recognition at the 1829 Exhibition, where one painting depicting a condemned man garnered significant attention and was purchased by Madame la Dauphine. This success marked a turning point in his career, leading to further commissions and a modest income.

Now financially stable, Fougeres furnished a studio and continued to produce art, remaining humble and dedicated to his craft. His friends admired his perseverance, even if they found his work lacking in originality. He maintained a simple lifestyle, investing his earnings with a notary, Cardot, while dreaming of greater artistic achievements and recognition.

One day, the wealthy Vervelle family approached Fougeres, eager for portraits. Their admiration flattered him, especially as he became drawn to their daughter, Virginie. As he painted their portraits, he grew enchanted by her, despite initial reservations about their social status. The Vervelles, impressed by his reputation and financial stability, began to consider him a potential match for their daughter.

As the sittings progressed, the Vervelles grew more familiar with Fougeres, who reciprocated their enthusiasm. They discussed their art collection and praised his work, solidifying their interest in him. The notary's positive remarks about Fougeres' character and financial prudence further enhanced the family's favorable view, making them more inclined to see him as a suitable partner for Virginie.

Caught up in the charm of the Vervelle family and his growing feelings for Virginie, Fougeres envisioned a future with her. He rationalized that wealth and stability were essential for happiness, believing that marrying into the Vervelle family could fulfill his dreams. As he painted, he contemplated the intertwining possibilities of love and success with his artistic ambitions.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
On the day Fougeres began painting Mademoiselle Virginie, he felt like part of the Vervelle family, who viewed the studio as a second home. During a sitting, Joseph Bridau burst in, frantic and disheveled, seeking financial help from Grassou, who was painting Virginie. Bridau's chaotic entrance shocked the Vervelles, who were embarrassed. He requested 500 francs, claiming creditors were after him, and Grassou reluctantly agreed to write a note for his notary.

As Bridau critiqued Grassou's work, he urged him to embrace nature in his painting. After Bridau left, Madame Vervelle worried about the impact of his visit on Virginie's portrait, but Grassou reassured her it had improved. Eager to impress the artist, Vervelle invited Fougeres to their country house for an enjoyable experience.

Grassou accepted the invitation and was warmly welcomed by the Vervelles at their villa in Ville d'Avray. They proudly showcased their meticulously maintained grounds and prepared a lavish dinner. Later, they took Grassou to their picture gallery, where he was astonished to find many of his own paintings misattributed to famous masters like Rubens and Rembrandt.

As Grassou feigned admiration, he realized the extent of the deception. Vervelle boasted about the value of his collection, while Grassou privately noted that he had sold those paintings to dealer Elie Magus for a fraction of their supposed worth. Impressed, Vervelle promised to double his daughter's dowry if Grassou could prove his claims.

Despite the absurdity, Grassou's reputation grew, and he became known as a fine portrait painter, earning a comfortable living and marrying into the Vervelle family. He lived happily with them, maintaining a successful career while grappling with the insecurity of being mocked by fellow artists. Aspiring to join the Academy, he secretly replaced his lesser works in the Vervelle collection with those of more renowned artists.

Grassou's life exemplified the complexities of mediocrity and ambition in the art world, where he was both respected and ridiculed. A devoted husband and father, he struggled with the feeling that his artistic peers looked down on him, yet he persisted in his work, hoping for greater recognition and success.
