In Paris, balls often split into two parts: a formal public party of superficial pleasantries and a more intimate follow-up where genuine conversation and laughter flourish. The latter, often hosted by women like Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches, preserves the old spirit of French wit and allows free-flowing ideas and camaraderie.

One evening at Mademoiselle des Touches' salon, notable figures including the Minister de Marsay gathered for lively discourse. The atmosphere—charged with wit and easy exchange—showcased the conversational style Paris is known for as anecdotes and confessions wove together.

As the night progressed, talk turned to de Marsay's early love: a passionate affair with a woman six years his senior. He recalled youthful infatuation marked by intense devotion and secrecy, maintaining a façade of indifference to shield their relationship. He described their love as a careful dance of appearances and feeling—riding past her carriage, exchanging silent signals through the flowers she carried—the bond thriving on mutual admiration and the thrill of concealment while he remained blind to betrayal.

A turning point came when he fell ill and sent a note canceling a meeting. Consumed by jealousy, he nevertheless visited her and saw her with another man, igniting a storm of emotion. Yet her calm demeanor and tender concern briefly soothed his doubts.

His narrative darkened as he grappled with the sense of betrayal and a profound internal shift: the end of innocence and the onset of a more cynical view of love. He wrestled with thoughts of revenge but ultimately asserted that true love should transcend the desire for vengeance.

The story resonated with the salon, prompting reflection on love, jealousy, and the social expectations that shape relationships—an evocative reminder of trust's fragility and love's transformative power.

Cured of my cold and my naïve passion, I embarked on an affair with a charming heroine whose beauty contrasted with my earlier, deceptive angel. I avoided quarreling with this clever woman, suspecting that true love might not equal the pleasures of her skilled deceit; such refined hypocrisy can pass for virtue.

I sought an artist on the Rue Boucher to make a keepsake from my hair for my new angel. He monopolized hair mementos and showed me work surpassing fairy tales, explaining current fashions like marking linen with hair. When I recognized a handkerchief he had crafted, my doubts about the authenticity of women’s affections were confirmed. I left still believing in pleasure but skeptical about love.

Two months later I was in this ethereal being’s boudoir, holding her hand and testing sentiment. In a tender moment I tried to elicit her sweetest falsehoods. Charlotte professed her love, declaring I was the only man in her world and that she had sought ways to bind me to her forever.

Her declaration offended the women listening to de Marsay, who felt he caricatured their airs; as I might have believed her, I bluntly asked, “When are you to marry the Duke?” The directness startled her; her reaction revealed guilt. She feigned astonishment, but I pressed on, suggesting she should marry him, since he was rich and devout.

Charlotte fell to her knees, insisting she loved no one but me. I urged truthfulness; she swore before God. I confessed my doubts about her fidelity; she quickly turned it against me, claiming I no longer loved her. She tried to escape but curiosity made her ask what I had seen. I revealed that I had noticed her attending mass with the Duke several times, which she dismissed as jealousy.

I argued her marriage to the Duke would elevate her socially and advised her to embrace the role; the Duke was virtuous and would expect a prude. Charlotte, in tears, lamented that if I had spoken sooner we could have been happy. I held it was too late; she had gone too far with the Duke.

She fretted about the Duke’s suspicions; I reassured her he likely suspected nothing, though she needed to prove devotion. As she paced, her demeanor shifted: she held out her hand, called me noble and charming, and vowed never to forget me. Her tactics were admirable, and I played the distressed lover. She then asked what would become of me, and I revealed advances from another woman. This startled Charlotte, who accused me of unfaithfulness; I countered that she had too much wit for her heart to suffer.

Charlotte declared disillusionment with love, claiming women could never be truly loved. I acknowledged her anger but argued taking love seriously in society was like romanticizing an actress. She accused me of betrayal; I feigned submission and asked if she would forget her grievances once she became a duchess.

Her bitter response confirmed she still felt wronged, and I realized I had successfully distanced myself. I had studied the lives of successful men with women and believed I had mastered the art of retreat.

The women in the room reacted with sympathy for Charlotte, while de Marsay bore a slight smile. The conversation shifted to the decline of social distinctions. Lady Dudley noted women often did not view their marriages as acts of inconstancy. De Marsay lamented the erosion of the aristocracy, saying duchesses and marquises were vanishing and the Civil Code had leveled hierarchies.

Blondet observed that aristocracy now began with viscountesses, while de Marsay thought countesses might survive in some form. He reflected that the last great ladies had vanished with the previous century’s splendor and that the Code had inadvertently created a new class of women.

The Comte de Vandenesse remarked the current system allowed anyone to claim nobility; Lord Dudley added that property had not been properly organized, hastening the aristocracy’s decline.

Joseph Bridau said the beauty of womanhood had diminished and the great lady’s fan was broken. Daniel d’Arthez noted the aristocracy’s retreat left behind women who might once have shaped society.

The talk turned to the modern “perfect lady” as a product of the age: she embodies good taste and grace but lacks the grandeur of past great ladies. Mademoiselle des Touches questioned the notion of progress, while Madame de Nucingen argued a woman’s worth was no longer tied to lineage but to character and presentation.

When Count Adam Laginski asked what made a “perfect lady,” Émile Blondet described her as someone who exudes elegance and refinement, commanding respect and admiration—graceful and poised across social settings.

The discussion underscored modern femininity’s complexity: the “perfect lady” navigates societal expectations with skill and charm, blending beauty and intellect to captivate those around her.

“Now, you will not talk for half an hour with a bourgeoise without her mentioning her husband,” Blondet began, gravely. “Yet a married lady will hide her husband so cleverly that you may not discover him. Often you only notice her glancing at a middle-aged man with a decoration who bows and leaves. She has ordered her carriage and departs.

“You are not the rose, but you have been with the rose, and you go to bed under a golden canopy of a delightful dream, which may linger even after Sleep has opened the ivory gates of dreams.

“At home, the lady sees no one before four; she is clever enough to keep you waiting. Her house is tastefully arranged; her luxury is for daily use and regularly refreshed. You will find no dusty shades or remnants of wrappings. The staircase is warm, and flowers abound—her only accepted gift, and only from select individuals, for they symbolize a promise. Fashionable trinkets are scattered about, but not in a museum-like way. She will sit by the fire in a low chair, not rising to greet you. Her conversation at home differs from that at the ball; there, she was our creditor; here, she owes you the pleasure of her wit. These are the nuances she masters. What she wants in you is a man to expand her circle, an object for her attentions. To draw you into her drawing-room she will be enchantingly charming. This shows how isolated women are today and why they desire a little world of their own, where they can shine like a constellation. Conversation often relies on generalities.

“Yes,” de Marsay agreed, “you have pinpointed the flaw of our age. The epigram—a volume in a word—no longer strikes at people or things but at sordid events, and fades quickly.”

“Hence,” Blondet went on, “a lady’s intelligence, if she has any, lies in casting doubt on everything. Here is the difference: the townswoman is virtuous; the lady hesitates and struggles with her virtue. This uncertainty is one of the last graces left to her by our dreadful times. She rarely attends church but will discuss religion; if you express free thought, she will try to convert you, falling into familiar phrases and gestures: ‘For shame! I thought you had too much sense to attack religion. Society is tottering; you deprive it of its support. Religion means you and me; it is property and the future of our children! Let us not be selfish! Individualism is the disease of the age, and religion is the only remedy; it unites families which your laws put asunder,’ and so on. She then delivers a neo-Christian discourse peppered with political notions that is neither Catholic nor Protestant—but very moral!—a blend of modern doctrines at odds with each other.”

The women laughed at Blondet’s satire.

“This explanation, dear Count Adam,” Blondet said to the Pole, “will show you that the ‘perfect lady’ embodies intellectual and political muddle, surrounded by flashy yet fleeting products of an industry that destroys its wares to replace them with new ones. When you leave her, you think: ‘She certainly has superior ideas!’ You believe it because she has delicately probed your heart and asked for your secrets; she feigns ignorance to learn everything. There are things she never knows, even when she does. You alone will feel uneasy, knowing nothing of her heart’s state. The great ladies of old flaunted their affairs; today’s lady arranges her passions like a musical score, with notes and rests, pauses and sharps. A merely weak woman avoids compromising her love, her husband, or her children’s future. Name, position, and fortune no longer shield her; the aristocracy no longer unites to protect her. Unlike the great lady of the past, she cannot crush anything underfoot; she is the one who would be crushed. Thus she practices Jesuitical compromises, shifting between propriety and anonymous passion. She fears her servants as an Englishwoman fears a divorce trial. So free at a ball and charming in public, she is a slave at home; she is never independent except in perfect privacy or in theory. Her task is to maintain her position as a lady.

“For today, a woman repudiated by her husband, reduced to a meager allowance, without a carriage, luxury, or opera box, is no longer a wife, maid, or townswoman; she is adrift, a chattel. The Carmelites will not accept a married woman; it would be bigamy. Would her lover still want her? That is the question. Thus your perfect lady may provoke calumny, but rarely outright slander.”

“It is all so horribly true,” said the Princesse de Cadignan.

“And so,” Blondet added, “our ‘perfect lady’ stands between English hypocrisy and the frankness of the eighteenth century—a bastard system symptomatic of an age where nothing that emerges resembles what has vanished, where transition leads nowhere, and everything is a matter of degree; great figures fade, and distinction becomes purely personal. I am convinced no woman, even born near a throne, can acquire before twenty-five the encyclopedic knowledge of trifles, the practice of maneuvering, the important small things, the musical tones and harmony of coloring, the angelic mischief and innocent cunning, the speech and silence, the seriousness and banter, the wit and obtuseness, the diplomacy and ignorance that make the perfect lady.”

“And where,” Mademoiselle des Touches asked, “would you put the female author? Is she a perfect lady, a woman comme il faut?”

“When she has no genius, she is a woman comme il n’en faut pas,” Blondet replied, emphasizing the phrase in a look that might be read as praise for Camille Maupin. “This epigram is not mine, but Napoleon’s,” he added.

“Do not blame Napoleon for that,” Canalis interjected. “He was foolishly jealous of literary genius—he had his flaws. Who can explain, depict, or understand Napoleon? A man with arms folded, who did everything: the greatest, most concentrated, most caustic force; a singular genius who spread an armed civilization without fixing it anywhere. He could do everything because he willed everything: a prodigious will, conquering illness by battle, yet destined to die of disease in bed after living amid balls and bullets; a man with law and sword in his head, word and deed; a clear spirit that foresaw everything but his own fall; a capricious politician who risked men by handfuls for economy yet spared three diplomats—Talleyrand, Pozzo de Borgo, and Metternich—whose deaths might have saved the French Empire. To him nature, as a rare privilege, gave a heart in a bronze frame: mirthful at midnight among women, the next morning manipulating Europe as a child splashes water for amusement. Hypocritical yet generous; loving both tawdriness and simplicity; devoid of taste yet protecting the arts; and despite contradictions, truly great by instinct or temperament—Caesar at twenty-five, Cromwell at thirty. He improvised public works, empires, kings, codes, verses, a romance—with more range than precision. He aimed to make all Europe France; after forcing us to shift the laws of gravitation, he left us poorer than before, and he, who took an empire by name, lost his name on its frontier in a sea of blood. A man of thought and action who understood Desaix and Fouché.”

“All despotism and all justice at the right moments. The true king!” de Marsay declared.

“Ah! what a pleasure it is to digest what you talk about,” Baron de Nucingen remarked.

“But do you think the treat we are giving you is common?” Joseph Bridau asked. “If you had to pay for the charms of conversation as you do for those of dancing or music, your fortune would be inadequate! There is no second performance of the same flash of wit.”

“And are we really so diminished as these gentlemen think?” the Princesse de Cadignan asked the women, smiling skeptically. “Because, under a regime that makes everything small, you prefer small dishes, small rooms, small pictures, small newspapers, small books—does that prove women have grown smaller? Why should the human heart change because you change your coat? In all ages, passions remain the same. I know of beautiful devotion and sublime sufferings that lack the publicity—the glory—that once gave luster to the errors of some women. But even if one has not saved a King of France, one is not less an Agnès Sorel. Do you believe our dear Marquise d’Espard is not the equal of Madame Doublet or Madame du Deffant, in whose rooms so much evil was spoken and done? Is not Taglioni a match for Camargo? Malibran the equal of Saint-Huberti? Are not our poets superior to those of the eighteenth century? If we lack a style of our own now, did not the Empire have its distinguishing stamp as the age of Louis XV did, and was not its splendor fabulous? Have the sciences lost anything?”

“I completely agree, madame; the women of this age are truly great,” the Comte de Vandenesse replied. “When posterity looks back, will not Madame Récamier appear in proportions as fine as the best of the past? We have made so much history that historians will be lacking. The age of Louis XIV had but one Madame de Sévigné; we have a thousand in Paris who certainly write better and do not publish their letters. Whether the Frenchwoman is called ‘perfect lady’ or great lady, she will always be the woman among women.

“Blondet has painted the fascinations of a woman of the day; but when needed, this creature who bristles or shows off, who echoes Mr. This and Mr. That, could be heroic. And your faults, mesdames, are all the more poetic because they are surrounded by greater perils. I have seen much of the world; in cases where the illegality of your feelings might be excused, I have often witnessed what you may call Providence inevitably overwhelming those we consider light women.”

“I hope,” Madame de Vandenesse said, “that we can be great in other ways——”

“Oh, let the Comte de Vandenesse preach to us!” Madame de Serizy exclaimed.

“With all the more reason because he has preached much by example,” the Baronne de Nucingen added.

“On my honor!” General de Montriveau said, “in all the dramas—a word you love,” he said, glancing at Blondet—“in which the finger of God has been visible, the most frightful I ever knew was very near being by my act——”

“Well, tell us all about it!” Lady Barimore cried. “I love to shudder!”

“It is the taste of a virtuous woman,” de Marsay replied, glancing at Lord Dudley’s lovely daughter.

“During the campaign of 1812,” General de Montriveau began, “I was the involuntary cause of a terrible disaster which may interest you, Doctor Bianchon,” turning to me, “since, while you devote yourself to the body, you concern yourself with the mind; it may help explain some problems of the will.

“I was on my second campaign, enjoying danger like the young and foolish lieutenant I once was. At the Beresina, discipline had collapsed. The army was a medley of nations, men making their way north to south. Soldiers would drive a starving general away. After crossing the river, disorder did not diminish. I had come alone, without food, from the marshes of Zembin, seeking a house to shelter me. Finding none, I saw a wretched Polish farm—one of those one-room wooden cabins where the smaller end stores forage.

“In the twilight, faint smoke rose above the house. Hoping for comrades, I approached. Inside, the table was laid. Several officers and a woman were eating potatoes, some horseflesh broiled over charcoal, and frozen beetroots. I recognized artillery captains from my former regiment. I was welcomed with a shout that would have amazed me beyond the Beresina; but then, amid the cold, my comrades were warm, they had food, and the room strewn with straw promised a delightful night. My comrades could be philanthropists for nothing; one of the commonest ways. I sat on a straw bundle.

“At the end of the table, by the door to the straw room, sat my old colonel—one of the most extraordinary men I met among the mixed company of soldiers. He was Italian. When Southern fairness is truly fine, it is sublime. He was tall—eight or nine inches above average—well proportioned, perhaps a little stout, but prodigiously powerful and clean-limbed, with abundant black curls against a complexion as white as a woman’s; small hands, a shapely foot, a pleasant mouth, and an aquiline nose that whitened when he was angry. His irascibility was beyond belief; no one could remain calm in his presence. He had taken a singular fancy to me and thought everything I did was right. When angry, his brow knit into a horseshoe-shaped mark more terrifying than his magnetic blue eyes; his whole frame quivered, and his strength became almost unbounded.

“He spoke with a strong guttural roll that gave force to his words. When calm, his blue eyes were angelic, his brow smooth. On parade with the army of Italy, no man could compare to him. Passion thrives on contrast; he exerted an irresistible influence on women”—and the general looked at the Princesse de Cadignan—“yet by a singular fatality he was not a lady-killer.”

“To illustrate his violence: we were dragging guns up a narrow road when another artillery regiment met us. Their colonel wanted our captain to give way; the captain refused. The wheel of the first gun struck our captain’s leg, breaking it and throwing him from his horse. Our Colonel, nearby, galloped up like a madman, roaring. He drew his sword, and in two seconds our Colonel’s opponent lay with his skull split. The soldiers backed away—quickly.

“The captain, nearly crushed, lay yelping in a puddle. His wife was a beautiful Sicilian from Messina—Rosina—who was not indifferent to our Colonel. This inflamed him; he felt pledged to protect the husband as he would the woman.

“In the hovel beyond Zembin, the captain sat opposite me, his wife at the other end facing the Colonel. Rosina was dark and fiery, thin from marches, her face dusted like fruit in the drought of a highroad. Ragged, exhausted, hair dishevelled under a shawl, she still had the graces of a woman—small mouth, white teeth, a figure that suggested love despite misery. Rosina’s frame seemed fragile but was wiry and springy. Her husband, a Piedmontese gentleman, had an ironical simplicity in his face, brave and well-informed, seeming unaware of the three-year bond between his wife and the Colonel. I attributed this unconcern to Italian manners or a domestic secret; yet his thin, restless underlip turned down at the corners suggested a cruelty beneath a phlegmatic exterior.

“Conversation was dull when I arrived. We shared misadventures, reflections on the campaign, the generals, the Russians, and the cold. After his meager meal the Colonel wiped his moustache, bid us goodnight, shot a black look at the Italian woman, saying ‘Rosina?’ and went into the hay-filled barn to sleep. The question’s meaning was clear. The young wife made a gesture of annoyance at seeing her thralldom flaunted, with a presentiment perhaps of her fate. Rosina stayed in her place.

“A minute later, perhaps when the Colonel was snug in straw, he called again—more brutally—‘Rosina?’ She turned pale, rose, passed behind us, and went to him.

“All sat in utter silence; I, offendingly, began to laugh, and then they all laughed too. ‘Tu ridi?—you laugh?’ said the husband.

“‘On my honor, old comrade,’ I replied, serious again, ‘I confess I was wrong; I ask your pardon a thousand times, and if you are not satisfied, I am ready to give you satisfaction.’

“‘Oh! it is not you who are wrong, it is I!’ he said coldly.

“We lay down and soon slept.

“Next morning, each set out alone, driven by the same selfishness that made our rout one of the most horrible dramas of self-seeking, melancholy, and horror ever enacted. Nevertheless, about seven or eight hundred paces on, we met again and walked together like geese in a flock. The same necessity urged us.

“From a knoll still overlooking the farmhouse, we heard sounds like lion roars and bull bellows—a noise unlike any cry. Amid that horrible roar came a woman’s feeble scream. We looked; the house had been barricaded and was in flames. Smoke swirled with hoarse cries and a pungent smell. A few yards behind, the captain quietly approached to join our caravan; we stared, but he, understanding, pointed to his breast with his right forefinger and, waving his left toward the fire, said, ‘Son’io.’ (‘It is I.’)

“We all walked on without speaking.”

“There is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep,” de Marsay remarked.

“It would be frightful to leave with this image in our memory,” Madame de Montcornet said. “I shall dream of it——”

“And what was the punishment of Monsieur de Marsay’s ‘First’?” Lord Dudley asked, smiling.

“When the English jest, their foils have the buttons on,” Blondet replied.

“Monsieur Bianchon can tell us, for he saw her dying,” de Marsay said, turning to me.

“Yes,” I replied; “and her end was one of the most beautiful I ever witnessed. The Duke and I had spent the night by the dying woman’s pillow; pulmonary consumption, in its last stage, left no hope; she had taken the sacrament the day before. The Duke had fallen asleep. The Duchess, waking around four, signed to me with a friendly smile to leave him to rest while she was about to die. She had become incredibly thin, but her face retained its sublime outline. Her pallor made her skin like porcelain with a light within. Her bright eyes and color contrasted with this languidly elegant complexion, and her countenance expressed calm pity. The silence was absolute. The room, softly lit by a lamp, resembled every sickroom at the hour of death.

“At that moment the clock struck. The Duke awoke, despairing at having fallen asleep. I did not see the gesture of impatience he made, though anyone but the dying woman might have mistaken it. A busy statesman, always thinking of France’s interests, the Duke had thousand odd ways on the surface—traits that often make a genius look mad, explained by the demands of intellect. He sat by his wife and looked fixedly at her. The dying woman reached and took his hand, and in a low but agitated voice said, ‘My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?’ Then she died, looking at him.”

“The stories the doctor tells us,” the Comte de Vandenesse remarked, “always leave a deep impression.”

“But a sweet one,” Mademoiselle des Touches said, rising.
