The story begins in a lawyer's office where clerk Simonnin mocks an old man, Colonel Chabert, who is believed dead after the battle of Eylau. Despite the clerks' ridicule, Chabert insists on waiting to speak with attorney M. Derville, demonstrating his determination. When he reveals his identity, the clerks are shocked, realizing he is not a beggar but a significant figure. Boucard, the head clerk, suggests Chabert return later, reflecting their indifference to his plight.

At Derville's office, Chabert insists on his appointment despite the porter’s indication that Derville is not yet in. When Derville arrives, he is startled by Chabert's ghostly appearance. Chabert recounts his story of commanding a cavalry regiment at Eylau, being gravely injured, and left for dead. He survived, was rescued by a woman, and after months of recovery, sought to reclaim his identity and fortune, only to find his wife, Countess Ferraud, had remarried and inherited his estate.

Derville listens intently as Chabert expresses despair over being treated as a madman and longing for vengeance against his wife. Moved by Chabert's plight, Derville offers to help him obtain documents to prove his identity and promises financial assistance. Chabert, grateful, recognizes Derville as a rare ally. Their discussion shifts Chabert's demeanor from despair to determination, and he expresses a willingness to fight for his rights.

Three months later, Derville is approached by Crottat, who requests the return of 600 francs given to Chabert. Derville hesitates to continue supporting Chabert financially, recalling that Chabert’s estate was managed by Roguin, a disreputable lawyer. Crottat informs him that Chabert's widow, Rose Chapotel, inherited about 300,000 francs after a contested will. Derville learns that Chabert is living in poor conditions with a dairyman named Vergniaud.

Upon visiting Chabert, Derville finds him in squalor, yet Chabert retains his dignity and expresses a desire to reclaim his life and fortune. Derville explains the complexities of legally proving Chabert's identity, warning that his wife may contest his claims. Disheartened by the prospect of a lengthy legal struggle, Chabert agrees to trust Derville with his case.

Derville plans to confront Madame Ferraud to gauge her reaction to Chabert's survival. When he meets her, she initially dismisses the idea but becomes defensive when warned of the legal implications. Realizing the gravity of her situation, Madame Ferraud becomes intrigued by leveraging Chabert's affection for a favorable outcome. Derville proposes a compromise, suggesting an amicable settlement that could benefit both parties, confident in navigating the situation to Chabert's advantage.

A week after Derville's visits, Colonel Chabert and his wife, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud, arrived at Derville's office from different parts of Paris. Chabert, now in a new outfit and wig, exuded soldierly dignity, while the Countess followed, elegantly dressed. The office atmosphere was light, with clerks joking about the unusual situation.

Derville prepared a memorandum outlining their agreement on Chabert's identity and the conditions for their marriage. The Countess initially resisted a lawsuit to annul Chabert's death certificate, wanting to avoid legal proceedings. When Chabert entered, tension escalated, revealing the complexities of their relationship. The Countess feigned ignorance about Chabert's identity, but her reaction suggested otherwise.

As the discussion progressed, Chabert expressed anger over the Countess's reluctance to acknowledge him and her proposed financial terms. Feeling cornered, she left abruptly, prompting Derville to caution Chabert about her potential traps. Despite his rage, Chabert was deeply affected and left the office in despair.

Later, at her country house, the Countess revealed her love for another man, Comte Ferraud. Chabert, though hurt, offered understanding and suggested they maintain dignity to avoid public scandal. During their time together, the Countess attempted to charm Chabert, but her ulterior motives became clear as she plotted to secure her position while keeping him at bay. After several days, Chabert sensed her manipulations, leading to a confrontation with Delbecq, her secretary, who suggested exploiting Chabert's situation for financial gain.

Feeling betrayed, Chabert confronted the Countess, who tried to manipulate him further. He ultimately decided to sacrifice his claim to his identity for her happiness, but her true intentions were revealed when she discussed putting him in an asylum if necessary. This shattered Chabert's trust, leading him to reject her and resolve to disappear from her life.

Chabert's subsequent life was marked by hardship, falling into poverty and obscurity. Derville, unaware of Chabert's fate, assumed the couple had reached a compromise. He later discovered Chabert imprisoned as a vagabond, stripped of his dignity. Despite his circumstances, Chabert maintained pride and contempt for societal norms.

In June 1840, attorney Godeschal visited Bicetre with Derville, where they encountered an elderly pauper drying his handkerchief. Derville revealed he was Colonel Chabert, sent to the workhouse due to reminders of their humble beginnings. Two days later, they returned to find Chabert drawing in the sand, claiming to be "Hyacinthe." An observing pauper noted that despite Chabert's apparent childishness, he was clever and philosophical. Derville lamented the fate of men like Chabert, who served Napoleon only to end up in a workhouse, reflecting on the disillusionment faced by those in the legal profession. Godeschal, having encountered similar cases, expressed a desire to escape the grim realities of Paris for a quieter life in the countryside, critiquing societal indifference towards those who have fallen from grace.
