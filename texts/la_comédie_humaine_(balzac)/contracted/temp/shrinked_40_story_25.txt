Early in the autumn of 1826 the Abbé Birotteau, the story’s central figure, was caught in a downpour returning from an evening with friends. Small, apoplectic and near sixty, he hurried across the deserted Cloister behind Saint-Gatien at Tours, fearing damp shoes. He was nonetheless in good spirits: he had just won three francs ten sous at rubber with Madame de Listomère, wrapped himself in a canon’s fur cape, and felt buoyed by talk that he might be named canon — a post he long coveted.

On the Grand’Rue side of the Cloister a Close had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette. Opposite, a single house, overshadowed by Saint-Gatien’s buttresses, had long housed abbés and belonged to an old maid, Mademoiselle Gamard. Bought by her father during the Terror and accepted by the restored Church, the dim, cramped dwelling suited only those of absolute nullity or uncommon fortitude. Devout and economical, Mademoiselle Gamard boarded priests and ran the house with punctilious thrift.

Birotteau had lived two years in that house and prized its rooms as highly as the hoped-for canonry. His two ambitions — being Mademoiselle Gamard’s boarder and becoming a canon — reflected a provincial priest’s craving for decent lodging, good food, clean clothes and the dignity of a title. His passion for the apartment had begun in admiration of his friend Chapeloud’s rooms and grown into obsession.

Chapeloud, who had become canon when worship was restored under Napoleon, had long occupied the south-facing apartment, improving it with modest legacies and gifts: an oak bookcase from a demolished château, waxed floors, painted woodwork, a long ebony table, Boulle cabinets, folios and Fathers of the Church. He transformed shabby rooms into a dignified oratory and a salon hung with red damask and mahogany. Birotteau, poor and a peasant’s son like Chapeloud, admired these changes and secretly yearned for them. When Chapeloud fell ill, Birotteau’s thought ran, “If Chapeloud dies I can have this apartment.” He never contemplated influencing his friend’s will.

Chapeloud, indulgent and aware of his friend’s weakness, forgave the covetousness and, on his deathbed while Birotteau read the Quotidienne, said, “This time you will certainly get the apartment.” He bequeathed his library and furniture to Birotteau, who, though saddened, found consolation in the inheritance and in Mademoiselle Gamard’s cheerful agreement to take him as lodger. He moved into the apartment and enjoyed comforts he had long imagined: meticulous linen, steady fires, well-selected wood, fragrant orris-root and the small attentions for which Chapeloud praised the spinster. Mademoiselle Gamard’s household was celebrated for its perfect order and attentive service.

Yet having obtained his long-desired comforts, Birotteau soon replaced the old obsession with a new one: the canonry. Encouraged by Madame de Listomère’s talk, he believed promotion was near. Absorbed in these hopes, he did not at once notice, on returning one evening, that his umbrella was gone and that small domestic attentions were missing. He found the outer door locked, Marianne the servant answering awkwardly that Mademoiselle had thought he was out; his candlestick had been moved to an antechamber; the fire in his room had not been burning; his slippers were not placed where they usually were. These trivial changes he read as deliberate signs of displeasure. By a sort of clerical logic he concluded that Mademoiselle Gamard intended to chill and humiliate him. The small humiliations mortified him deeply.

Birotteau was kindly but simple-minded and ignorant of worldly calculations; his goodness verged on stupidity. His learning was shallow, his life centered on mass and confession; he lacked frank self-knowledge about what he had done to provoke dislike. He could not perceive how unconscious selfishness and provincial narrowness — common failings — made him difficult. He was a clumsy egoist, unlike Chapeloud, who was a shrewd one.

Chapeloud had understood Mademoiselle Gamard: an old maid of about thirty-eight whose pretensions amounted to a strong sense of self. He judged she needed constant, precise attentions if one would live comfortably under her roof. So he kept limited contact, avoided breakfast and supper, and came to dinner a few minutes early to pay polite compliments about the food and service. By these attentions, and by never creating points of contact beyond politeness, Chapeloud secured peaceful coexistence and gained praise for being easy to live with.

Troubert, the other lodger, was Mademoiselle Gamard’s creature: obedient, patient and entirely under her rule, often suspected of coveting her property because of their intimate domestic life. When Chapeloud died the landlady at first considered offering the rooms to Troubert, but seeing Birotteau’s attachment she accepted him as lodger for her own advantage and even improved the rooms: replacing brick with wooden floors and rebuilding the chimney.

But Birotteau, dazzled by his new condition, did not adopt Chapeloud’s circumspection. He fell into Mademoiselle Gamard’s life with the timidity of a man who fears seeming rude and lingers too long in conversation. He began to spend his evenings there; the spinster, whose real wish had long been to make her salon a weekly center of company, welcomed the chance to display more prominent visitors. When the vicar soon deserted her salon for Madame de Listomère’s and Mademoiselle Salomon’s — preferring aristocratic circles to which the landlady did not belong — his withdrawal gravely wounded Mademoiselle Gamard; she had hoped to use him to attract society. Her disappointment, joined to an old-maid’s craving for return on social investment and wounded pride, turned into resentment. The vicar, however, never understood this. He perceived, belatedly and clumsily, signs of slights and concluded that ill will was directed at him though he could not see the reasons.

Mademoiselle Gamard, like many who refuse to adapt their habits to changing lives, indulged a petty despotism over small things: the basket of counters, the arrangement of cards, the order of household routine. Such trifles she magnified into tests of obedience. The vicar, unlike Chapeloud, could not accept being persistently cinched by such crochets; he withdrew rather than submitting, irritating her more by his absence than by any direct challenge.

Her friends, defending her, called Chapeloud amiable and blamed Birotteau for turning away from the old maid; society often sided with those who escaped the Cloister’s nightly round. Mademoiselle Gamard enjoyed the condolence she received, and Troubert explained the departure by saying the abbé sought fashion, luxury and witty talk — flattering her with the implication he preferred higher company.

In truth, the vicar’s abandonment was a clumsy desertion, and his naïveté and lack of social tact gradually made him intolerable to his landlady. Eighteen months after she had taken him in, when he mistook her silence for contentment, she had in fact begun a deliberate, underhand persecution. The locked door, the forgotten slippers, the unlit fire and the moved candlestick were its first signs. This prologue, recounting the small domestic developments that precede the greater drama, insists on particulars: the petty meannesses, jealousies and ambitions of bourgeois life inflict injuries as keen as those produced by grander interests, and their slow, minute growths require this account to explain the coming catastrophe.

The next morning Birotteau woke so absorbed in thoughts of the prospective canonry that he had already forgotten the four ominous signs of the previous night. He did not rise without a fire; he rang for Marianne and lay half-asleep in his usual reveries. When she had not come after twenty minutes he was about to ring again, but heard footsteps on the stairs. After a discreet knock, the Abbe Troubert, who visited him about once a month, entered. Seeing there was no fire, Birotteau sharply called for Marianne, opened the window and told her to come at once; then, quite naively, he told Troubert the steps he had taken and even the names Madame de Listomere was using in his favor—unaware that Troubert bore a grudge against that lady for twice having snubbed him when he was proposed as vicar-general by the bishop.

Troubert and Birotteau presented striking contrasts. Troubert, tall, lean, yellow-faced and bilious, had long, deeply wrinkled features that often expressed sarcasm or contempt; he spoke little, seemed grave, and was suspected of ambition. His imposing presence, cautious conduct, saintly life and failing health removed fears about his alleged ability; his chronic malady made it likely he would soon be vicar-general. Birotteau was plump, ruddy, kindly, talkative and simple—qualities that marked him permanently as a vicar and nothing higher. His robust appearance, triple chin and even his gout seemed to others guarantees of long life and offered no hope of elevation.

The Abbe Chapeloud, a sensible, amiable man popular in Tours, had quietly opposed Troubert’s rise by keeping him out of the best salons. Troubert treated Chapeloud with deference while Chapeloud remained unconvinced: “Distrust that lean stick of a Troubert,—Sixtus the Fifth reduced to the limits of a bishopric!” he had said to Birotteau.

Troubert had come that morning, the day after Mademoiselle Gamard—Birotteau’s landlady and the old maid who had seemingly declared war on the vicar—had behaved harshly toward him, simply to ask to borrow Birotteau’s Register of Bishops. Birotteau, pleased, sent him to the library while he dressed. Presently the breakfast bell rang; Birotteau, reflecting that without Troubert he would have had no fire, thought, “He’s a kind man.”

The two priests carried huge folios to the dining-room table. Mademoiselle Gamard greeted them sharply. “I hope you are not going to litter up my dining-room with your old books!” she snapped at Birotteau. Troubert explained he had borrowed them from the vicar. Mademoiselle Gamard remarked contemptuously that Birotteau did not often read such large books.

Birotteau answered with his usual mellifluous politeness. She replied that he had woken her the night before out of her first sleep and had kept her awake. Sitting down, she added, “Gentlemen, the milk is getting cold.” Stupefied at the ill-natured reception and alarmed at the prospect of a dispute, the timid vicar fell silent. He struggled inwardly between feeling he should not submit to discourtesy and his instinct to avoid quarrel.

To distract himself he examined the green lines painted on the oilcloth she habitually left on the breakfast table. They sat opposite each other; she dominated the head of the table from a high, cushioned chair near the stove. When Mademoiselle Gamard handed him his coffee, the vicar felt chilled by the silence and dared not meet Troubert’s dried features or the landlady’s threatening face. He turned to the fat pug on a cushion by the stove and addressed it, as he often did, to keep up conversation on his theory that speech favored digestion. The dog, once important in the household, merely closed his eyes peevishly.

Mademoiselle Gamard believed in health maxims and in talking at meals; Birotteau had for some mornings strained to find topics to loosen her tongue. Their conversations, if recorded, would have illustrated provincial ignorance and credulous gossip—absurdities about Napoleon’s death, the Dauphin, taxes, and other political and medical superstitions. But that morning Birotteau felt tongue-tied; after a long pause he risked, “This coffee is excellent,” and later, “It will be finer to-day than yesterday.” At the second remark she cast her most gracious look toward Troubert, then turned on Birotteau with terrible severity.

Mademoiselle Sophie Gamard was the quintessential old maid. To understand her is to see how society condemns the unproductive consumer, and how celibacy—whether from lack of charm, deliberate independence, or mischance—isolates and degrades a woman’s standing. Deprived of activities through which womanly qualities develop and are exercised, such women are starved of purpose. Bitterness, jealousy and wounded pride follow; they blame others for their condition, then themselves, and often turn to revenge. Their failure shows in an oblique, ashamed glance, a hardness of manner, and a certain selfishness: used to being useless, they prefer what is convenient for themselves to what would please others. Habits of self-centeredness produce peevishness and lack of refinement; deprived of love’s practice, their faculties wither.

All these truths could be read in Mademoiselle Gamard’s dull gray eyes, the dark circles under them, the straight lines of her wrinkles. Her sparse chin hair, thin lips barely covering long teeth, dark complexion, and the gaps where she badly adjusted a false front revealed a carelessness born of inward conflict. Her thin brown gowns, limp collar and thick, ribbed neck matched the hard, angular movements of a woman who had risen from rural origins—the daughter of a wood-merchant—and who carried in her face and gait the despotism of character and narrowness of mind. Her abrupt gestures, loud nasal blows on her handkerchief, and erect, blocklike stride betrayed a lack of grace. When in good humor she would recount missed chances of marriage and men’s lack of means, proof, to her, that worldly prudence had prevailed over the heart.

Her dining-room showed her taste: varnished paper with Turkish landscapes, two pier tables, a barometer, little faded worsted cushions before each abbé’s chair. Her salon, “the yellow salon,” was yellow in curtains, furniture and walls; the mantelpiece with a gilt mirror, crystal candlesticks and clock seemed sharp and precise. Her private apartment was never shown; conjecture thought it full of odds, worn-out furniture, and things dear to an old maid.

Unable to expend womanly activity naturally, she had converted it into petty intrigues, provincial cabals and self-seeking schemes—the occupation of many old maids. Birotteau had developed in her the only strong feeling she could muster: hatred. This hatred, previously latent beneath provincial calm, became intense because expended on petty things within a narrow sphere. Birotteau’s timidity and incapacity to see the world’s malice made him especially helpless before her. He was the sort of man doomed to suffer because he could not avoid the harms that beset him.

After breakfast, unnerved by the silence, Birotteau left with the coffee heavy in his stomach and strolled among the narrow, box-edged paths of the little garden, laid out in a star. Turning after a first round, he saw Mademoiselle Gamard and Troubert standing motionless on the threshold; he felt as if they were watching and counting his steps. Nothing is so distressing to a timid person as being closely examined, more so when those eyes express hatred. Embarrassed, he fancied he was in their way and, moved by fear and kindness, left for Saint-Gatien, where funerals, a marriage and baptisms occupied him and eased his pain.

When his watch told him dinner should be ready he hurried back, mindful of Mademoiselle Gamard’s punctuality. Passing the kitchen, he saw the first course had been removed. In the dining-room she said sourly, with evident pleasure at finding ground for reproach, “It is half-past four, Monsieur Birotteau. You know we are not to wait for you.” The vicar looked at the clock and noticed, by the moved gauze, that the hands had been set ahead of the cathedral clock. He said nothing; any remark would have provoked one of her fierce expositions, in which women of her class could be eloquently censorious. Mademoiselle Gamard instinctively divined a thousand petty annoyances that might provoke a timid man like Birotteau, and delighted in plotting against his domestic comfort—always so contrived as to leave her apparently never in the wrong. Her methods showed a profoundly malignant ingenuity: she contrived to torment him while keeping the appearance of justice and propriety.

Eight days after the events that began this story, changes in Abbé Birotteau’s household and in Mademoiselle Gamard’s behavior convinced him a long-simmering plot had matured. At first the annoyances—petty thefts, a tampered candlestick and clock—were trifling, but enough to make him feel watched. Gamard, pleased with her long-planned revenge, discharged it with the meticulous malice of a woman who channels life into petty observances.

These torments wounded the timorous, self-pitying vicar, who could not bring himself to confide in friends for fear of seeming ridiculous. The secrecy undermined his health and pride; finally he resolved to ask Abbé Troubert for official intervention. In Troubert’s cold, austere room the vicar, ashamed to intrude, described his plight. Troubert listened with some amusement and attributed the difficulty partly to Birotteau’s distraction by elevated thoughts; he offered to arbitrate but insisted on hearing both sides. He advised patience, suggesting Gamard’s temper shifts might stem from illness, and implied that if Birotteau stayed longer he might come to esteem her better.

Birotteau left baffled and, unable to consult anyone, decided to escape the tension by spending a week at Madame de Listomère’s country house, the Alouette—ironically the exact action Gamard wanted. Ten days into his stay, Gamard’s lawyer, Monsieur Caron, arrived in Tours to ask how long Birotteau intended to be away; his prolonged absence might provoke dispute. Dazed, the vicar asked for time to take advice.

At the Alouette the company rallied to his aid. Madame de Listomère suspected Troubert wanted the canonry; Monsieur de Listomère urged resistance; Monsieur de Bourbonne suggested Gamard might be scheming to turn him out. Then Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix arrived with decisive news: the vicar-general was gravely ill and had temporarily delegated his powers to Abbé Troubert, making the canonry effectively dependent on him. Abbé Poirel had recently discussed Birotteau’s supposed annoyances to Gamard in a way that cast blame on the vicar. Madame de Listomère bluntly asked whether Birotteau preferred becoming a canon or staying with Gamard; all advised securing the canonry. Acting on this counsel, a servant fetched Caron back.

Emotion overcame caution. In a brief conference Caron urged Birotteau to sign a paper relinquishing domicile. Confused, exhausted and pressured by respected friends, the vicar mechanically signed: he declared he had left Gamard’s house of his own will and had been properly lodged and fed. Caron asked where to send his belongings; Birotteau named Madame de Listomère. De Bourbonne, skeptical, examined the paper and noted it implied prior agreements between Birotteau and Gamard. The deed, he was told, lay in Birotteau’s library.

Encouraged by Madame de Listomère and Mademoiselle Salomon, who soothed him like mothers, Birotteau briefly forgot his misery at the prospect of living with them. But that night reality returned: moving meant scattering books and furniture, ending the habits that composed his life. He could not accept trading a year of peace in Gamard’s house for the canonry. He clung to Mademoiselle Salomon’s friendship but distrusted new attachments.

Mademoiselle Salomon’s character explains her intense sympathy: having once tended a mad lover for years, she had since devoted herself to the feeble and helpless. Early the next morning she returned Birotteau to Tours and left him at the cathedral quay to see to the Cloister. At his house he found his old apartment occupied; Marianne informed him Troubert was installed in his rooms. Troubert, comfortably seated in Chapeloud’s library, confirmed the depth of the conspiracy with a sardonic calm that left the abbé dumbstruck.

Troubert told him Gamard had notified him of Birotteau’s departure and that his installation had been necessary; if the furniture were Birotteau’s, Gamard’s scrupulous honesty would ensure its return; if Birotteau wished to return, Troubert would yield the apartment. Devastated, Birotteau sought Gamard and met her on the staircase. When he protested she should have allowed him to pack, she reminded him of the clause in their agreement: because there was an eight-hundred-franc yearly difference between the board paid by the late Abbé Chapeloud and Gamard’s charges—and because Gamard had lent sums to make up Chapeloud’s legacy duties—Birotteau had engaged, in consideration of those loans, to leave all his household property to Gamard at death or to transfer it should he voluntarily give up the apartment. With Poirel’s recent nomination as canon, Gamard exulted. Overwhelmed, Birotteau fled.

At Madame de Listomère’s he found his linen and papers already packed in a trunk. Poirel’s nomination meant he had no home, no means, and no furniture. Mademoiselle Salomon, thinking his distress sprang from the shock of the appointment, had him taken back to the Alouette; friends tended him until he recounted the morning’s events. De Bourbonne examined the deed and cursed Gamard for being “armed with claws.” Birotteau had never suspected anything would separate him from the house where he had hoped to live and die; in his eagerness to enter Gamard’s house he had signed without looking closely.

Madame de Listomère insisted she had made him sign and must restore him to happiness. De Bourbonne suggested the deed might be fraudulent; the Baron and others urged litigation. Privately, de Bourbonne warned that many friends would soon desert him and hinted that true peace might come if Birotteau resigned the vicariate and left Tours for a distant parish where Troubert could not pursue him—a thought that horrified the vicar, for he equated leaving Tours with death. Celibate attachments had been replaced by habits—walks, salons and small social pleasures—that sustained him.

Word spread that Madame la Baronne de Listomère had invited Birotteau to stay; the baroness’s reception implicitly censured Gamard and wounded her deeply. To understand Gamard’s resources one must note her network around Troubert: she made evening rounds with him to houses where circles of old maids, gossip-prone men and busybodies met. These women, ever ready to retail scandal, functioned as a gossip-police that transmitted household secrets to Troubert and shaped local opinion. The quarrel became the absorbing passion of these salons—small-town politics amplifying a private dispute.

Madame de Listomère took Birotteau to her lawyer, who found the case difficult. Friends who had earlier assumed justice would be easily had delayed action; Gamard’s allies had taken the initiative and were shaping public opinion. The lawyer, practiced with the devout, warned against litigation: the deed gave Gamard the law, and in equity judges and respectable laymen might view Birotteau as ungrateful. Gamard had lent him money to pay Chapeloud’s legacy duties without a formal receipt; Birotteau, inexperienced and unsuspicious, had lived under Gamard’s roof for only two years while others had been there far longer; suing could look like ingratitude. The lawyer refused to take the case and urged Madame de Listomère not to embroil herself.

De Bourbonne counseled caution; the Baron demanded action. A radical lawyer was finally engaged, a choice that polarized opinion and attracted political and anti-clerical energies. An expert from the Museum appraised the paintings and Gothic furniture at substantial sums: the Virgin of Valentin and the Christ of Lebrun at 11,000 francs, the bookshelves and furniture at about 12,000, and the total appraisal exceeding 36,000 francs. On this basis Birotteau could not plausibly have intended to give away such wealth; legally he had equitable grounds to challenge the agreement, and if refused, Gamard’s conduct might amount to intentional fraud. The radical lawyer’s writ was skillfully drafted and so aggressive in its denunciation of the old maid that copies were maliciously distributed through town, inflaming the controversy.

A few days after his quarrel with the old maid began, the Baron de Listomere—expecting promotion to corvette captain—received a letter warning he might be put on the retired list. Astonished, he went to Paris and to the minister, who laughed off his fears; yet the next day a secretary indiscreetly showed him a document confirming the news, awaiting only the director’s signature.

Hurrying to an uncle who was a deputy, the baron learned the true reason: the minister had first accused him of Radical sympathies, but the real issue was ecclesiastical. The baron had spoken slightingly of Abbe Troubert, the vicar-general and an influential Jesuit proconsul in the province. His uncle warned him to make peace at once; Troubert might become a bishop and could even harm the uncle’s prospects.

This explained to the baron the secretive activity that Birotteau had long wondered about: Troubert “sits up all night.” Troubert’s position, skill, and female network in Tours made him powerful; archbishop, general, prefect—all yielded to his covert influence. The Baron de Listomere resolved to avoid another attack.

Three days later he told his aunt the family must abandon support of “that fool of a Birotteau.” He detained Monsieur de Bourbonne for counsel. Bourbonne, clear-sighted and already aware of Troubert’s strength, advised a graceful retreat: bow to Troubert but not so low as to be trampled; feign an intention to leave the service; send Birotteau away to placate Mademoiselle Gamard; invite Troubert to the salon—women could win priests; make a slight menace to retain bargaining power. “If some clever Radical gets that empty head, he may cause trouble,” he warned, urging immediate dismissal of Birotteau lest a court verdict favor him and alarm Troubert.

The next morning Madame de Listomere, embarrassed, asked Birotteau to withdraw his suit against Mademoiselle Gamard and to leave her house. She explained plainly the wider stakes—the web of revenge and Troubert’s power, whose hatred of Chapeloud gave motive for seizing Chapeloud’s property and persecuting his friend. Birotteau, pale and shaken, listened as one at a precipice. The baroness promised he would lack nothing and that Bourbonne would arrange matters to save appearances. “Grant me the right to abandon you,” she begged.

The bewildered abbe, admitting Chapeloud had been right about Troubert’s malice, yielded: “I trust myself to you—I am but the stubble of the streets.” He asked only for Chapeloud’s portrait; the baroness promised to consult Mademoiselle Gamard and the archbishop.

Birotteau left dismayed; Troubert now seemed a pyramid whose hands were in Paris and elbows in the cloister. The Listomeres’ explanation to the town—that the nephew meant to marry and leave the navy, seeking the vicar’s apartment—masked their submission to the Congregation’s occult power. The lawsuit still awaited judgment; their opponents had yielded while menacing.

Mademoiselle Gamard then caught cold leaving the cathedral and grew dangerously ill. The town’s women bewailed her: “Mademoiselle Gamard’s sensitive nature could not bear the scandal; in spite of the justice of her cause she may die of grief—Birotteau has killed his benefactress.” Such talk spread through Tours.

Madame de Listomere went to pay her promised visit, but the old maid was too ill. She asked to see the vicar-general. Troubert, gratified to receive a woman who had hitherto ignored him, kept her waiting and then received her with consummate dissimulation. Bourbonne had coached the baroness to be a mediator, not an interested party. Their conversation became a duel of implied meanings. Madame de Listomere expressed regret at the lawsuit and offered a deed of relinquishment for the dying woman; she hoped to end the matter and show compassion to Birotteau, who had asked for Chapeloud’s portrait as the price of renunciation.

Troubert, with a calculating air, asked Mademoiselle Gamard and returned with her reply: she would not part with Chapeloud’s portrait—her feelings for her late friend were too faithful. Troubert himself stated he would not yield it if it were his: his loyalty justified his right. The baroness tried to smooth things—proposing a copy of the picture and inviting him to whist—but Troubert, stroked by vanity, promised to oblige for her sake.

The baroness left, hoping the archbishop would complete the peace. But Mademoiselle Gamard died the next day. Her will left everything to Abbe Troubert; the fortune was appraised at three hundred thousand francs. He promptly sent the Listomeres invitations to the services and funeral; the baroness insisted on attendance. Bourbonne added that the baron must go to the cemetery; the young lieutenant, unluckily, had not yet left Tours.

The funeral services displayed unusual ecclesiastical magnificence. Only Birotteau wept, kneeling in a side chapel; he believed himself guilty and prayed for the deceased, lamenting that he had not gained her forgiveness. Troubert followed the body to the grave and delivered an eloquent discourse that enlarged the old maid’s life to monumental proportions; in the peroration he declared her innocent and, revealing more joy than grief, sprinkled the coffin with holy water.

The next morning Mademoiselle Salomon told Madame de Listomere that “our poor Abbe Birotteau has just received a frightful blow: he is appointed curate of Saint-Symphorien.” Saint-Symphorien lay beyond the bridge, a three-mile suburb—an exile in sight of the town. The parsonage was cold and damp, the parish too poor; the removal seemed an infamous plot.

To end the history: five months later Troubert was made Bishop of Troyes; Madame de Listomere died, leaving an annuity of fifteen hundred francs to Abbe Birotteau. When the baroness’s will was read, Monseigneur Hyacinthe, Bishop of Troyes, delayed his departure from Tours on receiving the news and, furious at being foiled by the baroness—whom he had lately received while she had secretly aided a man he counted an enemy—Troubert again threatened the baron’s career and jeopardized his uncle’s peerage. He made one of those soft, vengeful priestly speeches in the archbishop’s salon. The Baron de Listomere then submitted; his later conduct showed entire subservience to the Jesuit’s will.

The new bishop deeded Mademoiselle Gamard’s house to the cathedral chapter, gave Chapeloud’s books and bookcases to the seminary, and presented the two disputed pictures to the Chapel of the Virgin—but he kept Chapeloud’s portrait. No one could explain this near-total renunciation of Gamard’s bequest; Bourbonne supposed the bishop had secretly kept invested monies to support his rank in Paris. Only the night before Troubert’s departure did Bourbonne unearth the hidden reason: the baron had contested Madame de Listomere’s legacy to Birotteau on grounds of undue influence. The deathblow to the feeblest victim was complete.

A few days after the case was brought the baron was promoted to captain. Ecclesiastical discipline followed: the curate of Saint-Symphorien—Birotteau—was suspended; his superiors judged him guilty. The murderer of Sophie Gamard was also a swindler; had Troubert kept the property openly, it would have been harder to censure Birotteau.

When Monseigneur Hyacinthe drove along the quay by Saint-Symphorien in a post-chaise on his way to Paris, poor Birotteau sat in an armchair in the sun on a terrace above the road. Pale and haggard, smitten by the archbishop, he was the skeleton of the man who less than a year earlier had rolled contentedly along the cloister. The bishop cast one look of pity and contempt, then went his way.

There is little doubt Troubert might once have been a Hildebrand or an Alexander VI. In our era the Church is no longer the political power it once was; celibacy concentrates a man’s faculties on a single passion—egotism—which makes celibates either useless or mischievous. We live in an age when governments tend to make Man for Society rather than Society for Man. There is a perpetual struggle between the Individual and a social system that uses him while he tries to use it. In earlier times man served first father, then clan or city; later caste or religion; today his life attaches to a vast country and perhaps someday to the whole universe.

Will such moral cosmopolitanism—Christian Rome’s hope of Brotherhood—prove only a sublime error? It is natural to believe in such a noble vision, but human nature lacks divine proportions. Souls vast enough to hold a world of feeling are rare. Those who bear a science, a nation, or a code may seem egotistical, but that passionate concentration creates peoples and ideas; to do so they unite the tenderness of woman and the force of God. The histories of Innocent III, Peter the Great, and other great leaders show this same vast thought of which Troubert was but a provincial representative.
