The story begins in a lawyer's office where clerk Simonnin mocks Colonel Chabert, an elderly man seeking help from attorney M. Derville. Initially perceived as a beggar, Chabert reveals he is a significant figure thought dead after the Battle of Eylau, illustrating societal disdain for those who have fallen from grace in post-war France.

Chabert, weary and desperate, insists on meeting Derville despite the clerks' mockery. When Derville arrives, he is startled by Chabert's ghostly appearance. Chabert recounts his survival after being gravely injured and left for dead, only to discover that his wife, Countess Ferraud, has remarried and inherited his estate. Moved by Chabert's plight, Derville agrees to help him reclaim his identity and fortune.

Three months later, Derville is approached by Crottat, who requests the return of 600 francs given to Chabert. Derville hesitates, recalling that Chabert's estate was managed by a disreputable lawyer who fled with clients' money. He learns that Chabert's widow inherited a substantial sum after a contested will and that Chabert is living in poor conditions with a dairyman named Vergniaud.

Upon visiting Chabert, Derville finds him in squalor but still dignified. Chabert expresses his desire to reclaim his life and status as a general. Derville explains the complexities of proving Chabert's identity legally, warning him of a potentially lengthy court battle against his wife, who may contest his claims. Despite feeling disheartened, Chabert agrees to trust Derville with his case.

Derville encounters Vergniaud, who expresses concern for Chabert's well-being and requests financial help. Derville decides to assist but insists that Chabert should also support Vergniaud. He plans to confront Madame Ferraud to gauge her reaction to Chabert's survival. When they meet, she initially dismisses the idea but becomes defensive when Derville warns her of the legal implications and potential public backlash.

Realizing the seriousness of her situation, Madame Ferraud becomes intrigued by the possibility of leveraging Chabert's affection for a favorable outcome. Derville proposes a compromise, suggesting an amicable settlement that could benefit both parties.

A week later, Colonel Chabert and Madame la Comtesse Ferraud arrive at Derville's office from different parts of Paris. Chabert, now dressed in a new outfit, embodies soldierly dignity, while the Countess follows elegantly. The atmosphere is light, with clerks joking about the unusual situation.

Derville prepares a memorandum regarding Chabert's identity and their marriage conditions. The Countess resists a lawsuit to annul Chabert's death certificate, wanting to avoid legal proceedings. When Chabert enters, tension rises as the Countess feigns ignorance about him, though her reaction suggests otherwise.

Chabert expresses anger over her reluctance to acknowledge him and her financial terms. Feeling cornered, the Countess leaves abruptly, prompting Derville to warn Chabert about her potential traps. Despite his rage, Chabert departs in despair.

Later, the Countess takes Chabert to her country house, revealing her love for another man, Comte Ferraud. Hurt but understanding, Chabert suggests they maintain dignity and avoid scandal. However, the Countess attempts to charm him while plotting to secure her position, leading to a confrontation with her secretary, Delbecq, who suggests exploiting Chabert financially.

Feeling betrayed, Chabert confronts the Countess, who tries to manipulate him further. He decides to sacrifice his claim to his identity for her happiness, but her true intentions surface when she mentions putting him in an asylum. This shatters his trust, prompting him to reject her and resolve to disappear from her life.

Chabert's subsequent life is marked by poverty and obscurity. Derville, unaware of Chabert's fate, assumes a compromise had been reached. He later discovers Chabert imprisoned as a vagabond, stripped of dignity yet maintaining pride. In a final act of integrity, Chabert seeks to repay Derville, demonstrating loyalty despite betrayal. Derville facilitates payment owed to him by the Countess, highlighting the tragic consequences of their lives. Ultimately, Chabert's story reflects on identity, love, and the harsh realities post-war. 

In June 1840, attorney Godeschal visits Bicetre with Derville, where they find Chabert, now a pauper. Chabert displays childlike innocence, claiming to be "Hyacinthe." An observing pauper notes his cleverness despite his apparent childishness. Derville laments the fate of men like Chabert, who served Napoleon only to end up in a workhouse, reflecting on the disillusionment faced by those in the legal profession and society's indifference to the fallen.
