**Domestic Peace**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage and Clara Bell*

**Summary**

The narrative unfolds in late November 1809, during the height of Napoleon's empire, marked by opulence and extravagant celebrations in Paris. The atmosphere is one of revelry, with crowned heads and the French aristocracy indulging in luxury, contrasting sharply with the austerity of the Republic. Women, influenced by the military's allure, engage in fleeting romances, reflecting a society intoxicated by power and wealth.

At a grand ball hosted by Comte de Gondreville, the guests await Napoleon's arrival, unaware of the personal turmoil brewing between him and Josephine, which foreshadows their impending divorce. Amidst the festivities, a young woman, described as delicate and beautiful, captures the attention of two men: Colonel Montcornet and Baron Martial de la Roche-Hugon. They speculate about her identity, with Montcornet suggesting she is a victim of the other women’s jealousy, while Martial is intrigued by her beauty.

As the evening progresses, the ball becomes a stage for various intrigues. Madame de Vaudremont, a prominent figure, is caught in a web of jealousy and rivalry, particularly concerning her relationship with Martial. Meanwhile, the mysterious young woman is revealed to be Madame de Soulanges, who is struggling with her husband’s infidelity and the emotional turmoil it brings.

Martial, unaware of her marital status, attempts to charm Madame de Soulanges, leading to a flirtation that culminates in a misunderstanding involving a diamond ring. This ring, originally belonging to Madame de Soulanges, symbolizes the complex relationships and emotional stakes at play. As the night unfolds, the tension between the characters escalates, revealing their desires, insecurities, and the societal pressures they face.

The climax occurs when Madame de Soulanges, feeling the weight of her husband’s betrayal and the societal expectations of women, ultimately rejects Martial’s advances. The ball concludes with a sense of unresolved tension, as the characters grapple with their emotions and the consequences of their actions.

In the end, Madame de Soulanges returns home to her husband, who is unaware of the events that transpired at the ball. Their reunion is bittersweet, highlighting the complexities of love, loyalty, and the societal constraints that dictate their lives. The story closes with a reflection on the nature of domestic peace, suggesting that true happiness often eludes those entangled in the web of social expectations and personal desires.

**Key Themes:**
- The contrast between wealth and poverty in post-Revolutionary France.
- The fleeting nature of love and relationships in a tumultuous society.
- The impact of societal norms on personal happiness and domestic life.
- The complexities of infidelity and emotional turmoil within marriage. 

**Characters:**
- **Comte de Gondreville**: The host of the ball, seeking to impress Napoleon.
- **Napoleon Bonaparte**: The Emperor, whose personal issues overshadow the festivities.
- **Colonel Montcornet**: A soldier drawn to the mysterious young woman.
- **Baron Martial de la Roche-Hugon**: A charming lawyer who flirts with Madame de Soulanges.
- **Madame de Vaudremont**: A prominent socialite entangled in romantic rivalries.
- **Madame de Soulanges**: The young woman at the center of the intrigue, struggling with her husband’s infidelity.

This summary encapsulates the essence of Balzac's exploration of social dynamics, personal relationships, and the quest for happiness amidst the constraints of society.
