In 1828, about one in the morning, two friends left a large house in the Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré near the Élysée-Bourbon. One was the physician Horace Bianchon; the other, the fashionable Baron Eugène de Rastignac. They had dismissed their carriages and chosen to walk; the night was fine.

“I’ll walk you as far as the boulevard,” Rastignac said. “Come to my house.”

They spoke of the Marquise d’Espard. Rastignac, smitten, praised her rank, beauty, and the prospect that a liaison or marriage might lift his debts and further his ambitions. Bianchon answered coolly, warning him not to jeopardize a secure position for an illusion. When Rastignac offered hopeful gossip about the Marquise’s age, Bianchon cut in with practical advice: cosmetics deceive, but the temples and the tip of the nose reveal age. A woman past thirty can remain attractive, he allowed, but maturity alters expectations.

Rastignac admitted that rank and advantage mattered nearly as much as passion: a woman of high standing makes a man visible in society, an indispensable public instrument. He dismissed the domestic, deferential wife as a political dead end. Bianchon mocked such ambitions and criticized women of fashion from his medical and social observations. He depicted the fashionable woman as a public figure who sacrifices feeling for triumph and power; beneath gauze and frippery she hides a tougher nature—strong appetites, quick nerves, and a cold heart. “A sound stomach excludes a good heart,” he remarked. The woman of fashion seeks heat and excitement; she becomes “sex in the brain”—polished and precise, public and inaccessible to real feeling. The Marquise, he concluded, showed the signs of this type: a sharp eye, polite distance, and a bit of steel beneath apparent fragility. Charming perhaps, she was likely selfish and possibly in debt.

Rastignac replied that for him a woman of the world was a tool of power: a smile behind a fan can be more valuable than the piety of a provincial angel; the reserve of a duchess gives a sweeter triumph than the fervor of a modest lover. He insisted the calculated manners and measured affection of a fashionable woman could be politically and emotionally more useful than homey devotion. Bianchon conceded with a grimace; Rastignac teased his friend’s revolutionary temper.

Bianchon agreed to try to help by speaking to his uncle, Jean-Jules Popinot, about appointing a commission favorable to the Marquise, though he doubted Popinot’s pliability. Popinot, Bianchon said, was the sort of judge who would not barter justice for social favors; “he is a judge, as Death is Death.” They parted at the Boulevard des Capucines; Bianchon hailed a hackney cab.

Rastignac returned to his turmoil of ambition and intrigue; Bianchon to his duty. The next morning he went to the Rue du Fouarre to see his uncle, a lower-court magistrate. Once a principal medieval street, the Rue du Fouarre had become mean and squalid, full of the poor who could not keep fuel in winter and drawn to police courts and poorhouses. Halfway down, an old house—perhaps rebuilt under Francis I—leaned, its brick and worm-eaten stone, sagging façade, low vaulted entry, and close, dirty windows showing age.

From this dilapidated passage opened a little square garden-court no larger than a few paces, with black soil, bare trellises, old potsherds, and gathered paper. Two wings let light into the decrepit yard whose walls wore powdery grime. The house’s common rooms displayed the tenants’ crafts: dyed wool skeins, shirts flapping, volumes with fresh marbled edges, women singing, children shouting, a carpenter sawing, a coppersmith at work. Wooden pillars and stone blocks made shallow arches; a creaking iron-balustraded staircase climbed up; outer doors with Utrecht velvet and brass nails hinted that the house once belonged to a councillor, a rich priest, or a treasurer under Louis XIV.

Popinot’s lodgings were on the first floor, a gloom natural to such levels and intensified by the narrow street. Known throughout the twelfth arrondissement as a beneficent, laborious magistrate, he could cure or alleviate nearly every sort of legal malady. His appearance betrayed no official splendor: he dressed always in black, but badly—clothes threadbare and creased, linen stained by long storage, trousers worn, stockings twisted into ill-shaped shoes. He renewed attire only when his housekeeper complained. His necktie was crumpled, his collar dog-eared from judicial bands, hair thin, shave intermittent; he never wore gloves and kept hands in empty pockets. Torn pocket-holes completed the picture of slovenliness.

Beneath this outward negligence, however, lay an uncommon inner character. His face, at first plain and meek—almost calf-like with a flat forehead, straight nose, and large ears—was redeemed by a gracious mouth. His lips were full, red, finely wrinkled, mobile with sweetness and kindness; they betrayed intelligence, lucidity, and a benevolent temper. The life of the man matched these features: secret labor, patient virtue, and a strong love of justice. Napoleon’s legal reorganization had revealed his gifts; guided by Cambacérès, Popinot had been named to an Imperial High Court early on. He never courted favor, and his modesty or lack of worldly ambition cost him advancement. Overlooked by ministers, he was pushed down to a common court where he served as a supernumerary judge for years. The legal world at first protested, then accepted this placement as fitting the established order.

A closer look at Popinot’s career explains why a man of clear gifts might remain obscure. There are many judges in France—about six thousand—and not everyone can be a great man; classification by superiors fixed Popinot’s role. Legal practitioners divide cases into law, which applies principles rigidly, and equity, which adapts justice to particular facts. Popinot’s mind suited an exacting, cerebral approach: he read facts like a physician reads symptoms, seeking the moral anatomy beneath appearances. He specialized in discerning truth behind pleading and falsehood, much as an anatomist reconstructs an extinct animal from fragments. His method was deductive: he probed minutiae until a larger truth emerged; often a new glimmer of understanding would surprise him at night. He favored equity when deep injustice called for it, which made him seem impractical to colleagues who preferred quicker, conventional reasoning. Because his judgments were finely tuned and often long, he was thought better suited to the exhaustive, meticulous duties of an examining judge than to the brisk performance of a civil bench.

Popinot’s modesty and compassion further complicated his career. He disliked ostentatious ambition and was content to work; his nature bent him toward leniency. The role of examining judge, though less tempting in rewards than influential civil posts, matched his patient industry: he rarely complained and performed onerous tasks with conscience and care. In criminal examinations he showed both severity and charity, capable of rigorous interrogation and humane provision—a clerk might slip a poor accused man money or warm clothing. He extracted confessions not by brute force but by judicial skill; his powers of observation and deduction unmasked the craftiest villains and subdued impostors. That unusual acuity sprang not only from study but from certain personal trials and the domestic life that had shaped him—a story the narrative promises to relate in more detail.

Thus Popinot presented a paradox: a shabby, absent-minded exterior and a sweet, kindly mouth; a life of humble, patient virtue and formidable judicial intelligence. He had been placed where the law needed his probing talents rather than public prominence, and it was unlikely he would be moved by social appeals such as those of the Marquise d’Espard. Bianchon doubted that even rank or skilful courtship could make Popinot sacrifice principle for patronage; he promised to do what his conscience allowed in speaking to the judge but warned Rastignac that Popinot’s sense of duty might be immovable. The tale of why Popinot’s perspicacity had been sharpened by personal trials remains to be told in his domestic history, which the narrator indicates will reveal how the judge’s inner life shaped his public mind.

Twelve years before this story opens, in 1816, during a severe scarcity that accompanied the Allies’ stay in France, Popinot was named President of the Commission Extraordinary to distribute food to the neighborhood poor just as he meant to leave the Rue du Fouarre, which they all disliked. A great lawyer and keen criminal judge—whose superiority unsettled colleagues—he had for five years observed legal outcomes without seeing their causes. Climbing into lofts, witnessing poverty, and studying the desperate needs that drive people to crime, compassion overtook him. The judge became something like a Saint Vincent de Paul to these grown children, these exhausted toilers.

The change was gradual. Charity has temptations; it consumes a saint’s purse as roulette consumes a gambler’s. Going from misery to misery, after a year of lifting rags that cover public pauperism, Popinot had become the Providence of his quarter. He joined the Benevolent Committee and the Charity Organization. Wherever gratuitous service was needed he was ready, doing everything without fuss, like a man who spends his life carrying soup where people starve.

But Popinot worked on a broader plane: he prevented crime, gave work to the unemployed, found refuge for the helpless, distributed aid where danger threatened, counseled widows, protected homeless children, and became the sleeping partner of small traders. No one at the Courts or in Paris knew of this secret life. Some virtues are so splendid they require obscurity; men hide them under a bushel. Those he succored—hard-working by day, tired by night—were little able to praise him; theirs is the gracelessness of children who cannot repay because they owe too much. There is such compulsory ingratitude; but who that sows good expecting gratitude can consider himself great?

By the end of his second year of apostolic work, Popinot had turned the storeroom at the bottom of his house into a parlor lit by three iron-barred windows. Whitewashed walls and ceiling, wooden school benches, a clumsy cupboard, a walnut writing-table, and an armchair comprised the furniture. In the cupboard were his donation registers, bread-order tickets, and diary. He kept his ledger like a tradesman to avoid ruin by kindness: all the neighborhood’s sorrows had a little account, as merchants’ customers have theirs. When help was in question, he could command information from the police.

Lavienne, a man made for his master, was his aide-de-camp. He redeemed and renewed pawn-tickets and visited the districts most threatened with famine while his master was in court.

From four till seven in summer, six till nine in winter, the room filled with women, children, and paupers while Popinot gave audience. The crowd warmed the air; Lavienne strewed straw on the wet floor. By long use the benches were polished; at shoulder height the wall bore a dark coating left by rags. The poor loved Popinot so much that when they assembled at his door before daybreak, neither women with foot-brasiers nor men swinging arms to keep warm disturbed his sleep. Rag-pickers and other night toilers knew the house and often saw a light burning in the lawyer’s private room at unholy hours. Even thieves respected it. He gave his morning to the poor, mid-day to criminals, evening to legal work.

Popinot’s observation was bifrons: he perceived a pauper’s virtues—good impulses nipped, promising actions, hidden self-sacrifice—just as he could read the faintest outlines of crime and infer the rest.

His inherited fortune yielded a thousand crowns a year. His wife, sister to M. Bianchon Senior, a doctor at Sancerre, had brought him about twice as much and, dying five years earlier, left it to him. As a supernumerary judge his salary was small, and he had been fully paid only four years, explaining his parsimony despite abundant beneficence. His indifference to dress—an absent-minded air—seemed a mark of scientific pursuit. To complete the portrait: he was one of the few judges of the Court of the Seine who had not received the Legion of Honor ribbon.

Such was the man ordered by the President of the Second Chamber—where Popinot had sat since his reinstatement—to examine the Marquis d’Espard at his wife’s request for a Commission in Lunacy.

Bianchon put his horse to a trot to find his uncle amid his business, smiling at the contrast Popinot’s appearance would make in Madame d’Espard’s drawing-room and promising himself he would persuade the judge to dress less ridiculously. “If only my uncle has a new coat!” he thought as he turned into the Rue du Fouarre, where a pale light shone from the parlor windows. At the sound of wheels half a dozen paupers stirred and touched their hats; the doctor, who treated gratuitously the sick recommended by the lawyer, was well known.

Bianchon found his uncle amid grotesquely dressed petitioners whose postures a draughtsman might have composed as a picture: an old man with a white beard and apostolic mien; a young woman suckling a newborn while another child clung to her; an old woman with the repulsive look of rebellious pauperism; a young workman with an intelligent eye dulled by necessity. The majority were women: husbands gone to work left them to plead the family’s cause. Torn bandanas, mud-deep skirts, and ragged kerchiefs hid eyes that burned. The assemblage first excited disgust, then terror when you perceived their resignation was a speculation on benevolence. Two tallow candles flickered in the fetid atmosphere.

The magistrate himself was not picturesque. He wore a rusty cotton night-cap; with no cravat his neck showed red and wrinkled beneath a threadbare dressing-gown. His face had the half-stupid look of absorbed attention; his lips puckered; knitted brows bore the burden of confided sorrows: he felt, analyzed, and judged them all. As watchful as a Jewish money-lender, he hardly raised his eyes from his books and registers, except to peer into the hearts of those he examined with the flashing glance of a miser alarmed.

Lavienne, ready to carry out orders, served as a sort of police and welcomed newcomers. When Bianchon appeared there was a stir. Popinot stretched and greeted him.

“I was afraid you might make an official visit I wished to speak to you about,” said Bianchon.

Popinot turned to a stout woman and pressed her for the cause of her visit; she spoke of a green-grocery truck, a baby to nurse, and debts. Popinot guessed the sequel, asked names—“La Pomponne” and husband “Toupinet”—and, finding the husband imprisoned for debt, lent her ten francs for stock. Lavienne took the money from a large bag and Popinot noted the loan. Seeing the delight that made the hawker tremble, Bianchon understood her apprehensions.

“Monsieur has had two hundred persons this morning, and there are eight to be turned off,” said Lavienne when Bianchon whispered to him. Popinot handed Bianchon addresses and sent him to attend to a young girl who had attempted asphyxiation and to bring a man to the hospital. Bianchon returned an hour later; the Rue du Fouarre was deserted; day was dawning; Popinot had gone upstairs; Lavienne’s money bag was empty.

“How are they going on?” asked the old lawyer.

“The man is dead,” replied Bianchon; “the girl will get over it.”

Popinot’s flat reflected its master’s single-mindedness: dust everywhere, objects deployed for wrong purposes, papers in flower vases, empty ink-bottles, misplaced plates, matches as tapers—chaos from plans for order never completed. The private room, disordered by incessant rummage, bore witness to his unresting pace: books scattered, registers on the floor, unpolished floors.

Shelves and tables were covered with ex votos, offerings of the grateful poor: blue glass jars, frames of artificial flowers with Popinot’s initials, boxes of cabinet work, letter-weights carved in convict style. These masterpieces of patience and withered bouquets gave the room the air of a toyshop and served as hiding-places for bills and scraps of paper. All were thick with dust and faded. Some beautifully stuffed birds, eaten by moths, perched amid the trumpery, presided over by an Angora cat—probably a charitable return to Madame Popinot. A local artist had painted portraits of M. and Madame Popinot. Even the bedroom contained embroidered pin-cushions and cross-stitch landscapes.

Window-curtains were black with smoke; hangings colorless. Between the fireplace and the large table where the magistrate worked, two cups of coffee waited on a small table and two mahogany armchairs stood ready. As daylight could not penetrate the windows, the cook had left two dips burning; their unsnuffed wicks produced a red light like a miser’s slow-burning candle.

“My dear uncle, you ought to wrap yourself more warmly when you go down to that parlor,” Bianchon said.

“I cannot bear to keep them waiting, poor souls! Well, and what do you want of me?”

“I have come to ask you to dine tomorrow with the Marquise d’Espard.”

“A relation of ours?” asked Popinot with genuine absence of mind that made his nephew laugh.

“No, uncle; the Marquise d’Espard is a high and puissant lady who has petitioned the Courts for a Commission in Lunacy against her husband, and you are appointed to examine him.”

“And you want me to dine with her! Are you mad?” said the lawyer, taking up the code of proceedings. “Here—read this article prohibiting any magistrate’s eating or drinking in the house of either party in a case he is to decide. Let her come and see me, your Marquise, if she has anything to say. I was to go tomorrow to examine her husband after working the case up tonight.”

He rose, took up a packet, read its title, and said: “Here is the affidavit. Since you take an interest in this high and puissant lady, let us see what she wants.”

Popinot wrapped his dressing-gown around him, which constantly slipped, leaving his chest bare; he sopped his bread in half-cold coffee and opened the petition, commenting as he read with Bianchon.

The petition began with the petitioner’s name, Mme. Jeanne Clémentine Athénaïs de Blamont-Chauvry, wife of M. Charles Maurice Marie Andoche, Comte de Nègrepelisse, Marquis d’Espard—a good family—and proceeded to allege that for a year past her husband’s moral and intellectual powers had declined into dementia and idiocy within Article 448 of the Civil Code, requiring remedies for the security of his fortune and person and to protect their children, whom he keeps living with him.

The petition claimed that for a long time all income from the Espard estates had been paid, without reason, into the hands of an old woman—Madame Jeanrenaud—sometimes living in Paris, sometimes at Villeparisis, for the benefit of her son, aged thirty-six, an officer placed by the Marquis in the King’s Guards as Major in the First Regiment of Cuirassiers. These two, once in extreme poverty in 1814, had since bought valuable property, including a large house, and Jeanrenaud was laying out large sums to settle there with the woman Jeanrenaud, intending to marry; sums already exceeded a hundred thousand francs. The marriage had been arranged through the Marquis’s influence with his banker Mongenod, whose niece he proposed for Jeanrenaud, and the Marquis had used his influence to procure him the title of baron by letters patent dated December 29 the previous year.

“That no reason, not even such as morality and the law would concur in disapproving, can justify the influence which the said Mme. Jeanrenaud exerts over M. d’Espard,” the petition declared; “it is impossible but that this cause should be criminal, malignant, wrongful, or of a nature to come under medical jurisdiction; unless this influence is of the kind which constitutes an abuse of moral power—such as can only be described by the word possession—”

“The devil!” exclaimed Popinot. “What do you say to that, doctor? These are strange statements.”

“They might certainly be an effect of magnetic force,” Bianchon offered.

“Do you believe in Mesmer’s nonsense?” Popinot asked.

“Yes,” said Bianchon gravely. “I have verified analogous facts showing the unlimited influence one man may acquire over another. Actions promised during sleep by a magnetized patient to the magnetizer have been performed on waking. The will of one had become the will of the other.”

“Even a criminal act?” Popinot pushed.

“Even a crime,” Bianchon answered.

“If it were not from you, I would not listen to such a thing,” the judge admitted.

Popinot observed that if this so-called possession fell into such phenomena, it would be hard to prove legally. He also noted that though Mme. Jeanrenaud’s ugliness was stressed, power of fascination could go back decades—perhaps she had once been young and beautiful, and rivalry between women could explain much. He half joked that “power of fascination is in direct proportion to ugliness” and mentioned smallpox.

The petition continued, averring that in 1815, to supply sums required by Jeanrenaud and his mother, the Marquis had gone with his two children to live in rooms unworthy of his rank, that he often lacked money and had had his furniture seized by a landlord, showing odd behavior by treating the bailiff like a man of rank. It alleged that for nearly ten years his thoughts had been consumed by China—its customs and history—and that he referred everything to a Chinese origin, confusing current events with Chinese matters and sometimes fancying himself in China. This monomania, the petition said, had driven him to join a commercial undertaking, drawing bills that endangered his honor and fortune; his debts to stationers, printers, lithographers, and colorists supplying materials for his publication, A Picturesque History of China, were so heavy that tradesmen had urged the petitioner to seek a commission in lunacy.

“The man is mad!” exclaimed Bianchon.

“You think so?” Popinot asked. “If you listen to only one bell, you hear only one sound.”

The petition added that the Marquis’s children’s education had been neglected; he taught them Chinese history and dialects, contrary to Catholic tenets. He kept them destitute of necessities, visits them only once a year, and the petitioner, despite her entreaties, could never see them. Some neighbors, including a medical student and a professor living under the same roof, thought his Chinese monomania might be the result of a scheme by Baron Jeanrenaud and his mother to deaden the Marquis’s faculties, since Mme. Jeanrenaud seemed to procure everything relating to China for him. Finally, Mme. d’Espard claimed she could show that moneys absorbed by Jeanrenaud and his mother between 1814 and 1828 amounted to not less than one million francs. She listed witnesses prepared to testify and asked the President to lay the petition and its documents before the King’s public prosecutor and charge a judge to report.

Popinot and Bianchon laughed at some passages—Desroches, the solicitor, and his clerk Godeschal’s style evoked amusement. Popinot observed that by proving too much the petitioner sometimes proved nothing: a mother is cunning in procuring for her children, and if she really wanted to provide for them, nothing would have stopped her. Yet the facts warranted inquiry.

“I shall go tomorrow with my registrar to see M. le Marquis, for this does not seem at all clear to me,” Popinot said. Bianchon urged that Popinot see Madame d’Espard in her own house: she was delicate and might faint in his rat-hole of a place; besides, it was as necessary to cross-question the Marquise as it was to examine the Marquis.

Popinot consented. “It is quite possible that it is she who is mad. I will go,” he said. Bianchon wrote in his engagement book: “Tomorrow evening at nine, Madame d’Espard,” and prepared to call for his uncle.

Thus ends the scene in which the benevolent, single-minded Popinot, who divides his days among the poor, criminals, and court, reads a detailed, troubling petition accusing the Marquis d’Espard of mental decay, mismanagement, and undue influence by Mme. Jeanrenaud and her son—charges that blend suspicious financial dealings, strange devotion to all things Chinese, and possible external domination—prompting Popinot to promise a careful inquiry, beginning with an interview with the Marquise that evening.

Next evening at nine Bianchon climbed his uncle’s dusty staircase and found him bent over a complicated judgment. The coat Lavienne had ordered had not arrived, so Popinot wore his old stained coat—the plain Popinot whose look amused those who did not know the secrets of his private life. Bianchon smoothed his cravat and buttoned his coat, hiding some stains by crossing the breast; the judge, in his habit, rucked his coat over his chest and stuffed his hands into his pockets. The coat wrinkled, made a hump in the back, and left a gap between waistcoat and trousers through which his shirt showed; Bianchon only noticed this when his uncle entered the Marquise’s room.

A brief sketch of the lady before whom the doctor and judge now found themselves is necessary. Madame d’Espard had been much the fashion in Paris for seven years. Since her husband separated from her in 1815—she must have married about 1812, for her children were fifteen and thirteen—she contrived, by artifice and regimen, to appear far younger than her register age of thirty-three. Elaborate curls, twilight receptions, affected illness, cold baths, horsehair mattresses, light diets, and scrupulous small habits preserved a clear complexion, an unwrinkled brow, and a lithe figure. She was utterly self-absorbed: men amused her, but she knew neither hatred nor love; when offended she avenged herself coldly and could ruin a man with a few words.

Having parted from M. d’Espard with satisfaction, she kept the house and carriage and soon reasserted herself socially. From 1821 to 1827 she lived in great style and supplanted other grande dames at the top of society. Her circle included a duchess attached to an exiled prince, a foreign lady consulted by a Russian diplomat, and an antiquated countess who shuffled political cards. Thus Madame d’Espard accumulated influence beyond mere fashion; “What do they say at Madame d’Espard’s?” became a question of political weight. Damaged politicians praised her diplomatic capacity; principal figures came to play whist at her house.

She had real talents and grave defects: indiscretion among them, and a vanity that sought influence rather than mere patronage. Her person and manner served her designs; she kept a cool distance that prevented any man from gaining advantage. Conquests and pleasure were means to keep her name at the centre of life’s largest circle.

Many young men courted her—de Marsay, de Ronquerolles, de Montriveau, and others; she received men whose wives were excluded, and ambitious men submitted. Two famous royalist bankers, de Nucingen and Ferdinand du Tillet, endured such terms. She had studied Paris life so thoroughly that her conduct never gave a man an advantage; no compromising paper had been produced against her.

Rastignac sought to attach himself to her, seeing ministerial prospects and using her as a tool while wishing to make her his. The Hôtel d’Espard required a large household; grand receptions were on the ground floor, she lived on the first; the whole establishment spoke of immense fortune. Popinot, noting every detail from the lodge to the footmen, studied the furniture and splendor to pierce the misery of grandeur.

“M. Popinot—M. Bianchon.” Their names were announced at the boudoir door. The Marquise sat in a rococo armchair she had set the fashion for. Rastignac at her left, and a tall, lean man in black stood by the chimney-piece—silent, grave, and severe, with a face like a knife-blade; his attitude suggested judgment. Popinot entered awkwardly, left hand in pocket, hat with greasy lining in hand. His foolish appearance amused Rastignac; the Marquise bowed and affected weakness to rise.

The Chevalier d’Espard measured Popinot with one eye and remained mute; his presence seemed to guide the Marquise. Popinot’s manner was simple and devoted to “the shop”: he questioned people to make them reveal more than they meant. Bianchon sat cold and stern, wishing his uncle could trample this woman as one might a viper—a feeling prompted by her long dress and lithe movements.

“Madame,” Popinot said politely, “you owe me nothing. Although my visit is not strictly according to Court practice, we ought to spare no pains to discover the truth in cases of this kind. Our judgment is guided less by the letter of the law than by conscience. Whether I seek the truth here or in my consulting-room, so long as I find it, all will be well.”

The Marquise began her prepared tale: early in 1816 M. d’Espard’s temper changed and he proposed going to an estate near Briançon—against her health and habits; she refused, suspected his mind was unsound, and he left her, taking their two children. When Popinot asked the children’s ages she corrected him—fifteen and thirteen—and then appeared surprised when he pursued whether her attorney had represented the children as unhappy. Popinot asked if she had ever applied to her husband for custody; she had tried in vain.

Popinot, in his blunt way, probed more of the background. He asked if M. d’Espard had known Madame Jeanrenaud before marriage; her sharp denial was visible only to Rastignac and the Chevalier. The Marquise described Madame Jeanrenaud as monstrous—fat, pitted with smallpox, male-footed, a Protestant who went to the Oratoire—scandalous in contrast with her own refinement. Popinot listened and then directed attention to the material facts: the Marquise’s income, the Jeanrenauds’ supposed lavish lifestyle, and the stark arithmetic of household expenses.

“You have twenty-six thousand francs a year?” Popinot asked.

“Yes,” she said.

Popinot observed the boudoir, the porcelain, the gilt decorations, the costly trifles—then remarked that to spend as she purported, with stables, carriages, servants, and furnishings, she must have much more income. If the Jeanrenauds lived as she said, they could not have the means she described; a million yields scarcely forty thousand a year. If they had twenty-six thousand, and kept the style she displayed, they might be deeply in debt. Popinot pressed the point: if she asked the court to deprive her husband of his faculties and control of property, the Court must examine not only the husband’s sanity but also the petitioner’s management and motives. If she had creditors or debts, her petition might be seen as motivated by self-interest.

The Marquise bristled. Popinot continued, calmly and bluntly: if the Marquis were pronounced incapable, a trustee would be appointed—who? “His brother,” she said; the Chevalier bowed. Popinot’s casual wit—pointing to porcelain and China in the room—stung the Marquise. Bianchon smiled; Rastignac stiffened.

Popinot’s reasoning was simple: the Court must investigate the petitioner’s finances and motives; a plaintiff living beyond means might be suspected of seeking control for gain. The Marquise tried to answer that she had incurred debts to secure her children’s future by keeping connections and entertaining; she claimed to have spent to prepare for them a brilliant career. Popinot accepted her devotion but insisted on facts—powers of attorney, investments, management of funds. Her answers betrayed carelessness: she had no power from her husband, she had no clear accounting, and she admitted living at an expensive rate while claiming only twenty-six thousand francs a year. The judge warned that opposing counsel would not be as indulgent and would probe any signs of self-interest.

The Marquise accused him of suspicion and cunning; he replied that the Court’s caution might be more severe in another judge’s hands and that, in any event, the law must be satisfied. She retorted that even if she owed thirty or fifty thousand francs, it would be little to the d’Espards and the Jeanrenauds—and asked whether her husband’s insanity would prevent a declaration of incapacity. Popinot answered no. She then pledged she could tell him nothing further and suggested he question M. d’Espard directly; proud and impertinent, she bowed and dismissed him.

Afterwards in the street, Popinot remarked dryly to Bianchon that “That woman owes a hundred thousand crowns,” and planned to summon Madame Jeanrenaud. Bianchon wondered at Rastignac’s entanglement with such a woman, and Rastignac declared Popinot not to be “gotten over” but to be “run over” instead.

Next day at four a very stout dame, like a cask in a gown and belt, arrived at Judge Popinot’s. Steaming from a green landau, she announced herself: “It is I, my dear sir—Madame Jeanrenaud.” She was red-pitted with smallpox, round-faced, with bright, honest eyes and a cheerful tone; her chestnut hair was caught under a cap and a shabby green bonnet. Her appearance belied any idea of seduction: she could not tie her shoes, stoop, or wear stays without risking health. She declared she had been a pretty girl, married Jeanrenaud, had a fine son who served in Napoleon’s Guard, and that after hard times—her husband drowned, she got smallpox—she was now what she was: plain and honest.

Popinot bluntly told her she was suspected of using seductive arts to induce M. d’Espard to give him considerable sums. She laughed in outrage: “Seductive arts? Look at me!” She denied any impropriety and professed she could not explain M. d’Espard’s reasons, but vowed that if any trouble came to him on her account she and her son would return every sou. Alarmed by the idea of being worried on her account, she vowed to go and inform Jeanrenaud at once. She bustled away, honest and indignant.

Popinot resolved to visit the Marquis d’Espard the next day to learn the rest of the story.

People who have passed the age when a man wastefully spends his vitality know how small incidents can influence great events, and will not be surprised at the importance here attached to a trivial fact. Next day Popinot had an attack of coryza — a harmless complaint, inadequately called a cold in the head.

The judge, not thinking the delay serious and feeling slightly feverish, stayed in his room and did not go to see the Marquis d’Espard. That lost day was to this affair what the cup of soup taken by Marie de Médici on the Day of Dupes had been; by delaying her meeting with Louis XIII, Richelieu reached Saint-Germain before her and regained his royal captive.

Before accompanying the lawyer and his registering clerk to the Marquis’s house, it is worth glancing at the home and private affairs of this father of sons whom his wife’s petition represented as mad.

In parts of old Paris a few buildings still show an old care for decoration and permanence. The house where M. d’Espard lived, in the Rue de la Montagne-Sainte-Geneviève, was one of those stone mansions, not without a certain stately style; time had blackened the stone and revolutions had injured it. Once occupied by University dignitaries, it had become subdivided and pressed into uses it was not meant to shelter. In the last century a printing establishment had worn the polished floors, soiled carved wood, blackened walls, and altered the main interior arrangements. Formerly the residence of a Cardinal, it was now split among plebeian occupants. The architecture dated from the reigns of Henry III, Henry IV, and Louis XIII, near the hôtels Mignon and Serpente and the Sorbonne.

A perron of several steps still marks the courtyard entrance; the garden front has a similar flight. Despite dilapidations, the balustrades and entrance porch show the architect’s luxury. M. le Marquis d’Espard lived on the ground floor, doubtless to enjoy the garden, spacious for that neighborhood and open for his children’s health. The house’s situation on a steep hill protected ground-floor rooms from damp. He had taken them when rents were moderate to be near the schools and oversee his boys’ education; the owner’s readiness to accommodate him because of needed repairs allowed the Marquis to live respectably without giving cause for complaint. The lofty rooms, paneling reduced to frames, and decorated ceilings still bore the dignity the prelacy stamped on its creations; even small relics of such houses keep an air of authority.

The Marquis had the rooms painted in rich brown tones favored by the Dutch and old Paris citizens, panels hung with plain paper to match, inexpensive curtains chosen for a pleasing effect, and judiciously placed, uncluttered furniture. Any visitor felt a sweet peacefulness from the calm, the unity of color, the careful keeping. Noble details, exquisite cleanliness, and a concord of men and things suggested suavity.

Few persons entered the rooms used by the Marquis and his two sons. A wing toward the street, on the third floor, still had three large rooms left in the grotesque bareness the printing works produced. These served as a writing-room, a depository, and a private room where M. d’Espard worked most of the day on the Picturesque History of China; after breakfast till four he remained there. Visitors usually found him on the third floor, and often the boys came there after school. Thus the ground-floor rooms formed a sanctuary where father and sons spent their evenings, and his domestic life was carefully closed to the public.

His household consisted only of a cook — an old woman long attached to the family — a man-servant of forty who had been with him since his marriage to Mademoiselle de Blamont, and the children’s nurse, who had remained as well. The minute care visible in the apartment testified to the nurse’s maternal attentions in the master’s interest, the house’s management, and the children’s charge. These three serious, uncommunicative people seemed to accept the idea governing the Marquis’ domestic life. Their contrast with ordinary servants cast an air of mystery over the house and fomented the calumny to which M. d’Espard himself gave occasion. Very laudable motives led him to refuse social visiting with other tenants: in educating his boys he wished to keep them from strangers and perhaps to avoid neighborly intrusion.

In a man of his rank, at a time when the Quartier Latin was distracted by liberal ideas, such conduct inevitably roused petty passions. Porters’ gossip and malevolent tattle, unknown to the Marquis and his retainers, turned his reserve into suspicion. His man-servant was stigmatized as a Jesuit, his cook as a sly fox; the nurse was said to collude with Madame Jeanrenaud to rob the madman — the Marquis himself. Gradually the other tenants construed many of his habits as proofs of madness, passing them through their coarse judgment without seeking reasonable motives.

Lacking belief in the History of China’s success, they persuaded the landlord M. d’Espard was penniless, just when, through the forgetfulness that often afflicts busy men, he had allowed the tax collector to send a summons for arrears. The landlord promptly claimed the quarter’s rent from January 1 by sending a receipt, which the portress detained at her leisure. On the 15th a summons to pay was served; the portress delivered it slowly, and the Marquis, living twelve years in the house, supposed it a misunderstanding. He was, however, seized by a bailiff when his man-servant had gone to carry the money for the rent to the landlord.

This arrest, diligently reported to those with whom he was negotiating about his undertaking, alarmed some who were already suspicious of his solvency because vast sums were said to pass from him to Baron Jeanrenaud and his mother. Indeed, such suspicions had some excuse in the Marquis’ extreme economy: he managed his household as a ruined man might. His servants always paid ready cash for trifling necessities, seemingly to avoid credit; had they sought credit, they might have been refused, so strong was the calumnious gossip. Tradesmen often prefer some customers to others for reasons unrelated to fairness; people behave in all classes in ways that compound gossip into prejudice. The shopkeeper who rails at the Court flatters those he envies; such are human inconsistencies.

In short, the Marquis’s and his children’s manners were bound to arouse ill-feeling among neighbors, culminating in malevolence when men do not hesitate to damage those they have themselves created.

M. d’Espard was a gentleman by birth and breeding, as his wife was a lady — noble types by ancient ideas, now rare in France. These characters rest on primitive beliefs and habits formed in infancy: belief in pure blood, in a privileged race, the conviction of standing above common men; education that teaches superiority from boyhood. For forty years merit, battle, glory, or genius have made noblemen; abolition of entail and eldest-son privileges has frittered estates, forcing nobles into business and patient toil to gain greatness — a new era. Regarded as a relic of feudalism, M. d’Espard deserved respectful admiration. If he believed himself by blood superior to others, he also believed in the obligations of nobility; he had the virtues and strength it demands. He raised his children in his principles, inculcating from the cradle the religion of their caste. A deep sense of dignity, pride of name, and the conviction of natural greatness gave them a kingly pride, knightly courage, and baronial protectiveness; their manners, harmonious with their notions, would have become princes and offended the world of the Rue de la Montagne-Sainte-Geneviève — a world of equality where everyone supposed M. d’Espard ruined, and where all refused noble privileges to a nobleman without money, while allowing an enriched bourgeois to usurp them. Thus the family’s lack of communion with neighbors was as much moral as physical.

In father and sons alike personality matched inward spirit. M. d’Espard, about fifty, might have posed as a model of aristocratic birth in the nineteenth century. Slight and fair, his face suggested native distinction and lofty sentiment, yet wore the impress of a deliberate coldness that commanded perhaps excessive respect. His slightly bent aquiline nose, blue eyes, and a high forehead with a thick brow ridge shading his eyes indicated rectitude, perseverance, and loyalty, while giving an odd look to his countenance. That penthouse forehead might hint at a touch of madness; his thick eyebrows added to apparent eccentricity. He had well-kept white hands, a high narrow foot, and hesitating speech — not a mere stammer but a faltering in the expression of ideas that made him seem to come and go, try everything, break off gestures, and finish nothing. This defect contrasted with a decisive mouth and strongly marked physiognomy. His jerky gait matched his speech. These peculiarities confirmed rumors of insanity. Despite an elegant appearance, he was systematically parsimonious in personal expenses, wearing the same black frock coat for three or four years, carefully brushed by his old man-servant.

The children were handsome, with a grace not excluding aristocratic disdain. They had bright coloring, clear eyes, transparent complexions showing regular life, and a proper balance of work and play. Both had black hair, blue eyes, and their father’s nose twist; their mother perhaps transmitted the Blamont-Chauvry dignity of speech and mien. Their voices, clear as crystal with an emotional softness, were seductive; they had voices that would invite a woman to listen after their looks had already kindled curiosity. Above all, they had the modesty of pride: a chastely reserved touch-me-not which at maturity might seem coyness, making others desire to know them.

The elder, Comte Clément de Négrepelisse, was nearly sixteen. For two years he had ceased to wear the pretty English round jacket which his brother, Vicomte Camille d’Espard, still wore. The Count, who for six months had forsaken the Collège Henri IV, dressed like a young man beginning to enjoy fashion. His father would not condemn him to a year’s useless study of philosophy; instead he sought consistency in Clément’s education through transcendental mathematics. At the same time the Marquis arranged for him instruction in Eastern languages, the international law of Europe, heraldry, and history from original sources — charters, early documents, and edicts. Camille had lately begun the study of rhetoric.

The day Popinot arranged to question M. d’Espard was a Thursday holiday. About nine in the morning, before their father awoke, the two brothers were playing in the garden. Clément, easily persuaded, agreed to accompany his brother to the shooting-gallery for the first time; they wrestled and quarrelled like schoolboys. Their noisy play woke their father, who, unnoticed, came to the window and amused himself watching them tumble and seize each other, their faces flushed with exertion. Two other people, one on the second and one on the first floor, also looked into the garden and, noting the scene, joked that the old madman amused himself by making his children fight. Heads soon appeared at the windows; the Marquis, seeing them, called his sons, who climbed up and jumped into his room. Clément obtained the permission Camille had asked for.

Throughout the house people repeated the tale of the Marquis’ new form of insanity. When Popinot arrived about noon with his clerk, the portress, full of gossip, announced that M. d’Espard had set his two children to fight that very morning, laughing like a monster when the younger bit the elder till he bled, implying he wanted them to kill each other. She led Popinot and the clerk upstairs; the stair landing and rooms showed neglect—muddy floor, dirty banisters, a dilapidated window, ceilings sooted with apprentices’ drawings—details that supported the Marquise’s account and inclined the judge to believe it.

“She there, gentlemen,” said the porter’s wife; “there is the manufactory, where the Chinese swallow up enough to feed the whole neighborhood.” The clerk smiled, Popinot struggled to keep an expression, and they entered the outer shop-room where a man in a gray blouse presided over prints—this was the Maitre Jacques of China. Shelves held published numbers; a wooden partition with a grating and green curtains formed a private office; a till marked the cash-desk.

Popinot asked for M. d’Espard. The shopman opened the next room, revealing a venerable white-headed man, simply dressed, wearing the Cross of Saint-Louis, seated at a desk. He rose, polite and gentlemanly. Popinot explained they wished to speak with him on business personal to himself. The old man announced the visitors to the Marquis, who sat by the fire reading a newspaper.

The Marquis’ innermost room was plain: shabby carpet, gray holland curtains, a few chairs and armchairs, a desk, and a dingy clock on the chimney. After civilities, Popinot explained his mission: he was a judge of the Inferior Court of Appeal for the Department of the Seine, charged by the President with examining the Marquis because the Marquise d’Espard had petitioned for a Commission in Lunacy. The old man withdrew; the clerk sat to take notes.

At this accusation the Marquis, usually pale, flushed scarlet with anger. He trembled, collected himself, and asked how he had had no notice of the petition. Popinot explained that law presumes those subject to such a commission lack reason, so notice is unnecessary; the Court’s duty is to verify the petitioner’s allegations. The Marquis, in pain, pleaded for secrecy, fearing blame might attach to his wife. Popinot and the Marquis exchanged assurances of discretion. Popinot dismissed his clerk, intending to question the Marquis alone.

Popinot announced the most serious accusation in the petition concerned dissipation of the Marquis’ fortune to the benefit of a certain Madame Jeanrenaud, widow of a bargemaster, or rather to her son, Colonel Jeanrenaud. The petition alleged the Marquis had procured an appointment for him, exhausted influence with the King, and arranged a favorable marriage for him—actions suggesting a friendship more devoted than propriety allowed. The Marquis, moved, explained that revealing his motives would expose family wounds and required secrecy; still, he felt obliged to explain.

He related his family’s history. The family name was Negrepelisse; d’Espard was a title acquired under Henri IV by a marriage that brought estates and titles on condition of bearing an escutcheon of pretence. The Crown had treated earlier members unjustly; his great-grandfather, like him, had been placed early at his head with little but entailed estates, which were then augmented by royal favor. But a blot stained their escutcheon—an unconfessed stain of blood and disgrace M. d’Espard had discovered while examining old deeds and letters.

He described events after the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. Favorites of Louis XIV enriched themselves by appropriating confiscated lands of Protestants who could not legally transfer their property. The lands of Negrepelisse and Gravenges, once belonging to a Protestant family, passed to his grandfather by royal gift. He had learned that the original owner, attempting to secure his affairs before leaving for Switzerland, was entrapped: arrested by order of the governor, the trustee confessed, and the poor merchant was hanged; ransom money sent by the refugee family was kept by the governor, who was his grandfather’s uncle. The letter he read revealed jocularity about the victim; the discovery filled him with horror.

The family who suffered this fate bore the name Jeanrenaud. This name explained the Marquis’ later conduct. He could not think of his family’s secret disgrace without pain. Determined to remedy the wrong, he wrote to Switzerland and tracked down the heirs of the Protestant victim. He found M. Jeanrenaud, a lieutenant in a cavalry regiment, the sole heir. To the Marquis the Jeanrenauds’ rights were clear: prescriptive rights presuppose some means of redress, and these refugees had none—justice, in his view, was absent when the State itself had been the persecutor. He did not wish his children to consider their nobility tainted by ancestral crime. Politically, refugees appealing against revolutionary confiscations should not keep property that originated in ancient crimes, he argued.

M. Jeanrenaud and his mother, to the Marquis’ astonishment, behaved with scrupulous honesty; they asked only the value of the lands when the King had bestowed them—eleven hundred thousand francs, payable at the Marquis’ convenience and without interest. To pay this he had to forego his income for a long time. Proposing they should leave Paris and live more cheaply so restitution could be completed faster, he found his wife contemptuous and unhelpful. Madame d’Espard would have approved his grandfather’s conduct, he felt; she was cold, indifferent toward their children, and unwilling to shoulder shared debts. Alarmed by her character and affectionless conduct, he resigned himself to educating the children himself and letting her keep command of her fortune after he paid the common debts. Investing his money in the funds allowed him to repay the sum faster than expected; he had lately repaid the whole eleven hundred thousand francs. He defended his payments to Madame Jeanrenaud and her son as acts of honor and restitution, not dissipation.

Popinot asked whether Madame d’Espard had known the motives for his retirement and the payments. “Yes, monsieur,” the Marquis replied. Popinot, visibly moved but judicial, indicated he would hear further points in the petition—such as the fact that the Marquis carried on a business not usually undertaken by a man of rank. The Marquis asked to discuss that matter apart and, with the old comte Nouvion present in the house, led the judge into the ground-floor rooms he used as his apartment.

Popinot admired the poetry of the dwelling: open windows, garden air, sunshine brightening the woodwork. The Marquis explained the rooms on the top floor were for the office of his undertaking—publishing a "Picturesque History of China"—and that the work was being brought out by a respectable Paris firm. He had partnered with the Comte de Nouvion, an emigré who had returned without fortune; the concern was less for profit than to give Nouvion comfort. The Marquis’ tutor had been the Abbé Grozier, Keeper of the Books at the Arsenal, who had taught him Chinese; at twenty-five he knew the language and loved China’s history, institutions, monuments, and administration. The undertaking had literary, iconographical, statistical, and religious importance, with two thousand five hundred subscribers—twelve hundred in France—and would produce income for the Comte. For his part the hundred thousand francs he made would pay for fencing lessons, horses, dress, theatres, masters—pleasures for his children he felt a father should provide. His twelve years of retirement had left him forgotten at Court; he had sacrificed prospects but believed his sons would become men of mark and that purifying the family past while securing a glorious future for his children was a noble, though secret, task.

At that moment the tramp of horses sounded in the courtyard; the two lads, fashionably but plainly dressed, entered—booted, spurred, whips in hand—beaming with health. They grasped their father’s hand, gave a look of affection, then bowed coldly to the lawyer. They told of knocking down six dolls at the shooting-gallery and of riding in the Bois, seeing their mother but not speaking to her; they suggested she perhaps did not care to be spoken to in public, implying a cool relation. These words clouded the Marquis’ brow, and Popinot enjoyed the picture of father and sons. The Marquis, moved and hesitant, observed that if anyone here were crazy it could only be the children for being so dearly attached to their father and the father for being so mad about his children—"that sort of madness rings true."

At that moment Madame Jeanrenaud bustled into the room despite the man-servant’s remonstrances. Indignant at the very notion that the law would brand the Marquis a lunatic, she declared she and her son were of one mind to give everything back if their honor were threatened; they would return all rather than cause him trouble. “They must be as stupid as pans without handles to call you a lunatic,” she cried. The two boys, alarmed by the word "criminal" that Popinot had been called, clung to their father and then, at his command, went into the garden.

Popinot addressed Madame Jeanrenaud calmly: the moneys paid to her had been legally due, though given from a lofty notion of honesty. If every possessor of confiscated goods were compelled to make restitution centuries later, few legitimate owners would remain. Past confiscations had enriched many noble families; law lets a proprietor dispose of his income without accounting for ancestral misdeeds. A Commission in Lunacy is only granted when actions lack reason; here, the remittances had a reason rooted in sacred, honorable motives. Hence she might keep what she had received without remorse and let the world misinterpret the noble action. Popinot pronounced that, given the present condition of society, the Marquis’ deeds were sublime rather than insane; he said he had never been more touched than by what he had seen and heard. He hoped the Marquis would feel certain of his silence and not be uneasy about any decision that might come before the Court.

Madame Jeanrenaud, delighted, praised the judge. The Marquis and Popinot exchanged an understanding look and a press of hands—two different natures in unison: the judge kindly practical, the Marquis lofty and noble. Popinot told the Marquis it was time to return home and advised him to counteract whatever influence had led his wife to petition. Popinot left, visibly touched by the scene; he even thought the rooms might suit him if the Marquis should vacate them.

The next morning Popinot went to the Palais de Justice intending prompt action; he had written his report. In the robing-room the usher informed him the Court President wanted to see him. The President told Popinot in private that the Keeper of the Seals, with whom he had dined, had been told Popinot had been to tea with Madame d’Espard in whose case he had been employed. It was thought proper that Popinot should not sit on the case: "Caesar's wife must not be suspected." Though the President believed Popinot had acted with strict independence—and recalled he himself had sometimes taken tea with those he tried—the Keeper’s comment made it risky for the Bench amid public opinion and journalism. To avoid scandal, Popinot was asked to signify his retirement from the case as a personal favor. As compensation, the President would see that Popinot received the Cross of the Legion of Honor, long due to him.

Popinot bitterly accepted the decision without denying the accusation. M. Camusot, a judge recently appointed to Paris from a provincial Court, was named in his place—a pale, fair young man, ambitious and servile. Popinot withdrew with a bow, scornful of the false charge. The story ends with the date, Paris, February 1836.
