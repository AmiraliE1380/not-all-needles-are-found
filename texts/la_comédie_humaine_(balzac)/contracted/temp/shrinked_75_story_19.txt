**GOBSECK**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated By Ellen Marriage*  

**DEDICATION**  
To M. le Baron Barchou de Penhoen.  

In the winter of 1829-30, at one o'clock in the morning, the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu's salon was nearly empty. After a young man departed, the Vicomtesse approached her daughter, Camille, who was distractedly listening to the sounds outside. Concerned about Camille's behavior towards the Comte de Restaud, the Vicomtesse warned her about his mother, Mlle. Goriot, whose questionable past could jeopardize Camille's future. Despite the young Count's admirable qualities, his family background raised alarms.

Derville, a family friend and attorney, overheard their conversation and decided to intervene. He had previously helped the Vicomtesse regain her family's lost properties, significantly improving her financial situation. His success earned him respect in high society, yet he remained humble and declined offers to advance his career further.

As Derville recounted a story that might alter Camille's perception of Restaud, he noted her growing affection for the young man. At a recent ball, Camille expressed confidence in Restaud's potential, believing he would achieve wealth and status through his abilities. Derville, however, pointed out that if Restaud were already wealthy, many girls would compete for his attention, hinting at Camille's feelings for him.

This realization shifted Camille's demeanor towards Derville, who had previously been more of a polite acquaintance than a friend. Her newfound attentiveness suggested a budding connection, despite the underlying tension of social obligations and gratitude that often complicates relationships between different social classes.

Derville reflects on a significant romantic event from his past, centering on a usurer named Gobseck. Gobseck is described as a sallow, impassive man with a meticulous lifestyle, living in a damp, dark house that resembles a convent. He is a solitary figure, rarely interacting with others except for Derville, who becomes his confidant over four years. Gobseck, born Jean-Esther Van Gobseck, is a wealthy usurer who has lived a life filled with adventure and danger, yet remains emotionally detached.

Derville recounts a conversation with Gobseck, who expresses his views on life, emphasizing the importance of gold as the ultimate form of power and reality. He dismisses societal norms and conventions, arguing that self-interest is the only constant in human nature. Gobseck's perspective is cynical; he believes that true happiness lies in the pursuit of wealth and the manipulation of others.

One day, Gobseck shares his experiences collecting debts, revealing two bills of exchange he must collect: one from a Countess and another from a struggling linen-draper named Fanny Malvaut. The Countess, who is beautiful yet burdened by her extravagant lifestyle, ultimately offers Gobseck a diamond in lieu of payment. Derville is captivated by her beauty and the contrast between her life and that of Fanny, who lives modestly and works hard.

Derville visits Fanny, who embodies innocence and sincerity, contrasting sharply with the Countess's world of luxury and moral decay. He contemplates the differences between their lives, feeling a sense of pity for both women. Gobseck's insights into human nature and the power dynamics of society leave Derville feeling disturbed and reflective about the nature of wealth and desire.

As Derville grapples with his feelings for the Countess and his admiration for Fanny, he realizes the profound impact of Gobseck's worldview. The usurer's life, driven by the pursuit of gold and power, serves as a stark reminder of the moral complexities and emotional voids that accompany such a lifestyle. Ultimately, Derville is left contemplating the true cost of wealth and the fleeting nature of beauty and happiness.

“Will you take a glass of _eau sucree_?” asked the Vicomtesse, interrupting Derville.

“I should be glad of it,” he replied.

Mme. de Grandlieu expressed skepticism about the relevance of the conversation, prompting Derville to invoke Sardanapalus. He recounted how Mademoiselle Camille's happiness had once depended on Daddy Gobseck, who had recently passed away, leaving M. de Restaud a fortune. Derville then mentioned his wife, Fanny Malvaut.

“I would proclaim it to the universe,” he said, affirming his love for her.

Derville continued, explaining that after submitting his thesis, he became a licentiate in law and later an advocate. Gobseck, who had previously underestimated him, began to consult him on complex matters, showing newfound respect. Eventually, Derville became head-clerk and moved into his employer’s house, marking a significant step in his career.

When he bid farewell to Gobseck, the old man showed no emotion but later sought Derville’s advice on a challenging expropriation case. Derville’s principal, a man of pleasure, fell into financial trouble and decided to sell his practice for 150,000 francs. Lacking funds, Derville approached Gobseck for a loan, determined to maintain his self-respect.

Gobseck, after assessing Derville’s situation, agreed to lend him the money at a rate of thirteen percent per annum, a deal Derville accepted despite the high interest. They discussed guarantees, and Gobseck insisted on receiving bills made payable to a third party. Derville agreed to provide legal advice to Gobseck for free as part of their arrangement.

Three months later, Derville became an attorney and successfully recovered estates for Mme. de Grandlieu, gaining recognition in the legal field. He married Fanny, whose inheritance helped him pay off Gobseck within five years. Their shared struggles deepened their bond, leading to a happy life together.

A year after acquiring his practice, Derville attended a bachelor breakfast hosted by M. de Trailles, a well-known dandy. The event was marked by extravagant displays and chaotic revelry, where Derville found himself charmed by de Trailles, who persuaded him to introduce him to Gobseck.

The next day, Derville hesitated but ultimately agreed to accompany de Trailles to meet Gobseck. Upon arrival, de Trailles appeared anxious, revealing a woman in a cab nearby, which excited him. Derville introduced de Trailles to Gobseck, who remained impassive despite de Trailles’ attempts at charm.

Gobseck refused to lend money to de Trailles, citing his debts and lack of security. De Trailles, undeterred, left the room to fetch something he believed would satisfy Gobseck. Gobseck expressed concern about de Trailles’ intentions, revealing a rare moment of vulnerability.

When de Trailles returned with a woman, Derville recognized her as the Countess, one of Goriot’s daughters. The Countess, visibly distressed, sought to pawn her diamonds for cash, hoping to retain the right to repurchase them. Derville explained the terms of the transaction, which relieved her but darkened de Trailles’ mood.

Gobseck examined the diamonds with an unsettling eagerness, revealing his obsession with wealth. His reaction was childlike, contrasting sharply with his usual stoicism. The scene underscored the complex interplay of desire, power, and desperation among the characters, particularly as they navigated the treacherous waters of social and financial relationships in Paris.

The dialogue begins with a discussion about a set of diamonds, valued highly before the Revolution, but now losing worth due to market saturation from Brazilian stones. Gobseck, a moneylender, examines the diamonds with delight, noting their flaws and qualities. The Count seeks a loan of one hundred thousand francs for three years, and Gobseck agrees but insists on weighing the diamonds first. The Countess appears distressed, hinting at her moral struggle.

As Gobseck weighs the diamonds, the narrator observes the Countess's turmoil, sensing her remorse. The narrator questions the Countess about the ownership of the diamonds, which she confirms are hers. However, he refuses to draft the loan agreement, citing legal issues since she is married. Gobseck then offers a deal: eighty thousand francs down, with the diamonds left as collateral. The Countess hesitates, and Maxime, a young man present, expresses his despair, suggesting he will end his troubles soon.

Ultimately, the Countess accepts Gobseck's offer, and he provides her with a check and cash. Tensions rise as Maxime insults Gobseck, who coolly threatens him with pistols. After the Countess leaves, Gobseck revels in his acquisition of the diamonds, showcasing his greed and delight in the transaction.

Days later, the Count visits the narrator, expressing confidence in him and discussing Gobseck's character. The Count reveals his intention to transfer his property to Gobseck, fearing for his health and future. The narrator warns the Count about disinheriting his other children, leading the Count to agree to include them in the counter-deed.

The narrative shifts to the Countess, who, despite her past, is determined to secure her children's future. She becomes increasingly desperate as her husband’s health declines. The Count, bedridden and resentful, forbids her from entering his room, leading her to spy on him. She believes he is hiding money and documents that could secure their wealth.

As the Count nears death, he confides in his son Ernest, entrusting him with a secret letter for the narrator. The Countess, sensing her husband's intentions, tries to manipulate Ernest into revealing the secret. The Count confronts her, leading to a dramatic scene where he accuses her of treachery and expresses his disdain for her actions.

After the Count's death, chaos ensues as the Countess searches for documents, revealing her desperation. The narrator discovers that the Count had hidden important papers, including the counter-deed, which the Countess inadvertently destroys in her panic. Gobseck, now the rightful owner of the property, contemplates the implications of the Countess's actions.

In the aftermath, the narrator reflects on the Countess's transformation and the consequences of her greed. Gobseck, despite his avarice, shows a peculiar understanding of human nature, believing that adversity teaches valuable lessons. The story concludes with the narrator contemplating the fate of the Countess and her children, as well as Gobseck's legacy.

The text explores themes of greed, morality, and the complexities of human relationships, illustrating the destructive power of ambition and the consequences of choices made in desperation.
