DEDICATION

To Monsieur le Comte Ferdinand de Gramont.

MY DEAR FERDINAND,—If fortune allows these lines to endure, they will repay a little for the trouble you have taken—you, the Hozier, the Cherin, the King-at-Arms of these Studies of Life; you to whom the Navarreins, Cadignans, Langeais, Blamont-Chauvrys, Chaulieus, Arthez, Esgrignons, Mortsaufs, Valois—the great names of the “Human Comedy”—owe their mottoes and bearings. The Armorial of the Etudes, devised by Ferdinand de Gramont, is a complete manual of French Heraldry, forgetting nothing, even the arms of the Empire, and I shall preserve it as a monument of friendship and patience. What knowledge of the feudal spirit is shown in the Beauseants’ Pulchre sedens, melius agens; the Espards’ Des partem leonis; the Vandenesses’ Ne se vend. Your poetic touch brings elegance and accuracy to countless learned details.

Your old friend,
DE BALZAC.

THE MUSE OF THE DEPARTMENT

On the skirts of Le Berry stands Sancerre, a Loire town that crowns the highest hill of the Nivernais range. Its slopes descend to fertile yellow alluvium and, where floods have lain sand, to flats. Because the hill dominates the river, the little port of Saint-Thibault handles Sancerre’s commerce in wine, oak staves, and produce. By the time this story opens, suspension bridges at Cosne and Saint-Thibault have replaced ferries, signaling the post-1830 improvements favored by the House of Orléans.

The town’s streets are steep and pebble-paved, girded by slopes called the Great Ramparts, and surrounded by vineyards. Wine is the chief industry: aromatic vintages close to Burgundy in character that must reach the Paris market because many won’t keep beyond seven or eight years. Below Sancerre lie small villages—Fontenoy and Saint-Satur—almost suburbs, their smiling vineyards recalling Neuchâtel.

Sancerre retains narrow streets, ancient houses, and a citadel—a feudal relic that survived one of the most terrible sieges of the religious wars when French Calvinists showed ferocity surpassing even Scott’s Cameronians. Once richer and strategically important, the town has lost martial consequence and faces decline as trade favors the Loire’s right bank despite two bridges to Cosne.

On the left bank, Sancerre prides itself on its view, pure air, fine vegetation, and citizens friendly and in tune with nature; though two-thirds are Calvinists, they are not Puritans. Small-town intrusions into private life coexist with a local patriotism grounded in home feeling.

With at most 3,500 inhabitants compared to Cosne’s over 6,000, Sancerre’s fortunes have waned. It boasts notable sons—Horace Bianchon, a glory in modern medicine, and Étienne Lousteau, an influential journalist—but political power lies with seven or eight large landowners who control elections. A small conspiracy to throw off this electoral yoke failed because jealousy among its leaders split them. The proposed remedy—uniting behind a single champion at the next election, ideally one of the two distinguished men from Paris—originated with the borough’s Superior Woman, whose personal stakes and past require a brief account of her antecedent career.

Sancerre, about 1836, could boast a Superior Woman, long misprized but now locally celebrated. This was also the era when two Sancerrois in Paris achieved distinction: Etienne Lousteau wrote reviews for a widely read paper, and Bianchon, already chief physician to a hospital, Officer of the Legion of Honor, and member of the Academy of Sciences, had just been made a professor.

If George Sand did not always imply blame, one might say she created “Sandism,” a sentimental school whose excesses could be charming but also revealed many superior women. Sand’s celebrity showed that France contained far more such women than had been acknowledged while the Maréchal de Saxe’s granddaughter held the field alone.

The Superior Woman of Sancerre lived at La Baudraye, a town- and country-house within ten minutes of Sancerre, in the suburb of Saint-Satur. Present La Baudrayes, like many newer namesakes, had supplanted the older family whose fame dated to the Crusades and Le Berry’s history. The story of La Baudraye must be told.

In Louis XIV’s time a sheriff named Milaud, whose ancestors had been fervent Calvinists, converted at the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. To encourage conversions in a Calvinist stronghold the King gave him an appointment in the “Waters and Forests,” arms, the title Sire de la Baudraye, and the fief. Yet later descendants of the famous Captain la Baudraye fell victim to new anti-heretic decrees and were hanged—an unworthy deed of the great King.

Under Louis XV, a Milaud rose from squire to Chevalier and used influence to obtain a cornet’s commission in the Musketeers for his son, who perished at Fontenoy, leaving a child. Louis XVI later granted this child, in memory of his father, the privileges of a farmer-general. This financier, elegant in society and fond of charades and verses for Chloris, lived among the nobility in exile but kept his money, aiding several highborn families. Tired of hoping and perhaps lending, he returned to Sancerre in 1800 and bought back La Baudraye out of vanity and pride—a decision intelligible in a sheriff’s grandson though the Consulate made prospects slender. He doubted his heir would perpetuate the la Baudraye line.

Jean Athanase Polydore Milaud de la Baudraye, the only son, was delicate from birth, plainly the child of an exhausted constitution brought on by excess and late marriage—a sign of degeneracy in high society. Madame de la Baudraye, a Casteran de la Tour, reared this sallow, sickly boy with devoted maternal care. Her death partly prompted Monsieur de la Baudraye’s return to France. On his death the Lucullus of the Milauds left his son the fief—stripped of feudal dues but adorned with weathercocks bearing his arms—plus a thousand louis-d’or in 1802 and certain receipts for claims on distinguished émigrés, enclosed in a pocketbook full of verses wrapped with Vanitas vanitatum et omnia vanitas.

Young La Baudraye did not die. He lived through monastic habits, the economy of action that Fontenelle preached as a religion for invalids, and above all the healthful air and elevation of Sancerre, which overlooks the Loire valley for leagues. Between 1802 and 1815 he added vineyard plots and devoted himself to viticulture. The Restoration initially seemed insecure; he dared not go to Paris to claim his debts. After Napoleon’s death he tried to convert his father’s autographs into money, failing to grasp the odd mixture of IOUs and verses. The winegrower wasted time trying to impress his identity on the Duke of Nivernais “and others,” returning to his vintage having won nothing but offers of service.

The Restoration’s elevation of nobility made La Baudraye desire an heir. At forty-three in 1823—years no doctor would have promised—he hoped to reap the reward of sober living. His choice, however, seemed imprudent given his frail constitution; townsfolk suspected some deeper calculation.

At that time the Archbishop of Bourges had converted a young woman, daughter of a citizen family that had upheld Calvinism and had escaped persecution under Louis XIV. The Piedefers—whose name suggested Reformation champions—had become respectable cloth merchants. In Louis XVI’s reign Abraham Piedefer fell into difficulties and left his children poor in 1786. One son, Tobie, went to the Indies; Moise Piedefer bought nationalized land during the Revolution, demolished abbeys with ancestral zeal, married a Catholic daughter of a Convention member who had been executed, and died in 1819, leaving a remarkably beautiful little girl named Dinah, raised in the Calvinist faith.

Mademoiselle Dinah Piedefer was sent to the Demoiselles Chamarolles’ school in Bourges and soon distinguished herself for mind and beauty, though snubbed by girls of birth intended for higher worlds. Determined to match them, Dinah resolved to renounce Calvinism in hopes the Cardinal would favor his proselyte. Her conversion at seventeen was an ambition-driven act.

The Archbishop, convinced Dinah would adorn society, sought her a husband. But families recoiled from a woman whose charms and education promised more expense than a husband could bear, even if she stood to inherit a modest share from La Hautoy. When these facts reached Polydore de la Baudraye, he went to Bourges; Madame Piedefer, devout and anxious for security, was ready to accept the first well-off suitor. The Cardinal welcomed Monsieur de la Baudraye, and La Baudraye, delighted to receive a wife from the Cardinal’s hands, only asked a formal promise that His Eminence would support his claims with the President of the Council to help recover debts from the Duc de Nivernais “and others.” The minister in the Pavillon Marsan found the plan too sharp, promising to attend to the business in good time rather than immediately assisting him.

The announcement of La Baudraye’s imprudent marriage excited the Sancerre district. “It is quite intelligible,” said President Boirouge; the little man had been startled hearing handsome young Milaud, the Attorney-General’s deputy at Nevers, claim La Baudraye’s turrets for himself. Little La Baudraye, a changeling, would hate such a colossal rival. In Nevers, a plebeian branch of the Milauds had grown rich in cutlery and placed a representative in the civil service.

To explain La Baudraye’s negotiations in Paris—and certain mysterious political phenomena that hampered the Ministry during the Restoration—it helps to recount his meeting with a Ministry emissary. The ministers’ promises being illusory, La Baudraye went to Paris when the Cardinal was called to the Chamber.

Lodging at the Hotel de Mayence on Rue Saint-Honoré, the provincial was visited one morning by a confidential agent of the Ministry, an expert in “winding up” business. The elegant visitor, shown to a small room, observed La Baudraye cooking coffee over a bedroom fire and noted his shabby dressing-gown and tin saucepan. The Parisian did not mince words.

“I will lay a wager, monsieur,” he said, “that you dine for forty sous at Hurbain’s in the Palais-Royal... All the princes’ creditors dine there. You recover scarcely ten per cent on debts from these fine gentlemen.” La Baudraye, thinking the man had come to buy his bills, was corrected: the visitor was Monsieur des Lupeaulx, Master of Appeals and Secretary-General to the Ministry, proposing an arrangement.

Des Lupeaulx explained the political arithmetic: nobles in the King’s favor had no money but must show well; the Duc de Navarreins, a first gentleman of the Bedchamber, could not repudiate his debt but could not pay. La Baudraye, a clever noble, could take payment in favors—royal or ministerial. The collectorship at Sancerre was vacant; La Baudraye could be given the place, hold it briefly, resign, and sell it to Monsieur Gravier for twenty thousand francs; the Order of the Legion of Honor would also be conferred. In return he would restore all claims on the Duc de Navarreins.

Tempted by money and a red ribbon, La Baudraye accepted. He returned to Sancerre as Collector of Taxes; six months later he was superseded by Monsieur Gravier, an agreeable financier of the Empire, whom La Baudraye introduced to his wife.

Thus, through vanity, ambition, ministerial bargaining, and the Restoration’s political maneuvers, the modest vineyard-owner of La Baudraye married into a promising match, sought to recover émigré claims, and was ultimately placated with a short-lived office and honors in place of actual payment. The episode illustrates how personal pride, social aspiration, and statecraft intersected in provincial France under the restored monarchy.

When released from his functions, Monsieur de la Baudraye returned to Paris to settle accounts with his debtors. He became Referendary under the Great Seal, a Baron, and an Officer of the Legion of Honor. After selling the referendary appointment and collecting remaining debts, he reappeared at Sancerre as Master of Appeals and secured a sinecure as Royal Commissioner to a commercial association in the Nivernais with a salary of six thousand francs. What seemed a financial blunder proved a shrewd match in marriage.

By thrift and an indemnity from his father’s estate—nationalized and sold in 1793—he achieved his ambition by 1827. After six years of strict economy he paid four hundred thousand francs down and committed to further instalments to buy the estate of Anzy on the Loire, two leagues above Sancerre, and its celebrated castle by Philibert de l’Orme, long the Uxelles family seat. An entail created by letters patent in December 1820 included Anzy, La Baudraye, and La Hautoy—though this did not erase Dinah’s long years of unacknowledged parsimony until 1835.

This financial story explains the first Baron de la Baudraye’s character. Those who know rural manias will recognize in him a land‑hunger that becomes a consuming passion—an outward avarice that often leads to ruin when mortgage interest outstrips agricultural yield. From 1802 to 1827, the ridiculed little man who trotted to Saint‑Thibault and tended his vineyards surprised his mockers when he seized the prize after the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse’s extravagance forced a sale.

Madame Piedefer came to live with her daughter; she accepted an annuity of twelve hundred francs on La Hautoy and handed it to her son‑in‑law, so the combined acknowledged income of Monsieur de la Baudraye and his mother‑in‑law reached about fifteen thousand francs.

Early in her married life Dinah transformed La Baudraye. She converted a forecourt into a formal garden, removed wine‑stores and shabby outhouses, and laid out a second garden behind the turreted manor with shrubs, flower‑beds and lawns screened from the vineyards by a creeper‑clad wall. She made the interiors as comfortable as circumstances allowed. To avoid being ruined by so superior a young woman, Monsieur de la Baudraye prudently kept silent about his Parisian recoveries; that secrecy lent him mystery and dignity in his wife’s eyes.

The changes at La Baudraye made everyone eager to see the mistress. When, one spring morning in 1825, she appeared on the Mall in a blue velvet dress with her mother in black, Sancerre buzzed. Visitors admired fine furnishings—shawl‑pattern carpets, a carved gilt writing‑table, brocade curtains, a Japanese bowl of flowers among recent books—and hearing Dinah play the piano without demur confirmed her reputation. To avoid carelessness or bad taste she kept abreast of fashions and luxury through active correspondence with Anna Grossetete, a schoolfriend who had married well; thus Dinah often led, and distanced, local fashion.

These trifles bred malignant envy among Sancerre’s ladies, but Dinah’s conversation and wit provoked absolute aversion. She tolerated no vacuous small talk, no old‑fashioned compliments, no petty slander. She would not endure tittle‑tattle; she preferred hearing of discoveries in science and art, the latest theatre pieces and poems, and by airing current cant she gave the impression of thinking deeply.

Several men found her company congenial. The Abbé Duret, an old worldly cleric fond of cards, welcomed her taste; the sous‑préfet, Vicomte de Chargeboeuf, found in her drawing‑room an oasis from provincial life. Monsieur de Clagny, the public prosecutor, so admired Dinah that he refused promotion and became a devoted worshipper—tall, lean, with a minatory countenance and terrible eyes beneath enormous eyebrows, eloquent though not amorous. Monsieur Gravier, a little, round man who had been a charming ballad‑singer in the Empire and later became Paymaster‑General, secured the Receivership at Sancerre by payment and affected conspicuous fashion—buff waistcoat, gray trousers, snug coats, a silk tie slipped through a diamond ring. He fancied himself superior to Clagny.

These four men extolled Dinah’s cultivation, taste and refinement, calling her a woman of superior mind. The women, however, whispered that “Madame de la Baudraye must laugh at us behind our back.” Branded a pedant for speaking grammatically, nicknamed the Sappho of Saint‑Satur, Dinah found herself ostracized: in a community of hunched backs a straight figure is monstrous. When she asked Clagny why neighborhood women made so few calls, he replied, “You are too superior a woman to be liked by other women.” Gravier, pressed, admitted that Dinah was not content with mere charm: she was clever, well educated, read every new book, loved poetry, played music and spoke delightfully—too much for jealous women to forgive.

Men told Monsieur de la Baudraye he was fortunate to have such a wife, and Madame Piedefer boasted likewise in society. Those who know the world, especially Paris, know how such reputations are made.

Two years later, by the end of 1825, Dinah de la Baudraye was accused of preferring only men as visitors; soon it was said she did not care for women—and that became a crime. Not a trifling action escaped criticism or misrepresentation. After making every sacrifice a well-bred woman could make and placing herself entirely in the right, Madame de la Baudraye was so rash as to say to a false friend who condoled with her isolation: “I would rather have my bowl empty than with anything in it!” The remark produced a terrible effect in Sancerre and was cruelly retorted on the Sappho of Saint-Satur when, being childless after five years of marriage, little de la Baudraye became a byword for laughter.

To explain the provincial witticism, recall the Bailli de Ferrette—some may have known him—of whom it was said he was the bravest man in Europe for daring to walk on his legs and who was accused of putting lead in his shoes to save himself from being blown away. Monsieur de la Baudraye, a sallow, almost diaphanous creature, would have been engaged by the Bailli de Ferrette as first gentleman-in-waiting if that diplomatist had been the Grand Duke of Baden rather than merely his envoy.

Monsieur de la Baudraye’s legs were so thin that, for decency, he wore false calves; his thighs were like the arms of an average man, his body not unlike that of a cockchafer, and he would have made an advantageous foil to the Bailli de Ferrette. As he walked his leg-pads often twisted onto his shins; he thanked anyone who warned him. He wore knee-breeches, black silk stockings, and a white waistcoat until 1824. After the marriage he adopted blue trousers and boots with heels, which made Sancerre declare that he had added two inches to his stature so as to come up to his wife’s chin. For ten years he was always seen in the same little bottle-green coat with large white-metal buttons and a black stock that accentuated his cold, stingy face, lighted by gray-blue eyes as keen and passionless as a cat’s. Gentle, as men with fixed habits are, he seemed to please his wife by never contradicting her; he allowed her to do the talking and moved with the deliberate tenacity of an insect.

Dinah, adored for her unrivaled beauty and admired for her cleverness by the most gentlemanly men of the place, encouraged their admiration by conversations it was later said she prepared beforehand. Finding her listeners rapt, she began to listen to herself, enjoyed haranguing them, and at last regarded her friends as the chorus in a tragedy, there only to give her cues. In fact, she had a fine collection of phrases and ideas, derived either from books or by assimilating the opinions of her companions, and thus became a sort of mechanical instrument, going off on a round of phrases as soon as some chance remark released the spring. To her credit, Dinah was full of knowledge and read everything—medical books, statistics, science, jurisprudence—because she did not know how to spend her days once she had reviewed her flower-beds and given orders to the gardener. Gifted with an excellent memory and the knack some women have for hitting the right word, she could talk on any subject with the lucidity of a studied style. Men came from Cosne, La Charité, Nevers, and from neighbouring towns to be introduced to Madame de la Baudraye, as in Switzerland they were introduced to Madame de Staël. Those who heard the tunes emitted by this musical snuff-box went away amazed and told such wonders of Dinah that all the women ten leagues round grew jealous.

There is an indescribable headiness in the admiration we inspire, or in the effect of playing a part, which keeps criticism from reaching the idol. An atmosphere, perhaps from unceasing nervous tension, forms a halo through which the world below is seen. How otherwise account for the perennial good faith that leads to repeated presentments of the same effects, and the constant ignoring of warnings given by children, so dreaded by parents, or by husbands familiar with the peacock airs of their wives? Monsieur de la Baudraye had the frankness of a man who opens an umbrella at the first drop of rain. When his wife started on Negro emancipation or prison reform, he would take up his little blue cap and vanish without a sound, certain he could get to Saint-Thibault to see off a cargo of puncheons and return an hour later to find the discussion near its close. Or, if he had no business, he would walk on the Mall, which commanded the Loire valley panorama, and take a draught of fresh air while his wife performed a sonata in words or a dialectical duet.

Once established as a Superior Woman, Dinah was eager to prove her devotion to remarkable creations of art. She threw herself into propaganda for the romantic school, extending Art to poetry, painting, literature, sculpture, furniture, and opera. She became a medievalist and prized Renaissance treasures, employing her allies as commission agents. Soon after marriage she acquired the Rougets’ furniture, sold at Issoudun in early 1824. She purchased good things in Nivernais and the Haute-Loire. At New Year and on her birthday her friends never failed to give curiosities. These fancies pleased Monsieur de la Baudraye; they made him seem to sacrifice a few crowns to his wife’s taste. In fact his land mania allowed him to think of nothing but the estate of Anzy.

These “antiquities” then cost much less than modern furniture. In five or six years the anteroom, dining-room, two drawing-rooms, and boudoir Dinah arranged on La Baudraye’s ground floor—every spot, even the staircase—were crammed with masterpieces collected in the four adjacent departments. Called “queer” by neighbors, they suited Dinah. All these marvels, soon to be the rage, struck strangers introduced to her; they came expecting something unusual and found their expectations surpassed when, behind a bower of flowers, they saw catacombs full of old things, piled as Sommerard used to pile them—that “Old Mortality” of furniture. These finds served as springs which, turned by a question, played off essays on Jean Goujon, Michel Colombe, Germain Pilon, Boulle, Van Huysum, and Boucher; on Clodion, Venetian mirrors, Brustolone’s boxwood carving; on the thirteenth through seventeenth centuries; on Bernard de Palissy’s glazes, Petitot’s enamels, Dürer’s engravings—whom she called “Dur”—on illuminations on vellum, and on Gothic architecture, early-decorated, flamboyant and pure—enough to rouse both old and young.

Possessed with the idea of waking up Sancerre, Madame de la Baudraye tried to form a so-called literary circle. The Presiding Judge, Monsieur Boirouge, who had a house and garden as part of the Popinot-Chandier property, favored the notion. The wily Judge discussed rules with Madame de la Baudraye; he proposed to figure as one of the founders and to let the house for fifteen years to the literary club. Within a year the members were playing dominoes, billiards, and bouillotte, and drinking mulled wine, punch, and liqueurs. A few elegant suppers were given and some masked balls during Carnival. As to literature—there were the newspapers. Politics and business were discussed. Monsieur de la Baudraye was constantly there—on his wife’s account, as she joked.

This result deeply grieved the Superior Woman, who despaired of Sancerre, and therefore collected the wit of the neighborhood in her own drawing-room. Nevertheless, despite the efforts of Messieurs de Chargeboeuf, Gravier, and de Clagny, of the Abbé Duret and the two chief magistrates, of a young doctor, and a young Assistant Judge—all blind admirers of Dinah—there were occasions when, weary of discussion, they allowed excursions into agreeable frivolity, the common basis of worldly conversation. Monsieur Gravier called this “from grave to gay.” The Abbé Duret’s rubber made another pleasing variety on the oracle’s monologues. The three rivals, tired of keeping their minds at the “high range of discussion,” but not daring to confess it, would sometimes turn with ingratiating hints to the old priest.

“Monsieur le Curé is dying for his game,” they would say.

The wily priest lent himself readily to the little trick. He protested.

“We should lose too much by ceasing to listen to our inspired hostess!” and so he would incite Dinah’s magnanimity to take pity at last on her dear Abbé.

This bold manoeuvre, a device of the Sous-préfet’s, was repeated with such skill that Dinah never suspected her slaves of escaping to the prison yard, so to speak, of the cardtable; they would leave her one of the younger functionaries to harry.

One young landowner, the dandy of Sancerre, fell from Dinah’s favor after some rash demonstrations. Having solicited admission to this circle, where he flattered himself he could snatch the blossom from the constituted authorities who guarded it, he was unfortunate enough to yawn in the middle of an explanation Dinah was favoring him with—for the fourth time, it is true—on the philosophy of Kant. Monsieur de la Thaumassière, grandson of the historian of Le Berry, was thenceforth regarded as a man entirely bereft of soul and brains.

The three devotees en titre each submitted to these exorbitant demands on their mind and attention in hope of a crowning triumph when Dinah should become human; for none dared imagine she would give up her innocence as a wife till she had lost all illusions. In 1826, when she was surrounded by adorers, Dinah completed her twentieth year, and the Abbé Duret kept her in a sort of fervid Catholicism; so her worshipers had to be content to overwhelm her with little attentions and small services, only too happy to be taken for the carpet-knights of this sovereign lady by strangers admitted to spend an evening or two at La Baudraye.

“Madame de la Baudraye is a fruit that must be left to ripen.” This was Monsieur Gravier’s opinion, who was waiting.

As to the lawyer, he wrote four-page letters, to which Dinah replied in soothing words as she walked, leaning on his arm, round and round the lawn after dinner.

Madame de la Baudraye, thus guarded by three passions and always under the eye of her pious mother, escaped the malignity of slander. It was evident to all Sancerre that no two of these three men would ever leave the third alone with Madame de la Baudraye; their jealousy was a comedy to lookers-on.

To reach Saint-Thibault from Caesar’s Gate there is a way much shorter than that by the ramparts, down what in mountainous districts is called a coursière, at Sancerre le Casse-cou, or Break-neck Alley. The name fits a steep, stony path shut in by high banks of vineyards. By the Break-neck the distance from Sancerre to La Baudraye is much abridged. The ladies of the place, jealous of the Sappho of Saint-Satur, were wont to walk on the Mall, looking down this Longchamp of the bigwigs, whom they would stop and engage in conversation—sometimes the Sous-préfet and sometimes the Public Prosecutor—who would listen with every sign of impatience or uncivil absence of mind. As the turrets of La Baudraye are visible from the Mall, many a younger man came to contemplate Dinah’s abode while envying the ten or twelve privileged persons who might spend their afternoons with the Queen of the neighborhood.

Monsieur de la Baudraye was not slow to discover the advantage he, as Dinah’s husband, held over his wife’s adorers, and he used them openly, obtaining remission of taxes and winning two lawsuits. In every litigation he used the Public Prosecutor’s name so effectively that matters were carried no further; like many undersized men, he was contentious and litigious in business, though in the gentlest manner.

At the same time, the more plainly guiltless she appeared, the less conceivable Madame de la Baudraye’s position seemed to prying women. Frequently, at the Présidente de Boirouge’s house, ladies of a certain age would spend whole evenings discussing the La Baudraye household among themselves. They all suspected a mystery, a secret such as always interests women experienced in life. And, in fact, at La Baudraye one of those slow, monotonous conjugal tragedies was being played out which would have remained forever unknown if the merciless scalpel of the nineteenth century, driven by the demand for novelty, had not dissected the darkest corners of the heart, or at least those which the decency of past centuries left unopened. That domestic drama sufficiently accounts for Dinah’s immaculate virtue during her early married life.

################################################
A young woman whose early triumphs sprang from pride and whose first scheme had succeeded was not likely to rest. Frail as Monsieur de la Baudraye seemed, he proved an unexpectedly good match for Mademoiselle Dinah Piedefer. Her first thoughts were practical: why was this country landowner of forty-four marrying a girl of seventeen, and what would this bargain mean for her?

The little man never behaved as Dinah expected. He let her have the five acres around La Baudraye for pleasure grounds and at first provided seven or eight thousand francs for house improvements so she could buy furniture and redecorate in various styles. For a year and a half she fancied either he was not miserly or that she had great influence. After his second trip to Paris she discovered the provincial miser beneath his gentle mask. On her first request for supplies he fixed strict sums: two hundred francs a month for her personal expenses, twelve hundred francs a year charged on La Hautoy—two hundred more than the marriage settlement—and nothing explicit for housekeeping. He reminded her that, as a bachelor, he had lived on fifteen hundred francs a year and now spent six thousand including rates and repairs; that a winegrower’s returns could be ruined by weather, bankrupt clients, or speculation; that small owners must keep a year’s income in hand and expect at best two-thirds of returns.

Resistance only spurred Dinah. She flung herself against his iron will masked with gentleness, tried to make him jealous or alarmed, but his confidence was imperturbable. He left her, when he went to Paris, with Medor’s conviction in Angélique’s fidelity. When she affected cold disdain to nettle him, Monsieur de la Baudraye watched her with a fixed, catlike stare. His mute indifference unnerved the young wife; she could not understand the selfish quiescence of a man who regulated his life with clocklike regularity. He evaded; she struck out—ten feet above his measured existence.

Dinah’s fury at being condemned to La Baudraye and Sancerre is easy to imagine. She had dreamed of handling a fortune and managing the dwarf whom she at first humored. Hoping to appear on a greater stage, she accepted the vulgar incense of admirers in the hope her husband’s name might be drawn from the electoral urn; she supposed him ambitious after seeing him return thrice from Paris, each time with a higher social tone. But striking at his heart was like tapping marble. The man who had held offices—Receiver-General, Referendary, Master of Appeals, Officer of the Legion of Honor, Royal Commissioner—was in reality a mole throwing up little hills round a vineyard. Lamentations to officials increased their devotion to this supposed victim; like many women, Dinah never spoke of her speculative schemes, and finding them vain she ceased to scheme.

In the autumn of 1827 news came of the Baron de la Baudraye’s purchase of Anzy. The little man showed a pride and glee that altered Dinah’s ideas for months; she fancied hidden greatness when she found him applying for a patent of entail. In triumph he cried, “Dinah, you shall be a countess yet!” There followed a patched-up reunion between husband and wife, humiliating to a woman whose apparent superiority was unreal while her unseen superiority was genuine. Dinah, ridiculous through the perversity of her cleverness, had real qualities of soul that provincial life never brought to light; Monsieur de la Baudraye, devoid of soul, strength, and wit, seemed a man of character only because he pursued a fixed plan he lacked power to change.

Their lives entered a six-year phase during which Dinah became utterly provincial. Paris has many kinds of women—the duchess, the financier’s wife, the ambassadress, the minister’s wife—but in the country there is but one: the provincial woman. This is a sore of modern society. Nineteenth-century France divides into Paris and the provinces. The provinces are jealous of Paris; Paris barely thinks of the provinces except to demand money. Once Paris was capital of the provinces and subject to court influence; now all Paris is the Court, and the country is the town.

However lofty or clever a girl from any department may be, if she marries in the country and remains there she inevitably becomes provincial. Second-rate ideas, indifference to dress, and the culture of vulgar people swamp the sublimer essence hidden in youth; everything decays. How should it be otherwise? Country girls see only provincials; their choice lies among mediocrities; provincial fathers marry daughters to provincial sons; crossing races is not considered, and mind degenerates. Men of talent, artists, and superior minds fly to Paris. The provincial woman is thus inferior in herself and twice so through her husband. How can she be happy under this twofold consciousness?

A third element deepens the aridity. One flattering balm for a woman is the assurance of being something in the life of a superior man she chose. But in the country husbands are often inferior, and bachelors no better. When a provincial wife commits her “little sin,” she falls for some local dandy who is at best merely well dressed; she knows her fancy is chasing the commonplace. Dinah was preserved from this danger by pride and the conviction of her own superiority. Even without her mother’s presence, her pride and sense of destiny would have protected her. Flattered by admirers, she saw no lover among them. No man realized the poetic ideal she and Anna Grossetete had sketched.

Stirred by homage she asked herself who she would choose. She preferred Monsieur de Chargeboeuf, a gentleman of good family, but his cold nature, selfishness, and narrow ambitions repelled her; he deserted a woman he once loved when his family feared scandal. Monsieur de Clagny, whose mind appealed to hers, was in some respects odious. When condemned to six years at Sancerre she nearly accepted Chargeboeuf’s devotion, but he was appointed to a prefecture and left. Clagny’s chief satisfaction was that the new Sous-préfet was a married man whose wife befriended Dinah. The lawyer’s only rival was Monsieur Gravier—the typical forty-year-old pursued by women who laugh at and encourage him. In six years among men introduced from twenty leagues round, none stirred Dinah’s anticipation of love or superior soul.

Thus her choicest faculties failed to develop; she swallowed many insults to her pride, constantly bruised by a husband who calmly played supernumerary in the drama of her life. Forced to bury her wealth of love, she showed only surface to the world. Now and then she tried to rouse herself with manly resolutions; but she was kept in leading strings by money. Slowly, despite ambitious protests and grievous inward recriminations, she underwent the provincial metamorphosis. Each day took a fragment of her spirited determination. Rules for her person were abandoned. Though at first she kept up with fashion, she had to limit purchases to her allowance: instead of six hats, caps, or gowns she resigned herself to one gown each season; an admired bonnet served two seasons. Often her artistic sense led her to sacrifice personal needs to obtain some Gothic furniture. By the seventh year her morning dresses were made at home by the best local needlewoman; her mother, husband, and friends pronounced her charming in these inexpensive costumes which credited her taste. With no standard of comparison she fell into provincial snares. A Parisian might make a defect an adornment; the provincial woman accepted the worst. Dinah, extremely slim, showed it to excess; reduced by dull weariness she began to look like a skeleton in clothes; those who saw her daily did not notice the gradual change.

This is a natural result of provincial life. In spite of marriage a young woman preserves her beauty some time; the town is proud of her, but everyone sees her every day and perception dulled. If she loses color it is scarcely remarked; a little neglect is thought charming. When Dinah ceased to have a new dress each season she seemed merely to have yielded to the place’s philosophy.

Speech, choice of words and ideas, rust if not rubbed up in Paris; but provincialism most stamps gesture, gait, and movement. The provincial walks as if there were nothing to avoid; being known and always in her place, everyone makes way and she loses the charm of the unforeseen. Life in common induces mimicry: gestures, tone, manner, attitudes, and countenance are copied. In six years Dinah sank to the pitch of her society. As she took on Monsieur de Clagny’s ideas she assumed his tone and unconsciously adopted masculine manners from seeing none but men; she thought laughing at their ridiculous traits kept her safe, but some hue of what she laughed at remained. A Parisian woman, seeing many examples of good taste, learns to seize the moment to appear advantageously; Madame de la Baudraye, accustomed to the stage, acquired an indefinable theatrical and domineering manner—the air of a prima donna—ironical smiles that would soon have been cured in the capital.

After she had adopted these absurdities, deceived by worshipers who imagined them graces, a moment of terrible awakening came like an avalanche. In a single day she was crushed by a frightful comparison.

In 1829, after Chargeboeuf’s departure, she expected the Baronne de Fontaine. Anna’s husband, now Director-General under the Minister of Finance, took advantage of leave after his father’s death to take his wife to Italy. Anna came to Sancerre; the meeting was disastrous. Anna, less handsome than Dinah at school, was now a thousand times handsomer despite fatigue and travel dress. She stepped from an elegant chaise loaded with Paris milliners’ boxes and had a lady’s maid whose airs frightened Dinah. All the difference between a Paris woman and a provincial was evident: Anna spent six thousand francs a year on herself—the same sum that kept the household at La Baudraye.

In twenty-four hours the friends exchanged confidences; the Parisian, seeing herself so far superior to the phoenix of Mademoiselle Chamarolles’ school, showed kindness and attentions that stabbed Dinah. She understood Anna’s advantages lay on the surface while her own were buried. When Anna left, Madame de la Baudraye, now twenty-two, fell into despair.

“What ails you?” Monsieur de Clagny asked, seeing her dejected.

“Anna,” she answered, “has learned to live, while I have been learning to endure.”

A tragi-comedy enacted in the house, in harmony with money quarrels and successive transformations—a drama the Abbé Duret and Monsieur de Clagny alone partly understood—when Dinah, in idleness or vanity, revealed the secret of her anonymous fame.

Though mixing verse and prose is odd in French literature, to give a fair idea of her unconfessed struggle it is necessary to analyze a poem born of her despair. Her patience broken by Chargeboeuf’s departure, Dinah took the Abbé’s advice to exhale her thoughts in verse—“You will find relief as those who write epitaphs or elegies,” he said. The strange production stirred the Allier, the Nièvre, and the Cher, proud of a poet to rival Paris. Paquita la Sévillane, published in the Echo du Morvan, was at once admired and suspected; some in Moulins thought Jan Diaz mocked a new school of eccentric verse imitating foreign mannerisms.

The poem opened with a ballad praising Spain’s fragrant plains and Sevillian dancers, contrasting them with Northern squalor. Dinah then wrote a magnificent if affected description of Rouen—where she had never been—contrasting a manufacturing town’s life with Spain’s careless poetry versus sordid money-making. Jan Diaz made Paquita a Seville native sovereign of her town at thirteen, loved by three toreadors, whose soldier-lover carried her away to Normandy only to leave for Russia. Parting and vows were painted with Byron-like feeling. Much of the poem describes Paquita’s sufferings waiting alone in Rouen; she lives on narcotics and dreams, almost dies, but remains faithful; the soldier returns pale and frozen by Russia, and she, true, is still beautiful. The poem then takes a bitter turn: Paquita, failing to find suitable luxury in Rouen, falls into vice, ending in a house of ill-fame described with stark realism. Finally she is faded and old, but it was she who once sang of Spain.

The six-hundred-line poem, with seguidillas framing it, had a gloomy vigor that alarmed Dinah, who found herself admired in three departments under the cloak of anonymity. While she enjoyed success, she feared provincial malignity: if the secret leaked, other women would trace likenesses between writer and Paquita. Reflection came too late; she shuddered at having “copied” some of her woes. “Write no more,” said the Abbé. “You will cease to be a woman; you will be a poet.” Moulins, Nevers, Bourges were searched for Jan Diaz; Dinah remained impenetrable.

To divert suspicion she wrote a charming two-canto poem on the Mass-Oak, a Nivernais legend about foes from Nevers and Saint-Saulge who, about to battle in the forest of Faye, are stayed by a priest who, by saying Mass under an oak, makes them friends. This poem, purer and far superior to Paquita, was less admired.

After these attempts Madame de la Baudraye, feeling herself a poet, had a light on her brow and a flash in her eyes that made her handsomer than ever. She cast longing looks at Paris, aspiring to fame—and fell back into her den at La Baudraye, daily quarrels with her husband, and a little circle whose characters had become a bore. Though literature gave relief—poetry echoed in her empty life and provided an outlet—it only increased her hatred of the gray, ponderous provincial atmosphere.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
When George Sand’s revival after the Revolution of 1830 renewed interest in Le Berry, La Châtre took pride in producing a local rival to Madame de Staël and Camille Maupin. Provincial towns, eager to acclaim any feminine talent, fostered a wave of would-be Muses—women and girls drawn from quiet lives by the lure of fame. French common sense persisted, but women were increasingly allowed to voice opinions they would not have owned a few years earlier.

Monsieur de Clagny exploited this freedom by collecting poems attributed to Jan Diaz in a small volume printed at Moulins, accompanied by a jaunty notice amusing to initiates. Practical jokes flourish while the author is anonymous; their force wanes once identity appears. The memoir of Jan Diaz—said to be born at Bourges in 1807, the son of a Spanish prisoner—might have misled a future biographer: it named Bourges professors and schoolmates like Lousteau and Bianchon, included early elegies and sonnets, local descriptions, and the tale Carola, and ended with the poet’s lonely death near Château-Chinon—details calculated to move regional feeling.

Two hundred cheap copies were printed and most sold locally, showing provincial appetite for letters. After Clagny distributed copies, Dinah (Madame de la Baudraye) still had several wrapped in newspapers. Copies sent to Paris vanished in editors’ piles, and countrymen like Nathan praised the author as if dead. Lousteau, alerted by schoolfellows who remembered no Jan Diaz, investigated and discovered that Jan Diaz was a pseudonym for a woman. The revelation made Madame de la Baudraye the region’s heroine; Sancerre and Bourges hailed her as a potential rival to George Sand. A poem that might have been ridiculed as anonymous was lauded: provincial feeling tended to extremes.

The Abbé who had counseled Madame de la Baudraye was dead; otherwise he might have restrained her public ambitions. Three years of unrecognized labor had weighed on Dinah’s soul; she welcomed fame as compensation for disappointed hopes. Poetry and dreams of celebrity, which had sustained her since meeting Anna Grossetête, no longer sufficed, and the Abbé Duret—who had once urged brave suffering—was gone to check her impulses.

The priest had tried to reveal her husband’s nature, warning Dinah that weak natures can conceal dark capacities. Finding her husband incapable of love, she denied his capacity to hate. “Do not confound hatred and vengeance,” the Abbé had said: hatred is petty, vengeance more calculating. He warned her to beware offending Monsieur de la Baudraye; he would forgive an infidelity he could exploit but would be implacable if wounded in his pride.

As the countryside praised Madame de la Baudraye as Jan Diaz, “little La Baudraye” felt his glory mortally struck—he alone knew the origin of Paquita la Sevillane. When that poem was mentioned, everyone pitied Dinah, and women rushed to condole with the one they had long felt oppressed by; she appeared elevated locally. Outwardly the shriveled husband showed no sign, but Dinah sometimes perceived an icy venom beneath his increased politeness. When she demanded an explanation, his cold impassibility prevailed. She raged, reproached him for eleven wasted years, staged a dramatic scene—and, ashamed of being bested, vowed never to write again. She kept that vow, and desolation spread through the Sancerrois: “Why did Madame de la Baudraye compose no more verses?” people asked.

In social life, Dinah had no enemies; visits multiplied, and the presiding judge’s wife and other bourgeoises sought favor by paying court. Dinah’s nine years leading local opposition made her the object of lavish praise under the new epithet “Superior Woman,” replacing older local nicknames.

Weary from brambles of disappointment and dark longings, Dinah fell into another trap. Observing Monsieur de Clagny’s self-sacrifice—his refusal of a Paris post favored by his family—she convinced herself he loved her and overcame repulsion. A Sancerre coalition formed to secure Clagny’s election as deputy; Dinah dreamed of following him to Paris. But the hoped-for hundred and fifty votes dwindled to fifty: jealousy and opposition, particularly from President de Boirouge and Monsieur Gravier, who suspected Clagny’s ascendancy in Dinah’s heart, and the machinations of a young sous-préfet who wanted promotion, defeated them. The sous-préfet, rewarded with a préfet’s post elsewhere, lamented he had not pleased Madame de la Baudraye.

Despite outward calm—two mismatched, resigned people maintaining propriety—a yoke clung to Dinah. Why did she long to throw off the mask worn for twelve years? Many conjure illusions of mastering a husband or being loved; Dinah, having little impulse to break duty, did not even enough care for Clagny to forgive his defeat. The move to the Château d’Anzy and arranging her treasures in Philibert de Lorme’s splendid surroundings occupied her for months and gave her leisure to plan a decisive action, later explained by gossip.

Struck by Lousteau’s reputation with women, Dinah set out to know him. She read his works and, more for his conquests than his talent, desired to attract him. She proposed Sancerre should elect one of Paris’s great men; Gatien Boirouge wrote to Bianchon, claiming kinship, and an old friend of Madame Lousteau was enlisted to stir the journalist’s ambition by suggesting Sancerre intended to elect a distinguished Parisian. Dinah wanted to meet illustrious men and give her fall the lustre of fame.

Lousteau and Bianchon did not respond at once—Bianchon could not leave his chair—until September, at vintage time, when both arrived during the harvest; no public demonstration could be held. “We have fallen flat,” said Lousteau. By 1836 Lousteau, worn by Paris struggle, looked older than his years: bald, adopting a Byronic air to mask decay, misanthropic yet with eyes still flashing. He dressed fashionably to hide lost youth. Horace Bianchon, decorated with the Legion of Honor, was fat, burly, patriarchal, a hard-working philosopher with calm air. Their prosaic reception at the inn lasted all morning; only by chance Monsieur de Clagny learned of their arrival. Madame de la Baudraye, desperate, sent Gatien—who had no vineyards—to beg them to spend a few days at Château d’Anzy. Monsieur Gravier, the public prosecutor, the presiding judge, and Gatien organized a banquet for the town’s literati.

Learning the beautiful Madame de la Baudraye was Jan Diaz, the Parisians went to Anzy in Gatien’s wagonette. Gatien praised his heroine as the handsomest local woman and a Superior Woman who might charm Paris. Bianchon and the mischievous journalist were secretly surprised when they found Dinah on the château steps in thin black cashmere framed by curls; the costume masked romantic affectations, and her figure and eyes suggested weariness. In Le Berry this artistic dress concealed the Superior Woman’s romantic pretensions.

Putting on solemn faces, the Parisians listened to Dinah’s set speech of thanks for relieving her monotony. Walking them round the flowered lawn, she explained provincial life’s aridity: a choice between despair and dull resignation, producing etiolated flowers of stagnant minds. She described provincial women’s occupations—jam-making, washing, piano drudgery, embroidering, dances with the same partners, solemn walks, polite calls, and endless talk of gowns—and warned that what Paris initially mocks, it often courts; a country woman’s attachment can become an all-consuming fate. Country-bred women, little observed, nurture secret passions that can culminate in sudden catastrophe; Parisian coquetry is unknown there.

Lousteau agreed that provincial women harbor surprises and that poets should not vilify them. Bianchon asked the cause of provincial woes; Dinah blamed Paris’s centralizing influence, which creates a dead uniformity of manners. Lousteau suggested a federation to recover individuality; Bianchon called it impractical. Dinah praised England for preserving country life—while still faulting its hypocrisies—and argued the English aristocracy’s residence in the country preserved individual charm.

Madame Piedefer, a tall, red-faced chatterbox, arrived and was gently mocked by Lousteau; Dinah curbed her loquacity. Etienne tried to recover footing, but Dinah’s advances shifted to Bianchon; later she relented to Lousteau again. Great meanness can be done for small ends: proud Dinah suddenly remembered her album of autographs, an oblong book mostly blank yet already prized—its pages filled by the Baronne de Fontaine with Rossini, Meyerbeer, Victor Hugo, Lamartine, Béranger, George Sand, Scribe, Berlioz; Monsieur de Clagny had added a song by Lacenaire and a note from Napoleon; Monsieur Gravier had secured Mademoiselle Mars. Dinah, the only person for ten leagues with such a book, begged her guests to enrich it.

Bianchon wrote an aphorism about the populace offering absolution for every crime and signed J. B. DE CLAGNY; he added a reflection distinguishing Napoleon and a water-carrier and invoking Nature, signing H. BIANCHON. Lousteau, amused, declared he would second any man brave enough to plead for the Monarchy; then, deeming his name insignificant, asked time to improvise something biting. Dinah, hopeful, handed him the book.

At five the next morning everyone at the Château d’Anzy was astir. Little La Baudraye had organized a day’s shooting for his Parisians—less as sport than to display twelve hundred acres he meant to reclaim, a costly project promising a large annual return.

“Do you know why the Public Prosecutor has not come out with us?” Gatien Boirouge asked Monsieur Gravier. Gravier said Lebas had to sit for minor cases. Gatien scoffed: his father told him Lebas would not join early because Monsieur de Clagny had begged him as deputy. Gravier looked startled; Bianchon and Lousteau urged not to meddle, but Gatien fretted. He could not imagine Madame de la Baudraye unguarded with de Clagny. He feared Clagny’s eloquence and ambition might seduce Dinah, who disliked Sancerre and dreamed of Paris. Lousteau teased and recommended they not trouble themselves, yet proposed a plot: after dinner tell lurid stories of murderous wives to watch Dinah’s and de Clagny’s faces.

At a copse Gravier went off with the others; when they reached a clearing, Bianchon suggested waiting for him. Gatien, certain Gravier would return to the château within twenty minutes, checked his watch and fretted that Dinah might be alone with de Clagny. The men argued playfully about ambition, virtue, and Paris; Lousteau mocked provincial prudery and promised to test Dinah’s innocence by provoking reaction with dramatic tales.

That evening Lousteau returned an album to Dinah with an elegy—“Spleen”—in which he posed as a blighted poet. Dinah looked pitying; de Clagny scowled at what he thought sham melancholy. He sat to play backgammon with the cure; Gravier placed a lamp to light Dinah’s embroidery. The conspirators sat near. Dinah told Lousteau the basket she worked was for Monsieur de la Baudraye. Conversation turned to literature and morality: Lousteau mocked Parisian disbelief in women’s virtue; de Clagny complained of Parisian literature’s preoccupation with adultery.

Etienne (Lousteau) launched an extravagant defense: ban every book referring to illicit love and you must burn Homer, Dante, Rousseau—most literature would go. He argued judges always represent established interests; they might condemn the novel or the miracle—Pilate, Anytus, modern prosecutors all act to enforce order. In their view Socrates and Jesus were criminals. To call religions or revolutions impostures is to think like a public prosecutor. De Clagny called this blasphemy; Etienne allowed the charge but not malicious intent, admitting many like him would make governance impossible. Madame Piedefer made faces; Bianchon warned Lousteau not to unsettle the company.

To the promised revenge tales. Lousteau recited a story told by Charles Nodier about a Chevalier de Beauvoir: after the 18th Brumaire a Maille envoy, arrested on landing, was confined in the Castle of l’Escarpe. Treated kindly by the commandant, he won the governor’s wife’s fancy. Whether by imprudence or fate he was fettered in a dungeon, fed on black bread. A turnkey, bribed, supplied him a file and knotted rope; Beauvoir filed the bars, pretended escape, then, when the guard returned, killed him with the iron bar he’d filed, donned his clothes, mimicked his gait, and walked out among unsuspecting warders. The tale chilled some listeners but left de Clagny and Dinah unmoved.

Bianchon then told “La Grande Bretèche,” the Spanish grandee walled in a cupboard, finishing to dramatic effect; Madame de la Baudraye admired a woman’s courage to risk all for love. Gravier offered a Spanish anecdote: once in Madrid a surgeon-major was blindfolded, taken to deliver a woman in secret; though the infant was dead he saved the mother. Later the distraught lover carried off the body; the surgeon, haunted, was ultimately shown an amputated arm with a telltale mole and was afterward stabbed to death by the lady’s husband. Gravier added that under Ferdinand VII he’d known a grandee with a motionless arm and a lovely wife—Spain bred strange passions.

De Clagny followed with a French horror: a Tours woman, once devout, whose husband vanished amid the Loire army’s disbandment, confessed to bleeding him to death, salting and packing the body into casks, and throwing pieces into the Loire; the pickled head was discovered by the examining judge. She was condemned and executed; earlier repentance had softened public feeling. The company reflected on the hidden tragedies of private life and the difficulty of judging crimes between spouses.

Later, the bachelors gathered in a passage by candlelight. Gravier revealed a scheme: they would seal Madame de la Baudraye’s and the Public Prosecutor’s doors to detect any nocturnal meeting. Like thieves, barefoot and cautious, Bianchon, Lousteau, Gravier, and Gatien stuck hairs across the two doors with wax—a common country-house trick—and agreed to inspect them at five. In the morning the hairs on both doors remained intact. Lousteau, surprised and delighted, declared this changed everything: if Dinah were virtuous, she was worth pursuing. He resolved to press his suit.

In the garden Lousteau found Madame de la Baudraye and engaged her. She invited him to La Baudraye to see provincial life and called provincials simple but sincere. Etienne confessed he’d only teased Gatien into revealing his adoration; her silence at dinner had looked like indiscretion, but he assured her innocence was proven. “If you fancied the magistrate,” he said, “you’d have lost my respect. I love perfection; I cannot believe the Public Prosecutor matches you.” His frankness and portrait of Paris—its daring axioms, decadence, and witticisms—intrigued Dinah. He spoke of having sought pleasure but never love; perhaps he had defects that made him less lovable. Dinah warmed to him, intoxicated when he disparaged a celebrated woman of Le Berry and implied Dinah could rival her. She spent one of her happiest days with him and Bianchon.

Their warmth spread: townsfolk exaggerated her reception of Etienne and word reached Sancerre. That evening sixteen people came to Anzy—family coaches, wagonettes, hired horses. Dinah, expecting triumph, lit the huge drawing‑room and displayed the furniture; the Parisians watched guests’ manners. Visitors flaunted ancestral laces and faded honors; the Présidente Boirouge complained of chest pains and Bianchon prescribed “tea after dinner” as an English remedy. Conversation turned to Bianchon’s fame and a proposed candidacy; Lousteau teased the idea of Bianchon in politics.

A footman brought letters and proofs; Dinah found a printers’ wrapper and a folded serial romance proof—“Olympia, or Roman Revenge.” Lousteau read a fragment—brigands, vanished heroes, a Duchess forgetting a glove—and mocked the lack of “local color”; Bianchon defended censored writers. The provincial company listened, half scandalized, half entertained.

Thus the evening passed at Anzy—stories, parodies, flirtations and a mingling of Parisian wit with provincial simplicities, while beneath the gaiety the bachelors’ scheme, jealous suspicions, and Lousteau’s courtship quietly set the season’s intrigues in motion.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The Public Prosecutor’s wife, erect and martial, nudged a smile at her neighbor as if to show she understood the scene. The Mayoress urged Lousteau to continue reading, and he forced a smile, called “Attention!” and resumed the torn proof. The fragment opened with Cardinal Borborigano confronting the Duchess and urging her to flee, while she retorted that the Duke of Bracciano would reappear when she wished to prove he was alive. The name “Cardinal Borborigano” delighted Bianchon; Dinah, pitying the eighteen faces watching Lousteau, glimpsed the whole melodrama: Rome, a presumed murder, a brazen wife sleeping on a volcano of crime.

Lousteau read on, elaborating the romance’s tropes: a French Adolphe embraced by the Duchess, Rinaldo the brigand as a melodramatic conspirator, and the whole theatrical parade of Victor Hugo–style excess. The company urged him on. He paused to explain printers’ proofs—waste sheets printed one side only, made-up sheets, folio marks and tailpieces—and from these clues deduced the physical make-up of the book (four duodecimo volumes, second edition) and that the action was edging toward a climax despite missing pages.

He translated a jumble of directions and quotes: a key swapped by Rinaldo, terror over a lost key leading to death, a tailpiece by Normand cut by Duplat. Skipping torn leaves, he jumped to a later full sheet marked IV.; J. There Rinaldo steals into the Baths of Vespasian, finds and uses a concealed door, then discovers he is trapped—what he thought a cellar of family treasures becomes a prison. Lousteau mocked the blunt Empire-era prose—panic expressed as a “hollow roar like that of a hyaena”—and contrasted it with modern writing, which elaborates inner life.

Bianchon defended contemporary criticism and taste: readers now demand invention, style, thought and feeling; critics attack obvious defects because they envy the creators. Lousteau, heedless of cautions to be concise, pushed on. A groan answered Rinaldo’s cry; a voice called “Santa Maria!” and claimed to be the Duke of Bracciano. In pitch darkness the Duke guided Rinaldo until iron bars confirmed a cage. The Duke had been confined thirty months—kept upright by a counterpoise beneath the Duchess’s bedroom so she could taunt him when she embraced her lover. Tricked in a duel of wits, he had his poison shown but withheld, and was forced to watch his wife with her lover.

The Duke begged Rinaldo for alliance. Rinaldo, the brigand, revealed files and pincers he had used to file through one bar; he lent a dagger. The spring clattered, the Duke sprang up, and Rinaldo—astonished at the Duke’s haste to leave—mused that he had sworn never to harm a woman. Lousteau added more printerly notes—another tailpiece, a Cupid on a snail, a blank page 230—and reached the fragmentary “Conclusion.” This portion was brief and voluptuous: the Duchess, bathed and goddess-like, finds Adolphe reclining; they profess love, and she presses the springs that open the hidden passage. Lousteau lamented the foreman’s tearing off of the rest of the proof but pronounced the author full of promise.

Reactions varied. Gatien Boirouge and Monsieur Gravier admitted confusion; Lousteau defended the piece as typical of Empire romance. Monsieur Gravier thought the brigand’s courtly talk betrayed an author ignorant of Italy; Madame Gorju scorned the fiction and praised Bianchon’s prescriptions. Bianchon, glancing at Mademoiselle Euphemie Gorju, counseled leniency: twenty pages out of a thousand proved little. Lousteau and Bianchon mused on authorship—perhaps a woman, like “Madame Hadot,” given women’s reputed extravagance (Frankenstein, Leone Leoni, Ann Radcliffe were cited)—and Dinah glowered when Monsieur de Clagny used those examples, feeling slighted.

Little La Baudraye, practical about revenge, observed that social punishment often suffices: a cuckolded woman may be reduced to penury and ostracism rather than slain; loss of finery and respect constitutes true misery. Madame de la Baudraye added that a woman might still find happiness if she had a lover. Monsieur de la Baudraye’s deadpan reply and the exchange between Bianchon and Lousteau produced wry asides. Dinah’s small laugh and the way her eyes met Louise’s remarks revealed to Bianchon intimations of Madame de la Baudraye’s inner life—wrinkles, a knowing smile, a reserve of experience—things the provincials missed.

Bianchon saw in a single glance what might otherwise take years to unfold: Dinah’s luminous look at Lousteau signified a readiness to be moved. Lousteau, who had long been a Parisian poet, journalist and libertine, displayed that tinsel of intelligence and freedom that attracted women who wished to feel chosen and remembered. He noticed Dinah’s superiority to the provincial visitors—her dress, grace, complexion by candlelight against shy, ill-dressed faces—and found himself drawn to her personality as much as to the château’s antique beauty. Madame de la Baudraye’s audacious prophecy—that Lousteau would be the happy man of Dinah’s choice—suddenly seemed plausible.

That night, as the party thinned and the Sancerre visitors prepared for their hour’s drive, only Monsieur de Clagny, Monsieur Lebas, Gatien and Monsieur Gravier remained to sleep at Anzy. Lousteau and Bianchon shared a room; Bianchon told him plainly, “You will be the happy man of this woman’s choice,” explaining how alluring a reputed lover can be: women like to feel they will be remembered longer than others, to appeal to experience, and to have a lover’s reputation confirm his value. Bianchon predicted Dinah’s beauty would be enhanced by love and pragmatically noted that a child would secure her fortune. Lousteau, half in jest, half in design, called making love to her perhaps an act of virtue and let Bianchon hint at plans for the next day.

Next morning, Madame de la Baudraye planned a trip: she would take Bianchon as far as Cosne, drop her mother at La Baudraye, go on with the Parisians to Cosne and return alone with Etienne. She was elegantly dressed; Lousteau wore his studied Parisian “war-paint.” Monsieur de Clagny and Monsieur Gravier watched the Parisians climb into the carriage feeling out of place. Gatien returned prudently from the stables and joined the chaise as escort; Dinah sighed that she had not had three hours’ liberty in thirteen years. Bianchon hatched a simple ruse—ask Gatien to fetch lecture notes at Anzy so Dinah could be rid of him—which delighted Madame de la Baudraye. The drive between Anzy and Sancerre, with views of the Loire, proved agreeable.

On the road Dinah and Lousteau discussed love in theory. He spoke in a tone of refined corruption: love obeys no law; it varies with character and circumstance; any woman, however firm, might be swept away in a few hours by an internal whirlwind known to God alone. He declared this idea the key to the adventures they’d been discussing for days.

Throughout the reading and conversation Lousteau’s literary asides punctuated the evening. He mocked careless printers for lacking correctors, contrasted terse Empire outlines—Lubin loves Toinette, Toinette refuses, Lubin kills and is guillotined—with modern writers who reveal inner life, and teased contemporary quarrels between critics and creators. Bianchon agreed: readers now demand the five literary senses and punish works lacking them; critics, lacking inventiveness, denounce the incomplete. The Sancerre guests took offense at these put-downs, and Madame Piedefer urged pacification; provincials quickly anger when they suspect ridicule.

By the time the visitors left, amusement had shifted to attraction: Lousteau’s interest in Dinah appeared natural. The evening closed with gossip, literary theory, and an implicit movement of flirtation. The reading had unveiled a melodramatic tale of entrapment and revenge—the Duke caged and mocked, the brigand’s reluctant aid—while the company displayed small-town temper, pretension, and social mechanics of flirtation. As they prepared to travel the next day, Lousteau’s claim that love is the key to all such adventures left both women and men reflecting on the thin line between plotting in fiction and plotting in life.

################################################
For three days Dinah’s imagination brimmed with perilous possibilities fed by Parisian talk; Lousteau watched, ready to seize any moment her hidden readiness overcame irresolution.

She planned to show La Baudraye to her two visitors. Gatien rode off on errands; Madame Piedefer went shopping; Dinah went to Cosne with Lousteau and Bianchon. Lousteau sat beside her while Bianchon rode behind; both men pitied a noble nature trapped in vulgar surroundings. Bianchon mocked the local magistrates and Gatien, bolstering Lousteau’s case by scorning the provincials.

Crossing the Loire, Bianchon told Dinah she had been drawn to Etienne; he loved her but was frivolous, poor, and bound to Paris while she was rooted in Sancerre. He counseled prudence: make Lousteau a friend, accept limited visits, and secure beauty, happiness, and fortune. He warned bluntly that without love she would grow old and hideous, an argument that unhinged her defences. Though she protested she would not be a passerby’s mistress, Etienne’s arrival interrupted her dissembling; he seemed to study Cosne as he drew near.

Bianchon prepared to leave, offering Dinah a final, almost paternal blessing and allowing Lousteau to take her hand. The touch thrilled Dinah; Lousteau felt triumphant, reading small gestures—adjusting her frock, fretting over muslin—as signs of choice. He shook Bianchon’s hand with studied indifference as the older man departed.

Back at La Baudraye Lousteau abruptly declared his love. Dinah trembled and insisted she would not entrust the best of herself to a man who would see her only as transient pleasure. Gatien’s return forced the moment: Lousteau demanded Gatien go back to the inn, then deliberately messed Dinah’s muslin so she would not be presentable—an outrageous “trick” he called a way to spare her reputation. Baffled and hurt, Gatien was sent away; humiliated, Dinah fled in tears. Lousteau followed, threw himself at her feet, and vowed devotion.

Madame Piedefer arrived; Lousteau suggested the mother find another dress. Hearing Gatien’s return, Dinah and her mother withdrew to change into something warmer, as Bianchon had advised. Gatien, bewildered by conflicting explanations—that the muslin was torn on a nail or trampled by Lousteau—felt mocked. Dinah reappeared in velvet, forced to hide coldness behind Lousteau’s presence, and was escorted to the carriage with him. Lousteau professed to renounce Paris and linger for her—rhetoric that, whether sincere or not, struck Dinah and made her laugh in contrition.

That evening the town whispered: she had gone out in muslin and returned in velvet. The story became scandal; the Presidente Boirouge thundered that Dinah’s poetry proved she must live what she wrote; others muttered that virtue lasts until happiness arrives, then Calumny follows. Monsieur de Clagny defended her, arguing she and Lousteau pursued some great project, but was mocked.

October’s beauty aided Dinah: as Bianchon predicted, genuine passion bloomed. Within a month she changed—alive, discovering dormant qualities, feeling Lousteau as an angel and delighting in being loved for herself. Lousteau, who prized her artlessness, played the consummate seducer, supplying every practiced flourish of passion and striving to imprint himself on her memory. Their long walks and evenings deepened mutual feeling; when lovers exhaust every step of the duet yet remain enamored, the love becomes real.

Lousteau’s time was limited—he planned to leave Anzy in early November. The day before his departure the master showed them the château’s front bearing the arms of Uxelles; he wanted to replace them with his own coat of arms, a sign of pride. Lousteau teased, and the old man’s remark that Dinah was still young—there was time—passed with a smile Lousteau did not fully grasp.

They postponed farewells; Dinah accompanied Lousteau as far as Cosne and secured many solemn promises. Back at La Baudraye she told Clagny, Gatien, and Gravier—each in turn—that Lousteau had taught her she had not been loved for herself, and she delivered a noble speech on man and base passions. She accepted Clagny’s steadfast devotion as confidant, lavishing on him the marks of friendship a woman can give to a suitor willing to be an adoring, submissive companion.

Thus Dinah’s brief, passionate conversion—engineered and complicated by Lousteau’s artful audacity and Bianchon’s counsel—transformed her social standing and inner life, provoking scandal in Sancerre even as it led her to embrace a new identity shaped by affection and theatrical devotion.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In Paris again, Lousteau soon lost the afterglow of his happy time at the Château d’Anzy. The reason was simple: he lived by his pen.

After the bourgeois triumph, to live by the pen was almost penal servitude—daily production, or the appearance of it, with imitation as costly as originality. Etienne’s weekly newspaper piece came each Monday like Sisyphus’s stone; besides that he wrote for literary magazines, two novels a year for weeklies, and miscellaneous articles. Facility and carelessness made him little more than a scrivener—respectable enough socially but cut off from claims to a higher literary position. In Paris, a man who writes but will not press for greater aims becomes merely a journalist, a hack.

His life nevertheless had pleasures. Blue-stockings, actresses, publishers, and authors made much of his ready pen. Lousteau lived cheaply in the outward sense—no ostentation beyond paying rent—but he kept theatre boxes and a social calendar full of dinners, invitations, visits, and lounging. The sale of reviewed books paid for his gloves; vanity paid in drawings and gifts. His serial, novels, and articles were the tax for this easy life, yet it had been ten years in coming.

Known and liked for mischief and good humor, Lousteau let himself float with the stream and never planned for the future. He led a small circle of newcomers, preserved fifteen-year habits with old companions, and indulged his wit. He earned seven to eight hundred francs a month, which he found insufficient for a prodigality peculiar to the impecunious; he was as hard up as when he first came to Paris and thought, “If I had but five hundred francs a month, I should be rich!”

The cause was simple: handsome ground-floor rooms with a garden in the Rue des Martyrs, splendidly furnished. He had secured them in 1833 by a deal with an upholsterer, but they were let for twelve hundred francs, and the quarterly rent cleaned him out. He took hackney cabs freely, spent extravagant sums on breakfasts, smoked costly cigars, and never refused a mistress a dinner or a dress. He lived on future earnings and could no more find a hundred francs on his chimney-piece now than when he first earned two hundred.

Sometimes tired of such vicissitudes and bored by perpetual amusement, Lousteau would sit in his small garden—no larger than a dinner-table—and ask intimates like Nathan or Bixiou what would be the end of them; white hairs were giving them respectful hints. Nathan joked about marriage as drama; Bixiou teased about each man having his own transient “Florine.” Etienne’s Florine was Madame Schontz.

Madame Schontz, a Loretta near Notre-Dame de Lorette, was pretty and took pride in teasing friends by boasting of a wit for her lover; she claimed a real interest in Lousteau. Such bohemian penury mingled with Parisian luxury inevitably affected Dinah’s life—the provincial girl who wrote to him from Sancerre. Lousteau could laugh about his Baroness with friends and even with Madame Schontz; to those who see such behavior as mean, explanation is futile.

When Bixiou asked what he had done in Sancerre, Etienne offered a witty, half-ironical account. Ten days later a letter from Sancerre arrived. He admired the phrasing, then threw the ten sheets into the fire: “That woman was born to reel off copy!” He was not much afraid of Madame Schontz, who loved him for himself; he had supplanted a friend in the heart of a marquise who sometimes dropped in at his rooms veiled and rifled his drawers.

Another packet came—a rapturous eight leaves—but thinking he heard the Marquise, he tossed the proofs of affection unread into the fire. Madame Schontz, entering to find hampers from Sancerre, praised the Tenth Muse’s domestic talents. Lousteau wrote a clever, insidious letter to Dinah—one suited to third-party readers rather than transparent feeling—and by December had ceased to read her letters; they lay in a heap in an unlocked drawer beneath his shirts, their scent lingering.

Chance then came in a form such bohemians should have seized. In mid-December Madame Schontz, who took a real interest in Etienne, begged him to call one morning on business. She told him bluntly: “My dear fellow, you have a chance of marrying.” He joked, but she explained. A young lady had “got into trouble” without her mother’s knowledge; her father, Maître Cardot, an honest notary, had kept the scandal quiet. He wanted to marry her off within a fortnight. He would give her a fortune of one hundred and fifty thousand francs and add a hundred thousand “under the rose” to cover damages. The family were established citizens of the Rue des Lombards.

Etienne objected: why did the lover not marry her? “Dead,” Madame Schontz replied—the man had died of pleurisy after leaving the theatre. He’d been a penniless head-clerk who had entrapped the daughter to marry into the business. Cardot sought a man of at least thirty and would be proud to have a famous son-in-law—someone like Etienne. If he married, he would pay his debts, have twelve thousand francs a year, and a fatherless child without trouble. There was fifty thousand francs of income in the house and the value of the connection; in time Etienne might expect fifteen thousand more. Cardot was Camusot’s brother-in-law, and such connections promised prospects: a sinecure as a librarian or an appointment through Camusot’s influence, and even a stake in a newspaper to yield income—ten thousand francs a year was floated as possible from combined investments and patronage. Madame Schontz asked bluntly, “Can you do better for yourself?”

She acknowledged the downside: a formidable mother-in-law. Madame Cardot was a bigot with lips like faded pink ribbons, prudish enough to destroy her daughter figuratively if she learned the truth. Cardot planned a respectable front—offering a house in the Rue Saint-Lazare bought at the July overthrow; selling it would give Etienne a place to come and go, see the daughter, and appear to possess property in Madame Cardot’s eyes. By Camusot’s influence Etienne might secure a public appointment; if he backed a newspaper, he could profit. Madame Schontz argued that marrying a “consolable widow”—a polite term for a woman with a past—was expedient and fashionable. She urged Lousteau to dine with Malaga the next day, where he would meet Cardot; Malaga had let the secret out and thus the notary would be easier.

The plain, blunt language appealed to Etienne. He agreed to think it over and then, when pressed, accepted. That evening he wrote a long letter to the Marquise explaining reasons for marriage: constant poverty, torpor of imagination, white hairs, moral and physical exhaustion—four pages of argument. “As to Dinah, I will send her a circular announcing the marriage,” he thought. Fearful the marriage might fail, he became eager; he was civil to the notary and assumed an impressive demeanor. “I knew monsieur your father,” he told Cardot, “at Florentine’s… My follies are past. To get on now a man must be free from debt, have a good income, a wife, and a family. If I pay taxes enough to qualify me, I may be a deputy yet.”

Cardot appreciated this profession of reform. Lousteau’s familiarity with the family past and apparent reformation put the notary at ease; soon he was presented as purchaser of the Rue Saint-Lazare house and dined with the family three days later.

Cardot’s house near the Place du Châtelet betrayed economical virtue: gilding under green gauze, holland-covered furniture—wealth disguised by thrift and boredom. Lousteau noticed that Cardot’s real life was elsewhere. He saw a tall, fair girl with blue eyes, shy and languishing—Félicie Cardot. The elder brother, a clerk destined to succeed his father, took a fancy to him; there was also a twelve-year-old sister. Lousteau, adopting a Jesuitical air, played Monarchist and Churchman for the mother’s benefit—smooth, deliberate, complimentary.

Within three weeks, at his fourth dinner, Félicie, who had watched him shyly, brought him a cup of coffee while he stood in the window recess and whispered through tears: “I will devote my whole life, monsieur, to thanking you for your sacrifice in favor of a poor girl—” Lousteau was touched; her look, accent, and attitude had feeling. He pressed her hand and thought, “She would make a good man happy.”

Madame Cardot regarded her future son-in-law as a man of prospects and was delighted by his moral tone. Etienne, prompted by the wily notary, had pledged that he had no natural children or ties that could endanger Félicie. The mother, prudish and cautious, warned that a woman who sinned against duty clung to her sin; such a fall made her ruinous to others—an admonition Lousteau treated lightly and later joked about to Malaga.

By now Lousteau had assumed airs of importance. His life had purpose at last: a house in the Rue Saint-Lazare, marriage to a charming woman, twelve thousand francs a year, respectable connections, and room for ambition. The young lady loved him; he would be linked to several families and could turn social ease into social position. He had seized a chance to exit penury, secure a roof and income, and convert bohemian ease into establishment—though not without costs: concealments, compromises, and the need to placate a rigid, suspicious mother-in-law.

The narrative leaves Etienne in the confident flush of impending fortune, already assuming the roles of husband and gentleman that his friends and new relations expect of him.

Madame Cardot had borrowed an illustrated Gil Blas as a pretext to inspect Lousteau’s bachelor rooms and judge him as a suitor for her niece Félicie. Others, like Madame Camusot, had spoken ill of the journalist; Lousteau, who mocked the bourgeoisie, treated the visit as ordinary.

One January day Madame Cardot and Félicie went to Rue des Martyrs to return the volumes and see Lousteau’s lodging. The porter was out; his daughter took the gold piece Madame Cardot slipped her and handed over the key. Lousteau, returning from breakfast, fancied he saw Dinah, the Baroness de la Baudraye, arrive by hired coach with trunks and a child. She announced she had left her country house to be with him, calling him “my love,” and claiming to be his wife and the child’s mother. Taken aback but practical, Lousteau tried to soothe her and conceal the situation as guests appeared: quick-witted Pamela, fourteen, and then Madame Cardot and Félicie, who slipped into the bedroom.

Madame Cardot made a scene, denouncing Lousteau as unfit for her niece and vowing Félicie would never marry him. Dinah wept and clung to Lousteau, who whispered comforts that reassured the provincial woman. Soon Lousteau conceived a stratagem: if Didine believed he intended to marry Félicie, she might renounce her claim for his prospects. He even wrote to his friend Bixiou to play a severe elder and frighten the Muse of Sancerre into yielding.

Meanwhile Lousteau installed Dinah, charmed her with attentions so she felt adored, and set Pamela to be her waiting-maid; Pamela ran to fetch dinner and borrow plates from Madame Schontz, who hurried to warn Cardot. The rooms’ superficially elegant air hid Lousteau’s poverty, but Dinah, intoxicated by romance, dreamed of domestic happiness.

Bixiou arrived mocking and contemptuous, painting the practical ruin of Lousteau’s course: no steady income or savings, destroyed prospects for social, political, and financial advancement, risk of scandal over an affair and a child. Lousteau, stung, still insisted he loved Dinah and preferred her and fatherhood to fortune.

The argument closed when Madame Schontz returned with news: Cardot had preserved Félicie’s prospects by placing her with the late head-clerk’s deputy, so Lousteau’s chance at a wealthy marriage was gone. Furious at losing fortune and a respectable match, and with rivals neutralized, Lousteau devoted himself entirely to Dinah, keeping her dependent by amusements and tender attentions. Thus began the familiar Parisian pattern: his selfish, calculated passion and her naive devotion produced an intoxicating intimacy in which passion drowned prudence and prophecy.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
This winter in Paris meant to Madame de la Baudraye what October had been at Sancerre. Etienne Lousteau, eager to make “his wife” a Parisienne, paired their honeymoon with theatre evenings where Dinah sat in the stage box. Initially clinging to provincial modesty, she soon yielded to Lousteau’s vanity and instruction in fashion and society. Her Sancerre air vanished; his friends praised his conquest. Etienne, writing little and running into debt, affected indifference; Dinah, proud, bought clothes from her savings and fancied she had cost him nothing. After three months she loved the opera, knew the plays and actors, and adapted to the sharp, witty Parisian world where cleverness reigns.

One morning, reading Lousteau’s papers, Dinah noticed a line that recalled Sancerre: “Monsieur le Baron de Clagny... appointed Deputy Public Prosecutor.” A journalist sneered that the lawyer loved her; she only said, “Poor man! He is following me.” At that dazzling stage of passion, familiarity and freshness coexisted; enchantment reigned without collision. Dinah spoke love’s hyperbole and retained modesty and ambition in intimacy; Lousteau involuntarily held her in high esteem. “There is in Dinah,” he told Bixiou, “the stuff to make both a Ninon and a De Staël.”

When Dinah’s pregnancy began to show, she planned to withdraw but insisted on attending a Nathan first night—the literary ceremonial. Dressed perfectly, she was abandoned by Lousteau during the evening and found herself exposed in the box. Monsieur de Clagny entered. He greeted her warmly but warned that a woman living openly with a man of letters invites curiosity and loss of respect; perhaps she would be happier at home with her mother. Dinah replied she had renounced society for love and was content. Clagny, moved, wondered whether Lousteau appreciated such sacrifice. Dinah invited him to see for himself; he left to avoid scandal when Lousteau returned, who boasted, “You are a greater hit than the piece.” The brief triumph pleased Dinah, but three days later reality arrived: they were penniless, with two quarters’ rent unpaid and distraint imminent. Dinah repented tempting Etienne into extravagance.

Unaccustomed to owing, she learned through Migeon she could pawn jewels; her “uncle” advanced nine hundred francs. She kept three hundred for the baby and gave Lousteau the rest so he could complete a novel for a periodical. “Finish without sacrificing to necessity,” she urged. “Polish the style; work up the subject. I will be housewife and attend to business.” For months Etienne had taken Dinah daily to the Café Riche; she was dismayed to find five hundred francs owing for the last fortnight. He shrugged: “It makes little difference whether we are robbed at a restaurant or by a cook.” She secured a kitchen and two servants’ rooms and wrote to her mother for linen and a loan. Her mother sent two trunks of linen, some plate, and two thousand francs.

Ten days after the theatre, Clagny called and found Dinah making a cap; the proud image of a woman at needlework moved him. He saw she intended to be Lousteau’s guardian spirit, to lead him to a nobler road. Dinah recognized her husband’s troubles sprang from indolence and lack of will; she strove to make him happy by economy and method. “His happiness will be my absolution,” she told Clagny.

But Clagny, deceived at first by Lousteau’s ingratiating manner, gradually saw the truth. Between April and July, while Dinah expected confinement, she discovered why Lousteau had not triumphed over poverty: he was idle and weak-willed. Genius requires will; talent without it remains unproductive. Men who lead their age deny themselves and keep their faculties ready. Lousteau’s cigarette habit dulled him; criticism as a profession—praising or tearing down for convenience—stultified him. He could not bridge the gulf between dexterity and art.

Dinah’s intellect quickened and she measured her idol. She saw him scamping work, writing at the last minute. She excused him—“He is a poet!”—and tried to help. Loving him, she sought through her mother to negotiate an allowance from her father without Etienne’s knowledge. A few days before July’s end she crumpled a letter from her mother conveying Monsieur de la Baudraye’s ultimatum: Dinah could live in perfect luxury at Anzy and therefore might return. Lousteau read the letter and, with ominous gallantry, vowed vengeance—words flattering to a woman whose antipathies were flattered.

Five days later Bianchon and Duriau were engaged at Lousteau’s; he made a display over the infant’s arrival. Monsieur de Clagny and Madame Piedefer were to be godparents. Dinah gave birth to a son of remarkable beauty. Bianchon and Clagny registered the child as the son of Monsieur and Madame de la Baudraye, unknown to Etienne. Lousteau rushed to a printer and had fifty circulars set up proclaiming: “Madame la Baronne de la Baudraye is happily delivered of a son. Monsieur Etienne Lousteau has the pleasure of informing you.” Clagny found a list of Sancerre names Lousteau planned to notify, confiscated proofs and remaining circulars, and had corrected proofs printed naming Monsieur le Baron de la Baudraye as informing party. Clagny destroyed the originals and intercepted sent copies, recovering thirty and leaving only one of the original notes—sent to Nathan. He implored Nathan to yield the one copy; at first Nathan laughed, but, convinced the notice could damage a woman and child ignorant of their financial and social peril, he surrendered.

When the jest was counteracted, Clagny reprimanded Lousteau in Madame Piedefer’s presence and found Etienne fuming. “I did it with a purpose!” Etienne protested. “Monsieur de la Baudraye has sixty thousand francs a year and refuses an allowance; I wished to make him feel the child is in my power.” Clagny coolly replied he had agreed to be godfather to prevent such schemes; if the child were registered as the Baudraye heir, it could inherit entailed estates. Lousteau retorted, “And is the mother to die of hunger?” Clagny promised to transact with Monsieur de la Baudraye. He left chilled; Dinah, his idol, seemed loved for her money; he pitied her.

Infant care and Madame Piedefer’s presence hindered writing; Lousteau moved to first-floor rooms. Obliged to attend first nights without Dinah and living apart, freedom proved alluring. He was dragged to jollifications and friends’ mistresses’ houses, seeing brilliantly dressed young women in whom economy seemed treason. Dinah, after nursing for three months, could not compete with such perishable charms.

Home life nonetheless had its charms. In three months the mother, Didine the Sancerre cook, and little Pamela transformed the apartment. Lousteau found breakfasts and dinners served with domestic luxury; Dinah anticipated his wishes; he felt king at home. Didine’s affection showed in every trifle. The deceptions of his unreal passion continued.

Dinah knew Lousteau’s life threatened ruin to love and household. After ten months she weaned her baby, installed her mother upstairs, and restored family intimacy. Daily domestic intercourse gives a woman power; men left too often to themselves become susceptible to release. Dinah intended to be indispensable and to infuse energy into Lousteau, whose weakness she mistook for security. She suggested subjects, sketched treatments, sometimes wrote whole chapters. Her collaboration revived dying talent; she produced two successful books while keeping her role secret from Madame Piedefer.

This galvanism improved pay, and they lived comfortably through 1838. Lousteau grew used to seeing Dinah do his work and paid her nothing—“monkey money.” The more she gave, the more she loved him; she felt she could never give him up.

But another child came, and that year proved a trial. Despite precautions, Etienne contracted debts and worked nearly to death to pay them while Dinah was ill; she thought him heroic. Appalled at supporting two women, children, and two maids, he could not continue sustaining them by his pen after failing even to maintain himself. So he let things take their chance and exaggerated home-made love to secure liberty abroad.

Dinah endured stoically without adequate support. Belief in his love gave her strength. She worked hard, sacrificing dress—dying gowns black—and risking beauty. Malaga mocked the stench of black; thrift irked Lousteau, who reproached her for dowdiness while secretly benefiting from her sacrifices.

She tried to steer this weak man; in doing so she lost some moral lustre; suspicion and reproach led to quarrels and bred disrespect. Lousteau entertained friends—Nathan, Bixiou, Blondet, Finot—whose manners and talk were depraving. They mocked her principles as provincial prudery and preached woman’s superiority. During Carnival 1840 she disguised herself to spy on Etienne at balls and suppers. The morning after Mid-Lent she returned from a fancy-dress ball intending to go to bed; she had gone to watch Lousteau, who, believing her ill, had engaged that evening to Fanny Beaupré. Warned by a friend, he behaved so as to deceive her, and she, eager to be deceived, was duped.

Stepping from the hired cab she met Monsieur de la Baudraye; the porter pointed her out. The old man took his wife’s arm and, with cold conjugal authority, confronted Dinah: “So this is you, madame!” To escape Etienne’s eye she had chosen a dress he would not detect; using her mask she slipped away, changed, and went to her mother’s rooms, where she found her husband waiting. Despite assumed dignity she blushed.

“What do you want of me, monsieur? Are we not separated forever?” she asked.

“Actually, yes. Legally, no,” he answered. Madame Piedefer signalled; Dinah understood his visit was prompted by interest. “Nothing could have brought you here but your own interests,” she said bitterly. “Our interests,” he replied coldly, “for we have two children. Your Uncle Silas Piedefer is dead in New York; after making and losing fortunes, he left some seven or eight hundred thousand francs—some say a million. I am chief in our common interests and act for you.”

“In everything that relates to business I trust Monsieur de Clagny,” she retorted. “He knows the law; come to terms with him.”

“I have no occasion for Monsieur de Clagny to take my children from you,” Monsieur de la Baudraye said.

“Your children!” Dinah laughed. “Your children to whom you have sent no sou!” The outburst froze under his coolness. “I do not intend to part from them. I shall take them to Anzy, if only to save them from seeing their mother disguised like a—”

“Silence!” Dinah cried. “What do you want of me?”

“A power of attorney to receive our Uncle Silas’ property.” Dinah wrote two lines to Clagny and asked her husband to return in the afternoon.

At five o’clock Clagny—now Attorney-General—explained her position and undertook to bargain with the old man. Monsieur de la Baudraye, needing her power of attorney to deal as he wished, bought it by concessions: he agreed to allow her ten thousand francs a year so long as she resided in Paris; the children were to be placed in his custody at six; and Clagny extracted payment in advance.

Little La Baudraye came jaunty to say good-bye in a white india-rubber overcoat. He resembled the La Baudraye of 1836 so that Dinah despaired. From the garden, where he smoked, Lousteau watched him cross the forecourt. This short scene altered the writer’s private calculus. Their life together had cost both sides; in moral accounting Lousteau considered himself the creditor. It was a favourable moment to desert her. Tired after three years of a comedy that could not be habit, he hid his weariness and forced smiles like a debtor. The advantages he had expected in the future—an allowance, secured possibilities—had sustained him; when Monsieur de la Baudraye embarked for America, Lousteau ceased to believe in that future.

Entering the drawing-room where Dinah had just left her husband, he found her grave. “Etienne,” she said, “do you know my lord proposed that if I wish to return to Anzy during his absence, he hopes my mother’s advice will weigh with me and that I shall go back with my children.” “It is very good advice,” Lousteau replied dryly. His tone struck Dinah, who lived only in love; two tears slipped unnoticed as she wiped them. She confessed she felt she had sold her children by selling the chance to remain, that he would have them at six and she could only see them in Sancerre—torture.

Lousteau knelt and kissed her hands, coaxing. “You do not understand me,” he said. “I blame myself; I am not worth such sacrifices. I am second-rate in literature. If I can no longer cut a figure at the bottom of the newspaper, the editors will throw me aside. We tight-rope dancers have no retiring pension! I am forty-two, idle as a marmot. My love can only be fatal to you. At twenty-two I lived on Florine; what was excusable in youth is a disgrace to a man of forty. Out of devotion you wear black and that does me no credit.” Dinah shrugged magnanimously. His prudence, measured like Clagny’s, gratified and staunched her tears. “He loves me for myself alone!” she thought, smiling.

After four years their intimacy combined every shade modern society creates: heart and head, passion, caprice, and taste. Lousteau could magnetize Dinah—one look or touch reduced her to obedience. Fascinated by his wit and magnetized by his airs, his vices seemed trivial; she even loved the cigar-smoke drifting into her room. She hated editors who refused Lousteau money and deluded herself that he was writing a remunerative novel rather than merely spinning out debts.

This, she argued, is true love: having placed herself outside the pale, why should he not sacrifice some of a man’s honour for her? “We live outside social convention,” she said. “Nathan can accept homage from Florine; why not from me? We will square accounts when we part; only death can part us. We shall be married some day—time enough to part when little La Baudraye is safe back again.” “I never thought of that,” Lousteau said simply, adding to himself, “Time enough to part when little La Baudraye is safe back again.”

From that day Etienne Lousteau lived in luxury; Dinah, at first nights, could hold her own with the best-dressed women in Paris. Lousteau affectingly adopted the air of a man crushed by fate, ruined by Madame de la Baudraye, and pretended despair to elicit pity. He took precautions because of Dinah’s jealousy when accepting invitations, yet remained shamelessly unfaithful. Monsieur de Clagny, in despair at seeing Dinah disgraced though she might have been rich and in the position she had sought, came to warn her — “You are betrayed” — and she only answered, “I know it.”

Reduced to silence for a moment, Clagny could still find the words to speak. He was interrupted by Madame de la Baudraye, who asked Lousteau plainly whether he still loved her. “I would lose my soul for you!” he cried, leaping up. The man’s eyes flashed; he trembled like a leaf at being accepted as his idol’s avenger; he felt rapture in the poor joy of being given that role. Dinah explained that such passion was how she loved Lousteau; the lawyer wept; even the stern judge who had once condemned a man to death had tears in his eyes.

Yet Lousteau’s satiety — the odious, slow dwindling of illicit passion — betrayed itself in countless little humiliations that Dinah had not perceived until they grew into insurmountable grievances. Finally Madame de la Baudraye understood Lousteau’s character: a poet, defenseless against misfortune, mean from laziness, rather inclined to pleasure, a great cat who could not be hated. She told her mother she would be a mother to him. That resolution showed love turning into burden and toil; the part she assumed became a painful sacrifice rather than joy.

Lousteau made that assumption still harder. When he wanted to go out after dinner he played tender comedies, addressing Dinah in words full of devotion; he would take her by the chain and, when he had bruised her, ask, “Did I wound you?” These mock caresses degraded Dinah, who hoped yet for a revival of his love. The mother yielded to the mistress with shameful readiness; she felt herself a mere plaything and at last confessed: “I will be his plaything!” — and found joy in it, a rapture of damnation.

A woman of sterner spirit might have preferred solitude, but Dinah feared the emptiness it implied. She preferred the miseries of fierce intimacy to the absence of the bittersweet joys that spring from remorse and struggle. Every night, she awaited Lousteau’s return as a thirsty traveler in a desert longing for the bitter pool; she was not fully alive until she heard his boots and the bell. She tried to restrain him by giving pleasure, hoped to rival his other lovers, and rehearsed partings as if in a drama. A single artless word or kiss would plunge her back into the depths of love. She sometimes contemplated suicide while pacing the small town garden. Her devotion, vast and inexhaustible, remained intact.

Adolphe was her Bible; she refused to be an Ellenore. She forbade herself the tactics of tears and reproach celebrated in the critic’s commentary she admired. She resolved not to dog his footsteps nor use silence as reproach; she held out the hope that if, for one day, he should recognize her merits and say, “That victim never uttered a cry!” that would suffice. And she consoled herself that others had him for an hour, while she had him for life.

While Dinah endured these humiliations, her husband, Monsieur de la Baudraye, acted with the cold, calculating spirit of a man devoted to his estate. He had been punishing his wife privately and, meanwhile, devoted himself to reclaiming three thousand acres of moorland — a project to which he had been committed since 1836. Living like a mouse, he manipulated the property of Silas Piedefer so ingeniously that he reduced the apparent value to eight hundred thousand francs while pocketing twelve hundred thousand. He did not announce his return to Paris; while his wife endured unspeakable suffering, he was building farms, digging trenches, ploughing rough ground, displaying a courage that made him one of the most remarkable provincial agriculturists.

The four hundred thousand francs he had taken from his wife were spent in three years on these improvements at Anzy, expected to return seventy-two thousand francs a year net after taxes. The eight hundred thousand he had invested at four and a half percent, bought during the financial panic of March, would yield forty-eight thousand francs for her expenses. In his mind he had restored her income sufficiently to discharge his obligation. He fancied himself on the road to being the richest proprietor in France, calculating an eventual income of a hundred and forty thousand francs, paying his taxes, working expenses, his wife’s allowance, and a small pension to his mother-in-law — and thus to be fit to petition for the title of Count. He boasted that his outlay amounted to a respectable sum and contemplated going to Paris to seek promotion to the peerage. He said these things with such composure that no one dared laugh; only the presiding judge remarked that a man like him should be happier if he had a daughter.

By early 1842 Madame de la Baudraye, feeling she was to Lousteau no more than a reserve, had again sacrificed herself completely to secure his comfort. She resumed wearing black as mourning; pleasure began to turn into remorse. Her mother watched for exhaustion, as their priest advised, believing that the collapse would surely come and that then the children could be reclaimed. Madame Piedefer urged separation from Lousteau, not asking her daughter to give him up entirely but to withdraw. In violent real-life situations, resolutions rarely close as in novels; they end in disgust, the blighting of the soul, habit, or another passion that robs a wife of interest. As sense, propriety, family interest, the fatigue of constant sacrifice, and some insult too cruel to describe combined to awaken Dinah’s revulsion, her mother prepared to intervene.

Clagny, who had taken on Dinah’s cause, assured her that if she gave up Etienne her husband would allow her to keep the children, live in Paris, and restore her command of her fortune. “With care and judgment, and the support of pious persons, you may have a salon and conquer a position. Paris is not Sancerre,” he said. Dinah left it to Clagny to negotiate reconciliation with her husband.

La Baudraye had been selling produce and timber and, without informing his wife, had come to Paris to invest two hundred thousand francs in a residence in the Rue de l’Arcade. He had been on the Departmental Council since 1826, and paying ten thousand francs in taxes now made him eligible, under new laws, for a peerage. Some time before the 1842 elections he offered himself as a candidate unless called to the Upper House; he asked for the title of Count and for promotion in the Legion of Honor. Monsieur de Clagny supported him, arguing to the authorities that promoting this enterprising agriculturist to the peerage would guarantee his undertakings. The thought of being a Count appealed to the Baron: he wanted to enjoy life.

He wrote to his wife, with a letter dictated by Clagny, asking her to live under his roof and furnish the house, giving play to her taste as shown at the Chateau d’Anzy. He requested that Clagny place sixty thousand francs at Madame la Comtesse’s disposal for decoration, even suggesting a marble tablet with the inscription Hotel de la Baudraye. He accounted for Silas Piedefer’s money, told her about the four and a half percent investment, and allowed her that income for expenses, including the children’s education. He would be compelled to stay in Paris during the sessions of the House of Peers and asked her to reserve for him a suite in an entresol over the kitchens.

Comparing this future grandeur with her present humiliation was unbearable to Dinah. Only the day before she had been shunned by Anna de Fontaine at Chamarolles’ school. The prospect of becoming a Countess — to have the peer’s blue hammer-cloth on her carriage, leaders of the literary world in her drawing-room, and to look with quiet triumph at those who had scorned her — thrilled her. The thought of rehabilitation, of restored rank and social victory, weighed heavily as she prepared to accept her destiny.

In May 1842 Madame de la Baudraye paid small household debts, left a modest sum after settling accounts, sent her mother and the children ahead, and prepared to confront Lousteau. At dinner, she told Etienne she had upset the pot and requested his company at the Rocher de Cancale. She bewildered him with the light, easy manners of a woman who had been his slave but had spent months acting the part of a mistress. He joked about her being “figged out,” but she demanded respect: “Do not forget the respect you owe to Madame de la Baudraye.” He put his arm round her: she replied that there was no longer any Didine; he had killed her. “I am taking you to the first performance of Madame la Comtesse de la Baudraye,” she said.

“It is true, then, that our insect is a peer of France?” he sneered.

“The nomination is to be gazetted tonight,” she answered. The revelation of his title and the changes in their circumstances concentrated Dinah’s sense of what she had lost and what she might now recover socially. She tried to control trembling and spoke gravely: they were parting forever. She had dismissed the servants, put the house in order, and wanted a calm, decent separation. “I shall always feel a mother’s affection for you, but in secret,” she said.

Lousteau, in his later anger, gave her a dark lecture about her reading of Benjamin Constant and her failure to understand men. He argued that she had read Adolphe with “a woman’s eyes,” seeing only Ellenore while failing to see Adolphe’s forfeited future — how a man sacrifices his prospects for love and can never become what he might have been. Men who sacrifice themselves for women lose their privileges and are often deserted. He accused Dinah of having spoiled his life and wrecked his prospects; she had made him give up ambition for a love that the woman herself might later abandon. Dinah, astonished, promised to restore to him all he had lost by her, to transform him if God gave him life and he renounced his courtesans and actresses.

Their bitterness rose; Lousteau suggested secrecy, that they hide their love and see each other in private. Dinah cried “Never!” — a definitive refusal that she uttered in fear of being swayed by him. “Never” she insisted when he taunted her with a suggestion she had earlier made. For Lousteau, that “Never” was a death-warrant of his power: Dinah remained insensible to his scorn. He could not suppress a tear at the loss of sincere affection; he had found in Dinah a gentle La Valliere and a delightful Pompadour, and like a boy who has killed a cockchafer by tormenting it, he wept. Dinah fled, paid the bill, and went home, calling herself a brute in her recollection.

Dinah, who had made her house a model of comfort, now remade herself to fit her new position — a transformation that cost thirty thousand francs more than her husband had anticipated. A fatal accident that removed the Orleans heir in 1842 forced the Chambers to meet in August; La Baudraye presented his titles to the Upper House sooner than expected and then saw what his wife had achieved. He was so delighted he paid the thirty thousand francs without complaint, as he had once paid eight thousand to decorate La Baudraye. After being presented in the Luxembourg by two peers — the Baron de Nucingen and the Marquis de Montriveau — he met the old Duc de Chaulieu walking; the contrast between himself and the great noble filled him with intoxicated pride.

Madame de la Baudraye, though consumed by melancholy she concealed by determined dignity, was charming, witty, and seemed young again in her court mourning. The Count lauded her youth to Monsieur de Nucingen: “You might declare the Countess was not yet thirty.” Her companions joked about her vanity and curiosities. La Baudraye’s pride was small but genuine; he admired the silver covers on the table and joked about the family’s fleeces.

After the Regency vote passed, the Count returned to Sancerre for the vintage and resumed his old habits. That winter the Countess, supported by the Attorney-General to the Court of Appeals, tried to form a small circle in Paris. She contrived an “at home” day, selected men of mark, went twice weekly to the Opera with her mother and Madame de Clagny, and, despite cleverness and social success, was never really happy but with her children, on whom she lavished disappointed affection. Clagny, who had championed her, recruited pious women as much as fashionable ones and tried to introduce famous foreign celebrities on Wednesdays, praising her as exceptional even to those she barely addressed. Her silence and attention impressed them; she conquered by charm and occasional smart sayings, the woman who had once resurrected Lousteau’s writing.

To Clagny, this phase seemed like an Indian summer after a sunless year. He affected a senior dignity and kept a respectful distance; he attempted a discreet devotion that he hoped would be returned. Dinah made fun of him to his face; yet he thought his lionizing might have been noticed. Once, on the way from the theatre, after seeing Leon Gozlan’s La Main Droite et la Main Gauche, Clagny noticed that Dinah was pensive. He asked what she was thinking about; she demurred, then confided that she had seen Lousteau and wondered whether he had money or a cigar. Clagny, unable to keep a secret any longer, told her the truth: Lousteau was living as a husband with Fanny Beaupre. He had hesitated to tell her for fear she might suspect an ungenerous motive.

Dinah seized his hand. “Your husband is one of the rarest souls!” she began to say, and then shrank into silence, reflecting that Lousteau had not matched Clagny’s generosity. The knowledge that Lousteau had degenerated into domestic respectability with Fanny cured Dinah’s melancholy; she threw herself into fashion and social triumphs. She achieved a certain success, though introductions among women were difficult. In March, through the influence of priests and Clagny, she was appointed receiver of subscriptions for Madame de Carcado’s charity to gather Royal Family donations for Guadeloupe earthquake relief. The Marquise d’Espard later called the maneuvers for her rehabilitation the finest she had seen in the world.

By early spring 1843 Paris smiled and the Champs-Elysées turned green. Lousteau’s attendant in Fanny Beaupre’s household had seen Madame de la Baudraye in a carriage, well dressed with dreamy eyes and her two boys at the windows, and was stung. He had been warring with shameful poverty, yet had maintained a point of honor to avoid public humiliation. Lousteau dined and smoked as if rich, indulged in expensive cigars, walked in patent-leather boots — but he feared execution on his goods; Fanny had pawned everything and advances from publishers had dried up. The journalist had borrowed a hundred francs from the poor Bixiou and faced humiliation: a yellow poster announced the sale of a handsome suite of furniture. He wandered the streets, searching for ideas, and heard Bixiou’s warning that persistent drunkenness would one day wake him mad.

On the Friday before the sale he felt doomed. He might once have answered, “The furniture is old; I will buy new,” but he could no longer perform such literary legerdemain. The markets were unsympathetic and publishers paid poorly. Walking with a cigar and an affected smile, he saw Madame de la Baudraye pass in a carriage; he had nothing left. At seven in the evening he arrived in a hackney at her door and begged the porter to send a note: would Madame la Comtesse receive him for a moment? He used the familiar seal they had once shared — the carnelian on which she had “Parce que” engraved. The sight of that seal made Dinah pale. She had just been dressing for the Opera, yet she resolved to see him.

Dinah received Lousteau in a boudoir with open doors, exquisitely dressed in pale blue brocade and pearls. He was the picture of a neat dandy. He told her he was drowning; his affairs were going to the devil. He listed his needs — to save his furniture from the auction he needed eighteen hundred francs, to repay friends as much again, to cover rent and to appease his ‘uncle.’ “And you — to live on?” she asked. He answered that he had his pen. “It is heavier to lift than people think,” she smiled, and promised to help. She did not have the entire sum on hand, but told him to come at eight the next morning: the bailiff would wait until nine, especially if Lousteau brought him away to be paid.

He thanked her, calling her a drop of water in the desert, sent by an angel. The tenderness between them revived: he pressed her hand to his heart, their eyes kindled with the old flame, they embraced, cheek to cheek. Dinah felt the old attraction; she tried to control it, but passion flared briefly. In a decisive moment she called to her mother to fetch money from the banker, and hurried her away in order to hide the scene in the boudoir from detection. Madame Piedefer, persuaded to fetch funds, later consulted the parish cure and lamented her daughter’s condition. The priest replied that any moral regeneration not grounded in strong religious sentiment and the Church is built on sand. He urged the mother to persuade Dinah to perform her duties if they hoped to save her.

Within ten days the Hotel de la Baudraye was shut. The Countess, the children, her mother, and household, including a tutor, had gone to Sancerre for the summer. Dinah intended to be everything that was “nice” to her husband; tongues said she had been compelled to return, and that the Count would soon have his wish for a little girl. Gatien and Monsieur Gravier lavished attention on the Countess; the tutor was supposed to have a chance at favor; Madame Piedefer urged devotion and religion.

In June 1844, as the Comte walked in Sancerre with his two fine boys, he met the Public Prosecutor Monsieur Milaud and proudly introduced them, saying, “These are my children, cousin.” The lawyer twinkled and replied with the familiar provincial banter: “Ah ha! so these are our children!”

Thus the Muse of the Department ends with the return of Dinah to family life, her brief re-entry to Parisian society, the ruin and humiliation of Lousteau, and the Count’s modest triumph as a provincial man made peer. Dinah’s moral position remains ambiguous: she sacrifices for love, seeks at once social rehabilitation and maternal devotion, and oscillates between tenderness, pride, and penitence. Lousteau, a charming, lazy poet and journalist, derives benefits from her tenderness, squanders them in libertinage, and finally collapses into poverty and dependence. Clagny, the good lawyer, is the faithful friend who counsels and aids Dinah, while Madame Piedefer and the local priest represent the moral and religious pressure that ultimately directs Dinah back to the domestic sphere.

ADDENDUM

Several personages in the tale figure in other stories of Balzac’s Human Comedy, linking this narrative to a larger social panorama: Fanny Beaupre (appears in A Start in Life; Modeste Mignon; Scenes from a Courtesan’s Life), Madame Berthier (Felicie Cardot) in Cousin Pons, Horace Bianchon in many works including Father Goriot and Lost Illusions, Bixiou in The Purse and many other tales, Camusot in A Distinguished Provincial at Paris, and other recurring characters such as Cardot, Falcon, the Grosstete brothers, Marshal Hulot, La Baudraye’s other appearances, the Laizomère, Lousteau himself, Lupeaulx, the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, Milaud, Raoul Nathan and his wife, the Duc de Navarreins, the Baron Nucingen, and numerous other figures who populate Balzac’s interlinked universe — characters who traverse novels like Cesar Birotteau, The Firm of Nucingen, The Member for Arcis, A Daughter of Eve, The Secrets of a Princess, and many more, thus binding the moral and social fates depicted in The Muse of the Department to the sweep of Balzac’s grand design.
