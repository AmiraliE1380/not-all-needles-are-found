**A Daughter of Eve**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati. This work is a token of gratitude for the joy your conversation brought to a traveler reminiscing about Paris.

---

**Chapter I: The Two Maries**  
In a luxurious boudoir in Paris, two sisters, Madame du Tillet and Madame Felix de Vandenesse, are in distress. Their upbringing under a strict mother left them innocent and unprepared for the complexities of life. They married into contrasting social spheres, with Angelique marrying a nobleman and Eugenie marrying a wealthy banker, Ferdinand du Tillet. Despite their different paths, they share a deep bond.

The sisters’ childhood was marked by a rigid education focused on piety and duty, leaving them unaware of their own worth and desires. Their marriages, while seemingly advantageous, reveal the oppressive nature of their husbands. Angelique finds herself in a loveless marriage, while Eugenie struggles under the weight of her husband's ambition and emotional distance.

**Chapter II: A Confidence Between Sisters**  
Angelique confides in Eugenie about her unhappiness, revealing the emptiness of her luxurious life. Eugenie, feeling trapped in her own marriage, empathizes with her sister's plight. They discuss the possibility of seeking help from their wealthy connections, but Eugenie feels powerless against her husband’s control.

**Chapter III: The History of a Fortunate Woman**  
Felix de Vandenesse, a man of noble character, marries Angelique, hoping to provide her with a loving home. Initially, their marriage flourishes, but as time passes, Angelique feels stifled by the monotony of her life. She longs for excitement and passion, which leads her to question her happiness.

**Chapter IV: A Celebrated Man**  
Raoul Nathan, a rising literary figure, enters the scene. He captivates Angelique with his charm and intellect, igniting her dormant desires. Their connection grows, but Nathan is entangled in his own struggles for success and recognition. He juggles his ambitions while navigating the complexities of love and societal expectations.

**Chapter V: Florine**  
Florine, an actress and Nathan's mistress, represents the allure of a bohemian lifestyle. She is both a source of comfort and a reminder of Nathan's precarious position. Their relationship is marked by passion and dependency, but it also highlights the sacrifices they both make for their respective careers.

**Chapter VI: Romantic Love**  
As Nathan and Angelique's relationship deepens, they grapple with the societal constraints that threaten to tear them apart. Their love is passionate yet fraught with complications, as both struggle to balance their desires with their responsibilities.

**Chapter VII: Suicide**  
Nathan's mounting pressures lead him to contemplate suicide, feeling overwhelmed by his failures and the weight of his ambitions. However, Angelique's love and support pull him back from the brink, illustrating the transformative power of love.

**Chapter VIII: A Lover Saved and Lost**  
Despite their efforts to navigate their tumultuous emotions, Nathan and Angelique find themselves caught in a web of societal expectations and personal ambitions. Their love story becomes a reflection of the struggles faced by many in their social circles, highlighting the sacrifices made in the name of love.

**Chapter IX: The Husband’s Triumph**  
Felix de Vandenesse, aware of the growing bond between his wife and Nathan, takes action to protect his family’s honor. He confronts the situation with a mix of understanding and authority, ultimately seeking to shield Angelique from the consequences of her choices.

---

**Addendum**  
The characters in this narrative intersect with other stories in Balzac's *Human Comedy*, illustrating the interconnectedness of lives in 19th-century France. Each character's journey reflects broader themes of ambition, love, and societal constraints, creating a rich tapestry of human experience.
