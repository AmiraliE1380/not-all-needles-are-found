The story begins with Doctor Monsieur Bianchon recounting a chilling tale about La Grande Bretèche, a dilapidated house near Vendôme, surrounded by a wild garden that hints at its former splendor. The house's decay evokes mystery and melancholy, suggesting a tragic history.

Bianchon imagines the house as a refuge for a country gentleman passionate about horticulture. The overgrown garden symbolizes lost joy, while the house's ruin seems cursed, raising questions about its abandonment.

During his stay in Vendôme, Bianchon becomes fascinated by the house and often sneaks into the garden, allowing his imagination to create stories of its past glory. One evening, he meets Monsieur Regnault, a local notary, who reveals that the house belongs to the estate of the late Comtesse de Merret. Regnault explains that her will forbids anyone from entering or repairing the house for fifty years after her death, adding to its mystery.

Regnault recounts the Comtesse's tragic story. After her husband, the Count de Merret, died, she became reclusive and eccentric, leaving La Grande Bretèche in disarray. Rumors about her mental state circulated. On her deathbed, she summoned Regnault to witness her will, intending to leave her estate to the hospital in Vendôme, with strict conditions regarding the house.

The will's peculiar stipulations intrigue Bianchon. Regnault, a former clerk turned notary, describes the Comtesse's decline marked by isolation and illness. Her last moments were filled with urgency to secure her wishes, culminating in her death shortly after finalizing her will.

As Bianchon reflects on the Comtesse's life and the fate of La Grande Bretèche, he becomes engrossed in local gossip. His landlady, Madame Lepas, reveals more about the Comtesse and her husband, depicting a once-happy couple whose relationship soured. The Count was known for his temper, while the Comtesse was admired for her beauty.

Madame Lepas also shares a secret involving a young Spanish prisoner who stayed at her inn during the war, hinting at possible connections to the Comtesse's tragic fate.

The narrative intertwines themes of love, loss, and the passage of time, as Bianchon grapples with the haunting legacy of La Grande Bretèche, prompting readers to ponder the nature of memory and the shadows of the past that shape the present.

In a parallel story, a woman recounts the mysterious disappearance of Count Férédia, a Spanish Grandee living in her house. After he fails to return one night, she finds a letter and a significant sum of money and diamonds he left behind, intended for masses of gratitude for his escape. Her husband searches for him and discovers the Count's clothes near the river, suggesting he escaped rather than drowned.

The story darkens as the woman suspects the Count's involvement in the death of Madame de Merret, whose crucifix resembled one owned by the Count. She grapples with the moral dilemma of keeping the Count's money, which she feels is rightfully hers.

Intrigued by Rosalie, the maid who seems to know secrets about Madame de Merret's death, the narrator seeks the truth. Rosalie reveals that Madame de Merret had been ill, and her husband, Monsieur de Merret, was unaware of her condition until he returned home late one night.

That evening, suspecting something was wrong, Monsieur de Merret decided to check on his wife instead of trusting Rosalie. He confronted Madame de Merret, who denied anyone being in the cupboard despite his suspicions. In a tense moment, she swore on a crucifix that no one was there, leading to a tragic outcome.

Believing in his wife's purity, Monsieur de Merret chose not to open the cupboard but planned to wall it up with mason Gorenflot. As the wall was built, Madame de Merret attempted to signal to the man inside, indicating hope for his escape.

After the wall was completed, Monsieur de Merret confined his wife to her room for twenty days, reminding her of her oath, leaving her in despair. The story concludes with the women at the table reflecting on the moral complexities and emotional turmoil that unfolded within La Grande Bretèche.
