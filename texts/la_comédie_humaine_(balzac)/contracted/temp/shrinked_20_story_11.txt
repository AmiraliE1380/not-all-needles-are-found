**La Grande Bretèche**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage and Clara Bell*

---

The story opens with Doctor Monsieur Bianchon sharing a chilling tale at a late-night gathering about La Grande Bretèche, a dilapidated house near Vendôme, surrounded by a wild garden that hints at its once-thriving estate. The house, now in ruins, evokes mystery and melancholy, suggesting a tragic history.

Bianchon imagines the house as a haven for a country gentleman passionate about horticulture. The overgrown garden symbolizes lost joy and forgotten lives, while the house's decay seems to carry a curse, raising questions about its abandonment.

During his stay in Vendôme, Bianchon becomes captivated by the house and often sneaks into the garden, allowing his imagination to conjure stories of its former glory. One evening, after a haunting visit, he meets Monsieur Regnault, a local notary, who reveals that the house belongs to the estate of the late Comtesse de Merret. Regnault explains that her will forbids anyone from entering or repairing the house for fifty years after her death, adding to its enigma.

Regnault recounts the Comtesse's tragic story. Following the death of her husband, the Count de Merret, she became increasingly reclusive and eccentric, leaving La Grande Bretèche in disarray. Rumors about her mental state circulated. On her deathbed, she summoned Regnault to witness her will, intending to leave her estate to the hospital in Vendôme, with strict conditions regarding La Grande Bretèche.

The will's peculiar stipulations intrigue Bianchon. Regnault, a former clerk turned notary, describes the Comtesse's decline marked by isolation and illness. Her last moments were filled with urgency to secure her wishes, culminating in her death shortly after finalizing her will.

As Bianchon reflects on the Comtesse's life and La Grande Bretèche's fate, he becomes engrossed in local gossip about the couple. His landlady, Madame Lepas, reveals more about the Comtesse and her husband, depicting a once-happy couple whose relationship soured. The Count was known for his temper, while the Comtesse was admired for her beauty and wealth.

Madame Lepas also shares a secret involving a young Spanish prisoner who stayed at her inn during the war. This handsome young man became a mysterious figure in town, often seen near the Comtesse's chapel, hinting at possible connections to her tragic fate.

The narrative intertwines themes of love, loss, and the passage of time, as Bianchon grapples with the haunting legacy of La Grande Bretèche. The house stands as a testament to the complexities of human relationships and the secrets that linger long after people have gone, prompting readers to ponder the nature of memory and the shadows of the past that shape the present.

################################################
In the narrative, a woman recounts the mysterious disappearance of Count Férédia, a Spanish Grandee living in her house. After he fails to return one night, she discovers a letter and a significant sum of money and diamonds he left behind, intended for masses of gratitude for his escape. Her husband searches for him and finds the Count's clothes near the river, leading them to believe he escaped rather than drowned.

The story darkens as the woman suspects the Count's involvement in the death of Madame de Merret, whose crucifix resembled one owned by the Count. She struggles with the moral dilemma of keeping the Count's money, which she feels is rightfully hers.

Intrigued by Rosalie, the maid who seems to know secrets about Madame de Merret's death, the narrator pursues her for the truth. Rosalie reveals that Madame de Merret had been ill, and her husband, Monsieur de Merret, was unaware of her condition until he returned home late one night.

On that fateful evening, suspecting something was wrong, Monsieur de Merret decided to check on his wife instead of trusting Rosalie. He confronted Madame de Merret, who denied anyone being in the cupboard despite his suspicions. In a tense moment, she swore on a crucifix that no one was there, leading to a tragic outcome.

Believing in his wife's purity, Monsieur de Merret chose not to open the cupboard but devised a plan to wall it up, enlisting mason Gorenflot. As the wall was built, Madame de Merret attempted to signal to the man inside, indicating hope for his escape.

After the wall was completed, Monsieur de Merret confined his wife to her room, refusing to let her leave or intercede for the man she sought to save. He remained with her for twenty days, reminding her of her oath, leaving her in despair.

The story concludes with the women at the table feeling the weight of the tale, reflecting on the moral complexities and emotional turmoil that unfolded within the walls of La Grande Bretèche.
