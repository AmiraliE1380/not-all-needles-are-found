“Ah! madame,” replied the doctor, “I have some appalling stories in my collection. But each one has its proper hour in a conversation—you know Chamfort’s jest to the Duc de Fronsac: ‘Between your sally and the present moment lie ten bottles of champagne.’”

“But it is two in the morning, and the story of Rosina has prepared us,” said the mistress of the house.

“Tell us, Monsieur Bianchon!” cried everyone.

The obliging doctor bowed, and silence fell.

“At about a hundred paces from Vendôme, on the banks of the Loir,” he began, “stands an old brown house with high roofs, completely isolated—nothing near it, not even a tannery or tavern. In front of the house a garden slopes to the river; once-clipped box shrubs now run wild. Willows rooted in the stream form a screen that half-hides the house. Wild plants clothed the bank; the fruit-trees, neglected for ten years, no longer bear, their suckers forming a thicket; the espaliers are a copse. Former gravel paths are overgrown; in fact, there is no path left. An arbor, or rather its wreck, and a table remain. Looking from the hilltop at the ruins of the Dukes of Vendôme, one feels this must once have been the delight of some country gentleman devoted to roses and choice fruit. On one wall there is a sundial with the homely motto, ‘Ultimam cogita.’

“The roof is dreadfully dilapidated; the shutters are always closed; balconies are hung with swallows’ nests; the doors forever shut. Grasses green the flagstones of the steps; the ironwork is rusty. Moon and sun, winter and snow have eaten into the wood, warped the boards, peeled off the paint. The silence is broken only by birds, cats, polecats, rats, and mice, free to scamper and fight. An invisible hand has written over it all: ‘Mystery.’

“If curiosity leads you to look from the street, you see a large gate pierced by children’s holes. I learned later that this door had been blocked for ten years. Through the breaches you see the courtyard in the same state: ruin. Tufts of weeds outline the paving; the walls are scored with cracks and blackened; the coping is laced with pellitory. The stone steps are disjointed; the bell-cord rotten; the gutter-spouts broken. What fire or curse has fallen here? Reptiles crawl but give no answer. This empty house is a vast enigma known to none.

“It was formerly a little domain, held in fief, known as La Grande Bretèche. During my stay at Vendôme, where Despleins had left me with a rich patient, the sight of this strange dwelling became one of my keenest pleasures. Was it not better than a ruin? Certain memories attach to a ruin; but this house, still standing though slowly destroyed, contained a secret, an unrevealed thought. At least, it testified to a caprice. More than once in the evening I climbed the hedge surrounding the enclosure. I braved scratches, entered this ownerless garden that was neither public nor private; I lingered there for hours, gazing at the disorder. I would not, at the price of the story to which this scene owed itself, have asked a single question of any gossiping native. On that spot I wove romances and indulged in a melancholy that enchanted me. Had I known the reason—perhaps commonplace—for this neglect, I should have lost the unwritten poetry. To me this refuge represented many phases of human life shadowed by misfortune: the peace of a cemetery without the dead, a house of lepers, the Atridae; above all, provincial life with its contemplative, hourglass existence. I often wept there; I never laughed.

“Often involuntary terrors seized me when I heard overhead the dull hum of a pigeon’s wings. The earth is dank; you must beware lizards, vipers, and frogs; and do not fear cold, for in minutes an icy cloak settles on your shoulders, like the Commendatore’s hand on Don Juan’s neck.

“One evening I felt a shudder; the wind had turned an old rusty weathercock, and the creak sounded like a cry from the house, just as I was finishing a gloomy drama to account for this monumental woe. I returned to my inn lost in gloomy thoughts. After supper the hostess entered my room with an air of mystery, and said, ‘Monsieur, here is Monsieur Regnault.’

“‘Who is Monsieur Regnault?’

“‘What, sir, do you not know Monsieur Regnault?—Well, that’s odd,’ she said, and left the room.

“A man appeared, tall and slim, dressed in black, hat in hand, with a receding forehead and a colorless face. He had the air of an usher, wearing an old coat but a diamond in his frill and gold rings in his ears.

“‘Monsieur,’ said I, ‘whom have I the honor of addressing?’

“He took a chair before my fire, put his hat on my table, and, rubbing his hands, answered: ‘Dear me, it is very cold.—Monsieur, I am Monsieur Regnault.’

“I encouraged him to speak.

“‘I am notary at Vendôme,’ he continued.

“‘I am delighted to hear it, monsieur,’ I exclaimed. ‘But I am not in a position to make a will for reasons best known to myself.’

“‘One moment!’ said he, holding up a hand as for silence. ‘Allow me, monsieur, allow me! I am informed you sometimes walk in the garden of La Grande Bretèche.’

“‘Yes, monsieur.’

“‘That constitutes a misdemeanor. Monsieur, as executor under the will of the late Comtesse de Merret, I come in her name to beg you to discontinue the practice. One moment! I am not a Turk, and do not wish to make a crime of it. And besides, you are free to be ignorant of the circumstances which compel me to leave the finest mansion in Vendôme to fall into ruin. Nevertheless, monsieur, you must be a man of education, and you should know that the laws forbid, under heavy penalties, any trespass on enclosed property. A hedge is the same as a wall. But the state in which the place is left may excuse your curiosity. For my part, I should be content to make you free to come and go in the house; but being bound to respect the will of the testatrix, I have the honor, monsieur, to beg that you will go into the garden no more. I myself, monsieur, since the will was read, have never set foot in the house, which, as I had the honor of informing you, is part of the estate of the late Madame de Merret. We have done nothing there but verify the number of doors and windows to assess the taxes I must pay annually out of funds left for that purpose by the late Madame de Merret. Ah! my dear sir, her will made a great commotion in the town.’

“The good man paused to blow his nose. I respected his volubility, perfectly understanding that the administration of Madame de Merret’s estate had been the most important event of his life—his reputation, his glory. As I was forced to bid farewell to my reveries and romances, I was to reject learning the truth on official authority.

“‘Monsieur,’ said I, ‘would it be indiscreet if I asked you the reasons for such eccentricity?’

“At these words an expression of pleasure, such as men feel who ride a hobby, overspread the lawyer’s countenance. He pulled up his collar, opened his snuffbox, offered me a pinch; on my refusing, he took a large one. He was happy. A hobby is the happy medium between a passion and a monomania. At that moment I understood Sterne’s charm and had an idea of the delight with which my uncle Toby bestrode his hobby-horse.

“‘Monsieur,’ said Monsieur Regnault, ‘I was head-clerk in Monsieur Roguin’s office in Paris. A first-rate house, made famous by an unfortunate bankruptcy.—Not having money to purchase a practice in Paris at the price asked in 1816, I came here and bought my predecessor’s business. I had relations in Vendôme; among others, a wealthy aunt, who allowed me to marry her daughter.—Monsieur,’ he paused, ‘three months after being licensed by the Keeper of the Seals, one evening, as I was going to bed—it was before my marriage—I was sent for by Madame la Comtesse de Merret, to her Château of Merret. Her maid, a good girl who is now a servant in this inn, was waiting at my door with the Countess’ carriage. Ah! one moment! I ought to tell you that Monsieur le Comte de Merret had gone to Paris to die two months before I came here. He came to a miserable end, flinging himself into dissipation. You understand?

“‘On the day he left, Madame la Comtesse had quitted La Grande Bretèche, having dismantled it. Some people say she burnt all the furniture, hangings—in short, all the chattels. Have you been to Merret, monsieur?—No,’ he answered himself; ‘Ah, it is a very fine place.’

“‘For about three months previously,’ he went on with a jerk of the head, ‘the Count and Countess had lived very eccentrically; they admitted no visitors; Madame lived on the ground floor, Monsieur on the first. When the Countess was left alone, she was seen only at church, and at home refused to see friends who called. She was already much altered when she left La Grande Bretèche to go to Merret. That dear lady—I say dear for she gave me this diamond though I saw her but once—was very ill; she had, no doubt, given up hope, for she died without sending for a doctor; many thought she was not quite right in her head. My curiosity was strangely excited by being called to her. That night all the town knew I was going to Merret.

“‘The waiting-woman vaguely answered my questions; she told me her mistress had received the Sacrament that day from the Curé of Merret and seemed unlikely to live through the night. About eleven I reached the chateau. I crossed large, lofty, dark rooms, diabolically cold and damp, and reached the state bedroom where the Countess lay. From rumors I had imagined her a coquette. Imagine my difficulty in seeing her in the great bed where she was lying. To light that enormous dusty room she had only an old Argand lamp. The bed was old with a high tester hung with chintz. A small table stood by it, on which I saw an ‘Imitation of Christ,’ which I bought for my wife, and the lamp. There was a deep armchair for her maid and two small chairs. There was no fire. That was all the furniture, hardly worth an inventory.

“‘If you had seen that vast brown-papered room you would have been transported into a romance. It was icy, funereal,’ and he paused with a theatrical gesture.

“‘As I approached the bed I saw Madame de Merret under the lamp. Her face was as yellow as wax, as narrow as two folded hands. She had a lace cap showing abundant hair, white as linen thread. She sat up and seemed to keep upright with great difficulty. Her large black eyes, dimmed by fever and half-dead, hardly moved under the bony arc of her brows.—There,’ he added, pointing to his own brow. ‘Her forehead was clammy; her fleshless hands were like bones covered with skin; veins and muscles were visible. She must have been very handsome; but then she startled me into indescribable emotion. Never, said those who wrapped her, had any living creature been so emaciated and yet alive. It was awful! Sickness so consumed her that she was a phantom. Her pale violet lips seemed not to move when she spoke.

“‘Though my profession has familiarized me with deathbeds, families in tears and agonies were as nothing compared to this lonely silent woman in her vast chateau. I heard no sound; I did not perceive the movement breathing should have given the sheets, and I stood absorbed in stupor. At last her large eyes moved; she tried to raise her right hand but it fell back, and she uttered these words, like a breath, for her voice was no longer a voice: “I have waited for you with the greatest impatience.” A bright flush rose to her cheeks. It was a great effort.

“‘“Madame,” I began.’ She signed to me to be silent. The old housekeeper rose and whispered, ‘Do not speak; Madame la Comtesse is not in a state to bear the slightest noise, and what you say might agitate her.’

“‘I sat down. A few moments after, Madame de Merret gathered strength to slip her right hand, with difficulty, under the bolster; she paused, and with a last effort withdrew a sealed paper, drops of perspiration rolling from her brow. “I place my will in your hands—Oh! God! Oh!” and she clutched a crucifix, lifted it to her lips, and died.

“‘The expression of her eyes still makes me shudder. She must have suffered much! There was joy in her last glance, and it remained stamped on her dead eyes.

“‘I brought away the will, and when opened I found Madame de Merret had appointed me her executor. She left the whole of her property to the hospital at Vendôme except a few legacies. But these were her instructions as relating to La Grande Bretèche: she ordered that the place, for fifty years from her death, be left in the state in which it might be at that time, forbidding anyone to enter the apartments, prohibiting any repairs, and even providing a salary for watchmen if needful to secure strict fulfilment. At the expiration of that term, if the will had been duly carried out, the house was to become my heirs’ property, for a notary cannot take a bequest. Otherwise La Grande Bretèche reverts to the heirs-at-law, but only on condition of fulfilling certain conditions set forth in a codicil not to be opened till the fifty years had passed. The will has not been disputed, so—’ He left the sentence unfinished, looking at me with triumph; I congratulated him.

“‘Monsieur,’ I said, ‘you have impressed me so vividly that I fancy I see the dying woman whiter than her sheets; her glittering eyes frighten me; I shall dream of her to-night.—But you must have formed some idea as to the instructions contained in that extraordinary will.’

“‘Monsieur,’ said he with comical reticence, ‘I never allow myself to criticise the conduct of a person who honors me with the gift of a diamond.’

“He soon loosened his tongue and communicated, with long digressions, the opinions of the deep politicians of both sexes whose judgments are law in Vendôme. These were contradictory and diffuse, and I nearly fell asleep despite my interest. The notary’s ponderous voice and monotonous accent, accustomed to listen to itself, were too much. Happily, he soon left.

“‘Ah, ha, monsieur,’ he called on the stairs, ‘a good many persons would be glad to live five-and-forty years longer; but—one moment!’ and he laid a finger to his nostril with a cunning look, as much as to say, “Mark my words!—To last as long as that—as long as that,” said he, “you must not be past sixty now.”’

“I closed my door, roused by the last speech, which the notary thought very funny; then I sat down with my feet on the fire-dogs. I had lost myself in a romance à la Radcliffe, built on Monsieur Regnault’s juridical base, when the door, opened by a woman’s cautious hand, turned on the hinges. My landlady came in, a buxom, florid dame, always good-humored, a Fleming who ought to have been painted by Teniers.

“‘Well, monsieur,’ said she, ‘Monsieur Regnault has no doubt been giving you his history of La Grande Bretèche?’

“‘Yes, Madame Lepas.’

“‘And what did he tell you?’

“I repeated briefly the creepy and sinister story of Madame de Merret. At each sentence my hostess leaned forward, scrutinizing me with an innkeeper’s keen eye—a compromise of constable, spy, and dealer.

“‘My good Madame Lepas,’ said I as I ended, ‘you seem to know more about it. Heh? If not, why have you come up to me?’

“‘On my word, as an honest woman——’

“‘Do not swear; your eyes are big with a secret. You knew Monsieur de Merret; what sort of man was he?’

“‘Monsieur de Merret—well, you see he was a man you never could see the top of, he was so tall! A very good gentleman, from Picardy, with his head close to his cap. He paid for everything down, so as never to have difficulties. He was hot-tempered, you see! All our ladies liked him very much.’

“‘And were they happy together?’

“‘Hm, hm! so-so—so far as can be guessed, for we of the common sort were not intimate with them.—Madame de Merret was kind and pleasant, who no doubt sometimes put up with her husband’s tantrums. But though he was haughty, we were fond of him. After all, it was his place to behave so. When a man is a born nobleman, you see——’

“‘Still, there must have been some catastrophe for them to part so violently?’

“‘I did not say there was any catastrophe, sir. I know nothing about it.’

“‘Indeed. Well, now, I am sure you know everything.’

“‘Well, sir, I will tell you the whole story.—When the Emperor sent Spaniards here, prisoners and others, I was required to lodge at the Government’s charge a young Spaniard sent to Vendôme on parole. Notwithstanding his parole, he had to show himself every day to the sub-prefect. He was a Spanish grandee—neither more nor less. He had a name in os and dia, something like Bagos de Férédia. I wrote his name in my books, and you may see it if you like. Ah! he was a handsome young fellow for a Spaniard, who are said to be ugly. He was not more than five feet two or three, but well made; and he had little hands he kept so beautifully! Ah! you should have seen them. He had as many brushes for his hands as a woman has for her toilet. He had thick black hair, a flame in his eye, a somewhat coppery complexion, which I admired. He wore the finest linen I have ever seen, though I have had princesses to lodge here, and, among others, General Bertrand, the Duc and Duchesse d’Abrantes, Monsieur Descazes, and the King of Spain. He did not eat much, but he had such polite and amiable ways that it was impossible to grudge him. Oh! I was very fond of him, though he did not say four words to me in a day; it was impossible to have talk with him; if he was spoken to, he did not answer; it is a way, a mania they all have, it would seem.

“‘He read his breviary like a priest, and went to mass and all the services quite regularly. And where did he post himself?—we found this out later.—Within two yards of Madame de Merret’s chapel. As he took that place the first time he entered the church, no one imagined a purpose. Besides, he never raised his nose above his book, poor young man! And then, monsieur, in the evening he walked on the hill among the ruins of the old castle. It was his only amusement, poor man; it reminded him of his native land. They say Spain is all hills!’

One evening, a few days after he had been sent here, he was out very late. I was uneasy when he did not come in until just on the stroke of midnight; but we all put up with his whims; he took the key of the door, and we never stayed up for him. He lived in a house of ours in the Rue des Casernes. One of our stable-boys told us that, going down to wash the horses in the river, he fancied he had seen the Spanish Grandee swimming a little way off, like a fish. When he came in, I told him to beware of the weeds, and he seemed put out at having been seen in the water.

At last, monsieur, one morning we did not find him in his room; he had not come back. Searching his things, I found a paper in his table-drawer and fifty doubloons, worth about five thousand francs, and in a little sealed box diamonds worth ten thousand francs. The paper said that if he did not return, he left us the money and diamonds in trust to found masses to thank God for his escape and salvation.

At that time my husband was still alive; he went off to look for the Spaniard. Here is the queer part: he brought back the Spaniard’s clothes, which he had found under a big stone on a breakwater along the river bank, nearly opposite la Grande Bretèche. My husband went early so no one saw him. After reading the letter he burnt the clothes, and, obeying Count Férédia’s wish, we announced that he had escaped.

The sub-prefect put the constabulary on his track; but, pshaw! he was never caught. Lepas believed the Spaniard had drowned himself. I have never thought so; I believe, on the contrary, that he had something to do with the business about Madame de Merret, for Rosalie told me that the crucifix her mistress was so fond of and buried with her was ebony and silver; now, at the beginning of his stay here Monsieur Férédia had one of ebony and silver which I never saw later.—And now, monsieur, do not tell me I ought not to keep the Spaniard’s fifteen thousand francs? Are they not really and truly mine?’

‘Certainly.—But have you never tried to question Rosalie?’ I asked.

‘Oh, to be sure I have, sir. But what is to be done? That girl is like a wall. She knows something, but it is impossible to make her talk.’

After chatting with my hostess, vague and sinister thoughts took me: romantic curiosity and a religious dread, like entering a dark church and discerning a feeble light under a lofty vault. La Grande Bretèche, with its rank grasses and shuttered windows, rose before me in vividness. I longed to enter that mysterious dwelling and search out the heart of this solemn story that had killed three persons.

Rosalie became, in my eyes, the most interesting person in Vendôme. Though healthy and blooming, she showed signs of an inward thought—something of ruth or of hope; her manner suggested a secret, like devout souls who pray too much or like a woman who forever hears her child’s last cry. Yet she was simple and clumsy; her vacant smile was not criminal. Seeing her, one would have called her innocent: the checked kerchief, the tight-laced bodice, the lilac- and white-striped gown. ‘No,’ thought I, ‘I will not leave Vendôme without the whole history of la Grande Bretèche. If necessary I will make love to Rosalie.’

One evening I addressed her.

‘Rosalie!’

‘Your servant, sir?’

‘You are not married?’ She started a little.

‘Oh! there is no lack of men if ever I take a fancy to be miserable!’ she laughed, recovering quickly.

‘You are fresh and good-looking enough not to lack lovers. But tell me, Rosalie, why did you become an inn-servant after leaving Madame de Merret? Did she not leave you an annuity?’

‘Oh yes, sir. But my place here is the best in Vendôme.’

This evasive answer confirmed my suspicion that Rosalie stood at the center of the affair. She held the last chapter of the romance; from that moment all my attention focused on her. By studying her, I found many good qualities: she was clean, neat, and soon possessed every charm desire can lend a woman. A fortnight after the notary’s visit, one night in the small hours, I said:

‘Come, tell me all you know about Madame de Merret.’

‘Oh!’ she said, ‘I will tell you; but keep the secret.’

‘All right, my child; I will keep all your secrets with a thief’s honor, the most loyal known.’

‘If it is all the same,’ said she, ‘I would rather it should be with your own.’

She arranged her head-kerchief and settled to tell the tale; confidence needs a proper hour. If I reproduced Rosalie’s diffuse eloquence exactly, a volume would scarce contain it. As her account stands midway between the notary’s gossip and Madame Lepas’s, I shall be brief.

Madame de Merret’s bedchamber at la Grande Bretèche was on the ground floor; a little built-in cupboard four feet deep served to hang her dresses. Three months before the events in question, Madame de Merret had been seriously ill, so much so that her husband slept on the first floor. By accident he came home two hours later than usual from the club. The invasion of France had stirred a violent debate; the billiards game had been keen; he had lost forty francs, a large sum in thrifty Vendôme.

For some time Monsieur de Merret had been content to ask Rosalie whether his wife was in bed; on her always answering yes, he would go to his own room. But this evening, struck by the charm of his wife’s appearance at dinner and thinking her convalescent, he resolved to go and tell her of his ill-luck and perhaps find consolation. Instead of calling Rosalie, who was in the kitchen watching the cook and the coachman play cards, he went to his wife’s room with a lantern set on the lowest stair. His step rang under the vaulted passage.

As he turned the key he fancied he heard the cupboard door close; when he entered, Madame de Merret stood alone before the fireplace. The unsuspecting husband thought Rosalie might be in the closet; yet a doubt rang in his ears. He read in his wife’s eyes an anxious, haunted expression.

‘You are very late,’ she said; her voice, usually clear and sweet, was slightly husky.

Monsieur de Merret made no answer. Rosalie then entered like a thunder-clap. The husband walked up and down, arms folded.

‘Have you had bad news, or are you ill?’ his wife asked timidly while Rosalie helped her undress. He made no reply.

‘You can go, Rosalie,’ said Madame de Merret; ‘I can put in my curl-papers myself.’ She wished to be alone. Rosalie lingered in the passage. Monsieur de Merret came and stood before his wife and said coldly, ‘Madame, there is some one in your cupboard!’

She looked at him calmly. ‘No, monsieur,’ she answered.

This ‘No’ wrung him; he did not believe it, yet she had never appeared purer than at that moment. He rose to open the closet. Madame de Merret took his hand, stopped him, and said in a voice of strange emotion, ‘Remember, if you should find no one there, everything must be at an end between you and me.’

Her dignity moved him and inspired a stern resolve.

‘No, Josephine,’ he said, ‘I will not open it. In either event we shall be parted for ever. Listen; I know all the purity of your soul, and you would not commit a deadly sin to save your life.’ At these words she looked at him with a haggard stare. ‘See, here is your crucifix,’ he went on, and hung an ebony-and-silver crucifix on its nail. ‘Swear to me before God that there is no one in that closet.’ She repeated the oath without flinching.

‘That will do,’ he said coldly. After a moment, examining the crucifix, he asked about it. She replied she had found it at Duvivier’s, bought from a Spanish monk when a troop of Spanish prisoners passed through Vendôme.

He rang the bell and had to wait for Rosalie. Taking Rosalie aside, he spoke in an undertone.

‘I know Gorenflot wants to marry you, that poverty alone prevents your setting up house, and that you told him you would not until he became a master mason. Go and fetch him; tell him to come with his trowel and tools. Wake no one but him. His reward will exceed his wishes. Above all, go out without saying a word—or else!’ and he frowned.

Rosalie was going, and he called her back. ‘Here, take my latch-key,’ he said.

‘Jean!’ Monsieur de Merret called in a voice of thunder. Jean, coachman and confidential servant, left his cards.

‘Go to bed, all of you,’ said his master, beckoning Jean close, and added in a whisper, ‘When they are all asleep—mind, asleep—you understand?—come down and tell me.’

Monsieur de Merret, who had not taken his eyes off his wife, returned to the fireside and talked of the club and billiards. When Rosalie returned she found Monsieur and Madame conversing amiably.

Not long before this, Monsieur de Merret had had new ceilings made to the reception rooms and had bought more plaster than needed. Plaster is costly at Vendôme, and he could always sell the surplus. This circumstance suggested his plan.

‘Gorenflot is here, sir,’ whispered Rosalie.

‘Tell him to come in,’ said her master aloud.

Madame de Merret turned paler at the sight of the mason.

‘Gorenflot,’ said her husband, ‘go and fetch some bricks from the coach-house; bring enough to wall up the door of this cupboard; you can use the plaster that is left for cement.’ Then, drawing Rosalie and the workman close—‘Listen, Gorenflot,’ said he low, ‘you are to sleep here to-night; tomorrow morning you shall have a passport to go abroad to a place I will tell you. I will give you six thousand francs for your journey. You must remain ten years in that country; if you dislike it you may settle elsewhere, but it must be the same country. Go through Paris and wait there until I join you. I will there give you an agreement for six thousand francs more, to be paid on your return, provided you have kept perfect silence. For that price you are to keep perfect silence as to what you do this night. To you, Rosalie, I will secure ten thousand francs, payable on your wedding day on condition of marrying Gorenflot; but to be married you must be silent. If not, no wedding gift!’

‘Rosalie,’ said Madame de Merret, ‘come and brush my hair.’

Her husband paced the room, watching the mason and his wife with no rude display. Gorenflot made some noise while working. Madame de Merret seized a moment when he was unloading bricks, and while her husband was at the other end of the room, said to Rosalie: ‘My dear child, I will give you a thousand francs a year if you will tell Gorenflot to leave a crack at the bottom.’ Then aloud, quite coolly: ‘You had better help him.’

Monsieur and Madame de Merret maintained a silence while Gorenflot walled up the door. The silence was intentional on the husband’s part; he would not give his wife the chance to say anything ambiguous. When the wall was half built the cunning mason, taking advantage of the master’s back being turned, broke one of the top panes with his pick. By this Madame de Merret understood Rosalie had spoken to him. All three then saw the face of a dark, gloomy man with black hair and flaming eyes.

Before her husband turned again the poor woman nodded to the stranger—the signal meant ‘Hope.’

At four o’clock, as day dawned in September, the work was done. The mason was placed in charge of Jean, and Monsieur de Merret slept in his wife’s room.

Next morning, as he rose, he said casually, ‘Oh, by the way, I must go to the Mairie for the passport.’ He put on his hat, took two or three steps, paused, and took the crucifix. His wife trembled with joy.

‘He will go to Duvivier’s,’ she thought.

As soon as he had left, Madame de Merret rang for Rosalie and cried in a terrible voice, ‘The pick! Bring the pick! and set to work. I saw how Gorenflot did it yesterday; we shall have time to make a gap and build it up again.’

Rosalie brought a sort of cleaver; Madame de Merret, with vehemence, set to demolish the wall. She had already got out a few bricks when, turning to strike a stronger blow, she saw Monsieur de Merret behind her. She fainted away.

‘Lay Madame on her bed,’ said he coldly.

Anticipating what would happen in his absence, he had laid this trap; he had only written to the Mairie and sent for Duvivier. The jeweler arrived just as the disorder in the room had been repaired.

‘Duvivier,’ asked Monsieur de Merret, ‘did you buy some crucifixes of the Spaniards who passed through town?’

‘No, monsieur.’

‘Very good; thank you,’ said he, flashing a tiger’s glare at his wife. ‘Jean,’ he added to his confidential valet, ‘you can serve my meals here in Madame de Merret’s room. She is ill, and I shall not leave her till she recovers.’

The cruel man remained in his wife’s room for twenty days. During the early time, when there was some little noise in the closet and Josephine wanted to intercede for the dying man, he said, without allowing her to speak, ‘You swore on the Cross that there was no one there.’

After this story all the ladies rose from table, and thus the spell under which Bianchon had held them was broken. Some of them had almost shivered at his last words.
