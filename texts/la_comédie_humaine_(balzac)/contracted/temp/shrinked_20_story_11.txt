“Ah! madame,” replied the doctor, “I have appalling stories in my collection. But each has its hour in conversation—you know Chamfort’s jest to the Duc de Fronsac: ‘Between your sally and the present moment lie ten bottles of champagne.’”

“But it is two in the morning, and the story of Rosina has prepared us,” said the mistress of the house.

“Tell us, Monsieur Bianchon!” cried everyone.

The doctor bowed, and silence fell.

“At about a hundred paces from Vendôme, on the banks of the Loir,” he began, “stands an old brown house, crowned with very high roofs, so isolated that there is nothing near it, not even a tannery or tavern. In front is a garden down to the river; box shrubs once edged the walks but now straggle. Willows rooted in the stream half hide the house. Wild plants clothe the bank; fruit trees, neglected for ten years, no longer bear. Espaliers have turned to copse; the once-graveled paths are overgrown—there is no trace of a path.

“From the hilltop where the ruins of the old castle cling, one sees this spot must once have been the joy of a gentleman devoted to horticulture. An arbor wreck with a table still standing remains. The garden evokes provincial peace, like reading a worthy tradesman’s epitaph. One wall bears a sundial with the motto, ‘Ultimam cogita.’

“The roof is dreadfully dilapidated; shutters are always closed; balconies are hung with swallows’ nests; the doors forever shut. Grasses outline flagstones; ironwork is rusty; moon and sun, winter and snow have eaten into the wood, warped the boards, peeled the paint. The silence is broken only by birds, cats, polecats, rats, and mice. An invisible hand has written over it all: ‘Mystery.’

“If you look from the street, you see a large gate with a round arch full of holes made by children. I later learned this door had been blocked for ten years. Through the breaches one sees the courtyard equally in ruin: weeds in the paving-stones; walls scored by cracks; blackened coping laced with pellitory. Stone steps are disjointed; the bell-cord rotten; the gutter-spouts broken. What fire from heaven could have fallen here? By what decree has salt been sown on this dwelling? These questions are left unanswered. This empty house is a vast enigma.

“It was formerly a small domain, La Grande Bretèche. During my stay at Vendôme, where Despleins had left me in charge of a rich patient, the sight of this strange dwelling became one of my keenest pleasures. Was it not better than a ruin? A ruin keeps memories; this house, standing though slowly destroyed, contained a secret, an unrevealed thought. More than once in the evening I climbed the wild hedge, braving scratches to enter the ownerless garden, lingering for hours in its disorder. I would not have traded the unwritten story that intoxicated me. There I wove romances and indulged in melancholy. Had I known the reason for the neglect, I would have lost the poetry that enchanted me. To me, this refuge represented phases of human life shadowed by misfortune; sometimes the peace of a graveyard without the dead; another day, the home of lepers; sometimes the house of the Atridae; but above all provincial life, contemplative and hour-glass-like. I often wept there; I never laughed.

“More than once involuntary terrors seized me at the dull hum of a hurrying wood-pigeon. The earth is dank; you must watch for lizards, vipers, and frogs wandering free; above all, you must fear cold, for in a few moments an icy cloak settles on your shoulders.

“One evening I felt a shudder; the wind had turned an old rusty weathercock, and the creak sounded like a cry from the house, just as I was finishing a gloomy drama to explain this monument of woe. I returned to my inn, lost in gloomy thoughts. After supper, the hostess came into my room with an air of mystery, saying, ‘Monsieur, here is Monsieur Regnault.’

“‘Who is Monsieur Regnault?’

“‘What, sir, do you not know Monsieur Regnault?—Well, that’s odd,’ she said, and left.

“A tall, slim man dressed in black appeared, hat in hand. He had a receding forehead, a small pointed head, and a colorless face like dirty water. You would have taken him for an usher. His coat was old, worn at the seams, but he wore a diamond in his shirt frill and gold rings in his ears.

“‘Monsieur,’ I said, ‘whom have I the honor of addressing?’ He sat, put his hat on the table, rubbed his hands, and answered, ‘Dear me, it is very cold.—Monsieur, I am Monsieur Regnault.’

“I encouraged myself with the thought: Seek!

“‘I am,’ he continued, ‘notary at Vendôme.’

“‘I am delighted,’ I exclaimed. ‘But I am not in a position to make a will for reasons best known to myself.’

“‘One moment!’ he said, holding up his hand. ‘Allow me. I am informed that you sometimes walk in the garden of La Grande Bretèche.’

“‘Yes, monsieur.’

“‘One moment! That constitutes a misdemeanor. As executor under the will of the late Comtesse de Merret, I beg you to discontinue the practice. I am not a Turk and do not wish to make a crime of it. You are free to be ignorant of the circumstances which compel me to leave a fine mansion to fall into ruin. You must be a man of education and should know the laws forbid, under heavy penalties, trespass on enclosed property. A hedge is the same as a wall. The state in which the place is left may excuse your curiosity. For my part, I should be content to make you free to come and go; but being bound to respect the will of the testatrix, I beg you will go into the garden no more. I myself have never set foot in the house since the will was read. We have done nothing there but verify doors and windows to assess the taxes I must pay. Ah! her will made a great commotion in the town.’

“The good man paused to blow his nose. I respected his volubility; the administration of Madame de Merret’s estate had been the important event of his life, his reputation, his glory. As I was forced to bid farewell to my reveries, I prepared to learn the truth on official authority.

“‘Monsieur,’ I said, ‘would it be indiscreet to ask the reasons for such eccentricity?’

“At these words an expression of pleasure spread across the lawyer’s countenance. He pulled up his collar, took out his snuffbox, offered me a pinch; on my refusing, he took a large one. He was happy.

“‘Monsieur,’ said Monsieur Regnault, ‘I was head-clerk in Monsieur Roguin’s office in Paris. A first-rate house—famous for an unfortunate bankruptcy. Not having enough money to buy a Paris practice in 1816, I came here and bought my predecessor’s business. I had relations in Vendôme; a wealthy aunt allowed me to marry her daughter. Three months after being licensed, one evening, before my marriage, I was sent for by Madame la Comtesse de Merret to her Château of Merret. Her maid, a good girl who now serves in this inn, was waiting at my door with the Countess’ carriage. Ah! one moment! I ought to tell you Monsieur le Comte de Merret had gone to Paris to die two months before I came; he came to a miserable end, flinging himself into every kind of dissipation. You understand?

“‘On the day he left, Madame la Comtesse had quitted La Grande Bretèche, having dismantled it. Some say she burnt the furniture and hangings—in short, everything used in furnishing the premises now let by the said M.—In short, that she burnt everything in the meadow at Merret. Have you been to Merret?—No,’ he answered himself, ‘Ah, it is a very fine place.’

“‘For about three months previous the Count and Countess lived eccentrically; they admitted no visitors; Madame lived on the ground floor and Monsieur on the first floor. When the Countess was left alone she was never seen except at church. Subsequently, at the château she refused to see friends. She was already altered when she left La Grande Bretèche to go to Merret. That dear lady—I say dear, for it was she who gave me this diamond, though I saw her but once—was very ill; she had, no doubt, given up hope, for she died without summoning a doctor; indeed many fancied she was not quite right in her head. Well, sir, my curiosity was excited by hearing Madame de Merret had need of my services. That very night, though late, all the town knew I was going to Merret.

“‘The waiting-woman replied vaguely on the way; nevertheless she told me her mistress had received the Sacrament that day and seemed unlikely to live through the night. It was about eleven when I reached the château. I went up the great staircase. After crossing large, lofty, dark rooms, diabolically cold and damp, I reached the state bedroom where the Countess lay. From the rumors current I had imagined a coquette. Imagine, then, my difficulty in seeing her in the great bed where she lay. To light that enormous room, with old heavy cornices and thick dust that made one sneeze, she had only an old Argand lamp. Ah! but you have not been to Merret. The bed is of the old world, high tester hung with flowered chintz. A small table stood by the bed; I saw an ‘Imitation of Christ,’ which, by the way, I bought for my wife, as well as the lamp. There was a deep armchair for her confidential maid and two small chairs. There was no fire. That was all the furniture.

“‘My dear sir, if you had seen that vast room, papered and hung with brown, you would have felt transported into a scene of a romance. It was icy, nay funereal,’ and he lifted his hand with a theatrical gesture and paused.

“‘By dint of seeking, as I approached the bed, at last I saw Madame de Merret under the glimmer of the lamp, which fell on the pillows. Her face was as yellow as wax, and as narrow as two folded hands. The Countess had a lace cap showing abundant hair, but as white as linen thread. She sat up in bed and seemed to keep upright with difficulty. Her large black eyes, dimmed by fever, half-dead already, hardly moved under the bony arch of her eyebrows.—There,’ he added, pointing to his own brow. ‘Her forehead was clammy; her fleshless hands were bones covered with soft skin; veins and muscles visible. She must have been very handsome; but at that moment I was startled into an indescribable emotion. Never, said those who wrapped her in her shroud, had any living creature been so emaciated and lived. In short, it was awful to behold! Sickness so consumed that woman, she was no more than a phantom. Her lips, pale violet, seemed not to move when she spoke.

“‘Though my profession has familiarized me with such spectacles, families in tears and the agonies I have seen were as nothing compared with this lonely and silent woman in her vast château. I heard no sound; I did not perceive the movement which a breathing sufferer ought to give to the sheets, and I stood motionless, absorbed in looking at her in a sort of stupor. In fancy, I am there still. At last her large eyes moved; she tried to raise her right hand, but it fell back on the bed, and she uttered these words, which came like a breath, for her voice was no longer a voice: “I have waited for you with the greatest impatience.” A bright flush rose to her cheeks. It was a great effort to speak.

“‘“Madame,” I began.’ She signed to me to be silent. The old housekeeper rose and said in my ear, “Do not speak; Madame la Comtesse is not in a state to bear the slightest noise, and what you say might agitate her.”

“‘I sat down. A few instants later, Madame de Merret collected all her remaining strength, slipped her right hand under the bolster, and with a last effort withdrew a sealed paper, drops of perspiration on her brow. “I place my will in your hands—Oh! God! Oh!” and that was all. She clutched a crucifix, lifted it to her lips, and died.

“‘The expression of her eyes still makes me shudder. She must have suffered much! There was joy in her last glance, and it remained stamped on her dead eyes.

“‘I brought away the will, and when opened, found Madame de Merret had appointed me her executor. She left the whole of her property to the hospital at Vendôme except for a few legacies. But these were her instructions regarding La Grande Bretèche: She ordered me to leave the place, for fifty years from her death, in the state it was at her death, forbidding anyone, whoever he might be, to enter the apartments, prohibiting any repairs whatever, and even settling a salary to pay watchmen if necessary to secure absolute fulfillment of her intentions. At the expiration of that term, if her will has been duly carried out, the house is to become the property of my heirs, for, as you know, a notary cannot take a bequest. Otherwise, La Grande Bretèche reverts to the heirs-at-law, but on condition of fulfilling certain conditions set forth in a codicil to the will, which is not to be opened until the expiration of the fifty years. The will has not been disputed, so——’ And without finishing, the lanky notary looked at me with triumph; I congratulated him.

“‘Monsieur,’ I said in conclusion, ‘you have so vividly impressed me I fancy I see the dying woman whiter than her sheets; her glittering eyes frighten me; I shall dream of her tonight.—But you must have formed some idea as to the instructions contained in that extraordinary will.’

“‘Monsieur,’ said he, with comic reticence, ‘I never criticize the conduct of a person who honors me with the gift of a diamond.’

“Soon I loosened the tongue of the discreet notary, who communicated, not without long digressions, the opinions of the deep politicians of both sexes whose judgments are law in Vendôme. But these opinions were so contradictory and diffuse I was near falling asleep. Happily he went away.

“‘Ah, ha, monsieur,’ said he on the stairs, ‘a good many persons would be glad to live five-and-forty years longer; but—one moment!’ and he laid his first finger to his nostril with a cunning look, as much as to say, ‘Mark my words!—To last as long as that—you must not be past sixty now.’

“I closed my door, roused from apathy by this last speech, and sat with my feet on the fire-dogs. I had lost myself in a romance à la Radcliffe, constructed on the juridical base given me by Monsieur Regnault, when the door, opened by a woman’s cautious hand, turned on the hinges. My landlady entered, a buxom, florid dame, always good-humored, who had missed her calling in life. She was a Fleming, fit for a picture by Teniers.

“‘Well, monsieur,’ said she, ‘Monsieur Regnault has no doubt been giving you his history of La Grande Bretèche?’

“‘Yes, Madame Lepas.’

“‘And what did he tell you?’

“I repeated in a few words the creepy story. At each sentence my hostess leaned forward, looking at me with an innkeeper’s keen scrutiny, a happy compromise between a police constable, a spy, and a dealer.

“‘My good Madame Lepas,’ said I as I ended, ‘you seem to know more about it. If not, why have you come up to me?’

“‘On my word, as an honest woman——’

“‘Do not swear; your eyes are big with a secret. You knew Monsieur de Merret; what sort of man was he?’

“‘Monsieur de Merret—well, you see he was a man you never could see the top of, he was so tall! A very good gentleman from Picardy, who had, as we say, his head close to his cap. He paid for everything down, so as never to have difficulties. He was hot-tempered, you see! All our ladies liked him very much.’

“‘Because he was hot-tempered?’ I asked.

“‘Well, maybe,’ she said; ‘and you may suppose, sir, that a man had to have something to show for a figurehead before he could marry Madame de Merret, who, without any reflection on others, was the handsomest and richest heiress in our parts. She had about twenty thousand francs a year. All the town was at the wedding; the bride was pretty and sweet-looking, quite a gem of a woman. Oh, they were a handsome couple in their day!’

“‘And were they happy together?’

“‘Hm, hm! so-so—so far as can be guessed, for, as you may suppose, we of the common sort were not hail-fellow-well-met with them.—Madame de Merret was a kind woman and very pleasant, who had no doubt sometimes to put up with her husband’s tantrums. But though he was rather haughty, we were fond of him. After all, it was his place to behave so. When a man is a born nobleman, you see——’

“‘Still, there must have been some catastrophe for Monsieur and Madame de Merret to part so violently?’

“‘I did not say there was any catastrophe, sir. I know nothing about it.’

“‘Indeed. Well, now, I am sure you know everything.’

“‘Well, sir, I will tell you the whole story.—When the Emperor sent Spaniards here, prisoners of war and others, I was required to lodge at the charge of the Government a young Spaniard sent to Vendôme on parole. Notwithstanding his parole he had to show himself every day to the sub-prefect. He was a Spanish grandee—Bagos de Férédia, or something like that. I wrote his name in my books. Ah! he was a handsome young fellow for a Spaniard, who are all ugly they say. He was not more than five feet two or three in height, but so well made; and he had little hands that he kept so beautifully! Ah! you should have seen them. He had as many brushes for his hands as a woman has for her toilet. He had thick, black hair, a flame in his eye, a somewhat coppery complexion, but which I admired all the same. He wore the finest linen I have ever seen, though I have lodged princesses and generals. He did not eat much, but had such polite and amiable ways it was impossible to grudge him. Oh! I was very fond of him, though he did not say four words in a day, and it was impossible to talk with him; if spoken to, he did not answer; it is a way, a mania they all have, it would seem.

“‘He read his breviary like a priest and went to mass regularly. And where did he post himself?—we found this out later.—Within two yards of Madame de Merret’s chapel. As he took that place the first time he entered the church, no one imagined there was any purpose in it. Besides, he never raised his nose above his book, poor young man! And then, monsieur, of an evening he went for a walk on the hill among the ruins of the old castle. It was his only amusement, poor man; it reminded him of his native land. They say that Spain is all hills!

One evening shortly after his arrival he stayed out late. I was uneasy when he didn’t return until midnight, but we were used to his whims; he took the key and we didn’t wait for him. He lived in a house we owned on Rue des Casernes. One of our stable-boys thought he saw the Spanish grandee swimming in the river. When he returned, I warned him about the weeds; he seemed annoyed at being seen.

Eventually, one morning we discovered he hadn’t returned. Searching his belongings, I found a note in his drawer, fifty doubloons—about five thousand francs—and a sealed box of diamonds worth ten thousand francs. The note said that if he didn’t come back, he left the money and diamonds to fund masses in gratitude for his escape.

My husband went searching for him and came back with the Spaniard’s clothes, found under a stone on a breakwater near la Grande Bretèche. He went early so no one saw him. After reading the letter he burned the clothes and, following Count Férédia’s wishes, we announced that he had escaped.

The sub-prefect sent the constabulary after him, but he was never caught. Lepas believed the Spaniard had drowned. I suspected otherwise: I thought he had something to do with Madame de Merret’s affair. Rosalie had told me the crucifix her mistress cherished and was buried with was ebony and silver; early in Monsieur Férédia’s stay he had a similar crucifix that I never saw again. Now, monsieur, do you think I should regret the Spaniard’s fifteen thousand francs? Aren’t they truly mine?

“Certainly. But have you ever questioned Rosalie?” I asked.

“Oh, I have, sir. But what can be done? That girl is like a wall. She knows something, but it’s impossible to make her talk.”

After that brief conversation my hostess left me with vague sinister thoughts, a romantic curiosity, and a religious dread—as if entering a dark church and seeing a faint light under a high vault. La Grande Bretèche, with overgrown grass, shuttered windows, rusted ironwork, and deserted rooms, came vividly to mind. I felt compelled to explore that house to uncover the heart of this solemn story, this drama that had claimed three lives.

Rosalie became the most intriguing person in Vendôme to me. Watching her, I sensed an inner thought beneath her healthy appearance—something like sorrow or obstinate hope; her manner suggested a secret. Yet she was simple and clumsy; her vacant smile seemed innocent; one would deem her harmless seeing the checked kerchief over her sturdy figure and striped gown. I resolved not to leave Vendôme without knowing the full story of la Grande Bretèche. To achieve this I intended to press Rosalie.

“Rosalie!” I called one evening.

“Your servant, sir?”

“You’re not married?” She flinched.

“Oh! There’s no shortage of men if I ever fancy being miserable!” she laughed, recovering quickly; every woman, from the highest lady to the inn-servant, has presence of mind.

“Yes; you’re attractive enough to never lack suitors! But tell me, Rosalie, why did you become an inn-servant after leaving Madame de Merret? Didn’t she leave you a small annuity?”

“Oh yes, sir. But my position here is the best in all Vendôme.”

Her answer was evasive. Rosalie seemed central to the affair; she seemed tied to its core. It wasn’t ordinary courtship material; this girl contained the final chapter of a romance. From that moment my attention fixed on her. Studying her, I noticed many admirable qualities: cleanliness, neatness, undeniable attractiveness. A fortnight after the notary’s visit, one morning I said:

“Come, tell me all you know about Madame de Merret.”

“Oh!” she replied, “I will tell you, but keep it a secret.”

“Of course, my child; I will guard your secrets with a thief’s honor, the most loyal known.”

“If it’s all the same to you,” she said, “I’d prefer it be with your own.”

She adjusted her head-kerchief and settled to tell the tale; a particular attitude of confidence is necessary for storytelling. The best tales are told at a certain hour—just as we were then at table. If I were to reproduce Rosalie’s diffuse eloquence exactly, a whole volume would scarcely suffice. Between the notary’s gossip and Madame Lepas’s account, I will summarize it concisely.

The room at la Grande Bretèche where Madame de Merret slept was on the ground floor; a small cupboard in the wall, about four feet deep, served as her wardrobe. Three months before the night in question Madame de Merret had been seriously ill; her husband had begun sleeping on the first floor. One evening he came home two hours later than usual from the club, delayed by animated discussions and losses at billiards. He had earlier relied on Rosalie to tell him if his wife was in bed; her affirmations led him to his room, trusting her. This night he decided to check on Madame de Merret to share his misfortunes, perhaps seek comfort. On entering, he thought he heard the closet door shut, but found Madame de Merret standing by the fireplace. A nagging doubt struck him; her face was anxious.

“You’re very late,” she said, voice slightly husky.

Monsieur de Merret remained silent as Rosalie entered, which startled him. He began walking the room, arms crossed.

“Have you received bad news, or are you unwell?” his wife asked timidly while Rosalie helped her undress. He didn’t answer.

“You can go, Rosalie,” Madame de Merret said; “I can manage my curl-papers myself.” She wanted to be alone with him. Once Rosalie left—though she lingered in the passage—Monsieur de Merret confronted his wife, stating coldly, “Madame, there’s someone in your cupboard!”

She calmly replied, “No, monsieur.”

This “No” pierced Monsieur de Merret’s heart; he did not believe it, yet she seemed more saintly than ever. He moved toward the closet, but Madame de Merret took his hand and stopped him, saying with strange emotion, “Remember, if you find no one there, everything must end between us.”

Her extraordinary dignity filled him with deep respect and inspired a resolve that could be legendary on a larger stage.

“No, Josephine,” he said, “I won’t open it. Regardless of the outcome, we would be forever parted. I know your soul’s purity; you wouldn’t commit a deadly sin to save your life.” At these words Madame de Merret looked at him haggardly. “Here is your crucifix,” he continued. “Swear to me before God that there’s no one in that closet; I will believe you—I will never open that door.”

Madame de Merret took the crucifix and swore, “I swear it.”

“Louder,” he insisted; “repeat: ‘I swear before God that there is nobody in that closet.’” She repeated the words without flinching.

“That will do,” said Monsieur de Merret coldly. After a moment he remarked, “You have a fine piece of work there, which I’ve never seen before,” examining the ebony and silver crucifix.

“I found it at Duvivier’s; last year, when that troop of Spanish prisoners passed through Vendôme, he bought it from a Spanish monk.”

“Indeed,” said Monsieur de Merret, hanging the crucifix back on its nail, and he rang for Rosalie.

He waited for her to arrive. “Go to bed, all of you,” he instructed, beckoning Jean, his coachman and confidential servant, to come close. He whispered, “When they’re all asleep—mind, asleep—you understand?—come down and tell me.”

Monsieur de Merret kept his gaze on his wife while giving orders, returning to her at the fireside to discuss the billiards game and the club. When Rosalie returned, she found them conversing amiably.

Not long before, Monsieur de Merret had had new ceilings installed in all the reception rooms on the ground floor. Plaster was scarce in Vendôme, and he had ordered a considerable quantity, knowing he could sell any excess. This circumstance inspired his plan.

“Gorenflot is here, sir,” Rosalie whispered.

“Tell him to come in,” he replied aloud.

Madame de Merret paled at the mason’s appearance.

“Gorenflot,” said her husband, “go fetch some bricks from the coach-house; bring enough to wall up the cupboard door; you can use the leftover plaster as cement.” Then, pulling Rosalie and the mason close, he whispered, “Listen, Gorenflot, you’ll sleep here tonight; tomorrow morning I’ll give you a passport to go abroad to a place I’ll specify. I’ll give you six thousand francs for your journey. You must live in that town for ten years; if you don’t like it you may settle elsewhere, but it must be in the same country. Go through Paris and wait there till I join you. I’ll give you an agreement for six thousand francs more, to be paid upon your return, provided you fulfill the bargain. In return, you must keep silent about what you do tonight. Rosalie, I’ll secure ten thousand francs for you, payable at your wedding, provided you marry Gorenflot; but to marry you must remain silent. If not, no wedding gift!”

“Rosalie,” Madame de Merret said, “come and brush my hair.”

Her husband quietly paced the room, keeping an eye on the door, the mason, and his wife, without showing suspicion. Gorenflot made some noise; Madame de Merret seized a moment when he was unloading bricks to say to Rosalie: “My dear child, I’ll give you a thousand francs a year if you tell Gorenflot to leave a crack at the bottom.” Then she added casually, “You’d better help him.”

Monsieur and Madame de Merret remained silent while Gorenflot wall ed up the door. The silence was deliberate: he would not give his wife a chance to equivocate; she would not give him cause. When the wall was half finished the mason, seizing an opportunity with his master’s back turned, broke one of the two panes in the top of the door. Madame de Merret understood that Rosalie had spoken; they all saw the face of a dark, gloomy-looking man with black hair and fiery eyes.

Before her husband turned around, the poor woman nodded to the stranger, signaling “Hope.”

At four o’clock in September the work was completed. The mason was placed in charge of Jean, and Monsieur de Merret slept in his wife’s room.

Next morning he casually mentioned, “Oh, by the way, I must go to the Maire for the passport.” He donned his hat, took a few steps toward the door, paused, and grabbed the crucifix. His wife trembled with joy—she thought he would seek Duvivier.

As soon as he left, Madame de Merret rang for Rosalie and cried in a terrible voice, “The pick! Bring the pick! We must work. I saw how Gorenflot did it yesterday; we have time to make a gap and build it up again.”

In an instant Rosalie brought a cleaver; with intensity beyond words she began demolishing the wall. She had removed a few bricks when, turning to deliver a stronger blow, she saw Monsieur de Merret behind her. She fainted.

“Lay madame on her bed,” he said coldly.

He had foreseen what might happen in his absence and set a trap; he had merely written to the Maire and summoned Duvivier. The jeweler arrived just as the disorder was being repaired.

“Duvivier,” Monsieur de Merret asked, “didn’t you buy any crucifixes from the Spaniards who passed through town?”

“No, monsieur.”

“Very well; thank you,” he said, casting a fierce glare at his wife. “Jean,” he added, turning to his valet, “you can serve my meals here in Madame de Merret’s room. She is ill, and I won’t leave her until she recovers.”

The cruel man remained in his wife’s room for twenty days. During the early days, when there was noise in the closet and Josephine wanted to intercede for the dying man, he said, without allowing her to speak, “You swore on the Cross that there was no one there.”

After this story all the ladies rose from the table, breaking the spell Bianchon had cast over them. Some of them shivered at the last words.
