Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, a weathered old house helped historians reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls revealed timber patterns and cracked plaster; a warped triangular roof overhung the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer. The upper story’s overlapping planks lightened the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man, cloaked like an antiquary, stood across from the house studying its peculiarities. He ignored the narrow boarded windows of the first floor and inspected the third floor’s modest sash windows, crudely made and clouded with green-tinted glass, occasionally glancing at the shop below where small comic details amused him.

A large wooden beam, supported by four pillars, bore many paint layers and a whimsical painting of a cat playing rackets — cat, oversized racket, absurd ball. A faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a remnant of the shop’s old charm.

The young man, in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, looked as if from a wedding or ball; his white gloves and disheveled hair hinted at recent revelry. Despite market gardeners hurrying by, the street was unusually still. His striking features, a mix of passion and calm, drew shopkeepers’ attention; his pale face, dazzling cravat, and expressive eyes betrayed varied emotion.

Three apprentices then appeared at the dormer window, laughing and sprinkling him with shaving cream, which he brushed off indifferently. A young girl’s face—recalling a Raphael Virgin in its innocence—briefly appeared and fled as the shutter slammed, leaving him disappointed.

At that moment the “Cat and Racket” shutters opened and a manservant hung the cloth sign. Behind iron bars the shop revealed many packages wrapped in brown linen. Though old-fashioned, Monsieur Guillaume was reputed Paris’s best-stocked cloth merchant: honest, profitable without flattery, while fellow traders grumbled about his notary’s heavy discounts.

The old merchant stood at his shop threshold on the Rue Saint-Denis, awakening from a long sleep. Monsieur Guillaume, in traditional dress, watched a stranger loitering by the shop. His patient, shrewd face bore years of experience; a staunch traditionalist, he lamented lost customs and imposed a strict routine on his three apprentices, who feared his keen gaze.

When work began, Guillaume noticed the stranger’s anxious glances and suspected ulterior motives. The eldest apprentice, curious, stepped outside and thought he saw Mademoiselle Augustine hurrying away. He hailed a cab, averting a prank aimed at Augustine.

Guillaume reprimanded the youths for idleness, recalling how he would have worked faster in his youth. Though the apprentices came from comfortable homes and paid for their board, he enforced a rigorous work ethic, unlike newer shops where early comforts were expected. The shop was meticulously kept, reflecting his valuing of order and economy; the apprentices were even excluded from the family dessert under Madame Guillaume’s frugality.

The family observed routine: they attended Mass together, and the eldest apprentice, Joseph Lebas, after years of service, earned a modest wage. Guillaume considered Joseph a possible husband for his elder daughter Virginie, though Joseph secretly loved the younger Augustine.

Household life ran on strict tradition. Virginie was devout and severe; Augustine, eighteen, was delicate and charming but overshadowed by maternal expectations. Augustine secretly longed for a more passionate life, fed by romantic novels, which deepened Joseph’s affection; Virginie, lacking suitors, also felt drawn to him. Amid the shop’s monotony, quiet feelings grew, and Guillaume worried about his daughters’ prospects in changing times.

From that day the old merchant, saddened by his eldest daughter’s decline, recalled marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like those of Joseph Lebas and Virginie. Marrying off his daughter would repay a sacred debt to an orphan benefactor. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, aware of the fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder sister marry first, suffered in silence. Warm-hearted and reserved, he kept his love hidden.

A few months earlier a young painter paused outside the darkened Cat and Racket, transfixed by a scene any artist would envy. The shop, cave-like, revealed a dining room lit by a hanging lamp that evoked Dutch interiors. The family—Augustine among them, with a chubby maid—formed a peaceful, modest tableau. Fresh from Rome and weary of grand art, the painter craved genuine nature. Augustine, pensive and scarcely touched by food, seemed almost angelic in the lamp’s glow. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

Determined to capture the moment, he retreated to his studio and kept returning to the Cat and Racket, even disguising himself to study Augustine. For eight months he lived in love and art, neglecting friends and routine. One morning his friend Girodet broke his isolation to ask about the Salon submission. The artist revealed two masterpieces; Girodet, sensing the deep feeling, embraced him and suggested love had made the work great. Despite warnings that the public might not understand them, the paintings were exhibited and created a sensation. The Interior launched a new genre, and the portrait became unforgettable, winning acclaim and offers the artist declined.

These public successes contrasted with the monastic life in Rue Saint-Denis until Madame Guillaume mentioned the exhibition during a visit. With her cousin’s help, Augustine obtained permission to see the Louvre. Trembling before the portrait, she recognized herself and felt terror and excitement. The artist, nearby, whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Fleeing the crowd, Augustine tried to leave but was drawn back to the celebrated painting. Overwhelmed, she wept and, in a quiet gesture, exchanged a nod with the artist. That brief connection ignited guilt and exhilaration; the Salon’s chaos and the painter’s gaze intoxicated her, revealing unfamiliar emotions. She feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition with her cousin, dreading her mother’s planned visit. That night she felt a void in her humdrum life and longed for the elegance she had always admired; the idea of being a genius’s wife filled her with hope despite her upbringing. The next day her parents returned disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. The paintings’ disappearance, however, revealed to Augustine a woman’s instinctive sensitivity.

The morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had won Augustine's heart, found it hard to declare his love because her household constrained them. Augustine sat in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume, watched by her mother, while Theodore, imagining rivals among the assistants, felt hemmed in by the household’s eyes. He bribed a maid to arrange discreet exchanges; they agreed to meet during Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family concentrated on stock-taking under Monsieur Guillaume’s supervision, tallying inventory and balancing accounts, ignoring Augustine’s longing. When the labor ended, they relaxed: attended a performance of Cendrillon and the apprentices enjoyed a boisterous night out.

The next morning Guillaume resumed routine and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to review the profitable results, offering to make him a partner in recognition of his work. Overwhelmed, Joseph blurted that he loved Augustine; Guillaume at first misheard and thought he meant Virginie. Once clarified, Guillaume agreed to consider Joseph’s feelings and suggested he accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie exchanged glances at a nervous Joseph. Madame later chastised Augustine for inattentiveness in church and sent her to her room, prompting a heated discussion with Monsieur about their daughter’s future. Augustine confessed her love for Theodore, stressing his respectable background. Monsieur voiced concern about artists, recalling past experiences, but Augustine insisted Theodore was well-born and prosperous.

Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore’s honors and fortune and advocated the match; her glowing report swayed the parents. By evening mood shifted to acceptance: Guillaume informed Joseph, the family gathered to celebrate, and Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket as a symbol of shared happiness. Still cautious about money, Monsieur consented; the lovers found solace and the household embraced a joyful future.

A few months after that memorable Sunday, Saint-Leu’s high altar saw two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore shone with happiness while Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to have Virginie married first, but the priests attended the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s sensible match and envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned such extravagance would bring ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt vindicated in his cautious approach to settlements. After a splendid ball and supper, the Guillaumes returned home and the Lebas family resumed their old residence. The ecstatic artist carried Augustine to their richly adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful: Theodore lavished affection, and Augustine, lost in love, believed her devotion would always enchant him. Naïve at times, she remained happily unaware of much around her. As pregnancy approached, Theodore felt the pull of work. He reconnected with friends and began frequenting the Duchesse de Carigliano’s salons, enjoying the social proof of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine liked society but soon felt out of place among Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was sincere but superficial, and a growing intellectual disconnect appeared between them. Theodore hoped studio life would draw her in, but she could not adapt; at gatherings her innocence clashed with the freer manners of artists and her discomfort showed.

Gradually Theodore’s warmth cooled and Augustine keenly felt his indifference. Despite trying to fulfill her duties, she could not bridge the widening gap. Three years into marriage, the once radiant Augustine sank into sorrow as Theodore’s involvement with the Duchesse intensified her fear of being supplanted.

Determined, Augustine set out to educate herself in poetry and art, believing culture might reconnect them, but her efforts faltered. She could not produce the natural wit Theodore admired; earnest attempts only highlighted her inadequacy. Seeking solace, she returned to her childhood home, where Virginie and Joseph Lebas ran their drapery with steady, ordinary happiness. Joseph’s practical, commercial view offered no comfort.

Augustine ultimately returned to her husband’s home, burdened by uncertainty and longing for the love she had lost, a desperate patient trying every remedy to heal a fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter warmly; her visit was a refreshing break from their monotony. For four years they had drifted, absorbed in reminiscences of business successes and failures—most notably the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume habitually checked on his shop, the Cat and Racket, and chatted with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume—appointed to the Army’s clothing consulting board through her son-in-law’s influence—filled their home with extravagant, tasteless decor. Their nostalgic stagnation struck Augustine as a stark contrast to her own turbulent life, leaving her both proud and appalled at their vacuity.

Despite her inner turmoil, Augustine displayed her accomplishments and aired her marital grievances, which her parents received with keen interest. Madame Guillaume was especially curious about Augustine’s husband, Theodore, a painter. When Augustine described his late nights and eccentric habits, her mother disapproved of his lifestyle and profession. Augustine defended him, and the family’s incredulity escalated into a debate about genius versus marital responsibility.

At the mention of divorce, her parents were shocked; Monsieur Guillaume offered practical support. Overwhelmed by their concern and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine recoiled from separation, realizing she was isolated in a way only those who had endured similar trials could understand.

Determined to change course, Augustine sought the Duchess de Carigliano—not to force Theodore back but to learn how to captivate him. Approaching the Duchess’s opulent home, she felt both envy and admiration and sensed an undercurrent of rivalry. The Duchess listened sympathetically and counseled shrewdly: with men of genius, one must project strength and self-control, cultivate mystery, and never reveal the whole measure of one’s affection. Though uneasy about such manipulation, Augustine began to accept these lessons, convinced her love required a new approach.

The Duchess offered a portrait of Augustine painted by Theodore to rekindle his interest. Hopeful, Augustine returned determined to embody the image. Theodore’s reaction—fury and jealousy—led to a violent quarrel that shattered her hopes. The next morning her mother found Augustine in despair amid the wreckage. Overwhelmed by the pressures of her tumultuous life, Augustine died young. Her tragic end remains a poignant reminder of the struggle women face pursuing love and fulfillment when entangled with the complexities of artistic genius; the inscription on her grave reflects this sorrow.
