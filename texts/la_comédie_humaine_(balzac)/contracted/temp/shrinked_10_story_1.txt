**AT THE SIGN OF THE CAT AND RACKET**

**DEDICATION**

To Mademoiselle Marie de Montheau

---

Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, stood a charming old house that allowed historians to reconstruct old Paris. Its dilapidated walls bore marks resembling hieroglyphics, with beams quivering at the slightest movement. The triangular roof, warped by the weather, extended over the street to protect the entrance from rain and to shelter a loft window. The upper story was constructed of planks, overlapping like shingles, to avoid overburdening the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning, a young man, wrapped in a cloak, stood under a shop awning across from this house, studying it with the enthusiasm of an antiquary. The building presented various peculiarities; the first floor had four tall, narrow windows filled with boards to create a dubious light for sales. The young man seemed disdainful of this part, focusing instead on the third floor's modest sash windows, clumsily crafted and glazed with green glass. Occasionally, he glanced down at the shop below, where amusing details caught his eye.

A massive wooden beam, resting on four pillars, appeared to sag under the weight of the house, adorned with layers of paint. In the center, an old painting depicted a cat playing rackets, which amused the young man. The cat, holding a racket as large as itself, aimed at a ball returned by a man in an embroidered coat. Time had rendered the painting even more grotesque, with the cat's tail resembling a spectator due to its wear. The sign read “Guillaume, successor to Master Chevrel,” its gilding faded by the elements.

These whimsical signs, once common among Paris merchants, were remnants of a time when they used living creatures to attract customers. The young man, however, was not there to admire the cat. His attire—a cloak, white silk stockings, and a pair of smart shoes—suggested he had just come from a wedding or ball. His black hair, styled in the Caracalla fashion, flowed over his shoulders, and his anxious face was accentuated by a dazzling white cravat.

Despite the noise of market gardeners rushing by, the street held a stillness known only to those who have wandered through deserted Paris. The young man’s presence intrigued the shopkeepers of the “Cat and Racket.” His expressive features, marked by a mix of gloom and sparkle, conveyed a sense of passion. His forehead, often furrowed with annoyance, could transform into a radiant expression of grace.

Suddenly, the dormer window of the loft swung open, revealing three laughing faces framed within. The apprentices, eager to enjoy the street's fresh air, pointed at the young man and playfully sprinkled him with shaving foam. He responded with indifference, shaking off the foam and casting a contemptuous glance at the empty window frame.

At that moment, a slender white hand opened one of the clumsy windows on the third floor, rewarding the young man for his patience. A young girl appeared, her face fresh and innocent, framed by a frill of muslin. Wrapped in brown fabric, she revealed glimpses of her neck and shoulders. Her calm demeanor and the beauty of her features echoed the grace found in Raphael's works. As she gazed out, her eyes met those of her admirer, causing her to retreat in embarrassment, inadvertently slamming the window shut.

Meanwhile, the shutters of the “Cat and Racket” shop were removed, and an elderly servant hung a cloth sign reading “Guillaume, successor to Chevrel.” Passersby would struggle to discern the nature of Monsieur Guillaume's trade, as packages wrapped in brown linen were barely visible behind iron bars. Despite the Gothic façade, Monsieur Guillaume was known for his extensive stock and impeccable commercial integrity. He was always ready to fulfill government contracts, unlike his competitors who relied on connections and bribes.

Monsieur Guillaume, a figure of old-world charm, stood at the shop's threshold, surveying the street like a man returning from a long voyage. His attire—a greenish coat, pepper-and-salt stockings, and square-toed shoes—reflected his age and experience. His gray hair was meticulously combed, and his little green eyes sparkled with shrewdness. The wrinkles on his face told tales of patience and cunning, traits essential for a successful merchant.

As the old merchant awaited the arrival of his three apprentices, he noticed the young man across the street, whose anxious glances at the shop piqued his curiosity. The daylight brightened, allowing the stranger to observe the cashier's corner, where immense ledgers were kept. The young man’s interest in the shop seemed suspicious to Monsieur Guillaume, who suspected he might be eyeing the till.

The eldest apprentice, noticing the young man's fixation on the third-floor windows, stepped out to see if Mademoiselle Augustine was present. The draper, annoyed by the apprentice's curiosity, turned his attention back to the street. Just then, the young man hailed a cab and departed, relieving the tension among the apprentices.

“Why are you standing there with your arms crossed?” Monsieur Guillaume asked his apprentices, recalling how, in his youth, he would have inspected more than two pieces of cloth by that hour.

“It was daylight earlier,” replied the second apprentice, whose duty it was to keep track of time.

The old merchant smiled, recognizing the difference between his time and theirs. His apprentices, sons of wealthy manufacturers, were accustomed to a life of ease, unlike the hard work he demanded of them. The apprentices were diligent, capable of handling a business that would overwhelm ten modern clerks.

The shop was a model of cleanliness and order, with every piece of furniture reflecting the strict discipline of the household. The apprentices, though they paid for their board, would never dare to remain at the master’s table during dessert. Madame Guillaume’s frugality was legendary, and the apprentices trembled at the thought of her strictness.

The two sisters, Virginie and Augustine, were raised under their mother’s watchful eye, learning the ways of domestic life. Virginie, at twenty-eight, resembled her mother, while eighteen-year-old Augustine was a delicate beauty, lacking any resemblance to her parents. Their upbringing focused on trade, leaving little room for the pleasures of the world.

When the family prepared for company, the sisters worked tirelessly to arrange the drawing-room, only to be met with their mother’s disapproval. Their lives revolved around the shop, and their mother’s strict rules left little room for joy. Augustine, however, felt a yearning for something beyond the confines of her home, a longing stirred by the romances she secretly read.

Augustine’s gentle demeanor and beauty captured the heart of Joseph Lebas, the chief assistant, who had been with the family for twelve years. However, he was aware of the obstacles posed by Monsieur Guillaume’s principles, which dictated that the elder daughter must marry before the younger. This unrequited love caused him great distress.

The state of affairs in the household resembled a cloister, with strict rules governing every aspect of life. Months before the story begins, a young painter, Theodore de Sommervieux, had paused outside the darkened shop of the Cat and Racket, captivated by the scene within. The family’s modest dinner, illuminated by a hanging lamp, struck him as a living picture, and he became enamored with Augustine.

After months of devotion to his art, Theodore exhibited two masterpieces at the Salon, one of which featured Augustine. The public lauded his work, but the events of the exhibition remained unknown to the quiet household of the Cat and Racket. Augustine, upon hearing about the exhibition from her cousin, was filled with curiosity and courage to see the paintings.

When she finally saw the portrait, she was overwhelmed, recognizing herself in the artwork. The moment was electric, and she felt a connection to the artist, who had captured her essence. This encounter ignited a flame of love within her, leading to secret exchanges between the two.

Theodore, driven by his passion, sought ways to connect with Augustine despite the barriers imposed by her family. He bribed the maid to facilitate their correspondence, and they agreed to meet during Mass at Saint-Leu. Meanwhile, Augustine’s demeanor changed, and her family began to notice her absent-mindedness.

As the household engaged in the annual stock-taking, Augustine found moments of escape from her family’s scrutiny. The completion of this task allowed for a brief reprieve, and the family attended a performance of _Cendrillon_, granting the apprentices a night out.

The next morning, Monsieur Guillaume, ever diligent, returned to work early, reflecting on his past and the legacy of Master Chevrel. He summoned Joseph Lebas to discuss the successful stock-taking and proposed making him a partner in the business. Joseph, overwhelmed with gratitude, expressed his feelings for Augustine, only to be met with the harsh reality that Monsieur Guillaume intended for Virginie to marry first.

The old merchant, torn between his affection for Joseph and his principles, found himself in a dilemma. Joseph’s heartfelt plea for Augustine’s hand softened Monsieur Guillaume’s resolve, leading him to consider the possibility of allowing the two to marry.

As the family gathered for breakfast, the atmosphere was tense. Madame Guillaume, unaware of the budding romance, scrutinized Joseph, while Augustine remained anxious about her feelings for Theodore. The day culminated in a confrontation between Augustine and her parents, where she bravely declared her love for Theodore.

Monsieur Guillaume, initially resistant, was swayed by the arrival of Madame Roguin, who championed Theodore’s character and prospects. Her persuasive words shifted the family’s perspective, leading to a reluctant acceptance of the match.

In the end, Monsieur Guillaume agreed to the marriage, provided that Theodore would secure a settlement for Augustine. The family celebrated the union, and the young lovers found happiness amidst the complexities of their lives. The story concludes with a sense of hope and the promise of a bright future for both couples, as they navigate the challenges of love and commerce in the bustling world of Paris.

A few months after a memorable Sunday, the high altar of Saint-Leu hosted two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore radiated happiness, while Virginie, dressed simply, followed her sister like a shadow. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to marry Virginie first, but the priests focused on the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie for making a sensible match, while others envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned that such extravagance would lead to ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt justified in his cautious approach to marriage settlements.

After a splendid ball and supper, Monsieur and Madame Guillaume returned to their home in Rue du Colombier, while Monsieur and Madame Lebas went back to their old home in Rue Saint-Denis. The artist, intoxicated with joy, carried Augustine to their apartment, adorned with artistic touches.

The passion between Theodore and Augustine made their first year together blissful and unclouded. Theodore showered Augustine with affection, and she surrendered herself to the joys of married love, believing her devotion would always charm him. However, she remained the simple girl from Rue Saint-Denis, unaware of the social graces required in their new life. When she occasionally expressed an opinion that clashed with Theodore's, he laughed off her naivety.

As the year passed, Theodore felt the need to return to his work and social life, especially as Augustine was expecting their first child. He resumed seeing friends and frequented the Duchesse de Carigliano's gatherings. Once Augustine recovered and could join him, she relished the admiration that came with being Theodore's wife, but this marked the beginning of the end of their marital bliss. Her lack of sophistication began to irritate Theodore, who had been captivated by her beauty and innocence.

Despite her love, Augustine struggled to connect with Theodore's artistic world. She admired his work but lacked the understanding to appreciate it fully. As Theodore's artistic ambitions grew, he found himself increasingly frustrated by Augustine's inability to engage with his creative spirit. He sought solace in his studio, hoping that exposure to the artistic community would inspire her, but Augustine's traditional values clashed with the bohemian lifestyle.

At a dinner, Augustine's discomfort became evident when she overheard a young painter's irreverent remarks about art. Her presence stifled the artists' freedom, and Theodore's growing coldness pushed them further apart. Augustine, feeling neglected, wrapped herself in silent grief, sensing that her husband reserved his wit and charm for the outside world.

Three years into their marriage, Augustine, once vibrant and admired, now suffered from deep sorrow. She resolved to fulfill her duties as a wife, hoping to rekindle Theodore's affection, but her efforts were in vain. Theodore's artistic lifestyle clashed with her frugality, leading to tension. Augustine's attempts to manage their finances were at odds with Theodore's carefree nature, and their honeymoon happiness faded into darkness.

One evening, Augustine learned of Theodore's growing attachment to the Duchesse de Carigliano, a woman of experience and allure. This revelation shattered her, and she began to reflect on her shortcomings. Despite her despair, she resolved to educate herself, believing that if she could understand art and poetry, she might win back Theodore's love.

Determined, Augustine immersed herself in literature and culture, but her efforts only highlighted her limitations. She could appreciate art but struggled to express herself in conversation. Her religious upbringing and ingrained modesty hindered her growth, and Theodore's perception of her as inferior became a barrier she could not overcome. Despite her loyalty, she felt increasingly isolated.

In a moment of desperation, Augustine sought solace in her family, returning to her childhood home. Her sister and brother-in-law received her coolly, and their mundane lives contrasted sharply with her own. Augustine's attempts to share her troubles were met with conventional advice, leaving her feeling more alone. Her parents, unaware of the complexities of her marriage, offered little understanding.

As Augustine grappled with her feelings, she realized that her struggles were not easily understood by those around her. The emotional turmoil of her marriage was a burden she could not share, and she felt trapped in her circumstances. Her attempts to find sympathy were met with indifference, deepening her sense of isolation.

In a moment of clarity, Augustine decided to confront the Duchesse de Carigliano, not to reclaim her husband but to learn how to win his heart back. She approached the Duchess with humility, seeking guidance on how to navigate the complexities of her marriage. The encounter revealed the stark differences between their worlds, and Augustine felt the weight of her inadequacies.

The Duchess, intrigued by Augustine's sincerity, offered her advice on how to regain Theodore's affection. She emphasized the importance of maintaining an air of mystery and not revealing the full extent of one's love. Augustine, torn between her natural instincts and the Duchess's counsel, felt the pressure to adapt to a world that felt foreign to her.

As Augustine returned home, she was filled with conflicting emotions. She devised plans to rekindle her husband's interest, but the thought of facing him filled her with dread. When Theodore returned home, she attempted to present herself as the woman he once adored, but his reaction was unpredictable. Their reunion was fraught with tension, and Theodore's wounded pride led to a confrontation that shattered Augustine's hopes.

The aftermath of their argument left Augustine devastated. She found herself in a state of despair, grappling with the reality of her marriage. The emotional toll of their relationship weighed heavily on her, and she felt the loss of the love they once shared. In the wake of their conflict, Augustine's spirit began to wane, and she faced the harsh truth of her situation.

Ultimately, Augustine's struggles culminated in tragedy. Her life, once filled with promise, became a reflection of the challenges faced by those caught between love and ambition. The inscription on her grave serves as a poignant reminder of the delicate balance between passion and the demands of life, leaving those who knew her to ponder the complexities of love and the sacrifices it entails.
