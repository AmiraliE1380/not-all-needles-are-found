Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis near the Rue du Petit-Lion, a weathered house helped historians reconstruct old Paris: dilapidated walls exposed timber and cracked plaster, a warped triangular roof overhung the street, and overlapping upper planks lightened the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man, cloaked like an antiquary, stood across studying its peculiarities. He ignored narrow first-floor windows, inspecting the third-floor sash windows of crude green-tinted glass and glancing at the shop below. A large beam on four pillars bore many paint layers and a whimsical cat with an oversized racket; a faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel.”

Dressed as if from a ball—cloak, white silk stockings and gloves, disheveled hair—his pale face and dazzling cravat drew shopkeepers’ eyes. Three dormer apprentices laughed, sprinkled him with shaving cream; he brushed it off as a girl's Raphael-like face flashed and vanished. The “Cat and Racket” shutters opened, a manservant displayed the sign, and behind iron bars brown-linen packages appeared. Monsieur Guillaume, old-fashioned in appearance, was reputed the city’s best-stocked, honest cloth merchant, though rivals grumbled about his notary’s heavy discounts.

The old merchant, Monsieur Guillaume, awakens in his Rue Saint-Denis shop and watches a stranger loitering outside. A staunch traditionalist with a shrewd face, he enforces a strict routine on three apprentices who fear his gaze. When work begins, Guillaume notices the stranger’s anxious looks; the eldest, curious, steps out, thinks he sees Mademoiselle Augustine hurrying away, and hails a cab, thwarting a prank.

Guillaume reprimands the youths for idleness, recalls swifter youth, and insists on rigorous labor despite the apprentices’ comfortable homes and board. The spotless shop and frugal household—apprentices barred from the family dessert—adhere to tradition: Mass together and modest long-service wages. Joseph Lebas, the eldest, earns little and is a possible match for Virginie, though he secretly loves Augustine. Virginie is devout and severe; Augustine, eighteen, is delicate, bookish, and yearns for passion. Guillaume worries about his daughters’ prospects in changing times.

From that day the old merchant, grieving his eldest daughter’s decline, recalled marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like Joseph Lebas’s and Virginie’s; marrying off his daughter would repay a debt to an orphan benefactor. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, aware of the fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder sister marry first, suffered in silence. Warm-hearted and tall, he kept his love concealed.

Months earlier a young painter had paused outside the dark Cat and Racket, transfixed by a scene any artist would envy: a cave-like shop opening to a lamp-lit dining room that evoked Dutch interiors. The family, Augustine among them with a chubby maid, formed a modest, peaceful tableau. Augustine, pensive and barely touching her food, seemed almost angelic in the lamp’s glow; the sight overwhelmed the painter, who left unable to eat or sleep.

Determined to paint it, he returned repeatedly—sometimes in disguise—studying Augustine. For eight months he lived in love and art, neglecting friends and routine. His friend Girodet, seeing two masterpieces intended for the Salon, recognized their depth and said love had inspired them, though he warned against public display. The paintings were nevertheless shown and caused a sensation: The Interior launched a new genre, and the portrait became unforgettable, winning acclaim and offers the artist declined.

Madame Guillaume mentioned the exhibition; with her cousin’s aid Augustine saw the Louvre. Trembling before the portrait she recognized herself; the artist whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” and she was stunned. Fleeing the crowd, she wept, exchanged a brief nod with him, feigned a headache to avoid talk, longed for the elegance of a genius’s wife, and next day learned the painter had removed the pictures—an absence that revealed her instinctive sensitivity.

The morning after the ball, Theodore de Sommervieux—who had won Augustine’s heart—hesitated to declare his love because her household constrained them. Augustine sat in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume, watched by her mother, while Theodore, fearing rivals among the assistants, bribed a maid to set up discreet meetings; they agreed to meet during Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family busied itself with stock-taking under Monsieur Guillaume, ignoring Augustine’s longing. After work they relaxed—attending Cendrillon and letting the apprentices have a night out. The next morning Guillaume resumed routine and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas, offering him partnership. Overwhelmed, Joseph blurted he loved Augustine; Guillaume at first misheard Virginie's name, then, once clear, suggested Joseph accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose; Madame chastised Augustine and sent her away, prompting a family discussion about her future. Augustine declared her love for Theodore and vouched for his respectable, prosperous origins. Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore’s honors and fortune, swaying the parents. By evening Guillaume consented, Joseph was told, Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket, and the household embraced a joyful future.

A few months after that memorable Sunday, Saint-Leu’s high altar witnessed two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore glowed while Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had wanted Virginie married first, but priests favored the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s sensible match and envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank; an old fan-maker warned such extravagance would bring ruin. After a splendid ball the Guillaumes returned home and the Lebas family resumed their old residence. The ecstatic artist carried Augustine to their richly adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful: Theodore lavished affection and Augustine believed her devotion would always charm him. As pregnancy approached, Theodore grew absorbed in work, reconnected with friends, and frequented the Duchesse de Carigliano’s salons, pleased to be seen with a beautiful wife. Augustine liked society but felt out of place among artists; her admiration was sincere yet superficial, and an intellectual gap grew. Studio life exposed her discomfort; at gatherings her innocence clashed with freer manners. Gradually Theodore cooled; three years in Augustine sank into sorrow as his involvement with the Duchesse deepened.

She tried to educate herself in poetry and art to reconnect, but her efforts only emphasized her inadequacy. Seeking solace at her childhood home—where Virginie and Joseph Lebas ran a drapery—brought no comfort. She returned to her husband’s house, burdened by uncertainty and longing, like a patient testing remedies for a fractured marriage.

The elderly Guillaumes welcomed their daughter; her visit broke four years of monotony spent in reminiscence—especially over the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume checked on his shop, the Cat and Racket, and chatted with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume, appointed to the Army’s clothing consulting board through her son-in-law, filled their home with extravagant, tasteless decor. Their nostalgic stagnation struck Augustine as a stark contrast to her turbulent life, leaving her both proud and appalled.

Augustine displayed her accomplishments and aired grievances about her husband Theodore, a painter, whom her mother criticized for late nights and eccentric habits. When she mentioned divorce, her parents were shocked; Monsieur offered practical support, but Augustine recoiled, realizing that only those who had suffered like her could understand her isolation.

Determined to change course, she sought the Duchess de Carigliano—not to compel Theodore but to learn how to captivate him. The Duchess counseled projecting strength and self-control, cultivating mystery, and never revealing the whole measure of affection. She even offered Augustine a portrait Theodore had painted as a means to rekindle him. Theodore’s jealousy provoked a violent quarrel; the next morning Augustine was found in despair amid the wreckage and died young. Her tragic end remains a poignant reminder of the struggle women face pursuing love amid the complexities of artistic genius; her grave’s inscription reflects that sorrow.
