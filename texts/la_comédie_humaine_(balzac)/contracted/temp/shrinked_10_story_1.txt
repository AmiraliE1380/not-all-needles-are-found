Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, stood a charming old house that helped historians reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls bore hieroglyphic-like timber patterns and cracked plaster. A weather-warped triangular roof overhung the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer window. The upper story was built of overlapping planks to avoid overburdening the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man, wrapped in a cloak, stood under a shop awning across from the house, studying it with the eye of an antiquary. The relic of sixteenth-century civic life presented peculiarities: he ignored the first floor’s narrow, boarded windows and focused on the third floor’s modest sash windows, poorly crafted and obscured by green-tinted glass. Occasionally he glanced down at the shop, where small amusing details caught his eye.

A large wooden beam, supported by four pillars, showed many layers of paint and a comical painting of a cat playing rackets that amused him. The cat, racket and oversized ball had become an increasingly absurd caricature. The faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a remnant of a whimsical past when such signs drew customers.

The young man, in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, looked as if he had come from a wedding or ball; his white gloves and disheveled hair suggested recent revelry. Despite market gardeners hurrying by, the street held a rare stillness. His striking features, a mix of passion and calm, attracted the attention of the shopkeepers at the “Cat and Racket.” His pale face contrasted with a dazzling white cravat, and his expressive eyes conveyed varied emotion.

Suddenly three apprentices appeared at the dormer window, laughing and teasing him. They playfully sprinkled him with shaving cream, but he remained indifferent, brushing it off. Then a young girl’s face emerged from the window, her beauty recalling a Raphael Virgin. Innocent and unembarrassed, she saw the young man, retreated, and the window slammed shut, leaving him disappointed.

Meanwhile the shutters of the “Cat and Racket” opened and a manservant hung a cloth sign: “Guillaume, successor to Chevrel.” The shop’s exterior, protected by iron bars, concealed numerous packages wrapped in brown linen. Despite its old-fashioned look, Monsieur Guillaume was known for having the best-stocked store among Parisian cloth merchants and a reputation for honesty. He secured profits without flattery or costly gifts, though fellow traders warned against his notary’s heavy discounts.

The old merchant stood at his shop threshold, surveying the Rue Saint-Denis as if awakening from a long sleep. Monsieur Guillaume, in traditional attire, scrutinized a stranger who seemed overly interested in his shop. His face, marked by patience and shrewdness, reflected years of experience. A staunch traditionalist, he lamented lost customs and imposed a strict routine on his three apprentices, who feared his keen gaze.

As the apprentices began their day, Guillaume noticed the stranger's anxious glances and suspected ulterior motives. The eldest apprentice, curious, stepped outside and thought he saw Mademoiselle Augustine departing in haste. He quickly hailed a cab, which relieved the apprentices about their prank on Augustine.

Guillaume reprimanded them for idleness, recalling how he would have worked faster in his youth. Despite their comfortable backgrounds, he enforced a rigorous work ethic, contrasting with modern shops where apprentices expected early comfort. The shop was meticulously kept, reflecting the order and economy he valued.

Though the apprentices paid for their board, they were excluded from family dessert, a measure against Madame Guillaume's frugality. They attended Mass with the family, and the eldest, Joseph Lebas, earned a modest wage after years of service. Guillaume considered him a possible husband for his elder daughter, Virginie, though Joseph secretly loved the younger daughter, Augustine.

The household ran on strict tradition. Virginie and Augustine were raised under their mother's austere rule. Virginie, resembling her mother, was devout and severe, while Augustine, eighteen, was delicate and charming but often overshadowed by maternal expectations. Their lives centered on domestic duties with little exposure to the outside world, so they found pleasure in family gatherings and the rare visitors.

Madame Guillaume's rigid schedule governed daily life: early dressing and a precise routine. Augustine, however, yearned for something beyond this cloistered existence, sometimes lost in thoughts of a more passionate life. Secret readings of romantic novels likely kindled her longing for adventure.

This longing deepened Joseph's affection for Augustine, while Virginie, lacking suitors, also found herself drawn to him. Their feelings developed quietly amid the monotony of shop life, where familiar faces fostered intimate ties. Monsieur Guillaume, aware of changing times, wondered about his daughters' prospects in a world where suitors might soon be scarce.

From that day the old merchant, saddened by his eldest daughter's decline, remembered how he had married Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like Joseph Lebas’s and Virginie’s. Marrying off his daughter would settle a sacred debt by repaying an orphan for a past benefit. Joseph Lebas, now thirty-three, knew the fifteen-year gap between him and Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rigid rule that the elder daughter marry first. The lovesick assistant, warm-hearted yet tall and broad-chested, therefore suffered in silence.

To understand, we must rewind a few months. One evening a young painter paused outside the darkened Cat and Racket, captivated by a scene any artist would envy. The unlit shop revealed a dining room glowing from a hanging lamp, its warm light like a Dutch interior. The family—Augustine among them, and a chubby maid—formed a peaceful, modest tableau. Fresh from Rome and weary of grand art, the painter longed for the simple, meditative maiden he had seen only in pictures. This living scene struck him: Augustine seemed pensive and untouched by food; the lamp made her face almost angelic. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

The next day he retreated to his studio determined to recapture the moment. He visited the Cat and Racket repeatedly, even disguising himself to study Augustine. For eight months he lived in love and art, neglecting friends and routine. One morning his friend Girodet broke his isolation, asking about his Salon submission. The artist revealed two masterpieces; Girodet, recognizing the depth of feeling, embraced him. “You are in love?” Girodet asked; the artist nodded. They agreed great works spring from such passion. Girodet warned against showing them at the Salon, doubting the public’s taste, but the works were exhibited and created a sensation. The Interior launched a genre shift, while the portrait became unforgettable, winning acclaim and offers the artist declined.

These public events contrasted sharply with the monastic life in Rue Saint-Denis until Madame Guillaume, the notary’s wife, mentioned the exhibition during a visit. Augustine, with her cousin’s help, obtained permission to visit the Louvre. Seeing the portrait, she trembled—recognizing herself, feeling terror and excitement. The artist, nearby, whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Intent on escaping the crowd, Augustine urged her cousin to leave, but they were drawn back to the celebrated painting. Overwhelmed, she wept at sight of the work. In a quiet exchange she made a gesture to the artist; he acknowledged her with a nod. This brief connection ignited guilt and exhilaration. The Salon’s chaos, combined with the artist’s gaze, intoxicated her; she felt loved and experienced emotions she had never before known.

As they left, the painter radiated joy, and Augustine realized she was loved. His words echoed, deepening her turmoil. She feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition with her cousin, but her mother’s plan to visit the Salon filled her with dread. That night she reflected, feeling a void in her humdrum life and longing for the elegance she’d always admired. The thought of being a genius’s wife filled her with hope despite the realities of her upbringing. She was suddenly in love, her heart full of dreams of a brighter future.

The next day her parents returned from the Salon with visible disappointment: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. Yet the news of the paintings’ disappearance revealed to Augustine a sensitivity women often possess instinctively.

The morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had won Augustine's heart, found it difficult to declare his love because of the household's watchful constraints. In the counting-house, seated between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume, Augustine was closely observed by her mother, and Theodore felt hemmed in by the eyes around them and by imagined rivals among the assistants. His passion led him to bribe a maid to facilitate communication, and they arranged a discreet meeting during Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family was absorbed in stock-taking, overseen by Monsieur Guillaume. They busied themselves verifying inventory and balancing accounts, oblivious to Augustine's emotional strain; despite her absent-mindedness, no one noticed her longing for Theodore. After weeks of labor the stock-taking concluded, giving the family a rare relaxation; they attended a performance of Cendrillon while the apprentices enjoyed a night out.

The next morning Monsieur Guillaume returned to routine, reflecting on the business and summing up the profitable results. He summoned his assistant, Joseph Lebas, and proposed making him a partner, acknowledging Joseph's contributions. Joseph, overwhelmed, blurted out his love for Augustine, but Guillaume misheard and thought he meant Virginie. After some confusion, Guillaume understood Joseph's attachment to Augustine and suggested Joseph accompany her to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie exchanged looks at a nervous Joseph. Madame, unaware of Augustine's romance with Theodore, chastised Augustine for inattentiveness during church. After the service she ordered Augustine to her room, prompting a heated discussion between Madame and Monsieur about their daughter's future.

Summoned before her parents, Augustine confessed her love for Theodore de Sommervieux, stressing his respectable background. Monsieur Guillaume voiced concern about artists, recalling past experiences and the family's precarious position. Augustine defended Theodore as prosperous and from a good family, which caught her father's interest.

Just then Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore's recent honors and increased wealth, and she advocated the match. Her persuasive account began to sway Monsieur and Madame Guillaume, who had earlier resisted the idea of their daughter marrying above her station.

As the day progressed the atmosphere shifted from conflict to acceptance. Monsieur Guillaume sought out Joseph to inform him of developments. By evening the family gathered to celebrate the prospect of the union, and Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket as a token of their shared happiness. Monsieur Guillaume, initially skeptical, consented to the marriage while emphasizing financial prudence and advising Augustine to consult him on money matters.

In the end the lovers found solace in mutual affection, and the household, once tense, embraced the prospect of a joyful future together.

A few months after a memorable Sunday, the high altar of Saint-Leu witnessed two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore radiated happiness, while Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to have Virginie wed first, but the priests attended the more elegant bride. Neighbors commended Virginie’s sensible match; others envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned that such extravagance might bring ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt his caution about settlements validated. After a splendid ball and supper, Monsieur and Madame Guillaume returned home, Monsieur and Madame Lebas resumed their old residence, and the artist, elated, carried Augustine to their beautifully adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful and unclouded. Theodore showered Augustine with affection; she, lost in love, believed her devotion would always charm him. She spoke the language of love and remained largely unaware of much around her, sometimes revealing a touching naivety. As the year progressed, however, Theodore felt the need to return to work—especially with a child expected—and reconnected with friends. He frequented the Duchesse de Carigliano’s gatherings, seeking the social validation of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine enjoyed society but soon perceived her lack of sophistication compared with Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was genuine but superficial, and a growing disconnect emerged between their intellectual worlds. Theodore, once captivated, began to feel the strain. He retreated to his studio, hoping the artistic environment would inspire Augustine, but she struggled to adapt to that life. Her discomfort surfaced at gatherings, where her innocence clashed with the more liberated attitudes of artists.

Over time Theodore’s affection cooled and Augustine felt the weight of his indifference. Despite efforts to fulfill her duties as a wife and revive his interest, she sensed an increasing distance. Three years into their marriage the once-vibrant Augustine became consumed by sorrow. The artist’s apparent enthusiasm for the Duchesse de Carigliano deepened her despair, and she feared being supplanted by a more experienced woman.

Her heartache pushed her to reflect on her shortcomings and the barriers between them. She resolved to educate herself, believing that understanding poetry and art might bridge the gap. Yet her attempts to change met frustration: she could not cultivate the natural wit and ease Theodore admired. Her earnestness often backfired, making her seem increasingly inadequate in his presence.

In desperation, Augustine sought refuge with family, returning to her childhood home. She found Virginie and her husband Joseph Lebas managing their drapery business with a calm stability that contrasted sharply with her turmoil. Joseph offered practical, commercially minded advice, but his suggestions left her more conflicted than comforted.

Ultimately Augustine returned home burdened by uncertainty, longing for the love she once shared with Theodore, like a desperate patient trying every remedy to heal a fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter warmly; her visit was a refreshing break from their monotony. For four years they had drifted, reminiscing about past business ups and downs, especially the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume frequently checked on his shop, the Cat and Racket, and spoke with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume—now on the Army’s clothing consulting board through her son-in-law’s influence—filled their home with extravagant, tasteless decor. Their nostalgia and stagnation struck Augustine as a stark contrast to her turbulent life, leaving her both proud and appalled at their vacuous existence.

Despite her inner turmoil, Augustine displayed her accomplishments and aired marital grievances, which her parents eagerly absorbed. Madame Guillaume was particularly curious about Augustine’s life with her husband Theodore, a painter. When Augustine described his late nights and eccentric habits, her mother disapproved of his lifestyle and profession. Their incredulity grew as Augustine defended him, sparking a heated debate about genius and marital responsibility.

When Augustine mentioned divorce, her parents were shocked into action; Monsieur Guillaume offered support. Overwhelmed by their concern and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine rejected separation and realized she was isolated in her pain—only those who had shared similar trials could truly empathize.

Resolved to change course, Augustine sought the Duchess de Carigliano—not to win Theodore back directly but to learn how to captivate him. Approaching the Duchess’s opulent home, she felt envy and admiration. Meeting the Duchess, Augustine was struck by her beauty, charm, and the subtle rivalry between them.

The Duchess met Augustine’s plea with sympathy and shrewd counsel, stressing the need to show strength and control with men of genius. She advised cultivating mystery and withholding the full extent of one’s affection. Though uneasy about manipulation, Augustine began to accept the Duchess’s lessons, seeing that her love for Theodore might demand a new approach.

The Duchess offered Augustine a portrait of herself painted by Theodore, believing it could rekindle his interest. Hopeful, Augustine returned home determined to become the image in the painting. When Theodore arrived, she presented the portrait eagerly; his reaction was fury and jealousy, sparking a tumultuous confrontation.

The quarrel escalated, exposing Theodore’s passion and insecurity and culminating in a violent outburst. In the aftermath Augustine was devastated; her dreams were shattered as she faced the true nature of her marriage. The next morning her mother found her in despair amid the remnants of their fight.

Augustine’s tragic fate was soon sealed: overwhelmed by the pressures of her tumultuous life, she died young. Her story is a poignant reminder of the struggles women face pursuing love and fulfillment, especially when entangled with the complexities of artistic genius. The inscription on her grave reflects this tragedy, leaving those who knew her to ponder the difficulty of balancing passion and stability in relationships.
