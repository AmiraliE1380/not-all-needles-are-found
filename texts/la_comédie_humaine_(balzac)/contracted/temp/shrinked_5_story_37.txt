PAZ

(La Fausse Maitresse)

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

Dedicated to the Comtesse Clara Maffei.

PAZ

(LA FAUSSE MAÎTRESSE)

I

In September, 1835, one of the richest heiresses of the faubourg Saint-Germain, Mademoiselle du Rouvre, the only daughter of the Marquis du Rouvre, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a young Polish exile.

We write these Polish names as pronounced to spare readers the Slav consonant fortifications.

The Marquis du Rouvre had squandered nearly a princely fortune originally obtained through his marriage to a Demoiselle de Ronquerolles. On her mother’s side Clémentine du Rouvre had the Marquis de Ronquerolles for an uncle, and Madame de Serizy for an aunt. On her father’s side another uncle was the eccentric Chevalier du Rouvre, an old bachelor enriched by land speculation. The Marquis de Ronquerolles lost both his children to cholera; Madame de Serizy’s only son perished in Africa. By a curious turn, Clémentine became, despite her father’s dissipation, a great heiress. The Marquis de Ronquerolles, Madame de Serizy, and the Chevalier du Rouvre agreed to settle fortunes on their niece, each pledging ten thousand francs a year from her marriage.

Comte Adam Laginski, though an exile, was not an expense to France. He belonged to an ancient Polish family allied to princely houses. But heraldry counted for little with the rising bourgeoisie of Louis-Philippe’s Paris. When Adam first appeared in 1833 on the boulevard des Italiens and at the Jockey-Club he lived the life of a young man taking pleasure in Parisian dissipation; at first he was thought a student.

By this period Polish nationality had fallen in French estimation because of political reactions. The Movement against Resistance had made a conquered people, long celebrated in France, the butt of ridicule. Parisians were hard to reconcile to a people who had been center of theatrical demonstrations at a famous review. Poles were associated with the republican party, though Poland was a republic of aristocrats. The bourgeoisie treated Polish exiles with contempt in 1835. There are indeed two sorts of Polish exiles: republican Poles, sons of Lelewel, and noble Poles, led by Prince Adam Czartoryski—fire and water. Exiles always bring their hatreds and divisions. This explains virulent attacks on Czartoryski and the dislike of noble exiles by Parisian shopkeeping Caesars.

In 1834 Adam Mitgislas Laginski was a butt for Parisian pleasantry.

“He is rather nice, though he is a Pole,” said Rastignac.

“All these Poles pretend to be great lords,” said Maxime de Trailles, “but this one does pay his gambling debts, and I begin to think he must have property.”

Without wishing to offend, one may note that the light-hearted, inconsistent Sarmatian character partly justified Parisian satire; the French would behave similarly in the same circumstances. The French aristocracy, helped by Polish lords during the Revolution, did not later repay their kindness; the faubourg Saint-Germain is still indebted to Poland.

Was Comte Adam rich, poor, or an adventurer? The question remained. Diplomatic salons, following official caution, treated Polish exiles discreetly, leaving them in social solitude. In a city of pleasure like Paris, a Pole’s heedless gaiety found ample encouragement to dissipation.

Adam had two points against him—his appearance and his mental habit. There are two species of Pole as there are two of Englishwoman: when an Englishwoman is not handsome she seems horribly ugly. Comte Adam looked pinched. His small, sharp face seemed pressed in a vise; his short nose, fair hair, and reddish beard gave him a goatish look, and his tarnished yellow eyes were oblique. Yet he had exquisite manners and a distinguished air, due partly to his dress and partly to training by his mother, a Radziwill. His courage was daring; his mind suited to ephemeral Parisian conversation. Young men of fashion talked too much then; real conversation needs leisure and social inequalities. Consequently, the dandies did not see the great seigneur in the careless young fellow who flitted from subject to subject, intent on amusement and freed from family constraints.

But one day in 1834 Adam suddenly bought a house in the rue de la Pépinière. Six months later his style of living rivaled the best in Paris. About the time he took himself seriously he had seen Clémentine du Rouvre at the Opera and fallen in love. A year later they were married. The salon of Madame d’Espard first sounded his praises. Mothers of daughters learned too late that the Laginski family was among the most illustrious of the North. By an act of prudence very un-Polish, his mother had mortgaged her property during the insurrection for an immense sum lent by two Jewish bankers in Paris. Comte Adam was now in possession of eighty thousand francs a year. When this was discovered society ceased to be surprised at the imprudence laid to the charge of Clémentine’s guardians. People jumped from one extreme to the other.

During the winter of 1836 Comte Adam was the fashion, and Clémentine Laginska one of the queens of Paris. Madame Laginska joined that circle of young women—Mesdames de Lestorade, de Portenduere, Marie de Vandenesse, du Guénic, and de Maufrigneuse—flowers of Paris, distinct from parvenus and speculators of the new regime.

This preamble shows the sphere in which one of those noble actions, less rare than calumniators admit, occurred—actions like pearls hidden in rough shells and lost in the tossing waves of society.

If architecture expresses manners and morals, it was proved after the insurrection of 1830. As old fortunes diminish, majestic mansions are demolished and replaced by phalansteries; peers of July occupy upper floors above newly enriched empirics below. A confused mixture of styles is used. Lacking a court or real nobility to set a tone, art has no harmony; yet architects find economical ways to imitate the real and solid and display resources in distributing space. Propose to an architect to build in a garden and he will run up a little Louvre with a courtyard, stables, and, if desired, a garden. Inside are many small rooms and passages; the eye is deceived into thinking there is space. It is still a nest of small rooms where a ducal family must turn about in space that once was a bakehouse.

The hôtel of the Comtesse Laginska, rue de la Pépinière, is one of these creations, standing between court and garden. On the right are kitchens and offices; to the left the coachhouse and stables. The porter’s lodge is between two charming portes-cochères. The chief luxury is a greenhouse at the end of a ground-floor boudoir opening on an admirable suite of reception rooms. An English philanthropist had built this bijou, designed the garden, added the greenhouse, polished doors, bricked the courtyard, and realized a dream resembling George IV’s Pavilion at Brighton. Parisian workmen moulded the doors; ceilings imitated the middle ages or a Venetian palace; marble veneering clung to outer walls. Steinbock and François Souchet designed mantelpieces and panels; Schinner painted ceilings. The staircase, white as a woman’s arm, rivaled that of the hôtel Rothschild. On account of riots and unsettled times the cost of this folly was about eleven hundred thousand francs. All this luxury, called princely by those who do not know real princes, was built in the garden of a house that had belonged to a purveyor who made a fortune by the Revolution, fled to Brussels, and died bankrupt. The Englishman died in Paris; his widow, a Methodist, ordered the establishment sold. Comte Adam bought it at a bargain; how he came to do so will be made known, for bargains were not in his line as a grand seigneur.

Behind the house lay the velvety lawn shaded by exotic trees, among which stood a Chinese pagoda. The greenhouse concealed the northern wall; climbing plants covered the opposite wall on poles painted green. This lawn, flower world, gravelled paths, simulated forest, and palisades were contained in twenty-five square rods, now worth four hundred thousand francs. Here, in solitude in the middle of Paris, birds sang. The greenhouse was an immense jardinière filling the air with perfume. Hot-water or steam pipes were cleverly concealed. The boudoir was large. Modern Parisian Architecture’s miracle is to get many things out of a limited bit of ground.

The boudoir of the young countess suited the taste of the artist who decorated the house. It was too full of pretty nothings to be a place of repose; one scarcely knew where to sit among carved Chinese work-tables, inlaid ivories, yellow topaz cups, mosaics, Dutch pictures, angels, and statuettes. Superb sketches by the best artists, lids of chests made into panels, fluted draperies of Italian silk, portières hanging from rods of old oak, Persian rugs, et cetera. For a last graceful touch, these elegant things were subdued by half-light filtered through embroidered curtains. On a table, among curiosities, lay a whip with a handle designed by Mademoiselle de Fauveau, showing that the countess rode.

Such is a lady’s boudoir in 1837—an exhibition of shop contents meant to amuse the eye, as if ennui were the worst danger in a social world that fears nothing more. Why is there nothing of an inner life? Because no one then was sure of the future; we live like prodigal annuitants.

One morning Clémentine seemed to be thinking. She lay full length on a marvellous couch, the greenhouse doors open letting tropical perfume pervade the room. The young wife looked at her husband who smoked a narghile, the only pipe she would suffer there. The portières gave a vista through two elegant salons, one white and gold, the other in a Renaissance style. The dining-room, rivalled only by the Baron de Nucingen’s, was at the end of a gallery decorated in the middle-ages manner. This gallery opened on the courtyard antechamber, through which could be seen the beautiful staircase.

The count and countess had just finished breakfast; the sky was blue and April nearly over. Married two years, Clémentine had discovered a secret or mystery in her household. The Pole is usually helpless before a woman; he is full of tenderness and becomes her inferior; Polish women make admirable wives. A Pole is even more vanquished by a Parisian woman. Comte Adam, pressed by questions, did not try roguery; he merely stipulated that he should not answer until he had finished his narghile.

“If any difficulty occurred when we were traveling,” said Clémentine, “you always dismissed it by saying, ‘Paz will settle that.’ You never wrote but to Paz. At home everybody keeps saying, ‘the captain, the captain.’ If I want the carriage—‘the captain.’ Is there a bill to pay—‘the captain.’ If I want my horse bitted, they must speak to Captain Paz. In short, it is like a game of dominoes—Paz is everywhere. I hear of nothing but Paz, but I never see Paz. Who and what is Paz? Why don’t you bring forth your Paz?”

“Isn’t everything going on right?” asked the count, taking the bocchettino from his narghile.

“Everything is going on so right that other people with an income of two hundred thousand francs would ruin themselves by going at our pace, and we have only one hundred and ten thousand.”

She pulled the bell-cord. A footman entered, dressed like a minister.

“Tell Captain Paz that I wish to see him.”

“If you think you are going to find out anything that way—” said Adam, laughing.

Adam and Clémentine had married in December 1835 and soon after had traveled two years in Italy, Switzerland, and Germany. Returning to Paris in November 1837, the countess entered society as a married woman and then became aware of a useful, half-suppressed factotum named Paz—spelt thus but pronounced “Patz.”

“Monsieur le capitaine Paz begs Madame la comtesse to excuse him,” said the footman returning. “He is at the stables; as soon as he has changed his dress Comte Paz will present himself to Madame.”

“What was he doing at the stables?”

“He was showing them how to groom Madame’s horse,” said the man. “He was not pleased with the way Constantin did it.”

The countess looked at the footman. He was perfectly serious.

“Ah! he was grooming Cora.”

“Madame la comtesse intends to ride out this morning?” said the footman, leaving the room without further answer.

“Is Paz a Pole?” asked Clémentine, turning to her husband, who nodded.

The footstep echoed through the gallery.

The countess beheld a tall, handsome man, well-made, bearing on his face signs of inward strength and secret sorrow. He wore a frogged overcoat called a polonaise. Thick black hair, rather unkempt, covered his square head; his broad forehead shone like white marble; he held his visored cap in his hand. His hand was like that of the Infant Hercules. Robust health flushed his face, marked by a large Roman nose and an Italian fervor in his jet eyes. The pleated trousers revealed fidelity to his land’s fashions. The contrast between the captain and the count, the little Pole and the stalwart soldier, was striking to a romantic woman.

“Good morning, Adam,” he said familiarly. Then he bowed courteously to ask Clémentine what he could do for her.

“You are Laginski’s friend!” exclaimed the countess.

“For life and death,” answered Paz, to whom the count smiled as he drew a last puff from his pipe.

“Then why don’t you take your meals with us? why did you not accompany us to Italy and Switzerland? why do you hide yourself so that I cannot thank you for the constant services you do for us?” said the countess with vivacity but no feeling.

She thought she perceived in Paz a voluntary servitude, a social amphibian half-secretary, half-bailiff, an embarrassing friend.

“Because, countess,” he answered with ease, “there are no thanks due. I am Adam’s friend, and it gives me pleasure to take care of his interests.”

“And you remain standing for your pleasure, too,” remarked Comte Adam.

Paz sat near the door.

“I remember seeing you about the time I was married, and afterwards in the courtyard,” said Clémentine. “But why put yourself in a position of inferiority—you, Adam’s friend?”

“I am perfectly indifferent to the opinion of Parisians,” he replied. “I live for myself, or, if you like, for you two.”

“But the opinion of the world as to a friend of my husband is not indifferent to me—”

“Ah, madame, the world will be satisfied if you tell them I am ‘an original.’”

After a silence he added, “Are you going out to-day?”

“Will you come with us to the Bois?”

“Certainly.”

So saying, Paz bowed and withdrew.

“What a good soul he is!” said Adam. “He has all the simplicity of a child.”

“Now tell me all about your relations with him,” said Clémentine.

“Paz, my dear,” said Laginski, “belongs to a noble family as old and illustrious as our own. One of the Pazzi of Florence, at the time of their disasters, fled to Poland, settled with some of his property, and founded the Paz family, to which the title of count was granted. This family distinguished itself in the glorious days of our republic and became rich. Some branches are rich and some poor. Our Paz is from a poor branch. He was an orphan with no fortune but his sword when he served in the regiment of the Grand Duke Constantine at the time of our revolution. Joining the Polish cause, he fought like a man who has nothing—three good reasons for fighting well. In his last affair, thinking he was followed by his men, he dashed upon a Russian battery and was taken prisoner. I was there. His brave act roused me. ‘Let us go and get him!’ I said to my troop, and we charged the battery like a lot of foragers. I got Paz—I was the seventh man; we started twenty and came back eight, counting Paz. After Warsaw was sold we were forced to escape the Russians. By chance Paz and I met again on the other side of the Vistula. I saw the poor captain arrested by Prussians. When we have fished a man out of the Styx we cling to him. This new danger made me so unhappy I let myself be taken too, thinking I could help him. Two men can get away where one will perish. Thanks to my name and some connections in Prussia, the authorities shut their eyes to my escape. I got my dear captain through as a man of no consequence, a family servant, and we reached Dantzic. There we took a Dutch vessel to London. It took us two months. My mother was ill in England and expecting me. Paz and I took care of her till her death, which the Polish troubles hastened. Then we left London for France. Men who go through such adversities become like brothers. When I reached Paris, at twenty-two, and found I had an income of over sixty thousand francs a year, I wanted to secure Paz’s future before launching into dissipation. I had seen sadness in his eyes—sometimes tears. I wanted him to be able to repay a benefit if he chose. I produced a certificate of Funds payable to bearer for a sum a year.”

Clémentine kissed him. “Dear treasure! Well, what did Paz do?”

“Thaddeus turned pale,” said the count, “but he didn’t say a word.”

“Oh! his name is Thaddeus?”

“Yes; Thaddeus folded the paper and gave it back to me, then said: ‘I thought, Adam, we were one for life or death, and that we should never part. Do you want to be rid of me?’ ‘If you take it that way, Thaddeus,’ I answered, ‘don’t let us say another word about it. If I ruin myself you shall be ruined too.’ ‘You haven’t fortune enough to live as a Laginski should,’ he said, ‘and you need a friend to take care of your affairs, be a father and a brother and a trusty confidant.’ My dear child, in his look and voice he had a maternal emotion, the gratitude of an Arab, the fidelity of a dog, the friendship of a savage, not displayed but ever ready. I seized him with a hand on each shoulder and kissed him on the lips. ‘For life and death, then! all that I have is yours—do what you will with it.’ He found this house and bought it for next to nothing. He sold my Funds high and bought in low, and we paid for this barrack with the profits. He knows horses, buys and sells to advantage; our stable costs little though we have the finest horses and equipages in Paris. Our servants, brave Polish soldiers chosen by him, would go through fire and water for us. Sometimes I had to force him to go to the theatre or my little tavern dinners. He doesn’t like social life.”

“What does he like, then?” asked Clémentine.

“Poland; he loves it and pines for it. His only spendings are sums he gives, more in my name than his own, to some of our poor brother-exiles.”

“Well, I shall love him, the fine fellow!” said the countess. “He looks as simple-hearted as he is grand.”

“All these pretty things you have about you,” continued Adam, “he picked up; he bought them at auction or as bargains. He’s keener than the dealers. If you see him rubbing his hands in the courtyard you may be sure he has traded away one horse for a better. He lives for me; his happiness is to see me elegant. The duties he takes are accomplished without fuss. One evening I lost twenty thousand francs at whist. ‘What will Paz say?’ I thought. Paz paid them—not without a sigh; but he never reproached me. That sigh restrained me more than remonstrances of uncles or wives could. ‘Do you regret the money?’ I asked. ‘Not for you or me,’ he replied; ‘but I was thinking twenty poor Poles could have lived a year on that sum.’ You must understand the Pazzi are fully the equal of the Laginski, so I couldn’t consider Paz an inferior. I never went out or came in without going first to Paz, as to my father. My fortune is his; and Thaddeus knows that if danger threatened him I would fling myself into it and drag him out, as I have done before.”

“And that is saying a good deal, my dear friend,” said the countess. “Devotion is like lightning. Men devote themselves in battle but no longer have the heart for it in Paris.”

“Well,” replied Adam, “I am always ready, as in battle, to devote myself to Paz. Our characters have asperities and defects, but mutual comprehension has tightened the bond. It is possible to save a man’s life and kill him afterward if he is a bad fellow; but Paz and I know that in each other which makes friendship indissoluble. There’s a constant exchange of happy thoughts and impressions; perhaps such a friendship is richer than love.”

A pretty hand closed the count’s mouth as promptly as a blow.

“Yes,” he said, “friendship, my dear angel, knows nothing of bankrupt sentiments and collapsed joys. Love, after giving more than it has, ends by giving less than it receives.”

“One side as well as the other,” remarked Clémentine laughing.

“Yes,” continued Adam, “whereas friendship only increases. You and I are as much friends as lovers; we have, at least I hope so, combined the two sentiments in our marriage.”

“I’ll explain why you and Thaddeus are such good friends,” said Clémentine. “The difference in lives comes from tastes and necessity, from likings not positions. As far as one can judge from seeing a man once and from what you tell me, there are times when the subaltern might become the superior.”

“Oh, Paz is truly my superior,” said Adam naively; “I have no advantage over him except mere luck.”

His wife kissed him for the generosity of the words.

“The extreme care with which he hides the grandeur of his feelings is one form of his superiority,” continued the count. “I said to him once: ‘You are a sly one; you have in your heart a vast domain within which you live and think.’ He has a right to the title of count; but in Paris he won’t be called anything but captain.”

“The Florentine of the middle ages has reappeared in our century,” said the countess. “Dante and Michael Angelo are in him.”

“That’s the truth,” cried Adam. “He is a poet in soul.”

“So here I am, married to two Poles,” said the young countess, with a gesture worthy of the stage.

“Dear child!” said Adam, pressing her to him, “it would have made me very unhappy if my friend did not please you. We were both rather afraid of it, though he was delighted at my marriage. You will make him very happy if you tell him that you love him—yes, as an old friend.”

“I’ll go and dress; the day is so fine; and we will all three ride together,” said Clémentine, ringing for her maid.

Paz led so subterranean a life that Parisian society asked who he was when the Comtesse Laginska was seen in the Bois de Boulogne riding between her husband and a stranger. During the ride Clémentine insisted Thaddeus should dine with them. This caprice compelled Paz to make an evening toilet. Clémentine dressed with a coquetry that impressed even Adam when she entered the salon where the two friends awaited her.

“Comte Paz,” she said, “you must go with us to the Opera.”

The tone meant: “If you refuse we shall quarrel.”

“Willingly, madame,” replied the captain. “But as I have not the fortune of a count, have the kindness to call me captain.”

“Very good, captain; give me your arm,” she said, taking it and leading the way to the dining-room with the flattering familiarity that enchants lovers.

The countess placed the captain beside her; his behavior was that of a poor sub-lieutenant at his general’s table. He let Clémentine talk, listened deferentially as to a superior, did not differ with her in anything, and waited to be questioned before he spoke. He seemed actually stupid to the countess, whose coquettish ways missed their mark before such frigid gravity and conventional respect. In vain Adam kept saying, “Do be lively, Thaddeus; one would really suppose you were not at home. You must have made a wager to disconcert Clémentine.” Thaddeus remained heavy and half asleep. When the servants left the room at the end of the dessert the captain explained that his habits were diametrically opposite to those of society—he went to bed at eight and rose early; he excused his dulness on the ground of being sleepy.

“My intention in taking you to the Opera was to amuse you, captain; but do as you prefer,” said Clémentine, rather piqued.

“I will go,” said Paz.

“Duprez sings ‘Guillaume Tell,’” remarked Adam. “But perhaps you would rather go to the ‘Variétés’?”

The captain smiled and rang the bell. “Tell Constantin,” he said to the footman, “to put the horses to the carriage instead of the coupé. We should be rather squeezed otherwise,” he said to the count.

“A Frenchman would have forgotten that,” remarked Clémentine, smiling.

“Ah! but we are Florentines transplanted to the North,” answered Thaddeus with a refinement of accent and a look that made his conduct at table seem assumed. There was a contrast between this involuntary self-revelation and his behavior during dinner. Clémentine examined the captain with covert glances that showed surprise and observation.

Silence reigned in the salon while the three took coffee, a silence annoying to Adam, who could not imagine its cause. Clémentine no longer tried to draw out Thaddeus. The captain retreated into his military stiffness and never came out again, neither on the way to the Opera nor in the box, where he seemed asleep.

“You see, madame, that I am a very stupid man,” he said during the dance in the last act of “Guillaume Tell.” “Am I not right to keep, as the saying has it, to my own specialty?”

“In truth, my dear captain, you are neither a talker nor a man of the world, but you are perhaps Polish.”

“Therefore leave me to look after your pleasures, your property, your household—it is all I am good for.”

“Tartufe! pooh!” cried Adam, laughing. “My dear, he is full of ardor; he is thoroughly educated; he can, if he chooses, hold his own in any salon. Clémentine, don’t believe his modesty.”

“Adieu, comtesse; I have obeyed your wishes so far; and now I will take the carriage and go home to bed and send it back for you.”

Clémentine bowed and let him go without replying.

“What a bear!” she said to the count. “You are a great deal nicer.”

Adam pressed her hand when no one was looking.

“Poor, dear Thaddeus,” he said, “he is trying to make himself disagreeable where most men would try to seem more amiable than I.”

“Oh!” she said, “I am not sure but what there is some calculation in his behavior; he would have taken in an ordinary woman.”

Half an hour later, when the chasseur, Boleslas, called out “Gate!” and the carriage was waiting, Clémentine asked her husband, “Where does the captain perch?”

“Why, there!” replied Adam; “His apartments are over the coachhouse.”

“Who lives on the other side?” asked the countess.

“No one as yet,” said Adam; “I mean that apartment for our children and their instructors.”

“He didn’t go to bed,” said the countess, observing lights in Thaddeus’s rooms as the carriage passed under the portico supported by columns copied from those of the Tuileries, which replaced a vulgar zinc awning painted in stripes.

The captain, in his dressing-gown with a pipe in his mouth, watched Clémentine as she entered the vestibule. The day had been hard for him. A great and terrible emotion had taken possession of his heart the day Adam made him go to the Opera to see and give his opinion on Mademoiselle du Rouvre; again when he saw her at her marriage, he had recognized the woman whom a man is forced to love exclusively. For this reason Paz had advised and promoted the long journey to Italy and elsewhere after the marriage. At peace so long as Clémentine was away, his trial was renewed on the household’s return. As he sat at his window on this memorable night, smoking his latakia in a pipe of wild-cherry wood six feet long, given him by Adam, these thoughts passed through his mind:

“I, and God, who will reward me for suffering in silence, alone know how I love her! But how shall I manage to have neither her love nor her dislike?”

His thoughts travelled far on this strange theme.

It must not be supposed that Thaddeus lived without pleasure amid his sufferings. The deceptions of this day were a source of inward joy. Since the count and countess’ return he had daily ineffable satisfactions in knowing himself necessary to a household that without his devotion would infallibly have gone to ruin. What fortune can bear the strain of reckless prodigality? Clémentine, brought up by a spendthrift father, knew nothing of household management; the women of the present day, however rich or noble, are often compelled to undertake it themselves. Adam, son of a great Polish lord who let themselves be preyed on by the Jews, was not of a nature to check his fancies or his wife’s. Left to himself he would probably have been ruined before his marriage. Paz had prevented him from gambling at the Bourse, and that says all.

Under these circumstances, Thaddeus, feeling that he loved Clémentine in spite of himself, had not the resource of leaving the house and travelling to forget his passion. Gratitude, the key-note of his life, held him bound to the household where he alone could look after the affairs of the heedless owners. The long absence of Adam and Clémentine had given him peace. But the countess returned more lovely than ever, enjoying the freedom marriage brings to a Parisian woman, displaying the graces of a young wife and the attraction gained from the happiness or independence bestowed upon her by a husband trustful, chivalric, and much in love. To know that he was the pivot on which the household’s splendor depended, to see Clémentine when she got out of her carriage after a fête, or when she took her drive, to meet her on the boulevards in her pretty equipage, looking like a flower in a whorl of leaves, inspired poor Thaddeus with mysterious delights that glowed in his heart but showed no sign upon his face.

How happened it that for five whole months the countess had never perceived the captain? Because he hid himself, carefully concealing the pains he took to avoid her. Nothing so resembles Divine love as hopeless human love. A man must have great depth of heart to devote himself in silence and obscurity to a woman. In such a heart is the worship of love for love’s sake only—sublime avarice, sublime because ever generous. Effect is nature, charming and belonging to the poet, painter, lover. But Cause belongs to a few privileged souls. Thaddeus had reached this height; filled with the unutterable joys of a creator he had attained in his love something of grandeur.

“No,” he thought as he watched his pipe’s smoke, “she was not entirely deceived. She might break up my friendship with Adam if she took a dislike to me; but if she coquetted with me to amuse herself, what would become of me?”

The conceit of this last supposition was so foreign to the modest, Teutonic timidity of the captain that he scolded himself for admitting it, and went to bed, resolved to await events before deciding a course.

The next day Clémentine breakfasted contentedly without Paz, not noticing his disobedience to her orders. It happened to be her reception day, when the house was semi-royally thrown open. She paid no attention to Comte Paz’s absence, on whom all the burden of such parade days fell.

“Good!” thought he, as he heard the last carriages driving away at two in the morning; “it was only the caprice or curiosity of a Parisian woman that made her want to see me.”

After that the captain returned to his ordinary habits, which the incident had somewhat upset. Diverted by her Parisian occupations, Clémentine seemed to have forgotten Paz. It must not be thought an easy matter to reign a queen over fickle Paris. Winters are to fashionable women what a campaign once was to soldiers. What works of art and genius are expended on a gown or a garland to make a sensation! A delicate creature will wear her stiff, brilliant harness of flowers and diamonds, silk and steel, from nine at night till two or three in the morning. She eats little to attract notice to her slender waist; she satisfies hunger with tea, sugared cakes, ices which heat her, or slices of heavy pastry. The stomach yields to coquetry’s orders. The awakening comes too late. A fashionable woman’s life contradicts nature, and nature is pitiless. She rises and makes an elaborate morning toilet and thinks of the one for afternoon; she must receive and make visits, go to the Bois, practise the art of smiling, and invent compliments that seem neither common nor far-fetched. Not all succeed. It is no surprise to find a young woman who entered society fresh and healthy faded and worn out after three years. Six months in the country will hardly heal winter wounds. We hear continually of mysterious ailments—gastritis and such—ills unknown when women busied themselves about households. In the olden time women only appeared at intervals; now they are always on the scene. Clémentine found she had to struggle for her supremacy. She was cited, which brought jealousies; the care and watchfulness of this contest left little time even to love her husband. Paz might well be forgotten. Nevertheless, in May, as she drove home from the Bois just before leaving Paris for Ronquerolles, her uncle’s estate in Burgundy, she noticed Thaddeus, elegantly dressed, sauntering in a side-path of the Champs-Élysées, in the seventh heaven of delight at seeing his beautiful countess in her elegant carriage—his beloved family admired by all.

“There’s the captain,” she said to her husband.

“He’s happy!” said Adam. “This is his delight. He knows there’s no equipage more elegant than ours, and he rejoices to think people envy it. Have you only just noticed him? I see him there nearly every day.”

“I wonder what he is thinking about now,” said Clémentine.

“He is thinking that this winter has cost a good deal, and that it is time we went to economize with your old uncle Ronquerolles,” replied Adam.

The countess stopped the carriage near Paz, and bade him take the seat beside her. Thaddeus grew as red as a cherry.

“I shall poison you,” he said; “I have been smoking.”

“Doesn’t Adam poison me?” she said.

“Yes, but he is Adam,” returned the captain.

“And why can’t Thaddeus have the same privileges?” asked the countess, smiling.

That divine smile triumphed over poor Paz’s heroic resolutions; he looked at Clémentine with all the fire of his soul, though tempered by the angelic gratitude of a man whose life was based on that virtue. The countess folded her arms in her shawl, lay back pensively on her cushions, ruffling her bonnet’s feathers, and watched people pass. That flash of a great and hitherto resigned soul reached her sensibilities. What was Adam’s merit in her eyes? Courage and generosity were natural. But Thaddeus—surely he possessed, or seemed to possess, some superiority over Adam. Dangerous thoughts took hold of the countess; she noticed the contrast of the fine presence that distinguished Thaddeus and the puny frame showing the degenerating effects of intermarriage among the Polish aristocracy. The devil alone knew her thoughts, for she sat still, with dreamy eyes, saying nothing until home.

“You will dine with us; I shall be angry if you disobey me,” she said as the carriage turned in. “You are Thaddeus to me, as you are to Adam. I know your obligations to him, but I also know those we are under to you. Both generosities are natural—but you are generous every day and all day. My father dines here today, also my uncle Ronquerolles and my aunt Madame de Sérizy. Dress yourself,” she said, taking his hand as she stepped from the carriage.

Thaddeus dressed with a joyful heart though shaken by dread. He went down at the last moment and behaved through dinner as he had on the first occasion—like a soldier fit only for his duties as steward. But this time Clémentine was not his dupe; his glance had enlightened her. The Marquis de Ronquerolles, one of the ablest diplomatists after Talleyrand, who had served with de Marsay during his short ministry, had been informed by his niece of Comte Paz’s real worth and knew how modestly he made himself steward of Laginski’s affairs.

“And why is this the first time I have the pleasure of seeing Comte Paz?” asked the marquis.

“Because he is so shy and retiring,” replied Clémentine with a look telling Paz to change his behavior.

Alas, to render the captain less interesting we must avow: Paz, though superior to his friend Adam in some ways, was not a man of parts. His apparent superiority owed much to misfortune. In his lonely, poverty-stricken life in Warsaw he had read and taught himself much; he had compared and meditated. But the gift of original thought that makes a great man he did not possess and can never be acquired. Paz, great in heart, approached the sublime; but in the sphere of sentiment, being more a man of action than of thought, he kept his thoughts to himself; they only served to eat his heart out. What, after all, is a thought unexpressed?

After Clémentine’s little speech, the Marquis de Ronquerolles and his sister exchanged a singular glance, embracing their niece, Comte Adam, and Paz. It was one of those rapid scenes that take place in France and Italy—regions where eyes can say everything. To make the eye speak needs either extreme servitude or complete liberty. Adam, the Marquis du Rouvre, and Clémentine did not observe this luminous by-play of the old coquette and the old diplomat; Paz, the faithful watchdog, understood its meaning. It was a two-second affair, but to describe the tempest it roused in the captain’s soul would take too much space.

“What!” he said to himself, “do the aunt and uncle think I might be loved? Then my happiness only depends on my audacity! But Adam—”

Ideal love and desire clashed with gratitude and friendship, all equally powerful, and for a moment love prevailed. The lover would have his day. Paz became brilliant; he tried to please and told the story of the Polish insurrection in noble words when the diplomatist questioned him. By the end of dinner Clémentine hung upon his lips and regarded him as a hero, forgetting that Adam too, after sacrificing a third of his fortune, had been an exile. At nine, after coffee, Madame de Sérizy kissed her niece’s forehead, pressed her hand, and left with Adam; the Marquis de Ronquerolles and the Marquis du Rouvre followed soon after. Paz and Clémentine were alone.

“I will leave you now, madame,” said Thaddeus. “You will of course rejoin them at the Opera?”

“No,” she answered, “I don’t like dancing, and they give an odious ballet to-night, ‘La Révolte au Sérail.’”

A moment’s silence.

“Two years ago Adam would not have gone to the Opera without me,” said Clémentine, not looking at Paz.

“He loves you madly,” replied Thaddeus.

“Yes, and because he loves me madly he is all the more likely not to love me tomorrow,” said the countess.

“How inexplicable Parisian women are!” exclaimed Thaddeus. “When they are loved to madness they want to be loved reasonably: and when they are loved reasonably they reproach a man for not loving them at all.”

“And they are quite right. Thaddeus,” she went on smiling, “I know Adam so well; I am not angry with him; he is volatile and above all grand seigneur. He will always be content to have me as his wife and will never oppose my tastes, but—”

“Where is the marriage in which there are no ‘buts’?” said Thaddeus gently, trying to give another direction to her mind.

The least presuming of men might well have thought what nearly drove this poor lover beside himself: “If I do not tell her now that I love her I am a fool,” he kept saying.

Neither spoke; between them came one of those deep silences crowded with thought. The countess examined Paz covertly; Paz observed her in a mirror. Buried in an armchair like a man digesting his dinner, the image of a husband or indifferent old man, Paz crossed his hands upon his stomach and twirled his thumbs mechanically, looking stupidly at them.

“Why don’t you tell me something good of Adam?” cried Clémentine suddenly. “Tell me that he is not volatile, you who know him so well.”

The cry was fine.

“Now is the time,” thought poor Paz, “to put an insurmountable barrier between us. Tell you good of Adam?” he said aloud. “I love him; you would not believe me; and I am incapable of telling you harm. My position is very difficult between you.”

Clémentine lowered her head and looked down at the tips of his varnished boots.

“You Northern men have nothing but physical courage,” she said complainingly; “you have no constancy in your opinions.”

“How will you amuse yourself alone, madame?” said Paz, assuming a careless air.

“Are you not going to keep me company?”

“Excuse me for leaving you.”

“What do you mean? Where are you going?”

The thought of a heroic falsehood came into his head.

“I—I am going to the Circus in the Champs-Élysées; it opens to-night, and I can’t miss it.”

“Why not?” said Clémentine, questioning him by a look half anger.

“Must I tell you why?” he said, coloring; “must I confide to you what I hide from Adam, who thinks my only love is Poland.”

“Ah! a secret in our noble captain?”

“A disgraceful one—which you will perhaps understand and pity.”

“You, disgraced?”

“Yes, I, Comte Paz; I am madly in love with a girl who travels all over France with the Bouthor family—people who have the rival circus to Franconi; they play only at fairs. I had the director at the Cirque-Olympique engage her.”

“Is she handsome?”

“To my thinking,” said Paz, melancholy. “Malaga (that’s her stage name) is strong, active, and supple. Why do I prefer her to all other women?—I can’t tell you. When I look at her, with her black hair tied with a blue satin ribbon, floating on her bare olive shoulders, dressed in a white tunic edged with gold, with a knitted silk bodice like a living Greek statue, carrying flags to martial music, jumping through paper hoops that tear as she goes, and lighting so gracefully on the galloping horse to such applause—no hired clapping—well, all that moves me.”

“More than a handsome woman in a ballroom?” asked Clémentine, amazed.

“Yes,” answered Paz chokingly. “Such agility, such grace under constant danger seems to me the height of triumph for a woman. Cinti and Malibran, Grisi and Taglioni, Pasta and Ellsler—all who reign on stage—can’t be compared, to my mind, with Malaga, who can jump on or off a horse at full gallop, stand on the point of one foot and fall easily into the saddle, knit stockings, break eggs, and make an omelette with the horse at speed—to the admiration of the people—peasants and soldiers. Malaga is dexterity personified; her little wrist or foot can rid her of three or four men. She is the goddess of gymnastics.”

“She must be stupid—”

“Oh, no,” said Paz, “I find her as amusing as the heroine of ‘Peveril of the Peak.’ Thoughtless as a Bohemian, she says everything that comes into her head; she thinks no more about the future than you do of the sous you fling to the poor. She says grand things sometimes. You couldn’t make her believe that an old diplomatist was a handsome young man, not if you offered her a million francs. Such love as hers is perpetual flattery to a man. Her health is positively insolent, and she has thirty-two oriental pearls in lips of coral. Her muzzle—that’s what she calls the lower part of her face—has, as Shakespeare expresses it, the savor of a heifer’s nose. She can make a man unhappy. She likes handsome men, strong men, Alexanders, gymnasts, clowns. Her trainer, a horrible brute, used to beat her to make her supple and intrepid—”

“You are positively intoxicated with Malaga.”

“Oh, she is called Malaga only on the posters,” Paz replied, piqued. “She lives in the rue Saint-Lazare, in a pretty third-floor apartment, all velvet and silk, like a princess. She has two lives: her circus life and the life of a pretty woman.”

“Does she love you?”

“She loves me—now you will laugh—solely because I’m a Pole. She saw an engraving of Poles rushing with Poniatowski into the Elster—for all France persists in thinking the Elster an impetuous flood where Poniatowski and his followers were engulfed. But in the midst of all this I am very unhappy, madame.”

A tear of rage fell from his eyes and touched the countess.

“You men have such a passion for singularity.”

“And you?” said Thaddeus.

“I know Adam so well that I am certain he could forget me for some mountebank like your Malaga. Where did you first see her?”

“At Saint-Cloud last September, on the fête-day. She was at a corner booth covered with flags, where the shows are given. Her comrades, all in Polish costumes, made a horrible racket. I watched her standing there, silent and dumb, and thought I saw a melancholy in her face; there was enough about her to sadden a girl of twenty. That touched me.”

The countess sat in a delicious attitude, pensive and rather melancholy.

“Poor, poor Thaddeus!” she exclaimed. Then, with the kindliness of a true great lady and not without a malicious smile, “Well go, go to your Circus.”

Thaddeus took her hand, kissed it, leaving a hot tear upon it, and went out.

Having invented this passion for a circus-rider, he bethought him to give it reality. The only truth in his tale was the momentary attention he had given Malaga at Saint-Cloud; he had since seen her name on Circus posters where a clown, for a tip of five francs, had told him she was a foundling, stolen perhaps. Thaddeus went to the Circus and saw her again. For ten francs one of the grooms informed him that Malaga was Marguerite Turquet and lived on the fifth story in the rue des Fossés-du-Temple.

The following day Paz went to the faubourg du Temple, found the house, and asked to see Mademoiselle Turquet, who during the summer substituted for the leading horsewoman at the Cirque-Olympique and was a supernumerary at a boulevard theatre in winter.

“Malaga!” cried the portress, rushing into the attic, “there’s a fine gentleman wanting you. He is getting information from Chapuzot, who is playing him off to give me time to tell you.”

“Thank you, Madame Chapuzot; but what will he think of me if he finds me ironing my gown?”

“Pooh! when a man’s in love he loves everything about us.”

“Is he an Englishman? they are fond of horses.”

“No, he looks to me Spanish.”

“That’s a pity; they say Spaniards are always poor. Stay here with me, Madame Chapuzot; I don’t want him to think I’m deserted.”

“Who is it you are looking for, monsieur?” asked Madame Chapuzot, opening the door for Thaddeus, who had now come upstairs.

“Mademoiselle Turquet.”

“My dear,” said the portress, with importance, “here is someone to see you.”

A line on which clothes dried caught the captain’s hat and knocked it off.

“What is it you wish, monsieur?” said Malaga, picking up the hat and giving it to him.

“I saw you at the Circus,” said Thaddeus, “and you reminded me of a daughter I have lost, mademoiselle; out of affection for my Héloïse, whom you resemble, I should like to be of service to you, if you will permit me.”

“Why, certainly; pray sit down, general,” said Madame Chapuzot; “nothing could be more straightforward, more gallant.”

“But I am not gallant, my good lady,” exclaimed Paz. “I am an unfortunate father who tries to deceive himself by a resemblance.”

“Then am I to pass for your daughter?” said Malaga, slyly, not suspecting his sincerity.

“Yes,” said Paz, “and I’ll come and see you sometimes. You shall be lodged in better rooms, comfortably furnished.”

“I shall have furniture!” cried Malaga, looking at Madame Chapuzot.

“And servants,” said Paz, “and all you want.”

Malaga looked suspicious.

“What countryman is monsieur?”

“I am a Pole.”

“Oh! then I accept,” she said.

Paz departed, promising to return.

“Well, that’s a stiff one!” said Marguerite Turquet, looking at Madame Chapuzot; “I’m half afraid he is wheedling me, to carry out some fancy of his own—Pooh! I’ll risk it.”

A month after this eccentric interview the circus-rider lived in a comfortable apartment furnished by Comte Adam’s upholsterer, Paz having judged it desirable to have his folly talked about at the hôtel Laginski. Malaga, to whom this adventure was like a leaf from the Arabian Nights, was served by Monsieur and Madame Chapuzot as friends and servants. The Chapuzots and Marguerite constantly expected some result; but after three months none could make out the meaning of the Pole’s caprice. Paz arrived and spent about an hour there once a week, sat in the salon, and never went into Malaga’s boudoir nor bedroom, despite Chapuzot and Malaga’s manoeuvres. The count would ask about the small events of Marguerite’s life, and each time he left two gold pieces of forty francs each on the mantelpiece.

“He looks as if he didn’t care to be here,” said Madame Chapuzot.

“Yes,” said Malaga, “the man’s as cold as an icicle.”

“But he’s a good fellow all the same,” cried Chapuzot, happy in a new blue cloth suit that made him look like a minister’s servant.

The sum Paz deposited weekly, joined to Malaga’s meagre salary, gave her means of sumptuous living compared with former poverty. Wonderful stories circulated at the Circus about Malaga’s good-luck. Her vanity increased the six thousand francs Paz had spent on her furniture to sixty thousand. According to the clowns and supers Malaga was squandering money; she now appeared at the Circus wearing burnous, shawls and elegant scarves. In the demi-monde Malaga was much talked about; the equivocal set attacked her good fortune with calumnies. They said she was a somnambulist and the Pole a magnetizer trying to find the philosopher’s stone. Other scandals drove her to tears as she reported them to Paz.

“When I want to injure a woman,” she said, “I don’t calumniate her; I don’t declare that someone magnetizes her to get stones out of her, but I say plainly she is humpbacked, and I prove it. Why do you compromise me?”

Paz maintained a cruel silence. Madame Chapuzot soon discovered Comte Paz’s name and title; then she heard facts at the hôtel Laginski: Paz was a bachelor and had never been known to have a daughter, alive or dead, in Poland or France. After that Malaga felt terror.

“My dear child,” Madame Chapuzot would say, “that monster”—a man who contented himself with only looking in a sly way, not daring to come out and say things, and such a beautiful creature too as Malaga—“that monster is trying to get a hold upon you, and make you do something illegal and criminal. Holy Father, if you should get into the police-courts! it makes me tremble from head to foot; suppose they should put you in the newspapers! I’ll tell you what I should do; I’d warn the police.”

One day, after many foolish notions had fermented in Malaga’s mind, Paz laid his money as usual on the mantelpiece; she seized the gold and flung it in his face, crying, “I don’t want stolen money!”

The captain gave the gold to Chapuzot, went away without a word, and did not return.

Clémentine was at her uncle’s place in Burgundy at this time.

When the Circus troop discovered Malaga had lost her Polish count much excitement followed. Malaga’s display of honor was thought folly by some and shrewdness by others. The Pole’s conduct, even when discussed by the cleverest women, seemed inexplicable. Thaddeus received the next week thirty-seven letters from women of their kind. Happily for him, his astonishing reserve did not excite fashionable curiosity and was only discussed in the demi-monde.

Two weeks later the handsome circus-rider, crippled by debt, wrote the following letter to Comte Paz, which, having fallen into Adam’s hands, was read by several dandies who pronounced it a masterpiece:

“You, whom I still dare to call my friend, will you not pity me after all that has passed—which you have so ill understood? My heart disavows whatever may have wounded your feelings. If I was fortunate enough to charm you and keep you beside me in the past, return to me; otherwise, I shall fall into despair. Poverty has overtaken me, and you do not know what horrid things it brings with it. Yesterday I lived on a herring at two sous, and one sou of bread. Is that a breakfast for the woman you loved? The Chapuzots have left me, though they seemed so devoted. Your desertion has caused me to see to the bottom of all human attachments. The dog we feed does not leave us, but the Chapuzots have gone. A sheriff has seized everything on behalf of the landlord, who has no heart, and the jeweller who refused to wait even ten days—for when we lose the confidence of such as you, credit goes too. What a position for women who have nothing to reproach themselves with but the happiness they have given! My friend, I have taken all I have of any value to my uncle’s; I have nothing but the memory of you left, and here is the winter coming on. I shall be fireless when it turns cold; for the boulevards are to play only melodramas, in which I have nothing but little bits of parts which don’t pose a woman. How could you misunderstand the nobleness of my feelings for you?—for there are two ways of expressing gratitude. You who seemed so happy in seeing me well-off, how can you leave me in poverty? Oh, my sole friend on earth, before I go back to the country fairs with Bouthor’s circus, where I can at least make a living, forgive me if I wish to know whether I have lost you forever. If I were to let myself think of you when I jump through the hoops, I should be sure to break my legs by losing a time. Whatever may be the result, I am yours for life.

“Marguerite Turquet.”

“That letter,” thought Thaddeus, shouting with laughter, “is worth the ten thousand francs I have spent upon her.”

Clementine came home the next day, and the day after that Paz saw her again, more beautiful and graceful than ever. After dinner, during which the countess treated Paz with perfect indifference, a little scene occurred in the salon between the count and his wife when Thaddeus had gone. On pretense of asking Adam’s advice, Thaddeus had left Malaga’s letter with him, as if by mistake.

“Poor Thaddeus!” said Adam, as Paz disappeared, “what a misfortune for a man of his distinction to be the plaything of the lowest kind of circus-rider. He will lose everything, and get lower and lower, and won’t be recognizable before long. Here, read that,” added the count, giving Malaga’s letter to his wife.

Clementine read the letter, which smelled of tobacco, and threw it aside with a look of disgust.

“Thick as the bandage is over his eyes,” continued Adam, “he must have found out something; Malaga tricked him, no doubt.”

“But he goes back to her,” said Clementine, “and he will forgive her! It is for such horrible women as that that you men have indulgence.”

“Well, they need it,” said Adam.

“Thaddeus used to show some decency—in living apart from us,” she remarked. “He had better go altogether.”

“Oh, my dear angel, that’s going too far,” said the count, who did not desire the sinner’s death.

Paz, who knew Adam thoroughly, had enjoined him to secrecy, pretending to excuse his dissipations, and had asked his friend to lend him a few thousand francs for Malaga.

“He is a very firm fellow,” said Adam.

“How so?” asked Clementine.

“Why, for having spent no more than ten thousand francs on her, and letting her send him that letter before he would ask me for enough to pay her debts. For a Pole, I call that firm.”

“He will ruin you,” said Clementine, with the sharpness of a Parisian woman’s distrust.

“Oh, I know him,” said Adam; “he will sacrifice Malaga, if I ask him.”

“We shall see,” remarked the countess.

“If it is best for his own happiness, I sha’n’t hesitate to ask him to leave her. Constantin says that since Paz has been with her he, sober as he is, has sometimes come home excited. If he takes to intoxication I shall be as grieved as if he were my own son.”

“Don’t tell me anything more about it,” cried the countess, with a gesture of disgust.

Two days later the captain perceived in the manner, tones, and, above all, the eyes of the countess the terrible results of Adam’s confidences. Contempt had opened a gulf between the beloved woman and himself. He was suddenly plunged into deep distress, for the thought gnawed him, “I have myself made her despise me!” His own folly stared him in the face. Life became a burden; the very sun turned gray. Yet amid these bitter thoughts he had moments of pure joy. There were times when he could give himself wholly to admiration for his mistress, who paid him no attention. Hanging about at her parties, silent, all heart and eyes, he never missed one of her attitudes nor a tone of her voice when she sang. He lived in her life; he groomed the horse she rode, studied the ways of her splendid establishment, to whose interests he was now more devoted than ever. These silent pleasures were buried in his heart like those of a mother, whose love a child never knows; for is it knowing anything unless we know it all? His love was more perfect than Petrarch’s for Laura, which found its reward in fame. The emotion the Chevalier d’Assas felt dying must have been to him a lifetime of joy. Such emotions Paz enjoyed daily—without dying, and without immortality as a guerdon.

But what is Love, that despite these ineffable delights Paz should still be unhappy? The Catholic religion has magnified Love and wedded it to respect and nobility. Love is therefore accompanied by sentiments and qualities of which mankind is proud; it is rare to find true Love where contempt exists. Thaddeus suffered from wounds his own hand had given him. The trial of his former life—living beside his mistress, unknown, unappreciated, but generously working for her—was better than this. Yes, he wanted the reward of his virtue, her respect, and he had lost it. He grew thin and yellow, ill with a constant low fever that in January forced him to keep his bed, though he refused a doctor. Comte Adam became uneasy; the countess had the cruelty to remark, “Let him alone; don’t you see it is only some Olympian trouble?” When Thaddeus heard this he found courage in despair; he left his bed, tried a few amusements, and recovered.

About the end of February Adam lost a large sum at the Jockey-Club and, afraid of his wife, begged Thaddeus to let the loss appear in the accounts as if spent on Malaga.

“There’s nothing surprising in your spending that sum on the girl; but if the countess finds out that I have lost it at cards I shall be lowered in her opinion, and she will always be suspicious in future.”

“Ha! this, too!” exclaimed Thaddeus with a sigh.

“Now, Thaddeus, if you will do me this service we shall be forever quits,—though, indeed, I am your debtor now.”

“Adam, you will have children; don’t gamble any more,” said Paz.

“So Malaga has cost us another twenty thousand francs,” cried the countess, some time later, when she discovered this new generosity. “First, ten thousand, now twenty more,—thirty thousand! the income of which is fifteen hundred! the cost of my box at the Opera, and the whole fortune of many a bourgeois. Oh, you Poles!” she said, gathering flowers in her greenhouse; “you are incomprehensible. Why are you not furious with him?”

“Poor Paz is—”

“Poor Paz, poor Paz, indeed!” she cried, interrupting him, “what good does he do us? I shall take the management of the household myself. You can give him the allowance he refused, and let him settle it as he likes with his Circus.”

“He is very useful to us, Clementine. He has certainly saved over forty thousand francs this last year. And besides, my dear angel, he has managed to put a hundred thousand with Nucingen, which a steward would have pocketed.”

Clementine softened but remained hard toward Thaddeus. A few days later she requested him to come to the boudoir where, a year earlier, she had been surprised into comparing him with her husband. This time she received him alone, without perceiving danger.

“My dear Paz,” she said, with the condescending familiarity of the great to their inferiors, “if you love Adam as you say you do, you will do a thing which he will not ask of you, but which I, his wife, do not hesitate to exact.”

“About Malaga?” said Thaddeus, bitterly.

“Well, yes,” she said; “if you wish to end your days in this house and continue friends with us, you must give her up. How an old soldier—”

“I am only thirty-five and haven’t a white hair.”

“You look old,” she said, “and that’s the same thing. How so careful a manager, so distinguished a—”

The horrible part was her evident intention to rouse in him a sense of honor she thought extinct.

“—so distinguished a man as you are, Thaddeus,” she resumed after a pause caused by a gesture of his hand, “can allow yourself to be caught like a boy! Your proceedings have made that woman celebrated. My uncle wanted to see her, and he did. Malaga receives a great many gentlemen. I did think you such a noble soul. For shame! Will she be such a loss that you can’t replace her?”

“Madame, if I knew any sacrifice I could make to recover your esteem I would make it; but to give up Malaga is not one—”

“In your position, that is what I should say myself, if I were a man,” replied Clementine. “Well, if I accept it as a great sacrifice there can be no ill-will between us.”

Paz left the room, fearing he might commit a folly, feeling wild ideas crowding his mind. He walked in the cold without being able to cool his burning cheeks.

“I thought you had a noble soul,”—the words rang in his ears.

“A year ago,” he said to himself, “she thought me a hero who could fight the Russians single-handed!”

He thought of leaving the hotel Laginski, taking service with the spahis and getting killed in Africa, but the same fear checked him. “Without me,” he thought, “what would become of them? they would soon be ruined. Poor countess! what a horrible life it would be for her if reduced to even thirty thousand francs a year. No, since all is lost for me in this world,—courage! I will keep on as I am.”

Since 1830 the carnival in Paris transformed into a European, burlesque, lively affair. Perhaps diminishing fortunes led Parisians to invent collective amusements, cheap salons without hostesses and without manners. The month of March was prodigal of balls, where dancing, joking, coarse fun, grotesque figures, and Parisian wit produced extravagant effects. These carnival follies had their Pandemonium in the rue Saint-Honore and their Napoleon in Musard, a small man born to lead an orchestra as noisy as the crowd, to set the time for the galop, that witches’ dance which did not take form till the grand galop in “Gustave.” That tremendous finale might symbolize an epoch in which for fifty years all things hurried by like a dream.

Now, the grave Thaddeus, with one divine and immaculate image in his heart, proposed to Malaga, queen of the carnival dances, to spend an evening at Musard; because he knew the countess, disguised, intended to come there with two friends, all three accompanied by their husbands, to look on at the curious spectacle.

On Shrove Tuesday, 1838, at four in the morning, the countess, wrapped in a black domino and sitting on the lower step of the platform in the Babylonian hall, saw Thaddeus, as Robert Macaire, threading the galop with Malaga in the dress of a savage, her head garnished with plumes like the horse of a hearse, bounding through the crowd like a will-o’-the-wisp.

“Ah!” said Clementine to her husband, “you Poles have no honor at all! I did believe in Thaddeus. He gave me his word that he would leave that woman; he did not know I should be here, unseen.”

A few days later she requested Paz to dine with them. After dinner Adam left them alone together, and Clementine reproved Paz and made plain she did not wish him to live in her house any longer.

“Yes, madame,” said Paz humbly, “you are right; I am a wretch; I did give you my word. But you see how it is; I put off leaving Malaga till after the carnival. Besides, that woman exerts an influence over me which—”

“An influence!—a woman who ought to be turned out of Musard’s by the police for such dancing!”

“I agree to all that; I accept the condemnation and I’ll leave your house. But you know Adam. If I give up the management of your property you must show energy yourself. I may have been to blame about Malaga, but I have taken the whole charge of your affairs, managed your servants, and looked after the least details. I cannot leave you until I see you prepared to continue my management. You have now been married three years, and you are safe from the temptations to extravagance which come with the honeymoon. I see that Parisian women—even titled ones—manage both fortunes and households. Well, as soon as I am certain not so much of your capacity as of your perseverance I shall leave Paris.”

“It is Thaddeus of Warsaw, and not that Circus Thaddeus who speaks now,” said Clementine. “Go, and come back cured.”

“Cured! never,” said Paz, his eyes lowered and fixed on Clementine’s pretty feet. “You do not know, countess, what charm, what unexpected piquancy of mind she has.” Then, feeling his courage fail, he added hastily, “There is not a woman in society, with her mincing airs, that is worth the honest nature of that young animal.”

“At any rate, I wish nothing of the animal about me,” said the countess, with an angry glance.

After that evening Comte Paz showed Clementine the exact state of her affairs; he made himself her tutor, taught her methods and difficulties of property management, the proper prices to pay, and how to avoid being cheated by servants. He told her she could rely on Constantin and make him major-domo. Thaddeus had trained the man thoroughly. By the end of May he judged the countess competent to carry on alone; for Clementine was one of those far-sighted women, full of instinct, who have an innate genius as mistress of a household.

This arrangement did not end without further cruel trials; his sufferings were not as sweet and tender as he tried to make them. The poor lover forgot to reckon on the hazard of events. Adam fell seriously ill, and Thaddeus, instead of leaving, stayed to nurse his friend. His devotion was unwearied. A woman who had any interest in employing her perspicacity might have seen in this devotion a sort of punishment imposed by a noble soul to repress an involuntary evil thought; but women see all, or see nothing—love is their sole illuminator.

For forty-five days Paz watched and tended Adam without appearing to think of Malaga, because he truly did not. Clementine, feeling Adam near death though he did not die, sent for the leading doctors of Paris in consultation.

“If he comes safely out of this,” said the chief of them, “it will only be by an effort of nature. It is for those who nurse him to watch for the moment when they must second nature. The count’s life is in the hands of his nurses.”

Thaddeus went to find Clementine and tell her this. He found her sitting in the Chinese pavilion, partly to rest and to leave the field to the doctors. As he walked along the winding path to the pavilion, Thaddeus felt in the depths like Dante’s abyss. The unfortunate man had never dreamed the possibility of becoming Clementine’s husband, and now he had drowned himself in a ditch of mud. His face convulsed with agony, his head like Medusa’s conveyed despair.

“Is he dead?” said Clementine.

“They have given him up; that is, they leave him to nature. Do not go in; they are still there, and Bianchon is changing the dressings.”

“Poor Adam! I ask myself if I have not sometimes pained him,” she said.

“You have made him very happy,” said Thaddeus; “you ought to be easy on that score, for you have shown every indulgence for him.”

“My loss would be irreparable.”

“But, dear, you judged him justly.”

“I was never blind to his faults,” she said, “but I loved him as a wife should love her husband.”

“Then you ought, in case you lose him,” said Thaddeus, in a voice Clementine had never heard, “to grieve less than if you lost a man who was your pride, your love, and all your life,—as some men are to you women. Surely you can be frank at this moment with a friend like me. I shall grieve, too; long before your marriage I had made him my child, I had sacrificed my life to him. If he dies I shall be without an interest on earth; but life is still beautiful to a widow of twenty-four.”

“Ah! but you know that I love no one,” she said, with the impatience of grief.

“You don’t yet know what it is to love,” said Thaddeus.

“Oh, as husbands are, I have sense enough to prefer a child like my poor Adam to a superior man. It is now over a month that we have been saying to each other, ‘Will he live?’ and these alternations have prepared me, as they have you, for this loss. I can be frank with you. Well, I would give my life to save Adam. What is a woman’s independence in Paris? the freedom to let herself be taken in by ruined or dissipated men who pretend to love her. I pray to God to leave me this husband who is so kind, so obliging, so little fault-finding, and who is beginning to stand in awe of me.”

“You are honest, and I love you the better for it,” said Thaddeus, taking her hand which she yielded, and kissing it. “In solemn moments like these there is unspeakable satisfaction in finding a woman without hypocrisy. It is possible to converse with you. Let us look to the future. Suppose God does not grant your prayer,—and no one cries to him more than I do, ‘Leave me my friend!’ Yes, these fifty nights have not weakened me; if thirty more days and nights are needed I can give them while you sleep,—yes, I will tear him from death if, as the doctors say, nursing can save him. But suppose that in spite of you and me, the count dies,—well, then, if you were loved, oh, adored, by a man of a heart and soul worthy of you—”

“I may have wished for such love, foolishly, but I have never met it.”

“Perhaps you are mistaken—”

Clementine looked fixedly at Thaddeus, imagining less love than cupidity in his thoughts; her eyes measured him and poured contempt upon him; then she crushed him with the words, “Poor Malaga!” uttered in tones only a great lady can find to express disdain. She rose, leaving Thaddeus half unconscious, slowly re-entered her boudoir, and went back to Adam’s chamber.

An hour later Paz returned to the sick-room, and began anew, with death in his heart, his care of the count. From that moment he said nothing. He struggled with the patient, whom he managed in a way that excited the doctors’ admiration. At all hours his watchful eyes were like lamps always lit. He showed no resentment to Clementine and listened to her thanks without accepting them; he seemed dumb and deaf. To himself he said, “She shall owe his life to me,” and wrote the thought in letters of fire on the walls of Adam’s room. On the fifteenth day Clementine was forced to give up nursing lest she utterly break down. Paz was unwearied. At last, toward the end of August, Bianchon told Clementine Adam was out of danger.

“Ah, madame, you are under no obligation to me,” he said; “without his friend, Comte Paz, we could not have saved him.”

The day after the meeting in the kiosk, the Marquis de Ronquerolles came to see his nephew. He was on the eve of starting for Russia on a secret diplomatic mission. Paz took occasion to say a few words to him. The first day Adam was able to drive out with his wife and Thaddeus, a gentleman entered the courtyard as the carriage was about to leave it, and asked for Comte Paz. Thaddeus, sitting on the front seat, took a letter stamped with the ministry of Foreign Affairs. Having read it, he pocketed it so Clementine and Adam could not speak of it. Nevertheless, by the time they reached the porte Maillot, Adam, curious as a sick man may be, said to Thaddeus: “There’s no indiscretion between brothers who love each other,—tell me what there is in that despatch; I’m in a fever of curiosity.”

Clementine glanced at Thaddeus with vexation and remarked to her husband: “He has been so sulky with me for the last two months that I shall never ask him anything again.”

“Oh, as for that,” replied Paz, “I can’t keep it out of the newspapers, so I may as well tell you at once. The Emperor Nicholas has had the grace to appoint me captain in a regiment which is to take part in the expedition to Khiva.”

“You are not going?” cried Adam.

“Yes, I shall go, my dear fellow. Captain I came, and captain I return. We shall dine together tomorrow for the last time. If I don’t start at once for St. Petersburg I shall have to go by land, and I am not rich, and I must leave Malaga a little independence. I ought to think of the only woman who has been able to understand me; she thinks me grand, superior. I dare say she is faithless, but she would jump—”

“Through the hoop, for your sake and come down safely on the back of her horse,” said Clementine sharply.

“Oh, you don’t know Malaga,” said the captain bitterly, with a sarcastic look that made Clementine thoughtful and uneasy.

“Good-by to the young trees of this beautiful Bois, which you Parisians love, and the exiles who find a home here love too,” he said. “My eyes will never again see the evergreens of the avenue de Mademoiselle, nor the acacias nor the cedars of the rond-points. On the borders of Asia, fighting for the Emperor, promoted to command, perhaps, by courage and risking my life, I may regret these Champs-Elysees where I have driven beside you, and where you pass. Yes, I shall grieve for Malaga’s hardness—the Malaga of whom I now speak.”

This was said in a manner that made Clementine tremble.

“Then you do love Malaga very much?” she asked.

“I have sacrificed for her the honor that no man should ever sacrifice.”

“What honor?”

“That which we desire to keep at any cost in the eyes of our idol.”

After that reply Thaddeus was silent until, as they passed a wooden building on the Champs-Elysees, he pointed and said, “That is the Circus.”

He went to the Russian Embassy before dinner, and thence to the Foreign office, and the next morning he had started for Havre before the count and countess were up.

“I have lost a friend,” said Adam, with tears, when he heard Paz had gone,—“a friend in the true meaning of the word. I don’t know what has made him abandon me as if a pestilence were in my house. We are not friends to quarrel about a woman,” he said, looking intently at Clementine. “You heard what he said yesterday about Malaga. Well, he has never so much as touched the little finger of that girl.”

“How do you know that?” said Clementine.

“I had the natural curiosity to go and see Mademoiselle Turquet, and the poor girl can’t explain even to herself the absolute reserve which Thad—”

“Enough!” said the countess, retreating into her bedroom. “Can it be that I am the victim of some noble mystification?” she asked herself. The thought had hardly crossed her mind when Constantin brought her the following letter written by Thaddeus during the night:—

“Countess,—To seek death in the Caucasus and carry with me your contempt is more than I can bear. A man should die untainted. When I saw you for the first time I loved you as we love a woman whom we shall love forever, even though she be unfaithful to us. I loved you thus,—I, the friend of the man you had chosen and were about to marry; I, poor; I, the steward,—a voluntary service, but still the steward of your household.

“In this immense misfortune I found a happy life. To be to you an indispensable machine, to know myself useful to your comfort, your luxury, has been a source of deep enjoyments. If these enjoyments were great when I thought only of Adam, think what they were to my soul when the woman I loved was the mainspring of all I did. I have known the pleasures of maternity in my love. I accepted life thus. Like the paupers who live along the great highways, I built myself a hut on the borders of your beautiful domain, though I never sought to approach you. Poor and lonely, struck blind by Adam’s good fortune, I was nevertheless the giver. Yes, you were surrounded by a love as pure as a guardian-angel’s; it waked while you slept; it caressed you with a look as you passed; it was happy in its own existence,—you were the sun of my native land to me, poor exile, who now writes to you with tears as he thinks of those first days.

“When I was eighteen, having no one to love, I took for my ideal mistress a charming woman in Warsaw, to whom I confided all my thoughts and wishes; I made her the queen of my nights and days. She knew nothing of it; why should she? I loved my love.

“You can fancy from this incident of my youth how happy I was merely to live in the sphere of your existence, to groom your horse, to find the new-coined gold for your purse, to prepare the splendor of your dinners and balls, to see you eclipsing the elegance of those whose fortunes were greater than yours, and all by my own management. Ah! with what ardor I have ransacked Paris when Adam would say to me, ‘She wants this or that.’ It was a joy I can never express. You wished for a trifle which kept me seven hours in a cab scouring the city; and what delight it was to weary myself for you. Ah! when I saw you, unseen by you, smiling among your flowers, I could forget that no one loved me. On some nights, when my happiness turned my head, I went and kissed the spot where your feet had left their traces. The air you had breathed was balmy; in it I breathed more life; I inhaled, as persons do in the tropics, a vapor laden with creative principles.

“I must tell you these things to explain the strange presumption of my involuntary thoughts—I would have died rather than avow it until now.

“You will remember those few days of curiosity when you wished to know the man who performed the household miracles you sometimes noticed. I thought,—forgive me, madame,—I believed you might love me. Your goodwill, your glances interpreted by me, a lover, seemed so dangerous—for me—that I invented that story of Malaga, knowing it was the sort of liaison which women cannot forgive. I did it in a moment when I felt that my love would be communicated, fatally, to you. Despise me, crush me with the contempt you have so often shown me when I did not deserve it; and yet I am certain that, if, on that evening when your aunt took Adam away from you, I had said what I now write, I should, like the tamed tiger that sets his teeth once more in living flesh, and scents the blood,—

“Midnight.

“I could not go on; the memory of that hour is still too living. Yes, I was maddened. Was there hope for me in your eyes? then victory with its scarlet banners would have flamed in mine and fascinated yours. My crime has been to think all this; perhaps wrongly. You alone can judge of that dreadful scene when I drove back love, desire, all the invincible forces of our manhood, with the cold hand of gratitude,—gratitude which must be eternal.

“Your terrible contempt has been my punishment. You have shown me there is no return from loathing or disdain. I love you madly. I should have gone had Adam died; all the more must I go because he lives. A man does not tear his friend from the arms of death to betray him. Besides, my going is my punishment for the thought that came to me that I would let him die, when the doctors said his life depended on his nursing.

“Adieu, madame; in leaving Paris I lose all, but you lose nothing now in my being no longer near you.

“Your devoted

“Thaddeus Paz.”

“If my poor Adam says he has lost a friend, what have I lost?” thought Clementine, sinking into a chair with her eyes fixed on the carpet.

The following letter Constantin had orders to give privately to the count:—

“My dear Adam,—Malaga has told me all. In the name of all your future happiness, never let a word escape you to Clementine about your visits to that girl; let her think Malaga has cost me a hundred thousand francs. I know Clementine’s character; she will never forgive you either your losses at cards or your visits to Malaga.

“I am not going to Khiva, but to the Caucasus. I have the spleen; and at the pace at which I mean to go I shall be either Prince Paz in three years, or dead. Good-by; though I have taken sixty-thousand francs from Nucingen, our accounts are even.

“Thaddeus.”

“Idiot that I was,” thought Adam; “I came near to cutting my throat just now, talking about Malaga.”

It is now three years since Paz went away. The newspapers have as yet said nothing about any Prince Paz. The Comtesse Laginska is immensely interested in the expeditions of the Emperor Nicholas; she is Russian to the core, and reads all the news from that distant land avidly. Once or twice every winter she says to the Russian ambassador, with an air of indifference, “Do you know what has become of our poor Comte Paz?”

Alas! most Parisian women, those who think themselves so clever and clear-sighted, pass and repass beside a Paz and never recognize him. Yes, many a Paz is unknown and misconceived, but—horrible to think!—some are misconceived even though they are loved. The simplest women in society exact a certain conventional sham from the greatest men. A noble love signifies nothing to them if rough and unpolished; it needs the cutting and setting of a jeweller to give it value in their eyes.

In January, 1842, the Comtesse Laginska, with her gentle melancholy, inspired a violent passion in the Comte de La Palferine, one of the most daring lions of the day. La Palferine knew that conquering a woman so guarded as the Comtesse was difficult, but he thought he could inveigle her by the aid of a certain friend of hers, a woman already jealous of her.

Quite incapable, despite her intelligence, of suspecting such treachery, the Comtesse imprudently went with her so-called friend to a masked ball at the Opera. About three in the morning, led by the excitement, Clementine, whom La Palferine had seduced, consented to accept a supper, and was about to enter the carriage of her faithless friend. At that moment her arm was grasped by a powerful hand, and she was taken, despite her struggles, to her own carriage, the door of which stood open though she had not known it was there.

“He has never left Paris!” she exclaimed as she recognized Thaddeus, who disappeared when the carriage drove away.

Did any woman ever have a like romance in her life? Clementine constantly hopes she may see Paz again.
