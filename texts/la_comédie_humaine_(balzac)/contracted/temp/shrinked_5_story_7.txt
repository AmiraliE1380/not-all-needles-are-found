The incident in this sketch occurred in late November 1809, at the height of Napoleon’s empire. The echoes of Wagram still haunted Austria as peace talks with the Coalition continued. Kings and princes orbited Napoleon, who reveled in his power and staged Parisian entertainments around his marriage to an Austrian archduchess. The French aristocracy’s new opulence—diamonds and gold on women and uniforms—contrasted with the Republic’s earlier austerity; military leaders indulged in wealth amassed through the sacrifices of countless soldiers.

Many women adopted a carefree, Louis XV–like attitude. Influenced by the fallen monarchy or the Imperial family, both sexes embraced a hedonism that seemed to foreshadow doom. The craze for military men served the Emperor, since frequent calls to arms made every peace temporary. Women’s affections shifted swiftly; many passed from mistress to widow in weeks.

Soldiers appealed as prospects of early widowhood, the lure of historical legacy, or the romantic ideal of courage. Women fancied their passions would remain secret, buried on the battlefield. This produced a culture that prized bravery and a mania for glittering possessions—diamonds, fireworks, and jeweled men modeled on figures like Murat.

Comte de Gondreville, formerly Citizen Malin, rose to prominence and delayed a grand entertainment to outshine rival courtiers and impress Napoleon. His drawing-rooms filled with ambassadors, influential figures, and princes expecting the Emperor. A private quarrel between Napoleon and Josephine, hinting at divorce, kept him away, unknown to the guests.

The most beautiful women in Paris flocked to Gondreville’s event, embodying luxury and elegance. The financial elite sought to rival military splendor, using grand balls to present daughters to Napoleon’s circle and trade wealth for favor. Amid the gaiety, smiles masked intent; friends were often treated as rivals in the pursuit of power and wealth.

These remarks are necessary to explain the incidents surrounding the little imbroglio that is the subject of this study, reflecting the tone prevalent in Paris drawing-rooms at the time.

“Look toward the pedestal supporting that candelabrum—do you see the young lady with her hair a la Chinoise? She’s in the corner to the left, with bluebells in her chestnut curls. She’s so pale you might think she’s ill, delicate-looking, and very small. Now she’s turning her head; her almond-shaped blue eyes seem made for tears. She’s leaning forward to see Madame de Vaudremont below the crowd.”

“I see her now. You only had to mention her lovely complexion; I would have recognized her. She has the whitest skin here, but she’s either a prude or a coquette; her bodice barely hints at her beauty. What shoulders! What lily-whiteness!”

“Who is she?” asked the first speaker.

“I don’t know.”

“An aristocrat! Do you want to keep them all to yourself, Montcornet?”

“You of all people to tease me!” replied Montcornet, smiling. “Do you think you can insult a poor general like me just because you’re a rival of Soulanges? Or is it because I’ve only just arrived in this Promised Land? Come, Monsieur le Maitre des Requetes, let us glean in the field you can only hold precariously. We have a right to live! If you knew the German women, you might help me with the Parisian you love best.”

“Well, General, since you’ve noticed that lady, have you seen her dance?”

“Where have you been? There’s a triple rank of the most undaunted coquettes of Paris between her and the swarm of dancing men. It’s a miracle anyone could spot her in that corner, buried in gloom despite the candles. Between her and us is a sparkle of diamonds and glances, so many floating plumes and fluttering lace that it’s impossible for any dancer to detect her among those stars. How could you think she’s the wife of some sous-préfet from Lippe or Dyle?”

“Oh, he will be!” exclaimed the Master of Appeals.

“I doubt it,” laughed the Colonel of Cuirassiers. “She seems as raw in intrigue as you are in diplomacy. I bet you don’t know how she got there.”

The lawyer looked at the Colonel with a mix of contempt and curiosity.

“Well,” Montcornet continued, “she probably arrived punctually at nine, the first of the company, and embarrassed the Comtesse de Gondreville, who can’t think straight. Repulsed by the mistress of the house and driven into the corner by jealous ladies, she became a victim of their coalition against her. Each of those sweet faces would have conspired to keep the men from taking her out. That’s how those tender-looking women would have formed a coalition against the stranger without saying a word beyond, ‘Do you know that little woman in blue?’ If you care to run the gauntlet of flattering glances and inviting questions, try to get through the rampart defending that Queen of Dyle, Lippe, or Charente. You’ll see how even the dullest woman will invent a wile to keep the plaintive stranger in the shadows. Doesn’t she look like an elegy?”

“Do you think so, Montcornet? Then she must be married?”

“Why not a widow?”

“She would be less passive,” said the lawyer, laughing.

“She might be the widow of a gambler,” replied the handsome Colonel.

“Of course; since the peace, there are many widows of that sort!” said Martial. “But my dear Montcornet, we are simpletons. That face is too innocent; there’s too much youth and freshness for her to be married. What splendid flesh-tints! Nothing has sunk in the modeling of her nose. Her lips and chin are as fresh as a white rosebud, though her expression is veiled by clouds of sadness. Who could make that young creature weep?”

“Women cry for little,” said the Colonel.

“I don’t know,” replied Martial; “but she’s not crying because she’s left without a partner; her grief is not new. It’s clear she beautified herself for this evening with intention. I would wager she’s in love already.”

“Bah! She’s perhaps the daughter of some German princeling; no one talks to her,” said Montcornet.

“Dear! How unhappy a poor child may be! Can there be anything more graceful and refined than our little stranger? Yet not one of those furies around her, who think they can feel, will say a word to her. If she would only speak, we’d see if she has fine teeth.”

“Bless me, you boil over like milk at the least increase of temperature!” cried the Colonel, nettled at finding a rival in his friend.

“What!” exclaimed the lawyer, ignoring the Colonel’s question. “Can nobody here tell us the name of this exotic flower?”

“Some lady companion!” said Montcornet.

“A companion! Wearing sapphires fit for a queen and a dress of Malines lace? Tell that to the marines, General. You wouldn’t shine in diplomacy if you jump from a German princess to a lady companion.”

Montcornet stopped a man by taking his arm—a fat little man with iron-gray hair and clever eyes, mingling unceremoniously with various groups.

“Gondreville, my friend,” said Montcornet, “who is that charming little woman sitting under that huge candelabrum?”

“The candelabrum? Ravrio’s work; Isabey made the design.”

“Oh, I recognized your lavishness and taste; but the lady?”

“Ah! I don’t know. Some friend of my wife’s, no doubt.”

“Or your mistress, you old rascal.”

“No, on my honor. The Comtesse de Gondreville is the only one capable of inviting people no one knows.”

Despite this acerbic comment, the fat little man’s lips retained a smile from the Colonel’s suggestion. Montcornet returned to the lawyer, who had rejoined a neighboring group, intent on asking, but in vain, for information about the fair unknown. He grasped Martial’s arm and said in his ear:

“Mind what you’re about. Madame de Vaudremont has been watching you for some time with ominous attentiveness; she can guess by the movement of your lips what you say to me. Our eyes have already told her too much; she’s thinking even more than we are about the little blue lady.”

“That’s too old a trick in warfare, my dear Montcornet! However, what do I care? Like the Emperor, when I make a conquest, I keep it.”

“Martial, your fatuity cries out for a lesson. You, a civilian, and so lucky as to be the husband-designate of Madame de Vaudremont, a widow of two-and-twenty with four thousand napoleons a year—who slips such a diamond on your finger,” he added, taking the lawyer’s left hand, which the young man complacently allowed; “and you affect the Lovelace, as if you were a colonel obliged to maintain the military reputation! Fie! Just think of all you may lose.”

“At any rate, I shall not lose my liberty,” replied Martial with a forced laugh.

He cast a passionate glance at Madame de Vaudremont, who responded only with a smile of unease, having seen the Colonel examining the lawyer’s ring.

“Listen to me, Martial. If you flutter around my young stranger, I shall set to work to win Madame de Vaudremont.”

“You have my full permission, my dear Cuirassier, but you will not gain this much,” and the young Maitre des Requetes put his polished thumb-nail under an upper tooth with a mocking click.

“Remember that I am unmarried,” said the Colonel; “that my sword is my whole fortune; and that such a challenge is setting Tantalus down to a banquet he cannot devour.”

“Prrr.”

This defiant roll of consonants was the only reply to the Colonel’s declaration, as Martial looked him over before turning away.

The fashion of the time required men to wear white kerseymere breeches and silk stockings at a ball. This costume showcased Montcornet’s fine shape. At thirty-five, he attracted attention with his stalwart height, emphasized by the handsome uniform of the Cuirassiers of the Imperial Guard. A black moustache highlighted his soldierly countenance, with a broad forehead, aquiline nose, and bright red lips. Montcornet’s manner, marked by a certain superiority from his command experience, might please a woman sensible enough not to aim at making a slave of her husband. The Colonel smiled as he looked at the lawyer, one of his favorite college friends, whose small figure made it necessary for Montcornet to look down slightly as he answered his raillery with a friendly glance.

Baron Martial de la Roche-Hugon was a young Provencal favored by Napoleon; his fate might lead to a splendid embassy. He had won the Emperor with his Italian suppleness, a talent for intrigue, and a knowledge of manners, which are good substitutes for the higher qualities of a sterling man. Though young and eager, his face had acquired the rigid brilliancy of tinned iron, a characteristic of diplomatists that allows them to conceal emotions. The heart of a diplomat may be an insoluble problem, as the most illustrious ambassadors of the time were distinguished by enduring hatreds and romantic attachments.

Martial, however, was one of those men capable of reckoning on the future amid their enjoyment; he had learned to judge the world and hid his ambition under the guise of a lady-killer, cloaking his talent under the commonplace of mediocrity as he observed the rapid advancement of those who gave the master little umbrage.

The two friends parted with a cordial handshake. The introductory tune, signaling the ladies to form squares for a fresh quadrille, cleared the men from the space they had filled while talking in the large drawing-room of Gondreville’s mansion. Their whispered conversation had taken place during the usual interval between dances. The chandeliers and flambeaux on the chimney-shelf illuminated their faces, failing to conceal the faint expressions of their feelings from the keen-sighted countess or the artless stranger. This espionage of thoughts is perhaps one of the pleasures idle people find in society, while many disappointed individuals are bored without daring to admit it.

To fully appreciate the interest of this conversation, it’s necessary to relate an incident that would serve as an invisible bond, drawing together the actors in this little drama, currently scattered through the rooms.

At about eleven o’clock, just as the dancers were returning to their seats, the company observed the entrance of the handsomest woman in Paris, the queen of fashion, the only person missing from the brilliant assembly. She made it a rule never to appear until a party reached a pitch of excitement that doesn’t allow women to preserve the freshness of their faces or dresses. This brief hour is the springtime of a ball. An hour later, when pleasure falls flat and fatigue sets in, everything is spoiled. Madame de Vaudremont never committed the blunder of remaining at a party to be seen with drooping flowers, hair out of curl, tumbled frills, and a face courting sleep—not always without success. She took care not to let her beauty be seen drowsy, as her rivals did; she cleverly maintained her reputation for smartness by always leaving a ballroom in brilliant order, as she had entered it. Women whispered with envy that she planned and wore as many different dresses as parties she attended in one evening.

On this occasion, Madame de Vaudremont was not destined to leave when she wished. Pausing on the threshold, she shot swift, observant glances at the women present, hastily scrutinizing their dresses to assure herself that her own eclipsed them all.

The illustrious beauty presented herself to the admiration of the crowd at the same moment as one of the bravest colonels of the Guards’ Artillery and the Emperor’s favorite, the Comte de Soulanges. The transient association of these two had an air of mystery. Upon hearing the names announced, a few women sitting by the wall rose, and men hurried in from side-rooms, pressing forward to the principal doorway. One of the jesters in the assembly remarked that “women have as much curiosity about seeing a man faithful to his passion as men do in studying a woman difficult to enthrall.”

Though the Comte de Soulanges, a young man of about thirty-two, possessed a nervous temperament that gives rise to fine qualities, his slender build and pale complexion were not initially attractive. His black eyes revealed great vivacity, but he was taciturn in company, and nothing in his appearance hinted at the oratory that would later distinguish him in the legislative assembly under the Restoration.

The Comtesse de Vaudremont, a tall woman, somewhat plump, with dazzlingly white skin and a small head carried well, inspired love through her gracious manner. She was one of those beings who keep all the promise of their beauty.

The pair, for a few minutes, became the center of general observation, but did not long give curiosity a chance to exercise itself. The Colonel and the Countess seemed to understand that accident had placed them in an awkward position. Martial, as they approached, hastened to join a group of men by the fireplace, watching Madame de Vaudremont with the jealous anxiety of a first flame of passion from behind a barrier of heads. A secret voice warned him that the success he prided himself on might be precarious. However, the coldly polite smile with which the Countess thanked Monsieur de Soulanges and her little bow of dismissal as she sat down by Madame de Gondreville relaxed the rigidity of his face, which jealousy had made tense. Seeing Soulanges still standing near the sofa where Madame de Vaudremont sat, apparently unaware of the glance that conveyed to him that they were both playing a ridiculous part, the volcanic Provencal knitted his brows and smoothed his chestnut curls to maintain his composure, watching the faces of the Countess and M. de Soulanges while still chatting with his neighbors. He then took the hand of Colonel Montcornet, who had just renewed their acquaintance, but he listened without hearing him; his mind was elsewhere.

Soulanges gazed calmly at the women, sitting four ranks deep around the immense ballroom, admiring the display of diamonds, rubies, gold, and shining hair, which almost outshone the blaze of waxlights, the cut glass of the chandeliers, and the gilding. His rival’s indifference unsettled the lawyer. Unable to control his impatience, Martial approached Madame de Vaudremont with a bow. Upon seeing the Provencal, Soulanges cast him a covert glance and impertinently turned away his head. A solemn silence reigned in the room, where curiosity peaked. Eager faces wore strange mixed expressions; everyone anticipated an outbreak that men of breeding carefully avoid. Suddenly, the Count’s pale face turned as red as the scarlet facings of his coat, and he fixed his gaze on the floor to hide his agitation. Spotting the unknown lady humbly seated by the pedestal of the candelabrum, he moved away with a melancholy air, passing in front of the lawyer, and took refuge in one of the cardrooms. Martial and the company thought Soulanges had publicly surrendered his position out of fear of the ridicule that follows a discarded lover. The lawyer proudly raised his head and looked at the strange lady; then, as he took his seat near Madame de Vaudremont, he listened so inattentively that he missed her words spoken behind her fan:

“Martial, please don’t wear that ring you took from me. I have my reasons and will explain them when we leave. You must give me your arm to go to the Princess de Wagram’s.”

“Why did you come in with the Colonel?” asked the Baron.

“I met him in the hall,” she replied. “But leave me now; everybody is looking at us.”

Martial returned to the Colonel of Cuirassiers. It was then that the little blue lady became the object of curiosity that agitated the Colonel, Soulanges, Martial, and Madame de Vaudremont.

When the friends parted after their challenge, the Baron flew to Madame de Vaudremont and led her to a place in the most brilliant quadrille. Favored by the intoxication dancing produces in a woman and the turmoil of a ball, where men appear in all the trickery of dress, which enhances their attractions as much as it does women’s, Martial thought he might yield to the charm that drew his gaze to the fair stranger. Though he managed to hide his first glances towards the lady in blue from the Countess’s watchful eyes, he was soon caught in the act; and while he excused himself once for his absent-mindedness, he could not justify the unseemly silence that followed the most insinuating question a woman can ask:

“Do you like me very much this evening?”

The more dreamy he became, the more the Countess pressed and teased him.

While Martial danced, the Colonel moved from group to group, seeking information about the unknown lady. After exhausting the good humor of even the most indifferent, he resolved to take advantage of a moment when the Comtesse de Gondreville seemed at liberty to ask her the name of the mysterious lady. He noticed a little space cleared between the pedestal of the candelabrum and the two sofas, which ended in that corner. The dance had left several chairs vacant, forming rows of fortifications held by mothers or middle-aged women; the Colonel seized the opportunity to make his way through this palisade of shawls and wraps. He began by making himself agreeable to the dowagers, and from one to another, through compliments, he finally reached the empty space next to the stranger. At the risk of catching on to the gryphons and chimeras of the huge candelabrum, he stood there, braving the glare and dripping wax, to Martial’s annoyance.

The Colonel, too tactful to speak suddenly to the little blue lady on his right, began by addressing a plain woman seated on the left:

“This is a splendid ball, madame! What luxury! What life! Every woman here is pretty! You’re not dancing—because you don’t care for it, no doubt.”

This vapid conversation aimed to induce his right-hand neighbor to speak; however, she, silent and absent-minded, paid no attention. The officer had prepared several phrases leading to: “And you, madame?”—a question from which he hoped for great things. But he was surprised to see tears in the strange lady’s eyes, which seemed wholly absorbed in gazing at Madame de Vaudremont.

“You are married, no doubt, madame?” he asked hesitantly.

“Yes, monsieur,” replied the lady.

“And your husband is here, of course?”

“Yes, monsieur.”

“And why, madame, do you remain in this spot? Is it to attract attention?”

The mournful lady smiled sadly.

“Allow me the honor, madame, of being your partner in the next quadrille, and I will ensure not to bring you back here. When so many are ready to ascend the throne, and Royalty is the mania of the day, I cannot imagine you will refuse the title of Queen of the Ball that your beauty may claim.”

“I do not intend to dance, monsieur.”

The curt tone of her replies was so discouraging that the Colonel found himself compelled to withdraw. Martial, guessing what the officer’s last request had been and the refusal he had met with, began to smile, stroking his chin and making the diamond on his finger sparkle.

“What are you laughing at?” asked the Comtesse de Vaudremont.

“At the poor Colonel’s failure, who just put his foot in it----”

“I begged you to take your ring off,” said the Countess, interrupting him.

“I did not hear you.”

“If you can hear nothing this evening, at least you see everything, Monsieur le Baron,” said Madame de Vaudremont, vexed.

“That young man is displaying a very fine diamond,” the stranger remarked to the Colonel.

“Splendid,” he replied. “The man is the Baron Martial de la Roche-Hugon, one of my most intimate friends.”

“I thank you for telling me his name,” she said; “he seems an agreeable man.”

“Yes, but he is rather fickle.”

“He seems to be on the best terms with the Comtesse de Vaudremont?” asked the lady, looking inquiringly at the Colonel.

“On the very best.”

The unknown turned pale.

“Hallo!” thought the soldier, “she is in love with that lucky devil Martial.”

“I fancied Madame de Vaudremont had long been devoted to M. de Soulanges,” said the lady, recovering from the suppressed grief that had clouded her beauty.

“For a week past, the Countess has been faithless,” replied the Colonel. “But you must have seen poor Soulanges when he came in; he’s still trying to disbelieve in his disaster.”

“Yes, I saw him,” said the lady. Then she added, “Thank you very much, monsieur,” in a tone that signified dismissal.

At that moment, the quadrille was coming to an end. Montcornet had only time to withdraw, telling himself, “She is married.”

“Well, valiant Cuirassier,” exclaimed the Baron, drawing the Colonel aside into a window-bay to breathe the fresh air from the garden, “how are you getting on?”

“She is a married woman, my dear fellow.”

“What does that matter?”

“Oh, deuce take it! I’m a decent sort of man,” replied the Colonel. “I have no intention of courting a woman I cannot marry. Besides, Martial, she expressly told me she did not intend to dance.”

“Colonel, I will bet a hundred napoleons to your gray horse that she will dance with me this evening.”

“Done!” said the Colonel, putting his hand in the coxcomb’s. “Meanwhile, I’m going to look for Soulanges; he might know the lady, as she seems interested in him.”

“You’ve lost, my good fellow,” cried Martial, laughing. “My eyes have met hers, and I know what they mean. My dear friend, you owe me no grudge for dancing with her after she has refused you?”

“No, no. Those who laugh last, laugh longest. But I’m an honest gambler and a generous enemy, Martial, and I warn you, she is fond of diamonds.”

With these words, the friends parted; General Montcornet made his way to the cardroom, where he saw the Comte de Soulanges sitting at a bouillotte table. Though there was no friendship between the two soldiers beyond the superficial comradeship from the perils of war and service, the Colonel of Cuirassiers was alarmed to see the Colonel of Artillery, known to be prudent, playing a game that could lead to ruin. The heaps of gold and notes piled on the fateful cards showed the frenzy of play. A circle of silent men stood around the players at the table. Occasionally, a few words were spoken—pass, play, I stop, a thousand Louis, taken—but the five motionless men seemed to converse only with their eyes. As the Colonel, alarmed by Soulanges’ pallor, approached him, the Count was winning. Field-Marshal the Duc d’Isemberg, Keller, and a famous banker rose from the table, completely cleaned out of considerable sums. Soulanges looked gloomier than ever as he swept up a quantity of gold and notes; he didn’t even count it; his lips curled with bitter scorn, defying fortune rather than being grateful for her favors.

“Courage,” said the Colonel. “Courage, Soulanges!” He tried to pull him from the gaming table. “Come with me. I have good news, but on one condition.”

“What is that?” asked Soulanges.

“That you will answer a question.”

The Comte de Soulanges rose, gathering his winnings; his look was so fierce no one stopped him; the players seemed relieved at his departure.

“Those soldiers are always as thick as thieves at a fair!” observed a diplomat who took Soulanges’ place. A single tired face turned to the newcomer and said, “When we say military men, we do not mean civil, Monsieur le Ministre.”

“My dear fellow,” Montcornet said to Soulanges, drawing him aside, “the Emperor praised you this morning, and your promotion to field-marshal is certain.”

“The Master does not love the Artillery.”

“No, but he adores the nobility, and you are an aristocrat. He said that men who married in Paris during the campaign are not in disgrace. Well?”

Soulanges looked confused.

“And now I hope you will tell me if you know a charming woman sitting under that huge candelabrum—”

At this the Count’s face brightened; he seized the Colonel’s hand. “My dear General,” he said, his voice altered, “if anyone else had asked me that, I would have cracked his skull with this gold. Leave me, I beg you. I feel more like blowing out my brains tonight than— I hate everything I see. I am going. This gaiety, this music, these stupid faces, all laughing, are killing me!”

“My poor friend!” Montcornet replied, squeezing the Count’s hand gently. “You are too vehement. What would you say if I told you that Martial is thinking so little of Madame de Vaudremont that he is quite smitten with that little lady?”

“If he speaks to her,” Soulanges shouted, trembling with rage, “I will thrash him flat, even if he’s in the Emperor’s lap!”

He sank into an easy chair; seeing Soulanges beyond consolation, Montcornet withdrew.

Back in the ballroom Montcornet noticed Madame de Vaudremont, agitated. He sat beside her.

“I wager something vexes you?” he said.

“A mere trifle, General. I want to leave for a ball at the Grand Duchess of Berg’s, but I must first stop at the Princesse de Wagram’s. Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon amuses himself by flirting with the dowagers.”

“That’s not the whole secret. I bet a hundred louis you will stay here all evening.”

“Impertinent man!”

“You are afraid of seeing Martial at the feet—”

“Of whom?” she exclaimed, feigning surprise.

“Of that candelabrum,” he replied, glancing at the fair stranger.

“You have guessed it,” she said, hiding her face behind her fan. “Old Madame de Lansac, as malicious as an old monkey, told me that Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon flirts with that stranger, who sits like a skeleton at a feast. I would rather see a death’s head than that face—so cruelly beautiful and pale. She is my evil genius. Madame de Lansac, who only comes to a ball to watch everything while pretending to sleep, has made me miserable. Martial shall pay dearly for this trick. Urge him, since he is your friend, not to make me unhappy.”

“I just left a man who promises to blow his brains out if he speaks to that lady. And he is a man, madame, to keep his word. But I know Martial; such threats encourage him. Besides, we have wagered—” The Colonel lowered his voice.

“Can it be true?” asked the Countess.

“On my word of honor.”

“Thank you, my dear Colonel,” Madame de Vaudremont replied, her glance inviting. “Will you do me the honor of dancing with me?”

“Yes; but the next quadrille. During this one, I want to find out what will come of this little intrigue and ascertain who the little blue lady is; she looks intelligent.”

Pleased to have made a start, the Colonel retired.

At such entertainments, older women like Madame de Lansac gather to watch younger people’s affairs. The Duchess of Lansac, a shrewd, mischievous dowager from an earlier generation, read hearts as if reading a book: a faint frown, a quiver, the slightest curl of the lips revealed impressions. From her large armchair she observed the young coquette who disguised annoyance as cheerfulness; Madame de Vaudremont felt as much sorrow as she feigned. She had believed Martial a man of talent who could adorn her life with power; now she realized her mistake. Her sudden passion for Martial, though recent, caused acute pain, like the amputation of a healthy limb.

Seizing an opportunity to interfere, the old Duchess dismissed her Ambassador and turned all her attention to the couple. Her sardonic glance frightened the Countess, who suspected the dowager’s hand in the stranger’s presence. Madame de Lansac, tall and tastefully dressed with diamonds and rouge, had eyes that promised epigrams; her politeness softened a mischievous mind. Her triumphant glance across the room brought a flush of hope to the pale cheeks of the young woman under the chandelier; the alliance between the Duchess and the stranger did not escape Madame de Vaudremont’s practiced eye, and she was determined to uncover the mystery.

The Baron de la Roche-Hugon, having questioned the dowagers without learning the blue lady’s name, turned in despair to the Comtesse de Gondreville, who said the lady had been introduced by the “ancient” Duchesse de Lansac. By chance the Baron caught the intelligence in the Duchess’s look aimed at the stranger and, despite their previous coldness, approached. The Duchess, smiling with sardonic irony, looked at Madame de Vaudremont; Montcornet laughed.

“If the old witch pretends to be friendly,” thought the Baron, “she is certainly going to play me a spiteful trick.” He addressed her: “Madame, you have, I am told, undertaken the charge of a very precious treasure.”

“Do you take me for a dragon?” said the old lady. “But of whom are you speaking?” she asked sweetly, reviving Martial’s hopes.

“Of that little lady, unknown to all, whom the jealousy of these coquettes has imprisoned in that corner. You, no doubt, know her family?”

“Yes,” said the Duchess. “But what concern have you with a provincial heiress, married some time ago, a woman of good birth, who goes nowhere.”

“Why does she not dance? She is such a pretty creature. May we conclude a treaty of peace? If you tell me all I want to know, I promise to urge a petition for the restitution of the woods of Navarreins by the Commissioners of Crown Lands on the Emperor.”

The Baron’s suggestion to petition for an estate belonging to the elder branch of Navarreins—an appeal to the Duchess’s past influence—was trickery revealing his ambition. The younger branch of Navarreins used the arms of Lansac; the duchess’s past favors had once secured her husband a title. The Duchess, with deceptive gravity, answered: “Monsieur, bring the Comtesse de Vaudremont to me. I promise to reveal the mystery of the interesting unknown. You see, every man in the room shares your curiosity. All eyes are turned to the corner where my protégée has modestly seated herself; she reaps the homage the women wished to deprive her of. Happy the man she chooses for her partner!” Fixing her eyes on Madame de Vaudremont she added, with a look that plainly said, “We are talking of you. I imagine you would rather learn the stranger’s name from your handsome Countess than from me.”

There was such marked defiance in the Duchess’s attitude that Madame de Vaudremont rose, approached her, and took the chair Martial had placed for her. Without acknowledging him she said, “I can guess, madame, that you are talking of me; but I admit my lack of perspicacity; I do not know whether it is for good or evil.”

Madame de Lansac pressed the young woman’s hand in her dry fingers and replied in a low, compassionate tone, “Poor child!”

The women looked at each other. Madame de Vaudremont understood that Martial was in the way and dismissed him with an imperious “Leave us.” The Baron, displeased to see the Countess under the spell of the dangerous sibyl, gave one of those decisive looks men use—potent over a blinded heart but ridiculous to a woman beginning to judge the man who attracted her.

Martial, a man of the world with wit and acumen, would not risk breaking with a woman favored at Court whom the Emperor wished to see married. He counted on jealousy as the surest means of discovering the secret of the Countess’s coolness and withdrew willingly as a new quadrille began.

The Baron leaned against a marble console, watching the two ladies talk. Comparing the Countess with the new beauty, made attractive by mystery, he fell prey to the self-interest common to adventurous lady-killers, hesitating between a fortune within reach and indulging his caprice. The light outlined his anxious face against white silk hangings, making him resemble an evil genius; observers might have said, “There is another poor wretch who seems to be enjoying himself!”

Meanwhile the Colonel, leaning in the doorway between ballroom and cardroom, laughed under his moustache. He enjoyed watching the dance’s turmoil, reading the Countess’s and Martial’s agitation, and wondering about the connection between Soulanges’ gloomy looks and the fair unknown’s face, which alternated between hope and dread. Montcornet, like a king of the feast, received inviting smiles; as Colonel of the Imperial Guard he was a fine match in the army.

It was nearly midnight. Conversation, gambling, dancing, flirtations, rivalries, and scheming reached a fervor that made a young man exclaim, “A fine ball!”

“My sweet little angel,” Madame de Lansac said to the Countess, “you are now at an age when I made many mistakes. Seeing you endure a thousand deaths, I thought I might give you some charitable advice. To go wrong at two-and-twenty means spoiling your future; it is like tearing the gown you must wear. My dear, it is not much later that we learn to go about in it without crumpling it. Go on, sweetheart, making clever enemies and friends without sense of conduct, and you will see what a pleasant life you will lead!”

“Oh, madame, it is very hard for a woman to be happy, do you not think?” the Countess eagerly exclaimed.

“My child, at your age, you must learn to choose between pleasure and happiness. You want to marry Martial, who is not wise enough to be a good husband, nor passionate enough to remain a lover. He is in debt, my dear; he is the man to squander your fortune; still, that would be nothing if he could make you happy. Do you not see how aged he is? He must have been ill; he is making the most of what is left him. In three years he will be a wreck. Then he will be ambitious; perhaps he may succeed. I do not think so. What is he? A man of intrigue, who may have business acumen and be able to gossip agreeably, but he is too presumptuous to have any real merit; he will not go far. Besides—look at him. Is it not written on his brow that, at this very moment, what he sees in you is not a young and pretty woman, but the two million francs you possess? He does not love you, my dear; he is calculating you as if you were an investment. If you are bent on marrying, find an older man with an assured position who is halfway in his career. A widow’s marriage should not be a trivial love affair. Is a mouse to be caught a second time in the same trap? A new alliance should be a good speculation on your part, and in marrying again, you ought at least to hope to be addressed as Madame la Maréchale!”

Her advice combined worldly calculation with pity: prudence, rank, and financial security over a romantic match with Martial, whom she painted as indebted, aging, and opportunistic. The Countess listened, torn between feeling and practical counsel. The salon’s intrigues continued around them—wagers, furtive glances, jealous men and calculating women—while the mystery of the pale stranger and the designs of the Duchess, the Colonel, the Baron, and Martial rotated like the dance itself, each player seeking advantage among love, ambition, and social maneuvering.

As she spoke, both women turned to Colonel Montcornet’s handsome face.

“If you prefer to flirt rather than marry,” the Duchess said bluntly, “you, better than anyone, know how to stir and then calm trouble. But do not disturb families or ruin wives’ happiness. I played that dangerous game. For a moment of vanity a virtuous woman can be destroyed—there are truly virtuous women—and we become mortally hated. I learned too late that, as the Duc d’Albe said, one salmon is worth a thousand frogs. Genuine affection brings far more happiness than fleeting passions. I came to preach to you; you are the reason for my visit to this lower-class house. We used to receive actors in our boudoir, never in the drawing-room! If you must play with men, toy only with those whose lives are unsettled and who have no duties. The others will not forgive us for the errors that make them happy. Take this advice from my long experience. Look at Soulanges: you’ve turned him into a lovesick fool for fifteen months. He’s been married two years to a charming wife whom he loves but neglects; she lives in tears. You’ve affected his whole life. He has felt remorse far worse than any pleasure you gave him. And you, little minx, have abandoned him. Come and see the result.”

Taking Madame de Vaudremont’s hand, the old lady rose. “There,” Madame de Lansac said, pointing to a pale, trembling figure under the candlelight, “that is my grandniece, the Comtesse de Soulanges. Today she finally yielded to my persuasion and left the sorrowful room where her child gives her little consolation. You think her charming? Imagine how she must have looked when happiness and love lit that now-blighted face.”

The Countess looked away, lost in thought. The Duchess led her toward the card-room door and, scanning the crowd, said, “And there is Soulanges!”

Soulanges’s appearance confirmed the Duchess’s words: pale, rigid in an easy chair, his indifference and the tension in his brow revealing deep suffering. Players passed him as if he were dead. The image of the tearful wife and morose husband, separated amid festivity, struck the Countess with foreboding about her own future. Though not wholly hardened, she pressed the Duchess’s hand in a childlike thanks.

“My dear child,” the old lady whispered, “remember, we can repel a man’s attentions as easily as we attract them.”

“She is yours if you’re not a fool,” Madame de Lansac murmured to Colonel Montcornet while the Countess remained absorbed in compassion for Soulanges, still loving him enough to wish to restore his happiness.

“Oh! I will talk to him!” the Countess declared.

“Do nothing of the sort!” the old lady exclaimed, returning to her chair. “Choose a good husband and shut your door to my nephew. A wife cannot accept her husband’s heart as a gift from another woman; she is far happier believing she has won it back. By bringing my niece here, I believe I’ve given her a chance to regain her husband’s affection. All I need from you is to play the Colonel.” She pointed to Montcornet, and the Countess smiled.

When they were alone, Martial asked the Countess if she knew the name of the unknown woman. “Yes,” she replied, playful. Martial, still believing she loved him, adopted a charming air. “Will you be annoyed if I seem to care about that name?”

“Will you be annoyed if a remnant of affection prevents me from telling you? And if I forbid you to approach that young lady? It could be dangerous.”

“To lose your favor would be worse than losing my life.”

“Martial,” she said sternly, “she is Madame de Soulanges. Her husband would kill you if he knew.”

“Ha! So the Colonel can leave the man in peace who has taken his love, yet would fight for his wife! What a reversal of principles! I beg you to let me dance with her. You’ll see how little love that icy heart could feel for you; if the Colonel allows me to dance with his wife after letting me—”

“But she loves her husband.”

“An even greater obstacle I shall enjoy overcoming.”

“But she is married.”

“A whimsical objection!”

“Ah!” the Countess said with a bitter smile, “you punish us for both our faults and our repentance!”

“Do not be angry!” Martial begged. “Forgive me. I will think no more of Madame de Soulanges.”

“You deserve that I should send you to her.”

“I’m off then,” he laughed, “and I’ll return more devoted to you than ever. You’ll see that the prettiest woman cannot capture the heart that is yours.”

“Meaning you want to win Colonel Montcornet’s horse?”

“Ah! Traitor!” he cried, playfully threatening his friend. The Colonel smiled and joined them; the Baron gave him a seat near the Countess, saying with sardonic tone, “Here, madame, is a man who boasted he could win your favor in one evening.”

He left, thinking he had cleverly piqued the Countess’ pride and done Montcornet a disservice, unaware of the irony in Madame de Vaudremont’s words: both men were unconsciously drawn toward one another.

At that moment the lawyer approached Madame de Soulanges, who sat pale and timid, seemingly alive only in her eyes. Her husband entered the ballroom with flashing anger. The Duchess, ever watchful, rushed to her nephew, asking for his arm to find her carriage, feigning boredom to prevent a scene. Before leaving she shot a knowing glance at her niece, signaling her to take revenge.

Madame de Vaudremont noticed the exchange and felt a sudden realization: the duplicitous Duchess, preaching morality, might be plotting against her. Pride stirred more than curiosity to unravel the intrigue, and in her distraction she lost control. The Colonel, misreading her embarrassment, grew more ardent. Old diplomats, entertained by the unfolding drama, perhaps had never seen so many simultaneous intrigues; the passions of the two couples were evident in the crowded rooms, reflected on surrounding faces and amplifying their own impotence.

Finally, the Baron found a seat beside Madame de Soulanges. He admired her beauty up close—the fresh neck, the graceful figure emphasized by the fashionable sash tied under the bosom—and was captivated by her perfection. “You haven’t danced once this evening, madame,” he said softly. “Not for lack of a partner, I presume?”

“I never go to parties; I’m quite unknown,” Madame de Soulanges replied coldly, unaware of her aunt’s signal to attract the Baron.

Martial, adjusting the diamond on his hand, felt a spark of hope as he caught the Countess’s eye. She blushed and looked at him with an indefinable expression. “Do you enjoy dancing?” he asked.

“Yes, very much, monsieur.”

Their eyes met; the earnestness in her voice touched him. “Then, am I too bold in offering to be your partner for the next quadrille?”

Artless confusion colored her cheeks. “But, monsieur, I have already refused one partner—a military man—”

“Was it that tall cavalry colonel over there?”

“Precisely.”

“Oh! He’s a friend of mine; don’t worry. Will you grant me the favor I hope for?”

“Yes, monsieur.”

Her tone revealed a new, deep emotion that stirred Martial’s world-weary heart. Overcome with shyness, he lost some confidence; his southern charm then ignited. The quadrille formed, and standing beside her eased his nerves. For men like Martial, dancing was a way to express themselves and attempt to win a woman’s heart by physical grace; his movements suggested he intended to show every charm he possessed.

He led his conquest to the quadrille, where the most brilliant women prized dancing above all. As the orchestra played, the Baron surveyed the ladies forming the square with pride. Madame de Soulanges’ dress rivaled Madame de Vaudremont’s; all eyes briefly fell on her, and a flattering murmur betrayed the men’s admiration. Her blush increased her charm. When she raised her eyes she looked at her partner as if to transfer that glory to him; she seemed to prize his opinion above all, blending innocence and vanity with the budding affection of youth. Modest and inexperienced in ballroom arts, she nevertheless knew when to glance at him.

When a new figure invented by a dancer named Trenis brought Martial face to face with the Colonel, the latter joked, “I have won your horse.” Montcornet retorted, “Yes, but you have lost eighty thousand francs a year!” Martial dismissed it lightly: “What do I care? Madame de Soulanges is worth millions!”

After the quadrille whispers circulated. The less attractive women made moral comments about Martial and the Comtesse; the more beautiful marveled at her apparent ease in surrendering to him. The men, puzzled by the Baron’s luck, did not consider him particularly attractive. A few sympathetic women advised caution, warning that if a mere glance or graceful dance could compromise a young wife, many would be ruined.

Only Martial grasped his happiness. During the last figure, as the ladies formed the moulinet, he clasped the Countess’s hand and felt her response through the thin glove. “Madame,” he said as the quadrille ended, “do not return to that corner where you’ve been hiding. Is admiration the only fruit you derive from your jewels? Come and enjoy the scene and yourself.”

Yielding to his charm, Madame de Soulanges walked through the crowded rooms with him, hesitating at each doorway until he reassured her, “He is not here.” They reached a grand picture gallery with a lavish collation for three hundred guests. As supper approached, Martial led her to an oval boudoir overlooking the garden, draped with flowers and blue hangings. The noise faded. Startled at first, the Countess resisted, then sat on an ottoman after glancing in a mirror, seemingly reassured they could be seen.

“This room is charming,” she remarked, admiring the decor. “All here is love and delight!” Martial, deeply moved, watched her face in the dim light: unease, modesty, eagerness—an expression that captivated him. She smiled, calming her inner struggle. Flirtatiously she took his left hand and slipped the ring from it, exclaiming, “What a fine diamond!”

Martial, intoxicated by her touch, urged, “Wear it, in memory of this hour, and for the love of—” He broke off, caught by her rapture. “You give it to me?” she asked. “I wish I had the whole world to offer you!” he replied. “Will you accept only my diamond?” She pressed, “You will never take it back?” “Never,” he assured her. She placed the ring on her finger. As Martial moved to embrace her, she suddenly rose and said clearly, “I accept the diamond, monsieur, with less scruple because it belongs to me.”

The Baron was taken aback. “Monsieur de Soulanges took it from my dressing-table and told me he had lost it,” she said. “You are mistaken, madame,” Martial protested irritably. “It was given to me by Madame de Vaudremont.” “Precisely,” she replied with a smile. “My husband borrowed it, gave it to her, and she gifted it to you. This ring has made a little journey, that is all. It may reveal secrets to me.” She touched a spring in the ring; a lock of M. de Soulanges’ hair appeared. Stunned, she fled into the crowded rooms, leaving Martial confounded.

He noticed the Colonel and Madame de Vaudremont laughing nearby; the Colonel teased, “Will you have my horse to chase after your prize?” Martial took their banter in stride. Grateful for their silence regarding the evening’s events, he felt he had exchanged his horse for a young wife.

As the Comtesse de Soulanges drove home across Paris to the Faubourg Saint‑Germain, anxiety consumed her. Before leaving the Hotel Gondreville she had tried to find her aunt and husband, who had departed without her. Disturbing suspicions plagued her: she had silently witnessed her husband’s torment since Madame de Vaudremont had ensnared him and had hoped for his repentance. Now she feared she had erred by consenting to her aunt’s scheme.

The evening darkened her innocent spirit. Alarmed first by her husband’s suffering, she became more distressed at her rival’s beauty and the weight of societal corruption. Crossing the Pont Royal, she discarded the hair from the diamond—the only token of pure affection—and wept, recalling her long-standing grief and shuddering at the thought that a woman seeking peace must bury her pain without complaint.

“Alas!” she pondered. “What can women do when they do not love? What sustains their devotion? I cannot believe, as my aunt claims, that reason alone is enough.”

Still sighing, she arrived home and rushed upstairs, startled to find her husband by the fire. “How long has it been since you attended balls without telling me?” he demanded, his voice strained. “You know a woman is out of place without her husband. You compromised yourself by hiding in the dark corner.”

“Oh, my dear Léon,” she coaxed, “I couldn’t resist the joy of seeing you without your knowing. My aunt took me to the ball, and I was very happy there!”

Her words softened the Count’s sternness; he had dreaded her return, fearing she would learn of his infidelity. Delighted to see him smile and grateful for his presence in a room he had frequented less often, the Countess looked at him tenderly, blushing. Her kindness further enchanted Soulanges, especially after the misery he had endured at the ball. He took her hand and kissed it gratefully; gratitude often accompanies love.

“Hortense, what is that on your finger that hurt my lip?” he asked laughing. “It’s my diamond, which you said you lost, and which I have found.”

General Montcornet did not marry Madame de Vaudremont despite their brief understanding; she later perished in the tragic fire that marred the ball hosted by the Austrian ambassador for Napoleon’s marriage to the daughter of Emperor Joseph II.
