**Summary of "Pierrette" by Honoré De Balzac**

**Dedication**
Honoré De Balzac dedicates "Pierrette" to Mademoiselle Anna Hanska, reflecting on the melancholy nature of the story and the importance of addressing sorrows, especially for someone cherished like Anna.

**I. The Lorrains**
In October 1827, a sixteen-year-old boy named Jacques Brigaut stands in a small square in Lower Provins, observing the houses and enjoying the morning silence. He is searching for a specific house, which belongs to Pierrette Lorrain, a girl he has feelings for. The house is newly renovated, contrasting with the older buildings around it. As he admires the details, he notices a calico curtain fluttering in an attic window, which prompts him to sing a traditional Breton wedding song.

As he sings, Pierrette appears at the window, and they exchange greetings. Brigaut reveals he has returned from Paris and wishes to settle near her. However, when a noise from below alarms Pierrette, she urges him to flee. He escapes just as an old maid, who lives in the house, awakens and looks out the window, missing him.

Pierrette, left alone, reflects on her life. She has endured three years of sorrow and feels trapped in her current situation. Memories of her childhood flood back, contrasting sharply with her present misery. She recalls her family history, including her mother, Madame Lorrain, who married a Breton officer, Major Lorrain, and their struggles after his death. Pierrette's mother inherited little after her father's death, and her subsequent marriage to a physician led to further financial ruin.

Pierrette's grandparents, the Lorrains, were unable to support her after their own financial decline. They had to send her to live with her relatives, the Rogrons, in Provins. The Rogrons, who were unaware of Pierrette's existence until a letter from her grandparents reached them, became her guardians. This unexpected turn of events set the stage for Pierrette's future, which was now in the hands of her new guardians.

The narrative highlights the themes of social class, familial duty, and the impact of financial struggles on personal relationships. Pierrette's delicate health and emotional turmoil reflect the broader societal issues of the time, particularly for women in precarious situations. The story foreshadows the challenges Pierrette will face as she navigates her new life with the Rogrons, who are characterized by their self-interest and lack of compassion. 

Overall, "Pierrette" serves as a poignant exploration of the intersection between personal aspirations and societal constraints, encapsulated in the life of a young girl caught in the web of her family's misfortunes.

**Summary of "The Rogrons"**

Pere Rogron, an innkeeper in Provins, is characterized by his unattractive appearance and selfish nature. He and his equally unappealing wife live off an annual income of about two thousand francs from their land and the sale of their inn. After selling the inn, they move into a dilapidated house inherited from old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandfather. The Rogrons have two children, both of whom are raised poorly, leading to their physical and emotional degeneration. Rogron's parenting philosophy is blunt: he intends to give his children minimal support and expects them to fend for themselves.

Sylvie, the daughter, is sent to Paris as an apprentice at a shop, where she eventually becomes a forewoman. Her brother, Jerome-Denis, follows a similar path, and together they save enough money to buy a retail establishment called the “Family Sister.” Despite their success, both siblings are unattractive and lack social skills, leading to a reputation for being miserly and unpleasant among their peers.

Sylvie is the more competent of the two, managing the business effectively while Jerome lacks the ability to grasp the broader aspects of their trade. Their lives revolve around their shop, and they are oblivious to the world outside of it. They dream of returning to Provins, where they long to improve their inherited property, but their aspirations are tinged with nostalgia and frustration.

When a letter from the Lorrains arrives, seeking to place their orphaned cousin Pierrette in the Rogrons' care, the siblings debate the implications. They remember their family history and view Pierrette as a potential threat to their inheritance. Ultimately, they decide to refuse the request, prioritizing their own interests over familial obligations.

Four years later, the Rogrons return to Provins, where their actions will significantly impact Pierrette’s life, setting the stage for future conflicts. Their story illustrates themes of selfishness, familial duty, and the harsh realities of social ambition.

### Summary of "Pathology of Retired Mercers"

When a provincial shopkeeper returns from Paris, he brings back some ideas but soon loses them to the habits of provincial life. This transition from shopkeeper to bourgeois often feels like an illness, as they struggle to adapt to the quieter lifestyle. With their savings, they indulge in long-held desires, whether through travel, politics, or hobbies like hunting or fishing. The Rogrons, a brother-sister duo, exemplify this as they remodel their old house into a fashionable new one, driven by a desire to emulate the wealthier bourgeois of Provins.

The renovations reflect their aspirations, with the contractor meticulously consulting them on every detail, emphasizing the importance of having items that were previously owned by local notables. The Rogrons' first year back in Provins is consumed by discussions about their home and attempts to integrate into local society, which they had previously avoided. They reconnect with former acquaintances, including the Julliards, Guepins, and Guenees, who dominate the social scene.

Madame Tiphaine, the belle of the town, is determined to elevate her husband, Monsieur Tiphaine, to a deputy position, using her charm to gain influence. She hosts gatherings that draw the local bourgeoisie, while the Rogrons, eager to fit in, struggle with their lack of social grace. Sylvie Rogron's attempts at card games reveal her poor manners, and her brother, Rogron, is socially awkward, leading to their exclusion from the elite circles.

Despite their efforts, the Rogrons are perceived as vulgar and unrefined, leading to their social ostracism. Their lavish dinners, intended to showcase their wealth, are met with indifference, and they soon find themselves isolated. Sylvie's attempts to maintain their social standing fail, and she resorts to frugality, dismissing their previous extravagant lifestyle.

As time passes, the Rogrons become increasingly lonely, reminiscing about their past business and feeling the weight of their isolation. They engage in mundane routines, with Rogron obsessively checking his barometer and seeking gossip from townsfolk. Their social ambitions crumble, and they find themselves in a state of ennui, longing for the excitement of their former lives.

In a bid to revitalize their household, Sylvie decides to invite her cousin Pierrette Lorrain to live with them, hoping to use her as a means to regain social standing. However, their plans are complicated by the revelations of Pierrette's unfortunate circumstances and the Rogrons' past misdeeds regarding family property. Their attempts to ingratiate themselves with the upper class backfire, leading to further alienation.

The Rogrons' growing resentment towards the local elite culminates in their alignment with the town's marginalized figures, such as Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet, who see potential in exploiting the Rogrons' wealth and ignorance for their own political ambitions. As the Rogrons navigate their new social landscape, they unwittingly become pawns in a larger game of political maneuvering, revealing the complexities of ambition, class, and social dynamics in provincial life.

**Summary of "Pierrette"**

After her grandparents entered a hospital, Pierrette Lorrain, feeling the weight of living on charity, was relieved to learn she had wealthy relatives. Brigaut, her childhood friend and an apprentice cabinet-maker, offered her money for her journey to Paris, which she accepted without thanks, reflecting their deep bond.

In October 1824, at eleven years old, Pierrette left Nantes, entrusted to a diligence conductor who took her to her cousins, Mademoiselle Sylvie and Rogron. Upon arrival, Pierrette was greeted with indifference, treated more like a package than a family member. Despite her modest attire, she radiated innocence and charm, but her cousins were more concerned with the costs associated with her arrival than with her well-being.

Sylvie and Rogron were astonished by Pierrette's presence, but their initial kindness quickly turned cold. They were preoccupied with her clothing needs, criticizing her lack of proper attire and planning an expensive wardrobe for her. Pierrette, who had previously known love and freedom in Brittany, felt a stark contrast in her new home, where her cousins' harshness and obsession with material possessions stifled her spirit.

As Pierrette settled into her new life, she was subjected to constant scrutiny and reprimands from her cousins. They were more focused on their financial investment in her clothing than on nurturing her happiness. Pierrette's innocent nature clashed with their coldness, leading her to feel increasingly isolated and misunderstood.

Despite her cousins' attempts to dress her like a little lady, Pierrette's playful spirit often led to wear and tear on her clothes, resulting in further criticism. The Rogrons' harshness transformed their relationship with Pierrette from one of initial curiosity to one of tyranny, as they imposed their rigid expectations on her.

The arrival of Vinet and Colonel Gouraud, who sought to manipulate the Rogrons for their own gain, further complicated Pierrette's situation. They encouraged Sylvie and Rogron to adopt a more adversarial stance against their social rivals, deepening their resentment and neglect towards Pierrette.

As Pierrette's life in Provins progressed, she became increasingly confined and controlled, losing the joy and freedom she once enjoyed. Her cousins' obsession with propriety and appearances stifled her natural vivacity, forcing her to suppress her true self in order to avoid their wrath. The once carefree child was now trapped in a cycle of fear and repression, struggling to navigate the harsh realities of her new life.

### Summary of "History of Poor Cousins in the Home of Rich Ones"

The narrative begins with Pierrette, a young girl living with her wealthy cousins, the Rogrons. During a gathering, she is coldly dismissed by Sylvie, her cousin, which deeply hurts her. Pierrette expresses her longing for the affection she received from her grandmother, contrasting it with the indifference of her new guardians. Sylvie and her husband, Vinet, dismiss Pierrette's feelings, emphasizing that her grandmother took her money while they would leave her theirs.

As Pierrette adjusts to her new life, she is subjected to harsh treatment and scolding from the Rogrons, who view her as a burden. They impose strict educational expectations on her, leading to feelings of inadequacy and despair. Pierrette's attempts to please them are met with criticism, and she becomes increasingly isolated, suffering both physically and emotionally.

Despite being given a writing master, Pierrette's learning is fraught with difficulties, and she is often punished for her perceived shortcomings. The Rogrons' lack of kindness and understanding exacerbates her struggles, leading her to feel like a servant in their home. The only person who shows her any warmth is Adele, the servant, but even this small comfort is taken away when Sylvie decides Pierrette should take on household duties herself.

As time passes, Pierrette's health deteriorates under the oppressive atmosphere of the Rogron household. Her cousins' relentless criticism and lack of affection contribute to her physical and emotional decline. The narrative highlights the stark contrast between Pierrette's innocent nature and the coldness of her relatives, illustrating the harsh realities faced by those who are dependent on the whims of wealthier family members.

The story also delves into the political and social dynamics of the time, with the Rogrons becoming involved in local politics through their connections with Vinet and Colonel Gouraud. Their ambitions lead to the establishment of a local newspaper, which further entangles them in the community's power struggles.

As Pierrette approaches her first communion, she finds solace in her faith, which offers her a sense of hope amidst her suffering. However, the Rogrons remain indifferent to her spiritual growth, viewing her as an obstacle to their own aspirations. The arrival of Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, a beautiful young woman, complicates matters further, as she becomes a rival for Rogron's affections and a source of jealousy for Sylvie.

Ultimately, the narrative paints a bleak picture of Pierrette's life, marked by neglect and cruelty from her relatives, who prioritize their ambitions over her well-being. The story concludes with Pierrette's health declining further, leaving her in a state of despair as she grapples with the harsh realities of her existence in the Rogron household.

**Summary of "An Old Maid's Jealousy"**

Before delving into the domestic turmoil brought about by Jacques Brigaut's arrival in the Rogron family, it is essential to understand how he came to be in Provins. Brigaut, deeply affected by Pierrette's distress, fled from her home, shaken by her transformation from a lively child to a sickly shadow of herself. He climbed to a vantage point to observe her house, consumed by worry for his friend, realizing the depth of his feelings for her.

Brigaut, at sixteen, was determined to improve himself for Pierrette's sake. He returned to Provins after a brief stint in Paris, securing a position as a journeyman with Monsieur Frappier, the town's best cabinet-maker. His dedication to his trade and his desire to protect Pierrette drove him to work hard, earning a modest living while keeping a watchful eye on her.

As Brigaut settled into his new life, he learned about the Rogrons' dubious acquisition of Pierrette's grandfather's property and the oppressive nature of her living situation. He often saw Pierrette in the market, where she appeared burdened and unhappy. Despite his longing to approach her, he sensed that she was in distress and wished to keep his distance for her sake.

Meanwhile, Pierrette was grappling with her own struggles. She felt the weight of her cousin Sylvie's jealousy and suspicion, which had intensified following Brigaut's arrival. Sylvie, an old maid, was fixated on marrying Colonel Gouraud, and her jealousy of Pierrette grew as she perceived a potential romantic connection between Pierrette and the colonel. Sylvie's insecurities and fears of aging led her to consult a doctor about the risks of marriage at her age, further complicating her feelings.

The dynamics within the Rogron household were fraught with tension. Sylvie's jealousy manifested in her treatment of Pierrette, whom she accused of having a secret admirer. Pierrette, innocent and unaware of the implications of Sylvie's accusations, remained silent, fearing the repercussions of speaking out. Sylvie's relentless scrutiny and harsh words exacerbated Pierrette's fragile health, leading her to suffer in silence.

As the story unfolds, Sylvie's jealousy becomes increasingly irrational. She misinterprets Pierrette's innocence as guilt and plots to rid herself of her cousin, convinced that Pierrette's presence threatens her chances with the colonel. The tension culminates in a breakfast scene where Sylvie's accusations and Pierrette's distress reach a breaking point, leaving Pierrette feeling isolated and ill.

Ultimately, the narrative explores themes of jealousy, innocence, and the complexities of familial relationships. Brigaut's love for Pierrette contrasts sharply with Sylvie's bitterness, highlighting the destructive nature of envy and the impact it has on those caught in its web. As Pierrette's health deteriorates under the strain of her cousin's jealousy, the story foreshadows a tragic outcome, driven by misunderstandings and the harsh realities of their lives.

### Summary of "Domestic Tyranny"

For a month, Colonel Gouraud had stopped delivering the “Constitutionnel” to the Rogrons, instead visiting them to collect the paper and engage in light conversation. Sylvie, eager to see him, dressed attractively and waited for his arrival with her brother, Rogron, who was reluctant to leave the house. When Gouraud entered, he complimented Sylvie's appearance, but she feigned concern about her cousin's illness to avoid going out.

Gouraud felt uneasy about his partnership with Vinet, who seemed to dominate their political endeavors, leaving Gouraud feeling sidelined. He was also aware of Rogron's growing infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, which made him wary of his own standing. To gauge Rogron's feelings, Gouraud mentioned his interest in Bathilde, causing Rogron to react with jealousy and hostility.

As Gouraud conversed with Sylvie, he suspected her intentions were influenced by Vinet, who had suggested he marry Pierrette instead. Sylvie, however, was determined to keep Pierrette under her control and was suspicious of any connection between her cousin and the colonel. Despite Gouraud's attempts to express his affection for Sylvie, she remained calculating and cautious, wanting to test his loyalty.

Sylvie's jealousy intensified when she noticed Pierrette's growing beauty and began to plot against her cousin. She sent Pierrette to dress nicely for dinner, suspecting that the colonel might be interested in her. Meanwhile, Bathilde and her mother arrived, with Bathilde dressed to impress and determined to win Rogron's affection. Vinet had encouraged Bathilde to see marriage as a means to gain power and influence, rather than for love.

During the evening, tensions rose as the guests played cards. Sylvie's jealousy flared when she believed Pierrette had helped Gouraud win a hand. After a confrontation, Pierrette fled the room, hitting her head in the process. Sylvie, indifferent to Pierrette's injury, focused on her own frustrations with the colonel and the evening's events.

The next day, Pierrette's injury became apparent, and Sylvie cruelly attributed it to divine punishment for disobedience. Rogron, showing a rare moment of concern, suggested care for Pierrette, but Sylvie dismissed it, revealing her harshness and control over her cousin. The dynamics of power, jealousy, and manipulation within the household continued to unfold, highlighting the domestic tyranny Sylvie exerted over Pierrette and the tensions among the characters.

**Summary of "The Loves of Jacques and Pierrette"**

The week for Pierrette was filled with torment, primarily due to her cousin Sylvie's cruel treatment. Pierrette suffered both physically and emotionally, particularly because she was determined to keep silent about Brigaut's arrival, fearing for his safety. Despite her pain, the thought of Brigaut brought her solace, akin to an exile longing for home.

Brigaut, aware of Pierrette's struggles, devised a plan to communicate with her by having her lower a letter from her window at midnight. Their mutual longing and understanding allowed them to connect despite their separation. One day in the market, Brigaut managed to slip a note to Pierrette, which she cherished deeply. The letter expressed his love and concern for her well-being, urging her to escape her oppressive situation and return to Brittany.

Pierrette, moved by Brigaut's words, wrote back, revealing her suffering and longing for freedom. She described the harsh treatment she received from her cousins and her desire to reunite with her grandmother. After a careful exchange of letters, Pierrette felt a renewed sense of hope, believing that Brigaut would help her escape her misery.

However, Sylvie grew suspicious of Pierrette's newfound happiness and began to monitor her closely. Pierrette, despite her illness, clung to the hope of Brigaut's nightly visits. When she fainted during a gathering, the colonel, who had feelings for her, expressed concern, but Sylvie dismissed it as a ruse.

As tensions escalated, Sylvie discovered Pierrette's secret correspondence and confronted her. In a violent struggle, Sylvie attempted to seize Pierrette's letter, leading to a brutal confrontation that left Pierrette injured. Just as Sylvie's cruelty reached its peak, Pierrette's grandmother arrived, having been alerted by Brigaut. The old woman intervened, taking Pierrette away from the oppressive environment.

Pierrette's grandmother, filled with anguish upon seeing her granddaughter's condition, vowed to seek medical help. She learned of the mistreatment Pierrette endured and resolved to take action against the Rogrons. Meanwhile, Brigaut, concerned for Pierrette's health, sought a doctor to ensure her recovery.

As Pierrette recuperated, her grandmother provided comfort and support, promising a better life for them both. The arrival of her grandmother and the prospect of a future with Brigaut filled Pierrette with hope, contrasting sharply with the torment she had faced. The narrative concludes with the promise of healing and the possibility of a brighter future, underscoring the themes of love, resilience, and the quest for freedom from oppression.

**Summary of "The Family Council"**

Monsieur Martener visits Monsieur Tiphaine to discuss the mistreatment of Pierrette by the Rogrons, which has led to her severe illness. The ongoing feud between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions in Provins intensifies as the Rogrons spread damaging rumors about the Tiphaine family. Tiphaine sees Pierrette's case as an opportunity to undermine the Rogrons, who are politically opposed to him.

Tiphaine consults with notary Auffray and Martener to strategize on how to legally remove Pierrette from the Rogrons' guardianship. They plan to convene a Family Council to appoint Auffray as her guardian, allowing them to seek her release from the Rogrons' control. News of Pierrette's distress spreads through Provins, leading to public outrage.

As the situation escalates, Vinet, the Rogrons' lawyer, learns of the potential criminal charges against them. He manipulates the Rogrons into agreeing to a marriage contract with Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, hoping to gain public favor and distract from the allegations against them. The court proceedings begin, with the Tiphaines and their supporters rallying around Pierrette, while the Rogrons attempt to defend their actions.

Pierrette is moved to a safer environment under Auffray's care, where she receives medical attention for her injuries. The doctors confirm that her condition is a result of the Rogrons' abuse, leading to a court ruling that prevents her return to their home. Despite Vinet's efforts to discredit Pierrette, the Family Council ultimately decides to remove Rogron as her guardian.

As the legal battle continues, Pierrette's health deteriorates. She expresses her love for her grandmother and Brigaut, her friend, while forgiving her cousins for their mistreatment. Tragically, she passes away, prompting a public funeral that the Rogrons dare not attend due to the backlash.

Brigaut, devastated by her death, constructs her coffin and vows to seek justice. The legal proceedings against the Rogrons collapse with Pierrette's passing, and Vinet exploits the situation for his political gain. The narrative concludes with reflections on the societal indifference to Pierrette's suffering and the moral decay of those involved, drawing parallels to historical injustices.

In the aftermath, the characters involved in Pierrette's life continue their lives, with Vinet rising in political power, while Brigaut becomes a soldier, haunted by his memories of Pierrette. The story serves as a critique of the legal system and societal norms that allow such tragedies to occur without accountability.
