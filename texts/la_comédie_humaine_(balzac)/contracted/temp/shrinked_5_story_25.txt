The story opens in a lawyer's office where clerk Simonnin mocks Colonel Chabert, an elderly man who seeks attorney M. Derville. Treated with scorn, Chabert reveals he is a significant figure believed dead after the Battle of Eylau, exposing societal disdain for those who have fallen from grace in post-war France.

Desperate to reclaim his identity and fortune, Chabert insists on meeting Derville despite the clerks' derision. Derville, shocked by Chabert's gaunt appearance, listens as Chabert recounts surviving severe wounds and being left for dead, only to return and find his wife, Countess Ferraud, remarried and in possession of his estate. Moved, Derville agrees to help.

Three months later, Crottat seeks the return of 600 francs given to Chabert. Derville learns Chabert’s affairs were mismanaged by a disreputable lawyer and that the widow inherited a substantial sum while Chabert lives in squalor with a dairyman, Vergniaud. Visiting Chabert, Derville finds him dignified despite his condition. Chabert hopes to regain his rank as a general, but Derville warns of legal and social complexities and the likely court battle against his wife. Chabert places his trust in Derville.

Derville meets Vergniaud, who requests financial help and expresses concern for Chabert. Derville agrees but insists Chabert contribute to his own support. He confronts Madame Ferraud, who at first dismisses Chabert’s survival but grows defensive when legal consequences are mentioned. Derville suggests a compromise to avoid public scandal and benefit both parties.

A week later, Chabert and Madame Ferraud arrive at Derville's office. The Countess resists annulling Chabert’s death certificate. When Chabert presses her about her reluctance and monetary terms, she storms out; Derville warns him of her manipulations.

At her country house, the Countess confesses love for Comte Ferraud. Hurt, Chabert proposes they part with dignity, but she attempts to charm him while scheming to protect her position. When she suggests committing him to an asylum, Chabert’s trust collapses; he rejects her and resolves to disappear from her life.

Chabert’s later life is marked by poverty. Derville, thinking a compromise was reached, remains unaware of Chabert’s decline until he finds him imprisoned as a vagabond—stripped of comforts but still proud. In a final act of integrity, Chabert tries to repay Derville, showing loyalty despite betrayal. Derville facilitates payment owed by the Countess, underscoring the tragic consequences of their choices. Ultimately Chabert’s story reflects on identity, love, and the harsh realities of post-war society; in June 1840, attorney Godeschal visits Bicêtre with Derville to find Chabert a pauper, displaying childlike innocence and the disillusionment faced by Napoleon’s veterans.
