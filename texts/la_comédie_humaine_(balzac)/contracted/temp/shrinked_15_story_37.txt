PAZ

(La Fausse Maitresse)

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

Dedicated to the Comtesse Clara Maffei.

PAZ

(LA FAUSSE MAÎTRESSE)

I

In September, 1835, one of the richest heiresses of the faubourg Saint-Germain, Mademoiselle du Rouvre, the only daughter of the Marquis du Rouvre, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a young Polish exile.

We write these Polish names as pronounced to spare readers the Slav consonant fortifications.

The Marquis du Rouvre had squandered nearly a princely fortune originally obtained through his marriage to a Demoiselle de Ronquerolles. On her mother’s side Clémentine du Rouvre had the Marquis de Ronquerolles for an uncle, and Madame de Serizy for an aunt. On her father’s side another uncle was the eccentric Chevalier du Rouvre, an old bachelor enriched by land speculation. The Marquis de Ronquerolles lost both his children to cholera; Madame de Serizy’s only son perished in Africa. By a curious turn, Clémentine became, despite her father’s dissipation, a great heiress. The Marquis de Ronquerolles, Madame de Serizy, and the Chevalier du Rouvre agreed to settle fortunes on their niece, each pledging ten thousand francs a year from her marriage.

Comte Adam Laginski, though an exile, was not an expense to France. He belonged to an ancient Polish family allied to princely houses. But heraldry counted for little with the rising bourgeoisie of Louis-Philippe’s Paris. When Adam first appeared in 1833 on the boulevard des Italiens and at the Jockey-Club he lived the life of a young man taking pleasure in Parisian dissipation; at first he was thought a student.

By this period Polish nationality had fallen in French estimation because of political reactions. The Movement against Resistance had made a conquered people, long celebrated in France, the butt of ridicule. Poles were associated with the republican party, though Poland was a republic of aristocrats. The bourgeoisie treated Polish exiles with contempt in 1835. There are indeed two sorts of Polish exiles: republican Poles, sons of Lelewel, and noble Poles, led by Prince Adam Czartoryski—fire and water. Exiles always bring their hatreds and divisions. This explains virulent attacks on Czartoryski and the dislike of noble exiles by Parisian shopkeeping Caesars.

In 1834 Adam Mitgislas Laginski was a butt for Parisian pleasantry.

“He is rather nice, though he is a Pole,” said Rastignac.

“All these Poles pretend to be great lords,” said Maxime de Trailles, “but this one does pay his gambling debts, and I begin to think he must have property.”

Without wishing to offend, one may note that the light-hearted, inconsistent Sarmatian character partly justified Parisian satire; the French would behave similarly in the same circumstances. The French aristocracy, helped by Polish lords during the Revolution, did not later repay their kindness; the faubourg Saint-Germain is still indebted to Poland.

Was Comte Adam rich, poor, or an adventurer? The question remained. Diplomatic salons, following official caution, treated Polish exiles discreetly, leaving them in social solitude. In a city of pleasure like Paris, a Pole’s heedless gaiety found ample encouragement to dissipation.

Adam had two points against him—his appearance and his mental habit. There are two species of Pole as there are two of Englishwoman: when an Englishwoman is not handsome she seems horribly ugly. Comte Adam looked pinched. His small, sharp face seemed pressed in a vise; his short nose, fair hair, and reddish beard gave him a goatish look, and his tarnished yellow eyes were oblique. Yet he had exquisite manners and a distinguished air, due partly to his dress and partly to training by his mother, a Radziwill. His courage was daring; his mind suited to ephemeral Parisian conversation. Young men of fashion talked too much then; real conversation needs leisure and social inequalities. Consequently, the dandies did not see the great seigneur in the careless young fellow who flitted from subject to subject, intent on amusement and freed from family constraints.

But one day in 1834 Adam suddenly bought a house in the rue de la Pépinière. Six months later his style of living rivaled the best in Paris. About the time he took himself seriously he had seen Clémentine du Rouvre at the Opera and fallen in love. A year later they were married. The salon of Madame d’Espard first sounded his praises. Mothers of daughters learned too late that the Laginski family was among the most illustrious of the North. By an act of prudence very un-Polish, his mother had mortgaged her property during the insurrection for an immense sum lent by two Jewish bankers in Paris. Comte Adam was now in possession of eighty thousand francs a year. When this was discovered society ceased to be surprised at the imprudence laid to the charge of Clémentine’s guardians. People jumped from one extreme to the other.

During the winter of 1836 Comte Adam was the fashion, and Clémentine Laginska one of the queens of Paris. Madame Laginska joined that circle of young women—Mesdames de Lestorade, de Portenduere, Marie de Vandenesse, du Guénic, and de Maufrigneuse—flowers of Paris, distinct from parvenus and speculators of the new regime.

This preamble shows the sphere in which one of those noble actions, less rare than calumniators admit, occurred—actions like pearls hidden in rough shells and lost in the tossing waves of society.

If architecture expresses manners and morals, it was proved after the insurrection of 1830. As old fortunes diminish, majestic mansions are demolished and replaced by phalansteries; peers of July occupy upper floors above newly enriched empirics below. A confused mixture of styles is used. Lacking a court or real nobility to set a tone, art has no harmony; yet architects find economical ways to imitate the real and solid and display resources in distributing space. Propose to an architect to build in a garden and he will run up a little Louvre with a courtyard, stables, and, if desired, a garden. Inside are many small rooms and passages; the eye is deceived into thinking there is space. It is still a nest of small rooms where a ducal family must turn about in space that once was a bakehouse.

The hôtel of the Comtesse Laginska, rue de la Pépinière, is one of these creations, standing between court and garden. On the right are kitchens and offices; to the left the coachhouse and stables. The porter’s lodge is between two charming portes-cochères. The chief luxury is a greenhouse at the end of a ground-floor boudoir opening on an admirable suite of reception rooms. An English philanthropist had built this bijou, designed the garden, added the greenhouse, polished doors, bricked the courtyard, and realized a dream resembling George IV’s Pavilion at Brighton. Parisian workmen moulded the doors; ceilings imitated the middle ages or a Venetian palace; marble veneering clung to outer walls. Steinbock and François Souchet designed mantelpieces and panels; Schinner painted ceilings. The staircase, white as a woman’s arm, rivaled that of the hôtel Rothschild. On account of riots and unsettled times the cost of this folly was about eleven hundred thousand francs. All this luxury, called princely by those who do not know real princes, was built in the garden of a house that had belonged to a purveyor who made a fortune by the Revolution, fled to Brussels, and died bankrupt. The Englishman died in Paris; his widow, a Methodist, ordered the establishment sold. Comte Adam bought it at a bargain; how he came to do so will be made known, for bargains were not in his line as a grand seigneur.

Behind the house lay the velvety lawn shaded by exotic trees, among which stood a Chinese pagoda. The greenhouse concealed the northern wall; climbing plants covered the opposite wall on poles painted green. This lawn, flower world, gravelled paths, simulated forest, and palisades were contained in twenty-five square rods, now worth four hundred thousand francs. Here, in solitude in the middle of Paris, birds sang. The greenhouse was an immense jardinière filling the air with perfume. Hot-water or steam pipes were cleverly concealed. The boudoir was large. Modern Parisian Architecture’s miracle is to get many things out of a limited bit of ground.

The boudoir of the young countess suited the taste of the artist who decorated the house. It was too full of pretty nothings to be a place of repose; one scarcely knew where to sit among carved Chinese work-tables, inlaid ivories, yellow topaz cups, mosaics, Dutch pictures, angels, and statuettes. Superb sketches by the best artists, lids of chests made into panels, fluted draperies of Italian silk, portières hanging from rods of old oak, Persian rugs, et cetera. For a last graceful touch, these elegant things were subdued by half-light filtered through embroidered curtains. On a table, among curiosities, lay a whip with a handle designed by Mademoiselle de Fauveau, showing that the countess rode.

Such is a lady’s boudoir in 1837—an exhibition of shop contents meant to amuse the eye, as if ennui were the worst danger in a social world that fears nothing more. Why is there nothing of an inner life? Because no one then was sure of the future; we live like prodigal annuitants.

One morning Clémentine seemed to be thinking. She lay full length on a marvellous couch, the greenhouse doors open letting tropical perfume pervade the room. The young wife looked at her husband who smoked a narghile, the only pipe she would suffer there. The portières gave a vista through two elegant salons, one white and gold, the other in a Renaissance style. The dining-room, rivalled only by the Baron de Nucingen’s, was at the end of a gallery decorated in the middle-ages manner. This gallery opened on the courtyard antechamber, through which could be seen the beautiful staircase.

The count and countess had just finished breakfast; the sky was blue and April nearly over. Married two years, Clémentine had discovered a secret or mystery in her household. The Pole is usually helpless before a woman; he is full of tenderness and becomes her inferior; Polish women make admirable wives. A Pole is even more vanquished by a Parisian woman. Comte Adam, pressed by questions, did not try roguery; he merely stipulated that he should not answer until he had finished his narghile.

“If any difficulty occurred when we were traveling,” said Clémentine, “you always dismissed it by saying, ‘Paz will settle that.’ You never wrote but to Paz. At home everybody keeps saying, ‘the captain, the captain.’ If I want the carriage—‘the captain.’ Is there a bill to pay—‘the captain.’ If I want my horse bitted, they must speak to Captain Paz. In short, it is like a game of dominoes—Paz is everywhere. I hear of nothing but Paz, but I never see Paz. Who and what is Paz? Why don’t you bring forth your Paz?”

“Isn’t everything going on right?” asked the count, taking the bocchettino from his narghile.

“Everything is going on so right that other people with an income of two hundred thousand francs would ruin themselves by going at our pace, and we have only one hundred and ten thousand.”

She pulled the bell-cord. A footman entered, dressed like a minister.

“Tell Captain Paz that I wish to see him.”

“If you think you are going to find out anything that way—” said Adam, laughing.

Adam and Clémentine had married in December 1835 and soon after had traveled two years in Italy, Switzerland, and Germany. Returning to Paris in November 1837, the countess entered society as a married woman and then became aware of a useful, half-suppressed factotum named Paz—spelt thus but pronounced “Patz.”

“Monsieur le capitaine Paz begs Madame la comtesse to excuse him,” said the footman returning. “He is at the stables; as soon as he has changed his dress Comte Paz will present himself to Madame.”

“What was he doing at the stables?”

“He was showing them how to groom Madame’s horse,” said the man. “He was not pleased with the way Constantin did it.”

The countess looked at the footman. He was perfectly serious.

“Ah! he was grooming Cora.”

“Madame la comtesse intends to ride out this morning?” said the footman, leaving the room without further answer.

“Is Paz a Pole?” asked Clémentine, turning to her husband, who nodded.

The footstep echoed through the gallery.

The countess beheld a tall, handsome man, well-made, bearing on his face signs of inward strength and secret sorrow. He wore a frogged overcoat called a polonaise. Thick black hair, rather unkempt, covered his square head; his broad forehead shone like white marble; he held his visored cap in his hand. His hand was like that of the Infant Hercules. Robust health flushed his face, marked by a large Roman nose and an Italian fervor in his jet eyes. The pleated trousers revealed fidelity to his land’s fashions. The contrast between the captain and the count, the little Pole and the stalwart soldier, was striking to a romantic woman.

“Good morning, Adam,” he said familiarly. Then he bowed courteously to ask Clémentine what he could do for her.

“You are Laginski’s friend!” exclaimed the countess.

“For life and death,” answered Paz, to whom the count smiled as he drew a last puff from his pipe.

“Then why don’t you take your meals with us? why did you not accompany us to Italy and Switzerland? why do you hide yourself so that I cannot thank you for the constant services you do for us?” said the countess with vivacity but no feeling.

She thought she perceived in Paz a voluntary servitude, a social amphibian half-secretary, half-bailiff, an embarrassing friend.

“Because, countess,” he answered with ease, “there are no thanks due. I am Adam’s friend, and it gives me pleasure to take care of his interests.”

“And you remain standing for your pleasure, too,” remarked Comte Adam.

Paz sat near the door.

“I remember seeing you about the time I was married, and afterwards in the courtyard,” said Clémentine. “But why put yourself in a position of inferiority—you, Adam’s friend?”

“I am perfectly indifferent to the opinion of Parisians,” he replied. “I live for myself, or, if you like, for you two.”

“But the opinion of the world as to a friend of my husband is not indifferent to me—”

“Ah, madame, the world will be satisfied if you tell them I am ‘an original.’”

After a silence he added, “Are you going out to-day?”

“Will you come with us to the Bois?”

“Certainly.”

So saying, Paz bowed and withdrew.

“What a good soul he is!” said Adam. “He has all the simplicity of a child.”

“Now tell me all about your relations with him,” said Clémentine.

“Paz, my dear,” said Laginski, “belongs to a noble family as old and illustrious as our own. One of the Pazzi of Florence, at the time of their disasters, fled to Poland, settled with some of his property, and founded the Paz family, to which the title of count was granted. This family distinguished itself in the glorious days of our republic and became rich. Some branches are rich and some poor. Our Paz is from a poor branch. He was an orphan with no fortune but his sword when he served in the regiment of the Grand Duke Constantine at the time of our revolution. Joining the Polish cause, he fought like a man who has nothing—three good reasons for fighting well. In his last affair, thinking he was followed by his men, he dashed upon a Russian battery and was taken prisoner. I was there. His brave act roused me. ‘Let us go and get him!’ I said to my troop, and we charged the battery like a lot of foragers. I got Paz—I was the seventh man; we started twenty and came back eight, counting Paz. After Warsaw was sold we were forced to escape the Russians. By chance Paz and I met again on the other side of the Vistula. I saw the poor captain arrested by Prussians. When we have fished a man out of the Styx we cling to him. This new danger made me so unhappy I let myself be taken too, thinking I could help him. Two men can get away where one will perish. Thanks to my name and some connections in Prussia, the authorities shut their eyes to my escape. I got my dear captain through as a man of no consequence, a family servant, and we reached Dantzic. There we took a Dutch vessel to London. It took us two months. My mother was ill in England and expecting me. Paz and I took care of her till her death, which the Polish troubles hastened. Then we left London for France. Men who go through such adversities become like brothers. When I reached Paris, at twenty-two, and found I had an income of over sixty thousand francs a year, I wanted to secure Paz’s future before launching into dissipation. I had seen sadness in his eyes—sometimes tears. I wanted him to be able to repay a benefit if he chose. I produced a certificate of Funds payable to bearer for a sum a year.”

Clémentine kissed him. “Dear treasure! Well, what did Paz do?”

“Thaddeus turned pale,” said the count, “but he didn’t say a word.”

“Oh! his name is Thaddeus?”

“Yes; Thaddeus folded the paper and gave it back to me, then said: ‘I thought, Adam, we were one for life or death, and that we should never part. Do you want to be rid of me?’ ‘If you take it that way, Thaddeus,’ I answered, ‘don’t let us say another word about it. If I ruin myself you shall be ruined too.’ ‘You haven’t fortune enough to live as a Laginski should,’ he said, ‘and you need a friend to take care of your affairs, be a father and a brother and a trusty confidant.’ My dear child, in his look and voice he had a maternal emotion, the gratitude of an Arab, the fidelity of a dog, the friendship of a savage, not displayed but ever ready. I seized him with a hand on each shoulder and kissed him on the lips. ‘For life and death, then! all that I have is yours—do what you will with it.’ He found this house and bought it for next to nothing. He sold my Funds high and bought in low, and we paid for this barrack with the profits. He knows horses, buys and sells to advantage; our stable costs little though we have the finest horses and equipages in Paris. Our servants, brave Polish soldiers chosen by him, would go through fire and water for us. Sometimes I had to force him to go to the theatre or my little tavern dinners. He doesn’t like social life.”

“What does he like, then?” asked Clémentine.

“Poland; he loves it and pines for it. His only spendings are sums he gives, more in my name than his own, to some of our poor brother-exiles.”

“Well, I shall love him, the fine fellow!” said the countess. “He looks as simple-hearted as he is grand.”

“All these pretty things you have about you,” continued Adam, “he picked up; he bought them at auction or as bargains. He’s keener than the dealers. If you see him rubbing his hands in the courtyard you may be sure he has traded away one horse for a better. He lives for me; his happiness is to see me elegant. The duties he takes are accomplished without fuss. One evening I lost twenty thousand francs at whist. ‘What will Paz say?’ I thought. Paz paid them—not without a sigh; but he never reproached me. That sigh restrained me more than remonstrances of uncles or wives could. ‘Do you regret the money?’ I asked. ‘Not for you or me,’ he replied; ‘but I was thinking twenty poor Poles could have lived a year on that sum.’ You must understand the Pazzi are fully the equal of the Laginski, so I couldn’t consider Paz an inferior. I never went out or came in without going first to Paz, as to my father. My fortune is his; and Thaddeus knows that if danger threatened him I would fling myself into it and drag him out, as I have done before.”

“And that is saying a good deal, my dear friend,” said the countess. “Devotion is like lightning. Men devote themselves in battle but no longer have the heart for it in Paris.”

“Well,” replied Adam, “I am always ready, as in battle, to devote myself to Paz. Our characters have asperities and defects, but mutual comprehension has tightened the bond. It is possible to save a man’s life and kill him afterward if he is a bad fellow; but Paz and I know that in each other which makes friendship indissoluble. There’s a constant exchange of happy thoughts and impressions; perhaps such a friendship is richer than love.”

A pretty hand closed the count’s mouth as promptly as a blow.

“Yes,” he said, “friendship, my dear angel, knows nothing of bankrupt sentiments and collapsed joys. Love, after giving more than it has, ends by giving less than it receives.”

“One side as well as the other,” remarked Clémentine laughing.

“Yes,” continued Adam, “whereas friendship only increases. You and I are as much friends as lovers; we have, at least I hope so, combined the two sentiments in our marriage.”

“I’ll explain why you and Thaddeus are such good friends,” said Clémentine. “The difference in lives comes from tastes and necessity, from likings not positions. As far as one can judge from seeing a man once and from what you tell me, there are times when the subaltern might become the superior.”

“Oh, Paz is truly my superior,” said Adam naively; “I have no advantage over him except mere luck.”

His wife kissed him for the generosity of the words.

“The extreme care with which he hides the grandeur of his feelings is one form of his superiority,” continued the count. “I said to him once: ‘You are a sly one; you have in your heart a vast domain within which you live and think.’ He has a right to the title of count; but in Paris he won’t be called anything but captain.”

“The Florentine of the middle ages has reappeared in our century,” said the countess. “Dante and Michael Angelo are in him.”

“That’s the truth,” cried Adam. “He is a poet in soul.”

So here I am, married to two Poles,” said the young countess, with a gesture worthy of the stage.

“Dear child!” said Adam, pressing her to him, “it would have made me very unhappy if my friend did not please you. We were both rather afraid of it, though he was delighted at my marriage. You will make him very happy if you tell him that you love him—yes, as an old friend.”

“I’ll go and dress; the day is so fine; and we will all three ride together,” said Clémentine, ringing for her maid.

Paz led so subterranean a life that Parisian society asked who he was when the Comtesse Laginska was seen in the Bois de Boulogne riding between her husband and a stranger. During one such ride Clémentine insisted Thaddeus should dine with them. This caprice forced Paz to prepare for an evening out. Clémentine, coquettish, dressed to charm; Adam and the captain waited in the salon.

“Comte Paz,” she said, “you must go with us to the Opera.” The tone implied: refuse and we shall quarrel.

“Willingly, madame,” replied the captain. “But as I have not the fortune of a count, call me captain.”

“Very good, captain; give me your arm,” she answered, taking it with the flattering familiarity that enchants lovers.

Placed beside the countess at table, Paz behaved like a poor sub-lieutenant at his general’s mess: deferential, taciturn, waiting to be questioned before he spoke. Clémentine’s coquettish attempts missed their mark before such cold gravity. Adam whispered, “Be lively, Thaddeus,” but the captain remained heavy and half-asleep. At dessert he excused his dulness because he kept early hours.

“My intention in taking you to the Opera was to amuse you,” said Clémentine, piqued.

“I will go,” Paz answered.

They debated the performance—Duprez in “Guillaume Tell” or the “Variétés”—and Paz calmly ordered the carriage horses rather than the coupé to avoid crowding. “A Frenchman would have forgotten that,” remarked Clémentine. “Ah! but we are Florentines transplanted to the North,” Thaddeus replied with a refined accent, a flash of manner that made his earlier stiffness seem assumed. Clémentine studied him with covert surprise.

Silence fell over the salon during coffee; Clémentine gave up trying to draw him out. At the Opera he seemed asleep. “You see, madame, that I am a very stupid man,” he murmured during the final act.

“In truth, you are neither a talker nor a man of the world, but perhaps Polish,” she replied.

“You are good for looking after your pleasures, property, household,” he said.

“Pooh!” cried Adam. “He is full of ardor and education; he can hold his own if he chooses. Don’t believe his modesty.”

At the evening’s end Paz excused himself and left to go home. Clémentine found him a bear; Adam pressed her hand. “Poor, dear Thaddeus,” he said. “He is trying to seem disagreeable where most men try to be amiable.”

“Oh!” she replied, “I think there is calculation in his behavior; he might have deceived an ordinary woman.”

Half an hour later, as the household left, Clémentine noticed lights in Paz’s rooms. The captain, in a dressing-gown with a pipe in his mouth, watched her enter the vestibule. The day had been hard: seeing and being near Clémentine had set his heart aflame. He had counselled Adam’s long journey to Italy to keep Clémentine away, for in spite of himself he loved her exclusively. Long absence had given him peace; her return renewed his trial. Smoking his six-foot wild-cherry pipe, a present from Adam, he asked himself how to have neither her love nor her dislike. The question haunted him.

Thaddeus did not lack pleasure amid suffering. Since the couple’s return he had daily inward satisfactions in knowing himself necessary to a household that without his devotion would have been ruined. Clémentine, brought up by a spendthrift father, knew nothing of household management; Adam, a careless son of a great Polish lord, would have been ruined. Paz had prevented him from gambling at the Bourse and looked after the family’s finances. Gratitude and devotion bound him to the household; despite his love he could not leave.

Clémentine, however, was seized by Parisian life. To reign in society demands wariness and energy: gowns, parties, drives in the Bois, the art of smiling and inventing compliments. Winters wear out young women; the fashion of constant appearance replaced the older intermittent society life, and Clémentine had little time to think of Paz. Nevertheless, one May day as she drove home from the Bois before leaving Paris for her uncle Ronquerolles’s estate, she noticed Thaddeus sauntering along a side-path of the Champs-Élysées, radiant at seeing his mistress’s elegant carriage.

“There’s the captain,” she said.

“He’s happy!” Adam replied. “That’s his delight—our equipage. I see him nearly every day.”

“I wonder what he is thinking about now,” she mused.

The countess stopped the carriage and bade him take the seat beside her. Thaddeus blushed. “I shall poison you,” he said; “I have been smoking.”

“Doesn’t Adam poison me?” she retorted.

“Yes, but he is Adam,” returned the captain.

“And why can’t Thaddeus have the same privileges?” she asked, smiling.

Her smile triumphed over his resolutions. She folded her arms and watched the passersby in rapt pleasure, and a flash of a great, previously restrained soul reached her sensibilities. She noticed in Thaddeus something superior to Adam: a fine presence where Adam seemed puny. Dangerous thoughts took hold; she sat still, dreamy-eyed, saying nothing until they reached home.

“You will dine with us; I shall be angry if you disobey me,” she said as they stepped from the carriage. “You are Thaddeus to me, as you are to Adam. Dress yourself.”

Thaddeus dressed with joy, shaken by dread. At dinner he behaved as before—soldierly, fit only for steward’s duties—but this time Clémentine’s glance had enlightened her. The Marquis de Ronquerolles, informed by his niece in a few words of Paz’s worth, asked why he had not met him before. Clémentine explained his shyness with a look urging him to change his behavior.

Paz, though superior to Adam in some respects, was not a man of parts; his apparent superiority owed much to misfortune. He had read and taught himself much in lonely Warsaw, but lacked original thought. Great-hearted and action-oriented, he kept his thoughts to himself; unexpressed thought eats the heart.

An exchanged glance among Clémentine’s relatives—one of those rapid Italian or French eye-plays—stirred the captain’s soul. The suggestion that the aunt and uncle thought he might be loved made love, gratitude, and friendship clash. For a moment love prevailed; Paz became brilliant, recounted the Polish insurrection nobly when questioned, and by the end of dinner Clémentine hung on his words, forgetting Adam’s sacrifices. At nine, after coffee, the others left. Paz and Clémentine were alone.

“I will leave you now, madame,” he said. “You will rejoin them at the Opera?”

“No,” she answered; “I don’t like dancing, and they give an odious ballet to-night.”

A silence heavy with meaning followed. “Two years ago Adam would not have gone to the Opera without me,” Clémentine observed.

“He loves you madly,” replied Paz.

“Yes, and because he loves me madly he is all the more likely not to love me tomorrow,” she said.

“How inexplicable Parisian women are!” Paz exclaimed. “When they are loved to madness they want to be loved reasonably, and when loved reasonably they complain.”

“And they are right,” she smiled. “I know Adam so well; he is volatile, a grand seigneur. He will be content to have me but will not oppose my tastes—”

“Where is the marriage without ‘buts’?” Paz gently interposed.

The most timid man might have thought what nearly drove Paz mad: “If I do not tell her now that I love her I am a fool.” Between them a deep, thought-filled silence; each watched the other—Clémentine in a mirror, Paz through a reflection. Buried in an armchair like a man digesting his dinner, Paz crossed his hands and twirled his thumbs; the image was of a husband or indifferent old man, mechanical and restrained.

“Why don’t you tell me something good of Adam?” cried Clémentine suddenly. “Tell me that he is not volatile—you who know him so well.”

“Now is the time,” thought Paz. “Tell you good of Adam?” he said aloud. “I love him; you would not believe me; I am incapable of telling you harm. My position is difficult between you.”

Clémentine lowered her head and looked at his varnished boots. “You Northern men have nothing but physical courage,” she complained; “you have no constancy in your opinions.”

“How will you amuse yourself alone, madame?” Paz asked, attempting carelessness.

“Are you not going to keep me company?” she queried.

“Excuse me for leaving you.”

“What do you mean? Where are you going?”

The idea of a heroic falsehood struck him. “I—I am going to the Circus in the Champs-Élysées; it opens to-night, and I can’t miss it.”

“Why not?” she asked, half anger.

“Must I tell you why?” he colored; “must I confide what I hide from Adam, who thinks my only love is Poland?”

“A secret in our noble captain?”

“A disgraceful one—which you will perhaps understand and pity.”

“You, disgraced?”

“Yes, I, Comte Paz; I am madly in love with a girl who travels with the Bouthor family’s rival circus; she plays at fairs. I had the director at the Cirque-Olympique engage her.”

“Is she handsome?”

“To my thinking,” said Paz, melancholy. “Malaga—that’s her stage name—is strong, active, and supple. When I look at her, with black hair tied in blue satin, floating on bare olive shoulders, dressed in white tunic edged with gold and a knitted silk bodice like a living Greek statue, carrying flags to martial music, jumping through paper hoops and alighting on a galloping horse to applause—no hired clapping—well, all that moves me.”

“More than a handsome woman in a ballroom?” Clémentine asked.

“Yes,” Paz choked. “Such agility and grace under danger seem to me the triumph of womanhood. Malaga can perform feats—stand on one foot, fall into the saddle, knit stockings, break eggs while riding—aptitude that makes peasants and soldiers alike admire her. She is dexterity personified.”

“She must be stupid,” said Clémentine.

“Oh no,” Paz replied. “She is thoughtless as a Bohemian, says everything that comes into her head, thinks little of the future, and sometimes speaks nobly. She loves handsome, strong men. Her trainer once beat her to make her supple—”

“You are intoxicated with Malaga,” Clémentine observed.

“She is called Malaga on the posters,” Paz said, piqued. “She lives in rue Saint-Lazare in a pretty third-floor apartment, velvet and silk, like a princess. She has two lives: the circus and that of a pretty woman.”

“Does she love you?”

“She loves me—now you will laugh—solely because I am a Pole. She saw an engraving of Poles rushing with Poniatowski into the Elster. But I am very unhappy, madame.” A tear fell.

“You men have such a passion for singularity,” she said. “And you?” he asked.

“I know Adam so well that I am certain he could forget me for some mountebank like your Malaga. Where did you first see her?”

“At Saint-Cloud last September. She stood at a corner booth among flags. Her companions in Polish costumes made a horrible racket. I watched her silent and thought I saw a melancholy in her face that touched me.”

Clémentine, pensive and rather melancholy, cried, “Poor, poor Thaddeus!” then, kindly and with a malicious smile, “Well go, go to your Circus.”

Thaddeus took her hand, kissed it, leaving a hot tear, and went out.

What began as invention soon gained reality. The only truth in his tale was that he had noticed Malaga at Saint-Cloud; thereafter he pursued the fiction. A clown for a five-franc tip told him she was a foundling; Paz learned she lived in rue des Fossés-du-Temple. The next day he went to the Faubourg du Temple and, with the portress and Madame Chapuzot’s help, found Mademoiselle Turquet.

“Malaga!” cried the portress when Thaddeus was announced.

The captain’s hat snagged on a drying line and fell; Malaga, ironing, picked it up and asked what he wanted. “I saw you at the Circus,” he said, “and you reminded me of a daughter I have lost, mademoiselle; out of affection for my Héloïse, whom you resemble, I should like to be of service to you, if you permit me.”

“Certainly; pray sit down, general,” said Madame Chapuzot, playing hospitable interpreter.

“But I am not gallant,” Paz exclaimed. “I am an unfortunate father who tries to deceive himself by a resemblance.”

“Then am I to pass for your daughter?” Malaga asked, smiling, not suspecting his sincerity.

“Yes,” said Paz, “and I’ll come and see you sometimes. You shall be lodged in better rooms, comfortably furnished.”

Malaga looked suspicious. “What countryman is monsieur?” she asked.

“I am a Pole.”

“Oh! then I accept,” she said.

Paz left, promising to return.

A month later the circus-rider lived in a comfortable apartment furnished by Comte Adam’s upholsterer; Paz saw to it deliberately that his folly should be talked about at the hôtel Laginski. Malaga, delighted and dazzled as by an Arabian-Nights gift, was served by Monsieur and Madame Chapuzot as friends and servants. The Chapuzots and Marguerite expected a result; after three months they could not divine Paz’s meaning. He came about an hour once a week, sat in the salon, never went into her boudoir despite their manoeuvres, and each visit left two gold pieces of forty francs each on the mantelpiece.

“He looks as if he didn’t care to be here,” Madame Chapuzot remarked.

“Yes,” Malaga answered, “the man’s as cold as an icicle.”

“But he’s a good fellow,” Chapuzot cried, proud in a new blue cloth suit bought with Paz’s money.

Paz kept his reserve. The sum deposited weekly, plus Malaga’s meagre salary, allowed her a sumptuousness she had never known. Tales at the Circus grew: her good fortune swelled from the six thousand francs Paz had spent on furniture to sixty thousand according to gossip. In the demi-monde she shone in burnous and elegant scarves; the equivocal set assailed her good luck with calumnies—some absurdly said she was a somnambulist and the Pole a magnetizer. Such scandals drove Malaga to tears as she told Paz.

“When I want to injure a woman,” she said, “I don’t calumniate her subtly; I say she is humpbacked, and I prove it. Why do you compromise me?”

Paz maintained his cruel silence. Madame Chapuzot soon discovered his name and title and heard facts at the hôtel Laginski: Paz had never been known to have a daughter. Malaga felt terror.

“My dear child,” Chapuzot warned, “that monster”—the man who looked in slyly without daring to speak—“wants to get a hold on you and make you do something criminal. If you should come before the police—if your name is in the papers! I tremble. I’d warn the police.”

After many foolish notions fermented in Malaga’s mind, one day Paz laid his gold on the mantelpiece as usual; she seized it and flung it in his face, crying, “I don’t want stolen money!”

The captain gave the gold to Chapuzot, went away without a word, and did not return.

Clémentine was at her uncle’s in Burgundy when the Circus troop discovered Malaga had lost her Polish count. Opinions divided: some thought her display of honor folly, others shrewd. Paz’s conduct appeared inexplicable even to clever women. He received thirty-seven letters from women of their kind. His reserve, however, did not excite fashionable curiosity and was discussed mainly in the demi-monde.

Two weeks later Malaga, crippled by debt, wrote Paz a letter that fell into Adam’s hands and was read by dandies who pronounced it a masterpiece. It read in essence:

“You, whom I still dare to call my friend, will you not pity me after all that has passed—which you have so ill understood? My heart disavows whatever may have wounded your feelings. If I charmed you, return to me; otherwise I shall fall into despair. Poverty has overtaken me; yesterday I lived on a herring at two sous and one sou of bread. Is that a breakfast for the woman you loved? The Chapuzots have left me. The sheriff has seized everything for the landlord; the jeweller refused to wait ten days—when we lose the confidence of such as you, credit is gone. My uncle has nothing to give; winter is coming and the boulevards will play only melodramas. How could you misunderstand the nobleness of my feelings? You who seemed so happy seeing me well-off, how can you leave me in poverty? If I were to think of you when I jump through hoops, I should break my legs. Whatever the result, I am yours for life.

“Marguerite Turquet.”

“That letter,” thought Thaddeus, laughing, “is worth the ten thousand francs I have spent upon her.”

Clementine returned the next day; two days later Paz saw her again, more beautiful and graceful than before. After dinner, when Thaddeus had left on the pretext of asking Adam’s advice and had left Malaga’s letter behind, a little scene occurred between the count and his wife.

“Poor Thaddeus!” Adam said as Paz departed. “What a misfortune for a man of distinction to be the plaything of the lowest kind of circus-rider. He will lose everything, get lower and lower, and won’t be recognizable before long. Here, read that,” he added, handing Malaga’s tobacco-scented letter to his wife.

Clementine read it, tossed it aside with disgust, and, stung by Adam’s comment, accused him of indulgence toward such women. Adam defended Thaddeus, calling him “a very firm fellow” for having spent only ten thousand francs on her and waited to ask Adam for money. Clementine was skeptical—“He will ruin you,” she said—yet Adam thought he could sacrifice Malaga if asked.

Paz, who knew Adam well, had begged him to secrecy while excusing his dissipations and had asked him to lend a few thousand francs for Malaga. The count consoled himself that if he asked Paz to leave her, Paz would comply for his own happiness; Adam feared only that Paz might take to intoxication. The countess, impatient and disdainful, said no more.

Two days later Adam noticed in Clementine’s manner, voice, and especially eyes the terrible effect of his confidences. Contempt had opened a gulf between the beloved woman and himself. Thaddeus was suddenly plunged into despair, convinced that he himself had made her despise him. Life became a burden: he suffered fits of sorrow, yet sometimes pure joy—silent raptures in attending her, admiring every gesture and tone, living in her life while she paid him no notice. He found happiness in tending her horses and household affairs; these pleasures were secret and maternal in their devotion. Yet despite these joys he remained unhappy: the Catholic ideal of love, with respect and nobility, could not exist with contempt. Thaddeus had voluntarily lived apart from her, serving and loving without recognition; now he was denied even esteem. He wasted away; in January a low fever forced him to bed, though he refused a doctor. Adam worried; the countess commented with cruel indifference, “Let him alone; don’t you see it is only some Olympian trouble?” Thaddeus, roused by despair, left his bed, tried divers amusements, and recovered.

In late February Adam lost a large sum at the Jockey-Club and, afraid of his wife’s reproach, begged Thaddeus to let the loss appear in the household accounts as if spent on Malaga.

“There’s nothing surprising in your spending that sum on the girl; but if the countess finds out that I have lost it at cards I shall be lowered in her opinion, and she will always be suspicious in future,” Adam explained.

Thaddeus sighed. Adam promised that if Paz did him this service they’d be quits. The countess later discovered that Malaga had cost thirty thousand francs—ten thousand then twenty thousand more—and denounced the Poles’ incomprehensible conduct. She proposed to take household management into her own hands and told Adam to give Paz an allowance and let him settle his affairs. Adam defended Paz, saying he had saved large sums for them and had put money with Nucingen that a steward might have pocketed. Clementine softened but remained hard toward Thaddeus.

A few days later she asked Thaddeus to her boudoir and demanded that if he loved Adam he must give up Malaga, implying that only such a sacrifice would allow him to stay in their house. Thaddeus, wounded, protested: giving up Malaga was not a sacrifice he could make. Clementine, intent on rousing some sense of honor she thought extinct, reproached him for being caught like a boy and for making Malaga celebrated. Thaddeus left deeply humiliated, feeling her words—“I thought you had a noble soul”—ringing in his ears. He considered dramatic exits—joining the spahis, seeking death in Africa—but feared the ruin his absence might bring them. He resolved to continue.

March brought the Paris carnival: cheap, boisterous balls where Musard’s orchestra set the galop. Thaddeus, who loved Malaga and knew the countess might come disguised to Musard’s, proposed to spend an evening there with her. On Shrove Tuesday, 1838, in the Babylonian hall, hidden and wrapped in a black domino, Clementine saw Thaddeus as Robert Macaire, threading the galop with Malaga in savage costume. Furious, she told Adam, “You Poles have no honor at all! I did believe in Thaddeus. He gave me his word that he would leave that woman; he did not know I should be here, unseen.”

A few days later she asked Paz to dine with them; after dinner Adam left and Clementine reproved Paz, telling him she did not wish him to live in her house any longer. Paz accepted the condemnation, explained he had delayed leaving Malaga until after carnival, and asked that before he withdrew he train Constantin to take over the household management—he had run it, managed servants, and supervised details. Clementine, convinced of her own abilities, agreed to learn. Paz, humbled, praised Malaga’s charm and defended her. Clementine, angered, said she wished for nothing of the animal about her.

Paz then set about instructing Clementine in the management of her affairs: methods, prices, how to avoid being cheated by servants, and to rely on Constantin as major-domo. By the end of May he judged her competent. But his suffering did not cease; events added cruel trials. Adam fell seriously ill, and Thaddeus, instead of leaving, stayed to nurse him. His devoted care could have been misread as punishment thrust upon a man to restrain passion, but women often saw either everything or nothing when love was involved.

For forty-five days Paz watched Adam without thinking of Malaga. Clementine, exhausted, summoned Paris’s leading doctors. “If he comes safely out of this,” the chief said, “it will only be by an effort of nature. The count’s life is in the hands of his nurses.” Thaddeus told Clementine the doctors’ decision; he found her in a Chinese pavilion, pale and in anguish. He expressed how deeply he had made Adam into a son in his own heart and that, if the count died, he would be bereft of his interest on earth. Clementine replied impatiently that she loved no one else and insisted she preferred a safe husband to a superior man. When Thaddeus suggested she might not yet know what it is to love, she looked on him with disgust and, with a tone that crushed him, said “Poor Malaga!” Then she left.

Thaddeus returned to the sick-room and resumed his watch, resolved to save Adam. He nursed the count in a manner that impressed the doctors: sleepless, constant, obedient to the physicians’ directions. He accepted Clementine’s thanks without pride and kept his resentment to himself. “She shall owe his life to me,” he told himself. When Clementine finally gave up nursing, worn out, Thaddeus continued without pause. At last, toward the end of August, the doctor Bianchon told Clementine Adam was out of danger.

The day after a meeting in the kiosk, the Marquis de Ronquerolles came to see his nephew; he was about to leave on a secret mission to Russia. When Adam was well enough to drive out with his wife and Thaddeus, a gentleman arrived and asked for Comte Paz; Thaddeus, on the front seat, took a dispatch stamped with the Foreign Office seal and, after reading it, pocketed it so Clementine and Adam could not discuss it. But by the time they reached the porte Maillot, curiosity got the better of Adam.

“There’s no indiscretion between brothers who love each other,—tell me what there is in that despatch; I’m in a fever of curiosity,” Adam said.

Clementine, irritated at his sulkiness of late, remarked she would never ask him anything again. Thaddeus replied his secret could not be kept out of the newspapers, and announced that the Emperor Nicholas had appointed him captain in a regiment bound for the expedition to Khiva. Adam was stunned and frightened at the idea of his friend’s leaving; Clementine quipped that Malaga would jump through the hoop for his sake. Thaddeus spoke with a bitterness that made Clementine uneasy: he would go fight for the Emperor, perhaps return promoted, and would regret the Champs-Elysees and Malaga’s hardness. When she asked if he loved Malaga very much, he answered, “I have sacrificed for her the honor that no man should ever sacrifice”—the honor by which one preserves esteem in the eyes of an idol.

As they passed the wooden circus building, he pointed it out. That night he went to the Russian embassy, then to the Foreign Office, and left the next morning from Havre before the count and countess were up. Adam, in tears, told Clementine, “I have lost a friend. I don’t know what has made him abandon me as if a pestilence were in my house. We are not friends to quarrel about a woman.” He added he had learned from Mademoiselle Turquet that Paz had never touched Malaga’s little finger. Clementine, troubled, received a letter from Thaddeus that night. It read, in essence:

He could not bear to seek death in the Caucasus with her contempt following him. When he first saw her he loved her as men love forever, though she had chosen another. He had found happiness in serving her, being indispensable, and enjoying the small graces of making her life comfortable. He had loved her like a mother loves a child, humble and secret. At eighteen he had adored an idealized Warsaw woman without her knowledge; likewise he had made Clementine the queen of his days. He confessed that he had invented the Malaga liaison at a moment of fear that his love would be revealed and ruin him—he chose to hide his love behind the story of a debauched woman. Her contempt had been his punishment; it proved there was no return from loathing. He could not remain where his friend lived and where her disdain would follow him. Moreover, he declared, a man does not tear his friend from death to betray him. Leaving Paris would be his penance for having thought, however fleetingly, that he might let Adam die when nursing could save him. He said good-by, relinquishing all that was his for the sake of her; he had taken sixty thousand francs from Nucingen and balanced the accounts; perhaps, he added bitterly, he would be Prince Paz in three years or dead.

When Clementine read this letter she felt stunned. “If my poor Adam says he has lost a friend, what have I lost?” she thought, sinking into a chair.

A separate note to the count, given privately through Constantin, was blunt: Malaga had told him everything, and in the name of Adam’s future happiness Adam must never mention his visits to Malaga to Clementine; let her think Malaga cost Paz a hundred thousand francs. Thaddeus then confessed he was not going to Khiva but to the Caucasus, and that at the rate he meant to go he’d either come back Prince Paz or be dead. He signed himself simply “Thaddeus.”

“I was an idiot,” Adam admitted; he had nearly cut his throat talking about Malaga. Paz had departed three years earlier; the newspapers had said nothing of any prince. Clementine, who was passionately interested in the Emperor Nicholas’s campaigns, sometimes asked the Russian ambassador idly, “Do you know what has become of our poor Comte Paz?” She hoped, with an unavowed eagerness, that she might see him again.

Alas, many Parisian women passed beside men like Paz without recognizing them: noble love, rough and unpolished, often lacked the conventional polish they expected; their hearts demanded a jeweler’s cut to give value to devotion.

In January 1842 Clementine’s gentle melancholy inspired a violent passion in the Comte de La Palferine, a daring lion of the day. He sought to inveigle her with the help of a woman she trusted, already jealous. Clem entine, incapable of suspecting treachery and trusting her friend, went with her to a masked ball at the Opera. About three in the morning, overcome by excitement, she consented to a supper and was about to enter her friend’s carriage when a powerful hand seized her arm and carried her to her own carriage—whose door was open though she had not noticed it. “He has never left Paris!” she exclaimed as she recognized Thaddeus, who vanished when the carriage drove off.

Thus Clementine, who had once despised him, constantly hopes she may see Paz again.
