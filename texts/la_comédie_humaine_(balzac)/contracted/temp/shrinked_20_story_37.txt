In September 1835 Mademoiselle du Rouvre, the only daughter of the Marquis du Rouvre, one of the richest heiresses of the faubourg Saint-Germain, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a young Polish exile. (The Polish names are written phonetically for readers’ ease.)

Clémentine du Rouvre’s fortune derived from curious family mishaps and alliances. Her father, the Marquis du Rouvre, had squandered much of the wealth he had gained by marrying a Demoiselle de Ronquerolles. On her mother’s side Clémentine was related to the Marquis de Ronquerolles and Madame de Serizy; on her father’s side she had an uncle, the eccentric Chevalier du Rouvre, who had made money in land speculation. The Marquis de Ronquerolles had lost both children to cholera; Madame de Serizy’s only son died in Africa. Though her father dissipated his resources, Clémentine, through settlements by the Marquis de Ronquerolles, Madame de Serizy, and the Chevalier du Rouvre, became a considerable heiress; each agreed to settle a marriage portion of ten thousand francs a year on her.

Comte Adam Laginski was a Polish nobleman by birth and bearing, allied with princely houses; but in Louis-Philippe’s Paris, heraldry and noble lineage counted for little against bourgeois opinion. When he first appeared in 1833 in fashionable Paris he seemed like a dandy and a young man living for pleasure. By the mid-1830s Polish nationality had lost much of its romantic prestige in France. Political reactions and the failure of the Polish cause had made Poles, once admired, objects of ridicule to many bourgeois Parisians suspicious of exiles and their internal divisions—between the republicans like Lelewel and the nobles under figures like Prince Czartoryski. Poles in Paris bore the brunt of this mixed disdain and interest.

People were uncertain whether Adam was penniless or an adventurer. Diplomats and high society, cautious, gave Polish exiles a reserved hospitality, and Parisian life offered temptations to dissipation. Adam himself had two things that worked against him in Parisian eyes—his odd, pinched face and his manner of mind. He looked thin and somewhat goatish, with a small face, short nose, fair hair, and a reddish beard, but he had polished manners and a distinguished air that came partly from his dress and the education given him by his mother, a Radziwill. His courage and conversational ease suited the salons; he moved lightly from topic to topic, the kind of flitting companion Paris loved.

Yet in 1834 Adam suddenly bought a house in the rue de la Pépinière. Within six months he displayed a style of living that rivaled the best in Paris. About this time he had fallen in love with Clémentine after seeing her at the Opera. A year later they were married. Society learned, to its surprise, that Adam’s mother had, prudently during the insurrection, mortgaged property to raise funds; by the time of the marriage Adam was in possession of about eighty thousand francs a year. The discovery of his means made people reassess him and the apparent imprudence of Clémentine’s guardians. During the winter of 1836 Adam and Madame Laginska were fashionably prominent; Clémentine entered a circle of young married women noted for refinement and taste.

This background sets the stage for a quietly noble action hidden amid society’s tumult—an action not unique but easily overlooked. Balzac then remarks on the architectural and social climate that had emerged after the 1830 revolution: old grand houses were being subdivided and altered, yielding a confused mixture of styles. Without a court or solid nobility to set a tone, art lacked harmony, though architects found clever ways to make small grounds and narrow lots seem princely. Comte Adam’s hôtel in the rue de la Pépinière exemplified such Parisians’ illusions of grandeur.

Adam’s house, standing between court and garden, had kitchens and offices to the right, a coach-house and stables to the left, and a porter’s lodge between two carriage-entrances. The chief luxury was a greenhouse attached to a ground-floor boudoir opening on a suite of reception rooms. The house’s lavish interiors were the product of celebrated decorators and painters; the staircase gleamed white and the cost of the whole had reached around eleven hundred thousand francs—considered princely by those unfamiliar with real princely houses. Yet this architectural show was built on a modest plot of about twenty-five rods, once the garden of a purveyor who had made and lost a fortune. Behind the house, a velvet lawn, exotic trees, a Chinese pagoda, and fragrant greenhouse formed a private, verdant solitude in the middle of Paris. The boudoir itself was an accumulation of elegant trinkets—Chinese work-tables, inlaid ivories, sketches by famous artists, Persian rugs and various curiosities—subdued by half-light and the scent from the greenhouse; the room displayed the era’s taste for an exhibition of goods rather than an inner life. Balzac notes with characteristic judgment that people then lived like prodigal annuitants, uncertain of the future.

One morning, late in April, while the boudoir’s greenhouse doors stood open, Clémentine lay thinking. She had been married two years and was now aware of a mysterious figure in her household—“Paz,” a name she heard everywhere. Her husband smoked a narghile and answered indolently. Whenever anything needed doing—arranging the carriage, paying a bill, bitting a horse—the household seemed to defer to Paz. Clémentine, curious and a little vexed, summoned him through a footman.

Paz, when presented, proved strikingly different from the count. Tall, well-made, bearing the marks of inward strength and secret sorrow, he wore a polonaise and had thick black hair over a broad forehead, a large Roman nose, and ardent black eyes. He had the look and bearing of a soldier and an air of reserved nobility. The contrast between the small, elegant count and the robust, stern-faced captain made a romantic impression on the young countess.

“Good morning, Adam,” Paz said familiarly, then bowed to Clémentine and asked what he could do for her. The countess, unsure what to make of this friend of her husband who seemed both servant and confidant, demanded why he put himself in a position of apparent inferiority and why he had not accompanied them on their travels. Paz replied simply that thanks were unnecessary; he was Adam’s friend and he took pleasure in tending Adam’s interests. He candidly said he was indifferent to Parisian opinion. Clémentine, puzzled, said she wanted the world to know who he was. “The world will be satisfied if you tell them I am ‘an original,’” he answered with composed humor. When she invited him to join them for a ride to the Bois he accepted, bowing and withdrawing.

After Paz left, Adam expounded his history and their relationship. Paz, whose Christian name was Thaddeus, belonged to an old Polish family—the Pazzi of Florence, as Adam explained by tradition—aristocratic but partly ruined. He had been an orphan with only his sword and served under the Grand Duke Constantine during the Polish revolution. Fighting in the insurrection, he had charged a Russian battery and been captured; Adam had rescued him in a desperate counter-charge that left both men among the few survivors. They had escaped across the Vistula, endured further peril in Prussia and Poland, and eventually made a slow passage to England. Adam’s mother died while they were in England; Adam and Paz had cared for her. The hardships welded them into a brotherhood.

When Adam discovered his income, he wanted Paz secure. He offered Paz a certificate of income—an act more practical than romantic; Paz took the paper, pale, and returned it, valuing their friendship more than a monetary dependence. Still, Adam insisted, and Paz accepted provision. The two men had weathered battles and exile together; Adam’s devotion was as real as in wartime. He found in Paz not only fidelity and martial courage but a man of deep feeling who hid his grandeur inwardly. Paz bought the rue de la Pépinière house at a bargain, sold funds astutely, used gains to pay for the place, and managed Adam’s affairs shrewdly. He knew horses and trades, and the Laginski household owed much to his thrift and care. Paz maintained the stable, selected servants—former soldiers—who were faithful; he gave money to poor Polish exiles under Adam’s name, and he seemed to prefer quiet, useful labors to fashionable life. Paz’s only costs were these gifts and the maintenance of the household, and he disliked society, much preferring the devotion of work and duty.

Adam recounted one episode that illustrated Paz’s character: once, after Adam had lost twenty thousand francs at whist, Paz paid the debt without complaint—but sighed, thinking how many poor Poles could have lived on that sum. That sigh, Adam said, restrained him more than any reproach. Paz, in Adam’s view, was superior in many ways. He concealed the grandeur of his feelings; he had in his heart a vast inner life. To Parisians he might be merely a “captain,” but Adam declared that Paz possessed the soul of an old Renaissance noble—“Dante and Michael Angelo are in him,” Clémentine exclaimed.

Their closeness appeared as if the two men combined the best parts of friendship and love. Adam hoped that in his marriage with Clémentine they had fused friendship and love as well. Clémentine, moved, said she would love Paz as an old friend, and they planned to ride together that fine day.

Balzac’s narrative thus sketches the household, the social milieu, and the peculiar intimacy that bound the count and his captain. Paz is portrayed as a man of action and inward nobility, self-effacing yet indispensable. Clémentine’s curiosity, the household’s deference to Paz, and Adam’s warm, almost filial regard for him set up the human dynamics at the heart of the story: an old soldier’s devotion, a young count’s generosity, and a society that measures men superficially, failing to see the moral wealth beneath appearances.

The description of the hôtel and its furnishings underscores a broader social portrait—of a Paris that sought to display wealth and taste in an era of uncertain fortunes. Balzac’s ironic tone about the façade of modern comfort is evident in his portrayal of the boudoir’s “pretty nothings” and in the observation that contemporary habits emphasize show over an inner life. Yet beneath the display, the Laginski household contained a genuine bond and a quiet ethic of service embodied by Paz: the unglamorous heroism of loyalty, labor, and self-sacrifice.

Thus, by the time Clémentine had finished dressing to ride, Balzac has introduced his chief characters and the central moral constellation: Adam, whose nobility is shown by courage, generosity, and open-hearted trust; Paz, whose modest station masks an inner grandeur and unwavering devotion; and Clémentine, whose curiosity and sensibility will be the audience for the revelation of this relationship. In Parisian society’s chatter—its readiness to accept or reject men on appearances—the profound friendship of Adam and Paz already stands apart, a hidden moral jewel.

Paz led so subterranean a life that Parisian society wondered who he was when the Comtesse Laginska was seen in the Bois de Boulogne riding between her husband and a stranger. During one such ride Clémentine insisted Thaddeus should dine with them. This caprice obliged Paz to make an evening toilet; Clémentine dressed with a coquetry that even Adam admired when she entered the salon where the two friends awaited her.

“Comte Paz,” she said, “you must go with us to the Opera.” The tone meant: “If you refuse we shall quarrel.”

“Willingly, madame,” replied the captain. “But as I have not the fortune of a count, call me captain.”

“Very good, captain; give me your arm,” she said, taking it with the flattering familiarity that charms lovers.

Placed beside the countess, Paz behaved like a dutiful sub-lieutenant at his general’s table. He let Clémentine talk, listened deferentially as to a superior, differed in nothing, and waited to be questioned before speaking. His frozen gravity frustrated her coquettish efforts. Adam urged him to be lively; Thaddeus remained heavy and half asleep. At dessert he explained his habits—bed at eight, up early—and excused his dulness as sleepiness.

“My intention in taking you to the Opera was to amuse you, captain; do as you prefer,” said Clémentine, piqued.

“I will go,” Paz answered.

“Duprez sings ‘Guillaume Tell,’” Adam remarked. “But perhaps you would prefer the ‘Variétés’?”

Paz smiled and rang for the carriage. “Tell Constantin to put horses to the carriage instead of the coupé. We should be rather squeezed otherwise,” he said to the count. “A Frenchman would have forgotten that,” Clémentine observed. “Ah! but we are Florentines transplanted to the North,” he answered with an accent and look which contradicted his table-stiffness. Clémentine watched him with surprising interest.

Silence reigned while they took coffee; Clémentine ceased trying to draw him out. Paz retreated into military stiffness and remained so, seeming asleep in the box at the Opera.

“You see, madame, that I am a very stupid man,” he said during the last act. “Am I not right to stick to my specialty?”

“In truth, you are neither a talker nor a man of the world, but perhaps you are Polish.”

“Therefore leave me to look after your pleasures and household—it is all I am good for.”

“Adieu, comtesse; I have obeyed your wishes so far; now I will take the carriage, go home to bed, and send it back for you.”

Clémentine let him go without reply. “What a bear!” she said to Adam. “You are a great deal nicer.” Adam squeezed her hand unobserved. “Poor, dear Thaddeus,” he murmured. “He is trying to make himself disagreeable where most men would try to seem more amiable.” Clémentine suspected calculation in his behavior; she thought an ordinary woman might have been taken in.

Half an hour later, as the carriage was waiting, Clémentine asked, “Where does the captain perch?”

“His apartments are over the coachhouse,” Adam answered. “No one lives on the other side yet; I mean that apartment for our children and their instructors.” Clémentine noticed lights in Thaddeus’s rooms as they passed and said, “He didn’t go to bed.”

The captain, in his dressing-gown with a pipe in his mouth, watched Clémentine enter the vestibule. That day had been hard for him. A great emotion had possessed him the day Adam made him go to the Opera to see Mademoiselle du Rouvre; again at her marriage he recognized the woman whom a man is forced to love exclusively. For this reason Paz had encouraged the long journey to Italy after the marriage. At peace while the Laginskis were away, his trial renewed on their return. As he sat at his window smoking a long wild-cherry pipe, he thought: “I, and God, who will reward me for suffering in silence, alone know how I love her! But how shall I manage to have neither her love nor her dislike?”

He felt inward joy in a daily, secret knowledge of his necessity to a household that without his devotion would go to ruin. Clémentine had been raised by a spendthrift father and knew little of household economy; Adam, son of a prodigal Polish lord, would likely have been ruined without Paz’s management. Paz had prevented him from gambling at the Bourse. Gratitude—key-note of Paz’s life—bound him to the household where he alone could look after the affairs of the heedless owners. The countess’s return, more lovely and free than before, brought the lover both delights and torments: to know he was the pivot of the household’s splendor and to see her in public, admired, inspired “mysterious delights” glowing within him but never showing on his face.

For five months Clémentine had not perceived the captain because he concealed himself, avoiding her. Nothing so resembles Divine love as hopeless human love: a man must have depth of heart to devote himself in silence and obscurity to a woman. In such a heart is worship for love’s sake only. Thaddeus had reached that height; filled with creative joy he attained something of grandeur. “She was not entirely deceived,” he thought. “She might break up my friendship with Adam if she took a dislike to me; but if she coquetted with me to amuse herself, what would become of me?” He scolded himself for such vanity and went to bed resolved to await events.

The next day Clémentine breakfasted without Paz, inattentive to his disobedience. It was her reception day; she paid no attention to the absent steward, on whom all the burden of parade days fell. “Good!” thought he as the last carriages drove away at two in the morning; “it was only a caprice or curiosity.”

After that he returned to his habits. Diverted by Parisian occupations, Clémentine seemed to have forgotten him. To reign in Paris is exhausting: fashionable women must be constantly on the scene—morning and afternoon toilettes, receptions, drives in the Bois—wearing art, flowers, diamonds; often they fade and exhaust themselves within years. Such cares left little time even to love Adam; Paz might easily be forgotten. Yet in May, as she drove home from the Bois before leaving Paris for Ronquerolles, her uncle’s estate in Burgundy, she noticed Thaddeus sauntering in a side-path of the Champs-Élysées, in heaven at seeing his countess in her elegant carriage—“his beloved family admired by all.”

“There’s the captain,” she said.

“He’s happy!” Adam answered. “He knows there’s no equipage more elegant than ours, and he rejoices to think people envy it. I see him nearly every day.”

“I wonder what he is thinking about now,” said Clémentine. The carriage stopped near Paz, and she bade him take the seat beside her. Thaddeus flushed.

“I shall poison you,” he said; “I have been smoking.”

“Doesn’t Adam poison me?” she asked.

“Yes, but he is Adam,” returned Paz.

“And why can’t Thaddeus have the same privileges?” Clémentine smiled. That smile overcame Paz’s resolutions; he looked at her with all the fire of his soul, tempered by grateful devotion. Dangerous thoughts took hold; she noticed his fine presence, a contrast to Adam’s punier frame. The devil alone knew her thoughts. She sat dreamy and said nothing until home.

“You will dine with us; I shall be angry if you disobey,” she said as the carriage turned in. “You are Thaddeus to me, as you are to Adam. I know your obligations to him, but I also know ours to you. Dress yourself,” she said, taking his hand.

Thaddeus dressed trembling but joyful and behaved at dinner as before—like a soldier fit only for his duties as steward. But this time Clémentine had been enlightened by his glance. The Marquis de Ronquerolles, a shrewd diplomatist who had served with de Marsay, had been told by his niece of Paz’s worth and knew how modestly he made himself steward of Laginski’s affairs.

“And why is this the first time I have the pleasure of seeing Comte Paz?” asked the marquis.

“Because he is so shy and retiring,” Clémentine replied with a look telling Paz to change his behavior.

Alas, to render him less interesting: Paz, though superior to Adam in heart and experience, lacked the gift of original thought that makes a great man. His superiority owed much to misfortune and inward cultivation; he was more a man of action than of speculative expression. He kept his thoughts to himself; they ate his heart. What is a thought unexpressed?

After Clémentine’s speech, the marquis and his sister exchanged a glance, embracing their niece, Adam, and Paz. In the quick French fashion eyes can say everything; Paz understood the exchange’s meaning. “Do the aunt and uncle think I might be loved? Then my happiness only depends on my audacity! But Adam—” Love and friendship clashed; for a moment love prevailed. The lover, at last daring, became brilliant. He pleased, narrated the Polish insurrection in noble terms when questioned by the diplomat, and by dinner’s end Clémentine hung on his lips, regarding him as a hero, forgetting Adam’s sacrifices.

After coffee the guests left; Paz and Clémentine were alone. “I will leave you now, madame,” he said. “You will of course rejoin them at the Opera?”

“No,” she answered; “I don’t like dancing, and they give an odious ballet to-night, ‘La Révolte au Sérail.’”

A silence followed. “Two years ago Adam would not have gone to the Opera without me,” she said, not looking at Paz.

“He loves you madly,” Paz replied.

“Yes, and because he loves me madly he is all the more likely not to love me tomorrow,” she said.

“How inexplicable Parisian women are!” exclaimed Paz. “When they are loved to madness they want to be loved reasonably, and when they are loved reasonably they reproach a man for not loving them at all.”

“And they are quite right. Thaddeus,” she smiled, “I know Adam so well; he is volatile and above all grand seigneur. He will always be content to have me as his wife and will never oppose my tastes, but—”

“Where is the marriage in which there are no ‘buts’?” said Thaddeus softly, trying to change her thought.

He kept thinking: if he did not tell her now he loved her he was a fool. Neither spoke; between them came one of those deep, crowded silences. Clémentine examined him covertly; Paz observed her in a mirror. Buried in an armchair like a man digesting his dinner, he crossed his hands and twirled his thumbs.

“Why don’t you tell me something good of Adam?” cried Clémentine suddenly. “Tell me that he is not volatile, you who know him so well.”

The cry was fine. “Now is the time,” thought Paz, “to put an insurmountable barrier between us.” He answered instead with painful frankness: “I love him; you would not believe me; I am incapable of telling you harm. My position is very difficult between you.”

Clémentine lowered her head to his varnished boots. “You Northern men have nothing but physical courage,” she complained; “you have no constancy in your opinions.”

“How will you amuse yourself alone, madame?” Paz asked, assuming carelessness.

“Are you not going to keep me company?”

“Excuse me for leaving you.”

“What do you mean? Where are you going?”

A heroic falsehood rose in him. “I—I am going to the Circus in the Champs-Élysées; it opens to-night, and I can’t miss it.”

“Why not?” she asked, half angered.

“Must I tell you why?” he said, coloring. “Must I confide to you what I hide from Adam, who thinks my only love is Poland?”

“Ah! a secret, then?”

“A disgraceful one—which you will perhaps understand and pity.”

“You, disgraced?”

“Yes, I, Comte Paz; I am madly in love with a girl who travels with the Bouthor family—they rival Franconi; they play only at fairs. I had the director at the Cirque-Olympique engage her.”

“Is she handsome?”

“To my thinking,” said Paz, melancholy. “Malaga (that’s her stage name) is strong, active, supple. Why do I prefer her to all other women? When I look at her, with her black hair tied with a blue satin ribbon on bare olive shoulders, dressed in a white tunic edged with gold, knitted bodice like a living Greek statue, carrying flags to martial music, jumping through paper hoops that tear as she goes, lighting gracefully on the galloping horse to applause—no hired clapping—well, all that moves me.”

“More than a handsome woman in a ballroom?” asked Clémentine, amazed.

“Yes,” Paz replied, choking. “Such agility and grace under danger seem the height of triumph for a woman. Cinti, Malibran, Grisi, Taglioni, Pasta, Ellsler—all who reign on stage—can’t be compared to Malaga, who can jump on or off a horse at full gallop, stand on the point of one foot and fall easily into the saddle, knit stockings and make an omelette on horseback—to the admiration of peasants and soldiers. Malaga is dexterity personified; her little wrist or foot can rid her of three or four men. She is the goddess of gymnastics.”

“She must be stupid—”

“Oh, no,” Paz objected. “She is as amusing as a heroine of Peveril. Thoughtless as a Bohemian, she says everything that comes into her head; she thinks no more about the future than you do of the sous you fling to the poor. She says grand things sometimes. You couldn’t make her believe an old diplomatist was handsome even for a million francs. Such love as hers is perpetual flattery. Her health is insolent; she has thirty-two oriental pearls in lips of coral. Her trainer used to beat her to make her supple and intrepid—”

“You are intoxicated with Malaga,” Clémentine said.

“She is called Malaga only on the posters,” Paz replied, piqued. “She lives in the rue Saint‑Lazare in a pretty third-floor apartment, velvet and silk, like a princess. She has two lives: her circus life and the life of a pretty woman.”

“Does she love you?”

“She loves me—now you will laugh—solely because I’m a Pole. She saw an engraving of Poles rushing with Poniatowski into the Elster—for all France persists in thinking the Elster an impetuous flood—and was charmed. But I am very unhappy, madame.” A tear of rage fell from his eyes and touched Clémentine.

“You men have such a passion for singularity,” she said.

“And you?” he asked.

“I know Adam so well that I am certain he could forget me for some mountebank like your Malaga. Where did you first see her?”

“At Saint‑Cloud last September. She was at a corner booth among the shows. Her comrades, in Polish costume, made a horrible racket. I watched her standing there, silent and dumb; I thought I saw melancholy in her. That touched me.”

Clémentine sat pensive. “Poor, poor Thaddeus!” she exclaimed, then, in the kindness of a great lady with a mischievous smile, “Well go, go to your Circus.”

Thaddeus kissed her hand, leaving a hot tear upon it, and went out.

Having invented this passion for a circus‑rider, he set about making it real. The only truth in his tale was the attention he had paid Malaga at Saint‑Cloud; he had seen her on posters since. For ten francs a groom informed him that Malaga was Marguerite Turquet, living in the rue des Fossés‑du‑Temple. The next day Paz found the house and asked to see Mademoiselle Turquet.

“Malaga!” cried the portress; the announcement spread upstairs. Thaddeus’s hat snagged on a clothesline and fell. Malaga picked it up. He said he was reminded of a daughter he had lost and, out of affection for that resemblance, wished to be of service to her. “Why, certainly; pray sit down, monsieur,” said Madame Chapuzot, the portress. Paz insisted he was not gallant but an unfortunate father who deceived himself by resemblance.

“Then am I to pass for your daughter?” Malaga said slyly.

“Yes,” Paz answered, “and I’ll come and see you sometimes. You shall be lodged in better rooms, comfortably furnished.”

Malaga looked at Madame Chapuzot, suspicious but tempted. “I accept,” she said when she learned he was a Pole.

Paz left, promising to return.

A month later the circus‑rider lived in a comfortable apartment furnished by Adam’s upholsterer; Paz had judged it desirable to have his folly talked about at the Hôtel Laginski. Malaga, delighted, was served by Monsieur and Madame Chapuzot as friends and servants. The Chapuzots and Malaga expected some meaning; after three months none could discover it. Paz came about an hour once a week, sat in the salon, and never entered Malaga’s boudoir nor bedroom, despite Chapuzot and Malaga’s maneuvers. Each visit he left two gold pieces of forty francs on the mantel.

“He looks as if he didn’t care to be here,” said Madame Chapuzot.

“Yes, the man’s as cold as an icicle,” said Malaga.

“But he’s a good fellow,” Chapuzot cried, happy in a new blue cloth suit that made him look like a minister’s servant.

Paz’s weekly sum, added to Malaga’s wages, raised her fortunes. Wonderful stories circulated at the Circus about her good luck. Her vanity magnified the six thousand francs Paz spent on furnishing to sixty thousand. In the demi‑monde she was much talked about; the equivocal set attacked her with calumnies: she was said to be somnambulist, magnetized, under the Pole’s influence. Other scandals drew tears from her.

“When I want to injure a woman,” she said, “I don’t calumniate her; I say plainly she is humpbacked, and I prove it. Why do you compromise me?”

Paz kept a cruel silence. Madame Chapuzot discovered his name and title; then she heard facts at the Hôtel Laginski: Paz was a bachelor with no known daughter in Poland or France. Malaga felt terror.

“My dear child,” Madame Chapuzot implored, “that monster—this man who merely looks and says nothing—is trying to get a hold on you and make you do something illegal and criminal. Holy Father, suppose they put you in the newspapers! I’ll warn the police.”

One day, after many foolish fears, Paz laid his money as usual on the mantel; Malaga seized the gold and flung it in his face, crying, “I don’t want stolen money!”

The captain gave the gold to Chapuzot, left without a word, and did not return. Clémentine was at Ronquerolles in Burgundy at this time.

When the troop discovered Malaga had lost her Polish count much excitement followed. Some thought Malaga’s display of honor folly; others thought it shrewd. Paz’s conduct, even when discussed by the cleverest women, seemed inexplicable. He received the next week thirty‑seven letters from women of their kind. Happily for him, his reserve did not excite fashionable curiosity and was only discussed in the demi‑monde.

Two weeks later Marguerite Turquet, crippled by debt, wrote to Comte Paz. The letter fell into Adam’s hands and was read by dandies who pronounced it a masterpiece:

“You, whom I still dare to call my friend, will you not pity me after all that has passed—which you have so ill understood? My heart disavows whatever may have wounded your feelings. If I was fortunate enough to charm you and keep you beside me in the past, return to me; otherwise I shall fall into despair. Poverty has overtaken me; you do not know what horrid things it brings. Yesterday I lived on a herring at two sous and one sou of bread. Is that a breakfast for the woman you loved? The Chapuzots have left me. Your desertion has shown me the worth of human attachments. A sheriff has seized everything for the landlord, and the jeweler would not wait ten days. What a position for a woman who has nothing to reproach herself with but the happiness she has given! My friend, I have taken all I have of any value to my uncle’s; I have nothing but the memory of you and the winter coming. I shall be fireless; the boulevards will play melodramas in which I have only small parts that do not suit a woman. How could you misunderstand the nobleness of my feelings? There are two ways of expressing gratitude. You, who seemed so happy to see me well off, how can you leave me in poverty? Oh, my sole friend on earth, before I go back to country fairs with Bouthor’s circus, where I can at least live, forgive me if I wish to know whether I have lost you forever. If I were to think of you while I jump through hoops, I should break my legs. Whatever the result, I am yours for life.

“Marguerite Turquet.”

“That letter,” thought Thaddeus, laughing, “is worth the ten thousand francs I have spent upon her.”

Clementine returned home the next day; two days later Paz saw her again, more beautiful and graceful than ever. After dinner, while the countess treated Paz with cold indifference, a small scene occurred in the salon when Thaddeus had left, ostensibly by accident, Malaga’s letter with Comte Adam.

“Poor Thaddeus!” Adam said to his wife as Paz slipped away. “What a misfortune for a man of his distinction to be the plaything of the lowest kind of circus-rider. He will lose everything and won’t be recognizable before long. Here, read that.” He handed Malaga’s tobacco‑scented letter to Clementine.

She read it with a look of disgust and threw it aside. Adam continued, convinced that Malaga had tricked Thaddeus. Clementine, practically contemptuous, declared that Thaddeus would forgive Malaga and that such women were exactly those for whom men showed indulgence. Adam defended the man’s firmness—he’d spent relatively little on Malaga and had been reluctant to ask Adam for money—yet admitted that, should he request it, Thaddeus might sacrifice Malaga. Clementine scoffed; Adam worried aloud that if Thaddeus took to intoxication he would be as grieved as if he were his own son.

Paz knew Adam well and had sworn him to secrecy; he had even asked Adam to lend him money for Malaga. But the countess’s tone and Adam’s confidences opened a gulf between her and Thaddeus. Contempt had taken root in her manner and eyes; the thought that he had made her despise him ruined Paz. He suffered periods of despair—life became a burden—and yet he had moments of pure joy in silently admiring and serving her: watching her, tending her horse, managing her household. Those secret pleasures were intense and private, like a mother’s unacknowledged love. Yet even these delights could not heal his unhappiness, because he longed for the reward of respect from the woman he loved—and he had lost it.

Thaddeus’s health suffered: he grew thin and feverish and at one point, in January, kept his bed though he refused a doctor. Adam became uneasy; the countess, with unfeeling cruelty, dismissed his distress as an “Olympian trouble.” Thaddeus recovered after forcing himself into a few amusements.

Late in February Adam lost a large sum at the Jockey‑Club and, afraid of Clementine’s disapproval, begged Thaddeus to let the loss appear in the household accounts as expenditure for Malaga. He feared humiliation before his wife if it were known he had gambled away so much. Thaddeus sighed; he understood the stain this would put upon him. Later, when Clementine discovered additional generosity toward Malaga—twenty thousand more francs—she denounced the waste: thirty thousand francs all told, a sum larger than many bourgeois fortunes. She attacked Thaddeus’s motives and usefulness, yet Adam defended him: Paz had saved forty thousand francs that year through his management and had put a hundred thousand with Nucingen—acting, in effect, as a prudent steward.

Clementine remained hard. She sent for Paz to her boudoir and, with condescension, demanded that if he loved Adam as he claimed he would give up Malaga. She reproached him for allowing himself to be ensnared—“for shame!”—and implied that a man of his distinction should not be caught like a boy. Paz protested; to him the request to give up Malaga was impossible. He explained that he had only delayed leaving her until after the carnival and that Malaga had an influence over him; he added, less successfully, that no respectable woman of society was worth the honest nature of that “young animal.”

Clementine, impatient and disdainful, answered that she wished nothing “of the animal” about herself. Despite the bitterness of the interview, Paz set about instructing the countess in household management. He showed her the state of her affairs, taught her methods of property administration, how to avoid being cheated, and recommended Constantin as major‑domo. By the end of May he judged her competent; she proved to be instinctive and far‑sighted, a woman with an innate managerial genius.

Yet the arrangement offered more trials. Adam fell gravely ill, and Thaddeus, instead of leaving, stayed to nurse him without seeming to think of Malaga—because he truly did not. For forty‑five days Paz tended Adam unweariedly. Clementine, fearful for her husband, sent for Paris’s leading doctors. The chief physician told them the patient’s life depended on the nurses’ vigilance and the right moment to second nature. Thaddeus briefed Clementine on this; he found her sitting in the Chinese pavilion, pale and exhausted. In walking to her he felt the depth of his despair: he had never dreamed to be Clementine’s husband, and now he saw himself drowned in humiliation. When he said Adam had been “very happy” and should be treated with indulgence, Clementine, distracted by worry, answered she had loved him as a wife should. Paz, with surprising frankness, confessed that if Adam died he would be bereft, as he had made a child of Adam long before marriage. He promised to pour every effort into saving him while pleading with her to be frank about the future and the possibility of love. Clementine, exasperated, replied that she loved no one as she wanted to be loved; she preferred a husband she could command to a superior man. When Paz suggested she might not yet know what true love was, she cut him to the quick with a look and a single cruel phrase—“Poor Malaga!”—expressed with the hauteur only a great lady can muster. She rose and left him half stunned.

Paz resumed his silent, relentless care for Adam. His eyes watched like lamps. He accepted Clementine’s thanks without showing resentment, though inwardly he vowed she would owe Adam’s life to him. After weeks of tireless nursing, Bianchon at last told Clementine that Adam was out of danger. On the first drive out, as the carriage left, a gentleman handed a dispatch addressed to Comte Paz. Thaddeus took it and, though Clementine and Adam pressed, kept it in his pocket. Adam’s curiosity prodded him to ask; Thaddeus answered only that the Emperor Nicholas had appointed him captain in a regiment bound for Khiva.

“You are not going?” Adam cried.

“Yes,” Paz said. “Captain I came, and captain I return. If I don’t start at once for St. Petersburg I shall have to go by land, and I am not rich. I must leave Malaga a little independence. I ought to think of the only woman who has been able to understand me; she thinks me grand. She may be faithless, but she would jump—”

Clementine cut him short. His bitter, sarcastic remark about Malaga unsettled her. He spoke of the trees of the Bois and the Champs‑Élysées he would leave. On the borders of Asia he might die unknown and forgotten—or return, perhaps promoted for courage. On that note he fell silent until, passing the wooden building on the Champs‑Élysées, he pointed out the Circus. He went on to the Russian Embassy and the Foreign Office and the next morning left Paris, bound for Havre, and then, ostensibly, for Russia.

News of his departure struck Adam hard. He felt abandoned and could not fathom the reason for Thaddeus’s sudden leave-taking. He remarked that Thaddeus had never touched Malaga’s finger. Clementine, bewildered and uneasy, received a letter from Thaddeus that very night. It was written in a single, burning strain.

Paz’s letter was a confession and a farewell. He explained how, a poor exile, he had found a happy life in being useful to her: grooming her horse, finding funds, arranging splendid dinners and balls, scouring Paris for trifles she desired—joys he could never express. He had loved her with the purity of someone who makes an idealized mistress the sun of his existence; this love had been a daily comfort, a sort of motherhood. He recalled a youthful passion in Warsaw, an unrequited ideal, to show the inward logic of his devotion. He admitted that on one dangerous evening of curiosity—when Clementine had wished to know the man behind her domestic miracles—he had, in a mad moment, invented the story of Malaga, knowing such a liaison would repel women. He had done so because he feared his love would be revealed and he thought that to give expression to it might destroy both their lives. But that was the crime he could not live with; the contempt he read in Clementine’s eyes had become his punishment. He could not bear to remain where her loathing would contaminate him. He insisted he would never betray Adam, that his nursing had been proof he would not let his friend die for the sake of passion. Therefore he left for the Caucasus, preferring to seek death far away rather than carry her contempt with him.

“Adieu, madame,” he wrote; “in leaving Paris I lose all, but you lose nothing in my being no longer near you.”

The night before leaving he also wrote to Adam that he was not bound for Khiva but to the Caucasus, and that his accounts with Nucingen were squared. He added a wry line: he had taken sixty thousand francs from Nucingen, but they were even. Adam, mortified with his own unconscious indiscretion, cursed his curiosity. The letter threw Clementine into deep reflection; the thought that she might have misjudged him hardly crossed her mind before she had the same pang as many Parisian women—the feeling they had passed by someone noble and never recognized him.

Three years passed. The newspapers had not announced any princely elevation for Paz. The Comtesse Laginska followed the distant campaigns of Emperor Nicholas with keen interest and occasionally asked the Russian ambassador, with affected casualness, what had become of “our poor Comte Paz.” The narrative reflects on how many a Paz—noble and misread—moves through Paris unnoticed or is misconceived even when loved. Parisian women often require a social polish before they can appreciate a raw, unvarnished nobility of feeling; to them genuine love must be set like a jewel to have value.

In January, 1842, the Comtesse Laginska, whose gentle melancholy incited a passionate pursuit by the daring Comte de La Palferine, found herself outwitted through the treachery of a woman she trusted. Incapable of suspecting such deceit, she went with that friend to a masked ball at the Opera and, at three in the morning and under excitement, consented to a supper, nearly entering her friend’s carriage. At that moment a powerful hand grasped her arm and took her, despite her struggles, to her own carriage whose door stood open though she had not known it was there. “He has never left Paris!” she cried as she recognized Thaddeus, who had disappeared by the time the carriage drove away.

Thus, in a final, touching turn, Clementine constantly hopes she may see Paz again—an outcome she did not foresee but now cherishes as a romantic possibility.
