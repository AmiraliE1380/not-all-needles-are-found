To Monsieur J. B. Nacquart, Member of the Royal Academy of Medicine.

Dear Doctor—Here is one of the most carefully hewn stones in the second course of the foundation of a literary edifice which I have slowly and laboriously constructed. I wish to inscribe your name upon it, as much to thank the man whose science once saved me as to honor the friend of my daily life.

De Balzac.

Felix de Vandenesse to Madame la Comtesse Natalie de Manerville:

I yield to your wishes. It is the privilege of the women whom we love more than they love us to make the men who love them ignore the ordinary rules of common-sense. To smooth the frown upon their brow, to soften the pout upon their lips, what obstacles we miraculously overcome! We shed our blood, we risk our future!

You exact the history of my past life; here it is. But remember this, Natalie; in obeying you I crush under foot a reluctance hitherto unconquerable. Why are you jealous of the sudden reveries which overtake me in the midst of our happiness? Why show the pretty anger of a petted woman when silence grasps me? Could you not play upon the contradictions of my character without inquiring into the causes of them? Are there secrets in your heart which seek absolution through a knowledge of mine? Ah! Natalie, you have guessed mine; and it is better you should know the whole truth. Yes, my life is shadowed by a phantom; a word evokes it; it hovers vaguely above me and about me; within my soul are solemn memories, buried in its depths like those marine productions seen in calmest weather and which the storms of ocean cast in fragments on the shore.

The mental labor which the expression of ideas necessitates has revived the old, old feelings which give me so much pain when they come suddenly; and if in this confession of my past they break forth in a way that wounds you, remember that you threatened to punish me if I did not obey your wishes, and do not, therefore, punish my obedience. I would that this, my confidence, might increase your love.

Until we meet,

Felix.

To what genius nourished by tears shall we one day owe the most touching elegy—the tale of torments silently borne by souls whose tender roots find stony ground at home? The true history of a childhood stunted by neglect and coldness would move pity more surely than public tragedies. Such was my childhood.

From infancy I knew a mixture of neglect and occasional scrutiny. After three years with a nurse in the country I returned to a household where indifference reigned; even the servants, who pitied me, seemed kinder than those near me by blood. My elder brother and sisters, encouraged by our mother's unequal temper and favoritism toward the eldest, persecuted me. The protective compact among children was annulled in my case. Often punished for my brother's faults and forbidden to plead my innocence, I learned mistrust early. These wrongs did not produce servility or vice; rather they hardened me. Constant expectation of fresh blows bred a sullen resignation and a premature pride that tempered the ill effects of my upbringing.

Sometimes my mother spoke of educating me herself; the very thought chilled me, and I blessed the neglect that let me wander through the garden, watch insects, and fix my attention on a star. One evening, crouched under a fig-tree and absorbed by a star, I was falsely accused by our governess, Mademoiselle Caroline, of mischief with a garden faucet and severely punished. The star, however, remained mine; forbidden pleasures grew more desirable, and I kept that glowing point in my imagination through boyhood.

Charles, five years older, was the household favorite: handsome, robust, indulged. I, puny and sickly, at five was sent as a day pupil to a town school with only a scanty lunch. The contrast between my hunger and my companions’ plenty wounded me deeply. Boys taunted me over rillons and delicacies I did not possess; one cruel trick—the withdrawal of a promised slice—left me ashamed and resolve-deepened. Once I was stoned; my mother heard of it and only lamented my supposed dangerous temperament.

Rejected by family and classmates, I distrusted myself and others. At twelve I was sent to Pont‑le‑Voy, an Oratorian school where dull boys languished. For eight years I lived a pariah’s life; my three francs a month barely covered study needs; I could not afford games and was excluded from playground life except by humiliating flattery of the rich. I took refuge beneath a tree with borrowed books and thought much. When I won prizes for composition and translation and my parents did not appear at the theatre, I burst into tears, burned my crowns, and felt the deeper ache of being forsaken.

The sense of injustice drove me toward religion. At first communion I sought consolation in prayer and martyrology; youthful zeal fed sublime visions that strengthened imagination and deepened my inner life—qualities that would later temper any talent I might have. My father, uneasy about the Oratorians, sent me to Paris to the Lepitre Academy at fifteen. There, as before, neglect continued: meager pocket-money, strict discipline, and social restrictions. Monsieur Lepitre—old, ceremonious, given to Bourbon sympathies—managed a small subvention for me but never enough.

In Lepitre’s house the porter Doisy, a smuggler of comforts for pupils, set up breakfasts and small luxuries for the well-to-do; resisting his blandishments was heroic for a hungry boy. Once my parents’ arrival in Paris promised escape: my brother planned to take us to dinner in the Palais‑Royal and to a theatre. But I owed a hundred francs to Doisy; my mother, infuriated, refused indulgence and reproached me with future ruin. Sent back to Lepitre’s charge, I lost the dinner and the play—the first meeting with my mother after twelve years apart ended in reproach.

At nineteen, still under Lepitre’s guardianship and constrained by lack of money and supervision, I grew desperate for the city. My aunt, the Marquise de Listomère, on the Île Saint‑Louis, was ceremonious and cold; she would not offer money and made me feel ashamed of my youth. Several attempts to slip away to the Palais‑Royal were thwarted by unlucky coincidences: once Monsieur Lepitre’s coach appeared, another time my mother unexpectedly arrived, and a third time a post-chaise caught me. My family’s anxieties, political as well as moral, kept me under restraint.

Study became both refuge and peril; absorbed in books, I delayed the normal stirrings of youth. In Tours, my mother’s coldness crushed me; on a journey home I threw myself at her feet, begged for affection, and was mocked as playing at “comedy.” Once at Blois I stood on the bridge intending to throw myself into the Loire but could not commit the act. Back at home I found only the slight mercies of my sisters and poor clothing. My mother’s ironical speeches and punctilious sense of duty made affection a painful, unreliable thing.

Yet life at Tours had public excitement. The Duc d’Angoulême passed in triumph; Touraine celebrated, and I longed to attend a ball given in his honor. Despite my mother’s mockery, secret preparations were made: a bottle-blue coat, silk stockings, and a frilled shirt—my first finery. The ball overwhelmed me: heat, music, a crush of people, military bands, and cries for the duke. At first the spectacle disgusted me, but then a strange ambition stirred—an appetite for glory and influence over the masses.

Amid the crowd I saw a woman who changed me. Mistaking her for my mother’s shoulders, I was rebuked by a single drawn word, “Monsieur!” Tears sprang to my eyes, and the look that followed—noble, angry, forgiving—awoke a passion I had never known. She moved with queenly grace and a purity that transformed my life: my star had become flesh. From then on the sky seemed bluer and I sought her everywhere in Touraine.

My mother, seeing my melancholy, sent me for a few weeks to Frapesle, a château on the Indre, hoping country air would restore me. For a young man newly in love but ignorant of his beloved’s name or station, this was an opportunity. Walking the country, I examined every house and old tower as if she might be there. The landscape—moors, woods, the valley of the Indre—enchanted me; in that region beauty is intrinsic to the air and my soul felt in tune with it.

From a walnut‑tree where I later loved to pause, I first saw the castle that housed the woman I sought. A white spot among the vines, cambric fluttering in an arbor, convinced me she was there. The castle, Clochegourde, belonged to the Comte de Mortsauf and his wife, a Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt—an ancient house, proud and secluded, their fortune limited, habits fixed. My host, Monsieur de Chessel, who knew the count and countess, proposed we cross the river and call; his teasing reading of my enthusiasm revealed how transparent my feelings had become.

Clochegourde was modest but tasteful: five windows on the façade, terraced gardens, acacias, vines, and neat outbuildings. From the road the ground‑floor seemed an upper story; a little tourelle and carved crest over the portico testified to lineage. The house sat in the valley like a jewel in green; approaching it quickened my heart with foreboding and delight.

We were announced shortly after the bell for breakfast; the count was at Azay but the countess—whose voice I had heard at the ball—welcomed us. My agitation nearly betrayed me; she returned to her embroidery and spoke with a calm dignity that both concealed and revealed feeling. Monsieur de Chessel explained my fatigue from walking and my being a Touraine native newly returned. Madame de Mortsauf offered hospitality with gentle courtesy; her glance, severe at first, softened when she saw my distress. She gave restoratives, words of comfort, and after consultation with Monsieur de Chessel invited us to dine. That invitation—accepted—seemed a promise: if I pleased her, my introducer might remain in the count’s good graces.

Madame de Mortsauf, though reserved, possessed a voice and manner that entranced me. At dinner she spoke of harvests and local affairs; at first she seemed to shut me out, but I discovered the charm of listening to her. The cadence of her speech, the brevity of her glances, the rare but luminous intonations moved me more than any artifice. I would prolong conversations and accept reproach merely to hear the melody of her voice and drink the subtle air of her presence.

Her appearance—white shoulders, gauze veiling her bosom, braids above a soft throat—had impressed me at the ball; up close, in everyday dress, she retained that luminous appeal. Her face defied easy description: an artist could best render the inward fires and restraint that made her expression unique. Her hair was fair and soft; her brow broad and thoughtful; her eyes green tinged with brown—usually wan but capable of sudden gleams touched by sorrow or joy. A Grecian nose and graceful lips gave spiritual beauty; her figure combined strength with unusual softness. She seemed both mother and maiden—grave from suffering, yet childlike in feeling. Her movements, rare glances, and simplicity of dress formed a charm that made men dream: she revealed, without ambition, a virginal purity and moral elevation that made others thoughtful.

Beyond physical features, it was the constant emanation of her soul—tenderness to family, cheerfulness in serenity, resignation amid storms—that impressed me most. Clochegourde’s salon, panelled and modestly ornamented, matched her spirit: gray paint, porcelain vases with Cape heather, cambric curtains, and an embroidery frame. From its windows the valley unrolled—Pont‑de‑Ruan, château d’Azay, Sache—images that would nourish my ideas and transform my mind. Many of my thoughts in science and politics seemed first to live there, in that composed, reflective atmosphere. The salon’s quiet regularity deepened the impression of a domestic epic lived in a small and noble sphere.

When Monsieur de Mortsauf returned, my curiosity about him vied with fear that he might obstruct my hopes. A little girl, Madeleine, entered—fragile and pale—and the mother’s brow clouded. Madeleine, though thin and wan, preserved a spirit; Jacques, her brother, similarly weak, followed the count. The children’s frailty suggested family sorrow; Madame de Mortsauf’s health and spirit bore the cost. Monsieur de Mortsauf, about forty‑five but aged by care, offered a polite, uneasy courtesy. His manner concealed more than it revealed, but the domestic picture—motherly care, frail children, the reserve of the house—revealed a life of tenderness restrained by trial.

As the visit continued, I grew more attentive to the count’s attitudes. Though outwardly civil, he was awkward and uneasy, like a man whose distrust springs from inability to understand rather than malice; yet his demeanor warmed when he learned my name and family connections. There was a respectful recognition of lineage: my father had served the Bourbons, and such devotion could not be disregarded by a man of old family like Mortsauf. The count’s complexity hinted at tragedies and virtues entangled by history—a man whose public reserve sheltered private tenderness.

Thus began a sequence of days at Clochegourde that would shape my life. The valley, the house, the maternal presence, and that first vivid encounter set in motion a passion that combined gratitude, reverence, and the consuming hunger of a first love. My lonely childhood had prepared me to adore and worship with an intensity only such neglect can generate. The domestic world of the Mortsaufs—humble and dignified—provided the soil into which my youthful devotion sank its roots.

I learned quickly the subtleties of Madame de Mortsauf’s manner: the economy of her speech, the weight of her silences, the glances scarce as jewels. Her conversation, even on trivial matters, carried an undertone betraying a depth of soul. Her benevolence and austere tenderness drew me into a state where admiration and self‑sacrifice mingled. I noted the simplicity of the household, the English tidiness of the salon, the embroidery, the backgammon board, the garden, the interlaced paths. Each small thing seemed to speak of moral discipline and inner warmth.

The more I saw of Clochegourde’s family life, the clearer it became that respect would come as easily as passion; here was a woman whose humanity compelled love. The contrast between their modest means and noble air made an impression: pride without display, ancestral dignity humbled by circumstance. This image of noble poverty added to my reverence.

In the strangeness of my past—neglected by my mother, punished unjustly, driven inward by study and prayer—the spectacle of such domestic devotion opened me like a flower. It is not only beauty that wins the heart, but constancy, sacrifice, and the gentle influence of a soul that turns small duties into holy acts. Thus Clochegourde, its valley, and above all its countess, became the scene and cause of a passion that would shape my destiny.

Our visit ended with impressions words could not fully render. Madame de Mortsauf’s presence, the music of her voice, her discreet tenderness for the children, and the quiet dignity of the home remained with me. I left with a mingled feeling of hope and helplessness—hope because I had shared an attentive hour in her presence; helplessness because I was too young, too dependent, and too ignorant of the world to know how to win her friendship beyond the simple worship of a distant admirer.

Thus closes the account of my early years to the point where my life after twenty begins under influences both tender and decisive. From the neglect of my family to the sanctuary of the valley, my character was formed—partly by solitude and hard study, partly by the sudden appearance of a woman whose moral and physical beauty made of her my guiding star. The meeting at the ball and at Clochegourde introduced an affection equal parts devotion and pain, opening the way to all that followed.

################################################
Though ten years of emigration and farm life had altered his body, the count still bore traces of nobility. Even a staunch liberal could admire his chivalric loyalty and steadfast convictions: a man devoted to a cause, honest in his politics, yet ignorant of France’s practical affairs. He was the sort of upright soul who, unable to serve his party, might inadvertently harm it—ready to sacrifice his life rather than his money.

At dinner I noticed the count’s hollow cheeks and furtive looks at his children, signs of a burdened mind and frail constitution he had passed on to them. He might blame himself but refused to be judged; his harsh demeanor, born of self-awareness, magnified his family’s misery. When his wife returned with the children, an atmosphere of unhappiness settled over the house.

After conversation shifted, the count introduced me to his wife and, with curious intimacy, revealed details about my family I had not known. He asked my age; when I answered, the countess expressed surprise at her daughter’s age, perhaps thinking me younger. Her maternal instincts ignited hope for her children and a cautious curiosity about me. She asked many questions but remained mostly silent.

“If study has made you ill,” she said, “the air of our valley will soon restore you.”

“Modern education harms children,” the count replied. “We burden them with mathematics and sciences, aging them prematurely. You must stay and rest here. You are overwhelmed by ideas. What future will universal education have unless we return it to religious hands?” His remark later explained why he refused to support a talented candidate: “I shall always distrust men of talent.”

He proposed a tour of the gardens.

“Monsieur—” his wife interjected.

“What is it, my dear?” he replied, revealing his desire to control his household.

“Monsieur de Vandenesse walked from Tours this morning, and Monsieur de Chessel has already taken him on foot over Frapesle.”

“Very imprudent of you,” the count said, shaking his head.

I quickly realized that his royalism’s rigidity required careful navigation. The manservant announced dinner. Monsieur de Chessel offered his arm to Madame de Mortsauf, while the count took mine, and we went to the dining room.

The dining room, with its white-tiled floor and varnished paper adorned with floral designs, was charming though unpretentious. Dinner was plentiful but modest: family silver, mismatched china, flowers on the table. I found joy in these quaint details, feeling contentment that briefly blinded me to country-life obstacles. I sat beside her, serving her wine, and felt a profound connection. That kiss had bound us in a secret that filled me with both joy and shame. I endeavored to please the count, lavishing affection on the dogs and trying to engage the children, hoping they would accept me as one of their own. I sensed any sign of gloom would jeopardize my place in their lives.

Dinner passed in a haze of happiness: I reveled in being under her roof, ignoring her apparent coldness and the count’s polite indifference. Love, like life, has an adolescence, and I was content in my feelings. I made several foolish remarks, driven by passion, but no one noticed their cause; even she was oblivious to love. The rest of my visit felt dreamlike as I returned across the Indre, enchanted by moonlight and nature’s sounds. That evening marked a turning point; I felt the weight of past struggles clash with the joy I had just experienced.

Approaching Frapesle, I glanced at Clochegourde and saw a small boat tied to a tree—Monsieur de Mortsauf’s fishing boat.

“Well,” said Monsieur de Chessel once we were out of earshot, “I needn’t ask if you found those shoulders; I must congratulate you on the reception Monsieur de Mortsauf gave you. You stepped into his heart at once.”

His words lifted my spirits, though I sensed underlying bitterness.

“How do you mean?” I asked.

“He has never received anyone so well,” he replied.

Monsieur de Chessel, whose true name was Durand, had abandoned his father’s merchant name—a successful businessman during the Revolution—to adopt an old parliamentary name. Ambition led him to claim a count’s title under Louis XVIII, a title his children enjoyed without understanding the sacrifices it cost him. His character bore pettiness and envy; his ambition, recently rekindled by royal favor, made him ostentatious yet kindly toward me, providing a peace I had longed for. His interest in my affairs felt paternal, a contrast to the neglect I knew; I was grateful for his hospitality.

The owners of Frapesle were intertwined with the dawn of my happiness, and I cherished those memories. Monsieur de Chessel’s ostentation annoyed some neighbors—fine horses, lavish entertaining—while the Comte de Mortsauf, with limited means, struggled to maintain his estate. Their relations were polite but lacked intimacy.

Jealousy was not the sole cause of the count’s isolation. His education had been superficial; he emigrated just as his learning should have deepened. Long idleness followed, and hopes for a restored monarchy kept him from seeking work; the trials of life eroded his spirit. His natural gaiety faded into moroseness; he fell ill with a disposition inclined to hypochondria. Hidden love affairs further deteriorated his health and prospects.

After twelve years of hardship he returned to France under a decree allowing emigrants back. Crossing the Rhine he cried “France! France!” like a child calling for its mother. Once wealthy, he came back impoverished, stripped of authority and influence, burdened by his name. His unyielding opinions and past left him vulnerable to ridicule in a society that often mocked weakness. He eventually settled in Maine on a forgotten farm that offered some stability.

The Lenoncourt family, having lost their fortune, welcomed him; he stayed with them while preparing a home. Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt, rather than rejecting him, accepted his proposal and lived with her aunt, the Duchesse de Verneuil, who guided her life. Madame de Verneuil, a member of a devout society, profoundly influenced the countess, who remained devout. After the duchess’s sudden death, Madame de Mortsauf received visits from Saint-Martin, who corrected his last works at Clochegourde. The duchess had endured trials and left Clochegourde to her niece, but her death cast a shadow over the early years of the marriage.

Monsieur de Mortsauf found some solace in the valley, but the birth of his first child, Jacques, brought despair: the doctor pronounced the child unviable, a truth the count concealed from his wife. Subsequent births confirmed his fears, deepening his sense of failure. The countess, sensing her husband’s turmoil, devoted herself to his care, striving to reconcile him with himself. She accepted his miserliness and mistrust, guiding him toward what was right, often at the expense of her own happiness. She resolved to remain at Clochegourde, fearing that leaving would harm their children. Her efforts masked the count’s incapacity, allowing him to find some peace in her presence.

This brief history also illuminated Monsieur de Chessel, who, despite worldly knowledge, could not fully grasp Clochegourde’s complexities. My own understanding deepened as I spent time there and felt the weight of unspoken truths. One evening I found Madame de Mortsauf watching a sunset, and I sensed a shared sorrow.

“Some days are hard to bear,” I remarked.

“You read my soul,” she replied, surprised.

“We touch at many points,” I said, recognizing our shared experiences of joy and sorrow.

She shuddered, and I felt compelled to express my understanding of our mutual suffering.

“Ah! My childhood was like a long illness—”

“I hear Madeleine coughing,” she interrupted, and left abruptly.

The countess welcomed my visits, seeing in me an amusement for her husband. I often played backgammon with the count, enduring his harshness and criticism. Despite his tyranny, the countess’s presence softened my hardships.

As I grew better at the game, the count’s temper flared when I began to win. I learned to balance our matches—letting him win at first, then evening the score. The countess understood my strategy and thanked me with a grateful gaze that filled me with joy.

One evening, after an intense game, the count’s mood shifted dramatically into rage. I left the room and waited in the garden. When she joined me, she revealed her husband’s condition and asked me to forget the incident.

“Promise me to bury the recollection of that scene,” she implored. “I don’t ask you to swear it; just give me your word of honor.”

“I will do all you wish,” I replied, “but I cannot change my opinion of him.”

She acknowledged my feelings and urged me to understand his struggles.

“Perhaps for my sake, you will forget what he said to you today,” she suggested. “He will wish to undo the wrong he did.”

“Five days without seeing you?” I lamented.

“Do not put such warmth into your manner of speaking to me,” she cautioned.

We walked in silence; she eventually said, “It is late; we will part.”

I wished to kiss her hand; she hesitated before allowing it.

“Never take it unless I give it to you,” she warned. “Leave me my freedom.”

As I departed I felt a profound connection, pledging my love in silence.

The next day I reflected on our bond, feeling purpose and commitment to her. I recognized her sacrifices and strength. Our shared sufferings had forged a connection beyond friendship, binding us in a deep understanding of each other’s pain and resilience.

In the days that followed, small moments deepened my affection. I was determined to support her, to be a source of strength, and to honor the bond we had formed. Navigating my feelings, I understood our connection was rare and precious, something I would cherish and protect. Beneath the stars I vowed to honor her spirit and nurture the love that had blossomed between us—an affection meant to endure through life’s trials.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
This scene happened on a Tuesday; I had waited until Sunday and had not yet crossed the river. Much had occurred at Clochegourde during those five days. The count’s promotion to general had been confirmed; he received the cross of Saint-Louis and a pension of four thousand francs. The Duc de Lenoncourt-Givry, restored as peer of France, reclaimed two forests and his place at court; his wife recovered unsold imperial property. As a result the Comtesse de Mortsauf became an heiress: her mother arrived at Clochegourde with a hundred thousand francs saved at Givry—the dowry the count had never demanded in their earlier poverty. With his savings he bought two neighboring estates yielding about nine thousand francs a year. Their son would inherit his grandfather’s peerage; the count thought to entail the estate on him without harming Madeleine, and the Duc de Lenoncourt might help secure a good marriage.

These events calmed the count. The duchesse’s presence at Clochegourde changed the neighborhood’s tone. I reflected that her caste spirit—hidden from me by my love for Henriette—now emerged. Who was I—poor and insignificant, with only courage and faculties? On Sunday morning, seated in the private chapel with Monsieur and Madame de Chessel and the Abbé de Quelus, I glanced toward the duchess’s pew and saw her with her daughter, the count and his children, at prayer. Henriette’s broad straw hat did not tremble; her composed indifference to my presence bound me to her more than all our past. She prayed with such abandonment she seemed a religious statue; it moved me profoundly.

After mass vespers followed. Madame de Chessel proposed going to Frapesle rather than crossing the Indre twice in the heat. Monsieur de Chessel offered his arm to the duchess, Madame de Chessel took the count’s, and I offered mine to the countess, feeling her arm against mine for the first time. Walking through the woods of Sache, where light filtered through foliage, sensations and proud thoughts overwhelmed me; my heart beat violently.

“What is the matter?” she asked.

“I have heard of your good fortune,” I said. “Will your new dignities change you and lessen your friendship?”

“Change me!” she cried. “Oh, fie! Another such idea and I shall—not despise you, but forget you forever.”

I looked at her in ecstasy.

“We profit by new laws which we have neither made nor sought,” she said. “We are neither place-hunters nor beggars; neither Monsieur de Mortsauf nor I can leave Clochegourde. By my advice he declined the command at the Maison Rouge. We are content my father should have it. This forced modesty has benefited our son. The king—whose household my father joins—will favor Jacques’ education. He must represent both Lenoncourt and Mortsauf. I can have no ambition except for him; he must live and be made worthy. Who will keep him safe in Paris? My friend,” she added, voice breaking, “be some day his guardian and sponsor. Go to Paris; if your family will not support you, ours—especially my mother, who rules society—will help you. Put your desires into a noble ambition—”

“I understand,” I interrupted. “Ambition will be my mistress. But I will not be rewarded for obedience here by favors there. I will go and make my own way. From you, I would accept everything; from others, nothing.”

“Child!” she murmured, pleased.

“Besides, I have taken my vows,” I continued. “I am resolved to bind myself to you by ties that can never be broken.”

She trembled and stopped.

“What do you mean?” she asked.

“First tell me how you wish me to love you.”

“Love me as my aunt loved me,” she answered. “I gave you her rights when I permitted you to call me by her chosen name.”

“Then I must love without hope and with absolute devotion. I will enter a seminary, become a priest, and educate your son. Jacques shall be myself in his own form; political conceptions, energy, patience—I will give him all. I will live near you; my love, enclosed in religion, can never be suspected of evil. I will consume myself in the flame and love you with a purified love.”

She turned pale. “Félix, do not bind yourself in a way that might be an obstacle. I should die of grief. Do you think despairing love a life’s vocation? Wait for life’s trials. Marry neither the Church nor a woman—marry not at all—I forbid it. Remain free. You are twenty-one—My God! can I have mistaken him? I thought two months sufficed to know some souls.”

“What hope have you?” I cried.

“Accept our help, rise in life, make your fortune, and you shall know my hope. And,” she added as if whispering, “never release the hand you are holding at this moment.”

She bent to my ear as she said it.

“Madeleine!” I exclaimed. “Never!”

We neared the wooden gate into Frapesle’s park. At that instant the thought of the count’s death flashed through me, and I said, “I understand you.”

“I am glad of it,” she answered, surprised.

Her purity provoked tears of admiration mingled with the bitterness of passion. I thought she did not love me enough even to wish me liberty; love that recoils from crime seemed limited. She did not love me, I thought. To hide my pain, I kissed Madeleine’s hair. “I am afraid of your mother,” I said.

“So am I,” she answered playfully. “Don’t forget to call her Madame la duchesse and speak of her in the third person. Young people have lost polite manners; you must learn them for my sake. It is good taste to respect women, no matter their age, and to recognize social distinctions. The respect shown to established superiority guarantees what is due to you. You have drunk the milk of the Revolution in your academy; but as you advance, you will find crude principles of liberty powerless to create happiness. Before considering what an aristocracy ought to be, my common sense tells me societies can exist only through a hierarchy. Be on our side—especially now,” she added, laughing, “when it triumphs.”

I was moved by her combination of political feeling and affection. In defending her husband she foresaw criticisms I might make of the obsequious distance he kept before the duchess. Monsieur de Mortsauf, king in his castle, had dignity; yet I was astonished at how humiliated he was before the duchess. Humiliated by his degradation, I understood the martyrdom of generous women yoked to petty men. I treated the duchess with respect because of my youth; where others saw a duchess, I saw Henriette’s mother, and that sanctified my homage.

At Frapesle the count presented me to the duchess, who examined me coldly. Madame de Lenoncourt was fifty-six, wonderfully preserved, erect and imposing, with a hard face and courtly manner reminiscent of my own mother. I behaved well; as I passed the countess she said softly, “You are perfect.”

The count took my hand. “You are not angry with me, Félix, are you? If I was hasty, pardon an old soldier. We shall probably stay for dinner, and I invite you on Thursday, the evening before the duchess leaves. I must go to Tours tomorrow. Don’t neglect Clochegourde. My mother-in-law is an acquaintance I advise you to cultivate. Her salon will set the tone for the faubourg St. Germain. She has all the traditions of the great world and immense social knowledge.”

The count’s changed fortunes softened him. The duchess was not patronizing; Monsieur and Madame de Chessel accepted the invitation. She questioned me about my family and, learning that Vandenesse in diplomacy was my brother, grew almost affectionate. She told me my great-aunt, the old Marquise de Listomere, was a Grandlieu, and that by a provision of the Charter my father was once more Marquis de Vandenesse.

“I am but one thing, the serf of Clochegourde,” I answered.

The Restoration’s transformations bewildered our generation. To me, the revolution meant nothing; the least word from Madame de Mortsauf was the single important event. I was ignorant of councils and politics; my sole ambition was to love Henriette better than Petrarch loved Laura. This indifference made the duchess take me for a child. A large company assembled at Frapesle, and to a young man unused to the world, seeing the woman he loves admired by all is intoxicating. The count, delighted, seemed almost young; his wife hoped for change; I amused myself with Madeleine, whose cleverness made others laugh. It was a happy day; a word, a hope awakened in the morning and illuminated nature. Seeing me joyous, Henriette was joyful too.

“That happiness smiling on my gray and cloudy life seems good,” she said to me the next day.

I spent that day at Clochegourde; the count left early for Tours. A serious disagreement arose between mother and daughter. The duchess wanted the countess to move to Paris, where she promised a place at court and where the count might accept a position. Henriette, concealing her husband’s incapacity and her own sufferings, opposed leaving Clochegourde because its air calmed his excitements and soothed the children’s health. She had persuaded him to go to Tours so as to hide his condition from the duchess. I alone knew Clochegourde’s dark secret.

Henriette saw that the duchess cared little for Jacques and Madeleine—a painful discovery. The duchess brooked no opposition; she used sweetness, then coldness, then sarcasm to force compliance. In those ten days Henriette endured the struggles a young woman must face to assert independence. The mother and daughter had nothing in common; the duchess could not imagine the reasons Henriette stayed. Tortured and wounded, Henriette loved the one who did not strike her, who would build a shield around her. I was happy when she rested upon my heart; she told me of her troubles. Day by day I learned what she meant by “Love me as my aunt loved me.”

“Have you no ambition?” the duchess asked me at dinner.

“Madame,” I replied, “I have enough in me to conquer the world; but I am only twenty-one, and I am all alone.”

She looked at her daughter in astonishment. The duchess’s visit meant constraint. The countess begged me to be cautious. The Thursday of ceremony was wearying, but the duchess returned to court and Clochegourde resumed its ordinary life.

My quarrel with the count had made me more at home; I could go there at all times. I clung like a climbing plant to the beautiful soul that had opened the world of shared emotions. Our fraternal marriage, based on trust, grew firmer; Henriette enfolded me with maternal care, while my love for her burned when I was away. I loved her with a double love—seraphic in her presence, searing when absent. At my age I was not yet man enough to torment her; she feared harm to her children and sat beside them when her husband allowed her a little rest. The mere sound of ardor shook her; she needed veiled love—support mingled with tenderness—the devotion she gave others. This brought languor and secret immolation. Her conscience and self-devotion awed me; the piety that bound her virtues filled the air with spiritual incense. She gave me only the back of her hand to kiss, never the palm, as though limiting sensuality. No two souls ever clasped each other with such ardor while remaining unbound.

I became part of the household and knew infinite sweetness—the privilege of being the mistress’s friend in a strange family. Servants treated me kindly; the children divined my affection and were discreet. True passions bloom more charmingly in barren soil.

But I also saw Monsieur de Mortsauf’s failings: perpetual scolding about nothing, complaints of imaginary ills, an inward dissatisfaction that demanded victims. On walks he chose the way yet complained whichever way we went; at home he blamed others. He took pleasure in touching his wife’s sensitive chords. Sometimes he fell into gloomy silence that alarmed her and brought tender attentions. Like a spoiled child, he let her serve him as she did the children. I discovered his petty tyranny in household details and realized how it stifled the family, hindered management, and impeded improvement of the estate. The countess had been noble in describing these daily struggles. My admiration for her rose above my love; I yielded to be a voluntary victim to come nearer her. She allowed me a place beside her and permission to share her sorrows; eager to die in her arena, I became a repentant apostate seeking heaven with my brethren.

“Were it not for you, I must have succumbed under this life,” Henriette said when the count had been more venomous than usual.

He had gone to bed. Henriette and I stayed under the acacias with the children playing in the setting sun. Our few words revealed mutual thoughts; when language failed our silence served, and our souls seemed to enter one another. We luxuriated in pensive languor and asked by a look, “Among all our days on earth, will there be one for us?”

Despite the evening’s poetry, desire crept over me. After three months of repression I could not accept my assigned fate. I took Henriette’s hand and caressed it. She withdrew at once; tears from my eyes fell and she offered her hand to my lips.

“You must know,” she said, “that this will cause me grief. A friendship that asks such a favor is dangerous.”

Then I lost control; I reproached her, spoke of my suffering and of a slight alleviation I dared request. I told her that at my age the soul had a sex and that I could meet death but not silence my cries. She forced me to silence with a proud glance. Ever since the gate of Frapesle, when I had taken her hope to be born from a grave, I had shunned the thought of staining her soul. She said she could never be wholly mine; if I could not be to her somewhat as her old confessor—less than a lover yet more than a brother—she must never see me again. She could die and offer her sufferings to God.

“I gave you,” she said, “more than I ought, so that nothing might be left to take, and I am punished.”

I calmed her, promised never to cause pain, and to love her as an old man loves his youngest child.

The next day I came early; there were no flowers in her gray salon. I rushed into the fields and vineyards to make two bouquets; as I gathered them it occurred to me that color and foliage have meanings, and by melody awaken memory. I enlisted Jacques and Madeleine and arranged two bouquets on the portico steps: white roses and lilies with blue flowers intermingled, symbolizing two purities—the child and the martyr. The countess discerned the sentiment and gave me a poignant glance, reward enough. I pressed Père Castel’s theories into service and recovered that lost science: the language of flowers. What charm in expressing our sensations through these daughters of the sun! I communed with the flora, seeking varieties that would complete my messages.

Twice a week I pursued this poetic enterprise, walking long distances to find flowers, taking pleasure in the world of feeling rather than form. Nature offered scenes rising to high moral conceptions: heather with dew like diamonds, mossy rocks like savage cries, anemone pulsatilla like my pure idol alone in her valley, sheets of water with floating life, a cottage garden as humble existence’s image, a forest path like a cathedral nave. I gathered these images and poured them into bouquets.

In September and October no bouquet cost less than three hours’ search. These floral symphonies expressed what no declaration could; my baffled desires found expression as Beethoven finds it in notes. In their presence Madame de Mortsauf was my Henriette. She looked at them often; they nourished her spirit. These neutral pleasures soothed me: abstinence exhausts, and a few crumbs of heavenly comfort keep one from disaster. Sometimes I found her standing before the bouquets, lost in agitation; she would lift her head and murmur, “Ah, how beautiful!”

Throughout the month I often saw her at the window when I arrived. If I was late, she wandered on the terrace and said she had come to meet me and tend her youngest child.

Backgammon had ended; the count’s purchases occupied him in inspecting his new properties. He was neither arrogant nor offensive, and our walks together were mutual joys. Once, the count, wishing to know what we talked about, asked; Henriette answered with words having a double meaning that satisfied him. Madeleine laughed. Our spiritual union continued.

The days passed with new joys. The grape harvest—the vintage—began in late September. The vintage brings abundance and hilarity: coopers and carts, girls and panniers. Classes are equal for the day; everyone shares the harvest. Jacques and Madeleine, who had never seen a vintage, were full of delight. We went to Villaines for baskets; none of us were to eat too many grapes. Jacques made me swear to stay at Clochegourde. The day in the vineyard was glorious: the children’s chatter, their racing about, the mother laughing with the joy of youth; she passed a hand round my neck and tapped my cheek—the only caress she ever gave me. I filled my basket and emptied it into the vat, moving slowly in regulated motion that kept my spirit free. I found the pleasure of external labor that regulates passion; I learned monastic discipline.

For the first time in weeks the count was neither surly nor cruel. His son’s rosy face rejoiced him. He had promised a dance in front of Clochegourde to celebrate the Bourbons’ return. As we returned, the countess leaned on my arm and whispered, “You bring us happiness.”

To me, burdened with knowledge of her sleepless nights and cares, those words were more than any other woman could give.

“The terrible monotony of my life is broken; all things are radiant with hope,” she said. “Oh, never leave me! Be the elder son, the protector of the younger.”

This is not romantic; in youth passion restrained by obstacles can fill the crater of a volcano with pure water.

We had another fête. The countess, wishing to accustom her children to practical life, gave Jacques the produce of the walnut trees and Madeleine that of the chestnuts. Gathering chestnuts and walnuts, seeing the children take pride in their small granaries, made charming scenes of innocent happiness amid autumn’s fading colors. Jacques’ walnuts suffered from rain; I advised him to hold back and sell later. His father urged prudence; the mother rejoiced to see Jacques listen. These homely scenes attached me ever more closely to Henriette.

I learned how, over ten years, the countess had changed cultivation—rotating crops and dividing the estate into four farms on share leases. She induced the count to cultivate a model farm and rebuild two farmhouses to relet for money rents afterward. Fearing to die before her husband, she sought to secure his income and her children’s property. Fruit trees planted in the last ten years were bearing; hedges and elms grew well. With the new purchases and farming system, Clochegourde—divided into four farms—could bring in forty thousand francs a year, not counting vineyards and woodlands. The new road to Chinon would attract tenants. The countess wished to invest fifteen thousand francs in each new estate to improve houses for better rents; this required thirty thousand francs, which she and her husband argued about. The fear “If I die tomorrow, what will become of them?” drove her. The count objected, but common sense won. That day we went to the Cassine and the Rhetorière to decide on buildings; she spoke gently, confident of success. A carrier wanted a large farm on the new route; another honest man was ready to take la Baude; the soil would attract tenants. In two years Clochegourde might yield twenty-four thousand francs. Gravelotte in Maine was rented for seven thousand a year; his pension was four thousand. Later additions might let her go to Paris and arrange Jacques’ education.

With what feeling she said “Paris!” I knew she wished not to be separated from her friend. I told her I would finish my education and work to be Jacques’ tutor. She looked grave.

“No, Félix,” she said. “That cannot be, any more than your priesthood. I thank you as a mother, but as a woman who loves you, I can never allow you to be the victim of your attachment. Such a position would be a social discredit to you. My father would never forgive your taking such a humble place; he would refuse you his protection. You must accept society as it is; never fly in its face.”

“Love,” I whispered.

“No, charity,” she said, controlling tears. “This wild idea enlightens me as to your character; your heart will be your bane. I shall claim the right to teach you sometimes. Let my woman’s eye see for you. From the solitudes of Clochegourde I will share in your successes. As to a tutor, do not fear; we shall find some good old abbé, my father will devote sums to the boy’s education. Jacques is my pride; he is eleven years old.”

At the Cassine Jacques, Madeleine, and I followed her like children. I left to speak with Martineau and saw how beloved she was. A laborer told me she was good and knew our hardships and treated the people well. I gave that man all the money I had.

A pony arrived for Jacques; the father, an excellent horseman, would accustom him gradually. The morning of the first lesson, with Madeleine shouting and all the household clustered round, was a maternal festival. Jacques wore a blue collar embroidered by his mother and rode fearlessly. The count smiled for the first time in months; the mother burst into tears and leaned on my arm, “I think I have never suffered. Do not leave us today.” The child jumped into her arms; I and Madeleine arranged bouquets for dinner. She said, “The fifteenth of October is certainly a great day for me. Jacques has taken his first riding lesson, and I have just set the last stitch in my furniture cover.”

“Then, Blanche,” the count laughed, “I must pay you for it.”

He offered her his arm; an open carriage her father had sent awaited with two English horses. As we returned, she whispered anxiously, “I am too happy; happiness is like illness—it overwhelms me; I fear it may vanish.”

I loved her so passionately I was jealous. She asked me what my eyes said; I told her. She was more touched than by any gift, then poured comfort into my heart. “Love me as my aunt loved me,” she said. “If I take your life, must I not be grateful?”

“It was time I finished my tapestry,” she added. “Men find occupations a resource against troubles; we women need physical exercise to control inward sufferings. Each stitch rocked my thoughts; to every stitch I confided my secrets. While doing my last chair I thought much of you. What you put into your bouquets I have said in my embroidery.”

Dinner was lovely. Jacques offered his garland to his mother with grace. In the afternoon we played backgammon; the count was charming. They accompanied me to Frapesle in the tranquil twilight. It was a day of days for the poor woman.

Soon the riding lessons became a source of contention. The countess feared the father’s harsh reprimands; Jacques grew thin and wore dark circles. I advised him to tell his father when tired, but the measure failed, and the old huntsman took over. Angry reproaches resumed. Then the cost of the Cassine and Rhetorière buildings exceeded estimates by half. The news, given first to Monsieur de Mortsauf instead of to his wife, sparked a quarrel that grew bitter until the count’s malady displayed itself.

That day I had gone from Frapesle to search for flowers with Madeleine. Returning, I could not find her and heard cries in the house. Madeleine ran out: “The general,” she cried, “is scolding mamma; go and defend her.”

I entered the salon unseen. Monsieur de Mortsauf was in a violent tirade.

“Never marry, Félix,” he said. “A woman is led by the devil; the most virtuous would invent evil if it did not exist; they are all vile.”

Arguments flew. He harked back to old troubles, cursed the new farming system, declared he could have been twice as rich had he managed Clochegourde differently. He sprang about, displacing furniture; then stopped, moaned his marrow was on fire, and accused his wife of ruining him.

“Yes, Blanche,” he cried, “you are my executioner; you are killing me; you are a monster of hypocrisy. She is smiling! Do you know why she smiles, Félix?”

I kept silence.

“That woman denies me all happiness; she is no more to me than to you, and yet she pretends to be my wife! She fulfills none of the duties of laws, human and divine; she lies to God and man. She puts all her art into keeping me away; she has made me mad through privations; she is killing me by degrees, and she thinks herself a saint and takes the sacrament every month!”

Henriette wept bitterly, answering only, “Monsieur! monsieur! monsieur!”

His words burned me with shame for him and anger because they struck at chastity and delicacy—the warp and woof of first love.

“She is virgin at my expense,” cried the count.

At this the countess cried, “Monsieur!”

“And what mean you by your imperious ‘Monsieur!’?” he shouted. “Am I not your master? Must I teach you that I am?”

He advanced, face white, eyes savage. Henriette slid from her chair to avoid a blow, fell full-length, and lost consciousness. The count stopped bewildered. I took her in my arms; the count opened her bedroom door. We laid her, still dressed, on the bed. When she revived she motioned to unfasten her belt; the count found scissors; I gave her salts and she opened her eyes. The count left the room, ashamed. Two hours passed in silence. Henriette’s hand lay in mine; she pressed it but could not speak. Once she rose on her elbow and whispered, “Unhappy man!—ah! if you did but know—” then sank back. The memory of past sufferings and the present shock threw her into convulsions I calmed by an instinctive magnetism. I smoothed her hair—the first time I touched it—and sat looking at the simple room: chintz curtains, a crucifix, her aunt’s portrait, the children’s drawings with locks of hair. What renunciations of luxury for such a woman—the chamber of a married nun, filled with holy resignation. Painful as the sight was, it exposed hidden, irreparable woe.

When the children and Manette came in I left. The count waited for me; he seemed to seek me as mediator. He caught my hands, exclaiming, “Stay, stay with us, Félix!”

“Unfortunately,” I said, “Monsieur de Chessel has a party, and my absence would cause remark. But after dinner I will return.”

He left the house with me and took me to the lower gate.

As I read the letter I felt a maternal warmth under my fingers that contrasted with my own mother's coldness; no wonder the countess had forbidden me to open it in Touraine, fearing I might break down. Soon after I reencountered my brother Charles, haughty and distant. His condescension and lectures on social matters made me awkward; only my deep love kept me from feeling foolish. Childhood sorrows and solitude had sharpened my perception of the affections around me, and since returning from Clochegourde I could compare experiences and see more clearly what genuine attachment meant.

I often visited the Duchesse de Lenoncourt, where Henriette's name was rarely spoken. Only the old duke, whose welcome eased my shyness, seemed aware of our bond. Just as I began enjoying society again, the events of March 20th forced a change. On Henriette's advice I followed my brother to Ghent, accompanying the Duc de Lenoncourt. The duke's amiability became protection when he perceived my loyalty to the Bourbons; he introduced me to the king, who valued my steadfastness in trying times. A letter from Madame de Mortsauf told me of Jacques' illness; her distress was plain and I felt compelled to help. Setting aside rivalry with my brother, I resolved to leave the court and serve my sovereign as a reliable messenger — a mission I embraced, eager to return to Clochegourde.

After carrying out the king's orders in France I had to evade Bonapartist authorities, travelling through the Vendée until I reached Clochegourde. Jacques was better; the count welcomed me, and in the salon I met Henriette. Her surprise and joy were immediate. She prepared my room, the count saw to my food, and at dinner we learned of the disasters at Waterloo and Napoleon's flight. My concern, however, centered on Henriette. She had even had an ice-house built for me; that simple, thoughtful effort showed a tenderness that moved me. During the following week she regained health and spirit, and absence had only intensified my love — her beauty and devotion impressed me anew.

Henriette spoke plainly of long nights at Jacques' bedside, her prayers and exhausted vigilance. Yet the count's harshness cut through her reflections; she wondered why her words never pleased him and bore the emotional toll. Still she held fast to duty, urging her children to respect their father despite the strain. She described their agricultural success with practical wisdom and a knack for foreseeing matters concerning her children, which she attributed to divine intervention. I admired her counsel and felt gratitude for her guidance.

She encouraged me to pursue opportunities in Paris, stressing the importance of being available to the king. Our conversations deepened our bond: we shared hopes, fears, and a growing sense of belonging. But the count's presence and the tension in their marriage were always present. One day she confided her fear that the count's behaviour threatened her children's well‑being; torn between family duty and personal happiness, she hesitated to assert herself. I urged firmness, yet she feared the consequences.

When the count's health declined, Henriette and I alternated caring for him. That shared duty drew us closer; I took comfort in supporting her. His slow recovery, however, brought back erratic, difficult behaviour, testing her patience and authority. Still she put her children's needs first, often at cost to her own happiness. Between trials we found moments of intimacy and mutual support that strengthened our connection, though always shadowed by the count's influence.

Eventually I returned to Paris, absorbed in responsibilities but faithful to Henriette. Our correspondence sustained us; her letters revealed both love and the heavy burden she bore. News of Jacques' relapse later reached me and the pain in her words was palpable; I felt powerless yet determined to remain devoted. Throughout, Henriette's resilience and maternal devotion shone: she navigated obligations with courage, frequently sacrificing personal happiness for her family. My loyalty grew with her suffering; our love remained a rare, guiding light I cherished and hoped would endure.

During a deeply emotional period I met a remarkable woman at the Élysée-Bourbon salons: immensely wealthy, of noble lineage and married to a distinguished British peer. Her beauty, grace, and wit made her the idol of Parisian society; she ruled with a firm yet charming demeanor.

The English, distant and reserved, live by strict laws and rigid virtues, producing an Englishwoman who, though delicate and charming, is trapped in hypocrisy and compelled to maintain appearances; her passion is intense but constrained. You may envy these women, but they are complex and convinced that love alone suffices.

My liaison with the Marchioness of Dudley became notorious. At a time when my better feelings were subdued, I was drawn to her; my fidelity to Madame de Mortsauf only increased her desire. Lady Dudley craved the chase, and my resistance inflamed her passion. Her mocking remark about my relationship with Madame de Mortsauf—“I am wearied to death of these turtle-dove sighings”—should have warned me.

Men have fewer means to resist women than women have to escape men. Social codes keep men from appearing harsh; women are expected to be coy. Though I loved Madame de Mortsauf, I was not immune to Lady Dudley’s beauty and rank. Her attention in society stirred me; she often hinted she would be my mistress. After one beguiling evening she made her intentions clear, provoking scandal in the British aristocracy.

Seemingly fragile, Lady Dudley possessed a strong will. Her physical courage was remarkable—she could outpace any horse—and she pursued her desires relentlessly. By contrast, Madame de Mortsauf was nurturing and expansive, drawing others into her warmth; Lady Dudley was intense and consuming. English customs reflect a materialist bent, valuing comfort and order often at the expense of spiritual depth. Lady Dudley embodied that materialism: compelling physically but leaving an emptiness I did not feel with Madame de Mortsauf, whose love was spiritual and boundless.

Lady Dudley’s jealousy was masked by charm; she sought to erase Madame de Mortsauf from my heart. I continued to write to Madame de Mortsauf, but my letters went unanswered, filling me with dread. When I finally returned to Clochegourde, a coldness revealed the harm my infidelity had caused.

Madame de Mortsauf’s demeanor had altered; she was distant and sorrowful, her beauty dimmed by grief. I felt the weight of my betrayal as I watched her withdraw, pride and pain evident. The count, unaware of the turmoil that had provoked such change, welcomed me warmly, while Madame de Mortsauf remained aloof.

Witnessing her, I realized the depth of my wrongdoing. The count, whose health was fragile, confessed his fears and the emotional toll he bore; Madame de Mortsauf’s health seemed delicate, and I was tormented by the thought that I was its cause. Devoted to her children, she sought solace in them, yet shadows of despair crept in.

In desperation I sought Madame de Mortsauf’s forgiveness, but she rebuffed me, insisting I had caused her too much pain. Left to grapple with guilt, I felt the weight of my actions pressing down on me. Torn between two women who represented different aspects of love—Lady Dudley’s passionate, material allure and Madame de Mortsauf’s nurturing devotion—I was forced to confront the true nature of love and sacrifice; my desires carried duties, and the harm I caused those I cared for weighed heavily on me.

I know nothing more destructive than the wit of an Englishwoman; it carries that pompous certainty with which the British mask their prejudices. French wit is lace, adorning life’s joys and quarrels; English wit is corrosive, stripping flesh until only bone remains. Arabella wielded her power like a sultan, cutting down rivals with her words.

“My angel,” she said, “I can speak morality too. I’ve questioned whether my love for you is a crime, and I find it natural and pious. God created beauty to inspire adoration. The real crime would be not to love you. That other woman insults you by comparing you to men; the laws of morality do not apply to you, for you are above them. Am I not drawing closer to divine love by loving you? Will God punish a woman for seeking the divine? My love is a weakness; that woman’s is strength. Don’t frown; I do not wish her ill. I adore the morality that has freed you for me—because you are mine forever, aren’t you?”

“Yes.”

“Forever and ever?”

“Yes.”

“Ah! I have found favor in my lord! I alone understand his worth! You say she knows how to cultivate her estate. I leave that to farmers; I cultivate your heart.”

Arabella’s intoxicating babble revealed who she was and what followed. Her impetuous affection sought to obliterate impressions left by Henriette’s dignified love. Her fierce attack revealed fear and admiration for her rival. The next morning she was tearful, lamenting a sleepless night.

“What troubles you?” I asked.

“I fear my love will ruin me,” she replied. “I have given all. That woman possesses something you still desire. If you prefer her, forget me; I will not trouble you. I will go far and die, like a plant deprived of sunlight.”

She forced pledges from me and, reassured, dried her eyes. “Go back to her; I do not want her to owe you to my force, but to your will. If you return, I shall know you love me as I love you, which I always doubted.”

Under Arabella’s influence I returned to Clochegourde. The falsity of my position did not strike me then; had I refused, Lady Dudley would have found in Henriette a triumph. Arabella, certain of me, would have taken me to Paris; but going to Clochegourde now insulted Madame de Mortsauf. Would any loving woman forgive crimes against love? Unless an angel, a loving woman would rather see her lover die than know him happy with another. Thus my leaving Clochegourde for the Grenadiere was fatal to my true love and favored the transient affection that held me. Lady Dudley had calculated a skilful plan.

When I met the countess that morning she was pale and depressed, like one who had not slept. Perception made me see acts the world ignores but lofty spirits call criminal. Like a child at a precipice, I felt separated from her by a universe. Henriette’s illusions were shattered; her heart had endured its passion. She refused to look at me, denying me the light that had shone on my life for six years. I felt the wrong of bringing into this calm roof a face that had known joy. Had I let Lady Dudley depart alone and returned to Clochegourde, perhaps Henriette would not have resolved to treat me as a brother. But now she paid me ostentatious attentions—humiliating, like caring for a sick man.

“You were out walking early,” said the count. “I hope you have brought back a good appetite, you whose stomach is not yet destroyed.”

That remark, which made Henriette smile, completed my sense of ridicule. Day and night I could not be at Clochegourde and Saint-Cyr. By day, the transition from lover to brother seemed like an illness. I was ashamed; I cursed the pleasure Lady Dudley gave me; I wished Henriette would demand my blood. I could not tear her rival apart before Henriette, for she avoided speaking of Arabella. Had I mentioned her, Henriette would have despised me. After five years of intimate intercourse, we had nothing to say; our words had no connection with our thoughts; we hid intolerable pain from each other.

Henriette feigned cheerfulness, but she was sad. She called herself my sister, and we had no common ground, remaining in constrained silence. She increased my misery by pretending to be the only victim.

“I suffer more than you,” I said once.

“How so?” she asked haughtily.

“Because I am the one to blame.”

Her manner grew so cold that I resolved to leave Clochegourde. That evening on the terrace I bade farewell. As I took my horse, she said, “Let us walk down the avenue together, alone.” At the grove marking the boundary she stopped.

“Farewell, my friend,” she said, resting her head on my chest and wrapping her arms around me. “Farewell, we shall never meet again. God has given me the sad power to see the future. Do you remember the terror when you first returned, so young, so handsome? I saw you turn your back on me as you do now. Once again, I have seen into the future. Friend, we speak for the last time. I can hardly say a few words; part of me speaks. Death has seized something in me. You have taken the mother from her children; I ask you to take her place. You can; Jacques and Madeleine love you, as if you had always made them suffer.”

“Die!” I cried, terrified by the fire in her eyes. “Henriette, I command you to live. Swear to me you will send for Origet and obey him in everything.”

“Would you oppose God’s mercy?” she cried, misreading me.

“You do not love me enough to obey blindly, as that miserable Lady Dudley does?”

“Yes, yes, I will do all you ask,” she cried, goaded by jealousy.

“Then I stay,” I said, kissing her eyelids.

Frightened, she escaped my arms and walked home without looking back. I followed; she was weeping and praying. We reached the lawn; I respectfully kissed her hand. This submission touched her.

“I am yours—forever, and as you will,” I said; “for I love you as your aunt loved you.”

She trembled and wrung my hand.

“One look,” I said, “one last of our old looks! The woman who gives herself wholly,” I cried, “gives less of life and soul than I have now received. Henriette, thou art my best-beloved—my only love.”

“I shall live!” she said; “but cure yourself as well.”

That look erased Arabella’s memory. Thus I was the plaything of two irreconcilable passions, influenced alternately. I loved an angel and a demon; two women equally beautiful—one adorned with virtues we decry through hatred of our imperfections, the other with vices we deify through selfishness. Returning down the avenue, seeing Madame de Mortsauf surrounded by her children waving handkerchiefs, I felt a transient pride in being arbiter of two destinies; proud of inspiring such passion that death must come to whichever I abandoned. That conceit punished me doubly.

I do not know what urged me to remain with Arabella and await the moment the count’s death might give me Henriette, for she would ever love me. Her harshness, tears, remorse, and Christian resignation testified to a feeling that could not be erased. Walking down that avenue, I was no longer twenty-five; I was fifty. A man passes quickly from youth to middle age. Perhaps evil thoughts came from the Tuileries, from the king’s cabinet. Who could resist the polluting spirit of Louis XVIII?

Back in Paris, Arabella and I grew intimate. We soon abandoned conventional restrictions I had tried to keep—the comedies and concealments that renew desire. Young passions, like youth, raze their forests instead of cutting timber. Arabella adopted none of the bourgeois strategies; she wished to exhibit me to all Paris as her “sposo.” She used her powers of seduction to keep me under her roof, not content with whispered scandal. Seeing her happy in an imprudence that frankly admitted her position, how could I not believe her love?

Once plunged into the comforts of our illegal union, despair seized me; I saw my life bound to a course defying Henriette’s ideas and advice. From then on I lived in a rage like consumptives who, knowing death near, cannot bear scrutiny. There was no corner of my heart where I could escape suffering; an avenging spirit filled me with forbidden thoughts. My letters to Henriette depicted this moral malady and did her infinite harm. “At the cost of so many treasures lost, I wished you to be at least happy,” she wrote in the only answer I received. But I was not happy. Happiness is absolute; it admits no comparisons. Once the first ardor fades, comparisons arise. All great passions first check character’s asperities; later, when lovers are accustomed, moral physiognomies reappear and natural antipathies surface. This period began with me. Less blinded, I undertook a critical examination of Lady Dudley that harmed her.

First, she lacked the qualities of mind that distinguish Frenchwomen. When a Frenchwoman loves, she metamorphoses: coquetry adorns love; she abandons dangerous vanity and claims no merit but loving well. She espouses her lover’s interests and acquires experience in a day; heedless and prodigal by nature, she manages with grace. She becomes mother, adviser, doctor, giving transformations a grace that reveals infinite love. She combines other nations’ special qualities and unifies them with wit that enlivens and varies love, relieving monotony. The Frenchwoman loves always, in public or solitude; in public she speaks by silence or looks; if speech and look fail, she finds other means. The Englishwoman, by contrast, makes her love bend to the world. Educated to icy manners, she opens and shuts her heart with a British mechanism. Passionate when no eye sees her, she is cold before the world. A lover doubts his empire seeing her immobility, her aloof countenance, the calm voice with which she leaves her boudoir. Hypocrisy becomes indifference; she has forgotten all.

A woman who can lay aside her love like a garment may be thought capable of changing it. What tempests in a man’s heart when he sees a woman take and drop her love like embroidery. These women, too completely mistress of themselves, never wholly belong to you; society’s influence prevents you reigning supreme. Where a Frenchwoman comforts with a look, an Englishwoman’s silence irritates and frets. They are so on their dignity that fashion often reigns over pleasures. An Englishwoman forces everything into form; Protestantism’s doubt and reasoning kill belief; it is the death of art and love. Passionate hearts flee worldliness.

You can understand my shock when I found Lady Dudley could not live without the world, and the English system of two lives was natural to her. When she loved, she loved madly—no woman could compare; but when the curtain fell, she banished memory of it. In public she never answered a look or smile; she was ambassadress-like, folding her phrases and elbows; her composure outraged my heart. She degraded love to a need rather than raising it to an ideal. She expressed neither fear nor regret nor desire; then, at a given hour, tenderness reappeared like a sudden fire.

Which of the two should I believe? By a thousand pin-pricks I felt the infinite differences between Henriette and Arabella. Madame de Mortsauf’s smallest movements—the sound of her gown, her lowered eyelids, her musical voice—were a continual caress, a consistent thought. She did not halve her soul for two atmospheres. Arabella’s mind existed for the world and sarcasm; she loved to rend and bite to satisfy craving. Madame de Mortsauf would have hidden her happiness; Lady Dudley chose to exhibit hers to all Paris yet kept an impenetrable English mask in public. This mixture of ostentation and dignity, love and coldness, wounded me. When I complained, she replied with triple sting, mingling boasts of love with cutting English sarcasms. In opposition she made amusement of hurting me, kneading me like dough. She caricatured my ideas, even daring to ask if I wished her to kiss me at the opera before all Paris. Despite passion, she was never meditative, self-contained, or reverent like Henriette; she was insatiable for excitement, noise, and celebrity; everything concerned herself, not me.

Henriette’s letter you have read—proof of how this virtuous woman obeyed the genius of a Frenchwoman—shows how she had studied my prospects, political relations, moral conquests, and with what ardor she took hold of my life in permissible directions. Lady Dudley affected the reticence of an acquaintance. She never inquired into my affairs as a man. Prodigal for herself without being generous, she separated self-interest and love too decidedly. I knew, without proving it, that to save me a pang, Henriette would have sought for me what she would never seek for herself. In any great misfortune I would have gone to Henriette for counsel; I would sooner have been dragged to prison than say a word to Lady Dudley.

The contrast extended to outward things. In France luxury expresses the man; it reproduces his ideas and gives attention value between lovers. English luxury, at first alluring, proved mechanical. The thousand attentions at Clochegourde Arabella considered servants’ business; each had a duty as if horses. She never attached to servants; the death of the best would not affect her; money could replace him. Her selfishness was candid and created laughter. Crimson draperies covered an iron nature. The siren of amorous folly could make a young man forget the hard Englishwoman; only gradually I discovered the stony rock where my seeds were wasted. Madame de Mortsauf had seen this at a glance. She was right; Arabella’s love became intolerable to me. I have since remarked that many women who ride well have little tenderness. Like Amazons, they lack a breast; their hearts are hard in some direction.

When weariness took possession of me, when I comprehended the sanctity true feeling imparts to love, when memories of Clochegourde brought the fragrance of roses, the warmth of the terrace, and the warble of nightingales, I received a blow whose echo still wakes in my heart.

I was working in the king’s cabinet when the Duc de Lenoncourt entered. The king asked news of the countess. I raised my head hastily; the king, offended, gave me the look that precedes harsh words.

“Sire, my poor daughter is dying,” replied the duke.

“Will the king deign to grant me leave of absence?” I cried, tears in my eyes.

“Go, my lord,” he answered, withholding reprimand in satire.

More courtier than father, the duke asked no leave but got into the carriage with the king. I left without bidding Lady Dudley goodbye, leaving a note that I was sent on a mission by the king. At the Croix de Berny I met his Majesty returning. He threw me an ironic look seeming to say: “If you mean to be anything in politics, come back; don’t parley with the dead.” The pompous equipages rolled away to cries of “Vive le Roi!” It seemed the court had driven over the dead body of Madame de Mortsauf with utter insensibility. Though the duke was an excellent man, he would no doubt play whist with Monsieur after the king retired. The duchess had long ago given her daughter the first stab by writing of Lady Dudley.

My hurried journey was like a ruined gambler’s dream; I despaired at receiving no news. Had the confessor excluded me? I accused everyone. Beyond Tours, on the poplar-bordered road to Poncher, I met Monsieur Origet. He guessed I was going to Clochegourde and told me: “I doubt if you find her living. She is dying a frightful death—of inanition. When she called me last June, no medical power could control the disease; she had the symptoms Monsieur de Mortsauf knows, for he thinks he has them. Madame la comtesse was not in a transient condition; her disease had reached a point where science is useless; it is the incurable result of grief. Her physical condition is produced by inertia of an organ as necessary as the heart. Grief has done the work of a dagger. Don’t deceive yourself; she is dying of hidden grief.”

“Hidden!” I exclaimed.

“No,” he said, looking at me, “and since she was so seriously attacked, Monsieur de Mortsauf ceased to torment her. I am no longer needed; Monsieur Deslandes is all-sufficient; nothing can be done; her sufferings are dreadful. Young, beautiful, and rich, to die emaciated—for she dies of hunger! During the last forty days the stomach has rejected all nourishment.”

Origet pressed my hand with respect. “Courage, monsieur,” he said, lifting his eyes.

These words were like poisoned arrows. I sprang into the carriage and promised a reward if I arrived in time.

Despite impatience, the distance seemed short as bitter reflections crowded my soul. Dying of grief, yet her children were well? Then she died through me! My conscience arraigned me. What weakness in human justice, which avenges open deeds but forgives murderers of the soul! How many victims—Madame de Beauseant, the Duchesse de Langeais, Lady Brandon—our period teems with such events. Who does not remember the young woman who poisoned herself from jealousy? Society and science are accomplices in crimes without assizes. The world declares no one dies of grief; our scientific nomenclature provides labels that serve as passports to the coffin. Is there some law we do not know? Must the century pitilessly strew earth with corpses? My God! do I belong to the race of tigers?

Remorse gripped me as I entered Clochegourde’s avenue on a damp October morning. Jacques, Madeleine, and the Abbe Dominis knelt at the foot of a wooden cross. I sprang from the carriage toward them, my heart aching. The old huntsman stood apart.

I kissed Jacques and Madeleine, who gave a cold look. The abbe rose; I took him by the arm.

“Is she still alive?” I implored. He bowed sadly and said it might be better to wait; she was greatly changed. “It is necessary to prepare her for this interview, or it might increase her suffering—death would be a blessed release.”

He told me how for six months the countess had suffered more despite Origet’s care. The doctor had come every evening for two months. “To heal the body, the heart must first be healed,” the doctor had said. As the illness increased, the countess’s words grew bitter. She called on earth to keep her, then repented. Such alternations rend the heart. “She falls, but each time she stumbles she rises higher toward heaven,” he said.

I cried, “Surely this pure lily will flower in heaven?”

“You left her still a flower,” he answered. “You will find her consumed, purified, pure as a diamond buried in ashes.”

The count, white-headed, hurried out, declaring, “She was right! He is here! ‘Felix, Felix, Felix has come!’ she kept crying. My dear friend, death is here. Why did it not take a poor madman like me?”

I walked toward the house but the Abbe Birotteau stopped me. “Madame la comtesse begs you will not enter yet.” Inside, servants moved, all dumb with grief. Manette searched for Madeleine; we paced the lawn. I saw the dear “mignonne” gathering autumn flowers. Overcome, I sat, broken.

“Poor Felix,” said the count, “she forbade me to write to you. She knew how much you loved her.”

All was gloom. The servants wept; work was left undone; the place seemed uninhabited. The count, speaking in bursts, blamed and then wept for his wife, mixing despair with accusations. Madeleine told me her mother was ready; the Abbe Birotteau accompanied me. He warned, “I have done all to prevent this meeting. The salvation of this saint required it. I shall be present to protect you against yourself and perhaps against her. Respect her weakness. She is dying of hunger and thirst. Since morning she suffers feverish irritation. Pray God she die not in a moment of doubt.”

We reached the chamber. Henriette sat in white, before the fireplace, flowers about her. The abbe’s amazement at the room’s restored tenderness showed she had prepared to receive me. Her shrunken face had the greenish pallor of a magnolia; hollow eyes shone; her forehead kept proportion; inward fires issued despite waxy features. The smile on her whitened lips resembled death’s grin. Her robe showed emaciation. She knew she was changed; thought filled her with bitterness. No longer the sublime Madame de Mortsauf, she was a nameless thing struggling against annihilation. I took her dry, burning hand to kiss. Her discolored lips tried a smile—the forced smile of one seeking to hide irony, expectation, or fury.

“Ah, my poor Felix, this is death,” she said, “and you do not like death; odious death. This is the end of love; I knew it would be so. Lady Dudley will never see you thus surprised. Ah! why have I so longed for you, Felix? You have come at last, and I reward your devotion by the same horrible sight that made the Comte de Rance a Trappist. I, who hoped to remain beautiful and noble in your memory, destroy your illusions! True love cannot calculate. But stay; do not go, stay. Monsieur Origet said I was better this morning; I shall recover. Your looks will bring me back to life. When I regain strength and nourishment, I shall be beautiful again. I am scarcely thirty-five; there are many years of happiness before me—we will leave Clochegourde and go to Italy.”

Tears filled my eyes; I turned to the window. She put her burning lips to my ear: “My anguish has been in not seeing thee! Did you not bid me live? I will live; I will ride on horseback; I will know life, Paris, fêtes, pleasures!”

Those tones pictured a lifetime’s battles, the anguish of true love lost. The countess rose with the motion of a child. The confessor fell on his knees and prayed. She flung an arm around my neck, kissed me violently, and said, “You shall not escape me now!” She fainted; I laid her on the bed. “You carried me thus before,” she murmured.

Deslandes entered, surprised at the decorations. “We must suffer much to die,” she said faintly. The doctor felt a pulse and murmured that it was the forty-second day since she had eaten or drunk. The Abbe Birotteau took me to the garden. “Let us leave her to the doctor,” he said; “they will wrap her in opium. Well, you have heard her—if indeed it is she.”

“No,” I said, “it is not she.”

Stupefied, I hid in a punt to be alone. I tried to detach myself from my life—torture like that which afflicts adulterers. My life was a failure. Despair suggested to die beside her or bury myself at Meilleraye among Trappists. I looked at the windows where Henriette was dying, fancying I saw the light burning the night I betrothed my soul to hers. Ought I not to have followed the simple life she created, keeping faithful while I worked in the world? Had she not bidden me become great to be saved from shameful passions? Chastity! Love as Arabella understood it disgusted me.

Madeleine walked slowly and sat on a bench. When I approached, she rose and returned to the house, hating me; she fled from her mother’s murderer. At the portico Jacques sat, expressionless. Young persons carrying death are often unmoved at funerals. I longed to question Jacques.

“You know, Jacques,” I said, “that in me you have a most devoted brother.”

“Your friendship is useless; I shall follow my mother,” he replied with a sullen look.

He showed me his handkerchief stained with blood.

They each had a fatal secret. The brother and sister avoided each other. Henriette lay low; all was ruins at Clochegourde.

“Madame is asleep,” Manette said, happy that the countess was out of pain. Small joys matter then. “Monsieur Deslandes ordered the flowers taken away; they excited Madame’s nerves,” Manette added. She thought the flowers caused delirium.

I returned as the sunset gilded Azay’s roofs. All was calm and pure. A soft light lit the bed where my Henriette lay wrapped in opium. The body seemed annihilated; the soul reigned. Blanche and Henriette, two sublime faces of one woman, reappeared. The abbes sat at the bed. The count stood stupefied. I took a seat on the sofa. We exchanged looks of admiration mingled with tears. The Abbe Dominis and I communicated in signs: yes, angels were watching. Her face cleared; the green of suffering gave way to pure white pallor; she seemed to embrace the divine.

Madeleine knelt, adoring, crying “My mother!” Jacques smiled; he would follow her. “She is entering the haven,” said the Abbe Birotteau. The Angelus rang; Nature’s poetry joined the sacred hour. We burst into tears. Even servants knelt and prayed. The doctor signaled that this sleep was the hour without pain; it was time for the last sacraments. At nine o’clock she awoke quietly with gentle eyes, and we beheld her in former beauty.

“Mother! you are too beautiful to die—life and health are coming back to you!” cried Madeleine.

“Dear daughter, I shall live—in thee,” she answered, smiling.

They embraced. The count kissed his wife. She colored when she saw me.

“Dear Felix,” she said, “this is, I think, the only grief I have ever caused you. Forget all I may have said—I, a poor creature beside myself.” She held out her hand; I took and kissed it. “As in the old days, Felix?”

We left the room during the last confession. I approached Madeleine. In the presence of others she could not escape civility; but, like her mother, she looked at no one.

“Dear Madeleine,” I said softly, “what have you against me? Why such coldness before death, which should reconcile us?”

“I hear in my heart what my mother says,” she replied, with a look like Ingres’ “Mother of God.”

“And you condemn me when your mother absolves me—if indeed I am guilty.”

“You, you—always yourself!” Her voice revealed determined hatred, implacable as Corsican judgment.

An hour passed in silence. The Abbe Birotteau returned after receiving the countess’s confession. We found her sitting up, dressed in the long white garment to be her shroud. She had had her letters burned—sacrifice she had delayed until this hour. She smiled with eyes moist and inward lucidity; she saw the promised land.

“Dear Felix,” she said, pressing my hand, “stay with us. You must be present at my last scene; be not absent from the least painful among many things in which you are concerned.”

She made a sign and the door closed. She knelt before the astonished count, then laid her head on his knees.

“Though I have been a faithful wife to you,” she said faintly, “I have sometimes failed in my duty. I have given a friendship outside my family more affectionate care than to you. Perhaps I have irritated you by comparisons. I have had a deep friendship which no one—not even he who has been its object—has fully known. Though virtuous by human laws and an irreproachable wife, other thoughts, voluntary or involuntary, have crossed my mind, and in this hour I fear I welcomed them too warmly. But having loved you and continued submissive, I now pray for your blessing with a clean heart. I shall die without a bitter thought if I can hear a tender word for your Blanche, for the mother of your children—if you forgive her those things she did not forgive herself till reassured by the great tribunal which pardons all.”

“Blanche, Blanche!” cried the broken man, shedding tears on her head. “Would you kill me?” He raised and kissed her solemnly. “Have I no forgiveness to ask? Have I never been harsh?”

“Perhaps,” she said. “But dear friend, indulge a dying woman’s weakness; tranquillize me. Will you grant that I leave to our friend here that pledge of my affection?” She showed a letter on the mantel. “He is now my adopted son, and that is all. The heart makes bequests; my last wishes impose a duty on that dear Felix. If you do not wish to marry Madeleine, at least seek my soul’s repose by making Monsieur de Mortsauf as happy as he can be. Farewell, long farewell like that I gave last night to our valley, where I shall rest and where you will often—will you not?—return.

Henriette.”

I fell into abyssal reflections as the depths of her life were lighted by this expiring flame. My clouds of egotism rolled away. She had suffered as much as I—more, for she was dead. She believed others would be kind to her friend; she was blind to her daughter’s enmity. That last proof of tenderness pained me. Poor Henriette wished to give me Clochegourde and her daughter.

From that dread day when I first followed my noble Henriette to the grave, the sun has been less warm, nights more gloomy, movement less agile, thought more dull. Some departed we bury in earth; others are buried within us, their memory mingling with our heartbeats; they are in us by a metempsychosis of love. A soul is within my soul. When some good thing is done by me, that soul acts and speaks. All that is good within me issues from that grave; sarcasm and bitterness are mine. Natalie, when next my eyes are darkened or my lips silent, do not ask, “Of what are you thinking?”

I ceased writing for days; these memories were too bitter. I owe you an account of events that followed; they need few words. Henriette’s letter set a star of hope before me. To live at Clochegourde with Madeleine, consecrating my life to her, seemed a rescue. But it was necessary to know her true feelings. As I bade the count farewell, we walked the terrace. He spoke of his loss but soon of the future; he feared his daughter, who lacked her mother’s gentleness. Madeleine’s firm character alarmed him; he foresaw a struggle between father and daughter that would end his days in bitterness. His son was to leave; his daughter marry; what son-in-law might she have? Though talking of dying, he feared being alone without sympathy.

Madeleine could see me from the terrace but would not come down. I seized a moment to beg the count to call her, saying I had a last wish of her mother to convey. The count fetched her and left us on the terrace alone.

“Dear Madeleine,” I said, “if I speak to you, it should be here where your mother listened to me. I know your thoughts; but are you not condemning me without knowledge of facts? My life and happiness are bound up in this place; you know that, yet you seek to banish me by coldness instead of brotherly affection that death should have strengthened. If my mother’s name still has power, in her name I beg you never to return me to Clochegourde so long as you are in it. The sight of me causes a repugnance I cannot express, which I shall never overcome.”

She stopped me.

“Monsieur,” she said, voice trembling, “I know all your thoughts; but I shall not change my feelings. I would rather fling myself into the Indre than ally myself to you. If my mother’s name has power, in her name I beg you never to return while I am here. The sight of you causes repugnance I cannot overcome.”

She bowed with dignity and returned to the house without looking back. The searching eye of that young girl had discovered her mother’s secrets; her hatred of the man she fancied fatal to her mother may have been increased by a sense of complicity.

All before me was chaos. Madeleine hated me without considering cause or victim. The edifice of my happiness fell in ruins. I alone knew the life of that unknown woman; none had fathomed her heart. Strange truth: how many families have their Henriette! How many noble feelings leave earth with no historian to measure their depths! Mothers know their children little; so with husbands, lovers, brothers. When Madeleine disappeared, I left with a broken heart. Bidding farewell to my host at Sache, I started for Paris, following the right bank of the Indre.
