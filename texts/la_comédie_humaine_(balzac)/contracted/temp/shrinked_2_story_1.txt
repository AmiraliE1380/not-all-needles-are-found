Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, stood a charming old house that let historians reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls bore hieroglyphic patterns of timbers and plaster cracks. The triangular roof, warped by weather, jutted over the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer. The upper story used overlapping planks to avoid overburdening the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning, a young man in a cloak stood beneath a shop awning across from the house, studying it with the zeal of an antiquary. The relic of sixteenth-century civic life displayed many peculiarities. He ignored the first floor’s narrow, boarded windows and focused on the third floor’s modest sash windows, poorly made and dimmed by green-tinted glass. Occasionally he glanced down at the shop, where small details amused him.

A large wooden beam, supported by four pillars, bore layers of paint and a comical depiction of a cat playing racket, which amused the young man. The caricatured cat, racket in paw and an oversized ball, had grown more absurd with age. The faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a remnant of a whimsical past when such signs lured customers.

The young man, in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, looked as if he had just come from a wedding or ball; white gloves and disheveled hair hinted at recent festivities. Despite market gardeners hurrying by, the street held a rare stillness. His striking features, a blend of passion and calm, drew the attention of the shopkeepers at the “Cat and Racket.” His pale face set off a dazzling white cravat, and his expressive eyes suggested many moods.

Suddenly, three apprentices appeared at the dormer window, laughing and teasing him. They playfully sprinkled him with shaving cream; he shook off the annoyance with indifference. Then a young girl’s face appeared at the window, her beauty like a Raphael Virgin—innocent and unembarrassed. On noticing the young man she withdrew and the window slammed shut, leaving him disappointed.

Meanwhile the shutters of the “Cat and Racket” opened and a manservant hung a cloth sign reading “Guillaume, successor to Chevrel.” The shop’s exterior, guarded by iron bars, concealed packages wrapped in brown linen. Despite its old-fashioned look, Monsieur Guillaume was known for having the best-stocked establishment among Parisian cloth merchants and a reputation for honesty. He knew how to secure profits without flattery or costly gifts, and his fellow traders often warned against dealing with his notary, notorious for heavy discounts.

The old merchant stood at his shop threshold, watching the Rue Saint-Denis as if waking from slumber. Monsieur Guillaume, in traditional attire, scrutinized a stranger overly curious about his business. His face, marked by patience and shrewdness, reflected years of experience. A staunch traditionalist, he lamented lost customs and kept a strict regime for his three apprentices, who feared his vigilance.

As the apprentices began the day, Guillaume noticed the stranger's anxious glances toward the shop and suspected ulterior motives. The eldest stepped outside and thought he saw Mademoiselle Augustine, Guillaume's daughter, leaving hastily. He hailed a cab, easing the apprentices' worries about their prank on Augustine.

Guillaume reprimanded them for idleness, recalling how he would have done far more work in his youth. Though from reasonably comfortable backgrounds, he demanded a rigorous work ethic, contrasting his order and economy with modern shops where apprentices expected early wealth. The shop itself was meticulously kept, a testament to his values.

Although the apprentices paid for their board, they were not allowed to join the family at dessert, a mark of Madame Guillaume's frugality. They attended Mass with the family, and the eldest, Joseph Lebas, earned a modest salary after years of service. Guillaume regarded him as a fitting husband for his elder daughter, Virginie, though Joseph secretly loved the younger daughter, Augustine.

The household was governed by strict traditions. Virginie and Augustine had been raised under their mother's austere rule. Virginie resembled her mother in piety and sternness; Augustine, eighteen, was delicate and charming but often overshadowed by maternal expectations. Their lives centered on domestic duties with little time outside, so they took pleasure in family gatherings and rare visitors.

Madame Guillaume's rigid schedule dictated their days: they dressed early and followed routine. Augustine, however, yearned for more than their cloistered existence, often lost in thoughts of a more passionate life. Her secret reading of romantic novels fed this longing for adventure.

That longing deepened Joseph's affection for Augustine while Virginie found herself drawn to him, despite lacking suitors. Their feelings developed quietly amid the shop's monotony, where the same faces encouraged a sense of connection. Monsieur Guillaume, aware of these shifting dynamics, mused about his daughters' futures in a world where suitors might become scarce.

From that day, the old merchant, saddened by his eldest daughter's decline, recalled marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like Joseph Lebas and Virginie. Marrying his daughter would settle a sacred debt—repaying an orphan for a past kindness. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, noted his fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rigid rule that the elder daughter marry first. The lovesick assistant, warm-hearted and tall, suffered in silence.

To grasp the situation, rewind months before the story begins. One evening a young man paused outside the darkened shop of the Cat and Racket, captivated by a scene any painter would cherish. The unlit shop resembled a cave; a dining room, lit by a hanging lamp, cast a warm Dutch-like glow. The family—Augustine and a chubby maid among them—formed a striking, modest tableau. The painter, just returned from Rome, hungered for simple nature after the grandeur of Italian art. His heart, once full of Italian passion, sought the meditative maiden he had only seen in paintings. The living scene before him stirred deep admiration for Augustine, pensive and scarcely touched by food. The lamp illuminated her face, making her seem almost angelic. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

The next day he retreated to his studio, intent on capturing that magic. He visited the Cat and Racket repeatedly, even in disguise, to study Augustine closely. For eight months he devoted himself to love and art, neglecting friends and previous pursuits. One morning his friend Girodet pierced his isolation to ask about his Salon submission. The artist eagerly revealed two masterpieces; Girodet embraced him, recognizing the depth of his friend's feeling.

“You are in love?” Girodet asked; the artist nodded. They agreed great work springs from such passion. Girodet warned against showing the pieces at the Salon, doubting the public would grasp their depth. Still, the works were exhibited and caused a sensation. The Interior sparked a genre shift, while the portrait became unforgettable, earning acclaim and offers the artist refused.

These events, though public, felt remote from the monastic life on Rue Saint-Denis. During a visit to Madame Guillaume, the notary’s wife mentioned the exhibition, arousing Augustine’s curiosity. With her cousin’s help she obtained permission to visit the Louvre. Seeing the portrait, she trembled: it was herself, and she felt terror mixed with excitement. The artist stood nearby and whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Desperate to escape the crowd, Augustine urged her cousin to leave, but they were carried toward the celebrated painting. Overcome, she wept at the sight. In a brief, silent exchange she gestured to the artist; he acknowledged her with a nod. That moment ignited guilt and exhilaration. The Salon’s chaos and the artist's gaze intoxicated her; new emotions surged where none had been before.

As they left, the painter glowed with joy; Augustine, caught in the whirlwind, realized she was loved. His words echoed in her mind, intensifying her turmoil. She feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition, yet her mother’s plans to visit the Salon filled her with dread. Augustine’s heart raced as she contemplated the implications of these feelings.

That night she reflected, feeling a void in her mundane life and longing for the elegance she had always desired. The thought of being the wife of a genius filled her with hope despite her modest upbringing. Suddenly in love, her heart brimmed with dreams of a brighter future. The next day her parents returned from the Salon, disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. Still, news of the paintings’ disappearance and the lost shawl revealed to Augustine a sensitivity she had not known she possessed.

On the morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had captured Augustine's heart, struggled to declare his love because of the household's constraints. Seated in a counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume, Augustine was closely watched by her mother, which made Theodore's approach difficult. Imagining a rival among the assistants, he felt trapped by the household’s vigilance. Driven by passion, he bribed a maid to facilitate exchanges, and they arranged to meet discreetly during Mass at Saint-Leu.

Meanwhile the Cat and Racket household was consumed by stock-taking, with Monsieur Guillaume overseeing inventory and accounts. The family busied themselves verifying goods, oblivious to Augustine’s turmoil; her absent-mindedness went unnoticed. After weeks of labor the stock-taking ended, granting the family a rare evening of relaxation when they attended a performance of Cendrillon while the apprentices had a night out.

The next morning Monsieur Guillaume resumed his routine, reflecting on the business as he summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to discuss the profitable results. Guillaume proposed making Joseph a partner in recognition of his contributions. Overwhelmed, Joseph revealed his love for Augustine, but Guillaume misunderstood and thought he meant Virginie. After clarification, Guillaume agreed to consider Joseph’s feelings for Augustine and suggested he accompany her to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie exchanged glances at a nervous Joseph. Madame Guillaume, unaware of the budding romance, chastised Augustine for inattention during church. After the service she sent Augustine to her room, prompting a heated discussion between her parents about their daughter’s future.

Summoned before them, Augustine confessed her love for Theodore de Sommervieux, stressing his respectable background. Monsieur Guillaume voiced concern about the nature of artists, recalling his misgivings; Augustine defended Theodore as wealthy and of good family, which intrigued her father. Madame Roguin then arrived with news of Theodore’s recent honors and fortune, advocating for the match. Her persuasive account began to sway both parents, who had been resistant to their daughter marrying above her station.

As the day progressed the atmosphere shifted from conflict to acceptance. Monsieur Guillaume informed Joseph of the developments. By evening the family gathered to celebrate the potential union, with Theodore presenting a painting of the Cat and Racket as a symbol of shared happiness. Monsieur Guillaume, still cautious, consented to the marriage, stressing financial prudence and advising Augustine to consult him on money matters.

In the end the lovers found solace in mutual affection, and the household, once tense, embraced the prospect of a joyful future together, relieved, grateful, and genuinely hopeful.

A few months after a memorable Sunday, the high altar of Saint-Leu hosted two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore were radiant, while Virginie, dressed simply, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to have Virginie married before Augustine, but the priests focused on the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie for making a sensible match, while others envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned that such extravagance would lead to ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt justified in his cautious approach to marriage settlements. After the ball and supper, Monsieur and Madame Guillaume returned home, while Monsieur and Madame Lebas went back to their old residence. The artist, joyful, carried Augustine to their well-adorned apartment.

Their first year together was blissful and unclouded. Theodore showered her with affection, and Augustine, lost in love, believed her devotion would always charm him. She remained unaware of the world, speaking the language of love and sometimes revealing her naivety. As the year progressed, Theodore needed to return to work, especially with a child on the way. He reconnected with friends and frequented the Duchesse de Carigliano's gatherings, seeking the social validation of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine enjoyed the social scene but soon realized her lack of sophistication compared to Theodore's artistic world. Her admiration for his work was genuine but superficial, and a growing disconnect emerged. Theodore, once captivated by her, began to feel the strain of their differing intellectual realms. He sought solace in his studio, hoping the environment would inspire Augustine, but she struggled to adapt to the lifestyle. Her discomfort showed at gatherings, where her innocence clashed with artists' more liberated attitudes.

Over time, Theodore's affection waned, and Augustine felt the weight of his indifference. Despite her efforts to fulfill her duties as a wife, she sensed a growing distance. Three years into their marriage, Augustine, once vibrant, became consumed by sorrow. She tried to uphold responsibilities and rekindle Theodore's love, but her efforts were in vain. The artist's enthusiasm for the Duchesse de Carigliano only deepened her despair, as she faced being replaced by a more experienced woman.

Her heartache led Augustine to reflect on her shortcomings and the barriers hindering their connection. She resolved to educate herself, believing that understanding poetry and art would bridge the gap. However, her attempts to change met with frustration; she could not cultivate the natural wit and charm Theodore admired. Her earnestness often backfired, and she felt increasingly inadequate in his presence.

In desperation, Augustine sought solace with her family, returning to her childhood home. She found Virginie and brother-in-law Joseph Lebas managing their drapery business with a stability that contrasted sharply with her turmoil. Despite their mundane happiness, Augustine felt a pang of envy as she shared her struggles. Joseph offered practical, businesslike advice, but it left her more conflicted.

Ultimately, Augustine returned home, burdened by uncertainty and longing for the love she once shared with Theodore. She felt like a desperate patient trying every remedy in hopes of healing her fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter warmly; her visit brightened their monotonous lives. For four years they had drifted, dwelling on past business successes and failures, notably the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume checked on his shop, the Cat and Racket, and traded memories with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume, now on the Army's clothing consulting board thanks to her son-in-law, filled their home with extravagant, often tasteless decor. Their nostalgia and stagnation struck Augustine as a stark contrast to her own turbulent experiences, leaving her both proud and appalled at their vacuous existence.

Despite her inner turmoil, Augustine displayed her recent achievements and aired marital grievances, which her parents eagerly absorbed. Madame Guillaume was especially curious about life with Theodore, Augustine's husband, a painter. Augustine mentioned his late nights and unconventional habits, prompting her mother’s disapproval of his lifestyle and profession. The couple’s incredulity grew as Augustine defended Theodore, sparking a heated debate about genius and marital responsibility.

When Augustine hinted at divorce, her parents were shocked into action; Monsieur Guillaume was ready to support her. Overwhelmed by their concern and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine rejected separation, realizing her pain could scarcely be understood by them. She felt isolated, convinced that only those who had shared similar experiences could truly empathize.

Resolved to win back Theodore’s affection by other means, Augustine decided to consult the Duchess de Carigliano—not to reclaim his heart outright but to learn how to captivate him. Approaching the Duchess’s opulent home, she felt both envy and admiration for its elegance. Meeting the Duchess, Augustine was struck by the woman’s beauty and charm but also sensed rivalry.

The Duchess responded with a mix of sympathy and shrewd counsel, stressing the importance of a composed facade and control when dealing with men of genius. She advised cultivating mystery and not revealing the full measure of one’s affections, framing such guardedness as strategy rather than mere manipulation. Initially disheartened, Augustine absorbed the Duchess’s lessons, recognizing that her love for Theodore might require a different approach.

The Duchess gave Augustine a portrait of herself painted by Theodore, believing it could rekindle his interest. Filled with hope, Augustine resolved to embody the image in the painting. When Theodore returned, she presented the portrait; his reaction was fury and jealousy, igniting a tumultuous confrontation.

The quarrel escalated, exposing the depths of Theodore’s passion and insecurity and culminating in a violent outburst. The aftermath left Augustine shattered; her dreams of mending their marriage lay in ruins. The next morning, her mother found her consumed by despair amid the remnants of the confrontation.

Augustine’s tragic fate was sealed as she succumbed to the pressures of her turbulent life, dying young. Her story stands as a poignant reminder of the struggles women face pursuing love and fulfillment, especially when entangled with the complexities of artistic genius. The inscription on her grave reflects that tragedy, prompting those who knew her to ponder the challenges of balancing passion and stability in relationships.
