In 1828, Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac walked through Paris, discussing Rastignac's interest in the Marquise d’Espard. Bianchon warned him against pursuing her, comparing her to a “one-eyed horse” and suggesting she was not as youthful as Rastignac thought. Despite Bianchon’s concerns about the Marquise's superficiality and potential debts, Rastignac was drawn to her wealth and social status, believing she could aid his ambitions.

Bianchon offered to introduce Rastignac to his uncle, Judge Jean-Jules Popinot, known for his integrity but often overlooked due to his modesty. As Bianchon approached Popinot’s home, he reflected on the challenges of persuading the judge to help Rastignac, highlighting the themes of ambition and societal expectations.

Twelve years earlier, Popinot had been appointed to distribute food during a severe scarcity. Initially hesitant, he became dedicated to alleviating suffering, transforming from a legal-focused judge to a compassionate benefactor, akin to Saint Vincent de Paul. His charitable work drained his resources, yet he found fulfillment in helping the poor, converting his storeroom into a parlor for those in need.

The narrative shifts to a case involving the Marquis d’Espard, whose wife sought a Commission in Lunacy for him. Bianchon, visiting Popinot, found him engaged with petitioners, reflecting his preoccupation with charity. Popinot read the petition regarding the Marquis's alleged mental decline and considered the Marquise's motives.

The next evening, Bianchon found Popinot absorbed in a complex judgment, appearing disheveled. They visited Madame d’Espard, who portrayed herself as a victim of her husband's behavior. Popinot questioned her motives, noting her luxurious lifestyle contradicted her claims of distress. After their meeting, he resolved to investigate further, planning to summon Madame Jeanrenaud, linked to M. d’Espard. This case promised to uncover the truth about the Marquis’s mental state and the lengths individuals would go to protect their interests in Parisian society.

His private life was supported by loyal servants, but his decision to isolate his children led to misunderstandings with neighbors, who viewed him as eccentric. Gossip portrayed him as mad, fueled by his frugal lifestyle and perceived financial ruin, exacerbated by tenants’ jealousy.
