The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, a dark, winding lane by the Hôtel de Ville ending at the Rue Martroi, was closed by a turnstile until 1823. Less than six feet wide at its broadest, it held pooled gutter water and remained perpetually muddy; sunlight barely reached it and dampness lingered. Residents lit lamps at five in June and kept them burning through winter. Approaching the Marais felt like walking through cellars; the corner house at Rue du Tourniquet and Rue de la Tixeranderie still bore iron rings from chains once used for public safety.

Built to combat humidity, the house had raised cellars and an entrance reached by three steps; the door formed a closed arch with a keystone carved as a female head. Three high, iron-barred windows lit a small dwelling where two beds draped in green serge and an old woman tending a brazier—preparing a porter’s-wife stew—were seen. The kitchen-dining room was sparse but kept with evident cleanliness and thrift.

The old woman’s pale, wrinkled face seemed part of the house; she sat by the hearth with mischief mingled with resignation while her daughter, Caroline, bent over an embroidery frame. Despite poverty, Caroline’s rosy cheeks and bright eyes showed an undimmed spirit. One August day in 1815 the mother noticed a new passerby—a tall, thin man in black, solemn, perhaps a clerk at the Préfet’s office—and awaited his return. When he passed again Caroline, stirred by a trace of suffering on his face, felt an unfamiliar sympathy and began to hope he might change her life.

For two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved erratically, no longer passing the Rue du Tourniquet at the same hour. Madame Crochard noticed his distracted manner—lost in thought and seldom acknowledging her or daughter Caroline. One mid-September morning Caroline’s lively presence caught his eye; their fleeting glances suggested a shared understanding.

Weeks later he began passing twice daily; brief, wordless encounters became a cautious routine—Caroline sensed his troubles and he hers, yet both feared deeper intimacy. Madame Crochard, frustrated by Caroline’s indecision, turned on the Gentleman who had once earned her mild approval. Overhearing a frank talk about their dire finances, he was moved and secretly left money; Caroline found it, and they exchanged silent gratitude.

In early May Caroline proposed a trip to Montmorency. Madame Crochard feigned sleep, giving Caroline and the Gentleman—now Roger—room for revealing talk. The countryside and easy conversation lifted his spirits; sharing family hardships deepened their bond. The day brimmed with laughter, and as evening fell Caroline confessed her happiness. Roger, contemplating lasting joy, playfully took her hand—a quiet promise of hope.

Roger and Caroline are in Paris. Caroline has lived a month in a Rue Taitbout flat, furnished with gray hangings, a writing table and bronze clock. One September day in 1816 she waits on the balcony in a green gown, heart racing. After fifteen minutes' impatience, Roger arrives and they embrace. He must attend a meeting with the Attorney‑General, disappointing her; she objects to his theatre outings, professing devotion. Perched on his lap, she vows to be Caroline de Bellefeuille, spending her earnings on the poor. She plays a sonata as he departs, watching him drive away with longing.

Five years after settling in her Rue Taitbout home, Caroline de Bellefeuille, twenty-four, is content with her four-year-old son Charles and infant daughter Eugenie. Embracing motherhood, she prefers simple pleasures, values goodness and beauty, and rejects society’s luxuries. While rocking Eugenie, she recalls joyful visits to Bellefeuille with Roger; their love deepened in a quiet life, yet his secrecy troubles her.

On May 6, 1822—an anniversary—Roger brings a document outlining Eugenie’s marriage portion. He insists Charles must learn self-sufficiency and Eugenie deserves a fortune. Their warm evening, capped by a lantern show for Charles, ends when Roger departs at night, promising a brief respite; his duty and secrecy leave Caroline in pain.

Soon after she hurries to her ailing mother, Madame Crochard. Unrecognized by the widow’s acquaintances, Caroline finds women scheming and a priest summoned for last rites. Tending her mother, Caroline hears Madame Crochard, lamenting their estrangement, say the priest forced a confession about Caroline’s benefactor—hinting at complex secrets.

The narrative returns to November 1805, when young barrister Monsieur de Granville, urged into a match with Angelique Bontems to secure his future despite her family’s stigma, visits Bayeux. At church he glimpses Angelique, rekindling a childhood affection and setting events in motion that entwine love, duty, and social obligation.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems he watched her in a dreary drawing-room as she quietly prepared her trousseau. She never argued religion; when he teased her rosary she calmly returned it to its bag. Firm in the Church’s teaching, Angelique insisted a mother must have religion and argued one could not judge between disbelievers and God. Impressed by her conviction, Granville sometimes mistook desire for love and even felt tempted to adopt her faith. Madame Bontems exacted a promise from him to respect Angelique’s practices and allow her freedom of conscience; Angelique’s gentle look and the Abbe Fontanon’s approval completed the marriage. After the wedding they moved to Paris, where Granville became public prosecutor.

Angelique persuaded him to take a ground-floor apartment in the Marais near a church and chapel for the sake of future children; Granville, preferring a more fashionable quarter, yielded. He set up his study and left furnishing to Angelique, who created a cold, solemn home reflecting her austere upbringing. He tried to excuse her ways and praised her love and gentleness, but grew frustrated by the lack of warmth.

For months they seemed harmonious: Granville was absorbed in work and overlooked her strict observances until November 1808, when the Canon of Bayeux, Angelique’s spiritual director, arrived in Paris alarmed at what he saw as her moral decline and imposed stricter practices. Misunderstandings followed as Angelique avoided social obligations; Granville, irritated, pressed her to fulfill her role. She appealed to the Pope, who replied that a wife should accompany her husband even at some personal cost. Rather than reconcile them, the decision widened the rift; Angelique and her director dismissed it.

Granville began asserting his preferences—insisting on meat daily despite her objections—and withdrew from society. The household became oppressive: Angelique’s austerity dimmed her warmth and beauty, their children suffered under rigid rules, sons were sent to school early, and daughters were hurried toward marriage. Increasingly isolated, Granville sought refuge in work and a legal book while Angelique’s efforts to reconvert him deepened their estrangement; the home became rigid and joyless rather than companionable.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

Between 1806 and 1825 the de Granville household showed little outward drama, but Madame de Granville was inwardly unhappy. After losing her husband's affection she prayed for reconciliation while Granville, now a prominent judge, lived in the entresol to avoid her. Each morning a housekeeper brought messages; he refused breakfast, citing urgent court business.

At thirty-five and prematurely aged, she grew bitter. When Granville declined the presidency of a provincial court, friends and her confessor suspected an affair—an idea that mortified her. During Lent in 1822 her health worsened from stress and fever, compounded by his indifference and her pious efforts that undermined intimacy.

Recovered in May, she consulted the Abbé Fontanon, who revealed Granville had lived seven years with a concubine, fathering two children and dissipating their fortune. Shocked and urged to forgive, she resolved to confront him. After a scene in which others blamed her and she fainted, she confronted Granville. He accused her of sacrificing his happiness to piety, admitted his long neglect and refusal to restore their marriage. The exchange made clear their marriage had failed; love could not be commanded, and she was left devastated.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Early one December night in 1833 a white‑haired man whose face showed grief more than years paced the Rue Gaillon and stopped before a modest two‑story house with a dim attic light. A young doctor, Horace Bianchon, came out; recognizing him, he offered his arm and bantered about the hour and the slippery pavement. The visitor—Comte de Granville—replied with weary pride that at fifty‑five he was still hale and often walked home from the Courts or the club.

Bianchon teased that a man of his station was courting robbery. Granville shrugged off the danger with melancholy and confessed, half amused, that he had been spying on a shadow in the third‑floor window—the garret had a morbid attraction for him. When Bianchon offered to tell him who lived there, Granville refused: he would not pay to learn whether the shadow was man or woman, happy or miserable. Nine years earlier he had ceased to be surprised that old men took pleasure in growing flowers; life had taught him to distrust human affection. “I grew old within a few days,” he said. He would attach himself to nothing living—only “unreasoning animals, or plants, or superficial things” appealed to him; even Taglioni’s grace moved him more than most people. He abhorred life and insisted nothing could interest him.

Bianchon probed: did he have children? Granville answered bitterly, listing them—an eldest daughter, the Comtesse de Vandenesse; another daughter married suitably through her sister; sons with successful legal careers: a viscount who had been public prosecutor at Limoges and was now President of the Court at Orléans, and a younger son who was public prosecutor in Paris. Each had their cares; none had devoted themselves to him. If one had tried to fill his void it would have ruined that child. Perhaps they now awaited his money. Bianchon protested; Granville, sarcastic and self‑aware, explained his philanthropy as self‑indulgence: he paid for sensations as he gambled, seeking illusions to make his heart glow while expecting no gratitude. He would see Bianchon die without blinking and asked the same indifference in return. Life’s events had flowed over his heart like lava over Herculaneum—the town remained, dead.

Unable to leave him in that mood, Bianchon reluctantly described the garret’s occupant. It was a stark picture: a woman of about thirty whose love had become fanatical. She adored a young man of pleasing appearance but every vice—Solvet—a gambler given to wine and women, guilty of acts punishable by law. This woman had abandoned a comfortable life and an adoring husband, the father of her children, for this man. She worked day and night; he had stolen from her even funds saved for the children’s food and clothes. Only three days earlier she had sold her hair—the finest Bianchon had ever seen—and when the lover returned she could not hide the gold quickly enough. For a smile or a kiss she would give away the price of a fortnight’s subsistence. The toil had hollowed her cheeks; the children’s cries had broken her heart; she lay ill on her wretched bed that night. When Bianchon visited, the family had nothing to eat; the children were so weak they could not even cry.

Despite himself Granville put his hand in his waistcoat pocket as if to help, but Bianchon begged him to pity the woman and help cure her passion. Granville recoiled: how could he pity a misery whose joys he might once have prized with his whole fortune? Thousands of dead and sick men would barter everything for a return of youth and feeling. At the name Bianchon murmured—“Poor Caroline!”—Granville shuddered, gripped the doctor’s arm, and asked hoarsely whether she was Caroline Crochard. On confirmation his reaction was violent: the name had pierced him with the worst pain his heart could endure, an emotion he called a gift from hell. He knew how to pay such debts.

They reached the Rue de la Chaussée d’Antin where a shriveled night scavenger stood by the curb. Granville asked if the scavenger ever found thousand‑franc notes and, hearing that sometimes he did, handed him such a note with a grotesque injunction: spend it in the wine shop, get drunk, start fights, beat your wife, blacken your friends’ eyes—give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist, perhaps to the police, public prosecutor, judge, and prison wardens. “Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later,” Granville said. He pointed out the scavenger’s corrupt face to Bianchon and declared he had thus “squared accounts with hell.” As for Caroline Crochard, he pronounced coldly that she might die of hunger and thirst listening to her starving children; he would not give a sou to rescue her. Because Bianchon had helped her, he said, he would see him no more.

Granville left Bianchon stunned and walked to the Rue Saint‑Lazare where a carriage waited. A servant informed him that his son, the attorney‑general, had been that afternoon and was waiting in his bedroom. Granville dismissed the servant, then confronted his son—in a tone at once imperious and unexpectedly tender—about coming after being ordered not to visit unless summoned.

Eugène, the younger man, spoke with tremor: that afternoon a very young man arrested in the house of a friend he had robbed had appealed to Granville, claiming to be his son. “His name?” Granville asked. “Charles Crochard,” Eugène answered. Granville waved him down: that sufficed. He stood a moment, then, speaking as a father, accepted the claim. He handed his son a considerable bundle of banknotes and authorized him to use it as he saw fit in the affair.

The mood shifted. Granville’s last words of counsel were almost tender: he warned Eugène about the weight of marriage—the most important act society requires—and urged him to choose a partner carefully and to consult him. He promised to write on the subject from Florence. Then, with quietness suggesting a final parting, he asked his son to kiss him and announced he would beg dismissal from the King and go to Italy. He would keep his dignity as a father and a former presiding officer.

The scene leaves two images of Granville: in the street a man hardened into cruelty and misanthropy, giving money to be squandered so social machinery may be set in motion on petty moral wreckage; in his bedroom a father handing money and last counsels to his son, preparing to depart. Between those moments stands Bianchon’s account of Caroline Crochard—a portrait of sacrifice and degradation—and the Comte’s icy rejection of any duty toward her, even as the name awakens a torment proving he is not wholly dead to feeling. Granville resolves to quit Paris and to cut off a friend who had awakened his pity, while secretly acknowledging familial ties he will attempt to leave behind with money and admonition.
