The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once a dark, winding lane near the Hôtel de Ville, ended at the Rue Martroi and was marked by a turnstile until 1823. Less than six feet wide at its broadest, it often pooled gutter water and remained muddy; sunlight came only briefly in summer, and dampness lingered.

Residents lit lamps at five in June and kept them burning through winter. Approaching the Marais felt like walking through cellars, as many old Paris streets were. The corner house at Rue du Tourniquet and Rue de la Tixeranderie showed its age: iron rings remained from chains once used for public safety.

Built to combat humidity, the house had cellars raised above ground and an entrance reached by three steps. The door had a closed arch with a keystone carved as a female head. Three high windows, barred with iron, lit a small dwelling. Inside, two beds draped in green serge were visible, and an old woman tended a brazier while preparing a porter’s-wife stew. The kitchen-dining room was sparsely furnished but kept with evident cleanliness and thrift.

The old woman’s pale, wrinkled face seemed part of the house. She sat by the fireplace with an expression of mischief mixed with resignation while her daughter worked at an embroidery frame. With slow fingers and antiquated spectacles, the mother occasionally glanced from her work out the window; the daughter bent over her needle with focused modesty.

Their modest home reflected the lives of working-class Parisian women. Despite poverty, the girl retained youthful charm—rosy cheeks and bright eyes suggesting a spirit not wholly dimmed by hardship. The old woman speculated about passersby and hoped for a better future for her daughter; the girl, absorbed in embroidery, usually kept her gaze down.

One August day in 1815 the mother noticed a new face among the regular passersby: a tall, thin man in black whose solemn bearing intrigued her. She fancied he was a clerk from the Préfet’s office, and her excitement was plain. Caroline, roused from indifference by her mother’s remarks, glimpsed the stranger as he passed.

The old woman eagerly awaited his return, convinced he would come by again. When he did, Caroline caught another glimpse and felt an unfamiliar sympathy, noting traces of suffering on his face. This was the first passerby to stir such feeling in her; she typically regarded people with indifference, focused on work and routine. For the first time she felt compassion for someone beyond her own troubles.

As she resumed her embroidery, Caroline found herself looking forward to another sight of him, intrigued by the possibility that this new acquaintance might alter the course of her life.

For two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved erratically, no longer passing the Rue du Tourniquet at the same time. Madame Crochard noticed his distracted manner—often lost in thought and rarely acknowledging her or her daughter, Caroline. One mid-September morning Caroline’s lively presence caught his eye; he watched the animated scene at the window, and a silent connection of fleeting glances suggested a deeper understanding.

Weeks passed and the Gentleman began to pass twice daily; their brief encounters became a routine they both anticipated. Their silent exchanges conveyed mutual concern: Caroline sensed when he was troubled, and he could tell when she was anxious about her work. Both remained cautious, fearing that greater intimacy might bring complications or heartache.

Madame Crochard, irritated by her daughter’s indecision, turned her displeasure toward the Gentleman, who had once been the object of her mild approval. As winter neared, life’s hardships weighed on Caroline, and the Gentleman noticed how they affected her spirit. One night he overheard a mother-and-daughter conversation that made their financial struggles plain. Touched, he secretly left money for them; Caroline found it the next day, and a moment of silent gratitude passed between them.

In early May Caroline proposed a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—more somber than usual—unexpectedly joined them. During the journey Madame Crochard pretended to sleep, giving Caroline and the Gentleman a chance for light-hearted talk that revealed their growing affection. The countryside’s beauty and the warmth of their exchange lifted his spirits; he began to see Caroline differently.

Their bond deepened as they shared personal stories. Caroline spoke of her family’s hardships and her mother’s sacrifices. The Gentleman, now called Roger, was drawn to her honesty and resilience. Their day in Montmorency brimmed with laughter and joy, a sharp contrast to their previously somber meetings.

As evening approached, Caroline voiced her newfound happiness, and Roger reflected on the possibility of joy beyond that day. Their shared understanding hinted at a deeper bond. Preparing to leave, they playfully held hands, solidifying the connection. Madame Crochard pretended not to notice, but the gesture marked a meaningful step—an unspoken promise and the hope of a brighter future together.

The adventures of Roger and Caroline continue in modern Paris, where newly built apartments suit honeymooners. In a pristine building on Rue Taitbout, Caroline has lived for over a month in a charming second-floor apartment, furnished with care and style. The rooms, freshly painted and elegantly appointed, reflect current trends influenced by English design.

Caroline's bedroom has gray hangings trimmed with green silk, comfortable chairs, and a writing table for love letters. A bronze clock showing Love crowning Psyche and a Gothic-patterned carpet complete the cozy setting. As she prepares for Roger's visit, her hairdresser flatters her, reminding her of his admiration.

On a chilly September day in 1816, Caroline wears a green grenadine gown trimmed with chinchilla. Eagerly she steps onto the balcony to watch for Roger; her heart races as she anticipates his arrival. After a quarter-hour of waiting, impatience replaces excitement, but she brightens when she finally sees him approaching.

Roger arrives, and they embrace passionately, rejoicing in their reunion after three days apart. He then tells her he must attend a meeting with the Attorney-General, which disappoints Caroline. She playfully objects to the idea of his enjoying the theatre without her, declaring her deep affection.

Despite the setback, Caroline quickly returns to the joy of being with Roger. They share tender moments, and she expresses her wish to take his name—Caroline de Bellefeuille—as a sign of commitment. Perched on his lap, she speaks of using her earnings to help the poor, underscoring her gratitude for their life together.

Her excitement grows while she plays a sonata, but Roger, mindful of obligations, prepares to leave. They exchange a playful farewell, and Caroline watches him drive away, savoring the moment and longing for his return.

Five years after Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille settled in her Rue Taitbout home, she sits serenely with her four-year-old son, Charles, who plays with a rocking horse. Caroline, now twenty-four, shows a beauty formed by happiness and fulfillment. She has embraced motherhood, devoted to nurturing her children and cultivating their appreciation for goodness and beauty. Content with these simple pleasures, she remains indifferent to societal expectations, never craving luxury or status.

As she rocks her infant daughter, Eugenie, Caroline recalls joyful visits to Bellefeuille with Roger. Their love has flourished in the simplicity of shared life, free from social complication. Caroline wonders quietly why Roger seeks happiness in secrecy, curious about the nature of their arrangement.

On May 6, 1822, the anniversary of a pivotal day for them, Caroline prepares for dinner, anticipating Roger's arrival. He brings a document outlining Eugenie’s marriage portion, prompting discussion about different provisions for their children. Roger explains he believes Charles, as a boy, should learn self-sufficiency, while Eugenie, as a girl, deserves a fortune.

Their evening is warm and lively, ending with a magical lantern show for Charles. But as night deepens, Roger must leave, and Caroline feels the familiar pang of separation. He reassures her he has three days off, yet duty hangs over him.

Soon after this anniversary, Caroline hastens to her mother, Madame Crochard, who is gravely ill. The old widow’s acquaintances, unaware Caroline is her daughter, have summoned a priest to offer last rites. Caroline arrives amid confusion and deceit: the old women hope to secure benefit from Madame Crochard’s wealth. Despite their intentions, Caroline’s love for her mother shows as she cares for her.

In her final moments, Madame Crochard expresses regret over their estrangement and reveals that the priest has made her confess about Caroline’s benefactor—an admission that hints at a complex web of relationships and secrets to follow.

To understand those connections, the narrative backtracks to events before Madame Crochard’s death. In November 1805, a young barrister, Monsieur de Granville, finds himself in a precarious position after leaving a gathering at the High Chancellor’s residence and is offered a chance to advance his career through his uncle’s influence.

Granville receives a letter from his father urging a swift return: a possible marriage to Mademoiselle Angelique Bontems could secure his future. The letter details the financial benefits of the match, despite the social stigma attached to the Bontems family. Torn between ambition and feeling, Granville recalls childhood affection for Angelique, whom he has not seen in years.

The next day he travels to Bayeux, greeted by his father and taken to the Bontems’ home, where the atmosphere is stifling under the widow’s strict religious observances. Granville dreads the match, yet his father’s insistence weighs heavily.

During a church service Granville glimpses Angelique, whose beauty captivates him anew. Their brief interaction rekindles his feelings, though he is acutely aware of family constraints. As the service ends, his impatience leads him to approach Angelique, setting the stage for a reunion that could alter their lives.

The intertwining stories of Caroline and Granville reveal complexities of love, duty, and social expectation, as both navigate personal desires against familial obligations and ambition.

For about two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he visited her regularly in her dreary drawing-room. During these visits he watched Angelique marking her trousseau; she never initiated religious talk. When Granville playfully handled her rosary or mocked practices, she quietly returned it to its bag with a serene smile. She firmly accepted the Church’s teachings, insisting a mother should have a religion and questioning how one could judge between disbelievers and God.

Granville, captivated by her conviction, sometimes mistook his desire for her as love. Angelique reconciled feeling and duty easily, leaving Granville unsure of his own emotions. He was grateful to Madame Bontems for instilling religious principles in Angelique, thinking they would help her face life’s challenges.

On the day of their marriage contract, Madame Bontems required Granville to promise to respect Angelique’s religious practices and let her freedom of conscience. Angelique’s innocent gaze convinced him; the Abbe Fontanon, who guided their consciences, smiled approvingly. Shortly after their wedding the couple moved to Paris, where Granville became public prosecutor.

Angelique persuaded him to choose a large ground-floor apartment in the Marais, near a church and chapel, arguing it would benefit their future children. Granville, who had preferred a more fashionable quarter, yielded. He set up his study and left the home decoration to Angelique, who delighted in furnishing their new home. But Granville was dismayed by the cold, solemn atmosphere she created, the reflection of her austere upbringing.

Despite trying to understand her choices, Granville grew increasingly frustrated by the lack of warmth and charm. He attributed the decor to Angelique’s Bayeux background, where taste seemed less refined, and tried to comfort her by insisting happiness came from her love and gentleness rather than material things.

From April through winter the couple appeared harmonious; Granville stayed busy with work and remained largely unaware of Angelique’s strict religious observances—Lenten meals she disguised as carelessness. He accompanied her to church on Sundays but did not grasp her fervor until a significant event disrupted their lives.

In November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux, who had guided Angelique, arrived in Paris alarmed by what he saw as her moral decline and sought to reclaim her from a lax lifestyle. Angelique adopted stricter practices, which caused misunderstandings, particularly over social obligations. Granville grew irritated by her frequent excuses to avoid events, especially balls.

After a tense evening he confronted her about her demeanor and attire, urging her to embrace her social role. Arguments escalated: Angelique cited her director’s prohibitions against attending balls; Granville insisted her priest was overstepping. The dispute turned theological and culminated in Angelique writing to the Pope for guidance.

The Pope’s reply affirmed Granville’s position, advising that a wife should accompany her husband even if it meant compromising her virtue. Rather than resolve matters, this deepened the divide; Angelique and her director dismissed the Pope’s advice. Frustrated by her strict observances, Granville began to assert his preferences more forcefully, ordering meat daily despite her objections.

As the home atmosphere grew oppressive, Granville withdrew from social life, weighed down by Angelique’s bigotry. The household became stifling and gloom-filled, affecting everyone including the servants. He recognized that their home had been transformed into a place devoid of joy, marked by rigid piety.

Granville’s discontent deepened as Angelique’s beauty was overshadowed by austerity. He felt trapped in a marriage where duty had replaced love, and her devotion left little room for affection. Their children, raised under her strict rules, suffered from the oppressive climate; Granville sent his sons to school early and resolved to marry off his daughters as soon as they came of age.

His isolation increased as he struggled with his marriage’s reality. He sought solace in work, attempting to write a book on law, but Angelique’s attempts to reconvert him only added to his frustration. The relationship deteriorated: Granville felt increasingly alienated from his wife and their home, which had become a place of coldness and rigidity rather than warmth and companionship.

The history of the de Granville household between 1806 and 1825 was largely uneventful. Madame de Granville, having lost her husband's affection, continued to pay for Masses hoping to discover her faults and restore their marriage. Granville, now a prominent judge, lived in the entresol of their home to avoid her. Each morning a housekeeper relayed messages; he habitually declined breakfast invitations, citing pressing court business.

At thirty-five and looking older, Madame de Granville awaited his return, eager to engage him with her narrow remarks. When Granville refused the presidency of a provincial court, her friends and confessor suspected an illicit affair; they took his refusal as a sign of liaison, which deeply distressed her, for she believed him incapable of such sin.

During Lent in 1822 her health declined from stress and a low fever, worsened by Granville's coldness. Attempts at affection were often undone by her recurring bigotry. By late May, as she recovered, she sought guidance from the Abbe Fontanon, who delivered devastating news: Granville had lived in sin with a concubine for seven years, fathering two children and squandering their fortune.

In disbelief, Madame de Granville struggled with the revelation, torn between faith and betrayal. The Abbe urged forgiveness and patience for Granville's eventual enlightenment, but she was consumed by turmoil. After a tumultuous day she left home, determined to confront him.

Meanwhile Roger and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille were in a tense exchange: she declared hatred for his wife, whom she blamed for their unhappiness. Roger reassured her of hopes for a shared future, but their conversation was cut short by Madame de Granville's sudden fainting. On regaining consciousness she confronted Granville, questioning his love and fidelity. He accused her of sacrificing his happiness to piety, insisting she had failed to fulfill her role as a loving wife.

Their exchange exposed deep faults. Granville argued true love requires sacrifice and understanding; Madame de Granville clung to moral righteousness. He explained that while he had sought happiness elsewhere, she had neglected their relationship through religious devotion. As the conversation escalated, her jealousy and anger flared, producing a painful recognition of their irreconcilable differences. Granville's indifference and admission of affection for another left her devastated. Despite tears and appeals for forgiveness, he remained resolute, saying he could not rekindle love faded by years of neglect.

The dialogue ended in a stark acknowledgment of their failed marriage: love could not be commanded or revived at will. Madame de Granville was left to confront the reality of his emotional detachment and to grapple with the consequences of their choices and the uncertain future of their relationship.

Early in December 1833 a white-haired man, his features more worn by grief than years, walked the Rue Gaillon at midnight and paused before a modest two-story house with a dim attic light. A young man emerged; recognizing him, he approached.

“What, is it you, Monsieur le Président? Alone at this hour, so far from the Rue Saint-Lazare. Allow me to give you my arm. The pavement is greasy this morning.”

The Comte de Granville answered, “I am no more than fifty-five, unfortunately for me. At that age a man is still hale and strong.”

“Then you are in waiting on a lady, I suppose,” said Horace Bianchon. “You do not go about Paris on foot when you keep such fine horses—”

“Sometimes I return from the Courts or the club on foot,” Granville said.

“And with large sums of money perhaps!” the doctor exclaimed. “It’s an invitation to the assassin’s knife.”

“I am not afraid,” Granville replied, with melancholy indifference.

“Then don’t stand about,” Bianchon urged, leading him toward the boulevard. “A little more and I shall believe you are bent on robbing me of your last illness—dying in some other hand than mine.”

“You caught me playing the spy,” Granville admitted. “For some time I have observed at a third-floor window the shadow of a person who seems to work with heroic constancy.”

He paused, seeming in pain. “I take as great an interest in that garret as a citizen of Paris must feel in the finishing of the Palais Royal.”

“Well,” Bianchon said eagerly, “I can tell you—”

“Tell me nothing,” Granville interrupted. “I would not give a centime to know whether the shadow is a man or a woman, nor whether the inhabitant is happy or miserable. I was surprised not to see anyone at work this evening. For nine years I have ceased to wonder that old men amuse themselves with growing flowers and planting trees; the events of life have taught them disbelief in all human affection. I grew old within a few days. I will no longer attach myself to any creature but unreasoning animals, plants, or superficial things. I think more of Taglioni’s grace than of all human feeling. I abhor life and the world in which I live alone. Nothing, nothing can move or interest me.”

“But you have children?” Bianchon asked.

“My children!” Granville repeated bitterly. “My eldest daughter is the Comtesse de Vandenesse; the other will make a good match through her sister. My sons have succeeded in their careers—the Viscount was public prosecutor at Limoges and is now President of the Court at Orléans; the younger is public prosecutor in Paris. They have their own cares. If one of those hearts had been devoted to me, if one had tried to fill this void”—he struck his breast—“that one would have failed in life, sacrificing it to me. And why should they? To bring sunshine into my few remaining years—and would they have succeeded? Might I not have accepted such generosity as a debt? But it is not for nothing that we teach them arithmetic and how to count. At this moment perhaps they are waiting for my money.”

“O Monsieur le Comte, how could such an idea enter your head—you who are kind, friendly, and humane!” Bianchon protested. “If I were not living proof of the benevolence you exercise so liberally—”

“To please myself,” Granville replied. “I pay for a sensation, as I would pay a pile of gold to recover the most childish illusion that would make my heart glow. I help my fellow-creatures for my own sake, just as I gamble; I look for gratitude from none. I should see you die without blinking; and I beg you to feel the same with regard to me. Life has swept over my heart like the lavas of Vesuvius over Herculaneum. The town is there—dead.”

“Those who have brought a soul as warm and living as yours to such indifference are indeed guilty!” Bianchon exclaimed.

“Say no more,” Granville said, shuddering.

“You have a malady which you ought to allow me to treat,” Bianchon insisted.

“What, do you know of a cure for death?” Granville cried irritably.

“I undertake, Monsieur le Comte, to revive the heart you believe frozen.”

“Are you a match for Talma, then?” Granville asked satirically.

“No. But Nature is as far above Talma as Talma is superior to me. Listen: the garret you watch is inhabited by a woman of about thirty whose love is fanatic. The object of her adoration is a young man of pleasing appearance but endowed with every conceivable vice. He is a gambler, addicted to wine and women, and guilty of acts punishable by law. To him this unhappy woman sacrificed a life of ease, a man who worshiped her, and the father of her children.”

“What is wrong, Monsieur le Comte?” Granville asked.

“Nothing. Go on,” he replied.

“She has allowed him to squander a fortune; she would give him the world if she had it. She works night and day; many times she has seen him rob her even of the money saved for her children’s clothes and food. Only three days ago she sold her hair, the finest I ever saw; he came in, and she could not hide the gold quickly enough. For a smile, for a kiss, she gave up the price of a fortnight’s life and peace. It is dreadful and yet sublime. Work is wearing her cheeks hollow. Her children’s crying has broken her heart; she is ill and now on her wretched bed. This evening they had nothing to eat; the children lacked strength to cry; they were silent when I went up.”

Bianchon stood still. Against his will, Granville put his hand into his waistcoat.

“I guess how she is still alive if you attend her,” Granville murmured.

“O poor soul!” Bianchon cried. “Who could refuse to help her? I only wish I were richer; I hope to cure her of her passion.”

“How can you expect me to pity a misery whose joys would seem cheaply bought with my whole fortune!” Granville exclaimed, withdrawing his hand empty of the notes Bianchon assumed he had. “That woman feels; she is alive! Would not Louis XV have given his kingdom to rise from the grave and have three days of youth and life? Is not that the history of thousands—dead men, sick men, old men?”

“Poor Caroline!” Bianchon lamented.

At the name, Granville shuddered and gripped the doctor’s arm tightly.

“Her name is Caroline Crochard?” he asked, voice breaking.

“Then you know her?” Bianchon said, astonished.

“And the wretch’s name is Solvet. Ay, you have kept your word!” Granville exclaimed. “You have roused my heart to the most terrible pain it can suffer until it is dust. That emotion, too, is a gift from hell, and I always know how to pay those debts.”

They reached the corner of the Rue de la Chaussée d’Antin. A night scavenger with a shriveled face stood by the curb.

“Do you ever pick up a thousand-franc note?” Granville asked.

“Now and then, master,” the scavenger replied.

“And you restore them?”

“It depends on the reward.”

“You’re the man for me,” Granville said, handing him a thousand-franc note. “Take this, but remember: spend it at the wineshop, get drunk, fight, beat your wife, black your friends’ eyes. That will give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist—perhaps to the police, the public prosecutor, the judge, and the prison warders. Don’t do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later.”

Granville pointed out the scavenger’s grotesque face to Bianchon, who stood stupefied. “Now I have squared accounts with hell and had some pleasure for my money,” he said. “As for Caroline Crochard—she may die of hunger and thirst, hearing the heartrending cries of her starving children, convinced of the baseness of the man she loves. I will not give a sou to rescue her; and because you have helped her, I will see you no more—”

He left Bianchon like a statue and walked briskly to the Rue Saint-Lazare, where a carriage waited at his door.

“Monsieur, your son, the attorney-general, came about an hour ago,” the servant said. “He is waiting for you in your bedroom.”

Granville dismissed the servant and entered. “What motive can be strong enough to require you to infringe the order I have given my children never to come to me unless I send for them?” he asked his son.

“Father,” the younger man replied tremulously, “I hope you will forgive me when you have heard me.”

“Your reply is proper,” Granville said. “Sit down.” After a pause: “But whether I walk up and down or take a seat, speak without heeding me.”

“Father,” the son continued, “this afternoon, at four o’clock, a very young man arrested in the house of a friend of mine, whom he had robbed, appealed to you. He says he is your son.”

“His name?” Granville asked hoarsely.

“Charles Crochard.”

“That will do,” Granville said, waving his hand imperiously.

He paced in silence; his son waited.

“My son,” he began, voice mild and fatherly—causing the young lawyer to start. “Charles Crochard spoke the truth. I am glad you came to me tonight, my good Eugène.” He handed him a bundle of banknotes. “Here is a considerable sum of money; you can make any use of it in this matter. I trust you implicitly and approve whatever arrangements you may make, now or in the future. Eugène, my dear son, kiss me. We part perhaps for the last time. I shall tomorrow crave my dismissal from the King, and I am going to Italy.

“Though a father owes no account of his life to his children, he is bound to bequeath to them the experience Fate sells him dearly—is it not part of their inheritance? When you marry,” he added, shuddering involuntarily, “do not undertake it lightly; that act is the most important society requires of us. Study the character of the woman who is to be your partner; consult me too, I will judge her myself. A lack of union between husband and wife leads to terrible misfortune; sooner or later we are punished for contravening the social law. I will write to you on this from Florence. A father who presides over a supreme court of justice must not have to blush in the presence of his son. Good-bye.”
