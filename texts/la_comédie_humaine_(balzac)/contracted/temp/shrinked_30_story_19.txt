To M. le Baron Barchou de Penhoen.

Of all the pupils of the Oratorian school at Vendôme, we are, I think, the only two who have since met in mid‑career of a life of letters. When we met, you were engaged on your works on German philosophy, and I on this study. So neither of us has missed his vocation; and you, seeing your name here, will feel as much pleasure as I do in inscribing my work to you. Your old schoolfellow, 1840 De Balzac.

It was one o’clock one winter night in 1829–30 in the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu’s salon; only two family guests remained. A young man took his leave; as his carriage died away, the Vicomtesse turned to her daughter Camille, who pretended to watch a fire‑screen while listening anxiously.

“Camille,” she said, “if you continue to treat young Comte de Restaud as you have this evening, I shall have to prevent him from visiting. Trust my love and let me guide you. At seventeen one cannot judge the past or the future or social standings. M. de Restaud has a mother, Mlle. Goriot, who squandered her father’s fortune and is of no birth. Though he behaves admirably toward her and his siblings, any family would hesitate to entrust their daughter’s future to him while she lives.”

Derville, a family friend who had overheard, interposed: “I have won, M. le Comte. I shall assist your niece.” On being challenged, he said he had read the Vicomtesse’s looks and settled by the fire. Camille’s uncle approached and the Vicomtesse sat between them.

“It’s time for a story that may change your view of Ernest de Restaud’s prospects,” Derville said.

The tale was meant for the Vicomtesse. By birth and fortune she led the Faubourg Saint‑Germain; that an attorney could be intimate with her seemed odd until the facts were known. After returning from exile with the Royal family, the Vicomtesse had been reduced to a pension from Louis XVIII, intolerable to her pride. Her Hôtel de Grandlieu had been sold during the Revolution. Derville discovered defects in the title, pursued legal action, and reclaimed it. He obtained judgments restoring significant property and indemnities, raising her income to about sixty thousand francs. He did this without exploiting his new popularity: a man of integrity, he refused preferment to keep his practice and his social ties, becoming a close family confidant.

His management of her affairs won him esteem and many aristocratic clients, but he did not seek to profit politically. His steadfast service revealed his talents; since Ernest de Restaud’s arrival—especially as Camille showed interest—Derville had visited the Hôtel de Grandlieu more often.

At a recent ball Derville had taunted Camille: “It’s a pity that young man doesn’t have two or three million francs, isn’t it?”

“Is it a pity? I don’t think so,” she replied. “M. de Restaud is capable and well educated. He will have as much money as he wishes when he comes into power.”

“But what if he were already rich?” Derville persisted.

“Rich already?” Camille blushed. “All the girls would be vying for him,” she said.

“Then Mlle. de Grandlieu might not be the one he favors,” he teased. Her blush betrayed her. Camille rose and left. Derville thought, “She loves him.”

Since that evening Camille had been unusually attentive to Derville, who approved of her feelings. Though grateful for his services, she showed respect rather than warmth; gratitude often weighs on the next generation, which may seek to escape it.

“This adventure,” Derville began, “recalls the one romantic episode of my life. It may seem odd that an attorney speaks of romance, yet once, at twenty-five, having seen strange things, I met a man I shall describe—a usurer whom you cannot know.

Imagine a sallow, wan face—what I would call lunar. His iron-gray hair was sleek and carefully combed; his features were impassive, his little yellow eyes without eyelashes peering from under a shabby cap as if afraid of light. Thin lips, a gimlet nose, and a low, suave voice that never rose made him look either prematurely aged or unnaturally preserved. His room matched him—clean but threadbare, like an elderly spinster’s. In winter the fire smoldered in ashes, never making a flame. He moved with pendulum regularity, hoarding every sensation; his life flowed soundlessly like hourglass sands. Yet toward evening his money seemed to turn to a heart: when he finished his day’s business he would rub his hands; inward glee creased his face into a silent laugh. Even then he spoke in monosyllables with an inscrutable expression.

He was my neighbor in the Rue de Grès while I was a second clerk finishing my legal studies. The house was damp and dark; its gloom smothered even the eldest sons’ gaiety before they reached his door. He and his lodging were alike; the oyster resembled its rock. I was the only one he spoke to—borrowing a light or a book, sometimes permitting me to visit in the evenings. These small courtesies had grown out of four years of neighborly contact and my sober conduct. I lived nearly like him through lack of funds. No one knew if he had relatives or friends, whether he was rich or poor; I never saw money in his room—his capital was in the Bank. He collected debts himself, running all over Paris on skinny legs. Once, when gold fell from his pocket, another lodger returned it.

“That isn’t mine!” he cried, surprised. “If I were rich, would I live as I do?”

He made coffee on a cast-iron chafing dish; his dinners came from a cookshop, and the porter’s wife tidied his room faithfully. A whimsical chance named him Gobseck. Later, when I did business for him, I learned he was about seventy-six when we met—born about 1740 near Antwerp, son of a Dutch father and Jewish mother. His full name was Jean-Esther Van Gobseck. When I mentioned the murder trial of La belle Hollandaise, he said without interest, “She is my grandniece.” That was the only remark he made about the death of his sole surviving relative, Sara Van Gobseck; when I asked why his grandniece bore his name, he smiled, “Women never marry in our family.” He had never cared to trace relatives beyond four generations of the female line. The thought of heirs disgusted him; the idea of his wealth passing to others after his death was intolerable.

Sent away at ten as a cabin boy to the Dutch Straits, he spent twenty years abroad. The inscrutable lines on his sallow brow concealed adventures, panics, sudden luck, and squandered fortunes. He had known Admiral Simeuse, M. de Lally, Tippoo Sahib, and notorious pirates in St. Thomas, had hunted treasure in Buenos Ayres, and observed the American War of Independence. Yet when he spoke of the Indies or America, he did so reluctantly, as if it were indiscreet. If sociability is a religion, Gobseck was an infidel; despite my efforts, his nature remained opaque.

Was he faithful to his mother’s religion? Had he converted? Did he view Gentiles as prey? I never learned his religious opinions; he appeared indifferent rather than unbelieving.

One evening I visited him. He sat motionless in his armchair, staring at the mantel as if reading his statements. A lamp burned behind him, making his face even paler. He gestured to a chair but said nothing. What could such a being think? Did he know God, feeling, women, happiness? I felt pity for him, like pity for a diseased creature. Yet I knew that while millions were at his command, he possessed the world in idea—having explored, ransacked, appraised, and exploited it.

“Good day, Daddy Gobseck,” I said.

He turned, contracting his bushy black eyebrows in an expression as expressive as a Southern smile. “You look as gloomy as when you heard that bookseller you admired had failed, though you were one of his victims.”

“One of his victims?” he repeated.

“Yes. You refused composition at the creditors’ meeting until he promised to pay you in full. He gave you bills accepted by the bankrupt firm. When he resumed business, he paid you the dividend on those bills.”

“He was sharp, but I got it out of him.”

“Do you have bills to protest? Today is the 30th, I believe.”

It was the first time I’d mentioned money to him. He looked at me ironically, then said blandly, “I am amusing myself.”

“So you amuse yourself now and again?”

“Do you think the only poets are those who print their verses?” he replied, pityingly.

I, knowing little of his life, thought, “Poetry in that head!” Then he surprised me.

“What life can be as glorious as mine?” he asked, eyes lighting. “You are young; your visions are colored by blood. You see women’s faces in the fire; I see coals. You have beliefs; I have none. Keep your illusions if you can. I’ll show you life with the discount taken off. You can go wherever you like or stay with your wife, but eventually you settle into a groove. Happiness consists in applying your faculties to realities. Anything more is false. My principles have varied with different men and latitudes. What we admire in Europe is punishable in Asia; a vice in Paris becomes a necessity elsewhere. There are no hard-and-fast rules, only conventions adapted to climate. Throw a man into various social melting pots and convictions become meaningless. The only constant is self-interest. If you lived as long as I have, you would know the only concrete reality worth caring about is GOLD. Gold means every form of human power. I’ve traveled and found hills or plains everywhere; place is irrelevant. Manners are universal; the battle between poor and rich is inevitable. It is better to exploit than be exploited. Pleasures are the same, and when sensations are exhausted, only Vanity remains. Vanity is satisfied only by gold. Our dreams need time and means to be realized; gold contains everything in embryo; it realizes all things for us.

“Only fools find pleasure in shuffling cards for a few pence. Only dupes think they’re useful making political rules amid unpredictable events. Only simpletons delight in discussing actors and repeating their sayings. What is all this but Parisian life? Let us seek a higher outlook: happiness in strong emotions that exhaust us or in methodical occupation that makes existence clockwork. A higher happiness is noble curiosity to learn Nature’s secrets, or to imitate Nature. In two words: Science and Art, or passion and calm. Every human passion, heightened by the struggle for existence, parades before me while I live in calm. As for scientific curiosity, I replace it with insight into the springs of action in man and woman. In short, the world is mine without effort and holds no claim on me. Listen,’ he continued, ‘I will tell you about my morning, and you’ll see my pleasures.”

He rose, bolted the door, drew a tapestry across it, and sat again.

“This morning,” he said, “I had only two amounts to collect; the rest of the bills due I gave away rather than cash yesterday. So much saved—when I discount a bill I always deduct two francs for a hired brougham; it’s absurd for my clients to make me trudge all over Paris for a few francs of discount when no man is my master, and I only pay seven francs in taxes.

“The first bill for a thousand francs was presented by a dandy—spangled waistcoat, eyeglass, tilbury, English horse. The bill bore the signature of one of the prettiest women in Paris, the wife of a Count, a great landowner. How did that Countess come to sign a bill of exchange, legally worthless but practically sound? Such women, poor things, fear the scandal of a protested bill and would rather pay than fail. I wanted to know what that bill truly meant—stupidity, imprudence, love, or charity?

“The second bill, signed ‘Fanny Malvaut,’ came from a linen-draper near bankruptcy. No one with bank credit comes to me. The first step to my door means despair; the news of failure will soon break, and he has been everywhere else first. When I see the stag at bay, a pack of creditors follows. The Countess lived in the Rue du Helder, and my Fanny in the Rue Montmartre. I made conjectures as I set out. If these two women could not pay, they would have more respect than their fathers. What tricks would the Countess try for a thousand francs? She would be so nice to me, speaking in that ingratiating tone of endorsers, pouring out coaxing words, while I...’ (he turned his pale eyes on me)—‘and I would remain unmoved, inexorable! So much for hypotheses. I reached the house.

“‘Madame la Comtesse is asleep,’ said the maid.

“‘When can I see her?’

“‘At twelve o’clock.’

“‘Is the Comtesse ill?’

“‘No, sir, but she returned home at three o’clock this morning from a ball.’

“‘My name is Gobseck; tell her I’ll call again at twelve,” I said, leaving muddy footprints on the carpet. I like leaving mud on a rich man’s carpet—not spitefully, but as a reminder of Necessity. In the Rue Montmartre, I entered a poor house and looked into a dark courtyard where sunlight never reached. The porter’s lodge was grimy, the window like a shabby gown—greasy and full of holes.

“‘Mlle. Fanny Malvaut?’

“‘She’s gone out; but if you’ve come about a bill, the money is waiting with the porter’s wife.’

“‘I’ll come again,’ I said.

“As soon as I knew the porter had the money, I wanted to know what the girl was like; I pictured her as pretty. I spent the rest of the morning looking at prints along the boulevard; just as it struck twelve, I entered the Countess’s antechamber.

“‘Madame has just rung for me,’ said the maid; ‘I don’t think she can see you yet.’

“‘I’ll wait,’ I said, sitting in an easy chair.

“Venetian shutters opened and the maid returned.

“‘Come in, sir.’

“From the soft tone I knew the mistress was not ready to pay. What a woman I saw! She had thrown an Indian shawl over her bare shoulders—covering herself while revealing the outline beneath. A loose gown trimmed with ruffles hinted that her laundress’s bills amounted to some two thousand francs a year. Dark curls escaped a bright handkerchief knotted carelessly on her head. The bed lay disorderly, suggesting broken slumber. A painter would have paid to see the scene: a crushed pillow in an eiderdown quilt, satin slippers on a bearskin rug, a crumpled gown, twisted stockings, ribbon garters, a half-open fan on the chimney-piece. Drawers stood open; flowers, diamonds, gloves, a bouquet, a girdle were scattered about. A vague sweet perfume filled the room. Beneath luxury and disorder, I saw Misery lurking—she felt those fangs. Her tired face epitomized the room strewn with relics of past revelry. The scattered gewgaws, pitiable this morning, had turned heads the night before.

“What efforts to drink from the Tantalus cup of bliss I read in these traces of love struck by remorse—in this visible account of a life of luxury and riot. There were faint red marks on her young face, signs of fine skin; her features were slightly hardened, and dark circles ringed her eyes. Yet Nature was vigorous; these traces of folly did not mar her beauty. Her eyes glittered. She resembled a da Vinci Herodias: magnificently alive and energetic; nothing in her was starved. She awakened desire; it seemed a passion stronger than love burned in her. I was taken with her. It had been long since my heart had throbbed; I was paid then and there—for I would give a thousand francs for a sensation that brought back youth’s memory.

“‘Monsieur,’ she said, finding a chair for me, ‘will you be so good as to wait?’

“‘Until this time tomorrow, madame,’ I replied, folding the bill. ‘I cannot legally protest this bill any sooner.’ Inwardly I thought, ‘Pay for your luxury, your name, your comfort, your monopoly! The rich have invented judges and courts to secure their goods; the guillotine gapes under silk, and remorse grinds teeth beneath smiles; the fantastical lions’ jaws await to sink into your heart.’

“‘Protest the bill! Can you mean it?’ she cried, eyes on me. ‘Could you have so little consideration for me?’

“‘If the King himself owed me money and did not pay, I would summon him even sooner than any other debtor.’

“While we spoke, someone tapped softly at the door.

“‘I cannot see anyone,’ she cried imperiously.

“‘But, Anastasie, I particularly wish to speak to you.’

“‘Not just now, dear,’ she answered softer, but without relenting.

“‘What nonsense! You are talking to someone,’ said the voice, and in came a man who could be only the Count.

“The Countess glanced at me. I saw how it was; she was utterly in my power. There was a time when I might have been foolish enough not to protest. At Pondicherry in 1763, I once let a woman off and she repaid me. I deserved it; why trust her?

“‘What does this gentleman want?’ asked the Count.

“I could see the Countess trembling; the white satin of her throat stood rough with gooseflesh. I laughed inwardly without moving a muscle.

“‘This gentleman is one of my tradesmen,’ she said.

“The Count turned his back; I drew the bill halfway from my pocket. After that inexorable movement, she came over and placed a diamond in my hand. ‘Take it,’ she said, ‘and be gone.’

“We exchanged values, and I bowed and left. The diamond was worth twelve hundred francs to me. Outside, a swarm of flunkeys brushed their liveries, polishing sumptuous equipages.

“‘This is what brings these people to me!’ I thought. ‘They steal millions with all due formalities to maintain this lifestyle. The great lord and the little man who apes him bathe in mud to save a splash or two when walking through the streets.’

“At that moment the great gates opened and a cabriolet admitted the young fellow who had brought the bill.

“‘Sir,’ I said as he alighted, ‘here are two hundred francs, which I beg you to return to Mme. la Comtesse, and please tell her I hold the pledge she deposited with me this morning for a week.’

“He took the two hundred francs and smiled ironically, as if to say, ‘Aha! So she’s paid, has she? … Faith, so much the better!’ I read the Countess’s future in his face. That fair, handsome young man is a heartless gambler; he will ruin himself, her, her husband, and the children—devouring their portions and wreaking more havoc in Parisian salons than a battery of howitzers.

“I returned to see Mlle. Fanny in the Rue Montmartre, climbed a steep staircase, and reached a neat two-room dwelling on the fifth floor. Everything was as tidy as a new ducat. In the first room Mlle. Fanny sat—neatly dressed, with a delicate fresh face and a winning look. Her neatly brushed chestnut hair formed a double curve on her forehead, lending refinement to her clear blue eyes. Daylight through short curtains softened her girlish face. A pile of shaped linen showed she was a sempstress. She looked like a spirit of solitude. When I offered the bill, she said the porter’s wife had the money. I pretended not to understand.

“‘You go out early, mademoiselle, it seems.’

“‘I very seldom leave my room; but when you work all night, you must bathe sometimes.’

“I looked at her. A glance revealed a life of toil and misfortune—honest farmer folk origins, a freckle or two, an air of goodness. There was an indefinable sincerity about her; it was refreshing. Poor child, she had faith in something; a crucifix and a sprig of green box adorned her little painted bedstead. I felt inclined to charge no more than twelve percent, to help her establish a good business.

“But perhaps she had a cousin who would raise money on her signature and sponge on her,’ I thought.

“So I restrained my generosity; benevolence, when unchecked, often ruins those who take it. I have often thought Fanny Malvaut would make a nice wife; I compared her pure, lonely life with the Countess’s—already sunk to a bill of exchange; she will sink further before she is done!

“Well,’ he said, ‘do you think it nothing to have this power to see into the deepest recesses of the human heart, to embrace many lives, to see the naked truth? There are no two dramas alike: hideous sores, deadly griefs, love scenes, misery soon lying under the Seine, young men’s joys leading to the scaffold, laughter of despair, and sumptuous banquets. Yesterday was a tragedy: a father drowned himself because he could not support his family. Tomorrow will be a comedy: some young man will attempt a theatrical scene. You have heard people praise eloquent preachers; I have wasted time on them; they might change opinions but not conduct. These priests and your Mirabeaus and Vergniauds are mere stammerers compared to my orators.

“Often a girl in love, a gray-headed merchant on the verge of bankruptcy, a mother concealing a son’s wrongdoing, a starving artist, or a great man whose influence wanes comes to me. Their pleading makes me shudder. Sublime actors play for me, for an audience of one, and they cannot deceive me. I read their inmost thoughts as God reads them. Nothing is hidden from me. The holder of the purse-strings can loose and bind. I am rich enough to buy the consciences of those who control ministers, from office boys to mistresses. Is that not power? I can possess the fairest women, receive soft caresses; is that not Pleasure? Your whole social economy is summed up in Power and Pleasure.

“There are ten of us in Paris—silent, unknown kings, arbiters of destinies. What is life but a machine set in motion by money? Methods are confounded with results; you cannot separate soul from senses, spirit from matter. Gold is the spiritual basis of society. We ten meet at the Café Thémis near the Pont Neuf; we reveal finance’s mysteries. No fortune deceives us; we possess family secrets in all directions. We keep a Black Book noting important bills, drafts on public credit, or bank instruments. We are the casuists of the Paris Bourse, an Inquisition weighing every man of fortune’s actions; our forecasts are infallible. One of us watches the judicial world, another the financial, another the administrative, another commerce. I keep an eye on eldest sons, artists, high society, and gamblers—on Paris’s most sensational side. Everyone who comes reveals neighbors’ secrets. Thwarted passion and mortified vanity are great babblers. Vice, disappointment, and vindictiveness are the best detectives. My colleagues and I have had all things, are sated, and love power and money for their own sake.

“Here,” he said, indicating his bare, chilly room, “the most high-mettled gallant who would draw a sword elsewhere will entreat with clasped hands. No city merchant is so proud, no woman so vain, no soldier so bold, but they all entreat me here, with tears of rage or anguish. Here they kneel—the famous artist, the man of letters whose name may go down to posterity. In short, all inheritances and concerns of Paris are weighed in the balance. Do you still think there are no delights behind that blank mask which has so often amazed you?” he asked, stretching out his livid face that reeked of money.

I returned to my room feeling stunned. The little wizened old man had grown great. He had become a strange visionary symbol—the power of gold personified. I shrank from life and my kind.

Is it really so? must everything be resolved into gold? It was long before I slept that night. I saw heaps of gold surrounding me. My thoughts were filled with the lovely Countess; I confess, to my shame, that she eclipsed another quiet, innocent figure—the woman who toiled in obscurity. But through the clouds of slumber the next morning Fanny’s sweet face rose before me in all its beauty, and I thought of nothing else.”

“Will you take a glass of eau sucrée?” the Vicomtesse offered, interrupting Derville.

“I should be glad of it.”

“But I see nothing here that concerns us,” Mme. de Grandlieu said, ringing the bell.

“Sardanapalus!” cried Derville. “Mademoiselle Camille’s happiness once depended on Daddy Gobseck; but with his death at ninety, M. de Restaud will soon inherit a fortune. This needs explanation. As for poor Fanny Malvaut, you know her; she is my wife.”

“Poor fellow, he would admit that, with his usual frankness, in front of a crowd!” said the Vicomtesse.

“I would proclaim it to the universe,” said the attorney.

“I left you in the Rue du Helder,” the uncle murmured, waking. “You had gone to see a Countess; what became of her?”

Derville resumed. After speaking with the miser Gobseck, he finished his law studies, became a licentiate, then an advocate. Gobseck’s opinion of him improved and the old man began consulting him gratis on delicate matters. Derville rose from clerk to head-clerk, moved into his employer’s house, and eventually earned 150 francs a month. When Gobseck’s peculiar attention persisted, Derville went to bid him farewell; Gobseck showed no sentiment, only a look that suggested second sight. Even after they drifted, Gobseck continued to consult him.

When Derville’s employer, a pleasure-loving man, fell into financial trouble and sold his practice for 150,000 francs, Derville—seventh child of a small tradesman from Noyon—had neither money nor connections to buy it. Rather than beg, he faced Gobseck boldly. The miser, now occupying Derville’s old room and watching callers through a grated window, guessed his purpose.

Derville proposed buying the practice: it yielded about 20,000 francs a year and might reach 40,000 under his management; with a ten-year plan he could pay 150,000 if Gobseck lent the sum. Gobseck assessed him—no guarantees, age just under twenty-five—then agreed to think it over. The next morning he consented on hard terms: for acquaintances he would take twelve and a half—then corrected himself—thirteen percent interest. He promised to pay the seller directly to secure the lien and demanded that Derville give bills for the amount—fifteen bills of ten thousand francs each—payable to a third party left unnamed. Derville puzzled over the need for his birth certificate; Gobseck, with a malicious smile, explained that youth’s integrity and brains are commodities one can mortgage—after thirty a man’s value becomes uncertain.

Further conditions followed: Derville would give him legal advice without fees for life so long as it cost no outlay; he would let Gobseck visit him, not live ostentatiously, and see him at dinner on Wednesdays and Saturdays after the “Change” at five. Gobseck would recommend him, send him business, and teach him “to know men, and what is more—women!” The old man delighted in investing capital in Derville’s head and watching it prosper.

Derville soon became an attorney, took on and won the suit to recover Mme. de Grandlieu’s estates, and his name spread. Despite the usurer’s enormous interest, he repaid the loan in under five years. He married Fanny Malvaut, whom he loved; through hard work, shared fortune, and a timely inheritance—one of her uncles left her seventy thousand francs—they cleared the debt. Derville summarized his happiness with modesty and returned to the company.

About a year after buying the practice, he was drawn into a bachelor breakfast lost as the forfeit of a bet. Among the guests was M. de Trailles, the consummate dandy: graceful in clothing and tandem, a master of taste, gambler and gourmand of society’s pleasures, spending perhaps a hundred thousand francs a year with no visible capital. Brilliant but frivolous, Trailles could charm or ruin; he mostly skimmed life’s surface.

Derville had tried to avoid Trailles, but his friend Goriot insisted. A bachelor breakfast, Derville explained, begins as a display of sumptuousness that dissolves into a chaotic orgy: neatness and refinement give way to broken glasses, ruined linen, loud jesting, songs, quarrels, smashed bottles, and a cacophony of voices—an exhausted Bacchanal. Amid this pandemonium Trailles, cool and calculating beneath an affected drunken air, engaged Derville and by evening secured his promise to introduce him to Papa Gobseck. Trailles’s golden tongue mixed words of honor, virtue, and urgent misfortune; he painted a young woman’s reputation and marriage at stake unless fifty thousand francs could be procured that morning.

The next day, uneasy but bound by his word, Derville accompanied Trailles to Gobseck. Trailles, outwardly courtly, was inwardly agitated; on the Rue de Grès he grew nervous, glancing about with sweat on his brow. At Gobseck’s, Derville vouched for him; Gobseck said he had no money except for his own clients’ business. Trailles sought to persuade him by boasting of fashionable partners and foreign revenues, quipping that spendthrifts and usurers were interdependent. Gobseck answered coldly: other creditors—Girard, Palma, Werbrust, Gigonnet—were full of Trailles’s paper and offered it at heavy losses; he would not lend a stiver to a man who owed thirty thousand francs and had nothing.

A cab arrived; Trailles left and returned shortly with a woman on his arm—one of Goriot’s daughters, the Countess. Derville had been watching unseen at the window; he saw the Countess enter Gobseck’s damp room, distressed beneath proud features. She shuddered at the instant she recognized Derville, and he felt pity for her despite her social brilliance and moral failings. Trailles had become her evil genius, ruling her by vanity and other strings.

The Countess asked whether it was possible to obtain the value of her diamonds while retaining the right of repurchase, and handed over a jewel-case. Derville stepped forward and explained that this would be a sale with faculty of redemption—a formal transfer with a stipulated term allowing the previous owner to recover title by paying a set sum. The Countess breathed freer; the Count looked black, fearing Gobseck would undervalue the stones after their fall in price.

Gobseck, impassive, took his magnifying glass and inspected the jewels. Derville never forgot the look that came over the old man—an unnatural glitter in his eyes as he rose and went to the light, holding the diamonds close to his toothless mouth, as if to devour them. He handled bracelets, sprays, necklaces, and tiaras like a child or a doting miser, mumbling, letting the light draw out every fire. That flash of greed, the only time Gobseck’s heart seemed to open, shocked Derville and left a lasting impression.

Earlier, when Trailles first argued his case, Gobseck had spoken with irony and calculation. He mocked the ruin of a man who seemed to have nothing, observed that Trailles’s bills were being circulated at losses, and made plain he wouldn’t be fooled. Yet when Trailles returned with the Countess and her diamonds, Gobseck’s mood changed; he rose in triumph, stretching out his arms to Derville and calling the arrival “Life” itself, as if the security had saved him. He admitted he had been guarded, unwilling to trust the elegant scoundrel alone. But now, having the jewels, he felt exultant—his ecstasy frightful in its intensity.

Gobseck then told Derville to stay; he rejoiced at the prospect of outwitting rivals Werbrust and Gigonnet and anticipated laughing at them that night. He sat again before his bureau, face reverting to the pale, impassive mask that made him resemble a statue. Yet the sight of the Countess—beautiful, suffering, and controlled by Trailles—had registered in him hardly beyond the value of her jewels. Derville reflected that Trailles had become the Countess’s tormentor: an angel-faced youth who, by charm and calculation, governed her life and misled her into degrading necessity.

Mme. de Grandlieu lamented how the Countess’s virtues had been subverted—how Trailles turned devotion into a weapon and made her pay for pleasures. Derville felt no urge to weep for her; he shuddered at the sight of her murderer, the glittering youth whose angelic look masked moral ruin. In Gobseck’s room they stood before the old man as if before a judge; Gobseck peered at them like an inquisitor, measuring guilt and bargaining worth.

The Countess’s plea and Gobseck’s examination revealed the truth Derville had learned: Gobseck’s judgments were never sentimental—people were valued for what they could secure and what they promised. The bargain Gobseck struck with Derville—interest, anonymous bills, visits, unpaid counsel—underscored a system in which personal ties and youthful promise were commodified, and financial life depended as much on shrewd calculation and social maneuver as on law or sentiment.

That evening, Gobseck’s slight intoxication of triumph at possessing the diamonds contrasted with his usual reserve. He had no tenderness for the Countess beyond the stones she offered; his joy at outwitting other lenders and at the glitter held to his hungry gaze revealed the fusion of avarice and childish delight in him. For Derville, who had benefited from Gobseck’s money but never from his heart, the scene was emblematic: the usurer’s power came not only from capital but from a cold, almost scientific knowledge of men’s weaknesses—knowledge he wielded to acquire dominion, and to profit emotionally as well as financially from the commodities people brought him, whether practices, promises, or jewels.

“Fine stones! This set would have fetched three hundred thousand francs before the Revolution. What water! Genuine Asiatic diamonds from Golconda or Visapur. Do you know their worth? No one in Paris but Gobseck can appreciate them. In the Empire, such a set would have cost another two hundred thousand francs!”

Gobseck’s eyes shone as he handled the case, alternating rapture and calculation. The Count wanted a loan of one hundred thousand francs for three years, offering the diamonds as security. The Countess asserted the jewels were hers, but I pointed out that, as a married woman, she could not legally alienate property without her husband’s consent; a deed of purchase would be void. Gobseck changed tack—possession being nearly as good as title—and offered eighty thousand francs if they left the diamonds with him.

Pride flared in the Count; the Countess seemed stupefied. Maxime, her lover, blustered; Gobseck, unmoved, produced a cheque for fifty thousand francs on the Bank of France and thirty thousand in protested drafts, even two pistols to mark his implacable temper. Cornered, they accepted. Gobseck exulted at obtaining such stones “tolerably cheaply”; his ferocious delight in possession chilled me.

Soon after, the Count burst into Gobseck’s room, furious that his family diamonds had been bought. Gobseck replied coldly: if Mme. la Comtesse could sign bills, she could engage in business; whether they were the Count’s could not be proven without bringing the wife into court and exposing her. He mocked the Count’s authority. Our dispute turned to law: equity perhaps, legally no. I advised the Count to settle—pay for the diamonds or secure a redemption clause—since litigation would be ruinous and humiliating, and the buyer could claim good faith. With my help the Count signed an acknowledgment of receipt of eighty-five thousand francs, including interest, in consideration of Gobseck’s promise to return the stones.

Days later the Count, agitated and fearful, asked me to draw papers to transfer his estate to Gobseck as a simulated sale, Gobseck to hold it in trust and later restore it to the eldest son. He feared his son’s favoritism for his mother and distrusted the boy’s future loyalty. I warned that such a counter-deed risked disinheriting other children; he wept and agreed to provide for them in the instrument. It was a desperate precaution.

The deed was signed but not delivered to me. Months later, Gobseck, dining at my house, hinted the Count was dying and that he and I were the only ones trusted by him. I resolved to visit; fearing for the counter-deed, I sued the Count for a sum ostensibly due to Gobseck so that, on the man’s death, I could attach the estate and secure the deed; I bribed a servant to tell me when the Count was gravely ill. My plan was to intervene quickly, threaten to affix seals, and secure the document before the Countess could act. Money governed these manoeuvres at the bedside.

For two months the Count lay prostrate, living in squalor he seemed to prefer. He hated daylight, rejected nurses, and reveled in discomfort. One morning he summoned his son Ernest and, in a lucid moment, entrusted the boy with a sealed packet addressed to me, begging him to carry it to the post and to keep the secret even from his mother. Ernest promised.

When Ernest returned, the Countess drew him to her knee and pressed him until he refused to repeat what his father had said. Furious, the Count confronted her. The scene was brutal: he accused her of filling his life with trouble and trying to corrupt their son; she begged for mercy and vowed to expiate her sins if the children could be spared. The Count, exhausted of affection, called her a bad wife and mother; she fainted. Priests administered the sacraments. At midnight he died.

Gobseck and I arrived soon after. The house was in chaos. In the death-chamber and adjoining salon the Countess had been ransacking drawers and trunks; her search had been violent—mattress overturned, drawers emptied, papers and furniture strewn. A paper bearing the Count’s arms lay at her feet; in panic she had thrust some documents into the grate and was burning them when we entered. I snatched an addressed paper from the hearth: it was to me. Confronted, the Countess was near collapse, torn between shame and fear. Gobseck, cold and triumphant, reminded her—without mercy—that he was the legal owner of what her husband had sold. He asked if I would judge him; I could not deny that under the law he had rights. I left her beside her husband’s corpse while Gobseck declared himself trustee of the estate.

From then on Gobseck managed the property with the hard efficiency of a usurer turned landowner—leasing the mansion, spending summers on the estates, repairing mills, planting timber. He became, in all but name, lord of the manor, showing no softness. When I suggested he help young Ernest, he scoffed: adversity is the best teacher; let the youth fend for himself. My attempts to move him to honor the Count’s intentions and provide for the children met the old man’s icy thrift.

Time passed. Gobseck grew feebler but retained his iron will. He kept his rooms exactly as always and received clients even from his bed. During financial reorganizations after France recognized the Haitian Republic, his knowledge of claims and fortunes made him influential; he devised agencies, took slices of large transactions, and accepted gifts—plates, snuff-boxes, game—without ever parting cash. He was as thin as ever, his avarice concentrated to a point.

Then Gobseck’s death came. Summoned to his bedside by his old porter, I found him kneeling by the grate, muttering bequests and naming people—half delirious, half lucid. He died with a final enumeration of riches. At the hearth, amid cinders, I dug and found metal: coins, receipts, tucked papers—proof of the vast hoard he had amassed. I ordered the justice of the peace to seal everything at once.

The rooms revealed the scale and eccentricity of his thrift: one chamber reeked of putrid pies and moldy fish among chests of tea and coffee; others were packed with furniture, silver-plate, vases, pictures, books, and curios—many likely presents or pledged goods. Jewel-cases bore ciphers and coats of arms; fine linen and weapons lay in confusion. Every item had been argued over, bartered, haggled for. Some bargains remained unsettled; dealers who had expected presents or returns balked at Gobseck’s stingy terms. The stench and clutter bespoke a miser’s final degradation.

There was talk of Gobseck’s intended bequests: he had given an address for a supposed heiress, a woman called “La Torpille” in the Rue Vivienne; I foresaw the absurdity of pouring an immense fortune into such a character’s hands. But more immediate was the question of the Restaud estate: the Count’s counter-deed, the burned fragments, the tormented Countess, and young Ernest’s uncertain inheritance. Despite Gobseck’s harshness, Ernest would, in time, inherit a fortune sufficient to marry Mlle. Camille and to provide for his mother and siblings—assuming the legal knots could be untangled and the provisions enforced.

Thus closed the arc of transactions, betrayal, and legal scrimmage: Gobseck’s cold mastery of money had altered an aristocratic family’s fate; the Count’s plans had been thwarted by rage and sickness; the Countess’s frantic attempts to save her children had degenerated into crime; and a miser’s death revealed treasures amid squalor. The outcome promised both restitution and new trials, as the rightful heirs would have to navigate tangled transfers and the old usurer’s secreted wealth.
