In Alencon, Chevalier de Valois, a 58-year-old gentleman claiming to be 50, lives modestly while dreaming of marrying a wealthy old maid to elevate his status. One spring morning, Suzanne, a young girl working for Madame Lardot, seeks to escape provincial life for Paris. The chevalier encourages her to pursue local bachelor Monsieur du Bousquier, revealing her boldness and contrasting with his genteel demeanor.

Meanwhile, Madame Granson struggles to support her son Athanase, who feels stifled in his job and is in love with his wealthy cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon. His feelings evolve from financial interest to genuine affection, complicated by societal expectations and rivals. At a dinner hosted by Cormon, tensions rise as Madame Granson tries to leverage Suzanne's situation against du Bousquier, while Athanase hopes to confess his feelings. The chapter highlights the complexities of love, ambition, and societal pressures, particularly for women like Cormon, who fear being valued only for their wealth.

To understand Mademoiselle Cormon's household, we must consider her and the Abbe de Sponde alongside their loyal servants: Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette. 

Jacquelin, a stout forty-year-old who had served for twenty-two years, performed various tasks and loved Josette, a thirty-year-old maid. They hoped Mademoiselle’s marriage would allow them to wed. Josette, practical and influential, and Mariette, the skilled cook, contributed to the household's dynamics. 

Mademoiselle Cormon, childless and single, projected her maternal instincts onto Penelope, the family mare, which became a source of conversation and care. However, her fixed ideas made her increasingly difficult to please, leading to petty quarrels that provided her with a sense of purpose.

Her life revolved around her household, health, and religious duties, often consulting doctors about minor ailments, which sparked amusing exchanges with her servants. Each season, she visited her estate at Prebaudet, tending to her apple trees and preparing for harvest, while townsfolk speculated about her marital status and her relationship with Monsieur du Bousquier, a rejected suitor.

During dinner parties, Mademoiselle Cormon hoped to attract a suitable husband but struggled with meaningful conversation, relying on the Chevalier de Valois for humor. Despite her lack of depth, she was kind-hearted, though often ridiculed. Conversations often turned to du Bousquier, and Mademoiselle Cormon's attempts to defend him revealed her naivety, sparking further gossip.

As she contemplated her future, particularly her feelings for Athanase, a young man connected to her, her thoughts were interrupted by du Bousquier's arrival. The next day, a letter from her uncle prompted her to return to town, as Monsieur de Troisville, a Russian officer, sought hospitality. Mademoiselle Cormon, filled with hope, envisioned herself as the Vicomtesse de Troisville.

When Monsieur de Troisville arrived, he was charming but revealed he had been married for sixteen years, causing Mademoiselle Cormon to faint. This incident fueled gossip throughout Alencon. As she recovered, her hopes for marriage were dashed, and she ultimately accepted du Bousquier's proposal to regain her dignity and silence the town's chatter.

Athanase Granson, who had long hoped for Mademoiselle Cormon, was devastated by the news of her engagement. He withdrew into despair, seeking solace in the Sarthe region. Rumors about the marriage circulated, marked by ominous signs, and the couple retreated to Prebaudet, where du Bousquier began modernizing the estate, alarming townspeople.

Athanase's despair deepened, leading him to contemplate leaving Alencon. After a heartfelt conversation with his mother, he took his own life, believing it would spare her pain. Madame Granson received the news with disbelief, later learning of his love for Mademoiselle Cormon, while the community expressed regret for not supporting him.

In the aftermath, Madame du Bousquier returned to Alencon, where she encountered Madame Granson, sharing a moment of silent understanding. Du Bousquier's marriage revealed its darker side, as he exerted control over his wife, diminishing her autonomy. 

Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage had significant repercussions, altering the political landscape and dividing the town. Du Bousquier leveraged his wealth to reshape Alencon, but his personal life remained fraught with tension. Ultimately, the story illustrates the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations, revealing how personal tragedies intertwine with broader social dynamics.
