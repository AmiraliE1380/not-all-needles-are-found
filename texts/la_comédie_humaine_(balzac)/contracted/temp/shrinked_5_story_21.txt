Railroads threaten to make certain transportation services around Paris obsolete. The Touchards, a father-and-son team, once dominated travel within a 45-mile radius but faced competition from local services, particularly the coucous, which excelled on short routes. In 1822 they contended with three rivals on the lucrative Paris–Beaumont-sur-Oise run, despite extending service to Chambly.

The Isle-Adam route had less rivalry, with only two coaches running smoothly. Pierrotin, a popular coachman, and his rival coordinated schedules to serve patrons of the Lion d’Argent inn, where the innkeeper’s wife handled bookings.

One Saturday morning in 1822 Pierrotin waited outside the inn. He had ordered a new coach to expand his business but was financially strained, needing a thousand francs for a more luxurious vehicle. A footman then sought a discreet ride for his master, the Comte de Serizy, who wished to travel incognito. Hoping for a generous tip, Pierrotin agreed, recalling the count’s steward, Monsieur Moreau, a respected figure who often employed him. This episode introduces themes of ambition, social dynamics, and the changing transportation landscape in early 19th-century France, focusing on Pierrotin’s struggles and hopes.

Monsieur Huguet de Serizy descends from a notable family tracing back to President Huguet, ennobled under François I. The current count became a State councillor at twenty-two and distinguished himself during the Revolution by remaining on his estate, shielded by his father’s reputation. After his father’s death in 1794 he rose under Napoleon, who appointed him to the Council of State and made him a count and senator.

In 1806 he married the widow of a Republican general, significantly increasing his wealth. By 1814 failing health forced him to resign and return to Paris, where he remained loyal to the Bourbons. After Napoleon’s second fall he resumed political duties, becoming vice-president of the Council of State and managing foreign claims without seeking glory. His life was marked by hard work and numerous honors.

Despite public success, the count suffered from unfulfilled love for his wife, who enjoyed considerable freedom. He respected her autonomy while staying devoted, even as his health declined. His dedication to work masked emotional pain born of jealousy and the complexities of their marriage.

The narrative shifts to a pressing matter: a farm encroaching on the count’s estate, Presles. The lease held by farmer Leger was expiring, and owner Margueron refused renewal. The count sought to buy the farm to end disputes and consulted his notary and lawyer, who advised inviting Margueron to dinner to finalize the sale.

Before the meeting the count began renovating Presles to surprise his wife but chose to travel incognito. Moreau, Presles’s steward, had a complex background: initially loyal, he later accepted bribes and engaged in dishonest dealings aimed at profiting from the farm sale.

As the count prepared to leave, Moreau conspired with Leger to inflate the farm’s price. Meanwhile retired officer Monsieur de Reybert and his wife sought to expose Moreau’s dishonesty. Madame de Reybert approached the count, claiming Moreau schemed to defraud him. Skeptical at first, the count began to doubt Moreau after reading a contradictory letter from the steward.

Leaving the Café de l’Echiquier, Pierrotin spotted Madame Clapart and her son Oscar at the Lion d’Argent. Pierrotin often transported goods for Moreau, who had a discreet arrangement with Madame Clapart. Despite hardship, her unwavering love for Oscar blinded her to his faults.

Preparing for the journey, Madame Clapart fussed over Oscar, urging him to be respectful. Embarrassed by her attentiveness, Oscar longed for independence. Eventually the coach filled with passengers, including the stout farmer Père Leger and the Comte de Serizy, who arrived last disguised as a bourgeois. As the journey began, group dynamics shifted: Oscar grappled with inadequacy while others engaged in light-hearted exchanges.

In public transport, passengers often begin in silence, watching each other until conversation starts, usually led by the most talkative. This happens in French stage-coaches, known for lively banter. Georges, a clever young man, sized up his fellow travelers, felt superior, and planned to amuse himself.

As the coach descended from La Chapelle, Georges decided to pose as a disguised Russian prince, bantering with Mistigris and spinning tales of Eastern adventures. He embellished his past with stories of battles and claimed to be the grandson of Czerni-Georges, a figure who fought the Ottomans.

At Saint-Denis they stopped at a tavern where Georges relished the attention. The count, intrigued but skeptical, suspected Georges was an impostor and discovered a portfolio identifying him as Crottat’s second clerk.

Resuming the journey, Georges kept up his military yarns, asserting key roles in battles. The count warned him about the dangers of boasting, but Georges persisted, entertaining the group with stories of a beautiful woman named Zena.

The trip became a stage for identity and storytelling. At Saint-Brice, Georges impressed Oscar but provoked jealousy by lighting a cigar. The innkeeper and Père Léger discussed a land deal involving the Comte de Serizy, hinting at a conspiracy that caught the count’s attention. As they drove off, Georges and a farmer admired the landscape, while Oscar boasted about his diplomatic future and drew laughter. Conversation turned to the countess, the count defended her honor, then left the coach, leaving the young men puzzled.

Oscar, shy and withdrawn, hid while Monsieur Moreau, in a blue overcoat, greeted him and introduced him to Mistigris and the painters sent by architect Monsieur Grindot. Moreau invited them to dinner, mentioned hunting opportunities, and sent his son Jacques to tell Madame Moreau of Oscar's arrival. He urged Oscar to learn riding and hunting despite Oscar’s reluctance, but said Oscar’s stay would be brief because Madame Moreau was present.

The steward’s house, attached to the château, contained a salon and a boudoir for Madame Moreau, who liked to assert her elevated status as a former maid. Tensions simmered between the Moreaus and the Reyberts over reminders of Madame Moreau’s past. The Moreaus cultivated ties with the local bourgeoisie, hosting gatherings and card games with the rector and mayor.

Madame Moreau eagerly awaited the painters to impress them, but was disappointed when only Mistigris’s packages arrived. When the artists finally came, she tried to display her art knowledge though her past sometimes showed. Just as the atmosphere relaxed, a maid announced the count’s sudden arrival, which shocked Madame Moreau.

The count confronted Moreau about gossip overheard during Oscar’s journey, and Moreau was dismissed, allowed to keep his earnings. As the count prepared for dinner, tension rose with the arrival of guests. Mistigris made light remarks while clerk Georges Marest got into trouble for antics.

Distraught at losing his position, Moreau accused Oscar of causing the scandal and demanded an apology to the count. Overwhelmed, Oscar resisted, prompting a confrontation during which Moreau forced him to apologize. The count dismissed Oscar, leaving Moreau to contemplate the evening’s fallout.

Preparing to leave, Moreau wrote to Madame Clapart, blaming Oscar’s indiscretion for his dismissal as steward. He resolved to send Oscar home, feeling the weight of his family’s future and his own ruined prospects. He lamented Oscar’s behavior and wished the boy had received better guidance.

That evening, Madame Clapart was knitting when her husband, Clapart, fretted about friend Poiret perhaps visiting. Clapart condemned Oscar, predicting trouble at Presles, while Madame Clapart defended their son. A carriage then arrived with Oscar; Brochon informed them of Moreau’s dismissal. Distressed, Madame Clapart fainted on reading Moreau’s letter. When she revived she told Oscar to go to bed as Clapart continued to berate him.

Despite the turmoil, Oscar slept soundly, unaware of the gravity of his situation. The next day, hungry, he was surprised life went on. His mother explained the dire consequences of his actions and the need for a career, outlining limited options: commerce, civil service, or the military. She warned of financial instability and urged him to enlist if necessary.

Oscar, naive and slow to grasp reality, listened as his mother wept and implored him to be more discreet. They considered seeking help from Uncle Cardot, who might assist.

Uncle Cardot, a wealthy widower, received them. Madame Clapart emphasized Oscar’s past promise, hoping to secure support. Cardot, polite but skeptical, agreed to help if Oscar showed potential. He offered to fund legal education on condition that Oscar worked hard and kept his integrity, advising diligence and steady improvement.

After their visit, Madame Clapart felt hopeful. Moreau later reassured her that Oscar could still succeed if disciplined, proposing that Oscar work for a young lawyer, Desroches, who would provide structure. Madame Clapart was grateful and urged Oscar to thank Moreau.

Ten days later, Oscar was presented to Maître Desroches, a young solicitor who took an interest. Desroches told his head clerk, Monsieur Godeschal, that Oscar needed strict supervision to prepare for law school. Godeschal, himself from humble origins, was assigned to guide Oscar, stressing hard work and integrity.

For two years Oscar led a monk-like life, rising early to work alongside Godeschal, running errands, and preparing for exams. His routine was austere: simple meals, long hours, and study until nightfall. He occasionally visited Uncle Cardot and spent Sundays with his mother, but was often chastised for any attempt at elegance.

Godeschal counseled frugality and modest dress. Eighteen months in, Oscar became the third clerk, unpaid but provided room and board. By the end of his second year he’d acquired substantial legal knowledge and performed well at the Palais.

Despite progress, Oscar felt constrained, envying well-dressed young men and longing for freedom. Godeschal sometimes took him out for diversion to relieve the monotony.

In November 1825, after passing exams, Oscar received funds from Moreau to improve his wardrobe and was promoted to second clerk with a salary. Yet his internal struggle persisted: he often contemplated abandoning the rigorous life. Godeschal kept watch to prevent temptations. Shortly after Oscar’s promotion, a new clerk, Frédéric Marest, arrived to learn the practice of law.

The next day Georges Marest, now head clerk to notary Hannequin, invited Oscar and the other clerks to a lavish gathering at the Rocher de Cancale, promising elegance. Desroches charged Oscar with retrieving a crucial legal document and warned that Derville might interfere. Eager, Oscar accepted.

After Georges left, Godeschal asked about the marquise; Frédéric joked she was a wealthy widow from Mexico. The clerks, keen to mix with high society, prepared for the event. The “marquise” was actually Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, a successful danseuse supported by her benefactor, Père Cardot. Florentine had risen in status and was courting Georges, who had newly inherited a fortune.

At the Rocher de Cancale Oscar met various guests—an old captain, a journalist—and found Georges charming and conciliatory about past slights. The feast produced heavy drinking among the clerks. Later at Florentine’s salon Oscar was struck by her transformation into a sophisticated beauty. He lost heavily at vingt-et-un, including the five hundred francs Desroches had entrusted to him, and despaired. Florentine offered him a thousand francs to recover, but he gambled and lost even more.

By morning Oscar slept on a sofa amid continuing festivities. He was roused by his benefactor Cardot, who scolded him for the night’s behavior. Tensions rose when Florentine revealed the extent of his losses; Cardot chastised him and, reluctantly, lent him five hundred francs—warning it would be the last time.

Desroches, furious at Oscar’s irresponsibility, resolved to dismiss him. At home, Madame Clapart learned of her son’s disgrace and was devastated. When Oscar asserted his independence, he deepened her sorrow.

Determined to change, Oscar drew a military number and enlisted in the cavalry. His fortunes improved after he was assigned to a noble regiment through the Comte de Serizy. Oscar distinguished himself in military service, earning honors and a solid reputation. Years later he encountered Georges, now in decline, a contrast to his own advancement. Oscar eventually married Georgette Pierrotin, matured into a respectable man, and secured a fulfilling civil career as a collector and later a receiver-general.
