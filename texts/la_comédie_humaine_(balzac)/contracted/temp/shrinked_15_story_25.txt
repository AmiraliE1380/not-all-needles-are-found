Early in the autumn of 1826 the Abbé Birotteau, the chief figure of this tale, was caught in a downpour as he returned from an evening at a friend's. He hurried, as his corpulence and gout allowed, across the deserted square called the Cloister behind the chancel of Saint-Gatien at Tours. Small, apoplectic and about sixty, he loathed having his shoes soaked because damp always brought gout. Yet he bore the rain resignedly: he had won three francs ten sous at rubber with Madame de Listomère, had wrapped himself in a canon’s fur cape, and was elated by talk that he might be named canon—a post he had long coveted.

The Cloister contained, on the Grand’Rue side, a cluster of cathedral houses forming a Close that had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette. Opposite, a single house, its walls overshadowed by Saint-Gatien’s buttresses, had always been occupied by abbés and belonged to an old maid, Mademoiselle Gamard. Time had covered the cathedral in dingy mantles, moss and lichens; the house too lay in shadow, a solitude only fit for those of absolute nullity or uncommon fortitude. Mademoiselle Gamard, devout and a careful housekeeper, took priests to board and kept the dwelling—bought by her father in the Terror—without objection from the restored Church.

Birotteau had lived two years in that house and had longed for its rooms as highly as he longed for a canonry. His two great desires—being Mademoiselle Gamard’s boarder and becoming a canon—were the ambitions of a provincial priest who sought decent lodging, good food, clean garments and the self-esteem of a title. The apartment he inhabited had been the cherished object of a passion begun when he first admired the rooms of his friend, the Abbé Chapeloud, and which grew into an obsession.

For years Chapeloud, who became canon when worship was restored under Napoleon, had lived in the south-facing apartment, gradually furnishing and renovating it with modest legacies and gifts: an oak bookcase from a demolished château, waxed floors, painted woodwork, a long ebony table, Boulle cabinets, folios and Fathers of the Church. The rooms had been transformed from shabby to dignified; an oratory and a salon with red damask and mahogany completed them. Birotteau, poor and the son of peasants like Chapeloud, admired these changes and secretly yearned to possess them. His desire increased until, when Chapeloud fell ill, thoughts he could not suppress ran, “If Chapeloud dies I can have this apartment.” Yet he never contemplated any means to influence his friend’s will.

Chapeloud, indulgent and knowing his friend, forgave his covetousness, and on his deathbed, as Birotteau read the Quotidienne, said, “This time you will certainly get the apartment.” Chapeloud bequeathed his library and furniture to Birotteau, who, though saddened, found consolation in the inheritance and in Mademoiselle Gamard’s cheerful agreement to take him as a lodger. He moved into the apartment and enjoyed comforts he had long imagined: meticulous linen, fires, well-chosen wood, fragrant orris-root, and the small attentions for which Chapeloud had praised the spinster. Mademoiselle Gamard’s household had been famous, Chapeloud had often declared, for its perfect order and attentive service.

Yet, having obtained his long-desired comforts, Birotteau soon replaced the earlier obsession with a new hunger: the canonry. Hopes fed at Madame de Listomère’s conversations persuaded him that promotion was near. Absorbed in these hopes, he did not notice until his return that he had left his umbrella and that some domestic attentions were missing. He found the outer door locked, Marianne the servant answering awkwardly that Mademoiselle had thought he was in; his candlestick had been moved to an ante-chamber; the fire in his room had not been burning; his slippers were not placed where they usually were. These trivial changes struck him as deliberate signs of displeasure. He concluded, by a sort of clerical logic, that Mademoiselle Gamard intended to chill and humiliate him. The small humiliations mortified him deeply.

Birotteau, kindly but simple-minded and ignorant of worldly calculations, lacked the frank self-knowledge to see what he himself had done to provoke dislike. He was a good man whose goodness verged on stupidity; his learning was shallowly acquired, and his life centered on mass and the confessional. He could not perceive how unconscious selfishness and provincial narrowness, common to many, had made him difficult. He was, in effect, a clumsy egoist, unlike Chapeloud, who was a shrewd one.

Chapeloud had understood Mademoiselle Gamard: an old maid of about thirty-eight with pretensions turned into strong self-esteem. The canon had judged she needed constant, precise attentions if he would live comfortably under her roof. So he kept limited contact with her, avoided breakfast and supper, and came to dinner a few minutes early to pay polite compliments about the food and service. By these attentions, and by never creating points of contact beyond politeness, Chapeloud secured a peaceful coexistence and was often praised as easy to live with.

Troubert, the other lodger, was her creature: obedient, patient and entirely under her rule, judged often to have designs on her property because of the intimacy of their domestic life. When Chapeloud died the landlady had thought at first to offer the rooms to Troubert, but seeing Birotteau’s attachment to the apartment she accepted him as lodger for her own advantage and even improved the rooms: replacing brick with wooden floors and rebuilding the chimney.

Yet Birotteau, dazzled by his new happiness, did not adopt Chapeloud’s circumspection. He fell into Mademoiselle Gamard’s life with the embarrassment and timidity of a man who fears seeming rude and who stays too long in conversation. He began to spend his evenings there, and the spinster, whose real desire had long been to make her salon a weekly center of company, welcomed the chance to display more prominent visitors. Taking pride in entertaining, she boasted that thanks to Birotteau she could gather respectable company.

But the vicar soon deserted her salon for Madame de Listomère’s and Mademoiselle Salomon’s, preferring aristocratic circles to which the landlady did not belong. His withdrawal gravely wounded Mademoiselle Gamard; she had expected to use him to attract society. Her disappointment, joined to an old-maid’s craving for return on social investment and wounded pride, turned into resentment. The vicar, however, never understood this. He perceived, belatedly and clumsily, signs of slights—small omissions and unkindnesses—and concluded that ill will was directed at him though he could not see the reasons.

Mademoiselle Gamard, like many who never adapted their habits to changing lives, indulged a petty despotism over small things—where the basket of counters lay, the arrangement of cards, the order of household routine. Such trifles she magnified into tests of obedience. The vicar, unlike Chapeloud, could not accept being persistently cinched by such crochets without protest; he withdrew rather than submitting, irritating her more by his absence than by any direct opposition.

Her friends, defending her, called Chapeloud amiable and blamed Birotteau for turning away from the old maid; society at large often sided, covertly, with those who escaped a nightly round in the Cloister. Mademoiselle Gamard enjoyed the condolence she received, and Troubert explained the departure by saying the abbe sought fashion, luxury and witty talk—thus cleverly flattering the spinster by implying he had higher tastes.

In truth, the vicar’s abandonment was a clumsy desertion, and his naïveté and lack of social tact made him gradually intolerable to Mademoiselle Gamard. Eighteen months after she had taken him in, when he mistook her silence for contentment, she had in fact begun a deliberate, underhand persecution. The locked door, the forgotten slippers, the unlit fire and the moved candlestick were the first signs. Birotteau, too innocent to see his own faults toward his landlady, could not understand why she had turned against him.

This prologue, recounting the small domestic developments that precede the greater drama, requires many particulars. The little meannesses, jealousies and petty ambitions of bourgeois life inflict injuries as keen as those produced by grander interests, and their slow, minute growths needed this account to explain the coming catastrophe.

The next morning Birotteau woke thinking so much of his prospective canonry that he forgot the four circumstances of the previous night that had seemed to portend coming misery. He did not rise without a fire. He rang for Marianne to make one, then lay absorbed in somnolent musings. Marianne’s custom was to rouse him gently by the murmured sound of her movements—a sound he loved. Twenty minutes passed and she had not come. Half a canon now, he was about to ring again when he heard a man’s step on the staircase. After a discreet knock, the Abbe Troubert obeyed Birotteau’s invitation and entered. Visits between the two abbés happened about once a month, so the canon’s surprise at Troubert’s appearance was small; he at once called to Marianne harshly for neglecting the fire, then, turning to his ecclesiastical brother, remarked, “If Mademoiselle knew that you had no fire she would scold Marianne.”

He inquired after Birotteau’s health and gently asked if there was any encouraging word about the canonry. The vicar explained the steps he had taken and naively named those with whom Madame de Listomere was exerting influence, unaware that Troubert harbored resentment against that lady for not admitting him—the Abbe Troubert, twice proposed by the bishop as vicar-general.

The contrasts between the two abbés were striking. Troubert, tall, lean, yellow and bilious, wore long, deeply wrinkled features that sometimes took a sarcastic or contemptuous expression; his habitual calm and lowered eyelids hid a piercing glance. Reddish hair and a meditative veil added gloom. Many mistook him for ambitious; those who knew him better thought him dull under Mademoiselle Gamard’s despotism or worn by fasting. He rarely spoke and never laughed; a feeble smile, when it came, lost itself in wrinkles.

Birotteau, by contrast, was plump, round-faced, ruddy, expansive and frank. He loved good things and was amused by trifles with simple amiability. Troubert’s presence produced involuntary fear; the vicar’s presence a kindly smile. Troubert’s solemn, bowed step and monumental cassock drew respect as he moved through Saint-Gatien; Birotteau trotted and ambled, seeming almost to roll himself along. Yet each was confined by his manner—Troubert’s austere air holding him down to canon, Birotteau’s domestic ways marking him perpetually as vicar.

At fifty, Troubert, partly by circumspect conduct and apparent lack of ambition and partly by a saintly life, had calmed fears his presence once inspired. His health had seriously failed during the year, and elevation to vicar-general seemed probable, though rivals hoped his chronic malady might soon remove him. Birotteau’s triple chin suggested sound health to those who coveted his canonry; even his gout seemed, proverbially, a pledge of longevity.

The Abbe Chapeloud, a sensible man amiable in society, had secretly opposed Troubert’s elevation and deftly kept him from the best salons. During Chapeloud’s lifetime Troubert treated him with great respect, yet Chapeloud persisted in warning Birotteau: “Distrust that lean stick of a Troubert,—Sixtus the Fifth reduced to the limits of a bishopric!”

Such was the friend and fixture of Mademoiselle Gamard who now came, the morning after that old maid had, as it were, declared war against the poor vicar, to pay his brother a social visit.

“You must excuse Marianne,” the canon said as she entered. “I suppose she went first to my rooms. They are very damp, and I coughed all night. You are most healthily situated here,” he added, looking up at the cornice.

“Yes; I am lodged like a canon,” replied Birotteau.

“And I like a vicar,” said Troubert humbly.

“But you will soon be settled in the archbishop’s palace,” said the kindly vicar, eager to see everyone happy.

“Yes, or in the cemetery, but God’s will be done!” Troubert raised his eyes to heaven resignedly. “I came,” he said, “to ask you to lend me the ‘Register of Bishops.’ You are the only man in Tours I know who has a copy.”

“Take it out of my library,” replied Birotteau, cheered by the memory of his greatest happiness.

The canon passed into the library while the vicar dressed. Presently the breakfast bell rang; Birotteau reflected that had Troubert not come he would have had no fire to dress by. “He’s a kind man,” he thought.

The two priests descended with huge folios which they laid on a side table in the dining-room. Mademoiselle Gamard, sharp of voice, rebuked them at once for “littering” her dining-room with old books. Troubert explained Birotteau had lent them to him; she scoffed that Birotteau didn’t often read books of that size. The vicar, in his mellifluous voice, inquired after her health.

“Not very well,” she replied shortly. “You woke me last night out of my first sleep, and I was wakeful for the rest of the night.” Sitting down, she added, “Gentlemen, the milk is getting cold.”

Stupefied at being so ill-naturedly received by his landlady—whom he half expected to apologize—yet frightened at the prospect of confrontation, the poor vicar sat in silence. His natural inclination was to avoid quarrels; reason urged him not to submit to discourtesy. Torn, he examined the broad green lines on the oilcloth the landlady always left on the table at breakfast, oblivious to its ragged edges. The priests sat opposite in cane-seated arm-chairs; Mademoiselle Gamard presided in a high chair on casters near the dining-room stove. The room and the salon were on the ground floor beneath Birotteau’s salon and bedroom.

When his coffee, duly sugared, arrived, the grim silence chilled him. He dared not look at Troubert’s dried features or the old maid’s threatening visage; he turned instead to the plethoric pug on a cushion near the stove—the most important but least troublesome person in the household since he had ceased to bark. Birotteau, fond of harmless chatter he thought aided digestion, addressed the dog: “Well, my pretty, are you waiting for your coffee?” The pug blinked and closed his eyes peevishly.

The vicar’s loquacity, usually trusted to start conversation with Mademoiselle Gamard, failed him now; forced to strain his mind for beguiling topics in recent mornings, he felt tongue-tied. He summoned courage to remark, “This coffee is excellent,” and then, looking at a scrap of sky between Saint-Gatien’s buttresses, added, “It will be finer today than yesterday.” That remark earned Mademoiselle Gamard’s most gracious look for Troubert and a terrible severity for Birotteau, who by then was looking at his plate.

Mademoiselle Sophie Gamard presented the very elegiac nature of an old maid. To understand her influence in the drama and on Birotteau’s life, it helps to summarize what makes an Old Maid as she embodied it. Some beings consume without producing; moral philosophy and political economy condemn such uselessness. Few old maids willingly enter this rank; society, especially in France, casts a strong stigma on a woman who has no one willing to share life’s blessings or ills. Unmarried women reach a period when they are judged for their rejection; if ugly, goodness should compensate; if handsome, some serious cause is suspected; if they chose celibacy for independence, mothers and men condemn their disloyalty to womanly devotion. To renounce womanhood is to abjure its poetry and forfeit consolations rightly due to mothers.

Moreover, generous sentiments and the refinements of taste develop only by exercise. Remaining unmarried, a woman may become void of meaning; selfish and cold, she provokes repulsion. The world’s judgment, harsh though it may be, presses upon such women and their faces show the distress. A life without the expression of happiness that adorns married women yields sharpness and peevishness; suffering breeds bitterness. Before an old maid blames herself for isolation she blames others; reproach can become a desire for revenge.

Because they did not exercise the feminine impulse to please, many old maids have little elegance or taste. They are used to choosing what serves themselves at the sacrifice of pleasing others; they end by feeling and suffering this difference. Jealousy is indelible in the female breast. An old maid’s soul is jealous yet void; she knows only the miserable side of the passion society allows women. Thwarted in all hopes and forced to deny their natures, they endure inward torment. Their glance becomes oblique from fear and shame rather than modesty; they never forgive society—or themselves—for their false position.

These sad truths were visible in Mademoiselle Gamard’s dulled gray eyes and the dark circles beneath them; all the wrinkles on her face ran in straight lines. Scattered hairs, once brown, grew on her chin. Thin lips scarcely covered long but white teeth; her complexion was dark and her hair, once black, had turned gray from frightful headaches that made her wear a false front poorly fitted to conceal the junction. Her gown—silk in summer, merino in winter—was always brown and rather tight on her angular figure. A limp collar exposed a red, ribbed neck. Her origin as the daughter of a wood-merchant explained some defects. She might once have been plump, but no trace remained. An aquiline nose proclaimed despotism of nature, a flat forehead narrowness of mind. Her movements had an odd abruptness; she held herself erect and walked as if her joints were consolidating, advancing in a solid block like a statue. When in good humour she would tell of chances to have married and of timely discoveries of her lovers’ lack of means—unwittingly proving her worldly prudence over her heart.

This typical old maid was framed by the varnished-paper Turkish landscapes that decorated the dining-room. The salon, the “yellow salon,” was consistently yellow—curtains, furniture, walls—gilt mirror, crystal candlesticks and clock. No one had seen Mademoiselle Gamard’s private room; conjecture imagined it full of odds and ends and keepsakes dear to the hearts of old maids.

Lacking the natural activity women are given, Mademoiselle Gamard spent her energies on petty intrigues, provincial cabals and self-seeking schemes—the usual occupation of old maids. Birotteau had developed in Sophie Gamard the only feeling possible for her: hatred. That passion, latent under provincial calm, would become intense because spent on petty things in a narrow sphere. Birotteau, one of those predestined to suffer because they cannot avoid hurts they do not see, became an object of her malice.

“Yes, it will be a fine day,” replied Troubert, after a pause, outwardly conforming to politeness.

Frightened at how long he had sat without speaking—he had, for the first time, taken coffee without conversation—Birotteau left the dining-room with his heart squeezed as if in a vise and walked the box-edged garden paths that formed a star. Turning after the first round, he saw Mademoiselle Gamard and Troubert standing stock-still on the threshold—Troubert with folded arms and the immobility of a tomb statue; she leaning against the blind door. Both seemed to be watching him and counting his steps. Nothing is more disquieting to a timid creature than to feel itself the focus of close examination, and if those eyes are full of hatred, the suffering is martydom.

Birotteau fancied he was blocking their path. Driven by fear and a desire not to inconvenience them, he left the garden and went to the church, no longer thinking of his canonry, absorbed by the old maid’s disheartening tyranny. Saint-Gatien offered occupation—funerals, a marriage, baptisms—and he forgot his troubles. When his stomach reminded him of dinner, he checked his watch and saw, alarmed, that it was some minutes after four. Mademoiselle Gamard’s punctuality was known; he hurried back.

Passing the kitchen door he saw that the first course had been removed. In the dining-room the old maid said with sour rebuke and joy at being able to blame him: “It is half-past four, Monsieur Birotteau. You know we are not to wait for you.” He noticed the gauze over the clock had been moved; the hands had been set ahead of the cathedral clock. He could make no remark: any protest would have invited one of those fierce, eloquent expositions in which Mademoiselle Gamard excelled. The thousand petty annoyances a servant will make her master or a woman her husband endure were instinctively divined and used against Birotteau. The way she delighted in plotting against his domestic comfort bore marks of a profoundly malignant genius, yet she contrived always, as far as could be seen, to be in the right.

################################################
Eight days after the opening events of this history, shifts in household relations made Abbé Birotteau perceive a plot long growing against him.

At first Mademoiselle Gamard’s hostility had been covert and the vicar could ignore it; after the candlestick and the altered clock he could no longer doubt her animus. He fell into despair, imagining her thin, clawlike fingers everywhere. Gamard, delighted by revenge, hovered above him like a bird of prey. She had long conceived a plan the simple priest could not foresee, and now executed it with the petty ingenuity of solitary people who, deprived of great passions, expend themselves on meticulous outward devotion.

These small torments prevented Birotteau, effusive and craving pity, from confiding in friends—another cruelty. His timidity made him dread seeming ridiculous by troubling anyone with such pettiness. Yet these petty matters were his life—he preferred the absence of strong feeling and found happiness in routine. His paradise turned to hell; his dread of confronting Gamard grew, and secret distress harmed his health. One morning, while putting on his mottled blue stockings, he noticed his calves had shrunk. Terrified, he resolved to ask Abbé Troubert to intervene officially between him and the landlady.

In Troubert’s bare, cheerless room the vicar, ashamed to bother a man so occupied, after many internal agonies explained his case. The canon listened coldly, suppressing an occasional smile. He blamed Birotteau’s failure to notice on his preoccupation with elevated thoughts, remarked that recluses once thought little of food and lodging, and suggested modern priests retire into the solitude of their hearts. He said he might arbitrate because his friendship with Gamard had always observed Church limits, but justice required hearing both sides. Troubert professed to see no change in Gamard, attributing temper fluctuations to pulmonary suffering, and urged patience; if Birotteau stayed longer he would come to esteem her. Birotteau left confounded.

Unable to consult anyone, he judged Gamard alone and fancied that leaving her house for a few days might extinguish her dislike. He resolved to spend a week at Madame de Listomère’s country house, the Alouette, a habit he often followed. Ironically, this was exactly what Gamard wished; only patient endurance could have foiled her plan. The vicar, incapable of divining this, was doomed to fall.

Ten days after his arrival at the Alouette, while breakfasting, he was told Monsieur Caron wished to speak with him. Caron was Gamard’s lawyer. Agitated and surprised, Birotteau learned Caron had been sent to ask how long he intended to remain. The original agreement had not foreseen a long absence and might lead to contest; Gamard, anxious to avoid dispute, had sent Caron to learn his plans. The abbé asked for time to seek advice.

Back at table, Birotteau described his trouble. The country-house company, bored with monotony, eagerly took Gamard’s side. Madame de Listomère supposed Troubert wanted Birotteau’s apartment; Monsieur de Listomère urged resistance. An old landowner, Monsieur de Bourbonne, suggested something more serious: Gamard intended to turn Birotteau out, and people like her risked nothing in such games. De Bourbonne, thin, shrewd, experienced in weighing words and actions, had a reputation that made him feared in local affairs.

Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix arrived from Tours with news that altered everything. She reported that the vicar-general was very ill and the archbishop had provisionally delegated his powers to Abbé Troubert, so the canonry would depend on him. At a recent gathering the Abbé Poirel had spoken of Birotteau’s annoyances to Gamard in a way that cast blame on the vicar. Madame de Listomère asked whether he preferred to be made canon or to continue living with Gamard; the company unanimously chose the canon. She suggested that by leaving Gamard’s house he might secure the canonry—“one good turn deserves another.” All applauded except her nephew, the Baron, who joked about wishing to see a fight.

Despite their support, forces were unequal: the city’s best society had less power than Gamard backed by Troubert. Acting on Madame de Listomère’s advice, a servant fetched Caron back. De Bourbonne counselled caution and delay, but passion prevailed. In a short conference Caron urged Birotteau to sign a paper declaring his relinquishment of domicile. Confused and overwhelmed, Birotteau read and mechanically signed it: he declared he left Gamard’s house of his own free will and that he had been properly fed and lodged. Caron asked where to send the abbé’s belongings; Birotteau said Madame de Listomère. De Bourbonne, suspicious, requested to see the paper and noted there must be prior documents between Birotteau and Gamard. The fatal deed, the vicar said, was in his library.

The helplessness of this innocent man touched Madame de Listomère and Mademoiselle Salomon, who soothed him like mothers consoling a child. They promised lodging and counsel, urged play to distract him, and spoke of asking Troubert for help. Dazzled by the prospect of living with Madame de Listomère, he temporarily forgot his ruined happiness. But that night the reality returned: thoughts of moving, of scattered books and furniture, and the end of long habits overwhelmed him. He could not reconcile one sweet first year in Gamard’s house with the cruel second; the canonry seemed a poor compensation. He clung to Mademoiselle Salomon’s friendship, but dared not wholly trust new attachments.

The narrator digresses to note that among spinsters there are heroic kinds—those who sacrifice themselves nobly. Mademoiselle Salomon belonged to this class: once beloved and young, she had tended a lover who went mad, refusing to call him insane; she had devoted five years to his mechanical well‑being, and after his death settled in Tours to do good. Simple, frank, and regular in features, she attached herself to the feeble, making her warmly interested in Birotteau.

Mademoiselle de Villenoix returned Birotteau to Tours the next morning and left him at the cathedral quay to go to the Cloister, bent on preserving the canonry and overseeing the removal of his furniture. At the house he found his old apartment occupied: Marianne told him Troubert was installed in his rooms. The sight of Troubert, comfortably seated in Chapeloud’s library and armchair, confirmed the depth of the conspiracy. Birotteau realized with sinking dread that Troubert intended to possess Chapeloud’s and his spoils. Facing Troubert’s sardonic glance, he felt struck dumb.

Troubert coolly told him Gamard had informed him of Birotteau’s departure and that he had been installed out of necessity; if the furniture belonged to Birotteau, Gamard’s scrupulous honesty guaranteed its return; if he wished to return, Troubert would cede the apartment. The words were terrible. Birotteau ran to find Gamard and met her on the staircase. Bowing despite her derisive smile, he protested that she should have allowed him to pack his books and furniture. She interrupted, asking if he had read his deed, and then, with the triumphant clarity of hatred, reminded him of the clause: by agreement, if he left her house his furniture would indemnify her for the difference in board between what he paid and what the late Abbé Chapeloud had paid. With Poirel’s appointment as canon, Gamard exulted in the arrangement. At these words Birotteau faintly bowed and fled, fearing a complete breakdown if he stayed.

He found at Madame de Listomère’s house his linen, clothing, and papers packed in a trunk, and sank, hiding his face in his hands. Poirel had been made canon; Birotteau had no home, means, or furniture. Mademoiselle Salomon, unaware of the deed’s terms, believed his distress due to Poirel’s nomination and, persuaded by the porter, took him in her carriage. Back at the Alouette, friends tended him until evening, when he recounted the morning’s events.

De Bourbonne asked to see the deed. Birotteau produced it. De Bourbonne read the clause providing that, because of an eight‑hundred‑franc yearly difference between Chapeloud’s board and Gamard’s charge, and because Birotteau could not pay full price, he had accepted sums lent by Gamard and, as indemnity, engaged to leave her all household property at death, or to transfer it should he voluntarily give up the apartment. De Bourbonne cursed the agreement and declared Gamard armed with claws.

Birotteau had never imagined anything would separate him from the house where he hoped to live and die. He had not remembered the clause, which he had not discussed; in his eagerness to enter the house he would have signed any document. Madame de Listomère, in hot indignation, declared she had made him sign and must restore his lost happiness. De Bourbonne advised law: the deed might constitute fraud. The Baron and others urged litigation. De Bourbonne privately warned that many now friendly would soon desert him, and if Birotteau wished peace he should resign the vicariate and retire to a distant parish where Troubert could not pursue him. This prospect horrified the priest—leaving Tours was for him like death; celibates replace feelings with habits, and Birotteau needed his routine: his walks, salons, and games.

Word quickly spread that Madame la Baronne de Listomère had invited Birotteau to stay. The baroness’s reception implicitly denied Gamard’s assertions and wounded Gamard deeply. To understand Gamard’s resources, one must see the network that served Troubert. Accompanied by Troubert, Gamard made evening rounds to houses where circles of old maids, a few men swayed by servants’ gossip, and other busybodies met. These women, ever sifting words and scrutinizing neighbors, kept exact accounts of fortunes and actions, retailing scandal and shaping local opinion. Spread through town like capillary fibres, they absorbed household secrets and transmitted them mechanically to Troubert. They formed a kind of informal police—dumb but perpetually talking—whose gossip could have terrible effects when it touched important interests. The quarrel became the absorbing passion of these salons; beneath it lay a class sentiment—a small‑town people‑versus‑senate struggle.

Yet these petty passions engaged their participants with an intensity equal to great affairs. Troubert’s hours, like those of a gambler or statesman, were heavy with anxious thought. The narrator asks, in passing, to consider how certain passions prompt virtues that widen a life beyond its selfish center.

Madame de Listomère returned to Tours and took Birotteau to her lawyer. The lawyer, whose practice lay among the devout, did not think the case easy. Friends who had believed justice certain had delayed bringing the suit until returning to town, and Gamard’s allies had taken the initiative, spreading the affair to Birotteau’s damage. According to the deed, Gamard had the law on her side; in equity, judges and respectable laymen might view Birotteau as having derogated from his formerly peaceable and mild character. Gamard had lent him money to pay Chapeloud’s legacy duties without a receipt; Birotteau, not of age or character to sign with full understanding, left Gamard’s house after two years while Chapeloud and Troubert had been there twelve and fifteen years respectively; the suit might appear like ingratitude. The lawyer refused to undertake it and besought Madame de Listomère to avoid embroiling herself.

That evening Birotteau told friends of the lawyer’s refusal. De Bourbonne advised continued caution and suggested only a radical lawyer might take the case. The Baron, inflamed, insisted Troubert’s involvement made it a public matter; valuable pictures and furniture had been implicated, and Birotteau could not have intended to give away such wealth for two years of board. He proposed getting Monsieur Salmon, an expert from the Museum, to appraise the paintings and furniture.

Two days later the suit began. Choosing the radical lawyer both helped and harmed Birotteau: opponents of government and critics of religion seized on the case, and the town buzzed. The Museum expert appraised Valentin’s Virgin and Lebrun’s Christ at eleven thousand francs; the bookshelves and Gothic furniture, fashionable in Paris, were worth at least twelve thousand. The full appraisal exceeded thirty‑six thousand francs. Clearly, Birotteau could not have intended to give Gamard such a sum under the deed; legally, he had equitable grounds to amend the agreement; if refused, Gamard’s conduct might constitute intentional fraud. The radical lawyer served a harsh writ on Gamard, a document of skillful legal argument backed by precedent and code citations; it so clearly condemned the old maid that many copies were maliciously distributed through the town.

A few days after hostilities began between Birotteau and the old maid, the Baron de Listomere, who expected promotion to corvette captain, received a letter warning he might be put on the retired list. He went to Paris and to the minister, who laughed at his fears; but the next day, despite the minister’s assurances, the baron made inquiries. By an indiscretion a secretary showed him a document confirming the news, awaiting only the director’s signature.

The baron hurried to an uncle, a deputy who could see the minister at the Chamber, and begged him to learn the minister’s intentions in a matter that threatened his future. Waiting anxiously in his uncle’s carriage, he was told on the way home: the minister had first accused him of leading Radicals in Tours and of objectionable politics; but the real trouble, his uncle learned, was ecclesiastical. The baron had spoken slightingly of Abbé Troubert, the vicar-general, an influential man representing the Jesuits in the province. His uncle warned him to make peace with Troubert at once, saying such priests were men with whom one must live in harmony; Troubert was likely to be raised to a bishopric and could even harm the uncle’s prospects.

These words explained to the baron Troubert’s secret activities that Birotteau had often wondered about: “I can’t think what he does with himself—sitting up all night.” Troubert’s position, skill, and female network in Tours had made him the Congregation’s proconsul there; archbishop, general, prefect—all were under his covert influence. The Baron de Listomere decided on his course. “I shall take care not to get another round shot below my water-line,” he told his uncle.

Three days later the baron, returning hurriedly, told his aunt the family must abandon support of “that fool of a Birotteau.” He detained Monsieur de Bourbonne as the old man was leaving a rubber of whist; Bourbonne's clear-sightedness seemed essential to navigate the reefs the Listomeres faced. The baron’s return and disquiet hinted he had met a check in his campaign against Gamard and Troubert. He revealed Troubert’s hidden power.

“I knew that,” said Bourbonne.

“Then why did you not warn us?” the baroness cried.

“Forget that I knew it, and I will forget that you did,” Bourbonne replied. “If we do not keep this secret now we shall be thought his accomplices. Pretend to be duped; but watch your steps. I warned you, but you would not understand me.”

Abandoning Birotteau was taken for granted by the three. “To beat a retreat with the honors of war has always been the triumph of the ablest generals,” said Bourbonne. “Bow to Troubert; if his hatred is less than his vanity you may win him; if you bow too low he will walk over you. Make believe you intend to leave the service, and you’ll escape him. Send away Birotteau, madame, and you will set things right with Mademoiselle Gamard. Ask Troubert, when you meet him at the archbishop’s, if he plays whist; he will say yes. Invite him to your salon; you are a woman and can win a priest. When the baron is promoted and his uncle made a peer, and Troubert a bishop, you can make Birotteau a canon if you choose. Meantime yield—but yield gracefully, with a slight menace. Your family can give him as much as he can give you. As for you, sailor, keep your deep-sea line about you.”

“Poor Birotteau?” said the baroness.

“Get rid of him at once,” Bourbonne replied. “If some clever Radical gets that empty head, he may cause trouble. The court might give a verdict in his favor, and Troubert must fear that. I have said my say.”

The next morning the baroness took Birotteau aside and, not without embarrassment, asked him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and to leave her house. As she spoke he turned pale. She explained, briefly and without disguising the gravity, the wide ramifications of the affair, making him see the net woven by revenge and the capacity and power of Troubert, whose hatred of Chapeloud now vented itself in seizing Chapeloud’s property and persecuting his friend. Birotteau clasped his hands and wept, listening as one at the edge of a precipice. The baroness ended: family duties must be considered before friendship; yield, and she would ensure he lacked nothing, arranging matters through Monsieur de Bourbonne to save appearances. “Grant me the right to abandon you,” she begged.

The bewildered abbé cried, “Chapeloud was right when he said that if Troubert could drag him by the feet out of his grave he would do it!” Yet he yielded. “I trust myself to you—I am but the stubble of the streets,” he said, using a local word for a pathetic bit of straw. He asked only for Chapeloud’s portrait, painted for him, and the baroness promised to see Mademoiselle Gamard herself and to consult the archbishop.

Birotteau left dismayed; Troubert now seemed to him a pyramid whose hands were in Paris and elbows in the cloister. The news of Birotteau’s removal from the Listomere house seemed mysterious: the baroness told people the nephew meant to marry and leave the navy, and wanted the vicar’s apartment. The Listomeres’ apparent neutrality implied recognition of the Congregation’s occult power. But the lawsuit still awaited judgment; their opponents yielded while threatening.

Then Mademoiselle Gamard took cold leaving the cathedral and was soon dangerously ill. The town burst into commiseration: “Mademoiselle Gamard’s sensitive nature could not bear the scandal; in spite of the justice of her cause she was likely to die of grief—Birotteau has killed his benefactress.” Such talk spread through the female conclave and the town.

Madame de Listomere went to pay her promised visit but the old maid was too ill to see her. She asked to speak to the vicar-general. Troubert, gratified to receive in Chapeloud’s library the woman who had hitherto ignored him, kept her waiting before admitting her. No courtier could have put more dissimulation into negotiating personal interests than the baroness and the priest did now.

Bourbonne had coached the baroness: “Don’t forget your cue. You are a mediator, not an interested party. Troubert is a mediator. Weigh your words; if he strokes his chin you have him.” Their conversation was a duel of implied meanings. Madame de Listomere began by expressing regret at Birotteau’s lawsuit and her desire to settle it.

“The harm is done, madame,” Troubert said gravely. “The pious Mademoiselle Gamard is dying.” (He intended to place the guilt on the baroness.)

“On hearing of her illness I entreated Monsieur Birotteau to relinquish his claims; I have brought the document intending to give it to that excellent woman,” she replied. (She meant to avoid compromising herself.) After a tense interchange of civilities and veiled thrusts about religion, politics, and the paintings, she laid the deed of relinquishment on the table and said she had come to reconcile two Christians.

“Your nephew has been to Paris, I believe,” Troubert said. “He returns tonight; the minister does not want him to leave the service.” “He has done us the service of getting a proper estimate on the value of those paintings,” Troubert added, eyeing the pictures. Madame de Listomere offered frames for Saint-Gatien if they were to be given to the chapel. He replied they did not belong to him. She handed him the relinquishment, saying it ended all discussion and that she had some compassion for Birotteau; he had asked, as the price of renunciation, for Chapeloud’s portrait. Troubert looked fixedly at her and went down to ask Mademoiselle Gamard.

He soon returned. “Madame,” he said, “I bring you the words of a dying woman. ‘The Abbé Chapeloud was so true a friend...that I cannot consent to part with his picture.’ As for me, if it were mine I would not yield it. My feelings to my late friend were so faithful that I should feel my right above others’.”

“That’s no need to quarrel over a bad picture,” the baroness said, offering to have a copy made and courteously inviting him to play whist at her house. Troubert, stroked his chin—caught by vanity—and promised he would oblige her for her sake.

Madame de Listomere went home hoping the archbishop would complete the peace. But Mademoiselle Gamard died the next day. Her will left everything to Abbé Troubert; the fortune was appraised at three hundred thousand francs. He sent the Listomeres invitations to the services and funeral; the baroness insisted they go. “We must go,” she said. Bourbonne added that the baron must go to the cemetery; the lieutenant, unluckily, had not left Tours.

The services were performed with unusual ecclesiastical magnificence. Only Birotteau wept, kneeling in a side chapel; he believed himself guilty and sincerely prayed for the deceased, lamenting that he had not obtained her forgiveness. Troubert followed the body to the grave and delivered a discourse that, by eloquence, enlarged the old maid’s life to monumental proportions. In the peroration he spoke of her life devoted to God and of Providence withdrawing her from trials; his tone suggested she rested innocent. After the sermon, in a gesture that betrayed more joy than grief, he sprinkled the coffin with holy water.

The next morning Mademoiselle Salomon came to tell Madame de Listomere that “our poor Abbé Birotteau has just received a frightful blow: he is appointed curate of Saint-Symphorien.” Saint-Symphorien lay beyond the bridge, a suburb three miles away—an exile in plain sight of the town. The parsonage was cold and damp, the parish too poor, and the removal seemed an infamous plot.

To end the history: five months later Troubert was made Bishop of Troyes; Madame de Listomere died, leaving an annuity of fifteen hundred francs to Abbé Birotteau. The day her will’s dispositions were announced, Monseigneur Hyacinthe, Bishop of Troyes, about to leave Tours for his diocese, delayed when he received the news. Furious at being foiled by the baroness—whom he had lately received while she had secretly aided a man he counted as an enemy—Troubert again threatened the baron’s career and jeopardized his uncle’s peerage. He made one of those soft, vengeful priestly speeches in the archbishop’s salon. The Baron de Listomere went to him the next day and must have submitted to hard conditions, for his later conduct showed entire submission to the Jesuit’s will.

The new bishop deeded Mademoiselle Gamard’s house to the cathedral chapter, gave Chapeloud’s books and bookcases to the seminary, and presented the two disputed pictures to the Chapel of the Virgin—but he kept Chapeloud’s portrait. No one could explain this near-total renunciation of Gamard’s bequest; Bourbonne supposed the bishop had secretly kept invested monies to support his rank in Paris. Only the night before Troubert’s departure did Bourbonne unearth the hidden reason: the baron had contested Madame de Listomere’s legacy to Birotteau on grounds of undue influence. The deathblow to the feeblest victim was complete.

A few days after the case was brought the baron was promoted to captain. As ecclesiastical discipline, the curate of Saint-Symphorien—Birotteau—was suspended; his superiors judged him guilty. The murderer of Sophie Gamard was also a swindler; had Troubert kept the property openly, it would have been harder to censure Birotteau.

When Monseigneur Hyacinthe drove along the quay Saint-Symphorien in a post-chaise on his way to Paris, poor Birotteau was placed in an armchair in the sun on a terrace above the road. Pale and haggard, smitten by the archbishop, he was the skeleton of the man who less than a year earlier had rolled contentedly along the cloister. The bishop cast one look of pity and contempt, then went his way.

There is no doubt Troubert might once have been a Hildebrand or an Alexander VI. In our era the Church is no longer the political power it once was; celibacy concentrates a man’s faculties on a single passion—egotism—which makes celibates either useless or mischievous. We live when governments tend to make Man for Society rather than Society for Man. There is a perpetual struggle between the Individual and a social system that uses him while he tries to use it. In earlier times man was first father, then lived for clan or city; later, for caste or religion, and his range of interests widened. Today his life attaches to a vast country; perhaps someday his family may be the whole universe.

Will such moral cosmopolitanism—Christian Rome’s hope of Brotherhood—prove only a sublime error? It is natural to believe in such a noble vision, but human nature lacks such divine proportions. Souls vast enough to hold a world of feeling belong to great exceptions. Physiologists who say as the brain enlarges the heart narrows are mistaken. The apparent egotism of those who bear a science, a nation, or a code is a highest passion; it is the maternity of the masses. To create peoples and ideas they must unite in their mighty brains the tenderness of woman and the force of God. The histories of Innocent III, Peter the Great, and other great leaders show the same vast thought of which Troubert was a provincial representative.
