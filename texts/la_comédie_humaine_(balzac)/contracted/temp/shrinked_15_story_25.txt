The story begins in a lawyer's office where clerk Simonnin mocks Colonel Chabert, who seeks help from attorney M. Derville. Initially treated as a beggar, Chabert reveals he is a significant figure thought dead after the Battle of Eylau, highlighting societal disdain for those fallen from grace in post-war France.

Chabert, desperate, insists on meeting Derville despite the clerks' mockery. When Derville arrives, he is shocked by Chabert's ghostly appearance. Chabert recounts his survival after being gravely injured and left for dead, only to find that his wife, Countess Ferraud, has remarried and inherited his estate. Moved by Chabert's plight, Derville offers to help him reclaim his identity and fortune.

Three months later, Derville is approached by Crottat, who requests the return of 600 francs given to Chabert. Derville recalls that Chabert's estate was mismanaged by a disreputable lawyer who fled with clients' money. He learns that Chabert's widow inherited a substantial sum after a contested will and that Chabert is living in poor conditions with a dairyman named Vergniaud.

Upon visiting Chabert, Derville finds him dignified despite his squalor. Chabert wishes to reclaim his life and status as a general. Derville explains the complexities of proving Chabert's identity legally, warning him of a potentially lengthy court battle against his wife. Despite feeling disheartened, Chabert agrees to trust Derville.

Derville meets Vergniaud, who expresses concern for Chabert and requests financial help. Derville decides to assist but insists Chabert should also support Vergniaud. He plans to confront Madame Ferraud about Chabert's survival. When they meet, she initially dismisses the idea but becomes defensive when Derville warns her of legal implications.

Realizing the seriousness of her situation, Madame Ferraud becomes intrigued by leveraging Chabert's affection for a favorable outcome. Derville proposes a compromise for an amicable settlement that could benefit both parties.

A week later, Chabert and Madame Ferraud arrive at Derville's office. Chabert, now dressed well, embodies soldierly dignity, while the Countess follows elegantly. Tension rises as Chabert expresses anger over her reluctance to acknowledge him. Feeling cornered, the Countess leaves abruptly, prompting Derville to warn Chabert about her potential traps.

Later, the Countess reveals her love for another man, Comte Ferraud. Hurt but understanding, Chabert suggests they maintain dignity. However, the Countess attempts to charm him while plotting to secure her position, leading to a confrontation with her secretary, Delbecq, who suggests exploiting Chabert financially.

Feeling betrayed, Chabert confronts the Countess, who tries to manipulate him further. He decides to sacrifice his claim to his identity for her happiness, but her true intentions surface when she mentions putting him in an asylum. This shatters his trust, prompting him to reject her and resolve to disappear from her life.

Chabert's subsequent life is marked by poverty and obscurity. Derville, unaware of Chabert's fate, assumes a compromise had been reached. He later discovers Chabert imprisoned as a vagabond, stripped of dignity yet maintaining pride. In a final act of integrity, Chabert seeks to repay Derville, demonstrating loyalty despite betrayal. Derville facilitates payment owed to him by the Countess, highlighting the tragic consequences of their lives. Ultimately, Chabert's story reflects on identity, love, and the harsh realities post-war.

In June 1840, attorney Godeschal visits Bicetre with Derville, where they find Chabert, now a pauper. Chabert displays childlike innocence, claiming to be "Hyacinthe." An observing pauper notes his cleverness despite his apparent childishness. Derville laments the fate of men like Chabert, who served Napoleon only to end up in a workhouse, reflecting on the disillusionment faced by those in the legal profession and society's indifference to the fallen.
