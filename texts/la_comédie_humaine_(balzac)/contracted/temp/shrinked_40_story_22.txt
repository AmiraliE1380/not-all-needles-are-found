Some provincial houses wear a melancholy like cloisters or ruins; silence and barrenness prevail until a pale, motionless face appears at a window. Such a dwelling crowned the steep street toward the château in Saumur. This little-frequented, narrow, tortuous lane, echoing under its pebbles, is lined with three-century-old wooden houses, their carved ends blackened by time. Slate-covered beams trace bluish lines along frail walls; rotten shingles sag; blackened window-sills with faint sculpture hold clay pots. Doors studded with enormous nails bear domestic hieroglyphics—marks of Protestantism, Leaguer curses, or trades—the history of France written in wood.

Next to a tottering artisan’s house rose a country gentleman’s mansion with battered armorial bearings. In this hilly street ground-floors are not shops but the simple ouvroirs of another age: low, deep, dark rooms without show-windows. Doors open in two parts; the upper half is fastened back while the lower, fitted with a spring-bell, swings continually. Light and air enter through the upper door or an open space between ceiling and low front wall, closed each evening by solid shutters and iron bars.

That low wall serves as a counter displaying mere samples—tubs of cod and salt, bundles of sail-cloth, cordage, copper wire, iron hoops, a few cloth pieces. Inside a neat girl in a white kerchief calls her father or mother, who sells what you want, phlegmatic, civil, or arrogant. A cooper may seem to own little beyond boat-ribs and laths, yet his wood-yard below supplies the cooperage of Anjou; a hot season makes him rich, a rainy one ruins him. Here, as in Touraine, weather controls commerce: wine-growers, proprietors, wood-merchants, coopers, inn-keepers, mariners—all watch the sun and dread frost, rain, drought. The barometer smooths, saddens, or brightens their faces; from end to end of this street men call out its signs: “Here’s golden weather,” or “It rains louis,” knowing what sun or rain will bring.

On Saturdays these traders, with vineyards and provisions arranged, close shop and spend two days in the country. Purchases and profits settled, they have hours for pleasure, observation, criticism, and spying. Neighbors remark on every partridge or pot; a girl peeking at a window is seen by idling groups. Consciences live in sunlight; the dark, silent houses hide no mysteries. Life is mostly at door thresholds where households breakfast, dine, quarrel, and examine every passer-by. Once strangers were bantered from door to door—origin of many stories and the nickname copieux for Angers folk famed for such urban sarcasm.

The ancient mansions of Saumur’s old town crown this street; the melancholy dwelling at its top is one of those venerable relics. Follow the thoroughfare to the dark recess hiding the door of Monsieur Grandet’s house. To explain why the town calls it by his name, we must relate Monsieur Grandet’s biography.

Grandet’s provincial reputation is best understood locally. In 1789, still called le Père Grandet, he was a master-cooper who could read, write, and cipher. That year he married the daughter of a rich wood-merchant; with about two thousand louis-d’or—his and his wife’s dowry—and help from two hundred double louis given by his father-in-law to the republican presiding over sales, Grandet bought cheaply one of the finest vineyards, an old abbey, and several farms. The inhabitants, not very revolutionary, admired his boldness and patriotism; in truth his thought was open only to vineyards. Appointed to administrate Saumur, his influence proved pacific and commercial: he protected former nobles when possible and supplied Republican armies with puncheons of white wine, taking payment in splendid fields then reserved for sale.

Under the Consulate Grandet became mayor, governed wisely, and reaped better pickings. Under the Empire he was called Monsieur Grandet; Napoleon supplanted him in office but he left without regret. He had built roads to his properties; his house and lands, assessed advantageously, paid moderate taxes; and his vineyards, through his care, became the “head of the country,” the local term for the finest yielders. He might well have asked for the Legion of Honor.

By 1806 Grandet was fifty-seven, his wife thirty-six, and their only daughter ten. That year he inherited three fortunes—those of Madame de la Gaudiniere (née de la Bertelliere), old Monsieur de la Bertelliere, and Madame Gentillet—a hoard amassed by avarice. Old Monsieur de la Bertelliere prized his sight of gold above all. The inhabitants computed his savings by “the revenues of the sun’s wealth,” as they said.

Grandet thus obtained a modern nobility. He farmed a hundred acres of vineyard yielding seven or eight hundred hogsheads in good years; he owned thirteen farms, an old abbey whose windows he had walled to save expense, one hundred and twenty-seven acres of meadow with three thousand poplars planted in 1793, and the house where he lived. This was his visible estate; as to other property, only two men could hazard a guess at its value: Monsieur Cruchot, a notary who managed Grandet’s usurious investments, and Monsieur des Grassins, Saumur’s richest banker, in whose profits Grandet had a secret share.

Cruchot and des Grassins, discreet though obsequious, prompted townspeople to estimate Grandet’s wealth by their deference. No one doubted he kept a private treasure, a nightly hoard of louis. Avaricious observers found proof in his eyes, a yellow-metal gleam. The look of a man used to drawing enormous interest becomes furtive, eager, mysterious—an unspoken language of passion. Grandet inspired respect as one who owed no man: skilful cooper and experienced wine-grower, he could tell whether to make a thousand puncheons or five hundred; he never failed in speculation and always had casks when they were dear. He stored vintages and sold them slowly; his famous 1811 brought over two hundred and forty thousand francs.

Financially, Grandet was part tiger, part boa-constrictor: lying in wait, springing on prey, swallowing masses of louis, then resting like a snake digesting—impassible, methodical, cold. Many felt the rending of those polished claws. For Cruchot he procured money at eleven per cent; for others des Grassins discounted bills at frightful deductions. Market talk daily mentioned Grandet. To some his fortune was a patriotic boast. Innkeepers and merchants told strangers: “We have two or three millionaire establishments; but as for Monsieur Grandet, he does not himself know how much he is worth.”

In 1816 Saumur reckoned his landed property near four millions; having made about a hundred thousand francs a year from 1793 to 1817, it was fair to presume he held cash nearly equal to his estate. After a game of boston or vine-talk, townspeople said: “Le Père Grandet? Le Père Grandet must have at least five or six millions.”

“You are cleverer than I; I have never been able to find out the amount,” Cruchot or des Grassins would answer when overheard.

If Parisians spoke of Rothschild or Lafitte, Saumur folk compared them to Grandet; incredulous if anyone said a Parisian was richer. Such a fortune explained his actions. Early oddities once ridiculed had long ceased. His slightest movements, like a blink, became laws to the countryside where men studied him like a naturalist studies instinct.

“It will be a hard winter,” said one; “Père Grandet has put on his fur gloves.”

“Père Grandet is buying quantities of staves; there will be plenty of wine this year.”

Grandet never bought bread or meat: his farmers supplied capons, chickens, eggs, butter, and his tithe of wheat. He owned a mill; the tenant took grain as rent and returned flour and bran. La Grande Nanon, his only servant, baked every Saturday. He arranged with gardeners and tenants for vegetables; he sold most fruits at market. Firewood came from hedgerows and half-rotten sheds whose planks farmers carted to town, cut, and stacked in his wood-house, receiving his thanks. His known expenses were consecrated bread, clothing for his wife and daughter, hire of their chairs in church, La Grande Nanon’s wages, tinning saucepans, lights, taxes, repairs, and industrial costs. He had six hundred acres of woodland recently bought, put under a neighbor’s care for a promised indemnity; after acquiring this, he ate game for the first time.

Grandet’s manners were simple: he spoke little in short sententious phrases in a soft voice. Early he had stuttered when speaking at length; this incoherence, attributed to defects of education, was assumed and will be explained later. Four brief sentences sufficed him: “I don’t know; I cannot; I will not; I will see about it.” He never said yes or no nor committed himself to writing. If people talked, he listened coldly, chin in his right hand, right elbow on the left, forming opinions he never changed. He reflected long before any business agreement; when opponents confidently revealed plans, Grandet answered: “I can decide nothing without consulting my wife.” His wife, reduced to helpless slavery, was a useful screen in business. He went nowhere among friends; he neither gave nor accepted dinners; he made no stir, economizing even in motion. He never disturbed others’ things out of respect for property, yet at home coarser habits surfaced where self-control relaxed.

Physically Grandet was five feet high, thick-set, square-built, with knotted knees and broad shoulders; his round face, pitted by small-pox, a straight chin, white teeth, and eyes with the calm, devouring expression of a basilisk. His forehead showed transverse wrinkles and prominent bumps; his yellow-gray hair was mockingly called silver and gold. His thick-ended nose bore a veined wen the common people said was full of malice. His countenance showed dangerous cunning, an integrity without warmth, and the egoism of a man used to concentrate every feeling on avarice’s enjoyments and upon the only human being who mattered to him—his daughter and sole heiress, Eugénie. Attitude, manners, bearing, all testified to the self-belief habit of success gives.

Though outwardly unctuous, Grandet’s nature was iron. His dress had not changed since 1791: stout shoes tied with thongs, thick woollen stockings, short coarse maroon breeches with silver buckles, a yellow-and-puce velvet waistcoat, a large maroon coat, a black cravat, and a quaker’s hat. His gloves, thick as a gendarme’s, lasted twenty months; to preserve them he always laid them on his hat in one particular spot. Saumur knew no more.

Only six persons had the right of entrance to Grandet’s house. The most important of the first three was a nephew of Monsieur Cruchot. Since his appointment president of the Civil courts of Saumur this young man had added Bonfons to Cruchot, signing himself C. de Bonfons. Any litigant who called him Monsieur Cruchot would soon feel the folly in court. The magistrate, thirty-three, owned Bonfons worth seven thousand francs a year; he expected to inherit his uncle the notary’s and another uncle Abbé Cruchot’s property. These three Cruchots, backed by cousins and allies, formed a party like the Medici.

Madame des Grassins, mother of a son of twenty-three, came assiduously to play cards with Madame Grandet, hoping to marry her Adolphe to Mademoiselle Eugénie. Monsieur des Grassins furthered his wife’s schemes by services constantly rendered to the old miser. The des Grassins had adherents and cousins. On the Cruchot side the abbé—the family Talleyrand—backed by his brother the notary, contested every inch with his female adversary and tried to obtain the heiress for his nephew the president.

This secret warfare for Eugénie’s hand kept Saumur agitated. Would Mademoiselle Eugénie marry Monsieur le président or Monsieur Adolphe des Grassins? Some said Monsieur Grandet would give his daughter to neither: eaten with ambition, he was said to seek a peer of France to whom an income of three hundred thousand francs would make the Grandet casks acceptable. Others replied the des Grassins were nobles and rich; Adolphe was personable; unless the old man had a papal nephew at his beck, such an alliance should satisfy a man who had come from nothing—whom Saumur remembered with an adze in his hand and the bonnet rouge. Certain wise heads noted Cruchot de Bonfons had the right of entry at all times whereas his rival was received only on Sundays; others trusted Madame des Grassins’ intimacy with the women. “It is diamond cut diamond,” said a Saumur wit.

The oldest inhabitants declared the Grandets knew better than to let the property go out of the family, and that Mademoiselle Eugénie Grandet of Saumur would marry the son of Monsieur Grandet of Paris, a wealthy wholesale wine-merchant. To this the Cruchotines and Grassinists replied: the two brothers had seen each other only twice in thirty years; Monsieur Grandet of Paris, mayor and deputy and colonel of the National Guard, disowned the Saumur Grandets and meant to ally with some ducal family favored by Napoleon. In short, nothing was not said of the heiress, talked of for fifty miles and even on conveyances from Angers to Blois.

At the beginning of 1811 the Cruchotines won an advantage. The estate of Froidfond, noted for its park, mansion, farms, streams, ponds, and forests and worth about three millions, was put up for sale by the young Marquis de Froidfond, who had to liquidate. Maître Cruchot, the president, and the abbé, aided by adherents, prevented sale in small lots. The notary concluded a bargain for the whole property, payable in gold, persuading the marquis that suits against purchasers of small lots would delay collection; it was better to sell the whole to Monsieur Grandet, solvent and able to pay in ready money. The marquisate of Froidfond was accordingly conveyed into Monsieur Grandet’s hands, who, to Saumur’s astonishment, paid for it under proper discount and formalities.

This affair echoed from Nantes to Orléans. Monsieur Grandet took advantage of a cart returning by Froidfond to see his chateau. After casting a master’s eye over the property, he returned to Saumur, satisfied he had invested at five per cent, and seized by the stupendous thought of extending the marquisate by concentrating his property there. Then, to fill his coffers, now nearly empty, he resolved to thin his woods and sell the poplars in the meadows.

One now understands what is meant by “the house of Monsieur Grandet”: a cold, silent, pallid dwelling above the town, sheltered by ruined ramparts. The porte-cochere’s two pillars and arch were of tufa, that soft Loire stone that hardly lasts two centuries; irregular holes, weather-eaten, made the arch and walls look like vermiculated work, a sort of jail gateway. Above the arch, a crumbling bas-relief representing the four seasons was blackened; a projecting plinth had taken root with random growths—pellitory, bindweed, nettles, plantain, even a little cherry-tree.

The archway door was solid oak, brown, shrunken, and split, held by symmetrical iron bolts; a small square grating with rust-red bars filled the middle panel and served as the knocker’s aperture. Through that grating—originally for recognizing friends in civil war—one could see, at the far end of the dark slimy vault, a few broken steps leading to a garden shut in by thick damp walls that nourished sickly herbage. These walls were the ruined ramparts under which stretched the gardens of several neighboring houses.

The principal ground-floor room opened directly from the porte-cochere vault. In small towns of Anjou, Touraine, and Berry the hall is antechamber, salon, office, boudoir, and dining-room all in one; it is the theatre of domestic life. There the barber came twice a year to cut Monsieur Grandet’s hair; farmers, the curé, the under-prefect, and the miller’s boy came on business. The room, with two street windows, was entirely of wood: gray panels with ancient mouldings covered the walls; ceiling beams painted gray showed between whitewashed spaces yellowed with age. An old brass clock inlaid with arabesques adorned the mantel of the ill-cut white-stone chimney-piece; above hung a greenish mirror whose bevel reflected a thread of light along a gothic damascened steel frame. Two copper-gilt candelabra decorated the hearth corners; by removing their side branches each main stem served as a candlestick. Antique chairs were covered with La Fontaine tapestry, faded and darned.

Closets or buffets surmounted by dirty shelves stood at the hall’s corners. An old marquetry card-table, its top a chessboard, stood between the windows; above it hung an oval barometer with a black border enlivened by gilt bands where flies had damaged the gilding. Opposite the chimney-piece were two pastel portraits said to represent Madame Grandet’s grandfather, old Monsieur de la Bertelliere, as a lieutenant in the French guard, and the deceased Madame Gentillet in shepherdess guise. The windows were draped with red gros de Tours curtains held back by silken cords with ecclesiastical tassels—luxuries little in keeping with Monsieur Grandet’s habits but included in the house’s purchase along with the steel pier-glass, the tapestries, and the rosewood buffets.

By the window nearest the door stood a straw chair on castors to lift Madame Grandet high enough to see passers-by; a stained cherry-wood work-table filled the embrasure, and Eugenie Grandet’s little armchair stood beside it. For fifteen years the mother and daughter had lived a peaceful round: from April to November daytime work; on November 1 they took their winter station by the chimney. Grandet permitted a fire only from November 1 to March 31, whatever the weather; a foot-warmer filled with embers—the la Grande Nanon contrivance—saved mother and daughter from chilly mornings and evenings. They managed family linen and domestic tasks themselves; if Eugenie wished to embroider a collar she had to steal time from sleep and deceive her father for light. For a long time the miser dispensed tallow candles to his daughter and la Grande Nanon as he rationed bread and other necessities.

La Grande Nanon was perhaps the only person capable of willingly accepting her master’s despotism; the town envied the Grandets for possessing her. Called “la Grande” for her five-foot-eight height, she had lived with Monsieur Grandet thirty-five years. Though paid only sixty francs a year, she was reputed among Saumur’s serving-women to be one of the richest: those sixty francs, accumulated over thirty-five years, had enabled her to invest four thousand francs in an annuity with Maître Cruchot. That result, the fruit of long, patient economy and hard slavery, seemed gigantic to others who envied her without seeing what had been sacrificed.

At twenty-two she had been unable to find a situation because her face repelled most; robust, ready for labor, and forced to leave a farm that had burned down, she came to Saumur. Le Père Grandet, then set on marriage and establishing his household, noticed the girl. A cooper by trade and a good judge of strength, he appreciated the work to be had from a woman shaped like a Hercules—firm on her feet, strong in the hips, square in the back, with hands like a cartman and honesty as sound as her unblemished virtue. Her warts, brick-tinted skin, and ragged garments did not dismay him. He fed, shod, and clothed her, paid wages, and set her to work without treating her too roughly. Nanon, grateful and attached, wept and bound herself sincerely to him. From that day he ruled and worked her with feudal authority.

Nanon did everything: she cooked, made lye, washed linen in the Loire and carried it home on her shoulders; she rose early and slept late; she prepared vine-dressers’ food during harvest, watched market-people, protected her master’s property like a faithful dog, and obeyed his absurd exactions without murmur. In the famous year 1811, when the grapes were gathered with unheard-of difficulty, Grandet resolved to give Nanon his old watch—the first present in twenty years of service. Though he turned over old shoes as gifts in other years, the watch was notable only because anything like a token so rarely passed his hands. Necessity had made the poor girl as niggardly as her master, and he had grown to love her as one loves a dog; Nanon accepted his spiked collar of control whose spikes no longer pricked. If Grandet cut bread with parsimony, she made no complaint; she gladly shared the household’s severe regime in which no one was ever ill. She laughed when Grandet laughed, felt gloomy or chilly with him, warmed herself and toiled as he did. Never did he need to find fault with her for pilfering grapes, plums, or nectarines. “Come, fall-to, Nanon!” he would say in generous years.

To the peasant who had earned nothing but harsh treatment, Grandet’s ambiguous laugh was like a sunbeam. Nanon’s simple heart and narrow head could hold only one feeling and one idea: gratitude. For thirty-five years she never ceased to see herself before Monsieur Grandet’s wood-yard, ragged and barefoot, and to hear him say, “What do you want, young one?” Her gratitude was ever new. Sometimes Grandet, reflecting that she had never heard a flattering word and might one day appear before God more chaste than the Virgin Mary, would say, looking at her, “Poor Nanon!” The exclamation, always followed by an undefinable look from the old servant, became a chain of friendship nothing would part. Such compassion, arising in the miser and accepted gratefully by the old spinster, had something inconceivably horrible about it: this cruel pity was to Nanon the sum total of happiness.

Many households in Saumur treated servants better but received less satisfaction in return. People asked what the Grandets had done to make Nanon so attached. Her kitchen, whose barred windows looked into the court, was always clean, neat, and cold—a true miser’s kitchen where nothing went to waste. When Nanon had washed dishes, locked up dinner remains, and put out her fire, she left the kitchen and went to spin hemp beside her masters. One tallow candle sufficed the family for the evening. The servant slept at the end of the passage in a closet lit only by a fan-light; her robust health enabled her to live in this hole, one ear always open like a watchdog.

Other parts of the dwelling will be described as events require, but this sketch of the hall, where the household’s whole luxury appears, suggests the nakedness of the upper floors.

In 1819, at the beginning of a mid-November evening, La Grande Nanon lit the fire for the first time that season. The autumn had been fine. That day was a fête well known to the Cruchotines and the Grassinists: the six antagonists, armed at all points, prepared to meet at the Grandets and outdo each other in testimonials of friendship. That morning all Saumur had seen Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet, accompanied by Nanon, on their way to Mass; people remembered the day as Mademoiselle Eugenie’s birthday. Calculating when the family dinner would end, Maître Cruchot, the Abbé Cruchot, and Monsieur C. de Bonfons hastened to arrive before the des Grassins and be first to pay compliments. All three brought enormous bouquets gathered in their greenhouses; the president’s stalks were ingeniously wound with a white satin ribbon fringed with gold.

In the morning Monsieur Grandet, following his custom on Eugenie’s birth and fête days, went to her bedside and solemnly presented his paternal gift—which for thirteen years had been a curious gold-piece. Madame Grandet gave her a winter or summer dress as needed. These two dresses and the gold pieces, supplemented by two more on New Year’s and his fête, gave Eugenie a little revenue of about a hundred crowns, which Grandet loved to see her amass: was it not putting money from one strongbox to another, and training his heiress’s parsimony? He sometimes demanded an account of her treasure, formerly increased by the Bertelliere gifts, saying, “It is to be your marriage dozen.”

The “marriage dozen” is an old custom still practiced in parts of central France: when a young girl marries, her family, or that of the husband, gives her a purse containing, according to means, twelve pieces, or twelve dozen, or twelve hundred pieces of gold. The poorest shepherd-girl never marries without her dozen, even if only coppers. They still tell of a “dozen” presented to a rich heiress containing one hundred and forty-four portugaises d’or. The custom anchors the idea that immediate trinkets and ceremonial largesse belong to marriage rites.

During dinner, pleased to see Eugenie in a new gown, Grandet exclaimed, “As it is Eugenie’s birthday let us have a fire; it will be a good omen.”

“Mademoiselle will be married this year, that’s certain,” said la Grande Nanon, carrying away the remains of the goose.

“I don’t see anyone suitable for her in Saumur,” said Madame Grandet, glancing timidly at her husband and revealing the conjugal slavery under which she languished.

Grandet looked at his daughter and said gaily, “She is twenty-three years old to-day; we must soon begin to think of it.” Eugenie and her mother exchanged a look of intelligence.

Madame Grandet was a dry, thin woman, yellow as a quince, awkward and slow—one born to be trodden upon. She had big bones, a big nose, a big forehead, big eyes; her features suggested mealy fruits without savor. Her teeth were black and few; her mouth wrinkled; her chin long and pointed. Yet she was excellent—angelic sweetness, resignation as to suffering, rare piety, a good heart, and unalterable equanimity that made her universally pitied and respected. Her husband never gave her more than six francs at a time for personal expenses. Ridiculous as it seems, this woman—who by fortune and inheritances had brought Père Grandet more than three hundred thousand francs—felt so profoundly humiliated by dependence, and her gentleness prevented revolt, that she had never asked for a penny or remarked on deeds Maître Cruchot brought for her signature. This secret pride, this nobility of soul perpetually misunderstood and wounded by Grandet, governed her conduct.

Madame Grandet habitually wore a greenish levantine silk gown, making it last nearly a year; a large white cotton kerchief, a bonnet of plaited straw, and almost always a black-silk apron. She seldom left the house and never asked anything for herself. Grandet, seized with occasional remorse when he remembered how long since he had given her the last six francs, would stipulate “the wife’s pin-money” when he sold his yearly vintage. The four or five louis presented by a buyer were the chief visible signs of Madame Grandet’s annual revenues. But after receiving those, her husband would often say, as though their purse were common, “Can you lend me a few sous?” and the poor woman, glad to help the man her confessor upheld as her lord and master, would return several crowns in winter out of the “pin-money.” When Grandet drew from his pocket the five-franc piece he allowed monthly for Eugenie’s small expenses—thread, needles, and toilet—he never failed to ask as he buttoned his breeches’ pocket, “And you, mother, do you want anything?”

“My friend,” Madame Grandet would answer, moved by maternal dignity, “we will see about that later.” Wasted dignity—Grandet thought himself generous to his wife. Philosophers who meet the like of Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie may say irony lies at the bottom of Providence.

After dinner, when Nanon fetched a bottle of black-currant ratafia from Monsieur Grandet’s bed-chamber and nearly fell on the stairs, Grandet scolded, then—because it was Eugenie’s birthday and Nanon had been frightened—offered her a little glass. Seeing her pale, he promised to mend the step that had given way. He took candle and tools and went to patch the worm-eaten staircase, whistling songs of his youth while the three Cruchots knocked.

“Is it you, Monsieur Cruchot?” asked Nanon at the grating; she opened the door and the hearth’s light enabled the Cruchots to find their way into the room. Grandet, recognizing them, excused his haste: “I’ll be with you in a moment. I’m not proud; I am patching up a step on my staircase.” The president, always solemn, approved the sentiment in a phrase: “A man’s house is his castle.”

The president, the Abbé Cruchot, and Maître Cruchot arrived with bouquets. The president, profiting by the darkness, took Eugenie by the elbows and kissed each side of her neck with complacency that made her blush. The abbé kissed her hand. Maître Cruchot showed officious familiarity. Grandet, entering, declared: “As this is Eugenie’s birthday let us illuminate,” and with the parsimonious ritual of his household he removed the candelabra branches, set two new tallow candles with paper, and seated himself, looking alternately at friends, his daughter, and the two candles.

The Cruchots were pleased but uneasy, aware of rivalries. The des Grassins had not come when the group first assembled, but soon arrived: Madame des Grassins, a lively plump woman who kept her youth by provincial calm and virtuous habits; her husband, a former quartermaster of the Imperial guard, retired and swaggering; and their son Adolphe, tall, blond, pale and slight, who had spent sums in Paris beyond his allowance. They brought gifts: the father presented a rare Cape heather in a little box; Adolphe offered a workbox with silver-gilt utensils, monogrammed E.G. As Eugenie opened it, she felt unexpected delight, blushed and trembled, and looked to her father for permission. Monsieur Grandet authorized her to accept, the tone making the scene most theatrical: “Take it, my daughter.”

The three Cruchots felt crushed as they saw Adolphe’s success with Eugenie: such riches, even in small personal gifts, were unheard-of to her. Monsieur des Grassins offered Grandet snuff, adjusted the Legion of Honor ribbon in his buttonhole, and glanced with a certain insolence at the Cruchots. Madame des Grassins eyed the blue vases holding the Cruchot bouquets with satirical interest. The abbé, joining Grandet at the window embrasure, muttered in the miser’s ear that their rivals “throw money out of the windows.” Grandet shrugged: “What does that matter if it gets into my cellar?” The abbé suggested that if Grandet wanted to give gilt scissors to his daughter he had the means; Grandet replied he would give something better.

“Don’t stand on ceremony,” Madame des Grassins advised; “we will join you at cards.” Grandet suggested loto for all, the two young people included, and Nanon set the tables. Madame des Grassins succeeded in seating her son beside Eugenie. The party played, provided with numbered pasteboard and blue-glass counters, while each man’s thoughts pivoted on Monsieur Grandet’s supposed millions. The old cooper, inwardly self-conceited, regarded his guests—including Madame des Grassins’ pink feathers, the banker’s martial head, Adolphe, the president, and the notary—as useful harpoons to fish with. The trivial gaiety in the old gray room dimly lit by two tallow candles, the whirr of Nanon’s spinning-wheel, the sincere smiles only on Eugenie’s and her mother’s lips, the mingling of small domestic pleasures with important interests—all made the scene a melancholy comedy.

It is a drama of all times and places: a young girl, like certain birds valued by others, is lured by proofs of friendship she does not understand; three pure simple hearts—Nanon’s, Eugenie’s, and her mother’s—are inspired by tender sentiments, while the modern god, money, dominates through Grandet’s figure. Tender sentiments are secondary here; money’s power is manifest in the miser’s face. Eugenie and her mother knew nothing of Grandet’s real wealth; they estimated life’s things by the small glimmer of their pale ideas, used to doing without, ignorant and therefore capable of a certain contentment. Their feelings, bruised though they did not realize it, were the spring of their existence and made them exceptions among lives purely material. Frightful condition of the human race! There is no joy that does not come from some species of ignorance.

At the moment Madame Grandet had won a loto of sixteen sous—the largest ever pooled in that house—and while la Grande Nanon laughed as Madame pocketed her small winnings, the knocker resounded on the house-door so loudly the women jumped in their chairs.

“There is no man in Saumur who would knock like that,” said the notary.

“How can they bang in that way!” exclaimed Nanon; “do they want to break in the door?”

“Who the devil is it?” cried Grandet.

Nanon took a candle and opened the door; Grandet followed. Madame Grandet, alarmed, ran to the room crying “Grandet! Grandet!” The loto‑players exchanged looks.

“Suppose we all go?” said Monsieur des Grassins; “that knock strikes me as ill‑omened.”

A young man entered, accompanied by a coach‑office porter with two trunks and a carpet‑bag. Grandet, sharp, ordered his wife back to the loto: “Madame Grandet, leave me to speak with monsieur.” He shut the door; the players paused.

Speculation ran: “From Saumur?” “A traveller.” “Paris, no doubt,” said the notary. Eugénie noticed Nanon’s absence. Adolphe whispered that the newcomer might be M. Grandet’s son—Monsieur Charles—whom he had seen at a ball. When Grandet returned with the traveller, he strutted before his guests.

“Sit near the fire,” Grandet said. The stranger saluted; the men inclined their heads, the women bowed. Madame Grandet tried to offer refreshment, but Grandet brusquely cut her off. The stranger thanked Eugénie—“Thank you, my cousin, but I dined at Tours”—and declined refreshment.

“From Paris?” Madame des Grassins asked. The young man was Monsieur Charles Grandet of Paris. With an eye‑glass he inspected the table and company and impertinently ogled Madame des Grassins. The loto numbers were called languidly as the guests watched Charles and Eugénie with curiosity.

Charles, twenty‑two and handsome, contrasted sharply with the provincial regulars. He was a thorough Parisian dandy: a full hunting costume, the finest gun, multiple waistcoats, Buisson coats, fine linen, gold trinkets—“implements of a man of fashion”—from a little whip to chased pistols. Though his father had warned against ostentation, Charles had taken a private coupe, had his hair refreshed at Tours, put on a black satin cravat and round collar, showed crossed cashmere waistcoats and a gold‑chain watch, gray trousers with black seams, gloves, and a cane with a gold knob. Only a Parisian could wear such foppery with a dashing air.

The Cruchots and Grassins, shabby, snuff‑taking provincials, stared as if at a giraffe. Monsieur des Grassins and his son, more versed in fashion, mocked him with satirical looks. Grandet, absorbed in a long letter and holding the only candle at the card table, paid no heed. Eugénie, who had mended her father’s clothes and never seen such elegance, found Charles almost seraphic: she inhaled the scent of his curls, envied his soft gloves and refined features, and watched him as if he were an engraving come to life. His manners, eye‑glass, and disdainful glance enchanted her; for the first time she dreamed of a phoenix cousin.

The game ended slowly. La Grande Nanon entered to ask for monsieur’s bed sheets; Madame Grandet followed. Madame des Grassins suggested they stop and keep their stakes. The guests gathered near the fire.

Moved by a girl’s instinct, Eugénie slipped away to prepare her cousin’s room. She arranged a warming‑pan, covered a table, insisted on fresh linens and a good fire, and decorated the chimneypiece with a lacquer tray, a crystal goblet, a gilt spoon, and an antique flask. She even offered a five‑franc piece meant for expenses to buy a wax candle and a little sugar. Madame Grandet protested—“What will your father say?”—but Eugénie coaxed Nanon with the plea that it was her cousin’s birthday. Nanon laughed and obeyed, though alert to the master’s scrutiny.

Meanwhile Madame des Grassins made overtures to Charles, flattering him into conversation and advising that Saumur could be pleasant if he avoided being shut up with the miserly uncle, the pious aunt, and the uneducated cousin. Monsieur des Grassins laughed; the notary, the president, and the abbé made discreet remarks. The Abbé Cruchot slyly suggested that Madame des Grassins should show Saumur’s honors to the visitor. Adolphe introduced himself—“I had the honor of dancing vis‑à‑vis with you at Baron de Nucingen’s ball”—which pleased Charles.

Charles, unbuttoning his coat and striking a theatrical pose, seemed to think he might succeed in Saumur. The des Grassins and the Cruchots traded witticisms and innuendo: he ogled the salon’s beauties, Madame des Grassins considered him promising marriage material, the abbé hinted at coquetries and social duties, and Adolphe might have a rival.

All the while Père Grandet’s attention flickered over a single letter he read by candlelight. The notary and the president, sensing his preoccupation, watched him. The letter, from Victor‑Ange‑Guillaume Grandet in Paris to his brother, announced a catastrophe: Victor declared he could not survive the disgrace of bankruptcy. Failures of his broker and notary had consumed his resources, leaving debts near four million francs and assets perhaps a quarter of that. The wine stock had fallen in value; in three days Paris would shout his dishonor. Unable to endure the infamy, he bewailed the ruin he left his adored son and, foreseeing his end, begged his elder brother to be a father to Charles. He entreated that his son be treated gently and provided means to enter trade or the Indies. Victor confessed he had nothing to leave and begged his brother to obtain from Charles a renunciation of any claim on his estate; if the brother refused, remorse would follow; if he accepted, a prayer would bless him. The letter closed with a blessing and a dying man’s trust.

Grandet folded the letter, slipped it into his waistcoat, and masked his feelings behind humble airs and hidden calculation. Recovering composure, he brusquely asked his nephew if he had warmed. “Thoroughly, my dear uncle,” Charles answered. Grandet, already thinking of practicalities, told him Nanon would show his room: “It isn’t a dandy’s room; but you will excuse a poor wine‑grower who never has a penny to spare. Taxes swallow up everything.”

The des Grassins rose to leave—“We do not wish to intrude, Grandet; you may want to talk to your nephew”—and offered parting bows. The notary fetched his lantern to accompany them. Madame des Grassins, who had not expected the evening to end early because her servant was absent, accepted the abbé’s arm at first but preferred her son; the abbé, indignant but playful, ended up walking with her husband’s party.

“He is a good‑looking young man,” the abbé whispered to Madame des Grassins. “Good‑bye to the grapes—Eugénie will belong to the dandy. Unless this cousin is enamoured of some Parisian woman, your son Adolphe will find another rival—”

“Not at all,” she answered coolly; she thought Eugénie plain and expected no danger. “This young man cannot fail to see that Eugénie is a little fool.”

“Always place yourself beside Eugénie, madame, and you need never speak ill of her; the young man will make his own comparisons.”

Madame des Grassins had already invited Charles to dine with her in two days. As the Cruchots and Grassins dispersed, they analyzed the evening with provincial acumen, sensing altered alliances. Usually rivals, they perceived the need for a temporary truce to oppose a common threat: they must hinder Eugénie from falling for her cousin and prevent the cousin from being taken in by opportunistic comparisons. They contemplated launching “treacherous insinuations, soft‑spoken calumnies, slanders full of faint praise and artless denials” to mislead him—an alliance of petty strategies to preserve their positions in Saumur society.

When the four relatives were left alone, Monsieur Grandet told his nephew it was too late to discuss business and that they would settle things the next day. He outlined the household routine—breakfast at eight, a light midday repast, dinner at five—and refused any invitation to accompany Charles into town, blaming work. He shrugged off talk of wealth, calling himself practically penniless and still working like an apprentice. Nanon brought a candle; Madame Grandet offered any help Charles might need. Charles, polite and amused, accepted a faded yellow Anjou candle from Nanon, and Grandet led him through the passage beside the kitchen rather than the hall door, stopping at an inner swing-door against the north wind. Nanon bolted the outer door and loosed the wolf-dog, whose muffled bark warned strangers.

Ascending worm-eaten stairs, Charles’s hopes sank as he saw smoke-stained walls and creaking steps. He mistook his aunt’s and cousin’s smiles for friendliness. At the landing he noticed three Etruscan-red doors; one above the kitchen was effectively walled off and only reachable through Grandet’s bedchamber. That room served as his office and strong-room: an iron-barred window, scales for weighing louis, hidden places for deeds and cash, and the solitary key in the miser’s hand. Grandet kept his accounts there like an alchemist guarding treasures; none, not even his wife, might enter.

Eugenie’s door faced the walled room; Madame Grandet’s opened next to hers. Grandet gave Charles lodging in the attic above his own room so he could overhear him if need be. After brief goodnights, Grandet showed Charles the attic. Nanon had made a warming-pan fire, which the miser deemed unnecessary and scolded her for. Charles, surprised by the shabby comforts, unpacked in a room with moth-eaten valances, a cold stone fireplace, yellow-wood chairs, a simple night-table, and a thin carpet. He chatted easily with Nanon—who, astonished by his silk dressing-gown, joked it would make a fine altar-cloth and daydreamed of embroidery and damask—while Eugenie dreamed of love.

That night Grandet brooded. He resented that his brother had left him this nephew and fretted that he had not even fifty francs to give the young man. He pictured the nephew’s finery as a burden. Madame Grandet, practiced in reading her husband’s moods, feigned indifference and kept quiet.

At dawn Eugenie awoke with unfinished prayers and a new awareness. For the first time she wished to appear attractive: she brushed and arranged her hair, smoothed her stockings, and put on a new gown that pleased her. Sitting by the window she studied the court and narrow garden—a well twined with vine, neatly stacked wood, lichened paving, disjointed steps, a rotten trellis, two stunted apple-trees, gravel walks, box-edged beds, and a terrace of old walls leading to lindens and raspberry-bushes, with a drooping walnut near the miser’s sanctum. Under the autumn sun the thawed frost and falling leaves stirred a tenderness in her; she feared she was too plain for Charles, though her modest beauty had an unassuming nobility she did not fully see.

Flushed, Eugenie went to ask Nanon for cream and a galette for her cousin’s breakfast. Nanon laughed at the plan, fretting about supplies and Grandet’s stinginess, and playfully scolded her. Grandet came down amid the fuss and a discussion about provisions followed: he insisted that if flour and butter were used, all baking must be done in one oven to save wood. Nanon, in a small rebellion, persuaded him to spare enough for a galette by promising to manage the fires. She admired Charles’s fine boots and linen with wonder and envy; Grandet grumbled that some people put more money into boots than into the men who wore them.

Grandet then took Eugenie for a morning walk to his fields by the Loire, accompanied by Maitre Cruchot, the notary, who knew Grandet’s eye for profit. On the riverbank, where thirty men were clearing poplars, Grandet explained his calculations—how removing poplars reclaimed hay and profit—and showed where to plant cheaper poplars on poor land to save nourishment. Cruchot marveled at his knack for turning trees into tens of thousands of francs.

When Cruchot mentioned the talk in Saumur about the dandy nephew from Paris and the need for a marriage-contract, Grandet declared bluntly that he would rather fling his daughter into the Loire than give her to that cousin. The cold refusal struck Eugenie like a blow: her budding hopes, tied to Charles since the previous evening, withered. She returned trembling, the light drained from the street. At the house, Cruchot showed Grandet a newspaper: Monsieur Grandet of Paris had shot himself after being ruined by his broker and notary. The notary feared the Saumur Grandet had earlier refused aid; Grandet merely said he had known, and that the son—Charles—didn’t yet know. Cruchot departed, understanding the reserve.

At breakfast, Madame Grandet and Eugenie sat knitting. Nanon reported Charles still asleep. Grandet, cold and harsh, announced the suicide: “His father has blown his brains out.” Eugenie wept at the thought of the poor young man; Nanon sympathized, but Grandet scoffed, saying the young man wasn’t worth a sou. He ordered that no one speak to Charles of the news until he returned at noon; he intended to talk with the nephew about his affairs then and warned Eugenie that Charles would be sent off to the Indies and she would never see him again.

Alone, Eugenie flung herself upon her mother, gasping that she was suffocating. The two exchanged a brief, intuitive tenderness. Madame Grandet, aware of her daughter’s emotion, counseled restraint, asking whether Eugenie already loved him; Eugenie protested that liking and the desire to help were not wrong. The women silently conspired to make Charles’s breakfast special. Eugenie pleaded for cream and strong coffee; Nanon volunteered to run and buy coffee, fearful of being seen by Grandet. She arranged the table: grapes from the attic, a pyramid of pears, vine leaves for presentation, listening at Charles’s door to his sleeping breaths. Nanon returned with two fresh eggs she’d begged from a farmer; Eugenie embraced her in gratitude.

Thus the household prepared in small furtive joys—Nanon’s schemes for paltry luxuries, the mother’s silent understanding, Eugenie’s innocent coquetry—while Grandet’s economy and dominant will cast a shadow. The possible marriage, the news from Paris, and Grandet’s firm determination to keep his daughter’s fortune from Charles intertwine, kindling inner turmoil: Eugenie’s tender awakening and anguish, Grandet’s miserly calculations and contempt, and the private locked world of the strong-room where gold and papers are jealously guarded. Each character’s habits and moods set the stage for conflicts to come, with love and pity on one side and avarice and pride on the other.

After two hours of anxious preparation—rising often to watch the coffee and listen for her cousin—Eugenie set a modest breakfast, a departure from their usual midday meal of bread, fruit, a little butter, and wine. She fretted that her father might return before Charles had eaten and checked the clock repeatedly. “Don’t be troubled, Eugenie; if your father comes in, I will take it all upon myself,” Madame Grandet soothed, and Eugenie wept with sudden gratitude and love for her mother.

Charles arrived about eleven, dressed with Parisian elegance, smiling and graceful; his presence quickened Eugenie’s heart. He joked about the ruin of his Anjou estate and sank into an easy-chair while the women—provincial, habit-bound—talked of church walks and haymakers. Accustomed to luxury, Charles admired small comforts like fresh eggs, butter, and a pretty watch; the women were tender with him, and he returned their kindness with caressing looks that stirred Eugenie, who blushed when she handled his ring.

Charles teased with vanity and good humor. He laughed at their boiled coffee in a plain pot and suggested a Chaptal coffee-pot, which Nanon dismissed as impractical when there were cows to feed. Eugenie offered to make it, earning a reproachful glance from her father even in his absence. As the three women grew silent with apprehension—each expecting Grandet’s return—the familiar knock came and Monsieur Grandet entered. His sharp eye took in the spread and the guest; with dry sarcasm he accused them of feasting.

“Ha! ha! so you have been making a feast for your nephew; very good!” he said, then, taking a glass, cut bread, spread butter from his own knife, and ate standing. He noticed the sugar and demanded to know its source; Nanon said it was bought from Fessard. Charles, tasting bitter coffee, looked for sugar, but Grandet had already pocketed it. He suggested adding milk instead. Eugenie, quietly bold, replaced the saucer her father had stowed away and offered grapes and preserves to Charles. Grandet scorned such extravagance and warned that Charles must not “revolutionize” his household with spendthrift habits.

When Grandet announced he had “something to tell” Charles and led him into the garden, all three women read the seriousness in his tone. In the garden, after pacing and noting box-borders and gnarled trees—images that seared into Charles’s memory—Grandet revealed that Charles’s father had shot himself. The newspapers, he said, were full of it, and he thrust an article into the young man’s hands. Charles, inexperienced in grief, burst into tears—a display Grandet welcomed, claiming it proved the fellow’s “sensibleness” and would keep him practical rather than indulgent in mourning.

Worse followed: Grandet declared that the father had failed and ruined the family, leaving Charles penniless. He explained, mercilessly, that to “fail” was a dishonorable crime—worse than highway robbery—because a bankrupt leaves nothing and hides behind the law, thus compromising honor. Eugenie was crushed; she believed her father’s harsh equation of failure with fraud. The young man’s sobs rang through the house; he fled to his room, grieving convulsively, while the women wept.

Downstairs Grandet admonished his wife and daughter for indulgence, complaining about the breakfast and insisting he would not pay for “stuffing a pauper nephew.” He announced he would only pay as far as Nantes for Charles to go to the West Indies to try to make a fortune. Eugenie threw herself into his arms in grateful affection, briefly softening the miser’s conscience. He lectured her about millions and Napoleons—fussing over figures, incapable of feeling beyond arithmetic. Madame Grandet suggested prayers and neuvaines; Grandet scoffed about spending and francs not growing on bushes.

A muffled cry upstairs made Grandet order Nanon to ensure Charles was not suicidal; then he left to see departing Dutchmen and to speak with Cruchot. Once the door closed, Eugenie and her mother breathed easier. Eugenie, newly aware of her father’s nature and of the world’s complexities—millions, vineyard sales, purchases—tried to reconcile these facts with her image of him. She asked about louis in a cask of wine and learned that sales sometimes fetched a hundred to two hundred francs, and that Cruchot had mentioned the possible purchase of Froidfond as a strain on the family.

Nanon reported that Charles lay like a calf on his bed, crying—a sight she judged a blessing. Eugenie’s sympathy had deepened toward a dawning passion; she wanted to console him. Madame Grandet warned, “you will love him!” but Eugenie, feeling the stirrings of womanhood, insisted. In Charles’s room she found him prostrate, moaning that if his father had confided his troubles he might have helped repair them. His plain, sincere sorrow moved both women; he lamented his father’s “failure” and implored God’s forgiveness.

Eugenie counseled, “Take courage! Your loss is irreparable; therefore think only of saving your honor,” urging him to focus on what remained within his control. Charles, in a burst of anguished pride, declared his honor the one thing left to protect, prayed forgiveness for his father, and sought solitude. The women withdrew, sewing in silence downstairs. Eugenie had noted the contrast in his chamber—signs of luxury like a gold-embossed razor and a kept dressing-case—making him paradoxically more attractive. The household was shaken: Eugenie newly conscious of love and independent thought, Madame Grandet torn between piety and maternal concern, and Grandet unmoved by sentiment, already calculating the crisis’s effect on money and household order. Charles’s sincere, consuming grief remained, while Grandet’s harsh judgment of bankruptcy began to alter how the family viewed him.

About four o’clock an abrupt knock at the door struck Madame Grandet’s heart. Grandet entered radiant; he rubbed his hands, and his secret escaped him: he had sold the vintage—two hundred francs a puncheon to a Belgian, half down, paid in gold. He produced six louis for his wife. Eugenie, calling him “little one,” asked if there had been a thousand puncheons; when he confirmed it, she calculated two hundred thousand francs and urged him to help Charles. The old miser’s rage, cold and concentrated, flared. He had hardly remembered his nephew until Eugenie had put the sum into the account.

He scolded his daughter for presuming to advise him and threatened to send her to the Abbaye des Noyers if she spoke of Charles again. Madame Grandet said Charles was weeping for his father. The old man, after a few embarrassed turns, went to his secret den to think over an investment: he had amassed large sums from selling timber and other estate produce and could now add the two hundred thousand from the wine sale. The twenty percent that Cruchot promised in the Funds tempted him; he ran calculations on the newspaper margin, already imagining future gains and hearing his nephew’s moans without really listening.

Nanon warned that Charles wouldn’t eat; Grandet replied coolly, “So much saved.” Dinner was eaten in silence. Madame Grandet suggested they put on mourning. Grandet jeered that mourning is a matter of the heart, not of clothes, and suggested merely a hat-band out of the six louis. Eugenie lifted her eyes to heaven but said nothing; the evening passed monotonously. Eugenie sewed, Madame Grandet knitted, Grandet brooded in calculations that would astonish Saumur the next day. No one visited; all Saumur buzzed with the news of the sale, the brother’s failure, and the nephew’s arrival. Nanon’s spinning was the only sound beneath the gray rafters.

When they went to bed Grandet offered to bid his nephew good-night and see if he would take anything. He found the young man weeping and criticized him; he sneered at the presence of a wax candle in Charles’s room, jeering that spendthrifts would tear down his ceilings to boil the fellow’s eggs. Mother and daughter, frightened, slipped back to their rooms.

Madame Grandet prayed for her husband’s soul; he cursed her prayer. The narrative pauses to reflect on miserliness as a creed that makes the present all and dismisses belief in a future life. The miser’s fixation on earthly gain hardens the heart and undermines social and moral life: institutions, books, and men conspire to weaken belief, and money comes to govern everything—laws, politics, and morals. This modern doctrine, spreading from bourgeoisie to populace, poses grave danger to society.

Grandet, uneasy, told his wife to wait; he would speak to her in the morning. Madame Grandet, terrified like a schoolboy before the master, tried to stifle sound, and Eugenie, in her nightgown, kissed her brow and promised to tell her father that she had used the wax candle; her mother warned that Grandet would send her to Noyers, so she should let her manage things. Eugenie listened and then, restless, slept fitfully, waking at intervals to hear her cousin’s sighs and sometimes imagining he was dying. Towards morning she heard a cry and ran in the dawn to his room. Charles slept dressed in an armchair; the candle had burned to the socket. Seeing him, his grief-stamped face and swollen eyes moved her to tears. He perceived her pity, and, half-awake, asked pardon for his state. She told him there were hearts to hear him and advised him to go to bed. She left, ashamed and happy; innocence and naïveté made the encounter seem like an espousal to her. Back in her chamber she reproached herself, fearing Charles would think she loved him—which inwardly she wished him to think.

At breakfast the next morning they waited anxiously for Grandet. He kissed Eugenie and took his hat, saying he would loiter about the market to find Cruchot. Grandet, a poor sleeper, often spent nights in calculations; his life was a constant exercise of power for self, a compact, intelligent egotism. He craved to play a commercial game and to win money legally, to impose upon others and feel superior. During the night his thoughts had shifted: instead of a simple charitable act, he had concocted a plot to save his brother’s honor and profit at the same time. He would invest his own funds for years and manage the Saumur property so as to appear generous to Charles without spending a penny. He resolved to draw the Parisians to him and stage a comedy that would make him the town’s admired man on the morrow at minimal cost—the Cruchots were necessary for the illusion; but he would not go to them; he would make them come and then play the good uncle cheaply.

In his absence Eugenie busied herself openly with Charles, spending wealth of pity. She checked on his breathing, arranged his breakfast—cream, eggs, fruits—each item a special care. She asked whether he would eat downstairs or in his room and, fearing to annoy her father, chose to bring it up to him. She directed Nanon to prepare Charles’s room; the old staircase, usually grim, seemed luminous to her: it had a voice and spoke young. Her mother lent herself to the child’s love; both women sat with Charles and kept him company. They drew from religion little sophistries to justify their conduct—Christian charity making consolation a duty. Charles, used to Paris society and expecting indifference, was touched by their gentle friendship; he revealed gratitude in tears and kissed Eugenie’s hand.

Eugenie’s simplicities—arranging linen, admiring his silver, pouring his coffee—so struck him that he could not hide his emotion. Her ingenuous interest seemed like splendor to him and made him admire her innocence. When she handed him candlesticks and turned away blushing, both felt the same sentiment: the future was theirs. This soft emotion was precious amid his grief because it was wholly unexpected.

Grandet returned with several choices from the park—hare, partridges, eels, pike—sent as tribute by the millers, and told Nanon to prepare them for dinner. He announced he had invited the two Cruchots. Nanon, taken aback, wondered how they would manage spices and lard. Grandet contemptuously dismissed the keeper of Froidfond when he came seeking a promised indemnity; he promised to see about it tomorrow and gave his wife five francs. Madame Grandet, relieved to buy peace for eleven francs, slipped ten francs to the keeper. Nanon, who wanted only three francs to buy provisions for the dinner, said the household would make up the rest.

The household bustled with an unusual liveliness: this was only the third time since their marriage that Grandet had given a dinner. At four o’clock, after they had set the table for six and Grandet had brought up bottles of good wine, Charles came down pale, with gestures and voice full of graceful sadness. Misery had leveled social distance: Charles was no longer the rich Parisian dandy but a relation in dreadful misery. Women are drawn to suffering, and in that equality Eugenie loved him the more. The two spoke with their eyes; Charles was proud, calm, silent, touched by the gentle, unexpected sympathy of his aunt and cousin. Eugenie’s caressing glances pulled him from sorrow to fields of hope; she loved to keep him at her side.

Throughout the day Grandet’s mind worked on the plot that would let him appear generous while spending as little as possible. He planned to decoy the Cruchots into coming, to have a public scene of charity that cost him no real sacrifice, and to invest his money elsewhere for lucrative returns. His behavior toward Charles would be determined by the economy of appearance rather than by genuine benevolence. The family’s outward show of compassion—Eugenie’s tenderness and Madame Grandet’s small sacrifices—contrasted with the master’s calculating heart.

Eugenie’s sudden awakening of emotional life, born of a simple sheltered upbringing, explains the naïveté and depth of her pity. Her entire inner world, having known little variety, magnified the importance of a single act of compassion: a young woman entering a young man’s chamber, preparing his breakfast, holding his hand—acts that, to certain souls, bear the meaning of espousals. Once her innocence had revealed itself, she could scarcely stand steady under the excitement. The novel’s narrator remarks that certain actions are only improbable when one fails to illuminate the psychological impulses behind them; here, Eugenie’s life makes her passionate and impulsive.

The dinner proceeded. Grandet’s guests included the two Cruchots—the Parisians whose presence would lend the old man the public recognition he craved. The household prepared with care and Nanon hustled about. The scene set the stage for the old miser’s petty comedy of generosity: he would host, make a show of kindness, but his heart and thought would be on his funds and future gain. He was already envisioning investments and the multiplication of his fortune.

Charles’s simple gratitude, his beauty in suffering, and his manners endeared him to the two women. Eugenie’s small acts of service—folding linen, arranging his toilet, handling his silver—proved potent. Her gestures were not self-conscious; they revealed the pure superiority of the woman who chooses to minister to suffering. For Charles, the unexpected tenderness of these provincial women contrasted with his experience in Paris and lent him the courage to endure the humiliation of dependence.

Thus the day closed on an uneasy harmony. The simple family life—Madame Grandet’s prayers and anxieties, Nanon’s spinning, the old man’s computations—continued in the same house, but a new drama had begun: love and pity stirred in Eugenie, a calcu-lating masquerade of generosity had been planned by Grandet, and Charles, reduced from his Parisian sphere, found in the humble household a tenderness that touched him more deeply than the world he had known. The dinner would be the next step in the old man’s plot to be admired by Saumur while spending as little as possible, and the intimacy that had grown between cousin and cousin would deepen under the gray rafters and the watchful eyes of a miser who measured even a wax candle by its cost.

Saumur buzzed more about the dinner Grandet gave the Cruchots than it had about his vintage sale the night before—though that sale had affronted the wine-growers. Had the politic old miser entertained from nobler motives he might have been praised; but Grandet felt superior to the townspeople and cared little for their opinion.

The des Grassins had learned of Guillaume Grandet’s failure and violent death and decided to call that evening to commiserate and to find out why Grandet had invited the Cruchots. At five Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle the notary arrived in Sunday clothes. They ate with appetite; Grandet was grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, Madame Grandet reserved. When they rose, Charles excused himself to begin a long, painful correspondence.

Once aunt and uncle were sure Charles could not overhear, Grandet dismissed his wife and daughter. Alone with the two Cruchots, he put on an extra layer of cunning—affecting a stutter and some deafness, tricks he used to steer conversation and avoid responsibility—so his real intentions would remain doubtful.

He began, stammering, about bankruptcy. Flattered by Grandet’s awkward respect, the president took the lead: a threatened merchant might avoid the dishonor of insolvency by liquidating—creditors could sell notes at a reduction, and a friend who bought them might arrange with creditors, pay off the debts, and thus free the estate honorably. Grandet professed ignorance of Paris tricks and said he tended vines and drains; he admitted he might help his nephew but professed he had neither time nor knowledge to engage personally.

The president—smooth, fond of snuff—elaborated, likening notes to merchandise and invoking abstract reasoning (Bentham). He suggested debts might be bought for twenty-five percent; a friend could go to Paris, buy up notes, secure extensions, and salvage the family’s honor. Grandet pretended distress at the complexity and expense, but his eye betrayed eagerness.

The notary, uneasy, warned his nephew to let Monsieur Grandet explain. Before Cruchot could resume, the des Grassins arrived with condolence. The notary seized the occasion to announce Grandet’s honorable intention to liquidate his brother’s affairs and that the president had offered to go to Paris to negotiate. The des Grassins, who had been gossiping on the way about the old man’s avarice—almost accusing him of fratricide—were astonished and delighted.

“Grandet is honorable to the backbone,” cried the banker, embracing him. The scene let the des Grassins present themselves as businesslike friends. The banker suggested the matter required a consummate businessman and offered to take charge, since he was going to Paris.

Dropping some of his assumed hesitancy, Grandet privately confided to des Grassins that he trusted the banker more than the president and had other plans. He wanted to buy a few thousand francs in the Funds while the price was low; the des Grassins knew how and could make the purchase by month’s end. He signaled secrecy; the banker, pleased, promised to begin by mail-post and to take final directions at five o’clock, just before dinner.

The parties lingered; des Grassins struck Grandet on the shoulder, murmuring how fortunate he was to have such a relation. Grandet, rubbing his wen, declared his love for his brother and his intention to prove it—unless costs prevented him. The des Grassins had cut the Cruchots out of the commission; the president, disappointed at being sidelined, complained to his uncle that he had begun as “de Bonfons” and ended as nothing but Cruchot. The notary counseled modesty: let the des Grassins take this tack, and Eugenie would remain the president’s wife all the same.

Soon the news of Grandet’s magnanimity spread through three houses and then the town. Everyone forgave his sale that defied pledged good faith and lauded his supposed generosity; the town was quick to forget and quick to praise.

When Père Grandet shut the door he called Nanon and instructed her not to let the dog loose and not to go to bed; they had work to do. At eleven Cornoiller would bring the chariot from Froidfond; Nanon was to tell him to come softly, for there must be no noise and no curious neighbors. Grandet returned to his private room, moving cautiously so as not to wake his wife and daughter and, above all, not to rouse his nephew, whom he already began to condemn for his pale, despairing looks.

About midnight Eugenie, anxious for Charles, thought she heard a cry and was about to go out when she saw Nanon’s heavy steps and heard horses. Peering into the corridor she met her father’s vague, unnoticing gaze and was terrified. The goodman and Nanon were yoked together by a stout stick; a rope over their shoulders carried a small keg like those Grandet made in the bakehouse—filled, Nanon complained, with heavy copper sous. Grandet cautioned them about the candlestick; the single candle on the staircase lit the scene.

Cornoiller had come with horses. Grandet told him there was nothing to fear, asked about pistols, and instructed Nanon to tell his wife he had gone into the country and would be back to dinner. The chariot drove off in secrecy; Nanon bolted the great door, let the dog loose, and went to bed with a bruised shoulder—neighbors unaware of the departure or its purpose. Grandet’s precautions and reticence were habitual; no one had ever seen a penny in that house, though it was filled with gold.

In the morning, port gossip said exchange on gold had doubled because of military preparations at Nantes and speculators had come to Angers to buy coin. The old wine-grower, having borrowed horses from his farmers, had seized the chance: he had gone to sell his gold and to bring back treasury notes, converting and increasing the sum he intended to put into the Funds by the advantage of the exchange.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
“My father has gone,” thought Eugenie when the news reached her from below. The house fell silent; a carriage’s rumble faded. A cry came from her cousin’s room. A thin strip of light slanted across the rotten stair.

“He suffers!” she cried, and ran up. A second moan stopped her on the landing. The door stood ajar; she pushed it open. Charles slept with his head drooped over the arm of an old chair, the pen fallen from his hand nearly touching the floor. His posture alarmed her; she entered quickly.

“He must be very tired,” she said to herself, and glanced at a dozen sealed letters on the table—carriage-makers, a tailor—proof he had been settling affairs for leaving France. Two letters lay open. The heading “My dear Annette” stunned her. Her heart beat; her feet felt nailed.

“His dear Annette! He loves! He is loved! No hope! What does he say to her?” Thoughts swarmed. “I ought to go away—What if I do read it?” She lingered beside him, gently set his head against the chair back; he rested like a sleeping child. She raised his drooping hand and kissed his chestnut hair. “Dear Annette!” a demon shrieked.

Torn between conscience and curiosity, she yielded to passion. As she read, the keen glow of first love made everything vital.

My dear Annette,—Nothing could have separated us but the great misfortune that has fallen on me. My father has killed himself; his fortune and mine are irretrievably lost. I am an orphan at an age when, by my education, I am still a child; yet I must lift myself as a man out of the abyss. If I wish to leave France an honest man—I am without a hundred francs to try my luck in the Indies or America. Yes, Anna, I must seek my fortune in those deadly climates. I cannot remain in Paris…think of owing two millions! I should be killed in a duel the first week; therefore I shall not return. Your love—the most tender and devoted love—cannot draw me back. I have no money to go to you, to give and receive a last kiss that might strengthen me.

“Poor Charles! I did well to read that. I have gold; I will give it to him,” Eugenie thought, wiping her eyes and reading on.

I have never thought of the miseries of poverty. If I had the hundred louis for the journey, I have not a sou for an outfit. But no—I have not the hundred louis, not even one. I don’t know what will be left after I have paid my debts in Paris. If I have nothing, I shall go quietly to Nantes and ship as a common sailor; I will begin in the new world like other men who started with nothing and made fortunes...I feel more courage than is supposed for a careless young man—above all, one used to caressing; oh, my father! Annette, he is dead!

I have thought of my position and yours. I have grown old in twenty-four hours. Dear Anna, if to keep me with you in Paris you were to sacrifice your luxury, your dress, your opera-box, we should still not have enough for my extravagant ways. Besides, I would never accept such sacrifices. No, we must part now and forever—

“He gives her up! Blessed Virgin! What happiness!” Eugenie quivered with joy; Charles stirred. He did not wake, so she read on.

When shall I return? I do not know. The climate of the West Indies ages a European who works hard. In ten years your daughter will be eighteen; she may be your companion, your spy. Society will be cruel, daughters sometimes cruel; remember four years of happiness and, if you can, be faithful to my memory. I cannot demand faithfulness, for I must adapt to my new life and take a commonplace view of it. Therefore marriage becomes a necessity; I have found here in Saumur, in my uncle’s house, a cousin whose face, manners, mind, and heart would please you, and who, besides, seems to me—

The letter broke off; Charles had fallen asleep again. Eugenie excused him. How could an innocent girl suspect cold-heartedness in such a letter? Young, piously brought-up girls see love’s light everywhere; they reflect warmth on their lover and attribute to him their highest thoughts. Eugenie’s simple heart read “My dear Annette” as the sweetest words; the tears at the young man’s lashes seemed noble. How could she know that Charles, though he loved his father and mourned him, was moved more by paternal indulgence than heroic resolve? Monsieur and Madame Guillaume Grandet had gratified every whim of their son, shielding him from the hard reckonings other sons face.

Charles, a Parisian youth schooled by society and Annette in the prudence of the world, had learned to calculate; beneath his youth was a habit of sizing up a friend’s purse. After folly and adulation, Annette had made him think soberly; with a perfumed hand in his curls she had spoken of his future, effeminized and materialized him. Such outward sense often wins a young woman’s trust. Eugenie, without suspicion, heard the last stirrings of his conscience in these lines, laid down the letter full of love, and smiled, watching him sleep. She vowed to love him always. Then she opened the other letter to seek further proof of his noble qualities.

My dear Alphonse,—When you receive this letter I shall be without friends; but while I doubt the friendship of the world, I have never doubted yours. I beg you to settle my affairs and get as much as you can out of my possessions. You know my situation. I have nothing left, and I intend to go at once to the Indies. I have written to those I think I owe money, and enclose a list. My books, furniture, pictures, horses, etc., ought to pay my debts. I do not wish to keep anything, except perhaps a few baubles for my outfit. I will send a power of attorney so you can make these sales. Send me all my weapons. Keep Briton for yourself; nobody would pay his value, and I would rather give him to you—like a mourning-ring bequeathed by a dying man to his executor. Farry, Breilmann, & Co. built me a travelling-carriage not yet delivered; persuade them to keep it and not ask for payment. If they refuse, do what you can and avoid anything dishonorable. I owe the British Islander six louis lost at cards; don’t fail to pay him—

“Dear cousin!” whispered Eugenie, and silently left the room with a candle. A thrill passed as she opened the drawer of an old oak cabinet topped with the faint royal salamander. She took out a large red velvet purse edged with gold fringe—a relic from her grandmother. She weighed it proudly and counted the forgotten hoard: portugaises, genovines, Spanish quadruples, Dutch ducats of 1756, Indian rupees, and a napoleon of forty francs received the day before. Her father loved these coins for their rarity, but Eugenie thought only of Charles. After a few miscalculations she made out about five thousand eight hundred francs in intrinsic value—nearly six thousand for collectors. She clapped like a child. Father and daughter had each counted their fortune—he, to sell his gold; she, to fling hers into the sea of affection. She put the coins back, took the purse, and ran upstairs without hesitation. The hidden misery of her cousin made her forget hour and propriety; she felt strong in conscience, devotion, and happiness.

At the threshold, candle and purse in hand, Charles woke and stared. Eugenie laid the candle on the table.

“My cousin, I must beg pardon for a wrong I have done you; but God will pardon me—if you—will help me to wipe it out.”

“What is it?” asked Charles, rubbing his eyes.

“I have read those letters.”

Charles colored.

“How did it happen?” she continued; “how came I here? Truly, I do not know. I am tempted not to regret too much that I have read them; they have made me know your heart, your soul, and—”

“And what?” asked Charles.

“Your plans, your need of a sum—”

“My dear cousin—”

“Hush, hush! not so loud; we must not wake others. See,” she said, opening her purse, “here are the savings of a poor girl who wants nothing. Charles, accept them! This morning I did not value money; you have taught me. It is but a means. A cousin is almost a brother; you can surely borrow your sister’s purse.”

Eugenie, both woman and girl, never dreamed of refusal; but Charles remained silent.

“Oh! you will not refuse?” cried Eugenie, whose heart could be heard beating in the silence.

His hesitation mortified her, but the need of his position became clearer, and she knelt.

“I will never rise till you have taken that gold!” she said. “My cousin, I implore you, answer me! Let me know if you respect me, if you are generous, if—”

Moved, Charles took her hands to keep her from kneeling; his tears fell on them. Eugenie sprang to the purse and poured its contents on the table.

“Ah! yes, yes, you consent?” she sobbed. “Fear nothing, my cousin, you will be rich. This gold will bring you happiness; some day you shall bring it back to me—are we not partners? I will obey all conditions. But you should not attach such value to the gift.”

Charles could finally speak.

“Yes, Eugenie; my soul would be small indeed if I did not accept. And yet—gift for gift, confidence for confidence.”

“What do you mean?” she said, frightened.

“Listen, dear cousin; I have here—” He pointed to a square box under a leather case on the drawers. “There,” he continued, “is something as precious to me as life itself. This box was a present from my mother. All day I have thought that if she could rise from her grave, she would herself sell the gold which her love for me lavished on this dressing-case; but were I to do so, it would seem a sacrilege.” Eugenie pressed his hand.

“No,” he added, after a pause with a tender look, “no, I will neither sell it nor risk its safety on my journey. Dear Eugenie, you shall be its guardian. Never did friend commit anything more sacred to another. Let me show it to you.”

He opened the case and revealed a richly wrought dressing-case with gold ornaments. “What you admire is nothing,” he said, pushing a spring that revealed a hidden drawer. He drew out two portraits, masterpieces by Madame Mirbel, richly set with pearls.

“Oh, how beautiful! Is it the lady to whom you wrote that—”

“No,” he smiled; “this is my mother, and here is my father, your aunt and uncle. Eugenie, I beg you on my knees, keep my treasure safely. If I die and your little fortune is lost, this gold and these pearls will repay you. To you alone could I leave these portraits; you are worthy to keep them. But destroy them at last, so they may pass into no other hands.” Eugenie was silent.

“Ah, yes, say yes! You consent?” he added with winning grace.

Hearing his words as if he addressed himself, she turned to him with a look of love—the first look of womanhood, half coquette, half depth. He kissed her hand.

“Angel of purity! between us two money is nothing, never can be anything. Feeling and sentiment must be all henceforth.”

“You are like your mother—was her voice as soft as yours?”

“Oh! much softer—”

“Yes, for you,” she said, dropping her eyelids. “Come, Charles, go to bed; I wish it; you must be tired. Good-night.” She disengaged her hand; he lighted her way to her room. At the threshold—

“Ah!” he said, “why am I ruined?”

“What matter?—my father is rich; I think so,” she answered.

“Poor child!” said Charles, stepping in and leaning against the wall, “if that were so, he would never have let my father die; he would not let you live in this poor way; he would live otherwise himself.”

“But he owns Froidfond.”

“What is Froidfond worth?”

“I don’t know; but he has Noyers.”

“Nothing but a poor farm!”

“He has vineyards and fields.”

“Mere nothing,” said Charles disdainfully. “If your father had only twenty-four thousand francs a year do you suppose you would live in this cold, barren room?” he added, edging forward. “Ah! there you will keep my treasures,” he said, glancing at the old cabinet, as if to hide his thought.

“Go and sleep,” she said, hindering his entrance into the disordered room.

They bade each other good-night with a mutual smile. Both fell asleep in the same dream; from that moment the youth began to wear roses with his mourning. The next morning, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found her daughter in the garden with Charles. He still wore a mournful air, fitting for one plunged in misfortune.

“My father will not be home till dinner-time,” said Eugenie, seeing her mother’s anxious face.

An unspoken concord bound her and her cousin. Charles spent the morning in the hall, his sadness respected. People came on business—the plumber, mason, slater, carpenter, diggers, farmers—some to drive bargains about repairs, others to pay or be paid. Nanon put away produce brought as tribute, always waiting for the master’s orders before deciding to use or sell. Such was the cooper’s custom: to drink bad wine and eat spoiled fruit, and hold off action until his say-so.

Toward five in the afternoon Grandet returned from Angers, having made fourteen thousand francs by exchange on his gold, bringing treasury-notes bearing interest until he should invest them in the Funds. He had left Cornoiller at Angers to look after the horses, nearly foundered, with orders to bring them home slowly after they were rested.

“I have got back from Angers, wife,” he said; “I am hungry.”

Nanon called from the kitchen: “Haven’t you eaten anything since yesterday?”

“Nothing,” he answered.

Nanon brought in the soup. Des Grassins arrived to take his client’s orders just as the family sat down. Grandet had not even observed his nephew.

“Go on eating, Grandet,” said the banker; “we can talk. Do you know what gold is worth in Angers? They have come from Nantes after it? I shall send some of ours.”

“Don’t send any,” said Grandet; “they have got enough. We are such old friends, I ought to save you from such a loss of time.”

“But gold is worth thirteen francs fifty centimes.”

“Say was worth—”

“Where the devil have they got any?”

“I went to Angers last night,” answered Grandet in a low voice.

The banker started. A whispered conversation began between the two, during which they frequently looked at Charles. Presently des Grassins gave a start of astonishment; probably Grandet was then instructing him to invest the sum which was to give him a hundred thousand francs a year in the Funds.

“Monsieur Grandet,” said the banker to Charles, “I am starting for Paris; if you have any commissions—”

“None, monsieur, I thank you,” answered Charles.

“Thank him better than that, nephew. Monsieur is going to settle the affairs of the house of Guillaume Grandet.”

“Is there any hope?” said Charles eagerly.

“What!” exclaimed his uncle, with well-acted pride, “are you not my nephew? Your honor is ours. Is not your name Grandet?”

Charles rose, seized Pere Grandet, kissed him, turned pale, and left the room. Eugenie looked at her father with admiration.

“Well, good-by, des Grassins; it is all in your hands. Decoy those people as best you can; lead ’em by the nose.”

The two diplomats shook hands. The old cooper accompanied the banker to the front door. After closing it, he came back and plunged into his armchair, saying to Nanon,—

“Get me some black-currant ratafia.”

Too excited to sit long, he got up, looked at the portrait of Monsieur de la Bertelliere, and began to sing, performing what Nanon called his dancing steps—

“Dans les gardes francaises
J’avais un bon papa.”

Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie exchanged silent glances. The master’s hilarity always frightened them when it reached its height. The evening soon passed. Pere Grandet chose to go to bed early, and when he went to bed everybody else was expected to do the same. Nanon, Charles, and Eugenie were as tired as the master. Madame Grandet lived always according to her husband’s will. During the two hours consecrated to digestion the cooper, more facetious than ever, uttered a number of his apothegms—one reflecting his mind: when he had drunk his ratafia he looked at his glass and said, “You have no sooner put your lips to a glass than it is empty! Such is life. You can’t have and hold. Gold won’t circulate and stay in your purse. If it were not for that, life would be too fine.”

He was jovial and benevolent. When Nanon came with her spinning-wheel, “You must be tired,” he said; “put away your hemp.”

“Ah, bah! then I shall get sleepy,” she answered.

“Poor Nanon! Will you have some ratafia?”

“I won’t refuse a good offer; madame makes it a deal better than the apothecaries. What they sell is all drugs.”

“They put too much sugar,” said the master; “you can’t taste anything else.”

Thus the household closed the day. The scene left clear Eugenie’s devotion, Charles’s weakness softened by gratitude, and Grandet’s mixture of thrift, pride, and triumph at a fortunate stroke of business—an upheaval that would shape fates under that stiff, parsimonious roof.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The next morning the family gathered for an early breakfast, their domestic intimacy intensified by recent grief: Madame Grandet, Eugenie, Charles, and even Nanon, who sympathized without fully understanding why. The old man, whose avarice made him almost indifferent to his dandy nephew—whom he expected soon to be rid of after covering travel expenses—left the two “children,” Charles and Eugenie, under Madame Grandet’s watch, trusting her sense of public and religious propriety. He returned to his accustomed occupations: fixing field boundaries, tending poplars along the Loire, winter vineyard work, and business at Froidfond.

For Eugenie, spring had arrived. After giving Charles a small portrait, her heart followed him; sharing the secret deepened their intimacies into a communion beyond ordinary life. Their near relation allowed gentleness and tender glances: love for Eugenie was like rocking a newborn—soft imaginings and a painted golden future. Their passion was innocent, caressing, and touched with sadness, fitting the plainness of their dilapidated house. In the garden, on a mossy seat at sunset, beside the well, or under church arches, they exchanged few words and many meaningful looks. Charles learned a purer kind of love than his earlier vain passions; he rose early to see Eugenie before Grandet doled out provisions and slipped into the garden when his uncle’s steps sounded. The small illicitness of these morning tête-à-têtes, which Nanon pretended not to notice, heightened their joy.

After breakfast, when Grandet left for the fields, Charles stayed with Madame Grandet and Eugenie, content to hold their skeins, watch them work, and listen to their quiet prattle. He admired the household’s simple, half-monastic rhythm, thinking such morals scarcely possible in France. Eugenie became, in his mind, a Goethean Margaret—before her fall. Day by day his looks and words enraptured her; she yielded with delicious non-resistance, clinging to happiness like a swimmer to a branch. Yet reminders of impending parting sobered them.

Three days after des Grassins left Saumur, Grandet took Charles to the civil courts for the formal renunciation of his father’s estate, and to Maître Cruchot to sign powers of attorney—one for des Grassins, one for a friend to sell his belongings. Charles obtained a passport, received mourning clothes from Paris, engaged the Saumur tailor, and sold his useless wardrobe. Grandet, delighted with savings, approved the plain black surtout.

When Charles confessed he had collected small valuables, Grandet interrupted to take and appraise them. Charles offered his mother’s thimble to Madame Grandet; Eugenie added two buttons, drawing tears from Mme Grandet. Grandet weighed the trinkets, declared them worth 989.75 francs, and offered to advance the money “in livres”—a careful phrase indicating payment in crown prices on the Loire coast. Charles, embarrassed to sell to a second-rate dealer, thanked him. Grandet then accepted sleeve-buttons Charles offered as a memento and declared he would pay for the nephew’s passage to the Indies, estimating the work and promising 1,500 francs—again “in livres,” to be arranged through Cruchot and Perrotet. He hurried off, delighted to appear generous while spending nothing of his own.

Eugenie, admiring yet sad, asked if he really had to go. Charles bowed his head: he must. In the following days his manner took on a grave dignity; he no longer fretted but assumed the courage of one obliged to face misfortune. Eugenie took heart at his plain mourning; she and her mother attended a Requiem for Guillaume Grandet. At a second breakfast Charles read letters from Paris. Grandet brusquely ordered him to keep his counsel about business—secrecy being a family code.

Alone in the garden, Charles told Eugenie he had trusted Alphonse, who had managed his affairs prudently: he owed nothing in Paris; his belongings had been sold; an old sea-captain advised a commercial outfit of European curiosities for the Indies; his trunks were sent to Nantes for a ship to San Domingo; he had ten thousand francs contributed by two friends. In five days they might part—possibly forever. He begged Eugenie not to measure her life by his; he might perish; she might find a good marriage. When she asked if he loved her, he answered yes; she vowed to wait. They stole a kiss in the darkest passage near Nanon’s den and were discovered; Nanon joked they were saying their prayers.

Once Charles announced his departure, Grandet busied himself to seem involved: he found a packer, berated prices, insisted on making cases himself from old planks, rose early to plane and nail strong boxes, sent the goods by boat down the Loire, insured them, and got them to Nantes. After their last kiss, time flew for Eugenie; she wept in the garden that now seemed narrow, thinking of the wide ocean he would cross. On the eve of his departure, when Grandet and Nanon were absent, Charles and Eugenie placed the two portraits and an empty velvet purse in the only lockable drawer of the old cabinet, sealed it with tears and kisses, and exchanged vows—words of purity that momentarily sanctified their love.

The morning of departure was sorrowful. Even Nanon, despite the gold-embroidered robe and Jeannette cross Charles had given her, had tears in her eyes and blessed him. At half-past ten the family escorted Charles to the diligence for Nantes. Tradesmen watched them through the narrow street; Maître Cruchot joined at the market-place. Grandet kissed Charles at the inn, offering the old blessing “depart poor, return rich,” and spoke of keeping family honor—an odd mix of tenderness and self-interest that moved Charles to tears. The coach departed; Eugenie and her mother watched until the handkerchief vanished. Eugenie longed for omnipotence to bring him back.

To understand the household’s subsequent life, one must glance at Grandet’s maneuvers in Paris via Monsieur des Grassins. A month after des Grassins left Saumur, Grandet acquired a certificate yielding a hundred thousand francs a year from investments in the Funds, bought at eighty francs net. How he remitted the purchase and obtained the certificate remained obscure even after his death; Maître Cruchot suspected Nanon—who was absent five days, claiming to set things right at Froidfond—of transporting money, an unlikely pretext.

Grandet’s intentions regarding Guillaume Grandet’s house were fully realized. The Bank of France and Parisian financiers knew the names des Grassins and Félix Grandet of Saumur. The Saumur banker’s arrival in Paris to liquidate Guillaume’s affairs avoided the disgrace of protested notes. The notary began an inventory; des Grassins convened the creditors, who elected him and François Keller as liquidators to protect family honor and creditors’ interests. Confidence in Grandet of Saumur’s credit and des Grassins’ hopes smoothed proceedings: Parisians cashed notes as they fell due, and within six months the liquidators distributed forty-seven percent to each creditor on their claims, raised by selling securities and property, paid with scrupulous fidelity. Creditors praised the Grandets’ apparent honor—and later demanded the rest.

Grandet then insisted vouchers and acquittances for the forty-seven percent be deposited with a notary for reconciliation, which provoked resistance. Some creditors angrily refused; others demanded guarantees. Grandet, who studied creditors’ fickleness, saw advantage and, after long correspondence and concessions, secured the deposits. Months stretched into years. Twenty-three months after Guillaume’s death many creditors were distracted by other business and began to believe forty-seven percent might be all they would ever receive. Grandet calculated on time’s softening power. By the end of the third year des Grassins reported he had coaxed creditors to accept ten percent on the two million four hundred thousand francs still due; Grandet argued living notaries and brokers who had caused his brother’s ruin might have recovered credit and should be sued to recover part of the deficit.

By the end of the fourth year liabilities were assessed at twelve hundred thousand francs after protracted negotiations. Wishing to end the affair, in the ninth month of the fourth year Grandet told the liquidators his nephew had made a fortune in the Indies and was willing to pay in full; he had written and awaited an answer. The mere promise of “payment in full” checked creditors until the middle of the fifth year. The ploy had been planned from the start; creditors were left in the position he chose.

Meanwhile, when the Funds rose to 115, Père Grandet sold out and withdrew two million four hundred thousand francs in gold, adding six hundred thousand francs of compound interest—large sums extracted with his customary prudence. Des Grassins lived in Paris; after being made a deputy he fell infatuated with an actress, relapsed into old habits, and behaved immorally by Saumur standards. His wife, luckier, secured her property, managed the Saumur banking-house in her name, and repaired damage from her husband’s extravagance. The Cruchotines gossiped about the quasi-widow and despaired of an alliance between Eugenie and their son; Adolphe joined his father in Paris and earned a worthless reputation. Grandet lent Madame des Grassins money on a securely endorsed note but made clear he felt under no obligation to her.

Returning to Eugenie: men’s grief is eased by work and future prospects, so Charles could look ahead; women confined at home face grief without distraction and suffer more. Eugenie, who loved and devoted herself, was denied consolation; her happiness, Bossuet's metaphor implied, was scant. The day after Charles left the house resumed its ordinary aspect for everyone except Eugenie, for whom it had grown empty. She secretly kept Charles’s room exactly as he left it; Madame Grandet and Nanon were willing accomplices. Hope—“Who knows but he may come back sooner than we think?”—sustained her grasp on small comforts. Nanon, who had come to like the young man, expressed pity frankly, mocking shallow suitors who circled for the master’s money like rats scenting cheese.

From that day Eugenie’s beauty acquired a new cast: the solemnity of love and the dignity of the beloved haloed her features. To Charles she had been the virgin before conception; after his departure she seemed like the Virgin Mother—having given birth to love. She attended Mass daily, bought a map to follow his westward course, and nailed it by her looking-glass to imagine his ship and wonder whether he thought of her under some distant star. Mornings she sat under the walnut tree on the worm-eaten bench where they had exchanged vows, seeking the small sliver of sky above the high walls. Her love became the fabric of her life; evenings, when Grandet’s would-be friends came for cards, she could appear gay, but mornings belonged to her remembrances. Nanon, pitying yet loyal to the old master, spoke to Eugenie with blunt affection and an earthy devotion—mocking shallow suitors but confessing she would follow a man she truly loved to the end, though she had none.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Two months passed. Domestic life, once monotonous, quickened with the intense interest of a secret that bound the women together: Eugenie had given her treasure to Charles. For them, Charles lived beneath the grim rafters of the hall. Night and morning Eugenie opened the dressing-case and looked at her aunt’s portrait, searching in its features for him. One Sunday morning her mother, finding her absorbed in that way, was drawn, at last, into the terrible secret.

“You gave him all!” cried the poor woman, terrified. “What will you say to your father on New Year’s Day when he asks to see your gold?”

They lived through mortal terror for more than half the morning, so troubled they missed high Mass and only went to military service. In three days 1819 would end; in three days a terrible bourgeois tragedy would begin — no poison or dagger, but in their hands more cruel than any fabled horror.

Madame Grandet fretted. For two months she had been anxious; the wool sleeves she needed were unfinished. Grandet’s anger made her chill and sweat. She suggested seeking help from Monsieur des Grassins in Paris — maybe he’d send gold like Eugenie’s; perhaps pledging property could have procured the sum. Eugenie’s answer was quiet and resolute: it was too late. She had chosen her course and repented nothing. If her mother had read Charles’s letter, she would think only of him.

January 1, 1820 dawned, one of the coldest winters; snow lay thick. Fear of Grandet’s inspection suggested the women try to avoid a formal entrance into his chamber. Madame Grandet called when she heard him stirring, pretending Eugenie must dress in her room because the child might get ill in her cold chamber. Grandet answered in his usual gruff way but agreed, making a joke about the Bertellieres, mentioning a truffled pâté-de-foie-gras des Grassins had sent, and whispering that a double napoleon would be in the package for Eugenie; he added he had no gold left. He kissed his wife and went out. The two women breathed a little.

Nanon, the maid, entered to light the fire and gossiped about the small coin Grandet had given her — a six-franc piece — admiring his kindness. Grandet’s sudden cheerfulness sprang from the complete success of a speculation: Monsieur des Grassins, after discounting and making up sums, had sent him thirty thousand francs in silver — the remainder of his first half-year’s interest — and informed him the Funds had risen. He had gained twelve percent in two months; the accounts simplified, he would receive fifty thousand francs interest every six months without taxes or repair costs, and he foresaw himself master of millions in a few years. The six francs for Nanon were a trivial reward.

As the tradespeople opened, they noted Grandet scurrying about with a porter and sacks, talking of his silver. He warned the porter to keep quiet, handed out a coin or two, had the sacks carried to his inner room, and shut himself in. “When breakfast is ready, knock on the wall,” he said, then disappeared.

During breakfast the two women hoped he would not ask to see Eugenie’s gold downstairs; perhaps they might replace the treasure before her fête-day. Grandet came down thinking of how to convert Parisian silver into solid gold; he resolved to invest everything until the Funds reached par. He was in a fatal reverie for the two women — the more he talked and ate the more their hearts shrank. Eugenie, however, drew strength from love. “For him! for him!” she cried inwardly. She shot a brave glance at her mother.

About eleven, Grandet cleared the table and casually suggested spreading the “little treasure” on it. He enumerated: “You possess, in actual value, five thousand nine hundred and fifty-nine francs and the forty I gave you just now. That makes six thousand francs, less one.” He tossed a coin to round it to six thousand and ordered Nanon away. Then he said she must give him back her gold.

The two women were dumb. Eugenie rose, took a few steps toward the door, then turned and said, quietly, “I have not got my gold.”

“You have not got your gold!” cried Grandet, starting like a horse at artillery. “You are mistaken, Eugenie.” “No,” she answered. “By the shears of my father!” he swore — their household oath that shook the rafters. Nanon cried out; Madame Grandet grew pale.

Grandet rushed upon his daughter. In her terror she fell at her mother’s knees. Grandet was momentarily softened by his wife’s pallor and helped her to bed, but returned to demand an explanation of what she had done with the gold. Eugenie stood firm. She picked up the napoleon from the chimney-piece and offered it to him. He seized it, pocketed it, and vowed he would never give her anything again. “Do you dare despise your father?” he demanded. “Have you no confidence in me? If he is nothing for you, he is nothing at all. Where is your gold?”

“My father,” Eugenie answered, “you have told me I have attained my majority. I have used my money as I chose. It was mine.” When he pressed to know what she had done with it, she simply said it was an inviolable secret — she had secrets as he had his. He called it theft; in his rising voice he accused her of giving the money to some seducer like Charles, of ruining them. Eugenie remained silent and impassive.

When he could, he raged that she must confess: “If you have invested it anywhere, you must have a receipt—” “Was I free—yes or no—to do as I would with my own?” she asked. “You are a child,” he retorted. “Of age,” she corrected. Her logic stung him. He cursed her, accused her of deceiving his affection, and thundered that if she would not submit to him she should be confined. “You will stay there till I give you permission to leave it. Nanon will bring you bread and water.” He declared priests told her to obey him. Eugenie wept and fled to her mother. Grandet, after pacing in the snow, stormed upstairs and found the two in Madame Grandet’s room, the girl’s face hidden on her mother’s bosom.

“You have no father!” he cried. “Can it be you and I who have given birth to such a disobedient child? A fine education — religious too! To prison, mademoiselle!” Madame Grandet, now feverish and angry, implored him to spare her daughter; she believed Eugenie innocent and asked him to revoke his sentence. She warned that his violence would kill her.

“If you want to keep her, carry her off! Clear out — out of my house, both of you!” he cried. Eugenie rose, withdrew to her room, and he turned the key.

“Nanon,” he ordered, “put out the fire in the hall.” Then he sat in an armchair beside his wife’s fire and vented his fury: she must reveal the gold, for it was the only rupees and ducats they had. Madame Grandet pleaded, saying Eugenie was their only child, and even if she had thrown the coins into the water — words Grandet dismissed with scorn. He insisted the mother could make the girl confess, saying women had the knack to do such things better than men. She answered that she had no more influence over her daughter than he did; Eugenie took after him. He suspected they were in league against him.

“Mr. Grandet, if you wish to kill me, you have only to go on like this,” Madame Grandet said, furious with sorrow. She told him plainly he did wrong by his daughter; the money belonged to her, she was capable only of good use of it, and God alone could know their good deeds. “Take Eugenie back into favor; forgive her,” she implored. “If you do this you will lessen the injury your anger has done me; perhaps you will save my life. My daughter! Oh, monsieur, give me back my daughter!”

He would not relent. “I shall decamp,” he said. The house was not habitable under such talk. “A mother and daughter talking and arguing like that! A fine New Year’s present you’ve made me, Eugenie.” He lectured on Charles’s worthlessness, his morocco boots and airs, and prophesied ruin.

When Grandet shut the street door, Eugenie came out of her room to her mother. Madame Grandet praised her courage but lamented where forbidden things could lead: Eugenie had forced her to tell a lie. Eugenie said she would ask God to punish only herself. Nanon rushed in, alarmed, asking if it were true Eugenie would be kept on bread and water for life. Eugenie received the news tranquilly; Nanon vowed she would not eat frippe while the daughter of the house ate dry bread. Eugenie begged her to say nothing.

For the first time in twenty-four years Grandet dined alone. He was rigid and stern; Nanon sardonically remarked he was like a widower. He ordered her to “hold her jaw” and asked about the grease on the stove; there would be company that night.

The usual guests arrived at eight — the Cruchots, Madame des Grassins, and her son — surprised to find neither Madame Grandet nor Eugenie. “My wife is not very well, and Eugenie is with her,” Grandet said, his face betraying no emotion. They conversed a while, then Madame des Grassins went upstairs to see Madame Grandet and returned with news: “Not at all well,” she announced; “her condition seems really alarming.” The visitors left puzzled, Madame des Grassins commenting that something was going on at the Grandets; the mother was very ill without apparent cause and the girl’s eyes were red with crying. “Can they be trying to marry her against her will?” she wondered.

After Grandet slept, Nanon stole softly to Eugenie’s room in her stockinged feet bearing a pâté in a saucepan — a hare Cornoiller had given her — made with lard and spices she’d bought with her six francs. “You eat so little that this pâté will last you a full week; in such frosty weather it won’t spoil. You shan’t live on dry bread; I’m determined,” she said, proud to have her own money and to do a small kindness.

The scene thus closed on the three women bound by the secret and their suffering: Eugenie, defiant and silent in her love-fuelled secrecy; Madame Grandet, torn between fear and devotion to her child; and Nanon, loyal and practical, covertly defying the master’s sentence to bring a little food and consolation. Grandet, for his part, brooded over his finances and imagined future wealth, convinced his authority had been insulted and resolved to punish disobedience. The household had become a theatre of petty cruelty and great passions, a bourgeois tragedy enacted in intimate spaces — a locked door, a pocketed coin, a mother’s fainting and a daughter’s obstinate, secret love.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For months the old wine-grower visited his wife constantly but never saw or mentioned his daughter. Madame Grandet stayed in her chamber and daily grew worse. Nothing softened the old man: he grew harsher, more taciturn, exacting in business, and sometimes erred in his accounts.

“Something is going on at the Grandets,” the town gossips said. Eugenie went to Mass with Nanon and answered questions evasively. After two months it leaked out that by her father’s order Mademoiselle Grandet had been confined to her room since New Year’s, without fire and living on bread and water, Nanon bringing secret little comforts at night; she could not see or tend her mother except when her father was absent.

Grandet’s conduct was harshly judged. Passersby pointed and muttered; evenings in Saumur asked, “What has happened in the Grandet family?” When Eugenie went to church, people watched the rich heiress’s gentle, melancholy face. Her imprisonment and her father’s condemnation seemed nothing to her; piety, conscience, and love sustained her.

Her chief sorrow was her mother’s failing health. Madame Grandet, gentle and tender, beautiful in her religious serenity, was wasting away. Eugenie blamed herself as the innocent cause of her mother’s slow decline, and her remorse deepened her love. Every morning, once her father left, Eugenie went to her mother’s bedside; Nanon brought breakfast. She turned to the old servant with mute tears, not daring to speak of her cousin Charles. The mother at last asked, “Where is he? Why does he not write?”

“Let us think of him, mother, but not speak of him. You are ill—you, before all.” Yet “all” meant Charles. Madame Grandet, serene and Christian, bore death courageously but begged her husband: “If you desire to ease my last moments, take back your daughter: be a Christian, a husband, and a father.” Grandet’s face remained stone, unmoved by her tears.

Since her illness he had not dared to use his terrible “Ta, ta, ta, ta!” but his despotism remained in stony silence. Public opinion stung Nanon when she appeared in the market; she defended her master’s honor, explaining old age’s hardness and insisting Mademoiselle lived well and that her masters had reasons.

Toward late spring Madame Grandet, worn out and unable to reconcile father and daughter, appealed to the Cruchots. “Keep a girl of twenty-three on bread and water!” cried the lawyer Bonfons. The notary, Cruchot, promised to stop the treatment. Eugenie emerged and, with proud composure, begged them not to interfere. “My father is master in his own house…he is accountable to God only. To blame my father is to attack our family honor.” Madame Grandet agreed. Cruchot suggested procuring Eugenie’s liberty; the notary gently hinted that the best way to stop rumors was to settle things.

At the hour Eugenie brushed her hair, Grandet took his usual walk in the little garden and sometimes hid behind the walnut trunk to watch her, torn between obstinacy and his natural desire to embrace his child. Cruchot found him on the old bench and spoke plainly: the town talked; Madame Grandet was very ill; after her death Eugenie could claim a division of property—she was her mother’s heir, and might force a partition or sale of Froidfond. This legal possibility struck Grandet like a thunderbolt. He had not considered such a division.

Cruchot advised treating Eugenie kindly and mentioned that she could relinquish her claim. Grandet, distraught, railed that she had given away her gold—“six thousand francs!”—and raged at being “robbed” by his daughter. Cruchot, partly interested in inventories and partitions, urged a settlement. The talk of funds and Froidfond unsettled Grandet; when Cruchot noted the public scandal and mentioned the Funds at ninety-nine, Grandet brightened at any pretext for calming himself: “Ninety-nine!” he cried, and decided to reconcile. He hurried to his wife’s room and proclaimed a conciliatory mood: “Come, mother, you may have your daughter to spend the day with you… This is our wedding-day,” he said, flinging coin for the Fete-Dieu altar.

Madame Grandet reproached him: “How can you receive the God of mercy when you refuse to forgive your daughter?” He answered with stilted “Ta, ta, ta, ta!” and a coaxing voice. Eugenie and her mother, reassured, allowed themselves small comforts. Grandet, however, hastened off to his vineyards, both to compose himself and to consider the danger of losing control of his fortune at his wife’s death. He returned resolved to unbend, coax Eugenie, and die maintaining the reins of his millions.

In his absence Eugenie had brought a beautiful dressing-case from the oak cabinet and placed it on her mother’s bed; the two women were comparing a portrait for likeness to Charles when Grandet burst in. At the sight of the box his face changed: he sprang upon it like a famished tiger. “Gold, good gold! All gold—it weighs two pounds! Charles gave you that?” He snatched it, carried it to the window, and, finding it was from Charles, raged that she could get back his money now. Eugenie trembled: “It is not mine. It is a sacred trust.”

Grandet began to pry at the case with his knife; Eugenie sprang to recover it, and he pushed her so violently she fell upon her mother. Madame Grandet lifted herself, alarmed. Grandet, knife ready, threatened the treasure. Eugenie fell to her knees, clasped his hands, and implored him in the names of saints and Christ to spare it: “It is neither yours nor mine. It is a trust placed in my hands by an unhappy relation: I must give it back to him uninjured!” Grandet retorted that looking at a trust was as bad as touching it.

Madame and daughter begged. Eugenie, in a startling voice that brought Nanon running, seized a nearby knife and declared she would stab herself if he so much as cut the gold—“You have already driven my mother to her death; you will now kill your child! Do as you choose! Wound for wound!” Grandet hesitated, looking from the gold to his daughter; Madame Grandet fainted. Nanon cried that the mother was dying. Abruptly, to the astonishment of mother and child, Grandet flung the case upon the bed and, with a sudden change of manner, kissed his wife’s hand and called peace: “Here, take it… we’ve made up—haven’t we, little one? No more dry bread; you shall have all you want.” He scattered a handful of louis on the bed—“Here, Eugenie! see, wife! all these are for you,” and promised a hundred louis d’or, saying, “She loves her cousin, and she may marry him if she wants; she may keep his case.”

Madame and daughter, astonished, asked only for his affection. He pocketed the coins, professed love, proposed simple pleasures, and soothed his wife, promising the finest altar in Saumur. He then summoned Monsieur Bergerin, the celebrated doctor, who, after examination, said Madame Grandet was very ill but that perfect peace, generous diet, and care might prolong her life until autumn.

In spite of Grandet’s promises and Eugenie’s devoted care, Madame Grandet grew weaker every day. She visibly wasted; a heavenly radiance seemed to transfigure her. In October 1822, after patient endurance and devoted love, she died without complaint—a lamb without spot, ascending to heaven, lamenting above all leaving her gentle daughter alone in a selfish world that sought her fleece and treasures. With her last breath she told Eugenie, “My child, there is no happiness except in heaven; you will know it some day.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The day after her mother’s death, Eugenie clung to her childhood home; every familiar object—window, chair—brought tears. She had thought her father hard, but at the funeral he showed unexpected tenderness: offering his arm, looking kindly, brooding over her as if she were precious. Neighbors mistook Pere Grandet’s attentiveness and tremors for age; only Maitre Cruchot, who knew Grandet’s secret, understood the real motive.

After dinner, with the doors closed, Grandet spoke to Eugenie about settling estate matters. Cruchot explained that Grandet would not allow the property to be divided, sold, or exposed to taxation through an inventory. He proposed that she formally renounce her legal rights to her mother’s estate—leaving her father the life’s use and full disposition of the undivided property—while he would guarantee her capital. Scarcely understanding the law, Eugenie asked where to sign. Grandet, agitated, softened the proposal: instead of a formal, costly deed, she could renounce informally and trust him to provide; he offered a monthly hundred francs. She agreed.

The notary warned that she was disinheriting herself without a true guarantee, but Eugenie brushed this aside; Grandet kissed and embraced her, called her virtuous, and insisted Cruchot prepare the relinquishment for the records. The next morning she signed the papers that completed her spoliation. In the first year, Grandet did not pay the promised hundred francs; when reminded, he fetched jewels taken from his nephew and flung them into her apron, sarcastically asking if she wanted them for her twelve hundred francs, promising more next year and rubbing his hands at the prospect of speculating on her feelings.

Although still physically robust, Grandet then set about instructing Eugenie in thrift. For two years he made her order meals in his presence, collect rents, and learn vineyard and farm yields. By the third year these practices became her habits; he entrusted her with household keys and installed her as mistress of the house. Five years passed in monotonous routine. Eugenie’s deep sadness showed, but she never complained or spoke of the love at the heart of her sorrow. Her only society was the Cruchots and a few friends they introduced; they taught her whist and visited nightly. In 1827, as Grandet’s infirmities increased, he further instructed her in estate management and told her to consult Maitre Cruchot when in doubt.

Late that year the eighty-two-year-old patriarch was seized by a rapidly advancing paralysis. Dr. Bergerin gave him up. With Charles absent, Eugenie clung to the last living link of affection; for her, love had been life. She devoted herself to nursing the father whose mind weakened while his instinctive avarice remained acute. The dying man positioned his chair facing the door of his secret room and demanded explanation for every noise, waking at rent days or when accounts were due. He made Eugenie open the inner room, watched while she placed bags of money into his receptacle, and fingered the key in his waistcoat as if it were a talisman.

Maitre Cruchot, hoping the heiress might marry his nephew the president if Charles did not return, redoubled efforts: he took Grandet’s orders, ran errands to Froidfond, sold vintages, and converted everything into coin to be carried in sacks to the hiding-place. The old man’s last struggle was rolling up his coverings and ordering Nanon to lock them away “for fear they should be stolen.” So long as he could open his eyes his whole being fixed on the door behind which his treasures lay, asking in panic, “Are they there? are they there?”

Eugenie spread coins on a table before him; hours passed with him staring at the money like a child seeing it. When the priest came for the last sacraments, the crucifix and silver vessels stirred him; he made a terrifying gesture as if to seize the crucifix—a final effort that cost him his life. He called for Eugenie though he did not see her and, as he died, told her, “Take care of it all. You will render me an account yonder!”—a grim confirmation that Christianity still sanctified the miser’s sense of duty.

Left alone in the gray house with only Nanon for solace, Eugenie found in the old servant the one person who loved her truly and could hear her. Nanon was more than a servant: a humble friend and comfort. From Maitre Cruchot she learned the full extent of her inheritance: an income of three hundred thousand francs from landed and personal property; six million francs invested at three percent in the Funds (bought at sixty, then worth seventy-six francs); two million francs in gold coin; one hundred thousand francs in silver crown-pieces; and unpaid interest—amounting in all to seventeen million francs. Her immediate thought was simply: “Where is my cousin?”—the absent Charles.

When Cruchot gave her the clear schedule of the inheritance, Eugenie sat with Nanon by the vacant fireplace, where every object was memory—from her mother’s chair to the glass Charles had used. “We are alone,” she said; Nanon replied that if she knew where Charles was she would go on foot to find him. Eugenie could only think of the ocean between them. As she wept with Nanon, word of her seventeen-million fortune spread from Nantes to Orleans.

Among her first acts after the funeral, Eugenie settled an annuity of twelve hundred francs on Nanon, making the old woman comfortably off since she already possessed six hundred francs. Less than a month later Nanon married Antoine Cornoiller, appointed keeper of Mademoiselle Grandet’s estates. Madame Cornoiller, though fifty-nine, looked not more than forty: conventual habits gave her a rosy, healthy appearance. On her marriage day she presented a content, vigorous figure envied in the neighborhood. Eugenie gave her three dozen silver forks and spoons as a wedding present.

Cornoiller, devoted to his mistress, spoke of her with tears; Madame Cornoiller, appointed housekeeper to Mademoiselle Grandet, delighted in her new duties as much as in her marriage. She took charge of weekly accounts, locked provisions, and issued them daily; she ruled two servants—a cook and a maid who mended linen and made dresses. Cornoiller combined keeper and bailiff duties. The new household ran strictly on Monsieur Grandet’s usages, and the farmers noticed no change.

By thirty, Eugenie had known none of life’s joys. Her pale, sad childhood beside a mother always wounded by life left her with remorse and regrets. Her first and only love—brief, intense, and thwarted—had cost her mother’s life and brought Eugenie only sorrow and fragile hopes. She felt the heart needed mutual exchange to live; without it, it withers. Wealth offered no consolation: she could not live except through love, religion, and faith in the future. Love explained eternity to her; her heart and the Gospel opened two worlds. She retreated inward, loving and believing herself beloved. For seven years her passion pervaded her life. Her real treasures were not her millions but Charles’s dressing-case, the portraits above her bed, the jewels recovered from her father and kept in a drawer, and her aunt’s thimble that her mother had used—symbols more precious than gold.

In mourning, a match seemed unlikely. The Cruchots and the old abbé contented themselves with surrounding the heiress, paying affectionate attentions, and cultivating influence. Each evening the hall filled with devoted Cruchotines praising their mistress. She had her doctor, an almoner, a chamberlain, a first lady of honor, a prime minister—and a chancellor eager to have much to say. If she had wished for a train-bearer, one would have been produced. Flattered by little minds hoping to worm themselves into favor, she at first felt embarrassed; gradually she grew used to the homage and perhaps to enjoying it.

Monsieur de Bonfons became the hero of this little court: his wit, education, and amiability were constantly praised. Visitors spoke of the Cruchots’ rising fortunes and prospects of official appointments. Bonfons affected youth—though forty and withered he dressed in the latest fashions to charm Eugenie and addressed her familiarly as “Our dear Eugenie.” Apart from more visitors, the house changed little: loto gave way to whist, and Monsieur and Madame Grandet were absent. The pack still pursued Eugenie and her millions; the pursuit grew more united. Madame des Grassins tried to stir the circle by urging Eugenie to consider the Marquis de Froidfond, arguing marriage to him would restore a ruined family and secure a title. Misreading Eugenie’s disdain, she loudly promoted the match, praising rank and peerage and suggesting Grandet’s investments sought to graft the family onto that stock.

One night, as she prepared for bed, Eugenie asked Nanon, “How is it that in seven years he has never once written to me?”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
While these events happened in Saumur, Charles was making his fortune in the Indies. His commercial venture prospered; he realized six thousand dollars. Crossing the Line cleared many cobwebs from his brain: he learned the surest road to fortune, in the tropics as in Europe, was to buy and sell men. He went to the African coast and bought Negroes, combining this traffic with other profitable merchandise. Jostling with men, traveling through many lands, and observing conflicting customs made him skeptical; what one country called crime another praised as virtue. In the perpetual struggle of selfish interests his heart grew cold. The Grandet blood did its work: Charles became hard and eager for prey. He sold Chinamen, Negroes, bird‑nests, children, artists; practised usury on a large scale; his habit of evading customs made him less scrupulous about others’ rights. He went to St. Thomas, bought goods captured by pirates and sold them where they fetched best. If Eugénie’s pure face had accompanied him on his first voyage like a Virgin on a mast and if he had then attributed his first success to her prayers, later women—blacks, mulattoes, whites, Indian dancing‑girls—orgies and adventures effaced the memory of his cousin, Saumur, the house, the stolen kiss. He remembered only the little garden behind the crumbling wall where he had learned his fate; he rejected his family. His uncle was an old dog who had filched his jewels; Eugénie had no place in his heart, though she remained in his accounts as a creditor for six thousand francs.

Such conduct explains Charles Grandet’s silence. In the Indies, at St. Thomas, on the African coast, at Lisbon, and in the United States he used the name Shepherd so as not to compromise his family. As Charles Shepherd he could be indefatigable, bold, grasping—resolved to snatch his fortune quibuscumque viis and get the villainy over with to live honestly thereafter.

Prosperity was rapid; in 1827 Charles returned to Bordeaux on the brig Marie Caroline, bringing nineteen hundred thousand francs in gold‑dust, from which he expected another seven or eight per cent at the Paris mint. On the brig he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, gentleman‑in‑ordinary to Charles X., returning from the West Indies to meet his extravagantly living wife and his ugly daughter, who had no dowry. The d’Aubrions belonged to an old southern house reduced to about twenty thousand francs of income. Madame d’Aubrion was determined to marry off her daughter despite the lack of a dot; she had used hygienic treatment, artful dressing, charming manners, melancholy glances, and coquettish tricks to give the girl an appearance of distinction. Mademoiselle d’Aubrion was long and spare, with a disdainful mouth and a heavy nose—sallow normally and red after a meal—an unpleasant feature in a pale, dull face; but her mother contrived full sleeves, deceptive pads, puffed dresses, and tight corsets to create developments worth showing.

Charles became intimate with Madame d’Aubrion precisely because she sought his intimacy; those on the brig said the handsome madame neglected no means to secure so rich a son‑in‑law. Landing at Bordeaux in June 1827, Monsieur, Madame, Mademoiselle d’Aubrion, and Charles lodged at the same hotel and set out together for Paris. The hôtel d’Aubrion was hampered with mortgages; Charles was to free it. Madame promised to give the ground‑floor to a son‑in‑law and to obtain a royal ordinance from Charles X. allowing him to take the d’Aubrion name and arms and, by buying the entailed estate for thirty‑six thousand francs a year, to succeed to the titles of Captal de Buch and Marquis d’Aubrion. By uniting fortunes, living together, and profiting by sinecures the two families might occupy the hôtel d’Aubrion with an income exceeding a hundred thousand francs.

“And when a man has a hundred thousand francs a year, a name, a family, and a position at court—for I will get you appointed gentleman‑of‑the‑bedchamber—you can do what you like,” she told Charles. “You can become master of the rolls, prefect, secretary to an embassy, ambassador if you like. Charles X. is fond of d’Aubrion; they have known each other from childhood.”

Intoxicated with ambition, Charles toyed with these hopes. Believing his father’s affairs settled by his uncle, he imagined himself anchored in the Faubourg Saint‑Germain—reappearing as the Comte d’Aubrion, like the Dreux reappearing in Bréze. Dazzled by the Restoration’s prosperity and fascinated by aristocratic splendor, his intoxication, begun on the brig, increased after he reached Paris, and he resolved to follow the course his would‑be mother‑in‑law indicated. His cousin counted for no more than a speck in this brilliant prospect; still, he went to see Annette, who advised him to marry and promised her support in his ambitious projects. Annette was delighted to fasten an ugly, uninteresting girl on Charles, whose West Indies life had made him very attractive: bronzed complexion, bold, decisive manners like a man used to sharp decisions, to rule, and to succeed. Charles breathed easier in Paris, conscious of his part to play.

Des Grassins, hearing of his return, approaching marriage, and fortune, came to see him and inquired about the three hundred thousand francs still required to settle his father’s debts. He found Grandet conferring with a goldsmith about jewels for Mademoiselle d’Aubrion’s corbeille. Charles had brought magnificent diamonds; the setting with plate and jewelry for the new establishment amounted to over two hundred thousand francs. When des Grassins, who had already called several times, presented himself, Charles treated him with the impertinence of a young man of fashion conscious of having killed four men in as many duels in the Indies. Coldly, and without fully grasping the plea, Charles replied:

“My father’s affairs are not mine. I am much obliged, monsieur, for the trouble you have been good enough to take—by which, however, I really cannot profit. I have not earned two millions by the sweat of my brow to fling them at the head of my father’s creditors.”

“But suppose that your father’s estate were within a few days to be declared bankrupt?”

“Monsieur, in a few days I shall be called the Comte d’Aubrion; you will understand, therefore, that what you threaten is of no consequence to me. Besides, you know as well as I do that when a man has an income of a hundred thousand francs his father has never failed.” So saying, he edged Monsieur des Grassins to the door.

At the beginning of August that year Eugénie sat on the little wooden bench where her cousin had sworn to love her eternally, where she usually breakfasted when the weather was fine. The poor girl was happy for the moment in the fresh summer air, letting memory recall the great and little events of her love and the catastrophes that followed. The sun had just reached the angle of the ruined wall full of chinks that no one, by the mistress’s caprice, was allowed to touch, though Cornoiller often told his wife it would fall and crush somebody one day. At that moment the postman knocked and gave a letter to Madame Cornoiller, who ran into the garden, crying:

“Mademoiselle, a letter!” She gave it to Eugénie, adding, “Is it the one you expected?”

The words rang in Eugénie’s heart. She turned pale and held the letter for a moment. She trembled and could not break the seal. La Grande Nanon stood before her, hands on her hips, her joy showing in the lines of her face.

“Read it, mademoiselle!”

“Ah, Nanon, why did he return to Paris? He went from Saumur.”

“Read it, and you’ll find out.”

Eugénie opened the letter with trembling fingers. A cheque on the house of “Madame des Grassins and Coret, of Saumur,” fluttered out. Nanon picked it up.

The letter ran that Charles had met with success, returned rich, and had followed his uncle’s advice; the deaths of his aunt and uncle left room for his succession. He remembered their youthful vows—the little wooden seat, the passage, the attic chamber—and acknowledged that these recollections had sustained him. Yet, having travelled and matured, he declared that an alliance had been proposed which satisfied his idea of matrimony. Love in marriage was a delusion; society and its conventions must be obeyed. He admitted he did not love Mademoiselle d’Aubrion, but his present income—he named it eighty thousand francs—enabled him to marry into the Aubrion family, securing a title, a place as gentleman‑of‑the‑bedchamber, and a brilliant position. Such a match would give his children a social rank and prospects—an entailed estate of forty thousand francs a year would make any State position attainable. “We owe ourselves to our children,” he wrote.

Charles professed to be frank: he would not deceive Eugénie. He offered her the choice; his conscience bound him to his word, yet if she, whom he called his friend, wished him to renounce social ambitions, he would willingly accept the pure and simple happiness she represented. The postscript enclosed a cheque on des Grassins’s bank for eight thousand francs, payable in gold, which included capital and interest of the sum she had lent him. He expected a case from Bordeaux containing a few things he begged her to allow him to offer as a mark of gratitude, and he told her she could send his dressing‑case by the diligence to the hôtel d’Aubrion.

“By the diligence!” said Eugénie. “A thing for which I would have laid down my life!”

Terrible and utter disaster! The ship that had borne all her hopes had gone down, leaving not a spar, not a plank, on a vast ocean of expectation. Some women, abandoned, will try to tear a lover from a rival, commit crimes, or rush to the ends of the earth. Others bow their heads and suffer in silence; they go their way dying, resigned, weeping, forgiving, praying, and recollecting until the last breath. This was Eugénie’s love after she read that dreadful letter. She raised her eyes to heaven, thinking of her dying mother’s last words, who, with the prescience of death, had looked into the future: Eugénie, remembering that prophetic life, measured her destiny. Nothing was left for her; she could only unfold her wings, stretch upward, and live in prayer until deliverance.

“My mother was right,” she said, weeping. “Suffer—and die!”

Eugenie returned slowly from the garden, avoiding the corridor where her cousin’s presence still lingered in traces of his morning cup and Sèvres sugar-bowl. The day promised to be grave: Nanon reported that the parish curate, allied with the Cruchots and Monsieur de Bonfons, had come to speak of marriage. The old abbé had urged him to admonish Mademoiselle Grandet about the duty of marriage for a woman of her station. Eugenie, preoccupied with her own grief, thought he had come about the thousand francs she gave monthly to the poor and told Nanon to fetch the alms; the curate only smiled.

He explained that his subject was a poor girl of Saumur who neglected her Christian duties through lack of charity to herself. Eugenie, overwhelmed, declared her intent to renounce the world and live for God in silence and seclusion. The curate counseled caution: marriage is life, the veil is death; to take the veil was to turn inward selfishly and to abandon social obligations. He reminded her that she supported many poor families, that her fortune was a trust to be preserved, and that a husband could help manage affairs and protect her property from lawsuits. She loved God too well not to find salvation within the world she adorned and owed example to.

At that moment Madame des Grassins arrived, burning with spite and the news she could not resist. She revealed, by a letter from her husband, that Charles Grandet had returned from the Indies, had been in Paris a month, and was on the verge of marrying Mademoiselle d’Aubrion—until the Marquis d’Aubrion, learning of Guillaume Grandet’s liabilities, threatened to refuse his daughter unless the uncle’s creditors were paid. Des Grassins intended to declare Guillaume bankrupt and to explain his position to the creditors, though he professed respect for Eugenie and postponed action until she had spoken. Eugenie, stung, read enough, then coldly returned the letter.

When pressed for fees owed by Charles, Madame des Grassins paid. Turning to the curate, Eugenie, with the composure of her father’s stiff manner, put a wrenching question: would it be a sin to remain a virgin after marriage? The curate evaded specifics and deferred to theological texts. She retreated to her father’s private room and spent the day alone, not appearing at dinner. In the evening she joined the assembled company, feigning calm under the weight of scandal; the town knew of Charles’s return and treachery. She smiled beneath her pain and met condolences with courtesy. As the game broke up late, she interrupted Monsieur de Bonfons’s departure.

Against provincial convention, she made an astonishing proposal. To the astonishment of the assembly and with equal parts irony and resolve, Eugenie offered her hand to the president—Monsieur de Bonfons—on strict terms. She demanded that he swear to leave her free in life, to claim none of the usual marital rights over her, and to accept that in her heart she could only give friendship to a husband. He might have the use of her hand and fortune only on this condition: that he undertake, at once, to go to Paris, learn the names of her uncle’s creditors, convene them, and pay every claim in full with interest at five percent from the date of each debt to the present day, obtaining legal receipts before a notary. Only then should he present the discharged claims to her cousin Charles. In proof of her earnestness she produced a certificate of Bank of France shares worth fifteen hundred thousand francs as immediate funds. The president, moved, vowed to obey.

When he left for Paris, Eugenie broke down and wept; with that act she severed her earlier attachment for Charles. The president reached Paris and, with des Grassins, summoned the creditors—none failed to appear. In a remarkable act of justice for Parisian commerce, he paid each claim in full with interest; the payments were legally registered and des Grassins received a fee for his services. Having secured the acquittances, the president sought out Charles at the hôtel d’Aubrion, where the young man had just quarrelled with his prospective father-in-law over marrying the Marquis’s daughter given the cloud on the family’s finances. The president handed Charles a letter from Eugenie.

In it Eugenie told her cousin that she appreciated his judgment and that, though she could not give him the pleasures of the world or participate in its calculations, she could not marry him while his father’s affairs remained unsettled. She ended the letter with a dignified farewell, saying he would always have her faithful friendship but no more. Charles, surprised that she had been so generous and disciplined with her fortune, was informed by the president that Eugenie had recently lost two millions—she had been said to possess about nineteen millions four days earlier but now had seventeen millions—and that the couple would have combined income enough for a comfortable position: an income of seven hundred and fifty thousand francs at marriage.

Charles tried to recover composure and speak of mutual enrichment; the president, smiling, hinted at the diminished but still enormous fortune. A dressing-case the president delivered to Charles might have contained a ring or trinket; Charles, enraged by the president’s apparent mockery, vowed to offend him. The president left, and three days later returned to Saumur to announce his marriage to Eugenie. In due course he rose rapidly: six months after the marriage he was appointed councillor in the Cour royale at Angers, then president of the superior courts, and he aimed for a seat in the Chamber of Deputies and even for a peerage. Provincials spoke sardonically of his kinship to royalty; la Grande Nanon remarked to Eugenie in awe that “the king will be his cousin.”

Nevertheless, Monsieur de Bonfons’s ambitions were only partially realized. Though he finally dropped his patronymic Cruchot and signaled social ascent, he died eight days after being elected deputy of Saumur. The text suggests Providence punished him for the sordid calculations and legal cunning with which he had drawn his marriage contract: it provided that husband and wife, if childless, would give all their property to each other without inventory, thereby enabling a husband to inherit all. This clause, and the manner of the contract, explain the president’s deferential treatment of his wife; he had respected her passion and solitude because that restraint protected his claim. Women called him considerate and delicate; they pitied him, and some imputed to Eugenie a passionate grief that they discreetly criticized.

Eugenie, possessing the intuitive acuity of an isolated soul formed by suffering and meditation, had understood that the president secretly desired her death to obtain the fortune he had calculated would accrue to the union through inheritances from his uncles. But she pitied him. Providence, the narrative suggests, punished his calculative selfishness by cutting him down just as he seemed to have achieved his ambitions; had he lived, his deference and the contractual clauses might have been used to his material advantage. To Eugenie, gold remained indifferent. She longed for heaven, lived piously and did good in secret, and never wearied of charity.

Widowed at thirty-six, Eugenie retained her beauty tempered by solemnity: a white, placid face, gentle voice, and simple manners. She combined the saintliness of a woman untouched by worldly contamination with the rigid bearing of an old maid and the petty, provincial habits of her upbringing. Despite her vast means she continued to live simply; the house’s hearth was only lit by rules set in her youth, and she dressed modestly. Her household seemed parsimonious, yet she disarmed accusation through noble uses of her wealth: she supported pious and charitable institutions, endowed a hospital for old age, founded Christian schools, and endowed a public library. The churches of Saumur benefited greatly from her gifts. Though sometimes ironically called “mademoiselle,” Madame de Bonfons inspired a reverential respect. Her large-hearted benefactions redeemed the narrowness of her education and the petty habits of her early life, even as money’s cold calculations had taught her a wary distrust of emotion.

“I have none but you to love me,” she confided to Nanon, revealing the loneliness beneath her public beneficence. She healed secret wounds in many families, and she departed for heaven attended by a train of benefactions. Eugenie remained, in the narrator’s words, in the world but not of it: created by nature to be supremely wife and mother, she had neither husband, children, nor family. Later, rumor briefly suggested she might marry again; Saumur whispered of the Marquis de Froidfond and of renewed social sieges like those once led by the Cruchots. Yet those rumors were unfounded. Nanon and Cornoiller, ignorant of worldly corruption, were incapable of the machinations and compromises that had touched Eugenie’s life.
