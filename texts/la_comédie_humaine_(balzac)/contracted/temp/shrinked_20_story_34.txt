DEDICATION
To my dear Alexandre de Berny.
His old friend,
De Balzac.

MADAME FIRMIANI

Many stories have settings that carry them naturally, but some human incidents demand the heart’s touch; certain delicate, almost anatomical traits must be rendered with equal delicacy. Some portraits require the infusion of a soul; they mean nothing unless the subtlest expressions of a speaking countenance are given. Certain revelations depend on secret harmonies—a particular day, hour, or inward mood—to be rightly told.

Such mysterious affinities are necessary for this simple history, which may please the grave and reflective who live on tender emotions. If the writer, like a surgeon beside his dying friend, feels a reverence for his subject, should not the reader share that inexplicable feeling? If the reader can be in unison with the vague, nervous sadness that casts gray tints about us—a semi-illness whose gentle sufferings are often pleasing—let him read on; otherwise he should lay aside this book. Those who have never buried a good old relative, infirm and poor, will not catch the tone of these pages, which some will find redolent of musk, others as colorless and virtuous as Florian. The reader must have known the luxury of tears, the silent pangs of passing memory, the vision of a dear yet far-off Shade—regrets for what earth has swallowed up, with smiles for vanished joys.

The writer will not, for the wealth of England, borrow a single poetic lie to embellish this narrative. The following is a true history; spend on it the treasures of your sensibility—if you have any.

In our day the French language contains as many idioms as there are varieties of Parisian men. It is curious and amusing to hear how different “species” of Parisian interpret the same person. Ask about Madame Firmiani and you will receive a dozen portraits reflecting more about the questioner than about her.

To the Practical man she is an inventory: “Fine house in the rue du Bac, salons handsomely furnished, good pictures, a hundred thousand francs a year, husband formerly receiver-general of the department of Montenotte.” The Practical—solid, fat, in black—measures everyone by figures, incomes, or acres and pronounces on social standing accordingly.

The Lounger turns her into a house: “Yes, I know her; I go to her parties; she receives on Wednesdays; highly creditable house.” For him she is an inn without a sign—good company, excellent tea, difficult of admission, therefore the best society. He goes, but won’t present you.

The Egotist sneers: “Her parties are as dull as the Court itself. What good is a mind unless to avoid such salons?” Egotists would keep the universe under lock and key, resent others’ pleasures, and prefer inferiors as equals.

The Student admires her as an “adorable woman,” a Nature’s excuse for ugly ones; he waxes affectionate and offers to present you, timid in intimacy yet bold in public.

The Fop, glancing at fashion, sketches a sensual portrait: “Between thirty and thirty-five, faded complexion, handsome eyes, flat figure, contralto voice worn, much dressed, rather rouged, charming manners—the remains of a pretty woman still worth the trouble of a passion.” Fops are pitiless in the morning.

The Connoisseur sees only her pictures: “Magnificent collection—go and see them.” To him she is an assemblage of canvases, and he rushes off to dealers.

A jealous woman warns other women away: “I don’t wish you to visit her.” To the Spiteful, she is dangerous, a siren; she dresses well and gives sleepless nights.

An Attache to an embassy, with continental airs, says she is from Antwerp and was very handsome in Rome ten years ago. Attaches speak in the refined dialect of countries, turning conversation into itineraries of Europe.

The Distinguished man notes her faubourg Saint-Germain connections, dropping the de to everyone—Monsieur Dupin senior, Monsieur Lafayette—marking those who live trying to do “the right thing” yet who, for their sins, may dwell in the Marais and have a husband who is a lawyer.

The Duke: “I do not know her.” He recognizes only women presented at court; he was one of Napoleon’s creations and is excused.

The Ninny invents: “Surely she used to sing at the Opera-house.” Ninnies prefer any reply to silence.

Two old magistrates’ wives gossip about her maiden name. The one asks; the other answers: “She was a Cadignan, niece of the old Prince de Cadignan, consequently cousin to the present Duc de Maufrigneuse.” To them, lineage explains much: Madame Firmiani is a Cadignan; that prejudice persists regardless of virtue, wealth, or youth.

An Original notes the lack of galoshes in her antechamber and deems her safe company: “You can visit and play cards without compromising yourself; if there are scoundrels, they are people of quality in carriages—such persons never quarrel.”

The Observer gives the most elaborate portrait, half oracle, half gossip: call on Madame Firmiani and you will find a beautiful woman at ease by her fireplace, scarcely rising except for women, ambassadors, dukes, and great distinction. She is gracious, charming, well-read, and likes to talk on many topics. Signs of a passionate nature show about her, but she has so many adorers she cannot have a favorite. If suspicion rested on two or three intimates we might name a “cavaliere servente,” but it does not. Her husband is mythical; Monsieur Firmiani is like that third post-horse for which we pay though we never behold it. Judges say she has the finest contralto voice in Europe, yet she has sung only two or three times since she came to Paris. She receives much company, but goes nowhere.

The Observer speaks as an oracle; his anecdotes and quotations must be accepted as truth or he will slander you in twenty salons. He is forty, never dines at home, declares himself no longer dangerous to women, wears a maroon coat, keeps a place at the Bouffons, and has a small estate in an unnamed department. He is sometimes confused with the Parasite, but has held real functions and owns property.

The Contradictor counters with authority: “She was Murat’s former mistress,” or other categorical corrections. Contradictors specialize in rectifying memoirs, dates, and facts; they are certain about everything, often “chevaliers” of the Legion of Honor, loud and inclined to play high.

The Envious dismisses the reports of a hundred thousand francs a year: “Nonsense! Some give millions carelessly. She’s a coquette who ruined a young man and prevents him from marrying well; if she were not handsome she would have no penny.” Envy requires no sketch; it is perennial and fruitless, a vice as well known as the cat.

Thus, through an array of Parisian types—Practical, Lounger, Egotist, Student, Fop, Connoisseur, Spiteful woman, Attache, Distinguished, Duke, Ninny, old ladies, Original, Observer, Contradictor, Envious—Madame Firmiani is variously reduced to figures, a house, a reputation, a collection, a scandal, a lineage, or an enigma. Each portrait reveals less of her than of the speaker.

Yet, amid these caricatures, certain details recur and form the outline of the woman: a handsome, gracious hostess in the rue du Bac with fine salons and pictures; a lady who receives company but rarely appears beyond her circle; a married woman whose husband is invisible, mythical; a woman of great personal charm and a contralto voice rarely heard; a Cadignan by birth, related to high families, whose past and present inspire rumor and contradiction alike. Around her, Paris projects its prejudices, envies, and classifications, and the same phrase—Madame Firmiani—becomes a mirror for every social species to see itself.

The narrative that follows will ask the reader for that sympathetic mood Balzac invoked: a tender sensibility, a remembrance of vanished affections, and a readiness to accept delicate picturing rather than abrupt fact. Only thus can the true history of Madame Firmiani be received, understood, and felt.

In January 1824 persons of every sort propagated so many different opinions about Madame Firmiani that listing them would be tedious. A man who wished to know her, yet would not or could not go to her house, could learn by hearsay that she was a widow or a wife, silly or wise, virtuous or not, rich or poor, soulless or full of feeling, handsome or plain—there were as many Madame Firmianis as there are classes in society or sects in Catholicism. Frightful reflection! We are all like lithographic blocks from which different copies may be drawn—the proofs resembling us more or less according to imperceptible shading, so that our reputation depends (beyond friends’ calumnies and newspapers’ witticisms) on the balance struck by critics between Truth that limps and Falsehood to which Parisian wit gives wings.

Madame Firmiani, like other noble women who make their hearts a sanctuary and disdain the world, was likely to be misjudged by Monsieur de Bourbonne, an old country magnate who had reason to think of her that winter. He belonged to the class of provincial planters—men living on their estates, keeping close accounts, bargaining with the peasantry. Thus employed, a man becomes sagacious in spite of himself. The old gentleman had come to Paris from Touraine to satisfy his curiosity about Madame Firmiani. He was a man of honor and breeding. His sole heir was a nephew he greatly loved, in whose interests he planted his poplars. When a man thinks without annoyance about his heir, watching trees grow finer for his future benefit, affection grows too with every blow of the spade around their roots. Though not common, this feeling is to be met with in Touraine.

This cherished nephew, Octave de Camps, descended from the famous Abbé de Camps—well known to bibliophiles and scholars. Provincials have the bad habit of branding with decent reprobation any young man who sells inherited estates. This antiquated prejudice had interfered with the stock-jobbing the government encouraged. Without consulting his uncle, Octave had lately sold an estate to the Bande Noire, a mysterious association of speculators that bought landed estates, cut them up, and sold parcels to peasants or others. The château de Villaines would have been pulled down were it not for the remonstrances the old uncle made to the representatives of the “Pickaxe company.” To increase the old man’s wrath, a distant relative—one of those cousins of small means and much astuteness about whom shrewd provincials remark, “No lawsuits for me with him!”—had, as it were by accident, come to visit Monsieur de Bourbonne and incidentally told him of his nephew’s ruin.

Monsieur Octave de Camps, he said, having wasted his means on a certain Madame Firmiani, was now reduced to teaching mathematics for a living, while awaiting his uncle’s death, not daring to let him know of his dissipations. This distant cousin, a sort of Charles Moor, was not ashamed to give this fatal news to the old gentleman as he sat by his fire, digesting a profuse provincial dinner.

But heirs cannot always rid themselves of uncles. The uncle refused to believe the story, and came victorious from the attack of indigestion produced by his nephew’s biography. Some shocks affect the heart, others the head; in this case the cousin’s blow fell on the digestive organs and did little harm, for the old man’s stomach was sound. Like a true disciple of Saint Thomas, Monsieur de Bourbonne came to Paris, unknown to Octave, resolved to make full inquiries as to his nephew’s insolvency. Having many acquaintances in the faubourg Saint-Germain—among the Listomères, the Lenoncourts, and the Vandenesses—he heard so much gossip, so many facts and falsities, about Madame Firmiani that he resolved to be presented to her under the name of de Rouxellay, that of his estate in Touraine. The astute old gentleman was careful to choose an evening when he knew Octave would be engaged in finishing a piece of work which was to pay him well—for this so-called lover of Madame Firmiani still went to her house; a circumstance that seemed difficult to explain. As to Octave’s ruin, that, unfortunately, was no fable, as Monsieur de Bourbonne at once discovered.

Monsieur de Rouxellay was not at all like the provincial uncle at the Gymnase. Formerly in the King’s guard, a man of the world and a favorite among women, he knew how to present himself in society with the courteous manners of the olden time; he could make graceful speeches and understand the Charter. Though he loved the Bourbons with noble frankness, believed in God as a gentleman should, and read nothing but the Quotidienne, he was not as ridiculous as the liberals of his department would fain have him. He could hold his own in the court circle, provided no one talked to him of “Moses in Egypt,” nor of the drama, or romanticism, nor of railways. He himself had never got beyond Voltaire, Buffon, Payronnet, and the Chevalier Gluck, the Queen’s favorite musician.

“Madame,” he said to the Marquise de Listomère, who was on his arm as they entered Madame Firmiani’s salons, “if this woman is my nephew’s mistress, I pity him. How can she live in the midst of this luxury, and know that he is in a garret? Hasn’t she any soul? Octave is a fool to have given up such an estate as Villaines for a—”

Monsieur de Bourbonne belonged to the species Fossil, and used the language of the days of yore.

“But suppose he had lost it at play?”

“Then, madame, he would at least have had the pleasure of gambling.”

“And do you think he has had no pleasure here? See! look at Madame Firmiani.”

The brightest memories of the old man faded at the sight of his nephew’s so-called mistress. His anger died away at the gracious exclamation that escaped him as he looked at her. By one of those fortunate accidents that happen only to pretty women, it was a moment when all her beauties shone with peculiar lustre, perhaps due to the wax-lights, to the charming simplicity of her dress, and to the ineffable atmosphere of elegance that surrounded her. One must have studied the transitions of an evening in a Parisian salon to appreciate the imperceptible lights and shades which color a woman’s face and vary it. There comes a moment when, content with her toilet, pleased with her own wit, delighted to be admired, and feeling herself the queen of a salon full of remarkable men who smile to her, the Parisian woman reaches a full consciousness of her grace and charm; her beauty is enhanced by the looks she gathers in—a mute homage which she transfers with subtle glances to the man she loves. At moments like these a woman is invested with supernatural power and becomes a magician, a charmer, without herself knowing that she is one; involuntarily she inspires the love that fills her own bosom; her smiles and glances fascinate. If this condition, which comes from the soul, can give attraction even to a plain woman, with what radiance does it not invest a woman of natural elegance, distinguished bearing, fair, fresh, with sparkling eyes, and dressed in a taste that wrings approval from artists and her bitterest rivals.

Have you ever met a woman whose harmonious voice lends to her speech the same charm that emanates from her manners? A woman who knows how to speak and to be silent, whose words are happily chosen, whose language is pure, and who concerns herself in your interests with delicacy? Her raillery is caressing, her criticism never wounds; she neither discourses nor argues, but she likes to lead a discussion and stop it at the right moment. Her manner is affable and smiling, her politeness never forced, her readiness to serve others never servile; she reduces the respect she claims to a soft shadow; she never wearies you, and you leave her satisfied with her and with yourself. Her charming grace is conveyed to all the things with which she surrounds herself. Everything about her pleases the eye; in her presence you breathe, as it were, your native air. This woman is natural. There is no effort about her; she is aiming at no effect; her feelings are shown simply, because they are true. Frank herself, she does not wound the vanity of others; she accepts men as God made them; pitying the vicious, forgiving defects and absurdities, comprehending all ages, and vexed by nothing, because she has had the sense and tact to foresee all. Tender and gay, she gratifies before she consoles. You love her so well that if this angel did wrong you would be ready to excuse her. If, for your happiness, you have met with such a woman, you know Madame Firmiani.

After Monsieur de Bourbonne had talked with her for ten minutes, sitting beside her, his nephew was forgiven. He perceived that whatever the actual truth might be, the relation between Madame Firmiani and Octave covered some mystery. Returning to the illusions that gild the days of youth, and judging Madame Firmiani by her beauty, the old gentleman became convinced that a woman so innately conscious of her dignity as she appeared to be was incapable of a bad action. Her dark eyes told of inward peace; the lines of her face were so noble, the profile so pure, and the passion he had come to investigate seemed so little to oppress her heart, that the old man said to himself, while noting all the promises of love and virtue given by that adorable countenance, “My nephew is committing some folly.”

Madame Firmiani acknowledged to twenty-five. But the Practicals proved that having married the invisible Firmiani (then a highly respectable individual in the forties) in 1813, at the age of sixteen, she must be at least twenty-eight in 1825. However the same persons also asserted that at no period of her life had she ever been so desirable or so completely a woman. She was now at an age when women are most prone to conceive a passion, and to desire it, perhaps, in their pensive hours. She possessed all that earth sells, all that it lends, all that it gives. The Attaches declared there was nothing of which she was ignorant; the Contradictors asserted that there was much she ought to learn; the Observers remarked that her hands were white, her feet small, her movements a trifle too undulating. But, nevertheless, individuals of all species envied or disputed Octave’s happiness, agreeing, for once in a way, that Madame Firmiani was the most aristocratically beautiful woman in Paris.

Still young, rich, a perfect musician, intelligent, witty, refined, and received by the Princesse de Blamont-Chauvry, that oracle of the noble faubourg, loved by her rivals the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse her cousin, the Marquise d’Espard, and Madame de Macumer,—Madame Firmiani gratified all the vanities which feed or excite love. She was therefore sought by too many men not to fall a victim to Parisian malice and its charming calumnies, whispered behind a fan or in a safe aside. It was necessary to quote the remarks given at the beginning of this history to bring out the true Firmiani in contradistinction to the Firmiani of society. If some women forgave her happiness, others did not forgive her propriety. Now nothing is so dangerous in Paris as unfounded suspicions—for the reason that it is impossible to destroy them.

This sketch of a woman who was admirably natural gives only a faint idea of her. It would need the pencil of an Ingres to render the pride of that brow, with its wealth of hair, the dignity of that glance, and the thoughts betrayed by the changing colors of her cheeks. In her were all things; poets could have found an Agnes Sorel and a Joan of Arc, also the woman unknown, the Soul within that form, the soul of Eve, the knowledge of the treasures of good and the riches of evil, error and resignation, crime and devotion, the Donna Julia and the Haïdee of Lord Byron.

The former guardsman remained after the other guests had gone; Madame Firmiani found him in an armchair at two in the morning, determined to stay like a persistent fly. As she rose, the old gentleman spoke.

“Madame,” he said, “I am the uncle of Monsieur Octave de Camps.”

Madame Firmiani sat again, visibly moved. It was hard to tell whether she paled from shame or pleasure; delicate women hide their feelings and modesty betrays them. Monsieur de Bourbonne, less charitable in his view of provincials, did not interpret her agitation so generously.

“Well, monsieur?” she asked with a gaze that questioned more than it revealed.

“Madame,” the old man answered, “do you know what they told me at home? That my nephew has ruined himself for you and is living in a garret while you are in silk and gold. Forgive my rustic sincerity; but it may be useful for you to know these calumnies.”

“Stop, monsieur,” she replied with an imperative gesture. “I know all that. You are too polite to continue when I request you to drop the subject, and too gallant—not to say old-fashioned—not to believe you have no right to question me. It would be ridiculous to defend myself. I trust you will hold a good opinion of my character and believe the profound contempt I feel for money. I was married, without fortune, to a man of immense wealth. It is nothing to me whether your nephew is rich or poor; I received him because he is worthy of my friendship. My friends respect one another; I lack the philosophy to admit those I do not esteem; perhaps that seems uncharitable, but I abhor tattle and dishonesty.”

Her voice rose and ended with the ease and self-possession of an accomplished coquette.

“Madame,” said Monsieur de Bourbonne, moved, “I am an old man; I am almost Octave’s father, and I ask your pardon most humbly for the question I shall now venture to put to you, giving you my word of honor that your answer shall die here,”—laying his hand on his heart—“Are these rumors true; do you love Octave?”

“Monsieur,” she replied, “to any other man I should answer that question by a look; but to you, and because you are almost the father of Monsieur de Camps, I reply by asking what you would think of a woman if to such a question she answered you? To avow our love for him we love, when he loves us—ah! that may be; but even when we are certain of being loved forever, believe me, monsieur, it is an effort for us, and a reward to him. To say to another—”

She did not finish; she rose, bowed, and withdrew into her private apartments; the doors closed with a language of their own to his ears.

“Ah! the mischief!” thought Monsieur de Bourbonne; “what a woman! she is either sly or an angel.” He took his hired coach and left.

Next morning, about eight, Monsieur de Bourbonne climbed the stairs to Octave de Camps’s lodging in the rue de l’Observance. Octave was astonished to see him; his lamp still burned: he had been up all night.

“You rascal!” said Monsieur de Bourbonne, sitting in the nearest chair. “Since when is it fashion to laugh at uncles who have twenty-six thousand francs a year in solid acres, to which we are sole heir? In the olden time we stood in awe of uncles. Come, speak up: what have you to say for yourself? Haven’t I been a proper uncle? Have I ever refused you money? When did I shut the door in your face on pretext that you came to look after my health? You write to me, or you don’t—no matter, I live on pledged affection. I’m making you the prettiest estate in Touraine, the envy of the department. And what do you do? Sell your property and live like a lackey!”

“Uncle—”

“I’m not talking about uncles, I’m talking nephew. I have a right to your confidence. Have you been gambling? Lost at the Bourse? Say, ‘Uncle, I’m a wretch,’ and I’ll hug you. If you tell me any lies greater than those I used to tell at your age I’ll sell my property, buy an annuity, and go back to the evil ways of my youth—if I can.”

“Uncle—”

“I saw your Madame Firmiani yesterday,” went on the old fellow, kissing the tips of his fingers. “She is charming. You have my consent and approbation, if that will do you any good. As to the sanction of the Church I suppose that’s useless—sacraments cost so much. Come, speak out, have you ruined yourself for her?”

“Yes, uncle.”

“Ha! the jade! I’d have wagered it. In my time women of the court were cleverer at ruining a man than the courtesans of today; but this one—she has something of the last century.”

“Uncle,” said Octave, with tender gravity, “you are mistaken. Madame Firmiani deserves your esteem and all the world’s adoration.”

“Youth, youth! always the same!” cried the old man. “Well, go on; tell me the same old story. Remember my experience in gallantry is not of yesterday.”

“My dear kind uncle, here is a letter which will tell you nearly all,” said Octave, taking a letter from an elegant portfolio—no doubt a gift from her. “When you have read it I will tell you the rest and you will then know a Madame Firmiani unknown to the world.”

“I haven’t my spectacles; read it aloud.”

Octave began.

“My beloved—” Monsieur de Bourbonne interrupted. “Then you are still intimate with her?”

“Why yes, of course.”

“You haven’t parted?”

“Parted!” Octave echoed; “we are married.”

“Heavens!” cried the old man, “then why do you live in a garret?”

“Let me go on.”

Octave resumed, reading passages with deep emotion.

“‘My beloved Husband,—You ask me the reason of my sadness. Has it, then, passed from my soul to my face; or have you only guessed it?—but how could you fail to do so, one in heart as we are? I cannot deceive you; this may be a misfortune, for it is one of the conditions of happy love that a wife shall be gay and caressing. Perhaps I ought to deceive you, but I would not do it even if the happiness with which you have blessed and overpowered me depended on it.

“‘Ah! dearest, how much gratitude there is in my love. I long to love you forever, without limit; yes, I desire to be forever proud of you. A woman’s glory is in the man she loves. Esteem, consideration, honor,—must they not be his who receives our all? Well, my angel has fallen. Yes, dear, the tale you told me has tarnished my past joys. Since then I have felt myself humiliated in you,—you whom I thought the most honorable of men, as you are the most loving, the most tender. I must indeed have deep confidence in your heart, so young and pure, to make you this avowal which costs me much. Ah! my dear love, how is it that you, knowing your father had unjustly deprived others of their property, that YOU can keep it?

“‘And you told me of this criminal act in a room filled with the mute witnesses of our love; and you are a gentleman, and you think yourself noble, and I am yours! I try to find excuses for you; I do find them in your youth and thoughtlessness. I know there is still something of the child about you. Perhaps you have never thought seriously of what fortune and integrity are. Oh! how your laugh wounded me. Reflect on that ruined family, always in distress; poor young girls who have reason to curse you daily; an old father saying each night: “We might not now be starving if that man’s father had been an honest man—”’”

“Good heavens!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne, interrupting. “Surely you have not been such a fool as to tell that woman about your father’s affair with the Bourgneufs? Women know more about wasting a fortune than making one.”

“They know about integrity. But let me read on, uncle.”

“‘Octave, no power on earth has authority to change the principles of honor. Look into your conscience and ask it by what name you are to call the action by which you hold your property.’”

The uncle lowered his head as the nephew read.

“‘I will not tell you all the thoughts that assail me; they can be reduced to one,—this is it: I cannot respect the man who, knowingly, is smirched for a sum of money, whatever the amount; five francs stolen at play or five times a hundred thousand gained by a legal trick are equally dishonoring. I will tell you all. I feel myself degraded by the very love which has hitherto been all my joy. There rises in my soul a voice which my tenderness cannot stifle. Ah! I have wept to feel that I have more conscience than love. Were you to commit a crime I would hide you in my bosom from human justice, but my devotion could go no farther. Love, to a woman, means boundless confidence, united to a need of reverencing, of esteeming, the being to whom she belongs. I have never conceived of love otherwise than as a fire in which all noble feelings are purified still more,—a fire which develops them.

“‘I have but one thing else to say: come to me poor, and my love shall be redoubled. If not, renounce it. Should I see you no more, I shall know what it means.

“‘But I do not wish, understand me, that you should make restitution because I urge it. Consult your own conscience. An act of justice such as that ought not to be a sacrifice made to love. I am your wife and not your mistress, and it is less a question of pleasing me than of inspiring in my soul a true respect.

“‘If I am mistaken, if you have ill-explained your father’s action, if, in short, you still think your right to the property equitable (oh! how I long to persuade myself that you are blameless), consider and decide by listening to the voice of your conscience; act wholly and solely from yourself. A man who loves a woman sincerely, as you love me, respects the sanctity of her trust in him too deeply to dishonor himself.

“‘I blame myself now for what I have written; a word might have sufficed, and I have preached to you! Scold me; I wish to be scolded,—but not much, only a little. Dear, between us two the power is yours—you alone should perceive your own faults.’”

“Well, uncle?” said Octave, eyes full of tears.

“There’s more in the letter; finish it.”

“Oh, the rest is only to be read by a lover,” Octave answered, smiling.

“Yes, right, my boy,” said the old man gently. “I have had many affairs in my day, but I beg you to believe that I too have loved, ‘et ego in Arcadia.’ But I don’t understand yet why you give lessons in mathematics.”

“My dear uncle, I am your nephew; isn’t that as good as saying I dipped into the capital left me by my father? After I read this letter a revolution took place within me. I paid my whole arrearage of remorse in one day. I cannot describe the state I was in. As I drove in the Bois a voice called to me, ‘That horse is not yours’; when I ate my dinner it said, ‘You have stolen this food.’ I was ashamed. The fresher my honesty, the more intense it was. I rushed to Madame Firmiani. Uncle! that day I had pleasures of the heart, enjoyments of the soul far beyond millions. Together we made out the account of what was due to the Bourgneufs, and I condemned myself, against Madame Firmiani’s advice, to pay three per cent interest. But all I had did not suffice to cover the full amount. We were lovers enough for her to offer, and me to accept, her savings—”

“What! besides her other virtues does that adorable woman lay by money?” cried his uncle.

“Don’t laugh at her, uncle; her position obliged her to be careful. Her husband went to Greece in 1820 and died there three years later. It has been impossible thus far to get legal proof of his death, or obtain the will leaving his property to his wife. These papers were either lost or stolen during the troubles in Greece—a country where registers are not kept as in France, and where we have no consul. Uncertain whether she might be forced to give up her fortune, she has lived with the utmost prudence. As for me, I wish to acquire property that shall be mine, to provide for my wife if she loses hers.”

“But why didn’t you tell me all this? My dear nephew, you might have known I love you enough to pay all your good debts, the debts of a gentleman. I’ll play the traditional uncle now, and revenge myself!”

“Ah! uncle, I know your vengeance! but let me get rich by my own industry. If you want to do me a real service, make me an allowance of two or three thousand francs a year till I see my way to an enterprise for which I shall want capital. At this moment I am so happy that all I desire is the means of living. I give lessons so that I may not live at the cost of any one. If you only knew the happiness I had in making that restitution! I found the Bourgneufs, after a good deal of trouble, living miserably and in need of everything. The old father was a lottery agent; the two daughters kept his books and took care of the house; the mother was always ill. The daughters are charming, but they had been cruelly taught that the world thinks little of beauty without money. What a scene it was! I entered their house the accomplice in a crime; I left it an honest man who had purged his father’s memory. Uncle, I don’t judge him; there is such excitement, such passion in a lawsuit that even an honorable man may be led astray. Lawyers can make the most unjust claims legal; laws have convenient syllogisms to quiet consciences. My visit was a drama. To be Providence itself; actually to fulfill that futile wish, ‘If heaven were to send us twenty thousand francs a year’—that silly wish we all make laughing; to bring opulence to a family sitting by the light of one miserable lamp over a poor turf fire—no, words cannot describe it. My extreme justice seemed to them unjust. Well! if there is a Paradise my father is happy in it now. As for me, I am loved as no man was ever loved yet. Madame Firmiani gives me more than happiness; she has inspired me with a delicacy of feeling I lacked. So I call her my dear conscience—a love-word expressing secret harmonies within our hearts. I find honesty profitable; I shall get rich in time by myself. I’ve an industrial scheme in my head, and if it succeeds I shall earn millions.”

“Ah! my boy, you have your mother’s soul,” said the old man, his eyes filling at the thought of his sister.

At that moment, the sound of a carriage could be heard in the street.

“There she is!” cried Octave. A few moments later Madame Firmiani entered the room.

“Ah!” she exclaimed, annoyed to see Monsieur de Bourbonne. “But our uncle is not in the way,” she added quickly, smiling. “I came to humbly entreat my husband to accept my fortune. The Austrian Embassy has just sent me a document which proves the death of Monsieur Firmiani, and also the will, which his valet was keeping safely to put into my hands. Octave, you can accept it all; you are richer than I, for you have treasures here”—laying her hand upon his heart—“to which none but God can add.” Then, unable to contain her happiness, she laid her head upon her husband’s breast.

“My dear niece,” said the old man, “in my day we made love; in yours, you love. You women are all that is best in humanity; you are not even guilty of your faults, for they come through us.”
