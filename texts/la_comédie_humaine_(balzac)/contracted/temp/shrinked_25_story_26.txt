Bianchon, a prominent Parisian physician, trained under the brilliant but troubled Desplein. Despite his acclaim, Desplein struggled to establish a lasting legacy and embraced atheism, feeling isolated in his beliefs. He rejected the existence of a soul, which he never encountered in his dissections.

Desplein's life was filled with contradictions that critics exploited. His eccentricities, such as his varied dress and complex personality, led to mixed judgments. He was both generous and miserly, often expressing disdain for humanity based on his observations.

Among his students, Bianchon stood out, having faced poverty while studying medicine. Known for his integrity and selflessness, he was a loyal friend. Desplein recognized Bianchon's potential and mentored him in medical practice and Parisian society.

Bianchon was surprised to see Desplein enter a church, given his atheism, which piqued his curiosity about Desplein's beliefs. During a dinner conversation, Desplein dismissed the significance of the mass, criticizing its historical implications and associated bloodshed.

Months later, when questioned about his church visit, Desplein offered a vague excuse. Intrigued, Bianchon observed Desplein's behavior and confirmed his attendance at mass again, discovering that Desplein had founded the very service he attended. This revelation deepened the mystery surrounding Desplein, highlighting the complexity of a man who, despite his atheistic convictions, engaged in rituals that contradicted his beliefs, leaving Bianchon pondering Desplein's enigmatic nature.

Doctor Bianchon took seven years to recount a pivotal incident in Desplein's life. After the Revolution of 1830, amidst the chaos in Paris, Bianchon saw Desplein entering Saint-Sulpice and joined him for mass. Intrigued by Desplein's attendance, Bianchon sought an explanation.

Desplein shared that he had faced extreme poverty in his early years, living in a dilapidated building where hunger and isolation plagued him as he struggled to finance his medical education. He described his life as a relentless battle against despair, lacking support from family or friends. His meals were often limited to stale bread soaked in milk, and he frequently lacked proper clothing or companionship.

Despite these hardships, Desplein was determined to succeed. He immersed himself in his studies, driven by the desire to escape his dire circumstances. He felt misunderstood by those who had never experienced true poverty, likening their ignorance to a princess suggesting the hungry should simply buy cake.

Desplein's bitterness arose from witnessing the selfishness and envy of others in the competitive Parisian environment. He expressed deep mistrust of humanity, feeling that mediocrity constantly undermined those striving for greatness.

During this challenging time, Desplein faced eviction due to unpaid rent. His neighbor, Bourgeat, a humble water-carrier, offered assistance by paying Desplein's debts and supporting his education, treating him like a son. Their bond deepened, and when Desplein became a house surgeon, he felt a profound loss leaving Bourgeat behind. When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein dedicated himself to his care, but despite his efforts, Bourgeat passed away, leaving Desplein heartbroken. He honored Bourgeat's wishes by arranging masses for his soul, reflecting on the man's unwavering faith and love.

Desplein's relationship with Bourgeat profoundly impacted his life, leading him to reconcile skepticism with gratitude. Ultimately, Bianchon pondered whether Bourgeat's faith might have opened the gates of Heaven for Desplein, highlighting themes of sacrifice, gratitude, and the complexities of belief.
