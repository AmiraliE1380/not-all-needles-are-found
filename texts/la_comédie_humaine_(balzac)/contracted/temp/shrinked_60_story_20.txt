Monsieur de Manerville, a Norman gentleman turned Gascon through marriage, served as major of the Gate Guards until his death in 1813. After his wife’s death in 1810, he became miserly, restricting resources for his son, Paul. Paul returned from college in 1810, living under strict control, which hindered his social development. After his father's death, he inherited a fortune but felt disillusioned with Bordeaux. He traveled Europe for six years, believing his noble title would attract a partner. In winter 1821, Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux, eager to restore his family mansion and marry Mademoiselle Evangelista, a celebrated beauty. Despite their attraction, both hesitated to express their feelings. As their bond deepened, Paul idealized Natalie, while negotiations over her dowry revealed tensions between their families, foreshadowing future challenges.

The next day, jeweler Elie Magus appraised Madame Evangelista's diamonds for the marriage contract of Comte Paul and Mademoiselle Natalie, valuing them at 250,000 francs but offering only 75,000 for the “Discreto” diamond due to depreciation. This appraisal sparked rumors about the marriage's stability, leading to speculation about Madame Evangelista selling her house and potential wedding cancellation. Despite notaries denying these claims, public skepticism persisted, fueled by jealousy of the Evangelista family's wealth.

To counter the gossip, Madame Evangelista organized an extravagant "fete of the camellias." As the signing day approached, she feared for her daughter's fortune upon realizing the contract's implications. The courtyard was transformed into a lavish tent, and tension grew as the entailment clause was explained, revealing potential financial ruin for Natalie if Paul died first.

Despite her frustrations, Madame Evangelista decided to proceed with the signing, vowing to protect her daughter's interests. As festivities began, she felt a mix of triumph and resentment, aware that her daughter's happiness was tied to her ambitions. 

The next day, she advised Natalie on managing her marriage, emphasizing the importance of nurturing love. As the wedding approached, Madame Evangelista proposed selling her jewelry to support Natalie, which Paul accepted, unaware of the underlying tensions. 

Five years later, Paul revealed to Monsieur Mathias that he had left Natalie to rebuild his fortune in Calcutta, citing massive debts. As he prepared to board a ship, he ignored important letters from Mathias. At sea, he opened them to find Natalie's anguish over his departure and a warning of a conspiracy against him, prompting feelings of anger and despair but a resolve to confront his challenges.
