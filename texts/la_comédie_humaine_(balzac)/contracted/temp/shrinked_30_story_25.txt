Simonnin, the lawyer’s clerk, leaned from a window and flung a breadcrumb at a shabby stranger in Maître Derville’s courtyard, jeering, “There is that old Box-coat again!” Boucard, the head clerk, admonished him—however poor, a client is a man—while others, including Godeschal, taunted the visitor and plotted a prank. Laughter filled the cluttered, musty office; such disorder was routine.

When the weary man entered and politely asked for M. Derville, Boucard brusquely told him to come back at one. The clerks jeered—nobleman or porter?—and laughed when he insisted he was Colonel Chabert, reported killed at Eylau. About one in the morning he knocked again. The porter said Derville had not returned, but the head clerk explained Derville preferred to work at night. When Derville arrived he was struck by the man’s thin, pale appearance and scars. The claim to be the colonel—presumed dead at Eylau—made Derville and Boucard suspect madness, but Chabert insisted on telling his story.

Chabert recounted Eylau: gravely wounded and buried among corpses, he had miraculously survived because his horse’s body shielded him. He woke in a grave, struggled free, and was rescued by a woman who took him to a hospital. After months he recovered but had lost all papers. Without documents, his attempts to reclaim his life met disbelief and ridicule; he wandered, was treated as mad, and spent two years confined in an asylum. Upon release he learned his wife, the Countess Ferraud, had remarried and borne children.

Sensing the gravity though skeptical, Derville offered help: he would obtain papers from Germany, gave a small sum for subsistence, and provided a letter to a notary. Overwhelmed, Chabert felt hope for the first time in years, voiced a longing for vengeance, and declared he would rather die than live in obscurity. Derville, recognising the case’s complexity, vowed to treat it seriously, restoring Chabert’s purpose.

About three months after the interview, in Derville’s room, the notary Crottat—who had taken the post after his chief fled in bankruptcy—demanded the six hundred francs the old soldier had received be refunded. Crottat mocked Derville’s philanthropy; Derville admitted his generosity had only reached twenty-five louis.

Seeing German-stamped papers on Crottat’s desk, Derville had his clerk Boucard translate them. A Berlin notary reported that requested documents would arrive and were legally stamped; witnesses to the recorded facts still lived in Eylau, Prussia, and the woman who had saved Comte Chabert was alive in Heilsberg. “This looks like business,” Derville observed.

Crottat then outlined the background and the villainy of Roguin—mockingly called “the unfortunate Roguin”—who had absconded with eight hundred thousand francs of clients’ money. As third clerk, Crottat had copied the papers and recited the family and estate history: Rose Chapotel, wife of Hyacinthe (known as Chabert, Count of the Empire), had married without settlement, so they held property in common. The personalty had been about six hundred thousand francs. Before marriage Colonel Chabert’s will bequeathed one-quarter to hospitals and one-quarter to the State; after the will was contested and assets sold, the widow was left only with the portion bequeathed to the treasury, and Chabert’s personal fortune was reduced to roughly three hundred thousand francs.

Derville found the Colonel in the Faubourg Saint-Marceau with an old quartermaster named Vergniaud. The house was squalid—bare, damp, nearly empty—yet Chabert, smoking his pipe, retained a soldier’s calm. When asked why he had not moved to Paris, Chabert said the local folk had fed him for a year and he could not leave them now that he had a little money. He insisted he had done no one wrong and slept in peace.

Derville told him the Heilsberg papers were expected and that the woman who saved him was alive. Chabert, desperate for money he did not possess, reacted with frustration. Derville outlined the legal difficulties: proving identity would require costly, lengthy proceedings; the supposed bigamy—Comtesse Ferraud’s remarriage—would provoke inquiries, translations, and foreign formalities that could drag on for years and exhaust resources. Overwhelmed, Chabert despaired; Derville reassured him a favorable outcome was possible and proposed a friendly suit to annul the death certificate and the subsequent marriage. He offered a power of attorney and financial help; Chabert, hopeless but trusting, agreed.

As Derville left, Louis Vergniaud asked for a loan to improve the Colonel’s lodgings. Derville agreed but insisted Chabert should help his friends. He then planned to visit Comtesse Ferraud to gauge her position and leverage.

Madame Chabert, now Comtesse Ferraud, had married Monsieur le Comte Ferraud after the Revolution and converted her share of Chabert’s fortune into a comfortable income. Her marriage was socially accepted; Ferraud was politically ambitious and uneasy about the match. Derville found her at the Hotel Ferraud amid luxury. She greeted him lightly until he revealed that Colonel Chabert lived. Her laughter faded as he warned of the legal and social danger if Chabert’s identity were proven and hinted that Ferraud might not support her. Struck by the implications, she asked, “Does he still love me?”—already considering how to turn affection into a settlement. Derville left confident he had gained the upper hand.

A week after Derville’s visits, Colonel Chabert and Madame la Comtesse Ferraud arrived at their lawyer’s office from opposite ends of Paris. With Derville’s help Chabert had been restored—dignified in a blue suit, wearing the Legion of Honor ribbon, his soldierly bearing returned. The Countess, made up to seem youthful, followed. Clerks noticed; sensing tension, Derville separated them and presented a memorandum: Chabert would formally acknowledge his identity in exchange for financial support from his wife. The Countess, aghast at public exposure, resisted.

Chabert confronted her about their past, exposing the pain of their separation. She feigned surprise and tried to leave; Derville urged her to stay. A heated exchange ensued. The Countess then took him in her carriage to her country house in Groslay, confessing she loved another man, Comte Ferraud, and feared scandal that would destroy her dignity. Hurt, Chabert offered a measured understanding, aware of the social consequences she dreaded.

At Groslay she treated him kindly and they reminisced; he found temporary solace though resentment lingered. Meanwhile she moved to secure her position, writing to Delbecq to neutralize Chabert’s claim. Discovering this betrayal, Chabert resolved to confront her but ultimately sacrificed his identity to protect her and their children, renouncing his name and withdrawing from society.

Months later Derville found him reduced to a vagabond in a courtroom, poor yet retaining dignity and scorn for society’s indifference. In June 1840 Godeschal accompanied Derville to Ris; near Bicêtre they found an old pauper under an elm, drying his handkerchief. In shabby clothes with a carved, grotesque look, he was recognized as Comte Chabert—likely consigned to the workhouse because he reminded the Countess of their past. Insisting he was now “Hyacinthe,” a number in the institution, he envied a condemned man for not being married, accepted snuff gratefully, saluted them as “brave troopers,” and cried “Vive Napoleon!” Derville reflected on Chabert’s tragic arc—from foundling to Napoleonic soldier to workhouse inmate—and on human baseness: betrayal, greed, and moral decay. An addendum lists characters from Balzac’s Human Comedy and their other appearances, including Napoleon and Comtesse Ferraud.
