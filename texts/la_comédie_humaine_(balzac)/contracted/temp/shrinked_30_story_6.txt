The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once a dark, winding lane near the Hôtel de Ville, ended at the Rue Martroi and was closed by a turnstile until 1823. Less than six feet wide at its broadest, it held pooled gutter water and stayed perpetually muddy; sunlight reached it briefly in summer, yet dampness lingered. Residents lit lamps at five in June and kept them burning through winter. Approaching the Marais felt like walking through cellars, as many old Paris streets were similarly gloomy. The corner house at Rue du Tourniquet and Rue de la Tixeranderie bore iron rings from chains once used for public safety.

Built to combat humidity, the house had cellars raised above ground and an entrance reached by three steps. The door formed a closed arch with a keystone carved as a female head. Three high windows, barred with iron, lit a small dwelling where two beds draped in green serge and an old woman tending a brazier—preparing a porter’s-wife stew—were visible. The kitchen-dining room was sparsely furnished but kept with evident cleanliness and thrift.

The old woman’s pale, wrinkled face seemed part of the house itself. She sat by the fireplace with a look of mischief mixed with resignation while her daughter worked at an embroidery frame. With slow fingers and antiquated spectacles, the mother occasionally glanced out the window; the daughter bent over her work with focused modesty. Their modest home reflected the lives of working-class Parisian women. Despite poverty, the girl retained youthful charm—rosy cheeks and bright eyes—showing a spirit not wholly dimmed. The mother speculated about passersby and hoped for a better future for her daughter; the girl usually kept her gaze down, absorbed in embroidery.

One August day in 1815 the mother noticed a new face among the regular passersby: a tall, thin man in black whose solemn bearing intrigued her. She fancied he was a clerk from the Préfet’s office and awaited his return. When he passed again Caroline, roused from indifference, felt an unfamiliar sympathy, noting traces of suffering on his face. For the first time a passerby stirred such feeling in her; she now looked forward to another glimpse, intrigued by the possibility he might alter her life.

For two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved erratically, no longer passing the Rue du Tourniquet at the same hour. Madame Crochard noticed his distracted manner—often lost in thought and seldom acknowledging her or her daughter, Caroline. One mid-September morning Caroline’s lively presence caught his eye; their fleeting glances suggested a silent understanding.

Weeks later he began passing twice daily, and their brief encounters settled into a mutual routine. Their wordless exchanges conveyed concern: Caroline sensed when he was troubled, and he could tell when she worried about her work. Both remained cautious, fearing greater intimacy might bring complications.

Madame Crochard, frustrated by her daughter’s indecision, turned her displeasure toward the Gentleman, who had once earned her mild approval. As winter wore on and hardships increased, he overheard a revealing mother-and-daughter conversation about their finances and, moved, secretly left money for them. Caroline found it the next day; a silent moment of gratitude passed between them.

In early May Caroline proposed a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—more somber than usual—unexpectedly joined. During the journey Madame Crochard pretended to sleep, giving Caroline and the Gentleman a chance for light, revealing talk. The countryside’s beauty and their easy exchange lifted his spirits; he began to see Caroline differently.

Their bond deepened as they shared personal stories—Caroline’s family hardships and her mother’s sacrifices, and the Gentleman—now called Roger—was drawn to her honesty and resilience. The day brimmed with laughter and joy, and as evening fell Caroline voiced her newfound happiness. Roger considered the possibility of lasting joy; playfully holding hands before leaving—Madame Crochard feigning not to notice—sealed an unspoken promise and hope for a brighter future.

Roger and Caroline’s adventures continue in modern Paris, where new apartments cater to honeymooners. In a pristine building on Rue Taitbout Caroline has lived for over a month in a charming second-floor flat, furnished with care. The rooms, freshly painted and elegantly appointed, follow current trends influenced by English design.

Her bedroom’s gray hangings trimmed with green silk, comfortable chairs and a writing table for love letters are completed by a bronze clock depicting Love crowning Psyche and a Gothic-pattern carpet. As she prepares for Roger’s visit, her hairdresser flatters her, echoing his admiration.

On a chilly September day in 1816 Caroline wears a green grenadine gown trimmed with chinchilla and steps onto the balcony to watch for Roger, her heart racing. After fifteen minutes impatience replaces excitement, but she brightens when she finally sees him. They embrace passionately, rejoicing in their reunion after three days apart. He then says he must attend a meeting with the Attorney‑General, which disappoints her; she playfully objects to his enjoying the theatre without her and declares her deep affection.

Returning to tenderness, she wishes to take his name—Caroline de Bellefeuille—and, perched on his lap, speaks of using her earnings to help the poor. She plays a sonata as Roger prepares to leave; after a playful farewell she watches him drive away, savoring the moment and longing for his return.

Five years after settling in her Rue Taitbout home, Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille sits contentedly with her four-year-old son Charles, who plays with a rocking horse. Now twenty-four, her beauty reflects happiness and fulfillment. She has embraced motherhood, devoted to raising her children and cultivating their appreciation for goodness and beauty. Content with simple pleasures, she is indifferent to society’s expectations and never seeks luxury or status.

Rocking her infant daughter Eugenie, Caroline recalls joyful visits to Bellefeuille with Roger. Their love has grown in the simplicity of shared life, free from social complication, yet she wonders why Roger insists on secrecy and what their arrangement truly is.

On May 6, 1822—the anniversary of a pivotal day—Caroline prepares for dinner, expecting Roger. He brings a document detailing Eugenie’s marriage portion and explains his view that Charles, as a boy, must learn self-sufficiency while Eugenie, as a girl, deserves a fortune. Their evening is warm and lively, ending with a magical lantern show for Charles. But as night deepens, Roger must leave; he promises three days off, yet duty and his habit of secrecy leave Caroline with the familiar ache of separation.

Soon after, Caroline hastens to her mother, Madame Crochard, who is gravely ill. The widow’s acquaintances, unaware Caroline is her daughter, have summoned a priest for last rites. Arriving amid confusion and deceit, Caroline finds local women hoping to secure benefit from the widow’s wealth. Despite their intentions, Caroline cares for her mother tenderly. In her final moments, Madame Crochard laments their estrangement and reveals that the priest made her confess about Caroline’s benefactor—an admission that hints at a complex web of relationships and secrets to follow.

To clarify those connections, the narrative backtracks to November 1805. A young barrister, Monsieur de Granville, precarious after leaving a gathering at the High Chancellor’s, receives a letter urging his swift return: a proposed marriage to Mademoiselle Angelique Bontems could secure his future despite the Bontems’ social stigma. Traveling to Bayeux, he finds the Bontems’ household stifling under the widow’s strict piety. During a church service he glimpses Angelique; her beauty captivates him and rekindles a childhood affection, setting the stage for a reunion that may alter both their lives. The intertwined stories of Caroline and Granville explore love, duty, and social expectation as each navigates personal desire against familial obligation.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

For about two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he visited her dreary drawing-room and watched Angelique mark her trousseau. She never opened discussions about religion; when he playfully handled her rosary or mocked practices she calmly returned it to its bag. Firm in the Church’s teachings, she insisted a mother must have a religion and questioned how one could judge between disbelievers and God.

Granville, taken by her conviction, sometimes mistook desire for love and even felt tempted to adopt her beliefs. Angelique reconciled feeling with duty, leaving him uncertain about his own emotions. He was grateful to Madame Bontems for Angelique’s religious grounding, seeing it as a resource for life’s trials. On the day of the marriage contract Madame Bontems extracted a promise from Granville to respect Angelique’s practices and allow her freedom of conscience; Angelique’s gentle look convinced him and the Abbe Fontanon approved. After their wedding they moved to Paris, where Granville became public prosecutor.

Angelique persuaded him to take a ground-floor apartment in the Marais near a church and chapel, arguing it would be better for their future children. Granville, preferring a more fashionable quarter, yielded. He set up his study and left furnishing to Angelique, who took delight in arranging the home. He was dismayed by the cold solemnity she created — a reflection of her austere upbringing. He tried to understand her choices and comforted her that happiness came from her love and gentleness, not material things, yet he grew frustrated by the lack of warmth and charm.

From spring through winter the couple seemed harmonious; Granville stayed absorbed in work and remained largely oblivious to Angelique’s strict observances—her Lenten austerities he passed off as carelessness. He went to church with her on Sundays but did not grasp her fervor until a disruptive event.

In November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux, Angelique’s spiritual director, arrived in Paris alarmed at what he saw as her moral decline and aimed to reclaim her from a lax lifestyle. Angelique adopted stricter practices that sparked misunderstandings over social obligations. Granville was irritated by her frequent excuses to avoid events, especially balls. After a tense evening he confronted her about her demeanor and dress, urging her to fulfill her social role. Arguments escalated: Angelique cited her director’s prohibitions; Granville argued the priest was overreaching. Angelique even wrote to the Pope for guidance.

The Pope’s reply backed Granville, saying a wife should accompany her husband even at some personal cost. Rather than reconcile them, the decision deepened the divide: Angelique and her director dismissed it. Frustrated, Granville began asserting his preferences more forcefully—ordering meat daily despite her objections—and retreated from social life, weighed down by what he saw as her bigotry.

Their home grew oppressive and joyless, its rigid piety affecting everyone, including the servants. Angelique’s austerity dimmed her beauty; duty replaced warmth. Granville felt trapped in a marriage that left little room for affection. Their children, raised under strict rules, suffered; he sent his sons away to school early and planned to marry off his daughters as soon as possible. Increasingly isolated, he sought refuge in work and a legal book, while Angelique’s efforts to reconvert him only worsened their estrangement. The household became a cold, rigid place rather than a home of companionship.

Between 1806 and 1825 the de Granville household showed little outward drama, but Madame de Granville was inwardly unhappy. After losing her husband's affection she persisted in Masses and prayers for insight and reconciliation, while Granville, now a prominent judge, took up residence in the entresol to avoid her. Each morning a housekeeper brought messages; he routinely refused breakfast, citing urgent court business.

At thirty-five and prematurely aged, she waited in a state of mounting bitterness. When Granville declined the presidency of a provincial court, friends and her confessor suspected an illicit affair—a notion that mortified her, as she could not imagine such sin. During Lent in 1822 her health worsened from stress and fever, made worse by his indifference and by her own pious efforts, which undermined her attempts at warmth.

Recovering in late May, she consulted the Abbe Fontanon, who revealed that Granville had lived for seven years with a concubine, fathering two children and dissipating their fortune. Shocked and torn between faith and betrayal, she struggled to accept it. The Abbe urged patience and forgiveness; she left determined to confront her husband.

Before she could, Roger and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille argued elsewhere; Mademoiselle declared hatred for Madame de Granville, blaming her for their unhappiness. Roger spoke of future hopes, but the exchange was interrupted when Madame de Granville fainted. On recovering she confronted Granville, who accused her of subordinating his happiness to piety and failing as a loving wife.

Their confrontation exposed irreconcilable differences: he insisted love requires sacrifice and sought happiness elsewhere after years of neglect; she clung to moral righteousness. His admission and refusal to restore what neglect had extinguished left her devastated. The dialogue ended with the stark truth that their marriage had failed—love cannot be commanded or revived by will—and Madame de Granville was left to face the consequences.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

Early one December night in 1833 a white‑haired man whose face showed grief more than years paced the Rue Gaillon and stopped before a modest two‑story house with a dim attic light. A young doctor, Horace Bianchon, emerged; recognizing the visitor, he offered his arm and bantered about the hour and the slippery pavement. The visitor—Comte de Granville—replied with weary pride that at fifty‑five he was still hale, and that when not visiting in the evening he often walked home from the Courts or the club.

Bianchon teased that a man of his station, with fine horses and large sums about him, was courting robbery. Granville shrugged off the danger with melancholy and confessed, with a hint of childish amusement, that he had been spying on a shadow in the third‑floor window of Bianchon’s house. He took a morbid interest in that garret, likening his curiosity to a citizen’s pleasure in watching the Palais‑Royal being finished.

When Bianchon offered to tell him who lived in the garret, Granville stopped him. He declared he would not pay to learn whether the shadow was man or woman, happy or miserable. Nine years earlier he had ceased to be surprised that old men found pastime in growing flowers and trees; life’s events had taught him to distrust all human affection. “I grew old within a few days,” he said. He would no longer attach himself to any living creature save “unreasoning animals, or plants, or superficial things”—even Taglioni’s grace seemed to move him more than human feeling. He abhorred life and insisted nothing could interest him.

Bianchon probed gently: did he have children? Granville answered bitterly, listing them—an eldest daughter the Comtesse de Vandenesse, another daughter suitably married through her sister, sons successful in law: a viscount who had been public prosecutor at Limoges and now President of the Court at Orléans, the younger a public prosecutor in Paris. Each had their cares; none had devoted themselves to him. If one had tried to fill the void in his breast, that child would have ruined his life by sacrificing itself to him—and why should it? Perhaps, he added acidly, they were now waiting for his money. Bianchon protested; Granville, sarcastic and self‑aware, explained his philanthropy as self‑indulgence: he paid for sensations the way he gambled, seeking illusions that might make his heart glow while expecting no gratitude. He would see Bianchon die without blinking and asked that Bianchon feel the same toward him. The events of life had flowed over his heart like lava over Herculaneum—the town remained, dead.

Unable to leave him in that mood, Bianchon insisted he could treat Granville’s malady and then, with reluctance, described the garret’s occupant. The picture was stark: a woman of about thirty whose love had become fanatical. She adored a young man of pleasing appearance but every vice—Solvet—a gambler given to wine and women, guilty of acts the law might punish. This woman had abandoned a comfortable life and an adoring husband, the father of her children, for this man. She worked day and night; he had stolen from her even funds saved for the children’s food and clothes. Only three days earlier she had sold her hair—the finest Bianchon had ever seen—and when the lover returned she could not hide the gold quickly enough. For a smile or a kiss she would give away the price of a fortnight’s subsistence. The toil had hollowed her cheeks; the children’s cries had broken her heart; she lay ill on her wretched bed that night. When Bianchon visited, the family had nothing to eat; the children were so weak they could not even cry.

Despite himself Granville put his hand in his waistcoat pocket as if to help, but Bianchon begged him to pity the woman and to help cure her passion. Granville recoiled: how could he pity a misery whose joys he might have prized with his whole fortune? A living woman who felt—did not thousands of dead men, thousands of sick and old men, long to barter everything for a return of youth and feeling? Bianchon murmured “Poor Caroline!” At the name Granville shuddered, gripped the doctor’s arm, and asked hoarsely whether the woman’s name was Caroline Crochard. When Bianchon confirmed it, Granville’s reaction was violent: he accused Bianchon of keeping his word in rousing the worst pain his heart could endure, an emotion he called a gift from hell. He knew how to pay such debts.

They had reached the Rue de la Chaussée d’Antin when a shriveled night scavenger stood by the curb. Granville asked if the scavenger ever found thousand‑franc notes and, hearing that sometimes he did, handed him such a note with a grotesque injunction: spend it in the wine shop, get drunk, start fights, beat your wife, blacken your friends’ eyes—give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist, perhaps to the police, public prosecutor, judge, and prison wardens. “Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later,” Granville said. He pointed out the scavenger’s corrupt face to Bianchon and declared he had thus “squared accounts with hell” and had found a perverse pleasure in the money. As for Caroline Crochard, he pronounced coldly that she might die of hunger and thirst listening to the cries of her starving children, convinced of her lover’s baseness; he would not give a sou to rescue her. Because Bianchon had helped her, he said, he would see him no more.

Granville left Bianchon stunned and walked to the Rue Saint‑Lazare where a carriage waited. A servant informed him that his son, the attorney‑general, had been to see him an hour earlier and was waiting in his bedroom. Granville dismissed the servant, then confronted his son—in a tone at once imperious and unexpectedly tender—about coming after being ordered not to visit unless summoned.

Eugène, the younger man, spoke with tremor: that afternoon a very young man arrested in the house of a friend he had robbed had appealed to Granville, claiming to be his son. “His name?” Granville asked. “Charles Crochard,” Eugène answered. Granville waved him down: that sufficed. He stood a moment, then spoke as a father. “Charles Crochard spoke the truth. I am glad you came to me tonight, my good Eugène.” He handed his son a bundle of banknotes—“a considerable sum”—and authorized him to use it as he saw fit in the affair. He trusted him implicitly and approved whatever arrangements Eugène might make now or later.

The mood shifted. Granville’s last words of parental counsel and farewell were almost tender. He warned Eugène about the weight of marriage—“that act is the most important society requires of us”—and urged him not to undertake it lightly, to study the character of the woman he would partner with and to consult him. A lack of union between husband and wife, Granville warned—with involuntary shudder—led to terrible misfortunes and social punishment. He promised to write on the subject from Florence. Then, with a quietness that suggested this might be their last parting, he asked his son to kiss him and announced his plan: tomorrow he would beg dismissal from the King and go to Italy. The conversation closed on his wish to preserve his dignity as a father and as a former presiding officer of a supreme court of justice; he would not wish to blush before his son.

Thus the scene leaves two images of Granville: in the street a man hardened into cruelty and misanthropy, giving a thousand francs to be squandered so that social machinery may be set to work on trivial moral wreckage; in his bedroom a father handing money and last counsels to his son, preparing to depart and to write advice about marriage from Florence. Between those moments stands Bianchon’s account of Caroline Crochard—a portrait of sacrifice and degradation—and the Comte’s icy rejection of any duty toward her, even as the name awakens in him a torment that proves he is not wholly dead to feeling. The narrative ends with Granville resolved to quit Paris and to cut off a friend who had awakened his pity, while secretly acknowledging familial ties he will attempt to leave behind with money and admonition.
