**Summary of "Vendetta" by Honoré de Balzac**

**Dedication**
The novel is dedicated to Puttinati, a Milanese sculptor.

**Prologue**
In October 1800, Bartolomeo di Piombo, a foreigner, stands outside the Tuileries Palace in Paris with his wife and daughter. They are in despair, having fled Corsica after Bartolomeo killed the Portas, a family that had wronged him. He seeks an audience with Napoleon Bonaparte, determined to confront his past.

**Chapter I: The Encounter**
Bartolomeo is initially denied entry to see Bonaparte but eventually meets Lucien Bonaparte, who takes him to his brother. Bartolomeo reveals his tragic story of vengeance against the Portas, who had killed his son and destroyed his home. Napoleon, intrigued yet cautious, offers Bartolomeo asylum in Paris but warns him against pursuing vendetta in the city, where the law prevails.

**Chapter II: The Studio**
Servin, a respected artist, opens a studio for young women to learn painting. His classes attract daughters from wealthy families, and he maintains a reputation for propriety. Among the students is Ginevra Piombo, Bartolomeo's daughter, who is talented and admired. However, political tensions arise as royalist students ostracize her due to her Bonapartist sympathies.

**Chapter III: Ginevra's Struggles**
Ginevra faces hostility from her peers, particularly Amelie Thirion, who leads the aristocratic clique. Despite her talent, Ginevra is marginalized, and her family's political affiliations complicate her social standing. She remains steadfast, focusing on her art and maintaining her dignity.

**Chapter IV: Love Blossoms**
Ginevra meets Luigi Porta, a young officer and fellow Corsican, who has been hiding from the authorities. Their love grows amidst the backdrop of political turmoil. Ginevra's parents disapprove of their relationship due to the vendetta against the Portas, but she is determined to marry Luigi.

**Chapter V: Marriage and Hardship**
Ginevra and Luigi marry in a modest ceremony, facing societal rejection and financial struggles. They work hard to make ends meet, but poverty begins to take a toll on their health and happiness. Ginevra's artistic career stalls, and Luigi's efforts to find work are met with challenges.

**Chapter VI: Tragedy Strikes**
As their situation worsens, Ginevra gives birth to a son, Bartolomeo. However, the joy of motherhood is overshadowed by their dire circumstances. Ginevra and Luigi struggle to provide for their child, leading to desperation. Ginevra's health declines, and ultimately, she dies from the effects of starvation and hardship, leaving Luigi heartbroken.

**Conclusion**
The story concludes with Luigi confronting the tragic consequences of their love and the vendetta that haunted their families. Bartolomeo di Piombo, now faced with the loss of his daughter, reflects on the cycle of vengeance and sorrow that has defined their lives. The narrative serves as a poignant exploration of love, sacrifice, and the destructive nature of familial feuds. 

**Addendum**
The novel connects to other works in Balzac's "Human Comedy," featuring characters and themes that resonate throughout his literary universe.
