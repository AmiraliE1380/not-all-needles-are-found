**La Grenadière** by Honoré de Balzac, translated by Ellen Marriage, depicts a charming house on the Loire River near Tours, surrounded by cliffs, white stone houses, gardens, and vineyards, all benefiting from a favorable climate for fruit cultivation.

Situated near the village church of Saint-Cyr, La Grenadière features terraced gardens, trellises, and a rose-filled garden with a wooden summer-house. The rustic house has large windows, a slate roof, and yellow-washed walls with green shutters. Inside, a crooked staircase leads to a dining room and a sitting room with saffron-colored wallpaper. The first floor has two simple bedrooms, and a modern kitchen connects to the main house, indicating it was once a retreat for townsfolk.

The estate includes a small vineyard and a vinedresser’s cottage, surrounded by stone walls and fruit trees. The terrace offers stunning views of the Loire, embodying a humble yet majestic dwelling rich in natural beauty.

In the spring of the Restoration, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, choosing La Grenadière for its distance from town. Her modest furnishings left her social status ambiguous. Tall and slender, with delicate features, she captivated locals during her evening walks along the Loire.

Mme. Willemsens ventured into Tours only twice, sparking curiosity about her background. Referred to as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, her life remained a mystery, marked by grace and a deep bond with her children, Louis-Gaston and Marie-Gaston. She dedicated herself to creating a nurturing environment filled with lessons and play, instilling values of moderation and resilience.

As summer progressed, her health declined, casting a somber atmosphere over La Grenadière. Despite her illness, she cherished time with her children, often walking in the gardens. Unbeknownst to her, Louis worked diligently at night, preparing for the entrance examination to the Ecole polytechnique.

Eventually, confined to her room, she expressed fears about their uncertain future. She revealed her impending death to Louis, urging him to care for Marie and pursue a career at sea. Finding solace in his determination, she felt peace before passing away surrounded by her children. After her funeral, Louis and Marie prepared for their new lives, with Louis taking on the responsibility of caring for Marie, resolved to honor his mother’s memory.
