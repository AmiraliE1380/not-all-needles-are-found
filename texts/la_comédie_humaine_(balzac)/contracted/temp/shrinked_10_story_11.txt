“Ah! madame,” said the doctor, “I have some appalling stories, but each has its proper hour in conversation—”

“But it is two in the morning, and the story of Rosina has prepared us,” the hostess replied.

“Tell us, Monsieur Bianchon!” they cried.

The doctor bowed and began.

About a hundred paces from Vendôme, on the bank of the Loir, stands an old brown house with very high roofs, solitary and utterly neglected—no tannery or tavern near it. A garden slopes to the river where once-clipped box shrubs now run wild; willows in the stream form a half-hidden fence. Weeds have taken the bank; fruit trees, neglected for ten years, bear no fruit, their suckers forming a thicket; espaliers look like a little copse. The paths are overgrown. From the hilltop where the ruins of the Dukes of Vendôme cling, one can see that this spot must once have been the pride of a country gentleman devoted to horticulture. An arbor’s wreck and a partially standing table testify to that bygone care. To complete the mournful air, one wall bears a sundial with the motto “Ultimam cogita” (think on your last).

The house itself is dreadfully dilapidated: the roof leaking, shutters always closed, swallows nesting on balconies, doors shut. Grasses grow in the flagstones; ironwork is rusty; paint has peeled and boards warped. Silence is broken only by birds, cats, polecats, rats, and mice; to the observer an invisible hand has inscribed one word over it all: Mystery.

From the street you see a large gate with a round-arched top, full of children’s holes; the gate had been blocked for ten years. Through breaches you view the courtyard: paving-stones choked with weeds, cracked walls, blackened coping laced with pellitory, disjointed stone steps, a rotten bell-cord, broken gutter-spouts. What fire from heaven, what decree, has struck this dwelling? The empty, deserted house is a vast enigma.

It was formerly a small fiefed estate called La Grande Bretèche. During the doctor’s stay in Vendôme—where he had been left to attend a rich patient—this odd dwelling became one of his keenest pleasures. Better than a ruin, it carried a secret, an unrevealed thought: the slow destruction of a standing house seemed like an avenging hand. Many evenings he climbed the wild hedge surrounding the enclosure, braved scratches to enter the ownerless garden, and lingered for hours in its disorder. He wove romances and indulged in melancholia there; had he known the reason for the neglect, the charm would have been gone. To him the place represented various phases of human misfortune: sometimes a graveyard without the dead, sometimes a lepers’ home, sometimes the house of the Atridae—but above all provincial life, contemplative and like an hour-glass. He often wept there; he never laughed.

Occasionally he felt involuntary terrors at the dull hum of a passing wood-pigeon. The damp earth required watching for lizards, vipers, and frogs; a cold would seem to settle on your shoulders like the Commendatore’s hand on Don Giovanni’s neck. One evening a weathercock creaked like a cry from the house as the doctor finished a gloomy drama to account for this enormous air of woe. He returned to his inn in a pensive mood. After supper the hostess came into his room with a mysterious air to announce, “Monsieur, here is Monsieur Regnault.”

“Who is Monsieur Regnault?” the doctor asked.

“What, sir, do you not know Monsieur Regnault?—Well, that’s odd,” she said and left.

A tall, slim man in black appeared: hat in hand, receding forehead, small pointed head, colorless face like dirty water—an usherly look. His coat was old, worn at the seams, but he wore a diamond in his shirt frill and gold rings in his ears.

“Monsieur,” the doctor asked, “whom have I the honor of addressing?”

The man sat by the fire, rubbed his hands and answered, “Dear me, it is very cold. I am Monsieur Regnault.”

“My curiosity is piqued,” the doctor said.

“I am notary at Vendôme,” Regnault replied. He quickly informed the doctor that the latter’s habit of walking in La Grande Bretèche’s garden constituted a misdemeanor since the estate was enclosed property, and as executor under the will of the late Comtesse de Merret he begged the doctor to desist. He insisted he meant no offense and would be glad to allow free access if he were not bound by the testatrix’s instructions: he had never set foot in the house since the will was read. They had done nothing there but verify doors and windows to assess taxes; administering Madame de Merret’s estate had been the most important event of his life. His account was long and voluble, but the doctor pressed him for the reason that La Grande Bretèche was left to ruin.

Regnault delighted in relating his own history and the tale of Madame de Merret. He had been head-clerk in a Paris office that went bankrupt; unable to buy a practice in Paris in 1816, he came to Vendôme, bought his predecessor’s business, and married into local relations. Three months after being licensed, just before his marriage, he was sent for to the Chateau of Merret by Madame la Comtesse. The Count had gone to Paris and died two months previously after falling into dissipation. On the day he left, Madame la Comtesse had quitted La Grande Bretèche, having dismantled it; some said she had burned the furniture and hangings. Regnault had been summoned: the Countess was very ill and seemed unlikely to survive the night, having received the Sacrament. The maid who met him at the door was now a servant in the inn.

When Regnault reached the chateau about eleven, he climbed the great staircase, crossed large, lofty, dark rooms—cold and damp—and reached the state bedroom where the Countess lay. Rumors had painted her as a coquette; reality confounded that image. The enormous, old-fashioned room, papered with brown and filled with dust, had only an old Argand lamp for light. The bed was antique, the tester hung with flowered chintz; on a small table lay an Imitation of Christ and the lamp; a deep armchair for the maid and two small chairs formed the whole furnishing; there was no fire. If you had seen that vast room you would have felt transported into a romance; it was icy, funereal.

Madame de Merret lay propped up, waxen and narrow-faced. Her hair, abundant but white as linen, was shown by a lace cap; her large black eyes, dimmed by fever, hardly moved beneath bony brows. Her forehead was clammy; her fleshless hands looked like bones covered with soft skin; veins and muscles showed—she must have once been striking, but disease had consumed her to a phantom. Her lips, pale-violet, scarcely moved when she spoke. Though Regnault’s work had exposed him to many deaths and last wishes, this solitary figure in her vast chateau was the most affecting: no sound, almost no breath, yet an immense presence. When her eyes moved, she tried to raise a hand, then, with difficulty, drew from under her pillow a sealed paper. “I place my will in your hands—Oh! God! Oh!” she uttered, then clutched a crucifix, kissed it, and died. Regnault remembered a joy stamped on her last glance, an expression that made him shudder, and he took away the will: she had appointed him her executor.

The will proved extraordinary. She left most of her property to the Vendôme hospital, with a few legacies. As for La Grande Bretèche, she ordered that, for fifty years from her death, it should remain precisely in the state it was at her death: nobody, whoever he might be, should enter the apartments, no repairs were to be made, and watchmen were even to be paid if necessary to secure this injunction. At the expiration of the term, if the will had been dutifully executed, the house would become the executor’s heirs’ property; otherwise it would revert to the heirs-at-law under terms set in a codicil not to be opened for fifty years. The will had not been contested. Regnault’s account thrilled him and he enjoyed the legal triumph.

The doctor, vivid imaginings spinning into a gothic romance, pressed Regnault for the specifics of the Countess’s eccentric injunctions, but the notary affected comical reticence. Eventually he regaled the doctor further with town gossip—diffuse, contradictory opinions that nearly put the doctor to sleep. Regnault’s monotone and self-satisfaction were too much for curiosity, and the notary took his leave, teasingly suggesting that many would wish to live fifty years longer, and hinting cryptically about age.

After Regnault left, the doctor, still stirred, sat in his armchair when his landlady, Madame Lepas, entered. A buxom, florid Flemish woman with the keen scrutiny of an innkeeper, she had overheard the notary’s history. The doctor repeated the story, and Madame Lepas leaned close, an instinctive mix of constable, spy, and dealer in her look.

When asked about Monsieur de Merret, she described him as extremely tall, a good gentleman from Picardy, taciturn and hot-tempered but generous in paying his debts, and well-liked by the common folk despite his haughty manner. He and Madame de Merret had once been a handsome couple; she was pretty, sweet, and wealthy—about twenty thousand francs a year. They were a splendid match and their wedding had filled the town. Were they happy? “So-so,” Madame Lepas said—the common folk were not intimate with their life. Madame de Merret was kind and pleasant but must sometimes have endured her husband’s temper.

Yet the hostess then confided what she knew. During the Emperor’s time, when Spaniards and prisoners of war were sent here, she lodged a young Spaniard sent to Vendôme on parole. Despite the parole, he had to present himself daily to the sub-prefect. He was a Spanish grandee with a fanciful name—Bagos de Férédia or something like it. For a Spaniard he was handsome though short—no more than five feet two or three—but well made, with small, fastidious hands; he kept his hands, she said, as carefully as a woman keeps brushes. He had thick black hair, a flame in his eye, a somewhat coppery complexion, and the finest linen she had ever seen—finer than anything even princesses had worn at her inn. He ate little but had such polite, amiable ways that he was impossible to dislike; he spoke scarcely at all.

He was devout: he read his breviary like a priest and went to mass regularly. Most notably, he always took his station within two yards of Madame de Merret’s chapel. At his first attendance there no one suspected intent; he never raised his eyes above his book. In the evenings he walked the hill among the ruins of the old castle—a pastime that must have reminded him of Spain, which, they said, is all hills.

Thus the scene closes on La Grande Bretèche, the Countess’s strange last will that sealed the house for half a century, and the shadow of a Spanish exile whose daily position near the Countess’s chapel and solitary walks among the ruins suggest a link between foreign passion and domestic catastrophe—an implication the doctor, Regnault, and Madame Lepas allow the listener to supply, while the dilapidated house stands mute and enigmatic by the Loir.

Shortly after he arrived, he stayed out late one evening and did not return until midnight. We were used to his whims; he had the key, so we didn’t wait. He lived in a house we owned on Rue des Casernes. A stable-boy claimed to have seen the Spanish Grandee swimming in the river. When he returned I warned him about the weeds; he seemed annoyed at being seen.

One morning he had not come back at all. Searching his things, I found a note in his drawer, fifty doubloons—about five thousand francs—and a sealed box of diamonds worth ten thousand francs. The note said that if he did not return, he left the money and diamonds to pay for masses in gratitude for his escape.

My husband went searching and returned with the Spaniard’s clothes, found under a stone on a breakwater near la Grande Bretèche. He had come early, unseen. After reading the letter my husband burned the clothes and, following Count Férédia’s wishes, we announced that he had escaped. The sub-prefect sent the constabulary after him, but he was never caught. Lepas thought the Spaniard had drowned. I suspected otherwise, believing him implicated in Madame de Merret’s affair; Rosalie had told me the crucifix her mistress cherished and was buried with was ebony and silver, and early in his stay Monsieur Férédia had owned a similar crucifix that I never saw again.

“My hostess asked, with a curious mixture of greed and conscience, whether she ought to feel remorse for the Spaniard’s fifteen thousand francs. Aren’t they truly mine?” I said, “Certainly. But have you ever questioned Rosalie?” She had tried; Rosalie was like a wall. The thought of la Grande Bretèche—its overgrown grass, shuttered windows, and deserted rooms—filled me with vague sinister curiosity and religious dread. I resolved not to leave Vendôme without learning the house’s secret. Rosalie, who seemed central to the affair, became my preoccupation.

She presented an odd mixture of bloom and reserve—obedient, simple, clumsy at times, yet with an inner sorrow or hope. I decided to pursue her to learn the truth. Calling her one evening, I teased about suitors and asked why she had become an inn-servant after leaving Madame de Merret; though the mistress had left her an annuity, Rosalie preferred her present post. Her evasiveness convinced me she knew more. After a fortnight I pressed her trust and she agreed to tell me the story on condition of secrecy.

Rosalie’s account, between the notary’s gossip and Madame Lepas’s version, I will summarize. Madame de Merret’s bedroom at la Grande Bretèche was on the ground floor; a small, four-foot-deep wall cupboard served as a wardrobe. Three months before the evening in question Madame de Merret had been seriously ill; her husband had begun sleeping on the first floor. One night he returned late from the club. He had used Rosalie to tell him if his wife was in bed; that evening he decided to check, perhaps to share his troubles or seek comfort. He entered thinking he heard a closet door shut; Madame de Merret stood by the fireplace, anxious.

“You’re very late,” she said huskily. Monsieur de Merret remained silent as Rosalie entered, which startled him. He began pacing. “Have you received bad news, or are you unwell?” his wife asked while Rosalie helped her undress. He did not answer.

“You can go, Rosalie,” Madame de Merret said; once she was gone—or lingering in the passage—Monsieur confronted his wife: “Madame, there’s someone in your cupboard!” She calmly replied, “No, monsieur.”

Her “No” pierced him. She appeared more saintly than ever. He moved to open the closet, but Madame de Merret took his hand and, with strange emotion, said: “Remember, if you find no one there, everything must end between us.” Her dignity disarmed him. “No, Josephine,” he said; “I won’t open it. I know your soul’s purity.” Then, producing an ebony-and-silver crucifix she owned, he made her swear on it that there was nobody in the closet. She swore without flinching.

Observing the crucifix, he remarked it was fine work; she said she had bought it from Duvivier, who had bought it from a Spanish monk when a troop of Spanish prisoners passed through Vendôme. Then he rang for Rosalie and, in the presence of his coachman Jean and others, gave directions: he wanted everyone to sleep and for Jean to tell him when they were asleep. He kept his gaze on his wife and gave no sign of suspicion.

A recent renovation had left Monsieur de Merret with a surplus of plaster, which gave him an idea. Gorenflot, a mason, was sent to wall up the cupboard door with bricks and plaster, ostensibly using the leftover materials. But the mason was to sleep there that night, and Monsieur de Merret secretly arranged to bribe him: a passport to leave the country, six thousand francs for the journey and another six thousand upon fulfilling conditions—going abroad, staying ten years, remaining silent. For Rosalie, he promised ten thousand francs, payable on her wedding day if she married Gorenflot and kept silent; otherwise no wedding gift. Rosalie, too, was bribed with smaller sums: a thousand francs a year if she persuaded Gorenflot to leave a crack at the bottom of the wall.

As Gorenflot worked, he broke one of the two panes in the top of the cupboard door to peer in. He and Rosalie saw the face of a dark, gloomy man with black hair and fiery eyes. The poor woman gave the man a nod—an emblem of “Hope.” By four in the morning the wall was completed; Gorenflot was to be kept near and Monsieur slept in his wife’s room.

Next morning Monsieur de Merret announced he must go to the Maire for the passport, took the crucifix, and left. Madame de Merret, joyously hopeful, immediately rang for the pick and cried, “We must work. I saw how Gorenflot did it yesterday; we have time to make a gap and build it up again.” Rosalie fetched a cleaver and began demolishing the wall with frantic energy. She had removed a few bricks when, turning to strike harder, she saw Monsieur de Merret standing behind her. She fainted.

“Lay madame on her bed,” he said coldly. He had set a trap in his absence—he had only written for the passport and had summoned Duvivier; the jeweler arrived as they tried to repair the disorder. Monsieur de Merret questioned Duvivier about buying crucifixes from Spanish passersby; Duvivier denied it. The husband then confined his wife to her room for twenty days, never leaving, and when the cupboard made noises and Josephine would plead for the dying man hidden there, he silenced her, reminding her of the oath she had sworn on the Cross.

When Bianchon finished this account, all the ladies at the table rose, shaken by the last words.
