**La Grande Bretèche**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage and Clara Bell*

---

The story begins with Doctor Monsieur Bianchon recounting a chilling tale at a late-night gathering. He describes La Grande Bretèche, an isolated, dilapidated house near Vendôme, surrounded by a wild garden that hints at a once-thriving estate. The house, now in ruins, evokes mystery and melancholy, suggesting a tragic history.

Bianchon reflects on the house's past, imagining it as a haven for a country gentleman who loved horticulture. The overgrown garden symbolizes lost joy and forgotten lives. The house's decay seems to carry a curse, prompting questions about its abandonment.

During his stay in Vendôme, Bianchon becomes fascinated with the house and often sneaks into the garden, allowing his imagination to run wild with stories of its former glory. One evening, after a haunting visit, he meets Monsieur Regnault, a local notary, who informs him that the house belongs to the estate of the late Comtesse de Merret. Regnault explains that the Comtesse's will forbids anyone from entering the house or making repairs for fifty years after her death, adding to the house's enigma.

Regnault shares the Comtesse's tragic story. After her husband, the Count de Merret, died, she became increasingly reclusive and eccentric, leaving La Grande Bretèche in disarray. Rumors circulated about her mental state. On her deathbed, she summoned Regnault to witness her will, revealing her intention to leave her estate to the hospital in Vendôme, with strict instructions regarding La Grande Bretèche.

The will's peculiar conditions intrigue Bianchon. Regnault, who had been a clerk before becoming a notary, recounts how the Comtesse's decline was marked by isolation and illness. Her last moments were filled with urgency to secure her wishes, culminating in her death shortly after handing over her will.

As Bianchon contemplates the Comtesse's life and the fate of La Grande Bretèche, he is drawn into local gossip surrounding the couple. His landlady, Madame Lepas, reveals more about the Comtesse and her husband, painting a picture of a once-happy couple whose relationship soured over time. The Count was known for his temper, while the Comtesse was admired for her beauty and wealth.

Madame Lepas also shares a secret involving a young Spanish prisoner who stayed at her inn during the war. This handsome, well-mannered young man became a mysterious figure in town, often seen near the Comtesse's chapel, hinting at possible connections to her tragic fate.

The narrative weaves together themes of love, loss, and the passage of time, as Bianchon grapples with the haunting legacy of La Grande Bretèche. The house stands as a testament to the complexities of human relationships and the secrets that linger long after the people have gone. Ultimately, the tale leaves readers pondering the nature of memory and the shadows of the past that continue to shape the present.

In the narrative, a woman recounts the mysterious disappearance of Count Férédia, a Spanish Grandee living in her house. After he fails to return one night, she finds a letter and a significant sum of money and diamonds he left behind, intended for masses of gratitude for his escape. Her husband searches for him and discovers the Count's clothes near the river, leading them to believe he escaped rather than drowned.

The story darkens as the woman suspects the Count's involvement in the death of Madame de Merret, whose crucifix resembled one owned by the Count. She grapples with the moral dilemma of keeping the Count's money, which she feels is rightfully hers.

Intrigued by Rosalie, the maid who seems to know secrets about Madame de Merret's death, the narrator pursues her for the truth. Rosalie eventually reveals that Madame de Merret had been ill, and her husband, Monsieur de Merret, was unaware of her condition until he returned home late one night.

On that fateful evening, suspecting something was wrong, Monsieur de Merret decided to check on his wife instead of trusting Rosalie. He confronted Madame de Merret, who denied anyone being in the cupboard despite his suspicions. In a tense moment, she swore on a crucifix that no one was there, leading to a tragic outcome.

Believing in his wife's purity, Monsieur de Merret chose not to open the cupboard but devised a plan to wall it up, enlisting mason Gorenflot. As the wall was built, Madame de Merret attempted to signal to the man inside, indicating hope for his escape.

After the wall was completed, Monsieur de Merret kept a close watch on his distraught wife. When he left to obtain a passport for Gorenflot, Madame de Merret tried to break down the wall to free the man inside but was caught by her husband, causing her to faint.

Monsieur de Merret then confined his wife to her room, refusing to let her leave or intercede for the man she sought to save. He remained with her for twenty days, reminding her of her oath, leaving her in despair.

The story concludes with the women at the table feeling the weight of the tale, reflecting on the moral complexities and emotional turmoil that unfolded within the walls of La Grande Bretèche.
