DEDICATION

To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author
of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron,—Your warm interest in my long, vast “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century,” and the encouragement you have given me to persevere, have made me feel entitled to associate your name with a portion of it. Are you not one of the most conscientious, studious representatives of Germany? Will not your approval secure for me the approval of others, and shield this attempt of mine? I am proud to have gained your good opinion, and have striven to deserve it by continuing my labors with the unflagging courage characteristic of your method of study and of that exhaustive documentary research by which you gave your monumental work to letters. Your sympathy with such labor as you yourself have bestowed upon the most brilliant civilization of the East has often sustained my ardor through nights of toil on the details of our modern civilization. You, whose naive kindliness can be compared only with our own La Fontaine’s, will be glad to know of this.

May this token of my respect for you and your work find you at Dobling, dear Baron, and remind you and yours of one of your most sincere admirers and friends.

DE BALZAC.

There stands a house at a corner of a street, in the middle of a town in one of the less important prefectures in France. The names of the street and town must be suppressed by convention; if a writer attempts to chronicle his own age he must touch many sore subjects, and discretion is due. The house was called the Hotel d’Esgrignon; let d’Esgrignon be a conventional name, as much a fiction as Belval or Floricour. Though the principal names will be disguised, truth will peep through contradictions and improbabilities, like shoots from a root you have thought uprooted.

The “Hotel d’Esgrignon” was simply the house of the old Marquis—Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol, Marquis d’Esgrignon. It was an ordinary house, but townspeople first joked about the name and, after twenty years, used it seriously.

The name Carol (or Karawl, as some would spell it) was glorious among Northmen chieftains who conquered Gaul and established feudalism. Their descent had been consistent: Carol never bowed before King, Commune, Church, or Finance. Charged once with guarding a French March, the marquisate in their family was a real office, not an empty title. Their fief had always been their domain. They were provincial nobles in every sense, with an unbroken line; neglected by court for two centuries, they were lords paramount locally, regarded with something like awe. The house of d’Esgrignon, buried in its remote border country, was preserved as one preserves charred piles of an ancient bridge—intact by reverence. For thirteen centuries daughters of the house married without dowries or took the veil; younger sons contented themselves with dower shares and went into the Church or the army; a cadet once became an admiral, duke, and peer of France, and died without issue. The Marquis of the elder branch would never accept a ducal title.

“I hold my marquisate as His Majesty holds the realm of France, and on the same conditions,” he told the Constable de Luynes—who seemed paltry to him then.

The d’Esgrignons lost heads on the scaffold during the troubles. The old blood showed proud even in 1789. The Marquis of that day refused to emigrate; he was answerable for his March. The countryside’s reverence saved his head, though sans-culottes’ hatred compelled him to pretend to flee, and for a while he lived in hiding. In the name of the Sovereign People, the d’Esgrignon lands were dishonored by the District, forests sold by the Nation despite the Marquis’s protest; he was forty then. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, his half-sister, saved some of the fief thanks to Chesnel, the young steward, who claimed for her the partage de présuccession—the right of a relative to a portion of an émigré’s lands. Thus the Republic made over the castle itself and some farms to Mlle. d’Esgrignon. Chesnel, faithful steward, bought in his own name the church, parsonage, castle gardens, and places dear to his patron—the Marquis advancing the money. Chesnel later became a notary.

The Terror’s slow, swift years passed, and the Marquis, esteemed in the countryside, decided he and his sister should return to the castle to improve the property Chesnel had saved from wreck. But the plundered, dismantled castle was too vast for a lord shorn of rights; too large for an estate whose woods had been sold piecemeal, yielding scarcely nine thousand francs’ income from residual estates.

In October 1800 Chesnel brought the Marquis back to the old feudal castle. The sight moved Chesnel deeply: his patron stood in an empty courtyard gazing at a filled moat and towers razed to roof level. The descendant of the Franks searched for missing Gothic turrets and weather vanes, his eyes turned to the sky as if to ask heaven why social upheaval had occurred. Only Chesnel could understand the deep anguish of the great d’Esgrignon, now called Citizen Carol. The Marquis stood long in silence, drinking in the place—his forefathers’ ancient home, the air about it—then flung out a melancholy exclamation.

“Chesnel,” he said, “we will come back again some day when the troubles are over; I could not bring myself to live here until the edict of pacification has been published; _they_ will not allow me to set my escutcheon on the wall.”

He waved to the castle, mounted his horse, and rode back beside his sister, who had driven in the notary’s shabby basket-chaise.

The Hotel d’Esgrignon in town had been demolished; factories now stood where the aristocrat’s house had been. So Chesnel spent the Marquis’s last louis on the old-fashioned building in the square—with its gables, weather-vane, turret, and dovecote. It had been the old courthouse and presidial; it had belonged to the d’Esgrignons for generations; and for five hundred louis it was made over in the title given by the Nation to its rightful lord. Half in jest, half seriously, the old house was christened the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

In 1800 little difficulty was made over removing names from the fatal list, and a few émigrés returned. Among the first were the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, ruined. M. d’Esgrignon generously offered them shelter; two months later the Baron died of grief in his house. The Nouastres were of the best provincial blood; Mlle. de Nouastre was twenty-two; the Marquis married her to continue his line. She died in childbirth, through an unskilful physician, leaving a son to bear the d’Esgrignon name. The old Marquis—only fifty-three but aged by adversity—saw the loveliest of women die, a noble figure in whom the charm of sixteenth-century femininity seemed to live. With her death joy left his old age. He stood beside her bed, kissed her forehead, drew out his watch, broke the mainspring, and hung it by the hearth. It was eleven in the morning.

“Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” he said, “let us pray God that this hour may not prove fatal yet again to our house. My uncle the archbishop was murdered at this hour; at this hour also my father died—”

He knelt and buried his face in the coverlet; his sister did likewise. Mlle. d’Esgrignon wept; the old Marquis’s eyes were dry. To stubborn Frankness he united Christian fortitude.

These events occurred in the second year of the nineteenth century. Mlle. d’Esgrignon was then twenty-seven, a beautiful woman. An ex-contractor for army forage with an income of six thousand francs, a district man, persuaded Chesnel to carry a marriage proposal to the lady. The Marquis and his sister were indignant at such presumption; Chesnel was almost heartbroken—he could not forgive himself for yielding. The Marquis’s manner toward his old servant changed; never again was their affection the same. From then on the Marquis’s gratitude wounded Chesnel; to some exalted natures gratitude seems an excessive payment—what they prefer is the equality that springs from mutual choice. Chesnel had known high friendship; the Marquis had raised him to his level. The Marquis saw his old servitor as more than a servant, less than a child—a voluntary liege man bound by affection. There were no balanced obligations; sincere affection made them unnecessary.

To the Marquis, Chesnel’s official dignity was nothing—he remained the notary in disguise. Chesnel, who felt part of the family, was furious with the presumed suitor du Croisier. He set others against Sieur du Croisier. Du Croisier, on the other hand, bore a grudge and nursed vengeance. He hated Chesnel and the d’Esgrignon family with the smothered, all-absorbing hate common in a country town. The rebuff ruined his standing among malicious provincials; in despair he courted a wealthy old maid and was refused again. He lost his hope of marrying Mlle. d’Esgrignon—access to the Faubourg Saint-Germain of the province— and his credit fell. He then married the old maid who had refused him and thereby worsened feuds.

In 1805 M. de la Roche-Guyon, an old family allied to the d’Esgrignons, proposed formally through Chesnel for Mlle. Marie Armande Clair d’Esgrignon. She declined, saying she was a mother—she had just put her nephew, little Victurnien, to bed.

The old Marquis rose, went up to his sister, returned from the cradle, kissed her hand reverently, and sat down again to say, “My sister, you are a d’Esgrignon.”

A quiver ran through the noble girl; tears stood in her eyes. M. d’Esgrignon, the father of the present Marquis, had married secondly the daughter of a tax farmer ennobled by Louis XIV—a shocking mesalliance to the family, though of no importance since a daughter was the sole child. Armande knew this; the brother, kind as he had been, still regarded her as a stranger. His words recognized her as one of the family. Her answer crowned eleven years of noble devotion: “I shall die Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” she said simply to Chesnel.

“For you there could be no fairer title,” returned Chesnel, intending a compliment. Poor Mlle. d’Esgrignon reddened.

“You have blundered, Chesnel,” the Marquis said, flattered but vexed that his sister was hurt. He discoursed on the family heraldry—“A d’Esgrignon may marry a Montmorency; their descent is not so pure as ours. The d’Esgrignons bear or, two bends, gules”—and spoke of their device, Cil est nostre, taken at a tournament in Philip Augustus’s reign, with supporters: a knight or on the right and a lion gules on the left.

“I do not remember that any woman I have ever met struck my imagination as Mlle. d’Esgrignon did,” Emile Blondet later wrote—who provides much of this history. He confesses his boyhood admiration perhaps colored by youth. If playing on the Parade and she walked by with her nephew Victurnien, seeing her thrilled him like galvanism. As a child he felt revived at sight of her. She had tawny-gold hair; a delicate down on her cheek with a silver gleam he loved to catch. Her dreamy emerald eyes flashed fire when they met his. He pretended to roll on the grass in games to admire her little feet up close; her soft whiteness, delicate features, clear forehead, and slender figure fascinated him without his fully understanding why. When she asked, in a musical voice, “What are you doing little one? Why do you look at me?” he would wriggle, bite his nails, red, and answer, “I do not know.” If she stroked his hair and asked his age, he would run and call, “Eleven!”

Every princess and fairy of his readings looked and walked like Mlle. d’Esgrignon; later when copying heads from the antique, he noticed their hair braided like hers. When fancies vanished, Mlle. Armande remained a type—he would see her as again Agnes Sorel, Marie Touchet, Gabrielle. He lent her all the love she never expressed. The angelic glimpses of her haunt him even as an adult.

Keep this portrait: it is faithful and instructive. Mlle. d’Esgrignon shows how pure goodness, without intelligence, may cause mischief.

Two-thirds of émigrés returned to France in 1804–1805; many exiles from the Marquis’s province returned. Some defected, entering Napoleon’s service or marrying upstart families; those who sided with the Empire regained fortunes and properties and mostly went to Paris. But eight or nine families remained true to the proscribed noblesse and to Bourbon monarchy: La Roche-Guyons, Nouastres, Verneuils, Casterans, Troisvilles, and others. Rich or poor, for them pedigree mattered above money. The Marquis d’Esgrignon was their acknowledged head. His house became their cenacle. There His Majesty, Emperor and King, was still “M. de Bonaparte”; “the King” meant Louis XVIII at Mittau; the Department was the Province; the prefecture the intendance.

The Marquis was honored for admirable behavior, noble loyalty, and undaunted courage; and respected in town for misfortunes, fortitude, and adherence to convictions. Such a man assumed the majestic dignity of ruined greatness. His chivalrous fair-mindedness was well known; litigants sometimes referred disputes to him. Even gentle Imperialists and authorities indulged his prejudices; but a rising section of new society—the Liberal party—laughed at an aristocratic oasis that one could not enter without proving irreproachable descent. Their animosity was all the keener because many town worthies believed the best society was to be found in the Marquis’s salon. The prefect’s wife, a Grandlieu, was sent as ambassadress.

Those excluded nicknamed the salon “The Collection of Antiquities,” and called the Marquis “M. Carol.” The receiver of taxes addressed his applications to “M. Carol (ci-devant des Grignons),” mockingly adopting an obsolete spelling.

“If I try to recall childhood,” Emile Blondet said, “the nickname always made me laugh despite my respect—my love—for Mlle. d’Esgrignon. The Hotel stood at the angle of two busy thoroughfares, not five hundred paces from the market. Two drawing-room windows looked on the street and two on the square; the room was like a glass cage: anyone passing could see through from side to side. Even at twelve I thought the salon a rare curiosity, on the border between reality and dream.”

The room, the ancient Hall of Audience, stood above cellars with grated air-holes—once prison cells of the old courthouse, now used as a kitchen. Blondet remembered the magnificent lofty hearth, covered in carved tracery like a melon. Above it an equestrian relief of Henri III., who reclaimed the ancient duchy for the crown, in a carved gilt frame. The ceiling between chestnut cross-beams bore scroll-work; remnants of gilding remained. The walls were hung with Flemish tapestry—six scenes from the Judgment of Solomon—framed with garlands, satyrs, and cupids. The parquet was laid by the present Marquis; Chesnel had acquired furniture at sale: Louis-Quatorze consoles, tables, clock-cases, andirons, sconces, and tapestry-covered chairs, which completed a stately room, large for the house. An ante-chamber, the old Salle des Pas Perdus, communicated with the magistrate’s deliberating chamber, used as dining-room.

Beneath old panelling, amid threadbare glories, eight or ten dowagers were arrayed in state in a quavering line—some palsied, others dark and shriveled, some erect, some bent—all tricked out in more or less fantastic outdated costumes with ribboned caps and faded lace. No painter or caricaturist ever caught the haunting fascination of those aged women; they come back in dreams. Whether misfortune taught me the secrets of irremediable disaster, or I grew to understand human feelings and the thoughts of Old Age and Regret, I know nowhere else have I seen the wan gray eyes or the dreadful brightness of others that I remember.

Neither Hoffmann nor Maturin ever gave Blondet such a thrill of terror as the automaton movements of those bodies sheathed in whalebone. Actors’ painted faces do not shock because the rouge is visible; but years had leveled those women’s faces and furrowed them into wrinkles, making them like carved German nutcrackers. Peering through the windows, Blondet noted battered bodies, ill-jointed limbs, lantern jaws, protuberant bones, abnormal hips; their movements as they came and went seemed as extraordinary as their sepulchral stillness at the card-tables.

The men looked gray and faded like the tapestries on the walls. Their dress conformed to the day, but their white hair, waxen faces, devastated foreheads, and pale eyes revealed kinship to the women, neutralizing any reality the costume might suggest.

The certainty of finding them at the same tables daily gave them in Blondet’s eyes a theatrical, unearthly interest. When he and schoolboys proposed to look at curiosities in the glass cage for fun, but seeing Mlle. Armande made him tremble. There was jealousy in his admiration for little Victurnien—he who belonged, they felt instinctively, to a different, higher order. It seemed strange that a fresh young creature should be in that awakened cemetery before its time. They could not explain it, but felt bourgeois and insignificant in presence of that proud court.

The disasters of 1813–1814, bringing Napoleon’s downfall, gave new life and hope to the Collection of Antiquities; but the events of 1815, the occupation, and government vacillation delayed fulfillment of their expectations. This story only begins to shape itself in 1822.

By 1822 the Marquis’s fortunes had not much improved despite Restoration changes for émigrés. Of all nobles hit by Revolutionary legislation, his case was hardest. Before 1789 the d’Esgrignons derived most income from manorial dues paid by their tenants; they had reduced holdings to swell quit-rents and heriots. Families in this position were hopelessly ruined. They were not helped by Louis XVIII’s ordinance returning unsold lands, and later indemnity laws could not restore suppressed rights; what revived was a land tax called domaines, with revenues going to the State.

The Marquis belonged to the small Royalist section that would tolerate no compromise with those they called, not Revolutionaries, but revolted subjects—or in parliamentary terms, Liberals or Constitutionnels. These Royalists, nicknamed Ultras by opposition, looked to Right-wing orators like M. de Polignac to protest the charter Louis XVIII had granted—regarded as an ill-advised, temporary concession, to be annulled when convenient. Thus the Marquis d’Esgrignon stood aloof, upholding the strictest Right until his vast fortune should be restored. He even scorned the indemnity advocated by the Villele ministry, intended to strengthen the Crown by ending lingering ownership distinctions.

The miracles of the Restoration in 1814, Napoleon’s return in 1815, their undoings, and reinstatements took the Marquis by surprise at sixty-seven. At that age many high-spirited men were worn out by long struggle; their activity in provincial retreats had hardened into immovable conviction, and they lived an enervating routine. Could anything be worse for a party than representation by old men when its ideas are already considered antiquated?

When Louis seemed firmly seated again in 1818, the Marquis asked what a man of seventy could do at court; content he was with the triumph of Monarchy and Religion while waiting for results of that indecisive victory. He continued as before, lord-paramount of his salon, the Collection of Antiquities.

But when victors of 1793 became vanquished, the nickname first given in jest was used in bitter earnest. The town, like other country towns, was full of party hatreds; du Croisier, unexpectedly, married the old maid who had refused him at first; this marriage produced a provincial war and hastened separation of great and small noblesse, of aristocratic and bourgeois elements which had briefly united under Napoleonic pressure. After the pressure lifted, class divisions revived with damage.

Vanity is the most national of French sentiments. Wounded vanity induced a thirst for Equality, although as the most ardent innovator will discover, Equality is impossible. Royalists pricked Liberals where they were most sensitive; this was sharpest in the provinces, where both sides accused the other of atrocities and employed the blackest deeds to secure public opinion. Individuals became identified with political opinions; opponents became private enemies. In a country town, theoretical disputes of Paris become personal combats—two duelists on watch for a chance to thrust steel. Each seeks to unbalance the other; political hatred becomes all-consuming. Epigram and slander are used to bring a party into discredit.

In such warfare, the nobles had the advantage of wit and delicate irony; du Croisier’s faction resorted to poisoned weapons. The Chevalier, who chose the aristocratic Mons Sacer and turned his back on mixed society, directed his jests at du Croisier’s salon, stoking fires he could not foresee. Only purists and loyal gentlemen and women entered the Hotel d’Esgrignon; they committed no indiscretions. If Liberals sought to ridicule the nobles, they fastened on political actions; meanwhile the intermediate class—officials and others who paid court to higher powers—kept nobles informed of Liberal doings, much of it laughable. Du Croisier’s adherents smarted under inferiority, which increased revenge thirst.

In 1822 du Croisier headed the province’s manufacturing interest while the Marquis headed the noblesse. Each represented a party. But du Croisier, rather than pose frankly as extreme Left, adopted positions later associated with the 221 deputies, thus keeping touch with magistrates, officials, and capitalists. His salon rivaled the d’Esgrignon one in power, larger, younger, and more energetic, felt across the countryside; the Collection of Antiquities remained inert, a passive appendage, often embarrassing central authority; Conservative pressure sometimes led the Government to fatal blunders.

The Liberals had never successfully carried a candidate. The department declined to obey them knowing du Croisier would, if elected, sit on the Left-Centre benches and lean Left. He corresponded with the Keller brothers, bankers linked to the nineteen deputies of the Left; M. Keller was related by marriage to Comte de Gondreville, a Constitutional peer still in favor. Thus the Constitutional Opposition was prepared to vote last-moment, not for its professed candidate but for du Croisier if necessary; yet du Croisier was routinely defeated by Royalists. The party leaders, taking tone from the Marquis, had thoroughly gauged him; each defeat made du Croisier more bitter. Nothing so stirs up strife as the failure of an elaborate snare.

By 1822 hostilities seemed to lull—both salons measured strengths and waited for opportunity. Ordinary folk were content with surface quiet that deceived the Government; those who knew du Croisier knew his implacable passion for revenge, especially in political ambition. Du Croisier, who used to turn white and red at the bare mention of d’Esgrignon or the Chevalier, chose an impassive countenance masking savage feeling. He smiled on enemies while hating them the more, watching them hourly. One partisan aiding him was M. du Ronceret, President of the Tribunal, a small squire who’d vainly tried to enter the Antiquities.

The d’Esgrignons’ small fortune, managed carefully by Chesnel, barely sufficed; though they lived without ostentation, they lived like nobles. The governor found an Oratorian tutor for the heir, the young Comte Victurnien d’Esgrignon, who lived with the family and drew a salary. The wages of a cook, a waiting-woman for Mlle. Armande, an old valet for the Marquis, and a few other servants, daily household expenses, and the cost of a thorough education—spared in no way—absorbed the family income despite Mlle. Armande’s economies and Chesnel’s careful management, and the servants’ affection. Chesnel had not yet begun repairs on the ruined castle; he waited for leases to fall in so he might raise farm rents—rents had been rising due to improved agriculture and currency depreciation, benefits the landlord would realize at lease expirations of 1809.

The Marquis knew nothing of his household’s management or finances. Nearing the end of his life, he and his circle clung to the illusion that the d’Esgrignon house, neglected at Court and by government, would revive in his handsome heir Victurnien—who would go to Court, enter royal service, and marry into some illustrious family. Their intimates—the Troisvilles, La Roche-Guyon, the Casterans, and the Duc de Verneuil—encouraged this belief. It might have seemed plausible only if forty years of history could be erased; the restored monarchy and public opinion now demanded not just noble birth but money, political relevance, and continuous prestige. The d’Esgrignons lacked the modern resources and uninterrupted prominence that mattered; old renown could not substitute for wealth and influence. The Troisvilles’ daughter’s marriage to General Montcornet, rather than flattering antiquated pride, nearly split social circles—evidence of how times had changed.

Only Chesnel, the family’s old notary and former steward, saw the situation clearly. Devoted but practical, he had witnessed the Revolution’s violence and the triumph of new social ideas and vested interests. He understood that confiscations and deaths under the Revolution were binding facts and that the new order would not be overturned; his attachment to the d’Esgrignons was therefore affectionate and lucid. He tried tactfully to warn the Marquis, who met these cautions with a prophet’s smile and the conviction that history would repeat in their favor. Chesnel feared the damage the family’s superstitions and self-delusions would do to Victurnien’s character and habits.

Idolized by his father and aunt, Victurnien was thoroughly spoiled yet justified their hopes in some ways. Mlle. Armande loved him as a mother might but lacked maternal intuition and severity; she could not foresee danger as a real mother could. Chesnel’s pragmatic vigilance resembled a mother’s instinct more closely than the aunt’s blind adoration, but the household generally paid little heed to his counsels—he had even fallen into a kind of disgrace after proposing an imprudent marriage for the family.

Victurnien was taught from infancy to assume superiority: peers among great nobles, the King his only superior, and the rest of mankind beneath him. With beauty, tact, a quick mind, and thorough education, he had every advantage. He was of average height, graceful, an excellent horseman and marksman, and intellectually promising—qualities that might have made him a great man. But indulged and unopposed in youth, he became insolent, audacious, and self-willed. The Chevalier, a charming old roue, misled the boy by glorifying the reckless manners and witty immorality of an earlier era; the Chevalier’s jocular maxims mingled with the family’s rigid emphasis on honor. Thus good and bad counsel shaped Victurnien.

At eighteen, Victurnien entered society and quickly misread appearances. Accustomed to deference at home and to unguarded talk in the presence of an elderly father, he did not see the hypocrisies and practical cautions of the larger world. His reckless behavior—shooting over neighbors’ lands, amorous imprudences requiring settlements and legal cover-ups—led to lawsuits and scandals quietly hushed by Chesnel, who paid to avert public shame. Encouraged by adroit enemies—particularly Du Croisier, who plotted revenge against the family—Victurnien’s faults were fanned. President du Ronceret and other officials, while outwardly lenient, fostered liberal clamor and public suspicion to discredit the nobles; du Croisier hoped to drive the d’Esgrignons into ruin through the young man’s vices. A young instigator, Fabien du Ronceret, befriended Victurnien to lead him on.

Between eighteen and twenty-one, Victurnien ran up considerable debts—nearly eighty thousand francs paid out by Chesnel, who hid this from Mlle. Armande and the Marquis. The family’s mode of life left little margin: the Marquis’s income barely covered the household. Victurnien’s tailor bills, tastes for English saddles and carriages, livery, and fashionable accessories swallowed his allowance. Chesnel, with private savings (about twelve thousand livres yearly), had been the young man’s secret benefactor, paying off lawsuits and bailing him out repeatedly. Chesnel hoped to see Victurnien settle into a sensible marriage, but the young man’s temperament—capable of lofty thought yet habitually surrendering to impulse and pleasure—made him dangerously likely to repeat mistakes.

Chesnel’s patience and warnings met with shrugging complacency from the Chevalier and indulgence from the family. The Chevalier dismissed debt as par for a gentleman’s course, recounted extravagant examples of aristocratic generosity, and minimized the danger. Chesnel, however, foresaw ruin. His fear and affection prompted a decisive plan: to have Victurnien sent to Paris and presented at court, hoping the change would sober the boy and provide opportunities befitting his rank.

One autumn evening in 1822, after cards and salon talk, the household confronted the truth about Victurnien’s debts. The Chevalier forced the disclosure to the Marquis: Chesnel had lent an enormous sum—“something like a hundred thousand livres”—to the Count. The Marquis was mortified that his devoted steward would be the family’s creditor; pride and the old notions of dignity made it painful to ask Chesnel for help in a straightforward way. Nevertheless, they resolved to send Victurnien to Paris to present himself to the King and secure an appointment for him; they accepted Chesnel’s loan rather than humiliate the family by other means.

Chesnel explained that the Count’s companions and enemies in town—the president, Du Croisier and his circle—were seeking vengeance by ensnaring Victurnien, provoking scandals to ruin the family. He had, accordingly, borrowed money himself, mortgaging Le Jard, and had made arrangements in confidence so the loan would not appear as a direct family shame. Mlle. Armande and the Marquis were moved; Chesnel kissed her hand and, with paternal tenderness, urged that Paris provide the boy with more amusement and diversion than provincial society could, while also placing him where discretion and better company might discipline him.

Preparations were hurried. The Marquis and Mlle. Armande instructed Victurnien that he was to go to court, serve His Majesty, and there ascertain a career—army, navy, diplomacy, or royal household. They promised a modest provision and sent Josephin, Victurnien’s trusted valet, with him as companion. Chesnel wrote to an old notary friend in Paris, Maître Sorbier, begging vigilant oversight of the young man’s conduct and intimating funds would be placed at Sorbier’s disposition to support him. Chesnel’s letter revealed his deep emotional tie to the family: he felt he had reared and protected the d’Esgrignons through revolution and decline, and that the young Count’s fate was intimately his concern.

Victurnien accepted a monthly allowance of two thousand francs and left amid the household’s blessings. He felt no genuine regret; provincial life had been gentle but narrow, and Paris promised to amplify his vanity and gratify his tastes. In the capital he discovered the city’s scale and the fact that noble names no longer guaranteed power or employment; the Court’s attention was limited, and new social forces and money mattered most. Undeterred, he took to luxury and fashion as necessary to command respect and assumed a posture of independence that concealed need.

In Paris, he sought to recover a position he imagined due to his birth. He bought rooms, stables, and equerries, all at great expense. His family’s reputation and the Faubourg Saint-Germain’s curiosity opened many doors: old names still acted as social passports. He found a circle of fashionable young men—de Marsay, Rastignac, de Ronquerolles, Maxime de Trailles, and others—who moved easily in the world he coveted and initiated him into its pleasures. The Vidame de Pamiers, a discreet source of gossip and introductions in the Faubourg, welcomed Victurnien and took him to salons and dinners where he met writers and courtiers, and where the Vidame encouraged him toward conquest and social display.

At a dinner with the Vidame, de Marsay, Rastignac, and the young writer Emile Blondet, Victurnien encountered the pleasures and perils of Parisian society. The Vidame introduced him to Mlle. des Touches’ salon and to fashionable circles where literature and wit mixed with vanity and intrigue. The Vidame, who kept a finger on public taste, predicted the young Count’s rapid rise—and fall—if he pursued extremes.

Victurnien was introduced to the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, a dazzling and complex beauty who immediately attracted him and whom others envied. The Duchesse presented herself as an ethereal, virginal figure—an affectation that masked cleverness and self-regard. She resolved to play an innocence that would grant her influence, a role she adopted to conceal and manipulate her passions. Her carefully constructed purity, full of artful glances and guarded gestures, made her all the more alluring; she used the appearance of sanctity to amplify the power of her charm.

Thus Victurnien found himself at the center of fashionable life. His family’s name, his beauty, and the patronage of the Faubourg made him desirable; his vanity made him determined to sustain an image of wealth and consequence. Chesnel’s precautions were circumvented by the mechanics of Parisian finance and by notaries eager for patronage. The young Count’s expenses mounted: lodgings, dress, horses, and the cost of entering high society quickly devoured the money set aside. Despite Chesnel’s hopes that Paris would rescue him from provincial follies, the city offered new temptations, alliances, and risks that promised either brilliant ascent or irreversible ruin.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs, and neither Chesnel nor Mlle. Armande suspected it. Victurnien had also taken two thousand crowns from Chesnel at intervals to conceal his sources, and sent deceiving letters to his poor father and aunt, who remained happily misled. Parisian life was quietly preparing a dreadful catastrophe for the great house; only du Croisier knew, and he was eager to secure not merely ruin but dishonor for the d’Esgrignons. He had been sure of it since discovering that Victurnien’s debts were growing dangerously heavy.

Du Croisier’s first move was to eliminate his most hated enemy, the good old notary Chesnel. The old man lived in the Rue du Bercail in a modest house with a small garden. At his quiet evening hour he sat in his black leather armchair, half dozing, pleased with his plans to round out the d’Esgrignon estates for future memory; his affections were wrapped up in that single attachment. His housekeeper entered one evening in alarm: “M. du Croisier wanting to speak to you—” The name chilled Chesnel; he dropped the tongs.

Du Croisier entered, bowed, sat, and produced a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, principal and interest, for sums advanced to Victurnien in bills of exchange drawn upon du Croisier and honored by him. He demanded immediate payment and threatened proceedings against the heir-presumptive. Chesnel turned over the unlucky letters and begged secrecy; du Croisier agreed to keep quiet if paid within forty-eight hours. Pressed for money, Chesnel had obliged manufacturers and resorted to the usual financial fictions; he was near tears. The only way to raise the money would be to mortgage his lands to their full value.

When du Croisier learned Chesnel could not repay, he suddenly offered to buy the old lawyer’s property. The sale was arranged within two days. Chesnel, horrified at the thought of the heir of the house undergoing imprisonment for debt, accepted; he was left with only his practice, the sums due to him, and the house where he lived. Stripped of lands, he paced his oak-paneled office and thought no longer of Le Jard but of Victurnien.

“What will become of him? He ought to come back; they must marry him to some rich heiress,” he said with dim eyes and a heavy head. He dreaded breaking the news to Mlle. Armande; the man who had paid the family’s debts quaked at the thought. He went from the Rue du Bercail to the Hotel d’Esgrignon, fearing the ruin of illusions.

Mlle. Armande had just received a charming, hypocritical letter. Her nephew was the happiest man alive: he had gone to baths, traveled in Italy with Mme. de Maufrigneuse, and sent a journal full of love—Venice, Milan, Florence—painting happiness everywhere. The poor aunt was enthralled and dreamt herself in Victurnien’s gondola, feeling the Duchess’ hand in his; she excused a married woman’s love for her nephew. Mlle. Armande was not an angelic ascetic but a slender, serene figure; at that moment blue buds were unfolding in her saintly eyes. She loved them as apart from life.

Even in her Venetian reverie, Chesnel’s heavy step on the garden walk recalled her to reality. “What is it?” she cried. “All is lost!” Chesnel answered, holding out the bills and describing the recent agony in simple, touching words. “He is deceiving us! The miserable boy!” cried Mlle. Armande. “Let us say mea culpa, mademoiselle,” Chesnel replied stoutly; “we have allowed him his own way; he needed stern guidance—he had no mother.” Fate sometimes deals terribly with a decaying noble house, she thought, with tears.

The Marquis joined them, having been reading his son’s letter from Italy, full of aristocratic travel highlights. He recounted ancestral glories and walked away, unconcerned. The two companions in distress sat together.

“What will become of him now?” Mlle. Armande asked. “Du Croisier has sent instructions to the Kellers; he is not to be allowed to draw any more without authorization,” Chesnel said. “And there are debts.” “If he is left without resources, what will he do?” “I dare not answer that.” “But he must be drawn out of that life; he must come back to us, or he will have nothing left.” “Yes,” Chesnel replied. “You and M. le Marquis own the estate conjointly; the larger part is yours. You can raise money on it without saying a word to him.”

That evening those at whist noticed Mlle. Armande’s agitation. “That poor heroic child!” murmured the old Marquise de Casteran. The next day it was arranged that Mlle. Armande should go to Paris to snatch her nephew from perdition. If anyone could carry him off, it was the woman whose motherly heart yearned over him. She determined to go to the Duchess and tell her all. For appearances she let it be thought she suffered from a complaint requiring consultation with famous Parisian physicians, a pretext to explain the journey. She saw that far more than her reputation was at stake. She set out with Chesnel’s last bag of louis, taking it and her capuchine and mittens without comment. “Generous girl!” Chesnel said, putting her into the carriage with her maid.

Du Croisier had thought out his revenge as provincials do—meticulously, with every contingency. Had he read Victurnien’s nature well enough to foresee how easily the young Count would lend himself to a scheme? Or was he simply seizing a long-watched opportunity? Perhaps the Kellers or the son of President du Ronceret had whispered news in his ear.

Du Croisier wrote to Victurnien that the Kellers had been instructed to advance no more money; the letter was timed to arrive when the Duchess and the Count faced poverty artfully concealed. In the letter informing Victurnien the Kellers would make no further advances without security, he left a wide blank space between the formal text and the signature. It was easy to tear off the upper part and convert the lower into a bill of exchange for any amount. The missive was enclosed in an envelope so the other side of the sheet was blank. When it arrived, Victurnien was in the lowest despair. After two years of luxurious, thoughtless life, he was faced with inexorable poverty: an absolute impossibility to procure money.

There had been throes before the journey’s end. With the Duchess’ help he had extorted various sums from bankers, but those very amounts were about to become overdue bills, with stern summonses from the Bank of France and the commercial court. All through the last weeks he had felt the point of impending ruin: at every supper-party a creeping warning that the sirocco of debt was near. For five years his purse had always been replenished; after Chesnel came du Croisier, and he had expected another gold-mine to pour out its wealth. He had been winning at play; luck had saved him unpleasant steps before. His life became like Don Giovanni’s chaotic finale—disorder, confusion, a frantic struggle to shut out debts and duels. He lived through this infernal poem alone, seeing visions of a friendless outcast reading the last words on the last page—THE END.

Yes; for him all would soon be at an end. He already imagined ironical associates amusing themselves over his downfall. Some played high and could not spare a banknote for a friend. There was no help—Chesnel must be ruined; he had been consumed by Victurnien’s extravagance.

He sat with the Duchess in their box at the Italiens, envied by the house, while inwardly despairing. To give the measure of his distress: he had even taken out pistols and thought of suicide. He who would never brook insult was abusing himself in private invective. He left du Croisier’s letter open on the bed; Josephin had brought it at nine. Victurnien’s furniture had been seized, but he slept none the less. After the Opera he and the Duchess had retreated to their secret, voluptuous lodging; appearances were cleverly saved—their love-nest seemed a garret to outsiders but was luxuriously arranged within. Now, on the brink of ruin, he longed to bid farewell to that refuge. Adversity would break the enchanted eggs; there would be no brood of white doves, no brilliant tropical birds of fancy. In three days he must be gone; his bills had been placed with money-lenders, and legal proceedings had reached the last stage.

An evil thought crossed him. He would flee with the Duchess to some undiscovered nook in America but only if he carried a fortune, leaving creditors to confront their bills. To carry out the plan he had only to cut off the lower portion of du Croisier’s letter bearing the signature and fill in figures to turn it into a bill, presenting it to the Kellers. There was a dreadful struggle with temptation; tears were shed; but the honor of the family triumphed—subject to one condition. Victurnien wanted to be sure of his beautiful Diane; he would do nothing unless she consented to their flight. So he went to the Duchess in the Rue Faubourg Saint-Honoré and found her in coquettish morning dress.

Mme. de Maufrigneuse’s mind was somewhat pensive. Cares gnawed at her, but she met them gallantly. Some women combine strength and insight with swift decision and a resolution that would unsettle a man; these powers lie beneath an appearance of graceful helplessness. Such women can rise to the highest sentiments or show selfish insensibility. The Duchess, pondering her fall, set love aside to consider safety: refuge abroad, confessing to the King, fascinating a du Tillet or a Nucingen, or gambling on the stock exchange—all were reviewed calmly. She could stand aloof and watch the crash as a spectator rather than be buried beneath it. That power was great but repulsive.

While dressing for a drive she had collected her thoughts and faced the possibilities of terrific downfall. She plucked love out of her heart to consider necessities and was ready to reinstate passion when her position was safe. She had none of Richelieu’s hesitation or Napoleon’s early doubts; she resolved.

Victurnien came in, terror in every line. His heart beat violently; he dared not touch the cornerstone that upheld his life with Diane. He spoke, with aristocratic disdain for sordid detail, of his hopeless situation. “What is the matter?” Diane asked. “I am hopelessly in debt. I have come to the end.” “Pshaw! it is nothing,” she said with a smile. “Money can always be arranged; nothing is irretrievable except disasters in love.” Relieved by her composure, he unrolled the bright web of his life’s past two years and a half—the seamy side—detailing his needs.

She listened, intent and grave with love. To have her thus seemed divine. When Victurnien proposed flight, she cried, “You are an angel!” The Duchess made no immediate answer; her mind weighed plans. “Good, very good,” she said at last. “But that is not the question, dear. Let us think of your affairs. Yes, we will go, and the sooner the better. Arrange it; I will follow. It is glorious to leave Paris and the world.” When a duchess speaks so, the sacrifice is made. Diane’s promise settled him; he could now conceal sordid details because she did not inquire. Victurnien would not go until the promise was sealed; he needed courage from happiness before committing an act that might be badly construed. He counted on his aunt, father, and Chesnel to hush up the affair; Chesnel might think of some compromise. Besides, this “business” was the only way to raise money on the family estate. With three hundred thousand francs he and Diane would lead a happy hidden life in Venice.

Next day Victurnien drew a bill for three hundred thousand francs and took it to the Kellers. They advanced the money, for du Croisier happened then to have a balance; but they wrote that he must not draw again without notice. Du Croisier, much astonished, asked for a statement of accounts. It was sent. Everything was explained. The day of his vengeance had arrived.

When Victurnien had withdrawn the money, he carried it to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who locked the banknotes in her desk and proposed they bid the world farewell by going to the Opera one last time. Victurnien was distracted; he worried that his place in the Duchess’ box might cost him dearly and thought it wiser, once the three hundred thousand francs were safe, to travel post to Chesnel and tell him everything. The Duchess, eager to linger once more in the nest she loved, gave him a look that kept him sleepless.

At three the next day Victurnien returned to the Hotel de Maufrigneuse to learn the Duchess’ plan for that night’s escape. “Why should we go?” she asked. “The Vicomtesse de Beauseant and the Duchesse de Langeais disappeared; if I go too it will be commonplace. We will brave the storm; it will be finer. I am sure of success.” Victurnien felt as if his skin were dissolving; he hesitated.

Diane, noticing his hesitation, reproached him with the merciless coldness of a proud woman. A tactful lover would have yielded, but Victurnien, stung, could not. She accused him of ingratitude after eighteen months of tenderness and turned away, implacable. He protested, urged that he had done much for her, and flung himself at her feet—pleading, weeping. For two hours she maintained a solemn agony; her silence and disdain were deliberate punishment. Two more hours passed; her hand, when seized, felt lifeless. Victurnien fell into a stupor of despair; memories of past rapture floated by like pictures on a black background. The Duchess toyed with her scarf, spoke cruel epigrams about his rivals—Vandenesse, de Marsay, Montriveau—each meant to wound. At last, out of patience, Victurnien cried, “You are mad!” and fled, driving recklessly.

As his horse bolted near the Quai d’Orsay and turned into the Rue de l’Universite, Josephin stopped him. “You cannot go home, sir,” the old servant said. “They have come with a warrant to arrest you.” In his agitation Victurnien imagined a criminal arrest, having forgotten the affair of the bills of exchange, which for days had been “stirred up” into orders to pay and legal proceedings. “It is all over with me,” he cried.

“No, M. le Comte. Drive as fast as you can to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine in the Rue de Grenelle. Mlle. Armande is waiting there, the horses are put in; she will take you with her.” Desperate, Victurnien seized this refuge. At the inn he flung himself into his aunt Mlle. Armande’s arms; she wept, half believing herself complicit in his ruin. They set out for Brest that night, and Paris slid away behind them. Both spoke at cross purposes—Victurnien thinking only of his forgery, his aunt thinking of debts and bills. He suggested sneaking into Chesnel’s house at night to hide; she soothed him like a child.

Chance, however, betrayed them. That evening Chesnel had been arranging to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir’s head-clerk—a negotiation that would bring him a hundred thousand francs. Chesnel’s successor, waiting in the Rue du Bercail, saw Mlle. Armande alight from a travelling carriage at an odd hour and, curious, watched. When Chesnel opened his door and saw Victurnien, he understood at once. He ushered the Count into a room beyond his private office—no one could enter without his consent.

“My good Chesnel,” said the Count, “you do not know the full extent of my troubles.”

Victurnien explained the forgery. Chesnel, thunderstruck but devoted, wept. He could not accept that a forger’s scandal should reach the old man’s father; he raged, suggested impossible remedies—soften du Croisier, buy back the draft from the Kellers, hide the Count in a cellar—and decided, by sheer devotion, to go to Paris himself. He packed clothes, took money, left the Count in the locked room, and told his housekeeper to keep all casual visitors away.

Chesnel reached Paris in three days by diligence. At the Kellers he learned the draft had already returned to du Croisier three days before; the document belonged to du Croisier, who could keep or return it. In despair, Chesnel went to the Duchess. She was in bed; by persuasion and insolence Chesnel had his way and was ushered into her room. He told her bluntly that the hundred thousand crowns in her keeping had been gained by a forgery committed for love of her. Struck by shame and fear of implication, she flung off the eiderdown, opened her desk, handed over the notes, and pledged to use her influence to save Victurnien. She promised to save him “or die,” and Chesnel left triumphant.

Back in the Rue du Bercail, despite Chesnel’s haste, he arrived an hour too late: the gendarmes had already come and taken Victurnien away. Chesnel, wild with grief, begged the officers to be merciful and, seeing the Count led out between gendarmes, whispered to him that if he could not hush the matter he must kill himself before the indictment was made. Victurnien, in stupor, could not comprehend.

Chesnel then dashed to the Hotel du Croisier. A criminal forgery charge must be brought by a private individual; du Croisier had the right to pursue it—or to withdraw. He found du Croisier surrounded that night by intimates—M. du Ronceret, President of the Tribunal; M. Sauvager, deputy Public Prosecutor; M. du Coudrai; the examining magistrate M. Camusot; and others. The gathering was half political and half social; inside, they discussed the d’Esgrignon affair with the excitement of men who saw in it a test of social and legal principle.

At du Croisier’s, Sauvager spoke with the complacent triumph of an official who fancies himself both zealous and undervalued. Camusot, the examining magistrate who had authorized the warrant, was perturbed that the arrest had been executed so promptly. The group debated whether the case would come to trial; Mme. Camusot warned that political pressure might work beneath the surface to influence the jury and the outcome. They spoke of counsel from Paris—Berryer for the defense—and of “a sensation at Landernau,” aware that high influence could intervene.

While the conspirators sat, Chesnel, hastily intent on saving the young house of d’Esgrignon, sought out the examining magistrate in the street and implored him: “If you are for the house of d’Esgrignon, the Duchess, the Prince de Cadignan, dukes and ministers will interest themselves in you.” He hinted that he would denounce suspected corruption at du Croisier’s if the magistrate were hostile. Camusot, won by his wife’s persuasive airs and perhaps the appeal to broader influence, listened.

Chesnel then tried to soften du Croisier. On bended knee he offered whatever was demanded: lands, service, anything if du Croisier would withdraw his charge. Du Croisier enjoyed his power. He saw in prosecuting the Count a chance to assert bourgeois dignity against the aristocracy and to teach the nobles a lesson. He delighted in the idea of exciting the Court and the public by bringing a proud house low. When Mme. du Croisier appeared and, touched by Chesnel’s plea, tried to appeal to her husband’s better judgment, du Croisier did not yield. He demanded more than money: he wanted political advantage and social recognition. He proposed that the d’Esgrignon family be bound by a surety and that the deposit be kept as leverage; he even insisted on a marriage arrangement tying property as future security—plans which Chesnel and Mme. du Croisier found odious.

Du Croisier pictured the affair as larger than personal revenge: it was “France,” “the people,” a lesson that the bourgeoisie would not be trampled on by nobles. He fancied his victory would be sealed in the Assize Court and by a matrimonial entanglement that would secure his political aims. Chesnel, in despair, then produced the hundred thousand crowns and implored Mme. du Croisier to accept them to spare the d’Esgrignons. Mme. du Croisier hesitated, torn between obedience to her husband and pity for the fallen family. Chesnel, pressing every religious and familial plea, persuaded her to take the money and promise to swear before magistrates that she had received it on a date prior to the drafting of the contested bill—an explicit falsehood he urged as a “venial sin” to save lives and honor.

To clinch this, Chesnel planned to involve the Abbé Couturier and the bishop, knowing clerical counsel would strengthen Mme. du Croisier’s resolve. He roused Mlle. Armande and sent her to rally ecclesiastical support while he went home to sleep, exhausted. He had also gone to the Kellers and the Duchess earlier and had thought through the magistrates’ personal ambitions and the judicial politics of the town, calculating how to influence them in the coming struggle.

The narrative then broadens to a picture of provincial justice. Magistrates and officials often begin careers in the provinces; those who remain divide into two classes. Some, cul-de-sac men resigned to local life, are torpid or content; others, younger and ambitious, nurture hopes of promotion to Paris and strive to shine at politically charged trials. In this town, the bench was split: conservative, settled men like President du Ronceret and old Blondet represented the resigned section; youthful, zealous men like Camusot and his deputy comprised the ambitious group eager to make reputations by zealous prosecutions. The provincial administration leaned bourgeois, often zealous in its own political passion, ready to act like spies and display public zeal that sometimes overstepped discretion.

President du Ronceret is described as a man of petty ambitions and resentments. Nominated president where he had once hoped for more, he harbors bitterness and inconsistency—willing to profess independence while opposing both sides. He and his wife maintained old-fashioned house and habits and gave boulevard soirées and occasional grand dinners to keep social standing; yet his private intrigues, even in arranging marriages for his son Fabien, betray petty rivalries. Old Blondet, by contrast, is a veteran jurist, an ex-public accuser whose conduct has been moderate and prudent; though sidelined, he is respected for judgment and steadiness.

Chesnel’s grasp of this judicial world—the factions, the ambitions, the petty rancours—shapes his strategy. He sees that many of these men, still bound to hopes of advancement, may be swayed if they think powerful patrons in Paris support them. Thus, he plans to use the Duchess’ influence and court connections as leverage. He also recognizes the dangers: du Croisier’s vindictiveness and the deputy public prosecutor’s eagerness for prominence threaten a trial that could ruin the d’Esgrignon family even if an acquittal were possible; disgrace would nonetheless endure.

Thus the crisis deepens: Victurnien is arrested; Chesnel has bribed, pleaded, and schemed; the Duchess has returned the notes and promised aid; du Croisier delights in his leverage and demands political recompense; Mme. du Croisier is torn but finally, under Chesnel’s urging and with the money in hand, agrees to a false testimony to avert dishonor; and the local magistrates, with their divided ambitions and political passions, loom as the battleground where the fate of the d’Esgrignons will be decided. Chesnel lies awake mapping out the men he must influence, promising to set great powers in motion through Mme. de Maufrigneuse, while the provincial world of judges, presidents, clerks, and aspiring officials gathers round the coming trial with all the pettiness and consequence of a local theater of justice.

Blondet’s legal talents might have carried him far, but he held his profession in contempt and reserved his ambitions, leisure, and energy for another pursuit: gardening. Corresponding with celebrated amateurs and keen on botanical discoveries, the old judge lived among flowers, his favorite the pelargonium. Court business and outward life mattered little beside the interior life of fancies and emotions he led in his greenhouse. Had he not been so absorbed, he would likely have been a deputy under the Empire and prominent in the Corps Législatif.

His marriage deepened his retreat from public life. At forty he rashly married an eighteen-year-old who bore him a son, Joseph. Three years later Mme. Blondet, then the town’s prettiest woman, inspired a passion in the prefect that lasted until her death; the prefect became the father of her second son, Emile. The whole town knew, and even old Blondet did. Rather than arousing ambition, the marriage encouraged his botanical tastes; neither husband nor wife wished to change places. Unequal to contest with youth and beauty, Blondet sought consolation in his greenhouse and kept a pretty servant to assist him. Mme. Blondet spent his substance on dress and finery; only Emile’s education interested her. She loved Emile and Blondet loved Joseph, each blinded by paternal partiality.

For twelve years Blondet bore these circumstances with eighteenth-century grand seigneur composure, though he profoundly disliked his younger son. When his wife died in 1818 he turned Emile out, sending him to Paris on a scant allowance; Emile would have perished without help from his real father. Blondet’s house, opposite the prefecture, had a neat court and admired flowerbeds; climbing plants covered walls, honeysuckle hid piers, and cactuses perched in vases. Through glass doors and windows one could see across to the garden, which extended into two acres. Blondet’s income was about four thousand livres from land, plus the house. He intended to leave his property and bench seat to Joseph, having made a will favoring him and setting aside money to enable Joseph to buy back portions of the estate that could not legally be taken from Emile.

Emile, turned adrift, found distinction in Paris as a man of letters and in society, aided by friendships like that with Mlle. de Troisville (later Mme. de Montcornet). Mme. Blondet had tried to link him to the Troisvilles, who later favored him when they returned to Paris. Emile’s precocious literary reputation and good looks strengthened his bond with the Countess de Montcornet; by the time his adventurous life pressed upon him, their attachment proved unshakeable. Old Blondet never knew of Emile’s success; he had been cursed in his mind and therefore remained ignorant of his son’s greatness.

Blondet’s integrity matched his passion for flowers. He knew law and botany alone. He met litigants courteously, accepted gifts of rare seeds, but on the bench he was impartial; litigants trusted his clear procedure. His steadiness made him indispensable to President du Ronceret, who, apart from matrimonial schemes, would have done all he could to prevent the vice-president’s retirement in favor of his son. Blondet did not know Emile could have fulfilled his wishes quickly.

Their household was frugal to parsimony: fruit saved until it spoiled, no new bread, fast days strictly observed. The maid Mlle. Cadot kept keys and watched expenses; she shared the father’s predilection for Joseph. Blondet tried to mold Joseph into a lawyer to satisfy Mlle. Blandureau’s parents, who insisted he be an assistant judge to marry their daughter. Joseph spent evenings at the Blandureaus’, where young Fabien du Ronceret also frequented. Everything was measured; the garden, however, escaped thrift. The gardener, a single man, kept paths and borders immaculate, and an amphitheatre of benches held thousands of pelargoniums that drew visitors from distant departments; Empress Marie Louise once visited and Blondet received the Legion of Honor. Blondet shunned society and was unaware of the President’s machinations.

Michu, a young judge with twelve thousand livres income, cared more for making himself agreeable to high society than for provincial routine. He did enough at the Tribunal to be useful and, with Parisian training as prosecuting counsel, could rapidly solve legal questions that baffled the President and Blondet. He was indispensable socially and professionally, able to mediate delicate matters.

When crisis came, the public prosecutor was away in Paris. The President and du Croisier seized his absence to engineer a charge of forgery against Victurnien d’Esgrignon. Normally such a case might be hushed to avoid scandal, but the President contrived to have M. Sauvager, a young Royalist deputy public prosecutor who owed his place to subservience to the Ministry, take up the charge without consulting his absent chief. The President and du Croisier persuaded Sauvager by promising advantageous matches and future fortunes if he showed devotion, urging him to act against the d’Esgrignons promptly to preempt Parisian intervention. Sauvager agreed.

Camusot’s unexpected defection from the President’s scheme terrified the plotters. Camusot, the examining magistrate, had his own background: son of a silk mercer with ambitions for him, married to Amélie Thirion (daughter of an usher of the Royal cabinet), he advanced by backstairs influence. His wife, Mme. Camusot, with Parisian instincts and impatience with provincial life, meddled in her husband’s career; she saw the d’Esgrignon affair as an opportunity to ingratiate the family with powerful circles, including the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, and to secure promotion and a Paris appointment for her husband. Perceiving the President’s underhand maneuvers, she advised Camusot to side with the d’Esgrignons, foreseeing advantage.

Chesnel, who knew the Tribunal’s circle, counted on the public prosecutor, Camusot, and Michu to support the d’Esgrignons; he also believed Blondet, if persuaded, would favor what he saw as justice for a cause that had been his lifelong hope. Chesnel planned to go to Blondet to avert the President’s treachery. Before dawn he was roused by a startling visitor: the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, disguised in man’s attire, had driven from Paris to save Victurnien. She declared she had brought a hundred thousand francs from the King’s private purse to buy Victurnien’s innocence if necessary; if bribery failed, she had brought poison to prevent an indictment. She had sent word to the public prosecutor, who was following her.

Chesnel embraced the Duchess’s aid. They went to see Camusot early; the Duchess intended to charm Mme. Camusot and secure Camusot’s cooperation. Playing her role, she understood provincial manners and boasted of the influence exerted by Thirion’s family connection to the King. She and Chesnel repeated the plan: the Duchess would speak to Mme. Camusot, and Chesnel would use his authority to persuade Camusot that rescuing the d’Esgrignon was in the kingdom’s interest. The Duchess insisted on secrecy.

Mme. Camusot, quick to grasp the situation, accompanied the Duchess to Blondet’s greenhouse, presenting the Duchess as a distinguished horticulturist. She warned Blondet of the President’s duplicity: the President aimed to thwart Blondet’s hopes for Joseph’s marriage to Mlle. Blandureau, and Fabien du Ronceret had received clandestine proposals for Mlle. Claire’s hand. Blondet, a man of flowers, was shocked. The Duchess promised that if Blondet resigned properly and awaited confirmation from the public prosecutor, his son’s appointment could be secured within a week; she urged him to consult Camusot and Michu to hush up the affair quickly, ensuring Joseph’s prospects. Blondet, overwhelmed and suspicious, hurried to seek confirmation.

At nine the three judges—Blondet, Camusot, and Michu—met secretly. Blondet, furious at the President’s trickery, insisted the matter was a trifle and argued against construing the episode as forgery: forgery requires an intent to defraud, which seemed absent here, since Chesnel had repeatedly squared accounts and had allegedly deposited money to meet the bill. Michu proposed removing the Count from peril by hastening the examination of du Croisier and his wife and issuing a decision the next morning. They agreed to act swiftly.

Du Croisier was summoned and questioned by Camusot; under searching interrogation he confirmed prior dealings with the Count and that Chesnel had often remitted sums. Camusot then asked whether du Croisier knew the money destined to meet the bill had been deposited with him beforehand; this unnerved him. Chesnel, summoned, confirmed he had deposited the money and produced letters to prove it; Mme. du Croisier corroborated his statement, declaring Chesnel had entrusted the sum to her in her husband’s absence. With these facts on record, the Count’s innocence emerged legally.

Du Croisier returned home enraged at his wife’s deposition; she showed him bundles of banknotes she had kept concealed and argued she had acted for his good and the family’s future advantage. He threatened hasty departure and secrecy, while his wife believed she had safeguarded their prospects.

The town buzzed with the Count’s arrest; yet by ten the court dismissed the charge, giving reasons that allowed the Count the right to sue for libel. Chesnel walked the streets exposing du Croisier’s plot. As the day closed, the Marquis d’Esgrignon and family fretted over debts and honor. The Duchess, who had stayed with them, advised practical measures: marry for money if necessary; the old privileges of nobility had been eroded by modern law and fortunes mattered more than lineage. She produced a tiny phial and joked about more decisive remedies; she counselled Victurnien to take lands and a quiet life rather than Paris flirtations. The romantic episode ended: the Duchess, once disguised and passionate, resumed her rank; for Victurnien and Diane, the brief romance dissolved with the crisis’s end.

Six months later Camusot became an assistant judge in Paris and later an examining magistrate. Goodman Blondet was appointed councillor to the Royal Court, kept the post until he secured a pension, then returned to his pretty house. Joseph Blondet took his father’s seat at the court until his death; with no hope of promotion he married Mlle. Blandereau and likely now leads, in the little flower-covered brick house, as dull a life as any carp in a marble basin. Michu and Camusot received the Cross of the Legion of Honor; Blondet became an Officer. M. Sauvager, deputy public prosecutor, was sent to Corsica, much to du Croisier’s relief; du Croisier certainly did not intend to give his niece to that functionary.

Urged by President du Ronceret, du Croisier appealed the Tribunal’s finding to the Court-Royal and lost. Liberals across the department held little d’Esgrignon guilty; Royalists spun frightful stories of plots by “that abominable du Croisier” to compass revenge. A duel followed: chance favored du Croisier; the young Count was dangerously wounded, and his antagonist stood by his words. The affair embittered party strife, and the Liberals invoked it at every opportunity. Meanwhile du Croisier never carried his election and saw no hope of marrying his niece to the Count, especially after the duel.

A month after the Tribunal’s decision was confirmed, Chesnel died, exhausted by the strain that had weakened him mentally and physically. He died at the moment of victory, like a faithful hound that brings the boar to bay and dies on its tusks. Though the great House was nearly ruined, the heir penniless and bored, and prospects of establishing himself gone, Chesnel’s exhaustion and bitter thoughts hastened his end. One comfort: at his sister’s entreaty the old Marquis restored their friendship. The great lord came to the Rue du Bercail and sat by his old servant’s bedside, unaware how much the servant had done and sacrificed. Chesnel sat up and repeated Simeon’s cry. The Marquis allowed burial in the castle chapel; they laid him crosswise at the foot of the tomb reserved for the Marquis himself, the last, in a sense, of the d’Esgrignons.

Thus died one of the last representatives of Service in its original meaning: the relation between feudal lord and servitor. Found now only in remote provinces or among a few old royal servants, it honored a noblesse that inspired affection and a bourgeoisie that could conceive it. Such magnificent devotion is no longer possible. Noble houses have no servitors; France no longer has a King, hereditary peerage, or land irrevocably bound to historic houses to perpetuate glorious names. Chesnel was not merely an obscure great man of private life; he was a great fact. There is something solemn and sublime in his sustained self-devotion, rising above a single beneficent deed or a moment’s supreme effort. Chesnel combined homely burgher virtues with heroic noble ideals enlightened by solid education — virtues belonging to classes between the people’s poverty and the aristocracy’s greatness.

Victurnien was not well regarded at Court; there was no chance of a great match or office. His Majesty refused to raise the d’Esgrignons to the peerage—the one royal favor that might have redeemed his position. He could not marry a bourgeois heiress during his father’s life, so he lived shabbily under the paternal roof, haunted by memories of two years’ splendor in Paris and the lost love of a great lady. He grew moody and depressed, vegetating with a careworn aunt and a half-broken father who blamed a wasting malady. Chesnel was gone.

The Marquis died in 1830. The great d’Esgrignon, followed by the less infirm noblesse of the Collection of Antiquities, went to wait upon Charles X at Nonancourt, swelling the meager train of the fallen king—an act then seen as heroism amid revolt. “The Gaul has conquered!” were the Marquis’ last words.

By that time du Croisier’s victory was complete. A week after his father’s death the new Marquis d’Esgrignon married Mlle. Duval. His bride brought three million francs; du Croisier and his wife settled the reversion of their fortunes upon her in the marriage contract. During the ceremony du Croisier declared the d’Esgrignon family the most honorable of all the ancient houses in France.

Someday the present Marquis will have an income exceeding a hundred thousand crowns. He comes to Paris every winter and leads a jolly bachelor life, treating his wife with more than the indifference of the grand seigneur of olden times—he takes no thought for her.

“As for Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” said Emile Blondet, source of these details, “if she is no longer like the divinely fair woman whom I saw by glimpses in my childhood, she is, at sixty-seven, the most pathetic and interesting figure in the Collection of Antiquities. She queens it among them still. I saw her on my last journey home to fetch the necessary papers for my marriage. When my father learned whom I had married he was struck dumb until I told him I was a prefect.

“‘You were born to it,’ he said, with a smile.

“As I took a walk around the town I met Mlle. Armande; she seemed taller than ever. I thought of Marius among the ruins of Carthage. Had she not outlived her creed and the beliefs that time destroyed? She is a sad and silent woman, with nothing of her old beauty left except her eyes, which shine with an unearthly light. I watched her on her way to mass, book in hand, and could not help thinking that she prayed to be taken out of the world.”

LES JARDIES, July 1837.
