The author addresses Mademoiselle Anna Hanska tenderly, noting the incongruity of dedicating a melancholy tale to one who brings household joy, yet insisting sorrow must sometimes be spoken—perhaps to prepare her gentle hands for future need.

On an October morning in 1827 a sixteen-year-old boy in plain clothes stood in a small square of Lower Provins, observing working mills and modest bourgeois houses. He found the white stone house he sought—recently restored, with gray blinds and neat balconies, the tidy home of a retired petty shopkeeper. As sunlight lit a calico curtain, the boy burst into a Breton ditty that recalled Brittany’s bittersweet turns. Pierrette, a frail girl in a garret, answered with a signal; he revealed a yellow broom-flower. He was Brigaut, a childhood companion who spoke of settling nearby. Pierrette, alarmed, urged him away when a grotesque old maid below—given to sharp observations and scandalous misreadings of harmless scenes—appeared.

Alone, Pierrette reflected on her precarious past: her mother, once a Demoiselle Auffray, came from a tangled family and a father who remarried, leaving a confused inheritance. Her mother later married Major Lorrain, who died in battle and left them poor. The Lorrains could not support Pierrette, so she was sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, in Provins. Unaware of the full intricacies and consequences of her lineage, Pierrette’s fate is increasingly shaped by these relatives, setting the stage for the ensuing drama.

Père Rogron, an indulgent, fat innkeeper in Provins, and his unattractive, selfish wife lived on modest means. After selling the inn they moved into a dilapidated house inherited from Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother. Their children, Sylvie and Jérôme-Denis, were neglected, ill-bred, and coarse-voiced; Rogron sent them to Paris under a doctrine of harsh minimal support.

Sylvie apprenticed on rue Saint-Denis, rose to forewoman and became independent; Jérôme became head-clerk. They pooled savings to buy the 'Family Sister.' Despite modest success, fierce competition and their severity made them notorious for stinginess and drove clerks away. Sylvie, shrewd but plain, showed a pleasing front to customers and a sour persona. While mourning their father they learned of cousin Pierrette’s inheritance; intent on preserving their wealth, they refused to take her in, delayed replying, and left for Provins four years before Brigaut’s arrival.

A shopkeeper returns from Paris; his Parisian airs quickly fade. The Rogrons use new wealth to pursue bourgeois status—remodeling house and dabbling in politics. Renovations signal ambition as they vie for the town’s elite. Madame Tiphaine advances him by hosting salons; the Rogrons lack polish—Sylvie takes up cards while Rogron remains awkward. Lavish dinners fail; invitations stop and ridicule follows. Sylvie’s faith that money buys status collapses; she becomes frugal; they sink into ennui and resentment at their old trade. Bringing cousin Pierrette worsens neglect and ostracism. Bitter, they court the lower bourgeoisie, exploited by opportunists like Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet. The story shows provincial hierarchies and social codes trump wealth.

After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette—ashamed to live on charity—felt relieved when wealthy relatives were found. Childhood friend Brigaut, son of her mother’s friend, gave her sixty francs for the trip to Paris; she accepted without thanks, a sign of their closeness. He often visited and ran after the coach in tears. Penniless in Paris, a conductor briefly paid for her hotel; a kinder one took her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s, where her cousins received her with astonishment.

She arrived in Breton dress with a small bundle; Sylvie and Rogron were startled. Pierrette, shy and overwhelmed, spoke little. Sylvie and the maid Adèle showed her a modest room that felt luxurious, then immediately fretted over her lack of clothes and resolved to buy a wardrobe.

In Brittany she had known affection and freedom; with the Rogrons she found coldness, criticism, and an obsession with appearances. Reproved for dirty shoes and treated as a burden, she suppressed joy and grew passive. Sylvie forbade visits and pursued social ambitions with Vinet and the colonel. As her cousins plotted for status, Pierrette was confined and transformed from a lively child into a shadow, enduring harsh, self‑interested care rather than love.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

At the start of a new phase with her wealthy cousins, Pierrette sought a goodnight kiss; Sylvie turned away, making her cry. When asked why, Pierrette said her rich cousin was not as kind as her poor grandmother; Sylvie retorted that the grandmother had taken money while the cousin would leave her some—Pierrette wanted affection, not wealth.

Sylvie then accused Pierrette of mischief—breaking a watch, disobeying orders—and Pierrette left ashamed. Rogron demanded punishment, Madame Vinet urged schooling, and the colonel thought instruction might quiet her. A writing master was hired; ink stains and scattered materials were blamed on Pierrette and she was harshly told to earn her keep. Rogron’s severity and Sylvie’s indifference deepened her isolation; curiosity stifled, she grew ill and her complaints were dismissed.

Only Adèle showed kindness. Plans to escape to grandparents in Brittany ended after a death and Rogron’s appointment as guardian; he secured a promise of eight thousand francs from Pierrette’s grandmother. Preparations for her first communion brought Monsieur Habert, whose fervent instruction gave Pierrette solace and led her to abandon escape plans and embrace faith. The Rogrons refused a convent school; later introductions to the Chargeboeufs and Bathilde’s interest in Rogron prompted Sylvie to make Pierrette a servant. Without Adèle’s protection, Pierrette faded from lively child to silent shadow while her cousins remained oblivious.

Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut's arrival would incite, it is necessary to explain how he came to Provins, since he plays a significant yet passive role.

Brigaut fled the Rogron house, shaken by Pierrette’s alarming decline from lively girl to one consumed by illness and sorrow. From the Upper town he watched her home, torn by concern and his own feelings. Hoping to better himself for her, he briefly went to Paris to learn a trade, returned after a fortnight, and found work as a journeyman cabinetmaker so he could stay near Pierrette. He suppressed his feelings while trying to learn more about the Rogrons; Madame Frappier disclosed their dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. He often saw the frail Pierrette at the market and near the church, where she signaled him to remain hidden, fearful of some danger.

Pierrette fretted over changes since Brigaut’s return but did not perceive the Rogrons’ internal strife—chiefly between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste), rivals for advantageous marriages. Sylvie, forty-two, feared aging alone; Céleste, about ten years younger and urged on by her priest brother, actively sought a husband. A political shift encouraged local Liberals and sharpened social competition. Sylvie, alarmed, consulted a doctor whose discouraging prognosis deepened her anxiety; Céleste saw opportunity and advised delay to remove a rival.

Vinet, a lawyer eyeing Bathilde’s advantage, manipulated the colonel into courting Pierrette while secretly scheming to divert the Rogron fortune. The colonel’s attentions inflamed Sylvie, who—after overhearing Brigaut serenade Pierrette—mistakenly suspected the colonel, accused Pierrette, and threatened her when she remained silent. Under hard labor, emotional strain, and relentless scrutiny, Pierrette’s health waned. Brigaut offered brief comfort, but love, jealousy, and ambition became entangled, setting the stage for the tragic events to come.

For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the Constitutionnel; Gouraud now called often, lingering to gossip and walk with Sylvie. Eager to see the colonel, Sylvie dressed her best; when Rogron excused himself she was left alone with Gouraud.

Noting her solemnity, he asked about her cousin’s health. Gouraud felt threatened: Vinet’s growing influence over the Rogrons risked sidelining him. As a half-pay officer he feared Vinet’s control of their plans, Rogron’s infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, and suspected Vinet might steer Rogron toward Pierrette to benefit Vinet and Bathilde.

When Sylvie mentioned Pierrette, Gouraud denied any attachment, saying he preferred a more mature wife. Relieved, Sylvie concealed her aims; seeing her interest, Gouraud declared he would marry her. She began planning but remained wary of Pierrette.

At dinner Sylvie chastised Pierrette for a stain while dressing her nicely, suspecting collusion. Bathilde arrived resplendent and, guided by Vinet, treated marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone. Tensions rose—Bathilde’s condescension and Vinet’s remarks sharpened rivalries. At cards Pierrette’s innocent words provoked Sylvie; Pierrette left in tears. That night Sylvie brooded on men’s duplicity. The next day Pierrette’s painful fall met Sylvie’s blame, widening the rift.

The week ended in torment for Pierrette under Sylvie’s escalating cruelty. Brigaut, whose arrival Sylvie concealed, supported Pierrette and arranged secret letters: posing as a fruit buyer he slipped a note and asked her to lower a string from her window to exchange nightly messages. Pierrette answered his ardent plea to escape, replying at midnight despite difficulty obtaining materials without waking Sylvie. Their correspondence brimmed with hope and plans, which only increased Sylvie’s jealousy. Under closer watch Pierrette’s health declined; she fainted one evening, alarming the colonel while Sylvie called it a ruse. When Pierrette cried for help and Sylvie tried to seize the letters, a violent struggle left Pierrette hurt, her hand injured. At Sylvie’s height of fury, Madame Lorrain—having come to Provins at Brigaut’s urgent appeal—arrived, found Pierrette traumatized, secured medical care, and vowed to ensure her safety. As Pierrette recovered, her bond with her grandmother and hope for a life with Brigaut renewed her determination to reclaim freedom and love.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At nine o’clock Monsieur Martener told Monsieur Tiphaine about Pierrette’s mistreatment by the Rogrons. Tiphaine brought in Auffray, a notary and relative; longstanding enmities between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions—and damaging truths about Madame Tiphaine’s family—gave the dispute political charge. Seeking to weaken the Rogrons, powerful royalist opponents, Tiphaine consulted the public prosecutor. They petitioned the courts to make Auffray Pierrette’s guardian so a Family Council, backed by medical testimony, could remove her from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday the town buzzed after neighbors heard Pierrette’s cries. Doctors Bianchon and Brigaut examined her and confirmed abuse. Vinet, the Rogrons’ lawyer, warned of criminal exposure; Sylvie, initially evasive, later admitted jealousy and the truth. Vinet suggested a marriage announcement to quiet the scandal; under pressure the Rogrons signed a contract serving political ends. He then filed a counter-petition accusing Brigaut and the widow Lorrain of abducting Pierrette, recasting his clients as victims.

The hearing was postponed and interest grew. Pierrette, cared for by her grandmother and Brigaut, was condemned by the physicians. When the court reconvened, Auffray’s medical report led the judge to bar the Rogrons pending the Family Council. Relatives voted to remove Rogron as guardian and appoint Auffray; a civil suit for neglect and abuse followed.

Pierrette’s health declined. Despite growing affection from Martener and the Auffray family, a March 1828 operation offered little hope; she forgave her cousins and soon died. Her crowded funeral lacked the Rogrons; Brigaut made her coffin. Community support impeded the Rogrons and Vinet suffered backlash, but they largely retained political standing. Only Martener, Brigaut and a few friends remembered the reality of Pierrette’s suffering. The account closes on questions of justice, accountability, and her lasting impact.
