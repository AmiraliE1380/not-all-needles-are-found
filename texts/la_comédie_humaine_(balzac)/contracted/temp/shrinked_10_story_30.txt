PIERRETTE

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

Dear Child,—You, the joy of the household, whose pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of Wierzschovnia, how can I dedicate to you a story full of melancholy? Yet should not sorrows be spoken of to a young girl like you, since the day may come when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them? It is difficult to find a subject worthy of your eyes, so I have no choice; perhaps you will feel fortunate when you read this story sent to you by your old friend, De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

On an October morning in 1827 a sixteen-year-old boy in modest clothes stood in a small square of Lower Provins, taking in working mills and simple bourgeois houses framed by linden trees. He recognized the house he sought: a recently restored white stone dwelling with gray blinds and yellow iron balconies, its neatness betraying the pride of a retired petty shopkeeper.

As the sun lit a calico curtain in a garret window, the boy began a Breton ditty for newlyweds, a song that stirred memories of Brittany and life’s bittersweet turns. A girl, Pierrette, signaled from the window; he offered a yellow broom-flower. He was Brigaut, her childhood companion, and he spoke of settling nearby. Pierrette, alarmed, urged him to flee when a grotesque old maid below—known for sharp observations and a readiness to imagine scandal—appeared at a window.

Overwhelmed by Brigaut’s visit, Pierrette reflected on her past. Seated in a shabby armchair, frail and thoughtful, she recalled childhood joys now tinged by illness and uncertainty. Her mother, born a Demoiselle Auffray, came from a tangled family: a father who married twice and left a complicated inheritance. Her mother later married Major Lorrain, a Breton officer who died in battle, leaving the family impoverished. The Lorrains endured hardship and could not care for Pierrette.

Ultimately she was sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, in Provins. Largely unaware of the full intricacies of her lineage and the consequences of her parents’ fortunes and losses, Pierrette finds her fate increasingly shaped by these relatives. The Rogrons’ control sets the stage for the unfolding drama of her life.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Père Rogron, an innkeeper in Provins, was a short, fat man with a ruddy face and a taste for indulgence. He and his equally unattractive, selfish wife lived roughly, eking out a modest living from their land and the sale of the inn. After selling it they moved into a dilapidated house inherited from old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother. Their two children, Sylvie and Jérôme-Denis, were neglected and ill-bred, their features coarsened and voices harsh. Rogron sent them to Paris to work in shops, following a doctrine of tough love and minimal support.

Sylvie apprenticed on rue Saint-Denis, eventually becoming independent and a forewoman; Jérôme followed and became a head-clerk. They met only occasionally, content with modest outings, and pooled savings to buy a retail shop called the “Family Sister.” Though they met some success, fierce competition and their own severity made them notorious for harshness and lack of empathy.

Sylvie, not conventionally attractive but shrewd, ran the business effectively; Jérôme lacked the intellect for broader trade. Their lives revolved around the shop, leaving them oblivious to the wider world. Stinginess and poor treatment of employees produced high clerk turnover. Sylvie kept a double persona: a pleasing saleswoman to customers and a sour spinster in private.

While mourning their father and weighing their future, they received a letter about cousin Pierrette’s inheritance. Considering selling the business to return to Provins, they rejected taking Pierrette in to preserve their wealth and delayed responding; they left for Provins four years before Brigaut’s arrival.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A petty shopkeeper returns from Paris to his provincial town with few Parisian airs that fade as provincial life reasserts itself. The transition to bourgeois feels like an illness: Paris’s vibrancy gives way to local stillness. With new wealth, the Rogrons, brother and sister, indulge desires—travel, politics, hunting—and remodel their house to mimic richer families.

Their renovations advertise ambition; contractor consults them on details to match elite taste. Their first year is consumed by talk of the house and attempts to enter the town’s social circle—led by the established Julliards, Guepins, and Guenées. Madame Tiphaine, the town belle, advances her husband toward deputyship by hosting influential gatherings. The Rogrons, contrast, lack polish: Sylvie studies card games while Rogron remains awkward and silent.

Lavish dinners fail to win acceptance; invitations dwindle and ridicule spreads. Sylvie’s faith that money guarantees status collapses; she turns frugal, abandons entertainments, and the siblings sink into ennui, nostalgic for business days and resentful of isolation. Seeking a bridge to the elite, Sylvie brings in cousin Pierrette Lorrain, but her neglect becomes public and worsens their ostracism.

Bitter and sidelined, the Rogrons court the lower bourgeoisie. Figures like Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet exploit the Rogrons’ wealth and naiveté for political ends. The story shows how provincial hierarchies and subtle social codes trump money in determining status and influence.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette—ashamed to live on charity—felt relieved when wealthy relatives were found. Brigaut, a childhood friend and son of her mother’s friend, gave her sixty francs for the trip to Paris; she accepted without thanks, a sign of their close bond. He often visited to console her and ran after the coach in tears when she left. Penniless in Paris, a conductor briefly paid for her hotel and treated her like a package; four days later a kinder conductor brought her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s, where her cousins received her with astonishment.

She arrived with a small bundle, dressed in Breton attire. Sylvie and Rogron were startled and questioned her, but Pierrette, shy and overwhelmed, spoke little. Sylvie and the maid Adèle showed her a modest room that seemed luxurious to Pierrette, and Sylvie immediately fretted over her lack of clothing, resolving to buy her a new wardrobe.

In Brittany Pierrette had known affection and freedom; with the Rogrons she found coldness and criticism. Exploring the house and garden the next morning, she was reproved for dirty shoes. Her cousins’ concern for possessions over her well‑being and their constant fault‑finding sharply contrasted with the love she’d known. Pierrette’s innocence made their disdain more painful; she began to suppress her joy and became passive for fear of scorn.

The Rogrons obsessed over her appearance, treating her as a burden and stifling her spirit. When she tore a dress while playing, she was devastated, expecting stern rebukes. Life in Provins worsened as Sylvie forbade visits to friends and pursued social ambitions encouraged by Vinet and the colonel. As her cousins plotted for status, Pierrette was increasingly confined—transformed from a lively child into a shadow of her former self, enduring a harsh, self‑interested care that was not love.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At the start of a new phase with her wealthy cousins, Pierrette went to say goodnight and Sylvie turned away coldly, making her cry. When asked why, Pierrette said her rich cousin was not as kind as her poor grandmother; Sylvie retorted the grandmother had taken money while the cousin would leave her some—Pierrette wanted affection, not wealth.

Sylvie then accused Pierrette of mischief—breaking a watch, disobeying orders—and Pierrette left ashamed. Rogron demanded punishment, Madame Vinet suggested sending her to school, and the colonel thought instruction might keep her quiet. Vinet called Pierrette a rough diamond needing polishing and a writing master was hired. His lessons produced ink stains and scattered papers, blamed on Pierrette, and she was sharply told she must earn her keep. Rogron’s severity and Sylvie’s indifference deepened her isolation; her curiosity and spirit were stifled and she began to feel ill, complaining of pains the cousins dismissed.

Only Adèle, the servant, showed kindness, secretly warming Pierrette’s bed. Sylvie believed in hardening children; the colonel and Vinet attributed Pierrette’s behavior to a stubborn Breton nature. Meanwhile Vinet and Rogron invested in a local newspaper to boost their standing, while Pierrette’s situation worsened.

She considered escaping to her grandparents in Brittany, but two events prevented it: a death and Rogron’s appointment as her guardian. Vinet hinted at an inheritance; Rogron secured from her grandmother a promise of eight thousand francs upon the old woman’s death. Preparations for Pierrette’s first communion shifted household dynamics. Sylvie insisted on the zealous priest Monsieur Habert, who profoundly influenced Pierrette. Finding solace in faith, she abandoned escape plans and embraced the Church’s teachings, a change that even made Sylvie adopt new piety.

Monsieur Habert’s sister offered to take Pierrette into her school, but the Rogrons refused, fearing a convent education. Later, Vinet introduced the impoverished but well-connected Madame and Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf. Bathilde de Chargeboeuf attracted Rogron; Sylvie saw Pierrette as an obstacle and forced her to learn domestic tasks, effectively turning her into a servant. Without Adèle’s protection, she was treated worse than staff—scolded for trifles, denied praise—and over the years her health and spirit faded until the once-vibrant child became a silent shadow while her cousins remained oblivious.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut's arrival would incite in the Rogron family, it is necessary to explain how he came to Provins, since he plays a significant yet passive role in the events.

Brigaut fled the Rogron house, shaken by Pierrette’s distressing change. He scarcely recognized her; her once-vibrant spirit had given way to illness and sorrow. From a high point in the Upper town he looked mournfully at her home, consumed by questions about her well-being and the depth of his feelings. Their rare, deep bond led him to try to improve his prospects for her: he went to Paris to learn a trade, but after a fortnight returned to Provins and soon found work as a journeyman cabinetmaker so he could stay near Pierrette.

He buried his feelings while trying to learn more about the Rogrons. Madame Frappier revealed their dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. He often saw Pierrette at the market, where her frailty distressed him. Sundays at church brought them close, though she signaled him to remain hidden, sensing danger. Determined to discover the cause of her pallor, he feared for her health.

Pierrette, meanwhile, fretted about the changes since Brigaut’s arrival but was unaware of the Rogrons’ internal strife, especially between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste), both angling for marriage. Sylvie, forty-two, feared growing old alone; Céleste, ten years younger and encouraged by her brother the priest, actively sought a husband. A recent political shift emboldened local Liberals and intensified competition for social standing in Provins.

Sylvie’s anxiety drove her to consult a doctor about marrying at her age; his discouraging prognosis heightened her fear. Céleste, seeing an opening, urged delay to remove a rival. Sylvie’s unease turned to jealousy when she noticed the colonel’s increasing attention to Pierrette. Vinet, a lawyer with designs on Bathilde, exploited Sylvie’s vulnerability: he plotted to separate the colonel from Pierrette and steer the Rogron fortune toward Bathilde.

Vinet persuaded the colonel to court Pierrette, painting a picture of happiness while secretly maneuvering to benefit Bathilde. The colonel, a mix of affection and reserve, began seeking Pierrette out more, which inflamed Sylvie’s fears. Her suspicions sharpened after overhearing Brigaut serenade Pierrette; she concluded the colonel was involved. On a tense morning she accused Pierrette of harboring a secret admirer, mocked her innocence, and demanded answers. Pierrette, frightened and bewildered, stayed silent, intensifying Sylvie’s wrath, who then threatened severe consequences if Pierrette did not confess.

As Pierrette endured the oppressive Rogron household, her health continued to decline. Her innocence sheltered her from some realities, but emotional strain and heavy labor eroded her strength. Brigaut’s presence offered brief comfort, yet Sylvie’s relentless scrutiny deepened Pierrette’s isolation. Amid this turmoil, love, jealousy, and ambition became entangled, setting the stage for a tragic sequence that would affect everyone involved.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the Constitutionnel to Gouraud, who now came himself and lingered to gossip and walk with Sylvie. Eager to see the colonel, Sylvie dressed in her best. When Gouraud arrived, Rogron excused himself, leaving Sylvie alone with him.

Noting her solemnity, Gouraud asked about her cousin; she said the cousin was unwell. The colonel grew uneasy: Vinet’s rising influence over the Rogrons made him feel sidelined. A half-pay officer, he feared Vinet’s control of their political plans and Rogron’s growing infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, which threatened his prospects. He even suspected Vinet might be manoeuvring to push him toward Pierrette as a ploy to advantage Vinet and Bathilde.

Alone with Sylvie he sensed her unease; when she tentatively mentioned Pierrette he dismissed any affection for the girl, saying he preferred a more mature partner. Relieved, Sylvie concealed her intentions and hinted her brother had once considered Pierrette a match. Sensing her interest, Gouraud declared he would marry Sylvie, delighting Rogron. Sylvie began planning their home but remained wary of Pierrette’s interactions with the colonel.

As dinner neared, Sylvie scolded Pierrette for a stain yet ordered her to dress nicely—suspecting collusion with Gouraud. The Chargeboeuf ladies arrived; Bathilde was spectacularly dressed. Under Vinet’s guidance she treated marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone to power, using beauty and connections for independence.

The evening grew tense: Bathilde’s condescension and Vinet’s remarks sharpened rivalries. At cards Sylvie tried to manipulate the game; Pierrette’s innocent comments provoked her, and Pierrette left in tears. That night Sylvie felt betrayed, brooding on men’s duplicity and her precarious position. The next day Pierrette suffered a painful fall; Sylvie blamed disobedience and showed no sympathy, revealing a widening rift between the cousins.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The week ended in relentless torment for Pierrette under Sylvie’s escalating cruelty. She concealed Brigaut’s arrival to avoid further anger, yet his memory sustained her; she longed for him and feared for his safety. Determined to communicate, Brigaut arranged to exchange letters: one morning he slipped a note to her while pretending to buy fruit and asked her to lower a string from her window so they could pass notes nightly.

Pierrette returned bearing his heartfelt letter, full of longing and a wish to escape their oppressive circumstances. Despite the difficulty of obtaining writing materials without waking Sylvie, she resolved to reply, pouring out her love and describing the suffering inflicted by her relatives. At midnight she sent the letter down; their correspondence continued, full of hope and plans, even as Sylvie’s latent jealousy grew.

Noticing Pierrette’s newfound joy, Sylvie became more watchful. Under her scrutiny Pierrette’s health declined; one evening she fainted, drawing concern from the colonel and others, though Sylvie dismissed the illness as a ruse. Desperate, Pierrette cried for help, provoking a violent confrontation when Sylvie tried to seize her letters. In the struggle Pierrette sustained injuries, including a hurt hand, and was left distressed.

At the height of Sylvie’s fury Madame Lorrain, Pierrette’s grandmother, arrived after receiving Brigaut’s urgent appeals to come to Provins. She witnessed the violence, rushed to Pierrette’s aid, and sought medical care. As Pierrette recovered, her bond with Madame Lorrain deepened; the promise of protection and a future with her grandmother and Brigaut offered a hopeful glimmer. The struggle for freedom and love continued, with Pierrette determined to reclaim her life.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At nine o’clock Monsieur Martener told Monsieur Tiphaine about Pierrette’s mistreatment by the Rogrons. Tiphaine brought in Auffray, a notary and relative, and long‑standing enmities between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions—exacerbated by damaging truths about Madame Tiphaine’s family—gave the dispute political force.

Seeing a chance to weaken the Rogrons, influential royalist opponents, Tiphaine consulted the public prosecutor. He, Auffray, and Martener arranged to petition the courts to make Auffray Pierrette’s guardian so a Family Council, backed by medical testimony, could remove her from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday the town buzzed: neighbors had heard Pierrette’s cries and rumors spread. Doctors Bianchon and Brigaut examined her and confirmed abuse. Vinet, the Rogrons’ lawyer, warned of criminal exposure; Sylvie, initially evasive, later admitted jealousy and the truth. Exploiting their fear, Vinet proposed a marriage announcement to quiet the scandal. Under pressure the Rogrons signed a marriage contract that served their political interests. Vinet then filed a petition accusing Brigaut and the widow Lorrain of abducting Pierrette, attempting to recast his clients as victims.

The hearing was postponed, heightening public interest. The Tiphaines rallied support for Pierrette, who, unaware of the turmoil, was cared for by her grandmother and Brigaut. The doctors, appalled by her condition, unanimously condemned the Rogrons’ treatment.

When the court reconvened, Auffray presented the physicians’ report to justify protecting Pierrette. Vinet countered, but the judge ordered that she be kept from the Rogrons until the Family Council could meet—a notable public rebuke. The Council of relatives and allies voted to remove Rogron as guardian and appoint Auffray. Despite Vinet’s defense, the evidence led to a civil suit for neglect and abuse.

As the legal battle continued, Pierrette’s health declined. Martener and the Auffray family grew fond of her; her grandmother’s care and Brigaut’s visits offered comfort. A critical operation in March 1828 offered little hope; she forgave her cousins and soon died. Her funeral filled the town; the Rogrons, fearing outrage, stayed away. Brigaut, grief‑stricken, made her coffin. Though community support hindered the Rogrons’ maneuvers and Vinet suffered backlash, the Rogrons and Vinet largely retained political standing; only a few—Martener, Brigaut, and like‑minded friends—remembered the reality of Pierrette’s suffering. The account closes on justice, accountability, and the lasting impact of her life and death.
