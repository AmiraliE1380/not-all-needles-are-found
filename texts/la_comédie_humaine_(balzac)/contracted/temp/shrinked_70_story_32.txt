**Dedication**  
This book explores the hidden injustices of domestic life, highlighting the decline of paternal authority and feminine weakness, and calls for a return to moral foundations in marriage and family.

---

**Chapter I**  
In 1792, Dr. Rouget was seen as malicious by the townspeople of Issoudun, making his wife, Madame Rouget, unhappy. Their son Jean-Jacques and daughter Agathe faced strained family dynamics. The wealthy Descoings family, linked to the Rougets, suffered during the Revolution. After Madame Rouget's death in 1799, Dr. Rouget led a debauched life, leaving Jean-Jacques unprepared for adulthood. Agathe, devoted yet naive, lost her inheritance and remained estranged from her family. Madame Descoings struggled with gambling, prompting Agathe to sell possessions to pay debts. The chapter illustrates familial relationships, financial struggles, and the complexities of love and loyalty, as Madame Bridau lives modestly, burdened by grief and financial worries for her children, Philippe and Joseph.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In 1816, Joseph Bridau established an art studio with support from his mother, Agathe, and Madame Descoings. While Joseph studied under Gros and Schinner, political unrest in France affected his brother Philippe, who was arrested for his involvement in riots. Concerned for his safety, Agathe encouraged Philippe to go abroad, leading him to join General Lallemand in the U.S. However, after a failed venture, Philippe returned in 1819, struggling with alcoholism.

Agathe tried to help Philippe by buying him clothes and indulging his drinking, despite her concerns. Philippe's gambling and reckless behavior worsened their financial situation. He pursued Mariette, a dancer, while attempting to leverage his past for personal gain. Despite securing a cashier position at a newspaper, he lived extravagantly without supporting his family.

Philippe's infatuation with Mariette shocked Agathe and Madame Descoings, especially when she rejected his marriage proposal. After Mariette left for London, Philippe faced despair over his finances and confided in Joseph about suicidal thoughts. He continued gambling, losing everything, and resorted to stealing from his family.

As Agathe's health declined from worry, Philippe's lifestyle remained reckless. On Christmas Eve, he stole from Madame Descoings, leading to her stroke after discovering the theft. Following her death, Philippe became estranged from his family. By February 1822, Agathe found work in a lottery office, while Joseph grew concerned about Philippe's behavior. When Philippe stole a painting from Joseph, Agathe, despite knowing he would squander any money given, continued to support him. News of Philippe's involvement in a conspiracy caused Agathe to faint, prompting Joseph to seek legal advice to protect their family's interests.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness pranked Pere Fario, eager to see his reaction. Max, concerned about the arrival of the Bridaus threatening his interests in Jean-Jacques Rouget's property linked to Flore Brazier, initially dismissed the warning but grew uneasy about potential heirs disrupting his plans. He sought the Knights' help to deal with the Bridaus while maintaining his relationship with Flore.

Flore, taken in by Rouget as a child, faced a lack of inheritance despite Rouget's affection. As tensions rose with the Bridaus' arrival, Max manipulated the situation, advising Rouget to welcome them. Joseph and Agathe arrived, with Joseph noting Flore's beauty despite her family's troubles. At dinner, Flore encouraged Rouget to pay Joseph for paintings, enhancing her status. Meanwhile, Max and Flore plotted to secure Rouget's wealth, leaving the Bridaus' future uncertain amid growing tensions.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Between midnight and one o’clock, the Knights of Idleness distributed food to the town's dogs, concluding their escapade around three in the morning. Max was attacked by Fario, who stabbed him before fleeing. Max cried out, prompting Lousteau-Prangin and Goddet to rush to his aid, but the townspeople, accustomed to false alarms, remained indifferent. After fainting, Max claimed to recognize Fario, leading Lousteau-Prangin to inform his father, the judge.

Max was taken home, where Goddet found the knife wound serious but not fatal. Seizing the moment, Max plotted to frame Joseph Bridau, the painter, for the attack. He instructed Flore to spread rumors of his impending death, hoping this would lead to Joseph's arrest and prompt his mother to flee to Paris.

Flore, feigning tears, accused Joseph of wanting Max dead to inherit from his uncle. The townspeople began searching for Joseph, who had left Hochon’s house at dawn. Alarmed by the crowd's anger, Hochon bolted his door. Meanwhile, Agathe, Joseph's mother, fainted upon hearing the news.

As the crowd grew, the police struggled to maintain order. Gendarmes arrived just in time to protect Joseph from the mob, who were convinced of his guilt. Despite his protests of innocence, Joseph was taken to the Palais for his safety. He recounted his whereabouts that morning, providing an alibi that could be verified by witnesses.

Initially skeptical, the magistrates agreed to investigate further but decided to detain Joseph for protection. He wrote a reassuring letter to his mother. Meanwhile, Max plotted to keep Joseph imprisoned, believing it would rid them of the Parisians.

As the investigation progressed, Max's deception unraveled. Witnesses confirmed Joseph's alibi, and Max's own letter to the judge further implicated him. Eventually, Joseph was released, warmly greeted by his relieved mother.

In the aftermath, the townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris, where Joseph's spirits lifted despite his brother Philippe's ongoing trial. Desroches, a lawyer, sought to help Philippe by arranging for his release and advising caution with Max.

On November 2nd, Philippe returned to Issoudun, stirring excitement as the brother of a wrongfully accused painter. Gilet, now recovered, had converted Pere Rouget’s mortgages into cash, raising concerns. Philippe visited Rouget, refusing financial help due to past grievances, while Gilet and Flore plotted to manipulate Rouget for wealth.

Philippe ingratiated himself with local officers, earning respect while plotting against Gilet. He discovered Gilet's associates were involved in dubious activities and reported this to Hochon. Philippe confronted Rouget about Flore's influence, insisting on a private discussion.

Tensions escalated as Philippe planned to protect his family's interests. Gilet, feeling cornered, resolved to confront Philippe. Meanwhile, rumors of Gilet's misdeeds spread, turning the townspeople against him. Philippe strategized to ensure Rouget's happiness and Flore's compliance, setting the stage for a dramatic confrontation between him and Gilet.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The day after the coronation banquet, tensions rose at Lacroix's restaurant when Max Gilet confronted Colonel Philippe Bridau, resulting in a duel that left both injured and Max dead. Philippe then plotted to marry Max's former lover, Flore Brazier, for her dowry. After marrying her, Philippe inherited 1.6 million francs upon Rouget's death and became Comte de Brambourg. By 1827, he distanced himself from his past, while Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist. Following Agathe's death, Joseph learned of Philippe's indifference and faced scrutiny during the July Revolution of 1830, ultimately dying in battle in 1839. Joseph inherited Philippe's estate, becoming Comte de Brambourg, an ironic title reflecting his brother's legacy.
