In the Rue Saint‑Denis an old house symbolized historic Paris, admired by a cloaked young man on a rainy March morning. He noted its sixteenth‑century details and the whimsical shop "Cat and Racket" below, bearing the faded inscription “Guillaume, successor to Master Chevrel.” His reverie was broken when apprentices doused him with shaving cream. A beautiful girl appeared at a window but withdrew, leaving him as if a star had vanished. Shopkeeper Monsieur Guillaume, known for integrity, opened his well‑stocked shop despite warnings about his notary's discounts, blending beauty, commerce, and neighborhood life.

Guillaume, in old‑fashioned dress with gray hair and a weathered face, embodied patience and shrewdness; he lamented lost local customs and imposed stern discipline on apprentices. Though from comfortable families, they labored under his authority, which stressed hard work and moral rectitude. The shop was immaculate; apprentices paid for board but ate apart from the family.

The eldest apprentice, Joseph Lebas, hoped to marry Guillaume's elder daughter, Virginie, but secretly loved the younger, Augustine—exposing the household’s rigid social and legal order. Virginie was patient and gentle; eighteen‑year‑old Augustine, craving excitement, provoked secret romances that stirred Lebas and worried Guillaume about suitable matches.

Months earlier a young painter had fallen for Augustine and spent months painting her. When the portrait was shown at the Louvre it caused a sensation; Augustine, unaware of her role, was struck by the likeness and felt love awaken amid their constrained lives.

The morning after a ball, painter Théodore de Sommervieux eagerly awaited a meeting with Augustine, who was closely watched by her mother, Madame Guillaume, and Mademoiselle Virginie. Frustrated by rivals and strict supervision, Théodore bribed a maid for secret exchanges, leading to clandestine meetings during Mass at Saint-Leu.

During the household stock-taking Augustine found brief respite from scrutiny. After weeks of work, the family celebrated the inventory's end. Monsieur Guillaume offered his assistant, Joseph Lebas, a partnership in recognition of his loyalty. Joseph, who loved Augustine, learned of her feelings for Théodore, provoking a tense confrontation with Guillaume, who insisted Augustine could not marry before Virginie.

As tensions rose, Augustine boldly declared her love for Théodore, saying marrying anyone else would make her miserable. Madame Guillaume voiced disdain for artists, but Guillaume softened when he heard of Théodore's respectable background. Madame Roguin, a family friend, then praised Théodore’s recent honors, shifting opinions and arousing both Guillaume and Madame Guillaume’s interest. Ultimately Guillaume consented to the marriage, with conditions to secure Augustine’s fortune.

Months later, at the high altar of Saint-Leu, two weddings took place: Augustine and Théodore, radiant, and Virginie, dressed simply. Despite Monsieur Guillaume’s wish for Virginie to marry first, the priests favored Augustine. After a grand celebration the newlyweds settled into their home, and Théodore’s passion made their first year blissful.

As time passed, Théodore felt compelled to return to work, especially with Augustine expecting their first child. He resumed socializing, particularly at the Duchesse de Carigliano’s gatherings. Augustine enjoyed society but felt inadequate beside her husband. Théodore’s artistic temperament craved deeper intellectual connection, which Augustine struggled to provide, leading to mutual dissatisfaction.

Three years into their marriage, Augustine grew consumed by grief; her efforts to rekindle Théodore’s affection went unrecognized. Feeling isolated, she turned to family, confiding in her sister Virginie and brother-in-law Joseph Lebas. Despite their counsel, Augustine remained conflicted about the failing relationship.

Desperate, Augustine sought guidance from the Duchesse de Carigliano, believing the duchess could help her regain Théodore’s heart. Overwhelmed by the duchess’s luxuries, Augustine contemplated manipulating her husband. She returned home hopeful but faced a tumultuous confrontation: Théodore destroyed a portrait of her, a violent symbol of their unraveling.

The next morning Madame Guillaume found Augustine in despair amid the shattered portrait. Her emotional collapse reflected the toll their marriage had taken, and she ultimately succumbed to the pressures of a tumultuous life. Her epitaph stands as a poignant reminder of those who love deeply but cannot endure the complexities of passion and genius.
