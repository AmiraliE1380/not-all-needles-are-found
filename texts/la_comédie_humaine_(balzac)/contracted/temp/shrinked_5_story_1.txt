Halfway down Rue Saint-Denis, near Rue du Petit-Lion, a weathered house helped historians reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls revealed timber patterns and cracked plaster; a warped triangular roof overhung the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer. The upper story’s overlapping planks lightened the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man, cloaked like an antiquary, stood across from the house studying its peculiarities. He ignored the narrow boarded first-floor windows, inspected the third floor’s modest sash windows—crudely made and clouded with green-tinted glass—and glanced at the shop below, where small comic details amused him.

A large timber, supported by four pillars, bore many paint layers and a whimsical painting of a cat with an oversized racket and an absurd ball. A faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a remnant of the shop’s old charm.

Dressed in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, he looked fresh from a wedding or ball; white gloves and disheveled hair hinted at recent revelry. Market gardeners hurried by, but the street was unusually still. His striking features—pale face, dazzling cravat, expressive eyes—drew shopkeepers’ attention.

Three apprentices then appeared at the dormer, laughing as they sprinkled him with shaving cream, which he brushed off indifferently. A young girl’s face, recalling a Raphael Virgin, briefly appeared and fled as the shutter slammed, leaving him disappointed.

At that moment the “Cat and Racket” shutters opened and a manservant hung the cloth sign. Behind iron bars the shop revealed many packages wrapped in brown linen. Though old-fashioned, Monsieur Guillaume was reputed Paris’s best-stocked cloth merchant: honest and profitable without flattery, while fellow traders grumbled about his notary’s heavy discounts.

The old merchant stood at his shop threshold on the Rue Saint-Denis, awakening from a long sleep. Monsieur Guillaume, in traditional dress, watched a stranger loitering by the shop. His shrewd, weathered face marked him a staunch traditionalist who lamented lost customs and imposed a strict routine on his three fearful apprentices.

At work Guillaume noticed the stranger’s anxious glances and suspected ulterior motives. The eldest apprentice, seeing Mademoiselle Augustine hasten away, hailed a cab and thus averted a prank aimed at her.

He reproved the youths for idleness, recalling his own youthful industry. Though the apprentices came from comfortable homes and paid board, he demanded rigorous labor, unlike newer, softer shops. The shop was meticulous; Madame Guillaume’s frugality even excluded apprentices from the family dessert.

They kept strict routine—attending Mass together—and Joseph Lebas, the eldest apprentice, after years of service earned a modest wage. Guillaume considered Joseph a possible husband for his elder daughter Virginie, though Joseph secretly loved the younger Augustine. Household life remained traditional. Virginie was devout and severe; Augustine, eighteen, was delicate and charming but smothered by maternal expectations. Augustine’s appetite for passion, fed by romantic novels, deepened Joseph’s affection, and Virginie, lacking suitors, felt drawn to him too. Amid the shop’s monotony quiet feelings grew, and Guillaume worried about his daughters’ prospects in changing times.

From that day the old merchant, grieving his eldest daughter’s decline, remembered marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like those of Joseph Lebas and Virginie. Marrying his daughter would repay a sacred debt to an orphan benefactor. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, aware of a fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder sister marry first, suffered in silence. Warm-hearted and reserved, he kept his love hidden.

Months earlier a young painter paused outside the dark Cat and Racket, transfixed by a scene any artist would envy. The cave-like shop opened onto a dining room lit by a hanging lamp that recalled Dutch interiors. The family—Augustine among them, with a chubby maid—formed a modest, peaceful tableau. Fresh from Rome and weary of grand art, the painter craved real life. Augustine, pensive and hardly eating, seemed almost angelic in the lamp’s glow. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

Determined to capture it, he returned to the Cat and Racket, even in disguise, to study Augustine. For eight months he lived in love and art, neglecting friends and routine. One morning his friend Girodet broke his isolation to ask about his Salon submission. The artist unveiled two masterpieces; Girodet, sensing deep feeling, embraced him and said love had made the work great. Despite warnings that the public might not understand them, the paintings were exhibited and caused a sensation. The Interior launched a new genre; the portrait was unforgettable, winning acclaim and offers the artist refused.

These successes contrasted with Augustine’s monastic life in Rue Saint-Denis, until Madame Guillaume mentioned the exhibition; with her cousin’s help Augustine obtained permission to see the Louvre. Trembling before the portrait she recognized herself, feeling terror and excitement. The artist, nearby, whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Fleeing the crowd Augustine tried to leave but was drawn back to the celebrated painting. Overwhelmed, she wept and exchanged a quiet nod with the artist. That brief contact ignited guilt and exhilaration; the Salon’s chaos and the painter’s gaze revealed unfamiliar feelings. She feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition, dreading her mother’s planned visit. That night she felt a void and longed for the elegance she had admired; the idea of being a genius’s wife filled her with hope. The next day her parents returned disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. Yet the paintings’ disappearance had awakened in Augustine a woman’s instinctive sensitivity.

The morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had captured Augustine's heart, found it hard to declare his love because her household constrained them. Augustine sat in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume under her mother's watch, while Theodore, imagining rivals among the assistants, felt hemmed in. He bribed a maid to arrange discreet exchanges and they agreed to meet during Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family focused on stock-taking under Monsieur Guillaume’s supervision, tallying inventory and balancing accounts, indifferent to Augustine’s longing. When the work ended they relaxed: attended a performance of Cendrillon and the apprentices enjoyed a boisterous night out.

The next morning Guillaume resumed routine and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to review the profitable results, offering to make him a partner in recognition of his work. Overwhelmed, Joseph blurted that he loved Augustine; Guillaume at first misheard and thought he meant Virginie. Once clarified, Guillaume agreed to consider Joseph’s feelings and suggested he accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie exchanged glances at a nervous Joseph. Madame later chastised Augustine for inattentiveness in church and sent her to her room, prompting a heated discussion about their daughter’s future. Augustine confessed her love for Theodore, insisting on his respectable background. Monsieur, wary of artists, protested until Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore’s honors and fortune and advocated the match. Her report swayed the parents. By evening Guillaume informed Joseph, the family celebrated, Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket, and, though cautious about money, Monsieur consented: the lovers and household embraced a joyful future.

A few months after that memorable Sunday, Saint-Leu’s high altar witnessed two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore shone with happiness while Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to have Virginie married first, but the priests attended the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s sensible match and envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned such extravagance would bring ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt vindicated in his cautious approach to settlements. After a splendid ball and supper, the Guillaumes returned home and the Lebas family resumed their old residence. The ecstatic artist carried Augustine to their richly adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful: Theodore lavished affection, and Augustine, lost in love, believed her devotion would always enchant him. Naïve at times, she remained happily unaware of much around her. As pregnancy approached, Theodore felt the pull of work. He reconnected with friends and began frequenting the Duchesse de Carigliano’s salons, enjoying the social proof of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine liked society but soon felt out of place among Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was sincere but superficial, and an intellectual disconnect grew between them. Theodore hoped studio life would draw her in, but she could not adapt; at gatherings her innocence clashed with the freer manners of artists and her discomfort showed.

Gradually Theodore’s warmth cooled and Augustine keenly felt his indifference. Despite trying to fulfill her duties, she could not bridge the widening gap. Three years into marriage, the once radiant Augustine sank into sorrow as Theodore’s involvement with the Duchesse intensified her fear of being supplanted. Determined, she tried to educate herself in poetry and art to reconnect, but her efforts faltered and only highlighted her inadequacy. Seeking solace, she returned briefly to her childhood home, where Virginie and Joseph Lebas ran their drapery with steady, ordinary happiness. Joseph’s practical, commercial view offered no comfort. Augustine ultimately returned to her husband’s home, burdened by uncertainty and longing, trying every remedy to heal a fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter warmly; her visit broke their years of monotonous reminiscence about past business successes and failures—most notably the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume habitually checked his shop, the Cat and Racket, and chatted with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume—appointed to the Army’s clothing consulting board through her son-in-law’s influence—filled their home with extravagant, tasteless decor. Their nostalgic stagnation struck Augustine as a sharp contrast to her own turbulent life, leaving her both proud and appalled.

Despite her turmoil, Augustine displayed her achievements and aired marital grievances, which her parents received with keen interest. Madame Guillaume was especially curious about Augustine’s husband, Theodore, a painter. When Augustine described his late nights and eccentric habits, her mother disapproved of his lifestyle and profession. Augustine defended him, and the family’s incredulity grew into a debate about genius versus marital responsibility.

At the mention of divorce her parents were shocked; Monsieur Guillaume offered practical support. Overwhelmed by their concern and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine recoiled from separation, realizing she was isolated in a way only those who had endured similar trials could understand.

Determined to change course, Augustine sought the Duchess de Carigliano—not to force Theodore back but to learn how to captivate him. Approaching the Duchess’s opulent home, she felt envy and sensed rivalry. The Duchess listened sympathetically and counseled shrewdly: with men of genius, one must project strength and self-control, cultivate mystery, and never reveal the whole measure of one’s affection. Though uneasy about such manipulation, Augustine began to accept these lessons, convinced her love required a new approach.

The Duchess offered a portrait of Augustine painted by Theodore to rekindle his interest. Hopeful, Augustine returned determined to embody the image. Theodore’s fury and jealousy erupted into a violent quarrel that shattered her hopes. The next morning her mother found Augustine in despair among the wreckage; overwhelmed by her tumultuous life, she died young. Her tragic end remains a poignant reminder of the struggle women face pursuing love and fulfillment amid the complexities of artistic genius; the inscription on her grave reflects this sorrow.
