DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller epitomizes a modern shift from mere material commerce to intellectual influence: he links provincial isolation to metropolitan ideas, equalizing products and importing Parisian innovations by persuasion. Self-educated, a mimic and rapid judge of character and solvency, he moves between village and capital with a surface knowledge of the world. One such man is Gaudissart—an acrobat of commerce in an olive-green overcoat and striped shirt—who made his fortune selling hats by charm and rapid talk. Jovial and adaptable, he read audiences and opportunities, sometimes out-trumping diplomats abroad.

After 1830, when ideas became commodities and Saint-Simonian thought and speculation spread, Gaudissart switched from hats to selling life insurance and periodicals. Quick to seize commissions, newspapers sought him to sell subscriptions; his employers applauded his skill at transforming chatter into profit.

CHAPTER II

Preparing to travel in mid‑April after a week immersed in Saint‑Simonism and finance, Gaudissart resisted tempting hat commissions and set off, joking with Jenny the florist about furnishing her room with his gains. Bragging of his expected earnings from insurance—especially the lucrative "Children’s Journal"—he stopped in Blois, then continued to his assigned towns.

CHAPTER III

In Vouvray he ran into provincial resistance. The Tourangians were witty, satirical, and hostile to Saint‑Simonism. At the Soleil d’Or inn, Monsieur Vernier, aware of Gaudissart’s fame, resolved to humble him and sent him to Margaritis, a reputed lunatic.

CHAPTER IV

Margaritis, obsessed with wine and newspapers, rambled incoherently. Despite Gaudissart’s efforts to explain life insurance, the conversation devolved into absurdity. Two women listened amused while Vernier watched, intent on exposing Gaudissart’s vanity; mockery and unpredictability made any sale impossible.

CHAPTER V

When the innkeeper revealed the setup, Gaudissart, insulted, demanded satisfaction. A duel loomed, then—after townsfolk and the innkeeper intervened over breakfast—the quarrel was patched up. Gaudissart left Vouvray mortified but wiser: commerce needs not only eloquence but the discernment to recognize when persuasion is futile, and he resolved to return to Paris and his usual work.
