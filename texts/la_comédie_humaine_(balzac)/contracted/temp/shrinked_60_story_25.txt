The story opens in a lawyer's office where clerk Simonnin mocks Colonel Chabert, who seeks help from attorney M. Derville. Chabert, believed dead after the Battle of Eylau, reveals his identity and plight, highlighting societal disdain for fallen figures in post-war France. Desperate to reclaim his status, he shares his story of survival and loss, as his wife, Countess Ferraud, has remarried and taken his estate. 

Derville agrees to help, but learns Chabert lives in poor conditions with dairyman Vergniaud. After meeting Madame Ferraud, who initially dismisses Chabert, Derville proposes a compromise. Tensions rise when the Countess feigns ignorance about Chabert's survival. 

Later, she reveals her love for another man and suggests institutionalizing Chabert, prompting him to reject her. Chabert's life becomes one of poverty, and Derville, unaware of his fate, assumes a resolution was reached. Ultimately, Chabert is found imprisoned as a vagabond, stripped of dignity yet maintaining pride, reflecting on the tragic consequences of their lives.
