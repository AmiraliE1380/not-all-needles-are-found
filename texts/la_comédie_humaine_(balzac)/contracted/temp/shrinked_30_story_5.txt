Some stories require a delicate, almost anatomical touch—portraits need a soul; certain truths emerge only through subtle harmonies of time, circumstance, or conscience.

That sensitivity is demanded here. This simple tale addresses grave, reflective readers who live on tender emotion. If the writer, like a surgeon at a dying friend’s side, reveres the subject, the reader should feel it. It speaks to those who have known private grief: the luxury of tears, the pangs of memory, regret, and smiles at past joys. Others will set the book aside; those who have never buried a beloved will not grasp these pages. The writer insists the tale is true and worth the reader’s feeling.

In contemporary France, speech varies by speaker: Parisians describe the same person in amusingly different ways. Madame Firmiani is sketched by many voices—a Practical man lists facts (house on the rue du Bac, well-furnished salons, an income of a hundred thousand francs, a husband once receiver-general); a Lounger praises her social standing and tea; an Egotist dismisses salons; a Student finds her enchanting; a Fop laments faded beauty yet admits charm. A Connoisseur praises her paintings; a Spiteful woman calls her a siren; an Attaché and old ladies recall ties to former beauty and the Cadignans. A Ninny wrongly says she sang at the Opera; jokes claim her salon is safe because its scoundrels are high-quality. Others allege affairs—Murat’s mistress—or coquetry that ruined a young man’s prospects.

Madame Firmiani thus appears through many lenses, each reflecting the observer as much as the observed and prompting reflection on deeper emotional truths.

In January 1824 opinions about Madame Firmiani were so contradictory—widow or wife, wise or foolish, virtuous or otherwise, rich or poor, beautiful or plain—that no clear portrait emerged; society warped her reputation like lithographic blocks producing different images from slight changes.

That winter Monsieur de Bourbonne, an elderly magnate from Touraine who adored his nephew Octave de Camps, grew uneasy. After Octave sold an estate without consulting him and a relative claimed Octave had squandered his fortune on Madame Firmiani and been reduced to teaching mathematics, the old man refused to believe the worst and went to Paris to investigate.

Adopting the name de Rouxellay and using faubourg Saint‑Germain connections, he arranged to meet Madame Firmiani while Octave was absent and soon confirmed Octave’s distress. De Rouxellay—no naive countryman, once in the King’s guard and versed in society—entered her salon with the Marquise de Listomère, professed pity, yet found himself captivated, revising his judgment.

Madame Firmiani, said to be twenty‑five though nearer twenty‑eight and married at sixteen, combined beauty, wealth, musical talent, intelligence, and wit, admired even by rivals and thus the target of persistent malicious gossip.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The former guardsman lingered after the guests left; Madame Firmiani found him still seated when the clock struck two. He announced himself as Monsieur Octave de Camps’s uncle and bluntly relayed rumors that his nephew had ruined himself for her while she enjoyed wealth. She sat again, moved, and answered calmly that she scorned money, had married without fortune, and welcomed Octave as a friend only if she esteemed him. She refused to be drawn into gossip, claiming a guardian angel kept her from dishonesty.

The old man, trembling, pleaded like a parent and asked one question only: did she love Octave? She would not confess to a third party: to any other man she would have answered with a look, but to him she asked what a woman who answered such a question would be thought of. “To confess love is the lover’s reward,” she said, and left the sentence unfinished, withdrew to her rooms, and declined to speak further. Monsieur de Bourbonne left puzzled, unsure whether she was sly or angelic.

Next morning he found Octave in his garret and scolded him for squandering the advantages of an uncle who meant to leave him an estate. Octave admitted he had “ruined” himself for Madame Firmiani and produced a letter she had given him. It began, in effect, “My beloved,” and explained why she was sad. She argued that a wife should be gay and confiding, and that a woman’s glory is in the man she loves; yet she had been humiliated on learning of Octave’s family. Knowing his father had unjustly deprived others of property made it impossible for her to respect him while he kept such gains. She urged him to consult his conscience: “If you come to me poor, my love shall be redoubled. If not, renounce it. I am your wife, not your mistress.” She did not command restitution; she appealed to his honor and begged him to perceive his faults himself.

Moved to tears, Octave implied more private confidences. The uncle, surprised by the gravity of the letter and that they considered themselves married, asked whether Octave had gone into teaching and whether he still had the uncle’s capital. Octave explained that the letter had sparked a revolution: conscience made every pleasure feel like theft. He had rushed to Madame Firmiani; together they calculated what ought to be paid to the Bourgneufs—the family his father had wronged. With limited means he condemned himself to pay three percent interest and to make restitution as far as he could. Madame Firmiani, prudent from past insecurity, offered her savings and he accepted them.

“Does that adorable woman actually save money?” the uncle cried, half mocking, half admiring. Octave asked him not to laugh. He then told the old man about Madame Firmiani’s past: her husband had gone to Greece in 1820 and died three years later; proof of his death and his will had been lost or stolen during the troubles there, so she had lived frugally to protect herself against possible loss of fortune. Octave had been trying to acquire property of his own—taking pupils rather than relying on others—to provide for her.

The act of restitution, he said, had brought him joys beyond millions. Finding the Bourgneufs in misery—an old agent father, daughters keeping books and house, a sick mother—he felt like Providence in relieving them. Making amends gave him a purity of feeling he had thought he lacked; he called Madame Firmiani “my dear conscience” and hoped to earn an honest fortune by industry. Touched, the old man exclaimed, “Ah, my boy, you have your mother’s soul.”

At that moment a carriage sounded and Madame Firmiani returned, a little annoyed to find Monsieur de Bourbonne still there yet smiling. She had come to entreat her husband’s acceptance of her fortune; the Austrian Embassy had just sent documents proving Monsieur Firmiani’s death and the will, which his valet had kept safe. Octave could accept it all—yet he was richer already by treasures of the heart “to which none but God can add.” She laid her head on his breast in unrestrained happiness.

Monsieur de Bourbonne concluded with an old-man’s reflection: “In my day we made love; in yours, you love. Women are all that is best in humanity; they are not even guilty of their faults, for they come through us.”
