Some stories can be told loosely, but others demand a delicate, almost anatomical touch—portraits need a soul; certain truths emerge only through subtle harmonies of time, circumstance, or conscience.

That sensitivity is required for this simple tale, aimed at grave, reflective readers who live on tender emotion. If the writer, like a surgeon at a dying friend’s side, reveres the subject, the reader should feel it. The narrative speaks to those who have known private grief: the luxury of tears, the silent pangs of memory, regret for loss, and smiles at past joys. Others will set the book aside; those who have never buried a beloved will not grasp these pages. The writer assures the tale is true and worth the reader’s feeling.

In contemporary France, speech varies by speaker; Parisians describe the same person in amusingly different ways. A Practical man lists facts about Madame Firmiani—house on the rue du Bac, well-furnished salons, an income of a hundred thousand francs, and a husband once receiver-general—solid, respectable details. A Lounger praises her social standing and excellent tea; an Egotist dismisses salons as trivial, insisting the mind should transcend small talk. A Student finds her enchanting; a Fop laments faded beauty but admits charm. A Connoisseur praises her paintings; a Spiteful woman brands her a dangerous siren. An Attaché recalls former beauty; a Distinguishing observer notes elite connections. A Duke feigns ignorance; a Ninny wrongly claims she sang at the Opera. Two old ladies trace ties to the Cadignans. A Joke says her salon is safe because any scoundrels are of high quality. Others allege affairs—Murat’s mistress—or coquetry that ruined a young man’s prospects.

Madame Firmiani thus appears through many lenses—each account reflecting the observer as much as the observed—and invites reflection on deeper emotional truths.

In January 1824 opinions about Madame Firmiani varied so widely—widow or wife, wise or foolish, virtuous or otherwise, rich or poor, beautiful or plain—that no clear portrait emerged; society distorted a reputation like lithographic blocks yielding different images from slight variations.

That winter Monsieur de Bourbonne, an elderly provincial magnate from Touraine who cherished his nephew Octave de Camps, grew uneasy. When Octave sold an estate without consulting him and a relative alleged Octave had squandered his fortune on Madame Firmiani and been reduced to teaching mathematics, the old man refused to accept the worst and went to Paris to investigate.

Using his faubourg Saint‑Germain connections, he took the name de Rouxellay and arranged to meet Madame Firmiani while Octave was absent. He soon confirmed Octave’s financial distress. De Rouxellay—no naive countryman, once in the King’s guard and versed in society’s manners and politics—entered her salon with the Marquise de Listomère. He voiced pity for Octave, wondering how a woman of such luxury could be linked to a man in such straits. Yet the salon’s atmosphere and her presence captivated him and forced him to reconsider his initial judgments.

Madame Firmiani, said to be twenty‑five but nearer twenty‑eight, had married the elusive Firmiani at sixteen. She combined beauty, wealth, musical talent, intelligence, and wit, admired even by rivals—making her a frequent target of malicious but persistent gossip, which revealed the dangers of societal suspicion and hinted at her complex character.

The former guardsman lingered after the other guests had gone; Madame Firmiani found him still seated when the clock struck two.

“Madame,” he began as she rose, “I am the uncle of Monsieur Octave de Camps.”

She sat again, moved; Monsieur de Bourbonne could not tell whether her pallor was shame or pleasure. He bluntly relayed rumors that his nephew had ruined himself for her while she enjoyed wealth. She stopped him. “I know of them,” she said. “You are too polite to press and too gallant to question me. It would be absurd to defend myself. Believe me: I scorn money. I married without fortune. Your nephew’s wealth or poverty matters not; I welcome him as a friend. My friends respect one another; I cannot accept those I do not esteem. My guardian angel has kept me from gossip and dishonesty.”

“Madame,” the old man insisted, trembling, “I am almost a father to Octave. Pardon me one thing. I swear as a gentleman your answer shall remain here.” He put his hand on his heart. “Do you love Octave?”

“To any other man I would answer with a look,” she replied. “To you I ask what you would think of a woman who answered such a question. To confess love is the lover’s reward. To tell another—” She left the sentence unfinished, rose, bowed, and withdrew to her rooms.

He left puzzled, thinking her either sly or angelic.

The next morning Monsieur de Bourbonne found Octave in his garret and berated him for squandering the advantages of an uncle who meant to leave him an estate. “Have you been gambling? Lost at the Bourse? Say, ‘Uncle, I’m a wretch,’ and I’ll hug you. But if you lie, I’ll return to my youthful ways.” He approved when Octave admitted he had “ruined” himself for Madame Firmiani.

Octave produced a letter the woman had given him and asked his uncle to read. It began, in effect, “My beloved—” and explained why she was sad. She argued that a wife should be gay and confiding and that a woman’s glory is in the man she loves, yet she had been humiliated by learning of Octave’s family. Knowing his father had unjustly deprived others of property made her feel she could not respect him while he kept such gains. She urged him to consult his conscience: “If you come to me poor, my love shall be redoubled. If not, renounce it. I am your wife, not your mistress. A man who loves a woman sincerely respects the sanctity of her trust too deeply to dishonor himself.” She did not command restitution; she appealed to his honor and begged him to perceive his faults himself.

Octave, moved to tears, said there was more, but the rest he smiled away as a lover’s secret. The uncle, surprised by the gravity of the letter and that they considered themselves married, asked whether Octave had gone into teaching and whether he still had the uncle’s capital. Octave explained that the letter had sparked a revolution in him; conscience made every pleasure feel like theft. He rushed to Madame Firmiani, and together they calculated what ought to be paid to the Bourgneufs—the family whom Octave’s father had wronged. Despite limited means, he condemned himself to pay three percent interest and to make restitution as far as he could. Madame Firmiani, prudent from past insecurity, offered her savings and he accepted them.

“You mean that adorable woman actually saves money?” the uncle cried, half mocking, half admiring.

“Do not laugh at her,” Octave replied. He told his uncle that Madame Firmiani’s husband had gone to Greece in 1820 and died three years later; proof of his death and his will had been lost or stolen during the troubles there, so she had lived frugally to protect herself against possible loss of fortune. Octave had been trying to acquire property of his own to provide for her; he took pupils rather than rely on others. But the act of restitution had brought him joys beyond millions: finding the Bourgneufs living in misery—an old agent father, daughters keeping books and house, a sick mother—he felt like Providence in relieving them. Making amends gave him a purity of feeling he had thought he lacked; he called Madame Firmiani “my dear conscience” and hoped to earn an honest fortune by industry.

Touched, the old man said, “Ah, my boy, you have your mother’s soul.” At that moment a carriage sounded outside. Madame Firmiani entered, a little annoyed to find Monsieur de Bourbonne there yet smiling. “I came to entreat my husband to accept my fortune,” she said. The Austrian Embassy had just sent documents proving Monsieur Firmiani’s death and the will, which his valet had kept safe. Octave could accept it all: he was richer than she by having “treasures here”—she laid her hand on his heart—“to which none but God can add.” She then laid her head on his breast in unrestrained happiness.

Monsieur de Bourbonne concluded with an old-man’s reflection: “In my day we made love; in yours, you love. Women are all that is best in humanity; they are not even guilty of their faults, for they come through us.”
