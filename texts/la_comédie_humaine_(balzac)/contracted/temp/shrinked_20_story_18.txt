I have longed to tell a simple true story that should strike terror into two young lovers and drive them to take refuge in each other's hearts, as children cling at the sight of a snake. At the risk of appearing a coxcomb, I state my intention at the outset.

I myself played a part in this almost commonplace tragedy; so if it fails to interest you, the fault will be partly mine, partly due to historical veracity. Many things in real life are uninteresting; it is half of art to select from realities those with poetic possibilities.

In 1819 I was traveling from Paris to Moulins. My finances obliged me to take an outside place. For the first few miles I found many reasons to agree with Englishmen who prefer those airy perches. A young fellow, evidently better off, chose the seat beside me and listened with inoffensive smiles. Near age, similar ways of thinking, a shared love of fresh air and the rich landscape through which the coach lumbered, and an indefinable magnetism drew us before long into one of those short-lived travellers' intimacies in which we unbend because the intercourse is transient and makes no demands upon the future.

We had not gone thirty leagues before we were talking of women and love. Then, with the circumspection such matters require, we naturally proceeded to our lady-loves. Young as we both were, we still admired "the woman of a certain age," that is, the woman between thirty-five and forty. Oh! any poet who should have listened to our talk, for how many stages beyond Montargis, would have reaped a harvest of flaming epithets, rapturous description, and tender confidences. Our bashful fears, our silent interjections, our blushes, as we met each other's eyes, were eloquent with a boyish charm I have ceased to feel. One must remain young, no doubt, to understand youth.

We understood one another admirably on all the essential points of passion. We had laid it down as an axiom from the outset that there was no such driveling nonsense as a certificate of birth; that many women were younger at forty than girls of twenty; and, to come to the point, that a woman is no older than she looks.

This theory set no limits to the age of love, so we struck into a boundless sea. When we had portrayed our mistresses as young, charming, and devoted—women of rank, taste, intellect; endowed them with tiny feet and delicate, fragrant skin—then came the admission: on his part that Madame Such-an-one was thirty-eight, and on mine that I worshiped a woman of forty. Thereupon, as if released from some vague fear, our confidences came thick and fast when we found that we were in the same confraternity of love. It was which of us should overtop the other in sentiment.

One of us had traveled six hundred miles to see his mistress for an hour. The other, at the risk of being shot for a wolf, had prowled about her park one night. Out came all our follies in fact. If it is pleasant to remember past dangers, is it not at least as pleasant to recall past delights? We live through the joy a second time. We told each other everything—our perils, our great joys, our little pleasures, and even the humors of the situation. My friend's countess had lighted a cigar for him; mine made chocolate for me and wrote to me every day when we did not meet; his lady had come to spend three days with him at the risk of ruin to her reputation; mine had done even better, or worse, if you will. Our countesses, moreover, were adored by their husbands; these gentlemen were enslaved by the charm possessed by every woman who loves; and, with almost supererogatory simplicity, afforded us that sufficient spice of danger which increases pleasure. Ah! how quickly the wind swept away our talk and our happy laughter!

When we reached Pouilly, I scanned my new friend with much interest; it was not difficult to imagine him the hero of a very serious love affair. Picture a young man of middle height, well proportioned, a bright expressive face, dark hair, blue eyes, moist lips, and even white teeth. A certain not unbecoming pallor spread his delicately cut features, and faint dark circles about his eyes as if recovering from illness. Add that he had white, shapely hands, which he tended as a pretty woman should; that he seemed very well informed and decidedly clever, and you will see he was more than worthy of a countess. Indeed, many a girl might have wished for such a husband; he was a Vicomte with an income of twelve or fifteen thousand livres, "to say nothing of expectations."

About a league out of Pouilly the coach was overturned. My luckless comrade, thinking to save himself, jumped to the edge of a newly-ploughed field instead of following the vehicle and clinging tightly to the roof, as I did. He either miscalculated or slipped; how it happened I do not know, but the coach fell upon him, and he was crushed under it.

We carried him into a peasant's cottage, and there amid moans wrung from him by horrible sufferings he contrived to give me a commission—a sacred task, since it was laid on me by a dying man's last wish. Poor boy, all through his agony he tortured himself with the thought of the shock his mistress would feel when she read of his death in a newspaper. He begged me to go and break the news to her. He bade me look for a key he wore on a ribbon about his neck. I found it half buried in the flesh, but the dying boy did not utter a sound as I extricated it as gently as possible from the wound it had made. He had scarcely given me the necessary directions—I was to go to his home at La Charite-sur-Loire for his mistress' love-letters, which he conjured me to return to her—when he grew speechless in the middle of a sentence; but from his last gesture I understood the fatal key would be my passport in his mother's house. It troubled him that he was powerless to utter a single word to thank me, for he had no doubt of my wish to serve him. He looked wistfully at me, then his eyelids drooped in token of farewell, his head sank, and he died. His death was the only fatal accident caused by the overturn.

"But it was partly his own fault," the coachman said to me.

At La Charite I executed the poor fellow's dying wishes. His mother was away, which in a manner was fortunate for me. Nevertheless, I had to assuage the grief of an old woman-servant, who staggered back at the tidings of her young master's death and sank half-dead into a chair when she saw the blood-stained key. But I had another and more dreadful sorrow to think of—the sorrow of a woman who had lost her last love; so I left the old woman to her prosopopeia and carried off the precious correspondence, carefully sealed by my friend of the day.

The Countess' chateau lay some eight leagues beyond Moulins, and then there was distance to walk across country. So it was not exactly easy to deliver my message. For divers reasons into which I need not enter, I had barely money to take me to Moulins. However, youthful enthusiasm determined me to hasten thither on foot as fast as possible. Bad news travels swiftly, and I wished to be first at the chateau. I asked for the shortest way and hurried through the field paths of the Bourbonnais, bearing, as it were, a dead man on my back. The nearer I came to the Chateau de Montpersan, the more aghast I felt at my strange self-imposed pilgrimage. Vast numbers of romantic fancies ran through my head. I imagined all kinds of situations in which I might find this Comtesse de Montpersan, or, to observe the laws of romance, this Juliette, so passionately beloved of my traveling companion. I sketched ingenious answers to the questions she might put. At every turn of a wood, in every beaten pathway, I rehearsed a modern version of the scene in which Sosie describes the battle to his lantern. To my shame be it said, I had thought at first of nothing but the part I was to play, of my own cleverness, of how I should demean myself; but now that I was in the country, an ominous thought flashed through my soul like a thunderbolt tearing through gray cloud.

What an awful piece of news for a woman whose whole thoughts were full of her young lover, who was looking forward hour by hour to a joy which no words can express, a woman who had been at a world of pains to invent plausible pretexts to draw him to her side. Yet, after all, it was a cruel deed of charity to be the messenger of death! So I hurried on, splashing and bemiring myself in the byways of the Bourbonnais.

Before very long I reached a great chestnut avenue with a pile of buildings at the further end—the Chateau of Montpersan stood out against the sky like a mass of brown cloud, with sharp fantastic outlines. All the doors of the chateau stood open. This in itself disconcerted me, and routed all my plans; but I went in boldly, and in a moment found myself between a couple of dogs, barking as your true country-bred animals can bark. The sound brought out a hurrying servant-maid, who, when informed that I wished to speak to Mme. la Comtesse, waved a hand towards the masses of trees in the English park which wound about the chateau with "Madame is out there—"

"Many thanks," said I ironically. I might have wandered for a couple of hours in the park with her "out there" to guide me.

In the meantime, a pretty little girl, with curling hair, dressed in a white frock, a rose-colored sash, and a broad frill at the throat, had overheard or guessed the question and its answer. She gave me a glance and vanished, calling in shrill childish tones:

"Mother, here is a gentleman who wishes to speak to you!"

And, along the winding alleys, I followed the skipping, dancing white frill, a sort of will-o'-the-wisp that showed me the way among the trees.

I must confess. I paused behind the last shrub in the avenue, adjusted my collar, brushed my shabby hat and trousers with my cuffs, dusted my coat and smoothed it once more. I buttoned my coat to show the least worn side, folded my trousers over my grass-stained boots. With this Gascon toilette I hoped the lady would not mistake me for the local rate collector; now I sometimes laugh at my precautions.

Suddenly, at a turning in the green walk among flowers lit by a ray of sunlight, I saw Juliette—Juliette and her husband. The little girl held her mother’s hand; the lady quickened her pace at the child’s ambiguous phrase. Taken aback, I bowed coolly, masking the disappointment I felt in a faint pout. My polished phrases fled me.

This hesitation allowed the husband to approach. A thousand thoughts flitted through my mind. To steady myself I made feeble inquiries—were they really M. le Comte and Mme. la Comtesse de Montpersan? These imbecilities bought time to form my conclusions at a glance and, with a perspicacity rare at that age, to study the pair whose solitude I was about to disturb.

The husband was a typical provincial nobleman. I noticed his big shoes first—more striking than a seedy coat, threadbare trousers, a flabby cravat, or a crumpled collar. There was something of a magistrate about him: the self-importance of a local mayor, the sour temper of an unsuccessful candidate. His face was wizened, wrinkled and sunburned; his scant hair sleekly gray. His character mixed homely sense with sheer silliness, a rich man’s overbearing ways and a lack of manners; led by his wife yet fancying himself master—domineering about trifles, careless of weightier matters.

But the Countess—what a contrast! Small, flat and graceful; so fragile you would fear to bruise her. She wore white muslin, a rose sash, and rose ribbons in her cap; her chemisette moulded to her shoulders and rounded contours, awakening an irresistible desire of possession. Her eyes were dark and expressive, her movements graceful, her foot charming. An experienced man of pleasure would not have given her more than thirty; her forehead was girlish. In character she recalled certain types fresh in any young man who had read Louvet.

In a moment I saw how things stood and took a diplomatic course creditable to an old ambassador. For once—perhaps the only time then—I used tact, showing the special skill of courtiers and men of the world. I had so many battles to fight later in life that such care for daily proprieties rarely had space in me.

“M. le Comte,” I said with an air of mystery, “I should like a few words with you,” and fell back a pace.

He followed. Juliette left us, going away unconcernedly, like a wife who knows she can learn her husband’s secrets whenever she chooses.

I told the Count briefly of the death of my traveling companion. His reaction convinced me his affection for his young collaborator was sincere, and this emboldened me.

“My wife will be in despair,” he cried; “I shall be obliged to break the news of this unhappy event with great caution.”

“Monsieur,” I said, “I addressed you first as duty required. I could not, without informing you, deliver a message to Mme. la Comtesse entrusted to me by a stranger; this commission is sacred, a secret I cannot dispose of. From the high idea he gave me of your character, I felt sure you would not oppose fulfilling a dying request. Mme. la Comtesse will be free to break the silence imposed on me.”

At this eulogy the Count inclined his head amiably, responded with a somewhat involved compliment, and left me a free field. We returned to the house. The bell rang, and I was invited to dinner. As we approached, Juliette stole a glance at us. Surprised to find her husband contriving a frivolous excuse to leave us together, she gave me that look only women can give—curiosity, disdain, involuntary tremors and alarms, the thought that an unexpected guest was tiresome when she had hoped for solitude with her love. I understood that mute eloquence and answered with a sad smile. I thought of her standing in the sunny alley with flowers on either hand; the picture rose before my eyes and I could not repress a sigh.

“Alas, madame, I have just made a very arduous journey—undertaken solely on your account.”

“Sir!”

“Oh! it is on behalf of one who calls you Juliette that I am come,” I continued. Her face grew white.

“You will not see him today.”

“Is he ill?” she asked, in a lower voice.

“Yes. But for pity’s sake, control yourself… He intrusted me with secrets that concern you, and you may be sure that never messenger could be more discreet nor more devoted than I.”

“What is the matter with him?”

“How if he loved you no longer?”

“Oh! that is impossible!” she cried; a faint smile broke over her face. Then a shudder ran through her; she reddened and asked with a wild glance, “Is he alive?”

Great God! What a terrible phrase. I was too young for that tone; I made no reply, only looked at the unhappy woman in helpless bewilderment.

“Monsieur, monsieur, give me an answer!” she cried.

“Yes, madame.”

“Is it true? Oh! tell me the truth; I can hear the truth. Tell me the truth! Any pain would be less keen than this suspense.”

I answered with two tears wrung from me by that voice. She leaned against a tree with a faint, sharp cry.

“Madame, here comes your husband!”

“Have I a husband?” and with those words she fled out of sight.

“Well,” cried the Count, “dinner is growing cold.—Come, monsieur.”

I followed him into the dining-room. Dinner was served with all the Parisian luxury. Five covers were laid: three for the Count and Countess and their child; mine, which should have been his; and another for the canon of Saint-Denis, who said grace and then asked, “Why, where can our dear Countess be?”

“Oh! she will be here directly,” said the Count. He helped himself to soup with portentous speed.

“Oh! nephew,” exclaimed the canon, “if your wife were here, you would behave more rationally.”

“Papa will make himself ill!” said the child, mischievous.

Shortly after, as the Count eagerly helped himself to venison, a housemaid entered: “We cannot find madame anywhere, sir!”

I sprang up; the dread showed plainly in my face, and the old canon followed me into the garden. The Count, for appearances’ sake, came partway, calling “Don't go, don't go! Don't trouble yourselves in the least,” but he did not offer to accompany us.

We three—the canon, the housemaid, and I—hurried through the garden and over the bowling-green, shouting, listening, growing more uneasy. As we hurried, I told the story of the fatal accident, and discovered how attached the maid was to her mistress—she took my dread more seriously than the canon. We searched by the pools and all over the park, but found no sign. At last we turned back, and under the walls of some outbuildings I heard a smothered, wailing cry, barely audible. It seemed to come from what might have been a granary. I went in at all risks, and there we found Juliette. With the instinct of despair she had buried herself deep in the hay, hiding her face to deaden her cries—pudency stronger than grief. She was sobbing and crying like a child, but with a more poignant, piteous sound. There was nothing left in the world for her. The maid pulled the hay from her; her mistress lay supine and listless as a dying animal. The maid could find nothing to say but, “There! madame; there, there—”

“What is the matter with her? What is it, niece?” the canon kept exclaiming.

With the girl's help I carried Juliette to her room, ordered that she not be disturbed and that everyone be told she had a sick headache. Then the canon and I came back to the dining-room.

Some time had passed; I had scarcely thought of the Count since we left him; his indifference had surprised me, and I was more amazed to find him seated philosophically at table. He had eaten almost the whole dinner, to his little daughter’s delight; the child smiled at her father’s flagrant breach of the Countess’ rules. His indifference was explained by a mild altercation with the canon: the Count was on a severe medical regimen and the ferocious hunger of a convalescent had overpowered all human sensibilities. In that short time I had seen human nature under two different aspects, with a grotesque element in the heart of a terrible tragedy.

The evening that followed was dreary. I was tired. The canon racked his brains for reasons for his niece’s tears. The Count silently digested his dinner; apparently content with the maid’s vague feminine explanation. We went early to bed.

As I passed the Countess’ door on the way to my room, I asked a servant timidly for news. She heard my voice and would have me come in, trying to speak, but could not utter a sound. She bowed her head, and I withdrew. Exhausted from my forced march and agitation, I slept.

Late in the night curtain rings grated. There sat the Countess at the foot of my bed; the lamp’s light fell full upon her face.

“Is it really true, monsieur, quite true?” she asked. “I do not know how I can live after that awful blow; but I feel calm now. I want to know everything.”

What calm! Her face was ghastly against her brown hair; those hours had bleached her; she had lost all womanly color. Her eyes were red and swollen; nothing of their beauty remained—only bitter, exceeding grief; it was as if a gray cloud covered where the sun had shone.

I told her the accident in a few words, without harrowing details. I spoke of our first day's journey, filled with recollections of her and of love. She listened eagerly, without tears, leaning toward me like a zealous doctor watching a patient’s change. When she seemed to have opened her whole heart to pain, deliberately plunging into misery with the frenzy of despair, I seized the moment and told her of the fears that had troubled the dying man and why he had given me that fatal message. Then her tears were dried by fires burning in the dark depths within her; she grew paler. When I drew the letters from beneath my pillow and offered them, she took them mechanically; then, trembling, she said in a hollow voice:

“And I burned all his letters!—I have nothing of him left!—Nothing! nothing!”

She struck her hand to her forehead.

“Madame—” I began.

She glanced at me in the convulsion of grief. “I cut this from his head, this lock of his hair.”

I gave her that last imperishable token that had been part of him she loved. If you had felt, as I did then, her burning tears on your hands, you would know what gratitude is when it follows so closely upon a kindness. Her eyes shone with a feverish glitter; a faint gleam of happiness lit her suffering as she grasped my hands and, choking, said:

“Ah! you love! May you be happy always. May you never lose her whom you love.”

She broke off and fled with her treasure.

Next morning that night-scene seemed like a dream; to make sure of the truth I looked under my pillow for the packet of letters and found nothing. There is no need to tell you how the next day went. I spent hours with Juliette, and in her lightest words and gestures I saw proof of the nobleness and delicacy of soul that made her one of those beloved, self-sacrificing natures so rare on earth.

In the evening the Comte de Montpersan came as far as Moulins with me. Embarrassed, he said:

“Monsieur, if it is not abusing your good-nature… would you, as you are going to Paris, have the goodness to remit a sum of money to M. de——, in the Rue du Sentier? I owe him an amount and he asked me to send it as soon as possible.”

“Willingly,” said I. In my innocence I took charge of a rouleau of twenty-five louis d’or, which paid my journey back to Paris; only when I arrived at the address to repay the amount did I understand the delicate art with which Juliette had obliged me. Was not all the genius of a loving woman revealed in such a way of lending, in her reticence about a poverty easily guessed?

And what rapture to have this adventure to tell to a woman who clung to you more closely in dread, saying, “Oh, my dear, not you! You must not die!”
