I have always wanted to tell a simple and true story that would terrify two young lovers, driving them to seek refuge in each other's hearts, much like children clinging together at the sight of a snake. I acknowledge that my intention may spoil the story and make me seem vain.

I played a part in this almost ordinary tragedy, so if it fails to engage you, the fault lies partly with me and partly with the mundane nature of reality. Life often lacks interest, and it is the artist's task to select from reality those elements that hold poetic potential.

In 1819, I was traveling from Paris to Moulins and, due to my finances, took an outside seat on the coach. An Englishman, seemingly better off, joined me, and we quickly bonded over our shared love for fresh air and the beautiful landscape. Our conversation soon turned to women and love, and we found ourselves admiring "the woman of a certain age," specifically those between thirty-five and forty. A poet listening to our discussion would have found a wealth of passionate expressions and tender confidences. Our bashful fears and silent interjections conveyed a youthful charm that I no longer possess.

We agreed that a certificate of birth was meaningless and that many women were younger at forty than some girls at twenty. This belief opened the door to a boundless exploration of love. As we described our mistresses—young, charming, and devoted—my companion revealed that his lady was thirty-eight, while I confessed to adoring a woman of forty. This admission freed us to share our romantic follies, each trying to outdo the other in sentiment.

One had traveled six hundred miles for a brief meeting with his mistress, while the other had risked danger to meet his lady in secret. We recounted our joys and perils, reliving those moments of delight. My friend's countess had lit a cigar for him; mine made chocolate and wrote daily when we were apart. Our countesses were adored by their husbands, who, in their simplicity, provided just enough danger to heighten our pleasure. But soon, our laughter was swept away by the wind.

Upon reaching Pouilly, I observed my new friend closely. He was a well-proportioned young man with an expressive face, dark hair, blue eyes, and a certain pallor that suggested he was recovering from an illness. He had shapely hands and appeared well-informed and clever, making him worthy of a countess. He was a Vicomte with a respectable income, and many a girl would have desired him.

However, disaster struck about a league out of Pouilly when the coach overturned. My unfortunate companion jumped to escape but miscalculated and was crushed beneath the vehicle. We carried him to a peasant's cottage, where, in agony, he entrusted me with a sacred task—delivering the news of his death to his mistress. He worried about the shock she would feel upon reading of his demise in a newspaper. He asked me to retrieve a key he wore around his neck, which I found embedded in his flesh. After giving me directions to his home in La Charite-sur-Loire for his love letters, he grew speechless and died, leaving me with the burden of his last wish.

The coachman remarked that it was partly my friend's fault, but I was consumed by the task ahead. At La Charite, I fulfilled his wishes, but his mother was away, leaving me to comfort an old servant who fainted at the news of her master's death. I took the sealed letters, burdened by the sorrow of a woman who had lost her last love.

The Countess' chateau was eight leagues beyond Moulins, and I had little money for the journey. Nevertheless, my youthful enthusiasm propelled me to walk as quickly as possible, eager to deliver the bad news before anyone else. As I approached the Chateau de Montpersan, I felt increasingly uneasy about my role as the bearer of such tragic news.

Upon reaching the chateau, I found the doors open, which unsettled me. I entered and was met by barking dogs and a servant-maid who directed me to the park where Madame was. I felt lost, unsure of how to find her. A little girl overheard my inquiry and, with a playful demeanor, led me through the winding paths of the park, calling for her mother.

I must confess that I paused behind the last shrub on the avenue, adjusting my shabby attire in hopes that the lady would not mistake me for a local rate collector. Reflecting on that youthful episode now brings me amusement.

Just as I composed myself, I spotted Juliette and her husband among the flowers, the lady's pace quickening at her child's ambiguous remark. Taken aback by my presence, she regarded me with a cool expression that revealed her disappointment. I struggled to recall the elegant phrases I had prepared.

This hesitation allowed her husband to approach. Thoughts raced through my mind as I awkwardly inquired if they were indeed M. le Comte and Mme. la Comtesse de Montpersan. My clumsy questions gave me time to assess the couple. The husband, a typical provincial nobleman, wore large shoes that left a deeper impression on me than his worn coat and trousers. He exuded a mix of self-importance and a lack of manners, a man led by his wife yet believing himself the master.

In stark contrast, the Countess was petite and graceful, her delicate figure and enchanting shape evoking a desire for possession. Dressed in white muslin with rose-colored accents, she appeared youthful and vibrant, reminiscent of the Comtesse de Lignolles and the Marquise de B----, two types familiar to any young man who has read Louvet's romance.

Recognizing the situation, I took a diplomatic approach, addressing the Count with an air of mystery, requesting a private word. He obliged, leaving Juliette to wander off, confident she could learn her husband's secrets at will.

I informed the Count of my traveling companion's death, noting his genuine affection for the man. He expressed concern for his wife, indicating the need for caution in delivering the news. I explained that I could not approach the Countess without first informing him, emphasizing the sacred nature of the message entrusted to me. The Count, flattered by my words, allowed me to proceed.

As we approached the house, Juliette cast a glance at us, her expression a mix of curiosity and disdain, likely due to my appearance and the unexpected guest. I recalled her beauty, standing in the sunlit alley, and sighed.

"Alas, madame, I have made a difficult journey solely for you," I began. Her face paled at my words.

"Is he ill?" she asked, her voice dropping.

"Yes, but please control yourself. He entrusted me with secrets concerning you, and I assure you, I am a discreet and devoted messenger."

"What is wrong with him?" she pressed.

"What if he no longer loves you?" I replied, and her reaction was immediate.

"That is impossible!" she exclaimed, a faint smile breaking through. But then, panic set in, and she asked, "Is he alive?"

The weight of her question struck me, and I could only nod in response. Her distress was palpable.

"Give me an answer!" she cried.

"Yes, madame."

"Is it true? Tell me the truth! Any pain would be better than this suspense."

I could only respond with tears, and she leaned against a tree, crying out in despair.

"Your husband is coming!" I warned.

"Do I have a husband?" she fled from sight.

The Count called me to dinner, and I followed him into the dining room, where a lavish meal awaited. The Countess was notably absent, and the canon of Saint-Denis inquired about her whereabouts. The Count dismissed the concern, hastily serving dinner.

After a while, a maid entered, reporting that the Countess was missing. Alarmed, I rushed out with the canon and the maid, searching the garden and park for her. As we called out, I recounted the tragic accident, and the maid's concern for her mistress became evident.

Eventually, we heard a muffled cry from a granary. Inside, we found Juliette, buried in hay, sobbing in despair. The maid and I helped her to her room, where I instructed that she not be disturbed, claiming she had a headache.

When we returned to the dining room, I was surprised to find the Count still eating, seemingly unfazed by his wife's absence. His indifference was explained by a serious medical condition that had left him ravenous, overshadowing his concern for Juliette.

The evening was somber. I was exhausted, and the canon pondered the reason for Juliette's tears. The Count remained preoccupied with his meal, content with a vague explanation relayed through the maid. We all retired early.

As I passed the Countess's room, I asked the servant for news. Juliette called me in, attempting to speak but unable to. I left, feeling the weight of her agitation.

Later that night, I was awakened by the sound of curtain rings. The Countess sat at the foot of my bed, her face pale and drawn.

"Is it true?" she asked, her voice trembling. "I don't know how I can live after this blow, but I want to know everything."

Her appearance shocked me; she had lost her youthful glow, her eyes red and swollen. I recounted the accident briefly, avoiding distressing details. She listened intently, leaning forward as if to catch every word.

When I shared the dying man's fears and the message he had entrusted to me, her tears dried, replaced by a deep pallor. I presented her with letters from him, and she took them mechanically, lamenting that she had burned all his letters, leaving her with nothing.

"I cut this lock of his hair," she said, revealing a precious token. I handed it to her, and her tears fell on my hands, a moment of profound gratitude and connection.

"Ah! You love! May you always be happy," she said, clutching the lock and fleeing with her treasure.

The next morning felt surreal, and I searched for the letters under my pillow, finding nothing. I spent the day with Juliette, witnessing her noble spirit and selflessness.

In the evening, the Comte de Montpersan accompanied me to Moulins, asking if I could deliver a sum of money to a contact in Paris. I agreed, taking a rouleau of twenty-five louis d'or, which covered my journey back. Upon arrival, I realized the delicate way Juliette had arranged the loan, revealing the depth of her love and thoughtfulness. What a joy it was to share this adventure with a woman who cherished me, saying, "Oh, my dear, not you! You must not die!"
