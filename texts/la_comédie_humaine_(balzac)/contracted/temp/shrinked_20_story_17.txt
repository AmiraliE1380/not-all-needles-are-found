**La Grenadière** by Honoré de Balzac, translated by Ellen Marriage, depicts a charming house on the right bank of the Loire River, near the Tours bridge. The area features picturesque cliffs, white stone houses, gardens, and vineyards, benefiting from a favorable climate for fruit cultivation.

Situated about a hundred paces from the village church of Saint-Cyr, La Grenadière is halfway up a hillside, with steps leading down to an embankment that serves as a causeway. The property includes terraced gardens, trellises, and a variety of flowers, creating a vibrant environment. A Gothic archway opens into a rose-filled garden with a wooden summer-house covered in climbing plants.

The rustic house has large windows, a slate roof, and yellow-washed walls with green shutters. Inside, a crooked staircase leads to a dining room with tiled floors and a sitting room adorned with saffron-colored wallpaper. The first floor has two simple bedrooms, and a modern kitchen connects to the main house, indicating that La Grenadière was once a retreat for townsfolk from Tours.

The estate also includes a small vineyard and a vinedresser’s cottage, surrounded by stone walls and fruit trees. The terrace offers breathtaking views of the Loire, showcasing the beauty of Touraine. La Grenadière embodies a humble yet majestic dwelling, rich in natural beauty and tranquility, serving as a cherished retreat for lovers and dreamers.

An Englishman renting La Grenadière for the summer pays a thousand francs for six months, plus extra for orchard fruit and vintage. This estate, valued since 1690, offers picturesque views of Touraine's valleys and cathedral towers, making it invaluable for health and happiness.

In the spring of the Restoration, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, seeking a home. She chose La Grenadière, likely attracted by its distance from town. The house was simply furnished, reflecting her modest lifestyle and leaving her social status ambiguous—whether upper middle or higher class. Her plain dress and reserved demeanor sparked local curiosity about her background.

Tall and slender, Mme. Willemsens had delicate features and a melancholic expression. Her dark eyes hinted at hidden anguish, yet she exuded quiet strength. Dressed in mourning attire, she seemed to retreat from the world. Despite her frail health, she took evening walks with her children along the Loire, captivating Tours' residents.

During her stay at La Grenadière, she ventured into Tours only twice: once to inquire about schools for her children and again to arrange lessons. Her rare appearances on the bridge piqued local interest, but little was known about her true status. The landlord referred to her as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, suggesting a noble background, though this remained largely within business circles.

Mme. Willemsens' life was a mystery, marked by her natural grace and deep bond with her children, enchanting those around her. Young men were drawn to her, but her sadness made them hesitant to approach. She discarded letters from admirers, preferring to focus on her life at La Grenadière.

Her sons, Louis-Gaston and Marie-Gaston, also attracted attention. Louis-Gaston, the elder, was robust and energetic, while Marie-Gaston was more delicate. Their close relationship was evident, with Louis often caring for his younger brother. Each morning, they began their day with a prayer, maintaining a meticulous routine to present themselves well for their mother.

The bond among the three was profound, characterized by mutual respect and empathy. If Mme. Willemsens was quiet, her sons mirrored her mood; Louis, perceptive and protective, often redirected their activities to ensure she was not overwhelmed. In this idyllic yet isolated life, the family thrived on shared experiences and emotional connections, illustrating the power of love and the importance of familial bonds.

Mme. Willemsens dressed beautifully for her children, striving to be a positive presence in their lives. She was available to hear their lessons from ten to three, with lunch in the summer-house. After lunch, while the children played, she relaxed on a sofa, enjoying the scenic landscape of Touraine.

The children explored the orchard and garden, often seeking their mother’s guidance. During lessons, Mme. Willemsens embroidered quietly, focusing on inspiring Louis while being less concerned about Marie. Afterward, she discussed Louis’ performance with his tutors to support him effectively.

Their days balanced work and play, fostering a harmonious environment. Mme. Willemsens instilled values of moderation and resilience in her children, nurturing their spirits with praise. Watching them play often brought tears of joy to her eyes, as they had never caused her distress. These moments represented her ideal of happiness, akin to a vision of Heaven.

In summer evenings, she read inspiring biographies to Louis while Marie slept, using these stories to teach him about perseverance. However, her happiness was tinged with sadness, as she feared for their future without her. One evening, she confided in Louis about her illness and the inevitability of her death, expressing her deep love and concern for their well-being. She urged him to be strong and promised to guide him in caring for Marie.

As summer progressed, Mme. Willemsens’ health declined, and the atmosphere at La Grenadiere grew somber. The once vibrant household became quiet, with the children adapting to their mother’s condition. Louis, feeling a strong connection to her, focused more on his studies, showing remarkable progress.

Despite her illness, Mme. Willemsens cherished her time with her children, often seen walking with them in the gardens. As autumn approached, she remained devoted to them, savoring each moment. Unbeknownst to her, Louis worked diligently at night, excelling in his studies and preparing for the entrance examination to the Ecole polytechnique, encouraged by his mother to balance his studies with physical activity. Through these experiences, Louis matured, developing a sense of purpose and determination, guided by his mother’s love.

In October, Mme. Willemsens rose at noon, enjoying the warm atmosphere reminiscent of the Bay of Naples. She cherished her time with Louis and Marie, who adored her and brought her flowers, creating a serene yet melancholic scene amid the autumn leaves.

Eventually, the doctor confined her to her room, where she remained surrounded by flowers and her children. In early November, she played the piano for the last time, reflecting on her life. When ordered to stay in bed, she asked Louis for one last view of their homeland, expressing fears about their uncertain future.

As her health declined, Louis and Marie stayed by her side, witnessing her suffering. The doctor prohibited flowers, signaling that the end was near. One quiet Sunday night, Mme. Willemsens called Louis to her, revealing that she would soon die. She instructed him to retrieve important papers, including their birth certificates and a letter to her estranged husband, Lord Brandon, forgiving him.

She shared her limited financial resources with Louis, urging him to keep the money close, as everything would be sealed after her death. Louis vowed to care for Marie, planning to send her to school while pursuing a career at sea. Mme. Willemsens found solace in her son’s determination, feeling a sense of peace before her passing.

The next morning, she received the sacrament surrounded by her children and Annette, their caretaker. As her breathing slowed, sorrow filled the room, and she eventually passed away, leaving her children in grief. They prepared her body for burial, marking her grave with a simple cross inscribed with a poignant message about her life.

After the funeral, Louis and Marie returned home for a final farewell before leaving with Annette. Louis entrusted Marie’s birth certificate and their limited funds to Annette, then took Marie to school, offering her heartfelt advice as they parted ways.

A month later, Louis-Gaston, now an apprentice on a warship, left France behind. As he gazed at the receding coastline, he felt the weight of solitude and responsibility. An old sailor comforted him, reminding him of God’s presence, and Louis accepted his new life at sea, determined to honor his mother’s memory. Thus, the story concludes with Louis embracing his future, embodying the resilience and hope instilled by his mother.
