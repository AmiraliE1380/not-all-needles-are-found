La Grenadiere is a quaint house located on the right bank of the Loire, about a mile downstream from the bridge of Tours. The river, broad and dotted with green islands, flows between cliffs adorned with white stone country houses nestled among gardens and vineyards. This region, with its southern exposure, produces some of the finest fruit, thanks to generations of labor that have created terraces in the cliffs, allowing for the cultivation of hot-climate produce outdoors.

A church spire marks the nearby village of Saint-Cyr, and further along, the Choisille river flows into the Loire through a fertile valley. La Grenadiere, situated halfway up the hillside and about a hundred paces from the church, is an old-fashioned house dating back two to three hundred years, typical of the picturesque spots in Touraine. A fissure in the rock provides steps leading down to the “dike,” the local embankment that keeps the Loire in its bed and serves as a causeway from Paris to Nantes. At the top of the steps, a gate opens onto a narrow footpath flanked by terraces, where walls prevent landslips. The path is almost concealed by trees, and the view of the river expands as one ascends.

At the end of the path, a crumbling Gothic archway leads into a small garden filled with flowers and rose bushes, won from the rock on the highest terrace. A wooden summer-house stands nearby, adorned with jessamine and honeysuckle. The house itself is centrally located in this garden, above a vine-covered flight of steps, with an arched doorway leading to extensive cellars carved into the rock. Surrounding the house are trellised vines and pomegranate trees, which give the property its name. The front features two large windows flanking a rustic door and three dormer windows in a high-pitched slate roof. The walls are washed in yellow, with green-painted doors and shutters.

Inside, a crooked staircase leads to a lobby. To the right is a large dining room with white tile flooring, and to the left is a similarly sized sitting room with saffron-colored walls and visible walnut rafters. The first floor contains two whitewashed bedrooms with simpler stone chimney-pieces. Most doors and windows face south, except for one leading to the vineyard. A small timber-framed kitchen, a modern addition, connects to the house and features a rustic pump and well, indicating that La Grenadiere was originally a vintage house for townsfolk in Tours, who would visit for picnics or during grape harvests.

The property includes two acres of vineyard, with a steep slope at the back. A vinedresser’s cottage leans against the western gable, and stone walls or espaliers surround the area, maximizing the use of the land. Nature fills any overlooked spaces with fig trees, wildflowers, or strawberries.

La Grenadiere is a humble yet imposing dwelling, rich in fruit and fragrant scents, embodying the beauty of Touraine. It features a variety of grapes, figs, peaches, and melons, alongside exotic plants like Spanish broom and Italian oleanders. The Loire flows below, and the evening breeze carries scents of the sea and distant flowers. The landscape transforms with the clouds, offering views of the south bank of the Loire, Tours, and the surrounding hills dotted with chateaux and parks. 

This charming vintage house, owned by a prosperous burgess of Tours, appeals to all imaginations, providing a peaceful retreat for lovers and dreamers alike. The atmosphere is imbued with happiness, and the sounds of the river inspire contemplation. The owner can sit among the perennial flowers and fruit, feeling the world’s vibrancy around him.

If an Englishman rents La Grenadiere for the summer, he pays a thousand francs for six months, excluding vineyard produce. If he wants orchard fruit, the rent doubles; for the vintage, it doubles again. What could La Grenadiere be worth? With its stone staircase, triple terrace, two acres of vineyard, flowering roses, and well-head, it seems priceless. It has not been on the market since it was sold in 1690 for forty thousand francs, remaining a cherished family possession since then. The view from La Grenadiere encompasses three valleys of Touraine and cathedral towers, making it impossible to assign a monetary value to the health and beauty it offers.

In the spring of a bright year during the Restoration, a lady named Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, a thirteen-year-old boy and an eight-year-old boy. She chose La Grenadiere, perhaps drawn by its distance from the town. She converted the drawing-room into a bedroom, gave the children the two rooms above, and the housekeeper slept in a closet. The dining-room served as a combined sitting and drawing room, furnished simply yet tastefully, with no luxury items. The walnut furniture was plain, and the house's charm lay in its neatness and harmony with the surroundings.

It was hard to classify Mme. Willemsens socially; her plain dress led to various assumptions, but her manners suggested a higher status. Her reserve piqued the curiosity of the local populace, who were eager for any distraction in their lives. Mme. Willemsens was tall and slender, with delicate features and a melancholy expression. Her dark eyes, sometimes revealing secret anguish, were framed by premature wrinkles. She wore a long black gown, a silk ribbon at the waist, and a bonnet in the English style, suggesting a commitment to mourning. Her health appeared fragile, and she took walks with her children to the bridge of Tours to enjoy the fresh air and sunset views.

During her time at La Grenadiere, she visited Tours only twice: once to inquire about the best teachers for her children and again to arrange their lessons. Her occasional appearances on the bridge attracted the interest of the townspeople, but little was known about her true status. The owner of La Grenadiere mentioned to friends that she signed the lease as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, hinting at her husband's name. This revelation remained within the business community.

Mme. Willemsens was a mystery to the local elite. Her natural manners and sweet voice charmed those around her, but her sadness and beauty made her seem almost untouchable. Several young men fell in love with her, but their timidity prevented them from approaching her. She burned all letters she received, indicating her desire for solitude and a life free from outside concerns.

The three tutors allowed at La Grenadiere admired the close bond between Mme. Willemsens and her children. The boys, resembling their mother, drew envy from local mothers. Louis-Gaston, the elder, had dark hair and a robust demeanor, while Marie-Gaston, the younger, had almost golden hair and a delicate appearance. Their contrasting looks highlighted their different personalities, with Louis displaying a protective nature towards his brother.

The boys were well-behaved and attentive, reflecting their mother's careful upbringing. They rose early, prayed, and maintained a scrupulous morning routine. Their interactions with their mother were filled with affection, laughter, and a deep understanding of her needs. Mme. Willemsens fostered a nurturing environment, ensuring her children felt loved and valued.

Their mornings included playful moments, with Louis often taking charge to protect Marie from fatigue. He sensed his mother's struggles, noting the signs of her declining health. Despite her reassurances, he remained vigilant, aware of the unspoken worries that lingered in their home. The bond between them was profound, characterized by a shared life of love and mutual understanding, untouched by external influences.

Mme. Willemsens dressed during her children's early breakfast and play, wanting to be a pleasing vision for them. She was always ready to hear their lessons from ten to three, with a lunch break in the summer-house where they all ate together. After lunch, the children played while she rested on a sofa, enjoying the beautiful landscape of Touraine.

The children explored the orchard and garden, chasing lizards and investigating nature, often running to their mother with questions. Mme. Willemsens embroidered during their lessons, attentively following along without interrupting. She focused on her eldest son, Louis, treating him with respect and nurturing his ambition, while she asked little about Marie. After lessons, she would walk the tutor to the gate, inquiring about Louis’ progress, and the tutors would honestly share his strengths and weaknesses.

Their days were filled with a balance of work and play, leaving no room for discouragement or quarrels. Mme. Willemsens’ love smoothed over any difficulties, teaching her sons moderation and submission through her nurturing. She often felt tears of joy watching them play, reflecting on their happiness and her own.

Those hours spent in the garden-house were precious, filled with laughter and the children’s voices. They spoke both English and French, thanks to their English nurse, and their mother skillfully guided their learning, ensuring they understood the world around them. She provided them with interesting books about great figures in history, using these stories to teach them about ambition and success.

As the children listened to stories, little Marie would often fall asleep on her mother’s knee. However, after these readings, Mme. Willemsens would sometimes become pensive and tearful. One evening, Louis noticed her tears and asked why she was crying. She explained that she feared for their future, knowing she would leave them alone and without resources. She expressed her deep love for them, which made her sadness even more profound.

Louis, confused, promised her he would take care of Marie. She reassured him that her love kept her alive, and perhaps the sunny air of Touraine would help her recover. From that day, as she spoke of her impending death, Louis became more focused on his studies, adapting their lives to her melancholy. The once vibrant household grew quieter, and the old servant, Annette, watched with concern as Mme. Willemsens’ health declined.

By August, the family’s life had changed significantly. Mme. Willemsens, still beautiful but increasingly frail, made an effort to maintain her appearance. The house, once lively, felt somber, and they rarely ventured out. Louis, feeling a deep connection to his mother, noticed her exhaustion and often suggested shorter walks.

As autumn arrived, bringing sunny days and ripe fruits, Mme. Willemsens spent her time solely with her children, cherishing each moment. Meanwhile, Louis worked diligently at night, making significant progress in his studies, preparing for the entrance examination to the Ecole polytechnique.

He often visited the bridge of Tours, where he met a retired naval officer who shared tales of adventure. Louis, eager for knowledge about life in the military, asked many questions and learned about the hardships of such a life. Encouraged by his mother, he began taking long walks to build his endurance, climbing trees and learning to swim. He transformed from a boy into a young man, his sunburned face reflecting a newfound purpose.

When October arrived, Mme. Willemsens could only rise at noon. The warmth of the Loire made the air feel almost like the Bay of Naples, prompting the faculty to recommend the area. At midday, she sat under the shade with her two boys, who never left her side. With lessons over, they sought a life of closeness, free from outside worries. The elder boy lay beside her, basking in her affection, while Marie, the more restless child, gathered flowers for her. Mme. Willemsens, with her tired eyes and languid movements, never complained, smiling at her vibrant children—a poignant scene enhanced by the autumn's melancholy beauty.

Eventually, the doctor ordered Mme. Willemsens to stay in her room. It was brightened daily by her favorite flowers, and her children were always near. One early November day, she sat at the piano for the last time, gazing at a Swiss landscape above it and her children outside. Her fingers danced over the keys, filled with passionate emotion. When the doctor arrived, he insisted she stay in bed, leaving her in stunned silence.

After he left, she asked her older son, Louis, to take her out to the terrace for one last view of her homeland. As they stepped outside, her gaze turned skyward, captivated by the clouds. A look of anguish crossed her face as she clutched her children's hands, lamenting their uncertain future. She feared for them, wondering what they would think of her life when they grew older. After a moment of solitude, she saw Louis and Marie kneeling beside her, their love evident. Wishing to take their smiles with her, she returned to bed, never to rise again.

Days passed in a blur, with Annette and Louis keeping vigil over her. The atmosphere was heavy with the dread of impending loss. On the fifth day, the doctor banned flowers from her room, signaling the decline of life. As her condition worsened, Louis refused to leave her side. One quiet Sunday night, he noticed her hand move the curtain, and she spoke solemnly, telling him that tomorrow would be her last day. She instructed him to retrieve important papers from her table, including their birth certificates and a letter she wished to send to Lord Brandon, her estranged husband.

As she dictated the letter, she hesitated at how to sign it, finally deciding on "Louis-Gaston." She then revealed that she had twelve thousand francs saved, all that remained for them. She urged him to keep the money close, as everything would be sealed after her death. Louis vowed to care for Marie, planning to send her to school while he apprenticed at sea, determined to return successful.

Her eyes sparkled with joy at his resolve, and she felt a surge of hope. However, her strength waned, and she requested a priest. Louis fetched Annette, who hurried to find one. The next morning, surrounded by loved ones, Mme. Willemsens received the sacrament, her children kneeling beside her. As her breathing slowed, the room fell silent, and she eventually passed away, leaving her children in shock.

After her death, Annette and the vinedresser’s wife prepared her body for burial, following local customs. Only a few mourners attended her funeral, a stark contrast to the grand send-off she would have received had she lived a different life. Marie, still too young to grasp the finality of death, cried as they buried her.

A simple cross marked her grave, inscribed with a plea for prayers. After the burial, the children returned to La Grenadiere for a final look at their home before leaving with Annette, who took them to her cousin’s house in Tours. Louis shared his plans with Annette, entrusting her with Marie’s birth certificate and the money for her care. The next day, he took Marie to school, offering her heartfelt advice before parting ways.

A month later, Louis-Gaston, now an apprentice on a warship, watched the French coast fade into the horizon. Alone on the vast ocean, he felt lost but was comforted by an old sailor's words. With newfound resolve, he embraced his life at sea, determined to honor his mother’s memory.
