La Grenadiere is a quaint house on the right bank of the Loire, a mile downstream from the bridge of Tours. The broad river, spotted with green islands, runs between cliffs topped with white stone country houses, gardens and vineyards; generations have terraced the slopes to grow warm-climate fruit. A church spire marks nearby Saint-Cyr, and further on the Choisille joins the Loire.

Halfway up the hillside, a hundred paces from the church, stands La Grenadiere, an old Touraine house two or three centuries old. It is reached by a fissure in the rock whose steps descend to the “dike,” the embankment serving as the Paris–Nantes causeway. At the top a gate opens onto a narrow path between terraces; trees partly conceal the house until the river view widens.

A crumbling Gothic archway gives into a small garden hewn from the highest terrace, with a wooden summer-house draped in jessamine and honeysuckle. The house perches above vine-covered steps; an arched doorway leads to cellars carved into the rock. Trellised vines and pomegranate trees—the source of its name—surround it. The front shows two large windows flanking a rustic door, three dormers set in a steep slate roof, and yellowwashed walls with green doors and shutters.

Inside, a crooked staircase rises to a lobby. To the right, a large dining room with white tile floors; to the left, a sitting room with saffron-coloured walls and exposed walnut rafters. The first floor holds two whitewashed bedrooms with simple stone chimneypieces. Most openings face south, save one toward the vineyard. A small timber-framed kitchen, a later addition, contains a rustic pump and well, suggesting La Grenadiere began as a vintner’s retreat for townsfolk of Tours who came for picnics or the grape harvest.

The property includes two acres of vineyard with a steep slope at the back. A vinedresser’s cottage leans against the western gable; stone walls and espaliers enclose the land while neglected corners host fig trees, wildflowers and strawberries.

Modest yet dignified, La Grenadiere is rich in fruit and fragrance—grapes, figs, peaches and melons—with Spanish broom and Italian oleanders adding an exotic note. The Loire flows below; evening breezes bring a sea-scent and changing light reveals the south bank, Tours and hills dotted with châteaux. Owned by a prosperous burgess of Tours, it is a peaceful retreat for lovers and dreamers, where the river’s sounds invite quiet contemplation among perennial flowers and fruit.

If an Englishman rents La Grenadiere for the summer, he pays a thousand francs for six months, excluding vineyard produce; orchard fruit doubles the rent, and the vintage doubles it again. Yet what price its stone staircase, triple terrace, two acres of vineyard, flowering roses, well-head and the view over three valleys of Touraine and cathedral towers? Healthy and beautiful, the house has not been on the market since a 1690 sale for forty thousand francs and has remained a cherished family possession.

In a bright Restoration spring, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, thirteen and eight, and took La Grenadiere—perhaps for its convenient distance from town. She made the drawing-room her bedroom, gave the children the two rooms above, and had the housekeeper sleep in a closet. The dining-room served as sitting and drawing room, plainly furnished in walnut; the house’s charm lay in neatness and harmony rather than luxury.

Mme. Willemsens resisted easy classification. Her plain dress suggested modesty, her polished manners higher breeding, and her reserve provoked local curiosity. Tall and slender, with delicate features and dark eyes that sometimes betrayed secret anguish and premature wrinkles, she always wore a long black gown with a silk ribbon at the waist and an English-style bonnet—signs of mourning. Her health seemed fragile; she often walked with the children to the bridge of Tours for fresh air and sunsets.

She visited Tours only twice—once to inquire about the best teachers and once to arrange lessons. Her rare appearances on the bridge interested townspeople, but little was known of her rank. The owner of La Grenadiere said she signed the lease as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, a hint at her husband’s name; the remark stayed within the business community.

To the local elite she remained a mystery. Her natural manners and sweet voice charmed, but her sadness and beauty made her untouchable. Several young men fell in love yet were kept away by timidity; she burned all letters she received, underscoring her desire for solitude.

The three tutors admired the bond between Mme. Willemsens and her sons. The boys, like their mother, drew envy from local mothers. Louis-Gaston, the elder, had dark hair and a robust demeanour; Marie-Gaston, the younger, had almost golden hair and a delicate appearance. Their contrasting looks matched temperaments—Louis protective of his brother. Well-behaved and attentive, they rose early, prayed and observed a strict morning routine. Their interactions with their mother were affectionate and intuitive; she fostered a nurturing home while hiding small worries. Louis watched her closely, and their life remained largely untouched by outside influence.

Mme. Willemsens dressed at an early breakfast, amused the children, and sat through lessons from ten to three, with a midday lunch in the shared summer-house. Afterwards the children played while she rested on the sofa, looking over the Touraine landscape. They roamed the orchard and garden, chased lizards, examined plants and insects, and returned with eager questions. Mme. Willemsens embroidered during lessons, following them without interrupting, concentrating on her eldest, Louis, and speaking little of Marie. After lessons she walked the tutor to the gate and frankly questioned him about Louis’s progress; the tutors answered honestly about his strengths and faults.

Their days balanced work and play, leaving little room for quarrels. Her steady love smoothed difficulties and taught her sons moderation and submission. She often wept for joy watching them; hours in the garden-house were precious, full of laughter. The children spoke both English and French thanks to their English nurse, and their mother guided their learning so they could understand the world. She gave them engaging books about great historical figures and used those stories to teach ambition and success.

While the children listened, little Marie often fell asleep on her mother’s knee. Afterwards Mme. Willemsens sometimes grew pensive and tearful. One evening Louis noticed her tears; she confessed fears that she might leave them alone and without resources, revealing the deep love behind her sorrow. Louis, puzzled, promised to take care of Marie. She reassured him that love kept her alive and hoped Touraine’s sunny air might help her recover.

As she spoke more of possible death, Louis grew serious and adapted the family’s life to her melancholy. The once vibrant household grew quieter; the old servant Annette watched with concern as Mme. Willemsens’ health declined. By August she remained beautiful but increasingly frail, striving to preserve her appearance; they rarely ventured far and Louis suggested shorter walks.

With autumn’s sunny days and ripe fruits, Mme. Willemsens spent nearly all her time with the children. Meanwhile Louis worked late preparing for the École Polytechnique exam, frequented the bridge of Tours to hear a retired naval officer’s tales, and—encouraged by his mother—took long walks, climbed trees, and learned to swim, slowly maturing into a young man whose sunburned face reflected a new purpose.

When October came, Mme. Willemsens could only rise at noon. The Loire’s warmth made the air almost like the Bay of Naples, a region recommended by the faculty. At midday she sat in the shade with her two children, Louis and Marie, who never left her side. After lessons they sought closeness free from outside worries: the elder child lying beside her while restless Marie gathered flowers. With tired eyes and languid movements, Mme. Willemsens smiled at her vibrant children—a poignant scene touched by autumnal melancholy.

The doctor soon ordered her to stay in bed, though her room was brightened daily by flowers and her children remained near. One early November day she sat at the piano for the last time, gazing at a Swiss landscape above it while her children played outside; her fingers moved with passionate feeling. When the doctor arrived he insisted she stay in bed; stunned, she asked Louis to take her to the terrace for one last view of her homeland. Outside she looked skyward, captivated by the clouds, then, anguished, clutched her children’s hands and lamented their uncertain future, fearing how they would judge her when older. Seeing Louis and Marie kneel beside her, she returned to bed, never to rise again.

Days blurred with Annette and Louis keeping vigil. On the fifth day the doctor banned flowers—an ominous sign. As her condition worsened Louis refused to leave. One quiet Sunday night she drew the curtain and calmly told him tomorrow would be her last day. She instructed him to fetch important papers—birth certificates and a letter she wished to send to Lord Brandon, her estranged husband. As she dictated the letter she hesitated over how to sign it, finally choosing “Louis-Gaston.” She revealed twelve thousand francs saved—their remaining funds—and urged him to keep it safe, as everything would be sealed after her death. Louis vowed to care for Marie, planning to send her to school while he apprenticed at sea, determined to return successful.

Her eyes brightened at his resolve. Strength fading, she requested a priest; the next morning, surrounded by loved ones, she received the sacrament with her children kneeling beside her. As her breathing slowed the room fell silent; she passed away.

Annette and the vinedresser’s wife prepared the body for burial according to local custom. Only a few mourners attended. Marie, too young to grasp finality, cried as they lowered the coffin; a simple cross marked the grave. Afterwards the children returned to La Grenadiere for a last look before leaving with Annette to her cousin’s house in Tours. Louis entrusted Annette with Marie’s birth certificate and the money; the next day he took Marie to school, gave earnest advice, and departed to apprentice on a warship. A month later, watching the French coast fade, he embraced life at sea with the resolve to honor his mother’s memory, comforted by an old sailor’s words.
