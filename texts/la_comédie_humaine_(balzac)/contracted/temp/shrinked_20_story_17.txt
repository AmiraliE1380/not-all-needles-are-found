La Grenadiere is a quaint house on the right bank of the Loire, about a mile downstream from the bridge of Tours. The broad river, dotted with green islands, flows between cliffs crowned with white-stone country houses set among gardens and vineyards. With a favorable southern exposure, the region yields some of the finest fruit, thanks to generations who terraced the cliffs to grow warm-climate produce outdoors.

A church spire marks the nearby village of Saint-Cyr; further on the Choisille joins the Loire through a fertile valley. La Grenadiere stands halfway up the hillside, roughly a hundred paces from the church. The house, two to three hundred years old, is typical of picturesque Touraine. A fissure in the rock provides steps down to the “dike,” the embankment that keeps the Loire in its bed and serves as the causeway from Paris to Nantes. At the top of the steps a gate opens onto a narrow footpath between terraces with retaining walls; trees partly conceal the path, and the river view widens as one ascends.

At the path’s end a crumbling Gothic archway leads into a small garden of flowers and rose bushes hewn from the rock on the highest terrace. A wooden summer-house, draped in jessamine and honeysuckle, stands nearby. The house sits centrally in this garden above a vine-covered flight of steps, with an arched doorway giving access to extensive cellars carved into the rock. Trellised vines and pomegranate trees—whose fruit gives the property its name—surround the house. The front has two large windows flanking a rustic door and three dormer windows in a steep slate roof; the walls are yellowwashed with green-painted doors and shutters.

Inside, a crooked staircase rises to a lobby. To the right is a large dining room with white tile floors; to the left, a sitting room of similar size with saffron-coloured walls and exposed walnut rafters. The first floor holds two whitewashed bedrooms with simple stone chimneypieces. Most doors and windows face south, except one that opens toward the vineyard. A small timber-framed kitchen, a later addition, contains a rustic pump and well, suggesting La Grenadiere was originally a vintner’s retreat for townsfolk of Tours who came for picnics or the grape harvest.

The property includes two acres of vineyard, with a steep slope at the back. A vinedresser’s cottage leans against the western gable; stone walls and espaliers enclose and maximize the land. Neglected corners fill with fig trees, wildflowers, or strawberries.

La Grenadiere is modest yet dignified, rich in fruit and fragrance, and embodies Touraine’s beauty. Its vines yield grapes along with figs, peaches, and melons, while Spanish broom and Italian oleanders add an exotic touch. The Loire flows below; evening breezes carry scents of the sea and distant flowers, and clouds alter the light to reveal views of the south bank, Tours, and hills dotted with châteaux and parks.

Owned by a prosperous burgess of Tours, this vintage house appeals to the imagination as a peaceful retreat for lovers and dreamers. Its atmosphere is suffused with happiness; the river’s sounds invite contemplation, and the owner can sit among perennial flowers and fruit, feeling the vibrant world around him.

If an Englishman rents La Grenadiere for the summer, he pays a thousand francs for six months, excluding vineyard produce; orchard fruit doubles the rent, and the vintage doubles it again. What could La Grenadiere be worth? With its stone staircase, triple terrace, two acres of vineyard, flowering roses, and well-head, it seems priceless. Unsold since its 1690 sale for forty thousand francs, it has remained a family possession. The view embraces three valleys of Touraine and cathedral towers, making a monetary valuation feel trivial compared with the health and beauty it offers.

In a bright spring during the Restoration, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, aged thirteen and eight. She chose La Grenadiere, perhaps for its distance from town. She turned the drawing-room into a bedroom, gave the children the two rooms above, and allotted the housekeeper a closet. The dining-room served as a combined sitting and drawing room, simply furnished in plain walnut; the house’s charm lay in neatness and harmony rather than luxury.

Mme. Willemsens was hard to place socially: her plain dress invited assumptions, but her manners suggested higher rank. Her reserve provoked local curiosity. Tall and slender with delicate features and a melancholy expression, she had dark eyes that sometimes revealed secret anguish and premature wrinkles. She wore a long black gown with a silk ribbon at the waist and an English-style bonnet, signaling mourning. Her health seemed fragile; she walked with her children to the bridge of Tours for fresh air and sunsets.

She went into Tours only twice—once to inquire about the best teachers for her children and once to arrange their lessons. Her occasional appearances on the bridge interested townspeople, but little was known of her status. The owner of La Grenadiere told friends she signed the lease as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, hinting at her husband’s name; this tidbit circulated within the business community but did not fully lift the mystery.

To the local elite she remained enigmatic. Her natural manners and soft voice charmed those around her, yet her sadness and beauty made her seem almost untouchable. Several young men fell in love, but timidity kept them away. She burned all letters she received, signaling a desire for solitude and a life free from outside entanglements.

The three tutors at La Grenadiere admired the close bond between Mme. Willemsens and her children. The boys resembled their mother and drew envy from local mothers. Louis-Gaston, the elder, had dark hair and a robust demeanor; Marie-Gaston, the younger, had almost golden hair and a delicate look. Their contrasting appearances reflected differing temperaments—Louis protective of his brother.

They were well-behaved and attentive, the product of careful upbringing: early rising, prayer, and a scrupulous morning routine. Their interactions with their mother were affectionate and intuitive; playful mornings, mutual laughter, and an instinctive understanding. Louis noticed small signs of his mother’s declining strength—worries she tried to hide—and, though she reassured him, he remained vigilant. Their bond was profound, shaped by a shared life of love largely untouched by the outside world.

Mme. Willemsens dressed during her children's early breakfast and play to be a pleasing sight for them. She was ready to hear their lessons from ten in the morning until three in the afternoon, with a midday lunch in the summer-house where they all ate together. After the meal the children played while she rested on a sofa, enjoying the Touraine landscape.

The children explored the orchard and garden, chasing lizards and studying plants and insects, and often ran to their mother with eager questions. Mme. Willemsens embroidered during their lessons, following attentively without interrupting. She concentrated on her eldest son, Louis, encouraging his ambition and asking little about Marie. When lessons ended she would walk the tutor to the gate and inquire about Louis’ progress; the tutors answered frankly about his strengths and weaknesses.

Their days struck a careful balance of work and play, leaving little room for discouragement or quarrels. Her steady nurturing taught her sons moderation and submission; she often shed tears of joy as she watched them play, reflecting on their happiness and her own contentment.

The hours in the garden-house were precious, full of laughter and the children’s voices. They spoke both English and French, thanks to their English nurse, and their mother guided their learning so they could understand the world. She supplied interesting books about great figures in history and used those stories to teach them about ambition and success.

While the children listened to these readings, little Marie would often fall asleep on her mother’s knee. Afterwards Mme. Willemsens sometimes grew pensive and tearful. One evening Louis noticed her tears and asked why she was crying. She confessed that she feared for their future, that she might leave them alone and without resources, and she spoke with the deep love that made her sorrow so poignant.

Puzzled, Louis promised he would take care of Marie. She reassured him that love kept her alive and hoped the sunny air of Touraine might help her recover. From that day, as she spoke more of her impending death, Louis became more focused on his studies and adjusted the family’s routine to her melancholy. The once vibrant household grew quieter; the old servant Annette watched with growing concern as Mme. Willemsens’ health declined.

By August their life had changed markedly. Mme. Willemsens remained beautiful but increasingly frail, making an effort to preserve her appearance. The house felt somber and they rarely ventured far; Louis noticed her exhaustion and often suggested shorter walks.

With autumn’s sunny days and ripe fruits, Mme. Willemsens spent almost all her time with the children, cherishing each moment. Meanwhile Louis worked late into the night, making steady progress toward the École Polytechnique entrance examination. He frequented the bridge of Tours, where a retired naval officer told him tales of adventure; Louis asked many questions and learned of the hardships of military life. Encouraged by his mother, he took long walks to build endurance, climbed trees and learned to swim, slowly changing from a boy into a young man whose sunburned face reflected a new purpose.

When October arrived, Mme. Willemsens could only rise at noon. The Loire’s warmth made the air almost like the Bay of Naples, and the faculty had recommended the area. At midday she sat in the shade with her two boys, who never left her side. With lessons over they sought a life of closeness, free from outside worries: the elder, Louis, lay beside her, basking in her affection, while Marie, the more restless child, gathered flowers. Mme. Willemsens, with tired eyes and languid movements, complained little, smiling at her vibrant children—a poignant scene made keener by autumn’s melancholy.

Eventually the doctor ordered her to stay in her room, brightened daily by her favorite flowers and by the constant presence of her children. One early November day she sat at the piano for the last time, gazing at a Swiss landscape above it and at her children playing outside. Her fingers moved over the keys with passionate feeling; when the doctor arrived he insisted she stay in bed, and she was stunned.

After he left she asked Louis to take her to the terrace for one last view of her homeland. Stepping outside she lifted her gaze to the sky, captivated by the clouds. Anguish crossed her face as she clutched her children’s hands, fearing their uncertain future and dreading what they might think of her life when they grew older. Then she saw Louis and Marie kneeling beside her, their love obvious. Wishing to carry their smiles with her, she returned to bed, never to rise again.

Days passed in a blur with Annette and Louis keeping vigil; the atmosphere grew heavy with dread. On the fifth day the doctor banned flowers from her room, a clear sign of decline. As her condition worsened Louis refused to leave her. One quiet Sunday night he noticed her hand move the curtain; she spoke solemnly, telling him tomorrow would be her last day. She instructed him to fetch important papers from her table—birth certificates among them—and a letter she wished to send to Lord Brandon, her estranged husband.

As she dictated the letter she hesitated over how to sign it, at last choosing “Louis-Gaston.” She then revealed that she had twelve thousand francs saved—their remaining funds—and urged him to keep the money close, since everything would be sealed after her death. Louis vowed to care for Marie, planning to send her to school while he apprenticed at sea, determined to return successful.

Her eyes brightened at his resolve and she felt a surge of hope, but her strength soon waned and she requested a priest. Louis fetched Annette, who hurried to find one. The next morning, surrounded by loved ones, Mme. Willemsens received the sacrament, her children kneeling beside her. As her breathing slowed the room fell silent; she eventually passed away, leaving them in shock.

Afterward Annette and the vinedresser’s wife prepared the body for burial according to local custom. Only a few mourners attended—far fewer than the grand send-off she might have had under different circumstances. Marie, still too young to grasp death’s finality, cried as they lowered the coffin. A simple cross marked the grave, inscribed with a plea for prayers.

The children returned to La Grenadiere for a last look before leaving with Annette, who took them to her cousin’s house in Tours. Louis entrusted Annette with Marie’s birth certificate and the money for her care. The next day he took Marie to school, offered her heartfelt advice, and then parted from her.

A month later Louis-Gaston, now an apprentice on a warship, watched the French coast recede. Alone on the vast ocean he felt lost but was comforted by an old sailor’s words. With newfound resolve he embraced life at sea, determined to honor his mother’s memory.
