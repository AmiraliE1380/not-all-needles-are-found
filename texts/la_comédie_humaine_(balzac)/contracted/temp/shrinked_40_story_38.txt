The Marquise de Listomere is a young woman raised in the spirit of the Restoration: devout in appearance, attending mass, taking the sacrament, and frequenting balls and operas with elegance. Her confessor permits a marriage of the worldly and the sanctified, and she conforms strictly to Church and society, embodying a current age whose motto seems to be "legality." Her demeanor combines the stern piety of Louis XIV’s last days with the gallantries of his earlier reign. Married seven years to the Marquis de Listomere, a deputy who hopes for a peerage, she practices virtue both by inclination and by policy to further her family’s ambitions—waiting, as many do, for the social moment that might prove their choices right or expose them.

The Marquis is an unremarkable man who stands well at court; his virtues are the absence of faults. As deputy he never speaks but votes RIGHT; at home he behaves as in the Chamber, so he is considered an excellent husband. He never scolds unless kept waiting; friends call him "dull weather"—neither bright nor dark. He resembles the ministers who have followed one another since the Charter. For a woman of principles he was an ideal match: fortunate, in a sense, to have married a man incapable of follies.

Occasionally, impertinent fops have pressed the Marquise’s hand while dancing, only to meet with contemptuous glances that froze any flattering hopes. Beaux, wits, and fops—men nourished on their canes, names, or fame—blanch before her. She has therefore the freedom to converse with agreeable men without inviting scandal. Some coquettish women might reserve such freedoms for later indiscretions, but to suppose any such design in the Marquise would be calumny.

I have had the pleasure of knowing this rare woman. She talks well; I listen; thus I please her and attend her parties—no small aim of my own ambition.

Madame de Listomere is neither plain nor prettily conventional: she has white teeth, clear skin, very red lips, is tall and well made, and has a small, well-shaped foot she does not display. Unlike many Parisian women, her eyes retain a gentle glow that becomes magical when she is animated—then a soul appears behind her reserve. When truly interested she reveals a grace otherwise hidden by coldness; she does not seek success yet often attains it. We often find what we do not seek; that is the moral underpinning of this episode, which I relate because of its echo in Parisian salons.

A month earlier she danced with a young man modest yet lively, full of qualities and of conspicuous defects: Eugène de Rastignac. Ardently skeptical, talented but often concealing it, alternately learned among aristocrats and aristocratic among the learned, he is one of those clever young men who try everything to sound the future. He mocks ambition while preparing for it, possessing both grace and originality—two rare, usually incompatible gifts. That evening he spoke with Madame de Listomere for nearly half an hour without an intent to win her. Their capricious conversation, beginning with the opera Guillaume Tell, reached the duties of women; he looked at her in a way that embarrassed her, then left and did not speak to her again. He danced, played écarta, lost some money, and went to bed. The affair occurred precisely thus; I add nothing and omit nothing.

The next morning Rastignac rose late, lingering in those reveries where the mind grows more active as the body sleeps. He rang for his valet, drank excessive tea, and wrote letters. The first was brief; the second, begun at eleven, took until midday to fill four closely written pages. "That woman keeps running in my head," he muttered as he folded the second letter without addressing it immediately. He lounged in his dressing-gown, feet on a stool, hands in pockets, gazing absently at the fire, the idlefender, and the gilded shovel—small comforts that accompany the pleasure of thinking of a woman.

I entered then; Rastignac, surprised, hastily directed and sealed the two letters and rang for his servant. "Take these," he said; Joseph left without a word.

We spoke briefly of an expedition to Morea, to which I hoped to be appointed as physician; Rastignac observed I would lose much time if I left Paris. We discussed other matters; I spare you the rest.

When the Marquise rose about half-past two that day, her maid Caroline handed her a letter which she read while having her hair done—an imprudence many young women commit. It began, "Dear angel of love, treasure of my life and happiness—" At that she nearly cast it into the fire, but curiosity, common to virtuous women, made her continue. By the fourth page she dropped her arms, visibly fatigued.

"Caroline, go and ask who left this letter."

"Madame, I received it myself from the valet of Monsieur le Baron de Rastignac."

Silence followed for some time.

"Does Madame intend to dress?" asked Caroline at last.

"No—He is certainly a most impertinent man," the Marquise reflected.

I leave women to imagine the reflections that followed—those private reckonings between virtue, curiosity, and the subtle dangers of attention in Parisian society.

Madame de Listomere ended her reply by ordering her porter to refuse Monsieur de Rastignac and to treat him with more than mere disdain; his presumption had gone beyond what she would tolerate. She thought at first of keeping the letter, but then burned it.

“Madame had just received such a fine love-letter; she read it,” Caroline told the housemaid.

“I should never have thought that of madame,” answered the other.

That evening Madame de Listomere went to a party at the Marquis de Beauseant’s, where Rastignac might appear. She stayed until two in the morning, determined to crush Eugène by coldness, but he did not come. A brilliant man—Stendhal—had given the fanciful name “crystallization” to the process by which Madame de Listomere’s thoughts hardened before, during, and after that night.

Four days later Eugène was scolding his valet.

“Ah ça! Joseph; I shall soon have to send you away, my lad.”

“What is it, monsieur?”

“You do nothing but make mistakes. Where did you carry those letters I gave you Saturday?”

Joseph became like a statue in a church porch, absorbed in thought; then he smiled idiotically and said, “Monsieur, one was for the Marquise de Listomere, the other was for monsieur’s lawyer.”

“You are certain?” Joseph could not answer. I was in Eugène’s apartment again and felt obliged to interfere.

“Joseph is right,” I said.

Eugène looked at me.

“I read the addresses quite involuntarily, and—”

“And,” interrupted Eugène, “one of them was not for Madame de Nucingen?”

“No, by all the devils, it was not. Consequently, my dear fellow, I supposed your heart was wandering from the rue Saint-Lazare to the rue Saint-Dominique.”

Eugène struck his forehead and laughed; Joseph perceived the blame was not his.

There are morals young men should consider. First mistake: Eugène thought it amusing to make Madame de Listomere laugh at the blunder that made her the recipient of a love-letter not meant for her. Second mistake: he did not call on her for several days after, allowing that virtuous woman’s thoughts to crystallize. There were other errors I will pass over, for the ladies to deduce.

Eugène at last called on the marquise; as he tried to enter the house the porter stopped him, saying Madame la marquise was out. As he was getting into his carriage, the Marquis de Listomere came home.

“Come in, Eugène,” he said. “My wife is at home.”

A husband, however good, is not perfect. As they climbed the stairs Rastignac perceived several blunders in worldly wisdom that had slipped into this page of his life.

When Madame de Listomere saw her husband ushering in Eugène she could not help blushing. The young baron noticed the color. If even the humblest man keeps some conceit, as every woman keeps some coquetry, who shall blame Eugène if he thought, “What! that fortress, too?” and adjusted his cravat accordingly. Young men may not be grasping but they like to gain a new coin.

Monsieur de Listomere took the Gazette de France to a window to consult journalism for an opinion on the state of France.

A woman, even a prude, is not long embarrassed; she always wears Eve’s fig-leaf. Thus when Eugène, interpreting in favor of his vanity the refusal to admit him, bowed to Madame de Listomere with some intention, she concealed her thoughts behind one of those feminine smiles more impenetrable than a king’s words.

“Are you unwell, madame? You denied yourself to visitors.”

“I am well, monsieur.”

“Perhaps you were going out?”

“Not at all.”

“You expected someone?”

“No one.”

“If my visit is indiscreet you must blame Monsieur le marquis. I had already accepted your mysterious denial, when he himself came up and introduced me into the sanctuary.”

“Monsieur de Listomere is not in my confidence on this point. It is not always prudent to put a husband in possession of certain secrets.”

The firm, gentle tones in which she said this, and the imposing glance she cast at Rastignac, made him aware he had posed prematurely.

“Madame, I understand you,” he said, laughing. “I ought, therefore, to be doubly thankful to Monsieur le marquis; he affords me an opportunity to offer you excuses which might be dangerous were you not kindness itself.”

The marquise looked at him in surprise, but answered with dignity.

“Monsieur, silence on your part will be the best excuse. As for me, I promise you complete forgetfulness, and the pardon which you scarcely deserve.”

“Monsieur,” said Rastignac hastily, “pardon is not needed where there was no offence. The letter,” he added in a low voice, “which you received, and which you must have thought extremely unbecoming, was not intended for you.”

The marquise could not help smiling though she wished to seem offended.

“Why deceive?” she said, with a disdainful air and gentle voice. “Now that I have duly scolded you, I am willing to laugh at a subterfuge not without cleverness. I know many women who would be taken in: ‘Heavens! how he loves me!’ they would say.”

She forced a laugh, then added indulgently, “If we desire to continue friends let there be no more mistakes, of which it is impossible that I should be the dupe.”

“Upon my honour, madame, you are so—far more than you think,” replied Eugène.

“What are you talking about?” asked Monsieur de Listomere, who had been listening without comprehending.

“Oh! nothing that would interest you,” his wife answered.

Monsieur de Listomere returned to his paper, then remarked, “Ah! Madame de Mortsauf is dead; your poor brother has, no doubt, gone to Clochegourde.”

“Are you aware, monsieur,” resumed the marquise, turning to Eugène, “that what you have just said is a great impertinence?”

“If I did not know the strictness of your principles,” he answered naïvely, “I should think you wished either to give me ideas I deny myself, or to tear a secret from me. But perhaps you are only amusing yourself with me.”

The marquise smiled; that smile annoyed Eugène.

“Madame,” he said, “can you still believe in an offence I have not committed? I hope chance may not enable you to discover the name of the person who ought to have read that letter.”

“What! can it still be Madame de Nucingen?” cried Madame de Listomere, more eager to find that secret than to revenge herself for the impertinence of the young man’s speeches.

Eugène flushed. A man must be more than twenty-five not to blush at being taxed with a fidelity that women mock—perhaps to hide envy. Yet he answered with tolerable self-possession, “Why not, madame?”

Such are the blunders we make at twenty-five.

This speech produced a violent commotion in Madame de Listomere’s bosom; but Rastignac did not yet know how to read a woman’s face by a rapid glance. The marquise’s lips paled, but that was all. She rang for wood, forcing Rastignac to rise and take his leave.

“If that be so,” she said, stopping him with a cold, rigid manner, “you will find it difficult to explain, monsieur, why your pen should, by accident, write my name. A name, written on a letter, is not a friend’s opera-hat, which you might have taken carelessly on leaving a ball.”

Eugène, discomfited, looked at her with an air both stupid and conceited. Feeling ridiculous, after stammering a few juvenile phrases he left the room.

A few days later the marquise obtained undeniable proof that Eugène had told the truth. For the last fortnight she has not been seen in society.

The marquis tells all who ask the reason of this seclusion: “My wife has an inflammation of the stomach.”

But I, her physician, who am now attending her, know it is really nothing more than a slight nervous attack, which she is making the most of to stay quietly at home.
