*Two Poets* is the first part of a trilogy focusing on Lucien, his sister Eve, and their friend David in Angoulême during a time dominated by traditional printing. The story centers on Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, a former journeyman pressman who started a small printing business after obtaining a license during the French Revolution, allowing him to operate without formal education.

Séchard transformed from a struggling pressman into a shrewd businessman, driven by greed and the desire to secure a future for his son, David. Although David excelled academically and was sent to Paris to learn the printing trade, Séchard favored practical experience over formal education. Upon returning home in 1819, David was ready to take over the family business, which held a monopoly on official printing. However, competition arose when the Cointet Brothers sought a second printing license, threatening Séchard's dominance.

As Séchard aged and became increasingly greedy, he aimed to pass the business to David while profiting from the transaction. This dual view of his son as both heir and rival strained their relationship. Séchard's cunning overshadowed paternal affection as he prepared to negotiate the sale, complicating their dynamic. The printing house mirrored Séchard's chaotic character, reflecting his avarice. Their relationship highlights themes of ambition, familial duty, and the harsh realities of business, exploring the tension between personal ambition and familial loyalty amid societal change.

In the sitting room of the late Mme. Séchard, Séchard, inebriated, brought David to review a valuation of the family printing business. David was shocked to find the equipment overvalued at sixteen hundred francs, deeming the old presses unworthy. Séchard insisted on their reliability, dismissing David's preference for modern machinery.

In the workshop, Séchard demonstrated the presses' functionality, boasting about their durability. However, David recognized their outdatedness and questioned the valuation, particularly the inflated worth of the type. Despite Séchard's insistence on the tools' quality, David felt trapped in an unfair negotiation.

Sensing David's hesitation, Séchard grew anxious, believing a lack of argument indicated acceptance. He emphasized the value of the business license and income from advertisements, urging David to see the profit potential. Ultimately, David agreed to terms that included a partnership allowing Séchard to retain a room in the building and share profits until the purchase was fully paid.

After moving in, David faced financial strain as Séchard refused to contribute to operational costs, insisting David manage alone. This led to a painful realization for David about his father's greed and lack of support, overshadowing any paternal concern.

Meanwhile, Séchard, now living in the countryside, became increasingly anxious about the business's success. He frequently visited David, who struggled to keep the printing house afloat amidst competition from the Cointet Brothers, who were gaining market share by slandering David and monopolizing local contracts.

David's friendship with Lucien Chardon, a talented but impoverished young man, blossomed as they shared aspirations. David offered Lucien a position in the printing house, hoping to help him find a path to success. Their friendship deepened as they discussed innovative ideas for the business, but their intellectual pursuits distracted them from practicalities. The Cointets recognized David's lack of business acumen, allowing his operation to continue in a diminished capacity.

The physical state of the printing house reflected Séchard's avarice, as he neglected repairs and maintenance. The building was dilapidated, and the workshop was cluttered, indicative of the struggles faced by David and Lucien.

In May 1821, David and Lucien sought refuge from their oppressive printing office. Their friendship flourished over three years as they immersed themselves in literature and art, sharing dreams of fame. David admired Lucien's brilliance, while Lucien aimed to elevate both their social standings.

Their artistic pursuits took a turn when a stranger arrived with a manuscript for printing, claiming to be an author sent by Mme. de Bargeton, the woman Lucien admired. Despite his feelings for her, Lucien recognized the social barriers between them and vowed not to pursue her unless David was welcomed too.

Lucien wandered through Angoulême, contemplating the societal divides between the upper town, home to the nobility, and L’Houmeau, where he lived. The strict social boundaries maintained by the upper town's old families intensified during the Restoration, further alienating the bourgeoisie from the nobility. Lucien's aspirations to rise above his humble origins were complicated by entrenched class prejudices. Mme. de Bargeton significantly influenced Lucien's ambitions, placing him at the center of a social conflict that could either elevate or destroy him. The narrative sets the stage for Lucien's struggle against societal constraints, driven by his passion for poetry and love for Mme. de Bargeton, while David remains a steadfast friend, embodying loyalty and sacrifice.

M. de Bargeton, a descendant of a Bordeaux alderman, witnessed his family's decline due to his grandfather's extravagance. By 1789, the family retained only seignorial rights to rents from their Angoumois lands, amounting to ten thousand francs annually. M. de Bargeton married Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, a noblewoman from an old but now obscure family. Her father, M. de Nègrepelisse, was a miser reliant on his wife’s small estate, while Anaïs received a broad education from Abbé Niollant, who instilled in her a love for music and literature.

Anaïs, or Naïs, developed a strong sense of self-worth and disdain for those around her, leading to a haughty demeanor. Her education fostered a sense of superiority that made her ill-suited for provincial life. After the Abbé's death in 1802, Naïs confronted her father's miserly nature and her rebellious spirit against marriage constraints. M. de Nègrepelisse sought a suitable husband for her, ultimately choosing M. de Bargeton, who was older and perceived as lacking intellect but manageable.

Naïs married M. de Bargeton, hoping for freedom and social connections in Paris, but their age difference and limited income constrained their lives in Angoulême. Over time, Naïs's once-vibrant qualities became absurdities, and her artistic inclinations turned into exaggerated expressions of emotion. She grew increasingly isolated, longing for excitement beyond her provincial existence.

Her disappointing marriage left her yearning for love and connection. The rise of the Empire rekindled her hopes, leading her to fall for a young officer, whose death in battle deepened her sorrow. This loss cast a shadow over her life, and as she faced aging without fulfillment, her pride kept her from engaging in the mundane affairs of provincial men.

Mme. de Bargeton’s life became a cycle of unfulfilled dreams and artistic aspirations, leading her to seek solace in literature and the arts. Despite her intellectual gifts, she remained trapped in a narrow existence, her dignity morphing into affectation. The arrival of M. du Châtelet, a charming but superficial man, introduced a new dynamic to her life. He ingratiated himself into her circle, recognizing her potential as a social asset, while the local bourgeoisie disapproved of his presence.

M. du Châtelet’s background made him intriguing, and he sought to cultivate a relationship with Mme. de Bargeton, seeing in her both a source of personal gain and a means to elevate his own status. His arrival disrupted the social order, highlighting the tensions within Angoulême society, where intellectual poverty coexisted with remnants of decayed gentility.

In summary, Mme. de Bargeton’s life reflects the struggles of a woman caught between her aspirations and the limitations imposed by her environment, ultimately leading to profound isolation and unfulfilled potential.

Political discourse was often verbose and insincere, with Louis XVIII viewed as a Jacobin. Women in society were generally awkward and poorly presented, diminishing their appeal. Châtelet found some solace in this environment due to his interest in Mme. de Bargeton. Despite their flaws, the nobility had a charm and tradition that set them apart from the bourgeoisie. Their loyalty to the Bourbon dynasty resembled a form of faithfulness, enhancing their perceived value.

Some women, flattered by Châtelet, admired qualities in him that their own men lacked, boosting their egos and aspirations to be close to the Imperial Highness. Châtelet endured rudeness but maintained his position by engaging with the clergy and indulging the queen of Angoulême's whims, sharing literature and poetry with her. Mme. de Bargeton, enamored with the Bourbons' resurgence, longed for Paris's artistic brilliance. Châtelet, believing he had found a local talent, introduced her to Lucien, a young poet from L’Houmeau, whose potential he exaggerated.

Lucien, unaware of social barriers, was excited about visiting the Hôtel de Bargeton. His sister Eve prepared him for this significant occasion with new clothes and advice on aristocratic behavior. Upon entering, Lucien found the Hôtel de Bargeton charming yet less grand than expected. He met Mme. de Bargeton, who was captivated by his youthful beauty. Their initial meeting was enchanting, and Lucien was intoxicated by her presence. Mme. de Bargeton saw him as a poetic embodiment of her aspirations.

Châtelet, sensing a rival in Lucien, attempted to assert dominance through condescension. Despite this, Lucien continued to visit Mme. de Bargeton, growing more comfortable in her presence. Initially grateful for the nobles' attention, he soon recognized their patronizing attitudes, fueling his resentment towards the aristocracy.

Lucien's infatuation with Mme. de Bargeton deepened as she treated him with a mix of affection and aloofness. She encouraged his ambitions, presenting herself as a mentor while grappling with her own feelings. Their relationship oscillated between admiration and jealousy, with Lucien striving to prove himself worthy of her affection.

As their bond grew, Lucien began to express his feelings more openly, leading to moments of intimacy that thrilled and terrified him. Mme. de Bargeton, torn between her social standing and feelings for Lucien, struggled with the implications of their relationship. Societal scrutiny intensified, leading to gossip about their connection.

Despite challenges, Mme. de Bargeton defended Lucien against societal backlash, arguing that genius transcends social class. She hosted gatherings to showcase his talent, drawing both admiration and criticism from the local aristocracy. Lucien's rise in prominence elicited mixed reactions, with some nobles resenting his presence while others celebrated his potential.

Feeling threatened, Châtelet sought to undermine Lucien while trying to win over Mme. de Bargeton. He organized a dinner to elevate Lucien's status, hoping to manipulate the situation. However, Lucien's talent shone through, complicating the dynamics among the three.

As Lucien navigated this new world, he became increasingly aware of the sacrifices required for success. Mme. de Bargeton encouraged him to embrace his ambitions, suggesting he adopt a noble name to solidify his status. This idea ignited Lucien's desire for recognition and prestige, prompting him to reconsider his values and aspirations.

In a moment of vulnerability, Lucien penned a heartfelt letter to Mme. de Bargeton, expressing his hopes and dreams while seeking her support for his friend David. This letter revealed his inner turmoil and desire for validation, showcasing the complexities of his character as he balanced ambition with loyalty.

Ultimately, Lucien's journey was marked by the tension between his aspirations and the realities of social hierarchy. His relationship with Mme. de Bargeton served as both inspiration and a reminder of the obstacles he faced in his pursuit of greatness, unfolding against a backdrop of societal contradictions and challenges.

As Lucien descended from Angoulême, he was troubled by thoughts of Mme. de Bargeton’s potential anger and whether she would accept his friend David. He recognized the social divide between himself and the aristocracy, realizing that David could not easily bridge that gap. Accepting David into their circle could damage Mme. de Bargeton’s reputation, as mingling with lower classes was frowned upon.

Lucien's instincts warned him of possible ridicule directed at David, who lacked the refinement that Lucien possessed. This led him to reconsider introducing David to Mme. de Bargeton, and he began to regret inviting him, envisioning negative consequences.

Lucien reminisced about his simple life and his love for Mme. de Bargeton. He believed it would be more admirable to achieve success through his own efforts rather than relying on a woman’s favor, inspired by Napoleon’s rise through merit. He resolved to pursue his ambitions independently, feeling ashamed of his previous thoughts.

Returning to L’Houmeau, Lucien was discontented with his humble surroundings, especially as he prepared to meet Mme. de Bargeton. He longed to escape his father's pharmacy, which reminded him of his family's modest status. His father, M. Postel, often reminisced about Lucien’s late father, deepening Lucien's sense of inadequacy.

When Lucien received a letter from Mme. de Bargeton, he was ecstatic. The letter expressed her willingness to help David but also revealed her prejudices against those of lower social standing. Lucien interpreted this as a victory, believing that David would shine in her company. His sister Eve noticed his joy and teased him about his apparent happiness, sensing it was connected to a romantic interest.

Meanwhile, David, deeply in love with Eve, struggled with feelings of unworthiness due to his humble background. Despite his talent, he felt trapped by societal expectations. David was determined to support Lucien’s ambitions, even at the cost of his own desires.

As Lucien prepared for his public reading, David advised him to choose a different poem, fearing that his biblical work might not resonate with the audience. Lucien, filled with confidence, was eager to impress both the audience and Mme. de Bargeton.

Eve and David shared a tender moment, both feeling a deep connection but hesitant to express their feelings openly. Their love blossomed quietly, marked by mutual respect and admiration.

At the reading, Lucien was nervous but determined to make an impression. He was aware of the social dynamics at play, particularly the presence of M. de Bargeton, who relied on his wife for social navigation. As Lucien navigated the evening, he felt torn between his ambitions and the loyalty he owed to his family and friends.

Lucien struggled to connect with the imposing M. de Bargeton, whose silent authority made their conversation awkward. M. du Châtelet's arrival intensified Lucien's discomfort, as Châtelet’s elegance highlighted Lucien’s poverty. The gathering included notable guests, while Mme. de Bargeton engaged little with Lucien, favoring Châtelet instead.

When Lucien began his reading, he faced an indifferent audience. His initial poem received mild applause, but many were uninterested. Despite the lack of engagement, Lucien pressed on, driven by a desire to connect. He read passionately, but the response remained tepid, underscoring the contrast between his artistic aspirations and the mundane realities of provincial life.

Infatuated with Mme. de Bargeton, Lucien wrote an ode he believed rivaled Chénier's. As he recited it, he felt both pride and vulnerability, while Mme. de Bargeton worried about the audience's reaction. The poem was met with indifference and sarcasm, dismissed as typical youthful verse. Critics found Lucien's work obscure and unoriginal, and despite polite responses, the atmosphere remained hostile.

In a parallel narrative, Eve prepared for a meeting with David, who expressed his feelings for her during a walk by the Charente River. He voiced concerns about Lucien's ambitions and the potential pitfalls of his relationship with Mme. de Bargeton. However, Eve remained optimistic, believing they could support Lucien until he found success.

David proposed marriage to Eve, emphasizing that their union would enable him to help Lucien. He envisioned a future where they could provide for him, but Eve hesitated due to their differing social standings. David reassured her, promising to work hard for their future.

As the evening progressed, Lucien returned, burdened by humiliation. He shared his frustrations with Eve and David, expressing his determination to win over Mme. de Bargeton. Unbeknownst to him, their engagement posed a challenge to his aspirations, as he feared Eve's marriage would hinder his social ambitions. David's father opposed the engagement, insisting Eve's lack of wealth made her unsuitable. Despite this, David remained committed to Eve, determined to build a life together, intertwining themes of ambition, love, and the harsh realities of social class.

In Angoulême, Lucien's reputation soared after the Bishop's praise and Mme. de Bargeton’s endorsement, making him a local hero. He eagerly shared news of his sister Eve's upcoming marriage to David with Louise, hoping it would bring him joy. Louise admired his literary talents and expressed her desire to create a happy life for him, despite societal envy.

With Louise's encouragement, Lucien began to see himself as important. He asked her to be his Beatrice, and she playfully challenged him to prove his worth. Lucien shared Eve's love for David, which Louise accepted, emphasizing that their love was paramount.

As Lucien frequented the Hôtel de Bargeton, he enjoyed the luxuries of his new status and felt secure in his relationship with Louise, supported by M. de Bargeton. However, rumors about their closeness circulated, fueled by jealousy and gossip, highlighting societal disparities.

Meanwhile, David prepared for his marriage to Eve, using his savings to improve their home. Lucien, now living comfortably as M. de Rubempré, felt optimistic about his writing career, believing his literary ambitions would soon pay off, deepening his sense of entitlement.

Tensions escalated when Châtelet, a rival, began spying on Lucien and Louise, hoping to catch them in a compromising situation. Despite their innocent interactions, societal pressure mounted, leading Louise to consider drastic measures to escape scrutiny.

Châtelet's schemes culminated in a scandal when he and Stanislas claimed to have seen Lucien and Louise in a compromising position. The rumor spread rapidly, with exaggerated details shocking the townspeople.

Amidst the chaos, Mme. de Bargeton confided in her husband about the incident, urging him to confront Stanislas for his slanderous remarks. M. de Bargeton, motivated by his wife's honor, prepared to challenge Stanislas, aware of the potential consequences. This drama underscored the fragility of reputation in their small town, illustrating the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations.

Châtelet recognized the implications of a late-night visit and suspected Naïs's involvement. He discreetly approached M. de Bargeton to mediate with Stanislas, who welcomed the intervention. Châtelet advised Stanislas to maintain decorum when addressing Bargeton about his comments regarding Naïs.

When they reconvened, Bargeton confronted Stanislas about his claims. Stanislas, dismissive, faced a challenge: if he did not retract his statements, Bargeton would demand a duel at dawn. Cornered, Stanislas reluctantly agreed.

As they returned to the gathering, the atmosphere shifted; Châtelet appeared calm while Stanislas looked pale, prompting whispers among the guests about the duel. The men debated Bargeton’s right to challenge Stanislas, recalling Bargeton’s past as a skilled marksman. Châtelet reassured Stanislas, emphasizing the advantage of youth.

The next morning, news of the duel spread quickly. Mme. Chardon informed Lucien that Bargeton had shot Stanislas, who would suffer lasting injury. Lucien defended Mme. de Bargeton’s innocence, but public sentiment had turned against them. A note from Louise urged Lucien to avoid public appearances and suggested they spend the day together, hinting at the emotional turmoil following the duel.

Preparations for Eve’s wedding were underway, but Lucien felt torn between family obligations and his desire to be with Louise. Captivated by her plans to move to Paris, where they could thrive socially and artistically, she encouraged him to seize opportunities in the capital.

Initially hesitant due to his sister’s marriage, Lucien was inspired by Louise’s passionate arguments about their potential future in Paris. He envisioned escaping provincial stagnation for the vibrant culture of the city.

Despite his excitement, Lucien grappled with guilt over abandoning his family during such a significant moment. Louise’s disdain for his hesitation fueled his internal conflict. Ultimately, she insisted he prioritize their relationship over familial duties, leading to a heated exchange that left Lucien distressed.

As Lucien prepared to leave, he sought financial support from his family, realizing their sacrifices for him. His sister and mother were heartbroken at the thought of his departure but recognized the necessity of his ambitions. David offered to help financially, despite the strain it would place on their resources.

In the end, Lucien’s departure was bittersweet. He felt the weight of his family’s sacrifices and the uncertainty of his future in Paris. As he set out with David, he was filled with both hope and trepidation, aware that the path ahead was fraught with challenges but also ripe with potential for greatness.
