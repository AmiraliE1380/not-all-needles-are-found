At the story’s start the Stanhope press and roller were rare in provincial shops; in Angoulême, tied to Paris by its paper mills, only wooden presses were used—“the press groans” is literal: leather ink-balls applied the ink and the pressman inked by hand. This old mechanism, cherished by Jérôme‑Nicolas Séchard, anchors the tale.

Séchard, once a journeyman pressman, survived the Requisition of 1793, became sole worker after his master’s death, and—though illiterate—obtained a master‑printer’s license and bought the business cheaply from a Representative of the People. He partnered with the noble M. le Comte de Maucombe to print decrees discreetly. By 1795 he needed a compositor and hired an Abbé who later became a bishop. Despite his lack of learning, Séchard learned to run the shop, mastering costs and accumulating avarice: buying paper when cheap, becoming his own landlord, and acquiring the old printing house.

He had one son, David, educated at grammar school and in Paris, who excelled despite his father’s scorn for book learning. The shop held a regional monopoly on official printing until the Cointet Brothers sought a second license, threatening Séchard’s position. Confident in David, Séchard anticipated retirement.

Now in his seventies, drinking since his wife’s death, Séchard’s bloated face, cunning gaze, three‑cornered hat and old‑fashioned clothes matched his habits. The long‑established shop—a front room with a glass door to the workshop—drew customers who watched the process. The vine‑grown yard, a kitchen where inky runoff mixed with household waste, and cramped, unchanged rooms above reflected the old printer’s stinginess and relentless pursuit of profit. The sitting‑room, partly modernized by the late Mme. Séchard with powder‑blue wainscoting and Oriental paper, remained sparsely furnished; she had died before completing planned improvements.

Séchard brought David to read a valuation of the printing plant. David scoffed at the old presses and tossed paper aside; Séchard, affronted, demonstrated their efficiency and defended the value of the type—cast at M. Vaflard’s foundry and, after twelve years, nearly new. The inventory totaled thirty thousand francs, including license and goodwill—three times the ten thousand francs Séchard had once paid. Séchard announced a partnership deed: he would rent the premises to David for twelve hundred francs a year and reserve a room for himself. Cornered but thinking he could manage the debt, David accepted.

The next day Séchard had the apprentice move David’s belongings to the attic, leaving him three empty rooms and no funds to pay wages. When David asked for help, Séchard refused, saying he had already paid his share. Humiliated and alone, David resolved to work hard. Séchard spoke of a “treasure” left behind—Marion, a capable country girl who ran many shop tasks—and departed for his Marsac vineyard, fretting over the thirty thousand francs and visiting less often as the Cointet Brothers gained ground.

The Cointets, aligned with the clergy, monopolized the market and smeared David as a Liberal because of his father. They prospered and offered to buy the printing house; despite Séchard’s initial hopes to protect Liberal clientele with a high price, David was ultimately forced to sell, marking the establishment’s decline. David’s fortunes worsened when Lucien Chardon, an old school friend reduced to poverty, came for work and became a reader. Lucien, dreaming of poetry and recounting his father’s schemes for refining sugar and making paper, inspired ambitious projects that distracted them. While they pursued plans and conversation, the Cointets consolidated their advantage; Séchard & Son scraped by as the building and workshop fell into disrepair and the household grew increasingly neglected.

One May day in 1821, David and Lucien slipped outside the office to sit under vines while the others dined. Sunlight revealed their differences: David was robust, weathered, and cautious—shaped by humble origins and melancholy. Lucien was graceful, beautiful, and ambitious—delicate in appearance but shrewd and willing to risk dubious means to rise. Low in rank, both nourished loftier ideals and resented reality. Lucien’s impetuous, adventurous temperament usually led; David, stronger but introspective, admired him and often yielded. For three years their friendship thrived on literature and art: they read voraciously, worked hard, and dreamed of fame as escape from hardship.

David brought a new volume of André de Chénier that stirred them, offering kinship with the poet’s struggles and hope of transcendence. Their reverie ended when Cérizet ushered in a stranger seeking to print a monograph. Lucien eagerly engaged the author, who mentioned Mme. de Bargeton—a woman Lucien admired. He blushed, confessed passionate love, and vowed not to visit her without David’s consent—a pledge showing loyalty and pride. David warned of social barriers, but Lucien hoped love could bridge them.

Walking through Angoulême, Lucien reflected on the gulf between his origins and Mme. de Bargeton’s world. The city, perched on a crag, was split between an upper town of nobility and a lower town of commerce. The aristocracy clung to old privileges, scorned merchants, and resisted reform; the bourgeoisie resented this disdain. Despite some modernization, a rigid hierarchy bred tension as the middle class pressed for recognition.

In that climate, Lucien’s ambition to enter Mme. de Bargeton’s circle was daunting. A druggist’s son marked him an outsider, yet his literary talent, charm, and Mme. de Bargeton’s artistic tastes gave him hope. David combined admiration with concern: he understood Lucien’s depth but saw the obstacles. Lucien’s determination and his promise to include David in any future visits encapsulate their bond as love and ambition unfold against entrenched divisions.

M. de Bargeton descended from a Bordeaux alderman ennobled under Louis XIII, but the family declined after his grandfather, “the Waster,” squandered its wealth. By 1789 only entailed lands and a house in Angoulême remained. In 1805 he married Marie-Louise-Anaïs (Naïs), from a once-prominent family whose father lived frugally on her estate, farming and distilling brandy. Taught languages and music by the Abbé Niollant, Naïs developed independence and a haughty superiority. Though she resisted marriage, she accepted M. de Bargeton—well bred but limited—at thirty-six, while he was fifty-eight, to pursue social ambitions. Expecting an inheritance and Parisian life that never came, she sank into petty provincial existence, theatrical affectations, and solitude hardened by the death of a young officer she loved—until the arrival of M. du Châtelet, a charming newcomer who used music to ingratiate himself and eyed Naïs as a potential wealthy widow, scandalizing the local elite and reviving her appetite for excitement.

Political opinion in town was loud and exaggerated: the Quotidienne showed only tepid loyalty, Louis XVIII was mocked, society women were awkward and ill-spoken, and in the countryside old nobility masked many defects while Bourbon loyalty remained a proud provincial badge.

Châtelet, seeking solace in society and proximity to Mme. de Bargeton (Naïs), flattered many women and embodied what their husbands lacked; all coveted his company. To keep his place he endured insolence, courted the clergy, pandered to the queen of Angoulême’s petty tastes, and shared new literature. Naïs, charmed by the Bourbon revival but craving Parisian brilliance, learned from Châtelet about a promising young poet in Angoulême.

The young poet Lucien, praised by his headmaster, expected a warm reception at the Hôtel de Bargeton. His sister Éve secretly supported him—saving for shoes and clothes and coaching him in aristocratic manners—leaving him proud and anxious. At the Hôtel de Bargeton Lucien found the house less grand than imagined, but Naïs embodied the poetry he loved. She admired his beauty and diffidence; their meeting brimmed with mutual attraction, and Lucien overlooked her faded airs, sensing intimacy.

Feeling a rival, Châtelet asserted influence over Lucien, offering unsolicited advice and claiming Naïs as his own. Despite Châtelet’s intimidation, Lucien continued to visit—at first flattered by noble condescension, later resentful. Naïs, treating Lucien as a project, became patroness and confidante: she spoke to him intimately, encouraged his literary ambitions, introduced him to music and books, and revealed vulnerabilities. A portrait of a dead lover provoked jealousy, yet Lucien declared his feelings and their relationship advanced.

When their liaison became public the town buzzed. Châtelet tried to undermine Lucien; Naïs defended him, arguing genius can rise from humble origins. To launch him she hosted salons and urged him to adopt the noble name Rubempré, promising courtly patronage. Exhilarated, Lucien’s aspirations widened; earlier egalitarian ideals yielded to social ambition. In a passionate letter he asked Naïs’s help for a friend, David, revealing his conflict between love, artistic sincerity, and the pursuit of recognition.

Descending from Angoulême, Lucien worried Naïs might be angry and whether she would receive David. He sensed the vast social gulf: receiving David could ruin Naïs by associating her with commoners. Lucien’s aristocratic instinct noted that noble bearing did not equal noble feeling; David lacked the graces and would suffer ridicule. Mme. de Bargeton’s reply offered help but showed prejudice toward lower standing—Lucien read it as triumph. David, feeling unworthy, declined the invitation, preferring his trade and affirming the social distance. His humility moved Lucien, yet ambition began to eclipse gratitude.

Preparing for his first public reading, Lucien and David chose different poems. David and Éve shared a quiet moment hinting at mutual affection. At the reading Lucien met the awkward, ineffectual M. de Bargeton, whose shallow pleasantries underscored the social dynamics Lucien must navigate. The evening, marked by Lucien’s ambition and strained loyalties, set the stage for future conflicts and aspirations.

There was talk of nominating the mute gentleman for deputy, but Lucien, who had only briefly visited, had not yet fathomed him. M. de Bargeton, reclining and silent, observed everything with a cool reserve that awed Lucien; imaginative, Lucien fancied him a sphinx and treated him deferentially.

“I am the first comer,” Lucien said, bowing more than usual.

“That is natural enough,” Bargeton replied—a remark Lucien took as a sly barb and which made him self-conscious. When Lucien asked why those from farther away arrived earlier, Bargeton shrugged and lapsed into silence. Lucien tried to draw him out—guessing Mme. de Bargeton was dressing, fretting over the room’s preparations and his own attire, even lingering by a Japanese jar—but Bargeton’s curt answers and intent scrutiny unnerved him.

The old servant announced “M. du Châtelet.” The Baron entered with easy confidence and inspected Lucien’s clothes, deepening his discomfort. Sixte du Châtelet, fadingly stylish, dismissed Lucien’s poetry as vaudeville. Mme. de Bargeton soon appeared, elaborately dressed and theatrically charming; Châtelet’s compliments delighted her, while she scarcely acknowledged Lucien.

Guests arrived in parade: the dignified Bishop and his Vicar‑General; Mme. de Chandour with her eccentric husband and rivalrous Amélie; the solemn M. de Saintot (Astolphe) with austere Élisa; music‑seeking M. de Bartas; the artist Alexandre de Brébian and his wife; fashion‑obsessed Lolotte and Fifine with dowdy husbands. The Comte de Senonches came with Zéphirine, tall and striking despite ill health; Francis, a devoted household friend, behaved unusually attentive to her. The wealthy Marquis de Pimentel and the Baron and Baroness de Rastignac received lavish attention from Mme. de Bargeton. The Prefect and the General arrived last, followed by a nervous country gentleman with a treatise on silkworms. Mme. and Mlle. du Brossard hovered, pressing social ties from poverty, while the childless widower M. de Séverac spoke of silkworm nurseries.

Throughout the evening Lucien felt scrutinized and diminished—most called him M. Chardon rather than M. de Rubempré, a stinging slight. The assembly was absorbed in flattery and rivalry, indifferent to his art, leaving him isolated.

When he read, excitement mixed with anxiety. Announcing poems by André de Chénier, he was misunderstood as affectation. Le Malade drew mild applause; L’Aveugle fell flat amid impatience. Sweating, Lucien drew courage from Louise’s supportive glance and proceeded to Chénier’s Iambes, which finally captured some attention though many missed the meaning. During the break Zéphirine sent Francis to inquire after the poems, and women whispered about Lucien’s origins. Amélie’s remarks about his noble name and printing background sparked gossip that complicated his standing, while Lucien, absorbed in the poet’s beauty, remained largely unaware of the mounting tension.

Lucien tried to immortalize his love for Mme. de Bargeton with an ode he fancied rivaled Chénier’s. Presenting it under her protective gaze, he felt secure while she privately fretted for him. The audience, however, was unimpressed: the Baron dismissed the poem as youthful—ornate but empty—while the women feigned admiration, their sarcastic whispers exposing contempt. Francis, a shrewd observer, spread rumors that Lucien’s mother had written the verse. The Bishop’s unwitting praise only intensified the aristocrats’ scorn. Overwhelmed and alienated, Lucien misread people and bore their disdain.

As the evening moved to games and music, Mme. de Bargeton, stung by her peers, withdrew with Lucien to her boudoir and consoled him, romanticizing suffering as the path to immortality and urging him to turn pain into art.

Meanwhile Lucien’s sister Eve and her lover David debated his ambitions and the danger of the liaison. David feared society would ruin Lucien; Eve hoped their support could sustain him. When David proposed, Eve hesitated over their unequal standings but accepted, moved by love and a wish to help. David vowed to provide so Lucien wouldn’t be alone. Frustrated, Lucien resolved to win Mme. de Bargeton’s love and rise above the aristocrats.

In Angoulême the Bishop’s epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s rejoinder made Lucien a local celebrity. Walking through Beaulieu he was envied and flattered. He told Louise of Eve’s pending marriage; she greeted him passionately, praised his reading, promised a poetic refuge, and urged him toward greatness. Calling himself M. de Rubempré, he enjoyed Louise’s intimacies and M. de Bargeton’s patronage and expected a literary breakthrough.

Eve and David postponed their wedding to support him. Their closeness drew Châtelet’s attention; he manipulated a misread outburst seen by Stanislas, which sparked gossip. Alarmed, Mme. de Bargeton pressed her husband to act. Though insecure, M. de Bargeton confronted Stanislas, winning respect and setting the stage for a confrontation that would entangle lives and expose the town’s fraught mix of love, honor, and social expectation.

Châtelet, noticing the gravity of a late-night visit to the bedbound M. de Bargeton and suspecting Naïs’s involvement, brought Bargeton to Amélie’s room after Bargeton asked to see Stanislas. Bargeton demanded a retraction about Mme. de Bargeton and M. de Rubempré; Stanislas, cornered, accepted a duel and chose pistols. Châtelet warned Stanislas to be decorous before the confrontation.

The duel altered the house’s mood and divided blame. Amélie, sensing the tension, fell ill and was taken away. Guests debated the consequences, recalling Bargeton’s former skill with firearms. Mme. Chardon soon arrived to report that Bargeton had nearly killed Stanislas—who was shot in the neck and left injured—after accusing Bargeton of compromising behavior. Lucien defended Mme. de Bargeton’s innocence.

Lucien then received a note from Louise urging him to avoid her home because of the duel’s fallout and to spend the day with her. David, preparing for his marriage, cautioned Lucien to limit visits to Mme. de Bargeton. Visiting her, Lucien found her at breakfast with her husband and M. de Nègrepelisse, who watched him closely. Louise revealed she planned to go to Paris to secure a political post for Bargeton, believing leaving Angoulême would contain the scandal, and pressed Lucien to join her.

Torn between Paris and family duty—Eve’s imminent wedding and their mother’s concerns—Lucien hesitated; after a heated exchange Louise fainted and he promised discretion and to be ready to leave the next night. Eve and their mother lamented his likely departure and wedding costs; David offered help and agreed to endorse a loan. They arranged to send Lucien’s belongings to Paris secretly. Suspecting Mme. de Bargeton’s departure, Châtelet sent someone to follow her. Early next morning Lucien left with David, who feigned business, as Mme. de Bargeton’s carriage approached—Lucien torn between anticipation and dread.
