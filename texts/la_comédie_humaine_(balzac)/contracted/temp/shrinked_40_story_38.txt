*Two Poets* is the first part of a trilogy focusing on Lucien, his sister Eve, and their friend David in Angoulême during the era of traditional printing. The story centers on Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, a former journeyman pressman who runs a small printing business after obtaining a license during the French Revolution, allowing him to operate without formal education.

Séchard transformed from a struggling pressman into a savvy businessman, motivated by greed and a desire to secure a future for his son, David. After excelling academically, David was sent to Paris to learn the printing trade, but Séchard dismissed formal education in favor of practical experience. Upon returning in 1819, David was ready to take over the family business, which had a monopoly on official printing. However, competition arose when the Cointet Brothers sought a second printing license, threatening Séchard's dominance.

As Séchard aged, his greed intensified, complicating his relationship with David, whom he viewed as both heir and rival. This duality strained their bond, as Séchard's cunning overshadowed paternal affection while negotiating the sale of the business.

In the sitting room of the late Mme. Séchard, an inebriated Séchard brought David to review the valuation of the printing business. David was shocked to find the equipment overvalued at sixteen hundred francs, deeming the old presses unworthy. Séchard insisted on their reliability, dismissing David's preference for modern machinery. In the workshop, Séchard demonstrated the presses' functionality, but David recognized their outdatedness and felt trapped in an unfair negotiation.

Sensing David's hesitation, Séchard grew anxious, believing silence indicated acceptance. He emphasized the value of the business license and potential profits, leading David to agree to terms that included a partnership allowing Séchard to retain a room and share profits until the purchase was fully paid.

After moving in, David faced financial strain as Séchard refused to contribute to operational costs, forcing David to manage alone. This led to a painful realization about his father's greed and lack of support. Meanwhile, Séchard, now living in the countryside, became increasingly anxious about the business's success, frequently visiting David, who struggled against competition from the Cointet Brothers.

David's friendship with Lucien Chardon, a talented but impoverished young man, blossomed as they shared aspirations. David offered Lucien a position in the printing house, hoping to help him succeed. Their friendship deepened through discussions of innovative ideas, though their intellectual pursuits distracted them from practicalities.

The printing house's physical state reflected Séchard's avarice, as he neglected repairs, mirroring the struggles faced by David and Lucien amid familial and financial pressures. In May 1821, David and Lucien sought refuge from their oppressive environment, contrasting David's robust yet melancholic demeanor with Lucien's delicate beauty.

Their artistic pursuits shifted when a stranger arrived with a manuscript for printing, claiming to be an author sent by Mme. de Bargeton, the woman Lucien admired. Despite his feelings, Lucien recognized social barriers and vowed not to pursue her unless David was welcomed too. Lucien wandered through Angoulême, contemplating societal divides and his aspirations to rise above his humble origins, influenced by Mme. de Bargeton. The narrative sets the stage for Lucien's struggle against societal constraints, driven by his passion for poetry and love for Mme. de Bargeton, while David embodies loyalty and sacrifice.

M. de Bargeton, from a once-prominent Bordeaux family, faced decline due to his grandfather's extravagance. By 1789, the family retained only seignorial rights to their Angoumois lands, yielding ten thousand francs annually. He married Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, a noblewoman from an obscure lineage. Her miserly father relied on his wife’s estate, while Anaïs received a broad education from Abbé Niollant, who nurtured her love for music and literature.

Anaïs, or Naïs, developed a strong sense of self-worth and disdain for others, leading to a haughty demeanor ill-suited for provincial life. After the Abbé's death in 1802, she confronted her father's miserly nature and her rebellious spirit against marriage constraints. M. de Nègrepelisse sought a husband for her, ultimately choosing M. de Bargeton, who was older and perceived as lacking intellect.

Naïs married M. de Bargeton, hoping for freedom and social connections in Paris, but their age difference and limited income constrained their lives in Angoulême. Over time, her vibrant qualities became absurdities, and her artistic inclinations turned into exaggerated expressions of emotion. Isolated and longing for excitement, her disappointing marriage left her yearning for love. The rise of the Empire rekindled her hopes, leading her to fall for a young officer, whose death in battle deepened her sorrow.

Mme. de Bargeton’s life became a cycle of unfulfilled dreams and artistic aspirations, leading her to seek solace in literature and the arts. Despite her intellectual gifts, she remained trapped in a narrow existence, her dignity morphing into affectation. The arrival of M. du Châtelet, a charming but superficial man, disrupted her life. He recognized her potential as a social asset, while the local bourgeoisie disapproved of his presence.

M. du Châtelet’s intriguing background allowed him to cultivate a relationship with Mme. de Bargeton, seeing her as a means to elevate his own status. His arrival highlighted tensions within Angoulême society, where intellectual poverty coexisted with remnants of decayed gentility. Some women admired Châtelet for qualities their own men lacked, boosting their aspirations to be close to the Imperial Highness. Châtelet engaged with the clergy and indulged the queen of Angoulême's whims, sharing literature and poetry with her.

Mme. de Bargeton, enamored with the Bourbons' resurgence, longed for Paris's artistic brilliance. Châtelet introduced her to Lucien, a young poet from L’Houmeau, whose potential he exaggerated. Lucien approached the Hôtel de Bargeton with excitement, meeting Mme. de Bargeton, who was captivated by his youthful beauty.

Châtelet, sensing a rival in Lucien, attempted to assert dominance, but Lucien continued to visit Mme. de Bargeton, growing more comfortable. Initially grateful for the nobles' attention, he soon recognized their patronizing attitudes, fueling his resentment towards the aristocracy. His infatuation with Mme. de Bargeton deepened as she treated him with affection and aloofness.

As their bond grew, Lucien expressed his feelings more openly, leading to moments of intimacy that thrilled and terrified him. Mme. de Bargeton, torn between her social standing and feelings for Lucien, struggled with the implications of their relationship. Societal scrutiny intensified, leading to gossip about them. Despite challenges, she defended Lucien against societal backlash, arguing that genius transcends social class.

She hosted gatherings to showcase his talent, drawing mixed reactions from the local aristocracy. Feeling threatened, Châtelet sought to undermine Lucien while trying to win over Mme. de Bargeton. He organized a dinner to elevate Lucien's status, but Lucien's talent shone through, complicating the dynamics among the three.

Ultimately, Lucien's journey was marked by the tension between his aspirations and the realities of social hierarchy. His relationship with Mme. de Bargeton served as both inspiration and a reminder of the obstacles he faced in his pursuit of greatness, unfolding against a backdrop of societal contradictions and challenges.

As Lucien descended from Angoulême, he was troubled by thoughts of Mme. de Bargeton’s potential anger and whether she would accept his friend David. He recognized the social divide between himself and the aristocracy, realizing that David's lower status could tarnish Mme. de Bargeton’s reputation. Lucien feared ridicule for David, who lacked the refinement he possessed, leading him to reconsider the introduction.

Lucien reminisced about his simple life and his love for Mme. de Bargeton, believing it more admirable to achieve success through his own efforts rather than relying on a woman’s favor. Inspired by Napoleon’s rise through merit, he resolved to pursue his ambitions independently, feeling ashamed of his previous thoughts.

Upon returning to L’Houmeau, Lucien felt discontented with his humble surroundings, especially as he prepared to meet Mme. de Bargeton. He longed to escape his father's pharmacy, which reminded him of his family's modest status. His father, M. Postel, often reminisced about Lucien’s late father, deepening Lucien's sense of inadequacy.

When Lucien received a letter from Mme. de Bargeton, he was ecstatic. The letter expressed her willingness to help David but also revealed her prejudices against those of lower social standing. Lucien saw this as a victory, believing David would shine in her company. His sister Eve noticed his joy and teased him about his apparent happiness, sensing it was connected to a romantic interest.

Meanwhile, David, deeply in love with Eve, struggled with feelings of unworthiness due to his humble background. Despite his talent, he felt trapped by societal expectations but was determined to support Lucien’s ambitions, even at the cost of his own desires.

As Lucien prepared for his public reading, David advised him to choose a different poem, fearing his biblical work might not resonate with the audience. Lucien, filled with confidence, was eager to impress both the audience and Mme. de Bargeton.

Eve and David shared a tender moment, both feeling a deep connection but hesitant to express their feelings openly. Their love blossomed quietly, marked by mutual respect and admiration.

At the reading, Lucien was nervous but determined to make an impression. He was aware of the social dynamics at play, particularly the presence of M. de Bargeton, who relied on his wife for social navigation. Lucien felt torn between his ambitions and loyalty to his family and friends. David’s selflessness and Eve’s quiet strength reminded him of the love surrounding him, even as he sought to carve out his identity.

Lucien struggled to connect with M. de Bargeton, whose silent authority made their conversation awkward. The arrival of M. du Châtelet intensified Lucien's discomfort, highlighting his poverty. The gathering included notable guests like the Bishop and Mme. de Chandour, with Mme. de Bargeton favoring Châtelet over Lucien. The evening was filled with provincial elites, each displaying their quirks, while discussions often strayed from Lucien’s poetry.

When Lucien finally began his reading, he faced an indifferent audience. His initial poem received mild applause, but many were uninterested, undermining his confidence. Despite the lack of engagement, Lucien pressed on, driven by a desire to connect. His passionate reading was met with indifference, leaving him feeling isolated amid social pretensions.

Infatuated with Mme. de Bargeton, Lucien wrote an ode he believed rivaled Chénier's. However, the poem was met with indifference and sarcasm from the aristocratic crowd, who dismissed it as typical youthful verse. Critics found Lucien's work obscure and unoriginal, and despite polite responses, the atmosphere remained hostile. Stung by rejection, Lucien vowed to prove himself worthy of high society.

In a parallel narrative, Eve prepared for a meeting with David, who expressed his feelings for her during a walk. He voiced concerns about Lucien's ambitions and the potential pitfalls of his relationship with Mme. de Bargeton. However, Eve remained optimistic, believing they could support Lucien until he found success.

David proposed marriage to Eve, emphasizing that their union would enable him to help Lucien. He envisioned a future where they could provide for him, but Eve hesitated due to their differing social standings. David reassured her, promising to work hard for their future.

As the evening progressed, Lucien returned, burdened by humiliation. He shared his frustrations with Eve and David, expressing his determination to win over Mme. de Bargeton. Unbeknownst to him, their engagement posed a challenge to his aspirations, as he feared Eve's marriage would hinder his social ambitions. David's father opposed the engagement, insisting Eve's lack of wealth made her unsuitable. Despite this, David remained committed to Eve, determined to build a life together, intertwining themes of ambition, love, and the harsh realities of social class.

In Angoulême, Lucien's status soared after the Bishop's epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s response, making him a local hero. He eagerly shared news of Eve's engagement to David with Louise, who supported his ambitions and expressed a desire to create a joyful life for him. Lucien, buoyed by Louise's affection, began to see himself as significant and asked her to be his Beatrice. She accepted his news about Eve and David, emphasizing their love's importance.

As Lucien frequented the Hôtel de Bargeton, he enjoyed the luxuries of his newfound status and felt secure in his relationship with Louise. However, rumors about their closeness began to circulate, fueled by jealousy and the town's gossip culture. The disparity in societal expectations became apparent, with some acting freely while others faced scrutiny.

Meanwhile, David prepared for his marriage to Eve, improving their home with his savings. Lucien, now living as M. de Rubempré, felt optimistic about his writing career, believing his family's sacrifices entitled him to success. However, rival Châtelet began spying on Lucien and Louise, hoping to catch them in a compromising situation. Despite their innocent interactions, tension grew, and Louise contemplated drastic measures to escape scrutiny.

Châtelet's schemes led to a scandal when he and Stanislas claimed to have seen Lucien and Louise in a compromising position. The rumor spread rapidly, with townspeople eagerly dissecting the situation. Mme. de Bargeton confided in her husband about the incident, urging him to confront Stanislas for his slanderous remarks. M. de Bargeton, motivated by his wife's honor, prepared to challenge Stanislas, aware of the lasting consequences.

Châtelet recognized the implications of a late-night visit and suspected Naïs's involvement. He discreetly approached M. de Bargeton, who agreed to confront Stanislas about his claims. When they reconvened, Bargeton challenged Stanislas to retract his statements or face a duel at dawn. Cornered, Stanislas reluctantly agreed.

The next morning, news of the duel spread quickly. Mme. Chardon informed Lucien that Bargeton had shot Stanislas, who would suffer lasting injury. Public sentiment turned against Lucien and Mme. de Bargeton. A note from Louise urged Lucien to avoid public appearances and suggested they spend the day together, hinting at emotional turmoil following the duel.

Preparations for Eve’s wedding continued, but Lucien felt torn between family obligations and his desire to be with Louise. Captivated by her plans to move to Paris, where they could thrive socially and artistically, she encouraged him to seize opportunities in the capital. Initially hesitant due to his sister’s marriage, Lucien was inspired by Louise’s passionate arguments about their potential future.

Despite his excitement, Lucien grappled with guilt over abandoning his family. Louise’s disdain for his hesitation fueled his internal conflict, leading to a heated exchange that left him distressed. As he prepared to leave, he sought financial support from his family, realizing their sacrifices. His family was heartbroken but recognized the necessity of his ambitions, with David offering financial help despite the strain it would cause.

Ultimately, Lucien’s departure was bittersweet. He felt the weight of his family’s sacrifices and the uncertainty of his future in Paris, filled with both hope and trepidation as he set out with David, aware of the challenges ahead but also the potential for greatness.
