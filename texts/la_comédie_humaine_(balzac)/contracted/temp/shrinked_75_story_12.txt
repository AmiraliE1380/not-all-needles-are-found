**ALBERT SAVARUS**  
*By Honore de Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage*  

**DEDICATION**  
To Madame Emile Girardin  

In the drawing-room of the Baronne de Watteville, a prominent figure in Besançon society, the Archbishop of Besançon was a frequent guest due to her devout nature. Monsieur de Watteville, a descendant of a notorious historical figure, was a gentle and unremarkable man, married to the wealthy heiress Mademoiselle de Rupt. Their marriage in 1815 brought together their families' fortunes, with Madame de Watteville becoming increasingly pious over time.

Monsieur de Watteville, a turner by trade, also collected geological specimens, a hobby some deemed eccentric. The couple's home, a blend of their noble heritages, was filled with fine furnishings and exquisite food, reflecting their social status. Madame de Watteville’s close ties with the Archbishop attracted several notable clergy to their gatherings.

During a ceremonial dinner in September 1834, the guests eagerly welcomed Monsieur l’Abbe de Grancey, who announced a surprising legal victory regarding a lawsuit. He credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron, for his success against a formidable opponent from Paris. This revelation piqued the interest of the guests, particularly Madame de Watteville, who inquired about Savaron’s background. The Vicar-General noted that Savaron lived nearby, but his origins were unknown, raising further curiosity among the attendees.

The conversation highlighted Savaron's remarkable abilities and the unexpected triumph of the church over liberal opposition, setting the stage for deeper exploration of his character and the unfolding events in Besançon.

Throughout history, France and England have engaged in a playful exchange of cultural influences, particularly in language and fashion, often circumventing customs regulations. Styles deemed "English" in Paris are considered "French" in London, highlighting a mutual fascination. For instance, the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli, while the hoop skirts that became fashionable in Paris were introduced by an Englishwoman but originated from a French invention.

In recent years, England has contributed new terms to the French lexicon, such as "dandy" and "lion." The latter term, popularized by Alfred de Musset's poetry, describes a fashionable man who embodies a certain flamboyance. This trend spread beyond Paris, influencing provincial towns like Besançon, where Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas emerged as a local "lion." Amedee, a young man of Spanish descent, sought to elevate his status in a town characterized by its military and conservative culture.

Living modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment, Amedee maintained a lavish lifestyle with the help of his young servant, Babylas, whom he dressed in stylish attire. Despite his extravagant appearance, Amedee struggled financially, managing to live on a limited income from his father's estate. He was perceived as a spendthrift, yet he cleverly balanced his expenses, prioritizing appearances over substance.

Amedee's social life revolved around impressing the local aristocracy, particularly aiming to win the affection of Rosalie de Watteville, a young woman from a wealthy family. Rosalie, raised in a strict household, was largely sheltered from the outside world, receiving a limited education focused on religious and domestic skills. At eighteen, she was a delicate figure, embodying the ideals of modesty and virtue, yet she harbored a strong spirit beneath her reserved exterior.

The dynamic between Amedee and Rosalie was complicated by their respective social standings and familial expectations. Amedee's aspirations to marry Rosalie were fueled by her substantial dowry, which he believed would secure his financial future. Meanwhile, Rosalie's mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective of her daughter, scrutinizing Amedee's intentions and maintaining strict control over Rosalie's interactions.

Despite Amedee's efforts to charm Rosalie and her mother, he faced challenges in gaining their approval. His attempts to present himself as a cultured and well-traveled gentleman were met with skepticism, as Rosalie remained indifferent to his advances. The tension between them was exacerbated by Madame de Watteville's rigid expectations and Rosalie's own internal struggles with her feelings.

As Amedee navigated the complexities of social life in Besançon, he became a figure of intrigue and admiration among the local elite. His fashionable attire and social maneuvers earned him a reputation as the most charming young man in town, yet his true motivations remained obscured. Ultimately, Amedee's pursuit of Rosalie was not merely a quest for love but a strategic move to elevate his social standing and secure a prosperous future.

In summary, the interplay of fashion, social status, and personal ambition shaped the lives of Amedee de Soulas and Rosalie de Watteville in the conservative milieu of Besançon. Their story reflects the broader themes of aspiration, societal expectations, and the complexities of human relationships in a changing cultural landscape.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Monsieur de Soulas recounted to the Baroness about a man named Albert Savaron, who arrived in Besançon from Paris, rented an apartment, and registered as a barrister. Mademoiselle de Watteville noted the prestigious lineage of the Savarons, but Amedee de Soulas clarified that Savaron was a Frenchman without noble birth, needing to add a bar-sinister to his name to claim any nobility.

Despite his qualifications, Savaron was largely ignored by the local legal community, who were puzzled by his presence. He worked diligently, rising early and keeping to himself, which led to his being forgotten. He attended early mass but otherwise remained a mystery. Monsieur de Soulas, who lived nearby, learned about Savaron through his servant and local gossip.

Savaron's reputation changed when he successfully defended a mentally challenged man accused of forgery, impressing the court and gaining clients. The Vicar-General, Abbe de Grancey, described Savaron's striking appearance and eloquence, noting his calm demeanor despite a passionate nature. His living conditions were modest, contrasting with his impressive intellect and oratory skills.

Madame de Chavoncourt expressed curiosity about Savaron's motivations for coming to Besançon, questioning why he had not made more effort to integrate into the community. The Abbe speculated that Savaron would eventually make a significant impact.

Rosalie de Watteville, intrigued by Savaron's character, found herself drawn to him, contrasting him with the more superficial suitors she knew. She became determined to see him, leading her to devise a plan to encounter him at church. After a brief confession with the Abbe, she caught a glimpse of Savaron during mass, feeling an intense connection.

As Rosalie's fascination grew, she encouraged her father to build a Belvedere in their garden, which would allow her to observe Savaron's private life. The project garnered attention in Besançon, with many speculating about the lavishness of the construction.

Meanwhile, Savaron established a fortnightly publication, the _Eastern Review_, aimed at promoting Besançon's interests and competing with larger cities. He cleverly managed the publication without drawing attention to himself, gaining support from local merchants.

Rosalie's father subscribed to the _Eastern Review_, allowing her to read its contents, including a tale attributed to Savaron. This reading experience captivated Rosalie, as she sought to uncover more about Savaron's life and emotions through his writing, deepening her infatuation with him. 

In summary, the narrative revolves around the arrival of Albert Savaron in Besançon, his initial obscurity, and eventual rise to prominence through his legal prowess and literary endeavors, all while capturing the attention and affection of Rosalie de Watteville, who becomes enchanted by his mysterious persona.

**AMBITION FOR LOVE’S SAKE - Summary**

In July 1823, two young men, Rodolphe and Leopold, set out from Lucerne for a holiday in Switzerland, planning to explore the scenic beauty of the Four Cantons. As they traveled by boat, Rodolphe became captivated by a new house in Gersau, where he spotted a woman, Fanny Lovelace, looking out. He decided to stay in Gersau, while Leopold continued their journey.

Rodolphe and Leopold were close friends, with contrasting backgrounds. Leopold was destined for a career as a notary in Paris, while Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, was raised by a devoted mother who sacrificed everything for his education. Rodolphe was passionate and sensitive, driven by his desires and dreams.

After breakfast at the Swan Inn, Rodolphe found lodging with the Stopfer couple, who offered him a room overlooking the lake. He learned more about Fanny Lovelace, who had moved to Gersau with her elderly father for his health. They lived modestly, relying on Fanny's work as a translator and writer. Rodolphe was intrigued by her beauty and musical talent.

Eager to meet Fanny, Rodolphe sought permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens, where Fanny and her mute servant, Gina, lived. Initially met with suspicion, he eventually gained access. While exploring, he noticed Gina's unusual appearance and suspected she had a hidden background. 

One evening, while listening to Fanny sing, Rodolphe overheard her speaking Italian with Gina, leading him to suspect they were Italian refugees. When he approached them, he was attacked and wounded by Gina, who mistook him for a threat. Fanny and Gina helped him recover, and he was drawn to Fanny's beauty and kindness.

As Rodolphe healed, he learned of his mother’s death, which plunged him into despair. Leopold returned to France, leaving Rodolphe in Gersau, where he found solace in Fanny's company. Their bond deepened as they shared their sorrows and dreams. Fanny revealed she was married to an older man, Emilio, but expressed hope for a future together.

Rodolphe's feelings for Fanny grew stronger, and he admired her strength and independence. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential. Their conversations revealed a shared passion for freedom and liberal ideals, further connecting them.

One day, Fanny received news that her family was no longer in danger, and they would soon return to Italy. This news brought joy to Fanny and Gina, but left Rodolphe feeling abandoned. He struggled with jealousy and insecurity as Fanny's past and her relationship with Emilio became clearer.

Despite the challenges, Rodolphe remained devoted to Fanny, vowing to wait for her. Their love blossomed amidst the backdrop of the stunning Swiss landscape, symbolizing both their hopes and the obstacles they faced. The story explores themes of ambition, love, and the complexities of human relationships against the backdrop of political turmoil and personal sacrifice.

For twenty days, Rodolphe spent his time at the Bergmanns’, captivated by Francesca, who embodied both youthful imprudence and the complexities of womanhood. He observed her relationship with her husband, Emilio, which was marked by gratitude and a fatherly affection. This dynamic intrigued Rodolphe, who found himself increasingly drawn to Francesca.

Their interactions were filled with secret joys and occasional quarrels, which Rodolphe found delightful. One evening, Francesca expressed her longing for luxury, comparing it to her love for art and beauty. She insisted on not accompanying Rodolphe to Geneva, fearing gossip that could tarnish her husband’s reputation. She declared her love for Rodolphe but emphasized the importance of maintaining discretion to protect their bond.

The next day, Rodolphe learned that the Bergmanns had left, prompting him to leave Gersau for Geneva. He settled in Eaux-Vives and inquired about Italian refugees, particularly a bookseller named Lamporani. His curiosity led him to the Villa Diodati, where he encountered Francesca in an elegant carriage, revealing her connection to the aristocratic Colonna family.

Rodolphe’s realization of Francesca’s true identity struck him hard, as he grappled with feelings of inadequacy for having loved a princess disguised as a commoner. He sought an audience with Prince Gandolphini, Francesca’s husband, who welcomed him warmly. Despite his desire to see Francesca, Rodolphe returned to his lodgings, consumed by thoughts of their future.

When Rodolphe finally saw Francesca at a gathering, her beauty captivated him. Their eyes met, igniting a profound connection that transcended their surroundings. Francesca’s subtle acknowledgment of their bond amidst the social elite deepened Rodolphe’s feelings, solidifying his love for her.

As they spent time together, Francesca revealed her understanding of Rodolphe’s ambition and desire for greatness, encouraging him to pursue his dreams while remaining youthful. Their conversations reflected a shared commitment to love faithfully, regardless of the challenges they faced.

Francesca’s background as a well-educated heiress, initially destined for a cloistered life, added depth to her character. Her marriage to Prince Gandolphini was a strategic move to preserve family status, yet her revolutionary spirit aligned with Rodolphe’s aspirations.

Winter passed quickly for Rodolphe in Geneva, but as spring approached, he struggled with feelings of longing and anxiety. Francesca’s calm demeanor contrasted with his emotional turmoil, leading him to question her feelings. Their discussions revealed the complexities of their love, with Francesca asserting her Italian identity and pride.

One day, a moment of tension arose when Rodolphe compared Francesca to a French noblewoman, inadvertently hurting her feelings. He sensed her distress and sought to comfort her, but she maintained her dignity, instructing him to leave until they could meet again in Naples.

This separation marked a pivotal moment in their relationship, highlighting the challenges they faced as lovers from different worlds. Francesca’s strength and resolve left a lasting impression on Rodolphe, who was determined to prove himself worthy of her love.

Upon returning to Paris, Rodolphe discovered a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner, who had surprisingly agreed to paint it for her. Francesca's charm had convinced the artist to create an original for Rodolphe and a duplicate for Emilio, leading to a regular correspondence between Rodolphe and Francesca. Driven by ambition and love, Rodolphe invested all his resources in a venture that ultimately failed, costing him three years. After the Villele ministry fell, he sought solace in Naples, where he enjoyed time at the Villa Gandolphini. Just as he was poised for success, the July 1830 revolution struck, thwarting his plans. Despite setbacks, Rodolphe perseveres, supported by love.

As Mademoiselle de Watteville concluded her reading, she was consumed by rage and jealousy over Francesca Colonna, the woman she believed Albert loved. This was her first encounter with such a passionate narrative, and it ignited a fierce desire within her to fight for Albert's affection. Despite her feelings, she doubted her worthiness, believing she lacked beauty and musical talent, leading her to think, “He will never love me!”

That night, Rosalie's imagination ran wild with schemes to connect with Albert, from sneaking into his garden to writing him a letter. She even considered creating a lawsuit to bring him into her social circle. As she gazed at Albert's window, she noticed a suspicious scene involving Jerome, Albert's servant, and Mariette, the maid. This discovery sparked a dangerous curiosity in Rosalie, who realized that a single moment could unravel her mother's strict upbringing.

The next day, Rosalie confronted Mariette about her relationship with Jerome, offering her silence in exchange for access to letters Albert sent through Jerome. Mariette, relieved, agreed, and Rosalie's obsession deepened as she became fixated on Albert's life and feelings.

Rosalie learned from her father that a copy of the _Eastern Review_ was sent to the Duchesse d’Argaiolo in Italy, further fueling her jealousy. She later intercepted a letter from Albert to his friend Leopold, revealing his noble lineage and ambitions. This letter painted a picture of Albert's struggles and aspirations, intensifying Rosalie's infatuation and leading her to view him as a heroic figure.

As Albert became more successful in his legal career, Rosalie's feelings morphed into a dangerous obsession. She felt compelled to read another letter from Albert to the Duchesse, which detailed his hopes and affections for her. The letters revealed Albert's deep love for the Duchesse, leaving Rosalie heartbroken yet determined to respect their bond.

Despite her turmoil, Rosalie resolved to step back, believing she should sacrifice her desires for the happiness of Albert and the Duchesse. She grappled with her own feelings of unrequited love, questioning when she would find someone who truly loved her. Ultimately, Rosalie's internal conflict highlighted the complexities of love, jealousy, and the sacrifices one makes in the name of affection.

In the spring of 1835, Rosalie, now nineteen, fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo, her ambitions fueled by solitude and secret desires. Meanwhile, Albert de Savarus made significant political strides in Besançon, gaining support from influential figures, including a wealthy contractor. He aimed to restore an aqueduct to provide the town with water from Arcier, a project that would benefit his political aspirations and the contractor's interests.

Savarus, who had won several lawsuits for local priests, was driven by a desire for success, masking his true motivations behind a facade of disinterestedness. In October 1834, he facilitated a financial arrangement for a merchant, which inadvertently positioned him for election. The Abbe de Grancey recognized Savarus's potential but noted his reluctance to align with the Wattevilles, fearing it would compromise his neutrality.

Rosalie, determined to introduce Savarus to her social circle, orchestrated a trip to les Rouxey, her family's estate. There, she learned of her father's legal troubles regarding land disputes with the village of Riceys, which prompted her to suggest hiring Savarus as their lawyer. She believed that securing his services would not only protect her future inheritance but also deepen her connection with him.

As Rosalie plotted to keep Savarus in Besançon, she also sought to undermine his political ambitions by alerting the Prefect about his Royalist leanings. Despite her efforts, Savarus was nominated as a candidate, and his eloquence during a meeting captivated the townsfolk, earning him their support.

However, the political landscape shifted as the Prefect and his allies worked to diminish Savarus's backing. The election process revealed the complexities of local politics, with various factions vying for influence. Despite initial enthusiasm, Savarus faced mounting challenges as the election approached, leading to a tense atmosphere filled with uncertainty.

In a moment of despair, Savarus reflected on his unrequited love for Francesca, feeling the weight of his ambitions and the pressures of the election. Just when he felt most defeated, the Abbe de Grancey arrived with news of hope, urging him to pursue a connection with the Wattevilles, particularly with Madame de Chavoncourt, whose daughter Sidonie could provide crucial support.

The Abbe's strategic advice led Savarus to consider a marriage alliance with Sidonie, which would secure him the necessary votes to win the election. As he prepared to navigate this new political landscape, he recognized the sacrifices he would need to make for his ambitions, including the potential marriage that could alter his future.

Ultimately, the intertwining of personal desires and political aspirations set the stage for a dramatic confrontation between love and ambition, as Savarus sought to balance his heart's yearnings with the demands of his political career.

The following evening, Madame la Baronne de Watteville hosted a gathering of Besançon’s aristocracy to discuss the election, particularly concerning the daughter of the Rupts. The former Master of Appeals was to be presented, and attendees included Madame de Chavoncourt and her daughters. The Abbe de Grancey circulated among the guests, offering sharp insights on the political situation.

By nine o’clock, Albert de Savarus had not arrived, causing concern among the guests. Madame de Chavoncourt suggested that his delay might be trivial, but Rosalie, the Baroness's daughter, suspected otherwise. As time passed, frustration grew, and the Abbe de Grancey speculated that Albert might be dead. They learned from a servant that Albert had left in a post-chaise, prompting mixed reactions of shock and relief.

As the evening wore on, only a few guests remained, including Madame de Chavoncourt and the Abbe de Godenars. The Abbe de Grancey received a letter that visibly affected Rosalie, revealing her feelings for Albert. He deduced that she was in love with him and learned that Albert had withdrawn from the election. Rosalie, desperate to keep this news secret, realized that if the election ended, so too would the prospect of her sister Sidonie marrying Albert.

In the morning, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette about the mysterious circumstances surrounding Albert Savaron's sudden disappearance. An old gentleman from Paris had visited Monsieur Savaron, causing him to become pale and agitated. After their meeting, Savaron quickly arranged for a journey, leaving behind his keys and telling his servant to find another job. The news of his departure stirred commotion in Besançon, leading to the election of Monsieur de Chavoncourt.

As time passed, Albert was forgotten by most, except for a few who were deeply affected by his absence. Rosalie, aware of the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo's death, felt increasingly troubled. Meanwhile, the Baroness de Watteville expressed concern for Rosalie's well-being, suggesting she marry Monsieur de Soulas. Rosalie, however, adamantly refused, insisting she was happy as she was.

The tension between Rosalie and her mother escalated, leading to a summer spent at les Rouxey, where Rosalie's refusal to marry became public knowledge. After the marriage of Mariette and Jerome, the Baron made improvements to les Rouxey, which Rosalie took pride in, further aggravating her mother.

Tragedy struck when the Baron drowned while trying to retrieve a shell from the lake. His death, attributed to Rosalie's defiance, deepened the rift between mother and daughter. Grief-stricken, Rosalie felt responsible and contemplated confessing her role in Albert's fate. She had intercepted letters between Albert and the Duchess, manipulating their correspondence to separate them.

The Vicar-General, upon learning of Rosalie's actions, expressed horror at her moral crimes. He urged her to seek redemption and understand the consequences of her actions. Rosalie resolved to write to Albert's friend, Monsieur Leopold Hannequin, to uncover his fate.

Hannequin's response revealed that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery, seeking peace after the heartbreak caused by the Duchess's marriage. The Vicar-General wrote to Albert, who confirmed his decision to remain in the monastery, forgiving Rosalie and wishing her well.

As the years passed, Rosalie's mother married Monsieur de Soulas, and Rosalie, now of age, was encouraged to marry as well. Despite her mother's attempts to control her, Rosalie remained resolute in her feelings for Albert. She sought to confront the Duchess, determined to make her aware of the pain she had caused.

At a ball, Rosalie publicly confronted the Duchess, revealing the truth about Albert's letters and their tragic separation. The encounter left the Duchess shaken, and Rosalie returned to Besançon, feeling a sense of closure.

In the end, Rosalie's journey was marked by loss, regret, and a quest for redemption, as she navigated the complexities of love, family, and the consequences of her actions.

In 1841, she left Besancon, reportedly to marry, but returned severely injured from a steamboat accident, losing her right arm and left leg, and suffering disfigurement. Now, she lives a life devoted to religious practices at les Rouxey.
