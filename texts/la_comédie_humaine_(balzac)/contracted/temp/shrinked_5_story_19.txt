In the winter of 1829–30 the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu’s salon was nearly empty at one in the morning. Worried about her daughter Camille’s attachment to the Comte de Restaud, the Vicomtesse warned her that Restaud’s family—particularly his mother, Mlle. Goriot—had a questionable past that could threaten Camille’s future. Though Restaud possessed admirable qualities, his family background troubled the Vicomtesse.

Derville, a family friend and attorney who had helped the Vicomtesse recover lost properties and improve her finances, overheard and intervened. He had noticed Camille’s growing affection for Restaud and remarked that if Restaud were wealthy, many women would pursue him. Derville then reflected on Gobseck, a wealthy usurer who worshiped gold as the ultimate power. Gobseck’s cynicism implied that real happiness lay in wealth and manipulation. Derville recounted his experiences collecting debts, contrasting a beautiful Countess who offered a diamond instead of payment with a struggling linen-draper, Fanny Malvaut. He felt pity for both women, torn between feelings for the Countess and admiration for Fanny.

When the Vicomtesse offered a glass of eau sucrée, Derville resumed, noting that Camille’s happiness had once depended on Daddy Gobseck, who had recently died and left M. de Restaud a fortune. He also spoke of his wife, Fanny Malvaut, affirming his love. After submitting his thesis, Derville became a licentiate in law and later an advocate. Initially underestimated by Gobseck, he gained respect after handling complex matters for the usurer. Derville rose to head-clerk and moved into his employer’s house, marking a significant career advance.

When Derville bid farewell to Gobseck, the old man showed no emotion but later sought Derville’s counsel on a difficult expropriation case. Derville’s principal, a man given to pleasure, fell into financial trouble and resolved to sell his practice for 150,000 francs. Lacking funds, Derville felt compelled to borrow to preserve his self-respect and approached Gobseck. Gobseck agreed to lend at thirteen percent per annum, insisting on bills payable to a third party and requiring legal advice from Derville for free as part of the deal.

Three months later, Derville became an attorney and successfully recovered estates for Mme. de Grandlieu, earning recognition. He married Fanny, whose inheritance enabled them to clear Gobseck’s debt within five years. Their shared struggles strengthened their bond and led to a happy life.

A year after buying his practice, Derville attended a bachelor breakfast hosted by M. de Trailles, a notorious dandy. The event’s extravagance charmed Derville, and de Trailles persuaded him to introduce a friend to Gobseck. Hesitant at first, Derville agreed to accompany de Trailles to the usurer’s. On arrival, de Trailles seemed anxious and hinted at a woman waiting in a nearby cab. Derville introduced him to Gobseck, whose habitual impassivity remained unbroken by de Trailles’ charm.

Gobseck refused to lend to de Trailles, citing debts and lack of security. De Trailles left to fetch something he hoped would change Gobseck’s mind. When he returned with a woman, Derville recognized her as the Countess, one of Goriot’s daughters. She sought to pawn her diamonds for cash while keeping the right to repurchase them. Derville explained the terms, which reassured her but soured de Trailles’ mood.

Gobseck examined the diamonds with unsettling eagerness, revealing his obsession. His reaction was almost childlike compared to his usual stoicism. The scene revealed the tangled interplay of desire, power, and desperation among Parisian society’s characters.

They discussed the diamonds as relics of pre-Revolutionary wealth, now diminished. Gobseck inspected them critically, noting their flaws. The Count sought a loan of one hundred thousand francs for three years; Gobseck consented but demanded the gems be weighed first. The Countess appeared distraught, hinting at moral struggle. When asked about ownership she affirmed the diamonds were hers, but Gobseck declined to draft a formal loan agreement, citing legal complications from her marriage. He instead offered eighty thousand francs down with the diamonds as collateral. The Countess hesitated; Maxime, a young man present, expressed despair.

Ultimately, the Countess accepted Gobseck’s offer; he provided a check and cash. Tensions rose when Maxime insulted Gobseck, who calmly threatened him. After the Countess departed, Gobseck exulted in acquiring the diamonds, his greed plain.

Days later the Count visited the narrator, professing confidence and discussing Gobseck’s character. He revealed plans to transfer property to Gobseck, concerned about his own health. The narrator cautioned him about disinheriting other children; the Count agreed to include them in the counter-deed.

The narrative then focuses on the Countess, determined to secure her children’s future despite her past. As her husband’s health declined, the bedridden Count forbade her from entering his room; she spied on him, convinced he was hiding money. Near death, the Count confided in his son Ernest, entrusting him with a secret letter for the narrator. The Countess tried to manipulate Ernest into revealing the secret; the Count confronted her, accusing her of treachery in a dramatic confrontation.

After the Count’s death, the household descended into chaos as the Countess searched for documents, exposing her desperation. The narrator discovers the Count had hidden vital papers, including the counter-deed, which in her panic the Countess inadvertently destroys. With those documents gone, Gobseck becomes the rightful owner of the property and contemplates the consequences of her actions.

In the aftermath, the narrator reflects on the Countess’s transformation and the consequences of her greed. Gobseck, despite his avarice, displays a peculiar understanding of human nature, asserting that adversity teaches valuable lessons. The story closes with the narrator pondering the fate of the Countess and her children and Gobseck’s legacy, probing themes of greed, morality, and the complex ties between wealth, power, and human relationships.
