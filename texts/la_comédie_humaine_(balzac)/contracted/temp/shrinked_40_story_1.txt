Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, a weathered house helped reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls revealed timber and cracked plaster; a warped triangular roof overhung the street, its overlapping upper planks emphasizing fragility.

On a rainy March morning a young man, cloaked like an antiquary, stood opposite studying its peculiarities. He ignored the narrow boarded first-floor windows and examined the third floor’s modest sash windows, clouded with green-tinted glass, occasionally glancing at the shop below where comic details amused him.

A large wooden beam, propped on four pillars and layered with paint, bore a whimsical painting of a cat with an oversized racket and absurd ball. A faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” recalling the shop’s old charm.

Dressed for a ball in a stylish cloak, white silk stockings and gloves, his disheveled hair hinted at recent revelry. Market gardeners hurried past; his pale face and dazzling cravat drew attention. Apprentices at the dormer laughed and sprinkled him with shaving cream. A Raphael-like girl peered and fled as shutters closed, then the “Cat and Racket” opened, a manservant hung the cloth sign, and behind iron bars many brown-linen-wrapped packages appeared. Monsieur Guillaume was old-fashioned but famed for a well-stocked, honest shop, though rivals grumbled at his notary’s heavy discounts.

Monsieur Guillaume, the merchant at his shop, watched a stranger loitering and suspected ulterior motives. A prank on Augustine was prevented when the eldest apprentice hailed a cab as she hurried off.

A traditionalist who lamented lost customs, Guillaume imposed a strict routine on his apprentices, reproving them and recalling his youth. He demanded rigorous labor unlike newer shops. Madame Guillaume’s frugality excluded apprentices from dessert, and they attended Mass together.

After years Joseph Lebas earned a modest wage; Guillaume favored him for his daughter Virginie, though Joseph loved Augustine. Virginie was devout and severe; Augustine, eighteen, delicate and stifled. Amid the shop’s monotony feelings grew, and Guillaume feared for his daughters’ prospects in changing times.

The old merchant, grieving his eldest daughter’s decline, remembered marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like those of Joseph Lebas and Virginie; marrying off his daughter would repay a sacred debt to an orphan benefactor. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, aware of the fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder sister marry first, suffered in silence, warm-hearted but reserved.

Months earlier a young painter had paused outside the darkened Cat and Racket, transfixed by a scene any artist would envy. The cave-like shop opened on a dining room lit by a hanging lamp that recalled Dutch interiors. The family—Augustine among them—formed a peaceful, modest tableau; Augustine, pensive and scarcely touched by food, seemed almost angelic in the lamp’s glow. Overwhelmed, the painter left, then returned repeatedly in disguise to study her. For eight months he lived in love and art, neglecting friends and routine.

One morning his friend Girodet found two masterpieces in his studio; recognizing deep feeling in them, Girodet said love had made the work great. Despite warnings, the paintings were exhibited and created a sensation. The Interior launched a new genre, the portrait became unforgettable, and offers followed—many declined.

Augustine, living a monastic life in Rue Saint-Denis, learned of the exhibition from Madame Guillaume and, with a cousin’s help, obtained permission to see the Louvre. Trembling before the portrait, she recognized herself and heard the artist whisper, “You see how love has inspired me.” Fleeing the crowd, she returned, wept, exchanged a brief nod with the painter, and felt guilt and exhilaration. Feigning a headache, she longed for the elegance she had admired and for being a genius’s wife. The next day her parents returned disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. The episode awakened in Augustine a woman’s instinctive sensitivity.

After the ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had won Augustine’s heart, hesitated to declare his love because her household constrained them. Augustine sat in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume under her mother's watch, while Theodore, fearing rivals among the assistants, bribed a maid to arrange discreet exchanges; they agreed to meet at Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family concentrated on stock-taking under Monsieur Guillaume, tallying inventory and accounts; afterward they relaxed and attended a performance of Cendrillon.

The next morning Guillaume, resuming routine, summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas, offered him partnership, and was startled when Joseph blurted that he loved Augustine—Guillaume at first misheard and thought he meant Virginie. Once clarified, Guillaume said he would consider Joseph’s feelings and suggested he accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose; Madame later chastised Augustine for inattentiveness in church and sent her away, prompting a heated discussion. Augustine confessed her love for Theodore and defended his respectability. Monsieur, wary of artists, protested until Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore’s honors and fortune. Her report swayed the parents; by evening Guillaume informed Joseph, the household celebrated, Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket, and Monsieur consented to the match.

A few months after that memorable Sunday, Saint-Leu’s high altar saw two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore radiated happiness while Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had wanted Virginie married first, but the priests attended the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s sensible match and envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank; an old fan-maker warned such extravagance could bring ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt vindicated. After a splendid ball and supper the Guillaumes returned home and the Lebas family resumed their modest life. The ecstatic artist carried Augustine to their richly adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful: Theodore lavished affection and Augustine, lost in love, believed her devotion would always enchant him. Naïve at times, she remained happily unaware of many things. As pregnancy neared, Theodore felt the pull of work, reconnected with friends, and began frequenting the Duchesse de Carigliano’s salons, enjoying the prestige of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine liked society but soon felt out of place among Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration was sincere but superficial; an intellectual disconnect grew. Studio life did not draw her in, and at gatherings her innocence clashed with freer manners. Gradually Theodore’s warmth cooled. Three years in, Augustine sank into sorrow as his intimacy with the Duchesse stoked fears of being supplanted. She tried to study poetry and art to reconnect but only revealed her inadequacy. A brief return to her childhood home—where Virginie and Joseph Lebas ran their drapery with steady, ordinary happiness—offered little comfort from Joseph’s practical view. Augustine ultimately returned to her husband’s house, burdened by uncertainty and longing, trying every remedy to heal a fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter, interrupting years of monotonous reminiscence about past business troubles—most notably the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume fussed over his shop, the Cat and Racket, and chatted with former competitors; Madame Guillaume, appointed to the Army’s clothing consulting board through her son-in-law, filled their home with extravagant, tasteless decor. Their nostalgic stagnation contrasted sharply with Augustine’s turbulent life, leaving her both proud and appalled.

Augustine displayed her achievements and aired marital grievances, which her parents received avidly. Madame Guillaume was especially curious about Augustine’s husband, Theodore, a painter. When Augustine described his late nights and eccentric habits, her mother disapproved, sparking a debate about genius versus marital responsibility. The mention of divorce shocked them; Monsieur offered practical help. Overwhelmed and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine felt an isolating loneliness only fellow sufferers could know.

Determined to change course, she sought the Duchess de Carigliano—not to force Theodore back but to learn how to captivate him. In the Duchess’s opulent home, Augustine felt envy and rivalry. The Duchess counseled strength, self-control, cultivated mystery, and withholding a full measure of affection, and lent a portrait Theodore had painted to rekindle him. Augustine tried to become that image; Theodore’s fury erupted into a violent quarrel. The next morning her mother found Augustine dead among the wreckage. She died young, her tragic end underscoring the plight of women seeking love and fulfillment amid the complexities of artistic genius; her grave’s inscription bears that sorrow.
