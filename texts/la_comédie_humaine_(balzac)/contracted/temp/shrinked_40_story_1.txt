Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, a charming old house allowed historians to reconstruct Paris. Dilapidated walls exposed timber-and-plaster patterns; a warped triangular roof jutted over the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer. The upper story used overlapping planks to lighten the load on the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man in a cloak stood beneath a shop awning opposite the house, studying it with antiquarian zeal. He ignored the first floor’s narrow boarded windows and examined the third floor’s modest sash windows, dimmed by green-tinted glass. Occasionally he glanced at the shop below, amused by its details: a large wooden beam, supported by four pillars, bore peeling layers of paint and a comical depiction of a cat playing with a racket and an oversized ball. The faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a whimsical remnant meant to attract customers.

Dressed with stylish care—cloak, white silk stockings and gloves, hair slightly dishevelled as if returning from a ball—he seemed spirited yet calm. Market gardeners hurried by, but the street was unusually still; his pale face, dazzling white cravat and expressive eyes drew the attention of the shopkeepers at the “Cat and Racket.”

Suddenly three apprentices at the dormer window teased him, even sprinkling shaving cream; he shrugged off the annoyance. A young girl’s face, pure and unembarrassed like a Raphael Virgin, peered then withdrew, leaving him disappointed. The shop’s shutters opened, a manservant hung the cloth sign, and behind iron bars packages of brown-linen-wrapped cloth were visible. Monsieur Guillaume, old-fashioned but famed for having Paris’s best-stocked cloth house and an honest reputation that earned steady profit, was nevertheless warned about his notary’s notorious discounts.

The old merchant stood at his shop threshold on the Rue Saint-Denis, waking as if from a long slumber. Monsieur Guillaume, in traditional dress, watched a curious stranger who seemed overly interested in his business. His patient, shrewd face showed experience. A strict traditionalist, he lamented lost customs and enforced a rigorous regime for his three apprentices, who feared his constant vigilance.

As the apprentices began the day, Guillaume noticed the stranger’s anxious glances and suspected ulterior motives. The eldest, Joseph Lebas, stepped outside and thought he saw Mademoiselle Augustine leaving hastily; he hailed a cab, which eased their worries about a prank on her.

Guillaume reprimanded them for idleness, recalling how hard he had worked in youth. Though the apprentices came from fairly comfortable families and paid for their board, Madame Guillaume’s frugality even deprived them of family desserts. He demanded strict industry, contrasting his order and economy with modern shops where young men expected quick wealth. The shop itself was meticulously kept, a testament to his values.

The household followed firm traditions: they attended Mass together, and after years of service Joseph earned a modest salary. Guillaume saw Joseph as a fitting husband for his elder daughter Virginie, though Joseph secretly loved the younger Augustine. Virginie resembled her mother in piety and sternness; Augustine, eighteen, was delicate and charming but constrained by maternal rule. Augustine’s yearning for more—fueled by secret novels—deepened Joseph’s affection, while Virginie, lacking suitors, found herself drawn to him. Guillaume mused about his daughters’ futures in a world where suitors might grow scarce.

From that day the old merchant, grieving his eldest daughter’s decline, remembered marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like Joseph Lebas and Virginie’s. Marrying his daughter would repay a sacred debt—an orphan’s past kindness. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, acutely felt the fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder daughter marry first. The warm‑hearted, tall assistant suffered in silence.

How it began: one evening a young painter paused before the dark Cat and Racket and saw a scene any artist would cherish. The unlit shop framed a lamp-lit dining room that gave Augustine—pensive, scarcely touched by food—an almost angelic face. Fresh from Rome and craving simple nature, the painter left overwhelmed, unable to eat or sleep.

He returned to his studio resolved to capture that moment, visiting the Cat and Racket repeatedly, sometimes in disguise, to study Augustine. For eight months he devoted himself to love and work, neglecting friends and former pursuits. One morning his friend Girodet pierced his isolation to ask about his Salon submission. The painter revealed two masterpieces; Girodet embraced him, recognizing their depth and asking, “You are in love?” They agreed great work springs from passion, and Girodet warned the public might not grasp these subtle pieces. The artist exhibited them anyway; they caused a sensation. The Interior sparked a genre shift, while the portrait became unforgettable, earning acclaim and offers the painter refused.

These public successes felt remote from the monastic life on Rue Saint‑Denis. During a visit, Madame Guillaume mentioned the exhibition, rousing Augustine’s curiosity. With her cousin’s help she obtained permission to visit the Louvre. Seeing the portrait, she trembled—it was herself. The artist stood nearby and whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Desperate to escape the crowd, she tried to leave, but the throng carried them toward the celebrated painting; overwhelmed, she wept. In a brief silent exchange she gestured to the artist; he acknowledged her with a nod. Guilt and exhilaration followed. The Salon’s chaos and the painter’s gaze awakened feelings she had not known. She feigned a headache to avoid talk of the exhibition; that night she longed for the elegance she’d always desired and felt hope at the thought of being the wife of a genius. The next day her parents returned disappointed—the painter had removed the pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl—but Augustine had discovered a new sensitivity.

The morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux—who had won Augustine’s heart—found it hard to declare his love because the household watched her closely. Augustine sat in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume under her mother’s eye, so Theodore bribed a maid to exchange notes and arranged a discreet meeting during Mass at Saint-Leu.

The Cat and Racket household was absorbed in stock-taking, with Monsieur Guillaume overseeing inventory while the family verified goods and barely noticed Augustine’s inward turmoil. After weeks of labor the accounts were settled and the family enjoyed a rare evening at Cendrillon while the apprentices took the night off.

The next morning Guillaume reviewed the profitable accounts and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas, proposing to make him a partner. Overwhelmed, Joseph tried to answer but confessed his love for Augustine; Guillaume at first thought he meant Virginie. Corrected, Guillaume agreed to consider Joseph’s feelings and suggested he accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie eyed a nervous Joseph. Madame Guillaume, unaware Augustine loved Theodore, chided her for inattention during church and ordered her to her room, prompting a heated parental debate about Augustine’s future. Summoned, Augustine openly declared her love for Theodore and defended his respectable background against Monsieur Guillaume’s old suspicions of artists.

Madame Roguin then arrived to report that Theodore had recently received honors and gained wealth, urging the match. Her account swayed the parents. By evening the household shifted from conflict to acceptance: Guillaume informed Joseph, the family celebrated the prospective union, Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket, and Guillaume consented—cautious about finances—while the lovers found solace in mutual affection.

A few months after a memorable Sunday, the high altar of Saint-Leu saw two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore glowed with happiness while Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had wanted Virginie married first, but the priests attended the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie for a sensible match; others envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned such extravagance would invite ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt justified in his cautious settlements. After a splendid ball and supper the couples returned home, and the artist carried Augustine to their beautifully adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful. Theodore showered Augustine with affection; she, lost in love, believed her devotion would always charm him. Naïve about the wider world, she spoke the language of love and sometimes revealed her innocence. As a child was expected, Theodore felt the need to return to work. He reconnected with friends and began frequenting the Duchesse de Carigliano’s gatherings, eager for the social validation of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine enjoyed society but soon realized she lacked the sophistication of Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was genuine yet superficial, and a disconnect grew. Theodore hoped the environment would inspire her, but she struggled to adapt; her innocence clashed with the more liberated attitudes at gatherings.

Over time Theodore’s affection waned, and Augustine felt the weight of his indifference. Three years in, once vibrant, she became consumed by sorrow. Attempts to rekindle his love failed as the artist’s attachment to the Duchesse deepened. Determined, Augustine tried to educate herself in poetry and art, but she could not acquire the natural wit Theodore admired; her earnestness often backfired.

In desperation she returned to her childhood home, finding Virginie and Joseph Lebas running their drapery with steady, practical happiness. Joseph’s commerce-minded advice intensified her conflict. Ultimately she returned home, burdened by uncertainty and longing, trying every remedy to heal their fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter Augustine; her visit brightened their monotonous lives. For four years they had drifted, dwelling on past business successes and failures like the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume inspected his shop, the Cat and Racket, trading memories with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume—now on the Army's clothing consulting board thanks to her son-in-law—filled their home with extravagant, often tasteless decor. Their nostalgia and stagnation struck Augustine as a sharp contrast to her own turbulent existence, leaving her both proud and appalled.

Augustine spoke of recent achievements and aired marital grievances, which her parents eagerly absorbed. Madame Guillaume was especially curious about life with Theodore, Augustine’s husband, a painter. Augustine mentioned his late nights and unconventional habits, prompting her mother’s disapproval of his profession. The couple’s incredulity grew as Augustine defended Theodore, sparking a heated debate about genius and marital responsibility.

When Augustine hinted at divorce, her parents were shocked; Monsieur Guillaume offered support. Overwhelmed by their concern and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine rejected separation, realizing her pain could scarcely be understood by them. She felt isolated, convinced only those with similar experience could empathize.

Determined to win back Theodore by other means, Augustine resolved to consult the Duchess de Carigliano—not to reclaim his heart outright but to learn how to captivate him. At the Duchess’s opulent home, Augustine admired the elegance and felt rivalry. The Duchess, beautiful and shrewd, advised a composed facade: cultivate mystery, never reveal the full measure of one’s affection, and use guardedness as strategy. She gave Augustine a portrait Theodore had painted of the Duchess, believing it might rekindle his interest.

When Augustine presented the portrait, Theodore’s reaction was fury and jealousy, igniting a violent confrontation that exposed his passion and insecurity. The aftermath left Augustine shattered; her hopes of mending their marriage lay in ruins. The next morning her mother found her consumed by despair amid the remnants of the quarrel. Augustine died young, her fate a poignant reminder of the precarious balance between passion and stability in relationships with artistic genius.
