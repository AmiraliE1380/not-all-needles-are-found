During the Restoration the Archbishop of Besançon was among the few who frequented the devout Baronne de Watteville’s salon. Monsieur de Watteville, though descended from the notorious Watteville, was gentle and unassuming. After living quietly in La Franche-Comté he married Mademoiselle de Rupt, heiress of the Rupt family; the match, arranged in 1802 and formalized in 1815 after the second Restoration, brought substantial income. After her grandparents’ deaths Madame de Watteville grew more devout, and her piety influenced Besançon’s upper circles.

Monsieur, thin and unremarkable, worked as a turner and collected geological specimens; some admired his noble spirit. Realizing he would never dominate his wife, he threw himself into work and hobbies. The Rupt house, well furnished and hospitable, contrasted with his modest estate, and their ties to the Archbishop attracted notable abbés.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834 guests welcomed Abbé de Grancey, who announced a favorable verdict. The Vicar-General, calling the victory unexpected, credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron; when asked, he said Savaron lived nearby but of unknown origin, a detail that piqued interest. Amedée de Soulas, present that evening, would later prove significant.

France and England have long exchanged cultural influences—especially in language and fashion—so that what seems "English" in Paris becomes "French" in London. Examples include Lulli composing "God Save the King" for a French play, hoop skirts that passed from ridicule to European fashion, and English words like "dandy" and "lion" entering French via Alfred de Musset, sparking a vogue for stylish young men from Paris outward.

In 1834 Besançon, Amedée-Sylvain de Soulas, of Spanish descent, embodied this cosmopolitan affectation. Ill at ease in the dull garrison town, he kept a modest, sparsely furnished apartment while cultivating an extravagant appearance. He employed a young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger," dressed him fashionably, and lavished expense on wardrobes he could barely afford—often dining out to avoid household costs. His grooming, Parisian knowledge, and distinctive touches (like trouser-straps) made him a local "lion," admired by the town's aristocratic "Colony" eager to seem modern before visiting Parisians.

Amedée’s aim was to impress the elite and win Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville. Raised strictly and modestly under her mother’s watchful, jealous supervision, Rosalie was sheltered, skilled in domestic duties but lacking social polish; she had an inner strength yet was forced to repress feelings as her mother's protectiveness complicated any courtship. Though Amedée charmed the elite and engagement rumors circulated, Rosalie’s upbringing and Madame de Watteville’s influence made the match fraught. His story illustrates social ambition, cultural exchange, and the constraints of provincial society.

“Madame,” Monsieur de Soulas said to the Baroness as he waited for his soup to cool, “one morning a gentleman from Paris arrived at the Hotel National and took the first‑floor rooms in Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron. He registered at the mairie as ‘ALBERT SAVARON’ and was listed among the barristers.”

“The Savaron name is famous,” Mademoiselle de Watteville observed.

“He’s a Frenchman, not of the Brabant line,” Amedee de Soulas replied. “If he claims the Savaron arms he must add a bar‑sinister. The only remaining Brabant heir is a rich Miss de Savarus.”

“The bar‑sinister denotes a bastard, but even a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble,” Rosalie added.

“Enough,” the Baroness interrupted. “You insisted she learn heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville said with a smile. “She knows it well.”

“Please continue,” the Baroness urged.

“In a town like Besançon everything is catalogued,” Soulas went on. “Albert Savaron was received without fuss by local lawyers, who wondered who had sent him and what he hoped to do—especially since he left his card with judges instead of calling. Three days later no one paid him much mind. He hired Jerome, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who could cook a little. Savaron kept to himself; no one had seen much of him.”

“Does he go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends the early service at Saint‑Pierre on Sundays. He rises very early, works until eight, breakfasts, walks in his garden—fifty or sixty turns—dines, and is in bed by six or seven.”

“How do you know all this?” Madame de Chavoncourt wanted to know.

“I live on Rue Neuve, opposite his house; my servant speaks with Jerome.”

“And you gossip with Babylas?” someone laughed.

“How did you come to hire a stranger for your defense?” the Baroness asked the Vicar‑General.

“The President of the Court assigned Savaron to defend a half‑witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man innocent and exposed the real culprits, who were arrested. That defense impressed the Court and the jury. Then a merchant put a difficult case in his hands, which he won. Monsieur Berryer advised us to employ him, promising success. I met him and had confidence in him—and I was right.”

“Is he extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” the Vicar‑General replied.

“Tell us more,” urged Madame de Watteville.

“The first time I met him,” the Abbé de Grancey recounted, “he received me in an outer room lined with books, modestly furnished. I had not expected a young advocate. His dress was odd—black dressing gown, red cord, red slippers, red waistcoat, red smoking cap.”

“The devil’s colors!” Madame de Watteville exclaimed.

“Yes,” the Abbé continued, “but he has a magnificent head. Thick black hair streaked with gray, a round white throat, a broad forehead marked by thought, an olive complexion, a square nose, and eyes like an eagle. He is passionate yet calm; his voice is sweet but can thunder.”

“Monsieur Savaron is of average height, with hands like a prelate,” the Abbé added.

“The second time I saw him he received me in his shabby bedroom; the third I found him having a frugal breakfast in his library. He is no ordinary man; there is something heavy on him. His face is terrible and gentle at once; he seems to carry a great burden.”

“Why would such an eloquent man leave Paris for Besançon?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. “Doesn’t he know how little a stranger can do here?”

“After seeing his head and hearing him reply to a leading Paris lawyer,” the Abbé said, “I believe he will yet make a name.”

The Abbé’s portrait of Savaron took root in Rosalie. For the first time she encountered someone who seemed truly exceptional, unlike the dull Count who had courted her. He presented an enigma: eloquent, serious, isolated, and apparently burdened. She replayed the Abbé’s words—especially about his forehead and hands—and felt a restless curiosity.

“Rosalie, why aren’t you eating?” her mother asked.

“I’m not hungry, mamma,” she answered, lost in thought.

She realized she had seen a lighted window from her bed that belonged to him. “I must see him,” she decided.

“Is the Chapter’s lawsuit settled?” she asked the Abbé, trying to appear casual.

“It might go to the Court of Appeal, but our adversaries will hesitate,” he said.

After dinner she listened for any mention of Savaron, but the talk moved on to other matters. Eager for bedtime, she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse his windows. When the hour came she felt a thrill watching the candlelight through the trees and thought of the furniture and color of his rooms. “While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God,” she imagined.

At home the question of educating girls appeared in a whisper: too much enlightenment risks precociousness, too much suppression breeds rebellion. For Rosalie, the Abbé’s portrait had pierced restraint and kindled a desire—part romantic, part intellectual—to know this man.

The next morning, while dressing, she saw Albert walking in the garden beside the Hotel de Rupt. “At least I can see him here,” she thought, and resolved to engineer an opportunity to enter his house and hear his voice.

She spent the day embroidering with distracted air. She timed a confession to leave just before the eight o’clock mass, hoping to encounter Savaron. She visited her father in his workshop and suggested he carve larger pieces and build a grotto in the garden to display his work. He delighted in the idea; she warned him not to tell her mother.

She imagined the grotto with an observatory that would give her a view into Savaron’s private room. Young girls often contrive elaborate lengths for the sake of a man, even if he never knows.

When Sunday arrived Rosalie dressed with care; her maid Mariette noticed her nervousness. She arrived at church before mass and took a place where she might see Savaron enter. He had a solemn, burdened air; his gaze was intense. To Rosalie, everything about him now seemed filled with meaning. Boldly she stood in his path to exchange glances, and a warmth surged through her.

After confession she found a seat to watch him; by the time Mass ended he had gone. “The Vicar‑General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besançon? I must know everything!” With renewed resolve she turned her thoughts to the grotto plan.

“Mamma,” she said one day, “my father has an idea for a Belvedere in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of whatever amuses your father,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly.

“From there we’ll be able to see into Monsieur de Soulas’ rooms, and he may see us—perhaps remarks will be made—”

“Do you presume to guide your parents, Rosalie?” her mother snapped.

“I’ll say no more, mamma,” she answered. But the plan pressed on. Monsieur de Watteville and their carpenter devised a grotto hidden from one neighbor yet giving a view toward Savaron’s windows. Periwinkle, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper would frame a rustic interior with a mirror, ottoman, and bark‑inlaid table. A chandelier carved by Monsieur de Watteville himself completed the idea. The garden project was commissioned and rumors spread: the Wattevilles were building a lovely belvedere—“a thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed.

Rosalie felt a flush of pride. No one suspected that the supposedly simple‑witted girl had planned the arrangement chiefly to see Savaron’s private study.

Meanwhile Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon forgotten amid envy from other lawyers. He remained secluded, avoiding social gatherings, which increased his anonymity. Yet he kept winning complex cases: three successes at the Commercial Tribunal brought him four prominent merchant clients who recognized his legal skill.

On the day the Wattevilles inaugurated their Belvedere, Savaron was launching an enterprise of his own. Quietly, through merchant connections, he planned a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review. It was financed by forty shares of five hundred francs each subscribed by his first ten clients, who saw the need to promote Besançon as a trade center.

To compete with Strasbourg and Dijon, Besançon needed commerce and enlightenment. Savaron wanted to challenge the literary dominance of the West and bring light to Eastern France. His ideas appealed to merchants who fancied they were furthering their own interests.

Savaron kept his name out of the public foreground. He let his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, handle accounts, while Savaron retained editorial control and a share of any profits. The Review invited contributions from various regions and, with a low subscription price, quickly gained five hundred subscribers.

To avoid bruising local pride, Savaron invited Alfred Boucher, Monsieur Boucher’s eager son, to manage the literary side. Savaron subtly guided Alfred’s contributions while letting him believe he was in charge. The merchants were pleased: with a few more subscribers the publication would yield dividends for shareholders, while the nominal editor remained unpaid.

After the third issue the Review began to be noticed by other French publications; Savaron read them at home. The third number contained a tale attributed to him that stirred talk in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room, though some of Besançon’s higher circles dismissed it for its liberal sentiments.

“Papa,” Rosalie said, “there’s a Review published in Besançon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room—mamma won’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.”

Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please Rosalie, subscribed and lent her the four issues. That night she devoured the tale—the first she had ever read. Given her limited reading experience, its effect on her was profound. She hoped to find in it the sentiments or even the life of Albert. From the opening pages she felt convinced it reflected him; critics would later note that the author had drawn from his own experiences as if confessing.

In 1823 two young men left Lucerne for a holiday around the lake of the Four Cantons. Passing the hamlet of Gersau, Rodolphe was captivated by a new house and a woman at the window and decided to stay; his friend Leopold continued the trip.

Rodolphe and Leopold had studied law together: Leopold was headed for a notary’s post in Paris, while Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a more unsettled past. His mother had sacrificed everything for his education; passionate and impulsive, he contrasted with Leopold’s steady calm.

Rodolphe lodged with the Bergmanns, who told him the woman was Fanny Lovelace, recently moved with her elderly father for his health. Moderately well off, they lived simply; Fanny supported her father by translating and writing and was noted for her beauty and musical talent. Exploring their gardens, Rodolphe noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, whose presence suggested a distinctive past. The Bergmanns were protective, but Rodolphe was determined to meet Fanny.

That evening he heard Fanny sing and was enchanted. When he later encountered Fanny and Gina, an unknown assailant attacked and wounded him; Fanny and Gina aided him and he was taken to safety. While recovering, Rodolphe learned his mother had died, a blow that plunged him into deep grief; he found solace in Fanny’s company. They bonded over shared interests; he confessed his feelings, only to learn she was married to an older man, Emilio.

Despite the marriage, Fanny’s affection for Rodolphe grew and they shared intimate moments. She revealed she had married Emilio under duress and that he was not as old as he seemed. Torn between love and reality, Rodolphe was encouraged by Fanny to pursue his ambitions, but jealousy and insecurity crept in—exacerbated by the appearance of Tito, a man from Fanny’s past. Their dreams of freedom and a future together clashed with Rodolphe’s ambitions and Fanny’s loyalty, the beautiful setting reflecting intense but precarious emotions.

For twenty days Rodolphe stayed with the Bergmanns, watching Francesca almost unconsciously. He saw in her a mix of youthful imprudence and an unbent spirit: she sometimes resisted love, sometimes yielded. She treated the old man like a father; her gratitude revealed a native nobility that intrigued him and made her an enigma.

Those days combined secret joys and melancholy; their quarrels were as delightful as their reconciliations. Rodolphe grew attached to Francesca’s steady tenderness, which often showed as jealous exactness over trifles.

One evening, when Francesca declared she wanted to leave Gersau because it lacked luxury, Rodolphe asked if she cared for luxury. She answered passionately, equating it with an appreciation for art and beauty and insisting she had never complained in hardship. With a mischievous air she argued it was natural for ordinary people to aspire to greatness, revealing her character.

She then begged Rodolphe not to accompany them to Geneva, citing town gossip and her wish to protect her husband’s reputation. Though she affirmed her love for Rodolphe, she demanded it remain concealed; she kissed him and left him astonished.

The next day the Bergmanns’ lodgers departed early. Restless, Rodolphe went to Vevay and then to Geneva at the end of October, taking a long route. To avoid the town’s discomforts he rented a room in Eaux‑Vives. He inquired about Italian refugees, expecting Lamporani; the landlord said the only new arrivals were Prince and Princess Colonna and Prince and Princess Gandolphini.

On his first walk to the Villa Diodati the beauty of the place struck him. Approaching, he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage with an old lady and felt dizzy with surprise. Learning they were at the Colonna residence lifted a veil: the implications of their past interactions became clear. He feared he had been a mere whim for Francesca and struggled with the reality of her rank.

He sent his card to Prince Gandolphini and was received by a man posing as Lamporani, who invited him to a concert that evening. Though curious, Rodolphe refrained from requesting an audience with the Princess and returned to Eaux‑Vives with mingled anticipation and anxiety, feeling he must elevate his position to match hers.

At the concert she was dazzling; their eyes met in an electric instant. Francesca’s beauty and grace captivated him, and he felt convinced she had remained faithful despite her rank. After the concert he was introduced to the Colonnas and to Francesca, who playfully styled herself “Princess Rodolphini.” The jest revived memories of Gersau; he was overwhelmed by her voice and presence.

In the following days Francesca perceived Rodolphe’s deep love, which for her felt natural. She said their affection should be faithful and enduring, whatever the distance. Rodolphe, however, was driven by ambition: he wanted greatness so Francesca could be proud.

Francesca combined gaiety and tenderness. Once destined for a cloister, family misfortunes had forced her to marry Prince Gandolphini to preserve her family’s standing. Despite noble birth, she had a taste for independence and liberal ideas, inclining her toward revolutionary sentiments.

Rodolphe spent the winter in Geneva where time flew in her company. With spring came inner turmoil: Francesca’s calmness contrasted with his unrest. He wished she would show more feeling. One day on the terrace he made a bitter complaint about the happiness of others, wounding Francesca, who withdrew in tears. He found her, kissed her in a moment of passion; she repelled him and asserted her dignity. She then ordered him to leave, declaring they would not meet again until Naples. Rodolphe, recognizing the strength of her will, complied.

On his return to Paris, Rodolphe received a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner, who had refused many women. Rodolphe doubted the Prince could persuade him, but Francesca charmed Schinner into painting an original for Rodolphe and a duplicate for Emilio, announcing it in a delightful letter — the start of a regular correspondence between Rodolphe and Francesca, their sole indulgence.

Ambitious and in love, Rodolphe invested everything in a venture that failed through deceit, costing him three years' struggle. The Villele ministry fell just as he faced ruin. Seeking solace, he turned to politics but first went to Naples to recover at Villa Gandolphini, where the Prince and Princess had been restored. A three-month respite healed him and renewed his courage.

He tried to rebuild his fortune; talents noticed and a high post promised were dashed by the July 1830 storm. Only Francesca and God witnessed his brave, unlucky efforts. Sustained by love, he returned to new challenges, guided by a faithful presence and devoted heart. Lovers! Pray for him!

When Mademoiselle de Watteville finished her tale, Rosalie blushed and wept. It was her first encounter with such a love story—told through a man's experiences—and she became consumed by jealousy toward Francesca Colonna, whom she believed to be Albert's beloved.

Driven by curiosity and a fierce desire to win Albert, Rosalie, insecure about her looks and lack of musical talent, feared he could never love her. Her imagination spun schemes typical of a sheltered girl: sneaking into his garden, writing to him, or contriving meetings at her father's land dispute to draw him into her drawing-room.

While watching Albert's window she saw something that might expose his private life: Jérôme, Albert's servant, being helped over a wall by Mariette, the maid. Rosalie noted Mariette's plainness but also her steadiness and means—explanations for Jérôme's interest—and learned a dangerous lesson: her mother's strict upbringing could be undermined by a servant's choices.

The next morning Rosalie shadowed Mariette to Mass and confronted her about the secret meetings. Offering support in exchange for access to letters Albert passed through Jérôme, Rosalie found Mariette tempted and willing.

Obsession deepened as Rosalie read Albert's correspondence. A letter to his friend Léopold revealed noble lineage, past struggles, and ambitious plans, while portraying a man devoted to the woman he loved—Francesca. Entranced, Rosalie felt both admiration and acute inadequacy. She wrestled with guilt for spying but rationalized it as proof of love, taking on penances and self-imposed suffering to demonstrate devotion.

When Mariette later brought a letter meant for Francesca, Rosalie, despite hesitation, read it. It confirmed Albert's enduring love and hopes for a shared future. Struck by their fidelity and her own longing, she chose to step back, return the letter with Mariette, and begin to accept the sorrow of unrequited affection.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil; by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. In solitude that imagined struggle fed her passion and dark plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist, and her story is a cautionary tale.

That winter Albert de Savarus made notable advances in Besançon, eagerly awaiting the Chamber’s dissolution. Backed by a wealthy contractor allied with moderates, he championed a contested water supply issue: while the Doubs flowed nearby, he argued the river water was undrinkable and promoted bringing water from Arcier, a distant hill, even proposing the restoration of a Roman aqueduct. Local Review articles echoed him, and townspeople across factions bought the romance of drinking like the Romans. The campaign, driven by hidden interests and lucrative contracts, dismissed opponents as old-fashioned. After eighteen months Savarus had stirred the stagnant town and gained influence without genuine popularity.

He also won several lawsuits for local priests over the winter, and his ambition consumed him. His apparent indifference to money masked a form of moral usury: he expected far greater returns. In October 1834 he financed a struggling merchant with funds from Léopold Hannequin, which also aided his electability. The Abbé de Grancey, who saw Savarus’s ambition clearly, remarked on his formidable nature and hinted he’d chosen a wrong turn in life.

Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hôtel de Rupt, at first content to watch him from afar. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, produced ten thousand francs a year, but management was neglected by the Baron, who left it to an old servant, Modinier. The Baron’s indifference sprang from his wife’s substantial income; the family often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque spot shaped by the famous Watteville. Watteville had built a dam and lake, enhancing the estate, but his heirs maintained a dubious claim over the village of Riceys, which historically held rights to the land. The Baron’s actions left a legacy of usurpation that his descendants continued.

Rosalie’s efforts were complex and subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey; the lake’s beauty captivated her and she associated it with love. On a suggested boat trip her father confided legal troubles with Riceys over land rights. Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus as their lawyer, insisting the Dent de Vilard must be secured and that a lawsuit would enliven their lives. She pressed to bring Savarus to les Rouxey, revealing her determination to secure her future and create beautiful gardens.

Four days later the Abbé de Grancey visited Savarus to press him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing such alignment would compromise his neutrality. The Abbé urged him to consider the political advantages, especially votes. Rosalie meanwhile tried to keep Savarus in Besançon and prevent his election, even writing to the préfet, who ignored her.

As the election approached, influential men in Besançon nominated Savarus—surprising him. He delivered a passionate speech outlining his vision and ambitions, yet faced opposition: the préfet worked to undermine him and his support eroded. The first voting day disappointed him; he returned in despair and thought of Francesca. Just then the Abbé arrived with encouraging news: he had secured some support, promising that winning the Rouxey lawsuit could bring crucial votes and urging Savarus to visit the Wattevilles. The Abbé even suggested marriage to Sidonie de Chavoncourt to solidify Savarus’s standing. Initially resistant, Savarus recognized the advantages. With the Abbé’s guidance, he prepared to navigate the intertwined complexities of love and politics, aware that his future hinged on imminent choices.

By nine the next evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were full of Besançon’s aristocracy, assembled over the unusual decision to go to the polls for the daughter of the Rupts. The man to be presented was the former Master of Appeals, secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch. Madame de Chavoncourt arrived with her younger daughter Sidonie, elegantly dressed; her elder daughter, secure in her relationship, had made no effort—an omission noted in a small town. The Abbe de Grancey moved among the groups, listening and directing the talk.

“If the Elder Branch returns,” he asked an elderly statesman, “what politicians would they find?” They spoke of Berryer, isolated on his bench and likely to unsettle the Government, of the Duc de Fitz-James in Toulouse, and how a vote for Monsieur Savarus might draw Republicans toward them instead of the Moderates.

By half-past nine Albert had not arrived, and Madame de Watteville took his lateness as a personal slight. Madame de Chavoncourt urged calm, suggesting he might be detained by a consultation. Rosalie, watching the two, noted her mother’s indulgence toward Albert, which hinted at a possible match between him and Sidonie.

At ten the delay grew embarrassing; some gentlemen began to play cards. The Abbe de Grancey, perplexed, went to a window where Rosalie stood and exclaimed, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville, and Rosalie went into the garden to check Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent. “Jérôme!” Rosalie called; the servant confirmed Albert had left in a post-chaise. The Abbe cried, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” Rosalie’s face, however, betrayed a joy the Vicar-General pretended to ignore.

Back in the drawing-room Monsieur de Watteville announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By eleven-thirty only about fifteen guests remained: Madame de Chavoncourt, the Abbe de Godenars, the two Chavoncourt girls, Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbe de Grancey, Rosalie, Amedée de Soulas—and a few others. The Abbe stationed himself near the Baroness to watch Rosalie, visibly agitated.

A servant then brought a letter for the Abbe de Grancey on a silver tray. Urged to read it, he watched Rosalie turn pale, recognized the handwriting, folded the letter and slipped it into his pocket. He understood she loved Albert. As he prepared to leave, Rosalie stopped him. “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!” she cried, and, when pressed, burst out, “I love him! What is the matter?” He told her Albert was withdrawing from the election. Stunned, she begged him to keep the news secret, knowing that if the election failed so might her sister’s chance with Albert.

On her way to Mass, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette that Albert had abruptly disappeared. An elderly Parisian had arrived at the Hotel National, summoned Monsieur Savaron, and left with a large package after a long meeting; Savaron, pale and distraught, bought an old carriage, ordered post-horses, left his keys, told his servant to find other employment, and fled. The Prefect quickly granted him a passport. The episode stirred Besançon prejudice against outsiders; most soon forgot Albert, except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news. The Baroness de Watteville pressed Rosalie to marry; the Baron offered her the family estate, les Rouxey, but the Baroness, worried about finances, arranged a match with Monsieur de Soulas. Rosalie firmly refused. The refusal became public during a summer at les Rouxey; after Mariette and Jerome married the Baron improved the estate, which the Baroness resented. The Vicar-General tried to mediate.

Rosalie then confessed to him that she had interfered in Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo — intercepting letters and engineering their separation. The Vicar-General, horrified, stressed the moral gravity of her actions and urged her to seek forgiveness.

Her guilt deepened when a Paris clerk arrived to sell Albert’s effects and revealed that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote and received a letter from Albert expressing complete forgiveness and his wish to remain in solitude. He urged Rosalie to reconcile with her mother and accept de Soulas, but she refused to sacrifice her mother’s happiness. After the Vicar-General’s death, Madame de Watteville married de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support.

Now of age and wealthy, Rosalie encouraged that marriage to secure her independence, went to Paris determined to confront the Duchess, and at a ball publicly confessed her role in Albert’s downfall, shocking the Duchess. She returned to Besançon burdened by the emotional cost. The story closed as a lasting tale of love, betrayal, and the consequences of ambition.

Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone at les Rouxey—riding, hunting and improving her estate—and was well known in the Eastern provinces. Madame de Soulas, mother of two, looks younger than her husband, who has grown old; he told young Chavoncourt, "My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!"

Known for eccentric "vagaries," she yearly visited the walls of the Grande Chartreuse, perhaps imitating a grand-uncle who once burst through its walls. In 1841 she left Besançon—rumored to be marrying—but returned after a Loire steamboat boiler explosion that cost her a right arm and a left leg and left disfiguring facial scars. Her health declined; in constant pain, she now lives in religious seclusion at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
