In Baronne de Watteville's drawing-room, a center of Besançon society, the Archbishop frequently visited due to her devout nature. Monsieur de Watteville, from a notorious lineage, married wealthy heiress Mademoiselle de Rupt in 1815, which intensified Madame de Watteville's piety. Their home, adorned with fine furnishings, showcased their social status and attracted notable clergy.

During a dinner in September 1834, guests welcomed Monsieur l’Abbe de Grancey, who announced a legal victory by young lawyer Monsieur Savaron. This piqued the interest of the guests, especially Madame de Watteville, who inquired about Savaron’s background. The Vicar-General noted Savaron lived nearby, but his origins were unclear, raising curiosity about his character.

Historically, France and England have influenced each other culturally, particularly in language and fashion. Styles deemed "English" in Paris are seen as "French" in London, reflecting mutual fascination. Recently, England introduced terms like "dandy" and "lion," the latter describing a flamboyant man, influencing towns like Besançon, where Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas emerged as a local "lion." Amedee, of Spanish descent, sought to elevate his status in a conservative town.

Living modestly, Amedee maintained a lavish lifestyle with the help of his stylish servant, Babylas. Despite his extravagant appearance, he struggled financially, relying on a limited income. His social life revolved around impressing the local aristocracy, particularly Rosalie de Watteville, a sheltered young woman from a wealthy family.

The dynamic between Amedee and Rosalie was complicated by social standings and familial expectations. Amedee aspired to marry Rosalie for her dowry, while her mother scrutinized his intentions. Despite Amedee's charm, he faced challenges in gaining their approval, as Rosalie remained indifferent. Ultimately, Amedee's pursuit of Rosalie was a strategic move to elevate his social standing, reflecting broader themes of aspiration and societal expectations.

Monsieur de Soulas shared the story of Albert Savaron, who moved from Paris to Besançon, rented an apartment, and registered as a barrister. Mademoiselle de Watteville recognized Savaron's noble lineage, but Amedee clarified that he was a commoner who had added a bar-sinister to his name to suggest nobility.

Initially overlooked by the local legal community, Savaron worked hard and kept to himself, leading to his obscurity. He attended early mass but remained enigmatic. His reputation shifted when he successfully defended a mentally challenged man accused of forgery, impressing the court and attracting clients. The Vicar-General noted Savaron's striking appearance and eloquence, emphasizing his calm demeanor despite a passionate nature.

Madame de Chavoncourt questioned Savaron's motivations for coming to Besançon and his lack of community integration. The Abbe speculated that Savaron would eventually make a significant impact.

Rosalie de Watteville, intrigued by Savaron, found him more appealing than her superficial suitors. She planned to meet him at church and felt a strong connection upon seeing him during mass. As her fascination grew, she encouraged her father to build a Belvedere in their garden to observe Savaron's private life. Meanwhile, Savaron launched a fortnightly publication, the _Eastern Review_, promoting Besançon's interests, which Rosalie's father subscribed to, allowing her to read its contents and deepening her infatuation.

In July 1823, friends Rodolphe and Leopold traveled to Switzerland. Rodolphe became captivated by Fanny Lovelace in Gersau and decided to stay while Leopold continued. Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, was passionate and sensitive, while Leopold was set to become a notary.

Rodolphe found lodging with the Stopfer couple and learned Fanny lived modestly as a translator and writer. Eager to meet her, he gained access to her gardens, where he suspected her mute servant, Gina, had a hidden background. 

One evening, while listening to Fanny sing, Rodolphe overheard her speaking Italian with Gina, suspecting they were refugees. After a misunderstanding led to Gina attacking him, Fanny and Gina helped him recover, deepening Rodolphe's attraction.

As Rodolphe healed, he learned of his mother’s death, which plunged him into despair. Leopold returned to France, leaving Rodolphe in Gersau, where he found solace in Fanny's company. Their bond deepened as they shared sorrows and dreams. Fanny revealed she was married to an older man, Emilio, but expressed hope for a future together.

Rodolphe's feelings for Fanny intensified, admiring her strength and independence. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential. Their conversations revealed a shared passion for freedom and liberal ideals. When Fanny received news that her family was safe and would return to Italy, she felt joy, but Rodolphe struggled with jealousy regarding her past. Despite these challenges, Rodolphe remained devoted to Fanny, vowing to wait for her. Their love blossomed against the stunning Swiss landscape, symbolizing their hopes amid political turmoil and personal sacrifice.

For twenty days, Rodolphe stayed with the Bergmanns, enchanted by Francesca, who embodied youthful imprudence and the complexities of womanhood. He was drawn to her affectionate yet playful relationship with her husband, Emilio. One evening, Francesca expressed her yearning for luxury, likening it to her love for art, and declined to join Rodolphe in Geneva, fearing gossip that could tarnish Emilio’s reputation. While professing her love for Rodolphe, she emphasized the need for discretion to protect their bond.

After the Bergmanns left, Rodolphe traveled to Geneva, settling in Eaux-Vives and seeking information about Italian refugees, particularly a bookseller named Lamporani. His curiosity led him to Villa Diodati, where he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage, revealing her ties to the aristocratic Colonna family.

Rodolphe struggled with feelings of inadequacy for loving a princess disguised as a commoner. He sought an audience with Prince Gandolphini, Francesca’s husband, who welcomed him warmly. Despite wanting to see Francesca, Rodolphe returned to his lodgings, preoccupied with thoughts of their future.

When Rodolphe finally encountered Francesca at a gathering, her beauty captivated him. Their eyes met, igniting a profound connection. Francesca’s subtle acknowledgment of their bond amidst the elite deepened Rodolphe’s feelings, solidifying his love for her. As they spent time together, Francesca encouraged Rodolphe to pursue his ambitions while remaining youthful, reflecting their shared commitment to love faithfully despite challenges.

Francesca’s background as a well-educated heiress, initially destined for a cloistered life, added depth to her character. Her strategic marriage to Prince Gandolphini contrasted with her revolutionary spirit, aligning with Rodolphe’s aspirations.

As spring approached, Rodolphe grappled with longing and anxiety. Tension arose when he compared Francesca to a French noblewoman, inadvertently hurting her. Sensing her distress, he sought to comfort her, but she instructed him to leave until they could meet again in Naples, marking a pivotal moment in their relationship.

Upon returning to Paris, Rodolphe discovered a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner, inspired by Francesca. Driven by ambition and love, he invested in a venture that failed, costing him three years. After the Villele ministry fell, he sought solace at Villa Gandolphini in Naples. Just as he was poised for success, the July 1830 revolution struck, but Rodolphe persevered, supported by love.

Meanwhile, Mademoiselle de Watteville was consumed by jealousy over Francesca, believing she loved Albert. This ignited a fierce desire to win his affection, yet she doubted her worthiness. Rosalie's imagination ran wild with schemes to connect with Albert, leading her to confront Mariette about her relationship with Jerome, offering silence in exchange for access to letters from Albert. 

Rosalie learned from her father that a copy of the _Eastern Review_ was sent to the Duchesse d’Argaiolo in Italy, fueling her jealousy. She intercepted a letter from Albert revealing his noble lineage and ambitions, intensifying her infatuation. Ultimately, Rosalie resolved to step back, believing she should sacrifice her desires for Albert and the Duchesse's happiness, grappling with unrequited love.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

In the spring of 1835, nineteen-year-old Rosalie sought to surpass the Duchesse d’Argaiolo, motivated by her solitude and desires. Albert de Savarus was advancing politically in Besançon, gaining support from influential figures to restore a crucial aqueduct. He concealed his true ambitions behind a facade of indifference, having previously arranged financing for a merchant's election bid. The Abbe de Grancey recognized Savarus's potential but noted his hesitation to align with the Wattevilles, fearing it would compromise his neutrality.

Visiting her family estate, les Rouxey, Rosalie learned of her father's legal troubles and suggested hiring Savarus as their lawyer to protect her inheritance. To keep him in Besançon, she attempted to undermine his political ambitions by informing the Prefect of his Royalist leanings. Despite her efforts, Savarus was nominated, and his eloquence garnered the townsfolk's support.

As the election neared, the Prefect and his allies worked to diminish Savarus's backing, revealing the intricacies of local politics. In despair over his unrequited love for Francesca, Savarus received encouragement from the Abbe, who urged him to connect with the Wattevilles, particularly Madame de Chavoncourt, whose daughter Sidonie could provide vital support. This led Savarus to contemplate a marriage alliance with Sidonie, recognizing the sacrifices required for his ambitions.

The following evening, Madame la Baronne de Watteville hosted a gathering of Besançon’s aristocracy to discuss the election. Concern grew when Savarus did not arrive, and while Madame de Chavoncourt dismissed his delay, Rosalie suspected otherwise. The Abbe speculated on his fate, and they learned he had left in a post-chaise, prompting mixed reactions.

As the evening progressed, only a few guests remained. The Abbe received a letter that affected Rosalie, revealing her feelings for Albert. He deduced her love for him and discovered that Albert had withdrawn from the election, leaving Rosalie desperate to keep this news secret to protect Sidonie's prospects.

The next morning, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette about Albert's sudden disappearance after a visit from an old gentleman, stirring Besançon and leading to Monsieur de Chavoncourt's election. Over time, most forgot Albert, except for a few, including Rosalie, who struggled with the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo's death and resisted her mother’s suggestion to marry Monsieur de Soulas.

Tensions escalated at les Rouxey, culminating in tragedy when the Baron drowned while retrieving a shell, deepening the rift between mother and daughter. Grief-stricken, Rosalie contemplated confessing her role in Albert's fate. The Vicar-General urged her to seek redemption, and Hannequin revealed that Albert had entered a monastery after heartbreak. Years later, Rosalie's mother married Monsieur de Soulas, but Rosalie remained devoted to Albert. She confronted the Duchess at a ball, revealing the truth about Albert's letters, leaving the Duchess shaken. Rosalie returned to Besançon, feeling a sense of closure marked by loss and a quest for redemption.

Now living alone at les Rouxey, Mademoiselle de Watteville is known for her solitary pursuits. After marrying in 1841, she returned severely injured from a steamboat accident, losing her right arm and left leg, and now leads a life devoted to religious practices at les Rouxey.
