                             DEDICATION

  To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

  Dear Child,--You, the joy of the household, you, whose pink or
  white pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of
  Wierzschovnia like a will-o’-the-wisp, followed by the tender eyes
  of your father and your mother,--how can I dedicate to _you_ a
  story full of melancholy? And yet, ought not sorrows to be spoken
  of to a young girl idolized as you are, since the day may come
  when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them? It is
  difficult, Anna, to find in the history of our manners and morals
  a subject that is worthy of your eyes, that no choice has been
  left me; but perhaps you will be made to feel how fortunate your
  fate is when you read the story sent to you by
                                                    Your old friend,
                                                         De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

At dawn of an October day in 1827 a sixteen-year-old youth, dressed in what modern phrase calls proletary, stood in a small square of Lower Provins. At that hour he could study the houses around the oblong space unnoticed. The mills by the river already whirred; their wheels, echoed from the Upper Town, intensified the silence so that the wheels of a distant diligence could be heard a league off. The two long sides of the square, separated by an avenue of lindens, were built in the simple style of bourgeois life. No signs of commerce were visible; the porte-cochères of the rich were few and seldom used, except that of Monsieur Martener, a physician with a cabriolet. Some fronts were covered by vines or roses climbing to second-story windows, scenting the air. One end of the square opens on the main street, the gardens reaching the river bank; the other opens onto a street parallel to the main road.

At that quieter end the youth recognized the house he sought: a front of white stone grooved as courses, closed gray blinds, slender iron balconies with yellow-painted rosettes. Above the ground and first floors rose three dormer windows in a slate roof; on the peak of the central one was a new weather-vane of a hunter shooting a hare. Three stone steps led to the door. A leaden pipe discharged sink-water into a gutter on one side, indicating the kitchen; on the other were two windows closely shuttered with heart-shaped openings to admit light, those of the dining-room. Vent-holes to the cellar were closed by painted iron shutters cut in open-work. Everything was new. This restored house, fresh against the older facades, suggested the paltry taste and self-satisfaction of a retired petty shopkeeper.

The young man inspected these details with a pleasure tinged with sadness; his eyes roved from the kitchen to the roof. The rosy glow of the rising sun fell on a calico curtain at one garret window; the others lacked that luxury, and his face brightened. He stepped back, leaned against a linden, and sang in the drawling tone of western France the Breton ditty, a song often sung to newly married couples:

  “We’ve come to wish you happiness in marriage,
    To m’sieur your husband
    As well as to you:

  “You have just been bound, madam’ la mariee,
    With bonds of gold
    That only death unbinds:

  “You will go no more to balls or gay assemblies;
    You must stay at home
    While we shall go.

  “Have you thought well how you are pledged to be
    True to your spouse,
    And love him like yourself?

  “Receive these flowers our hands do now present you;
    Alas! your fleeting honors
    Will fade as they.”

This native air, tender and melancholy, would awaken a Breton maiden’s memories, picturing the manners and feelings of her noble old land. As he finished the first couplet, the singer, never taking his eyes from the attic curtain, saw no sign of life. While he sang the second, the curtain stirred. At “Receive these flowers,” a face appeared; a white hand cautiously opened the casement, and a girl made a sign with her head as he closed with the melancholy thought, “Alas! your fleeting honors will fade as they.”

To her the young workman suddenly produced a yellow flower, common in Brittany and sometimes found in La Brie—the furze, or broom.

“Is it really you, Brigaut?” she said in a low voice.

“Yes, Pierrette, yes. I am in Paris. I have started to make my way; but I’m ready to settle here, near you.”

A window on the first floor beneath Pierrette’s attic creaked. The girl showed terror and whispered: “Run away!”

The lad jumped like a frightened frog to a bend in the street where a mill projected as the square opens into the main thoroughfare; but despite his agility his hob-nailed shoes echoed on the stones, easily distinguished from the mill’s music, and no doubt heard by the person who opened the window.

That person was a woman — an old maid. No man would have torn himself from a morning nap to listen to a minstrel in a jacket; only a maid wakes to songs of love. She opened her blinds with the furtive motion of a bat, looked about, saw nothing, and only heard the flying footfalls. Few sights are more dreadful than the matutinal apparition of an ugly old maid at her window. This keen-eared spinster lacked her usual embellishments; her false front and collarette were absent; she wore a little bag of black silk to cover her skull, revealed beneath a pushed-aside night-cap. This rumpled condition gave a menacing look to her head, such as painters bestow on witches. Wrinkles marked temples, ears, and nape in scarlet lines contrasting with the whiteness of her bed-gown. The gaping of that garment revealed a breast like that of an old peasant who cares little for appearance. The fleshless arm hung like a stick. At her window she seemed tall from the length and angularity of her face, recalling the exaggerated proportions of certain Swiss heads. Her countenance, lacking harmony and softened line, expressed an unfeeling spirit. Usually such features were softened by a commercial smile—a bourgeois smirk that mimicked good humor; in public she might pass for kindly. She owned the house with her brother. He slept so soundly that the orchestra of the Opera-house could not have awakened him.

The old maid stretched her neck from the window, raised cold pale-blue eyes with short lashes toward the attic, tried to see Pierrette, and, finding it useless, retreated like a tortoise drawing in her head. The blinds were then closed, and the silence of the street resumed, broken only by peasants coming in or very early persons moving about.

When there is an old maid in a house, watch-dogs are unnecessary; not the slightest event escapes her observation or comment. This trifling incident was therefore destined to excite grave suppositions and to open the way for one of those obscure family dramas—none the less terrible because secret.

Pierrette did not go back to bed. To her Brigaut’s arrival was an immense event. During the night—Eden of the wretched—she escaped the vexations and fault-findings she bore by day. Like the hero of a ballad, her sleep seemed her happy life; her waking hours a bad dream. She had her only pleasurable waking in three years. The memories of her childhood sang their melodious ditties in her soul. The first couplet was heard in a dream; the second made her spring from bed; at the third she doubted her ears; but when the fourth was sung, standing in her night-gown with bare feet at the window, she recognized Brigaut, the companion of her childhood. Ah, yes! it was truly the well-known square jacket with bobtails, pockets sticking out at the hips—the blue cloth jacket of Brittany; there, too, the printed cotton waistcoat, the linen shirt fastened by a gold heart, the large rolling collar, the earrings, stout shoes, and trousers of blue-gray drilling unevenly colored—those humble durable things of Breton peasantry. The big white-horn buttons that fastened the jacket made the girl’s heart beat. When she saw the bunch of broom her eyes filled with tears; then a dreadful fear drove back the budding happiness. She thought her cousin sleeping in the room below might have heard the noise she made in jumping to the window. The fear was just; the old maid had appeared, and Pierrette made Brigaut the terrified sign which he obeyed without understanding it. Such instinctive submission to a girl’s bidding reveals one of those innocent and absolute affections that blossom now and then in life. Whoever had seen the lad as he ran away would have loved the ingenuous chivalry of his feeling.

Jacques Brigaut was worthy of Pierrette Lorrain, who was just fifteen. Two children! Pierrette could not help crying as she watched his flight in the terror her gesture had conveyed. Then she sat in a shabby armchair before a small table topped by a mirror. Resting her elbows, head in hands, she thought for an hour, calling to memory the Marais, the village of Pen-Hoel, perilous pond voyages in a boat untied from an old willow by little Jacques; the faces of her grandfather and grandmother, her mother’s sufferings, Major Brigaut’s handsome face—in short her careless childhood. It was a dream, a luminous joy on the gloomy present.

Her beautiful chestnut hair escaped in disorder from her cap, rumpled in sleep—a cambric cap she had made. Little ringlets escaped from gray curl-papers at each side of her forehead. From the back hung a heavy braid half unplaited. Her whiteness betrayed the malady of girlhood, chlorosis, which deprives the body of color, destroys appetite, and disorders the organism. Waxy tones marked her flesh. The neck and shoulders betrayed wasted arms, flung forward and crossed upon the table. Her feet seemed enervated, the night-gown barely reached her knees, showing flaccid muscles, blue veins, and impoverished legs. The cold, which she heeded not, turned her lips violet; a sad smile drew up the corners of a sensitive mouth that showed small, ivory-white teeth. Her ears were dainty, and despite the roundness of her face she was lovely. All animation was in the eyes: the iris brown like Spanish tobacco with black flecks and golden reflections round brilliant pupils. Pierrette was made to be gay but was sad. Her lost gaiety still showed in the vivacious eye, the ingenuous grace of her brow, the smooth curve of her chin. The long eyelashes lay upon cheek-bones made prominent by suffering. Paleness rendered the lines and details purely distinct. The ear alone was a little masterpiece of modeling. Perhaps you would like to know her history, and this is it.

Pierrette’s mother was a Demoiselle Auffray of Provins, half-sister by the father to Madame Rogron, mother of the present owners of the house.

Monsieur Auffray had married at eighteen and again near sixty-nine. By his first marriage he had an only, plain daughter, married at sixteen to innkeeper Rogron; by his second he had a charming daughter, so that Madame Rogron was fifty when the half-sister was born and already had two grown children.

The youngest daughter married at eighteen a man of her choice, a Breton officer named Lorrain, captain in the Imperial Guard. The captain, anxious to rise to a colonelcy, exchanged into a line regiment. While he, then a major, and his wife enjoyed themselves in Paris on the allowance of Monsieur and Madame Auffray, or scoured Germany, old Auffray (formerly a grocer) died at eighty-eight without making a will. His property was administered by his daughter, Madame Rogron, and her husband so completely in their interests that nothing remained for the widow beyond the house she lived in on the little square and a few acres. This widow, the mother of Madame Lorrain, was thirty-eight at her husband’s death. Like many widows, she unwisely remarried. She sold the house and land to her step-daughter, Madame Rogron, and married a young physician named Neraud, who wasted her fortune. She died two years later of grief and misery.

Thus the share of her father’s property that should have come to Madame Lorrain almost entirely disappeared, reduced to a small sum of eight thousand francs. Major Lorrain was killed at the battle of Montereau, leaving his wife, then twenty-one, with a little daughter of fourteen months and only a pension and an eventual inheritance from his parents, Monsieur and Madame Lorrain, retail shopkeepers at Pen-Hoel in the Marais. These Lorrains, Pierrette’s grandparents, sold wood, slates, tiles, pipes. Their business, from incapacity or ill-luck, did poorly and gave them scarcely enough. The failure of Collinet at Nantes, caused by 1814 events and a fall in colonial products, deprived them of twenty-four thousand francs they had deposited with that house.

The arrival of their daughter-in-law was welcome. Her pension of eight hundred francs was a handsome income at Pen-Hoel. The eight thousand francs which the widow’s half-brother and sister Rogron sent her from her father’s estate were placed in the Lorrains’ business; they gave her a mortgage on a little house they owned in Nantes, let for three hundred francs and barely worth ten thousand.

Madame Lorrain the younger, Pierrette’s mother, died in 1819. The child was small, delicate, and weakly; the damp climate of the Marais did not agree with her. But the husband’s family persuaded her, to keep her with them, that in no other place could she find a healthier region. She was so petted that her death brought nothing but honor to the old Lorrains.

Some declared that Brigaut, an old Vendeen who had served under Charette and other leaders in the wars against the Republic, counted for much in the younger Madame Lorrain’s willingness to remain in the Marais. All Pen-Hoel saw him—called respectfully Major Brigaut—spending his days and evenings in the Lorrains’ parlor beside the window of the imperial major. Toward the last, the curate of Pen-Hoel urged old Madame Lorrain to have her daughter-in-law marry Brigaut, promising to have the major appointed justice of the peace through the Vicomte de Kergarouet’s influence. The young woman’s death ended the matter.

Pierrette was left with her grandparents, who owed her four hundred francs a year, interest on the little property placed in their hands. This small sum supported her. Growing less fit, they soon faced an active competitor who ruined them. With his connivance, the Lorrains borrowed on notes they could not meet, driving them into bankruptcy. Pierrette’s claim on the house in Nantes was superseded by her grandmother’s legal rights, enforced to secure daily bread. The house sold for nine thousand five hundred francs, of which one thousand five hundred went for costs. The remaining eight thousand came to Madame Lorrain, who lived on their income in a humble place at Nantes called Saint-Jacques, where the two old people had bed and board for a small payment.

As it was impossible to keep Pierrette with them, the old Lorrains wrote to her uncle and aunt Rogron in Provins. These Rogrons were dead. The letter might easily have been lost, but the post persistently traced it to Monsieur Rogron, son of the deceased, a mercer in the rue Saint-Denis in Paris; curiosity about a possible inheritance ensured its transmission, and the Treasury its fee.

These Rogrons, toward whom the old Lorrains, though dreading to part with their dear granddaughter, stretched their supplicating hands, became unexpectedly the masters of Pierrette’s destiny. It is therefore indispensable to explain both their antecedents and their character.

Pere Rogron, the innkeeper of Provins who had married old Auffray’s daughter by his first wife, presented a face inflamed by drink: a veiny nose, Bacchic redness, and cheeks to match. Short, fat, pot-bellied, with stout legs and thick hands, he had the alert shrewdness of a Swiss innkeeper but no beauty. His wife resembled him perfectly. Rogron loved good living and to be waited on by pretty girls. He was of that blunt, egoistic sort whose conduct is coarse and whose vices are displayed without shame. Grasping, selfish, indecent, and indulgent of his whims, he spent until his teeth failed but kept his selfishness. In old age he sold his inn, collected much of his late father-in-law’s estate, and moved into the little house in the Provins square bought cheaply from the widow of old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother.

The Rogrons lived on about two thousand francs a year from twenty-seven local lots and had twenty thousand from selling the inn. They occupied Auffray’s dilapidated house—“old rats in wrack and ruin.” Rogron took to gardening, enlarging the plot toward the river and building a stone embankment where aquatic flora grew.

Early in their marriage they had a son and a daughter, both plain, as neglect and coarse rural upbringing degraded their looks. Sent cheaply to nurse and left to cry while wet-nurses worked, they returned coarsened, voices harshened; their mother’s vanity suffered, and she tried stern corrections that their father’s severity made seem tender. The children were often left to run in stables, courtyards, or the streets, sometimes whipped, sometimes sent to the surly grandfather Auffray. The Rogrons justified taking most of the old man’s property by accusing him of wrongs, yet Rogron nevertheless provided for his children’s training: he sent his son to school, bought a man to save him from conscription, apprenticed his daughter Sylvie at thirteen to Parisians with six hundred francs for board, and two years later did the same for his son Jérôme-Denis. When asked what he intended for his children, Rogron was blunt: he would give them “a kick” to launch them on their own, recalling his own three kicks but claiming he would leave them ten louis. He insisted his children owed him a life and he expected no return.

Sylvie, apprenticed in rue Saint-Denis, “came to par” in two years and cost her parents nothing. At nineteen she earned four hundred francs; at twenty she was second demoiselle at the wholesale silk Maison Julliard. Jérôme-Denis joined Maison Guepin, a large mercer. Sylvie, as forewoman at twenty-one, earned a thousand francs a year; Jérôme at eighteen was head-clerk with twelve hundred. They saved and, around the end of 1815, pooled about twenty thousand francs to buy the property and goodwill of the “Family Sister,” a large retail shop. Sylvie kept the books and handled correspondence; Jérôme-Denis was head-clerk. By 1821, after five years of fierce competition, maintaining the business had consumed all their savings and kept them struggling.

Sylvie, despite being under forty, looked fifty from hard work and a sour expression. Jérôme, thirty-eight, presented a silly face: a retreating forehead, close-cut hair, dull eyes without flame or thought. Short and fat like his father, he seemed feeble rather than robust; his complexion had become livid from cramped back-shop life. Their small intelligence had been wholly absorbed by business routine—threads, needles, ribbons, pins, buttons, and tailors’ trimmings left them ignorant of anything beyond rue Saint-Denis. They had no knowledge of Paris outside their street and saw no field beyond the shop. Clever at scolding clerks and young women, they took pride in the glib exchanges of a busy counter; on slow days their mood darkened: “Bad weather for sales,” they would say.

Rogron’s parcel-tying skill made him admired by apprentices: he could fold, tie, and keep watch on shop and street, returning a parcel with a courteous phrase. Yet he lacked a head for the business as a whole and would have failed without his sister. Sylvie had practical sense and a genius for trade; she advised purchases and sent him to remote parts of France to save trifles. Her shrewdness drifted into speculation; necessity—the need to sustain a business—kept the machine in motion.

Rogron was essentially head-clerk: self-interest had not taught him wider understanding. He was often astonished when Sylvie directed the sale of an article below cost to clear it, yet he admired her decisions without reasoning them. He had the clownish face and vacuity of someone formed by solitary, deprived youth—reduced to needs. Sylvie had kept him from marrying, fearing a rival who might take power and bring expense.

His silliness showed in loquacity: he chattered to clerks, explaining minutiae with flat jokes—the shopkeeper’s “chaff.” Retail necessity cultivates a certain phraseology; petty dealers learn to utter sentences with no real meaning but with marketable success. Removed from their thousand explanations, they are, relative to thought, like fish out of water.

Both siblings lacked the feelings that give heart to life; their natures were shriveled by toil, privation, and the memory of hard apprenticeship. They were not implacable so much as unhelpful to those in misfortune: to them, virtue, honor, and loyalty meant the payment of bills. Irritable and stingy, they had a dreadful reputation among rue Saint-Denis merchants. Had it not been for their Provins connections—trips three or four times a year when the shop could be shut—they would have no clerks or young women. Old Rogron sent them the unfortunate young folk of his neighborhood, promising close training and eventual succession; families, seeing hope for a child’s advancement, sent those who were “most in the way.” But when clerks or young women escaped, they rejoiced, further tarnishing the Rogrons’ name—and the supply of new victims continued thanks to the indefatigable old father.

From fifteen Sylvie managed two faces: the amiable seller and the sour spinster. Her acquired commercial charm—smiles and a wheedling voice—was practiced; her true face, glimpsed through half-opened blinds, could be terrifying. Around the time the Lorrains’ letter arrived, they were mourning their father and reckoning their gains: they had inherited the house that had been, in truth, seized from Pierrette’s grandmother, plus certain lands bought by their father and money from usurious loans and mortgages to peasants. The annual stock-taking was done and the “Family Sister” fully paid. They owned about sixty thousand francs of merchandise, forty thousand in bank or cash, and the business’s value. Behind their private counter, seated on a striped-green Utrecht-velvet bench, they consulted what to do.

Every retail shopkeeper aspires to be bourgeois. By selling a business’s goodwill they might obtain over one hundred and fifty thousand francs—without counting the father’s inheritance. Placing their property in public funds could yield about four thousand francs a year apiece; selling the shop would free proceeds to repair and improve the inherited house and make it a good investment. Then they could live in Provins. Their forewoman was the daughter of a rich Donnemarie farmer who had lost seven of nine children in five years and was thus an object of interest; Rogron had tried, and failed, to marry her. Sylvie opposed that match and planned instead for the forewoman to succeed her.

To grasp a certain class of shopkeepers one must dig beneath the surface: their life rests on hope. They dream of managing a theatre, civic honors, a country-house ten miles from Paris with a “park,” plaster statues, fountains, and a place in the National Guard. For the Rogrons, Provins was a feverish obsession. Provins, with medicinal springs and celebrated roses, combined romance and rural charm; it became the promised land amid the muck of Saint-Denis.

Crossing the gray wheat plains to Provins, one reaches a hill and suddenly a town watered by two rivers appears, with a verdant valley stretching below. The upper town, with its ruins and ramparts, is solemn and airy; the lower, watered by the Voulzie and the Durtain, is a town of mills, inns, shops, retired merchants, diligences, and waggons—rich in historical memory, ravines, gardens, and riverbanks. Its children, who go out to make fortunes, always return; Provins inspires a faithfulness like that proverb “Die in one’s form.”

Jérôme and Sylvie constantly thought of Provins. While he sold thread, Jérôme saw the upper town; while piling button cards he imagined the valley; while rolling ribbons he followed the rivers. The square in the lower town became his specific dream: he pictured improving the inherited house—new front, bedrooms, salon, billiard-room, dining-room—and turning the kitchen garden into an English pleasure-ground with lawns, grottos, fountains, and statuary. Though their Paris bedrooms were sparsely furnished, they fancied fine furniture: Jérôme admired upholsterers’ windows and told his sister he had found pieces “that will just do.” He coveted and discarded goods in fancy, always imagining them better for Provins. Leaning on his doorstep with a vacant eye, he visualized gilded facades and gardens.

Sylvie, pen in hand, played bourgeois hostess in salon mirrors; the rue Saint-Denis atmosphere felt unhealthy to both. The market mud made them long for Provins roses. They were prey to nostalgia and monomania, held back only by the need to keep selling until their business sold. The valley’s promise comforted them because mercer’s business had been a hard desert to cross.

The Lorrains’ letter arrived amid these meditations. They knew little of their cousin Pierrette; their father had secured the Auffray property after they left home and said little of affairs. A genealogical discussion revealed Aunt Lorrain as the younger sister of their mother by their grandfather Auffray’s second marriage. It suggested that the second marriage divided property and had hurt their interests—an old grievance of their father’s.

They read the Lorrains’ request through that memory, and it did not favor Pierrette. Taking charge of an orphan cousin who might become legal heir if unmarried raised pragmatic anxieties. They had never seen Pierrette; how troublesome might a young girl prove? Would they be obliged to keep or marry her? If Jérôme later sought a Provins heiress, shouldn’t they preserve property for his children? Sylvie defined a suitable match as a stupid, rich, ugly girl who would accept governance. After debate they decided to refuse the Lorrains. Sylvie agreed to write the reply, but business delayed her; when their forewoman offered to buy the “Family Sister” stock and goodwill—and they accepted—the matter slipped from the old maid’s mind.

Sylvie and Jérôme departed for Provins four years before Brigaut’s entry into Pierrette’s story. Their doings in Provins would prove as consequential to Pierrette’s fate as their commercial past in Paris.

When the petty shopkeeper who has gone to Paris returns to the provinces there is always a trace of Parisian ideas that soon fades into provincial habits, leaving only superficial scratches where Paris has touched the town. The ex-shopkeeper’s transition into substantial bourgeois life acts like an illness. No retail shopkeeper can pass with impunity from perpetual chatter to provincial silence, from Parisian bustle to the stillness of a small town. Those who have saved property spend part of it on some long-nursed desire that now absorbs impulses once held in check by business. Some travel or take municipal posts; others hunt, fish, become usurers or speculators. The Rogrons, brother and sister, were seized by the desire to use the trowel and remodel their old house into something charming.

This fixed idea produced on the square of Lower Provins the front Brigaut examined, its interior arrangements, and handsome furnishings. The contractor never drove a nail without consulting the owners, requiring signatures on plans and specifications, and explaining every item’s manufacture and price. For choicer things they said the pieces had belonged to Monsieur Tiphaine or Madame Julliard or other notables. The desire to have things like the rich bourgeois of Provins prevailed.

“Oh, if Monsieur Garceland has it, put it in,” said Mademoiselle Rogron. “His taste is good.”

“Sylvie, see, he wants ovolos in the cornice of the corridor.”

“What an odd name!”

“It means eggs—from the Latin ovum.”

“What queer fellows you architects are!” cried Rogron. “It’s stepping on egg-shells to deal with you.”

“Shall we paint the corridor?” asked the builder.

“Good heavens, no!” cried Sylvie. “That would be five hundred francs more!”

“Oh, the salon and staircase are too pretty not to have the corridor decorated,” urged the man. “That Madame Lesourd had hers painted last year.”

“And now her husband, as king’s attorney, must leave Provins.”

“Ah, he’ll be chief justice some day,” said the builder.

“How about Monsieur Tiphaine?”

“He’s got a pretty wife and will get on. He’ll go to Paris. Shall we paint the corridor?”

“Yes, yes,” said Rogron. “The Lesourds must see that we are as good as they.”

The first year after their return the Rogrons spent their days in such discussions, watching workmen, answering townspeople’s questions, and trying to make themselves socially intimate with Provins’ principal families.

They had never been into Parisian society; knowing no one there, they were now athirst for social life. In Provins they found their former masters returned from Paris—Monsieur and Madame Julliard of the “Chinese Worm,” the Guepin clan whose youngest now kept the “Three Distaffs,” and Madame Guenee, from whom they had bought the “Family Sister.” These families had spread through the town. The mayor, Monsieur Garceland, was a Guepin son-in-law; the curate, Abbé Peroux, brother to Madame Julliard; the judge, Monsieur Tiphaine junior, brother to Madame Guenee.

The queen of the town was the beautiful Madame Tiphaine junior, daughter of Madame Roguin, a rich Parisian’s wife. Clever, delicate, pretty, taken from a convent to marry in the provinces to please her mother, Mélanie Tiphaine behaved admirably in Provins. Handsomely dowered and still hopeful of advancement, her ambition was to have Monsieur Tiphaine elected deputy; as deputy he would become a judge in Paris, and she was determined to push him into the Royal courts. To that end she courted all vanities and pleased all parties; she succeeded. Twice a week she received the bourgeoisie of Provins in her Upper-town salon. At twenty she had made no blunders; she gratified every self-love and became the pride of Provins. She had not said a word of her designs, yet every elector awaited Monsieur Tiphaine’s nomination. Every man regarded the future deputy as friend and protector; of course he would attain honors and reward Provins.

Madame Guenee had married her daughters into the town’s leading families and married Monsieur Garceland the collector herself; mother and daughters regarded Monsieur Tiphaine as the richest, ablest man in the family. The prosecuting attorney hoped to replace his uncle as judge. The four ladies formed a court around Madame Tiphaine, following her ideas and advice. Monsieur Julliard, eldest son of the old merchant and married to a farmer’s daughter, had fallen into a secret, disinterested passion for Madame Tiphaine. She played on him, advised him to start a journal, and acted as his Egeria. For two years Julliard had published the “Bee-hive,” filled with literary, archaeological, and medical articles by the family; advertisements paid expenses, subscriptions provided profit. Occasional incomprehensible verses addressed “TO HER!!!” united the Julliards with other clans; Madame Tiphaine’s salon naturally became the first in town. The few aristocrats had their own salon at the old Comtesse de Breautey’s.

During their first six months the Rogrons were received, first by Madame Julliard the elder and Madame Garceland, then, after difficulty, by Madame Tiphaine. All studied them before admitting them. Society aims to amalgamate persons of equal wealth, education, manners, accomplishments, and character. The Guepins, Guenees, and Julliards were better positioned than the Rogrons, whose father had been held in contempt for his private life and conduct over the Auffray property—facts known to notary Auffray.

In the social life Madame Tiphaine dignified, everything was homogeneous; people understood one another. The Rogrons fancied they would be accepted after being received by Mayor Garceland. Sylvie practiced boston; Rogron, unable to play, twirled his thumbs and could talk of nothing but his new house. Words choked him; he would rise, try to speak, become frightened, and sit down with a comic distortion of the lips. Sylvie betrayed herself at cards—sharp, irritable, whining when she lost, insolent when she won; nagging and quarrelsome, she became a scourge. Yet the pair possessed silly, open ambition and tried to play a part in a town already dominated by a close corporation of twelve allied families. Having spent thirty thousand francs restoring their house, and possessing between them at least ten thousand francs a year, they believed themselves wealthy and sought to impress society, which quickly measured their vulgarity, ignorance, and envy.

At their presentation to Madame Tiphaine, she had already eyed them and remarked to Julliard junior, who lingered: “You all seem taken with those Rogrons.”

“No,” he said. “They bore my mother and annoy my wife. When Mademoiselle Sylvie was apprenticed to my father thirty years ago, none could endure her.”

“I have a great mind,” said Madame Tiphaine, putting her pretty foot on the fender bar, “to make it understood my salon is not an inn.”

Julliard raised his eyes to the ceiling as if to admire her wit.

“I wish my society to be select; and it certainly will not be if I admit those Rogrons.”

“They have neither heart, nor mind, nor manners,” said Monsieur Tiphaine. “If, after selling thread for twenty years, as my sister did for example—”

“Your sister, my dear,” said his wife, “cannot be out of place in any salon.”

“If,” he continued, “people are stupid enough not to throw off the shop and polish their manners, if they mistake the Counts of Champagne for the accounts of a wine-shop, as Rogron did this evening, they had better stay at home.”

“They are simply impudent,” said Julliard. “They talk as if no other handsome house in Provins but theirs existed. They want to crush us; yet they have hardly enough to live on.”

“If it was only the brother,” said Madame Tiphaine, “one might put up with him; give him a Chinese puzzle and he’d stay in a corner; but Mademoiselle Sylvie, with that voice like a hoarse hyena and those lobster-claws of hands! Don’t repeat all this, Julliard.”

When Julliard left, Madame Tiphaine told her husband, “I have aborigines enough whom I must receive; these two will kill me. I shall deprive myself of their society.”

“You are mistress in your house,” he replied, “but that will make enemies. The Rogrons will fling themselves into the opposition, which so far has no real strength in Provins. That Rogron is already intimate with Baron Gouraud and the lawyer Vinet.”

“Then,” Mélanie laughed, “they will do you service. Where there are no opponents, there is no triumph. A liberal conspiracy, an illegal cabal, a struggle of any kind, will bring you into the foreground.”

The justice looked at his wife with alarmed admiration.

Next day it was whispered the Rogrons had not succeeded in Madame Tiphaine’s salon. Her “inn” remark was much admired. A month passed before she returned Mademoiselle Sylvie’s visit; such insolence is noticed in the provinces.

During the evening Sylvie had spent at Madame Tiphaine’s, a disagreeable scene occurred between her and old Madame Julliard over boston—Sylvie accusing the old lady of cheating. The next time the mistress had the card-tables made up before Sylvie arrived; Sylvie was reduced to wandering between tables as an onlooker, players glancing at her scornfully. At Madame Julliard senior’s they played whist, a game Sylvie did not know.

The old maid finally understood she was banned, but not why. She fancied jealousy. Invitations stopped, but the Rogrons persisted in paying evening visits. Satirical people teased them—amusingly, not spitefully—tempting them to talk absurdly about the eggs in their cornice and their wonderful cellar of wine.

Before long the house was finished. The Rogrons resolved to give sumptuous dinners both to return civilities and exhibit luxury. The invited guests accepted from curiosity. The first dinner was for town leaders: Monsieur and Madame Tiphaine (who had never dined with them), Monsieur and Madame Julliard senior and junior, Monsieur Lesourd, Monsieur le curé, and Monsieur and Madame Garardon. It was one of those long provincial dinners from five to nine. Madame Tiphaine had introduced the Parisian custom of leaving once coffee was served. That evening she had company and was anxious to go. The Rogrons accompanied her and her husband to the door; on returning disconcerted at not keeping them, the rest prepared to imitate Madame Tiphaine’s fashion with cruel provincial promptness.

“They won’t see our salon lighted up,” said Sylvie, “and that’s the show.”

They had counted on surprising their guests; it was the first admission to the “Rogron palace,” and the company at Madame Tiphaine’s awaited her opinion.

“Well!” cried Madame Martener. “You’ve seen the Louvre; tell us about it.”

“All? Well, it would be like the dinner—not much.”

“But describe it.”

Madame Tiphaine complied, sketching the house: the gilded front door opening on a long corridor that divided the house unevenly; a portico and lawn with a sun dial and a painted plaster statue of Spartacus; a staircase painted to imitate black marble, a walnut balustrade, cellar stairs under it; a dining-room communicating by folding doors with a salon overlooking the garden. The corridor supplied an ante-chamber. The Rogrons boasted of using only French woods: chestnut floors, chestnut sideboards and chairs. White calico curtains with red borders held by red straps on wooden rods ending in brass lion-paws. A dial hanging by a gilded napkin over a sideboard amused Madame Tiphaine only as a conceit. Lamps like restaurant counter lights, an ornate barometer Rogron adored, a white porcelain stove in a niche, crimson and gold paper like that in restaurants—everything clean, new, and harsh in tone. The dining-room, she conceded, had character but was marred by five engravings—Poniatowski, Napoleon, defence at Clichy, two Mazepas—in gilt frames of the vilest description. She preferred Madame Julliard’s Louis XV pastels and old provincial silver and china: “The provinces are provinces; they are only ridiculous when they mimic Paris.”

“As for the salon,” she said, “it is all red—the red Mademoiselle Sylvie turns when she loses at cards.”

“Sylvan-red,” said Monsieur Tiphaine.

“Window-curtains, red; furniture, red; mantelpiece, red veined yellow; heavy, common candelabra and clock mounted on bronze, Roman standards with Greek foliage; an inevitable lion decoratively holding a ball; applied mouldings on a glass over the chimney; a chandelier wrapped in green muslin in the worst taste; walls papered in red flock to imitate velvet with chromo-lithographs in stucco festooned frames; furniture in cashmere and elm in rigid uniformity—two sofas, two easy-chairs, two armchairs, six common chairs; an alabaster vase a la Medicis under glass; card-tables before the windows; an Aubusson carpet in the most vulgar pattern. The room looks as if no one lived there: no books, no engravings, no knick-knacks, no flowers—cold and barren like Mademoiselle Sylvie. Buffon says the style is the man; salons have styles.”

From this sketch one sees the Rogrons’ house and the absurdities a clever builder had foisted on them—new inventions, fantastic ornaments, systems for preventing smoke and damp, painted marquetry panels, colored glass, superfine locks—vulgarities that make a house expensive and gratify bourgeois taste.

No one chose to visit the Rogrons; their social plans failed. Invitations were refused—“already engaged” to Madame Garceland and others. The Rogrons had supposed a few dinners would secure a position. Only young men who would mock them and habitués who dined everywhere accepted.

Frightened at the loss of forty thousand francs swallowed in her “dear house,” Sylvie began to recover by economy. She gave no more dinners, sent away her cook, took a country-girl, and did her own cooking “for pleasure.”

Fourteen months after returning, they had fallen into solitude and idleness. Banished from society, Sylvie developed a strong hatred for the Tiphaines, Julliards, and the rest of the “clique”; she wanted a rival clique, but the lesser bourgeoisie comprised only small shopkeepers free only on Sundays and stained individuals like Vinet and the Bonapartist Baron Gouraud, whom Rogron thoughtlessly befriended despite warnings.

The brother and sister sat by the stove, recalling their former business, customers, and things they had wanted to forget. Ennui weighed on them by the second winter. They did not know how to pass each day: sometimes, as they went to bed, they said, “There’s another over!” They lingered in bed and dressed slowly. Rogron shaved daily, examined his face, consulted his sister on imagined changes, quarreled with the servant about hot water, wandered into the garden to inspect shrubs, the kiosk, joinery, settling walls, or fretted about a sick hen and damp spots. The barometer was his most useful possession; he tapped it like a friend. Breakfast took ages; their digestion was perfect. They read the “Bee-hive” and the “Constitutionnel,” the cost shared by Vinet and Baron Gouraud. Rogron delivered the paper to Gouraud, enjoying his long yarns and puzzling over warnings he had received—yet he foolishly repeated “the clique’s” suspicions to the colonel, who in turn talked about Madame Tiphaine and the ministerialists.

About two o’clock Rogron took a walk, happy if a shopkeeper stopped him to ask news. He would gather gossip, go to the Upper town or ravines, meet old men, and sometimes learned scholars. His bewilderment at hearing a deputy-judge, Desfondrilles, an archaeologist, speak to the learned Monsieur Martener about Provins’ springs and catacombs was great. Desfondrilles thought Provins should rival Spa; Martener explained towns and houses rarely recover from ruin. Rogron learned Provins had been a capital in the twelfth century and that the Upper town stood on extensive catacombs like cathedral naves—an archaeological hobby that occupied Rogron for days and gave topics for evenings.

Rogron’s walks gave him bits of information he narrated to Sylvie. He would ask the same person the same day, “Well, what’s the news?” and then fling himself on the sofa as if exhausted. Dinner came after he had gone to the kitchen twenty times and compared clocks. When they stayed home, the evenings were desert-like. Passersby sometimes heard unearthly sounds that were only yawns. The two, having nothing to grind between their rusty wheels, creaked at each other. Rogron spoke of marrying in despair; the thought of a woman frightened him. Sylvie, realizing the need for a third person, remembered the little cousin about whom no one had inquired—perhaps presumed dead—Pierrette Lorrain.

Sylvie, too careful to lose anything, pretended to have found the Lorrains’ letter to raise the prospect of Pierrette coming. She answered Madame Lorrain’s letter half affectionately, half commercially, blaming delay on change of abode, hinting Pierrette might inherit twelve thousand francs a year if Jérôme did not marry.

Like caged beasts, the siblings impatiently awaited the cousin. Three days after writing, they already wondered when she would arrive. Sylvie perceived in this spurious benevolence a way to recover social standing. She went to call on Madame Tiphaine to mention Pierrette’s coming, deploring the girl’s misfortune and posing as eager to succor her.

“You have been rather long in discovering her,” said Madame Tiphaine with sarcasm.

A few words by Madame Garceland during cards recalled the shameful conduct of old Rogron over the Auffray property; the notary explained the iniquity. Monsieur Tiphaine asked, “Where is the little girl now?”

“In Brittany,” said Rogron.

“Brittany is a large place,” remarked Monsieur Lesourd.

“Her grandfather and grandmother Lorrain wrote to us—when was that?” Rogron asked his sister.

Sylvie, asking Madame Garceland where she had bought her gown’s stuff, answered hastily, “Before we sold the business.”

“And have you only just answered the letter, mademoiselle?” the notary asked.

Sylvie turned red. “We wrote to the Institution of Saint-Jacques,” Rogron said.

“That is an almshouse for old people,” said Desfondrilles, knowing Nantes. “They receive no one under sixty.”

“She is there, with her grandmother,” Rogron insisted.

“Her mother had a little fortune, the eight thousand francs your grandfather left to her,” the notary said, making the blunder intentionally.

“Ah!” said Rogron, stupidly missing the sarcasm.

“Then you know nothing about your cousin’s position or means?” asked Monsieur Tiphaine.

“If Monsieur Rogron had known it,” the deputy-judge said, “he would never have left her so long in such an establishment. I remember a Nantes house belonging to Monsieur and Madame Lorrain was sold under court order, and Mademoiselle Lorrain’s claim was swallowed up. I was commissioner at the time.”

The notary spoke of Colonel Lorrain, who, had he lived, would have been amazed his daughter was in such an institution. The Rogrons retreated, blaming malicious world. Sylvie saw her benevolence had missed its effect; she had lost ground. Thereafter the Rogrons no longer concealed their hatred of that class. The brother told Sylvie the scandals Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet had suggested about the Tiphaines, Guenees, Garcelands, and Julliards: accusing Madame Tiphaine’s family of fraud and claiming her fortune was stolen—“and such people set up to be proud!” he fumed.

Once they began to declaim against “the clique,” they set out, unknowingly, to have a society of their own: their house was to become a rendezvous for the floating elements of the liberal party. Colonel Gouraud and the lawyer Vinet, who felt ostracized and shared opinions, had watched the Rogrons’ launch with curiosity. Both professed patriotism—partly to become of consequence. The Liberals in Provins were confined to one old soldier who kept a cafe, an innkeeper, Monsieur Cournant the notary, Doctor Neraud, and a few scattered farmers. The colonel and the lawyer, delighted to find a fool whose money could aid their schemes and whose house could serve as a meeting-ground, exploited the Rogrons’ ill-will toward the upper class. They already shared a subscription to the “Constitutionnel”; it would not be hard for the colonel to convert the ex-mercer into a Liberal, though Rogron knew so little of politics he might take Sergeant Mercier’s exploits for those of a brother shopkeeper.

Pierrette’s expected arrival made the duo’s selfish ideas ripen. Gouraud, a veteran accustomed to horrors, felt little repelled by the thought of a poor cousin and began eyeing Sylvie’s fortune. The imperial colonel, short and fat with enormous ear rings and bushy tufts, wore sparse grizzled whiskers called “fins.” His jolly red face was discolored from campaigns. He had a cavalry-built stomach, a gray moustache hiding a blustering mouth, a sabre-cut on his nose, thick nasal speech, short broad hands “the hands of a rascal,” slender legs—fat, active, indolent but lawless, experienced in life, with contempt for social convention. He had the Legion of Honor cross; his emoluments and retired pay gave him about three thousand francs a year.

The lawyer Vinet, tall and thin, had liberal opinions instead of talent and earned meagerly. In Provins lawyers plead their own cases; the court was unfavorable to Vinet because of his opinions, so even agrarian Liberals preferred judges’ congenial lawyers. Vinet was disfavored also for having seduced a rich girl near Coulommiers, forcing her parents to marry her to him. Madame Vinet was a Chargeboeuf, an old La Brie family; her parents had arranged to leave their fortune to their son, perhaps charging him to provide for his sister’s children. Vinet’s first bold efforts failed. Driven by poverty and ashamed of failing to give his wife a fitting appearance, he had tried public life but the Chargeboeufs refused influence. Repulsed, filled with hatred for his wife’s family and the government that denied him a place, Vinet became a Liberal, hoping opposition triumph would raise him. He lived miserably in the Upper town; his wife seldom left. Madame Vinet had no defenders except an old Madame de Chargeboeuf in Troyes. Vinet’s conscience grew elastic; his gall increased. His face, viperish head, slit mouth, gleaming eyes through glasses, and sharp rasping voice frightened courtroom acquaintances. His muddy green-yellow skin showed the jaundice of balked ambition. He could talk, argue, and was shrewd; accustomed to view everything for personal advantage, he was fitted for politics; a man who shrinks from nothing so long as it’s legal is strong—Vinet’s strength lay there.

This future parliamentary athlete had a terrible influence on Pierrette’s fate. At present he aimed to found a newspaper at Provins. After studying the Rogrons from a distance (with the colonel’s aid), he concluded the brother might be made useful. He was not mistaken: Vinet’s days of poverty were ending after seven wretched years. The day Gouraud told him the Rogrons had quarrelled with the Upper-town bourgeois aristocracy, he nudged the colonel and said, with a knowing look:—

“One woman or another—handsome or ugly—you don’t care; marry Mademoiselle Rogron and we can organize something at once.”

“I have thought of it,” replied Gouraud, “but they’ve sent for Colonel Lorrain’s daughter; she’s their next of kin.”

“You can get them to make a will in your favor. Ha! you’d get a very comfortable house.”

“As for the little girl—well, let’s see her,” said the colonel, leering wickedly, showing how little respect he felt for any girl.

After her grandparents were admitted into a hospital where they expected to die, Pierrette, young and proud, could not bear living on charity. She was relieved when she learned she had well-off relations. Brigaut, the son of her mother’s friend the major and her childhood companion, apprenticed as a cabinet-maker in Nantes, offered the sixty francs he had saved in tips to pay her way to Paris—money she gratefully accepted with the indifference of true affection.

Brigaut used to visit every Sunday to play with Pierrette and comfort her. The vigorous young workman took pleasure in protecting the girl he loved. They had planned futures together: Brigaut, armed with his plane, would scour the world to make their fortune while Pierrette waited.

In October 1824, just after her eleventh birthday, the two old people and Brigaut, sorrowful, entrusted Pierrette to the diligence conductor from Nantes to Paris, begging him to put her on the Provins coach and take good care of her. Poor Brigaut followed the coach as far as he could; when he finally stopped his tear-wet eyes still watched the vehicle. Pierrette cried too, leaning from the window until the coach vanished.

The Lorrains and Brigaut were so ignorant of the world that Pierrette arrived in Paris penniless. The conductor, whom she had told about her "rich friends," paid her hotel expenses and arranged with the Provins conductor to take care of the child and to be reimbursed by the family, treating her like a parcel. Four days after leaving Nantes, about nine on a Monday night, a kind old Messageries-royales conductor took Pierrette by the hand while porters discharged packages and passengers in the Grand’Rue and led her—her only luggage a bundle with two dresses, two chemises, and two pairs of stockings—to Mademoiselle Rogron’s house, pointed out by the coach-office director.

“Good-evening, mademoiselle and the rest of the company. I’ve brought you a cousin, a nice little girl. You have forty-seven francs to pay me, and sign my book.”

Mademoiselle Sylvie and her brother were dumb with astonishment.

“The coach is waiting. Sign my book and pay me forty-seven francs, sixty centimes, and whatever you please for myself and the conductor from Nantes; we’ve cared for the child as if she were our own—paid for her beds and food, her fare to Provins, and other things.”

“Forty-seven francs, twelve sous!” said Sylvie.

“You’re not going to dispute it?” cried the man.

“Where’s the bill?” asked Rogron.

“Bill! look at the book.”

“Stop talking, and pay him,” said Sylvie.

Rogron fetched the money and gave the man forty-seven francs, twelve sous.

“And nothing for my comrade and me?” asked the conductor.

Sylvie took two francs from her old velvet key-bag.

“Thank you, no,” said the man; “keep ’em yourself. We rather cared for the little one for her own sake.” He left, muttering to the servant-girl about crocodiles out of Egypt.

“Such men are always brutal,” said Sylvie, overhearing.

“They took good care of the little girl, anyhow,” said Adele, hands on hips.

“We don’t have to live with him,” said Rogron.

“Where’s the little one to sleep?” asked Adele.

Thus Pierrette Lorrain arrived at her cousins’ home: tossed to them like a parcel, with no transition between the wretched chamber at Saint-Jacques and their dining-room, which seemed a palace. She was shy and speechless. To others she might have been enchanting in her coarse blue flannel petticoat, pink cambric apron, thick shoes, blue stockings, white kerchief, and red worsted mittens edged with white, with a dainty Breton cap washed for the journey forming a halo round her face. The cap made her virginal grace; her rosy, artless face glowed with health.

“Well, are you not going to say anything? I am your cousin Sylvie, and that is your cousin Rogron.”

“Do you want something to eat?” Rogron asked.

“When did you leave Nantes?” asked Sylvie.

“Is she dumb?” Rogron asked.

“Poor little dear, she has hardly any clothes,” cried Adele, opening the bundle.

“Kiss your cousin,” said Sylvie.

Pierrette kissed Rogron; then Sylvie.

“She is tired out with her journey, poor little thing; she wants to go to sleep,” said Adele.

A sudden, invincible aversion for her two relatives overwhelmed Pierrette—a feeling none had ever before excited in her. Sylvie and the maid took her up to a modest bedroom where Brigaut later noticed the white cotton curtain. The room held a little bed with a blue pole and a calico curtain, a walnut bureau, a small table, a looking-glass, a common night-table, and three chairs. The walls were hung with shabby paper; the tiled floor was icy. A narrow carpet lay by the bedside; on the mantelpiece sat a mirror, two candelabra, and a vulgar alabaster cup.

“You will be comfortable here, my little girl?” Sylvie asked.

“Oh, it’s beautiful!” the child said in a silvery voice.

“She’s not difficult to please,” muttered the servant. “Sha’n’t I warm her bed?” she added.

“Yes,” said Sylvie, “the sheets may be damp.”

Adele brought one of her own night-caps when she returned with the warming-pan. Pierrette, who had only known coarse linen sheets, was amazed at the fineness of the cotton ones. When Adele and Sylvie went downstairs, Adele could not help saying, “All she has isn’t worth three francs, mademoiselle.”

Since Sylvie’s careful household regime began she had compelled the maid to sit in the dining-room so one fire and one lamp could do for all, except on evenings when Colonel Gouraud and Vinet came.

Pierrette’s arrival enlivened the evening. “We must get her some clothes to-morrow,” said Sylvie; “she has absolutely nothing.”

“No shoes but those she had on, which weigh a pound,” said Adele.

“That’s always so, in their part of the country,” remarked Rogron.

“How she looked at her room! though it isn’t handsome enough for a cousin of yours, mademoiselle.”

“It is good enough; hold your tongue,” Sylvie snapped.

“Gracious, what chemises! coarse enough to scratch her skin off; not a thing can she use here,” said Adele, emptying the bundle.

Master, mistress, and servant debated until late what cambric to buy, how many stockings, how many under-petticoats, and what material, reckoning the cost.

“You won’t get off under three hundred francs,” Rogron declared.

“Three hundred francs!” cried Sylvie.

“Yes, three hundred. Add it up.”

They recalculated and found the cost would indeed be about three hundred francs, not counting the making.

“Three hundred francs at one stroke!” Sylvie said to herself before bed.

Pierrette belonged to those children of love whom love endows with tenderness, vivacity, gaiety, nobility, and devotion. Nothing had yet wounded her fawn-like heart, but the cold greeting of her cousins repressed it painfully. If Brittany had been outwardly poor, it had been full of affection. The old Lorrains were poor merchants, incapable in business but loving and frank, incautious and uncalculating. At Pen-Hoel Pierrette had received no education but that of nature: she roamed freely on a boat on the pond, in the fields with Jacques Brigaut, living the simple joys of childhood—fishing, catching insects, gathering flowers, gardening, sliding and snow games—welcomed everywhere.

When education began, misfortunes came too. Jacques, left without means after his father’s death, was apprenticed and fed by charity. Until taken to Saint-Jacques, Pierrette had known only affection and protection. Accustomed to confide in such love, she missed it in her eagerly desired rich relations. Even conductors had shown her more kindness. Bewildered, she wanted to cry without knowing why; being very tired, she slept.

Next morning, waking early as country children do, she dressed softly and admired the staircase’s painted walls, brasses, and fixtures. She went to the garden-door but couldn’t open it, so waited until Adele stirred, then flew out to the garden, claimed it, ran to the river, marvelled at the kiosk, and sat there content until her cousins rose. At breakfast Sylvie scolded:

“Was it you, trotting over my head by daybreak and making that racket on the stairs? You woke me so I couldn’t get to sleep. You must be very good and quiet, and amuse yourself without noise. Your cousin doesn’t like noise.”

“And you must wipe your feet,” said Rogron. “You went into the kiosk with dirty shoes and tracked the floor. Your cousin likes cleanliness. A great girl like you ought to be clean. Weren’t you clean in Brittany? But I recollect when I went down there to buy thread it was pitiable to see the folks—they were like savages. At any rate she has a good appetite.”

Thus Pierrette was wounded by remarks she could not fully understand. Her straightforward nature, previously unreflective, was slow to perceive her cousins’ hardness. After breakfast the brother and sister took her to the salon to show its splendors and instruct her not to touch them. Many celibates, through loneliness, substitute artificial affections for natural ones; Rogron and Sylvie had come to love their house and furniture immoderately. Sylvie began helping Adele in the mornings to dust, under pretence of teaching Pierrette how to keep things pristine. Dusting became an occupation; the furniture, instead of losing value, became ever more precious. Sylvie maintained a closet of wool, wax, varnish, brushes, feather dusters; her cold blue eyes scoured the furniture and under it; you could as soon find a tender spot in her heart as a bit of fluff under the sofa.

After a remark at Madame Tiphaine’s, Sylvie, no longer ashamed of the expense, agreed to spend three hundred francs on Pierrette’s clothes. For the first week much of their time was consumed in ordering frocks, trying them on, cutting out chemises and petticoats, and engaging a seamstress. Pierrette did not know how to sew.

“That’s pretty bringing up!” said Rogron. “Don’t you know how to do anything, little girl?”

Pierrette, who knew only how to love, made an ingenuous gesture.

“What did you do in Brittany?” Rogron asked.

“I played,” she answered simply. “Everybody played with me. Grandmamma and grandpapa told me stories. Ah! they all loved me!”

“Hey! didn’t you take it easy!” Rogron sneered.

Pierrette’s simple replies earned her a reputation for stupidity with Sylvie, who complained to Mademoiselle Borain, the best seamstress in Provins, while the seamstress looked kindly at the child. Pierrette preferred the sewing-women to her relatives; she watched their work and made the pretty speeches of childhood that Sylvie tried to silence. The seamstresses liked her, though their work caused grumbling.

“That child will make us pay through the nose!” cried Sylvie.

“Stand still, my dear, and don’t plague us; it is all for you and not for me,” she would snap when Pierrette was measured. Sometimes, when Pierrette asked the seamstress a question, Sylvie rebuked her: “Let Mademoiselle Borain do her work, and don’t talk to her; it is not you who are paying for her time.”

Mademoiselle Borain sewed firmly at Sylvie’s insistence. Sylvie poured the same vanity into Pierrette’s wardrobe as into the house, determined her cousin should be as well dressed as Madame Garceland’s little girl. She bought fashionable boots, fine cotton stockings, a well-made corset, a dress of blue reps, a pretty cape lined with white silk; the underclothes matched; Pierrette’s chemises were of fine Madapolam calico; she had embroidered drawers like the sub-prefect’s daughters and a charming drawn bonnet of blue velvet lined with white satin.

Thus attired, Pierrette was the most enchanting little girl in Provins. On Sunday after church all the ladies kissed her; Mesdames Tiphaine, Garceland, Galardon, Julliard and the rest fell in love with the sweet Breton child. This enthusiasm flattered Sylvie’s self-love; she credited it to her own benevolence. Yet she ended by resenting Pierrette’s success. The child’s constant invitations out and triumphs over “those ladies” enraged Sylvie, who allowed visits only to parade her own generosity. Pierrette welcomed the affectionate manners she found elsewhere; a mother would have delighted in her happiness, but the Rogrons had taken Pierrette for themselves, not for her sake; their feelings were dyed in selfishness and commercial calculation.

The handsome outfit and frequent outings began Pierrette’s troubles. Unused to restraint, she was hard on clothes and shoes. A mother reproves gently and for the child’s sake; the Rogrons considered only their money. Children instinctively sense whether they are loved or merely tolerated; Pierrette, pure and innocent, was distressed by subtle shades of difference. The lectures about propriety and economy were merely the corollary of the recurrent theme: “Pierrette will ruin us.”

These persistent fault-findings restored the Rogrons to their old shop-keeping habits from which their move to Provins had briefly freed them. Accustomed to ruling, making inquisitions, and reprimanding clerks, they had lacked victims. Sylvie and Rogron, with narrow natures, expanded by persecuting to relieve themselves; self-interest explained much of their conduct.

From her arrival Pierrette became a necessity to the Rogrons’ lives: first for her outfit, then because a third presence was novel. Sylvie began by calling her “my dear” or “little one,” but soon reverted to the curt “Pierrette,” and her reprimands escalated from cross to sharp and angry. The pair believed they were doing the child a service, as they had once thought harshness beneficial to apprentices.

Pierrette’s sensitivity, the opposite of their hardness, made her dread scolding; tears instantly filled her eyes. She stifled the sprightliness that had made her a favorite elsewhere, and displayed it only in the homes of friends. By the end of the first month she had learned to be passive in her cousins’ house—so passive that Rogron asked if she were ill. She would then run to the garden and cry by the river.

One day, despite care, she tore her best reps frock at Madame Tiphaine’s, where she had been happy. The child burst into tears, imagining the cruel things her cousins would say. Madame Tiphaine, to comfort her, had some reps like it and added a new breadth. Mademoiselle Rogron discovered this kindness and, calling it a trick the “little devil” had played her, refused thereafter to let Pierrette go to any of “those women’s” houses.

Pierrette’s life in Provins had three phases. The first, already described, mixed some joy with the cousins’ cold kindness and sharp reproaches and lasted three months. Sylvie’s refusal to let her go to Madame Tiphaine’s, followed by the start of her education, ended the first, bearable phase.

These events were observed by Vinet and Colonel Gouraud with the caution of foxes approaching a poultry-yard. They called occasionally—seldom, so as not to alarm Sylvie—talked with Rogron under pretexts, and insinuated themselves into his mind with feigned modesty. The colonel and the lawyer were present the night Sylvie angrily refused to let Pierrette visit Madame Tiphaine. Told of the refusal, they exchanged looks as if they knew Provins well.

“Madame Tiphaine intended to insult you,” said the lawyer. “We have long warned Rogron of what would happen. There’s no good to be got from those people.”

“What can you expect from the anti-national party!” cried the colonel, twirling his moustache. “If we had warned you earlier you might have supposed we had malicious motives. If you like a game of cards in the evening, why not have it at home? Why not play boston here, in your own house? Vinet and I know how to play; we can find a fourth. Vinet might present his wife to you; she is charming, and, what is more, a Chargeboeuf. You won’t be so exacting as those apes of the Upper town; you won’t require a proper little housewife to dress like a duchess. Poor woman—she has the courage of a lion and the meekness of a lamb.”

Sylvie showed her teeth as she smiled upon the colonel. “If we are only four we can’t play boston every night,” she said.

“Why not? An old soldier of the Empire like me needs occupation,” the colonel replied. “And Vinet is free. Besides, you’ll have plenty of other visitors; I warrant you that.”

“What you ought to do,” said Vinet, “is take an open stand against the ministerialists in Provins and form an opposition salon. You would soon be popular; the Tiphaines would be furious. Why not laugh at others, if others laugh at you?—and they do; the clique doesn’t mince matters.”

Provincial gossip leaks among social sets. Vinet knew all the slurs the Rogrons endured in salons from which they were excluded. The deputy-judge Desfondrilles, independent, recounted what he heard on both sides; Vinet amplified Madame Tiphaine’s barbed words. By showing Rogron and Sylvie the ridicule they had incurred, he roused a lasting hatred in their bitter natures, which needed an object for petty passions.

A few days later Vinet brought his wife, a modest, well-bred woman, neither pretty nor plain, timid, gentle, deeply conscious of her awkward position. Madame Vinet was fair, faded by household cares and plainly dressed. No woman could have pleased Sylvie more. Madame Vinet endured Sylvie’s airs and bent like one accustomed to subjection. On her rounded brow and timid cheek and in her slow look were traces of deep reflection, of thoughts women accustomed to suffering bury in silence.

The influence of the colonel’s flattery, in contrast to his usual brusqueness, and Vinet’s astuteness, soon harmed the Breton child. Shut up in the house and no longer allowed to go out except with her old cousin, Pierrette, the pretty little squirrel, became the object of the constant cry: “Don’t touch that, child, let that alone!” She was perpetually lectured on carriage and behavior; if she stooped or rounded her shoulders, Sylvie would call to her to be as erect as herself—Sylvie rigid as a soldier presenting arms—and sometimes enforced it with slaps on the back.

Thus the free and joyous child of the Marais gradually learned to repress all liveliness and to behave like an automaton.

One evening early in Pierrette’s second stay at her cousins’ house, the child, unseen all evening by the three guests, came in to kiss them good-night. Sylvie turned her cheek coldly to avoid kissing her; the motion was so cruel that Pierrette burst into tears.

“Did you prick yourself, little girl?” said the atrocious Vinet.

“What is the matter?” asked Sylvie, severely.

“Nothing,” said the child, going up to Rogron.

“Nothing? That’s nonsense; nobody cries for nothing,” Sylvie snapped.

“What is it, my little darling?” Madame Vinet asked.

“My rich cousin isn’t as kind to me as my poor grandmother was,” sobbed Pierrette.

“Your grandmother took your money,” Sylvie retorted, “and your cousin will leave you hers.”

The colonel and the lawyer exchanged glances.

“I would rather be robbed and loved,” said Pierrette.

“Then you shall be sent back whence you came.”

“What has the dear little thing done?” Madame Vinet asked.

Vinet gave his wife the stern look with which men enforce dominion. The hapless child, repeatedly punished for lacking that which was wanted of her—a fortune—took up her cards. Sylvie, throwing up her head so violently that the yellow wall-flowers in her cap nodded, declared, “She is always looking about to annoy us. She opened my watch to see the inside, meddled with the wheel and broke the mainspring. Mademoiselle pays no heed to what is said to her. I am all day long telling her to take care of things, and I might just as well talk to that lamp.”

Ashamed before strangers, Pierrette crept from the room.

“I am thinking all the time how to subdue that child,” Rogron said.

“Isn’t she old enough to go to school?” Madame Vinet suggested, but was silenced by a look from her husband, who had concealed his own and the colonel’s schemes.

“This is what comes of taking charge of other people’s children!” cried the colonel. “You may yet have some of your own. Why don’t you both marry?”

Sylvie smiled at the colonel; for the first time in her life she met a man for whom the idea she might marry did not seem absurd.

“Madame Vinet is right,” Rogron said; “perhaps teaching would keep Pierrette quiet. A master wouldn’t cost much.”

“If you will be security for that opposition journal I mentioned,” Vinet said, “you will find an excellent master in the managing editor; we intend to engage that poor schoolmaster deprived by the clergy’s encroachments. My wife is right; Pierrette is a rough diamond needing polishing.”

While Vinet explained how the paper would benefit them all, Pierrette dissolved in tears. She blamed her cousin and believed charity should be whole. She hated her handsome frocks and their cost, and resolved to behave so no fault could be found. She recalled Brigaut’s gift of his savings, thought her troubles could not worsen, unaware further misfortunes were being planned in the salon.

Soon Pierrette had a writing-master to teach reading, writing, and ciphering. This was said to bring enormous injury to the Rogrons’ house: ink-spots on tables, furniture, and her clothes; copy-books and pens abandoned; sand scattered; torn books. She was told to earn her living and not be a burden; such remarks choked her. She restrained tears or was scolded for weeping as an insult to her cousins’ kindness. Rogron found his mode—he scolded her as he scolded clerks, summoned her from play to study, made her repeat lessons, becoming almost savage. Sylvie taught her the little she knew of women’s work. Neither had softness. Their narrow minds, finding pleasure in worrying the child, passed from outward kindness into severity, justified by what they called Pierrette’s self-will. They could not adapt instruction to the pupil’s intelligence—a difference between public and private education. Pierrette took an age to learn the rudiments and was called stupid, clumsy, awkward for trifles. Abused incessantly in words and by harsh looks, she acquired the submissive habits of a sheep, daring nothing of her own impulse, awaiting her cousins’ orders and sheltering her mind in passive obedience. Her brilliant colors faded. At times she complained of feeling ill; when asked “Where?” she answered, “Everywhere.”

“Nonsense! who ever heard of suffering everywhere?” Sylvie scoffed. Rogron lectured that “everywhere” meant nowhere—“you’re complaining for complaining’s sake.”

Pierrette fell silent, for her girlish remarks were met with ignorant commonplaces she knew were ridiculous.

“You complain,” Rogron said, “but you’ve got the appetite of a monk.”

Only Adele, the fat servant, did not bruise the little flower. She warmed Pierrette’s bed on the sly after Sylvie scolded her. Sylvie’s belief was firm: children should be hardened—they had been. “You’ll make Pierrette a peakling,” she said, using their word for a puny, suffering being.

The child’s natural affection was treated as dissimulation. Her fresh devotion was pitilessly crushed. If she tried to soften their ferocity, they accused her of ulterior motives. “Tell me at once what you want?” Rogron would say, “you are not coaxing me for nothing.” Neither brother nor sister believed in affection; Pierrette’s whole being was affection. Colonel Gouraud, anxious to please Sylvie, approved all she did. Vinet encouraged them, attributing her so-called misdeeds to Breton obstinacy as incurable. Flattered, Rogron agreed to go security for the “Courrier de Provins,” and Sylvie invested five thousand francs.

The colonel and lawyer then took the field. They got a hundred shares of five hundred francs among farmers and the “independents” and buyers of national-domain lands—whose fears they exploited. Each shareholder subscribed to the paper; the judicial advertisements were split between the “Bee-hive” and the “Courrier.” The paper’s first issue contained a pompous eulogy of Rogron, presenting him as Provins’ Laffitte. The public, nudged thus, expected contested elections. Madame Tiphaine, dreaming of taking her husband to Paris as deputy, despaired when an article in the new paper attacked her and Julliard junior: “There is always a scoundrel close to a dupe,” she said.

As the “Courrier” spread, Vinet bought new clothes and adopted the gray slouch hat of liberals; his wife took a servant and appeared as the wife of a prominent man. Vinet proved grateful out of policy. He and his ally Cournant, a liberal notary opposed to the ministerial Auffray, became close advisers to the Rogrons, performing services: leases made by old Rogron in 1815 were expiring, and horticulture and gardening had developed near Provins. The lawyer and notary worked to increase the Rogrons’ rentals. Vinet won two suits involving planting trees and five hundred poplars; the proceeds, with the Rogrons’ savings—who for three years had set aside six thousand francs a year at high interest—were invested in improved lands. Vinet also carried out the ejectment of peasants who had defaulted on debts secured by the elder Rogron. The cost of the fine house was thus partly recouped. Their lands, chosen by their father’s innkeeper’s eye, were divided into small holdings, the largest not exceeding five acres, rented to safe tenants whose other holdings secured leases. By 1826 these investments brought in five thousand francs a year; taxes charged to tenants, no buildings needed insurance or repairs.

By the end of Pierrette’s second period in Provins, life had become so harsh—cold indifference, silly fault-finding, total absence of affection—that she felt a chill like a grave creeping round her. She thought of escaping on foot to Brittany to her grandparents. Two events prevented it: old Lorrain died, and Rogron was appointed her guardian. Had the grandmother died first, Rogron, advised by Vinet, would have claimed Pierrette’s eight thousand francs and reduced the old man to penury.

“You may, perhaps, inherit from Pierrette,” Vinet said with a horrid smile. “Who knows who may live and who may die?”

Illuminated by that remark, Rogron pressured Madame Lorrain until she secured for Pierrette the reversion of the eight thousand francs at her death.

Deeply shocked and on the point of making her first communion—a reason to abandon escape—Pierrette’s ceremony led to changes at the Rogrons’. Sylvie learned that Monsieur le curé Peroux taught the Julliards, Lesourds, Garcelands; so she insisted Pierrette be instructed by the vicar, Monsieur Habert, a priest thought to belong to the Congregation—zealous, feared, austere, ambitious. His sister, an unmarried woman about thirty, kept a school for young ladies; both siblings were thin, yellow, black-haired.

Like a true Breton girl, rooted in Catholic practice and poetry, Pierrette opened to the priest’s words. Suffering predisposes to devotion; many young girls are inclined to mysticism, and the priest found good soil. He changed the current of her thoughts: Pierrette loved Jesus as taught at first communion—as a celestial bridegroom—giving meaning to her sufferings; she saw God’s finger in events. Her soul, hurt though she could not accuse her cousins of outright wrong, took refuge in Faith, Hope, Charity. She abandoned thoughts of escape. Surprised by the priest’s effect on Pierrette, Sylvie too became pious under his influence. Jerome Rogron, however, who could not be gripped by this so-called Jesuit (then the late Constitutionnel was a stronger influence), remained faithful to Colonel Gouraud, Vinet, and Liberalism.

Mademoiselle Rogron befriended Mademoiselle Habert; the two spinsters loved each other as sisters. Mademoiselle Habert offered to take Pierrette to her school to spare Sylvie the annoyance of education, but the Rogrons declared the house would be too lonely without Pierrette; their attachment seemed excessive.

When Gouraud and Vinet learned of Mademoiselle Habert’s appearance, they feared the priest had matrimonial designs for his sister like the colonel’s plans for Sylvie. “Your sister wants to get you married,” Vinet told Rogron. “With whom?” Rogron asked. “With that old sorceress of a schoolmistress,” the colonel sneered. Rogron naively replied that she hadn’t spoken of it. Sylvie was certain an old maid like Mademoiselle Habert would make good progress toward salvation, increasing the priest’s influence, potentially converting Rogron and sending Pierrette to a convent. The two liberals, alarmed, saw in the priest and his sister adversaries as strong as themselves. Monsieur and Mademoiselle Habert visited the Rogrons nightly and could play whist and boston; their assiduity matched that of the colonel and Vinet. Each side suspected the other, and the salon turned into a battlefield, each wanting the whole prey.

The liberals made the first move. Vinet, pursuing self-interest, contacted Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf and her mother, living in poverty in Troyes on two thousand francs a year. Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, who believed in love-based marriage until twenty-five, was persuaded by Vinet’s promise that, joining means with his, she could live in Provins and marry the fool Rogron, taking her place in the best society.

Their arrival was a great reinforcement for the liberal party and a shock to Provins’ aristocrats and the Tiphaine clique. Madame de Breautey, horrified that two women of rank should be misled, begged them to come to her and railed against the selfishness degrading France—the consequence, she said, of materialism and the laws’ emphasis on money: nobility and beauty no longer mattered. Such creatures as the Rogrons, Vinets, could now contend with the King of France.

Bathilde de Chargeboeuf had not only beauty but dress; she was dazzlingly fair. At twenty-five her figure was developed; her throat, hair, smile, carriage, features, eyes, and motions were noble and high-bred. Her hands and feet were beautiful; health gave her perhaps too much the look of a handsome barmaid—“but that can’t be a defect in the eyes of a Rogron,” Madame Tiphaine sighed. Bathilde’s dress on first appearing at the Rogrons was simple: a brown merino gown edged with green embroidery, low-necked, with a tulle fichu carefully drawn down though opening a little in front, caught with a sevigne. She removed her velvet bonnet and shawl to reveal gold ear-drops and wore a black velvet ribbon with a heart—tricks of a girl seeking marriage. She entreated Rogron to fasten a cuff-button to show him her wrist; the dazzled fool refused, masking his emotions with indifference. Sylvie and Celeste Habert were deceived by his manner; Vinet was not.

The colonel, courting Sylvie as Bathilde courted Rogron, put on a clean shirt each evening, velvet stocks, a new blue surtout, a red rosette, even refrained from smoking. He adopted the air of a party leader preparing to drive out the Bourbons.

Vinet and the colonel played a crueler trick: they insinuated the priest sympathized with their ideas; soon Provins called him a liberal ecclesiastic. When the bishop heard, Monsieur Habert was summoned and admonished to stop visiting the Rogrons; his sister persisted. Thus the Rogron salon became established and powerful.

Before the year ended political intrigues matched the matrimonial plots. The Villele ministry fell in 1826; Vinet, the Liberal candidate who had borrowed money to buy a domain to qualify, nearly defeated Monsieur Tiphaine, who saved the election by two votes. The Rogrons’ salon was Liberal headquarters; habitués included notary Cournant and Doctor Neraud, whose stormy youth gave way to study and, Liberals claimed, more capability than the aristocratic Martener. The Rogrons themselves hardly understood their present triumph.

Bathilde, to whom Vinet had portrayed Pierrette as an enemy, was disdainful of the girl. It seemed everyone’s schemes demanded humiliation of that poor victim. Madame Vinet could do little for Pierrette, crushed as she was by her husband’s will. Pierrette often pressed against Madame Vinet as if divining her thoughts, asking to be shown a stitch or taught embroidery. Treated gently, she could learn and succeed marvellously. But after the arrival of Madame and Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, Madame Vinet ceased to visit the Rogrons.

Sylvie, now thinking of marrying, considered Pierrette an obstacle. Nearly fourteen, the pallid whiteness of her skin—symptomatic of illness overlooked by the ignorant old maid—made her exquisitely lovely. Sylvie thought to balance Pierrette’s cost by making her a servant. The habitués advised sending away Adele: why not have Pierrette keep the house and cook? If needed, Sylvie could employ the colonel’s woman-of-all-work. The lawyer counseled every girl should learn to keep house and know prices. Pierrette, generous and self-devoted, offered willingly, pleased to earn the bitter bread she ate. Adele was sent away, and Pierrette lost the only protector she had.

Crushed physically and mentally, she was treated with less consideration than a servant—she belonged to them. She was scolded for the smallest dust on a glass globe or mantelpiece. The ornaments she once admired became odious. No matter how she strove to do right, her cousins always found cause to reproach her. In two years she received no praise or kindly word; happiness was simply not being scolded. She bore with angelic patience the morose ill-humor of two celibates to whom tenderness was unknown and who daily made her feel her dependence.

Such life, pressed between two vise-like jaws, worsened her health. She began to feel violent internal distresses, secret pangs undermining her strength and arresting development. Slowly, through dreadful hidden sufferings, the poor child came to the state in which the companion of her childhood had found her when he sang the Breton ditty at dawn on that October day.

VI. AN OLD MAID’S JEALOSTY

Before relating the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut’s coming provoked in the Rogron family, it is best to explain how the lad came to be in Provins; he is a somewhat mute personage on the scene.

When he ran from the house Brigaut was not only frightened by Pierrette’s gesture, he was horrified by the change he saw in his little friend. He could scarcely recognize the voice, the eyes, the gestures that had once been lively, gay, and tender. When he had gained some distance his legs trembled; hot flushes ran down his back. He had seen Pierrette’s shadow, but not herself. Climbing to the Upper town, he found a spot from which he could see the square and the house where Pierrette lived. He gazed at it mournfully, lost in thoughts that rent his heart, revealing to his own soul the extent of his love for his little adopted sister.

Passion between two children of opposite sexes is rare. The charming story of Paul and Virginia, like that of Pierrette and Brigaut, does not answer that strange moral fact. Modern history shows only a few such examples. Such was the sudden perfect affection in the Breton workman. Pierrette and he had often protected each other; he had blissfully given her money for her journey, had almost killed himself running after the diligence when she left. Pierrette had known nothing of this; for him, the recollection warmed his hard life. For Pierrette’s sake he had struggled to improve himself; he had learned his trade for her; he had come to Paris to make his fortune for her. After a fortnight in the city he could not resist seeing her, and he had walked from Saturday night to Monday morning. He intended to return to Paris, but the sight of his little friend nailed him to Provins. A wonderful, unconscious magnetism acted upon him. Tears rolled from his eyes when they rose in hers. If to her he was Brittany and childhood, to him she was life itself.

At sixteen Brigaut had not yet learned to draw or model a cornice; ignorant of much, he had nevertheless earned, by piece-work during his apprenticeship, some four or five francs a day. On this he could live in Provins and be near Pierrette; he chose the best cabinet-maker in town and meant to learn the rest by working for him, thus keeping watch over his darling.

Brigaut’s mind was made up. He went back to Paris, fetched his certificate, tools, and baggage, and three days later was a journeyman in the establishment of Monsieur Frappier, the best cabinet-maker in Provins. Active, steady workmen, not given to junketing, are rare, and masters hold to young men like Brigaut. By the end of the month he was made foreman and was fed and lodged by Frappier, who taught him arithmetic and line drawing. The house and shop were in the Grand’Rue, not a hundred feet from the little square where Pierrette lived.

Brigaut buried his love and committed no imprudence. He made Madame Frappier tell him all she knew about the Rogrons, including how their father had laid hands on the property of old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandfather. He obtained other information about the brother and sister. He met Pierrette sometimes in the market with her cousin and shuddered to see the heavy basket she carried. On Sundays he went to church to look for her, dressed in his best clothes. There, for the first time, he became aware that Pierrette was Mademoiselle Lorrain. Pierrette saw him and made a hasty sign to keep out of sight. To him there was a world in that little gesture, as there had been earlier in the sign from her window telling him to run away. Ah! what a fortune he must make in the coming ten years to marry his little friend, to whom, he was told, the Rogrons were to leave their house, a hundred acres of land, and twelve thousand francs a year, not counting their savings!

The persevering Breton set about thoroughly educating himself in his trade; for principles of the craft he could learn in Provins as well as Paris and thus remain near Pierrette, to whom he became anxious to explain his projects and the protection he could offer. He was determined to know the reason for her pallor and the debility beginning to show in the eyes; he would also discover the cause of the sufferings that made her look as if death might take her at any moment. The two signs—the gestures not denying friendship but imploring caution—alarmed him. Evidently Pierrette wished him to wait; otherwise there was danger. As she left the church she gave him one look, and he saw her eyes full of tears. But he could as soon have squared the circle as have guessed what had happened in the Rogrons’ house during the fortnight since his arrival.

It was not without keen apprehension that Pierrette came downstairs the morning after Brigaut had invaded her dreams. She thought Sylvie must have heard the song, or she would not have risen and opened her window; but Pierrette was ignorant of the powerful reasons that made the old maid so alert. For the last eight days strange events and bitter feelings had agitated the minds of the Rogron salon’s chief personages. These hidden matters, carefully concealed by all concerned, were destined to fall like an avalanche on Pierrette. Such secret schemes and calculations lie at the base of great revolutions, whether political, social, or domestic; their subtle significance cannot be rendered by a simple narrative. To set down all circumlocutions, oratorical precautions, protracted conversations, and honeyed words that gloss venom would make as long a book as Clarissa Harlowe.

Mademoiselle Habert and Mademoiselle Sylvie were equally desirous of marrying, but Celeste Habert was ten years older and could hope her children would inherit all the Rogron property. Sylvie was forty-two, an age at which marriage is perilous. In confiding to each other their ideas, Celeste, instigated by her vindictive brother the priest, enlightened Sylvie as to the risks. Sylvie trembled; she was terribly afraid of death, an idea that shakes all celibates. But at that time the Martignac ministry came to power—a Liberal victory overturning Villele. The Vinet party carried their heads high in Provins. Vinet himself became a personage, the Liberals prophesying his advancement to deputy and attorney-general. The colonel would be made mayor. Ah, to reign as Madame Garceland reigned! Sylvie could not resist that hope; she determined to consult a doctor, though it would cover her with ridicule. Consulting Monsieur Neraud, the Liberal physician and rival of Martener, would be a blunder. Celeste offered to hide Sylvie in her dressing-room while she herself consulted Monsieur Martener on this delicate matter. Whether Martener was Celeste’s accomplice need not be discovered; at any rate he told Celeste that even at thirty the danger, though slight, did exist. “But,” he added, “with your constitution, you need fear nothing.”

“But how about a woman over forty?” asked Celeste.

“A married woman who has had children has nothing to fear.”

“But I mean an unmarried woman, like Mademoiselle Rogron, for instance?”

“Oh, that’s another thing,” said Martener. “Successful childbirth is then one of those miracles God sometimes allows, but rarely.”

“Why?” asked Celeste.

The doctor gave a terrifying pathological account: the elasticity of youthful muscles and bones does not exist later, especially in sedentary women. “So you think an unmarried woman ought not to marry after forty?”

“Not unless she waits some years,” replied the doctor. “But then, of course, it is not marriage; it is only an association of interests.”

The interview’s clinical result was clear: an unmarried woman would make a great mistake marrying after forty. When the doctor departed Celeste found Sylvie in a frightful state, green and yellow, pupils dilated.

“Then you really love the colonel?” asked Celeste.

“I still hoped,” replied Sylvie.

“Well, then, wait!” cried Mademoiselle Habert, Jesuitically, aware that time would rid her of the colonel.

Sylvie’s new devotion to the church warned her that the morality of such a marriage might be doubtful. She sounded her conscience in the confessional. The stern priest explained Church opinions, which see marriage primarily as propagation and rebuke second marriages and all passion without social purpose. Sylvie’s perplexities were great. These internal struggles gave extraordinary force to her passion, investing it with that attraction which, from Eve’s day, the forbidden has for women. Mademoiselle Rogron’s perturbation did not escape the lynx-eyed lawyer.

One evening, after the game had ended, Vinet approached Sylvie, took her hand, and led her to a sofa.

“Something troubles you,” he said.

She nodded sadly. He let the others depart; Rogron walked home with the Chargeboeufs, and when Vinet was alone with the old maid he wormed the truth from her.

“Cleverly played, abbé!” he thought. “But you’ve played into my hands.”

The foxy lawyer, more decided even than the doctor, advised marriage in ten years. Inwardly he vowed the whole Rogron fortune should go to Bathilde. He rubbed his hands and hurried home. The influence exercised by Monsieur Habert, physician of the soul, and by Vinet, doctor of the purse, balanced each other. Rogron had no piety; the black-robed pair were fairly matched.

On discovering Celeste’s victory in her anxiety to marry Rogron over Sylvie, the lawyer saw his chance to drive the colonel from the field. He knew Rogron well enough to be certain he could marry him to Bathilde; Jerome had already inwardly succumbed to her charms, and Vinet knew the first time the pair were alone the marriage would be settled. Rogron had reached the point of keeping his eyes fixed on Celeste to avoid looking at Bathilde. Vinet had now learned Sylvie’s secrets and saw the force of her love for the colonel. He fully understood the struggle of such passion in an old maid gripped by religion and saw a way to rid himself of Pierrette and the colonel alike by making each the cause of the other’s overthrow.

The next day, after court, Vinet met the colonel and Rogron walking their daily walk. Whenever the three men were seen in company the town talked. This triumvirate, held in horror by the sub-prefect, the magistracy, and the Tiphaine clique, was a pride to the Liberals. Vinet, sole editor of the “Courrier,” was head of the party; the colonel, its arm; Rogron, by his purse, its nerves. The Tiphaines declared the three men always plotted evil to the government; the Liberals admired them as defenders of the people. When Rogron turned to go home, recalled by dinner-hour, Vinet stopped the colonel by taking Gouraud’s arm.

“Well, colonel,” he said, “I am going to take a fearful load off your shoulders; you can do better than marry Sylvie; if you play your cards properly you can marry that little Pierrette in two years’ time.”

He then related the Jesuit’s manoeuvre and its effect on Sylvie.

“What a skulking trick!” cried the colonel; “and spreading over years, too!”

“Colonel,” said Vinet gravely, “Pierrette is a charming creature; with her you can be happy for the rest of your life; your health is so sound that the difference in ages won't seem disproportionate. But you mustn’t think it easy to change a dreadful fate to a pleasant one. To turn a woman who loves you into a friend and confidant is as perilous as crossing a river under fire. You must study the position and manoeuvre your forces with the same wisdom you have displayed and which has won us our present position. If I get to be attorney-general you shall command the department. Oh! if you had been an elector we should be further advanced; I would have bought votes by threatening men with the loss of their places, and we should have had a majority.”

The colonel had long thought about Pierrette but concealed his feelings with utmost dissimulation. His roughness to the child was only a mask. She could not understand why the man who claimed to be her father’s old comrade usually treated her so ill when sometimes, if alone with her, he would chuck her under the chin and give a friendly kiss. After Vinet’s conversation about Sylvie’s fears, Gouraud began to seek opportunities to find Pierrette alone; the rough colonel made himself as soft as a cat. He told her how brave her father was and what a misfortune it had been that she lost him.

A few days before Brigaut’s arrival Sylvie had suddenly come upon Gouraud and Pierrette talking together. Jealousy rushed into her heart with monastic violence. Jealousy, credulous and suspicious, gives fancy freedom but not intelligence; it filled her with fantastic ideas. When she heard Brigaut’s ditty a few mornings later she jumped to the conclusion that the man who had used the words “Madam’ le mariee,” addressing them to Pierrette, must be the colonel. She was certain, for she had noticed a change in his manners for a week. He was the only man who, in her solitary life, had ever paid her attention. Consequently she watched him with all her eyes and mind; by hope and despair she brought the matter to enormous proportions and saw all things in a mental mirage. By dint of staring she saw nothing. Alternately she repelled, admitted, and conquered the idea of rivalry. She compared herself with Pierrette: forty-two with gray hair, versus Pierrette delicately fair with soft eyes. She had heard men of fifty apt to love young girls of that kind. Before the colonel came to the house Sylvie had heard in the Tiphaines’ salon strange stories of his life and morals. Old maids preserve exaggerated Platonic sentiments; they hold to fixed doctrines like those with little experience of life. The mere thought of being jilted by the colonel was torture to Sylvie. She lay in bed going over her desires, Pierrette’s conduct, and the song that had awakened her with the word “marriage.” Foolishly, instead of looking through the blinds to see the lover, she opened her window without reflecting that Pierrette would hear her. If she had had the instinct of a spy she would have seen Brigaut, and the fatal drama begun would never have taken place.

It was Pierrette’s duty, weak as she was, to unbar and open the wooden shutters of the kitchen, then the glass door from corridor to garden. She fetched various brooms and utensils with a care and particularity no servant, not even a Dutchwoman, gives her work. She hated reproof. Happiness for her was seeing her cousin’s cold blue eyes—not satisfied but calm—after glancing about with the look of an owner. Pierrette’s skin was moist with labor when she returned to put the kitchen in order and light the stove so she might carry up hot water to her two cousins (a luxury she never had), and to prepare the dining-room fire. For these she fetched wood from the cellar, leaving warm rooms for damp air; such sudden transitions, made quickly to escape harsh words, aggravated her condition. She did not know she was ill, yet she suffered. She began to have strange cravings; she liked raw vegetables and salads and ate them secretly. The innocent child was unaware her condition was serious and required utmost care. If Neraud, the Rogrons’ doctor, had told this to Pierrette before Brigaut’s coming she would have only smiled; life was so bitter she could smile at death. But now her feelings changed; the child, whose physical suffering was increased by Breton homesickness, was suddenly content to be in Provins. The sight of that yellow flower, the song, the presence of her friend revived her like rain on a parched plant. Unconsciously she wanted to live and even thought she did not suffer.

Pierrette slipped timidly into her cousin’s bedroom, made the fire, left the hot water, said a few words, and went to wake Rogron and do the same for him. Then she went down to take in the milk, bread, and other provisions. She stood some time on the door-sill hoping Brigaut would come; by then he was already on his way to Paris.

She had finished arranging the dining-room and was busy in the kitchen when she heard Sylvie coming down. Mademoiselle Rogron appeared in a brown silk dressing-gown with a cap, false front awry, night-gown showing above the silk, slippers down at heel. She cast an eye about and came straight to Pierrette, who awaited orders.

“Ha! here you are, lovesick young lady!” said Sylvie mockingly.

“What is it, cousin?”

“You came into my room like a sly cat and crept out the same way, though you knew I had something to say to you.”

“To me?”

“You had a serenade this morning, as if you were a princess.”

“A serenade!” exclaimed Pierrette.

“A serenade!” mimicked Sylvie. “And you’ve a lover, too.”

“What is a lover, cousin?”

Sylvie avoided answering. “Do you dare tell me a man did not come under your window and talk to you of marriage?”

Persecution had taught Pierrette the wariness of slaves; she answered bravely: “I don’t know what you mean—”

“Who means?—your dog?” said Sylvie sharply.

“I should have said ‘cousin,’” replied the girl humbly.

“And didn’t you get up and go in your bare feet to the window?—which will give you an illness; and perhaps you didn’t talk to your lover, either?”

“No, cousin.”

“You have many faults, but I did not think you told lies. You will have to explain this affair to your cousin and to me, or I shall take severe measures.”

The old maid, exasperated with jealousy and curiosity, meant to frighten the girl. Pierrette, who endured more than she had strength for, kept silence. Silence is the weapon by which such victims sometimes conquer; it baffles envy’s charges and suspicion’s skirmishings and is absolute. Sylvie watched Pierrette narrowly. The girl colored, but the color came out in patches on her cheekbones—burning and significant spots. A mother would have changed tone at once, taken the child on her lap, and questioned her; she would have seen the signs of weakness and known that digestive disturbance and other infirmities threatened the lungs. But an old maid who never awakened family instincts or knew the precautions required for adolescence had neither indulgence nor the compassionate intelligence of a mother; Pierrette’s sufferings only hardened her.

“She blushes, she is guilty!” thought Sylvie.

Pierrette’s silence thus was turned to her injury.

“Pierrette,” Sylvie continued, “before your cousin comes down we must have some talk together. Come,” she said softer, “shut the street door; if any one comes they will ring and we shall hear them.”

In spite of the damp mist rising from the river, Sylvie took Pierrette along the winding gravel path across the lawn to the rock terrace—an ornamental quay covered with iris and aquatic plants. She changed tactics, thinking firmness might catch Pierrette tripping; the hyena became a cat.

“Pierrette,” she said, “you are no longer a child; you are nearly fifteen; it is not surprising you should have a lover.”

“But, cousin,” said Pierrette, raising her eyes with angelic sweetness to Sylvie’s cold face, “what is a lover?”

It would have been impossible for Sylvie to define a lover truthfully and decently. Instead of seeing adorable innocence, she considered the question insincere.

“A lover, Pierrette, is a man who loves us and wishes to marry us.”

“Ah,” said Pierrette, “when that happens in Brittany we call the young man a suitor.”

“Well, remember that in owning your feelings for a man you do no wrong, my dear. The wrong is in hiding them. Have you pleased some of the men who visit here?”

“No.”

“Do you love any of them?”

“No.”

“Certain?”

“Quite certain.”

“Look at me, Pierrette.”

Pierrette looked.

“A man called to you this morning in the square.”

Pierrette lowered her eyes.

“You went to your window, you opened it, and you spoke to him.”

“No cousin, I went to look out and I saw a peasant.”

“Pierrette, you have much improved since your first communion; you have become pious and obedient, you love God and your relations; I am satisfied with you. I don’t say this to puff you up with pride.”

The horrible creature had mistaken submission and the silence of wretchedness for virtues.

Pierrette raised grateful eyes to her cousin, feeling she could almost forgive the suffering she had caused.

“But if it is all hypocrisy, if I find you a serpent that I have warmed in my bosom, you will be a wicked girl, an infamous creature!”

“I think I have nothing to reproach myself with,” said Pierrette, painfully shocked by the sudden change from praise to hyena tones.

“You know that to lie is a mortal sin?”

“Yes, cousin.”

“Well, you are now under the eye of God,” said the old maid, with a solemn gesture to the sky; “swear to me that you did not know that peasant.”

“I will not swear,” said Pierrette.

“Ha! he was no peasant, you little viper.”

Pierrette rushed away like a frightened fawn. Sylvie called her in a dreadful voice.

“The bell is ringing,” she answered.

“Artful wretch!” thought Sylvie. “She is depraved in mind; now I am certain the little adder has wound herself round the colonel. She has heard us say he was a baron. To be a baroness! little fool! Ah! I’ll get rid of her; I’ll apprentice her out, and soon too!”

Sylvie was so lost in thought she did not notice her brother coming down the path lamenting the frost’s injury to his dahlias.

“Sylvie! what are you thinking about? I thought you were looking at the fish; sometimes they jump out of the water.”

“No,” said Sylvie.

How did you sleep?” he began to tell his dreams. “Don’t you think my skin is getting tabid?”—a Rogron word.

Ever since Rogron had desired to marry Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf he had been uneasy about himself and his health. At this moment Pierrette came down and called that breakfast was ready. At sight of her, Sylvie’s skin turned green and yellow; her bile was in commotion. She looked at the corridor floor and declared Pierrette ought to rub it.

“I will rub it now if you wish,” said the little angel, unaware of the injury such work may do a girl.

The dining-room was irreproachably in order. Sylvie pretended through breakfast to want this and that—which she would never have thought to ask in a quieter mood—only to make Pierrette rise again and again just as she began to eat. But mere teasing was not enough; she wanted fault and was angry with herself for not finding one. She scarcely answered her brother’s remarks yet kept her eyes on him and avoided Pierrette. Pierrette, deeply conscious, brought milk mixed with cream in a large silver goblet after heating it in a bain-marie. The cousins poured coffee. When Sylvie carefully prepared hers she saw an atom of coffee-grounds floating on the surface. On this the storm broke.

“What is the matter?” asked Rogron.

“The matter is that mademoiselle has put dust in my milk. Do you suppose I am going to drink coffee with ashes in it? Well, I am not surprised; no one can do two things at once. She wasn’t thinking of the milk! a blackbird might have flown through the kitchen today and she wouldn’t have seen it! and then it was my coffee, ha! that didn’t signify!”

As she spoke she laid aside the coffee-grounds that had run through the filter.

“But, cousin, that is coffee,” said Pierrette.

“Oh! then it is I who tell lies, is it?” cried Sylvie, blasting her with a fearful flash from her eyes.

Organizations not exhausted by emotion often have much vital fluid at their service. This extreme clearness of eye in anger was marked in Mademoiselle Rogron because she often exercised it in her shop to inspire dependents with fear.

“You had better dare to give me the lie!” continued Sylvie; “you deserve to be sent from the table to eat by yourself in the kitchen.”

“What’s the matter with you two?” cried Rogron, “you are as cross as bears this morning.”

“Mademoiselle knows what I have against her,” said Sylvie. “I leave her to make up her mind before speaking to you; for I mean to show her more kindness than she deserves.”

Pierrette looked out the window to avoid her cousin’s eyes, which frightened her. “Look at her! she pays no attention to what I am saying than if I were that sugar-basin! And yet mademoiselle has a sharp ear; she can hear and answer from the top of the house when some one talks to her from below. She is perversity itself,—perversity, I say; and you needn’t expect any good of her; do you hear me, Jerome?”

“What has she done wrong?” asked Rogron.

“At her age, too! to begin so young!” screamed Sylvie.

Pierrette rose to clear the table to give herself something to do; she could hardly bear the scene. Though such language was not new, she had never grown used to it. Her cousin’s rage seemed to accuse her of a crime. She imagined what fury would be if they knew about Brigaut; perhaps they would send him away and she would lose him. All the rapid thoughts of a slave came to her, and she resolved to keep absolute silence about a circumstance her conscience told her was innocent. But the cruel words and wounding suspicion so shocked her that as she reached the kitchen she was taken with a convulsion of the stomach and turned deadly sick. She dared not complain; she was not sure anyone would help her. When she returned to the dining-room she was white as a sheet and, saying she was not well, went to bed, dragging herself up step by step by the baluster and thinking she was going to die. “Poor Brigaut!” she thought.

“The girl is ill,” said Rogron.

“She ill! That’s only shamming,” replied Sylvie loudly that Pierrette might hear. “She was well enough this morning, I can tell you.”

This last blow struck Pierrette to the earth; she went to bed weeping and praying to God to take her out of this world.

For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the “Constitutionnel” to Gouraud; the colonel nevertheless came dutifully to fetch his paper, gossip a little, and take Rogron out walking when the weather allowed. Sure of seeing the colonel and able to question him, Sylvie dressed as coquettishly as she could: a green gown, a yellow shawl with a red border, and a white bonnet with stray gray feathers. About the hour the colonel usually came she stationed herself in the salon with her brother, whom she kept at home in dressing-gown and slippers.

“It is a fine day, colonel,” said Rogron when Gouraud entered with his heavy step. “But I’m not dressed; my sister wanted to go out, and I was going to keep the house. Wait for me; I’ll be ready soon.”

Rogron left Sylvie alone with the colonel.

“You are dressed divinely,” observed Gouraud, noting a new solemnity on the old maid’s pock-marked face.

“I wanted to go out, but my little cousin is ill,” Sylvie answered. “She had to go to bed.”

Gouraud’s distrust of Vinet was constant. Vinet controlled the paper, reigned as master, and took the revenues; the colonel, the responsible editor, earned little. Vinet and Cournant had done the Rogrons great services; Gouraud, a half-pay colonel, could do nothing. Who, then, would be deputy or chief authority among the liberals? Vinet. The colonel knew, too, how passionately Rogron adored Bathilde de Chargeboeuf; Rogron trembled at her voice, hid his desires, and dared not hope. When Gouraud had hinted at asking for her hand, Rogron had turned pale and since then had behaved coldly toward the colonel.

Thus Vinet reigned supreme in the Rogron household while the colonel had no hold there except through a mendacious affection for Sylvie, whose feelings were uncertain. When the lawyer told him of the priest’s manoeuvre and advised breaking with Sylvie to marry Pierrette, he flattered Gouraud’s vanity; but after thinking it over the colonel suspected Vinet intended to separate him from Sylvie and, by exploiting her fears, throw the Rogron property into Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf’s hands.

Left alone with Sylvie, Gouraud’s perspicacity caught signs of her uneasiness. He saw she was on guard and had planned to see him alone. Suspecting Vinet of a trick, he went instantly on the defensive. The colonel never believed a woman’s word; when Sylvie said Pierrette had gone to bed before midday he concluded Sylvie had locked the girl up in jealousy.

“She is getting to be quite pretty, that little thing,” he said easily.

“She will be pretty,” Sylvie replied.

“You ought to send her to Paris and put her in a shop,” the colonel continued. “She would make her fortune. The milliners all want pretty girls.”

“Is that really your advice?” Sylvie asked, troubled.

Good! thought the colonel; Vinet had advised him to marry Pierrette to spoil his chance with Sylvie. He feigned agreement with Vinet’s plot and then, aloud, insisted age and experience mattered more to him. He spoke of his campaigns and losses, of being “broken down” and preferring a companion like Sylvie, whose money, joined to his pension, would give him ease. At forty-eight — he added a few years to match Sylvie’s — he said he calculated and did not want children.

Sylvie’s face read like an open book. Her next question confirmed Vinet’s perfidy.

“Then you don’t love Pierrette?” she asked.

“Heavens! are you out of your mind, my dear Sylvie?” he replied. “Can those who have no teeth crack nuts? Thank God I’ve got some common-sense and know what I’m about.”

Sylvie, reassured, resolved not to show her hand, thinking she was clever to put the idea into her brother’s head.

“Jérôme thought of the match,” she said.

“How could your brother take up such an incongruous idea? Why, it is only a few days ago that, to find his secrets, I told him I loved Bathilde. He turned as white as your collar.”

“My brother! does he love Bathilde?” asked Sylvie.

“Madly,—and yet Bathilde is only after his money.” One for you, Vinet! thought the colonel. “I can’t understand why he told you that about Pierrette. No, Sylvie,” he added, taking her hand and drawing near, “I desire no wife but you. Though such a marriage may look like one of convenience, I feel sincere affection for you.”

“But if I wish you to marry Pierrette? if I leave her my fortune—eh, colonel?”

“But I don’t want to be miserable in my home and in less than ten years see a popinjay hovering round my wife and addressing verses to her in the newspapers. I’m too much of a man to stand that. No, I will never make a marriage that is disproportionate in age.”

“We will talk seriously of this another time,” Sylvie said, casting a glance she imagined full of love though it was more like that of an ogress; her pale blue lips drew back from yellow teeth, and she thought she smiled.

Rogron returned, and the colonel bowed to the old maid like a lover.

Gouraud resolved to press his marriage to Sylvie and make himself master of the house; he planned during the honeymoon to rid himself, through his influence over Sylvie, of Bathilde and of Céleste Habert. During their walk he told Rogron he had been joking before: he had no real intention of marrying Bathilde, was not rich enough for an unfortified match, and had long chosen Sylvie for her good qualities — he aspired to be Rogron’s brother-in-law.

“Ah, colonel, my dear baron! if nothing is wanting but my consent you have it,” cried Rogron, delighted to be rid of a rival.

Sylvie spent the morning arranging the new household: build a second storey for her brother, furnish the rest for herself and husband; but true to old-maid suspicion, she resolved to test the colonel’s heart and morals and ensure Pierrette had no private intercourse with him.

Pierrette came down before dinner to lay the table. Sylvie, forced to cook, had sworn at that “cursed Pierrette” for a grease spot on her gown—wasn’t it plain that if Pierrette had done her work Sylvie wouldn’t have got that stain? The thought “You did not tell the truth this morning, therefore you lie about everything” was a hammer with which Sylvie battered the poor girl.

To Pierrette’s astonishment, Sylvie sent her to dress in her best clothes after dinner. Suspicion makes an old maid inventive; Sylvie wanted to consult Vinet and planned to keep Pierrette near to judge, by her face, whether the colonel had told the truth.

That evening the Chargeboeuf ladies arrived first. At Vinet’s advice Bathilde dressed elaborately in blue velveteen and jewels, with queenly manners and girlish coquetry aimed at Rogron. Her mother preserved an aristocratic insolence. Bathilde was intelligent; during a two-month stay at Vinet’s house he had discovered and enlarged her political ambitions. Wounded by the fruitlessness of her beauty and youth, contemptuous of a world that worshipped money, Bathilde did not marry for children or mere possession but to gain freedom and influence: to be called “madame,” to act as men act, to create a husband in office whom she could push into power. Rogron was nothing but a name to her; she expected to make something of him — a voting deputy she would instigate — and to avenge herself on a family who had neglected her. Vinet had strengthened these ideas.

“If I could only have married you, Bathilde, I should to-day be Keeper of the Seals,” he had said, sketching a plan in which she would marry Rogron, get him elected deputy from Provins as Vinet secured precincts, place him as receiver-general, and later, when Rogron died, perhaps marry a titled man. “Put yourself in a good position,” Vinet urged. “Your poverty has taught you what men are worth. We must use them as we do post-horses.”

Vinet left his wife at home and every night visited the Rogrons with the Chargeboeufs, presenting himself in the trappings of success: gold spectacles, silk waistcoat, white cravat and a confident air. Where he had been pale and snarling he was now suave and insinuating; the old hatred had become soft-spoken perfidy.

“Good-evening, my dear; how are you?” said Madame de Chargeboeuf to Sylvie.

Bathilde went straight to the fireplace, took off her bonnet, looked in the glass, and placed her pretty foot on the fender for Rogron to admire.

“What is the matter with you?” she said to him. “You have not bowed to me. Pray why should we put on our best velvet gowns to please you?”

She pushed past Pierrette to lay down her hat; Pierrette took it from her as if she were a servant. No beast equals the gentle cruelty and disdain of a young woman who thinks herself superior. Bathilde’s “Thank you, mademoiselle” to Pierrette was a poem in scorn. Bathilde was tall, lively, in velvet; Pierrette small, weak, wearing plain stuff; Bathilde fair and privileged, Pierrette on charity. Pierrette understood this painful contrast.

“Good-evening, little girl,” Madame de Chargeboeuf said, condescending.

Vinet completed the insult with a mocking, “Oh! oh! oh! how fine we are to-night, Pierrette!”

“You should say that to Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, not to me,” Pierrette answered.

“Oh! she is always beautifully dressed,” replied Vinet, turning to Rogron and seizing his hand.

“Yes,” said Rogron.

“Why do you force him to say what he does not think?” said Bathilde; “nothing about me pleases him. Isn’t that true?” she added, going up and standing before him.

Rogron looked at her and gently closed his eyes like a cat whose head is being scratched.

“You are too beautiful,” he said; “too dangerous.”

“Why?”

Rogron looked at the fire and was silent. Just then Mademoiselle Habert entered, followed by the colonel.

Céleste Habert, whom everybody now regarded as the common enemy, could only count on Sylvie’s hostility. Yet each person showed her civility because each undermined her. Her brother, though no longer on the scene, was aware of what was happening and, on seeing his sister’s hopes crushed, became a terrible antagonist to the Rogrons.

Mademoiselle Habert was textbook schoolmistress: a cap, prim airs, a stern eye, a grim mouth, strings under a wrinkled chin, and the ungraceful habit of taking snuff. Her presence heightened the salon’s formality.

They sat down to Boston. Mademoiselle Habert faced Sylvie, the colonel sat opposite Madame de Chargeboeuf; Bathilde was near her mother and Rogron. Sylvie placed Pierrette between herself and the colonel; Rogron had set out a second card-table in case more guests arrived. Two lamps and candles lit the room.

“Come, Pierrette, take your work,” Sylvie said in treacherous softness, noticing that the girl watched the colonel’s play.

Pierrette took up her embroidery but still watched Gouraud. He behaved as if unaware she was near. Sylvie noticed his seeming indifference and thought it suspicious. Presently she undertook a grande misère in hearts, the pool full of counters and twenty-seven sous.

More guests arrived, among them the deputy-judge Desfondrilles, who had lately drifted away from the Tiphaine party toward the Vinets. He stood before the chimney, back to the fire, looking at the salon made to enhance Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf’s style of beauty. Silence reigned; Pierrette watched the game, Sylvie’s attention hooked by the misère.

“Play that,” Pierrette said to the colonel, pointing to a heart in his hand.

The colonel began a sequence in hearts; the hearts lay between him and Sylvie; he won her ace, though protected by five small hearts.

“That’s not fair!” she cried. “Pierrette saw my hand, and the colonel took her advice.”

“But, mademoiselle,” said Céleste, “it was the colonel’s game to play hearts after you began them.”

Monsieur Desfondrilles smiled; his keen mind found amusement in the private interests at play.

“Yes, it was certainly the colonel’s game,” said Cournant the notary, uncertain of the issue.

Sylvie cast a feline, cruel look at Mademoiselle Habert.

“Pierrette, you did see my hand,” Sylvie accused, fixing her eyes on the girl.

“No, cousin.”

“I was looking at you all,” said Desfondrilles, “and I can swear Pierrette saw no one’s hand but the colonel’s.”

“Pooh!” said Gouraud, alarmed. “Little girls know how to slide their eyes into everything.”

“Ah!” exclaimed Sylvie.

“Yes,” Gouraud continued. “I dare say she looked into your hand to play you a trick. Didn’t you, little one?”

“No,” the truthful Breton said. “I wouldn’t do such a thing; if I had, it would have been in my cousin’s interests.”

“You know you are a story-teller and a little fool,” cried Sylvie. “After what happened this morning do you suppose I can believe a word you say? You are a—”

Pierrette did not wait for Sylvie to finish; foreseeing a torrent of insults she rushed away without a light and ran to her room. Sylvie turned white with anger and muttered between her teeth, “She shall pay for this!”

“Shall you pay for the misère?” Madame de Chargeboeuf asked.

As she spoke Pierrette struck her head on the open passage door.

“Good! I’m glad of it,” cried Sylvie at the sound of the blow.

“She must be hurt,” Desfondrilles said.

“She deserves it,” replied Sylvie.

“It was a bad blow,” observed Mademoiselle Habert.

Sylvie thought she might escape paying her misère if she went to see Pierrette, but Madame de Chargeboeuf stopped her.

“Pay us first,” she laughed; “you will forget it when you come back.”

The remark, based on Sylvie’s trickery and notorious bad faith at cards, was approved. Sylvie sat down and seemed to think no more of Pierrette; an indifference that surprised no one. When the game ended about half past nine she flung herself into an easy chair by the fireplace and did not rise as guests departed. The colonel tormented her; she did not know what to think of him.

“Men are so false!” she cried as she went to bed.

Pierrette had given herself a terrible blow above the ear, at the spot where young girls part their hair. The next day a large swelling appeared.

“God has punished you,” Sylvie said at breakfast. “You disobeyed me; you treated me with disrespect in leaving the room before I had finished my sentence; you got what you deserved.”

“Nevertheless,” Rogron said, “she ought to put on a compress of salt and water.”

“Oh, it is nothing at all, cousin,” Pierrette replied.

The poor child had reached a state where even such a remark seemed to her a proof of kindness.

The week ended as it had begun: in continual torture. Sylvie grew inventive in her refinements of tyranny, and Pierrette dared not complain of her vague sufferings or the real pains in her head. Determined to keep silence with stubborn Breton obstinacy, her thoughts turned continually to Brigaut — fearing for him if discovered, yet longing for his nearness. The single glimpse she had of him had the power of an exile’s sight of his country or a martyr’s lift to heaven.

Brigaut, aware of Pierrette’s look, sought a way to communicate. He proposed a simple plan: at a certain hour she should let down a letter by a string from her window. Sustained by the hope of this contact, Pierrette clung to the idea that “Brigaut is here!” and endured her daily torments.

One morning in the market Brigaut, lying in wait, slipped a note to Pierrette while calmly buying fruit near Sylvie. Though trembling, Pierrette concealed the letter in her apron; the flushed excitement marked them both deeply. Sylvie, who did not recognize the Breton accent, took no notice, and Pierrette returned home with her treasure.

These letters, later to be documents in a terrible inquiry, begin with Brigaut’s: he promises nightly visits at midnight, asks Pierrette to let down a string with any note she wishes to send, and reproaches the relatives who have reduced her from the daughter of a colonel to a servant. He urges her to return to Brittany with him — saying he can earn enough for both, offering money if needed, and describing his prayers for her. He hopes they can pray together in church on Sundays. The note closes with a promise to come that night.

Pierrette read it that evening and, despite not having pen or paper, crept to the dining-room to fetch writing materials. At midnight she wrote back, confessing that only Brigaut, Jacques, and her grandmother remained dear to her. She spoke of the ill-treatment at her cousins’ hands, how they scolded and made her scrub, and how she had once prayed for death. She expressed desire to return to Brittany and asked whether her grandmother had indeed taken money from her estate. She told Jacques of her illness — pains in her head, bones, and back — her lack of proper food, and the shame of being forbidden even to weep openly. She promised to love him always and ended as “Your faithful servant, Pierrette Lorrain,” then made a weight for the letter by hollowing a crust of bread and fastened it to the string.

At midnight she lowered the letter; Brigaut undid the cord, stole the note, and they signalled each other for over an hour, Pierrette reluctant to send him away, until at last she closed her window and slept with his letter under her pillow. The moral uplift of their communion made Pierrette rise next day light and radiant, a change Sylvie noticed with jealousy. Suspicions roused, Sylvie resolved to spy. At dinner she taunted Pierrette about seeming well, accused her of putting on illness, and kept her from the salon that evening, ordering her to stay in the dining-room with a candle and sew. Pierrette, silent, resolved to await Brigaut at midnight.

That night Brigaut left a letter instructing a signal: he would imitate an owl’s cry thrice, a sound Pierrette would recognize, and asked her not to tire herself waiting because he could not come for several days; he promised to bring good news soon and hinted at some impediment without saying what. He declared his resolve to rescue her, to appeal to the law if he failed, and promised to be for her either a lover or a devoted servant until he earned fortune enough to match her worth.

Brigaut also wrote to Madame Lorrain at Nantes in a separate letter, urging her to come, warning that Pierrette would die of ill-treatment, and enclosing Pierrette’s note as proof. He seemed to fear the grandmother might be dead.

Pierrette, in her naive faith, believed Brigaut’s promise that relief would come in days; still, a dreadful foreboding came to her: “Poor Jacques,” she thought, “he does not know the hole into which I have fallen!” Sylvie had indeed heard some of the exchanges and, jumping out of bed, peered through the blinds to see a man hurrying from the square. She found a light in Pierrette’s room, spied through the keyhole, and, entering, saw the cord Pierrette had forgotten. Pierrette, surprised, said nothing when Sylvie demanded why a candle burned so late. The old maid, instead of exploding, determined to surprise Pierrette and the colonel together and confound their supposed lovers. Pierrette, sensing danger, sewed the letters into her corset and covered them with calico.

For a few nights Brigaut withheld visits, and Sylvie spent sleepless hours watching, searching the attic during confession, and finding nothing. Her fury grew; jealousy became an occupation, and her scrutinies intensified. Pierrette’s health declined further. Doctor Neraud did not come that week; the colonel, fearing disgrace, avoided showing concern. Bathilde and others explained the change by natural growth, but at last Pierrette’s sufferings overwhelmed her; one Sunday evening she fainted in the salon. The colonel caught her and carried her to a sofa. Sylvie asserted the fainting was a ploy; the colonel insisted on sending for a doctor. When Sylvie was ordered to unfasten Pierrette’s gown and about to touch the corset, Pierrette, roused by alarm, sat up and refused help, but Sylvie had already felt the papers hidden there. She declared the Girl’s illness a pretence.

After the card-playing, Sylvie, furious and seeking vengeance, detained Vinet, the lawyer, and confided in him. Vinet, who owed Rogron services, proposed a plan of political and personal calculation: he would help Sylvie secure power and arrange advantageous marriages. He told her the colonel was a gambler who had squandered his opportunities, and advised Sylvie to wait until Vinet’s rise to make a better match; to marry her brother to Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf so her family’s property would be secured and to strike at Gouraud and Pierrette. The suggestion delighted Sylvie; she would leave Pierrette to work for her living and deny her inheritance. Vinet left, having planted the plan in Sylvie’s stubborn mind.

Meanwhile, the colonel confronted Vinet in the square about intrigues; Vinet promised to find a wealthy young wife for him before elections. The colonel demanded such a wife or threatened consequences. Their quarrel underscored the tangled ambitions surrounding Pierrette’s fate.

That night three owl-cries sounded, imitated by Brigaut; Pierrette rose, opened her window, and let down a ball of silk with a letter. Sylvie, restless and wary, heard the sound and rushed in time to see Jacques running. She burst into Pierrette’s room, caught her unwinding the silk and tried to seize the letter. A violent struggle ensued. Pierrette, by a surge of courage, clenched the letter in her hand; Sylvie, in a frenzy, seized and tried to force it from her. The scene turned savage: Sylvie twisted the child’s arm, dug her nails into her flesh, and in frenzy bit the wrist. Pierrette’s eyes met Sylvie’s with a look of defiant innocence. Sylvie struck the closed fist upon the window-sill and then the mantelpiece as she tried to wrench the letter away. Pierrette cried “Help! help! they are murdering me!” and bled.

At that moment loud knocks came at the front door. Rogron, awoken and terrified at the noise, opened to Brigaut, who rushed in, followed by a sort of phantom. Sylvie’s eyes fell on Pierrette’s corset; remembering the papers, she sprang upon it like a tiger, tearing it open and revealing the letters. She flaunted them with a savage smile. Pierrette, exhausted, fell to her knees; then, from the doorway, a woman with white hair and an aged but resolute face appeared. It was Pierrette’s grandmother, who had come to fetch her. She caught her grandchild in her arms and, without speaking to Sylvie, carried her away toward Frappier’s house. The old woman’s majesty and accusation appalled Sylvie; she fancied death, returned home, ripped open the corset, read Brigaut’s letters, and went to sleep in perplexity, unaware of the terrible consequences that would follow.

Brigaut’s letters to the old woman, received just as she learned of new means, arrived at a time when hope and sorrow were mingled. Madame Lorrain had lately been enriched by an exceptional act of reparation. Monsieur Collinet, a merchant wronged in 1814 and later prosperous in America, had returned to Nantes, found the widow Lorrain in the institution of Saint-Jacques, and, moved by her resignation and her story, restored to her the capital and interest of forty-two thousand francs. He further promised to pay another forty thousand to cover her husband’s debts. The gift brought the old woman instant joy: she resolved to live again with Pierrette and to marry the girl to Brigaut, who could now be supported by the fortune. She set out that night, taking the mail and by diligence reached Provins by half-past eleven, determined to rescue her grandchild.

At Frappier’s Brigaut, shocked by Pierrette’s state, promised to bring her to her grandmother; they hurried to the Rogron house where Pierrette’s screams were heard. Rogron opened the door in fright; the grandmother, with the strength of righteous anger, lifted Pierrette and bore her to Brigaut’s lodgings at Frappier’s. There, on a half-made bed, Pierrette was laid and fainted, her hand still clenched and deeply wounded by the nails that had dug into it. Brigaut, Frappier, his wife, and the old woman looked on in silent amazement.

The grandmother, seeing blood and the condition of the child, issued a cry of conviction: “They have killed her!” She sank to her knees on the floor and made a vow to Saint Anne d’Auray. Then, with quick decision, she bade Brigaut fetch the greatest doctor in Paris, offering bank-bills; she unfastened her gown to take out forty-two notes and gave them to him. Frappier, practical, suggested first consulting the local Doctor Martener, then sending to Paris. Brigaut took Martener’s advice and hurried away in the next diligence for Paris.

Monsieur Martener examined Pierrette, declared she could not have inflicted those wounds herself, and suspected the extent of her neglect. The grandmother recounted how her granddaughter had been summoned by the Rogrons in a letter promising care but reduced to servitude. Martener, inspecting Pierrette’s head and pulse, found an abscess above the left ear and thought the case serious. He insisted on a consultation with the Parisian physician he had named — Horace Bianchon — and said the local surgeon should be called. He gave a quieting draught for sleep and advised that Pierrette be made to tell all.

Left alone with the old woman, Pierrette gradually admitted the torments she had endured: the scolding, the menial labors, the deprivation, and the violence of Sylvie’s attack that night. The grandmother told her of the money and promised a home with Brigaut; the girl’s relief was immense. The grandmother’s discovery opened to her a new world of wrongs she had silently borne. The old woman, who had supported her miseries with a resilient, sacrificial heart, now revealed the means to restore comforts and justice.

Those present watched the grandam with awe: for in her yellow, parchment face, with its wrinkled gravitas, revenge and mercy mingled. Two big tears rose in her eyes and rolled down her cheeks without wetting them. She ordered that a first-class doctor be fetched, charging Brigaut to hurry. Brigaut departed; Martener would await the arrival of the Parisian doctor. While Pierrette sank into a frail, soothed sleep under the draught, the grandmother sat by her bed, watching and kissing the girl’s forehead, hair, and hands as if in the litany of a mourner finding relief.

At nine that morning Monsieur Martener went to Monsieur Tiphaine and described the scene between Pierrette and Sylvie, the moral and physical tortures the Rogrons had inflicted, and the illnesses their cruelty had produced. Tiphaine summoned Auffray the notary, a maternal relative of Pierrette’s.

The feud between the Vinet and Tiphaine parties was fierce. The Rogrons’ followers had spread scandalous stories about Madame Tiphaine’s mother’s liaison with the banker du Tillet and her father’s bankruptcy; private wounds became partisan fuel in Provins. Tiphaine saw Pierrette’s case as an opportunity to humiliate the Rogrons, who hosted the salon that had spawned anti-monarchist plans and an opposition journal. The public prosecutor was notified; in a secret consultation with Auffray and Martener it was decided to appoint Auffray guardian so he could convene a Family Council and, backed by three doctors’ testimony, demand Pierrette’s release from the Rogrons. The procureur, Lesourd, would steer the affair toward a criminal inquiry.

By midday the town buzzed. Pierrette’s faint cries were amplified into public horror. A crowd gathered at Frappier’s shop as he described how Pierrette had been brought to him with broken fingers and a bloody hand. Doctor Bianchon’s post-chaise arrived about one; Martener and the hospital surgeon were summoned. The Rogrons were accused of deliberate ill-treatment and nearly killing their cousin.

Vinet, at the law courts, rushed to the Rogrons. Sylvie, evasive about the night, admitted nothing until pressed. Vinet warned bluntly that criminal charges would depend on Pierrette’s injuries. Under questioning Sylvie confessed jealousy and, painfully, that her suspicions were baseless. Vinet exploited their terror, insisting he needed a clear motive to represent them publicly; they contrived Rogron’s immediate marriage to Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, to be announced that day with banns posted Sunday. Sylvie agreed to appear in the marriage contract as assigning her capital to her brother while retaining the income. Vinet urged antedating the contract to bind the Chargeboeufs publicly and preserve their visits; he promised to defend them if they signed.

By noon Vinet had full authority to draw the contract and defend the Rogrons. At twelve a petition was made to Monsieur Tiphaine, sitting in chambers, against Brigaut and the widow Lorrain for abducting the minor Pierrette from her guardian’s house—turning the defenders into complainants. The judge postponed the hearing till four; Tiphaine wanted Auffray at the hearing with the doctors’ report.

The marriage announcement and Sylvie’s sacrifices alienated two supporters, Mademoiselle Habert and the colonel, who ostentatiously remained with the Rogrons but meant to hurt them. When Martener mentioned Pierrette’s head injury, Céleste and the colonel told of a blow Pierrette had given herself the evening she was forced to leave the salon and recounted Mademoiselle Rogron’s heartless remarks. Vinet had foreseen opposition, but he had secured the Rogrons’ fortune for Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf and planned to use that alliance to dominate Provins socially and politically.

From midday to four the Tiphaine circle checked on Pierrette. She, suffering, was unaware of the tumult; she found ineffable happiness in being reunited with her grandmother and Brigaut. Bianchon protested such barbarity and insisted all town physicians examine the case; Dr. Neraud, a friend of the Rogrons, was present. The unanimous report explained Pierrette’s malady, a sadly common but horrifying condition.

At four, President Tiphaine took his seat. Madame Lorrain, Auffray, Brigaut and others entered the court; Vinet alone faced them. He claimed two strangers had forced entry into the Rogron home and abducted the minor. Auffray, as surrogate guardian, presented the physicians’ report—signed by a Paris authority and all Provins doctors—asserting that Pierrette’s near-fatal condition resulted from ill-treatment by Rogron and his sister. Auffray asked that Pierrette not be returned to the guardian’s household but remain with a relative designated by the judge and proposed convening a Family Council to consider deposing the guardian.

Vinet objected that he had not seen the report to rebut it. The judge replied that it might have been submitted to the procureur du roi; the case was heard. Tiphaine’s order forbade returning Pierrette to her guardian until the Family Council met, commissioned the attending physician and the hospital surgeon to inspect her, and stipulated that if her injuries became alarming the criminal courts would intervene, without prejudice to Auffray’s civil suit.

Vinet scoffed, calling it fuss over a girl supposedly in an intrigue with an apprentice. He left to rally his party, arguing the Tiphaines made an unjust spectacle of Rogron. To his supporters, Sylvie’s seizure of letters and her supposed severity were natural; Rogron’s culpability was downplayed. Vinet added political color, casting the dispute as partisan.

Frappier’s house was later judged unsuitable for Pierrette; she was moved to Auffray’s with care and ostentation. Laid on a stretcher, accompanied by a Gray Sister and her grandmother, she passed under many windows, her pallor advantaging the Auffrays’ case. Installed on the second floor, with Martener and the hospital surgeon attending, Pierrette was tenderly cared for.

That evening exaggerations grew on both sides. The Rogron salon was crowded; Vinet had rallied the Liberals, the Chargeboeuf ladies dined with the Rogrons to sign the contract. He downplayed the Pierrette episode, confident the Royal courts would vindicate them; to some the Chargeboeuf alliance wiped the Rogrons’ slate clean and painted Pierrette as depraved. In Madame Tiphaine’s salon, vengeance for past slanders was taken out on the Rogrons, now monsters to be tried. Journalists offered opposing accounts: one article suggested no case existed; the Rogron paper hesitated, advising patience for the law.

The Family Council, selected by the juge de paix, included Rogron and the two Auffrays as nearest relatives, Monsieur Ciprey (maternal nephew), Mademoiselle Habert, Pierrette’s confessor, and Colonel Gouraud, a friend of her father. The judge’s choice seemed impartial—Habert and Gouraud being considered Rogron friends. Rogron requested Vinet for the council, thereby postponing it until late December when Tiphaine would be in Paris, a tactic to delay and influence the outcome.

Vinet spoke three hours before the council, alleging an intrigue between Pierrette and Brigaut to justify Sylvie’s severity, and portraying the guardian’s delegation of duties as natural. Despite his efforts the council unanimously removed Rogron's guardianship; Auffray became guardian and Ciprey surrogate. Testimony included Adele the servant against her former masters, Mademoiselle Habert recounting Mademoiselle Rogron’s cruel remarks, and Brigaut’s letter from Pierrette describing her ill-treatment. The council concluded Pierrette’s illness resulted from neglect by the guardian, and the case would proceed publicly. Rogron, advised by Vinet, opposed the council’s report being accepted by the court. Authorities intervened as Pierrette’s condition worsened; the trial, placed on the docket, was postponed until March 1828.

Meanwhile Rogron married Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf; Sylvie moved to the second floor to share with her. Madame Rogron supplanted Madame Tiphaine socially. Tiphaine, backed by Nucingen and du Tillet, was promoted to civil judge and moved to Paris; his son inherited and sold the Provins house—evidence, in Vinet’s narrative, of Tiphaine’s contempt for the town. The promotion brought little benefit to the Vinet faction, but Vinet exploited the events politically. Thus Pierrette’s brutal martyrdom—even the trepanning performed by Martener under Bianchon’s advice—was mired in legal delay by an unscrupulous lawyer while she languished in agonizing pain.

Martener and the Auffrays grew fond of Pierrette and her grandmother; their Roman antiquity of feeling impressed them. Martener, who had begun his career in Paris but returned to provincial life, devoted himself to curing her, treating her as a poem of suffering he would not discuss. His dedication imposed reverence and quiet on the Auffrays’ household; even the children kept silence for her sake. The Auffray house, on a terrace beneath the chateau ruins overlooking the valley, became her refuge. Pierrette’s room above Auffray’s study allowed her to see the valley, a landscape she had rarely known.

Brigaut, unable to work, visited thrice daily, sorrowful and faithful. The grandmother, concealing her despair to comfort Pierrette, made her a Breton cap recalling earlier, happier days. Pierrette’s delicate features, pale skin and serious expression rendered her an embodiment of melancholy; all served her with devotion. Madame Martener sent a piano; music transfixed her, resigned yet touching. Her religious comforters admired a saintly resignation that convinced even skeptics.

Martener corresponded with Bianchon and sought to drain the abscess; periodic improvements—a better appetite, some color—cheered them, but the head disease remained grave. Bianchon came from Paris with the celebrated Desplein, who warned Martener that only a miracle could save the child: caries of the bone had begun and, at her age, bones were fragile. The operation was performed in early March 1828 by Desplein, who cautioned the odds. Martener had even proposed a daring procedure akin to lithotrity—passing a hollow instrument into the head—that Desplein refused. Returning from Paris, Martener announced to the Auffrays, Madame Lorrain, the priests and Brigaut that science could do no more; recovery was left to God. They responded with masses and vows.

While Pierrette lay dying, Vinet calumniated her in court. The judge accepted the Family Council’s report; Vinet appealed. The procureur du roi required further evidence, including testimony from Pierrette. When Desfondrilles came to the Auffrays to record it, Pierrette was dying, with her confessor near for extreme unction. She begged forgiveness for her cousins, saying judgment belonged to God; she told her grandmother to leave all to Brigaut and to give Adele a thousand francs, insisting if Adele had stayed with the Rogrons she would not be dying.

At three o’clock on the Tuesday of Easter week, on a bright day, Pierrette died. Her grandmother kept vigil through the night and sewed her shroud with stiff fingers. Brigaut went to Frappier’s to make the coffin. Frappier watched as Brigaut planed, his plane cutting ribbons of wood wet with tears. He fashioned the coffin alone and lined it with lead; by chance the lead cost exactly the sum Pierrette had given Brigaut for her journey—the coincidence overwhelmed him. He burned his tools, settled his accounts, and bade farewell; his grief made him a participant in the awful finale of the Rogrons’ tyranny.

At the Auffray home, the room where Pierrette lay was crowded. Rogron’s emissaries hovered near the body, and the surgeon in a dissecting apron prepared instruments; Madame Lorrain, on her knees, cried out, believing they intended to open the body as they had while she was alive. Brigaut, having brought the coffin, seized a chisel, threatened the men, and swore he would kill any who touched her. Auffray and the curate tried to restrain him; the lawyers and doctors left. The plumber soldered the lead to make the coffin less likely to be opened. With Pierrette dead, the guardianship suit collapsed; Rogron did not dare attend her funeral.

Brigaut left Provins and walked to Paris, petitioned the Dauphiness to join the Royal guard and was admitted. When the Algerian expedition opened he sought service and rose: he became sub-lieutenant, then advanced; he distinguished himself, rose to major, and later received a commission as an officer—taciturn, solitary, trusted, and never wounded. He inherited forty-six thousand francs from old Madame Lorrain, who died in Paris in 1829.

In public life, Vinet rose: elected deputy in 1830 and later procureur-general; his influence secure, he remained in power. Rogron became receiver-general in the same town where Vinet held legal functions; Tiphaine, after aligning with the July dynasty, became president of the Royal court there. The ex-beautiful Madame Tiphaine and Madame Rogron lived amicably; Vinet was close with Tiphaine. Rogron, declining in health and witless, made absurd remarks; Sylvie managed his property, lent money at high interest, and lived frugally.

General Baron Gouraud married Mademoiselle Matifat, daughter of a druggist, as Vinet had arranged; he became a departmental commander and was later made peer of France for his conduct during riots under Casimir Perier. None of those involved in Pierrette’s death showed remorse. Desfondrilles, to further his prospects, was installed as president of the Provins court through Vinet’s efforts. Sylvie preserved a small circle.

Over time locals downplayed the scandal. Travelers returning from Paris, who had visited Mademoiselle Rogron, heard softened versions blaming poor constitution or an imprudent liaison. Frappier was mocked as given to wine; Habert’s motives were dismissed as personal.

Two men remembered Pierrette daily: Doctor Martener and Major Brigaut; they alone knew the hideous truth. To give full scale to that truth one thinks of Beatrice Cenci—another young woman condemned by intrigues like those that destroyed Pierrette. As in those cases, legality serves scoundrelism unless one believes in a higher justice.
