**The Vicar of Tours: Summary**

**Dedication**
De Balzac dedicates "The Vicar of Tours" to David, a sculptor, acknowledging the enduring nature of David's work compared to the uncertain permanence of his own literary contributions.

**I**
In autumn 1826, Abbe Birotteau, a short, corpulent priest nearing sixty, is caught in the rain while returning home from a friend's house. Despite his discomfort, he is buoyed by the prospect of being nominated as canon at the cathedral of Saint-Gatien, a long-held ambition. He reflects on his life, particularly his desire for a comfortable existence, which includes good food, clean clothes, and the coveted canonry.

Birotteau lives with Mademoiselle Gamard, an old maid who boards priests. He has long envied the apartment of his deceased friend, Abbe Chapeloud, who had created a beautiful living space filled with books and comfort. After Chapeloud's death, Birotteau inherits his library and furniture, which brings him joy but also a sense of guilt over his friend's passing.

As Birotteau settles into his new life, he enjoys the comforts provided by Mademoiselle Gamard, who is known for her meticulous care of her lodgers. However, he soon becomes aware of a shift in her treatment towards him. Small signs of neglect, such as a lack of fire in his room and the absence of his slippers, lead him to suspect that Mademoiselle Gamard harbors ill will against him.

Despite his good nature, Birotteau struggles to understand the reasons behind her hostility. He reflects on his relationship with her, realizing that he has taken for granted the attentions he once received. Mademoiselle Gamard, feeling slighted by Birotteau's social engagements outside her home, begins to withdraw her kindness, leading to a growing tension between them.

Birotteau's longing for the canonry intensifies, and he becomes increasingly oblivious to the brewing conflict with Mademoiselle Gamard. His naivety prevents him from recognizing her resentment, which stems from his social aspirations and his failure to appreciate her efforts.

As the story unfolds, Birotteau's internal struggles and the dynamics of his relationship with Mademoiselle Gamard reveal the complexities of human desires, ambitions, and the often-unseen consequences of social interactions. The narrative explores themes of envy, ambition, and the fragility of happiness, ultimately illustrating how misunderstandings and unacknowledged grievances can lead to profound personal conflicts.

The following morning, Birotteau awoke with thoughts of his potential canonry, momentarily forgetting the ominous signs he had perceived the night before. He rang for his servant, Marianne, to light a fire, but when she did not come, he was interrupted by the arrival of Abbe Troubert, who entered after knocking. Troubert, a tall and lean man with a serious demeanor, contrasted sharply with the plump and jovial Birotteau. Their monthly visits were routine, and Troubert expressed concern over Birotteau's health and prospects for the canonry, unaware of the vicar's naivety regarding Troubert's ambitions.

The two men were opposites: Troubert's somber, sarcastic nature clashed with Birotteau's cheerful disposition. While Troubert was respected for his austere presence, Birotteau's amiable nature kept him in his position as vicar. Troubert, now fifty and in declining health, was expected to be promoted to vicar-general, while Birotteau's robust health made him a less appealing candidate for advancement.

Abbe Chapeloud, a sensible man, had quietly opposed Troubert's rise, but Troubert maintained a facade of respect towards him. The morning after Mademoiselle Gamard had declared war on Birotteau, Troubert visited him, asking to borrow a book. Birotteau, pleased to help, felt grateful for Troubert's kindness, especially since it meant he would have a fire to dress by.

As they descended for breakfast, Mademoiselle Gamard, Birotteau's landlady, scolded him for bringing books into the dining room. She was irritable, having been disturbed the previous night, and her demeanor soured the atmosphere. Birotteau, feeling the tension, struggled to engage in conversation, ultimately falling silent during breakfast. He attempted to break the ice with small talk, but Gamard's disdainful responses left him feeling more isolated.

Gamard embodied the archetype of an old maid, her life marked by bitterness and unfulfilled desires. Her character reflected societal judgments against unmarried women, leading to her sharpness and lack of charm. She was a product of her circumstances, having never married, and her bitterness manifested in her interactions with others, particularly Birotteau.

Despite her harshness, Birotteau remained oblivious to the full extent of Gamard's malice. He was caught in a cycle of trying to please her while enduring her disdain. After breakfast, he retreated to the church, where he found solace in his duties, momentarily forgetting his troubles. However, upon returning home late for dinner, he discovered that Gamard had manipulated the clock to ensure he missed the meal, a tactic that exemplified her vindictive nature.

Birotteau's life was increasingly overshadowed by Gamard's petty tyranny, and he found himself trapped in a web of her schemes, unable to escape the emotional turmoil she inflicted upon him.

Eight days into the story, Abbe Birotteau discovers a plot against him orchestrated by Mademoiselle Gamard, who has been harboring resentment for months. Initially, Birotteau was oblivious to her malicious intentions, but after a series of unsettling incidents, he realizes he is under her scrutiny and feels increasingly tormented. His once peaceful life devolves into despair as he grapples with the old maid's vindictiveness, which he perceives as a personal attack.

Birotteau, feeling isolated and ashamed to confide in friends about his troubles, decides to seek help from Abbe Troubert, hoping for intervention. However, Troubert's response is dismissive, suggesting that Birotteau's issues are trivial and attributing Gamard's behavior to her health problems. Troubert's indifference only deepens Birotteau's sense of helplessness.

In an attempt to escape the situation, Birotteau plans a short stay at Madame de Listomere's country house, unaware that this move plays into Gamard's hands. While at the Alouette, he is confronted by Monsieur Caron, Gamard's lawyer, who informs him that his absence could lead to legal complications regarding his living arrangements. Birotteau, confused and distressed, returns to his friends, who rally around him, urging him to stand firm against Gamard.

Madame de Listomere and others analyze the situation, suspecting that Troubert covets Birotteau's apartment. They encourage him to resist Gamard's influence, but Birotteau's naivety leads him to sign a document relinquishing his rights to his belongings in Gamard's house, believing it to be a mere formality. This act seals his fate, as he later learns that Gamard intends to keep his possessions as compensation for the difference in board prices.

Upon returning to confront Gamard, Birotteau is met with her triumphant demeanor and realizes the extent of his loss. Troubert has taken over his former apartment, and Gamard claims his furniture as part of their agreement. Devastated, Birotteau seeks solace at Madame de Listomere's, where he is comforted by friends who promise to help him reclaim his dignity and possessions.

Mademoiselle Salomon, a devoted friend, takes Birotteau back to the Alouette, where he struggles to comprehend the betrayal he has faced. His friends, including Monsieur de Bourbonne, begin to strategize a legal response against Gamard, believing they can expose her deceit. However, the local lawyer advises against pursuing the case, suggesting that Birotteau's actions may be perceived as ingratitude.

Despite the discouragement, Birotteau's friends remain committed to supporting him. They recognize the social implications of the conflict, as it pits them against Gamard and Troubert, who wield significant influence in their community. The struggle escalates, drawing attention from the townspeople and igniting a fierce rivalry between the factions.

As the legal battle unfolds, Birotteau's friends rally to gather evidence of the value of his possessions, which far exceed what he owed Gamard. The appraisal reveals that Birotteau's belongings are worth a substantial sum, bolstering his case against Gamard's claims. The situation becomes a public spectacle, with gossip and speculation swirling around the town.

Ultimately, Birotteau's plight serves as a microcosm of the broader social dynamics at play, illustrating the complexities of human relationships and the often-hidden motivations that drive individuals. The narrative captures the tension between personal integrity and societal expectations, as Birotteau navigates the treacherous waters of betrayal, friendship, and the quest for justice.

**Summary:**

A few days after hostilities began between Birotteau and the old maid, Baron de Listomere received alarming news about his potential retirement from the Navy. Concerned for his future, he sought clarification from the minister, who dismissed his fears. However, further inquiries revealed that a document confirming his retirement was pending signature. The baron turned to his uncle, a deputy, for help. His uncle warned him that his political opinions had made him unpopular, particularly with the influential Abbe Troubert, vicar-general and a key figure in the province. The uncle advised the baron to reconcile with Troubert to secure his career.

Three days later, the baron returned home, anxious about the family's support for Birotteau, whom he deemed a liability. He consulted Monsieur de Bourbonne, who suggested they distance themselves from Birotteau and appease Troubert. The baroness then approached Birotteau, asking him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house, citing family interests. Birotteau, distressed, reluctantly agreed but insisted on retrieving a portrait of Abbe Chapeloud, which belonged to him.

Madame de Listomere promised to negotiate with Mademoiselle Gamard for the portrait. However, shortly after, Gamard fell ill and died, leaving her fortune to Troubert. The town buzzed with gossip, blaming Birotteau for her death. Madame de Listomere visited Troubert to negotiate peace, presenting Birotteau's relinquishment of claims. Troubert, feigning indifference, ultimately refused to part with Chapeloud's portrait, claiming it was a cherished memento.

Despite her efforts, Mademoiselle Gamard's death led to Birotteau's further isolation. He was appointed curate of Saint-Symphorien, a remote parish, effectively exiling him from his community. Meanwhile, Troubert ascended to Bishop of Troyes, consolidating his power. Madame de Listomere passed away, leaving an annuity to Birotteau, but Troubert contested this legacy, threatening the baron's career.

In the end, Birotteau's life deteriorated, marked by illness and despair, while Troubert's influence grew unchecked. The narrative reflects the intricate interplay of power, ambition, and the consequences of ecclesiastical politics in provincial France, illustrating how personal vendettas can shape destinies.
