To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

Dear Child,—You, the joy of the household, whose pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of Wierzschovnia, how can I dedicate such a melancholy story to you? Yet sorrows should be spoken of to a young girl like you, since the day may come when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them. It is difficult to find a subject worthy of your eyes, so I have no choice; perhaps you will feel fortunate when you read this story sent to you by your old friend, De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

On an October morning in 1827 a sixteen-year-old boy in modest clothes stood in a small square of Lower Provins, watching mills and simple bourgeois houses framed by linden trees. He noted a recently restored white-stone dwelling with gray blinds and yellow iron balconies—a neatness betraying the pride of a retired petty shopkeeper.

As the sun lit a calico curtain in a garret window, the boy began a Breton ditty for newlyweds. A girl, Pierrette, signaled from the window; he offered a yellow broom-flower. He was Brigaut, her childhood companion, and told her he planned to settle nearby. Alarmed, Pierrette urged him to flee when a grotesque old maid below—sharp-eyed and prone to imagining scandal—appeared.

Overwhelmed by Brigaut’s visit, Pierrette reflected on her past. Seated in a shabby armchair, frail and thoughtful, she remembered childhood pleasures shaded by illness and uncertainty. Her mother, a Demoiselle Auffray from a tangled family—the father had married twice and left a complicated inheritance—later married Major Lorrain, a Breton officer who died in battle, leaving them impoverished; the Lorrains could not provide for Pierrette.

Ultimately she was sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, in Provins. Unaware of all the intricacies of her lineage and her parents’ fortunes and losses, Pierrette’s fate becomes increasingly shaped by these relatives—the Rogrons’ control setting the stage for the drama to follow.

Père Rogron, an indulgent, ruddy-faced innkeeper in Provins, and his unattractive, selfish wife scraped by on their land and inn until inheriting a dilapidated house from old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother. Their children, Sylvie and Jérôme-Denis, were neglected and ill-bred; Rogron sent them to Paris to work in shops with little guidance.

In Paris Sylvie apprenticed on rue Saint-Denis and became a forewoman; Jérôme rose to head-clerk. They rarely saw each other but pooled savings to buy the retail “Family Sister,” achieving modest success. Severe and competitive, Sylvie ran the business shrewdly though plainly; Jérôme proved ill-suited to broader trade. Obsessed with the shop, stingy and harsh to clerks, they were pleasant to customers but sour at home.

While mourning their father they learned of cousin Pierrette’s inheritance. To protect their wealth they refused to take Pierrette in, delayed replying, and returned to Provins four years before Brigaut arrived.

Back in Provins their Parisian airs quickly faded as provincial norms reasserted themselves. The city’s vibrancy gave way to local stillness. With new means they took up travel, politics, hunting, and remodeled their house to mimic wealthier families, trying to join the town’s social circle led by the Julliards, Guepins, and Guenées. Despite lavish dinners and ostentation—Madame Tiphaine promoted her husband through influential gatherings—the Rogrons lacked polish. Invitations dwindled and ridicule spread. Sylvie’s faith that money ensured status collapsed; the siblings sank into ennui, nostalgic for business life. Bringing Pierrette back exposed their neglect. Bitter and sidelined, they befriended lower bourgeois figures like Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet, who exploited their wealth. The story shows provincial hierarchies and subtle social codes ultimately trump money.

After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette—ashamed to live on charity—was relieved when wealthier relatives agreed to take her in. Brigaut, a childhood friend and her mother’s friend’s son, gave her sixty francs for the trip to Paris; she accepted without thanks. Penniless, she was briefly aided by a conductor who paid for a hotel but treated her like baggage; four days later a kinder conductor took her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s, where her cousins received her with astonishment.

Dressed in Breton clothes and carrying a small bundle, Pierrette was shy and spoke little while Sylvie and Rogron questioned her. Shown a modest room that seemed luxurious to her, she quickly became the object of Sylvie’s anxiety over her lack of proper clothes; Sylvie resolved to buy her a wardrobe. The welcome revealed a chilling preoccupation with appearances. Where Pierrette had known affection and freedom in Brittany, the Rogrons prized possessions and outward respectability. Reproached the next morning for dirty shoes, she found their constant fault-finding and concern for material matters sharply at odds with the love she expected. Her innocence made their disdain especially painful; she began to stifle her joy and grow passive to avoid scorn.

The Rogrons obsessed over her appearance, treating her as a burden and suffocating her spirit. When she tore a dress playing, she braced for stern rebuke. Life in Provins worsened as Sylvie forbade visits to friends and pursued social ambitions with Vinet and the colonel. As they plotted for status, Pierrette was increasingly confined—transformed from a lively child into a pale, silent shadow—enduring self-interested care that was not love.

One night, when Pierrette went to say goodnight, Sylvie turned away coldly and made her cry. Pierrette protested that her rich cousin was not as kind as her poor grandmother; Sylvie retorted that the grandmother had taken money while the cousin would leave her some—Pierrette wanted affection, not wealth. Accused of mischief, disobedience, and breaking a watch, she left ashamed. Rogron demanded punishment; Madame Vinet suggested school; the colonel hoped instruction might keep her quiet. Vinet called her a rough diamond to be polished and hired a writing master. The lessons produced ink stains and scattered papers blamed on Pierrette, and she was sharply told she must earn her keep. Rogron’s severity and Sylvie’s indifference deepened her isolation; complaints of pains were dismissed.

Only Adèle, the servant, showed kindness, secretly warming Pierrette’s bed. Meanwhile the colonel and Vinet blamed her Breton nature and pursued local prestige—investing in a newspaper—while Pierrette’s situation worsened. She considered escaping to her grandparents, but a death and Rogron’s appointment as guardian prevented it; Vinet hinted at an inheritance and Rogron secured a promise of eight thousand francs from her grandmother.

Preparations for Pierrette’s first communion altered the household. Sylvie insisted on the zealous priest Monsieur Habert, whose influence gave Pierrette solace; she abandoned escape plans and embraced the Church’s teachings, a change even Sylvie imitated. Habert’s sister offered to take her to school, but the Rogrons refused, fearing a convent education. Later, with the arrival of the Chargeboeufs and the alluring Bathilde, Sylvie forced Pierrette into domestic tasks. Without Adèle’s protection she was treated worse than the servants—scolded for trifles, denied praise—and over the years her health and spirit faded into a silent, overlooked shadow.

Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut’s arrival would provoke in the Rogron family, it is necessary to explain how he came to Provins, since he plays a significant but passive role.

Distressed by Pierrette’s change from lively to pale and sorrowful, Brigaut fled the Rogron house and watched her from the Upper town. After a short apprenticeship in Paris he returned to Provins as a journeyman cabinetmaker so he could stay near her, concealing his feelings while learning about the family. Madame Frappier disclosed the Rogrons’ dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. Brigaut often glimpsed Pierrette at market and church; her frailty and secret signals for him to remain hidden deepened his concern for her.

Pierrette fretted about changes since Brigaut’s return but did not grasp the Rogrons’ internal strife, especially between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste), who maneuvered for advantageous marriages. Sylvie, forty-two, feared aging alone; Céleste, ten years younger and urged on by her priest brother, actively sought a husband. A recent political shift had emboldened local Liberals and intensified competition for social standing in Provins.

Sylvie consulted a doctor about marrying at her age; his discouraging prognosis heightened her anxiety. Céleste urged delay to remove a rival. Sylvie grew jealous of the colonel’s attention to Pierrette. Vinet, a lawyer with designs on Bathilde, exploited Sylvie: he plotted to separate the colonel from Pierrette and divert the Rogron fortune to Bathilde, persuading the colonel to court Pierrette while secretly scheming for Bathilde’s benefit. After overhearing Brigaut serenade Pierrette, Sylvie concluded the colonel was involved and, one tense morning, accused Pierrette of harboring a secret admirer. Pierrette’s silence and the household’s pressure worsened her health. Brigaut offered brief comfort, but Sylvie’s relentless scrutiny deepened Pierrette’s isolation, entwining love, jealousy, and ambition and setting the stage for the tragedy to follow.

For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the Constitutionnel to Gouraud, so Gouraud began coming himself to gossip and walk with Sylvie. Eager to see him, Sylvie dressed her best; when he arrived Rogron excused himself, leaving them alone.

Gouraud asked after Sylvie’s cousin; she said the cousin was unwell. He grew uneasy: Vinet’s rising influence with the Rogrons made him feel sidelined. A half-pay officer, he feared Vinet controlled their political plans and that Rogron’s infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf threatened his prospects. He even suspected Vinet might push him toward Pierrette as a ploy to favor Vinet and Bathilde.

Alone with Sylvie he perceived her unease; when she tentatively mentioned Pierrette he dismissed any affection for the girl, claiming he preferred a more mature partner. Relieved, Sylvie concealed her intentions and hinted her brother had once considered Pierrette a match. Sensing her interest, Gouraud declared he would marry Sylvie, delighting Rogron. Sylvie began planning their home but remained wary of Pierrette’s interactions with the colonel.

As dinner neared Sylvie scolded Pierrette for a stain but ordered her to dress nicely—suspecting collusion. The Chargeboeuf ladies arrived; Bathilde, spectacularly dressed at Vinet’s urging, treated marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone to power, using beauty and connections for independence.

The evening grew tense: Bathilde’s condescension and Vinet’s remarks sharpened rivalries. At cards Pierrette’s innocent comments provoked Sylvie, and Pierrette left in tears. That night Sylvie brooded on men’s duplicity and her precarious position. The next day Pierrette suffered a painful fall; Sylvie blamed disobedience and showed no sympathy, widening the rift.

The following week was relentless torment for Pierrette under Sylvie’s escalating cruelty. She concealed Brigaut’s arrival to avoid anger, but longed for him. Determined to communicate, Brigaut arranged a nightly note exchange: he slipped a message while pretending to buy fruit and asked her to lower a string from her window.

Pierrette returned with his heartfelt letter and, despite difficulty obtaining paper without waking Sylvie, replied. Their hopeful correspondence continued while Pierrette’s health declined. One evening she fainted; in a struggle over her letters she was hurt and distressed. At Brigaut’s appeals Madame Lorrain arrived, witnessed the violence, summoned medical aid, and promised protection. As Pierrette recovered, her bond with Madame Lorrain deepened and a future with her grandmother and Brigaut offered a hopeful glimmer. Pierrette remained determined to reclaim her life.

################################################
At nine o’clock Monsieur Martener told Monsieur Tiphaine about Pierrette’s mistreatment by the Rogrons. Tiphaine brought in Auffray, a notary relative, and longstanding enmities between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions—fueled by damaging truths about Madame Tiphaine’s family—gave the dispute political weight.

Seeing an opportunity to weaken the royalist Rogrons, Tiphaine consulted the public prosecutor. He, Auffray, and Martener arranged to petition the courts to make Auffray Pierrette’s guardian so a Family Council, backed by medical testimony, could remove her from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday the town buzzed as neighbors heard Pierrette’s cries and rumors spread. Doctors Bianchon and Brigaut examined her and confirmed abuse. Vinet, the Rogrons’ lawyer, warned of criminal exposure; Sylvie, initially evasive, later admitted jealousy and the truth. To stifle scandal, Vinet arranged a marriage contract that also served the Rogrons’ political aims, then filed a petition accusing Brigaut and the widow Lorrain of abducting Pierrette to cast his clients as victims.

The hearing was postponed, increasing public interest. The Tiphaines rallied support for Pierrette, who, unaware of politics, was cared for by her grandmother and Brigaut. The doctors, appalled by her condition, unanimously condemned the Rogrons’ treatment.

At the reconvened hearing, Auffray presented the physicians’ report to justify protecting Pierrette. Vinet countered, but the judge ordered she be kept from the Rogrons until the Family Council met—a public rebuke. The Council removed Rogron as guardian and appointed Auffray. Evidence produced a civil suit for neglect and abuse.

As the legal battle continued, Pierrette’s health declined. Martener and the Auffrays grew fond of her; her grandmother’s care and Brigaut’s visits comforted her. A critical operation in March 1828 offered little hope; she forgave her cousins and soon died. Her funeral filled the town; the Rogrons, fearing outrage, stayed away. Community sympathy hindered their maneuvers and Vinet faced backlash, yet they largely retained political standing. Only a few—Martener, Brigaut, and like‑minded friends—remembered the reality of Pierrette’s suffering, leaving unanswered questions of justice and accountability.
