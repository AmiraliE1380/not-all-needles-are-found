To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

Dear Child,—You, the joy of the household, whose pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of Wierzschovnia, how can I dedicate to you a story full of melancholy? Yet should not sorrows be spoken of to a young girl like you, since the day may come when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them? It is difficult to find a subject worthy of your eyes, so I have no choice; perhaps you will feel fortunate when you read this story sent to you by your old friend, De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

One October morning in 1827 a sixteen-year-old boy in modest dress stood in a small square of Lower Provins, among mills and bourgeois houses. He found the neat white-stone house he sought; its tidy air belonged to a retired petty shopkeeper. As the sun lit a calico garret curtain, the boy sang a Breton ditty for newlyweds—recalling Brittany and life’s bittersweet turns. Pierrette, at the window, signaled; he offered a yellow broom-flower. He was Brigaut, her childhood companion, newly returned and planning to settle nearby. Alarmed by a grotesque old maid below, sharp-eyed and ready to imagine scandal, Pierrette bade him go.

Overwhelmed, she thought of her fragile childhood: illness, faded joys, and a tangled lineage. Her mother, born a Demoiselle Auffray, had endured several marriages and a complicated inheritance, then wed Major Lorrain, who died in battle leaving poverty. Sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, Pierrette remained largely ignorant of her lineage and its financial consequences, setting the stage for what follows.

Père Rogron, an indulgent, ruddy-faced innkeeper, and his unattractive, selfish wife moved into a dilapidated house inherited from old Auffray. Their children, Sylvie and Jérôme-Denis, were neglected and sent to Paris to work with little support. Sylvie became a forewoman on rue Saint-Denis and Jérôme a head-clerk; together they bought the retail “Family Sister,” achieving modest success but gaining a reputation for severity and stinginess. Sylvie ran the business shrewdly though plainly; Jérôme was less competent. Their parsimony and harsh treatment of clerks caused high turnover. Pleasant to customers, Sylvie was a sour spinster at home.

After their father’s death they learned of cousin Pierrette’s inheritance. They considered selling and returning to Provins but refused to take Pierrette in, delayed replying, and left for Provins four years before Brigaut arrived. Back home, their Paris airs faded. New wealth funded travel, politics, hunting, and ostentatious renovations intended to mimic aristocracy, but they failed to enter established circles led by Madame Tiphaine. Ridiculed and sidelined, Sylvie’s faith that money bought status collapsed; she became frugal and nostalgic for business. Bringing Pierrette back exposed their neglect, and they fell in with lower bourgeois figures who exploited them—showing provincial social codes outweighed money.

After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette—ashamed to depend on charity—felt relieved when distant wealthy relatives were located. Brigaut, a childhood friend and her mother’s friend’s son, gave her sixty francs for the trip to Paris; she accepted without thanks, an intimacy that moved him to visit often and run after the coach in tears when she left. Penniless in Paris, a conductor briefly paid for her hotel; four days later a kinder one brought her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s, where her cousins received her with astonishment.

She arrived in Breton clothes with a small bundle. Sylvie and the maid Adèle were surprised; Pierrette, shy and overwhelmed, spoke little. Adèle and Sylvie showed her a modest room that felt luxurious to her, and Sylvie immediately fretted over Pierrette’s lack of clothing and resolved to buy a new wardrobe.

In Brittany Pierrette had known affection and freedom; with the Rogrons she found coldness and constant criticism. Exploring the house and garden the next morning, she was reproved for dirty shoes. Their concern for possessions, their continual fault‑finding, and their emphasis on appearances sharply contrasted with the love she’d known. Pierrette’s innocence made their disdain more painful; she began to suppress her joy and became passive for fear of scorn. When she tore a dress playing, she was devastated; the Rogrons treated such mishaps as proof she was a burden rather than a child.

Life in Provins worsened as Sylvie forbade visits to friends and pursued social ambitions encouraged by Madame Vinet and the colonel. As her cousins schemed for status, Pierrette was increasingly confined—transformed from a lively child into a shadow of her former self, enduring harsh, self‑interested care that was not love. At night Sylvie’s coldness made her cry; when Pierrette said her poor grandmother had been kinder, Sylvie countered that the grandmother had taken money while a rich cousin would leave her something—revealing their interest in inheritance over affection.

Accused of mischief and disobedience, she was punished and urged into schooling to be “hardened” and kept quiet. Vinet dubbed her a rough diamond and hired a writing master; the messy lessons and stained papers were blamed on Pierrette, who was told she must earn her keep. Only Adèle showed genuine kindness, secretly warming her bed. The colonel and Vinet blamed her supposed “Breton” nature while investing in social schemes like a local newspaper.

She considered escaping to her grandparents, but a death and Rogron’s appointment as guardian prevented it. Vinet hinted at an inheritance and Rogron secured a promise of eight thousand francs from her grandmother. Preparations for Pierrette’s first communion brought the zealous priest Monsieur Habert into her life; his influence led her to find solace in faith and abandon escape plans. Though Habert’s sister offered a school place, the Rogrons refused for fear it might lead to a convent.

Later social introductions and Sylvie’s ambitions intensified Pierrette’s drudgery. Bathilde and other visitors drew Rogron’s attention, and Sylvie forced Pierrette into domestic tasks. With Adèle’s protection diminished, she was treated worse than the staff—scolded for trifles, denied praise—and over the years her health and spirit faded into a silent shadow while her cousins remained oblivious.

Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut’s arrival would provoke in the Rogron household, it is necessary to explain how he came to Provins, since he plays a significant yet passive role.

Brigaut had fled the Rogron house, shaken by Pierrette’s decline from a lively girl to a pale, sorrowful figure. From a high point in the Upper town he watched her home, consumed by concern. Their rare bond pushed him to better himself: he briefly went to Paris to learn a trade, then returned to Provins and found work as a journeyman cabinetmaker so he could stay near Pierrette. He kept his feelings hidden and learned more about the Rogrons after Madame Frappier revealed their dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. He often saw Pierrette at the market or near the church, where she signaled him to keep away, and worried about her frailty.

Pierrette sensed changes but did not grasp the Rogrons’ internal strife between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste), both angling for advantageous marriages. Sylvie, forty-two, feared aging alone; Céleste, about thirty-two and urged on by her priest brother, sought a husband. A recent political shift favoring local Liberals intensified the scramble for social position. Sylvie’s consultation with a discouraging doctor heightened her anxiety; Céleste urged delay to remove a rival. Vinet, a lawyer who coveted Bathilde, exploited Sylvie—encouraging the colonel to court Pierrette while secretly plotting to divert the Rogron fortune to Bathilde. After overhearing Brigaut serenade Pierrette, Sylvie accused her of secret admirers; Pierrette’s frightened silence and increased toil worsened her health. Brigaut offered fleeting comfort, but love, jealousy, and ambition had become entangled, foreshadowing tragedy for all.

For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the Constitutionnel to Gouraud, so Gouraud came himself and lingered to gossip and walk with Sylvie. Eager to see him, Sylvie dressed her best; when Gouraud arrived Rogron excused himself, leaving them alone.

Gouraud’s questions about his cousin were met with evasiveness: the cousin was unwell. Uneasy about Vinet’s growing influence with the Rogrons, Gouraud—only a half-pay officer—felt sidelined. He feared Vinet controlling plans and Rogron’s infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf would ruin his prospects; he even suspected Vinet of manoeuvring to push him toward Pierrette to benefit Vinet and Bathilde. With Sylvie alone, he denied any interest in Pierrette and favored a more mature match. Encouraged, Sylvie hinted her brother had once thought Pierrette suitable; sensing her interest, Gouraud declared he would marry Sylvie, delighting Rogron. Sylvie began planning their home and watched Pierrette and the colonel with suspicion.

At dinner Sylvie scolded Pierrette for a stain but insisted she look presentable—suspecting collusion. The Chargeboeuf ladies arrived; Bathilde, gorgeously dressed under Vinet’s guidance, treated marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone to power, using beauty and connections for independence. The evening grew tense: Bathilde’s condescension and Vinet’s remarks sharpened rivalries. Pierrette’s innocent comments at cards provoked Sylvie, and Pierrette left in tears. That night Sylvie brooded on men’s duplicity and her precarious hold.

The next day Pierrette suffered a painful fall; Sylvie blamed her and showed no sympathy. A week of torment followed. Concealing Brigaut’s arrival, Pierrette longed for him; he arranged clandestine correspondence—slipping notes while buying fruit and asking her to lower a string from her window. Pierrette replied at midnight, pouring out love and describing her relatives’ abuse. Under Sylvie’s scrutiny her health declined; she fainted. Sylvie dismissed it and violently tried to seize Pierrette’s letters, wounding her. Brigaut’s urgent appeals brought Madame Lorrain, who witnessed the violence, aided Pierrette, and got medical care. As she recovered, her bond with Madame Lorrain deepened; the promise of protection and a future with her grandmother and Brigaut offered a hopeful glimmer. Pierrette resolved to reclaim her life.

At nine o’clock Monsieur Martener told Monsieur Tiphaine about Pierrette’s mistreatment by the Rogrons. Tiphaine brought in Auffray, a notary-relative; long-standing enmities between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions—fueled by damaging truths about Madame Tiphaine’s family—gave the dispute political force.

Tiphaine consulted the public prosecutor; with Auffray and Martener he arranged to petition the courts to make Auffray Pierrette’s guardian so a Family Council, backed by medical testimony, could remove her from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday the town buzzed: neighbors had heard Pierrette’s cries and rumors spread. Doctors Bianchon and Brigaut examined her and confirmed abuse. Vinet, the Rogrons’ lawyer, warned of criminal exposure; Sylvie, initially evasive, later admitted jealousy and the truth. To quiet the scandal Vinet proposed a marriage announcement; under pressure the Rogrons signed a marriage contract that served their political aims. Then Vinet filed a petition accusing Brigaut and the widow Lorrain of abducting Pierrette, trying to recast his clients as victims.

The hearing was postponed while Tiphaines rallied supporters; Pierrette, unaware of the turmoil, was cared for by her grandmother and Brigaut. When court reconvened, Auffray presented the physicians’ report and the judge ordered she be kept from the Rogrons until the Family Council met—a public rebuke. The Council removed Rogron as guardian and appointed Auffray; evidence prompted a civil suit for neglect and abuse.

As the legal battle continued, Pierrette’s health declined. Martener and the Auffray family grew fond of her; a critical operation in March 1828 offered little hope. She forgave her cousins and soon died. Her funeral filled the town; the Rogrons, fearing outrage, stayed away. Brigaut made her coffin. Community support hampered Rogron maneuvers and Vinet faced backlash, yet they largely retained political standing. The account closes on questions of justice, accountability, and Pierrette’s lasting impact.
