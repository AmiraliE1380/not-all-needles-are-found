DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

THE ILLUSTRIOUS GAUDISSART

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller, unknown to antiquity, is one of the striking figures of our age. He may mark the transition from an epoch of individual creative power to one of universal, levelling influence—equalizing products, spreading them among the masses, and itself governed by the principle of unity, the final expression of social organization. Do we not see the dead level of barbarism succeed the saturnalia of popular thought and the final struggles of civilizations that concentrated the world’s treasures in a single direction?

The commercial traveller is to ideas what our stage-coaches are to men and things: their vehicle. He takes from the luminous centre a handful of light and scatters it among the drowsy populations of duller regions. This human pyrotechnic is a scholar without learning, a juggler deceived by his own tricks, an unbelieving priest who expounds mysteries and dogmas all the better for his want of faith. Curious being! He has seen everything, known everything, and lays claim to all the ways of the world. Soaked in the vices of Paris, he affects provincial familiarity. He links village and capital—neither Parisian nor provincial, but a traveller. He sees nothing to the core: men and places he knows by name; things he surveys at the surface, measures with a little tape-line. His glance skims all and penetrates none. He keeps busy with much, yet nothing truly occupies him.

Jester and bon vivant, he courts every political opinion and is patriotic to the marrow. A capital mimic, he can turn on smiles of persuasion, satisfaction, and good-humour, and drop them to reveal his ordinary face. In his trade he must be a certain kind of observer: probing men with a glance to guess habits, wants, and above all solvency. To economize time he comes to quick decisions about his chances—practice that makes him more or less a man of judgment; on this basis he sets up as a critic of theatres, discoursing about Parisian and provincial stages alike.

He knows all the good and bad haunts in France “de actu et visu.” He can pilot you equally to vice or virtue. With an eloquence like a hot-water spigot, he can open or check a stream of phrases kept on tap, producing upon his victims a moral shower-bath. Loquacious as a cricket, he smokes, drinks, wears trinkets, overawes the common people, passes for a lord in villages, and never allows himself to be “stumped”—a bit of his own slang. He knows how to slap his pockets at the right moment and make his money jingle if he thinks suspicious servants might take him for a thief. Activity is among his most astonishing qualities. No hawk, stag, or hound can rival his rapid dart for a commission, his agility to trip up rivals and get ahead, or his keenness in scenting a customer and finding where his wares will sell.

What great qualities such a man must possess! In all countries one finds these low diplomats: consummate negotiators selling calico, jewels, frippery, wines, often showing truer diplomacy than ambassadors, who mostly know only its forms. No one in France doubts the powers of the commercial traveller: that intrepid soul who boldly brings the genius of civilization and Parisian inventions to reckon with the plain common sense of remote villages and their stolid routines. Who can forget the manoeuvres by which he worms himself into the minds of the populace, bringing a volume of words to bear upon the refractory, like the slow file eating into porphyry? Would you know the utmost force of language or the strongest pressure a phrase can exert against rebellious lucre, against the miserly proprietor in his country lair—listen to one of these great ambassadors of Parisian industry as he revolves, works, and sucks like an intelligent piston of the steam-engine called Speculation.

“Monsieur,” said a shrewd political economist, the director-cashier-manager and secretary-general of a celebrated fire-insurance company, “out of every five hundred thousand francs of policies to be renewed in the provinces, not more than fifty thousand are paid up voluntarily. The other four hundred and fifty thousand are got in by the activity of our agents, who go among those in arrears and worry them with stories of horrible incendiaries until they are driven to sign the new policies. Thus you see that eloquence, the labial flux, is nine tenths of the ways and means of our business.”

To talk and make people listen—that is seduction itself. A nation with two Chambers, a woman who lends both ears, are soon lost. Eve and her serpent are the everlasting myth of an hourly fact which began, and may end, with the world itself.

“A conversation of two hours ought to capture your man,” said a retired lawyer.

Let us walk round the commercial traveller and look at him well. Note his olive-green overcoat, his cloak with morocco collar, his striped blue cotton shirt. In this original figure—so distinct that it cannot be rubbed out—how many personalities we meet! What an acrobat, circus, battery all at once! Intrepid mariner, he plunges armed with a few phrases to catch five or six thousand francs in the frozen seas of France’s interior, among the red Indians of the countryside. The provincial fish will not rise to harpoons; it can only be taken with seines, nets, and gentle persuasions. The traveller’s business is to extract gold from country caches by a purely intellectual operation, pleasantly and without pain. Think of the flood of phrases which, renewed each dawn, leaps over the length and breadth of sunny France.

You know the species; now take a look at the individual.

There lives in Paris an incomparable commercial traveller, the paragon of his race, a man who possesses the qualifications necessary to his success in the highest degree. His speech is both vitriol and glue—glue to catch and entangle his victim, vitriol to dissolve hard heads, close fists, and close calculations. His line was once the hat; his skill at snaring the wariest provincial made him a celebrity, so that all vendors of “article Paris” sought him and begged him to take their commissions.

When he returned to Paris between triumphs in the provinces, he lived in perpetual festivity: weddings and suppers. In the smaller towns the correspondents made much of him; in Paris, the great houses feted him. Welcomed, flattered, and fed wherever he went, to breakfast or dine alone became novelty. He lived like a sovereign, or better, like a journalist; in fact he was the perambulating “feuilleton” of Parisian commerce.

His name was Gaudissart; his renown and the flatteries heaped on him won him the surname Illustrious. Wherever he went—behind a counter or before a bar, into a salon or to the top of a stage-coach, up to a garret or dining with a banker—everyone said, on seeing him, “Ah! here comes the illustrious Gaudissart!” No name suited him better. All things smiled upon our traveller, and he smiled back. “Similia similibus”—he believed in homoeopathy. Puns, horse-laugh, monkish face, friar’s skin, true Rabelaisian exterior—clothing, body, mind, and features—combined to give devil-may-care jollity to every inch of him. Free-handed and easy-going, he might be recognized at once as the grisettes’ favourite: he jumped lightly to the top of stage-coaches, helped timid ladies down, joked with postillions and sold them caps, smiled at maids and caught them by the waist or heart; gurgled at dinner like a bottle of wine, pretended to draw a cork by a brisk blow on his cheek, played tunes with his knife on champagne glasses without breaking them, dared the company to copy him; chaffed the timid traveller, contradicted the knowing one, lorded it at table and managed to get the titbits. Strong as he was, he could throw aside nonsense and mean business when he flung away a cigar stump and said, with a glance at some town, “I’ll go and see what those people have got in their stomachs.”

Buckled down to work he became the slyest and cleverest diplomat. All things to all men, he could accost a banker like a capitalist, a magistrate like a functionary, a royalist with pious sentiments, a bourgeois as one of themselves. Where he went he was what he ought to be; he left Gaudissart at the door when he entered, and picked him up when he came out.

Until 1830 the illustrious Gaudissart was faithful to the article Paris. Close contact with humanity’s caprices taught him the windings of the human heart. He learned persuasive eloquence, the knack of loosening the tightest purse-strings, the art of rousing desire in husbands, wives, children, and servants; and how to satisfy it. No one better inveigled a merchant by the charms of a bargain and vanished at the crisis of desire. Grateful to the hat trade, he always declared that capping heads had enabled him to understand interiors; he had “capped and crowned” so many people that he felt at liberty to fling himself at their heads. His jokes about hats and heads were irrepressible, if not dazzling.

Yet after August and October 1830 he abandoned the hat trade and the article Paris, tearing himself from visible wares to mount into the higher spheres of Parisian speculation. “He forsook,” in his words, “matter for mind; manufactured products for the infinitely purer elaborations of human intelligence.” This needs explanation.

The general upheaval of 1830 brought forth many old ideas recomposed by clever speculators. After 1830 ideas became property. A writer, too wise to publish, once said, “more ideas are stolen than pocket-handkerchiefs.” Perhaps we may one day have an Exchange for thought; even now ideas, good or bad, have their consols, are bought, imported, exported, sold, and quoted like stocks. If ideas are not on hand, speculators pass off words in their stead and live on them as a bird lives on millet seeds. Do not laugh: a word is worth as much as an idea in a land where the ticket on a sack is more important than its contents. Have we not seen libraries working off the word “picturesque” when literature would have slain the word “fantastic”? Fiscal genius has guessed the proper tax on intellect; it has estimated the profits of advertising and registered a prospectus of thought’s quantity and worth, weighing intellectual property at the Stamp Office in the Rue de la Paix.

Having become an article of commerce, intellect and its products must obey the laws that bind other manufactured interests. Thus ideas conceived by idle Parisians—who nevertheless fight many a moral battle over champagne and pheasants—are handed at birth to commercial travellers employed to spread them “urbi et orbi,” seasoned with advertisement and prospectus, by which they catch in their rat-trap the departmental rodent called subscriber, sometimes stockholder, occasionally corresponding member or patron, but invariably fool.

“I am a fool!” many a poor country proprietor has said when, caught by the prospect of being the first to launch a new idea, he finds that he has launched his thousand or twelve hundred francs into a gulf.

“Subscribers are fools who never can be brought to understand that to go ahead in the intellectual world they must start with more money than they need for the tour of Europe,” say the speculators.

Consequently there is endless warfare between the recalcitrant public, which refuses to pay Parisian imposts, and the tax-gatherer who, living by his receipt of custom, lards the public with new ideas, turns it on the spit of lively projects, roasts it with prospectuses, basting with flattery, and finally gobbles it up with some toothsome sauce. Moreover, since 1830 what honors and emoluments have been scattered throughout France to stimulate the zeal and self-love of the “progressive and intelligent masses”! Titles, medals, diplomas, a sort of legion of honor for martyrs, have followed each other rapidly. Speculators in the manufactured products of the intellect have developed a spice, a ginger, all their own. From this have come premiums, forestalled dividends, and that conscription of noted names levied without the knowledge of the unfortunate writers who bear them, who thus find themselves associated with more enterprises than there are days in the year; the law takes no account of the theft of a patronymic. Worse is the rape of ideas which these caterers for the public mind, like slave-merchants, tear from the parental brain before they are matured, and drag half-clothed before the eyes of their sultan—the terrible public—which, if they don’t amuse it, will cut off their heads by curtailing the ingots and emptying their pockets.

This madness of our epoch reacted upon the illustrious Gaudissart, and here follows how it happened. A life-insurance company, having been told of his irresistible eloquence, offered him an unheard-of commission, which he accepted. The bargain concluded, our traveller was put in training by the secretary-general, who schooled him in the business: its dark holes, its dialect, the particular public to be gullible, impromptu replies, and unanswerable arguments—sharpening the file of the tongue which was about to operate upon the life of France.

The puppet amply rewarded the pains bestowed upon him. The heads of the company boasted of the illustrious Gaudissart so loudly that the financial managers of two then-prominent newspapers—since defunct—were seized with the idea of employing him to get subscribers. The proprietors of the “Globe,” an organ of Saint-Simonism, and the “Movement,” a republican journal, each invited him to a conference and proposed paying ten francs a head for every subscriber if he brought in a thousand, but only five francs if he got five hundred. The cause of political journalism did not interfere with his pre-accepted life-insurance work; the bargain was struck. Gaudissart even demanded indemnity from the Saint-Simonians for eight days spent studying their doctrines, asserting a prodigious effort of memory and intellect was necessary to get to the bottom of that “article,” though he asked nothing from the republicans. He inclined to republican ideas and had already dipped into conspiracies; he had been arrested and released for want of proof; and, as he told the newspaper proprietors, he had lately grown a mustache and needed only a hat of certain shape and a pair of spurs to represent the Republic suitably.

CHAPTER II

For a week this commanding genius went every morning to be Saint-Simonized at the “Globe,” and every afternoon to the life-insurance company, where he learned financial diplomacy. His aptitude and memory were prodigious; by April 15, the usual opening of the spring campaign, he could set out. Two large commercial houses, alarmed at declining business, begged him not to desert the article Paris and seduced him with large offers. The king of travellers yielded; enormous premiums and old friendships swayed him.

“Listen, my little Jenny,” he said in a hackney-coach to a pretty florist.

All truly great men delight in allowing themselves to be tyrannized over by a feeble being, and Gaudissart had found his tyrant in Jenny. He was bringing her home at eleven from the Gymnase, where he had taken her in full dress to a first-tier box.

“On my return, Jenny, I shall refurnish your room in superior style. That big Matilda, who pesters you with comparisons and her India shawls, won’t have a word to say then. I consecrate to the adornment of your room all the ‘Children’ I shall get in the provinces.”

“That’s a pretty thing to say! Monster of a man! Do you dare to talk to me of your children? Do you suppose I am going to stand that sort of thing?”

“Oh, what a goose you are, my Jenny! That’s only a figure of speech in our business.”

“A fine business, then!”

“Well, but listen; if you talk all the time you’ll always be in the right.”

“I mean to be. You take things easy!”

“You don’t let me finish. I have taken under my protection a superlative idea—a newspaper written for children. In our profession, when travellers have caught, say, ten subscribers to the ‘Children’s Journal,’ they say, ‘I’ve got ten Children,’ just as I say when I get ten subscriptions to the ‘Movement,’ ‘I’ve got ten Movements.’ Now don’t you see?”

“That’s all right. Are you going into politics? If you do you’ll get into Saint-Pelagie, and I shall have to trot down there after you. Oh! if one only knew what one puts one’s foot into when we love a man, by my word of honor we would let you alone to take care of yourselves, you men! However, if you are going away to-morrow we won’t talk of disagreeable things—that would be silly.”

The coach stopped before a pretty fourth-story house in the Rue d’Artois, the abode of Mademoiselle Jenny Courand, reputed privately married to the illustrious Gaudissart, a rumor he did not deny. To maintain her supremacy, Jenny required innumerable attentions and threatened to dismiss him for the slightest omission. She ordered him to write from every town and render a minute account of his proceedings.

“How many ‘Children’ will it take to furnish my chamber?” she asked, throwing off her shawl by a good fire.

“I get five sous for each subscriber.”

“Delightful! And is it with five sous that you expect to make me rich? Perhaps you are like the Wandering Jew with your pockets full of money.”

“But, Jenny, I shall get a thousand ‘Children.’ Think that children have never had a newspaper to themselves before. But what a fool I am to try to explain matters to you—you can’t understand such things.”

“Can’t I? Then tell me—tell me, Gaudissart, if I’m such a goose why do you love me?”

“Just because you are a goose—a sublime goose! Listen, Jenny. I am going to undertake the ‘Globe,’ the ‘Movement,’ the ‘Children,’ the insurance business, and some of my old article Paris; instead of earning a miserable eight thousand a year, I’ll bring back twenty thousand at least from each trip.”

“Unlace me, Gaudissart, and do it right; don’t tighten me.”

“Yes, truly,” said the traveller complacently. “I shall become a shareholder in the newspapers, like Finot, one of my friends, the son of a hatter, who now has thirty thousand francs income, and is going to make himself a peer of France. When one thinks of that little Popinot—ah, mon Dieu! I forgot to tell you that Monsieur Popinot was named minister of commerce yesterday. Why shouldn’t I be ambitious too? Ha! ha! I could easily pick up the jargon of those fellows who talk in the chamber, and bluster with the rest of them. Now, listen to me:—

“Gentlemen,” he said, standing behind a chair, “the Press is neither a tool nor an article of barter: it is, under its political aspects, an institution. We are bound, as legislators, to consider all things politically, and therefore we must examine the Press and ask if it is useful or noxious, if it should be encouraged or taxed or free. These are serious questions. I feel that I do not waste the time of this Chamber by examining this article—the Press—and explaining to you its qualities. We are on the verge of an abyss. Undoubtedly the laws have not the nap they ought to have—Hein?” he said, looking at Jenny. “All orators put France on the verge of an abyss. Don’t I know their dodges? I’m up to all the tricks of all the trades. Do you know why? Because I was born with a caul; my mother has got it, but I’ll give it to you. You’ll see! I shall soon be in the government.”

“You!”

“Why shouldn’t I be the Baron Gaudissart, peer of France? Haven’t they twice elected Monsieur Popinot as deputy? He dines with Louis-Philippe. There’s Finot; he is going to be, they say, a member of the Council. Suppose they send me as ambassador to London? I tell you I’d nonplus those English! No man ever got the better of Gaudissart, and nobody ever will. Yes, I say it! No one ever outwitted me, and no one can—in any walk of life, politics or impolitics. But, for the time being, I must give myself wholly to the capitalists; to the ‘Globe,’ the ‘Movement,’ the ‘Children,’ and my article Paris.”

“You will be brought up with a round turn, you and your newspapers. I’ll bet you won’t get further than Poitiers before the police will nab you.”

“What will you bet?”

“A shawl.”

“Done! If I lose that shawl I’ll go back to the article Paris and the hat business. But as for getting the better of Gaudissart—never! never!”

And the illustrious traveller threw himself into a Napoleonic attitude. Gaudissart was thirty-eight, of medium height, stout and fat like men who roll about in stage-coaches, with a round face, ruddy cheeks, and regular features of the type sculptors adopt as models for Abundance, Law, Force, Commerce. His stomach swelled in a pear shape; he caught Jenny up and kissed her.

“Hold your tongue, young woman!” he said. “What do you know about Saint-Simonism, Fourierism, heroic enterprise, or woman’s freedom? I’ll tell you what they are—ten francs for each subscription, Madame Gaudissart.”

“On my word of honor, you are going crazy, Gaudissart.”

“More and more crazy about you,” he replied, flinging his hat upon the sofa.

The next morning, after a glorious breakfast with Jenny, Gaudissart departed on horseback to work the chief towns of his district, assigned by his various enterprises. After forty-five days beating the country between Paris and Blois, he remained two weeks at Blois to write correspondence and make short visits. The night before leaving Blois for Tours he indited a letter to Mademoiselle Jenny Courand which proves their tie and his triumphs: he boasted of lodging two millions between Paris and Blois, selling many Ternaux shawls at Orléans, lamented that the “Movement” did not move the republicans, recounted making a hundred “Globes,” and rejoiced at getting two thousand “Children” subscriptions. He described cajoling a dynastic diner into subscribing to the “Children’s Journal” rather than quarrel, and promised to do Amboise in two days and “topple” Tours.

CHAPTER III

Five days later Gaudissart started from the Hotel des Faisans in Tours and went to Vouvray, a rich district where the public mind seemed susceptible. Trotting along the embankment, he admired the landscape, little dreaming his commercial infallibility was about to perish in Vouvray.

A note on the Tourangian mind: stamped by Rabelais’ spirit, Touraine’s people are polished, witty, poetic, voluptuous, yet their impulses subside quickly. The softness of the atmosphere, ease of life, and jovial manners smother sentiment of art and enervate will. At home the Tourangian sits like an Indian on his mat or a Turk on his divan, employing wit in laughing at neighbors and making merry all his days. Touraine is like Rabelais’ Abbaye of Theleme—good cheer reigns.

The illustrious Gaudissart encountered in Vouvray one of those indigenous jesters whose wit is perfection. The Tourangians especially hated and villified Saint-Simonian doctrines, their hatred taking the form of superb disdain and witty maliciousness. For his sins, after arriving and consulting the landlord Mitouflet, Gaudissart betook himself to the town’s merry-maker, a former dyer named Monsieur Vernier, influential among the small proprietors, a leader of gossip and squabbles.

“Monsieur Vernier,” Gaudissart began, “I come to solicit your aid. I am sent into the provinces on an enterprise of the utmost importance, undertaken by bankers who—”

“Who mean to win our tricks,” said Vernier, used to travellers.

“Precisely,” replied Gaudissart. “But with your fine tact, Monsieur, you must be aware we can’t win tricks unless it is their interest to play at cards. I am no longer a common traveller. The most distinguished bankers in Paris take part in this affair; the most respectable houses are concerned, and their interests guarantee—” and Gaudissart drew forth his string of phrases.

Vernier let him go the length of his tether, listening with apparent interest which completely deceived him. But after “guarantee” Vernier ceased attention and plotted to play him a malicious trick: to fetch the town’s supposed banker—Margaritis, a half-insane man—and have Gaudissart deal with him.

At the head of the Valley Coquette lived Margaritis, of Italian origin, half-insane, married, childless, cared for by a devoted wife. He carried two long-bladed knives, sometimes threatened his wife, and insisted on keeping a sack of flour and two puncheons of wine in the cellar. He made incessant demands for newspapers and, for seven years, read the same old “Indre-et-Loire” without perceiving the repetition. When company came he often sat silent, then at ten o’clock on a certain clock he would rise with mechanical precision and say sternly, “Go away!” He had lucid intervals when he advised on wine sales; at times he became annoying, ransacking closets and stealing delicacies. Once in five years his being told “the good-man does not hear anything” drove him to frenzy, knife flourishing.

The evening before Gaudissart reached Vouvray, Madame Margaritis had found it hard to deceive her husband, who insisted on watching his puncheons; she had begged neighbor Pierre Champlain to roll two puncheons into their cellar to quiet him. Vernier told his wife he would take Benoîte Fontanieu as witness to the scene, to spread the joke.

Vernier escorted Gaudissart, who believed he had made a conquest of both ladies with wit and humor, to La Fuye, the modest house of Margaritis. The salon opened to the field; Margaritis, seated in a yellow Utrecht-velvet arm-chair, did not stir. Madame Vernier introduced the traveller and bade him “put away your mathematics and talk.” Margaritis rose and invited Gaudissart to sit.

“Monsieur has doubtless been in business?” Gaudissart began.

“Public business,” answered Margaritis. “I pacificated Calabria under Murat.”

“In that case we shall quickly understand each other,” said Gaudissart.

Margaritis’s peculiarities—pear-shaped bald head, sunken eyes, thin nose, long chin—gave him a look between a retired professor and a rag-picker. Gaudissart, turning his watch-key with a rotatory click that kept the lunatic quiet, launched into his patter about life insurance. Time, he said, is the most precious social wealth; to economize time is to become wealthy. The bankers and capitalists who had seen the waste of rising men's time had “capitalized” future prospects, cashing talents by discounting time, representing in money the products developed by time—the moral qualities and talents as living forces. He explained with literal examples: suppose a painter has a glorious future; the company will recognize and insure his intellectual capital, paying his family in case of death; the painter pays a small annual premium, three percent, to secure his family and thus free himself of anxieties distracting him from success. Insurance, Gaudissart called it, is the exchequer of beneficence, a mutual insurance against poverty, the cashing of talent.

“That is usury!” cried Margaritis.

Gaudissart switched tack and offered wine; Margaritis poured Vouvray of 1819 and claimed it as Head of Vouvray, a wine for kings. The lunatic delighted in pointing out the etymology: capital, caput—head—the Head of Vouvray. He even offered to sell his puncheons.

Gaudissart returned to his argument: the insured man, once recognized and holding company paper as security, could borrow on it at the Bourse and among bankers; civilization denies bread to the men who give it luxury, he declared, and insurance bridges the gulf. The company also offered annuities on a sliding scale superior to tontines. Margaritis urged another glass; Gaudissart praised the wine.

When Gaudissart turned to the “Globe” and Saint-Simonian doctrine, Margaritis fell into position and professed comprehension, though answering with local touches: “Twice; on foot.” Gaudissart praised the paper as full of life, science, and as something to make time pass agreeably in the country. It cost eighty francs; Margaritis would not pay but offered seven francs for the “Children’s Journal” and consented to push the “Globe”’s interests if Gaudissart took his wine, bargaining humorously over the price. The two men strolled among the vines; the three ladies and Vernier watched, laughing at the spectacle of the urbane traveller and the lunatic discussing, gesticulating, and walking with vehemence.

Finally Gaudissart sat at a card-table and wrote an order for delivery of the two casks. Margaritis read it, counted out seven francs for the “Children’s Journal,” and gave them to the traveller. Gaudissart, a Norman who required reciprocal agreements, demanded Margaritis sign a bond to deliver the two puncheons. Margaritis did. Gaudissart left humming triumphantly.

CHAPTER V

At the Soleil d’Or Gaudissart conversed with landlord Mitouflet, an old soldier, about Margaritis. The host, who never laughed at a jest but took it with a soldier’s gravity, hinted that Margaritis was a lunatic.

“May be; the good-man knows a deal. He knows too much for other folks,” said Mitouflet.

Gaudissart, enraged when Vernier’s prank was revealed, marched to the dyer’s salon where Vernier was laughing with friends. “Monsieur,” he began, “you are a scoundrel and a blackguard; under pain of being thought a turn-key—you will give me satisfaction for the insult you dared to offer me in sending me to a lunatic! Do you hear me, Monsieur Vernier, dyer?”

Vernier, delighted at the audience, replied that it was right to make fun of a man who wanted them to feather his nest by subscribing to a paper preaching a new religion that denied inheritance. “Père Margaritis said things a great deal more sensible,” he added.

Gaudissart flew at Vernier to slap him but the crowd interposed; his wig fell on Mademoiselle Clara Vernier’s head. He declared he would be at the Soleil d’Or until morning, ready to show what giving satisfaction meant; he had “fought in July.” Vernier answered he would “fight in Vouvray,” and make the traveller stay longer than he imagined.

For the first time the prince of travellers did not dine jovially. The town was in a ferment over the duel. Madame Fontanieu and the assistant-mayor implored Mitouflet to arrange things to avoid a violent death. He took it upon himself. In the evening he brought pens and paper to Gaudissart.

“If you are going to fight to-morrow,” Mitouflet said, “you had better make some settlement of your affairs; perhaps you have letters to write. Are you a good swordsman? I have foils.” They put on foils and masks; Mitouflet’s experience won him sixty-two passes. He advised pistols, suggesting they be loaded to fire wide so both could retire with honor. Gaudissart grumbled about possibly killing the man; Mitouflet calmed him.

Next morning the adversaries met by the bridge of La Cise. Vernier nearly shot a cow; they embraced after firing in the air. “Your joke was rough, but it was a good one,” said Gaudissart; Vernier announced twenty subscriptions to the “Children’s Journal.” “Why shouldn’t we all breakfast together?” said Gaudissart. Men who fight are often those who come to good understanding.

Back at the inn Gaudissart wanted to send a summons to Margaritis for delivery of the two casks. “But he has not got them,” said Vernier. Gaudissart demanded indemnity and considered himself outwitted if he failed to pursue the claim. Madame Margaritis, alarmed, brought thirty francs to the traveller; Gaudissart accepted it and left Vouvray content with the settlement, though recognizing the region’s resistance to new ideas.

On returning from his trip through the southern departments, Gaudissart rode in a diligence and explained the enigmas of life to a young man as if to an infant. Passing Vouvray the young man exclaimed at the fine site; Gaudissart, patronizing, replied, “Yes, Monsieur, but not habitable on account of the people. You get into duels every day. Why, it is not three months since I fought one just there,” pointing to the bridge, “with a damned dyer; but I made an end of him—he bit the dust!”
