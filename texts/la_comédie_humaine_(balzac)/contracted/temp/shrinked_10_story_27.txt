At one in the morning Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac left a house discussing the Marquise d’Espard. Bianchon distrusted her; Rastignac, dazzled by her beauty and rank, believed she could elevate his standing and settle his debts. Bianchon warned it was a mistake, likening her to a “one-eyed horse,” dismissing fashionable women as insincere facades. Rastignac brushed this off, insisting a woman of fashion could be an ally. Bianchon agreed to introduce him to his uncle, Judge Jean-Jules Popinot, though he doubted the stern judge would help. Next morning Bianchon visited Popinot’s house in a decayed quarter—a faded building. Popinot, unkempt and unambitious, was a supernumerary judge: devoted to justice, able to elicit confessions without force, yet frustrated by the system. Bianchon reflected how hard it would be to persuade such an uncompromising man.

Twelve years earlier, during the 1816 famine, Popinot was named President of the Commission Extraordinary to distribute food in his neighborhood. A prominent lawyer and criminal judge, he had long recorded legal outcomes without probing causes; direct contact with the destitute awoke a deep compassion that made him a protector of the poor, likened to Saint Vincent de Paul.

The change was incremental: one act of charity led to another until Popinot became central to local benevolence—preventing crime, finding work, and offering discreet assistance while living largely unknown to Parisians. He converted a storeroom into a modest parlor with benches and chairs, kept meticulous records of donations and requests, and relied on his aide Lavienne to manage pawn tickets and visit the worst districts. The parlor filled from dawn with women, children, and the needy; its occupants, even criminals, respected the household. Popinot spent mornings tending the poor, afternoons in legal practice, and evenings on judicial duties, which sharpened his sense for hidden virtues and wrongdoing. His modest inheritance, his late wife’s savings, and a restrained judge’s salary forced frugality, especially given his charity; despite his service he lacked the Legion of Honor.

His routine was disrupted when the President of the Second Chamber, at the Marquise d’Espard’s request, sent Popinot to examine the Marquis d’Espard for a Lunacy Commission. Bianchon visited Popinot’s Rue du Fouarre parlor—Popinot in a threadbare nightcap, ledger in hand, Lavienne organizing petitions—and watched him interrupt an urgent matter to help a woman sustain her grocery while her husband was jailed for debt. By midmorning he had aided over two hundred people and the room was emptied. His home—dust, scattered papers, his late wife’s belongings, tokens of gratitude—revealed a divided life. Bianchon urged him to accept the Marquise’s hospitality; though magistrates normally avoid such ties, Popinot agreed, considering the Marquis’s mental state and its family implications.

At nine the next evening Bianchon climbed his uncle’s dusty staircase and found him bent over a judgment. When Lavienne's tailor failed to send the coat, Popinot wore an old stained one; Bianchon straightened his cravat, but the judge’s movements revealed a gap between waistcoat and trousers, an absurdity he noticed as his uncle entered the Marquise d’Espard’s room.

A brief account frames the interview. For seven years Madame d’Espard was a Parisian fashion‑figure. After separating from her husband in 1815, the thirty‑three‑year‑old Marquise, mother of two boys (15 and 13), kept her vogue with elaborate hair, cold baths, strict diet; emotionally detached, she admitted neither love nor hate and gladly parted from M. d’Espard when he took the children, whom she saw as obstacles.

With 26,000 francs she lived quietly until 1820, when she re‑emerged, hosting gatherings and supplanting other prominent women. She cultivated ties with duchesses and countesses, turning her salon into a discreet political hub. Admired by ambitious men, she used intimacy to influence, avoiding compromise.

Rastignac sought an alliance; Popinot’s visit confirmed household opulence. He probed the Marquise about her separation; her crafted story and extravagant living clashed with claims of hardship, suggesting self‑serving motives. Warned that the court would scrutinize her, she tried to deflect. Afterwards Bianchon and Rastignac feared her influence; Popinot resolved to investigate and summoned Madame Jeanrenaud.

Next day Madame Jeanrenaud arrived—stout, unrefined, candid about M. d’Espard’s situation. Her straightforwardness intrigued Popinot. Tracing D’Espard’s finances, he found the case more complex and in need of careful examination.

Those who have matured know how trivial incidents can shape major events: Popinot’s minor illness delayed a crucial meeting when the judge, feeling unwell, stayed home instead of visiting the Marquis d’Espard, losing a day that echoed larger historic delays.

Before following the lawyer and his clerk to the Marquis’s residence, consider the father’s home life, which his wife’s petition branded as mad. In old Paris some buildings still show permanence. The house on Rue de la Montagne-Sainte-Genevieve where M. d’Espard lived was such a stone structure: once grand, now weathered and partitioned, partly given over to a printing establishment. He occupied the ground floor—likely chosen for his children because the garden was unusually large—and paid modest rent in a depressed market. Dating from Henry III–Louis XIII, the house still bore traces of former glory, including a perron and the garden.

The Marquis furnished his rooms in rich browns, creating a quiet, harmonious atmosphere. Few visitors were admitted; the family life was secluded. The household comprised the Marquis, his two sons, a long-serving cook and a man-servant whose discipline kept order. To shield his children he avoided tenants, inviting gossip: neighbors called him mad and his servants untrustworthy, and his frugality and cash payments fueled rumors of ruin. Noble by birth, the family’s dignity clashed with egalitarian neighbors. The Marquis, about fifty, had a distinguished, somewhat cold air—eccentric in appearance and frugal in dress. His sons, Comte Clément de Négrepelisse and Vicomte Camille d’Espard, were handsome; the elder, nearly sixteen, favored fashion. The Marquis concentrated on broad education—mathematics, Eastern languages, and history—eschewing what he deemed unproductive studies.

The day Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard fell on a Thursday holiday. Before their father rose, the two boys were already in the garden; the younger begged to visit the shooting-gallery and pestered Clément, who yielded. Their noisy roughplay, encouraged by the Viscount, roused the Marquis’ laughter and drew neighbors’ jests that he seemed mad to let his children fight. When the boys were called, Clément hesitated but obtained permission from Camille.

Through the house people whispered of the Marquis’ supposed madness. When Popinot arrived near noon with his clerk, the portress led him to the third floor and reported that the Marquis had made his children fight that morning and had laughed when the younger had bitten the elder until he bled; she added that the Marquis rarely showed himself. The muddy landing and grimy banisters lent the gossip the appearance of truth and made Popinot, though impartial, receptive.

They entered an outer office where an old clerk sat amid shelves of publications; a grating separated a private room and a cash-desk revealed the enterprise’s commercial side. A shopman opened the inner room to show the Marquis: a venerable, simply dressed old man wearing the Cross of Saint-Louis, seated at a desk. Popinot explained he was a judge on the Inferior Court of Appeal for the Department of the Seine, charged with examining M. d’Espard in response to a petition by the Marquise for a Commission in Lunacy. The Marquis reddened and trembled, then steadied himself.

Surprised to have had no notice, the Marquis listened as Popinot explained that the law presumes those subject to such a commission may lack reason and that the Court must verify the petitioner’s claims. Popinot urged him to answer fully and discreetly, especially about delicate accusations involving Madame Jeanrenaud—widow of a bargemaster—or her son Colonel Jeanrenaud, for whom the Marquis was accused of having dissipated his fortune and used influence at Court. The Marquis feared that explaining would wound his family’s honor, but Popinot promised confidentiality and courtesy if a misunderstanding were found. Freed from reserve, the Marquis spoke with clarity and passion.

He began with his marriage, which had brought heavy expenses forcing him to borrow. He could not afford a steward; most title-deeds had been taken to Paris by his father, who feared scrutiny. The family name was Negrepelisse; the title d’Espard came through marriage in Henri IV’s time. An ancestor had lost the town of Negrepelisse in the religious wars and been ruined by property destruction; later relatives regained favor and fortune but a stain of blood and disgrace remained. The Marquis had long been determined to erase that stain.

He discovered his grandfather had obtained the Negrepelisse lands by means tied to the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes: a Protestant merchant named Jeanrenaud was arrested and hanged, and the land passed to his ancestor by iniquitous law. The merchant’s family had the rightful claim. Unable to endure the disgrace, the Marquis sought the heirs. He eventually found M. Jeanrenaud living in poverty, reduced to a lieutenant under Napoleon. Convinced their rights were incontestable and they lacked the means to sue, he offered to pay the lands’ value at the time they were taken—eleven hundred thousand francs—payable at his convenience and without interest.

To effect restitution he lived frugally for years. When he suggested to Madame d’Espard that they move to the country and live on half her income, she treated him as mad; her coldness convinced him she would have condoned his ancestors’ crimes. Appalled, he resolved to leave her in control of her fortune once debts were settled and to take charge of educating their children himself, determined to raise them as gentlemen. He invested prudently, repaid debts sooner than expected, and had recently completed the eleven hundred thousand francs payment, making restitution without damaging his children’s prospects. These were the reasons for the payments to Madame Jeanrenaud and her son.

When Popinot asked if Madame d’Espard knew his motives, the Marquis answered yes. Popinot, moved, excused his clerk and inquired about other allegations—particularly that the Marquis was engaged in a business unbecoming his rank. The Marquis summoned the Comte de Nouvion, explaining that he used the rooms as offices for publishing the Picturesque History of China by a reputable firm, not as a private residence. He showed Popinot his ground-floor apartment: a simple, touching harmony that convinced the judge a madman would hardly create such beauty. The pleasant weather and garden further belied lunacy.

The Marquis outlined his plan: remain until his younger son finished studies, then travel through Europe with the children to perfect their languages and manners. His China project, with Comte de Nouvion as partner and the Abbé Grozier—Keeper of the Books at the Arsenal—as tutor, would be an honest literary enterprise. They had two thousand five hundred subscribers—twelve hundred in France—the work would cost about three hundred francs per set, and he hoped the venture would yield a hundred thousand francs to fund his children’s education and pleasures; the Comte might earn six to seven thousand francs a year. Twelve years of retirement had cost him his standing at Court, but he believed he had purified his family’s past and secured a noble future for his children.

At that moment the two boys burst in, robust and sunburnt from riding. Their greeting and obvious devotion confirmed Popinot’s impression of a devoted father. When Clément admitted that their mother avoided speaking to them publicly, a cloud passed over the Marquis’ brow. Popinot felt deep sympathy: if anyone was mad, he thought, it could only be the children who were a little crazy about their father, and the father who was very crazy about his children—but that kind of madness rang true.

Madame Jeanrenaud then appeared, bypassing the manservant’s protests. Firmly and courteously bowing, she declared she had come too late because the criminal judge was there; she and her son were of one mind to return everything—their honor demanded it. “They must be as stupid as pans without handles to call you a lunatic,” she added. The boys clung to their father, alarmed.

Popinot intervened: legally the payments were due but had been prompted by the Marquis’ scrupulous honesty; the law did not require descendants to repay confiscations centuries old and a Commission in Lunacy is for those who act without reason. Here the remittances sprang from the highest motives; she could keep the money without remorse and let the world misinterpret a noble action. Popinot declared he would rather see the Marquis crowned than subjected to a Commission. Madame Jeanrenaud applauded, and the Marquis and judge exchanged a humane, private understanding. Popinot advised the Marquis to counteract any influence his wife might be under and then left, impressed by the scene.

The next morning Popinot, after writing his report, went to the Palais de Justice. As he prepared to don his gown, the President called him privately and, invoking propriety—“Caesar’s wife must not be suspected”—asked Popinot to step aside from the case as a personal favor, citing the Keeper of the Seals’ suggestion to avoid any appearance of conflict. In recompense, the President would secure the Cross of the Legion of Honor for him. M. Camusot, a newly appointed judge, was named in Popinot’s place. Popinot, ironic but acquiescent, bowed and withdrew.
