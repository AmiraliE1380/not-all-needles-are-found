**The Commission in Lunacy**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Clara Bell*  

**Dedication**  
Dedicated to Monsieur le Contre-Amiral Bazoche, Governor of the Isle of Bourbon, by the grateful writer.  

---

In 1828, around one o’clock in the morning, Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac left a house on Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré and decided to walk to the boulevard, as neither had a carriage. Their conversation turned to the Marquise d’Espard, whom Rastignac admired. Bianchon cautioned him against pursuing her, likening her to a “one-eyed horse” and suggesting she was not as youthful as Rastignac believed.

Rastignac argued that the Marquise’s social status and wealth made her an attractive prospect, especially since he was struggling financially after marrying off his sisters. Bianchon, a physician, expressed disdain for fashionable women, asserting they often lacked genuine character and were driven by selfishness. He warned Rastignac that the Marquise might be in debt despite her charm.

Bianchon recounted his experiences with high society, describing its superficiality and moral decay. He believed a woman of true virtue would not be fashionable, as the qualities that elevate women in society often stem from vices. Rastignac countered that a fashionable woman could provide significant advantages for an ambitious man, likening her to a tool for success.

Despite Bianchon’s warnings, Rastignac remained enamored with the idea of marrying the Marquise, valuing her social connections over the virtues of a more devoted partner. Bianchon reluctantly agreed to help Rastignac by introducing him to his uncle, Judge Jean-Jules Popinot, known for his strict adherence to the law.

The next morning, Bianchon set out to visit Popinot, who lived in a dilapidated area of Paris. The judge was characterized by his unkempt appearance and lack of ambition, having been overlooked for promotions despite his legal acumen. His life was marked by a commitment to justice, often at the expense of his own comfort and reputation.

Popinot was a diligent and compassionate judge, respected for his knowledge of criminal law but remained a supernumerary judge due to his modest nature and lack of political maneuvering. His life exemplified the struggles of a good man in a flawed system, where ambition often overshadowed merit.

As Bianchon approached Popinot’s residence, he reflected on the challenges of persuading the judge to assist Rastignac. Despite the difficulties, he was determined to fulfill his friend’s request, aware of the complexities involved in navigating high society and the legal system.

In summary, the narrative explores themes of ambition, social status, and the moral dilemmas faced by individuals in pursuit of success. Bianchon’s skepticism about the Marquise serves as a counterpoint to Rastignac’s aspirations, highlighting the tension between genuine character and societal expectations. The story critiques the superficiality of fashionable society while portraying the struggles of a principled man within the judicial system.

Twelve years before this story, in 1816, Popinot was appointed President of the Commission Extraordinary to distribute food during a severe scarcity. Initially reluctant to stay in the Rue du Fouarre, he became deeply committed to alleviating local suffering. Over five years, he evolved from a judge focused on legal outcomes to a compassionate figure, akin to Saint Vincent de Paul, dedicated to helping the poor. His charitable work drained his resources, but he found fulfillment as a protector and benefactor.

By the end of his second year, Popinot had converted his storeroom into a parlor for the needy, meticulously recording donations and requests for assistance. His aide, Lavienne, helped manage the influx of desperate individuals seeking aid, filling the room from dawn until late morning. Despite the stifling atmosphere, the poor revered Popinot, who tirelessly provided food, shelter, and employment.

With a modest inherited fortune and his late wife's wealth, Popinot lived frugally while dedicating himself to charity. Known for his absent-mindedness regarding personal appearance, a trait often linked to intellectual pursuits, he remained unrecognized by his peers, having not received the Legion of Honor.

The narrative shifts to a case involving the Marquis d’Espard, whose wife sought a Commission in Lunacy for him. Bianchon, a doctor and Popinot's nephew, visits the Rue du Fouarre, where he finds his uncle amidst a crowd of impoverished individuals. The scene vividly depicts various characters representing the struggles of the poor, each with their own stories of hardship.

Popinot's disheveled attire reflects his preoccupation with charitable work. He engages with petitioners, providing assistance while Lavienne manages the flow of people. Bianchon expresses concern about the Marquis d’Espard, and Popinot reads the petition detailing the Marquis's alleged mental decline and the influence of a woman named Jeanrenaud.

The petition outlines the Marquis's erratic behavior, financial mismanagement, and obsession with China, suggesting he may be under undue influence. Popinot and Bianchon discuss the implications, with Bianchon suggesting the influence could resemble possession. While skeptical, Popinot acknowledges the need for further investigation.

As their conversation unfolds, Popinot reflects on the nature of the allegations and potential motivations behind them. He considers that the Marquise may have ulterior motives in seeking the Commission in Lunacy for her husband. Ultimately, Bianchon persuades Popinot to visit Madame d’Espard to hear her side of the story, emphasizing the importance of understanding both perspectives.

Popinot agrees to the visit, recognizing the necessity of gathering all relevant information before making a judgment. The chapter concludes with anticipation for the upcoming meeting, highlighting the complexities of human relationships and the challenges of navigating the legal and moral implications of the case.

The following evening, Bianchon climbed his uncle Popinot's staircase to find him engrossed in a complex judgment. Popinot, dressed in an old, stained coat due to a delay from the tailor, appeared disheveled, but Bianchon managed to tidy him up before they entered the Marquise’s room.

Madame d’Espard had been a notable figure in Paris for seven years after separating from her husband in 1815. At thirty-three, she maintained a youthful appearance through strict beauty regimens, reminiscent of historical figures like Diane de Poitiers. Her efforts to preserve her looks drew comparisons to a famous Polish lady known for her longevity. Despite being a mother of two, Madame d’Espard was still considered fashionable, largely due to her social connections and lack of visible aging.

After her separation, Madame d’Espard lived quietly until 1820, when she re-entered society, hosting gatherings and establishing herself among the elite. She became close with influential figures, including a duchess and a countess, which helped her gain political influence. Her drawing-room became a hub for ambitious men, and she skillfully navigated Parisian social life, maintaining a façade of innocence while wielding considerable power.

Popinot and Bianchon arrived at the Marquise’s boudoir, where she sat elegantly, flanked by Rastignac and the silent Chevalier d’Espard. The Marquise’s appearance was striking, with a carefully curated environment reflecting her status. Popinot, however, focused on the case involving her estranged husband, M. d’Espard, whom she claimed was mentally unstable.

As the conversation unfolded, Madame d’Espard recounted her version of events leading to their separation, portraying herself as a victim of her husband’s whims. Popinot probed deeper, questioning her motives and the financial implications of her situation. He noted her luxurious lifestyle, which seemed at odds with her claims of financial distress. The Marquise attempted to deflect his inquiries, but Popinot remained steadfast, suggesting that her lavish spending could indicate ulterior motives in her petition for her husband’s incapacity.

Tension escalated as Popinot pressed for clarity on her financial situation, hinting that her request might be driven by self-interest rather than genuine concern for her children. The Marquise, feeling cornered, defended her actions as necessary for securing her children’s future, but Popinot’s scrutiny revealed cracks in her narrative.

After their meeting, Popinot resolved to investigate further, planning to summon Madame Jeanrenaud, linked to M. d’Espard, to uncover more about the situation. The next day, Madame Jeanrenaud arrived, presenting herself as a comically unassuming figure, far from the seductive temptress implied by the Marquise. She expressed concern for M. d’Espard, indicating a willingness to cooperate, which intrigued Popinot about the true dynamics at play.

As the story unfolded, it became clear that the Marquise’s polished exterior masked a complex web of ambition and manipulation, while Popinot’s role as a judge required him to navigate these treacherous waters with caution. The case promised to reveal not only the truth about M. d’Espard’s mental state but also the lengths individuals would go to protect their interests in the cutthroat world of Parisian society.

The text examines the life of M. le Marquis d’Espard, a nobleman residing in a dilapidated Paris mansion, highlighting how minor events can lead to significant outcomes. A minor illness delayed a crucial meeting with a judge, echoing historical instances where small incidents had major repercussions.

D’Espard's once-grand home, now in disrepair, symbolizes the decline of French nobility. Situated on a steep area, it was chosen for its garden, which benefited his children's health. Despite its condition, d’Espard cultivated a peaceful and dignified atmosphere, with modest decor that conveyed order and tranquility.

His family life was private, supported by a few loyal servants. D’Espard’s choice to isolate his children led to misunderstandings with neighbors, who viewed him as eccentric. Gossip portrayed him as a madman, fueled by his frugal lifestyle and the perception of financial ruin. This reputation was exacerbated by tenants’ jealousy and resentment towards his noble status, which they believed was undeserved without wealth.

D’Espard and his wife embodied true aristocratic values, instilling dignity and pride in their children, who reflected their noble heritage. At around fifty, the Marquis appeared refined but was seen as cold and eccentric due to his mannerisms. His children, Comte Clement and Vicomte Camille, were handsome and graceful, with Clement nearing sixteen and transitioning into young adulthood, while Camille remained in a youthful educational phase.

Overall, the narrative illustrates the complexities of social status, the effects of isolation, and the misunderstandings stemming from societal perceptions, particularly regarding the declining nobility in a changing France.

On a Thursday holiday, Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard. Early that morning, his sons, Clement and Camille, played in the garden, wrestling and making noise, which woke their father, the Marquis. The Marquis enjoyed watching them, finding joy in their antics. However, upon Popinot's arrival, the portress informed him of the Marquis' supposed madness, claiming he encouraged his children to fight.

Popinot was led to the third floor, where he found a shabby office filled with papers and an old clerk. After a brief introduction, he met the Marquis, who was reading by the fire. The judge explained he was there to investigate a petition for a Commission in Lunacy filed by the Marquise d’Espard. The Marquis, initially taken aback, regained his composure and asked why he had not been notified of the petition. Popinot explained that such notifications were unnecessary for those deemed insane.

The Marquis expressed concern about the implications of the inquiry on his wife. Popinot assured him of confidentiality and asked for an explanation regarding allegations of financial impropriety involving a Madame Jeanrenaud. The Marquis, visibly distressed, revealed a family secret tied to the Jeanrenaud family, whose fortune had been wrongfully acquired during the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. He explained that his ancestors had benefited from the execution of a Protestant merchant, and he felt compelled to make restitution to the Jeanrenauds, whom he had located in France.

Despite his wife's lack of understanding, the Marquis had committed to repaying the Jeanrenauds, which strained his finances. He chose to educate his sons himself, sacrificing his social standing to ensure they grew up with integrity. The judge listened intently, moved by the Marquis' noble intentions and the burden of his family's past.

As the conversation continued, the Marquis invited Popinot to his home, revealing that he had rented the office space for his publishing venture on China, which was financially successful. He expressed a desire to provide for his children while maintaining their honor. Just then, his sons returned from riding, full of energy and affection for their father, illustrating the strong bond they shared.

Madame Jeanrenaud soon entered, expressing her desire to return the money given to the Marquis, fearing for their honor. Popinot intervened, explaining that the payments were legally justified and rooted in honorable motives. He reassured the Marquis that his actions would not be misinterpreted and praised him for his integrity.

Afterward, Popinot returned to his office, reflecting on the encounter and the nobility of the Marquis. The next day, he was summoned by the President of his Court, who informed him that he would be removed from the case due to a potential conflict of interest stemming from his visit with Madame d’Espard. Although Popinot protested, he ultimately accepted the decision, recognizing the importance of maintaining the Court's integrity.

In summary, the narrative explores themes of honor, familial duty, and the complexities of social standing, as the Marquis d’Espard grapples with his family's dark history while striving to restore their honor through acts of restitution and dedication to his children.
