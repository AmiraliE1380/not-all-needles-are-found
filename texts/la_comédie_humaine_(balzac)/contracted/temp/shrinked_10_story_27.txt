In 1828, at one in the morning, Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac left a house on the Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré and walked to the boulevard because they had no carriage. Their talk turned to the Marquise d’Espard. Bianchon distrusted her character; Rastignac, captivated by her rank and beauty, believed she could raise his social standing and help pay his debts.

Bianchon warned Rastignac that pursuing the Marquise was a mistake, likening her to a “one-eyed horse” and suggesting she was older than she claimed. As a physician, he insisted physical signs reveal truth despite cosmetics and expressed disdain for fashionable women, whom he saw as lacking genuine feeling and motivated by selfishness. He described the Marquise as a polished figure hiding her true nature. Rastignac dismissed these moral reservations: for him a woman of fashion could be a valuable ally, offering connections, influence, and opportunities.

Despite misgivings, Bianchon agreed to introduce Rastignac to his uncle Jean-Jules Popinot, a judge who might intervene in the Marquise’s affairs, though he doubted Popinot’s willingness given the judge’s reputation for inflexibility.

The next morning Bianchon went to Popinot’s residence in a poor, dilapidated quarter of Paris, where the building’s aged decay contrasted with former grandeur. Popinot himself was unkempt and unambitious; his career had stalled despite talent—he had been passed over for promotion and remained a supernumerary judge.

Popinot’s life was defined by a strict commitment to justice, often to his own detriment. He was known for kindness and a rare ability to win confessions without coercion, blending compassion with firmness. His dedication coexisted with frustration at legal injustices.

As Bianchon approached Popinot’s door, he reflected on how difficult it would be to persuade his uncle to help Rastignac, aware of the legal complexities and obstacles ahead.

Twelve years before this narrative, in 1816, during a severe food scarcity in France, Popinot was appointed President of the Commission Extraordinary to distribute food to the impoverished in his neighborhood. He had intended to leave the Rue du Fouarre. As a prominent lawyer and criminal judge, Popinot had spent five years observing legal outcomes without understanding their causes. His work among the destitute fostered deep compassion, transforming him into a protector of the poor, akin to a modern Saint Vincent de Paul.

The change was gradual; charity, like vice, has its temptations. Popinot moved from one act of benevolence to another, becoming central in his community and involved in various charitable organizations. He worked tirelessly to prevent crime, provide employment, and assist the vulnerable, while keeping a private life few in Paris recognized. The gratitude of those he helped was often unspoken, their burdens leaving them little energy to acknowledge him.

By the end of his second year of charitable work, Popinot had converted a storeroom in his home into a parlor for the needy. Furnished simply with wooden benches and basic chairs, the room became a hub where the poor could seek assistance. Popinot meticulously recorded donations and requests to manage resources effectively. His aide, Lavienne, handled pawn tickets and visited the areas most affected by famine.

From early morning until late, the parlor was crowded with women, children, and the destitute seeking help. The atmosphere was frequently stifling, but the crowd's warmth made heating unnecessary. The poor revered Popinot, often waiting outside before dawn; even criminals respected his house. He dedicated mornings to the needy, afternoons to legal work, and evenings to his judicial duties.

Popinot’s keen observation let him discern hidden virtues in the impoverished and detect faint signs of wrongdoing in others. His modest inherited fortune was supplemented by his late wife's wealth, but his judge’s salary was limited, requiring frugality that his charity further strained. Despite significant contributions, he remained one of the few judges in the Court of the Seine without the Legion of Honor.

The President of the Second Chamber of the Court tasked Popinot with examining the Marquis d’Espard at the Marquise’s request for a Commission in Lunacy. The Rue du Fouarre, bustling with the needy in the early hours, would be desolate by nine. Bianchon, Popinot’s nephew, rode to find his uncle amid his charitable duties, expecting a contrast between Popinot’s appearance and the Marquise d’Espard’s expectations.

On arrival, Bianchon found the parlor full—a tableau of hardship, faces showing despair and resilience. Popinot, in a worn nightcap and dressing gown, was engrossed in his work, embodying a man committed to alleviating suffering. Lavienne facilitated the flow of requests, keeping the process efficient.

Bianchon interrupted with an urgent matter while Popinot handled consultations. After a brief exchange, Popinot resumed attending to a woman who needed funds to sustain her grocery business while her husband was imprisoned for debt; he provided assistance and documented the transaction in his ledger.

As the morning progressed, Bianchon learned Popinot had seen over two hundred individuals that day, with more waiting. He was sent to attend urgent medical matters; on his return he found the parlor empty, the last of the needy having been helped.

Popinot’s home reflected his chaotic life: dust, disarray, papers cluttering every surface, and the lingering remnants of his late wife’s possessions. Despite the disorder, tokens of gratitude from those he aided adorned the room, poignant reminders of his impact.

Preparing for breakfast, Bianchon urged Popinot to dress suitably for the upcoming meeting with the Marquise d’Espard. Popinot resisted, citing legal restrictions against magistrates accepting hospitality from parties involved in cases they oversee. Bianchon argued it would be beneficial for Popinot to meet her in her own environment, where she could express concerns more comfortably.

After some persuasion, Popinot agreed to visit the Marquise, recognizing the importance of understanding her perspective in the case. He made a note of the appointment and prepared to delve into the complexities surrounding the Marquis d’Espard’s mental state and the implications for his family.

The next evening at nine, Bianchon climbed his uncle’s dusty staircase to find him bent over a complicated judgment. Since the tailor had not sent the coat Lavienne ordered, Popinot wore his old stained coat, amusing those unaware of his private life. Bianchon straightened his uncle's cravat and buttoned his coat to hide the stains, but the judge's habitual movements soon rucked it up, revealing a gap between his waistcoat and trousers. Bianchon realized the absurdity just as his uncle entered the Marquise’s room.

To follow the ensuing interview, a brief account of Madame d’Espard is necessary. For seven years she had been a fashionable figure in Paris, where trends can elevate or diminish people. After separating from her husband in 1815, the Marquise, about thirty-three, maintained her vogue despite being the mother of two boys, fifteen and thirteen. Her ability to remain in fashion—usually favoring youth and beauty—was remarkable.

Appearing much younger than her years, she used elaborate hairstyles and a regimen of cold baths and minimal food to preserve her looks, an effort likened to a famous Polish lady who retained youthful vitality. Her complexion was clear and her figure retained allure; emotionally she remained detached, knowing neither love nor hatred. She parted from M. d’Espard with satisfaction, for he took their children, whom she regarded as obstacles to her ambitions.

Living on an income of twenty-six thousand francs a year, the Marquise hesitated before deciding how to manage her life after the separation. Though able to live lavishly, she chose a more subdued existence for some years. By 1820 she re-emerged into society, hosting gatherings and establishing herself among the elite, eventually taking the place of other prominent women in Paris.

Madame d’Espard cultivated ties with influential figures—a duchess, a countess—which amplified her political clout. Her drawing-room became a hub for political discussion; she was known for discretion and an ability to navigate Parisian society’s complexities. Ambitious young men admired her, and she skillfully maintained independence while wielding influence.

Despite her charm, she was also indiscreet and vain, treating relationships as means to extend her power rather than for genuine connection. Her drawing-room drew notable figures, and she managed interactions to avoid personal compromise.

Rastignac, a young man seeking alignment with her, recognized her as a potential ally but found her cunning a challenge. The Marquise’s household reflected wealth and status, and when Popinot arrived he noted the opulence. The Marquise welcomed him with feigned weakness; a third man, the Chevalier d’Espard, stood silently observing.

Popinot, though outwardly simple, was perceptive and quickly assessed the situation. He engaged Madame d’Espard about her husband’s mental state and their separation. She offered a crafted narrative: a change in her husband’s temperament led to estrangement. Popinot remained skeptical, probing circumstances and the children’s welfare.

As he questioned her, inconsistencies emerged. Popinot noted the lavishness of her life against claims of financial hardship, suggesting her motives for gaining control over her husband’s affairs might be self-serving. The Marquise, feeling cornered, tried to deflect, but Popinot’s persistence forced her to face the implications.

Tension rose as Popinot outlined potential consequences. He emphasized transparency and warned that the court would scrutinize her motives. Despite efforts to remain composed, the Marquise realized her carefully constructed facade risked collapse.

Afterward Bianchon and Rastignac discussed Popinot’s character and the case’s implications. They recognized its complexity and the potential for scandal; Bianchon worried about the Marquise’s influence. Popinot resolved to investigate further, planning to summon Madame Jeanrenaud, the woman linked to M. d’Espard, to clarify matters.

The following day Madame Jeanrenaud arrived at Popinot’s office, presenting a stark contrast to the Marquise. Stout and unrefined, she denied wrongdoing and expressed concern for M. d’Espard’s well-being. Her honest, straightforward manner contrasted sharply with the Marquise’s calculated charm.

Intrigued by Jeanrenaud’s sincerity, Popinot began to piece together M. d’Espard’s financial dealings. He recognized the case was more complicated than it had first appeared, with potential implications for both husband and wife. Preparing to unravel these entanglements, he understood the truth would require careful examination of all involved.

People who have matured know how trivial incidents can shape major events: Popinot's minor illness delayed a crucial meeting. The judge, feeling unwell, stayed home instead of visiting the Marquis d’Espard, losing a day whose absence echoed other historic delays.

Before following the lawyer and his clerk to the Marquis’s residence, consider this father's home life, which his wife’s petition branded as mad. In old Paris, some buildings still show a taste for decoration and permanence. The house where M. d’Espard lived on Rue de la Montagne-Sainte-Genevieve was such a stone structure: once grand, now weathered and scarred by time and revolutions. Formerly home to dignitaries, it had become a multi-tenant dwelling, its interior degraded by a printing establishment.

M. d’Espard took the ground floor, likely for his children’s sake, since the garden was unusually large for the area. He secured the rooms at a reasonable rent given the building’s disrepair and the depressed market. The house, dating from the reigns of Henry III–Louis XIII, still bore traces of former glory, including a perron and the garden.

The Marquis furnished his rooms in rich browns, creating a peaceful atmosphere with carefully chosen pieces and a harmonious scheme. Few visitors were admitted to the family’s private life, which remained largely secluded. The household consisted of the Marquis, his two sons, and a small staff — a long-serving cook and a man-servant — both contributing to the house’s orderly quiet.

M. d’Espard avoided socializing with other tenants to shield his children from outside influences. That choice, however, bred gossip and suspicion: neighbors labeled him mad and his servants untrustworthy. Tenants believed the Marquis was financially ruined, a view reinforced by his extreme frugality and habit of paying cash, avoiding credit.

The Marquis and his family were noble by birth, embodying ideals becoming rare in France. They retained a deep sense of dignity and pride in their lineage, which clashed with egalitarian sentiments among their neighbors. This disconnect fostered animosity, as tenants resented the d’Espards for perceived superiority despite apparent financial strain.

M. d’Espard, about fifty, exemplified the aristocracy of his time. He had a distinguished, somewhat cold appearance that commanded respect. Physical traits — a slightly crooked nose and a prominent forehead — lent an air of eccentricity that fueled rumors of madness. Despite elegant manners, he was frugal, wearing the same coat for years.

His sons, Comte Clément de Négrepelisse and Vicomte Camille d’Espard, were handsome and displayed aristocratic grace. They inherited their father’s features and their mother’s dignified manner. The elder, nearly sixteen, had moved to more fashionable attire, while the younger still wore a round jacket. The Marquis concentrated on giving them a rounded education, emphasizing mathematics, Eastern languages, and history, while avoiding what he considered unproductive studies.

The day Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard fell on a Thursday holiday. About nine, before their father rose, the two brothers were in the garden. The younger begged to visit the shooting-gallery for the first time and pressed Clément to support the request to the Marquis. The Viscount often took advantage of Clément’s weakness and delighted in wrestling with him. Their noisy quarrel roused their father, who watched amused from the window. Two neighbors commented that the Marquis seemed mad to make his children fight. Noticing them, the Marquis called the boys; they leapt into his room and Clément, after a moment’s hesitation, obtained permission from Camille.

All through the house people were talking about the Marquis’ supposed new madness. When Popinot arrived near noon with his clerk, the portress conducted him up to the third floor, remarking that M. d’Espard had made his children fight that morning and had laughed when the younger bit the elder until he bled; she added that the Marquis rarely showed himself. The landing’s muddy floor and grimy banisters lent some credibility to her gossip and made Popinot, though impartial, receptive to the tale.

“This is the man who swallows enough to feed the whole neighborhood,” the porter’s wife declared. The clerk smiled; Popinot fought to remain composed. They entered an outer office where an old man, probably the office clerk, sat amid shelves of published numbers; a grating separated a private room, and a cash-desk hinted at the enterprise’s commercial aspect.

“M. d’Espard?” Popinot asked the man in a gray blouse. The shopman opened the next room to reveal the Marquis, a venerable, simply dressed old man wearing the Cross of Saint-Louis, seated at a desk. Popinot explained they wished to speak on private business. The old man announced their intent to the Marquis, who rose and listened.

After formalities the Marquis asked their purpose. Popinot measured him and said, “Monsieur le Marquis, my duties and the inquiry that brings me here require us to be alone, although such inquiries are usually public. I am a judge on the Inferior Court of Appeal for the Department of the Seine, charged with examining you regarding a petition for a Commission in Lunacy filed by the Marquise d’Espard.” The old man withdrew; the clerk sat to take notes. The Marquis reddened and trembled, then steadied himself.

“How is it,” he asked, “that I have had no notice of such a petition?” Popinot explained that notice is unnecessary because those deemed subject to such a commission are presumed to lack reason; the Court must verify the petitioner’s claims. The Marquis accepted this and asked what he must do. Popinot urged him to answer fully and confidentially, especially concerning delicate accusations Madame d’Espard might make. The Marquis feared that his explanations could bring blame on his wife. Popinot promised discretion and the customary courtesy should the inquiry reveal a misunderstanding.

Then Popinot confronted the major allegation: that the Marquis had dissipated his fortune for the benefit of Madame Jeanrenaud, widow of a bargemaster, or her son Colonel Jeanrenaud, for whom he allegedly procured an appointment and exhausted influence with the King. The Marquis flushed; tears came, but pride returned quickly.

“You place me in a strange dilemma,” he said. Revealing his motives would mean disclosing secret wounds and trusting the family’s honor to strangers. Popinot assured him of confidentiality. Freed from his usual reserve, the Marquis then spoke with clarity and passion.

After his marriage his wife had incurred heavy expenses that forced him to borrow. He could not afford a steward; most of his title-deeds had been taken to Paris by his father, who feared scrutiny. Their family name was Negrepelisse; the title d’Espard had been acquired through marriage in the time of Henri IV, when they lost the town of Negrepelisse during religious wars and an ancestor was ruined by the destruction of his property. Though a later ancestor secured favor with Louis XIV and fortune, a stain of blood and disgrace remained, and the Marquis was determined to erase it.

He discovered that his grandfather had obtained the lands of Negrepelisse through means tied to the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes: a Protestant merchant was arrested and hanged, and the land passed to his ancestor by iniquitous means. The merchant’s family had the rightful claim; the victim’s name was Jeanrenaud. The Marquis could not bear the disgrace and resolved to find the heirs. Eventually he located M. Jeanrenaud, reduced to poverty and serving as a lieutenant under Napoleon. Believing the heir’s rights incontestable and their lack of means to sue, he offered to pay them the lands’ value at the time they were taken—eleven hundred thousand francs—payable at his convenience and without interest.

To effect restitution he lived on little for a long time. When he proposed to Madame d’Espard that they move to the country to live on half her income, she treated him as mad; her coldness convinced him she would have condoned his ancestors’ crimes. Terrified by her attitude, he resolved to leave her in control of her fortune after settling their debts, and to take charge of educating their children himself, determined to raise them as gentlemen. He invested prudently, repaid the debt sooner than expected, and had recently completed the payment of the eleven hundred thousand francs, making restitution without harming his children's prospects. These were the reasons for payments to Madame Jeanrenaud and her son.

Popinot asked whether Madame d’Espard knew his motives for retiring from public life. “Yes, monsieur,” the Marquis replied. Popinot, visibly moved, excused his clerk. He then inquired about other allegations, such as that the Marquis was engaged in a business unbecoming his rank. The Marquis declined to discuss it there and summoned Comte de Nouvion, an old family friend, explaining that he used the rooms as offices for the publication of the Picturesque History of China by a reputable firm, not as his private residence.

He showed Popinot his ground-floor apartment—a place of simple, touching harmony that struck the judge. The pleasant weather and garden convinced Popinot that a madman would hardly produce such beauty. The Marquis said he intended to remain until his younger son finished his studies, then travel with the children to European capitals to further their education and languages. He explained his China project: Comte de Nouvion, returned to France without fortune, was his partner; the Abbé Grozier, Keeper of the Books at the Arsenal, had been his tutor and taught him about China. Though enamored of China’s history, he remained a French gentleman. Financially, they had two thousand five hundred subscribers—twelve hundred in France—the book would cost about three hundred francs, and the Comte would earn six to seven thousand francs a year from it; the hundred thousand francs he hoped to make would cover his children’s education and pleasures. Twelve years of retirement to educate his children had cost him his memory at Court and political prospects, but he believed he had purified his family’s past and secured a glorious future for his children—a noble, if secret, task.

At that moment horses clattered in the courtyard. The two boys entered, robust and sunburnt, dressed for riding. They greeted their father and the lawyer. Popinot needed no further evidence of the Marquis’ bond with his sons. The boys reported shooting success and that they had ridden in the Bois, where they had seen their mother. “Why didn’t you speak to her?” the Marquis asked. “I think I noticed, father, that she doesn’t want us to speak to her in public,” Clément whispered. A cloud passed over the Marquis’ brow; Popinot felt deep sympathy at the scene of fatherly devotion. “Justice may look in here at any time,” the Marquis said. “If anyone is mad, it can only be the children, who are a little crazy about their father, and the father who is very crazy about his children—but that kind of madness rings true.”

Madame Jeanrenaud then entered, disregarding protests from the man-servant. “I take no roundabout ways!” she exclaimed. Bowing to those present, she announced she had come too late because the criminal judge was there. She declared that she and her son were of one mind to return everything, for their honor was at stake; they preferred to give back everything rather than cause trouble. “They must be as stupid as pans without handles to call you a lunatic,” she added. The boys clung to their father, alarmed.

“Silence, madame,” Popinot interposed. The Marquis sent his children to the garden. Popinot addressed Madame Jeanrenaud: the payments made by the Marquis were legally due, though prompted by an expansive sense of honesty. If every possessor of confiscated goods were compelled to make restitution every century and a half, few legitimate owners would remain in France. The law allowed M. d’Espard to dispose of his income without accounting for it. A Commission in Lunacy is appropriate only when actions lack reason; here, the remittances were driven by the highest motives. She could keep the money without remorse and let the world misinterpret a noble action. Popinot declared he would rather see the Marquis crowned than subjected to a Commission. Madame Jeanrenaud applauded his defense, calling it judge-like and warmly praising his words.

The Marquis extended his hand to Popinot, who pressed it gently; a private understanding passed between the humane judge and the noble, sublime man. “Monsieur le Marquis,” Popinot added, “from the first words of this inquiry, I regarded my clerk as unnecessary.” Leading M. d’Espard to the window, he said it was time to return home and that Madame la Marquise had likely acted under an influence the Marquis should counteract. Popinot left, the scene lingering with him. “Those rooms would suit me perfectly,” he thought. “If M. d’Espard leaves them, I will take up his lease.”

The next morning, having written his report the night before, Popinot went to the Palais de Justice to pursue prompt justice. As he prepared to put on his gown, the usher said the President wished to see him privately. “Good morning, my dear Popinot,” the President greeted. “I’ve been waiting.” Popinot asked if something was wrong. The President called it a silly trifle: the Keeper of the Seals, with whom he had dined, had suggested it would be best if Popinot did not sit on the case. Despite Popinot’s protest that he had left Madame d’Espard’s house before tea, the President cited propriety—“Caesar’s wife must not be suspected”—and the need to avoid any appearance of conflict that might expose the Bench to journalistic malice. It was to be a matter of propriety, not discipline.

Popinot was to signal his retirement from the case as a personal favor; in recompense, the President would secure the Cross of the Legion of Honor for him, long deserved. M. Camusot, a newly appointed judge from a provincial court, had been named in Popinot’s place. Popinot noted with ironic amusement Camusot’s pale ambition. He bowed and withdrew, choosing not to contest the implication.
