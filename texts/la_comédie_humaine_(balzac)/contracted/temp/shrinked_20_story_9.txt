The Marquise de Listomere embodies the spirit of the Restoration, balancing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married for seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she maintains a virtuous facade, possibly to support her husband's political ambitions. While some women reserve judgment until he becomes a peer, the marquise's conduct is strictly virtuous, aligning with her principles.

The marquis is a bland figure, known for his negative qualities rather than any virtuous reputation. He is a silent deputy who votes correctly but lacks charisma, earning him the nickname "dull weather." His unremarkable nature is seen as a safe choice for a virtuous woman, as he is incapable of folly.

Despite her reserved demeanor, the marquise commands respect and disdain from men who attempt to flirt with her, receiving only contempt in return. She engages in conversations with agreeable men without attracting gossip, unlike other women who may have ulterior motives. Her character is not one to play coy; she is genuinely principled.

The narrator, who admires the marquise, describes her physical attributes: she is neither plain nor conventionally pretty but possesses a captivating presence. Her eyes, when animated, reveal a deeper soul, and she exudes charm without actively seeking it. This quality is evident in her recent encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man who embodies both modesty and liveliness.

Rastignac, who is adept at navigating social circles, converses with the marquise at a ball, discussing topics ranging from opera to women's duties. Their interaction is marked by a mutual interest, though he abruptly leaves her side afterward. The next morning, Rastignac reflects on their conversation, finding himself preoccupied with thoughts of her.

As he writes letters in his comfortable setting, he becomes increasingly absorbed in his feelings for the marquise. His thoughts drift to the pleasures of romance, and he is interrupted by the arrival of his friend Horace, with whom he discusses various topics, including a potential expedition to Morea.

Meanwhile, the marquise receives a letter from Rastignac while getting ready for the day. The letter begins with affectionate terms, prompting her curiosity about its content. As she reads on, her initial impulse to discard it transforms into a desire to understand the sentiments expressed. The letter's conclusion leaves her fatigued, and she reflects on the impertinence of Rastignac, pondering the implications of his words.

This moment encapsulates the tension between her virtuous exterior and the stirrings of emotion within, setting the stage for the complexities of her character and the dynamics of her relationships.

That evening, Madame de Listomere attended a party at the Marquis de Beauseant's, anticipating Rastignac's arrival. By two in the morning, she realized he was not coming, despite her intention to confront him with coldness. Stendhal described this mental process as "crystallization."

Four days later, Rastignac scolded his valet, Joseph, for misplacing letters he had given him. Joseph, lost in thought, eventually recalled that one was for Madame de Listomere and the other for Rastignac's lawyer. When Rastignac learned that the letter was not for Madame de Nucingen, he laughed, realizing his heart had strayed.

Eugene's mistakes began with his intention to amuse Madame de Listomere by discussing the blunder of the love-letter. He compounded this by not visiting her for several days, allowing her thoughts to crystallize further.

When he finally called, the porter informed him that Madame de Listomere was out. However, the Marquis de Listomere invited him in, unaware of the tension. As they ascended the stairs, Rastignac noted several social blunders.

Upon seeing Eugene, Madame de Listomere blushed, which did not go unnoticed by him. He thought, "What! that fortress, too?" and adjusted his cravat. Monsieur de Listomere, distracted by a newspaper, was oblivious to the undercurrents of the conversation.

Madame de Listomere, despite her initial embarrassment, quickly regained her composure. When Eugene inquired about her health, she assured him she was well. He continued, suggesting that if his visit was unwelcome, it was due to the marquis's introduction. She replied that her husband was not privy to all her secrets, hinting at the complexity of their relationship.

Eugene, attempting to lighten the mood, suggested that silence would be the best excuse for his visit. Madame de Listomere responded with a promise of forgetfulness, though she maintained a dignified demeanor. When he mentioned the letter, she feigned offense but could not hide her amusement.

Eugene insisted that no offense was intended, and the letter was not meant for her. Madame de Listomere, while pretending to be offended, expressed her willingness to laugh at the situation, but warned against further mistakes.

Monsieur de Listomere, still engrossed in his paper, interrupted, mentioning a mutual acquaintance's death. This led to a discussion that revealed the marquise's irritation with Eugene's earlier comments. He naively suggested that she might be trying to extract a secret from him, which only piqued her curiosity further.

When Eugene mentioned Madame de Nucingen, Madame de Listomere's demeanor shifted. She rang for wood, signaling that it was time for him to leave. She coldly confronted him about the letter, questioning how his pen could mistakenly write her name. Discomfited, Eugene stumbled over his words before departing.

Days later, Madame de Listomere confirmed Eugene's claim about the letter's true recipient. She withdrew from society, and the marquis explained her absence as due to a stomach ailment, while her physician recognized it as a nervous condition she was using to justify her seclusion.
