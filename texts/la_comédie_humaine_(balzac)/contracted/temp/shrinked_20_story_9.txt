DEDICATION

To the Marquis Jean-Charles di Negro.

STUDY OF A WOMAN

The Marquise de Listomere is one of those young women bred in the spirit of the Restoration: devout enough to fast, take the sacrament, and consult a confessor who allows a comfortable blend of piety and fashion; worldly enough to frequent balls and operas in the most elegant dress. Always conforming to Church and society, she exemplifies a present-day woman for whom "legality" seems the motto. Her devotion is sufficient to recall the gloomy piety of a later Maintenon, while her worldliness could revive the gallantry of early Louis XIV. At present she is strictly virtuous — from policy, perhaps from inclination — and, married seven years to the Marquis de Listomere, one of those deputies with hopes of a peerage, she may view such conduct as useful to her family's ambitions. Some wait to judge her until Monsieur de Listomere becomes a peer and she reaches thirty-six, an age when many women realize they have been dupes of society.

The marquis himself is an insignificant man: neither notably good nor bad, he stands well at court, never speaks in the Chamber, but votes RIGHT. At home, he is as unremarkable as in politics. He does not scold, unless kept waiting, and his friends have nicknamed him "dull weather." For a woman with principles, he is an ideal husband: incapable of follies, he guarantees a respectable household life.

Some fops, impertinently bold, have dared to press the marquise's hand while dancing. They met only contemptuous glances; her cold indifference crushed the fragile hopes of flatterers. Beaux, wits, and fops of every stripe blanch before her. She has the right to converse freely with any agreeable man without being reported in gossip. Certain coquettish women might play such a reserved game for years to gratify future fancies; to suppose the Marquise de Listomere cunningly saving herself for such a scheme would be calumny.

I have had the happiness of knowing this singular woman. She talks well; I know how to listen; consequently I please her, and thus attend her parties — which was, in truth, the object of my ambition.

Madame de Listomere is neither plain nor pretty; she has dazzling skin, very red lips, white teeth, is tall and well formed, with a small, slender foot she keeps modestly hidden. Her eyes, far from the dullness common in Parisian women, have a gentle glow that becomes almost magical when she interests herself in conversation; then a soul is perceived behind her reserve. When animated, a grace buried beneath cold demeanor appears, and she is charming. She does not seek success, yet often attains it; we find what we do not seek — a truth that forms the moral of this little adventure, which echoes through the salons of Paris.

A month ago the marquise danced with a young man modest yet lively, full of good qualities, though showing his defects. He is ardent but laughs at ardor, talented yet conceals his talent; with aristocrats he plays the learned man, with learned men the aristocrat. Eugene de Rastignac is one of those clever young men who try everything, testing others to see what future holds. While he waits for the age of ambition, he scoffs at much; he possesses that rare combination of grace and originality. On this occasion he spoke with Madame de Listomere for nearly half an hour, without any deliberate plan to please her. Their conversation wandered from the opera Guillaume Tell to the duties of women; he looked at her in ways that embarrassed her, then left her and did not speak to her again that evening. He danced, played écarté, lost some money, and went home to bed. I assure you the affair happened precisely thus. I add nothing and suppress nothing.

The next morning Rastignac slept late and indulged in one of those reveries where a young man glides through many silken or cotton draperies of thought. At last he rose without yawning, rang for his valet, ordered tea, and drank immoderately. To explain this oddity: by then he was dispatching letters. Seated with his feet more often on the andirons than on the rug, he wrote; the first letter was finished and folded unaddressed; the second, begun at eleven, occupied him till noon, four closely filled pages.

"That woman keeps running in my head," he muttered, folding the second epistle to address later. He sank back in an easy-chair, stopped even poking the fire — though, when thinking of women, playing with the fire is one of our greatest pleasures, the mind finding speeches in the tiny blue flames. We interpret the crackling, the sudden ember that leaps upon the carpet, as if the fire itself spoke of love. This little meditation on "burgundian" sparks — a noisy detonation that throws a coal upon the floor — need not detain us longer; it only shows how trivial objects animate lovers' minds.

At that moment I entered. Rastignac sprang up.

"Ah! there you are, dear Horace; how long have you been here?"

"Just come."

He took up his two letters, directed them, and rang for his servant. "Take these," he said; "deliver them." Joseph departed without a word — an admirable servant.

We began to talk of the Morea expedition, to which I hoped to be appointed as physician. Eugene warned I should lose time away from Paris. We then conversed on various matters; I leave that conversation unmentioned.

When the Marquise de Listomere rose about half-past two that day, her waiting-maid Caroline gave her a letter she read while Caroline dressed her hair — an imprudence many young women commit. The letter began, "Dear angel of love, treasure of my life and happiness—" At those words the marquise nearly flung the letter into the fire, but a fancy — which virtuous women will readily understand — made her see how a man who began thus could possibly end. She read to the fourth page and then let her arms drop like someone fatigued.

"Caroline, go and ask who left this letter."

"Madame, I received it myself from the valet of Monsieur le Baron de Rastignac."

After that came a silence.

"Does Madame intend to dress?" Caroline asked at last.

"No — He is certainly a most impertinent man," reflected the marquise. I invite all women to imagine, for themselves, the chain of reflections that began with that first thought.

Madame de Listomere ended her correspondence by formally forbidding her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac and resolved to show him more than mere disdain in society; his insolence had exceeded what she usually tolerated. At first she thought of keeping the misdirected letter, but on second thought she burned it.

"Madame had just received such a fine love-letter; she read it," Caroline told the housemaid.

"I should never have thought that of madame," the other replied, surprised.

That evening Madame de Listomere attended a party at the Marquis de Beauseant's to cold-shoulder Rastignac, who would likely come. He did not appear; by two in the morning she found herself waiting in vain. Stendhal famously called the mental process she underwent before, during, and after that evening "crystallization."

Four days later Eugene scolded his valet.

"Ah ça! Joseph; I shall soon have to send you away, my lad."

"What is it, monsieur?"

"You do nothing but make mistakes. Where did you carry those letters I gave you Saturday?"

Joseph went stony, then smiled idiotically and said, "Monsieur, one was for the Marquise de Listomere, the other was for Monsieur's lawyer."

Joseph was speechless. I happened to be in Eugene's apartment and intervened.

"Joseph is right," I said. Eugene looked at me.

"I read the addresses quite involuntarily, and—"

"And," interrupted Eugene, "one of them was not for Madame de Nucingen?"

"No, by all the devils, it was not. Consequently, I supposed, my dear fellow, that your heart was wandering from the rue Saint-Lazare to the rue Saint-Dominique."

Eugene struck his forehead and laughed; Joseph perceived the blame was not his.

Young men should note certain morals. First mistake: Eugene thought it amusing to make Madame de Listomere laugh about the blunder that placed her as the recipient of a love-letter not meant for her. Second: he did not call on her for several days after the affair, allowing her virtuous imagination to crystallize. There were other errors I will pass over for the ladies to deduce.

Eugene finally went to call on the marquise; as he tried to enter, the porter said she was out. As he returned to his carriage, the Marquis de Listomere came home.

"Come in, Eugene," he said. "My wife is at home."

Pray excuse the marquis; a husband, however good, never attains perfection. As they ascended, Rastignac noticed a dozen blunders in worldly wisdom had slipped into his life.

When Madame de Listomere saw her husband usher in Eugene she blushed. The young baron saw the color and, like any self-regarding youth, thought, "What! that fortress, too?" He adjusted his cravat. Monsieur de Listomere seized the Gazette de France and carried it to a window to read the state of France.

A woman, even a prude, is not long embarrassed; she always keeps her fig-leaf. So when Eugene, interpreting her refusal to admit him in a way flattering to his vanity, bowed to Madame de Listomere in a somewhat intentional manner, she covered her thoughts with one of those feminine smiles more impenetrable than a king's words.

"Are you unwell, madame? You denied yourself to visitors."

"I am well, monsieur."

"Perhaps you were going out?"

"Not at all."

"You expected someone?"

"No one."

"If my visit is indiscreet you must blame Monsieur le marquis. I had already accepted your mysterious denial, when he himself came up and introduced me into the sanctuary."

"Monsieur de Listomere is not in my confidence on this point. It is not always prudent to put a husband in possession of certain secrets."

The marquise's firm, gentle tones and imposing glance made Rastignac aware he had posed prematurely.

"Madame, I understand you," he said, laughing. "I ought, therefore, to be doubly thankful that Monsieur le marquis met me; he affords me an opportunity to offer you excuses which might be full of danger were you not kindness itself."

The marquise, surprised, answered with dignity, "Monsieur, silence on your part will be the best excuse. As for me, I promise you entire forgetfulness, and the pardon which you scarcely deserve."

"Madame," said Rastignac hastily, "pardon is not needed where there was no offence. The letter," he added softly, "which you received, and which you must have thought extremely unbecoming, was not intended for you."

The marquise could not help smiling though she wished to seem offended.

"Why deceive?" she said with disdainful air, voice gentle. "Now that I have duly scolded you, I am willing to laugh at a subterfuge not without cleverness. I know many women who would be taken in: 'Heavens! how he loves me!' they would say."

She gave a forced laugh, then added indulgently, "If we desire to continue friends let there be no more mistakes, of which it is impossible that I should be the dupe."

"Upon my honor, madame, you are so—far more than you think," replied Eugene.

"What are you talking about?" asked Monsieur de Listomere, who had been listening without comprehending.

"Oh! nothing that would interest you," replied his wife.

Monsieur de Listomere calmly returned to his paper and presently said, "Ah! Madame de Mortsauf is dead; your poor brother has, no doubt, gone to Clochegourde."

"Are you aware, monsieur," resumed the marquise to Eugene, "that what you have just said is a great impertinence?"

"If I did not know the strictness of your principles," he answered naïvely, "I should think that you wished either to give me ideas I deny myself, or to tear a secret from me. But perhaps you are only amusing yourself with me."

The marquise smiled; that smile annoyed Eugene.

"Madame," he said, "can you still believe in an offence I have not committed? I hope chance may not enable you to discover the name of the person who ought to have read that letter."

"What! can it be still Madame de Nucingen?" cried Madame de Listomere, now more eager to penetrate the secret than to revenge the impertinence of his speech.

Eugene colored. A man above twenty-five would not blush at being taxed with a fidelity women laugh at—perhaps to hide envy. However he replied with tolerable self-possession, "Why not, madame?"

Such are the blunders we all make at twenty-five.

This answer caused a violent commotion in Madame de Listomere's bosom; but Rastignac did not yet know how to read a woman's face by a rapid glance. The marquise's lips paled, but that was all. She rang for wood, forcing Rastignac to rise and take his leave.

"If that be so," she said, stopping him with a cold, rigid manner, "you will find it difficult to explain, monsieur, why your pen should, by accident, write my name. A name, written on a letter, is not a friend's opera-hat, which you might have taken carelessly on leaving a ball."

Eugene, discomfited, looked at her with an air both stupid and conceited. He felt ridiculous; after stammering a few juvenile phrases he left the room.

A few days later the marquise acquired undeniable proof that Eugene had told the truth. For the last fortnight she has not been seen in society.

The marquis tells all who ask the reason for this seclusion: "My wife has an inflammation of the stomach."

But I, her physician, now attending her, know it is really nothing more than a slight nervous attack, which she is making the most of to stay quietly at home.
