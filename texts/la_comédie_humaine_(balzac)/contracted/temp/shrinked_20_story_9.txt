The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration spirit, blending religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she sustains a virtuous facade—partly to support his political ambitions. While some women withhold judgment until he becomes a peer, the marquise’s conduct remains strictly principled.

The marquis is notable for his lack of qualities rather than any merit. A silent deputy who votes correctly but lacks charisma, he is nicknamed “dull weather.” His unremarkable nature makes him a safe match for a virtuous woman, since he is incapable of folly.

Despite her reserve, the marquise elicits both respect and scorn from men who attempt to flirt; she answers only with contempt. She converses with agreeable men without provoking gossip, unlike those who pursue ulterior motives. She does not affect coyness; her probity is genuine.

The narrator admires her presence: neither plain nor conventionally pretty, she has a captivating air. Her animated eyes suggest a deeper soul, and she charms without seeking it. This quality is evident in her recent encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man combining modesty with liveliness.

At a ball Rastignac talks with the marquise about subjects from opera to women’s duties; their exchange hints at mutual interest, though he abruptly departs afterward. The next morning, while writing letters, Rastignac finds himself preoccupied with thoughts of her and increasingly absorbed in feelings approaching romance, until his friend Horace interrupts a conversation that ranges as far as an expedition to Morea.

While dressing, the marquise receives a letter from Rastignac that opens with affectionate terms, stirring curiosity about his intent. Her initial impulse is to discard it, then to understand his sentiments. The letter leaves her fatigued; she reflects on his impertinence and its implications.

The episode captures the tension between her virtuous exterior and inward stirrings, foreshadowing the complexities of her character and relationships.

Madame de Listomere forbade her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac and resolved to treat him with more than disdain; his recent insolence exceeded previous offenses she had tolerated. She had thought of keeping a letter he sent but ultimately burned it. "Madame received a fine love‑letter," Caroline remarked to the housemaid, who was surprised.

That evening Madame de Listomere went to a party at the Marquis de Beauseant expecting Rastignac; by two in the morning she realized he would not come, despite intending to confront him with coldness. Stendhal called this mental process "crystallization."

Four days later Rastignac scolded his valet, Joseph, for misplacing letters. Joseph, distracted, remembered one was for Madame de Listomere and the other for Rastignac's lawyer. When Rastignac learned the letter had not been for Madame de Nucingen, he laughed at his own wandering heart. Eugène’s errors began when he planned to amuse Madame de Listomere by discussing the mix‑up and worsened them by staying away for several days, letting her resentment harden.

When he finally called, the porter said Madame de Listomere was out. The Marquis, unaware of tensions, invited him in. As they climbed the stairs Rastignac noted several social faux pas. On seeing him, Madame de Listomere blushed; he thought, "What! that fortress, too?" and adjusted his cravat. Monsieur de Listomere remained absorbed in his newspaper, oblivious.

Madame de Listomere quickly composed herself. When Eugène asked after her health she assured him she was well. He suggested his visit might be unwelcome and blamed the marquis's introduction; she replied that her husband did not know all her secrets, hinting at their complicated intimacy.

Eugène tried to lighten the mood, suggesting silence as his best excuse. She promised to forget, keeping her dignity. When he mentioned the letter she feigned offense but could not hide amusement. He insisted he meant no harm and that the letter was not for her; she pretended to be hurt but warned him against further mistakes.

Monsieur de Listomere, still engrossed in his paper, interrupted to announce a mutual acquaintance's death. The ensuing talk exposed the marquise's irritation with Eugène’s earlier remarks. He naively suggested she might be trying to extract a secret from him, which only increased her curiosity. When he named Madame de Nucingen her demeanor shifted: she rang for wood, signaling his visit should end, and coldly confronted him about how his pen could have written her name. Discomfited, Eugène stammered and departed.

Days later Madame de Listomere confirmed Eugène's claim about the letter's true recipient. She withdrew from society; the marquis explained her absence as a stomach ailment, while her physician diagnosed a nervous condition she used to justify seclusion. The episode highlights the complexities of social interaction and misunderstanding, especially in romantic contexts, and reveals characters' emotional reactions and social roles.
