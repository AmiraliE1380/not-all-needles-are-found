The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration spirit, balancing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she preserves a virtuous façade to support his political prospects. While some women wait to judge him only when he reaches the peerage, the marquise’s conduct is strictly principled.

The marquis himself is a bland figure—an uncharismatic deputy who votes correctly but inspires little admiration, earning the nickname “dull weather.” His unremarkable nature makes him a safe match for a woman of integrity, unlikely to provoke folly.

Though reserved, the marquise commands both respect and scorn from would-be suitors, returning contempt to advances. She converses with agreeable men without inciting gossip, not out of coyness but from genuine principle.

The narrator, an admirer, sketches her appearance: neither plain nor conventionally pretty, yet possessing a captivating presence. Animated eyes suggest a deeper soul; she charms without seeking it. This quality is evident in a recent encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man who mixes modesty and vivacity.

At a ball Rastignac engages her on topics from opera to women’s duties; their exchange shows mutual interest before he abruptly departs. The next morning he is still preoccupied, writing letters in a comfortable setting and imagining the pleasures of romance, until his friend Horace interrupts their talk of a possible expedition to Morea.

Meanwhile the marquise, preparing for the day, receives a letter from Rastignac that begins with affectionate terms, provoking curiosity. Though tempted to discard it, she reads on to gauge his feelings; its closing leaves her fatigued as she contemplates the impertinence and implications of his tone.

The moment captures the tension between her virtuous exterior and an emerging emotion, foreshadowing the complexities of her character and future relationships.

Madame de Listomere forbade her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac, determined to show him more than disdain for what she deemed intolerable insolence; she had thought of keeping a letter he sent but ultimately burned it.

"Madame received a fine love-letter," Caroline told the housemaid, surprising her.

That evening Madame de Listomere went to a party at the Marquis de Beauseant expecting Rastignac; by two in the morning she realized he would not come, despite having resolved to confront him with coldness. Stendhal called this mental process "crystallization."

Four days later Rastignac scolded his valet Joseph for misplacing letters. Distracted, Joseph recalled one was for Madame de Listomere and another for Rastignac's lawyer. When Rastignac learned the letter was not for Madame de Nucingen he laughed, recognizing his heart had strayed. His errors began with a misguided plan to amuse Madame de Listomere by discussing the blunder and worsened by his subsequent absence for several days, which let her resentment crystallize.

When he finally called, the porter said Madame de Listomere was out. The Marquis de Listomere, unaware of the tension, invited him in. As they climbed the stairs Rastignac noticed social blunders. On seeing him, Madame de Listomere blushed; he thought, "What! that fortress, too?" and adjusted his cravat. Monsieur de Listomere, absorbed in his newspaper, was oblivious.

Madame de Listomere quickly regained composure. When Eugene asked after her health she said she was well; he suggested his visit might be unwelcome because of the marquis's introduction, and she replied that her husband did not share all her secrets, hinting at a complex marriage. To ease the mood he said silence would excuse his visit; she promised forgetfulness while maintaining a dignified air. When he mentioned the letter she feigned offense but could not hide amusement. Eugene insisted no offense was intended and that the letter was not meant for her; she pretended to be offended yet warned him against further mistakes.

Monsieur de Listomere, still engrossed in his paper, interrupted to mention a mutual acquaintance's death, prompting a discussion that revealed the marquise's irritation with Eugene's earlier remarks. He naively suggested she might be trying to extract a secret from him, which only increased her curiosity. When Eugene brought up Madame de Nucingen the marquise's demeanor shifted: she rang for wood, signaling that it was time for him to leave, and coldly confronted him about the letter, asking how his pen could have written her name. Embarrassed, Eugene stumbled away.

Days later Madame de Listomere confirmed his claim about the letter's true recipient. She withdrew from society; the marquis explained her absence as a stomach ailment, while her physician recognized it as a nervous condition she used to justify seclusion. The episode underscores the complexity of social interaction and misunderstandings in romantic contexts, revealing characters' emotional responses and social roles.
