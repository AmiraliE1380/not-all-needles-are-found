The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration ideal: religious devotion married to worldly elegance. Married seven years to a bland but politically useful marquis, she keeps a virtuous façade to support his prospects; acquaintances reserve judgment until he attains the peerage. The marquis is dull—a silent deputy who votes correctly—while she commands respect and occasional disdain: she rebuffs flirtations, converses with agreeable men without inviting gossip, and is genuinely principled rather than coy.

The narrator, an admirer, finds her neither plain nor classically pretty but possessing a captivating presence—animated eyes suggesting depth and effortless charm. This impression solidified in her encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever, modestly vivacious young man. At a ball he spoke with her about opera and women’s duties in a mutually engaged exchange, then abruptly left. The next morning Rastignac, distracted and imagining romantic pleasures, wrote letters until his friend Horace arrived to discuss an expedition to Morea. Meanwhile the Marquise received Rastignac’s affectionate letter; intrigued at first, she grew offended by its impertinence and implications, creating tension between her virtuous exterior and a stirring emotion.

Offended, Madame de Listomere forbade her porter to admit Rastignac and burned the letter she had briefly considered keeping. Caroline, the housemaid, reported, surprised: “Madame received a fine love‑letter.” That evening the marquise attended the Marquis de Beauseant’s party expecting Rastignac; by two in the morning she realized he would not come. Stendhal calls the hardening of her resentment “crystallization.”

Four days later Rastignac scolded his valet Joseph for misplacing letters; Joseph recalled one had been for Madame de Listomere and another for Rastignac’s lawyer. Learning the misplaced letter was not for Madame de Nucingen, Rastignac laughed—his affections had wandered. His trouble stemmed from a foolish attempt to amuse Madame de Listomere by discussing the blunder and worsened when he failed to visit for several days, letting her displeasure fester.

When he finally called, the porter said she was out, but the marquis, unaware of the tension, invited him in. On the stairs Rastignac sensed awkwardness. The marquise blushed at his appearance; he adjusted his cravat, uneasy. She hinted her husband did not share all her secrets. Eugène tried to ease the mood; she promised to forget the incident while preserving dignified reserve. When he mentioned the letter she feigned offense but could not hide amusement; he insisted it was not meant for her. She rang for wood to signal his departure and coldly confronted him about how his pen could have written her name. Embarrassed, he left. Days later she confirmed his claim about the actual recipient and withdrew from society; the marquis blamed a stomach ailment while her physician diagnosed a nervous condition. The episode highlights social misunderstandings, emotional reactions, and rigid romantic roles.
