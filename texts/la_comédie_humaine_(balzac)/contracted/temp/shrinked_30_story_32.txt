DEDICATION

To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy.

Dear Nodier, this book uncovers actions hidden behind domestic doors where divine intervention often supplants human justice. Its morals focus on family and motherhood and on harms that follow when paternal authority—the traditional tribunal for domestic wrongs—is weakened. A loving mother cannot replace patriarchal power. The story defends indissoluble marriage and warns against unchecked selfishness in a society that exalts wealth and success while the law is impotent. I hope such a society will return to Catholic moral guidance through different education.

In “Scenes from Military Life” I will portray noble self-sacrifice and also show war’s corrupting influence on those at home who live as if on a battlefield. You have observed our times keenly; I entrust this tale to your name as a shield from criticism.

Your sincere admirer,  
De Balzac  
Paris, November 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792 the townspeople of Issoudun disliked Dr. Rouget; gossip blamed him for his beautiful wife’s unhappiness, though the household’s true nature remained secret. Madame Rouget, née Descoings, had been frail from youth. They had a son, Jean-Jacques, and, ten years later, a daughter, Agathe.

Rouget’s insistence that men show strength caused much female suffering. The Descoings family—once wealthy wool merchants who invested in forfeited estates—were rich and miserly. Descoings, Rouget’s brother-in-law, left Issoudun for Paris, failed as a grocer, and later pursued a beautiful widow. Expecting his wife’s death, Rouget sent Agathe to Paris to secure Descoings’ favor and a share of an inheritance.

Agathe, the town’s admired beauty, provoked a quarrel between Rouget and their friend Lousteau, who then left town. Madame Rouget, learning her daughter had gone, predicted she would never see Agathe again—a prophecy that held as her health declined, worsened by her son’s neglect; Jean-Jacques offered little comfort.

Agathe reached Paris as Descoings was imprisoned for imprudent political remarks during the famine and was later executed alongside the poet André Chénier—an event that silenced local grocers and left a sharper immediate mark on the community than posterity might record.

Agathe married Bridau, a ministry secretary, with Rouget’s approval; Rouget hoped to profit from any Descoings inheritance. Bridau, captivated by Agathe, supported Napoleon, earned promotion in 1804, and worked relentlessly. In 1808 he died of overwork just as another promotion was imminent—an honor the Emperor had intended but that came too late.

After Bridau’s death, Agathe received no letters from Issoudun. Her mother died shortly before the birth of her second son; her father died around the Emperor’s coronation, an event that distracted Bridau. Jean-Jacques remained absent. Madame Hochon, Agathe’s godmother, warned she would inherit nothing unless she granted power of attorney to her brother; Agathe hesitated. Bridau’s prudent savings provided only a modest income; afterwards Agathe and Madame Descoings lived on twelve thousand francs.

By 1809 Madame Descoings, sixty-five but fond of theater and fine food, was secretly gambling on lotteries and hiding mounting debts, while concealing family secrets about her son Bixiou. Devoted and refusing to remarry, Agathe took on maternal and household duties. Her limited judgment and blind trust in Madame Descoings proved disastrous when the lottery debts emerged; Agathe, without reproach, sold possessions and liquidated investments to settle them—a decisive turning point in their lives.

One of the bleakest stretches of Paris was rue Mazarin between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, shadowed by the high walls of the college and Cardinal Mazarin’s library. There, on the third floor of a damp, cold house, lived the poor widow Madame Bridau (Agathe). From her windows she could see the Institute buildings opposite, the rapins at their examinations and the students’ uproar; to brighten the room she defied police regulations and set boxes of earth on the sill to make little gardens.

The shallow house, reached by a winding staircase, contained three rooms—a dining room, a small salon, and a bedroom—plus a kitchen and two single rooms. Agathe had chosen the flat because it was cheap (four hundred francs a year), close to her sons’ school and in a neighborhood she knew. The interior matched the building’s dreariness: yellowed wallpaper, unglazed tiles, and only essential furniture. The salon held an Aubusson carpet given to her and a mahogany sofa bearing a pastel portrait of her late husband, Bridau, which captured his noble essence; a Napoleon hung above a card table.

After Bridau’s death Agathe’s life narrowed. She filled the room with two large birdcages—canaries and Java sparrows—and the floor and furniture were littered with millet, chickweed and cat hair, outward signs of grief and neglected personal care. She preserved everything belonging to her husband, even wrapping his last pen with a note. Grief seemed to erode her sense of womanhood; she devoted herself entirely to her children and to managing the precarious household.

Agathe lived for her sons and worried ceaselessly over their finances. The Emperor provided three hundred francs a year for each child, with scholarships supplementing this. Her aunt, Madame Descoings, who lived on the second floor, assigned three thousand francs from her annuity to help Agathe; the Descoings themselves now lived modestly on twelve hundred francs a year and shared dinners with the two widows. Madame Descoings doted on Philippe and Joseph, indulging them with treats and small sums. Distrustful of the future, Agathe scrimped and saved—she set aside three thousand francs from her pension—hoping to secure some inheritance for her boys. Thus both widows had passed from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty, each for different reasons.

The house, the students’ uproar, and her husband’s portrait formed the setting that would shape her youngest son, Joseph Bridau, into a notable painter of the modern French school. Philippe, the elder, resembled his mother in looks but had a bold demeanor often mistaken for courage; at fifteen his charm reinforced Agathe’s hopes. Joseph, three years younger, took after his father in less favorable ways: untidy, often scolded, and plainly less favored by his mother.

Joseph’s artistic bent was sparked by a visit to the Tuileries, where a caricature on an Institute wall captivated him and made him want to draw. He began sneaking into the Institute courtyard, enduring teasing from older students until his persistence won their respect. He later visited the sculptor Chaudet’s atelier; Chaudet recognized and encouraged his talent. Agathe feared art meant hardship and tried to dissuade him, but Chaudet defended the vocation as a divine calling, insisting true talent must be followed.

Meanwhile Philippe, enchanted by military life, secretly petitioned the Emperor to enlist. He was sent to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr and rose rapidly, becoming a captain by nineteen. Joseph, after his scholarship was withdrawn, tried to earn a living by art with encouragement from Madame Descoings. In 1816 Joseph persuaded his mother to let him convert the adjoining garret into an atelier—partly financed by Madame Descoings—installing a skylight and continuing studies under Gros and Schinner. Unlike the two widows, who treated painting as a trade, Joseph embraced it passionately.

Political unrest simmered in France. Philippe was implicated in conspiracies, briefly arrested, then released for lack of evidence, though his military pay was cut and Agathe feared for him. Friends urged the widows to send him abroad for safety; though she resisted the idea of him serving a foreign power, Philippe joined General Lallemand in the United States to help found the Champ d’Asile—an enterprise that failed. Agathe had equipped him earlier with ten thousand francs; by late 1817 the household had adjusted to reduced means and Joseph lived frugally to support the family while his talent developed.

In 1818 the notary Roguin absconded with funds intended for Madame Descoings. Soon after, Philippe sent a bill for a thousand francs, having lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. Agathe, distraught, blamed herself for urging his departure. Despite her own difficulties, Madame Descoings continued to support them. Desperate, Joseph appealed to Gérard, who arranged for him to paint copies of a portrait of Louis XVIII; Joseph worked tirelessly, selling panels to cover Philippe’s debt.

When Philippe returned, Agathe welcomed him warmly but failed to notice how America had hardened him. He became self-centered—drinking, gambling and swaggering—and lived off others, disappearing for days in cafés and gaming halls. He refused to serve the Bourbons, neglected family duties, and sought to impress Mariette; a proposal to turn his experiences into journalism and a cashier’s post at a newspaper, secured by Agathe and Madame Descoings, brought temporary relief but little reform. Joseph felt increasingly overshadowed and unappreciated.

Philippe’s return and altered behavior strained relationships and finances, exposing the consequences of choices, the limits of maternal devotion, and the fragile balance between ambition and family duty that governed the Bridau household.

Three months later the colonel, who ate for four, still criticized the food while the widows—reluctant to press him—kept spending though he had not paid a penny. His mother and Madame Descoings avoided reminding him of his promise; months passed without the “tigers with five claws,” as Leon Gozlan joked, migrating from Philippe’s pocket into the household purse. The colonel eased his conscience by seldom dining at home. “Well, he is happy,” his mother said, “he has a place.”

Urged by a feuilleton edited by Vernou, Mariette debuted at the Porte-Saint-Martin and triumphed with Begrand. A wealthy general, enchanted and connected to Philippe and Giroudeau, became her impresario and arranged her debut—an example of solidarity among the infatuated. Bixiou soon told his grandmother and Agathe that Philippe, the cashier, was in love with Mariette, the celebrated ballet-dancer. The widows were appalled; Agathe deemed stage women morally suspect and both feared they would spend freely. “Do you think my brother will spend on Mariette? Such women ruin rich men,” Joseph protested. Bixiou suggested Mariette might be stolen by higher-ranking admirers; he’d even heard an ambassador was infatuated.

Partly for financial reasons, Philippe proposed; Mariette declined, perhaps suspecting his intent or valuing freedom, hoping instead for a Grand Opera engagement. Over the next year Philippe visited his mother only twice a month, spending days at the office, the theatre, or with Mariette, enjoying pleasures and mixing with Tullia and other entertainers. The carnival lasted eighteen months but grew stormy. After her Opera debut in January 1821 Mariette attracted a distinguished duke. Philippe, driven by passion, began dipping into the funds he managed—taking eleven hundred francs by May. Mariette left for London to seek patrons while the temporary opera house was prepared. Philippe, despite her infidelities and her using him as a stepping stone, remained deeply in love.

Forced to relinquish Mariette, Philippe returned to his mother’s apartment, unable to imagine life without recent pleasures. Suicide crossed his mind—not just over the deficit but because he could not bear life without Mariette. He entered Joseph’s studio, where Joseph was copying a picture for a dealer and discussing earnings. Pale and subdued, Philippe asked how long his portrait would take, saying he wanted to leave his mother a likeness. When Joseph asked if he was leaving again, Philippe said he was going never to return and, pressed, confessed he had taken money and could not live with the dishonor.

Joseph tried to reassure him that repayment would save his honor, but Philippe, despairing, fled downstairs, leaving Joseph anxious. Joseph told Madame Descoings, who was horrified. Agathe was devastated, crying at the thought of Philippe’s dishonor. Madame Descoings blamed a bad woman for his ruin; Agathe lived in terror for her son. That day Philippe calmly submitted his accounts, claiming he’d retained the money for safekeeping. He then went to a gambling house—winning at first, then losing everything. Returning home, he found his family’s concern and affection, which briefly comforted him. “Promise not to kill yourself, and all shall be forgotten,” his mother pleaded. Philippe, sensing the plea had worked, feigned gratitude and affection; they shared dinner and he felt momentarily relieved.

The next day Agathe seemed older and more anxious, aware the family’s finances were precarious. Madame Descoings suggested consulting Desroches to manage the affair and preserve their honor. Desroches, diplomatically, advised Agathe to sign a transfer of funds and petition for Philippe’s reinstatement in the army, assuring that he would regain his rank and easing some fear. Yet Agathe’s faith was shaken and she lived in constant dread of his downfall.

Philippe returned to a life of leisure—gambling, drinking, looking disheveled and indifferent to the family’s plight. His love for Mariette had hardened him; he treated women with contempt. Joseph, focusing on painting, gained recognition and Madame Descoings’s support; she pinned her hopes on his future. Noticing discrepancies, Joseph began to suspect Philippe and confided in Madame Descoings. They planned to catch him. When Joseph discovered Philippe had indeed taken money from him, he felt betrayed; despite anger he still struggled to confront his brother. Madame Descoings urged sympathy for Philippe’s struggles, but Joseph remained wary of his influence on the family’s future.

Despite Philippe’s careful nightly gambling, he was sometimes “cleaned out.” Needing his usual ten francs, he began stealing from family—pilfering from his brother and whatever Madame Descoings or Agathe left about. After a dream in which Philippe robbed her, Agathe recognized his decline and begged him to ask for money instead of stealing. Within two weeks her savings were gone; she took worsted work for twenty sous a day, endured sleepless nights waiting for Philippe, and watched his increasing drunkenness and mood swings.

When Madame Descoings suggested new clothes, Philippe retorted that his mother had no money and that he had mortgaged his pension. He admitted borrowing from Giroudeau, calling it a “debt of honor.” Agathe excused him as out of his element, while Joseph criticized his neglect of appearance and duty. Philippe sank further into debauchery and gambling, losing the steadiness and good looks he once had.

One day Philippe asked Joseph for money to pay a tobacconist; Joseph refused. Philippe then guessed where Madame kept her savings and, expecting she’d be out buying Christmas treats, broke into her room. He found twenty napoleons hidden in the mattress and took the money to gamble.

At the gambling house Philippe won substantial sums over several sessions and, intoxicated by success, amassed over one hundred fifty thousand francs by night’s end. Pressed by greed, he continued and lost everything. The next morning he awoke in despair, knowing he had ruined his family.

Agathe and Madame Descoings waited for Philippe at Christmas dinner; when he failed to show they worried. Madame had pinned hopes on a lottery stake, but upon checking her savings she found the money gone and fainted. The concierge confirmed Philippe had been in the apartment. Agathe returned pale and shaken; the family confronted Philippe. He dismissed their accusations, claiming he’d taken what was owed him. A fight erupted between Joseph and Philippe, and Madame Descoings suffered a stroke amid the chaos.

Joseph offered his mother money afterward, but she refused, clinging to lottery hope. When the winning numbers were announced it was clear she’d missed her chance; she died shortly after. Philippe, recovering from illness, was told by Agathe that their finances had collapsed and urged to rejoin the army. Indifferent and feeling abandoned, he accepted the money she reluctantly offered and left to seek the company of those he called true friends. Agathe, heartbroken, prayed for her son and resigned herself to the loss of the child she once loved.

By February 1822 Madame Bridau had been moved into the attic above the kitchen of her former apartment, recently occupied by her son Philippe. Seeing his mother reduced, Joseph set out to make her comfortable: he rearranged the garret, added better furnishings, and took Agathe daily to a table d’hôte that cost him nearly a hundred francs a month. Alarmed by the expense and urged by her confessor, Agathe accepted a seven-hundred-franc-a-year post at a lottery office that demanded long hours.

She adapted to the routine, preparing Joseph’s breakfasts, mending his clothes, keeping the studio tidy, and aiding his work despite having no artistic training. Their bond deepened through these small acts of tenderness. They spoke often of Philippe—Agathe feared his reckless life, but Joseph dismissed her worries, convinced Philippe was prospering in New York.

One afternoon Joseph visited Philippe, who worked at a newspaper. Philippe invited Joseph to dine; Joseph declined to stay with his mother. When he described Philippe afterward, Agathe felt hopeful. She decorated Joseph’s studio and prepared an elaborate breakfast for a sitting for Philippe’s portrait; overwhelmed, she hid and watched the two sons together. After the sitting Philippe invited them to a sumptuous dinner, which Agathe eagerly accepted while Joseph felt uneasy at the ostentation. At another sitting she learned more of Philippe’s circle—journalists and actresses—and heard boastful plans, though he remained vague about money.

Later Joseph postponed a session for a commission. Philippe, mistaking a valuable copy of a Rubens for an original, took it and sold it for a fraction of its worth. Discovering the theft devastated Joseph; Agathe was heartbroken, feeling she had lost her son to vice.

Two months after, an old officer told Agathe Philippe was ill and needed money. Working at the lottery, she had little to spare and at first refused to help the son she felt she had banished from her heart; Joseph insisted Philippe go to the hospital. When she later found Philippe destitute on the street, she gave what she could and despaired.

Agathe then learned Philippe had been implicated in a conspiracy and was arrested; she fainted. Joseph consulted Desroches, who urged them to secure the family inheritance and gather evidence of undue influence; he planned to visit Issoudun at the holidays to help. Agathe’s letter to her godmother brought a discouraging reply about an unhelpful brother. Agathe and Joseph set off for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to face the consequences and Agathe to confront their deep estrangement.

Issoudun, noted since Caesar and Rigord, yielded in excavations by M. Armand Peremet a fifth‑century basilica—likely unique in France—built from stones of a Roman temple to Isis. The town’s name, from “Isis” plus “dun” (a religious and military settlement), points to Celtic origins.

In the Middle Ages Issoudun was powerful: Richard Coeur‑de‑Lion built a tower above the basilica, and later the town harbored Routiers and Cottereaux mercenaries sent by Henry II against his son Richard. Its prominence declined. After the 1830 insurrection vine‑growers resisted government intervention, burned tax offices, and asserted autonomy—about eight thousand strong.

In the fourteenth century Issoudun held 16–17 thousand people, prospering on the wool trade and producing blankets and gloves; by Louis XIV’s time it showed some elegance. Today the population is about twelve thousand, including vine‑growers from nearby suburbs. The economy—especially wine—suffers from outdated practices: growers resist modernization and produce poor wine that undermines prosperity.

This decline reflects broader stagnation. Local leaders even opposed a proposed Paris–Toulouse road, fearing higher food prices—an instance of provincial preference for tradition over progress. The disappearance of the nobility removed a source of competition; the complacent bourgeoisie and intermarried leading families run ineffectual administration with weak accountability, while the clergy exert little revitalizing influence.

Against this backdrop the town’s youth, feeling trapped, formed the “Knights of Idleness,” led by Maxence Gilet—rumored an illegitimate son of an official who rose from poverty, distinguished himself in the military, and returned to clash with the bourgeoisie. Their disruptive yet creative pranks, sheltered by a tavern run by Père and La Cognette, express youthful frustration with Issoudun’s decline.

As Joseph and Madame Bridau journeyed from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a prank: an old Spanish ex-prisoner left his empty cart at the foot of the Issoudun tower, and Maxence arrived at the rendezvous proposing they haul it up the embankment. In an hour they dismantled and hoisted the cart like artillery, reassembled it above, and the hungry, thirsty Knights returned to Mère Cognette’s to await the Spaniard’s astonishment.

Their diversions were not limited to pranks; the twenty youths met for hunts, vintages, and winter skating, and Max became their idol. François Hochon and Baruch Borniche, Madame Hochon’s grandsons, treated Max as a cousin and relied on him for money and adventures denied them at home.

After twelve to fifteen bottles of wine one night, François and Baruch warned Max that Madame Bridau and her son were coming to town and that Rouget’s sister might secure the property when he died. Max, thirty-four and soldierly, masked unease with confidence. When François flippantly used a contemptuous nickname for Flore Brazier, Max snapped, defending her. He then spoke openly for the first time about Flore—Rouget’s servant-mistress, derided by society—and his attachment to her.

Max explained that people believed he lived off Flore’s supposed money and that he planned to marry her when Rouget died. He denied mercenary motives but admitted he loved her and preferred her to Mademoiselle Fichet, the town’s richest heiress being courted by Goddet. He asked the Knights—within the limits of their fooleries—to help him deal with these Parisians if needed, clarifying he wouldn’t ask them to kill or poison anyone. His candidness moved the group to pledge loyalty.

They joked and toasted, and Max arranged the cart prank’s completion. At half-past three the Knights slipped away in soft shoes. Max returned to Place Saint-Jean, concealing his worries; his time in Cabrera had taught him to hide unease. The forty thousand francs a year from Rouget’s property anchored his affection for Flore, but news of legitimate heirs unnerved him: Rouget’s savings, amassed over seventeen years and held in a doctor’s name, and some seven hundred and fifty thousand francs lodged with various notaries could not be quickly liquidated in a gossiping town. Furious that Flore had never mentioned her sister and nephews in seven years, Max decided the priority was to get rid of these relatives so they could secure the property. Exhausted, he used a pass-key to enter Père Rouget’s house and went to bed, hoping clearer thoughts would come the next day.

The narrative then traces how Flore Brazier became known as the “Rabouilleuse” and became quasi-mistress of Jean-Jacques Rouget’s household. As Doctor Rouget aged he saw his son’s ineptitude and tried to impose a routine, only to find Jean-Jacques yielding to the first authority figure. One day in September 1799, returning from his rounds, the doctor found a captivating little girl by a brook, emerging from the water in a tattered petticoat and makeshift hat. She said she was from Vatan and was catching crabs for her uncle Brazier, who joked about republican liberty.

Struck by the child’s looks, the doctor took her in, promising her uncle wages and providing food, clothing, and gifts to encourage study. Flore’s peasant habits made her dislike books, and the doctor soon gave up formal schooling, though for five years he enjoyed instructing her without scandalous complications despite town gossip.

Born in 1787 and raised amid Revolutionary upheaval, Flore grew up with shaken traditional values and a pragmatic self-interest rather than strict virtue. As Rouget neared death, his notary urged him to provide for her; Rouget’s cynical refusal to leave her anything revealed bitterness and a desire to punish what he perceived as unreciprocated attachment. That refusal set the stage for the inheritance struggle now threatening Max’s position and Flore’s hopes.

Jean-Jacques Rouget did not mourn his father’s death, though Flore Brazier did; the old doctor had made his son miserable but had provided Flore the comforts prized in the country. After the funeral, when Fanchette asked Flore what would become of her, Jean-Jacques displayed animation for the first time.

At seventeen Flore retained the beauty that had attracted the doctor, though she was growing plump like other countrywomen. Jean-Jacques hesitated to speak; his father’s death and benefactions weighed on him, and Flore left him puzzled by his silence.

When his father died Jean-Jacques was thirty-seven: timid, submissive, and childlike. His torpor left him passionate but lacking confidence, awkward and unattractive, preferring solitude. Too late the doctor recognized how his son’s temperament had suffered. Wanting him to marry but fearing loss of control over his property, the doctor sought a daughter-in-law and tried to instill avarice in Jean-Jacques to give him some financial instinct.

Secretly, Jean-Jacques was in love with Flore, the only woman whose educated presence he could comfortably observe; he took pride in the instruction his father gave her, thinking it would make her easier to win. Flore, unaware of his feelings, waited for him to declare himself, but he remained silent.

After three weeks of uncertainty Flore awoke one night to find Jean-Jacques crouched outside her door and took it as proof of his love; the next day she found him less repellent. Their halting conversations—about returning to the fields and her honesty—revealed his growing affection even as he struggled to express it.

Ten months later Jean-Jacques brightened and paid attention to his appearance, much to Flore’s satisfaction; the townspeople noticed and speculated. Fanchette, who had disapproved of Flore’s place, eventually left; Flore anticipated this and learned to cook and manage the house, surpassing the former cook and taking pride in keeping things neat.

Over nine years Flore gained complete control over Jean-Jacques, treating him with familiarity and respect and becoming the dominant figure while he, childlike and dependent, relied on her for everything.

By 1815 Flore was at the height of her beauty; in 1816 she fell in love with Maxence Gilet, a penniless officer. Her new passion altered the household: she pressed Jean-Jacques about his wealth and empty rooms and suggested inviting Max. With Max in the home Jean-Jacques found the companionship and conversation he had lacked, while Flore shirked household duties, hired a new cook, refined her appearance, bought Max a horse, and took on a servant. The household shifted—Flore and Max became central and Jean-Jacques receded into the background, increasingly dependent on them.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to claim her threatened inheritance, Jean-Jacques Rouget had grown semi-vegetative. After Max provided financial aid, Flore improved Rouget’s care, feeding him from Vedie’s kitchen. Better looked after, he nonetheless weakened and became utterly submissive; his devotion to Flore was his only motive and made him seem far older than fifty-seven.

Max’s apartment reflected that devotion: provincial comforts, elegant wallpapers and mahogany furniture. A rush matting muffled footsteps so Max could return late without waking anyone — not even Rouget, who remained ignorant of Max’s nocturnal activities.

One morning Flore, in a cotton gown, found Max asleep and noticed his late return. He told her Rouget’s family were coming to Issoudun, likely to evict them. Flore vowed confrontation; Max urged caution, asking for coffee and suggesting they plan later. As she began making coffee, Baruch interrupted: Fario was searching for his cart.

Max joined the crowd. Fario, a small peculiar man, claimed his cart had been stolen. Max led him to the tower embankment where, to everyone’s surprise, the cart was found at the top. Amid laughter Max theatrically tossed the cart down, humiliating Fario and turning the episode into town gossip.

The incident heightened concern. François informed Max that the Bridaus would arrive that night. Max resolved they should not appear hostile; he proposed letting the Hochons handle driving them out. Meanwhile Flore, furious that Rouget’s sister and nephew were coming, punished Rouget by withdrawing her affection, leaving him bewildered and distressed. Vedie urged him to placate Flore. Rouget denied inviting them and blamed Madame Hochon for the visit.

Max argued Rouget should welcome his kin for appearances, counselled him to keep his will while being polite, and schemed finances so the Hochons would help deal with the Bridaus.

Opposite Rouget’s, the Hochon household was modest. Monsieur Hochon, a retired tax collector, was famed for frugality. Madame Hochon prepared to receive her goddaughter Agathe, and their granddaughter Adolphine secretly hoped to win Joseph Bridau’s affection. News of the Bridaus’ arrival split Issoudun: cautious bourgeoisie offered measured support, while the Knights of Idleness remained vehemently loyal to Max. The Fario incident and the town’s division underscored how fragile reputations and power were, and how a few calculated actions could shape the looming confrontation over inheritance and influence.

Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales in Issoudun at three. Though tired, Madame Bridau felt revived by familiar sights and memories. News spread: Madame Hochon greeted Agathe like a daughter, clinging to that affection after years of loss.

Agathe met the reserved Monsieur Hochon and the household: the young men Baruch and François, and shy Adolphine, who noticed Joseph. Monsieur Hochon, miserly, remarked on Joseph’s recent hospital stay and hunger; the youths were struck by his looks. “This is my son, the painter; my good Joseph,” Agathe said proudly. Joseph’s blunt remark about resembling his father was misread by Madame Hochon as affection for Agathe. Baruch hurried off to spread the news, emphasizing Joseph’s striking appearance.

Townspeople quickly gossiped, likening Joseph to a brigand and airing disdain for artists; many planned to call on the Hochons that evening to satisfy curiosity about the Parisians.

Installed in modest attic rooms, Joseph contrasted his Parisian artistic life with the drab provincial existence. Dinner revealed the Hochons’ frugality—plain bouilli, hard eggs, scant accompaniments. Accustomed to better fare, Joseph was disappointed but tried to adapt. Agathe learned her childhood home had been sold to the Borniches, and Madame Hochon braced for an influx of visitors. Joseph noted the family’s strict ways and limited hospitality.

After dinner the women gathered in the salon. Agathe and Madame Hochon exchanged life stories—Agathe recounting hardships, Madame Hochon offering hard-won advice about endurance—continually interrupted by callers eager to meet the Parisians. Joseph remained detached, studying visitors with a critical eye that made them uneasy. Later, as the house quieted, the two women resumed their heartfelt exchange about the burdens they carried.

Elsewhere the Knights of Idleness convened to plot mischief against the Parisians. Max proposed a rat prank to sabotage merchant Fario’s grain; the gang welcomed the scheme as a way to drive the Bridaus away without harming the Hochons.

Next morning at breakfast Joseph glimpsed his brother Jean-Jacques Rouget, disheveled and with the conspicuous Flore Brazier. Joseph admired Flore’s beauty; Agathe feared her influence. A letter from Jean-Jacques inviting Joseph to dine arrived, which the Hochons received with mixed feelings, suspecting Flore’s involvement. Monsieur Hochon advised Joseph to observe closely and speak frankly with his brother about his nephews. With tensions simmering and rival schemes in motion, a fraught family reunion was imminent.

At four o’clock Joseph crossed from the Rouget to the Hochon house, where Kouski announced him. The table was set in the large hall; Joseph greeted his uncle Rouget, Flore, and Maxence Gilet with light banter, admired Flore and the house, and noted Max’s good looks. Baruch and Francois arrived and Flore suggested a walk before dinner. While she prepared, Joseph examined the house’s paintings, which Rouget said had come from the Descoings and been bought during the Revolution. Ecstatic, Joseph inspected more upstairs with Max. Flore urged Rouget to give the paintings to Joseph, arguing Rouget didn’t care for them.

Rouget offered the pictures to Joseph—without frames—and Flore proposed paying him four thousand francs to make copies. Overwhelmed, Joseph accepted. News spread through Issoudun and enhanced Flore’s standing. Dinner that night included local dignitaries. Joseph, somewhat tipsy, went home at eleven; Rouget, having drunk heavily, was carried to bed. Left alone, Max and Flore agreed it was better to befriend the Bridaus than antagonize them and planned to send the pictures to Joseph the next day.

The next morning the paintings were propped against Rouget’s wall. Agathe, Joseph’s mother, visited Rouget to thank him after Hochon urged her—he feared the Bridaus’ influence and warned that Joseph had been fooled. Hochon urged Agathe to cultivate Flore to gain access to Rouget. Agathe was warmly received; Rouget was ill from the previous night. Flore feigned servitude and Agathe hinted Rouget should marry her, but Flore insisted Rouget would remember her in his will.

In the following days the Knights of Idleness—local troublemakers—released rats and mice into Fario’s grain store, causing havoc. The Bridaus’ hold on Rouget seemed to strengthen: Flore claimed Rouget was too weak for walks and needed a carriage; they bought a shabby vehicle that drew town attention. Rouget signed a power of attorney granting Max authority over his investments, while Flore handled local transactions. Fario returned to find his store devastated and suspected Max’s involvement; he began gathering evidence against the Knights.

Agathe believed Rouget had not made a will and intended to ask him directly, while Hochon urged her to stay longer and use religious influence to counter Max and Flore—advice she reluctantly considered. Progress for the Bridaus was slow; Hochon stressed persistence.

Meanwhile Joseph, sensing the paintings’ real value, sent them to Desroches in Paris for appraisal, convinced they were worth far more than the 4,000-franc copying job. Agathe warned him to keep their worth quiet, but word leaked and searches for old artworks spread through Issoudun. Max regretted aspects of their scheme, now fearful of the Church’s influence if the paintings’ value became public.

Letters from Desroches and Schinner advised keeping Agathe in Issoudun, warned about Max, and suggested Rouget might already have a will—so the Bridaus’ prospects depended on a struggle between Flore and the Church. Disturbed, Joseph decided to return to Paris. He bade Rouget farewell, resolved to rely on his talent, and planned later to return the pictures to Rouget. The Hochon family suspected Max and Flore had known the paintings’ value and manipulated Joseph; Agathe defended her son, but Hochon warned that returning the paintings might jeopardize any chance at inheritance.

Between midnight and one the Knights of Idleness fed the town’s dogs; their expedition lasted until three. After supper at Cognette’s, Max, turning from rue l’Avenier into Grande rue at half-past four, was stabbed by Fario, who had lurked in a recess, fled toward Vilatte, and wiped the knife on his handkerchief. Fario then washed the handkerchief in the Rivière forcée and returned to his lodgings at Saint-Paterne, where his new watchman later found him in bed.

Max fell, crying out and alarming Lousteau-Prangin and young Goddet, who ran up the street calling for help; the townspeople, used to false alarms, stayed in bed. Max had fainted. He recognized Fario but, on regaining consciousness and seeing the wound was not fatal, decided to exploit the incident: “I think I recognized that cursed painter!” Lousteau-Prangin hurried to his father, the judge. Max was carried home by Cognette, Goddet, and others. Goddet, suspicious of Max’s words, asked Gritte if Joseph Bridau was in bed; she replied that Joseph had left at half-past four, restless all night. The revelation shocked the group, and they entered Rouget’s house to find Max in a bloody shirt.

Joseph had spent the night anxious about how the townspeople would view him, fearing they might think he had robbed his uncle. He longed to flee to Paris and confront Max; at dawn he walked to the poplar avenue toward Tivoli to steady his nerves. Max plotted revenge. After Goddet examined the wound and found it serious but not fatal, news spread and a crowd gathered at the place Saint-Jean. Max whispered to Flore that they could use the incident to rid themselves of the Parisians: pretend he was dying and have Joseph arrested. Flore, feigning tears, spread the story that Joseph had attacked Max out of jealousy over paintings. Incited by her, the crowd demanded Joseph’s capture; the mob grew hostile.

Monsieur Hochon, warned by Gritte, locked himself in, fearing for his safety and wealth. Madame Hochon, anxious for their daughter Agathe, hoped she would sleep through the shock. Agathe, however, woke, learned of the accusations, and fainted at the sight of the gendarmes. Monsieur Hochon urged the magistrates to act rapidly; the lieutenant reported a large crowd demanding justice.

Joseph was spotted returning home and nearly seized by the mob. Gendarmes intervened and, despite his protests of innocence, took him to the Palais for his safety. There Joseph calmly insisted on his innocence and provided an alibi, recounting his morning’s movements. The magistrates were somewhat swayed by his composure but kept him in custody while they verified his claims. Joseph wrote to his mother, reassuring her he would soon be free.

Max, recovering, remembered a crucial detail: the attacker had worn list shoes, while Joseph had left in boots. He wrote to the magistrate urging Joseph’s release. As the investigation progressed, witnesses confirmed Joseph’s alibi and he was freed. He returned to his mother’s warm relief; the ordeal strengthened their bond and Joseph felt grateful for the support.

The townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians as a victory; Max and Flore were pleased. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris; Joseph’s spirits improved, while Agathe fretted about her son Philippe, whose trial loomed. To escape stress, Joseph went to the Comte de Sérizy’s chateau to work.

Philippe, punished for actions during the unrest, was sentenced to five years’ surveillance and exiled to Autun. Desroches intervened, securing a change of residence to Issoudun and urging Philippe to reclaim his inheritance from Uncle Rouget. Motivated to restore the family’s fortunes and confront Max and the Rabouilleuse, Philippe prepared to return to Issoudun and, after a brief dinner, departed to face the challenge.

On All-Souls’ Day, November 2, Philippe Bridau registered at the Issoudun commissary and was told to stay on rue l’Avenier. His arrival, as a banished officer linked to a painter falsely accused in the affair, unnerved the town. Maxence Gilet, recently recovered, had converted all of Père Rouget’s mortgages into cash, alarming many. Monsieur Hochon, after consulting notary Héron—who thought Rouget’s heirs should thank him for preventing Gilet’s profit—resolved to protect the Bridaus, long resentful of Gilet and fearful of losing property.

Gilet and Flore Brazier welcomed Philippe, confident they could manipulate Rouget to secure his funds. Philippe presented himself to his uncle in tattered clothes; his disordered appearance showed hardship, yet he refused to beg. His blunt pride impressed Flore and Gilet. Gilet sensed a latent threat but avoided open conflict.

Philippe then visited the Hochons, meeting curiosity and fear—Madame Hochon especially linked him to past tragedies. Candid about living on little, he won some household members with charm and honesty. Requesting secrecy, he asked Monsieur Hochon for names of officers hostile to Gilet—Mignonnet and Carpentier. Befriending them, Philippe impressed them with knowledge of the recent conspiracy against the Bourbons and, by careful behavior, integrated into the local military circle while concealing his aims.

He dined regularly with Mignonnet and Carpentier, cultivated a respectable image, took fencing lessons and practiced shooting. His disdain for Gilet was evident, yet Gilet remained cautious.

Philippe’s inquiries led him to Fario, who informed him about the Knights of Idleness and their schemes. Suspecting Gilet and Flore planned to secure Rouget’s income and flee, Philippe urged Rouget to take walks to loosen Flore’s influence. He told Hochon that Gilet sought power of attorney to access Rouget’s funds; they agreed to watch Rouget’s movements. When Gilet proposed going to Vatan to secure the money and marry—Flore, fearful of Philippe’s influence, reluctantly consented—tensions rose as Philippe’s resolve to protect his family and restore their honor hardened, setting the stage for a dramatic town showdown.

As Philippe walked arm in arm with Rouget along the boulevard Baron, he contemplated the looming confrontation between Colonel Bridau and Captain Maxence Gilet—the town hoped it would settle the struggle over Rouget’s fortune. Philippe bluntly told Rouget that Flore loved Maxence and wanted Rouget out of the way so she could marry him. Though Rouget still loved her, Philippe insisted he could make her obedient again—by killing Maxence—if Rouget would refuse to sign the power of attorney they demanded before December third. That document, Philippe warned, would let them sell Rouget’s assets and flee to Paris. Rouget recoiled at Philippe taking Maxence’s place; Philippe answered that fear and force were the only ways to handle women.

They met Mignonnet and Carpentier, whom Philippe instructed to arrange the banquet seating so he could force a public confrontation with Gilet. They agreed but warned of Gilet’s skill as a duelist. Philippe reassured Rouget that after proper mourning he and Flore would soon be together.

At home Rouget found Kouski in tears: Flore had left with Vedie. Rouget’s frantic search failed. Gilet, sensing growing hostility over his conduct, felt pressure to act before a duel; Captain Potel urged him to face Bridau. Yet Gilet remained focused on securing Rouget’s fortune first, plotting to transfer assets to Flore to keep them from Philippe—believing the power of attorney would let him control events.

Aware of these moves, Philippe prepared to counter them, suspecting Gilet would seek reconciliation with Flore. He planned to use Baruch to secure the power of attorney on Rouget’s behalf while distracting or keeping Gilet at bay, determined to protect his uncle’s interests and return Flore to Rouget.

Rouget drafted a letter threatening to revoke his will in Flore’s favor unless she returned; Philippe encouraged it to force her back. Town gossip swelled about the impending duel and the estate’s fate. When Flore returned, Philippe asserted control and warned her of consequences if she disobeyed. Rouget, emotionally fragile, clung to her; by morning the town speculated on the duel, and Philippe, confident in his banquet plan, believed it would decide both Rouget’s fate and his own.

Around four the next afternoon officers of the old army gathered at the place du Marché in Issoudun for a coronation banquet, awaiting Colonel Philippe Bridau. Conversation turned to Max’s recent dismissal from old Rouget’s house; only Potel and Renard defended him, while an old sub‑lieutenant said Max should have resisted. When Max arrived he was coldly received—sharply contrasted with Philippe’s warm welcome—which stiffened Max’s resolve to confront him.

At dinner Philippe toasted the Grand Army and several soldiers echoed him. Mignonnet toasted those who had tried to restore the Emperor’s son, and Max, whose reputation lent weight, rose with an ironic wish to meet again next year. The meal ended tensely as Philippe and Max exchanged barbs: Philippe stayed composed while Max struggled to control himself. A duel was arranged for the next morning with sabres, Goddet, a former army surgeon, to attend.

That night Monsieur and Madame Hochon urged Philippe to pray; Madame Hochon offered a relic, which he declined, though she vowed to pray for him. The next morning both appeared composed, but Max felt intimidated by Philippe’s superior stance. Max attacked first but was quickly disarmed; Philippe paused to let him recover his weapon, increasing the strain. When they resumed they exchanged fierce blows; Philippe was wounded but delivered a fatal stroke. Max died—his ambitions and the Order of Idleness ended—which brought relief to the town.

Flore Brazier, devastated, fell ill with fever and inflammation. Philippe, recovering from his wounds, wrote letters outlining plans to secure his inheritance and manage Flore’s situation, noting her illness prevented her from fleeing and intending to use her to his advantage.

By February 1823, urged by others and influenced by Philippe’s suggestion that marriage would secure her future, a recovering Flore was persuaded to accept his arranged match. The contract granted her a substantial dower and a life annuity. Agathe, Philippe’s mother, delighted in his apparent reform and the town’s approval, but the family lawyer Desroches warned that Philippe’s cunning would likely keep Rouget’s property from Flore and Joseph.

Once married, Philippe asserted control: demanding a power of attorney from Rouget, using Flore to manage her uncle’s affairs while keeping her under close watch, and seeking to reestablish himself in the army and in Paris. Backed by local endorsements and letters to the minister of war, he was reinstated as lieutenant‑colonel.

Lieutenant-Colonel Philippe Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, whom he took to the Treasury to transfer his uncle’s income to himself. The old man and the Rabouilleuse were soon absorbed into extravagant Parisian life; Rouget overindulged after a lavish supper with the dancer Lolotte and died, reportedly of indigestion.

Madame Rouget prospered in Paris, though Philippe kept Mariette to watch her. In October 1823 Philippe went to Issoudun to settle his uncle’s estate, quickly amassing 1,600,000 francs (excluding valuable paintings held by Monsieur Hochon), and entrusted the funds to Mongenod and Sons.

Philippe married and legally protected his wife’s fortune should she die childless. He bought her a furnished apartment and secretly acquired a grand hôtel on rue de Clichy, lavishing money on renovations and furnishings. The rise of Charles X benefited the Duc de Chaulieu’s circle; because Philippe supplied information about past conspiracies he was appointed lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse’s regiment and, through court connections, ennobled as Comte de Brambourg.

Ambitious and insatiable, Philippe sought further honors—aide-de-camp to the Dauphin and ultimately a general’s rank—distancing himself from old friends who might jeopardize his ascent. He planned to marry Mademoiselle Amélie de Soulanges and secure military distinctions to cement his status.

Meanwhile Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist, supported by his mother Agathe, who ran a lottery office. Despite talent, Joseph endured poverty and criticism while Philippe’s success overshadowed him. Agathe, deceived by appearances, accepted Philippe’s neglect; a cold reply to her plea for help devastated her and hastened her decline. Near death she realized the contrast between her sons and died; Joseph buried her alone, heartbroken.

Joseph found solace among friends. When Flore, Philippe’s wife, later begged for help in destitution, Joseph and Bixiou arranged care and plotted to expose Philippe’s abandonment. Philippe continued to rise until his fortunes collapsed in the July Revolution of 1830. Ultimately Joseph inherited Philippe’s estate and achieved success in art, becoming Comte de Brambourg; he remained humble, often reflecting on his family’s tragedy.
