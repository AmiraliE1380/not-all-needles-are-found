I. EARLY MISTAKES

On a Sunday morning in April 1813 a luxurious cabriolet drew up before the Tuileries, carrying an anxious, aged father and his beautiful daughter, Julie. He prided himself on the attention she drew as they walked through the gardens; she was eager to see the military review commanded by Napoleon, the last before a disastrous campaign. They reached the gates to disappointment; the review had not begun, and he suggested leaving. Julie insisted on staying, afraid of missing the Emperor. An officer recognized her and admitted them to a prime vantage point. The spectacle was grand: the Old Guard prepared, the crowd buzzed, and anticipation mounted for Napoleon’s arrival.

When Napoleon appeared, cheers arose. Julie was captivated, but her attention soon fixed on a young officer, Colonel Victor d’Aiglemont, on the Emperor’s staff. Seeing him among others, she felt a pang of jealousy and an unexpected attraction, while her father’s anxious manner reminded her of their precarious situation. As the review ended, her father warned her about future happiness, fearing youthful dreams could lead to sorrow. He advised against falling for a man like Victor, whom he deemed unworthy. Despite his cautions, Julie felt drawn to Victor and their attachment deepened.

Nearly a year later, in March 1814, Julie and Victor were traveling together. The once vibrant girl had become a shadow; her health faltered as Victor, now a colonel, was absorbed in military duties, leaving her isolated. Their relationship strained; her yearning for love and connection grew. During their travels they met an Englishman, Arthur, detained in France. Julie felt a strange kinship with him; Arthur’s quiet admiration contrasted sharply with Victor’s indifference, and she found herself drawn to Arthur’s gentleness. Despite growing feelings, Julie remained bound by her marriage. Torn between duty and desire, she wrestled with emotions that deepened her inner conflict.

As Napoleon fell and the political landscape shifted, Victor’s ambitions changed; he became more involved with the new regime. Julie continued to think of Arthur, who lingered in her thoughts as a steady presence. The relationship reached crisis when Victor’s infidelities surfaced. Julie’s heartache deepened as she grasped his selfishness. In desperation she sought solace in Arthur, though the weight of her choices pressed upon her.

Over time Julie’s health declined further and she became increasingly isolated. The once vibrant woman was now a mere shadow trapped in a loveless marriage. Her longing for connection remained unfulfilled, and the hard reality of her situation closed in. At last, in a moment of clarity, Julie resolved to take control of her life. She refused to remain passive. With newfound determination she sought to reclaim her happiness, even at the cost of defying societal expectations.

Julie’s journey becomes a poignant exploration of love, sacrifice, and the complexities of womanhood, forcing her to confront the choices that define her existence.

Between the Seine and the Loing lies a flat, dreary country bordered by the Forest of Fontainebleau, with towns like Moret, Montereau, and Nemours marking its southern limits. The landscape presents a monotonous horizon where melancholy seems to thrive. In the center of this plain stands the old château of Saint-Lange, surrounded by avenues and gardens, a remnant of diminished wealth.

In late 1820 a young woman, known in Paris for her beauty and wit, arrived at Saint-Lange, startling villagers who had not seen gentry in years. The Marquise d’Aiglemont’s presence provoked curiosity, especially as she seemed frail and withdrawn; locals speculated that her husband’s gambling in Spain had caused financial ruin and that the estate might be sold, prompting them to consider their own interests.

The Marquise remained isolated, rarely leaving her room and only dining briefly with her daughter, whom she could not emotionally reach. Her profound sorrow and silence prompted gossip, but interest waned, leaving her to solitude. At twenty-six she was grappling with an unacknowledged grief that had extinguished maternal instincts and left her feeling like a ghost in her own life.

Her anguish sprang from a lost love, a man who had died to preserve her honor. Bound by social expectations, she felt unable to express her pain, producing a deep internal conflict. Her beauty and youth felt meaningless as she was haunted by the absence of true love and the joy it might have brought. She reflected on her past and recognized the emptiness of her marriage, which, while socially acceptable, lacked the emotional fulfillment she desired.

Sitting in her room, the flat horizon mirrored her desolation. Seasons changed but her heart remained heavy with unfulfilled longing; she felt trapped in a life offering no hope or joy, her thoughts consumed by what might have been. Her internal struggle was compounded by guilt and the pressure to maintain a façade of happiness.

One day the village priest, M. le Curé, visited, sensing her suffering. He spoke of the universal nature of grief and the importance of faith, but she initially dismissed his comfort, feeling her pain unique and insurmountable. The priest told of a father who lost all his sons in battle, emphasizing resilience in despair. This story resonated, and she found some solace in the idea that others had endured similar suffering.

Gradually she opened up to the priest, revealing feelings of isolation and the emptiness of her motherhood. She felt disconnected from her daughter Hélène, whom she saw as a reminder of her lost love. The Marquise believed true motherhood required a deep emotional bond she lacked; her relationship with Hélène was marked by duty rather than affection, deepening her despair.

The priest encouraged her to find strength in her maternal role, but she struggled to reconcile her inadequacy. She lamented the societal constraints dictating her life, feeling trapped in a marriage that resembled a prison. Her bitterness toward norms grew as she recognized the hypocrisy of a system that forced women into roles that stifled their true selves.

With spring came some distraction: managing the estate offered a temporary reprieve from grief. Yet her emotional turmoil remained unresolved. By the time she left Saint-Lange in October she had regained some vitality, though the scars of sorrow lingered. She encountered the curé once more, but her avoidance of him signaled a desire to move on from the painful memories tied to the château.

The Marquise d’Aiglemont’s journey through grief and isolation reflects the struggle of a woman caught between societal expectations and her desires: a story of profound loss, the search for identity, and the complexities of motherhood in a rigidly structured society.

Madame Firmiani hosted a ball where M. Charles de Vandenesse, a promising young diplomat, came to take leave of Paris before his appointment to the Congress of Laybach. Having enjoyed Parisian pleasures yet accustomed to a transient life, he felt little regret. Near thirty, philosophical about existence, he favored ambition over romance and regarded the social scene as largely superficial and full of empty intrigues.

Surveying the assembly, he noted the elite’s emptiness: men and women seemed devoid of genuine feeling, engaged in trivial pursuits. His reflections were interrupted when Madame Firmiani introduced him to the enigmatic Marquise d’Aiglemont, a woman wrapped in mystery and known for past attachments. Vandenesse was intrigued by her beauty and the depth of her character, which contrasted sharply with the surrounding frivolity.

The Marquise, also thirty, possessed a delicate beauty and an air of melancholy that drew him in. Her demeanor suggested unfulfilled dreams and resignation. He perceived a stark disparity between her sensibility and her husband, a dull man who had risen by her influence. Beneath her outward calm, Vandenesse sensed profound sadness, which deepened his interest.

Their conversation shifted from light banter to themes of love and feeling, revealing a connection that excited and troubled him. He felt captivated, torn between admiration and longing, and before leaving he asked to visit again, drawn to her despite the complications of her past.

In the days that followed, the Marquise occupied his thoughts. He oscillated between hope and doubt about her feelings and decided to see her. Their meetings mixed friendship and unspoken desire, with Vandenesse wrestling over the implications of affection for a married woman.

She, too, was drawn to him but struggled with loyalty to the memory of a deceased lover. Aware of societal constraints, her heart was split between the past and the possibility of new love. Though reserved, she could not deny their bond.

As their relationship deepened, obstacles became clearer. Vandenesse admired the Marquise’s strength yet felt the weight of her history. Their love blossomed amid social expectations and personal memory, producing moments of joy and anguish. One evening a tender kiss marked a turning point, but the intrusion of her husband shattered the moment and forced them to confront reality. Their connection attested to love’s power to transcend social bounds, yet remained entangled with unfulfilled desire and the haunting presence of the past, leaving both to navigate treacherous waters of love and convention.

Between the Barrière d’Italie and the Barrière de la Santé, the boulevard toward the Jardin des Plantes opens a view of Paris able to stir the jaded. From an elevated point a tree-lined avenue descends into a valley of factories set among greenery and the brown streams of the Bièvre. Across the slope, the squalor of the Faubourg Saint-Marceau is contrasted by the majestic Panthéon and the somber dome of the Val-de-Grâce towering over dense rooftops and poplars. To the left the observatory’s windows throw odd shadows; beyond, the lantern tower of the Invalides rises between the Luxembourg and Saint-Sulpice. Architecture and foliage merge and shift with the light.

To the right the canal Saint-Martin runs along reddish quays shaded by lime trees, with Romanesque granaries on its banks. Beyond, the smoke-streaked slopes of Belleville, dotted with houses and windmills, blend into the clouds. Between that horizon and the valley’s roofs lies a vast, veiled city alive with sounds that assert its presence. When sunlight pours over this portion of Paris it transforms windows and tiles and suffuses the scene with a magical atmosphere: an idyll of human and natural life. From the cypresses of Père-Lachaise the whole capital seems at peace.

One morning, while admiring this view and thinking bitterly of those wealthy wanderers who scorn their homeland, my reverie was broken by the sound of a kiss. A young woman, simply elegant, stood with a handsome young man and a charming little boy; their joy was evident in movement and expression. A second child — a little girl — stood apart. Dark-haired and serious, perhaps seven or eight, she watched with a gravity that suggested premature maturity. The boy, fair and carefree, contrasted with her. Their clothes were alike, but the girl’s plainer collar hinted at some maternal preference.

The boy offered his sister a small toy; she scowled, and an uneasy complexity showed on her face. Their interaction, indifferent at first, turned hostile. When the little boy advanced toward her she pushed him away; he stumbled, fell into the river and sank beneath the murky water. The scene happened with terrifying swiftness. I rushed to help but it was too late. The mother arrived frantic; nothing could be done. The loss imprinted itself on her — the grief she would carry home — and the darker implication of the daughter’s action hung in silence, haunting the family.

Years later, the Marquis de Vandenesse, now mourning his father, entertained a notary at dinner. The notary, oblivious to social cues, monopolized the conversation. The Marquise, impatient to go to the theatre with her children, and her husband dropped hints to end the visit, but the notary persisted, blind to the couple’s desire to leave. As nine o’clock struck the Marquis interrupted, insisting the notary go so he might escort his wife. The notary still failed to comprehend, prolonging his talk and making misunderstandings worse.

At that moment the Marquise’s husband returned unexpectedly with the children, who looked upset: the little girl’s eyes were red from crying; the boy was fretful. They had come back from a play that had distressed them. The Marquise’s face hardened as she realized the nature of their agitation. The notary, attempting to be jocular and unaware of the emotional weight in the room, only inflamed the situation with crude remarks about family dynamics and parental preference.

Overwhelmed, the Marquise sent her daughter away. The notary, continuing in his tone-deaf way, still spoke until the Marquis, unable to bear it, demanded he leave. Confused and flustered, the notary withdrew. Later, in reflection, he failed to grasp the real cause of the evening’s strain, attributing the episode to aristocratic sensitivities. His wife, seeing the truth, warned him to restrict himself to business in future dealings. The notary’s inability to perceive social nuance promised further misunderstandings in his professional life.

One of Napoleon’s orderly staff-officers, known as the General or the Marquis, spent the spring at Versailles and, having amassed a fortune during the Restoration, took a country house near Montreuil for solitude close to Paris. The house, once a retreat for a nobleman’s fleeting romances, had expansive grounds; its entrance still faced the street, a reminder of a once-quiet lane.

One winter evening the family was alone; the servants had gone to Versailles for a wedding. It was Christmas time, and as the revelers lingered, the General felt uneasy when the clock struck eleven with no sign of their return. Outside, silence lay over the countryside, broken only by the wind and distant footsteps.

Inside, the family enjoyed a cozy evening. The General sat relaxed in a high-back armchair by the fire, watching his youngest son resist his mother’s attempts to undress him and prefer to play with his sister. Their laughter filled the room; the Marquise, beautiful and content, watched them with affection. The General, a rugged soldier, took joy in their innocence and the warmth of family life.

At a nearby table their thirteen-year-old son read the Arabian Nights, absorbed in the tale. His sister, Helene, worked on a tapestry; striking in beauty and thoughtful in demeanor, she contrasted with the younger children’s play. The family’s dynamic mixed childlike play with older, more serious reflection.

As the evening wore on, the General expressed concern over the absent servants, and the Marquise, sensing tension, urged their son to bed. Then a series of hurried knocks echoed from the street, signaling urgency. The General, armed and cautious, answered the door to find a breathless man seeking refuge, claiming he was being pursued.

The stranger, desperate and disheveled with bloodied hands, insisted on secrecy and water. Despite hesitation, the General agreed to shelter him, feeling an inexplicable pull toward the man. He led the stranger to a hidden room, torn between unease and obligation.

The Marquise, sensing something amiss, questioned her husband about the newcomer, but the General urged secrecy. Helene, curious and concerned, overheard their low conversation and felt compelled to investigate. Approaching the locked room, she was struck by the stranger’s presence: despite his grim state he exuded a magnetic power. Their eyes met and an unspoken connection formed, leaving Helene intrigued and unsettled. She returned to the family with her mind racing.

As the night advanced, the General’s thoughts were consumed by the implications of harboring a fugitive. Tension in the household grew; the Marquise, noting her daughter’s distraction, pressed Helene to explain. Torn between loyalty to her family and fascination with the stranger, Helene struggled to articulate her feelings.

The General, now fully aware of the danger the stranger’s presence invited, resolved to protect his family at all costs. He demanded answers about the servants’ delay and called for them to return, his anger reflecting the stakes. Gendarmes soon arrived seeking the fugitive, and the General prepared to confront them, his protectiveness surging. The stranger remained silent, contemplating his next move as the household braced for confrontation.

In the ensuing chaos Helene’s feelings for the stranger deepened, forcing her to question her desires and the life she had known. The night culminated in a dramatic encounter that compelled the General to face the moral complexities of his hospitality and its consequences for his family.

Ultimately, the General’s decision to shelter the fugitive had unforeseen repercussions: Helene’s connection to the stranger altered the course of their lives. What began as an idyllic evening became a night of turmoil, exposing the fragility of their happiness and the weight of the choices they had made.

It was a June day in 1844 when the Marquise d’Aiglemont, a woman of about fifty, walked in the garden of her daughter’s Paris mansion. She lingered by a row of windows, sitting to gaze at the dome of the Invalides and the family grounds. The neighborhood was quiet; the aristocracy was only beginning to stir.

Having lost her two sons, Gustave and Abel, the Marquise had given her fortune to her daughter, the Comtesse Moïna de Saint-Héreen. Her affection for Moïna was profound, a contrast to the faint memories she retained of her deceased children. Society regarded the Marquise’s devotion as a kind of superstition and largely overlooked both her past and her coolness toward her daughter-in-law.

Despite her sacrifices, the Marquise was often alone; Moïna was absorbed in her social life. Her love for her daughter was evident but shaded by sadness and a sense of loss. Gossip suggested she might regret giving up her fortune, yet most praised her devotion. When she did appear in society she felt eclipsed by Moïna’s youthful charm.

As she sat in the garden the Marquise reflected on her life; her face showed experience and beauty faded by sorrow and time. She was aware of Moïna’s flirtation with Alfred de Vandenesse, a man she regarded with disdain, and feared it could end in heartbreak.

The Comte de Saint-Héreen had been away for months, and Moïna’s flirtation seemed perilous. Feeling powerless, the Marquise knew her warnings would likely be ignored; her heart ached at the thought of her daughter on a precipice she could not prevent.

In a moment of despair she recalled the small incidents that had accumulated over the years, each marking Moïna’s growing distance. She went to Moïna’s room hoping to speak, only to meet indifference. The Countess’s dismissive attitude stung, and the Marquise felt the weight of contempt.

When she finally confronted Moïna their conversation grew tense. The Marquise warned of the risks posed by Alfred; Moïna’s flippant replies deepened her sorrow. Leaving the room, the Marquise felt a physical pain in her heart, realizing her daughter had lost respect for her.

Outside, overwhelmed by grief and the sense of irrevocable estrangement, she collapsed on a bench. Moïna, unaware of the gravity of the situation, believed she could repair things with a few kind gestures. The Marquise’s condition worsened; she was carried inside, lifeless.

Servants rushed for a physician, but the Marquise’s fate was sealed. Struck by remorse as she watched her mother decline, Moïna suddenly grasped how she had taken that love for granted. It was too late for reconciliation. As she wept, the Marquise, in her final moments, managed a smile at her daughter — a testament to a mother’s enduring love and forgiveness. Moïna stood before her family hollow with regret, realizing the depth of her mother’s sacrifice.
