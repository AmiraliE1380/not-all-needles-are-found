A WOMAN OF THIRTY

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Ellen Marriage

DEDICATION

To Louis Boulanger, Painter.

A WOMAN OF THIRTY

I. EARLY MISTAKES

On a Sunday morning in April 1813, a luxurious cabriolet pulled up in front of the Tuileries, carrying a father and his beautiful daughter, Julie. The father, looking anxious and aged, was proud of the attention his daughter attracted. As they walked through the gardens, Julie was eager to see the military review commanded by Napoleon, which was to be the last of its kind before a disastrous campaign. The father noted her impatience and felt a mix of joy and sorrow for her fleeting happiness.

As they approached the gates, they were met with disappointment; the review had not yet begun, and the father suggested they leave. Julie, however, was determined to stay, fearing she might miss seeing the Emperor. An officer recognized her and allowed them entry, leading them to a prime viewing spot. The spectacle was grand, with the Old Guard preparing for the review, and the atmosphere was charged with anticipation as the crowd awaited Napoleon's arrival.

When Napoleon finally appeared, the crowd erupted in cheers. Julie was captivated by the scene, but her attention soon shifted to a young officer, Colonel Victor d’Aiglemont, who was part of the Emperor's staff. She felt a pang of jealousy as she watched him interact with others, but her father’s anxious demeanor reminded her of the weight of their situation.

As the review concluded, Julie's father expressed his concerns about her future happiness, fearing that her youthful dreams might lead to disappointment. He warned her against falling in love with a man like Victor, whom he deemed unworthy. Despite her father's warnings, Julie felt drawn to Victor, and their connection deepened.

Nearly a year later, in March 1814, Julie and Victor were traveling together. The once vibrant and carefree Julie had become a shadow of her former self, her health deteriorating. Victor, now a colonel, was preoccupied with his military duties, leaving Julie feeling isolated. Their relationship was strained, and Julie's longing for love and connection grew.

As they traveled, they encountered an Englishman, Arthur, who had been detained in France. Julie felt a strange connection to him, and their paths crossed frequently. Arthur's quiet admiration for her contrasted sharply with Victor's indifference, and Julie found herself drawn to his gentleness.

Despite her growing feelings for Arthur, Julie remained committed to her marriage. She struggled with her emotions, torn between her duty as a wife and her desire for love. The tension between her responsibilities and her heart's yearnings created a deep inner conflict.

As the political landscape shifted with the fall of Napoleon, Julie's life took another turn. Victor's ambitions grew, and he became more involved in the new regime. Julie, meanwhile, continued to grapple with her feelings for Arthur, who remained a constant presence in her thoughts.

Their relationship reached a breaking point when Victor's infidelities became apparent. Julie's heartache deepened as she realized the extent of her husband's selfishness. In a moment of desperation, she sought solace in Arthur, but the weight of her choices loomed over her.

As time passed, Julie's health continued to decline, and she found herself increasingly isolated. The once vibrant woman was now a mere shadow, trapped in a loveless marriage. Her longing for connection and love remained unfulfilled, and she faced the harsh reality of her situation.

In a moment of clarity, Julie resolved to take control of her life. She would no longer be a passive participant in her own story. With newfound determination, she sought to reclaim her happiness, even if it meant defying societal expectations.

As the narrative unfolds, Julie's journey becomes a poignant exploration of love, sacrifice, and the complexities of womanhood. Her struggles resonate with the timeless themes of desire, duty, and the quest for personal fulfillment, ultimately leading her to confront the choices that define her existence.

Between the Seine and the Loing lies a flat, dreary country bordered by the Forest of Fontainebleau, with towns like Moret, Montereau, and Nemours marking its southern limits. The landscape is characterized by its monotonous horizon, where melancholy seems to thrive. In the center of this plain stands the old chateau of Saint-Lange, surrounded by stately avenues and gardens, a remnant of wealth now diminished.

In late 1820, a young woman, well-known in Paris for her beauty and wit, arrived at Saint-Lange, shocking the local villagers who had not seen gentry in years. The Marquise d’Aiglemont’s presence stirred curiosity, especially as she appeared frail and withdrawn, leading villagers to speculate about her financial ruin due to her husband’s gambling in Spain. They anticipated the estate might be sold off, prompting them to consider their own financial interests.

The Marquise, however, remained isolated, rarely leaving her room and only dining briefly with her daughter, whom she seemed unable to connect with. Her profound sorrow and silence led to speculation among the locals, but as time passed, their interest waned, leaving her to her solitude. At twenty-six, she was grappling with a deep, unacknowledged grief that had extinguished her maternal instincts and left her feeling like a ghost in her own life.

Her anguish stemmed from a lost love, a man who had died to preserve her honor. Bound by societal expectations, she felt she could not express her pain, leading to a profound internal conflict. The Marquise’s beauty and youth felt meaningless to her, as she was haunted by the absence of true love and the joy it could have brought. She reflected on her past, recognizing the emptiness of her marriage, which, while socially acceptable, was devoid of the emotional fulfillment she craved.

As she sat in her room, the flat horizon mirrored her desolation. The seasons changed, but her heart remained heavy with unfulfilled longing. She felt trapped in a life that offered no hope or joy, her thoughts consumed by the memory of what could have been. The Marquise’s internal struggle was compounded by her sense of guilt and the societal pressures that demanded she maintain a façade of happiness.

One day, the village priest, M. le Curé, visited her, sensing her suffering. He spoke of the universal nature of grief and the importance of faith, but she dismissed his attempts at comfort, feeling that her pain was unique and insurmountable. The priest shared a story of a father who lost all his sons in battle, emphasizing resilience in the face of despair. This resonated with the Marquise, who found solace in the idea that others had endured similar suffering.

Despite her initial resistance, she began to open up to the priest, revealing her feelings of isolation and the emptiness of her motherhood. She felt disconnected from her daughter, Helene, whom she viewed as a reminder of her lost love. The Marquise expressed her belief that true motherhood required a deep emotional bond, which she felt she lacked. Her relationship with Helene was marked by a sense of duty rather than genuine affection, leading to further despair.

The priest encouraged her to find strength in her role as a mother, but she struggled to reconcile her feelings of inadequacy. She lamented the societal constraints that dictated her life, feeling trapped in a marriage that felt more like a prison than a partnership. The Marquise’s bitterness towards societal norms grew, as she recognized the hypocrisy of a system that forced women into roles that stifled their true selves.

As spring arrived, the Marquise found some distraction in managing her estate, which provided a temporary reprieve from her grief. However, her emotional turmoil remained unresolved. When she left Saint-Lange in October, she had regained some vitality, but the scars of her sorrow lingered. She encountered the curé one last time, but her avoidance of him signified her desire to move on from the painful memories associated with her time at the chateau.

In summary, the Marquise d’Aiglemont’s journey through grief and isolation reflects the struggles of a woman caught between societal expectations and her own desires. Her story is one of profound loss, the search for identity, and the complexities of motherhood, ultimately highlighting the challenges faced by women in a rigidly structured society.

Madame Firmiani was hosting a ball, and M. Charles de Vandenesse, a promising young diplomat, attended to bid farewell to Paris before his appointment at the Congress of Laybach. Although he had enjoyed the city's pleasures, he felt little regret, having grown accustomed to the transient nature of his life. At nearly thirty, he viewed life philosophically, prioritizing ambition over romantic entanglements, and found the social scene filled with superficiality and empty intrigues.

As he surveyed the ball, he reflected on the emptiness of the Parisian elite, noting that the women and men present seemed devoid of genuine emotion, caught in a cycle of trivial pursuits. His thoughts were interrupted when Madame Firmiani introduced him to the enigmatic Marquise d’Aiglemont, a woman shrouded in mystery and known for her past romantic entanglements. Intrigued, Vandenesse was struck by her beauty and the depth of her character, which contrasted sharply with the frivolity around him.

The Marquise, at thirty, possessed a delicate beauty and an air of melancholy that drew Vandenesse in. Her demeanor suggested a life filled with unfulfilled dreams and a resignation to her fate. He recognized the disparity between her and her husband, a dull man who had risen to prominence through her influence. Despite her outward calm, Vandenesse sensed a profound sadness within her, which only deepened his interest.

Their conversation flowed from light banter to deeper themes of love and sentiment, revealing a connection that both excited and troubled Vandenesse. He found himself captivated by her, feeling a mix of admiration and longing. As he prepared to leave, he requested to visit her again, feeling a strong pull towards her despite the complexities of her past.

In the days that followed, Vandenesse's thoughts were consumed by the Marquise. He oscillated between hope and doubt regarding her feelings for him, ultimately deciding to visit her. Their meetings became a blend of friendship and unspoken desire, with Vandenesse grappling with the implications of his growing affection for a married woman.

The Marquise, too, found herself drawn to Vandenesse, yet she struggled with her loyalty to the memory of her deceased lover. She was aware of the societal constraints that bound them, and her heart was torn between the past and the possibility of new love. Despite her reservations, she could not deny the connection they shared.

As their relationship deepened, Vandenesse became increasingly aware of the challenges they faced. He admired the Marquise's strength and resilience, yet he also felt the weight of her past. Their love blossomed amidst the tension of societal expectations and personal histories, leading to moments of both joy and anguish.

One evening, as they shared a quiet moment, their emotions culminated in a tender kiss, marking a turning point in their relationship. However, the intrusion of the Marquise's husband shattered the moment, forcing them to confront the reality of their situation. Vandenesse's ambitions and the Marquise's loyalty to her memories created a complex web of desire and duty, leaving them both to navigate the treacherous waters of love and societal norms.

In the end, their connection was a testament to the power of love, capable of transcending the boundaries set by society, yet fraught with the dangers of unfulfilled desires and the haunting presence of the past.

Between the Barrière d’Italie and the Barrière de la Santé, the boulevard leading to the Jardin des Plantes offers a breathtaking view of Paris that can inspire even the most jaded observer. From a higher vantage point, the boulevard, lined with tall trees, reveals a deep valley filled with factories nestled among greenery and the brown streams of the Bièvre.

On the opposite slope, the Faubourg Saint-Marceau's squalor contrasts sharply with the majestic cupola of the Panthéon and the somber dome of the Val-du-Grace, which rise above a densely packed town. The two monuments appear as giants overshadowing the small houses and tall poplars below. To the left, the observatory stands out, its windows casting strange shadows, while in the distance, the elegant lantern tower of the Invalides rises between the Luxembourg and Saint-Sulpice. The architecture blends with the surrounding foliage, shifting with the changing light and sky. 

To the right, the canal Saint-Martin stretches out, bordered by reddish stone quays and lime trees, with public granaries in a Roman style lining its banks. Beyond, the smoke-laden slopes of Belleville are dotted with houses and windmills, merging with the clouds. Between this horizon and the valley's rooftops lies a vast city, hidden yet alive, echoing with the sounds of life, as if to declare its presence.

When sunlight floods this part of Paris, it transforms the landscape, illuminating windows, brightening tiles, and creating a magical atmosphere. The scene is idyllic, filled with the sounds of humanity and nature, a harmonious blend that captivates the soul. Beneath the cypress trees of Pere-Lachaise, the entire capital lies in peaceful splendor.

One morning, as I admired this beautiful view, I reflected bitterly on the disdain some express for our homeland, lamenting the wealthy who travel abroad only to mock France. I gazed lovingly at modern Paris, lost in thought until the sound of a kiss interrupted my reverie. I spotted a young woman, dressed simply yet elegantly, with a handsome young man and a charming little boy. Their joy was palpable, and their connection was evident in their movements and expressions. 

A second child, a little girl, stood apart, her demeanor suggesting a deeper, more troubled nature. As I observed the family, I noted the stark contrast between the siblings. The girl, perhaps seven or eight, had dark hair and a serious expression, while her younger brother was fair and carefree. Their clothing was similar, but the girl's plain collar hinted at a hidden preference from their mother. 

The boy approached his sister with a small toy, but she scowled, revealing a complexity in her character that belied her age. Her gaze was intense, suggesting a burden of thought too heavy for a child. I was drawn to her, intrigued by the shadows of maturity in her young face. 

As the young couple continued their playful interaction, the little girl’s expression shifted from indifference to something darker. When her brother called out to her, she reacted with hostility, pushing him away. In a tragic moment, he stumbled and fell into the river, disappearing beneath the murky water. The scene unfolded with terrifying speed, and I rushed to help, but it was too late. The mother arrived, frantic, but there was no hope of saving the child. 

The incident left a haunting mark on the mother, who would have to face the consequences of this tragedy upon returning home. The weight of her grief and the implications of her daughter’s actions loomed large, a silent witness to the unfolding drama.

Years later, the Marquis de Vandenesse, now in mourning for his father, hosted a dinner with a notary present. The notary, oblivious to the tension in the room, monopolized the conversation, unaware of the Marquise’s impatience to leave and join her children at the theater. Despite the couple’s subtle hints to end the discussion, the notary continued, oblivious to their discomfort.

As the clock struck nine, the Marquis finally interrupted, insisting the notary leave so he could escort his wife. The notary, still unaware of the gravity of the situation, continued to speak, leading to a frustrating exchange. The Marquis, exasperated, attempted to correct the notary’s misunderstandings, but the atmosphere grew increasingly tense.

Just then, the Marquise’s husband returned unexpectedly with their children, who appeared upset. The little girl’s eyes were red from crying, and the boy was fretful. The Marquise’s concern deepened as she realized the children’s distress was linked to the play they had attended. The boy recounted a scene from the theater, revealing the source of their sudden return.

The Marquise’s face hardened as she processed the implications of her daughter’s emotional state. The notary, attempting to lighten the mood, inadvertently exacerbated the situation with his comments about family dynamics and preferences, which only heightened the tension in the room.

The Marquise, overwhelmed, sent her daughter away, while the notary continued to speak, oblivious to the gravity of his words. The Marquis, unable to contain his anger, confronted the notary, demanding he leave. The notary, confused and flustered, exited, leaving the couple to grapple with the emotional fallout of the evening.

As the notary reflected on the events, he failed to grasp the underlying issues at play, attributing the tension to the whims of the aristocracy. His wife, however, recognized the truth of the situation, advising him to stick to business in future interactions. The notary’s inability to understand the nuances of social dynamics would likely lead to further misunderstandings in his professional life.

V. TWO MEETINGS

One of Napoleon’s orderly staff-officers, referred to as the General or the Marquis, spent the spring at Versailles. He had amassed a fortune during the Restoration and, due to his court duties, took a country house near Montreuil, allowing him solitude close to Paris. The house, originally a retreat for a nobleman’s fleeting romances, had expansive grounds, yet its entrance faced the street, suggesting a once-quiet road.

One winter evening, the family was alone in the house, as the servants had gone to Versailles for a wedding. It was Christmas time, and the revelers lingered, causing the General to feel some concern as the clock struck eleven without their return. Outside, a deep silence enveloped the countryside, broken only by the wind and the sounds of distant footsteps.

Inside, the family enjoyed a cozy evening. The General, relaxed in a high-back armchair by the fire, watched his youngest son, who resisted his mother’s attempts to undress him, preferring to play with his sister. The children’s laughter filled the room, and their mother, beautiful and content, observed them with affection. The General, a rugged soldier, found joy in their innocence, embodying the warmth of family life.

At a nearby table, their thirteen-year-old son read the _Arabian Nights_, absorbed in the story. His sister, Helene, worked on a tapestry, her beauty striking and her demeanor thoughtful. The family’s dynamic was rich, with the children’s play contrasting with the older siblings’ more serious reflections.

As the evening progressed, the General expressed concern over the absence of the servants, while the Marquise, sensing tension, urged their son to go to bed. Just then, a series of hurried knocks echoed from the street, signaling an urgent situation. The General, armed and cautious, answered the door to find a breathless man seeking refuge, claiming he was being pursued.

The stranger, desperate and disheveled, insisted on his need for secrecy and water. The General, despite his initial hesitation, agreed to shelter him, feeling an inexplicable pull towards the man. The stranger, revealing bloodied hands, explained he had been involved in a duel. As the General led him to a hidden room, he felt a mix of unease and obligation.

Meanwhile, the Marquise sensed something was amiss and questioned her husband about the stranger. The General, maintaining his promise of secrecy, urged his family to remain unaware of the situation. However, Helene, curious and concerned, overheard their conversation and felt compelled to investigate.

When Helene approached the locked room, she was struck by the presence of the stranger, who, despite his grim circumstances, exuded a magnetic power. Their eyes met, and an unspoken connection formed, leaving Helene both intrigued and unsettled. She returned to her family, her mind racing with thoughts of the man she had just encountered.

As the night wore on, the General’s thoughts were consumed by the implications of harboring a fugitive. The tension in the household grew palpable, and the Marquise, sensing her daughter’s distraction, pressed her to explain her feelings. Helene, torn between loyalty to her family and her fascination with the stranger, struggled to articulate her emotions.

The General, now fully aware of the danger posed by the stranger’s presence, resolved to protect his family at all costs. He called for the servants to return, his anger boiling over as he demanded answers about their delay. The atmosphere in the house shifted, filled with a sense of impending crisis.

As the General prepared to confront the gendarmes who had arrived seeking the fugitive, he felt a surge of protectiveness for his family. The stranger, aware of the growing tension, remained silent, contemplating his next move. The General’s resolve hardened as he faced the reality of the situation, determined to shield his loved ones from the consequences of his decision to offer sanctuary.

In the ensuing chaos, Helene’s feelings for the stranger deepened, leading her to question her own desires and the life she had known. The night culminated in a dramatic confrontation, forcing the General to confront the moral complexities of his actions and the impact they would have on his family.

Ultimately, the General’s hospitality would lead to unforeseen consequences, as Helene’s connection to the stranger would alter the course of their lives forever. The family’s idyllic evening had transformed into a night of turmoil, revealing the fragility of their happiness and the weight of their choices.

It was a June day in 1844 when the Marquise d’Aiglemont, a woman of about fifty, was walking in the garden of her daughter’s mansion in Paris. She was focused on a row of windows, occasionally sitting on a bench to gaze at the dome of the Invalides and her own grounds. The neighborhood was quiet, as the aristocracy was just waking up.

The Marquise had given her fortune to her daughter, the Comtesse Moïna de Saint-Héreen, after losing her two sons, Gustave and Abel. Her affection for Moïna was profound, a stark contrast to her feelings for her deceased children, whom she remembered only faintly. The Marquise’s devotion to Moïna was seen as a kind of superstition by society, which largely overlooked her past and her coldness towards her daughter-in-law.

Despite her sacrifices, the Marquise was often alone, as Moïna was busy with her social life. The Marquise’s love for her daughter was evident, but it was tinged with sadness and a sense of loss. Gossip suggested that she might regret giving up her fortune, but most praised her for her devotion. The Marquise rarely attended social events, and when she did, she felt overshadowed by her daughter’s youthful charm.

As she sat in the garden, the Marquise reflected on her life, her face revealing the weight of her experiences. Her beauty had faded, marked by sorrow and the passage of time. She was aware of her daughter’s flirtation with Alfred de Vandenesse, a man she viewed with disdain. The Marquise feared for Moïna, knowing that this relationship could lead to heartbreak.

The Comte de Saint-Héreen had been away for months, and Moïna was indulging in a flirtation that the Marquise believed was dangerous. She felt powerless to intervene, knowing that her warnings would likely fall on deaf ears. The Marquise’s heart ached as she realized that her daughter was on the brink of a precipice, and she could do nothing to save her.

In a moment of despair, the Marquise recalled the small, painful moments that had accumulated over the years, each one a reminder of her daughter’s growing distance. She approached Moïna’s room, hoping to speak to her, but was met with indifference. The Countess’s dismissive attitude stung, and the Marquise felt the weight of her daughter’s contempt.

When she finally confronted Moïna, the conversation turned tense. The Marquise attempted to warn her about the risks of her relationship with Alfred, but Moïna’s flippant responses only deepened the Marquise’s sorrow. As she left the room, the Marquise felt a physical pain in her heart, realizing that her daughter had lost respect for her.

Outside, she collapsed on a bench, overwhelmed by grief and the realization that her daughter was lost to her. Moïna, unaware of the gravity of the situation, thought she could easily mend things with a few kind gestures. However, the Marquise’s condition worsened, and she was carried inside, lifeless.

Moïna, witnessing her mother’s decline, was struck by remorse. She had taken her mother’s love for granted, and now it was too late for reconciliation. As she wept, the Marquise, in her final moments, managed to smile at her daughter, a testament to a mother’s enduring love and forgiveness.

Servants rushed to fetch a physician, but the Marquise’s fate was sealed. Moïna, now fully aware of her loss, stood before her family, a hollow figure of regret, realizing the depth of her mother’s sacrifice and love.
