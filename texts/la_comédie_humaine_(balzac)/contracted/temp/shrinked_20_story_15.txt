DEDICATION

(To a Polish Lady.)

CHAPTER I. THE CHALET

In October 1829 the notary Simon Babylas Latournelle walks from Havre to Ingouville with his family and Jean Butscha, his head clerk. Latournelle confides to his son Exupère a contrived plan to test whether Modeste Mignon has a lover: after formalities at the Chalet, Exupère is to hurry back after a walk and let Modeste overhear him whisper to Dumay, “The young man has come.” The Latournelles, especially the matronly Madame Latournelle—proud, pedantic, and provincially grand—serve as chaperones for Modeste under Dumay’s vigilance.

The Chalet is a small ornamental cottage on the Ingouville ridge, once a porter’s lodge, now a charming dwelling leased to Jean-François Bernard Dumay—that stubborn Breton former lieutenant and faithful cashier of the ruined house of Mignon. The villa’s proud new owner, Vilquin, resents Dumay for clinging to his lease and occupying the bright red Chalet, a jewel in Vilquin’s garden. Dumay, despite offers and temptations, insists on preserving the Chalet as a home for Madame Mignon and her daughter Modeste.

CHAPTER II. A PORTRAIT FROM LIFE

The evening the Latournelles arrive finds a small, attentive company assembled: Gobenheim, a frugal young banker; the blind, stately Madame Mignon; her daughter Modeste; the faithful dwarf-like Butscha; Madame Latournelle; and Dumay and his wife. Dumay has been visibly agitated—he has even laid pistols on the chimney and kept watch with his two Pyrenean dogs—because he believes Modeste may be in love. The household’s anxiety, and Madame Mignon’s private conviction, set a trap around Modeste. Yet she remains absorbed in embroidery, apparently unaware, while the others exchange furtive glances.

Modeste is twenty: pale-gold-haired, delicate, thoughtful, and reserved. Her beauty is subtle rather than flamboyant—an inner chastity and poetic sensibility. She is devoted to her blind mother and seems to live a double life: outwardly dutiful and domestic, inwardly given to deep reading, music, and imaginative reverie. Dumay’s intensity derives from an oath to the absent father, Colonel Charles Mignon: guard Modeste’s honor as if life depended on it.

CHAPTER III. PRELIMINARIES

Jean Dumay’s past is recounted: born at Vannes, son of a revolutionary official later executed, he fled to join the army and became close to Charles Mignon (Comte de La Bastie), a handsome Provençal officer. They were lifelong friends through campaigns and imprisonment. After the fall of the Empire, Mignon turned merchant in Havre, prospered, and made Dumay his cashier. Dumay’s loyalty, honesty, and savvy helped build the Mignon fortune; he married an American, set up comfortably, and was rewarded by the colonel with residence at the Chalet and a substantial account.

In 1826 Mignon’s house collapses under commercial reverses. On the eve of his departure abroad to seek recovery, he confides to Dumay and others the fierce charge to guard his wife and daughters. The colonel sails away, leaving Dumay, Latournelle, and Gobenheim to wind up affairs and protect the family. Their public notice of suspension of payment is dignified and honorable; the friends sell assets and pay debts, and Madame Mignon and Modeste retire to the Chalet, where Dumay vows to defend Modeste’s purity.

CHAPTER IV. A SIMPLE STORY

The ruin coincides with private tragedy. Bettina Caroline, Modeste’s elder sister—once seduced by a dashing Charles d’Estourny—had been abandoned and ruined in spirit; she returned ill after the house’s failure and died young. Her death and the shame of the seduction haunt the family. Bettina on her deathbed entreated Modeste to be guarded against clandestine love: “Never give your heart without your hand; tell everything to papa and mamma.” She left Modeste a ring inscribed “Think of Bettina, 1827,” to be kept until marriage.

After the national crisis and the family catastrophe, Modeste and her mother live quietly at the Chalet. The community conjectures, criticizes, and sometimes ridicules them. Dumay, obsessive in his charge, keeps watch by night, inspects the grounds, and trains his dogs; Madame Dumay and Madame Latournelle act as vigilant chaperones. The household routines and whist games mask the deeper currents of Modeste’s inner life, which only the blind mother, by feeling and tenderness, perceives. Madame Mignon senses a change—subtle signs of happiness and expectation—and voices fears about a hidden lover.

CHAPTER V. THE PROBLEM STILL UNSOLVED

One evening the conspirators carry out Latournelle’s test. Exupère bursts in, announcing aloud, “The young man is here!” Dumay rushes for his pistols and runs out; the household trembles for Modeste’s safety. Yet she remains calm, explaining she is going to prepare her mother for bed. The trap fails to expose any interloper: Dumay finds no trace but his own fears. The friends debate whether Modeste has a lover. Latournelle and Dumay suggest she might be merely romantic, given to reading Byron and foreign poets; but Madame Mignon insists by intuition that Modeste is in love. The company is divided between suspicion, protectiveness, and the comic awkwardness of provincial surveillance.

CHAPTER VI. A MAIDEN’S FIRST ROMANCE

We learn Modeste’s inner life. After Bettina’s fall and the father’s disgrace, Modeste devoted herself to reading and composing airy romances in her head—an inner theatre where she tried on lives and loves. She cultivated a Platonic longing: not for ordinary men, but for genius—poets and artists, a companion of rare sensibility. Her reading in French, English, and German nourished this idealism; she fasted, prayed, and saw a saintly life as a path to fulfill her yearning. She refused commonplace suitors; her pride combined with hunger for a spiritual, intellectual attachment.

Her reveries included attraction to two contemporary literary figures. A lithograph of Canalis in a bookseller’s window and a novel by Arthez set her imagination alight. She resolved to correspond with Canalis, whose lyrics had soothed her. With the help of Francoise Cochet, a maid, she arranged to receive replies “poste restante” at Havre, and wrote to the poet in an ingenuous, ardent note signed “O. d’Este M.”—a trembling overture.

CHAPTER VII. A POET OF THE ANGELIC SCHOOL

Canalis is sketched: a compact, urbane poet of the faubourg Saint-Germain—ambitious, vain, yet gifted with charm. Comfortable incomes from official sinecures and literary patronage sustain him, but his vanity and careerism compromise poetic authenticity. He has a young secretary, Ernest de La Brière, a modest, honest man who admires Canalis but gradually sees the poet’s ego and artifice.

Canalis and La Brière open Modeste’s letter with different temperaments. Canalis, half-amused, half-cynical, doubts anonymous affection and the sincerity of fan letters; La Brière reads the note with sympathy and predicts it might touch a man’s heart. Canalis composes a long, prudential, paternalistic reply warning the unknown correspondent of the dangers of loving a poet and of incompatibilities between poetic life and domestic claims. He counsels prudence, the sanctity of family duties, and the blunt truth that poets may be vain, inconsistent, and ill-suited to domestic bliss. The letter, though elegant and sincere in tone, chills Modeste’s romantic intoxication.

Modeste, who had waited with trembling hope, reads Canalis’s reply at midnight. The prose, thoughtful though it is, dashes the fevered images she cherished. She weeps, then moves from despair to anger: convinced the poet’s counsel is condescending and self-satisfied, she is determined to answer in a way that will force an inconsistency, to meet his pity with a spirited retort. Thus she begins, by the moonlit sea and a nightingale’s song, to compose a reply kindled less by love than by wounded vanity and the proud resolve of a young romantic wanting to be seen and understood.

To Monsieur de Canalis:

Monsieur,--You are certainly a great poet, and you are something more,--an honest man. After showing such loyal frankness to a young girl who was stepping to the verge of an abyss, have you enough left to answer without hypocrisy or evasion the following question?

Would you have written the letter I now hold in answer to mine,--would your ideas, your language have been the same,--had some one whispered in your ear (what may prove true), Mademoiselle O. d’Este M. has six millions and does intend to have a dunce for a master?

Admit the supposition for a moment. Be with me what you are with yourself; fear nothing. I am wiser than my twenty years; nothing that is frank can hurt you in my mind. When I have read your confidence, if you deign to make it, you shall receive from me an answer to your first letter.

Having admired your talent, often so sublime, permit me to do homage to your delicacy and your integrity, which force me to remain always,

Your humble servant, O. d’Este M.

Ernest de La Brière held this letter awhile and paced the boulevards in turmoil. A Parisian might have called her a flirt, but La Brière’s conscience set three judges within him—Truth, Justice, Honor. Truth accused him of hypocrisy: would he not have courted a rich woman? Justice reminded him that wealthy girls are often sought for money and Modeste had rebelled against that. Honor demanded a sincere, frank answer.

He could not presume she had six millions; yet the question rankled. Six days later Modeste received a cautious reply:

Mademoiselle,--You are not a D’Este. The name is a feigned one to conceal your own. Do I owe the revelations which you solicit to a person who is untruthful about herself? Question for question: Are you of an illustrious family? or a noble family? or a middle-class family? Undoubtedly ethics cannot change, but obligations vary by station. Just as the sun lights scenes diversely, morality adapts to rank and position. The peccadillo of a soldier is a crime in a general. Observances differ for a gleaner, for a seamstress, for the daughter of a shopkeeper, for a bourgeoise, for the heiress of a noble family. A king must not stoop to pick up a coin, but a laborer should retrace his steps for ten sous; both obey economy, yet duties differ. A daughter of Este, worth six millions, may ride like an amazon into the presence of a poet and say, “I love poetry;” a daughter of the people would ridicule herself imitating her. To what class do you belong? Answer sincerely, and I will answer your question.

As I have not the honor of knowing you personally, and yet am bound to you, in a measure, by poetic communion, I avoid commonplace compliments. Perhaps you already enjoy making a man of books squirm.

Modeste replied at once, claiming noble origin: a count for a father, quarterings enough for any court. La Brière, uneasy in Paris’s intrusive society, went to Havre, obtained secrecy from the post-office, followed Françoise Cochet to Ingouville, and saw Modeste at the chalet window. Struck by her beauty, he learned the villa belonged to the Vilquins and that the d’Hérouvilles were visiting.

Back in Paris he wrote a long, honest letter admitting that had he known she possessed vast wealth he would have behaved differently: wealth tempts men to flatter and seek security rather than self-respect; success in such pursuit might bring position but not true happiness. He confessed spying at Havre and blushed at it; seeing her altered his view—she might be an exception. He swore to make his fortune and choose a companion without cupidity; he urged her, if of ordinary station, to obey social law, though he admired her soul. He counselled women to restrain public ambition for the sake of home, and closed with a warm, reverent farewell.

This letter relieved Modeste; she saw her mistake and altered arrangements to secure the post, determined to receive letters herself. La Brière’s confession moved her: to have touched such a noble heart vindicated her intuition. She rejoiced and dreamed of her father’s return to make them happy.

To Monsieur de Canalis:

My friend,--Suffer me that name,--you have delighted me; I would not have you other than you are in that letter, the first--oh, may it not be the last! Who but a poet could have excused and understood a young girl so delicately?

I wish to speak with the same sincerity. First, be glad you do not know me. I am neither the hideous Mademoiselle Vilquin nor the withered d’Hérouville. The Cardinal d’Hérouville flourished a century earlier; I take no account of lieutenants-general and abbés writing trumpery verses.

I do not live in Villa Vilquin; there is not a drop of that chilly counter-blood in me. I come partly from Germany, partly from southern France; my mind loves reverie, my blood has Provençal vivacity. I am noble on both sides; on my mother’s side I might be in the Almanach de Gotha. My precautions are taken. It is not in any man’s power, nor the law’s, to unmask my incognito. I shall remain veiled, unknown.

As to my person and my “belongings,” be easy. I am at least as handsome as the little girl on whom your eyes chanced in Havre; and I do not call myself poverty-stricken, though ten sons of peers may not accompany me. I have seen the humiliating comedy of the heiress sought for her millions. Make no attempt to reach me. Though free as air, I am watched and guarded—by myself and by people of nerve who would not hesitate to stab you if you tried to penetrate my retreat. I do not say this to tempt you; I believe I need no such incentive.

Now to the second edition of your sermon.

Will you have a confession? When I saw you distrustful and mistaking me for Corinne, I thought muses had taken you into valleys of boarding-school Parnassus. You are safe with me; I may love poetry, but I have no little verses, and my stockings remain white. Should I ever say “Come!” you will not find an old maid or a poor ugly creature.

Ah, my friend, if you only knew how I regret that you came to Havre! You have lowered the charm of my romance. God alone knew the treasure I reserved for a man noble and perspicacious enough to come having faith in my letters, to meet me with the simplicity of a child: that was my dream. Now you have spoiled my treasure! But I forgive you; you live in Paris and a man dwells in a poet.

Because I tell you this will you think me some little girl of illusions? You, witty and wise, have not guessed that when I received your pedantic lesson I said to myself: “No, my first letter was not a pebble flung by a vagabond child but a net cautiously thrown by a fisherman hoping for a miraculous draught.”

All you say about family has my approval. The man who pleases me will have my heart and life—with my parents’ consent; I will neither grieve nor surprise them. I reign over them, and they have no prejudice. I have built my fortress and fortified it with devotion; I have armor engraved “Disdain.” I detest calculating motives and worship the beautiful and ideal without being merely romantic; though I have been in dream. I recognize the truth of society’s demands.

For now we are two friends. Why seek an unseen friend? Your person may be unknown, but your mind and heart I know; they please me, and within my soul are thoughts that need a man of genius for a confidant. I do not wish my poem to be wasted; I would have it known to you as to God. What a precious thing is a true comrade! Later, if our souls are sisters, an old serving-man shall lead you to the cottage, villa, castle—I don’t know what bower it will be—but you admit it is poetic, and that I am agreeable. Have I not left you free? Have I gone with jealous feet to watch you in Paris salons? No; I ask only a spiritual and mystic alliance. Come to me when you are unhappy, wounded, weary. Tell me all; I have balms for your ills. I am twenty, but I have the sense of fifty and, through another’s experience, I know love’s horrors and delights. I know baseness and infamy; yet I am honest. I have no illusions; I have beliefs and religion. See! I open the ball of our confidences.

Whoever I marry—if I choose him—may go to the East Indies and find me at home, working at tapestry begun before he left, each stitch a verse of his poem. I have resolved never to follow my husband where he does not wish me; I will be the divinity of his hearth. That is my religion. But why should I not test and choose the man to whom I shall be like life to body? Is a woman who thwarts the man she loves anything but disease? By life I mean joyous health.

But to return to your letter: it contains the prosaic sentiments necessary to family life; without them no happiness is possible. To act as an honest man, to think as a poet, to love as women love—that is what I longed for, and it is no longer chimera.

Adieu, my friend. I am poor at present. That is partly why I cling to concealment. I have read your last verses in the “Revue” with delight now that I am initiated in your austere loftiness.

Will it make you unhappy to know a young girl prays for you; that you are her solitary thought—except her father and mother? Can there be any reason for rejecting pages full of you, written for you, seen by no eye but yours? Send me your counterpart. Your confidences—if full and true—will suffice for my happiness.

O. d’Este M.

La Brière read, enchanted and alarmed: had he already fallen? The unseen correspondent fascinated him. For a young secretary newly out of Paris’s bustle, epistolary intimacy was intoxicating. He answered with guarded earnestness, irony, and real feeling:

Mademoiselle,--Is it loyal to enthrone yourself in a poor poet’s heart with intent to abandon him if he is not exactly what you wish, leaving him long regrets—showing a moment’s image of perfection, giving a foretaste of happiness? I was short-sighted to solicit this letter in which you begin to unfold your fabric of thoughts. A man can enamor easily of such a mysterious unknown: fearlessness, originality, imagination, feeling. I therefore make the most of my remaining sense to offer humble remonstrances.

Do you really believe letters—more or less true, expressions of the moment—can replace the revelations of daily intercourse? Man is dual: an invisible life of heart, which letters may serve, and a material life, to which more importance is given than you know. These must harmonize in your ideal; and this is rare.

The pure, disinterested homage of a solitary soul consoles the literary man; I thank you with an impulse equal to your own. But after this poetical exchange, what next? What do you expect? I have neither Byron's genius nor his halo of fictitious damnation. What could you have hoped from him? Pride devoured him; vanity discouraged friendship. I, less than he, may have character discordances, and make friendship a burden. In exchange for your reveries, what will you gain? The dissatisfaction of a life not wholly yours. The compact is madness. Let me tell you why.

Your projected poem is a plagiarism. A young German girl adored Goethe with girlish intoxication, made him her god though he was married; Madame Goethe indulged her with sly good-nature. The young enthusiast later married a younger, handsomer man. If a girl had made herself handmaid to a man of genius and then retired in solitude, she might have lived forever inlaid in his glory. If that is sublime, what of the reverse? As I am neither Goethe nor Byron, I cannot expect a cult. I am not disposed to martyrdom. I have ambition and a heart; I am young and have a career. See me for what I am. The king’s bounty and ministers’ protection give me a sufficient living. I have the outward bearing of an ordinary man. If I drive to soirées, my wheels know nothing of invested capital. If I am not rich, neither am I in garret penury like worthier men.

Ah, what prosaic conclusions your young enthusiasm will find to these visions. Let us stop. If I have seemed to you a terrestrial paragon, you have been to me a thing of light. May nothing tarnish this episode. Were we to continue, I might love you; I might conceive a passion that rends obstacles. Suppose I pleased you—would we not end in the common way—marriage, household, children? Therefore, adieu.

To Monsieur de Canalis:

My Friend,--Your letter gives me as much pain as pleasure. But perhaps one day we shall find nothing but pleasure in writing. Understand me. The soul speaks to God and asks; he is mute. I seek in you the answers God does not make. Cannot Mademoiselle de Gournay and Montaigne’s friendship be revived in us? Do you not remember Sismondi’s household in Geneva? The most lovely home ever known, I have been told; like the Marquis de Pescaire’s. Ah, friend, is it impossible that two hearts, two harps, should answer each other from a distance, vibrating in unison? Man alone is harp, musician, and listener. Do you think me jealous like ordinary women? I know you go into the world and meet the handsomest and wittiest women in Paris. May not some mermaid have clasped you and inspired your prosaic answer? There is something more beautiful than Parisian garlands; there grows a flower on Alpine peaks—men of genius—which fertilize humanity with lofty dews. I seek to cultivate that flower.

Do me the honor to believe there is nothing low in me. Were I Bettina, I should never have become Madame von Arnim; had I been one of Byron’s loves, I should be in a cloister. You have touched me. You do not know me, but shall. I feel something sublime in me that I dare to name without vanity. God has put in my soul the roots of that Alpine flower; I cannot plant it in a window-pot and see it die. No, that cup of single beauty shall not be dragged through vulgar life; it is yours—yours before any eye has blighted it, yours forever! Yes, my poet, to you belong my thoughts—secret and gay; my heart is yours without reserve and with infinite affection. If you personally do not please me, I shall never marry. I can live in the life of the heart on your mind and sentiments. I will be what I am—your friend. Yours is a noble moral nature; I have recognized and appreciated it, and that suffices. Do not laugh at a young handmaiden who shrinks not from being the old companion of a poet—a sort of mother, housekeeper; the guide of his judgment and a source of his wealth. This handmaiden—devoted, precious—will be Friendship, pure, disinterested, to whom you will tell all. She knits by lamp-light and waits when the poet returns vexed. Such shall be my destiny if not that of a happy wife; I shall neither be an old maid nor childless—I shall make myself a mother by caring for others and by secret cooperation in a great man’s existence.

I hate commonplaceness. If free and rich, I will never belong to a ninny because he is a peer’s son, nor to a merchant who might ruin us, nor to a handsome creature who would be a woman in the household, nor to any man who would make me blush daily. Make yourself easy. My father adores my wishes; he will not oppose them. If I please my poet, and he pleases me, our love shall be built so high as to be inaccessible to misfortune. I am an eaglet; you will see it in my eyes.

I shall not repeat, but will compress: I should be the happiest of women imprisoned by love as I now am imprisoned by paternal care. Ah, friend, let us see our romance to a real end: listen to its argument:

A young girl locked in a tower longs to run in the park; she invents a way out, jumps from the casement, scales the wall, frolics on the neighbor’s sward. That girl is my soul; the neighbor’s park is your genius. Is it not natural? Was there ever a neighbor who did not complain of broken trellises? I leave it to my poet to answer.

But do you want better reasons? Here they are. Marriages usually defy common sense. Parents inquire: the Leander is not a thief, has necessary fortune, comes from college or law and wears gentlemanly clothes—so he is allowed to meet the girl, who is instructed to guard her tongue and not reveal heart or soul, smiling like a dancer at a pirouette. Commands say not to seem alarmingly well educated. If settlements agreed, the parents let the pair see each other briefly. Each is as dressed in soul as in body. This comedy, mixed with bouquets, jewels, and theatre-parties, is called “paying your addresses.” It revolts me: I want actual marriage to be the result of a prior long marriage of souls. A woman has only one moment when reflection and experience should govern her; she risks her all and is forced to be a spectator. I have the right, will, and power to make my own unhappiness, and I use them, as my mother did, who married generously by instinct. I know you are free, a poet, noble-looking. Be sure I would not choose one of your Apollo brothers who was already married. If my mother was won by beauty, why should I not be attracted by spirit and form united? Shall I not know you better by correspondence than by the vulgar receiving of addresses? This is the question.

But my proceedings at least do not bind us personally. I know love has illusions and every illusion its to-morrow; hence many partings among lovers vowed for life. The proof of love lies in suffering and happiness. When, after these trials, two beings have shown each other defects and virtues and truly observed characters, they may go to the grave hand in hand. Who told you our little drama would have no future? In any case, shall we not have enjoyed our correspondence?

I await your orders, monseigneur, and am with all my heart,

Your handmaiden,

O. d’Este M.

La Brière answered with passion and trembling: he could no longer be only a friend; she must give hope or he must renounce. Her reply startled him: she had moved toward the possibility of life united in soul, yet would not meet him without learning more. Her compact required him to write his life plainly, tell what he did day by day, and then she would see him. That step involved her entire future and her parents’ consent; she longed to know how far great spirits could bend to family life. She justified her boldness and proclaimed that, if she found in him genius and nobleness, she would be faithful, devoted, and the companion of his life—either as wife or as the devoted domestic friend of a man of genius. Her letter ended in affectionate banter of a girl who believed herself taciturn.

Many letters between Ernest and Modeste in September are omitted; they depict a passionate, reciprocal intimacy. For La Brière they were treasures; for Modeste they became love. The correspondence knitted two souls: hers the ideal of devotion, his the complement of Canalis’s fame. This epistolary marriage fed both imaginations and made each the other's chief preoccupation.

Modeste sent a decisive letter proposing a test: Canalis was to be at Havre the next Sunday, attend morning mass, walk round the nave without speaking, wear a white rose in his buttonhole, and return to Paris to await her reply. She warned she might not say what he wished, and that she would not accept without having seen him.

This letter came just after the quarrel between Dumay and Modeste and the family’s anxious days. Modeste’s mother noticed her disturbance and sought assurances. Madame Mignon, blind and broken, feared that any breach with her husband or failure in duty would kill him. She entreated Modeste to promise never to leave her father, to allow him to choose a husband after proper inquiry, and to keep him informed. Modeste kissed her mother and swore never to marry without her father’s consent and to think always of Bettina.

At that moment Dumay hurried up the hill to the chalet with wondrous news: three letters had ruined them; one letter might restore them. A sea-captain had arrived from the China seas with a letter for Jean Dumay from Charles Mignon. It announced that Charles had made at least seven millions through opium and indigo trade, had bought a brig called the "Mignon," and intended to return soon. He sent a large check to Dumay and intended to keep the true amount of his fortune secret on his return, arranging through Mongenod to secure La Bastie and entail it, so that one of his sons-in-law might inherit. He wished Dumay to be his bailiff. Charles declared his love for his family and asked Dumay to prepare them for his return.

Madame Mignon said, dryly, “Your father is coming.” Modeste asked how she knew, and her mother answered, “Nothing else could make Dumay hurry himself.” Dumay announced that the colonel had never been ill and was coming back with a fine ship and a fortune. He wanted secrecy. He believed the colonel sought his lost daughter Bettina, and though she was dead, he would learn of that only upon his return.

Madame Mignon feared the shock of learning Bettina’s death might kill her husband. Dumay urged Modeste to write to her father, prepare him for grief, and promised to deliver the letter in Paris. Modeste, afraid that Canalis and Dumay might meet, hurried to write to her poet and postpone any rendezvous. Before leaving, she asserted proudly to Dumay and her mother that her hand belonged to her family but her heart was her own; she swore she would never act contrary to her pledges to her sister and mother.

Dumay, reassured, smiled at Modeste’s spirited declarations and praised her truthfulness. Madame Mignon, though joyful at the news of the father’s return, still felt deep sadness; she would see him but feared the tale he would bring.

Thus the return of Charles Mignon transformed the family’s fortunes and cast new light on Modeste’s future. The correspondence between her and the poet had deepened into a potential union of minds, to be tested by a meeting and, above all, by the consent and prudence of family ties.

At this moment Modeste, glad at her father’s return yet anxious like Perrette with broken eggs, paced her room. She had hoped her father would bring more than Dumay had said; nothing would satisfy her new ambition for her poet except at least half the six millions she had named in her second letter. Torn between two joys and the grief of comparative poverty, she sat at the piano and put her wishes into music. Dumay conversed with his wife in the garden, telling her of their fortune and asking her desires. Monsieur and Madame Dumay had no family except the Mignons. They agreed to live in Provence if the Comte de La Bastie meant to, and to leave their money to whichever of Modeste’s children might need it most.

“Listen to Modeste,” said Madame Mignon. “None but a girl in love can compose such airs without having studied music.”

Houses may burn, fortunes sink, fathers return from distant lands, empires crumble, cholera ravage cities, but a maiden’s love persists as surely as nature pursues hers.

Inspired, Modeste set certain stanzas to music—printed in the second volume of Dauriat’s edition—adjusting lines to her melody and taking liberties that shocked admirers of a poet famed for exact measure.

THE MAIDEN’S SONG

Hear, arise! the lark is shaking
  Sunlit wings that heavenward rise;
Sleep no more; the violet, waking,
  Wafts her incense to the skies.

Flowers revived, their eyes unclosing,
  See themselves in drops of dew
In each calyx-cup reposing,
  Pearls of a day their mirror true.

Breeze divine, the god of roses,
  Passed by night to bless their bloom;
See! for him each bud uncloses,
  Glows, and yields its rich perfume.

Then arise! the lark is shaking
  Sunlit wings that heavenward rise;
Nought is sleeping--Heart, awaking,
  Lift thine incense to the skies.

“It is very pretty,” said Madame Dumay. “Modeste is a musician, and that’s all.”

“The devil is in her!” cried the cashier, suspicion forced into his heart.

“She loves,” Madame Mignon insisted.

Madame Mignon’s persuasion turned Dumay’s happiness sour. The poor Breton returned to Havre, heavy-hearted, and then to his desk at Gobenheim’s counting-house; before dinner he went to Latournelle to beg the notary’s advice.

“Yes, my dear friend,” Dumay said on Latournelle’s steps. “I now agree with Madame; she loves—yes, I am sure of it; and the devil knows the rest. I am dishonored.”

“Don’t make yourself unhappy, Dumay,” answered the little notary. “We can outwit the little puss; sooner or later every girl in love betrays herself—you may be sure. We will talk this evening.”

Thus all devoted to the Mignon family were as troubled and uncertain as before the old soldier’s experiment. Dumay’s ill success stirred his sense of duty: he decided not to go to Paris to secure his patron’s announced fortune until he had guessed Modeste’s heart. These friends, who prized feeling above interest, knew that unless the daughter were pure and innocent, the father would die of grief when he learned of Bettina’s death and his wife’s blindness. Dumay’s distress impressed the Latournelles so much they even forgot Exupere’s parting for Paris that morning. During dinner Monsieur and Madame Latournelle and Butscha turned the problem over and over.

“If Modeste loved any one in Havre she would have shown some fear yesterday,” said Madame Latournelle; “her lover, therefore, lives elsewhere.”

“She swore to her mother this morning,” said the notary, “in Dumay’s presence, that she had not exchanged a look or word with any living soul.”

“Then she loves after my fashion!” exclaimed Butscha.

“And how is that?” asked Madame Latournelle.

“Madame,” said the little cripple, “I love alone and afar—as far as from here to the stars.”

“How do you manage it, you silly fellow?” she laughed.

“Ah, madame! what you call my hump is the socket of my wings.”

They laughed at his fanciful reply, noticing his seal bore a star and the words “Fulgens, sequar”—“Shining One, I follow thee”—the motto of the house of Chastillonest.

“A beautiful woman may feel as distrustful as the ugliest,” Butscha murmured; “Modeste is clever enough to fear she may be loved only for her beauty.”

Butscha’s mind worked with concentrated intensity: where others dissipated energy, his curvature seemed to store and project it in flashes of feeling and insight. Such beings sometimes possess peculiar faculties—whimsy, malice, or rare goodness—living inwardly like instruments awaiting a touch.

Nearly at the key to the puzzle, Butscha, with all the anxious solicitude of a hopeless lover, set himself to capture Modeste’s secret. He resolved to lay a trap—an intercepted glance, a sudden quiver, some small surgeon’s probe. That evening he followed his patrons to the Chalet, clouded with care, determined to hide from watchful eyes and ears the device he intended. Gobenheim did not appear; Butscha allied himself with Dumay against Monsieur and Madame Latournelle. During a brief moment of Modeste’s absence, about nine to prepare for her mother’s bedtime, the others spoke openly; the poor clerk, depressed by conviction of Modeste’s love, seemed remote.

“Well, what’s the matter with you, Butscha?” cried Madame Latournelle; “one would think you hadn’t a friend in the world.”

Tears shone in the poor fellow’s eyes; he was the son of a Swedish sailor, his mother dead.

“I have no one in the world but you,” he answered. “Your compassion is part of your religion; I can never lose it—and I will never deserve to lose it.”

This struck a sensitive chord in all present.

“We love you, Monsieur Butscha,” said Madame Mignon, with feeling.

“I have six hundred thousand francs,” cried Dumay, “and you shall be a notary and Latournelle’s successor.”

The American wife took the poor hunchback’s hand.

“What! six hundred thousand francs!” exclaimed Latournelle, prickling at Dumay’s words; “and you allow these ladies to live as they do! Modeste ought to have a fine horse; why doesn’t she continue lessons in music and painting and—”

“He has had the money only a few hours!” said the little wife.

“Hush!” murmured Madame Mignon.

While words were exchanged, Butscha’s august mistress turned to him.

“My son,” she said, “you are so surrounded by true affection that I never thought how my thoughtless phrase might be construed; but you must thank me, for it has shown you what friends your noble qualities have won.”

“Then you must have news from Monsieur Mignon,” resumed the notary.

“He is on his way home,” said Madame Mignon; “but let us keep the secret. When my husband learns how faithful Butscha has been to us, how he has shown the warmest and most disinterested friendship when others have given the cold shoulder, he will not let you alone provide for him, Dumay. And so, my friend,” she added, turning her blind face toward Butscha, “you can begin at once to negotiate with Latournelle.”

“He’s of legal age, twenty-five and a half. As for me, making the purchase easy for you will be paying a debt,” said the notary.

Butscha kissed Madame Mignon’s hand; his face was wet with tears as Modeste opened the salon door.

“What are you doing to my Black Dwarf?” she demanded. “Who is making him unhappy?”

“Ah! Mademoiselle Mignon, do luckless fellows cradled in misfortune ever weep for grief? They have shown me as much affection as I could feel if they were my own relations. I’m to be a notary; I shall be rich. Ha! the poor Butscha may become the rich Butscha,” he cried.

He struck his chest and sat before the fireplace, casting a glance at Modeste that slipped like a ray of light between his half-closed eyelids. He perceived, in this incident, a chance to interrogate his sovereign’s heart. Dumay thought the clerk dared aspire to Modeste, and exchanged a rapid glance with the others, who began to eye the little man with a mix of terror and curiosity.

“I, too, have my dreams,” said Butscha, not taking his eyes from Modeste.

The young girl lowered her eyelids with a movement that revealed much to the young man.

“You love romance,” he said. “Let me tell you mine; tell me whether it could be. To me wealth would bring greater happiness than to others; the highest joy I can imagine is to enrich the one I loved. You, mademoiselle, who know so many things, tell me if a man can make himself beloved independently of his person, be he handsome or ugly, and be loved for his spirit only?”

Modeste looked at Butscha, a piercing, questioning glance; she shared Dumay’s suspicion of his motive.

“Let me be rich, and I will seek some beautiful poor girl, abandoned as I was, who has suffered. I will write to her and console her, be her guardian spirit; she shall read my heart and possess my two gifts—my gold, delicately offered, and my thought robed in splendor that birth denied my grotesque body. I shall remain hidden, like a cause science seeks. God himself may not be glorious to the eye. Naturally the maiden will be curious; she will wish to see me; I shall tell her I am a monster of ugliness; I shall picture myself hideous.”

At these words Modeste gave Butscha a look that pierced him through. Had she said aloud “What do you know of my love?” she could not have been more explicit.

“If I have the honor of being loved for the poem of my heart, if some day such love may make a woman think me only slightly deformed, shall I not be happier than the handsomest man—as happy as a man of genius beloved by some celestial being like yourself?”

Color suffused the young girl’s face; the cripple read nearly all he sought.

“Well, if that be so,” he went on, “if we enrich the one we love, if we please the spirit and withdraw the body, is that not the way? At any rate it is the dream of your poor dwarf—a dream of yesterday; for today your mother gives me the key to future wealth by promising means to buy a practice. But before I become another Gobenheim, I seek to know whether this dream could be carried out. What do you say, mademoiselle?”

Modeste was so astonished she scarcely noticed the question. The lover’s trap was better baited than the soldier’s; the poor girl was speechless.

“Poor Butscha!” whispered Madame Latournelle. “Do you think he is going mad?”

“You want to realize Beauty and the Beast,” Modeste said at length; “but you forget the Beast turned into Prince Charming.”

“Do you think so?” said the dwarf. “I have always thought that transformation meant the phenomenon of the soul made visible, obliterating form under spirit’s light. If I were not loved I should stay hidden, that is all. You and yours, madame,” he added to his mistress, “instead of a dwarf at your service, will have a life and fortune.”

So saying, Butscha resumed his seat, remarking to the whist-players with assumed calm, “Whose deal is it?” but within he whispered sadly: “She wants to be loved for herself; she corresponds with some pretended great man; how far has it gone?”

“Dear mamma, it is nearly ten o’clock,” said Modeste.

Madame Mignon said good-night and went to bed.

Those who love in secret may have Pyrenean hounds, mothers, Dumays, and Latournelles to spy upon them and yet be safe; but when it comes to a lover—ah! that is diamond against diamond, flame against flame, mind to mind, an equation whose terms are mutual.

On Sunday morning Butscha arrived at the Chalet before Madame Latournelle, and blockaded the house awaiting the postman.

“Have you a letter for Mademoiselle Mignon?” he asked the humble functionary when he appeared.

“No, monsieur, none.”

“This house has been a good customer to the post of late,” remarked the clerk.

Modeste watched the exchange from her chamber window, where she always posted herself behind the blinds at that hour to watch for the postman. She ran downstairs, went into the little garden, and called imperatively, “Monsieur Butscha!”

“Here am I, mademoiselle,” said the cripple, reaching the gate as Modeste opened it.

“Will you tell me whether among your various titles to a woman’s affection you count that of shameless spying, in which you are now engaged?” she demanded, trying to crush her slave with a queenly glance.

“Yes, mademoiselle,” he answered proudly. “Ah! I never expected the grub could serve a star—but so it is. Would you rather your mother and Monsieur Dumay and Madame Latournelle had guessed your secret than one excluded from life who seeks to be one of those flowers you cut and wear for a moment? They all know you love; but I, I alone, know how. Use me as a vigilant watchdog; I will obey you, protect you, and never bark; neither will I condemn you. I ask only to be of service. Your father has made Dumay keeper of the hen-roost; take Butscha to watch outside—poor Butscha who asks for nothing, not so much as a bone.”

“Well, I’ll give you a trial,” said Modeste, who most wished to rid herself of so clever a watcher. “Please go at once to all the hotels in Graville and Havre, and ask if a gentleman has arrived from England named Monsieur Arthur—”

“Listen, mademoiselle,” Butscha interrupted respectfully. “I will go and take a walk on the seashore, for you don’t want me to go to church today; that’s what it is.”

Modeste looked at him with stupefied astonishment.

“Mademoiselle, you have wrapped your face in cotton-wool and a silk handkerchief, but nothing is the matter with you; you have put that thick veil on to see someone yourself without being seen.”

“Where did you acquire such perspicacity?” cried Modeste, blushing.

“Moreover, mademoiselle, you have not put on your corset; a cold in the head wouldn’t oblige you to disfigure your waist and wear half a dozen petticoats, nor hide your hands in old gloves, nor hide your pretty feet in hideous shoes, nor dress like a beggar-woman, nor—”

“That’s enough,” she said. “How am I to be certain you will obey me?”

“My master must go to Sainte-Adresse. He does not like it, but he is so good he won’t deprive me of my Sunday; I will offer to go for him.”

“Go, and I will trust you.”

“You are sure I can do nothing for you in Havre?”

“Nothing. Hear me, mysterious dwarf—look,” she said, pointing to the cloudless sky; “can you see a single trace of that bird that flew by just now? No; then my actions are pure as the air and leave no stain. Reassure Dumay and the Latournelles and my mother. That hand,” she held up a pretty delicate hand, the rosy fingers slightly turned, “will never be given, it will never even be kissed by what people call a lover until my father has returned.”

“Why don’t you want me in church today?”

“Do you venture to question me after all I have done and asked?”

Butscha bowed and departed to find his master, rapturous at being taken into the service of his goddess.

Half an hour later Monsieur and Madame Latournelle came to fetch Modeste, who complained of a horrible toothache.

“I really have not had the courage to dress myself,” she said.

“Then stay at home,” replied the worthy chaperone.

“Oh, no!” said Modeste. “I would rather not. I have bundled myself up, and I don’t think it will do me any harm to go out.”

And Mademoiselle Mignon marched off beside Latournelle, refusing his arm lest he question the outward trembling that betrayed her inward agitation at the thought of seeing her great poet. One look, the first—was it not about to decide her fate?

Is there a more delightful instant in a man’s life than a first rendezvous? Do not the secret sensations of the heart find fresh expression? We rediscover nameless pleasures—Ernest de La Briere knew them as he sharpened his razors, laid out his finest shirt and collar, and donned his best clothes. We deify the garments associated with that supreme moment; we weave poetic fancies within us like women, and when either party guesses them they take wing. Such feelings stirred in La Briere, showing that, like many poor fellows ground down by toil, he had never yet been loved. Arriving at Havre overnight, he slept off his fatigue, bathed, and dressed in a costume chosen to show him at his best.

This is perhaps the right moment for a portrait of him, to justify the letter Modeste was to write. Born to a good family in Toulouse and allied by marriage to a minister who patronized him, Ernest showed the air of breeding from early education; the habit of business had given him a sedateness not yet pedantic. He was of ordinary height; his face, winning in delicacy and sweetness, lacked color but was relieved by a small moustache and an imperial à la Mazarin—without which he might have seemed a young woman in disguise. The shape of his face, the cut of his lips, the ivory regularity of his teeth were almost feminine. His manner of speech and gentle blue eyes with Turkish eyelids matched the expression of his face; the minister sometimes called his young secretary Mademoiselle de La Briere.

A full forehead framed by abundant black hair suited the generally melancholy cast of his features. The prominent arch of the upper eyelid shadowed the glance and gave a physical sadness produced by the droop of the lid. This inward doubt—modesty—pervaded him. The oval of his head wanted more length; his face looked compressed. Hard work had already furrowed his brow between eyebrows too thick and too near together, like those of a jealous nature. Though then slight, he belonged to temperaments that after thirty often take on unexpected flesh.

To a reader of French history the young man suggested the royal, almost inconceivable figure of Louis XIII—melancholy modesty without cause; pallid beneath a crown; loving war and hunting but hating work; timid with his mistress, so indifferent that he let his friend’s head be cut off—perhaps explained only by remorse. Was he a Catholic Hamlet, or the victim of disease? In Ernest the undying worm was simple distrust of himself—the timidity of a man to whom no woman had ever said, “Ah, how I love thee!” and the spirit of self-devotion without an object. After his patron’s ministry fell, he leaped upon a rock covered with exquisite mosses named Canalis; he was still seeking a power to love, and this spaniel-like devotion gave him outwardly the air of a man who had found his master. This play of feeling and a general tone of suffering made his face more truly beautiful than he knew; he had long been annoyed to be classed among the “handsome disconsolate,” a class fallen from fashion when every man blows his own trumpet.

Ernest rested his immediate hopes on the fashionable clothes he meant to wear. For this sacred interview, where everything depended on first impression, he put on black trousers and polished boots, a sulphur-colored waistcoat revealing a fine shirt with opal buttons, a black cravat, and a small blue surtout clinging to his shoulders. The ribbon of the Legion of Honor was in his buttonhole. He wore well-fitting kid gloves and carried his cane and hat with a gesture worthy of the Grand Monarch, showing his carefully arranged hair to the light. He stood in the church porch before service began, observing the Christians—especially the female Christians—who dipped their fingers in the holy water.

An inward voice cried to Modeste as she entered, “It is he!” That surtout, and indeed the whole bearing of the young man, were essentially Parisian; the ribbon, the gloves, the cane, the perfume of his hair were not of Havre. When La Briere turned to examine the tall imposing Madame Latournelle, the notary, and the bundled figure of Modeste, the poor child—though she had tutored herself for the event—received a violent blow when her eyes rested on this poetic figure, illuminated by the full light streaming through the open door. She could not be mistaken; a small white rose nearly hid the ribbon of the Legion. Would he recognize his unknown mistress muffled in an old bonnet with a double veil? Modeste, in fear of love’s clairvoyance, began to stoop in her walk like an old woman.

“Wife,” said little Latournelle as they took their seats, “that gentleman does not belong to Havre.”

“So many strangers come here,” answered his wife.

“But,” said the notary, “strangers never come to look at a church like ours, which is less than two centuries old.”

Ernest remained in the porch throughout the service without seeing any woman who realized his hopes. Modeste could not control her trembling until Mass was nearly over. She was in the grasp of a joy that none but she could depict. At last she heard a gentleman’s footfall on the aisle pavement. After the service La Briere made a circuit of the church, where no one remained but the punctiliously pious, whom he proceeded to analyze. He noticed a prayer-book shaking violently in the hands of a veiled woman as he passed; since she alone hid her face his suspicions were aroused and then confirmed by Modeste’s dress, which the lover’s eye now scanned and noted. He left the church with the Latournelles and followed them at a distance to the rue Royale, where he saw them enter a house accompanied by Modeste, who customarily stayed with friends till vespers. After examining the little house, ornamented with scutcheons, he asked the owner’s name and learned it was Monsieur Latournelle, the chief notary in Havre. As Ernest lounged along the rue Royale hoping for a glimpse into the house, Modeste caught sight of him and declared herself too ill to go to vespers. Poor Ernest thus had his trouble for his pains. He dared not wander about Ingouville; moreover, he made it a point of honor to obey orders, and therefore returned to Paris, previously writing a letter which Françoise Cochet duly delivered next day with the Havre postmark.

It was Monsieur and Madame Latournelle’s custom to dine at the Chalet every Sunday when they brought back Modeste after vespers. As soon as the invalid felt better they set out for Ingouville, accompanied by Butscha. Once home, the happy Modeste forgot her pretended illness and veil, dressing charmingly and humming as she came down to dinner,

“Nought is sleeping—Heart! awaking,
Lift thine incense to the skies.”

Butscha shuddered slightly at sight of her; she was so changed. The wings of love were fastened to her shoulders; she had the air of a nymph, a Psyche; her cheeks glowed with the color of happiness.

“Who wrote the words to which you have put that pretty music?” her mother asked.

“Canalis, mamma,” she answered, flushing from throat to forehead.

“Canalis!” cried the dwarf, to whom the blush and voice told what he had not yet guessed. “He, that great poet, does he write songs?”

“They are only simple verses,” she said, “which I have ventured to set to German airs.”

“No, no,” interrupted Madame Mignon, “the music is your own, my daughter.”

Modeste, crimson, slipped into the garden calling Butscha after her.

“You can do me a great service,” she said. “Dumay is keeping a secret from my mother and me as to the fortune my father is bringing back. Did not Dumay send papa over five hundred thousand francs when he first went away? Yes. Well, papa is not the kind to stay four years and only double his capital. It seems he is coming back on a ship of his own, and Dumay’s share amounts to almost six hundred thousand francs.”

“There is no need to question Dumay,” said Butscha. “Your father lost, as you know, about four millions when he went away, and he has doubtless recovered them. He would of course give Dumay ten per cent of his profits; the worthy man admitted the other day how much it was, and my master and I think the colonel’s fortune must now be six or seven millions—”

“Oh, papa!” cried Modeste, crossing her hands on her breast and looking up, “twice you have given me life!”

“Ah, mademoiselle!” said Butscha, “you love a poet. That kind of man is more or less a Narcissus. Will he know how to love you? A phrase-maker, always busy fitting words together, must be a bore. Mademoiselle, a poet is no more poetry than a seed is a flower.”

“Butscha, I never saw so handsome a man.”

“Beauty is a veil which often hides imperfections.”

“He has the most angelic heart of heaven—”

“I pray God you may be right,” said the dwarf, clasping his hands, “—and happy! That man shall have, as you have, a servant in Jean Butscha. I will not be notary; I shall give that up; I shall study the sciences.”

“Why?”

“Ah, mademoiselle, to train up your children, if you will deign to make me their tutor. But, oh! if you would only listen to some advice. Let me take up this matter; let me look into the life and habits of this man—find out if he is kind, bad-tempered, gentle, if he commands the respect you merit in a husband, if he can love utterly, preferring you to everything, even his own talent—”

“What does that signify if I love him?”

“Ah, true!” cried the dwarf.

At that instant Madame Mignon was saying to her friends, “My daughter saw the man she loves this morning.”

“Then it must have been that sulphur waistcoat which puzzled you so, Latournelle,” said his wife. “The young man had a pretty white rose in his buttonhole.”

“Ah!” sighed the mother, “the sign of recognition.”

“And he also wore the ribbon of an officer of the Legion of Honor. He is a charming young man. But we are all deceiving ourselves; Modeste never raised her veil, and her clothes were huddled on like a beggar-woman’s—”

“And she said she was ill,” cried the notary; “but she has taken off her mufflings and is just as well as ever.”

“It is incomprehensible!” said Dumay.

“Not at all,” said the notary; “it is now as clear as day.”

“My child,” said Madame Mignon to Modeste as she entered, followed by Butscha, “did you see a well-dressed young man at church this morning, with a white rose in his button-hole?”

“I saw him,” said Butscha quickly, perceiving by everybody’s strained attention that Modeste might fall into a trap. “It was Grindot, the famous architect, with whom the town is in treaty for restoring the church. He has just come from Paris, and I met him this morning examining the exterior as I was on my way to Sainte-Adresse.”

“Oh, an architect, was he? he puzzled me,” said Modeste, for whom Butscha had thus gained time to recover composure.

Dumay looked askance at Butscha. Modeste, fully warned, recovered her impenetrable calm. Dumay’s distrust was thoroughly aroused, and he resolved to visit the mayor’s office early the next morning to ascertain if the architect had been in Havre the previous day. Butscha, on the other hand, was determined to go to Paris and learn more about Canalis.

Gobenheim came to play whist, calming the household’s excitement. Modeste waited impatiently for her mother’s bedtime to write; she wrote only at night. Here is the letter love dictated while the world slept.

To Monsieur de Canalis,—Ah, my friend, my beloved! How false those shop-window portraits are! I was foolish to delight in that lithograph. I am humbled to love one so handsome; Parisian women must have adored you. I do not believe your claims of loneliness and obscure desire; you’ve been loved too well—your brow betrays it. Who am I to be called to life by you? For a moment my soul broke free from the body; the world spoke to me. The old church shone like an Italian cathedral; the organ sang angelic melodies. Havre’s pavements became a meadow; the sea spoke like an old friend. The roses seemed to smile as I left the church and I heard your name, Melchior, in the bells and clouds. I live because of you; my poet, more beautiful than cold Lord Byron. One glance of yours pierced my veil and sent warmth through me. My life will answer to your thought only. I know now music’s divine purpose—to utter love. Ah, to have genius and beauty is too much; a man should choose between them at birth.

You have given me tenderness beyond dreaming, especially this month; sometimes I think it a dream. Yet you hide something—what woman can give you to me and not die? Jealousy has entered my heart. Strange revulsion—I would rather you be ugly.

How foolish I was after coming home! Yellow dahlias recalled your waistcoat, white roses were my friends. I shall remember every trifle of that day—the atmosphere, a ray of sun on a pillar, the prayer your step interrupted, the incense, the priestly blessing as you passed me. The good Abbé Marcelin married us then! My happiness is beyond earth; I unmask. Come back soon; I unmask with joy.

You have heard of the house of Mignon in Havre? Through misfortune I am its sole heiress. But do not look down on us; the Mignon de La Bastie arms will honor Canalis. Our motto is “Una fides, unus Dominus!”—one true faith and one master. My name is Modeste—you are not deceived by the signature “O. d’Este M.” Our fortune will be what made you so virtuous. Let it free our happiness; when fancy takes us, “Come, let us go in a comfortable carriage” without a thought of money. Thus Modeste Mignon can serve you, and her gold will be nobly used.

As to your servant—you saw her once at her window. The Modeste of long ago is dead; this Modeste has received from you the life of life. Love, sanctioned by my father’s return rich, has raised me. I tremble lest I not deserve my fate. The king has come in his glory; my lord has now a subject who apologizes for liberties taken. My poet! I will be thy Mignon—happier than Goethe’s, for you will keep me in your heart. A nightingale in the park answers for you. If his note does not lie to me, tell me so. My father will pass through Paris; go to him, but do not tell him how I love you—let that be between us and God. I will tell my mother; she will justify me. You have the daughter’s confession; obtain my father’s consent. P.S.—Do not come to Havre without my father’s consent.

Dumay caught Modeste up late and questioned her; she claimed to be writing to her father because he was leaving in the morning. That night she sealed two letters—one to Canalis, one to her father—but she mistakenly reversed the envelopes. François Cochet, terrified when he found an envelope addressed to Canalis among those mailed from Havre, brought a letter to Modeste and took away the one she had written to Canalis. The letter he brought was from Ernest de La Brière, who confessed that in his embarrassment the veiled, disguised woman he had seen might be Modeste. The letter revealed the secretary’s yearning; he wished to cast off his disguise and be honest.

Modeste was unsettled. She demanded an immediate explanation from Ernest.

Meanwhile other events in Havre complicated matters. Dumay discovered no architect had been in town, and Butscha had gone to Paris, claiming his father had been found there. Dumay suspected a conspiracy and rushed to Latournelle and then to the Chalet. Latournelle had seen a shipping notice that Captain Mignon’s ship—the Bettina Mignon—had arrived from Marseilles, so the colonel must be in Paris. Dumay resolved to go after Butscha to learn what he knew about Modeste.

Modeste, thinking Butscha had been sent to investigate her poet’s morals, fainted with alarm. She had given Dumay the letter she thought addressed to Canalis; Dumay, seeing the address “Monsieur le Baron de Canalis, rue de Paradis-Poissoniere,” cried out in surprise. Madame Mignon told them that the stanzas Modeste had set to music were by Canalis and that his portrait hung upstairs. Modeste snatched back the letter and vowed that if Dumay interfered with Canalis she would leave the house forever. Dumay left for Paris in haste, Monte to fetch Canalis or to pursue Butscha; the parents and friends rallied round the distraught Modeste.

Modeste confessed everything to her mother—then sank between hope and shame. Her mother listened with a mixture of rebuke and understanding. Modeste read her letters aloud and wept. She declared her love pure, divine, and eternal, but then feared being exposed to vulgar scrutiny and the tearing of her “diaphanous wings.” She fell ill, chilled and feverish; her mother and friends tended her as Dumay raced to Paris. The narrative shifts to the city.

Ernest de La Brière, modest and sensitive, was elated to be believed handsome by Modeste. But his joy turned to dread when he realized she thought him to be Canalis and that she had substantial fortune. Conscience made him determine to confess everything and to find Modeste’s father in Paris, so he sought the banker Mongenod to trace the colonel.

Dumay, a Breton soldier by nature, disdained poets as idle, shabby fools—until he arrived at Canalis’s house and found evidence of rank: servants, a groom washing a carriage, a valet like a banker. Canalis was not a garret poet but president of the Court of Claims attached to the foreign ministry. Surrounded by tokens of patronage and honor, Dumay was taken aback and allowed Canalis to speak first.

Canalis received Dumay with calm superiority. Dumay accused him of seducing the young and rich Modeste of Havre and demanded his intentions. Canalis feigned ignorance and declaimed about preserving the letters of young admirers in a precious casket, treating their devotion with veneration rather than suspicion. He allowed Dumay to search the casket to prove his point. Dumay, outraged but silenced by the poet’s manner, begged for help in investigating. Canalis promised to assist as opportunity allowed. Dumay left bewildered; Butscha, meanwhile, had cleverly been investigating Canalis in his own way.

La Brière entered Canalis’s study and confessed to the poet that Modeste loved him, believing him to be Canalis, and that Modeste’s father, Colonel Mignon, supposedly had great wealth. He revealed he was the true correspondent and that his conscience impelled him to come clean. Canalis, who had in truth forgotten Modeste’s letter, masked disappointment with gentility and magnanimously offered to further Ernest’s hopes—yet inwardly considered the girl’s fortune a dazzling prize. La Brière, idealistic, swore himself to Modeste.

The Comte de La Bastie—Colonel Mignon—sank into grief upon hearing of Bettina's death, his wife's infirmity, and Modeste’s secret. He wept inwardly, then received La Brière. Ernest, humble and honest, declared his love and explained the deception. When Mignon read the letters, he disclosed the principal error: Modeste would not have six million francs as rumored; at most two hundred thousand and questionable prospects. La Brière, relieved, felt his path to happiness cleared. Mignon, impressed by La Brière’s candor, set a test: both La Brière and Canalis should come to Havre for the last two weeks of October; Modeste would compare the “real” man and the “poet,” the illusion and the substance. Ernest must bring Canalis himself and refrain from revealing the earlier deception. Mignon would go to Havre and expected both to follow. La Brière accepted, broken but honorable.

Canalis, having reflected, saw the possibility of rank and riches—the match might make him a peer. He was furious at missing an opportunity and at La Brière’s inadvertent advantage. But by the time La Brière returned the poet resolved to help him—and privately to compete. Both men, despite differing motives—La Brière by duty, Canalis by ambition—prepped to present themselves in Normandy. Mignon and Dumay set off to Havre; the colonel planned a lesson in character for his daughter.

On arrival at the Chalet the household greeted the count’s return with joy. The colonel, tender with his wife, watched Modeste with delight; Modeste, pale but composed, lavished kisses on her father. Dumay’s silence and the colonel’s resolve unnerved her. The colonel told Modeste to rise early next day for a walk by the shore—there would be talk about her poems. On the walk he told her he knew all and more than she did. He reproached her for writing to a stranger without consulting family and for treating marriage as a poem rather than a matter of prudence. Modeste defended herself: she had been drawn by genius, not by a stranger. The colonel argued family oversight existed to spare girls from the dangers of romantic illusion. He insisted Modeste must choose between La Brière—the plain, honorable secretary—and Canalis—the famed poet she admired. To his surprise, Modeste swore to obey him in the matter of marriage. He warned her that the man she loved might be a deceiver; she insisted she loved an honest, candid soul. The colonel insisted the family must guide her and set a plan: she must see both candidates in the country among ordinary pursuits and choose freely. Modeste promised to obey.

Disenchanted, Modeste fell from poetic heights into the cold plain of reality. Her imagination—the source of her idealizing love—found in daily life only humiliation; she became numb and morose. Once exultant, she now tasted savage pleasure in her suffering and withdrew into morbid melancholy. Yet the shock clarified her mind: her head cleared, malice and discernment grew. She developed a resolve to humiliate La Brière—revenge on the false Canalis—though her mother counseled courtesy.

A third suitor soon arrived. Latournelle declared that the Duc d’Herouville, a titled but impecunious grand equerry and peer of France, had offered himself after seeing Modeste at the Vilquins. Rumor already exaggerated the Mignon fortune, and the duke’s family pressed the match. The duke, thin, pale, and timid but with courtly prestige, was as much a caricature of faded nobility as many such provincial peers; yet titles still had weight. The house gossip noted that Modeste, now thought an heiress, had revived attention and competition.

Gobenheim and others speculated about Colonel Mignon’s wealth. The colonel, refusing to part with his chosen servants, installed them and began to live in a style that stirred local rumor. In time the arrival of Canalis and La Brière at a villa at Sanvic was announced; they would come to the Chalet the following evening. Canalis, anxious to make a strong impression, engaged Germain, his valet, to arrange a grand household, and in Havre Germain’s air and insistence on treating La Brière as a secretary suggested the social gap that would work against Ernest. Canalis sedulously cultivated effects—carriage painted with arms, servants, and airs; he planned to appear indifferent and disdainful to pique Modeste. La Brière, sincere and despondent, saw himself as prose against Canalis’s poetry. Nevertheless, both men planned to spend an evening at the Chalet.

Canalis’s entrance at the Chalet was studied: he adopted pompous postures, declamatory diction, and the theatrical manner he had perfected under the Duchesse de Chaulieu’s flattery. His affectations, harmless in Paris, were conspicuous in provincial Havre. He lavished public homage on war and nation and on the local veterans, flattering Dumay and praising Napoleon’s soldiers with lofty rhetoric to win the provincials’ respect. He charmed the women and Modeste with talk of poetry and politics, arguing poets could become statesmen. Modeste, determined to worship him, sat as at a theater; La Brière, modest, felt eclipsed but listened to her. Canalis complimented La Brière exaggeratedly, calling him “Pylades” and praising his talents; poor Ernest, flattered, took it as a balm.

The visit ended with an invitation to dinner. Modeste and the mothers debated whether Canalis was rich, and whether modish fame or real fortune mattered. The colonel suggested Modeste might appear in the splendor of an heiress if she ordered her Paris wardrobe. Many of the household, including Gobenheim, quipped about marriages and disguises. They expected more visitors soon.

At night on the seashore, La Brière brooded on his hopeless love. Butscha, the hunchbacked clerk and unofficial guardian of Modeste’s reputation, revealed himself and spoke with surprising eloquence about devotion and study of women. He claimed to be one of three pledged to serve Modeste and to guard her happiness. He knew the local intrigues, the duchess’s vindictiveness, and the valet Germain’s ties; Butscha promised to watch Canalis and to see whether the great poet could love truly. La Brière, heartened by Butscha’s fidelity, treated him as a comrade. Butscha declared he studied love as a science and would soon reveal Canalis’s true character, and he intended to ensure Modeste’s happiness. La Brière resolved to make a friend of the dwarf.

The next morning the two Parisian guests rode and explored the country; both felt the strain between devotion and ambition. Canalis smugly assumed an advantage in society; Ernest resolved on stoic endurance, to love Modeste quietly and honorably even if she chose another. Germain bought flamboyant horses and the ducal and Canalis households prepared to stake their reputations.

Desplein, the famous surgeon, arrived, examining Madame Mignon’s cataract; he promised the mother she might regain sight. His brisk competence, studious manner, and a moment of gallantry toward Modeste impressed her—an encounter that revealed to her the difference between true genius and the pose of Canalis. She served his simple breakfast; he promised to help her mother see again, and his gravity and skill charmed her. The household hoped meanwhile for the forthcoming theatre of suitors, as Modeste prepared to see three men—the poet, the honest secretary, and the titled duke—determine in practice whether she would choose fame, substance, or rank.

This visit of the great surgeon was the event of the day and left a luminous trace in Modeste’s soul. The young enthusiast admired the man whose life belonged to others and in whom long study of pain had destroyed manifestations of egoism. That evening, with Gobenheim, the Latournelles, Butscha, Canalis, Ernest, and the Duc d’Herouville in the salon, they all congratulated the Mignon family on the hopes Desplein encouraged. The conversation, with Modeste again predominant, turned on the man whose genius, appreciable chiefly by the faculty and men of science, drew praise and a characteristic remark from Gobenheim—“He makes a mint of money.”

“They say he is very grasping,” added Canalis.

Modeste’s praise of Desplein annoyed the poet. Vanity behaves like a woman; both men feel defrauded when love or praise is given to another. Canalis challenged the judgment of genius.

“Do you think, monsieur,” said Modeste, smiling, “that we should judge genius by ordinary standards?”

“Perhaps we ought first to define the man of genius,” replied Canalis. “One condition is invention—form, system, force. Napoleon invented war-making; Scott, Linnaeus, Cuvier renewed arts and sciences. But Desplein merely applies known laws skillfully; he is an executive genius, like Moscheles or Paganini—performers, not creators. To one belongs the immortal crown, to the other innumerable five-franc pieces.”

Ernest de La Brière quietly opposed this exclusiveness. “Genius must be estimated by utility,” he said. “Parmentier popularized the potato, Jacquard the loom: they change a state. In that sense Desplein is a man of genius; he follows a generation of sufferers whose pains he relieves.”

Modeste opposed Ernest’s view. “If that be so, then the man who could discover a way to mow wheat without injuring the straw, by a machine that could do the work of ten men, would be a man of genius.”

“Yes, my daughter,” said Madame Mignon; “and the poor would bless him—he that is blessed by the poor is blessed of God.”

“That is putting utility above art,” Modeste objected.

“Without utility what would become of art?” said Charles Mignon. “What would support it? You must be lodged and paid.”

“Oh! my dear papa, such opinions are flat and antediluvian!” Modeste retorted, insolent. Her impertinence astonished Madame Mignon, Madame Latournelle, and Dumay. Butscha looked to Monsieur Mignon for indignation.

“A little more, young lady, and you will be wanting in respect for your father,” said the colonel, smiling.

“I am your only child,” she answered saucily.

“Child, indeed,” remarked the notary, significantly.

“Monsieur,” Modeste turned upon him, “my father is delighted to have me for his governess; he gave me life and I give him knowledge; he will soon owe me something.”

“There seems occasion for it,” said Madame Mignon.

Canalis rose, standing before the fireplace in one of his grandest attitudes, and weighed in. “God has given food and clothing to man, but not art. We need the life of the soul as much as that of the body—hence, two utilities. True, an epic does not feed like a charity kitchen. But industrial perfections will not breathe life into a people nor tell futurity of a civilization. Works of genius are the summum of civilization; they presuppose utility. Nations cherish survival beyond death; the survival of a people is the work of men of genius. France, though excelled by England in commerce, leads the world in art and taste. We shall reign longer by books than by swords. In La Brière’s system, all that is glorious and lovely would be suppressed. All useful things are ugly; a kitchen is indispensable, but you live in the salon and adorn it. There is nothing beautiful but what seems useless.”

“Rubbish! yes, that may be, but my rubbish is dear to me,” laughed the Duc d’Herouville.

Butscha attacked Canalis. “Does art exist at all? Isn’t it a splendid lie? I don’t want a landscape painting when I can see one done by God. Happiness and virtue exist above art and genius.”

“Bravo, Butscha!” cried Madame Latournelle.

Canalis’s long rhetoric lost the country-folk. Modeste’s indifference toward La Brière and disrespect for her father depressed the young man. The duke took up Butscha’s argument, declaring Saint Theresa’s ecstasies superior to Lord Byron. Modeste countered, “Hers was purely personal poetry, whereas the genius of Byron and Molière benefits the world.”

Charles Mignon teased Modeste. The exchange widened the gulf between Modeste and Ernest. La Brière’s quiet voice contrasted with the baron’s peremptory tone. Canalis asserted that every work of art implies a social utility: books employ paper-makers, printers, booksellers; a symphony or an opera needs hands and machinery. Works of genius have an expensive basis and are useful to labor. He spoke with fine metaphor and inward complacency, then returned unknowingly to La Brière’s position.

“He will make an admirable constitutional minister,” said the little duke slyly.

“Oh!” replied Canalis with a grand gesture, “what is the use of these discussions? All things are true and false according to surroundings.”

“He is a poet,” whispered Gobenheim.

Canalis, ten leagues above his audience, took the coolness as provincial ignorance, content that Modeste understood him. He spoke of the Duchesse de Chaulieu with rapture, declaring his gratitude and affection. Modeste, affected, said, “Who can think of a poet without a muse!”

“He would be without a heart,” replied Canalis.

Dumay reminded Canalis of having denied feeling to which Canalis ironically replied with a boast of loving as well as La Brière and being able to give his love literary form. His triumph led him to declaim; Modeste, swept into his labyrinth of fancy, thought, “He loves me!” Butscha noted Canalis’s declamations and inattention and later commented cruelly: Canalis’s habit of not listening often offended and wounded others. Not to listen is disrespect; Canalis often forgot to answer a speech, passing abruptly to what preoccupied him. That impertinence breeds hatred.

Modeste begged Canalis to read some poems. He took the volume and cooed an imitation of Moore’s “Loves of the Angels” titled “Vitalis,” greeted by yawns. Gobenheim’s remark about whist raised a laugh; Canalis flung the volume impatiently on a table. The incident showed the peril of a drawing-room hero stepping out of his sphere.

The whist game began with Canalis, the duke, Gobenheim, and Latournelle as partners. Modeste sat near the poet, much to Ernest’s disappointment. La Brière watched Modeste and the progress of Canalis’s fascination. He lacked the gift of seduction; there is always a comic element in the actor-poet’s art of feigning sentiment to win. Ernest imitated the duke, becoming gloomy and watchful, prey to concentrated jealousy; he could not obtain a glance from Modeste. He left the room with Butscha.

“It is all over!” he said. “She is caught by him; I am more disagreeable to her, and she is right. Canalis is charming; there’s intellect in his silence, passion in his eyes, poetry in his rhodomontades.”

“Is he an honest man?” asked Butscha.

“Oh, yes,” La Brière replied. “He is loyal and chivalrous, capable of getting rid, under Modeste’s influence, of those affectations Madame de Chaulieu taught him.”

“You are a fine fellow,” said the hunchback; “but will he love her?”

“I don’t know,” answered La Brière.

He asked whether Modeste had said anything about him. Butscha repeated Modeste’s speech about disguises. La Brière was beside himself, flung onto a bench, and wept. He described how he had tried to undeceive Modeste before she took off the mask and how her recent letter had been flattering to the individual he wished. Butscha urged him to show himself as he was and not let his rival practice his exercises. La Brière lamented his inability to speak when she was present, the strange physical sensations her looks caused.

At this moment Modeste, followed by Canalis who had lost the rubber, came out with her father and Madame Dumay to breathe the starry night. La Brière left. The next day Modeste, favoring Canalis, walked alone with him across the gravel. The d’Herouville ladies interrupted, turning conversation to court appointments and the distinction of rank: “To have a duke for a son,” said one, “is a fortune.” Canalis, displeased, asked why the duke had had little success despite title; the ladies shot him venomous glances. Ten days later the tide at the Chalet turned against Canalis. The poet’s prodigality of wit tired those preferring the solid; forced to behave like an ordinary man, his faults were exposed. La Brière, by contrast, was sober, discreet, and gaining ground: modesty, simplicity, and utility won favor.

Canalis, impulsive and ambitious, displayed impatience, depression, and capricious moods noted by Madame Latournelle and others. The attention shifted to La Brière. For two days the poet worked to fascinate Modeste alone, weaving passionate language. The d’Herouville ladies slandered him. At dinner the duke proposed arranging a hunt in a crown forest; glances among the d’Herouville group said, “The heiress is ours!” The poet, sensing danger, resolved to get a pledge then and there. Modeste, half-frightened, hinted at the calumnies to him and begged him to be silent; he promised.

“These stabs of the tongue are fair in the great world,” he said. “They shock you; I laugh at them; I am pleased. These ladies must feel the duke’s interests are in peril.”

Canalis entered his defence with warmth and passion and declared himself plainly with vows and protestations. Modeste listened.

“Monsieur,” she said, “freedom granted me by my parents has allowed me to listen to you; but you must address them.”

“But,” cried Canalis, “tell me that if I obtain their consent, you will ask nothing better than to obey them.”

“I know beforehand,” she replied, “my father has fancies that may wound your family’s pride. He wishes his name borne by grandsons.”

“Ah, dear Modeste, what sacrifices would I not make to commit my life to an angel like you.”

“You will permit me not to decide in a moment the fate of my whole life,” she said, turning to rejoin the d’Herouville ladies.

Butscha waylaid Modeste at the portico. “Mademoiselle, I do hope you don’t call him Melchior.”

“I’m very near it, my Black Dwarf,” she said sweetly.

“Good God!” exclaimed Butscha.

“You?” she said, “would your La Brière accept me without a fortune?”

“Ask your father,” replied Butscha. “I am ready to give not only my life but my honor for you. Has that sublime Canalis made the disinterested offer you flung as a reproach at poor Ernest?”

“Yes.”

“Do you believe it?”

“That question undervalues the strength of my self-love.”

“You are laughing, mademoiselle; then there’s no danger: I hope you are only making a fool of him.”

“Pray what would you think of me, Monsieur Butscha, if I allowed myself to make fun of those who do me the honor to wish to marry me? Even if a girl affects to despise the most despicable attentions, she is flattered by them.”

“Then I flatter you?” he said, illuminated.

“You give me the most precious friendship—a feeling as disinterested as a mother’s for a child. Compare yourself to no one; even my father is obliged to be devoted to me.” She paused. “I cannot say I love you, in the sense men give the word, but what I give you is eternal.”

“Then,” said Butscha, bowing, “suffer me to watch over you as a dragon guards a treasure. The poet covered you with lace-work and tinsel of his promises. If, as soon as he finds your small fortune, he changes, will you still marry him? shall you still esteem him?”

“He would be another Francisque Althor,” she said bitterly.

“Make him play that pretty little comedy, and—”

“That I will! he shall play it through and through within three days—on Wednesday. Until then amuse yourself with the little tunes of the lyre so the discords may come out more distinctly.”

Modeste returned to the salon where La Brière, sitting by the window, rose respectfully as she approached. He followed her with a furtive look; she sat by Canalis, who would have looked at her more boldly. La Brière and Butscha paced the terrace.

“It is nine o’clock,” cried Ernest. “I shall start for Paris; I can get there by tomorrow morning. From you she will accept anything; let me give her a riding-whip in your name.”

“Ah, you are happy,” said Butscha ruefully; “you have money!”

He rode to Paris, secured a place on a mail-coach back, and bought a gold fox-head whip-handle with a ruby—seven thousand francs—engraved with La Bastie’s arms and placed in a morocco case stamped with interlaced M.’s. He returned Wednesday morning to breakfast with Canalis. Butscha met him and instructed Germain the valet to put the box on Modeste’s dressing-table.

Canalis and Germain spoke apart. Germain reported gossip from the quays and fishermen indicating the brig connected with Monsieur Mignon had been sold to another’s account, and the cargo was worth only three or four hundred thousand francs at most. Canalis served champagne and took the gossip lightly. Butscha, hidden prompter, requested the secretary to say nothing about his trip and not to interfere. He had exploited an unfavorable feeling in Havre about Monsieur Mignon’s fortune by urging fishermen to speak ill; they obliged. Butscha was a lively slanderer, and over wine he poured forth scandalous gossip. He declared Modeste might have two or three hundred thousand in ready money and that Dumay’s property might add to her fortune—perhaps six hundred thousand francs when the old soldiers died.

This coarse honesty sobered Canalis. To Butscha such a sum seemed colossal. La Brière, bound by a secrecy oath to the colonel, had not meant to disclose the earlier rumor to Canalis. Pressed, Ernest revealed that Colonel Mignon had told him in Paris he did not possess the colossal fortune spoken of; he intended to give two hundred thousand francs to his daughter in ready money, entailing his estates for descendants to retain the title. Canalis, who had expected to marry with a fortune to equal his own, was shaken.

“Will you promise me on your word of honor to forget what I tell you and to behave to me as though this confidence had never been made?” Ernest asked.

“I’ll swear that,” Canalis answered.

Butscha dozed; Canalis brooded: “Fool! then take her,” he said, “and break your oath.” But he feared losing both love and office. Yet Butscha urged directness: go speak frankly to Colonel Mignon.

Canalis rose to dress for the excursion. Butscha, pursued by servants’ laughter, took a kiosk and wallowed in triumph at having fooled a great man.

Modeste appeared in a bottle-green habit, showing the gifted whip. “Did it come from you, Monsieur le duc?” she said, holding the handle toward him. “There was a card saying ‘Guess if you can.’ Françoise and Dumay credit Butscha; my father sent his with a turquoise top.”

“I would give ten years of my old age,” said the duke, “to offer you that jewel.”

“Ah, here comes the audacious giver!” cried Modeste as Canalis rode up. “La Brière rode at full gallop from Havre?”

“Does your secretary take such liberties?” she asked, turning pale, and threw the whip to Françoise with scorn. “Give me your whip, papa.”

“Poor Ernest, half-dead with fatigue!” said Canalis, overtaking her. “You are pitiless, mademoiselle. The poor fellow said, ‘I have but this one chance to remain in her memory.’”

“And should you think well of a woman who could take presents from half the parish?” she returned.

She received no answer; Canalis’s inattention attributed to horse’s feet. His admiration seemed too intense to be natural. At a cross-road overlooking the Seine, Canalis let Modeste and the duke pass, then joined the colonel.

“Monsieur le comte,” he said, “you are an open-hearted soldier. I am twenty-nine, without landed estates, full of ambition. Mademoiselle Modeste pleases me. The question of money is of the utmost importance. I have about thirty thousand francs a year; if my wife brought me as much more, I should be about equal. Shall you give Mademoiselle a million?”

“We have not reached that point,” said the colonel cautiously.

“Then suppose we go no further. Give me your word of honor to say nothing, even to Mademoiselle Modeste, because my circumstances may change and I may ask for her without dowry.”

“I promise,” said the colonel. “People exaggerate fortunes. I await accounts of my agents; the sale of my merchandise and ship is not yet concluded. I did say to La Brière in Paris I would guarantee two hundred thousand francs in ready money. I wish to entail my estates and enable grandchildren to inherit my arms and title.”

Canalis barely listened. They reached a table-land with a view of the Seine valley. “Butscha was right; God is the greatest landscape painter,” he said.

Modeste remarked on fictitious wants and vanities; Canalis called it a bucolic written on tablets of gold. The colonel invited the duke and Canalis to dine in riding-dress. That evening Modeste gave La Brière’s whip back: “Please ask Monsieur de La Brière to keep it until you know whether we are rich enough to buy it back.” The colonel kissed her forehead approvingly.

Canalis, alarmed at the indecency of his earlier conduct and its consequences, tried to excuse himself to Modeste, pleading jealousy and the delicacy of a poet’s sensibility. “I attach more value to words and looks than ordinary men,” he said, “and demand infantile security.” Modeste countered, calling his demand excessive and resenting his mention of the duchess. “I admire you, my dear poet,” she said lightly, but rebuked his presumption. Their intercourse cooled; Canalis left early, and the field opened to the duke. Butscha, walking with Madame Latournelle, whispered in Modeste’s ear, “Was I right?”

“Alas, yes,” she answered. “Anger got the better of me.”

“Well, so much the better. When you are both so angry you can’t speak, I will make him desperately in love until you are deceived yourself.”

“Come, Butscha; he is a great poet; he is a gentleman; he is a man of intellect.”

“Your father’s millions are more to him than all that,” answered Butscha.

“Eight millions!” cried Modeste.

“My master, who sold his practice, is going to Provence to attend to buying lands your father’s agent suggested. The purchase is four millions; your father has agreed. You are to have a dot of two millions and another million for a Paris establishment. Count up.”

“If it hadn’t been for that comedian Canalis you would have kept his whip thinking it came from me,” said Butscha, advocating La Brière.

Modesty’s reaction sealed matters. Canalis, sleepless, composed a letter to the Duchesse de Chaulieu explaining his absence and his interference on La Brière’s behalf. He described Mademoiselle Modeste as “a rather pale, insignificant, thread-papery little thing” who liked literature and had coquetted with the duchess’s Melchior. He asked Eleonore to verify facts through banker Mongenod about Monsieur Mignon’s fortune and to help La Brière by transmission of a father-in-law’s title. His confidential reminiscences were too intimate.

Meanwhile Butscha, under the name Jean Jacmin, had received and replied to a letter from his fictitious cousin Philoxene, and the duchess, wounded by Melchior’s silence, dictated and read replies and then summoned Mongenod to inquire about Monsieur Mignon’s fortune. Finding evidence that Modeste had wealth, Eleonore—who had felt betrayed and angry—quickly changed to generosity after reading Canalis’s letter: if he had not been faithless, she would give him the girl and her millions—provided Mademoiselle Mignon was as ugly as Canalis had written.

The duke arrived. The duchess, eager, wrote to Diane de Maufrigneuse and to Mademoiselle d’Herouville to arrange an invitation and hinted to secure the hunt. Eleonore sent Canalis a sharp answer:

My dear poet,—Mademoiselle de La Bastie is very beautiful; Mongenod has proved she has millions. I thought of marrying you to her; I am displeased at your want of confidence. You are a child. The duke thinks you less than a gentleman, reflecting on your mother’s honor. Now I intend to see things for myself and will manage to have you invited to Rosembray.

Canalis flung the letter at Ernest across breakfast. “That’s the two thousandth love-letter I have had from that woman,” he cried. “Marry, try your luck! The worst marriage is better than this halter.”

Caught between two lies, Canalis was at a loss. “Play by rule, and you lose!” he muttered, then resolved that if he must retire, his ten years of fidelity to Eleonore would ensure some good match. The hunt would unite passions and prove a truce among adversaries. Canalis wished to appear courteous and simply charming; he talked finances with Gobenheim and war with the colonel, biasing them in favor of La Brière. The d’Herouville ladies continued their intrigues to secure Modeste’s dot. Invitations from the Duc de Verneuil and the Master of the Hunt extended to the Basties to stay at Rosembray for a grand hunt.

La Brière, full of dark presentiments, craved Modeste’s presence. The duke arrived to arrange Modeste’s departure; the colonel invited the suitors to breakfast the morning of the start. Canalis planned to reconquer Modeste and throw over the duchess, La Brière had resolved to bid eternal farewell, and each suitor prepared a last plea. The colonel pressed Modeste for decision.

“Our position with the d’Herouville family will be intolerable at Rosembray,” he said. “Do you mean to be a duchess?”

“No, father,” she answered.

“Then do you love Canalis?”

“No, papa, a thousand times no!” she exclaimed; the colonel rejoiced.

“Ah, I chose in Paris the man of fixed principles and sound moralities,” he said slyly.

“I was not even thinking of him, papa. Please leave me liberty to refuse the duke myself; after a glimpse of court life at Rosembray I will tell you my secret.”

“Monsieur de La Brière,” cried the colonel, “I hope you are going to this hunt?”

“No, colonel, I have come to take leave; I return to Paris—”

“You have no curiosity,” said Modeste.

“If that is all, you must stay to please me; I wish it,” said the colonel, moving away to meet Canalis and leaving daughter and suitor together.

“Mademoiselle,” said La Brière, raising his eyes, “let me carry away with me your forgiveness. My life can never be happy; it will be full of remorse—”

“Before we part forever,” said Modeste interrupting, “I wish to ask you one thing; though you once disguised yourself, I think you cannot be so base as to deceive me now. Will you be frank?”

La Brière related the scene in the poet’s study and the challenge that followed. “You have said enough,” said Modeste, restraining emotion. “If you have not my heart, monsieur, you have at least my esteem.” These words gave him a violent shock; staggering, he leaned against a tree. Modeste left him but returned, taking his hand. “What is the matter?” she asked.

“Forgive me—I thought you despised me.”

“But,” she answered, distant, “I did not say that I loved you.”

She left him. Yet the esteem she’d given filled him with joy. Modeste saw in La Brière’s sensitivity the possibility of a love that might fulfill the ideal every woman hopes for.

Canalis approached Modeste later. “In spite of the little value you attach to my sentiments, my honor is concerned in effacing a stain under which I have suffered,” he said, and let her read lines from the duchess’s letter that proved Mongenod’s inquiry and intention to marry Modeste to La Brière—he tore that passage from the letter and gave the fragment to Modeste. “I cannot let you read the rest,” he added; “but a girl who could accuse me of ignoble motives is capable of suspecting collusion. Ah, Modeste,” he added with tears, “your poet has as much poetry in his heart as in his mind. Suspend judgment until you have seen the duchess.”

“Oh, dear!” she said; “it seems they are all angels—and not marriageable; the duke is the only one that belongs to humanity.”

Butscha appeared with a parcel. He had been busy with Spanish bits and had shown a piece to the colonel, who thanked him generously. The dwarf departed like a man who’d heard of a rich uncle.

“My dear father,” Modeste said to the colonel in the salon, “I would like that beautiful whip—suppose you ask Monsieur de La Brière to exchange it for your picture by Van Ostade.” She looked furtively at Ernest as the colonel made the proposition; she thought, “He will be at the hunt.”

Curiously, Modeste’s three lovers each went to Rosembray with hearts full of hope. Rosembray—bought by the Duc de Verneuil—was a chateau of the grand order, approached by an avenue of elms, presenting a courtyard with orange trees and a façade with fluted pilasters and a pediment carved by Coysevox. Its park extended to immense ponds, gardens in the French style, and thirty acres of woodland contiguous with other forests, royal and state—a noble landscape fitting the drama to come.

Modeste’s arrival at Rosembray created a sensation. A carriage in the liveries of France drew up, followed by the grand equerry, the colonel, Canalis, and La Briere on horseback, an outrider in full dress ahead, six servants including Negroes and a mulatto behind, and the colonel’s britzka bearing two waiting-women and luggage. Four horses drew the carriage; the postilions were dressed with the exacting elegance enforced by the grand equerry. Dazzled by the display and the little Versailles before her, Modeste feared appearing awkward before the duchesses and regretted having wished for a hunt.

As she alighted she saw a plump, calm old man in a frizzed blond wig, with a fatherly smile and a monastic cheerfulness—the Duc de Verneuil, master of Rosembray. The duchess, spare, erect, and devout, the only daughter of a deceased rich chief-justice and mother of four, resembled Madame Latournelle with an abbatial grace.

“Mademoiselle d’Herouville” kissed the duchess and presented Modeste. “We have heard so much of you,” said the duchess. The Duc de Verneuil welcomed the Comte de La Bastie with admiration, and complimented Modeste as a treasure.

The duchess took Modeste into a vast salon where a dozen women grouped about the fireplace; the men lingered on the terrace except Canalis, who respectfully went to the superb Eleonore. The Duchesse de Chaulieu, at an embroidery frame, was showing Mademoiselle de Verneuil how to shade a flower.

If Modeste had pricked her finger she could not have felt a sharper sting than Madame de Chaulieu’s cold, contemptuous stare. For an instant Modeste saw nothing but that one woman and saw through her. Eleonore’s radiance masked a menacing, implacable reserve; Canalis’s attempts at intimacy slid off her like oil. Modeste’s beauty, rivaling Diane de Maugrigneuse herself, kindled Eleonore’s carefully restrained wrath.

All the women rushed to the windows to see the new wonder alight from the carriage, accompanied by her three suitors. Diane exclaimed, “She is divine! where in the world does she come from?” and they returned to their seats, pretending composure though Eleonore’s heart brimmed with poisoned feeling.

“Mademoiselle d’Herouville murmured to the Duchesse de Verneuil that Eleonore received her Melchior ungraciously,” someone commented. “The Duchesse de Maugrigneuse thinks there is a coolness between them,” added Laure de Verneuil.

“Why so?” Modeste asked Helene.

“The great poet,” said the pious duchess, motioning her daughter to silence, “left Madame de Chaulieu without a letter for more than two weeks after claiming to go to Havre for his health—while she was trying to get him the Legion of Honor command and a post at Baden.”

“Oh, that was shameful in Canalis; he owes everything to her,” said Mademoiselle d’Herouville.

“Why did not Madame de Chaulieu go to Havre?” Modeste asked innocently.

“My dear,” replied the Duchess de Verneuil, “she would let herself be cut to pieces without a cry. Look at her—she is regal; her head would smile, like Mary Stuart’s, after it was cut off.”

“Did she not write to him?” Modeste persisted.

“Diane says,” the duchess, prompted to speak, informed her, “that in answer to Canalis’s first letter she sent a cutting reply a few days ago.”

Modeste flushed on Canalis’s account; she longed, not to crush him, but to take a sharp revenge. She looked haughtily at the Duchesse de Chaulieu.

“Monsieur Melchior!” she said.

All the women sniffed and glanced from the duchess, who was whispering to Canalis over the embroidery frame, to the young girl daring to interrupt what society expected to be a lovers’ meeting. Diane nodded as if to say Modeste was right. The women, enraged at a woman of fifty-six still handsome enough to claim the dues of youth, smiled at each other in shared malice. Melchior’s look to Modeste was feverish; the duchess, with a lowered head and lioness movement, sent red, epigrammatic glances that stabbed the poet.

“Monsieur Melchior!” Modeste repeated, asserting her right to be heard.

“What, mademoiselle?” demanded Canalis. He rose reluctantly, stalled between the embroidery frame and the fireplace where Modeste sat with the Duchesse de Verneuil. Bitter reflections assailed him: obeying Modeste would end everything with his protectress; refusing would confess his dependence and squander the fruits of his scheming. Modeste’s beauty and money versus Eleonore’s rights and influence made his hesitation agonizing, like a matador’s peril in the arena.

“Mademoiselle d’Herouville hurried me from the carriage, and I left behind me,” Modeste said to Canalis, “my handkerchief—”

Canalis shrugged.

“And,” she continued, ignoring his gesture, “I had tied into one corner of it the key of a desk which contains the fragment of an important letter; have the kindness, Monsieur Melchior, to get it for me.”

Between an angel and an enraged tiger—between Modeste’s imperious youth and Eleonore’s controlled wrath—Canalis, turned livid, no longer hesitated. He was about to comply and thus compromise himself irretrievably when La Briere appeared at the salon door, seeming to him like an archangel.

“Ernest, here, Mademoiselle de La Bastie wants you,” the poet hastily said, returning to his chair.

Ernest darted to Modeste without ceremony, saw only her, took the commission with joy, and sped from the room with the secret approbation of the assembled women.

“If you speak to her, if you ever look at her, all is over between us,” the duchess had whispered to Canalis, not satisfied with Ernest’s intervention; “and remember, if I am not present, I leave eyes behind that will watch you.”

She rose—a woman of medium height but slightly too stout, still retaining beauty—and moved toward Diane de Maugrigneuse, stepping daintily like a deer. Beneath her plumpness showed the delicacy and nervous vigor that controlled and vitalized the flesh. None but women of long descent know, as Eleonore did, how to be majestic despite buxom tendencies. A philosopher might pity Philoxene yet admire the careful elegance with which Eleonore wore a morning dress like a queen’s ease. Her abundant undyed hair was simply plaited; she bared her snowy throat and shoulders and displayed the celebrated hand and arm with pardonable pride. Modeste and the duchess’s other rivals were forced to admit, “She eclipses us.” Eleonore was one of the rare grandes dames whose nobility of bearing and practiced arts defied dissection.

Madame de Chaulieu bowed to Helene and her aunt and, in a pure, even tone, said to Diane, “Is it not time to dress, duchess?” She left accompanied by her daughter-in-law and Mademoiselle d’Herouville. As she passed, she spoke softly to the old maid, who pressed her arm and murmured “You are charming,” a phrase meaning gratitude for a service rendered. Mademoiselle d’Herouville then returned to the salon to resume her rôle as spy, and her first glance apprised Canalis that the duchess had made her threat good. The poet recognized his diplomacy was insufficient for such a struggle; compelled to a humbler course, his wit now served to salvage what he could.

When Ernest returned with Modeste’s handkerchief, the poet seized his arm and led him to the terrace.

“My dear friend,” he said, “I am not only the most unfortunate but the most ridiculous man; get me out of the hornet’s nest into which I have run. Modeste is a demon; she sees my difficulty and laughs at it. She has just spoken to me of a fragment of a letter of Madame de Chaulieu which I foolishly gave her; if she shows it I can never make my peace with Eleonore. Go at once and ask Modeste to send me back that paper, and tell her, from me, that I make no pretensions to her hand. Count on her delicacy and propriety as a young girl to behave as if we had never known each other. I beg her not to speak to me; I implore her to treat me harshly—though I hardly dare ask her to feign a jealous anger, which would aid my interests amazingly. Go; I will wait here for an answer.”

On re-entering the salon, Ernest de La Briere found the Vicomte de Serizy, a young officer of the Havre guard who had come from Rosny to announce that Madame must be present at the opening of the Chambers. The importance of this constitutional solemnity—when Charles X delivered his speech with the royal family, Madame la Dauphine and Madame in their gallery—made the choice of emissary a mark of attention to Diane, adored by the charming Vicomte, son of a minister, gentleman in ordinary, and sole heir to an immense fortune. The Duchesse de Maufrigneuse tolerated his attentions chiefly to contrast his youth with Madame de Serizy’s age; whispered chronicles said she had once deprived Lucien de Rubempre of his heart.

“You will do us the pleasure, I hope, to remain at Rosembray,” said the severe duchess to the young officer.

While scandal was discussed, the devout lady closed her eyes to the duke’s carefully chosen guests; these excellent women endured much under the pretense of reclaiming lost sheep. “We reckoned without our constitutional government,” said the grand equerry; “and Rosembray, Madame la duchesse, will lose a great honor.”

“We shall be more at our ease,” replied a tall thin old man of about seventy-five, dressed in blue cloth and wearing his hunting-cap by permission of the ladies. Resembling the Duc de Bourbon, he was the Prince de Cadignan, Master of the Hunt, one of the last great French lords. Just as La Briere tried to slip behind the sofa to speak with Modeste, a man of thirty-eight, short, fat, and common in appearance entered.

“My son, the Prince de Loudon,” said the Duchesse de Verneuil to Modeste, who could not restrain her amazement at seeing the heir of a historic name reduced to such a commonplace figure.

“Gaspard,” called the duchess; he came, and she motioned to Modeste, “Mademoiselle de La Bastie, my friend.”

The heir presumptive, whose marriage with Desplein’s daughter had lately been arranged, bowed without seeming struck by her beauty. Modeste could compare this modern youth with the old reverence she had seen: the Prince de Cadignan’s homage to womanhood seemed as much to royalty as to femininity. The Duc de Rhetore, eldest son of the Duchesse de Chaulieu, bowed cavalierly. The contrast of fathers and sons suggested that young men no longer felt themselves great beings and dispensed with the duties of greatness. The fathers retained the politeness of vanished grandeur, like mountain-tops still gilded when twilight fills the valley.

Ernest at last slipped a word into Modeste’s ear, and she rose immediately.

“My dear,” thought the duchesse, mistaking Modeste for someone going to dress, and pulling a bell-rope, “they shall show you your apartment.”

Ernest accompanied Modeste to the foot of the grand staircase, presenting the plea of the luckless poet and trying to touch her feelings by describing Melchior’s agony.

“You see, he loves—he is a captive who thought he could break his chain.”

“Love in such a rapid seeker after fortune!” retorted Modeste.

“Mademoiselle, you are at the entrance of life; you do not know its defiles. The inconsistencies of a man who falls under the dominion of a woman much older than himself should be forgiven; he is really not accountable. Think how many sacrifices Canalis has made to her. He has sown too much seed of that kind to resign the harvest; the duchess represents to him ten years of devotion and happiness. You made him forget all that, and unfortunately, he has more vanity than pride; he did not reflect on what he was losing until he met Madame Chaulieu here today. If you really understood him, you would help him. He is a child, always mismanaging his life. You call him a seeker after fortune, but he seeks very badly; like all poets, he is a victim of sensations—childish, easily dazzled by anything that shines, and pursuing its glitter. He used to love horses and pictures, and he craved fame—well, he sold his pictures to buy armor and old Renaissance and Louis XV furniture; just now he is seeking political power. Admit that his hobbies are noble things.”

“You have said enough,” replied Modeste; “come,” she added, seeing her father, whom she called with a motion of her head to give her his arm; “come with me, and I will give you that scrap of paper; you shall carry it to the great man and assure him of my condescension to his wishes, but on one condition—you must thank him in my name for the pleasure I have taken in seeing one of the finest German plays performed in my honor. I have learned that Goethe’s masterpiece is neither Faust nor Egmont—” and, seeing Ernest’s puzzled look, she added, “It is Torquato Tasso! Tell Monsieur de Canalis to re-read it.” She smiled. “Repeat to your friend word for word what I say; it is not an epigram, it is the justification of his conduct—with this difference, that he will, I trust, become more and more reasonable, thanks to the folly of his Eleonore.”

The duchess’s head-woman conducted Modeste and her father to their apartment, where Francoise Cochet had already put everything in order; the colonel was astounded when he heard there were thirty other apartments in the chateau decorated with the same taste.

“This is what I call a proper country-house,” said Modeste.

“The Comte de La Bastie must build you one like it,” replied her father.

“Here, monsieur,” said Modeste, giving the bit of paper to Ernest; “carry it to our friend and put him out of his misery.”

The word our friend struck the young man’s heart. He looked at Modeste to see if there was anything real in the community of interests she seemed to admit, and she, understanding perfectly what his look meant, added, “Come, go at once, your friend is waiting.”

La Briere colored excessively, and left the room in a state of doubt and anxiety less endurable than despair. For the true lover the path that approaches happiness is like the narrow way Catholic poetry calls the entrance to Paradise—a dark passage echoing with the last cries of earthly anguish.

An hour later this illustrious company was assembled in the salon; some played whist, others conversed; the women had their embroideries, and all awaited the announcement of dinner. The Prince de Cadignan drew Monsieur Mignon out upon China and his campaigns under the empire, making him speak of the Portendueres, the L’Estorades, and the Maucombes, Provencal families; he blamed him for not seeking service and assured him nothing would be easier than to restore him to his rank as colonel of the Guard.

“A man of your birth and your fortune ought not to belong to the present Opposition,” said the prince, smiling.

This society of distinguished persons not only pleased Modeste but enabled her, during her stay, to acquire a perfection of manners she might otherwise have lacked. Seeing these women was an education; Modeste had the instinct to appropriate the distinctive qualities of Madame de Maufrigneuse and Madame de Chaulieu. Whereas a bourgeois would have ridiculed or made absurd their ways by clumsy imitation, a well-born, well-educated, right-minded young woman like Modeste fell naturally into connection with them and saw at once the differences separating the aristocratic world from the bourgeois world, the provinces from the faubourg Saint-Germain. She caught almost imperceptible shadings and perceived the grace of the grande dame without doubting she could acquire it. She noticed also that her father and La Briere appeared infinitely better in this Olympus than Canalis. The great poet, abdicating his real power—the mind—became nothing more than a courtier seeking a ministry, intriguing for an order, flattering the galaxy. Ernest de La Briere, without ambitions, could be himself; while Melchior became, to use a vulgar expression, a mere toady, courting the Prince de Loudon, the Duc de Rhetore, the Vicomte de Serizy, or the Duc de Maufrigneuse, like a man not free to assert himself, as Colonel Mignon did, proud of his campaigns and of Napoleon’s confidence. Modeste noted the strained efforts of the man of real talent seeking witticisms or compliments to please these grand personages. In a word, to Modeste’s eyes the peacock had plucked out his tail-feathers.

Toward the middle of the evening the young girl sat down with the grand equerry in a corner of the salon. She led him there purposely to end a suit she could no longer encourage if she wished to retain her self-respect.

“Monsieur le duc, if you really knew me,” she said, “you would understand how deeply I am touched by your attentions. It is because of the profound respect I feel for your character, and the friendship which a soul like yours inspires in mine, that I cannot endure to wound your self-love. Before your arrival in Havre I loved sincerely, deeply, and forever one who is worthy of being loved, and my affection for whom is still a secret; but I wish you to know—and in saying this I am more sincere than most young girls—that had I not already formed this voluntary attachment, you would have been my choice, for I recognize your noble and beautiful qualities. A few words your aunt and sister have said to me as to your intentions lead me to make this frank avowal. If you think it desirable, a letter from my mother shall recall me, on pretense of her illness, tomorrow morning before the hunt begins. Without your consent I do not choose to be present at a fete I owe to your kindness, and where, if my secret should escape me, you might feel hurt and defrauded. You will ask why I have come here at all. I could not withstand the invitation. Be generous enough not to reproach me for what was almost a necessary curiosity. But this is not the chief, not the most delicate thing I have to say you. You have firm friends in my father and myself—more so than perhaps you realize; and as my fortune was the first cause that brought you to me, I wish to say—but without intending to use it as a sedative to calm the grief gallantry requires you to testify—that my father has thought over the affair of the marshes, his friend Dumay thinks your project feasible, and they have already taken steps to form a company. Gobenheim, Dumay, and my father have subscribed fifteen hundred thousand francs, and undertake to get the rest from capitalists who will feel it in their interest to take up the matter. If I have not the honor of becoming the Duchesse d’Herouville, I have almost the certainty of enabling you to choose her, free from all trammels in your choice, and in a higher sphere than mine. Oh! let me finish,” she cried, at a gesture from the duke.

“Judging by my nephew’s emotion,” whispered Mademoiselle d’Herouville to her niece, “it is easy to see you have a sister.”

“Monsieur le duc, all this was settled in my mind the day of our first ride, when I heard you deplore your situation. This is what I have wished to say to you. That day determined my future life. Though you did not make the conquest of a woman, you have at least gained faithful friends at Ingouville—if you will deign to accord us that title.”

This careful, charming speech moved the grand equerry to tears; he seized her hand and kissed it.

“Stay during the hunt,” he said; “my want of merit has accustomed me to these refusals; but while accepting your friendship and that of the colonel, you must let me satisfy myself by the judgment of competent scientific men, that the draining of those marshes will be no risk to the company you speak of, before I agree to the generous offer of your friends. You are a noble girl, and though my heart aches to think I can only be your friend, I will glory in that title, and prove it to you at all times and in all seasons.”

“In that case, Monsieur le duc, let us keep our secret. My choice will not be known, at least I think not, until after my mother’s complete recovery. I should like our first blessing to come from her eyes.”

“Ladies,” said the Prince de Cadignan, as the guests prepared to separate for the night, “I know that several of you propose to follow the hounds with us tomorrow, and it becomes my duty to tell you that if you will be Dianas you must rise, like Diana, with the dawn. The meet is for half-past eight o’clock. I have seen many women display greater courage than men, but for a few seconds only; and you will need a strong dose of resolution to keep you on horseback the whole day, except for a halt for breakfast, which we shall take, like true hunters and huntresses, on the nail. Are you still determined to show yourselves trained horse-women?”

“Prince, it is necessary for me to do so,” said Modeste, adroitly.

“I answer for myself,” said the Duchesse de Chaulieu.

“And I for my daughter Diane; she is worthy of her name,” added the prince. “So, then, you all persist in your intentions? However, I shall arrange, for the sake of Madame and Mademoiselle de Verneuil and others who stay at home, to drive the stag to the further end of the pond.”

“Make yourself quite easy, mesdames,” said the Prince de Loudon, when the Royal Huntsman had left the room; “that breakfast ‘on the nail’ will take place under a comfortable tent.”

At dawn the next day the weather promised a fine hunt: pale gray vapor gave way to blue the northwest wind would clear. As the party left the chateau, the Master of the Hunt, the Duc de Rhetore, and the Prince de Loudon rode ahead, noting the chateau’s white mass against autumn foliage.

“The ladies are fortunate in their weather,” remarked the Duc de Rhetore.

“Oh, in spite of all their boasting,” replied the Prince de Cadignan, “I think they will let us hunt without them!”

“So they might, if each had not a squire,” said the duke.

Their attention was drawn by a loud altercation at the forest of Rosembray, the meet. The cause was plain: the Prince de Loudon, enamored of English ways, had brought his own British hunting establishment and placed it under the Master of the Hunt. One of his men, a small fair Englishman—insolent, phlegmatic, neatly dressed—had posted himself on one side. John Barry wore a scarlet frock with buttons bearing the De Verneuil arms, white breeches, top-boots, a striped waistcoat, and carried a small hunting-whip and brass horn. He was first whipper-in to fifteen horses and sixty English hounds, which had cost the Duc de Verneuil much.

John found himself forestalled by three other whippers-in in charge of two royal packs brought in carts. They were the three best huntsmen of the Prince de Cadignan, a marked contrast to the Englishman in character and costume. Each wore a broad three-cornered hat above a swarthy, wrinkled face; lean, sinewy, vigorous—men for whom sport was passion. They bore immense horns of Dampierre, wound with green worsted, but chiefly controlled their hounds by eye and voice. Those noble animals, marked white, black, or liver, had distinct looks; some young ones yelped impatiently, the old scarred hounds lay quiet with ears to the earth.

As the Englishman came up, the royal dogs and huntsmen looked at each other as though to say, “If we cannot hunt by ourselves his Majesty’s service is insulted.”

Jests grew fiercer between Monsieur Jacquin La Roulie, the old French whipper-in, and John Barry. The two princes guessed the subject from afar, and the Master of the Hunt, setting spurs to his horse, brought it to an end with authority.

“Who drew the wood?”

“I, monseigneur,” said the Englishman.

“Very good,” said the Prince de Cadignan, proceeding to take Barry’s report.

Dogs and men became silent and respectful before the Royal Huntsman, as all recognized his dignity. The prince laid out the day’s work; a hunt is like a battle, and the Master of Charles X.’s hounds was their Napoleon. Thanks to the admirable system he had introduced into French venery, he could concentrate on the science and strategy of it. He assigned driving the stag to water to the Prince de Loudon’s establishment, expecting the royal hounds to send the stag into the Crown forest on the horizon before the chateau. He soothed his old huntsmen’s pride by giving them the most arduous task, while employing the Englishman at his specialty, letting the two systems face each other and show their strengths.

“Does monseigneur wish us to wait any longer?” said La Roulie.

“I know what you mean, old friend,” said the prince. “It is late, but—”

“Here come the ladies,” said the second whipper-in.

At that moment sixteen riders approached, four ladies at the front with green veils. Modeste, accompanied by her father the grand equerry and La Briere, rode in advance beside the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, escorted by the Vicomte de Serizy. Behind them rode the Duchesse de Chaulieu, flanked by Canalis, on whom she smiled without rancor. When they reached the open where the huntsmen in red coats and brass bugles, surrounded by hounds, made a tableau, the Duchesse de Chaulieu, despite her embonpoint, rode up to Modeste, deeming it more dignified than avoiding the young woman she'd ignored the evening before.

When the Master of the Hunt finished his compliments on their punctuality, Eleonore observed the magnificent whip glittering in Modeste’s little hand and graciously asked to see it.

“I have never seen anything more beautiful,” she said, showing it to Diane de Maufrigneuse. “It suits its possessor,” she added, returning it.

“You must admit, Madame la duchesse,” answered Mademoiselle de La Bastie, with a tender malicious glance at La Briere, “that it is a rather strange gift from a future husband.”

“I should take it,” said Madame de Maufrigneuse, “as a declaration of my rights, in remembrance of Louis XIV.”

La Briere’s eyes filled; for a moment he dropped his reins; a second glance from Modeste bade him not betray his happiness. The hunt began.

The Duc d’Herouville said in a low voice to his fortunate rival, “Monsieur, I hope you will make your wife happy; if I can be of use, command me—I should be glad to contribute to the happiness of so charming a pair.”

This great day, on which such vast interests of heart and fortune were decided, caused but one anxiety to the Master of the Hunt—whether the stag would cross the pond and be killed on the lawn before the house; huntsmen of his calibre are chess-players who can foresee checkmate. The happy old man attained his wish; the run was magnificent, and the ladies were freed from his attendance for the hunt two days later—which, however, proved rainy.

The Duc de Verneuil’s guests stayed five days at Rosembray. On the last day the Gazette de France announced Baron de Canalis’s appointment as commander of the Legion of Honor and minister at Carlsruhe.

Early in December, after Desplein’s operation, Madame de La Bastie regained her sight; on seeing Ernest de La Brière she pressed Modeste’s hand and whispered, “I should have chosen him myself.”

By late February Latournelle had signed the deeds for the Provence estates, and the La Bastie family obtained the king’s signature to the marriage contract and the ordinance transmitting their title and arms to La Brière, who thereupon took the name La Brière‑La Bastie. Letters‑patent entailed the La Bastie estate at the end of April. La Brière’s witnesses at his marriage were Canalis and the minister he had served five years as secretary; the bride’s were the Duc d’Herouville and Desplein, whom the Mignons remembered with gratitude after showing their regard.

Later in this history we shall again meet Monsieur and Madame de La Brière‑La Bastie; readers will see how sweet and easy married life with an educated, intelligent woman can be. Modeste, avoiding the follies of pedantry, is the pride and happiness of her husband, her family, and all who surround her.
