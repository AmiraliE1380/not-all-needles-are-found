CHAPTER I. ONE OF MANY CHEVALIERS DE VALOIS

In various provinces of France, many Chevaliers de Valois exist, including one in Alencon, who is the focus of this tale. These chevaliers, while sharing a name, were largely unaware of each other and accepted the Bourbon monarchy, recognizing that Henri IV. ascended the throne due to the lack of a male heir from the Valois line. The Alencon chevalier, like others, was an old gentleman, tall and thin, who had seen better days. He had spent his youth in Paris, where the Revolution interrupted his romantic pursuits.

The Chevalier de Valois of Alencon was well-regarded in the local aristocracy, known for his excellent manners and wit, often sharing anecdotes from the reign of Louis XV. He was a regular at social gatherings, where his charm and discretion made him a favorite. At fifty-eight, he claimed to be fifty, maintaining a youthful appearance despite his age. His notable features included a large nose and a pale complexion, which turned red on one side due to a supposed "hot liver" condition, a claim he used to justify his voracious appetite.

Despite his frail appearance, the chevalier was vigorous and passionate, with a face that bore the marks of a life well-lived. He took great care in his grooming, presenting himself with a youthful elegance that belied his years. His attire, though somewhat worn, was always clean and well-maintained, reflecting a commitment to aristocratic standards. He lived modestly in a rented room, dining out daily and spending little on himself, relying on a modest annuity for his income.

The chevalier was a beloved figure in Alencon, known for his card-playing skills and amiable nature. He was a master of conversation, able to engage with both the high and low of society, and his presence was often sought after at social events. Despite his charm, he was also a figure of intrigue, with rumors circulating about his private life, including a supposed secret marriage to a woman named Cesarine.

Living in a modest apartment above Madame Lardot's laundry, the chevalier's life was marked by a careful balance of social engagement and personal discretion. His financial situation was precarious, yet he managed to maintain a semblance of dignity and respectability. His reputation as a gentleman was upheld by his ability to navigate the complexities of provincial life with grace.

CHAPTER II. SUSANNAH AND THE ELDERS

One spring morning, the chevalier was interrupted by the arrival of Suzanne, a young girl from Madame Lardot's laundry. She brought news that could bring him both pleasure and pain, though she feigned indifference to his rumored connection with Cesarine. Suzanne, ambitious and clever, sought to escape Alencon for Paris, where she believed she could find a better life.

The chevalier, recognizing her charm and ambition, encouraged her to pursue her dreams while maintaining a paternal demeanor. He advised her to seek out Monsieur du Bousquier, a wealthy bachelor who had shown interest in her. The chevalier understood the dynamics of their society and the potential for Suzanne to leverage her beauty and wit to secure a favorable match.

As Suzanne left to pursue her plans, the narrative shifted to du Bousquier, a man of dubious reputation who had once enjoyed wealth and influence. Now returned to Alencon, he harbored ambitions of marrying Mademoiselle Cormon, a wealthy old maid, to restore his fortunes. His past as a purveyor during the Revolution had left him with a tarnished reputation, yet he remained determined to regain his status.

Du Bousquier's character contrasted sharply with that of the chevalier. While the chevalier embodied the elegance of the old regime, du Bousquier represented the more brusque and opportunistic nature of the new political landscape. Their rivalry over Mademoiselle Cormon was subtle yet intense, with each man vying for her favor while navigating the complexities of their respective social standings.

As the story unfolds, Suzanne's ambitions and the machinations of both men intertwine, revealing the intricate dance of desire, ambition, and societal expectations in the provincial setting of Alencon. The characters' interactions reflect the broader themes of class, gender, and the pursuit of happiness in a changing world.

Madame Granson, a widow living on a modest pension and her own property, struggled to support her son, Athanase, who contributed to their income with a small salary from the mayor’s office. Their home in a provincial town was characterized by its simplicity and the weight of poverty, which shaped their lives and morals. Athanase, a pale and introspective young man of twenty-three, harbored a deep passion for his cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he believed to be wealthy. His feelings were complicated by his awareness of societal expectations and the ridicule he might face for loving an older woman.

Athanase's love for Mademoiselle Cormon began as a practical consideration for securing a better life for himself and his mother, but it evolved into genuine affection. He admired her from afar, feeling both drawn to her and constrained by his circumstances. His talent and potential remained unrecognized in their provincial setting, where ambition often clashed with the harsh realities of life.

Madame Granson, aware of her son’s feelings, saw the potential benefits of a match with Mademoiselle Cormon, despite the obstacles posed by Athanase's lack of fortune and her cousin's age. She remained hopeful and patient, waiting for the right moment to act. Meanwhile, Athanase's thoughts were consumed by his love for Mademoiselle Cormon, and he often fantasized about a future together, despite the barriers that seemed insurmountable.

The arrival of Suzanne, a young woman from a less fortunate background, introduced a new dynamic. She felt an immediate connection to Athanase, sensing his struggles and aspirations. However, her own ambitions led her to consider a different path, one that involved deception to secure a better future. After a brief encounter with Madame Granson, Suzanne left the town, equipped with money and new clothes, while the community mourned her departure.

As Athanase prepared for a dinner with Mademoiselle Cormon, his mother urged him to present himself well, hoping to enhance his chances with her. Athanase, unaware of the full extent of his mother’s plans, navigated his feelings of love and ambition amidst the constraints of their provincial life. The evening promised to be pivotal, as the intertwining fates of Athanase, Mademoiselle Cormon, and Suzanne began to unfold, each character driven by their desires and the societal pressures surrounding them. 

In the end, Suzanne's departure to Paris marked a significant turning point, as she sought to redefine her life away from the constraints of her past. Her journey reflected the struggles of many women of her time, caught between societal expectations and personal aspirations. Meanwhile, Athanase remained in Alencon, grappling with his unfulfilled dreams and the weight of his love for Mademoiselle Cormon, as the complexities of their lives continued to intertwine in the backdrop of provincial society.

In many second-class prefectures of France, there exists a salon that serves as a gathering place for influential yet not elite members of the community. The hosts of these salons are prominent figures in their towns, invited to various events, but they do not mingle with the highest social echelons, who prefer to maintain distance, communicating through formal invitations instead.

This salon, where lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates convene, wields significant influence. Its members are characterized by their indifference to luxury and fashion, focusing instead on practical matters like land acquisition. They are resistant to outside influences, particularly from Paris, and are known for their gossip, which often takes on the weight of judgment. Young women are rarely present, and when they are, it is typically to seek validation.

In Alencon, the upper classes had gathered at the home of an old maid, Mademoiselle Rose-Marie-Victoire Cormon, whose fortune was coveted by her relatives and two old bachelors. She lived with her uncle, a former grand-vicar, and her family had a long history of serving the nobility. The Cormon family had intermarried with the nobility, giving them a semblance of aristocracy despite their middle-class status.

Mademoiselle Cormon's house, built in the time of Henri IV, was a source of envy due to its prime location and well-maintained condition. The mansion featured a courtyard with a flowerbed, reflecting the meticulous care of its owner, an old maid whose attention to cleanliness was evident. The house's architecture was robust, with decorative elements that showcased its historical significance.

The interior of the house was divided into reception areas, including a salon adorned with family portraits and a dining room that exuded a provincial charm. The salon was a hub for social activities, where card games and conversations about local affairs took place. Mademoiselle Cormon's social circle consisted of about 150 individuals, mostly of mature age, who rarely traveled and had lived their lives in the provinces.

Wednesdays were special occasions when Mademoiselle Cormon hosted dinners, drawing a larger crowd. The women dressed modestly, and the atmosphere was one of familiarity and routine. Conversations often revolved around local gossip, land disputes, and the behaviors of the aristocracy, reflecting the community's values and concerns.

Mademoiselle Cormon, despite her wealth and social standing, remained unmarried, largely due to her desire to marry a nobleman and the societal constraints of her time. The political climate from 1788 to 1815 made it difficult for her to find a suitable match, as many eligible men were either too old or too young, or lacked the qualities she sought. Her fear of being married for her fortune led her to set traps for potential suitors, testing their intentions without their knowledge.

As she aged, her desire for love intensified, yet she remained oblivious to the affections of those around her, including Athanase Granson, one of her suitors. Mademoiselle Cormon's innocence and naivety prevented her from recognizing genuine feelings, leading to a series of missed opportunities. Her character became increasingly rigid, and societal pressures began to weigh heavily on her.

Despite her piety and virtuous nature, Mademoiselle Cormon was often the subject of ridicule in Alencon, her unmarried status becoming a local proverb. She lived modestly, eschewing luxury and maintaining a lifestyle that resonated with the values of her community. Her fortune, while considerable, did not elevate her above the expectations of provincial life, and her attempts at marriage were viewed with skepticism.

As the years passed, Mademoiselle Cormon's hopes for a suitable husband dwindled, and she found herself increasingly isolated in her desires. The return of disbanded soldiers and officers offered a glimmer of hope, yet none met her standards. Her longing for companionship and motherhood became a source of despair, as she grappled with the reality of her situation.

Ultimately, Mademoiselle Cormon's story reflects the complexities of love, societal expectations, and the challenges faced by women in her position. Her life, marked by a desire for connection and a struggle against the constraints of her environment, serves as a poignant commentary on the nature of happiness and fulfillment in a world governed by rigid social norms.

To complete the picture of the household of Mademoiselle Cormon, we must consider her and the Abbe de Sponde alongside their loyal servants: Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette, who all contributed to the comfort of the uncle and niece.

Jacquelin, a short, stout man with a ruddy complexion, had served the household for twenty-two years. He performed various tasks, from waiting at table to grooming the mare, Penelope, who was cherished by the household. He was in love with Josette, a thirty-year-old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if she married him. They quietly saved their wages, hoping for Mademoiselle’s marriage to free them to wed. Josette, plump and practical, was said to rule her mistress. Mariette, the cook, had been with the family for fifteen years and was skilled in preparing the local delicacies.

Penelope, the old brown-bay mare, was a source of affection and conversation among the household, as Mademoiselle Cormon, lacking children, directed her maternal instincts toward the animal. The four servants, along with Penelope, maintained a routine that reflected their long-standing roles. Mademoiselle Cormon, however, became increasingly difficult to please, fixating on trivial matters due to her idle mind. She often fretted over small details, such as the state of the household linens or the timing of Penelope’s meals, leading to minor disputes that provided her with a sense of purpose.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s life revolved around her household, her uncle, and her religious duties, magnifying the minutiae of her existence. Her health was a constant concern, leading her to obsess over minor ailments and dietary choices. Each season, she would visit her estate at Prebaudet to oversee her apple trees and prepare for the upcoming harvest, marking the occasion with a farewell dinner for her friends, which was a notable event in Alencon.

On the day of her departure, the town buzzed with gossip about her and her potential suitors, particularly Monsieur du Bousquier, whom she had previously rejected. The evening of her farewell dinner was filled with social interactions, where Mademoiselle Cormon attempted to engage her guests in conversation, often struggling to find the right words. Her efforts to be amiable were met with mixed reactions, as her lack of wit became a source of amusement among her more astute guests.

As the evening progressed, the conversation turned to local politics and the controversial figure of du Bousquier, who was rumored to be involved with a young woman named Suzanne. Mademoiselle Cormon, in an attempt to defend du Bousquier, made a remark that unexpectedly garnered laughter and admiration from her guests, showcasing her ability to contribute to the evening’s entertainment, albeit unintentionally.

The dinner concluded with light-hearted banter about du Bousquier’s character, revealing the town’s divided opinions on him. Mademoiselle Cormon’s innocence and naivety were evident as she navigated the complexities of social interactions and the underlying tensions regarding marriage and morality.

As the guests departed, discussions continued about Mademoiselle Cormon’s prospects and the implications of du Bousquier’s actions. The evening left her contemplating her role in the community and her own desires, setting the stage for future developments in her life and the lives of those around her. The dynamics of love, ambition, and societal expectations intertwined, creating a rich tapestry of relationships that would continue to evolve in the small town of Alencon.

The following day, Mademoiselle Cormon, packed into her old carriole with Josette, set off for Prebaudet, unaware that an unexpected event would hasten her marriage. The morning after her arrival, while having breakfast, she was interrupted by Jacquelin, who brought news of a letter from her uncle, the Abbe de Sponde. The letter announced that Monsieur de Troisville, a grandson of one of the abbe's friends, wished to stay in Alencon and needed hospitality. Alarmed by the urgency, Mademoiselle Cormon decided to return to town immediately to help her uncle prepare for the guest.

Despite the rain, she insisted on a quick departure, driven by her excitement and the prospect of marriage. As they arrived in Alencon, her return caused quite a stir, with townspeople speculating about the reasons behind her sudden arrival. Josette and Jacquelin were left to manage the household while Mademoiselle Cormon focused on preparing for Monsieur de Troisville's arrival.

The news of the viscount's impending visit spread quickly, and the townsfolk were abuzz with gossip. Mademoiselle Cormon, filled with hope, began to worry about the state of her home and her appearance. She felt a mix of excitement and anxiety, fearing that her old-fashioned decor might not impress the viscount. As preparations were made, she sacrificed some of her prized liqueurs to impress her guest.

When Monsieur de Troisville finally arrived, he was greeted by the abbe and Mademoiselle Cormon. He was a distinguished-looking man, and Mademoiselle Cormon felt an immediate attraction. Their dinner conversation flowed, with the viscount complimenting her home and expressing admiration for the region. Mademoiselle Cormon felt she was finally being seen as a desirable woman.

However, the evening took a turn when the Chevalier de Valois and other guests arrived, drawn by curiosity about the new visitor. During the introductions, the viscount casually mentioned that he was married, which devastated Mademoiselle Cormon. She fainted, and du Bousquier, a local suitor, caught her and carried her to her room. The townspeople, who had been eagerly watching the events unfold, were left to speculate about the implications of the evening.

As Mademoiselle Cormon recovered, she realized that her hopes for marriage had been dashed. The next day, the news of her fainting and the viscount's marital status spread throughout Alencon, leading to a mix of laughter and sympathy. Mademoiselle Cormon, feeling the weight of her situation, contemplated her options. Both du Bousquier and the Chevalier de Valois sought her attention, each hoping to win her favor.

Ultimately, Mademoiselle Cormon decided to accept du Bousquier's proposal, seeing it as a way to regain her standing in the community. She required him to agree to a lie about their engagement, which he readily accepted. As they prepared to announce their engagement, the Chevalier de Valois arrived, unaware of the developments. The evening ended with Mademoiselle Cormon feeling a mix of triumph and uncertainty, as the town buzzed with the news of her engagement, marking a new chapter in her life.

The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage devastated Athanase Granson, though he masked his inner turmoil. He first learned of it while at the home of Chief Justice du Ronceret, where his mother was playing cards. Athanase had long pinned his hopes on Mademoiselle Cormon, and the news felt like a catastrophe. He was a product of the Imperial education system, shaped by a sense of fatalism that left him feeling lost and indifferent. His mother noticed his pallor but attributed it to deep thought about his work.

In the wake of the marriage announcement, Athanase wandered the picturesque Sarthe region, a place often overlooked by locals. He found solace in its beauty, though his heart was heavy with despair. His mother, concerned for him, speculated that he was contemplating a significant project. Athanase withdrew from politics and social life, becoming a figure of little interest to the townspeople, who were unaware of his suffering.

Meanwhile, Suzanne, a young woman inspired by tales of romance and adventure, returned to Alencon with plans to help Athanase financially, though their paths did not cross. The town buzzed with gossip about Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage, with conflicting reports about its legitimacy. Eventually, the marriage contract was signed, and the wedding took place amid ominous signs, including a priest accidentally reading from the De Profundis.

The marriage of Mademoiselle Cormon and du Bousquier was marked by a lack of celebration, and the couple quickly retreated to Prebaudet. Du Bousquier, eager to modernize the Cormon estate, began renovations that alarmed the townsfolk, who feared the loss of their provincial charm. Athanase, meanwhile, was consumed by his grief and ultimately took his own life, leaving a letter for his mother that she misinterpreted as a sign of independence.

The news of Athanase’s suicide shocked Alencon, and his mother was left to mourn in silence, grappling with the depth of her loss. The Chevalier de Valois revealed Athanase’s unrequited love for Mademoiselle Cormon, which only deepened the tragedy in the eyes of the townspeople. Madame Granson’s grief was profound, as she alone understood the true nature of her son’s struggles.

In the aftermath, Madame du Bousquier returned to Alencon, where she encountered Madame Granson, whose sorrow was palpable. The two women’s paths crossed in a moment heavy with unspoken words. Madame Granson sought solace in the church, imploring the rector for a proper burial for her son, which was conducted discreetly to avoid scandal.

As time passed, Madame du Bousquier’s marriage to du Bousquier became a source of societal gossip. Initially, she appeared content, but the reality of her situation soon set in. Du Bousquier, while politically ambitious and outwardly successful, was a tyrant at home, leaving her feeling powerless and unfulfilled. The couple had no children, and Madame du Bousquier’s longing for motherhood became a source of pain.

The Chevalier de Valois, embittered by the marriage, sought revenge against du Bousquier, who had become a figure of ridicule in the community. Despite the initial laughter at Mademoiselle Cormon’s misfortune, sympathy for Madame du Bousquier grew as her struggles became apparent. The chevalier’s machinations only served to deepen the animosity between du Bousquier and the aristocracy.

Madame du Bousquier’s life became a series of contradictions as she navigated her duties as a wife while grappling with her feelings of resentment. She devoted herself to her husband, even as she felt trapped by his oppressive nature. The marriage, while socially advantageous, left her emotionally desolate.

The Chevalier de Valois’s death coincided with significant political changes in France, marking the end of an era. His demise, along with that of Suzanne’s mother, left a void in the lives of those who had known them. Suzanne, now seeking to honor her past, purchased a memento from the chevalier’s estate, a symbol of lost connections.

Ultimately, Madame du Bousquier’s life became a testament to the complexities of love, duty, and societal expectations. She remained a figure of pity and admiration, enduring the consequences of her choices while reflecting on the life she might have had. The narrative serves as a poignant exploration of the human condition, illustrating the interplay between personal desires and societal pressures.
