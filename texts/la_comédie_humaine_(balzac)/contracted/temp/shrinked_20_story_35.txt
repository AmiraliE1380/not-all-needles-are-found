DEDICATION
To Monsieur Eugene-Auguste-Georges-Louis Midy de la Greneraye Surville, Royal Engineer of the Ponts et Chausses. As a testimony to the affection of his brother-in-law, De Balzac

CHAPTER I. ONE OF MANY CHEVALIERS DE VALOIS

Across France there were many Chevaliers de Valois; one in Alençon is the subject here. Though sharing the name, these chevaliers rarely knew one another; they accepted the Bourbon monarchy, recognizing that Henri IV succeeded because the Valois left no male heir. The Alençon chevalier was an elderly, tall, thin gentleman whose Parisian youth and amorous pursuits were interrupted by the Revolution.

Respected among local aristocracy for manners, wit, and anecdotes of Louis XV’s reign, he was a regular at gatherings. At fifty-eight he insisted he was fifty, preserving a sprightlier air than his years suggested. He had a large nose and a pale complexion, reddened on one side—an odd “hot liver” explanation he used to excuse a hearty appetite.

Seemingly frail but vigorous and passionate, his face showed the marks of a full life. He groomed himself carefully and upheld aristocratic standards: his clothes, though worn, were always clean. Living modestly in a rented room above Madame Lardot’s laundry, he dined out daily and depended on a small annuity. Beloved for card-playing and amiability, he conversed easily across ranks; his discretion and charm made him desirable company. Yet curiosity surrounded his private life, including rumors of a secret marriage to a woman named Césarine.

Despite precarious finances, he maintained dignity and the reputation of a gentleman, navigating provincial life with grace and tact.

CHAPTER II. SUSANNAH AND THE ELDERS

One spring morning the chevalier was visited by Suzanne, a young worker from Madame Lardot’s laundry. She brought news that could bring pleasure and pain, feigning indifference to his rumored link with Césarine. Ambitious and shrewd, Suzanne wanted to go to Paris to better herself.

The chevalier, touched by her charm and drive, encouraged her in a kindly, paternal way and advised she court Monsieur du Bousquier, a wealthy bachelor who had shown interest. He recognized how beauty and wit could secure a favorable match and counseled her on social strategy.

The narrative then turns to du Bousquier: once wealthy and influential, now back in Alençon, he hoped to marry Mademoiselle Cormon, a rich old maid, to restore his fortunes. His past as a purveyor during the Revolution had tarnished his standing; he represents brusque opportunism rather than aristocratic grace.

Du Bousquier and the chevalier contrast sharply—the chevalier as the old regime’s refined elegance, du Bousquier as the new order’s opportunist. Their subtle rivalry over Mademoiselle Cormon and their interactions with Suzanne intertwine, revealing the provincial dance of desire, ambition, and social expectation, and illuminating broader themes of class, gender, and the pursuit of happiness in a changing France.

Madame Granson, a widow living on a modest pension and on property she owned, struggled to support her son Athanase, who added a small salary from the mayor’s office. Their provincial home was simple and marked by scarcity; that poverty shaped both their daily life and their morals. Athanase, a pale, introspective twenty-three-year-old, carried a deep passion for his cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he believed to be wealthy. His feelings were complicated by social expectations and the risk of ridicule for loving a woman older than himself.

What began as a practical calculation—to secure a better life for himself and his mother—quietly became genuine affection. Athanase admired Mademoiselle Cormon from afar, at once drawn to her and constrained by his circumstances. His talent and potential went largely unrecognized in their provincial setting, where private ambition too often collided with harsh realities and limited opportunity.

Madame Granson, conscious of her son’s attachment, recognized the possible advantages of a match with Cormon despite Athanase’s lack of fortune and the cousin’s age. She remained hopeful and patient, waiting for a moment to advance his prospects. All the while Athanase’s thoughts were consumed by fantasies of a future that seemed blocked by class, reputation, and the small-town gaze.

The arrival of Suzanne, a young woman from a more precarious background, introduced a new dynamic. She felt an immediate sympathy for Athanase, sensing his struggles and aspirations, yet her own ambitions drove her toward deception as a vehicle for escape. After a brief encounter with Madame Granson, during which she secured money and new clothes, Suzanne left town; the community lamented her departure and the scandal it implied.

As Athanase prepared for a decisive dinner with Mademoiselle Cormon, his mother urged him to present himself well, hoping to improve his odds. Unaware of the full scope of his mother’s plans, he navigated love and ambition under the weight of provincial constraint. Suzanne’s flight to Paris became a turning point—her attempt to redefine herself away from limits shared by many women of the time—while Athanase remained in Alençon, grappling with unfulfilled dreams and the heavy hold of his love as their fates continued to intertwine.

In many second-class prefectures of France there exists a salon that gathers influential—but not elite—members of the community. Its hosts are prominent locally and invited to events, yet they do not mix with the highest social echelons, who keep their distance and communicate by formal invitation.

This salon, frequented by lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates, wields considerable local influence. Its members are indifferent to luxury and fashion, focused on practical concerns such as land acquisition. They resist outside influences, especially from Paris, and their gossip often functions as judgment. Young women are seldom present; when they do appear it is usually to seek family and public validation.

In Alençon the upper classes assembled at the house of Mademoiselle Rose-Marie-Victoire Cormon, an unmarried woman whose fortune was coveted by relatives and two old bachelors. She lived with her uncle, a former grand-vicar, and her family had long served the nobility. Through advantageous marriages they acquired a semblance of aristocracy despite a fundamentally middle-class status.

Her house, dating from the time of Henri IV, provoked envy by its prime location and careful upkeep. The mansion’s courtyard with a neat flowerbed testified to her meticulousness and insistence on cleanliness. Robust architecture and decorative elements recalled its historical standing.

Inside, the house was arranged for reception: a salon hung with family portraits and a dining room that exuded provincial charm. The salon was a hub of social life—card games and conversations about local affairs took place there. Mademoiselle Cormon’s circle numbered about 150 people, mostly mature, who rarely traveled and were firmly rooted in provincial life.

Wednesdays were special: she hosted dinners that drew a larger assemblage. The women dressed modestly and the atmosphere was one of familiarity and routine. Conversations centered on local gossip, land disputes, and judgments about aristocratic behavior, reflecting the community’s concerns and values.

Despite her wealth and standing, Mademoiselle Cormon remained unmarried largely because she wished to marry a nobleman and because of the era’s constraints. The political upheavals from 1788 to 1815 made suitable matches scarce—many eligible men were too old or too young or lacked the qualities she sought. Fear of being married merely for her fortune led her to set subtle tests for suitors, designed to expose mercenary intentions without revealing her motive.

As she aged, her longing for love intensified, yet she remained oblivious to some genuine attachments, including that of Athanase Granson, a suitor whose affection she failed to recognize. Her innocence and naivety produced missed opportunities; over time her character hardened and the weight of social expectations grew heavier.

Although pious and virtuous, she became a figure of local ridicule; her unmarried state turned into a proverb. She lived modestly, shunning ostentation, and her considerable fortune did not exempt her from scrutiny—attempts at marriage were viewed skeptically. Occasional returns of disbanded soldiers and officers offered glimmers of hope, but none met her standards. Her yearning for companionship and motherhood gradually became a source of despair.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s story illuminates the complexities of love, social expectation, and the particular challenges faced by women in her position. Her life—marked by a deep desire for connection and a struggle against restrictive norms—serves as a poignant commentary on happiness and fulfillment in a world governed by rigid class conventions.

To complete the picture of Mademoiselle Cormon’s household, we must consider her and the Abbé de Sponde alongside their loyal servants: Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette, who all contributed to the comfort of the uncle and niece.

Jacquelin, a short, stout man with a ruddy complexion, had served the household for twenty-two years, doing everything from waiting at table to grooming the mare Pénélope. He was in love with Josette, a thirty-year-old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if she married him. The couple quietly saved their wages, hoping Mademoiselle’s marriage would free them to wed. Josette, plump and practical, was said to rule her mistress. Mariette, the cook, had been with the family for fifteen years and was skilled in preparing local delicacies.

Pénélope, the old brown-bay mare, attracted affection and conversation, since Mademoiselle Cormon, lacking children, directed maternal instincts toward the animal. The servants and the mare kept a steady routine that reflected long-standing roles. Mademoiselle, however, grew increasingly difficult to please; her idle mind fixated on trivial matters. She fretted over household linens and the timing of Pénélope’s meals, turning small disputes into a sense of purpose.

Her life revolved around the household, her uncle, and her religious duties, which magnified the minutiae of existence. Worried about her health, she obsessed over minor ailments and dietary choices. Each season she visited her estate at Prébaudet to oversee apple trees and prepare for harvest, marking the occasion with a farewell dinner that was a notable event in Alençon.

On the day of her departure the town buzzed with gossip about her and potential suitors, especially M. du Bousquier, whom she had rejected. The farewell dinner was full of social maneuvering; Mademoiselle Cormon tried to engage guests but often struggled to find the right words, her lack of wit amusing the sharper attendees.

As the evening progressed conversation turned to local politics and the controversial du Bousquier, rumored to be involved with a young woman named Suzanne. In defending him, Mademoiselle made a remark that unexpectedly drew laughter and admiration, showing she could contribute—if unintentionally—to the evening’s entertainment.

The dinner closed with light banter about du Bousquier’s character, revealing a town divided. Mademoiselle’s innocence and naivety were plain as she navigated social complexities; when the guests departed, talk continued of her prospects and the implications of du Bousquier’s behavior. The evening left her contemplating her role in the community and her own desires, setting the stage for future developments where love, ambition, and societal expectations would continue to shape relationships in Alençon.

The day after arriving at Prebaudet in her old carriole with Josette, Mademoiselle Cormon’s plans shifted when Jacquelin brought a letter from her uncle, the Abbé de Sponde. It explained that Monsieur de Troisville, a grandson of one of the abbé’s friends, wished to stay in Alençon and required hospitality. Alarmed by the request’s urgency, she returned to town immediately despite the rain, driven by excitement and the hope of a favorable match.

Their arrival in Alençon caused a stir. Josette and Jacquelin were left to manage the household while Mademoiselle Cormon focused on preparing for the viscount’s visit. News of his impending arrival spread, and gossip swirled. Worried that her old-fashioned house and appearance might disappoint, she even sacrificed prized liqueurs to make a better impression.

When Monsieur de Troisville arrived he was received by the abbé and Mademoiselle Cormon. He looked distinguished, and she felt an immediate attraction. Over dinner he complimented her home and the region, making her feel noticed and desirable. The atmosphere changed, however, when the Chevalier de Valois and other curious guests arrived. During introductions the viscount casually revealed he was married. The disclosure devastated Mademoiselle Cormon; she fainted and was carried to her room by Du Bousquier while the townspeople watched and speculated.

Recovering, she realized her hopes of marrying the viscount were crushed. The next day news of her fainting and his marital status swept through Alençon, provoking a blend of laughter and sympathy. Pressured by reputation and desirous of restoring her standing, she weighed her options as both Du Bousquier and the Chevalier sought her favor.

In the end she accepted Du Bousquier’s proposal as a means to regain social standing, stipulating that he agree to a public lie about their engagement—a condition he readily accepted. As they prepared to announce it, the Chevalier de Valois arrived unaware of the arrangement. The evening closed with Mademoiselle Cormon feeling a mixture of triumph and uncertainty as the town buzzed over her engagement, marking a new chapter in her life.

The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage devastated Athanase Granson, though he concealed his turmoil. He first heard it at Chief Justice du Ronceret’s house, where his mother played cards. Athanase had long pinned his hopes on Mademoiselle Cormon; the announcement felt catastrophic. Shaped by an Imperial education that bred fatalism, he drifted into indifference and despair. His mother noticed his pallor but assumed he was merely preoccupied with work.

After the marriage was announced, Athanase wandered the Sarthe region, finding bitter solace in its overlooked beauty. He withdrew from politics and society, becoming a figure of little interest to townspeople who remained unaware of the depth of his suffering. His mother worried and speculated that he might be contemplating a major project, never grasping the true cause of his withdrawal.

Suzanne, a young woman inspired by romantic tales and adventure, returned to Alençon intending to help Athanase financially, though their paths did not meet. The town buzzed with gossip about Mademoiselle Cormon’s match, with conflicting reports about its legitimacy. Eventually the marriage contract was signed and the wedding took place amid ominous signs — a priest accidentally read from the De Profundis — setting an uneasy tone.

The marriage of Mademoiselle Cormon and du Bousquier lacked celebration; the couple soon retreated to Prebaudet. Du Bousquier, intent on modernizing the Cormon estate, began renovations that alarmed locals who feared losing their provincial charm. Athanase, consumed by grief, ultimately took his own life, leaving a letter his mother misread as evidence of newfound independence rather than the despair it expressed.

Athanase’s suicide shocked Alençon. Madame Granson was left to mourn privately, grappling with the full weight of her loss that others barely comprehended. The Chevalier de Valois revealed Athanase’s unrequited love for Mademoiselle Cormon, deepening the town’s sense of tragedy. Madame Granson’s grief was profound; she alone understood the true nature of her son’s struggles and bore them in silence.

Madame du Bousquier returned to Alençon and encountered Madame Granson in a meeting heavy with unspoken words. Madame Granson sought solace in the church and implored the rector for a proper burial for her son; the ceremony was conducted discreetly to avoid scandal, reflecting the community’s desire to contain the episode.

As time passed, Madame du Bousquier’s marriage became gossip and cautionary tale. At first she appeared content, but the reality was different. Du Bousquier, outwardly successful and politically ambitious, proved a tyrant at home. The marriage brought social advantage but left her feeling powerless and unfulfilled; they had no children, and her longing for motherhood deepened her pain.

The Chevalier de Valois, embittered by the match, sought revenge against du Bousquier, whose conduct made him a figure of ridicule. Public mockery at Mademoiselle Cormon’s misfortune gave way to growing sympathy for Madame du Bousquier as the true hardships of her life became known. The chevalier’s schemes only widened the rift between du Bousquier and the local aristocracy, intensifying animosity rather than restoring honor.

Madame du Bousquier lived amid contradictions: she performed the duties of a devoted wife while inwardly resenting her husband’s oppression. The marriage, beneficial in status, left her emotionally desolate; she clung to duty even as she mourned the life she might have had.

The Chevalier de Valois’s death coincided with sweeping political changes in France, marking an end of an era and creating a void for those who had known him. Suzanne, seeking to honor the past, purchased a small memento from the chevalier’s estate, a symbol of lost connections and vanished possibilities.

Ultimately, Madame du Bousquier’s life illustrates the complexities of love, duty, and social expectation. She remained both pitied and admired — enduring the consequences of her choices and reflecting on what might have been — while the narrative exposes how private suffering and public reputation intertwine.
