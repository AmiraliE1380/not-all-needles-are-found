Across France there were many Chevaliers de Valois; this tale centers on the one in Alençon. Though sharing the name, these chevaliers mostly did not know one another and had accepted the Bourbon monarchy after the Valois line produced no male heir. The Alençon chevalier was an elderly, tall, thin gentleman whose youth in Paris and romantic pursuits had been interrupted by the Revolution.

Well regarded among the local aristocracy, he was noted for his manners, wit, and anecdotes from Louis XV’s reign. A regular at social gatherings, his charm and discretion made him a favorite. At fifty-eight he claimed to be fifty, keeping a youthful air despite age. He had a prominent nose and a pale complexion that reddened on one side—his excuse for the flush, and for a voracious appetite, was a “hot liver.”

Though seemingly frail, he remained vigorous and passionate; his face bore the marks of a lived life. He groomed himself carefully and kept his clothes clean though worn, maintaining aristocratic standards. Living modestly in a rented room above Madame Lardot’s laundry, he dined out daily on a small annuity. Beloved in Alençon for card-playing and amiability, he was a master of conversation who mingled with all ranks and whose presence was sought. Yet curiosity attended his private life, including persistent rumors of a secret marriage to a woman named Césarine. Financially precarious, he managed provincial life with dignity.

One spring morning Suzanne, a young girl from Madame Lardot’s laundry, interrupted him with news that might bring pleasure or pain; she feigned indifference to his rumored link with Césarine. Ambitious and clever, Suzanne aspired to leave Alençon for Paris, convinced she could better herself. The chevalier, struck by her charm and drive, encouraged her in a paternal tone and advised courting Monsieur du Bousquier, a wealthy bachelor who had shown interest.

Du Bousquier, by contrast, bore a dubious reputation: once wealthy and influential, his role as a purveyor during the Revolution had tarnished him. Returned to Alençon, he hoped to marry Mademoiselle Cormon, a rich old maid, to restore his fortunes. His brusque opportunism contrasted with the chevalier’s elegance; their rivalry over Mademoiselle Cormon, and Suzanne’s ambitions, set in motion an intricate dance of desire, ambition, and societal expectation in a changing world.

Madame Granson, a widow on a modest pension and a small property, struggled to support her son Athanase, who added only a meager salary from the mayor’s office. Their simple, provincial home shaped their lives and morals. Athanase, a pale, introspective twenty-three-year-old, secretly loved his cousin Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he believed to be wealthy. His passion was tangled with social expectations and the possible ridicule of loving an older woman.

What began as a practical hope to secure a better life for himself and his mother grew into genuine affection. He admired Mademoiselle Cormon from afar, constrained by circumstances that smothered his talent and ambition. Madame Granson, aware of his feelings, saw the match’s advantages despite Athanase’s lack of fortune and the cousin’s age; she waited patiently for the right moment while Athanase dreamed of a future together despite daunting barriers.

The arrival of Suzanne, an even poorer young woman, changed the dynamics. She felt an immediate bond with Athanase and grasped his struggles, yet her ambition pushed her toward deception as a way out of poverty. After a brief meeting with Madame Granson, Suzanne left town with money and new clothes; the community lamented her departure.

As Athanase prepared for a dinner with Mademoiselle Cormon—encouraged by his mother—their fates began to diverge: Suzanne headed for Paris to remake her life, while Athanase remained in Alençon, weighed down by unfulfilled dreams and provincial pressures.

In many second‑class French prefectures a salon gathered influential—but not elite—locals. Hosted by prominent residents, it included lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates who kept a formal distance from the highest echelons. The salon wielded local influence: members prized practicality over luxury, cared about land and provincial concerns, resisted Parisian fashions, and used gossip as communal judgment. Young women rarely appeared except to seek validation.

In Alençon the upper classes met at the house of Mademoiselle Rose‑Marie‑Victoire Cormon, an unmarried heiress coveted by relatives and two aged bachelors. Living with her uncle, a former grand‑vicar, she descended from a family long in service to the nobility and, through advantageous marriages, had acquired an air of aristocracy despite middle‑class origins. Her house, dating from Henri IV, provoked envy by its prime location and careful upkeep: a neat courtyard, flowerbed, solid architecture, and period decor signaled meticulousness and status. Inside, rooms were arranged for reception: a portrait‑hung salon for card games and local talk, and a dining room exuding provincial charm. Her circle numbered about 150 people, mostly mature, rooted in provincial life and rarely traveling.

Wednesdays were notable: dinners drew larger assemblies where modestly dressed women spoke familiarly about gossip, land disputes, and judgments of aristocratic behavior—topics reflecting the community’s concerns and values.

Despite wealth and standing, Mademoiselle Cormon remained unmarried. She had hoped to wed a nobleman, but the upheavals of 1788–1815 left few suitable matches; many were too old, too young, or otherwise inappropriate. Fearing that suitors sought her fortune, she set subtle tests. As she aged her longing for love intensified—especially for Athanase Granson—exposing her innocence and naivety. Pious and virtuous, she stiffened under social expectation and became a local byword; occasional returning soldiers offered hope, but none met her standards. Her yearning for companionship and motherhood turned to despair, revealing the limits placed on women by rigid class conventions.

Completing the household picture: Mademoiselle, the Abbé de Sponde, and loyal servants Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette, who ensured the uncle and niece’s comfort. Jacquelin, short and stout with a ruddy face, had served twenty‑two years, doing everything from waiting at table to grooming the family mare Pénélope. He loved Josette, a thirty‑year‑old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if Josette married him; the couple quietly saved, hoping Mademoiselle’s eventual marriage would free them to wed. Josette, plump and practical, seemed to rule her mistress. Mariette, cook for fifteen years, prepared local delicacies. Pénélope, the old brown‑bay mare, received Mademoiselle’s maternal affections: childless, she directed her care toward the animal. The servants and mare kept a steady routine while Mademoiselle grew harder to please, fixing on trifles—linens, Pénélope’s meals—and magnifying minutiae through religious duty and worry about her health.

Each season she visited her estate at Prébaudet to inspect the apple trees and prepare for harvest, marking the occasion with a farewell dinner that was an Alençon event. Town gossip swirled about suitors, notably M. du Bousquier, whom she had rejected. At the dinner she tried to engage but often lacked wit, amusing sharper guests. When talk turned to du Bousquier’s rumored involvement with Suzanne, her clumsy defense produced a remark that unexpectedly drew laughter and admiration, leaving her to ponder her place amid love, ambition, and social expectation in Alençon.

The next day Mademoiselle Cormon, packed into her old carriole with Josette, set off for Prebaudet, unaware that an event there would hasten her marriage. The morning after their arrival, while they breakfasted, Jacquelin brought a letter from her uncle, the Abbé de Sponde: Monsieur de Troisville, a grandson of one of the abbé’s friends, wished to stay in Alençon and required hospitality. Alarmed by the urgency, and driven by excitement and the hope of marriage, Mademoiselle Cormon returned to town at once despite the rain.

Their sudden arrival provoked town gossip; Josette and Jacquelin were left to manage the household while Mademoiselle Cormon prepared for the viscount. She fretted over her old-fashioned home and appearance, even sacrificing prized liqueurs to make a better impression.

When Monsieur de Troisville arrived he was greeted by the abbé and Mademoiselle Cormon. Distinguished and courteous, he immediately attracted her attention. Their dinner conversation flowed: the viscount praised her house and the region, speaking in ways that made her feel noticed and desirable.

The evening shifted when the Chevalier de Valois and other guests arrived. During introductions the viscount casually mentioned he was married, which devastated Mademoiselle Cormon; she fainted. Du Bousquier caught her and carried her to her room amid townspeople’s watching and speculation. As she recovered she realized her hopes for marriage were dashed and the news of her fainting and the viscount’s marital status swept Alençon.

To restore her standing—and with both Du Bousquier and the Chevalier now seeking her favour—she accepted Du Bousquier’s proposal on the condition he consent to a public falsehood about their engagement, which he agreed to. The Chevalier remained unaware; the evening closed with Mademoiselle Cormon feeling a mingled triumph and uncertainty as the town buzzed about her supposed engagement and a new chapter began.

The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage shattered Athanase Granson, though he hid his turmoil. He first heard it at Chief Justice du Ronceret’s house while his mother was playing cards. Having long pinned hopes on Mademoiselle Cormon and schooled in an Imperial fatalism, he fell into indifference and despair; his mother mistook his pallor for mere preoccupation with work.

After the announcement Athanase wandered the Sarthe countryside, finding a bitter consolation in its overlooked beauty. He withdrew from politics and society, becoming of little interest to townspeople who could not grasp the depth of his suffering. His mother fretted and guessed he might be planning some grand enterprise, unaware of the true cause of his withdrawal.

Suzanne, stirred by romantic tales of adventure, returned to Alençon determined to help Athanase financially, though they never met. The town buzzed with gossip about Mademoiselle Cormon’s match, with conflicting reports about its motives and legitimacy. Eventually the contract was signed and the wedding took place amid ominous signs—a priest accidentally read from the De Profundis—setting an uneasy tone.

The union of Mademoiselle Cormon and du Bousquier lacked celebration; the couple soon retreated to Prébaudet. Du Bousquier began modernizing the Cormon estate, alarming locals who feared losing their provincial charm. Consumed by grief, Athanase ultimately took his own life, leaving a letter his mother misread as evidence of newfound independence rather than the despair it revealed.

Athanase’s suicide shocked Alençon. Madame Granson was left to mourn privately, carrying a grief others barely comprehended. The Chevalier de Valois disclosed Athanase’s unrequited love for Mademoiselle Cormon, deepening the town’s sense of tragedy. Madame Granson bore the true weight of her son’s struggles in silence.

Madame du Bousquier returned to Alençon and met Madame Granson in a tense, wordless encounter. Madame Granson arranged a discreet burial to avoid scandal; the ceremony was quietly conducted, reflecting the community’s desire to contain the episode.

Over time Madame du Bousquier’s marriage became gossip and a cautionary tale. Though she at first appeared content, she was powerless and unfulfilled. Du Bousquier, outwardly successful and politically ambitious, proved a domestic tyrant. The marriage brought social advantage but no children, deepening her longing for motherhood.

The Chevalier de Valois, embittered by the match, plotted against du Bousquier; his schemes widened the rift with local aristocracy, intensifying animosity rather than restoring honor. Public mockery of Mademoiselle Cormon shifted toward sympathy for Madame du Bousquier as the hardships of her life emerged. She fulfilled the duties of a devoted wife while inwardly resenting her husband and mourning the life she might have had. The Chevalier’s death and sweeping political changes marked the end of an era. Suzanne, honoring the past, bought a small memento from the Chevalier’s estate—a token of lost connections and vanished possibilities—leaving Madame du Bousquier to endure the consequences of choices made within constraining social limits.
