The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once one of the darkest, most tortuous lanes near the Hôtel de Ville, wound around the little gardens of the Paris Prefecture and ended at the Rue Martroi by a long-razed wall. A turnstile that gave the street its name remained until 1823, when the Municipality built a ballroom on the adjoining garden for the Duc d’Angoulême’s fête.

At its widest, where it opened into the Rue de la Tixeranderie, the Rue du Tourniquet measured under six feet. In rain the gutter ran deep, sweeping dust and refuse into corners carts could not reach; inhabitants trusted storms to wash their miry alley. In summer the sun touched this blackness briefly but never dried the permanent damp that rose to the first story. Residents who lit lamps at five in June kept them burning through winter. A traveller approaching the Marais along the quays, past the Rue du Chaume and the Rues de l’Homme-Armé, des Billettes, and des Deux-Portes, might think he had passed through cellars all the way.

Many streets of old Paris were like this damp labyrinth, where antiquaries find curiosities. On the corner where the Rue du Tourniquet met the Rue de la Tixeranderie, two iron ring clamps remained, relics of chains the watch once strung nightly. The corner house testified to the unhealthiness of such dwellings: cellar arches rose about two feet above the ground, the entrance required three steps, and the door was crowned by a closed arch whose keystone bore a female head and weathered arabesques. Three windows, sills about five feet from the ground, lit a small suite of rooms opening on the Rue du Tourniquet, protected by strong iron bars curved outward.

By day a passer-by could see little; only in July, when light strengthened, could one discern in the second room two beds hung with green serge side by side in an old-fashioned alcove. But by about three, when candles were lighted, the nearer pane showed an old woman on a stool tending a brazier to simmer a porter’s-wife stew; a few utensils hung against the wall. A trestle table, bare of linen, held pewter spoons and the old woman’s dish; three wretched chairs furnished the multipurpose kitchen-dining room. Above the chimney hung a small mirror, a tinder-box, three glasses, matches, and a large cracked white jug. Despite the gloom, the floor, utensils, and hearth testified to cleanliness and thrift.

The old woman’s pale, withered face matched the street’s darkness and mustiness. Motionless, inseparable from the house as a snail from its shell, her face, alert with a vague mischief, was framed in a net cap that barely covered white hair; her fine gray eyes were quiet as the street, and wrinkles ran like cracks in the walls. Whether born to poverty or fallen from better days, she seemed resigned to melancholy.

From sunrise till dark, except when cooking or buying provisions, the old woman sat by the farther window opposite a young girl. A passer-by could see the needlewoman in an old red velvet chair bending over an embroidery frame, stitching indefatigably. The mother worked net on a green pillow with feeble sight, spectacles resting on her nose. By night they set a lamp between them, its light concentrated by two globe-shaped water bottles, letting the elder see the network on her pillow and the younger the pattern’s delicate details.

The window’s outward bend held a box of earth on the sill where sweet peas, nasturtiums, a sickly honeysuckle, and convolvulus twined up the iron bars. These etiolated plants bore a few pale flowers, adding a sadness and sweetness to the framed picture of the two figures.

Even the most selfish observer would take away a vivid image of working-class Parisian women’s lives: the embroideress evidently lived by her needle. Many passing through the turnstile wondered how a girl could keep her color living in a cellar. A student crossing to the Quartier Latin might liken this vegetative existence to ivy clinging to chill walls, or to peasants who toil and die unknown. A house-owner, valuing the property, would ask, “What will become of those two women if embroidery goes out of fashion?”

Among the men obliged to pass this street for the Hôtel de Ville or Palais de Justice there might have been charitable souls. Some widower or Adonis of forty, often glimpsing these sad lives, might have reckoned on the mother and daughter’s poverty and hoped to make the innocent work-woman his at little cost—her nimble fingers, youthful figure, and pale skin (a charm due to the sunless street) drawing admiration. Perhaps some honest clerk with twelve hundred francs a year, seeing diligence and purity, waited only for better prospects to unite his life to hers, to bring a man’s arm and calm affection, pale like the window flowers, to uphold the home.

Vague hope kept life in the mother’s dim gray eyes. Each morning after a frugal breakfast she took up her pillow—chiefly for show—laid her spectacles on a little mahogany worktable as old as herself, and watched from about half-past eight till ten the regular passers, noting gait, dress, and countenance as if trying to attract attention to her daughter. This small review was as good as a play to her, perhaps her sole amusement.

The daughter rarely looked up. Modesty, or the painful consciousness of poverty, kept her eyes fixed on the frame; only some exclamation from her mother showed her small features. Then a clerk in a new coat or one appearing with a woman might catch sight of the girl’s slightly upturned nose, rosy mouth, and gray eyes, bright despite toil. Late hours left pale circles beneath each eye on her fresh cheeks.

The child seemed made for love and cheerfulness: love had drawn two perfect arches above her eyelids and given her a mass of chestnut hair she might hide under like a tent; cheerfulness widened her nostrils into quivering animation, carved dimples in her cheeks, and made her quick to forget troubles—this blossom of hope gave strength to face life’s barren path.

Her hair was always neatly dressed. As Paris needlewomen, she thought her toilet complete after brushing smooth and tucking the short curls at each temple against her white skin. The pretty growth at the nape and a clear brown line suggested youth and charm; an observer seeing her bent over work might deem her a coquette. This promise caught more than one young man’s eye, who turned in vain hope to glimpse that modest countenance.

“Caroline, there is a new face that passes regularly by, and not one of the old ones to compare with it.”

These words, spoken in a low voice by her mother one August morning in 1815, vanquished the young needlewoman’s indifference, and she looked out; the stranger was gone.

“Where has he flown to?” she asked.

“He will come back no doubt at four; I shall see him coming, and will touch your foot with mine. I am sure he will come back; he has been through the street regularly for the last three days; but his hours vary. The first day he came by at six o’clock, the day before yesterday it was four, yesterday as early as three. I remember seeing him occasionally some time ago. He is some clerk in the Prefet’s office who has moved to the Marais.—Why!” she exclaimed after glancing down the street, “our gentleman of the brown coat has taken to wearing a wig; how much it alters him!”

The gentleman of the brown coat seemed to close the daily procession; the old woman put on her spectacles and took up her work with a sigh, glancing at her daughter with a look that mingled admiration, gratitude, hope, and pride in such a pretty child.

At about four the old lady pushed her foot against Caroline’s, and the girl looked up quickly enough to see a new actor, whose regular advent would thenceforth lend variety to the scene. He was tall and thin, wore black, a man of about forty, with a certain solemnity; as his piercing hazel eye met the old woman’s dull gaze, he made her quake, for she felt as though he had the gift of reading hearts, or had much practice in it, and his presence seemed as icy as the air of this dank street. Was the dull, sallow complexion of that ominous face due to excess of work, or to delicate health?

The old woman supplied many answers; but next day Caroline discerned the lines of long mental suffering on a brow quick to frown. His hollow cheeks bore sorrow’s seal. Though the girl’s expression at first was one of lively but innocent curiosity, it took on gentle sympathy as the stranger receded like a last relation following a funeral.

The weather was so hot and the gentleman so absent-minded that he had taken off and forgotten his hat as he passed the squalid street. Caroline noted how his brushed hair altered his countenance. The strong, uncharming impression he made was unlike any other passenger; for the first time she felt pity for someone besides herself and her mother. She said nothing to her mother’s conjectures, drew her needle in silence, and only regretted not seeing him more closely, looking forward to the morrow to form a definite opinion.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For two months the “Black Gentleman”—as they called him—moved about the Rue du Tourniquet with an irregular habit; he did not always pass at the same hour. The old woman sometimes saw him in the evening when he had not come in the morning; he lacked the punctuality of the clerks who served Madame Crochard. Except on the first occasion, when his glance alarmed the mother and lingered on the odd picture of two female gnomes in the window, he seemed lost in thought, looking at the ground or straight ahead as if reading the future in the fog of the street. With only two carriage-gates and a dark ironmonger’s beside them, curiosity could not excuse his abstraction; Madame Crochard was piqued to see her “Black Gentleman” always absorbed. One mid-September morning, however, Caroline Crochard’s lively face, fresh amid belated flowers and faded leaves, gleamed against the room’s dark background; the daily scene brightened with the pretty needlewoman at work. The stranger, perhaps roused by the clatter her irritated mother made with bobbins, glanced up and studied this living picture.

Their eyes met briefly; both knew they would think of each other. When he passed again at four, Caroline heard his step and they looked steadily at one another with evident purpose; his kind expression made him smile, and Caroline blushed, which Madame Crochard noted with satisfaction. After that afternoon the Gentleman in Black passed twice daily with rare exceptions, observed by mother and daughter. From his irregular hours they judged he was not a precise subordinate.

Through the first three winter months they saw each other twice a day for the few moments it took him to cross the stretch by their door and windows. Day by day the brief meetings took on a friendly, then fraternal, tone. They seemed to understand each other at once, learning each other’s faces. It became a duty of the Unknown to visit Caroline; if he failed to give the half-smile or cordial glance she expected, her day was marred. The charm of these meetings was mutual; neither could hide vexation or fatigue from the other.

“He must have had trouble yesterday,” Caroline would think when his features altered. “Oh, he has been working too hard!” she decided from another shade. He could tell when she had spent Sunday finishing a dress, when the quarter-day approached and her face darkened, when she had sat up late, and he marked how the gloom that dimmed her delicate features slowly lifted as their acquaintance warmed. When winter killed the window plants and the window was closed, he noticed the concentrated light at Caroline’s head, the small fire and the frosty redness of the two women’s faces showing the home’s poverty; if his face ever showed pity, the girl met it with assumed cheerfulness.

Yet their feelings remained unspoken; no incident revealed how deep they were in each other. They had never heard each other speak, and both kept at a distance as if closer acquaintance might bring disaster. Was it shyness or friendship that restrained them? Fear of encountering selfishness, or the city’s mutual distrust? Each perhaps hoped to preserve an illusion: he seeming to fear a vulgar word from lips as fresh as a flower, she feeling unworthy of a mysterious person who evidently had power and wealth.

Madame Crochard, reproachful at her daughter’s indecision, now showed sulkiness to the “Black Gentleman,” on whom until then she had smiled with servility. She complained of being forced to cook at her age, of catarrh and rheumatism, and could not promise the same number of ells of net as before.

By late December, when bread was dear and the 1816 corn shortage began to bite, the stranger saw secret sorrow in Caroline’s face which his kind smiles could not dispel. He noticed the dimness in her eyes from long hours at night. One night near month’s end he passed the Rue du Tourniquet at one in the morning. In the silence he heard Madame Crochard’s lachrymose voice and Caroline’s sadder tone mingling with sleet. He crept along slowly and, risking trouble with the police, stopped beneath the window to listen through the largest hole in the yellow muslin curtain. He saw a sheet of paper on the table by the lamp and water-filled globes—a writ. Madame Crochard wept; Caroline’s voice was thick.

“Why be so heartbroken, mother? Monsieur Molineux will not sell us up or turn us out before I have finished this dress; only two nights more and I shall take it home to Madame Roguin.”

“And supposing she keeps you waiting as usual? And will the money for the gown pay the baker too?”

The stranger, versed in reading faces, fancied the mother’s grief false while the daughter’s was genuine; he turned away, came back, and peeped again. Madame Crochard lay in bed; Caroline bent over her frame, embroidering with indefatigable diligence. On the table with the writ lay a triangular hunch of bread, placed to sustain her through the night and remind her of the reward of industry. Tremulous with pity, the stranger slipped his purse through a cracked pane so it would fall at the girl’s feet, then fled with tingling cheeks.

Next morning the shy, melancholy stranger passed with a preoccupied look, but could not avoid Caroline’s gratitude: she had opened her window and pretended to dig in the snow-buried window-box so he would not see her only through the glass. Her eyes brimmed with tears as she bowed, saying with a look, “I can only repay you from my heart.”

The Gentleman in Black affected not to understand this gratitude. That evening, when he passed, Caroline mended the window with a sheet of paper and smiled, showing her pearly teeth like a promise. Thenceforth the Stranger went another way and was no more seen in the Rue du Tourniquet.

One early May day, as Caroline watered a honeysuckle root, she caught sight of a narrow strip of cloudless blue and said gaily to her mother, “Mamma, we must go tomorrow for a trip to Montmorency!” She had hardly spoken when the Gentleman in Black passed, sadder than ever. Caroline’s innocent look might have seemed an invitation. Indeed, the next day, at the corner of the Rue du Faubourg Saint-Denis and the Rue d’Enghien, Madame Crochard, dressed in a claret merino pelisse, silk bonnet, and striped shawl, found her Unknown standing like a man waiting for his wife. A smile lit his face at sight of Caroline in plum-colored gaiters and a white dress tossed by a breeze that showed her graceful form. Her rice-straw bonnet lined with pink silk shaded a forehead white as snow; her hair parted in two brown bands gave an expression of innocence. She seemed lit from heaven; when she saw him, radiant hope brightened her. The Stranger, in doubt perhaps whether to be her escort, had his decision made by her delight. He hired a vehicle for Saint-Leu-Taverny and offered seats beside him. The mother accepted, but soon complained of the heat and her cough, making civil speeches about being an inconvenience. By Saint-Denis she seemed fast asleep; her snores seemed dubious to the Stranger, who frowned suspiciously.

“Oh, she is fast asleep,” Caroline said guilelessly; “she never ceased coughing all night. She must be very tired.”

Her companion said nothing but looked at the girl with a smile that seemed to mean, “Poor child, you little know your mother!”

Despite distrust, as they passed the poplar avenue toward Eaubonne, the Stranger, perhaps loosened by the bright sky, pure country air, and the first green scents, entered on a conversation with his young companion as aimless as the branches’ sway, devious as butterflies, illogical as the fields’ murmur, and full of a mysterious love. At that season the country is like a bride in her marriage robe; no heart remains unmoved on leaving the Marais for Montmorency’s smiling valley, lifting a charmed gaze to eyes as infinite and loving as the view.

He found Caroline sprightly rather than witty, affectionate but ill educated; her giddy laugh hid genuine feeling. Under his shrewd questioning she spoke with the effusiveness of the lower classes, not guarded as people of the world. Her frankness brightened his face, renewing a youthful look beneath former sadness. Gradually his sternness softened to a handsome youthfulness that made Caroline proud and happy. Her light prattle lifted the last veil from his real youth and character; he seemed to relinquish ideas that haunted him and to show the liveliness beneath his solemnity.

Their talk grew so intimate that by the time the carriage stopped at the straggling houses of Saint-Leu, Caroline was calling him Monsieur Roger. Then, for the first time, the old mother awoke.

“Caroline, she has heard everything!” Roger whispered suspiciously.

Caroline replied with an exquisite smile of disbelief, dispelling the cloud his fear of some plot had caused. Madame Crochard, amazed at nothing, approved everything and followed them into the park, where the young pair wandered through meadows and fragrant copses famed by Queen Hortense.

“How lovely!” Caroline cried when, on the green ridge where Montmorency’s forest begins, she saw the valley: combes sheltering villages, a horizon of blue hills, church towers, meadows sending a murmur like the ocean. They walked beside an artificial stream to the Swiss valley and its chalet once sheltering Hortense and Napoleon. Caroline sat with reverence on the mossy bench where emperors had rested. Madame Crochard wished to see a rustic bridge and left them, saying she would not go out of sight.

“What, poor child!” Roger asked, “have you never longed for wealth and luxury? Have you never wished to wear the beautiful dresses you embroider?”

“It would not be truth,” Caroline answered, “if I said I never think how happy the rich must be. Oh yes! I often fancy, especially as I fall asleep, how glad I should be to see my poor mother no longer compelled to go out, whatever the weather, to buy provisions at her age. I should like her to have a servant to bring her coffee sweetened with white sugar, and to see her wearing out her eyes over her favorite novels rather than twisting bobbins. And a little good wine. In short, I should like her comfortable—she is so good.”

“Then she has shown you great kindness?”

“Oh yes,” the girl said with conviction, then paused as they watched Madame Crochard shaking her finger at them from the bridge. Caroline added, “She sold her last silver forks to apprentice me to the old maid who taught me to embroider. And my poor father! What did she not go through to make him end his days in happiness!” She shivered at remembrance and hid her face; “Well! come! let us forget past sorrows!” she rallied, blushing when Roger too seemed moved but daring not to look at him.

“What was your father?” he inquired.

“He was an opera-dancer before the Revolution,” she replied simply, “and my mother sang in the chorus. My father, who led stage figures, was present at the siege of the Bastille. Recognized by some assailants, who asked whether he could lead a real attack, since he led on stage, he accepted, led the insurgents, and was rewarded with the rank of captain in the Sambre-et-Meuse army, where he rose to colonel. But at Lützen he was so badly wounded that, after a year’s suffering, he died in Paris. The Bourbons returned; my mother got no pension, and we sank into such misery that we had to work. She has been ailing, poor dear, and I have never known her so little resigned; she complains much, and I cannot wonder, for she once knew an easy life. I cannot pine for delights I have never known; I have but one wish.”

“And that is?” Roger asked eagerly.

“That women may continue to wear embroidered net dresses, so I may never lack work.”

Her frankness touched him and softened his look toward Madame Crochard as she slowly returned.

“Well, children, have you had a long talk?” the old woman asked with half-laughing indulgence. “When I think, Monsieur Roger, that the ‘little Corporal’ has sat where you are sitting,” she went on, “Poor man! how my husband worshiped him! Ah! Crochard did well to die; he could not have borne to think of him where they have sent him!”

Roger put his finger to his lips; the good woman went on very gravely, unhooking a bit of her bodice to show a ribbon and cross tied round her neck by black ribbon, “they shall never hinder me from wearing what he gave to my poor Crochard, and I will have it buried with me.”

At that seditious speech, Roger suddenly rose, and they returned to the village through park walks. He left briefly to order a meal at the best Taverny eating-house, then returned to fetch them and led them through the forest alleys.

Dinner was cheerful. Roger was no longer the melancholy figure of the Rue du Tourniquet but a confiding young man ready to take life as it came, like the two hardworking women who might lack bread the next day; his smile was affectionate and childlike.

After the meal ended with a few glasses of champagne, Roger proposed they join the village ball under the chestnuts, where he and Caroline danced together. Their hands met sympathetically, their hearts beat with the same hopes, and under the blue sky and slanting, rosy sunset beams, their eyes sparkled with fires that to them made heaven pale. The power of an idea and desire seemed boundless; nothing seemed impossible. In such moments the soul foresees nothing but happiness. The day had created memories neither had known before. Would the source prove more beautiful than the river, the desire more enchanting than its fulfilment?

“So the day is already at an end!” Caroline sighed when the dance finished, noticing Roger’s face shade into sadness.

“Why should you not be as happy in Paris as here?” she asked. “Is happiness only found at Saint-Leu? I think I can never be unhappy anywhere.”

Roger, struck by her unguarded words, felt what she did; for the first time since their first glance, they shared the same idea. Frightened by the mutual silence, they went to the carriage. Before entering, they playfully took hands and ran down the dark avenue in front of Madame Crochard. When they could no longer see the white net cap, Roger, in a tremulous voice, said, “Caroline!”

Startled, the girl drew back a few steps, understanding the invitation; she held out her hand, which he passionately kissed, then she withdrew it quickly, standing on tiptoe to see her mother.

Madame Crochard affected blindness, playing the supernumerary, as if by a remnant of her old stage parts.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The adventures of these two young people were not continued in the Rue du Tourniquet. To find Roger and Caroline again we must leap into modern Paris, where newly built houses contain apartments made for newlyweds on their honeymoon. There the paper and paint were as fresh as the couple, decorations in blossom like their love; everything suited youthful notions and ardent wishes.

Halfway down the Rue Taitbout, in a house with still-white stone walls and a spotless hall, a second-floor apartment was fitted as though the architect had foreseen its use. A simple airy ante-room with a stucco dado opened into a combined drawing- and dining-room, from which came a pretty bedroom and a bathroom beyond. Every chimney-shelf bore a mirror; doors and cornices showed tasteful arabesques and the neat, decorative fitness that marked modern French architects’ work.

For more than a month Caroline had lived in this apartment, furnished by an upholsterer advised by an artist. A brief picture of the main room shows the pleasures Roger’s attentions had provided. Gray hangings trimmed with green silk adorned her bedroom; seats in light wool sateen were easy and fashionable; a simple inlaid chest held her toiletries; a matching writing-table served for love-letters on scented paper; an antique-draped bed with muslin hangings suggested thoughts of love; drab silk curtains with green fringe subdued the light; a bronze clock represented Love crowning Psyche; a red Gothic carpet set off the rest. A small dressing-table before a long glass was where the needlewoman sat, out of patience with Plaisir, the famous hairdresser.

“Do you think you will have done today?” she asked.

“Your hair is so long and so thick, madame,” replied Plaisir.

Caroline smiled; the man’s flattery doubtless revived Roger’s praises of her hair. When the hairdresser finished, a waiting-maid advised her about the dress Roger would like best. It was the beginning of September 1816 and cold; she chose a green grenadine trimmed with chinchilla. Dressed, Caroline flew into the drawing-room, opened a window, and stepped onto the elegant balcony that adorned the front. There she stood, arms crossed, not to display herself to passersby but to look down on the Boulevard at the foot of the Rue Taitbout. This side view, like an actor’s peephole in a stage drop-scene, let her catch glimpses of elegant carriages and crowds sweeping by like Ombres Chinoises. Not knowing whether Roger would arrive in a carriage or on foot, she watched both pedestrians and tilburies—the light English cabs.

After a quarter of an hour, no herald of him had appeared. Scorn and love passed over her face; disdain for all others showed as if passersby were ants. Her gray eyes, bright with fun, flamed; given over to passion, she avoided admiration as some cultivate it—she cared for no eyes below her, only one face and one thought. When the spotted head of a certain bay horse crossed the strip between the rows of houses, Caroline shivered and stood on tiptoe, hoping to recognize the white traces and the tilbury’s color. It was he.

Roger turned the corner, saw the balcony, whipped his horse to a gallop, and stopped at the bronze-green door he knew as well as his master did. The maid, having heard her mistress’ exclamation, opened at once. Roger rushed up, clasped Caroline to him, and embraced her with the effusive feeling natural to lovers who rarely meet. They went, by common impulse and with arms about each other, into the quiet fragrant bedroom; a settee stood ready by the fire. For a moment they looked at each other in silence, expressing happiness by clasped hands and a fond gaze.

“Yes, it is he!” she said at last. “Yes, it is you. Do you know, I have not seen you for three long days—an age!—But what is the matter? You are unhappy.”

“My poor Caroline—”

“There, you see! ‘poor Caroline’—”

“No, no, do not laugh, my darling; we cannot go to the Feydeau Theatre together this evening.”

Caroline pouted briefly, then the pout vanished.

“How absurd I am! How can I think of the play when I see you? Is not the sight of you the only spectacle I care for?” she cried, pushing her fingers through Roger’s hair.

“I am obliged to go to the Attorney-General’s. We have a knotty case. He met me in the great hall at the Palais; and as I am to plead, he asked me to dine with him. But, my dearest, you can go to the theatre with your mother, and I will join you if the meeting breaks up early.”

“To the theatre without you!” she cried in amazement; “enjoy any pleasure you do not share! O my Roger! you do not deserve a kiss,” she added, throwing her arms round his neck with artless passion.

“Caroline, I must go home and dress. The Marais is some way off, and I still have business to finish.”

“Take care what you are saying, monsieur,” she interrupted. “My mother says that when a man begins to talk about his business, he is ceasing to love.”

“Caroline! Am I not here? Have I not stolen this hour from my pitiless—”

“Hush!” she said, laying a finger on his mouth. “Don’t you see I am in jest?”

Back in the drawing-room, Roger’s eye fell on an object brought home that morning by the cabinetmaker. Caroline’s old rosewood embroidery frame, by which she and her mother had earned their bread when they lived in the Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, had been refitted and polished, and a net dress of elaborate design already stretched on it.

“Well, then, my dear, I shall do some work this evening. As I stitch, I shall fancy myself gone back to those early days when you used to pass by without a word, though not without a glance; the days when the memory of your look kept me awake all night. Oh my dear old frame—the best piece of furniture in my room, though you did not give it me!” she said, seating herself on Roger’s knees; he, overcome, had dropped into a chair. “Listen. All I can earn by my work I mean to give to the poor. You have made me rich. How I love that pretty home at Bellefeuille, less for what it is than because you gave it me! But tell me, Roger, I should like to call myself Caroline de Bellefeuille—can I? Is it legal or permissible?”

At Roger’s small affirmative grimace—for he hated the name Crochard—Caroline jumped for joy and clapped her hands.

“I feel,” she said, “as if I should more especially belong to you. Usually a woman gives up her own name and takes her husband’s—” An idea forced itself on her and made her blush. She took Roger’s hand and led him to the open piano. “Listen,” she added, “I can play my sonata now like an angel!” Her fingers were already running over the ivory keys when she felt herself seized round the waist.

“Caroline, I ought to be far from hence!”

“You insist on going? Well, go,” she said, with a pretty pout, but she smiled as she looked at the clock and exclaimed joyfully, “At any rate, I have detained you a quarter of an hour!”

“Goodbye, Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille,” he said, with the gentle irony of love.

She kissed him and saw him to the door; when his steps had died on the stairs she ran to the balcony to watch him get into the tilbury, gather up the reins, catch a parting look, hear the crack of his whip and the sound of wheels on the stones, watch the handsome horse, the master’s hat, the tiger’s gold lace, and at last stand gazing long after the dark corner of the street had eclipsed the vision.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Five years after Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille had settled in the pretty house in the Rue Taitbout, we revisit a domestic scene that shows how affection can bind two people. In the blue drawing-room, at the window opening on the balcony, a four-year-old boy made a tremendous noise whipping his rocking-horse; his cherubic face, framed in curls, smiled up at his mother when she said from an easy-chair, “Not so much noise, Charles; you will wake your little sister.”

The curious child slipped off his horse, tiptoed across the carpet with one finger between his teeth, and lifted the muslin veil that covered the rosy face of the infant sleeping on her mother’s knee.

“Is Eugenie asleep, then?” he said, astonished. “Why is she asleep when we are awake?” He looked up with large liquid black eyes.

“That only God can know,” Caroline answered, smiling.

The mother and boy gazed at the child, only that morning baptized.

Caroline, now about twenty-four, had blossomed under cloudless happiness. In her the woman was complete. Eager to please Roger, she had acquired accomplishments she once lacked; she played the piano tolerably and sang sweetly. Ignorant of the world’s etiquette and indifferent to its empty talk, she worked to become a good mother. Her chief pleasures were never to lose sight of her boy, to give him careful training from the cradle, to shelter him from evil influences, and to perform both the exhausting duties of a nurse and the tender offices of a parent.

From the first day she had resigned herself never to step beyond the enchanted sphere of her happiness; after six years of intimate relation she still knew her lover only as Roger. A print of Psyche lighting her lamp to gaze on Love against his prohibition hung in her room and reminded her of the conditions of her happiness. Throughout those six years she had never importuned Roger with ambitious wishes; he, full of tenderness, had been a treasure-house of kindness. She had refused carriages and diamonds; to watch for Roger’s cab from her balcony, to go with him to the theatre, to make excursions about Paris and to long for him—all these made her life, poor in incident but rich in contentment.

Rocking the infant and singing, she recalled their happy past, especially the Septembers spent at Bellefeuille: days that combined the charms of every season—flowers and fruit, mild evenings and bright mornings. In early days she ascribed Roger’s even temper to the rarity of their meetings and their nonconjugal mode of life, but she remembered with rapture that, despite her foolish fears at their first stay on the Gatinais estate, she had always found him tender and smiling. Tears rose as she thought she could not love him enough and sometimes viewed her ambiguous position as Fate’s tax on love. Invincible curiosity urged her to wonder what had led Roger to clandestine pleasure; she invented romances rather than face the truth she half suspected. She rose, took the baby to the dining-room to oversee dinner preparations, set the linen, arranged dessert. It was 6 May 1822, the anniversary of the excursion to the Park of Saint-Leu—the turning point of her life—which they always celebrated.

Caroline placed the infant in her pretty cot and went to the balcony, where she saw the carriage in which Roger now traveled. After receiving Caroline’s embraces and the little rogue’s kisses—he called Roger “papa”—Roger went to the cradle, kissed the sleeping daughter’s forehead, and produced a document covered with black writing.

“Caroline,” he said, “here is the marriage portion of Mademoiselle Eugenie de Bellefeuille.”

She gratefully took the deed of gift of State funds. “But why have you given Eugenie three thousand francs a year, and Charles only fifteen hundred?” she asked.

“Charles, my love, will be a man,” he replied. “Fifteen hundred francs are enough. A man of courage is above poverty; with so little he’ll be tempted to work. If he turns out a nonentity, I don’t want him able to play the fool. If he is ambitious, a small income will teach him the taste for work.—Eugenie is a girl; she must have a little fortune.”

He then played with his boy, who showed effusive affection and the independence of his upbringing. The family’s cheerfulness was genuine. In the evening a magic-lantern delighted Charles and made Roger and Caroline laugh. Later, after the boy was asleep, the baby woke and sought nourishment. By lamplight in the chimney corner, Roger watched the child cling to Caroline’s bosom; her long brown curls framed her fresh face. The lamplight enhanced her grace. The calm woman’s face struck Roger as sweeter than ever; he gazed tenderly at the rosy lips from which no harsh word had ever come. Caroline looked at him with the soft, sad resignation of one bearing a sacrifice and, after his assumed regretful excuses, he rose to go.

“I must be going. I have a serious case to finish, and I am expected at home. Duty before all things—don’t you think so, my darling?”

“Good-bye, then,” she said. “Go, for if you stay an hour longer I cannot so lightly set you free.”

“I have three days’ holiday,” he smiled. “I am supposed to be twenty leagues from Paris.”

A few days after that anniversary, Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille hurried to the Rue Saint-Louis in the Marais. An express messenger had sent word that her mother, Madame Crochard, was sinking under complications from chronic catarrh and rheumatism. The hackney coachman flogged up his horses at Caroline’s urgent sign and promised a handsome present. In the neat second-floor rooms where the old widow lived, three timid old female friends—accustomed companions in boston and Jardin Turc promenades—had brought in a priest. They hoped to profit from the dying woman’s end: fearing for their share of any bequest, they had persuaded the widow—who had little conscience after years of gossip—to receive spiritual counsel. Francoise, the maid, did not know that the pretty lady at whose house Madame Crochard often dined was her daughter; she suggested a confessor hoping the priest might also favor herself.

The three ancient females sat in the drawing-room, taking turns at the bedside and offering consolation or sly counsel. They were keen to learn of any heir, for the widow enjoyed “a thousand crowns a year,” and Francoise had hopes she might inherit something. One of the women ordered the Abbe Fontanon sent for, fearing the old woman would soon be unable to write. The toothless old cook fetched the priest. He was a bland, small-minded man with flabby cheeks and a double chin; his powdered hair and flat forehead lent a pleasant look until he raised small brown eyes.

“Monsieur l’Abbe,” said Francoise, “I thank you for your advice; but I have taken the greatest care of the dear soul.”

When the Abbe politely listened to the honeyed bigotry of the three dowagers, he then sat at Madame Crochard’s bedside. Decency impelled the women and Francoise to remain in the sitting-room making the proper grimaces. Francoise, sighing over a life of service that had rewarded her with paltry legacies, stayed by the cupboard to eavesdrop.

Within the room, Madame Crochard, in a feeble but purposeful voice, confessed and revealed a relic about her neck—the Imperial Cross of the Legion of Honor—which startled the priest. The old woman, trembling, asked the Abbe, “Woe upon me! Do not desert me. What, Monsieur l’Abbe, do you think I shall be called to account for my daughter’s soul?” She added that the villain had left her nothing she could bequeath; when he robbed her of Caroline, he parted them and allowed her only three thousand francs a year, the capital belonging to her daughter.

Francoise, hearing parts of this through the partition, burst into the drawing-room: “Madame has a daughter, and nothing to live on but an annuity.” The three old crones whispered among themselves, their eyes gleaming at the prospect of dividing the widow’s “pickings.”

At the Abbe’s word the dowagers scuttered away, leaving Francoise alone with her mistress. Madame Crochard’s sufferings increased; she rang and Francoise, distracted, rummaged in cupboards as if hunting a lost lottery ticket. At that moment Caroline entered the room and stood by her mother’s bed, lavishing filial tenderness.

“Oh my dear mother, how criminal I have been! You are ill, and I did not know it; my heart did not warn me. However, here I am—”

“Caroline—”

“What is it?”

“They fetched a priest—”

“But send for a doctor, bless me!” cried Caroline. “Francoise, a doctor! How is it that these ladies never sent for a doctor?”

“They sent for a priest—” the old woman gasped.

“She is so ill—and no soothing draught, nothing on her table!” Caroline cried, attentive to her mother’s vague sign. Madame Crochard, with final effort, whispered, “They brought a priest—to hear my confession, as they said.—Beware, Caroline!” and added that the priest made her tell him her benefactor’s name.

“But who can have told you, poor mother?” Caroline asked.

The old woman died, trying to look knowingly cunning. If Caroline had watched her face she would have seen what no one ever will see—Death laughing.

To understand the forces behind this opening, we must now step aside from these actors and recall certain prior incidents, the last of which concerned Madame Crochard’s death. The two parts form a whole, a story of two sets of actions that converge.

In late November 1805, a young barrister about twenty-six descended the stairs of the hôtel where the High Chancellor of the Empire resided around three one morning. Having reached the courtyard in full evening dress under a keen frost, he found no hackney coach and no sounds of Parisian coachmen. Only the pawing of the Chief Justice’s horses, the young man having left him at bouillotte with Cambacérès, sounded in the scarcely lit court. Suddenly the Chief Justice laid a hand on his shoulder. The old man, who had served the Convention and whose influence was great, pretended casualness.

“All cats are gray in the dark,” he said. “The Chief Justice cannot compromise himself by putting a pleader in the way!” At a gesture, the young lawyer climbed into the carriage.

“Where do you live?” asked the Minister.

“Quai des Augustins, monseigneur.”

The young man—Monsieur de Granville—found himself alone with a great patron who had apparently avoided him all evening. The Minister praised his recent defense in the Simeuse and Hauteserre case and announced opportunities.

“Get a judgeship in the lower courts; we want men,” he said. “Your uncle helped us through a stormy season; services of that kind are not forgotten. Before long I shall have three vacancies in the Lower Courts and in the Imperial Court in Paris. Come to see me, and take the place you prefer. Work hard, but do not be seen at my receptions. Cambacérès and I, by not speaking to you tonight, have averted the accusation of favoritism.”

The carriage stopped at the Quai des Augustins. As the young lawyer thanked his patron, the lodgekeeper handed him a letter. He thought it from his father and by candlelight read its contents: his father urged him to set off at once to Caen; Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems had lost a sister and was now an only child, and her mother could leave her about forty thousand francs a year. The father had prepared the way. He calculated that Angelique would have three hundred thousand francs; Granville’s mother’s property and his father’s portion could combine to give the social standing desired. “So if you choose a judgeship, my dear son, you are in a position to become a senator as much as any other man,” his father wrote.

Young Granville went to bed full of schemes. With the protection of the High Chancellor, the Chief Justice, and his influential relatives, and with the expected marriage, he saw himself rising to an honored bench and obscuring his modest inherited income with the fortune the match would yield. He called to mind the guileless face of Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems, companion of his childhood. As boys they had been close enough to think of each other as “little spouses,” but as they grew his proud parents discouraged intimacy. Still, he had seen her twice the previous vacation and noted her downcast demeanor.

By seven the next morning he was at the coach office and lucky to find a seat in the diligence for Caen. The sight of Bayeux’s cathedral spires moved him; his hopes were intact. After a welcome feast he and his father were shown to a low dark parlor in the Bontems house on the Rue Teinture. The room, more convent parlor than drawing-room, bore polished walnut panels, dark worsted chairs, an old mirror, and twisted candle brackets. The place felt like a chapel: a crucifix, blessed box wreaths, three religious paintings, and conventual cleanliness. The contrast with Parisian splendor chilled him. The old Count, seeing his son’s dismay, took him aside.

“Listen, my boy,” said he. “Old Bontems’ widow is a frenzied bigot. She gives alms to priests, restores chapels, goes to Mass every day, and amuses herself with church ornaments. She has even prevented my giving away some precious pictures. If you do not marry her daughter, Angelique is done for; they counsel her to live a virgin martyr. Marry her and you’ll carry her off to Paris, where married life will cure her of hair shirts and confessionals.”

“But the fifty thousand francs a year from Church property—will not that be lost?” Granville objected.

“That is the point,” the Count said. “In consideration of the marriage—Madame Bontems’ vanity being flattered by the alliance—she will settle her fortune absolutely on the girl, reserving only a life-interest. The priesthood are set against the match; but I have had the banns published, everything is ready. In a week you will be out from your mother’s clutches. You will have a pretty little soul who will give you no trouble.”

Granville hesitated and then proposed a bargain. “I am sure I am right. But I have thought you still have ten thousand francs a year of your original fortune. If you give me fifty thousand francs, I will remain a bachelor, take the judgeship, and perhaps marry someone richer later. Fifty thousand would make me independent of this marriage.”

“Are you crazy?” his father cried. “You were not brought up under the old regime. A man of our rank does not let his wife be in his way.” The Count fretted about the Revolution’s influence and all the talk of National Virtue, but he knew what was best for the family name and his son’s prospects. They entered the cathedral, and on the way the Count hummed a tune from Rose et Colas despite the sanctity.

The service of the Sacred Heart was beginning. The affiliated ladies stood near the choir; the father and son leaned against a column where their view commanded a mass of faces. Suddenly Granville heard a voice sweeter than possible—a song like the first nightingale when winter is past. Though mixed with other voices and the organ, it struck his nerves. He traced it to a young figure in a large white bonnet; he thought he recognized Angélique. The Count’s gaze confirmed the sighting: a modest, veiled girl and an elderly woman beside her.

Angélique raised her face, gazing at the altar; in the dim light Granville saw features that shook his resolve. Her white bonnet framed a regular oval face, pale gold hair parted in two bands, finely arched brows, a firm nose, and delicately traced rose lips. Her light-blue eyes expressed innocence. Though rigid in reserve, Granville ascribed it to devotion. The sanctity around her seemed to add to her charm as if prayer flowed from rows of pearls. He leaned toward her, instinctively drawn. His movement attracted her notice; she glanced toward him, recognized the companion of her childhood, and a vivid memory flushed her face; she blushed. Granville rejoiced that earthly remembrance overpowered sanctuary glow, but Angélique dropped her veil, assumed calm, and sang on without betraying emotion. When the service ended, he could not let her leave unaddressed; he came forward to make his greeting in the cathedral porch before the congregation. Madame Bontems was tremulous with pride as she took his father's arm; Granville’s impatience had triumphed over prudence.

Thus the two threads—Caroline’s domestic sorrow and Granville’s rising ambition and sentimental entanglement—begin to converge, each shaped by social constraint, secret motives, and the cunning of those around them. Caroline, content within her limited world yet vulnerable to the machinations of the priest-ridden circle attending her dying mother, and Granville, poised between a lucrative alliance and the desire for independence, both face choices that will determine the fortunes and reputations of their families.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
About a fortnight before their wedding, the Vicomte de Granville frequented the gloomy drawing-room where Mademoiselle Bontems lived, studying Angélique’s character with cautious attention after their first meeting. He always found her at a little West Indian-wood table, marking trousseau linen. Angélique never spoke first on religion; if he toyed with her handsome rosary in its green velvet bag she would quietly take it back. When Granville made mischievous remarks about religious practices she answered with an obstinate smile.

“You must either believe nothing, or believe everything the Church teaches,” she said. “Would you wish to have a woman without a religion as the mother of your children?—No. What man may dare judge between disbelievers and God? And how can I then blame what the Church allows?”

Her charity and the conviction in her eyes sometimes tempted Granville toward her faith. But he mistook desire’s enchantment for love: seduced by her beauty and what seemed harmony between heart and duty, he inferred moral perfection from physical charm. During that fortnight he read her like a book and was grateful to Madame Bontems, who had deeply implanted religious principles that made the girl resilient.

At the contract-signing Madame Bontems made her son-in-law solemnly promise to respect his daughter’s religious practices: allow her perfect liberty of conscience, let her take communion, attend church and confession as often as she pleased, and never control her choice of priestly advisers. Angélique looked at him with such innocent eyes that Granville gave his word. The Abbé Fontanon, pale and directing consciences in the household, smiled; Mademoiselle Bontems, by a slight nod, seemed to promise she would not take unfair advantage of this freedom.

A few days after the wedding they went to Paris, where his appointment as public prosecutor to the Supreme Court of the Seine recalled him. Looking for a residence, Angélique used honeymoon influence to persuade him to take a large ground-floor apartment at the corner of the Vieille Rue du Temple and the Rue Neuve Saint-François, chiefly because of its proximity to the Rue d’Orléans church and a small chapel in the Rue Saint-Louis. She pointed out the Marais was near the Palais de Justice and the lawyers they knew; a large garden and good stables suited a young couple and possible children. Granville had preferred the Chaussee d’Antin, near theatres and amusements, but yielded to his young wife’s choice.

Busy with exacting duties, he furnished his study and left the house’s decoration to Angélique, ashamed of depriving her of his company yet glad to let her have her taste. But his eye recoiled from the cold, arid solemnity pervading the rooms: panels hollowed with arabesques, heavy colors, dark green velvet benches, an antique-pattern lamp, black-and-white marble pavement and paper imitating mossy stone gave a gloomy impression. A handsome but not new barometer emphasized the void. Granville saw his wife pleased by a red braid binding cotton curtains and a decent statue on a large Gothic stove; he lacked courage to challenge her convictions, excusing her choices as provincial naivety rather than admitting dealers had likely sold old-fashioned goods to a tasteless little bigot.

Other rooms were worse. The then-fashionable School of David’s antique luxury found no favor in Angélique’s house. The square drawing-room remained Louis XV in white and tarnished gold, consoles and candelabra bearing incongruous military trophies. Bigotry, with its tiresome humility and pride, hated light, cheerful colors; she seemed to think brown and purple befitted a magistrate’s dignity and feared luxury that might suggest evil. Granville despaired, but mistook her regret at his lack of enthusiasm for affection rather than wounded self-conceit.

“Happiness, dear Angélique, does not depend on a more or less elegant piece of furniture; it depends on the wife’s sweetness, gentleness, and love.”

“Why, it is my duty to love you,” she answered mildly, “and I can have no more delightful duty to carry out.”

From April until early winter they lived in outward union. Love and hard work made Granville indifferent to many household matters. If on a Friday he found Lenten fare and asked for meat, Angélique, forbidden by the Gospel to lie, would cloak purpose under some pretext and scold the cook. Young lawyers did not keep every fast and vigil; Granville at first failed to notice the regularity of the Lenten meals. On week-days he did not know whether his wife went to Mass; on Sundays he indulged her and accompanied her. The theatres were unbearable in summer heat, and young love made him less exacting about amusements. Thus he failed to perceive the coldness, dignity and reserve of a woman who might be a bigot waiting for love in folded arms.

He believed himself happy until a fatal event in November 1808: the Canon of Bayeux Cathedral, who had formerly governed Madame Bontems’ and her daughter’s consciences, came to Paris seeking a church and perhaps a bishopric. Horrified to find Angélique so changed, he strove to reclaim her to his chilly fold. Frightened by this priest of provincial Catholicism and inflexible strictness, Madame de Granville did penance and returned from what had been perceived as laxity.

A few events that followed sufficed to show how the household wrecked itself. The first serious misunderstanding involved society invitations. Angélique accepted solemn functions but long declined balls, excusing herself with headaches. One evening Granville, impatient with these assumed indispositions, destroyed a written invitation to a Councillor of State’s ball and only gave his wife a verbal one; seeing her health sound, he took her to the entertainment. On return he reprimanded her.

“My dear,” he said, “your position, your rank, and fortune impose duties which no divine law can relieve you from. Are you not your husband’s pride? You are required to go to balls when I go, and to appear becomingly.”

“And what is so disastrous in my dress?”

“It is your manner,” he answered. “When a young man approaches you look so serious that a spiteful person might believe you doubtful of your own virtue. You seem to fear lest a smile should undo you. The world, my dearest, is not a convent. But as you mentioned your dress, I may confess it is a duty to conform to Society’s customs and fashions.”

“Do you wish that I should display my shape like those indecent women who wear gowns so low that impudent eyes can stare at their bare shoulders and their—”

“There is a difference, my dear,” he interrupted. “You wear three rows of net frills that cover your throat to your chin. You look as if you had desired your dressmaker to destroy the graceful line of your shoulders and bosom. Your bust is wrapped in so many folds that everyone was laughing at your affectation of prudery. You would be grieved if I repeated the ill-natured remarks about your appearance.”

“Those who admire such obscenity will not have to bear the burden if we sin,” she retorted.

“And you did not dance?” he asked.

“I shall never dance,” she replied.

“If I tell you that you ought to dance!” he said sharply. “Yes, you ought to follow fashions, to wear flowers in your hair and diamonds. Remember, my dear, rich people—and we are rich—are obliged to keep up luxury in the State. Is it not far better to encourage manufacturers than distribute money as alms through the clergy?”

“You talk as a statesman!” said Angélique.

“And you as a priest,” he retorted.

The argument grew bitter. Her sweet voice hid obstinacy born of priestly influence. When she appealed to the rights secured by his promise, she added that her director forbade balls; he replied the priest overstepped Church regulations. The quarrel culminated when Granville proposed the play and, defiant, Angélique referred the question in writing to Rome: could a woman wear low gowns and go to the play and balls without risking salvation?

Pope Pius VII replied decisively, condemning his daughter’s recalcitrancy and blaming the priest. His letter, in the tone of Fenelon, insisted that a wife should go wherever her husband takes her and would not be ultimately held responsible even if she sinned by his command. These sentences only made Angélique and her director accuse him of irreligion.

Before the letter arrived, Granville had discovered his wife’s strict fasting and ordered his servants to serve him meat every day. It is intolerable to be compelled to do what one would otherwise do freely; of all tyrannies, the most odious robs the soul of the merit of its thoughts and deeds.

Soon Granville ceased to invite friends or give dinners; the house might as well have been shrouded in crape. A mistress’s bigotry creates its own atmosphere. The servants, chosen for piety, wore sanctimonious expressions; bigots know one another and recommend like-minded servants from household to household. To outsiders these homes have an indescribable squalor: an appearance of avarice, mystery, and the dank scent of cold incense. This methodical meanness and narrowness of thought are summed by one word—Bigotry. In these houses bigotry is written on furniture, prints, and faces; speech and silence alike are bigoted. Everyone is ill at ease; stiffness and formality infect everything; the lady perches on a throne of ice.

One morning Granville perceived with grief that bigotry had invaded his home. Different causes produce similar effects: dulness hedges these houses with walls of brass, enclosing a desert of horrors. The home becomes not merely a tomb but a worse thing—a convent. He studied his wife dispassionately and saw narrowness in the low hair on her forehead, a rigid regularity in her features that soon made him hate the assumed sweetness that had bewitched him. He felt the thin lips might one day say, “My dear, it is for your good!”

Angélique’s complexion took on a dull pallor and austere expression that killed joy. Was this change ascetic pharisaism rather than genuine piety? Beauty without expression is an imposture. Her imperturbable smile seemed a Jesuitical formula of happiness; her charity offended and her soulless beauty became monstrous. She acted on duty, not feeling.

Some faults a wife may yield to experience or a husband’s warnings, but false religious ideas are immutable. In those minds the apotheosis of egotism—Self beyond the grave—triumphs. Thus even the Pope was censured at the tribunal of the priest and the young devotee. To be always in the right consumes every other feeling in these tyrannous souls.

A secret struggle had been going on, and Granville soon wearied of a battle without end. What man can endure a hypocritically affectionate face and categorical resistance to his slightest wishes? What is to be done with a wife who takes advantage of his passion to protect her coldness, who appears determined to be blandly inexorable, prepares ecstatically to play martyr, and looks on her husband as a scourge from God—a means of flagellation that may spare her purgatory? Women who make virtue hateful by defying Saint John’s “Love one another” are hard to bear.

If a bonnet condemned to languish in a milliner’s window existed, Granville was certain to see it on his wife’s head; if a hideous material was found, she would select it. Want of taste seemed inseparable from false pietism.

Thus in a home needing fullest sympathy Granville had no true companionship. He went alone to parties and theatres. Nothing in his house appealed to him. A huge Crucifix hung between his bed and Angélique’s, figurative of his destiny: a Man-God killed in the prime of life and beauty. The ivory of that cross was less cold than Angélique crucifying her husband under the plea of virtue. One Ash Wednesday brought the pale form of Fasting and Total Abstinence commanded in severe tones—and Granville did not deem it wise to write to the Pope and take the Consistory’s opinion on observances.

His misfortune was too great to complain: he had a pretty young wife attached to duty, virtuous—a model. She bore a child every year, nursed them herself, and brought them up in high principles; the dowagers of her circle admired her piety and regarded her as a martyr. They blamed not the wife’s scruples but the husband’s barbarous philoprogenitiveness.

Granville, worn by work and bereft of conjugal consolation, by thirty-two had sunk into the Slough of Despond. He hated life. Not wishing to set an example of dissipation, he tried to deaden feeling by hard study and began a great book on Law.

But he could not find monastic peace. When Angélique saw him forsake society to work at home, she tried to convert him. She wept thinking he might die impenitent, and could not rest until she had tried to snatch him from eternal fires. Thus Granville became the object of the mean ideas, vacuous arguments, and narrow views by which she—thinking she had achieved a first victory—aimed for a second by bringing him back within the Church’s pale.

This was the last straw. What can be more intolerable than the blind struggle of a bigot’s obstinacy against a lawyer’s acumen? What more terrible than acrimonious pin-pricks to which a passionate soul prefers a dagger-thrust? Granville neglected his home. Everything was unendurable. His children, broken by their mother’s frigid despotism, dared not go with him to the play; he could never give them pleasure without punishment from their terrible mother. His loving nature withered into indifference, a selfishness worse than death. He saved his boys from this home by sending them early to school and insisting on his right to train them; he seldom interfered with daughters, resolving they should marry as soon as possible.

Had he sought violent measures he could find no justification; his wife, backed by dowagers, would have condemned him publicly. Thus Granville had no choice but to live in complete isolation; crushed by misery he avoided intimacy with worldly women, having no hope of consolation.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The improving history of this melancholy household produced no notable events between 1806 and 1825. Madame de Granville remained unchanged after losing her husband’s affection. She paid for Masses, beseeching God and the Saints to reveal what faults displeased him and to show her how to win him back; yet the more fervent her prayers, the less Granville was seen at home.

For about five years, after obtaining a high judicial post, Granville lived in the entresol to avoid his wife. Every morning a scene took place—one that, if evil tongues are believed, repeats in many households broken by incompatibility or vice. At eight a housekeeper, nunlike in appearance, rang at Granville’s door. Admitted to the room next to his study, she repeated the same message to the footman in the same tone:

“Madame would be glad to know whether Monsieur le Comte has had a good night, and if she is to have the pleasure of his company at breakfast.”

“Monsieur presents his compliments to Madame la Comtesse,” the valet would answer after speaking with his master, “and begs her to excuse him; important business compels him to be in court this morning.”

A minute later she returned to ask if she might see the Count before he went out.

“He is gone,” was always the reply, though often his carriage still waited.

This proxy dialogue became ceremonial. Granville’s servant, a favorite with his master and cause of quarrels for his irreligious, dissipated conduct, sometimes entered the master’s room when the Count was absent and returned with the same formula.

The injured wife watched for his return, standing on the steps like an embodiment of remorse. Petty aggressiveness at the root of a monastic temper formed Madame de Granville’s character; she was thirty-five and looked forty. When forced by decency to speak to her husband or dine at home, she inflicted acid-sweet remarks and the intolerable dulness of her narrow circle, trying to put him in the wrong before servants and charitable friends.

When the post of President in a provincial court—a stepping-stone to a peer’s seat—was offered to the Comte de Granville, he begged to remain in Paris. This refusal, known only to the Keeper of the Seals, provoked conjecture among the Countess’s intimates and her director. Granville, a wealthy man of a leading Norman family earning a hundred thousand francs a year, abandoning his great book on Law and the prospect of promotion, and living dissipatedly for six years, seemed inexplicable. The Countess’s confessor, who eyed a bishopric as much through family influence as service, insinuated calumnies: perhaps Granville feared provincial life because he would have to lead a regular moral life with the Countess—unless some illicit connection had alienated him. The sanctimonious woman accepted these hints, and they destroyed her remaining illusions.

Ignorant of the world and of love’s follies, Angélique could not conceive of a marriage unlike the one that had estranged her husband and believed him incapable of crime. When he ceased to demand anything of her, she supposed the calm was natural; after giving him all her love, the priest’s suggestions undermined her convictions. She defended her husband yet could not erase the suspicion so ingeniously sown.

These alarms wrecked her weak mind and made her ill; low fever consumed her. During Lent 1822 she would not omit her austerities and fell into a decline that endangered her life. Granville’s indifference added to her torment; his care was perfunctory, like a nephew obliged to an old uncle.

Though the Countess had given up nagging and tried to greet her husband with affection, the bigot’s sharpness showed through; one speech could undo a week’s progress.

By late May, spring and better food restored some strength. One morning after Mass she sat on a garden bench, the sun recalling early married days, and reviewed the years to find where she had failed as wife and mother. She was interrupted by the Abbé Fontanon in agitation.

“Has any misfortune befallen you, Father?” she asked.

“Ah! I only wish,” cried the priest, “that all the woes inflicted on you by God were visited on me; but, my admirable friend, there are trials to which you must bow.”

“Can any worse punishments await me than those with which Providence crushes me by making my husband the instrument of His wrath?”

“You must prepare yourself,” said Fontanon, “for yet worse mischief than we or your pious friends have conceived.”

“Then I may thank God,” said the Countess, “for using you as messenger of His will.”

“He measures your sufferings by the strength of your resignation and the weight of your sins.”

“Speak; I am ready to hear!” she said, looking up to heaven. “Speak, Monsieur Fontanon.”

“For seven years Monsieur Granville has lived in sin with a concubine, by whom he has two children; on this adulterous connection he has spent more than five hundred thousand francs that ought to have belonged to his legitimate family.”

“I must see it to believe it!” cried the Countess.

“Far be it from you!” the Abbé exclaimed. “You must forgive, my daughter, and wait in patience and prayer till God enlightens your husband; unless you choose to use remedies offered by human laws.”

The long conversation that followed produced an extraordinary change. She abruptly dismissed the priest, summoned her servants—alarmed by her flushed face and frantic energy—ordered her carriage and countermanded it, changed her mind repeatedly; but at last, about three, as if resolved, she went out, leaving the household amazed.

“Is the Count coming home to dinner?” she asked his servant.

“No, madame.”

“Did you go with him to the Courts this morning?”

“Yes, madame.”

“And to-day is Monday?”

“Yes, madame.”

“Then do the Courts sit on Mondays nowadays?”

“Devil take you!” cried the man, as his mistress drove off after saying to the coachman, “Rue Taitbout.”

Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille was weeping; Roger, at her side, held one of her hands. He was silent, looking at little Charles—who, not understanding his mother’s grief, stood speechless—at the cot where Eugénie slept, and at Caroline’s face, on which sorrow had fallen like rain across cheerful beams.

“Yes, my darling,” said Roger after a long silence, “that is the great secret: I am married. But some day I hope we may form one family. My wife has been given over since last March. I do not wish her dead; yet if God takes her, I believe she will be happier in Paradise than here, where she is indifferent to grief and pleasure.”

“How I hate that woman! How could she make you unhappy? Yet it is to that unhappiness I owe my happiness!” Her tears ceased.

“Caroline, let us hope,” cried Roger. “Do not be frightened by anything the priest may have said to you. Though my wife’s confessor is a man to be feared for his power in the congregation, if he should try to blight our happiness I would find means—”

“What could you do?”

“We would go to Italy: I would fly—”

A shriek from the adjoining room made Roger start and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille quake; she rushed into the drawing-room and found Madame de Granville in a dead faint. When she recovered, she sighed deeply on finding herself supported by the Count and her rival, whom she instinctively pushed away. Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille rose to withdraw.

“You are at home, madame,” said Granville, taking Caroline by the arm. “Stay.”

The Judge took his wife, carried her to the carriage, and got in with her.

“Who has brought you to the point of wishing me dead, of resolving to fly?” asked the Countess with grief and indignation. “Was I not young? You thought me pretty—what fault have you to find with me? Have I been false? Have I not been virtuous? My heart cherished no image but yours, my ears listened to no other voice. What duty have I failed in? What have I denied you?”

“Happiness, madame,” said the Count severely. “You know there are two ways of serving God. Some think that by saying set prayers, attending Mass regularly and avoiding sin they will win heaven—but they, madame, will go to hell; they have not loved God for Himself, they have not worshiped as He chooses, they have made no sacrifice. Though mild in seeming, they are hard on their neighbors; they see the law’s letter, not its spirit. This is how you have treated me—your earthly husband; you have sacrificed my happiness to your salvation. You were always absorbed in prayer when I came to you in gladness; you wept when you should have cheered my toil; you never tried to satisfy my demands.”

“And if they were wicked,” cried the Countess hotly, “was I to lose my soul to please you?”

“It is a sacrifice another, a more loving woman, has dared to make,” said Granville coldly.

“Dear God!” she cried, bursting into tears, “Thou hearest! Has he been worthy of the prayers and penance I have lived in, wearing myself out to atone for his sins and my own?—Of what avail is virtue?”

“To win Heaven, my dear. A woman cannot be both a husband’s wife and the spouse of Christ; that would be bigamy. By stripping your soul of the love and devotion God commands you to have for me, you have cherished hatred—”

“Have I not loved you?” she interposed.

“No, madame.”

“Then what is love?” the Countess involuntarily asked.

“Love, my dear,” replied Granville with a kind of ironic surprise, “you are incapable of understanding it. The cold sky of Normandy is not that of Spain. To yield to caprices, to guess them, to find pleasure in pain, to sacrifice the world’s opinion, your pride, your religion even, and still regard these offerings as mere incense burnt to an idol—that is love—”

“The love of ballet-girls!” cried the Countess in horror. “Such flames cannot last and leave only ashes and despair. A wife should bring true friendship, equable warmth—”

“You speak of warmth as negroes speak of ice,” retorted the Count with a sardonic smile. “Even the humblest daisy has more charm than the gaudiest hawthorn. At the same time,” he added, “I will do you justice. You have kept so precisely to the straight path of imagined duty that to show wherein you have failed toward me I should enter into details that would offend your dignity and seem to undermine all morality.”

“And you dare speak of morality when you have just left the house where you have dissipated your children’s fortune in debaucheries?” cried the Countess, maddened by his reticence.

“There, madame, I must correct you,” said the Count coolly. “Though Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille is rich, it is at nobody’s expense. My uncle, master of his fortune and with several heirs, in his lifetime and out of pure friendship, gave her the estate of Bellefeuille. As for anything else, I owe it to his liberality—”

“Such conduct is worthy only of a Jacobin!” said the sanctimonious Angélique.

“Madame, you forget that your own father was one of the Jacobins you scorn,” said the Count sternly. “Citizen Bontems was signing death-warrants at a time when my uncle was doing France good service.”

Madame de Granville was silenced. But the sight she had seen reawakened the jealousy nothing could kill, and she murmured, as if to herself, “How can a woman thus destroy her own soul and that of others?”

“Bless me, madame,” replied the Count, tired of the dialogue, “you yourself may one day have to answer that question.” The Countess was frightened. “You perhaps will be excused by the merciful Judge, who will weigh our sins,” he went on, “in consideration of the conviction with which you have worked out my misery. I do not hate you—I hate those who have perverted your heart and reason. You have prayed for me, just as Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille has given me her heart and crowned my life with love. You should have been my mistress and the prayerful saint by turns. Do me the justice to confess I am no reprobate, no debauchee. My life was not sordid. Alas! after seven years of wretchedness, the craving for happiness led me, by an imperceptible descent, to love another woman and make a second home. Do not imagine I am singular; thousands of husbands here live such twofold lives.”

“Great God!” cried the Countess. “How heavy the cross Thou hast laid on me to bear! If the husband Thou hast given me below can only be made happy through my death, take me to Thyself!”

“If you had always breathed such admirable sentiments and devotion, we should be happy yet,” said the Count coldly.

“Indeed,” cried Angélique, melting into a flood of tears, “forgive me if I have done wrong. Yes, monsieur, I am ready to obey you in all things; henceforth I will be all you can wish your wife to be.”

“If your purpose, madame, is to compel me to say I no longer love you, I shall find the cruel courage to tell you so. Can I command my heart? Can I wipe out in an instant traces of fifteen years of suffering?—I have ceased to love. These words contain a mystery as deep as the words ‘I love.’ Esteem, respect, friendship may be won, lost, regained; but as to love—I might school myself a thousand years and it would not blossom again, especially for a woman too old to respond to it.”

“I hope, Monsieur le Comte, I sincerely hope such words may not be spoken to you one day by the woman you love,” she said.

“Will you put on a dress a la Grecque this evening and come to the Opera?”

The shudder with which the Countess received the suggestion was a mute reply.

Early in December 1833 a man with perfectly white hair and worn features, aged by grief more than years, walked at midnight along the Rue Gaillon. He paused before a modest two-story house and looked up at an attic window set in the roof. A dim light showed through panes, some mended with paper. He watched the wavering glimmer with the idle curiosity of a Parisian when a young man came out. As the street lamp fell on his face, the young man approached with the hesitancy of one afraid of mistaking an acquaintance.

“What, is it you,” cried he, “Monsieur le President? Alone at this hour, and so far from the Rue Saint-Lazare. Allow me the honor of giving you my arm.—The pavement is so greasy this morning that if we do not hold each other up,” he added to soothe the elder man’s susceptibilities, “we shall find it hard to escape a tumble.”

“But, my dear sir, I am no more than fifty-five, unfortunately for me,” replied the Comte de Granville. “A physician of your celebrity must know that at that age a man is still hale and strong.”

“Then you are in waiting on a lady, I suppose,” Horace Bianchon answered. “You're not in the habit of going about Paris on foot; when a man keeps such fine horses—”

“Still, when I am not visiting in the evening, I commonly return from the Courts or the club on foot,” the Count answered.

“And with large sums of money about you, perhaps!” cried the doctor. “It is a positive invitation to the assassin’s knife.”

“I am not afraid of that,” said Granville with melancholy indifference.

“But at least do not stand about,” said the doctor, leading the Count toward the boulevard. “A little more and I should believe you bent on robbing me of my last illness, and dying by some hand other than mine.”

“You caught me playing the spy,” said the Count. “Whether on foot or in a carriage, I've for some time noticed at a third-floor window of your house the shadow of a person who seems to work with heroic constancy.”

The Count paused as if feeling sudden pain. “And I take as great an interest in that garret,” he went on. “I would not give a centime to know whether the shadow that moves across that shabby blind is that of a man or a woman, nor whether the inhabitant of that attic is happy or miserable. Though I was surprised to see no one at work there this evening, and though I stopped to look, it was solely for the pleasure of indulging in conjectures as numerous and idiotic as those of idlers who see a building left half finished. For nine years—” the Count hesitated; then, with a bitter wave of the hand, “No, I will not say friend—I hate everything that savors of sentiment.—Well, for nine years past I have ceased to wonder that old men amuse themselves with flowers and trees; the events of life have taught them disbelief in all human affection; I grew old within a few days. I will no longer attach myself to any creature but to unreasoning animals, or plants, or superficial things. I think more of Taglioni’s grace than of all human feeling. I abhor life and the world in which I live alone. Nothing—nothing,” he added, startling Bianchon, “no, nothing can move or interest me.”

“But you have children?” the doctor asked.

“My children!” He repeated bitterly. “Yes—my eldest daughter is the Comtesse de Vandenesse; the other will make a good match through her. My sons succeeded in their careers—the Viscount was public prosecutor at Limoges and is now President of the Court at Orléans; the younger is public prosecutor in Paris. They have their own cares. If one of them had been devoted to me, tried by entire affection to fill up the void here,” and he struck his breast, “that one would have failed in life, have sacrificed it for me. And why should he? Would I not have taken such generosity as a debt? But, doctor,” and the Count smiled with deep irony, “it is not for nothing that we teach them arithmetic and how to count. At this moment perhaps they are waiting for my money.”

“O Monsieur le Comte, how could such an idea enter your head—you who are kind and humane!” protested Bianchon.

“To please myself,” replied the Count. “I pay for a sensation, as I would pay to recover the most childish illusion that would make my heart glow.—I help my fellow-creatures for my own sake, just as I gamble; and I look for gratitude from none. I should see you die without blinking; and I beg you to feel the same with regard to me. I tell you, young man, the events of life have swept over my heart like the lavas of Vesuvius over Herculaneum. The town is there—dead.”

“Those who have brought a soul as warm and living as yours to such indifference are indeed guilty!” Bianchon said.

“Say no more,” answered the Count with a shudder.

“You have a malady which you ought to allow me to treat,” Bianchon said, moved.

“What, do you know of a cure for death?” cried the Count irritably.

“I undertake, Monsieur le Comte, to revive the heart you believe frozen.”

“Are you a match for Talma, then?” asked the Count satirically.

“No, Monsieur le Comte. But Nature is as far above Talma as Talma is superior to me.—Listen: the garret you are interested in is inhabited by a woman of about thirty whose love is carried to fanaticism. The object of her adoration is a young man of pleasing appearance but endowed by some malignant fairy with every conceivable vice. This fellow is a gambler, and it is hard to say which he is most addicted to—wine or women; he has, to my knowledge, committed acts deserving punishment by law. To him this unhappy woman sacrificed a life of ease, a man who worshiped her, and the father of her children.—But what is wrong, Monsieur le Comte?”

“Nothing. Go on.”

“She has allowed him to squander a perfect fortune; she would give him the world if she had it; she works night and day; many a time she has, without a murmur, seen the wretch rob her even of the money saved to buy the children’s clothes and food. Only three days ago she sold her hair, the finest I ever saw; he came in, she could not hide the gold piece quickly enough, and he asked her for it. For a smile, for a kiss, she gave up the price of a fortnight’s life and peace. Is it not dreadful, and yet sublime? Work has hollowed her cheeks. Her children’s crying has broken her heart; she is ill, and at this moment on her wretched bed. This evening they had nothing to eat; the children had not strength to cry—they were silent when I went up.”

Bianchon stopped. The Count, despite himself, had put his hand into his waistcoat pocket.

“I can guess, my young friend, how it is that she is yet alive if you attend her,” the elder man said.

“O poor soul!” cried the doctor.

At the name the Count shuddered and grasped the doctor’s arm with an iron grip, as it seemed to Bianchon.

“Her name is Caroline Crochard?” asked the President in a voice broken.

“Then you know her?” asked the doctor, astonished.

“And the wretch’s name is Solvet.—Ay, you have kept your word!” exclaimed Granville. “You have roused my heart to the most terrible pain it can suffer till it is dust. That emotion, too, is a gift from hell, and I always know how to pay those debts.”

They reached the corner of the Rue de la Chaussée d’Antin. A night-bird with a basket and crook, a scavenger with a shriveled face, stood by the curb. Granville addressed him.

“Do you ever pick up a thousand-franc note?”

“Now and then, master.”

“And you restore them?”

“It depends on the reward offered.”

“You’re the man for me,” cried the Count, giving the scavenger a thousand-franc note. “Take this, but remember, I give it to you on condition of your spending it at the wineshop, of getting drunk, fighting, beating your wife, blacking your friends’ eyes. That will give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist—perhaps to the police, the public prosecutor, the judge, and the prison warders. Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later.”

A draughtsman would need the pencils of Charlet and Callot, and the brushes of Teniers and Rembrandt, to render the scene.

“Now I have squared accounts with hell, and had some pleasure for my money,” said the Count, pointing out the scavenger’s indescribable physiognomy to the stupefied doctor. “As for Caroline Crochard—she may die of hunger and thirst, hearing the heartrending shrieks of her starving children and convinced of the man’s baseness. I will not give a sou to rescue her; and because you have helped her, I will see you no more—”

He left Bianchon standing like a statue and walked briskly to the Rue Saint-Lazare, soon reaching his little house, where, to his surprise, a carriage waited at the door.

“Monsieur, your son, the attorney-general, came about an hour since,” said the man-servant, “and is waiting for you in your bedroom.”

Granville signed the man to leave him.

“What motive can be strong enough to require you to infringe the order I have given my children never to come to me unless I send for them?” asked the Count of his son as he entered.

“Father,” the son replied with trembling respect, “I venture to hope that you will forgive me when you have heard me.”

“Your reply is proper,” said the Count. “Sit down.” He pointed to a chair. “But whether I walk up and down, or take a seat, speak without heeding me.”

“Father,” the son went on, “this afternoon, at four o’clock, a very young man who was arrested in the house of a friend of mine, whom he had robbed to a considerable extent, appealed to you.—He says he is your son.”

“His name?” asked the Count hoarsely.

“Charles Crochard.”

“That will do,” said the father with an imperious wave.

Granville paced the room in solemn silence. His son did not break it.

“My son,” he began in a voice so mild and fatherly that the young lawyer started, “Charles Crochard spoke the truth.—I am glad you came to me to-night, my good Eugène,” he added. “Here is a considerable sum of money”—and he handed him a bundle of banknotes—“you can make any use of it you think proper in this matter. I trust you implicitly, and approve beforehand whatever arrangements you may make, either now or for the future.—Eugène, my dear son, kiss me. We part perhaps for the last time. Tomorrow I shall crave my dismissal from the King, and I am going to Italy.

“Though a father owes no account of his life to his children, he is bound to bequeath the experience Fate sells him—part of their inheritance.—When you marry,” the Count continued with involuntary shuddering, “do not undertake it lightly; that act is the most important demanded by society. Remember to study at your leisure the character of the woman who is to be your partner; but consult me too, I will judge of her myself. A lack of union between husband and wife, whatever the cause, leads to terrible misfortune; sooner or later we are punished for contravening the social law.—But I will write to you on this subject from Florence. A father who presides over a supreme court must not have to blush in the presence of his son. Good-bye.”

PARIS, February 1830–January 1842.
