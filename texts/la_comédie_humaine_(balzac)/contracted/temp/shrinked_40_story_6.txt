The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, a dark, winding lane near the Hôtel de Ville that ended at the Rue Martroi, was closed by a turnstile until 1823. Less than six feet wide at its broadest, it lay perpetually muddy with pooled gutter water; sunlight touched it only briefly in summer and dampness lingered. Residents lit lamps at five in June and kept them burning through winter. Approaching the Marais felt like walking through cellars, as many old Paris streets did. The corner house at Rue du Tourniquet and Rue de la Tixeranderie still bore iron rings from chains once used for public safety.

To combat humidity, the house raised its cellars above ground; an entrance reached by three steps formed a closed arch with a keystone carved as a female head. Three high, iron-barred windows lit a small dwelling where two beds draped in green serge and an old woman tending a brazier—preparing a porter’s-wife stew—were visible. The kitchen-dining room was spare but kept with evident cleanliness and thrift.

The old woman’s pale, wrinkled face seemed part of the house; she sat by the fireplace with a look of mischief mingled with resignation while her daughter worked at an embroidery frame. The girl, modest and industrious, retained youthful charm—rosy cheeks and bright eyes—despite poverty. One August day in 1815 the mother noticed a new face among the passersby: a tall, thin man in black, solemn and suffering. She watched for him, and Caroline, unusually stirred, felt a sympathy that made her look forward to another glimpse, as if his arrival might alter her life.

For two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved erratically, no longer passing the Rue du Tourniquet at his usual hour. Madame Crochard noticed his distraction—often lost in thought and seldom acknowledging her or her daughter Caroline. One mid‑September morning Caroline’s lively presence caught his eye; their fleeting glances hinted at a silent understanding.

Weeks later he resumed passing twice daily; their brief encounters became a guarded routine. Their wordless exchanges showed concern: Caroline sensed his troubles, and he knew when she worried about work. Both remained cautious, fearing intimacy might cause trouble. Annoyed by her daughter’s indecision, Madame Crochard turned her displeasure toward the Gentleman, who had once met her mild approval. As winter brought harder times he overheard a revealing mother‑and‑daughter talk about finances and, moved, secretly left money for them. Caroline found it the next day; a silent gratitude passed between them.

In early May Caroline proposed a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—more somber than usual—unexpectedly joined. Madame Crochard pretended to sleep, letting them speak freely. The countryside and their easy talk lifted his spirits; he began to see Caroline differently. Sharing family hardships and personal stories deepened their bond. The day brimmed with laughter, and as evening fell Caroline voiced her newfound happiness. Roger—by that name now—playfully held her hand before leaving, sealing an unspoken promise.

Their story continues in Paris. Caroline has spent a month in a charming second‑floor flat on Rue Taitbout, English‑styled with gray hangings trimmed in green silk, comfortable chairs, a writing table for love letters, a bronze clock of Love crowning Psyche, and a Gothic carpet. Preparing for Roger’s visit, her hairdresser flatters her. On a chilly September day in 1816, dressed in green grenadine trimmed with chinchilla, she watches from the balcony; after fifteen minutes impatience gives way to joy when he arrives and they embrace passionately. He must attend a meeting with the Attorney‑General, disappointing her; she objects to his theatre visits without her, declares her deep affection, and, perched on his lap, says she wishes to take his name—Caroline de Bellefeuille—and use her earnings to help the poor. She plays a sonata as he drives away, longing for his return.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

Five years after settling in her Rue Taitbout home, Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille sits contentedly with her four-year-old son Charles, who plays with a rocking horse. Now twenty-four, her look reflects happiness. She has embraced motherhood, devoted to raising her children and cultivating their sense of goodness and beauty, indifferent to society’s demands for luxury or status.

Rocking her infant daughter Eugénie, Caroline recalls joyful visits to Bellefeuille with Roger. Their love has deepened in the simplicity of shared life, yet she wonders why Roger insists on secrecy and what their true arrangement is.

On May 6, 1822—the anniversary of a pivotal day—Caroline prepares for dinner, expecting Roger. He brings a document detailing Eugénie’s marriage portion and explains his view that Charles, as a boy, must learn self-sufficiency while Eugénie deserves a fortune. Their evening is warm, ending with a lantern show for Charles. But as night deepens, Roger must leave; he promises three days off, yet duty and secrecy leave Caroline with the familiar ache of separation.

Soon after, Caroline rushes to her mother, Madame Crochard, who is gravely ill. The widow’s acquaintances, unaware Caroline is her daughter, have summoned a priest for last rites. Arriving amid confusion and opportunistic neighbors hoping to secure the widow’s wealth, Caroline tends her mother tenderly. In her last moments Madame Crochard laments their estrangement and reveals the priest forced a confession about Caroline’s benefactor—hinting a complex web of secrets.

The tale then backtracks to November 1805: a young barrister, Monsieur de Granville, precarious after leaving a High Chancellor’s gathering, receives a letter proposing marriage to Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems to secure his future despite the family’s stigma. In Bayeux he finds a household stifled by the widow’s piety; during a church service Angélique’s beauty revives a childhood affection, setting a reunion that will test love, duty, and social expectation.

For about two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he frequented her dreary drawing-room and watched Angelique mark her trousseau. She never argued about religion; when he playfully handled her rosary or mocked practices she calmly returned it to its bag. Firm in the Church’s teachings, she insisted a mother must have a religion and asked how one could judge between disbelievers and God. Granville, taken by her conviction, sometimes mistook desire for love and was even tempted to adopt her beliefs. Angelique reconciled feeling with duty, leaving him uncertain about his own emotions.

At the signing of the marriage contract Madame Bontems exacted a promise from Granville to respect Angelique’s practices and allow her freedom of conscience; Angelique’s gentle look convinced him and Abbe Fontanon approved. After their wedding they moved to Paris, where Granville became public prosecutor. Angelique persuaded him to take a ground-floor apartment in the Marais near a church and chapel for the sake of future children. Granville, who preferred a more fashionable quarter, yielded. He installed his study and left furnishing to Angelique, who delighted in arranging the home. He was dismayed by the cold solemnity she created—a reflection of her austere upbringing. He tried to comfort her that happiness came from love and gentleness, not material things, yet grew frustrated by the lack of warmth and charm.

For a time the couple seemed harmonious; Granville stayed absorbed in work and ignored Angelique’s strict observances, passing off her Lenten austerities as singular zeal. He went to church with her on Sundays but did not grasp her fervor until a disruptive event. In November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux, Angelique’s spiritual director, arrived in Paris alarmed at her perceived moral decline and aimed to reclaim her from laxity. Angelique adopted stricter practices and frequently excused herself from social events, especially balls. After a tense evening Granville confronted her; she cited her director’s prohibitions and even wrote to the Pope for guidance.

The Pope’s reply sided with Granville, saying a wife should accompany her husband even at some personal cost. Rather than reconcile them, the decision deepened the divide: Angelique and her director dismissed it. Frustrated, Granville began asserting his preferences more forcibly—ordering meat daily despite her objections—and retreated from social life. Their home grew oppressive; Angelique’s austerity dimmed her beauty and replaced warmth with duty. Granville felt trapped in a marriage that left little room for affection. Their children, raised under strict rules, suffered: he sent his sons away to school early and planned to marry off his daughters as soon as possible. Increasingly isolated, he sought refuge in work and study, while Angelique’s efforts to reconvert him only worsened their estrangement. The household became a cold, rigid place rather than a home of companionship.

From 1806 to 1825 the de Granville household showed little outward drama, but Madame de Granville was inwardly miserable. She turned to Mass as Granville lived in the entresol to avoid her, citing court work.

By thirty-five she was aged. When Granville declined a provincial presidency, friends and her confessor suspected an affair. In Lent 1822 her health fell from stress and fever; her piety undermined attempts at warmth.

Late in May Abbé Fontanon revealed Granville had lived seven years with a concubine, fathered two children, and dissipated their fortune. Shocked, despite the Abbé's counsel she resolved to confront him. Before she could, a quarrel with Mlle de Bellefeuille—who declared she hated and blamed her—ended with her fainting. Recovering, Granville accused her of putting piety before his happiness, admitted the affair, refused restitution, and declared the marriage failed.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Early one December night in 1833 a white‑haired man whose face showed grief more than years paced the Rue Gaillon and stopped before a modest two‑story house with a dim attic light. A young doctor, Horace Bianchon, emerged; recognizing him, he offered his arm and bantered about the late hour and the slippery pavement. The visitor—Comte de Granville—answered with weary pride that at fifty‑five he was still hale and often walked home from the Courts or the club when not visiting in the evening.

Bianchon teased that a man of his station, with fine horses and money, was courting robbery. Granville shrugged off the danger with melancholy, confessing he had been spying on a shadow in the third‑floor garret of Bianchon’s house. He treated his curiosity as a trivial amusement, like watching the Palais‑Royal being finished. When Bianchon offered to tell him who lived there, Granville refused: he would not pay to learn whether the shadow was man or woman, happy or miserable. Nine years earlier he had stopped expecting human affection to endure. “I grew old within a few days,” he said. He had renounced attachment to people, clinging only to “unreasoning animals, or plants, or superficial things”—even the grace of Taglioni affected him more than human feeling. He professed to abhor life and insisted nothing could interest him.

Bianchon pressed him: did he have children? Granville replied bitterly, listing them: an eldest daughter, the Comtesse de Vandenesse; another daughter suitably married through the sister; a viscount son who had been public prosecutor at Limoges and now President of the Court at Orléans; and a younger son a public prosecutor in Paris. Each had their cares; none had devoted themselves to him. If one had tried to fill the void in his breast, that child would have ruined his life by sacrificing itself to him—and why should it? Perhaps they now waited for his money. Granville described his philanthropy as a form of self‑indulgence: he paid for sensations the way he gambled, seeking illusions that might make his heart glow while expecting no gratitude. He said life’s events had flowed over his heart like lava over Herculaneum—the town remained, dead. He declared he would see Bianchon die without blinking and asked Bianchon to feel the same toward him.

Unable to leave him in that mood, Bianchon insisted he could treat Granville’s malady and, reluctantly, described the garret’s occupant. His portrait was stark: a woman of about thirty whose love had become fanaticism. She adored a young man of pleasing appearance but every vice—Solvet—a gambler given to wine and women, guilty of acts the law might punish. This woman had abandoned a comfortable life and an adoring husband, the father of her children, for Solvet. She worked day and night; he had stolen from her even funds saved for the children’s food and clothes. Only three days earlier she had sold her hair—the finest Bianchon had ever seen—and when the lover returned she could not hide the gold quickly enough. For a smile or a kiss she would give away the price of a fortnight’s subsistence. The toil had hollowed her cheeks; the children’s cries had broken her heart; she lay ill on her wretched bed that night. When Bianchon visited, the family had nothing to eat; the children were so weak they could not even cry.

Despite himself Granville slid his hand toward his waistcoat pocket as if to help, but Bianchon begged him to pity the woman and to help cure her passion. Granville recoiled: how could he pity a misery whose joys he might have prized with his whole fortune? Did not thousands of dead men, thousands of sick and old men, long to barter everything for a return of youth and feeling? Bianchon murmured “Poor Caroline!” At the name Granville shuddered, gripped the doctor’s arm, and asked hoarsely whether the woman’s name was Caroline Crochard. When Bianchon confirmed it, Granville’s reaction was violent: he accused Bianchon of rousing the worst pain his heart could endure, an emotion he called a gift from hell. He vowed he knew how to pay such debts.

They had reached the Rue de la Chaussée d’Antin when a shriveled night scavenger stood by the curb. Granville asked if the scavenger ever found thousand‑franc notes and, hearing that sometimes he did, handed him such a note with a grotesque injunction: spend it in the wine shop, get drunk, start fights, beat your wife, blacken your friends’ eyes—give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist, perhaps to the police, public prosecutor, judge, and prison wardens. “Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later,” Granville said. He pointed out the scavenger’s corrupt face to Bianchon and declared he had thus “squared accounts with hell” and had found a perverse pleasure in the money. As for Caroline Crochard, he pronounced coldly that she might die of hunger and thirst listening to the cries of her starving children, convinced of her lover’s baseness; he would not give a sou to rescue her. Because Bianchon had helped her, he said, he would see him no more.

Granville left Bianchon stunned and walked to the Rue Saint‑Lazare where a carriage waited. A servant informed him that his son, the attorney‑general, had been to see him an hour earlier and was waiting in his bedroom. Granville dismissed the servant, then confronted his son—in a tone at once imperious and unexpectedly tender—about coming after being ordered not to visit unless summoned.

Eugène, the younger man, spoke with tremor: that afternoon a very young man arrested in the house of a friend he had robbed had appealed to Granville, claiming to be his son. “His name?” Granville asked. “Charles Crochard,” Eugène answered. Granville waved him down: that sufficed. He stood a moment, then spoke as a father. “Charles Crochard spoke the truth. I am glad you came to me tonight, my good Eugène.” He handed his son a considerable bundle of banknotes and authorized him to use it as he saw fit in the affair, trusting him implicitly and approving whatever arrangements Eugène might make.

The mood shifted. Granville’s last words of parental counsel and farewell were almost tender. He warned Eugène about the weight of marriage—“that act is the most important society requires of us”—and urged him not to undertake it lightly, to study a woman’s character and to consult him. A lack of union between husband and wife led to terrible misfortunes and social punishment, he warned with an involuntary shudder. He promised to write on the subject from Florence. Then, with a quietness that suggested this might be their last parting, he asked his son to kiss him and announced his plan: tomorrow he would beg dismissal from the King and go to Italy. He wished to preserve his dignity as a father and as a former presiding officer of a supreme court of justice; he would not wish to blush before his son.

Thus the scene presents two images of Granville: in the street a man hardened into cruelty and misanthropy, cursing pity and spending money to foment petty ruin; in his bedroom a father handing cash and last counsels to his son, preparing to depart. Between those moments stands Bianchon’s account of Caroline Crochard—a portrait of sacrifice and degradation—and the Comte’s icy rejection of any duty toward her, even as the name awakens a torment proving he is not wholly dead to feeling. The narrative closes with Granville resolved to quit Paris and to cut off a friend who had awakened his pity, while privately acknowledging familial ties he attempts to leave behind with money and admonition.
