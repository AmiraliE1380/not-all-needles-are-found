Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, a dark winding lane ending at Rue Martroi, was shut by a turnstile until 1823. Under six feet wide, it gathered gutter water and mud; sunlight touched it briefly, and dampness lingered. Residents trimmed lamps in summer, kept them burning through winter. Passing the Marais felt like moving through cellars, and at the corner of Rue de la Tixeranderie iron rings from safety chains jutted from the walls.

A corner house built to combat damp—raised cellars, three steps up—had a closed-arch door crowned by a carved keystone and three high iron-barred windows. Through them passersby glimpsed two beds and an old woman tending a brazier, preparing a simple porter’s stew. The kitchen was sparse but kept clean and thrifty.

The old woman, pale and wrinkled, sat by the hearth with resignation while her daughter worked at an embroidery frame. The mother, with slow fingers and worn spectacles, often glanced at the street; the daughter bent over her work with a downcast gaze. Their home mirrored working life, yet the girl kept youthful charm—rosy cheeks and bright eyes—while the mother dreamed of a better future for her.

In August 1815 the mother noticed a new face: a tall thin man in black, solemn—the sort she guessed worked in the Prefect’s office. Excited, she awaited his return. When he returned, Caroline, usually indifferent, felt sudden sympathy at the suffering in his face and began to look forward to his passing, as if he might alter her life.

For over two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved oddly, no longer passing the Rue du Tourniquet at his usual hour. Madame Crochard noticed his distracted air—he was often lost in thought and seldom saw her or her daughter, Caroline. One mid-September morning Caroline’s liveliness caught him; he paused to watch her at the window, and a silent exchange of furtive glances began, signaling a growing understanding.

After that he came twice daily, and their brief meetings became a quiet routine. Their guarded exchanges showed mutual concern: Caroline sensed his troubles, and he knew when she worried about work. Both remained cautious, fearing closer ties might lead to complications; Madame Crochard, frustrated by Caroline’s indecision, grew displeased with the Gentleman.

As winter neared, family hardships weighed on Caroline. Hearing a revealing mother-daughter conversation one night, the Gentleman was moved to leave them money in secret; Caroline found it the next day, and a wordless gratitude passed between them.

In early May Caroline suggested a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—more somber than usual—accompanied them. During the carriage ride Madame Crochard feigned sleep, allowing a light conversation that brightened him and changed how he saw Caroline. Sharing personal stories—her sacrifices, his own—deepened their bond. The day brimmed with laughter, and as they left they playfully held hands, a discreet yet pivotal sign of unspoken promises and hope for a brighter future together.

The adventures of Roger and Caroline continue in modern Paris, where new apartments suit honeymooners. In a pristine building on Rue Taitbout, Caroline has lived over a month in a charming second-floor apartment, tastefully furnished in English style. Her bedroom, with gray hangings edged in green silk, cozy seating, and a writing table for love letters, is warmed by a bronze clock showing Love crowning Psyche and a Gothic-patterned carpet. As she prepares for Roger’s visit, her hairdresser flatters her and confesses admiration.

On a chilly September day in 1816, Caroline slips into a green grenadine gown trimmed with chinchilla and watches from the balcony, heart racing. After a quarter-hour she sees Roger approaching; they embrace passionately after three days apart. He then must attend a meeting with the Attorney-General, disappointing her. She protests the theatre without him, reaffirms her devotion, vows to be Caroline de Bellefeuille and help the poor, plays a sonata, and watches him drive away, longing for his return.

Five years after settling on Rue Taitbout, twenty-four-year-old Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille is quietly content. Embracing motherhood, she spends her days with her four-year-old son Charles and infant daughter Eugénie, caring little for luxury or social ambition and remembering happy visits to Bellefeuille with Roger, whose secrecy she wonders at. On May 6, 1822—an anniversary she marks—Roger arrives with a document detailing Eugénie’s marriage portion, sparking a discussion about unequal provisions: Roger insists Charles should learn self-reliance while Eugénie, as a girl, must be more securely provided for. Their warm evening ends with a lantern show for Charles, but Roger is soon called away on duty.

Shortly after, Caroline hurries to her mother, Madame Crochard, gravely ill. Unaware acquaintances—who do not know Caroline is her daughter—summon a priest for last rites while scheming old women hope to profit from the widow’s wealth. Caroline’s devoted care contrasts with their greed. In her final moments Madame Crochard laments their estrangement and reveals the priest pressed her to confess about Caroline’s benefactor, hinting at a tangle of relationships and secrets.

A flashback to November 1805 explains some connections. Young barrister Monsieur de Granville, returning from a gathering at the High Chancellor, faces a career opportunity aided by an uncle but receives a letter urging him home: a proposed marriage to Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems would secure his future despite the Bontems’ social stigma. He recalls a childhood fondness for Angélique, travels to Bayeux where his father escorts him to the piously strict household, and, though dreading the match, glimpses Angélique in church—her beauty rekindling his attachment and prompting him to approach her. The intertwined stories probe love, duty, and social expectation as characters confront desire and obligation.

For about two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he frequented her dreary drawing-room and watched Angelique mark her trousseau. She never initiated religious talk; when Granville playfully handled her rosary or mocked practice she quietly returned it to its bag with a serene smile. Firmly believing in the Church, she insisted a mother must have a religion and questioned how one could judge between disbelievers and God. Granville, captivated by her conviction, sometimes mistook desire for love. Angelique, reconciling feeling with duty, left him unsure of his own sentiments. He thanked Madame Bontems for instilling religion in Angelique, thinking it would sustain her in adversity.

At the marriage-contract signing Madame Bontems made Granville promise to respect Angelique’s religious practices and freedom of conscience; the Abbe Fontanon approved. They married and moved to Paris, where Granville became public prosecutor. Angelique persuaded him to take a ground-floor apartment in the Marais, near a church and chapel, arguing it would be good for future children. Granville, who preferred a more fashionable district, yielded and concentrated on his work, leaving decoration to Angelique, who delighted in furnishing their new home. He soon found the apartment cold and solemn, reflecting her austere upbringing.

Though he tried to understand her choices, Granville grew frustrated by the lack of warmth and charm and attributed the sober decor to her Bayeux background. He attempted to comfort Angelique, believing happiness would come from her love and gentleness rather than material comforts. From April through the winter their life seemed outwardly harmonious: Granville kept busy with work, accompanied her to church on Sundays, and failed to notice many strict observances she enforced—Lenten meals and other austerities she passed off as carelessness.

In November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux, who had once guided Angelique, arrived in Paris alarmed and sought to reclaim her from what he saw as laxity; she instead adopted stricter practices and began avoiding social obligations, especially balls, which enraged Granville. After a tense evening he confronted her; arguments escalated into theology. Angelique wrote to the Pope, whose reply sided with Granville—saying a wife should accompany her husband even if it risked compromising her virtue—but she and her director dismissed it.

Frustrated, Granville asserted his preferences—ordering meat daily despite her objections—and withdrew from social life, feeling weighed down by her bigotry. The household grew oppressive; Angelique’s beauty was overshadowed by austerity, duty replaced affection, and the children suffered. Granville sent his sons to school early, resolved to marry off his daughters, and sought refuge in work, while Angelique tried to convert him. Their relationship deteriorated, leaving their home a place of coldness and rigidity rather than warmth and companionship.

Between 1806 and 1825 the de Granville household displayed little outward drama but suffered deep estrangement. Madame de Granville, unchanged after losing her husband's affection, devoted herself to Masses and prayer to restore their relationship. Granville, a prominent judge, withdrew to the entresol of their house to avoid her; every morning a housekeeper relayed messages while he habitually declined breakfast, citing urgent court business. At thirty-five she looked older and made narrow-minded remarks meant to engage him. When he refused the presidency of a provincial court, friends and her confessor suspected an illicit affair—an idea she found inconceivable.

In Lent of 1822 her health declined with stress and a low fever, worsened by Granville’s indifference. She tried to be more affectionate, but her piety and rigidity undermined her efforts. In late May she consulted the Abbé Fontanon, who revealed that Granville had lived with a concubine for seven years, fathering two children and squandering much of their fortune. Stunned and torn between faith and betrayal, she struggled to accept the news. The Abbé urged forgiveness and patience, but she left home determined to confront him.

At that moment Roger and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille were in a tense exchange—she declared hatred for Roger’s wife and blamed her for their unhappiness; Roger spoke of a future together. Their conversation was interrupted when Madame de Granville fainted. Revived, she demanded whether Granville loved her and had been faithful. He retorted that she had sacrificed his happiness to piety and failed as a loving wife, arguing love needs sacrifice and understanding. He admitted seeking happiness elsewhere and acknowledged his affection for another, leaving her devastated. The confrontation exposed the irreparable gulf: their marriage was effectively over, and Madame de Granville was left to face the consequences and an uncertain future.

Early in December 1833 a white-haired, worn man, aged more by grief than years, paced the Rue Gaillon at midnight. He stopped before a modest two-story house; a dim light flickered in the attic. A young physician, Horace Bianchon, recognized him and approached.

The Comte de Granville answered Bianchon’s teasing offers of escort with a weary pride in his fifty-five years and a touch of melancholy. He confessed he had been loitering to watch a shadow at a third-floor window, indulging idle conjectures. For nine years he had ceased to expect affection from people; life’s events had taught him to attach himself only to animals, plants, and superficial pleasures. He declared himself bored and incapable of interest in human feeling.

Bianchon pressed him, noting that such indifference was a malady he could treat. Granville scoffed but allowed the doctor to speak. Bianchon described the garret’s occupant: a woman of about thirty whose love had become fanaticism for a young scoundrel named Solvet—a gambler and drunkard, morally ruined, and perhaps criminal. This woman, Caroline Crochard, had sacrificed ease, a devoted husband, and the father of her children to him; she had let him squander her savings, even the money for her children’s clothes and food. She had worked and sold her hair to support him; her children’s cries had broken her heart; she was ill and, that evening, her house had nothing to eat. Bianchon had been up to her garret and found the children too weak to cry.

Granville, despite himself, put his hand into his waistcoat pocket. Bianchon’s story moved him: the living feeling in Caroline stirred a longing in Granville for what his life lacked. Yet his bitterness rose. He insisted he would not give a sou to rescue her; he would not risk hope or compassion again. He mocked the idea that his children might care for him, recalling their positions and suggesting they were likely thinking of his money. He admitted to helping others for his own sensations, like gambling for a thrill; gratitude was not his expectation. He said he had grown old in a day and would attach himself only to things that could not betray him.

At the name Caroline Crochard Granville shuddered violently and gripped Bianchon’s arm. When Bianchon confirmed the woman’s name and that the lover was Solvet, Granville’s restraint snapped: he called the emotion this knowledge aroused “a gift from hell” and spoke of paying debts to that hell. They reached the Rue de la Chaussée-d’Antin where a shriveled night scavenger stood by the curb. Granville asked if scavengers ever found large banknotes and if they returned them. When the man replied that it depended on the reward, Granville handed him a thousand-franc note with grotesque instructions: spend it at the wine-shop, get drunk, fight, beat your wife, black your friends’ eyes—create trouble to keep the city’s servants of order busy. With this cynical, almost theatrical gift, Granville claimed he had “squared accounts with hell” and had bought himself some pleasure.

He then told Bianchon that Caroline might starve listening to her children’s cries, convinced of her lover’s baseness, and that he would not aid her; because Bianchon had helped her, Granville declared he would see the doctor no more. He left abruptly and took a carriage to his house on the Rue Saint-Lazare.

At home his servant announced that his son, the attorney-general, had been there an hour earlier and was waiting in the bedroom. Granville dismissed the servant and confronted his son with a brusque question about why he had broken the order forbidding the children to visit unbidden. The younger man, tremulous, explained that a very young man arrested that afternoon for robbing a friend had claimed to be Granville’s son, and that the man’s name was Charles Crochard.

Granville waved the name away at first, then, after a pause, turned mild and fatherly. He admitted Charles had spoken the truth. The revelation transformed his tone; he was glad his son Eugène had come. He handed Eugène a considerable bundle of banknotes and entrusted him with discretion and whatever arrangements were needed. He revealed that he might be leaving France: he would ask the King the next day to dismiss him from his post and was going to Italy.

Granville then gave paternal counsel. He said a father owes his children not an account of his life but the experience Fate sells dearly; that he must warn them before marriage, the most momentous act demanded by society. He urged Eugène to study the character of any woman he might marry and to consult him; lack of union between husband and wife inevitably leads to misfortune and punishment, he insisted. He promised to write about such matters from Florence and closed with a final, tender gesture: asking to be kissed by his son and saying farewell, as if they might part for the last time.

Throughout, Granville embodied a mingling of hauteur and vulnerability: bitterly disillusioned, capable of cruelty to others and himself, yet capable too of an involuntary tenderness when confronted with the possibility of filial connection. Bianchon’s tale of Caroline Crochard had momentarily roused in him the very feeling he condemned. His reaction—refusing help yet secretly moved enough to waste money on a night scavenger’s riot—revealed both the depth of his despair and the theatricality of his attempts to master emotion. His final exchange with Eugène suggested that despite his proclamations of indifference, he still wished to transmit what he had learned and to salvage some dignity and guidance in his declining years.
