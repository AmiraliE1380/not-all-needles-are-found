DEDICATION

To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy.

My dear Nodier, here is a book of deeds concealed from legal action behind the closed doors of domestic life; in these matters the finger of God, often called chance, stands in for human justice, and the moral, though sometimes ridiculed, remains striking and instructive.

These deeds teach much about Family and Maternity. We may yet understand, perhaps too late, the effects of the diminution of paternal authority. That authority, which formerly ceased only at the father’s death, was once the sole human tribunal before which domestic crimes could be arraigned; kings sometimes aided its execution. However good and tender a mother may be, she cannot perform the patriarchal function any more than a woman can take a king’s place on the throne. Here I have perhaps more plainly shown how essential to European society is the indissoluble marriage bond, how fatal feminine weakness can be, and how dangerous selfish interests are when indulged without restraint. May a society that worships wealth shudder to see the impotence of the law in dealing with a system that deifies success and forgives every means of attaining it. May it return to the Catholic religion for purification of its masses and an education other than that of a lay university.

In the “Scenes from Military Life” many noble self-devotions will be set forth; allow me to point out the depraving effect of war’s necessities on minds who then act at home as if on the battlefield.

You have cast a sagacious glance over our time; your philosophy shines in many bitter reflections. You have better than most perceived the havoc wrought in our minds by the existence of four distinct political systems. I cannot more fitly dedicate this history than under your protection. Your name may defend my work against the criticisms sure to follow it—for where is the patient who keeps silence when the surgeon lifts the dressing from his wound?

To the pleasure of dedicating this Scene to you I add the pride of making known your friendship for one who here subscribes himself

Your sincere admirer,
De Balzac
Paris, November, 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792 the people of Issoudun had a physician named Rouget, whom they held to be consummately malignant. Rumor said he made his wife miserable, though she was the neighborhood beauty. Whether she was silly or not, friends and enemies never laid bare that household. The doctor was of the “not pleasant to deal with” sort, and townsmen treated him civilly. His wife, a demoiselle Descoings, weak in health as a girl (said to be why the doctor married her), bore a son and, ten years later, a daughter, Agathe, whose birth surprised even the doctor.

These facts must be told, for without them a man like Rouget would appear a monster, when in fact he followed the evil tendencies many shelter under the axiom that “men should have strength of character,” a phrase that has caused much female misery.

The elder Descoings, father-in-law and mother-in-law of the doctor, were commission merchants in the wool trade, buying for manufacturers and selling for producers, pocketing commission on both sides, and growing rich and miserly. Their son Descoings disliked Issoudun, went to Paris, set up as a grocer in the rue Saint-Honoré, and there, by love and patience and a little money from his parents, married the widow of his predecessor, Monsieur Bixiou.

In 1792 Descoings seemed to prosper. The old Descoings had retired and invested in buying forfeited estates. Doctor Rouget, foreseeing his wife’s death, sent Agathe to Paris to his brother-in-law both to let her see the capital and to carry out his scheme: he judged that his brother-in-law and sister, childless and older, might grow fond of the child. Agathe, the handsomest maiden of Issoudun, did not resemble either parent. Her birth had caused a breach between Doctor Rouget and his friend Monsieur Lousteau, a former sub-delegate who left town when sub-delegates were suppressed. Madame Rouget spent much time with Madame Hochon, Lousteau’s sister and Agathe’s godmother, to whom she confided her griefs; Madame Hochon later told most of Issoudun what it knew of Madame Rouget.

When Rouget told his wife he would send Agathe to Paris her first words were, “I shall never see my daughter again.” Madame Hochon thought she was right. The poor mother grew yellow with grief; tongues said the doctor was killing her by inches. The behavior of her foolish son Jean-Jacques, who owed her little attention or respect, added to her misery. Jean-Jacques resembled his father in the worst ways; the doctor himself was unsatisfactory at best.

Agathe’s arrival did not bring happiness to her uncle. In the same period Descoings was imprisoned on a hint from Robespierre. He had expressed doubts of the famine to customers in his shop, and citoyenne Duplay, Robespierre’s housekeeper, took offense and denounced him for “accaparation.” His wife’s ill-judged efforts to obtain his release only made matters worse. She knew Bridau, secretary to Roland, and put him on the war-path. That incorruptible official sought to enlighten the arbiters, but they only replied, “Why do you meddle?” Bridau advised patience. Madame Descoings fretted and even appealed to a member of the Convention, who promised to speak to Robespierre and then forgot. A little sugar or liqueur to citoyenne Duplay would likely have saved Descoings. The lesson of those days is that it is as dangerous to employ honest men as scoundrels; one must rely on oneself. Descoings perished on the scaffold with André Chénier. His death produced more sensation then than Chénier’s; it has taken thirty years to prove France lost more by Chénier’s death than by Descoings’s.

That neighborhood shop, so near Robespierre’s lodging, seemed cursed: César Birotteau later failed there as well. During the visits Bridau paid the widow, he was struck by Agathe’s cold, calm, innocent beauty and married her with the consent of her father. Doctor Rouget, eager to secure his design, hurried to Paris to draw the marriage contract to his advantage. Bridau’s ardent and disinterested love gave carte blanche to the perfidious doctor, who made full use of his son-in-law’s blindness, as this history will show.

Madame Rouget, or rather the doctor, inherited the elder Descoings’ property; within two years both had died, and Rouget, after his wife’s death in 1799, acquired vineyards, farms, and iron-works. Jean-Jacques proved incapable of doing anything; the father destined him for the life of a proprietor and allowed him to grow up in wealth and silliness. After 1799 the cipherers of Issoudun put at least thirty thousand francs’ income to the doctor’s credit. From his wife’s death he led a debauched life, kept within his house; this man of “strength of character” died in 1805. Jean-Jacques remained a bachelor for certain reasons partly his father’s fault, as will appear.

It is well to ask the result of the secret vengeance the doctor took on a daughter he did not recognize as his own, though legitimately his. Agathe bore a strong likeness to the doctor’s mother; resemblances sometimes skip generations, as vexing to science as any other mystery of heredity. In turn, Agathe’s eldest child, who resembled her physically, possessed the moral qualities of his grandfather, Doctor Rouget.

Agathe’s beauty was of a pure, placid kind—an oval face, clear whiteness of complexion, golden hair, composed mouth, long lashes, and tender dark-blue eyes. Bridau’s portrait of her hangs in his atelier. She was the ideal provincial housekeeper, pious though not sanctimonious, with an education given by the Church. In ordinary terms she was an accomplished wife, yet her ignorance of life opened the way to misfortunes; the Roman epitaph “She did needlework and kept the house” fitted her.

Under the Consulate Bridau adored Napoleon and in 1804 became head of a department at the ministry of the interior with a salary of twelve thousand francs and handsome emoluments. From 1804 to 1808 the Bridaus lived in a handsome suite on the Quai Voltaire, near the ministry and the Tuileries. A cook and footman were their only servants. Agathe, early afoot, went to market with her cook and prepared breakfast; Bridau never went to the ministry before eleven. When together, she prepared his breakfast, watched him off at the window, cleared the table, dressed, played with the children, and later sat silent, knitting by his writing table when he worked late. Sometimes they attended the theatre or used a ministerial box; Agathe enjoyed the occasional restaurant as any provincial new to Paris. Bridau received friends Thursdays and gave an annual Shrove-Tuesday ball.

Their conjugal life had but three events: the birth of two children three years apart and Bridau’s death in 1808, killed by overwork just as Napoleon was about to appoint him director-general, count, and councillor of state. Napoleon, impressed by Bridau’s devotion, was about to reward him when the effort to finish a work before the Emperor’s departure caused an inflammatory fever that killed him. Touched by a devotion that received none of the brilliant recognitions given to soldiers, Napoleon planned an order to requite civil services, the Order of the Reunion, worn with a blue ribbon, though Providence thwarted that profanation. After Bridau’s death the Emperor inquired into his widow’s circumstances: both sons received scholarships at the Imperial Lyceum paid from the Emperor’s privy purse, and Madame Bridau was given a pension of four thousand francs.

From her marriage to her husband’s death Agathe had had no communication with Issoudun. Her mother died as she was about to bear her youngest son, and when her father died, the coronation of the Emperor kept Bridau busy and Agathe unwilling to leave him. Her brother Jean-Jacques had not written. Once a year she received a letter from her godmother Madame Hochon, to which she replied with commonplaces and paid little heed to the pious advice therein.

Some time before Doctor Rouget’s death Madame Hochon warned Agathe she would get nothing from her father’s estate unless she gave a power of attorney to Monsieur Hochon. Agathe was reluctant to harass her brother. Whether because Bridau thought the spoliation legal or because he shared his wife’s magnanimity, he would not listen to Roguin, his notary, who urged him to use his ministerial position to contest the deeds by which the father had deprived his daughter. Thus husband and wife tacitly sanctioned the transfer. Roguin, however, forced Bridau to reflect that if he died first Agathe would be left without property. Reviewing their finances, Bridau found that between 1793 and 1805 they had used nearly thirty thousand of the fifty thousand francs that old Rouget had given to Agathe at her marriage. He invested the remaining twenty thousand in the public funds then quoted at forty, from which Agathe received about two thousand francs a year. As a widow, she could live suitably on an income of six thousand francs. With provincial good sense she planned to change residence, dismiss the footman, and keep only a cook; but her intimate friend Madame Descoings, insisting on being considered her aunt, sold her own establishment and came to live with Agathe, taking Bridau’s study as her bedroom.

The two widows pooled their revenues, possessing jointly about twelve thousand francs a year. Such simple, “natural” measures often deserve attention, for fools excuse themselves by calling wrongs natural. Behold the serpent amid flowers.

In 1809 Madame Descoings, who never told her age, was sixty-five. Once called a beauty, she had kept her looks by constitution; medium height, plump, fresh, with fine shoulders and a rather rosy complexion, her blond hair showed no gray despite her husband’s fate. She loved good cheer, the theatre, and a secret vice—lottery shares. Like women lucky enough to stay young, she spent on dress and was the pleasantest of companions, never opposing anyone and giving and taking a jest. If she did not marry again, it was no doubt the times: during the Empire marrying men found young women too easily.

Madame Descoings often cheered Madame Bridau with theatre and drives, prepared excellent dinners, and even tried to marry her to her own son by her first husband, Bixiou. To do this she revealed a family secret carefully kept by her, her late husband, and their notary: her son Bixiou, thirty-five and a widowed major who later perished at Lützen, had a son—a grandson she secretly saw and claimed as the child of her first wife. That boy, educated with Agathe’s sons at the Imperial Lyceum on a half-scholarship, proved clever, witty, and gifted as a draughtsman and designer.

Agathe, who lived only for her children, declined to remarry out of fidelity to her husband and good sense. But being a good mother is harder than being a good wife; a widow must both love and command her children. Few women are firm enough to perform that double duty. Thus Agathe, despite many virtues, was the innocent cause of grave unhappiness. Through her lack of intelligence and blind confidence, she fell victim to Madame Descoings, who nursed a combination of three numbers in a lottery and used the joint household money to pay stakes, hoping at last to win and enrich her grandson, Agathe, and the little Bridaus.

When the debts reached ten thousand francs she increased her stakes; failing to win, the debt rolled up. At twenty thousand francs she lost her head, still hoping for a trey. She attempted to mortgage her property to help, but Roguin, the notary, showed that the late Doctor Rouget had secured her a life-interest only by disinheriting Madame Descoings in favor of his son Jean-Jacques. No money-lender would advance twenty thousand francs to a sixty-six-year-old on an annuity of about four thousand when ten percent could be obtained elsewhere. One morning Madame Descoings fell at Agathe’s feet and, sobbing, confessed the state of things. Agathe reproached her not; she dismissed the footman and cook, sold all but the bare necessities of her furniture, sold three-fourths of her government funds, paid the debts, and bade farewell to her appartement.

One of the worst corners in Paris was the section of the rue Mazarin between the rue Guenegard and the rue de Seine, behind the Institute. The high gray walls of the college and Cardinal Mazarin’s library cast chill shadows; the sun seldom shone and the north wind blew. The poor ruined widow, Madame Bridau, took a third-floor lodging in a shallow house at that damp, dark corner. Opposite were the Institute buildings with the studios of young artists—rapins—then still there. From Madame Bridau’s windows one could peer into those melancholy barred cages. Looking north the view closed on the dome of the Institute; up the street the only movement was a file of hackney-coaches. For a while she planted boxes of earth at the windows, cultivating illicit aerial gardens whose vegetables improved the air. The staircase wound round upon itself; the third floor was the last. Three windows opened to three rooms—dining-room, a small salon, and a chamber on one side of the landing; on the other a little kitchen and two single rooms; above, an immense garret without partitions. She chose this lodging because it cost only four hundred francs a year on a nine-year lease, because it was near her sons’ school, and because she was used to the quarter.

The interior matched the outside. The dining-room, hung with yellow paper patterned with small green flowers and floored with unglazed tiles, contained only a table, two sideboards, and six chairs brought from the other apartment. The salon had an Aubusson carpet given to Bridau when the ministry was refurnished, and a common mahogany sofa with Egyptian heads covered in green silk. Over it hung an amateur pastel portrait of Bridau that at once drew the eye: the noble, obscure brow, serene yet proud eyes, prudent lips, and frank smile suggested a man of duty—“Justum et tenacem,” as the Emperor had said. On the opposite wall, over a card-table, flashed a picture of the Emperor in brilliant colors by Vernet, Napoleon riding rapidly, attended by his escort.

Agathe had given herself two large birdcages—canaries and Java sparrows—and adopted this childish fancy since her husband’s loss. After three months the widowed chamber had become a litter: cats domiciled on the sofa; canaries, occasionally let loose, left droppings on the furniture. She scattered millet and chickweed and put tidbits for the cats in broken saucers. Garments lay everywhere. Everything of Bridau’s was scrupulously preserved: his desk implements were kept with the care one might give a knight’s armor; she had wrapped and sealed the last pen he used and marked it so. The cup from which he drank his last draught stood on the fireplace. After his death no trace of coquetry or ordinary self-care remained in the young widow of thirty-five. Parted from the only man she had known and loved—one who had never caused her unhappiness—she seemed to have renounced her womanhood. Some people, through love, transfer the self to another; when that other dies, no life of their own remains.

Agathe now lived only for her children and was infinitely sad at the privations their ruin would bring. From the move to the rue Mazarin a melancholy took her face. She hoped a little in the Emperor; he could do no more than he already was doing—giving three hundred francs a year to each child from his privy purse, besides scholarships.

The brilliant Descoings occupied an apartment on the second floor, similar to her niece’s above. She had assigned Madame Bridau three thousand francs out of her annuity; Roguin, the notary, arranged it, but it would take seven years of slow repayment to make good the loss. The Descoingses, reduced to an income of twelve hundred francs, lived modestly with her niece. They employed a woman-of-all-work for mornings only; Madame Descoings liked to cook. In evenings a few old friends—persons at the ministry who owed their places to Bridau—came for cards with the two widows. Madame Descoings still cherished her trey, declaring it obstinate about turning up; she hoped by one stroke to repay the loan she’d made upon her niece. She was dear to the Bridaus partly from a feeling of wrong she had done them and partly because her niece never reproached her under privation. So Philippe and Joseph were cosseted; the old gambler of the Imperial Lottery cooked them nice dinners with sweets. Later, they could extract small sums from her—one for pencils and paper, the elder for tennis-shoes and marbles. Madame Descoings’s passion forced her to be content with fifty francs a month for domestic expenses so she could gamble with the rest.

Madame Bridau, by motherly love, kept her expenses to the same sum. As penance for former overconfidence she cut off little enjoyments. Timid and distrusting, she exaggerated risk into virtue. She feared the Emperor might die or forget them; she shuddered at the risk her children ran of being left without means. Unable to understand Roguin’s assurance that in seven years Descoings’s assignment would restore the sold sum, she trusted neither notary, aunt, nor government; she trusted only herself and practiced privation. By laying aside three thousand francs every year from her pension she fancied she would have thirty thousand in ten years, yielding fifteen hundred a year to her children. At thirty-six she might live twenty years more; if she kept to the plan she could leave each child enough for bare necessities.

Thus the two widows passed from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty—one through a vice, the other through the purest virtue. These petty details matter: the view into student dens below, the tumult of the rapins, the need to look up at the sky to escape the damp street, the presence of that portrait full of soul, the sight of rich old colors in the calm home, the mother’s preference for her elder child and opposition to the younger’s tastes—these facts prefaced this history and perhaps explain Joseph Bridau, one of the greatest painters of the modern French school.

Philippe, the elder, strikingly resembled his mother. Blond and blue-eyed, he had a daring look taken for intrepidity and courage. Old Claparon, who had entered the ministry with Bridau and was one of the whist players, tapped Philippe’s cheek and prophesied he’d “stand to his guns.” Stimulated, the boy assumed a resolute manner, became adroit at bodily exercises; fights at the Lyceum taught endurance and contempt for pain, laying the foundation of military valor. He acquired a distaste for study; public education failed to develop mind and body together.

Agathe believed physical resemblance implied moral likeness; she confidently expected delicacy of feeling combined with manly vigor. Philippe was fifteen when the family moved to rue Mazarin; his winning ways confirmed maternal belief. Joseph, three years younger, resembled his father but only in defective ways. His thick black hair was always disorderly; Philippe’s was neat. By some fatality Joseph could not keep his clothes clean; dress him in new clothes and he made them look old. The elder took care of his things out of vanity. Unconsciously Agathe scolded Joseph and held up his brother as example; she never treated them alike. Fetching them from school she worried especially about Joseph. Such trifles drove her into maternal preference.

No one among the ordinary circle—Du Bruel, Claparon, Desroches, or even the Abbé Loraux—noticed Joseph’s faculty for observation. Absorbed in his own tastes, the future colorist paid no attention to himself; during childhood this torpor alarmed his father. His face, which later gained originality, wore a sullen expression. The features were pinched, contracted by the close attention Joseph paid to his surroundings. Philippe flattered his mother with clever sayings and lively answers, prompting hope. Joseph was taciturn, a dreamer. The mother hoped great things of Philippe and expected nothing of Joseph.

Joseph’s leaning to art began simply. During Easter holidays of 1812, returning from a walk in the Tuileries with his brother and Madame Descoings, he saw a pupil drawing a caricature of a professor on the wall of the Institute and stopped in admiration. The next day he stood at the window watching pupils enter by the rue Mazarin door; then slipped into the long courtyard with statues, busts, half-finished marbles, plasters, and baked clays; he gazed feverishly—his instinct awakened, his vocation stirred. He entered a ground-floor room with a half-open door where a dozen young men drew from a statue; they began to make fun of him.

“Hi! little one,” cried the first, scattering crumbs.

They mocked his ugliness. For a quarter hour Joseph bore the teasing in Chaudet’s atelier. But his persistency and expression struck the pupils; when asked what he wanted, Joseph said he wished to learn to draw. They encouraged him. Won by friendliness, he said he was Madame Bridau’s son.

“Oh! if you are Madame Bridau’s son,” they cried, “you will certainly be a great man!” They teased and tested him—one pupil set up a “telegraph trial,” stretching Joseph’s arms and daring him to hold them so. Joseph, at thirteen, stood motionless for five minutes amid shouts; at ten minutes sweat stood on his brow. Then a bald-headed, pale, sickly man entered—Chaudet. He lowered Joseph’s arms, patted him, and asked what brought him. Hearing Joseph wanted to be an artist and was Madame Bridau’s son, Chaudet, struck by recollection of Bridau’s service, welcomed him. He gave him a board, paper, and chalks, sent a pupil for cakes and sugar-plums, and petted the child. The scene—rough, warm-hearted, and real—impressed Joseph. Chaudet, for whom the Emperor’s protection earlier opened prospects later cut short by premature death, appeared like a vision. Joseph told his mother nothing, but he spent two hours every Sunday and Thursday in Chaudet’s atelier. Madame Descoings, who humored the boy’s fancies, supplied pencils, red chalks, prints, and paper. At school he sketched masters, drew comrades, and showed surprising assiduity in drawing-class. Lemire, his drawing-master, reported Joseph’s faculty to Madame Bridau. She, like a true provincial with little knowledge of art, was terrified. When Lemire left, she wept.

“What is to become of me!” she cried to Madame Descoings. “Joseph, whom I meant to make a government clerk—protected by his father’s memory at the ministry—wants to be a painter, a vagabond! I always knew that child would give me nothing but trouble.”

Madame Descoings confessed she had encouraged Joseph’s passion, taking him to the Salon where his interest in pictures was, she declared, miraculous.

“If he understands painting at thirteen,” she said, “your Joseph will be a man of genius.”

“Yes; and see what genius did for his father—killed him with overwork at forty!” Agathe answered.

As Joseph entered his fourteenth year, Agathe, against Madame Descoings’s entreaties, went to Chaudet and requested he cease to “debauch” her son. She found the sculptor in a blue smock modeling his last statue, working with spasmodic jerks born of passion. He received the widow roughly at first but, moved by Bridau’s memory and by devotion to craftsmanship, he burst into holy wrath at her plea. He declared that a real vocation was a call, that an artist was like a king in his realm, happier and independent, and that to force Joseph into a clerk’s life would be to murder him. He dismissed the work for the day and refused to give up the boy’s training.

Agathe, alarmed by the sight of a nude woman in a corner, left horrified. Chaudet told his pupils she was not to bring the little Bridau any more; they jeered as she closed the door. When the students learned she opposed the Institute, their happiness turned to the project of getting Joseph among them. He often slipped into Regnauld the painter’s studio where he was encouraged to paint. When the widow complained the bargain was not kept, Chaudet’s pupils insisted they were not Chaudet and composed a long mocking song about her.

That evening Agathe refused cards and sat drowned in tears.

“What is the matter, Madame Bridau?” asked old Claparon.

“She thinks her boy will have to beg his bread because he has the bump of painting,” Madame Descoings said. The men offered their usual advice: let the boy follow his bent; if not a genius, he could later be placed in an office. Desroches spoke of keeping a son hard at work to make a man of him; Du Bruel warned against letting a boy choose a profession at his age. Claparon and others counselled resignation to Joseph’s tastes. Their unanimity only increased Agathe’s anxieties. She feared the atelier’s naked women; Madame Descoings joked about “good fires.”

Soon after, the disasters of the retreat from Moscow became known; Napoleon returned to Paris to organize troops. Philippe, tired of school, wanted to serve the Emperor; he saw a review at the Tuileries—the last Napoleon ever held—and became infatuated with soldier’s life. Military splendor and uniforms seduced him. Without his mother’s knowledge he wrote a petition to the Emperor asking to enter the army. Within twenty-four hours the Emperor sent him to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr; six months later, in November 1813, he was appointed sub-lieutenant in a cavalry regiment. He spent that winter in cantonments, learned to ride, and was dispatched to the front. During the campaign in France he was made lieutenant after an outpost affair where his bravery saved his colonel’s life. The Emperor named him captain at La Fere-Champenoise and took him on his staff. Inspired, Philippe won the cross at Montereau, witnessed Napoleon’s farewell at Fontainebleau, refused to serve the Bourbons, and returned in July 1814 to find his mother ruined.

Joseph’s scholarship was withdrawn after the holidays; Madame Bridau vainly entreated that it be inscribed on the ministry rolls. Joseph, more devoted than ever to painting, begged to go to Regnauld and promised to earn his living, saying he was advanced enough in the second class to do without rhetoric. Philippe, a captain at nineteen and decorated, who had served the Emperor as aide-de-camp in two battles, flattered the mother immensely. Coarse and blustering, without merit beyond cavalry bravery, he was to her a man of genius; whereas Joseph, puny, sickly, unkempt, and dreaming an artist’s glory, would only worry her.

The winter of 1814–1815 favored Joseph. Secretly encouraged by Madame Descoings and Bixiou, a pupil of Gros, he worked in that celebrated atelier, forming an intimacy with Schinner. The return from Elba came; Captain Bridau joined the Emperor at Lyons, accompanied him to the Tuileries, and commanded a squadron in the Guard’s dragoons. After Waterloo—where he was slightly wounded and took the officer’s Legion of Honor—he was near Marshal Davoust at Saint-Denis and, through Davoust’s intercession, retained his cross and rank though placed on half-pay.

Joseph, anxious about his future, studied with ardor through these turbulent times, sometimes to the point of illness.

“It is the smell of the paints,” Agathe said to Madame Descoings. “He ought to give up a business so injurious to his health.”

Agathe’s anxieties remained for her lieutenant-colonel son. When he returned in 1816 reduced from a nine-thousand-franc commander’s salary to half-pay, she fitted attic rooms for him and spent her savings. Philippe belonged to the faithful Bonapartes of café Lemblin; he acquired the habits and airs of a half-pay officer, exaggerated them, spurned the Bourbons, and refused infantry opportunities. To his mother he seemed noble. “The father himself could have done no more,” she said.

Philippe’s half-pay sufficed; he cost little at home, whereas Joseph’s expenses were paid by the widows. From then Agathe’s preference for Philippe was openly shown. Up to that time it had been secret; but the colonel’s persecution for the Emperor and his wound drew forth maternal tenderness. “He is unfortunate” explained everything to her. Joseph, simple in the artist’s way and brought up to admire his big brother, was not hurt by maternal preference and shared his worship of the hero who had carried Napoleon’s orders. How could he doubt the superiority of the brother he had seen in green and gold, commanding on the Champ de Mars?

Agathe, notwithstanding preference, was an excellent mother. She loved Joseph, though she could not understand him. Joseph adored his mother; Philippe let her adore him. Toward her the dragoon softened his military roughness yet never concealed contempt for Joseph—expressed in a patronizing, almost friendly way. He nicknamed the weak, shrunken seventeen-year-old “Cub.” Philippe’s patronizing manners would have wounded anyone less indifferent than Joseph, who believed soldiers hid goodness beneath brutal exteriors. Joseph did not yet know that soldiers of genius can be gentle and courteous; all genius is alike.

“Poor boy!” said Philippe to his mother, “we mustn’t plague him; let him do as he likes.” To Agathe, the colonel’s contempt seemed fraternal affection. “Philippe will always love and protect his brother,” she thought.

In 1816 Joseph obtained his mother’s permission to convert the garret next to his attic into an atelier; Madame Descoings contributed some money for his needs. The two widows treated painting as a trade, but Joseph arranged the humble studio himself: Madame Descoings persuaded the owner to add a skylight; Joseph painted the hall chocolate and hung sketches. Agathe reluctantly installed an iron stove so her son could work at home without abandoning Gros’s or Schinner’s studios.

The constitutional party, backed by half-pay officers and Bonapartists, stirred emeutes around the Chamber of Deputies; conspiracies were rumored. Philippe, flirting with these affairs, was arrested and released for lack of proof, though the minister of war restored him to the active list, ending his half-pay. France seemed unsafe; Agathe feared provocateurs. Philippe spent time in disaffected cafés, drinking and acquiring bad habits, while Agathe lived in mortal dread. The Grecian sages still visited Madame Bridau nightly; Claparon, retained at the ministry though cautious, whispered Moniteur news. Desroches, retired after old Du Bruel, still fought for his pension. Their friends urged Agathe to send Philippe abroad.

“They talk of conspiracies, and your son will be certain to fall a victim,” they warned.

“Philippe is cut from the wood the Emperor made into marshals,” Du Bruel replied; “he mustn’t give up his profession. Let him serve in the East, in India—”

“Think of his health,” Agathe answered.

“Why doesn’t he get some place, some business?” Desroches asked. “There are private offices to be had.” Agathe insisted Philippe was a soldier and wouldn’t want anything else. Du Bruel suggested royal favor could make him lieutenant-colonel, though Madame Descoings doubted his prospects without noble birth.

To please his mother, Philippe took up the notion of joining General Lallemand in the United States and helping found the Champ d’Asile, one of the most disastrous swindles carried off under the name of a national subscription. Agathe gave ten thousand francs and saw him off at Havre. By the end of 1817 she lived on the six hundred francs left from her funds and, by prudent investment, secured seven percent on some savings. Joseph emulated her frugality: dressed plainly, denied himself comforts, and lived on bread, milk, and Brie; only Madame Descoings and Bixiou showed sympathy.

Joseph later recalled how the coming summer saved him the cost of charcoal. Now a colorist as capable as Gros, he consulted his master only occasionally. He was already planning a revolt against classical traditions, aiming to free art to represent Nature — a struggle that began with his first Salon exhibition and never ceased.

1818 was terrible for the family. Roguin, Madame Descoings’s notary, absconded with seven years’ withheld annuity funds. Three days later a bill of exchange for a thousand francs arrived from New York: Philippe had lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. The letter told of debts contracted and comrades who had indorsed for him. Agathe, blaming herself, wept. The two women faced selling silver, linen, and furniture. Joseph, alarmed, told Gérard, who secured an order for two royal copies of Louis XVIII’s portrait at five hundred francs each and supplied Joseph with materials. Joseph painted four panels in ten days, sold them, and brought his mother the thousand francs to meet Philippe’s draft. Then came a letter announcing Philippe’s imminent return on a packet whose captain had trusted him for passage; he would need a thousand francs at Havre.

Joseph promised his mother the money after finishing the copies. Agathe, ecstatically grateful, kissed him and called him “his father all over.” Madame Bridau rushed to Havre and watched beyond the round tower daily for the packet. The sight of home softened Philippe, but his misfortunes in Texas and New York had developed his worst traits: he had grown brutal, selfish, rude, drank and smoked excessively, and imagined perpetual persecution. Such men either live by honest virtues or by treating every want as a necessity; Philippe had drifted into the latter, becoming cunning and self-centered while keeping an appearance of frank soldierly simplicity. His stature had grown somewhat rotund, his face bronzed, his speech terse and decisive; exposed to hardship, he had become the kind of man who makes himself feared. To his mother he remained a hero; in truth he had become, bluntly, a blackguard.

At Havre Agathe bought him clothes and could not, at first, restrain his indulgence. The scheme of conquering Texas with imperial remnants had failed less for lack of idea than for treachery and poor management; the Liberals had used the enterprise selfishly, abandoning the devoted heroes. Agathe, a household manager with shrewd common sense, saw through the political chicane and recognized the subscription’s failure; she could not make her son see he had been duped rather than persecuted. During the costly week at Havre she could not persuade him to make terms with the royal government. She brought him back to Paris before her funds gave out.

On arrival, those awaiting them noted how much Agathe had aged. Philippe greeted Madame Descoings coolly. Joseph, now twenty-one and confident, was hurt by his brother’s contempt. At a family dinner the friends — Du Bruel, Claparon, Desroches, and the Abbe Loraux — observed the exile’s roughened manners and coarse voice. While Joseph prepared the card tables, the intimate friends asked Agathe what she intended for Philippe. She could not get him to serve the Bourbons; Du Bruel suggested other roles like writing plays, as his son had once done. Philippe, Bixiou, and young Desroches played écarté. Madame Descoings, eager to celebrate the prodigal’s return, provided funds for play; Joseph secretly slipped twenty francs to Philippe.

The evening grew lively. Philippe, a poor player, ended owing fifty francs. He showed bad temper and quarreled with young Desroches and then with Desroches senior, who forbade further play though his son was right. Madame Descoings silenced her grandson’s barbed witticisms for fear of provoking the colonel. Philippe’s temper and gambling continued into the night.

The next morning the two widows, counting resources, resolved to economize: Madame Descoings proposed selling furniture and giving up apartments to save seven hundred francs a year and pay Philippe fifty francs monthly until he found something. They pooled incomes — about five thousand three hundred francs, four thousand from Madame Descoings as a life annuity — and had consumed their savings. Philippe promised to find a situation and asked only for board and lodging. Agathe kissed him; Madame Descoings slipped him a hundred francs to cover his losses.

Within ten days the furniture was sold and the household rearranged. Philippe’s habits hardened: he left after breakfast, returned at dinner, and spent evenings in cafés drinking brandy, reading newspapers, playing billiards at the Café Minerve, smoking many cigars, and gambling at night. He boasted of a regular life and, while often winning, returned home talking of smashing the ultras and the Bourbon bodyguard.

He dined often with army comrades who spoke freely of hopes based on plots and inventions like a submarine to restore the Emperor. He particularly liked an old dragoons’ captain, Giroudeau, whose company led Philippe into further dissipation and into seeing women as resources. Giroudeau, a bluff old roue, introduced Philippe to the theatre and to a young dancer, Florentine, and to another, Marie Godeschal — Mariette — who was beautiful, cold, and ambitious. Mariette, orphaned and poor, had a devoted brother who clerked in Derville’s law office; she resolved to use her beauty and talent to help him. Poorly dressed yet striking, she provoked Philippe’s passion. To Mariette, Philippe was a Guard staff-officer, and a liaison with him would elevate her above rivals. Florentine and Giroudeau encouraged a kind of morganatic connection between Philippe and Mariette.

Giroudeau promised to speak to his nephew Finot at a theatrical journal and to get Philippe a place on a newspaper. Philippe revealed his poverty, but Giroudeau reassured him: his nephew could put him on a paper or help him get patronage. Mariette, thinking Philippe would write for a newspaper and help her career, accepted him.

The next day Philippe met Giroudeau at the subscription office where the veteran lived in a cramped room labeled “Subscription Office.” There they encountered Coloquinte, a one-armed man, and then climbed to Giroudeau’s nephew Finot’s attic, where Finot, cool and sardonic, eyed Philippe. Giroudeau praised the colonel; Finot mocked the Texan affair a little, then gave Philippe a plan: exploit his position as a victim of the Liberals and demand an account of the Champ d’Asile subscription. A new Opposition journal was starting; Finot could make Philippe its cashier with a salary of three thousand francs if Philippe found someone to go security for twenty thousand francs. “Threaten loudly,” Finot advised, “and we’ll silence you with a place.” Philippe was grateful and impressed by Finot’s worldly cleverness.

That night at cafés Philippe fulminated against the Liberals, declaring he would demand an accounting of the moneys collected. He went to Mariette to tell her of the expected post on a newspaper with ten thousand subscribers and how he would advance her claims.

Agathe and Madame Descoings waited anxiously for Philippe one night amid the political alarm following the assassination of the Duc de Berry. He returned with good news: he had been promised the cashiership of a new journal at three thousand francs on condition they could go security for twenty thousand francs by depositing their shares in the Funds as guarantee while drawing the interest. The friends urged Agathe to go security; she and Madame Descoings, overjoyed at the prospect of providing for Philippe, transferred her shares — representing thirteen hundred francs in interest — to secure him. Philippe promised to pay one hundred francs monthly for board and lodging and was acclaimed the best of sons. Those who had doubted him congratulated Agathe and admitted they had been unjust. Joseph, not to be outdone, resolved to pay for his own support and succeeded.

Three months later the colonel, who ate and drank enough for four, found fault with the food and forced the poor widows, on the ground of his pay, to spend much on their table, though he had not yet paid a penny. His mother and Madame Descoings, out of delicacy, would not remind him of his promise. The year passed without one of those coins Leon Gozlan so vigorously called “tigers with five claws” finding its way from Philippe’s pocket to the household purse. He quieted his conscience by seldom dining at home.

“Well, he is happy,” said his mother; “he is easy in mind; he has a place.”

Through the influence of a feuilleton edited by Vernou, a friend of Bixiou, Finot, and Giroudeau, Mariette appeared not at the Panorama-Dramatique but at the Porte-Saint-Martin, where she triumphed beside the famous Begrand. Among the directors was a rich general in love with an actress, for whose sake he had become an impresario. This officer knew Philippe and Giroudeau. Mariette’s first appearance, heralded by Finot’s journal and by Philippe, was promptly arranged by the three officers; there seems to be solidarity among the passions in folly.

The mischievous Bixiou soon revealed to his grandmother and the devoted Agathe that Philippe, the cashier, the hero of heroes, was in love with Mariette, the celebrated ballet-dancer. The news was a thunder-clap to the two widows; Agathe’s religious principles taught her that women on the stage were brands in the burning; she and Madame Descoings thought such women dined off gold, drank pearls, and wasted fortunes.

“Now do you suppose,” said Joseph to his mother, “that my brother is such a fool as to spend his money on Mariette? Such women only ruin rich men.”

“They talk of engaging Mariette at the Opera,” said Bixiou. “Don’t be worried, Madame Bridau; the diplomatic body often comes to the Porte-Saint-Martin, and that handsome girl won’t stay long with your son. I heard an ambassador was madly in love with her. By the bye, another piece of news! Old Claparon is dead, and his son, who has become a banker, has ordered the cheapest kind of funeral for him. That fellow has no education; they wouldn’t behave like that in China.”

Philippe, prompted by mercenary motives, proposed marriage to Mariette; but she, knowing herself on the eve of an engagement at the Grand Opera, refused, either because she guessed the colonel’s motive or because she prized independence for her fortune. For the remainder of the year Philippe came home no more than twice a month. Where was he? Either at his office, the theatre, or with Mariette. No light as to his conduct reached the household of the rue Mazarin. Giroudeau, Finot, Bixiou, Vernou, Lousteau saw him leading a life of pleasure. Philippe shared the gay amusements of Tullia, a leading singer at the Opera, of Florentine, who took Mariette’s place at the Porte-Saint-Martin, of Florine and Matifat, Coralie and Camusot. After four o’clock, when he left his office, until midnight, he amused himself; some party had usually been arranged the night before—a good dinner, a card-party, a supper by some one of the set. Philippe was in his element.

This carnival, which lasted eighteen months, was not altogether without troubles. The beautiful Mariette no sooner appeared at the Opera in January, 1821, than she captured one of the most distinguished dukes of Louis XVIII’s court. Philippe tried to make head against the peer, and by April, compelled by his passion and despite some luck at cards, he dipped into the funds he was cashier of. By May he had taken eleven hundred francs. In that fatal month Mariette started for London to see what could be done with the lords while the temporary opera house in the Hotel Choiseul, rue Lepelletier, was being prepared. The luckless Philippe had ended, as often happens, in loving Mariette despite her infidelities; she herself had never thought him anything but a dull-minded, brutal soldier, the first rung of a ladder she had never intended to remain on. Foreseeing the time when Philippe would have spent all his money, she captured other journalistic support which released her from depending on him; nevertheless, she felt the peculiar gratitude that that class of women acknowledge towards the first man who smooths their way among the difficulties and horrors of a theatrical career.

Forced to let his terrible mistress go to London without him, Philippe went into winter quarters—returned to his attic room in his mother’s apartment. He made gloomy reflections as he went to bed and when he rose. He was conscious of the inability to live otherwise than he had for the last year. The luxury that surrounded Mariette—the dinners, the suppers, the evenings in the side-scenes, the animation of wits and journalists, the racket around him, the delights that tickled his senses and vanity—such a life, found only in Paris, offering daily the charm of something new, was now more than habit—it had become to Philippe as necessary as his tobacco or brandy. He saw plainly that he could not live without these continual enjoyments. The idea of suicide came into his head; not on account of the deficit which must soon be discovered, but because he could no longer live without Mariette’s atmosphere of pleasure. Full of gloomy thoughts, he entered his brother’s painting-room for the first time, where he found Joseph in a blue blouse, copying a picture for a dealer.

“So that’s how pictures are made,” said Philippe, by way of opening.

“No,” said Joseph, “that is how they are copied.”

“How much do they pay you for that?”

“Eh! never enough; two hundred and fifty francs. But I study the manner of the masters and learn much; I found out the secrets of their method. There’s one of my own pictures,” he added, pointing to a sketch with colors still moist.

“How much do you pocket in a year?”

“Unfortunately, I am known only to painters. Schinner backs me; he has got me work at the Chateau de Presles, where I am going in October to do arabesques, panels, and other decorations, for which the Comte de Serizy, no doubt, will pay well. With such trifles and with orders from the dealers, I may manage to earn eighteen hundred to two thousand francs a year over and above expenses. I shall send that picture to the next exhibition; if it hits the public taste, my fortune is made. My friends think well of it.”

“I don’t know anything about such things,” said Philippe in a subdued voice, causing Joseph to look at him.

“What is the matter?” the artist asked, seeing Philippe very pale.

“I should like to know how long it would take you to paint my portrait.”

“If I worked steadily, and the weather were clear, I could finish in three or four days.”

“That’s too long; I have only one day. My poor mother loves me so I wished to leave her my likeness. We will say no more.”

“Why! are you going away again?”

“I am going never to return,” replied Philippe with forced gaiety.

“Look here, Philippe, what is the matter? If it is anything serious, I am a man and not a ninny. I am used to hard struggles, and if discretion is needed, I have it.”

“Are you sure?”

“On my honor.”

“You will tell no one, no matter who?”

“No one.”

“Well, I am going to blow my brains out.”

“You!—are you going to fight a duel?”

“I am going to kill myself.”

“Why?”

“I have taken eleven hundred francs from the funds in my hands; I must send in my accounts tomorrow. Half my security is lost; our poor mother will be reduced to six hundred francs a year. That would be nothing! I could make a fortune for her later; but I am dishonored! I cannot live under dishonor—”

“You will not be dishonored if it is paid back. To be sure, you will lose your place and only have the five hundred francs a year from your cross, but you can live on five hundred francs.”

“Farewell!” said Philippe, running downstairs and not waiting for another word.

Joseph left his studio and went down to breakfast with his mother; Philippe’s confession had taken away his appetite. He took Madame Descoings aside and told her the terrible news. The old woman made a frightened exclamation, let fall the saucepan of milk she held, and flung herself into a chair. Agathe rushed in; from one exclamation to another the mother gathered the fatal truth.

“He! to fail in honor! the son of Bridau to take the money that was trusted to him!”

The widow trembled; her eyes dilated and then grew fixed; she sat down and burst into tears.

“Where is he?” she cried amid sobs. “Perhaps he has flung himself into the Seine.”

“You must not give up hope,” said Madame Descoings, “because a poor lad has met a bad woman who led him to do wrong. Dear me! we see that every day. Philippe has had such misfortunes! he had so little chance to be happy and loved that we ought not to be surprised at his passion for that creature. All passions lead to excess. My own life is not without reproach of that kind, and yet I call myself an honest woman. A single fault is not vice; and after all, it is only those who do nothing that are never deceived.”

Agathe’s despair overcame her so much that Joseph and the Descoings had to lessen Philippe’s wrongdoing by assuring her such things happened in all families.

“But he is twenty-eight years old,” cried Agathe, “he is no longer a child.”

Terrible revelation of the poor woman’s inward thought about her son’s conduct.

“Mother, I assure you he thought only of your sufferings and of the wrong he had done you,” said Joseph.

“Oh, my God! let him come back to me, let him live, and I will forgive all,” cried the poor mother, who pictured Philippe dragged dead out of the river.

Gloomy silence reigned a short time. The day passed with cruel alternations of hope and fear; all three ran to the window at the least sound and gave way to every sort of conjecture. While the family grieved, Philippe was quietly getting matters in order at his office. He had the audacity to submit accounts with a statement that, fearing some accident, he had retained eleven hundred francs at his house for safe keeping. The scoundrel left the office at five, taking five hundred francs more from the desk, and coolly went to a gambling-house he had avoided since his paper connection, for a cashier must not be seen frequenting such places. The fellow was not wanting in acumen. His past conduct showed he derived more from his grandfather Rouget than from his virtuous sire Bridau. Perhaps he might have made a good general; but in private life he was one of those scoundrels who shelter their schemes behind a screen of strict legality and the privacy of the family roof.

At this conjuncture Philippe maintained his coolness. He won at first and gained as much as six thousand francs; but he let himself be dazzled by the idea of getting out of his difficulties at one stroke. He left the trente-et-quarante, hearing that the black had come up sixteen times at roulette, and was about to put five thousand francs on the red when the black came for the seventeenth time. The colonel then put a thousand francs on the black and won. In spite of this luck, his head grew weary; he felt it, though he continued to play. But that divining sense which leads a gambler comes in flashes and began failing him. Intermittent perceptions, fatal to gamblers, set in. Lucidity of mind, like the rays of the sun, can have no effect except by continuity; it can divine only if not breaking that line; the curvettings of chance bemuddle it. Philippe lost all. After such a strain, the careless mind as well as the bravest weakens. When Philippe went home that night he was not thinking of suicide; he had never really meant to kill himself; he no longer thought of his lost place, nor of the sacrificed security, nor of his mother, nor of Mariette, the cause of his ruin; he walked along mechanically. When he got home, his mother in tears, Madame Descoings and Joseph all fell on his neck and kissed him and brought him to a seat by the fire.

“Bless me!” thought he, “the threat has worked.”

The brute at once assumed an air suitable to the occasion; the more easily because his ill-luck at cards had deeply depressed him. Seeing her atrocious Benjamin so pale and woe-begone, the poor mother knelt beside him, kissed his hands, pressed them to her heart, and gazed at him for a long time with eyes swimming in tears.

“Philippe,” she said in a choking voice, “promise not to kill yourself, and all shall be forgotten.”

Philippe looked at his sorrowing brother and at Madame Descoings, whose eyes were full of tears, and thought, “They are good creatures.” Then he took his mother in his arms, raised her and put her on his knee, pressed her to his heart and whispered as he kissed her, “For the second time, you give me life.”

The Descoings managed to serve an excellent dinner and add two bottles of old wine with a little “liqueur des iles,” a treasure left from her former business.

“Agathe,” she said at dessert, “we must let him smoke his cigars,” and she offered some to Philippe.

These two poor creatures fancied that if they let him take his ease he would like his home and stay; both tried to endure his tobacco-smoke, though each loathed it. That sacrifice was not so much noticed by Philippe.

On the morrow Agathe looked ten years older. Her terrors calmed, reflection returned, and the poor woman had not closed an eye through that horrible night. She was now reduced to six hundred francs a year. Madame Descoings, like all fat women fond of good eating, was growing heavy; her step on the staircase sounded like chopping logs; she might die at any moment; with her life, four thousand francs would disappear. What folly to rely on that resource! What should she do? What would become of them? With her mind made up to become a sick-nurse rather than be supported by her children, Agathe did not think of herself. But Philippe—what would he do if reduced to live on the five hundred francs of a Legion of Honor officer? During the past eleven years Madame Descoings, by giving up three thousand francs a year, had paid her debt twice, yet she still sacrificed her grandson’s interests to the Bridau family. Though Agathe’s honorable feelings were shocked by this disaster, she said to herself: “Poor boy! is it his fault? He is faithful to his oath. I have done wrong not to marry him. If I had found him a wife, he would not have entangled with this danseuse. He has such a vigorous constitution—”

Madame Descoings had likewise reflected on saving the family honor. At daybreak she went to her friend’s room.

“Neither you nor Philippe should manage this delicate matter,” she urged. “Our two old friends Du Bruel and Claparon are dead, but we still have Desroches, who is very sagacious. I’ll go see him this morning. He can tell the newspaper people that Philippe trusted a friend and has been made a victim; that his weakness makes him unfit to be a cashier; what happened may happen again, and that Philippe prefers to resign. That will prevent his being turned off.”

Agathe, seeing this lie would save her son’s honor in others’ eyes, kissed Madame Descoings, who went out early to finish the dreadful affair.

Philippe, meanwhile, had slept the sleep of the just. “She is sly, that old woman,” he remarked when his mother explained why breakfast was late.

Old Desroches, the last remaining friend of these two poor women, who despite his harsh nature never forgot that Bridau had obtained his place, fulfilled like a diplomat the mission Madame Descoings had confided to him. He dined that evening with the family and notified Agathe she must go the next day to the Treasury, rue Vivienne, sign the transfer of funds involved, and obtain a coupon for the six hundred francs a year that remained to her. The old clerk did not leave without obliging Philippe to sign a petition to the minister of war asking for reinstatement in the active army. Desroches promised to follow the petition at the war office, and to profit by the triumph of a duke over Philippe in the matter of the danseuse to obtain that nobleman’s influence.

“Philippe will be lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse’s regiment within three months,” he declared, “and you will be rid of him.”

Desroches went away smothered with blessings from the two poor widows and Joseph. As to the newspaper, it ceased to exist in two months, just as Finot predicted. Philippe’s crime had, so far as the world knew, no consequences. But Agathe’s motherhood had received a deadly wound. Her belief in her son once shaken, she lived in perpetual fear, mingled with some satisfactions as she saw her worst apprehensions unrealized.

When men like Philippe, endowed with physical courage yet cowardly and ignoble morally, see things resume their course after a catastrophe in which honor is almost lost, family kindness or friendliness is a premium of encouragement. They count on impunity; their minds distorted, their passions gratified, they study how they succeeded in getting round social laws; the result is they become alarmingly adroit.

A fortnight later Philippe, once more a man of leisure, lazy and bored, renewed his fatal café life—drams, long billiard games with punch, nightly resort to the gambling-table where he risked trifling stakes and won enough to pay his dissipations. Apparently very economical, the better to deceive his mother and Madame Descoings, he wore a greasy hat with the nap rubbed off, patched boots, a shabby overcoat on which the red ribbon scarcely showed so discolored and dirty, his buckskin gloves greenish, and he gave up his satin neckcloth only when ragged enough to look like wadding. Mariette was the sole object of his love, and her treachery had greatly hardened his heart. When he won more than usual, or supped with Giroudeau, he followed some Venus of the slums with brutal contempt for the whole sex. Otherwise regular in his habits, he breakfasted and dined at home and came in about one o’clock. Three months of this life restored Agathe to some confidence.

As for Joseph, working at the splendid picture that later brought him fame, he lived in his atelier. On Bixiou’s prediction, Madame Descoings believed in Joseph’s future glory and showed him every motherly kindness; she took his breakfast, ran errands, blacked his boots. The painter was never seen till dinner-time, and his evenings were spent at the Cenacle among friends. He read much and gave himself the deep education that comes through the mind itself, which men of talent seek between twenty and thirty. Agathe, seeing little of Joseph and feeling no uneasiness, lived only for Philippe, who gave her alternations of fear and relief necessary to maternity. Desroches, who came once a week, gave her hopes. The Duc de Maufrigneuse had asked to have Philippe in his regiment; the minister of war had ordered an inquiry; and as Bridau’s name did not appear on any police list nor at the Palais de Justice, Philippe would be reinstated early in the coming year.

To this result Desroches set all his influence in motion. At the prefecture of police he learned Philippe spent his evenings gambling and thought best to tell this privately to Madame Descoings, exhorting her to keep an eye on the lieutenant-colonel, for one outbreak would imperil all; as it was, the minister of war was not likely to inquire whether Philippe gambled. Once restored under the flag, he might abandon a vice taken up from idleness. Agathe, who no longer received friends, sat in the chimney-corner reading her prayers, while Madame Descoings consulted the cards, interpreted dreams, and applied the rules of the “cabala” to her lottery ventures. This jovial fanatic never missed a drawing; she still pursued her trey—which never turned up. It was nearly twenty-one years old, just approaching majority; on this ridiculous idea she pinned her faith. One of its three numbers had stayed at the bottom of all wheels since the lottery’s institution. Accordingly Madame Descoings laid heavy stakes on that number and on all combinations of the three numbers. The last mattress of her bed was where she stored savings; she unsewed the ticking, put in bits of gold saved from her needs, wrapped them in wool, and sewed the mattress up again. She intended, at the last drawing, to risk all her savings on the different combinations of her treasured trey.

This passion, so universally condemned, has never been fairly studied. No one has understood this opium of poverty. The lottery, all-powerful fairy of the poor, bestowed the gift of magic hopes. The turn of the wheel which opens to the gambler a vista of gold and happiness lasts no longer than a flash, but the lottery gave five days’ existence to that flash. What social power today, for five sous, gives us five days’ happiness and launches us ideally into all the joys of civilization? Tobacco, a craving far more immoral than play, destroys the body, attacks the mind, and stupefies a nation; while the lottery did none of that. This passion was forced to keep within limits by the long periods between drawings and by the choice of wheels each investor clung to. Madame Descoings never staked on any but the “wheel of Paris.” Full of confidence that the trey cherished for twenty-one years was about to triumph, she imposed enormous privations to stake a large sum on the last drawing. When she dreamed cabalistic visions (not all corresponding to lottery numbers), she told Joseph, who alone would listen and not scold her but soothe her follies with kindly words. All great talents respect and understand a real passion; they explain it to themselves by finding its roots in their own hearts. Joseph’s idea was that his brother loved tobacco and liquors, Maman Descoings loved her trey, his mother loved God, Desroches the younger loved lawsuits, Desroches the elder loved angling—in short, all the world loved something. He himself loved the “beau idéal” in all things; he loved Byron’s poetry, Gericault’s painting, Rossini’s music, Walter Scott’s novels. “Everyone to his taste, maman,” he would say; “but your trey does hang fire terribly.”

“It will turn up, and you will be rich, and my little Bixiou as well.”

“Give it all to your grandson,” cried Joseph; “at any rate, do what you like with it.”

“Hey! when it turns up I shall have enough for everybody. In the first place, you shall have a fine atelier; you sha’n’t deprive yourself of going to the opera so as to pay for your models and colors. Do you know, my dear boy, you make me play a pretty shabby part in that picture of yours?”

By way of economy, Joseph had made the Descoings pose for his magnificent painting of a young courtesan taken by an old woman to a Doge of Venice. This picture, one of the masterpieces of modern painting, was mistaken by Gros himself for a Titian, and paved the way for recognition Joseph received in the Salon of 1823.

“Those who know you know very well what you are,” he answered gayly. “Why need you trouble about those who don’t know you?”

For the last ten years Madame Descoings had taken on the ripe tints of a russet apple at Easter. Wrinkles had formed in her superabundant flesh, now pallid and flabby. Her eyes, full of life, were bright with thoughts still young and vivacious, and might be considered grasping; there is always something of that spirit in a gambler. Her fat face bore traces of dissimulation and mental reservations hidden in her heart. Her vice necessitated secrecy. There were also indications of gluttony in the motion of her lips. Thus, although she was an excellent and upright woman, the eye might be misled by her appearance. She was an admirable model for the old woman Joseph wished to paint. Coralie, a young actress of exquisite beauty who died in youth, the mistress of Lucien de Rubempre, one of Joseph’s friends, had given him the idea of the picture. This noble painting has been called a plagiarism of other pictures, while in fact it was a splendid arrangement of three portraits. Michel Chrestien, one of his companions at the Cenacle, lent his republican head for the senator, to which Joseph added a few mature tints, just as he exaggerated Madame Descoings’s expression. This fine picture, destined to make a great noise and bring the artist much hatred, jealousy, and admiration, was just sketched out; but compelled to work for a living, he laid it aside to copy old masters for dealers; thus he penetrated their processes, and his brush is one of the best trained of the modern school. The shrewd sense of an artist led him to conceal profits he began to lay by from his mother and Madame Descoings, aware each had her road to ruin—the one in Philippe, the other in the lottery. This astuteness is seldom wanting among painters; busy for days in their studios, engaged in work which up to a point leaves the mind free, they are in some respects like women—their thoughts turn to the little events of life and they contrive to get at their hidden meaning.

Joseph had bought one of those magnificent chests or coffers of a past age, then ignored by fashion, with which he decorated a corner of his studio, where light danced upon the bas-reliefs and gave lustre to a masterpiece of sixteenth-century artisans. He saw the necessity for a hiding-place and had begun to accumulate a little store of money. With an artist’s carelessness, he put the sum he allowed for monthly expenses in a skull standing on one compartment of the coffer. Since his brother returned home, he found a constant discrepancy between the amount he spent and the sum in this receptacle. The hundred francs a month disappeared with incredible rapidity. Finding nothing one day when he had only spent forty or fifty francs, he remarked for the first time: “My money must have got wings.” The next month he paid more attention; but add as he might, like Robert Macaire, sixteen and five are twenty-three, he could make nothing of it. When, for the third time, he found a still more important discrepancy, he confided his painful suspicions to Madame Descoings, who loved him with a maternal, tender, confiding, enthusiastic love he had never had from his own mother—a love necessary to the early life of an artist. To her alone could he confide his horrible suspicions. He was as sure of his friends as of himself; and the Descoings, he knew, would take nothing to put in her lottery. At the idea that then suggested itself the poor woman wrung her hands. Philippe alone could have committed this domestic theft.

“Why didn’t he ask me, if he wanted it?” cried Joseph, stirring a dab of color into the palette without seeing what he did. “Is it likely I should refuse him?”

“It is robbing a child!” cried Madame Descoings, her face expressing deepest disgust.

“No,” replied Joseph, “he is my brother; my purse is his: but he ought to have asked me.”

“Put in a special sum, in silver, this morning, and don’t take anything out,” said Madame Descoings. “I shall know who goes into the studio; and if he is the only one, you will be certain it is he.”

The next day Joseph had proof of his brother’s forced loans upon him. Philippe came to the studio when Joseph was out and took the little sum he wanted. The artist trembled for his savings.

“I’ll catch him at it, the scamp!” he said, laughing, to Madame Descoings.

“And you’ll do right: we ought to break him of it. I, too, have missed little sums out of my purse. Poor boy! he wants tobacco; he’s accustomed to it.”

“Poor boy! poor boy!” cried the artist. “I’m rather of Fulgence and Bixiou’s opinion: Philippe is a dead-weight on us. He runs his head into riots and must be shipped to America, and that costs the mother twelve thousand francs; he can’t find anything to do in the forests, so he comes back and that costs twelve thousand more. Under pretense of delivering two words of Napoleon to a general, he thinks himself a great soldier and makes faces at the Bourbons; meanwhile, what does he do? amuse himself, travel, see foreign countries! As for me, I’m not duped by his misfortunes; he doesn’t look like a man who fails to get the best of things! Somebody finds him a good place, and there he is, leading the life of a Sardanapalus with a ballet-girl, and guzzling the funds of his journal; that costs the mother another twelve thousand francs! I don’t care two straws for myself, but Philippe will bring that poor woman to beggary. He thinks I’m of no account because I was never in the dragoons of the Guard; but perhaps I shall be the one to support that poor dear mother in her old age, while he, if he goes on as he does, will end I don’t know how. Bixiou often says to me, ‘He is a downright rogue, that brother of yours.’ Your grandson is right. Philippe will be up to some mischief that will compromise the honor of the family, and then we shall have to scrape up another ten or twelve thousand francs! He gambles every night; when he comes home, drunk as a templar, he drops on the staircase the pricked cards on which he marks the turns of red and black. Old Desroches is trying to get him back into the army, and, on my word, I believe he would hate to serve again. Would you ever have believed that a boy with such heavenly blue eyes and the look of Bayard could turn out such a scoundrel?

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In spite of the coolness and discretion with which Philippe played his nightly trifling game, he was sometimes what gamblers call “cleaned out.” Driven by the necessity of having his evening stake of ten francs, he plundered the household, taking his brother’s money and whatever Madame Descoings or Agathe left about. The poor mother had a dreadful vision in her first sleep: Philippe entered the room and took from her gown every coin he could find. Agathe pretended to sleep but spent the night crying. “One wrong act is not a vice,” Madame Descoings had once said; after so many repetitions, vice was unmistakable. Agathe could doubt no longer: her beloved son had neither delicacy nor honor.

The morning after that frightful vision, before Philippe left after breakfast, Agathe drew him into her chamber and begged him to ask her for whatever money he needed. After that, his applications were so numerous that in two weeks Agathe was drained of all her savings. She was without a penny and began to think of working. She and Madame Descoings had discussed taking in worsted work from a shop called the “Pere de Famille,” which paid about twenty sous a day. Madame Descoings guessed the motive of this desire despite Agathe’s silence. Her appearance changed: the fresh face withered, skin clung to temples and cheekbones, deep lines cut her forehead; her eyes lost their clearness; an inward fire consumed her; she wept most nights.

A chief cause of this ruin was the need to hide her anguish. She never slept until Philippe came in; she listened for his step, learned the inflections of his voice, the variations of his walk, even the sound of his cane on pavement. Nothing escaped her. She knew how drunk he was by the way he stumbled and the state of his boots. When he had drunk and won, his voice was gruff and his cane dragged; when he had lost, his step was sharp and angry, he hummed in a clear voice and carried his cane as if presenting arms. At breakfast, if he had won, he was gay and affectionate; gloomy when he had lost, his brusque speech, hard glance, and depression frightened them. Debauchery and liquor debased a once-handsome countenance: veins swelled, features coarsened, eyelashes fell away, eyes became hard and dry. No longer careful of his person, Philippe smelled of taverns and muddy boots.

“You ought,” said Madame Descoings to Philippe in late December, “to get yourself new clothes from head to foot.”

“And who is to pay for it?” he answered sharply. “My poor mother hasn’t a sou; and I have five hundred francs a year. It would take my whole pension; besides I have mortgaged it for three years—”

“What for?” asked Joseph.

“A debt of honor. Giroudeau borrowed a thousand francs from Florentine to lend me. I am not gorgeous, that’s a fact; but when one thinks that Napoleon is at Saint Helena, and has sold his plate for the means of living, his faithful soldiers can manage to walk on their bare feet,” he said, showing his boots without heels, as he marched away.

“He is not bad,” said Agathe, “he has good feelings.”

“You can love the Emperor and yet dress properly,” said Joseph. “If he would take any care of himself and his clothes, he wouldn’t look so like a vagabond.”

“Joseph! have some indulgence for your brother,” cried Agathe. “You do as you like, while he is certainly not in his right place.”

“What did he leave it for?” demanded Joseph. “What can it matter to him whether Louis the Eighteenth’s bugs or Napoleon’s cuckoos are on the flag, if it is the flag of his country? France is France! For my part, I’d paint for the devil. A soldier ought to fight, if he is a soldier, for the love of his art. If he had stayed quietly in the army, he would have been a general by this time.”

“You are unjust to him,” said Agathe. “Your father, who adored the Emperor, would have approved of his conduct. However, he has consented to re-enter the army. God knows the grief it has caused your brother to do what he considers treachery.”

Joseph rose to return to his studio, but his mother took his hand and begged him to be good to his brother; he was so unfortunate.

Back in his painting-room, followed by Madame Descoings, Joseph found Philippe there, to their amazement.

“Joseph, my boy,” Philippe said off-hand, “I want some money. Confound it! I owe thirty francs for cigars at my tobacconist’s, and I dare not pass the cursed shop till I’ve paid it. I’ve promised to pay it a dozen times.”

“Well, I like your present way best,” said Joseph; “take what you want out of the skull.”

“I took all there was last night, after dinner.”

“There was forty-five francs.”

“Yes, that’s what I made it,” replied Philippe. “I took them; is there any objection?”

“No, my friend, no,” said Joseph. “If you were rich, I should do the same by you; only, before taking what I wanted, I should ask you if it were convenient.”

“It is humiliating to ask,” Philippe remarked; “I would rather see you taking as I do, without a word; it shows more confidence. In the army, if a comrade dies, and has a good pair of boots, and you have a bad pair, you change, that’s all.”

“Yes, but you don’t take them while he is living.”

“Oh, what meanness!” shrugged Philippe. “Well, so you haven’t got any money?”

“No,” said Joseph, determined not to show his hiding-place.

“In a few days we shall be rich,” said Madame Descoings.

“Yes, you; you think your trey is going to turn up on the 25th at the Paris drawing. You must have put in a fine stake if you think you can make us all rich.”

“A paid-up trey of two hundred francs will give three millions, without counting the couplets and the singles.”

“At fifteen thousand times the stake—yes, you are right; it is just two hundred you must pay up!” cried Philippe.

Madame Descoings bit her lips; she knew she had spoken imprudently. Philippe, as he went downstairs, thought: “That old witch! where does she keep her money? It is as good as lost; I can make a better use of it. With four pools at fifty francs each, I could win two hundred thousand francs, and that’s much surer than the turning up of a trey.”

He tried to think where she might have hidden it. On festival days Agathe went to church and stayed long; she might prepare for communion. It was the day before Christmas; Madame Descoings would certainly go out to buy revelry dainties and perhaps pay up her stake. The Paris lottery was drawn on the twenty-fifth of each month, and the lists closed on the twenty-fourth at midnight. Philippe studied these points and set himself to watch. He came home at midday; the Descoings had gone out and taken the key of the apartment. That was no difficulty. Philippe pretended to have forgotten something and asked the concierge to fetch a locksmith, who came at once and opened the door. The villain’s first thought was the bed; he uncovered it, passed his hands over the mattress and felt pieces wrapped in paper at the lower end. He ripped the ticking, picked out twenty napoleons, and, without sewing up the mattress, remade the bed neatly so Madame Descoings would suspect nothing.

The gambler stole off, resolving to play three times, three hours apart, each time for only ten minutes—a regimen seasoned players adopted after past ruin. But before reaching that experience they lost fortunes. The whole science of gambling houses rests on the impassibility of the bank, drawn games where half the money goes to the bank, and the bad faith allowed by government refusing to hold or pay players’ stakes except optionally. The gambling-house, which refuses the game of a cool rich player, devours the fortune of the obstinate fool, carried away by the rapid motion of the table; the croupiers at “trente et quarante” move nearly as fast as the ball.

Philippe had acquired the sang-froid of a commanding general, enabling him to keep his eye clear and mind prompt amid tumult. He had reached that statesmanship of gambling which in Paris is the livelihood of those strong enough to look nightly into an abyss without vertigo. With four hundred francs, he resolved to make his fortune that day. He put aside two hundred francs in his boots and kept the other two hundred in his pocket. At three o’clock he went to the gambling-house at the Palais-Royal, where the bank accepted large sums. He came out half an hour later with seven thousand francs. He then paid Florentine the five hundred francs he owed her, proposed supper at the Rocher de Cancale, and returned to play. By six he had won twenty-five thousand francs and stopped after ten minutes as promised. That night, by ten o’clock, he had won seventy-five thousand francs. After the magnificent supper, drunk and confident, he returned to the table at midnight. In defiance of his rule, he played for an hour and doubled his fortune. The bankers, from whom he had extracted one hundred and fifty thousand francs by his system, watched with curiosity. “Will he go away now, or will he stay?” they signalled. “If he stays he is lost.”

Philippe thought luck had struck and stayed. Towards three in the morning the hundred and fifty thousand francs were back at the bank. The colonel, having imbibed much grog while playing, left drunk, and a waiter from the gambling-house found him and took him to one of those terrible lodging-houses. The waiter paid for the ruined gambler who was put to bed, where he remained till Christmas night. The managers of gambling-houses show some consideration for high players. Philippe awoke about seven in the evening, mouth parched, face swollen, in the grip of a nervous fever. His constitution’s strength enabled him to get home on foot, where meanwhile he had, without willing it, brought mourning, desolation, poverty, and death.

The evening before, when dinner was ready, Madame Descoings and Agathe expected Philippe. They waited until seven. Agathe always went to bed at ten; she wished to be present at midnight mass and lay down after dinner. Madame Descoings and Joseph remained in the little salon; the old woman asked the painter to add up the amount of her great stake on the famous trey, which she was to pay that evening at the Lottery office. She wished to put in for doubles and singles, to seize all chances. After feasting on the poetry of her hopes and relating dreams that seemed to demonstrate certain success, she felt only the difficulty of waiting until ten the next day when winning numbers were declared. Joseph, who knew nothing of the four hundred francs required to pay up the stakes, asked about them. The old woman smiled and led him into her former salon, now her bed-chamber.

“You shall see,” she said.

Madame Descoings hastily unmade the bed and searched for scissors to rip the mattress; she put on spectacles, looked at the ticking, saw the hole, and let fall the mattress. Hearing a sigh from the depths of her breast, as though strangled, Joseph instinctively held out his arms and placed the poor creature fainting in a chair, calling for his mother. Agathe ran in, applied ordinary remedies—eau-de-cologne, cold water, a burnt feather under the nose—and presently her aunt revived.

“They were there this morning; HE has taken them, the monster!” she said.

“Taken what?” asked Joseph.

“I had twenty louis in my mattress; my savings for two years; no one but Philippe could have taken them.”

“But when?” cried the mother, overwhelmed, “he has not been in since breakfast.”

“I wish I might be mistaken,” said the old woman. “But this morning in Joseph’s studio, when I spoke before Philippe of my stakes, I had a presentiment. I did wrong not to go down and take my little all and pay for my stakes at once. I meant to, and I don’t know what prevented me. Oh, yes—my God! I went out to buy him some cigars.”

“But,” said Joseph, “you left the door locked. Besides, it is so infamous. I can’t believe it. Philippe couldn’t have watched you, cut open the mattress, done it deliberately—no, no!”

“I felt them this morning, when I made my bed after breakfast,” repeated Madame Descoings.

Agathe, horrified, went downstairs and asked if Philippe had come in during the day. The concierge related the tale of his return and the locksmith. The mother, heart-stricken, returned a changed woman. White as her chemise, she walked as a spectre, slowly and noiselessly, moved as if mechanically. She held a candle whose light fell full upon her face and showed her eyes fixed with horror. Unconsciously her hands had dishevelled the hair about her brow; Joseph stood rooted in awe at the apparition of that remorse—the vision of a statue of terror and despair.

“My aunt,” she said, “take my silver forks and spoons. I have enough to make up the sum; I took your money for Philippe’s sake; I thought I could put it back before you missed it. Oh! I have suffered much.”

She sat down. Her dry, fixed eyes wandered.

“It was he who did it,” whispered the old woman to Joseph.

“No, no,” cried Agathe; “take my silver plate, sell it; it is useless to me; we can eat with yours.”

She went to her room, took the box that contained the plate, felt its light weight, opened it, and saw a pawnbroker’s ticket. The poor mother uttered a dreadful cry. Joseph and the Descoings ran to her, saw the empty box; her noble falsehood was of no avail. All three were silent and avoided looking at each other; then, by an almost frantic gesture, Agathe laid her finger on her lips as if to entreat secrecy no one desired to break. They returned to the salon and sat beside the fire.

“Ah! my children,” cried Madame Descoings, “I am stabbed to the heart: my trey will turn up, I am certain of it. I am not thinking of myself, but of you two. Philippe is a monster,” she continued, addressing her niece; “he does not love you after all you have done for him. If you do not protect yourself against him he will bring you to beggary. Promise me to sell out your Funds and buy a life-annuity. Joseph has a good profession and he can live. If you will do this, dear Agathe, you will never be an expense to Joseph. Monsieur Desroches has just started his son as a notary; he would take your twelve thousand francs and pay you an annuity.”

Joseph seized his mother’s candlestick, rushed to his studio, and returned with three hundred francs.

“Here, Madame Descoings!” he cried, giving her his little store; “it is no business of ours what you do with your money; we owe you what you have lost, and here it is, almost in full.”

“Take your poor little all—the fruit of those privations that have made me so unhappy! are you mad, Joseph?” cried the old woman, torn between faith in the coming trey and the sacrilege of accepting such a sacrifice.

“Oh! take it if you like,” said Agathe, moved to tears by this action of her true son.

Madame Descoings took Joseph by the head and kissed him on the forehead: “My child,” she said, “don’t tempt me. I might only lose it. The lottery, you see, is all folly.”

No more heroic words were ever uttered in the hidden dramas of domestic life. It was affection triumphant over inveterate vice. At that instant the clocks struck midnight.

“It is too late now,” said Madame Descoings.

“Oh!” cried Joseph, “here are your cabalistic numbers.”

The artist sprang at the paper and rushed down the staircase to pay the stakes. When he was no longer present, Agathe and Madame Descoings burst into tears.

“He has gone, the dear love,” cried the old gambler; “but it shall all be his; he pays his own money.”

Unhappily, Joseph did not know the way to any of the lottery offices, which then were as familiar to most people as the cigar-shops to a smoker now. He ran along, reading street names on lamps. When he asked passers-by to show a lottery office, he was told they were all closed except the one under the Palais-Royal portico which was sometimes kept open a little later. He flew there: the office was shut.

“Two minutes earlier, and you might have paid your stake,” said a vendor under the portico, where he cried: “Twelve hundred francs for forty sous,” offering tickets all paid up.

By the glimmer of a street lamp and the lights of the Café de la Rotonde, Joseph examined these tickets to see if any bore the Descoings’s numbers. He found none and returned home grieved at having done his best in vain. Agathe and her aunt went together to midnight mass at Saint-Germain-des-Prés. Joseph went to bed. The collation did not take place. Madame Descoings had lost her head; in Agathe’s heart was eternal mourning.

They rose late on Christmas morning. Ten o’clock had struck before Madame Descoings began to stir; breakfast was not ready until half-past eleven. At that hour the oblong frames with winning numbers are hung over lottery doors. If Madame Descoings had paid and held her ticket she would have gone by half-past nine to learn her fate at a building near the Ministry of Finance. On drawing days a curious crowd of old women, cooks, and old men gathered about that door.

“Well, here you are, rolling in wealth!” said old Desroches, coming in just as the Descoings took her last drop of coffee.

“What do you mean?” cried poor Agathe.

“Her trey has turned up,” he said, producing the list of numbers on a bit of paper.

Joseph read. Agathe read. The Descoings read nothing; she was struck as by a thunderbolt. At the change in her face and the cry she gave, old Desroches and Joseph carried her to bed. Agathe fetched a doctor. The poor woman was seized with apoplexy and only recovered consciousness at four in the afternoon; old Haudry then said that, despite improvement, she ought to settle her affairs and think of salvation. She uttered only two words: “Three millions!”

Old Desroches, with caution, related many instances where fortunes escaped those who forgot to pay stakes; but he understood how fatal this blow could be after twenty years’ perseverance. About five, as a deep silence reigned in the little apartment and the sick woman lay watched by Joseph and his mother, the sound of Philippe’s step and cane resounded on the staircase.

“There he is! there he is!” cried the Descoings, sitting up in bed and suddenly able to use her paralyzed tongue.

Agathe and Joseph were deeply impressed by the effect on the old woman. Their suspense ended when Philippe entered, convulsed and purple-faced, staggering, his eyes deeply sunken and haggard; he had a strong chill and his teeth chattered.

“Starvation in Prussia!” he cried, looking about him. “Nothing to eat or drink?—and my throat on fire! Well, what’s the matter? The devil is always meddling in our affairs. There’s my old Descoings in bed, looking at me with her eyes as big as saucers.”

“Be silent, monsieur!” said Agathe, rising. “At least, respect the sorrows you have caused.”

“_Monsieur_, indeed!” he cried, looking at his mother. “My dear little mother, that won’t do. Have you ceased to love your son?”

“Are you worthy of love? Have you forgotten what you did yesterday? Go and find another home; you cannot live with us any longer,—that is, after to-morrow,” she added; “for in the state you are in now it is difficult—”

“To turn me out—is that it?” he interrupted. “Ha! are you going to play the melodrama of ‘The Banished Son’? Well done! is that how you take things? You are all a pretty set! What harm have I done? I’ve cleaned out the old woman’s mattress. What the devil is the good of money kept in wool? Do you call that a crime? Didn’t she take twenty thousand francs from you? We are her creditors, and I’ve paid myself as much as I could get—that’s all.”

“My God! my God!” cried the dying woman, clasping her hands and praying.

“Be silent!” exclaimed Joseph, springing at his brother and putting his hand before his mouth.

“To the right about, march! brat of a painter!” retorted Philippe, laying his strong hand on Joseph’s head and twirling him round, flinging him on a sofa. “Don’t dare to touch the moustache of a commander of a squadron of the dragoons of the Guard!”

“She has paid me back all she owed me,” cried Agathe, rising and turning an angry face to her son; “and besides, that is my affair. You have killed her. Go away, my son,” she added, with a gesture that took all her remaining strength, “and never let me see you again. You are a monster.”

“I kill her?”

“Her trey has turned up,” cried Joseph, “and you stole the money for her stake.”

“Well, if she is dying of a lost trey, it isn’t I who have killed her,” said the drunkard.

“Go, go!” said Agathe. “You fill me with horror; you have every vice. My God! is this my son?”

A hollow rattle sounded in Madame Descoings’s throat, increasing Agathe’s anger.

“I love you still, my mother—you who are the cause of all my misfortunes,” said Philippe. “You turn me out of doors on Christmas-day. What did you do to grandpa Rouget, to your father, that he should drive you away and disinherit you? If you had not displeased him, we should all be rich now, and I should not be reduced to misery. What did you do to your father—you who are a good woman? You see by yourself, I may be a good fellow and yet be turned out of house and home—I, the glory of the family—”

“The disgrace of it!” cried the Descoings.

“You shall leave this room, or you shall kill me!” cried Joseph, springing on his brother with the fury of a lion.

“My God! my God!” cried Agathe, trying to separate the brothers.

At that moment Bixiou and Doctor Haudry entered. Joseph had just knocked his brother over and stretched him on the ground.

“He is a regular wild beast,” he cried. “Don’t speak another word, or I’ll—”

“I’ll pay you for this!” roared Philippe.

“A family explanation,” remarked Bixiou.

“Lift him up,” said the doctor, looking at him. “He is as ill as Madame Descoings; undress him and put him to bed; get off his boots.”

“That’s easy to say,” cried Bixiou, “but they must be cut off; his legs are swollen.”

Agathe took scissors. When she had cut down the boots, which were worn outside the clinging trousers, ten pieces of gold rolled on the floor.

“There it is—her money,” murmured Philippe. “Cursed fool that I was, I forgot it. I too have missed a fortune.”

He was seized with a horrible delirium of fever and began to rave. Joseph, assisted by old Desroches and Bixiou, carried him to his room. Doctor Haudry was obliged to write to the Hopital de la Charité and borrow a strait-waistcoat; the delirium ran so high he feared Philippe might kill himself. At nine o’clock calm was restored. The Abbé Loraux and Desroches endeavored to comfort Agathe, who never ceased to weep at her aunt’s bedside. She listened in silence and obstinately shook her head; Joseph and the Descoings alone knew the depth of her inward wound.

“He will learn to do better, mother,” said Joseph, when Desroches and Bixiou had left.

“Oh!” cried the widow, “Philippe is right—my father cursed me: I have no right to—Here, here is your money,” she said to Madame Descoings, adding Joseph’s three hundred francs to the two hundred found on Philippe. “Go and see if your brother does not need something,” she said to Joseph.

“Will you keep a promise made to a dying woman?” asked Madame Descoings, who felt her mind failing.

“Yes, aunt.”

“Then swear to me to give your property to young Desroches for a life annuity. My income ceases at my death; and from what you have just said, I know you will let that wretch wring the last farthing out of you.”

“I swear it, aunt.”

The old woman died on the thirty-first of December, five days after the terrible blow old Desroches had so innocently given her. The five hundred francs—the only money in the household—were barely enough for her funeral. She left a small amount of silver and some furniture, the value of which Madame Bixiou paid over to her grandson Bixiou. Reduced to an eight hundred franc annuity paid by young Desroches, who had bought a business without clients and himself took the capital of twelve thousand francs, Agathe gave up her third-floor apartment and sold all superfluous furniture. When, after a month, Philippe seemed convalescent, his mother coldly explained that the costs of his illness had taken all her ready money, that she would be obliged to work for her living, and urged him with the utmost kindness to re-enter the army and support himself.

“You might have spared me that sermon,” said Philippe, looking at his mother with indifference. “I have seen all along that neither you nor my brother love me. I am alone in the world; I like it best!”

“Make yourself worthy of our affection,” answered the poor mother, struck to the heart, “and we will give it back to you—”

“Nonsense!” he cried, interrupting her.

He took his old hat, rubbed white at the edges, stuck it over one ear, and went downstairs, whistling.

“Philippe! where are you going without any money?” cried his mother, who could not repress her tears. “Here, take this—”

She held out a hundred francs in gold, wrapped in paper. Philippe came up the stairs and took the money.

“Well; won’t you kiss me?” she said, bursting into tears.

He pressed his mother in his arms, but without the warmth that would have given value to the embrace.

“Where shall you go?” asked Agathe.

“To Florentine, Giroudeau’s mistress. Ah! they are real friends!” he answered brutally.

He went away. Agathe turned back with trembling limbs, failing eyes, and aching heart. She fell on her knees, prayed God to take her unnatural child into His keeping, and abdicated her woeful motherhood.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
By February 1822 Madame Bridau had taken the attic room over the kitchen of her old apartment. The painter’s studio and bedroom faced it across the stair. Seeing his mother reduced, Joseph resolved to make her comfortable. After his brother left he refurnished the garret with an artist’s touch: a rug, a simple monastic bed, glazed cotton on the walls, a double door with a portière, and a blind to soften the light. Though Agathe’s life was stripped to the plainest circumstances in Paris, Joseph made her as decent as he could.

To spare her frugal housekeeping, Joseph took Agathe daily to dine at a table-d’hôte on the rue de Beaune frequented by well-bred women, deputies, and the titled; dinners there cost ninety francs a month. Agathe, paying only for breakfast, resumed old habits from her married life. Alarmed when she learned the dinner cost nearly a hundred francs a month, and not imagining Joseph earned money by painting naked women, she obtained through her confessor, the Abbé Loraux, a place worth seven hundred francs a year in a lottery office belonging to the Comtesse de Bauvan. Lottery offices often sufficed to support a manager’s family; after the Restoration some titled women could have two offices yielding six to ten thousand a year, hiring paid managers and assistants. The Comtesse promised the manager’s place when it vacated; meanwhile Agathe took an assistant’s post at six hundred francs. Her hours were long: at the office by ten, scarcely time for dinner, then back by seven to midnight. For two years Joseph fetched her every night, often taking her to dinner; friends saw him leave the opera or a brilliant salon to be punctual at midnight at the rue Vivienne office.

Agathe soon adopted the monotonous regularity that supports those who have suffered. Mornings she tidied her room—no cats or birds now—prepared breakfast and carried it into the studio to eat with Joseph, arranged his bedroom, put out her own fire, and sat sewing by the iron stove, retreating when a comrade or model entered. She understood nothing of art and made no pretence; amazed at the fuss about color, composition, and drawing, she later examined canvases and found nothing to justify the painters’ disputes. She made Joseph’s shirts, mended stockings, cleaned his palette, supplied rags, and kept the studio in order. Joseph, noticing her care over trivial things, lavished attentions on her. Their bond was tenderness rather than shared art.

One morning, while Joseph sketched a large picture, Agathe, petting him, spoke of Philippe as if casually: Had he suffered? Had poverty changed him? Joseph denied it, saying Philippe kept carnival in New York as he did in Paris, and that if he were suffering he’d gladly give him money but did not want to see him—“he killed our poor Descoings.” Agathe hoped Joseph would paint Philippe’s portrait to have one of him; Joseph consented for his mother’s sake. She pressed him tenderly; “He is your passion,” Joseph said.

That afternoon Joseph went to the rue du Sentier and found Philippe, who had taken Giroudeau’s place at a weekly journal that Finot’s nephew ran; the old dragoon had been promoted to cashier. Finot, though still owner of another paper, favored his nephew Lousteau (connected to the Bridaus’ past), and to please him had cut Philippe’s salary. Philippe earned six hundred francs and five hundred from his cross—sufficient for a warm office day and free theatre passes at night—needing little but food and a place to sleep. Coloquinte, the office boy, watched Philippe, who oddly behaved well. When Joseph arrived, Philippe proposed dinner, the opera, and introductions; Joseph declined, saying he would take his mother to dine at the table d’hôte. Philippe agreed to sit for his portrait in uniform; though he worked in a cramped “hen-coop” from nine to five, he said two Sundays would suffice to sit.

When Joseph told Agathe of Philippe’s position on their way to the rue de Beaune, she trembled with joy and breathed as if relieved of a weight. She showed gratitude by decorating the studio with flowers and buying two plant stands. On the first Sunday Philippe sat, the ex-dragoon sending his uniform the night before; Agathe, hiding behind a screen, prepared a charming breakfast and cried silently. Philippe posed for two hours before and two after breakfast. At three he dressed ordinary and proposed dining at the Palais-Royal, jingling gold. Joseph, uneasy, feared seeing gold about Philippe; Agathe insisted on going. Philippe gave a splendid dinner at the Rocher de Cancale costing a hundred francs. Drunk and boasting a run of luck, he took them to the theatre later, but Agathe feigned illness and refused; back at rue Mazarin she sank into gloom.

On the next sitting Sunday Agathe was visibly present. She served breakfast and questioned Philippe, learning he lodged with journalists, actresses, and booksellers as a cashier and lived on kirsch before posing, boasting of future greatness but silent about his means. Joseph, saying the Salon was near and he needed money for frames and to finish a copy of a Rubens ordered by dealer Elie Magus (the original on loan from a Swiss banker for ten days), postponed the sitting till the following Sunday. That evening Joseph prepared a stratagem: when Pierre Grassou, a fellow painter, came to see the finished copy, Joseph varnished it and placed it where the original had been, setting the original on his easel. Grassou was taken in and delighted. Magus did not come as agreed. Joseph left the key with the concierge and dined at his table d’hôte with Grassou.

An hour later Philippe arrived, told the concierge he was to sit that evening, and the concierge gave him the key. Philippe went upstairs, took the copy thinking it the original, returned the key claiming he’d forgotten something, and hurried off to sell the Rubens for three thousand francs. He had sent Magus a note asking him not to call till the next day. When Joseph returned that night with his mother, the concierge told him Philippe’s trick. Joseph feared ruin and ran to the studio. Relieved to find the original still there, he cursed Philippe as a “vile scoundrel.” Agathe, bewildered by his words, wept when told. Joseph insisted they warn the concierge and keep the keys themselves; he vowed to paint his brother’s blackguard face from memory. Agathe, heart-broken, asked him to leave the portrait as it was—she could not bear to look at it.

Philippe knew that the money from the Rubens would be used to prevent his brother’s ruin, yet still took the copy. After this crime Agathe never mentioned him; her face became cold, concentrated, bitter. One thought possessed her: “Some day we shall hear of a Bridau in the police courts.”

Two months later an old officer—Giroudeau—calling himself Philippe’s friend, came to their studio on urgent business. His appearance—fishy gray eyes, piebald mustache, greasy hair, a butter-colored skull, an overlarge stomach in a shabby iron-gray coat with a faded Legion of Honor ribbon, gold rings in his ears—gave him a debauched, libidinous look. Joseph thought him a “noceur,” a loose companion. Giroudeau told them Philippe could no longer work at the office; he was lodged with Mademoiselle Florentine in a miserable attic and was dying; unless they paid for doctor and medicines he would be taken to the Capuchins’ hospital. He had pledged his pension of the cross and had literally nothing. Giroudeau and Florentine would pay half the cost of keeping Philippe where he was if Agathe paid the rest—three hundred francs for a nurse and two months’ care. Agathe thanked him but said the son was banished from her heart and she had no money—she was an assistant in a lottery office to avoid burdening Joseph.

Giroudeau pressed Joseph too; Joseph blurted that Giroudeau had come to swindle them on false pretences. He refused to give anything; Giroudeau left mortified and disappointed. Three months later, in July, as Agathe crossed the Pont Neuf, she saw a man along the Quai de l’Ecole who looked like Philippe in the last stages of poverty: ragged neck-cloth, scurfy hat, patched boots, threadbare coat, dirty collar, black hands, a waistcoat in place of a shirt. His nearly bald head, sallow skin, sunken face showed he had just left the Hôpital du Midi. His blue overcoat, whitened at the seams, still bore the ribbon of his cross; passersby noticed the rosette with curiosity and commiseration.

Agathe trembled. Forgetting past crimes—stealing, gambling, debauchery—she saw only a man recovering from illness and likely to die of starvation. Impulsively she crossed the quay, thrust her purse into Philippe’s hand, and fled as if guilty. For two days she ate nothing, haunted by the image of her son dying in Paris and thinking Giroudeau had not deceived them; at forty-seven she grew to look seventy. Her eyes were dimmed with tears and prayers. But worse was to come: a conspiracy among officers was discovered, and Moniteur articles hawked lists of arrests. Agathe, hearing Philippe’s name in her lottery office, fainted; the manager, understanding, gave her two weeks’ leave.

Joseph went to Desroches to consult about Philippe. While the artist confided the matter to the younger Desroches—now reputed a clever lawyer—Giroudeau called on the widow Desroches and persuaded Agathe that twelve thousand francs would secure Philippe’s release by buying the silence of two witnesses. Agathe, desperate, promised to get the money without knowing how. She wrote to her godmother, Madame Hochon, begging her to ask Jean-Jacques Rouget to send the sum or to lend it and she would repay in two years. Madame Hochon’s reply, mailed by return courier, refused. She explained that her brother Hochon, with an income of at least forty thousand francs and savings estimated at over six hundred thousand, would give nothing to nephews he had never seen. She could not act without her husband’s signature and said Rouget would refuse; she could not dispose of money while her husband lived. She hinted that Agathe’s presence in Issoudun might neutralize the concubine’s influence over Jean-Jacques Rouget and suggested Agathe come and stay with her; she promised to try to make her husband consent and hinted at using a will to coerce him if necessary. She urged Agathe to employ a good lawyer and to come to Issoudun at once.

The letter impressed Agathe, who showed it to Joseph after telling him of Giroudeau’s demand. Joseph advised telling everything to Desroches. The next morning at six they went to find Desroches in the rue de Bussy. The lawyer, cold, stern, with a cracked voice, rough skin, implacable eyes and a fox-like visage, bounded like a tiger when he heard of Giroudeau’s proposal and scornfully warned Agathe not to be duped. He urged her not to give a farthing; he’d answer for Philippe and would rather see him convicted—brought before the Court of Peers—if that would save his future. He advised securing the property at Issoudun and getting evidence of undue influence if Jean-Jacques had made a will favoring the woman; he offered to go to Issoudun himself in the holidays if needed. His briskness alarmed Joseph; Desroches winked and detained Joseph a moment to tell him Philippe was a “great scoundrel” and might have been the cause of the conspiracy’s discovery—either a fool or a traitor—and would be under police surveillance.

“Go to Issoudun with your mother,” Desroches urged. “Try to save the property.” Joseph, having sold two pictures to fund the trip, told his mother they would start for Berry. After writing to Madame Hochon, Agathe and Joseph left that evening for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to his fate. On the road, Agathe, reflecting on the Luxembourg prison to which Philippe had been transferred, said, “If it were not for the Allies he would never be there!” Joseph, gallant, embraced his mother and praised her maternal devotion: “You are a mother just as Raphael was a painter. And you will always be a fool of a mother!”

Agathe’s mind, distracted by the journey, turned to the task. She re-read Madame Hochon’s letter, struck by the words “concubine” and “slut” used by a septuagenarian pious as she and by “imbecile” applied to Rouget. She wondered how her presence could save the inheritance. Joseph, ignorant of the Code, admitted he was at a loss. He recalled Desroches had said to gather evidence of undue influence if Jean-Jacques had made a will favoring the woman. “Don’t trouble your heads uselessly,” Agathe said; “when we get to Issoudun my godmother will tell us what to do.” Their naivety in the task Desroches set them was plain as they entered the Sologne.

Returning to Issoudun after thirty years’ absence, Agathe was about to find changes in its manners that require a portrait of the town. Without it the reader would scarcely understand Madame Hochon’s heroism in helping her goddaughter, or the strange position of Jean-Jacques Rouget. That a brother should for thirty years give no sign of life to a sister was extraordinary and suggested peculiar circumstances; any other sister and nephew would long since have inquired. There is, moreover, a connection between Issoudun’s condition and the Bridau family’s interests, which will appear as the story goes on.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Issoudun, without disparaging Paris, ranks among France’s oldest cities. Tradition calls Probus the Noah of the Gauls; Caesar praised Champ-Fort wine (“de Campo Forti”), still one of Issoudun’s best. Rigord records the town’s former population and commerce, but Armand Peremet’s excavations revealed older layers: beneath the celebrated tower lay a fifth-century basilica—likely unique in France—whose stones came from a Roman temple once on the site, preserving traces of an earlier civilization.

Peremet argues town names ending in “-dun” indicate autochthonous, Druidical, or Celtic origins. Beneath the Gaulish dun likely stood a Roman temple to Isis, hence Chaumon’s name Issous-Dun—“Is” for Isis. Richard Coeur-de-lion probably built the famous tower—where he coined money—over the fifth-century basilica, enveloping the church in feudal fortifications for protection. Issoudun once hosted the brief power of Routiers and Cottereaux, adventurers Henry II sent against his son Richard during the Poitou rebellion.

The unwritten history of Aquitaine may remain obscure with the Benedictines gone. Another sign of Issoudun’s ancient importance is the Tournemine’s conversion into a canal: a stream raised above the Theols that surrounds the town—undoubtedly Roman engineering. A northern suburb traversed by a rue de Rome is inhabited by people who call themselves Romans, mostly vine-growers with distinct features and customs, possibly descendants of those who exterminated the Cottereaux and Routiers on the plain of Charost in the twelfth century.

After the 1830 insurrection, national agitation overshadowed Issoudun’s own vine-growers’ uprising; a dreadful affair there remained unpublished for good reasons. The bourgeois refused military entry, asserting an old municipal claim to govern their city. Backed by seven or eight thousand vine-growers, they burned archives and offices of indirect taxation, and dragged a customs officer through the streets, shouting at every lantern, “Let us hang him here!” The National Guard saved him by carrying him to prison under pretext of an indictment. The general only entered after compromise; when he appeared at the hôtel-de-ville a man from the faubourg de Rome swung a volant—a huge pruning-hook—round his neck, crying, “No more clerks, or there’s an end to compromise!” A leader promised suppression of excisemen and intervened to save the general.

In the fourteenth century Issoudun had sixteen or seventeen thousand inhabitants—vestiges of a larger medieval population. Charles VII owned a mansion known into the eighteenth century as the Maison du Roi. Once a center of the woollen trade, the town supplied much of Europe with wool—for blankets, hats, and Chevreautin gloves. Under Louis XIV., Issoudun, birthplace of Baron and Bourdaloue, was famed for elegance and correct speech; the curate Poupard praised its subtlety and wit. Today wit and splendor have faded. Issoudun now has barely twelve thousand inhabitants, including vine-dressers of four large suburbs—Saint-Paterne, Vilatte, Rome, and Alouette—that are almost small towns. The bourgeoisie are scattered through the streets. Issoudun still holds Berry’s fleeces market, a commerce threatened by improvements in stock-breeding slowly spreading in Berry.

The vineyards produce a wine drunk across two departments and could rival Burgundy or Gascony if cultivated differently. Yet conservative habits prevail: vine-growers leave grape refuse (marc) in the juice during fermentation, making the wine detestable though it could be a source of wealth. They claim the refuse’s bitterness lessens with age, so a vintage will keep a century—an excuse echoed by Guillaume le Breton.

Issoudun’s decline stems from sluggish torpor. When a highroad between Paris and Toulouse was planned, the natural line from Vierzon to Châteauroux would have passed through Issoudun, shorter than the present route via Vatan. But leading citizens and the town council demanded the road go by Vatan, arguing a road through Issoudun would raise provisions and force them to pay thirty sous for a chicken—short-sighted parochialism like the resistance that once forced a Sardinian highway to detour eight miles.

Falling wine prices please the bourgeoisie seeking cheap provisions but threaten vine-growers with ruin as costs and taxes rise—just as the woollen trade suffers from lack of improvement in sheep breeding. Country folk fear change; a peasant may prefer poverty to the social sting of adopting a new habit. Many bourgeois express this sentiment with inward pride.

In town, social stagnation has set in. Families live for themselves; society lacks the class distinctions that give manners character. Nobility has vanished—Cottereaux, Routiers, Jacquerie, religious wars, and the Revolution abolished them, a triumph Issoudun applauds. The town repeatedly refused a garrison, claiming cheap provisions, thus losing intercourse with the age and the profits troops bring; before 1756 it had been a delightful garrison town. A judicial feud between a lieutenant-general and the Marquis de Chapt—whose son, a dragoon, was put to death—deprived the town of a garrison. The forty-fourth demi-brigade’s sojourn during the civil war did not reconcile inhabitants to soldiers.

Bourges, likewise declining, suffers the same malady. Vitality leaves such communities. The government is to blame: administration should find and heal wounds by sending energetic men with power to change things, but instead it approves fatal tranquillity. Who would accept being buried in such arrondissements, where useful work lacks glory? Any ambitious outsider soon sinks into local inertia; Issoudun would have benumbed Napoleon.

In 1822 the arrondissement’s administrators were all locals. Administration became nullity or farce—except in rare cases. The procureur du roi, Monsieur Mouilleron, was cousin to much of the community; his substitute from a town family. The court judge, before his seat, became infamous for a provincial saying: ending a summing-up, he told the accused, “My poor Pierre! the thing is as plain as day; your head will be cut off. Let this be a lesson to you.” The commissary of police, in office since the Restoration, had relations throughout the arrondissement. Religion’s influence was weak; even the curate was held in little esteem.

This radical, ignorant bourgeoisie—fond of annoying others—spread tales, some comic, about Jean-Jacques Rouget and his servant-woman. Still, children went to Sunday-school, schools were kept up, mass said, taxes paid (the sole thing Paris extracts from the provinces), and the mayor passed resolutions. But these acts were routine; the laxity of local government matched the moral and intellectual state of the governed. Subsequent events show effects of this condition, common in many provinces, especially the South. The state to which bourgeois ascendency has reduced Issoudun may spread across France, even to Paris, if the bourgeois continue to rule domestic and foreign policy.

Topography: Issoudun stretches north-south along a hillside curving toward the Châteauroux road. At the hill’s foot a canal, now called the “Rivière forcée,” fed from the Theols, was built when the town flourished—to supply manufactories or flood rampart moats. The “Rivière forcée” is an artificial arm of the Tournemine, joining other streams beyond the Rome suburb. These threads of water irrigate wide meadows, enclosed by yellowish or white terraces dotted with dark specks—the vineyards’ appearance for many months. Vine-growers cut plants annually to ugly stumps, unsupported, sheltered by barrels. A traveller from Vierzon, Vatan, or Châteauroux, weary of monotony, is pleasantly surprised by Issoudun’s meadows—an oasis supplying vegetables across thirty miles. Below the Rome suburb lies a vast tract of kitchen-gardens, upper and lower Baltan. A long poplar avenue leads from town across the meadows to the ancient convent of Frapesle, whose English gardens, grandly named Tivoli, are a Sunday rendezvous for lovers.

Traces of ancient grandeur appear in town divisions. The château, once almost a town with walls and moats, remains a distinct quarter entered by ancient gateways via three bridges across two river arms, resembling an old city. Ramparts show formidable foundations on which houses have sprung. Above the château stands the famous tower, once the citadel. Conquering the city, which lay around these two fortified points, still left the tower and castle to be won.

The Saint-Paterne suburb, a palette-shaped extension beyond the tower encroaching on meadows, was likely part of the ancient city. In 1822 this seemed certain because of the then-existence of the charming church of Saint-Paterne, recently pulled down by the heir of the buyer from the nation. This Romanesque church, one of France’s finest, perished without a single drawing of its preserved portal. The lone voice raised to save it found no echo in town or department. Though the château appears like an old town with narrow streets and mansions, the main city, repeatedly captured and burned—especially during the Fronde—has a modern air. Spacious streets and well-built houses contrast with the citadel’s aspect, earning Issoudun the epithet “pretty” in some geographies.

In such a town, lacking activity, taste for art, or learned occupations, many young men after the Restoration in 1816 had no career until marriage or inheritance. Bored at home and lacking distraction, they “shed their cuticle” by sowing wild oats at the town’s expense. Avoiding daytime escapades to prevent recognition and arraignment before police courts, they chose the night. Thus, amid traces of lost civilizations, a last flare of drollery burst forth.

These young men amused themselves as courts and soldiers once did, banding together for mutual help and tricks. They became an organized conspiracy—the “Knights of Idleness.” By day they played saints, sleeping late after nights of mischief. The Knights began with commonplace pranks—unhooking and changing signs, ringing bells, flinging casks into neighbors’ cellars, rousing inhabitants with crashes suggesting explosions. In Issoudun, cellars are entered by openings near the door, covered by a wooden scuttle with iron hinges and padlock.

In 1816 such pranks persisted. But in 1817 the Order acquired a Grand Master and, until 1823, Maxence Gilet, called Max, led mischief that spread a sort of terror in Issoudun and kept artisans and bourgeois perpetually uneasy.

Maxence Gilet’s background, energy, and bearing suited him for the role. Town rumor supposed him the natural son of the sub-delegate Lousteau—the brother of Madame Hochon whose gallantries were remembered—and some said he might be Doctor Rouget’s son; in truth his father was a dragoon officer garrisoned at Bourges. Because Rouget and Lousteau had been close friends, both claimed paternity, leaving the matter undecided. Max’s mother, wife of a poor sabot-maker in the Rome suburb, was strikingly beautiful—a Trasteverine beauty she transmitted to her son. Madame Gilet, pregnant with Max in 1788, enjoyed the town’s attributions of his paternity, perhaps to set reputed fathers against each other; she colluded with old Gilet to keep protectors ignorant. Madame Hochon paid sixty francs a year for the lad’s schooling—a liberality attributed to her brother at Sancerre.

Doctor Rouget, unlucky in sons, seeing Max’s beauty, paid the youngster’s seminary board until 1805. Lousteau died in 1800, and the doctor continued payments, leaving paternity unresolved. Max, the butt of jests, was soon forgotten when in 1806, a year after Rouget’s death, he began a venturesome life. Gifted with strength and agility, he plotted with Monsieur Hochon’s grandsons to vex grocers, gathered fruit before owners could pick it, scaled walls, excelled at physical exercises, and loved hunting. He spent the doctor’s money on powder and shot for a pistol old Gilet had given him. In autumn 1806, at seventeen, Max committed an involuntary homicide: startling a pregnant woman in dusk while stealing fruit, her death was blamed on him. Threatened with the guillotine by Gilet, Max fled to Bourges, enlisted with a regiment bound for Egypt, and nothing came of the woman’s death.

In three campaigns Max distinguished himself and rose to captain; his lack of formal education did not impede him. In Portugal, 1809, he was left for dead in an English battery. Taken prisoner, he was sent to the Spanish hulks at Cabrera, the most horrible prison station. Friends petitioned for the Legion of Honor and major’s rank, but the Emperor, campaigning in Austria and displeased with Portuguese events, withheld favors from those taken prisoner. Max was at Cabrera from 1810 to 1814. There he was demoralized: the hulks were like galleys, full of crime and infamy. To maintain will and protect against corruption, he killed in duels seven bullies among his fellow-prisoners, ridding the island of tyrants. He then reigned in his hulk by skill with weapons, strength, and cunning.

Though deprived of good example, he returned innocent when peace came in April 1814. He found his parents dead in the almshouse and destitute. At Napoleon’s landing at Cannes he went to Paris to ask for rank and decoration. A marshal then minister of war, recalling Max’s conduct in Portugal, put him in the Guard as captain—giving him the grade of major in infantry—but could not secure the cross. “The Emperor says that you will know how to win it at the first chance,” said the marshal. Indeed, Napoleon put Max on the decoration list the evening after Fleurus, where he distinguished himself.

After Waterloo Max retreated to the Loire. At disbandment Marshal Feltre refused to recognize his grade or cross. Enraged, Max declared he would not serve without rank or decoration. The war-office deemed his demands presumptuous; he resigned, losing half-pay. Issoudun was roused at seeing the brave young man left with only twenty napoleons; the mayor gave him a six-hundred-franc post, which Max held a few months before giving it up. Captain Carpentier, a faithful Napoleonic officer, replaced him.

By now Max was grand master of the Knights of Idleness and lived in ways that lost him the bourgeoisie’s goodwill, though none openly told him, for he was feared, even by officers who, like him, had refused to serve the Bourbons and returned to Berry. Issoudun, small and unimportant, contained more Bonapartists than any town in France; these men became, as is known, nearly all Liberals.

In Issoudun and vicinity a dozen officers shared Max’s position. They admired and followed him, except Captain Carpentier and Major Mignonnet. Carpentier, a cavalry officer risen from the ranks, had married into the town’s best family; Mignonnet, from the École Polytechnique, served in a corps that held itself superior. In the Imperial armies two types existed: a majority who despised civilians, and others—particularly artillerymen—more republican and not adopting the military-civil dichotomy. Thus Major Potel and Captain Renard befriended Max, while Mignonnet and Carpentier sided with the bourgeoisie, judging his conduct dishonorable.

Major Mignonnet, lean and dignified, busied himself with steam-engine problems and lived modestly with Monsieur and Madame Carpentier. His gentle manners and scientific pursuits won town respect; he and Carpentier were often said to be “quite another thing” from Potel, Renard, Max, and the café Militaire habitués, who retained soldierly manners and the Empire’s defective morals.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun, Max was excluded from society. He showed self-respect by not appearing at the club or complaining, though he was the town’s handsomest, best-dressed man, spent much money, and kept a horse—a rarity at Issoudun as Byron’s horse in Venice. We shall see how Max, poor and apparently without means, became the town’s dandy. His disgrace, earning bourgeois and religious contempt, connected with interests that brought Agathe and Joseph to Issoudun.

Judging by his audacious bearing and face, Max cared little for public opinion; he expected revenge and to lord it over those who condemned him. Although the bourgeoisie thought ill of him, the common people admired his courage, daring, and decision—his degradations were mostly unknown, even to them. Max played a role like the blacksmith in The Fair Maid of Perth: champion of Bonapartism and Opposition, counted on in great occasions.

An incident illustrates his heroism. In 1819 a battalion commanded by royalist officers, fresh from the Maison Rouge, passed through Issoudun to Bourges. They lingered at the café Militaire—rendezvous of Bonapartists—where they asked for royalist papers. The fat landlady refused, offering the “Commerce” (the “Constitutionnel”). A captain tore it up, spat on it, and cried, “Bring dominos!” Word spread: “Let us tell Max!” Max entered the café with Major Potel and Captain Renard, followed by thirty young men. After a little comedy about the newspaper, Max, enraged, slapped the nearest officer and fought. They fought in the allée de Frapesle, three against three; Max killed his man. Major Potel wounded another who died in hospital; the third escaped. The battalion left that night. The affair spread through Berry and firmly established Max as a hero.

The Knights of Idleness, all young—the eldest not more than twenty-five—admired Max. Some envied his position. Under such a leader they accomplished great mischief. After May 1817, rarely a week passed without uproar. Max imposed conditions; statutes were drawn. These youths trained like pupils of Amoros—bold, agile, clever and strong as criminals. They practised roof-climbing, scaling houses, noiseless walking, mixing mortar, walling up doors, and collected ropes, ladders, tools, and disguises. They perfected invention and execution of pranks, achieving a Panurge-like genius for evil that provoked laughter while covering victims with ridicule and silencing complaints. Being sons of good families, they had household information facilitating outrages.

Sometimes they lay in ambush along the Grand’rue or the Basse rue, towns’ arteries. Crouched in wall angles at the hour households slept, they called along the town in tones of terror: “What’s the matter?” “What is it?” repeated cries woke citizens who hurried out in shirts and caps with lights, asking questions and showing queer faces.

A poor old bookbinder who believed in hobgoblins worked in a small basement shop. The Knights, disguised as devils, invaded at night, put him in his cutting-press, and left him shrieking as if among hell's souls. He roused the neighbors with tales of Lucifer and was nearly driven insane.

In a severe winter the Knights took down the collector of taxes’ chimney and rebuilt it overnight so noiselessly that they left no trace, but arranged the flue to send all smoke into the house. The collector suffered two months before discovering the cause and had to rebuild a new chimney.

Another time they put three trusses of hay dusted with brimstone and oiled paper down the chimney of a pious old woman friend of Madame Hochon. In the morning, lighting her fire, she thought she had started a volcano. Fire-engines came; the population rushed to help; Knights among the firemen deluged her house, frightening her as much with water as with the “fire.” She grew ill with fear.

To keep someone on guard and in terror, they wrote anonymous letters warning of an impending robbery, then stole softly around his house or under his windows, whistling as if to call each other.

A famous performance was sending letters to all heirs of a miserly old woman likely to leave large property, announcing her death and requesting they be present when the seals were affixed. Eighty persons arrived from neighboring towns in deep mourning—widows, sons, children, some in carrioles or carts. Imagine the scene between servants and arrivals and the notaries’ consultations; it created a sort of riot.

At last the sub-prefect waked to the intolerable state, made worse by the difficulty of discovering perpetrators. Suspicion fell on several youths; but the National Guard was a mere name, there was no garrison, and the lieutenant of police had only eight gendarmes—no patrols—so proof was impossible. The sub-prefect was made an object of mockery. He breakfasted on two fresh eggs, kept chickens, boiled eggs himself with watch in hand, boasting his egg-boiling skill. For two years he had boiled eggs successfully, earning many witticisms. Then, for a month, each night his eggs were taken and replaced by hard-boiled ones, ruining his reputation as the “sous-préfet à l’oeuf.” He lost his egg-boiling laurels and never suspected the Knights, who had planned instead. Max greased the sub-prefect’s stoves nightly with fetid oil, making the house unbearable. His wife, going to mass one morning, found her shawl glued inside by a tenacious substance and had to go without it. The sub-prefect finally requested transfer. His cowardly submissiveness helped establish the Knights’ weird and comic authority.

Beyond the rue des Minimes and the place Misère lay a quarter enclosed between an arm of the “Rivière forcée” and the ramparts, from the place d’Armes to the pottery market. This square was filled with poor houses crowded together, divided by streets so narrow two abreast could not pass. This quarter, a sort of cour des Miracles, housed poor people and lowly trades—hovels and “blind houses.” It has been, from earliest ages, an accursed quarter, haunt of evil-doers; one thoroughfare is called “the street of the Executioner.” For five centuries it’s been customary for the executioner to have a red door. The executioner of Châteauroux’s assistant is said to live there, seen rarely; vine-dressers alone keep intercourse with him, for he inherits the craft of curing wounds and fractures. In Issoudun’s heyday women wandered this quarter. It was home to second-hand sellers and old-clothes dealers whose wares infected the air; in short, the rendezvous of that apocryphal population found in such city parts, where two or three Jews sometimes gain ascendancy.

At a corner in the livelier half of the quarter existed, from 1815 to 1823 and perhaps later, a public-house kept by a woman called Mère Cognette. The house was well built of white stone with ashlar and cement infill, one storey with an attic; over the door hung a huge pine branch like a Florentine bronze. A blue poster showed, above the words “Good Beer of Mars,” a soldier pouring foam in an arched jet toward a very décolletée woman—an image to stir the imagination.

The ground floor was an immense hall serving as kitchen and dining-room, from whose beams provisions hung on huge nails. A winding staircase led to the upper storey; at the foot a door opened into a low, long room lit from a narrow, sunken provincial court like a chimney flue. Hidden by a shed and walls, this low room was where the Bad Boys held their plenary court. Ostensibly Pere Cognet boarded country folk on market-days; secretly he was landlord to the Knights. Pere Cognet, formerly a groom in a rich household, had married La Cognette, a cook in a good family. In the Rome suburb the town follows the Latin custom of placing a feminine termination on the husband’s name and giving it to the wife.

By uniting savings Pere Cognet and his spouse bought their house. La Cognette, about forty, tall and plump, with a Roxelane nose, swarthy skin, jet-black hair, lively brown eyes and a mirthful intelligence, was chosen by Max as the Order’s Leonarde for her character and cookery. Pere Cognet, about fifty-six, thick-set and under his wife’s rule—“he only sees things with a good eye,” she joked, being blind of the other—kept absolute silence about nightly gatherings. From 1816 to 1823 neither revealed what went on. Their devotion was absolute, but self-interest secured their affection and silence. Whenever the Knights knocked a certain way Pere Cognet would get up, light the fire and candles, open the door, and fetch a particular wine laid in for the Order; La Cognette prepared an excellent supper, eaten before or after expeditions planned the previous evening or day.

While Joseph and Madame Bridau were traveling from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played one of their best tricks. An old Spaniard, a former prisoner of war who lived near Issoudun and sold grain, left his empty cart at the foot of the tower early one morning. Maxence, who had come for a rendezvous of the Knights there, was asked in a whisper, “What are we to do tonight?”

“Here’s Père Fario’s cart,” he answered. “I nearly cracked my shins over it. Let us get it up on the embankment of the tower first, and we’ll decide afterwards.”

When Richard Cœur-de-Lion built the Issoudun tower he raised it on the ruins of a basilica, itself above Roman and Celtic remains; these ruins make a cone of monuments of three ages. The tower stands on the apex of this cone with steep descents, reached only by steps; toward the town its pedestal was eighty feet high. In an hour the Knights took the cart off its wheels and hoisted it, piece by piece, to the top of the embankment at the tower’s base—a feat like soldiers carrying artillery over a mountain pass. The cart was remounted; then the Knights, hungry and thirsty, returned to Mère Cognette’s to laugh at how Père Fario would fume when he came for his barrow in the morning.

The Knights did not pull pranks every night; three clever fellows could not invent 365 mischiefs a year. Sometimes the moon shone, or a prank had angered their betters; sometimes a relation was involved and a member refused to join. But if they did not meet nightly, they gathered by day for hunting, the vintages, and winter skating. Among these twenty youths, some were closer to Maxence and made him their idol. Two grandsons of Madame Hochon—François Hochon and Baruch Borniche—were his henchmen. Accepting family gossip about Lousteau’s left-handed parentage, they regarded Max as kin. Max lent them money, took them hunting, and exercised more influence than their grandfather did. Both were orphans kept under Monsieur Hochon’s guardianship for reasons to be explained later.

That night François and Baruch sat one on each side of Max at the middle of a table lit by four tallow candles. A dozen or fifteen bottles had just been drunk; only eleven Knights were present. Baruch—his name showing the Calvinist memory in Issoudun—said to Max as the wine loosened tongues, “You are threatened in your stronghold.”

“What do you mean by that?” asked Max.

“My grandmother has had a letter from Madame Bridau, her goddaughter, saying she and her son are coming here. My grandmother’s getting two rooms ready.”

“What’s that to me?” Max asked, gulping his glass with a comic gesture.

Max then was thirty-four. A candle lit his soldierly face—brow, clear skin, ardent eyes, and black hair that bristled in those five black tongues called the “five points.” Despite this contrast his face was sweet, Raphael-like in outline, with a well-cut, gracious mouth; when he laughed he showed thirty-two good teeth. About five feet six, well proportioned, broad-shouldered and active, he had the bearing of an officer and the charm of someone born to advantage. He possessed both grace and the noble blood of his real father.

“Don’t you know, Max,” cried young Goddet—son of a former surgeon-major and now the best doctor in town—“that Madame Hochon’s goddaughter is the sister of Rouget? If she’s coming with her son, she might secure the property when he dies, and then—good-by to your harvest!”

Max frowned, watched the effect of the news around the table, and again asked, “What’s that to me?”

“But,” said François, “I should think that if old Rouget revoked his will—in case he made one in favor of the Rabouilleuse—”

Max cut him short. “I’ve stopped the mouths of people who dared meddle with you, my dear François,” he said; “and this is how you pay your debts? You use a contemptuous nickname for a woman to whom I am known to be attached.”

Max had never before spoken so distinctly of his relation with Mademoiselle Flore Brazier, called the “Rabouilleuse” by respectable Madame Hochon. Everyone knew it was a ticklish subject; none joked about Flore unless Max began; to risk his anger was dangerous. In other company, Major Potel would say, “If he is the natural brother of Jean-Jacques Rouget where else would you have him live?” Captain Renard would add, “The girl is worthless, and if Max lives with her where’s the harm?” After this snub François could not catch his thread; he was subdued when Max, gently, said, “Go on.”

“Faith, no!” cried François.

“You needn’t get angry, Max,” said Goddet; “didn’t we agree to talk freely at Mère Cognette’s? Outside, we’d be mortal enemies if we remembered what’s said here. All the town calls Flore Brazier the Rabouilleuse; and if François let the nickname slip, is that a crime against the Order of Idleness?”

“No,” said Max, “but against our personal friendship. However, I thought better of it; I recollected we were in session, and that’s why I said ‘Go on.’”

Silence followed, so uncomfortable Max broke it. “I’ll go on for him—for all of you—and tell you what you are thinking. You think that Flore, the Rabouilleuse, La Brazier, the housekeeper of Père Rouget—you think that woman supplies all my wants since I returned to Issoudun. If I am able to throw three hundred francs a month to the dogs, treat you to suppers—as tonight—and lend money to all of you, you think I get the gold out of Mademoiselle Flore Brazier’s purse? Well, yes. Yes, Mademoiselle Brazier is aiming straight for the old man’s property.”

“She gets it from father to son,” observed Goddet.

“You think I intend to marry Flore when Père Rouget dies, and so this sister and her son, of whom I hear to-night for the first time, will endanger my future?”

“That’s just it,” cried François.

“That is what everyone thinks,” said Baruch.

“Don’t be uneasy, friends,” answered Max. “Forewarned is forearmed! Now then, I address the Knights of Idleness. If, to get rid of these Parisians I need the help of the Order, will you lend me a hand? Oh! within the limits we have marked out for our fooleries,” he added, seeing hesitation. “Do you suppose I want to kill them—poison them? Thank God I’m not an idiot. Besides, if the Bridaus succeed, and Flore has nothing but what she stands in, I should be satisfied; do you understand that? I love her enough to prefer her to Mademoiselle Fichet—if Mademoiselle Fichet would have me.”

Mademoiselle Fichet was the richest heiress in Issoudun; her name figured in rumor about the younger Goddet’s passion for a mother. Frankness prized, all the Knights rose as one: “You are a fine fellow, Max!” “We’ll stand by you!” “A fig for the Bridaus!” “Hurrah for Max!” Eleven acclamations, toasts, and relaxed morality reflected their mood. It is easy to see why Max became their grand master: by leading youths of the best families and doing them services, he built a support to recover position later. He rose, waved his glass, and addressed them:

“As a mark of the ill-will I bear you, I wish you all a mistress equal to the beautiful Flore! As to this irruption of relations, I don’t feel present uneasiness; and as to the future, we’ll see what comes—”

“Don’t forget Fario’s cart!”

“Hang it! that’s safe enough!” said Goddet.

“Oh! I’ll engage to settle that business,” cried Max. “Be in the market-place early, all of you, and let me know when the old fellow goes for his cart.”

At half-past three in the morning the Knights slipped out silently to their homes, gliding close to the walls in list shoes. Max returned slowly to the place Saint-Jean—the quarter of the rich bourgeoisie. He had concealed his fears, but the news struck home. His time on the hulks at Cabrera had taught him deep dissimulation. Above all, the forty thousand francs a year from Rouget’s landed property was the core of Max’s passion for Flore Brazier. By his bearing one saw how much confidence she had given him in the financial future she expected via the old bachelor. Still, the arrival of legitimate heirs shook Max’s faith in Flore’s influence. Rouget’s savings, accumulated over seventeen years, still stood in his own name; even if the will Flore declared had been made in her favor were revoked, these savings could at least be secured by putting them into Mademoiselle Brazier’s name.

“That fool of a girl never told me, in all these seven years, a word about the sister and nephews!” cried Max, turning from the rue de la Marmouse into the rue l’Avenier. “Seven hundred and fifty thousand francs placed with different notaries at Bourges, Vierzon, and Châteauroux can’t be turned into money and put into the Funds in a week without everybody knowing in this gossiping place! The important thing is to get rid of these relations; once they’re driven away we must hurry to secure the property. I must think it over.”

Tired, Max let himself into Père Rouget’s house with a pass-key and went to bed without noise, saying, “To-morrow my thoughts will be clear.”

Now it is necessary to relate why the sultana of place Saint-Jean picked up the nickname “Rabouilleuse,” and how she became quasi-mistress of Jean-Jacques Rouget’s home.

As old Doctor Rouget—father of Jean-Jacques and Madame Bridau—aged he perceived his son’s nonentity and treated him harshly, preparing him to submit to first tyranny. Coming home one day from his rounds, the malignant and vicious old man encountered a bewitching little girl at the edge of some fields along the avenue de Tivoli. Hearing the horse, the child sprang up from the brook, half naked, wearing a forlorn petticoat of coarse strips, a rough paper hat, and tattered garments; yet beneath these rags was the loveliest head and figure. One companion wading in the water—her uncle—raised his head and said, “What are you about, Flore? While you are talking instead of catching, the creatures will get away.”

“Why have you come here from Vatan?” said the doctor.

“I am catching crabs for my uncle Brazier here.”

“Rabouiller” is a Berrichon word meaning troubling the water to bring crabs to the surface; Flore Brazier held her rabouilloir with childlike grace.

“Has your uncle got permission to hunt crabs?”

“Hey! aren’t we all under a Republic that is one and indivisible?” cried the uncle.

“We are under a Directory,” said the doctor, “and I know of no law that allows a man to come from Vatan and fish in the territory of Issoudun. Have you got a mother, little one?”

“No, monsieur; my father’s in the asylum at Bourges. He went mad from a sunstroke in the fields.”

“How much do you earn?”

“Five sous a day while the season lasts; I catch them as far as the Braisne. In harvest I glean; in winter I spin.”

“You are about twelve years old?”

“Yes, monsieur.”

“Do you want to come with me? You shall be well fed and well dressed, and have some pretty shoes.”

“No, my niece will stay with me; I am responsible to God and man for her,” said Uncle Brazier, who had come up. He wore a sun-rotted peasant hat, a dark, sunken face, patchwork jacket and crash towelling trousers.

“I am Doctor Rouget,” said that man; “as you are the guardian, bring her to my house in place Saint-Jean. It will not be a bad day’s work for you nor for her.”

The doctor spurred his horse and returned to Issoudun. He had hardly sat to dinner before his cook announced the citoyen and citoyenne Brazier.

“Sit down,” said the doctor to uncle and niece. Flore and her guardian, still barefoot, looked round the doctor’s dining-room with wonder; never had they seen such a place.

Rouget’s house, inherited from the Descoings family, stands in the middle of place Saint-Jean, a long narrow square planted with a few sickly lindens. The Descoings house has three windows on the first storey and a porte-cochère leading to a courtyard and garden. Under the archway is a large hall lighted by two street windows, beyond which lie the kitchen and stair to upper floors, a wood-shed, wash-house, stables and coach-house.

The hall that amazed the little peasant was decorated with wooden carvings and a handsome marble chimney-piece with a large gilded mirror. On the panelled walls hung pictures rescued from religious houses—old masters and sacred subjects that declared the family’s taste and wealth. The furniture showed a luxury little valued in Issoudun: a Boulle clock between silver candlesticks, carved oak armchairs covered with tapestry, and a rich console. But Rouget, his son, and the cook took little care: they spat upon a delicate hearth, the chandelier and ceiling bore black speckles from flies; brocatelle curtains were torn; a chest worth many thousand francs served as a sideboard.

“Here, Fanchette,” cried Rouget to his cook, “bring two glasses; give us some of the old wine.”

Fanchette, a big Berrichon countrywoman considered a better cook than even Mère Cognette, ran in with alacrity, showing Rouget’s despotism and her own curiosity.

“What is an acre of vineyard worth in your parts?” asked the doctor, pouring a glass for Brazier.

“Three hundred francs in silver.”

“Well, then! leave your niece here as my servant; she shall have three hundred francs in wages, and, as you are her guardian, you can take them.”

“Every year?” exclaimed Brazier, eyes wide.

“I leave that to your conscience,” said the doctor. “She is an orphan; up to eighteen she has no right to what she earns.”

“Twelve to eighteen—that’s six acres of vineyard!” said the uncle. “Ay, she’s a pretty one, gentle as a lamb, well made and active, and obedient as a kitten. She were the light o’ my poor brother’s eyes.”

“I will pay a year in advance,” observed the doctor.

“Bless me! say two years, and I’ll leave her with you; she’ll be better off with you than with us; my wife beats her; there’s none but I to stand up for her, and the little saint of a creature is as innocent as a new-born babe.”

When he heard “innocent,” the doctor, struck, made a sign to the uncle and took him into the courtyard and garden, leaving the Rabouilleuse at the table with Fanchette and Jean-Jacques, who questioned her and to whom she naively related her meeting with the doctor.

“There now, my little darling, good-by,” said Uncle Brazier, kissing Flore; “you can say I’ve made your happiness leaving you with this kind and worthy father of the poor; you must obey him as you would me. Be a good girl and behave nicely and do everything he tells you.”

“Get the room over mine ready,” said the doctor to Fanchette. “Little Flore—I am sure she’s worthy of the name—will sleep there. To-morrow we’ll send for a shoemaker and dressmaker. Put another plate on the table; she shall keep us company.”

That evening all Issoudun talked of the sudden appearance of the little rabouilleuse in Doctor Rouget’s house; in that satirical region the nickname stuck to Mademoiselle Brazier before, during, and after her good fortune.

The doctor likely intended something for Flore similar in miniature to Louis XV’s conduct with Mademoiselle de Romans, but he was too late and too old. From twelve to fourteen the charming Rabouilleuse lived a life of unmixed happiness. Well dressed and often finer than Issoudun’s richest girls, she sported a gold watch and jewels given by the doctor to encourage studies, and had a master to teach her to read, write, and cipher. But the peasant life had instilled in Flore a deep repugnance to the bitter cup of knowledge, so the doctor stopped her education at that point. His intentions toward the child, whom he cleansed, clothed, and taught, were interpreted variously by the town’s gossip—fatal in such communities. Provincial minds insist on full explanations and cling to preferred versions; to recognize truth requires impartiality.

“What do you suppose that old gorilla wants at his age with a little girl only fifteen years old?” society still said two years after Flore’s arrival.

“Ah! that’s true,” they answered. “His days of merry-making are long past.”

“My dear fellow, the doctor is disgusted at his son’s stupidity, and he persists in hating his daughter Agathe; he may intend to marry little Flore; suppose she gave him a fine, active boy like Max?” said one.

“Bah! don’t talk nonsense! After such a life as Rouget and Lousteau led from 1770 to 1787, is it likely either would have children at sixty-five? The old villain has read the Scriptures and does as David did in his old age; that’s all.”

“They say Brazier, when drunk, boasts in Vatan that he cheated him,” cried one who always believed the worst.

“Good heavens! neighbor; what won’t they say at Issoudun?”

From 1800 to 1805 the doctor enjoyed educating Flore sans the annoyances that accompany some protégées. The Rabouilleuse was envied by peasant girls within ten miles, although her conduct might be religiously reprehensible. Flore, born in 1787, grew up amid the saturnalias of 1793 and 1798, whose effects reached these country regions then deprived of priests and faith; marriage had become more a legal coupling, and revolutionary maxims left a deep impression. By 1802 Catholic worship was scarcely re-established; the Emperor found it difficult to obtain priests. In 1806 many parishes still lacked clergy, so slowly had the dispersed clergy returned after the Revolution.

In 1802 therefore nothing was likely to reproach Flore Brazier except conscience; and conscience was sure to be weaker than self-interest in Uncle Brazier’s ward. If, as everyone supposed, the cynical doctor was compelled by age to respect a child of fifteen, the Rabouilleuse was still considered very “wide awake,” a local phrase. Some thought she could claim a certificate of innocence from the cessation of the doctor’s care in his last two years, during which he showed her less than coldness.

Old Rouget had killed too many people not to know when his end was near; and his notary, finding him on his death-bed, pressed him to make provision in favor of the young girl, then seventeen.

“So I do,” he said cynically; “my death sets her at liberty.”

This phrase revealed the old man’s nature. Cloaking his evil deeds with witty sayings, he obtained indulgence in a land that applauds wit—especially when tied to self-interest. The notary read in those words a concentrated hatred of a man whose calculations had been balked by Nature, revenging himself upon the innocent object of impotent love. The doctor’s obstinate resolution to leave nothing to the Rabouilleuse—saying with a bitter smile when urged again, “Her beauty will make her rich enough!”—confirmed this opinion.

CHAPTER IX

Jean-Jacques Rouget did not mourn his father, though Flore Brazier did. The old doctor had made his son unhappy since Jean-Jacques came of age in 1791; but he had provided the little peasant girl with the comforts dear to country folk. After the funeral, when Fanchette asked Flore, “Well, what is to become of you, now that monsieur is dead?” Jean-Jacques’s eyes lighted; for the first time his dull face showed feeling.

“Leave the room,” he said to Fanchette, who was clearing the table.

At seventeen Flore retained delicate features and the distinction of beauty that had attracted the doctor, though the embonpoint of a life without toil had begun to appear. Her bust and shoulders were developed; the face’s outline remained faultless and the chin delicate.

“Flore,” Jean-Jacques said in a trembling voice, “you feel at home in this house?”

“Yes, Monsieur Jean.”

As he prepared to declare himself, he fell silent, troubled by recollection of the dead man and uncertain how far his father’s indiscretions might have gone. Flore, unable to suspect his simplicity, waited; Jean-Jacques left her without finishing. It would be long before she understood his character.

At his father’s death, Jacques—then thirty-seven—was as timid and submissive to paternal discipline as a child of twelve. That timidity explains his life. There are kinds of timidity—of mind and of nerves; when both unite a man becomes a cipher. Jean-Jacques’s timidity sprang from a torpor of his faculties, which a strong teacher might have roused. In him, the sense of love had more force than intellect, though he had enough sense for ordinary affairs. Passion, without the romantic ideal youths typically invest in it, only increased his fear. He had never courted any woman in Issoudun: mean stature, awkwardness, a vulgar flattened face, pallid skin and large, prominent light-green eyes made him seem old before his time. A woman’s presence stultified him; desire petrified his speech. Paralyzed between passion and lack of ideas, he sought solitude.

The doctor, too late, saw the havoc of this temperament. He wanted his son married but hesitated because a wife would manage the property; to avoid surrendering control he tried to instill avarice in Jean-Jacques, training him in mechanical habits and fixed ideas about investing revenues. He kept estates in good order and long-leased to spare him management difficulties. But one fact escaped notice: timidity hid Jean-Jacques’s passionate love for the Rabouilleuse. Flore was the only woman in the bachelor’s presence, the light of his paternal home; he secretly contemplated her at all hours and rejoiced in his father’s education of her as making her easier to win. Passion that can reflect gives even ninnies a kind of intelligence; animal instinct persists with the force of thought.

The day after the funeral Flore, expecting a declaration, waited; Jean-Jacques kept near her and glanced with passion but could not speak. At last, during dessert, he managed awkwardly to ask if she liked life there and whether she was honest. Flore, astonished by his timidity yet touched, answered readily. He asked if she was still the barefoot girl her uncle had brought; she blushed. Encouraged, Rouget stumbled, turned red, and could only say, “Are you the same?” She answered on her honor; he took her hand and, unable to say more, declared that she could stay, hinting she should be mistress of the house.

This strange situation lasted three weeks. One night Flore found Jean-Jacques crouched like a dog on the landing; she thought, “He loves me,” and, indulgent, did not mind. At last, during another dessert, Flore told him the truth about his father: the old man had been a worthy man and a joker, but had had some spite and might have meant harm to Jean-Jacques. Rouget, taking her hand, said, “If your father was nothing to you you can stay here with me, can’t you?” She admitted she would if he wished it. He confessed he had always loved her since she came barefoot. Flore made no direct answer at first; when silence became embarrassing, Jean-Jacques employed a practical argument: “Wouldn’t it be better than returning to the fields?” She assented.

Yet he got no further. Men of his nature want certainty; the effort to avow love costs too much and they quail. Ten months after his father’s death Jean-Jacques changed: his leaden face cleared and he breathed happiness. Flore insisted he take better care of himself and watched him go out. The town noticed the transformation.

“Have you heard?” people said. “Jean-Jacques inherits everything, even the Rabouilleuse. She’s sly! She’s very handsome and will make him marry her.” Some thought he ought to marry a woman of family; others praised Flore for devotion to the poor fellow. This is how Flore obtained management of the Rouget household—from father to son, as young Goddet had said. It is necessary to sketch that management for the edification of old bachelors.

Fanchette, the cook, alone thought it wrong that Flore should be queen over Jean-Jacques. She protested with injured virtue but was humiliated by a child mistress. Fanchette owned three hundred francs a year in the Funds and had an annuity from the doctor; she could live without working and left the house nine months after the funeral, April 15, 1806. That date may indicate when Flore ceased to be an honest girl.

Foreseeing Fanchette’s departure, Flore resolved to do without a servant. For six months she had studied Fanchette’s culinary operations. The doctor, trained under Rouelle in Paris, had applied chemistry to cooking and improved provincial techniques. He taught subtleties—how to treat omelettes, the use of a “cagnard,” methods to prevent browned butter’s tartness—secrets honed in his kitchen.

Flore, a born fryer and roaster, soon surpassed Fanchette and became a cordon-bleu with care for Jean-Jacques’s comfort. She rubbed up furniture, kept everything clean, managed the wash and mended linen with thrift. She learned family business, consulted the notary Monsieur Heron, and advised Jean-Jacques well. Two months before the doctor’s death Flore’s uncle Brazier had died after a fall, leaving a wine-shop where he had spent most of his time. Flore had also lost her father; thus she served her master with an orphan’s gratitude.

This period was paradise for poor Jean-Jacques. He acquired gentle habits of an animal, trained into monastic regularity. Flore woke him with breakfast ready; after a morning walk he read papers at three and spent much time at dinner, then went to bed early to save fire and candles—provincial economies that contribute to empty-mindedness.

For nine years their life was regular. Jean-Jacques lent at five percent on first mortgages, with release of the wife’s rights if married, never more than a third of a property’s value, and required notes for an extra two and a half percent—his father’s rules. Usury, that clog on peasant ambition, destroyed rural regions; Rouget’s seven and a half percent levy seemed reasonable and gave him investment choice; notaries reaped commissions.

During these nine years Flore obtained absolute control. At first she treated him familiarly; then, surpassing him in mind and force, she became in fact his superior and he the servant of his servant. He met this mastery halfway by letting her care for him as a son clings to a mother’s protection. Flore kept the house and managed all business; Jean-Jacques left everything to her so completely that life without her seemed impossible. She indulged all his fancies and understood him thoroughly. Her brightness was his chief pleasure; when it clouded his state betrayed her power, which she sometimes exercised. Secure in her power, she did not think to make him marry her.

Towards the close of 1815 Flore, then twenty-seven, had reached full beauty: plump, fresh, white-skinned, the ideal “buxom housewife,” likened to a handsome barmaid of a higher type. In 1816 she saw Maxence Gilet and fell in love at first sight. Maxence, a penniless officer, saw more than a passing fancy in Flore once he learned of her and Jean-Jacques’s positions. He gladly took up abode at the Rougets’, recognizing the old bachelor’s weakness. Flore’s passion affected household affairs: she became sullen and gave Jean-Jacques flashes of temper like a wife suspecting infidelity. He asked why; her eyes were full of hatred and her voice contemptuous.

“You have neither heart nor soul!” she cried. “Sixteen years I have spent my youth in this house, and I have only just found out you have a stone there” (striking her breast). “For two months you have seen that brave captain, a victim of the Bourbons, cut out for a general and down in poverty, hunted into a hole where there’s no way to make a penny! He’s forced to sit on a stool all day in the mayor’s office to earn—what? Six hundred miserable francs—and here are you, with six hundred and fifty-nine thousand well invested, and sixty thousand francs’ income—thanks to me, who never spend more than three thousand a year—and you never think of offering him a home here, though the second floor is empty! You’d rather rats and mice run riot than put a human being there—and he’s a lad your father always allowed to be his own son! Do you want to know what you are? A fratricide! And I know why. You see I take an interest in him, and that provokes you. Well, yes! I do take an interest in him.”

But Jean-Jacques stammered and could only plead ignorance of any such wish. She answered bitterly that there ought to be a man in the house for safety and reputation, and that Max could swallow three burglars as a flash. People might gossip; the proper story would be that his father commanded care of the poor Max. For nine years she had eaten his bread and accepted many offers of court; she had always replied she would not abandon the “poor innocent.” Jean-Jacques, terrified at the thought of losing her, promised to invite Maxence.

He did so politely, before witnesses. A reconciliation took place; but Jean-Jacques noticed signs that Flore’s affections had wholly shifted. For weeks she complained to tradespeople about Rouget’s alleged tyranny in inviting his natural brother. No one was deceived: they saw Flore as clever and artful. Old Rouget, however, found himself comfortable after Max became master of his house; he gained a companion who paid him attention without servility. Gilet talked, discussed politics, and sometimes walked with Rouget. After Max’s arrival Flore stopped cooking, saying it spoiled her hands. Mere Cognette, grand master of idleness, produced a relative, an old maid who had lost a curate master and promised devotion: an excellent cook whose fidelity would be rewarded with an annuity of three hundred francs after ten years. This Vedie, pitted by smallpox and ugly, took over the kitchen.

Flore now adopted the title Madame Brazier. She wore corsets, silk or fine woollen and cotton dresses, expensive neckerchiefs, embroidered caps, collars, lace ruffles and boots. She renewed her youth through richness and elegance, trimming a rough diamond into worth. She wished to honor Max. In 1817 she brought a horse, called English, from Bourges for the weary cavalry captain. Max had found in Issoudun an old lancer of the Imperial Guard, a Pole named Kouski, who, now poor, asked to quarter himself at Rouget’s as the captain’s servant. Max idolized Kouski, especially after the duel with three royalists. Thus from 1817 the household numbered five persons, three of whom were masters, and expenses rose to about eight thousand francs a year.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At the time Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to claim—Maitre Desroches said—an inheritance in jeopardy, Jean-Jacques Rouget had become almost semi-vegetative. After Max’s instalment, Flore set the household on a strict footing. Exposed to better food, Rouget ate more, tempted by Vedie’s excellent cooking; he did not fatten but weakened, fatigued perhaps by digestion, his eyes ringed by dark circles. When neighbors questioned him he always said he had never been better. Thought mentally deficient, people failed to notice his failing faculties. His love for Flore kept him alive; he existed only for her, obeying her slightest look, watching her like a dog. As Madame Hochon remarked, at fifty-seven he seemed older than Monsieur Hochon, an octogenarian.

Everyone supposed, rightly, that Max’s apartment matched his charm. In six years the captain had improved the comfort and adorned it for himself and Flore. But it was only Issoudun luxury—colored tiles, somewhat elegant wallpapers, mahogany furniture, gilt mirrors, muslin curtains with red borders, a canopy bed, provincial draperies—luxury in Issoudun though common in Paris. One small oddity caused talk: rush-matting on the stairs to muffle footsteps. Max came in at daybreak without waking anyone; Rouget did not suspect his guest was an accomplice in the Knights of Idleness’ nocturnal performances.

About eight the next morning Flore, in a cotton dressing-gown with narrow pink stripes, a lace cap, and furred slippers, softly opened Max’s door; seeing him asleep she stood beside the bed.

“He came in so late,” she thought. “Half-past three. He must have a good constitution. I wonder what they did last night.”

“Oh, there you are, my little Flore!” said Max, waking like a soldier trained to be instantly ready.

“You are sleepy; I’ll go away.”

“No, stay; there’s something serious going on.”

“Were you up to mischief last night?”

“Ah, bah! It concerns you and me and that old fool. You never told me he had a family! Well, his family are coming—coming here—no doubt to turn us out.”

“Ah! I’ll shake him well,” said Flore.

“Mademoiselle Brazier,” said Max gravely, “things are too serious for giddiness. Send me my coffee; I’ll take it in bed and think over what we had better do. Come back at nine and we’ll talk. Meanwhile, act as if you’ve heard nothing.”

Frightened, Flore went to make his coffee; a quarter-hour later Baruch burst into Max’s room, crying to the grand master,—

“Fario is hunting for his barrow!”

In five minutes Max was dressed and in the street; though he sauntered, he soon reached the tower embankment, where a crowd had gathered.

“What is it?” asked Max, pushing through and reaching the Spaniard.

Fario was a withered little man, ugly as though blue-blooded grandee. His fiery, close-set eyes were piercing; calm, slow in movement, people called him “goodman Fario.” His gingerbread skin and gentle manner hid a half-Moorish peasant nature from Granada, long unroused from phlegmatic indolence.

“Are you sure,” Max asked after hearing his grievance, “that you left your cart here? There are no thieves in Issoudun.”

“I left it just there—”

“If the horse was harnessed, hasn’t he drawn it somewhere?”

“Here’s the horse,” said Fario, pointing to the animal harnessed thirty feet away.

Max gravely went to where the horse stood, from which one could see the bottom of the tower at the top of the embankment; the crowd followed, as the scoundrel wanted.

“Has anybody thoughtlessly put a cart in his pocket?” cried Francois.

“Turn out your pockets, all of you!” said Baruch.

Laughter rose. Fario swore; with a Spaniard oaths mark high anger.

“Was your cart light?” asked Max.

“Light!” cried Fario. “If those who laugh had it on their feet, their corns would never hurt them again.”

“Well, it must be devilishly light,” answered Max, “for look there!” pointing to the foot of the tower; “it has flown up the embankment.”

All eyes lifted, and for a moment there was uproar in the market-place. Each pointed at the bewitched barrow and talked.

“The devil makes common cause with the inn-keepers,” said Goddet to the astonished Spaniard. “He means to teach you not to leave your cart about in the streets, but to put it in the tavern stables.”

The crowd hooted; Fario was thought a miser.

“Come, my good fellow,” said Max, “don’t lose heart. We’ll go up and see how your barrow got there. Thunder and cannon! we’ll lend you a hand! Come along, Baruch.”

“As for you,” he whispered to Francois, “get the people to stand back, and make sure there is nobody at the foot of the embankment when you see us at the top.”

Fario, Max, Baruch, and three other knights climbed. During the perilous ascent Max and Fario noticed no trace of the barrow’s passage; Fario imagined witchcraft and lost his head. At the top it seemed impossible the cart had got there.

“How shall I ever get it down?” said the Spaniard, whose small eyes for the first time showed fear; his yellow face whitened.

“How?” said Max. “Why, that’s not difficult.”

Taking advantage of Fario’s stupefaction, he raised the barrow by the shafts with his robust arms and prepared to fling it down, calling as it left his grasp, “Look out there, below!”

No accident happened; the crowd, persuaded by Francois and curious, had retired to a distance. The cart dashed to pieces in a very picturesque manner.

“There! you have got it down,” said Baruch.

“Ah, brigands! ah, scoundrels!” cried Fario; “perhaps it was you who brought it up here!”

Max, Baruch, and their comrades laughed at the Spaniard’s rage.

“I wanted to do you a service,” said Max coolly, “and in handling the damned thing I came very near flinging myself after it; and this is how you thank me, is it? What country do you come from?”

“I come from a country where they never forgive,” replied Fario, trembling. “My cart will be the cab in which you shall drive to the devil!—unless,” he suddenly grew meek, “you give me a new one.”

“We will talk about that,” said Max, descending.

At the bottom Max took Fario by the jacket and said,—

“Yes, my good Fario, I’ll give you a magnificent cart, if you will give me two hundred and fifty francs; but I won’t warrant it to go, like this one, up a tower.”

At this jest Fario cooled as though making a bargain.

“Damn it!” he said, “give me the wherewithal to replace my barrow, and it will be the best use you ever made of old Rouget’s money.”

Max turned livid; he raised his fist to strike, but Baruch, knowing the blow would be misdirected, plucked Fario away and whispered,—

“Don’t commit such a folly!”

Called to order, the grand master laughed and said to Fario,—

“If I, by accident, broke your barrow, and you try to slander me, we are quits.”

“Not yet,” muttered Fario. “But I am glad to know what my barrow was worth.”

“Ah, Max, you’ve found your match!” said a spectator not of the Order.

“Adieu, Monsieur Gilet. I haven’t thanked you yet for lending me a hand,” cried the Spaniard, as he kicked his horse and vanished amid hurrahs.

“We will keep the tires of the wheels for you,” shouted a wheelwright who had come to inspect the damage.

One shaft stuck upright in the ground as straight as a tree. Max stood by, pale and thoughtful, annoyed by Fario’s speech. For five days the Spaniard’s barrow was all Issoudun talked of; Goddet said it would travel abroad—indeed the tale reached Berry, and in a week the affair, to Fario’s satisfaction, was the talk of three departments. Because of the Spaniard’s vindictive speech, Max and the Rabouilleuse were whispered about in Issoudun and openly discussed in Bourges, Vatan, Vierzon, and Chateauroux. Maxence Gilet knew how envenomed such comments would become.

“We can’t stop their tongues,” he said. “Ah! I did a foolish thing!”

“Max!” said Francois, taking his arm. “They are coming to-night.”

“They! Who!”

“The Bridaus. My grandmother has had a letter from her goddaughter.”

“Listen, my boy,” said Max low. “I have been thinking deeply. Neither Flore nor I ought to seem opposed to the Bridaus. If these heirs are to be got rid of, it is for you Hochons to drive them out. Find out what sort of people they are. Tomorrow at Mere Cognette’s, after I’ve taken their measure, we can decide what to do, and how to set your grandfather against them.”

“The Spaniard found the flaw in Max’s armor,” said Baruch to his cousin Francois as they turned into Monsieur Hochon’s and watched their comrade entering his own door.

While Max was thus occupied, Flore, despite advice, could not restrain her wrath and burst upon the poor bachelor. When Jean-Jacques incurred her anger, his little attentions and vulgar fondlings—his joy—were suppressed. Flore sent him, as children say, into disgrace. No more tender glances or caressing words—“my kitten,” “my old darling,” “my bibi,” “my rat”—were heard. A cold, sharp, ironic “you” cut through the heart of the miserable old bachelor; the “you” was a declaration of war. Instead of helping him with his toilet, handing what he wanted, forestalling his wishes, or admiring him—“You look as fresh as a rose!”—she left him to dress alone. If he called, she answered from the foot of the stairs, “I can’t do everything at once; how can I look after your breakfast and wait upon you? Are not you big enough to dress yourself?”

“Oh, dear! what have I done to displease her?” he asked that morning, after a rebuff when calling for shaving-water.

“Vedie, take up the hot water,” cried Flore.

“Vedie!” exclaimed the poor man, stupefied. “Vedie, what is the matter with Madame this morning?”

Flore Brazier required her master, Vedie, Kouski, and Max to call her Madame.

“She seems to have heard something about you which isn’t to your credit,” Vedie said, pretending great concern. “I’m only a poor servant and have no right to poke my nose; but I say you may search through all the women and you won’t find the equal of Madame. You ought to kiss the ground she steps on. Goodness! if you make her unhappy, you’ll only spoil your own life. There she is, poor thing, with her eyes full of tears.”

Vedie left the poor man cast down; he dropped into an armchair and stared into vacancy like the melancholy imbecile he was, and forgot to shave. These alternations of tenderness and severity had on this feeble creature, whose only life was through his amorous fibre, the same morbid effect that sudden climatic changes have on the body. It was a moral pleurisy wearing him out like disease. Flore alone could thus affect him; to her and her alone he was as good as foolish.

“Well, haven’t you shaved yet?” she said, appearing at his door.

Her sudden presence made him start; from pale and cast down he grew red, yet dared not complain.

“Your breakfast is waiting,” she added. “You can come down as you are, in dressing-gown and slippers; for you’ll breakfast alone, I can tell you.”

Without waiting, she disappeared. To breakfast alone was his worst punishment; he loved to talk to her as he ate. At the foot of the stairs he was seized with a coughing fit; emotion excited his catarrh.

“Cough away!” said Flore in the kitchen, without caring if he heard. “Confound the old wretch! he is able enough to get over it without bothering others. If he coughs up his soul, it will only be after—”

Such were the amenities the Rabouilleuse addressed to Rouget when angry. The poor man sat in deep distress at a corner of the table and looked at his old furniture and pictures with a disconsolate air.

“You might at least have put on a cravat,” said Flore. “Do you think it pleasant for people to see such a neck as yours, redder and more wrinkled than a turkey’s?”

“But what have I done?” he asked, lifting his big light-green eyes, full of tears, to her and trying to meet her hard countenance.

“What have you done?” she exclaimed. “As if you didn’t know! Your sister Agathe—who is as much your sister as I am sister of the tower of Issoudun, if one’s to believe your father, and who has no claim at all upon you—is coming from Paris with her son, a miserable two-penny painter, to see you.”

“My sister and my nephews coming to Issoudun!” he said, bewildered.

“Oh, yes! play surprised; try to make me believe you didn’t send for them! Don’t fash yourself; we won’t trouble your Parisians—before they set their feet in this house we shall have shaken the dust off ours. Max and I will be gone, never to return. As for your will, I’ll tear it in quarters under your nose and to your very beard—do you hear? Leave your property to your family if you don’t think we are your family; and then see if you’ll be loved for yourself by a lot of people who have not seen you for thirty years—who in fact have never seen you! Is that the sort of sister who can take my place? A pinchbeck saint!”

“If that’s all, my little Flore,” said the old man, “I won’t receive my sister, or my nephews. I swear this is the first I heard of their coming. It is all got up by Madame Hochon—a sanctimonious old—”

Max, who had overheard, entered suddenly and said in a masterful tone,—

“What’s all this?”

“My good Max,” said the old man, glad for the soldier’s protection who by agreement with Flore always took his side, “I swear by all that is most sacred, I now hear this news for the first time. I never wrote to my sister; my father made me promise not to leave her any property; to leave it to the Church sooner than to her. Well, I won’t receive my sister Agathe or her sons—”

“Your father was wrong, my dear Jean-Jacques, and Madame Brazier is still more wrong,” answered Max. “Your father no doubt had his reasons, but he is dead, and his hatred should die with him. Your sister is your sister, and your nephews your nephews. You owe it to yourself to welcome them, and you owe it to us as well. What would people say in Issoudun? Thunder! I’ve enough on my shoulders, without hearing people say we shut you up and don’t allow you a will of your own, or that we influence you against your relations and are trying to get hold of your property. The devil take me if I don’t pull up stakes if calumny is flung at me! the other is bad enough! Let’s eat our breakfast.”

Flore, now mild as a weasel, helped Vedie set the table. Old Rouget, full of admiration for Max, took him by both hands and led him to a window recess, saying low,—

“Ah! Max, if I had a son, I couldn’t love him better than I love you. Flore is right: you two are my real family. You are a man of honor, Max, and what you have just said is true.”

“You ought to receive and entertain your sister and her son, but not change your arrangements about property,” Max said. “In that way you will do right in the world’s eyes and yet keep your promise to your father.”

“Well! my dear loves!” cried Flore gayly, “the salmi is getting cold. Come, my old rat, here’s a wing for you,” she said, smiling on Jean-Jacques.

At the words the long-drawn face lost its cadaverous tints, a smile flickered on his lips; but he had another fit of coughing; joy excited as violent an emotion as punishment. Flore rose, pulled a little cashmere shawl from her shoulders, and tied it round his throat, exclaiming: “How silly to put yourself in such a way about nothing. There, you old goose, that will do you good; it has been next my heart—”

“What a good creature!” said Rouget to Max, while Flore fetched a black velvet cap for the nearly bald head.

“As good as she is beautiful,” answered Max, “but she is quick-tempered, like all people who carry their hearts in their hands.”

This scene, played again and again with variations, is in its coarse way the type of scenes women enact on whatever social rung when interest draws them from obedience and induces them to grasp power. Between Flore Brazier and a duchess, between a duchess and the richest bourgeoise, between a bourgeoise and the luxuriously kept mistress, differences are those of education and surroundings. The pouting of a fine lady is the same as the violence of a Rabouilleuse. At all levels, bitter sayings, ironical jests, cold contempt, hypocritical complaints, false quarrels win as much success as low outbursts of this Madame Everard of Issoudun.

Max began to relate, with humor, Fario and his barrow, making the old man laugh. Vedie and Kouski in the kitchen exploded, and Flore laughed convulsively. After breakfast, while Jean-Jacques read the newspapers (they subscribed to the Constitutionnel and the Pandore), Max carried Flore to his quarters.

“Are you sure he has not made any other will since the one leaving property to you?”

“He hasn’t anything to write with,” she answered.

“He might have dictated it to some notary,” said Max; “we must look out for that. Therefore be cordial to the Bridaus, and at the same time turn those mortgages into money. The notaries will be glad to make the transfers; it is grist to their mill. The Funds are rising; we shall conquer Spain, deliver Ferdinand VII and the Cortez, and they will be above par. You and I could make a good thing by putting the old fellow’s seven hundred and fifty thousand francs into the Funds at eighty-nine. Only you must try to get it done in your name; it will be so much secured anyhow.”

“A capital idea!” said Flore.

“And as there will be an income of fifty thousand francs from eight hundred and ninety thousand, we must make him borrow one hundred and forty thousand francs for two years, to be paid back in two instalments. In two years, we shall get one hundred thousand francs in Paris, and ninety thousand here, and risk nothing.”

“If it were not for you, my handsome Max, what would become of me now?” she said.

“Oh! tomorrow night at Mere Cognette’s, after I have seen the Parisians, I shall find a way to make the Hochons themselves get rid of them.”

“Ah! what a head you’ve got, my angel! You are a love of a man.”

The place Saint-Jean is at the centre of a long street called at the upper end rue Grand Narette, and at the lower rue Petite Narette. “Narette” in Berry expresses a steep street. The Grand Narette rises rapidly from place Saint-Jean to the port Vilatte. The house of old Monsieur Hochon is exactly opposite Jean-Jacques Rouget’s. From Madame Hochon’s usual sitting-room it was easy to see what went on at the Rouget household, and vice versa, when curtains were drawn back or doors left open. The Hochon house resembled the Rouget house; doubtless the same architect built both. Monsieur Hochon, formerly tax-collector at Selles in Berry, born at Issoudun, had returned home and married the sister of the sub-delegate, the gay Lousteau, exchanging his office at Selles for one at Issoudun. Having retired before 1787, he escaped the Revolution’s dangers, to whose principles, however, he adhered like other “honest men” who howl with the winners. Monsieur Hochon had the reputation of miser; a single episode will show him.

At his daughter’s wedding, necessary to give a dinner to the Borniche family, the bridegroom, heir to a large fortune, had mismanaged his property and been mortified by his parents’ refusal to help. The old people were delighted to see Monsieur Hochon step in as guardian to secure his daughter’s dowry. While the marriage contract was solemnly read by young Heron, the notary, the cook came in and asked Monsieur Hochon for some twine to truss the turkey—an essential of the repast. The old man dove into his coat and gave an end of string already used to tie a parcel, and before she left the room called out, “Gritte, mind you give it back to me!” (Gritte is Berry for Marguerite.)

Year by year Hochon grew more petty and penurious; at eighty-five he belonged to men who stop mid-conversation to pick up a pin, remarking as they stick it in their coat sleeve, “There’s the wife’s stipend.” He complained of cloth quality and how his coat had lasted ten years. Tall, gaunt, thin, sallow; saying little, reading little, doing nothing to fatigue himself; observant of forms, he enforced at home strict abstemious discipline, weighing and measuring food and drink for his family: his wife née Lousteau, his grandson Borniche with a sister Adolphine, heirs of old Borniche, and his other grandson Francois Hochon.

Hochon’s eldest son, taken by the 1813 draft which drew sons of well-to-do families into the “guards of honor,” was killed at Hanau. He had married early a rich woman to avoid conscription; after enrolment he wasted his substance, foreseeing his end. His wife, who followed the army at a distance, died at Strasburg in 1814, leaving debts Hochon refused to pay—answering creditors with an axiom: “Women are minors.”

The house, though large, was scantily furnished; on the second floor two rooms suited Madame Bridau and Joseph. Old Hochon repented having kept them with two beds, each with an old armchair of natural wood covered with needlework, and a walnut table with a wide-mouthed water-pitcher in a basin. He kept winter stores of apples, pears, medlars, and quinces on heaps of straw in these rooms where rats and mice ran riot, exhaling a mixed odor of fruit and vermin. Madame Hochon directed everything be cleaned; wallpaper fastened with wafers; she decorated windows with curtains pieced from old hoards. Denied a strip of drugget, she laid her bedside carpet for little Agathe—“Poor little thing!” she said—who was now over forty-seven. Madame Hochon borrowed two night-tables from a neighbor and boldly hired two chests of drawers with brass handles from a second-hand dealer next to Mere Cognette. She had preserved two candlesticks carved in choice woods by her father, who had the “turning” mania; from 1770 to 1780 it was fashionable for rich people to learn a trade, and Monsieur Lousteau had been a turner. These candlesticks were ornamented with circlets from roots of rose, peach, and apricot trees. Madame Hochon actually risked using her relics. These preparations and sacrifices increased old Hochon’s anxiety; until now he had not believed the Bridaus’ arrival.

The morning of the Fario trick Madame Hochon said after breakfast,—

“I hope, Hochon, you will receive my goddaughter, Madame Bridau, properly.” Then, making sure her grandchildren were out of hearing, she added, “I am mistress of my own property; don’t oblige me to make up to Agathe in my will for any incivility on your part.”

“Do you think, madame,” answered Hochon mildly, “that at my age I don’t know forms of decent civility?”

“You know very well what I mean, you crafty old thing! Be friendly to our guests, and remember I love Agathe.”

“And you love Maxence Gilet also, who is getting the property away from your dear Agathe! Ah! you’ve warmed a viper in your bosom; but after all, the Rouget money is bound to go to a Lousteau.”

Having said this about Max and Agathe, Hochon turned to leave; but Madame Hochon, still erect and spare, wearing a round cap with ribbon knots, powdered hair, a taffeta petticoat of changeable colors like a pigeon’s breast, tight sleeves, and high-heeled slippers, placed her snuff-box on a little table and said,—

“Really, Monsieur Hochon, how can a man of your sense repeat absurdities which cost my poor friend her peace and Agathe the property she ought to have had from her father. Max Gilet is not the son of my brother, whom I often advised to save the money he paid for him. You know as well as I do that Madame Rouget was virtue itself—”

“And the daughter takes after her; for she strikes me as uncommonly stupid. After losing all her fortune, she brings her sons up so well that here is one in prison and likely to be indicted before the Court of Peers for a conspiracy worthy of Berton. As for the other, he is worse—a painter. If your protégés stay here till they have extricated that fool Rouget from Gilet and the Rabouilleuse, we shall eat a good deal more than half a measure of salt with them.”

“That’s enough, Monsieur Hochon; you had better wish they may not have two strings to their bow.”

Monsieur Hochon took his hat and his cane with an ivory knob and went away petrified by that speech; he had no idea his wife could show such resolution. Madame Hochon took her prayer-book to read the service; her age prevented daily church attendance; she got there with difficulty on Sundays. Since receiving her goddaughter’s letter she had added a petition to her prayers, supplicating God to open Jean-Jacques Rouget’s eyes, bless Agathe, and prosper the expedition she had drawn her into. Concealing the fact from her grandchildren, whom she accused of being “parpaillots,” she had asked the curate to say a mass for Agathe’s success during a neuvaine held by her granddaughter Adolphine Borniche, who thus made her prayers in church by proxy.

Adolphine, then eighteen, who for seven years had sewn at her grandmother’s side in that cold household of monotony and method, undertook the neuvaine hoping to inspire some feeling in Joseph Bridau, in whom she took an interest because of the monstrosities her grandfather attributed to the young Parisian.

All the old and sensible people of the town and fathers of families approved Madame Hochon’s conduct in receiving her goddaughter; their good wishes for her success matched the secret contempt for Maxence Gilet’s conduct. Thus news of Rouget’s sister and nephew raised two parties in Issoudun—the higher and older bourgeoisie, who offered good wishes and watched without assisting, and the Knights of Idleness and Max’s partisans, who were capable of high-handed outrages against the Parisians.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales office in the place Misere at three o’clock. Madame Bridau, weary but revived by her native town, felt each step recall girlish memories. Word of the Parisians spread instantly through Issoudun; within ten minutes everyone knew they had come. Madame Hochon met her godchild at the door and kissed her like a daughter. After seventy-two years of a barren life, marked by the graves of three unhappy children, she cherished this friendship and took an immediate interest in Bridau. Agathe was led into the salon where Monsieur Hochon sat, cool as a tepid oven.

“Here is Monsieur Hochon; how does he seem to you?” his wife asked.

“Exactly as when I last saw him,” Agathe answered.

“Ah! it is easy to see you come from Paris; you are so complimentary,” the old man sneered.

Introductions followed: Baruch Borniche, twenty-two; Francois Hochon, twenty-four; and little Adolphine, blushing and awkward, anxious not to look at Joseph Bridau, who in turn was closely observed from different angles by the two young men and old Hochon. The miser thought, “He’s just out of the hospital; he’ll be as hungry as a convalescent.” The young men muttered about his brigand-like face and wild expression.

“This is my son, the painter; my good Joseph,” Agathe said, the emphasis betraying a mother’s heart and her thoughts in the Luxembourg prison.

“He looks ill,” said Madame Hochon. “He is not at all like you.”

“No, madame,” Joseph answered with blunt candor; “I am like my father, and very ugly at that.”

Madame Hochon squeezed Agathe’s hand as if to say, “Ah! my child; I understand now why you prefer your good-for-nothing Philippe.” She added aloud that she had heard of Joseph’s talent from the late Madame Descoings. “Talent!” Joseph replied. “Not yet; but with time and patience I may win fame and fortune.”

“By painting?” Monsieur Hochon sneered.

The household bustled: introductions, trunks to the attic chambers, and the town’s curiosity flaring. Baruch rushed into town to announce the Bridau arrival and describe Joseph’s haggard, brigand-like face; that evening Joseph was the talk of every household. The town’s gossip painted him monstrous, dangerous, and capable of anything, and some proposed having their portraits painted to make use of his presence.

After inspecting the bare, damp house—plain walls, staircase and furniture—Joseph felt the sharp fall from poetic Paris to the arid province. Coming down to find Monsieur Hochon slicing bread with Molière’s Harpagon-like parsimony, he thought they should have stayed at an inn. Dinner confirmed his fears: a watery soup, ceremoniously garnished bouilli, separate vegetables counted as parts of the repast, hard-boiled eggs on sorrel, a salad dressed with nut-oil, cups of custard flavored with burnt oats in place of vanilla, butter, radishes, pickled gherkins, horseradish. Madame Hochon nodded contentedly; her husband shrugged as if to say, “See the extravagances you force me to commit!”

After Monsieur Hochon sliced the bouilli into soles-of-dancing-shoe thickness he replaced it with three pigeons. The wine was the 1811 country vintage. Adolphine had decorated the table with flowers. “At Rome as the Romans do,” thought Joseph, and began to eat—like a man who had breakfasted on execrable coffee. When he asked for more bread, Monsieur Hochon unlocked a cupboard and broke off a section of a twelve-pound loaf with the resigned sanctity of a man performing a ritual. Joseph took his half-slice and understood asking for more was forbidden. Conversation continued; Agathe learned her birthhouse had been bought by the Borniches and wished to see it.

Gritte, the sixty-year-old servant, brought dessert—the famous ripe cheese of Touraine and Berry, with mold like vine-leaves—nuts and time-honored biscuits. When asked for fruit she insisted there was none rotten; Joseph laughed, seeing that parsimony had become habit here. “Bah! we can eat them all the same,” he exclaimed, and Monsieur Hochon fetched peaches, pears, and Saint Catherine plums. Adolphine was ordered to gather grapes. Joseph looked at the two young men as if to ask how they owed their healthy faces to such fare; Baruch smiled, both cousins moderate and frequenters of Mere Cognette’s for feasts.

“We will take the liqueur in the salon,” Madame Hochon said, and whispered to Agathe as they left: “This dinner won’t give you indigestion; it is always Lent here; you will get enough just to keep life in you, and no more. So you must bear it patiently.” The old woman’s kindness pleased Joseph. She produced a flask of ratafia she made from a nuns’ recipe and they drank. Agathe remarked the immutable provincial custom made her feel as if her father’s turn had come round.

“Hochon will go to his club presently to read the papers, and we shall have a little time to ourselves,” the old lady said. Ten minutes later three women and Joseph were alone in the salon, where the sparse décor looked exactly as when Agathe left; Monarchy, Revolution, Empire, and Restoration had left no trace in this room. They spoke of life’s trials; Madame Hochon counselled resignation and turned the conversation to the Rouget windows across the street where, she said, the enemy sat.

Adolphine, destined for a cloister, kept looking out the window, eager for more about Maxence Gilet, the Rabouilleuse, and Jean-Jacques. Madame Hochon promised they would have many curious visitors that night. She recounted Maxence’s influence over Rouget and the Rabouilleuse’s doings with all local embroidery. The Borniches, Beaussier, Lousteau-Prangin, Fichet, Goddet-Herau families—fourteen persons in all—were expected.

“It will not be easy to get this property out of the wolf’s jaws,” the old lady said.

“It seems so difficult,” Joseph replied. “A scoundrel such as you represent him, and a daring woman like that crab-girl—defeating them would take a year in Issoudun and mean stooping to meannesses. My mother has only two weeks’ leave; her place is permanent. As for me, in October I have an important commission; my future is in my brushes.”

Madame Hochon, superior to the town but scarcely a believer in painting, glanced at her goddaughter and pressed her hand. Joseph muttered that Maxence was like a second volume of Philippe from Paris—only cleverer and better behaved. Madame Hochon counseled a couple of weeks, judiciously used, could produce results, urging them to heed her advice. Joseph admitted an incapacity for domestic statesmanship.

Mesdames Borniche, Goddet-Herau, Beaussier, Lousteau-Prangin and Fichet, with their husbands, arrived. When the fourteen were seated, Madame Hochon presented Agathe and Joseph. Joseph sat in his armchair all evening, slyly studying the faces which, from five until half-past nine, posed for him gratis. His sarcastic glances and sinister visage left the company ruffled and uneasy.

After ten, when the household slept, Madame Hochon kept Agathe in her chamber until midnight. The two women, secure from interruption, exchanged sorrows. Agathe perceived the immensity of the desert in which this noble, unrecognized soul had been wasted and knew she, despite city-life’s small joys, was not the most unhappy. “You who are so pious,” she said, “explain my shortcomings; tell me what God punishes in me.” “He is preparing us,” the old woman answered, “for the striking of the last hour.”

At midnight the Knights of Idleness assembled under the boulevard Baron trees, whispering. Francois thought Maxence meant merely to give them supper. Baruch feared his grandfather would be alarmed at two extra mouths to feed. Maxence appeared and suggested they go to Mere Cognette’s. The cry “To Mere Cognette’s!” rang through the town like troops rushing. A supper worthy of La Cognette brightened the faces of twenty-two guests; the whole Order was present. At two, after the feast, Max rose to speak.

“My dear fellows! the honor of your grand master was grossly attacked this morning after our memorable joke with Fario’s cart—attacked by a vile peddler, and what is more, a Spaniard (oh, Cabrera!); and I have resolved to make the scoundrel feel my vengeance—always, of course, within our rules. After reflecting, I have a trick worth execution—a famous trick that will drive him crazy. While avenging the insult, we shall feed the sacred animals of the Egyptians—little beasts unjustly persecuted. Thus good is the child of evil. I order you all, under pain of displeasing your humble grand master, to procure clandestinely, each, twenty rats, male or female. Collect them within three days. Keep the rodents without food so they are ravenous. I will accept house-mice and field-mice as rats. Twenty-two times twenty gives four hundred; four hundred let loose in the old Capuchin church where Fario stores his grain will eat a not insignificant quantity! Be lively; Fario delivers most grain soon, and I am determined that Spaniard shall find a terrible deficit. I have not the merit of this invention,” Max added; “render to Caesar that which is Caesar’s. My scheme reproduces Samson’s foxes; but Samson was an incendiary, no philanthropist; we, like Brahmins, protect a persecuted race. Mademoiselle Flore Brazier has set mouse-traps, and Kouski hunts field-mice. I have spoken.”

Goddet boasted he knew where to find an animal worth forty rats; others proposed squirrels, a monkey, or pigeons to attract thousands. Max named Beaussier director of the pigeon scheme and appointed commissariat for the Army of Rats. They resolved to be secretive—turn soles of shoes backward, make any watchman drunk—and Max offered his shotgun or his horse as prize for anyone who devised a trick against the Parisians that would get them into trouble with the Hochons or force them away without harming the venerable ancestors of his two friends. Thus twenty brains set to work to plot against Agathe and her son.

Next morning Agathe and Joseph came down before the second breakfast at ten. In Hochon’s household the first breakfast meant milk and bread and butter taken in bed or on rising. While waiting for Madame Hochon, Joseph noticed Jean-Jacques Rouget planted at his door across the street. Agathe could hardly recognize her brother, so altered was he.

“That is your brother,” said Adolphine.

“What an idiot he looks like!” Joseph exclaimed.

Agathe clasped her hands and looked to heaven. “What a state they have driven him to! Good God! can that be a man only fifty-seven?” She saw Flore Brazier standing behind him—hair dressed, snowy shoulders and a dazzling bosom through a gauze neckerchief trimmed with lace; her grenadine dress had leg-of-mutton sleeves and a gold chain rippled over her bosom as she leaned forward to give Jean-Jacques his black silk cap—the scene evidently staged.

“Hey!” cried Joseph. “There’s a fine woman, and a rare one! She’s made, as they say, to paint. What flesh-tints! Oh, the lovely tones! what surface! what curves! Ah, those shoulders! She’s a magnificent caryatid. What a model she would have been for one of Titian’s Venuses!”

Adolphine and Madame Hochon thought him talking Greek; Agathe signed behind his back that she was used to such jargon.

“So you think a creature who is depriving you of your property handsome?” Madame Hochon asked.

“That doesn’t prevent her from being a splendid model—just plump enough not to spoil the hips and contour,” Joseph replied.

“My son, you are not in your studio,” said Agathe. “Adolphine is here.”

“Ah, true! I did wrong. But you must remember that since leaving Paris I’ve seen nothing but ugly women—”

“My dear godmother,” Agathe said hastily, “how shall I meet my brother if that creature is always with him?”

“Bah!” said Joseph. “I’ll go and see him myself. I don’t think him such an idiot now I find he has the sense to rejoice his eyes with a Titian’s Venus.”

“If he were not an idiot,” said Monsieur Hochon, who had come in, “he would have married long ago and had children; and then you would have no chance at the property. It is an ill wind that blows no good.”

“Your son’s idea is very good,” said Madame Hochon; “he ought to pay the first visit. He can make his uncle understand that if you call there he must be alone.”

“That will affront Mademoiselle Brazier,” said old Hochon. “No, no, madame; swallow the pill. If you can’t get the whole property, secure a small legacy.”

The Hochons, though not clever enough to match Max, were shrewd in their own way. In the middle of breakfast Kouski brought a letter from Monsieur Rouget to his sister. Monsieur Hochon read it aloud:

My dear Sister,—I learn from strangers of your arrival in Issoudun. I can guess the reason which made you prefer Monsieur and Madame Hochon’s house to mine; but if you will come to see me you shall be received as you ought. I should certainly pay you the first visit if my health did not compel me to keep the house; for which I offer my affectionate regrets. I shall be delighted to see my nephew, whom I invite to dine with me tomorrow—young men are less sensitive than women about the company. It will please me if Messrs. Baruch Borniche and Francois Hochon will accompany him.

Your affectionate brother,

J.-J. Rouget.

“Say we are at breakfast, but Madame Bridau will send an answer presently, and the invitations are all accepted,” said Monsieur Hochon to the servant. When the street-door closed, he threw one of his slyest looks at his wife and Agathe, remarking, “He is just as capable of writing that note as I am of giving away twenty-five louis; it is the soldier who corresponds with us!” Madame Hochon, fearful of leaving Agathe a legacy that might fall to him, shared the desire to see the matter decided soon. An enormous fortune was at stake; Monsieur Hochon’s keen intelligence, lulled by provincial monotony, was roused. Madame Hochon was pleasantly surprised to find such an able auxiliary on the Bridau side.

Towards midday the foursome—Monsieur and Madame Hochon, Agathe and Joseph—drafted an answer, designed to counter Max and Flore:

My dear Brother,—If I have stayed away from Issoudun, and kept up no intercourse with any one, not even with you, the fault lies not merely with the strange and false ideas my father conceived about me, but with the joys and sorrows of my life in Paris; for if God made me a happy wife, he has also deeply afflicted me as a mother. You are aware that my son, your nephew Philippe, lies under accusation of a capital offence for his devotion to the Emperor. Therefore you can hardly be surprised if a widow, compelled to take a humble situation in a lottery-office for a living, should come to seek consolation among those among whom she was born.

The profession adopted by the son who accompanies me is one that requires great talent, many sacrifices, and prolonged studies before any results can be obtained. Glory for an artist precedes fortune; is not that to say that Joseph, though he may bring honor to the family, will still be poor? Your sister, my dear Jean-Jacques, would have borne in silence the penalties of paternal injustice, but you will pardon a mother for reminding you that you have two nephews; one who carried the Emperor’s orders at Montereau and served in the Guard at Waterloo, now in prison for his devotion; the other, from his thirteenth year, has been impelled by natural gifts to enter a difficult though glorious career.

I thank you for your letter, my dear brother, with heartfelt warmth, for my own sake and Joseph’s, who will certainly accept your invitation. Illness excuses everything, and I shall therefore go to see you in your own house. A sister is always at home with a brother, whatever life he has adopted.

I embrace you tenderly.

Agathe Rouget

“There’s the matter started. Now, when you see him,” Monsieur Hochon told Agathe, “you must speak plainly to him about his nephews.”

Gritte returned ten minutes later with the town’s gossip. “Since yesterday Madame has had the whole house cleaned up,” she reported. “That’s what they call the Rabouilleuse. She left the salon and Monsieur Rouget’s part of the house in a pitiable state; but since yesterday the rooms have been made like before Monsieur Maxence moved in. La Vedie says Kouski rode off at five and came back at nine with provisions. It’s going to be a grand dinner—a dinner fit for the archbishop of Bourges! Monsieur pokes into everything. He says, ‘I want to do honor to my nephew,’ and he is delighted to find his sister so amiable. Oh! she had on such a dress! Two diamonds in her ears—Vedie says three thousand francs apiece; such lace! rings and bracelets! you’d think she was a shrine; and a silk dress like an altar cloth. She had little black satin slippers and stockings marvels—flowers in silk and openwork—and a lovely little apron Vedie says cost more than two years’ wages.”

“Well done! We shall have to dress up,” laughed Joseph.

“What do you think of all this, Monsieur Hochon?” asked Madame Hochon after Gritte left.

She made Agathe observe her husband, who sat with his head in his hands, elbows on chair-arms, plunged in thought. “You have to do with a Maitre Bonin!” the old man said at last. “With your ideas, young man,” he added to Joseph, “you haven’t force enough to struggle with a practised scoundrel like Maxence Gilet. No matter what I say, you will commit some folly. But tell me everything you see, hear, and do to-night. Go, and God be with you! Try to get alone with your uncle. If, despite your genius, you can’t manage it, that in itself will throw light on their scheme. But if you do get a moment alone, out of earshot, damn it, pull the wool from his eyes about the situation those two have put him in, and plead your mother’s cause.”

At four o’clock Joseph crossed the avenue of weak lindens from the Rouget house to the Hochon house. Kouski announced him. The table was set in the large hall; Joseph, who recognized his uncle with ease, went up, kissed him, and bowed to Flore and Max.

“We have not seen each other since I came into the world, my dear uncle,” the painter gayly announced.

“You are very welcome, my friend,” said the old man, looking at his nephew in a dull way.

“Madame,” Joseph said to Flore with an artist’s vivacity, “this morning I was envying my uncle the pleasure of admiring you every day.”

“Isn’t she beautiful?” the old man murmured; his dim eyes brightened.

“Beautiful enough to be the model of a great painter.”

“Nephew,” said Rouget, nudged by Flore, “this is Monsieur Maxence Gilet; he served the Emperor, like your brother, in the Imperial Guard.”

Joseph rose and bowed.

“Your brother was in the dragoons, I believe,” Maxence said. “I was only a dust-trotter.”

“On foot or on horseback,” said Flore, “you both risked your skins.”

Joseph and Max inspected one another. Max, a dandy with an eager face and his father’s gray eyes, gave Joseph the impression—through the artist’s eye—that the household promised tedious company but handsome compensation in Flore; he called it a triangular household.

At that instant Baruch and François entered.

“Have you seen the tower of Issoudun?” Flore asked Joseph. “No? Then, if you’d like a walk before dinner in an hour, we’ll show you the great curiosity of the town.”

“Gladly,” said the artist, incapable of seeing any impropriety.

While Flore went to put on her bonnet, gloves, and shawl, Joseph jumped up to look at the pictures.

“Ah! you have pictures, indeed, uncle!” he cried at one that struck him.

“They came to us from the Descoings, who bought them during the Revolution, when the convents and churches in Berry were dismantled,” the old man answered.

Joseph was lost in admiration.

“Magnificent! Oh, what painting! That fellow didn’t spare his canvas. Better and better, as at Nicolet’s—”

“There are seven or eight very large ones up in the garret, kept on account of the frames,” said Gilet.

“Let me see them!” cried the artist; Max took him upstairs.

Joseph returned wildly enthusiastic. Max whispered to the Rabouilleuse, who drew the old man to a window embrasure while Joseph heard her say, “Your nephew is a painter; you don’t care for those pictures; be kind, and give them to him.”

“It seems,” said Jean-Jacques, leaning on Flore’s arm to reach Joseph by an Albano, “—it seems that you are a painter—”

“Only a ‘rapin,’” said Joseph.

“What may that be?” asked Flore.

“A beginner,” replied Joseph.

“Well,” continued Jean-Jacques, “if these pictures can be of any use to you in your business, I give them to you,—but without the frames. Oh! the frames are gilt, and besides, they are very funny; I will put—”

“Well done, uncle!” cried Joseph, enchanted; “I’ll make you copies of the same dimensions, which you can put into the frames.”

“But that will take your time, and you will want canvas and colors,” said Flore. “You will have to spend money. Come, Père Rouget, offer your nephew a hundred francs for each copy; here are twenty-seven pictures, and I think there are eleven very big ones in the garret which ought to cost double,—call the whole four thousand francs. Oh, yes,” she added, turning to Joseph, “your uncle can well afford to pay you four thousand francs for the copies, since he keeps the frames—but bless me! you’ll want frames; and they say frames cost more than pictures; there’s more gold on them. Answer, monsieur.” She shook the old man’s arm. “Hein? It isn’t dear; your nephew will take four thousand francs for new pictures in place of the old ones.”

“Well, nephew, I will pay you four thousand francs for the copies—”

“No, no!” said the honest Joseph; “four thousand francs and the pictures, that’s too much; the pictures, don’t you see, are valuable—”

“Accept, simpleton!” said Flore; “he is your uncle, you know.”

“Very good, I accept,” said Joseph, bewildered; for he had recognized a Perugino.

The artist beamed as he went out with the Rabouilleuse on his arm, which suited Maxence’s plans. Neither Flore, nor Rouget, nor Max, nor anyone in Issoudun knew the true value of the pictures, and Max reckoned he had secured Flore’s triumph cheaply as she paraded the town, leaning on the master’s nephew. People flocked to their doors to see the crab-girl’s triumph. This sensation helped Max; when they returned at five, everyone talked of the cordial understanding between Max, Flore, and the nephew. The incident of the pictures and the four thousand francs circulated. The dinner, with Lousteau, one of the court judges, and the Mayor, was splendid and lasted five hours. By nine o’clock, at dessert, Joseph, seated opposite his uncle and between Flore and Max, had fraternized with the soldier and thought him a good fellow. Joseph returned home at eleven somewhat tipsy. Rouget was carried to bed dead-drunk; he had soaked up the wine like sand.

“Well,” Max said to Flore when alone, “isn’t this better than making faces at them? The Bridaus are well received, given presents, smothered with attentions; they will sing our praises and leave us in peace. Tomorrow you and I and Kouski will take down the pictures and send them over to the painter; we’ll put the frames in the garret and cover the walls with one of those varnished papers representing scenes from Telemachus.”

“Oh, that will be much prettier!” said Flore.

Next morning Joseph did not wake till midday. From bed he saw the pictures, brought in while he slept, leaning against the opposite wall. While he examined them anew, recognizing each masterpiece and searching for signatures, his mother had gone to see and thank her brother, urged by old Hochon, who, having heard of the painter’s follies the night before, despaired of the Bridau cause.

“Your adversaries have the cunning of foxes,” he said to Agathe. “In all my days I never saw a man carry things with such a high hand as that soldier; they say war educates young men! Joseph has let himself be fooled. They shut his mouth with wine, those miserable pictures, and four thousand francs! Your artist hasn’t cost Maxence much!”

The long-headed old man instructed Madame Bridau about a line of conduct: enter into Maxence’s ideas, cajole Flore, set up intimacy, and thus obtain a few moments’ interview with Jean-Jacques alone. Madame Bridau was warmly received by her brother, to whom Flore had taught her lesson. The old man was ill from his excesses. Agathe, under the circumstances, could scarcely at once speak of family matters; Max thought it proper and magnanimous to leave brother and sister alone. The calculation worked: poor Agathe found her brother so ill she would not deprive him of Madame Brazier’s care.

“Besides,” she said to the old bachelor, “I wish to know a person to whom I am grateful for the happiness of my brother.”

These words pleased the old man, who rang for Madame Flore. Flore, not far off, showed servile attentions and tenderness to her master, fussing over him like a new bride, and the poor creature received it with feeling.

“We owe you much gratitude, mademoiselle,” said Agathe, “for the attachment you have long shown my brother, and for the way you watch over his happiness.”

“That is true, my dear Agathe,” said the old man; “she has taught me what happiness is; she is a woman of excellent qualities.”

“And therefore, my dear brother, you ought to have recompensed Mademoiselle by making her your wife. Yes! I am too sincere in my religion not to wish to see you obey the precepts of the church. You would each be more tranquil in mind if you were not at variance with morality and the laws. I have come here, dear brother, to ask for help in my affliction; but do not suppose that we wish to make any remonstrance as to the manner in which you may dispose of your property—”

“Madame,” said Flore, “we know how unjust your father was to you. Monsieur here can tell you,” she went on, looking at Jean-Jacques, “that the only quarrels we have ever had were about you. I have always told him that he owes you part of the fortune he received from his father, and your father, my benefactor,—for he was my benefactor.” She added tears: “I shall ever remember him! But your brother, madame, has listened to reason.”

“Yes,” said the old man, “when I make my will you shall not be forgotten.”

“Don’t talk of these things, my dear brother; you do not yet know my nature.”

After such a beginning, it is easy to imagine how the visit went on. Rouget invited his sister to dinner on the next day but one.

During these three days the Knights of Idleness captured an immense quantity of rats and mice, kept them half-famished, then let four hundred and thirty-six into a grain storage one night, including some breeding mothers. They also put a dozen pigeons into the old church roof. These animals revelled while the watchman, enticed away by drink, took no care of his master’s property.

Madame Bridau believed, contrary to old Hochon, that her brother had as yet made no will; she intended to ask about Mademoiselle Brazier as soon as she could take a walk with him alone—a hope Flore and Maxence constantly dangled and disappointed.

Meantime the Knights searched for a way to put the Parisians to flight, finding none practicable.

At the end of a week—half the time the Parisians were to stay—the Bridaus had made no progress. “Your lawyer does not understand the provinces,” old Hochon told Madame Bridau. “What you have come to do can’t be done in two weeks, nor in two years; you ought never to leave your brother, but live here and try to give him some ideas of religion. You cannot countermine the fortifications of Flore and Maxence without getting a priest to sap them. That is my advice, and it is high time to set about it.”

“You certainly have very singular ideas about the clergy,” said Madame Hochon to her husband.

“Bah!” the old man exclaimed, “that’s just like you pious women.”

“God would never bless an enterprise undertaken in a sacrilegious spirit,” said Madame Bridau. “Use religion for such a purpose! Why, we should be more criminal than Flore.”

This conversation took place at breakfast with François and Baruch listening.

“Sacrilege!” exclaimed old Hochon. “If some good abbé, keen as I have known many, knew what a dilemma you are in, he would not think it sacrilege to bring your brother’s lost soul back to God, force him to send away the woman who causes the scandal (with a proper provision, of course), and show him how to set his conscience at rest by giving a few thousand francs a year to the seminary and leaving his property to the rightful heirs.”

The passive obedience the old miser exacted from his children and grandchildren prevented Baruch and François from showing surprise, but they exchanged glances about how dangerous such a scheme would be to Max’s interest.

“The fact is, madame,” said Baruch, “that if you want to secure your brother’s property, the only sure way will be to stay in Issoudun for the necessary length of time—”

“Mother,” said Joseph hastily, “you had better write to Desroches about all this. As for me, I ask nothing more than what my uncle has already given me.”

After recognizing the great value of his thirty-nine pictures, Joseph had carefully unnailed the canvases, covered them with paper, gummed the edges, and laid them one above another in an enormous wooden box, which he sent to Desroches by the carrier’s wagon, proposing to write him a letter by post. The precious freight had been sent off the night before.

“You are satisfied with a pretty poor bargain,” said Monsieur Hochon.

“I can easily get a hundred and fifty thousand francs for those pictures,” replied Joseph.

“Painter’s nonsense!” exclaimed old Hochon.

“Mother,” said Joseph, “I am going to write to Desroches and explain the state of things here. If he advises you to remain, you had better do so. As for your situation, we can always find another like it.”

“My dear Joseph,” said Madame Hochon, following him from the table, “I don’t know anything about your uncle’s pictures, but they ought to be good, judging by the places from which they came. If they are worth only forty thousand francs—a thousand apiece—tell no one. Though my grandsons are discreet, they might, without intending harm, speak of this windfall; it would be known all over Issoudun; and it is very important that our adversaries should not suspect it. You behave like a child!”

In fact, before evening many persons in Issoudun, including Max, had heard this estimate, which caused a search for old paintings and the appearance of many execrable daubs. Max repented having driven Rouget into giving away the pictures, and his rage against the heirs, after hearing old Hochon’s ecclesiastical scheme, increased. The influence of religion on such a feeble creature as Rouget was the one fear. The news decided Maxence to turn Rouget’s investments into money and to borrow on his landed property to buy into the Funds as soon as possible; but he considered it even more important to get rid of the Parisians at once.

Flore, by Max’s advice, pretended Monsieur was too feeble to take walks and ought to have a carriage. This pretext avoided inquiry when they went to Bourges, Vierzon, Châteauroux, Vatan, and other places where withdrawing investments obliged Max and Flore to be with Rouget. At the close of the week, all Issoudun was amazed that the old man had gone to Bourges to buy a carriage—a step the Knights regarded as favorable to the Rabouilleuse. Flore and Max selected a hideous “berlingot,” twenty-two years old, a sort of demi-fortune carriage. It only cost four hundred and fifty francs; Max bought a stout mare and repainted the carriage dark brown, buying a tolerable harness at a bargain. The town awaited Pere Rouget’s equipage; at its first appearance every household was on its doorstep.

The second time the old bachelor went out he drove to Bourges, went before a notary, and signed a power of attorney in favor of Maxence Gilet to make all the transfers enumerated. Flore reserved to herself the business of making Monsieur sell investments in Issoudun and its neighborhood. The principal notary in Bourges was requested by Rouget to get him a loan of one hundred and forty thousand francs on his landed estate. Nothing was known at Issoudun of these secret proceedings. Maxence, a good rider, went with his horse to Bourges and back between five in the morning and five in the afternoon. Flore never left the old bachelor. Rouget consented without objection to the actions Flore dictated; but he insisted that the investment in the Funds producing fifty thousand francs a year should stand in Flore’s name as holding a life-interest only, and in his as owner of the principal. His tenacity in this domestic dispute worried Max; he thought it the result of reflections inspired by seeing the natural heirs.

Amid these movements, which Max concealed, he forgot the Spaniard and his granary. Fario returned to Issoudun to deliver his corn after various trips to raise the price of cereals. The morning after his arrival he noticed the church roof black with pigeons. He hurried to the storehouse, where he found half his grain devoured. Thousands of mice and rat marks showed further ruin. The church was a Noah’s ark. But anger turned the Spaniard white when he found the grain at the bottom sprouting from water introduced by tin tubes into the pile. The pigeons and rats could be instinct, but the wetting showed human hand.

Fario sat on the chapel steps with his head between his hands. After half an hour he spied the squirrel Goddet had sent him playing on a cross-beam. He rose, turned to his watchman with a calm face, made no complaint, but hired laborers to gather sound grain and spread the moist to dry, saving as much as possible; after estimating his losses at about three fifths, he attended to filling his orders. But his previous manipulations had raised the price of cereals, and he lost on the three fifths he had to buy to fill orders; his losses were really over half. The Spaniard, with no enemies, at once attributed this revenge to Gilet. He was convinced Maxence and others were the authors of the nocturnal mischief and intended to ruin him. It was a matter of over three thousand francs—nearly his whole capital since the peace. Driven by the desire for vengeance, the man displayed the persistence of a detective. Hiding at night in different parts of Issoudun, he soon acquired proof of the Knights’ proceedings: he saw them, counted them, watched their rendezvous, and knew of their suppers at Mère Cognette’s; he lay in wait, witnessed their deeds, and thus became well informed of their nocturnal habits.

In spite of Max’s journeys and preoccupations, he did not neglect his nightly employments—first, because he did not wish his comrades to suspect his operations with Père Rouget’s property; second, to keep the Knights in hand. They were convened for a prank to be talked of for years: poisoned meat was to be thrown to every watchdog in town and environs. Fario overheard them congratulating each other after a supper on the probable success of this massacre and laughing over the general mourning it would cause.

“It will make people forget Fario’s cart,” said Goddet.

Fario needed no further proof; his mind was made up.

After three weeks in Issoudun, Agathe and Madame Hochon were convinced of the old miser’s observation that it would take years to destroy Max and Flore’s influence. She had made no progress in Jean-Jacques’s confidence and was never left alone with him. On the other hand, Mademoiselle Brazier triumphed openly by taking Agathe to drive in the caleche, sitting beside her on the back seat while Monsieur Rouget and his nephew occupied the front. Mother and son impatiently awaited an answer to the confidential letter they had written to Desroches. The day before the night on which the dogs were to be poisoned, Joseph, nearly bored to death in Issoudun, received two letters: one from the great painter Schinner and the second from Desroches.

Schinner’s letter, from Beaumont-sur-Oise, said Joseph was recommended for the chateau de Presles for the Comte de Serizy; Schinner was off to Italy; Joseph should take his place at the Salon if need be. Desroches’s letter gave practical advice:

My dear Joseph,—Your Monsieur Hochon strikes me as an old man full of common-sense, and you give me a high idea of his methods; he is perfectly right. My advice, since you ask it, is that your mother should remain at Issoudun with Madame Hochon, paying a small board—say four hundred francs a year—to reimburse her hosts. Madame Bridau ought, in my opinion, to follow Monsieur Hochon’s advice in everything; for your excellent mother will have many scruples in dealing with persons who have no scruple at all, and whose behavior to her is a masterstroke of policy. That Maxence, you are right enough, is dangerous. He is another Philippe, but of a different calibre. The scoundrel makes his vices serve his fortunes, and gets his amusement gratis; whereas your brother’s follies are never useful to him. All that you say alarms me, but I could do no good by going to Issoudun. Monsieur Hochon, acting behind your mother, will be more useful to you than I. As for you, you had better come back here; you are good for nothing in a matter which requires continual attention, careful observation, servile civilities, discretion in speech, and a dissimulation of manner and gesture wholly against the grain of artists.

If they have told you no will has been made, you may be sure they have one. But wills can be revoked, and as long as your uncle lives he is susceptible of being worked upon by remorse and religion. Your inheritance will be the result of a combat between the Church and the Rabouilleuse. There will come a time when that woman will lose her grip and religion will be all-powerful. So long as your uncle makes no gift of the property during his lifetime, and does not change his estate, all may come right when religion gets the upper hand. For this reason you must beg Monsieur Hochon to keep an eye on the condition of your uncle’s property. It is necessary to know if the real estate is mortgaged, and if so, where and in whose name the proceeds are invested. It is so easy to terrify an old man with fears about his life, in case you find him despoiling his own property, that almost any heir with a little adroitness could stop the spoliation at its outset. But how should your mother, with ignorance of the world and religious ideas, manage such an affair? I cannot throw more light. All you have done probably gave the alarm, and your adversaries may already have secured themselves—

“That is what I call an opinion in good shape,” exclaimed Monsieur Hochon, proud of being appreciated by a Parisian lawyer.

“Oh! Desroches is a famous fellow,” answered Joseph.

“It would be well to read that letter to the two women,” said the old man.

“There it is,” said Joseph, giving it to him; “as to me, I want to be off to-morrow; and I am now going to say good-by to my uncle.”

“Ah!” said Monsieur Hochon, “I see that Monsieur Desroches tells you in a postscript to burn the letter.”

“You can burn it after showing it to my mother,” said the painter.

Joseph dressed, crossed the little square, and called on his uncle, who was finishing breakfast. Max and Flore were at table.

“Don’t disturb yourself, my dear uncle; I have only come to say good-by.”

“You are going?” said Max, exchanging glances with Flore.

“Yes; I have work at the chateau of Monsieur de Serizy, and I am all the more glad because his arm is long enough to do a service to my poor brother in the Chamber of Peers.”

“Well, well, go and work,” said old Rouget, with a silly air. Joseph thought him extraordinarily changed within a few days. “Men must work—I am sorry you are going.”

“Oh! my mother will be here some time longer,” remarked Joseph.

Max made a movement with his lips which Flore observed, meaning: “They are going to try the plan Baruch warned me of.”

“I am very glad I came,” said Joseph, “for I have had the pleasure of making your acquaintance and you have enriched my studio—”

“Yes,” said Flore, “instead of enlightening your uncle on the value of his pictures, which is now estimated at over one hundred thousand francs, you have packed them off in a hurry to Paris. Poor dear man! he is no better than a baby! We have just been told of a little treasure at Bourges,—what did they call it? a Poussin,—which was in the choir of the cathedral before the Revolution and is now worth, all by itself, thirty thousand francs.”

“That was not right of you, my nephew,” said Jean-Jacques, at a sign from Max, which Joseph could not see.

“Come now, frankly,” said the soldier, laughing, “on your honor, what should you say those pictures were worth? You’ve made an easy haul out of your uncle! and right enough, too—uncles are made to be pillaged. Nature deprived me of uncles, but damn it, if I’d had any I should have shown them no mercy.”

“Did you know, monsieur,” said Flore to Rouget, “what your pictures were worth? How much did you say, Monsieur Joseph?”

“Well,” answered the painter, red as a beetroot,—“the pictures are certainly worth something.”

“They say you estimated them to Monsieur Hochon at one hundred and fifty thousand francs,” said Flore; “is that true?”

“Yes,” said the painter, with childlike honesty.

“And did you intend,” said Flore to the old man, “to give a hundred and fifty thousand francs to your nephew?”

“Never, never!” cried Jean-Jacques, on whom Flore had fixed her eye.

“There is one way to settle all this,” said the painter, “and that is to return them to you, uncle.”

“No, no, keep them,” said the old man.

“I shall send them back to you,” said Joseph, wounded by Max and Flore’s offensive silence. “There is something in my brushes which will make my fortune, without owing anything to any one, even an uncle. My respects to you, mademoiselle; good-day, monsieur—”

Joseph crossed the square in a state of irritation artists can imagine. The entire Hochon family were in the salon. Seeing Joseph gesticulate and talking to himself, they asked him what was the matter. The painter, as open as the day, related before Baruch and François the scene; two hours later it was the town’s talk, embroidered with various circumstances. Some persons insisted the painter was maltreated by Max; others that he had misbehaved to Flore and been turned out.

“What a child your son is!” said Hochon to Madame Bridau; “the booby is the dupe of a scene they have been keeping for the last day of his visit. Max and the Rabouilleuse have known the value of those pictures for the last two weeks—ever since he foolishly told it before my grandsons, who blurted it out to all the world. Your artist had better have taken himself off without taking leave.”

“My son has done right to return the pictures if they are really so valuable,” said Agathe.

“If they are worth, as he says, two hundred thousand francs,” said old Hochon, “it was folly to put himself in a way to be obliged to return them. You might have had that, at least, out of the property; whereas, as things are going now, you won’t get anything. And this scene with Joseph is almost a reason why your brother should refuse to see you again.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Between midnight and one, the Knights of Idleness began their nightly rounds distributing food to the town’s dogs, an expedition that lasted until three when they supped at Cognette’s. At half-past four they crept home. As Max turned from the rue l’Avenier into the Grande rue, Fario, lurking in a recess, struck him in the heart, withdrew the blade, and fled toward Vilatte, wiping the knife on his handkerchief. The Spaniard washed the handkerchief in the Riviere forcee and returned quietly to his lodgings at Saint-Paterne, entering by an open window and going to bed; later a watchman found him asleep.

Max uttered a fearful cry as he fell. Lousteau-Prangin, the judge’s son and distant relation of the sub-delegate, and young Goddet of the lower Grande rue ran up, shouting, “They are killing Max! Help!” The town, used to false alarms, remained mostly asleep. They found him fainting and roused Monsieur Goddet, the surgeon. Max had recognized Fario; but when he revived among bystanders and felt the wound was not mortal, he seized the chance to make capital of the attack and whispered, “I think I recognized that cursed painter!”

Lousteau-Prangin ran to his father the judge. Max was carried home by Cognette, young Goddet, and two others, while Mere Cognette and Monsieur Goddet walked beside the stretcher. Those who carried him glanced at Monsieur Hochon’s door and saw Hochon’s servant sweeping the steps. Gritte, like most provincial households, was early astir. The few words Max had spoken roused Monsieur Goddet’s suspicions. He asked the woman if Monsieur Joseph Bridau was in bed.

“He went out at half-past four,” she answered. “He walked up and down his room all night.”

Such an answer produced exclamations of horror; Gritte, curious, came over and saw Max in his bloody shirt half-fainting on a mattress. Artists could guess what had kept Joseph restless all night: he had dreaded the gossip at Issoudun, imagined the town would say he had fleeced his uncle and been deceitful. He had early at dawn gone to the poplar avenue to walk and calm himself.

While Joseph comforted himself by vowing never to return to Issoudun, Max prepared a theatrical outrage for his sensitive spirit. Monsieur Goddet, after probing the wound and finding that a small pocket-book had deflected the knife so the blow was serious but not mortal, did what country surgeons do to enhance their credit: he “did not answer for the patient’s life,” dressed him, delivered a grave verdict to the Rabouilleuse, Jean-Jacques Rouget, Kouski, and the Vedie, then left the house. The Rabouilleuse wept over Max; Kouski and the Vedie declared the captain likely to die. Nearly two hundred people gathered about the place Saint-Jean and the two Narettes.

“I sha’n’t be a month in bed; and I know who struck the blow,” Max whispered to Flore. “We’ll profit by it to get rid of the Parisians. I said I thought I recognized the painter; pretend I’m expected to die and try to have Joseph Bridau arrested. Let him taste prison for a couple of days and his mother will be off to Paris when she gets him out. Then we needn’t fear the priests they talk of setting on the old fool.”

When Flore came downstairs the crowd was ready to take the impression she meant to give. She went out in tears and, sobbing, related how the painter, “who had just the face for that sort of thing,” had been angry with Max the night before about some pictures he had “wormed out” of Pere Rouget.

“That brigand—look at him!—thinks that if Max were dead his uncle would leave him his fortune,” she cried. “The old one told me Max is Doctor Rouget’s son!”

“Ah! he meant to do the deed before he left Issoudun; he chose his time, for he was going away today,” said one of the Knights of Idleness.

“Max hasn’t an enemy in Issoudun,” said another.

“Besides, Max recognized the painter,” said the Rabouilleuse.

“Where’s that cursed Parisian? Let us find him!” they cried.

“Find him? Why, he left Monsieur Hochon’s at daybreak.”

A Knight of Idleness ran to Monsieur Mouilleron. The crowd grew, the tumult threatened. Groups filled the Grande-Narette and formed before Saint-Jean; a mass assembled at porte Vilatte. Lousteau-Prangin, Monsieur Mouilleron the commissary of police, the lieutenant of gendarmes, and two men struggled through hedges of people whose cries prejudiced them against the Parisian, though he was unjustly accused—circumstances, however, pointed against him.

After conferring with Max, Monsieur Mouilleron sent the commissary and a sergeant with one gendarme to inspect the “theatre of the crime.” Messieurs Mouilleron and Lousteau-Prangin, with the gendarme lieutenant, crossed to the Hochon house now guarded by two gendarmes in the garden and two at the door. The crowd swelled; the whole town surged in the Grande rue.

Gritte rushed, terrified, to inform Monsieur Hochon: “Monsieur, we shall be pillaged! The town is in revolt; Monsieur Maxence Gilet has been assassinated; they say Monsieur Joseph did it!”

Monsieur Hochon, alarmed for his gold, dressed quickly, peered out, then bolted the house. He learned from Gritte that his guest had left at daybreak—after pacing his room all night—and not yet returned. Madame Hochon, already awake from the noise, heard the frightful tidings.

“He is innocent, of course,” she said.

“Before his innocence can be proved, the crowd may pillage us,” replied Hochon, white with fear.

“Where is Agathe?”

“Sound asleep.”

“So much the better,” said Madame Hochon. “A shock might kill the poor child.”

But Agathe woke half-dressed, disturbed by evasive answers and the turmoil. She found Madame Hochon pale, weeping at a salon window beside her husband.

“Courage, my child. God sends us our afflictions,” the old lady said. “Joseph is accused—”

“Of what?”

“Of a bad action he could never have committed,” Madame Hochon answered.

Seeing the lieutenant of gendarmes enter with the magistrates, Agathe fainted.

“There now!” said Monsieur Hochon. “Carry off Madame Bridau; women are only in the way at these times. Take her to her room and stay there, both of you. Sit down, gentlemen. The mistake to which we owe your visit will soon, I hope, be cleared up.”

“Even if it should be a mistake,” said Monsieur Mouilleron, “the crowd is so exasperated I fear for the accused’s safety. I should like to arrest him; that might pacify these people.”

“Who would have believed Monsieur Maxence Gilet inspired so much affection?” asked Lousteau-Prangin.

“One of my men says twelve hundred more are coming from the faubourg de Rome,” said the lieutenant; “they threaten to kill the assassin.”

“Where is your guest?” asked Mouilleron of Hochon.

“He went to walk in the country, I believe.”

“Call Gritte,” said the judge gravely. “The crime was committed not far from here.”

The three functionaries exchanged looks.

“I never liked that painter’s face,” said the lieutenant.

When Gritte appeared trembling, Lousteau-Prangin asked if she had seen Monsieur Joseph leave that morning. She confirmed it. Lousteau ordered warrants fetched. Monsieur Hochon pleaded caution: Joseph may have been agitated for other reasons—he meant to return to Paris that day for business in which Gilet and Mademoiselle Brazier had questioned his honor. Mouilleron added that the quarrel over the pictures yesterday had been hot; with artists it can be a word and a blow. Lousteau declared no one in Issoudun had cause to kill Gilet. Hochon noted, “But what was Monsieur Gilet doing in the streets at four in the morning?”

A clamour rose from the other end of town: “Here he is! Here he is—he’s arrested!” Joseph, returning quietly past the mill at Landrole to breakfast, had been seen at the place Misere. Two gendarmes ran in time to snatch him from the faubourg de Rome inhabitants, who had already pinioned him and threatened him with death.

“Give way! Give way!” cried the gendarmes as they formed a protective escort.

“You see, monsieur,” one who held the painter said, “it concerns our skin as well as yours. Innocent or guilty, we must protect you. The people adore Monsieur Gilet and want to take justice into their own hands. Didn’t we see them in 1830 dusting the jackets of the tax-gatherers?”

Joseph, pale, forced himself to go on. Amid hoots, insults, and stones, he was led from place Misere to place Saint-Jean; the gendarmes drew sabres, one was wounded, and Joseph received missiles on his legs, shoulders, and hat.

“We must disperse the crowd; I see but one way,” said the lieutenant to the magistrates. “Take Monsieur Bridau to the Palais accompanied by all of you; my gendarmes will make a circle. One can’t answer for anything before a furious crowd of six thousand.”

“You are right,” said Monsieur Hochon, trembling for his gold.

“Do you wish your friend’s house pillaged?” asked the lieutenant. “Could we beat back a crowd pushed from behind by an angry populace that knows nothing of legal forms?”

“That will do; let us go; we can explain later,” said Joseph, who had recovered composure.

“Give way, friends! He is arrested, and we are taking him to the Palais,” the lieutenant cried. “Respect the law!” said Mouilleron.

“Wouldn’t you prefer to see him guillotined?” jeered a gendarme at a group.

“Yes, yes, they shall guillotine him!” one madman shouted; the women echoed, “They are going to guillotine him!” By the time they reached the end of the Grande-Narette the crowd cried: “They are taking him to the guillotine! They found the knife upon him! That’s what Parisians are! He carries crime on his face!”

Though blood rushed to his head, Joseph walked from place Saint-Jean to the Palais with calm. In Monsieur Lousteau-Prangin’s private office he declared his innocence: he had been out past five, stood at half-past five before Saint-Cyr’s facade, talked with the sexton, crossed the vegetable market where women had assembled, passed place Misere, went as far as the mill of Landrole by the Pont aux Anes and watched the ducks five or six minutes—miller’s men must have seen him—and then followed the avenue to Tivoli where he spoke with the gardener. He urged the magistrates to verify these facts and begged not to be arrested, promising to remain in the office until cleared.

These sensible, confident statements impressed the magistrates.

“Yes, summon all those persons,” said Mouilleron; “but it is more than a day’s affair. Make up your mind to be imprisoned in the Palais.”

“Provided I can write to my mother to reassure her—oh, you can read the letter,” Joseph added.

This just request was granted, and Joseph wrote to his mother assuring her the mistake could be rectified and that he expected to be free by evening or tomorrow.

Madame Bridau, suffering a nervous attack and medicated by Monsieur Goddet without relief, read the letter and was soothed into the usual depressed state. When Goddet returned, he found her regretting leaving Paris.

“How is Monsieur Gilet?” asked Madame Hochon of Goddet.

“His wound, though serious, is not mortal,” the doctor replied. “With a month’s nursing he will be all right. I left him writing to Monsieur Mouilleron to request Joseph’s release,” he added. “Oh! Max is a fine fellow. He then remembered something proving the assassin was not your son; the man wore list shoes, whereas it is certain Monsieur Joseph left in boots—”

A man had left Max, after dark, a typed note that read: “Captain Gilet ought not to let an innocent man suffer. He who struck the blow promises not to strike again if Monsieur Gilet will have Monsieur Joseph Bridau set at liberty,” without naming the attacker. After reading and burning this letter, Max wrote to Monsieur Mouilleron relating the list-shoe detail from Goddet and begged Joseph’s release and asked Mouilleron to visit so he might explain more.

By the time Max’s letter arrived, Lousteau-Prangin had verified Joseph’s account through the bell-ringer, market-women, washerwomen, and the miller’s men. Max’s note clinched Joseph’s innocence, and Monsieur Mouilleron himself escorted him back to the Hochons’. His mother greeted him with overflowing tenderness; the poor misunderstood son was grateful: he had found affection from misfortune.

“Oh,” said Monsieur Mouilleron with self-satisfaction, “I knew by your look before the crowd that you were innocent; but the only way to protect you was to arrest you as we did. You carried your head high.”

“I was thinking of something else,” replied Joseph. “An officer once told me he had been stopped in Dalmatia under similar circumstances returning from a walk. The recollection made me think of painting a revolt of 1793. Besides, I kept saying, Blackguard that I am! I deserve this for coming to look after an inheritance instead of painting in my studio.”

“If you allow me a word of advice,” said the procureur du roi, “take the postmaster’s carriage to-night and return to Paris by the diligence from Bourges.”

“That is my advice too,” said Monsieur Hochon, burning to see them go.

“My most earnest wish is to get away,” said Agathe, kissing Madame Hochon’s hand. “When shall I see you again?”

“Ah, my dear, never until we meet above. We have suffered enough here below,” the old woman answered.

Shortly after, Monsieur Rouget surprised them with a farewell visit; Jean-Jacques came to bid his sister good-by and offer his caleche for the drive to Bourges. “Ah! your pictures have been a great evil to us,” said Agathe. “Keep them, my sister,” said Rouget, who still doubted their value. He let them kiss him and went away without manifesting feeling. Baruch, at a hint from his grandfather, had seen the postmaster. At eleven that night the two Parisians, ensconced in a wicker cabriolet drawn by one horse and a postilion, left Issoudun. Adolphine and Madame Hochon parted with tears; they alone regretted Joseph and Agathe.

“They are gone!” said Francois Hochon, going with the Rabouilleuse into Max’s bedroom.

“Well done! The trick succeeded,” answered Max, now tired and feverish.

“What did you say to old Mouilleron?” asked Francois.

“I told him I had given my assassin some cause to waylay me; that he was dangerous and likely, if I pursued the affair, to kill me before capture. Consequently, I begged Mouilleron and Prangin to make an ostensible active search but really to let the assassin go in peace, unless they wished to see me dead.”

“I do hope, Max,” said Flore, “that you will be quiet at night for some time to come.”

“At any rate, we are delivered from the Parisians!” cried Max. “The fellow who stabbed me had no idea what a service he was doing us.”

The next day the Parisians’ departure was celebrated as a provincial victory over Paris by nearly everyone in Issoudun, except the sober, staid inhabitants who shared Monsieur and Madame Hochon’s view. A few of Max’s friends spoke harshly of the Bridaus.

“Do those Parisians think we are all idiots?” cried one. “They came to fleece and have been shorn themselves,” said another. “They actually consulted a lawyer in Paris—what plan had they?” “A plan to get possession of old Rouget. But the Parisians were not clever enough; that lawyer can’t crow over us Berrichons!” “How abominable!” “That’s Paris for you!” “The Rabouilleuse knew they came to attack her, and she defended herself.” “She did gloriously right!”

To the townspeople the Bridaus were Parisians and foreigners; they preferred Max and Flore.

We can imagine the comfort with which Joseph and Agathe re-entered their lodging in the rue Mazarin. On the journey Joseph recovered spirits lost by arrest and a day’s confinement; but his mother could not be cheered. The Court of Peers was about to try the military conspirators, and that kept Agathe anxious. Philippe’s conduct, despite a clever defender recommended by Desroches, raised suspicions unfavorable to his character. To escape hearing about the trial—lasting twenty days—Joseph, after briefing Desroches fully on Issoudun, set off with Mistigris for the Comte de Serizy’s chateau.

Contemporary histories need not be repeated here: whether Philippe had played an agreed part or was truly an informer, he was condemned to five years’ surveillance by the police and ordered to leave Paris that day for Autun, the director-general’s choice for his exile. The punishment resembled parole with a town for a prison. Learning that the Comte de Serizy, one of the peers on the court-martial, was employing Joseph to decorate his chateau at Presles, Desroches sought an audience and found Monsieur de Serizy favorably disposed; they had met. Desroches explained the brothers’ financial plight, recalled their father’s services and neglect under the Restoration, and urged the minister to induce the director-general of police to change Philippe’s residence from Autun to Issoudun and to grant a dole of sixty francs a month to the former lieutenant-colonel, a matter of shame for the minister of war to deny.

“I will obtain all you ask of me, for I think it just,” replied the count.

Three days later Desroches, armed with authority, fetched Philippe from the Court of Peers’ prison and took him to his house in the rue de Bethizy. There the young barrister read Philippe a plain, unanswerable lecture in which lawyers reduce clients’ sentiment to blunt terms. He humbled the former staff-officer by reproaching his reckless dissipations, his mother’s misfortunes, and the death of Madame Descoings, then explained Issoudun’s situation and probed Maxence Gilet and the Rabouilleuse’s characters. Philippe, who grasped such matters keenly, listened more to this part than to the reproaches.

“Under these circumstances,” Desroches continued, “you can repair some of the injury you have done—so far as possible; you cannot restore life to the poor mother you have nearly killed. But you alone can—”

“What can I do?” asked Philippe.

“I have obtained a change of residence for you from Autun to Issoudun.”

Philippe’s sunken, almost sinister face brightened.

“And,” Desroches went on, “you alone can recover your uncle’s inheritance; half may already be in Gilet’s hands. I suggest no plan; everything depends on local circumstances. You have to deal with a strong, astute man. The way he tried to recover the pictures he’d given to Joseph, and the audacity with which he laid the crime on your brother, shows he’s capable of anything. Be prudent; behave properly out of policy if not decency. Without telling Joseph, whose pride as an artist would revolt, I have sent the pictures to Monsieur Hochon, telling him to give them up to no one but you. By the way, Maxence Gilet is a brave man.”

“So much the better,” said Philippe; “I count on his courage for success; a coward would leave Issoudun.”

“Consult Monsieur Hochon,” urged Desroches. “You have your orders from the police; I have taken your place in the Orleans diligence for half-past seven tonight. I suppose your trunk is ready; come and dine.”

“I own nothing but what I have on my back,” said Philippe, opening his threadbare blue overcoat; “but I only need three things, which you must tell Giroudeau, the uncle of Finot, to send me—my sabre, my sword, and my pistols.”

“You need more than that,” said the lawyer, shuddering at his client’s appearance. “You will receive a quarterly stipend which will clothe you decently.”

“Bless me! are you here, Godeschal?” cried Philippe, recognizing in Desroches’s head-clerk the brother of Mariette.

“Yes, I have been with Monsieur Desroches for two months.”

“And he will stay with me till he gets business of his own,” said Desroches.

“How is Mariette?” asked Philippe, moved.

“She is getting ready for the opening of the new theatre.”

“It would cost her little trouble to get my sentence remitted,” said Philippe. “However, as she chooses!”

After a meagre dinner—Desroches boarded his head-clerk—the two lawyers put the political convict in the diligence and wished him good luck.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
On the second of November, All-Souls’ day, Philippe Bridau registered at the Issoudun police commissary and, on the official’s advice, took rooms in rue l’Avenier. News that an officer banished for a recent military conspiracy had come to town spread quickly and caused more stir when people learned he was the brother of the painter who had been wrongly accused. Maxence Gilet, recovered from his wound, had converted all Père Rouget’s mortgages into cash and placed the proceeds in the “grand-livre.” The loan of 140,000 francs on Rouget’s real estate caused a scandal—everything leaks fast in the provinces. Monsieur Hochon, anxious for the Bridaus and still smarting from the terror he had felt at the thought of his house being ransacked, questioned old Monsieur Heron, the Bourges notary, about it.

Heron insisted that, but for him, old Rouget would have kept the 50,000-franc income in Maxence Gilet’s name; he had advised Mademoiselle Brazier to rely on the will and cautioned Maxence and his mistress to let the sudden change be forgotten. Hochon, uneasy, urged protection for the Bridaus. Maxence and Flore, feeling secure, welcomed another nephew’s arrival; they knew they could have old Rouget sign a power of attorney transferring funds to Max or Flore if danger threatened. If the will leaving Flore the principal were revoked, the 50,000-franc income and the newly extracted mortgage of 140,000 would be a consolation.

The day after arriving, Philippe called on his uncle about ten o’clock, in shabby clothes. Flore shivered at his repulsive appearance; Gilet felt the same preternatural chill that warns of a hidden enemy. Philippe’s ruined blue greatcoat was buttoned to the throat; his trousers and boots were ragged and muddy, his hat greasy, his malacca cane dull. His face had the exhausted, sallow look of a gambler—sunken cheeks, red-rimmed lids, a menacing brow, a fringe of hair around a bald crown, and cold steel-blue eyes. He introduced himself hoarsely as Philippe Bridau, a lieutenant-colonel ill-treated by the Bourbons, complaining that by police order he must live on a pay of sixty francs a month. He made a point of disdain for relying on his uncle.

“Good-morning, uncle,” he said, offering his hand; Rouget took it. He declared he would sooner cut off his hand than beg from his uncle after the way his mother and brother had been treated in Issoudun, though he had felt it proper to pay respects. Flore invited him to breakfast; he declined, saying he had already eaten. He hinted that he must safeguard the Bridaus’ honor, spoke curtly about pictures at Monsieur Hochon’s for identification, and left. The violence with which he slammed the door shocked Flore and Maxence more than his appearance had.

They watched him cross to the Hochons’. Gilet, though furious, kept his temper; he knew the need for political prudence. Flore urged Rouget not to admit him again. At the Hochons’, curiosity and unease were thick—Madame Hochon shuddered at the father of Agathe’s griefs and the murderer of Madame Descoings. Philippe, polite and composed, explained he had come to introduce himself and to manage living on sixty francs. He spoke well, admitted past faults, promised reformation, and praised Lousteau the journalist, which won the old lady’s favor. Madame Hochon advised him gently; he said prison had taught him much and he meant to lead another life.

Outside, in a whisper to Monsieur Hochon, Philippe warned that town gossip could be ruinous and they must avoid any public appearance of advising him. Desroches had instructed Philippe to use Hochon’s counsel discreetly; accordingly they agreed to meet “accidentally” in open places when needed. Philippe asked for names of veterans in Issoudun who were not Maxence’s friends. Hochon named Mignonnet, an artillery captain of the Guard, and Captain Carpentier, a cavalry officer who had replaced Gilet as mayoral clerk and befriended Mignonnet. Philippe mentioned his need for work; Hochon suggested a mutual insurance agency that might pay fifty francs a month—enough.

Within a week Philippe had bought on credit a decent suit, boots, gloves, and a hat. Giroudeau sent linen, weapons, and a letter to Carpentier, who welcomed Philippe and introduced him to Mignonnet, recommending him as a man of merit. Philippe narrated parts of the conspiracy to them in a manner that won admiration: the recent plot, he explained, was a continuation of older army efforts against the Bourbons—an extensive northern operation with a Paris diversion. Betrayed, the leaders had reduced the Paris plot to a barrack affair; when the larger scheme failed Philippe was to break links and let only the smaller plot be exposed. His apparent misery and ruined costume had served to minimize the conspiracy’s size in the authorities’ eyes. He portrayed himself as a man playing both sides, preserving favor with influential figures while waiting to join whichever party served his interests. Carpentier and Mignonnet, impressed by this explanation and by his apparent self-devotion, brought him into their circle.

They recommended him for the insurance office Hochon had mentioned; the position required only three hours a day. Philippe frequented the officers’ club; his manners and their praise earned him respect. Determined to seem sober and respectable and to lull Gilet’s suspicions, he pretended simple-mindedness while quietly observing his uncle’s property and plotting. Hochon had given him details of Rouget’s wealth, which stirred his covetousness. He and Hochon agreed not to alarm Maxence: if Rouget were spirited off to Bourges, the game would be lost.

Philippe dined once a week with Mignonnet, once with Carpentier, and every Thursday with Hochon; soon he had engagements most nights. He never spoke of his uncle, his brother, or his mother except concerning their stay in Issoudun. The three officers, decorated men in town, walked together daily; Philippe, wearing a rosette, carried himself like a man of consequence. To Max’s friends his demeanor and story made him seem a “sabreur,” an ostentatious soldier of the old army. “His conduct before the Court of Peers shows him either a dupe or a spy,” said Max; others defended him. Philippe fenced with Carpentier in a house in the faubourg Saint-Paterne and practiced pistols with Mignonnet and Carpentier—ostensibly sport, really to persuade Max that he preferred pistols for a duel. When he met Gilet he returned a bow with a cavalier touch to his hat, an officer’s condescension. Gilet gave no sign of impatience and never spoke of Bridau at the Cognettes’, though the pranks of the Order of Idleness were temporarily suspended since Fario’s attack.

Philippe’s contempt and Gilet’s restrained bearing became the talk of Issoudun. Some suspected Philippe of setting a trap; others thought Max might be baiting him. Philippe had investigated the circumstances of his brother’s arrest and of Gilet and the Rabouilleuse’s past, and he came into contact with Fario the Spaniard. Finding Fario reliable, Philippe made him a confidant and promised indemnity for past losses—Fario, a willing henchman, put his knowledge of the Knights of Idleness and his services at Philippe’s disposal. The town, abuzz with gossip, expected a deadly clash between the two men who intensely despised one another.

Toward the end of November Philippe told Hochon he had discovered that Hochon’s grandsons, Baruch and François, were intimate with Maxence and had spied in his house, informing Max of what was said there. He claimed to have overheard them and learned from Fario of their nocturnal visits to drinking-shops in Maxence’s company; they owed Maxence over three thousand francs. Philippe suspected that Maxence and the Rabouilleuse intended to secure the 50,000-franc income and then run away to marry. They must learn what was happening under Rouget’s roof, Philippe said; he did not yet know how to proceed. Hochon promised to think.

Jean-Jacques Rouget, meanwhile, suffered as never before since Philippe’s first visit. Flore, fearing some evil to Maxence, planned to leave Issoudun and marry Max in Paris after getting the Funds’ income transferred. Rouget refused to transfer, insisting Flore be sole heir—he believed she would abandon him if made rich. She tried cajolery and then coldness; Vedie found Rouget one morning with swollen eyes from weeping, and for a week he breakfasted alone.

The day after Philippe’s talk with Hochon he returned to Rouget’s house. Flore played tender and solicitous in his presence; Gilet stayed away. Philippe, discerning danger, judged it time to strike. He forced Flore to look him in the face and demanded whether Rouget was free to walk alone with him. Flore stammered that he was. Philippe insisted Rouget accompany him on foot, implying that refusal would confirm the town’s belief that Rouget was under Flore’s dominion. Seeing Rouget hesitate, Philippe pretended to go; he caught Flore making a menacing gesture at her uncle and told Rouget to meet him at the door in ten minutes or he would take other measures. He left for the Hochons’.

At nine that night Hochon, Madame Hochon, Monsieur Heron the notary, and the grandsons gathered. Hochon, enraged, denounced Baruch and François for their nocturnal life with Maxence and for informing the Knights of Idleness; he accused them of betraying secrets for money and even of facilitating past crimes by transmitting plans. He read the guardianship accounts: the Borniche children’s net fortune was 70,000 francs, part of their mother’s dowry, but Hochon, as creditor to his daughter, controlled much of it. Baruch’s portion was twenty thousand francs. Hochon declared he would release their property but no longer tolerate their conduct; he discharged his guardianship and made their futures contingent on strict behavior. Baruch was to be sent to Paris to study banking under Mongenod; François, in debt, would have only six hundred francs a year and go to Poitiers to study law. Hochon gloated over what he called a quick vengeance: he would watch them and test them by years of conduct. Having settled the legal business and obtained receipts, Heron prepared to leave when Philippe arrived. Madame Hochon withdrew with her grandsons to see what effect the scene had produced.

Philippe and Hochon spoke in the window embrasure. Hochon had consulted Heron and learned that the security for the 50,000-franc income could not be sold without the owner’s signature or a power of attorney; Rouget had not given any power before a notary since his arrival. If Rouget attempted to give one in Issoudun they would know instantly; if he left town to sign, registration would reveal it. Therefore, if Rouget left, they would follow him and learn his movements. Philippe reported seeing his uncle cross the street and detailed his last visit. He told Hochon that Fario and a man named Benjamin Bourdet—a soldier Philippe had helped in Vatan—were watching; they would follow Rouget secretly. “If they let him walk with you,” Hochon mused, “Maxence must believe he has found some means to win the game.” Philippe said Maxence was afraid of him but could not evade him; Fario had stirred public censure against Maxence and the gossip was now in motion.

When Philippe left, Flore hurried to Maxence’s room with a full account of his boldness. Max resolved that before resorting to fighting him they would play a bold card. He told Flore to send at once to Père Cognette for a horse and a char-à-banc; pack their things and go to Vatan pretending to stay, taking Vedie and the twenty thousand francs in Rouget’s drawer. If Philippe brought Rouget to Vatan, Flore must refuse to return unless Rouget signed a power of attorney. As soon as they obtained it, Max would slip off to Paris while she came back to Issoudun. When Jean-Jacques found her gone he would be desperate and likely follow; Max planned to give him a stern talking-to when he did.

While that plot was unfolding, Philippe walked arm in arm with his uncle along the boulevard Baron.

“The two great tacticians are coming to close quarters at last,” thought Monsieur Hochon as he watched the colonel escorting his uncle. “I am curious to see the end of the game, and what becomes of the stake of ninety thousand francs a year.”

“My dear uncle,” said Philippe, with a Parisian air, “you love this girl, and you are devilishly right. She is damnably handsome! Instead of fondling you she makes you trot like a valet; but she wants you six feet under the ground so she may marry Max, whom she adores.”

“I know that, Philippe, but I love her all the same.”

“Well, I have sworn by the soul of my mother, who is your sister,” Philippe went on, “to make your Rabouilleuse as supple as my glove, as she was before that scoundrel, unworthy of the Imperial Guard, came to quarter in your house.”

“Ah! if you could do that!” cried the old man.

“It is easy,” answered Philippe, cutting him short. “I’ll kill Max as I would a dog; but—on one condition.”

“What is that?” Rouget asked, looking bewildered.

“Don’t sign that power of attorney they want before the third of December; put them off till then. Your torturers only want it to enable them to sell the fifty thousand a year you have in the Funds, so they may run off to Paris and pay for their wedding festivities out of your millions.”

“I am afraid so,” Rouget replied.

“Whatever they say or do, put off the signing until next week.”

“Yes; but when Flore speaks to me she stirs my very soul. When she looks at me her blue eyes seem like paradise, and I am no longer master of myself—especially if she has been harsh to me.”

“Well, whether she is sweet or sour, don’t do more than promise to sign the paper, and let me know the night before you intend to do it. That will answer. Maxence shall not be your proxy unless he first kills me. If I kill him, you must agree to take me in his place, and I’ll undertake to break that handsome girl and keep her at your beck and call. Yes, Flore shall love you; and if she doesn’t satisfy you—thunder! I’ll thrash her.”

“Oh! I never could allow that. A blow struck at Flore would break my heart.”

“But it is the only way to govern women and horses. A man makes himself feared, or loved, or respected. Now that is what I wanted to whisper in your ear—Good-morning, gentlemen,” he said to Mignonnet and Carpentier, who came up at the moment; “I am taking my uncle for a walk, as you see, and trying to improve him; for we are in an age when children are obliged to educate their grandparents.”

They all bowed. Philippe explained briefly Rouget’s position.

“Gentlemen,” he concluded, “you see there are no two ways of saving him: either Colonel Bridau must kill Captain Gilet, or Captain Gilet must kill Colonel Bridau. We celebrate the Emperor’s coronation the day after tomorrow; I rely on you to arrange the seats at the banquet so that I shall sit opposite Gilet. You will do me the honor, I hope, of being my seconds.”

“We will appoint you to preside and sit ourselves on either side of you. Max, as vice-president, will of course sit opposite,” said Mignonnet.

“Oh! the scoundrel will have Potel and Renard with him,” said Carpentier. “In spite of what Issoudun now knows about his midnight maraudings, those two worthy officers, who have been his seconds, remain faithful to him.”

“You see how it all maps out, uncle,” said Philippe. “Therefore, sign no paper before the third of December; the next day you shall be free, happy, and beloved by Flore, without coaxing for it.”

“You don't know him, Philippe,” said the terrified old man. “Maxence has killed nine men in duels.”

“Yes; but ninety thousand francs a year didn’t depend on it,” answered Philippe.

“A bad conscience shakes the hand,” remarked Mignonnet sententiously.

“In a few days,” Philippe resumed, “you and the Rabouilleuse will be living together as sweet as honey—after she gets through mourning. At first she’ll twist and weep; but never mind, let the water run!”

The two soldiers approved and tried to hearten old Rouget, walking with him for nearly two hours. At parting Philippe said:

“Don’t take any steps without me. I know women. I have paid for one, who cost me far more than Flore can ever cost you. But she taught me how to behave to the fair sex for the rest of my days. Women are bad children; they are inferior animals to men; we must make them fear us; the worst condition in the world is to be governed by such brutes.”

About half-past two in the afternoon, the old man reached home. Kouski opened the door in tears—by Max’s orders, signaling that something was wrong.

“Oh! Monsieur, Madame has gone away, and taken Vedie with her!”

“Gone—away!” said Rouget, the blow so violent he sat down on the stairs.

He searched the house, returned to the salon, threw himself into a chair, and burst into tears.

“Where is she?” he sobbed. “Oh! where is she? where is Max?”

“I don’t know,” answered Kouski. “The captain went out without telling me.”

Gilet, knowing it was politic to be seen sauntering, left the old man alone with his despair to deepen his docility. To keep Philippe from aiding Rouget, he had ordered Kouski not to open to anyone. Flore away, the miserable old man grew frantic; the situation approached a crisis.

During his walk through the town, Maxence Gilet was avoided by many who hours earlier would have shaken hands. A reaction against him had set in. The deeds of the Knights of Idleness and the clarification of Joseph Bridau’s arrest had disgraced Max and shown his life in its true light. He met Captain Potel, who was distraught.

“What’s the matter with you, Potel?”

“My dear fellow, the Imperial Guard is being black-guarded all over the town! These civilians are crying you down! and it goes to the bottom of my heart.”

“What are they complaining of?” asked Max.

“Of what you do at night.”

“As if we couldn’t amuse ourselves a little!”

“But that isn’t all,” said Potel. “The Guard is against the Guard. Bridau has set all these bourgeois on you. It’s all wrong; the Guard is disgraced—here at Issoudun! where it was once so honored.”

“Come, Potel, don’t worry yourself,” Max answered; “even if you do not see me at the banquet—”

“What! do you mean you won’t be there the day after to-morrow?” cried Potel. “Do you wish to be called a coward? No, the unmounted grenadiers of the Guard cannot draw back before the dragoons. Arrange your business in some other way and be there!”

“One more to send to the shades!” said Max. “Well, I think I can manage my business so as to get there—for,” he thought, “that power of attorney ought not to be in my name; as old Heron says, it would look too much like theft.”

Tangled in Philippe’s net, Max muttered as he walked off; he avoided looks and returned home by the boulevard Vilatte, talking to himself.

“I will have that money before I fight,” he said. “If I die, it shall not go to Philippe. I must put it in Flore’s name. She will follow my instructions and go straight to Paris. Once there, she can marry, if she chooses, the son of some marshal of France. I’ll have that power of attorney made in Baruch’s name, and he’ll transfer the property by my order.”

Max, cool and composed in appearance though boiling inside, combined many qualities of a great general. Had his career not been stalled by captivity, the Emperor might have found him necessary to great enterprises. He entered the room where the hapless Rouget still wept, feigned surprise, and heard of Flore’s departure with well-acted amazement. He questioned Kouski about her destination.

“Madame said like this,” Kouski replied—“that she had taken twenty thousand francs in gold from his drawer, thinking monsieur wouldn’t refuse her that amount as wages for the last twenty-two years.”

“Wages?” exclaimed Rouget.

“Yes,” said Kouski. “‘Ah! I shall never come back,’ she said to Vedie as she drove away. ‘Poor Vedie, who is so attached to monsieur, remonstrated with madame. “No, no,” she answered, “he has no affection for me; he lets his nephew treat me like the lowest of the low”’—and she wept.”

“Eh! what do I care for Philippe?” cried the old man. “Where is Flore? how can we find her?”

“Philippe, whose advice you follow, will help you,” said Max coldly.

“Philippe?” said the old man. “What has he to do with the poor child? There is no one but you, my good Max, who can find Flore. She will follow you— you could bring her back to me—you could.”

“I don’t wish to oppose Monsieur Bridau,” observed Max.

“As for that,” cried Rouget, “if that hinders you, he told me he meant to kill you.”

“Ah!” exclaimed Gilet, laughing, “we will see about it!”

“My friend,” said the old man, “find Flore, and I will do all she wants of me.”

“Someone must have seen her as she passed through the town,” said Maxence to Kouski. “Serve dinner; put everything on the table, and then go and make inquiries. Let us know, by dessert, which road Mademoiselle Brazier took.”

This order quieted the poor creature for a time. Rouget, who hated Max, thought his tormentor an angel in that moment. The passion of the miserable old man for Flore resembled childhood. At six o’clock, the Pole, who had merely taken a walk, returned to announce Flore had driven toward Vatan.

“Madame is going back to her own people, that’s plain,” said Kouski.

“Would you like to go to Vatan tonight?” said Max. “The road is bad, but Kouski knows how to drive, and you’ll make your peace better tonight than tomorrow morning.”

“Let us go!” cried Rouget.

“Put the horse in quietly,” Max told Kouski; “manage, if you can, that the town shall not know of this nonsense, for Monsieur Rouget’s sake. Saddle my horse,” he added in a whisper, “I will ride on ahead of you.”

Monsieur Hochon had already notified Philippe of Flore’s departure; the colonel rose from Mignonnet’s dinner-table to hurry to the place Saint-Jean; he at once guessed the meaning of the manoeuvre. When Philippe presented himself at his uncle’s house, Kouski answered through a window that Monsieur Rouget could see no one.

“Fario,” said Philippe to the Spaniard stationed in the Grande-Narette, “tell Benjamin to mount his horse; it is important I know what Gilet does with my uncle.”

“They are now putting the horse into the calèche,” said Fario, who had been watching Rouget’s stable.

“If they go towards Vatan,” Philippe said, “get me another horse, and come yourself with Benjamin to Monsieur Mignonnet’s.”

“What do you mean to do?” asked Monsieur Hochon, who had come out when he saw Philippe and Fario together.

“The genius of a general, my dear Monsieur Hochon,” said Philippe, “consists not only in observing the enemy’s movements, but in guessing his intentions and modifying his own plan. Now, if my uncle and Max drive out together they are going to Vatan; Maxence will have promised to reconcile him with Flore—if that’s the line they take, I don’t yet know what I shall do; my uncle can’t sign a power of attorney at ten o’clock at night; the notaries will be in bed. If, as I fancy, Max goes on ahead to teach Flore her lesson—which seems probable—the rogue is lost! you’ll see what revenge we old soldiers take. I need a helper for this last stroke; I must go back to Mignonnet’s and arrange with Carpentier.”

Shaking hands with Hochon, Philippe went to Mignonnet’s. Ten minutes later Hochon saw Max ride off at a quick trot; his curiosity made him stay at his window, eagerly expecting the wheels of the old demi-fortune.

At this moment a lad from the faubourg de Rome brought a letter for Baruch. The two grandsons, subdued by the morning’s events, had kept to their rooms. Baruch, richer than Francois and with most to lose, counselled surrender and promised amendment to save their prospects and have their debt to Max paid; Madame Hochon encouraged this.

“Read it to me yourself; I haven’t my spectacles,” said old Hochon. Baruch read the letter aloud:

My dear Friend,—I hope you will not hesitate, under the serious circumstances in which I find myself, to do me the service of receiving a power of attorney from Monsieur Rouget. Be at Vatan tomorrow morning at nine o’clock. I shall probably send you to Paris, but don’t be uneasy; I will furnish you with money for the journey, and join you there immediately. I am almost sure I shall be obliged to leave Issoudun, December third.

Adieu. I count on your friendship; rely on that of your friend,

Maxence

“God be praised!” exclaimed Hochon. “The property of that old idiot is saved from the claws of the devil.”

“It will be if you say so,” said Madame Hochon; “and I thank God. The prosperity of the wicked is always fleeting.”

“You must go to Vatan and accept the power of attorney from Monsieur Rouget,” the old man told Baruch. “Their object is to get fifty thousand francs a year transferred to Mademoiselle Brazier. They will send you to Paris, and you must seem to go; but stop at Orleans and wait till you hear from me. Let no one know where you lodge; go to the first inn you come to; no one must find you.”

At that instant Francois, who had run to the window at the noise of wheels in the Grande-Narette, cried, “Here’s something new—Père Rouget and Colonel Bridau coming back together in the calèche, Benjamin and Captain Carpentier following on horseback!”

“I’ll go over,” cried Hochon; his curiosity overcame caution.

Hochon found old Rouget in his bedroom, writing the following letter at his nephew’s dictation:

Mademoiselle,—If you do not start to return here the moment you receive this letter, your conduct will show such ingratitude for all my goodness that I shall revoke the will I have made in your favor, and give my property to my nephew Philippe. You will understand that Monsieur Gilet can no longer be my guest after staying with you at Vatan. I send this letter by Captain Carpentier, who will put it into your own hands. I hope you will listen to his advice; he will speak to you with authority from me.
Your affectionate
J.-J. Rouget.

“Captain Carpentier and I MET my uncle, who was so foolish as to follow Mademoiselle Brazier and Monsieur Gilet to Vatan,” said Philippe, sarcastic, to Hochon. “I have made my uncle see he was running into a noose; that letter will bring her back this very night, or I’m mistaken. I promise to make her as pliable as whalebone for the rest of her days, if my uncle allows me to take Maxence Gilet’s place; which, in my opinion, he ought never to have had.”

“Neighbor,” said Hochon, “you have taken the best means to get peace. Destroy your will, and Flore will be once more what she used to be in the early days.”

“No, she will never forgive me for what I have made her suffer,” whimpered the old man; “she will no longer love me.”

“She shall love you; I’ll take care of that,” said Philippe.

“Read this,” said Hochon, showing Rouget Maxence’s letter to Baruch. “What infamy!” Carpentier exclaimed at the contents as Rouget read and wept.

“Is that plain enough, uncle?” demanded Philippe. “Hold that hussy by her interests and she’ll adore you as you deserve.”

“She loves Maxence too well; she will leave me,” cried Rouget.

“But, uncle, Maxence or I—one or the other of us—won’t leave our footsteps in the dust of Issoudun three days hence.”

“Well then go, Monsieur Carpentier,” said Rouget; “if you promise me to bring her back, go! You are a good man; say to her in my name all you think you ought to say.”

“Captain Carpentier will whisper in her ear that I have sent to Paris for a woman whose youth and beauty are captivating; that will bring the jade back in a hurry!”

The captain departed, driving himself in the old calèche; Benjamin accompanied him on horseback, for Kouski was nowhere to be found. Though threatened with arrest and loss of his place, the Pole had gone to Vatan on a hired horse to warn Max and Flore of the adversary’s move. After fulfilling his mission, Carpentier, who did not wish to drive back with Flore, was to change places with Benjamin and take his horse.

When Philippe learned of Kouski’s flight he said to Benjamin, “You will take the Pole’s place from this time on. It is all mapping out, papa Hochon!” cried the lieutenant-colonel. “That banquet will be jovial!”

“You will come and live here, of course,” said the old miser.

“I have told Fario to send me all my things,” answered Philippe. “I shall sleep in the room adjoining Gilet’s apartment—if my uncle consents.”

“What will come of all this?” cried the terrified old man.

“Mademoiselle Flore Brazier is coming, gentle as a paschal lamb,” replied Hochon.

“God grant it!” exclaimed Rouget.

“It is now seven o’clock,” said Philippe; “the sovereign of your heart will be here at half-past eleven: you’ll never see Gilet again, and you will be as happy ever after as a pope. If you want me to succeed,” he whispered to Hochon, “stay here till the hussy comes; you can help me in keeping the old man up to his resolution; together, we’ll make that crab-girl see on which side her bread is buttered.”

Hochon felt the reasonableness of the request and stayed. Old Rouget gave way to childish lamentations, quieted only by Philippe repeating many times: “Uncle, you will see that I am right when Flore returns to you as tender as ever. You shall be petted; you will save your property: be guided by my advice, and you’ll live in paradise for the rest of your days.”

About half-past eleven wheels were heard in the Grande-Narette; all wondered whether the carriage would return full or empty. Rouget’s face changed from agony to excessive joy when he saw two women as the carriage turned into the courtyard.

“Kouski,” said Philippe, giving Flore a hand to help her down. “You are no longer in Monsieur Rouget’s service. You will not sleep here tonight; get your things together, and go. Benjamin takes your place.”

“Are you the master here?” said Flore sarcastically.

“With your permission,” replied Philippe, squeezing her hand as if in a vice. “Come! we must have an understanding, you and I”; he led the bewildered woman into the place Saint-Jean.

“My fine lady,” began the old campaigner, stretching out his right hand, “three days hence, Maxence Gilet will be sent to the shades by that arm, or his will have taken me off guard. If I die, you will be the mistress of my poor imbecile uncle; ‘bene sit.’ If I remain on my pins, you’ll have to walk straight, and keep him supplied with first-class happiness. If you don’t, I know girls in Paris who are, with all due respect, much prettier than you; they are only seventeen years old: they would make my uncle excessively happy, and they are in my interests. Begin your attentions this very evening; if the old man is not as gay as a lark tomorrow morning, I have only a word to say to you; it is this—there is but one way to kill a man without the interference of the law, and that is to fight a duel with him; but I know three ways to get rid of a woman: mind that, my beauty!”

During this address, Flore shook.

“Kill Max—?” she said, gazing at Philippe in the moonlight.

“Come, here’s my uncle.”

Old Rouget, deaf to Hochon’s remonstrances, took Flore by the hand, drew her back into the house and shut the door.

“This is Saint-Lambert’s day, and he who deserts his place loses it,” remarked Benjamin to the Pole.

“My master will shut your mouth for you,” answered Kouski, departing to join Max who established himself at the hotel de la Poste.

On the morrow, women talked from door to door. The story of the Rouget household changed spread; the upshot of all the gossip was the same: “What will happen at the banquet between Max and Colonel Bridau?”

Philippe said few words to Vedie—“Six hundred francs’ annuity, or dismissal.” They sufficed to keep her neutral for a time between the two powers, Philippe and Flore.

Knowing Max’s life might be in danger, Flore became more affectionate to Rouget than in the first days. In love, self-interested devotion sometimes pleases more than sincere passion; that is why many men put up with clever deceivers. The Rabouilleuse did not appear until the next morning, when she came down to breakfast with Rouget on her arm. Tears filled her eyes as she saw Philippe in Max’s place, with his sombre blue eyes and cold, sinister expression.

“What is the matter, mademoiselle?” he said after wishing his uncle good-morning.

“She can’t endure the idea of your fighting Maxence,” said Rouget.

“I have not the slightest desire to kill Gilet,” answered Philippe. “He need only take himself off from Issoudun and go to America on a venture. I would advise it; he could make a fortune there, which is far more honorable than turning Issoudun topsy-turvy at night and playing the devil in your household.”

“A-mer-i-ca!” she cried, sobbing.

“It is better to kick his legs about in a free country than have them rot in a pine box in France. However, perhaps you think he is a good shot and can kill me—it’s on the cards,” observed the colonel.

“Will you let me speak to him?” begged Flore humbly.

“Certainly; he can come here and pack up his things. I will stay with my uncle during that time; I shall not leave the old man again,” replied Philippe.

“Vedie,” cried Flore, “run to the hotel, and tell Monsieur Gilet that I beg him—”

“—to come and get his belongings,” said Philippe, interrupting.

“Yes, yes, Vedie; that will be a pretext to see me; I must speak to him.”

Terror controlled her hatred; the shock of encountering this strong, pitiless nature overwhelmed her, and she bowed before Philippe as Rouget had once done. Vedie returned with a formal refusal from Max, who requested Mademoiselle Brazier send his things to the hotel de la Poste.

“Will you allow me to take them to him?” she said to Rouget.

“Yes, but will you come back?” he asked.

“If Mademoiselle is not back by midday, you will give me a power of attorney to attend to your property,” said Philippe, looking at Flore. “Take Vedie with you, to save appearances, mademoiselle. In future you are to think of my uncle’s honor.”

Flore could get nothing out of Max. Desperate at having been routed before the whole town, Gilet was too proud to run from Philippe. The Rabouilleuse suggested they fly together to America; Max, who did not want Flore without her money and yet did not wish her to see him reveal his heart, insisted on killing Philippe.

“We have committed a monstrous folly,” he said. “We ought to have gone to Paris and spent the winter there; but who could foresee that fellow’s strength? I took him for a fire-eater and blundered. As I didn’t double early, I won’t be such a coward as to back down now. He has lowered me in the town, and I cannot get back what I have lost unless I kill him.”

“Go to America with forty thousand francs. I’ll find a way to get rid of that scoundrel, and join you. It would be much wiser.”

“What would people say of me?” he exclaimed. “No; I have buried nine already. The fellow doesn’t seem to know much; he went from school to the army, always fighting till 1815; then America. I doubt if the brute ever set foot in a fencing-alley, while I have no match with the sabre. The sabre is his arm; I shall seem generous offering it to him—for I mean, if possible, to let him insult me—and I can easily run him through. That is my wisest course. Don’t be uneasy; we shall be masters of the field in a couple of days.”

Thus a stupid point of honor influenced Max more than policy. When Flore returned home she shut herself up to cry. The whole day gossip ran wild in Issoudun; the duel between Philippe and Maxence seemed inevitable.

“Ah! Monsieur Hochon,” said Mignonnet, who, with Carpentier, met Hochon on the boulevard Baron, “we are very uneasy; for Gilet is clever with all weapons.”

“Never mind,” said the provincial diplomatist; “Philippe has managed well from the beginning. I should never have thought that big, easy-going fellow would have succeeded as he has. The two have rolled together like a couple of thunder-clouds.”

“Oh!” said Carpentier, “Philippe is a remarkable man. His conduct before the Court of Peers was a masterpiece of diplomacy.”

“Well, Captain Renard,” said one of the townsfolk to Max’s friend, “they say wolves don’t devour each other, but it seems Max is going to set his teeth in Colonel Bridau. That’s pretty serious among you gentlemen of the Old Guard.”

“You make fun of it, do you? Because the poor fellow amused himself a little at night, you are all against him,” said Potel. “But Gilet is a man who couldn’t stay in a hole like Issoudun without finding something to do.”

“Well, gentlemen,” remarked another, “Max and the colonel must play out their game. Bridau had to avenge his brother. Don’t you remember Max’s treachery to the poor lad?”

“Bah! nothing but an artist,” said Renard.

“But the real question is about the old man’s property,” said a third. “They say Monsieur Gilet was laying hands on fifty thousand francs a year, when the colonel turned him out of his uncle’s house.”

“Gilet rob a man! Come, don’t say that to any one but me, Monsieur Canivet,” cried Potel. “If you do, I’ll make you swallow your tongue—and without any sauce.”

Every household in town prayed for the honor of Colonel Bridau.

Towards four o’clock the next day the old army officers at Issoudun gathered in the place du Marché, in front of Lacroix’s eating-house, waiting for Colonel Philippe Bridau. The coronation banquet was to begin at five. Groups discussed Max’s disgrace and dismissal from old Rouget’s house; the officers would dine at Lacroix’s, the common soldiers would meet at a wine-shop. Only Potel and Renard defended Max.

“Is it any of our business what takes place among the old man’s heirs?” Renard asked; Potel blamed Max’s weakness with women. An old sub-lieutenant predicted sabres, Mignonnet observed that a failed folly becomes a crime.

Max joined the old soldiers and was received in heavy silence. Potel and Renard walked arm-in-arm with him. Philippe appeared in full dress; his manner contrasted with the forced gratitude Max showed his few supporters. Bridau was warmly greeted by Mignonnet, Carpentier, and others, and this welcome destroyed the last scruples Max had felt from Flore’s entreaties.

“We shall fight,” Max told Renard; “to the death. Don’t talk to me—let me play my part well.”

The three Bonapartists returned to the officers’ group. Max first bowed to Bridau; their glance was frigid. Potel invited everyone to sit. Renard proposed a toast to the Little Corporal, now in the paradise of heroes. The company entered Lacroix’s long dining-hall; at Philippe’s request the two adversaries were seated opposite each other. Town youths lingered outside, anticipating a duel.

Throughout the meal both men masked vigilance with apparent joviality. At dessert Philippe proposed the first toast, “To the Grand Army!” and all repeated it. Mignonnet claimed the second toast: “Let us drink to those who attempted to restore his son!” All but Maxence Gilet bowed to Philippe and raised their glasses. Max rose and, amid expectancy, bowed ironically to Philippe.

“May we all meet again at this time next year,” said Max; his level voice raised tension. Kouski and others murmured predictions. Philippe told the soldiers their affairs didn’t concern the bourgeoisie; Renard assured him they would obey orders. The dessert passed; libations were plentiful. The antagonists and their seconds arranged for a duel that would not be an ordinary brawl. Both behaved like gentlemen, calming the anxious crowd. They agreed on sabres at ten that night; the rendezvous was behind the chancel of the Capuchins’ church at eight the next morning. Goddet, the former army surgeon, would be present. The seconds fixed the combat to last ten minutes.

At eleven that night Monsieur and Madame Hochon surprised Colonel Bridau in his rooms. Madame Hochon, tearful, implored him to say his prayers and presented a relic: a tooth of Sainte Solange mounted on black velvet, urging him to wear it. Philippe joked about its protection; Benjamin was paid thirty francs to sew the relic into Philippe’s trousers. The old lady left sure Sainte Solange would protect him.

On the morning of December third, cloudy and cold, Max, his seconds, and the Pole arrived on the meadow by the Capuchins’ apse. Philippe and his seconds waited, Benjamin among them. Potel and Mignonnet paced off twenty-four feet and attendants drew lines: no man could retreat beyond his line under pain of dishonor. Each was to stand at his line and advance only on the seconds’ word.

“Do we take off our coats?” Philippe asked coldly; Maxence adopted a bully’s manner: “Of course.” They stripped to shirts; their muscles hardly trembled. Goddet, the seconds, and two soldiers admired their composure.

As the signal was given Max caught sight of Fario’s sinister face peering through a hole in the church roof; those eyes, full of hatred and revenge, dazzled him. Philippe adopted an inside guard that marked a duellist of the first rank. Max sensed inferiority.

He tried a moulinet to bewilder Philippe, but felt the colonel’s wrist was iron and spring-like. Philippe’s parries were cool and precise; between two men of such calibre victory depended on a flash of calculation or a single false movement. At a bad parry by Max the colonel sent his sabre spinning from his hand.

“Pick it up,” Philippe said; “I am not the man to kill a disarmed enemy.” The grandeur of the words, their conscious superiority, stunned onlookers. When Max resumed position, he had lost coolness. He resolved to redeem his shame by a bold stroke; taking his sabre in both hands he rushed furiously. Philippe received a cut slashing his forehead and part of his face, but with a terrible oblique moulinet he cleft Max’s head. These two savage blows ended the combat in the ninth minute. Fario came down to gloat over his enemy’s convulsions; Philippe was carried back to his uncle’s house.

Thus perished a man who might have achieved great deeds in other circumstances; nature had given him courage, self-possession, and political sagacity, but not the nobility of conduct needed for greatness. He was not much regretted, given the perfidy by which his adversary—worse in character—had brought him into disrepute. His death ended the exploits of the Order of Idleness, to Issoudun’s relief. Philippe had little to fear as the duel’s circumstances were spread with praise for the bravery of both combatants.

“But they had better both have been killed,” Monsieur Mouilleron remarked; “it would have been a good riddance for the Government.”

Flore Brazier’s situation, which might have been embarrassing for Philippe, was saved by her collapse after Max’s death. A brain fever set in, combined with dangerous inflammation from her escapade to Vatan. She hovered between life and death for three months, attended by Goddet, who also cared for Philippe.

As soon as Philippe could write he sent letters. To Monsieur Desroches he reported he had “killed the most venomous of the two reptiles,” though his own head had been split by a sabre. He noted the other “viper” remained and he must come to terms with her because his uncle clung to her. He said illness had kept Flore in bed, which he hoped Providence would use to call her soul if she repented. He claimed Goddet and others had been won to his side; Goddet preached resignation to Flore and had promised to keep her in bed for three months. He had bought over the cook, who spread tales suggesting Max would have ill-treated Flore. “All goes well so far,” he wrote; “my uncle, by old Hochon’s advice, has destroyed his will.”

To Monsieur Giroudeau, care of Mademoiselle Florentine, he requested a girl—Cesarine, if available—or some ballet-girl who could appear as the respectable daughter of a brave soldier and be ready at his call; if she answered his purpose he would give her fifty thousand francs on his uncle’s death. He described the duel and hinted at lively times ahead.

Agathe, informed by Madame Hochon, rushed to Issoudun and was given Philippe’s former room. Her maternal tenderness revived as she listened to town praises for her son.

“After all, my child,” Madame Hochon said, “youth must have its fling. The dissipations of a soldier under the Empire must, of course, be greater than those of young men looked after by their fathers. Thanks to your son, Issoudun now breathes and sleeps in peace. If he can be kept away from the temptations of Paris, he will end by being a comfort to you.”

Philippe played the saint to his mother because he needed her. He did not wish to resort to Cesarine unless he remained an object of horror to Mademoiselle Brazier. He recognized Flore was essential to his uncle’s life and preferred to use her rather than bring in a dancer who might marry the old man. He conceived the idea of making his uncle marry Flore, legitimizing her position and keeping her—and the property—within reach. He asked his mother to visit Flore and treat her sisterly.

“I must confess, my dear mother,” he said in an affected tone before Monsieur and Madame Hochon, “that my uncle’s way of life is not becoming; he could, however, make Mademoiselle Brazier respected by the community if he chose. Wouldn’t it be far better for her to be Madame Rouget than the servant-mistress of an old bachelor? She had better obtain a definite right to his property by a marriage contract. If you, or Monsieur Hochon, or some good priest would speak to both parties, you might put a stop to the scandal which offends decent people.”

The next day Agathe and Madame Hochon repeated Philippe’s words at Flore’s bedside. In Issoudun the colonel was praised for his supposed nobility toward Flore. For a month Flore heard Goddet, Madame Hochon, Agathe, and Rouget urging a marriage. When the idea of becoming Madame Rouget enticed her, it was made clear she would be unwelcome in the Rouget family if she intended to turn Philippe out.

“Besides,” the doctor whispered, “you really owe him this good fortune. Max would never have allowed you to marry old Rouget. And, if you have children, you can revenge Max, for that will disinherit the Bridaus.”

Two months after the duel, in February 1823, the sick woman, urged by those around her and implored by Rouget, consented to receive Philippe; the sight of his scars moved her to tears, and his softened manner calmed her. By Philippe’s wish they were left alone.

“My dear child,” he said, “it is I who, from the start, have advised your marriage with my uncle; if you consent, it will take place as soon as you are recovered.”

“So they tell me,” she replied.

“Circumstances have compelled me to give you pain; it is natural therefore that I should wish to do you all the good I can. Wealth, respect, and a family position are worth more than what you have lost. You wouldn’t have been that fellow’s wife long after my uncle’s death, for I happen to know, through friends of his, that he intended to get rid of you. Come, my dear, let us understand each other and live happily. You shall be my aunt and nothing more. You will take care that my uncle does not forget me in his will; on my side, you shall see how well I will have you treated in the marriage contract. Keep calm, think it over, and we will talk later. All sensible people, indeed the whole town, urge you to put an end to your illegal position; no one will blame you for receiving me. It is well understood in the world that interests go before feelings. By the day of your marriage you will be handsomer than ever. The pallor of illness has given you an air of distinction, and on my honor, if my uncle did not love you so madly, you should be the wife of Colonel Bridau.”

Philippe left after planting the idea of revenge through children—a hint that stirred her. In this scene his manipulative phrasing masked calculation as sentiment; self-interest did the work of persuasion.

At the beginning of April 1823 Rouget’s hall hosted a splendid dinner to celebrate signing the marriage contract between Mademoiselle Flore Brazier and the old bachelor. Guests included Monsieur Heron, the witnesses, Mignonnet, Carpentier, Hochon, Goddet, the mayor, the curate, Agathe Bridau, Madame Hochon and Madame Borniche. The bride, well protected by Philippe and the two ladies, shone; the curate had instructed her for a fortnight and she was to take first communion the next day. Local journals praised the marriage as a revival of religion and moral correction; the “Journal du Cher” and the “Journal de l’Indre” lauded Philippe’s supposed disinterestedness.

By the contract Rouget secured to Flore a dower of one hundred thousand francs and a life annuity of thirty thousand.

After the wedding Agathe returned to Paris triumphant and told Joseph and Desroches the “good news.”

“Your son Philippe is too wily a man not to keep his paw on that inheritance,” said Desroches.

“You, and Joseph too, will always be unjust to that poor boy,” replied Madame Bridau, defending her son as statesmanlike.

“Why do you suppose that he will not succeed by honest means?” she demanded.

“You will see!” exclaimed Desroches. “Fortunate or unfortunate, Philippe will remain the man of the Rue Mazarin, the murderer of Madame Descoings, the domestic thief. But don’t worry yourself; he will manage to appear honest to the world.”

The morning after the marriage Philippe took Madame Rouget aside.

“My dear aunt,” he said, “you now belong to the family. Thanks to me, the law has tied the knot. Now, no nonsense. I intend that you and I should play above board. I know the tricks you will try against me; and I shall watch you like a duenna. You will never go out of this house except on my arm; and you will never leave me. As to what passes within the house, damn it, you’ll find me like a spider in the middle of his web.” He showed her a letter from Mariette and friends describing a plan to provide a trained actress to entrap Rouget and secure the property for Philippe; the letter detailed the girl’s arts and the social circle ready to exploit any fortune. The letter terrified Madame Rouget and exposed the calculated danger she had escaped.

“I trust you,” he said, fixing her with terrible meaning, “as you see; but I expect some return. I have made you my aunt intending to marry you some day. You are worth more to me than Esther in managing my uncle. In a year from now we must be in Paris; you will amuse yourself much better there than here; it is a perpetual carnival. I shall return to the army and become a general, and you will be a great lady. There’s our future; now work for it. But I must have a pledge to bind this agreement. You are to give me, within a month from now, a power of attorney from my uncle, which you must obtain under pretence of relieving him of the fatigues of business. Also, a month later, I must have a special power to transfer the income in the Funds. When that stands in my name, you and I have an equal interest in marrying each other. There it all is, my beautiful aunt, as plain as day. Between you and me there must be no ambiguity. I can marry my aunt at the end of a year’s widowhood; but I could not marry a disgraced girl.”

He left without waiting for an answer. Vedie, clearing the table, found her mistress pale and sweating. Flore felt as if she had fallen to the bottom of a precipice; the future seemed black. The damp chill of vaults, an instinctive fear of the man, oppressed her; yet a voice told her she deserved to have him for her master. She was helpless.

Flore had once had a room in Rouget’s house; now Madame Rouget belonged to her husband and was deprived of freedom. She briefly held out hope of bearing a child, but Philippe’s constant watch made that impossible. Benjamin served as Philippe’s innocent spy; Vedie trembled under his scrutiny. Flore felt deserted; without knowing how, she was certain that if he suspected pregnancy her doom would be sealed. The sound of his voice, the gambler’s eye, the slightest movement of the soldier—politeness cloaking brutality—made her shudder. The power of attorney he demanded he obtained as soon as he wanted it; Flore fell under his dominion as France had under Napoleon.

Like a butterfly caught in wax, Rouget rapidly dissipated into decay; in his decline the nephew remained cold and impassible as diplomats in convulsed times.

Philippe, now skeptical of Napoleon II., wrote to the minister of war through intermediaries to offer his services to the King. He explained Napoleon was gone; having been faithful, he was now free to serve the monarch. He asked for consideration and reinstatement at his rank, professing submission and assuring the minister of his fidelity. Accompanying the letter were requests for permission to go to Paris on urgent family business and endorsements from the mayor, the sub-prefect, and the commissary of police of Issoudun, praising Philippe’s conduct and citing newspaper articles about Rouget’s marriage.

Two weeks later Philippe received permission and a letter informing him that by order of the King he was, as a preliminary favor, reinstated lieutenant-colonel in the royal army.

Lieutenant-Colonel Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the helpless Rouget, and three days after their arrival escorted them to the Treasury, where Jean-Jacques assigned his income to Philippe. The exhausted old man and the Rabouilleuse were plunged by their nephew into the dissipations of actresses, journalists, artists, and equivocal women among whom Philippe had already wasted his youth; old Rouget found excitements that soon killed him. Instigated by Giroudeau, Lolotte, one of the handsomest of the Opera ballet-girls, was the amiable assassin of the old man. Rouget died after a splendid supper at Florentine’s, and Lolotte blamed a slice of pâté de foie gras; as the Strasburg masterpiece could make no defence, it was considered settled that the old man died of indigestion.

Madame Rouget thrived amid this décolleté society; but Philippe put her in charge of Mariette, and that monitress prevented the widow—whose mourning was diversified with a few amusements—from committing any actual follies.

In October, 1823, Philippe returned to Issoudun with a power of attorney from his aunt to liquidate his uncle’s estate; the business was soon over, and he returned to Paris in March, 1824, with sixteen hundred thousand francs—the net proceeds of old Rouget’s property, not counting the pictures, which had never left Monsieur Hochon’s hands. Philippe put the whole into Mongenod and Sons, where young Baruch Borniche was employed, on Hochon’s assurances of solvency. This house took his funds at six per cent per annum, on condition of three months’ notice for withdrawal.

One day Philippe visited his mother and invited her to his marriage, witnessed by Giroudeau, Finot, Nathan, and Bixiou. By the contract, the widow Rouget, whose portion was a million francs, secured to her future husband her whole fortune should she die childless. No invitations were sent; Philippe had designs. He lodged his wife in an appartement in the rue Saint-Georges, bought ready-furnished from Lolotte. Madame Bridau the younger thought it delightful; her husband rarely set foot in it. Without her knowledge, Philippe purchased in the rue de Clichy a magnificent hotel for two hundred and fifty thousand francs, paying one hundred and fifty thousand down and taking two years for the rest. He spent large sums altering the interior and furnishing it; in fact, he put his income for two years into this outlay. The restored pictures, then estimated at three hundred thousand francs, shone there.

The accession of Charles X. brought greater court favor to the family of the Duc de Chaulieu, whose eldest son, the Duc de Rhetore, was in the habit of seeing Philippe at Tullia’s. Under Charles X., the elder branch, believing itself secure, followed advice to encourage adherence of former Imperial soldiers. Philippe, who had likely made invaluable revelations about the conspiracies of 1820 and 1822, was appointed lieutenant-colonel in the regiment of the Duc de Maufrigneuse. That nobleman felt bound to protect the man from whom he had taken Mariette. The corps-de-ballet went for something, therefore, in the appointment. Moreover, it was decided in private councils to give a tinge of liberalism to the Dauphin’s surroundings. Philippe, now a sort of equerry to the Duc de Maufrigneuse, was presented to the Dauphin and Dauphine, who liked brusque soldierly characters noted for past fidelity. Philippe understood the part the Dauphin had to play and turned this spurious liberalism to his profit by getting appointed aide-de-camp to a marshal who stood well at court.

In January, 1827, Philippe, promoted in the Royal Guard as lieutenant-colonel in a regiment commanded by the Duc de Maufrigneuse, solicited ennoblement. Under the Restoration, nobility became a perquisite to “roturiers” serving in the Guard. Colonel Bridau had bought the estate of Brambourg and asked to entail it under the title of count. The favor was granted through his intimacies in high society, where he now appeared with the luxury of horses, carriages, and liveries; in short, the surroundings of a great lord. As soon as he saw himself gazetted as Comte de Brambourg, he began to frequent the house of a lieutenant-general of artillery, the Comte de Soulanges.

Insatiable and backed by mistresses of influential men, Philippe solicited the honor of being one of the Dauphin’s aides-de-camp. He had the audacity to tell the Dauphin that “an old soldier, wounded on many a battle-field and who knew real warfare, might, on occasion, be serviceable to Monseigneur.” Philippe, who could assume all tones of sycophancy, became in the highest social regions exactly what the position required; just as at Issoudun, he had copied Mignonnet’s respectability. He had a fine establishment and gave fêtes and dinners, admitting none of his old friends whose position might compromise him. He was pitiless to companions of former debauches and curtly refused Bixiou when that lively satirist asked him to say a word for Giroudeau, who wanted re-entry into the army after Florentine’s desertion.

“The man has neither manners nor morals,” said Philippe.

“Ha! did he say that of me?” cried Giroudeau. “Of me, who helped him get rid of his uncle!”

“We’ll pay him off yet,” said Bixiou.

Philippe intended to marry Mademoiselle Amelie de Soulanges and become a general commanding a Guard regiment. He asked so many favors that, to keep him quiet, they made him a Commander of the Legion of Honor and of Saint Louis. One rainy evening, as Agathe and Joseph returned home along the muddy streets, they met Philippe in full uniform, bedizened with orders, leaning back in a coupe lined with yellow silk, whose armorial bearings were surmounted with a count’s coronet. He was on his way to a fête at the Élysée-Bourbon; the wheels splashed his mother and brother as he waved a patronizing greeting.

“He’s going it, that fellow!” said Joseph. “Nevertheless, he might send us something better than mud in our faces.”

“He has such a fine position, in such high society, that we ought not to blame him for forgetting us,” said Madame Bridau. “When a man rises to so great a height, he has obligations to repay, sacrifices to make; it is natural he should not come to see us, though he may think of us all the same.”

“My dear fellow,” said the Duc de Maufrigneuse one evening to the new Comte de Brambourg, “I am sure your addresses will be favorably received; but to marry Amelie de Soulanges you must be free. What have you done with your wife?”

“My wife?” said Philippe, with a gesture, look, and accent worthy of Frederick Lemaitre. “Alas! I have the melancholy certainty of losing her. She has not a week to live. My dear duke, you don’t know what it is to marry beneath you. A woman who was a cook, who has the tastes of a cook! Who dishonors me—ah! I am much to be pitied. I explained my position to Madame la Dauphine. At the time of the marriage, it was a question of saving the family a million which my uncle left to that person. Happily, my wife took to drinking; at her death, I come into possession of that million, which is now in Mongenod and Sons. I have thirty thousand francs a year in the five per cents, and my entailed landed property brings forty thousand more. If Monsieur de Soulanges gets a marshal’s baton, I am on the high-road, with my title, to becoming general and peer of France. That will be the proper end of an aide-de-camp of the Dauphin.”

After the Salon of 1823, one of the leading painters obtained the management of a lottery-office near the Markets for Joseph Bridau’s mother. Agathe was soon able to exchange it for an office in the rue de Seine, in a house where Joseph had his atelier. The widow now hired an agent herself and was no longer an expense to her son. And yet, as late as 1828, though she directed an office owed to Joseph’s fame, Madame Bridau still doubted that fame, which was hotly contested as all true glory is. The great painter, struggling with genius, had enormous wants; he did not earn enough to pay for the luxuries that his relations and position in the young School of Art demanded. Though sustained by his Cénacle friends and by Mademoiselle des Touches, he did not please the bourgeoisie. The bourgeoisie, from whom money comes, never unties its purse for genius that is in question; unfortunately, Joseph had the classics and the Institute and their critics against him. Though backed by Gros and Géricault (Gérard), by whose influence he was decorated after the Salon of 1827, he obtained few orders. Moreover, Joseph gave way too much to imaginative fancies, producing inequality in his work which enemies used to deny his talent.

“High art is at a low ebb,” said his friend Pierre Grassou, who made daubs for bourgeois tastes.

“You ought to have a whole cathedral to decorate; that’s what you want,” declared Schinner; “then you would silence criticism with a master-stroke.”

Such speeches alarmed Agathe and confirmed her long-held judgment about Philippe and Joseph. Facts sustained that judgment in a provincial woman’s mind. Philippe, her favorite, was now a great man; his early errors were only youth’s ebullitions. Joseph, whose productions she could not appreciate—having seen them in progress too often—seemed no more advanced in 1828 than in 1816. Poor Joseph owed money and was bowed by debt; she felt he had chosen a worthless career that gave no return. She could not conceive why they had given him the Legion cross. Philippe, rich enough to cease gambling, a guest at Madame’s fêtes, the brilliant colonel appearing at reviews and processions in splendid uniforms with two crosses, realized all her maternal dreams. One day of public ceremony effaced from Agathe’s mind the sight of Philippe’s earlier misery; on that day he passed her at the same spot, in attendance on the Dauphin, with plumes in his shako and a pelisse gorgeous with gold and fur. Agathe, who to her artist son was now a devoted sister-figure, felt herself the mother of none but the dashing aide-de-camp. Proud of Philippe, she felt he made her life easy and happy—forgetting that the lottery-office enabling her to live came through Joseph.

One day Agathe noticed Joseph more worried than usual by his color-man’s bill and resolved, though cursing his profession in her heart, to free him from debts. She kept the house with the office’s proceeds and never asked Joseph for a farthing; she had no money of her own but relied on Philippe’s good heart and purse. For three years she had expected his visits; she imagined that if she appealed he would bring an enormous sum, and pictured the happiness of giving it to Joseph, whose judgment of his brother she found unfair.

Without telling Joseph, she wrote the following letter to Philippe:

  To Monsieur le comte de Brambourg:

  My dear Philippe,—You have not given the least little word of remembrance to your mother for five years. That is not right. You should remember the past, if only for the sake of your excellent brother. Joseph is now in need of money, and you are floating in wealth; he works, while you are flying from fête to fête. You now possess, all to yourself, the property of my brother. Little Borniche tells me you cannot have less than two hundred thousand francs a year. Well, then, come and see Joseph. During your visit, slip into the skull a few thousand-franc notes. Philippe, you owe them to us; nevertheless, your brother will feel grateful to you, not to speak of the happiness you will give

  Your mother,

  Agathe Bridau, née Rouget

Two days later the concierge brought to the atelier the terrible reply:

  My dear Mother,—A man does not marry a Mademoiselle Amelie de Soulanges without the purse of Fortunatus, if under the name of Comte de Brambourg he hides that of

  Your son,

  Philippe Bridau

Agathe fell half-fainting on the sofa; the letter dropped. The slight noise and her smothered exclamation startled Joseph, who had been rubbing in a sketch. He leaned his head round the canvas, saw his mother stretched out, and rushed to lift what seemed lifeless. He carried Agathe to her bed and sent for Horace Bianchon. When he could question her she told him of her letter and its reply. Joseph went to the atelier, picked up Philippe’s concise brutality, which had broken his mother’s heart and shattered the edifice of trust. When Joseph returned to her bedside he was silent; he did not speak of his brother during the three weeks of the poor woman’s death agony. Bianchon, who watched with devotion, told Joseph the truth on the first day of her seizure.

“At her age,” he said, “and under the circumstances, all we can hope is to make her death as little painful as possible.”

She herself felt so surely called that she asked for Abbé Loraux, her confessor for over twenty-two years. Alone with him, having poured out her griefs, she said—as she had said to Madame Hochon and repeated to herself—“What have I done to displease God? Have I not loved Him with all my soul? Have I wandered from the path of grace? What is my sin? Can I be guilty of wrong when I know not what it is? Have I time to repair it?”

“No,” said the old man gently. “Alas! your life seems pure and your soul spotless; but the eye of God is keener than that of his ministers. I see the truth too late; for you have misled even me.”

Hearing such words from lips always peaceful, Agathe rose suddenly and opened wide her eyes in terror.

“Tell me! tell me!” she cried.

“Be comforted,” said the priest. “Your punishment is a proof that you will receive pardon. God chastens his elect. Woe to those whose misdeeds meet with fortunate success; they will be kneaded again in humanity until sorely punished and brought to maturity. Your life, my daughter, has been one long error. You gave your heart to an unnatural son, in whom you made your glory, and you have misunderstood the child who is your true glory. You have been so unjust that you never saw the striking contrast between the brothers. You owe the comfort of your life to Joseph, while your other son has pillaged you repeatedly. The poor son, who loves you without equal return, gives you all the comfort your life has had; the rich son, who never thinks of you, despises you and desires your death—”

“Oh no,” she cried.

“Yes,” resumed the priest. “Your humble position stands in the way of his proud hopes. Mother, these are your sins! You have sinned by ignorance. Love Joseph now; give him your heart and pray for him, as I shall for you.”

The mother’s eyes opened to her life’s course. Illuminated, she wept. The old priest, moved by her repentance, left the room hastily.

Joseph returned about two hours after the confessor had left. He had been to borrow the money needed to pay pressing debts and sat quietly, thinking his mother asleep. He sprang up with a chill as he heard her say, brokenly, “Will he forgive me?”

“What is it, mother?” he exclaimed, shocked at her expression.

“Ah, Joseph! can you pardon me, my child?” she cried.

“For what?” he said.

“I have never loved you as you deserved to be loved.”

“Oh, what an accusation!” he cried. “Not loved me? For seven years have we not lived alone together? Have you not taken care of me? Are you not my gentle and indulgent companion? I was saying to Grassou only yesterday: ‘What comforts me is that I have such a good mother. She sees to everything; she takes care of my material wants without troubling me.’”

“No, Joseph, no; you have loved me, but I have not returned love for love. Ah! would I could live a little longer—Give me your hand.”

Agathe took his hand, kissed it, held it to her heart, and looked long at him, letting him see tenderness she had hitherto bestowed only on Philippe. The painter, apt at judging expression, saw how his mother’s heart opened and took her to his breast, crying, “My mother! oh, my mother!”

“Ah! I feel that I am forgiven!” she said. “God will confirm the child’s pardon of its mother.”

“You must be calm: don’t torment yourself; hear me. I feel loved enough in this one moment for all the past,” he said, laying her back on pillows.

During the two weeks’ struggle between life and death, each effusion of feeling seemed an expression of a lifetime. The mother thought only of her son; sustained by love, she was unaware of her sufferings. D’Arthez, Michel Chrestien, Fulgence Ridal, Pierre Grassou, and Bianchon often kept Joseph company, the friends serving as a band of brothers in thought and action. Joseph, inspired by feeling and genius, divined in his mother’s occasional expressions a desire hidden in her heart and said to d’Arthez, “She has loved that brigand Philippe too well not to want to see him before she dies.”

Joseph begged Bixiou, who frequented Bohemian regions where Philippe was still occasionally found, to persuade that shameless son to play, out of pity, a little comedy that might fold the mother in an illusory peace. Bixiou, an observing misanthropic scoffer, undertook the mission. When he told the Comte de Brambourg—who received him in a bedroom hung with yellow damask—Philippe laughed.

“What the devil do you want me to do there?” he cried. “The only service the poor woman can render me is to die as soon as she can; she would be a sorry figure at my marriage with Mademoiselle de Soulanges. The less my family is seen, the better my position. You can easily understand that I should like to bury the name of Bridau under all the monuments in Père-Lachaise. My brother irritates me by bringing the name into publicity. Look at it as if it were your own: if you were a deputy, with a tongue like yours, you would be as much feared as Chauvelin; you would be made Comte Bixiou, and director of the Beaux-Arts. Once there, how should you like it if your grandmother Descoings were to turn up? Would you want that worthy woman hanging on you? Damn it, you’d wish her six feet under ground. Come, breakfast with me, and let us talk of something else. I am a parvenu, my dear fellow, and I know it. I don’t choose that my swaddling-clothes shall be seen. My son will be more fortunate than I. The scamp will wish me dead; I expect it—or he won’t be my son.”

He rang for breakfast. “The fashionable world wouldn’t see you in your mother’s bedroom,” said Bixiou. “What would it cost you to seem to love that poor woman for a few hours?”

“Whew!” cried Philippe, winking. “So you come from them, do you? I’m an old camel, who knows all about genuflections. My mother makes the excuse of her last illness to get something out of me for Joseph. No, thank you!”

When Bixiou related this to Joseph, the poor painter was chilled to the soul.

“Does Philippe know I am ill?” asked Agathe the day after Bixiou’s errand.

Joseph left, suffocating with emotion. The Abbé Loraux, sitting by the bed, took her hand and said, “Alas! my child, you have never had but one son.” The words, which Agathe understood, conveyed a shock that began the end. She died twenty hours later.

In the delirium preceding death, she murmured, “Whom does Philippe take after?”

Joseph followed his mother to the grave alone. Philippe had gone on business to Orléans; in reality he was driven from Paris by the letter Joseph wrote a moment after Agathe’s last sigh:

  Monster! my poor mother has died of the shock your letter caused her. Wear mourning, but pretend illness; I will not suffer her assassin to stand at my side before her coffin.

  Joseph B.

Joseph, who no longer had the heart to paint though grief needed the distraction of labor, was surrounded by friends who vowed never to leave him alone. Thus, two weeks after Agathe’s funeral, Bixiou sat in the atelier with a group when the servant brought a letter from an old woman below.

  Monsieur,—To you, whom I scarcely dare to call my brother, I am forced to address myself, if only on account of the name I bear.--

Joseph turned the page and read the signature: “Comtesse Flore de Brambourg.” He shuddered, foreseeing new atrocity from his brother.

“That brigand,” he cried, “is the devil’s own. And he calls himself a man of honor! And he wears crosses on his breast! And he struts at court instead of being bastinadoed! And the scoundrel is called Monsieur le Comte!”

“There are many like him,” said Bixiou.

“After all,” said Joseph, “the Rabouilleuse deserves her fate. She’s not worth pitying; she’d have had my neck wrung without saying, ‘He’s innocent.’”

Joseph flung the letter; Bixiou caught and read it aloud:

  Is it decent that the Comtesse Bridau de Brambourg should die in a hospital, no matter what her faults? If so, will you, who are the friend of Doctor Bianchon, ask him for a permit to let me enter a hospital?

  The person who carries this letter has been eleven consecutive days to the hotel de Brambourg, rue de Clichy, without getting any help from my husband. Poverty prevents me from employing a lawyer to demand what is due me, that I may die with decency. Nothing can save me, I know that. If you are unwilling to see your unhappy sister-in-law, send me, at least, the money to end my days. Your brother desires my death; he has always desired it. He warned me that he knew three ways of killing a woman, but I had not the sense to foresee the one he has employed.

  In case you will relieve me and judge for yourself my misery, I live in the rue du Houssay, corner of the rue Chantereine, on the fifth floor. If I cannot pay my rent to-morrow I shall be put out--and then, where can I go?

  Your sister-in-law,

  Comtesse Flore de Brambourg.

“What a pit of infamy!” cried Joseph; “there is something under it all.”

“Let us send for the woman who brought the letter; we may get the preface,” said Bixiou.

The woman appeared, a mass of old gowns fringed with mud, mounted on thick legs with ragged stockings and leaking shoes. Above the mound rose a head like Charlet’s scavenger-women, caparisoned with a filthy bandanna.

“What is your name?” said Joseph as Bixiou sketched her, leaning on an umbrella dated year II.

“Madame Gruget, at your service. I’ve seen better days,” she said. “If my poor girl hadn’t loved some one too much, you wouldn’t see me what I am. She drowned herself in the river, my poor Ida,—saving your presence! I’ve had the folly to nurse up a quaterne, and that’s why, at seventy-seven, I’m obliged to take care of sick folks for ten sous a day, and go—”

“—without clothes?” said Bixiou. “My grandmother nursed up a trey, but she dressed properly.”

“Out of my ten sous I pay for lodging—”

“What’s wrong with the lady you nurse?”

“In the first place, she hasn’t any money; and she has a disease that scares doctors. She owes me for sixty days’ nursing; that’s why I keep on. The husband, who is a count—she is really a countess—will no doubt pay me when she is dead; so I’ve lent her all I had. I haven’t anything left; all I did have has gone to the pawn-brokers. She owes me forty-seven francs and twelve sous, besides thirty francs for nursing. She wants to kill herself with charcoal. I tell her it ain’t right; I’ve had to get the concierge to look after her while I’m gone, or she’ll jump out the window.”

“But what’s the matter with her?” said Joseph.

“Ah! monsieur, the doctor from the Sisters’ hospital came; but as to the disease,” said Madame Gruget, modestly, “he told me she must go to the hospital. The case is hopeless.”

“Let us go and see her,” said Bixiou.

“Here,” said Joseph, “take these ten francs.”

Plunging his hand into his skull and taking out all his remaining money, Joseph called a coach and went for Bianchon, who was fortunately home. Bixiou ran to the rue de Bussy for Desroches. The four friends reached Flore’s retreat in the rue du Houssay an hour later.

“That Mephistopheles on horseback, named Philippe Bridau,” said Bixiou as they climbed the steep stair, “has sailed his boat cleverly to get rid of his wife. You know Lousteau? well, Philippe paid him a thousand francs a month to keep Madame Bridau in the society of Florine, Mariette, Tullia, and the Val-Noble. When Philippe saw his crab-girl so used to pleasure and dress that she couldn’t do without them, he stopped paying and left her to get it as she could—it is easy to know how. By the end of eighteen months the brute forced his wife lower and lower; then, by the help of a young officer, he gave her a taste for drinking. As he rose, his wife sank; the countess is now in the mud. I don’t know what means Philippe has lately taken to get rid of her. I am anxious to study this drama, for I am determined to avenge Joseph. Alas, friends,” he added, leaving them in doubt whether jesting, “give a man over to a vice and you’ll get rid of him. Didn’t Hugo say: ‘She loved a ball, and died of it’? So it is. Vice! vice! my dear friends, do you want to know what vice is? It is the Bonneau of death.”

“Then you’ll die of a joke,” said Desroches, laughing.

Above the fourth floor, they climbed a ladder-like attic stair. Though Joseph remembered Flore’s beauty, he was unprepared for the hideous spectacle. In a room with bare walls under the attic roof, on a cot with a scanty mattress, a woman lay green as a body drowned, thin as a consumptive. Her head, nearly hairless, was bound with a checked handkerchief; circles round the hollow eyes were red; eyelids like the pellicle of an egg. Nothing remained of the captivating body but an ignoble bony structure. As Flore saw Joseph, two great tears rolled down her cheeks.

“She can still weep!” whispered Bixiou. “A strange sight—tears from dominos! It’s like the miracle of Moses.”

“How burnt up!” cried Joseph.

“In the fires of repentance,” said Flore. “I cannot get a priest; I have nothing, not even a crucifix, to help me see God. Ah, monsieur!” she cried, raising arms like carved wood, “I am a guilty woman; but God never punished any one as he has punished me! Philippe killed Max, who advised dreadful things, and now he has killed me. God uses him as a scourge!”

“Leave me alone with her,” said Bianchon, “and let me see if the disease is curable.”

“If you cure her, Philippe Bridau will die of rage,” said Desroches. “I will draw up a statement of her condition. He has not brought her before the courts as an adulteress, and thus her conjugal rights stand: he shall have the shame of a suit. But first we must remove the Comtesse to Doctor Dubois’s private hospital in the rue du Faubourg-Saint-Denis. She will be cared for there. Then I will summon the count for restoration of the conjugal home.”

“Bravo, Desroches!” cried Bixiou. “What a pleasure to do so much good that will make some people feel so badly!”

Ten minutes later Bianchon joined them. “I am going straight to Despleins,” he said. “He can save her by an operation. Ah! he will take care of the case, for her abuse of liquor has developed a disease thought lost.”

“Wag of a mangler! Isn’t there but one disease in life?” cried Bixiou.

Bianchon was already gone. Two hours later Flore was removed to Dubois’s hospital. Three weeks later the “Hospital Gazette” published an account of one of modern surgery’s boldest operations on a case designated by the initials “F. B.” The patient died—more from exhaustion produced by misery and starvation than from treatment.

No sooner did she die than the Comte de Brambourg went, in deep mourning, to call on the Comte de Soulanges and inform him of his sad loss. Soon after, fashion whispered that the Comte de Soulanges would marry his daughter to a parvenu of merit about to be appointed brigadier-general and given command of a Royal Guard regiment. De Marsay told Eugène de Rastignac this news at the Rocher de Cancale, where Bixiou happened to be.

“It shall not take place!” said the witty artist to himself.

Among the many old friends Philippe refused to recognize were some unable to revenge themselves; but he had wounded Bixiou, who, thanks to brilliant qualities, was everywhere received and never forgave an insult. One day at the Rocher de Cancale, before many well-bred people, Philippe had replied to Bixiou—who spoke of visiting him at the hotel de Brambourg—“You can come and see me when you are made a minister.”

“Am I to turn Protestant before I can visit you?” said Bixiou, feigning misunderstanding; but he thought, “You may be Goliath, but I have my sling.”

The next day he went to an actor friend, metamorphosed himself by dress into a secularized priest with green spectacles, drove to the hotel de Soulanges, and, received by the count on a message that he had serious business, related in a feigned voice the whole story of the dead countess and the secret particulars confided to him by Bianchon; the histories of Agathe’s death, of old Rouget’s death (which the Comte had boasted of), of Madame Descoings’s death, of the theft from the newspaper, and of Philippe’s early private morals.

“Monsieur le comte, don’t give him your daughter until you have made every inquiry; interrogate his former comrades,—Bixiou, Giroudeau, and others.”

Three months later the Comte de Brambourg gave a supper to du Tillet, Nucingen, Eugène de Rastignac, Maxime de Trailles, and Henri de Marsay. The host accepted their half-consolatory condolences about his rupture with Soulanges with nonchalance.

“You can do better,” said Maxime de Trailles.

“How much must a man have to marry a demoiselle de Grandlieu?” asked Philippe of de Marsay.

“You? They wouldn’t give you the ugliest of the six for less than ten millions,” answered de Marsay insolently.

“Bah!” said Rastignac. “With an income of two hundred thousand francs you can have Mademoiselle de Langeais; she is thirty, ugly, and penniless; that ought to suit you.”

“I shall have ten millions two years from now,” said Philippe Bridau.

“It is now the 16th of January, 1829,” cried du Tillet, laughing. “I have been hard at work for ten years and have not made as much as that yet.”

“We’ll take counsel of each other,” said Bridau; “you shall see how well I understand finance.”

“How much do you really own?” asked Nucingen.

“Three millions, excluding my house and estate, which I shall not sell; in fact, I cannot, for the property is now entailed and goes with the title.”

Nucingen and du Tillet exchanged a sly glance; du Tillet said to Philippe, “My dear count, I shall be delighted to do business with you.”

De Marsay intercepted the look, which meant, “We will have those millions.” The two bank magnates were central in political affairs and could, at a given time, manipulate matters at the Bourse to play a sure game against Philippe when probabilities seemed for him but were secretly against him.

The occasion came. In July, 1830, du Tillet and Nucingen had helped the Comte de Brambourg make fifteen hundred thousand francs; he trusted them. Philippe, owing his rise to the Restoration, was misled by profound contempt for “civilians”; he believed in the triumph of the Ordonnances and was bent on playing for a rise; du Tillet and Nucingen, sure of a revolution, played against him for a fall. The crafty pair confirmed his judgment and seemed to share his convictions; they encouraged his hopes of doubling his millions and apparently took steps to help. Philippe fought like a man with four millions depending on the struggle. His devotion was so noticeable that he received orders to go to Saint-Cloud with the Duc de Maufrigneuse and attend a council. This mark probably saved his life; for when the order came on July 25th he was intending to charge and sweep the boulevards, where he would undoubtedly have been shot by his friend Giroudeau, who commanded a division of attackers.

A month later nothing was left of Colonel Bridau’s immense fortune but his house and furniture, his estates, and the pictures from Issoudun. He committed the folly of believing in the Restoration of the elder branch, to which he remained faithful until 1834. Jealous of Giroudeau’s colonelcy, he re-entered the service. Unluckily, in 1835 he obtained command of a regiment in Algeria, where he remained three dangerous years, always hoping for a general’s epaulets. But some malignant influence—General Giroudeau—continually balked him. Grown hard and brutal, Philippe exceeded ordinary severity and was hated, despite bravery à la Murat.

At the beginning of 1839, while making a dash upon Arabs during a retreat before superior forces, he flung himself against the enemy with only a single company and fell in with the main body. The battle was bloody and terrible, man to man, and few horsemen escaped. Seeing their colonel surrounded, these men, at some distance, were unwilling to perish attempting to rescue him. They heard his cry: “Your colonel! to me! a colonel of the Empire!” but they rejoined the regiment. Philippe met a horrible death: the Arabs hacked him to pieces with scimitars and cut off his head.

Joseph, who was married about this time through Comte de Serizy to the daughter of a millionaire farmer, inherited his brother’s Paris house and the estate of Brambourg by the entail, which Philippe, had he foreseen this, would have broken. The chief pleasure Joseph derived from his inheritance was the fine collection of paintings from Issoudun. He now possesses an income of sixty thousand francs, and his father-in-law continues to pile up five-franc pieces. Though Joseph Bridau paints magnificent pictures and renders important services to artists, he is not yet a member of the Institute. As result of a clause in the deed of entail, he is now Comte de Brambourg, a fact which often makes him roar with laughter among his atelier friends.
