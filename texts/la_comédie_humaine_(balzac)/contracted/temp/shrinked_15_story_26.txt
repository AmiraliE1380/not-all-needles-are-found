Bianchon, a Parisian physician, trained under the troubled Desplein, who grappled with atheism and sought a legacy. Desplein, rejecting the soul's existence, faced mixed judgments about his eccentricity. Despite his poverty, Bianchon was known for his integrity, and Desplein mentored him in medicine and society. Intrigued by Desplein's church visit, Bianchon noted Desplein's dismissal of mass's significance. When questioned about his attendance, Desplein vaguely claimed he had founded the service, adding to his mystery.

After the 1830 Revolution, Bianchon attended mass with Desplein, seeking answers. Desplein recounted his past of extreme poverty and struggles to finance his education, feeling misunderstood by those who hadn’t faced similar hardships. His bitterness stemmed from witnessing others' selfishness in competitive Paris. A humble neighbor, Bourgeat, supported Desplein through debts and education. Their bond deepened, and Desplein cared for Bourgeat until his death, honoring his faith with masses and reflecting on their complex relationship.
