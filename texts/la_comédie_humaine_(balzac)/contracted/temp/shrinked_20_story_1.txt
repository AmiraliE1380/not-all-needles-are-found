Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, stood a charming old house that let historians reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls displayed timber patterns and plaster cracks. A weather-warped triangular roof jutted over the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer; the upper story used overlapping planks to avoid overburdening the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man in a cloak stood beneath a shop awning across from the house, studying it with the zeal of an antiquary. The relic of sixteenth-century civic life showed many peculiarities. He ignored the first floor’s narrow boarded windows and focused on the third floor’s modest sash windows, poorly made and dimmed by green-tinted glass. Occasionally he glanced down at the shop, where small details amused him.

A large wooden beam, supported by four pillars, bore layers of paint and a comical depiction of a cat playing racket, which pleased the young man. The caricatured cat, racket in paw and an oversized ball, had grown more absurd with age. The faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a remnant of a whimsical past when such signs lured customers.

Dressed in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, he looked as if he had just come from a wedding or ball; white gloves and disheveled hair hinted at recent festivities. Despite market gardeners hurrying by, the street was unusually still. His striking features—pale face set off by a dazzling white cravat and expressive eyes—drew the attention of the shopkeepers at the “Cat and Racket.”

Suddenly three apprentices appeared at the dormer window, laughing and teasing him, playfully sprinkling him with shaving cream; he shrugged off his annoyance. Then a young girl’s face appeared at the window, her beauty like a Raphael Virgin—innocent and unembarrassed. On seeing him she withdrew and the window slammed shut, leaving him disappointed.

Meanwhile the shutters of the “Cat and Racket” opened and a manservant hung the cloth sign reading “Guillaume, successor to Chevrel.” The shop’s exterior, guarded by iron bars, hid packages wrapped in brown linen. Despite its old-fashioned look, Monsieur Guillaume was known for having the best-stocked establishment among Parisian cloth merchants and a reputation for honesty; he secured profits without flattery or costly gifts, though fellow traders warned against his notary, notorious for heavy discounts.

The old merchant stood at his shop threshold on the Rue Saint-Denis, awakening to the morning bustle. Monsieur Guillaume, in traditional attire, watched a curious stranger inspect his business. His patient, shrewd face reflected years of experience and a staunch attachment to old customs. He ran a strict household and maintained a rigorous regime for his three apprentices, who feared his vigilance.

As the apprentices began their day, Guillaume noticed the stranger’s anxious glances and suspected ulterior motives. The eldest stepped outside and thought he saw Mademoiselle Augustine leaving in haste; he hailed a cab, which eased the apprentices’ worries about a prank played on her.

Guillaume reprimanded them for idleness, recalling how he had worked harder in his youth. Though the apprentices came from reasonably comfortable backgrounds and paid for their board, he demanded a strong work ethic, contrasting his order and economy with modern shops where youths expected quick wealth. The shop itself was meticulously kept, a testament to his values. The apprentices were not permitted to join the family at dessert, a sign of Madame Guillaume’s frugality, though they still attended Mass with the family.

The household was governed by strict traditions set by Madame Guillaume. The elder daughter, Virginie, mirrored her mother’s piety and sternness; Augustine, eighteen, was delicate and charming but overshadowed by maternal expectations. Their lives centered on domestic duties with little time outside, so they treasured family gatherings and the occasional visitor.

Augustine secretly yearned for more than their cloistered existence, feeding a desire for passion with romantic novels. That longing deepened Joseph Lebas’s affection for her; Joseph, the eldest apprentice, earned a modest salary after years of service. Guillaume regarded Joseph as a suitable match for Virginie, though Joseph’s true feelings were for Augustine. Guillaume mused about his daughters’ futures in a changing world where suitable suitors might become scarce.

From that day the old merchant, grieving his eldest daughter's decline, remembered marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances resembling Joseph Lebas and Virginie. Marrying his daughter would settle a sacred debt—repay an orphan for a past kindness. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, noted the fifteen-year gap between him and Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder daughter must marry first. The warm-hearted, tall assistant suffered in silence.

To understand, rewind months before the story begins. One evening a young painter paused outside the darkened shop of the Cat and Racket, seized by a scene any artist would covet. The unlit shop seemed cavernous; a dining room lit by a hanging lamp cast a warm, Dutch-like glow. The family—Augustine and a chubby maid among them—formed a modest, striking tableau. Fresh from Rome and weary of Italian grandeur, the painter hungered for simple nature. He had sought the meditative maiden of his pictures; the living scene stirred deep admiration. Augustine, pensive and scarcely touched by food, was illuminated by the lamp into something almost angelic. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

The next day he retreated to his studio to capture that spell. He visited the Cat and Racket repeatedly, sometimes in disguise, to study Augustine. For eight months he devoted himself to love and art, neglecting friends and previous pursuits. One morning his friend Girodet pierced his isolation to ask about his Salon submission. The artist produced two masterpieces; Girodet, recognizing his friend's passion, embraced him.

“You are in love?” Girodet asked; the artist nodded. They agreed great work springs from such feeling. Girodet warned against showing the pieces at the Salon, doubting the public would grasp their depth. Still, the works were exhibited and caused a sensation. The Interior sparked a genre shift, while the portrait became unforgettable, earning acclaim and offers the artist refused.

These public events felt remote from the monastic life on Rue Saint-Denis. During a visit to Madame Guillaume, the notary’s wife mentioned the exhibition, rousing Augustine’s curiosity. With her cousin’s help she obtained permission to visit the Louvre. Seeing the portrait, she trembled: it was herself, and she felt terror mixed with excitement. The artist stood nearby and whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Desperate to escape the crowd, Augustine urged her cousin to leave, but they were carried toward the celebrated painting. Overcome, she wept at the sight. In a brief, silent exchange she gestured to the artist; he acknowledged her with a nod. That moment kindled both guilt and exhilaration. The Salon’s chaos and the artist's gaze intoxicated her; new emotions surged where none had been before.

As they left, the painter glowed with joy; Augustine, caught in the whirlwind, realized she was loved. She feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition, yet her mother’s plans to visit the Salon filled her with dread. That night she reflected, feeling a void in her mundane life and longing for the elegance she had always desired. The thought of being the wife of a genius filled her with hope despite her modest upbringing. The next day her parents returned from the Salon disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. Still, news of the paintings’ disappearance and the lost shawl revealed to Augustine a sensitivity she had not known she possessed.

On the morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had won Augustine’s heart, found it hard to declare his love because of the household’s constraints. Augustine sat in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume and was closely watched by her mother, making any approach difficult. Fearing a rival among the assistants, Theodore bribed a maid to arrange discreet exchanges and they agreed to meet during Mass at Saint-Leu.

Meanwhile the Cat and Racket household was absorbed in stock-taking, with Monsieur Guillaume overseeing inventory and accounts. The family busied themselves verifying goods; Augustine’s absent-mindedness passed unnoticed. After weeks of work the stock-taking ended and the family enjoyed an evening out to see Cendrillon while the apprentices celebrated elsewhere.

The next morning Guillaume resumed his routine and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to discuss the profitable results. Impressed by Joseph’s contributions, Guillaume proposed making him a partner. Overwhelmed, Joseph confessed his love for Augustine; Guillaume first misunderstood, thinking he meant Virginie. Once corrected, Guillaume agreed to consider Joseph’s feelings and suggested Joseph accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie exchanged glances at a nervous Joseph. Madame Guillaume, unaware of the budding romance between Augustine and Theodore, chastised Augustine for inattention at church and sent her to her room, prompting a heated discussion between her parents about their daughter’s future.

Summoned before them, Augustine confessed she loved Theodore de Sommervieux, emphasizing his respectable background. Monsieur Guillaume voiced concern about the uncertainties of artists, but Augustine defended Theodore as wealthy and of good family, which intrigued her father. Then Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore’s recent honors and fortune, advocating for the match. Her persuasive account began to sway parents who had been reluctant about their daughter marrying above her station.

As the day progressed the atmosphere shifted from conflict to acceptance. Monsieur Guillaume informed Joseph of the developments. By evening the family gathered to celebrate the potential union; Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket as a symbol of shared happiness. Monsieur Guillaume consented to the marriage, though he stressed financial prudence and advised Augustine to consult him on money matters.

In the end the lovers found solace in mutual affection, and the household, once tense, embraced the prospect of a joyful future together, relieved and hopeful.

A few months after a memorable Sunday, the high altar of Saint-Leu saw two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore were radiant; Virginie, dressed simply, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to have Virginie married before Augustine, but the priests focused on the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie for making a sensible match, while others envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned that such extravagance would lead to ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt justified in his cautious approach to settlements. After the ball and supper, Monsieur and Madame Guillaume returned home, Monsieur and Madame Lebas to their old residence, and the joyous artist carried Augustine to their well-adorned apartment.

Their first year together was blissful. Theodore showered Augustine with affection; she, lost in love, believed her devotion would always charm him and often revealed a naïveté about the world. As the year passed, Theodore returned to work—especially with a child expected—and reconnected with friends, frequenting the Duchesse de Carigliano's gatherings for the social validation of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine enjoyed society but soon realized her lack of sophistication compared to Theodore's artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was genuine but superficial, and a disconnect grew. Theodore, once captivated, began to feel strained by their differing intellectual realms. He hoped the studio environment might inspire Augustine, but she struggled to adapt; her innocence clashed with the artists’ freer manners at gatherings.

Gradually Theodore's affection waned and Augustine felt the weight of his indifference. Despite trying to fulfill her duties and reignite his love, she sensed a widening distance. Three years into their marriage, she became consumed by sorrow. The artist's increasing enthusiasm for the Duchesse only deepened her despair, as she feared being supplanted by a more experienced woman.

Determined to bridge the gap, Augustine resolved to educate herself in poetry and art. Her attempts, however earnest, met frustration: she could not cultivate the natural wit and ease Theodore admired, and her efforts often backfired, leaving her feeling more inadequate.

In desperation she sought refuge with her family, returning to her childhood home where Virginie and brother-in-law Joseph Lebas ran their drapery with a steady contentment that contrasted sharply with her turmoil. Joseph's practical, businesslike advice only heightened her conflict, showing a different, more mundane kind of happiness that made her envy them yet doubt herself.

Ultimately Augustine returned home burdened by uncertainty, longing for the love she once shared with Theodore, like a patient trying every remedy in hope of healing a fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter warmly; her visit brightened their monotonous lives. For four years they had drifted, dwelling on past business successes and failures, notably the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume checked on his shop, the Cat and Racket, and traded memories with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume, now on the Army's clothing consulting board thanks to her son-in-law, filled their home with extravagant, often tasteless decor. Their nostalgia and stagnation struck Augustine as a stark contrast to her own turbulent experiences, leaving her both proud and appalled at their vacuous existence.

Despite her inner turmoil, Augustine displayed her recent achievements and aired marital grievances, which her parents eagerly absorbed. Madame Guillaume was especially curious about life with Theodore, Augustine's husband, a painter. Augustine mentioned his late nights and unconventional habits, prompting her mother’s disapproval of his lifestyle and profession. The couple’s incredulity grew as Augustine defended Theodore, sparking a heated debate about genius and marital responsibility.

When Augustine hinted at divorce, her parents were shocked into action; Monsieur Guillaume was ready to support her. Overwhelmed by their concern and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine rejected separation, realizing her pain could scarcely be understood by them. She felt isolated, convinced that only those who had shared similar experiences could truly empathize.

Resolved to win back Theodore’s affection by other means, Augustine decided to consult the Duchess de Carigliano—not to reclaim his heart outright but to learn how to captivate him. Approaching the Duchess’s opulent home, she felt both envy and admiration for its elegance. Meeting the Duchess, Augustine was struck by the woman’s beauty and charm but also sensed rivalry.

The Duchess responded with a mix of sympathy and shrewd counsel, stressing the importance of a composed facade and control when dealing with men of genius. She advised cultivating mystery and not revealing the full measure of one’s affections, framing such guardedness as strategy rather than mere manipulation. Initially disheartened, Augustine absorbed the Duchess’s lessons, recognizing that her love for Theodore might require a different approach.

The Duchess gave Augustine a portrait of herself painted by Theodore, believing it could rekindle his interest. Filled with hope, Augustine resolved to embody the image in the painting. When Theodore returned, she presented the portrait; his reaction was fury and jealousy, igniting a tumultuous confrontation.

The quarrel escalated, exposing the depths of Theodore’s passion and insecurity and culminating in a violent outburst. The aftermath left Augustine shattered; her dreams of mending their marriage lay in ruins. The next morning, her mother found her consumed by despair amid the remnants of the confrontation.

Augustine’s tragic fate was sealed as she succumbed to the pressures of her turbulent life, dying young. Her story stands as a poignant reminder of the struggles women face pursuing love and fulfillment, especially when entangled with the complexities of artistic genius. The inscription on her grave reflects that tragedy, prompting those who knew her to ponder the challenges of balancing passion and stability in relationships.
