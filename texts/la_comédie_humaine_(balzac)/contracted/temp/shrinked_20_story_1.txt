Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, stood a charming old house that helped historians reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls bore hieroglyphic-like timber patterns and cracked plaster. A weather-warped triangular roof overhung the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer window; the upper story used overlapping planks to avoid overburdening the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man, cloaked like an antiquary, stood under a shop awning across from the house, studying its sixteenth-century peculiarities. He ignored the first floor’s narrow boarded windows and focused on the third floor’s modest sash windows—poorly crafted and obscured by green-tinted glass. Occasionally he glanced at the shop below, where small amusing details caught his eye.

A large wooden beam, supported by four pillars, showed many layers of paint and bore a comical painting of a cat playing rackets. The cat, racket in paw and oversized ball nearby, had become an increasingly absurd caricature. A faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a relic of a whimsical past when such signs drew customers.

The young man, in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, looked as if he had come from a wedding or ball; his white gloves and disheveled hair hinted at recent revelry. Despite market gardeners hurrying by, the street felt unusually still. His striking features—a pale face set against a dazzling white cravat—and expressive eyes drew the attention of the shopkeepers at the “Cat and Racket.”

Suddenly three apprentices appeared at the dormer window, laughing and teasing him. They sprinkled him with shaving cream; he remained indifferent and brushed it off. Then a young girl’s face emerged, her beauty recalling a Raphael Virgin. Innocent and unembarrassed, she caught sight of him, retreated, and slammed the window shut, leaving him disappointed.

Meanwhile the shutters of the “Cat and Racket” opened and a manservant hung a cloth sign: “Guillaume, successor to Chevrel.” The shop’s iron-barred exterior concealed numerous packages wrapped in brown linen. Despite its old-fashioned look, Monsieur Guillaume was known for having the best-stocked store among Parisian cloth merchants and a reputation for honesty—securing profits without flattery, though fellow traders warned against his notary’s heavy discounts.

The old merchant stood at his shop threshold on the Rue Saint-Denis, surveying the street as if waking from a long sleep. Monsieur Guillaume, in traditional attire, watched a stranger who lingered too long, his patient, shrewd face betraying years of experience. A staunch traditionalist, he lamented lost customs and imposed a strict routine on his three apprentices, who feared his keen eye.

When the apprentices began their day, Guillaume noticed the stranger’s anxious glances and suspected ulterior motives. The eldest, curious, stepped outside and thought he saw Mademoiselle Augustine hurrying away; he quickly hailed a cab, which relieved them about a prank they had been planning on her. Guillaume soon reproached them for idleness, recalling how he worked faster in his youth. Despite their comfortable backgrounds, he insisted on a rigorous work ethic, in contrast to modern shops where apprentices expected early comforts. The shop itself was kept meticulously, reflecting the order and economy he prized.

Though the apprentices paid for board, they were excluded from the family dessert—a mark of Madame Guillaume’s frugality. They attended Mass with the family; the eldest apprentice, Joseph Lebas, earned a modest wage after years of service. Guillaume thought him a suitable match for his elder daughter, Virginie, though Joseph secretly loved the younger daughter, Augustine.

The household ran on strict tradition. Virginie and Augustine had been raised under their mother’s austere rule: Virginie resembled her mother—devout and severe—while eighteen-year-old Augustine was delicate, charming, and often overshadowed by maternal expectations. Their lives centered on domestic duties with little exposure to the outside world, so they prized family gatherings and rare visitors.

Augustine yearned for something beyond this cloistered existence; secret readings of romantic novels likely kindled her longing for a more passionate life. This longing deepened Joseph’s affection for her, while Virginie, lacking suitors, also felt drawn to him. Amid the monotony of shop life, quiet attachments formed, and Monsieur Guillaume, aware of changing times, wondered about his daughters’ prospects in a world where suitors might grow scarce.

From that day the old merchant, saddened by his eldest daughter's decline, recalled how he had married Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like Joseph Lebas’s and Virginie’s. Marrying off his daughter would settle a sacred debt by repaying an orphan for a past benefit. Joseph Lebas, now thirty-three, knew the fifteen-year gap between him and Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rigid rule that the elder daughter marry first. The lovesick assistant, warm-hearted yet tall and broad-chested, therefore suffered in silence.

To understand, we must rewind a few months. One evening a young painter paused outside the darkened Cat and Racket, captivated by a scene any artist would envy. The unlit shop revealed a dining room glowing from a hanging lamp, its warm light like a Dutch interior. The family—Augustine among them, and a chubby maid—formed a peaceful, modest tableau. Fresh from Rome, the painter craved genuine nature after immersion in grand art; his Italian passion left him longing for a simple, meditative maiden he’d only seen in pictures. The living scene stirred him: Augustine appeared pensive and untouched by food; the lamp made her face almost angelic. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

The next day he retreated to his studio determined to capture the moment. He visited the Cat and Racket repeatedly, even disguising himself to study Augustine. For eight months he lived in love and art, neglecting friends and routine. One morning his friend Girodet broke his isolation, asking about his Salon submission. The artist revealed two masterpieces; Girodet, recognizing the depth of feeling, embraced him. “You are in love?” Girodet asked; the artist nodded. They agreed great works spring from such passion. Girodet warned against showing them at the Salon, doubting public taste, but the works were exhibited and created a sensation. The Interior launched a genre shift, while the portrait became unforgettable, winning acclaim and offers the artist declined.

These public events contrasted sharply with the monastic life in Rue Saint-Denis until Madame Guillaume, the notary’s wife, mentioned the exhibition during a visit. Augustine, with her cousin’s help, obtained permission to visit the Louvre. Seeing the portrait, she trembled—recognizing herself, feeling terror and excitement. The artist, nearby, whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Intent on escaping the crowd, Augustine urged her cousin to leave, but they were drawn closer to the celebrated painting. Overwhelmed, she wept at sight of the work. In a quiet exchange she made a gesture to the artist; he acknowledged her with a nod. This brief connection ignited guilt and exhilaration. The Salon’s chaos, combined with the artist’s gaze, intoxicated her; she felt loved and experienced emotions she had never before known.

As they left, the painter radiated joy, and Augustine realized she was loved. Her words echoed, deepening her turmoil. She feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition with her cousin, but her mother’s plan to visit the Salon filled her with dread. That night she reflected, feeling a void in her humdrum life and longing for the elegance she’d always admired. The thought of being a genius’s wife filled her with hope despite her upbringing. The next day her parents returned disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. Yet the paintings’ disappearance revealed to Augustine a sensitivity women often possess instinctively.

On the morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had won Augustine's heart, struggled to declare his love because her household constrained every approach. Seated in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume, Augustine was closely watched by her mother, making it difficult for Theodore to come near. Fearing a rival among the assistants and feeling hemmed in by the household's eyes, he bribed a maid to facilitate exchanges, and they arranged to meet discreetly during Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family was absorbed in stock-taking under Monsieur Guillaume's supervision, verifying inventory and balancing accounts. They were oblivious to Augustine's emotional strain; despite her absent-mindedness, no one noticed her longing for Theodore. After weeks of work the stock-taking finished, and the family relaxed: they attended a performance of Cendrillon while the apprentices enjoyed an evening out.

The next morning Guillaume returned to routine and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to review the profitable results; he proposed making Joseph a partner in recognition of his contributions. Overwhelmed, Joseph revealed his love for Augustine, but Guillaume misheard and thought he meant Virginie. After some confusion, Guillaume agreed to consider Joseph's feelings for Augustine and suggested he accompany her to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie exchanged looks at a nervous Joseph. Madame, unaware of Augustine's romance with Theodore, chastised Augustine for inattentiveness during church and, after the service, ordered her to her room, prompting a heated discussion between her parents about their daughter's future.

Summoned before them, Augustine confessed her love for Theodore de Sommervieux, stressing his respectable background. Monsieur Guillaume voiced concern about artists, recalling past experience, but Augustine defended Theodore as wealthy and from a good family, which caught her father's interest. At that moment Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore's recent honors and increased fortune, advocating the match. Her persuasive account began to sway Monsieur and Madame Guillaume, who had been resistant to their daughter marrying above her station.

As the day passed the atmosphere shifted from conflict to acceptance. Monsieur Guillaume informed Joseph of the developments. By evening the family gathered to celebrate the prospect of the union, with Theodore presenting a painting of the Cat and Racket as a symbol of their shared happiness. Monsieur Guillaume consented to the marriage while emphasizing financial prudence and advising Augustine to consult him on money matters. In the end the lovers found solace in mutual affection, and the household embraced a joyful future together.

A few months after a memorable Sunday, the high altar of Saint-Leu saw two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore radiated happiness, while Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to have Virginie wed first, but the priests attended to the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s sensible match; others envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned such extravagance would bring ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt justified in his cautious approach to settlements. After a splendid ball and supper, Monsieur and Madame Guillaume returned home, and the Lebas family resumed their residence. The artist, elated, carried Augustine to their beautifully adorned apartment.

The passion between Theodore and Augustine made their first year blissful. Theodore showered her with affection; Augustine, lost in love, believed her devotion would always charm him. She remained blissfully unaware of much around her, speaking the language of love and sometimes revealing her naivety. As the year passed and a child was expected, Theodore felt the need to return to work. He reconnected with friends and frequented the Duchesse de Carigliano’s gatherings, seeking the social validation of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine enjoyed society but soon realized her lack of sophistication compared with Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was genuine but shallow, widening a growing disconnect. Theodore, once captivated, began to feel the strain of their differing intellectual worlds. He retreated to his studio, hoping the artistic environment might inspire Augustine, but she struggled to adapt. At gatherings her innocence clashed with the more liberated attitudes of artists, making her increasingly uncomfortable.

Over time Theodore’s affection waned and Augustine felt the weight of his indifference. Though she tried to fulfill her duties as a wife, she sensed a growing distance. Three years into their marriage, Augustine, once vibrant, became consumed by sorrow. The artist’s enthusiasm for the Duchesse de Carigliano intensified her despair as she feared being replaced by a more experienced woman.

Her heartache led Augustine to examine her shortcomings and the barriers between them. She resolved to educate herself, believing that understanding poetry and art would bridge the gap. But her efforts met frustration; she could not cultivate the natural wit and charm Theodore admired, and her earnestness often backfired, leaving her feeling inadequate.

In desperation Augustine sought solace with family, returning to her childhood home. She found sister Virginie and brother-in-law Joseph Lebas managing their drapery business with a steadiness that contrasted sharply with her turmoil. Though their mundane happiness provoked envy, Joseph offered practical, commercially minded advice that only deepened her conflict.

Ultimately Augustine returned home, burdened by uncertainty and longing for the love she once shared with Theodore, like a desperate patient trying every remedy to heal a fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter warmly; her visit was a refreshing break from their monotony. For four years they had drifted, dwelling on past business successes and failures—especially the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume kept checking on his shop, the Cat and Racket, and talking with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume—now on the Army’s clothing consulting board thanks to her son-in-law’s influence—filled their home with extravagant, tasteless decor. Their nostalgia and stagnation struck Augustine as a stark contrast to her own turbulent life, leaving her both proud and appalled at their vacuous existence.

Despite her inner turmoil, Augustine displayed her accomplishments and aired marital grievances, which her parents eagerly absorbed. Madame Guillaume was particularly curious about Augustine’s life with her husband Theodore, a painter. Augustine described his late nights and eccentric habits; her mother disapproved of his lifestyle and profession. Their incredulity grew as Augustine defended him, sparking a heated debate about genius and marital responsibility.

When Augustine mentioned divorce, her parents were shocked into action; Monsieur Guillaume offered support. Overwhelmed by their concern and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine rejected separation and realized she was isolated in her pain—only those who had shared similar trials could truly empathize.

Determined to change course, Augustine sought out the Duchess de Carigliano—not to win Theodore back directly but to learn how to captivate him. Approaching the Duchess’s opulent home, she felt envy and admiration for its elegance. Meeting the Duchess, Augustine was struck by her beauty, charm, and a palpable rivalry beneath the civility.

The Duchess responded with a mix of sympathy and shrewd counsel, stressing the need to present strength and control with men of genius. She urged Augustine to cultivate mystery and not reveal the full extent of her affections. Though uneasy about the idea of manipulation, Augustine began to accept the lessons, seeing that her love for Theodore might require a new approach.

The Duchess offered Augustine a portrait of herself painted by Theodore, believing it could rekindle his interest. Hopeful, Augustine returned home determined to become the image in the painting. When Theodore arrived, she presented the portrait eagerly; his reaction was fury and jealousy, igniting a violent confrontation. The quarrel exposed his passion and insecurity and left Augustine devastated; her dreams were shattered as she faced the true nature of her marriage. The next morning her mother found her in despair amid the remnants of the fight.

Overwhelmed by the pressures of her tumultuous life, Augustine died young. Her story is a poignant reminder of the struggles women face pursuing love and fulfillment, especially when entangled with the complexities of artistic genius; the inscription on her grave reflects that enduring tragedy.
