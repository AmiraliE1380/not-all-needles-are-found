Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, stood a charming old house that allowed historians to reconstruct old Paris. Its dilapidated walls were adorned with hieroglyphic-like patterns formed by the timbers and cracks in the plaster. The triangular roof, warped by the weather, extended over the street to protect the entrance and the dormer window. The upper story was constructed of overlapping planks to avoid overburdening the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning, a young man, wrapped in a cloak, stood under the awning of a shop across from this house, studying it with the enthusiasm of an antiquary. The building, a relic of sixteenth-century civic life, presented various peculiarities. He ignored the first floor's narrow windows, filled with boards to manipulate light, and focused instead on the third floor's modest sash windows, which were poorly crafted and obscured by green-tinted glass. Occasionally, he glanced down at the shop, where amusing details caught his eye.

A large wooden beam, supported by four pillars, bore layers of paint and featured a comical painting of a cat playing rackets, which amused the young man. The cat, depicted with a racket and an oversized ball, was a humorous caricature that had become even more absurd over time. The faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a remnant of a whimsical past when such signs were used to attract customers.

The young man, dressed in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, appeared to have just come from a wedding or ball, as evidenced by his white gloves and disheveled hair. Despite the noise from market gardeners rushing by, the street was enveloped in a rare stillness. His striking features, marked by a mix of passion and calm, drew the attention of the shopkeepers at the “Cat and Racket.” His pale face contrasted with a dazzling white cravat, and his expressive eyes conveyed a range of emotions.

Suddenly, three apprentices appeared at the dormer window, laughing and teasing the young man. They playfully sprinkled him with shaving cream, but he remained indifferent, shaking off the annoyance. Just then, a young girl’s face emerged from the window, her beauty reminiscent of a Raphael Virgin. She appeared innocent and unembarrassed, but upon noticing the young man, she retreated, causing the window to slam shut, leaving him disappointed.

Meanwhile, the shutters of the “Cat and Racket” shop were opened, revealing a manservant who hung a cloth sign reading “Guillaume, successor to Chevrel.” The shop's exterior, protected by iron bars, concealed numerous packages wrapped in brown linen. Despite its old-fashioned appearance, Monsieur Guillaume was known for having the best-stocked store among Parisian cloth merchants, with a reputation for honesty. He was adept at securing profits without the need for flattery or expensive gifts, and his fellow traders often warned against dealing with his notary, known for heavy discounts.

The old merchant stood at the threshold of his shop, observing the Rue Saint-Denis and its surroundings as if awakening from a long slumber. Monsieur Guillaume, dressed in traditional attire, scrutinized a stranger who seemed overly interested in his shop. The merchant's appearance reflected years of experience, with a face marked by patience and shrewdness. He was a staunch traditionalist, often lamenting the loss of old customs and maintaining a strict regime for his three apprentices, who feared his watchful eye.

As the apprentices began their day, Guillaume noticed the stranger's anxious glances toward his shop, suspecting ulterior motives. The eldest apprentice, curious about the stranger, stepped outside and thought he saw Mademoiselle Augustine, Guillaume's daughter, leaving in haste. The young man soon hailed a cab, relieving the apprentices of their worries about their prank on Augustine.

Guillaume reprimanded his apprentices for their idleness, recalling how he would have completed more work in his youth. Despite their wealthy backgrounds, he enforced a rigorous work ethic, contrasting with modern shops where apprentices expected wealth early. The shop was meticulously maintained, reflecting the order and economy valued by Guillaume.

The apprentices, though paying for their board, were not allowed to join the family at dessert, fearing Madame Guillaume's frugality. They attended Mass with the family, and the eldest apprentice, Joseph Lebas, earned a modest salary after years of service. Guillaume viewed him as a potential husband for his elder daughter, Virginie, though Joseph secretly loved the younger daughter, Augustine.

Guillaume's household was governed by strict traditions. His daughters, Virginie and Augustine, were raised under their mother's austere rule. Virginie resembled her mother, known for her piety and strict demeanor, while Augustine, at eighteen, was delicate and charming, though often overshadowed by her mother's expectations. The sisters' lives revolved around domestic duties, with little exposure to the outside world, leading them to find joy in family gatherings and the rare occasions when they entertained guests.

Madame Guillaume's rigid schedule dictated their lives, requiring them to dress early and adhere to a strict routine. Augustine, however, felt a yearning for something beyond their cloistered existence, occasionally lost in thoughts of a more passionate life. Her secret reading of romantic novels may have sparked her longing for adventure.

This longing ignited a deep affection in Joseph for Augustine, while Virginie found herself drawn to him as well, despite her lack of suitors. Their mutual feelings developed quietly amidst the monotony of shop life, where the constant presence of the same faces fostered a sense of connection. Monsieur Guillaume, aware of the changing dynamics, mused about the future of his daughters in a world where suitors might soon be scarce.

From that day, the old merchant, saddened by his eldest daughter's decline, recalled how he had married Mademoiselle Chevrel under similar circumstances to Joseph Lebas and Virginie. Marrying off his daughter would settle a sacred debt by repaying an orphan for a past benefit. Joseph Lebas, now thirty-three, recognized the fifteen-year age gap between him and Augustine and understood Monsieur Guillaume’s rigid principles, knowing the elder daughter would marry first. Thus, the lovesick assistant, warm-hearted yet tall and deep-chested, suffered in silence.

To fully grasp the situation, we must rewind to months before the story's opening. One evening, a young man paused outside the darkened shop of the Cat and Racket, captivated by a scene that could inspire any painter. The shop remained unlit, resembling a cave, with a dining room illuminated by a hanging lamp that cast a warm glow reminiscent of Dutch paintings. The family’s figures, including Augustine and a chubby maid, formed a striking tableau, radiating peace and modesty. The young painter, recently returned from Rome, was eager for genuine nature after being immersed in grand art. His heart, once consumed by Italian passion, longed for a simple, meditative maiden, which he had only seen in paintings. The living picture before him ignited profound admiration for Augustine, who appeared pensive and untouched by food. The lamp's light illuminated her face, making her seem almost angelic. Overwhelmed by emotion, he left, unable to eat or sleep.

The next day, he retreated to his studio, determined to capture the magic of the scene on canvas. He visited the Cat and Racket multiple times, even disguising himself to observe Augustine more closely. For eight months, he immersed himself in love and art, neglecting friends and his usual pursuits. One morning, his friend Girodet broke through his isolation, asking about his Salon submission. The artist eagerly revealed his two masterpieces, prompting an emotional embrace from Girodet, who recognized the depth of his friend's feelings.

“You are in love?” Girodet asked, and the artist nodded. They both understood that the greatest works stem from such passionate sentiments. Girodet cautioned against submitting the pieces to the Salon, believing the public wouldn’t appreciate their depth. Despite this, the works were exhibited, causing a sensation in the art world. The _Interior_ sparked a genre revolution, while the portrait became unforgettable, earning the artist acclaim and offers he refused.

While these events captivated the public, they remained distant from the monastic life in Rue Saint-Denis. During a visit to Madame Guillaume, the notary’s wife mentioned the exhibition, piquing Augustine’s interest. With her cousin’s help, she secured permission to visit the Louvre. Upon seeing the portrait, she trembled, recognizing herself and feeling a mix of terror and excitement. The artist, standing nearby, whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her in shock.

Desperate to escape the crowd, Augustine urged her cousin to leave, but they were swept closer to the celebrated painting. Overwhelmed by emotion, she shed tears at the sight of the artwork. In a moment of connection, she made a silent gesture to the artist, who acknowledged her with a nod. This exchange ignited a sense of guilt and exhilaration within her. The chaos of the Salon, combined with the artist's gaze, intoxicated her, and she felt a surge of emotions she had never experienced before.

As they left, the young painter radiated joy, and Augustine, caught in a whirlwind of feelings, realized she was loved. The artist’s words echoed in her mind, deepening her emotional turmoil. She feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition with her cousin, but her mother’s plans to visit the Salon filled her with dread. Augustine’s heart raced as she contemplated the implications of her newfound feelings.

That night, she reflected on her emotions, feeling a void in her mundane life and a longing for the elegance she had always desired. The thought of being the wife of a genius filled her with hope, despite the stark reality of her upbringing. She was suddenly in love, her heart filled with dreams of a brighter future. The next day, her parents returned from the Salon, their disappointment evident. The painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. However, the news of the paintings’ disappearance revealed to Augustine a sensitivity that women often possess instinctively.

On the morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had captured Augustine's heart, faced challenges in expressing his love due to the constraints of her household. Seated in a counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume, Augustine was closely monitored by her mother, making it difficult for Theodore to approach her. He imagined a rival in one of the assistants and felt trapped by the watchful eyes of the household. His intense passion led him to seek creative ways to communicate with Augustine, ultimately deciding to bribe a maid to facilitate their exchanges. They arranged to meet discreetly during Mass at Saint-Leu.

Meanwhile, the household at the Cat and Racket was consumed by the labor of stock-taking, with Monsieur Guillaume overseeing the process. The family was busy verifying inventory and balancing accounts, oblivious to Augustine's emotional turmoil. Despite her absent-mindedness, no one noticed her longing for Theodore. After weeks of hard work, the stock-taking concluded, allowing the family a rare moment of relaxation. They attended a performance of _Cendrillon_, while the apprentices were given a night out.

The next morning, Monsieur Guillaume resumed his routine, reflecting on his past and the business. He summoned his assistant, Joseph Lebas, to discuss the stock-taking results, which had yielded significant profits. Guillaume proposed making Joseph a partner, recognizing his contributions. Joseph, overwhelmed with gratitude, revealed his love for Augustine, but Guillaume, misunderstanding, thought he meant Virginie. After some confusion, Guillaume agreed to consider Joseph's feelings for Augustine, suggesting he accompany her to Mass.

At breakfast, tension arose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie exchanged glances at Joseph, who was visibly nervous. Madame Guillaume, unaware of the budding romance, chastised Augustine for her inattentiveness during church. After the service, Madame Guillaume ordered Augustine to her room, leading to a heated discussion between her and Monsieur Guillaume about their daughter's future.

Augustine, summoned before her parents, confessed her love for Theodore de Sommervieux, emphasizing his respectable background. Monsieur Guillaume expressed concern about the nature of artists, recalling his experiences with them. Augustine defended Theodore, claiming he was wealthy and of good family, which piqued her father's interest. Just then, Madame Roguin arrived, bringing news of Theodore's recent honors and wealth, advocating for the match. Her persuasive arguments began to sway Monsieur and Madame Guillaume, who were initially resistant to the idea of their daughter marrying above her station.

As the day progressed, the atmosphere shifted from conflict to acceptance. Monsieur Guillaume sought out Joseph to inform him of the developments. By evening, the family gathered to celebrate the newfound union, with Theodore presenting a painting of the Cat and Racket, symbolizing their shared happiness. Monsieur Guillaume, while initially skeptical, agreed to the marriage, emphasizing the importance of financial prudence and advising Augustine to consult him on any financial matters.

In the end, the lovers found solace in their mutual affection, and the household, once fraught with tension, embraced the prospect of a joyful future together.

A few months after a memorable Sunday, the high altar of Saint-Leu hosted two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore radiated happiness, while Virginie, dressed simply, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume attempted to have Virginie married first, but the priests focused on the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie for making a sensible match, while others envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned that such extravagance would lead to ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt justified in his cautious approach to marriage settlements. After a splendid ball and supper, Monsieur and Madame Guillaume returned to their home, while Monsieur and Madame Lebas went back to their old residence. The artist, filled with joy, carried Augustine to their beautifully adorned apartment.

The passion between Theodore and Augustine made their first year together blissful and unclouded. Theodore showered her with affection, and Augustine, lost in love, believed her devotion would always charm him. She remained blissfully unaware of the world around her, expressing herself in the language of love, though occasionally revealing her naivety. As the year progressed, Theodore felt the need to return to his work, especially with a child on the way. He reconnected with friends and frequented the Duchesse de Carigliano's gatherings, seeking the social validation that came with being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine enjoyed the social scene but soon realized her lack of sophistication compared to Theodore's artistic world. Her admiration for his work was genuine but lacked depth, leading to a growing disconnect. Theodore, once captivated by her, began to feel the strain of their differing intellectual realms. He sought solace in his studio, hoping that the artistic environment would inspire Augustine, but she struggled to adapt to the artistic lifestyle. Her discomfort became apparent during social gatherings, where her innocence clashed with the more liberated attitudes of artists.

As time passed, Theodore's affection waned, and Augustine felt the weight of his indifference. Despite her efforts to fulfill her duties as a wife, she sensed a growing distance between them. Three years into their marriage, Augustine, once vibrant, became consumed by sorrow. She tried to adhere to her responsibilities, hoping to rekindle Theodore's love, but her efforts were in vain. The artist's enthusiasm for the Duchesse de Carigliano only deepened her despair, as she faced the reality of being replaced by a more experienced woman.

Augustine's heartache led her to reflect on her shortcomings and the barriers that hindered their connection. She resolved to educate herself, believing that understanding poetry and art would bridge the gap between them. However, her attempts to change were met with frustration; she could not cultivate the natural wit and charm that Theodore admired. Her earnestness often backfired, and she felt increasingly inadequate in his presence.

In a moment of desperation, Augustine sought solace in her family, returning to the home of her childhood. She found her sister Virginie and brother-in-law Joseph Lebas managing their drapery business with a sense of stability that contrasted sharply with her own turmoil. Despite their mundane happiness, Augustine felt a pang of envy as she shared her struggles. Joseph offered practical advice, analyzing her situation with a commercial mindset, but his suggestions only left her feeling more conflicted.

Ultimately, Augustine returned home, burdened by uncertainty and longing for the love she once shared with Theodore. She felt like a desperate patient trying every remedy in hopes of healing her fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter with heartfelt warmth, as her visit brought a refreshing change to their monotonous lives. For four years, they had drifted aimlessly, reminiscing about past successes and failures in business, particularly the infamous bankruptcy of Lecocq. Monsieur Guillaume would often check on his shop, the Cat and Racket, and engage with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume, now a member of the Army's clothing consulting board due to her son-in-law's influence, filled their home with extravagant yet tasteless decor. Their lives, filled with nostalgia and stagnation, struck Augustine as a stark contrast to her own tumultuous experiences, leading her to feel a mix of pride and horror at their vacuous existence.

Despite her internal struggles, Augustine showcased her newfound accomplishments and shared her marital grievances, which her parents eagerly absorbed. Madame Guillaume was particularly intrigued by the details of Augustine's life with her husband, Theodore, a painter. The conversation took a turn when Augustine revealed Theodore's late nights and unconventional habits, prompting her mother to express disapproval of his lifestyle and profession. The old couple's incredulity grew as Augustine defended her husband, leading to a heated discussion about the nature of genius and the responsibilities of marriage.

As Augustine confided her serious grievances, the mention of divorce shocked her parents into action, with Monsieur Guillaume eager to support her. However, Augustine, overwhelmed by their concern, ultimately rejected the idea of separation, realizing that her struggles were difficult to convey to her parents. She felt isolated in her pain, understanding that only those who shared her experiences could truly empathize.

In her despair, Augustine resolved to seek the Duchess de Carigliano, not to reclaim her husband's heart but to learn the art of captivating him. With trepidation, she approached the Duchess's opulent home, feeling both envy and admiration for the elegance that surrounded her. Upon meeting the Duchess, Augustine was struck by the woman's beauty and charm, but also sensed the rivalry that lay between them.

Augustine's plea for guidance was met with a mix of sympathy and shrewd advice from the Duchess, who emphasized the importance of maintaining a facade of strength and control in relationships with men of genius. The Duchess shared her insights on the dynamics of power in marriage, suggesting that women should cultivate an air of mystery and not reveal the full extent of their affections. Augustine, initially disheartened by the notion of manipulation, began to absorb the Duchess's teachings, realizing that her love for Theodore might require a different approach.

The Duchess offered Augustine a portrait of herself painted by Theodore, believing it would serve as a powerful tool to rekindle her husband's interest. Augustine, filled with hope, returned home, determined to transform herself into the image of the portrait. When Theodore arrived, she eagerly presented the painting, but his reaction was one of fury and jealousy, leading to a tumultuous confrontation.

The scene escalated, revealing the depths of Theodore's passion and insecurity, culminating in a violent outburst. The aftermath left Augustine devastated, her dreams shattered as she faced the reality of her marriage. The following morning, her mother found her in a state of despair, surrounded by the remnants of their confrontation.

Augustine's tragic fate was sealed, as she succumbed to the pressures of her tumultuous life, ultimately passing away at a young age. Her story serves as a poignant reminder of the struggles faced by women in the pursuit of love and fulfillment, particularly when entangled with the complexities of artistic genius. The inscription on her grave reflects the tragedy of her life, leaving those who knew her to ponder the challenges of balancing passion and stability in relationships.
