**AT THE SIGN OF THE CAT AND RACKET**

**By Honore De Balzac**  
**Translated by Clara Bell**

**DEDICATION**  
To Mademoiselle Marie de Montheau

---

Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, stood a charming old house that allowed historians to reconstruct old Paris. Its dilapidated walls were adorned with hieroglyphics formed by the timbers and cracks in the plaster. The structure, crowned by a triangular roof, projected over the street to protect the entrance from rain and to shelter a loft's dormer window. The upper story was built of planks, overlapping like slates to avoid overburdening the fragile house.

On a rainy March morning, a young man, wrapped in a cloak, stood under the awning of a shop across from this old house, studying it with the enthusiasm of an antiquary. The building, a relic of sixteenth-century civic life, presented various peculiarities. The first floor had four tall, narrow windows filled with boards to create a dubious light for sales. The young man seemed scornful of this part, his gaze drawn instead to the third floor, where modest sash frames held small, green-tinted glass panes. Occasionally, he glanced down at the shop, where amusing details caught his eye.

A massive wooden beam, resting on four pillars, appeared to sag under the weight of the house, coated in layers of paint. In the center of this beam was a comical painting of a cat playing rackets, which amused the young man. The cat, depicted with a racket as large as itself, aimed at a ball returned by a man in an embroidered coat. Time had rendered the painting even more grotesque, with the cat's tail resembling a spectator due to its wear. The faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” its letters worn by the elements.

These whimsical signs, once common among Paris merchants, were remnants of a time when they used living animals to attract customers. The young man, however, was not there to admire the cat. His cloak, draped in an antique style, revealed smart shoes and white silk stockings, now splattered with mud. He held a pair of white gloves, suggesting he had just come from a wedding or ball, and his hair, styled in the fashion of the day, flowed over his shoulders.

Despite the noise from late market gardeners, the street was eerily quiet, a stillness known only to those who have wandered through deserted Paris. The young man, with his striking features and expressive face, drew the curiosity of the shopkeepers. His pale complexion contrasted with a dazzling white cravat, and his black eyes sparkled with intensity. His forehead, marked by furrows of annoyance, hinted at a passionate nature.

Suddenly, the dormer window of the loft swung open, revealing three laughing faces framed within. The apprentices, eager for mischief, sprinkled the young man with a fine white powder, remnants of their recent shaves. He shook off the powder with indifference, his contempt evident as he glanced up at the empty window frame.

At that moment, a slender white hand opened one of the clumsy windows on the third floor. A young girl appeared, her face fresh and innocent, framed by a frill of muslin. Wrapped in brown fabric, she revealed glimpses of her neck and shoulders. Her calm expression and blue eyes mirrored the grace of Raphael's Virgins. As she looked down, her gaze met that of her admirer, causing her to start back in embarrassment. The window's pulley gave way, and the sash fell, obscuring her from view, leaving the young man feeling as if a radiant star had been hidden.

Meanwhile, the heavy shutters of the “Cat and Racket” shop were removed, and an elderly servant opened the door, hanging a cloth square embroidered with “Guillaume, successor to Chevrel.” Passersby would find it hard to guess the nature of Monsieur Guillaume's trade. Behind iron bars, packages wrapped in brown linen were barely visible. Despite the Gothic front's primitive appearance, Monsieur Guillaume was known for having the best-stocked store in Paris, with a reputation for commercial honesty.

While other merchants courted patrons and made costly gifts, Guillaume found clever ways to maximize profits without such efforts. He was known for his ability to fulfill government contracts, even when others could not, and his dealings with a notary often resulted in heavy discounts, leading to the saying among traders: “Heaven preserve you from Monsieur Guillaume’s notary!”

The old merchant, Monsieur Guillaume, stood at the threshold of his shop, surveying the Rue Saint-Denis and its surroundings as if awakening from a long slumber. He noted that nothing had changed, but soon noticed a stranger watching him with keen interest. Dressed in loose black velvet breeches, pepper-and-salt stockings, and square-toed shoes with silver buckles, Guillaume's appearance reflected his old-fashioned values. His coat, worn and faded, and his meticulously combed gray hair added to his air of patience and commercial shrewdness, traits essential for a successful merchant.

Guillaume was a staunch traditionalist, often lamenting the loss of the Provost of Merchants and referring to the Tribunal of Commerce as the "Sentence of the Consuls." He stood waiting for his three assistants, ready to reprimand them for tardiness. However, his attention was drawn to the stranger, who seemed overly interested in the shop's interior, particularly the cashier’s corner where ledgers were kept. Guillaume suspected the young man had ulterior motives, perhaps eyeing the shop's profits.

The eldest apprentice, noticing the stranger's gaze, stepped outside and thought he saw Mademoiselle Augustine, Guillaume's daughter, leaving in haste. This revelation momentarily distracted Guillaume, but the stranger soon hailed a cab and departed, easing the tension for the apprentices.

“Why are you standing there with your arms folded?” Guillaume scolded his assistants, recalling how he would have been more productive in his youth. The second assistant pointed out that daylight came earlier back then, prompting a smile from Guillaume. Despite the wealth of two apprentices’ families, he maintained strict discipline, making them work diligently, unlike the modern clerks who expected easy riches.

The shop was a model of order and cleanliness, reflecting Guillaume's values. The apprentices, though they paid for their board, were not allowed to join the family at dessert, and they feared Madame Guillaume's frugality. Each Sunday, they accompanied the family to Mass, with the daughters walking ahead under their mother’s watchful eye.

Guillaume's elder daughter, Virginie, resembled her mother, Madame Guillaume, who was known for her piety and strict demeanor. Virginie, now twenty-eight, was patient and gentle, while her younger sister, Augustine, at eighteen, was delicate and charming, lacking any resemblance to her parents. Augustine's innocence and quiet demeanor hinted at a longing for a life beyond her constrained existence.

The sisters, always modestly dressed, found joy in cleanliness and hard work, fulfilling their mother’s expectations. Their education was limited to practical skills, and they rarely ventured beyond their home, which their mother regarded as their entire world. Family gatherings provided their only glimpses of joy.

When preparing for company, the sisters worked tirelessly, only to hear their mother declare, “Children, we have done nothing today,” after the event. On rare occasions, Madame Guillaume allowed dancing, which was considered a great privilege. Once a year, Monsieur Guillaume hosted an extravagant party, yet the daughters often felt embarrassed by their shabby dresses and strict rules that limited their interactions with guests.

Their daily lives were regimented, with Madame Guillaume enforcing early rising and strict schedules. Augustine, however, occasionally felt the emptiness of this routine, her blue eyes reflecting a yearning for something more. She secretly read romantic novels, which may have sparked her desire for a different life.

Augustine's gentle demeanor ignited a deep admiration in Joseph Lebas, the chief assistant, who harbored feelings for her. Conversely, Virginie found herself drawn to the senior apprentice, who, despite his humble beginnings, had captured her attention. These unspoken affections blossomed in the confines of the shop, where the monotony of daily life fostered a longing for connection.

Monsieur Guillaume, aware of the slow pace of potential suitors, mused to himself about the future of his daughters, reflecting on the changing times and the challenges they faced in finding love.

From that day, the old merchant, saddened by his eldest daughter’s decline, recalled how he had married Mademoiselle Chevrel under similar circumstances to Joseph Lebas and Virginie. Marrying off his daughter would not only be a good business move but also a way to repay a debt to an orphan, just as he had benefited from his predecessor. Joseph Lebas, now thirty-three, recognized the fifteen-year age gap between him and Augustine as an obstacle. He understood Monsieur Guillaume’s rigid principles, knowing that the younger daughter would never marry before the elder. Thus, the lovesick assistant, whose heart was warm and legs long, suffered in silence.

To fully grasp the situation, we must rewind several months before the story begins. One evening, a young man paused outside the darkened shop of the Cat and Racket, captivated by a scene that could inspire any painter. The shop remained unlit, resembling a cave, with a dining room illuminated by a hanging lamp that cast a warm glow reminiscent of Dutch paintings. The family gathered around the table, their faces radiating peace and modesty, creating a tableau that seemed impossible to capture. The young painter, who had won a prestigious award seven years prior and had just returned from Rome, felt a deep yearning for genuine beauty after being surrounded by grand art. His heart, once consumed by Italian passion, now craved the simplicity of a modest maiden, which he had only seen in paintings.

Overwhelmed by the enchanting scene, he became enamored with Augustine, who appeared pensive and detached from the meal. The light illuminated her face, making her seem almost angelic. This unexpected emotion flooded his heart, and after a moment, he reluctantly left, unable to eat or sleep.

The next day, he rushed to his studio, determined to capture the magic of the moment on canvas. For eight months, he devoted himself to his art and his love, neglecting friends and the outside world. One morning, his friend Girodet broke through his isolation, asking about his plans for the Salon. The artist eagerly revealed his work, and after studying the pieces, Girodet embraced him, recognizing the depth of his feelings.

“You’re in love,” Girodet stated, and the artist nodded in agreement. They both understood that the greatest masterpieces often stem from such passionate emotions. Girodet cautioned against submitting the works to the Salon, believing the public wouldn’t appreciate their depth. Despite this, the paintings were exhibited, causing a sensation in the art world. The _Interior_ sparked a genre revolution, while the portrait became unforgettable, earning the artist acclaim and offers he refused.

While these events captivated the public, they remained distant from the quiet life in Rue Saint-Denis. During a visit to Madame Guillaume, the notary’s wife mentioned the exhibition in front of Augustine, igniting her curiosity. With her cousin’s help, Augustine secured permission to visit the Louvre. As she navigated the crowd, she trembled upon recognizing herself in the painting. Terrified, she searched for Madame Roguin, only to encounter the artist’s passionate gaze.

“You see how love has inspired me,” he whispered, leaving her stunned. Summoning courage, she urged her cousin to leave the crowded gallery, but fate pushed them closer to the celebrated painting. Augustine was overwhelmed with emotion, tears streaming down her face as she beheld the artwork. In a moment of silent communication, she and the artist exchanged glances, igniting a connection that felt both thrilling and terrifying.

As they left the gallery, the young painter radiated joy, while Augustine experienced a whirlwind of emotions. She felt a mix of rapture and fear, realizing she had brought happiness to someone renowned for his talent. The thought that she was loved filled her with a sense of wonder. When she returned home, she feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition, but her mother’s comments about the fame surrounding their home made her anxious.

Monsieur Guillaume dismissed the artist’s work, lamenting the loss of his wife’s shawl and the removal of the paintings. Joseph Lebas attempted to defend the art, but the conversation reflected a lack of appreciation for creativity. Augustine, however, was lost in her thoughts, reflecting on the day’s events like a dream. She felt a void in her mundane life and a newfound treasure in her heart. The idea of being the wife of a genius filled her with hope, contrasting sharply with her dreary surroundings.

In her youthful naivety, she believed she could bring happiness to the artist, unaware of the complexities that often accompany such unions. The next day, her parents returned from the Salon, their disappointment evident. The absence of the paintings and the lost shawl only added to their woes. Yet, for Augustine, the knowledge that she had inspired the artist filled her with a delicate sense of pride and longing, hinting at the profound changes love could bring to her life.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
On the morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux awaited his beloved Augustine, unaware of the obstacles posed by her guardians, Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume. The constraints of her household intensified Theodore's passion, as he struggled to find ways to connect with her amidst the watchful eyes of her family. He imagined rivals among the assistants and felt trapped by the stern presence of Madame Guillaume and Monsieur Guillaume, who were preoccupied with their business.

Desperate to communicate, Theodore resorted to bribing a maid, leading to a series of secret exchanges over the following weeks. They arranged to meet during Mass at Saint-Leu, while Theodore sought to win over Augustine's relatives, hoping to gain their support for his love. Despite her apparent distraction, Augustine's feelings remained unnoticed by her family, who were consumed by the demands of their drapery business, particularly during the taxing stock-taking period.

After weeks of labor, the stock-taking concluded, allowing the family a rare moment of relaxation. Monsieur Guillaume, ever diligent, returned to work early the next day, reflecting on his past and the legacy of his mentor, Master Chevrel. He summoned his assistant, Joseph Lebas, to discuss the business's success and proposed making him a partner, which brought Joseph to tears of gratitude. However, the conversation took a turn when Guillaume revealed he was considering a match for Joseph with his daughter Virginie, unaware of Joseph's feelings for Augustine.

As the day progressed, tensions rose within the household. Augustine's secret love for Theodore was discovered by her mother during Mass, leading to a confrontation that left Augustine distressed. Madame Guillaume's anger and disapproval of Theodore's profession as a painter only heightened the conflict. Augustine bravely confessed her love for Theodore to her parents, asserting that marrying another man would make her miserable.

Monsieur Guillaume, recalling his own experiences with artists, expressed his concerns about Theodore's suitability. Just as the situation seemed dire, Madame Roguin arrived, advocating for Theodore and revealing his noble background and financial stability. Her persuasive arguments began to sway Monsieur and Madame Guillaume, who were initially resistant to the idea of their daughter marrying above her station.

Despite their initial reluctance, the prospect of a union with Theodore, now seen as a potential advantage for the family, began to take shape. Monsieur Guillaume sought out Joseph to inform him of the developments, while the family gathered for dinner, where Theodore presented a painting of their shop, symbolizing the newfound harmony.

In a heartfelt moment, Monsieur Guillaume agreed to the marriage, emphasizing the importance of financial prudence and advising Augustine to consult him on any financial matters. The evening concluded with a sense of peace, as the lovers found solace in each other's company, ready to face the future together.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A few months after a memorable Sunday, the high altar of Saint-Leu hosted two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore radiated happiness, dressed elegantly, while Virginie, in simple attire, followed her sister like a shadow. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to marry Virginie first, but the priests focused on the more glamorous bride. Neighbors praised Virginie for making a sensible match, while others envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned that such extravagance could lead to ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt justified in his cautious approach to marriage settlements.

After a splendid ball and a hearty supper, Monsieur and Madame Guillaume returned to their home in Rue du Colombier, while Monsieur and Madame Lebas went back to their old home in Rue Saint-Denis. The artist, intoxicated with joy, carried Augustine to their apartment, adorned with artistic touches.

The passion between Theodore and Augustine made their first year together blissful and unclouded. Theodore showered each day with joy, alternating between passionate moments and serene hours of connection. Augustine, too happy to reflect, immersed herself in the sanctity of married love, believing her devotion would always charm her husband. Her beauty, enhanced by love, filled her with pride, and she felt confident in her ability to captivate Theodore.

Despite her happiness, Augustine remained the simple girl from Rue Saint-Denis, unaware of the social graces required in her new life. Her expressions of love revealed a certain naivety, and when her ideas clashed with Theodore’s, he laughed off her misunderstandings, much like one would with a foreigner.

As the year passed, Theodore felt the need to return to his work and old habits, especially with Augustine expecting their first child. He reconnected with friends and sought diversion in fashionable circles, particularly at the Duchesse de Carigliano's gatherings. Once Augustine recovered and their son required less attention, Theodore craved the social validation that came from being seen with a beautiful wife.

For Augustine, attending social events with Theodore was a new source of joy, but it marked the beginning of the end of their marital bliss. She unintentionally wounded his vanity by revealing her ignorance and lack of sophistication. Theodore, once captivated by love, began to feel the weight of their differences. He realized that Augustine could not appreciate the poetry and imagination that fueled his creativity, leaving him feeling stifled.

The artist sought solace in his studio, hoping that the company of fellow artists would inspire Augustine to develop her intellect. However, she felt out of place among them, and her discomfort became apparent. Her mother’s excessive propriety influenced Augustine, who, despite her previous mockery, began to mirror her mother’s primness. This behavior led to light-hearted jests among Theodore’s friends, which he found amusing but ultimately alienating.

As time passed, a coldness settled between them. Theodore viewed Augustine as incapable of understanding the complexities of his artistic life, leading him to hide aspects of himself from her. Augustine, sensing this emotional distance, felt increasingly isolated and mourned the loss of the intimacy they once shared. Though Theodore treated her with respect, she noticed that he reserved his wit and charm for the outside world, deepening her sense of inadequacy.

Three years into their marriage, Augustine, who once enjoyed a life of luxury and admiration, found herself engulfed in sorrow. She lost her vibrancy and began to reflect on her situation, realizing that her attempts to fulfill her duties as a wife were in vain. Despite her efforts to support Theodore financially and manage their household, she felt the strain of his artistic lifestyle, which clashed with her own values.

The turning point came when Augustine learned of Theodore’s growing admiration for the Duchesse de Carigliano, a flirtatious woman of the court. At just twenty-one, Augustine felt abandoned for a woman much older than herself. Surrounded by a world of celebration that felt hollow, she struggled to comprehend her own worth and the envy she inspired in others. Her demeanor shifted, marked by melancholy and resignation, as she grappled with the realization that her upbringing had created barriers to a deeper connection with Theodore.

Despite her despair, Augustine held onto hope. Determined to educate herself and bridge the gap between them, she resolved to understand the poetry that captivated her husband. With unwavering will, she attempted to change her character and expand her knowledge. However, her efforts yielded only modest improvements, as natural wit and conversational grace eluded her.

Theodore’s perception of her became fixed, and he often mocked her in front of others, further diminishing her confidence. Augustine’s attempts to please him only seemed to irritate him, as he equated her fidelity with insensitivity. She struggled to abandon her reason and adapt to his whims, but her sacrifices went unrecognized, and the moment for their souls to connect slipped away.

Eventually, Augustine withdrew into solitude, seeking solace in her family. One morning, she returned to her childhood home, reflecting on the changes in her life. The familiar facade of her old home stirred memories, but the atmosphere had shifted. Her sister, now managing the family business, received her with a coolness that highlighted the distance that had grown between them.

During her visit, Augustine observed the stability and contentment of her sister’s marriage, which contrasted sharply with her own turmoil. Virginie and Joseph Lebas had built a life based on mutual respect and practicality, devoid of the emotional highs and lows that characterized Augustine’s relationship with Theodore. When Augustine confided her struggles, she was met with conventional advice that failed to address her deeper emotional needs.

Joseph Lebas analyzed her situation with a pragmatic lens, suggesting legal options that would not satisfy Augustine’s lingering love for Theodore. After their conversation, she returned home feeling more conflicted than before, still yearning for the connection she once had with her husband. In her desperation, she contemplated seeking guidance from her parents, hoping to find a remedy for her heartache.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The elderly couple welcomed their daughter with heartfelt enthusiasm, which deeply moved her. Her visit brought a refreshing change to their monotonous lives, as they had spent the last four years reminiscing about past successes and failures in business. Monsieur Guillaume, now a member of the Army's clothing consulting board thanks to his son-in-law, maintained a routine of visiting shops and keeping open house three times a week. Madame Guillaume, feeling the weight of her husband's status, filled their home with extravagant yet tasteless decorations, creating an atmosphere of stale opulence.

Augustine observed her parents' stagnant existence, contrasting it with her own tumultuous life, which she felt was worth a thousand such mundane lives. Despite her internal struggles, she masked her feelings and shared her marital grievances, knowing that older generations often enjoy such confidences. Madame Guillaume was particularly curious about Augustine's life, expressing shock at her husband’s late nights and unconventional lifestyle.

As Augustine defended Theodore, her mother’s disapproval grew. Madame Guillaume criticized Theodore's habits, including his late nights and peculiar dietary choices, suggesting he was immoral and unfit for marriage. Augustine attempted to explain the nature of artistic genius, but her parents remained unconvinced, leading to discussions of divorce, which shocked them both. However, Augustine staunchly refused to consider separation, feeling that her struggles were not understood by her parents.

Returning home, Augustine felt isolated in her sorrow, realizing that her intellectual and emotional struggles were beyond her parents' comprehension. She became disillusioned with the world, feeling her life was a failure. In a moment of desperation, she resolved to seek out the Duchesse de Carigliano, not to reclaim her husband’s heart but to learn how to win his affection back.

Timid yet determined, Augustine visited the Duchess's opulent home, feeling both envy and despair at the elegance surrounding her. When she finally met the Duchess, she was struck by the woman’s beauty and charm, but also felt the weight of her own inadequacies. Augustine confessed her plight, asking the Duchess for guidance on how to regain Theodore’s love. The Duchess, initially amused by Augustine's sincerity, offered her advice on navigating the complexities of love and marriage.

The Duchess explained that women must often disguise their true feelings to maintain power in relationships, advising Augustine to be strategic in her approach to Theodore. She emphasized that while passion is important, it can lead to vulnerability if not managed carefully. The conversation left Augustine feeling conflicted, as she grappled with the idea of manipulating her husband’s affections.

After their meeting, Augustine returned home, filled with a mix of hope and anxiety. She prepared to confront Theodore, using the portrait of herself that the Duchess had promised to return. When Theodore arrived, Augustine attempted to rekindle their connection by presenting the portrait, but his reaction was explosive. He accused her of seeking revenge against the Duchess and expressed his fury at her perceived betrayal.

The confrontation escalated, leading to a violent outburst from Theodore, who destroyed the portrait in a fit of rage. Augustine, devastated by his actions, found herself in a state of despair, realizing the depth of their marital discord. The next morning, her mother discovered her in a state of distress, surrounded by the remnants of the portrait and the emotional fallout from the previous night.

Augustine's suffering culminated in her untimely death at a young age, leaving behind a poignant reminder of the struggles faced by those caught in the tumult of love and ambition. Her story serves as a reflection on the challenges of navigating relationships with individuals of genius, highlighting the often-unrecognized sacrifices made by those who love them.

In the end, the inscription on her grave speaks to the tragedy of her life, suggesting that perhaps a stronger woman could have endured the trials of loving a man of genius, while also pondering the fragility of those who bloom in the shadows of greatness.
