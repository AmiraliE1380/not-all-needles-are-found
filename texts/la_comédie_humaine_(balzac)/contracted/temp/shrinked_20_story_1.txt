Halfway down Rue Saint-Denis, near Rue du Petit-Lion, a weathered house helped historians reconstruct old Paris. Its dilapidated walls revealed timber frames and cracked plaster; a warped triangular roof overhung the street, sheltering the dormer and entrance, while overlapping upper planks lightened the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man, cloaked like an antiquary, stood opposite studying its peculiarities. He ignored the narrow boarded first-floor windows, noted the third floor’s sash windows clouded with green glass, and glanced at the shop below, where small comic details amused him. A large timber on four pillars bore layers of paint and a whimsical cat with an oversized racket. A faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel.”

Dressed in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, he looked fresh from a wedding or ball; white gloves and dishevelled hair hinted at recent revelry. Market gardeners hurried by; the street was unusually still, his pale face and dazzling cravat drawing attention. Three apprentices at the dormer laughed, sprinkling him with shaving cream he brushed off indifferently. A young girl’s face, recalling a Raphael Virgin, flashed and fled as a shutter slammed. The “Cat and Racket” shutters opened as a manservant hung the cloth sign. Behind iron bars, rows of brown-linen-wrapped packages filled the shop. Though old-fashioned, Monsieur Guillaume was reputed Paris’s best-stocked cloth merchant—honest and profitable—while rivals grumbled about the notary’s heavy discounts.

Guillaume kept a strict routine and lamented changes. An apprentice stopped a prank when Augustine left. Guillaume condemned idleness, insisted on hard work—unlike newer shops; apprentices were from comfortable homes; Madame denied dessert. Their routine included Mass; Joseph earned wages. Guillaume thought him suited to Virginie, though Joseph loved Augustine. Devout Virginie stifled Augustine, eighteen, who read romances. As feelings deepened, Guillaume fretted about his daughters' prospects.

From that day the old merchant, grieving his eldest daughter’s decline, recalled marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like Joseph Lebas and Virginie’s: marrying his daughter would repay a sacred debt to an orphan benefactor. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, aware of the fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder sister marry first, suffered in silence. Warm-hearted but reserved, he kept his love hidden.

Months earlier a young painter had paused outside the dark Cat and Racket, transfixed by a scene any artist would envy. The cave-like shop opened on a dining room lit by a hanging lamp that recalled Dutch interiors. The family—Augustine among them, with a chubby maid—formed a modest, peaceful tableau. Fresh from Rome and tired of grand art, the painter craved real life. Augustine, pensive and hardly eating, seemed almost angelic in the lamp’s glow. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

Determined to capture her, he returned to the Cat and Racket, even in disguise, and for eight months lived in love and study, neglecting friends and routine. One morning his friend Girodet broke his seclusion to ask about the Salon submission. The artist unveiled two masterpieces; Girodet, sensing deep feeling, embraced him and declared that love had made the work great. Despite warnings that the public might not understand them, the paintings were shown and caused a sensation. The Interior launched a new genre; the unforgettable portrait won acclaim and offers the artist refused.

These successes contrasted with Augustine’s monastic life in Rue Saint-Denis until Madame Guillaume mentioned the exhibition; with her cousin’s help Augustine obtained permission to see the Louvre. Trembling before the portrait she recognized herself, torn between terror and excitement. The artist, nearby, whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned. Fleeing the crowd she tried to leave but was drawn back, wept, and exchanged a quiet nod with him. That brief contact ignited guilt and exhilaration; she feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition, dreading her mother’s planned visit. The next day her parents returned disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. Yet the paintings’ disappearance had awakened in Augustine a woman’s instinctive sensitivity.

After the ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had won Augustine's heart, hesitated to declare his love because her household constrained them. Augustine sat under her mother's watch in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume. Fearing rivals among the assistants, Theodore bribed a maid to arrange discreet meetings, and they agreed to meet during Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family focused on stock-taking under Monsieur Guillaume, indifferent to Augustine’s longing; after work they relaxed, watching Cendrillon and the apprentices carouse. The next morning Guillaume resumed business, summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to review the profits, and offered him a partnership. Overwhelmed, Joseph blurted he loved Augustine; Guillaume at first misheard “Virginie.” Corrected, he promised to consider Joseph’s feelings and suggested Joseph accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast Madame scolded Augustine for inattentiveness in church and sent her away; Augustine then confessed her love for Theodore and insisted on his respectability. Monsieur, wary of artists, protested until Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore’s honors and fortune and advocated the match. Her report swayed the parents. By evening Guillaume informed Joseph; the family celebrated as Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket. Though Monsieur remained cautious about money, he consented, and the household embraced a joyful future.

A few months after that memorable Sunday, Saint-Leu’s high altar hosted two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore were radiant while Virginie, simply dressed, followed. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to marry Virginie first, but the priests favored the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s sensible match and envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned such extravagance would bring ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt vindicated by his cautious settlements. After a splendid ball the Guillaumes returned home and the Lebas family resumed their old residence. The ecstatic artist carried Augustine to their richly adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful: Theodore lavished affection and Augustine, lost in love, believed she would always charm him. Naïve, she remained largely unaware of the wider world. As pregnancy neared, Theodore felt the pull of work, reconnected with friends, and frequented the Duchesse de Carigliano’s salons, enjoying the prestige of being seen with a handsome, accomplished wife.

Augustine liked society but soon felt out of place among Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was sincere but superficial, and an intellectual disconnect grew. Theodore hoped studio life would engage her, but she could not adapt; her innocence clashed with the freer manners of artists and made her uncomfortable.

Gradually his warmth cooled, and three years in Augustine sank into sorrow as his intimacy with the Duchesse deepened. Attempts to learn poetry and art only underscored her inadequacy. She briefly sought solace at her childhood home, where Virginie and Joseph Lebas ran their drapery with steady, ordinary happiness, but Joseph’s practical consolations helped little. Augustine returned to her husband’s house, burdened and longing, trying every remedy to heal their fractured marriage.

The elderly Guillaumes welcomed their daughter Augustine, whose visit broke years of reminiscence about the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur inspected his shop, the Cat and Racket, and chatted with competitors, while Madame, appointed to the Army’s clothing board, filled their home with décor. Their stagnation contrasted with Augustine’s turbulent life.

Augustine, despite turmoil, spoke of achievements and marital grievances. Madame queried her husband Theodore, a painter; hearing of his late nights and eccentric habits she disapproved. Augustine defended him, turning family incredulity into a debate over genius versus marital duty. When divorce was mentioned the parents were shocked; Monsieur offered support. Overwhelmed and unable to convey her suffering, Augustine recoiled from separation and felt isolated.

Seeking, she visited Duchess de Carigliano—not to force Theodore back but to learn to captivate him. At the Duchess’s house she felt envy; the Duchess advised strength and reserve and gave Augustine a portrait by Theodore to rekindle him. Embodying that image provoked Theodore’s fury; a violent quarrel shattered her hopes. The next morning her mother found Augustine despairing amid the wreckage; she died young. Her death became a poignant reminder of the struggle women face pursuing love and fulfillment beside artistic genius; her grave’s inscription records this sorrow.
