The Marquise de Listomere personifies the Restoration spirit, blending religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married seven years to an unremarkable Marquis—whose safe, fault-prone, uncharismatic conduct as a silent deputy has earned him the nickname “dull weather”—she maintains a virtuous façade to support his political prospects until he reaches the peerage.

Reserved and principled, she commands respect and sometimes disdain from would-be flirts, replying with contempt yet speaking gracefully with suitable men. The narrator admires her: not plain, her animated eyes reveal depth and an unforced charm.

This emerges in a recent meeting with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever, modestly vivacious young man. At a ball they converse from the opera to women’s duties; he leaves abruptly. Next morning Rastignac, distracted, imagines romance while writing letters until Horace mentions an expedition to Morea. The Marquise reads Rastignac’s affectionate letter, tempted to discard it but ultimately wearied by its impertinence and the emotional tension it implies.

Madame de Listomere forbade her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac after finding his insolence intolerable and even burned a letter he’d sent. Caroline carried the gossip: “Madame received a fine love‑letter.” That evening the marquise waited at the Marquis de Beauseant’s party for Rastignac until two in the morning, then realized he would not come—Stendhal called her hardening “crystallization.”

Four days later Rastignac scolded his valet Joseph for misplacing letters; one had been for Madame de Listomere. He laughed when he learned the lost note was not for Madame de Nucingen, aware his affections had shifted. His errors began with a foolish attempt to amuse the marquise by discussing the blunder and worsened when he stayed away for several days, allowing her displeasure to harden.

When he finally called, the porter said she was out but the marquis admitted him. Social awkwardness mounted: the marquise blushed, then recovered with dignity. Eugène tried to smooth things; she promised to forget the incident yet remained reserved. Mention of the letter provoked feigned offense and suppressed amusement; a reference to Madame de Nucingen altered her tone—she rang for wood to signal his departure and coldly accused him. Embarrassed, he left. Days later she accepted his explanation about the recipient and withdrew from society—her husband blamed a stomach ailment, her physician a nervous condition. The episode reveals social misunderstandings, emotional pride, and rigid romantic roles.
