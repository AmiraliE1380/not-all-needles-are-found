**Study of a Woman**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*

**Dedication**  
To the Marquis Jean-Charles di Negro.

---

The Marquise de Listomere embodies the spirit of the Restoration, balancing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married for seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she upholds a virtuous facade, possibly to support her husband's political ambitions. The marquis, known for his lack of charisma, earns the nickname "dull weather," ensuring a stable home life that aligns with the Marquise's principles.

Despite her reserved nature, the Marquise commands respect and skillfully avoids the advances of flirtatious men, maintaining her dignity and reputation. She engages in conversations with agreeable men without attracting scandal, a challenging feat in her social circle. Her beauty is understated; her striking features come alive when animated, revealing her depth of character.

Recently, she danced with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man who embodies charm and complexity. Their conversation, which began with light topics and shifted to women's roles, left a lasting impression on both. Rastignac, ambitious yet playful, found himself captivated by the Marquise but did not pursue her further that evening.

The next morning, Rastignac awoke contemplatively, reflecting on the Marquise while writing letters. Enjoying the comfort of his surroundings, he was lost in thoughts of her. His friend Horace arrived, and they discussed various topics, including a potential expedition to Morea, but the conversation was ultimately unremarkable.

Meanwhile, the Marquise received a letter from Rastignac while preparing for the day. The letter began with romantic declarations, prompting her to read on despite her initial impulse to discard it. As she absorbed the contents, she felt a mix of fatigue and intrigue, leading her to ponder the implications of Rastignac's bold approach.

In this moment, the Marquise's thoughts reflect the complexities of her situation—caught between societal expectations and her own desires. The letter's audacity challenges her perceptions of propriety and ignites curiosity about Rastignac's intentions. 

This encounter sets the stage for a deeper exploration of the Marquise's character and the societal dynamics at play, as she navigates the delicate balance between virtue and desire in a world where appearances dictate reality.

Madame de Listomere decided to forbid her porter from admitting Monsieur de Rastignac and resolved to show him disdain in social settings due to his insolence. Initially considering keeping a love letter she received, she ultimately burned it, surprising her housemaid, Caroline.

That evening, at a party hosted by the Marquis de Beauseant, Madame de Listomere anticipated Rastignac's arrival. By 2 AM, realizing he would not come, her thoughts underwent a process Stendhal termed "crystallization."

Four days later, Rastignac scolded his valet, Joseph, for misplacing letters. Joseph revealed one was for Madame de Listomere, prompting Rastignac to realize he had mistakenly sent her a love letter not meant for her. This blunder led him to reflect on his mistakes, particularly not visiting her soon after, allowing her to dwell on the incident.

When Rastignac finally visited, the porter informed him that Madame de Listomere was out, but the marquis invited him in. Upon entering, Rastignac noticed Madame de Listomere blush at his presence, which flattered his vanity. The marquis read the newspaper while Madame de Listomere maintained her composure despite the awkwardness.

Rastignac attempted to explain the misunderstanding about the letter, but Madame de Listomere, feigning offense, seemed amused. She insisted on forgetting the incident, but Rastignac clarified that no offense had occurred. Their conversation revealed underlying tensions, with Madame de Listomere expressing skepticism about his intentions.

As the discussion progressed, Rastignac inadvertently suggested that Madame de Nucingen was the intended recipient of the letter, causing a visible reaction in Madame de Listomere. She dismissed him coldly, pointing out the absurdity of the situation. Feeling foolish, Rastignac left after a few awkward exchanges.

Days later, Madame de Listomere withdrew from society, claiming illness, while the marquis attributed her absence to stomach inflammation. In reality, she was experiencing a nervous attack, using it as an excuse to avoid social interactions.

The narrative highlights the complexities of social dynamics and misunderstandings in romantic pursuits among the young and inexperienced.
