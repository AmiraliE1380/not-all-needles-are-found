The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration spirit, balancing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she preserves a virtuous façade to support his political prospects; while some reserve judgment until he reaches the peerage, her conduct remains strictly principled.

The marquis is a bland, safe figure—more notable for faults than virtues. A silent deputy who votes correctly but lacks charisma, he has earned the nickname “dull weather.” His unremarkable nature suits a woman of restraint, incapable of folly.

Her reserve provokes respect and sometimes disdain; she meets flirtations with contempt and converses with agreeable men without inciting gossip. Never affectingly coy, her behavior is genuinely principled.

The narrator, an admirer, sketches her: neither plain nor conventionally pretty, she nonetheless has a captivating presence; animated eyes reveal a deeper soul, and she charms without seeking it. This emerges in a recent meeting with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man who mixes modesty with vivacity. At a ball he speaks with her on topics from opera to women’s duties—an exchange of mutual interest though he abruptly departs. The next morning Rastignac, preoccupied, imagines romantic pleasures while writing letters until his friend Horace interrupts with talk of a possible expedition to Morea.

Meanwhile the marquise receives Rastignac’s letter; its affectionate terms arouse curiosity. Initially inclined to discard it, she reads on and ends fatigued by his impertinent tone and implications—foreshadowing tension between her virtuous exterior and an emerging emotion.

Madame de Listomere forbade her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac after finding his insolence intolerable; she had considered keeping a letter he sent but ultimately burned it. Caroline told the housemaid, surprised: “Madame received a fine love‑letter.” That evening the marquise went to the Marquis de Beauseant’s party expecting Rastignac; by two in the morning she realized he would not come. Stendhal called the hardening of her resentment “crystallization.”

Four days later Rastignac scolded his valet Joseph for misplacing letters; Joseph remembered one was for Madame de Listomere and another for Rastignac’s lawyer. When Rastignac learned the misplaced letter was not for Madame de Nucingen he laughed, aware his affections had wandered. His errors began with a foolish attempt to amuse Madame de Listomere by discussing the blunder and worsened when he failed to visit for several days, letting her displeasure set.

When he finally called, the porter said she was out but the marquis, unaware of the tension, invited him in. As they climbed the stairs Rastignac noticed social awkwardness. On seeing him the marquise blushed; he adjusted his cravat, thinking “What! that fortress, too?” The marquis remained absorbed in his newspaper. Madame de Listomere quickly regained composure. Asked about her health, she said she was well; Eugene suggested his presence might be unwelcome because of the marquis’s intervention, and she hinted that her husband did not share all her secrets.

Eugene tried to ease the mood; she promised to forget the incident while keeping a dignified reserve. When he mentioned the letter she feigned offense but could not hide amusement; he insisted it was not meant for her. The marquis interrupted to mention a mutual acquaintance’s death, prompting discussion that revealed her irritation. Eugene naively suggested she might be fishing for a secret, which increased her curiosity. When he brought up Madame de Nucingen her demeanor shifted: she rang for wood to signal his departure and coldly confronted him about how his pen could have written her name. Embarrassed, he left. Days later she confirmed his claim about the true recipient and then withdrew from society, the marquis blaming a stomach ailment while her physician diagnosed a nervous condition. The episode highlights social misunderstandings, emotional reactions, and rigid romantic roles.
