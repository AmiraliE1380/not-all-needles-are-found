The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration spirit, balancing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she maintains a virtuous façade to support his political prospects. While some women reserve judgment until his elevation to the peerage, the marquise’s conduct is strictly principled.

The marquis is a bland figure, known more for faults than virtues. A silent deputy who votes correctly but lacks charisma, he has earned the nickname “dull weather.” His unremarkable nature makes him a safe match for a virtuous woman, incapable of folly.

Despite a reserved manner, the marquise provokes both respect and disdain from men who attempt to flirt with her, receiving only contempt in return. She converses with agreeable men without generating gossip, unlike women with ulterior motives. She does not play coy; her behavior is genuinely principled.

The narrator, an admirer, sketches her appearance: neither plain nor conventionally pretty, she possesses a captivating presence. Animated eyes reveal a deeper soul, and she exudes charm without pursuing it. This quality appears in her recent encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man who combines modesty and vivacity.

Rastignac, adept at navigating society, speaks with the marquise at a ball about topics from opera to women’s duties. Their exchange reflects mutual interest, though he abruptly leaves afterward. The next morning, still preoccupied by their talk, he writes letters in a comfortable setting and finds his thoughts increasingly absorbed by her.

He imagines the pleasures of romance, and is interrupted by his friend Horace, with whom he discusses various matters, including a possible expedition to Morea.

Meanwhile, the marquise receives a letter from Rastignac as she prepares for the day. It begins with affectionate terms that spark her curiosity. Initially inclined to discard it, she reads on to understand his sentiments. The letter’s conclusion leaves her fatigued as she contemplates the impertinence of his tone and the implications of his words.

This moment captures the tension between her virtuous exterior and emerging emotion, foreshadowing the complexities of her character and the dynamics of her relationships.

Madame de Listomere forbade her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac, resolving to show him more than disdain because she found his insolence intolerable. She had considered keeping a letter he sent but ultimately burned it. "Madame received a fine love-letter," Caroline told the housemaid, who was surprised.

That evening Madame de Listomere went to a party at the Marquis de Beauseant expecting Rastignac; by two in the morning she realized he was not coming, despite intending to confront him with coldness. Stendhal called this mental process "crystallization."

Four days later Rastignac scolded his valet Joseph for misplacing letters. Joseph, distracted, recalled one was for Madame de Listomere and another for Rastignac's lawyer. When Rastignac learned the letter was not for Madame de Nucingen, he laughed, recognizing his heart had strayed. His mistakes began when he tried to amuse Madame de Listomere by discussing the blunder and worsened by not visiting for several days, allowing her resentment to harden.

When he finally called, the porter said Madame de Listomere was out; the Marquis de Listomere, unaware of the tension, invited him in. As they climbed the stairs, Rastignac noted several social blunders. On seeing him, Madame de Listomere blushed; he thought, "What! that fortress, too?" and adjusted his cravat. Monsieur de Listomere, absorbed in his newspaper, was oblivious to the undercurrent.

Despite initial embarrassment, Madame de Listomere quickly regained composure. When Eugene asked about her health she said she was well. He suggested his visit might be unwelcome because of the marquis's introduction; she replied her husband did not share all her secrets, hinting at a complex marriage. Trying to ease the mood, Eugene said silence would excuse his visit. She promised forgetfulness while preserving a dignified air. When he mentioned the letter she feigned offense but could not hide amusement.

Eugene insisted no offense was intended and the letter was not meant for her. Madame de Listomere, pretending to be offended, said she could laugh at the situation but warned him against further mistakes. Monsieur de Listomere, still engrossed in his paper, interrupted to mention a mutual acquaintance's death, prompting discussion that exposed the marquise's irritation. Rastignac naively suggested she might be trying to extract a secret from him, which only increased her curiosity.

When Eugene brought up Madame de Nucingen, Madame de Listomere's demeanor shifted. She rang for wood, signaling it was time for him to leave, and coldly confronted him about the letter, asking how his pen could have written her name. Embarrassed, Eugene stumbled and departed. Days later she confirmed his claim about the letter's true recipient. She withdrew from society; the marquis explained her absence as a stomach ailment, while her physician recognized it as a nervous condition she used to justify seclusion.

The episode underscores the complexity of social interactions and misunderstandings in romantic contexts, revealing characters' emotional responses and social roles.
