I have long wanted to tell a simple true story that would terrify two young lovers and drive them to seek refuge in one another, like children clinging at the sight of a snake. I played a part in this ordinary tragedy; if it fails to move you, blame me and the mundanity of reality.

In 1819, traveling from Paris to Moulins and short of money, I took an outside seat on the coach. An Englishman, better off, joined me; we bonded over fresh air and the landscape. Conversation turned to women and love: we agreed a birth certificate meant little—some women at thirty-five or forty were younger in spirit than girls at twenty. We celebrated “the woman of a certain age.” He said his mistress was thirty-eight; I confessed mine was forty. Freed by this, we traded follies: one had traveled six hundred miles for a brief meeting; the other had risked danger for a secret rendezvous. His countess had lighted a cigar for him; mine made chocolate and wrote daily when we were apart. Both were adored by their husbands, which only heightened the peril. Then the wind took our laughter.

At Pouilly I looked closer: dark-haired, blue-eyed, pale, well-proportioned, with shapely hands and a clever air—a Vicomte with means whom many girls would desire.

About a league out of Pouilly the coach overturned. He jumped to escape, misjudged, and was crushed beneath it. We carried him to a peasant’s cottage where, in agony, he entrusted me: tell his mistress of his death so she would not learn it from a newspaper. He asked me to retrieve a key from around his neck; I found it embedded in his flesh. He gave directions to his home in La Charite-sur-Loire for the love letters, then grew speechless and died. The coachman muttered it was partly his fault; I was consumed by duty.

At La Charite I fulfilled his wishes. His mother was away; an old servant fainted at the news. I took the sealed letters, burdened by the sorrow of a woman who had lost her last love. The countess’s chateau lay eight leagues beyond Moulins. With little money but resolute youth, I walked quickly to bring the news before anyone else. Nearing the Chateau de Montpersan, unease about my role as bearer of such news came over me.

At the chateau the open doors unsettled me. Barking dogs and a servant-maid directed me to the park where Madame was. Feeling lost, I was led by a playful little girl who, calling for her mother, guided me through winding paths.

I paused behind the last shrub on the avenue, adjusting my shabby clothes so the lady would not mistake me for a collector. I spotted Juliette and her husband among the flowers; at her child's ambiguous remark her pace quickened. Surprised by my presence, she regarded me coolly, disappointment visible. I fumbled the elegant phrases I had prepared, and my hesitation let her husband approach. I asked if they were indeed M. le Comte and Mme. la Comtesse de Montpersan, using the question to size them up. He was a typical provincial nobleman: large, self-important, badly mannered, more pretension than polish. The Countess was the opposite: petite, graceful, in white muslin with rose-colored accents, youthful and lively—any Romantic reader would recognize her.

Choosing diplomacy, I took the Count aside with an air of mystery and requested a private word. He obliged, confident his wife could learn his secrets at will, and left her to wander. I told him of my traveling companion’s death and of his sincere attachment to Juliette. Concerned for his wife, the Count urged caution; I explained I could not approach her without first informing him, stressing the sacredness of the message entrusted to me. Flattered, he allowed me to proceed.

As we neared the house, Juliette glanced at us with curiosity and disdain, likely because of my appearance and our unexpected presence. I recalled her beauty in the sunlit alley and sighed. “Alas, madame, I have made a difficult journey solely for you,” I began; her face paled. She asked if he was ill; I answered that he was but urged her to control herself, saying he had entrusted me with secrets concerning her. Pressing for details, she demanded whether he still loved her. I could only nod. Distress overwhelmed her; she cried out and leaned against a tree.

“Your husband is coming!” I warned.

“Do I have a husband?” she fled.

The Count called me to dinner; the Countess was absent. The canon of Saint-Denis inquired after her, but the Count shrugged and hastily served the lavish meal. After a while a maid reported the Countess missing. Alarmed, I rushed out with the canon and the maid to search the garden and park. As we called, I recounted the tragic accident; the maid’s concern for her mistress was evident.

At last we heard a muffled cry from a granary. Inside we found Juliette buried in hay, sobbing. The maid and I helped her to her room; I instructed that she not be disturbed, claiming she had a headache. Returning to the dining room, I was surprised the Count still ate, seemingly unfazed. His indifference was explained: a serious medical condition had left him ravenous, overshadowing his concern for Juliette. The evening was somber; exhausted, we retired early.

Passing the Countess’s room, I asked the servant for news. Juliette called me in, tried to speak but could not. I left, feeling the weight of her agitation.

Later that night I was awakened by the sound of curtain rings. The Countess sat at the foot of my bed, pale and drawn. “Is it true?” she asked, voice trembling. “I don’t know how I can live after this blow, but I want to know everything.” Her eyes were red and swollen; she had lost her youthful glow. I related the accident briefly, avoiding gruesome details. When I described the dying man’s fears and the message he had entrusted to me, her tears dried into a deep pallor. I handed her letters from him; she took them mechanically, lamenting that she had burned all his other letters and now had nothing. “I cut this lock of his hair,” she said, revealing a precious token. I gave it to her; her tears fell on my hands, a moment of profound gratitude and connection.

“Ah! You love! May you always be happy,” she cried, clutching the lock and fleeing with her treasure.

The next morning felt surreal. I searched under my pillow for the letters but found nothing. I spent the day with Juliette, witnessing her noble spirit and selflessness. That evening the Comte de Montpersan accompanied me to Moulins and asked if I could deliver a sum of money to a contact in Paris. I agreed, taking a rouleau of twenty-five louis d’or, which covered my journey back. On arrival I appreciated the delicate way Juliette had arranged the loan, revealing the depth of her love and thoughtfulness. What a joy to share this adventure with a woman who cherished me, saying, “Oh, my dear, not you! You must not die!”
