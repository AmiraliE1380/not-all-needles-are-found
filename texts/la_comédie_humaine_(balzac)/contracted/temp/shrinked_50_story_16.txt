In the spring of 1822 Parisian doctors sent M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil to Lower Normandy to recover from an inflammatory illness likely caused by overwork or excess. Seeking rest, light diet, and fresh air, he settled in Bayeux with relatives.

After a few evenings with his cousin, Mme. de Sainte-Severe, Gaston met the local elite, who illustrated provincial French types. One was an ancient but impoverished noble family: a patriarch, a resolute sportsman contemptuous of modernity and the new political order; a loud, domineering wife convinced their daughters’ prospects rested on their name. They clung to obsolete customs—nobility without wealth or influence.

In contrast was a wealthier, more recent family attuned to Parisian trends: the wife a society woman, the husband a shrewd member of the Departmental Council who managed finances and political gossip. Their Royalism was mild; they engaged with modern life while keeping ancestral ties.

The local bishop, a former Vicar-General, mediated between families—respected yet reminded of plebeian origins. Retired officers and courtly-dressed women preserved a veneer of aristocracy; a few wealthy townspeople and ecclesiastics added bourgeois wit. Conversation and judgment were antiquated and immovable; intellectual life stagnated and critics were branded misfits.

Gaston, twenty-three, was welcomed after his cousin disclosed an income of eighteen thousand livres from land (his father negotiating more woodland), omitting that his elder brother was consumptive. At first he amused himself sketching their quirks, but weeks of monotony left him weary and trapped; ennui threatened to turn him into one more fossil among the fossils.

One evening Gaston de Nueil sat between a dowager and a vicar-general in a gray-paneled drawing-room where family portraits overlooked four card tables and sixteen people playing whist. After dinner he absorbed provincial habits—the old cards, threadbare cloths—and their indifference to luxury, which made Paris seem remote.

He noticed a shy young lady with red hands, but a casual remark about Mme. de Beauseant stirred him more. An elderly guest revealed that the Vicomtesse, supposedly ill and withdrawn to Courcelles, had a scandalous liaison with M. d’Ajuda-Pinto and, after separating from her husband, was shunned—an exile that intrigued him.

Enthralled by the idea of the Vicomtesse as a focus of hope amid provincial stagnation, he feared a direct letter would be refused and resolved to have the Marquis, through M. de Champignelles, arrange an introduction. Champignelles’s message, at first resisted, aroused her curiosity and she agreed to receive him. Excited and anxious, Gaston prepared carefully and went to meet the enigmatic Vicomtesse.

A great fire burned on the hearth despite mild weather, casting a soft light on a young woman in a high‑backed bergère. The Vicomtesse, with graceful movements, stirred the coals, picked up a fallen glove, and motioned to a chair for M. de Nueil. Her charm and poised, fastidious carelessness set her apart from the automata he had met during two months’ exile in Normandy; in her presence he felt transported back to Paris and its sophistication.

Mme. de Beauseant had fair hair, dark eyes, and a pale complexion that gave her a regal, almost fallen‑angel air; her hair, braided in a crown, heightened that bearing. Beneath her beauty lay an underlying sadness—traces of past unhappiness and lost love. Alone in her silent salon she seemed cut off from the world, haunted by memories of a once‑vibrant youth. M. de Nueil felt humbled and captivated by this blend of beauty, nobility, and misfortune.

When she acknowledged him he felt the weight of his deception. He had come to deliver a message from M. de Champignelles, but consciousness of having deceived her made him flush. Summoning courage he confessed, “Madame, I do not deserve the happiness of seeing you. I have deceived you basely.” She rang for a servant to escort him out; her manner turned icy.

As he left he realized that if he went away now he would never return, and the Vicomtesse would think him a fool. He hesitated, then turned back, saying he had forgotten something; the servant followed unknowingly. A half‑sad smile—“Jacques lighted me to the door”—softened her; she invited him to sit. He began to plead greater blame, and she, shifting between playfulness and gravity, complained that she could not act as she pleased, accused him of disrespect, and rejected pity—preferring scorn. She urged him to recognize the impropriety of his conduct and commanded him not to return, fearing gossip.

M. de Nueil owned his fault, blaming youthful enthusiasm, exile’s weariness, and an idealized longing for love that led to this foolish intrusion, for which he sought indulgence.

An impassioned voice warmed the chill solitude with youthful charm. Mme. de Beauseant, moved by its sincerity, found herself captivated by M. de Nueil’s noble countenance—Gaston, in the bloom of youth, seemed the ideal man, unaffected by ambition or vanity. Both were drawn to one another, reflecting on and concealing their feelings. He perceived in the Vicomtesse not only beauty but depth and goodness; she, hearing his youthful troubles, recognized the purity of someone resisting society’s corruptions.

Gaston’s approach was instinctive; Mme. de Beauseant’s deliberate—she tested him with opposing opinions. Her wit and grace put him at ease, prompting his exclamation, “Oh! madame, how could any man have left you?” The remark revived her sense of innocence, condemned the world for her past, and offered an absolution that lifted her above former shame.

She checked the clock and, smiling, acknowledged the fleeting nature of their encounter. Gaston wished to prolong it; she insisted on preserving her solitary life, fearing a new connection would tarnish her reputation. Candid about a forsaken love, she urged they remain strangers. Torn between admiration and fear of appearing foolish, he confessed his love and offered to heal her old wounds. Moved yet resolute, she refused. He left uncertain, then covertly returned to Courcelles and, after much hesitation, wrote a heartfelt letter laying bare his feelings, awaiting her response while doubts and longing gnawed at him.

M. de Nueil received a letter from Mme. de Beauseant, sealed with the arms of Burgundy, in which she refused his advances. She explained she had tried to spare him a blunt rebuff: youthful passion would not move her. Though she cared for him, she did not share his love and would not yield to impulse. Nearly thirty while he was just over twenty, she argued, they could not predict how feelings would change with age; vows made lightly might become burdens she could not ask him to bear. Her consolation was in God, not earthly ties; she offered only the friendship of an older woman and urged him to remain at home rather than wreck his life for an illusion.

Gaston answered immediately and fervently: if he could cease to love her and accept the ordinary life she suggested, he would deserve his fate; his fidelity could be absolved only by death, and she might take his life if she feared his remorse. When the messenger returned, Mme. de Beauseant was already preparing to leave by carriage, so Gaston followed her to Geneva, convinced that if she truly cared she would choose the freedom of Switzerland.

She had rented a small lakeside house. One summer evening he surprised her there; she dropped her book and embraced him as he explained his pursuit. For three years they lived in the villa on Lake Geneva, alone and seemingly in dreamlike happiness: a simple home, beautiful views, and tranquil days treasured together.

After the deaths of his father and brother, weighty obligations called Gaston back to France. They kept the villa unsold and bought the family house; Mme. de Beauseant sold her property to buy an estate near Manerville adjoining Gaston’s lands. They lived together, his mother taking the family house, and resumed the secluded happiness they had known. For nine years they enjoyed an almost indescribable bliss, yet the legal obstacle remained: Mme. de Beauseant’s husband, the Marquis, was still alive, so any lawful union was impossible. The dowager comtesse would never accept her, and the Marquise feared the dowager’s influence might one day pull Gaston away. Though tempted to return to Geneva, Mme. de Beauseant would not act in a way that betrayed distrust.

The arrival of a young heiress, Mlle. de la Rodiere, and Gaston’s meeting with her disturbed this fragile peace. Mme. de Beauseant answered with a letter revealing pride and inner turmoil: she had not known love until him and had felt no jealousy for nine years, but now she feared, at forty, becoming a burden to his future and suspected his devotion might be pity. Too proud to bind his future to her fading life, she urged him to be frank—if he still loved her he should come, and she would love him more than ever; if he sought freedom she would conceal her suffering and put his happiness first. Learning that her letter had reached Gaston, she was stunned and numb. Reading it, M. de Nueil found himself plunged into a painful dilemma of passion, duty, pride, and the prospect of a conventional future.

By this time Gaston had nearly yielded to his mother’s entreaties and the practical attraction of Mlle. de la Rodiere, a plain young woman with a forty-thousand-livre dowry. Mme. de Nueil pressed for a respectable match, arguing that Mme. de Beauseant, who had refused many distinguished suitors, should encourage a marriage that might one day bring him eighty thousand livres. Her plea left him torn.

Just as his passion for Mme. de Beauseant resisted convention, a letter from her altered everything: he resolved to end their liaison and marry. “One must live a man’s life,” he told himself, though he knew the choice would wound her. Vanity and conscience deepened his sorrow; he felt pity for her and meant to prepare her gently. For a time he planned to sacrifice Mlle. de la Rodiere to soften the blow, marrying later if necessary to honor Mme. de Beauseant’s wishes. He intended a letter to allay her suspicions, though he feared a woman so perceptive might be devastated by mere words.

When Jacques delivered the note, Mme. de Beauseant trembled; dread of loss closed in. If he did not come in person, she feared all was lost. As she read, an inner cry declared, “He lies!” She saw the message as calculated, not true. In despair she tore the letter and cast it into the fire, feeling betrayed and abandoned.

Jacques then brought word that “Madame la Marquise has left the chateau.” M. de Nueil opened the paper and found, to his amazement, his own letter from nine years earlier in which he had sworn eternal fidelity to Mme. de Beauseant; Claire de Bourgogne had scrawled beneath it, “Monsieur, you are free.” Stunned, he consulted his mother and, within three weeks, married Mlle. Stephanie de la Rodiere.

If the story stopped there it might seem trivial, but its notoriety touches those who have known profound love and loss. Mme. de Beauseant never left Valleroy after their parting; she lived in seclusion, rarely seen except by servants, and attended mass daily.

Soon after the marriage M. de Nueil grew pensive. A trivial-seeming incident revealed deeper turmoil: returning from a hunt he had Jacques set aside game for the Marquise, then brought home an empty bag while Jacques delivered the spoil to her. His letters to her were returned unopened. One evening, finding her in despair and threatening to throw herself from a window if he approached, he fled terrified. He wrote again—an hour later the servant brought back the unopened note. In despair M. de Nueil retired and shot himself.

The tragic end, though at odds with fashionable conventions, seems inevitable to those who have known a soul-connection. Deep love needs time and nourishment; exchanging it for a loveless marriage yields either despair or a cold, material life. A mistress cannot accept humiliation—the purity of her passion demands respect.

ANGOULEME, September 1832.
