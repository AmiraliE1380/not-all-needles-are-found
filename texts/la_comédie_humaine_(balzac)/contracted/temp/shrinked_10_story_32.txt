DEDICATION

To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy, etc.

Dear Nodier, this book reveals actions concealed behind domestic doors, where supposed divine intervention often replaces human justice. Its moral concerns are family and motherhood. We may yet see the consequences of weakening paternal authority, historically the sole tribunal for domestic wrongs; a mother, however loving, cannot replace that patriarchal function. This story emphasizes the indissoluble marriage bond and the perils of unchecked selfishness. May a society built on wealth recognize the law's impotence in a system that exalts success at any cost, and may it return to Catholicism for moral uplift through a different education.

In “Scenes from Military Life,” I will show noble self-sacrifice while exposing war’s corrupting influence on those who live domestically as if on a battlefield.

You have observed our times keenly, and your reflections on political chaos have been insightful. I entrust this story to your esteemed name, which may shelter it from inevitable criticism.

Your sincere admirer,

De Balzac  
Paris, November 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792 the townspeople of Issoudun disliked Dr. Rouget. Rumor held he made his beautiful wife unhappy, though she may have been somewhat foolish. Despite gossip, the household’s real life remained hidden. Rouget was ill-liked but treated civilly. His wife, a demoiselle Descoings, had been frail in youth; some said that explained Rouget’s choice. They had a son, Jean-Jacques, and, unexpectedly ten years later, a daughter, Agathe.

These facts illuminate Rouget’s character: he believed men must show strength, a notion that caused much female suffering. The Descoings, his in-laws, were wealthy wool merchants who grew rich and miserly. Descoings, Rouget’s brother-in-law, left Issoudun for Paris, failed as a grocer, and was driven by love for a beautiful widow.

By 1792 Descoings was thought prosperous; his parents had moved from wool into investments in forfeited estates. Expecting his wife’s death, Dr. Rouget sent Agathe to Paris to stay with Descoings, hoping she would win their favor. Agathe, the most beautiful girl in Issoudun, resembled her mother, which caused a rift between Rouget and his friend Monsieur Lousteau, who left town after their quarrel.

Madame Rouget, learning of her daughter’s departure, predicted she would never see Agathe again—a sentiment that proved true. Her health then declined, worsened by her son’s neglect. Jean-Jacques, like his father, offered little comfort.

Agathe’s arrival in Paris coincided with Descoings’ imprisonment after imprudent political remarks about the famine that angered powerful men. His wife’s attempts to free him failed; he was executed alongside the poet André Chénier. The episode made local grocers wary of politics for years and affected the community more than Chénier’s death at the time, though history later judged otherwise.

After this, Agathe married Bridau, a ministry secretary, with Dr. Rouget’s approval; Rouget sought to profit from the Descoings inheritance. Bridau, captivated by Agathe’s beauty, married unaware of Rouget’s manipulations.

Once the Descoings’ wealth passed on, Dr. Rouget descended into debauchery until his death in 1805, leaving Jean-Jacques, who remained a bachelor for reasons tied to his father’s influence. Agathe, resembling her mother, became a devoted wife and mother, yet her ignorance of the world made her vulnerable.

Bridau, an ardent supporter of Napoleon, was promoted in 1804. He worked relentlessly, neglected his health, and in 1808 died from overwork just as a further promotion loomed—the Emperor had intended to reward him but was too late.

After Bridau’s death no one in Issoudun wrote to Agathe. She lost her mother shortly before giving birth to her second son; her father’s death coincided with the Emperor’s coronation, which kept Bridau occupied. Her brother had not contacted her since she left home, and she gradually accepted the family’s indifference.

Madame Hochon, Agathe’s godmother, warned that Agathe would inherit nothing unless she granted power of attorney to her brother. Agathe hesitated to burden him; Bridau, either unaware or indifferent, did not contest the arrangement. Their tacit acceptance left them financially exposed.

Concerned for Agathe, Bridau had invested their remaining funds prudently to secure a modest income. After his death, Agathe and Madame Descoings pooled resources and lived on a combined income of twelve thousand francs a year. This practical arrangement concealed underlying strains.

By 1809 Madame Descoings, now sixty-five, retained youthful looks and charm, enjoyed good food and the theater, and secretly gambled on lotteries. She also harbored a significant secret about her son, Bixiou, a major in the army, who had a son being educated alongside Agathe’s children.

Agathe, devoted to her children, chose not to remarry, believing it her duty to be both mother and authority. Her lack of worldly understanding and blind trust in Madame Descoings, however, led to ruin. Madame Descoings, hoping to enrich the family through lotteries, racked up heavy debts and finally confessed.

Agathe, without reproach, took charge: she sold possessions and liquidated investments to settle the debts, a decisive turning point in their lives.

One of the bleakest spots in Paris was the stretch of rue Mazarin between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, hemmed in by the high gray walls of the college and library given by Cardinal Mazarin. There, on the third floor of a damp, cold building, lived a poor widow, Madame Bridau. Opposite were the Institute buildings, where aspiring artists — the rapins — strove amid the tumult of exams to win prizes; that artistic center has since moved to the Palais des Beaux-Arts.

From her windows Madame Bridau watched the students’ cages below. To brighten her rooms she risked police fines by setting boxes of earth outside the windows to grow little gardens. The house was shallow, reached by a winding staircase, and contained three rooms — a dining room, a small salon, and a bedroom — plus a kitchen and two single rooms. She had chosen it because it was affordable (four hundred francs a year), near her sons’ school, and in a neighborhood she knew.

The interior matched the house’s general dreariness. The dining room, with yellow wallpaper and unglazed tile, held only essentials. The salon bore an Aubusson carpet given to her and a mahogany sofa that supported a pastel portrait of her late husband, Bridau. The portrait captured his nobility; above a card table hung a vivid painting of Napoleon.

After her husband’s death Madame Bridau had taken to keeping birds: two large cages, one with canaries, the other with Java sparrows. Her room grew cluttered with cat hair, millet, and bits of chickweed — signs of grief and neglected self-care. She preserved everything belonging to Bridau, even wrapping his last pen with a note. Having lost him, she seemed to lose a sense of herself as a woman, concentrating entirely on her children.

Agathe now lived for her sons and fretted over their prospects. Since moving to rue Mazarin a melancholy had settled on her face. She looked to the Emperor for support; he granted three hundred francs a year for each child, along with scholarships. Her aunt, Madame Descoings, on the second floor, had assigned three thousand francs from her annuity to help Agathe. The Descoings themselves lived modestly on twelve hundred francs a year, employed a woman for morning chores, and shared dinners with the two widows. Madame Descoings doted on Philippe and Joseph, indulging them with treats and small sums.

Driven by maternal love and distrustful of the future, Madame Bridau kept expenses low, cutting small pleasures and saving three thousand francs from her pension to secure a modest inheritance for her children. Thus both widows moved from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty, each for different reasons.

The view from her windows, the students’ tumult, and her husband’s portrait would shape her younger son, Joseph Bridau, into a notable painter of the modern French school.

Philippe, the elder, resembled his mother in looks and carried a bold demeanour often mistaken for courage. Agathe believed physical likeness signaled moral likeness and hoped he would inherit her sensitivity. At fifteen Philippe’s charm reinforced those hopes. Joseph, three years younger, took after his father in less praise-worthy ways: untidy, frequently scolded, and plainly less favored by his mother.

Joseph’s artistic bent began during a visit to the Tuileries, where a caricature drawn on an Institute wall enthralled him. This sparked his desire to draw and led him to sneak into the Institute courtyard, where older students at first teased him. His persistence won their respect, and they encouraged him toward art.

He began visiting the atelier of the sculptor Chaudet, who recognized and fostered his talent. Agathe feared for Joseph’s future and tried to dissuade him, convinced an artistic life meant hardship. Chaudet defended the vocation passionately, insisting true talent is a calling.

As Joseph pursued art, Agathe’s anxiety increased when Philippe expressed a wish to join the military. Enchanted by soldiering, Philippe secretly petitioned the Emperor to enlist. He was soon sent to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr and rose rapidly, becoming a captain by nineteen.

Meanwhile Joseph’s scholarship was withdrawn and he sought to earn a living through art, encouraged by Madame Descoings. Despite Agathe’s concerns about his health and security, Joseph thrived in the artistic milieu, forming close bonds with fellow students and mentors.

As politics shifted, Philippe returned home decorated while Joseph faced uncertainty. Agathe’s preference for Philippe became more evident as she admired his military achievements and sacrifices for the Emperor. Joseph, less celebrated, remained devoted to his mother, who struggled to grasp his artistic aims.

Despite their divergent paths, both brothers navigated the challenges of their careers, with Agathe’s love and persistent concern for their futures shaping their choices and fortunes.

In 1816 Joseph won his mother’s consent to convert the adjoining garret into an atelier, partly funded by Madame Descoings. The two widows treated painting as a trade, but Joseph embraced it passionately, installing a skylight and chocolate-colored walls while continuing to study under Gros and Schinner.

Political unrest simmered in France: conspiracies and riots surrounded the Chamber of Deputies. Philippe, implicated in plots, was arrested and released for lack of evidence, but his military pay was cut and Agathe feared for him. The widows’ friends urged Agathe to send Philippe abroad for safety; she resisted the idea of him serving a foreign power.

Ultimately Philippe joined General Lallemand in the United States to help found the Champ d’Asile, an enterprise that failed. Agathe equipped him with ten thousand francs, and by late 1817 she had adjusted to reduced means. Joseph lived frugally to support the family while his artistic talent developed.

In 1818 Roguin, the notary, absconded with funds meant for Madame Descoings. Soon after, Philippe sent a bill for a thousand francs, having lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. Agathe, distraught, blamed herself for urging his departure. Despite their own difficulties, Madame Descoings continued to support her.

Desperate for funds, Joseph turned to Gerard, who arranged for him to copy a portrait of Louis XVIII. Joseph worked tirelessly, selling panels to cover Philippe’s debt. Philippe then returned from America; Agathe welcomed him with open arms, not perceiving how much he had changed.

Time abroad had hardened him. He became self-centered, indulged in drinking and gambling, and adopted a swaggering arrogance. Agathe, blinded by maternal love, excused his faults, viewing him as a victim of circumstance. The family’s finances deteriorated, and friends discussed selling possessions to help Philippe.

Philippe’s conduct alarmed acquaintances: he rejected serving the Bourbons, which hampered employment prospects, yet he lived as if supported by others. He frequently disappeared for days, sustained a lavish lifestyle, and grew distant from family responsibilities. He favored cafes and gambling halls, deepened friendships with former comrades, and showed increasing disregard for others.

Still, Philippe sought to impress Mariette, a young dancer. Giroudeau encouraged the pursuit and promised to help Philippe enter journalism, advising him to exploit his experiences. Philippe’s ambitions clashed with reality; he struggled to adapt to civilian life and grew more hostile to the Liberal party, blaming it for his misfortunes and nursing a desire for revenge against those he felt had wronged him.

When a potential job materialized, Agathe and Madame Descoings breathed easier and agreed to secure Philippe’s position, overlooking the sacrifices already made. The role—as cashier at a newspaper—brought temporary relief but did little to reform him. His arrogance continued to grow, and Joseph felt increasingly overshadowed and unappreciated despite his sacrifices and rising talent.

By the end, Philippe’s return and changed behavior had strained relationships and exposed fault lines in loyalty, ambition, and judgment. The once close-knit household faced tests that revealed the consequences of choices in pursuit of dreams, the limits of maternal devotion, and the fragile balance between personal aspiration and familial duty.

Three months later the colonel, who ate enough for four, still criticized the food while the widows, reluctant to press him for payment, paid lavishly for meals he did not help fund. His mother and Madame Descoings hesitated to remind him of his promise. A year passed without any of the “tigers with five claws” Leon Gozlan mentioned moving from Philippe’s pocket into the household purse. The colonel rarely dined at home. “Well, he is happy,” his mother remarked, “he has a place.”

Mariette, influenced by a feuilleton edited by Vernou, made her debut at the Porte-Saint-Martin, triumphing with the famous Begrand. A wealthy general, enamored with an actress and acquainted with Philippe and Giroudeau, became her impresario and arranged her first engagement—an example of the solidarity among those captivated by folly.

Bixiou soon revealed to his grandmother and Agathe that Philippe, the cashier, was in love with Mariette, the celebrated ballet-dancer. This shocked the widows; Agathe’s piety made her view all stage women as morally corrupt, and both believed such women lived extravagantly.

“Do you think my brother is foolish enough to spend money on Mariette? Such women ruin rich men,” Joseph objected. Bixiou reassured them that Mariette might be engaged by the Opera and that diplomatic visitors to the Porte-Saint-Martin could soon take her from Philippe; he even heard an ambassador was madly in love with her.

Philippe, partly motivated by securing her financially, proposed marriage, but Mariette—anticipating an engagement at the Grand Opera—declined, perhaps sensing his motives or valuing independence. For the rest of the year Philippe visited his mother only twice a month, spending his time at the office, the theatre, or with Mariette. He enjoyed himself with entertainers like Tullia and others.

This carnival lasted eighteen months but brought trouble. After her Opera debut in January 1821 Mariette quickly attracted a distinguished duke. Philippe, driven by passion, began dipping into the funds he managed, taking eleven hundred francs by May. Mariette then left for London to seek lords who might support her while the temporary opera house was prepared. Despite her infidelities, Philippe remained infatuated; she saw him as a stepping stone.

Forced to let Mariette go, Philippe returned to his mother’s apartment, unable to live without the pleasures of the past year. Suicide crossed his mind—not because of the deficit alone but because he could not endure life without Mariette. He entered Joseph’s studio, where Joseph was painting, and began a fragile conversation. When Joseph explained his work and earnings, Philippe, pale and subdued, asked how long it would take to paint his portrait—revealing he wished to leave his mother a likeness. When Joseph asked if he meant to go away again, Philippe replied, “I am going never to return.”

Pressed by Joseph, Philippe confessed he had taken money from the funds and could not live with the dishonor. Joseph reassured him he would not be disgraced if he repaid the sum, but Philippe, hopeless, rushed downstairs, leaving Joseph anxious.

Joseph informed Madame Descoings, who was horrified. Agathe, learning the truth, was devastated, fearing for Philippe’s life and the family’s honor. “He! to fail in honor! the son of Bridau to take the money that was trusted to him!” she cried. Madame Descoings urged hope, attributing Philippe’s ruin to the influence of a bad woman; Agathe, however, lived in terror for her son.

That day Philippe calmly submitted his accounts, inventing that he had merely retained the money for safekeeping. He then went to a gambling house, where he first won but ultimately lost everything. Returning home he found concern and affection from his family, which comforted him.

“Promise not to kill yourself, and all shall be forgotten,” his mother pleaded. Philippe, realizing the appeal had worked, feigned gratitude and affection, embracing her. They shared dinner, and Philippe, despite earlier despair, felt relieved.

The next day Agathe appeared older and more anxious, aware their financial situation was dire. Madame Descoings, mindful of the family’s reputation, suggested consulting Desroches, who could manage the affair. Agathe agreed, hoping to protect Philippe’s name.

Desroches, acting in his diplomatic capacity, advised Agathe to sign the transfer of funds and petition for Philippe’s reinstatement in the army. He assured them Philippe would soon regain his rank, relieving some worry. Still, Agathe’s faith was shaken and she lived in constant fear of his potential downfall.

Philippe resumed a life of leisure—gambling, drinking, and appearing disheveled—indifferent to his family’s plight. His passion for Mariette had hardened him; he treated women with contempt. Meanwhile Joseph devoted himself to painting, gaining recognition and support from Madame Descoings, who believed in his future success.

As Madame Descoings pursued her lottery dreams she remained oblivious to growing tensions. Joseph, noticing discrepancies in his finances, suspected Philippe and confided in Madame Descoings, who shared his concerns. They planned to catch Philippe in the act, hoping to confront him.

When Joseph discovered Philippe had indeed taken money from him, he felt deeply betrayed. Despite anger, he still recognized Philippe as his brother and struggled with confronting him. Madame Descoings urged sympathy for Philippe’s struggles, but Joseph remained wary of his brother’s influence on the family’s future.

Despite Philippe's careful approach to his nightly gambling, he sometimes found himself "cleaned out." Needing his evening stake of ten francs, he began stealing from his family—taking from his brother and whatever Madame Descoings or Agathe left about. After a distressing dream in which Philippe robbed her, Agathe realized the depth of his decline. She begged him to ask for money instead of stealing, but within two weeks her savings were gone and she considered work. She took worsted work for a meager twenty sous a day. The strain of her hidden anguish showed in her appearance; she spent sleepless nights waiting for Philippe, noting his drunkenness and mood swings tied to gambling.

Madame Descoings suggested Philippe get new clothes; he retorted that his mother had no money and that he had mortgaged his pension. He had borrowed from Giroudeau, calling it a "debt of honor." Agathe defended him, believing he was out of his element, while Joseph criticized his neglect of appearance and duty. Philippe's gambling worsened and he became more debauched, losing his former good looks.

One day Philippe asked Joseph for money to pay a debt to the tobacconist; Joseph refused. Philippe then schemed to find where Madame Descoings kept her savings, guessing she would be out buying Christmas treats. He broke into her room and discovered twenty napoleons hidden in the mattress. He took the money, intending to gamble it.

At the gambling house Philippe won substantial sums over several sessions. By the end of the night he had amassed over one hundred fifty thousand francs. Intoxicated by success, he pressed on and lost everything. The next morning he woke in despair, having ruined his family.

Agathe and Madame Descoings waited for Philippe to return for Christmas dinner. When he failed to show, they worried. Madame Descoings had pinned hopes on her lottery stake, but when she checked her savings she found the money gone and fainted. Agathe, horrified, sought confirmation from the concierge, who revealed Philippe had been in the apartment that day.

Agathe returned pale and shaken; the family confronted Philippe. He dismissed their accusations, claiming he had taken what was owed him. The confrontation escalated into a physical altercation between Joseph and Philippe. As tensions peaked, Madame Descoings suffered a stroke and the family rushed to her side.

Afterward Joseph tried to help his mother with money, but she refused, clinging to the lottery hope. When the winning numbers were announced it became clear she had missed her chance, and she declined further. She died shortly after, leaving Agathe and Joseph to deal with the aftermath of Philippe's betrayal.

As Philippe recovered from his own illness, Agathe told him their financial situation had drastically changed and urged him to rejoin the army and support himself. He responded with indifference, feeling abandoned. Despite her tears she offered him money; he took it and left, seeking the company of those he called true friends. Agathe, heartbroken, prayed for her son and resigned herself to the loss of the child she once loved.

By February 1822, Madame Bridau had moved into the attic above the kitchen of her former apartment, recently occupied by her son Philippe. Seeing his mother’s reduced circumstances, Joseph resolved to make her comfortable: he rearranged the garret, added tasteful decor and a double door, and took Agathe to dine daily at a table-d’hôte that cost him nearly a hundred francs a month. Alarmed by the expense, Agathe, with her confessor’s help, accepted a seven-hundred-franc-a-year post at a lottery office that demanded long hours.

Agathe adapted to the routine, preparing Joseph’s breakfast and managing his household despite her lack of art knowledge. She mended his clothes, kept the studio tidy, and aided his work; their bond deepened through small acts of tenderness. They discussed Philippe, whose reckless life worried Agathe, but Joseph dismissed her fears, convinced Philippe was prospering in New York.

One afternoon Joseph visited Philippe, who worked at a newspaper. Philippe invited Joseph to dine, but Joseph declined to prioritize his mother. When he later told Agathe about Philippe, she felt renewed hope. She decorated Joseph’s studio and prepared a special breakfast for Philippe’s portrait sitting. During the session she hid, overwhelmed as she watched the two sons together.

After posing, Philippe invited them to dinner, which made Joseph uneasy because of Philippe’s apparent wealth; Agathe eagerly accepted. The lavish meal heightened Joseph’s suspicions about Philippe’s finances. At the next sitting, Agathe learned more about Philippe’s social circle—journalists and actresses—and heard his boastful ambitions, though he remained evasive about money.

Joseph had to postpone another sitting because of a pressing commission. When Philippe visited the studio he mistakenly took a valuable copy of a Rubens, believing it the original, and sold it for a fraction of its worth. Discovering the theft devastated Joseph; Agathe was heartbroken, feeling she had lost her son to vice.

Two months later an old officer told Agathe of Philippe’s dire condition: ill and needing money for care. Working at the lottery office, she had little to spare and refused to help the son she had banished from her heart. Joseph agreed, insisting Philippe should go to the hospital.

Some time after, Agathe encountered Philippe on the street—destitute and recently discharged from the hospital. Maternal instinct overcame her; she gave him money despite knowing it would likely be squandered, then fell into despair over his future.

Agathe later learned Philippe had been involved in a conspiracy that led to his arrest; she fainted when she heard his name. Joseph consulted Desroches, who advised them to secure the family inheritance. Desperate, Agathe wrote to her godmother for help but received a discouraging reply about her brother’s unwillingness to assist.

Desroches urged quick action to protect the children’s rights, suggesting they gather evidence of undue influence over Philippe and announcing plans to visit Issoudun during the holidays to help. Agathe and Joseph set off for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to face the consequences. As they traveled, Agathe reflected on the changes in Issoudun and the long estrangement from her brother, determined to confront the situation on her return.

Issoudun, one of France's oldest cities, is noted by Caesar and Rigord for its population and commerce. Recent excavations by Monsieur Armand Peremet uncovered a fifth-century basilica, likely unique in France, built from stones of a Roman temple to Isis. The town’s name—derived from "Isis" with "dun" indicating a religious and military settlement—points to ancient Celtic origins.

In the Middle Ages Issoudun was a center of power: Richard Coeur-de-lion built a tower above the basilica as part of fortifications, and the town later served as a stronghold for Routiers and Cottereaux mercenaries sent by Henry II against his son Richard. Yet its prominence has faded. After the 1830 insurrection vine-growers resisted government intervention, burned tax offices, and asserted autonomy, showing the force of a community of about eight thousand vine-growers.

In the fourteenth century Issoudun housed sixteen to seventeen thousand people, prospering on the wool trade and producing blankets and gloves. By Louis XIV’s time it enjoyed some elegance and social standing. Today the population has shrunk to about twelve thousand, including vine-growers from nearby suburbs. The local economy, especially wine, suffers from outdated practices; vine-growers oppose modernization, producing poor-quality wine that undermines potential prosperity.

This decline stems from a pervasive spirit of stagnation. When a proposal arose for a new road linking Paris and Toulouse through Issoudun, local leaders opposed it, fearing higher food prices. Their resistance exemplifies a broader provincial tendency to favor tradition over progress.

Issoudun’s social fabric has also deteriorated. The disappearance of the nobility after historical upheavals removed a source of social competition and dynamism. The bourgeoisie, preserving routine social forms, have grown complacent, contributing to stagnation in governance and community life. Administration is dominated by intertwined local figures whose familial ties produce ineffectual leadership and weak accountability. The clergy exert little influence, further diminishing civic vitality.

Against this background the town’s youth, feeling trapped, formed the "Knights of Idleness," led by Maxence Gilet. Max, rumored to be the illegitimate son of a local official, was charismatic and adventurous. Raised in poverty and directionless, he enlisted in the military and distinguished himself in campaigns. Returning to Issoudun after the war, he clashed with the bourgeoisie, whose disapproval of his lifestyle and pranks only boosted his notoriety.

Max became a local hero after a violent confrontation with royalist officers, and his leadership of the Knights invigorated the town with mischief. The group staged pranks that both entertained and alarmed townspeople, executing elaborate hoaxes and frightful stunts that revealed their creativity and camaraderie. While disruptive, these antics brought excitement to an otherwise stagnant community.

The local tavern, run by Père and La Cognette, served as the Knights' secret meeting place where they planned escapades and shared meals. The couple, unaware of the full extent of the Knights’ activities, supported them out of self-interest, ensuring their silence and cooperation.

Issoudun’s ancient grandeur and archaeological significance contrast sharply with its modern decline—marked by economic backwardness, social complacency, and weak institutions. The Knights of Idleness, under the magnetic Maxence Gilet, embody the youth’s frustration and thirst for excitement in a town that has lost much of its former vibrancy.

As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a clever prank. An old Spaniard, a former prisoner of war, left his empty cart at the foot of the Issoudun tower one morning. Maxence, arriving at a Knights' rendezvous, was asked, “What are we to do tonight?”

“Here’s Père Fario’s cart,” he replied. “Let’s get it up on the embankment first, and then decide.”

The tower of Issoudun, built by Richard Coeur-de-Lion, stands on the ruins of a basilica, which itself rests above a Roman temple and a Celtic dun — a mound rich with layers of history. Its pedestal towers eighty feet above the town. In an hour the cart was disassembled and hoisted piece by piece to the top of the embankment, like soldiers moving artillery over the Grand Saint-Bernard. Once reassembled, the Knights, now hungry and thirsty, returned to Mère Cognette’s to await the old Spaniard’s astonishment when he came for his cart the next morning.

They did not prank people nightly; sometimes members refused for personal reasons. But they gathered regularly for daytime hunts, autumn vintages, and winter skating. Among the twenty youths some were especially close to Max, who had become their idol. The grandsons of Madame Hochon, François Hochon and Baruch Borniche, regarded Max as a cousin and relied on him for money and forbidden adventures.

That night François and Baruch sat near Max at a dimly lit table after twelve to fifteen bottles of wine. Emboldened, Baruch warned Max, “You are threatened in your stronghold.”

“What do you mean?” Max asked.

“My grandmother received a letter from Madame Bridau, her goddaughter, saying she and her son are coming here. She’s preparing two rooms for them.”

“What’s that to me?” Max replied, drinking.

Max, thirty-four, had a striking, soldierly appearance: a sharply cut face, clear skin, expressive eyes, slightly curly black hair, and a winning smile. He was well proportioned and might have been a general.

“Don’t you know, Max,” called a young man from the other end of the table, “that Madame Hochon’s goddaughter is Rouget’s sister? If she’s coming with her son, she likely wants to secure the property when he dies, and then—goodbye to your harvest!”

Max frowned, assessing the group. “What’s that to me?” he repeated.

“But,” François urged, “if old Rouget revokes his will—”

Max cut him off. “I’ve silenced those who meddled with you, François. Is that how you repay me? By calling a woman I care about a contemptuous name?”

Max had never before spoken so openly about Flore Brazier, Rouget’s servant-mistress, whom society disparaged. He had always avoided discussing her, knowing it was sensitive. His friends had long learned to tread carefully.

François hesitated; Max encouraged him. “Go on.”

“Faith, no!” François replied.

“Don’t be angry, Max,” said Goddet. “Didn’t we agree to speak freely here? If François slipped up, is that a crime against the Order of Idleness?”

“No,” Max conceded, “but it is against our friendship. I remembered we were in session, which is why I said, ‘Go on.’”

An awkward silence followed. Max then declared, “I’ll continue for him—and for all of you—and tell you what you’re thinking. You believe Flore, the Rabouilleuse, has been providing for me since I returned to Issoudun. If I can throw away three hundred francs a month and treat you to dinners, you think it comes from her purse? Yes, ten thousand times yes! Flore aims for Rouget’s property.”

“She gets it from father to son,” Goddet remarked.

“You think I plan to marry Flore when Rouget dies, and that the arrival of these Parisians threatens my future?” Max continued.

“That’s it,” François affirmed.

“Don’t worry, friends,” Max reassured them. “If I need help to deal with these Parisians, will you assist me? Within the limits of our fooleries, of course. I’m not asking you to kill or poison them. If the Bridaus succeed and Flore has nothing but what she stands in, I’d be satisfied. I love her enough to prefer her to Mademoiselle Fichet, if she would have me.”

Mademoiselle Fichet was the wealthiest heiress in Issoudun, courted by Goddet. The Knights, moved by Max’s frankness, rose in unison.

“You’re a fine fellow, Max!”

“We’ll stand by you!”

“A fig for the Bridaus!”

“After all, it’s only three swains to a shepherdess.”

“Père Lousteau loved Madame Rouget; isn’t it better to love a housekeeper who isn’t tied down?”

“If Rouget is Max’s father, the affair is in the family.”

“Liberty of opinion nowadays!”

“Hurrah for Max!”

“Down with all hypocrites!”

“Here’s to the beautiful Flore!”

Their cheers reflected the Knights’ easy morality. Max’s leadership furthered his interests, creating a support network to secure his position. He raised his glass of claret.

“As a mark of the ill-will I bear you, I wish you all a mistress as fine as Flore! As for this influx of relatives, I feel no immediate unease; we’ll see what the future holds—”

“Don’t forget Fario’s cart!”

“That’s safe enough!” Goddet replied.

“I’ll handle that,” Max assured them. “Be in the market early, and let me know when the old fellow comes for his cart.”

At half-past three the Knights slipped away quietly to their homes, moving along the walls in soft shoes. Max returned to the place Saint-Jean, concealing his unease. His time in Cabrera had taught him deep dissimulation. The forty thousand francs a year from Rouget’s property underpinned his affection for Flore. Despite outward confidence, the news of legitimate heirs unsettled him. Rouget’s accumulated savings remained in his name, and even if Flore claimed a will in her favor, those funds could be secured under her name.

“That fool of a girl never mentioned her sister and nephews in all these seven years!” Max exclaimed as he turned from the rue de la Marmouse into the rue l’Avenier. “Seven hundred and fifty thousand francs with various notaries can’t be liquidated quickly without everyone knowing in this gossiping town! The priority is to get rid of these relatives; once they’re gone, we can secure the property. I need to think this through.”

Exhausted, Max entered Père Rouget’s house with a pass-key and went to bed, telling himself, “Tomorrow, my thoughts will be clear.”

Now it’s essential to recount how Flore Brazier acquired the nickname “Rabouilleuse” and became the quasi-mistress of Jean-Jacques Rouget’s household.

As Doctor Rouget aged, he recognized his son’s ineptitude and treated him harshly, trying to impose a routine. This inadvertently prepared Jean-Jacques to submit easily to the first authority he found.

One day in September 1799, returning from his rounds, the doctor encountered a captivating little girl by a brook. She emerged from the water, a vision of rustic beauty, with a tattered petticoat and a makeshift hat. The child, Flore, was catching crabs for her uncle Brazier.

“Where do you come from?” the doctor asked.

“I belong in Vatan,” she replied.

“Why are you here?” he continued.

“I’m catching crabs for my uncle.”

“Has your uncle permission to fish here?” the doctor inquired.

“Hey! Aren’t we all under a Republic that is one and indivisible?” the uncle called from the brook.

Recognizing the child’s striking looks, the doctor offered to take her in, promising a better life. Despite the uncle’s reluctance, he agreed to leave Flore with the doctor, who would pay her wages.

Flore’s life changed as she moved into the doctor’s home, where she was well-fed and educated. She wore fine clothes and received gifts to encourage her studies. Yet her peasant upbringing bred a deep aversion to books; the doctor eventually halted her schooling. The townspeople speculated about the nature of the doctor’s intentions toward Flore, interpreting his care in various ways. Gossip swirled about the propriety of his attention given his age.

For five years the doctor enjoyed the pleasures of educating Flore without complexities. Flore, born in 1787, grew up amid the late 18th-century upheavals that had loosened traditional morals. By 1802 the Catholic Church was only beginning to reestablish itself, and social norms had shifted. Flore was envied by local girls for her privileged life despite the moral implications. Raised during upheaval, she viewed her circumstances through self-interest rather than virtue. The doctor’s affection for her became fodder for gossip that often obscured truth.

As Rouget neared death, he was urged by his notary to make provisions for Flore. His cynical response reflected a man who harbored resentment and sought to take revenge on the innocent object of his unfulfilled desires. His refusal to leave anything to Flore underscored his bitterness, suggesting he believed her beauty would suffice for her future.

Jean-Jacques Rouget did not mourn his father, though Flore Brazier did. The old doctor had made his son unhappy, especially after Jean-Jacques came of age in 1791, but he provided Flore with the material comforts that made country life bearable. After the funeral, when Fanchette asked Flore what would become of her now that Monsieur was dead, Jean-Jacques’s demeanor changed; for the first time his dull face showed animation.

“Leave the room,” he instructed Fanchette, who was clearing the table.

At seventeen, Flore still had the beauty that had attracted the doctor, though she was beginning to show the plumpness common among countrywomen. Jean-Jacques, feeling an urge to speak, hesitated, recalling his father’s recent death and his benefactions. Flore, unable to understand his silence, left him without knowing what to think.

At his father’s death Jean-Jacques was thirty-seven, timid and submissive, resembling a child. His timidity sprang from a torpor of the faculties that a strong influence might have roused. He had a passionate nature, but lack of confidence kept him from courting any woman in Issoudun. His awkwardness and unattractive features made him feel isolated, and he sought solitude where he felt at ease.

The doctor recognized too late the damage his son’s temperament had caused. Wishing to marry Jean-Jacques but fearing loss of control over his property, he searched for a suitable daughter-in-law and tried to instill avarice in his son to give him some financial instinct.

Despite his father’s efforts, Jean-Jacques was secretly in love with Flore, the only woman he could comfortably observe. He delighted in the education his father provided her, believing it would make her easier to win. Flore, unaware of his feelings, waited for him to express himself, but he remained silent.

Three weeks passed in this uncertain situation. One night Flore awoke to find Jean-Jacques crouched outside her door, which led her to believe he loved her. The next day she noticed a change in her feelings toward him, finding him less unattractive.

Their conversations continued, with Jean-Jacques asking if she wanted to return to the fields. Flore, curious about his intentions, assured him of her honesty. These exchanges revealed Jean-Jacques’s growing affection, though he struggled to articulate his feelings.

After ten months Jean-Jacques transformed; his demeanor brightened and he became more attentive to his appearance, much to Flore’s satisfaction. The townspeople noticed and speculated about their relationship and Flore’s intentions.

Fanchette, the cook, was the only one who disapproved of Flore’s position in the household. She eventually left, and Flore, anticipating this, had learned to cook and manage the house. Flore’s culinary skills surpassed those of the former cook, and she took pride in keeping a clean, orderly home.

During the nine years that followed, Flore gained complete control over Jean-Jacques. She treated him with an easy familiarity and a calculated respect, gradually becoming the dominant figure in their relationship. Jean-Jacques, childlike in his dependence, relied on her for everything, and she indulged his whims while shaping their life.

By 1815 Flore had reached the peak of her beauty, resembling a buxom housewife. In 1816 she fell in love with Maxence Gilet, a penniless officer. Her newfound passion altered her relationship with Jean-Jacques, who noticed her changing demeanor.

Flore’s anger and frustration with Jean-Jacques grew as she felt he neglected her needs. She confronted him about his wealth and the empty rooms in their home, suggesting he invite Max to live with them. Jean-Jacques, fearing the loss of Flore, reluctantly agreed.

After Max moved in, Jean-Jacques found comfort in his company; Max provided companionship and the conversation Jean-Jacques had lacked. Flore, however, began to distance herself from household duties, claiming they spoiled her hands. A new cook was employed, and Flore adopted a more elegant appearance to impress Max.

As their household expanded, Flore’s ambitions grew. She bought a horse for Max and welcomed a servant to assist him. The household dynamics shifted: Flore and Max became the central figures while Jean-Jacques receded into the background, increasingly dependent on them.

After Max’s financial aid, Flore improved Rouget’s living conditions and fed him from Vedie’s kitchen. Despite better food, he weakened physically; utterly submissive, his devotion to Flore was his sole motive, making him seem older than his fifty-seven years.

Max’s apartment showed his devotion: provincial comforts, colored tiles, elegant wallpapers and mahogany furniture. A rush matting on the stairs muffled footsteps so Max could come home late without waking anyone — Rouget included, who remained ignorant of Max’s nocturnal activities.

One morning Flore, in a cotton gown, found Max asleep and noticed his late return. He told her a serious matter: Rouget’s family was coming to Issoudun, likely to evict them. Flore vowed confrontation; Max urged caution, asking for coffee and suggesting they plan later. As Flore began making coffee, Baruch announced that Fario was searching for his cart.

Max joined the crowd. Fario, a small peculiar man, claimed his cart had been stolen. Max led him to the tower embankment where the cart was inexplicably found at the top. Amid the crowd’s laughter, Max pretended to assist, theatrically tossing the cart down to Fario’s dismay.

The incident became Issoudun gossip: Fario’s anger and Max’s antics drew attention. Max grasped the implications and felt the need to manage the fallout, especially with the Bridaus’ arrival.

François informed Max that the Bridaus would arrive that night. Max decided they should not appear hostile; he proposed letting the Hochons drive them out instead. Meanwhile Flore, unable to contain her anger, punished Rouget by withdrawing her affection, leaving him confused and distressed.

Flore’s harshness struck Rouget hard; he depended on her emotionally. He asked what he had done, while Vedie urged him to placate Flore. Flore’s fury sprang from learning Rouget’s sister and nephew were coming, whom she saw as threats.

Rouget, bewildered, denied inviting them and blamed Madame Hochon for the visit. Max arrived and argued Rouget should welcome his kin, since public perception mattered. Flore, mollified, prepared breakfast. Rouget thanked Max, who counseled him to keep his will while being polite to his sister.

After breakfast Max and Flore discussed finances and plans to secure Rouget’s wealth while handling the incoming family. Flore admired Max’s cleverness; they plotted so the Hochons would help deal with the Bridaus.

The Hochon household, opposite Rouget’s, was similarly modest. Monsieur Hochon, a retired tax collector, was famed for extreme frugality. His wife, Madame Hochon, resolved to properly receive her goddaughter Agathe and prepared the house despite her husband’s reluctance.

Madame Hochon cleaned and borrowed furniture, hoping to shield Agathe from Max and Flore’s influence. She prayed for Rouget’s awareness and Agathe’s success. Her granddaughter Adolphine secretly hoped to win Joseph Bridau’s affection.

News of the Bridaus’ arrival split Issoudun into factions: the older bourgeoisie, who offered measured support from a distance, and the Knights of Idleness, more vehemently loyal to Max. The tension around the Bridaus foreshadowed a clash of interests in the small town.

Throughout, Max maneuvered socially and politically to protect Flore and Rouget’s position, exploiting public spectacle and allies. Flore used affection and withdrawal to control Rouget; the Hochons prepared to receive the intruding relatives as a counterweight. The Fario incident and the town’s divided sentiment underscored how fragile reputations and power were in Issoudun, and how a few calculated actions could shape the looming confrontation over inheritance and influence.

Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales in Issoudun at three o’clock. Though tired, Madame Bridau felt revived by familiar sights and memories of youth. News of their arrival spread quickly; Madame Hochon greeted Agathe warmly, treating her like a daughter after years of loss and monotony had made her cling to that affection.

Agathe met Monsieur Hochon, as uninviting as ever, and the rest of the family: the young men Baruch and François, and shy little Adolphine, who noticed Joseph’s presence. The miserly Hochon observed Joseph’s recent hospital stay and apparent hunger; the youths were intrigued by his looks. “This is my son, the painter; my good Joseph,” Agathe said with maternal pride. Joseph, blunt as usual, remarked on his likeness to his father, which Madame Hochon took as a sign of his affection for Agathe. Baruch hurried off to spread the news, concentrating on Joseph’s striking appearance.

Townspeople soon gossiped, likening Joseph to a brigand and airing their disdain for artists. Their curiosity about the Parisians grew; many planned to visit the Hochons that evening to see them for themselves.

Settled into modest attic rooms, Joseph contrasted his artistic life in Paris with the drab provincial existence. Dinner revealed the Hochons’ frugality: plain bouilli, hard-boiled eggs, and meager sides. Accustomed to better fare, Joseph found the meal disappointing but tried to adapt.

Agathe learned her childhood home had been sold to the Borniches, and Madame Hochon braced for many visitors that night. Joseph observed the family’s strict ways and the limits of their hospitality.

After dinner the women gathered in the salon. Agathe and Madame Hochon exchanged life stories: Agathe spoke of her struggles and Madame Hochon offered hard-won wisdom about enduring trials. Their conversation was interrupted by a stream of callers eager to meet the Parisians. Joseph remained detached, studying the visitors with a critical eye that made them uneasy. Later, as the house quieted, Agathe and Madame Hochon resumed their heartfelt exchange, reflecting on the burdens they carried.

Elsewhere in town, the Knights of Idleness convened to plot mischief against the Parisians. Max, their leader, proposed a prank involving rats to sabotage the grain supply of a disliked merchant, Fario. The gang welcomed the scheme and sought a way to drive the Bridaus away without harming the Hochons.

The next morning, at breakfast, Joseph glimpsed his brother Jean-Jacques Rouget, unrecognizable and disheveled, accompanied by Flore Brazier, a striking and conspicuous woman. Joseph’s artistic eye admired Flore’s beauty, but Agathe feared her influence over Jean-Jacques. The family debated how to approach him; Joseph suggested he visit first to assess matters.

During breakfast a letter arrived from Jean-Jacques inviting Joseph to dine with him. The Hochons received the invitation with mixed feelings, suspecting Flore’s involvement and worrying over motives. They discussed strategies for handling the tangled family dynamics.

Gritte, the servant, later reported the Rouget household’s ostentation and Flore’s extravagant display. The Hochons realized the need to dress suitably for the occasion, and Joseph prepared to meet his uncle, hoping to unearth the truth about the family’s affairs.

Monsieur Hochon advised Joseph to be observant and to speak frankly with Jean-Jacques about his nephews, stressing the importance of understanding the situations with Max and Flore. With tensions simmering and rival schemes in motion, the stage was set for a complex family reunion that promised confrontation and revelations.

At four o’clock Joseph crossed from the Rouget to the Hochon house, where Kouski announced him. The table was set in the large hall; Joseph greeted his uncle, Flore, and Max.

“We haven’t seen each other since I was born, dear uncle,” Joseph said cheerfully.

“You are very welcome,” Rouget replied, looking at him dully.

“Madame,” Joseph told Flore, “I envied my uncle the pleasure of admiring you every day.”

“Isn’t she beautiful?” Rouget remarked.

“Beautiful enough to be a model for a great painter,” Joseph replied.

Prompted by Flore, Rouget introduced Maxence Gilet, who had served in the Imperial Guard like Joseph’s brother. Joseph noted Max’s handsome features and thought the household formed a triangle: Rouget dull, Flore compensating, Max the attractive outsider.

Baruch and Francois entered. Flore asked Joseph if he had seen the tower of Issoudun; when he had not she suggested a walk before dinner. While she prepared, Joseph eagerly examined the pictures in the house. Rouget said they came from the Descoings, who had bought them during the Revolution. Joseph admired them openly.

“There are more upstairs,” Max said, and took Joseph to see them. Back downstairs ecstatic, Joseph heard Flore whisper to Rouget, urging him to give the paintings to Joseph since he “didn’t care for them.”

“It seems you are a painter,” Rouget said.

“Only a ‘rapin,’” Joseph replied.

“If these pictures can help you, I give them to you, but without the frames,” Rouget said.

“Uncle, I’ll make you copies!” Joseph exclaimed.

Flore suggested paying Joseph four thousand francs for the copies; Rouget agreed. Overwhelmed, Joseph accepted.

As they left, Joseph felt triumphant; the news spread through Issoudun, enhancing Flore’s status. Dinner that night was splendid, attended by local dignitaries. Joseph, somewhat tipsy, returned home at eleven, while Rouget was carried to bed, having indulged heavily.

Max, alone with Flore, observed it was better to befriend the Bridaus than antagonize them. They planned to send the pictures to Joseph the next day.

The following morning Joseph awoke late to find the pictures leaning against the wall. His mother, Agathe, visited Rouget to thank him, encouraged by old Hochon, who warned her, “Your adversaries are cunning. Joseph has been fooled.” Hochon advised Agathe to cultivate a relationship with Flore to gain access to Rouget. She was warmly received by her brother, who was ill from the previous night’s excesses. Flore feigned servitude; Agathe expressed gratitude. “Your brother should marry her,” Agathe suggested, but Flore insisted Rouget would remember her in his will.

In the next days the Knights of Idleness captured rats and mice to release in Fario’s grain store, causing havoc. Agathe believed Rouget had not made a will and intended to ask him about his intentions regarding Flore. Meanwhile the Knights sought ways to drive the Parisians away.

After a week the Bridaus had made little progress. Hochon urged Agathe to stay longer, insisting religious influence was needed to counter Max and Flore’s hold over Rouget. Agathe resisted using religion as manipulation, but Hochon pressed that it was necessary.

Joseph, recognizing the paintings’ value, sent them to Desroches, believing they were worth much more than he had been given. “Don’t disclose their value,” Agathe warned, fearing attention. But news of the paintings’ worth spread, prompting searches for old artworks in Issoudun. Max regretted his actions, fearing the Church’s influence over Rouget.

Flore, following Max’s lead, claimed Rouget was too weak for walks and needed a carriage. They bought a shabby vehicle, which drew attention in Issoudun. Rouget signed a power of attorney giving Max authority to manage his investments, while Flore handled local transactions.

Fario returned to find his grain store devastated by rats and pigeons, suspecting Max’s involvement. Driven by vengeance, Fario began gathering evidence against the Knights of Idleness.

Meanwhile Joseph received letters from Schinner and Desroches. Desroches advised him to keep his mother in Issoudun and warned him to be cautious of Max, suggesting Rouget might have a will and that the Bridaus’ inheritance depended on the larger struggle between Flore and the Church.

Joseph decided to return to Paris, saying goodbye to Rouget, who seemed altered. Max and Flore exchanged glances, sensing Joseph’s departure fit their plans. Irritated by talk of the paintings’ worth and the manipulation, Joseph resolved to return them to Rouget, asserting he would succeed on his own rather than profit by intrigue.

The Hochon family, witnessing Joseph’s agitation, speculated about what had happened. Hochon noted that Joseph had been manipulated by Max and Flore, who had known the paintings’ value all along. Agathe defended Joseph’s decision to return the paintings, but Hochon warned that doing so might jeopardize their chances of inheritance.

Between midnight and one, the Knights of Idleness distributed food to the town's dogs. Their expedition lasted until three, after which they went to Cognette’s for supper. At half-past four, as Max turned from rue l’Avenier into Grande rue, Fario, hiding in a recess, stabbed him and fled toward Vilatte, wiping the knife on his handkerchief. Fario washed the handkerchief in the Rivière forcée and returned to his lodgings at Saint-Paterne, where his new watchman later found him in bed.

Max fell, crying out; Lousteau-Prangin and young Goddet rushed up, shouting for help, but the townspeople, used to false alarms, stayed in bed. When they reached Max he had fainted. He recognized Fario but, on regaining consciousness and finding the wound not fatal, decided to exploit the situation, exclaiming, “I think I recognized that cursed painter!”

Lousteau-Prangin hurried to inform his father, the judge. Max was carried home by Cognette, Goddet, and others, while Goddet’s suspicions were piqued by Max’s words. He asked Gritte if Joseph Bridau was in bed; she replied that he had left at half-past four, restless all night. The revelation shocked the group, and they entered Rouget’s house, where they found Max in a bloody shirt.

Joseph had spent the night anxious about the townspeople’s opinion, fearing they would think he had robbed his uncle. He wished he could escape to Paris and confront Max. At dawn he walked to the poplar avenue leading to Tivoli to calm his nerves.

Meanwhile Max plotted revenge. After Goddet examined the wound and found it serious but not fatal, he left, and news of Max’s condition spread, drawing a crowd to the place Saint-Jean. Max whispered to Flore that they could use the incident to rid themselves of the Parisians, suggesting they pretend he was dying to have Joseph arrested.

Flore, feigning tears, spread the story that Joseph had attacked Max out of jealousy over paintings. The crowd, incited by her, demanded to find Joseph, who had left Hochon’s house at dawn. The situation escalated and the mob grew increasingly hostile.

Monsieur Hochon, alerted by Gritte about the tumult, locked himself inside his house, fearing for his safety and wealth. He learned Joseph had left early, and the news of Max’s attack was causing chaos. Madame Hochon, concerned for her daughter Agathe, wished she would sleep through the shock.

Agathe, however, woke and learned of the accusations, fainting at the sight of the gendarmes. Monsieur Hochon, fearing for the family, urged the magistrates to act quickly. The lieutenant of gendarmes reported a large crowd was gathering, demanding justice for Max.

As the situation intensified, Joseph was spotted returning and was nearly seized by the mob. Gendarmes intervened, protecting him from the angry townspeople who believed he was the murderer. Despite his protests of innocence, Joseph was taken to the Palais for safety, as the crowd threatened violence.

At the Palais Joseph insisted on his innocence and provided an alibi, detailing his movements that morning. The magistrates were somewhat swayed by his calm demeanor and logical explanation, but decided he should remain in custody while they verified his claims.

Joseph wrote to his mother, reassuring her he would soon be free. Meanwhile Max, recovering, remembered a detail that could exonerate Joseph: the attacker had worn list shoes, while Joseph had left in boots. Max wrote to the magistrate urging Joseph’s release.

As the investigation progressed, witnesses confirmed Joseph’s alibi, leading to his release. He returned home to his mother’s warm welcome; she was relieved and their bond strengthened, and Joseph felt grateful for the support he received.

In the aftermath, the townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians, viewing it as a victory. Max and Flore, however, were pleased with the outcome, having successfully rid themselves of what they saw as disruptive outsiders.

Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris, where Joseph’s spirits improved despite the recent turmoil. Agathe remained anxious about her son Philippe, whose trial was approaching. To escape the stress, Joseph travelled to the château of the Comte de Sérizy to focus on his work.

Philippe, meanwhile, faced consequences for actions during the political upheaval. He was sentenced to five years’ surveillance and exiled to Autun. Desroches, a family friend, intervened, securing a change of residence to Issoudun and urging him to reclaim his inheritance from their uncle Rouget.

Motivated to restore the family’s fortunes, Philippe prepared to return to Issoudun. Desroches advised caution and strategy in dealing with Max and the Rabouilleuse, who threatened their plans. Eager to act, Philippe resolved to confront the situation head-on. After a brief dinner he departed for Issoudun, ready to reclaim his family’s legacy and face the challenges ahead.

Tensions rose as Philippe’s resolve to protect his family and restore their honor hardened, preparing the ground for a dramatic showdown in the small town of Issoudun.

Mignonnet then toasted those who had tried to restore the Emperor’s son, and Max rose to make an ironic wish that everyone meet again next year. Tension spread as Max’s reputation made his words heavy with meaning. Philippe and Max exchanged barbs; Philippe remained calm while Max struggled to keep his composure. The meal continued tensely until dessert, and the duel was set for the next morning with sabres; Goddet, a former army surgeon, was to attend.

That night Monsieur and Madame Hochon visited Philippe to urge him to pray before the duel. Madame Hochon offered a relic for protection, which he lightly declined, though she insisted on praying for him. The next morning Max arrived at the duel site with his seconds and prepared for combat. Both men tried to appear composed, but Max felt intimidated by Philippe’s superior stance.

When the duel began Max launched an aggressive move but was quickly disarmed by Philippe, who paused to let Max recover his weapon. This courtesy increased the tension. When fighting resumed they exchanged fierce blows; Philippe was wounded but landed a fatal stroke. Max died, ending his ambitions and the Order of Idleness, much to the town’s relief.

Flore Brazier, devastated by Max’s death, fell ill with fever and inflammation. Philippe, recovering from his wounds, wrote letters outlining plans to secure his inheritance and manage Flore’s situation. He observed that Flore’s illness prevented her from fleeing and intended to use her to his advantage.

By February 1823 Flore, urged by those around her and by Philippe’s suggestions that marriage would secure her future, began to consider marrying Rouget. Philippe manipulated the situation, presenting himself as supportive while secretly arranging to benefit from the match. Flore, recovering, was persuaded to accept Philippe’s proposal, which he framed as a path to respectability.

The marriage contract gave Flore a substantial dower and a life annuity. Agathe, Philippe’s mother, delighted in her son’s apparent reform and the town’s admiration. But Desroches, the family lawyer, warned Agathe that Philippe’s cunning would likely prevent her and Joseph from inheriting Rouget’s property.

Once married to Flore, Philippe asserted control over her and the household, demanding a power of attorney from Rouget. He planned to use Flore to manage his uncle’s affairs while keeping a close watch on her. His ambitions extended beyond Issoudun: he sought to reestablish himself in the army and secure a future in Paris.

In a letter to the minister of war Philippe professed loyalty to the new regime and requested reinstatement in the royal army, enclosing endorsements from local officials praising his conduct. These references ultimately led to his reinstatement as lieutenant-colonel.

Lieutenant-Colonel Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, whom he took to the Treasury to transfer his uncle's income to himself. The old man and the Rabouilleuse were soon swallowed by Parisian excess; Rouget indulged and died after a lavish supper, his death blamed on indigestion with Lolotte, a ballet dancer, implicated.

Madame Rouget prospered in the new surroundings, but Philippe kept her under watch through Mariette to prevent serious mistakes. In October 1823 Philippe settled his uncle's estate at Issoudun and quickly amassed a fortune of sixteen hundred thousand francs, not counting valuable paintings retained by Monsieur Hochon. He entrusted the money to Mongenod and Sons for investment.

Philippe married, legally protecting his wife's fortune should she die childless. He bought her a furnished apartment and secretly acquired a grand hôtel on rue de Clichy, lavishly renovating and furnishing it. The restoration of Charles X benefited the Duc de Chaulieu’s circle; because Philippe supplied information about past conspiracies he was appointed lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse’s regiment. Court connections brought promotion and ennoblement—he became Comte de Brambourg.

Ambitious and insatiable, Philippe sought more honors, aiming for an aide-de-camp post to the Dauphin. He severed ties with his past, shunning old friends who might endanger his standing. He planned to marry Mademoiselle Amelie de Soulanges and to accumulate military distinctions to propel himself toward a general’s rank.

Meanwhile Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist, sustained by his mother Agathe, who ran a lottery office. Despite talent, Joseph faced poverty and criticism while Philippe’s success overshadowed him. Agathe, misled by appearances, convinced herself Philippe’s rise excused his neglect.

Desperate, Agathe wrote to Philippe asking for financial help for Joseph. His cold reply crushed her and hastened her decline. On her deathbed she sought consolation from her confessor, who revealed the painful contrast between her sons—one ambitious and callous, the other neglected and devoted—deepening her sorrow. She died soon after.

Joseph, heartbroken, buried his mother alone. Philippe, indifferent to her death, was repelled by Joseph’s bitter letter. Bereft, Joseph found solace among friends who supported him through grief.

Later Joseph received a desperate letter from Flore, Philippe’s wife, pleading for help amid destitution. Moved, Joseph and Bixiou visited and found a shadow of her former self. They arranged medical care, revealing the depth of Philippe’s cruelty and neglect. As Flore’s condition worsened, Joseph and his friends plotted to expose Philippe’s abandonment and secure justice for her.

Philippe continued to climb socially, blind to the wreckage he left behind, but his fortunes collapsed in the July Revolution of 1830, precipitating his downfall. Ultimately Joseph inherited Philippe’s estate and achieved success in his art, becoming Comte de Brambourg; despite his honor and prosperity, he remained humble and reflective about his family’s tragic fate and the lessons learned.
