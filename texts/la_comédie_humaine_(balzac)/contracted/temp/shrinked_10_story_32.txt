Dedication  
To Monsieur Charles Nodier, a member of the French Academy. This book examines the hidden injustices of domestic life, where chance often replaces justice, reflecting on the decline of paternal authority and the consequences of feminine weakness, urging a return to the moral foundations of marriage and family.

---

Chapter I  
In 1792 the townspeople of Issoudun suspected Dr. Rouget of malice, blaming him for the unhappiness of his beautiful wife, Madame Rouget. Their son Jean-Jacques was followed ten years later by a daughter, Agathe, whose birth strained the family. The Rougets were linked to the prosperous Descoings family, wool merchants whose fortunes and misfortunes affected the town. One of Descoings’ brothers sought his luck in Paris but failed. Descoings himself was imprisoned during the Revolution and eventually executed, a shock that deepened Madame Rouget’s decline as her husband grew neglectful and their son indifferent.

Agathe was sent to Paris to live with her uncle, but his imprisonment at the same time left her destitute and separated from her mother, who fell ill fearing she would never see her child again. Agathe’s beauty attracted Bridau, a ministry secretary, and she married him with Dr. Rouget’s approval. After inheriting the Descoings fortune, Dr. Rouget led a debauched life following his wife’s death in 1799, leaving Jean-Jacques ill-prepared for adulthood.

Agathe resembled her mother in beauty and devotedness but lacked worldly knowledge, which brought misfortunes. Bridau, loyal to Napoleon, worked tirelessly in the ministry until his death in 1808, just before a significant promotion. Agathe then received a pension and scholarships for her sons but remained distant from her Issoudun relations. Despite warnings from her godmother, Madame Hochon, Agathe did not contest her father’s will, which deprived her of an inheritance; instead she and Madame Descoings pooled resources and lived modestly.

Madame Descoings, sixty-five yet appearing younger, tried to cheer Agathe and often took her to the theater, while secretly raising her grandson Bixiou. Agathe, faithful to her children, refused to remarry, but her lack of authority as a mother caused problems. Madame Descoings’ gambling worsened their finances, as she used household funds to feed a lottery habit. Unaware of the full extent, Agathe trusted her aunt until debts mounted. Madame Descoings eventually confessed, and Agathe sold possessions and cut expenses to pay creditors.

This background frames a drama of family ties, financial strain, and choices shaped by social expectations, exploring love, loyalty, and hidden burdens in domestic life.

In Paris, the rue Mazarin, between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, is a dreary quarter dominated by Cardinal Mazarin’s college and library. There, Madame Bridau, a widow, lives on the third floor of a shabby building opposite the Institute, which trains aspiring artists known as “rapins” who compete for prestigious prizes.

Madame Bridau’s modest apartment reflects her frugality. Since her husband’s death, she has grown disheveled and consumed by grief, neglecting appearance and home. Financial worries about her children Philippe and Joseph haunt her despite some imperial support. Her aunt Madame Descoings also struggles but helps where she can; the two widows share a close bond and social evenings playing cards with ministry friends. Madame Descoings dotes on Philippe and Joseph, while Madame Bridau lives sparingly to save for their futures.

Philippe, the elder, resembles his mother and has a bold, physical temperament, preferring action to study. Joseph is introverted and artistic, careless with cleanliness. Both are loved, though Madame Bridau favors Philippe somewhat. Joseph’s interest in art is sparked by seeing a student sketch at the Institute; he is drawn into the artistic world and visits sculptor Chaudet’s studio, where his talent is noticed. Madame Bridau, fearing the hardships of an artist’s life, is distressed by his aspirations, but Madame Descoings encourages Joseph.

Meanwhile Philippe, inspired by military splendor, secretly petitions the Emperor and is accepted into the Imperial Lyceum; his boldness soon propels him into rapid advancement. As Napoleon returns from exile and political changes ripple through society, both brothers encounter difficulties: Joseph loses his scholarship and resolves to make a living by art, while Philippe, now a captain, comes home to find the family in dire financial straits. Their divergent paths underscore different temperaments and the complexities of maternal affection, setting the scene for the struggles each will face.

In 1816 Joseph Bridau set up an art studio in his attic with moral encouragement from his mother Agathe and financial backing from Madame Descoings; both widows considered painting a respectable profession. Joseph studied under Gros and Schinner while political unrest in France produced conspiracies and riots. His brother Philippe became involved, was arrested and released, but his military prospects were damaged when he was returned to active duty.

Agathe fretted over Philippe’s safety and company—he frequented disreputable cafés—while acquaintances advised sending him abroad. Philippe joined General Lallemand in the United States and took part in the ill-fated Champ d’Asile venture. Agathe invested ten thousand francs to support him. When Philippe returned in 1819 he was altered—hardened, alcoholic, and selfish after his experiences.

Trying to help, Agathe bought him new clothes and indulged his drinking despite misgivings about his character. His tales of misfortune masked a growing selfishness. Back in Paris his conduct alarmed Agathe and Madame Descoings; they faced their own financial strains. Agathe suggested selling furniture to support him, but Philippe continued gambling and drinking, worsening their situation.

Philippe’s social life revolved around old army comrades and gambling; he became reckless. He bonded with Giroudeau, who introduced him to Paris nightlife. Philippe shifted ambitions from political influence to pursuing Mariette, a young dancer, even as he schemed to exploit his past—planning to demand accountability for the funds raised for Champ d’Asile. Agathe and Madame Descoings secured him a cashier position at a new newspaper, hoping it would stabilize him financially, yet he lived extravagantly without supporting them while Mariette gained fame through his connections.

Bixiou informed Agathe and Madame Descoings of Philippe’s infatuation with Mariette, shocking them as they viewed stage women as morally suspect. Philippe proposed marriage; she declined, looking for wealthier prospects. Distancing himself from family, Philippe indulged in a hedonistic lifestyle that led to embezzlement from his job.

After Mariette left for London, Philippe returned home despairing over his finances and confessed to Joseph that his embezzlement had driven him to suicidal thoughts. Joseph urged him to reconsider and suggested repayment to avert disgrace. Philippe fled, leaving his family distraught. Agathe and Madame Descoings feared for his life.

Visiting a gambling house, Philippe initially won but ultimately lost everything. He came home feigning distress; Agathe begged him not to harm himself. In the following days Agathe aged visibly from worry as their finances deteriorated. Madame Descoings suggested consulting a lawyer, Desroches, to manage the fallout. Desroches devised a plan to protect Philippe’s reputation, framing the theft as resulting from misplaced trust and helping temporarily shield the family.

Philippe resumed a life of leisure while Joseph focused on his art, gaining recognition. Agathe remained tormented by Philippe’s condition, vacillating between hope and dread. Desroches promised Philippe might be reinstated in the army but warned that his gambling could ruin that chance. As Philippe continued recklessly, Joseph suspected him of stealing and resolved to confront him. Philippe’s charm, however, let him evade accountability, deepening the family’s struggles and leaving Joseph anxious about their future.

Despite precautions, Philippe was occasionally “cleaned out.” Desperate for his nightly stake, he stole from his family, including Agathe and Madame Descoings. Heartbroken, Agathe recognized these thefts as signs of deeper moral decay. After a dream in which Philippe robbed her, she implored him to ask for money rather than steal, but within two weeks she was left penniless and contemplated working to support herself.

As Agathe weakened from worry, Philippe sank further into debauchery and neglected his appearance. Madame Descoings urged him to buy new clothes; he dismissed her, claiming poverty. He had even mortgaged his military pension to cover gambling debts.

On Christmas Eve Philippe planned to steal from Madame Descoings, who had saved money for lottery tickets. He broke into her apartment while she was out, found twenty napoleons hidden in her mattress, and took them. He gambled that night, experiencing a streak of luck and winning substantial sums, but greed led him to abandon his own gambling rules and he lost everything.

Agathe and Madame Descoings awaited Philippe for Christmas dinner. He arrived drunk and tensions erupted as Agathe reproached him. Philippe, enraged, dismissed their concerns even as Madame Descoings suffered the shock of losing her savings.

The next day Madame Descoings learned her lottery ticket had won three million francs, but discovered Philippe had stolen the money before she could pay for her stake. The revelation caused her to have a stroke and become incapacitated. Agathe and Joseph were left to cope with the ruin Philippe had wrought.

While Madame Descoings lay ill, Philippe returned home, oblivious to the chaos he caused. Agathe confronted him about the theft and the family’s pain. Philippe remained indifferent, convinced he was justified; the confrontation escalated into violence, and he was restrained in delirium.

Madame Descoings later died, leaving Agathe a meager annuity. Philippe, estranged, continued his reckless lifestyle without remorse. Agathe, heartbroken and defeated, continued to pray for her son, realizing that motherhood had become a source of profound sorrow.

By February 1822 Agathe had moved to a modest attic above her former apartment; Joseph aimed to make her comfortable after Philippe’s departure. Joseph decorated the room and took Agathe to table-d’hôte dinners, which cost nearly a hundred francs a month. Worried about expenses, Agathe secured a job as an assistant in a lottery office, earning seven hundred francs a year and working long hours.

Despite the labor, Agathe found solace in routine. She prepared Joseph’s breakfast and kept his studio tidy, though she felt out of place in artistic discussions. Their bond deepened through small acts of care even as they remained distant about art. When Agathe expressed concern for Philippe, Joseph visited him. Philippe, now a newspaper cashier, invited Joseph to dinner; Joseph declined to prioritize his mother.

As Philippe sat for a portrait, Agathe watched secretly, sensing a fragile hope for family repair. But Joseph grew suspicious about Philippe’s finances after a lavish dinner and evasive talk. During another sitting Philippe boasted of ambitions but was vague about money.

One evening Philippe stole a valuable painting from Joseph’s studio, thinking it a copy. When Joseph discovered the theft he was devastated; Agathe was heartbroken, realizing the depth of Philippe’s betrayal. Her despair deepened when she learned Philippe needed medical care. Despite Giroudeau’s pleas for financial help, Agathe refused to aid Philippe further, feeling betrayed and exhausted.

Months later Agathe encountered Philippe on the street, a shadow of his former self, recently discharged from a hospital. Maternal instinct overcame her; she gave him money despite knowing it would likely be wasted. Her health declined as she wrestled with the pain of her son’s ruin.

When news arrived of Philippe’s involvement in a conspiracy, Agathe fainted in fear. Joseph sought Desroches’s legal advice; Desroches urged protecting the family inheritance from Philippe’s influence over their wealthy uncle, Jean-Jacques Rouget. Agathe wrote to her godmother, Madame Hochon, seeking help but learned Rouget was unlikely to assist. Desroches advised Agathe to travel to Issoudun to protect her interests and prompted reflections on how Issoudun had changed and how estranged she had become from her brother.

Issoudun, one of France’s oldest cities, has a long history and once-thriving commerce, evidenced by archaeological finds like a fifth-century basilica built over a Roman temple. Its name, deriving from “Dun,” suggests Celtic origins and continuous settlement. Historically a wool-trade center, Issoudun had about 16,000 inhabitants in the fourteenth century but has declined to roughly 12,000 today, with surrounding vine-growers contributing to its population.

This decline stems largely from resistance to change, particularly in viticulture where outdated methods hinder wine quality. The local bourgeoisie clings to medieval traditions and resisted military presence that might have revived the economy, reflecting broader regional inertia seen in towns like Bourges.

Issoudun’s topography—canal and fertile meadows—could support prosperity, but its social fabric has frayed. The absence of nobility and military presence has flattened social distinctions that once energized community life. A local government populated by familiar, complacent faces has become ineffective, further entrenching the town’s stagnation.

In the early 19th century a group of local youths, the “Knights of Idleness,” led by Maxence Gilet, a charismatic and controversial figure, terrorized the town with pranks. Rumored to be the illegitimate son of an official, Max endeared himself to common folk despite bourgeois disdain. Under his direction the Knights executed elaborate mischief that humiliated authorities and provoked civic disorder, even forcing the sub-prefect’s resignation. This episode illustrates how social stagnation and youthful rebellion interplay in Issoudun’s decline, showing how the past shapes the town’s present dynamics.

################################################
As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a prank on Père Fario by hoisting his cart onto an embankment, then returned to Mère Cognette’s for food and laughter, eager to see Fario’s reaction.

The Knights enjoyed leisure, and Maxence Gilet (Max) was especially admired by Madame Hochon’s grandsons, François and Baruch, who relied on him financially. Baruch warned Max that Madame Bridau and her son’s arrival could threaten Max’s interest in Jean-Jacques Rouget’s property; Rouget, an old bachelor, was entangled with Flore Brazier, his servant-mistress. Though Max initially dismissed the warning, he grew uneasy because Rouget’s wealth effectively rested on Flore, and legitimate heirs might upset Max’s plans.

François speculated that if Rouget altered his will in Flore’s favor it could imperil Max. Max, confident in his hold over Flore, proposed that the Knights back him in managing the Bridaus—not by harming them but by securing his position. The Knights pledged loyalty, toasting Flore and dismissing the Bridaus, reflecting their lax morals. Max intended to cultivate their allegiance as future leverage to reclaim his status.

As night ended, Max weighed the Bridaus’ arrival. Flore had not mentioned her family, a silence that threatened their financial future. Max’s affection for Flore mingled with interest in her possible inheritance from Rouget, pushing him to act before the Bridaus could assert a claim.

Flore’s past: Doctor Rouget found her as a child and took her in because of her beauty and innocence. Under his care she received an education and luxuries that set her apart in Issoudun. Despite gossip about their relationship, Flore thrived; Rouget, however, nearing death, refused to bequeath her anything, believing her beauty would secure her future.

Jean-Jacques Rouget, thirty-seven, was timid and socially awkward, especially around women. He did not mourn his father as Flore did, having benefited materially from him. Rouget’s father had hoped to marry him off but feared a wife’s influence and instilled avarice in Jean-Jacques. Jean-Jacques secretly loved Flore—the only woman in his life.

Flore began to see Rouget differently after noting his silence and devotion. Their relationship evolved; he expressed his desire for her to stay, and she, intrigued by his affection, found him less unattractive. Over time Flore took charge of the household, surpassing Rouget in management and influence. Her beauty attracted attention, and she eventually fell for Maxence Gilet, straining her bond with Rouget.

Flore’s passion for Max caused tension: she accused Rouget of stinginess while Rouget, reluctant, agreed to let Max move in, hoping to keep Flore’s affection. Max’s presence shifted the dynamics, complicating love, power, and dependency among the three.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to safeguard her inheritance, Rouget had slipped into a semi-vegetative state, animated mainly by his affection for Flore. One morning Flore found Max asleep after a late night and learned Rouget’s family was coming to claim their inheritance. Max advised Flore to act unaware.

A commotion erupted when Fario reported his cart missing. Max feigned indifference but gathered a crowd, joking that the cart had ascended the tower, and the town laughed at Fario’s expense. Max and his companions eventually destroyed the cart, further straining his situation with Flore.

As tensions rose, François told Max the Bridaus were imminent. Max chose manipulation over confrontation. Flore, distressed, took out her frustration on Rouget, withholding affection. Max counseled Rouget to welcome his family to avoid scandal, stressing appearances.

After breakfast, Max and Flore discussed financial strategies to secure Rouget’s wealth and keep the Bridaus from interfering. Meanwhile, the Hochon household readied for the Bridau visit; Madame Hochon urged civility while Hochon suspected the Bridaus’ motives. News of the Bridaus’ return divided the town, escalating tensions over Rouget’s inheritance and shifting local power.

Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales in Issoudun; Agathe felt nostalgic. Madame Hochon welcomed her warmly, maternal toward Agathe. The family gathered in the salon, scrutinizing Joseph, whose appearance drew unflattering remarks about his health and character.

Joseph noted the stark contrast between his artistic life in Paris and the austere provincial lifestyle. Dinner highlighted the Hochons’ frugality, disheartening him, though Madame Hochon expressed hopes for his success. The evening brimmed with gossip about local elites, especially Maxence Gilet and the influence of the Rabouilleuse (Flore) over Joseph’s uncle, Rouget.

After dinner, the Hochons and the Bridau family plotted reclaiming Rouget’s property from Max and Flore. Joseph doubted their chances against such entrenched opposition. The conversation turned to his upcoming visit to Rouget, where he was urged to make a favorable impression.

The next morning Joseph saw his uncle, transformed under Flore’s influence. Agathe was distressed; Joseph noticed Flore’s beauty despite her role in the family’s troubles. The Hochons advised Joseph to see Rouget alone to discuss family matters without Flore’s interference.

A letter from Rouget inviting Joseph to dinner raised Hochon suspicions about Flore’s manipulations. They crafted a response highlighting Agathe’s struggles and Joseph’s artistic aspirations to secure their standing.

As dinner preparations proceeded, Gritte, the servant, reported on Flore’s extravagant display. The Hochons realized they must proceed cautiously given the situation’s complexity. Joseph was urged to be observant during the visit; the stakes were high.

At four o’clock Joseph arrived at Rouget’s house and met Kouski, Flore, and Gilet. He admired Flore, which pleased Rouget. Flore suggested a walk to see the tower of Issoudun; Joseph eagerly agreed. Exploring Rouget’s home, Joseph discovered notable paintings, and Flore encouraged Rouget to pay Joseph four thousand francs for copies, to his surprise.

News of Joseph’s commission spread, enhancing Flore’s status. Dinner was lavishly attended by local dignitaries, and Joseph left feeling satisfied while Rouget indulged heavily in wine.

Max and Flore plotted to keep the Bridaus at bay while extracting benefit from Rouget’s wealth. The next day Joseph found the paintings moved; Agathe visited Rouget to thank Flore for her care. Old Hochon advised Agathe to cultivate a relationship with Flore, but Agathe struggled to speak privately with her brother.

As the Bridaus sought Rouget’s favor, Joseph received letters from painter Schinner and lawyer Desroches. Frustrated, he decided to return to Paris, bidding farewell to Rouget, who seemed oblivious to the value of his paintings. Feeling insulted by Flore and Max’s manipulation, Joseph returned the paintings, asserting his independence.

The incident became local gossip, with exaggerated tales about Joseph’s departure. Old Hochon warned Agathe that Joseph’s actions might jeopardize their inheritance chances, leaving the Bridaus’ future uncertain amid growing tension with Maxence and Flore.

Between midnight and one, the Knights of Idleness distributed food to the town’s dogs, ending their prank around three a.m. Max was attacked by Fario, who stabbed him before fleeing. Max cried out; Lousteau-Prangin and Goddet hurried to help, but the townspeople, used to false alarms, stayed indifferent. After fainting, Max claimed to recognize Fario, and Lousteau-Prangin informed his father, the judge.

Max was taken home; Goddet judged the wound serious though not fatal. Max seized the chance to frame Joseph Bridau, the painter. He instructed Flore to spread rumors that he was dying, hoping Joseph would be arrested and his mother would flee to Paris. Feigning tears, Flore accused Joseph of wanting Max dead to inherit an uncle’s fortune. The story roused the townspeople, who began searching for Joseph. He had left Hochon’s house at dawn after a sleepless night. Alarmed by the angry crowd, Hochon bolted his door, fearing for his gold. Agathe, Joseph’s mother, fainted at the news.

As the crowd swelled, the police struggled to maintain order. Gendarmes arrived just in time to protect Joseph from the mob, convinced of his guilt. Despite asserting his innocence and giving an alibi verifiable by witnesses, Joseph was taken to the Palais for safety. The magistrates, initially skeptical but persuaded to investigate, detained him for protection. He wrote a calming letter to his mother. Max, recovering, plotted to keep Joseph imprisoned, convinced this would rid them of the Parisians.

The investigation eventually unraveled Max’s deception. Witnesses confirmed Joseph’s alibi, and Max’s own letter to the judge describing the assailant’s shoes implicated him. Joseph was released and warmly greeted by his relieved mother. The townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians as a victory. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris; Joseph’s spirits improved despite his brother Philippe’s ongoing trial for actions during the political unrest. Desroches, a lawyer, sought to help Philippe by arranging for his release and warning him to be wary of Max, who was clever and dangerous. Philippe was ultimately released and prepared to confront Max and reclaim his family’s standing.

On November 2, All-Souls’ Day, Philippe Bridau returned to Issoudun, registered with the local police, and lodged on rue l’Avenier. His arrival as a banished officer linked to a military conspiracy stirred excitement, especially since he was the brother of the wrongfully accused painter. Maxence Gilet, recovered, had converted Pere Rouget’s mortgages into cash, causing concern. Monsieur Hochon asked notary Monsieur Heron about Gilet’s actions, worried for the Bridau family.

Gilet and Flore Brazier felt secure with Philippe’s arrival only insofar as they could manipulate old Rouget for his money. Philippe visited his uncle Rouget in shabby clothing, reflecting hardship but retaining dignity; he refused Rouget’s money because of past grievances. Flore, spotting Philippe’s hostility, advised Rouget to avoid him.

Philippe later called on the Hochons, presenting himself and announcing he intended to live modestly on limited means. He won over Madame Hochon by discussing her nephew, journalist Lousteau, and asked for a private talk with Hochon to avoid gossip, aiming to connect with other former officers opposed to Gilet. He quickly acquired a new wardrobe on credit and befriended Mignonnet and Carpentier, fellow officers who disapproved of Gilet. He shared knowledge of the failed conspiracy against the Bourbons, earning respect while plotting to undermine Gilet and protect his uncle’s fortune.

As Philippe ingratiated himself with the local elite, he maintained a respectable facade in contrast to Gilet’s dubious reputation. He often dined with Mignonnet and Carpentier, solidifying his position. Gilet, meanwhile, saw Philippe as a rival. Philippe learned that Gilet’s associates—including Rouget’s grandsons—were involved in shady dealings and reported this to Hochon, which heightened tensions. He confronted his uncle about Flore’s sway and demanded a private discussion.

Hochon told Philippe that Rouget had not signed any power of attorney, preventing Gilet from accessing his funds. Philippe planned to watch Rouget and Gilet closely to safeguard the family’s interests. Alarmed by Philippe’s boldness, Flore informed Gilet, who plotted to trick Rouget into signing a power of attorney.

Walking with Rouget along boulevard Baron, Philippe considered the impending showdown between Colonel Bridau and Captain Gilet, both contending for Rouget’s favor and money. He expressed contempt for Gilet and hinted he would eliminate him if necessary to secure Rouget’s happiness with Flore. Rouget, however, was easily swayed by Flore’s charms, making Philippe’s task harder. Philippe insisted he would handle Gilet and promised to make Flore obedient to Rouget. They encountered Mignonnet and Carpentier, aware of Rouget’s precarious situation. Philippe concluded that either Gilet must die or he would have to kill Gilet to save his uncle.

Gilet, sensing rising animosity in Issoudun because of his reckless behavior, met Captain Potel, who urged him to confront Bridau to restore honor. But Gilet was preoccupied with securing Rouget’s wealth for himself and Flore. At Rouget’s house, Kouski, acting under Gilet’s orders, pretended sorrow to tell Rouget that Flore had left with Vedie, devastating the old man who searched for her in vain. Gilet tried to keep up his reputation while scheming to keep Rouget under control.

As rumors of Gilet’s misdeeds spread, townspeople turned against him. Potel lamented the division in the Imperial Guard, now riven by Gilet’s actions. Feeling cornered, Gilet resolved to confront Bridau, believing he could outmaneuver him in a duel. Philippe, anticipating Gilet’s move, prepared countermeasures, suspecting further manipulation of Flore and Rouget. When Rouget drafted a letter threatening to revoke his will if Flore did not return, Philippe urged him to send it with Carpentier for personal delivery, hoping to bring her back and regain Rouget’s authority.

That evening Philippe plotted to enforce Flore’s compliance and Rouget’s welfare, planning to take Gilet’s place and promise to keep Flore in line. When Flore returned, Philippe confronted her, asserting control and warning of consequences if she failed Rouget. Word of the impending duel between Philippe and Gilet spread through Issoudun, prompting speculation. Flore, sensing the stakes, grew more attentive to Rouget to secure her position, while Gilet remained defiant. Tensions rose as townspeople rallied behind Philippe, hoping his victory would restore order and honor to the Imperial Guard, setting the stage for a dramatic confrontation that would determine their fates.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The day after the coronation banquet, officers of the old army gathered at Lacroix's restaurant in Issoudun to discuss Max Gilet's dismissal from Rouget's house. Tensions rose when Max arrived, receiving a colder welcome than Colonel Philippe Bridau. During the banquet Philippe and Max exchanged insults, aware of their scheduled duel the next morning. Philippe was visited by Monsieur and Madame Hochon, who urged him to pray and offered a protective relic, which he accepted despite skepticism.

On duel day both men appeared calm. Philippe quickly disarmed Max, but a desperate counterattack produced serious injuries for both. Ultimately Philippe delivered a fatal blow, ending Max's ambitions and the influence of the Order of Idleness in Issoudun.

As Philippe recovered, he plotted to marry Flore Brazier, Max's former lover, to secure Rouget's inheritance. After Max's death Flore fell ill; Philippe framed marriage to restore her respectability. Flore eventually agreed and the contract was signed, securing her a substantial dowry.

Philippe returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, managing the transfer of Rouget's income to himself. He indulged them lavishly, precipitating Rouget's death, attributed to indigestion. In October 1823 Philippe settled his uncle's estate, netting 1.6 million francs, which he invested. By March 1824 he married Madame Rouget, consolidating her fortune.

By January 1827 Philippe had risen to the Royal Guard, distancing himself from his past, while Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist, overshadowed by Philippe's success. After Agathe's death Joseph learned of Philippe's indifference and of Flore's subsequent suffering. Philippe faced scrutiny and financial ruin during the July Revolution of 1830, and ultimately met a tragic end in battle in 1839. Joseph, having married well, inherited Philippe's estate and continued to paint, gaining recognition though never achieving the fame he desired.
