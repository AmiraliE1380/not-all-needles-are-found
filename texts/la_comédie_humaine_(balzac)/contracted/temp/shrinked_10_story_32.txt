DEDICATION

To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy.

Dear Nodier, this book reveals actions hidden behind domestic doors where divine intervention often supplants human justice. Its moral lessons center on family and motherhood and on the harms that follow when paternal authority—the traditional tribunal for domestic wrongs—is weakened. A loving mother cannot replace patriarchal power. The story defends the indissoluble marriage and warns against unchecked selfishness in a society that exalts wealth and success while the law is impotent. I hope such a society will return to Catholic moral guidance through a different education.

In “Scenes from Military Life” I will portray noble self-sacrifice and also expose war’s corrupting influence on those at home living as if on a battlefield. You have observed our times keenly; I entrust this tale to your name as a shield from criticism.

Your sincere admirer,
De Balzac  
Paris, November 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792 the townspeople of Issoudun disliked Dr. Rouget; gossip blamed him for his beautiful wife’s unhappiness, though the household’s true nature remained secret. Madame Rouget, née Descoings, had been frail in youth. They had a son, Jean-Jacques, and, ten years later, a daughter, Agathe.

Rouget’s conviction that men must display strength caused much female suffering. The Descoings family—wealthy wool merchants turned investors in forfeited estates—were rich and miserly. Descoings, Rouget’s brother-in-law, had left Issoudun for Paris, failed as a grocer, and been driven by love for a beautiful widow. By 1792 he seemed prosperous; Rouget, expecting his wife’s death, sent Agathe to Paris to secure Descoings’ favor and a share of the inheritance.

Agathe, the most beautiful girl in Issoudun and like her mother in looks, provoked a quarrel between Rouget and their friend Lousteau, who then left town. Madame Rouget, learning her daughter had gone, predicted she would never see Agathe again—a prophecy that proved true as her health declined, worsened by her son’s neglect; Jean-Jacques offered little comfort.

Agathe’s arrival in Paris coincided with Descoings’ imprisonment for imprudent political remarks during the famine and his subsequent execution alongside the poet André Chénier—an event that silenced local grocers and left a sharper immediate mark on the community than history would later reckon.

Afterward Agathe married Bridau, a ministry secretary, with Rouget’s approval; Rouget had hoped to profit from any Descoings inheritance. Bridau, captivated by Agathe’s beauty, supported Napoleon, won promotion in 1804, worked relentlessly, and in 1808 died of overwork just as another promotion was imminent—an honor the Emperor had meant to confer but belatedly.

Following Bridau’s death, Agathe received no letters from Issoudun. Her mother died shortly before the birth of her second son; her father died around the Emperor’s coronation, an event that distracted Bridau. Jean-Jacques remained absent. Madame Hochon, Agathe’s godmother, warned that Agathe would inherit nothing unless she granted power of attorney to her brother; Agathe hesitated to burden him. The arrangement left them financially fragile. Bridau’s prudent investments had secured only a modest income; after his death Agathe and Madame Descoings pooled resources and lived on a joint twelve thousand francs.

By 1809 Madame Descoings, sixty-five but still enjoying the theater and fine food, secretly gambled on lotteries and concealed mounting debts. She also kept secrets about her son Bixiou, a major whose child had been educated alongside Agathe’s. Devoted to her children and refusing to remarry, Agathe undertook both maternal and household duties. Her limited intelligence and blind trust in Madame Descoings proved disastrous when the lottery debts were revealed; Agathe, without reproach, sold possessions and liquidated investments to settle them—a decisive turning point in their lives.

One of the bleakest stretches of Paris was rue Mazarin between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, shaded by the high gray walls of the college and library given by Cardinal Mazarin. There, on the third floor of a damp, cold house, lived a poor widow, Madame Bridau (Agathe). Opposite were the Institute buildings, where aspiring artists, the rapins, worked amid the tumult of examinations; from her windows Agathe could see their cages and the students’ uproar. To brighten her room she defied police regulations and set boxes of earth on the sill to make little gardens.

The shallow house was reached by a winding staircase and contained three rooms: a dining room, a small salon, and a bedroom, plus a kitchen and two single rooms. Agathe had chosen the flat because it was cheap—four hundred francs a year—near her sons’ school and in a neighborhood she knew. The interior matched the building’s dreariness: yellowed wallpaper, unglazed tiles, and only essential furniture. The salon held an Aubusson carpet given to her and a mahogany sofa bearing a pastel portrait of her late husband, Bridau, which captured his noble essence; a vivid Napoleon hung above a card table.

After Bridau’s death Agathe’s life narrowed. She filled the room with two large birdcages—canaries and Java sparrows—a hobby that left her space littered with millet, chickweed, and cat hair, signs of grief and neglected personal care. She preserved everything belonging to her husband, even wrapping his last pen with a note. Seized by sorrow, she seemed to lose her sense of womanhood and devoted herself entirely to her children.

Agathe lived for her sons and worried ceaselessly over their finances. The Emperor provided three hundred francs a year for each child, and scholarships supplemented this. Her aunt, Madame Descoings, on the second floor, assigned three thousand francs from her annuity to help Agathe; the Descoings themselves now lived modestly on twelve hundred francs a year, employed a woman for morning chores, and shared dinners with the two widows. Madame Descoings doted on Philippe and Joseph, indulging them with treats and small sums. Distrustful of the future, Agathe scrimped and saved—she set aside three thousand francs from her pension—hoping to secure some inheritance for her boys. Thus both widows had passed from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty, each for different reasons.

The house, the students’ uproar, and her husband’s portrait formed the setting that would shape her youngest son, Joseph Bridau, into a notable painter of the modern French school. Philippe, the elder, resembled his mother in appearance but had a bold demeanor often mistaken for courage; Agathe hoped his looks promised similar moral qualities. At fifteen his charm reinforced her hopes. Joseph, three years younger, took after his father in less favorable ways: untidy, often scolded, and plainly less favored by his mother.

Joseph’s artistic bent was sparked by a visit to the Tuileries, where a caricature on an Institute wall captivated him and made him want to draw. He began sneaking into the Institute courtyard, enduring teasing from older students until his persistence won their respect. He later visited the sculptor Chaudet’s atelier; Chaudet recognized and encouraged his talent. Agathe feared art meant hardship and tried to dissuade him, but Chaudet defended the vocation as a divine calling, insisting true talent must be followed.

Meanwhile Philippe, enchanted by military life, secretly petitioned the Emperor to enlist. He was sent to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr and rose rapidly, becoming a captain by nineteen. Joseph, after his scholarship was withdrawn, tried to earn a living by art, encouraged by Madame Descoings. As politics shifted, Philippe returned home a decorated officer while Joseph faced an uncertain career. Agathe’s preference for Philippe became more apparent as she admired his success; yet Joseph remained devoted to his mother. Despite divergent paths, both brothers pursued their callings, with Agathe’s constant love and anxiety marking every step of their futures.

In 1816 Joseph won his mother’s consent to convert the adjoining garret into an atelier, partly funded by Madame Descoings. While the two widows treated painting as a trade, Joseph embraced it passionately, installing a skylight and chocolate-colored walls and continuing his studies under Gros and Schinner.

Political unrest simmered in France: conspiracies and riots swirled around the Chamber of Deputies. Philippe, implicated in plots, was arrested and released for lack of evidence; his military pay was cut, and Agathe feared for him. Friends urged the widows to send Philippe abroad for safety; she resisted the idea of him serving a foreign power, but he ultimately joined General Lallemand in the United States to help found the Champ d’Asile—an enterprise that failed. Agathe equipped him with ten thousand francs, and by late 1817 had adjusted to reduced means. Joseph lived frugally to support the family while his talent developed.

In 1818 Roguin, the notary, absconded with funds intended for Madame Descoings. Soon after, Philippe sent a bill for a thousand francs, having lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. Agathe, distraught, blamed herself for urging his departure. Despite her own difficulties, Madame Descoings continued to support the family. Desperate, Joseph turned to Gérard, who arranged for him to paint copies of a portrait of Louis XVIII; Joseph worked tirelessly, selling panels to cover Philippe’s debt.

When Philippe returned from America, Agathe welcomed him warmly, not seeing how time abroad had hardened him. He became self-centered, indulged in drinking and gambling, and adopted a swaggering arrogance. Agathe, blinded by maternal love, excused his faults as consequences of circumstance. The family’s finances deteriorated; friends discussed selling possessions to help him.

Philippe rejected serving the Bourbons, hampering employment, yet lived as if supported by others—disappearing for days, frequenting cafés and gambling halls, and neglecting family duties. He sought to impress Mariette, and Giroudeau proposed journalism as a way to exploit his experiences. A cashier’s post at a newspaper, secured by Agathe and Madame Descoings, brought temporary relief but did little to reform him. Joseph felt increasingly overshadowed and unappreciated. Ultimately Philippe’s return and altered behavior strained relationships, revealing the consequences of choices, the limits of maternal devotion, and the fragile balance between personal ambition and family duty.

Three months later the colonel, who ate enough for four, still criticized the food while the widows—hesitant to press him—spent lavishly on meals although he had not paid a penny. His mother and Madame Descoings avoided reminding him of his promise; months passed without any of the “tigers with five claws,” as Leon Gozlan joked, migrating from Philippe’s pocket into the household purse. The colonel quieted his conscience by seldom dining at home. “Well, he is happy,” his mother said, “he has a place.”

Urged by a feuilleton edited by Vernou, Mariette debuted at the Porte-Saint-Martin and triumphed with the famous Begrand. A wealthy general, enchanted and acquainted with Philippe and Giroudeau, became her impresario and arranged her first appearance—an example of the solidarity among those taken by folly.

Bixiou soon told his grandmother and Agathe that Philippe, the cashier, was in love with Mariette, the celebrated ballet-dancer. This appalled the widows; Agathe’s piety cast all stage women as morally suspect, and both feared such women lived extravagantly. “Do you think my brother will spend on Mariette? Such women ruin rich men,” Joseph protested. Bixiou suggested Mariette might be taken from Philippe by higher-ranking admirers; he’d even heard an ambassador was infatuated with her.

Partly from financial motive, Philippe proposed; Mariette declined, perhaps suspecting his intent or valuing her freedom, and hoped instead for an engagement at the Grand Opera. Over the next year Philippe visited his mother only twice a month, spending days at the office, the theatre, or with Mariette, enjoying pleasures and mixing with Tullia and other entertainers.

The carnival lasted eighteen months but grew stormy. After her Opera debut in January 1821 Mariette attracted a distinguished duke. Philippe, driven by passion, began dipping into the funds he managed—taking eleven hundred francs by May. Mariette left for London to seek patrons while the temporary opera house was prepared. Philippe, despite her infidelities, remained deeply in love; to her he was merely a stepping stone.

Forced to relinquish Mariette, Philippe returned to his mother’s apartment, unable to imagine life without recent pleasures. Suicide crossed his mind—not from the deficit alone but because he could not bear life without Mariette’s company. He entered Joseph’s studio where Joseph was copying a picture for a dealer and explaining his earnings. Pale and subdued, Philippe asked how long it would take to paint his portrait, saying he wanted to leave his mother a likeness. When Joseph asked if he was leaving again, Philippe replied, “I am going never to return,” and, pressed, confessed he had taken money from the funds and could not live with the dishonor.

Joseph tried to reassure him that repayment would save his honor, but Philippe, despairing, fled downstairs, leaving Joseph anxious. Joseph informed Madame Descoings, who was horrified. Agathe was devastated, crying at the thought of Philippe failing in honor. Madame Descoings urged hope, attributing his ruin to a bad woman; Agathe lived in terror for her son.

That day Philippe calmly submitted his accounts, inventing a story that he’d retained the money for safekeeping. He then went to a gambling house—winning at first, then losing everything. Returning home, he found concern and affection from his family, which briefly comforted him. “Promise not to kill yourself, and all shall be forgotten,” his mother pleaded. Philippe, realizing the threat had worked, feigned gratitude and affection, and they shared dinner; he felt momentarily relieved.

The next day Agathe appeared older and more anxious, aware the family’s finances were precarious. Madame Descoings suggested consulting Desroches to manage the affair and preserve honor. Desroches, in his diplomatic way, advised Agathe to sign a transfer of funds and petition for Philippe’s reinstatement in the army, assuring them Philippe would soon regain his rank and easing some fear. Yet Agathe’s faith was shaken and she lived in constant dread of his downfall.

Philippe returned to a life of leisure—gambling, drinking, appearing disheveled and indifferent to the family’s plight. His love for Mariette had hardened him; he treated women with contempt. Joseph, concentrating on painting, gained recognition and Madame Descoings’s support, who pinned her hopes on his future. As Madame Descoings pursued lottery dreams, she remained oblivious to growing tensions. Joseph, noticing discrepancies in his finances, began to suspect Philippe and confided in Madame Descoings. They planned to catch him. When Joseph discovered that Philippe had indeed taken money from him, he felt betrayed; despite anger, he still struggled to confront his brother. Madame Descoings urged sympathy for Philippe’s struggles, but Joseph remained wary of his influence on the family’s future.

Despite Philippe’s careful approach to nightly gambling, he was sometimes “cleaned out.” Needing his usual ten francs, he began stealing from his family—pilfering from his brother and whatever Madame Descoings or Agathe left about. After a dream in which Philippe robbed her, Agathe recognized his decline and begged him to ask for money instead of stealing. Within two weeks her savings were gone; she took worsted work for twenty sous a day, spent sleepless nights waiting for Philippe, and watched his increasing drunkenness and mood swings.

When Madame Descoings suggested Philippe get new clothes, he retorted that his mother had no money and that he had mortgaged his pension. He admitted borrowing from Giroudeau, calling it a “debt of honor.” Agathe excused him as out of his element, while Joseph criticized his neglect of appearance and duty. Philippe sank further into debauchery and gambling, losing the steadiness and good looks he once had.

One day Philippe asked Joseph for money to pay a tobacconist; Joseph refused. Philippe then guessed where Madame kept her savings and, expecting she’d be out buying Christmas treats, broke into her room. He found twenty napoleons hidden in the mattress and took the money to gamble.

At the gambling house Philippe won substantial sums over several sessions and, intoxicated by success, amassed over one hundred fifty thousand francs by night’s end. Pressed by greed, he continued and lost everything. The next morning he awoke in despair, knowing he had ruined his family.

Agathe and Madame Descoings waited for Philippe at Christmas dinner; when he failed to show they worried. Madame had pinned hopes on a lottery stake, but upon checking her savings she found the money gone and fainted. The concierge confirmed Philippe had been in the apartment. Agathe returned pale and shaken; the family confronted Philippe. He dismissed their accusations, claiming he’d taken what was owed him. A fight erupted between Joseph and Philippe, and Madame Descoings suffered a stroke amid the chaos.

Joseph offered his mother money afterward, but she refused, clinging to lottery hope. When the winning numbers were announced it was clear she’d missed her chance; she died shortly after. Philippe, recovering from illness, was told by Agathe that their finances had collapsed and urged to rejoin the army. Indifferent and feeling abandoned, he accepted the money she reluctantly offered and left to seek the company of those he called true friends. Agathe, heartbroken, prayed for her son and resigned herself to the loss of the child she once loved.

By February 1822 Madame Bridau had been moved into the attic above the kitchen of her former apartment, recently occupied by her son Philippe. Seeing his mother reduced, Joseph set out to make her comfortable: he rearranged the garret, added better furnishings, and took Agathe daily to a table d’hôte that cost him nearly a hundred francs a month. Alarmed by the expense and urged by her confessor, Agathe accepted a seven-hundred-franc-a-year post at a lottery office that demanded long hours.

She adapted to the routine, preparing Joseph’s breakfasts, mending his clothes, keeping the studio tidy, and aiding his work despite having no artistic training. Their bond deepened through these small acts of tenderness. They spoke often of Philippe—Agathe feared his reckless life, but Joseph dismissed her worries, convinced Philippe was prospering in New York.

One afternoon Joseph visited Philippe, who worked at a newspaper. Philippe invited Joseph to dine; Joseph declined to stay with his mother. When he described Philippe afterward, Agathe felt hopeful. She decorated Joseph’s studio and prepared an elaborate breakfast for a sitting for Philippe’s portrait; overwhelmed, she hid and watched the two sons together. After the sitting Philippe invited them to a sumptuous dinner, which Agathe eagerly accepted while Joseph felt uneasy at the ostentation. At another sitting she learned more of Philippe’s circle—journalists and actresses—and heard boastful plans, though he remained vague about money.

Later Joseph postponed a session for a commission. Philippe, mistaking a valuable copy of a Rubens for an original, took it and sold it for a fraction of its worth. Discovering the theft devastated Joseph; Agathe was heartbroken, feeling she had lost her son to vice.

Two months after, an old officer told Agathe Philippe was ill and needed money. Working at the lottery, she had little to spare and at first refused to help the son she felt she had banished from her heart; Joseph insisted Philippe go to the hospital. When she later found Philippe destitute on the street, she gave what she could and despaired.

Agathe then learned Philippe had been implicated in a conspiracy and was arrested; she fainted. Joseph consulted Desroches, who urged them to secure the family inheritance and gather evidence of undue influence; he planned to visit Issoudun at the holidays to help. Agathe’s letter to her godmother brought a discouraging reply about an unhelpful brother. Agathe and Joseph set off for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to face the consequences and Agathe to confront their deep estrangement.

Issoudun, one of France’s oldest cities, is noted by Caesar and Rigord for its population and commerce. Recent excavations by M. Armand Peremet uncovered a fifth-century basilica—likely unique in France—built from stones of a Roman temple to Isis. The town’s name, derived from “Isis” with “dun” indicating a religious and military settlement, points to ancient Celtic origins.

In the Middle Ages Issoudun was a center of power: Richard Coeur-de-lion built a tower above the basilica as part of its fortifications, and the town later served as a stronghold for Routiers and Cottereaux mercenaries sent by Henry II against his son Richard. Its prominence has since faded. After the 1830 insurrection vine-growers resisted government intervention, burned tax offices, and asserted autonomy—demonstrating the force of a community of about eight thousand vine-growers.

In the fourteenth century Issoudun held sixteen to seventeen thousand people, prospering on the wool trade and producing blankets and gloves; by Louis XIV’s time it enjoyed some elegance. Today the population is about twelve thousand, including vine-growers from nearby suburbs. The local economy, especially wine, suffers from outdated practices: vine-growers resist modernization and produce poor-quality wine that undercuts potential prosperity.

This decline reflects a broader spirit of stagnation. When a proposed new road linking Paris and Toulouse through Issoudun arose, local leaders opposed it, fearing higher food prices—an example of provincial preference for tradition over progress.

Issoudun’s social fabric has also deteriorated. The disappearance of the nobility after past upheavals removed a source of competition and dynamism. The bourgeoisie, content with routine social forms, have grown complacent, leading to stagnant governance. Administration is dominated by intermarried local families whose ties produce ineffectual leadership and weak accountability; the clergy exert little influence, further diminishing civic vitality.

Against this background the town’s youth, feeling trapped, formed the “Knights of Idleness,” led by Maxence Gilet. Rumored to be an illegitimate son of a local official, Max was charismatic, rose from poverty, distinguished himself in the military, and returned to clash with the bourgeoisie. Under his leadership the Knights staged pranks and hoaxes—disruptive but creative antics—meeting secretly at a tavern run by Père and La Cognette, who sheltered them for self-interest. Issoudun’s ancient grandeur and archaeological importance thus stand in sharp contrast to its modern decline; the Knights embody the youth’s frustration and thirst for life.

As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a prank. An old Spaniard, a former prisoner of war, left his empty cart at the foot of the Issoudun tower. Maxence, arriving at a Knights’ rendezvous, proposed, “Here’s Père Fario’s cart. Let’s get it up on the embankment first, and then decide.” In an hour the cart was taken apart and hoisted piece by piece to the embankment like artillery; once reassembled, the Knights, hungry and thirsty, returned to Mère Cognette’s to await the Spaniard’s astonishment.

Their amusements varied; they did not prank nightly. The twenty youths met for hunts, autumn vintages, and winter skating. Some were especially close to Max, now their idol. François Hochon and Baruch Borniche, grandchildren of Madame Hochon, treated Max as a cousin and relied on him for money and the adventures their grandfather denied them.

That night, after twelve to fifteen bottles of wine, François and Baruch sat beside Max at a dim table. Baruch warned, “You are threatened in your stronghold.”

“What do you mean?” Max asked.

“My grandmother got a letter from Madame Bridau—her goddaughter—saying she and her son are coming here. She’s preparing two rooms for them.”

Max, thirty-four and soldierly in appearance, was unsettled despite his outward confidence. From the other end of the table someone remarked, “Rouget’s sister will secure the property when he dies—and goodbye to your harvest!”

Max bristled. “What’s that to me?” When François suggested Rouget might revoke a will, Max snapped, “I’ve silenced those who meddle with you, François. Is this how you repay me? By using a contemptuous nickname for a woman I care about?”

For the first time Max spoke openly about Flore Brazier, Rouget’s servant-mistress whom society disparaged. His friends knew to tread carefully on the subject. With Max in command, François fell silent and Goddet reminded the Order of their freedom of speech. Max steered the conversation.

“You believe Flore, the Rabouilleuse, supports me since I returned. That I throw away three hundred francs a month and treat you to dinners because of her money? Yes—Flore aims for Rouget’s property,” he said.

“You think I plan to marry Flore when Rouget dies, and that these Parisians threaten my future?”

“That’s it,” François admitted.

“Don’t worry,” Max went on. “If I need help to deal with these Parisians, will you assist me—within the limits of our fooleries, of course. I’m not asking you to kill or poison them. If the Bridaus succeed and Flore has nothing but what she stands in, I’d be satisfied. I love her enough to prefer her to Mademoiselle Fichet, if she would have me.”

Mademoiselle Fichet, the richest heiress in Issoudun, was being courted by Goddet. Moved by Max’s frankness, the Knights cheered: “You’re a fine fellow, Max!” “We’ll stand by you!” “A fig for the Bridaus!” Their relaxed morality and loyalty reflected Max’s influence. He raised his glass of claret.

“As a mark of ill-will I wish you all a mistress as fine as Flore! As for this influx of relatives, I feel no immediate unease; we’ll see what the future holds—”

“Don’t forget Fario’s cart!”

“That’s safe enough!” Goddet replied.

“I’ll handle that,” Max assured them. “Be in the market early, and let me know when the old fellow comes for his cart.”

At half-past three the Knights slipped away in their soft shoes. Max returned to place Saint-Jean, masking his worries; his time in Cabrera had taught him concealment. The forty thousand francs a year from Rouget’s property anchored his affection for Flore. Despite his bluster, news of legitimate heirs unnerved him: Rouget’s savings, amassed over seventeen years and amounting to large sums, remained in the doctor’s name; even if Flore could claim a will, those funds might be secured elsewhere.

“That fool of a girl never mentioned her sister and nephews in all these seven years!” Max muttered entering rue l’Avenier. “Seven hundred and fifty thousand francs with various notaries can’t be liquidated quickly without everyone knowing in this gossiping town! The priority is to get rid of these relatives; once they’re gone, we can secure the property. I need to think this through.”

Exhausted, Max used a pass-key to enter Père Rouget’s house and went to bed, telling himself, “Tomorrow, my thoughts will be clear.”

The narrative then traces how Flore Brazier became known as the “Rabouilleuse” and the quasi-mistress of Jean-Jacques Rouget’s household. As Doctor Rouget aged he saw his son’s ineptitude and tried to impose a routine, only to see Jean-Jacques submit to the first authority that came along. One day in September 1799, returning from his rounds, the doctor found a captivating little girl by a brook, emerging from the water in a tattered petticoat and makeshift hat. The child said she was from Vatan and was catching crabs for her uncle Brazier, who joked about republican liberty from the brook.

Struck by the child’s looks, the doctor offered to take her in, promising a better life and wages to her uncle. Flore’s life changed: she was fed, clothed, and given gifts to encourage study, though her peasant habits made her dislike books and the doctor eventually stopped her schooling. Town gossip soon colored perceptions of impropriety around the doctor’s attention; yet for five years he enjoyed educating her without scandalous complications.

Born in 1787 and raised amid the upheaval of the Revolution and its aftermath, Flore grew up with shaken traditional values and a pragmatic self-interest rather than strict virtue. As Rouget neared death, his notary urged him to make provisions for her; Rouget’s cynical refusal to leave her anything revealed bitterness and a desire to punish what he saw as an unreciprocated attachment. That refusal set the stage for the inheritance struggle that now threatened Max’s position and Flore’s hopes.

Jean-Jacques Rouget did not mourn his father’s death, though Flore Brazier did. The old doctor had made his son miserable, especially after Jean-Jacques came of age, yet he had provided Flore with the material comforts prized in the country. After the funeral, when Fanchette asked Flore what would become of her now that Monsieur was dead, Jean-Jacques showed animation for the first time.

At seventeen Flore still had the beauty that had attracted the doctor, though she was growing plump like other countrywomen. Jean-Jacques hesitated to speak; the memory of his father’s death and benefactions weighed on him, and Flore left him puzzled by his silence.

When his father died Jean-Jacques was thirty-seven: timid, submissive, and childlike. His torpor left him passionate but lacking confidence, awkward and unattractive, preferring solitude. Too late the doctor recognized how his son’s temperament had suffered. He wanted Jean-Jacques to marry but feared losing control of his property, so he sought a daughter-in-law and tried to instill avarice in his son to give him some financial instinct.

Despite his father’s efforts, Jean-Jacques was secretly in love with Flore, the only woman whose education and presence he could comfortably observe. He took pride in the instruction his father gave her, thinking it would make her easier to win. Flore, unaware of his feelings, waited for him to declare himself, but he remained silent.

Three weeks of uncertainty passed. One night Flore awoke to find Jean-Jacques crouched outside her door and took it as proof of his love; the next day she found him less repellent. Their conversations remained halting: Jean-Jacques asked if she wanted to return to the fields, and Flore, curious about his intentions, assured him of her honesty. These exchanges revealed his growing affection even as he struggled to express it.

After ten months Jean-Jacques brightened and became attentive to his appearance, much to Flore’s satisfaction. The townspeople noticed and speculated. Fanchette, the cook who had disapproved of Flore’s place in the household, eventually left; Flore had anticipated this and learned to cook and manage the house, surpassing the former cook and taking pride in keeping things neat.

Over the next nine years Flore gained complete control over Jean-Jacques. She treated him with a blend of familiarity and respect and gradually became the dominant figure; he, childlike and dependent, relied on her for everything, and she indulged his whims.

By 1815 Flore was at the height of her beauty; in 1816 she fell in love with Maxence Gilet, a penniless officer. Her new passion altered the household. Feeling neglected, she pressed Jean-Jacques about his wealth and the empty rooms and suggested inviting Max. Fearing to lose her, Jean-Jacques reluctantly agreed. With Max in the home Jean-Jacques found the companionship and conversation he had lacked, while Flore shirked household duties, hired a new cook, refined her appearance to impress Max, bought him a horse, and took on a servant. The household shifted: Flore and Max became central and Jean-Jacques receded into the background, increasingly dependent on them.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to claim her threatened inheritance, Jean-Jacques Rouget had become semi-vegetative. After Max’s financial aid, Flore raised Rouget’s standard of living, feeding him from Vedie’s kitchen. Despite better food, he grew weaker and utterly submissive; his devotion to Flore was his sole motive and made him seem much older than fifty-seven.

Max’s apartment reflected his devotion: provincial comforts, colored tiles, elegant wallpapers and mahogany furniture. A rush matting on the stairs muffled footsteps so Max could come home late without waking anyone—not even Rouget, who remained ignorant of Max’s nocturnal activities.

One morning Flore, in a cotton gown, found Max asleep and noticed his late return. He told her a serious matter: Rouget’s family were coming to Issoudun, likely to evict them. Flore vowed confrontation; Max urged caution, asking for coffee and suggesting they plan later. As she began to make coffee, Baruch interrupted: Fario was searching for his cart.

Max joined the crowd. Fario, a small peculiar man, claimed his cart had been stolen. Max led him to the tower embankment where, to everyone’s surprise, the cart was found at the top. Amid laughter Max theatrically tossed the cart down, humiliating Fario and turning the episode into town gossip.

The incident heightened concern. François informed Max that the Bridaus would arrive that night. Max resolved they should not appear hostile; he proposed letting the Hochons handle driving them out. Meanwhile Flore, unable to contain her anger at the news that Rouget’s sister and nephew were coming, punished Rouget by withdrawing her affection, leaving him bewildered and distressed. Vedie urged him to placate Flore. Rouget denied inviting them and blamed Madame Hochon for the visit.

Max argued Rouget should welcome his kin for appearances. Rouget thanked him; Max counseled him to keep his will while being polite to his sister. After breakfast Max and Flore discussed finances and plans to secure Rouget’s wealth while managing the incoming family. Flore admired Max’s cleverness; they plotted so the Hochons would help deal with the Bridaus.

Opposite Rouget’s, the Hochon household was modest. Monsieur Hochon, a retired tax collector, was famed for extreme frugality. Madame Hochon prepared to receive her goddaughter Agathe despite her husband’s reluctance, and their granddaughter Adolphine secretly hoped to win Joseph Bridau’s affection.

News of the Bridaus’ arrival split Issoudun: the cautious bourgeoisie offered measured support, while the Knights of Idleness were vehemently loyal to Max. The Fario incident and the town’s divided sentiment underscored how fragile reputations and power were in Issoudun, and how a few calculated actions could shape the looming confrontation over inheritance and influence.

Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales in Issoudun at three. Though tired, Madame Bridau felt revived by familiar sights and memories. News spread quickly; Madame Hochon greeted Agathe like a daughter, her years of loss making her cling to that affection.

Agathe met the uninviting Monsieur Hochon and the rest of the household: the young men Baruch and François, and shy Adolphine, who noticed Joseph. The miserly Hochon remarked on Joseph’s recent hospital stay and apparent hunger; the youths were struck by his looks. “This is my son, the painter; my good Joseph,” Agathe said proudly. Joseph’s blunt comment about resembling his father was taken by Madame Hochon as affection for Agathe. Baruch hurried off to spread news, emphasizing Joseph’s striking appearance.

Townspeople quickly gossiped, likening Joseph to a brigand and airing disdain for artists; many planned to visit the Hochons that evening to satisfy their curiosity about the Parisians.

Installed in modest attic rooms, Joseph contrasted his Parisian artistic life with the drab provincial existence. Dinner revealed the Hochons’ frugality: plain bouilli, hard eggs, and meager accompaniments. Accustomed to better fare, Joseph found it disappointing but tried to adapt. Agathe learned her childhood home had been sold to the Borniches, and Madame Hochon braced for an influx of visitors. Joseph observed the family’s strict ways and limited hospitality.

After dinner the women gathered in the salon. Agathe and Madame Hochon exchanged life stories—Agathe describing her struggles, Madame Hochon offering hard-won wisdom about endurance—continually interrupted by a stream of callers eager to meet the Parisians. Joseph remained detached, studying visitors with a critical eye that made them uneasy. Later, as the house quieted, the two women resumed their heartfelt exchange about the burdens they carried.

Elsewhere the Knights of Idleness convened to plot mischief against the Parisians. Max, their leader, proposed a prank involving rats to sabotage the grain supply of a disliked merchant, Fario; the gang welcomed the scheme and sought a way to drive the Bridaus away without harming the Hochons.

Next morning at breakfast Joseph glimpsed his brother Jean-Jacques Rouget, unrecognizable and disheveled, accompanied by the conspicuous Flore Brazier. Joseph admired Flore’s beauty; Agathe feared her influence. A letter from Jean-Jacques invited Joseph to dine, which the Hochons received with mixed feelings, suspecting Flore’s involvement. Gritte reported the Rouget household’s ostentation and Flore’s display; Monsieur Hochon advised Joseph to observe carefully and speak frankly with Jean-Jacques about his nephews. With tensions simmering and rival schemes in motion, the stage was set for a fraught family reunion.

At four o’clock Joseph crossed from the Rouget to the Hochon house, where Kouski announced him. The table was set in the large hall; Joseph greeted his uncle Rouget, Flore, and Maxence Gilet with light banter, admiring Flore and the house and noting Max’s good looks, suspecting a triangular household. Baruch and Francois arrived and Flore suggested a walk before dinner. While she prepared, Joseph eagerly examined the house’s paintings, which Rouget said had come from the Descoings, bought during the Revolution. Joseph was ecstatic; Max showed him more upstairs. Flore urged Rouget to give the paintings to Joseph, arguing Rouget didn’t care for them.

Rouget offered them to Joseph—without frames—and Flore proposed paying Joseph four thousand francs to make copies. Overwhelmed, Joseph accepted. The news spread through Issoudun and enhanced Flore’s standing. Dinner that night was splendid, with local dignitaries. Joseph, somewhat tipsy, went home at eleven; Rouget, having drunk heavily, was carried to bed. Left alone, Max and Flore agreed it was better to befriend the Bridaus than antagonize them and planned to send the pictures to Joseph the next day.

The next morning the paintings were propped against Rouget’s wall. Agathe, Joseph’s mother, visited Rouget to thank him, prompted by old Hochon, who feared the Bridaus’ influence. Hochon warned Agathe that Joseph had been fooled and urged her to cultivate Flore to gain access to Rouget. Agathe was warmly received; Rouget was ill from the previous night. Flore feigned servitude and Agathe hinted Rouget should marry her, but Flore insisted Rouget would remember her in his will.

In the following days the Knights of Idleness—local troublemakers—released rats and mice into Fario’s grain store, causing havoc. The Bridaus’ hold on Rouget seemed secure: Flore claimed Rouget was too weak for walks and needed a carriage; they bought a shabby vehicle that drew town attention. Rouget signed a power of attorney granting Max authority over his investments, while Flore handled local transactions. Fario returned to find his store devastated and suspected Max’s involvement; he began gathering evidence against the Knights.

Agathe believed Rouget had not made a will and intended to ask him directly, while Hochon urged her to stay longer and use religious influence to counter Max and Flore—advice Agathe reluctantly considered. Progress for the Bridaus was slow; Hochon stressed persistence.

Meanwhile Joseph, sensing the paintings’ real value, sent them to Desroches in Paris for appraisal, convinced they were worth far more than the 4,000-franc copying job. Agathe warned him to keep their worth quiet, but word leaked and searches for old artworks spread through Issoudun. Max regretted aspects of their scheme, now fearful of the Church’s influence if the paintings’ value became public.

Desroches and Schinner’s letters advised keeping Agathe in Issoudun, warned about Max, and suggested Rouget might already have a will—so the Bridaus’ inheritance depended on a struggle between Flore and the Church. Disturbed, Joseph decided to return to Paris. He said goodbye to Rouget, who seemed altered by drink and flattery. Max and Flore saw Joseph’s departure as part of a larger plan. Irritated by talk about the paintings’ worth and the manipulations around him, Joseph resolved to return the pictures to Rouget later and rely on his own talent.

The Hochon family observed Joseph’s agitation and suspected Max and Flore had known the paintings’ value all along and manipulated him; Agathe defended her son’s decision, but Hochon warned that returning the paintings might jeopardize any chance at inheritance.

Between midnight and one the Knights of Idleness fed the town’s dogs; their expedition lasted until three. After supper at Cognette’s, Max, turning from rue l’Avenier into Grande rue at half-past four, was stabbed by Fario, who had lurked in a recess, fled toward Vilatte, and wiped the knife on his handkerchief. Fario then washed the handkerchief in the Rivière forcée and returned to his lodgings at Saint-Paterne, where his new watchman later found him in bed.

Max fell, crying out and alarming Lousteau-Prangin and young Goddet, who ran up the street calling for help; the townspeople, used to false alarms, stayed in bed. Max had fainted. He recognized Fario but, on regaining consciousness and seeing the wound was not fatal, decided to exploit the incident: “I think I recognized that cursed painter!”

Lousteau-Prangin hurried to his father, the judge. Max was carried home by Cognette, Goddet, and others. Goddet, suspicious of Max’s words, asked Gritte if Joseph Bridau was in bed; she replied that Joseph had left at half-past four, restless all night. The revelation shocked the group, and they entered Rouget’s house to find Max in a bloody shirt.

Joseph had spent the night anxious about how the townspeople would view him, fearing they might think he had robbed his uncle. He longed to flee to Paris and confront Max; at dawn he walked to the poplar avenue toward Tivoli to steady his nerves.

Max plotted revenge. After Goddet examined the wound and found it serious but not fatal, news spread and a crowd gathered at the place Saint-Jean. Max whispered to Flore that they could use the incident to rid themselves of the Parisians: pretend he was dying and have Joseph arrested. Flore, feigning tears, spread the story that Joseph had attacked Max out of jealousy over paintings. Incited by her, the crowd demanded Joseph’s capture; the mob grew hostile.

Monsieur Hochon, warned by Gritte, locked himself in, fearing for his safety and wealth. He learned Joseph had left early; Madame Hochon, anxious for their daughter Agathe, hoped she would sleep through the shock. Agathe, however, woke, learned of the accusations, and fainted at the sight of the gendarmes. Monsieur Hochon urged the magistrates to act rapidly; the lieutenant reported a large crowd demanding justice.

Joseph was spotted returning home and nearly seized by the mob. Gendarmes intervened and, despite his protests of innocence, took him to the Palais for his safety. There Joseph calmly insisted on his innocence and provided an alibi, recounting his morning’s movements. The magistrates were somewhat swayed by his composure but kept him in custody while they verified his claims. Joseph wrote to his mother, reassuring her he would soon be free.

Max, recovering, remembered a crucial detail: the attacker had worn list shoes, while Joseph had left in boots. He wrote to the magistrate urging Joseph’s release. As the investigation progressed, witnesses confirmed Joseph’s alibi and he was freed. He returned to his mother’s warm relief; the ordeal strengthened their bond and Joseph felt grateful for the support.

The townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians as a victory; Max and Flore were pleased. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris; Joseph’s spirits improved, while Agathe fretted about her son Philippe, whose trial loomed. To escape stress, Joseph went to the Comte de Sérizy’s chateau to work.

Philippe, punished for actions during the unrest, was sentenced to five years’ surveillance and exiled to Autun. Desroches intervened, securing a change of residence to Issoudun and urging Philippe to reclaim his inheritance from Uncle Rouget. Motivated to restore the family’s fortunes and confront Max and the Rabouilleuse, Philippe prepared to return to Issoudun and, after a brief dinner, departed to face the challenge.

On All-Souls’ Day, November 2, Philippe Bridau registered at the Issoudun police commissary and was told to stay on rue l’Avenier. News that a banished officer linked to the painter falsely accused in the affair had arrived unsettled the town. Maxence Gilet, recently recovered, had converted all of Père Rouget’s mortgages into cash, alarming many. Monsieur Hochon, after consulting notary Héron—who thought Rouget’s heirs should thank him for preventing Gilet from profiting—resolved to protect the Bridaus, long resentful of Gilet and fearful of losing property.

Gilet and Flore Brazier welcomed Philippe, confident they could manipulate Rouget to secure his funds. Philippe presented himself to his uncle in tattered clothes; his disordered appearance betrayed hardship, but he made clear he would not beg from Rouget. His blunt pride impressed Flore and Gilet. Gilet sensed a latent threat but avoided open confrontation.

Philippe then visited the Hochons, meeting curiosity and fear—Madame Hochon especially linked him to past tragedies. Candid about living on limited means, he won some household members with charm and honesty. Discreetly asking Monsieur Hochon for guidance, he requested secrecy; Hochon supplied names of officers hostile to Gilet—Mignonnet and Carpentier. Befriending them, Philippe gained respect by revealing knowledge of the recent conspiracy against the Bourbons and, through strategic behavior, integrated into the local military circle while concealing his true aims.

He dined regularly with Mignonnet and Carpentier, cultivated a respectable image, took fencing lessons and practiced shooting, maintaining a façade of propriety. His disdain for Gilet was evident, yet Gilet remained cautious.

Philippe’s inquiries led him to Fario, who supplied information about the Knights of Idleness and their schemes. Suspecting Gilet and Flore planned to secure Rouget’s income and flee, Philippe insisted Rouget take walks with him to loosen Flore’s influence. He later told Hochon that Gilet sought a power of attorney to access Rouget’s funds; they agreed to monitor Rouget’s movements. Meanwhile Gilet proposed leaving for Vatan to secure the money and marry; Flore, fearful of Philippe’s influence, reluctantly consented. Tensions rose as Philippe’s resolve to protect his family and restore their honor hardened, setting the stage for a dramatic town showdown.

As Philippe walked arm in arm with his uncle Rouget along the boulevard Baron, he thought about the looming confrontation between Colonel Bridau and Captain Maxence Gilet—the town hoped it would settle the struggle over Rouget’s fortune. Monsieur Hochon listened as Philippe outlined a ruthless plan to regain control of Flore, the Rabouilleuse.

Philippe told Rouget bluntly that Flore loved Maxence and wanted Rouget out of the way so she could marry him. Though Rouget still loved her, Philippe insisted he could make her obedient again—by killing Maxence—if Rouget would refuse to sign the power of attorney they demanded before December third. That document, Philippe warned, would let them sell Rouget’s assets and flee to Paris. Rouget recoiled at Philippe taking Maxence’s place; Philippe answered that fear and force were the only ways to handle women.

They met Mignonnet and Carpentier, whom Philippe instructed to arrange the banquet seating so he could force a public confrontation with Gilet. They agreed but warned of Gilet’s skill as a duelist. Philippe reassured Rouget that after proper mourning he and Flore would soon be together.

At home Rouget found Kouski in tears: Flore had left with Vedie. Rouget’s frantic search failed. Gilet, sensing growing hostility over his conduct, felt pressure to act before a duel; Captain Potel urged him to face Bridau. Yet Gilet remained focused on securing Rouget’s fortune first, plotting to transfer assets to Flore to keep them from Philippe—believing the power of attorney would let him control events.

Aware of these moves, Philippe prepared to counter them, suspecting Gilet would seek reconciliation with Flore. He planned to use Baruch to secure the power of attorney on Rouget’s behalf while distracting or keeping Gilet at bay, determined to protect his uncle’s interests and return Flore to Rouget.

Rouget began drafting a letter threatening to revoke his will in Flore’s favor unless she returned; Philippe encouraged it to force her back. Town gossip swelled about the impending duel and the fate of Rouget’s estate. When Flore returned, Philippe asserted control and warned her of consequences if she disobeyed. Rouget, emotionally fragile, clung to her with hope; by morning the town speculated on the duel, and Philippe, confident in his banquet plan, believed it would decide both Rouget’s fate and his own.

Around four the next afternoon officers of the old army gathered at the place du Marché in Issoudun, waiting for Colonel Philippe Bridau at a coronation banquet. Conversation turned to Max’s recent dismissal from old Rouget’s house; only Potel and Renard defended him. An old sub-lieutenant said Max should have resisted. When Max arrived he was coldly received, a sharp contrast to Philippe’s warm welcome, which stiffened Max’s resolve to confront him.

At dinner Philippe toasted the Grand Army and was echoed by eleven soldiers, including Benjamin and Kouski. Mignonnet toasted those who had tried to restore the Emperor’s son, and Max, whose reputation gave weight to his words, rose with an ironic wish to meet again next year. Tension mounted as Philippe and Max exchanged barbs: Philippe remained composed while Max struggled to control himself. The meal ended tensely; the duel was set for the next morning with sabres, Goddet, a former army surgeon, to attend.

That night Monsieur and Madame Hochon urged Philippe to pray; Madame Hochon offered a relic, which he declined, though she vowed to pray for him. The next morning both men appeared composed, but Max felt intimidated by Philippe’s superior stance. When the duel began Max attacked but was quickly disarmed; Philippe paused to let him recover his weapon, increasing the strain. When they resumed they exchanged fierce blows; Philippe was wounded but delivered a fatal stroke. Max died, his ambitions and the Order of Idleness ended, much to the town’s relief.

Flore Brazier, devastated, fell ill with fever and inflammation. Philippe, recovering from his own wounds, wrote letters outlining plans to secure his inheritance and manage Flore’s situation, noting her illness prevented her from fleeing and intending to use her to his advantage.

By February 1823, urged by others and influenced by Philippe’s suggestions that marriage would secure her future, Flore, recovering, was persuaded to accept a match he had orchestrated. The contract granted her a substantial dower and a life annuity. Agathe, Philippe’s mother, delighted in her son’s apparent reform and the town’s approval, but the family lawyer Desroches warned that Philippe’s cunning would likely prevent her and Joseph from inheriting Rouget’s property.

Once married, Philippe asserted control: demanding a power of attorney from Rouget, using Flore to manage her uncle’s affairs while keeping her under close watch, and seeking to reestablish himself in the army and in Paris. After letters to the minister of war, backed by local endorsements, he was reinstated as lieutenant-colonel.

Lieutenant-Colonel Philippe Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, whom he took to the Treasury to transfer his uncle’s income to himself. The old man and the Rabouilleuse were soon absorbed into extravagant Parisian life; Rouget overindulged after a lavish supper with the dancer Lolotte and died, said to be of indigestion.

Madame Rouget prospered in Paris, though Philippe had Mariette watch her to prevent serious lapses. In October 1823 Philippe went to Issoudun to settle his uncle’s estate, quickly amassing 1,600,000 francs (excluding valuable paintings held by Monsieur Hochon), and entrusted the funds to Mongenod and Sons.

Philippe married, legally securing his wife’s fortune if she died childless. He bought her a furnished apartment and secretly acquired a grand hôtel on rue de Clichy, spending lavishly on renovations and furnishings. The rise of Charles X aided the Duc de Chaulieu’s circle; because Philippe supplied information about past conspiracies he was appointed lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse’s regiment and, through court connections, ennobled as Comte de Brambourg.

Ambitious and insatiable, Philippe sought further honors—aide-de-camp to the Dauphin and ultimately a general’s rank—distancing himself from old friends who might jeopardize his ascent. He planned to marry Mademoiselle Amélie de Soulanges and acquire military distinctions to cement his status.

Meanwhile Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist, supported by his mother Agathe, who ran a lottery office. Despite talent, Joseph endured poverty and criticism while Philippe’s success overshadowed him. Deceived by appearances, Agathe accepted Philippe’s rise as justification for his neglect. Desperate, she wrote to Philippe for help; his cold reply devastated her and hastened her decline. Near death she learned painfully of the contrast between her sons, realized she had misplaced her affections, and died; Joseph buried her alone, heartbroken, while Philippe remained indifferent.

Joseph found solace among friends. Later Flore, Philippe’s wife, wrote pleading for help in destitution. Joseph and Bixiou visited, arranged care, and with friends plotted to expose Philippe’s abandonment and secure justice. Philippe continued to rise socially until his fortunes collapsed in the July Revolution of 1830. Ultimately Joseph inherited Philippe’s estate and achieved success in art, becoming Comte de Brambourg; he remained humble, often reflecting on his family’s tragedy and its lessons.
