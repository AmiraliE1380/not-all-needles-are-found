**La Grande Bretèche**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage and Clara Bell*

---

The story begins with a doctor, Monsieur Bianchon, recounting a chilling tale at a late-night gathering. He describes an isolated, dilapidated house near Vendôme, known as La Grande Bretèche, surrounded by a wild garden that hints at a once-thriving estate. The house, now in ruins, evokes a sense of mystery and melancholy, suggesting a tragic history.

Bianchon reflects on the house's past, imagining it as a haven for a country gentleman who loved horticulture. The garden, overgrown and neglected, symbolizes lost joy and forgotten lives. The house's decay is so profound that it seems to carry a curse, prompting questions about its abandonment.

During his stay in Vendôme, Bianchon becomes fascinated with the house and often sneaks into the garden, allowing his imagination to run wild with stories of its former glory. One evening, after a particularly haunting visit, he meets Monsieur Regnault, a local notary, who informs him that the house belongs to the estate of the late Comtesse de Merret. Regnault explains that the Comtesse's will forbids anyone from entering the house or making repairs for fifty years after her death, a stipulation that adds to the house's enigma.

Regnault shares the Comtesse's tragic story. After her husband, the Count de Merret, died, she became increasingly reclusive and eccentric. She left La Grande Bretèche in disarray, and rumors circulated about her mental state. On her deathbed, she summoned Regnault to witness her will, which revealed her intention to leave her estate to the hospital in Vendôme, with strict instructions regarding La Grande Bretèche.

The will's peculiar conditions, including the prohibition of entry and repairs, intrigue Bianchon. Regnault, who had been a clerk before becoming a notary, recounts how the Comtesse's decline was marked by isolation and illness. Her last moments were filled with a desperate urgency to secure her wishes, culminating in her death shortly after handing over her will.

As Bianchon contemplates the Comtesse's life and the fate of La Grande Bretèche, he is drawn into the local gossip surrounding the couple. His landlady, Madame Lepas, reveals more about the Comtesse and her husband, painting a picture of a once-happy couple whose relationship soured over time. The Count was known for his temper, while the Comtesse was admired for her beauty and wealth.

Madame Lepas also shares a secret involving a young Spanish prisoner who was lodged at her inn during the war. This young man, who was handsome and well-mannered, became a mysterious figure in the town, often seen near the Comtesse's chapel. His presence adds another layer to the story, hinting at possible connections to the Comtesse's tragic fate.

The narrative weaves together themes of love, loss, and the passage of time, as Bianchon grapples with the haunting legacy of La Grande Bretèche. The house stands as a testament to the complexities of human relationships and the secrets that linger long after the people have gone. Ultimately, the tale leaves readers pondering the nature of memory and the shadows of the past that continue to shape the present.

In the narrative, a woman recounts the mysterious disappearance of a Spanish Grandee, Count Férédia, who had been living in her house. After he failed to return one night, she discovered a letter and a significant sum of money and diamonds he left behind, intended for masses of gratitude for his escape. Her husband searched for him and found the Count's clothes near the river, leading them to believe he had escaped rather than drowned.

The story takes a darker turn as the woman suspects the Count's involvement in the death of Madame de Merret, whose crucifix was similar to one the Count owned. She expresses her moral conflict over keeping the Count's money, which she believes is rightfully hers.

The narrator becomes intrigued by Rosalie, the maid, who seems to hold secrets about the events surrounding Madame de Merret. He decides to pursue her to uncover the truth. Rosalie eventually agrees to share what she knows, revealing that Madame de Merret had been ill and her husband, Monsieur de Merret, had been unaware of her condition until he returned home late one night.

On that fateful evening, Monsieur de Merret, suspecting something was amiss, decided to check on his wife instead of relying on Rosalie's assurances. He confronted Madame de Merret, who denied anyone being in the cupboard, despite his suspicions. In a tense moment, she swore on a crucifix that there was no one there, which led to a tragic resolution.

Monsieur de Merret, believing in his wife's purity, chose not to open the cupboard. However, he devised a plan to wall it up, enlisting the help of a mason, Gorenflot. As the wall was built, Madame de Merret attempted to signal to the man inside, indicating hope for his escape.

After the wall was completed, Monsieur de Merret kept a close watch on his wife, who was distraught. When he left to obtain a passport for Gorenflot, Madame de Merret attempted to break down the wall to free the man inside, but was caught by her husband, leading to her fainting.

Monsieur de Merret then trapped his wife in her room, refusing to let her leave or intercede for the man she had tried to save. He remained with her for twenty days, during which he reminded her of her oath, leaving her in despair.

The story concludes with the women at the table feeling the weight of the tale, reflecting on the moral complexities and emotional turmoil that unfolded within the walls of La Grande Bretèche.
