Honoré De Balzac dedicates "Pierrette" to Mademoiselle Anna Hanska, highlighting the story's melancholy and the necessity of confronting sorrows for loved ones.

In October 1827, sixteen-year-old Jacques Brigaut admires Pierrette Lorrain's house in Lower Provins but is urged by her to flee when she hears a noise. Alone, Pierrette reflects on her sorrowful life, marked by her father's death and her mother's financial struggles after marrying a physician. Living with her guardians, the self-interested Rogrons, she becomes increasingly dependent on them.

The narrative addresses themes of social class, familial duty, and financial struggles. The Rogrons, particularly Pere Rogron and his unattractive wife, live modestly and raise socially inept children, Sylvie and Jerome-Denis. When the Lorrains wish to care for Pierrette, the Rogrons refuse, fearing for their inheritance.

Sylvie invites Pierrette to live with them to regain social standing, but their plans are complicated by Pierrette's circumstances. After her grandparents are hospitalized, she feels burdened by charity until discovering wealthy relatives. Upon arriving at her cousins' home, she faces indifference and harsh expectations, leading to feelings of inadequacy. Pierrette's health declines under the Rogrons' neglect, contrasting her innocence with their coldness, while she finds solace in her faith.

Jacques Brigaut's return to Provins disrupts the Rogron family, particularly affecting Pierrette, who has become frail under her cousin Sylvie's oppressive care. Brigaut, a journeyman cabinet-maker, is determined to improve himself for Pierrette's sake. He learns of the Rogrons' dubious acquisition of Pierrette's grandfather's property and observes her unhappiness.

Sylvie's jealousy intensifies with Brigaut's presence, fearing a romantic connection between Pierrette and Colonel Gouraud, whom she desires. This jealousy leads to tension in the household, with Sylvie accusing Pierrette of having a secret admirer. After a confrontation, Pierrette injures herself, which Sylvie cruelly attributes to divine punishment.

Despite their mutual longing, Pierrette's health deteriorates, and Sylvie's suspicions grow. When Pierrette faints, Sylvie dismisses concerns, and tensions escalate after she discovers Pierrette's letters to Brigaut. Pierrette's grandmother vows to seek justice against the Rogrons, but despite community efforts, Pierrette tragically passes away, prompting Brigaut to pursue justice amid societal indifference.
