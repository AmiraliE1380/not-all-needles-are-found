Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman gentleman, married a wealthy heiress and became a Gascon after selling his estate. He served as major of the Gate Guards until his death in 1813, having avoided the Revolution by going to Martinique. Upon returning, he found his estate well-managed by Mathias, a progressive notary’s clerk. After Madame de Manerville's death in 1810, he became increasingly miserly, depriving his only son, Paul, of resources.

Paul de Manerville returned from college in 1810 and lived under his father's strict control for three years, stunting his emotional growth and making him timid. He felt trapped in his father's mansion, unable to socialize due to financial constraints. His father took him to royalist gatherings, where Paul felt out of place among the aging nobility. To cope, he engaged in physical activities like fencing and horseback riding, while his intellectual pursuits were limited to novels.

After his father's death, Paul inherited a fortune but was disillusioned with Bordeaux and Lanstrac. He spent six years traveling Europe, eventually exhausting his capital and relying on his estate's revenues. Realizing he needed to settle down, Paul returned to Bordeaux, believing his noble title would attract a suitable partner.

Now a count, Paul recognized the societal value of marriage. He confided in his friend de Marsay about his desire to marry, but de Marsay warned him against it, arguing it would lead to a loss of freedom. Paul defended his longing for companionship and stability, envisioning a life shared with a wife. Despite de Marsay's skepticism about love dynamics post-marriage, Paul remained resolute in his desire for emotional connections, highlighting the tension between societal expectations and personal desires.

In the winter of 1821, Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux, eager to restore his family mansion and uphold his reputation for elegance, earning the nickname “the pink of fashion.” His charm and Parisian sophistication helped him navigate social circles, endearing him to the local elite.

Paul de Manerville's success in provincial society stemmed from his attention to appearance and gentle demeanor. However, he soon recognized that marriage was essential for a fulfilling life in the provinces. He longed for companionship, particularly with Mademoiselle Evangelista, the celebrated beauty of Bordeaux.

Mademoiselle Evangelista, daughter of a wealthy Spanish merchant, was raised in luxury by her mother, Madame Evangelista, after her father's death. By the time Paul returned, Natalie was a stunning young woman, but rumors about her extravagant upbringing made suitors hesitant, with many believing only a prince could afford to marry her.

When Paul and Natalie first met, they were immediately drawn to each other, each envisioning a future together. Paul, aware of the Evangelista wealth, felt no need for inquiries that would typically deter a suitor and dismissed warnings about the family's extravagance, confident in managing the financial implications of marrying Natalie.

As Paul frequented the Evangelista household, he became enchanted by both mother and daughter. Madame Evangelista, still strikingly beautiful at forty, harbored ambitions for her daughter to rise in Parisian society. Paul appreciated their warmth, leading society to speculate about his intentions toward Natalie.

Despite the growing attention, neither Paul nor Natalie openly acknowledged their feelings. Natalie’s reserved demeanor intrigued Paul, while Madame Evangelista subtly encouraged their connection. Paul began to view marriage as a means to secure his social standing, believing that wealth would ease the challenges of married life.

As their relationship developed, Paul idealized Natalie, overlooking potential flaws in her character. Captivated by her beauty, he failed to recognize the complexities that might arise in their future together. Societal pressures surrounding their union added to the allure, as both families seemed to endorse the match.

However, Natalie's upbringing and her mother’s ambitions posed potential challenges. While Paul was drawn to the idea of a life with Natalie, he remained unaware of the deeper dynamics, including Madame Evangelista’s desire for power through her daughter's marriage.

In the winter of 1822, Paul sought to marry Natalie with the help of his great-aunt, Baronne de Maulincour. After consulting Madame Evangelista, the baroness informed Paul that the marriage was likely to succeed but warned him about the dowry: Natalie's fortune was tied to her father's patrimony, and her mother would not contribute.

The baroness advised Paul to consult their notary, Maitre Mathias, to ensure the marriage contract protected his interests, leaving Paul anxious about potential deceit from his future mother-in-law. Following this advice, Paul approached Madame Evangelista to discuss the marriage contract, and she suggested that both families’ notaries meet to negotiate terms.

Madame Evangelista faced pressure as she owed her daughter a significant sum from her late husband’s estate, which she could not pay. Fearing Paul might withdraw if he learned the truth, she resolved to manipulate the situation. She summoned her ambitious notary, Maitre Solonet, who assured her they could navigate negotiations favorably.

As evening approached, both notaries met to discuss the marriage contract. Solonet proposed terms benefiting Madame Evangelista, while Mathias insisted on protecting Paul’s interests. Their discussions highlighted stark differences: Mathias represented traditional values, while Solonet embodied modern, opportunistic strategies.

During dinner, Paul and Natalie shared tender moments, but the tension regarding the marriage contract loomed. Ultimately, a compromise was reached where Madame Evangelista would secure her daughter’s inheritance while ensuring her own financial stability. The notaries agreed on terms that would protect Paul’s interests and provide for future generations, though underlying tensions hinted at challenges ahead.

The next day, Elie Magus, a Bordeaux jeweler, met with Madame Evangelista to appraise her mother’s diamonds for the marriage contract of Comte Paul and Mademoiselle Natalie. He valued the necklace and earrings at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” diamond at 100,000 francs but offered only 75,000 francs for the diamond and 160,000 for the jewelry, citing depreciation.

This appraisal fueled rumors among the household staff and community about the marriage's stability, suggesting Madame Evangelista was selling her house and that the marriage might not occur. Despite notaries denying these claims, public skepticism persisted, driven by jealousy of the Evangelista family's wealth.

In response to the gossip, Paul organized a hunting party, which further ignited speculation. Madame de Gyas visited Natalie and her mother, feigning concern while relaying the rumors. Aware of the motivations behind the gossip, Madame Evangelista dismissed it, asserting that the marriage contract was a private matter.

To counter the rumors, Madame Evangelista planned an extravagant celebration called the "fete of the camellias," intending to showcase her wealth. Preparations took over a month, during which the marriage contract's legalities were finalized, and the community buzzed with anticipation.

On the day of the contract signing, Madame Evangelista had a moment of realization regarding the implications of the contract. Recalling her discussions with the notaries, she feared her daughter's fortune might be compromised. Despite her initial confidence, she resolved to demand clarity on the contract's terms.

Lavish preparations transformed the courtyard into a tent filled with camellias, creating an opulent atmosphere. As guests arrived, excitement heightened, but the signing of the contract loomed ominously. Paul and Natalie engaged in playful banter while the notaries read the contract details.

Madame Evangelista listened intently, and when the entailment clause was explained, she realized the potential financial ruin for her daughter. The entail would limit Natalie’s inheritance, leaving her vulnerable should Paul die first. This revelation struck Madame Evangelista deeply, leading her to conclude that her daughter was at risk of losing her fortune.

Despairing, she recognized that breaking off the marriage was no longer an option; the public nature of the preparations made it impossible. She felt trapped, realizing her earlier manipulations had backfired, jeopardizing her daughter’s future.

As the contract was finalized, Madame Evangelista confronted the notaries, expressing her anger. Solonet, one of the notaries, suggested delaying the signing, but she felt that would worsen their reputation. Ultimately, she decided to proceed with the marriage, vowing to protect her daughter’s interests and planning to invest wisely for Natalie’s security.

The signing marked a turning point, with Madame Evangelista determined to navigate her new reality. As festivities began, she felt a mix of triumph and resentment, knowing her daughter’s happiness was intertwined with her own ambitions and fears. The night unfolded with dancing and celebration, but underlying tensions simmered, setting the stage for future conflicts.

As Paul prepared for his marriage, he was troubled by doubts, particularly regarding Madame Evangelista. While he reassured himself about their financial arrangements, he remained cautious, mindful of Mathias's warnings.

Unbeknownst to Paul, dynamics had shifted. Madame Evangelista, once supportive, was now contemplating withdrawing her interests. Paul struggled to understand her complex Creole nature, now stirred by the prospect of losing her daughter.

The next day, Madame Evangelista and Natalie reminisced about their harmonious life. She expressed sorrow over Natalie’s marriage, warning that love could fade if not managed carefully. She advised Natalie to establish her authority as a wife while maintaining control.

Initially hesitant, Natalie was influenced by her mother’s insights. Madame Evangelista cautioned against constant proximity in marriage, suggesting social engagements to maintain allure. She stressed that a woman must never fully surrender her power, advising Natalie to cultivate mystery.

As the wedding approached, Madame Evangelista revealed her plan to sell her jewelry to support Natalie, proposing an exchange that would benefit her daughter. Paul, moved by this sacrifice, agreed, unaware of the underlying tensions.

Determined to step back, Madame Evangelista insisted her presence might hinder their marriage. She expressed a desire to manage her own affairs, believing her absence would allow Natalie and Paul to bond without her influence. Paul felt relieved by this arrangement.

As the wedding neared, Natalie felt anxious about her new life without her mother. Despite her fears, she was determined to be a good wife. The ceremony took place at midnight, often associated with ominous omens. Separated from her mother, Natalie was filled with doubt about her future.

Guests noted the unusual circumstances of the wedding, with some criticizing Paul for excluding Madame Evangelista. Meanwhile, Madame Evangelista, having played her part, left Natalie with a final piece of advice: to be a wife, not a mistress.

After the ceremony, she feigned despair to extract promises from Paul, ensuring his commitment to Natalie. As they departed for Paris, Natalie signaled to her mother that Paul had won the initial battle, but she was already beginning to assert her influence over him.

Five years later, in November, Comte Paul de Manerville visited his old friend, Monsieur Mathias, in Bordeaux. Mathias was absent, so Paul rested in Madame Mathias's room until evening. Upon Mathias's return, he noticed Paul's changed demeanor and expressed sorrow over Paul's financial troubles.

Mathias lamented the loss of Paul's estate, feeling personally shamed by the public seizures. Paul revealed he had left his wife, Natalie, without her knowledge, planning to sail to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune under the name Camille. Mathias was shocked by Paul's decision to enter commerce, believing it beneath his dignity.

Paul explained that he had incurred massive debts due to lavish spending and the costs of his marriage, having given Natalie expensive gifts and sold her investments to maintain their lifestyle. Mathias was incredulous at the scale of Paul's debts, which exceeded a million and a half francs. Paul insisted he could not burden Natalie with their financial troubles and preferred to separate their assets.

Mathias suggested Paul could live off Natalie's income while recovering, but Paul rejected this, wanting to work for his fortune rather than rely on her. He believed women should not be involved in financial matters, while Mathias felt a wife should share both joys and burdens of marriage.

The next day, Mathias received a bill of exchange for 150,000 francs from de Marsay, a friend of Paul’s. Despite his concerns, Mathias accepted Paul's determination to leave for India. At the quay, they faced curiosity and gossip about Paul's downfall.

As Paul prepared to board, Mathias signaled him with a handkerchief, indicating he had important letters. However, the sailor delivering the letters did not convey their urgency. Paul, wanting to avoid being swayed, pocketed the letters unread.

Once at sea, Paul reflected on his life. Overcome by seasickness, he spent days in his cabin contemplating his situation. Eventually, he opened the letters from Natalie and de Marsay. Natalie's letter expressed her anguish over Paul's departure and her desire to support him, revealing she believed she was pregnant. In contrast, de Marsay's letter detailed Paul's financial ruin and warned of a conspiracy against him, urging him to return to Paris instead of going to India.

As Paul absorbed these harsh realities, he felt anger and despair but resolved to confront his challenges head-on, determined to rebuild his life and restore his honor.
