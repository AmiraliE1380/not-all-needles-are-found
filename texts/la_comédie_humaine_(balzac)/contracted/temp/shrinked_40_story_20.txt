Monsieur de Manerville, a Norman who married a wealthy Bordeaux heiress, became attached to her Gascon estate, the château de Lanstrac. He escaped the Revolution by going to Martinique in 1790 and returned to find Lanstrac well managed by Mathias, a progressive notary’s clerk. After Madame de Manerville’s death in 1810 the old man grew miserly and deprived his only son, Paul, of resources.

Paul came home from college in 1810 and suffered three years of strict paternal control that stifled his character. Though physically brave, he lacked the will to oppose his father and became timid in society, isolated at his father’s royalist gatherings and unable to connect with peers confined by the old mansion.

To relieve monotony he practiced fencing and riding—accomplishments his father demanded—and read novels. His father’s death freed him: he inherited substantial, well-kept estates but disliked Bordeaux and Lanstrac. He invested in consols, traveled across Europe and served at embassies; years of indulgence and poor management depleted much of his fortune. Resolving to return as a country gentleman, conscious of rank he felt he must marry well. He had wronged no woman and kept a tasteful household. De Marsay warned marriage brings ridicule and responsibility; Paul said he sought companionship and was ready for family duties.

Despite earlier doubts, the Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux in winter 1821 resolved to carry out his plans. Restoring the family mansion and furnishing it in Parisian taste enhanced his reputation for elegance; his manners soon won him a leading place in Bordeaux’s royalist society. An old marquise dubbed him “the pink of fashion,” a phrase liberals mocked and royalists admired.

Paul de Manerville relished attention. Charming, witty, and solicitous toward women, he combined foppery with genuine care for his appearance. Of medium height and graceful in movement, he preferred receiving happiness to bestowing it and sought in marriage the understanding and encouragement he felt he lacked. Young and averse to domestic routine, he nevertheless accepted that provincial life required matrimony and variety. His inclination was sharpened by a secret love for Mademoiselle Evangelista.

Years earlier an affluent Spaniard, Evangelista, had settled in Bordeaux and, through fortune and connections, entered noble salons. His Creole wife, from an illustrious Spanish family, sustained their social standing with extravagant tastes, indulging whims regardless of cost and backed by her devoted husband. After Evangelista’s death in 1813, Madame Evangelista—now a wealthy widow with a beautiful daughter—continued to live lavishly even as her fortune declined.

By Paul’s return Natalie was striking yet unmarried, largely because her mother’s extravagance discouraged suitors; gossip exaggerated the situation, claiming only a prince could afford her. Accustomed to luxury and adulation, she knew little of life beyond privilege. Six months after Paul’s arrival they met at a high-society event and found mutual charm. Madame perceived his interest and hoped he would become her son-in-law; Paul, who had long known of the Evangelista fortune, began to picture Natalie as his future wife, brushing aside warnings with Parisian disdain.

Spending time with the Evangelistas, he felt proud of their company; society soon treated him as Natalie’s suitor. Royalist circles called the match prudent: Natalie’s beauty and her mother’s intelligence and connections made the union seem advantageous. Madame, forty and still striking, retained a reputation for virtue that enhanced her allure. A year after his return people concluded he was courting Natalie, though neither mother nor daughter pressed for marriage. Natalie’s reserve intrigued him; she was attracted but inexperienced, and he, enamored yet cautious, wondered if he could manage someone who might not fully appreciate him.

The narrative widens to note that in French society marriages are often governed by negotiations over marriage contracts—frequently overlooked in literature but crucial in shaping couples’ lives. Those impending discussions will influence Paul and Natalie’s future, foreshadowing social and personal complexities ahead.

In winter 1822 Paul de Manerville sought Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista’s hand through his great-aunt, the Baronne de Maulincour of Médoc. After discussing the dowry with Madame Evangelista, the baroness reported the match likely: Natalie’s dowry would be her patrimony, her mother contributing nothing. She urged Paul to consult Maître Mathias to protect his interests.

Paul, anxious, visited Madame Evangelista, who privately admitted she owed her daughter a large sum from her late husband’s estate and could not pay. Fearing Paul might withdraw and harm Natalie’s prospects, she summoned Maître Solonet, who promised they could conceal the difficulty and craft a favorable contract.

That evening Mathias and Solonet met. Mathias, cautious and traditional, sought to safeguard Paul; Solonet favored practical flexibility. While Paul and Natalie exchanged tender moments at dinner, the notaries debated risk: Mathias warned the family had squandered wealth; Madame Evangelista and Solonet suggested selling her house. They compromised: Madame Evangelista would place an entail on her property, protecting Natalie while limiting Paul’s exposure. The marriage could proceed, but tensions remained.

The next day Elie Magus, in Bordeaux, appraised Madame Evangelista’s jewelry—necklace and earrings at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” at 100,000—warning sales would incur heavy loss. This sparked staff rumors and society gossip that the marriage might be jeopardized. Notaries denied it, but public skepticism, fueled by envy, persisted; Paul’s organizing a hunting party only intensified speculation. Madame de Gyas spread the tales; Natalie and her mother dismissed them as jealous.

To counter rumors and display means, Madame Evangelista planned a grand fête of the camellias and to finalize the contract. At signing the notaries revealed that if Paul died first Natalie would inherit only a fraction of her expected fortune. Struck, Madame Evangelista resolved—despite the personal cost—to protect her daughter’s future, shifting from careless spender to determined strategist and setting the stage for future conflict.

The fête had briefly distracted Paul from his anxieties, which returned that night. He feared Madame Evangelista might have deceived him without Mathias’s intervention, though he comforted himself that in two days Natalie would be his wife and their finances settled. He did not see that his future had already shifted: the astute woman he trusted had become an adversary, contemplating withdrawing her interests from their shared future. He failed to perceive the cunning of his Creole mother-in-law—part European intellect, part tropical passion, part Eastern indifference—awakened by the prospect of losing her daughter. With a day until the marriage, Madame Evangelista realized she could shape the couple’s life: Natalie trusted her completely, and a single conversation might alter their fate.

The next morning, seated on Natalie’s bed, Madame Evangelista recalled their harmonious past and mourned the thought of her daughter bound to a man she might have to obey. Natalie dismissed obedience, expecting love to govern. Madame warned that conjugal love can fade if not guarded and urged Natalie to establish authority from the start. She proposed leaving to avoid complicating the new household and counseled Natalie to feign obedience while subtly asserting dominance.

Their talk became a practical lesson in marital strategy: keep an active social life to hold Paul’s interest; avoid constant proximity that breeds indifference; preserve allure and independence; and keep him entertained so the relationship stays vibrant. Overwhelmed, Natalie sought guidance. Madame promised continued contact and visits but insisted that some separation was necessary for Natalie’s happiness and prospects.

When Paul arrived he tried to hide his unease; Madame noticed and offered to sell her jewelry to support the couple, framing it as a sacrifice for Natalie’s vanity and future. Paul, moved, accepted. Solonet, the notary, reported potential buyers for Madame Evangelista’s property, which could provide a substantial financial boost; Natalie agreed to the sale.

Determined to secure her daughter’s prospects, Madame resolved to step back from Parisian life and support them from afar so the newlyweds could form their own dynamic. As the midnight wedding neared, preparations carried a foreboding air and guests wondered at her absence. After the ceremony she played the grieving mother, extracted promises from Paul to secure her influence, and sent the couple to Paris. Alone with Paul, Natalie began to assert her authority, setting the stage for the complex dynamics of their marriage.

Five years later, in November, Comte Paul de Manerville, cloaked and somber, arrived at his old friend Mathias’s home in Bordeaux. The retired notary had preserved the estate titles though the usufruct was lost; public notices of Paul’s financial ruin embarrassed him. Paul revealed he had left Natalie without her knowledge and intended to sail to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune—no longer considering himself a count, he planned to succeed under another name. He outlined enormous debts from lavish spending and gifts to Natalie, sales and heavy borrowing to sustain their lifestyle.

Despite Mathias’s doubts and hopes about de Marsay’s promised funds, Paul insisted India was his only chance and vowed to return wealthy by forty. The next day Mathias received de Marsay’s bill of exchange confirming some relief, but Paul remained determined to leave.

At sea, seasick and reflective, Paul read letters from Natalie and de Marsay. Natalie’s letter begged support: she pledged her jewels to pay his debts and promised to live at Lanstrac with her mother and economize. De Marsay’s letter detailed Paul’s ruin and exposed the mother-in-law’s profiteering. Confronted with love and treachery, Paul felt anger, helplessness, and remorse as he sailed away from France uncertain of the future.
