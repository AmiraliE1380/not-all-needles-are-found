DEDICATION

(To a Polish Lady.)

CHAPTER I. THE CHALET

In October 1829 the notary Simon Babylas Latournelle walks from Havre to Ingouville with his family and his head clerk Jean Butscha. He instructs his son Exupère to execute a contrived test: after formalities at the Chalet, Exupère will return from a walk and let Modeste overhear him whisper to Dumay, “The young man has come,” to see if she betrays a lover. The Latournelles—especially the pedantic, provincially grand Madame Latournelle—act as chaperones for Modeste under Dumay’s vigilant care.

The Chalet is a small ornamental cottage on the Ingouville ridge, once a porter’s lodge, now a charming dwelling leased to Jean-François Bernard Dumay: a stubborn Breton, former lieutenant, and faithful cashier to the ruined house of Mignon. Vilquin, the villa’s new owner, resents Dumay for clinging to the lease and the Chalet’s bright red jewel-house, but Dumay insists on preserving it as home for Madame Mignon and her daughter Modeste.

CHAPTER II. A PORTRAIT FROM LIFE

When the Latournelles arrive a discreet company is assembled: Gobenheim, a frugal banker; the blind, stately Madame Mignon; her daughter Modeste; the dwarf-like Butscha; Madame Latournelle; and Dumay and his wife. Dumay has been agitated—he has even laid pistols on the chimney and kept watch with his two Pyrenean dogs—because he fears Modeste may be in love. The household’s anxiety and Madame Mignon’s private conviction set a trap, yet Modeste, absorbed in embroidery, seems unaware while others exchange furtive glances.

Modeste is twenty: pale-gold-haired, delicate, thoughtful, reserved. Her beauty is subtle—an inner chastity and poetic sensibility. Devoted to her blind mother, she lives a double life: outwardly dutiful, inwardly absorbed in reading, music, and reverie. Dumay’s intensity springs from an oath to Colonel Charles Mignon, the absent father: to guard Modeste’s honor as if life depended on it.

CHAPTER III. PRELIMINARIES

Dumay’s past: born at Vannes, son of a revolutionary official later executed, he joined the army and became close to Charles Mignon (Comte de La Bastie), a handsome Provençal officer. After the Empire’s fall Mignon became a prosperous merchant in Havre and made Dumay his cashier. Dumay’s loyalty, thrift, and honesty helped build the Mignon fortune; he married an American and was rewarded with the Chalet and a substantial account.

In 1826 the house collapses under commercial reverses. On the eve of sailing abroad to recover their fortunes, Mignon charges Dumay and friends to guard his wife and daughters. The colonel departs, leaving Dumay, Latournelle, and Gobenheim to wind up affairs and protect the family. Their dignified public notice and asset sales discharge creditors, and Madame Mignon and Modeste retire to the Chalet, where Dumay vows to defend Modeste’s purity.

CHAPTER IV. A SIMPLE STORY

Ruin coincides with private tragedy. Bettina Caroline, Modeste’s elder sister—seduced by Charles d’Estourny and abandoned—returned ill after the house’s failure and died young. Her death and the shame of the seduction haunt the family. On her deathbed Bettina implores Modeste: “Never give your heart without your hand; tell everything to papa and mamma,” and leaves a ring engraved “Think of Bettina, 1827,” to be kept until marriage.

After the national crisis and the family catastrophe, Modeste and her mother live quietly at the Chalet amid community conjecture and ridicule. Dumay, obsessive in his charge, keeps night watch, inspects the grounds, and trains his dogs; Madame Dumay and Madame Latournelle act as vigilant chaperones. Household routines and whist games mask Modeste’s inner life, which only the blind mother perceives as subtle signs of expectation; Madame Mignon fears a hidden lover.

CHAPTER V. THE PROBLEM STILL UNSOLVED

One evening the conspirators enact Latournelle’s test. Exupère bursts in, announcing, “The young man is here!” Dumay rushes for his pistols and runs out; the household trembles for Modeste’s safety. She remains calm, saying she will prepare her mother for bed. The trap exposes only Dumay’s fears; nothing is found. The friends debate whether Modeste has a lover. Latournelle and Dumay suggest she may be merely romantic—given to Byron and foreign poets—while Madame Mignon insists by instinct that Modeste is in love. The company oscillates between suspicion, protectiveness, and the comic awkwardness of provincial surveillance.

CHAPTER VI. A MAIDEN’S FIRST ROMANCE

Modeste’s inner life is revealed. After Bettina’s fall and the father’s disgrace, Modeste devoted herself to reading and composing airy romances—an inner theater of imagined lives and loves. She cultivated a Platonic longing for genius: poets and artists, a companion of rare sensibility, not commonplace suitors. Her reading in French, English, and German nourished this idealism; she fasted, prayed, and envisioned a saintly path. Her pride and spiritual hunger made her refuse ordinary proposals.

Her reveries centered on two literary figures. A lithograph of Canalis in a bookseller’s window and a novel by Arthez inflamed her imagination. She resolved to correspond with Canalis, whose lyrics had soothed her. With maid Françoise Cochet’s help she arranged to receive replies “poste restante” at Havre, and wrote to the poet in an ingenuous, ardent note signed “O. d’Este M.”—a trembling overture.

CHAPTER VII. A POET OF THE ANGELIC SCHOOL

Canalis is sketched: compact, urbane, ambitious, vain, yet charming—an official poet sustained by sinecures and patronage. His young secretary, Ernest de La Brière, modest and honest, admires him but perceives his ego.

Canalis and La Brière open Modeste’s letter with different temperaments. Canalis, half-amused and cynical about anonymous affection, composes a long, paternalistic reply warning the correspondent of the dangers of loving a poet, the incompatibility between poetic life and domestic claims, and poets’ vanity and inconsistency. The elegant, sincere prose chills Modeste’s romantic intoxication.

She reads the reply at midnight. The letter dashes her fevered images; she weeps, then moves from despair to anger—convinced of Canalis’s condescension—and resolves to answer in a way that will expose his inconsistency. Thus, by the moonlit sea and a nightingale’s song, she composes a reply kindled less by love than by wounded vanity and the proud resolve of a young romantic who wants to be seen and understood.

CHAPTER VIII. BLADE TO BLADE

To Monsieur de Canalis:

Modeste—writing under the veiled signature O. d’Este M.—challenges Ernest de La Brière, a poet she has captivated in correspondence. After he has written a candid, moral letter to a young woman, she presses him: would his language and sentiments have been the same if he had known she was wealthy—“Mademoiselle O. d’Este M. has six millions”? She asks him to reply without hypocrisy; she can bear frankness.

La Brière reads the note and is conflicted. Paris might call her a coquette, but his conscience resists Parisian levity. Truth, justice, honor pull at him: would money have warped his feelings? He delays six days before answering cautiously. He begins by doubting her name—“You are not a D’Este; that is a feint”—and demands to know her social rank. He argues that ethical behavior is constant in principle, but its observance often varies with station: what is frivolous for a laborer would debase a king; an heiress can behave boldly where a bourgeoise would be ridiculed. Thus he insists on the truth of her class before answering whether wealth would have altered him.

Modeste promptly claims noble birth: a count for a father, genealogical quarterings to satisfy any court. La Brière, though uncomfortable with Parisian curiosity, feels obliged to verify. He covertly follows Françoise Cochet to Ingouville near Havre, watches from a lane, glimpses a girl in the chalet window and the villa, learns the house belongs to the Vilquins and that the d’Hérouvilles are visitors. Struck by her beauty, he returns to Paris and writes a long candid letter.

In that reply he admits temptation: had he known she possessed six million francs, he might have been tempted to flatter for rank and security rather than for love; men often chase fortune for position that preserves worldly security rather than nobility of life. He confesses to following her at Havre and is ashamed of his furtiveness; yet seeing her in person altered him—she might be the rare exception, where ideal and reality coincide. He vows to make his fortune honestly and to choose a companion without cupidity; he counsels modest women to respect social proprieties for the good of a home, though he admires her soul. His frankness and vow to resist worldly temptation relieve Modeste: his admission proves he struggled and chose honor, confirming her suspicion that noble sentiment can survive the lure of riches. She resolves to secure post-office secrecy and to receive his letters herself; she dreams that her father’s return will produce universal happiness.

CHAPTER IX. THE POWER OF THE UNSEEN

To Monsieur de Canalis:

Modeste writes again, delighted by his frankness and insisting on the value of remaining hidden. She denies being the chilly Vilquin or the withered d’Hérouville, explains her mixed German and Provençal blood, and asserts she is noble on both sides—her precautions will keep her incognito. She assures him she is beautiful and not destitute, and warns against any attempt to penetrate her retreat: she is guarded and would not hesitate to defend herself. She wants him to feel the mystery that preserves the romance.

She confesses that she had hoped he would come to her by faith in letters, simply and childlike, but his Havre appearance spoiled that ideal; yet she forgives him because he is Parisian and cannot help being worldly. She reveals that her first letter was not a childish fling but a deliberately cast “net.” She accepts his discussion of family and social rank: she will not surprise or grieve her parents, who trust her, and she has built an inner fortress against mercenary motives. She hates calculating passion and worships the beautiful and ideal without being purely romantic.

Now calling him “friend,” she asks why their alliance should remain unseen. Though his person is unknown, his mind and heart are known and please her; she longs to share confidences with a kindred spirit. She invites a spiritual, mystic companionship: if their souls match, an old servant will one day lead him to whatever dwelling—cottage, villa, castle—she may inhabit. She insists she has not followed him into salons nor watched him jealously; she asks only for a soul-to-soul alliance, to be his solace when he is unhappy, a balm for wounds. Though twenty, she claims maturity beyond her years, knowledge of love’s horrors and delights by sympathy, and an ethical seriousness. Her image of married life is domestic service as reverent devotion—stitching tapestries while her husband is away, the hearth’s divinity—yet she claims the right to test a man’s spirit before consenting to marriage. She cannot join a man who would drag her inner cup of beauty through vulgar life.

Her closing parry is both a threat and a promise: she will be his confidante and, possibly, his wife, but she insists on parental consent and a long moral union before any conventional courtship. She asks him to send the counterpart of his confidences. Her tone is affectionate, playful, and resolute.

La Brière, charmed but alarmed, answers with guarded earnestness. He warns of the danger of enthroning an ideal in a poor poet’s heart only to abandon him if the reality falls short. Letters reveal the inner life—tenderness, intellect, ideals—but daily life requires practical harmonies that are rare. He thanks her for the homage but asks what she expects beyond correspondence: he has pride, vanity, career ambitions, and attachments that might conflict with her ideal. If forced to choose, he fears the prosaic demands of marriage might make it impossible to sustain their dream. Thus with reluctance he edges toward an adieu unless she can reconcile their spiritual union with the facts of ordinary life.

CHAPTER X. THE MARRIAGE OF SOULS

To Monsieur de Canalis:

Modeste replies passionately. She pleads for a continuance of their spiritual communion: can not the friendship of Mademoiselle de Gournay and Montaigne, or Sismondi’s Geneva household, be revived in them? She imagines two hearts and harps answering each other from a distance and disdains the vulgarities of Parisian flattery and fashion. She claims no jealousy of ordinary women, asserts a deep “Alpine” spirituality in her nature that cannot be planted in a window-pot and survive, and proclaims that, if her poet does not personally please, she will never marry; she can live on a life of the heart—always his friend, handmaiden, and collaborator.

She describes her willingness to be a poet’s faithful companion—knitting by lamp-light, waiting, guiding judgment, and secretly aiding his success—rather than the conventional woman of fashion. She scorns marrying for rank or fortune: if free and rich, she will not be won by a peer’s son merely for title nor by a merchant who could ruin their life, nor by a handsome man who would be merely a household tyrant. She insists parental consent will be sought and will not be opposed: her parents trust her instinctive choices. If her poet pleases and is pleased, their love could be built high enough to resist misfortune. She calls herself an eaglet ready to show it in her eyes.

She expands her case against conventional courtship: marriages commonly proceed by etiquette rather than real knowledge—parents arrange meetings, instruct girls to conceal their minds, and couples are praised for outward decorum. She wants marriage to result from a prior long marriage of souls so that, after having tested each other’s virtues and defects through letters, they may face life together. Her method does not bind them personally now; she is aware of illusion’s dangers and accepts that many lovers part. Yet she asks for the enjoyment and truth of their correspondence and awaits his answer.

La Brière cannot remain merely a friend. He writes that he is in love and demands hope. Modeste responds by asking for a plain statement of his day-to-day life so she can judge whether his spirit and domestic reality could harmonize. Her request is decisive: she stakes her future and insists on parental consent. If she finds genius and nobleness in him, she vows devotion, as wife or as devoted domestic companion. Her letter ends with affectionate daring and playful intimacy.

CHAPTER XI. WHAT COMES OF CORRESPONDENCE

Through September their correspondence deepens into a sustained intimacy. Many letters—summarized here—record mutual ardor, confidences, and idealization. For La Brière the letters are treasures; for Modeste they become love. Their epistolary intimacy binds them: she offers devotion and a domestic ideal; he offers poetry, conscience, and pledges to resist worldly temptation. Each becomes the other’s consuming preoccupation.

Modeste proactively sets a test: Canalis is to come to Havre on the next Sunday, attend morning mass, walk the nave in silence with a white rose in his buttonhole, then return to Paris and await her reply. She warns she may not say what he hopes and will not consent without first seeing him. This theatrical condition comes amid family turbulence.

Earlier, household tensions had already arisen. Madame Mignon, frail and nearly blind, fears any shock might kill her husband; she implores Modeste to promise never to leave her father, to allow him to choose a husband after due inquiry, and to keep him informed. Modeste swears she will never marry without her father’s consent and will always remember her dead sister Bettina.

At that instant Dumay, the family’s energetic steward, brings astonishing news: three letters have altered their fortunes; one may restore them. A sea-captain from the China seas delivered a missive from Colonel Charles Mignon—Modeste’s father—declaring he had amassed, through opium and indigo trade, at least seven millions, bought a brig named the Mignon, and intended to return. He sent a large check to Dumay and wants Dumay to be his bailiff, asking that his family be prepared for his arrival yet not told the full story of his wealth until legal arrangements are made. He professes enduring love for his family and hopes to find his lost daughter Bettina; Dumay believes the colonel is hale and in search of her.

Madame Mignon responds stoically, as if inevitable. Modeste, inquisitive, asks how she knew: her mother answers that nothing else could have sent Dumay in such haste. Dumay confirms the colonel’s return and wealth but asks secrecy. He imagines the colonel seeks Bettina; if she is dead, the shock might be fatal on first hearing. He urges Modeste to write to soften any such revelation. Modeste fears untimely encounters between Canalis and Dumay at Havre and hastily asks the poet to postpone any rendezvous. She asserts that her hand is for her family—that she will obey parental authority—while her heart remains her own; she vows to honor sisterly and maternal obligations.

Dumay, reassured by Modeste’s oath and outgoing spirit, smiles; Madame Mignon, though heartened by her husband’s looming return and restored fortune, remains anxious at the possibility of grief over Bettina that might overwhelm him. The family’s sudden reversal promises new options but complicates Modeste’s covert romance: a returned father with great wealth will impact considerations of rank, obligation, and marriage. Meanwhile, the courtship conducted through letters—this private world where two souls have created an ideal union—faces the practical strain of family duty, social realities, and the need for parental approval.

Thus the letters have done what letters often do: they forge a private realm of intimacy that may or may not withstand real life. Modeste, faithful to her promises and mindful of filial duty, will protect her family while secretly tending the “marriage of souls” with the poet—an alliance founded on ideal sympathy but requiring prudence, truth, and the test of actual meeting.

At this moment Modeste, glad at her father’s return yet inwardly despondent like Perrette with broken eggs, paced her room. She had expected her father to bring more than Dumay had announced; nothing short of half the six million she had demanded in her second letter would satisfy her new ambition for the poet. Torn between hope and the grief of comparative poverty, she sat at the piano and let music voice what words could not.

Below, Dumay and his wife stood in the garden under the windows, talking over their sudden fortune and plans. Monsieur and Madame Dumay, childless except for the Mignons, agreed to follow the Comte de La Bastie to Provence and leave their money to whichever of Modeste’s children most needed it. Madame Mignon, listening, teased that Modeste’s airs could only come from a girl in love.

Modeste, inspired, set certain stanzas—later printed in Dauriat’s edition—to music, adapting lines to suit her melody, an innovation that scandalized strict admirers of the poet. The verses spoke of larks waking, flowers reviving, a heart lifting incense to the skies. “It is very pretty,” said Madame Dumay. “Modeste is a musician, and that’s all.” Dumay, however, felt a sudden, dark suspicion. “She loves,” Madame Mignon insisted, and Dumay, infected by her certainty, felt the comfort of the returned fortune vanish.

Disturbed, the Breton returned to Havre and to his desk at Gobenheim’s counting-house heavy-hearted; before dinner he went to Latournelle to confess his fears and seek the notary’s counsel. “I now agree with Madame,” he told the notary on his steps. “I am sure of it; I am dishonored.” The little notary tried to soothe him: a girl in love will betray herself sooner or later; they would outwit her. Dumay resolved not to press his patron’s promise in Paris until he had ascertained Modeste’s heart. All feared, above all, that if the daughter were not pure the father would die of grief learning of Bettina’s death and his wife’s blindness. Dumay’s distress preoccupied the Latournelles; at dinner they turned the problem over and over.

“If Modeste loved anyone in Havre she would have shown fear yesterday,” said Madame Latournelle; “so her lover must live elsewhere.” “She swore to her mother this morning,” added the notary, “in Dumay’s presence, that she had not exchanged a look or word with any living soul.” Butscha, the cripple, then spoke of loving alone and afar—“as far as from here to the stars”—and of being loved for spirit rather than form. His remark, at once whimsical and intense, set the others thinking.

Butscha’s mind, concentrated by deformity, stored and projected feeling in flashes; he had a temperament often attributed to those whose outward lack seems to condense inward power. He was by turns whimsical, malicious, and capable of rare goodness. He resolved, with the anxious solicitude of a hopeless lover, to capture Modeste’s secret by some small surgeon’s probe—an intercepted glance, a quiver, a casual test. That evening he followed his patrons to the Chalet and allied himself with Dumay against Monsieur and Madame Latournelle. During a brief interval when Modeste was absent, the others spoke openly; the poor clerk, depressed by conviction of Modeste’s love, seemed remote.

Moved by the group’s compassion, Butscha poured out his heart. He was the son of a Swedish sailor; his mother had died. “I have no one in the world but you,” he told them, and their sympathy touched him deeply. Madame Mignon praised his affection. Dumay, in a mood of grand promises, cried, “I have six hundred thousand francs, and you shall be a notary and Latournelle’s successor.” The Latournelles, awakened by the mention of money, debated Modeste’s future—why had she not continued music and painting?—and Madame Mignon suggested keeping the secret for the present. If Mr. Mignon learned how faithful Butscha had been, he might not leave provision only to Dumay.

Touched, Butscha kissed Madame Mignon’s hand; his face was wet when Modeste returned. “What are you doing to my Black Dwarf?” she asked, suspicious of his melancholy. Butscha, pretending to be buoyant, announced his impending fortune as if to hide a deeper motive. Then, keeping his eyes on Modeste, he offered an intimate confession—his dream of being loved for his heart and spirit rather than his deformity. He spoke of enriching the one he loved, of writing to a poor beautiful girl to console and protect her, to be a guardian spirit whose gold and thought would make him noble despite his grotesque body. He imagined remaining hidden, telling the woman he was hideous so that when she loved his spirit she would be moved to call even his deformity slight. “If I were loved for the poem of my heart,” he said, “shall I not be happier than the handsomest man?”

Modeste’s color rose; the dwarf read nearly all he sought. “You want to realize Beauty and the Beast,” she said at length, and Butscha answered that transformation was the soul made visible, spirit obliterating form. He resolved to remain hidden if not loved. Within, he whispered in doubt: she corresponds with some great man; how far has it gone?

Next morning Butscha arrived at the Chalet before Madame Latournelle and waited for the postman. Modeste, who habitually watched for post behind her blinds, saw him and ran downstairs. She confronted him with a queenly glance, accusing him of shameless spying. He owned to being a watchdog for her: “They all know you love; but I, I alone, know how. Use me as a vigilant watchdog; I will obey you, protect you, and never bark; neither will I condemn you. I ask only to be of service.” He was proud to be taken into her confidence and even prouder at the thought of being useful to the star he adored.

Modeste, most eager to rid herself of his intrusive watchfulness yet in need of a shield, tested him. She asked whether he had found a letter for her; there was none. She then commanded him to search the hotels in Graville and Havre for a gentleman from England named Monsieur Arthur. Butscha, however, interrupted, perceiving that she had dressed carefully to conceal a meeting: she had wrapped her face, worn a thick veil, omitted her corset—signs to him that she intended to see someone unseen. “You have wrapped your face in cotton-wool and a silk handkerchief, but nothing is the matter with you,” he observed, teasing and astute. He read that she wanted to avoid church and preferred to send him on a pretext. Laughing, he offered instead to take a walk on the seashore for her, claiming he would go to Sainte-Adresse to fetch his master and thus be absent from the church where questions might be asked.

Flushed with surprise and a little aghast at his perspicacity, Modeste pressed him: “How am I to be certain you will obey me?” He pledged obedience; she entrusted him with the key task—watch and reassure Dumay and Latournelle and her mother that no lover had touched her hand. “That hand,” she said, holding up her delicate fingers, “will never be given, it will never even be kissed by what people call a lover until my father has returned.” Her vow was proud and absolute.

Butscha, rapturous at being taken into the service of his goddess, bowed and departed to find his master. Half an hour later Monsieur and Madame Latournelle came to fetch Modeste. She complained of a horrible toothache and that she had not had the courage to dress; yet she had bundled herself up and insisted on going out. Refusing Latournelle’s arm lest he question the outward trembling that betrayed her inward agitation, she marched off beside him. One look, the first, she felt, might decide her fate—would the great poet see and alter everything?

Is there a more delightful moment in a man’s life than a first rendezvous? Such sensations, the nameless pleasures, quicken Ernest de La Briere as he prepares: sharpening razors, laying out his finest shirt, an irreproachable collar, and his best clothes. He deifies the garments and weaves poetic fancies; when guessed by the other party, they take wing. These stirrings in La Briere show that, like many whose lives began in toil, he had never yet been loved.

Arriving at Havre overnight, he slept off fatigue, bathed, and dressed with painstaking care to show himself at his best. This is the moment for a portrait to justify Modeste’s forthcoming letter. Born into a good Toulouse family and allied by marriage to the minister who protected him, Ernest bore the air of breeding and the sedateness of business. Of ordinary height, his face was delicate and sweet, warm in flesh-tints though without color, relieved by a small moustache and an imperial à la Mazarin; without this sign he might have resembled a young woman in disguise. The shape of his face, cut of his lips, and regular ivory teeth were almost feminine; his speech, manner, and gentle blue eyes with their Turkish lids matched the face, so that the minister sometimes called him “Mademoiselle de La Briere.” A full clear forehead framed by abundant black hair was dreamy, in keeping with a generally melancholy cast. The upper eyelid’s prominent arch shadowed his glance, adding a physical sadness; modesty expressed in his whole person.

The oval of his head seemed compressed; hard work had furrowed his brow between eyebrows too thick and too close together, suggesting jealousy. Though slight then, his temperament hinted at later flesh. To a student of French history he might recall Louis XIII—melancholy modesty without cause, pallid beneath the crown, loving war and hunting but hating work, timid with his mistress—whether a Catholic Hamlet or the victim of disease. In Ernest, the undying worm was simple distrust of himself: the timidity of a man to whom no woman had ever said, “Ah, how I love thee!” and an undirected spirit of self-devotion. After his patron’s fall he had clung to Canalis like a rock covered with exquisite mosses, still seeking a power to love. This spaniel-like search for a master gave him outwardly the air of a king who has found his. The play of feeling and a tone of suffering made his face more beautiful than he knew; he resented being classed among the “handsome disconsolate,” a class fallen from fashion as men now trumpet themselves.

For this sacred interview where everything depended on first impression he chose black trousers, polished boots, a sulphur-colored waistcoat showing an exquisitely fine shirt with opal buttons, a black cravat, and a small blue surtout clinging to his shoulders. The ribbon of the Legion of Honor was in his buttonhole. He wore well-fitting kid gloves of Florentine bronze and carried his cane and hat in his left hand with a gesture worthy of the Grand Monarch, showing his carefully arranged hair to the light. He stood in the church porch before service, watching the Christians—more particularly the female Christians—dip their fingers in the holy water.

When Modeste entered, an inward voice cried to her, “It is he!” That surtout and his bearing were essentially Parisian; the ribbon, gloves, cane, and perfume of his hair were not of Havre. La Briere turned to examine the tall imposing Madame Latournelle, the notary, and the bundled Modeste; the poor girl, who had tutored herself for the event, received a violent blow when her eyes rested on this poetic figure, illuminated by the light streaming through the open door. A small white rose nearly hid the Legion ribbon. Would he recognize his unknown mistress muffled in an old bonnet with a double veil? Modeste was so fearful of love’s clairvoyance that she began to stoop like an old woman.

“Wife,” said little Latournelle as they took seats, “that gentleman does not belong to Havre.”

“So many strangers come here,” answered his wife.

“But,” said the notary, “strangers never come to look at a church like ours, which is less than two centuries old.”

Ernest lingered in the porch throughout the service without seeing any woman who realized his hopes. Modeste could not control her trembling until Mass was nearly over. She was in the grasp of a joy only she could depict. At last she heard a gentleman’s footfall on the aisle pavement. After the service La Briere made a circuit of the church, where no one remained but the punctiliously pious; he noticed a prayer-book shaking in the hands of a veiled woman and, seeing Modeste’s dress, confirmed his suspicions. He left with the Latournelles and followed them at a distance to the rue Royale, watching them enter a house accompanied by Modeste, who usually stayed with friends till vespers. He learned the house belonged to Monsieur Latournelle, chief notary of Havre. As he lounged hoping for a glimpse into the house, Modeste caught sight of him and declared herself too ill to go to vespers. Ernest thus had his trouble for his pains. He dared not wander about Ingouville and, true to his point of honor, returned to Paris after writing a letter later delivered by Francoise Cochet with the Havre postmark.

Monsieur and Madame Latournelle customarily dined at the Chalet every Sunday when they brought back Modeste after vespers. As soon as the invalid felt better they set out for Ingouville, accompanied by Butscha. Once home, Modeste forgot her pretended illness and dress and descended to dinner charmingly, humming:

“Nought is sleeping—Heart! awaking,
Lift thine incense to the skies.”

Butscha shuddered at her change; love’s wings had fastened to her shoulders. She seemed a nymph, a Psyche; her cheeks glowed with divine happiness.

“Who wrote the words?” her mother asked.

“Canalis, mamma,” Modeste answered, blushing.

“Canalis!” cried the dwarf, to whom voice and blush made the truth plain. “He, that great poet, does he write songs?”

“They are only simple verses,” she said, “which I have set to German airs.”

“No, no,” Madame Mignon interrupted, “the music is your own, my daughter.”

Modeste slipped into the garden, calling Butscha after her.

“You can do me a great service,” she said. “Dumay is keeping a secret from my mother and me about the fortune my father is bringing back. Didn’t Dumay send papa over five hundred thousand francs when he first went away? Yes. Well, papa is not the kind to stay four years and only double his capital. It seems he is coming back on a ship of his own, and Dumay’s share amounts to almost six hundred thousand francs.”

“There is no need to question Dumay,” said Butscha. “Your father lost about four millions when he went away and has doubtless recovered them. He would give Dumay ten percent of his profits; the worthy man admitted the other day how much it was, and my master and I think the colonel’s fortune must now be six or seven millions—”

“Oh, papa!” cried Modeste, crossing her hands and looking up, “twice you have given me life!”

“Ah, mademoiselle!” said Butscha, “you love a poet. That kind of man is more or less a Narcissus. Will he know how to love you? A phrase-maker, always busy fitting words together, must be a bore. A poet is no more poetry than a seed is a flower.”

“Butscha, I never saw so handsome a man.”

“Beauty is a veil which often hides imperfections.”

“He has the most angelic heart—”

“I pray God you may be right,” the dwarf said, clasping his hands. “That man shall have, as you have, a servant in Jean Butscha. I will not be notary; I shall give that up; I shall study the sciences.”

“Why?”

“To train up your children, if you will deign to make me their tutor. But, oh! if you would only listen. Let me look into the life and habits of this man—find out if he is kind, bad-tempered, gentle, if he commands the respect you merit in a husband, if he can love utterly, preferring you to everything, even his own talent—”

“What does that signify if I love him?”

“Ah, true!” cried the dwarf.

Meanwhile, downstairs, talk swirled. “My daughter saw the man she loves this morning,” Madame Mignon announced.

“Then it must have been that sulphur waistcoat which puzzled you, Latournelle,” said his wife. “The young man had a pretty white rose in his buttonhole.”

“Ah!” sighed the mother, “the sign of recognition.”

“And he also wore the ribbon of an officer of the Legion of Honor. He is a charming young man. But we are all deceiving ourselves; Modeste never raised her veil, and her clothes were huddled on like a beggar-woman’s—”

“And she said she was ill,” cried the notary; “but she has taken off her mufflings and is just as well as ever.”

“It is incomprehensible!” said Dumay.

“Not at all,” said the notary; “it is now as clear as day.”

Madame Mignon, seeking confirmation, asked Modeste if she had seen a well-dressed young man with a white rose in his buttonhole that morning.

“I saw him,” Butscha answered quickly, perceiving the trap that might expose Modeste. “It was Grindot, the famous architect, with whom the town is in treaty for restoring the church. He has just come from Paris, and I met him this morning examining the exterior as I was on my way to Sainte-Adresse.”

“Oh, an architect, was he? he puzzled me,” said Modeste, for whom Butscha had thus bought time to recover composure.

Dumay looked askance at Butscha. Modeste, fully warned, recovered her impenetrable calm. Dumay’s distrust was thoroughly aroused; he resolved to visit the mayor’s office early next morning to ascertain if the architect had been in Havre the previous day. Butscha, on the other hand, was determined to go to Paris and learn more about Canalis.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Gobenheim came to play whist, calming the household’s excitement. Modeste waited for her mother’s bedtime to write; she always wrote at night. Her letter to “Monsieur de Canalis” poured out ecstatic devotion: she condemned painted portraits as false, confessed to being humbled by his beauty, and denied his claims of lonely obscurity—he must have been loved often. Melchior—“my poet, more beautiful than cold Lord Byron”—had made church and sea and roses sing his name. One glance had pierced her veil and given life; music’s purpose was to utter love. She marveled at his genius and beauty, feared such gifts together were too much, and, though he had given her tenderness beyond dreaming, suspected he hid something; jealousy entered her heart and she perversely wished him less handsome.

She recalled trivial details of the day—the church, a ray of sun, a priestly blessing—and fancied the Abbé Marcelin had married them in spirit. She signed herself unmasked, begged him to return, mentioned the house of Mignon in Havre (then under misfortune, the sole heiress’s domain), pleaded he not despise their arms or La Bastie’s motto “Una fides, unus Dominus!” and revealed her name, Modeste, disguised only by the signature “O. d’Este M.” She promised her fortune would be nobly used to ensure their happiness and offered to go with him in a comfortable carriage. The servant he’d glimpsed at the window was herself reborn; love sanctioned by her father’s return had raised her, and she offered to be his Mignon, happier than Goethe’s. She begged him to meet her father in Paris without revealing her passion—leave that to her and God—and to obtain the colonel’s consent. P.S.—do not come to Havre without her father’s consent.

Late that night Dumay intercepted Modeste; she said she’d been writing to her father, who was leaving in the morning. She sealed two letters—one for Canalis, one for her father—but reversed the envelopes. François Cochet, alarmed when he found among mailed letters one addressed to Canalis, brought Modeste a letter and took away the one she had meant for Canalis. The letter he delivered was from Ernest de La Brière, who, ashamed, confessed the veiled woman he had seen might be Modeste and admitted he had been the disguised admirer. Ernest’s letter revealed his yearning and desire to be honest.

Startled, Modeste demanded an explanation from Ernest.

Meanwhile events in Havre complicated matters. Dumay discovered no architect had been in town, Butscha had gone to Paris claiming his father had been found, and Latournelle had seen a shipping notice that Captain Mignon’s ship, the Bettina Mignon, had arrived from Marseilles—so the colonel must be in Paris. Dumay suspected a conspiracy and rushed to Latournelle and then to the Chalet, resolved to pursue Butscha to learn what he knew about Modeste.

Fearing Butscha had been sent to spy on her poet, Modeste fainted with alarm. She had given Dumay the letter she thought addressed to Canalis; Dumay, seeing the address “Monsieur le Baron de Canalis, rue de Paradis-Poissonniere,” cried out. Madame Mignon confirmed the stanzas Modeste set to music were by Canalis and that his portrait hung upstairs. Modeste snatched back her letter and vowed that if Dumay interfered with Canalis she would leave the house forever. Dumay left for Paris in haste—Monte to fetch Canalis or pursue Butscha; the parents and friends rallied round the distraught Modeste.

Modeste confessed everything to her mother and then collapsed between hope and shame. Her mother listened with rebuke and understanding as Modeste read her letters aloud and wept. She insisted Modeste’s love was pure and divine, but feared exposure to vulgar scrutiny and the tearing of her “diaphanous wings.” She fell ill with fever and chills; her mother and friends tended her while Dumay raced to Paris. The narrative shifts to the city.

Ernest de La Brière, modest and scrupulous, was flattered to be thought handsome but horrified she had mistaken him for Canalis, especially when fortune was rumored to lie behind the match. Conscience drove him to confess and to find Modeste’s father in Paris. He sought the banker Mongenod to trace the colonel.

Dumay, a blunt Breton soldier who despised poets, went to Canalis’s house intending to confront a ruffian. There he found evidence of rank: servants, a groom washing a carriage, a valet, and learned that Canalis was president of the Court of Claims attached to the foreign ministry. Canalis was not a garret figure but a man of standing. Dumay, taken aback, allowed Canalis to speak first.

Canalis received Dumay with cool superiority. Dumay accused him of seducing Modeste and demanded his intentions. Canalis declaimed innocence: letters from young admirers were venerated tokens kept in a precious casket. He allowed Dumay to inspect the casket to prove his point. Dumay, outraged but silenced by the poet’s manner, begged for help investigating. Canalis promised assistance when convenient. Dumay left bewildered even as Butscha pursued his own inquiries.

La Brière then entered Canalis’s study and told him that Modeste loved him under the impression it was Canalis and that Modeste’s father supposedly had great wealth. Ernest revealed he was the true correspondent and that conscience had driven him to confess. Canalis, who had in truth forgotten Modeste’s letter, masked disappointment with magnanimity and offered to further Ernest’s hopes—while privately calculating the match’s advantages: rank, influence, and perhaps a peerage. La Brière, idealistic and sincere, swore himself to Modeste.

Colonel Mignon—count of La Bastie—had sunk into grief over the presumed loss of the Bettina and his wife’s infirmity, compounded by Modeste’s secret. He received La Brière and, after reading the letters, corrected a key error: Modeste would not have six million francs as rumored; at most two hundred thousand with uncertain prospects. La Brière, relieved, felt his path cleared. Mignon, impressed by La Brière’s candor, proposed a test: both La Brière and Canalis should come to Havre for the last two weeks of October; Modeste would see the “real” man and the “poet,” the illusion and the substance. Ernest must bring Canalis himself and refrain from revealing the earlier deception. Mignon would go to Havre and expected both to follow. La Brière accepted, humbled but honorable.

Canalis, on reflection, recognized the opportunity to knit rank to genius. Furious at a missed chance, he nevertheless resolved—after pride and calculation—to assist Ernest publicly while privately preparing to compete if chance allowed. Thus both men, for different motives—La Brière from duty, Canalis from ambition—prepared to present themselves in Normandy. Mignon and Dumay set off to Havre; the colonel intended to school his daughter.

At the Chalet the count’s return was greeted warmly. The colonel, tender with his wife, watched Modeste; she, pale but composed, kissed her father. Dumay’s silence and the colonel’s resolve unnerved her. The colonel told Modeste to rise early the next day for a walk by the shore—there would be talk about her poems. On the walk he told her he knew all and more than she did. He reproached her for writing to a stranger and treating marriage as a poem rather than prudence. Modeste defended herself: she had been drawn by genius, not by a random admirer. The colonel insisted family oversight spared girls from romantic illusion and demanded she choose between La Brière—the plain, honorable secretary—and Canalis—the famed poet. To his surprise she swore to obey him in marriage matters. He warned her the man she loved might be a deceiver; she insisted she loved an honest soul. The colonel set his plan: she must see both candidates in the country, among ordinary pursuits, and choose freely. Modeste promised to obey.

The fall from poetic heights left Modeste morose and mortified; her imagination, once the source of idealizing love, found only humiliation in daily life. Yet the shock clarified her mind: malice and discernment rose along with resolve. She took a private, savage pleasure in her suffering and even nursed a desire to humiliate La Brière as revenge on the false Canalis, though her mother counseled courtesy.

A third suitor soon appeared. Latournelle announced the Duc d’Herouville, a titled but impecunious grand equerry and peer of France, had offered himself after seeing Modeste at the Vilquins. Rumor inflated the Mignon fortune, and the duke’s family urged the match. The duke, a thin, pale, timid caricature of faded nobility, brought prestige though little money. Titles still mattered; provincial gossip brimmed with schemes.

Gobenheim and others speculated about Colonel Mignon’s wealth. The colonel, refusing to part with chosen servants, installed them and began living in a way that fed rumor. Soon the arrival of Canalis and La Brière at a villa at Sanvic was announced; they would come to the Chalet the following evening. Canalis, anxious to impress, ordered Germain, his valet, to arrange an imposing household; Germain’s airs and insistence on treating La Brière as a secretary emphasized their social gap. Canalis cultivated visible effects—armorial carriage, servants, complacent indifference—to pique Modeste. La Brière, sincere and despondent, felt like domestic prose facing poetic glamour.

Canalis’s entrance at the Chalet was theatrically studied: pompous postures, declamatory diction, the polished manner honed under the Duchesse de Chaulieu. He lavished homage on war and nation to flatter Dumay and praised Napoleon’s soldiers to win provincial respect. He charmed the women and Modeste with talk of poetry and politics and argued poets could be statesmen. Modeste, resolved to worship him, sat enthralled as at a play. La Brière, modest, felt eclipsed but listened. Canalis exaggerated compliments to La Brière, calling him “Pylades,” and poor Ernest took flattery for candor.

That night on the seashore La Brière brooded. Butscha, the hunchbacked clerk and unofficial guardian of Modeste’s reputation, revealed his loyalty and spoke with surprising eloquence about devotion and the study of women. He claimed to be one of three pledged to serve Modeste and guard her happiness. He knew local intrigues and Germain’s ties; Butscha promised to watch Canalis and test whether the great poet could love truly. La Brière, heartened, treated him as a comrade.

The next morning both Parisian guests rode through the country; each felt the strain between devotion and ambition. Germain bought flamboyant horses as households prepared to stake reputations. Desplein, the famous surgeon, arrived to examine Madame Mignon’s cataract and promised she might regain sight. His brisk competence and a moment of gallantry toward Modeste impressed her—revealing the difference between true skill and Canalis’s pose. The household anticipated the coming contest as Modeste prepared to judge three men—the poet, the honest secretary, and the titled duke—on whether she would choose fame, substance, or rank.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The visit of the great surgeon Desplein was the event of the day and left a vivid impression on Modeste. She admired a man whose life belonged to others, in whom long study of pain had blunted selfishness. That evening, in the Mignon salon—where Gobenheim, the Latournelles, Butscha, Canalis, Ernest de La Brière, and the Duc d’Herouville had gathered—everyone congratulated the family on the hopes Desplein had inspired. Conversation, with Modeste again prominent, turned to the man whose genius, chiefly appreciable by the faculty and scientists, drew praise and an acid remark from Gobenheim: “He makes a mint of money.”

“They say he is very grasping,” Canalis added.

Modeste’s admiration annoyed the poet—vanity behaves like a woman; both feel defrauded when love or praise is given elsewhere. Canalis challenged the notion of genius.

“Do you think, monsieur,” Modeste asked with a smile, “that we should judge genius by ordinary standards?”

“Perhaps we ought first to define genius,” replied Canalis. “One condition is invention—form, system, force. Napoleon invented war; Scott, Linnaeus, Cuvier renewed arts and sciences. Desplein merely applies known laws skillfully; he is an executive genius, like Moscheles or Paganini—performers, not creators. To one belongs the immortal crown, to the other innumerable five-franc pieces.”

Ernest de La Brière opposed such exclusiveness. “Genius must be estimated by utility,” he said. “Parmentier popularized the potato, Jacquard the loom: they change a state. In that sense Desplein is a man of genius; he follows a generation of sufferers whose pains he relieves.”

Modeste countered with an example of purely utilitarian invention—mowing wheat without injuring straw—to mock Ernest’s test. “That is putting utility above art,” she objected. Madame Mignon defended utility: “Without utility what would become of art? What would support it? You must be lodged and paid.” Charles Mignon protested aghast at Modeste’s flippancy toward her father; she retorted saucily, claiming she gave him knowledge in return for life, even suggesting he would soon owe her something.

The debate widened. Canalis rose and delivered a florid defense of art: God did not give art as he did bread; nations live by works of genius that outlast walls and armies. Canalis’s rhetoric, lofty and rhetorical, exalted literature and art as civilization’s summit. But his rhetoric fell flat with the country folk; Butscha attacked the notion that art is anything but an agreeable lie—Happiness and virtue surpass it. The duke joked that he preferred his rubbish. Canalis shifted to argue that works of genius imply social utility—books create jobs, operas employ hands—thus circling back to La Brière’s position in another form.

Modeste’s indifferent support for La Brière’s ideas and her disrespect for her father depressed Ernest. The duke favored Butscha’s anti-art view at times, championing spiritual ecstasies over Byron; Modeste defended broader genius—Byron and Molière benefit the world. Charles Mignon teased his daughter; the exchange widened the gulf between Modeste and Ernest. Canalis’s grand manner and voluptuous speaking charmed Modeste and alienated some; his inattentiveness to others—his habit of dwelling on his own preoccupations—wounded friends.

Modeste asked Canalis to read poems. He recited an imitation of Moore called “Vitalis,” greeted by yawns, and his impatience showed when his volume was flung aside. The episode exposed the peril of a drawing-room hero overreaching his sphere.

Whist began; Modeste sat near Canalis, much to Ernest’s distress. La Brière watched them, lacking the poet’s seductive gift; there is always a comic element in a would-be lover’s attempted feeling. Ernest, gloomy and watchful, became prey to concentrated jealousy; he could not obtain a glance from Modeste and left with Butscha, lamenting that “She is caught by him; Canalis is charming; there’s intellect in his silence, passion in his eyes, poetry in his rhodomontades.”

But is he honest? Butscha asked. La Brière assured him Canalis was loyal and chivalrous, capable of shedding affectations; yet he doubted whether the poet would truly love Modeste. The hunchback encouraged Ernest to show himself as he was and not let his rival practice empty exercises. La Brière confessed he could not speak in her presence; her looks provoked strange physical sensations. He wept as Butscha urged him to reveal himself honestly.

Modeste, followed by Canalis who had lost the rubber, came out to breathe the night air. La Brière left. The next day Modeste favored Canalis and walked alone with him across the gravel. The d’Herouville ladies interrupted, steering conversation to courtly distinctions and the advantage of a ducal son; Canalis, displeased, rebuked the duke for lacking success despite title. Ten days later, the tide at the Chalet turned against Canalis. His prodigality of wit tired those preferring the solid; forced to behave like an ordinary man, his faults were exposed. La Brière, sober and discreet, gained ground: modesty, simplicity, and utility won favor. Canalis’s impatience, caprice, and fits of depression—noticed by Madame Latournelle and others—further alienated him.

The poet worked two days to fascinate Modeste, weaving passionate language; but the d’Herouville ladies slandered him. At one dinner the duke proposed arranging a hunt in a crown forest; glances among the d’Herouville party signaled “The heiress is ours!” The poet, sensing danger, resolved to secure a pledge from Modeste. She, half-frightened, hinted at calumnies and begged him to be silent; he promised.

“These stabs of the tongue are fair in the great world,” he said. “They shock you; I laugh at them. These ladies must feel the duke’s interests are in peril.” He declared himself plainly with vows. Modeste listened, cautious.

“Monsieur,” she said, “freedom granted me by my parents has allowed me to listen to you; but you must speak to them.”

“But,” cried Canalis, “tell me that if I obtain their consent, you will ask nothing better than to obey them.”

“I know beforehand,” she replied, “my father has fancies that may wound your family’s pride. He wishes his name borne by grandsons.”

“Ah, dear Modeste, what sacrifices would I not make to commit my life to an angel like you,” he sighed.

“You will permit me not to decide the fate of my life in a moment,” she said, turning to rejoin the d’Herouville ladies.

Butscha intercepted Modeste on the portico. He feared she’d call Canalis “Melchior.” She teased him: “I can’t say I love you, in the sense men give the word, but what I give you is eternal.” Butscha warmly vowed to guard her like a dragon watches treasure. He advised that Modeste let Canalis play his comedy: “He shall play it through and through within three days—on Wednesday. Until then amuse yourself with the little tunes of your lyre so the discords may come out more distinctly.”

Modeste returned to the salon where La Brière rose respectfully as she approached. He followed her with a furtive look; she sat by Canalis, who would have looked at her more boldly. La Brière and Butscha paced the terrace. Ernest announced he would start for Paris: “From you she will accept anything; let me give her a riding-whip in your name.” He rode to Paris, secured a place on a mail-coach back, and bought a gold fox-head whip-handle set with a ruby—seven thousand francs—engraved with La Bastie’s arms and placed in a morocco case stamped with interlaced M.’s. He returned Wednesday morning to breakfast with Canalis. Butscha met him and instructed Germain the valet to put the box on Modeste’s dressing-table.

Gossip, however, was fermenting. Canalis and Germain spoke apart: fishermen and quaysmen hinted the brig connected with Monsieur Mignon had been sold to another’s account, making the cargo worth only three or four hundred thousand francs at most. Canalis served champagne and treated the talk lightly. Butscha, ever a slanderer, had agitated the fishermen to spread unfavorable whispers, and over wine he poured out scandalous gossip. He declared Modeste might have two or three hundred thousand in ready money and that Dumay’s property might add perhaps six hundred thousand francs when the old soldiers died.

This coarse honesty sobered Canalis. La Brière, bound by Colonel Mignon’s confidence, had not meant to disclose an earlier rumor to Canalis. Pressed, Ernest revealed that the colonel had told him in Paris he did not possess the colossal fortune reported; he intended to give two hundred thousand francs to his daughter in ready money, entailing his estates for descendants to retain the title. Canalis, who had imagined a marriage that would equal his position, was shaken.

“Will you promise me on your word of honor to forget what I tell you and to behave to me as though this confidence had never been made?” Ernest asked.

“I’ll swear that,” Canalis answered.

But inwardly Canalis struggled: “Fool! then take her,” he muttered; “and break your oath.” He feared losing both love and office. Butscha urged him to be direct with Colonel Mignon. Canalis rose to dress for the excursion. Butscha, exultant at having fooled a great man, indulged in triumph.

Modeste appeared in a bottle-green habit, showing the gifted whip. “Did it come from you, Monsieur le duc?” she demanded, handing the handle toward him. “There was a card saying ‘Guess if you can.’ Françoise and Dumay credit Butscha; my father sent his with a turquoise top.”

“I would give ten years of my old age,” said the duke, “to offer you that jewel.”

“Ah, here comes the audacious giver!” cried Modeste as Canalis rode up. “La Brière rode at full gallop from Havre?”

“Does your secretary take such liberties?” she asked, turning pale, and threw the whip to Françoise with scorn. “Give me your whip, papa.”

Canalis, overtaking her, pretended concern for the fatigue of the sender: “The poor fellow said, ‘I have but this one chance to remain in her memory.’” Modeste retorted sharply: “And should you think well of a woman who could take presents from half the parish?”

At a cross-road overlooking the Seine, Canalis let Modeste and the duke pass, then joined the colonel. He spoke plainly: “Monsieur le comte, you are an open-hearted soldier. I am twenty-nine, without landed estates, full of ambition. Mademoiselle Modeste pleases me. The question of money is of the utmost importance. I have about thirty thousand francs a year; if my wife brought me as much more, I should be about equal. Shall you give Mademoiselle a million?”

“We have not reached that point,” the colonel answered cautiously.

“Then suppose we go no further. Give me your word of honor to say nothing, even to Mademoiselle Modeste, because my circumstances may change and I may ask for her without dowry.”

“I promise,” said the colonel. “People exaggerate fortunes. I await accounts of my agents; the sale of my merchandise and ship is not yet concluded. I did say to La Brière in Paris I would guarantee two hundred thousand francs in ready money. I wish to entail my estates and enable grandchildren to inherit my arms and title.”

Canalis barely listened. At the table-land with a view of the Seine valley he remarked philosophically: “Butscha was right; God is the greatest landscape painter.” Modeste commented on fictitious wants and vanities; Canalis called it a bucolic written on tablets of gold. The colonel invited the duke and Canalis to dine in riding-dress. That evening Modeste returned La Brière’s whip: “Please ask Monsieur de La Brière to keep it until you know whether we are rich enough to buy it back.” The colonel kissed her forehead approvingly.

Canalis, alarmed at the indecency of his earlier conduct and its consequences, tried to excuse himself to Modeste, pleading jealousy and the delicacy of a poet’s sensibility. “I attach more value to words and looks than ordinary men,” he said, “and demand infantile security.” Modeste called his demand excessive and rebuked his mention of the duchess. “I admire you, my dear poet,” she said lightly, but punished his presumption. Their intercourse cooled; Canalis left early, and the field opened to the duke. Butscha, walking with Madame Latournelle, whispered that anger would make Modeste more vulnerable to further deception; Madame Latournelle admitted that anger had indeed prevailed.

Word of possible fortune quickly found its way to Paris, reached the Duchesse de Chaulieu, and set another counterplot in motion. Canalis, sleepless and hoping to make amends, composed a letter to the duchess explaining his absence and his interference on La Brière’s behalf. In it he described Mademoiselle Modeste in unflattering terms—“a rather pale, insignificant, thread-papery little thing”—who liked literature and had coquetted with the duchess’s Melchior. He asked Eleonore to verify facts through banker Mongenod about Monsieur Mignon’s fortune and to help La Brière by transmitting a father-in-law’s title. His confidences were too intimate: by airing his doubts and criticizing Modeste’s looks and conduct, he gave the duchess both a wound to repair and a handle to meddle.

Eleonore, wounded and angry at her lover’s tone and his assumed fidelity to another woman, nevertheless quickly turned generous when Mongenod’s inquiry confirmed that Modeste had wealth. If Canalis had not been faithless, she might have aided him; now, finding the girl both pretty and rich, she resolved to act—and to signal her displeasure to Canalis. She wrote a sharp answer: she found Modeste beautiful, Mongenod had proved she had millions, and she blamed Canalis’s want of confidence, calling him a child whose behavior lowered him in the duke’s eyes. She announced she intended to see things for herself and to manage to have him invited to Rosembray.

Canalis flung Eleonore’s letter at Ernest across breakfast in fury. “That’s the two thousandth love-letter I have had from that woman,” he cried. “Marry, try your luck! The worst marriage is better than this halter.” Torn between pride and expediency, bitter at the duchess and jealous of La Brière, Canalis resolved he could either defy her or accept what she offered. The hunt promised to gather the players: it would unite passions and perhaps force a truce among adversaries.

Canalis tried to act smoothly; he even talked finances with Gobenheim and war with the colonel, biasing them subtly toward La Brière. The d’Herouville ladies continued their intrigues to secure Modeste’s dowry. Invitations from the Duc de Verneuil and the Master of the Hunt extended to the Basties to stay at Rosembray for the grand hunt.

La Brière, full of dark presentiments, yearned for Modeste’s presence. The duke came to arrange Modeste’s departure; the colonel invited the suitors to breakfast on the morning of the start. Canalis planned to reconquer Modeste and throw over the duchess; La Brière had resolved to bid eternal farewell; each suitor prepared a last plea. The colonel pressed Modeste for decision.

“Our position with the d’Herouville family will be intolerable at Rosembray,” he said. “Do you mean to be a duchess?”

“No, father,” she answered.

“Then do you love Canalis?”

“No, papa, a thousand times no!” she exclaimed; the colonel rejoiced.

“I chose in Paris the man of fixed principles and sound moralities,” he said slyly, pleased with his choice.

“I was not even thinking of him, papa. Please leave me liberty to refuse the duke myself; after a glimpse of court life at Rosembray I will tell you my secret.”

“Monsieur de La Brière,” cried the colonel, “I hope you are going to this hunt?”

“No, colonel, I have come to take leave; I return to Paris—”

“You have no curiosity,” said Modeste. “If that is all, you must stay to please me; I wish it,” said the colonel, moving away to meet Canalis and leaving daughter and suitor together.

“Mademoiselle,” said La Brière, raising his eyes, “let me carry away with me your forgiveness. My life can never be happy; it will be full of remorse—”

“Before we part forever,” said Modeste, interrupting, “I wish to ask you one thing; though you once disguised yourself, I think you cannot be so base as to deceive me now. Will you be frank?”

La Brière recounted the scene in the poet’s study and the challenge that followed. “You have said enough,” said Modeste, restraining emotion. “If you have not my heart, monsieur, you have at least my esteem.” These words staggered him. Staggering, he leaned against a tree; she left him, then returned, taking his hand. “Forgive me—I thought you despised me,” he whispered.

“But,” she answered, distant, “I did not say that I loved you.” She left him again, but the esteem she’d given filled him with joy. Modeste saw in La Brière’s sensitivity the possibility of a love that might fulfill the ideal every woman hopes for.

Canalis approached later with a torn fragment of Eleonore’s letter that proved Mongenod’s inquiry and the duchess’s intention to marry Modeste to La Brière if Canalis failed. “I cannot let you read the rest,” he said; “but a girl who could accuse me of ignoble motives is capable of suspecting collusion. Ah, Modeste,” he added with tears, “your poet has as much poetry in his heart as in his mind. Suspend judgment until you have seen the duchess.”

“Oh, dear!” she said; “it seems they are all angels—and not marriageable; the duke is the only one who belongs to humanity.”

Butscha reappeared with the whip parcel; Modeste asked her father to have La Brière exchange it for his picture by Van Ostade, thinking he would be at the hunt. Curiously, Modeste’s three lovers each went to Rosembray with hearts full of hope.

Rosembray itself was a fitting stage: bought by the Duc de Verneuil, a château of grand order approached by an avenue of elms, its courtyard of orange-trees and façade with fluted pilasters and a pediment carved by Coysevox. Its park extended to vast ponds, formal gardens, and thirty acres of woodland contiguous with royal forests—a noble setting for the drama to come.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Modeste’s arrival at Rosembray created a sensation. A carriage in royal livery drew up, inspected by the grand equerry; the colonel, Canalis, and La Briere rode beside it, with an outrider, six servants — including Negroes and a mulatto — and the colonel’s britzka for two waiting-women and luggage. The four-horse carriage and polished postilions gave Modeste the feeling of entering a little Versailles. Dazzled and uneasy at meeting celebrated duchesses, she already regretted having wished for a hunt.

She descended to find a calm, plump old man in a blond wig — the Duc de Verneuil — whose face showed a fatherly, almost monastic cheerfulness. The duchess, pious, spare and erect, with an abbess’s grace and the air of Madame Latournelle, received her. Mademoiselle d’Herouville introduced Modeste as “our little angel, Mademoiselle de La Bastie.” “We have heard so much of you,” the duchess said; the duke, with gentle compliments, added his welcome and noted she possessed “more than one treasure.” The grand equerry presented the colonel as Monsieur le Comte de La Bastie.

The duchess led Modeste into a vast salon where a dozen women clustered about the fireplace; the men remained with the duke on the terrace except Canalis, who drifted toward the superb Eleonore de Chaulieu. Eleonore sat at an embroidery frame, teaching Mademoiselle de Verneuil, but her stare at Modeste was cold and haughty. For an instant Modeste perceived only that woman and the small cruelties women can show one another. Her involuntary admiration, which might have disarmed most, only inflamed Eleonore: radiant yet menacingly reserved, a courtesy that masked steel. Canalis’s attempts to soften things slid off, and the duchess’s gracious manner concealed a stern, icy wrath. Modeste’s beauty and fine dress, enough to rival Diane de Maugrigneuse’s, set Eleonore brooding.

All the women had rushed to the window to see the new wonder descend from the royal carriage, attended by her three suitors. Diane’s exclamation — “She is divine! where in the world does she come from?” — stung Madame de Chaulieu, who rebuked the curiosity and returned to her seat composed, while Eleonore’s heart seethed. Mademoiselle d’Herouville murmured to the Duchess de Verneuil that Eleonore received “her Melchior” ungraciously. The duchess explained briefly: the great poet Canalis had gone to Havre on a supposed health trip and left Madame de Chaulieu without a letter for more than two weeks, while she had been trying to secure him preferment — the Legion of Honor and a post at Baden. “Oh, that was shameful in Canalis; he owes everything to her,” Mademoiselle d’Herouville cried.

Modeste, curious, asked why Madame de Chaulieu had not gone to Havre. The duchess answered that Eleonore would “let herself be cut in pieces without a word,” smiling like Mary Stuart after her head was cut off; she added that Diane said Eleonore had made a cutting reply to Canalis’s first letter a few days before. Modeste flushed for the man before her; she neither wished to crush him nor to spare him entirely, but to revenge herself with a malicious act sharper than a dagger. She looked haughtily at Madame de Chaulieu and, daring the tense air, called, “Monsieur Melchior!”

The women sniffed and glanced between the duchess and the girl ill-bred enough to disturb a lovers’ scene. Diane’s nod seemed to encourage Modeste; the group, resentful of a woman of fifty-six still handsome enough to claim youth’s dues, smiled in complicity. Melchior — Canalis — looked at Modeste with feverish impatience and made the gesture of a master to a valet, while Eleonore lowered her head like a lioness disturbed — her eyes throwing red flames toward the poet, each word a triple insult.

“Monsieur Melchior!” Modeste repeated.

He snapped, rose, and stood halfway between the embroidery frame and the fireplace where Modeste sat with the duchess. Bitter reflections entered his ambitious mind as he caught Eleonore’s glance. Obeying Modeste would end everything with his protectress; disobeying would avow his slavery and lose the gains of recent scheming. Modeste’s beauty and money stood against Eleonore’s rights and influence; the choice was as perilous as a matador’s. Canalis, palpitating, seemed near collapse.

Modeste then spoke methodically: Mademoiselle d’Herouville had hurried her from the carriage and she had left behind a handkerchief — and tied into one corner of it, the key of a desk that contained a fragment of an important letter; she asked Canalis to fetch it. Caught between angel and tiger, Canalis nearly complied — the tiger seemed less dangerous — when La Briere appeared at the door like the archangel Gabriel, relieving him.

“Ernest, here, Mademoiselle de La Bastie wants you,” the poet said hurriedly, returning to his chair.

Ernest rushed to Modeste, saw only her, took his commission joyfully, and darted from the room with the secret approbation of every woman present. The duchess hissed to Canalis: “If you speak to her, if you ever look at her, all is over between us. And remember, if I am not present, I leave eyes behind that will watch you.” Then she rose, moved daintily toward Diane de Maugrigneuse’s group, and displayed that majestic ease and sovereign superiority that left the others feeling eclipsed. Though a little too stout for her height, Eleonore retained an exquisite delicacy and a nervous vigor; she bowed to Hélène and her aunt, and in a pure, even tone said to Diane, “Is it not time to dress?” She left with her daughter-in-law and Mademoiselle d’Herouville, murmuring to the old maid, “You are charming,” in thanks for aiding her cause.

Mademoiselle d’Herouville returned to the salon like a spy; her first glance at Canalis told him the duchess’s threat was not empty. The poet discovered that his diplomatic skill was insufficient for such a struggle; his wit would have to find a more honest, humbler stand. When Ernest returned with Modeste’s handkerchief, Canalis seized him and took him out on the terrace.

“My dear friend,” he said, “I am the most unfortunate and ridiculous man in the world; get me out of the hornet’s nest. Modeste is a demon; she sees my difficulty and laughs. She has just spoken to me of a fragment of Madame de Chaulieu’s letter which I foolishly gave her; if she shows it I can never make my peace with Eleonore. Will you ask Modeste to send me back that paper at once, and tell her, from me, that I make no pretensions to her hand? Say I count upon her delicacy and propriety to behave as if we had never known each other. I beg her not to speak to me; I implore her to treat me harshly — even feign jealous anger, which would help my interests. Go; I will wait here for an answer.”

Thus Canalis, torn between ambition and passion, implored Ernest to retrieve a compromising fragment and to beg Modeste’s discretion — a desperate attempt to save his favor with the proud duchess and avert the ruin a single revealed scrap might bring.

On re-entering the salon, Ernest de La Briere found the Vicomte de Serizy, a young officer from Havre who had come from Rosny to announce that Madame must attend the opening of the Chambers. The constitutional solemnity—when Charles X delivered his speech with Madame la Dauphine and Madame in their gallery—made this choice of emissary an attention to Diane, adored by the charming young man, son of a minister, gentleman in ordinary of the chamber, and heir to a large fortune. The Duchesse de Maufrigneuse tolerated his attentions partly to emphasize the age of his mother, Madame de Serizy, who gossip hinted had once captivated Lucien de Rubempre.

“We reckoned without our constitutional government,” said the grand equerry; “and Rosembray, Madame la duchesse, will lose a great honor.”

“We shall be more at our ease,” remarked a tall, thin old man of about seventy-five, wearing his hunting-cap by permission of the ladies. Resembling the Duc de Bourbon, he was the Prince de Cadignan, Master of the Hunt, one of the last great French lords. At that moment a man of thirty-eight, short, fat, and commonplace entered.

“My son, the Prince de Loudon,” announced the Duchesse de Verneuil to Modeste, who could not hide her surprise at seeing so historic a name attached to such an ordinary figure.

“Gaspard,” called the duchess; he came; she motioned, “Mademoiselle de La Bastie, my friend.”

The heir presumptive, recently betrothed to Desplein’s daughter, bowed to the young girl without being struck, as his father had been, by her beauty. Modeste observed the contrast between today’s youth and the older generation: the venerable Prince de Cadignan showed a reverence that seemed homage to womanhood as well as to royalty, while younger men like the Duc de Rhetore affected impertinent manners and bowed cavalierly. Fathers and sons presented a moral difference: fathers retained the politeness of a vanished grandeur; sons no longer felt themselves great and dispensed with the duties of greatness.

Ernest at last slipped a word into Modeste’s ear, and she rose immediately.

“My dear,” said the duchesse, thinking Modeste was going to dress, and pulling a bell-rope, “they shall show you your apartment.”

Ernest accompanied Modeste to the foot of the grand staircase, presenting the entreaty of the luckless poet Canalis and trying to touch her feelings by describing Melchior’s agony.

“You see, he loves—he is a captive who thought he could break his chain.”

“Love in such a rapid seeker after fortune!” retorted Modeste.

“Mademoiselle, you are at the entrance of life; you do not know its defiles. The inconsistencies of a man who falls under the dominion of a woman much older than himself should be forgiven, for he is really not accountable. Think how many sacrifices Canalis has made to her. He has sown too much seed of that kind to resign the harvest; the duchess represents to him ten years of devotion and happiness. You made him forget all that, and unfortunately, he has more vanity than pride; he did not reflect on what he was losing until he met Madame Chaulieu here today. If you really understood him, you would help him. He is a child, always mismanaging his life. You call him a seeker after fortune, but he seeks very badly; like all poets he is a victim of sensations—childish, easily dazzled by anything that shines, and pursuing its glitter. He used to love horses and pictures, and he craved fame—well, he sold his pictures to buy armor and old Renaissance and Louis XV furniture; just now he is seeking political power. Admit that his hobbies are noble things.”

“You have said enough,” replied Modeste; she called to her father with a motion of her head and added, “Come with me, and I will give you that scrap of paper; you shall carry it to the great man and assure him of my condescension to his wishes, but on one condition—you must thank him in my name for the pleasure I have taken in seeing one of the finest German plays performed in my honor. I have learned that Goethe’s masterpiece is neither Faust nor Egmont—” she paused as Ernest looked puzzled, and added: “It is Torquato Tasso! Tell Monsieur de Canalis to re-read it.” Smiling, she continued, “Repeat to your friend word for word what I say; it is not an epigram, it is the justification of his conduct—with this trifling difference, that he will, I trust, become more and more reasonable, thanks to the folly of his Eleonore.”

The duchess’s head-woman conducted Modeste and her father to their apartment, where Francoise Cochet had already put everything in order—an elegance that astonished the colonel, especially after learning there were thirty other such apartments in the chateau.

“This is what I call a proper country-house,” said Modeste.

“The Comte de La Bastie must build you one like it,” replied her father.

“Here, monsieur,” said Modeste, giving the scrap of paper to Ernest; “carry it to our friend and put him out of his misery.”

The phrase “our friend” struck the young man’s heart. He looked at Modeste to see if there was any real bond in the community of interests she seemed to admit; she, understanding his look, added, “Come, go at once, your friend is waiting.”

La Briere colored excessively and left in a state of doubt and anxiety less endurable than despair. For the true lover the path to happiness is like the narrow way Catholic poetry calls the entrance to Paradise—a dark passage echoing with the last cries of earthly anguish.

An hour later the company had gathered in the salon; some played whist, others conversed; the women worked their embroideries, and all awaited dinner. The Prince de Cadignan drew Monsieur Mignon out on China and his campaigns under the empire, making him talk about Provencal families; he blamed him for not seeking service and assured him nothing would be easier than to restore him to his rank as colonel of the Guard.

“A man of your birth and your fortune ought not to belong to the present Opposition,” said the prince, smiling.

This society not only pleased Modeste but educated her in manners she might otherwise have lacked. The sight of Madame de Maufrigneuse and Madame de Chaulieu taught her subtleties a bourgeois would have ridiculed or clumsily imitated. A well-born, well-educated young woman like Modeste fell naturally into connection with these people and perceived at once the differences between the aristocratic world and the bourgeois world, the provinces and the faubourg Saint-Germain. She caught almost imperceptible shadings and saw the grace of the grande dame as something she could acquire. She also noticed her father and La Briere appeared infinitely better in this Olympus than Canalis. The great poet, abdicating his real power—the mind—became nothing more than a courtier seeking a ministry, intriguing for an order, flattering the galaxy. Ernest de La Briere, without ambitions, could be himself; Melchior became, to use a vulgar expression, a mere toady, courting the Prince de Loudon, the Duc de Rhetore, the Vicomte de Serizy, or the Duc de Maufrigneuse, like a man not free to assert himself, unlike Colonel Mignon, proud of his campaigns and Napoleon’s confidence. Modeste noted the strained efforts of this man of talent seeking witticisms or compliments to please grand personages. In short, the peacock plucked out his tail-feathers.

Toward the middle of the evening Modeste sat with the grand equerry in a corner of the salon to end a suit she could no longer encourage without losing self-respect.

“Monsieur le duc, if you really knew me,” she said, “you would understand how deeply I am touched by your attentions. It is because of the profound respect I feel for your character, and the friendship which a soul like yours inspires in mine, that I cannot endure to wound your self-love. Before your arrival in Havre I loved sincerely, deeply, and forever one who is worthy of being loved, and my affection for whom is still a secret; but I wish you to know—and in saying this I am more sincere than most young girls—that had I not already formed this voluntary attachment, you would have been my choice, for I recognize your noble and beautiful qualities. A few words your aunt and sister have said to me as to your intentions lead me to make this frank avowal. If you think it desirable, a letter from my mother shall recall me, on pretense of her illness, tomorrow morning before the hunt begins. Without your consent I do not choose to be present at a fete I owe to your kindness, and where, if my secret should escape me, you might feel hurt and defrauded. You will ask why I have come here at all. I could not withstand the invitation. Be generous enough not to reproach me for what was almost a necessary curiosity. But this is not the chief, not the most delicate thing I have to say you. You have firm friends in my father and myself—more so than perhaps you realize; and as my fortune was the first cause that brought you to me, I wish to say—but without intending to use it as a sedative to calm the grief gallantry requires you to testify—that my father has thought over the affair of the marshes, his friend Dumay thinks your project feasible, and they have already taken steps to form a company. Gobenheim, Dumay, and my father have subscribed fifteen hundred thousand francs, and undertake to get the rest from capitalists who will feel it in their interest to take up the matter. If I have not the honor of becoming the Duchesse d’Herouville, I have almost the certainty of enabling you to choose her, free from all trammels in your choice, and in a higher sphere than mine. Oh! let me finish,” she cried, at a gesture from the duke.

“Judging by my nephew’s emotion,” whispered Mademoiselle d’Herouville to her niece, “it is easy to see you have a sister.”

“Monsieur le duc, all this was settled in my mind the day of our first ride, when I heard you deplore your situation. This is what I have wished to say to you. That day determined my future life. Though you did not make the conquest of a woman, you have at least gained faithful friends at Ingouville—if you will deign to accord us that title.”

This carefully considered address moved the grand equerry to tears; he seized her hand and kissed it.

“Stay during the hunt,” he said; “my want of merit has accustomed me to these refusals; but while accepting your friendship and that of the colonel, you must let me satisfy myself by the judgment of competent scientific men, that the draining of those marshes will be no risk to the company you speak of, before I agree to the generous offer of your friends. You are a noble girl, and though my heart aches to think I can only be your friend, I will glory in that title, and prove it to you at all times and in all seasons.”

“In that case, Monsieur le duc, let us keep our secret. My choice will not be known, at least I think not, until after my mother’s complete recovery. I should like our first blessing to come from her eyes.”

“Ladies,” said the Prince de Cadignan as guests prepared to leave, “if you would be Dianas you must rise with the dawn. The meet is at half-past eight. I have seen women show greater courage than men — but only for a few seconds; you will need resolution to keep on horseback the whole day, except for a breakfast halt, which we shall take, like true hunters, on the nail. Are you still determined to show yourselves trained horse-women?”

“Prince, it is necessary for me to do so,” said Modeste, adroitly.

“I answer for myself,” said the Duchesse de Chaulieu.

“And I for my daughter Diane; she is worthy of her name,” added the prince. “So you all persist? I shall arrange, for the sake of those who stay at home, to drive the stag to the further end of the pond.”

“Make yourself easy, mesdames,” said the Prince de Loudon; “that breakfast ‘on the nail’ will be under a comfortable tent.”

At dawn the next day a gray sky showed blue patches the northwest wind would likely clear. As the party left the chateau, the Master of the Hunt, the Duc de Rhetore, and the Prince de Loudon rode ahead, noting the white chateau against autumn foliage.

“The ladies are fortunate in their weather,” remarked the Duc de Rhetore.

“Oh, in spite of all their boasting,” replied the Prince de Cadignan, “I think they will let us hunt without them!”

“So they might, if each had not a squire,” said the duke.

They reached the open at Rosembray to find a loud altercation. The Prince de Loudon, enamored of English ways, had brought an English hunting establishment and put it under the Master of the Hunt. One of Loudon’s men, a small fair Englishman—insolent, phlegmatic, neatly dressed—had stationed himself on one side. John Barry wore a short scarlet frock bearing the De Verneuil arms, white breeches and top-boots, and carried a small hunting-whip and brass horn. He was first whipper-in to fifteen horses and sixty English hounds, which had cost the Duc de Verneuil a large sum.

Barry found himself forestalled by three French whippers-in in charge of two royal packs brought in carts. They were the Prince de Cadignan’s best huntsmen: swarthy, sinewy, vigorous, wearing broad three-cornered hats, bearing immense horns of Dampierre wound with green worsted. They mainly controlled their hounds by eye and voice. The noble French hounds, marked with white, black, or liver, carried distinct character—young ones yelped impatiently; old scarred hounds lay quiet with ears to the earth.

Royal dogs and huntsmen looked at the Englishman as though to say, “If we cannot hunt by ourselves his Majesty’s service is insulted.” Jest grew fiercer between Monsieur Jacquin La Roulie, the old French whipper-in, and John Barry. The two princes guessed the subject from afar, and the Master of the Hunt, setting spurs to his horse, brought it to an end with authority.

“Who drew the wood?”

“I, monseigneur,” said the Englishman.

“Very good,” said the Prince de Cadignan, and took Barry’s report.

Dogs and men became silent and respectful before the Royal Huntsman. The prince laid out the day’s work; a hunt is like a battle, and the Master of Charles X.’s hounds was their Napoleon. Thanks to the admirable system he had introduced into French venery, he could concentrate on strategy. He assigned the duty of driving the stag to water to Prince de Loudon’s English establishment, expecting the royal hounds to send the stag into the Crown forest on the horizon before the chateau. He soothed his old huntsmen’s pride by giving them the most arduous task, while employing the Englishman at his specialty, letting the two national systems face each other and show their strengths.

“Does monseigneur wish us to wait any longer?” said La Roulie.

“I know what you mean, old friend,” said the prince. “It is late, but—”

“Here come the ladies,” said the second whipper-in.

At that moment the cavalcade of sixteen riders approached, the four ladies in front with green veils. Modeste, accompanied by her father, the grand equerry, and La Briere, rode in advance beside the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, whom the Vicomte de Serizy escorted. Behind them rode the Duchesse de Chaulieu, flanked by Canalis, on whom she smiled without rancor. When they reached the open where the huntsmen in red coats and brass bugles, surrounded by the hounds, made a tableau, the Duchesse de Chaulieu—despite her embonpoint—rode up to Modeste, finding it more dignified not to avoid the young woman to whom she had said nothing the evening before.

When the Master of the Hunt finished his compliments on their punctuality, Eleonore observed the magnificent whip glittering in Modeste’s little hand and graciously asked to see it.

“I have never seen anything more beautiful,” she said, showing it to Diane de Maufrigneuse. “It suits its possessor,” she added, returning it to Modeste.

“You must admit, Madame la duchesse,” answered Mademoiselle de La Bastie, with a tender malicious glance at La Briere, “that it is a rather strange gift from the hand of a future husband.”

“I should take it,” said Madame de Maufrigneuse, “as a declaration of my rights, in remembrance of Louis XIV.”

La Briere’s eyes filled; for a moment he dropped his reins, but a second glance from Modeste bade him not betray his happiness. The hunt began.

The Duc d’Herouville said in a low voice to his fortunate rival, “Monsieur, I hope that you will make your wife happy; if I can be of use to you, command me—I should be glad to contribute to the happiness of so charming a pair.”

The Master of the Hunt’s only anxiety was whether the stag would cross the pond and be killed on the lawn before the house; huntsmen of his caliber foresee checkmates. The old man attained his wish: the run was magnificent, and the ladies were freed from his attendance for the hunt two days hence—which, however, proved rainy.

The Duc de Verneuil’s guests stayed five days at Rosembray. On the last day the Gazette de France announced Baron de Canalis’s appointment as commander of the Legion of Honor and minister at Carlsruhe.

Early in December, after Desplein’s operation, Madame de La Bastie regained her sight; on seeing Ernest de La Briere she pressed Modeste’s hand and whispered, “I should have chosen him myself.”

By late February Latournelle had signed the Provence deeds, and the La Bastie family secured the king’s signatures to the marriage contract and the ordinance transmitting their title and arms to La Briere, who took the name La Briere‑La Bastie. Letters‑patent entailed the La Bastie estate at the end of April. La Briere’s witnesses were Canalis and the minister for whom he had served five years as secretary; Modeste’s were the Duc d’Herouville and Desplein, whom the Mignons remembered gratefully.

We shall again meet Monsieur and Madame de La Briere‑La Bastie; readers will see how sweet and easy married life with an educated woman can be. Modeste, without pedantry, is the pride and joy of her husband, family, and all around her.
