Dedication: To David, Sculptor—Balzac thanks sculpture for its permanence versus fickle fame.

In autumn 1826 Abbé Birotteau, short, corpulent, about sixty and subject to gout, was caught in a rainstorm after visiting a friend. He had hurried across the deserted Cloister behind Saint‑Gatien cathedral, careless of wet shoes because he was elated from winning at cards with Madame de Listomère and hopeful of securing a long‑desired canonry; that ambition consumed him so completely he even neglected his umbrella.

The Cloister, once for Chapter dignitaries, had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette. On its left stood a single house long occupied by priests and kept by the devout, discreet spinster Mademoiselle Gamard, who preserved it through the Restoration by taking two clerical boarders. For twelve years she sheltered Abbé Troubert and the late Abbé Chapeloud; when Chapeloud died Birotteau—who had long admired the apartment’s furnishings—succeeded him.

Chapeloud had been a tactful friend; his death left Birotteau bereaved and relieved. He inherited Chapeloud’s tasteful library and furniture—comforts and status he had envied—and imagined domestic ease, though his gratitude soon reverted to renewed obsession with preferment.

One rainy evening he found the door locked and, after ringing, was told Gamard assumed he was inside. Small omissions—no slippers, no fire—made him feel slighted. Gamard, who had hoped for a salon and craved recognition, nursed a quiet discontent that turned these domestic slights into a developing conflict of pride, naiveté, and provincial intrigue—a prologue to a wider tale.

The next morning Birotteau, absorbed by hopes of a canonry, briefly forgot the previous night’s ill omens. He rang for Marianne, but Abbé Troubert arrived first. This routine visit began with Troubert noting the absent fire, calling for Marianne, and inquiring after Birotteau’s health and the canonry. Unaware that Troubert resented Madame de Listomère for excluding him, Birotteau cheerfully recounted his efforts to obtain the position.

The two abbés contrasted sharply: Troubert, tall, thin, serious, with a sarcastic, wrinkled face and a dulled intellect shaped by subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard; Birotteau, plump, round, and jovial, amiable and popular. Neither seemed likely to rise high—Troubert, at fifty and ill, might at best become vicar-general; Birotteau’s good nature promised comfortable mediocrity. Troubert borrowed the Register of Bishops; carrying the heavy books downstairs they met Mademoiselle Gamard’s sharp disapproval, and she scolded him for cluttering her dining room.

Breakfast was tense. Gamard complained of disturbance and beset Birotteau with reproach; her bitterness, born of resentment and social frustration as an unmarried woman, showed in harsh features and abrupt movements. Oppressed, Birotteau retreated to the garden, then to church duties; returning, he found he had missed dinner—Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting, a petty manipulation to preserve her propriety.

Eight days later Birotteau realized Gamard’s hostility was part of a six-month plot against him. Anxiety worsened his health; he sought Troubert’s help, awkwardly describing provocations. Troubert listened with restrained amusement and advised understanding. Desperate, Birotteau stayed at Madame de Listomère’s country house, unaware Gamard would exploit his absence.

After ten days, Caron, Gamard’s lawyer, demanded clarification to avoid disputes. Under pressure Birotteau signed a deed relinquishing his residence and, he later learned, possessions Gamard claimed as board compensation. Devastated, he fled to Madame de Listomère, then was returned to the Alouette where friends consoled him. Monsieur de Bourbonne examined the deed, condemned the exploit, and urged legal action. The group debated suing to recover property versus compromising to avoid scandal and resolved to support Birotteau. An expert appraisal exposed Gamard’s fraud; as legal proceedings opened, town gossip intensified and the outcome remained uncertain against Gamard and Troubert’s alliance.

After Birotteau’s quarrel, Baron de Listomere—expecting promotion—received a letter warning he might be retired. He hurried to Paris; the minister dismissed it, but inquiries revealed a retirement order awaiting signature. His deputy uncle said the minister thought the baron had sided with Radicals and offended Abbé Troubert; urged to placate the clergy, he resolved to avoid conflict.

Fearing fallout, the family followed Bourbonne’s advice to appease Troubert and distance themselves. The baroness demanded Birotteau withdraw his suit against Gamard and leave her house; he complied, requesting Chapeloud’s portrait, which she promised.

When Gamard fell ill, gossip blamed Birotteau. The baroness offered his withdrawal; Troubert returned with Gamard’s refusal to surrender the portrait, and she accepted. Gamard died, left her fortune to Troubert, who eulogized her while Birotteau mourned alone. Troubert became Bishop of Troyes; despite an annuity from Madame de Listomere, Birotteau was sent to a distant parish, ill and bereft—revealing the moral cost of ambition and clerical power.
