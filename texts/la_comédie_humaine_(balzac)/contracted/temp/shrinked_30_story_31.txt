In the autumn of 1826 Abbé Birotteau, short, corpulent, about sixty and subject to gout, was caught in a rainstorm returning from a friend's house. He hurried across the deserted Cloister behind Saint‑Gatien cathedral, careless of wet shoes because he was in high spirits after winning at cards with Madame de Listomère and buoyed by hope of obtaining a long‑desired canonry. That ambition consumed him so completely he even neglected his umbrella.

The Cloister, once home to Chapter dignitaries, had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette. On its left stood a single house long occupied by priests and owned by the devout, discreet old maid Mademoiselle Gamard, who had preserved the property through the Restoration by taking priests as boarders. She could accommodate only two lodgers; for twelve years she had sheltered Abbé Troubert and the late Abbé Chapeloud. When Chapeloud died, Birotteau—who had long admired the apartment’s furnishings during visits—succeeded him.

Chapeloud had been a close friend; his death left Birotteau both bereaved and relieved. He inherited Chapeloud’s tasteful library and furniture, rewards he had envied and seen as part of the comforts and status he craved. Becoming Gamard’s boarder and securing the canonry seemed to promise those comforts. Installing himself in the rooms he had coveted, he enjoyed imagined attentions and domestic ease; but gratitude soon gave way to renewed obsession with preferment.

One rainy evening he returned to find the house door locked and had to ring repeatedly; his servant Marianne explained Gamard assumed he was already in. He noticed other petty omissions—no slippers waiting, no fire—and felt slighted. These trivial neglects pricked his vanity and suggested intentional disrespect. Innocent and lacking worldly tact, he could not see that the lapses were deliberate.

Mademoiselle Gamard’s discontent had grown during Birotteau’s eighteen months’ tenancy. She prized social recognition and dreamed of a salon—ambitions frustrated by his indifference and frequent evenings out. As he distanced himself, she nursed a quiet vengeance. The contrast between Chapeloud’s tact and Birotteau’s guilelessness turns small domestic grievances into a deeper conflict of pride, naiveté, and provincial intrigue—a prologue to the wider tale.

The next morning Birotteau woke preoccupied with a possible canonry, briefly forgetting the previous night’s ill omens. He rang for Marianne, who did not appear; before he could ring again Abbé Troubert entered after knocking. This monthly visit was expected. Troubert noticed the absent fire, called for Marianne, and inquired about Birotteau’s health and the canonry. Unaware that Troubert resented Madame de Listomère for excluding him, Birotteau cheerfully described his efforts to obtain the position.

The two abbés contrasted sharply. Troubert was tall, thin, serious, with a wrinkled, sarcastic face; Birotteau was plump, round, and jovial. Troubert’s calm exterior masked a dulled intellect some attributed to subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard; Birotteau’s good nature made him popular. Neither seemed likely to rise far—Troubert, at fifty, hampered by poor health and conservative piety, might at best win a vicar-generalship; Birotteau’s amiability kept him comfortably mediocre. Abbé Chapeloud quietly opposed Troubert’s promotion, yet Troubert treated him respectfully despite Chapeloud’s skepticism.

Troubert asked to borrow the Register of Bishops, which Birotteau lent. Carrying the heavy books downstairs, they encountered Mademoiselle Gamard’s sharp disapproval; she scolded Birotteau for cluttering her dining room and stung him with her ill temper, a jarring contrast to his usual cheerfulness.

Breakfast was tense. Gamard complained of being disturbed the previous night; weighed down by her displeasure, Birotteau could not respond. Her bitter demeanor—shaped by resentment and social frustration as an unmarried woman—showed in sharp features and abrupt movements. Feeling oppressed, Birotteau left for the garden, found Gamard and Troubert watching him, escaped into church duties, and on returning discovered he had missed dinner—Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting, a petty manipulation protecting her propriety at his expense.

Eight days later Birotteau realized Gamard’s hostility—visible since the candlestick-and-clock episode—was part of a six-month plot against him. Previously oblivious, he now felt suffocated by her malice as she savored revenge while he, consumed by minor worries, confided in no one.

Anxiety worsened his health, so he sought Troubert’s help. Embarrassed to complain, he described Gamard’s provocations; Troubert listened with restrained amusement and advised understanding her, attributing her behavior to ill health.

Desperate, Birotteau hoped a temporary stay at Madame de Listomère’s country house would ease tensions, not realizing Gamard could exploit his absence. After ten days, Monsieur Caron, Gamard’s lawyer, reported she was alarmed by his prolonged leave and wanted clarity to avoid disputes. Back among friends, gossip and speculation followed. Madame de Listomère suggested Troubert coveted Birotteau’s apartment. Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix warned that the vicar-general’s illness might let Troubert gain appointment power.

Madame de Listomère urged Birotteau to pursue the canonry rather than endure Gamard. Friends proposed yielding to Gamard and Troubert if it aided his advancement. Pressed, Caron returned asking Birotteau to sign a document relinquishing his residence; under pressure he signed.

Despair deepened when he learned he had signed away possessions Gamard claimed as compensation for board differences. Confronted, she coldly cited the agreement. Devastated, he fled to Madame de Listomère; finding his belongings packed, he was taken back to the Alouette by Mademoiselle Salomon, where friends consoled him.

Monsieur de Bourbonne examined the deed, found it exploitative, and urged legal action. The group debated suing to recover property versus compromising to avoid scandal; they resolved to support Birotteau, though Bourbonne warned many would desert him if the fight grew bitter. An expert appraisal exposed the true value of his goods and Gamard’s fraud. As legal proceedings opened, town gossip intensified; the stakes were high and the outcome uncertain against Gamard and Troubert’s alliance.

Days after Birotteau’s quarrel with the old maid, Baron de Listomere—expecting promotion to corvette captain—received a letter warning he might be retired. He rushed to Paris; the naval minister dismissed the fear, but inquiries uncovered a document confirming retirement pending the director’s signature. Desperate, the baron consulted his deputy uncle, who said the minister believed he had sided with Radicals in Tours and had offended the powerful Abbé Troubert, the vicar-general. His uncle urged reconciliation: good relations with the clergy were vital to the baron’s career, so the baron resolved to avoid further conflict.

Three days later, fearing political fallout from supporting Birotteau, the family followed Monsieur de Bourbonne’s advice to appease Troubert and distance themselves from Birotteau. The baroness confronted Birotteau, asking him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house. Distressed, he agreed and requested Chapeloud’s portrait; the baroness promised to negotiate.

Mademoiselle Gamard fell ill and gossip blamed Birotteau’s suit. The baroness met Troubert in a duel of polite insinuations, presented Birotteau’s withdrawal, and Troubert—feigning indifference—was privately pleased. He later returned with Gamard’s refusal to surrender the portrait; the baroness accepted it to keep the peace. Gamard died, leaving her fortune to Troubert, who delivered a grand eulogy while Birotteau mourned alone. Troubert became Bishop of Troyes; Madame de Listomere’s death left Birotteau an annuity, but Troubert’s rise continued to threaten the baron and to overshadow Birotteau’s fate. Ultimately Birotteau was sent to a distant parish, ill and grieving—Troubert’s ascent exposing the moral costs of ambition and clerical power.
