In the autumn of 1826 Abbé Birotteau, short, corpulent, about sixty and prone to gout, was caught in a rainstorm returning from a friend’s house. He hurried across the deserted Cloister behind Saint‑Gatien cathedral, careless of wet shoes after winning at cards with Madame de Listomère and buoyed by hopes of a long‑desired canonry; so preoccupied he even neglected his umbrella.

The Cloister, once home to Chapter dignitaries, had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette. On its left stood a single house long let to priests and owned by the devout, discreet Mademoiselle Gamard, who preserved the property through the Restoration by boarding clerics. She could shelter only two lodgers; for twelve years she had housed Abbé Troubert and the late Abbé Chapeloud. When Chapeloud died, Birotteau—who had long admired the apartment’s furnishings during visits—took his place.

Chapeloud had been a close friend; his death left Birotteau both bereaved and relieved. He inherited Chapeloud’s tasteful library and furniture, comforts he had envied and saw as markers of the status he sought. Becoming Gamard’s boarder and securing the canonry seemed to promise those comforts. Installed in the rooms he had coveted, he enjoyed imagined attentions and domestic ease; but gratitude soon gave way to renewed obsession with preferment.

One rainy evening he returned to find the house door locked and had to ring repeatedly; his servant Marianne explained Gamard assumed he was already in. He noticed other petty omissions—no slippers, no fire—and felt slighted. These trivial neglects pricked his vanity and suggested intentional disrespect. Innocent and lacking worldly tact, he could not perceive that the lapses were deliberate.

Mademoiselle Gamard’s discontent had been growing during Birotteau’s eighteen months of tenancy. She prized social recognition and dreamed of a salon—ambitions frustrated by his indifference and evenings with friends. As he distanced himself, she nursed a quiet vengeance. Absorbed by hopes of preferment, Birotteau confronted symptoms, not causes, turning small domestic grievances into a deeper conflict of pride, naiveté, and provincial intrigue—a prologue to the wider tale.

The next morning Birotteau woke absorbed by hopes of a possible canonry, briefly forgetting the previous night’s ill omens. He rang for Marianne; before he could ring again Abbé Troubert, on his expected monthly visit, entered after knocking. Troubert noticed the absent fire, called for Marianne, and asked after Birotteau’s health and the canonry. Unaware Troubert resented Madame de Listomère for not inviting him to her house, Birotteau described his efforts to obtain the position.

The two abbés contrasted sharply. Troubert was tall, thin, serious, with a wrinkled, sarcastic face and a calm exterior that masked a dulled intellect some blamed on subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard; Birotteau was plump, round-faced, and jovial, his amiability making him popular. Neither seemed likely to rise far—Troubert, fifty and in failing health with pious conservatism, might at best become vicar-general; Birotteau’s good nature kept him comfortable. Though Abbé Chapeloud quietly opposed Troubert’s promotion, Troubert treated him respectfully despite Chapeloud’s skepticism.

Troubert asked to borrow the Register of Bishops, which Birotteau lent. Carrying heavy books downstairs, they met Mademoiselle Gamard’s sharp disapproval; she scolded Birotteau for cluttering her dining room, stinging him with ill temper—a jarring contrast to his usual cheerfulness. Breakfast was tense; weighed down by her displeasure, Birotteau could not engage. Gamard’s bitter demeanor, borne of social frustration and resentment as an unmarried woman, showed in sharp features and abrupt movements. Feeling oppressed, Birotteau left for the garden, found Gamard and Troubert watching him, escaped into church duties, and on returning discovered he had missed dinner—Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting, a petty manipulation protecting her propriety at his expense.

Eight days later Birotteau discovered that Gamard’s hostility—visible since the candlestick-and-clock incidents—was part of a six-month plot against him. Previously oblivious, he now felt suffocated by her malice as she savored revenge while he, absorbed by minor worries, had confided in no one. His anxiety worsened his health, so he sought Troubert’s help. Embarrassed to complain, he described Gamard’s provocations; Troubert listened with restrained amusement and advised understanding her, attributing it to ill health.

Desperate, Birotteau hoped a stay at Madame de Listomère’s country house would ease tensions, not realizing Gamard could exploit his absence. After ten days, Monsieur Caron, Gamard’s lawyer, reported she was alarmed by his prolonged leave and wanted clarity to avoid disputes. Back among friends, speculation followed: Madame de Listomère suggested Troubert coveted Birotteau’s apartment; Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix warned the vicar-general’s illness might put appointment power in Troubert’s hands.

Madame de Listomère urged Birotteau to pursue the canonry. Friends proposed yielding to Gamard and Troubert if it aided advancement. But the struggle intensified. Caron returned asking Birotteau to sign a document relinquishing his residence; pressed by friends, he signed. He then learned he had signed away possessions Gamard claimed as compensation for board differences. Confronted, she coldly cited the agreement. Devastated, he fled to Madame de Listomère and found his belongings packed. Mademoiselle Salomon, fearing for his sanity, took him back to the Alouette, where he confided in friends.

Monsieur de Bourbonne examined the deed, found it exploitative, and urged legal action. The group debated suing to recover property versus compromising to avoid scandal; they resolved to support Birotteau, though Bourbonne warned many would desert him if the fight grew bitter. An expert appraisal exposed the true value of his goods and Gamard’s fraud. As legal proceedings opened, town gossip and political divisions intensified; the outcome remained uncertain against the alliance of Gamard and Troubert.

A few days after Birotteau’s quarrel began, Baron de Listomere—expecting promotion—received a letter warning he might be retired. Alarmed, he went to Paris; the naval minister dismissed him, but inquiries found a retirement document awaiting the director’s signature.

His uncle said the minister believed the baron had sided with Radicals and offended Abbé Troubert, and urged reconciliation: clerical favor was essential. Fearing political fallout, the family followed Bourbonne’s advice to appease Troubert and distance themselves from Birotteau. The baroness demanded he withdraw his suit against Gamard and leave; distressed, he complied and asked for the portrait, which she agreed to try to secure.

Gamard fell ill and gossip blamed the suit. The baroness and Troubert met in a duel of insinuations: she presented Birotteau’s withdrawal; Troubert feigned indifference but was pleased. He later returned with Gamard’s refusal to surrender the portrait; to keep the peace the baroness accepted.

When Gamard died she left her fortune to Troubert, worsening the Listomeres’ plight. At her funeral Troubert eulogized grandly while Birotteau mourned alone. Troubert became Bishop of Troyes. Madame de Listomere’s death left Birotteau an annuity, but Troubert’s rise still threatened the family. Birotteau was sent to a distant parish, ill and grieving.
