Balzac's works, though rich in character, do not depict real individuals. In "Beatrix," he draws on traits from figures like Mademoiselle des Touches, reflecting George Sand, and others inspired by the Comtesse d’Agoult and Liszt. Brittany's towns, with their medieval architecture and salt production, embody a melancholic atmosphere. The du Guaisnic mansion symbolizes noble heritage despite financial struggles, illustrating the connection between environment and identity. As night falls, Gasselin leaves after prayers, while Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel seeks to marry her niece to Calyste du Guenic. The baroness fears modern ideas will corrupt Calyste, who defends Mademoiselle des Touches, a writer navigating societal pressures and the complexities of love.

A few hundred yards from Guerande, Brittany transitions into salt-marshes and dunes, leading to Felicite's country house, Les Touches. Despite losing feudal rights during the Revolution, the estate generates income. Felicite restored the house, keeping its desolate charm.

Calyste, enamored with Felicite, finds solace at Les Touches, where art and music inspire him. His admiration grows despite her rejections. When he discovers her emotional turmoil over love, she suggests he intoxicate Claude to test his feelings for her. Moved, Calyste offers to return her fortune if she cannot accept his love.

The narrative also introduces Beatrix, the Marquise de Rochefide, who struggles with societal expectations and is drawn to composer Gennaro Conti. Calyste, torn between his upbringing and Parisian allure, becomes infatuated with Beatrix. He arrives at Les Touches, feeling overshadowed by her connection with Conti. Overhearing discussions about past feelings leaves him conflicted.

Determined to see Beatrix, he confesses he no longer loves Camille, but her cautious response intensifies their tension, setting the stage for further complications.

Calyste, consumed by thoughts of Beatrix and doubts about Camille, writes her a heartfelt letter, which she rejects due to her commitment to Camille. Despite suggestions to marry Charlotte de Kergarouet for the family estate, Calyste remains devoted to Beatrix. Tensions rise during a dinner confrontation between Beatrix and Camille. 

After professing his love to Beatrix, she cannot fully reciprocate, and an accident deepens their bond. However, Beatrix's ties to Conti force her to leave Calyste, leading to his heartbreak. His family arranges a marriage with Charlotte, which he rejects. Following his father's death, Calyste's despair grows until Camille offers to take him to Paris to find Beatrix, rekindling his hope.

Felicite sells her house to reclaim the du Guenic estates for Calyste, who considers marrying Sabine de Grandlieu, resembling Beatrix. On the wedding day, he initially rejects gifts but later agrees to marry Sabine, who struggles with jealousy and her husband's lingering feelings for Beatrix. Their marriage faces challenges, leading to Sabine's despair and health decline. Meanwhile, Monsieur de Rochefide embraces bachelorhood after Beatrix's departure, navigating social life until he meets Madame Schontz, whose ambitions complicate their relationship.
