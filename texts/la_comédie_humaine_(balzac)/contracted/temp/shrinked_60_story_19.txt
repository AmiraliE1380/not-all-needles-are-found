In the winter of 1829-30, the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu's salon was nearly empty at 1 a.m. She expressed concern about her daughter Camille's feelings for the Comte de Restaud, warning her about his mother, Mlle. Goriot, whose past could jeopardize Camille's future. Despite Restaud's admirable traits, his family background raised alarms.

Derville, a family friend and attorney, overheard their conversation and noted Camille's growing affection for Restaud. He suggested that if Restaud were wealthy, many girls would pursue him. Derville reflected on Gobseck, a wealthy usurer who believed gold was supreme power, contrasting the experiences of a beautiful Countess offering a diamond for debt payment with a struggling linen-draper, Fanny Malvaut, whom he admired.

The Vicomtesse interrupted to offer Derville a drink, and their conversation shifted to Camille's past reliance on Gobseck, who had recently died, leaving M. de Restaud a fortune. Derville, now a licentiate in law, gained Gobseck's respect and became head-clerk, marking a significant career advancement.

When Gobseck sought Derville’s advice on a challenging case, Derville, facing financial trouble, approached Gobseck for a loan to maintain his self-respect. Gobseck agreed but at a high interest rate, and Derville also offered free legal advice as part of their arrangement.

Three months later, Derville became an attorney, successfully recovering estates for Mme. de Grandlieu and marrying Fanny, whose inheritance helped him repay Gobseck. Their shared struggles strengthened their bond.

At a bachelor breakfast, Derville was persuaded to introduce M. de Trailles to Gobseck. When de Trailles sought a loan, Gobseck refused due to his debts. De Trailles returned with a distressed Countess wanting to pawn her diamonds. Gobseck's eagerness to inspect the diamonds revealed his obsession with wealth.

After the Countess accepted Gobseck's loan offer, tensions rose as Maxime insulted Gobseck. Following the Count's death, chaos ensued as the Countess searched for documents, revealing her desperation. The narrator discovered that the Count had hidden important papers, which the Countess inadvertently destroyed. Gobseck, now the rightful owner, contemplated the implications of her actions, leading to reflections on greed, morality, and human relationships.
