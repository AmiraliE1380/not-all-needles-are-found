The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, a narrow street near the Hotel de Ville, housed Madame Crochard and her daughter Caroline, who lived modestly. Despite their poverty, Madame Crochard hoped for a better future for Caroline, who caught the eye of the "Black Gentleman." After two months of silent exchanges, their connection deepened, though they avoided discussing their feelings. Frustrated by Caroline's indecision, Madame Crochard felt bitterness, especially during winter's financial strain. Moved by their situation, the Gentleman secretly provided them with money, which Caroline appreciated.

In May, Caroline invited the Gentleman, now known as Roger, to Montmorency, where they bonded over shared dreams. Their relationship blossomed at a village ball, culminating in a tender kiss. Fast forward to modern Paris, Caroline and Roger live happily, but she feels disappointed when he must attend a meeting. Five years later, Caroline prepares for dinner with Roger and their newborn daughter, Eugenie, while dealing with her ailing mother and the scheming of her friends. Meanwhile, Monsieur de Granville contemplates marrying Mademoiselle Angelique Bontems, reflecting on his childhood feelings for her. The intertwined lives of Caroline, Roger, and Granville explore themes of love and social struggle.

For two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville's engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he observed her character as she prepared her trousseau. Although Angelique rarely discussed religion, she emphasized its importance in raising children, and Granville agreed to respect her practices. After moving to Paris, Angelique chose a large ground-floor apartment near a church, reflecting her strict upbringing.

Granville, focused on his work as a public prosecutor, left home decoration to Angelique, who filled their space with austere furnishings. He soon found the decor cold, contrasting with the vibrant Parisian life he desired. Their early married life seemed harmonious, but Granville grew frustrated as Angelique declined social invitations, leading to conflict when he insisted she attend a ball. Her refusal, influenced by a priest, created a rift, deepening when she sought guidance from the Pope.

As Granville observed Angelique's rigidity, he felt isolated despite having children. Emotional strife marked their household, with Madame de Granville praying for her husband's love while her health deteriorated. In 1822, she learned of Granville's infidelity, shattering her illusions. Confronted, Granville coldly criticized her devotion to religion over their relationship.

In December 1833, Granville encountered young Horace Bianchon, expressing indifference to emotions. He later confirmed a young man named Charles Crochard as his son. The narrative concludes with Granville preparing to leave for Italy, reflecting on familial complexities and the burdens of his past.
