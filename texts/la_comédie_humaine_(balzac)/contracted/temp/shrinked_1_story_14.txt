A DAUGHTER OF EVE

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

                              DEDICATION

To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati.

If you remember the pleasure your conversation gave a traveller by recalling Paris to his memory in Milan, you will not be surprised to find him testifying his gratitude for many pleasant evenings passed beside you by laying one of his works at your feet, and begging you to protect it with your name, as in former days that name protected the tales of an ancient writer dear to the Milanese.

You have an Eugenie, already beautiful, whose intelligent smile promises she has inherited from you the most precious gifts of womanhood, and who will certainly enjoy during her childhood and youth all those happinesses which a rigid mother denied to the Eugenie of these pages. Though Frenchmen are taxed with inconstancy, you will find me Italian in faithfulness and memory. While writing the name of “Eugenie,” my thoughts have often led me back to that cool stuccoed salon and little garden in the Vicolo dei Cappucini, which echoed to the laughter of that dear child, to our sportive quarrels and our chatter. But you have left the Corso for the Tre Monasteri, and I know not how you are placed there; consequently, I am forced to think of you, not among the charming things with which no doubt you have surrounded yourself, but like one of those fine figures due to Raffaelle, Titian, Correggio, Allori, which seem abstractions, so distant are they from our daily lives.

If this book should wing its way across the Alps, it will prove to you the lively gratitude and respectful friendship of

Your devoted servant,
De Balzac.

A DAUGHTER OF EVE

CHAPTER I. THE TWO MARIES

In one of the finest houses in the rue Neuve-des-Mathurins, at half-past eleven at night, two young women sat before the fireplace of a boudoir hung with tender blue velvet. Soft folds of blue cashmere fell over doors and windows; a silver lamp studded with turquoise hung from the ceiling. The decorative system extended to a fluted blue silk ceiling, white cashmere bands caught by ropes of pearl, a warm Belgian carpet with blue posies; carved ebony furniture gave solidity. Etageres held trinkets; a slate-blue marble chimney-piece bore Dresden figures around a platinum clock; above gleamed a Venice mirror. Jardinieres held exotic flowers.

This cold, orderly boudoir, arranged as if for sale, showed none of the gay disorder of a happy home. At present the two young women were weeping. Pain predominated. The owner’s name, Ferdinand du Tillet, one of the richest bankers in Paris, explains the luxury.

Though without rank, Du Tillet had married, in 1831, the daughter of the Comte de Granville, a notable magistrate who became a peer of France after the revolution of July. This marriage of ambition came about by du Tillet’s agreeing to acknowledge in the contract a dowry not received, equal to that of her elder sister, who had married Comte Felix de Vandenesse. Thus the bank repaired rank's breach. Had Comte de Vandenesse foreseen that three years later he would lose his coronet while his father-in-law gained one, he might not have married; but who foresaw the upheavals of 1830?

Bending forward on a low chair, Madame du Tillet pressed to her bosom and occasionally kissed the hand of her sister, Madame Felix de Vandenesse. Society added the baptismal name to the surname to distinguish the countess from her sister-in-law, the Marquise Charles de Vandenesse, widow of the Comte de Kergarouet, Mademoiselle Emilie de Fontaine.

Half lying on a sofa, handkerchief in hand, the countess had been making confidences such as sisters make when they truly love each other; and these two sisters loved tenderly. In an era when sisters in antagonistic spheres might not love one another, a glance at their childhood explains their affection.

Brought up in a gloomy house in the Marais by a narrow-minded “devote” mother, Marie-Angelique and Marie Eugenie de Granville entered marriage (Angelique at eighteen, Eugenie at twenty) without ever leaving the domestic zone where a rigid maternal eye controlled them. They had never been to a play; churches were their theatre. Their education was as rigorous as in a convent: they slept in a room adjacent to their mother’s, the door always open. Time not spent on person-care, religious duties, and necessary studies was given to needlework for the poor or to walks kept as decorous as Sunday promenades.

Their education did not extend beyond the limits imposed by confessors, chosen from the strictest Jansenist clergy. They were delivered to their husbands absolutely pure and virgin; their mother seemed to regard that as the fulfilment of maternal duty. They had never read a tale or heard a romance; their drawings were awkward, their teacher an old maid. A priest instructed them in grammar, French, history, geography, and the little arithmetic deemed necessary. Reading was of “authorized” books, done aloud in the evening in the presence of the confessor; even Fenelon’s Telemaque was thought dangerous.

The Comtesse de Granville meant to make angels of her daughters, but the girls would have preferred a less virtuous, more affectionate mother. Religion, imposed sternly, wearied them; it repressed their natural feelings. Without experience or weapons to meet misfortune, their joys were in each other: whispers at night, secret glances, cloudless skies, flowers, garden walks, finishing an embroidery.

Their mother’s social circle—rigid old women whose talk concerned preachers, confessors, petty indispositions—only darkened their ideas. Men in the salon were cold and resigned, natural sensibilities eaten away by religious egotism. Silent games of cards occupied evenings. Only one figure stood out: a music-master.

The confessors had decided music a Christian art; thus the girls were permitted to study it. At ten the Comte de Granville insisted on a master, and the family consented. The master was a Catholic German who seemed always fifty yet had an infantile gaiety. His iron-gray hair and ecstatic air suggested solemnity but concealed a foolish charm. This man, Schmucke, formerly chapel-master to the Margrave of Anspach, gave himself wholly to his art and the girls. They loved him; after marriage they gave him an annuity. Schmucke had thirty pupils in the Marais; his success showed in new buckles and cleaner linen. He made witty remarks in a German-Gallic patois; his gaiety delighted the two girls.

Accustomed to accompanying their master to the door, they showed him kind words and, in music, a life apart from the maternal jail. Melody, harmony, and composition were a rampart against daily life; Mozart, Beethoven, Gluck, Paesiello, Cimarosa, and Haydn developed a passion that remained chaste but intense. When a work pleased their master they embraced and he called them his Saint Cecilias.

The two Maries were taken to balls only at sixteen, four times a year, and never left their mother’s side without strict instructions. Their dresses were piously irreproachable, muslin up to the chin and sleeves to the wrist, forming Egyptian-like blocks from which tender melancholy heads rose. They felt pity and resented it; what woman does not desire envy?

No dangerous ideas sullied their minds; their hands were rough from work, their health glowing. They left their mother’s house as pure as Eve, obedient to the injunction to obey their husbands. To them, the houses they entered seemed preferable to the maternal convent.

Why did the Comte de Granville not protect the girls from such despotism? He and his wife had separated in effect: he took charge of his sons’ education, leaving the daughters to the mother. He feared masculine minds would be more injured by the mother’s system. The countess regarded her sons as too ill-trained to be intimate with their sisters; communication was strictly watched. The boys breakfasted with their mother and sisters, then left with their father. Except on family festivals, the sisters saw little of their brothers. This domestic disunion is prevalent: modern laws multiply family by family and foster individualism.

In this solitude Angelique and Eugenie saw their father seldom. When he dined at home he wore the grave look of a magistrate. As they matured, they perceived his kind heart beneath the sternness; he sometimes paused in the garden to kiss their foreheads and confide that he would marry them early to relieve their unhappiness. Eugenie’s reply—that they would take the first man who offered—revealed the fruit of such an education.

This stern upbringing explains their marriages. Many men prefer girls from convents and saturated with piety to worldly girls. The two Maries were small and slender, similar in figure. Eugenie was fair-haired like her mother; Angelique dark-haired like her father. Both had pearly complexions and ingenuous expressions. They were well-made; their shoulders would broaden later. On the night this scene opens, the elder, Angelique, was weeping while Eugenie consoled her. Each had nursed a child—one a boy, the other a girl. Eugenie had been treated with special severity as a child and seemed giddy to her mother; Angelique, noble and proud, was later thrown into the upper spheres of Parisian life.

CHAPTER II. A CONFIDENCE BETWEEN SISTERS

Madame de Vandenesse, Marie-Angelique, who seemed broken under troubles too heavy, lay back on a sofa with flowers in her hair and others scattered on the carpet. Tears mingled with pearls on her bosom. She had rushed to her sister’s house after a brief appearance at the Opera. In this luxury her distress seemed horrible.

“Poor darling!” said Madame du Tillet; “what a mistaken idea you have of my marriage if you think that I can help you!”

Hearing the revelation, the countess looked stupefied; her tears stopped.

“Are you in misery as well, my dearest?” she asked softly.

“My griefs will not ease yours.”

“Tell them to me, darling; I am not yet too selfish to listen. Are we to suffer together once more, as we did in girlhood?”

“But alas! we suffer apart,” said the banker’s wife. “You and I live in two worlds at enmity. I go to the Tuileries when you are not there. Our husbands belong to opposite parties. I am the wife of an ambitious banker,—a hard man; while you have a noble, kind, generous husband.”

“Oh! don’t reproach me!” cried the countess. “To understand my position, a woman must have borne the weariness of a vapid and barren life, and have entered suddenly into a paradise of light and love; she must know the happiness of feeling her whole life in that of another; of espousing the infinite emotions of a poet’s soul; of living a double existence; suffering and sharing his griefs, rising on the wings of his pleasures, developing her faculties on a vast stage; and all this while living calm, serene, and cold before an observing world. Ah! dearest, what happiness—to feel life move in a thousand trifles! But youth is brief, and winter comes.”

Madame du Tillet, terrified, covered her face as her sister’s passionate speech poured forth.

“I did not even think of reproaching you, my beloved,” she said at last. “You have cast into my soul more brands than I have tears to quench. Yes, the life I live would justify the love you picture. Let me think that if we had seen one another oftener we should not now be where we are. If you had seen my sufferings, you might have helped me resist my tyrant. Your misery is an incident chance may change; mine is daily and perpetual. To my husband I am a peg on which to hang his luxury and a sign-post of his ambition. He has no real affection for me and no confidence. Ferdinand is hard and polished as that marble,”—striking the chimney-piece—“He distrusts me. Whatever I may want for myself is refused before I ask; but what flatters his vanity he spares no expense. He would lace his children’s swaddling-clothes but never hear their cries. I am covered with diamonds when I go to court, but I have not a hundred francs I can call my own. If the father cares little for his child, he cares less for its mother. He has made me feel that he bought me, and that in marrying me without a dot he was wronged. I might have won him to love me, but there is an outside influence—a widow of a notary—who rules him. I shall never be free so long as he lives. My life is regulated like that of a queen; I give orders only in name. In the midst of this hateful opulence I regret the past and think our mother was kind; at least she left us nights when we could talk, and I lived with a dear being who loved me; whereas here I live in a desert.”

The countess kissed her sister’s hand, weeping.

“How can I help you?” said Eugenie in a low voice. “He would be suspicious. We should be forced to lie to him. But enough of my own miseries; let us think of yours. The forty thousand francs you want would be nothing to Ferdinand, who handles millions with Baron de Nucingen. Often, at dinner in my presence, they talk of financial schemes that make me shudder. They no more mind destroying a man than if he were an animal. I am tempted to cry ‘Beware!’ But if I did, what would become of me? So I keep silence. This splendid house is a cut-throat’s den! Ferdinand is buying the old du Tillet castle to rebuild it, add a forest, and make his son a count. Nucingen is building a palace. His wife is a friend of mine—she might help us; she is bold and has her fortune in her own right.”

“Dear heart, I have but a few hours left; let us go to her this evening, instantly,” cried Madame de Vandenesse, throwing herself into her sister’s arms.

“I can’t go out at eleven o’clock,” replied her sister.

“My carriage is here.”

“What are you two plotting?” said du Tillet, opening the door, his torpid face now showing specious amiability. The sisters had not noticed his arrival. The countess, quick and shrewd from social life, evaded danger.

“I thought my sister richer than she is,” she said, looking at him. “Women are sometimes embarrassed for money, and do not wish to tell their husbands, like Josephine with Napoleon. I came to ask Eugenie a service.”

“She can easily do that, madame. Eugenie is very rich,” replied du Tillet with concealed sarcasm.

“Is she?” replied the countess with a bitter smile.

“How much do you want?” asked du Tillet, eager to entangle his sister-in-law.

“I have told you we do not wish to let our husbands into this affair,” said Madame de Vandenesse cautiously. “I will come to-morrow and talk with Eugenie.”

“To-morrow?” said the banker. “No; Madame du Tillet dines to-morrow with the future peer de Nucingen, who is to leave me his place in the Chamber of Deputies.”

“Then permit her to join me in my box at the Opera,” said the countess.

“She has her own box, madame,” said du Tillet.

“Very good; then I will go to hers.”

“It will be the first time you have done us that honor,” said du Tillet. The countess felt the sting and laughed.

“Well, never mind; you shall not be made to pay anything this time. Adieu, my darling.”

“She is an insolent woman,” said du Tillet, picking up fallen flowers. “You ought,” he said to his wife, “to study Madame de Vandenesse. I’d like to see you before the world as insolent and overbearing as your sister has just been. You have a silly, bourgeois air I detest.”

Eugenie raised her eyes to heaven.

“Ah ca, madame! what have you both been talking of?” he continued, pointing to the flowers. “What has happened to make your sister so anxious to go to your opera-box?”

Eugenie attempted to plead sleepiness and go to her dressing-room, but du Tillet drew her back into the candlelight and said coldly, “Your sister came to borrow forty thousand francs for a man she interests—he’ll be in debtor’s prison within three days.”

She trembled.

“You alarm me,” she said. “But my sister loves her husband too much to be interested in another to that degree.”

“Quite the contrary,” he said dryly. “Girls raised like you have a thirst for liberty; they desire happiness and make bad wives.”

“Speak for me,” said Eugenie bitterly, “but respect my sister. The Comtesse is happy; her husband gives her too much freedom to make her unattached.”

“It is true,” he said, “and I forbid you to have anything to do with it. My interests demand that the man shall go to prison. Remember my orders.”

Madame du Tillet left the room. “She will disobey me, of course, and I shall find out all the facts by watching her,” thought du Tillet. “These poor fools always think they can do battle against us.” He rejoined his wife.

The confidence to Madame du Tillet by Madame Felix de Vandenesse links to points in the latter’s history for the last six years; a succinct account follows.

CHAPTER III. THE HISTORY OF A FORTUNATE WOMAN

Among men whose destiny owed much to the Restoration was Felix de Vandenesse, removed to the Chamber of Peers during Charles X’s last days. This misfortune led him to marriage, as many men marry out of a disgust for gallantry. At thirty he determined to marry, wanting a young girl trained in strict Catholicism. He found in Marie-Angelique de Granville the type he sought. He had suffered under a mother’s despotism and sought, in marriage, a blend of paternal tenderness and conjugal feeling. He knew how to be indulgent and confiding; such men are safe husbands.

Marie-Angelique left maternal purgatory into conjugal paradise in rue du Rocher. Felix made himself her purveyor: he explained life’s things, opened the mysteries of high society to her, taught genealogies, guided dress and taste, took her to theatres, and made her study literature and history. He educated her as a lover, father, master, and husband, without garroting her. After four years he had formed one of the most lovable young women of their time.

Marie-Angelique felt toward Felix the friendship, gratitude, and fraternal love he desired to inspire, mingled with a noble tenderness. She was a good mother. Felix’s delight in shaping her was like an artist’s pleasure in his creation; he enjoyed both the work and its fruition. This mild, well-ordered happiness offers little for literature; it was tender and serene.

In 1833, this edifice began to crumble, weakened at its base without his knowledge. The heart of a woman changes; there are ages in womanhood. Vandenesse had suppressed desire by satisfying every need; sustained happiness stifled desire, that vital engine. Madame de Vandenesse began to feel a power unemployed; monotony bred a craving for some disturbance. She felt a new self. Vandenesse, kindly by nature, did not vary his treatment to rouse desire. He relied on habit; he never sought reward or acknowledgment for his efforts. His patience, taken as temperament, left her wanting something unresolved.

As her intelligence grew, she tried the part of prima-donna. Her husband delighted in her wit. She mingled in conversation: intelligent ideas and delicate observations made her noticed. Success emboldened her. She drew jealousies, notably from her sister-in-law Madame de Listomere, whose patronage turned to envy as Marie shone in society. Others who had known Felix as a lover felt dulled by his marriage and envied the countess. The disasters of July 1830 had disrupted society; by 1833 a few salons reopened, including those where legitimist and new society mingled.

Attached to the exiled family, Vandenesse, faithful to his convictions, took his wife into general society. His former mistresses were surprised by the bride they had deemed childish. Mesdames d’Espard, de Manerville, and Lady Dudley felt jealous but surrounded Marie with friendship and flattery. Felix watched these intimacies and warned her to distrust them. Her social vogue caused coldness in some relations and vexation in the Marquise Charles de Vandenesse, her sister-in-law.

French literature then sought to defend itself against apathy by producing books and dramas of conjugal delinquencies; the lover became a leading literary topic. Women talked of such matters; sometimes the most prudent were the most curious. Where a woman spoke freely of such affairs, she was often herself undecided.

During that winter the Comtesse heard society’s voice and felt its gusts. Her pretended friends—who maintained reputations—sowed seeds in her. When she asked, naively, what difference there was between a lover and a husband, the answers—“We vegetate with a husband, we live with a lover,” “Marriage is purgatory, love paradise,”—piqued her curiosity. She often returned home uneasy and curious, beginning to feel something lacking in her life, though not yet lonely.

CHAPTER IV. A CELEBRATED MAN

The most amusing but mixed society Madame Felix de Vandenesse frequented was that of the Comtesse de Montcornet, who received artists, financiers, writers, testing them rigidly yet admitting them to exclusive contact.

During the winter of 1833 a rising man, Raoul Nathan, after long struggle, forced his way into public gaze and profited from the infatuation for form among Young France. He assumed the singularities of a man of genius, and his outward form harmonized with his mind.

Raoul Nathan would be more singular if left to nature than he is with his studied accompaniments. His worn, haggard face looked as one who had fought with angels or devils; signs of constant struggle appeared in the hollow cheeks and furrowed skull. He was thin and tall, long hair always in disorder, a Byron with heron legs and stiff knees, hands like crab’s claws and long wiry fingers. His Napoleonic blue eyes pierced to the soul; his crooked nose was shrewd; his mouth charming with very white teeth. There was fire and genius in his brow. He made a luminous spot in a salon and attracted all eyes.

He made himself noticed by his negligence: clothes twisted and frayed by effect. He kept one hand in his waistcoat in a pose that ruffled his shirt front. His movements were convulsive; his beard untrimmed; his hair greased whatever it touched. His conversation, caustic and satirical, could abruptly turn sweet and poetic. He was boldly awkward in society, contemptuous of conventions, yet women often liked him; he could charm them into forgetting his singularities.

“Why present yourself like that?” asked the Marquise de Vandenesse.

“Pearls live in oyster-shells,” he answered.

“To another,” she replied, “If I were charming to all the world, how could I seem better still to the one woman I wish to please?”

Raoul’s intellectual disorder matched his appearance. He began as a critic and a great critic but felt cheated by the trade. His articles were as good as books he said. Press and theatre lured him; lacking the slow application for stagecraft, he associated with a vaudevillist who worked his ideas into active pieces. Humiliated, Nathan produced alone a serious drama at the Theatre-Francais which fell amid thunderous articles. He had earlier produced a romantic play that excited the Odeon. This second piece gave him real reputation among connoisseurs.

“Make another failure like that,” said Emile Blondet, “and you’ll be immortal.”

Yet necessity led him to vaudeville and costume plays, dissipating his talent though he always believed in his vein. He had published three novels and kept others in press. One of his novels had obtained brilliant success, which he called the finest book of the period. Raoul complained of art’s exigencies and contributed to judging all creation by the standard of Art. He had begun with verses, then passed to press and theatre, scattering his talent but never losing belief in himself.

Nathan resembled a man of genius; had he marched to the scaffold, as he sometimes wished, he might have struck his brow like André Chénier. Seized with political ambition by the rise of a dozen authors, professors, metaphysicians, and historians who clustered on the machine during the turmoils of 1830 and 1833, he regretted not having written political rather than literary articles. He fancied himself superior to those parvenus, whose success filled him with consuming jealousy. He belonged to that class of minds ambitious of everything, capable of all things, from whom success seems stolen; they dash at a hundred bright points and settle on none, exhausting the goodwill of others.

At this time he drifted from Saint-Simonism toward republicanism, perhaps to return to ministerialism. He searched every corner for a bone to gnaw on, seeking a safe place where he could bark without being kicked and make himself feared. He was mortified to find himself of no account to de Marsay, then at the head of the government, who had no respect for authors—finding neither what Richelieu called a consecutive mind nor continuity of ideas in them—and who, as any minister would, counted on the constant embarrassment of Raoul’s affairs. Necessity would sooner or later force him to accept conditions instead of imposing them.

Raoul Nathan’s real but concealed character matches his public career. He is a comedian in good faith, selfish as if the State were himself, and a very clever orator. None knows better how to play on sentiments, assume false grandeur, deck himself with moral beauty, praise his nature in language, and pose like Alceste while behaving like Philinte. His egotism moves protected by this cardboard armor and often nearly reaches its aim. Lazy to a superlative degree, he does nothing until prodded by the bayonets of need. He is incapable of persistent labor to create a work; but in paroxysms of rage over wounded vanity, or when creditors press, he leaps barriers and attains some triumph of intellect. After which, wearied and surprised at his own creation, he drops into the marasmus of Parisian dissipation; wants become formidable; he lacks strength to face them; and then he descends from his pedestal and compromises.

Influenced by a false sense of grandeur and future—measured against the noble success of a former comrade, one of the few great talents uncovered by the revolution of July—he allows himself, to escape embarrassment, certain laxities of principle with friendly persons. These never surface publicly but remain buried in private life, where they go unnoticed. The shallowness of his heart and the impurity of his hand, which clasps that of all vices, treacheries, and opinions, have made him as inviolable as a constitutional king. Venial sins that would outrage a man of high character are forgiven in him; the world hastens to excuse them. Men who might otherwise despise him shake hands, fearing the day they may need him. He has, in fact, so many friends that he might wish for enemies.

Judged literarily, Nathan lacks style and cultivation. Like many young men hungry for literary fame, he vomits up today what he learned yesterday. He has neither the time nor the patience to write carefully; he does not observe, he listens. Incapable of constructing a solid plot, he sometimes compensates by impetuous ardor of sketching. He “does passion,” as the argot has it; but instead of arousing ideas, his heroes are magnified individuals eliciting only fugitive sympathy; they are not connected with life’s great interests and thus represent nothing. Nevertheless Nathan holds his ground by quickness of mind and those lucky hits billiard-players call a “good stroke.” He is the cleverest shot at ideas on the fly in Paris. His fecundity is not his own but that of his epoch; he lives on chance events and distorts their meaning to control them. In short, he is not true; his presentation is false; as Comte Félix said, he is a born juggler. Moreover, his pen draws ink from an actress’s boudoir.

Raoul Nathan typifies Parisian literary youth—false grandeurs and real misery. He represents that youth by incomplete beauties and headlong falls, by the torrent of existence with sudden reverses and unforeseen triumphs. He is the child of a century consumed with envy—a century with a thousand rivalries under many systems, which feed to their profit that hydra of anarchy that wants wealth without toil, fame without talent, success without effort, but whose vices finally force it, after much rebellion and skirmishing, to accept the budget under the powers that be. When many young ambitions meet at the same point, there is contention of wills, extreme wretchedness, bitter struggles. In this dread battle the most overbearing or the most adroit selfishness wins; it is envied and applauded despite outcries, as Molière noted.

When, as an enemy to the new dynasty, Raoul was introduced in Madame de Montcornet’s salon, his apparent grandeurs flourished. He was accepted as the political critic of the de Marsays, the Rastignacs, and the Roche-Hugons who had risen to power. Émile Blondet, ever hesitating and repugnant to action that concerned only himself, continued the trade of scoffer, took no side, and kept well with all. He was friendly with Raoul, with Rastignac, with Montcornet.

“You are a political triangle,” de Marsay laughed at him at the Opera. “That geometric form, my dear fellow, belongs only to the Deity, who has nothing to do; ambitious men ought to follow curved lines, the shortest road in politics.”

From a distance Raoul Nathan was a fine meteor. Fashion accepted his ways and appearance. His borrowed republicanism gave him the Jansenist harshness assumed by defenders of the popular cause while they inwardly scoff—a quality not without charm to women. Women like to perform prodigies, break rocks, and soften natures that seem of iron.

Raoul’s moral costume matched his clothes. He was fitted to be what he became to the Eve bored in her rue du Rocher paradise—the fascinating serpent, the fine talker with magnetic eyes and harmonious motion who tempted the first woman. No sooner had the Comtesse Marie seen Raoul than she felt an inward emotion that caused a species of terror. The glance of that fraudulent great man exercised a physical influence on her, quivering in her heart and troubling it. But the trouble was pleasure. The purple mantle that momentarily draped Nathan dazzled the ingenuous young woman. When tea was served she rose from a knot of talking women where she had been striving to see and hear that extraordinary being. Her silence and absorption were noticed by false friends.

The countess approached the divan in the center of the room, where Raoul was perorating. She stood with her arm in that of Madame Octave de Camp, an excellent woman who kept the secret of Marie’s involuntary trembling, which betrayed the violent emotion. Though the eyes of a captivated woman shed wonderful sweetness, Raoul was too occupied then in letting off fireworks—epigrams like rockets, flaming portraits drawn in fire—to notice one little Eve’s naive admiration concealed among women. Marie’s curiosity—like that which would send all Paris to the Jardin des Plantes to see a unicorn—intoxicates secondary minds as much as it saddens great ones; Raoul was enchanted by it, though he was then too anxious to secure all women to care especially for one.

“Take care, my dear,” said Marie’s kind companion, “and go home.”

The countess looked at her husband for his arm; Félix took it and led her home.

“My dear friend,” Madame d’Espard whispered Raoul, “you are a lucky fellow. You have made more than one conquest to-night, among them the charming woman who left so abruptly.”

“Do you know what the Marquise meant?” Raoul asked Rastignac, when they were fairly alone between one and two in the morning.

“I’m told the Comtesse de Vandenesse has taken a violent fancy to you. You are not to be pitied!” said Rastignac.

“I did not see her,” Raoul said.

“Oh! but you will see her, you scamp!” cried Émile Blondet, standing by. “Lady Dudley will ask you to her grand ball so you may meet the pretty countess.”

Raoul and Blondet went off with Rastignac, who offered his carriage. All three laughed at the combination of an eclectic under-secretary of State, a ferocious republican, and a political atheist.

“Suppose we sup at the expense of the present order of things?” said Blondet, who wanted to revive suppers.

Rastignac took them to Véry’s, sent away his carriage, and all three sat down to analyze society with Rabelaisian laughs. During the supper, Rastignac and Blondet advised their provisional enemy not to neglect such a capital chance of advancement as this offered. The two “roués” satirically related the history of Madame Félix de Vandenesse, driving the scalpel of epigram and sharp points of wit into that innocent girlhood and happy marriage. Blondet congratulated Raoul on meeting a woman guilty of nothing worse so far than horrible red-chalk drawings, feeble water-colors, slippers embroidered for a husband, sonatas played with best intentions—a girl tied to her mother’s apron-strings till eighteen, trussed for religious practices, seasoned by Vandenesse, and cooked to a point by marriage. At the third bottle of champagne Raoul unbosomed himself as never before.

“My friends,” he said, “you know my relations with Florine; you know my life, and you will not be surprised to hear me say I am ignorant of what a countess’s love may be like. I have often felt mortified that I, a poet, could not give myself a Beatrice, a Laura, except in poetry. A pure and noble woman is like an unstained conscience—she represents us to ourselves in noble form. Elsewhere we may soil ourselves, but with her we are always proud, lofty, and immaculate. Elsewhere we lead ill-regulated lives; with her we breathe the calm, the freshness, the verdure of an oasis—”

“Go on, go on, my dear fellow!” cried Rastignac; “twang that fourth string with the prayer in ‘Moses’ like Paganini.”

Raoul remained silent, eyes fixed, apparently musing.

“This wretched ministerial apprentice does not understand me,” he said after a moment.

So, while the poor Eve in the rue du Rocher went to bed in the sheets of shame, frightened at the pleasure with which she had listened to that sham great poet, these three bold minds trampled with jests over the tender flowers of her dawning love. If women only knew the cynical tone such men, humble and fawning in their presence, take behind their backs—how they sneer at what they claim to adore! Fresh, pure, gracious being—the scoffing jester disrobes and analyzes her; but the more she loses veils, the more her beauty shines.

Marie at that moment compared Raoul and Félix without imagining the danger such comparisons might bring. Nothing presented a greater contrast than the disorderly, vigorous Raoul and Félix de Vandenesse, who cared for his person like a dainty woman, wore well-fitting clothes, had a charming desinvoltura, and practiced English nicety in which Lady Dudley had trained him. Marie, a good and pious woman, soon forbade herself even to think of Raoul, considering herself a monster of ingratitude for making the comparison.

“What do you think of Raoul Nathan?” she asked her husband at breakfast the next day.

“He is something of a charlatan,” Félix replied; “one of those volcanoes easily calmed by a little gold dust. Madame de Montcornet makes a mistake in admitting him.”

This annoyed Marie, especially as Félix supported his opinion with facts he knew of Raoul’s precarious life mixed with a popular actress.

“If the man has genius,” he concluded, “he certainly has neither the constancy nor the patience that sanctify it and make it divine. He strives to impose on the world by placing himself on a level he does nothing to maintain. True, painstaking and honorable talent follows its path courageously; it accepts its pains and penalties and does not cover them with tinsel.”

A woman’s thought is elastic. When she receives a blow she bends, seems crushed, and then renews her natural shape after a time.

“Félix is no doubt right,” she thought.

But three days later she was again thinking of the serpent, recalled by that singular, painful yet sweet emotion the first sight of Raoul had given her. The count and countess went to Lady Dudley’s grand ball, where, by the way, de Marsay appeared socially for the last time; he died about two months later, leaving the reputation of a great statesman because, as Blondet remarked, he was incomprehensible.

Vandenesse and his wife again met Raoul at this ball, which gathered several personages of the political drama who were astonished to find themselves together. It was one of the first solemnities of the great world. The salons presented a magnificent spectacle—flowers, diamonds, and brilliant head-dresses; every resource of the toilette was in play. The ballroom might be compared to a choice conservatory where rich horticulturists collect superb rarities—same brilliancy, same delicacy of texture. Here gauzes like dragonfly wings, crepes, laces, and tulles varied as entomological fantasies; dentelled, waved, scalloped; spider’s webs of gold and silver; silks embroidered by fairy fingers; tropical plumes drooping from haughty heads; pearls twined in braided hair; shot, ribbed, and brocaded silks as if arabesque genius presided over French manufacture—all this luxury harmonized with the beauties collected as if to realize a Keepsake. The eye received impressions of the whitest shoulders, some amber-tinted, others polished; some dewy, some plump and satiny, as though Rubens had prepared their flesh; in short, every shade known in white. Eyes sparkled like onyx or turquoise fringed with dark lashes; faces of varied outline presented graceful types of many lands; foreheads noble or softly rounded, as if thought or will reigned; beautiful bosoms shown boldly or pressed together after various fashions. The prettiest feet pointed for the dance, the slimmest waists encircled in the waltz, stimulated even indifferent gazes. The murmur of sweet voices, the rustle of gowns, the cadence of dances, and the whir of the waltz harmonized with music. A fairy’s wand seemed to command this revelry—melody of perfumes, iridescent lights from crystal chandeliers and candelabra. This assemblage of prettiest women in their prettiest dresses stood out against a gloomy background of men in black coats, among whom one noted the elegant profiles of nobles, the ruddy beards and grave faces of Englishmen, and the gracious faces of the French aristocracy. All the orders of Europe glittered on breasts and necks.

Examining this society carefully, one saw not only brilliant tones, colors, and outward adornment, but a soul—it lived, felt, thought. Hidden passions gave it a physiognomy; mischievous or malignant looks were exchanged; fair and giddy girls betrayed desires; jealous women whispered scandals behind their fans or paid exaggerated compliments. Society, anointed, curled, and perfumed, surrendered to gaiety that went to the brain like a heady liquor. It seemed ideas and sentiments exhaled from foreheads and hearts, condensed, and stirred even the coldest persons present. At the most animated moment of this intoxicating party, in a corner of a gilded salon where bankers, ambassadors, and the immoral old English earl Lord Dudley played cards, Madame Félix de Vandenesse was irresistibly drawn to converse with Raoul Nathan. Possibly she yielded to that ball-intoxication that sometimes wrings avowals from the most discreet.

At sight of such a fête and the splendors of a world in which he had never before appeared, Nathan was stirred by fresh ambition. Seeing Rastignac—whose younger brother had just been made bishop at twenty-seven, whose brother-in-law Martial de la Roche-Hugon was a minister, and who himself was under-secretary of State and about to marry, rumor said, the only daughter of the Baron de Nucingen with an illimitable dot—and seeing in the diplomatic body an obscure writer he had known translating articles for a newspaper turned dynastic since 1830, and professors now made peers of France—he felt anguished to be left behind by advocating the overthrow of this new aristocracy of lucky talent, cleverness crowned by success, and real merit. Even Blondet, so unfortunate and so used by others in journalism but so welcomed here, who could, if he liked, enter public service through Madame de Montcornet's influence, seemed to Nathan a striking example of the power of social relations. Secretly he resolved to play the game of political opinions like de Marsay, Rastignac, Blondet, Talleyrand—the leader of this set; to rely on facts only, turn them to his profit, regard his system as a weapon, and not interfere with a society so well constituted, shrewd, and natural.

“My influence,” he thought, “will depend on the influence of some woman belonging to this class of society.”

With that thought, born of frenzied desire, he fell upon the Comtesse de Vandenesse like a hawk on prey. That charming young woman, in her marabout head-dress producing the delightful “flou” of Lawrence’s paintings and harmonizing with her gentle nature, was pierced by the foaming vigor of this poet wild with ambition. Lady Dudley, who observed everything, aided the tête-à-tête by throwing the Comte de Vandenesse with Madame de Manerville. Strong in her former ascendancy over him, Natalie de Manerville amused herself by leading Félix into mazes of witty teasing, blushing half-confidences, coy regrets cast like flowers at his feet, and recriminations in which she excused herself to be in the wrong.

These former lovers were speaking for the first time since their rupture; while her husband’s former love tried the embers for a spark, Madame Félix de Vandenesse felt those violent palpitations a woman experiences at the certainty of doing wrong and stepping on forbidden ground—emotions not without charm that awaken dormant faculties. Women are fond of using Bluebeard’s bloody key, that fine myth for which we are indebted to Perrault.

The dramatist—who knew his Shakespeare—displayed his wretchedness, related his struggle with men and things, made his hearer aware of his baseless grandeur, his unrecognized political genius, his life without noble affections. Without a single definite word, he contrived to suggest to this charming woman that she should play the noble part of Rebecca in Ivanhoe, love and protect him. It was all, of course, in the ethereal regions of sentiment. Forget-me-nots are not more blue, lilies not more white than the images, thoughts, and radiantly illumined brow of this accomplished artist, who was likely to send his conversation to a publisher. He played his reptile part to this poor Eve so cleverly, made the fatal bloom of the apple dazzling to her eyes, that Marie left the ballroom filled with the species of remorse that resembles hope—flattered in her vanities, stirred in every corner of her heart, caught by her own virtues, allured by her native pity for misfortune.

Perhaps Madame de Manerville had led Vandenesse into the salon where his wife was talking with Nathan; perhaps he had come to fetch Marie and take her home; perhaps his conversation with his former flame had awakened slumbering griefs. Certain it is that when his wife took his arm to leave the ballroom she saw his face sad and his look serious. The countess wondered if he was displeased with her. No sooner were they seated in the carriage than she turned to Félix and said with a mischievous smile,—

“Did I not see you talking half the evening with Madame de Manerville?”

Félix was not out of the tangled paths into which his wife had led him by this charming little quarrel when the carriage turned into their court-yard. This was Marie’s first artifice dictated by her new emotion; she even took pleasure in triumphing over a man who, until then, had seemed to her so superior.

################################################
Between the rue Basse-du-Rempart and the rue Neuve-des-Mathurins, Raoul kept, on the third floor of an ugly narrow house in the Passage Sandrie, a poor, cold, bare lodging ostensibly for the public: literary neophytes, creditors, duns, and other annoying persons whom he kept at the threshold of private life. His real home, existence, presentation before friends, was in the house of Mademoiselle Florine, a second-class comedy actress, where, for ten years, his friends—journalists and authors—disported themselves among equally illustrious actresses. For a decade Raoul had so closely attached himself to this woman that he passed more than half his life with her; he took all his meals at her house unless he invited a friend or had an engagement elsewhere.

To perfect corruption, Florine added a lively wit sharpened by intercourse with artists and practice. It is commonly supposed that people who spend their lives showing things outwardly have nothing within, yet the small number of actors per century has produced many dramatic authors and fascinating women. The criticism that personal sentiment is destroyed by the plastic presentation of passions is refutable: dramatic artists put into their art only gifts of mind, memory, and imagination. Great artists, to quote Napoleon, can cut at will the link Nature has put between senses and thought. Moliere and Talma, in old age, were more in love than most men all their lives.

Accustomed to journalists who guessed by putting two and two together, writers who foresaw, and political personages who watched one another at her house, Florine presented a mixture of devil and angel that made her fitted to receive these roues. They delighted in her cool self-possession; her anomalies of mind and heart entertained them prodigiously. Her house, enriched by gallant tributes, displayed the extravagant magnificence of women who, caring little for cost, valuing objects by caprice—women who would break a fan fit for queens in a passion and scream if a servant smashed a ten-franc saucer.

Florine’s dining-room, filled with distinguished offerings, conveyed this pell-mell of regal and fantastic luxury. The room was paneled in oak with dead-gold lines; panels framed in relief showed children playing with fantastic animals while light touched a sketch by Bixiou, a cast of an angel holding holy water (a present from Francois Souchet), a coquettish painting by Joseph Bridau, a gloomy Spanish alchemist by Hippolyte Schinner, an autograph of Lord Byron to Lady Caroline Lamb framed in ebony, and, facing it, a letter from Napoleon to Josephine. These items were placed without symmetry but with almost imperceptible art. On the exquisitely carved oak chimney-piece stood an apparently Florentine ivory statuette attributed to Michael Angelo, representing Pan discovering a woman under the skin of a young shepherd—the original in Vienna’s royal palace. Renaissance candelabra flanked it. A Boule clock on a tortoiseshell stand inlaid with brass sparkled between two statuettes, doubtless from a demolished abbey. Lamps of royal magnificence awkwardly adapted to Japanese vases occupied corners. On a sideboard was a silver service given by an English lord, and high-relief porcelains; such was the luxury of an actress who had no other property than her furniture.

Her bedroom, all in violet, was a dream: curtains of violet velvet lined with white silk looped over tulle; a ceiling of white cashmere with violet satin rays; an ermine carpet beside the bed; and in the bed, whose curtains resembled an inverted lily, a lantern to read morning plaudits or criticisms. A yellow salon with Florentine-bronze trimmings harmonized with the rest; further detail would resemble an auction poster. For comparisons one might go next door to a certain Rothschild house.

Sophie Grignault, baptized Florine by the theatre, had made her first appearances, despite her beauty, on very inferior boards. Her success and money she owed to Raoul Nathan. This association of fates, usual in dramatic and literary circles, did Raoul no harm; he kept outward conventions of a man of the world. Florine’s means were precarious; salary and leaves barely sufficed for dress and household expenses. Nathan gave her perquisites which he managed to levy as a critic on new enterprises of industrial art. His protection, though gallant, was not regular or solid.

This uncertainty did not alarm Florine; she believed in her talent and beauty. Her robust faith was comic to those who heard her stake her future upon it when remonstrated with.

“I can have income enough when I please,” she used to say; “I have invested fifty francs on the Grand-livre.”

No one could understand how Florine had been obscure for seven years; she had been enrolled as a supernumerary at thirteen and debuted two years later at a shabby boulevard theatre. At fifteen, neither beauty nor talent exist; a woman is all promise. She was now twenty-eight—the age at which French beauties are in their glory. Painters admired the lustre of her white shoulders, tinted with olive at the nape, firm and polished so that light shimmered like watered silk. When she turned her head, superb folds formed about her neck, the admiration of sculptors. She carried on this triumphant neck the small head of a Roman empress—the delicate, round, self-willed head of Pompeia—with features of elegant correctness and a smooth forehead denoting a woman who drives care and reflection away, who yields easily but can balk like a mule. That forehead, as if cut with one chisel-stroke, set off the beauty of her golden hair, raised in front in two equal masses and twisted up behind to prolong the neck’s line.

Black, delicate eyebrows, like a Chinese brush, encircled soft eyelids threaded with rosy fibres. Her pupils, extremely bright though streaked with brown, gave her glance the cruel fixity of a beast of prey and betrayed the cold maliciousness of a courtesan. Her gray eyes, fringed with black lashes—expressing calm contemplative voluptuousness—showed circles beneath them from fatigue, but the artful way she could turn or fix her eyeballs, learned on the stage, made them among the most extraordinary—terrible and soft. Rouge had by then destroyed the diaphanous tints of her cheeks, but the flesh was still delicate; though she could no longer blush or turn pale, she had a thin nose with rosy, passionate nostrils made to express irony—the mocking irony of Moliere’s women-servants. Her sensual mouth, expressive of sarcasm and love of dissipation, was adorned with a deep furrow uniting upper lip and nose. Her chin, white and rather fat, betrayed passion’s violence. Her hands and arms were worthy of a sovereign.

But she bore an ineradicable sign of low birth—short, fat feet. She tried everything short of amputation to remedy them. The feet were obstinate like the Breton race she came from; they resisted all treatment. Florine now wore long boots stuffed with cotton to give length and the semblance of an instep. Her figure was of medium height, threatened with corpulence, yet well-balanced and well-made.

Morally, she was adept at the attitudinizing, quarrelling, alluring, and cajoling of her trade, giving those actions a savor by feigning childlike innocence while slipping philosophical malignities into artless speeches. Apparently ignorant and giddy, she was astute in money matters and commercial law—because she had undergone misery before attaining precarious success. She had come down story by story from garret to first floor through many vicissitudes. She knew life from Brie to pineapples; from garret chores to convoking tribes of pot-bellied chefs. She had lived on credit and not killed it; she was familiar with everything honest women ignore; she spoke all languages: by experience one of the populace, by beauty noble in appearance. Suspicious as a spy, judge, or old statesman, she was hard to impose upon and therefore saw clearly. She knew how to manage tradespeople and evade snares, and was as versed in prices as a public appraiser. Lying on her sofa like a young bride, fresh and white, holding her part and learning it, one would think her a child of sixteen—ingenuous, ignorant, weak, with innocence as her only artifice. Let a creditor enter, and she rose like a startled fawn, swearing a good oath.

“Hey! my good fellow; your insolence is too dear an interest on the money I owe you,” she would say. “I am sick of seeing you. Send the sheriff here; I’d prefer him to your silly face.”

Florine gave charming dinners, concerts, and well-attended soirées where play ran high. Her female friends were all handsome; no old woman ever appeared within her precincts. She was not jealous; jealousy would have seemed an admission of inferiority. She had known Coralie and La Torpille in their lifetimes and now knew Tullia, Euphrasie, Aquilina, Madame du Val-Noble, Mariette—those women who pass through Paris like gossamer through the air, to-day queens, to-morrow slaves. She knew actresses, rivals, and prima-donnas; in short, that whole exceptional feminine society, so kindly, so graceful in its easy sans-souci, which absorbs into its Bohemian life all who allow themselves to be caught in its frantic whirl, eager abandonment, and contemptuous indifference to the future.

Though this Bohemian life displayed itself at her house in tumultuous disorder amid artists’ laughter, the queen of the revels had ten fingers to count on better than any guest. There, secret saturnalias of literature, art, politics, and finance were carried on; desire reigned sovereign; caprice and fancy were as sacred as honor and virtue to a bourgeoise. Blondet, Finot, Etienne Lousteau, Vernou the feuilletonist, Couture, Bixiou, Rastignac in earlier days, Claude Vignon the critic, Nucingen the banker, du Tillet, Conti the composer— in short, that whole devil-may-care legion of selfish materialists—were friends of Florine and of the singers, actresses, and danseuses who collected about her. They hated or liked one another according to circumstances.

This Bohemian resort, whose only ticket of admission was celebrity, was a Hades of the mind, the galleys of the intellect. No one could enter without conquering fortune, doing ten years of misery, strangling two or three passions, and acquiring some celebrity—by books or waistcoats, dramas or fine equipages. Plots were hatched there, means of making fortune scrutinized; all things were discussed and weighed. But every man leaving resumed the livery of his opinions; there he could, without compromising himself, criticise his own party, admit the knowledge and good play of his adversaries, formulate thoughts no one owned to thinking—in short, say all as if ready to do all. Paris alone has such eclectic houses where all tastes, vices, and opinions are received under decent guise. Thus it is not certain Florine would remain to the end of her career a second-class actress.

Florine’s life was not idle or enviable. Many, misled by the stage’s pedestal, suppose an actress lives a perpetual carnival. In the dark recesses of a porter’s lodge, under an attic roof, many a poor girl dreams, on returning from the theatre, of pearls and diamonds, gold-embroidered gowns and sumptuous girdles; she fancies herself adored, applauded, courted; but little she knows of the treadmill life in which an actress is forced to rehearsals under pain of fines, to reading new pieces, and to constant study of new roles. At each representation Florine changed her dress two or three times; often she came home exhausted and half-dead; but before resting she must wash off cosmetics and clean powder from her hair if she had played an eighteenth-century part. She scarcely had time for food. Playing today, she could live no life of her own. Florine often had no time to sup. Returning from a play that now lasted till after midnight, she did not get to bed before two in the morning; yet must rise early to study, try on dresses, breakfast, discuss with the leader of the claque the place for plaudits, pay in cash for last month’s triumphs, and bespeak those of the month ahead. In Saint-Genest’s day, when the canonized comedian fulfilled his duties piously, an actor’s life may not have demanded such activity. Sometimes Florine, seized with a bourgeois desire to go to the country and gather flowers, pretended illness to the manager.

But these mechanical operations are nothing compared with intrigues, pains of wounded vanity—preferences by authors, parts taken away or given to others, exactions of male actors, spite of rivals, nagging stage managers, struggles with journalists—which require more hours of life. Beyond that is the art of acting: the expression of passion, practice of positions and gesture, minute care and watchfulness on stage where a thousand opera-glasses can detect a flaw—labors which consumed Talma, Lekain, Baron, Contat, Clairon, Champmesle. In these infernal coulisses self-love has no sex; the artist who triumphs, man or woman, has all the others against them. As to money, whatever her engagements, Florine’s salary did not cover the costs of her stage toilet, which besides costumes requires immense variety of gloves, shoes, and frippery, exclusive of personal clothing. The first third of such a life is spent struggling and imploring; the next in getting a foothold; the last in defending it. If happiness is frantically grasped, it is because it is rare, long desired, and found amid odious fictitious pleasures and smiles.

For Florine, Raoul’s press power was a protecting sceptre; he spared her many cares. She clung to him less as lover than as prop; she cared for him like a father, deceived him like a husband, and would readily have sacrificed all she had for him. Raoul could and did everything for her vanity as an actress, for the peace of her self-love, and for her stage future. Without a successful author there is no successful actress; Champmesle owed Racine as Mars owed Monvel and Andrieux. Florine could do nothing in return for Raoul, though she wished to be useful and necessary to him. She counted on habit’s charms to keep him; she was always ready to open salons and display the luxury of her dinners for his friends and to further his projects. She desired to be to him what Madame de Pompadour was to Louis XV. All actresses envied Florine’s position, and some journalists envied Raoul’s.

After ten years of lawless Bohemian life—ups and downs, fêtes and sheriffs, orgies and forced sobrieties—Raoul was attracted to the idea of another love: the gentle, harmonious house and presence of a great lady, just as the Comtesse Felix desired intense emotion to break the monotony of happiness. This law of life is the law of all arts, which exist by contrasts. A work done without this incentive is the loftiest genius expression, as the cloister is the highest expression of Christian life.

Returning to his lodging from Lady Dudley’s ball, Raoul found a note from Florine brought by her maid which invincible sleepiness prevented him from reading. He fell asleep, dreaming of the gentle love his life had lacked. Some hours later he opened the note and found important news that neither Rastignac nor de Marsay had allowed to transpire: an indiscretion of a government member had revealed to the actress the coming dissolution of the Chamber after the present session. Raoul instantly went to Florine’s and sent for Blondet. In the actress’s boudoir, feet on the fender, Emile and Raoul analyzed France’s political situation in 1834. On which side lay the best chance of fortune? They reviewed all parties—various republicans, constitutionals, ministerial conservatives and absolutists; and various Rights: aristocratic, legitimist, henriquinquist, and Carlist. Between the party of resistance and that of action there was no discussion; they might as well have hesitated between life and death.

A swarm of newspapers promoted every shade of opinion. Blondet, Raoul Nathan’s judicious but impractical friend, advised him not to apostatize abruptly and counseled a gradual shift into politics—becoming leader of the Left Centre instead of a sudden renunciation. Nathan, bent on founding a daily political journal to dominate the press, would not be dissuaded. He planned to gather collaborators—Massol, Finot, Pantin, Étienne Lousteau, Claude Vignon, Félicien Vernou, du Tillet—and asked Blondet to convene them. Blondet estimated heavy costs: three principal partners would need to put up a hundred thousand francs each to keep the paper solvent long enough for a rise and fall in Paris.

Florine, an actress and Nathan’s longtime lover, theatrically feigned distress to test friends and then sold nearly all her furniture and jewels to appear poor while pursuing an English lord; the sale also furnished funds for Nathan’s enterprise. A festive launch dinner christened the new journal; Florine moved into Raoul’s rooms while he lodged at the new office. Her devotion was practical—she planned provincial tours to earn for him—while Nathan pursued ambition and glory.

After Lady Dudley’s ball Nathan fell under the spell of the innocent Countess Marie de Vandenesse. Both believed themselves loved: Marie, charmed by the heroic role she imagined in the poet’s life; Raoul, weary of dissipation and eager for an ideal refuge. Their love was pure and enthusiastic. Marie delighted in adorning herself to please him and indulged the fantasy of inspiring his genius. Raoul, in turn, adopted gentility and restraint to please her, practicing civility in company and accepting society’s conventions when advised by Blondet.

At the Opera and in salons Raoul’s passion showed in looks, a single stolen kiss, and ardent letters. Yet his life was strained: he had to keep up an appearance of leisure in the Bois, attend society, run a newspaper demanding ceaseless work, write plays for money, and manage political ambitions. Marie, ignorant of these demands, misread his absences as indifference until he explained his burdens. She responded with admiration and sacrifice, promising to wait and be his support; this deepened their devotion. Still, the obligations his passion imposed—daily riding, late nights, editorial toil, theatrical commitments—sapped the hours needed for his enterprise.

Back in the office, Nathan’s collaborators were not friends. Massol and du Tillet intended to use his talent and control or discard him when convenient. Du Tillet, a banker, secretly manipulated finance and politics: he and Nucingen conspired for advantage, and Rastignac maneuvered for his own position. The paper’s costs outran receipts. Du Tillet advanced a loan and renewed notes on short terms, keeping Nathan dependent. Florine contributed fifty thousand but used much to refurnish her house; later she added debt. Du Tillet’s leverage grew as subscriptions dwindled and expenses mounted.

In January, pressed for cash, Nathan borrowed from a usurer, Gigonnet, but the notes were protested; Florine obtained a brief extension. The ledger showed receipts covered only two-thirds of expenses. A final demand from du Tillet, who wished to block Nathan’s candidacy for the electoral college, led Gigonnet to sue. Arrest for debt would ruin Nathan politically. Crushed by shame at potential failure, surrounded by false friends and conspirators, and seeing no way to preserve his position without scandal, Nathan, faint and desperate, attempted suicide by charcoal. He was discovered in time at his office, carried away by the countess Marie, and revived in a hotel where he confessed his woes. Marie vowed to save him.

Eugénie du Tillet, Marie’s practical sister, suspected danger and took action. She urged Marie to help: Marie sold her diamonds rather than borrow from her husband or father, unwilling to compromise honor. Eugénie proposed a plan: have Madame de Nucingen provide a loan in exchange for endorsed notes. The sisters secretly arranged for Schmucke, an old German music master and former pupil, to sign four notes for ten thousand francs each as endorsements; Marie visited Schmucke’s squalid lodging and obtained his ingenuous signature. Schmucke, simple and adoring, signed without understanding the consequence, and Marie carried the papers to Madame de Nucingen, who—touched and complicit—ordered the cashier to supply the money and keep the transaction secret.

When du Tillet learned the loan had been paid and that Nucingen’s wife had advanced the funds, he was furious—his scheme to have Nathan arrested and thus prevented from standing in elections had been foiled. Du Tillet accused his wife, who defied him, and a domestic scene followed. Despite his plotting, the danger passed: the four notes and Marie’s guarantee were in Vandenesse’s hand. Félix de Vandenesse, magnanimous, drew forty thousand francs from the Bank of France to reimburse Madame de Nucingen and retrieved the guarantee. He intended not to punish Marie but to protect her and to recover Nathan’s letters to prevent scandal.

Félix planned a controlled exposure. He decided to take Marie to a masked ball at the Opera, disguised, to confront Florine and Nathan and reclaim the letters—both to spare Marie further humiliation and to humble the journalist. At the ball Vandenesse contrived to involve Florine; in a private encounter he told the actress that Nathan had tried to kill himself for want of money and that Marie had carried him away to save him. He hinted that letters proving Nathan’s infatuation with the countess lay under Florine’s roof. Florine, confident and scornful of society women, denied Nathan would love a countess and challenged the plot’s truths. The count watched Nathan’s discomfort and prepared to bring the affair into the open, feeling that by revealing the deception he might both rescue his wife’s reputation and teach Nathan a salutary lesson.

Thus, in a world where journalism, politics, finance, love, vanity, and betrayal intertwine, Raoul is elevated and undone by his ambition and passions; Marie’s innocent, heroic devotion saves his life and summons schemes of money and influence; Florine’s theatrical constancy and du Tillet’s cold opportunism complicate every allegiance. The masked ball approaches as a stage for revelation, reprisal, and judgment.

Nathan, having hunted the foyer, returned to where the mask had addressed him. Seeing an expression he could not conceal, Florine planted herself before him and said imperiously, “I don’t wish you to leave me again; I have my reasons for this.”

At her husband's prompt, the countess approached Raoul and whispered, “Marie. Who is this woman? Leave her at once, and meet me at the foot of the grand staircase.”

In that difficult moment Raoul let go of Florine’s arm; though she caught his hand and held it forcibly, she had to let him go. Nathan melted into the crowd.

“What did I tell you?” Felix said into Florine’s astonished ear, offering his arm.

“Come,” she replied; “whoever you are, come. Have you a carriage here?”

Vandenesse hurried Florine away, followed by his wife. Soon the three masks, driven by Vandenesse’s coachman, reached Florine’s house. As soon as she entered her apartments the actress unmasked. Madame de Vandenesse could not help a quiver of surprise at Florine’s beauty as she stood choking with anger—superb in her wrath and jealousy.

“There is, somewhere in these rooms,” Vandenesse said, “a portfolio, the key to which you never had; the letters are probably in it.”

“Well, well, I am bewildered; you know something I have been uneasy about,” cried Florine, rushing into the study for the portfolio.

Florine’s rooms betrayed more of her intimacy with Nathan than any ideal mistress would wish revealed. A woman’s eye takes such truths at once, and the countess noticed vestiges of Nathan that confirmed Vandenesse’s suggestion. Florine returned with the portfolio.

“How am I to open it?” she asked.

She rang for the cook’s knife and brandished it, crying in an ironical tone, “With this they cut the necks of ‘poulets.’” The remark, which made the countess shiver, explained more clearly than Vandenesse the depths of the abyss into which she had almost fallen.

“What a fool I am!” said Florine; “his razor will do better.” She fetched one of Nathan’s razors from his dressing-table and slit the portfolio’s leather cover; Marie’s letters spilled out. Florine snatched one and skimmed it.

“Yes, she must be well-bred. It looks to me as if there were no mistakes in spelling here.”

The count gathered the letters and handed them to his wife, who took them to a table as if to ensure they were all there.

“Now,” said Vandenesse to Florine, showing five bank-bills of ten thousand francs each, “will you let me have those letters for these? They’ll replace what you have paid for him.”

“Ah!” cried Florine. “Didn’t I kill myself body and soul in the provinces to get him money—I, who’d have cut my hand off to serve him? But that’s men! Damn your soul for them and they’ll march over you rough-shod! He shall pay me for this!”

Madame de Vandenesse was leaving with the letters.

“Hi! stop, my fine mask!” Florine called. “Leave me one to confound him with.”

“Not possible,” said Vandenesse.

“Why not?”

“That mask is your ex-rival; but you needn’t fear her now.”

“Well, she might have had the grace to say thank you,” Florine snapped.

“But you have the fifty thousand francs now,” Vandenesse added, bowing to her.

It is extremely rare for young men driven to suicide to try again if the first attempt fails. When it doesn’t end life, it destroys all desire for voluntary death. Raoul felt no wish to repeat his attempt when he found himself in a more painful position than the one from which he had just been rescued. He tried to see the countess and explain the nature of his love, which now burned more vividly than ever. But at their first meeting in society, Madame de Vandenesse gave him that fixed, contemptuous look that at once and forever puts an impassable gulf between a man and a woman. Despite his assurances, Nathan never dared during the rest of the winter to speak to or even approach the countess.

He opened his heart to Blondet, speaking of his Laura and Beatrice in relation to Madame de Vandenesse. He even paraphrased the following passage by Théophile Gautier, one of our most remarkable poets:

“Ideala, flower of heaven’s own blue, with heart of gold, whose fibrous roots, softer, a thousandfold, than fairy tresses, strike to our souls and drink their purest essence; flower most sweet and bitter! thou canst not be torn away without the heart’s blood flowing, without thy bruised stems sweating with scarlet tears. Ah! cursed flower, why didst thou grow within my soul?”

“My dear fellow,” Blondet said, “you are raving. I’ll grant it was a pretty flower, but it wasn’t a bit ideal, and instead of singing like a blind man before an empty niche, you’d better wash your hands and submit to the powers. You are too much of an artist ever to be a good politician; you have been fooled by men of not one-half your value. Think about being fooled again—but elsewhere.”

“Marie cannot prevent my loving her,” Nathan said; “she shall be my Beatrice.”

“Beatrice, my good Raoul, was a little girl of twelve when Dante last saw her; otherwise she would not have been Beatrice. To make a divinity, it won’t do to see her one day wrapped in a mantle, the next in a low dress, and the third on the boulevard, cheapening toys for her last baby. When a man has Florine, who is in turn duchess, bourgeoise, Negress, marquise, colonel, Swiss peasant, virgin of the sun in Peru (only way she can play the part), I don’t see why he should go rambling after fashionable women.”

Du Tillet, to use a Bourse term, executed Nathan, who, for lack of money, gave up his place on the newspaper; the celebrated man received only five votes in the electoral college where the banker was elected.

After a long, happy journey in Italy, the Comtesse de Vandenesse returned to Paris late the following winter, and her husband’s predictions about Nathan were justified. He had followed Blondet’s advice and negotiated with the government, which employed his pen. His affairs were in such disorder that one day on the Champs‑Élysées Marie saw her former suitor on foot in shabby clothes, giving his arm to Florine. When a man grows indifferent to the heart of a woman who once loved him, he often seems to her ugly, even horrible—especially when he resembles Nathan. Madame de Vandenesse felt personal humiliation at having once cared for him. Had she not already been cured of extra‑conjugal passion, the contrast between the count and this man—ever less worthy of public favor—would have sufficed to end any lingering feeling.

Today the ambitious Nathan, ink-rich and will-poor, has capitulated to a sinecure like any commonplace man. Having lent his pen to every disorganizing effort, he now lives in peace under a ministerial organ’s protecting shade. The cross of the Legion of Honor, once the fruitful text of his satire, now adorns his button-hole. “Peace at any price,” formerly the stock-in-trade of his revolutionary editorship, is the topic of his laudatory articles. He once attacked heredity in Saint-Simonian phrases; he now defends it with solid arguments. This illogical conduct stems from the change of front by Raoul and many others during recent political evolutions.
