**The Atheist's Mass**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Clara Bell*  

**Dedicated to Auguste Borget by his friend De Balzac**

**Summary:**

Bianchon, a prominent physician in Paris, initially trained as a surgeon under the renowned Desplein, whose genius in surgery was unmatched but ultimately transient. Desplein's brilliance, while celebrated, did not translate into lasting legacy; he lacked the ability to pass on his knowledge, and his atheism, shaped by his scientific observations, left him isolated in his beliefs. He viewed the world through a lens of skepticism, rejecting the notion of a soul, which he never found in his dissections.

Despite his genius, Desplein's life was marked by contradictions that his detractors seized upon. His eccentricities, such as fluctuating between flamboyant and indifferent dress, and his complex personality, led to mixed judgments about him. He was both generous and miserly, often displaying a deep contempt for humanity after scrutinizing their behaviors.

Among his students, Bianchon stood out, having endured poverty while studying medicine. He was a loyal friend, known for his integrity and willingness to help others without expecting anything in return. Desplein recognized Bianchon's potential and took him under his wing, introducing him to the intricacies of medical practice and the elite of Paris.

One day, Bianchon witnessed Desplein entering a church, which surprised him given Desplein's staunch atheism. This incident sparked Bianchon's curiosity, leading him to question Desplein's beliefs. During a dinner conversation, Desplein dismissed the significance of the mass, criticizing its historical implications and the bloodshed associated with its establishment.

Months later, another physician questioned Desplein about his church visit, to which Desplein provided a vague excuse. Bianchon, intrigued, decided to observe Desplein's behavior over time. The following year, he confirmed Desplein's attendance at mass again, discovering that Desplein had founded the very service he attended.

This revelation deepened the mystery surrounding Desplein, highlighting the complexity of his character—a man who, despite his atheistic convictions, engaged in rituals that contradicted his beliefs. Bianchon was left pondering the enigmatic nature of Desplein's actions, which seemed to defy the straightforwardness of his scientific reasoning.

Doctor Bianchon took seven years to discuss a significant incident from his friend Desplein's life. After the Revolution of 1830, amidst chaos in Paris, Bianchon spotted Desplein entering Saint-Sulpice and joined him for mass. Curious about Desplein's attendance, given his atheism, Bianchon pressed for an explanation.

Desplein revealed that he had endured extreme poverty during his early years, living in a dilapidated building where he faced hunger, isolation, and the struggle to finance his medical education. He described his life as a relentless battle against despair, with no support from family or friends. His meager meals consisted of stale bread soaked in milk, and he often went without proper clothing or companionship.

Despite his hardships, Desplein was determined to succeed. He immersed himself in his studies, driven by the desire to escape his dire circumstances. He recounted how he felt misunderstood by those who had never experienced true poverty, likening their ignorance to a princess who suggested the hungry should simply buy cake.

Desplein's bitterness stemmed from witnessing the selfishness and envy of others, particularly in the competitive environment of Paris. He expressed a deep mistrust of humanity, feeling that mediocrity constantly undermined those striving for greatness.

During this challenging period, Desplein faced eviction due to unpaid rent. His neighbor, Bourgeat, a humble water-carrier, offered to help. Bourgeat paid Desplein's debts and shared his aspirations of buying a horse and barrel for his work. In return, he supported Desplein's education, providing food and encouragement, treating him like a son.

Desplein described Bourgeat as a devoted figure, embodying the virtues of selflessness and care. Their bond deepened over time, with Bourgeat taking on domestic responsibilities and nurturing Desplein's ambitions. When Desplein became a house surgeon, he felt a profound loss when he had to leave Bourgeat behind, but he remained committed to repaying his kindness.

Bourgeat fell ill, and Desplein dedicated himself to his care, trying every possible means to save him. Despite his efforts, Bourgeat eventually passed away, leaving Desplein heartbroken. He honored Bourgeat's wishes by arranging masses for his soul, reflecting on the man's unwavering faith and love.

Desplein's relationship with Bourgeat profoundly impacted his life, leading him to reconcile his skepticism with a sense of gratitude. He expressed a desire for Bourgeat's faith to inspire him, acknowledging the depth of their connection.

In the end, Bianchon, who witnessed Desplein's final days, could not definitively label him an atheist, pondering whether Bourgeat's faith might have opened the gates of Heaven for his friend. The narrative concludes with a reflection on the enduring bond between the two men, highlighting themes of sacrifice, gratitude, and the complexities of belief.
