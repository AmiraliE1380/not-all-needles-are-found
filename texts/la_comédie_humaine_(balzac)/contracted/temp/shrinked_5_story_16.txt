                         PARIS, August 1835.

THE DESERTED WOMAN

In the early spring of 1822, Paris doctors sent a young man convalescing from an inflammatory complaint, due to overstudy or excess, to Lower Normandy. His recovery required rest, a light diet, bracing air, and freedom from excitement; the fat lands of Bessin seemed to offer these. To Bayeux, a picturesque town six miles from the sea, the patient went and was welcomed with the cordiality of relatives who live retired lives and regard a new arrival as a godsend.

All little towns are much alike save for local customs. After two or three evenings in his cousin’s house, or with the friends who formed Mme. de Sainte-Severe’s circle, M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil quickly met the persons this exclusive society considered “the whole town.” He recognized in them the stock characters one finds in every provincial capital.

First is the family whose claim to ancient nobility is unquestioned locally though unknown fifty leagues away. This miniature royal family is distantly connected to old names; its head is inevitably a determined sportsman with no manners, who crushes others with his nominal superiority, tolerates the sub-prefect as he submits to taxes, and refuses to acknowledge nineteenth-century innovations, citing the prime minister’s lack of birth as a political monstrosity. His wife speaks loud, has had admirers, takes the sacrament at Easter, brings up her daughters poorly, and believes their name will secure them. Neither cares for modern luxury; they keep a livery fit for the stage and cling to old plate, furniture, equipages, language, and manner. This antique state suits provincial thrift; they are lords of a bygone manor without quitrents or hounds, full of honor among themselves and devoted to princes seen only at a distance. Among them you will find an uncle or brother, a lieutenant-general or old courtier wearing the red ribbon of Saint-Louis, like a stray leaf from the time of Louis XV.

This fossil greatness meets a rival in a wealthier but less ancient house. Husband and wife spend some months in Paris and bring back its frivolity and short-lived fashions. Madame is a woman of fashion, though conscious of her clothes and often behind the mode; she scoffs at her neighbors’ affected ignorance. Her plate is modern; she keeps grooms, a valet, and such. The eldest son drives a tilbury and does nothing (the estate is entailed upon him); the younger is an auditor at the Council of State. The father knows official scandals and anecdotes of Louis XVIII. and Madame du Cayla; he invests in five per cents, avoids the topic of cider, but sometimes yields to speculating about local fortunes. A member of the Departmental Council, he buys clothes from Paris and wears the Legion of Honor. He has fully grasped the Restoration’s significance and profits at the Chamber, though his Royalism is less pure than his rival’s; he reads the Gazette and the Debats, while the other family reads the Quotidienne.

The Bishop, once Vicar-General, vacillates between these powers; both pay him the respect due to religion, yet sometimes remind him of La Fontaine’s ass laden with relics. His origin is distinctly plebeian.

Next come men of family with modest incomes—captains in the navy or cavalry, or idlers—who on the roads rank halfway between the cure bearing sacraments and the tax collector. Nearly all have been Pages or in Household Troops and now end their days in a faisance-valoir, more interested in felling timber and cider prospects than in politics. Still they talk of the Charter and Liberals while the cards are making or over backgammon, and have married off everybody according to genealogies they know by heart. Their women assume the airs of Court ladies in basket chaises, wrap in shawls and caps as full dress, and twice a year, after deliberation, receive a new bonnet from Paris. They are mostly exemplary and garrulous wives.

These are the principal elements of aristocratic gentility, with a few outlying old maids of good family—spinsters who have solved the problem of remaining absolutely stationary. They might be sealed up in their houses; their faces and dresses are fixtures in the town and province. They are its tradition, its memory, the genius loci incarnate, frigid and monumental; they know when to laugh and when to shake their heads, and occasionally give utterances that pass as witticisms.

A few rich townspeople have crept into this miniature Faubourg Saint-Germain thanks to money or aristocratic leanings. Despite being forty, the circle will say of them, “Young So-and-so has sound opinions,” and make such men deputies. Elderly spinsters are often their patronesses, not without comment.

Finally, the set includes two or three ecclesiastics, admitted for the sake of their cloth or wit; these great nobles find their own society dull and introduce the bourgeois element into their drawing-rooms as a baker puts leaven into dough.

The sum total of minds is a stock of antiquated notions with a few new inflections brewed at evening gatherings. Like sea-water in a creek, the phrases representing these ideas surge up daily, obeying conversational tides; you hear the hollow echo of yesterday, today, tomorrow, and forever. They pass immutable judgments that form a body of tradition into which wit or sense seldom penetrates. Their lives revolve like clockwork in an orbit of use and wont, admitting no more deviation than their fixed opinions on religion, politics, morals, or letters.

If a stranger is admitted to the cenacle, each member will say (with a trace of irony), “You will not find the brilliancy of your Parisian society here,” and then criticize the life of the others as if he alone had vainly tried to enlighten them. But any stranger who echoes their criticisms is pronounced ill-natured, a heathen, an outlaw, a Parisian “as Parisians mostly are.”

Before Gaston de Nueil appeared in this world of strict etiquette—where every detail is part of a whole, and everything is known—he had been weighed in Bayeusaine judgment. His cousin, Mme. de Sainte-Severe, had already declared the amount of his fortune and expectations, produced the family tree, and praised the talents, breeding, and modesty of this branch. He received precisely the attentions to which he was entitled; accepted as a worthy scion of good stock and, at twenty-three, made welcome without ceremony, though some young ladies and mothers of daughters regarded him with favor.

He had an income of eighteen thousand livres from land in the valley of the Auge; sooner or later his father would, as duty required, leave him the chateau of Manerville with its lands. No one questioned his education, political prospects, or qualities. His land was undeniable: rentals steady, plantations excellent, tenants paying repairs, rates, and taxes; apple-trees thirty-eight years old; and his father negotiating for two hundred acres of woodland to enclose with walls. No earthly hopes of a political career or fame could compare with such advantages.

Whether from malice or design, Mme. de Sainte-Severe omitted that Gaston had an elder brother; nor did Gaston himself mention him. The brother was consumptive and seemed likely to die soon, lamented and forgotten.

At first Gaston amused himself at the circle’s expense. He drew portraits for his mental album of their angular, wrinkled faces, hooked noses, crotchets, and eccentricities of dress—portraits full of racy truth. He delighted in their “Normanisms,” in the primitive quaintness of their ideas and characters. For a short time he threw himself into their squirrel’s life of busy gyrations in a cage. Then he began to feel the want of variety and grew tired. It was like a life of the cloister cut short before it had well begun. He drifted on until he reached a crisis neither spleen nor disgust but combining both. When a human being is transplanted into uncongenial soil and leads a starved, stunted existence, discomfort at the transition is certain. Gradually, if nothing removes him, he grows accustomed to surroundings and adapts to the vacuity that renders him powerless. Even now Gaston’s lungs were used to the air; he was willing to discern a kind of vegetable happiness in days with no mental exertion and no responsibilities. The constant stirring of life’s sap, the fertilizing influences of mind on mind he had sought so eagerly in Paris, began to fade from his memory, and he was in fair way to become a fossil with these fossils, ending his days among them, content, like Ulysses’ companions, in his gross envelope.

One evening Gaston de Nueil sat between a dowager and one of the vicars-general in a gray-paneled drawing-room with large white tiles. Family portraits watched four card-tables where some sixteen people chatted over whist. Gaston, digesting one of those provincial dinners he had looked forward to all day, found himself justifying the country customs.

He began to understand why these good folk still played with yesterday’s cards on a threadbare tablecloth, and why they had ceased to dress for themselves or others. He saw a kind of philosophy in the even tenor of their perpetual round, the calm of their methodical monotony, their ignorance of the refinements of luxury. He almost concluded that luxury profited little; Paris, with its passions and pleasures, was now scarcely more than a childhood memory.

He admired the red hands and shy, bashful manner of a young lady who at first seemed an awkward simpleton, unattractive and ridiculous. His fate was sealed. From the provinces to Paris and back again—he would have sunk into provincial life but for a chance remark that sent through him an emotion like a fine strain of music amid a tedious opera.

“You went to call on Mme. de Beauseant yesterday, did you not?” an elderly lady asked the head of the local royal family.

“I went this morning. She was so poorly and depressed that I could not persuade her to dine with us tomorrow.”

“With Mme. de Champignelles?” exclaimed the dowager.

“With my wife,” the noble assented calmly. “Mme. de Beauseant is descended from the House of Burgundy, on the spindle side, ’tis true, but the name atones for everything. My wife is attached to the Vicomtesse, and the poor lady has lived alone so long—”

The Marquis de Champignelles spoke with an air of cool unconcern that made it impossible to tell whether he meant to pity Mme. de Beauseant or to pay homage to her birth; whether he felt flattered to receive her or, on the contrary, was proud to force the country families to meet her. The women exchanged a glance; a silence fell; their attitude was one of disapproval.

“Does this Mme. de Beauseant happen to be the lady whose adventure with M. d’Ajuda-Pinto made so much noise?” Gaston asked his neighbor.

“The very same,” came the reply. “She came to Courcelles after the Marquis d’Ajuda’s marriage; nobody visits her. She has too much sense to ignore that she is in a false position, so she has made no attempt to receive. M. de Champignelles and a few gentlemen called on her, but she would see no one but M. de Champignelles, perhaps because he is a connection. They are related through the Beauseants; the father of the present Vicomte married a Mlle. de Champignelles of the older branch. But though the Vicomtesse de Beauseant is supposed to be of Burgundian descent, you can understand we could not admit a wife separated from her husband into our society here. We cling to old ideas. There was less excuse for the Vicomtesse, because M. de Beauseant is a well-bred man who would have listened to reason. But his wife is quite mad—”

M. de Nueil heard the words but gathered little of their sense; his brain was full of thick-coming fancies. What else could one call the alluring charm of an adventure that tempts the imagination and sets vague hopes springing up—sensations of coming events, mysterious felicity, and fear where no facts yet exist? Over these fancies thought hovers, conceiving impossible projects and giving in the germ all the joys of love. Perhaps all passion is contained in that thought-germ, as a flower’s beauty and fragrance are packed in the seed.

Gaston did not know that Mme. de Beauseant had taken refuge in Normandy after a notoriety women often envy and condemn, especially when youth and beauty excuse the transgression. Any kind of celebrity bestows prestige: for women, as for families, the glory of the crime can efface the stain. A noble house may be proud of its tales of executed ancestors; a young pretty woman becomes more interesting through dubious renown—a happy love or a scandalous desertion makes her more compelling. We pity or admire what rises above the commonplace. The mob gives involuntary respect to anyone who lifts themselves above it and is not particular about the means.

Some such motives may have influenced Gaston, or perhaps curiosity, a craving for interest, or that crowd of inexplicable impulses we call “fatality,” drew him to Mme. de Beauseant.

The figure of the Vicomtesse rose before him with gracious associations. She was a new world—a world of fears and hopes to fight for and conquer. Inevitably he felt the contrast between this vision and the shabby room; she was a woman unlike any he had seen in this dull, little world of calculation and formal courtesy, where the simplest ideas were alarming. Her name revived a young man’s dreams and awakened urgent desires that had lain dormant.

Gaston was absent-minded the rest of the evening. He pondered how to gain access to Mme. de Beauseant, a task no easy matter. She was believed clever. If gifted people may be captivated by something subtle or eccentric, they are also exacting and can read beneath the surface; after the first step, success and failure are equally possible. Besides, the Vicomtesse had the dignity of her name; her seclusion was the least barrier. For these reasons it seemed unlikely a stranger could hope for admittance. Yet the next morning found M. de Nueil walking toward Courcelles, a dupe of illusions natural to his age. Several times he circled the garden walls, peering through gaps at closed shutters or open windows, hoping for some romantic chance on which to found a scheme of introduction, ignoring impracticability. Morning after morning he walked thus; the vision of a woman living apart, love’s martyr, loomed larger and was enshrined in his soul. A gardener’s heavy footstep would set his heart beating with hope.

He thought of writing her but, on reflection, what could one say to a woman one had never seen? Gaston had little self-confidence; like most young people with many illusions, he feared mortifying silence more than almost anything and shuddered at sending a first tender epistle into that chance. Distracted by conflicting ideas, inventing chimeras, weaving romances, and beating his brains, he at last hit upon a hopeful stratagem: a means that would make clear to any woman that a man took a fervent interest in her. Social conventions place as many barriers between lovers as any fairy tale; in real life, as in stories, the woman belongs to the man who can reach and set her free. The poorest calender in love is, by distance only, scarcely farther from a lady than Gaston from Mme. de Beauseant. She knew nothing of his wanderings; his love grew with the obstacles; distance lent enchantment.

One day confident in his inspiration, he hoped to win everything by the love in his eyes. He thought spoken words more eloquent than letters and would engage feminine curiosity to plead for him. He went, therefore, to M. de Champignelles, proposing to employ the Marquis in his enterprise. He told him he had been entrusted with a delicate commission concerning the Vicomtesse and feared she would not trust a stranger’s letter. Would the Marquis, on his next visit, ask if she would consent to receive M. de Nueil? While asking Champignelles to keep the secret in case of refusal, Gaston insinuated reasons for his admission to be passed on—implying the right sort of motive. Love lends a young man the self-possession and craft of an old ambassador; the Marquis’ harmless vanities were gratified and he was completely duped. He tried to fathom Gaston’s secret, but the young man turned aside his questions with Norman shrewdness until the Marquis, as a gallant, complimented him on his discretion.

M. de Champignelles hurried to Courcelles, eager to serve a pretty woman, and in the Vicomtesse’s position such a message was likely to excite curiosity. Though her memory supplied no reason for receiving M. de Nueil, she saw no objection—after prudent inquiries about his family and condition. At first she refused, then, after discussion with the Marquis and guided by his discretion, she revoked her negative answer. The discussion and the Marquis’ reserve had piqued her curiosity.

The Marquis would not appear ridiculous; he professed the air of a man who can keep counsel, insisting that the Vicomtesse must know the visitor’s purpose. Mme. de Beauseant, in sincerity, had no idea. She connected Gaston with people he had never met, chased various wild conjectures, and asked herself if she had seen him before. No love-letter, however skilfully indited, could have produced so much effect as this riddle. Again and again she puzzled over it.

When Gaston learned he might call, his rapture was mingled with singular embarrassment. How contrive a suitable sequel to this stratagem?

“Bah! I shall see her,” he kept telling himself as he dressed. “See her, and that is everything!”

He hoped that once across Courcelles’ threshold an expedient would reveal itself. There are believers in necessity’s omnipotence who never turn back; the nearness of danger inspires them. Gaston was one of these.

He took pains with his dress, imagining, as youth does, that success depends on a curl, forgetting that youth itself is charming. In any case, women like Mme. de Beauseant are chiefly attracted by wit or uncommon character. Greatness flatters their vanity and seems to promise a great passion; wit amuses and answers their subtlety, making them feel understood. And what do women want but to be amused, understood, or adored? Only after long reflection do we comprehend the coquetry of neglecting dress and reserve at a first interview; by the time we learn the strategy, we are often too old to profit.

While Gaston borrowed charms from his clothes, Mme. de Beauseant was also attending to her toilette. “I would rather not frighten people,” she said as she arranged her hair.

M. de Nueil had an unconscious originality that lent flavor to his words and deeds and absolved them. He was highly cultivated, had a keen brain, and a face as mobile as his nature, winning goodwill. The promise of passion in his bright eyes matched a kindly heart. His resolution as he entered Courcelles fitted his frank nature and ardent imagination. Bold in love, his heart nevertheless beat violently as he crossed the great court laid out like an English garden. The man-servant, who had taken his name to the Vicomtesse, returned to say she would receive him.

“M. le Baron de Nueil.”

Gaston entered slowly but with sufficient ease; and it is harder, it is said, to enter a room where there is only one woman than a room that holds a score.

A great fire burned on the hearth; by the soft candlelight he saw a young woman sitting on a high-backed bergere by the hearth. The low seat let her move her head freely; every turn was graceful, whether she bent forward or raised it languidly, could cross her feet and let them appear, or draw them back under the folds of a long black dress.

The Vicomtesse made as if to lay the book she was reading on a small round stand; as she did so the volume, insecurely placed, fell to the ground between the stand and the sofa. This did not disconcert her. She looked up, bowing almost imperceptibly to his greeting without rising from the depths of the low chair. Bending forward, she stirred the fire briskly, stooped to pick up a fallen glove, drawing it over her left hand, while her eyes sought its fellow. The glance was checked when she stretched out a thin, white, almost transparent right hand, with flawless ovals of rose-colored nail at the tips of slender, ringless fingers, and pointed to a chair as if to bid Gaston be seated. He sat, and she turned her face questioningly toward him. No words could describe the subtlety of the winning charm and inquiry in that gesture; deliberate in its kindliness, gracious yet exact in expression, it came from early education and constant practice of life’s graces. These swift, deft movements blended a pretty woman’s fastidious carelessness with the high-bred manner of a great lady.

Mme. de Beauseant stood out in strong contrast to the automatons among whom he had spent two months of exile in that out-of-the-world district of Normandy; she seemed the realization of his romantic dreams, and defied comparison with other women he had formerly admired. Here in her presence, in a drawing-room like some salon of the Faubourg Saint-Germain, full of costly trifles, flowers, and books, he felt as if he were back in Paris. It was a real Parisian carpet beneath his feet; he saw once more the high-bred Parisienne, the fragile outline of her form, her exquisite charm, her disdain of the studied effects that spoil provincial women.

Mme. de Beauseant had fair hair, dark eyes, and the pale complexion of blondes. She held her brow nobly, like a fallen angel grown proud, disdainful of pardon. The way she gathered her thick hair into a crown of plaits above the curving bandeaux on her forehead added to the queenliness of her face. One could imagine a ducal coronet in the spiral threads of her golden hair; the courage of her house seemed to gleam from her brilliant eyes, a courage used to repel audacity yet full of tenderness for gentleness. The little head, admirably poised above the long white throat, the delicate features, the subtle curves of the lips, the mobile face, wore an expression of discreet irony suggestive of craft and insolence. Yet those failings were softened by lines that came out in her forehead whenever her face was not in repose, and by her upward glances—those pathetic tricks of manner—which told unmistakably of unhappiness, of a passion that had almost cost her life. A woman sitting in the great, silent salon, cut off from the world in this remote valley, alone with memories of brilliant, impassioned youth, of continual gaiety and homage now replaced by the void—was there not something awe-inspiring in the sight? Consciousness of her own value lurked in her smile. She was neither wife nor mother; she was an outlaw: she had lost the one heart that might have set her pulses beating without shame; she had nothing from without to support her reeling soul; she must draw strength from within, live her own life, cherish no hope save that of forsaken love, which looks forward to Death and hastens his lagging footsteps. And this while life was in its prime. To feel destined for happiness and to die—never having given nor received it! A woman too! What pain! These thoughts flashed across M. de Nueil’s mind like lightning and left him humble before the greatest charm with which woman can be invested. The triple aureole of beauty, nobleness, and misfortune dazzled him; he stood in dreamy, almost open-mouthed admiration of the Vicomtesse. But he found nothing to say.

Mme. de Beauseant, by no means displeased at his surprise, held out her hand with a kindly but imperious gesture; then summoning a smile to her pale lips, as if still obeying the woman’s impulse to be gracious, she said:

“I have heard from M. de Champignelles of a message which you have kindly undertaken to deliver, monsieur. Can it be from—”

With that unfinished phrase Gaston understood, more clearly than before, his own ridiculous position, the bad taste and bad faith of his conduct toward a woman so noble and unfortunate. He reddened. The thoughts crowding upon him showed in his troubled eyes; but suddenly, with the courage youth draws from a sense of its own wrongdoing, he gained confidence and interrupted very humbly.

“Madame,” he faltered, “I do not deserve the happiness of seeing you. I have deceived you basely. However strong the motive, it can never excuse the pitiful subterfuge I used to gain my end. But, madame, if your goodness will permit me to tell you—”

The Vicomtesse glanced at M. de Nueil with haughty disdain. She stretched her hand to the bell and rang it.

“Jacques,” she said, “light this gentleman to the door,” and she looked with dignity at the visitor.

She rose proudly, bowed to Gaston, and stooped for the fallen volume. If her movements at his entrance had been caressingly dainty and gracious, her every gesture now was no less severely frigid. M. de Nueil rose, but stood waiting. Mme. de Beauseant flung another glance at him that seemed to say, Well, why do you not go?

There was such cutting irony in that look that Gaston grew white as if about to faint. Tears sprang to his eyes, but he would not let them fall; burning shame and despair dried them. He looked back at the Vicomtesse, a pride and consciousness of his own worth mingled with humility; she had a right to punish him, but ought she to exercise it? Then he went out.

As he crossed the ante-chamber, his head cleared and his wits sharpened by passion to grasp the danger of his situation.

“If I leave this house, I can never come back to it again,” he said to himself. “The Vicomtesse will always think me a fool. It is impossible that a woman, and such a woman, should not guess the love she has called forth. Perhaps she feels a little vague regret for dismissing me so abruptly—but she could not recall her sentence. It rests with me to understand her.”

At that thought Gaston stopped on the stairs with an exclamation; he turned sharply, saying, “I have forgotten something,” and went back to the salon. The lackey, respectful of a baron and the rights of property, was deceived by the natural utterance and followed him. Gaston returned quietly and unannounced. The Vicomtesse, thinking the intruder the servant, looked up and beheld M. de Nueil.

“Jacques lighted me to the door,” he said with a half-sad smile that dispelled any suspicion of jest, while the tone went to the heart. Mme. de Beauseant was disarmed.

“Very well, take a seat,” she said.

Gaston eagerly took a chair. His eyes shone with happiness; the Vicomtesse, unable to endure the brilliant light in them, looked down at the book. She enjoyed the delicious, ever-new sensation; a man’s delight in her presence is an unfailing feminine instinct. Besides, he had divined her, and a woman is grateful to the man who masters the apparently capricious yet logical reasoning of her heart, who can track her thought through the contradictory workings of her mind and read the sensations, shy or bold, written in fleeting red—a bewildering maze of coquetry and self-revelation.

“Madame,” Gaston exclaimed in a low voice, “my blunder you know, but you do not know how much I am to blame. If you only knew what joy it was to—”

“Ah! take care,” she said, holding up one finger with an air of mystery, as she put out her hand toward the bell.

The charming gesture, the gracious threat, called up some sad memory of the old happy time when she could be wholly charming and gentle without afterthought; when the gladness of her heart justified caprice and put charm into every movement. Lines gathered between her brows and the expression of her face grew dark in the soft candlelight. Then, looking across at M. de Nueil gravely but not unkindly, she spoke like a woman who deeply feels the meaning of every word.

“This is all very ridiculous! Once upon a time, monsieur, when thoughtless high spirits were my privilege, I should have laughed fearlessly over your visit. But now my life is very much changed. I cannot do as I like; I am obliged to think. What brings you here? Is it curiosity? In that case I am paying dearly for a little fleeting pleasure. Have you fallen passionately in love already with a woman you have never seen, a woman whose name slander has, of course, besmirched? If so, your motive is disrespect, an error which accident has made notorious.”

She flung her book down scornfully, then, with a terrible look at Gaston, went on: “Because I once was weak, must it be supposed that I am always weak? This is horrible, degrading. Or have you come to pity me? You are very young to offer sympathy with heart troubles. Understand this clearly, sir: I would rather have scorn than pity. I will not endure compassion from anyone.”

There was a brief pause.

“Well, sir,” she continued, and the face she turned to him was gentle and sad, “whatever motive induced this rash intrusion upon my solitude, it is very painful to me, you see. You are too young to be totally without good feeling, so surely you will feel that this behavior of yours is improper. I forgive you for it, and, as you see, I speak of it without bitterness. You will not come here again, will you? I entreat when I might command. If you come to see me again, neither you nor I can prevent the whole place from believing you my lover, and you would cause me great additional annoyance. You do not mean to do that, I think.”

She said no more, but looked at him with a great dignity which abased him.

“I have done wrong, madame,” he said, with deep feeling in his voice, “but it was through enthusiasm and thoughtlessness and eager desire of happiness, the qualities and defects of my age. Now I understand that I ought not to have tried to see you.” He added, appealing to feeling rather than intellect, that the desire had been natural, and described the weariness of his enforced exile. He drew a portrait of a young man in whom the fires of life were burning themselves out, conveying the impression of a heart worthy of tender love, a heart which had never known the joys of love for a young and beautiful woman of refinement and taste. He explained, without seeking to justify, his unusual conduct. He flattered Mme. de Beauseant by showing that she had realized for him the ideal lady of a young man’s dream, the ideal sought so often in vain. Then he touched upon his morning prowlings under the walls of Courcelles and his wild thoughts at the first sight of the house, till he awakened that vague indulgence a woman can feel for the follies committed for her sake.

An impassioned voice spoke in the chill solitude; the speaker brought a warm breath of youth and the charms of a cultivated mind. It was long since Mme. de Beauseant had been so stirred by delicately expressed feeling; it affected her strongly. In spite of herself, she watched M. de Nueil’s expressive face and admired the noble countenance of a soul not yet broken by the cruel discipline of the world, unfretted by continual scheming to gratify ambition or vanity. Gaston was in the flower of his youth; he impressed her as a man with something in him, unaware as yet of the great career before him. So both made reflections dangerous for their peace of mind, and both strove to conceal their thoughts. M. de Nueil saw in the Vicomtesse a rare type of woman, always the victim of her perfections and tenderness; her graceful beauty was the least of her charms for those privileged to know the infinite feeling, thought, and goodness within; a woman whose instinctive feeling for beauty ran through all expressions of love, purifying its transports and turning them to something almost holy — that wonderful secret of womanhood Nature so seldom bestows. And the Vicomtesse, listening to the ring of sincerity in Gaston’s voice while he told of youthful troubles, began to understand what grown children of five-and-twenty suffer from diffidence when hard work has kept them from corrupting influences and intercourse with the world whose sophistical reasoning destroys youth’s fair qualities. Here was the ideal of a woman’s dreams: a man unspoiled by family egoism or the narrow selfishness that blights the first impulses of honor, devotion, self-sacrifice, and high demands of self; all the flowers that so soon wither, enriching the life of delicate but strong emotions and keeping alive the loyalty of the heart.

But these two, once launched into the vast of sentiment, went far indeed in theory, sounding their depths and testing sincerity; whereas Gaston’s experiments were unconscious, Mme. de Beauseant had a purpose in all she said. Bringing her natural and acquired subtlety to the work, she sought to learn M. de Nueil’s opinions by advancing, as far as she could, views diametrically opposed to her own. So witty and gracious, so completely herself with this stranger she felt sure they would never meet again, that after some delicious epigram Gaston exclaimed unthinkingly:

“Oh! madame, how could any man have left you?”

The Vicomtesse was silent. Gaston reddened; he thought he had offended her; but she was not angry. The first deep thrill of delight since her calamity surprised her. The skill of the cleverest roué could not have made the impression M. de Nueil made with that cry from the heart. That verdict wrung from a young man’s candor gave her back innocence in her own eyes, condemned the world, laid the blame upon the lover who had left her, and justified her solitary drooping life. The world’s absolution, the heartfelt sympathy, the social esteem so longed for and so harshly refused — all her secret desires were given her in that exclamation, made fairer by the heart’s sweetest flatteries and the admiration women always relish. He understood her, understood all, and he had offered, as if natural, the opportunity of rising higher through her fall. She looked at the clock.

“Ah! madame, do not punish me for my heedlessness. If you grant me but one evening, vouchsafe not to shorten it.”

She smiled at the pretty speech.

“Well, as we must never meet again,” she said, “what signifies a moment more or less? If you were to care for me, it would be a pity.”

“It is too late now,” he said.

“Do not tell me that,” she answered gravely. “Under any other circumstances I should be very glad to see you. I will speak frankly, and you will understand how it is that I do not choose to see you again, and ought not to do so. You have too much magnanimity not to feel that if I were so much as suspected of a second trespass, everyone would think of me as a contemptible and vulgar woman; I should be like other women. A pure and blameless life will bring my character into relief. I am too proud not to endeavor to live like one apart in the world, a victim of the law through my marriage, man’s victim through my love. If I were not faithful to the position I have taken up, then I should deserve all the reproach heaped upon me; I should be lowered in my own eyes. I had not enough lofty social virtue to remain with a man I did not love. I have snapped the bonds of marriage in spite of the law; it was wrong, it was a crime, it was anything you like, but for me the bonds meant death. I meant to live. Perhaps if I had been a mother I could have endured the torture of a forced marriage of suitability. At eighteen we scarcely know what is done with us, poor girls that we are! I have broken the laws of the world, and the world has punished me; we both did rightly. I sought happiness. Is it not a law of our nature to seek happiness? I was young, I was beautiful... I thought I had found a nature as loving, as apparently passionate. I was loved indeed; for a little while...”

She paused.

“I used to think,” she said, “that no one could leave a woman in such a position as mine. I have been forsaken; I must have offended in some way. Yes, in some way, no doubt, I failed to keep some law of our nature, was too loving, too devoted, too exacting — I do not know. Evil days have brought light! For a long while I blamed another; now I am content to bear the whole blame. At my own expense, I have absolved that other of whom I once thought I had a right to complain. I had not the art to keep him; fate has punished me heavily for my lack of skill. I only knew how to love; how can one keep oneself in mind when one loves? So I was a slave when I should have sought to be a tyrant. Those who know me may condemn me, but they will respect me too. Pain has taught me that I must not lay myself open to this a second time. I cannot understand how I am living yet, after the anguish of that first week of the most fearful crisis in a woman’s life. Only three years of loneliness could give strength to speak of that time as I speak now. Such agony, monsieur, usually ends in death; but this — well, it was the agony of death with no tomb to end it. Oh! I have known pain indeed!”

The Vicomtesse raised her beautiful eyes to the ceiling; and the cornice, no doubt, received confidences a stranger might not hear. When a woman is afraid to look at her interlocutor, there is in truth no gentler, meeker, more accommodating confidant than the cornice. The cornice is quite an institution in the boudoir; what is it but the confessional minus the priest?

Mme. de Beauseant was eloquent and beautiful at that moment, nay, coquettish if the word were not too heavy. By justifying herself and love she stimulated every sentiment in the man before her; the higher she set the goal, the more conspicuous it grew. At last, when her eyes lost the too eloquent expression given them by painful memories, she let them fall on Gaston.

“You acknowledge, do you not, that I am bound to lead a solitary, self-contained life?” she said quietly.

So sublime was she in her reasoning and her madness that M. de Nueil felt a wild longing to throw himself at her feet; but he was afraid of making himself ridiculous, so he held his enthusiasm and thoughts in check. He was afraid, too, that he might totally fail to express them, and in no less terror of some awful rejection or mockery, an apprehension that strikes like ice to the most fervid soul. The revulsion that led him to crush down every feeling cost him the intense pain diffident and ambitious natures experience when compelled to stifle their longings. Yet, in spite of himself, he broke the silence in a faltering voice:

“Madame, permit me to give way to one of the strongest emotions of my life, and own to all that you have made me feel. You set the heart in me swelling high! I feel within me a longing to make you forget your mortifications, to devote my life to this, to give you love for all who ever have given you wounds or hate. But this is a very sudden outpouring of the heart, nothing can justify it to-day, and I ought not----”

“Enough, monsieur,” said Mme. de Beauseant; “we have both of us gone too far. By giving you the sad reasons for a refusal I am compelled to give, I meant to soften it and not to elicit homage. Coquetry only suits a happy woman. Believe me, we must remain strangers. At a later day you will know that ties which must inevitably be broken ought not to be formed at all.”

She sighed lightly; her brows contracted, but almost immediately cleared.

“How painful it is for a woman to be powerless to follow the man she loves through all the phases of his life! And if that man loves her truly, his heart must surely vibrate with pain at the deep trouble in hers. Are they not twice unhappy?”

There was a short pause. Then she rose smiling.

“You little suspected, when you came to Courcelles, that you were to hear a sermon, did you?”

Gaston felt further than at first from this extraordinary woman. Was the charm of that delightful hour due after all to the coquetry of the mistress of the house? Had she been anxious to display her wit? He bowed stiffly to the Vicomtesse and went away in desperation.

On the way home he tried to detect the real character of a creature supple and hard as a steel spring; but he had seen her pass through so many phases that he could not decide. The tones of her voice rang in his ears; her gestures, the little movements of her head, and the varying expression of her eyes grew more gracious in memory, more fascinating as he thought of them. The Vicomtesse’s beauty shone again in the darkness; reviving impressions called up others, and he was enthralled anew by womanly charm and wit, which at first he had not perceived. He fell to wandering musings in which the most lucid thoughts grow refractory and contradict each other, and the soul passes through a brief frenzy. Youth only can understand all that lies in the dithyrambic outpourings of youth when, after a stormy siege of the most frantic folly and the coolest common-sense, the heart finally yields to the latest comer—be it hope or despair, as some mysterious power determines.

At three-and-twenty, diffidence nearly always rules a man’s conduct; he is perplexed with a young girl’s shyness, a girl’s trouble; he is afraid he will express his love ill, sees nothing but difficulties, and takes alarm; he would be bolder if he loved less, for he has no confidence in himself, and with a growing sense of the cost of happiness comes a conviction that the woman he loves cannot easily be won; perhaps he is giving himself up too entirely to his own pleasure and fears he can give none; and when his idol inspires him with awe, he worships in secret and afar, and unless his love is guessed it dies away. Then it often happens that one of these dead early loves lingers on, bright with illusions in many a young heart. What man is there but keeps within him these virgin memories that grow fairer every time they rise before him, memories that hold up to him the ideal of perfect bliss? Such recollections are like children who die in the flower of childhood, before their parents have known anything of them but their smiles.

So M. de Nueil came home from Courcelles, the victim of a mood fraught with desperate resolutions. Even now he felt that Mme. de Beauseant was one of the conditions of his existence, and that death would be preferable to life without her. He was still young enough to feel the tyrannous fascination which fully developed womanhood exerts over immature and impassioned natures; consequently he was to spend one of those stormy nights when a young man’s thoughts travel from happiness to suicide and back—nights in which youth rushes through a lifetime of bliss and falls asleep from sheer exhaustion. Fateful nights are they, and the worst misfortune is to awake a philosopher afterwards. M. de Nueil was far too deeply in love to sleep; he rose and betook himself to inditing letters, but none were satisfactory, and he burned them all.

The next day he went to Courcelles to make the circuit of her garden walls, but waited till nightfall; he was afraid she might see him. The instinct that led him to act thus arose out of so obscure a mood that none but a young man, or one in like case, can fully understand its mute ecstasies and vagaries,—matters to set those lucky enough to see life only in its matter-of-fact aspect shrugging their shoulders. After painful hesitation, Gaston wrote to Mme. de Beauseant. Here is the letter, a sample of the epistolary style peculiar to lovers, a performance, like the secret drawings children prepare for a parent’s birthday, found insufferable by every mortal except the recipient:

“MADAME,—Your power over my heart, my soul, myself, is so great that my fate depends wholly upon you to-day. Do not throw this letter into the fire; be so kind as to read it through. Perhaps you may pardon the opening sentence when you see that it is no commonplace, selfish declaration, but that it expresses a simple fact. Perhaps you may feel moved, because I ask for so little, by the submission of one who feels himself so much beneath you, by the influence that your decision will exercise upon my life. At my age, madame, I only know how to love, I am utterly ignorant of ways of attracting and winning a woman’s love, but in my own heart I know raptures of adoration of her. I am irresistibly drawn to you by the great happiness I feel through you; my thoughts turn to you with the selfish instinct which bids us draw nearer to the fire of life when we find it. I do not imagine that I am worthy of you; it seems impossible that I, young, ignorant, and shy, could bring you one-thousandth part of the happiness I drink in at the sound of your voice and the sight of you. For me you are the only woman in the world. I cannot imagine life without you, so I have made up my mind to leave France, and to risk my life till I lose it in some desperate enterprise, in the Indies, in Africa, I care not where. How can I quell a love that knows no limits save by opposing to it something as infinite? Yet, if you will allow me to hope, not to be yours, but to win your friendship, I will stay. Let me come, not so very often, if you require it, to spend a few such hours with you as those stolen hours of yesterday. The keen delight of that brief happiness to be cut short at the least over-ardent word from me will suffice to enable me to endure the boiling torrent in my veins. Have I presumed too much upon your generosity by this entreaty to suffer an intercourse in which all the gain is mine alone? You could find ways of showing the world, to which you sacrifice so much, that I am nothing to you; you are so clever and so proud! What have you to fear? If I could only lay bare my heart to you at this moment, to convince you that it is with no lurking afterthought that I make this humble request! Should I have told you that my love was boundless, while I prayed you to grant me friendship, if I had any hope of your sharing this feeling in the depths of my soul? No, while I am with you, I will be whatever you will, if only I may be with you. If you refuse (as you have the power to refuse), I will not utter one murmur, I will go. And if, at a later day, any other woman should enter into my life, you will have proof that you were right; but if I am faithful till death, you may feel some regret perhaps. The hope of causing you a regret will soothe my agony, and that thought shall be the sole revenge of a slighted heart....”

Only those who have passed through the exceeding tribulations of youth, who have seized on chimeras with white pinions, the nightmare fancies at the disposal of fervid imagination, can realize the horrors that seized upon Gaston when he supposed his ultimatum was in Mme. de Beauseant’s hands. He saw the Vicomtesse, wholly untouched, laughing at his letter and his love, as those laugh who have ceased to believe in love. He could have wished to have his letter back. It was an absurd letter. There were a thousand and one things, now that he thought of it, he might have said—infinitely better and more moving than those stilted phrases, those accursed, sophisticated, pretentious, fine-spun words—though, luckily, the punctuation had been poor and the lines shockingly crooked. He tried not to think, not to feel; but he felt and thought, and was wretched. If he had been thirty, he might have got drunk, but the innocence of three-and-twenty knew nothing of opium nor of the expedients of advanced civilization. Nor had he at hand one of those good friends of the Parisian pattern who understand so well how to say Poète, non dolet! by producing a bottle of champagne, or alleviate the agony of suspense by carrying you off somewhere to make a night of it. Capital fellows are they, always in low water when you are in funds, always off to some watering-place when you go to look them up, always with some bad bargain in horse-flesh to sell you; it is true, that when you want to borrow of them, they have always just lost their last louis at play; but in all other respects they are the best fellows on earth, always ready to embark with you on one of the steep down-grades where you lose your time, your soul, and your life!

At length M. de Nueil received a missive through Jacques, a letter sealed with the arms of Burgundy, written on vellum.

He locked himself away and read and reread her letter:—

“You are punishing me very severely, monsieur, both for the friendliness of my attempt to spare you a rebuff, and for the attraction which intellect always has for me. I put confidence in the generosity of youth, and you have disappointed me. And yet, if I did not speak unreservedly (which would have been perfectly ridiculous), at any rate I spoke frankly of my position, so that you might imagine that I was not to be touched by a young soul. My distress is the keener for my interest in you. I am naturally tender-hearted and kindly, but circumstances force me to act unkindly. Another woman would have flung your letter, unread, into the fire; I read it, and I am answering it. My answer will make it clear to you that while I am not untouched by the expression of this feeling which I have inspired, albeit unconsciously, I am still far from sharing it, and the step which I am about to take will show you still more plainly that I mean what I say. I wish besides, to use, for your welfare, that authority, as it were, which you give me over your life; and I desire to exercise it this once to draw aside the veil from your eyes.

“I am nearly thirty years old, monsieur; you are barely two-and-twenty. You yourself cannot know what your thoughts will be at my age. The vows that you make so lightly to-day may seem a very heavy burden to you then. I am quite willing to believe that at this moment you would give me your whole life without a regret, you would even be ready to die for a little brief happiness; but at the age of thirty experience will take from you the very power of making daily sacrifices for my sake, and I myself should feel deeply humiliated if I accepted them. A day would come when everything, even Nature, would bid you leave me, and I have already told you that death is preferable to desertion. Misfortune has taught me to calculate; as you see, I am arguing perfectly dispassionately. You force me to tell you that I have no love for you; I ought not to love, I cannot, and I will not. It is too late to yield, as women yield, to a blind unreasoning impulse of the heart, too late to be the mistress whom you seek. My consolations spring from God, not from earth. Ah, and besides, with the melancholy insight of disappointed love, I read hearts too clearly to accept your proffered friendship. It is only instinct. I forgive the boyish ruse, for which you are not responsible as yet. In the name of this passing fancy of yours, for the sake of your career and my own peace of mind, I bid you stay in your own country; you must not spoil a fair and honorable life for an illusion which, by its very nature, cannot last. At a later day, when you have accomplished your real destiny, in the fully developed manhood that awaits you, you will appreciate this answer of mine, though to-day it may be that you blame its hardness. You will turn with pleasure to an old woman whose friendship will certainly be sweet and precious to you then; a friendship untried by the extremes of passion and the disenchanting processes of life; a friendship which noble thoughts and thoughts of religion will keep pure and sacred. Farewell; do my bidding with the thought that your success will bring a gleam of pleasure into my solitude, and only think of me as we think of absent friends.”

Gaston de Nueil read the letter and wrote:—

“MADAME,—If I could cease to love you, to take the chances of becoming an ordinary man which you hold out to me, you must admit that I should thoroughly deserve my fate. No, I shall not do as you bid me; the oath of fidelity which I swear to you shall only be absolved by death. Ah! take my life, unless indeed you do not fear to carry a remorse all through your own—”

When the servant returned from his errand, the Baron asked with whom he had left the note.

“I gave it to Mme. la Vicomtesse herself, sir; she was in her carriage and just about to start.”

“For the town?”

“I don’t think so, sir. Mme. la Vicomtesse had post-horses.”

“Ah! then she is going away,” said the Baron.

“Yes, sir,” the man answered.

Gaston at once prepared to follow Mme. de Beauseant. She went as far as Geneva without suspecting he followed. Amid the many thoughts that assailed him during the journey, one question filled his mind: “Why did she go away?” Theories arose; he favored the one that flattered him—that if the Vicomtesse cared for him, a clever woman would choose Switzerland, where nobody knew either of them, rather than France with its critics. An impassioned lover of a certain stamp would not feel attracted to a woman clever enough to choose her own ground; such women are too clever. But there was no proof of Gaston’s supposition.

The Vicomtesse took a small house by the lake. As soon as she was installed, Gaston came one summer twilight. Jacques showed no surprise and announced M. le Baron de Nueil like a discreet domestic. At the sound of his name and sight of him, Mme. de Beauseant let her book fall; her surprise allowed him to come close and say in tones that sounded like music:

“What a joy it was to me to take the horses that brought you on this journey!”

To have the inmost desire of the heart so fulfilled—where is the woman who could resist such happiness? An Italian woman once remarked on French novels that she could not see why lovers took so long to come to terms, that the affair should be the work of a single morning. Why not, the remark implied, spare the reader excessive delay? Some passages of coquetry are pleasant; Mme. de Beauseant’s demurs and sweet delays might have been sketched rather than exhaustively narrated. M. de Nueil was at an age when a man is the dupe of such caprices, charmed by the fencing women prolong to dictate terms or savor their power. But these boudoir protocols are too small to be of consequence in the history of this passion.

For three years Mme. de Beauseant and M. de Nueil lived in the villa on Lake Geneva. They lived alone, received no visitors, caused no talk, rose late, went out on the lake together—knew, in short, the simple happiness of which we all dream. It was a little house, with green shutters, broad balconies shaded with awnings, a place contrived for lovers—white couches, soundless carpets, fresh hangings—everything reflecting their joy. Every window showed some new view of the lake; in the distance lay mountains, visions of changing color; above them the sunny sky; before them the broad sheet of water, never the same. All their surroundings seemed to dream for them; all things smiled upon them.

Weighty matters recalled M. de Nueil to France. His father and brother died, and he had to leave Geneva. The lovers bought the house; if they could they would have removed the hills and drawn off the lake, they said.

Mme. de Beauseant followed him. She realized her property and bought a considerable estate near Manerville, adjoining Gaston’s lands; here they lived together; Gaston graciously gave up Manerville to his mother for the present in consideration of the bachelor freedom she left him.

Her estate was close to a little town in a picturesque spot in the Auge. Here the lovers raised barriers between themselves and society, barriers no creature could overleap, and here the happy days of Switzerland were lived over again. For nine years they knew happiness which it serves no purpose to describe in detail; happiness to be divined by those who can comprehend poetry and prayer in their manifestations.

All this time Mme. de Beauseant’s husband, the present Marquis (his father and elder brother having died), enjoyed the soundest health. There is no better aid to life than a certain knowledge that our demise would confer a benefit on some fellow-creature. M. de Beauseant was one of those ironical and wayward beings who, like holders of life-annuities, wake with an extra relish for health. He was a man of the world, somewhat methodical and ceremonious, a calculator of consequences, who could make a declaration of love as quietly as a lackey announces “Madame is served.”

This brief notice explains why the Marquise could not marry M. de Nueil.

So, after a nine years’ lease of happiness—the sweetest agreement to which a woman ever put her hand—M. de Nueil and Mme. de Beauseant were still in a position as natural and as false as at the beginning. Yet they had reached a fatal crisis, which may be stated as clearly as any problem in mathematics.

Mme. la Comtesse de Nueil, Gaston’s mother, a strait-laced and virtuous person who had made the late Baron happy in strictly legal fashion, would never consent to meet Mme. de Beauseant. Mme. de Beauseant understood that the dowager must of necessity be her enemy and try to draw Gaston from his unhallowed life. The Marquise would willingly have sold her property and gone back to Geneva, but she could not bring herself to do it; it would mean distrusting M. de Nueil. Moreover, he had taken a great fancy to this Valleroy estate, making plantations and improvements. She would not deprive him of that small routine-work which women always wish for their husbands, and even for their lovers.

A Mlle. de la Rodiere, twenty-two, an heiress with a rent-roll of forty thousand livres, had come to live nearby. Gaston always met her at Manerville when obliged to go there. These personages stood to each other as terms in a proportion sum; the following letter will throw light on the appalling problem Mme. de Beauseant had been trying for the past month to solve:—

“My beloved angel, it seems like nonsense, does it not, to write to you when there is nothing to keep us apart, when a caress so often takes the place of words, and words too are caresses? Ah, well, no, love. There are some things a woman cannot say when she is face to face with the man she loves; at the thought of them her voice fails, and the blood goes back to her heart; she has no strength, no intelligence left. It hurts me to feel like this when you are near me, and it happens often. I feel that my heart should be wholly sincere for you; that I should disguise no thought, however transient, in my heart; and I love the sweet carelessness, which suits me so well, too much to endure this embarrassment and constraint any longer. So I will tell you about my anguish—yes, it is anguish. Listen to me! do not begin with the little “Tut, tut, tut” that you use to silence me, an impertinence that I love, because anything from you pleases me.

“Dear soul from heaven, wedded to mine, let me first tell you that you have effaced all memory of the pain that once was crushing the life out of me. I did not know what love was before I knew you. Only the candor of your beautiful young life, only the purity of that great soul of yours, could satisfy the requirements of an exacting woman’s heart. Dear love, how very often I have thrilled with joy to think that in these nine long, swift years, my jealousy has not been once awakened. All the flowers of your soul have been mine, all your thoughts. There has not been the faintest cloud in our heaven; we have not known what sacrifice is; we have always acted on the impulses of our hearts. I have known happiness, infinite for a woman. Will the tears that drench this sheet tell you all my gratitude? I could wish that I had knelt to write the words!

“Well, out of this felicity has arisen torture more terrible than the pain of desertion. Dear, there are very deep recesses in a woman’s heart; how deep in my own heart I did not know until to-day, as I did not know the whole extent of love. The greatest misery which could overwhelm us is a light burden compared with the mere thought of harm for him whom we love. And how if we cause the harm, is it not enough to make one die? This is the thought that weighs upon me. But it brings in its train another thought that is heavier far, a thought that tarnishes the glory of love, and slays it, and turns it into a humiliation which sullies life as long as it lasts. You are thirty years old; I am forty. What dread this difference calls up in a woman who loves! It is possible that, first unconsciously, afterwards in earnest, you have felt the sacrifices you made by renouncing all in the world for me. Perhaps you have thought of your future from the social point of view, of the marriage which would increase your fortune, give you avowed happiness and children who would inherit your wealth; perhaps you have thought of reappearing in the world and filling your place there honorably. And then, if so, you must have repressed those thoughts, and felt glad to sacrifice heiress and fortune and a fair future to me without my knowledge. In your young man’s generosity, you must have resolved to be faithful to the vows which bind us each to each in the sight of God. My past pain has risen up before your mind, and the misery from which you rescued me has been my protection. To owe your love to your pity! The thought is even more painful to me than the fear of spoiling your life for you. The man who can bring himself to stab his mistress is very charitable if he gives her her deathblow while she is happy and ignorant of evil, while illusions are in full blossom.... Yes, death is preferable to the two thoughts which have secretly saddened the hours for several days.

“To-day, when you asked ‘What ails you?’ so tenderly, the sound of your voice made me shiver. I thought that, after your wont, you were reading my very soul, and I waited for your confidence to come, thinking that my presentiments had come true, and that I had guessed all that was going on in your mind. Then I began to think over certain little things that you always do for me, and I thought I could see in you the sort of affection by which a man betrays a consciousness that his loyalty is becoming a burden. And in that moment I paid very dear for my happiness. I felt that Nature always demands the price for the treasure called love. Briefly, has not fate separated us? Can you have said, ‘Sooner or later I must leave poor Claire; why not separate in time?’ I read that thought in the depths of your eyes, and went away to cry by myself. Hiding my tears from you—the first tears I have shed for sorrow for these ten years; I am too proud to let you see them, but I did not reproach you.

“Yes, you are right. I ought not to be so selfish as to bind your long and brilliant career to my so-soon out-worn life.... And yet—how if I have been mistaken? How if I have taken your love melancholy for a deliberation? Oh, my love, do not leave me in suspense; punish this jealous wife of yours, but give her back the sense of her love and yours; the whole woman lies in that—that consciousness sanctifies everything.

“Since your mother came, since you paid a visit to Mlle. de Rodiere, I have been gnawed by doubts dishonoring to us both. Make me suffer for this, but do not deceive me; I want to know everything that your mother said and that you think! If you have hesitated between some alternative and me, I give you back your liberty.... I will not let you know what happens to me; I will not shed tears for you to see; only—I will not see you again.... Ah! I cannot go on, my heart is breaking..................
I have been sitting benumbed and stupid for some moments. Dear love, I do not find that any feeling of pride rises against you; you are so kind-hearted, so open; you would find it impossible to hurt me or to deceive me; and you will tell me the truth, however cruel it may be. Do you wish me to encourage your confession? Well, then, heart of mine, I shall find comfort in a woman’s thought. Has not the youth of your being been mine, your sensitive, wholly gracious, beautiful, and delicate youth? No woman shall find henceforth the Gaston whom I have known, nor the delicious happiness that he has given me.... No; you will never love again as you have loved, as you love me now; no, I shall never have a rival, it is impossible. There will be no bitterness in my memories of our love, and I shall think of nothing else. It is out of your power to enchant any woman henceforth by the childish provocations, the charming ways of a young heart, the soul’s winning charm, the body’s grace, the swift communion of rapture, the whole divine cortege of young love, in fine.

“Oh, you are a man now, you will obey your destiny, weighing and considering all things. You will have cares, anxieties, ambitions, and concerns that will rob her of the unchanging smile that made your lips fair for me. The tones that were always so sweet for me will be troubled at times; and your eyes that lighted up with radiance from heaven at the sight of me will often be lustreless for her. And besides, as it is impossible to love you as I love you, you will never care for that woman as you have cared for me. She will never keep a constant watch over herself as I have done; she will never study your happiness at every moment with an intuition which has never failed me. Ah, yes, the man, the heart and soul, which I shall have known will exist no longer. I shall bury him deep in my memory, that I may have the joy of him still; I shall live happy in that fair past life of ours, a life hidden from all but our inmost selves.

“Dear treasure of mine, if all the while no least thought of liberty has risen in your mind, if my love is no burden on you, if my fears are chimerical, if I am still your Eve—the one woman in the world for you—come to me as soon as you have read this letter, come quickly! Ah, in one moment I will love you more than I have ever loved you, I think, in these nine years. After enduring the needless torture of these doubts of which I am accusing myself, every added day of love, yes, every single day, will be a whole lifetime of bliss. So speak, and speak openly; do not deceive me, it would be a crime. Tell me, do you wish for your liberty? Have you thought of all that a man’s life means? Is there any regret in your mind? That I should cause you a regret! I should die of it. I have said it: I love you enough to set your happiness above mine, your life before my own. Leave on one side, if you can, the wealth of memories of our nine years’ happiness, that they may not influence your decision, but speak! I submit myself to you as to God, the one Consoler who remains if you forsake me.”

When Mme. de Beauseant knew that her letter was in M. de Nueil’s hands, she sank into utter prostration; the pressure of many thoughts so numbed her faculties that she seemed drowsy. She suffered a pain not always proportioned to a woman’s strength, a pain women alone know. And while the unhappy Marquise awaited her doom, M. de Nueil, reading her letter, felt that he was “in a very difficult position,” to use the phrase young men apply to such a crisis.

By this time he had all but yielded to his mother’s importunities and to the attractions of Mlle. de la Rodiere, a somewhat insignificant pink-and-white young person, as straight as a poplar. In accordance with the rules for marriageable young ladies she scarcely opened her mouth, but her rent-roll of forty thousand livres spoke sufficiently. Mme. de Nueil, with a mother’s sincere affection, tried to entangle her son in virtuous courses. She pointed out that it was flattering to be preferred by Mlle. de la Rodiere, who had refused many great matches; urged him to think of his future, that such an opportunity might not repeat itself, that some day he would have eighty thousand livres of income from land; insisted that money made everything bearable; and said that if Mme. de Beauseant loved him for his own sake she ought to be the first to urge him to marry. In short, the well-intentioned mother used every feminine argument on the masculine mind, and had brought her son into a wavering condition.

Mme. de Beauseant’s letter arrived just as Gaston’s love for her held out against the temptations of a settled conventional life. That letter decided the day. He made up his mind to break with the Marquise and to marry.

“One must live a man’s life,” he said to himself.

Then followed some inkling of the pain this decision would cause Mme. de Beauseant. His vanity and the lover’s conscience exaggerated that pain, and sincere pity for her seized him. All at once the immensity of the misery became apparent, and he thought it necessary and charitable to deaden the deadly blow. He hoped to bring Mme. de Beauseant to a calm frame of mind by gradually reconciling her to the idea of separation; while Mlle. de la Rodiere, always a shadowy third between them, should be sacrificed at first, only to be imposed upon her later. His marriage should take place later, in obedience to Mme. de Beauseant’s expressed wish. He even meant to enlist the Marquise’s nobleness, pride, and all the great qualities of her nature to help him in this compassionate design. He would write a letter at once to allay her suspicions. A letter! For a woman of exquisite perception, and the intuition of passionate love, a letter in itself was a sentence of death.

So when Jacques came and brought Mme. de Beauseant a sheet of paper folded in a triangle, she trembled, poor woman, like a snared swallow. A mysterious cold spread from head to foot, wrapping her in an icy winding sheet. If he did not rush to her feet, if he did not come to her in tears, pale, and like a lover, she knew that all was lost. And yet a woman who loves has so many hopes that she is only killed by stab after stab, and loves on till the last drop of blood drains away.

“Does madame need anything?” Jacques asked gently as he went away.

“No,” she said.

“Poor fellow!” she thought, brushing a tear from her eyes, “he guesses my feelings, servant though he is!”

She read: “My beloved, you are inventing idle terrors for yourself...” The Marquise gazed at the words, and a thick mist spread before her eyes. A voice in her heart cried, “He lies!” Then she glanced down the page with the clairvoyant eagerness of passion, and saw at the foot, “Nothing has been decided as yet...” Turning to the other side with convulsive quickness, she saw the mind of the writer through the intricacies of the wording; this was no spontaneous outburst of love. She crushed the paper in her fingers, twisted it, tore it with her teeth, flung it in the fire, and cried aloud, “Ah! base that he is! I was his, and he had ceased to love me!”

She sank half dead upon the couch.

M. de Nueil went out as soon as he had written his letter. When he came back, Jacques met him on the threshold with a note. “Madame la Marquise has left the chateau,” said the man.

M. de Nueil, in amazement, broke the seal and read:—

“MADAME,—If I could cease to love you, to take the chances of becoming an ordinary man which you hold out to me, you must admit that I should thoroughly deserve my fate. No, I shall not do as you bid me; the oath of fidelity which I swear to you shall only be absolved by death. Ah! take my life, unless indeed you do not fear to carry a remorse all through your own...”

It was his own letter, written to the Marquise as she set out for Geneva nine years before. At the foot of it Claire de Bourgogne had written, “Monsieur, you are free.”

M. de Nueil went to his mother at Manerville. In less than three weeks he married Mlle. Stephanie de la Rodiere.

If this commonplace story of real life ended here, it would be to some extent a sort of mystification. The first man you meet can tell you a better. But the widespread fame of the catastrophe (for, unhappily, this is a true tale), and all the memories it may arouse in those who have known the divine delights of infinite passion and lost them by their own deed or through the cruelty of fate,—these things may perhaps shelter the story from criticism.

Mme. la Marquise de Beauseant never left Valleroy after her parting from M. de Nueil. After his marriage she still continued to live there, for some inscrutable woman’s reason; any woman may assign the one which most appeals to her. Claire de Bourgogne lived in complete retirement so that none of the servants, save Jacques and her own woman, ever saw their mistress. She required absolute silence, and only left her room to go to the chapel on the Valleroy estate, whither a neighboring priest came to say mass every morning.

The Comte de Nueil sank a few days after his marriage into something like conjugal apathy, which might be read as happiness or unhappiness equally easily.

“My son is perfectly happy,” his mother said everywhere.

Mme. Gaston de Nueil, like many young women, was a rather colorless character, sweet and passive. A month after her marriage she expected to become a mother. This was in keeping with ordinary views. M. de Nueil was very nice to her; but two months after his separation from the Marquise, he grew notably thoughtful and abstracted. But then he always had been serious, his mother said.

After seven months of this tepid happiness, a little thing occurred—one of those seeming trifles that imply such development of thought and wide soul-trouble that only the bare fact can be recorded; the interpretation must be left to each mind. One day, after shooting over Manerville and Valleroy, M. de Nueil crossed Mme. de Beauseant’s park on his way home, summoned Jacques, and asked him whether the Marquise liked game as ever.

Jacques answered in the affirmative, and Gaston offered him a good sum (with specious reasoning) for a small service. Would he set aside for the Marquise the game the Count would bring? It seemed to Jacques of no great importance whether the partridge on which his mistress dined had been shot by her keeper or by M. de Nueil, especially since the latter particularly wished that the Marquise should know nothing about it.

“It was killed on her land,” said the Count, and for some days Jacques lent himself to the harmless deceit. Day after day M. de Nueil went shooting, and came back at dinner-time with an empty bag. A whole week went by. Gaston grew bold enough to write a long letter to the Marquise, and had it conveyed to her. It was returned to him unopened. The Marquise’s servant brought it back about nightfall. The Count, sitting in the drawing-room listening while his wife at the piano mangled a Caprice of Hérold’s, suddenly sprang up and rushed out to the Marquise, as if flying to an assignation. He dashed through a well-known gap into the park, and went slowly along the avenues, stopping now and again to still the loud beating of his heart. Smothered sounds as he neared the chateau told him that the servants must be at supper, and he went straight to Mme. de Beauseant’s room.

Mme. de Beauseant never left her bedroom. M. de Nueil could gain the doorway without making the slightest sound. There, by the light of two wax candles, he saw the thin, white Marquise in a great armchair; her head bowed, her hands hung listlessly, her eyes fixed on some object she did not seem to see. Her whole attitude spoke hopeless pain. There was a vague something like hope in her bearing, but it was impossible to say whether Claire de Bourgogne looked forwards to the tomb or backwards into the past. Perhaps M. de Nueil’s tears glittered in the deep shadows; perhaps his breathing sounded faintly; perhaps unconsciously he trembled—again it may have been impossible that he should stand there, his presence unfelt by that quick sense which becomes an instinct, the glory, the delight, the proof of perfect love. However it was, Mme. de Beauseant slowly turned her face toward the doorway, and beheld her lover of bygone days. Then Gaston de Nueil came forward a few paces.

“If you come any further, sir,” exclaimed the Marquise, growing paler, “I shall fling myself out of the window!”

She sprang to the window, flung it open, and stood with one foot on the ledge, her hand on the iron balustrade, her face turned towards Gaston.

“Go out! go out!” she cried, “or I will throw myself over.”

At that dreadful cry the servants began to stir, and M. de Nueil fled like a criminal.

When he reached home he wrote a few lines and gave them to his own man, telling him to give the letter himself into Mme. de Beauseant’s hands, and to say it was a matter of life and death for his master. The messenger went. M. de Nueil returned to the drawing-room where his wife was still murdering the Caprice, and sat down to wait till the answer came. An hour later, when the Caprice had ended and the husband and wife sat in silence on opposite sides of the hearth, the man came back from Valleroy and gave his master his own letter, unopened.

M. de Nueil went into a small room beyond the drawing-room, where he had left his rifle, and shot himself.

The swift and fatal ending of the drama, contrary to the habits of young France, is only what might have been expected. Those who have closely observed, or known by delicious experience, the perfect union of two beings will understand Gaston de Nueil’s suicide. A woman does not bend and form herself in a day to the caprices of passion. The pleasure of loving, like some rare flower, needs careful culture. Time alone, and two souls attuned to each other, can discover all its resources, and call into being the tender and delicate delights for which we are steeped in a thousand superstitions, imagining them inherent in the loving heart. It is this wonderful response of one nature to another, this religious belief, this certainty of peculiar happiness in the presence of one we love, that accounts for perdurable attachments and long-lived passion. If a man who has once possessed this love shuts himself out from it by his own act and sinks to a loveless marriage; if, by some incident in married life, the woman with whom he hoped to know the same felicity makes it clear it will never be revived; if, with the sweetness of divine love still on his lips, he has dealt a deadly wound to her—his wife in truth, whom he forsook for a social chimera—then he must either die or adopt a materialistic, selfish, heartless philosophy, from which impassioned souls shrink.

As for Mme. de Beauseant, she doubtless did not imagine her friend’s despair could drive him to suicide when he had drunk deep of love for nine years. Possibly she thought she alone was to suffer. At any rate she did rightly to refuse the most humiliating of all positions; a wife may stoop for weighty social reasons to a kind of compromise which a mistress must hold in abhorrence, for in the purity of her passion lies all its justification.

ANGOULEME, September 1832.
