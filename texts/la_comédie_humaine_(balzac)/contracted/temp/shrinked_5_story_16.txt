In the spring of 1822 Parisian doctors sent a young man, M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil, to Lower Normandy to recover from an inflammatory illness likely due to overwork or excess. Seeking rest, a light diet, and fresh air, he settled in Bayeux, where his relatives welcomed him warmly.

After a few evenings with his cousin, Mme. de Sainte-Severe, Gaston soon met the local elite, who embodied provincial French types. One was an ancient but impoverished noble family: a patriarch, a determined sportsman contemptuous of modernity and the new political order; a loud, domineering wife who assumed their daughters’ fortunes rested solely on their name. They clung to obsolete customs, representing a nobility without wealth or influence.

In contrast stood a wealthier, more recent family who embraced contemporary Parisian trends. The wife was a society woman—slightly behind the latest fashions—while the husband, a member of the Departmental Council, was shrewd with finances and versed in political gossip. Their Royalism was less zealous than the old nobles’, engaging with modern life while keeping ancestral ties.

The local bishop, a former Vicar-General, mediated between these families: respected for his office but often reminded of his plebeian origins. Retired military officers and their families also featured in the circle; they preserved a veneer of aristocracy but focused on local affairs rather than national politics. The women dressed as if for court life, yet their days were governed by routine and tradition.

This exclusive social set included a few wealthy townspeople who had infiltrated the aristocracy, regarded skeptically by the older generation. Ecclesiastics added variety, bringing wit and a bourgeois element to otherwise staid gatherings.

Collectively, the society was marked by antiquated beliefs and resistance to change. Conversations returned to familiar themes, members issued immutable judgments, and intellectual life stagnated. New ideas were unwelcome; any outsider who criticized their way of life was quickly labeled a misfit.

Before his arrival, Gaston had already been appraised by the local elite: his cousin had disclosed his fortune and family background. At twenty-three, he was welcomed as a promising young man with an income of eighteen thousand livres from his land in the Auge valley; his father was negotiating to buy more woodland, further securing their status.

Yet Gaston’s family situation was complicated by an elder brother who was consumptive and likely dying—a fact Mme. de Sainte-Severe conveniently omitted. At first Gaston amused himself by observing and sketching the quirks of his acquaintances, enjoying their quaintness. But as weeks passed he grew weary of their monotony, feeling trapped in a cloistered existence that drained his spirits.

This confinement produced a crisis of spirit: ennui and mounting discontent. As he adapted to the unchanging environment, Gaston began losing touch with the vibrant life he had known in Paris. The once vivid memories of intellectual engagement dimmed, and he risked becoming just another fossil among the fossils—content to exist in a dull routine, like the companions of Ulysses who settled for complacency.

One evening Gaston de Nueil sat between a dowager and a vicar-general in a gray-paneled drawing-room where family portraits looked down on four card tables and sixteen people playing whist. After a pleasant dinner he observed provincial customs—the old cards and threadbare tablecloths—and their philosophic indifference to luxury, which made him question the value of opulence. The vibrant life of Paris felt distant.

His attention was caught by a young lady with red hands and a shy demeanor, at first awkward and unattractive. A chance remark about Mme. de Beauseant, however, stirred him like a passage of good music in a tedious opera. An elderly lady noted the Vicomtesse was unwell and had declined an invitation to dinner, which piqued Gaston’s curiosity. The conversation revealed Mme. de Beauseant’s scandalous past with M. d’Ajuda-Pinto and her ensuing isolation at Courcelles. Despite noble lineage, she was shunned because of her separation from her husband—a circumstance that intrigued him.

He became captivated by the idea of Mme. de Beauseant, seeing her as a figure of hope and desire amid the dullness of provincial life. An obsession grew; he pictured her as a woman worth pursuing in a stagnant world. His thoughts turned to how to gain access to her, conscious of the obstacles posed by her intelligence and social standing.

The next morning he wandered near Courcelles, hoping for a chance encounter, his imagination suffused with romance. He considered writing but feared rejection. Instead he planned to enlist M. de Champignelles to arrange an introduction. He told the Marquis he had a delicate matter to discuss with the Vicomtesse and asked him to inquire whether she would receive him. Gaston’s charm and sincerity convinced the Marquis, who was eager to oblige a pretty man.

M. de Champignelles hurried to Courcelles and, conveying Gaston’s request, aroused Mme. de Beauseant’s curiosity despite her initial reluctance. After deliberation she agreed to meet him, intrigued by the mystery surrounding this young suitor.

Meanwhile Gaston was filled with excitement and anxiety about the meeting, hoping to impress her. He prepared meticulously, believing his appearance would shape her perception—unaware that true attraction would depend more on wit and character than looks.

As he approached Courcelles he felt a mix of confidence and nervousness, conscious that entering a room with one woman could be more daunting than facing a crowd. When he was announced he entered with a blend of ease and trepidation, ready to engage the enigmatic Vicomtesse.

A great fire burned on the hearth despite the mild weather, casting a soft light over a young woman seated in a high-backed bergère by the flames. The Vicomtesse, with graceful movements, stirred the coals and picked up a fallen glove, then pointed with her delicate hand to a chair for M. de Nueil. Her charm and poise combined a fastidious carelessness with high-bred elegance, distinguishing her from the automatons he had encountered during his two-month exile in Normandy. In her presence he felt transported back to Paris, immersed again in the beauty and sophistication he had missed.

Mme. de Beauseant had fair hair, dark eyes, and a pale complexion that gave her a regal air like a fallen angel; her hair, gathered into a crown of plaits, heightened that noble appearance. Yet beneath this beauty was an underlying sadness, a trace of past unhappiness and lost love. Alone in her silent salon, she seemed cut off from the world, haunted by memories of a youth once vibrant but now emptied. M. de Nueil felt humbled by her presence, captivated by the mixture of beauty, nobility, and misfortune that surrounded her.

When she acknowledged him, he felt the weight of his deception. He had come to deliver a message from M. de Champignelles, but the consciousness of his dishonesty made him flush. Summoning his courage, he confessed, “Madame, I do not deserve the happiness of seeing you. I have deceived you basely.” His apology, however, met with disdain; she rang for her servant to escort him out, and her manner shifted from gracious to icy.

As he left, he realized that if he went away now he would never return, and the Vicomtesse would think him a fool. He hesitated, considering that she might repent her dismissal. On a sudden impulse he turned back, claiming he had forgotten something; the servant, ignorant of the true reason for his return, followed him back into the salon.

“Jacques lighted me to the door,” he said with a half-sad smile, which softened Mme. de Beauseant’s demeanor. She invited him to sit; he did so eagerly, his eyes shining with happiness. She felt a thrill at his delight, grateful for his apparent comprehension of her complex emotions.

“Madame,” he began, “my blunder you know, but you do not know how much I am to blame.” She interrupted him with a playful gesture, warning him to be careful. Her expression then darkened as she reflected on the change in her life. “This is all very ridiculous! I cannot do as I like; I am obliged to think. What brings you here? Is it curiosity? If so, I am paying dearly for a fleeting pleasure.”

She accused him of disrespect and asked whether he had come to pity her, asserting she would rather be scorned than pitied. The tension in the room grew as she expressed her pain at his intrusion, urging him to see the impropriety of his conduct. She commanded him not to return, fearful that any further contact would provoke gossip about their relationship.

M. de Nueil acknowledged his fault, attributing it to youthful enthusiasm and a craving for happiness. He explained that his longing to see her had been natural—the weariness of exile and the inner fires that drove him. He painted himself as a young man yearning for love, who had made her the ideal woman of his dreams. He spoke of the feelings and wild thoughts that had led him to seek her out, hoping to awaken in her some indulgence for the folly he had committed in her name.

An impassioned voice warmed the chill solitude with youthful charm. Mme. de Beauseant, moved by its genuine feeling, found herself captivated by M. de Nueil’s noble countenance—Gaston, in the bloom of youth, seeming the ideal man, untouched by ambition or vanity. Both were drawn to each other, reflecting on their feelings while concealing them. M. de Nueil saw in the Vicomtesse not only beauty but depth of feeling and goodness. Mme. de Beauseant, hearing Gaston’s youthful troubles, recognized the struggles of someone kept pure by hard work against society’s corruptions.

As they explored their sentiments, Gaston’s approach was instinctive; Mme. de Beauseant’s had purpose—she probed his views by posing opposing opinions. Her wit and grace put him at ease, prompting his exclamation, “Oh! madame, how could any man have left you?” The remark struck her, reviving a sense of innocence, condemning the world for her past, and offering a kind of absolution that helped her rise above former shame.

She glanced at the clock and, with a gentle smile, acknowledged the fleeting nature of their encounter. Gaston, sensing its gravity, wished to prolong the moment. Mme. de Beauseant, however, insisted on preserving her solitary life, fearing further connection would tarnish her reputation. She spoke candidly of a forsaken love and the lessons solitude had taught her.

Her eloquence and beauty captivated Gaston, who longed to declare his feelings but feared rejection. Torn between admiration and dread of appearing foolish, he eventually confessed his love and offered to heal her old wounds. Mme. de Beauseant, moved yet resolute, reminded him they must remain strangers and spoke of the pain of being unable to follow the man she loved through life’s changes.

Afterward Gaston left feeling more distant, uncertain whether her charm was genuine or mere coquetry. He returned home consumed by thoughts of her, alternating between despair and hope. At twenty-three he was hampered by diffidence, feeling unworthy of her love and anxious about expressing himself.

That night he was restless and began writing letters, none satisfactory. The next day he returned to Courcelles at night, cautious to avoid being seen. After much deliberation he penned a heartfelt letter, laying bare his deep feelings and the mark she had left on his life. He implored her not to dismiss his words, exposing his vulnerability and desperation.

As he awaited her response, doubts gnawed at him; he feared she would laugh at his letter and at his love. He wished to retract his words, finding them inadequate and pretentious. Overwhelmed by emotion, he longed for friends to distract him from his turmoil.

In this state of despair, Gaston confronted the harshness of unrequited passion, bearing the weight of youthful longing and the uncertainty of a future without Mme. de Beauseant.

M. de Nueil received a letter from Mme. de Beauseant, marked with the arms of Burgundy. He locked himself away to read:

“You are punishing me for my attempt to spare you a rebuff and for my attraction to intellect. I trusted in your youth's generosity, but you have disappointed me. I spoke frankly to show you that I am not to be swayed by youthful passion. My distress is heightened by my interest in you; I am kind-hearted, but circumstances force me to act otherwise. Another woman might have discarded your letter, but I read it and am responding. I acknowledge your feelings, but I do not share them. I wish to use the authority you give me over your life to help you see clearly.

“I am nearly thirty; you are just over twenty. You cannot know what your thoughts will be at my age. The vows you make lightly now may become burdensome later. I believe you would give your life for a moment's happiness, but as you age you will find it hard to make sacrifices for me, and I would feel humiliated to accept them. A day will come when everything will urge you to leave me, and I have said that death is preferable to desertion. I must tell you that I do not love you; I cannot, and I will not. It is too late for me to yield to a blind impulse of the heart. My solace comes from God, not from earthly attachments. I see hearts too clearly to accept your friendship. I forgive your youthful folly, but for your sake and mine, I urge you to stay in your own country. Do not ruin a fair life for an illusion that cannot last. In the future, when you have achieved your destiny, you will appreciate my answer, even if it seems harsh now. You will cherish the friendship of an older woman, untainted by passion, and it will be sweet and precious to you. Farewell; think of me as you would an absent friend.”

Gaston de Nueil wrote back at once:

“MADAME,--If I could cease to love you and accept the ordinary life you propose, I would deserve my fate. I will not do as you ask; my oath of fidelity will only be absolved by death. Take my life, unless you fear to carry remorse.”

When the messenger returned, M. de Nueil asked where he had left the note.

“I gave it to Mme. la Vicomtesse; she was in her carriage, about to leave.”

“For the town?”

“I don’t think so; she had post-horses.”

“Ah! then she is going away,” said the Baron.

“Yes, sir.”

Gaston de Nueil immediately prepared to follow Mme. de Beauseant. She traveled to Geneva, unaware of his pursuit. His mind was consumed with one question: Why did she leave? He supposed that if she cared for him she would choose Switzerland, where they could be free from judgment.

The vicomtesse rented a small house by the lake. One summer evening Gaston visited. Jacques, the servant, announced him without surprise. Mme. de Beauseant dropped her book in shock, giving him a moment to approach and express his joy at following her.

For three years they lived in the villa by Lake Geneva, alone and without visitors, enjoying a happiness that seemed like a dream. Their home was simple but suited lovers: beautiful views of the lake and mountains, quiet days cherished together. Weighty matters called M. de Nueil back to France after the deaths of his father and brother. They sold nothing then; they bought the family house, wishing they could take the villa with them.

Mme. de Beauseant followed, selling her property to acquire an estate near Manerville, adjacent to Gaston’s lands. They lived together; Gaston allowed his mother to occupy Manerville, and once more they enjoyed secluded happiness.

For nine years they knew bliss beyond description. But Mme. de Beauseant’s husband, the Marquis, remained in good health, and his steadiness made a lawful union impossible. The situation grew precarious: Mme. la Comtesse de Nueil, Gaston’s mother, would never accept Mme. de Beauseant. The Marquise saw that the dowager might try to draw Gaston away. She thought of selling her estate and returning to Geneva but could not bring herself to do so, fearing it would show distrust in Gaston.

A young heiress, Mlle. de la Rodiere, moved nearby, and Gaston met her at Manerville. This provoked a letter from Mme. de Beauseant revealing her inner turmoil:

“My beloved angel, it seems absurd to write when we are together, but there are things I cannot say face to face. I feel anguish. You have erased my past pain, and I did not know what love was until I met you. I have never felt jealousy in our nine years together, and I have known infinite happiness. But now I fear that you may feel burdened by our relationship. You are thirty; I am forty. This age difference weighs on me. You may think of your future, of a marriage that would bring you wealth and children. To owe your love to pity is a painful thought.

“Have you considered leaving me? I sensed your hesitation, and it made me cry. I am too proud to let you see my tears, but I did not reproach you. I should not bind your future to my fading life. But what if I am mistaken? If you still love me, come to me after reading this letter. I will love you more than ever. Speak openly; do not deceive me. If you wish your freedom, I will not let you see my suffering. I love you enough to prioritize your happiness over mine.”

When Mme. de Beauseant learned her letter was in M. de Nueil’s hands, she was overwhelmed; her thoughts numbed. M. de Nueil, reading her words, found himself in a difficult position—a crisis that many young men face.

By this time he had nearly yielded to his mother’s pleadings and the attraction of Mlle. de la Rodiere, a plain young woman with a forty-thousand-livre dowry. Mme. de Nueil, with maternal concern, urged him toward this respectable match, stressing the honor of being chosen by a woman who had refused many distinguished suitors. She reminded him to consider his future: he might one day secure eighty thousand livres from land and thus an easier life. If Mme. de Beauseant truly loved him, she should encourage his marriage. Using every argument, his mother left him torn.

Just as Gaston’s attachment to Mme. de Beauseant resisted the pull of convention, a letter from her changed everything. He resolved to end their liaison and marry. “One must live a man’s life,” he told himself, though he knew the choice would wound Mme. de Beauseant. Vanity and conscience deepened his sorrow; he felt genuine pity for her. He decided to prepare her gently for their separation, at first sacrificing Mlle. de la Rodiere to ease the transition. He planned to marry later, to honor Mme. de Beauseant’s wishes, and meant to write her a letter to quiet her suspicions. But he knew a woman of such perception might be devastated by a mere note.

When Jacques delivered the letter, Mme. de Beauseant trembled, dread of impending loss closing in. If he did not come to her in person, she feared all was lost; yet a woman in love can endure much pain. As she read, an inner voice cried, “He lies!” She saw the words were not a true declaration but a calculated message. In despair she tore the letter and cast it into the fire, feeling betrayed and abandoned.

M. de Nueil went to fetch Jacques, who soon returned with a note. “Madame la Marquise has left the chateau,” he said. M. de Nueil opened the note and found it was his own letter from nine years earlier, in which he had sworn eternal fidelity to Mme. de Beauseant; at the bottom Claire de Bourgogne had written, “Monsieur, you are free.” Stunned, he went to his mother and, within three weeks, married Mlle. Stephanie de la Rodiere.

If the tale ended there it would seem trivial. But the notoriety of this true tragedy resonates with those who have known love’s profound joys and losses. After their parting Mme. de Beauseant never left Valleroy. She lived in seclusion, rarely seen except by servants, and attended mass daily.

M. de Nueil, soon after his marriage, fell into a state of emotional indifference—interpreted variously as happiness or unhappiness. His mother insisted he was content. Mme. Gaston de Nueil, like many young brides, was sweet but shallow. A month after their wedding she expected a child, fitting social expectations. Two months later, however, M. de Nueil grew increasingly pensive.

After seven months of this lukewarm contentment a seemingly trivial incident hinted at deeper turmoil. One day after hunting, M. de Nueil crossed Mme. de Beauseant’s park and asked Jacques if she still enjoyed game. Jacques confirmed, and M. de Nueil paid him to set aside whatever he shot for her. Jacques saw no harm in this, and for days M. de Nueil returned with an empty bag while Jacques discreetly delivered the game to the Marquise.

Eventually M. de Nueil dared to write Mme. de Beauseant, but his letter was returned unopened. One evening, as his wife played the piano, he suddenly rushed to the Marquise’s room. There he found her in despair, her posture emptied of hope. When she saw him, she threatened to throw herself from the window if he approached. Terrified, M. de Nueil fled.

Back home he wrote another letter, insisting it was a matter of life and death, and sent it with his servant. An hour later the servant returned with the unopened note. In despair M. de Nueil retired to another room and shot himself.

The tragic end, though at odds with young France’s conventions, was perhaps inevitable. Those who have experienced a profound soul-connection will understand Gaston de Nueil’s suicide. Love requires time and nurturing; when a man abandons deep love for a loveless marriage, he faces either despair or a cold, material life.

Mme. de Beauseant likely did not foresee that her friend’s anguish might lead to such an extreme act. She may have believed herself the only sufferer. Still, she rightly refused to accept humiliation; a wife may compromise for social reasons, but a mistress cannot submit to such degradation, for the purity of her passion demands respect.

ANGOULEME, September 1832.
