Unfortunately, many young women like Rosalie exist, and this story serves as a cautionary tale for them.

During that winter, Albert de Savarus made significant strides in Besancon, eagerly anticipating the dissolution of the Chamber. He had garnered support from a wealthy contractor influential in the moderate party. The Romans had historically invested heavily in ensuring a good water supply for their towns, and Besancon sourced its water from Arcier, a distant hill. The idea of restoring an aqueduct to drink the same water as the Romans seemed absurd, especially in a town already served by the Doubs River. However, if the citizens could be convinced, it would lead to substantial expenses benefiting the contractor.

Savarus believed the river water was unfit for drinking, advocating instead for the Arcier water. Articles in the local _Review_ echoed this sentiment, and the townspeople, regardless of their political affiliations, agreed on the need to drink the same water as the Romans. This issue became a focal point in Besancon, driven by hidden interests. Those opposing the scheme were dismissed as old-fashioned, while Savarus’s projects dominated discussions. After eighteen months of effort, he had stirred the stagnant town, gaining influence without being popular.

Throughout the winter, he won several lawsuits for local priests, and his ambition consumed him. His apparent disinterest in financial gain was, in reality, a form of moral usury, as he anticipated a much greater reward.

In October 1834, he helped a struggling merchant with funds from Leopold Hannequin, which also provided him with a qualification for election. The Abbe de Grancey, who understood Savarus’s ambitions, remarked on his remarkable nature, suggesting he had taken a wrong turn in life.

Meanwhile, Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hotel de Rupt, initially content to observe him from afar. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, was worth ten thousand francs a year, but its management was neglected by the Baron, who left it to an old servant named Modinier. The Baron's indifference stemmed from his wife's substantial income, and they often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque location created by the famous Watteville.

Watteville constructed a dam and lake, enhancing the beauty of the area, but his heirs maintained a questionable claim over the village of Riceys, which had long enjoyed rights to the land. The Baron's death left a legacy of usurpation, and his descendants continued to assert control over the village.

Rosalie’s efforts to achieve her goals were complex and subtle. In May 1835, she left Besancon with her father for les Rouxey, where she was captivated by the lake’s beauty, associating it with love. She suggested a boat trip to explore the lake, during which her father confided about legal troubles with the village of Riceys over land rights. Rosalie encouraged him to hire Savarus as a lawyer, emphasizing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for their estate.

She argued that a lawsuit would add excitement to their lives and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey. Rosalie’s determination to secure her future at les Rouxey was evident, as she envisioned creating beautiful gardens there.

Four days later, the Abbe de Grancey visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. However, Savarus was hesitant to align himself with them, fearing it would compromise his neutrality. The Abbe urged him to consider the political advantages of supporting the Wattevilles, especially given the potential for votes.

Rosalie, meanwhile, was determined to keep Savarus in Besancon and prevent his election. She wrote a letter to the Prefet, revealing Savarus’s ambitions, but the Prefet disregarded it. Rosalie understood the political landscape and sought to manipulate it to her advantage.

As the election approached, a meeting of influential men in Besancon decided to nominate Savarus as a candidate, much to his surprise. He was initially unaware of the political machinations at play. The Abbe de Grancey, who had been observing the situation, warned Savarus of potential betrayals and advised him to be transparent about his past.

Savarus delivered a passionate speech outlining his vision and ambitions, captivating his audience. However, he faced challenges as the Prefet worked to undermine his support, leading to a decline in his voter base. Despite his efforts, the political landscape shifted against him, and he struggled to maintain his position.

On the day of the first voting, the results were disappointing for Savarus, who felt the weight of his ambitions and the pressure of the election. He returned home in despair, reflecting on his love for Francesca and the uncertainty of his future.

Just as he was about to succumb to hopelessness, the Abbe de Grancey arrived with news of hope. The priest had been working behind the scenes to secure support for Savarus, urging him to visit the Wattevilles and promising that their case would be resolved in time for the election.

The Abbe revealed that by winning the Rouxey lawsuit, Savarus could secure crucial votes, and he encouraged him to pursue a marriage with Sidonie de Chavoncourt to further solidify his political standing. Savarus was initially resistant but realized the potential benefits of such a union.

The Abbe’s guidance proved invaluable, and Savarus prepared to navigate the complexities of both love and politics, understanding that his future depended on the choices he made in the coming days.

By nine o’clock the following evening, Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were filled with the aristocracy of Besançon, gathered to discuss the unusual decision to go to the polls for the daughter of the Rupts. It was known that the former Master of Appeals, a secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch, would be presented that evening. Madame de Chavoncourt attended with her younger daughter Sidonie, who was elegantly dressed, while her elder sister, confident in her relationship, had not made an effort with her appearance. In small towns, such details are noted. The Abbe de Grancey moved among the groups, listening intently and offering incisive comments that shaped the conversation.

“If the Elder Branch returns,” he asked an elderly statesman, “what politicians would they find?” The statesman replied that Berryer, alone on his bench, was uncertain and could disrupt the Government if he had enough support. They discussed the nomination of the Duc de Fitz-James in Toulouse and how voting for Monsieur Savarus could align Republicans with them instead of the Moderates.

By nine-thirty, Albert had still not arrived, and Madame de Watteville began to view his tardiness as disrespectful. Madame de Chavoncourt advised her not to let such trivial matters affect serious issues, suggesting that Albert might be delayed by a consultation. Rosalie, observing her mother’s exchange with Madame de Chavoncourt, noted her leniency towards Albert, hinting at a potential marriage between Sidonie and him.

At ten o’clock, Albert was still absent, and frustration grew among the guests. Some gentlemen began playing cards, while the Abbe de Grancey, perplexed, went to the window where Rosalie was hidden, exclaiming in disbelief, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, along with Monsieur de Watteville and Rosalie, ventured into the garden to check Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent.

“Jerome!” Rosalie called to the servant below. When he confirmed that Albert had left in a post-chaise, the Abbe de Grancey exclaimed, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” Rosalie’s expression betrayed her joy, which the Vicar-General noticed but pretended to ignore. He wondered about her involvement in the situation.

Returning to the drawing-room, Monsieur de Watteville shared the shocking news of Albert’s departure without explanation. By half-past eleven, only fifteen guests remained, including Madame de Chavoncourt, the Abbe de Godenars, the two Chavoncourt girls, Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbe de Grancey, Rosalie, Amedee de Soulas, and a retired magistrate who had supported Albert’s election. The Abbe de Grancey positioned himself near the Baroness to observe Rosalie, whose usually pale face now showed signs of agitation.

“What could have happened to Monsieur de Savarus?” Madame de Chavoncourt wondered aloud. At that moment, a servant brought a letter for the Abbe de Grancey on a silver tray. The Baroness urged him to read it. As he did, he noticed Rosalie turn pale, recognizing the handwriting. He folded the letter and discreetly pocketed it, having exchanged glances with Rosalie that hinted at her feelings.

“She is in love with Albert Savarus!” he concluded. As he prepared to leave, Rosalie intercepted him, saying, “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!” When he questioned how she recognized the writing, her passionate response revealed her feelings: “I love him! What is the matter?” He informed her that Albert was withdrawing from the election. Rosalie, taken aback, asked him to keep this information confidential, realizing that if the election ended, so too would the prospect of her sister’s marriage to Albert.

In the morning, on her way to Mass, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette about the circumstances surrounding Albert’s sudden disappearance. An old gentleman from Paris had arrived at the Hotel National, prompting a visit to Monsieur Savaron. The lawyer turned pale upon seeing the gentleman's card, and they met for over an hour. Afterward, the gentleman left with a large package, while Savaron appeared distressed. He then went to the Prefecture, purchased an old carriage, and ordered post horses, leaving behind his keys and telling his servant to find another job.

The next day, news of Savaron's disappearance caused a stir in Besançon, with the Prefect quickly granting him a passport. This event fueled local prejudices against outsiders, and soon Albert was forgotten by most, except for Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo's death.

By April 1836, there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness de Watteville suggested Rosalie should marry, as she had changed significantly. The Baron agreed to give Rosalie the family estate, les Rouxey, but the Baroness was concerned about the financial implications. They decided on a marriage with Monsieur de Soulas, but Rosalie firmly rejected the idea, insisting she was happy as she was.

The conflict between Rosalie and her mother escalated, leading to a summer spent at les Rouxey, where Rosalie's refusal of the marriage became known. After Mariette and Jerome married, the Baron made improvements to the estate, which the Baroness viewed with resentment. As tensions grew, the Vicar-General attempted to mediate between mother and daughter.

One day, Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General that she had interfered in Albert's relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo. She had intercepted letters, leading to their separation. The Vicar-General was horrified by her actions, emphasizing the moral weight of her crimes. He urged her to seek forgiveness and to understand the consequences of her actions.

Rosalie's guilt deepened when she learned of Albert's fate. A clerk from Paris, representing Albert, sought to sell his belongings, revealing that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery, receiving a letter from Albert expressing his complete forgiveness for Rosalie and his desire to remain in solitude.

As the Vicar-General shared this news with Rosalie, he urged her to reconcile with her mother and marry Monsieur de Soulas. However, Rosalie remained defiant, unwilling to destroy her mother’s happiness. The Vicar-General's death left a void, and soon after, Madame de Watteville married Monsieur de Soulas, believing Rosalie was supportive.

Rosalie, now legally of age, encouraged her mother to marry, securing her own financial independence. With a substantial fortune, Rosalie entered Parisian society, where she sought to confront the Duchess d’Argaiolo. At a ball, she publicly revealed her role in Albert's downfall, leaving the Duchess shaken.

Rosalie returned to Besançon, having achieved her goal of exposing the truth, but the emotional toll of her actions weighed heavily on her. The story of love, betrayal, and the consequences of ambition unfolded, leaving a lasting impact on all involved.

Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone on her estate of les Rouxey, engaging in riding, hunting, and land improvement, which made her a notable figure in the Eastern provinces. Madame de Soulas, who has two children, appears younger, while her husband, Monsieur de Soulas, has aged significantly. He remarked to young Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

Mademoiselle de Watteville is known for her eccentric behavior, with people noting her “vagaries.” Each year, she visits the walls of the Grande Chartreuse, possibly inspired by her grand-uncle, who broke through monastery walls to find freedom. In 1841, she left Besancon, reportedly to marry, but the true reason remains unclear. She returned home after a tragic accident on a steamboat on the Loire, where a boiler explosion severely injured her, resulting in the loss of her right arm and left leg, along with disfiguring scars on her face. Her health deteriorated, leaving her in constant pain, and she now lives a life devoted to religious practices at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
