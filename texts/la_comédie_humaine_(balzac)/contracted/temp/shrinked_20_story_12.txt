One of the few drawing-rooms where, under the Restoration, the Archbishop of Besancon was sometimes seen was that of the Baronne de Watteville, to whom he was attached because of her religious sentiments.

A word about this lady, the most important of Besancon.

Monsieur de Watteville, descended from the notorious Watteville of violent adventures, was in this nineteenth-century form gentle and peaceable. After living in the Comte like a wood-louse in a wainscot crack, he married the heiress of the celebrated house of Rupt. Mademoiselle de Rupt brought twenty thousand francs a year to add to the Baron de Watteville’s ten thousand francs in real estate. The Swiss gentleman’s coat-of-arms was borne as an escutcheon of pretence on the old shield of the Rupts. The match, arranged in 1802, was solemnized in 1815 after the second Restoration. Within three years of their daughter’s birth all her grandparents had died and their estates wound up. The Baron’s house was sold, and they settled in the Rue de la Prefecture in the fine old mansion of the Rupts, with an immense garden stretching to the Rue du Perron. Devout from youth, Madame de Watteville became still more so after marriage, prominent in the saintly brotherhood that gives Besancon’s upper circles a solemn, prudish air.

Monsieur le Baron de Watteville was a dry, lean man lacking intelligence, worn out in a way no one could explain; some wits suggested he had been ground down by his red‑haired, stern wife—“as sharp as Madame de Watteville” became a proverb. Rosalie was the only offspring of the union.

The Baron spent his life at a lathe; he was a turner. As a subsidiary pursuit he took up collecting—shells and geological fragments from around Besancon—a hobby some doctors regard as an early degree of mental aberration. Others, especially women, explained more charitably that he perceived he would never be his wife’s master, so he took refuge in mechanical work and good living.

The house of the Rupts still bore traces of Louis XIV magnificence. Cut‑glass chandeliers, brocades, damasks, carpets, gilt furniture, old liveries and servants, blackened family plate and Dresden china combined with excellent cookery. The wines, selected by Monsieur de Watteville—who, to occupy himself and vary his employments, acted as his own butler—had local fame. Madame de Watteville’s fortune was considerable; her husband’s Rouxey estate yielded about ten thousand francs a year. Because of Madame de Watteville’s intimacy with the Archbishop, several clever abbés not averse to good feeding were often at home there.

At a ceremonial dinner early in September 1834—women standing in a circle around the fire and men in groups by the windows—everyone brightened at the announcement of Monsieur l’Abbé de Grancey’s entrance.

“Well, and the lawsuit?” they all cried.

“Won!” replied the Vicar‑General. “The verdict of the Court, from which we had no hope—”

He referred to the First Court of Appeal of 1830, from which the Legitimists had largely withdrawn. “The verdict is in our favor on every point, and reverses the decision of the Lower Court.”

“Everybody thought you were done for.”

“And we should have been, but for me. I told our advocate to be off to Paris, and at the crucial moment I secured a new pleader, to whom we owe our victory, a wonderful man—”

“At Besancon?” said Monsieur de Watteville, guilelessly.

“At Besancon,” replied the Abbé de Grancey.

“Oh yes, Savaron,” added a handsome young man near the Baroness, named de Soulas.

“He spent five or six nights over it; he devoured documents and briefs; he had seven or eight interviews of several hours with me,” continued the Abbé, who had just reappeared at the Hotel de Rupt for the first time in three weeks. “In short, Monsieur Savaron has completely beaten the celebrated lawyer our adversaries had sent from Paris. This young man is wonderful, the bigwigs say. Thus the chapter is twice victorious: it has triumphed in law and also in politics, since it has vanquished Liberalism in the person of the Counsel of our Municipality. ‘Our adversaries,’ our advocate said, ‘must not expect readiness on all sides to ruin the Archbishoprics.’ The President was obliged to enforce silence. All the townsfolk of Besancon applauded. Thus possession of the buildings of the old convent remains with the Chapter of the Cathedral of Besancon. Monsieur Savaron, however, invited his Parisian opponent to dine with him after the court. He accepted, saying, ‘Honor to every conqueror,’ and complimented him on his success without bitterness.”

“And where did you unearth this lawyer?” said Madame de Watteville. “I never heard his name before.”

“Why, you can see his windows from here,” replied the Vicar‑General. “Monsieur Savaron lives in the Rue du Perron; the garden of his house adjoins yours.”

“But he is not a native of the Comte,” said Monsieur de Watteville.

“So little is he a native of any place, that no one knows where he comes from,” said Madame de Chavoncourt.

“But who is he?” asked Madame de Watteville, taking the Abbé’s arm to go into the dining‑room. “If he is a stranger, by what chance has he settled at Besancon? It is a strange fancy for a barrister.”

“Very strange!” echoed Amedee de Soulas, whose biography is necessary to the understanding of this tale.

In all ages France and England have traded trifles, their exchange all the more constant because it escapes the Custom-house. What is called English in Paris is called French in London, and vice versa. The nations’ antagonism is suspended over two trivialities: the use of words and dress. “God Save the King,” the English national air, was written by Lulli for the chorus of Esther or Athalie. Hoops, introduced at Paris by an Englishwoman, are said to have been invented in London by the notorious Duchess of Portsmouth; at first jeered at, they were adopted and reigned over European ladies for half a century. After 1815 Paris mocked the English long waists, yet by 1817 the Frenchwoman’s high belt had fallen to the hips.

In the past decade England gave two small words to French speech. The Incroyable, the Merveilleux, the Elegant—petit-maître successes of discreditable etymology—have given way to “dandy” and “lion.” The lion is not derived from lionne; that comes from Alfred de Musset’s famous song:

  Avez vous vu dans Barcelone
  .... C’est ma maitresse et ma lionne.

The two words and their ideas have fused in Parisian imagination. When an absurdity amuses Paris, which devours masterpieces and fads with equal appetite, the provinces hardly escape it. As soon as the lion strutted through Paris with his mane, beard and moustaches, waistcoats, eyeglass, and the contraction of cheek and eye-socket that kept it in place, provincial towns produced sub-lions who, by the smartness of their trouser-straps, protested against the untidiness of their compatriots.

Thus in 1834 Besançon boasted a lion in Monsieur Amédée-Sylvain de Soulas—spelt Souleyas during the Spanish occupation—perhaps the only man of Spanish descent in the town. The Soulas family had stayed after Spanish administrators left, owing to ties with Cardinal Granvelle. Young Amédée was always talking of leaving Besançon—a dull, church-going, not literary garrison town whose manners and physiognomy deserve remark. He lodged, like a man uncertain of the future, in three scant rooms at the Rue Neuve’s end, near the Rue de la Préfecture.

Amédée could not live without a tiger: Babylas, the farmer’s son, a thick-set boy of fourteen. The lion dressed his tiger splendidly—an iron-gray tunic coat belted with patent leather, bright blue plush breeches, red waistcoat, polished top-boots, a shiny hat with black lacings, and brass buttons with the Soulas arms. Amédée provided him white cotton gloves and washing, and paid thirty-six francs a month—four hundred and twenty francs a year—an enormous sum for Besançon grisettes. Extras included proceeds from selling outgrown clothes, gifts when Soulas changed horses, and the perquisite of the manure.

Amédée’s two horses, treated parsimoniously, cost about eight hundred francs a year together. His bills for items from Paris—perfumery, cravats, jewelry, patent blacking, and clothes—amounted to another twelve hundred francs. Add the groom, the horses, a superior style of dress, and six hundred francs a year for rent, and his yearly outlay reached three thousand francs.

His father had left him only four thousand francs a year, the income from cottage farms with precarious rents. That left the lion hardly three francs a day for food, amusements, and gambling. He often dined out and breakfasted frugally; when forced to dine at home he sent Babylas to fetch two dishes from a cookshop, never spending more than twenty-five sous.

People supposed Amédée a reckless spendthrift, but he managed his household with the skill of a thrifty housewife. Besançon folk underestimated how costly small vanities could be—six francs for polish, fifty-sou yellow gloves secretly cleaned repeatedly to look new, ten-franc cravats lasting three months, four waistcoats at twenty-five francs, trousers cut close to boots. How could he do otherwise, when women in Paris worshipped trifles that distinguish the successful fop—fifteen louis could buy at once a man’s charm, a coiffure, and a fine linen shirt?

If such economy seems to have made Amédée a lion cheaply, consider his travels: three trips to Switzerland by coach in short stages, twice to Paris, once from Paris to England, travels to Lombardy and the Italian lakes. He could say, “In England, where I went...” and be treated as well-informed. He read new books. When cleaning his gloves, the tiger Babylas answered callers, “Monsieur is very busy.” Someone had called him a “man of advanced ideas,” partly because he could utter the fashionable commonplaces with native gravity, which passed for enlightenment among the nobility.

In 1834 Amédée was twenty-five, of medium height, dark, broad-chested with well-made shoulders, rather plump legs, fat feet, white dimpled hands, a chin beard, garrison-worthy moustaches, and a good-natured, rubicund face with a flat nose and brown, expressionless eyes—nothing Spanish about him. He was rapidly approaching obesity, which would ruin his pretensions. His nails were neat, his beard trimmed, and he attended to the smallest details of dress with English precision. Consequently he was regarded as the finest man in Besançon; even a hairdresser kept at a fixed hour—a luxury of sixty francs a year—presented him as the local arbiter of fashion.

His routine: sleep late, dress and go out by noon to one of his farms or to practise pistol-shooting with the importance of an aristocratic pastime. At three he returned, admired on horseback by grisettes and ladies at their windows. After a pretence of business or study until four, he dressed to dine out, spent evenings playing whist in the aristocratic drawing rooms, and went to bed at eleven. No life could seem more respectable: he punctually attended church services on Sundays and holy days.

To understand how exceptional this life was, one must note Besançon’s resistance to progress. Anyone sent from Paris to fill an official or military post is called “the Colony,” neutral ground where the upper rank and townsfolk meet. A word, look, or gesture can start feuds between houses and women of rank and citizens’ wives that last a lifetime, widening an impassable gulf between classes. Except for a few old families—the Clermont-Mont-Saint-Jean, Beauffremont, de Scey, and Gramont—Besançon’s aristocracy dates only to the time of Louis XIV’s conquest, a parlementary world proud, stiff, solemn, and uncompromising even compared to Vienna; it would shame Viennese drawing rooms. Local glories like Victor Hugo, Nodier, and Fourier are never mentioned. Marriages among the elite are arranged in the cradle; no stranger finds his way into their houses, and introducing outsiders to colonels or titled officers quartered there requires diplomatic efforts Talleyrand might have envied.

In 1834 Amédée was the only man in Besançon wearing trouser-straps; this alone made him a lion. A local anecdote illustrates the city: the prefecture needed an editor from Paris for the official newspaper to compete with the little Gazette and the Republican Patriot. Paris sent a young man ignorant of Franche-Comté who wrote a Charivari-style leading article. The moderate municipal chief warned him: “You must understand, monsieur, that we are serious, more than serious—tiresome; we resent being amused, and are furious at having been made to laugh. Be as hard of digestion as the toughest disquisitions in the Revue des Deux Mondes, and you will hardly reach the level of Besançon.” The editor took the hint and thereafter produced obscure philosophical jargon; his success was complete. If Amédée’s reputation did not fall, it was through the aristocracy’s vanity—pleased to affect a modern air and to show visiting Parisians a young man who resembled them.

All Amédée’s apparent folly and hidden thrift had an object: to win a good marriage by proving his farms were not mortgaged and that he had savings. He wanted the town’s talk, to be its best-dressed man, and thereby gain the hand of Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville.

In 1830, when Amédée began his dandyism, Rosalie was fourteen; by 1834 she was eighteen and vulnerable to the peculiar attractions that made him conspicuous. Many lions were such from self-interest. The Wattevilles had for twelve years drawn fifty thousand francs a year yet spent only twenty-four thousand, hosting the upper circle of Besançon every Monday and Friday—dinner and evening parties—so twenty-six thousand francs a year were saved and invested prudently. People thought Madame de Watteville had placed her savings in three per cents in 1830, and that Rosalie’s dowry would amount to about twenty thousand francs a year. For five years Amédée had worked like a mole to ingratiate himself with the severe Baroness, flattering Mademoiselle’s conceit.

Madame de Watteville knew Amédée’s devices and esteemed him for them. He had placed himself under her wing when she was thirty and dared to admire and idolize her; he had been allowed—unique among men—to pour into her ear the unseemly gossip that very precise women love to hear, authorized by their virtue to look into the abyss without falling. Hence the lion refrained from any proper intrigue: he lived publicly, as though on the street, to play the part of a lover sacrificed to duty in the Baroness’s eyes, feeding her mind with sins she forbade her senses. A man privileged to whisper light stories to a bigot is charming to her. Had he better known human nature he might have risked flirtations with Besançon grisettes who idolized him; this might even have helped with the strict Baroness. To Rosalie he professed prodigality; he pretended a life of elegance and showed her the brilliant role of a woman of fashion in Paris, where he planned to become a député.

His manoeuvres succeeded. In 1834 the mothers of Besançon’s forty noble families named Amédée de Soulas the town’s most charming young man; he was undisputed cock of the walk at the Hôtel de Rupt, and all Besançon considered him Rosalie de Watteville’s future husband. There had even been private exchanges between the Baroness and Amédée, and the Baron’s apparent indifference lent some certainty.

Rosalie had been raised exclusively within the Hôtel de Rupt’s precincts; her mother rarely left it, devoted to her dear archbishop. Strictly repressed by religious education and maternal despotism, Rosalie knew nothing of the world. She had learned geography from Guthrie, sacred and ancient history, French history, and arithmetical rules through an old Jesuit sieve. Dancing and music were forbidden as likely to corrupt. The Baroness taught her every stitch of women’s work—sewing, embroidery, netting. At seventeen Rosalie had read only Lettres édifiantes and some heraldry; no newspaper had defiled her sight. She attended Mass daily, returned to needlework after a little garden walk, and received visitors while sitting with her mother until dinner. Except on Mondays and Fridays, she accompanied Madame de Watteville to other houses and spoke only by maternal permission.

At eighteen Rosalie was slight and thin, fair, colorless, and extremely unremarkable. Her very light blue eyes borrowed beauty from lashes that cast a shadow when lowered. A few freckles marred her shapely forehead. Her face resembled Dürer’s saints or pre-Perugino painters’ figures: the same slender modeling, delicacy saddened by ecstasy, and severe guilelessness. Everything in her, even her posture, suggested virgins whose beauty reveals itself mystically to the studious connoisseur. She had fine, though red, hands and a pretty aristocratic foot.

Her habitual dress was simple checked cotton, with silk allowed on Sundays and evenings. Besançon’s local dressmakers made gowns that almost made her ugly while her mother tried to borrow Parisian grace via Monsieur de Soulas, through whom she procured small Parisian trimmings. Rosalie had never worn silk stockings or thin boots—always cotton stockings and leather shoes; on high days she wore muslin with plainly dressed hair and bronze kid shoes.

This strict education and modest demeanor concealed in Rosalie a will of steel. Physiologists might say temperaments, wit, or genius reappear in families like hereditary diseases; talent, like the gout, sometimes skips generations. The decisive character and romantic daring of the famous Watteville reappeared in his grand-niece, reinforced by the Rupts’ tenacity and pride. These traits were buried in Rosalie’s apparently yielding soul like seething lava under a hill. Madame de Watteville alone perhaps suspected this inheritance; she was so severe she answered the Archbishop, who thought her too hard on the child, “Leave me to manage her, monseigneur. I know her! She has more than one Beelzebub in her skin!”

The Baroness kept keen watch because she considered her maternal honor at stake. Clotilde de Rupt, then thirty-five and nearly widowed—her husband occupied with making egg-cups, wheels, and snuff-boxes—flirted in strict propriety with Amédée. When he was present she alternately dismissed and recalled her daughter, probing for signs of jealousy to repress them. She policed Rosalie like the authorities police republicans, but in vain: Rosalie showed no rebellion, and the Baroness accused her daughter of insensibility. Rosalie, understanding that liking Monsieur de Soulas would draw a smart reproof, answered so cautiously—what some call Jesuitical—that her mother regarded her as a dissembler. If any spark of the Watteville and Rupt nature flared up, the mother wielded filial respect like a weapon to reduce Rosalie to passive obedience.

This covert struggle went on behind closed doors. The Vicar-General, Abbé Grancey—the late Archbishop’s friend and the diocese’s chief confessor—could not determine whether hatred existed between mother and daughter, whether the mother was jealous in anticipation, or whether Amédée’s courtship had overstepped bounds. Being a friend of the family, neither confessed to him. Rosalie, morally harried by young de Soulas, could not abide the Abbé; when he tried to win her heart by surprise she received him coldly. This aversion, noted only by her mother, was a constant subject of admonition.

“Rosalie, I cannot imagine why you affect such coldness towards Amédée. Is it because he is a friend of the family, and because we like him—your father and I?”

“Well, mamma,” replied the poor child, “if I made him welcome, should I not be still more in the wrong?”

“What do you mean by that?” cried Madame de Watteville. “What is the meaning of such words? Your mother is unjust, no doubt, and according to you, would be so in any case! Never let such an answer pass your lips again to your mother—” and so the rebuke ran on.

This quarrel lasted three hours and three-quarters; Rosalie noted the duration. Her mother, pale with fury, sent her to her room, where Rosalie pondered over the scene without understanding it, so guileless was she. Thus the young Monsieur de Soulas, whose neckcloths were set and whose moustaches waxed in pursuit of his goal—which required so much curling and smart waistcoats, so many horseshoes and stays—was in truth further from it than any casual suitor, despite the intervention of the worthy and noble Abbé de Grancey.

“Madame,” said Monsieur de Soulas to the Baroness as his soup cooled, “one morning the mail coach brought a stranger from Paris to the Hotel National. He sought apartments and took a first-floor suite in Mademoiselle Galard’s house in Rue du Perron. He registered at the Mairie with every political qualification, had his name entered on the list of barristers at the Court under the name ALBERT SAVARON, and left his card with Judges, Ministerial officials, Councillors, and members of the bench.”

“The name Savaron is renowned,” observed Mademoiselle de Watteville, schooled in heraldry. “The Savarons of Savarus are among Brabant’s oldest, noblest, and richest families.”

“He is a Frenchman, nobody’s son,” replied Amedee de Soulas. “If he wishes to bear the Savarus arms, he must add a bar-sinister. The Brabant family is extinct except for an unmarried heiress, Mademoiselle de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister marks a bastard; yet even a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is noble,” said Rosalie.

“Enough, mademoiselle,” the Baroness interposed. “You were set to learn heraldry, and she knows it thoroughly,” Monsieur de Watteville added.

“Continue, Monsieur de Soulas,” the Baroness urged.

“In a town as orderly as Besancon, lawyers received Albert Savaron without ceremony. They noted he was a stranger; who sent him here? What could he hope for? He committed a faux pas by sending cards to the Judges instead of calling. Within three days he had vanished from their minds. He took Jerome, old Galard’s servant, as his man; Galard being dead, Jerome can cook. Savaron was more forgotten because he was seen nowhere.”

“Does he not go to Mass?” asked Madame de Chavoncourt.

“He goes on Sundays to Saint-Pierre, but to the eight o’clock early service. He rises every night between one and two, works until eight, breakfasts, works again; walks fifty or sixty times in his garden, returns to dine, and sleeps between six and seven.”

“How do you know all that?” asked Madame de Chavoncourt.

“I live in Rue Neuve at the corner of Rue du Perron and look toward his house; my tiger and Jerome keep me informed.”

“You gossip with Babylas?” she teased.

Monsieur de Soulas told how the President of the Court had compelled this pleader to defend at the Assizes a half-witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron not only proved the man innocent but exposed him as a tool of the real culprits, leading to the arrest and condemnation of two witnesses. His speech impressed the Court and jury. A merchant, struck by his skill, entrusted a difficult case to him the next day and Savaron won it. Monsieur de Garcenault advised employing Savaron; Monsieur Berryer couldn’t come. Soulas had faith as soon as he heard him.

“Is he so extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” replied the Vicar-General. “The first time I saw him he received me in his outer room—the old Galard drawing-room—painted to resemble oak, lined with law-books likewise painted oak. The only decoration; furniture was a carved writing table, six tapestry armchairs, gray curtains bordered with green, a green carpet; the ante-room stove heated this library. He came out in a black merino dressing-gown tied with a red cord, red slippers, a red flannel waistcoat, and a red smoking-cap.”

“The devil’s colours!” cried Mademoiselle de Watteville.

“Yes,” said the Abbé; “but a magnificent head. Black hair streaked with gray, thick shining curls, hair stiff as horsehair; a round white throat; a splendid forehead furrowed by a median line stamped by great schemes; olive complexion veined with red; a square nose; eyes of flame; hollow cheeks with two long lines of suffering; a sardonic mouth with a small, too-short chin; crow’s feet at the temples; deep-set eyes that moved like burning balls; yet his manner was calm, resigned, and his voice of penetrating sweetness, a true orator’s voice, clear and appealing, sometimes insinuating, sometimes thunderous and trenchant in sarcasm. He is of middle height, neither stout nor thin; his hands resemble a prelate’s.”

The Abbé continued. The second visit found Savaron’s bedroom sparsely furnished: a miserable chest of drawers, shabby carpet, a camp-bed, cotton curtains. His private room admitted no visitors; Jerome merely knocked and waited. On a third visit Savaron breakfasted in the library, having spent the night over documents; the Abbé, with his long-winded attorney, had time to study him: “More than one secret hides behind that face—terrible and gentle, patient yet restless, broad and hollow; he stoops slightly, as though bearing a heavy load.”

“Why did such an eloquent man leave Paris for Besancon? He must have known how little a stranger could expect to succeed here. Why did he effort so little that it needed a trick by the President to bring him forward?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

The Abbé, smiling at the interruption, said he believed the man, perhaps thirty-five, would one day make a great sensation. “Why discuss him further? You won your suit and paid him,” Mademoiselle de Watteville said, watching her daughter Rosalie, who had listened to the Abbé’s portrait as if enchanted.

Rosalie—young, romantic, and impressionable—was captivated. The portrait offered something she had not encountered: the marvelous and exceptional that fires youthful imagination. Here was an ideal: gloomy, unhappy, eloquent, laborious—nothing like the chubby count or the fashionable cavaliers she knew. Amedee had cost her quarrels; Albert Savaron promised enigmas. She repeated his name to herself: “Albert Savaron de Savarus.” To see him, to glimpse him: this became her new desire.

She analyzed the Abbé’s phrases, rehearsed the descriptions, and thought of the lighted window she had noticed from her bed, a glow through the trees between two gardens in the night. “Then that was his light! I might see him!” she resolved. She bolded asked the Abbé at table if the Chapter’s lawsuit was fully settled, and when he answered prudently, she feigned interest in legalities to mask her real motive.

That evening she listened for Albert Savaron’s name but heard only congratulation for the Abbé; then bed. She had promised herself to wake between two and three to watch his dressing-room windows, and when the hour came she found pleasure in watching the glimmer through the thinning trees. By the help of a sharp young sight she fancied she could distinguish the color of his furniture—red. A thick column of smoke rose from the chimney above the roof. “While all the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought.

This awakening of curiosity raises the old question of girls’ education: should they be enlightened or restrained? Enlightenment risks early passions; restraint risks explosions like Molière’s Agnès. In Rosalie the Abbé’s portrait of Savaron precipitated such a crisis.

The next morning Rosalie saw Savaron walking in the adjoining garden. “If he had lived elsewhere what would have become of me? Here I can at least see him. What is he thinking?” Having seen in him the only striking face in Besancon, she resolved to discover his reasons for secrecy, hear his voice, and obtain even a glance from those eyes. How to manage it?

She adopted a plot. Dressing with a dreamy air, she decided to go to confession. After Mass she contrived a brief interview with Abbé Giroud at Saint-Pierre and engineered her confession for Sunday at half-past seven—early, before the eight o’clock Mass—telling a dozen small lies so she could be in church when the lawyer arrived. A sudden warmth for her father made her visit his workroom; she asked about turning, suggested larger pieces and twisted pillars, and, for the fondest pretext of closeness to Savaron, recommended using a heap of stones in the garden to build a grotto on which a small Belvedere or temple might be placed to display the pillars. She kissed him and begged him not to tell her mother, who would scold. The Baron promised discretion. Thus Rosalie planted the seed of a charming observatory from which her eye would command the lawyer’s private room.

Sunday came. Rosalie dressed carefully, happier in her plots. She feared her mother’s disapproval but felt fire in her feet. She planned to be with her confessor at quarter-past eight, saying eight to her mother, so she would have about fifteen minutes near Albert. She reached the church early, checked if Abbé Giroud was in his confessional to pass the time, and made ready to see Albert as he came in.

To a young girl's gaze, every man becomes handsome; but Albert Savaron, striking in himself, made an exceptional impression—his mien, his walk, his dress all stamped with an indescribable Mystery. He entered, and the gloomy church seemed to Rosalie to light up. His slow, solemn demeanor suggested a man who bears great burdens; his deep gaze and gestures expressed devouring thought. The Vicar-General’s portrait fitted perfectly. Those tawny-brown, golden-sparked eyes showed ardor that flashed suddenly. Rosalie, reckless, stood in his path to exchange glances; the look she caught sent her blood into tumult.

She found a place to view him while the Abbé might leave her. Mariette whispered, “Here is Monsieur Giroud,” and the interview that seemed brief ended with Mass. When she left the confessional, Albert had already gone. “The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why should this eagle swoop down on Besancon? I must know everything!” Under that desire, Rosalie stitched her work with exquisite precision and hid her daydreams under an innocent look to fool her mother.

From that Sunday onward she championed the Belvedere. One day she told her mother that her father was turning columns for a Belvedere on the garden’s heap of stones. Madame de Watteville, with affected sweetness, reproved what she perceived as her daughter’s presumptuousness. “Do you presume to instruct your parents on propriety?” she asked. Rosalie backed down. The Baron, approving, chose a discreet spot at the garden’s end, unseen from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows yet overlooking Savaron’s rooms. A builder was sent; periwinkle, iris, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper would stray into the rockwork path. The Baroness demanded rustic woodwork inside, a mirror, an ottoman forming a box, and an inlaid bark table. Monsieur de Soulas suggested asphalt flooring; Rosalie proposed a rustic chandelier carved by her father. Rumor called it a charming whim and the townsfolk sneered that the Wattevilles could spend a thousand crowns on a caprice.

No one guessed that the young girl reputed foolish had schemed to gain a closer view of Savaron’s private study. Meanwhile the lawyers’ envy cooled praise for Savaron. He kept to his seclusion and stayed unseen, increasing his obscurity. Yet he still pleaded three times before the Commercial Tribunal in knotty cases that had to go to the superior Court and thus gained four leading merchants as clients who trusted his sense and placed claims in his hands.

On the day the Wattevilles inaugurated their Belvedere, Albert Savaron was founding another monument. Using connections among merchants, he proposed a fortnightly, the Eastern Review, to promote Besancon as the commercial hub between Mulhouse and Lyons and the Rhône, and to wrest literary and scientific importance from Strasbourg and Dijon. Ten merchants took forty shares each at five hundred francs; Savaron quietly induced his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, to front the finance while he kept editorship and a portion of the profits. He arranged the management so as not to offend provincial pride, placing the literary direction under Boucher’s eldest son, a twenty-two-year-old eager for fame and inexperienced in literary pitfalls, whom Albert guided artfully. Alfred Boucher became devoted, coming each morning to discuss articles; the trial number printed a “Meditation” by Alfred approved by Albert. Albert slipped ideas and subjects to Alfred, who believed these were his own accomplishments. The commercial world, enchanted by the Review, had paid only three-tenths of their shares; with two hundred more subscribers the periodical would pay a five percent dividend while the editor remained unpaid.

Albert avoided placing his name in the fore; he kept to the editorship and read the exchange papers at home. The third number bore a tale signed “A. S.,” attributed to the lawyer, and the Review was accused of Liberal leanings in Besancon’s higher circles. Yet the novel, the first the county had seen, came under discussion that midwinter in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing-room.

“Papa,” Rosalie told her father, “a Review is published in Besancon; you ought to take it and keep it in your room, for mamma would not let me read it, but you will lend it to me.” Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please his affectionate daughter who for months had shown marked filial attention, subscribed for a year and lent the four numbers to Rosalie. That night she devoured the tale—the first she had ever read. To a girl who had known little life, the effect was intense. Whether or not the composition matched Parisian brilliance, to Rosalie it was a masterpiece.

Moreover, she was convinced by instinct that the tale contained the sentiments or even the life of Albert Savaron. From the first pages the conviction took hold; after finishing the fragment she was certain. Here was a confession. In the drawing-room critics accused Albert of imitating modern writers who expose their private joys, sorrows, or mysterious events. But to Rosalie it was confirmation: the eloquent, suffering man she had seen had poured his soul into that tale.

Thus the Belvedere and the Review, two monuments begun the same day in Besancon, advanced along parallel lines. The Wattevilles’ rustic tower aimed at seeing a man; Albert Savaron’s review aimed at making the East of France significant in commerce and letters. Both were acts of private ambition: one a youthful romantic stratagem, the other a deliberate campaign to reshape provincial destiny. Rosalie’s plan had succeeded in coaxing her parents to build the observatory that would assure her furtive vision of him; Albert’s plan drew the town’s commercial leaders around a literary enterprise that, though criticized, lent him influence and provided a slender public for his writing.

The town watched both enterprises in the trivial, eager way provincial life always watches oddities. The Belvedere was admired and gossiped over. The Review was sneered at for liberalism, yet it acquired exchange recognition and began to blink in the county’s intellectual life. Rosalie kept reading and dreaming. The picture of Savaron—the lone, suffering man, vehement yet resigned—grew in her mind as she threaded the two projects together: the observatory that would bring a glance, and the Review that had brought a confession. Between the grotto’s rustic chandelier and the tale signed “A. S.” Rosalie cultivated a hybrid of curiosity and love that neither prudence at home nor provincial malice could easily extinguish.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In 1823 two young men, having agreed to tour Switzerland for a holiday, set out from Lucerne one fine July morning in a boat pulled by three oarsmen, bound for Fluelen and intending to stop at every notable spot on the lake of the Four Cantons. The scenery between Lucerne and Fluelen offered every wanted combination of mountains and waters: austere solitudes and charming headlands, trim meadows, forests crowning granite cliffs, deserted verdant reaches, and valleys whose beauty increased with the dreamy distance.

As they passed the little hamlet of Gersau, one friend lingered, watching a newly built wooden house on a promontory nearly bathed by the water. In an upper window a woman’s head was raised to admire the lake; one of the young men met her indifferent glance.

“Let us stop here,” he said. “We meant to make Lucerne our headquarters; Leopold, you will not mind if I stay and take charge of our possessions? You can go where you please; my journey ends.” The other agreed without objection.

They were true friends of the same age, schoolmates who had studied law and were taking the classic Swiss tour. Leopold was already pledged to a notary’s office in Paris by his father’s arrangement: orderly, gentle, rational, he accepted the future of a Parisian notary with philosophical resignation. His companion, Rodolphe, was different. The natural son of a man of rank who had died early, Rodolphe’s mother had sold what she inherited to buy a life annuity so she could educate him and leave him a small capital at majority. Rodolphe was highly sensitive and ardent; desire guided his imagination and actions. His mother, fearing his impetuous temper, had encouraged his friendship with Leopold, seeing in the cool, faithful Leopold a kind of guardian. She herself, handsome at forty-three, had inspired in Leopold an ardent passion, making the two young men even closer.

So it surprised everyone when Rodolphe stopped the planned journey because of a single glance at that upper window. While breakfast was prepared at the Swan Inn, the friends walked the hamlet and found the new house; Rodolphe discovered a residence of decent folk willing to take him as a boarder—a Swiss custom. For a hundred francs a month (the Bergmanns insisted on three months’ residence and a month’s rent in advance), he rented a bedroom overlooking the lake and mountains. He deposited his traveling effects and watched Leopold row away. When Leopold’s boat was out of sight, Rodolphe examined the house, hoping to see the unknown. The house seemed lifeless, but at dinner with Monsieur and Madame Stopfer, retired coopers from Neufchatel, he learned the neighbor’s story: the fair stranger was called Miss Lovelace (pronounced Loveless).

Miss Lovelace had come for her father’s health; physicians had recommended Lucerne’s air. They had arrived with only one servant, a fourteen-year-old deaf-mute girl much attached to Miss Fanny, and had settled two winters before with Monsieur and Madame Bergmann, retired head-gardeners of Count Borromeo. The Bergmanns let the top story of their house to the Lovelaces for three years at a rent of two hundred francs a year. Old Lovelace, about ninety and broken, was too poor to go out; his daughter translated and wrote to support him. They could not afford boats or guides. The Bergmanns, who had made beautiful gardens and a hothouse, were thought to profit from the bargain despite their modest appearance. Miss Fanny, said to be nineteen, adored flowers and music and had even hired a piano from Lucerne.

“She loves flowers and music, and she is unmarried!” thought Rodolphe—what luck.

Next day he asked to visit the hothouses and gardens. The Bergmanns suspiciously asked for his passport, which delayed him; it was returned the next morning via the cook, who expressed her master’s pleasure at showing him around. Rodolphe, dressed carefully, toured the grounds, kept at a distance by the gardeners’ distrust. His attention was soon drawn to the little deaf-mute, whom he recognized as of African or Sicilian origin: golden-brown complexion, long lashes, very dark hair, strong sinewy limbs. She watched him with unusual curiosity and effrontery.

“To whom does that little Moresco belong?” he asked.

“To the English,” Monsieur Bergmann replied.

“But she was not born in England!”

“They may have brought her from the Indies,” Madame Bergmann suggested.

Rodolphe said he loved music and would be delighted to join Miss Lovelace, but the gardeners said they received no visitors. Disappointed, he left. That evening he rowed on the lake and heard a piano and a lovely voice from the house. He had the boat wait; after the song, he landed, sent away the boatmen, and sat under a granite shelf by an acacia hedge. After listening, he crept to the end of the avenue and concealed himself to watch two women talking in Italian. To his surprise, the “mute” spoke Italian and was not mute. The two were the “English” pair, and Rodolphe guessed they were Italian refugees, living in fear, speaking in Italian after dark.

Crawling through the hedge to surprise them, he rose and spoke in French to the Italian. Immediately he felt a sharp pain in his side and fell. “Nel lago con pietra!” cried the deaf-mute. He found he’d been stabbed with a stiletto that had grazed him; had it been a little higher, it would have been fatal. He drew the stiletto from his wound and, in spite of the pain, begged forgiveness and asked Francesca (the name he had heard) to show him the way out. Francesca helped him up; Gina—who was not dumb—opened his shirt and sucked the wound; Francesca applied a plaster. Each took an arm and led him to a side gate; Gina stayed to see him to the Stopfers’ door and fled after ringing. Rodolphe thought the patriots did not play at killing, and later sent to Lucerne for the best surgeon, insisting on secrecy for the sake of his honor.

Leopold returned the day Rodolphe first got out of bed with fatal news: Rodolphe’s mother was dead. The letter had reached Lucerne while they were on their way to Fluelen. Rodolphe fell ill with a nervous fever; once convalescent, he sent Leopold to France with a power of attorney so he could stay at Gersau, the only place where his grief might quieten. His sorrow won him widespread pity and interest. Every morning the “mute” came to bring news of her mistress.

When Rodolphe could go out, he visited the Bergmanns to thank Miss Fanny Lovelace and her father for their concern. For the first time the old Italian admitted a stranger to his room. Francesca by candlelight struck him: her smiles threw roses on his grieving heart, and she sang grave, fitting melodies. Later she took him on the balcony to a rustic bench overlooking the sublime scenery. Rodolphe asked her age.

“Nineteen,” she said.

“If anything could soothe my sorrow it would be the hope of winning you,” he confessed, declaring a deep, eternal passion and offering, trembling, half his small inheritance to help her fortune. Francesca interrupted: “Zitto! I am not free. I have been married these three years.” He fainted from the shock.

When revived, he pleaded, but she warned him gently: her husband, Emilio, was sixty-five—disguised to seem older to mislead the police. Francesca spoke with frank dignity: married without her consent, she accepted marriage and would never wish to be free; she could love, but would not abandon her duty. She asked Rodolphe to understand that she could restrain passion when it conflicted with her principles. She desired to be loved and waited nobly, and all she could give was an “infinite tenderness” expressed within the heart’s bounds. She urged him to make a splendid fortune and to use his talents for mankind; fame might bridge the gulf between them. Her manner and words were plain, true, and stamped with sincerity.

In their long conversation Rodolphe discovered Francesca’s enthusiasm for liberal ideas—the worship of liberty that had led to revolutions in Naples, Piedmont, and Spain. On leaving, Gina showed him to the door. At eleven, when all was still, Rodolphe took Gina aside and offered a gold piece for information; she named Francesca’s master as the bookseller Lamporani of Milan, a leader of the revolution whom Austria would like to have in the Spielberg. Gina also said Francesca’s father’s name was Colonna. Emboldened, Rodolphe fitted an awning and cushions to his boat and asked Francesca for a row on the lake; she accepted but brought Gina and behaved with the dignity of high birth. Her actions betrayed superior education and rank; Rodolphe felt cut off by her proud manner. Gina gave a mocking side glance.

“Where would you go, Signora Lamporani?” he asked.

“Towards Lucerne,” she replied in French.

He sat by her, begging for her hand; she withdrew it. She explained more fully that she had the strength to stifle feeling if it conflicted with her idea of true love. Married to an old man who generously allowed her liberty, three years of marriage implied acceptance of its rules. Emilio knew her character and would not take her hand without her heart; therefore, no intoxication would make her wish to be free. Despite this, she promised to be coquettish, gay, and childlike within permitted confines.

Rodolphe accepted this “treaty” gladly, seeing in her reserved tenderness a sublime kind of love. Francesca’s dignity and modest poverty—she said their work provided their luxuries—moved him. He declared he would wait faithfully, burned with respect and ambition to be worthy of her. She smiled with a subtle, Gioconda-like expression and, with serious eyes, encouraged his ambition for mankind rather than for her. Their mutual understanding brought serene delight; this hour of reciprocal confidence, set against the spectacular scenery, felt like a unique rebirth for Rodolphe, who mourned his mother but found consolation in Francesca’s tenderness.

Their bliss was interrupted by a boat from Lucerne. Gina, watching, started with joy. A young man approached—Tito. Francesca called out and the boats drew alongside. Tito and Francesca exchanged rapid words in a dialect Rodolphe did not understand; Gina’s delight and Tito’s handsome face provoked Rodolphe’s displeasure. Tito tossed a leather bag to Gina and letters to Francesca, who read them with evident excitement.

“La liberta!” she cried—freedom! Gina echoed, “E denaro!”—and Francesca explained: no more poverty; she had been working for eleven months and was weary; within three weeks they would be at Geneva and living at ease. Tito was the Secretary of State in the Colonna shop—he had come by sea via Marseilles and crossed France. Francesca was radiant: within a year there might be an amnesty for their cause. Gina wept with relief. Rodolphe felt crushed: he had been intimate in Francesca’s sorrow and could not share in her political joy.

Back at the Bergmanns’, Rodolphe found the old man transformed by the good news. He now appeared about sixty, straight as an I, with black hair showing a white skull, bright eyes, and a sardonic smile—the librarian in his natural form. Francesca declared, simply, “Here is my husband under his natural form.” The old man, who had played many parts in his life, admitted he had disguised himself; he had acted roles even in Paris under the Empire and boasted an education that had enabled him to survive. He called himself Emilio, and said Francesca had been obliged to work to maintain him.

Rodolphe, suspicious, kept watchful silence. Francesca, meanwhile, laughed at his question about her husband’s youth. “He is but sixty-five,” she said, “which is something to be thankful for!” Yet she insisted Emilio was simple as a child and under her protection. She related, half banter, half pride, episodes of family honor and devotion: Emilio once risked life and fortune because of her Liberal opinions, despite not sharing them—was that not love? Her mixture of dignity and playfulness made her irresistibly charming. The dinner and evening were full of cheer for the refugees but left Rodolphe depressed.

“Can she be fickle?” he wondered. He blamed himself for expecting Parisian propriety from an impulsive Italian who showed no hypocrisy and was carried away by feeling. Rodolphe wanted Francesca to be like a Parisian woman, but she was Italian: proud, sincere, ardent, and independent in both heart and mind.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The next day and for twenty days after, Rodolphe spent all his time at the Bergmanns’, watching Francesca without having resolved to watch her. In some souls admiration implies a kind of penetration. The young Frenchman perceived Francesca’s girlish imprudence, the true nature of a woman not yet broken—sometimes resisting love, sometimes yielding to it. The old man treated her like a father, and she returned gratitude that awoke her native nobility. The situation and the woman remained an impenetrable enigma to Rodolphe, and solving it increasingly attracted him.

Those days were full of secret joys alternating with melancholy, with tiffs and quarrels more delightful than hours of agreement. He grew fascinated by a constant tenderness, an affection jealous of trifles.

“You care very much for luxury?” he asked one evening, when Francesca lamented missing things in Gersau.

“I!” she cried. “I love luxury as I love art—a Raphael, a fine horse... After all, is it not natural for plain folks to aspire to grandeur?” She teased Rodolphe and her husband, then showed her pretty feet and offered a hand. “Leave us,” she told her husband; “I want to speak to him.”

The old man, ingenuous and confident, retired, sure of his wife.

“I will not have you come with us to Geneva,” she told Rodolphe. “It is a gossiping town. Though I am above its nonsense, I will not be calumniated, not for myself but for him. I pride myself on being that old man’s glory, his only protector. We are leaving; stay here a few days. When you come to Geneva, call on my husband first; he will introduce you. Let us hide our unchangeable affection from the world. I love you; you know it; but I will prove it by never giving you cause for jealousy.”

She drew him into a balcony corner, kissed his forehead, and fled, leaving him astonished.

Next day Rodolphe learned the Bergmanns’ lodgers had left at daybreak. It seemed intolerable to remain in Gersau, and he set out for Vevay by the longest route, reaching Geneva by the end of October, drawn by the lake where the beautiful Italian awaited him. To avoid town inconveniences he took rooms at Eaux-Vives, outside the walls. His first inquiry was whether Italian refugees from Milan had come to Geneva.

“Not so far as I know,” the landlord, a retired jeweler, replied. “Prince and Princess Colonna of Rome have taken Monsieur Jeanrenaud’s house for three years; it stands between the Villa Diodati and Monsieur Lafin-de-Dieu’s, let to the Vicomtesse de Beauseant. Prince Colonna has come to see his daughter and son-in-law Prince Gandolphini, a Neapolitan or Sicilian, an old adherent of King Murat’s and victim of the last revolution. These are the newest arrivals. Geneva is careful not to offend the Holy Alliance; many foreigners—Russians and English—come here.”

“Even some Geneva folk?”

“Yes, monsieur, our lake is so fine! Lord Byron lived at Villa Diodati.”

“You cannot tell me whether a bookseller from Milan has arrived with his wife—named Lamporani, a leader of the last revolution?”

“I could find out at the Foreigners’ Club,” said the jeweler.

Rodolphe’s first walk was naturally to Villa Diodati, whose recent occupant’s death added to its attraction: death consecrates genius.

The road to Eaux-Vives follows the lake and is narrow; a few yards from Jeanrenaud’s house, as he approached, Rodolphe heard a carriage behind him. Finding himself in a sunk road, he climbed a rock to let it pass and saw an elegant English phaeton. He felt dizzy as he saw Francesca, beautifully dressed, beside an old lady like a cameo; a servant blazing with gold lace stood behind. Francesca recognized Rodolphe and smiled at the sight like a statue. The carriage turned into a gate of a country house; Rodolphe ran toward it.

“Who lives here?” he asked the gardener.

“Prince and Princess Colonna, and Prince and Princess Gandolphini.”

“Have they not just driven in?”

“Yes.”

In that instant a veil fell from Rodolphe’s eyes; he understood the meaning of the past.

“If only this is her last piece of trickery!” he thought, thunder-struck.

He trembled at the thought he might have been the plaything of a whim, for he had heard what capriccio could mean in an Italian. What crime had he committed in a woman’s eyes—accepting a born princess as a citizen’s wife?—in believing a daughter of an ancient house to be a bookseller’s wife! The consciousness of his error increased his desire to see whether he would be ignored. He asked for Prince Gandolphini, left his card, and was received by the false Lamporani, who welcomed him and led him to a terrace overlooking Geneva, the Jura, villas, and the lake.

“My wife is faithful to the lakes, you see,” he remarked. “We have a sort of concert this evening,” he added. “I hope you will honor the Princess and me. Two months of poverty endured in intimacy are equal to years of friendship.”

Though consumed by curiosity, Rodolphe dared not ask to see the Princess; he returned to Eaux-Vives, waiting for the evening. Anxiety and suspense heightened his passion. He now understood the necessity of making himself notable so that he might one day be socially equal to his idol. Francesca’s simplicity and ease in Gersau had made her great in his eyes. Her parents’ rank alarmed him; the secrecy Princess Gandolphini had enjoined now seemed a proof of affection. By refusing to compromise the future, had she not confessed that she loved him?

At nine o’clock he took a carriage to Villa Jeanrenaud—Prince Gandolphini’s.

He saw Francesca without being seen. The Princess stood near the piano; her thick hair was bound with a golden fillet. Her face, in candlelight, had the pale brilliance of Italians, which looks best by artificial light. She wore full evening dress, revealing alluring shoulders, the figure of a girl and arms like an antique statue. Her beauty surpassed rivalry, though charming women of Geneva and Italians like Princess Varese and the singer Tinti were present.

Rodolphe, leaning against the door-post, fixed on her a tenacious, attracting gaze charged with desire. Francesca, as if magnetized, glanced toward the door and met Rodolphe’s eyes. A slight thrill quivered through her superb face and body; she blushed. Rodolphe felt a whole life in that lightning exchange: he was loved. The lofty Princess, amid her world, kept the promise made in the disguised exile’s capricious lodging. The moment intoxicated him. A faint, refined smile curved Francesca’s lips; at a moment when she did not feel observed, she looked at Rodolphe with a look that seemed to ask his pardon for deceiving him about her rank.

After the song, Rodolphe was led to the Prince and presented to Princess and Prince Colonna and to Francesca. Later the Princess took part in the famous quartet Mi manca la voce, sung with Tinti, the tenor Genovese, and a well-known exiled Italian prince whose voice alone would have made him a prince of art.

“Take that seat,” said Francesca to Rodolphe, pointing to her chair. “Oime! I think there is some mistake in my name; I have for the last minute been Princess Rodolphini.”

She said it with the artless grace that revived the happy days at Gersau. Rodolphe reveled in listening to the woman he adored, sitting so close that the stuff of her dress nearly brushed his cheek and the gauze of her scarf. During Mi manca la voce, sung by some of Italy’s finest voices, tears came to his eyes.

In love, as in all else, trivial circumstances born of prior incidents can have immense importance: a small touch, a word, an unexpected proof of affection that epitomizes the past and links to the future. This recognition between Rodolphe and Francesca at the party, public yet intimate, was one of those intense moments that join future to past and rivet attachment. It was perhaps one of those incidental rivets Bossuet had in mind when he likened the rarity of happy moments to them.

Next to the pleasure of admiring the beloved comes that of seeing her admired by others. Rodolphe enjoyed both: her charm and the applause she drew. Love is a treasury of memories; his was already full, yet he added more—smiles meant only for him, stolen glances, tones in her singing that addressed him though they made Tinti pale with jealousy. All his desire and the special expression of his soul were thrown over the beautiful Roman, who became the beginning and end of his thoughts and actions. Rodolphe loved with a force and constancy that made Francesca the substance of his heart; he felt her blood mingle with his as purer blood, her soul as a more perfect soul; she underlay his every effort like golden sand beneath the Mediterranean waves. His lightest aspiration became a living hope.

After a few days Francesca understood this boundless love; it was natural and perfectly shared by her, not surprising. She was worthy of it.

“What is there that is strange?” she asked as they walked on the garden terrace, when he had been betrayed into French conceit in expressing his feelings. “What is extraordinary in your loving a young and beautiful woman, artist enough to earn her living like Tinti, and giving you some pleasures of vanity? Any lout might become an Amadis then. The point is that we both love faithfully, persistently; perhaps separated for years, with the sole satisfaction of knowing we are loved.”

“Alas!” said Rodolphe, “will you think my fidelity has no merit when you see me absorbed in devouring ambition? Do you imagine I want to see you exchange the fine name of Gandolphini for that of a nobody? I want to become one of the most remarkable men in my country, rich and great—so that you may be as proud of my name as of your Colonna.”

“I should be grieved to see you without such sentiments,” she replied with a bewitching smile. “But do not wear yourself out too soon in ambition. Remain young. Politics soon make a man old.”

One rare gift in women is a gaiety that does not lessen tenderness. This combination of deep feeling and youthful lightness added an enchanting grace to Francesca. This quality is the key to her character: she laughs and is touched; she grows enthusiastic and returns to arch raillery with a readiness that makes her charming and exquisite, a reputation known beyond Italy. Under her graces she concealed vast learning, a result of the monotonous, almost monastic life in the Colonnas’ castle.

This rich heiress had at first been intended for the cloister as the fourth child of Prince and Princess Colonna; but the deaths of her two brothers and elder sister brought her out of retirement and made her one of the Papal States’ most brilliant matches. Her elder sister had been betrothed to Prince Gandolphini; Francesca married him instead so the family’s position would not change—the Colonnas and Gandolphinis always intermarried.

From nine to sixteen, under a Cardinal’s direction, she read through the Colonnas’ library to temper her ardent imagination by studying science, art, and letters. From these studies she acquired a taste for independence and liberal ideas, which led her and her husband into the revolution’s ranks. Rodolphe had not yet learned that, besides five modern languages, Francesca knew Greek, Latin, and Hebrew. The charming creature understood that for a woman the first condition of being learned is to keep it deeply hidden.

Rodolphe spent the whole winter at Geneva; it passed like a day. When spring returned, despite the delights of society with a clever, young, lovely woman, the lover endured cruel sufferings, borne with courage but sometimes visible in his manner, perhaps because he believed Francesca shared them. It annoyed him that she kept calm. Like an Englishwoman, she seemed to pride herself on expressing nothing; her serenity defied love. He longed to see her agitated and accused her of having no feeling, dwelling on the tradition that Italian women are feverishly excitable.

“I am a Roman!” Francesca replied gravely when she took his banter seriously.

There was depth in her tone, a hint of irony that quickened his pulse. May spread its fresh verdure; the sun was sometimes as powerful as midsummer. The two lovers stood where the rock falls abruptly to the lake, leaning over a stone parapet above steps to a landing-stage. From a neighboring villa a boat shot out like a swan, its flag flaming, crimson awning over a lovely woman reclining on red cushions, her hair wreathed with real flowers; a young man dressed like a sailor rowed with graceful attention under her eye.

“They are happy!” exclaimed Rodolphe bitterly. “Claire de Bourgogne, the last of the only house that can rival the French royal family—”

“Oh! of a bastard branch, and that a female line.”

“At any rate, she is Vicomtesse de Beauseant; and she did not—”

“Did not hesitate, you would say, to bury herself here with Monsieur Gaston de Nueil, you would say,” Francesca cut in. “She is only a Frenchwoman; I am an Italian, my dear sir!”

She turned from the parapet, leaving Rodolphe chilled; he followed, suspecting he had wounded her soul, simple and wise, proud and humble. She signed him away, yet he did not heed and saw her wiping tears. Tears! in so strong a nature.

“Francesca,” he said, taking her hand, “is there a single regret in your heart?”

She was silent, freed her hand that held an embroidered handkerchief, and dried her eyes.

“Forgive me!” he cried, and, with a rush, kissed her eyes to wipe away the tears.

Francesca did not seem aware of his impulse; she was violently agitated. Rodolphe, thinking she consented, grew bolder; he put his arm round her, clasped her to his heart, and snatched a kiss. She freed herself with a dignified movement of offended modesty, and, standing a yard off, looked at him without anger but with firm resolve.

“Go this evening,” she said. “We meet no more till we meet at Naples.”

Her order was stern and it was obeyed, for it was Francesca’s will.

On his return to Paris Rodolphe found in his rooms a portrait of Princess Gandolphini painted by Schinner, who had passed through Geneva on his way to Italy. As the artist had refused to paint several women, Rodolphe did not believe the Prince—though eager for a likeness of his wife—could overcome Schinner’s objections; but Francesca, no doubt, had bewitched him and obtained—almost a miracle—an original for Rodolphe and a duplicate for Emilio. She told him this in a charming letter in which the mind compensated for the reserve required by propriety. The lover replied. Thus began, never to cease, a regular correspondence between Rodolphe and Francesca, the sole indulgence they allowed themselves.

Possessed by an ambition sanctified by his love, Rodolphe set to work. First he longed to make his fortune and risked everything in an enterprise to which he devoted all his faculties and capital; but, an inexperienced youth, he had to contend with duplicity, which prevailed. Thus three years were lost in a vast undertaking—three years of struggling and courage.

The Villele ministry fell just when Rodolphe was ruined. The valiant lover thought to seek in politics what commerce had denied him; but before braving that career he went, wounded and sick at heart, to Naples to recover, where the Prince and Princess had been reinstated on the King’s accession. In the midst of his warfare this was a delightful respite; he spent three months at the Villa Gandolphini, rocked in hope.

Rodolphe then began anew to build his fortune. His talents were already known; he was about to attain his ambitions; a high position seemed the reward of his zeal, devotion, and past services, when the storm of July 1830 broke and again his bark was swamped.

She and God! These are the only witnesses of the brave efforts and daring attempts of a young man gifted with fine qualities but to whom, so far, the protection of luck—the god of fools—has been denied. This indefatigable wrestler, upheld by love, returns to fresh struggles, lighted on his way by an always friendly eye, an ever faithful heart.

Lovers! Pray for him!

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
As she finished this narrative, Mademoiselle de Watteville’s cheeks were on fire; a fever was in her blood. She wept—but with rage. This little novel, in the fashionable literary style, was the first such reading Rosalie had ever devoured. Love was depicted in it, not by a master-hand, perhaps, but by a man who seemed to give his own impressions; truth, even if unskilled, touched a virgin soul. Hence Rosalie’s agitation, fever, and tears: she was jealous of Francesca Colonna.

She never doubted the sincerity of this poetical flight; Albert had taken pleasure in telling the story of his passion while changing names. Rosalie was possessed by curiosity. What woman would not want to know her rival’s name—when she too loved? While reading, contagiously moved, she had solemnly told herself, “I love him!” She loved Albert and felt a gnawing desire to fight for him, to snatch him from this unknown rival. She reflected that she knew nothing of music and was not beautiful.

“He will never love me!” she thought.

That thought intensified her anxiety to know whether she might be mistaken—whether Albert really loved an Italian princess and was loved by her. That night the decisive faculty, which had characterized the famous Watteville, fully developed in Rosalie. She devised whimsical schemes that occur to young girls confined by strict mothers: descending a ladder from the kiosk into Albert’s garden; taking advantage of the lawyer being asleep to look through his window; writing to him; or bursting the fetters of Besancon society by bringing Albert into the drawing-room of the Hôtel de Rupt. Even the Abbé de Grancey would have deemed such an enterprise impossible, yet it was a passing thought.

“Ah!” she said. “My father has a dispute about his land at les Rouxey. I will go there! If there is no lawsuit, I will make one, and he shall come into our drawing-room!” she cried, springing to the window to look at the gleam in Albert’s rooms. The clock struck one; he was still asleep.

“I shall see him when he gets up; perhaps he will come to his window.”

At that instant Rosalie saw an incident that promised means of discovering Albert’s secrets. By moonlight she saw a pair of arms stretched from the kiosk to help Jérôme, Albert’s servant, over the coping into the little building. In Jérôme’s accomplice she at once recognized Mariette, the lady’s-maid.

“Mariette and Jérôme!” she thought. “Mariette, such an ugly girl! Certainly they must be ashamed.”

Though Mariette was ugly and thirty-six, she had inherited plots of land. She had been seventeen years with Madame de Watteville, who valued her for bigotry, honesty, and service. Earning about ten louis a year, she had likely saved and invested enough to have not less than ten thousand francs.

In Jérôme’s eyes ten thousand francs altered the optics; he saw in Mariette a neat figure, invisible pox scars and all. Ever since Savaron had brought him near the Wattevilles, Jérôme had courted the maid, who, prim and sanctimonious like her mistress, was far more exacting than the handsomest.

To Rosalie, though she could not fully account for the night-scene, it gave the most dangerous lesson: a servant girl could undo a mother’s strict rearing in one hour. Rosalie returned to bed, considering how she might exploit her discovery.

Next morning, as she went to Mass accompanied by Mariette—her mother being unwell—Rosalie took the maid’s arm, surprising the country wench.

“Mariette,” she said, “is Jérôme in his master’s confidence?”

“I do not know, mademoiselle.”

“Do not play the innocent,” said Rosalie dryly. “You let him kiss you last night under the kiosk; I no longer wonder you approved my mother’s improvement plans.”

Mariette trembled.

“I wish you no ill,” Rosalie went on. “Be easy; I shall not tell my mother, and you can meet Jérôme as often as you please.”

“But, mademoiselle,” said Mariette, “it is respectable; Jérôme means to marry me—”

“But then,” said Rosalie, “why meet at night?”

Mariette was dumfounded and made no reply.

“Listen, Mariette; I am in love too! In secret and without return. I am my parents’ only child. You have more to hope for from me than anyone else—”

“Certainly, mademoiselle, you may count on us for life or death,” Mariette exclaimed, rejoiced.

“In the first place, silence for silence,” said Rosalie. “I will not marry Monsieur de Soulas; but I must have your help on one condition.”

“What is that?”

“I must see the letters Monsieur Savaron sends by Jérôme.”

“But what for?” Mariette asked.

“Oh! merely to read them; you shall post them afterwards. It will cause a little delay.”

They entered the church, each lost in thought.

“Dear, dear, how many sins are there in all that?” thought Mariette.

Rosalie, whose soul, brain, and heart were completely upset by the story, already regarded it as history written for her rival. Thinking of nothing else, she came to believe that the Eastern Review was forwarded to Albert’s lady-love.

“Oh!” she said, head buried in her hands; “how can I get my father to look through the list of people to whom the Review is sent?”

After breakfast she coaxed her father into the kiosk and urged him to see if the Review was read abroad.

“It is but just started—”

“Well, I will wager it is.”

“It is hardly possible.”

“Just go and find out, and note any subscribers out of France.”

Two hours later Monsieur de Watteville said to his daughter:

“I was right; there is not one foreign subscriber yet. They hope for some at Neufchâtel, Berne, and Geneva. One copy, in fact, is sent to Italy, but it is not paid for—to a Milanese lady at her country house at Belgirate, on Lago Maggiore.”

“What is her name?”

“The Duchesse d’Argaiolo.”

“Do you know her, papa?”

“I have heard of her. She was born Princess Soderini, a Florentine, a very great lady, as rich as her husband, who has one of the largest fortunes in Lombardy. Their villa on the Lago Maggiore is one of Italy’s sights.”

Two days after, Mariette placed the following letter in Rosalie’s hand:

Albert Savaron to Léopold Hannequin.

“Yes, ’tis so, my dear friend; I am at Besançon, while you thought I was traveling. I would not tell you till success began, and now it is dawning. Yes, after so many abortive undertakings, wasted efforts, and a wasted three years, I have made up my mind to follow the beaten path—the longest but safest. I see you start in your lawyer’s chair!

But do not suppose anything is changed in my personal life, of which you alone know the secret, under the reservations she insists on. I was horribly weary of Paris. The outcome of my first enterprise, on which I had founded all my hopes, ended badly because of rascality among partners. I gave up the pursuit of a fortune after losing three years. One of these years was in the law courts; perhaps I should have fared worse if I had not studied law when I was twenty.

I determined to go into politics, so I might some day be named to the Senate under the title of Comte Albert Savaron de Savarus, and revive a good name extinct in Belgium—though I am neither legitimate nor legitimized.

You know how conscientiously I studied as an obscure journalist and faithful secretary to a statesman who was true to me in 1829. Thrown down by the revolution of July just when my name rose, I erred in remaining faithful to the fallen and fighting for them without them. Oh! why was I but thirty-three, and why did I not apply to you to make me eligible? I concealed my devotedness and dangers. We should not have agreed.

Ten months ago, though you saw me gay and contented, I was in despair; I foresaw my fate at thirty-seven with two thousand francs and no fame, having failed in founding a daily paper—an enterprise that could have been great. I wandered gloomy through Paris, thinking of my crushed ambitions, never giving them up. What frantic letters I wrote to her, my other self! Sometimes I asked, ‘Why demand everything? Why not wait for happiness while devoting myself to a mechanical employment?’

I looked for a modest appointment. I nearly became editor of a paper under a manager, but I took fright. Would she accept a man who had stooped so low? That thought made me young again. But how the soul is worn by these perplexities! What must the caged eagles suffer!

Seven months ago I resolved to make my name at the Paris Bar, seeing vacancies left by promotions. But remembering the rivalry in the press and difficulty in Paris, I decided to go to Besançon, where I might meet no opposition and canvass alone. The people of the Comte will not admit an outsider to drawing-rooms; the outsider will win the commercial class, which elects deputies. I will study commercial questions, win their suits, be the greatest pleader in Besançon, and start a Review to defend the country’s interests. By gaining votes I will reach the tribune. So I packed my books, sent furniture to Besançon, secured rooms, and set up a private room where the portrait of my divinity reigns. I engaged a servant and sat, like a hibernating marmot, for five months.

My name was entered on the lawyers’ list. I was called to defend an unhappy wretch at the Assizes; one influential merchant on the jury listened, and I saved my client, arrested the real criminals, and won public admiration. I fought a case for my merchant and won; the Cathedral Chapter chose me to defend a long action against the town—I won that too. After these trials I became the most famous advocate of Franche-Comté.

I bury my life in mystery and hide my aims. I accept no invitations, consult only between six and eight in the morning, go to bed after dinner, and work at night. The Vicar-General, a man of parts, entrusted the Chapter’s case to me. ‘Monsieur,’ I said, ‘I will win your suit, but I want no fee; I want more.’ I told him I was in Besançon to leave as deputy and would defend any priest’s case for nothing in return for secret votes when needed. He later gave me a note for five hundred francs, but the votes remained a bargain. I think I have made a friend in him.

Now I am overwhelmed with business, but I accept only commercial cases, which attach business men to me and let me make influential friends. In a few months I shall purchase a house to qualify for election; I count on you to lend me the capital. If I die or fail, the loss will be small. You will get interest from the rental.

My whole life is at stake; every day wears me out. If happiness does not crown me, I shall want to cease from living. Power and fame are secondary; they are means to happiness, the pedestal for my idol. To reach the goal and die, like antiquity’s runner! To win the beloved woman just when love is extinct! Many men have been so fated.

I play my last card in the game of ambition. If anything thwarts my plan—if after stooping to provincial life and fighting for votes I fail—I shall rebel. There are days when deep disgust surges up, especially when I dream of the joys of love. At present my life is fair, illuminated by faith, work, and love.

Farewell, my friend; I send love to your children.—Yours, ALBERT.”

Rosalie read the letter twice, and its purport was stamped on her heart. She suddenly saw all of Albert’s previous existence; her quick intelligence illuminated details. By adding this information to the little novel in the Review, she fully understood Albert. She exaggerated the greatness of his soul and will, and her love became a passion, strengthened by youth, solitude, and the unspent force of her character. Love in a young girl is natural law; when centered in an exceptional man, it mixes with enthusiasm. Mademoiselle de Watteville, in days, reached a morbid, dangerous stage of infatuation. The Baroness was pleased; Rosalie, under the spell of absorbing thoughts, never resisted her mother, seemed devoted to feminine occupations, and realized her mother’s ideal of a docile daughter.

The lawyer was now engaged in Court several times a week. Though overwhelmed with business, he attended trials, called on merchants, and conducted the Review, keeping his personal mystery, convinced that covert influence is more real. He neglected no means of success, reading the list of Besançon electors, learning their interests, characters, friendships, and antipathies. Did ever a Cardinal hope to be Pope with more toil?

One evening Mariette, dressing Rosalie for a party, handed her—after many groans—a letter whose address made Rosalie shiver:

To Madame la Duchesse d’Argaiolo (née Princesse Soderini) At Belgirate, Lago Maggiore, Italy.

The direction blazed in her eyes like Mene, Tekel, Upharsin. After concealing the letter, Rosalie accompanied her mother to Madame de Chavoncourt’s; during the evening she was tormented by remorse. She had violated the secrecy of Albert’s letter to Léopold; she asked whether, if he knew her crime—infamous because unpunished—the high-minded Albert could esteem her. Conscience answered “No.”

She imposed penances: fasting, mortifying herself on her knees, arms outstretched for hours, repeating prayers. She compelled Mariette to similar repentance; her passion mingled with asceticism and was thus more dangerous.

“Shall I read that letter, shall I not?” she asked while listening to the Chavoncourt girls. She viewed her two friends as children because they were not secretly in love. After an hour’s hesitation she decided: “If I read it, it shall be the last. Since I have seen what he wrote to his friend, why not know what he says to her? If it is a horrible crime, is it not a proof of love? Oh, Albert! am I not your wife?”

When in bed she opened the letter, dated day by day, to give the Duchess a faithful picture of Albert’s life and feelings.

25th.

“My dear Soul, all is well. I have just done a service for one of the most influential men who work elections. He wanted to show gratitude without loosening his purse by saying, ‘Would you care to sit in the Chamber? I can get you returned as deputy.’

‘If I ever enter politics,’ I replied, ‘it would be to devote myself to the Comté, which I love, and where I am appreciated.’

And so, my beloved angel, say what you will, my perseverance will be rewarded. Ere long from the French Tribune I shall come before my country, before Europe. My name will be flung at you by a hundred voices of the French press. Besançon has aged me; but like Sixtus V. I shall be young again after my election. I shall enter on my true life. Shall we not then stand in the same line? Count Savaron de Savarus may surely marry a Princess Soderini, the widow of the Duc d’Argaiolo! Triumph restores youth.”

26th.

“We have entered the twelfth year since that blessed evening when, by a look, the beautiful Duchess sealed the promises made by the exile Francesca. You, dear, are thirty-two, I thirty-five; the dear Duke is seventy-seven. My patience is almost as great as my love. I am in good spirits to-day; hope makes me laugh. Sadness or gladness, all comes through you. The hope of success always carries me back to the day I first saw you, when my life became one with yours.”

27th.

“No, dearest, do not go to Milan; stay at Belgirate. Milan terrifies me. Solitude preserves a woman’s purity.”

28th.

“Is your statue never to be finished? I wish to have you in marble, painting, miniature—to beguile my impatience. I still wait for the view of Belgirate from the south and of the balcony. I am so busy that to-day I can only write nothing—but that nothing is everything. Was it not of nothing that God made the world? That nothing is God’s word: I love you!”

30th.

“Ah! I have received your journal. So you found pleasure in seeing our first acquaintance set down? We had no stories, and a Review without stories is a beauty without hair. Not inventive by nature, I took the only poetry in my soul and pitched it in a key to bear telling; I never ceased to think of you while writing. Did not the transformation of your fierce Sormano into Gina make you laugh?

My health is better than in Paris. Though I work enormously, the peaceful surroundings soothe me. What ages me is mortified vanity, the friction of Paris. Your letter gives me immense pleasure: the pattern of your new dress, your boating—trifles that delight me. I read the books you read. Your letters have upheld me like a light and perfume for eleven years.

Do not fail me! The day before they are due I suffer anxiety. O mia cara diva, keep up your music. The first time I pleaded here I fancied you were listening; inspiration flashed. If I am returned to the Chamber—oh! you must come to Paris for my first appearance!”

30th, Evening.

“How I love you! I have intrusted too much to hope. For three years I have not seen you; my heart beats at the thought of Belgirate. To see you, hear your voice, embrace that ivory skin, admire your fingers on the keys—these are life. What folly to run after power! But at Belgirate there is everything: poetry, glory. I ought to have been your steward, or cavaliere servente, but our passion was too fierce.

Farewell, my angel, forgive this fit of sadness; hope has sent a beam of light.”

“How he loves her!” cried Rosalie, dropping the letter, which seemed heavy. “After eleven years to write like this!”

“Mariette,” Rosalie said next morning, “go and post this letter. Tell Jérôme I know all I wish to know, and that he is to serve Monsieur Albert faithfully. We will confess our sins, you and I, without saying whose letters they were or to whom they went. I was wrong; I alone am guilty.”

“Mademoiselle has been crying?” asked Mariette.

“Yes, but I do not want my mother to notice; give me some very cold water.”

Amid the storms of passion Rosalie often listened to conscience. Touched by the fidelity of these two hearts, she resolved to be resigned and respect the happiness of two worthy beings, submitting to fate without criminal acts or wishes. She felt better and imagined herself sacrificing for him.

“She does not know how to love,” she thought. “Ah! if it were I—I would give up everything to a man who loved me so.—To be loved!—When, and by whom, shall I be loved? That little Monsieur de Soulas loves my money; if I were poor, he would not look at me.”

“Rosalie, my child, what are you thinking about? You are working beyond the outline,” said the Baroness, as Rosalie made worsted slippers for the Baron.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 torn by secret tumults; by April, as she turned nineteen, she sometimes dreamed of triumphing over a Duchesse d’Argaiolo. In silence and solitude the thought inflamed her passion and dark designs. She encouraged her romantic daring with repeated plans. Such Rosalies are rare but not unique; this story contains a moral to warn them.

During that winter Albert de Savarus had been quietly advancing in Besançon. Confident of success, he impatiently awaited the dissolution of the Chamber. Among moderates he had won the support of a powerful local contractor.

Wherever they settled, the Romans took immense pains and spent large sums to secure good water. Besançon, lying in a horseshoe of the Doubs, drank water from Arcier, a hill some distance away. Restoring an aqueduct to drink the Roman water—a seemingly absurd whim—had taken hold in a place governed by exemplary gravity and would reward those with connections, including the influential contractor. Savarus held the Doubs water fit only to run under the suspension bridge and thought Arcier’s water the only potable. Articles in the Review reflected Besançon’s commercial interests. Nobility and citizens, moderates and legitimists, government and opposition—all agreed they must drink the Roman water and boast a suspension bridge. The Arcier water question dominated the town. As with proposed railways to Versailles and other local abuses, private interests energized the idea. Those few who opposed it were dismissed; Savarus’s projects consumed public talk. After eighteen months of underground work, the ambitious lawyer had stirred the most stagnant town, exerting preponderant influence without leaving his room—he had learned to be powerful without being popular.

That winter he had gained seven lawsuits for Besançon priests. Sometimes he breathed freely at the thought of his coming triumph. This intense desire, which wove many interests together, absorbed the last strength of his strained soul. His disinterestedness was lauded; he took clients’ fees without comment, but it was moral usury—he counted on a reward far greater than money.

In October 1834 he had, ostensibly to serve a merchant in difficulty and with money lent by Léopold Hannequin, taken a house that qualified him for election. He had not seemed to seek the bargain. “You are really a remarkable man,” said the Abbé de Grancey, who had watched and understood him. The Vicar-General came to introduce a canon needing counsel. “You are a priest who has taken the wrong turning,” he added; the remark struck Savarus.

Rosalie meanwhile resolved to get Monsieur de Savarus into the Hôtel de Rupt drawing room and acquainted with its society. So far she had limited herself to seeing and hearing Albert; she acquiesced to a truce with her passion.

Les Rouxey, inherited by the Wattevilles, yielded only ten thousand francs a year but could have produced far more. The Baron, indifferent and leaving management to his factotum Modinier, went there with his wife when they left town. The château and park were the work of the famous Watteville, who in his old age had passionately loved the spot.

Between two precipitous hills—great and little Rouxey—Watteville built a huge dam with two overflow cuttings. Above, he made a lake; below, two cascades united to form a river for irrigating the valley. He enclosed the hills and built a retreat on the dam, widened by soil removed to make channels.

When the Baron obtained the lake he owned the two hills but not the upper valley flooded by the dam, where a right-of-way had always existed to the horseshoe under the Dent de Vilard. Watteville was feared; no claim had been asserted while he lived. He left the slopes joined by a strong wall to protect lateral valleys, controlling the Dent de Vilard; his heirs claimed protectorate over the village of Riceys and maintained their usurpation. The old abbé planted trees and made a fine road over a Rouxey shoulder. The estate remained extensive but ill-cultivated, wild and deserted, yet full of sublime, unexpected beauty.

It is unnecessary to recount all Rosalie’s troubles and resourceful inventiveness by which she achieved her end without suspicion. Suffice that, obeying her mother, she left Besançon in May 1835 in an antique carriage with hired horses and accompanied her father to les Rouxey.

To a young girl love lurks everywhere. Morning after arrival, Rosalie looked from her bedroom and saw the lake, mists in firs and larches rolling up the hills; she cried out with admiration. “They loved by the lakes! She lives by a lake! A lake is full of love!” she thought. A snow-fed lake, shut between granite masses covered with pines and a brooding silence, must evoke exclamation.

“We owe that,” said her father, “to the notorious Watteville.”

“On my word,” said Rosalie, “he did his best to earn forgiveness. Let us go in a boat to the further end; it will give us an appetite for breakfast.”

The Baron called two gardener lads who could row and took Modinier. The lake, about six acres across and four hundred in length, brought them to the upper end below the Dent de Vilard.

“Some difficulties have arisen since 1830 between Riceys and me about this Dent de Vilard,” Modinier said, “and I want to settle the matter without your mother knowing, for she is stubborn; she might fling fire and flames, particularly if she heard the mayor of Riceys—a republican—got up this action as a sop to his people.”

For a century the Dent had been used by both parties without extremes; it stood as a party wall, yielding little profit since it lay snow-covered six months each year. The revolution of 1830, however, had stirred the people. Chantonnit, the maire since 1830, hoped to dramatize his term with the affair.

“My dear father,” said Rosalie as they reembarked, “I agree with Modinier. If you wish joint possession, act decisively and get a legal opinion to protect you against Chantonnit. Why be afraid? Get the famous lawyer Savaron—engage him at once, lest Chantonnit place the village’s interests in his hands. The man who won the Chapter's case can certainly win Watteville versus Riceys! Besides, les Rouxey will some day be mine. Don’t leave me with a lawsuit. I like this place; I shall often live here and add to it. On those banks I shall cut flowerbeds and make the loveliest English gardens. Let us go to Besançon and bring back Abbé de Grancey, Monsieur Savaron, and my mother, if she cares. But in your place I should have decided already. Your name is Watteville, and you are afraid of a fight! If you should lose—I will never reproach you!”

“Oh, if that is the way you take it,” said the Baron, “I am quite ready; I will see the lawyer.”

“A lawsuit is great fun. It brings interest into life, with coming and going, raging over it,” Rosalie said. “We did not see the Abbé de Grancey for three weeks—he was so busy!”

“He saved the Chapter!” the Baron observed.

“Listen,” Rosalie whispered, “if you secure Monsieur de Savaron, you will gain your suit. You cannot get him except through Monsieur de Grancey. Let us talk to the Abbé without my mother, for I know a way to persuade him to bring the lawyer.”

“It will be difficult to avoid telling your mother!”

“The Abbé de Grancey will settle that afterwards. But promise your vote to Monsieur de Savaron at the next election, and you’ll see!”

“Go to the election! take the oath?” cried the Baron.

“What then!” she answered.

“And what will your mother say?”

“She may even desire you to do it,” Rosalie replied, knowing from Albert’s letter to Léopold how deeply the Vicar-General had pledged him.

Four days later the Abbé de Grancey called early on Albert de Savarus, having announced his visit. The old priest had come to win the great lawyer to the Wattevilles, showing how much tact and subtlety Rosalie must have used.

“What can I do for you, Monsieur le Vicaire-Général?” asked Savarus.

The Abbé told his story with frankness; Albert listened coldly.

“Monsieur l’Abbé,” said he, “I cannot defend the Wattevilles. My part is to remain perfectly neutral. I must remain a mystery till the eve of my election. To plead for the Wattevilles would mean nothing in Paris, but here, where everything is discussed, I should be supposed to be an ally of your Faubourg Saint-Germain.”

“What! do you suppose you can remain unknown at the election, when candidates oppose each other? On the day the candidates are known, it will be known that you are Savaron de Savarus, Master of Appeals, a man of the Restoration!”

“On the day of the election,” said Savarus, “I will be all I am expected to be; and I intend to speak at the preliminary meetings.”

“If you have the support of Monsieur de Watteville and his party, you will get a mass of a hundred votes, far more reliable than those you now count on. It is always possible to divide interests; convictions are inseparable.”

“The deuce is in it!” said Savarus. “I am attached to you and could do much, Father! Perhaps we may compound with the Devil. Whatever Monsieur de Watteville’s business, by engaging Girardet and prompting him, it will be possible to drag proceedings out till the elections are over. I will not plead till the day after I am returned.”

“But there are questions requiring inspection of the localities,” said the Vicar-General.

“Girardet can go,” said Savarus. “I cannot risk anything in a town I know so well which might compromise the supreme interests that lie beyond my election.”

The Abbé left after giving him a keen look, seeming to laugh at the young man’s uncompromising politics while admiring his firmness.

“Ah! I would have dragged my father into a lawsuit—I would have done anything to get him here!” cried Rosalie to herself, standing in the kiosk and looking at the lawyer’s window the day after Albert’s interview, which the Abbé had reported to her father. “I would have committed any mortal sin, and you will not enter the Wattevilles’ drawing room; I may not hear your fine voice! You make conditions when your help is required by the Wattevilles and the Rupts! Well, God knows I meant to be content with small joys: seeing you, hearing you speak, going with you to les Rouxey so your presence might make the place sacred. That was all I asked. But now I mean to be your wife. Yes—look at her portrait, her drawing room, her bedroom, the four sides of her villa, the points of view from her gardens. You expect her statue? I will make her marble herself towards you! After all, the woman does not love. Art, science, books, singing, music have absorbed half her senses and intelligence. She is old—past thirty; my Albert will not be happy!”

“What is the matter that you stay here, Rosalie?” asked her mother, interrupting. “Monsieur de Soulas is in the drawing room and observed your attitude, which betrays more thoughtfulness than is due at your age.”

“Then, is Monsieur de Soulas a foe to thought?” asked Rosalie.

“It is time you knew our intentions. Amédée likes you and you will not be unhappy as Comtesse de Soulas.”

Rosalie, as white as a lily, made no reply, stupefied by contending feelings. In the presence of the man she had begun to hate, she forced the smile of a ballet dancer. She even laughed; she had the strength to conceal her rage, which subsided because she resolved to use this fat simpleton to further her designs.

“Monsieur Amédée,” she said, as her mother affected to leave them together, “were you not aware that Monsieur Albert Savaron de Savarus is a Legitimist?”

“A Legitimist?”

“Until 1830 he was Master of Appeals to the Council of State, attached to the supreme Ministerial Council, and in favor with the Dauphin and Dauphiness. It would be good of you to say nothing against him, but better if you attend the election this year, carry the day, and hinder Monsieur de Chavoncourt from representing Besançon.”

“What sudden interest have you in this Savaron?”

“Monsieur Albert, the natural son of the Comte de Savarus—pray keep secret—if returned will be our advocate in the Rouxey suit. Les Rouxey will be my property; I intend to live there. I should be broken-hearted to see that fine work destroyed.”

“The devil!” thought Amédée as he left. “The heiress is not such a fool as her mother thinks.”

Monsieur de Chavoncourt is a Royalist of the famous 221. After July he preached taking the oaths and resisting the present order, like Tories against Whigs. This doctrine was not acceptable to all Legitimists, who, defeated, trusted inertia and Providence. De Chavoncourt was not wholly trusted by his party but seemed the best choice to Moderates; they preferred his half-hearted opinions to a Republican who might combine enthusiast and patriot votes. High in Besançon, his fortune of about fifteen thousand a year was not offensive. Electors adore the beau ideal of parliamentary virtue.

Madame de Chavoncourt, forty and once a beauty, economized in the country while the Chamber sat. In winter she received creditably once a week. Young Chavoncourt, twenty-two, and another friend, Monsieur de Vauchelles, no richer than Amédée, were intimate allies. They went together to Granvelle and shooting, inseparable and always invited together.

Rosalie, intimate with the Chavoncourt girls, knew the three young men shared confidences. She reflected that if Monsieur de Soulas repeated her words, they would reach his two companions. Monsieur de Vauchelles wished to marry Victoire, the eldest Chavoncourt, on whom an old aunt would settle an estate of seven thousand a year and a hundred thousand francs. Thus Chavoncourt and Vauchelles would warn Monsieur de Chavoncourt of Albert’s candidacy.

But this did not satisfy Rosalie. She wrote the préfet a letter with her left hand, signed “A friend to Louis-Philippe,” informing him of Savarus’s secret intentions and the support a Royalist orator might lend Berryer, revealing the lawyer’s artful course in Besançon. The préfet, a capable man and enemy of the Royalist party devoted to the July government, read the letter and burned it.

Rosalie aimed to prevent Albert’s election, to keep him five years longer in Besançon.

Elections were party fights; the Ministry chose its ground and moment. The three months before voting hang heavy when a man’s life depends on an election. Rosalie understood how much latitude Albert’s absorbed state would give her during those months. By promising Mariette to take both her and Jérôme into her service, she induced the maid to bring her all letters Albert sent to Italy and those addressed to him from there. All the while she pondered machinations, yet worked slippers for her father with an air of innocence—because candor would serve her ends.

“My daughter grows quite charming!” said Madame de Watteville.

Two months before the election a meeting at Monsieur Boucher senior’s included the contractor expecting the aqueduct work, Boucher’s father-in-law Monsieur Granet whom Savarus had aided, Girardet the lawyer, the Review’s printer, and the Chamber of Commerce president—twenty-seven provincial bigwigs in all. Each claimed influence; one was devoted to the préfet, a false brother hoping for ministerial favor.

At this preliminary meeting it was agreed to name Savaron the candidate, a decision greeted with unexpected enthusiasm. Albert, waiting for Alfred Boucher, chatted with Abbé de Grancey, who had become his political guide. The Chapter disliked de Chavoncourt, whose wife’s brother-in-law as President of the Tribunal had lost their famous suit in lower court.

“You are betrayed, my dear fellow,” the Abbé said in a gentle voice.

“Betrayed!” cried the lover, struck.

“By whom I know not,” the priest replied. “But at the Prefecture your plans are known. I have no advice to give today. Such affairs need thought. For tonight, take the bull by the horns—tell them your past and thus soften the discovery’s effect.”

“Oh, I was prepared,” said Albert.

“You would have benefited from my advice; you could have made an impression at the Hôtel de Rupt—”

“What?”

“The unanimous support of the Royalists, immediate readiness to go to the election—above a hundred votes. Add the ecclesiastical vote and you would have commanded the ballots. Under such circumstances, a man may temporize and make his way.”

Alfred Boucher arrived, full of enthusiasm, to announce the meeting’s decision, finding the Abbé and Albert cold, calm, and grave.

“Good-night, Monsieur l’Abbé,” said Albert. “We will talk of your business when the elections are over.”

He took Alfred’s arm after pressing the Abbé’s hand. The priest looked at him as if at a general who heard the first gun; he raised his eyes to heaven and left, saying to himself, “What a priest he would make!”

Eloquence is not usually found at the bar or pulpit; it bursts at the Chamber when an ambitious man stakes all or, stung, must speak. Feeling the need for supporters, Albert displayed all his faculties. He entered gravely and without dismay before the twenty or thirty men. Before Monsieur Boucher could speak, Albert asked for silence and warned them of an unexpected danger.

“My young friend, Alfred Boucher, has done me an honor. But before your decision is irrevocable,” he said, “I ought to explain who and what your candidate is, so you may withdraw if my declaration disturbs your conscience.”

The silence that followed seemed to some a noble impulse. Albert sketched his career, gave his real name, his action under the Restoration, revealed he was a new man in Besançon, and pledged himself for the future. He argued that a statesman born to govern is better than a mere voting machine; what a glory to have found a Mirabeau in Provence! His eloquence spellbound the men, each seeing in him a potential great minister and imagining themselves profiting from his influence. The address, judged by one capable observer, was a masterpiece of skill and feeling. The whirlwind carried the electors; if votes had been taken then, Albert would have prevailed. But speech wins immediate effect; reflection kills it when the spell fades. He had to conquer anew each day for two months.

Albert went home quivering. The townsfolk had applauded, and he had silenced pre-emptively the malignant talk about his early career. The commercial interest had nominated Albert Savaron de Savarus.

Alfred Boucher’s initial enthusiasm became clumsy. The préfet, alarmed, counted ministerial votes and contrived a secret interview with Monsieur de Chavoncourt to build a coalition. Every day the Boucher committee’s voters diminished without Albert’s knowing how.

Nothing could resist the slow grinding of the Prefecture. Some men told Albert’s clients, “Will the deputy defend you and win your suits? Will he give advice, draw contracts, arrange compromises? He will be your slave for five years if you return him now; if you hold him out for five years hence, you keep him.” This calculus hurt Savarus, also because some merchants’ wives were swayed. The interests behind the bridge and Arcier water could not resist a Ministerialist’s convincing talk. Each day was a check, though Albert fought daily with speeches and proceedings. He dared not see the Abbé, and the Abbé remained invisible. Albert rose and went to bed in a fever, his brain on fire.

At last the day of the first struggle—the show of hands—arrived. Votes are counted; clever men can predict outcomes. The Radical party named a candidate; De Chavoncourt appeared; Albert was accused by his committee and the Radicals of being an uncompromising man of the Right, a second Berryer. The Ministry’s candidate was a stalking-horse to gather purely ministerial votes. Divided, the votes yielded no result: the Republican had twenty, the Ministry fifty, Albert seventy, De Chavoncourt sixty-seven. But the Prefecture’s party had perfidiously made thirty devoted adherents vote for Albert to deceive the enemy. De Chavoncourt’s votes plus the Prefecture’s eighty would carry the election if the Prefect could lure a few Radicals. A hundred and sixty votes were not recorded: those of the Abbé’s following and the Legitimists.

The show of hands is deceptive, like a dress rehearsal. Albert came home putting a brave face on it, but half dead. He had, within a fortnight, won two staunch supporters—Girardet’s father-in-law and a shrewd merchant whom the Abbé had sent. These men, his self-appointed spies, pretended to oppose him in the hostile camp and then told him toward the end of the show that thirty voters unknown were working against him in his party, playing the same trick the Prefecture played on the other side.

A criminal marching to execution could not have suffered as Albert did coming home. He walked the streets alone between eleven and midnight. At one in the morning, sleepless for days, he sat in his library pale, hands limp, in a forlorn attitude. Tears hung on his lashes—tears that dim the eyes but did not fall. Fierce thought drank them up; the fire of the soul consumed them. Alone, he might weep. Under the kiosk he saw a white figure that reminded him of Francesca.

“And for three months I have had no letter from her! What has become of her? I have not written for two months, but I warned her. Is she ill? Oh, my love! My life! Will you ever know what I have gone through? What a wretched constitution is mine! Have I an aneurism?” he asked, feeling his heart thunder.

Three distinct taps sounded. Albert hurried to the door and almost fainted at seeing the Abbé de Grancey’s cheerful, triumphant mien. He threw his arms about the Abbé, clung and let his head fall on the old man’s shoulder. He betrayed his weakness to no one but this priest, on whose face hope shone.

“Forgive me, dear Abbé, but you come at one of those moments when the man vanishes, for you are not to think me vulgarly ambitious.”

“Oh! I know,” replied the Abbé. “You wrote ‘Ambition for love’s sake!’—Ah, my son, I was made a priest in 1786 through love in despair. I have refused three bishoprics; I mean to die at Besançon.”

“Come and see her!” cried Savarus, seizing a candle and leading the Abbé into the handsome room to show the portrait of the Duchesse d’Argaiolo, which he lighted.

“She is one of those women born to reign!” said the Abbé. “But there is pride on that brow; it is implacable; she would never forgive an insult. It is the Archangel Michael, the angel of Execution—‘All or nothing’ is the motto of that head. There is something divinely pitiless in it.”

“You have guessed well,” cried Savarus. “For more than twelve years she has reigned over my life, and I have no thought to blame myself for—”

“Ah, if you could only say the same of God!” said the priest simply. “Now, to your affairs. For ten days I have been at work. If you are a real politician, follow my advice. You would not be where you are if you had gone to the Wattevilles when I first told you. But you must go tomorrow; I will take you in the evening. The Rouxey estates are in danger; the case must be defended within three days. The election will not be over in three days. They will take care not to appoint examiners the first day. There will be several voting days, and you may be elected by ballot—”

“How can that be?” asked Savarus.

“By winning the Rouxey lawsuit you will gain eighty Legitimist votes; add the thirty I can command and you have a hundred and ten. Then, as twenty remain to you from the Boucher committee, you will have a hundred and thirty.”

“Well,” said Albert, “we must get seventy-five more.”

“Yes,” said the priest, “since the rest are Ministerial. But, my son, you have two hundred votes and the Prefecture no more than a hundred and eighty.”

“I have two hundred votes?” said Albert, astonished.

“You have those of Monsieur de Chavoncourt,” said the Abbé.

“How?”

“You will marry Mademoiselle Sidonie de Chavoncourt.”

“Never!”

“You will marry Mademoiselle Sidonie de Chavoncourt,” the priest repeated.

The Abbé meant Albert to secure the Chavoncourt family’s support through a pledged marriage with Sidonie. He arranged that tomorrow evening at the Hôtel de Rupt Albert would meet Madame de Chavoncourt and her second daughter and obtain from her what he needed. The Abbé denied personal involvement beyond advice; he had confided only in Madame de Watteville.

“I will bring the Duchess to you to be blessed!” cried Savarus.

After seeing out the Abbé, Albert went to bed swaddled in the dreams of power.

Next evening, by nine o’clock Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were crowded with the aristocracy of Besancon. They debated the exceptional step of going to the poll to oblige the daughter of the Rupts. It was known that the former Master of Appeals, secretary to one of the most faithful ministers of the Elder Branch, was to be presented that evening. Madame de Chavoncourt was there with her second daughter Sidonie, exquisitely dressed; her elder sister, secure of her lover, had not bothered with toilette—an omission noticed in a country town. The Abbe de Grancey moved among groups, listening, appearing detached yet cutting through questions with his incisive remarks.

“If the Elder Branch returned,” he asked an old statesman, “what politicians would they find?”—“Berryer, alone on his bench, would not know which way to turn; with sixty votes he could upset Ministries!”—“The Duc de Fitz-James is to be nominated at Toulouse.” —“You will enable Monsieur de Watteville to win his lawsuit.” —“If you vote for Monsieur Savarus, the Republicans will vote with you rather than with the Moderates!”—and so on.

At nine o’clock Albert had not arrived. Madame de Watteville regarded the delay as an impertinence.

“My dear Baroness,” said Madame de Chavoncourt, “do not let such serious issues turn on a trifle. The varnish on his boots is not dry—or a consultation detains Monsieur de Savarus.”

Rosalie shot a glance at Madame de Chavoncourt.

“She is very lenient to Monsieur de Savarus,” she whispered to her mother.

“You see,” said the Baroness with a smile, “there is talk of a marriage between Sidonie and Monsieur de Savarus.”

Mademoiselle de Watteville hastily went to a window overlooking the garden.

At ten o’clock Albert still had not appeared. The patience of some guests broke; a few sat down to cards. The Abbe de Grancey, puzzled, went to the window where Rosalie stood and exclaimed, “He must be dead!”

The Vicar-General stepped into the garden, followed by Monsieur de Watteville and his daughter; the three went to the kiosk. In Albert’s rooms all was dark; no light showed.

“Jerome!” cried Rosalie, seeing the servant below. The Abbe looked at her in astonishment. “Where in the world is your master?” she asked the man at the foot of the wall.

“Gone—in a post-chaise, mademoiselle.”

“He is ruined!” exclaimed the Abbe de Grancey, “or he is happy!”

Rosalie’s triumph could not be fully concealed; the Vicar-General observed it but pretended not to notice. He wondered what part the girl had in the affair.

They returned to the drawing-room, where Monsieur de Watteville announced the surprising news of the lawyer’s departure, without assigning any reason. By half-past eleven only fifteen persons remained: among them Madame de Chavoncourt and the Abbe de Godenars, another Vicar-General hoping for a bishopric; the two Chavoncourt girls; Monsieur de Vauchelles; the Abbe de Grancey; Rosalie; Amedee de Soulas; and a retired magistrate, one of Besancon’s influential men who had been eager for Albert’s election. The Abbe de Grancey sat near the Baroness to watch Rosalie, whose usually pale face now wore a feverish flush.

“What can have happened to Monsieur de Savarus?” said Madame de Chavoncourt.

A servant in livery then brought a letter for the Abbe de Grancey on a silver tray.

“Pray read it,” said the Baroness.

The Vicar-General read; he saw Rosalie suddenly turn as white as her handkerchief. He fancied she recognized the handwriting. He folded the letter and calmly put it in his pocket without comment. Three glances from Rosalie were enough for him to guess the rest.

“She is in love with Albert Savarus!” he thought.

He rose to leave. In the next room Rosalie overtook him and said, “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!”

“How do you know that, to recognize it from so far?” he asked.

Caught in her impatience and anger, her reply seemed to him sublime.

“I love him!--What is the matter?” she added after a pause.

“He gives up the election,” the Abbe answered.

Rosalie put her finger to her lip.

“I ask you to be as secret as if it were a confession,” she said before returning to the drawing-room. “If there is an end of the election, there is an end of the marriage with Sidonie.”

In the morning on her way to Mass, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette why Albert had suddenly vanished at the most critical moment of his life. An elderly gentleman from Paris had arrived at the Hotel National the previous day in a great carriage with a courier and a servant. Jerome, who saw it, declared he could only be a prince or milord. The stranger called on Monsieur Savaron at two o’clock and was shown in; Monsieur turned as pale as death, but treated the visitor with extreme respect. They stayed together more than an hour. Jerome saw the servant leave with a very large package—four feet long, like a canvas painting—and the stranger carried a thick parcel of papers. The courier departed at three.

After the visitor’s call, Monsieur Savaron went at once to the Prefecture, then to Monsieur Gentillet to buy an old traveling carriage formerly belonging to Madame de Saint-Vier; he ordered post horses for six. He returned home to pack, wrote many letters, settled accounts with Monsieur Girardet, who stayed until seven; Jerome delivered a note to Monsieur Boucher, with whom his master had been to dine, and at half-past seven the lawyer left, dismissing Jerome with three months’ wages. Monsieur Savaron left his keys with Girardet, whom he escorted home; both Girardet and Jerome were deeply upset. When Savaron mounted his carriage he looked like death and told the postilion: “The Geneva Road!”

Because the old gentleman did not intend to stay, no one asked his name; his servant pretended not to speak French. The mysterious caller had then gone away as quietly as he came. Mademoiselle Galard called him “mysterious.” People assumed the Prefect had easily given Monsieur Savaron a passport across the frontier, removing their only opponent; the next day Monsieur de Chavoncourt was elected by a majority of one hundred forty votes. Locals muttered that “Jack is gone by the way he came,” and the affair increased hostility to strangers already inflamed by a recent Republican newspaper controversy. Ten days later Albert de Savarus was never spoken of again publicly. Only Girardet the attorney, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie were deeply affected. Girardet had seen the stranger’s card and knew him to be Prince Soderini; the Vicar-General was told; and Rosalie, better informed, had known for three months that the Duc d’Argaiolo was dead.

By April 1836 no news of Albert had arrived. Jerome and Mariette planned to marry, though the Baroness wished Mariette to wait until her daughter’s marriage so the weddings might coincide. The Baroness pressed her husband to get Rosalie married; she thought Rosalie, now nineteen, had changed alarmingly. The Baron—fond of Rosalie and inclined to indulge her—offered to give her the estate les Rouxey. The Baroness warned that this decision could cost so much that the family would be left with nothing; the Baron confessed he felt his legs grow weaker and indicated a fondness for eating and working with the lathe, an oddity his wife blamed for his ailments.

They decided that Rosalie would be married to Monsieur de Soulas; Rosalie, summoned, declared she would not marry and that she was content with her parents. Her mother dismissed that as mere speeches: since Rosalie was not in love with Monsieur de Soulas, she would be married to him regardless. Rosalie stubbornly refused. Her mother told her that unless she married as directed she would receive nothing from her, taking the matter into a violent quarrel. The enmity grew so bitter that father and daughter left for les Rouxey for the summer; life at the Hotel de Rupt had become unbearable. It was widely known in Besancon that Rosalie had flatly refused the Comte de Soulas.

After their marriage, Mariette and Jerome moved to les Rouxey to take the place of a former servant, Modinier. The Baron restored and improved the house to suit Rosalie; when the Baroness learned the work had cost about sixty thousand francs she suspected spite in her daughter. The Baron bought outlying plots and a small estate worth thirty thousand francs. Away from her mother, Rosalie showed practical sense: she raised the value of les Rouxey, bought a riding habit, rode about, and made her father happy; his complaints ceased and he grew fat. The Vicar-General visited les Rouxey to negotiate peace ahead of the Baroness’s name-day, and locals remarked, “That little Rosalie has a head on her shoulders.” After paying ninety thousand francs on les Rouxey, the Baroness grudgingly allowed her husband a thousand francs per month; the father and daughter agreed to return to Besancon for August.

After dinner one day the Vicar-General took Rosalie aside to discuss marriage, warning it was vain to wait for Albert, of whom they had had no news for a year. Rosalie led him to a seat under rhododendrons overlooking the lake and, with a mixture of tenderness and resolve, confessed that she had committed crimes to make Albert her husband; she then produced a newspaper with an item from Florence: the Duc de Rhetore had married the Duchesse d’Argaiolo, née Princess Soderini, with great splendor, and she had inherited her late husband’s fortune. “The woman he loved is married,” Rosalie said, and then added, “I divided them.”

Before she could explain how, a cry from the gardeners interrupted them: the Baron had fallen into the lake. Trying to reach a rock where he fancied he saw an impression of a shell, he had slipped into the deepest part beneath the roadway. The men got him to a pole where the water bubbled; they pulled him out, covered in mud into which he had sunk deeper by struggling. He had dined heavily and his digestion was interrupted. Undressed, washed, and placed in bed, he was in such danger that two servants rode for help—one to Besancon, another for the nearest doctor and surgeon. When Madame de Watteville arrived eight hours later with aid, the Baron was past hope. The fright had produced effusion on the brain; the shock to digestion hastened his decline. Madame de Watteville declared it would not have happened had he stayed in Besancon, blamed Rosalie’s obstinacy, developed an aversion to her daughter, and abandoned herself to exaggerated grief. The Baron’s body was buried on an island in the lake at les Rouxey, where the Baroness had a white marble Gothic monument erected.

A month later mother and daughter returned to the Hotel de Rupt in savage silence. Rosalie was truly sorrowful and blamed herself for causing her father’s death; she feared another disaster she thought was her fault. Neither Girardet nor the Abbe de Grancey could obtain information about Albert. In a paroxysm of repentance Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General the horrible machinations by which she had separated Francesca (the Duchess) and Albert.

Her confession was clear: while Albert was consumed by election business, she had intercepted Albert’s letters to the Duchess and one from Francesca telling Albert her husband was ill and she could no longer write while attending him. Rosalie had kept only two letters from the Duchess—the notice of danger to the Duc d’Argaiolo and the announcement of his death. After several nights of labor, she had forged Albert’s handwriting perfectly and substituted three forged letters of her own for three letters of Albert’s. In the forgeries—written as though by Albert—she prepared the Duchess for a change in the Frenchman’s feelings, falsely presenting him as faithless, and answered the news of the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death by announcing his imminent marriage to Mademoiselle de Watteville. The two letters, meant to cross, did so. Francesca, struck by what she believed was Albert’s perfidy, had answered with four words: “You are free. Farewell.” The forgeries were accurate enough to terrify even the Abbe, who read them twice in horror. The Abbe declared that purely moral crimes that escape human justice are the most atrocious and detestable; they often bring divine punishments on earth.

The Abbe spoke of the youthful page who, falsely accused and carrying a letter ordering execution, sets out without modern malice and is miraculously protected—he invoked the glory of virtue and the horror of secret crimes, especially breaking the seal of a letter. He told Rosalie he was not attempting to scold her as her spiritual director but to warn her as a friend who feared her punishment. He asked what had become of Albert—had he killed himself? He suspected Prince Soderini had come to reclaim his daughter’s letters and portraits, and that the thunderbolt of that revelation had fallen on Albert, who had gone away to try to justify himself. But how, the Abbe wondered, had fourteen months passed without a word?

Rosalie declared that if she could marry Albert, he would be happy. The Abbe told her the truth: Albert did not love her; she had no great fortune to offer; her mother detested her; and she had made a bitter reply that would ruin her. When the Baroness had told Rosalie obedience could repair her errors, Rosalie had retorted, “If you are so fond of him, marry him yourself, mother!” The Abbe warned that in a few months Madame de Watteville might become Comtesse de Soulas and be likely to have children; she might then reduce Rosalie’s share. Materially and sentimentally she had done badly. Instead of going to her mother or to religion for counsel, Rosalie had trusted passion and ignorance.

The Abbe told her to ascertain the full extent of her wrong: to place the real and forged letters in his hands, confess everything, and ask how to expiate her sins. Above all, she must restore Albert’s innocence in the eyes of the woman he had adored. Though he had lost his happiness, the Abbe insisted, Albert must still have hope for justification. Rosalie promised to obey, hoping the Abbe’s steps might bring Albert back.

Not long after, a clerk arrived from Monsieur Leopold Hannequin, Albert’s friend and notary in Paris, with a power of attorney and came first to Girardet, begging help to sell Savaron’s house. The clerk sold the furniture and repaid some debts Savaron had left; Girardet asked what had become of the pleader, and the clerk said no one knew. The notary seemed distressed by the contents of Albert’s last letter. On hearing this, the Abbe wrote to Leopold. The notary’s reply was bleak: Albert had renounced the world and become a novice in the monastery of the Grand Chartreuse near Grenoble. The General of the Order had put a barrier between Leopold and Albert; Leopold knew Albert to be the victim of an odious plot, but everything was ended. The Duchesse d’Argaiolo, now Duchesse de Rhetore, had carried her severity to an extreme. At Belgirate she had left instructions that led Albert to think she lived in London. From London, Albert went to Naples and Rome, where she was engaged to the Duc de Rhetore. Albert saw Madame d’Argaiolo at her marriage ceremony in Florence and swooned in the church; he could get no explanation from her. For seven months he pursued a cruel woman who seemed to think it sport to elude him; he could not catch her. Leopold had seen him in Paris and would not risk mentioning the Duchess for fear of wrecking Albert’s reason. Leopold concluded Albert longed for rest and had entered the cloister for peace.

The Abbe then wrote to the General of the Carthusian order and received a letter from Albert himself. Albert, now Brother Albert at La Grande Chartreuse, wrote that he recognized the Abbe’s tender soul and understood the General’s demand to make sure Albert’s vocation was sincere; since Albert intended to preserve silence and renounce the world, he would not be allowed to return to justify himself in worldly courts. Grace had done its work; the struggle had been short but keen, and his vocation was now sincere. He forgave Rosalie entirely and would pray to God to forgive her and to give Madame de Rhetore happiness. He accepted sorrow and the cloister’s oblivion, laying the remains of his life at the altar. He declared he would write no more and named the Abbe in his prayers.

When the Abbe showed this letter to Rosalie, she kissed the lines that contained her forgiveness; he asked whether now that Albert was lost to her she would reconcile with her mother and marry Monsieur de Soulas. She replied she would do so only if Albert ordered it, but the General would not permit consultation; no Carthusian sees visitors—indeed, he said only the Queen of France might enter a Carthusian monastery. Thus no excuse remained for refusing Monsieur de Soulas. Rosalie demurred, insisting she did not wish to destroy her mother’s happiness, to which the Abbe retorted harshly.

Towards the end of that winter the Abbe de Grancey died, removing the one figure able to mediate between the Baroness and her daughter. The Abbe’s prophecy soon came true. In August 1837 Madame de Watteville married Monsieur de Soulas in Paris, following Rosalie’s counsel; the girl displayed sweetness and apparent warmth toward her mother, who believed the behavior genuine. Madame de Watteville devoted herself to avenging Savarus by torturing his rival. Mademoiselle de Watteville had been declared legally of age and signed a release renouncing claims on les Rouxey, while Rosalie urged her mother to settle her fortune on Soulas—“Let us each be perfectly free,” she said. The Comtesse, touched by Rosalie’s apparent generosity, made her a present of six thousand francs a year as conscience money. The Comtesse’s income was forty-eight thousand francs; Mademoiselle de Watteville remained an heiress of eighteen hundred thousand francs. Les Rouxey, with improvements, might yield twenty thousand francs a year, besides house value, rents, and preserves. With money and introductions, mother and daughter were readily admitted to the best society.

In February 1838 Rosalie achieved the meeting she had so long pursued: to see the Duchesse de Rhetore, the woman whose life she had helped ruin, and to overwhelm her with remorse. Rosalie adopted extreme elegance and vanities so she could face the Duchess on equal terms. Their first encounter occurred at a post-1830 annual ball for pensioners. A young man, prompted by Rosalie, pointed her out to the Duchess as “a strong-minded lady who drove a clever man into a monastery—Albert de Savarus,” naming her as Mademoiselle de Watteville. The Duchess turned pale; Rosalie’s eyes met hers with a flash as fatal in intent as pistols. Francesca Soderini, who had suspected Albert might be innocent, hurried from the ballroom distraught. Rosalie then sent an anonymous note asking the Duchess to come to the Opera ball on Tuesday with a marigold if she wished to hear more about Albert. At that ball Rosalie placed in the Duchess’s hand all of Albert’s letters, the Abbe de Grancey’s letter to Leopold Hannequin, the notary’s reply, and Rosalie’s own confession to the Abbe.

“I will not be the only sufferer,” she told her rival, “for one has been as ruthless as the other.” Having savored the dismay on the Duchess’s face, Rosalie withdrew from public view for a time and then returned with her mother to Besancon.

Thus the sequence of events—an arrival of a mysterious stranger that precipitated Savaron’s flight, the clerical and legal scramble that followed, Rosalie’s secret forgery and subsequent confession, the Baron’s death, Albert’s renunciation and retreat to the Grand Chartreuse, and Rosalie’s bitter, strategized confrontation with the woman she had wronged—closed with Rosalie socially elevated and wealthy but inwardly burdened. Her outward victory in forcing the Duchess to see the truth of her acts was matched by the eternal solitude Albert chose and that Rosalie had ultimately helped bring about.

Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone at Les Rouxey—riding, hunting, rejecting two or three offers a year, wintering often in Besançon and improving her land. Regarded as eccentric, she was a celebrity of the Eastern provinces.

Madame de Soulas has two children, a boy and a girl, and seems younger; Monsieur de Soulas has aged noticeably.

“My fortune has cost me dear,” he said to young Chavoncourt. “Really to know a bigot it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

Mademoiselle de Watteville behaved extraordinarily—“she has vagaries,” people said. Each year she went to gaze at the walls of the Grande Chartreuse; perhaps she fancied, like her grand-uncle Watteville—who once broke through monastery walls to regain his liberty—that she might force the monastery's walls to find a husband.

She left Besançon in 1841, purportedly to marry, but returned in a state that forbids her appearing in society. By the chance the Abbé de Grancey mentioned, she was on a Loire steamboat whose boiler burst. She was so severely injured that she lost her right arm and her left leg; her face bears fearful scars that have taken her beauty; her health is shattered and she has few days free from suffering. In short, she never leaves the Chartreuse of Les Rouxey, where she lives wholly devoted to religious practices.

PARIS, May 1842.
