**ALBERT SAVARUS**  
*By Honore de Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage*  

**DEDICATION**  
To Madame Emile Girardin  

In the drawing-room of Baronne de Watteville, a prominent figure in Besançon society, the Archbishop frequently visited due to her devout nature. Monsieur de Watteville, a descendant of a notorious figure, was a gentle man married to wealthy heiress Mademoiselle de Rupt. Their 1815 marriage united their fortunes, with Madame de Watteville becoming increasingly pious.

Monsieur de Watteville, a turner by trade, also collected geological specimens, a hobby some found eccentric. Their home, reflecting noble heritage, was filled with fine furnishings and exquisite food, showcasing their social status. Madame de Watteville’s ties with the Archbishop attracted notable clergy to their gatherings.

During a ceremonial dinner in September 1834, guests welcomed Monsieur l’Abbe de Grancey, who announced a surprising legal victory in a lawsuit, crediting young lawyer Monsieur Savaron for his success against a formidable Parisian opponent. This revelation intrigued the guests, particularly Madame de Watteville, who inquired about Savaron’s background. The Vicar-General noted that Savaron lived nearby, but his origins were unknown, raising further curiosity.

The conversation highlighted Savaron's remarkable abilities and the church's unexpected triumph over liberal opposition, setting the stage for a deeper exploration of his character and events in Besançon.

Throughout history, France and England have exchanged cultural influences, particularly in language and fashion, often circumventing customs regulations. Styles labeled "English" in Paris are viewed as "French" in London, highlighting a mutual fascination. For instance, the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli, while hoop skirts, fashionable in Paris, were introduced by an Englishwoman but originated from a French invention.

Recently, England has contributed new terms to the French lexicon, such as "dandy" and "lion." The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset's poetry, describes a flamboyant fashionable man. This trend spread beyond Paris, influencing provincial towns like Besançon, where Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas emerged as a local "lion." Amedee, a young man of Spanish descent, sought to elevate his status in a town known for its military and conservative culture.

Living modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment, Amedee maintained a lavish lifestyle with the help of his stylishly dressed servant, Babylas. Despite his extravagant appearance, Amedee struggled financially, relying on a limited income from his father's estate. He was perceived as a spendthrift but cleverly balanced his expenses, prioritizing appearances over substance.

Amedee's social life revolved around impressing the local aristocracy, particularly aiming to win the affection of Rosalie de Watteville, a young woman from a wealthy family. Rosalie, raised in a strict household, was largely sheltered and received a limited education focused on religious and domestic skills. At eighteen, she embodied modesty and virtue, yet harbored a strong spirit beneath her reserved exterior.

The dynamic between Amedee and Rosalie was complicated by their social standings and familial expectations. Amedee's aspirations to marry Rosalie were fueled by her substantial dowry, which he believed would secure his financial future. Meanwhile, Rosalie's mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective, scrutinizing Amedee's intentions and controlling Rosalie's interactions.

Despite Amedee's efforts to charm Rosalie and her mother, he faced challenges in gaining their approval. His attempts to present himself as a cultured gentleman were met with skepticism, as Rosalie remained indifferent to his advances. The tension between them was exacerbated by Madame de Watteville's rigid expectations and Rosalie's internal struggles.

As Amedee navigated the complexities of social life in Besançon, he became a figure of intrigue among the local elite. His fashionable attire and social maneuvers earned him a reputation as the most charming young man in town, yet his true motivations remained obscured. Ultimately, Amedee's pursuit of Rosalie was not merely a quest for love but a strategic move to elevate his social standing and secure a prosperous future.

In summary, the interplay of fashion, social status, and personal ambition shaped the lives of Amedee de Soulas and Rosalie de Watteville in the conservative milieu of Besançon, reflecting broader themes of aspiration, societal expectations, and the complexities of human relationships in a changing cultural landscape.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Monsieur de Soulas recounted the story of Albert Savaron, who moved from Paris to Besançon, rented an apartment, and registered as a barrister. Mademoiselle de Watteville noted Savaron's prestigious lineage, but Amedee de Soulas clarified that he was a common Frenchman who had added a bar-sinister to his name to imply nobility.

Initially, Savaron was overlooked by the local legal community, who found him puzzling. He worked diligently, rising early and keeping to himself, which led to his being forgotten. He attended early mass but remained largely a mystery. Monsieur de Soulas learned about him through his servant and local gossip.

Savaron's reputation changed when he successfully defended a mentally challenged man accused of forgery, impressing the court and attracting clients. The Vicar-General, Abbe de Grancey, noted Savaron's striking appearance and eloquence, highlighting his calm demeanor despite a passionate nature. His modest living conditions contrasted with his impressive intellect and oratory skills.

Madame de Chavoncourt expressed curiosity about Savaron's motivations for coming to Besançon and his lack of community integration. The Abbe speculated that Savaron would eventually make a significant impact.

Rosalie de Watteville, intrigued by Savaron, found him more appealing than her superficial suitors. Determined to meet him, she planned to encounter him at church. After a brief confession with the Abbe, she glimpsed Savaron during mass and felt an intense connection.

As Rosalie's fascination grew, she encouraged her father to build a Belvedere in their garden to observe Savaron's private life, attracting attention in Besançon.

Meanwhile, Savaron launched a fortnightly publication, the _Eastern Review_, aimed at promoting Besançon's interests and competing with larger cities. He managed it discreetly, gaining support from local merchants.

Rosalie's father subscribed to the _Eastern Review_, allowing her to read its contents, including a tale attributed to Savaron, deepening her infatuation with him. 

In summary, the narrative follows Albert Savaron's arrival in Besançon, his rise to prominence through legal and literary achievements, and the growing affection of Rosalie de Watteville, enchanted by his mysterious persona.

**AMBITION FOR LOVE’S SAKE - Summary**

In July 1823, friends Rodolphe and Leopold traveled to Switzerland to enjoy the beauty of the Four Cantons. While on a boat, Rodolphe became captivated by Fanny Lovelace, who was looking out from a house in Gersau. He chose to stay in Gersau, while Leopold continued their journey.

Rodolphe and Leopold had contrasting backgrounds: Leopold was set to become a notary in Paris, while Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, was raised by a devoted mother who sacrificed for his education. Rodolphe was passionate and sensitive, driven by his desires.

After breakfast at the Swan Inn, Rodolphe found lodging with the Stopfer couple, who provided him a room with a lake view. He learned that Fanny had moved to Gersau with her elderly father for his health, living modestly on her work as a translator and writer. Rodolphe was enchanted by her beauty and musical talent.

Eager to meet Fanny, Rodolphe sought permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens, where she lived with her mute servant, Gina. Initially met with suspicion, he eventually gained access. While exploring, he noticed Gina's unusual appearance, suspecting a hidden background.

One evening, while listening to Fanny sing, Rodolphe overheard her speaking Italian with Gina, leading him to suspect they were Italian refugees. When he approached them, Gina attacked him, mistaking him for a threat. Fanny and Gina helped him recover, deepening Rodolphe's attraction to Fanny's beauty and kindness.

As Rodolphe healed, he learned of his mother’s death, which plunged him into despair. Leopold returned to France, leaving Rodolphe in Gersau, where he found solace in Fanny's company. Their bond deepened as they shared sorrows and dreams. Fanny revealed she was married to an older man, Emilio, but expressed hope for a future together.

Rodolphe's feelings for Fanny intensified, admiring her strength and independence. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential. Their conversations revealed a shared passion for freedom and liberal ideals.

When Fanny received news that her family was safe and would return to Italy, she felt joy, but Rodolphe struggled with jealousy and insecurity regarding her past and her relationship with Emilio. Despite these challenges, Rodolphe remained devoted to Fanny, vowing to wait for her. Their love blossomed against the stunning Swiss landscape, symbolizing their hopes and the obstacles they faced, exploring themes of ambition, love, and the complexities of human relationships amid political turmoil and personal sacrifice.

For twenty days, Rodolphe stayed with the Bergmanns, captivated by Francesca, who embodied youthful imprudence and the complexities of womanhood. He observed her relationship with her husband, Emilio, marked by gratitude and a fatherly affection, which intrigued him and drew him closer to her.

Their interactions were filled with secret joys and occasional quarrels that Rodolphe found delightful. One evening, Francesca expressed her longing for luxury, likening it to her love for art and beauty. She refused to accompany Rodolphe to Geneva, fearing gossip that could tarnish her husband’s reputation. While she professed her love for Rodolphe, she emphasized the need for discretion to protect their bond.

The next day, upon learning that the Bergmanns had left, Rodolphe departed Gersau for Geneva. He settled in Eaux-Vives and inquired about Italian refugees, particularly a bookseller named Lamporani. His curiosity led him to the Villa Diodati, where he encountered Francesca in an elegant carriage, revealing her connection to the aristocratic Colonna family.

Rodolphe was struck by the realization of Francesca’s true identity, grappling with feelings of inadequacy for loving a princess disguised as a commoner. He sought an audience with Prince Gandolphini, Francesca’s husband, who welcomed him warmly. Despite his desire to see Francesca, Rodolphe returned to his lodgings, consumed by thoughts of their future.

When Rodolphe finally saw Francesca at a gathering, her beauty captivated him. Their eyes met, igniting a profound connection that transcended their surroundings. Francesca’s subtle acknowledgment of their bond amidst the social elite deepened Rodolphe’s feelings, solidifying his love for her.

As they spent time together, Francesca encouraged Rodolphe to pursue his ambitions while remaining youthful. Their conversations reflected a shared commitment to love faithfully, despite the challenges they faced.

Francesca’s background as a well-educated heiress, initially destined for a cloistered life, added depth to her character. Her marriage to Prince Gandolphini was strategic, yet her revolutionary spirit aligned with Rodolphe’s aspirations.

Winter passed quickly for Rodolphe in Geneva, but as spring approached, he struggled with feelings of longing and anxiety. Francesca’s calm demeanor contrasted with his emotional turmoil, leading him to question her feelings. Their discussions revealed the complexities of their love, with Francesca asserting her Italian identity and pride.

Tension arose when Rodolphe compared Francesca to a French noblewoman, inadvertently hurting her feelings. Sensing her distress, he sought to comfort her, but she maintained her dignity, instructing him to leave until they could meet again in Naples. This separation marked a pivotal moment in their relationship, highlighting the challenges they faced as lovers from different worlds. Francesca’s strength left a lasting impression on Rodolphe, who was determined to prove himself worthy of her love.

Upon returning to Paris, Rodolphe discovered a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner, who had surprisingly agreed to paint it. Francesca's charm inspired the artist to create an original for Rodolphe and a duplicate for Emilio, leading to regular correspondence. Driven by ambition and love, Rodolphe invested all his resources in a venture that ultimately failed, costing him three years. After the Villele ministry fell, he sought solace in Naples at the Villa Gandolphini. Just as he was poised for success, the July 1830 revolution struck, but Rodolphe persevered, supported by love.

As Mademoiselle de Watteville finished her reading, she was consumed by rage and jealousy over Francesca Colonna, the woman she believed Albert loved. This narrative ignited a fierce desire in her to win Albert's affection, yet she doubted her worthiness, thinking, “He will never love me!”

That night, Rosalie's imagination ran wild with schemes to connect with Albert, from sneaking into his garden to writing him a letter. She even considered a lawsuit to bring him into her social circle. While gazing at Albert's window, she noticed a suspicious scene involving Jerome, Albert's servant, and Mariette, the maid. This discovery sparked a dangerous curiosity in Rosalie, who realized that a single moment could unravel her mother's strict upbringing.

The next day, Rosalie confronted Mariette about her relationship with Jerome, offering her silence in exchange for access to letters Albert sent through Jerome. Mariette, relieved, agreed, deepening Rosalie's obsession with Albert's life.

Rosalie learned from her father that a copy of the _Eastern Review_ was sent to the Duchesse d’Argaiolo in Italy, further fueling her jealousy. She later intercepted a letter from Albert to his friend Leopold, revealing his noble lineage and ambitions. This letter intensified Rosalie's infatuation, leading her to view him as a heroic figure.

As Albert's legal career flourished, Rosalie's feelings morphed into a dangerous obsession. She felt compelled to read another letter from Albert to the Duchesse, detailing his hopes and affections for her. The letters revealed Albert's deep love for the Duchesse, leaving Rosalie heartbroken yet determined to respect their bond.

Despite her turmoil, Rosalie resolved to step back, believing she should sacrifice her desires for Albert and the Duchesse's happiness, grappling with her feelings of unrequited love and questioning when she would find someone who truly loved her. Ultimately, Rosalie's internal conflict highlighted the complexities of love, jealousy, and the sacrifices made in the name of affection.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

In the spring of 1835, nineteen-year-old Rosalie fantasized about overcoming the Duchesse d’Argaiolo, driven by her solitude and secret desires. Meanwhile, Albert de Savarus made political strides in Besançon, gaining support from influential figures, including a wealthy contractor, as he aimed to restore an aqueduct from Arcier, benefiting both his ambitions and the contractor's interests.

Savarus, who had successfully represented local priests in lawsuits, was motivated by a desire for success, masking his true intentions behind a facade of disinterest. In October 1834, he arranged financing for a merchant, inadvertently positioning himself for election. The Abbe de Grancey recognized Savarus's potential but noted his reluctance to align with the Wattevilles, fearing it would compromise his neutrality.

Determined to introduce Savarus to her social circle, Rosalie planned a trip to les Rouxey, her family estate. There, she learned of her father's legal troubles over land disputes with Riceys and suggested hiring Savarus as their lawyer, believing it would protect her inheritance and strengthen their connection.

While trying to keep Savarus in Besançon, Rosalie also sought to undermine his political ambitions by informing the Prefect of his Royalist leanings. Despite her efforts, Savarus was nominated as a candidate, and his eloquence during a meeting won the townsfolk's support.

However, the political landscape shifted as the Prefect and his allies worked to weaken Savarus's backing. The election process revealed the complexities of local politics, with various factions vying for influence. As the election approached, Savarus faced increasing challenges, creating a tense atmosphere filled with uncertainty.

In despair, Savarus reflected on his unrequited love for Francesca, feeling the weight of his ambitions. Just when he felt defeated, the Abbe de Grancey arrived with hopeful news, urging him to connect with the Wattevilles, particularly Madame de Chavoncourt, whose daughter Sidonie could provide crucial support.

The Abbe's advice led Savarus to consider a marriage alliance with Sidonie, which would secure the votes he needed to win the election. As he prepared to navigate this new political landscape, he recognized the sacrifices he would need to make for his ambitions, including a potential marriage that could alter his future. Ultimately, the intertwining of personal desires and political aspirations set the stage for a dramatic confrontation between love and ambition.

The following evening, Madame la Baronne de Watteville hosted a gathering of Besançon’s aristocracy to discuss the election, particularly concerning the Rupts' daughter. The former Master of Appeals was to be introduced, with attendees including Madame de Chavoncourt and her daughters. The Abbe de Grancey mingled among the guests, offering sharp political insights.

By nine o’clock, Albert de Savarus had not arrived, raising concern. While Madame de Chavoncourt suggested his delay was trivial, Rosalie, the Baroness's daughter, suspected otherwise. As time passed, frustration grew, and the Abbe de Grancey speculated that Albert might be dead. They learned from a servant that he had left in a post-chaise, prompting mixed reactions of shock and relief.

As the evening continued, only a few guests remained, including Madame de Chavoncourt and the Abbe de Godenars. The Abbe de Grancey received a letter that visibly affected Rosalie, revealing her feelings for Albert. He deduced she was in love with him and discovered that Albert had withdrawn from the election, leaving Rosalie desperate to keep this news secret to protect her sister Sidonie's prospects of marrying him.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

In the morning, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette about Albert Savaron's sudden disappearance. An old gentleman from Paris had visited Savaron, leaving him pale and agitated. Following their meeting, Savaron quickly arranged for a journey, leaving behind his keys and instructing his servant to find another job. His departure caused a stir in Besançon, leading to the election of Monsieur de Chavoncourt.

As time passed, most forgot Albert, except for a few deeply affected by his absence. Rosalie, aware of the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo's death, grew increasingly troubled. The Baroness de Watteville suggested Rosalie marry Monsieur de Soulas, but Rosalie adamantly refused, insisting she was content as she was.

The tension between Rosalie and her mother escalated during a summer at les Rouxey, where Rosalie's refusal to marry became public knowledge. After Mariette and Jerome's marriage, the Baron improved les Rouxey, which Rosalie took pride in, further aggravating her mother.

Tragedy struck when the Baron drowned while retrieving a shell from the lake. His death, attributed to Rosalie's defiance, deepened the rift between mother and daughter. Grief-stricken, Rosalie felt responsible and contemplated confessing her role in Albert's fate, having intercepted letters between him and the Duchess to manipulate their correspondence.

The Vicar-General, horrified by Rosalie's actions, urged her to seek redemption. Rosalie resolved to write to Albert's friend, Monsieur Leopold Hannequin, to uncover his fate.

Hannequin revealed that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery, seeking peace after the heartbreak caused by the Duchess's marriage. The Vicar-General wrote to Albert, who confirmed his decision to remain in the monastery, forgiving Rosalie and wishing her well.

Years later, Rosalie's mother married Monsieur de Soulas, and Rosalie, now of age, was encouraged to marry as well. Despite her mother's attempts to control her, Rosalie remained resolute in her feelings for Albert. She confronted the Duchess at a ball, revealing the truth about Albert's letters and their tragic separation. The encounter left the Duchess shaken, and Rosalie returned to Besançon, feeling a sense of closure.

Ultimately, Rosalie's journey was marked by loss, regret, and a quest for redemption as she navigated love, family, and the consequences of her actions.
