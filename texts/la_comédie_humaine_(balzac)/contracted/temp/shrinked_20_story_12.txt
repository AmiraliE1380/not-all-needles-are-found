During the Restoration one of the few drawing-rooms frequented by the Archbishop of Besançon was Baronne de Watteville’s, because of her devoutness.

Monsieur de Watteville, a descendant of the infamous Watteville, was gentle and unassuming. After a quiet life in La Franche-Comté he married Mademoiselle de Rupt, heiress of the Rupt family; the match was arranged in 1802 and formalized in 1815 after the second Restoration. Following her grandparents’ deaths, Madame de Watteville grew more devout, adopting a pious life that influenced Besançon’s upper circles.

Monsieur de Watteville, thin and plain in appearance, occupied himself as a turner and collector of local geological specimens. Observers detected a certain nobility of soul in him: realizing early he would not dominate his wife, he threw himself into work and hobbies.

The Rupt residence reflected both families’ standing—grand rooms, elegant furnishings, and fine cuisine—with Monsieur sometimes acting as his own butler. Madame de Watteville’s fortune was considerable; her husband’s estate was modest. Their intimacy with the Archbishop attracted several notable abbés to their home.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834 guests received Monsieur l’Abbé de Grancey, who announced a favorable verdict in a lawsuit. The Vicar-General said the legal success had been unexpected given the political climate and credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron, for the decisive role. Curious, Madame de Watteville asked about Savaron’s origins; the Vicar-General replied he lived nearby but his background was unknown, which piqued the guests’ interest. Amedée de Soulas, present that evening, would later prove significant to the story that followed.

In all ages France and England have exchanged cultural influences, especially in language and fashion, often skirting customs. What is "English" in Paris is "French" in London, reflecting mutual borrowing. For example, the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli for a French play, and hoop skirts, first derided, became a dominant European fashion for decades. By 1815 English long waists were mocked in Paris, yet French styles soon shifted in response.

More recently England supplied French with terms like "dandy" and "lion." The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset, sparked a trend of stylish young men in Paris that spread to the provinces. In 1834 Besançon had its own "lion" in Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, a young man of Spanish descent who felt ill at ease in the dull, military town. He lived modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment but maintained an ostentatious appearance, keeping a young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger." Amedee dressed Babylas fashionably and lavished expense on his own wardrobe while subsisting on scant income.

Though seen as a spendthrift, Amedee was adept at economizing, often dining out to avoid household costs. His costly grooming and clothes were a façade aimed at impressing the local aristocracy and winning Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a wealthy heiress. His travels and Parisian knowledge helped him cultivate a reputation as well-informed despite financial strain.

Besançon was conservative and resistant to change; the local aristocracy, "the Colony," kept strict social hierarchies. Amedee’s unusual fashions—like wearing trouser-straps—set him apart and bolstered his status as a "lion." The town’s nobles wished to appear modern to visiting Parisians, and Amedee enabled that image.

Amedee’s chief ambition was to marry Rosalie, who had been raised in a strict, religious household with little contact with the wider world. At eighteen she was modest and unassuming, educated under her mother’s watchful eye and instructed more in domestic skills than social polish. Sheltered from modern influences, she lacked awareness of the social expectations surrounding courtship.

Despite a passive exterior, Rosalie had a resolute spirit from her lineage. Her mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective and controlling, convinced her daughter’s honor was at stake. This produced tension as Amedee courted Rosalie: maternal jealousy and Rosalie’s uncertainty complicated matters.

As Amedee pursued Rosalie, he navigated Besançon’s social intricacies to secure a favorable match while protecting his reputation. Gossip of a possible engagement circulated among the nobility, but Rosalie’s upbringing and her mother’s influence hindered his efforts. Amedee de Soulas’s life in Besançon thus illustrates the interplay of social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraints as he strives to win Rosalie de Watteville’s heart.

“The name Savaron is well-known,” Mademoiselle de Watteville said; “it’s one of the oldest and richest families in Belgium.”

“He’s a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amedee de Soulas returned. “If he claims the Savaron arms, he must add a bar-sinister. The only remaining member of the Brabant family is a rich heiress, Mademoiselle de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister marks a bastard,” Rosalie put in, “but a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble.”

“Enough, mademoiselle!” the Baroness cut in. “You insisted she learn heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville murmured; “she knows it well.” They urged Monsieur de Soulas to continue.

They moved on to other topics, but the Vicar-General’s portrait of Savaron captivated Rosalie. Albert Savaron presented enigmas: “Albert Savaron de Savarus,” she mused, longing to see him, replaying the Abbe’s words and fixating on his description. “What a fine forehead!” she thought, comparing him in her mind to the men at the table.

“Rosalie, why aren’t you eating?” her mother asked.

“I’m not hungry, mamma,” Rosalie replied, lost in thought about the prelate’s hands. She recalled a lighted window seen from her bed and realized it belonged to him. “I must see him!” she decided.

Albert Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon forgotten amid envy from other lawyers. He remained secluded, avoiding social gatherings, increasing the likelihood of being overlooked as a stranger. Nevertheless, he successfully argued three complex cases at the Commercial Tribunal, gaining four prominent merchants as clients who recognized his legal skill.

On the day the Watteville family inaugurated the Belvedere, Savaron was also launching a new venture. Through quiet connections among local merchants, he was starting a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review, funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each subscribed by his first ten clients, convinced that Besancon needed promotion as a trade hub.

To compete with Strasbourg, Besancon needed to become a center of enlightenment and commerce. Albert’s vision was to challenge the literary dominance of Strasbourg and Dijon and bring light to Eastern France. His ideas appealed to merchants eager to believe in their own enterprise.

Savaron wisely kept his name out of the spotlight, letting his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, handle finances while he retained editorial control and a share of the profits. The Review appealed to various regions, and with a low subscription price it quickly garnered five hundred subscribers.

To avoid offending local pride, Albert invited the Bouchers’ eager son, Alfred, to manage the literary side. Albert maintained control, subtly guiding Alfred’s contributions while letting him think he was gaining experience. The commercial community, pleased with the Review’s success, only had to pay a fraction of their shares. With two hundred more subscribers, the publication would yield dividends for shareholders, while the editor remained unpaid.

After the third issue the Review gained recognition among French publications. Albert began reading other journals at home. The third number featured a tale attributed to him, which sparked discussion in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room, though higher circles dismissed it for liberal views.

Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please Rosalie who had shown him so much affection, subscribed and lent her the four issues already published. That night Rosalie devoured the tale—the first story she had ever read. Given her limited experience, the impact on her was striking. She hoped to find in it the sentiments and perhaps the life of Albert. From the first pages, she became convinced it reflected him, as the critics suspected Albert had drawn from his own life in this confession.

In 1823 two young men set out from Lucerne for a holiday in Switzerland, planning to tour the lake of the Four Cantons. The journey offered striking mountain, river, and meadow views. As they passed the hamlet of Gersau, one friend, Rodolphe, was captivated by a newly built house and a woman at the window and decided to stay while his friend Leopold continued the tour.

Rodolphe and Leopold had studied law together. Leopold was headed to be a notary in Paris; Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a more troubled background. His mother had sacrificed everything for his education, hoping to secure his future. Rodolphe was passionate and sensitive, often driven by desire, in contrast to Leopold’s calm composure.

In Gersau Rodolphe lodged with the couple who rented to the Lovelaces and learned the woman was Fanny Lovelace, who had moved there with her elderly father for his health. They were of modest means; Fanny supported her father by translating and writing. Rodolphe was struck by her beauty and musical talent.

The next day Rodolphe sought permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens, who rented to the Lovelaces. After hesitation he was allowed to explore and noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, whose background seemed unusual. Eager to meet Fanny, he encountered resistance from the protective Bergmanns.

That evening Rodolphe heard Fanny singing and was enchanted. He later encountered her and Gina, but their conversation was interrupted when Rodolphe was attacked and wounded by an unknown assailant. Fanny and Gina aided him and he was taken back to safety.

While he recovered Rodolphe received devastating news: his mother had died. The loss plunged him into deep depression, yet he found solace in Fanny’s companionship. They bonded over shared interests, and Rodolphe confessed his feelings, only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite this, Fanny’s affection for Rodolphe grew and they shared intimate moments.

Fanny later revealed that her husband was not as old as he had seemed and that she had married him under duress. Rodolphe was torn between his feelings and the reality of her marriage. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing he had great potential.

Their relationship deepened, but Rodolphe struggled with jealousy and insecurity, especially when Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past, sent news and eventually arrived. Tito’s presence stirred rivalry and fear in Rodolphe of losing Fanny’s affection.

Despite these challenges their connection remained strong. They shared dreams of freedom and a future together, yet Rodolphe’s ambition, Fanny’s loyalty to her husband, and social constraints loomed, forcing him to confront the tensions between love, aspiration, and the sacrifices required for happiness.

At ten Albert remained absent and impatience grew. Some gentlemen began to play cards; the Abbé, perplexed, went to the window where Rosalie had withdrawn, exclaiming, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville, and Rosalie went into the garden to check Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent.

“Jérôme!” Rosalie called to the servant below. When he confirmed that Albert had left in a post-chaise, the Abbé declared, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” Rosalie’s face betrayed a sudden joy, which the Vicar-General noticed but pretended not to, wondering at her involvement.

Back in the drawing-room Monsieur de Watteville announced the shocking news of Albert’s unexplained departure. By half-past eleven only fifteen guests remained: Madame de Chavoncourt, the Abbé de Godenars, the two Chavoncourt girls, Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbé de Grancey, Rosalie, Amédée de Soulas, and a retired magistrate who had supported Albert’s election. The Abbé stationed himself near the Baroness to observe Rosalie, whose normally pale face now showed agitation.

“What could have happened to Monsieur de Savarus?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. A servant then brought a letter for the Abbé on a silver tray. The Baroness urged him to read it. As he did, he noticed Rosalie turn pale; he recognized the handwriting. He folded the letter, discreetly pocketed it, and their exchanged glances hinted at her feelings.

“She is in love with Albert Savarus!” he concluded. As he prepared to leave, Rosalie stopped him: “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!” When he questioned how she knew the writing, her passionate reply—“I love him! What is the matter?”—revealed her. He told her that Albert was withdrawing from the election. Stunned, Rosalie begged him to keep the news secret, realizing that the election’s end would destroy the prospect of her sister’s marriage to Albert.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
On her way to Mass, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette how Albert had suddenly vanished. An elderly Parisian had visited Monsieur Savaron at the Hotel National; Savaron turned pale at the visitor’s card, they met for over an hour, and the visitor left with a large package. Savaron, distraught, went to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage, ordered post horses, left his keys, and told his servant to seek other work.

Savaron’s abrupt departure shook Besançon; the Prefect quickly granted him a passport. The affair stirred local prejudice against outsiders, and Albert was soon forgotten by most—except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness de Watteville urged Rosalie to marry, noting how much she had changed. The Baron agreed to give Rosalie the family estate, les Rouxey, but the Baroness fretted over finances. A match with Monsieur de Soulas was arranged; Rosalie refused, insisting she was content as she was. Conflict between Rosalie and her mother escalated, and they spent a tense summer at les Rouxey. After Mariette and Jerome wed, the Baron invested in the estate—actions that only increased the Baroness’s resentment. The Vicar-General tried to mediate.

One day Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General that she had sabotaged Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo: she had intercepted letters and caused their separation. The Vicar-General was horrified and urged her to seek forgiveness and face the consequences.

Rosalie’s guilt deepened when a Paris clerk, acting for Albert, came to sell his belongings and revealed that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received a letter in which Albert expressed complete forgiveness for Rosalie and a desire to remain in solitude.

When the Vicar-General conveyed this, he urged Rosalie to reconcile with her mother and accept Monsieur de Soulas. She remained defiant, unwilling to sacrifice her mother’s happiness. The Vicar-General’s death left a void; soon after, Madame de Watteville married Monsieur de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support.

Now of age and financially independent, Rosalie entered Parisian society determined to confront the Duchess. At a ball she publicly revealed her role in Albert’s downfall, leaving the Duchess shaken. Rosalie returned to Besançon having exposed the truth, but the emotional cost weighed heavily—this tale of love, betrayal, and ambition left a lasting impact on all involved.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone on her les Rouxey estate, riding, hunting, and improving the land, making her notable in the Eastern provinces. Madame de Soulas, mother of two, looks younger, while her husband, Monsieur de Soulas, has visibly aged. He remarked to young Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

Mademoiselle de Watteville was known for her eccentric “vagaries.” Each year she visited the walls of the Grande Chartreuse, perhaps recalling a grand-uncle who once broke monastery walls to gain freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon—reportedly to marry, though the true reason remains unclear. She returned after a steamboat boiler explosion on the Loire that cost her the use of her right arm and left leg and scarred her face. Her health deteriorated; in constant pain, she now devotes herself to religious practices at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
