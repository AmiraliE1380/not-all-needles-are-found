One of the few drawing-rooms where the Archbishop of Besançon was seen during the Restoration was that of the Baronne de Watteville, who was closely associated with him because of her devout nature.

Monsieur de Watteville, a descendant of the notorious Watteville, was gentle and unassuming compared with his ancestor. After living quietly in La Franche-Comté, he married the Rupt heiress, Mademoiselle de Rupt, an arranged match of 1802 formalized in 1815 after the second Restoration. Following her grandparents’ deaths, Madame de Watteville grew more devout, and her piety influenced Besançon’s upper circles.

Thin and unremarkable, Monsieur de Watteville passed his days as a turner and collector of local geological specimens, especially fossils. Some perceived a nobility in him: recognizing he would never dominate his wife, he devoted himself to work and hobbies.

The Rupt residence was grand, reflecting both families’ status, with elegant furnishings and ceremonious meals—often served with such formality that Monsieur de Watteville frequently acted as his own butler. Madame’s wealth was considerable; her husband’s estate, modest. Their close ties with the Archbishop drew several notable abbés to their home.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834, guests warmly received Monsieur l’Abbé de Grancey, who announced a favorable verdict in a lawsuit over family property. The Vicar-General, calling the victory unexpected in the present political climate, credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron. When Madame de Watteville inquired about him, she was told he lived nearby but that his precise origins were unknown—an ambiguity that intensified interest. Amedee de Soulas, present that evening, would later prove significant to what followed.

In all ages France and England have exchanged cultural influences, especially in language and fashion, often bypassing customs. The label "English" in Paris is "French" in London, reflecting mutual influence: the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli for a French play, and hoop skirts, once ridiculed, became dominant across Europe. By 1815 English long waists were mocked in Paris, yet French styles rapidly evolved. England contributed terms to French usage, such as "dandy" and "lion," the latter popularized by Alfred de Musset and sparking a vogue for stylish young men in Paris that spread to the provinces.

In 1834 Besançon had its own "lion" in Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, a young man of Spanish descent who felt out of place in the dull military town. He lived modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment but maintained a lavish appearance, employing a young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger." Amedee dressed Babylas fashionably and spent extravagantly on his wardrobe while surviving on limited income. Though seen as a spendthrift, he was adept at budgeting—often dining out to avoid household costs—and his expensive grooming and clothes were a deliberate façade to impress the local aristocracy and win the favor of Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a wealthy heiress. His travels and knowledge of Parisian culture gave him an air of worldliness despite financial strain.

Besançon was conservative and resistant to change; the local aristocracy—called "the Colony"—maintained strict hierarchies. Amedee’s distinctive fashion, even details like trouser-straps, set him apart and reinforced his status as a "lion." The town’s elite, eager to seem modern before visiting Parisians, welcomed his cosmopolitan touch.

Amedee aimed to marry Rosalie, who had been raised in a strict religious household with little exposure to the wider world. At eighteen she was modest and unassuming, trained in domestic skills rather than social graces, and sheltered by her mother’s watchful eye. Despite a passive demeanor, Rosalie possessed a strong spirit inherited from her family; her mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective and controlling, convinced her daughter’s honor was at stake. That maternal strictness caused Rosalie to suppress feelings, creating a complex relationship marked by jealousy and uncertainty.

As Amedee courted Rosalie he navigated Besançon’s social intricacies, seeking a favorable marriage while preserving his reputation. The town’s elite found him charming and engagement rumors circulated, yet Rosalie’s upbringing and her mother’s influence complicated his efforts, since she remained largely unaware of the romantic expectations around her. Amedee’s life in Besançon thus exemplifies the interplay of social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraints as he strives to win Rosalie amid rigid social structures.

“Madame,” Monsieur de Soulas told the Baroness as he waited for his soup to cool, “one fine morning a gentleman from Paris arrived at the Hotel National and settled on the first floor of Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron. He registered at the Mairie and had his name, ALBERT SAVARON, entered on the list of barristers at the Court, leaving his card with local officials.”

“The Savaron name is famous,” Mademoiselle de Watteville observed, “one of the oldest and richest in Belgium.”

“He’s a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amedee de Soulas returned. “If he wants the Savaron arms, he must add a bar-sinister. The only remaining Brabant is a rich heiress, Mademoiselle de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister denotes a bastard, but even a bastard of a Comte de Savarus remains noble,” Rosalie chimed in.

“Enough, that will do!” the Baroness cut across.

“You insisted she learn heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville noted. “So she knows it well.”

“Please go on, Monsieur de Soulas.”

“In Besancon, where everyone is catalogued, Albert Savaron was received without fuss by our lawyers. They wondered who sent him and what he aimed at—especially as he sent cards to judges rather than calling. Three days later he was forgotten. He hired Jerome, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who could cook a little. Savaron remained overlooked because he kept to himself.”

“Doesn’t he go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends the early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He rises between one and two in the morning, works until eight for breakfast, walks in the garden—fifty or sixty turns—dines, and is in bed between six and seven.”

“How do you know all this?” Madame de Chavoncourt pressed.

“I live on Rue Neuve, overlooking his house, and my servant communicates with Jerome.”

“And you gossip with Babylas? What else to do while riding?” Monsieur de Soulas shrugged.

“How did you come to hire such a stranger?” the Baroness asked the Vicar-General.

“The President of the Court assigned him to defend a half-witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man innocent, exposed the real culprits, and they were arrested. His defense impressed the Court and jury. A merchant then entrusted him with a difficult case, which he also won. Monsieur Berryer advised us to employ Savaron. On meeting him I felt confidence—and I was right.”

“Is he truly extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” replied the Vicar-General.

“Tell us more,” urged Madame de Watteville.

“The first time I met him,” the Abbe de Grancey said, “he received me in an outer room adorned in old oak and lined with law books. It was simply furnished: an old writing-table, six armchairs, gray curtains, and a green carpet. I did not expect him to be young. The setting matched his appearance: a black dressing gown with a red cord, red slippers, waistcoat, and a red smoking-cap.”

“The devil’s colors!” Madame de Watteville exclaimed.

“Yes,” the Abbe continued, “but he has a magnificent head. Thick, curly black hair streaked with gray; a round white throat; a splendid, deeply lined forehead; an olive complexion; a square nose; and fiery eyes. He combines passion with a calm, sweet voice that can thunder when necessary. He is of average height, neither stout nor thin, with hands like a prelate.”

“The second time I visited, he received me in his bedroom, shabby. The third, he was having a frugal breakfast in his library. Observing him more closely, I saw he was not ordinary; behind that terrible yet gentle face seemed a heavy burden.”

“Why would so eloquent a man leave Paris for Besancon?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. “Doesn’t he know how little chance a stranger has here?”

“After studying his head and hearing him answer a prominent Parisian lawyer,” the Abbe replied, “I believe he will one day make a great mark.”

“Why discuss him? You’ve won and paid him,” Madame de Watteville said, glancing at her daughter, who listened raptly.

The Vicar-General’s portrait of Savaron entranced Rosalie. For the first time she encountered someone exceptional, in stark contrast to the banal Count who had courted her. Savaron presented enigmas to unravel: the fine forehead, the dreadful but gentle face, the eloquence. She replayed the Abbe’s description, scrutinizing the men present and finding none comparable.

“Rosalie, why aren’t you eating?” her mother asked.

“I’m not hungry, mamma,” she answered, lost in thoughts of the prelate’s hands. She remembered having seen a lighted window from her bed and realized it belonged to him. “I must see him!”

“Is the Chapter’s lawsuit settled?” she asked Monsieur de Grancey. Her mother, sensing Rosalie’s unusual interest, dismissed the question. The Abbe’s reply—perhaps the case might go to the Court of Appeal—failed to dampen Rosalie’s absorption.

After dinner she listened for any mention of Savaron but found none beyond congratulations to the Abbe. Eager for bedtime, she resolved to rise between two and three to glimpse Albert’s windows. When the hour arrived she thrilled at the candlelight through the trees, saw him writing, and imagined the color of his furniture. “While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought.

This episode raises the broader problem of educating girls: whether to inform them fully or restrain their minds. The University of France has long avoided the question. The religious system tends to restrain thought; too much enlightenment makes maturity premature, too little can cause sudden outbursts—recall Moliere’s Agnes. For Rosalie, the Abbe’s portrait of Savaron ignited such an outbreak.

Next morning, dressing, Rosalie looked out to see Albert walking in the hotel garden beside the Hotel de Rupt. “What if he lived elsewhere? At least I can see him here. What is he thinking?” Determined, she resolved to enter his house, discover his mystery, hear his voice, and glimpse his eyes.

All day she embroidered with the narrowed attention of one who seems idle but is deeply thoughtful. She arranged to go to confession at half-past seven the next morning, hoping to encounter Albert after mass. She visited her father in his workshop and suggested he carve larger pieces and build a grotto in the garden to show his work. He delighted in the idea but she cautioned him not to tell her mother.

Rosalie pictured an observatory from which she could see Albert’s private room. Girls often contrive far for the sake of a man.

The eagerly awaited Sunday arrived. Rosalie dressed with such care that her maid Mariette noticed her fidgetiness. Her heart raced as she crossed the courtyard; she had never walked the streets thus, feeling her mother could read and forbid her thoughts. She arrived at church before mass, prayed briefly, and checked that Abbe Giroud was in his confessional, taking a place to see Albert enter.

No man seemed unattractive to her in that heightened state. When Albert Savaron appeared, strikingly handsome, his solemn demeanor and deep, intense gaze confirmed the Vicar-General’s portrait. Those eagle-like eyes suggested hidden ardor. Boldly, she stood in his path to exchange glances: a warmth stirred within her.

Albert sat during the service; Rosalie sought a place to watch him during her confession. By the time she emerged, mass had ended and he had gone. “The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besancon? I must know everything!” She wore innocence outwardly and redirected her energies into the scheme for the Belvedere.

“Mamma,” she said one day, “my father has a unique idea for a Belvedere in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of everything your father does,” Madame de Watteville said dryly. “Why object to something that amuses him?”

“Because from there we’ll see into Monsieur de Soulas’ rooms, and he’ll see us. Perhaps remarks will be made—”

“Do you presume to instruct your parents, Rosalie, as though you know more about life and propriety than we do?”

“I’ll say no more, mamma. Besides, my father mentioned a cool room in the grotto where we can take coffee.”

Madame de Watteville inspected the columns and fully approved. She chose a garden spot that could not be seen from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows but would afford views into Albert Savaron’s rooms. A builder was hired to construct a grotto with a path lined in periwinkles, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper. The interior would be rustic, with a mirror, ottoman, and table of inlaid bark. Monsieur de Soulas proposed an asphalt floor; Rosalie suggested a rustic chandelier carved by her father.

“The Wattevilles are building something lovely,” rumors spread in Besancon. “They’re wealthy and can afford such whims—” “A thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed. “Yes, a thousand crowns,” young Monsieur de Soulas confirmed. “A man from Paris is coming to rusticate the interior; it will be very pretty. Monsieur de Watteville is carving the chandelier himself.” An Abbe added that Berquet was making a cellar beneath it; young Monsieur de Soulas corrected him: the kiosk was on a concrete foundation to prevent damp. Madame de Chavoncourt, sour, glanced at her daughter waiting to marry. Mademoiselle de Watteville glowed with pride at the project’s success; no one suspected that the girl deemed a witless goose had devised it to get a closer look at Albert Savaron’s study.

Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon overshadowed by envy among lawyers. He kept to himself, avoided social gatherings, and remained at risk of being overlooked. Yet he successfully argued three complex cases at the Commercial Tribunal, gaining four prominent merchants as clients who recognized his skill.

On the day the Watteville family inaugurated their Belvedere, Savaron was launching another enterprise. Using quiet connections among local merchants, he founded a fortnightly paper—the Eastern Review—funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each subscribed by his first ten clients, convinced of the need to promote Besancon’s trade interests.

To compete with Strasbourg, Besancon needed to become a center of enlightenment and commerce. Albert’s vision was to challenge the literary dominance of Strasbourg and Dijon and bring light to Eastern France. His ideas resonated with merchants who believed in their own civic and commercial potential.

Savaron prudently kept his name discreet, allowing his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, to handle finances while he retained editorial control and a share of the profits. The Review appealed across regions with a low subscription price and quickly gained five hundred subscribers.

To avoid offending local pride, Albert invited the Bouchers’ eager son, Alfred, to manage the literary side. Albert guided the content subtly, letting Alfred think himself the manager. The commercial community, pleased with the Review’s success, needed to pay only a fraction of their shares. With two hundred more subscribers, the publication would yield dividends to shareholders, while the editor remained unpaid.

After the third issue the Review was noticed among French publications. Albert began reading other reviews at home. The third number contained a tale attributed to him, which stirred talk in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room despite higher circles dismissing it for its liberal views.

“Papa,” Rosalie said, “there’s a Review published in Besancon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room, for mamma wouldn’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.”

Eager to please Rosalie, who had shown him much affection, Monsieur de Watteville subscribed to the Eastern Review and lent her the four issues published so far. That night Rosalie devoured the tale—the first she had ever read. Given her limited reading experience, the work’s effect on her was intense. She hoped to find in it the sentiments and perhaps the life of Albert. From the first pages she believed it reflected his experiences; critics remarked that Albert had drawn from his own life in this confession.

In 1823 two young men left Lucerne for a holiday in Switzerland, intending to tour the Lake of the Four Cantons. The journey showed striking mountain, river, and meadow views. Passing the hamlet of Gersau, one friend, Rodolphe, was captivated by a newly built house and a woman at the window and decided to stay; his friend Leopold continued the trip.

Rodolphe and Leopold had studied law together: Leopold was bound for a notary’s post in Paris, while Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a more unsettled background. His mother had sacrificed everything to secure his education, hoping to improve his prospects. Passionate and sensitive, Rodolphe was often led by desire, a contrast to Leopold’s calm demeanor.

In Gersau Rodolphe lodged with a couple who took him in. He learned the woman at the window was Fanny Lovelace, who had moved there with her elderly father for his health. They were modestly off, and Fanny supported her father by translating and writing. Rodolphe was struck by her beauty and musical talent.

The next day he sought permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens; the Bergmanns rented land to the Lovelaces and were initially hesitant but allowed him to explore. There he noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, who suggested a distinctive past. Eager to meet Fanny, Rodolphe encountered resistance from the protective Bergmanns.

That evening he heard Fanny singing and was enchanted. He later met Fanny and Gina in person, but their conversation was cut short when an unknown assailant attacked and wounded him. Fanny and Gina aided him and he was taken to safety.

While recovering, Rodolphe received devastating news: his mother had died. The loss plunged him into deep depression, but he found solace in Fanny’s company. They bonded over shared interests, and Rodolphe confessed his feelings, only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite this, Fanny’s affection for Rodolphe grew and they shared intimate moments.

Fanny disclosed that her husband was not as old as he appeared and that she had married him under duress. Rodolphe was torn between his love for her and the reality of her marriage. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential.

Their relationship deepened, yet Rodolphe struggled with jealousy and insecurity, particularly when news arrived from Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past. Tito’s appearance provoked rivalry in Rodolphe, who feared losing her affection.

Despite these challenges, Rodolphe and Fanny’s connection remained strong. They shared dreams of freedom and a future together, but the complexities of their circumstances—Rodolphe’s ambition and Fanny’s loyalty to her husband—created persistent tension. As they navigated their feelings, Rodolphe wrestled with the demands of love, ambition, and the sacrifices required for happiness. The beauty of their surroundings mirrored the intensity of their emotions, yet the realities of their lives threatened to overshadow their dreams.

For twenty days Rodolphe stayed at the Bergmanns’, watching Francesca. He observed in her a youthful imprudence and an unbroken spirit: at times resisting love, at times yielding. The old man treated her like a father; her gratitude awakened a native nobility that intrigued Rodolphe and made her an enigma.

Those days mixed secret joys and melancholy, with quarrels as delightful as their reconciliations. Rodolphe grew increasingly captivated by Francesca’s steady tenderness and became often jealously exacting over trifles.

One evening, when Francesca said she wanted to leave Gersau because it lacked luxury, Rodolphe asked if she cared for luxury. She answered passionately, linking her taste for luxury to a love of art and beauty, and insisted she had never complained through hardships. Playfully, she argued it was natural for ordinary people to aspire to greatness, revealing her charming character.

Then she insisted Rodolphe not accompany them to Geneva, fearing town gossip and wanting to protect her husband’s reputation. She affirmed her love for Rodolphe but demanded it remain concealed. She kissed him and left him astonished.

The next day the Bergmanns’ lodgers departed early; restless, Rodolphe went to Vevay and then to Geneva by the end of October, taking a longer route. To avoid the town’s discomforts he rented a room in Eaux-Vives. He inquired after Italian refugees, especially a bookseller named Lamporani, but the landlord said the only newcomers were Prince and Princess Colonna and Prince and Princess Gandolphini.

Rodolphe’s first walk took him to the Villa Diodati, where the beauty of the place struck him. Approaching, he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage with an old lady and was dizzy with surprise. Learning they were at the Colonna residence, a veil seemed to lift: the implications of their past interactions became clear. He feared he had been a mere whim, struggling with the reality of her noble standing.

He sent his card to Prince Gandolphini and was received by a false Lamporani, who invited him to a concert that evening. Though curious, Rodolphe refrained from requesting a private audience with the Princess and returned to Eaux-Vives with mingled anticipation and anxiety.

As evening approached his passion deepened and he felt compelled to elevate his social position to match hers. When he finally saw her at the concert she was dazzling; their eyes met with immediate recognition. Francesca’s beauty and grace captivated him and he felt a profound connection, convinced she had remained faithful to their past despite her rank.

After the concert Rodolphe was introduced to the Colonnas and to Francesca, who playfully styled herself “Princess Rodolphini.” The jest revived memories of their time in Gersau; he was overwhelmed by the beauty of her voice and the intimacy of their proximity.

In the ensuing days Francesca perceived Rodolphe’s deep love, which for her felt natural. She said their affection should be faithful and enduring, whatever the distance. Rodolphe, however, was driven by ambition; he wanted to achieve greatness so Francesca could be proud of him.

Francesca combined gaiety and tenderness. Once destined for a cloister, family misfortunes had compelled her to marry Prince Gandolphini to preserve her family’s standing. Yet despite noble birth she favored independence and liberal, even revolutionary, ideas.

Rodolphe spent the winter in Geneva where time flew in Francesca’s intelligent and charming company. But with spring came inner turmoil: Francesca’s calmness contrasted with his unrest; he longed for more visible feeling. One day on the terrace he voiced a bitter complaint about the happiness of others, singling out a woman of noble lineage; his words wounded Francesca and she withdrew. Rodolphe followed and found her in tears. In a moment of passion he kissed her, but she repelled him and asserted her dignity.

Francesca then ordered him to leave, declaring they would not meet again until Naples. Her command was firm; Rodolphe, recognizing the strength of her will, complied.

On his return to Paris, Rodolphe discovered a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner, an artist who had refused many. Rodolphe doubted the Prince could persuade him, but Francesca charmed Schinner into painting an original for Rodolphe and a duplicate for Emilio. He turned to politics but went to Naples first to recuperate at Villa Gandolphini, where the Prince and Princess had been restored to their posts. The three-month respite restored his hope.

He tried to rebuild his fortune. His talents were noticed and a high post promised for his dedication. But the July 1830 storm dashed his ambitions again.

Only Francesca and God witnessed his brave efforts. This young man, endowed with fine qualities but ill-fated, was sustained by love and returned to new challenges, guided by a faithful presence and devoted heart.

As Mademoiselle de Watteville concluded her narrative, Rosalie's cheeks burned and tears flowed. The novel—popular in style and her first encounter with a love story told through a man's experience—stirred intense feeling. Her agitation sprang from jealousy of Francesca Colonna, whom she believed to be Albert's beloved.

Consumed by curiosity and a fierce desire to win Albert, Rosalie battled insecurities about her looks and lack of musical talent, fearing he would never love her. That night her imagination ran through the few schemes available to a young woman raised under strict rules: sneaking into Albert's garden, writing to him, or contriving a visit tied to her father's land dispute to bring him to her drawing-room.

Watching Albert's window, she saw something that might reveal his secrets: Jerome, Albert's servant, being helped over the wall by Mariette, the maid. Rosalie noted Mariette's plainness but also her steadiness and means, which explained Jerome's interest. The sight taught Rosalie a dangerous lesson: her mother's rigid upbringing could be undermined by a servant's choices.

The next morning Rosalie accompanied Mariette to Mass and seized the chance to question her about Jerome. She confronted Mariette about their secret meetings and, surprisingly, offered support in return for access to the letters Albert sent through Jerome. Relieved, Mariette agreed.

Rosalie's obsession deepened as she read a letter from Albert to his friend Leopold that revealed his noble lineage, past struggles, ambitions, and devotion. She became enthralled by his striving for success and the complexity of his character—especially his devotion to the woman he loved, Francesca.

As she continued to read Albert's correspondence, Rosalie grew acutely aware of her inadequacy and the moral implications of spying. She wrestled with guilt over invading his privacy but rationalized it as proof of love. Her passion slid into dangerous infatuation, prompting penances and self-imposed suffering meant to demonstrate her devotion.

One evening Mariette brought another letter intended for Francesca, plunging Rosalie into jealousy and doubt. She hesitated but yielded to the urge to read it. The letter confirmed Albert's enduring love for Francesca and outlined his hopes for a shared future.

Rosalie's heart ached as she absorbed the depth of his feeling for another. Torn between admiration for their fidelity and her own longing, she experienced a moment of clarity: she chose to step back, recognizing the nobility of their bond and acknowledging her shortcomings. Preparing to return the letter with Mariette, Rosalie reflected on the nature of love, feeling sorrow and resignation. She understood that true love often requires sacrifice, and, though she longed for the devotion Albert and Francesca shared, she came to terms with her unrequited affection.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil; by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. Alone, that struggle fed a dark passion and drove her to devise numerous plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist; her story is a cautionary tale.

During that winter Albert de Savarus made important advances in Besançon as he anticipated the Chamber’s dissolution. He secured the backing of a wealthy contractor influential in the moderate party. The Romans had once supplied town water, and Besançon drew water from Arcier, a distant hill. Restoring an aqueduct to access Roman-quality water struck many as absurd where the Doubs already served the town, but convincing citizens would trigger large expenditures benefiting the contractor.

Savarus argued the river water was unfit and promoted Arcier water. Articles in the local Review echoed him, and townspeople across political lines took up the idea of drinking like the Romans. The issue became a focus for hidden interests: opponents were dismissed as old-fashioned while Savarus’s projects dominated discussion. After eighteen months he had stirred the stagnant town and gained influence without popularity.

Throughout the winter he won several lawsuits for local priests; his ambition consumed him. His apparent indifference to money masked a form of moral usury—he expected a far greater reward. In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with funds from Léopold Hannequin, which also qualified him to stand for election. The Abbé de Grancey, who saw Savarus’s ambitions clearly, called him remarkable and suggested he’d taken a wrong turn in life.

Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hôtel de Rupt, at first content to observe him from afar. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, produced ten thousand francs a year but was poorly managed by the Baron, who left oversight to an old servant, Modinier. The Baron’s indifference sprang from his wife’s substantial income; the family often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque spot shaped by the famous Watteville.

Watteville had built a dam and lake, enhancing the area, but his heirs maintained a dubious claim over the village of Riceys, long enjoying rights to the land. The Baron’s death left a legacy of usurpation which his descendants continued to assert.

Rosalie’s efforts were subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey; the lake’s beauty captivated her and she associated it with love. On a boat trip her father confided legal troubles with Riceys over land rights. Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus, stressing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for the estate. She argued a lawsuit would enliven their lives and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey. Her determination to secure her future there was clear: she envisioned creating beautiful gardens.

Four days later the Abbé de Grancey visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing such an alignment would compromise his supposed neutrality. The Abbé pressed him to consider the political advantage of supporting the Wattevilles, especially given potential votes.

Rosalie, meanwhile, was resolved to keep Savarus in Besançon and prevent his candidacy from succeeding. She wrote to the préfet, revealing Savarus’s ambitions, but the préfet disregarded her. Rosalie understood the political landscape and sought to manipulate it to her advantage.

As the election approached, a meeting of influential men in Besançon decided to nominate Savarus as a candidate, much to his surprise; he had been initially unaware of the machinations behind him. The Abbé, who had been watching, warned Savarus of potential betrayals and advised transparency about his past.

Savarus delivered a passionate speech outlining his vision and ambitions, captivating his audience. Yet he faced problems: the préfet worked to undermine his support and his voter base began to erode. Despite his efforts, the political tide shifted against him and he struggled to maintain his position.

On the day of the first vote the results were disappointing; Savarus felt the weight of his ambitions and the pressure of the election. He returned home in despair, reflecting on his love for Francesca and the uncertainty of his future.

Just as hope faded, the Abbé de Grancey arrived with encouraging news. He had worked behind the scenes to secure support for Savarus, urging him to visit the Wattevilles and promising their case would be resolved in time for the election. The Abbé revealed that winning the Rouxey lawsuit could secure crucial votes and even suggested Savarus pursue a marriage with Sidonie de Chavoncourt to solidify his standing. Savarus was initially resistant but soon saw the potential benefits.

The Abbé’s guidance proved invaluable; Savarus prepared to navigate the intertwined complexities of love and politics, realizing his future depended on choices he would soon have to make.

By nine the next evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were filled with Besançon’s aristocracy, gathered over the unusual decision to go to the polls for the daughter of the Rupts. The former Master of Appeals, secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch, was to be presented. Madame de Chavoncourt had come with her younger daughter Sidonie, elegantly dressed; her elder sister, confident in her relationship, had made no effort with her appearance—an omission noted in small towns. The Abbe de Grancey moved among the groups, listening and making incisive remarks that steered the conversation.

“If the Elder Branch returns,” he asked an elderly statesman, “what politicians would they find?” The statesman answered that Berryer, isolated on his bench, was uncertain and might disrupt the Government if supported. They spoke of the Duc de Fitz-James in Toulouse and how a vote for Monsieur Savarus could align Republicans with them rather than with the Moderates.

By nine-thirty Albert had not arrived, and Madame de Watteville found his lateness disrespectful. Madame de Chavoncourt advised not to let trifles affect serious issues, suggesting Albert might be delayed by a consultation. Rosalie, watching her mother and Madame de Chavoncourt, noticed her leniency toward Albert and hinted at a possible marriage between Sidonie and him.

At ten Albert’s absence provoked frustration; some gentlemen began playing cards. The Abbe de Grancey, perplexed, went to a window where Rosalie stood and exclaimed, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville and Rosalie went into the garden to check Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent.

“Jérôme!” Rosalie called. When he confirmed Albert had left in a post-chaise, the Abbe cried, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” Rosalie’s expression betrayed joy, which the Vicar-General noticed but pretended to ignore, wondering about her involvement.

Back in the drawing-room, Monsieur de Watteville announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By half-past eleven only fifteen guests remained: Madame de Chavoncourt, the Abbe de Godenars, the two Chavoncourt girls, Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbe de Grancey, Rosalie, Amedee de Soulas, a retired magistrate who had supported Albert’s election, and others. The Abbe de Grancey stationed himself near the Baroness to observe Rosalie, whose usually pale face now showed agitation.

“What could have happened to Monsieur de Savarus?” Madame de Chavoncourt wondered aloud. At that moment a servant brought a letter for the Abbe de Grancey on a silver tray. The Baroness urged him to read it. As he did he saw Rosalie turn pale and recognized the handwriting. He folded the letter and discreetly pocketed it, after exchanging a glance with Rosalie that hinted at her feelings.

“She is in love with Albert Savarus!” he concluded. As he prepared to leave, Rosalie intercepted him. “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!” she cried. When he asked how she knew the handwriting, her passionate reply revealed her feelings: “I love him! What is the matter?” He told her that Albert was withdrawing from the election. Stunned, Rosalie begged him to keep the news secret, realizing that if the election ended so too would the prospect of her sister’s marriage to Albert.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
One morning on her way to Mass, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette of Albert’s sudden disappearance. An elderly Parisian had arrived at the Hotel National and called on Monsieur Savaron. The lawyer went pale at the card; they met for over an hour. The visitor left with a large package, and Savaron, distraught, went to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage, ordered post-horses, left his keys, and told his servant to seek other employment.

Savaron’s flight the next day caused a stir in Besançon; the Prefect promptly granted him a passport. The affair fed local prejudice against outsiders, and Albert was soon forgotten by most — except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness de Watteville pressed Rosalie to marry, noting how much she had changed. The Baron offered Rosalie the family estate, les Rouxey, but his wife fretted over finances and arranged a match with Monsieur de Soulas. Rosalie firmly refused, insisting she was content. The dispute escalated, and a summer at les Rouxey made her refusal public. After Mariette and Jérôme married, the Baron improved the estate, provoking the Baroness’s resentment. The Vicar-General attempted to mediate.

One day Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General that she had interfered in Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo: she had intercepted letters and caused their separation. The Vicar-General was horrified, stressing the moral gravity of her deception and urging her to seek forgiveness and recognize the consequences she had set in motion.

Rosalie’s guilt deepened when a Paris clerk came to sell Albert’s effects and revealed that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received a letter from Albert expressing complete forgiveness for Rosalie and his wish to remain in solitude.

When the Vicar-General shared Albert’s letter, he urged Rosalie to reconcile with her mother and accept Monsieur de Soulas. She remained defiant, unwilling to sacrifice her mother’s happiness. The Vicar-General’s death left a void; soon after, Madame de Watteville married Monsieur de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support. Now of age, Rosalie encouraged the marriage and thus secured her independence.

With a substantial fortune she entered Parisian society intent on confronting the Duchess d’Argaiolo. At a ball she publicly revealed her role in Albert’s downfall, leaving the Duchess shaken. Rosalie returned to Besançon, having exposed the truth, but the emotional toll of her actions weighed heavily. The tale of love, betrayal, and the consequences of ambition left a lasting impact on all involved.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone at les Rouxey, known in Eastern provinces for riding, hunting, and improving her land. Madame de Soulas, mother of two, seems younger; her husband, Monsieur de Soulas, had grown old and told young Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

She was famed for eccentricities. Each year she visited the Grande Chartreuse, perhaps following a grand-uncle who once broke out of a monastery. In 1841 she left Besançon, supposedly to marry, though reasons were unclear. She returned after a Loire steamboat boiler explosion that cost her a right arm and a left leg and disfigured her. Her health declined; in constant pain she now lives in religious observance at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
