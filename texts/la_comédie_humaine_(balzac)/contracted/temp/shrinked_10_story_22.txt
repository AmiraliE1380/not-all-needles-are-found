EUGENIE GRANDET

I

Some provincial houses wear a melancholy like cloisters or ruins; silence and barrenness prevail until a pale, motionless face appears at a window. Such was a dwelling in Saumur at the top of the steep street toward the chateau. This little-frequented, narrow, tortuous lane, echoing under its pebbles, is lined with three-century-old wooden houses, their carved ends blackened by time. Slate-covered beams trace bluish lines along frail walls; rotten shingles sag; blackened window-sills with faint sculpture hold clay pots. Doors studded with enormous nails bear domestic hieroglyphics—marks of Protestantism, Leaguer curses, or trades—the history of France written in wood.

Next to a tottering artisan’s house rose a country gentleman’s mansion with battered armorial bearings. In this hilly street the ground-floors are neither shops nor warehouses but the simple ouvroirs of another age: low, deep, dark rooms without show-windows. Doors open in two parts; the upper half is fastened back while the lower, fitted with a spring-bell, swings continually. Light and air enter through the upper door or an open space between ceiling and low front wall, closed each evening by solid shutters and iron bars.

This wall serves as a counter displaying mere samples—tubs of cod and salt, bundles of sail-cloth, cordage, copper wire, iron hoops, a few cloth pieces. Inside a neat girl in a white kerchief calls her father or mother, who sells what you want, phlegmatic, civil, or arrogant. A cooper may seem to own little beyond boat-ribs and laths, yet his wood-yard below supplies the cooperage of Anjou; a hot season makes him rich, a rainy one ruins him. Here, as in Touraine, weather controls commerce: wine-growers, proprietors, wood-merchants, coopers, inn-keepers, mariners—all watch the sun and dread frost, rain, drought. The barometer smooths, saddens, or brightens their faces; from end to end of this street men call out its signs: “Here’s golden weather,” or “It rains louis,” knowing what sun or rain will bring.

On Saturdays these traders, with vineyards and provisions arranged, close shop and spend two days in the country. Purchases and profits settled, they have hours for pleasure, observation, criticism, and spying. Neighbors remark on every partridge or pot; a girl peeking at a window is seen by idling groups. Consciences live in sunlight; the dark, silent houses hide no mysteries. Life is mostly at door thresholds where households breakfast, dine, quarrel, and examine every passer-by. Once strangers were bantered from door to door—origin of many stories and the nickname copieux for Angers folk famed for such urban sarcasm.

The ancient mansions of Saumur’s old town crown this street; the melancholy dwelling at its top is one of those venerable relics. Follow the thoroughfare to the dark recess hiding the door of Monsieur Grandet’s house. To explain why the town calls it by his name, we must relate Monsieur Grandet’s biography.

Grandet’s provincial reputation is best understood locally. In 1789, still called le Père Grandet, he was a master-cooper who could read, write, and cipher. When the Republic sold church property in the arrondissement, this forty-year-old cooper had just married the daughter of a rich wood-merchant. With about two thousand louis-d’or—his and his wife’s dowry—and help from two hundred double louis given by his father-in-law to the republican presiding over sales, Grandet bought, cheaply, one of the finest vineyards, an old abbey, and several farms. The inhabitants, not very revolutionary, admired his boldness and patriotism; in truth his thought was open only to vineyards. Appointed to administrate Saumur, his influence proved pacific and commercial: he protected former nobles when possible and supplied Republican armies with puncheons of white wine, taking payment in splendid fields then reserved for sale.

Under the Consulate Grandet became mayor, governed wisely, and reaped better pickings. Under the Empire he was called Monsieur Grandet; Napoleon supplanted him in office but he left without regret. He had built roads to his properties; his house and lands, assessed advantageously, paid moderate taxes; and his vineyards, through his care, became the “head of the country,” the local term for the finest yielders. He might well have asked for the Legion of Honor.

By 1806 Grandet was fifty-seven, his wife thirty-six, and their only daughter ten. That year he inherited three fortunes—those of Madame de la Gaudiniere (née de la Bertelliere), old Monsieur de la Bertelliere, and Madame Gentillet—a hoard amassed by avarice. Old Monsieur de la Bertelliere prized his sight of gold above all. The inhabitants computed his savings by “the revenues of the sun’s wealth,” as they said.

Grandet thus obtained a modern nobility. He farmed a hundred acres of vineyard yielding seven or eight hundred hogsheads in good years; he owned thirteen farms, an old abbey whose windows he had walled to save expense, one hundred and twenty-seven acres of meadow with three thousand poplars planted in 1793, and the house where he lived. This was his visible estate; as to other property, only two men could hazard a guess at its value: Monsieur Cruchot, a notary who managed Grandet’s usurious investments, and Monsieur des Grassins, Saumur’s richest banker, in whose profits Grandet had a secret share.

Cruchot and des Grassins, discreet though obsequious, prompted townspeople to estimate Grandet’s wealth by their deference. No one doubted he kept a private treasure, a nightly hoard of louis. Avaricious observers found proof in his eyes, a yellow-metal gleam. The look of a man used to drawing enormous interest becomes furtive, eager, mysterious—an unspoken language of passion. Grandet inspired respect as one who owed no man: skilful cooper and experienced wine-grower, he could tell whether to make a thousand puncheons or five hundred; he never failed in speculation and always had casks when they were dear. He stored vintages and sold them slowly; his famous 1811 brought over two hundred and forty thousand francs.

Financially, Grandet was part tiger, part boa-constrictor: lying in wait, springing on prey, swallowing masses of louis, then resting like a snake digesting—impassible, methodical, cold. Many felt the rending of those polished claws. For Cruchot he procured money at eleven per cent; for others des Grassins discounted bills at frightful deductions. Market talk daily mentioned Grandet. To some his fortune was a patriotic boast. Innkeepers and merchants told strangers: “We have two or three millionaire establishments; but as for Monsieur Grandet, he does not himself know how much he is worth.”

In 1816 Saumur reckoned his landed property near four millions; having made about a hundred thousand francs a year from 1793 to 1817, it was fair to presume he held cash nearly equal to his estate. After a game of boston or vine-talk, townspeople said: “Le Père Grandet? Le Père Grandet must have at least five or six millions.”

“You are cleverer than I; I have never been able to find out the amount,” Cruchot or des Grassins would answer when overheard.

If Parisians spoke of Rothschild or Lafitte, Saumur folk compared them to Grandet; incredulous if anyone said a Parisian was richer. Such a fortune explained his actions. Early oddities once ridiculed had long ceased. His slightest movements, like a blink, became laws to the countryside where men studied him like a naturalist studies instinct.

“It will be a hard winter,” said one; “Père Grandet has put on his fur gloves.”

“Père Grandet is buying quantities of staves; there will be plenty of wine this year.”

Grandet never bought bread or meat: his farmers supplied capons, chickens, eggs, butter, and his tithe of wheat. He owned a mill; the tenant took grain as rent and returned flour and bran. La Grande Nanon, his only servant, baked every Saturday. He arranged with gardeners and tenants for vegetables; he sold most fruits at market. Firewood came from hedgerows and half-rotten sheds whose planks farmers carted to town, cut, and stacked in his wood-house, receiving his thanks. His known expenses were consecrated bread, clothing for his wife and daughter, hire of their chairs in church, La Grande Nanon’s wages, tinning saucepans, lights, taxes, repairs, and industrial costs. He had six hundred acres of woodland recently bought, put under a neighbor’s care for a promised indemnity; after acquiring this, he ate game for the first time.

Grandet’s manners were simple: he spoke little in short sententious phrases in a soft voice. Early he had stuttered when speaking at length; this incoherence, attributed to defects of education, was assumed and will be explained later. Four brief sentences sufficed him: “I don’t know; I cannot; I will not; I will see about it.” He never said yes or no nor committed himself to writing. If people talked, he listened coldly, chin in his right hand, right elbow on the left, forming opinions he never changed. He reflected long before any business agreement; when opponents confidently revealed plans, Grandet answered: “I can decide nothing without consulting my wife.” His wife, reduced to helpless slavery, was a useful screen in business. He went nowhere among friends; he neither gave nor accepted dinners; he made no stir, economizing even in motion. He never disturbed others’ things out of respect for property, yet at home coarser habits surfaced where self-control relaxed.

Physically Grandet was five feet high, thick-set, square-built, with knotted knees and broad shoulders; his round face, pitted by small-pox, a straight chin, white teeth, and eyes with the calm, devouring expression of a basilisk. His forehead showed transverse wrinkles and prominent bumps; his yellow-gray hair was mockingly called silver and gold. His thick-ended nose bore a veined wen the common people said was full of malice. His countenance showed dangerous cunning, an integrity without warmth, and the egoism of a man used to concentrate every feeling on avarice’s enjoyments and upon the only human being who mattered to him—his daughter and sole heiress, Eugénie. Attitude, manners, bearing, all testified to the self-belief habit of success gives.

Though outwardly unctuous, Grandet’s nature was iron. His dress had not changed since 1791: stout shoes tied with thongs, thick woollen stockings, short coarse maroon breeches with silver buckles, a yellow-and-puce velvet waistcoat, a large maroon coat, a black cravat, and a quaker’s hat. His gloves, thick as a gendarme’s, lasted twenty months; to preserve them he always laid them on his hat in one particular spot. Saumur knew no more.

Only six persons had the right of entrance to Grandet’s house. The most important of the first three was a nephew of Monsieur Cruchot. Since his appointment president of the Civil courts of Saumur this young man had added Bonfons to Cruchot, signing himself C. de Bonfons. Any litigant who called him Monsieur Cruchot would soon feel the folly in court. The magistrate, thirty-three, owned Bonfons worth seven thousand francs a year; he expected to inherit his uncle the notary’s and another uncle Abbé Cruchot’s property. These three Cruchots, backed by cousins and allies, formed a party like the Medici.

Madame des Grassins, mother of a son of twenty-three, came assiduously to play cards with Madame Grandet, hoping to marry her Adolphe to Mademoiselle Eugénie. Monsieur des Grassins furthered his wife’s schemes by services constantly rendered to the old miser. The des Grassins had adherents and cousins. On the Cruchot side the abbé—the family Talleyrand—backed by his brother the notary, contested every inch with his female adversary and tried to obtain the heiress for his nephew the president.

This secret warfare for Eugénie’s hand kept Saumur agitated. Would Mademoiselle Eugénie marry Monsieur le président or Monsieur Adolphe des Grassins? Some said Monsieur Grandet would give his daughter to neither: eaten with ambition, he was said to seek a peer of France to whom an income of three hundred thousand francs would make the Grandet casks acceptable. Others replied the des Grassins were nobles and rich; Adolphe was personable; unless the old man had a papal nephew at his beck, such an alliance should satisfy a man who had come from nothing—whom Saumur remembered with an adze in his hand and the bonnet rouge. Certain wise heads noted Cruchot de Bonfons had the right of entry at all times whereas his rival was received only on Sundays; others trusted Madame des Grassins’ intimacy with the women. “It is diamond cut diamond,” said a Saumur wit.

The oldest inhabitants declared the Grandets knew better than to let the property go out of the family, and that Mademoiselle Eugénie Grandet of Saumur would marry the son of Monsieur Grandet of Paris, a wealthy wholesale wine-merchant. To this the Cruchotines and Grassinists replied: the two brothers had seen each other only twice in thirty years; Monsieur Grandet of Paris, mayor and deputy and colonel of the National Guard, disowned the Saumur Grandets and meant to ally with some ducal family favored by Napoleon. In short, nothing was not said of the heiress, talked of for fifty miles and even on conveyances from Angers to Blois.

At the beginning of 1811 the Cruchotines won an advantage. The estate of Froidfond, noted for its park, mansion, farms, streams, ponds, and forests and worth about three millions, was put up for sale by the young Marquis de Froidfond, who had to liquidate. Maître Cruchot, the president, and the abbé, aided by adherents, prevented sale in small lots. The notary concluded a bargain for the whole property, payable in gold, persuading the marquis that suits against purchasers of small lots would delay collection; it was better to sell the whole to Monsieur Grandet, solvent and able to pay in ready money. The marquisate of Froidfond was accordingly conveyed into Monsieur Grandet’s hands, who, to Saumur’s astonishment, paid for it under proper discount and formalities.

This affair echoed from Nantes to Orléans. Monsieur Grandet took advantage of a cart returning by Froidfond to see his chateau. After casting a master’s eye over the property, he returned to Saumur, satisfied he had invested at five per cent, and seized by the stupendous thought of extending the marquisate by concentrating his property there. Then, to fill his coffers, now nearly empty, he resolved to thin his woods and sell the poplars in the meadows.

One now understands the full meaning of “the house of Monsieur Grandet”—that cold, silent, pallid dwelling above the town, sheltered by the ruined ramparts. The two pillars and the arch of the porte-cochere were built, like the house, of tufa, a soft white Loire stone that hardly lasts two centuries. Irregular holes, bored or eaten by the weather, made the arch and side walls look like vermiculated stonework, like a jail gateway. Above the arch was a bas-relief in harder stone representing the four seasons, its faces crumbling and blackened. A projecting plinth above it had taken root in chance growths—pellitory, bindweed, nettles, plantain, and even a little cherry-tree.

The archway door was solid oak, brown, shrunken, and split; though frail it was held by iron bolts in symmetrical patterns. A small square grating with close bars, red with rust, filled the middle panel and served as the knocker’s aperture; a ring struck upon the grinning head of a huge nail. Through the grating—originally for recognizing friends in civil war—one could perceive, at the far end of the dark slimy vault, a few broken steps leading to a garden shut in by thick damp walls nourishing sickly herbage. These walls were the ruined ramparts under which stretched the gardens of several neighboring houses.

The principal room on the ground floor was a large hall entered directly from the porte-cochere vault. In small towns of Anjou, Touraine, and Berry the hall is antechamber, salon, office, boudoir, and dining-room all in one; it is the theatre of domestic life. There the barber came twice a year to cut Monsieur Grandet’s hair; farmers, the curé, the under-prefect, and the miller’s boy came on business. This room, with two windows onto the street, was entirely of wood: gray panels with ancient mouldings covered the walls; ceiling beams painted gray showed between whitewashed spaces yellowed with age. An old brass clock inlaid with arabesques adorned the mantel of the ill-cut white stone chimney-piece, above which hung a greenish mirror whose bevel reflected a thread of light along a gothic damascened steel frame. Two copper-gilt candelabra decorated the chimney-piece corners; by removing their side branches each main stem served as a candlestick. Antique chairs were covered with tapestry of La Fontaine’s fables, their colors faded and darned.

At the four corners of the hall were closets or buffets, surmounted by dirty shelves. An old marquetry card-table, its top a chessboard, stood between the windows. Above it hung an oval barometer with a black border enlivened by gilt bands where flies had damaged the gilding. Opposite the chimney-piece were two pastel portraits, said to represent Madame Grandet’s grandfather, old Monsieur de la Bertelliere, as a lieutenant in the French guard, and the deceased Madame Gentillet in shepherdess guise. The windows were draped with red gros de Tours curtains held back by silken cords with ecclesiastical tassels—luxuries little in keeping with Monsieur Grandet’s habits but included in the purchase of the house along with the steel pier-glass, the tapestries, and the rosewood buffets.

By the window nearest the door stood a straw chair on castors to lift Madame Grandet high enough to see passers-by. A stained cherry-wood work-table filled the embrasure, and Eugenie Grandet’s little armchair stood beside it. Here their lives had flowed peacefully for fifteen years in a round of work from April to November; on November 1 they took their winter station by the chimney. Grandet permitted a fire only from November 1 to March 31, regardless of early spring or wintry autumn chills. A foot-warmer filled with embers—the la Grande Nanon contrivance—saved mother and daughter from chilly mornings and evenings. Mother and daughter managed the family linen, spending their days in labor; if Eugenie wished to embroider a collar she had to take time from sleep and deceive her father to obtain light. For a long time the miser dispensed tallow candles to his daughter and la Grande Nanon just as he rationed bread and other necessities.

La Grande Nanon was perhaps the only person capable of willingly accepting her master’s despotism. The whole town envied Monsieur and Madame Grandet for possessing her. So called for her height of five feet eight inches, she had lived with Monsieur Grandet thirty-five years. Though she received only sixty francs a year, she was reputed one of Saumur’s richest serving-women; those sixty francs, accumulated over thirty-five years, had enabled her to invest four thousand francs in an annuity with Maître Cruchot. This result of long, persistent economy seemed gigantic; every servant in town envied the old spinster, never considering the hard slavery through which it had been won.

At twenty-two she had been unable to find a situation because her face repelled most, though some might have admired it on a grenadier’s shoulders. Forced to leave a farm where she kept cows because the dwelling burned down, she came to Saumur, robust and ready for labor. Le Père Grandet, then set on marriage and setting up his household, noticed the girl who had been rejected door to door. A good judge of strength from his trade as a cooper, he appreciated the work to be had from a woman shaped like a Hercules—firm on her feet, strong in the hips, square in the back, with hands like a cartman and honesty as sound as her unblemished virtue. Neither the warts on her martial visage, nor the brick tints of her skin, nor her ragged garments dismayed him. He fed, shod, and clothed her, paid wages, and set her to work without treating her too roughly. Seeing herself thus welcomed, Nanon wept and attached herself sincerely to her master, who from that day ruled and worked her with feudal authority. Nanon did everything: she cooked, made lye, washed linen in the Loire and carried it home on her shoulders; she rose early and slept late; she prepared vine-dressers’ food during harvest, watched market-people, protected her master’s property like a faithful dog, and obeyed his absurd exactions without murmur.

In the famous year 1811, when the grapes were gathered with unheard-of difficulty, Grandet resolved to give Nanon his old watch—the first present in twenty years of service. Though he turned over his old shoes, it is impossible to call this a gift, for the shoes were always thoroughly worn out. Necessity had made the poor girl so niggardly that Grandet had grown to love her as one loves a dog, and Nanon allowed him to fasten a spiked collar round her throat whose spikes no longer pricked. If Grandet cut bread with parsimony, she made no complaint; she gladly shared the household’s severe regime in which no one was ever ill. Nanon was, in fact, one of the family; she laughed when Grandet laughed, felt gloomy or chilly, warmed herself, and toiled as he did. Never did the master need to find fault with her for pilfering grapes, plums, or nectarines. “Come, fall-to, Nanon!” he would say in generous years.

To the poor peasant who had earned nothing but harsh treatment, Grandet’s ambiguous laugh was like a sunbeam. Nanon’s simple heart and narrow head could hold only one feeling and one idea. For thirty-five years she never ceased to see herself before Monsieur Grandet’s wood-yard, ragged and barefoot, and to hear him say, “What do you want, young one?” Her gratitude was ever new. Sometimes Grandet, reflecting that she had never heard a flattering word and might one day appear before God more chaste than the Virgin Mary, would say, looking at her, “Poor Nanon!” The exclamation was always followed by an undefinable look from the old servant. The words, uttered from time to time, formed a chain of friendship nothing ever parted, each exclamation adding a link. Such compassion, arising in the miser and accepted gratefully by the old spinster, had something inconceivably horrible about it. This cruel pity, recalling a thousand pleasures to the cooper, was to Nanon the sum total of happiness. Who does not likewise say, “Poor Nanon!” God will recognize his angels by the inflexions of their voices and by their secret sighs.

Many households in Saumur treated servants better but received less satisfaction in return. People asked, “What have the Grandets ever done to make their Grande Nanon so attached? She would go through fire and water for them!” Her kitchen, whose barred windows looked into the court, was always clean, neat, and cold—a true miser’s kitchen where nothing went to waste. When Nanon had washed dishes, locked up dinner remains, and put out her fire, she left the kitchen, separated by a passage from the living-room, and went to spin hemp beside her masters. One tallow candle sufficed the family for the evening. The servant slept at the end of the passage in a closet lit only by a fan-light. Her robust health enabled her to live in this hole; she could hear the slightest noise through the deep silence that reigned day and night. Like a watchdog, she slept with one ear open and took her rest alert.

A description of other parts of the dwelling will appear with the events of this history, though this sketch of the hall, where the household’s whole luxury appears, may enable the reader to surmise the nakedness of the upper floors.

In 1819, at the beginning of an evening in mid-November, la Grande Nanon lighted the fire for the first time. The autumn had been fine. This day was a fête well known to the Cruchotines and the Grassinists: the six antagonists, armed at all points, prepared to meet at the Grandets and surpass each other in testimonials of friendship. That morning all Saumur had seen Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet, accompanied by Nanon, on their way to Mass; everyone remembered the day as Mademoiselle Eugenie’s birthday. Calculating when the family dinner would end, Maître Cruchot, the Abbé Cruchot, and Monsieur C. de Bonfons hastened to arrive before the des Grassins and be first to pay compliments. All three brought enormous bouquets gathered in their greenhouses. The president’s flower stalks were ingeniously wound with a white satin ribbon fringed with gold. In the morning Monsieur Grandet, following his custom on Eugenie’s birth and fête days, went to her bedside and solemnly presented his paternal gift—which for thirteen years had been a curious gold-piece. Madame Grandet gave her a winter or summer dress as needed. These two dresses and the gold pieces, supplemented by two more on New Year’s day and her father’s fête, gave Eugenie a little revenue of about a hundred crowns, which Grandet loved to see her amass. Was it not putting money from one strongbox to another, and training his heiress’s parsimony? He sometimes demanded an account of her treasure, formerly increased by the Bertelliere gifts, saying: “It is to be your marriage dozen.”

The “marriage dozen” is an old custom still in force in parts of central France. In Berry and Anjou, when a young girl marries, her family, or that of the husband, gives her a purse containing, according to means, twelve pieces, or twelve dozen, or twelve hundred pieces of gold. The poorest shepherd-girl never marries without her dozen, even if only coppers. They still tell in Issoudun of a “dozen” presented to a rich heiress containing one hundred and forty-four portugaises d’or. Pope Clement VII., uncle of Catherine de’ Medici, gave her when he married her to Henri II. a dozen antique gold medals.

During dinner, pleased to see Eugenie in a new gown, Grandet exclaimed, “As it is Eugenie’s birthday let us have a fire; it will be a good omen.”

“Mademoiselle will be married this year, that’s certain,” said la Grande Nanon, carrying away the remains of the goose—the pheasant of tradesmen.

“I don’t see anyone suitable for her in Saumur,” said Madame Grandet, glancing timidly at her husband and revealing the conjugal slavery under which she languished.

Grandet looked at his daughter and exclaimed gaily, “She is twenty-three years old to-day; we must soon begin to think of it.”

Eugenie and her mother silently exchanged a glance of intelligence.

Madame Grandet was a dry, thin woman, yellow as a quince, awkward and slow—one born to be trodden upon. She had big bones, a big nose, a big forehead, big eyes, and bore the vague resemblance of mealy fruits without savor. Her teeth were black and few, her mouth wrinkled, her chin long and pointed. She was an excellent woman, a true la Bertelliere: angelic sweetness, resignation like that of an insect tortured by children, rare piety, a good heart, and unalterable equanimity made her universally pitied and respected. Her husband never gave her more than six francs at a time for personal expenses. Ridiculous as it seems, this woman, who by fortune and inheritances had brought Père Grandet more than three hundred thousand francs, felt so profoundly humiliated by dependence—and her gentleness prevented revolt—that she had never asked for a penny or remarked on the deeds Maître Cruchot brought for her signature. This foolish secret pride, this nobility of soul perpetually misunderstood and wounded by Grandet, ruled her conduct.

Madame Grandet habitually wore a greenish levantine silk gown, making it last nearly a year; a large white cotton kerchief, a bonnet of plaited straw, and almost always a black-silk apron. As she seldom left the house she wore out few shoes and never asked anything for herself. Grandet, seized with occasional remorse when he remembered how long since he had given her the last six francs, always stipulated “the wife’s pin-money” when he sold his yearly vintage. The four or five louis presented by the Belgian or Dutch purchaser of the wine were the chief visible signs of Madame Grandet’s annual revenues. But after receiving these, her husband would often say, as though their purse were common, “Can you lend me a few sous?” and the poor woman, glad to help a man her confessor upheld as her lord and master, returned several crowns in winter out of the “pin-money.” When Grandet drew from his pocket the five-franc piece he allowed monthly for his daughter’s minor expenses—thread, needles, and toilet—he never failed to ask as he buttoned his breeches’ pocket, “And you, mother, do you want anything?”

“My friend,” Madame Grandet would answer, moved by maternal dignity, “we will see about that later.”

Wasted dignity! Grandet thought himself generous to his wife. Philosophers who meet the like of Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie have a right to say that irony lies at the bottom of Providence.

After the dinner at which Eugenie’s marriage was first mentioned, Nanon fetched a bottle of black-currant ratafia from Monsieur Grandet’s bed-chamber and nearly fell coming down the stairs.

“You great stupid!” said her master; “are you going to tumble about like other people, hey?”

“Monsieur, it was that step on your staircase which has given way.”

“She is right,” said Madame Grandet; “it ought to have been mended long ago. Yesterday Eugenie nearly twisted her ankle.”

“Here,” said Grandet, seeing Nanon pale, “as it is Eugenie’s birthday, and you came near falling, take a little glass of ratafia to set you right.”

“Faith! I’ve earned it,” said Nanon; “most people would have broken the bottle; but I’d sooner have broken my elbow holding it up.”

“Poor Nanon!” said Grandet, filling a glass.

“Did you hurt yourself?” asked Eugenie kindly.

“No, I didn’t fall; I threw myself back on my haunches.”

“Well! as it is Eugenie’s birthday,” said Grandet, “I’ll have the step mended. You people don’t know how to set your foot in the corner where the wood is still firm.”

Grandet took the candle, leaving his wife, daughter, and servant with only the hearthlight, and went into the bakehouse to fetch planks, nails, and tools.

“Can I help you?” cried Nanon, hearing him hammer.

“No, no! I’m an old hand at it,” he answered.

While Grandet mended the worm-eaten staircase and whistled the songs of his youth, the three Cruchots knocked.

“Is it you, Monsieur Cruchot?” asked Nanon, peeping through the grating.

“Yes,” answered the president.

Nanon opened the door, and the hearth’s light enabled the Cruchots to find their way into the room.

“Ha! you’ve come a-greeting,” said Nanon, smelling the flowers.

“Excuse me, messieurs,” cried Grandet, recognizing their voices; “I’ll be with you in a moment. I’m not proud; I am patching up a step on my staircase.”

“Go on, go on, Monsieur Grandet; a man’s house is his castle,” said the president sententiously.

Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet rose. The president, profiting by the darkness, said to Eugenie, “Will you permit me, mademoiselle, to wish you, on this your birth-day, a series of happy years and the continuance of health?” He offered a huge bouquet and, taking the heiress by the elbows, kissed each side of her neck with complacency that made her blush. The president, who looked like a rusty iron nail, felt his courtship progressing.

“Don’t stand on ceremony,” said Grandet, entering. “How well you do things on fête-days, Monsieur le président!”

“When it concerns mademoiselle,” said the abbé, armed with his own bouquet, “every day is a fête-day for my nephew.” The abbé kissed Eugenie’s hand. Maître Cruchot boldly kissed her on both cheeks, remarking, “How we sprout up, to be sure! Every year is twelve months.”

As he replaced the candlestick beside the clock, Grandet, who never forgot his jokes and repeated them when he fancied them funny, said, “As this is Eugenie’s birthday let us illuminate.” He carefully removed the candelabra branches, put a socket on each pedestal, took from Nanon a new tallow candle with paper twisted around its end, set it in the hollow, made it firm, lit it, and sat down beside his wife, looking alternately at friends, his daughter, and the two candles. The Abbé Cruchot, a plump puffy man with a red wig plastered down and a face like an old female gambler, stretched his feet well shod in stout shoes with silver buckles and asked, “The des Grassins have not come?”

“Not yet,” said Grandet.

“But are they coming?” asked the notary, twisting his face into a queer grimace.

“I think so,” answered Madame Grandet.

“Are your vintages all finished?” asked Monsieur de Bonfons.

“Yes, all of them,” said the old man, rising to walk up and down with chest swelling with pride as he repeated the words, “all of them.” Through the passage door he saw la Grande Nanon sitting by her fire with a candle, preparing to spin so as not to intrude among guests.

“Nanon,” he said, going into the passage, “put out that fire and that candle, and come and sit with us. Pardieu! the hall is big enough for all.”

“But monsieur, you are to have the great people.”

“Are not you as good as they? They are descended from Adam, and so are you.”

Grandet returned to the president and said, “Have you sold your vintage?”

“No, not I; I shall keep it. If the wine is good this year, it will be better two years hence. The proprietors, you know, have agreed to keep up the price; and this year the Belgians won’t get the better of us. Suppose they are sent off empty-handed for once, faith! they’ll come back.”

“Yes, but let us mind what we are about,” said Grandet in a tone that made the president tremble.

“Is he driving some bargain?” thought Cruchot.

At that moment the knocker announced the des Grassins, interrupting a conversation begun between Madame Grandet and the abbé.

Madame des Grassins was one of those lively, plump little women with pink-and-white skins who, thanks to provincial calm and virtuous habits, keep their youth past forty. She dressed well, took fashions from Paris, set the tone in Saumur, and gave parties. Her husband, formerly a quartermaster in the Imperial guard, wounded at Austerlitz and since retired, still retained the seeming frankness of an old soldier.

“Good evening, Grandet,” he said, holding out his hand and affecting a superiority with which he always crushed the Cruchots. “Mademoiselle,” he added to Eugenie after bowing to Madame Grandet, “you are always beautiful and good; I do not know what to wish you.” So saying, he offered a little box from his servant containing a Cape heather—a recently imported, very rare flower.

Madame des Grassins kissed Eugenie affectionately, pressed her hand, and said, “Adolphe wishes to make you my little offering.”

A tall, blond young man, pale and slight, with tolerable manners and seeming shy though he had spent eight or ten thousand francs beyond his allowance in Paris while studying law, came forward and kissed Eugenie on both cheeks, offering her a workbox with silver-gilt utensils—mere show trumpery, despite the monogram E.G. in well-engraved gothic letters. As she opened it, Eugenie felt an unexpected delight that made her blush and tremble. She looked to her father for permission, and Monsieur Grandet replied, “Take it, my daughter,” in a tone that would have made an actor illustrious.

The three Cruchots felt crushed as they saw the joyous look cast upon Adolphe des Grassins by the heiress, to whom such riches were unheard-of. Monsieur des Grassins offered Grandet snuff, took some himself, shook off grains that fell on the Legion of Honor ribbon in his buttonhole, then looked at the Cruchots with an air that seemed to say, “Parry that thrust if you can!” Madame des Grassins cast her eyes on the blue vases holding the Cruchot bouquets, regarding the enemy’s gifts with the pretended interest of a satirical woman. At this delicate juncture the Abbé Cruchot left the company seated in a circle round the fire and joined Grandet at the lower end of the hall. As they reached the far window embrasure the priest said in the miser’s ear, “Those people throw money out of the windows.”

“What does that matter if it gets into my cellar?” retorted the old wine-grower.

“If you want to give gilt scissors to your daughter, you have the means,” said the abbé.

“I give her something better than scissors,” answered Grandet.

“My nephew is a blockhead,” thought the abbé as he looked at the president, whose rumpled hair added to the ill grace of his brown countenance. “Couldn’t he have found some little trifle which cost money?”

“We will join you at cards, Madame Grandet,” said Madame des Grassins.

“We might have two tables, as we are all here.”

“As it is Eugenie’s birthday you had better play loto all together,” said Père Grandet; “the two young ones can join,” and the old cooper, who never played, motioned to his daughter and Adolphe. “Come, Nanon, set the tables.”

“We will help you, Mademoiselle Nanon,” said Madame des Grassins, quite joyous at the joy she had given Eugenie.

“I have never in my life been so pleased,” the heiress said to her; “I have never seen anything so pretty.”

“Adolphe brought it from Paris, and he chose it,” Madame des Grassins whispered.

“Go on! go on! damned intriguing thing!” thought the president. “If you ever have a suit in court, you or your husband, it shall go hard with you.”

The notary, sitting in his corner, looked calmly at the abbé, thinking, “The des Grassins may do what they like; my property and my brother’s and my nephew’s amount in all to eleven hundred thousand francs. The des Grassins, at most, have not half that; besides, they have a daughter. They may give presents; heiress and presents will be ours one of these days.”

At half-past eight the two card-tables were set. Madame des Grassins succeeded in seating her son beside Eugenie. The actors in this scene, so full of interest though commonplace, were provided with bits of pasteboard striped in many colors and numbered, and counters of blue glass. They seemed to listen to the notary’s jokes, who never drew a number without a remark, while in fact all thought of Monsieur Grandet’s millions. The old cooper, with inward self-conceit, contemplated Madame des Grassins’ pink feathers and fresh toilette, the banker’s martial head, the faces of Adolphe, the president, the abbé, and the notary, saying to himself:—

“They are all after my money. Hey! neither the one nor the other shall have my daughter; but they are useful—useful as harpoons to fish with.”

This family gaiety in the old gray room dimly lighted by two tallow candles; this laughter, accompanied by the whirr of Nanon’s spinning-wheel, sincere only on Eugenie’s and her mother’s lips; this triviality mingled with important interests; this young girl, who, like certain birds made victims by the price put on them, was now lured by proofs of friendship of which she was the dupe—all contributed to make the scene a melancholy comedy. It is, moreover, a drama of all times and places, though here in simplest expression. The figure of Grandet, playing his own game with false friendship and reaping enormous profits, dominates the scene and throws light upon it. The modern god—the only god in whom faith is preserved—money, is here, in all its power, manifested in a single countenance. Tender sentiments hold a secondary place; only three pure simple hearts—Nanon’s, Eugenie’s, and her mother’s—were inspired by them. How much ignorance there was in their simplicity! Eugenie and her mother knew nothing of Grandet’s wealth; they estimated life’s things by the glimmer of their pale ideas, and neither valued nor despised money because they were used to do without it. Their feelings, bruised though they did not know it, ever-living, were the secret spring of their existence and made them curious exceptions among lives purely material. Frightful condition of the human race! There is no joy that does not come from some species of ignorance.

At the moment Madame Grandet had won a loto of sixteen sous—the largest ever pooled in that house—and while la Grande Nanon laughed watching Madame pocket her riches, the knocker resounded on the house-door so loudly the women jumped in their chairs.

“There is no man in Saumur who would knock like that,” said the notary.

“How can they bang in that way!” exclaimed Nanon; “do they want to break in the door?”

“Who the devil is it?” cried Grandet.

Nanon took a candle and opened the door; Grandet followed. Madame Grandet, startled, ran to the room’s door crying, “Grandet! Grandet!” The loto-players exchanged looks.

“Suppose we all go?” said Monsieur des Grassins; “that knock strikes me as evil-intentioned.”

A young man arrived, accompanied by a coach-office porter with two trunks and a carpet-bag. Grandet turned on his wife sharply: “Madame Grandet, go back to your loto; leave me to speak with monsieur.” He shut the door; the players paused their game.

“Is he from Saumur?” asked Madame des Grassins.

“No, a traveller.”

“From Paris, no doubt,” said the notary, checking his watch. “It’s nine; the Grand Bureau diligence is never late.”

“He looks young,” added the abbe.

“Nanon does not come back,” observed Eugenie.

“Perhaps your relation,” remarked the president.

“Let us go on with our game,” Madame Grandet said gently, signalling that her husband might dislike talk.

Adolphe whispered that the newcomer was probably M. Grandet’s son—Monsieur Charles—whom he’d met at a ball. When Grandet returned, followed by the traveller, the guests saw him like a peacock among poultry.

“Sit near the fire,” Grandet said.

The stranger saluted; the men inclined their heads, the women bowed. Madame Grandet tried to offer refreshment, but her husband brusquely cut her off. The stranger, surprised by the household’s bluntness, warmed himself, thanked Eugenie—“Thank you, my cousin, but I dined at Tours”—and told Grandet he needed nothing.

“From Paris?” Madame des Grassins asked.

The young man, Monsieur Charles Grandet of Paris, used an eye-glass to inspect the table and company and impertinently ogled Madame des Grassins. The loto-numbers were called languidly; the players noticed Charles and watched him and Eugenie with curiosity.

Charles, handsome and twenty-two, contrasted sharply with the provincial regulars. He was the quintessential Paris dandy newly arrived to import refinements: a full hunting costume, the finest gun, multiple waistcoats, Buisson coats, fine linen, a gold toilet-set, and other trivial luxuries—“implements of a man of fashion,” from the little whip meant to provoke quarrels to chased pistols that might end them. Though his father had admonished modest travel, Charles had taken a private coupe, had his hair refreshed at Tours, put on a black satin cravat and a round collar, showed crossed cashmere waistcoats and a watch on a gold chain, gray trousers with black seams, gloves, and a cane with a gold knob. Only a Parisian could wear such foppery with a dashing air.

The Cruchots and Grassins, shabby, snuff-taking provincials, stared as if at a giraffe. Monsieur des Grassins and his son, more versed in fashion, mocked him with satirical looks. Grandet, absorbed in a long letter and holding the only candle at the card-table, paid no heed. Eugenie, who had mended her father’s clothes and never seen such elegance, found Charles seraphic: she inhaled the scent of his curls, envied his soft gloves and refined features, and watched to see him use an embroidered handkerchief. To an ignorant young girl, he seemed an engraving come to life; his manners, eye-glass, and disdainful glance at the coffer enchanted her. For the first time she dreamed of a phoenix cousin.

The game ended slowly. La Grande Nanon entered to ask for the sheets for monsieur’s bed. Madame Grandet followed her out; Madame des Grassins softly proposed they stop playing and keep their stakes. The guests gathered near the fire.

Eugenie, moved by a young girl’s impulse, slipped away to prepare her cousin’s room. She arranged a warming-pan, covered a table, insisted on fresh linens and a good fire, and decorated the chimney-piece with a lacquer tray, a crystal goblet, a gilt spoon, and an antique flask. She even offered a five-franc piece meant for expenses to buy a wax candle and a little sugar. Madame Grandet protested—“What will your father say?”—but Eugenie coaxed Nanon on the plea of being the girl’s birthday. Nanon laughed and obeyed, though aware of how watchful her master was.

Meanwhile Madame des Grassins made overtures to Charles, flattering him into conversation and advising that Saumur had amusements if he avoided being shut up with the miserly uncle, the pious aunt, and the uneducated cousin. Monsieur des Grassins laughed; the notary, the president, and the abbe made discreet remarks. The Abbe Cruchot slyly hinted that Madame des Grassins could best show Saumur’s honors to the visitor. Adolphe introduced himself—“I had the honor of dancing vis-à-vis with you at Baron de Nucingen’s ball”—which pleased Charles.

Charles, unbuttoning his coat and striking a theatrical pose, felt he might succeed in Saumur. The des Grassins and the Cruchots traded witticisms and innuendo: Charles ogled the salon’s beauties, Madame des Grassins thought him promising marriage material, the abbe hinted at coquetries and social duties, and Adolphe might find a rival.

All the while Père Grandet’s attention flickered over a single letter he read by candlelight. The notary and the president, sensing the master’s preoccupation, watched him for clues. The letter, from Victor-Ange-Guillaume Grandet in Paris to his brother, announced a catastrophe: Victor declared he could not survive the disgrace of bankruptcy. Failures of his broker and notary had consumed his resources, leaving debts near four million francs and assets no more than a quarter of that. The wine stock had fallen in value; in three days Paris would shout his dishonor. Unable to endure the infamy, he bewailed the ruin he left his adored son, and, foreseeing his end, begged his elder brother to be a father to Charles. He entreated that his son be treated gently and provided means to enter trade or the Indies. Victor confessed he had nothing to leave and begged his brother to obtain from Charles a renunciation of any claim on his estate; if the brother refused, remorse would follow; if he accepted, a prayer would bless him. The letter closed with a blessing and the dying man’s trust.

Grandet carefully folded the letter and slipped it into his waistcoat, masking his feelings behind a humble air and hidden calculations. Then, recovering composure, he asked his nephew if he had warmed. “Thoroughly, my dear uncle,” Charles answered. Grandet, already thinking of practicalities, brusquely inquired about the women, then told Charles that Nanon would show him his room: “It isn’t a dandy’s room; but you will excuse a poor wine-grower who never has a penny to spare. Taxes swallow up everything.”

The des Grassins rose to leave—“We do not wish to intrude, Grandet; you may want to talk to your nephew”—and offered parting bows. The notary fetched his lantern to accompany them. Madame des Grassins, who had not expected an early end to the evening because her servant was absent, accepted the abbe’s offered arm at first but then preferred her son; the abbe, indignant but playful, ended up walking with her husband’s party.

“He is a good-looking young man,” the abbe whispered to Madame des Grassins, pressing her arm. “Good-by to the grapes—Eugenie will belong to the dandy. Unless this cousin is enamoured of some Parisian woman, your son Adolphe will find another rival—”

“Not at all,” she answered coolly; she considered Eugenie plain and thought a cautious mother need not fear. “This young man cannot fail to see that Eugenie is a little fool—she was as yellow as a quince.”

“Perhaps you made him notice?” the abbe suggested.

“No, I did not take the trouble—”

“Always place yourself beside Eugenie, madame, and you need never speak ill of her; the young man will make his own comparisons.”

Madame des Grassins had already invited Charles to dine with her two days hence. The abbe urged her to cultivate the opportunity. The president loudly declared that Monsieur Grandet of Paris had evidently sent his son with matrimonial intentions. Monsieur des Grassins noted the old miser’s mysteries.

As the Cruchots and Grassins dispersed, they analyzed the evening’s event with provincial acumen, sensing it altered alliances. The Grassins and the Cruchots, usually rivals, perceived the necessity of a temporary truce to oppose a common threat: they must hinder Eugenie from falling for her cousin, and must prevent the cousin from being taken in by opportunistic comparisons. They contemplated launching “treacherous insinuations, soft-spoken calumnies, slanders full of faint praise and artless denials” to circle about him and mislead him—an alliance of petty strategies intended to manipulate the newcomer’s impressions and preserve their positions in Saumur society.

################################################
When the four relatives were left alone, Monsieur Grandet told his nephew it was too late to talk business and that they would settle matters the next day. He outlined the household routine—breakfast at eight, a light midday repast, and dinner at five—and dismissed any suggestion of accompanying Charles about town, saying his work prevented it. He shrugged off talk of his wealth, declaring himself practically penniless and still working like an apprentice. Nanon brought a candle; Madame Grandet offered any help Charles might need. Charles, polite and amused, accepted a faded yellow Anjou candle from Nanon, and Monsieur Grandet led him through the passage beside the kitchen rather than the hall door, stopping at an inner stair-separating swing-door against the north wind. Nanon bolted the outer door and loosed the wolf-dog, whose muffled bark warned strangers.

Ascending the worm-eaten stairs, Charles’s expectations sank as he took in smoke-stained walls and creaking steps. He glanced at his aunt and cousin, who smiled in a way he mistook for friendliness. At the landing he noticed three Etruscan-red doors; one above the kitchen was effectively walled off and only reachable through Grandet’s bedchamber. That room was his office and strong-room: iron-barred window, scales for weighing louis, hidden places for deeds and cash, and the solitary key in the miser’s hand. Grandet kept his accounts there like an alchemist guarding treasures, and none, not even his wife, might enter.

Eugenie’s door faced the walled room. Madame Grandet’s room opened next to hers; the husband’s bedchamber and the strong-room were separated by thick walls. Grandet gave Charles lodging in the attic above his own room so he could overhear him if need be. After brief goodnights, Grandet showed Charles his attic. Nanon had made a warming-pan fire, which the miser deemed unnecessary and scolded her for. Charles, surprised by the shabby comforts, unpacked in a room with moth-eaten valances, a cold stone fireplace, yellow-wood chairs, a simple night-table, and a thin carpet. He chatted easily with Nanon—who was astonished by his silk dressing-gown and joked it would make a fine altar-cloth—promising perhaps to leave it as charity. Nanon, delighted, dreamed of embroidery and damask; Eugenie dreamed of love.

That night Grandet brooded. He resentfully reflected that his brother had left him this nephew and that he had not even fifty francs to give the young man. He pictured the nephew’s finery and worried at the burden. Madame Grandet, practiced in reading her husband’s moods, feigned indifference and kept quiet.

At dawn, Eugenie awoke early, her prayers unfinished, and began dressing with a new awareness. For the first time she wished to appear attractive; she brushed and arranged her hair, smoothed her stockings, and put on a new gown that made her feel pleasing. Sitting by the window, she studied the court and narrow garden: a well with a vine, neatly stacked wood, lichened paving, disjointed steps, a rotten trellis with creepers, two stunted apple-trees, gravel walks, box-edged beds, a terrace of old walls leading to lindens and raspberry-bushes, and a drooping walnut near the miser’s sanctum. Under the autumn sun the frost thawed, and the scene stirred a novel delight and tenderness in her. She watched the leaves fall and felt her heart quicken, measuring herself against an inner ideal: she feared she was too plain for Charles, yet her modest, plain beauty contained an unassuming nobility she did not fully appreciate.

Flushed with new feeling, Eugenie went down to ask Nanon for cream and a galette for her cousin’s breakfast. Nanon laughed at the plan, worried about supplies and Grandet’s stinginess, and playfully scolded her. Grandet came down as Nanon fussed, and a discussion about provisions followed. He was parsimonious: if they were to use flour and butter, they must do all the cooking in one oven to save wood. Nanon, in a little rebellion, persuaded him to spare a bit of flour and butter for a galette by promising to manage the fires. She noted Charles’s fine boots and linen and admired them with wonder and envy. Grandet grumbled that some people put more money into boots than into the men who wear them.

Grandet then took Eugenie for a morning walk to his fields by the Loire, followed by Maitre Cruchot, the notary, who knew Grandet always had an eye for profit. On the riverbank, where thirty men were clearing poplars, Grandet explained his calculations—how removing poplars reclaimed hay and profit—and delighted in showing where to plant cheaper poplars on poor land to save nourishment. Cruchot marveled at Grandet’s knack for turning trees into tens of thousands of francs.

Cruchot then mentioned the talk in Saumur about the dandy nephew from Paris and anticipated drafting a marriage-contract. Grandet declared bluntly that he would rather fling his daughter into the Loire than give her to that cousin. The cold refusal struck Eugenie like a blow: her budding hopes, attached to Charles since the previous evening, withered. She returned trembling, the light drained from the street. At the house, Cruchot showed Grandet a newspaper: Monsieur Grandet of Paris had shot himself after being ruined by his broker and notary. The notary feared the Saumur Grandet had refused aid earlier; Grandet merely remarked he had known, and that the son—Charles—didn’t yet know. Cruchot departed, understanding now why Grandet had been so reserved.

At breakfast, Madame Grandet and Eugenie sat knitting. Nanon reported Charles sleeping on, unaware. Grandet, cold and harsh, revealed the suicide: “His father has blown his brains out.” Eugenie wept at the thought of the poor young man, and Nanon sympathized, but Grandet scoffed, saying the young man wasn’t worth a sou. He ordered that no one speak to Charles of the news until he returned at noon; he intended to talk with the nephew about his affairs then and warned Eugenie that Charles would be sent off to the Indies and she would never see him again. He left, rigid and relentless.

Alone, Eugenie flung herself upon her mother, gasping that she was suffocating. The two exchanged a brief, intuitive tenderness. Madame Grandet, aware of her daughter’s emotion, gently counseled restraint, asking whether Eugenie already loved him; Eugenie protested that liking and the desire to help were not wrong. The two silently conspired to make Charles’s breakfast special. Eugenie pleaded for cream and strong coffee; Nanon volunteered to run and buy coffee, fearful of being discovered by Grandet. Eugenie quietly arranged the table: grapes from the attic, a pyramid of pears, vine leaves for presentation. She listened at Charles’s door to his sleeping breaths and mused that sorrow watched over him in sleep. Nanon returned with two fresh eggs she had begged from a farmer; Eugenie embraced her in gratitude.

Thus the household prepared in small, furtive joys and humilities—Nanon scheming for paltry luxuries, the mother’s silent understanding, Eugenie’s innocent coquetry—while Grandet’s economy and dominant will cast a shadow. The possible marriage, the news from Paris, and Grandet’s firm determination to keep his daughter’s fortune from Charles intertwined, kindling inner turmoil: Eugenie’s tender awakening and anguish, Grandet’s miserly calculations and contempt, and the private, locked world of the strong-room where gold and papers were jealously guarded. Each character’s habits and moods—Nanon’s practical devotion and small rebellions, Madame Grandet’s timid devotion, Grandet’s harsh thrift, Charles’s courteous ease—set the stage for the conflicts to come, with love and pity on one side and avarice and pride on the other.

After two hours of nervous preparation—rising frequently to watch the coffee and listen for her cousin—Eugenie at last arranged a modest breakfast, a break from their usual midday standing meal of bread, fruit, a little butter, and wine. She feared her father’s reaction if he should return before Charles had eaten and checked the clock often.

“Don’t be troubled, Eugenie; if your father comes in, I will take it all upon myself,” said Madame Grandet, and Eugenie wept with gratitude and sudden love for her mother.

Charles arrived about eleven, dressed with Parisian dandyism, smiling and graceful; his presence made Eugenie’s heart beat. He joked about the ruin of his Anjou estate and sank into an easy-chair as the women sat near the fire. Habit-bound and provincial, they described their simple lives—church walks, haymakers—while Charles, used to luxuries, joked about partridges and admired small comforts like fresh eggs, butter, and a pretty watch. The women were tender with him; he returned kindness with caressing looks that stirred Eugenie, who was half-aware of her own blushes when she handled his ring.

Charles teased gently, a mixture of vanity and good-nature. He laughed at their boiled coffee in a plain pot and suggested a Chaptal coffee-pot, a fuss Nanon scorned as impractical when there were cows to feed. Eugenie volunteered to make it, earning a reproachful glance from her father even in his absence. The mood shifted as the three women grew silent with apprehension—each expecting the old man’s return.

At that moment the familiar knock sounded and Monsieur Grandet entered. His sharp eye took in the spread and the guest, and with dry sarcasm accused them of feasting.

“Ha! ha! so you have been making a feast for your nephew; very good!” he said, and, taking a glass, cut a slice of bread, spread butter from his own knife, and ate standing. He noticed the sugar and demanded to know where it had come from; Nanon said it was bought from Fessard. Charles, tasting bitter coffee, sought the sugar, but Grandet had already pocketed it; he suggested adding milk instead. Eugenie, with quiet boldness, replaced the saucer her father had stowed away—a small act of defiance—and offered grapes and preserves to Charles. Grandet scorned the extravagance and warned that Charles must not “revolutionize” his household with such spendthrift habits.

When Grandet curtly announced he had “something to tell” Charles in the garden, all three women read the seriousness in his tone. He led the young man out and, after pacing the garden and noting its box-borders and gnarled trees—images seared into Charles’s memory—revealed that his father was ill. The son guessed the truth and demanded horses and flight, but Grandet, blunt and unsparing, told him his father had shot himself and that the newspapers were full of it. He shoved an article into Charles’s hands. The young man, immature in feeling, burst into tears—a reaction Grandet found encouraging because it proved the fellow’s “sensibleness” might preserve his interest in more practical matters than grief.

Grandet added the worst: the father had failed and ruined the family; Charles was penniless. He explained, in merciless, practical terms, that to “fail” was a dishonorable crime—worse, he said, than highway robbery—because the bankrupt leaves nothing and hides behind the law; thus Charles’s honor was compromised. The words crushed Eugenie, who, simple and upright, believed her father’s harsh categorization of failure as fraud.

The young man’s sobs rang through the house. He fled to his room, grieving convulsively, while the women wept too. Back downstairs, Grandet admonished his wife and daughter not to indulge in “extravagance,” complaining at the breakfast served and insisting he would not pay for such stuffing of a pauper nephew. Eugenie began to see her father differently; his coldness toward mourning galled her.

Grandet announced he would pay only as far as Nantes for Charles to go to the West Indies to try to make a fortune, which made Eugenie throw herself into his arms in grateful affection that briefly pierced the miser’s conscience. He explained wildly to her what a million and a napoleon were—fussing over figures and numbers, incapable of feeling beyond arithmetic. Madame Grandet suggested prayers and neuvaines for the dead, to which Grandet scoffed about spending and francs not growing on bushes.

A muffled cry upstairs made Grandet order Nanon to see the young man was not suicidal; then he left to see the departing Dutchmen and talk with Cruchot. Once the door closed, Eugenie and her mother breathed easier. Eugenie, newly aware of her father’s nature and of the world’s complexities—millions, vineyard sales, purchases—tried to reconcile these facts with her image of him. She asked trivial questions about louis in a cask of wine and learned from her mother that sales sometimes fetched a hundred to two hundred francs, and that Cruchot had mentioned the purchase of Froidfond as a possible strain on the family.

Nanon reported that Charles lay like a calf on his bed, crying—a sight she deemed a blessing. Eugenie, whose sympathy had already deepened into a dawning passion, wanted to go console him. Madame Grandet warned her to be careful—“you will love him!”—but Eugenie, already feeling the stirrings of womanhood, insisted. In Charles’s room she found him prostrate in grief, moaning that if his father had confided his troubles he might have helped repair them. The plain, sincere sorrow affected both women; Charles, speaking of honor, lamented that his father had “failed” and implored God’s forgiveness for him.

Eugenie, delicate and instinctive, counseled, “Take courage! Your loss is irreparable; therefore think only of saving your honor,” trying to focus him inward toward what he could preserve—himself. Charles, in a burst of anguished pride, declared his honor the one thing left to protect. He tossed aside his hair, crossed his arms, and prayed forgiveness for his father, hiding his face in his hands. The women, comprehending but helpless before this raw grief, withdrew when he indicated he wanted solitude.

Downstairs, they sat sewing in silence, each absorbed. Eugenie had noticed in his chamber the contrast of grief with signs of luxury—razors embossed with gold, a carefully kept dressing-case—which made him paradoxically more attractive. The serious event had, for the first time, stirred these secluded lives. Eugenie spoke of wearing mourning; Madame Grandet deferred to the father’s decision. Eugenie’s thoughts circled Charles: her first desire was to share his sorrow and the burden of his uncertain future.

Thus the episode closed with the household shaken—Eugenie newly conscious of love and independent thought, Madame Grandet torn between piety and maternal concern, and Grandet unmoved by sentiment but pragmatic and parsimonious, already calculating what the crisis meant for his money and household order. The young man’s grief—sincere, virgin, and consuming—remained with them, while Grandet’s harsh judgment of bankruptcy as moral theft began to alter the family’s view of him.

About four o’clock an abrupt knock at the door struck Madame Grandet’s heart.

“What can have happened to your father?” she asked her daughter.

Grandet entered joyous. After taking off his gloves he rubbed his hands hard; his tanned skin saved them from injury. Presently his secret escaped him.

“Wife,” he said without stuttering, “I’ve trapped them all! Our wine is sold! The Dutch and the Belgians have gone. I walked about the market-place before their inn, pretending to do nothing. That Belgian fellow—you know who I mean—came up to me. The owners of the good vineyards have kept back their vintages, intending to wait; well, I didn’t hinder them. The Belgian was in despair; in a minute the bargain was made. He takes my vintage at two hundred francs the puncheon, half down. He paid me in gold; the notes are drawn. Here are six louis for you. In three months wines will have fallen.”

These words, quietly uttered and bitterly sarcastic, would have chilled Saumur’s market-place had its inhabitants heard them: their panic would have destroyed prices.

“Did you have a thousand puncheons this year, father?”

“Yes, little one.”

That term for his daughter was the superlative expression of the old miser’s joy.

“Then that makes two hundred thousand pieces of twenty sous each?”

“Yes, Mademoiselle Grandet.”

“Then, father, you can easily help Charles.”

Grandet’s rage, cold and concentrated, was fiercer than any spectacle of Babylon’s doom. He had forgotten his nephew until his daughter put him into the account.

“What’s this? Ever since that dandy set foot in my house everything goes wrong! You behave as if you had a right to buy sugar-plums and make feasts and weddings. I won’t have that sort of thing. I hope I know my duty at my time of life! I certainly sha’n’t take lessons from my daughter, or from anybody else. I shall do for my nephew what is proper, and you have no need to poke your nose into it. As for you, Eugenie,” he added, facing her, “don’t speak of this again, or I’ll send you to the Abbaye des Noyers with Nanon, see if I don’t; and no later than to-morrow either, if you disobey me! Where is that fellow, has he come down yet?”

“No, my friend,” answered Madame Grandet.

“What is he doing then?”

“He is weeping for his father,” said Eugenie.

Grandet looked at his daughter without finding a word; after all, he was a father. He made a couple of turns up and down the room, then hurried to his secret den to think over an investment he was meditating in the public Funds. The thinning of his two thousand acres of forest had yielded six hundred thousand francs; adding sales of poplars and other gains he had nine hundred thousand francs, not counting the two hundred thousand from the sale just concluded. The twenty percent Cruchot promised in the Funds tempted him. He figured on the margin of the newspaper that gave his brother’s death account, all the while hearing his nephew’s moans but not listening. Nanon knocked on the wall to summon him to dinner. On the last step he was saying to himself—

“I’ll do it; I shall get eight per cent interest. In two years I shall have fifteen hundred thousand francs, which I will then draw out in good gold,—Well, where’s my nephew?”

“He says he doesn’t want anything to eat,” answered Nanon; “that’s not good for him.”

“So much saved,” retorted her master.

“That’s so,” she said.

“Bah! he won’t cry long. Hunger drives the wolves out of the woods.”

Dinner was eaten in silence.

“My good friend,” said Madame Grandet when the cloth was removed, “we must put on mourning.”

“Upon my word, Madame Grandet! what will you invent next to spend money on? Mourning is in the heart, and not in the clothes.”

“But mourning for a brother is indispensable; and the Church commands us to—”

“Buy your mourning out of your six louis. Give me a hat-band; that’s enough for me.”

Eugenie raised her eyes to heaven without words. Her generous instincts, long repressed and now suddenly awakened, were galled. The evening passed as other monotonous evenings, yet it was certainly the most horrible. Eugenie sewed without lifting her head; Madame Grandet knitted; Grandet twirled his thumbs for four hours, absorbed in calculations whose results would astonish Saumur the next day. No one visited; the town buzzed with news of Grandet’s sale, his brother’s failure, and the arrival of his nephew. The upper and middle-class wine-growers gossiped at Monsieur des Grassins, fulminating imprecations against the ex-mayor. Nanon spun; the whirr of her wheel was the only sound beneath the gray rafters.

“We don’t waste our tongues,” she said, showing her teeth like peeled almonds.

“Nothing should be wasted,” answered Grandet, rousing from reverie. He saw a perspective of eight millions in three years and sailed along that sheet of gold. “Let us go to bed. I will bid my nephew good-night for the rest of you, and see if he will take anything.”

Madame Grandet remained on the first landing to hear the coming conversation; Eugenie went up two stairs.

“Well, nephew, you are in trouble. Yes, weep, that’s natural. A father is a father; but we must bear our troubles patiently. I am a good uncle to you, remember that. Come, take courage! Will you have a little glass of wine?” (Wine costs nothing in Saumur.) “Why!” added Grandet, “you have got no light! That’s bad, very bad; you ought to see what you are about,” and he walked to the chimney-piece. “What’s this?” he cried. “A wax candle! How the devil did they filch a wax candle? The spendthrifts would tear down the ceilings of my house to boil the fellow’s eggs.”

Hearing this, mother and daughter slipped back into their rooms and burrowed in bed like frightened mice getting to their holes.

“Madame Grandet, have you found a mine?” said the man, coming into his wife’s chamber.

“My friend, wait; I am saying my prayers,” said the poor mother in a trembling voice.

“The devil take your good God!” growled Grandet.

Misers have little belief in a future life; the present is their all. This thought casts a terrible light upon an epoch in which money sways laws, politics, and morals. Institutions, books, and men conspire to undermine belief in a future life—a belief on which the social edifice rested for eighteen hundred years. The grave, once a feared transition, is now less so; the future has been imported into the present. To obtain, by any means, a terrestrial paradise of luxury and enjoyment, hardening the heart and macerating the body for fleeting possessions, is now a widespread thought—written even in laws that ask, “What do you pay?” instead of “What do you think?” When this doctrine passes from bourgeoisie to populace, where will the country be?

“Madame Grandet, have you done?” asked the old man.

“My friend, I am praying for you.”

“Very good! Good-night; to-morrow morning we will have a talk.”

The poor woman slept like a schoolboy who, unprepared, fears his master’s anger. As she drew the sheet over her to stifle sound, Eugenie, in her night-gown and with bare feet, ran to her and kissed her brow.

“Oh! my good mother,” she said, “to-morrow I will tell him it was I.”

“No; he would send you to Noyers. Leave me to manage it; he cannot eat me.”

“Do you hear, mamma?”

“What?”

“He is weeping still.”

“Go to bed, my daughter; you will take cold in your feet: the floor is damp.”

Thus passed the solemn day which was to weigh upon the whole life of the rich and poor heiress, whose sleep was never again so calm or so pure. Certain actions seem improbable because we omit to turn psychological light upon the impulses that cause them. Perhaps Eugenie’s deep passion should be analyzed in its fibres; it became a malady that influenced her existence. Many prefer to deny results rather than estimate the force of hidden ties that link facts in the moral order. Here, Eugenie’s past life offers an explanation of her naive want of reflection and sudden emotions. The more tranquil her life, the more vivid her pity, the simpler the sentiments now developed.

Restless from the day’s events, she woke at intervals to listen for her cousin, thinking she heard his sighs. Sometimes she saw him dying or fainting from hunger. Towards morning she was certain she heard a cry. She dressed and ran, in the dawning light, to his chamber, whose door he had left open. The candle had burned to the socket. Charles, overcome, slept dressed in an armchair beside the bed, his head on the bedpost; he dreamed as men dream on an empty stomach. Eugenie might weep at her ease; she could admire the handsome face blotted with grief, the eyes swollen as if with tears. Charles felt her presence; he opened his eyes and saw her pity.

“Pardon me, my cousin,” he said, evidently not knowing the hour or place.

“There are hearts who hear you, cousin, and we thought you might need something. You should go to bed; you tire yourself by sitting thus.”

“That is true.”

“Well, then, adieu!”

She escaped, ashamed and happy. Innocence alone can be so bold. Once enlightened, virtue calculates like vice. Eugenie, who had not trembled beside her cousin, could scarcely stand when she regained her chamber. Her ignorant life had suddenly ended; she reproached herself.

“What will he think of me? He will think that I love him!”

That was what she most wished him to think. An honest love presages itself. What an event for this poor solitary girl to have entered a young man’s chamber! There are acts in love that to certain souls have the meaning of espousals. An hour later she dressed her mother as usual. They sat before the window waiting for Grandet with the cruel anxiety that freezes or warms the heart when a scene is feared. The goodman came down; he spoke to his wife absent-mindedly, kissed Eugenie, and sat to table without appearing to remember his threats.

“What has become of my nephew? The lad gives no trouble.”

“Monsieur, he is asleep,” answered Nanon.

“So much the better; he won’t want a wax candle,” said Grandet with a jeer.

This clemency and bitter gaiety struck Madame Grandet; she studied her husband. The goodman—who in regions like Touraine and Anjou is called “goodman” at a certain age whether kind or harsh—took his hat and gloves saying as he went out,—

“I am going to loiter about the market-place and find Cruchot.”

“Eugenie, your father certainly has something on his mind.”

Grandet, a poor sleeper, spent half his nights in preliminary calculations, giving accuracy to his schemes and assuring their success. All human power is a compound of time and patience. The miser’s life is constant exercise of power for self: self-love and self-interest, compact and intelligent, form egotism. From this arises the persistent curiosity and the habit of trading on others. Where is the man without desire? and what social desire can be satisfied without money?

Grandet had “something on his mind.” Like all misers, he craved to play a commercial game with men and win their money legally. To impose upon others was a sign of power, a proof he had the right to despise those who let themselves be preyed upon. Oh! who has ever truly understood the lamb lying peacefully at the feet of God?—the emblem of terrestrial victims. The miser fattens and slaughters the lamb, then despises it. His pasture is money and disdain. During the night his ideas had taken another course, explaining his sudden clemency. He had hatched a plot to trick the Parisians, to decoy and snare them, to make them sweat and hope—a plot to amuse himself, the old provincial cooper, sitting under his gloomy rafters. His nephew occupied his mind. He wished to save his dead brother’s honor without spending a penny on the son or himself. His own funds he would invest for three years; he needed only to manage his Saumur property. Finding nutriment for mischief in his brother’s failure, he resolved to crush the Parisians on Charles’s behalf and to play the good brother at the cheapest terms. The Cruchots were necessary; but he would not seek them—he resolved to make them come to him and to stage, that evening, a comedy whose plot he had conceived, which would make him the town’s admired man on the morrow without costing him a penny.

In his absence Eugenie had the happiness of openly busying herself with her cousin, spending upon him fearlessly the treasures of her pity—woman’s sublime superiority, the sole she desires to have recognized, the sole she pardons man for letting her assume. Three or four times she went to listen to his breathing to see if he slept, then when he rose she turned to his breakfast—the cream, eggs, fruits, the plates and glasses—each item a special care. At length she ran up the old staircase to hear the noise he made. Was he dressing? Did he still weep? She reached the door.

“My cousin!”

“Yes, cousin.”

“Will you breakfast downstairs, or in your room?”

“Where you like.”

“How do you feel?”

“Dear cousin, I am ashamed of being hungry.”

This conversation, held through the closed door, was like an episode in a poem to Eugenie.

“Well, then, we will bring your breakfast to your own room, so as not to annoy my father.”

She flew to the kitchen.

“Nanon, go and do his room!”

That staircase, often traversed and echoing to the slightest noise, lost its decaying aspect in Eugenie’s eyes. It grew luminous; it had a voice and spoke to her; it was young—young like the love it was now serving. Her kind indulgent mother lent herself to the caprices of the child’s love, and after the room was put in order both went to sit with the unhappy youth and keep him company. Does not Christian charity make consolation a duty? The two women drew little sophistries from religion to justify their conduct. Charles became the object of tender and loving care. His saddened heart felt the sweetness of the gentle friendship these two souls, crushed under restraint, displayed when, for an instant, they were unfettered in the regions of suffering.

Claiming relationship, Eugenie began to fold linen and arrange the toilet articles Charles had brought; she could marvel at ease over each luxurious bauble and knick-knack of silver and gold, holding them under pretext of examination. Charles could not see without emotion the generous interest his aunt and cousin felt; he knew Paris society and, placed as he was, expected indifference. Eugenie thus appeared in splendor, and he admired the innocence of life and manners he had been inclined to ridicule. When Eugenie took from Nanon the bowl of coffee and cream and poured it with real feeling, giving him a kindly glance, the Parisian filled with tears; he took her hand and kissed it.

“What troubles you?” she said.

“Oh! these are tears of gratitude,” he answered.

Eugenie abruptly turned to the chimney-piece to take the candlesticks.

“Here, Nanon, carry them away!” she said.

When she looked again she blushed, but her looks could at least deceive, not betray the excess of joy that flooded her heart; yet the eyes of both expressed the same sentiment—the future was theirs. This soft emotion was precious to Charles amid heavy grief because it was wholly unexpected. The knocker recalled the women to duty; happily they ran downstairs with speed to be seated at their work when Grandet entered; had he met them under the archway his suspicions might have been roused. After breakfast, which the goodman took standing, the keeper from Froidfond, whose promised indemnity had not yet been paid, appeared with a hare and partridges from the park, eels and two pike sent as tribute by the millers.

“Ha, ha! poor Cornoiller; here he comes, like fish in Lent. Is all that fit to eat?”

“Yes, my dear, generous master; it has been killed two days.”

“Come, Nanon, bestir yourself,” said Grandet; “take these things, they’ll do for dinner. I have invited the two Cruchots.”

Nanon opened her eyes, stupefied, and looked at everybody.

“Well!” she said, “and how am I to get the lard and the spices?”

“Wife,” said Grandet, “give Nanon six francs, and remind me to get some of the good wine out of the cellar.”

“Well, then, Monsieur Grandet,” said the keeper, prepared to settle the indemnity, “Monsieur Grandet—”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta!” said Grandet; “I know what you want to say. You are a good fellow; we will see about it to-morrow, I’m too busy to-day. Wife, give him five francs,” he added to Madame Grandet as he decamped.

The poor woman was too happy to buy peace at eleven francs. She knew Grandet would let her alone for a fortnight after he had thus taken back, franc by franc, the money he had given her.

“Here, Cornoiller,” she said, slipping ten francs into his hand, “some day we will reward your services.”

Cornoiller could say nothing and went away.

“Madame,” said Nanon, who had donned her black coif and taken her basket, “I want only three francs. You keep the rest; it’ll go fast enough somehow.”

“Have a good dinner, Nanon; my cousin will come down,” said Eugenie.

“Something very extraordinary is going on, I am certain,” said Madame Grandet. “This is only the third time since our marriage that your father has given a dinner.”

About four o’clock, just as Eugenie and her mother had finished setting the table for six and the master had brought up a few bottles of the exquisite wine provincials cherish, Charles came down into the hall. He was pale; his gestures, expression, glance, and voice had a sadness full of grace. He truly suffered; the veil of pain gave him an air dear to women. Eugenie loved him the more for it. Sorrow had leveled the distance between them—Charles was no longer the rich, distinguished young man above her sphere, but a relation plunged into frightful misery. Misery begets equality. Women, like angels, claim suffering humanity. Charles and Eugenie spoke only with their eyes; the poor fallen dandy, orphaned and impoverished, sat apart, proud, calm, silent. From time to time the gentle caressing glance of the young girl shone upon him and drew him from sad thoughts into fields of hope, where she loved to hold him at her side.

################################################
Saumur was more excited about the dinner Grandet gave the Cruchots than it had been the night before at his vintage sale, though that sale had affronted the wine-growers. Had the politic old miser entertained from nobler motives he might have been praised; but believing himself superior to the townspeople, he cared little for their opinion.

The des Grassins soon learned of Guillaume Grandet’s failure and violent death and resolved to call that evening to commiserate and to discover why Grandet had invited the Cruchots. At precisely five Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle the notary arrived in their Sunday clothes. They dined with appetites; Grandet was grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, Madame Grandet reserved. When they rose, Charles said he must retire to undertake a long and painful correspondence.

Once aunt and uncle were sure Charles could not overhear, Grandet dismissed his wife and daughter and, alone with the two Cruchots, put on more than ever the cunning that had earned him the nickname “the old dog.” He affected a stutter and some deafness—tricks he used to control conversation and avoid taking responsibility—so as to keep his real intentions in doubt.

He began, stammering, to raise the subject of bankruptcy. The president, flattered by Grandet’s awkwardly respectful address, took the lead and explained how a threatened merchant might avoid dishonor by liquidating rather than being declared insolvent. He described how creditors might sell notes in the market at a reduction so that if one held the debts and paid them off by agreement, the estate would be honorably released. Grandet, feigning ignorance, professed that he knew nothing of such Paris tricks and that he lived in Saumur attending to vines and drains. He admitted he might be able to help his nephew, but he must consider, for he had no time or knowledge to engage personally.

The president—smooth, sniffing snuff—went on about notes as merchandise, invoking Bentham’s abstract reasoning, and suggested that debts might be bought for twenty-five percent. The notary laughed; the president added practicalities: a friend could go to Paris, buy up the notes at a reduction, obtain extensions from creditors, and thus save the family’s honor. Grandet pretended distress at the complexity and expense, repeatedly claiming he was only a poor wine-grower; but his eye betrayed eagerness.

The notary, uneasy, warned his nephew to let Monsieur Grandet explain his intentions. Before Cruchot could resume, the des Grassins arrived with expressions of condolence. The notary seized the opportunity to announce Grandet’s honorable intention to liquidate his brother’s affairs, and that the president had offered to go to Paris to negotiate with the creditors. Des Grassins, who had been gossiping on the way about the old man’s avarice—almost accusing him of fratricide—were astonished and delighted.

“Grandet is honorable to the backbone,” cried the banker, embracing him. The scene allowed the des Grassins to present themselves as friends with business sense. The banker suggested that such a commercial matter required a consummate businessman and offered to take charge, since he was going to Paris and could attend to it while on his own affairs.

Dropping some of his assumed hesitancy, Grandet confided privately to des Grassins that he trusted the banker more than the president and had other plans. He wanted to buy a few thousand francs in the Funds while the price was low; des Grassins knew about such things and could make the purchase by the end of the month. He signaled that none of this should be told to the Cruchots. The banker, pleased, promised to begin by the mail-post and to take final directions at five o’clock, just before dinner.

The parties lingered a little; des Grassins struck Grandet on the shoulder, murmuring that it was fortunate to have such a relation. Grandet, rubbing his wen, declared his love for his brother and his intention to prove it—unless costs prevented him. Des Grassins had cut the Cruchots out of the commission; the president, disappointed at being sidelined, complained to his uncle that he had begun by being “de Bonfons” and ended as nothing but Cruchot. The notary advised modesty: let the des Grassins take this tack, and Eugenie would remain the president’s wife all the same.

Soon the news of Grandet’s magnanimity spread through three houses and then the town. Everyone forgave his sale made in defiance of pledged good faith and lauded forsworn generosity; such sudden enthusiasm is French—quick to forget and quick to praise.

When Père Grandet shut the door he called Nanon and instructed her not to let the dog loose and not to go to bed; they had work to do. At eleven Cornoiller would bring the chariot from Froidfond; Nanon must tell him to come softly, for there must be no nocturnal racket and no neighborly curiosity. Grandet returned to his private room, moving cautiously so as not to wake his wife and daughter and, above all, not to rouse his nephew, whom he had already begun to condemn for his pale, despairing looks.

About the middle of the night Eugenie, anxious for Charles, fancied she heard a cry like that of a dying person. She wrapped herself in a pelisse and, thinking of the light under her cousin’s door, was about to go out when she saw Nanon’s heavy steps and heard horses. Peering into the corridor she met her father’s vague, unnoticing gaze and was terrified. The goodman and Nanon were yoked together by a stout stick; a thick rope over their shoulders carried a small keg like those Grandet made in the bakehouse—filled, as Nanon complained, with heavy copper sous. Grandet cautioned them to be careful of the candlestick; the scene was lit by a single candle on the staircase.

Cornoiller had come with horses; Grandet asked if he had pistols—there was nothing to fear—and told Nanon to say to his wife that he had gone into the country and would be back to dinner. The chariot drove off with all possible secrecy; Nanon bolted the great door, let the dog loose, and went to bed with a bruised shoulder, the neighbors unaware of either the departure or its purpose. Grandet’s precautions and reticence were habitual; no one had ever seen a penny in that house, though it was filled with gold.

In the morning, gossip at the port reported that exchange on gold had doubled because of military preparations at Nantes and that speculators had come to Angers to buy coin. The old wine-grower, having borrowed horses from his farmers, had seized the chance: he had gone to sell his gold and to bring back treasury notes, converting the sum he intended to put into the Funds and increasing it by advantage of the exchange.

“My father has gone,” thought Eugenie, having heard the news from the head of the stairs. Silence fell; the distant rumble of the carriage faded. A cry came from her cousin’s room; a narrow strip of light showed under the door and slanted across the rotten stair.

“He suffers!” she cried, and rushed up. A second moan brought her to the landing. The door stood ajar; she pushed it open. Charles slept with his head drooped over the arm of an old chair, the pen fallen from his hand nearly touching the floor. His strained posture alarmed her; she entered quickly.

“He must be very tired,” she thought, and glanced at the dozen sealed letters on the table—carriage-makers, a tailor—guessing he had been settling affairs to leave France. Her eyes fell on two open letters. The words “My dear Annette” at the head of one stunned her. Her heart beat fast; her feet felt nailed.

“His dear Annette! He loves! He is loved! No hope! What does he say to her?” Thoughts rushed through her. “I ought to go away—What if I do read it?” She looked at Charles, then gently set his head against the chair back; he let her, like a sleeping child. She raised his drooping hand and kissed his chestnut hair. “Dear Annette!” a demon shrieked in her ear.

“I am doing wrong; but I must read it,” she decided, torn. Passion and curiosity triumphed. As she read, the keen glow of first love made every feeling precious.

My dear Annette,—Nothing could have separated us but the great misfortune that has fallen on me. My father has killed himself; his fortune and mine are irretrievably lost. I am an orphan at an age when, by my education, I am still a child; yet I must lift myself as a man out of the abyss. If I wish to leave France an honest man—I am without a hundred francs to try my luck in the Indies or America. Yes, Anna, I must seek my fortune in those deadly climates. I cannot remain in Paris. Neither my nature nor my face are made to bear the affronts, neglect, disdain shown to a ruined man, the son of a bankrupt! Good God! think of owing two millions! I should be killed in a duel the first week; therefore I shall not return. Your love—the most tender and devoted love—cannot draw me back. I have no money to go to you, to give and receive a last kiss that might strengthen me.

“Poor Charles! I did well to read the letter. I have gold; I will give it to him,” thought Eugenie. She wiped her eyes and read on.

I have never thought of the miseries of poverty. If I had the hundred louis for the journey, I have not a sou for an outfit. But no—I have not the hundred louis, not even one. I don’t know what will be left after I have paid my debts in Paris. If I have nothing, I shall go quietly to Nantes and ship as a common sailor; I will begin in the new world like other men who started with nothing and made fortunes. I have faced my future coolly. It seems more horrible to me because I was so petted by a mother who adored me, so indulged by a kindly father, so blessed to have known the love of an Anna. The flowers of life are all I have known. Such happiness could not last. Nevertheless I feel more courage than is supposed for a careless young man—above all, one used to caressing; oh, my father! Annette, he is dead!

I have thought of my position and yours. I have grown old in twenty-four hours. Dear Anna, if to keep me with you in Paris you were to sacrifice your luxury, your dress, your opera-box, we should still not have enough for my extravagant ways. Besides, I would never accept such sacrifices. No, we must part now and forever—

“He gives her up! Blessed Virgin! What happiness!” Eugenie quivered with joy. Charles moved; terror chilled her. He did not wake, and she resumed reading.

When shall I return? I do not know. The climate of the West Indies ages a European who works hard. In ten years your daughter will be eighteen; she may be your companion, your spy. Society will be cruel, daughters sometimes cruel; remember four years of happiness and, if you can, be faithful to my memory. I cannot demand faithfulness, for I must adapt to my new life and take a commonplace view of it. Therefore marriage becomes a necessity; I have found here in Saumur, in my uncle’s house, a cousin whose face, manners, mind, and heart would please you, and who, besides, seems to me—

The letter broke off; Charles had fallen asleep again. Eugenie excused him. How could an innocent girl suspect cold-heartedness in such a letter? Young, piously brought up girls see love’s light everywhere; they reflect warmth on their lover and attribute to him their highest thoughts. A woman’s errors often spring from belief in good and trust in truth. Eugenie’s simple heart heard “My dear Annette” like the sweetest words; the tears on the young man’s lashes seemed noble. How could she know that Charles, though he loved his father and mourned him, was moved more by paternal indulgence than by heroic resolve? Monsieur and Madame Guillaume Grandet had gratified every fancy of their son and lavished pleasures on him, keeping him from the hard reckonings other sons face.

Yet Charles was a Parisian youth, schooled by society and by Annette to calculate; beneath youthful grace he had learned the prudence of social life—where to “see clear” one must believe in nothing and weigh a friend’s purse daily. After folly and adulation, Annette had made him think soberly; with a perfumed hand among his curls she spoke to him of his future, taught worldly prudence, effeminized and materialized him. Such outward sense often wins the trust of young women. Had Eugenie been more suspicious, she might still have been unable to distrust a cousin whose manners and words matched youthful hopes. Fate had laid before her Charles’s last effusions; she heard the last stirrings of his conscience. She laid down the letter—so full of love—and smiled, watching him sleep; the fresh illusions of life were still on his face; she vowed to love him always. Then she glanced at the other letter, reading it for new proofs of noble qualities she already believed him to possess.

My dear Alphonse,—When you receive this letter I shall be without friends; but while I doubt the friendship of the world, I have never doubted yours. I beg you to settle my affairs and get as much as you can out of my possessions. You know my situation. I have nothing left, and I intend to go at once to the Indies. I have written to those I think I owe money, and enclose a list. My books, furniture, pictures, horses, etc., ought to pay my debts. I do not wish to keep anything, except perhaps a few baubles for my outfit. I will send a power of attorney so you can make these sales. Send me all my weapons. Keep Briton for yourself; nobody would pay his value, and I would rather give him to you—like a mourning-ring bequeathed by a dying man to his executor. Farry, Breilmann, & Co. built me a travelling-carriage not yet delivered; persuade them to keep it and not ask for payment. If they refuse, do what you can and avoid anything dishonorable. I owe the British Islander six louis lost at cards; don’t fail to pay him—

“Dear cousin!” whispered Eugenie, throwing down the letter and running softly back to her room, carrying a lighted candle. A thrill passed as she opened the drawer of an old oak cabinet topped with the faint royal salamander. She took out a large red velvet purse edged with gold fringe—a relic from her grandmother. She weighed it proudly and began counting the forgotten hoard: portugaises, genovines, Spanish quadruples, Dutch ducats of 1756, Indian rupees, and a napoleon of forty francs received the day before. Her father loved these coins for rarity, but Eugenie thought only of Charles. After a few miscalculations she made out about five thousand eight hundred francs in intrinsic value—nearly six thousand for collectors. She clapped her hands like a child. Father and daughter had each counted their fortune—he, to sell his gold; she, to fling hers into the sea of affection. She put the coins back, took the purse, and ran upstairs without hesitation. The hidden misery of her cousin made her forget hour and propriety; she was strong in conscience, devotion, and happiness.

At the threshold, holding the candle and purse, Charles woke and was speechless with surprise. Eugenie set the candle on the table.

“My cousin, I must beg pardon for a wrong I have done you; but God will pardon me—if you—will help me to wipe it out.”

“What is it?” asked Charles, rubbing his eyes.

“I have read those letters.”

Charles colored.

“How did it happen?” she continued; “how came I here? Truly, I do not know. I am tempted not to regret too much that I have read them; they have made me know your heart, your soul, and—”

“And what?” asked Charles.

“Your plans, your need of a sum—”

“My dear cousin—”

“Hush, hush! not so loud; we must not wake others. See,” she said, opening her purse, “here are the savings of a poor girl who wants nothing. Charles, accept them! This morning I did not value money; you have taught me. It is but a means. A cousin is almost a brother; you can surely borrow your sister’s purse.”

Eugenie, both woman and girl, never dreamed of refusal; but Charles remained silent.

“Oh! you will not refuse?” cried Eugenie, whose heart could be heard beating in the silence.

His hesitation mortified her, but the need of his position became clearer, and she knelt.

“I will never rise till you have taken that gold!” she said. “My cousin, I implore you, answer me! let me know if you respect me, if you are generous, if—”

Moved, Charles took her hands to keep her from kneeling; his tears fell on them. Eugenie sprang to the purse and poured its contents on the table.

“Ah! yes, yes, you consent?” she sobbed. “Fear nothing, my cousin, you will be rich. This gold will bring you happiness; some day you shall bring it back to me—are we not partners? I will obey all conditions. But you should not attach such value to the gift.”

Charles could finally speak.

“Yes, Eugenie; my soul would be small indeed if I did not accept. And yet—gift for gift, confidence for confidence.”

“What do you mean?” she said, frightened.

“Listen, dear cousin; I have here—” He pointed to a square box covered by a leather case on the drawers. “There,” he continued, “is something as precious to me as life itself. This box was a present from my mother. All day I have thought that if she could rise from her grave, she would herself sell the gold which her love for me lavished on this dressing-case; but were I to do so, it would seem a sacrilege.” Eugenie pressed his hand.

“No,” he added after a pause with a tender look, “no, I will neither sell it nor risk its safety on my journey. Dear Eugenie, you shall be its guardian. Never did friend commit anything more sacred to another. Let me show it to you.”

He opened the case and revealed a richly wrought dressing-case with gold ornaments. “What you admire is nothing,” he said, pushing a spring that revealed a hidden drawer. He drew out two portraits, masterpieces by Madame Mirbel, richly set with pearls.

“Oh, how beautiful! Is it the lady to whom you wrote that—”

“No,” he smiled; “this is my mother, and here is my father, your aunt and uncle. Eugenie, I beg you on my knees, keep my treasure safely. If I die and your little fortune is lost, this gold and these pearls will repay you. To you alone could I leave these portraits; you are worthy to keep them. But destroy them at last, so they may pass into no other hands.” Eugenie was silent.

“Ah, yes, say yes! You consent?” he added with winning grace.

Hearing his words as if he addressed himself, she turned to him with a look of love—the first look of womanhood, half coquette, half depth. He kissed her hand.

“Angel of purity! between us two money is nothing, never can be anything. Feeling and sentiment must be all henceforth.”

“You are like your mother—was her voice as soft as yours?”

“Oh! much softer—”

“Yes, for you,” she said, dropping her eyelids. “Come, Charles, go to bed; I wish it; you must be tired. Good-night.” She disengaged her hand; he lighted her way to her room. At the threshold—

“Ah!” he said, “why am I ruined?”

“What matter?—my father is rich; I think so,” she answered.

“Poor child!” said Charles, stepping in and leaning against the wall, “if that were so, he would never have let my father die; he would not let you live in this poor way; he would live otherwise himself.”

“But he owns Froidfond.”

“What is Froidfond worth?”

“I don’t know; but he has Noyers.”

“Nothing but a poor farm!”

“He has vineyards and fields.”

“Mere nothing,” said Charles disdainfully. “If your father had only twenty-four thousand francs a year do you suppose you would live in this cold, barren room?” he added, edging forward. “Ah! there you will keep my treasures,” he said, glancing at the old cabinet, as if to conceal his thoughts.

“Go and sleep,” she said, hindering his entrance into the disordered room.

They bade each other good-night with a mutual smile.

Both fell asleep in the same dream; from that moment the youth began to wear roses with his mourning. The next morning, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found her daughter in the garden with Charles. The young man was still sad, fitting for one plunged in misfortune and measuring his future.

“My father will not be home till dinner-time,” said Eugenie, seeing her mother’s anxious face.

There was an evident unison of thought between her and her cousin. Charles spent the morning in the hall, his sadness respected. Everyone busied themselves with tasks. Grandet had left affairs unattended, and several people came on business—the plumber, mason, slater, carpenter, diggers, farmers—some to drive bargains about repairs, others to pay or be paid. Nanon put away produce brought as tribute, always waiting for her master’s orders before deciding to use or sell. It was the goodman’s custom, like many country gentry, to drink bad wine and eat spoiled fruit.

Toward five in the afternoon Grandet returned from Angers, having made fourteen thousand francs by the exchange on his gold, bringing treasury-notes bearing interest until he should invest them in the Funds. He had left Cornoiller at Angers to look after the horses, nearly foundered, with orders to bring them home slowly after they were rested.

“I have got back from Angers, wife,” he said; “I am hungry.”

Nanon called from the kitchen: “Haven’t you eaten anything since yesterday?”

“Nothing,” he answered.

Nanon brought in the soup. Des Grassins arrived to take his client’s orders just as the family sat down. Grandet had not even observed his nephew.

“Go on eating, Grandet,” said the banker; “we can talk. Do you know what gold is worth in Angers? They have come from Nantes after it? I shall send some of ours.”

“Don’t send any,” said Grandet; “they have got enough. We are such old friends, I ought to save you from such a loss of time.”

“But gold is worth thirteen francs fifty centimes.”

“Say was worth—”

“Where the devil have they got any?”

“I went to Angers last night,” answered Grandet in a low voice.

The banker started. A whispered conversation began between the two, during which they frequently looked at Charles. Presently des Grassins gave a start of astonishment; probably Grandet was then instructing him to invest the sum which was to give him a hundred thousand francs a year in the Funds.

“Monsieur Grandet,” said the banker to Charles, “I am starting for Paris; if you have any commissions—”

“None, monsieur, I thank you,” answered Charles.

“Thank him better than that, nephew. Monsieur is going to settle the affairs of the house of Guillaume Grandet.”

“Is there any hope?” said Charles eagerly.

“What!” exclaimed his uncle, with well-acted pride, “are you not my nephew? Your honor is ours. Is not your name Grandet?”

Charles rose, seized Pere Grandet, kissed him, turned pale, and left the room. Eugenie looked at her father with admiration.

“Well, good-by, des Grassins; it is all in your hands. Decoy those people as best you can; lead ’em by the nose.”

The two diplomats shook hands. The old cooper accompanied the banker to the front door. After closing it, he came back and plunged into his armchair, saying to Nanon,—

“Get me some black-currant ratafia.”

Too excited to sit long, he got up, looked at the portrait of Monsieur de la Bertelliere, and began to sing, performing what Nanon called his dancing steps—

“Dans les gardes francaises
J’avais un bon papa.”

Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie looked at each other in silence. The master’s hilarity always frightened them when it reached its climax. The evening soon passed. Pere Grandet chose to go to bed early, and when he went to bed everybody else was expected to do the same. Nanon, Charles, and Eugenie were as tired as the master. Madame Grandet lived always according to her husband’s will. During the two hours consecrated to digestion the cooper, more facetious than ever, uttered a number of his apothegms—one of which gives the measure of his mind. When he had drunk his ratafia he looked at his glass and said,—

“You have no sooner put your lips to a glass than it is empty! Such is life. You can’t have and hold. Gold won’t circulate and stay in your purse. If it were not for that, life would be too fine.”

He was jovial and benevolent. When Nanon came with her spinning-wheel, “You must be tired,” he said; “put away your hemp.”

“Ah, bah! then I shall get sleepy,” she answered.

“Poor Nanon! Will you have some ratafia?”

“I won’t refuse a good offer; madame makes it a deal better than the apothecaries. What they sell is all drugs.”

“They put too much sugar,” said the master; “you can’t taste anything else.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The following day the family met at eight for the early breakfast, a picture of domestic intimacy. Grief had brought Madame Grandet, Eugenie, and Charles into close accord; even Nanon sympathized without understanding why. The four now made one family. The old man, whose avarice and certainty that he would soon be rid of the dandy without paying more than his journey to Nantes made him nearly indifferent, left the two children, as he called Charles and Eugenie, free to behave as they pleased under Madame Grandet’s watch, in whom he had implicit confidence for public and religious morality. He busied himself with straightening field boundaries, tending poplar plantations by the Loire, winter vineyard work, and matters at Froidfond. These things occupied him.

For Eugenie the springtime of love had come. Since the night she gave her little treasure to her cousin, her heart had followed it. Confederates in the same secret, they exchanged looks and a mutual intelligence that deepened their communion, putting them beyond ordinary life. Their near relationship seemed to license gentleness and tender glances. Eugenie took delight in lulling her cousin’s pain with the childish joys of new love. There are sweet parallels between the birth of love and the birth of life: one rocks the babe with songs and soft glances, tells tales of a golden future, and Hope spreads her wings above it. Childhood and love were one to Eugenie and Charles; it was a first passion, childlike play—the more caressing because wrapped in sadness. Struggling at birth against the gloom of mourning, their love matched the plainness of that gray, ruined house. As they exchanged few words beside the well, lingered in the garden at sunset on a mossy seat saying the infinite nothings of love, or mused between the house and the ramparts as under church arches, Charles learned the sanctity of love; his great lady Annette had taught him only its stormy troubles. He left worldly passion, coquettish and vain, for true, pure love. He loved even the house, whose customs no longer seemed ridiculous. He rose early to speak with Eugenie before her father came to dole out provisions; when Grandet’s steps sounded he escaped to the garden. The small criminality of these morning tête-à-têtes, which Nanon pretended not to see, gave their innocent love the charm of forbidden joy.

After breakfast, when Grandet had gone to his fields, Charles remained with the mother and daughter, finding pleasure in holding their skeins, watching them work, and listening to their quiet prattle. The simplicity of this half-monastic life, revealing the beauty of these souls, touched him. He had believed such morals impossible in France, admitting their existence only in Germany; even so, they seemed fabulous, only real in La Fontaine novels. Soon Eugenie became to him a Margaret of Goethe—before her fall. Day by day his words and looks enraptured the poor girl, who yielded with delicious non-resistance to the current of love; she caught her happiness like a swimmer seizes an overhanging branch to draw himself ashore. Did dread of coming absence sadden those fleeting days? Daily some small circumstance reminded them of the impending parting.

Three days after des Grassins’ departure, Grandet took his nephew to the civil courts with the solemnity country people attach to legal acts, that he might sign a deed surrendering his rights in his father’s estate. Terrible renunciation! Charles also went before Maitre Cruchot to make two powers of attorney—one for des Grassins, the other for the friend charged with selling his belongings. He attended formalities to obtain a passport; when he received mourning clothes from Paris he sent for the Saumur tailor and sold his useless wardrobe. This last act pleased Grandet exceedingly.

“Ah! now you look like a man prepared to embark and make your fortune,” he said when Charles appeared in a plain black surtout. “Good! very good!”

“I hope you will believe, monsieur,” answered his nephew, “that I shall always try to conform to my situation.”

“What’s that?” said his uncle, eyes lighting on a handful of gold Charles carried.

“Monsieur, I have collected all my buttons and rings and other superfluities which may have some value; not knowing anyone in Saumur, I wanted to ask you to—”

“To buy them?” said Grandet, interrupting.

“No, uncle; only to tell me of an honest man who—”

“Give me those things; I will estimate their value,” said Grandet, taking the mass of gold.

“Cousin,” said Grandet, “may I offer you these two buttons? They can fasten ribbons round your wrists; that sort of bracelet is fashionable now.”

“I accept,” she answered, giving him an understanding look.

“Aunt, here is my mother’s thimble; I have always kept it carefully,” said Charles, presenting a pretty gold thimble to Madame Grandet, who had longed for one.

“I cannot thank you; no words are possible, my nephew,” said the poor mother, tears in her eyes. “Night and morning in my prayers I shall add one for you, the most earnest of all—for those who travel. If I die, Eugenie will keep this treasure for you.”

“They are worth nine hundred and eighty-nine francs, seventy-five centimes,” said Grandet, opening the door. “To save you the pain of selling them, I will advance the money—in livres.”

The word livres on the Loire littoral signified that crown prices of six livres were to be accepted as six francs without deduction.

“I dared not propose it,” answered Charles; “but it was repugnant to me to sell my jewels to some second-hand dealer in your town. People should wash their dirty linen at home, as Napoleon said. I thank you for your kindness.”

Grandet scratched his ear; silence followed.

“My dear uncle,” resumed Charles, uneasy as if fearing to wound him, “my aunt and cousin have accepted a trifling remembrance of me. Will you allow me to give you these sleeve-buttons, now useless? They will remind you of a poor fellow who, far away, will always think of those who are henceforth all his family.”

“My lad, my lad, you mustn’t rob yourself! Let me see, wife, what have you got?” he added, turning eagerly to her. “Ah! a gold thimble. And you, little girl? Diamond buttons? Yes, I’ll accept your present, nephew,” he answered, shaking Charles’s hand. “But—you must let me—pay—your—yes, your passage to the Indies. I wish to pay your passage because, d’ye see, my boy, in valuing your jewels I estimated only the weight of the gold; very likely the workmanship is worth something. So let us settle that I give you fifteen hundred francs—in livres; Cruchot will lend them to me. I haven’t got a copper farthing here—unless Perrotet, behindhand with his rent, should pay up. By the bye, I’ll go and see him.”

He took his hat, put on his gloves, and went out.

“Then you are really going?” said Eugenie to her cousin, with a sad look mingled with admiration.

“I must,” he said, bowing his head.

For days Charles’s bearing, manners, and speech took on the dignity of a man who, despite profound affliction, feels obligations and gathers courage from misfortune. He no longer repined; he became a man. Eugenie never augured better of his character than when she saw him in plain black that suited his pale face. That day the two women put on mourning, and all three assisted at a Requiem in the parish church for the late Guillaume Grandet.

At the second breakfast Charles received letters from Paris and began to read them.

“Well, cousin, are you satisfied with the management of your affairs?” said Eugenie in a low voice.

“Never ask such questions, my daughter,” said Grandet. “What the devil! do I tell you my affairs? Why do you poke your nose into your cousin’s? Let the lad alone!”

“Oh! I haven’t any secrets,” said Charles.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta, nephew; you’ll soon find out that you must hold your tongue in business.”

When the two lovers were alone in the garden, Charles drew Eugenie down on the old bench beneath the walnut-tree and said,—

“I did right to trust Alphonse; he has done famously. He has managed my affairs with prudence and good faith. I now owe nothing in Paris. All my things have been sold; he took advice of an old sea-captain and spent three thousand francs on a commercial outfit of European curiosities sure to be in demand in the Indies. He has sent my trunks to Nantes, where a ship is loading for San Domingo. In five days, Eugenie, we must bid each other farewell—perhaps forever, at least for years. My outfit and ten thousand francs, which two of my friends send me, are a very small beginning. I cannot look to return for many years. My dear cousin, do not weigh your life in the scales with mine; I may perish; some good marriage may be offered to you—”

“Do you love me?” she said.

“Oh, yes! indeed, yes!” he answered, with depth that revealed equal feeling.

“I shall wait, Charles—Good heavens! there is my father at his window,” she said, repulsing him who leaned forward to kiss her.

She ran under the archway. Charles followed. At the foot of the staircase she opened the swing-door and, scarcely knowing where she was going, reached the corner near Nanon’s den, in the darkest end of the passage. There Charles caught her hand and drew her to his heart. Passing his arm about her waist, he made her lean upon him. Eugenie no longer resisted; she received and gave the purest, sweetest, most unreserved of kisses.

“Dear Eugenie, a cousin is better than a brother, for he can marry you,” said Charles.

“So be it!” cried Nanon, opening the door of her lair.

The two lovers, alarmed, fled into the hall, where Eugenie took up her work and Charles began to read the litanies of the Virgin in Madame Grandet’s prayer-book.

“Mercy!” cried Nanon, “now they’re saying their prayers.”

As soon as Charles announced his immediate departure, Grandet bestirred himself to show interest. He became liberal of what cost him nothing; took pains to find a packer; declared the man asked too much for his cases; insisted on making them himself out of old planks; got up early to plane and nail strips into strong cases in which he packed all Charles’s effects; he also sent them by boat down the Loire, insured them, and got them to Nantes in time.

After the kiss in the passage, hours fled for Eugenie with frightful rapidity. Sometimes she thought of following him. Those who have known that most endearing of passions—the one shortened by time, age, mortal illness, and human chances—will understand her tortures. She wept as she walked in the garden, now so narrow; indeed the court, the house, the town seemed narrow. She launched in thought upon the wide expanse of ocean he was about to traverse. At last the eve of his departure came. That morning, in the absence of Grandet and Nanon, the case containing the two portraits was solemnly installed in the only drawer of the old cabinet that could be locked, where the now empty velvet purse lay. This deposit was not made without many tears and kisses. When Eugenie placed the key within her bosom she had no courage to forbid the kiss with which Charles sealed the act.

“It shall never leave that place, my friend,” she said.

“Then my heart will be always there.”

“Ah! Charles, it is not right,” she said, as though blaming him.

“Are we not married?” he said. “I have thy promise—then take mine.”

“Thine; I am thine forever!” they each said, repeating the words twice.

No promise made upon this earth was ever purer. The innocent sincerity of Eugenie sanctified for a moment the young man’s love.

On the morrow the breakfast was sad. Nanon herself, despite the gold-embroidered robe and Jeannette cross bestowed by Charles, had tears in her eyes.

“The poor dear monsieur who is going on the seas—oh, may God guide him!”

At half-past ten the whole family started to escort Charles to the diligence for Nantes. Nanon let loose the dog, locked the door, and insisted on carrying the young man’s carpet-bag. All the tradesmen in the tortuous old street were on their sills to watch the procession, which was joined in the market-place by Maitre Cruchot.

“Eugenie, be sure you don’t cry,” said her mother.

“Nephew,” said Grandet in the doorway of the inn from which the coach started, kissing Charles on both cheeks, “depart poor, return rich; you will find the honor of your father safe. I answer for that myself, I—Grandet; for it will only depend on you to—”

“Ah! my uncle, you soften the bitterness of my departure. Is it not the best gift that you could make me?”

Not understanding his uncle’s words which he had interrupted, Charles shed tears of gratitude upon the tanned cheeks of the old miser, while Eugenie pressed her cousin’s and father’s hands with all her strength. The notary smiled, admiring the sly speech of the old man, which he alone had understood. The family stood about the coach until it started; then as it disappeared on the bridge and its rumble grew fainter, Grandet said:

“Good-by to you!”

Happily no one but Maitre Cruchot heard the exclamation. Eugenie and her mother had gone to a corner of the quay from which they could still see the diligence and wave their white handkerchiefs, to which Charles answered by displaying his.

“Ah! mother, would that I had the power of God for a single moment,” said Eugenie, when she could no longer see his handkerchief.

Not to interrupt the current of events about the Grandet family, it is necessary to glance at the operations the goodman carried on in Paris through Monsieur des Grassins. A month after the latter’s departure from Saumur, Grandet became possessed of a certificate of a hundred thousand francs a year from his investment in the Funds, bought at eighty francs net. The particulars revealed at his death by the inventory of his property threw no light on the methods his suspicious nature used to remit the price and receive the certificate. Maitre Cruchot thought that Nanon, unknown to herself, was the trusty instrument by which the money was transported; about this time she was absent five days under pretext of putting things to rights at Froidfond—as if the goodman were capable of leaving anything out of order!

In all that concerned Guillaume Grandet’s house the old cooper’s intentions were fulfilled. The Bank of France affords exact information about large fortunes. The names des Grassins and Felix Grandet of Saumur were well known there; they enjoyed the esteem of financial celebrities whose wealth comes from unencumbered territorial possessions. The Saumur banker’s arrival to honorably liquidate Guillaume Grandet of Paris averted the shame of protested notes. The seals on the property were taken off in presence of the creditors, and the notary employed by Grandet began the inventory. Des Grassins called a meeting of the creditors, who unanimously elected him, conjointly with Francois Keller, head of a rich banking-house and a principal interested party, as liquidators, with full power to protect family honor and claimants’ interests. The credit of Grandet of Saumur and the hopes he diffused through des Grassins facilitated the transactions. Not a single creditor proved recalcitrant; each said confidently, “Grandet of Saumur will pay.”

Six months went by. Parisians redeemed the notes as they fell due and kept them locked. First result sought by the old cooper! Nine months after the preliminary meeting, the two liquidators distributed forty-seven per cent to each creditor on his claim. This amount was obtained by selling securities and property of the late Guillaume Grandet and was paid with scrupulous fidelity. Unimpeachable integrity was shown. The creditors acknowledged the remarkable honor displayed by the Grandets. When praises circulated, the creditors asked for the rest of their money. It became necessary to write a collective letter to Grandet of Saumur.

“Here it comes!” said the old man as he threw the letter into the fire. “Patience, my good friends!”

In answer, Grandet demanded that all vouchers for claims against his brother’s estate be deposited with a notary, together with acquittances for the forty-seven per cent already paid; he made this demand under pretense of sifting accounts and finding the estate’s exact condition. It roused difficulties. Generally, the creditor is a species of maniac, ready to agree one day, breathe fire the next, later grow amicable and easy-going. To-day his wife is good-humored, his baby cuts its first tooth, and he is determined not to lose a sou; on the morrow it rains and he is gloomy; he agrees to anything that ends the affair; on the third day he demands guarantees; by the month's end he wants his debtor’s head and feels like an executioner. Grandet had studied creditors’ atmospheric variations, and his brother’s creditors justified his calculations. Some were angry and refused to give in their vouchers.

“Very good; so much the better,” said Grandet, rubbing his hands over des Grassins’ letter.

Others agreed only if their rights were fully guaranteed; they renounced none and even reserved the power to compel failure. This began long correspondence, ending with Grandet of Saumur agreeing to all conditions. By this concession placable creditors were able to bring the dissatisfied to reason. The deposit was then made, but not without complaints.

“Your goodman,” they said to des Grassins, “is tricking us.”

Twenty-three months after Guillaume Grandet’s death many creditors, swept away by more pressing Paris business, had forgotten their claims or only thought to say: “I begin to believe that forty-seven per cent is all I shall ever get.”

The old cooper had calculated on the power of time, which, as he used to say, is a pretty good devil. By the end of the third year des Grassins wrote that he had brought creditors to accept ten per cent on the two million four hundred thousand francs still due by the house of Grandet. Grandet answered that the notary and broker whose failures caused his brother’s death were still living, that they might have recovered their credit, and should be sued to recover something toward the deficit.

By the end of the fourth year liabilities were definitively estimated at twelve hundred thousand francs. Many negotiations took place between creditors and liquidators, and between liquidators and Grandet. To make a long story short, anxious to get out of the affair, about the ninth month of the fourth year Grandet told the liquidators his nephew had made a fortune in the Indies and was disposed to pay his father’s debts in full; therefore he could not settle without consulting him; he had written and awaited an answer. The creditors were held in check until the middle of the fifth year by the words “payment in full,” which the wily miser put out from time to time, smiling and cursing, “Those Parisians!”

But the creditors were reserved for an unprecedented fate in commerce. When events bring them back into notice, they will be found still in the position Grandet had resolved from the first.

As soon as the Funds reached a hundred and fifteen, Pere Grandet sold out and withdrew two million four hundred thousand francs in gold, to which he added the six hundred thousand francs compound interest from the capital. Des Grassins lived in Paris. First he had been made a deputy; then he became infatuated (father of a family though he was, and bored by provincial Saumur) with a pretty actress at the Theatre de Madame, known as Florine, and relapsed into old army habits. His conduct was considered profoundly immoral in Saumur. His wife was fortunate in having her property settled on herself and in possessing ability to keep up the Saumur banking-house, which was managed in her name and repaired the breach caused by her husband’s extravagance. The Cruchotines made much talk about the quasi-widow’s false position; she married her daughter badly and gave up hope of an alliance between Eugenie Grandet and her son. Adolphe joined his father in Paris and became, it was said, worthless. The Cruchots triumphed.

“Your husband hasn’t common sense,” said Grandet as he lent Madame des Grassins money on a note securely endorsed. “I am very sorry for you, for you are a good little woman.”

“Ah, monsieur,” said the poor lady, “who could have believed that when he left Saumur to go to Paris on your business he was going to his ruin?”

“Heaven is my witness, madame, that up to the last moment I did all I could to prevent him from going. Monsieur le president was most anxious to take his place; but he was determined to go, and now we all see why.”

In this way Grandet made it plain he was under no obligation to des Grassins.

In all situations women have more cause for suffering than men, and they suffer more. Man has strength and the power of exercising it; he acts, moves, thinks, occupies himself; he looks ahead, and sees consolation in the future. It was thus with Charles. But the woman stays at home; she is always face to face with the grief from which nothing distracts her; she goes down to the abyss which yawns before her, measures it, and often fills it with her tears and prayers. Thus did Eugenie. To feel, to love, to suffer, to devote herself—is not this the sum of woman’s life? Eugenie was to be in all things a woman, except in the one thing that consoles for all. Her happiness, picked up like nails scattered on a wall—to use Bossuet’s simile—would never so much as fill the hollow of her hand. Sorrows are never long in coming; for her they came soon. The day after Charles’s departure the house resumed its ordinary aspect in the eyes of all except Eugenie, to whom it grew suddenly empty. She wished, if it could be done unknown to her father, that Charles’s room be kept as he had left it. Madame Grandet and Nanon were willing accomplices in this statu quo.

“Who knows but he may come back sooner than we think?” she said.

“Ah, don’t I wish I could see him back!” answered Nanon. “I took to him! He was such a dear, sweet young man—pretty too, with his curly hair.” Eugenie looked at Nanon. “Holy Virgin! don’t look at me that way, mademoiselle; your eyes are like those of a lost soul.”

From that day the beauty of Mademoiselle Grandet took a new character. The solemn thoughts of love which filled her soul, and the dignity of the woman beloved, gave her features an illumination such as painters render by a halo. Before her cousin, Eugenie might be compared to the Virgin before the conception; after he had gone, she was like the Virgin Mother—she had given birth to love.

Returning from Mass the morning after Charles’s departure—having vowed to hear it daily—Eugenie bought a map of the world which she nailed beside her looking-glass, that she might follow her cousin westward, put herself day by day into the ship that bore him, and ask a thousand questions—“Art thou well? Dost thou suffer? Dost thou think of me when the star, whose beauty and usefulness thou hast taught me to know, shines upon thee?” In the mornings she sat pensive beneath the walnut-tree on the worm-eaten bench covered with gray lichens where they had said so many precious things, where they had built pretty castles of a future home. She thought of the future as she looked upward to the bit of sky the high walls allowed; then she turned her eyes to the angle where the sun crept, and to the roof above the room in which he had slept. Hers was the solitary, persistent love that glides into every thought and becomes the tissue of life. When Pere Grandet’s would-be friends came in the evening for their game at cards, she was gay and dissembling; but all the morning she talked of Charles with her mother and Nanon. Nanon had brought herself to pity her young mistress without failing in duty to the old master, and she would say to Eugenie,—

“If I had a man for myself I’d—I'd follow him to hell; yes, I’d exterminate myself for him; but I’ve none. I shall die and never know what life is. Would you believe, mamz’elle, that old Cornoiller (a good fellow all the same) is always round my petticoats for the sake of my money—just like the rats who come smelling after the master’s cheese and paying court to you? I see it all; I’ve got a shrewd eye, though I am as big as a steeple. Well, mamz’elle, it pleases me, but it isn’t love.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Two months passed. Domestic life, once monotonous, quickened with the intense interest of a secret that bound the women together. For them Charles lived beneath the grim gray rafters of the hall. Night and morning Eugenie opened the dressing-case and gazed at her aunt’s portrait. One Sunday morning her mother discovered her absorbed in searching Charles’s features in his mother’s face. Madame Grandet was then for the first time drawn into the terrible secret: Charles had been given Eugenie’s treasure.

“You gave him all!” cried the poor mother, terrified. “What will you say to your father on New Year’s Day when he asks to see your gold?”

Eugenie’s eyes fixed, and the two women lived through mortal terror for more than half the morning. So troubled they missed high Mass and only went to the military service. In three days 1819 would end. In three days a terrible drama would begin — a bourgeois tragedy without poison or dagger, but, regarding the actors, more cruel than any fabled horror in the house of Atreus.

“What will become of us?” said Madame Grandet, letting her knitting fall. For two months she had been anxious; the woollen sleeves she needed were unfinished. For want of those sleeves, a chill seized her amid a sweat caused by a terrible explosion of anger from her husband.

“I thought, my poor child, if you had confided in me we could have written to Monsieur des Grassins in Paris. He might have sent us gold pieces like yours; though Grandet knows them all, perhaps—”

“Where could we have got the money?”

“I would have pledged my property. Besides, Monsieur des Grassins would have—”

“It is too late,” said Eugenie in a broken voice. “Tomorrow morning we must go and wish him a happy New Year in his chamber.”

“But, my daughter, why not consult the Cruchots?”

“No, no; it would be delivering me up to them and putting ourselves in their power. Besides, I have chosen my course. I have done right and repent nothing. God will protect me. His will be done! Ah! mother, if you had read his letter, you would have thought only of him.”

January 1, 1820 dawned. The winter of 1819–1820 was one of the coldest of the epoch; snow lay thick on the roofs. The fear of Grandet’s inspection suggested a natural excuse to escape the formal entrance into his chamber.

Madame Grandet called when she heard him stirring. “Grandet, will you let Nanon light a fire here for me? The cold is so sharp I am freezing. Eugenie shall come and dress here; the poor child might get ill dressing in her cold room. Then we will go and wish you a happy New Year by the hall fire.”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta, what a tongue! a pretty way to begin the new year, Madame Grandet!” he answered. After a pause he consented, with a gruff jest about the Bertellieres, coughed, and entered his wife’s room fully dressed. “Yes, it is cold enough to freeze you. We shall have a fine breakfast, wife. Des Grassins has sent me a truffled pate-de-foie-gras! I am going now to get it at the coach-office. There’ll be a double napoleon for Eugenie in the package,” he whispered. “I have no gold left, wife. I had a few stray pieces—but I had to let them go in business.”

He kissed her on the forehead and went out. The two women breathed a little.

Nanon entered to light the fire. “First he said, ‘Good-morning; happy New Year, you big fool! Go and light my wife’s fire, she’s cold’; and then he gave me a six-franc piece, which isn’t worn one bit. Oh, the kind man! He is a good man, that’s a fact. He’s getting soft, improving with time, like your ratafia! He’s a good, good man—”

Grandet’s joy sprang from the complete success of his speculation. Monsieur des Grassins, after deducting what Grandet owed for discounting a hundred and fifty thousand francs in Dutch notes and for the surplus he had advanced to make up the sum required for an investment yielding a hundred thousand francs a year, had sent him thirty thousand francs in silver, the remainder of his first half-year’s interest, and informed him the Funds had risen. They were then quoted at eighty-nine; the shrewdest bought later at ninety-three. Grandet had thus gained twelve percent on his capital in two months; he had simplified accounts and would henceforth receive fifty thousand francs interest every six months without taxes or repair costs. He understood investing in public securities; at the end of five years he found himself master of six million francs capital, which increased without much effort and, joined to his land, made him enormously wealthy. The six francs for Nanon were perhaps the reward for some small service rendered unknowingly.

“Oh! oh! where’s Pere Grandet going? He has been scurrying about since sunrise,” said the tradespeople as they opened shops. When they saw him returning from the wharf followed by a porter with a barrow laden with sacks, they commented on his gold. “Silver sous,” whispered the porter. “If you want me to take care of you, keep your tongue between your teeth,” Grandet warned as they reached the door. He handed twenty sous to someone and bade them be off. The sacks were carried to his inner room, where he shut himself in. “When breakfast is ready, knock on the wall,” he said, then disappeared.

The family breakfasted at ten. “Your father will not ask to see your gold downstairs,” Madame Grandet hoped after Mass. “You must pretend to be very chilly. We may have time to replace the treasure before your fête-day.”

Grandet came down thinking of his speculation and wondering how to metamorphose his Parisian silver into solid gold; he resolved to invest everything until the Funds reached par. Fatal reverie for Eugenie. As soon as he came in, the two women wished him a happy New Year—his daughter by putting her arms round his neck and caressing him; Madame Grandet gravely.

“Ha! ha! my child,” he said, kissing both her cheeks. “I work for you, don’t you see? I think of your happiness. Must have money to be happy. Here! there’s a new napoleon for you. I sent to Paris for it. On my word of honor, it’s all the gold I have; you are the only one that has any gold. I want to see your gold, little one.”

“Oh! it is too cold; let us have breakfast,” Eugenie answered.

“Well, after breakfast, then; it will help digestion. That fat des Grassins sent me the pate. Eat as much as you like, my children, it costs nothing. Des Grassins is getting along very well. I am satisfied with him. The old fish is doing Charles a good service, and gratis too. He is making a very good settlement of that poor deceased Grandet’s business. Hoo! hoo!” he muttered with his mouth full, “how good it is! Eat, wife; that will feed you for at least two days.”

“I am not hungry. I am very poorly; you know that.”

“Ah, bah! you can stuff yourself as full as you please without danger, you’re a Bertelliere; they are all hearty. You are a bit yellow, that’s true; but I like yellow, myself.”

The expectation of ignominious and public scrutiny is perhaps less horrible to a condemned criminal than the anticipation of what was coming after breakfast to Madame Grandet and Eugenie. The more gleefully the old man talked and ate, the more their hearts shrank. Eugenie, however, had an inward prop—she drew strength through love.

“For him! for him!” she cried within her, “I would die a thousand deaths.”

At this thought she shot a glance at her mother that flamed with courage.

“Clear away,” Grandet said to Nanon about eleven, “but leave the table. We can spread your little treasure upon it,” he said, looking at Eugenie. “Little? Faith! no; it isn’t little. You possess, in actual value, five thousand nine hundred and fifty-nine francs and the forty I gave you just now. That makes six thousand francs, less one. Well, now see here, little one! I’ll give you that one franc to make up the round number. Hey! what are you listening for, Nanon? Mind your own business; go and do your work.”

Nanon disappeared.

“Now listen, Eugenie; you must give me back your gold. You won’t refuse your father, my little girl, hein?”

The two women were dumb.

“I have no gold myself. I had some, but it is all gone. I’ll give you in return six thousand francs in livres, and you are to put them just where I tell you. You mustn’t think anything more about your ‘dozen.’ When I marry you (which will be soon) I shall get you a husband who can give you the finest ‘dozen’ ever seen in the provinces. Now attend to me, little girl. There’s a fine chance for you; you can put your six thousand francs into government funds, and you will receive every six months nearly two hundred francs interest, without taxes, or repairs, or frost, or hail, or floods, or anything else to swallow up the money. Perhaps you don’t like to part with your gold, hey, my girl? Never mind, bring it to me all the same. I’ll get you some more like it—like those Dutch coins and the portugaises, the rupees of Mogul, and the genovines—I’ll give you some on your fête-days, and in three years you’ll have got back half your little treasure. What’s that you say? Look up, now. Come, go and get it, the precious metal. You ought to kiss me on the eyelids for telling you the secrets and the mysteries of the life and death of money. Yes, silver and gold live and swarm like men; they come, and go, and sweat, and multiply—”

Eugenie rose; but after a few steps toward the door she turned abruptly, looked her father in the face, and said,—

“I have not got my gold.”

“You have not got your gold!” cried Grandet, starting up like a horse that hears a cannon.

“No, I have not got it.”

“You are mistaken, Eugenie.”

“No.”

“By the shears of my father!”

Whenever the old man swore that oath the rafters trembled.

“Holy Virgin! Madame is turning pale,” cried Nanon.

“Grandet, your anger will kill me,” said the poor mother.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta! nonsense; you never die in your family! Eugenie, what have you done with your gold?” he cried, rushing upon her.

“Monsieur,” said the daughter, falling at Madame Grandet’s knees, “my mother is ill. Look at her; do not kill her.”

Grandet, frightened by his wife’s pallor, was momentarily softened. “Nanon, help me to bed,” she murmured; “I am dying—” Nanon gave her an arm, Eugenie another; with difficulty they got her upstairs; she fell with exhaustion at every step. Grandet remained. In a few moments he went up six or eight stairs and called,—

“Eugenie, when your mother is in bed, come down.”

“Yes, father.”

She soon came after reassuring her mother.

“My daughter,” Grandet said, “you will now tell me what you have done with your gold.”

“My father, if you make me presents of which I am not the sole mistress, take them back,” she answered coldly, picking up the napoleon from the chimney-piece and offering it to him.

Grandet seized the coin and slipped it into his breeches pocket. “I shall certainly never give you anything again. Not so much as that!” he said, clicking his thumb-nail against a front tooth. “Do you dare despise your father? have you no confidence in him? Don’t you know what a father is? If he is nothing for you, he is nothing at all. Where is your gold?”

“Father, I love and respect you, in spite of your anger; but I humbly ask you to remember I am twenty-three. You have told me I have attained my majority, and I do not forget it. I have used my money as I chose, and you may be sure it was put to a good use—”

“What use?”

“That is an inviolable secret,” she answered. “Have you no secrets?”

“I am the head of the family; I have my own affairs.”

“And this is mine.”

“It must be something bad if you can’t tell it to your father, Mademoiselle Grandet.”

“It is good, and I cannot tell it to my father.”

“At least you can tell me when you parted with your gold?”

Eugenie shook her head.

“You had it on your birthday, hein?”

She repeated the negative sign, crafty through love as he was through avarice.

“Was there ever such obstinacy! It’s a theft,” cried Grandet, his voice rising through the house. “What! here, in my own home, under my very eyes, somebody has taken your gold—the only gold we have—and I’m not to know who has got it! Gold is a precious thing. Virtuous girls go wrong sometimes, and give—I don’t know what; they do it among the great people, and even among the bourgeoisie. But give their gold—for you have given it to some one, hein?”

Eugenie was silent and impassive.

“Was there ever such a daughter? Is it possible that I am your father? If you have invested it anywhere, you must have a receipt—”

“Was I free—yes or no—to do as I would with my own? Was it not mine?”

“You are a child.”

“Of age.”

Dumbfounded by his daughter’s logic, Grandet turned pale and stamped. When at last he found words, he cried: “Serpent! Cursed girl! Ah, deceitful creature! You know I love you, and you take advantage of it. She’d cut her father’s throat! Good God! you’ve given our fortune to that ne’er-do-well—that dandy with morocco boots! By the shears of my father! I can’t disinherit you, but I curse you—you and your cousin and your children! Nothing good will come of it! Do you hear? If it was to Charles—but, no; it’s impossible. What! has that wretched fellow robbed me?—”

He looked at his daughter, who continued cold and silent.

“She won’t stir; she won’t flinch! She’s more Grandet than I’m Grandet! Ha! you have not given your gold for nothing? Come, speak the truth!”

Eugenie looked at her father with a sarcastic expression that stung him.

“Eugenie, you are here, in my house—in your father’s house. If you wish to stay here, you must submit to me. The priests tell you to obey me.” Eugenie bowed. “You affront me in all I hold most dear. I will not see you again until you submit. Go to your chamber. You will stay there till I give you permission to leave it. Nanon will bring you bread and water. You hear me—go!”

Eugenie burst into tears and fled to her mother. Grandet, after marching two or three times round the garden in the snow without heeding the cold, suspected his daughter had gone to her mother; only too happy to find her disobedient, he climbed the stairs with the agility of a cat and appeared in Madame Grandet’s room just as she was stroking Eugenie’s hair, the girl’s face hidden on her motherly bosom.

“Be comforted, my poor child,” she was saying; “your father will get over it.”

“She has no father!” said the old man. “Can it be you and I, Madame Grandet, who have given birth to such a disobedient child? A fine education—religious, too! Well! why are you not in your chamber? Come, to prison, to prison, mademoiselle!”

“Would you deprive me of my daughter, monsieur?” said Madame Grandet, now red with fever.

“If you want to keep her, carry her off! Clear out—out of my house, both of you! Thunder! where is the gold? what’s become of the gold?”

Eugenie rose, looked proudly at her father, and withdrew to her room. Grandet turned the key.

“Nanon,” he cried, “put out the fire in the hall.”

Then he sat down in an armchair beside his wife’s fire and said to her,—

“Undoubtedly she has given the gold to that miserable seducer, Charles, who only wanted our money.”

“I knew nothing about it,” she answered, turning away from his savage glances. “I suffer so much from your violence that I shall never leave this room, if I trust my presentiments, till I am carried out in my coffin. You ought to have spared me this suffering, monsieur—you, to whom I have caused no pain, I think. Your daughter loves you. I believe her to be as innocent as an unborn babe. Do not make her wretched. Revoke your sentence. The cold is very severe; you may give her some serious illness.”

“I will not see her, neither will I speak to her. She shall stay in her room, on bread and water, until she submits to her father. What the devil! shouldn’t a father know where the gold in his house has gone to? She owned the only rupees in France, perhaps, and the Dutch ducats and the genovines—”

“Monsieur, Eugenie is our only child; and even if she had thrown them into the water—”

“Into the water!” cried her husband; “into the water! You are crazy, Madame Grandet! What I have said is said; you know that well enough. If you want peace in this household, make your daughter confess, pump it out of her. Women understand how to do that better than we do. Whatever she has done, I sha’n’t eat her. Is she afraid of me? Even if she has plastered Charles with gold from head to foot, he is on the high seas, and nobody can get at him, hein!”

“But, monsieur—” Excited by the crisis and by her daughter’s fate, Madame Grandet suddenly observed a frightful movement of her husband’s wen, and, in replying, she changed her speech without changing tone—“But, monsieur, I have not more influence over her than you have. She has said nothing to me; she takes after you.”

“Tut, tut! Your tongue is hung in the middle this morning. Ta, ta, ta, ta! You are setting me at defiance, I do believe. I daresay you are in league with her.”

He looked fixedly at his wife.

“Monsieur Grandet, if you wish to kill me, you have only to go on like this. I tell you, monsieur—and if it were to cost me my life, I would say it—you do wrong by your daughter; she is more in the right than you are. That money belonged to her; she is incapable of making any but a good use of it, and God alone has the right to know our good deeds. Monsieur, I implore you, take Eugenie back into favor; forgive her. If you do this you will lessen the injury your anger has done me; perhaps you will save my life. My daughter! oh, monsieur, give me back my daughter!”

“I shall decamp,” he said; “the house is not habitable. A mother and daughter talking and arguing like that! Broooouh! Pouah! A fine New Year’s present you’ve made me, Eugenie.” He lectured on Charles’s worth, his morocco boots and airs, and the ruin that would come.

When the street-door was shut, Eugenie came out of her room and went to her mother.

“What courage you have had for your daughter’s sake!” she said.

“Ah! my child, see where forbidden things may lead us. You forced me to tell a lie.”

“I will ask God to punish only me.”

“Is it true,” cried Nanon, rushing in alarm, “that mademoiselle is to be kept on bread and water for the rest of her life?”

“What does that signify, Nanon?” said Eugenie tranquilly.

“Goodness! do you suppose I’ll eat frippe when the daughter of the house is eating dry bread? No, no!”

“Don’t say a word about all this, Nanon,” Eugenie begged.

“I’ll be as mute as a fish; but you’ll see!”

Grandet dined alone for the first time in twenty-four years.

“So you’re a widower, monsieur,” said Nanon; “it must be disagreeable to be a widower with two women in the house.”

“I did not speak to you. Hold your jaw, or I’ll turn you off! What is that I hear boiling in your saucepan on the stove?”

“It is grease I’m trying out.”

“There will be some company tonight. Light the fire.”

The Cruchots, Madame des Grassins, and her son arrived at the usual hour of eight, surprised to see neither Madame Grandet nor her daughter.

“My wife is not very well, and Eugenie is with her,” said the old wine-grower, whose face betrayed no emotion.

After an hour of idle conversation, Madame des Grassins, who had gone up to see Madame Grandet, came down, and all asked, “How is Madame Grandet?”

“Not at all well,” she answered; “her condition seems really alarming. At her age you ought to take every precaution, Papa Grandet.”

“We’ll see about it,” said the old man absently.

They all wished him good-night. When the Cruchots reached the street, Madame des Grassins said, “There is something going on at the Grandets. The mother is very ill without her knowing it. The girl’s eyes are red, as if she had been crying all day. Can they be trying to marry her against her will?”

When Grandet had gone to bed Nanon came softly to Eugenie’s room in her stockinged feet and showed her a pate baked in a saucepan.

“See, mademoiselle,” said the good soul, “Cornoiller gave me a hare. You eat so little that this pate will last you a full week; in such frosty weather it won’t spoil. You sha’n’t live on dry bread, I’m determined; it isn’t wholesome.”

“Poor Nanon!” said Eugenie, pressing her hand.

“I’ve made it downright good and dainty, and he never found it out. I bought the lard and the spices out of my six francs: I’m the mistress of my own money”; and she disappeared rapidly, fancying she heard Grandet.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For several months the old wine-grower visited his wife’s room constantly but never mentioned or saw his daughter. Madame Grandet stayed in her chamber and daily grew worse. Nothing softened the old man; he remained harsh and cold as granite. He continued his business but spoke less, grew more obdurate in transactions, and often made mistakes in his accounts.

“Something is going on at the Grandets,” said the Grassinists and the Cruchotines. “What has happened in the Grandet family?” became a fixed question at Saumur’s little evening-parties. Eugenie went to Mass escorted by Nanon. If Madame des Grassins spoke to her after church she answered evasively. After two months it could no longer be concealed that Eugenie was confined to her room. Pretexts failed; at last, by an unknown betrayer, the town learned that since New Year’s Day Mademoiselle Grandet had been kept in her room without fire, on bread and water, by her father’s orders, and that Nanon cooked little dainties and took them to her secretly at night. It was even known that Eugenie could not see or care for her mother except when her father was out.

Grandet’s conduct was severely condemned. Remembering his treachery and hardness, the town more or less outlawed him. Passersby pointed and muttered. When his daughter went to Mass or Vespers, inhabitants ran to windows to study the bearing of the rich heiress, whose face showed angelic gentleness and melancholy. Her imprisonment and her father’s condemnation seemed nothing to her; she had her little bench, the garden, the corner of the wall. Pious and pure before God, her conscience and love helped her bear her father’s wrath patiently.

Her chief grief was her mother’s failing health. Madame Grandet, gentle and tender, beautified by a religious serenity, was perishing. Eugenie often reproached herself as the innocent cause of the slow malady wasting her away; this remorse bound her closer to her love. Every morning, as soon as her father left, she went to her mother’s bedside; Nanon brought her breakfast. The poor girl, sad and suffering over her mother’s pain, would turn her face to the old servant with a mute, weeping gesture, yet not daring to speak of her cousin. Madame Grandet first found courage to say, “Where is he? Why does he not write?”

“Let us think about him, mother, but not speak of him. You are ill—you, before all.”

“All” meant “him.”

“My child,” said Madame Grandet, “I do not wish to live. God protects me and enables me to look with joy to the end of my misery.”

Every utterance of this woman was unfalteringly pious and Christian. During the first months of the year, when her husband came to breakfast and paced the room, she would speak a few religious words with angelic sweetness and the firmness of one who faces death courageously.

“Monsieur, I thank you for the interest you take in my health,” she would answer his commonplace inquiries; “but if you really desire to render my last moments less bitter and ease my grief, take back your daughter: be a Christian, a husband, and a father.”

Grandet would sit by the bed with the air of a man who sees rain coming and shelters himself till it is over. Absolute forgetfulness of his daughter seemed graven on his stony brow and closed lips. He was unmoved by the tears that flowed down his unhappy wife’s cheeks as she listened to his meaningless answers.

“May God pardon you,” she said, “even as I pardon you! You will some day stand in need of mercy.”

Since Madame Grandet’s illness he had not dared to use his terrible “Ta, ta, ta, ta!” Yet his despotic nature was not disarmed by this angel of gentleness, whose homely features were slowly transfigured by inward light. If his language ceased to be contemptuous, an imperturbable silence, which saved his dignity as master, took its place.

When Nanon appeared in the market, many quips about the master whistled in her ears; but however loudly public opinion condemned Monsieur Grandet, the old servant defended him for the honor of the family.

“Well!” she would say, “don’t we all get hard as we grow old? Why shouldn’t he? Stop telling lies. Mademoiselle lives like a queen. She’s alone, that’s true; but she likes it. Besides, my masters have good reasons.”

At last, toward the end of spring, Madame Grandet, worn out by grief and unsuccessful in reconciling father and daughter, confided her troubles to the Cruchots.

“Keep a girl of twenty-three on bread and water!” cried Monsieur de Bonfons. “That constitutes wrongful cruelty; she can contest—”

“Come, nephew, spare us your legal jargon,” said the notary. “Set your mind at ease, madame; I will put a stop to such treatment to-morrow.”

Eugenie, hearing herself mentioned, came out of her room.

“Gentlemen,” she said proudly, “I beg you not to interfere. My father is master in his own house. As long as I live under his roof I must obey him. His conduct is not subject to the world’s approbation or disapprobation; he is accountable to God only. I appeal to your friendship to keep total silence in this affair. To blame my father is to attack our family honor. I thank you for your interest; you will do me an additional service if you put a stop to the offensive rumors current in the town.”

“She is right,” said Madame Grandet.

“Mademoiselle, the best way to stop such rumors is to procure your liberty,” answered the notary respectfully, struck by the beauty which seclusion, melancholy, and love had stamped upon her face.

“Well, my daughter, let Monsieur Cruchot manage the matter if he is so sure of success. If you wish to see me happy for my few remaining days, you must, at any cost, be reconciled to your father.”

On the morrow Grandet, following his habit since Eugenie’s confinement, took walks in the little garden at the hour when Eugenie brushed and arranged her hair. He hid behind the walnut trunk sometimes to watch her movements, hesitating between obstinacy and his natural desire to embrace his child. Sometimes he sat on the rotten bench where Charles and Eugenie had vowed eternal love; she, too, looked at her father secretly in the mirror. If he rose and continued his walk, she sat obligingly at the window and looked at the angle of the wall where pale flowers hung and Venus-hair grew.

Maitre Cruchot came early and found the old wine-grower sitting on the little bench, his back against the division wall, watching his daughter.

“What may you want, Maitre Cruchot?” he said.

“I came to speak on business.”

“Ah! have you brought some gold in exchange for my silver?”

“No, not about money; it is about your daughter Eugenie. All the town is talking of her and you.”

“What does the town meddle for? A man’s house is his castle.”

“Very true; but your wife is very ill. You ought to consult Monsieur Bergerin; she may die. If she dies without proper care you will not be at ease.”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta! these doctors, if they once get their foot in—”

“You have the right to choose your course. Besides, there is another thing which may have serious results for you. After your wife’s death, your daughter can claim a division of property; you enjoy your wife’s estate only during her lifetime. At her death your daughter can force a partition, perhaps force you to sell Froidfond. In short, she is her mother’s heir.”

These words fell like a thunderbolt on Grandet, who had not thought of legal division.

“Therefore I advise you to treat her kindly,” added Cruchot.

“But do you know what she has done, Cruchot?”

“What?”

“She has given away her gold!”

“Well, wasn’t it hers?” said the notary.

“They all tell me that!” cried the old man, letting his arms fall in despair. “Are you going—for a mere nothing—to put obstacles in the way of concessions which you will be obliged to ask from your daughter as soon as her mother dies?”

“Do you call six thousand francs a mere nothing?” said Grandet. His agitation was tragic.

“Hey! my old friend, do you know what the inventory of your wife’s property will cost, if Eugenie demands division? Two, three, four thousand francs, perhaps! The property might have to be sold at auction. Instead of that, if you are on good terms—”

“By the shears of my father!” cried Grandet, turning pale and sitting down. “We will see about it, Cruchot.”

After a silence full of anguish he said, “Life is very hard! It has many griefs! Cruchot, you would not deceive me? Swear to me upon your honor that all you’ve told me is legally true. Show me the law; I must see the law!”

“My poor friend,” said the notary, “don’t I know my own business?”

“Then it is true! I am robbed, betrayed, killed, destroyed by my own daughter!”

“It is true that your daughter is her mother’s heir.”

“Why do we have children? Ah! my wife, I love her! Luckily she’s sound and healthy; she’s a Bertelliere.”

“She has not a month to live.”

Grandet struck his forehead, came back, cast a dreadful look at Cruchot, and said, “What can be done?”

“Eugenie can relinquish her claim to her mother’s property. Should she do this you would not disinherit her, I presume; but if you want such a settlement, you must not treat her harshly. What I am telling you is against my own interests. What do I live by, if it isn’t liquidations, inventories, conveyances, divisions of property?—”

“We’ll see! Don’t let’s talk any more about it; it wrings my vitals. Have you received any gold?”

“No; but I have a few old louis, a dozen or so, which you may have. My good friend, make it up with Eugenie. Don’t you know all Saumur is pelting you with stones?”

“The scoundrels!”

“Come, the Funds are at ninety-nine. Do be satisfied for once in your life.”

“At ninety-nine! Are they, Cruchot?”

“Yes.”

“Hey, hey! Ninety-nine!” cried the old man, and, agitated, he went up to his wife’s room and said, “Come, mother, you may have your daughter to spend the day with you. I’m going to Froidfond. Enjoy yourselves, both of you. This is our wedding-day, wife. See! here are sixty francs for your altar at the Fete-Dieu; you’ve wanted one for a long time. Come, cheer up, enjoy yourself, and get well! Hurrah for happiness!”

He threw ten silver pieces upon the bed, kissed his wife’s forehead, and crooned coaxing words.

“How can you think of receiving the God of mercy in your house when you refuse to forgive your daughter?” she said with feeling.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta!” said Grandet in a coaxing voice. “We’ll see about that.”

“Merciful heaven! Eugenie,” cried the mother, flushing with joy, “come and kiss your father; he forgives you!”

But the old man had disappeared. He was hurrying toward his vineyards, trying to arrange the confused ideas that assailed him. Grandet had entered his seventy-sixth year. During the last two years his avarice had increased; the sight and possession of gold had become a monomania. His despotic spirit had grown with his avarice, and to part with any control at his wife’s death seemed to him “against nature.” To declare his fortune to his daughter, to inventory his property for division—“it would be cutting my throat!” he cried aloud in a field where he pretended to examine a vine.

He returned to Saumur in time for dinner, resolved to unbend to Eugenie and coax her, that he might die regally, holding the reins of his millions so long as he had breath. As he entered the house stealthily, Eugenie had brought a beautiful dressing-case from the oak cabinet and placed it on her mother’s bed. Mother and daughter, in Grandet’s absence, allowed themselves the pleasure of looking for a likeness to Charles in the portrait of his mother.

“It is exactly his forehead and his mouth,” Eugenie was saying as the old man opened the door. At the look his glance cast upon the gold, Madame Grandet cried, “O God, have pity upon us!”

The old man sprang upon the box like a famished tiger. “What’s this?” he said, snatching the treasure and carrying it to the window. “Gold, good gold! All gold—it weighs two pounds! Ha, ha! Charles gave you that for your money, did he? Hein! Why didn’t you tell me so? It was a good bargain, little one! Yes, you are my daughter, I see that—” Eugenie trembled. “This came from Charles, of course, didn’t it?”

“Yes, father; it is not mine. It is a sacred trust.”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta! He took your fortune, and now you can get it back.”

“Father!”

Grandet took his knife to pry out some of the gold; he placed the dressing-case on a chair. Eugenie sprang forward to recover it; but her father, watching her and the treasure, pushed her back so violently that she fell upon her mother’s bed.

“Monsieur, monsieur!” cried the mother, lifting herself.

Grandet had opened his knife and was about to apply it to the gold.

“Father!” cried Eugenie, falling on her knees and clasping his hands, “father, in the name of all the saints and the Virgin! in the name of Christ who died upon the cross! in the name of your eternal salvation, father! for my life’s sake, father—do not touch that! It is neither yours nor mine. It is a trust placed in my hands by an unhappy relation: I must give it back to him uninjured!”

“If it is a trust, why were you looking at it? To look is as bad as touching it.”

“Father, don’t destroy it, or you will disgrace me! Father, do you hear?”

“Oh, have pity!” said the mother.

“Father!” cried Eugenie in so startling a voice that Nanon ran upstairs terrified. Eugenie sprang upon a knife that was close at hand.

“Well, what now?” said Grandet coldly, with a callous smile.

“Oh, you are killing me!” said the mother faintly.

“Father, if your knife so much as cuts a fragment of that gold, I will stab myself with this one! You have already driven my mother to her death; you will now kill your child! Do as you choose! Wound for wound!”

Grandet held his knife over the dressing-case and hesitated as he looked at his daughter.

“Are you capable of doing it, Eugenie?” he said.

“Yes, yes!” said the mother.

“She’ll do it if she says so!” cried Nanon. “Be reasonable, monsieur, for once in your life.”

The old man looked at the gold and then at his daughter alternately for an instant. Madame Grandet fainted.

“There! don’t you see, monsieur, that madame is dying?” cried Nanon.

“Come, come, my daughter, we won’t quarrel for a box! Here, take it!” he cried hastily, flinging the case upon the bed. “Nanon, go and fetch Monsieur Bergerin! Come, mother,” he added, kissing his wife’s hand, “it’s all over! There! we’ve made up—haven’t we, little one? No more dry bread; you shall have all you want—Ah, she opens her eyes! Well, mother, little mother, come! See, I’m kissing Eugenie! She loves her cousin, and she may marry him if she wants; she may keep his case. But don’t die, mother; live a long time yet, my poor wife! Come, try to move! Listen! you shall have the finest altar that ever was made in Saumur.”

“Oh, how can you treat your wife and daughter so!” said Madame Grandet weakly.

“I won’t do so again, never again,” cried her husband; “you shall see, my poor wife!” He went to his inner room and returned with a handful of louis, which he scattered on the bed. “Here, Eugenie! see, wife! all these are for you,” he said, fingering the coins. “Come, be happy, wife! feel better, get well; you sha’n’t want for anything, nor Eugenie either. Here’s a hundred louis d’or for her. You won’t give these away, will you, Eugenie, hein?”

Madame Grandet and her daughter looked at each other in astonishment.

“Take back your money, father; we ask for nothing but your affection.”

“Well, well, that’s right!” he said, pocketing the coins; “let’s be good friends! We will all go down to dinner to-day, and we’ll play loto every evening for two sous. You shall both be happy. Hey, wife?”

“Alas! I wish I could, if it would give you pleasure,” said the dying woman; “but I cannot rise.”

“Poor mother,” said Grandet, “you don’t know how I love you! and you too, my daughter!” He took her in his arms and kissed her. “Oh, how good it is to kiss a daughter when we have been angry with her! There, mother, don’t you see it’s all over now? Go and put that away, Eugenie. Go, don’t be afraid! I shall never speak of it again, never!”

Monsieur Bergerin, the celebrated doctor of Saumur, presently arrived. After an examination he told Grandet that his wife was very ill; but that perfect peace of mind, a generous diet, and great care might prolong her life until the autumn.

“Will all that cost much?” said the old man. “Will she need medicines?”

“Not much medicine, but a great deal of care,” answered the doctor, scarcely able to hide a smile.

“Now, Monsieur Bergerin,” said Grandet, “you are a man of honor, are you not? I trust to you! Come and see my wife how and when you think necessary. Save my good wife! I love her,—don’t you see?—though I never talk about it; I keep things to myself. I’m full of trouble. Troubles began when my brother died; I have to spend enormous sums on his affairs in Paris. Why, I’m paying through my nose; there’s no end to it. Adieu, monsieur! If you can save my wife, save her. I’ll spare no expense, not even if it costs me a hundred or two hundred francs.”

In spite of Grandet’s vows, his show of consideration, and the tender care Eugenie lavished, Madame Grandet rapidly grew weaker. Every day she visibly wasted, fragile as autumn foliage; the radiance of heaven seemed to shine through her. It was a death worthy of her life. In October, 1822, after angelic patience and love for her daughter, she passed away without a murmur. Lamb without spot, she went to heaven, regretting only the sweet companion of her cold and dreary life, for whom her last glance seemed to prophesy sorrows. She shrank from leaving her ewe-lamb, white as herself, alone in a selfish world that sought to strip her of her fleece and grasp her treasures.

“My child,” she said as she expired, “there is no happiness except in heaven; you will know it some day.”

################################################
The day after her mother’s death, Eugenie felt an intensified attachment to her childhood home, where every object—window, chair—brought tears. She had thought her father’s heart hard, yet after the funeral he gave her tender care: offering his arm at breakfast, looking kindly, brooding over her as if precious. Observers like Nanon and the Cruchot family mistook Pere Grandet’s attentiveness and tremors for age; only Maitre Cruchot, who knew Grandet’s secret, perceived the real motive.

After dinner, with doors closed, Grandet spoke to Eugenie about settling estate matters. Cruchot explained that Grandet did not want the property divided, sold, or taxed through an inventory. He proposed that she renounce her legal rights to her mother’s estate—leaving her father life’s use and disposition of the undivided property—while he would guarantee her capital. Eugenie, scarcely understanding the law, asked where to sign. Grandet, agitated, softened the proposal: instead of a costly formal deed, she could renounce informally and trust him to provide, for which he offered a monthly hundred francs. She agreed.

The notary warned she was disinheriting herself without guarantee, but Eugenie brushed this aside; Grandet kissed and embraced her, called her virtuous, and insisted Cruchot prepare the relinquishment deed for the court records. The next morning she signed the papers that completed her spoliation. Yet in the first year Grandet did not pay the promised hundred francs; when reminded, he fetched jewels taken from his nephew and flung them into her apron, sarcastically asking whether she wanted them for her twelve hundred francs. He promised more the next year, rubbing his hands at the prospect of speculating on her feelings.

Although still robust, Grandet began instructing Eugenie in thrift. For two years he made her order meals in his presence, collect rents, and learn vineyard and farm yields. By the third year his practices became her habits; he entrusted her with household keys and installed her as mistress of the house.

Five years passed in monotonous routine. Eugenie’s deep sadness showed, but she never complained or spoke of the love at the heart of her sorrow. Her only society was the Cruchots and a few friends they introduced; they taught her whist and visited nightly. In 1827, as Grandet’s infirmities increased, he further instructed her in estate management and told her to consult Maitre Cruchot in doubt.

Late that year the eighty-two-year-old patriarch was seized by a paralysis that progressed rapidly. Dr. Bergerin gave him up. With Charles absent, Eugenie clung to the last living link of affection; for her, love had been life. She devoted herself to nursing the father whose mind weakened while his instinctive avarice remained acute. The dying man positioned his chair facing the secret-room door and demanded explanation for every noise, waking at rent days or when accounts were due. He made Eugenie open the inner room, watched while she placed bags of money into his receptacle, and fingered the key in his waistcoat as if it were a talisman.

Maitre Cruchot, hoping the heiress might marry his nephew the president if Charles did not return, redoubled efforts: he took Grandet’s orders, ran errands to Froidfond, sold vintages, and converted everything into coin to be carried in sacks to the hiding-place. The old man’s last struggle was rolling up his coverings and ordering Nanon to lock them away “for fear they should be stolen.” So long as he could open his eyes his whole being fixed on the door behind which his treasures lay, asking, in panic, “Are they there? are they there?”

Eugenie spread coins on a table before him; hours passed with him staring at the money like a child seeing it. When the priest came for the last sacraments, the crucifix and silver vessels stirred him; he moved his lips and made a terrifying gesture as if to seize the crucifix—a final effort that cost him his life. He called for Eugenie though he did not see her and, as he died, told her, “Take care of it all. You will render me an account yonder!”—a grim confirmation that Christianity still sanctified the miser’s sense of duty.

Left alone in the gray house with only Nanon for solace, Eugenie found in the old servant the one person who loved her truly and could hear her. Nanon was more than a servant: a humble friend and comfort. From Maitre Cruchot she learned the full extent of her inheritance: an income of three hundred thousand francs from landed and personal property in the Saumur arrondissement; six million francs invested at three percent in the Funds (bought at sixty, then worth seventy-six francs); two million francs in gold coin; one hundred thousand francs in silver crown-pieces; and unpaid interest—amounting in all to seventeen million francs. Her immediate thought was simply: “Where is my cousin?”—the absent Charles.

When Cruchot gave her the clear schedule of the inheritance, Eugenie sat with Nanon by the vacant fireplace, where every object was memory—from her mother’s chair to the glass Charles had used. “We are alone,” she said; Nanon replied that if she knew where Charles was she would go on foot to find him. Eugenie could only think of the ocean between them. As she wept with Nanon, word of her seventeen-million fortune spread from Nantes to Orleans.

Among her first acts after the funeral, Eugenie settled an annuity of twelve hundred francs on Nanon, making the old woman comfortably off since she already possessed six hundred francs. Less than a month later Nanon married Antoine Cornoiller, appointed keeper of Mademoiselle Grandet’s estates. Madame Cornoiller, though fifty-nine, looked not more than forty: her conventual habits gave her a rosy, healthy appearance, and she seemed to laugh at age. On her marriage day she presented a content, vigorous figure envied in the neighborhood. Townspeople joked about her “fast colors,” admired Cornoiller’s good fortune, and complimented her as she went to church. Eugenie gave her three dozen silver forks and spoons as a wedding present.

Cornoiller, devoted to his mistress, spoke of her with tears; Madame Cornoiller, appointed housekeeper to Mademoiselle Grandet, delighted in her new duties as much as in her marriage. She took charge of weekly accounts, locked provisions, and issued them daily; she ruled two servants—a cook and a maid who mended linen and made dresses. Cornoiller combined keeper and bailiff duties. The new household ran strictly on Monsieur Grandet’s usages, and the farmers noticed no change.

By thirty, Eugenie had known none of life’s joys. Her pale, sad childhood beside a mother always wounded by life left her with remorse and regrets. Her first and only love—brief, intense, and thwarted—had cost her mother’s life and brought Eugenie only sorrow and fragile hopes. She felt the heart needed mutual exchange to live; without it, it withers. Wealth offered no consolation: she could not live except through love, religion, and faith in the future. Love explained eternity to her; her heart and the Gospel opened two worlds. She retreated inward, loving and believing herself beloved. For seven years her passion pervaded her life. Her real treasures were not her millions but Charles’s dressing-case, the portraits above her bed, the jewels recovered from her father and kept in a drawer, and her aunt’s thimble that her mother had used—symbols more precious than gold.

In mourning, a match seemed unlikely. Eugenie’s genuine piety was known. The Cruchots, guided by the old abbé, contented themselves with surrounding the heiress, paying affectionate attentions, and cultivating influence. Each evening the hall filled with devoted Cruchotines who praised their mistress. She had her doctor in ordinary, a grand almoner, a chamberlain, a first lady of honor, a prime minister—and a chancellor eager to have much to say. If she had wished for a train-bearer, one would have been produced. She was treated as a queen, flattered by little minds hoping to worm themselves into favor. The nightly gatherings, which called her Mademoiselle de Froidfond, outdid each other in admiration. At first the praise embarrassed her; gradually she grew used to homage and, perhaps, to enjoying the incense laid at her feet.

Monsieur de Bonfons became the hero of this little court: his wit, person, education, and amiability were praised perpetually. Visitors spoke of the Cruchots’ rising fortunes, of offers for practices and prospects of official appointments. Rumors circulated that the Cruchots together had an income of forty thousand francs, or that one would sell his practice if appointed juge de paix. Bonfons affected youth: though forty and withered, he dressed in the latest fashions, toyed with a bamboo cane, and avoided snuff at Eugenie’s house to charm her. He addressed her familiarly as “Our dear Eugenie.” Apart from more visitors, the change in the house was only that loto had given way to whist and Monsieur and Madame Grandet were absent. The pack still pursued Eugenie and her millions; the scent lay surer and the pursuit more united. If Charles had returned from the Indies he would have found the same people and the same interests. Madame des Grassins still tormented the Cruchots; Eugenie remained the central figure; Charles would still have been sovereign in her heart. Small things had changed: flowers the president once presented on her birthdays had become a daily institution; every evening he brought a huge bouquet that Madame Cornoiller displayed and then secretly threw away after visitors left.

Early in spring Madame des Grassins tried to stir the Cruchot circle by urging Eugenie to consider the Marquis de Froidfond, arguing that marriage to him would restore the ruined family and secure a marquise’s title. She lauded peerage and rank until, misreading Eugenie’s disdain, she went about proclaiming that marriage to Monsieur Cruchot was not as certain as people thought. “Though Monsieur de Froidfond is fifty,” she argued, “he does not look older than Monsieur Cruchot. He is a widower with children, and he is a marquis; he will be peer of France—where will you find a better match?” She added that Pere Grandet’s investment in Froidfond had aimed to graft himself onto that stock.

One night, as she prepared for bed, Eugenie asked Nanon, “How is it that in seven years he has never once written to me?”

While these events happened in Saumur, Charles was making his fortune in the Indies. His commercial outfit sold well; he realized six thousand dollars. Crossing the line cleared many cobwebs from his brain; he perceived that the surest way to fortune in the tropics, as in Europe, was to buy and sell men. He went to the African coast and bought Negroes, combining this traffic with other profitable merchandise. He plunged into the business tirelessly, driven by the desire to return to Paris with the prestige of a large fortune and the hope of regaining an even more brilliant position than the one he had lost.

Jostling with men, traveling through many lands, and observing conflicting customs modified his ideas and made him skeptical. He ceased to have fixed principles, since what one country called crime another praised as virtue. In the perpetual struggle of selfish interests his heart grew cold and contracted. The blood of the Grandets did its work: Charles became hard and eager for prey. He sold Chinamen, Negroes, bird-nests, children, artists; practised usury on a large scale; his habit of defrauding custom-houses made him less scrupulous about others’ rights. He went to St. Thomas and bought, for a song, goods captured by pirates and took them to ports where he could sell them well. If Eugenie’s pure face had accompanied him on his first voyage, like an image of the Virgin on a mast, and if he attributed his first success to her prayers, later women—blacks, mulattoes, whites, Indian dancing-girls—orgies and adventures effaced recollection of his cousin, Saumur, the house, the bench, the stolen kiss. He remembered only the little garden behind the crumbling wall, where he had learned his fate; but he rejected his family. His uncle was an old dog who had filched his jewels; Eugenie had no place in his heart, though she remained in his accounts as a creditor for six thousand francs.

Such conduct explains Charles Grandet’s silence. In the Indies, at St. Thomas, on the African coast, at Lisbon, and in the United States, the adventurer used the name Shepherd so as not to compromise his family. As Charles Shepherd he could be indefatigable, bold, grasping, and greedy, like a man resolved to snatch his fortune quibuscumque viis and get the villainy over with to live honestly thereafter.

With such methods prosperity was rapid; and in 1827 Charles returned to Bordeaux on the brig Marie Caroline, belonging to a royalist house. He brought nineteen hundred thousand francs worth of gold-dust, from which he expected another seven or eight per cent at the Paris mint. On the brig he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, a gentleman-in-ordinary to Charles X., a worthy old man who had married a fashionable woman with a fortune from the West Indies. To meet Madame d’Aubrion’s extravagance he had gone to the Indies to sell property and was now returning with his family.

Monsieur and Madame d’Aubrion, of the old southern family d’Aubrion de Buch, were reduced to about twenty thousand francs income and had an ugly daughter whom the mother was resolved to marry off without a dot—the family fortune barely sufficing for her own expenses in Paris. The mother almost despaired of placing such a girl, but she made the best of her. Mademoiselle d’Aubrion was long, spare, with a disdainful mouth and a nose too long, thick at the end, sallow normally and very red after a meal—an unpleasant feature in a pale, dull face. Madame d’Aubrion made her daughter appear distinguished: hygienic treatment kept the nose manageable, she taught the art of dressing, charming manners, melancholy glances that interest a man, and the coquettish peep of the foot beneath a petticoat at the moment the nose reddened. By full sleeves, deceptive pads, puffed dresses, and tight corsets she had achieved strange feminine developments worth showing as an example to other mothers.

Charles became intimate with Madame d’Aubrion precisely because she sought his intimacy. Persons on the brig said the handsome Madame neglected no means to secure so rich a son-in-law. Landing at Bordeaux in June 1827, Monsieur, Madame, Mademoiselle d’Aubrion, and Charles lodged at the same hotel and set out together for Paris. The hotel d’Aubrion was hampered with mortgages; Charles was to free it. The mother told him she would gladly give the ground-floor to a son-in-law. Not sharing Monsieur d’Aubrion’s prejudices about nobility, she promised Charles that she would obtain a royal ordinance from Charles X. allowing him to take the d’Aubrion name and arms and, by buying the entailed estate for thirty-six thousand francs a year, to succeed to the titles of Captal de Buch and Marquis d’Aubrion. By uniting fortunes, living together, and profiting by sinecures, the two families might occupy the hotel d’Aubrion with an income exceeding a hundred thousand francs.

“And when a man has a hundred thousand francs a year, a name, a family, and a position at court—for I will get you appointed gentleman-of-the-bedchamber—you can do what you like,” she told Charles. “You can become master of the rolls, prefect, secretary to an embassy, ambassador if you like. Charles X. is fond of d’Aubrion; they have known each other from childhood.”

Intoxicated with ambition, Charles toyed with the hopes thus presented in confidences poured from heart to heart. Believing his father’s affairs settled by his uncle, he imagined himself anchored in the Faubourg Saint-Germain—reappearing as the Comte d’Aubrion, like the Dreux reappearing in Bréze. Dazzled by the Restoration’s prosperity, which had been tottering when he left France, fascinated by aristocratic splendor, his intoxication, begun on the brig, increased after he reached Paris, and he resolved to take the course his would-be mother-in-law indicated. His cousin counted for no more than a speck in this brilliant prospect; but he went to see Annette. A woman of the world, Annette advised him to marry and promised her support in his ambitious projects. In her heart she was delighted to fasten an ugly, uninteresting girl on Charles, whose West Indies life had made him very attractive: bronzed complexion, bold, decisive manners like a man used to sharp decisions, to rule, and to succeed. Charles breathed easier in Paris, conscious of his part to play.

Des Grassins, hearing of his return, his approaching marriage, and his fortune, came to see him and inquired about the three hundred thousand francs still required to settle his father’s debts. He found Grandet conferring with a goldsmith about jewels for Mademoiselle d’Aubrion’s corbeille. Charles had brought magnificent diamonds; the setting with plate and jewelry for the new establishment amounted to over two hundred thousand francs. When des Grassins, who had already called several times, presented himself, Charles treated him with the impertinence of a young man of fashion conscious of having killed four men in as many duels in the Indies. Coldly, and without fully grasping the plea, Charles replied:

“My father’s affairs are not mine. I am much obliged, monsieur, for the trouble you have been good enough to take—by which, however, I really cannot profit. I have not earned two millions by the sweat of my brow to fling them at the head of my father’s creditors.”

“But suppose that your father’s estate were within a few days to be declared bankrupt?”

“Monsieur, in a few days I shall be called the Comte d’Aubrion; you will understand, therefore, that what you threaten is of no consequence to me. Besides, you know as well as I do that when a man has an income of a hundred thousand francs his father has never failed.” So saying, he edged Monsieur des Grassins to the door.

At the beginning of August that year, Eugenie was sitting on the little wooden bench where her cousin had sworn to love her eternally, where she usually breakfasted when the weather was fine. The poor girl was happy for the moment in the fresh summer air, letting memory recall the great and little events of her love and the catastrophes that followed. The sun had just reached the angle of the ruined wall full of chinks that no one, by the mistress’s caprice, was allowed to touch, though Cornoiller often told his wife it would fall and crush somebody one day. At that moment the postman knocked and gave a letter to Madame Cornoiller, who ran into the garden, crying:

“Mademoiselle, a letter!” She gave it to her mistress, adding, “Is it the one you expected?”

The words rang as loudly in Eugenie’s heart as they echoed from wall to wall of the court and garden.

“Paris—from him—he has returned!”

Eugenie turned pale and held the letter for a moment. She trembled and could not break the seal. La Grande Nanon stood before her, hands on her hips, her joy showing in the lines of her face.

“Read it, mademoiselle!”

“Ah, Nanon, why did he return to Paris? He went from Saumur.”

“Read it, and you’ll find out.”

Eugenie opened the letter with trembling fingers. A cheque on the house of “Madame des Grassins and Coret, of Saumur,” fluttered out. Nanon picked it up.

My dear Cousin,—

“No longer ‘Eugenie,’” she thought, and her heart quailed.

You—

“He once said ‘thou.’” She folded her arms and dared not read further; great tears gathered.

“Is he dead?” asked Nanon.

“If he were, he could not write,” said Eugenie.

She read the whole letter, which ran as follows:

My dear Cousin,—You will, I am sure, hear with pleasure of the success of my enterprise. You brought me luck; I have come back rich, and I have followed my uncle’s advice, whose death, together with my aunt’s, I have just learned from Monsieur des Grassins. The death of parents is in the course of nature, and we must succeed them. I trust you are by this time consoled. Nothing can resist time, as I know. Yes, my dear cousin, the day of illusions is, unfortunately, gone for me. How could it be otherwise? Traveling through many lands, I have reflected on life. I was a child when I went away—I have come back a man. Today I think of many I did not dream of then. You are free, my dear cousin, and I am free still. Nothing apparently hinders the realization of our early hopes; but my nature is too loyal to hide from you the situation in which I find myself. I have not forgotten our relations; I have always remembered, throughout my long wanderings, the little wooden seat—

Eugenie rose as if sitting on live coals and went to the stone steps of the court.

—the little wooden seat where we vowed to love each other forever, the passage, the gray hall, my attic chamber, and the night when, by your delicate kindness, you made my future easier. Yes, these recollections sustained my courage; I said in my heart that you were thinking of me at the hour we had agreed upon. Have you always looked at the clouds at nine o’clock? Yes, I am sure of it. I cannot betray so true a friendship—no, I must not deceive you. An alliance has been proposed to me which satisfies all my ideas of matrimony. Love in marriage is a delusion. My experience warns me that in marrying we are bound to obey social laws and meet the world’s conventional demands. Between you and me there are differences which might affect your future more than mine. I will not speak here of your customs, inclinations, education, nor your habits, none of which fit Parisian life or the future I have marked out. My intention is to keep my household stately, to receive much company—in short, to live in the world; and I think I remember that you love a quiet and tranquil life. I will be frank and make you judge my situation; you have the right to understand and judge it.

I possess now an income of eighty thousand francs. This fortune enables me to marry into the Aubrion family, whose heiress, nineteen years old, brings me a title, a place as gentleman-of-the-bedchamber, and a brilliant position. I will admit, my dear cousin, that I do not love Mademoiselle d’Aubrion; but in marrying her I secure to my children a social rank whose advantages will become incalculable: monarchical principles are gaining favor. Thus my son, when he becomes Marquis d’Aubrion, having an entailed estate with a rental of forty thousand francs a year, can obtain any State position he chooses. We owe ourselves to our children.

You see, my cousin, with what good faith I lay my heart, hopes, and fortune before you. Possibly, after seven years’ separation, you have yourself forgotten our youthful loves; but I have never forgotten your kindness or my own words. I remember all, even lightly uttered words—words by which a less conscientious man, with a heart less upright, would scarcely feel bound. In telling you the marriage I propose is solely one of convenience, that I still remember our childish love, am I not putting myself entirely in your hands and making you the mistress of my fate? Am I not telling you that if I must renounce my social ambitions, I shall willingly content myself with the pure and simple happiness of which you have shown me so sweet an image?

“Tan, ta, ta—tan, ta, ti,” sang Charles Grandet to the air of Non più andrai, as he signed himself,—

Your devoted cousin, Charles.

“Thunder! that’s doing it handsomely!” he said, looking for the cheque; having found it, he added:

P.S.—I enclose a cheque on the des Grassins bank for eight thousand francs to your order, payable in gold, which includes the capital and interest of the sum you were kind enough to lend me. I am expecting a case from Bordeaux which contains a few things which you must allow me to offer you as a mark of my unceasing gratitude. You can send my dressing-case by the diligence to the hotel d’Aubrion, rue Hillerin-Bertin.

“By the diligence!” said Eugenie. “A thing for which I would have laid down my life!”

Terrible and utter disaster! The ship had gone down, leaving not a spar, not a plank, on a vast ocean of hope. Some women, abandoned, will try to tear a lover from a rival, commit crimes, or rush to the ends of the earth. Others bow their heads and suffer in silence; they go their way dying, resigned, weeping, forgiving, praying, and recollecting until the last breath. This was Eugenie’s love after she read that dreadful letter. She raised her eyes to heaven, thinking of her dying mother’s last words, who, with the prescience of death, had looked into the future: Eugenie, remembering that prophetic life, measured her destiny. Nothing was left for her; she could only unfold her wings, stretch upward, and live in prayer until deliverance.

“My mother was right,” she said, weeping. “Suffer—and die!”

Eugenie came slowly in from the garden, avoiding the corridor. The memory of her cousin lingered in the dim hall and on the chimney-piece where her morning saucer and Sèvres sugar-bowl stood.

This day was to be solemn and eventful. Nanon announced the parish curate, allied with the Cruchots and Monsieur de Bonfons’s interests. The old abbé had urged him to speak to Mademoiselle Grandet about the duty of marriage for a woman of her station. Eugenie thought he had come for the thousand francs she gave monthly to the poor and told Nanon to fetch them; the curate only smiled.

“To-day, mademoiselle,” he said, “I have come to speak of a poor girl in whom Saumur takes an interest, who, through lack of charity to herself, neglects her Christian duties.”

“Monsieur le curé, you have come at a time when I cannot think of my neighbor; I am filled with thoughts of myself. I am very unhappy; my only refuge is in the Church; her bosom is large enough to hold all human woe.”

“Mademoiselle, in speaking of this young girl we shall speak of you. If you wish to insure your salvation you have two paths—either leave the world or obey its laws. Obey either your earthly destiny or your heavenly destiny.”

“Yes, God has sent you to me; I will bid farewell to the world and live for God alone, in silence and seclusion.”

“My daughter, think long before you take so violent a step. Marriage is life, the veil is death.”

“Yes, death—a quick death!” she cried with dreadful eagerness.

“Death? but you have obligations to society, mademoiselle. Are you not the mother of the poor who receive clothes and wood and work in summer? Your fortune is a loan to be returned; you accepted it as such. To bury yourself in a convent would be selfishness; to remain an old maid is to fail your duty. Can you manage your vast property alone? You may lose it, be involved in lawsuits. Believe your pastor: a husband is useful; you are bound to preserve what God has bestowed upon you. You love God too truly not to find salvation among his world, of which you are a noble ornament and to which you owe example.”

At that moment Madame des Grassins arrived, driven by vengeance and despair.

“Mademoiselle—Ah! here is monsieur le curé; I am silent. I came to speak on business; but I see you are conferring with—”

“Madame,” said the curé, “I leave the field to you.”

“Oh! monsieur le curé,” said Eugenie, “come back later; your support is necessary.”

“Ah, yes, indeed, my poor child!” said Madame des Grassins.

“What do you mean?” asked Eugenie and the curé together.

“Don’t I know about your cousin’s return and his marriage with Mademoiselle d’Aubrion? A woman doesn’t carry her wits in her pocket.”

Eugenie blushed then assumed the impassive countenance her father had been known for.

“Well, madame,” she said ironically, “no doubt I carry my wits in my pocket, for I do not understand you. Speak, say what you mean, before monsieur le curé; you know he is my director.”

“Read this,” Madame des Grassins said, handing a letter from her husband.

Eugenie read:

My dear Wife,—Charles Grandet has returned from the Indies and has been in Paris about a month—

“A month!” thought Eugenie; her hand fell. She read on.

I had to dance attendance before I was allowed to see the future Vicomte d’Aubrion. Though all Paris is talking of his marriage and the banns are published—

“He wrote to me after that!” thought Eugenie. She did not cry out but felt contempt.

The marriage, however, will not come off. The Marquis d’Aubrion will never give his daughter to the son of a bankrupt. I went to tell Grandet of the steps his uncle and I took in his father’s business, and the manoeuvres by which we had kept the creditors quiet until now. The insolent fellow had the face to say to me—that his father’s affairs were not his! A solicitor might claim thirty or forty thousand francs in fees, one per cent on the debts. But patience! there are twelve hundred thousand francs legitimately owing to the creditors, and I shall at once declare his father a bankrupt.

I went into this business on the word of that old crocodile Grandet, and I have made promises in the name of his family. If Monsieur le vicomte d’Aubrion does not care for his honor, I care for mine. I shall explain my position to the creditors. Still, I have too much respect for Mademoiselle Eugenie (to whom under happier circumstances we once hoped to be allied) to act before you have spoken to her about it—

Eugenie paused and coldly returned the letter without finishing it.

“I thank you,” she said to Madame des Grassins.

“Ah! you have the voice and manner of your deceased father,” Madame des Grassins replied.

“Madame, you have eight thousand francs to pay us,” said Nanon, producing Charles’s cheque.

“That’s true; come with me now, Madame Cornoiller.”

“Monsieur le curé,” said Eugenie, with composure inspired by what she was about to say, “would it be a sin to remain a virgin after marriage?”

“That is a case of conscience. If you wish to know what Sanchez says in ‘De Matrimonio,’ I shall tell you to-morrow.”

The curé left. Eugenie withdrew to her father’s secret room and spent the day alone, not coming to dinner despite Nanon’s entreaties. She appeared in the evening when the usual company arrived. The news of Charles’s return and treachery had spread through the town. Expecting scrutiny, Eugenie allowed none of the cruel emotions that wrung her; she met mournful looks and melancholy speeches with a smile, hiding misery behind courtesy. Around nine the games ended and players rose to leave. At the moment the assembly stood, an unexpected event resounded through Saumur.

“Stay, monsieur le président,” said Eugenie to Monsieur de Bonfons as she saw him take his cane.

No one in that assembly was unmoved. The president turned pale and was forced to sit down.

“The president gets the millions,” said Mademoiselle de Gribeaucourt.

“It is plain enough; the president marries Mademoiselle Grandet,” cried Madame d’Orsonval.

“All the trumps in one hand,” said the abbé.

“A love game,” said the notary.

Provincial conventions are rigid; such an infraction seemed a solemn promise.

“Monsieur le président,” said Eugenie with some emotion when they were left alone, “I know what pleases you. Swear to leave me free during my whole life, to claim none of the rights which marriage gives you over me, and my hand is yours. Oh!” she added, seeing him about to kneel, “I have more to say. I must not deceive you. In my heart I cherish one inextinguishable feeling. Friendship is the only sentiment I can give to a husband. I wish neither to affront him nor to violate the laws of my heart. But you can possess my hand and my fortune only at the cost of doing me an inestimable service.”

“I am ready for all things,” said the president.

“Here are fifteen hundred thousand francs,” she said, drawing a certificate of a hundred Bank of France shares. “Go to Paris—not tomorrow, but instantly. Find Monsieur des Grassins, learn the names of my uncle’s creditors, call them together, pay them in full with interest at five per cent from the day the debt was incurred to the present. Obtain full legal receipts before a notary. You are a magistrate, and I can trust this to you. You are a man of honor; I will put faith in your word and meet the dangers of life under your name. Let us have mutual indulgence. We have known each other so long we are almost related; you would not wish to render me unhappy.”

The president fell at her feet, heart wrung with joy.

“I will be your slave!” he said.

“When you obtain the receipts, monsieur,” she resumed coldly, “you will take them with the other papers to my cousin Grandet, and you will give him this letter. On your return I will keep my word.”

The president understood her bitterness and hurried to obey, lest time effect a reconciliation.

When he left, Eugenie fell back in her chair and burst into tears. All was over.

The president took the mail-post and reached Paris the next evening. The morning after his arrival he went to see des Grassins; together they summoned the creditors to meet at the notary’s office, where the vouchers had been deposited. Not a single creditor failed to appear. In an exercise of justice they were all punctual. Monsieur de Bonfons, in the name of Mademoiselle Grandet, paid the amount of their claims with interest. Paying interest was remarkable in Parisian commerce. When the receipts were legally registered and des Grassins had received fifty thousand francs for his services, the president went to the hôtel d’Aubrion and found Charles entering after a serious quarrel with his prospective father-in-law. The old marquis had told him plainly he should not marry his daughter until all Guillaume Grandet’s creditors were paid in full.

The president gave Charles the following letter:

My Cousin,—Monsieur le président de Bonfons has undertaken to place in your hands the acquittance for all claims upon my uncle, also a receipt by which I acknowledge having received from you the sum total of those claims. I have heard of a possible failure, and I think that the son of a bankrupt may not be able to marry Mademoiselle d’Aubrion. Yes, my cousin, you judged rightly of my mind and of my manners. I have, it is true, no part in the world; I understand neither its calculations nor its customs; and I could not give you the pleasures that you seek in it. Be happy, according to the social conventions to which you have sacrificed our love. To make your happiness complete I can only offer you your father’s honor. Adieu! You will always have a faithful friend in your cousin

Eugenie.

The president smiled at the exclamation the ambitious young man could not restrain on receiving the documents.

“We shall announce our marriages at the same time,” remarked Monsieur de Bonfons.

“Ah! you marry Eugenie? Well, I am delighted; she is a good girl. But,” added Charles, struck by a thought, “she must be rich?”

“She had,” said the president with a mischievous smile, “about nineteen millions four days ago; but she has only seventeen millions to-day.”

Charles looked thunderstruck.

“Seventeen mil—”

“Seventeen millions; yes, monsieur. We shall muster, Mademoiselle Grandet and I, an income of seven hundred and fifty thousand francs when we marry.”

“My dear cousin,” Charles said, recovering some assurance, “we can push each other’s fortunes.”

“Agreed,” said the president. “Here is also a little case I am charged to give into your own hands,” and he placed a leather dressing-case on the table.

“Well, my dear friend,” said Madame d’Aubrion, entering without noticing the president, “don’t pay any attention to what poor Monsieur d’Aubrion has just said; the Duchesse de Chaulieu has turned his head. I repeat, nothing shall interfere with the marriage—”

“Very good, madame. The three millions which my father owed were paid yesterday.”

“In money?” she asked.

“Yes, in full, capital and interest; and I am about to do honor to his memory—”

“What folly!” exclaimed his mother-in-law. “Who is this?” she whispered to Grandet, perceiving the president.

“My man of business,” he answered low.

The marquise bowed superciliously to Monsieur de Bonfons.

“We are pushing each other’s fortunes already,” said the president, taking up his hat. “Good-by, cousin.”

“He is laughing at me, the old cockatoo! I’d like to put six inches of iron into him!” muttered Charles.

The president was out of hearing. Three days later he returned to Saumur and announced his marriage with Eugenie. Six months after the marriage he was appointed councillor in the Cour royale at Angers, later president of the superior courts, and he hoped for election to the Chamber of Deputies, yearning even for a peerage.

“The king will be his cousin, won’t he?” said la Grande Nanon as she listened to her mistress recount the honors.

Nevertheless, Monsieur de Bonfons (having finally dropped his patronymic Cruchot) did not realize all ambitions. He died eight days after his election as deputy of Saumur. Providence, which sees all, no doubt punished him for his sordid calculations and the legal cleverness with which, accurate Cruchot, he had drawn up his marriage contract, in which husband and wife gave to each other, “in case they should have no children, their entire property of every kind, landed or otherwise, without exception or reservation, dispensing even with the formality of an inventory; provided that said omission shall not injure their heirs and assigns…” This clause explains the profound respect the president always showed for Madame de Bonfons’s wishes and solitude. Women cited him as considerate and delicate, pitied him, and even blamed Eugenie’s passion and grief with cruel but discreet insinuations.

Eugenie, endowed with the delicate perception of a solitary soul trained to divination by suffering and meditation, knew well that the president desired her death to step into possession of their immense fortune, augmented by property from his uncle the notary and his uncle the abbé, recently dead. The poor solitary pitied him. Providence avenged her for his calculations and indifference; he had respected her hopeless passion because it was his surest safeguard. To give life to a child would have ruined his hopes—those hopes of selfishness and ambition he cherished.

God thus flung piles of gold upon this prisoner to whom gold was indifferent. She longed for heaven, lived pious and good, succored the unfortunate in secret, and never wearied of charity. Madame de Bonfons became a widow at thirty-six. She remained beautiful in the manner of a woman near forty: face white, placid and calm; voice gentle; manners simple. She possessed the saintliness of one who never soiled her soul by contact with the world, yet retained the rigid bearing of an old maid and the petty habits of provincial life. Despite vast wealth she lived as the poor Eugenie had once lived. The fire in her Saumur house was never lighted until the day her father had allowed it in the hall, and it was put out according to the rules that governed her youth. She dressed as her mother had dressed. Her house, without sun, always in shadow, mirrored her life. She carefully accumulated income and might seem parsimonious but disarmed criticism by noble uses of her wealth. Pious and charitable institutions, a hospital for old age, Christian schools, and a richly endowed public library testified against charges of avarice. The churches of Saumur owed much to her. Madame de Bonfons (sometimes ironically called mademoiselle) inspired reverential respect: yet that noble heart, beating only with tender emotions, had always been subjected to calculations of human selfishness; money had cast its frigid influence upon that hallowed life and taught distrust of feelings to a woman who was all feeling.

“I have none but you to love me,” she said to Nanon.

Her hand staunched secret wounds in many families. She went to heaven attended by a train of benefactions. The grandeur of her soul redeemed the narrowness of her education and the petty habits of her early life.

Such is the history of Eugenie Grandet, who is in the world but not of it; created to be supremely a wife and mother, she had neither husband nor children nor family. Lately there was talk of her marrying again. Saumur people spoke of the Marquis de Froidfond and of the family laying siege to the rich widow as once the Cruchots had beset the heiress. It was said that Nanon and Cornoiller favored the marquis; nothing could be more false. Neither la Grande Nanon nor Cornoiller had sufficient mind to understand the corruptions of the world.
