There is a particular twilight when day yields to night that draws reflective artists into their work. In that hour a young painter, finishing a large canvas, falls from his ladder, strikes his head, and loses consciousness.

He is awakened by a young woman and her mother who rush to his aid. As he recovers he notices the girl’s beauty—matching an ideal he had imagined—and the older woman’s refined, marquise-like air. The mother tends him with gentle advice; both are reserved and withdraw without pressing for acquaintance.

After they leave, the porter’s wife identifies them as Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, long-time residents of the building. The painter, Hippolyte Schinner, has just moved into the attic studio and begun to gain recognition. Determined to secure a better life for his own mother, who sacrificed much for him, he keeps his private affairs guarded despite modest success.

During convalescence Hippolyte replays the encounter, especially the image of the girl, which lingers. Returning to his studio he is eager to visit them, drawn by a kinship born of imagined shared misfortune.

In their modest home he sees signs of poverty but feels only empathy, noting the contrast between their humble surroundings and the elegance they maintain. The room holds faded traces of past luxury—worn finery kept meticulously clean. Conversation starts awkwardly but soon turns to art, allowing Hippolyte to speak openly about his work and feelings. Madame Leseigneur reveals a cherished portrait of her late husband, a naval officer, is decaying. Hippolyte offers to paint a new portrait, hoping the gesture will deepen their acquaintance; they accept with grace, and he senses the emotional weight behind the request.

Adelaide briefly leaves and returns with candles to brighten the room. The mood shifts when a visitor arrives—someone who clearly knows her. A quick jealousy stirs in Hippolyte. The newcomer, an older gentleman in a coat of bygone fashion, carries an air of nobility mixed with libertinism; their exchange hints at layered history and social distinctions. Throughout the evening Hippolyte wrestles with admiration and uncertainty, drawn to her spirit while aware of the fragile boundary between their worlds and a possible connection amid the shadows.

A remarkable figure stood beside the “Louis XIV’s light infantry,” a Bonapartist nickname for the shabby remnants of the Monarchy: a lean, aged man, a faded echo of his younger self—coat worn, powdered hair dulled, intellect diminished. Rivarol’s quip about Champcenetz seemed apt. The silent old man puzzled the painter Chevalier; no one addressed him. His relation to the gallant man—friend, relative, or companion—was unclear, and his presence suggested a possible past link to the gallant’s deceased wife without offering answers.

One of the least dilapidated men approached Madame de Rouville, kissed her hand, and sat beside her; another bowed and took a seat nearby. Adelaide leaned over the old gentleman’s chair in a posture recalling a famous painting, though his familiarity seemed to irritate her. “Are you sulky with me?” he asked, casting a shrewd glance at the painter Schinner as if measuring him.

Madame de Rouville introduced Hippolyte, the celebrated painter. The old man noticed Hippolyte’s red ribbon and contrasted it with his own hard-earned honors. Hippolyte, captivated by Adelaide’s beauty, was briefly blind to the squalor around them; enthralled, he spoke little while the old gentleman lamented his losing streak at cards. Adelaide offered help; he insisted she remain where he could see her. When the game ended, the gentleman complained of losses; the silent figure only echoed the hour, prompting Hippolyte to take his leave, still under the spell of budding affection.

The next day Hippolyte longed to see Adelaide again. He waited until afternoon before visiting Madame de Rouville and awkwardly asked for the Baron’s portrait. Adelaide, alone in the drawing-room, sent him to fetch it, revealing modesty with a charged glance. Hippolyte took the portrait and promised to return it soon.

Their relationship blossomed over the following days with laughter and shared thoughts. Schinner, keen to learn piquet, often lost, while the unspoken bond between Hippolyte and Adelaide deepened. They became inseparable; Adelaide’s affection grew and Hippolyte’s jealousy flared when the old Count showed familiarity with her. He worried Adelaide’s social life harmed his health and expected more from her. Their love unfolded without grand gestures, founded on mutual trust and subtle exchanges. Within two months Hippolyte visited daily, the old gentleman’s visits faded, and Hippolyte took his place at the card table, sharing equally in the losses.

Still, Hippolyte fretted about Madame de Rouville’s finances. He spent two months on the Baron’s portrait—considering it among his finest—but was puzzled by the Baroness’s silence about payment. One day Adelaide visited his studio and was awestruck by the portrait; moved, Hippolyte declared his desire to marry her. Their moment was interrupted by the old vice-admiral, who praised the work and hinted at financial support for both commissions.

That evening Hippolyte noticed Madame de Rouville’s unusual excitement during cards. After a night of turmoil he found his purse missing and, in pain, suspected Adelaide and her mother of theft. A friend’s mention of rumors unsettled him further. Torn between love and doubt, he confronted Adelaide; struck by her pallor and sadness, he felt ashamed of his suspicions. During a tender evening of cards, Adelaide discreetly placed a new purse before Hippolyte to replace the lost one. Overwhelmed, he declared his intention to marry her.

The old vice-admiral soon returned with news of a promotion and offered support to the young couple, praising the portrait and dispelling lingering doubts. The evening closed with renewed unity. Ultimately the tale follows characters navigating love, trust, and misunderstanding, finding solace through faith, reconciliation, and shared bonds.
