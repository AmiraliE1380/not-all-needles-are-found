For souls apt to feel deeply, there is a delightful hour between day and night: twilight softens lights and shadows, favors a dreamy mood, and, in the prevailing silence, becomes especially dear to artists. They step back from pictures they can no longer work on, pass judgment, and are rapt by meanings that flash on the inner eye. In that clear-obscure, the material skill that produced illusion disappears; figures seem to speak and live, shade is shadow, flesh breathes, and imagination heightens every realistic detail. At such an hour illusion reigns, carrying the soul into a world of fancies where the artist forgets the mundane flow of time.

It was in this magic hour that a young, talented painter who worshipped Art alone stood on a step-ladder, absorbed in finishing a large canvas. Lost in admiration and self-criticism, he was carried away by reverie until night fell. Whether through misjudgment or fatigue, he fell from the ladder, struck his head on a footstool, and lay unconscious for an uncertain time.

A sweet voice roused him. A bright light made him close his eyes; through the mist he heard two women whispering and felt gentle hands supporting his head. By the light of an Argand lamp he saw the most charming face he had yet beheld—an ideal of beauty close to the delicate refinement of Prud’hon and the poetic sentiment of Girodet: fresh temples, regular brows, virginal innocence. Her figure was slight and her dress plain, revealing neither wealth nor poverty.

As he came to, he murmured thanks and admiration. A handkerchief pressed to his forehead; the air smelled of ether used to revive him. An old woman, looking like a marquise of the old school, held the lamp and advised the younger. The girl explained they had heard the noise and, alarmed by the ensuing silence, had entered—finding the key in the latch—and found him motionless. The elder had fetched what was needed to bathe his head. He had cut his forehead, but felt better and asked only for a cab. The older woman fretted about leeches and bleeding—common remedies for a fall—while the young girl stole concerned glances at him and at the pictures; her curiosity seemed an almost absent-minded interest, a natural sympathy for suffering. They left without insistence or familiarity, their manner noble and simple; their anxious kindness made a quiet impression on him that lingered in memory.

On the landing the old lady gently chided Adelaide for leaving the door open; the painter joked that it was to hear him fall. They accompanied him as far as the dark stairs with maternal solicitude. The incident mattered because the painter, only days earlier, had set up his studio in the attics of this house on a darker, muddier stretch of the Rue de Suresnes, near the Madeleine and close to his rooms in the Rue des Champs-Elysees. Fame was beginning to resist want; he was spared the long walks to cheaper studios and enjoying his last privations. Among artists recently lauded, he had begun to be esteemed, and his life and manners had particular attraction to people around him.

Hippolyte Schinner inspired interest but revealed little of his past. He was the idol of a mother who had raised him amid severe privations. Mademoiselle Schinner, his mother, an Alsatian farmer’s daughter who had never married, had been cruelly wounded in youth by an unprincipled lover. She renounced the world, refused his alms, turned shame into a kind of austere glory, and devoted herself to maternal love. Through work and saving she accumulated a treasure intended for her son. Later, fortune repaid her: at the last exhibition Hippolyte received the Cross of the Legion of Honor, newspapers hailed him as a genius, artists recognized him as a master, and dealers lavished praise and gold on his canvases. At twenty-five he understood his standing. He wanted to restore to his mother the pleasures she had been denied: to make her rich, respected, and the center of society. Selecting friends among the honorable and distinguished, fastidious in choice, he sought to raise further the position his talent had given him. His solitary work from boyhood left him with early-formed beliefs and a soul still open to shy emotions—poetic, virginal hopes that the worldly call puerile but which are deep in their simple intensity.

Schinner’s gentle, polite manners came from the heart; his voice stirred noble sentiments and testified to modesty. People felt attracted to him by some moral charm they could not analyze. These qualities explain why, when the porter’s wife chattered about his rescuers, Hippolyte did not interrogate her. He answered her questions about the accident but let her gossip—she supposed they were Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, tenants of four years, mysterious in their means of livelihood. “In the morning, an old half-deaf woman helps them; in the evening, old gentlemen with ribbons come to see them and stay late; one comes in a carriage with servants and has sixty thousand francs a year,” she said, adding that Mademoiselle was smart when she walked in the Tuileries and followed by many young men whom she shut out. The porter’s wife concluded they were quiet and economical tenants.

The coach arrived and Schinner went home. His mother dressed his wound and forbade his return to the studio the next day. For three days he rested; during this enforced idleness the scene of the fainting rose again in fragments. The young girl’s profile reappeared against his inner darkness; the mother’s faded features returned; Adelaide’s touch, a graceful gesture, a tone of voice—little moments surfaced like objects from deep water. When he could resume work, he hurried back to the studio, and more to visit his neighbors. Passion brought inexplicable pleasure as he climbed to the fourth-floor door of Mademoiselle Leseigneur.

Parisian manners often hide strange privacies: elegant toilettes, brilliant women, and homes that betray doubtful comfort. Schinner, sensitive to appearances, noted the contrast in his neighbors’ rooms. The house’s landlord was of the miserly type who hates repairs; the studio’s attics bore the signs of neglect: dark greasy paint, stains, and shabby woodwork. These poverty marks had a certain poetry for an artist.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur opened the door; recognizing him, she bowed and tactfully shut a glass partition that might have revealed linen hung over patent stoves, a camp-bed, charcoal and irons, and a household’s cooking gear—everyday utensils. Muslin curtains screened this clutter, lit by windows facing a neighboring yard. With an artist’s eye, Hippolyte noted the room’s divisions: an ante-room/dining-room with old salmon-rose paper and a patched flock border; prints of Alexander’s battles in gilt frames; a massive mahogany table worn at the edges; a small stove before a blocked hearth; chairs with red morocco seats and gilt nails, their splendor scarred like veterans. The space was a museum of both luxury and poverty: a fine telescope hung over discolored glass; a painted sideboard pretended to be mahogany; faded rugs and cleaned but aging furniture shone with a hard cleanliness suggesting thrift.

Adelaide opened the inner room door and announced him with evident pleasure. Schinner, who had seen similar poverty at his mother’s, entered with sympathy rather than scorn. The old lady rose from an antique armchair and offered him a seat. When he thanked them, mentioning Adelaide’s hearing him fall, the girl lit the Argand lamp, replacing a tallow candle, and sat slightly behind him to look at him while appearing to fuss with the lamp. The stratagem embarrassed both.

As they talked, Hippolyte took in more details: Egyptian figures on fire-dogs under cinders; weak brands before a sham log; an old Aubusson carpet over cold tiles; walls hung with faded imitation silk paper; a crack and the outline of double doors closing a recess used for sleeping; a dim portrait of an officer above a chest of drawers; faded red silk curtains; a malachite tray holding exquisitely painted coffee cups; an Empire clock and tall candlesticks; porcelain vases with dusty artificial flowers. A card-table with new packs suggested either honest poverty or gambling as livelihood. Yet in Adelaide’s presence his youthful heart believed in her innocence and attributed the odd furniture mix to honorable causes.

Madame Leseigneur, offered a shawl by Adelaide, warmed herself by the fire. Hippolyte, mindful that direct pity might insult, steered conversation lightly. The ladies began by talking of painting, and the exchange, easing the first-call awkwardness, led him to speak of his art and reveal the habits and sentiments of his mind. The old woman’s face—traces of courtly refinement, shrewd eyes—made it hard to tell whether squalor masked vice or high virtue. Women’s faces often hold inscrutable lines between frankness and artifice. At Schinner’s age the instinct is to believe in goodness; Adelaide’s noble brow and soulful eyes seemed to breathe modest virtue. He seized an opportunity to discuss a faded pastel portrait—its chalk flaking away—hung in the room.

“You are attached to that picture for the likeness, no doubt, mesdames, for the drawing is dreadful?” he observed.

“It was done at Calcutta, in great haste,” the mother replied, her voice trembling with memory.

She looked at the formless sketch with a poignant absorption: the face of a lost happiness. Schinner took this as proof of a life once happy and sat beside her.

“Madame,” he offered, “the colors of that pastel will soon vanish. The portrait will survive only in memory. Will you allow me to reproduce the likeness on canvas? It will preserve it longer. As a neighborly favor, grant me the pleasure of doing this work. Sometimes an artist finds relaxation in such modest tasks.”

The offer touched them. Adelaide’s glance was deep; Madame Leseigneur took the proposal with the calm self-possession of someone who had endured shame and kept dignity. An obligation of that sort could forge a gentle tie between them.

Hippolyte guessed the uniform: naval. She spoke then of the portrait’s subject. “Yes,” the mother said, “it is the uniform of a captain. Monsieur de Rouville—my husband—died at Batavia of a wound received fighting an English ship on the Asiatic coast. He commanded a frigate of fifty-six guns; the Revenge carried ninety-six. The struggle was unequal; he defended his ship bravely and escaped at nightfall. When I returned to France, Bonaparte was not yet in power, and I was refused a pension. When I applied again, recently, I was cruelly told that if the baron had emigrated he would not have been lost; by now he would have been a rear-admiral; his Excellency quoted degrees of forfeiture. I do not like holding out my hand in the name of grief that robs a woman of voice and strength. I have always hated begging for money that measures the value of a life.”

“Dear mother, this subject always does you harm,” Adelaide murmured, and the Baronne Leseigneur bowed and fell silent—a woman at once proud and wearied by memory.

A carriage stopping at the door interrupted the quiet. Adelaide rose, fetched a pair of tall gilt candlesticks with partly burned tapers, and set them in the outer room. She opened the door before the bell finished ringing, returning a sound of a kiss given and received that touched Hippolyte’s heart. He wondered about the man who treated Adelaide familiarly. The newcomers spoke in undertone with the young girl before entering. At last Mademoiselle de Rouville returned, followed by two men whose costumes and countenances would require longer telling. The first, about sixty, wore a coat of the Louis XVIII fashion—neither fully civil nor military—decorated with fleurs-de-lis embroidery, gilt buttons, and ribboned buttonholes. His powdered hair betrayed no hat-crease; he might be fifty and seemed robust, his frank emigre expression mingled with the reckless gallantry of an old musketeer. His gestures suggested a man who had not renounced royalism, religion, or love affairs.

A really remarkable figure stood behind the specimen of “Louis XIV.’s light infantry”—the old Bonapartist nickname for those Monarchy survivors. Though it ought to have been the picture’s chief subject, he was only an accessory: a lean, dry man dressed like the first, but seeming his reflection. The coat that was new on the one was old on the other; the powder in his hair looked less white, the fleurs-de-lis less bright, the shoulder straps more dog-eared; his intellect seemed feebler, his life nearer its end. He was the moonlight to the other man’s radiance, Rivarol’s quip on Champcenetz made flesh: a paler, poorer double.

The Chevalier was a mystery to the painter. He spoke not, and no one spoke to him. Was he a friend, a poor relation, a companion—some mixture of dog, parrot, and confidant? Had he saved his patron’s fortune, or only his life? Had an attachment once bound him to the dead Comtesse de Kergarouet? At the Baronne de Rouville’s house, as elsewhere, he piqued but did not satisfy curiosity.

The older gentleman—less dilapidated of the two—approached Madame de Rouville with gallantry, kissed her hand, and sat beside her. The Chevalier bowed and placed himself a couple of chairs away. Adelaide leaned on the back of the old man’s armchair in a posture unconsciously recalling the Dido of Guerin.

Though the gentleman’s familiarity was fatherly, it annoyed the young girl a little. When she appeared sulky he shot a shrewd, diplomatic look at Schinner—the kind that asks, “Is he one of us?”

“This is our neighbour,” the Baronne said, pointing to Hippolyte. “Monsieur is a celebrated painter; you must know his name.”

The Chevalier, seeing his friend’s playfulness in not mentioning it, bowed. “Certainly,” he said. “I heard much about his pictures at the Salon. Talent has immense privileges,” he added, noting the red ribbon. “That mark which we must earn at the cost of blood and service you can win in youth; but all glory is kin,” he added, touching his Cross of Saint-Louis.

Hippolyte murmured thanks and fell again into silence, enthralled by Adelaide, forgetting the poverty of the rooms. To him her head shone against a luminous atmosphere. He answered questions faintly, his attention inward; like all who have known that double consciousness, he listened while a soul elsewhere contemplated. Hope gave him visions of happiness; he refused to analyze small incidents.

He soon noticed the old lady, her daughter, and the gentleman playing cards. The Chevalier, faithful to his role as shadow, watched his friend’s game from behind his chair, mimicking gestures and offering approving nods. The Count lamented his bad luck; the Baroness teased him; Adelaide coyly refused to give advice because she liked being looked at. At eleven the game ended; the old man threw two louis on the table in temper. The Chevalier looked at the painter and repeated, “It is eleven o’clock.”

Hippolyte, hearing this more clearly than the rest, felt it proper to retire. He took leave of the Baronne, Adelaide, and the strangers and went away full of that first, unreasoning rapture of love.

Next day the painter ached to see Adelaide again. Though he longed to present himself at dawn, he waited till afternoon and went to ask Mademoiselle Leseigneur for the portrait of the Baron. Adelaide, apparently having heard him, ushered him in. She couldn’t unhook the portrait without standing on a chest of drawers and, blushing, asked him to get it down. The modesty in her request was a genuine blush of reserve; the painter read it and replied with the glance of understanding sweetest in love. Adelaide dropped her eyes. Hippolyte, shy and almost speechless, took down the picture, examined it at the window, and left saying, “I will return it soon.”

That brief contact made both hearts tremble with the kind of agitation like a stone thrown into a still lake. The ripple of thought persisted all day. Hippolyte returned to his easel, prepared a fresh canvas, and worked whimsically till dinner. That evening he was again at the Baronne’s until eleven. The routine repeated—two old men arriving, the same piquet, the same small talk, the same money lost by the friend—only now Hippolyte, a little bolder, ventured to talk with Adelaide.

A week passed, and the painter’s and Adelaide’s feelings deepened with the usual slow, delicious transformations of intimacy. Each day her welcome grew warmer and more confiding; their talk grew candid and childlike, as if they’d known each other years. Schinner wanted to learn piquet and lost game after game. Without a word of love they came to be everything to one another. Hippolyte delighted in exerting a gentle influence over Adelaide; she, devoted and shy, yielded to him. Frivolous caprices between them put an end to the old familiarity with the Count: she understood the painter’s melancholy, the sudden tones he used when the old man kissed her hands.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur soon demanded accounts of Hippolyte’s comings and goings; jealous she scolded him when he delayed. Adelaide, too, showed natural jealousy at his late visits to brilliant salons. Conscience and passion combined to bind him to the little apartment on the fourth floor, where everything about the place attracted him.

Their love was pure and ardent. Confidence and delicacy made sacrifices unnecessary; the interchange of affection was continuous and mutual. Two months after their first meeting their lives were one. Adelaide awoke each morning assured he was near; Hippolyte visited his mother at dinner but always looked in on the neighbours; evenings found him with lover’s punctuality at the Rue de Suresnes. He rendered himself impeccable in her eyes, and she tasted the fulfillment of youthful dreams.

The old gentleman came less often; Hippolyte had taken his place at the card-table and shared in the same unlucky streak. Yet once in a while guilt-laden thoughts disturbed the painter—forty francs every night? An importunate suspicion, nourished by certain trifling details, haunted him. He could not confess the ugly thought to himself. Yet he could not wholly lay it aside.

Hippolyte worked two months on the portrait and felt it one of his best pieces. Madame de Rouville had never mentioned it since, whether from indifference or pride he did not know; but he and Adelaide decided to hang it while the Baroness took her usual walk. That day Adelaide went up to the studio on the pretext of seeing the picture in good light. She stood mute in ecstasy, all her feelings merged in admiration of the man she loved. When the painter leaned forward she took his hand; two tears fell. He covered her hand with kisses; for a minute they looked at one another, both longing to speak and unable. At last Adelaide said simply, “You will make my mother very happy.” “What, only your mother?” he whispered. “Oh, I am too happy.” He bowed his head, afraid of the vehemence kindled in him. They went downstairs and hung the portrait. Hippolyte dined there for the first time with the Baroness, who embraced him, weeping.

That evening the old émigré, the Baron de Rouville’s comrade, arrived to announce he had been promoted to vice-admiral; his travels overland counted as naval campaigns. Seeing the portrait, he praised it and said he’d give five hundred pistoles to be so well rendered. The Baroness exchanged a grateful look with Adelaide. Hippolyte suspected the admiral wished to buy both portraits while paying for his own; his pride and jealousy flared. “Monsieur,” he answered bluntly, “if I were a portrait-painter I should not have done this one.” The admiral sat down to cards in pique.

Hippolyte played piquet with Adelaide and observed a new excitement in Madame de Rouville towards the game: a keener desire to win and an almost visible joy in fingering the old gentleman’s gold. Suspicion stung—did Madame de Rouville gamble to live? Pay rent with cards? Why did a rich old man choose to lose there night after night? Why had he been so familiar in old days and now withheld his supposed rights to Adelaide?

These reflections sharpened his jealousy and unease. He stayed after the others left to resolve doubts. Taking out his purse to pay, he absentmindedly left it on the table and, after a brief reverie, went to fetch it. “I left my purse here!” he said. Adelaide denied it with a blush. He searched and felt his pockets: the purse was gone. Fifteen louis and change were missing. The theft, so flagrant and denied so boldly, ended his doubt about his neighbours’ morals. He was stunned: knees shaking, dizzy, cold with the blow. Minute observations, insignificant alone, now confirmed his fear and unveiled a life that, to him, seemed dishonourable. Had they waited till they received the portrait before robbing him? The thought made the deed worse. Yet love held on; he tried to rationalize—had the purse fallen? Was it still somewhere on him? He searched himself, remembered the sight of it on the green cloth and the faces of the mother and daughter. He could not accept that Adelaide’s proud, noble face was a lie.

That night he did not sleep; the heartache of losing a dreamed future was crueler than losing a known joy. He walked under the Tuileries’ shade and met his intimate friend Francois Souchet, the sculptor. When Hippolyte told his misery Souchet laughed it off as the product of a ridiculous affair: the Baronne’s shawl, her way of holding a bag—such signs, he declared, marked them as inferior. Artists joined the banter; Bridau and Bixiou added their jests about the mother’s gray gown and matin walk. They urged Hippolyte to watch their gait as proof.

The careless ridicule stung Hippolyte. He had confidences and reticences; public mockery of his love hurt him more than argument. Nevertheless, Souchet’s words lodged in his mind. He left them, torn. He loved Adelaide so much that even thinking ill of her was intolerable. He resolved, instead, to be her protector; the romantic image of a rescuer appealed to his high-strung nature. Why not love on and attempt to redeem her, if redemption were needed? The idea was noble and seductive.

At his studio he sat before the portrait, his brush idle, tears coming to his eyes. Night fell and he crossed the house, met the old admiral, and fled. He had intended to call on the ladies; the admiral’s presence chilled him. Once more he wondered what interest drew this rich prodigal to a fourth-floor hovel where he lost forty francs every evening. He suspected reasons he did not yet name.

In the following days Hippolyte threw himself into work to conquer his passion. Study partly soothed him, though the memory of Adelaide persisted. One evening he saw the ladies’ door half open and Adelaide standing in the window recess. He bowed with an air of indifference meant to be cold. Thinking of the pain his look must cause her, he suffered inwardly. Had she been waiting for him every night? Perhaps the purse had been found. He chastised himself and, ashamed of his week-long reserve, went that evening to Madame de Rouville.

At sight of Adelaide, pale and thin, all his suspicions melted. “Good heavens, what ails you?” he cried. He listened as the Baroness gave excuses—Adelaide had sat up late to finish a little piece of sewing—and Hippolyte, seeing their frank faces, felt remorse for ever having accused them. The evening was blissful. Adelaide read his thoughts and forgave him in silence. He tried to atone by renewed affection.

But when Madame de Rouville suggested playing cards—her old friend Kergarouet not letting her off—old fears flared. He hesitated but sat to play; Adelaide asked him to be her partner because he knew not the game. All through the play the Baroness and her daughter exchanged looks of intelligence that alarmed Hippolyte, especially because he was winning. At last a hand left the lovers in the Baroness’s debt. Reaching for money, he saw before him a purse Adelaide had slipped with such quickness that he almost missed it: she held the old one and was looking into it to pay her mother. The blood rushed to his face.

The purse had been replaced. The new purse, embroidered with gilt beads, contained his fifteen louis. The ornaments and taste revealed Adelaide’s care and the likelihood that she had spent her small savings on decorating it. It was a delicate, inarticulate way of saying his gift could be repaid only by affection. Overcome, Hippolyte felt small for suspecting them. Tears rose; he sprang up, clasped Adelaide, kissed her, and cried out in the artist’s blunt sincerity, “I ask for her for my wife!” He looked to the Baroness for consent.

Before she could answer a bell rang. The old vice-admiral returned with his shadow and Madame Schinner—Hippolyte’s mother—who, alarmed by slander against Adelaide, had inquired among friends and brought the admiral with righteous fury. He declared he would crop the scoundrels’ ears. He explained to Madame Schinner that he had been losing at cards on purpose to help the Baroness, a confession that cleared every suspicion.

After Madame Schinner paid her respects, the Baroness looked round at the company—Comte de Kergarouet, the Chevalier du Halga, Hippolyte and Adelaide—and, with gracious modesty, said, “So we are a family party this evening.”

PARIS, May 1832
