**A Woman of Thirty**  
*By Honoré de Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage*  

**Dedication**  
To Louis Boulanger, Painter.

**Summary**

**I. Early Mistakes**

On a bright April morning in 1813, a cabriolet arrives at the Tuileries, where an anxious father helps his beautiful daughter, Julie, out of the carriage. As they walk through the gardens, the father notes the curious glances from young men, jokingly suggesting they think she is his wife. However, Julie is preoccupied with the upcoming military review commanded by Napoleon, which she is eager to see.

The father reflects on the impending campaign that will lead to Napoleon's downfall, while Julie grows impatient as they are delayed from entering the review. When they finally reach the gates, they are turned away, and Julie expresses her disappointment, fearing she may never see the Emperor again. An officer, recognizing her, allows them entry, and Julie is captivated by the spectacle of the troops.

As the review begins, Julie's attention shifts to Colonel Victor d’Aiglemont, a handsome officer who captures her heart. The father observes his daughter's infatuation with concern, fearing for her future happiness. 

**II. A Year Later**

In March 1814, a year later, Julie and Victor are married, but their relationship is strained. They travel to Tours, where Julie's health declines. She feels trapped in a loveless marriage, haunted by her father’s warnings about Victor’s character. Despite her initial joy, she realizes that Victor is selfish and lacks the depth she desires.

Julie finds solace in her daughter, Helene, born shortly after their marriage. However, her health continues to deteriorate, and she becomes increasingly isolated. Victor, focused on his ambitions, neglects her emotional needs, leading to a growing rift between them.

**III. The Englishman**

During this time, Julie encounters Lord Grenville, an Englishman who becomes infatuated with her. Their connection deepens, and Julie feels torn between her duty as a wife and her feelings for Grenville. She struggles with guilt over her attraction while still bound to Victor.

As their relationship develops, Julie grapples with her identity and the constraints of her marriage. She seeks to maintain her dignity while navigating the complexities of love and loyalty. The tension between her desires and responsibilities intensifies, leading to moments of despair.

**IV. The Crisis**

One evening, after a confrontation with Victor, Julie confesses her feelings to Grenville. They share a passionate moment, but the reality of their situation looms over them. Julie is determined to protect her daughter from the pain of a broken home, yet she longs for the love and connection she lacks with Victor.

As the story unfolds, Julie's internal conflict escalates. She must confront the consequences of her choices and their impact on her family. The narrative explores themes of love, sacrifice, and the struggle for personal fulfillment within societal expectations.

**V. The Resolution**

Ultimately, Julie's journey leads her to a painful realization about her marriage and desires. She must decide whether to pursue her happiness at the cost of her family or remain in a loveless union for her daughter's sake. The story concludes with Julie facing the complexities of her life, embodying the struggles of many women of her time.

**II. A HIDDEN GRIEF**

Nestled between the Seine and the Loing, a flat, desolate landscape stretches out, bordered by the Forest of Fontainebleau and marked by the towns of Moret, Montereau, and Nemours. This dreary region, characterized by its monotonous horizon, is home to the old chateau of Saint-Lange, surrounded by stately avenues and gardens, yet steeped in melancholy.

In late 1820, a young woman known for her beauty and wit in Paris arrived at Saint-Lange, shocking the local villagers who had not seen gentry in years. The Marquise d’Aiglemont’s presence stirred curiosity, but her frail demeanor suggested she was retreating from a life of despair, possibly due to financial ruin linked to her husband, the Marquis, who was away in Spain.

As the Marquise isolated herself in the chateau, she rarely left her room, only dining briefly and avoiding contact with her servants. Her little girl, Helene, was often kept at a distance, as the Marquise struggled with overwhelming grief that extinguished her maternal instincts. The villagers, initially intrigued, soon lost interest as her eccentricities became apparent.

At twenty-six, the Marquise grappled with profound sorrow, having experienced a loss that left her feeling incomplete. Her anguish stemmed from a love that had been unfulfilled; she had lost a man she loved deeply, who had died to preserve her honor. Bound by societal expectations, she felt unable to express her grief openly, leading to deep internal conflict.

The Marquise’s suffering was unique, tied to the loss of a first love, a pain often unrecognized and unexpressed. This anguish, unlike other forms of grief, left her feeling hollow and disconnected from life's joys. She reflected on her past, realizing that her marriage, though socially acceptable, was devoid of true happiness.

As she sat in her room, the flat horizon mirrored her desolation. The beauty she once possessed now felt burdensome, and she questioned her existence. Memories of childhood joy contrasted sharply with her current state, leaving her feeling trapped in a life that no longer resonated with her spirit.

One day, the village priest, M. le Curé, visited her, sensing her deep sorrow. He shared a story of a father who had lost all his sons in battle yet found the strength to live on. His compassion struck a chord with the Marquise, who began to see him as a potential confidant. However, her bitterness and despair quickly returned, overshadowing any glimmer of hope.

In subsequent visits, the curé attempted to guide her towards faith and acceptance, but the Marquise remained skeptical. She expressed disdain for societal norms, particularly marriage, which she viewed as legalized oppression for women. Her reflections revealed deep-seated anger towards a world that had failed to provide her with the love and fulfillment she craved.

Despite the curé’s efforts, the Marquise felt increasingly isolated in her grief. She longed for a love that transcended societal expectations, lamenting the loss of a connection that could have brought her true happiness. Her relationship with Helene was strained, as she struggled to reconcile her feelings of motherhood with unresolved sorrow.

As spring approached, the Marquise found some distraction in managing her estate, which helped alleviate her melancholy. By October, she had grown more composed, her initial grief transforming into quieter sadness. However, as she departed the chateau, she encountered the curé once more, choosing to avoid him, aware that he had seen through her facade.

Ultimately, the Marquise’s journey reflects the complexities of grief, love, and societal expectations, illustrating how deeply personal pain can shape one’s existence and relationships.

**Summary of "At Thirty Years"**

Madame Firmiani hosted a ball where M. Charles de Vandenesse, a young diplomat, attended to bid farewell to Paris before his appointment at the Congress of Laybach. Although he enjoyed the city's pleasures, he felt detached from its superficial society, prioritizing ambition over romance at thirty.

At the ball, he met the enigmatic Marquise d’Aiglemont, known for her seclusion and mystery. Captivated by her beauty and depth, Vandenesse found her presence a stark contrast to the frivolity around him. Despite her delicate appearance, the Marquise revealed a life marked by sorrow and unfulfilled love.

Their conversation began lightly but soon turned to deeper themes of love. Vandenesse expressed regret about leaving Paris, while the Marquise suggested that happiness might outweigh intellectual pursuits. He requested to visit her again, feeling a strong connection.

During his visits, Vandenesse learned about the Marquise's tragic past—a lost love and an unhappy marriage. She confided about a man who had died for her honor, revealing the weight of her memories and the emptiness of her current life. This confession deepened Vandenesse's admiration for her noble spirit.

Despite their growing bond, the Marquise struggled with her feelings, torn between loyalty to her deceased lover and her affection for Vandenesse. She feared that pursuing new love would betray her past. Meanwhile, Vandenesse fell deeply in love, experiencing the intensity of first passion.

Their relationship evolved with moments of tenderness and unspoken understanding, yet societal constraints complicated their feelings. The Marquise's husband, General d’Aiglemont, unwittingly interrupted their intimacy, highlighting the tension between their desires and societal realities.

As time passed, the Marquise acknowledged her feelings for Vandenesse but remained conflicted about the possibility of happiness outside societal norms. Their profound love was fraught with complexities of memory, loyalty, and fear of judgment. Ultimately, the story explores the intricate dynamics of love, loss, and the struggle for personal fulfillment within the confines of social expectations.

**IV. THE FINGER OF GOD**

Between the Barrière d’Italie and the Barrière de la Santé, a picturesque view of Paris captivates artists and tourists alike. From a higher vantage point along the boulevard, one can see a valley filled with factories nestled among trees and streams, while the squalor of Faubourg Saint-Marceau lies on the opposite slope. The Panthéon and Val-du-Grace dominate the skyline, towering over the small houses below. The observatory and the elegant lantern tower of the Invalides enhance the architectural beauty, which shifts with the changing light and sky.

To the right, the canal Saint-Martin stretches out, bordered by public granaries. In the distance, the smoke-covered slopes of Belleville blend with the clouds, hinting at a vast city hidden from view. This city, caught between life and death, sends forth a muffled roar, asserting its presence.

On a sunny spring morning, I admired this vibrant landscape, reflecting on the disdain some express for France while longing for its beauty. My reverie was interrupted by a scene nearby: a young woman, radiating joy, shared a tender moment with a handsome man and their charming little boy. Their happiness contrasted sharply with the sullen demeanor of a second child, a girl named Helene, who seemed burdened by thoughts beyond her years.

As I observed, I noted the stark differences between the siblings. Helene, with her dark hair and intense gaze, appeared older than her years, while her brother, Charles, was carefree and innocent. Their mother’s subtle favoritism was evident in their clothing, hinting at deeper family dynamics. Helene’s jealousy and resentment towards her brother culminated in a tragic moment when she pushed him, causing him to fall into the river. The scene turned chaotic as the mother rushed to the water's edge, but the child was lost in the murky depths, leaving behind a haunting silence.

Years later, the Marquis de Vandenesse mourned his father and hosted a notary at dinner. The notary, oblivious to the tension in the room, monopolized the conversation, much to the Marquise de Vandenesse's frustration. She longed to join her children at the theater, but the notary continued his rambling, unaware of the couple's growing impatience.

When the Marquis finally intervened, the notary remained oblivious, continuing to discuss business matters. Just as the Marquis attempted to dismiss him, the children returned unexpectedly, revealing they had left the theater due to a distressing scene. The little girl, Helene, had been crying, and the atmosphere shifted dramatically.

The Marquise, recognizing the implications of the children’s return, felt a wave of dread. The notary, attempting to engage the children, inadvertently exacerbated the situation by asking about the play. Helene’s tears and the boy’s recounting of the play’s events revealed a deeper family tragedy, and the Marquise’s composure began to crack under the weight of her emotions.

In a moment of anger, the Marquis chastised the notary for his insensitivity, leading to a confrontation that left the notary bewildered. As he exited, he reflected on the evening’s events, unaware of the emotional turmoil he had stirred.

At home, the notary’s wife pointed out his blunders, emphasizing the need for discretion in social situations. Despite his confusion, he resolved to seek clarification from the Marquis, unaware that the complexities of human relationships often elude even the most astute observers.

**Summary of "Two Meetings"**

The narrative unfolds in spring at Versailles, centering on the General, or the Marquis, a former orderly officer of Napoleon. He has a country house near Montreuil, where he enjoys solitude while remaining close to Paris. The house, originally a retreat for a nobleman’s fleeting romances, features expansive grounds that provide seclusion despite its city proximity.

On a winter evening during Christmas, the General's family gathers at home, with servants absent due to a wedding celebration in Versailles. The atmosphere is warm and joyful as the family engages in playful activities. The General, content, watches his children, especially his youngest, Moïna, who playfully resists his wife’s attempts to dress him for bed. Their older children, Gustave and Helene, are also present, each absorbed in their own pursuits.

Helene, a beautiful and thoughtful girl, is somewhat melancholic, reflecting on her life and the expectations placed upon her. The family dynamic is affectionate but hints at underlying tensions, particularly between Helene and her mother, the Marquise. The Marquise’s strictness contrasts with Helene’s burgeoning independence and emotional depth.

As the evening progresses, a sudden commotion interrupts their domestic bliss. A stranger, pursued by unknown assailants, arrives seeking refuge. Despite initial hesitation, the General offers sanctuary, driven by honor and duty. The stranger, desperate and agitated, insists on secrecy, revealing he is in grave danger.

The General leads the stranger to a secluded room, locking him in for safety. Meanwhile, the Marquise and Helene remain in suspense, unaware of the unfolding drama. The General grapples with the moral implications of harboring the fugitive.

Tension escalates when gendarmes arrive, searching for the fugitive, who is revealed to be a murderer. The General, caught between his promise of hospitality and the law, faces a moral dilemma. When questioned by the gendarmes, he maintains composure, concealing the truth.

As night deepens, Helene, compelled by curiosity, ventures upstairs to investigate. She encounters the fugitive, a complex character embodying both danger and allure. Their charged interaction draws Helene in, creating a connection that transcends the immediate circumstances.

Upon discovering Helene’s encounter with the fugitive, the General is filled with horror and rage. He confronts the stranger, demanding he leave. The fugitive challenges the General’s authority, asserting his moral justification. This confrontation reveals Helene’s feelings and the conflict between her loyalty to her family and her emerging sense of self.

In a climactic moment, Helene declares her intention to leave with the fugitive, asserting her autonomy and rejecting her mother’s authority. The General, devastated by his daughter’s choice, feels a profound sense of loss. Helene’s departure marks a turning point, as she embraces a life defined by passion and danger, leaving behind her family’s safety.

The narrative shifts to the aftermath of Helene’s decision, exploring the consequences of her choice. The General, haunted by the loss of his daughter, reflects on the fragility of familial bonds and the complexities of love and duty. The story concludes with a poignant reminder of the sacrifices made in the name of love, as the General grapples with the reality of his daughter’s new life and the irrevocable changes it brings to their family.

Ultimately, "Two Meetings" delves into themes of love, sacrifice, and the moral complexities of human relationships, set against a tumultuous historical backdrop. The characters navigate their desires and responsibilities, leading to a powerful exploration of the choices that define their lives.

**Summary of "The Old Age of a Guilty Mother"**

On a June day in 1844, the Marquise d’Aiglemont, around fifty, strolls through her daughter’s mansion garden in Paris. After losing her two sons, she has devoted her life and fortune to her youngest child, the Comtesse Moïna de Saint-Héreen. Her deep affection for Moïna contrasts with her more muted feelings for her deceased children, leading society to view her devotion as both admirable and questionable, as she has sacrificed her own life for her daughter's happiness.

Moïna, beautiful and charming, has become the Marquise's entire focus. However, the Marquise is deeply concerned about Moïna's flirtation with Alfred de Vandenesse, whom she considers unscrupulous. Despite her fears, she feels powerless to intervene, knowing Moïna would dismiss her warnings. Complicating matters is the Marquise's friendship with Alfred's father, which further hinders her ability to express disapproval.

As the Marquise reflects on her life, she feels sorrow and resignation, recognizing the toll her emotional struggles have taken on her health. Tears fall as she contemplates Moïna's future and the potential consequences of her choices.

When the Marquise tries to discuss Moïna's relationship with Alfred, she faces resistance and indifference. Moïna's dismissive attitude deepens her mother's anguish. After a painful exchange, the Marquise leaves, overwhelmed by the realization that her daughter no longer respects her.

In despair, the Marquise collapses in the garden, and her lifeless body is carried inside. Initially preoccupied with her own feelings, Moïna is struck by the seriousness of her mother’s condition. Witnessing her mother’s suffering, she begins to grasp the depth of her love and sacrifices.

The story concludes with Moïna mourning the mother she took for granted, realizing her loss. The Marquise's final moments highlight the profound bond between mother and daughter, emphasizing themes of love, regret, and the complexities of familial relationships. This narrative poignantly explores motherhood, sacrifice, and the often-unrecognized struggles that shape women's lives.
