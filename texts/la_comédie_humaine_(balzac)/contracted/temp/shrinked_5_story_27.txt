In 1828, at around one o’clock in the morning, Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac left a house in the Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré and walked to the boulevard because neither had a carriage. Their talk turned to the Marquise d’Espard. Bianchon was skeptical of her character; Rastignac, infatuated with her status and beauty, believed she could elevate his social standing and help pay his debts.

Bianchon warned Rastignac that pursuing the Marquise would be a mistake, likening her to a “one-eyed horse” and suggesting her real age exceeded her claims. He argued a woman’s physical signs reveal truth despite cosmetics. Rastignac countered that he sought the Marquise for social power and the opportunities she offered, dismissing Bianchon’s moral reservations.

A physician, Bianchon expressed disdain for fashionable women, asserting they often lack genuine emotion and are motivated by selfishness. He described the Marquise as a polished figure who conceals her true nature behind a facade. Rastignac maintained that a woman of fashion could be a valuable ally for an ambitious man, providing connections and influence.

Despite his misgivings, Bianchon agreed to help by introducing Rastignac to his uncle Jean-Jules Popinot, a judge who might assist in the Marquise’s affairs. Yet Bianchon doubted Popinot’s willingness, given the judge’s reputation for inflexibility.

The next morning Bianchon set out for Popinot’s residence in a poor, dilapidated part of Paris. The building’s description emphasized its age and the contrast between past grandeur and present decay. Popinot himself was unkempt and lacked ambition, his career stalled despite intelligence and capability; he had been passed over for promotions and remained a supernumerary judge.

Popinot’s life was defined by a commitment to justice, often to his own detriment. He was known for kindness and a talent for extracting confessions without coercion, combining compassion with firmness in his judicial role. His dedication to duty coexisted with frustration over the injustices he witnessed in the legal system.

As Bianchon approached Popinot’s door, he reflected on the difficulty of persuading his uncle to assist Rastignac, conscious of the complexities of the legal world and the obstacles that lay ahead.

Twelve years before this narrative, in 1816, during a severe food scarcity in France, Popinot was appointed President of the Commission Extraordinary to distribute food to the impoverished in his neighborhood. He had intended to leave the Rue du Fouarre, a place he and his wife disliked. As a prominent lawyer and criminal judge, Popinot had spent five years observing legal outcomes without understanding their causes. His experiences with the destitute fostered deep compassion, transforming him into a protector of the poor, akin to a modern Saint Vincent de Paul.

The change was gradual; charity, like vice, has its temptations. Popinot moved from one act of charity to another, becoming a central figure in his community and involved in various charitable organizations. He worked tirelessly to prevent crime, provide employment, and assist the vulnerable, while maintaining a private life that few in Paris recognized. The gratitude of those he helped was often unspoken, as they were too burdened to acknowledge it.

By the end of his second year of charitable work, Popinot had converted a storeroom in his home into a parlor for the needy. Furnished simply with wooden benches and basic chairs, the room became a hub where the poor could seek assistance. Popinot meticulously recorded donations and requests, ensuring he could manage resources effectively. His aide, Lavienne, handled pawn tickets and visited areas most affected by famine.

From early morning until late, the parlor was crowded with women, children, and the destitute seeking help. The atmosphere was frequently stifling, but the crowd's warmth made heating unnecessary. The poor revered Popinot, often waiting outside before dawn; even criminals respected his home. He dedicated mornings to the needy, afternoons to legal work, and evenings to his judicial duties.

Popinot’s keen observation allowed him to discern hidden virtues in the impoverished and the faintest signs of wrongdoing in others. His modest inherited fortune was supplemented by his late wife's wealth, but his salary as a judge was limited, necessitating frugality that his charitable efforts further strained. Despite significant contributions, he remained one of the few judges in the Court of the Seine without the Legion of Honor.

The President of the Second Chamber of the Court tasked Popinot with examining the Marquis d’Espard at the request of the Marquis’s wife, who sought a Commission in Lunacy. The Rue du Fouarre, bustling with the needy in the early hours, would be desolate by nine. Bianchon, Popinot’s nephew, rode to find his uncle amid his charitable duties, expecting a contrast between Popinot’s appearance and the Marquise d’Espard’s expectations.

On arrival, Bianchon found the parlor full—a tableau of hardship, with faces showing despair and resilience. Popinot, in a worn nightcap and dressing gown, was engrossed in his work, embodying a man committed to alleviating suffering. Lavienne facilitated the flow of requests, keeping the process efficient.

Bianchon interrupted with an urgent matter while Popinot handled consultations. After a brief exchange, Popinot resumed attending to a woman who needed funds to sustain her grocery business while her husband was imprisoned for debt; he provided assistance and documented the transaction in his ledger.

As the morning progressed, Bianchon learned that Popinot had seen over two hundred individuals that day, with more waiting. He was sent to attend urgent medical matters; on his return he found the parlor empty, the last of the needy having been helped.

Popinot’s home reflected his chaotic life: dust, disarray, papers cluttering every surface, and the lingering remnants of his late wife’s possessions. Despite the disorder, tokens of gratitude from those he aided adorned the room, poignant reminders of his impact.

Preparing for breakfast, Bianchon urged Popinot to dress suitably for the upcoming meeting with the Marquise d’Espard. Popinot resisted, citing legal restrictions against magistrates accepting hospitality from parties involved in cases they oversee. Bianchon argued it would be beneficial for Popinot to meet her in her own environment, where she could express concerns more comfortably.

After some persuasion, Popinot agreed to visit the Marquise, recognizing the importance of understanding her perspective in the case. He made a note of the appointment and prepared to delve into the complexities surrounding the Marquis d’Espard’s mental state and the implications for his family.

The next evening at nine, Bianchon climbed his uncle’s dusty staircase to find him working on a complicated judgment. Since the tailor had not sent the coat Lavienne ordered, Popinot wore his old stained coat, which amused those unaware of his private life. Bianchon straightened his uncle's cravat and buttoned his coat, attempting to hide the stains, but the judge's habitual movements soon rucked it up, revealing a gap between his waistcoat and trousers. Bianchon realized this absurdity just as his uncle entered the Marquise’s room.

To understand the ensuing interview, a brief overview of Madame d’Espard is necessary. For seven years, she had been a fashionable figure in Paris, where trends can elevate or diminish individuals. After separating from her husband in 1815, Madame d’Espard, around thirty-three, maintained her status as a fashionable woman, despite being a mother of two boys aged fifteen and thirteen. Her ability to remain in vogue was remarkable, as Fashion often favors youth and beauty.

The Marquise, who appeared much younger, employed various methods to maintain her looks, including elaborate hairstyles and a strict regimen of cold baths and minimal food. Her efforts mirrored those of a famous Polish lady who, despite her age, retained the vitality of youth. Madame d’Espard’s complexion was clear, and her figure retained the allure that attracted men, though she remained emotionally detached, knowing neither love nor hatred. She had parted from M. d’Espard with satisfaction, as he took their children, whom she viewed as obstacles to her ambitions.

Living on an income of twenty-six thousand francs a year, the Marquise hesitated before deciding how to manage her life post-separation. Although she had the means to live lavishly, she chose a more subdued lifestyle during the years following her separation. By 1820, she re-emerged into society, hosting gatherings and establishing herself among the elite, eventually taking the place of other prominent women in Paris.

Madame d’Espard cultivated relationships with influential figures, including a duchess and a countess, which enhanced her political clout. Her drawing-room became a hub for political discussions, and she was known for discretion and an ability to navigate the complexities of Parisian society. Among her admirers were ambitious young men, and she skillfully maintained her independence while wielding considerable influence.

Despite her apparent charm, Madame d’Espard was also known for indiscretion and vanity. She viewed relationships as means to an end, seeking to expand her influence rather than genuine connections. Her drawing-room was frequented by notable figures, and she adeptly managed interactions to avoid compromising herself.

Rastignac, a young man seeking to align himself with her, recognized her potential as a powerful ally but found himself challenged by her cunning. The Marquise’s household was grand, reflecting her wealth and status, and when Popinot arrived, he noted the opulence around him. The Marquise welcomed him with feigned weakness, and a third man, the Chevalier d’Espard, stood silently, observing.

Popinot, while appearing simple, was astute and quickly assessed the situation. He engaged Madame d’Espard in conversation, questioning her about her husband’s mental state and their separation. She presented a carefully crafted narrative, claiming her husband’s change in temperament led to their estrangement. Popinot, however, remained skeptical, probing deeper into the circumstances surrounding their separation and the well-being of her children.

As the conversation progressed, Popinot’s inquiries revealed inconsistencies in Madame d’Espard’s story. He noted the lavishness of her lifestyle compared to her claims of financial hardship, suggesting her motives for seeking control over her husband’s affairs might be self-serving. The Marquise, feeling cornered, attempted to deflect his questions, but Popinot’s persistence forced her to confront the implications of her situation.

The tension in the room escalated as Popinot laid bare the potential consequences of her actions. He emphasized the need for transparency and honesty, warning her that the court would scrutinize her motives. Despite her attempts to maintain composure, the Marquise realized that her carefully constructed facade was at risk of crumbling.

After the meeting, Bianchon and Rastignac discussed Popinot’s character and the implications of the case. They recognized the complexity of the situation and the potential for scandal, with Bianchon expressing concern over the Marquise’s influence. Popinot, meanwhile, resolved to investigate further, planning to summon Madame Jeanrenaud, the woman linked to M. d’Espard, to clarify the situation.

The following day, Madame Jeanrenaud arrived at Popinot’s office, presenting a stark contrast to the Marquise. Described as stout and unrefined, she denied any wrongdoing and expressed concern for M. d’Espard’s well-being. Her honest demeanor and straightforwardness provided a clear contrast to the Marquise’s calculated charm.

Popinot, intrigued by her sincerity, began to piece together the narrative surrounding M. d’Espard’s financial dealings. He recognized that the case was more complicated than it initially appeared, with potential implications for both the Marquise and her husband. As he prepared to confront the complexities of the situation, he understood that the truth would require careful examination of all parties involved.

People who have matured understand how trivial incidents can significantly influence major events, as Popinot's minor illness delayed a crucial meeting. The judge, feeling unwell, stayed home instead of visiting the Marquis d’Espard, causing a lost day that echoed historical delays with wide consequences.

Before following the lawyer and his clerk to the Marquis d’Espard’s residence, consider the home life of this father, whom his wife’s petition labeled a madman. In old Paris, some buildings still show a taste for decoration and permanence. The house where M. d’Espard lived on Rue de la Montagne-Sainte-Genevieve was one such stone structure: once grand, now weathered and damaged by time and revolutions. Formerly home to dignitaries, it had become a multi-tenant residence, its interior degraded by a printing establishment.

M. d’Espard took the ground floor, likely for his children’s sake, since the garden was unusually spacious for the area. He secured the rooms at a reasonable rent, given the building’s disrepair and the depressed market. The house, dating from the reigns of Henry III–Louis XIII, still bore traces of former glory, including a perron and a garden.

The Marquis had furnished his home in rich brown tones, creating a peaceful atmosphere with carefully chosen pieces and a harmonious scheme. Few visitors were admitted to the family’s private life, which remained largely secluded. The household consisted of the Marquis, his two sons, and a small staff, including a long-serving cook and a man-servant, both contributing to the orderly quiet of the house.

M. d’Espard avoided socializing with other tenants to shield his children from outside influences. That choice, however, bred gossip and suspicion: neighbors labeled him mad and his servants untrustworthy. Tenants believed the Marquis was financially ruined, a view reinforced by his extreme frugality and habit of paying cash, avoiding credit.

The Marquis and his family were noble by birth, embodying ideals that had become rare in France. They retained a deep sense of dignity and pride in their lineage, which clashed with the egalitarian sentiments of their neighbors. This disconnect fostered animosity, as tenants resented the d’Espards for perceived superiority despite apparent financial strain.

M. d’Espard, about fifty, exemplified the aristocracy of his time. He had a distinguished, somewhat cold appearance that commanded respect. Physical traits — a slightly crooked nose and a prominent forehead — gave an air of eccentricity that fueled rumors of madness. Despite elegant manners, he was frugal, wearing the same coat for years.

His sons, Comte Clément de Négrepelisse and Vicomte Camille d’Espard, were handsome and displayed aristocratic grace. They inherited their father’s features and their mother’s dignified manner. The elder, nearly sixteen, had moved to more fashionable attire, while the younger still wore a round jacket. The Marquis concentrated on giving them a rounded education, emphasizing mathematics, Eastern languages, and history, while avoiding what he considered unproductive studies.

The day Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard was a Thursday holiday. Around nine, before their father woke, the brothers were playing in the garden. Clement tried to refuse his brother, who begged to visit the shooting-gallery for the first time and urged him to back the request to the Marquis. The Viscount often exploited Clement's weakness and enjoyed wrestling with him. Their noisy quarrel woke their father, who watched amused from the window. Two neighbors commented on the Marquis’ apparent madness for making his children fight. Noticing them, the Marquis called his sons; they jumped into his room, and Clement received permission from Camille.

Throughout the house people discussed the Marquis’ alleged new madness. When Popinot arrived near noon with his clerk, the portress led him to the third floor, remarking that M. d’Espard had made his children fight that morning and laughed when the younger bit the elder until he bled. She added that he rarely showed himself. As they reached a door plastered with notices for the Picturesque History of China, the landing’s muddy floor and dirty banisters corroborated her story and led Popinot to credit her testimony despite his impartiality.

“There you are, gentlemen,” said the porter's wife, “the man who swallows enough to feed the whole neighborhood.” The clerk smiled; Popinot struggled to remain composed. They entered an outer room where an old man, likely the office clerk, sat among long shelves of published numbers; a partition with a grating formed a private office and a cash-desk hinted at the venture’s commercial side.

“M. d’Espard?” Popinot asked the man in a gray blouse. The shopman opened the next room to reveal a venerable, simply dressed old man wearing the Cross of Saint-Louis seated at a desk. “The Marquis d’Espard?” Popinot inquired. The old man rose. “No, monsieur. What do you want with him?” Popinot explained they wished to speak on personal business. The old man announced their intent to the Marquis, who sat in the furthest room reading.

The room’s shabby furnishings belied the dignity of its occupant. After courtesies, the Marquis asked their purpose. Popinot, measuring him, said, “Monsieur le Marquis, my functions and the inquiry that brought me here require us to be alone, though such inquiries are generally public. I am a judge on the Inferior Court of Appeal for the Department of the Seine, charged with examining you regarding a petition for a Commission in Lunacy filed by the Marquise d’Espard.” The old man withdrew; the clerk sat to take notes. The Marquis reddened with anger and trembled before steadying himself.

“How is it, monsieur,” he asked, “that I have had no notice of such a petition?” Popinot explained that notice is unnecessary since those subject to such a commission are presumed not to have use of reason; the Court must verify the petitioner’s claims. The Marquis accepted this and asked what he must do. Popinot urged him to answer fully and confidentially, especially about delicate reasons Madame d’Espard might allege. The Marquis worried that his explanations could bring blame upon his wife. Popinot promised discretion and the usual courtesy should the inquiry find misunderstanding.

Popinot then raised a major allegation: that the Marquis had dissipated his fortune for the benefit of a Madame Jeanrenaud, widow of a bargemaster, or her son Colonel Jeanrenaud, for whom he allegedly procured an appointment and exhausted influence with the King. The Marquis’s face flushed; tears rose, but pride returned quickly.

“You place me in a strange dilemma,” he said. Revealing his motives would mean disclosing secret wounds and trusting the family’s honor to strangers. Popinot assured him of confidentiality. The Marquis then spoke clearly and passionately, freed from his usual reserve.

After his marriage his wife had incurred heavy expenses, forcing him to borrow. He could not afford a steward; most of his title-deeds had been carried to Paris by his father, who feared scrutiny. Their family name was Negrepelisse; d’Espard was a title acquired through marriage in Henri IV’s time when they lost Negrepelisse, a town famous in religious wars, and an ancestor was ruined by the destruction of his property. The Crown had been unjust to M. de Negrepelisse; though his great-grandfather later secured favor with Louis XIV and fortune, a stain of blood and disgrace weighed on the family—and the Marquis was determined to erase it.

He discovered that his grandfather had acquired the lands of Negrepelisse by means tied to the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes: a Protestant merchant was arrested and hanged, and the land passed to his ancestor through iniquitous means. The merchant’s family had the rightful claim. The victim’s name was Jeanrenaud. The Marquis could not bear the disgrace and sought the heirs. Eventually he found M. Jeanrenaud, reduced to poverty and serving as a lieutenant under Napoleon. Believing their rights incontestable and that they lacked means to appeal, he offered to pay them the lands’ value at the time they were taken—eleven hundred thousand francs—payable at his convenience and without interest.

To make restitution he forewent his income for a long time. When he proposed to Madame d’Espard that they move to the country to live on half her income, she treated him as mad; her coldness convinced him she would have condoned his ancestors’ crimes. Terrified by her attitude, he resolved to leave her in control of her fortune after settling debts and to take charge of educating their children himself, determined to raise them as gentlemen. He invested prudently, repaid the debt sooner than expected, and had recently completed the payment of the eleven hundred thousand francs, effecting restitution without harming his children. These were the reasons for payments to Madame Jeanrenaud and her son.

Popinot asked if Madame d’Espard knew his motives for retiring from public life. “Yes, monsieur,” the Marquis replied. Popinot, visibly moved, called his clerk away. He then asked about other allegations, such as that the Marquis was conducting a business unbecoming his rank. The Marquis declined to discuss it there and summoned Nouvion, an old family friend, stating he used the rooms as offices for the History’s publication by a reputable firm, not as his private residence.

He showed Popinot his ground-floor apartment—a place of tender harmony that touched the judge. The pleasant weather and garden air convinced Popinot that a madman would hardly produce such beauty. The Marquis said he intended to stay until his younger son finished his studies, then travel with the children to European capitals to deepen their education and language skills. He explained his China project: the Comte de Nouvion, returned to France without fortune, was his partner; the Abbe Grozier, Keeper of the Books at the Arsenal, had been his tutor and taught him about China. Though enamored of China’s history, he remained a French gentleman. Financially, they had two thousand five hundred subscribers—twelve hundred in France—the book would cost about three hundred francs, and the Comte would earn six to seven thousand francs a year from it, which was the real motive; the hundred thousand francs he hoped to make would cover his children’s education and pleasures. Twelve years of retirement to educate his children had cost him his Court remembrance and political prospects, but he believed he had purified his family’s past and secured a glorious future for his children, a noble though secret task.

At that moment horses sounded in the courtyard. The two boys entered, bright with health, dressed for riding. They greeted their father and the lawyer. Popinot needed no further evidence of the Marquis’ relationship with his sons. The boys reported shooting success and that they had ridden in the Bois, where they had seen their mother. “Why didn’t you speak to her?” the Marquis asked. “I think I noticed, father, that she doesn’t want us to speak to her in public,” Clement whispered. Popinot noticed a cloud pass over the Marquis’ brow and felt deep sympathy at the scene of fatherly devotion. “Justice may look in here at any time,” the Marquis said. “If anyone is mad, it can only be the children, who are a little crazy about their father, and the father who is very crazy about his children—but that kind of madness rings true.”

Madame Jeanrenaud then entered, disregarding protests from the man-servant. “I take no roundabout ways!” she exclaimed. Bowing to the company, she announced she had come too late because the criminal judge was present. She declared that she and her son were of one mind to return everything, as their honor was threatened, preferring to give back everything rather than cause trouble. “They must be as stupid as pans without handles to call you a lunatic,” she added. The boys clung to their father, alarmed.

“Silence, madame,” Popinot interposed. The Marquis sent his children to the garden. Popinot addressed Madame Jeanrenaud: the payments made by the Marquis were legally due, though prompted by an expansive sense of honesty. If every possessor of confiscated goods were compelled to make restitution every century and a half, few legitimate owners would remain in France. The law allowed M. d’Espard to dispose of his income without accounting for it. A Commission in Lunacy is only appropriate when actions lack reason; here, the remittances were driven by the highest motives. She could keep the money without remorse and let the world misinterpret a noble action. Popinot declared he would rather see the Marquis crowned than subjected to a Commission. Madame Jeanrenaud applauded his defense, calling it judge-like and warmly praising his words.

The Marquis extended his hand to Popinot, who gently pressed it; a private understanding passed between the commonplace yet humane judge and the noble, sublime man. “Monsieur le Marquis,” Popinot added, “from the first words of this inquiry, I regarded my clerk as unnecessary.” Leading M. d’Espard to the window, he said it was time to return home and that Madame la Marquise had likely acted under an influence the Marquis should counteract. Popinot left, reflecting on the scene, which would remain with him. “Those rooms would suit me perfectly,” he thought on his way back. “If M. d’Espard leaves them, I will take up his lease.”

The next morning, having written his report the night before, Popinot went to the Palais de Justice to secure prompt justice. As he prepared to put on his gown, the usher said the President wished to see him privately. “Good morning, my dear Popinot,” the President greeted him. “I’ve been waiting.” Popinot asked if something was wrong. The President called it a silly trifle: the Keeper of the Seals, with whom he had dined, had suggested it would be best if Popinot did not sit on the case. Despite Popinot’s protest that he left Madame d’Espard’s house before tea, the President cited propriety—“Caesar’s wife must not be suspected”—and the need to avoid any appearance of conflict that could endanger the Bench’s dignity in the face of journalism’s malice. He would make it a matter of propriety, not discipline.

Popinot was to signal his retirement from the case as a personal favor; in compensation, the President would secure the Cross of the Legion of Honor for him, long deserved. M. Camusot, a newly appointed judge from a provincial court, had been named in Popinot’s place. Popinot noted with ironic amusement Camusot’s pale ambition. He withdrew with a bow, choosing not to contest the false implication.
