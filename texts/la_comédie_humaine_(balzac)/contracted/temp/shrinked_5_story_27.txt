In 1828 Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac walked through Paris discussing Rastignac’s interest in the Marquise d’Espard. Bianchon warned him against pursuing her, likening her to a “one‑eyed horse” and insisting she was less youthful than Rastignac imagined. Though Bianchon distrusted the Marquise’s sincerity and feared hidden debts, Rastignac was attracted to her wealth and social position, convinced she could advance his ambitions.

Bianchon offered to introduce Rastignac to his uncle, Judge Jean‑Jules Popinot, a man of integrity whose modesty kept him out of public notice. Approaching Popinot’s house, Bianchon reflected on how hard it might be to persuade the judge to assist Rastignac, underscoring themes of ambition and social expectation.

Twelve years earlier Popinot had been charged with distributing food during a severe scarcity. Initially reluctant, he became devoted to relieving suffering, shifting from a strictly legal judge to a compassionate benefactor, almost saintlike. His charitable work exhausted his means, yet he found meaning in helping the poor, even converting his storeroom into a parlor for those in need.

The narrative moves to a case involving the Marquis d’Espard, for whom his wife sought a Commission in Lunacy. Bianchon found Popinot occupied with petitioners and absorbed in charity when he read the petition alleging the Marquis’s mental decline and weighed the Marquise’s motives.

The following evening Bianchon discovered Popinot engrossed in a difficult judgment, disheveled from prolonged work. They visited Madame d’Espard, who presented herself as a victim of her husband’s behavior. Popinot questioned her motives, noting that her luxurious lifestyle undercut claims of distress. After the meeting he resolved to investigate further, planning to summon Madame Jeanrenaud, connected to M. d’Espard. The case promised to reveal both the Marquis’s true condition and the lengths people would go to protect their interests in Parisian society.

The text follows M. le Marquis d’Espard, a nobleman in a decaying Paris mansion, showing how small events can have large consequences: a minor illness delayed a crucial meeting with a judge, illustrating the ripple effects of trivial incidents.

D’Espard’s once-grand house—chosen for its garden to aid his children’s health—retained a peaceful atmosphere despite decline. His private life depended on loyal servants, but his decision to isolate his children provoked misunderstandings; neighbors labeled him eccentric. Gossip, fueled by his frugality, apparent financial ruin, and tenants’ envy, painted him as mad.

He and his wife raised their sons, Comte Clément and Vicomte Camille, with dignity. Around fifty, the Marquis remained refined but reserved; the narrative highlights the complexities of social status and perceptions of declining nobility in a changing France.

On a Thursday, Popinot came to question M. d’Espard but learned of the alleged madness and a petition by the wife for a Commission in Lunacy. Distressed by accusations involving Madame Jeanrenaud, the Marquis disclosed a family secret about the Jeanrenaud fortune that had strained his finances. Moved by the Marquis’s honorable motives, Popinot listened carefully, reflected on the encounter, and accepted recusal to preserve the Court’s integrity.

The episode explores themes of honor, familial duty, and the tangled realities of social standing.
