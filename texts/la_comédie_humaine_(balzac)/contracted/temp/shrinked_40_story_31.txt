THE LILY OF THE VALLEY

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

To Monsieur J. B. Nacquart,
Member of the Royal Academy of Medicine.

Dear Doctor—Here is one of the most carefully hewn stones in the second course of the foundation of a literary edifice which I have slowly and laboriously constructed. I wish to inscribe your name upon it, as much to thank the man whose science once saved me as to honor the friend of my daily life.

De Balzac.

ENVOI

Felix de Vandenesse to Madame la Comtesse Natalie de Manerville:

I yield to your wishes. It is the privilege of the women whom we love more than they love us to make the men who love them ignore the ordinary rules of common-sense. To smooth the frown upon their brow, to soften the pout upon their lips, what obstacles we miraculously overcome! We shed our blood, we risk our future!

You exact the history of my past life; here it is. But remember this, Natalie; in obeying you I crush under foot a reluctance hitherto unconquerable. Why are you jealous of the sudden reveries which overtake me in the midst of our happiness? Why show the pretty anger of a petted woman when silence grasps me? Could you not play upon the contradictions of my character without inquiring into the causes of them? Are there secrets in your heart which seek absolution through a knowledge of mine? Ah! Natalie, you have guessed mine; and it is better you should know the whole truth. Yes, my life is shadowed by a phantom; a word evokes it; it hovers about me and within my soul are solemn memories, buried in its depths like those marine productions seen in calm weather and which the ocean’s storms cast in fragments on the shore.

The mental labor which the expression of ideas necessitates has revived the old, old feelings which give me so much pain when they come suddenly; and if in this confession of my past they break forth in a way that wounds you, remember that you threatened to punish me if I did not obey your wishes, and do not, therefore, punish my obedience. I would that this, my confidence, might increase your love.

Until we meet,

Felix.

CHAPTER I. TWO CHILDHOODS

What genius fed on tears shall one day write the tale of tortures silently borne by souls whose tender roots find stony domestic soil, whose earliest buds are torn by those appointed to nurture them? Such hearts oppressed by caretakers would tell the true history of my childhood.

What vanity or infirmity had I provoked as a newborn to earn my mother’s coldness? Sent to nurse in the country and forgotten for more than three years, I returned to a household where servants pitied my neglect and my siblings, taught by fear and flattery, turned childish cruelty into sport. No compact among children protected me; I was punished for my brother’s faults and denied any chance of proving injustice. Whether by imitation, desire to test power, or sheer lack of pity, they withheld fraternal tenderness.

Disinherited of affection, I could not show love outwardly, though nature had made me loving. Many in such circumstances harden into hatred; in me the suppressed feelings formed a reservoir that later gushed into life. Constant torments developed moral resistance rather than submissiveness. Expecting fresh griefs, I lived in sullen resignation that smothered childhood gaiety, appearing foolish enough to seem to justify my mother’s prophecies. The certainty of injustice prematurely roused my pride and perhaps checked evil tendencies my upbringing might have encouraged.

Though my mother neglected me, she sometimes spoke of my education as if to attend to it herself; the thought chilled me. Daily intercourse with her would have been torture; I blessed the neglect that let me stay alone in the garden, play with pebbles, watch insects, and gaze at the sky. My taste for contemplation was first awakened by an incident that illustrates my early afflictions. Once the governess forgot to send me to bed; I crouched under a fig-tree watching a star. When a garden faucet drenched the other children, Mademoiselle Caroline falsely accused me. I denied it, was punished, and forbidden to stay after dark. Tyrannical restraints enhance the charm of the forbidden; children think of nothing but what’s forbidden. I was often whipped for my star. Unable to confide in my kind, I told it my troubles in that inward prattle with which we stammer our first ideas. At twelve, long after school began, I still watched that star with indescribable delight.

My brother Charles, five years older and my parents’ favorite, was robust and had a tutor; I, puny and ill, became a day pupil at five, taken to school by my father’s valet with a scanty lunch while others enjoyed rillons. My comrades teased me and flaunted their food. Ridiculed, I sometimes fought but had no protection. Once I was struck in the back by stones tied in a handkerchief. When the valet told my mother she exclaimed, “That dreadful child! he will always be a torment to us.”

At school I sank into distrust of myself. The boys I liked were scamps, and pride held me aloof. The master, noting my gloom and unfriendliness, confirmed my family’s verdict. When I could read and write, my mother sent me to Pont-le-Voy to the Oratorians’ “class of the Latin steps” for dull lads. I remained eight years, seen rarely by my family, living the life of a pariah partly because I received but three francs a month—barely enough for pens and ink. Unable to buy playthings, I lost admission to games and refused to flatter the wealthy. I lived under a tree, reading books lent monthly by the librarian. How many griefs lay in that solitude; what genuine anguish filled my neglected life! At the distribution of prizes, where I won awards for theme and translation, neither parent was present. Instead of kissing the distributor, I burst into tears and threw myself on his breast; that night I burned my crowns in the stove. While the parents of other boys were in town, my parents, though only a few miles away, did not come.

My unhappiness grew as my social spheres widened. I wrote passionate letters to persuade them to see me; they mocked my style. I implored my sisters in vain. As the distribution of prizes neared I doubled my entreaties and awaited them with a beating heart. The old porter called my comrades one by one; never did he call my name. Once, accusing myself to my confessor of cursing my life, he pointed to heaven and recalled “Beati qui lugent.” From my first communion I plunged into prayer, drawn to religious ideas; my ecstasy brought dreams that fed my imagination and strengthened my thought. I attribute those visions to the guardian angel that moulded my spirit, giving me the faculty of seeing the inner soul of things.

My father, doubting the Oratorians, took me from Pont-le-Voy and at fifteen sent me to an institution in the Marais under Monsieur Lepitre. Examined, I—who had been in rhetoric—was placed in the third class. The sufferings of family and school continued at Lepitre. My father gave no money; Monsieur Lepitre, though sympathetic, could only help modestly. The Lepitre establishment in the Joyeuse mansion had a porter named Doisy who breakfasted with well-to-do pupils and smuggled in luxuries. Long I resisted Doisy’s blandishments, but finally I yielded to a café-au-lait and bread—small dissolutions but mighty to a chastened boy.

About the end of the second year my parents came to Paris. My brother wrote that they would dine at the Palais-Royal and see the Théâtre-Français; I was excited but ashamed because I owed Doisy a hundred francs. I begged my brother to be Doisy’s mouthpiece. My father inclined to forgiveness, but my mother raged: “What should I be later if at seventeen I committed such follies? Did I mean to ruin my family?” Sent back to school in charge of my brother, I lost the dinner and the play. Such was my first interview with my mother after twelve years’ separation.

When school ended my father left me under Monsieur Lepitre’s guardianship to study higher mathematics, a year of law, and philosophy. Though nineteen, my parents insisted on restrictive oversight. Lepitre sent a man-of-all-work to escort me to the law school. Paris alarmed them; I was watched as if I were about to commit a crime. The Palais-Royal, an Eldorado of delights, remained an asymptote to me, impossible to reach. Presented to an aunt on the Île Saint-Louis, the Marquise de Listomère, I dined under surveillance and once meant to escape to the Palais-Royal, but Lepitre’s coach and my mother’s arrival thwarted me. Fate interposed three times between me and that pleasure; once my mother appeared in a post-chaise and took me away when my life there seemed poised on the verge of ruin.

Driven by repressed desires and weariness, study became a passion and nearly fatal to my health. At twenty, small and thin, I had read and thought so much that I knew life metaphysically, poised between puberty and manhood, ready to feel and to love.

The journey from Paris to Tours chilled me with my mother’s coldness. At Orléans I fell at her feet, clasped her knees, implored her; she called me unnatural. At Blois I went to the parapet of the bridge to drown myself; the height prevented it. At home my sisters were at first surprised but soon showed relative kindness. I received a third-story garret; my mother left me without linen or clothes. She was tall, spare, card-playing, egotistical and insolent; her religion was duty. Despite her despotism, our love for our mother lasted until later judgment.

In despair I took refuge in my father’s library and read all I could. Sometimes my eldest sister tried to comfort me, but often I desired to die. The return of the Bourbons stirred Touraine; the Duc d’Angoulême’s procession excited a festival and a ball at which I longed to be present. My mother, too ill to go, angrily resisted my request, then secretly had a bottle-blue coat made for me. In that costume I sneaked into a tent to see the duke. Suffocated by heat and dazzled by the spectacle, I felt disgust and withdrew to a corner, motionless and sullen. Mistaken for a sleepy child, a woman sat beside me; my senses were arrested. I kissed her white, rounded shoulder as a child might a mother’s breast. She cried, turned, and said, “Monsieur!” Her saintly anger and noble face struck me; tears fell. She moved away like a queen.

That stolen kiss altered me. A new soul had burst its chrysalis. My star descended and became a woman. The first irruption of love in a man’s heart is strange: I had seen women before but none like her. Some conjunction made that woman unique to me at that hour. The thought that she lived in Touraine made the air delicious. My mother, fearing for my health, sent me to Frapesle, a château on the Indre belonging to a friend, offering the freedom I craved. I determined to search every chateau on foot, convinced she might live in any one of them.

Leaving Tours, I walked across moors and by the Indre. At midday, beneath a walnut-tree, I saw the château whose white cambric dress gleamed among the vines—Clochegourde. Love filled the valley; the river Indre’s ribbon, poplars, oak-woods, and dim horizons became part of my vision. If you would see nature beautiful and virgin as a bride, go there in spring. My heart deceived me not—she lived there.

My host at Frapesle carried me over his estate and, as we ascended, I kept looking toward the pretty castle. “It is Clochegourde,” he said; “a pretty house belonging to the Comte de Mortsauf, whose wife is a demoiselle de Lenoncourt. They never leave Clochegourde. Madame de Mortsauf is a woman who would hold the highest position wherever she might be.” When he added that she had been at the Duc d’Angouleme’s ball, I exclaimed, “It was she!”

We crossed the river and reached Clochegourde at four. The building, modest yet effective, sits on a slope with a terraced garden by the Indre. Its five-windowed front, projecting end-windows, and little bell-tower with carved escutcheon give it an air of genteel neglect. The house, with its stables and greenhouses, seems dainty as a flower set in orchards and vineyards. I admired the groups of trees and felt my heart beat faster at approaching events that would change me.

Warned by the watchdog, a servant told us the count had gone to Azay but Madame la comtesse was at home. My companion looked at me; I trembled lest he decline to see her in her husband’s absence, but he announced us. A golden voice bade us enter. I recognized her voice from the ball and, blushing, nearly fled; our eyes met. Too confused for speech, she returned to her embroidery and counted stitches while we were seated. Monsieur de Chessel explained who I was: a young man weakened by study whom he had met and led to Clochegourde to rest. Distrustful at first, Madame de Mortsauf offered me restoratives and a few kind words that gave me speech. I blushed like a girl and thanked her.

She invited my companion to dine; as a man of the world he read the shades of meaning; I believed in her sincerity and was astonished when my host later remarked, “If you do not make it all right, I may find myself on bad terms with my neighbors.” His faith that I could please her gave me power and hope.

At dinner she talked of harvest and vintage—subjects beyond me; but the music of her voice enthralled me. Her utterance made each syllable a charm; I stayed silent often only to hear her speak. Her way of pronouncing certain sounds was like a bird’s song; her speech led the listener into higher regions. Many times I continued a discussion simply to listen to the harmonies of her voice.

Her inattention let me examine her. I saw the mole where the shoulder line began, the golden hair, the pure neck. Her face cannot be fully drawn; it required an artist to paint inner fires. Her soft fair hair caused her suffering, her brow was round and full of unuttered thoughts, her green-brown eyes were weak except on rare keen emotions when they sent forth a subtle gleam. A Grecian nose and curved lips gave her face harmony; her skin was like a white camellia warmed with rose. Her figure, though well developed and the mother of two, retained youthful freshness. She seemed simple, bashful, and dreamily grave through suffering; she charmed without striving. The rarity of her gestures and glances added solemnity to all she said; except when speaking to her children she seldom looked at anyone.

On this morning she wore a rose-colored gown, a wide-collared hem, a black belt, and little boots; her hair twisted in a tortoise-shell comb. The constant emanation of her soul made Clochegourde’s life luminous; the salon, paneled and painted gray, with neat porcelain vases and a backgammon board, conveyed a calm and composed life. No apartment has given me such teeming impressions; many of my ideas in science and politics were born in that room. Through the windows the valley spread from Pont-de-Ruan to Azay; there the regularity of her occupations made itself felt.

Had I first met her in the domestic gloom among her husband and children, I might have been different; but having stolen a kiss at the ball, having breathed her perfume, I longed for the companionship of the fields and nights, to paddle the river, to climb trees, to linger in the sun. Had she asked me impossible things, I would have sought them to obtain those other riches for her.

When she mentioned Monsieur de Mortsauf I felt hatred and fear—hatred of obstacles and fear of the struggle and of her. “Here is Monsieur de Mortsauf,” she said. I rose, startled. The count entered with his little son. A child cried, “Here’s papa!” and a pale little girl, whom I thought six but learned was nine, came forward and sat on her mother’s lap. The child’s pallor and fragility revealed something of the grief upon the countess’s brow. Jacques, the son, showed the same weakness. Their frailty beside a mother so healthy made the cause of her silence plain.

As the count bowed, his glance at me was uneasy and awkward—a distrust not born of malice but of uncertainty. I studied his features. Though forty-five, he seemed nearer sixty. A crescent of hair fringed the back of his bald head; his face suggested a white wolf with blood about its muzzle—his nose inflamed by long poisoning. His flat forehead and narrow pointed face bore signs of a life in the open air but not of vigorous intellect; his cheekbones were brown and prominent amid general pallor, and his light-yellow eye was bright without warmth, anxious without thought. His mouth was violent and domineering; his chin flat and long. Thin and very tall, he relied on the conventional value of his caste. The carelessness of country life left his dress neglected; his hands brown and wiry showed he wore gloves only on rare occasions, and his shoes were common.

Though ten years of exile and another decade of country life had altered him physically, the Count de Mortsauf still bore marks of his nobility: chivalric loyalty, rigid convictions, devotion to a cause, honest political antipathies, and a certain naive ignorance of contemporary France. He was the sort of upright man who was good for nothing—obstinate, ready to die at his post but unwilling to spend money. Even at dinner his slack cheeks and furtive glances at his children betrayed an inward torment: he had transmitted to them fragile constitutions. If he blamed himself, he refused to let others judge him. Harshly self-aware yet without greatness or charm to redeem his misery, his private life must have been full of irritability, visible in his angular features and restless eye. When his wife returned with the children clinging to her, unhappiness passed over the narrator like the sense of crossing a vault and feeling depths beneath; seeing the four together dimmed his evening.

When conversation paused the count introduced his wife and mentioned family matters the narrator had not known. Asking the narrator’s age, the countess expressed surprise at her daughter’s age—perhaps having mistaken the narrator for a boy—another subtle tie between them. The narrator read her soul then: her motherhood trembled with a belated ray of hope. Observing his slight, delicate youth and nervous strength, she seemed inwardly to cry, “They too will live!” She looked at him searchingly; the ice between them melted. She had many questions but asked none. “If study has made you ill,” she said, “the air of our valley will restore you.” The count grumbled about modern education ruining children with ideas and sciences until they grew old before their time, adding that public schooling should be in the hands of religious bodies—words that later echoed in his distrust of talented men.

He proposed a garden tour. The countess warned that Monsieur de Vandenesse had walked from Tours that morning, and the count rebuked the narrator for imprudence with kindly severity. The servant announced dinner; the company crossed to the dining room—Touraine tiles, Boulle buffets, family silver, Dresden china, flowers—a household scene that enchanted the narrator. He sat at the countess’s right hand and, in three hours, felt his life mingled with hers. A terrible kiss had bound them in a secret that inspired mutual shame; a glorious self-abasement took hold of him. He strove to please the count, fondled the dogs, amused the children, and would gladly have been claimed by them. Love has intuitions like genius: he perceived that gloom, discontent, and hostility would destroy his footing in that household.

The dinner passed with inward happiness. He disregarded the countess’s apparent coldness and the count’s polite indifference. Love, like life, has an adolescence when it suffices unto itself. The rest of his visit was a dream until, by moonlight, he recrossed the Indre, watching white visions embellish meadows and hills and listening to a tree-frog’s melancholy note; from that night he never heard that cry without pleasure. A sense then came of a marble wall against which his feelings had dashed. Would it always be so? Unhappy memories rose, struggling with personal pleasure. Before reaching Frapesle he looked back at Clochegourde and saw beneath its windows Monsieur de Mortsauf’s little punt swaying by an ash-tree—the count’s fishing punt.

Monsieur de Chessel, the narrator’s friendly host at Frapesle, raised his spirits by remarking on the unusual warmth of the count’s reception. Durand by birth, called Monsieur de Chessel, petty and jealous but favored by the crown, was kind to the narrator; with him he found peace and rest—hospitality contrasted with the neglect that had marked his life. The owners of Frapesle were associated with the dawn of his happiness. Monsieur de Chessel enjoyed ostentation, while the Comte de Mortsauf had been a Tourangean of limited means restored in some distinction by royal favor. His greeting to the narrator, the younger son of a ruined but ancient house, was meant to show contempt for a neighbor not of noble birth; Chessel understood this polite distance. Clochegourde and Frapesle, separated only by the Indre, lacked that everyday intimacy neighborly families should have.

Jealousy was not the only cause of the count’s isolation. His early education, like that of great families, was interrupted by emigration; he missed later formative training and held to the immediate restoration of the monarchy. Exile became long idleness. When Condé’s army, in which he had served bravely, disbanded, he waited for another post and never sought a livelihood as other emigrants did. Daily postponed hopes, scruples of honor, and trials undermined his spirit. Long marches on poor rations, betrayals, and illness dissolved him where misery might harden others; he belonged to the sort destroyed by such trials.

Tramping foreign roads, refusing to serve France’s enemies, he returned only when emigrants were allowed back and, seeing Strasburg on his crossing of the Rhine, cried “France!” Born to wealth, he now found himself poor, commanded without authority, turned into an embarrassment by his name because of his want of money. His unalterable opinions, memories, and wasted health left him unequipped for the sarcasms of a France he hardly knew. Half dead, he reached Maine, and by accident one of his farms had not been sold; a tenant still recognized him as owner. The Lenoncourts at Givry invited him to stay; his birth made him a suitable husband for their daughter. Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt accepted the marriage with the idea of living with her aunt, the Duchesse de Verneuil.

Madame de Verneuil, close to the Duchesse de Bourbon, belonged to a pious circle led by Monsieur Saint-Martin, whose disciples practiced a mystical faith of spiritual progress, contempt of worldly suffering, and care of an inner angel. Active prayer and pure love marked this religion, a return to primitive Christianity. Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt remained devout. Cruelly tried by the Revolution’s horrors, the duchess acquired a passionate piety that shed celestial love on her niece. After the duchess’s death, Madame de Mortsauf received visitors at Clochegourde, and Saint-Martin corrected his last books there. The duchess had given Clochegourde to her niece; her sudden death threw gloom over the early days of the marriage and associated the estate with sorrow for the bride. The first period of her married life was not the happiest but the least troubled of her life.

After exile’s trials Monsieur de Mortsauf dreamed of secure repose and found some happiness in agriculture. But the birth of their first child, Jacques, was a thunderbolt that destroyed past and future. The doctor declared the child lacked vitality; the count concealed this from his wife but sought further advice confirmed at Madeleine’s birth. These events and an inward sense of their cause amplified his diseased tendencies. His name seemed doomed; a pure young woman made miserable beside him and deprived of the joy of motherhood—this confluence crushed his spirit and completed his ruin.

The countess, quick to discern past from present, read the future in their children’s fragility. Though making a man happy who feels himself to blame is difficult, she set herself to that impossible task, worthy of an angel. She became stoical: descending into an abyss from which she still saw the sky, she devoted herself to the care of one man as a sister of charity devotes herself to many. To reconcile him to himself she forgave what he could not forgive. The count grew miserly; she accepted privations. Like those whose knowledge breeds suspicion, he feared betrayal; she lived in solitude and submitted without murmur to his mistrust. With womanly tact she made him will to do right until he fancied the ideas his own, enjoying in him honors never his. After the experience of married life she resolved never to leave Clochegourde; she feared outbreaks that gossip would turn against her children. Thus, thanks to her, few suspected Monsieur de Mortsauf’s real incapacity; she wrapped his ruins in ivy. The embittered man found rest in his wife; his secret anguish was soothed by her balm.

This brief family history, partly forced from Monsieur de Chessel, explained much, but the narrator’s prescience of love could not be misled by Madame de Mortsauf’s sublime attitude. One night, alone in his little bedroom, the full truth seized him; he could not bear to stay at Frapesle when he saw Clochegourde’s lighted windows. He dressed, left the château by a tower door, crossed the Indre by a little bridge, took the punt, and rowed before Clochegourde, where a brilliant window shone. Beneath her casement, charmed by nightingale and sedge-warbler, his passion fixed on her. Again and again he cried, “Will she be mine?” During preceding days the universe had enlarged to him, but in one night he found its center. On her his will henceforth fastened; he desired to be all in all to her, to soothe and fill her lacerated heart. That night he betrothed his soul to her with the faith of a poor Castilian knight.

At daybreak he returned to Frapesle unobserved. After breakfast he went back to the meadows, to his punt and to the willows near Clochegourde. All was silent and palpitating: dragonflies hovered, cattle chewed the cud, the vineyard earth glowed. Running up the road, he met the count walking toward a riverbank gate and was invited to see the rye being cut. The count showed him yards, outbuildings, pleasure-grounds, orchards, vineyards, and the kitchen garden until an avenue by the river brought them to where the countess sat with her children. A woman is very lovely in such light and quivering shade. The count made him admire the view; the narrator might have thought himself in Switzerland. Approaching, the countess took Jacques upon her knees as he coughed violently.

The count reproached his wife sharply for exposing Jacques to the river’s damp and letting him sit on a stone bench; she replied he was burning hot and had been suffocating. The count’s remark—“When they have brought such sickly children into the world they ought to learn how to take care of them”—was cruel, an instance of self-love that drove him to defend himself at his wife’s expense. She hurried into the portico. Monsieur de Mortsauf sat with bowed head in gloomy silence. The narrator’s position was awkward; the count neither spoke nor looked at him. At last he rose abruptly, and they took leave. The count suggested fetching a physician but the countess insisted Jacques’s cough was nervous and that she had soothed him with a lozenge and stories the previous night. “Poor woman!” the count said, taking her hand with a tearful look; “I knew nothing of it.” “Why be troubled when there is no occasion?” she replied. The narrator proposed learning agriculture; she motioned to the count as his master.

Two months later the narrator learned she had passed that night in anxiety, fearing croup—while he, rocking in his boat with thoughts of love, imagined she might catch sight of him from her window adoring the gleam of a candle lighting her furrowed brow. The croup, prevalent at Tours, could be fatal. When they left the gate the count, in a voice of emotion, cried, “Madame de Mortsauf is an angel!” The narrator, still little acquainted with the family, inwardly asked what right he had to trouble such perfect peace.

Eager to find a listener he could lord over, the count talked of the future the Bourbons would secure. The narrator listened to childish nonsense: the count’s ignorance of verifiable facts, fear of educated people, rejection of superiority, and scorn of progress. He discovered many sensitive fibers in the man requiring caution; conversation with him was a strain. When noticing the count’s failings, the narrator conformed with the same suppleness his wife showed to soothe him. Asking about prices and cultivation, he found the count glad to answer. On that first day the count said to his wife, “Monsieur Félix is a charming young man.” The narrator wrote to his mother to send his clothes and linen and resolved to remain at Frapesle for two and a half months before returning to Paris.

The initial part of his stay, while he studied the count, was full of painful impressions. He found him irascible without cause, hasty and at times dangerous, alternating between flashes of the brave soldier he had once been—sudden will and light in the eyes—and deep melancholy, brutality, bitter complaints, cold hatreds, impulses like latent madness, childish moans, cries of despair, and unexpected fury. The narrator feared he might detect the look in his eyes and kill him. He was still young and tender; passion shook him like throes, and though he feared death, he would not die until he knew mutual love. He watched, made the children love him, and tried to identify with the family.

Gradually the count restrained himself less in the narrator’s presence; the narrator learned the man’s sudden outbreaks. His future depended on the eccentricity of one variable will. Entering Clochegourde became a torment of uncertainty—“How will he receive me?”—and the narrator fell under the man’s dominion; the suffering taught him to understand Madame de Mortsauf. In forty days of trials they exchanged looks of comprehension; tried by the same fire, they discovered many things—actual bitterness, tacit joys, hopes alternately submerged and buoyant. Once, finding her pensive at sunset, he said, “Some days are hard to bear.” “You read my soul,” she answered. “We touch at many points,” he replied. “Surely we belong to the few born to highest joys and deepest sorrows.” She shuddered and left, noticing Madeleine’s cough.

The countess showed no displeasure at his constant visits for two reasons: she was pure and her thoughts wandered into no forbidden regions; and the narrator amused the count and soothed him. Monsieur de Mortsauf proposed to teach him backgammon; he accepted. The countess betrayed compassion for his perilous position with a look that said, “You fling yourself into the lion’s jaws.” The narrator’s patience—born of a miserable childhood—ripened under this trial. The count delighted in jeering him for mistakes in the game; if he reflected the count complained of slowness, if he played fast the count was angry; if he forgot to mark points he was declared too rapid. When they played for money the count’s winnings gave him abject delight.

A word from the countess could console him. But soon miseries multiplied: his money dwindled in losses. Though the count was present during his visits until late, he cherished hope that a moment might come to speak a word to touch his idol’s heart; to gain it he was forced to continue weary games that lacerated his feelings. There were moments of silence with sunlit meadows or moonlit sandbanks where they only said, “Night is beautiful!” But without money such evenings would end. He wrote to his mother for funds; she scolded him and sent enough to last a week. Where could he get more? His existence seemed to depend upon it. Thus, in the dawn of his first great happiness, he found the same sufferings that had assailed him elsewhere; in Paris he could abstain from certain wants, at Frapesle deprivation was active and sharp, pushing him to desperate thoughts that he subdued without losing self-respect.

In this crisis he found a treatise on backgammon in Monsieur de Chessel’s library and studied it. Learning the game, he improved and soon could beat Monsieur de Mortsauf. But every win enraged the count, whose eyes glittered like a tiger’s; he flung down dice, bit the dice-box, and hurled insults. Such violence ceased only when the narrator learned to manage the game to be nearly equal until the last moment; the count never admitted his skill. The countess, understanding his maneuvers, gave him mute thanks; from that evening she always looked at him when she spoke. The narrator left elated: her look bathed him with light, and her farewell, “Adieu, monsieur,” haunted him. He felt reborn: her voice became palpable, an atmosphere of light and fragrance enveloped him. Her greeting the next day expressed unuttered feeling; he was initiated into the secrets of her voice.

That day was decisive. After dinner they walked across a plain gilded by sunset; he held Madeleine’s hand, Madame de Mortsauf led Jacques, and the count, in front, suddenly struck the earth with his cane and said in a dreadful tone, “Such is my life!—but before I knew you,” looking at his wife. The countess turned pale. The narrator extolled the view and playfully proposed a game of backgammon; the count, distracted, heard bells and fell into reverie. Back at the salon, an indefinable dread possessed them. The count flung himself into an armchair, absorbed; the countess avoided interrupting. The narrator tried to coax the count to play; he dallyed, complained of giddiness and choking, then sighed. At last he consented. Madame de Mortsauf left to put the children to bed and to lead family prayers. All went well in her absence; the narrator allowed Monsieur de Mortsauf to win and his delight made him beside himself. This sudden swing from gloom to wild joy, a crazy laugh, distressed the narrator. Their intimacy bore fruit in that the count no longer restrained himself before him. Day by day he tried to extend his tyranny and feed his temper; moral disease appeared to seek empire.

Presently the countess sat near the table, ostensibly to embroider by its light though anxiety placed her there. A bit of ill-luck changed the count’s face from gaiety to gloom; then he made a fatal throw. Instantly he sprang, flung the table at the narrator and the lamp to the floor, struck the chimney-piece, jumped about the room, and poured out a torrent of insults, imprecations, and incoherent words like those of a possessed man. Imagine the narrator’s position! The countess pressed his hand and bade him go into the garden. He fled before the count could see him. On the terrace, he paced and heard the count’s shouts and moans from the adjoining room. At intervals through that storm he heard the countess’s voice, rising like a nightingale after rain.

He walked the acacia-shaded terrace waiting for her. For days an explanation seemed poised between them; a word might set it flowing. Perhaps she loved, as he did, these quiverings of spirit resembling fear and numbing sensibilities while they held their lives unuttered. An hour passed; he sat on the balustrade when her steps sounded. “Monsieur de Mortsauf is sleeping,” she said. “When he is thus I give him an infusion of poppies; the attacks are infrequent and this remedy never loses effect. Monsieur,” she continued with persuasive tone, “this accident has revealed a secret sedulously kept; promise me to bury the recollection of that scene. Do this for my sake, I beg you. I don’t ask you to swear; give me your word of honor and I shall be content.”

“Need I give it to you?” he asked. “Do we not understand each other?” He promised. “You must not judge Monsieur de Mortsauf unfavorably; you see effects of his sufferings under emigration,” she urged. “Tomorrow he will forget all that he has said and done; you will find him kind and excellent as ever.”

“Do not seek to excuse him, madame,” he replied. “I will do all you wish. I would fling myself into the Indre if I could restore his health and ensure you a happy life. The only thing I cannot change is my opinion. I can give you my life, but not my convictions; I can pay no heed to what he says, but can I hinder him from saying it? No. In my opinion Monsieur de Mortsauf is—”

“I understand you,” she interrupted hurriedly. “You are right. The count is as nervous as a fashionable woman,” she added, trying to soften his madness, “but he is only so at intervals, once a year when the weather is very hot. Ah, what evils from the emigration! How many fine lives ruined! He would have been a great soldier—”

“I know,” he said, interrupting to show the futility of deception.

She stopped, laid a hand lightly on his brow, and looked at him. “Who has sent you here into this home? Has God sent me help, a true friendship to support me?” She paused, then as she laid her hand upon his, “For you are good and generous—” She raised her eyes to heaven as if invoking testimony and then rested them on him. Electrified, he acted with a social want of tact that some natures would call a brave desire to meet danger and avert evil.

“Before we say more,” he said, voice shaken, “suffer me to purify one memory of the past.”

“Hush!” she answered quickly, touching his lips and withdrawing her finger instantly. She looked at him with the glance of a woman too exalted for insult. “Be silent; I know of what you are about to speak—the first, the last, the only outrage ever offered to me. Never speak to me of that ball. If as a Christian I have forgiven you, as a woman I still suffer from your act.”

“You are more pitiless than God himself,” he cried, forcing back tears.

“I ought to be so; I am more feeble,” she returned.

“But,” he insisted, “listen to me now if only for the first, the last, the only time in your life.”

“Speak, then,” she said. “Speak, or you will think I dare not hear you.” Feeling the moment decisive, he spoke in a commanding tone; he told her that all other women had been nothing to him, but when he met her, a studious, not bold nature, he had been possessed by a frenzy no one could condemn. That no heart had been so filled with passion that conquers all things—even death. She stopped him. “And contempt?” she asked. “Did you despise me?” he cried. “Let us say no more,” she replied. “No, let me say all!” he begged. “It concerns my life, my whole being; it holds a secret you must know or I must die in despair. It also concerns you, who, unawares, are the lady in whose hand is the crown promised to the victor in the tournament!”

Then he related his childhood and youth, not coldly but as a young man whose wounds still bled: cruel details of neglect and hope, the treasure of his hopes, the virgin gold of his desires, a burning heart kept alive beneath perpetual winter. When, bowed by his memories, he awaited her reply, she illumined the darkness with one look and one sentence: “We have had the same childhood!” she said, turning a face on which a halo of martyrdom shone.

After a pause in which their souls seemed wedded in the thought, “I am not alone in suffering,” the countess told him, in the voice she used for her little ones, how unwelcome she had been as a girl when sons were desired. Her trials as a daughter bound to her mother had differed from a boy’s sending to school. Her youth’s desolate neglect seemed paradise compared to the millstone under which her soul was ground until her aunt saved her. After the duchess’s death she received Clochegourde and set up a home; but the duchess’s death left gloom.

“You will live happy, I must die of pain. Life is what we make of it, and mine is made forever. No power can break the heavy chain to which a woman is fastened by this ring of gold—the emblem of a wife’s purity,” she said.

They found themselves twins of one misfortune; she never dreamed of half-confidence between brothers. After a short sigh she told him of married life’s deceptions and disillusions, the rebirth of childhood's misery. Like him she had suffered under trifles; great sensitivity makes souls quiver at the least shock. When she married she possessed some girlish savings; these, when needed, she gave to her husband without telling him. He never saw himself as her debtor. How she went from trial to trial! Monsieur de Mortsauf habitually neglected household funds; when she asked he was surprised and spared her the mortification of petitioning for necessities. What terror filled her at his first outburst; what bitter reflections before admitting he was a wreck! What calamities had come of bearing children! What anguish at infants born almost dead! With courage she said in her heart: “I will breathe the breath of life into them; I will bear them anew day by day!” Then conceive the bitterness of finding her greatest obstacle in the heart and hand that should have sustained her. As each difficulty was conquered, new deserts opened until the day she thoroughly understood her husband’s condition, the constitution of her children, and the character of the neighborhood; a day when she trained her feet to trample mud and snow, braced her nerves to bullets, and made all her being passive obedience.

“You would have to live here many months,” she said, “to understand what difficulties I have met improving Clochegourde; what persuasions to make him do what was important. You cannot imagine the childish glee he shows when anything I advise was not at once successful. All that turns out well he claims for himself. Yes, I need infinite patience to bear his complaints when I am half-exhausted trying to amuse his weary hours, to sweeten his life and smooth paths he himself has strewn with stones. The reward is that awful cry: ‘Let me die, life is a burden to me!’ When visitors are here and he enjoys them, he forgets gloom and is courteous. I cannot explain that want of loyalty in a man chivalrous. He is quite capable of riding to Paris to buy me ornaments, as he did before the ball. Miserly in his household, he is lavish upon me if I wish it. I would it were reversed; I need nothing for myself, but household wants are many. In my desire to make him happy, I began by letting him treat me as a victim—I who could then, by a few caresses, have led him like a child—but I was unable to play a disgraceful part. Now, the family’s welfare requires me to be calm and stern as Justice—and yet, I too have a heart that overflows with tenderness.”

“But why,” he asked, “do you not use this influence to master him and govern him?”

“If it concerned myself only I should attempt less to conquer the dogged silence that for days meets my arguments, nor answer his irrational remarks. I have no courage against weakness, any more than against childhood; they may strike me as they will, I cannot resist. Perhaps I might meet strength with strength, but I am powerless against those I pity. If I were required to coerce Madeleine in some matter that would save her life, I should die with her. Pity relaxes my fibres and unstrings my nerves. The violent shocks of the last ten years have broken me down; my feelings, so often battered, are numb; nothing can revive them; even the courage with which I once faced troubles begins to fail me. Yes, sometimes I am beaten. For want of rest—sea-baths to recover nervous strength—I shall perish. Monsieur de Mortsauf will have killed me, and he will die of my death.”

“Why not leave Clochegourde for a few months? Surely you could take your children to the seashore.”

“In the first place, Monsieur de Mortsauf would think he were lost if I left him. Though he will not admit his condition he is aware of it. He is both sane and mad, two natures in one, explaining many irrational actions. Besides, nothing would go right if I were absent. You may have seen in me a mother watchful to protect her young; harder still are cares imposed by Monsieur de Mortsauf, who constantly cries as he follows me, ‘Where is Madame?’ I am Jacques’ tutor and Madeleine’s governess; but that is not all, I am bailiff and steward too. Working an estate here is the most fatiguing of employments. We get small returns; farms are cultivated on shares needing close supervision. If I left, no servant would be in it in a week. So I am attached to Clochegourde as those leaden finals are to a roof. I have no reserves with you. The countryside is ignorant of our secrets, but you know them. Say nothing but what is kind and friendly, and you shall have my esteem—my gratitude. On those terms you are welcome at Clochegourde, where you will find friends.”

“Ah!” he exclaimed, “I see I have never really suffered, while you—”

“No, no!” she cried, with a smile that might melt granite. “Do not be astonished at my frank confidence; it shows life as it is, not as imagination pictures it. We all have defects and good qualities. If I had married a spendthrift he would have ruined me. If I had given myself to an ardent young man perhaps I could not have retained him; he might have left me, and I should have died of jealousy. For I am jealous!” she cried. “But Monsieur de Mortsauf loves me as much as he can; all that his heart contains of affection he pours at my feet, like Magdalen’s cup of ointment. Believe me, a life of love is an exception to earthly laws; all flowers fade; great joys have a morrow of evil—if a morrow at all. Real life is anguish; yet here there are smiles in the sky, few but compensating for grief. Women who are mothers live and love more through sacrifices than pleasures. Here I draw upon myself storms I fear may break upon my children; in doing so I feel something I cannot explain which gives secret courage. Time was when my children’s condition filled me with despair; to-day as they grow they grow healthier and stronger. And then, our home is improved and beautified, our means improving. Who knows but Monsieur de Mortsauf’s old age may be a blessing? Those who stand before the Great Judge with palms in their hands, leading to Him beings who cursed their lives, they have turned sorrows into joy. If my sufferings bring about my family’s happiness, are they sufferings at all?”

“Yes,” he said, “they are; but they were necessary, as mine have been, to make us understand the true flavor of the fruit ripened on our rocks. Now, surely, we shall taste it together; surely we may admire its wonders, the sweetness of affection it has poured into our souls. Have we not followed the same star? Are we not more than brother and sister? That which heaven has joined we must not keep asunder. The sufferings you reveal are the seeds scattered by the sower for a harvest ripening in sunshine. Shall we not gather it sheaf by sheaf? What strength in me that I dare address you thus! Answer, or I will never again cross that river!”

“You have spared me the word love,” she said sternly, “but you have spoken of a sentiment I know nothing of and which is not permitted me. You are a child; and again I pardon you, but for the last time. Understand, Monsieur, that my heart is intoxicated with motherhood. I love Monsieur de Mortsauf neither from social duty nor from desire to win blessings, but from an irresistible feeling that fastens all the fibres of my heart upon him. Was my marriage a mistake? My sympathy for misfortune led to it. It is women’s part to heal woes, to comfort those who rush into the breach and return wounded. How shall I make you understand me? I have felt selfish pleasure in seeing you amuse him; is not that pure motherhood? Did I not make you see just now the three children to whom I am bound, to whom I shall never fail, on whom I strive to shed a healing dew and the light of my soul without withdrawing or adulterating a single particle? Do not embitter the mother’s milk! Though as a wife I am invulnerable, you must never again speak thus to me. If you do not respect this simple command, the door of this house will be closed to you. I believed in pure friendship, a voluntary brotherhood more real than blood. I was mistaken. I wanted a friend who was not a judge, a friend to listen in moments of weakness when reproof is killing, a sacred friend from whom I had nothing to fear. Youth is noble, truthful, capable of sacrifice; seeing your persistency I believed in some divine purpose; I thought I should find a soul that would be mine, a heart to pour my troubles into when they deluged mine. Could I but have this friend, my life precious to these children might be prolonged until Jacques grew to manhood. But that is selfish! The Laura of Petrarch cannot be lived again. I must die at my post, like a soldier, friendless. My confessor is harsh, austere, and—my aunt is dead.”

Two tears filled her eyes, gleamed in moonlight, and rolled down her cheeks; he caught them and drank them with avidity, moved by ten years of secret woe, wasted feelings, and ceaseless care—years of lofty heroism. She looked at him with gentle stupefaction.

“It is the first communion of love,” he said. “Yes, I am now sharer of your sorrows. I am united to your soul as our souls are united to Christ in the sacrament. To love, even without hope, is happiness. What woman could give me a joy equal to receiving your tears! I accept the contract ending in suffering to myself. I give myself to you with no ulterior thought. I will be to you that which you will me to be—”

She stopped him with a motion and said in a deep voice, “I consent if you will promise never to tighten the bonds that bind us.”

“Yes,” he said; “but the less you grant the more evidence of possession I ought to have.”

“You begin by distrusting me,” she replied with melancholy doubt.

“No, I speak from pure happiness. Listen; give me a name by which no one calls you; a name to be ours only, like the feeling that unites us.”

“That is much to ask,” she said, “but I will show I am not petty. Monsieur de Mortsauf calls me Blanche. One only person, my dear aunt, called me Henriette. I will be Henriette once more, to you.”

He took her hand and kissed it. She left it in his hand with the trustfulness that makes a woman superior to men. She leaned on the balustrade and gazed at the river.

“Are you not unwise to rush to friendship’s extremes? You have drained the cup at a draught. It is true a real feeling is never piecemeal; it must be whole. Monsieur de Mortsauf,” she added after a short silence, “is above all things loyal and brave. Perhaps for my sake you will forget what he said today; if he forgets to-morrow I will tell him what occurred. Do not come to Clochegourde for a few days; he will respect you more if you do not. On Sunday, after church, he will go to you. I know him; he will wish to undo the wrong, and he will like you better for treating him as a responsible man.”

“Five days without seeing you, without hearing your voice!”

“Do not put such warmth into your manner,” she said.

They walked twice round the terrace in silence. Then she said with a command that she had taken possession of his soul, “It is late; we will part.” He wished to kiss her hand; she hesitated, then gave it and said entreatingly: “Never take it unless I give it to you; leave me my freedom; if not, I shall be simply a thing of yours, and that ought not to be.”

“Adieu,” he said.

She opened the lower terrace gate for him. Just as she was about to close it she opened it again and offered her hand, saying: “You have been truly good to me this evening; you have comforted my whole future; take it, my friend, take it.” He kissed it again and again; when he raised his eyes he saw tears in hers. She returned to the upper terrace and he watched her from the meadow. On the road to Frapesle he saw her white robe in moonbeam; moments later a light burned in her bedroom.

“Oh, my Henriette!” he cried, “to you I pledge the purest love that ever shone upon this earth.” He turned at every step as he regained Frapesle. Ineffable contentment filled him. A way opened for the devotion swelling in youth and long inert in him. Like a priest entering a new life, his vows were taken; he was consecrated. A simple “Yes” had bound him to keep his love within the soul and never to lead this woman step by step to love. Noble feelings were awakened, and he heard their murmur. Before confining himself within a room he stopped beneath a heaven sown with stars, listened to the ring-dove plaints of his heart, and gathered in the air emanations of that soul which henceforth must ever seek him. How grand she seemed, with absolute forgetfulness of self, her religion of mercy to wounded hearts, her declared allegiance to her legal yoke. She stood serene upon her pyre of saint and martyr. He adored her face as it shone in darkness.

Suddenly he fancied a meaning in her words, a mysterious sign making her sublime. Perhaps she longed that he should be to her what she was to the little world around her. Perhaps she sought to draw from him strength and consolation, putting him within her sphere, her equal, or perhaps above her. The stars—their light seemed to confirm his new destiny.

Spirits quenched by tears, hearts misunderstood, saintly Clarissa Harlowe forgotten, children neglected, exiles innocent of wrong, all who enter life through barren ways, cease your complaints! You alone can know the infinitude of joy in that moment when one heart opens to you, one ear listens, one look answers. A single day effaces past evil. Sorrow, despondency, despair, and melancholy passed but not forgotten are links by which the soul fastens to its mate. Woman falls heir to all our past and gives them back ennobled; she explains former griefs as destiny’s payment for joys eternal which she brings when souls are wedded. Angels alone can utter the new name of that sacred love, and none but women, dear martyrs, truly know what Madame de Mortsauf became to him—to him, poor and desolate.

As I read that letter I felt the maternal heart beneath my fingers; I understood why the countess had forbidden me to open it in Touraine—she feared I would fall at her feet and weep.

I met my brother Charles, whom I had hardly known. He affected a haughtiness that kept us apart; kindness depends on the likeness of souls, and there was none between us. He used me as a foil for his social maxims. Solitude and my intimacy with Madame de Mortsauf sharpened my perceptions; I accepted my primogeniture more willingly, not as my brother’s dupe.

I frequented the Duchesse de Lenoncourt’s alone; Henriette’s name was seldom mentioned there, except by the simple old duke whose welcome implied his daughter’s commendation. Just as I was overcoming the awkwardness of youth and learning to use Henriette’s maxims, the events of March 20 changed everything.

My brother followed the court to Ghent; by Henriette’s advice I went there too with the Duc de Lenoncourt. The duke’s kindness became sincere protection when he saw my devotion to the Bourbons; he presented me to the king. Devotion counted at Ghent, and Louis XVIII judged men with a clear eye—I had the happiness of pleasing him.

A letter from Madame de Mortsauf, brought by a Vendean emissary, enclosed a note telling me Jacques was ill. Torn between care of her son and household obligations, the countess needed a friend to lighten her burden. Though ambitious and eager to rival my brother—then at the Congress of Vienna—I resolved, moved by her sad face, to leave Ghent and return to Clochegourde in the king’s service. Fortune favored me: the emissary could not return and the king wanted a faithful messenger. The Duc de Lenoncourt requested the mission for me; after an audience with the king I set out, able to serve my sovereign and to go back to Henriette.

I carried the king’s instructions successfully in Paris and in the Vendée. By late May, denounced to Bonapartist authorities, I fled, traveling on foot through parks and woods across the Bocage and Poitou. I reached Saumur, Chinon, and then the woods of Nueil, where the count found me and brought me safely to Clochegourde.

“Jacques is better,” he said. When we entered the salon Henriette exclaimed, “Felix!” We looked at one another with the hunger of two souls making up for lost months. She had prayed for my safety and prepared my room. At dinner the news of Waterloo and the Allies’ march to Paris mattered politically, but for us small domestic joys prevailed: she told me, with shining pleasure, “You shall have ice to-day!”—an ingenuous proof of affection that stirred me. Her health and spirit revived in days; my heart opened in response. Absence had strengthened my love; reunion was like spring reviving a valley. Her recovery was delicate; she spoke of nights by Jacques’s bedside, of having lived for three months within herself, alternately listening for moans and a voice.

When she spoke of visions—“While I slept my heart watched”—the count interrupted harshly, calling her “half crazy.” He wounded her; she asked whether he had no indulgence for her weakness as a woman. His habitual arrows pierced her; though he regretted the murmur, the pain revealed the long strain of his treatment.

I tried to divert her with household matters. She told me of the success of her agricultural plans—new leases, planted trees, income—hoping that if the king returned their pension would be restored and Jacques’s fortune secured. She revealed a kind of spiritual insight: in fits of deep meditation she saw luminous or misty visions of her children, which she took as divine signs; she saw me brilliantly illuminated and heard a mental voice guiding me. I clung to her, crying that my obedience was due to none but her, and she smiled. She advised me, when the king returned, to go to Paris—“it may be degrading to beg, but dangerous to be out of the way of receiving favors.” She called me “my son” in her heart and took my arm contentedly.

That evening I witnessed a scene that moved me: the household knelt in prayer, made solemn by Madeleine’s childish voice. The count presented me with backgammon; the countess brought her embroidery and said it had languished while her child was ill. That night, my love burning, I went to her door and listened to her breathing; foolish and dangerous, it was the impulse that urges some men to the brink of ruin.

I plucked flowers and made bouquets; I spent days at Clochegourde and dined at Frapesle. The French army held Tours; though my presence seemed to strengthen the countess, she urged me to go to Paris by Châteauroux and Orléans to be near the king. I promised to write every night. The duke’s patronage was decisive: he told me the king had spoken of me and asked whether I lived. That night I was appointed master of petitions to the Council of State and given a private place in Louis XVIII’s service—a confidential position that became the source of my prosperity. Madame de Mortsauf had judged rightly; she guided and purified me. The post brought rooms at the Château, a carriage, allowances, and proximity to the sovereign—advantages as important as talent in that world.

The king once teased me, calling me “Mademoiselle de Vandenesse” for my reserve, but he favored me. He arranged that I should have a colleague and leave for six months; I returned to Touraine a changed man—dressed with Parisian refinement and feeling the satisfaction of having repaid, in part, the woman whose counsels had shaped me. The new avenue to Clochegourde, the iron gate, the circular enclosure—small marks of prosperity—impressed me. I had hoped to surprise the countess, but she preferred to be prepared.

When she saw the young man I had become she blushed, was pale, yet let me kiss her hand. She feared my being lost to the world; she begged that I promise never to abandon her. We voiced the impossible balances: I swore, “Sacredly... forever,” and she, half in jest, made me promise to remain a child at Clochegourde—she could refuse nothing to a child. In private she asked directly if I loved her “sacredly,” and I answered in vows that combined chivalry and hope, restrained by conscience. She told me to be a child there and a man with the king; in effect, she wanted my devotion within safe limits.

The count, jealous and full of contradictions, resumed his complaints and oddities. He loved his wife yet made her life intolerable by petty tyranny and medical fancies—changing orders about windows, food, and temperature, inventing maladies, and expecting others to adapt. He attacked her for every imagined fault, blaming her when the children fell ill. The household suffered under his whims; servants were exhausted. Henriette had to shield the children, serving them at different hours to spare them his outbursts.

After some days I pressed her: had she erred in making the count idle? She replied that her thoughts were consumed by the children’s health and that diversions would only shift the burden back upon her. She had even thought of silk-worm nurseries, but feared they would only increase his tyranny. She knew weakness breeds insatiable demands: “what you yield to them to-day they exact to-morrow.” Monsieur de Mortsauf delighted in getting the better of her but deceived himself about his goodness.

One morning she dragged me into the vineyard, frantic with fear that the count “will kill me!” She felt the strain unbearable—never a respite, always walking among thorns. She dared not decide on separation after fifteen years’ marriage; her devotion to her children forbade desertion. She resolved to consult her confessor, the new Abbé Birotteau, for courage. When the count approached we hid; he complained of nausea and went to bed. We took our chance to go in a punt on the river—the quiet beauty of nature eased our spirits. The boat ride and mutual attention deepened our intimacy. Yet when a huntsman warned that the count was ill, her first thought was for her son; she breathed more freely learning it was the count. In that instant I realized: though she loved me, her love was of a different order—maternal, pious, not the tumultuous passion I had imagined. I reproached myself gently for not making more decisive claims, for fearing to bind what seemed to be love too tightly.

The count’s illness grew grave. I hurried to Tours for Dr. Origet and obtained a surgeon; bleeding saved him from death. The countess blamed herself, thinking she had brought on the crisis; she wept and offered acts of contrition. The doctor prescribed nursing and a long convalescence; she and I shared the watches. He warned that the moral state of patient and nurses is potent: “a single word, a fear expressed, has the effect of poison.” Origet watched us with scrutiny but, seeing our devotion, he respected us.

For fifty-two days the count hovered between life and death; Henriette and I each watched twenty-six nights. Our shared duty brought a new intimacy. The count’s room became tidy; we learned to serve each other in countless small attentions. She took my hand readily in service; we were, in effect, half-married in devotion. She entrusted me increasingly with household affairs so she could nurse. In the evenings we discussed estate business and children; I sat where a husband might sit, and she encouraged me with innocent pleasure. Illness stripped the world’s conventions; in that concentrated duty we revealed our true selves.

Two small moments revealed deeper feelings, though never consummated: once she came quietly to my watch and I found her with moist, trembling hands; another time I surprised her weeping by the window. When the count showed signs of convalescence his old habits reappeared; he resumed quarrels over food and management, tormenting the household again. Yet the doctor’s orders gave Henriette authority; she governed him with calm firmness and sometimes yielded to pity. She had learned to be stricter, and I took joy in seeing her exercise control. But in private she would indulge him, and her compassion made me both happy and jealous.

She once asked me to accompany her to church to return thanks for the count’s recovery; I left her at the door to visit friends, and she reproached me tenderly for being “a man.” She confessed such questions tortured her: could she abandon the old man who was the father of her children? If she failed in duty, the children would be exposed to his domination. She could not sacrifice them. The thought of marrying me, of turning away from duty for personal happiness, was morally impossible for her. She lived by feelings intertwined with conscience and religion. I, more impatiently passionate, could have sold eternity for a day; she resisted such compromises.

One afternoon, while the count sat in the sun and his face extinguished all hope of earthly passion, I received a letter from Tours bearing the king’s signet—my recall. The countess read it and we felt sudden emptiness: we were necessary to one another and feared the future apart. She knelt and gave me a lock of hair—precious, symbolic—asking me to keep it. We kissed in a solemn, pure way; there was a sacrifice in the gesture. That evening I left; we parted at the walnut-tree where I had first seen her years earlier. She traveled with Madeleine as I returned to Paris.

In Paris my life resumed: council work and my position kept me occupied. I wrote to the countess weekly; she answered twice a month. The letters and our duties made our attachment a disciplined devotion—a private religion whose observances preserved feeling. My story at court spread; my reserve and loyalty attracted notice. Society was polite and women kind; after the Duc de Berry’s marriage the court regained splendor and the city revived. I was drawn into salons but remained inwardly distant, sustained by the memory of Clochegourde.

Five months after leaving, in midwinter, the countess wrote despairing of Jacques’s serious illness—though he was out of danger, precautions for his lungs troubled her. Then Madeleine fell ill: the little girl, once gay and blooming, became pale and anemic. The countess, exhausted by Jacques’s long illness, found no courage for these added cares. The household relapsed into old storms; Monsieur de Mortsauf regained power. She wrote that she felt “turned to stone by grief,” asking if I could love her now when she was “callous, ungrateful, turned to stone by grief.” She feared the loss of the children, the emptiness of life, and confessed that Clochegourde was very sad.

Throughout these trials her nature remained noble and self-sacrificing. She had the courage to consult pastors and to bear suffering for her children’s sake. My love for her had been chastened into something combining worship, tenderness, and sorrow. I remained her friend, steward, and confidant, conscious that our intimacy had grown out of faithful service rather than conquest. The count’s illness had opened to me the inner life of the one woman I loved—her conscience, maternal heart, and piety—a love at once celestial and austere, which, in the end, prevented the consummation I desired but sanctified the devotion I offered.

It was when I felt most alive, full of morning light and crimson evenings, that I first met in the salons of the Élysée-Bourbon a celebrated woman of society who ruled by wealth, blood, and an “iron hand in a velvet glove.” Immensely rich and allied to an old British peer, she combined beauty, manners, and a wit that dazzled before it charmed. She reigned because she used pride and calculation as weapons.

You know the English character: a distance and coldness, a Channel between them and anyone not properly presented. To them humanity is an ant-hill; only a few are admitted. The English make law and routine omnipotent; they polish fortifications around the Englishwoman, who becomes irresistibly attractive. Their customs train married women carefully in hypocrisy, forcing them between extremes—death or social station—with no middle way; this produces a special charm: an enthusiasm for love as life, meticulous care of person, and a delicate passion—Juliet without the Italian fervor.

You, who envy them, know these white sirens: impenetrable yet fathomable, who believe love suffices love, who turn enjoyments to satiety by never varying them; a soul with one note only—an ocean of love in themselves. My relations with the Marchioness of Dudley had a damaging fame. At an age when senses rule and when my own had been repressed by the image of the saint at Clochegourde, that ideal shone so vividly I could resist seductions. It was this fidelity’s lustre that attracted Lady Dudley. My resistance inflamed her passion: like many Englishwomen she desired singularity; boredom drives them to the romantic and the difficult. The more I retreated, the more she pressed; curiosity and the struggle became triumph’s obligations. I might have been saved had someone repeated to me her cruel comment on my relations with Madame de Mortsauf: “I am wearied to death of these turtle-dove sighings.”

Do not excuse me; a man has fewer means of resisting a woman than she has of escaping him. Manners forbid the brutality of repressing a woman; masculine pride ridicules a man’s modesty; social conventions grant women the monopoly of modesty so that they can bestow favors. Reverse the case, and man succumbs to sarcasm. Though I was protected by love, I still responded to pride, devotion, and beauty. When Arabella displayed the homage of a ballroom where she reigned, watched my glance to see if I approved her dress, trembled with pleasure at pleasing me, I was touched. She occupied a social position where I could not avoid her; in diplomatic circles invitations and rank carried her everywhere. With feminine cleverness she contrived to seat me beside her. On such occasions she whispered, “If I were loved like Madame de Mortsauf, I should sacrifice all.” She promised discretion and asked only to love me.

After an evening when she made herself irresistible, she came to me. The scandal echoed through England; Lady Dudley abandoned her place and wealth, and endeavored to eclipse by sacrifice the virtue that had caused her ruin. She delighted, like the devil on the temple’s pinnacle, in showing me all the riches of her passionate kingdom.

Read me indulgently: this involves one of those crises most men face. This slender, milk-white creature seemed yielding and gentle, but she had an iron nature; a hand that could bind without seeming to tire. Her body breathed fire; her love was African—desires like desert whirlwinds. What a contrast to Clochegourde! East and west: one drawing moisture, the other exuding soul. Have you reflected on English manners and morals? Is it not the deification of matter, a careful Epicureanism? England has a science of existence that makes women’s slippers exquisite, linens fragrant, tea served at fixed hours—matter becomes a polished pulp where the soul expires. Thus in British luxury I knew a woman who caught me by love excited by my indifference; I opposed stern continence, she opposed persistence. It was an electricity that carried us to the skies: a love monstrous and cruel, which laughs at the bodies it kills; love without memory, like the policy of the English nation. Most men yield to it.

You see the problem: man is matter and spirit; animality ends and the angel begins. There is a struggle between the future destiny we sense and the influence of anterior instincts—carnal love and divine love. One man combines them, another abstains; some seek satisfaction across the sex, others idealize one woman who is their world. If you consider the different organisms, their dislikes and affinities, you will have indulgence for sorrows the world is pitiless toward. Lady Dudley gratified the instincts—the mistress of the body; Madame de Mortsauf was wife of the soul. The mistress’ love has limits; matter is finite and saturable; often I felt a void near Lady Dudley. Infinitude is the heart’s region—love had no limits at Clochegourde. I loved Lady Dudley passionately; she was magnificent in animality and superior in mind, her satire wide-ranging. But I adored Henriette. At night I wept with happiness; in the morning with remorse.

Some women hide jealousy under angelic kindness; Arabella was over thirty, knowing how to feel and calculate; she pressed today’s juices and thought of the future. She could stifle a natural moan with the will of a huntsman. Without naming Madame de Mortsauf, Arabella sought to kill her in my soul; her passion increased with awareness of that invincible love. Intending to triumph by comparison, she watched me like a lioness with prey, guarding me captive. I wrote to Henriette under her eyes, but she never read a line; she never pried at my letters. She seemed to think, “If I lose him it shall be my own fault,” relying proudly on a love that would give me her life—she often said she would kill herself if I left her. “In India that is a distinction reserved for the higher classes,” she once said; “we should rise to greatness by unparalleled devotions. To love with our heads up, in defiance of law; to die for the idol we have chosen; to never betray that man; these are heights common women cannot reach.”

Pride was her instrument; she flattered vanities by deifying them. She put me on high so she might live at my feet; the seductions of her spirit were expressed in acts of submission. How shall I describe those first six months when I was lost in enervating enjoyments, entangled in pleasures learned by experience but masked by transports of passion? These pleasures, the revelation of the poetry of the senses, bind young men to older women; they are the chain of the galley-slave, leaving an ineffaceable brand and filling the soul with distaste for pure, innocent love.

Recalling my early dreams at Clochegourde, I found sophistries to excuse drinking that jeweled cup. Often, lost in lassitude, my soul thought these pleasures might abolish matter and let spirit soar. Sometimes Lady Dudley, profiting by my exaltation, wrung blasphemies from my lips against the angel at Clochegourde. Once a traitor, I became a scoundrel. I continued to write to Madame de Mortsauf in the tone of the lad she first knew, but her gift of second sight terrified me at the danger a slip of an indiscreet word might bring. Often, in pleasure, sudden horror seized me; I heard Henriette’s name above me like Scripture’s voice demanding, “Cain, where is thy brother Abel?”

At last my letters went unanswered. Seized with anxiety, I wished to leave for Clochegourde. Arabella did not oppose it; she even proposed to accompany me to Touraine, thinking the journey might detach me from my rival; blind with fear and guilelessly unsuspecting, I did not see her trap. She proposed humble concessions: she would stay near Tours, disguised, refrain from daylight outings, meet me only evenings. I left Tours on horseback, choosing the road I had used on foot years earlier, and stopped beneath my walnut-tree. From there I saw Madame de Mortsauf on the terrace in white. Instantly I rode across country to her. She heard my horse and said calmly, “Oh, you here!”

Those three words blasted me. She knew my treachery. Who had told her? Her mother, whose hateful letter she later showed me. The voice once so full of life revealed settled grief. The tempest of infidelity had torn through her soul, leaving a desert where verdure had been. I led my horse through the gate; he lay on the grass at my command and the countess came forward slowly, exclaiming, “What a fine animal!” She stood with folded arms lest I try to take her hand; I guessed her meaning.

“I will let Monsieur de Mortsauf know you are here,” she said, and left me.

I stood confounded, watching her noble, slow figure mount the terraces of her château, pride and torture combined. I cursed Arabella and wondered if my sin would destroy the calm life at Clochegourde. Presently the whole family came down; Jacques ran eagerly; Madeleine, a gazelle with mournful eyes, walked with her mother. Monsieur de Mortsauf greeted me warmly. Madame de Mortsauf stood with her back toward us, under pretext of showing the horse to Madeleine. “Ha, the devil! that’s what women are,” cried the count; “admiring your horse!” Madeleine turned and I kissed her hand, looking at the countess who colored.

“Madeleine seems much better,” I said.

“Poor little girl!” said the countess, kissing her forehead.

“Yes, for the time being they are all well,” answered the count. “Except me, Felix; I am as battered as an old tower about to fall.”

“The general is still depressed,” I remarked to Madame de Mortsauf.

“We all have our blue devils—is not that the English term?” she replied.

The party moved toward the vineyard with a sense something serious had happened. She had no wish to be alone with me, though I was her guest. The count confided to me that Madame de Mortsauf’s disposition had changed completely over six weeks; she, so gentle and devoted, was now extraordinarily peevish. Manette later told me the countess had fallen into a depression making her indifferent to the count’s provocations. No longer finding a soft surface to wound, he became uneasy, like a child whose prey ceases to move. He now needed a confidant, as the hangman needs a helper.

“Try to question Madame de Mortsauf,” he urged. “Find out what is the matter. A woman always has secrets from her husband; perhaps she will tell you. I would sacrifice everything to make her happy; I would die for her. If I have not that angel at my side as I grow old I shall be the most wretched of men. I do desire to die easy. Tell her I shall not be here long. Yes, Felix, I am going fast. I hide the truth from everyone; why worry them? The trouble is in the stomach. I have discovered the true cause: my sensibility is killing me. All our feelings affect the gastric centre.”

“Then do you mean,” I said, smiling, “that the best-hearted people die of their stomachs?”

“Don’t laugh, Felix; too keen sensibility irritates the mucous membrane of the stomach… I have not six months to live.”

I listened in terror. On meeting the countess I had noticed the yellow skin and feverish eyes. I led the count toward the house seeming to listen, but my thoughts were with Henriette; I wanted to observe her. We found her in the salon, listening to a mathematics lesson for Jacques while showing Madeleine an embroidery stitch. Formerly she would have laid aside every occupation on my arrival to be with me. My love was so deep I forced back grief and noticed the fatal yellow tinge on that celestial face, which resembled a painter’s halo. I felt the icy wind of death pass over me. When her eyes, no longer softened by former light, fell upon me I shuddered; the slender lines upon her forehead had deepened; her temples with violet veins seemed burning and concave; her eyes were sunken, circles browned like fruit in decay. I, who had sought to pour happiness into her soul, had embittered the spring she hoped would refresh life and renew courage. I sat beside her and asked, with tears, “Are you satisfied with your health?”

“Yes,” she answered, plunging her eyes into mine. “My health is there,” she added, motioning to Jacques and Madeleine.

Madeleine, now fifteen, had victoriously overcome anaemia and was blooming; she had a slim figure, an air of coquetry smoothing her magnificent black hair—a delicate medieval statuette, a bud of superb development. Jacques, fragile at seventeen, had a large head and feverish eyes, a voice deep with danger under the roses. It was Henriette’s intellect and soul devouring a body without stamina. Seeing him drawing, I shivered at death hidden beneath the roses and thanked my ignorance. Henriette’s maternal pleasure shone.

“When I see my children thus, happiness stills my griefs,” she said. “If other affections fail, duties done and crowned with success compensate. Jacques will be like you, a man of highest education; Madeleine will have a noble heart; my motherhood, once tried, is happy now. God makes my joy blossom in these sanctified affections, and turns to bitterness those that might have led me astray—”

“Good!” cried the abbé. Just then Jacques coughed.

“This situation is intolerable,” I whispered.

“Did I create it?” she asked. “Dear child,” she said aloud with cruel levity, “don’t you read history? France and England are enemies. Madeleine knows that; a broad cold sea separates them.”

The vases on the mantel had given place to candelabra—perhaps to deprive me of filling them with flowers; I found them later in my room. When my servant arrived I went out to give orders; he had brought certain things I wished to place in my room.

“Felix,” said the countess, “do not make a mistake. My aunt’s old room is now Madeleine’s. Yours is over the count’s.”

Though guilty, I had a heart; those words were dagger thrusts coldly given at its tenderest spot. Moral suffering depends on sensitiveness. The countess had trod each round of life’s ladder; but for that reason the kindest woman could be cruel. I looked at Henriette, but she averted her head. I went to my room and burst into tears. Henriette heard me as she entered with a bunch of flowers.

“Henriette,” I said, “will you never forgive a wrong that is indeed excusable?”

“Do not call me Henriette,” she said. “She no longer exists, poor soul; but you may feel sure of Madame de Mortsauf, a devoted friend, who will listen and love you. Felix, we will talk later. If you have any tenderness for me let me grow accustomed to seeing you. When words will not rend my heart, if ever I recover courage, I will speak; not till then. Look at the valley,” she added, pointing to the Indre, “it hurts me, I love it still.”

“Ah, perish England and all her women! I will send my resignation to the king; I will live and die here, pardoned.”

“No, love her; love that woman! Henriette is not. This is no play, and you should know it.”

She left, betraying in her tone the depth of her wounds. I ran after her and held her. “Do you no longer love me?”

“You have done me more harm than all my other troubles put together. Today I suffer less, therefore I love you less. Be kind; do not increase my pain; if you suffer, remember that I live.”

She withdrew her hand cold and moist from mine and darted through the corridor.

At dinner the count subjected me to unexpected torture. “So the Marchioness of Dudley is not in Paris?” he said.

I blushed but answered, “No.”

“She is not in Tours?”

“She is not divorced; she can go back to England. Her husband would be glad if she would return.”

“Has she children?” asked Madame de Mortsauf in a changed voice.

“Two sons,” I replied.

“Where are they?”

“In England, with their father.”

“Come, Felix,” interposed the count; “be frank; is she as handsome as they say?”

“How can you ask him such a question?” cried the countess. “Is not the woman you love always the handsomest?”

“Yes, always,” I said firmly, with a glance she could not sustain.

“You are a happy fellow,” said the count. “Ha! in my young days I should have gone mad over such a conquest—”

“Hush!” said Madame de Mortsauf, reminding him of Madeleine by a look.

After the table I followed the countess to the terrace. “How is it possible that some women can sacrifice their children to a man?” she exclaimed.

“Yes, and such women would give even more if they had it; they sacrifice everything.”

The world reversed before her; the grandeur of the thought struck her. She stood dumb, then rose grand and saintly.

“Love her well, Felix,” she said with tears. “She shall be my happy sister. I will forgive her the harm she has done me if she gives you what you could not have here. You are right; I have never told you that I loved you, and I never have loved you as the world loves. But if she is a mother how can she love you so?”

“Dear saint,” I answered, “I must be less moved than I am to explain how you soar above her. She is a woman of earth, daughter of decaying races; you are the child of heaven, an angel worthy of worship; you have my heart, she my flesh only. She knows this and it fills her with despair; she would change parts with you even for cruellest martyrdom. But all is irremediable: to you the soul, the thoughts, the pure love; to her the desires and joys of passing passion; to you remembrance forever, to her oblivion—”

“Tell me that again!” she cried, sitting down and bursting into tears. “If that be so, virtue, purity, a mother’s love are not mistakes. Oh, pour that balm upon my wounds! Repeat the words that bear me back to heaven.” She turned to me, praying for a look—a sacred word—and forgave me for the sufferings of the last two months.

“Henriette, there are mysteries in men’s lives of which you know nothing,” I said. “I met you at an age when heart feelings stifle desires; many scenes, the memory of which will kindle my soul to death, must have told you that age was drawing to a close… A love without possession is maintained by exasperated desire, but there comes a moment when all is suffering within—for in this we have no resemblance to you. We possess a power we cannot abdicate. Nature cannot long be silenced; some trifling accident awakens it to violence. No, I have not loved, but I have thirsted in the desert.”

“The desert!” she said bitterly. “How he reasons! faithful hearts are not so learned.”

“Henriette, do not quarrel with me for a phrase. No, my soul has not vacillated, but I have not been master of my senses. That woman is not ignorant you are the only one I ever loved. She plays a secondary part in my life; she knows it and is resigned. I have the right to leave her as men leave courtesans.”

“And then?”

“She tells me she will kill herself,” I answered, thinking the threat would startle her. But she smiled disdainfully. “My dear conscience,” I continued, “if you would take into account my resistance and the seductions that led to my fall you would understand the fatal—”

“Yes, fatal!” she cried. “I believed in you too much. I believed you capable of priestly virtue. All is over. I owe you much; you have extinguished in me the fires of earthly life. The worst of the way is over; age is coming on. I am ailing now, soon ill; I can never be the brilliant fairy who showers you with favors. Be faithful to Lady Dudley. Madeleine, whom I was training to be yours, ah! who will have her now? Poor Madeleine!” she repeated.

She looked at the warm setting sun, seized with compassion for our shipwrecks and turned back to memories of our pure past. It was her last hour of pleasure, enjoyed with the purity of her Catholic soul. She believed my words and saw where I placed her—in the skies.

“My friend,” she said, “I obey God; his hand is in all this.”

I did not know then how deep those words were. We slowly returned up the terraces. She took my arm and leaned on it resignedly, bleeding yet bandaged. “Human life is thus,” she said. “What had Monsieur de Mortsauf done to deserve his fate? It proves a better world exists. Alas, for those who walk in happier ways!” She judged life so truly that cold statements revealed disgust for all below. At the portico she dropped my arm and said, “If God has given us the desire for happiness, ought he not to take charge of innocent souls who have found sorrow only here? Either that must be so, or God is not, and our life is a cruel jest.”

She entered quickly and I found her crouching on the sofa. “What is the matter?” I asked.

“I no longer know what is virtue,” she replied; “I have no consciousness of my own.”

We were silent, petrified. “If I am mistaken in my life she is right in hers,” she added at last. Thus her last struggle followed her last happiness. When the count came in she complained of illness. I implored her to tell me where she suffered; she refused and went to bed, leaving me prey to remorse. Madeleine went with her mother, and the next day I heard the countess had been seized with nausea from the day’s violent excitements. Thus I, who longed to give my life for hers, was killing her.

“Dear count,” I said to Monsieur de Mortsauf, who made me play backgammon, “I think the countess very seriously ill. There is time to save her; send for Origet and persuade her to follow his advice.”

“Origet, who half killed me?” cried the count. “No, no; I’ll consult Carbonneau.”

Those days were anguish—the beginning of a paralysis of the heart—my vanity mortified, soul rent. One must have been the centre of all to understand the horror of the void about me. All things were the same, but the spirit that gave life to them was extinct. I now understood lovers’ desire never to see each other again when love has flown. To be nothing where once we were everything! I came at last to envy the dismal ignorance of my youth. My despair became so great that I thought the countess felt pity for it. One evening after dinner I made a last effort for forgiveness; I took Henriette to the punt.

“Henriette,” I said, “one word of forgiveness, or I throw myself into the Indre! I have sinned—it is true; but am I not like a faithful dog? I return ashamed. Strike me, bruise me, but give me back your heart.”

“Poor child,” she said, “are you not always my son?” She took my arm and rejoined her children, leaving me to the count, who began to talk politics.

We returned to the garden scenes of family life, small domestic reconciliations that pierced me with remorse. The count, sometimes perceiving truth, joked that husbands are always suppressed. The countess answered with gentle dignity: “If virtue does not consist in sacrificing everything to our children and husband, what is virtue?” Her firmness and saintliness shone. In tender moments, she admitted to me, “I do wrong… I have many wrongs to repent of towards you; I wished to fill you with despair when I ought to have received you mercifully. Dear, you are kindness itself, and I alone can appreciate it.”

She laid her head on my breast, pleading “Forgive me!” and spoke with a voice new and agonizing. She would be a “light from above,” sparkling and cold, unchanging. “Only let me not love the brother I have chosen without return. Love me, cherish me! The love of a sister has no dangerous tomorrow. Turn our doubtful attachment into this sweet and sacred love. I will begin tonight by taking Lady Dudley’s hand.”

Our emotions were so great we barely noticed the heavy rain. When the storm eased we drove toward Clochegourde; Arabella, alerted by her dog, galloped to meet us on the moor. The dog’s barking had told Arabella I was in the carriage. We saw her in moonlight; she examined us swiftly and with English contempt disappeared into the underbrush. “Drive quickly to Clochegourde,” cried the countess for that cutting look was an axe blow.

As we neared the moor Arabella galloped up crying, “Come! away! away!” I followed her to Saint-Cyr about midnight. “That lady is in perfect health,” said Arabella as she dismounted, and when I forbade her sarcasms about Madame de Mortsauf she replied with frank insularity and a vow: “If you betray me, I shall not be found alive; I was born in Lancashire, where women die for love.” She led me to her rooms where comfort spread its charms. She was not a moralist in Henriette’s sense; she mocked solemn religion and cultivated a practical, muscular morality. She could love and die for me, but she did not understand the sanctities that governed Henriette’s heart.

“You poor child know only how to love; you don’t know how to live,” she said. “Still, I am capable of great efforts; I will learn to preach; you shall have no more kisses without verses of the Bible interlarded.” She used and abused her power when she saw my ardent eyes. She triumphed, and I complacently told myself the woman who loses all and makes love her only virtue is far above Catholic polemics. Arabella’s sharp, worldly monologue about women, virtue, and love ended with the same fatal distinction: she offered life and passion, while Madame de Mortsauf offered the soul and sacrifice. Between them I stood divided, consumed by remorse and desire, caught in the terrible contradiction of my own nature.
