Dedication: To David, Sculptor—Balzac expresses gratitude for the permanence of sculpture compared with the precariousness of other fame.

In autumn 1826 Abbé Birotteau, short, corpulent, about sixty and subject to gout, was caught in a rainstorm returning from a friend’s house. He had hurried across the deserted Cloister behind Saint‑Gatien cathedral, careless of wet shoes because he was in high spirits after winning at cards with Madame de Listomère and buoyed by hopes of obtaining a long‑desired canonry. That ambition consumed him so completely he even neglected his umbrella.

The Cloister, once home to Chapter dignitaries, had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette. On its left stood a single house long occupied by priests and owned by the devout, discreet old maid Mademoiselle Gamard, who preserved the property through the Restoration by taking priests as boarders. She could accommodate only two lodgers; for twelve years she had sheltered Abbé Troubert and the late Abbé Chapeloud. When Chapeloud died, Birotteau—who had long admired the apartment’s furnishings during visits—succeeded him.

Chapeloud had been a close, tactful friend; his death left Birotteau both bereaved and relieved. He inherited Chapeloud’s tasteful library and furniture—comforts and status he had envied. Settling into the rooms, he imagined domestic ease, but gratitude soon gave way to renewed obsession with preferment.

One rainy evening he found the house door locked and, after ringing, was told Gamard assumed he was already inside. He noticed other petty omissions—no slippers waiting, no fire—and felt slighted. Innocent and lacking worldly tact, he mistook these lapses for disrespect. Gamard’s discontent, nursed over his eighteen months’ tenancy—she had hoped for a salon and craved recognition—grew into a quiet vengeance. The contrast between Chapeloud’s tact and Birotteau’s guilelessness turns small domestic grievances into a deeper conflict of pride, naiveté, and provincial intrigue—a prologue to a wider tale.

The next morning Birotteau, preoccupied with a possible canonry, briefly forgot the previous night’s ill omens. He rang for Marianne, but Abbé Troubert entered after knocking. This expected monthly visit began with Troubert noticing the absent fire, calling for Marianne, and asking after Birotteau’s health and the canonry. Unaware Troubert resented Madame de Listomère for excluding him, Birotteau cheerfully recounted his efforts to obtain the position.

The two abbés were strikingly different: Troubert, tall, thin, serious, with a sarcastic, wrinkled face and dulled intellect attributed to subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard; Birotteau, plump, round, and jovial, was amiable and popular. Neither seemed likely to rise high—Troubert, at fifty and ill, might at best become vicar-general; Birotteau’s good nature made him comfortably mediocre. Troubert asked to borrow the Register of Bishops, which Birotteau lent; carrying the heavy books downstairs they met Mademoiselle Gamard’s sharp disapproval. She scolded him for cluttering her dining room, stinging him with her ill temper.

Breakfast was tense. Gamard complained of being disturbed and weighed Birotteau down with reproach. Her bitter demeanor—shaped by resentment and social frustration as an unmarried woman—showed in sharp features and abrupt movements. Oppressed, Birotteau left for the garden; finding Gamard and Troubert watching, he escaped into church duties. Returning, he discovered he had missed dinner—Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting, a petty manipulation to preserve her propriety at his expense.

Eight days later Birotteau grasped that Gamard’s hostility was part of a six-month plot against him. Previously oblivious, he now felt suffocated as she savored revenge while he confided in no one. Anxiety worsened his health; he sought Troubert’s help, awkwardly describing Gamard’s provocations. Troubert listened with restrained amusement and advised understanding her, blaming ill health.

Desperate, Birotteau stayed at Madame de Listomère’s country house, not seeing that Gamard could exploit his absence. After ten days, Caron, Gamard’s lawyer, demanded clarity to avoid disputes. Gossip spread; friends urged Birotteau either to pursue the canonry or yield to secure advancement. Pressed, Caron brought a document relinquishing his residence; he signed under pressure and later learned he had signed away possessions Gamard claimed as board compensation. Devastated, he fled to Madame de Listomère, then was returned to the Alouette where friends consoled him.

Monsieur de Bourbonne examined the deed, called it exploitative, and urged legal action. The group debated suing to recover property versus compromising to avoid scandal, and resolved to support Birotteau. An expert appraisal revealed the true value of his goods and Gamard’s fraud. As legal proceedings opened, town gossip intensified and the outcome remained uncertain against Gamard and Troubert’s alliance.

Days after Birotteau’s quarrel with an old maid, Baron de Listomere—expecting promotion to corvette captain—received a letter warning he might be retired. He rushed to Paris; the naval minister brushed it off, but inquiries produced a retirement order awaiting the director’s signature. His deputy uncle explained the minister believed the baron had sided with Radicals in Tours and had offended Abbé Troubert, the vicar-general. Urged to reconcile with the clergy, the baron resolved to avoid further conflict.

Fearing political fallout from supporting Birotteau, the family followed Monsieur de Bourbonne’s advice to appease Troubert and distance themselves from Birotteau. The baroness confronted him, asking that he withdraw his suit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house; distressed, he agreed and requested Chapeloud’s portrait, which she promised to negotiate.

When Gamard fell ill, gossip blamed Birotteau. The baroness and Troubert traded polite insinuations; she presented Birotteau’s withdrawal, and Troubert—privately pleased—returned with Gamard’s refusal to give up the portrait. To keep the peace the baroness accepted. Gamard died, left her fortune to Troubert, and he gave a grand eulogy while Birotteau mourned alone. Troubert became Bishop of Troyes; despite an annuity from Madame de Listomere, Birotteau was ultimately sent to a distant parish, ill and bereft—Troubert’s rise revealing the moral cost of ambition and clerical power.
