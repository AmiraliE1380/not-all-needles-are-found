De Balzac dedicates "The Vicar of Tours" to sculptor David, emphasizing the enduring nature of David's work compared to the transient quality of his own literary efforts.

In autumn 1826, Abbe Birotteau, a short, corpulent priest nearing sixty, is caught in the rain while returning home from a friend's house. Despite his discomfort, he feels uplifted by the prospect of being nominated as canon at the cathedral of Saint-Gatien, a long-held ambition promising a comfortable life.

Birotteau lives with Mademoiselle Gamard, an old maid who boards priests. He has long envied the apartment of his deceased friend, Abbe Chapeloud, who created a beautiful living space filled with books. After Chapeloud's death, Birotteau inherits his library and furniture, which brings him joy but also guilt.

As Birotteau settles into his new life, he enjoys Gamard's meticulous care but soon notices signs of neglect, like a lack of fire in his room, leading him to suspect she harbors ill will against him. Despite his good nature, Birotteau struggles to understand her hostility, reflecting on their relationship and realizing he has taken her attentions for granted. Gamard, feeling slighted by his social engagements, withdraws her kindness, creating tension between them.

Birotteau's longing for the canonry intensifies, blinding him to the conflict with Gamard. His naivety prevents him from recognizing her resentment, stemming from his social aspirations and failure to appreciate her efforts. The narrative explores themes of envy, ambition, and the fragility of happiness, illustrating how misunderstandings can lead to profound personal conflicts.

The next morning, Birotteau awoke thinking of his potential canonry, momentarily forgetting the previous night's ominous signs. He rang for his servant, Marianne, but when she did not come, Abbe Troubert entered after knocking. Troubert, a tall, serious man, contrasted sharply with the jovial Birotteau. Their monthly visits were routine, and Troubert expressed concern over Birotteau's health and prospects for the canonry, unaware of Birotteau's naivety regarding Troubert's ambitions.

The two men were opposites: Troubert's somber nature clashed with Birotteau's cheerfulness. Troubert, respected for his austere presence, was expected to be promoted to vicar-general, while Birotteau's robust health made him a less appealing candidate for advancement.

Abbe Chapeloud quietly opposed Troubert's rise but maintained a facade of respect towards him. After Mademoiselle Gamard declared war on Birotteau, Troubert visited him to borrow a book. Birotteau, pleased to help, felt grateful for Troubert's kindness, especially since it meant he would have a fire to dress by.

As they descended for breakfast, Gamard scolded Birotteau for bringing books into the dining room, souring the atmosphere. Birotteau struggled to engage in conversation, ultimately falling silent as his attempts at small talk were met with disdain from Gamard, leaving him feeling isolated.

Gamard embodied the archetype of an old maid, marked by bitterness and unfulfilled desires. Despite her harshness, Birotteau remained oblivious to her malice, caught in a cycle of trying to please her. After breakfast, he sought solace in the church, but upon returning home late for dinner, he discovered that Gamard had manipulated the clock to ensure he missed the meal, exemplifying her vindictive nature. Birotteau's life was increasingly overshadowed by Gamard's petty tyranny.

Eight days into the story, Birotteau discovers a plot against him by Mademoiselle Gamard, who has harbored resentment for months. Initially oblivious to her malicious intentions, Birotteau becomes tormented as unsettling incidents reveal he is under her scrutiny. His once peaceful life spirals into despair as he perceives Gamard's vindictiveness as a personal attack.

Feeling isolated and ashamed to confide in friends, Birotteau seeks help from Abbe Troubert, who dismisses his concerns as trivial, attributing Gamard's behavior to health issues, which deepens Birotteau's helplessness.

To escape, Birotteau plans a short stay at Madame de Listomere's country house, unaware this plays into Gamard's hands. At the Alouette, he is confronted by Monsieur Caron, Gamard's lawyer, who warns that his absence could lead to legal complications regarding his living arrangements. Confused and distressed, Birotteau returns to his friends, who urge him to stand firm against Gamard.

Madame de Listomere and others analyze the situation, suspecting Troubert covets Birotteau's apartment. They encourage him to resist Gamard's influence, but Birotteau's naivety leads him to sign a document relinquishing his rights to his belongings in Gamard's house, believing it to be a mere formality. This act seals his fate, as he later learns Gamard intends to keep his possessions as compensation for the difference in board prices.

Upon returning to confront Gamard, Birotteau is met with her triumphant demeanor and realizes the extent of his loss. Troubert has taken over his former apartment, and Gamard claims his furniture as part of their agreement. Devastated, Birotteau seeks solace at Madame de Listomere's, where friends promise to help him reclaim his dignity and possessions.

Mademoiselle Salomon, a devoted friend, takes Birotteau back to the Alouette, where he struggles to comprehend the betrayal. His friends, including Monsieur de Bourbonne, strategize a legal response against Gamard, believing they can expose her deceit. However, the local lawyer advises against pursuing the case, suggesting Birotteau's actions may be seen as ingratitude.

Despite this, Birotteau's friends remain committed to supporting him, recognizing the social implications of the conflict against Gamard and Troubert. As the legal battle unfolds, they gather evidence of the value of Birotteau's possessions, which far exceed what he owed Gamard, illustrating the complexities of human relationships and motivations.

A few days later, Baron de Listomere received concerning news about his potential retirement from the Navy. Seeking clarification, he approached the minister, who dismissed his fears. However, further inquiries revealed a document confirming his retirement was pending signature. The baron sought help from his uncle, a deputy, who warned that his political views had made him unpopular, particularly with the influential Abbe Troubert. He advised the baron to reconcile with Troubert to secure his career.

Three days later, the baron returned home, anxious about the family's support for Birotteau, whom he considered a liability. He consulted Monsieur de Bourbonne, who suggested distancing themselves from Birotteau and appeasing Troubert. The baroness then asked Birotteau to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house for family interests. Distressed, Birotteau reluctantly agreed but insisted on retrieving a portrait of Abbe Chapeloud.

Madame de Listomere promised to negotiate with Mademoiselle Gamard for the portrait. Shortly after, Gamard fell ill and died, leaving her fortune to Troubert, which led to gossip blaming Birotteau for her death. Madame de Listomere visited Troubert to negotiate peace, presenting Birotteau's relinquishment of claims. Troubert, feigning indifference, refused to part with the portrait, claiming it was a cherished memento.

Gamard's death further isolated Birotteau, who was appointed curate of Saint-Symphorien, effectively exiling him. Meanwhile, Troubert ascended to Bishop of Troyes, consolidating his power. After Madame de Listomere's death, Troubert contested Birotteau's annuity, threatening the baron's career. Ultimately, Birotteau's life deteriorated amid illness and despair, while Troubert's influence grew unchecked, highlighting the consequences of ecclesiastical politics in provincial France.
