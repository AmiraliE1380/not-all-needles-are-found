THE VICAR OF TOURS

In the autumn of 1826 Abbé Birotteau, short, corpulent, about sixty and gout‑prone, was caught in a rainstorm returning from a friend’s house. He hurried across the deserted Cloister behind Saint‑Gatien cathedral, careless of wet shoes after winning at cards with Madame de Listomère and buoyed by hopes of a long‑desired canonry, so preoccupied he even neglected his umbrella.

The Cloister, once home to Chapter dignitaries, had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette. On its left stood a single house long occupied by priests and owned by the devout, discreet old maid Mademoiselle Gamard, who preserved the property through the Restoration by taking priests as boarders. She could shelter only two lodgers; for twelve years she had housed Abbé Troubert and the late Abbé Chapeloud. When Chapeloud died, Birotteau—who had long admired the apartment’s furnishings during visits—succeeded him.

Chapeloud had been a close friend; his death left Birotteau both bereaved and relieved. He inherited Chapeloud’s tasteful library and furniture, comforts he had envied and viewed as part of the status he sought. Becoming Gamard’s boarder and securing the canonry seemed to promise those comforts. Installing himself in the rooms he had coveted, he enjoyed imagined attentions and domestic ease; but gratitude soon gave way to renewed obsession with preferment.

One rainy evening he returned to find the house door locked and had to ring repeatedly; his servant Marianne explained Gamard assumed he was already in. He noticed other petty omissions—no slippers waiting, no fire—and felt slighted. These trivial neglects pricked his vanity and suggested intentional disrespect. Innocent and lacking worldly tact, he could not see that the lapses were deliberate.

Mademoiselle Gamard’s discontent had been growing during Birotteau’s eighteen months of tenancy. She prized social recognition and dreamed of cultivating a salon—ambitions frustrated by his indifference and frequent evenings with friends. As he distanced himself, she felt her plans slipping away and began to nurse a quiet vengeance. Her coolness—locked doors, omitted attentions—was the first sign.

Absorbed by hopes of preferment, Birotteau confronted symptoms, not causes, convinced he must somehow have displeased her. The contrast between Chapeloud’s worldly tact and Birotteau’s guilelessness turns small domestic grievances into a deeper conflict of pride, naiveté, and provincial intrigue—a prologue to the wider tale to follow.

The next morning Birotteau woke absorbed by hopes of a possible canonry, briefly forgetting the previous night’s ill omens. He rang for Marianne; before he could ring again Abbé Troubert, making his expected monthly visit, entered after knocking. Troubert noticed the absent fire, called for Marianne, and asked after Birotteau’s health and the canonry. Unaware Troubert resented Madame de Listomère for not inviting him to her house, Birotteau described his efforts to obtain the position.

The two abbés contrasted sharply. Troubert was tall, thin, serious, with a wrinkled face suggesting sarcasm; Birotteau was plump, round-faced, and cheerful. Troubert’s calm exterior masked a dulled intellect some blamed on subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard; Birotteau’s joviality made him popular. Neither seemed likely to rise far—Troubert, at fifty with failing health and pious conservatism, might at best become vicar-general; Birotteau’s amiability kept him comfortable. Though Abbé Chapeloud quietly opposed Troubert’s promotion, Troubert treated him respectfully despite Chapeloud’s skepticism.

Troubert asked to borrow the Register of Bishops, which Birotteau lent. Carrying heavy books downstairs, they met Mademoiselle Gamard’s sharp disapproval; she scolded Birotteau for cluttering her dining room and stung him with ill temper, a jarring contrast to his usual cheerfulness. Breakfast was tense. Gamard complained of being disturbed the previous night; weighed down by her displeasure, Birotteau could not engage. Her bitter demeanor—shaped by the resentment and social frustration of an unmarried woman—showed in sharp features and abrupt movements. Feeling oppressed, Birotteau left for the garden, found Gamard and Troubert watching him, escaped into church duties, and on returning discovered he had missed dinner—Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting, a petty manipulation protecting her propriety at his expense.

Eight days after these events, Abbé Birotteau discovered that Mademoiselle Gamard’s hostility—visible since the candlestick-and-clock incidents—was part of a six-month plot against him. Previously oblivious, he now felt suffocated by her malice as she savored revenge while he, absorbed by minor worries, confided in no one. His anxiety about confronting Gamard worsened his health, so he sought Abbé Troubert’s help. Embarrassed to complain, he described Gamard’s provocations; Troubert listened with restrained amusement and advised understanding her, blaming ill health for her behavior.

Desperate, Birotteau hoped a temporary stay at Madame de Listomère’s country house would ease tensions, not realizing Gamard could exploit his absence. After ten days, Monsieur Caron, Gamard’s lawyer, reported she was alarmed by his prolonged leave and wanted clarity to avoid disputes. Back among friends, Birotteau recounted his troubles and speculation followed. Madame de Listomère suggested Troubert coveted Birotteau’s apartment. An old landowner warned this was only the start. Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix brought news that the vicar-general was ill and Troubert might be appointed, potentially giving him appointment power.

Madame de Listomère urged Birotteau to consider the canonry rather than endure Gamard. Friends proposed yielding to Gamard and Troubert if it aided his advancement. But the struggle intensified. Caron returned asking Birotteau to sign a document relinquishing his residence; pressed by friends, he signed. Despair deepened when he learned he had signed away possessions Gamard claimed as compensation for board differences. Confronted, she coldly cited the agreement. Devastated, he fled to Madame de Listomère and found his belongings packed. Mademoiselle Salomon, fearing for his sanity, took him back to the Alouette, where he confided in friends.

Monsieur de Bourbonne examined the deed, found it exploitative, and urged legal action. The group debated suing to recover property versus compromising to avoid scandal; they resolved to support Birotteau, though Bourbonne warned many would desert him if the fight grew bitter. An expert appraisal exposed the true value of his goods and Gamard’s fraud. As legal proceedings opened, town gossip and political divisions intensified; the outcome remained uncertain against the alliance of Gamard and Troubert.

A few days after Birotteau’s quarrel with the old maid began, Baron de Listomere—expecting promotion to corvette captain—received a letter warning he might be retired. Alarmed, he hurried to Paris; the naval minister dismissed his fears, but inquiries uncovered a document confirming the retirement pending the director’s signature.

Desperate, the baron consulted his deputy uncle, who explained the minister believed he had sided with Radicals in Tours and had offended the influential Abbé Troubert, the vicar-general. His uncle urged reconciliation with Troubert, insisting good relations with the clergy were vital to the baron’s career. Seeing the danger, the baron resolved to avoid further conflict.

Three days later, fearing political fallout from supporting Birotteau, the family followed Monsieur de Bourbonne’s advice to appease Troubert and distance themselves from Birotteau as a liability. The baroness confronted Birotteau, asked him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house. Distressed, he agreed and requested Chapeloud’s portrait; the baroness promised to negotiate.

Mademoiselle Gamard fell ill and gossip blamed Birotteau’s suit. The baroness sought Troubert; their meeting was a duel of polite insinuations. She presented Birotteau’s withdrawal; Troubert feigned indifference but was privately pleased. He later returned with Gamard’s refusal to surrender the portrait; the baroness accepted it to keep the peace.

Gamard died, leaving her fortune to Troubert, further worsening the Listomeres’ plight. At her funeral Troubert delivered a grand eulogy while Birotteau mourned alone. Troubert became Bishop of Troyes; though Madame de Listomere’s death left Birotteau an annuity, Troubert’s influence continued to threaten the baron and contest Birotteau’s legacy. Ultimately Birotteau was sent to a distant parish, ill and grieving—Troubert’s ascent exposing the moral costs of ambition and clerical power.
