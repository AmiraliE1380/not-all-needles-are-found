DEDICATION

To David, Sculptor:

The permanence of the work on which I inscribe your name—twice made illustrious in this century—is very problematical; whereas you have graven mine in bronze which survives nations—if only in their coins. The day may come when numismatists, discovering amid the ashes of Paris existences perpetuated by you, will wonder at the number of heads crowned in your atelier and endeavor to find in them new dynasties.

To you, this divine privilege; to me, gratitude.

De Balzac.

THE VICAR OF TOURS

I

In the autumn of 1826, Abbé Birotteau, the story’s protagonist, was caught in a rainstorm returning from a friend’s house. Short, corpulent, about sixty and subject to gout, he hated wet shoes. Yet he was in high spirits, having won money at cards with Madame de Listomère and buoyed by hope of a long-desired canonry at the cathedral—an ambition that preoccupied him and made him oblivious to rain and discomfort.

He hurried across the deserted square called “The Cloister,” behind the cathedral of Saint-Gatien in Tours. Once housing Chapter dignitaries, it had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette after ecclesiastical property confiscations. On its left stood a single house shadowed by the cathedral, long occupied by priests and owned by an old maid, Mademoiselle Gamard. Devout and discreet, she had retained the property through the Restoration by boarding priests.

Birotteau had lived there two years. To be Mademoiselle Gamard’s boarder and to secure a canonry were his two chief desires, embodying the comforts and status he craved. The apartment had become an object of obsession pursued with hope and impatience.

Gamard could take only two lodgers. For twelve years she had housed the Abbé Troubert and the late Abbé Chapeloud. When Chapeloud died, Birotteau took his place, having long admired the apartment’s furnishings during visits. Chapeloud had been a close friend; his death left Birotteau bereaved but also relieved to inherit the library and tasteful furniture he had coveted.

Chapeloud had gradually enriched the rooms with books and tasteful furnishings, inciting Birotteau’s envy and longing. The vicar’s wish for that comfort grew until it bordered on obsession; he sometimes found himself wishing for Chapeloud’s passing to obtain the apartment, though he never schemed about his friend’s will. Chapeloud, aware of these feelings, forgave them and, just before dying, told him, “This time you will certainly get the apartment. I feel it is all over with me now.” After the funeral, Birotteau arranged his inherited possessions and felt contented despite being passed over for the canonry.

Mademoiselle Gamard welcomed him as boarder; he enjoyed the attentions and comforts he had long imagined. Yet his gratitude soon shifted to renewed longing for the canonry, which became an obsession that preoccupied him so much he left his umbrella in the rain. Arriving home, he found the door locked and had to ring several times. His servant Marianne explained Gamard assumed he was already in. He noticed the absence of usual attentions—no slippers, no fire—and felt slighted.

These small neglects pricked his vanity and made him suspect intentional disrespect. He reflected on possible causes and convinced himself he must have displeased her somehow. His innocence and lack of worldly experience left him vulnerable; he failed to perceive that Gamard’s coolness was the beginning of a subtle persecution. Where Chapeloud had managed the relationship with shrewd tact, Birotteau’s simplicity left him blind to mounting resentment.

Mademoiselle Gamard’s discontent had been building over the eighteen months of his tenancy. She prized social recognition and had hoped to create a salon—ambitions frustrated by Birotteau’s indifference to her expectations. As he resumed former social habits and spent evenings with friends who provided the engagement she desired, he further distanced himself from Gamard. She felt her plans slipping away and began to nurse a quiet vengeance.

The neglect he experienced—the locked door, the omission of small domestic attentions—manifested her growing animosity. Birotteau, naive and self-absorbed in his desire for preferment, remained oblivious to the calculations behind these omissions. He confronted only the symptoms, not their cause, and hoped for clarity in the morning, unaware the reasons for Gamard’s hostility would remain hidden to him.

The contrast between the two priests was clear: Chapeloud had navigated Gamard’s vanity with worldly tact; Birotteau’s simplicity made him an easy target for her petty reprisals. As the vicar sank into discontent and sought solace in outside company, he further alienated Gamard, who, thwarted in her ambitions, began to plot revenge. These nights are only a prologue to a fuller exploration of pride, naiveté, and social maneuvering that will follow.

The next morning Birotteau woke preoccupied with his hopes for a canonry, briefly forgetting the ominous signs from the night before. He called for Marianne to light a fire; when she did not appear he was about to call again when Abbé Troubert entered after knocking. This monthly visit was expected; Troubert noticed the missing fire, summoned Marianne, and asked after Birotteau’s health and any news of the canonry. Birotteau, unaware that Troubert privately resented Madame de Listomère for not inviting him to her home, recounted his efforts to secure the position.

The two abbés formed a sharp contrast. Troubert was tall, thin, serious, his lined face suggesting sarcasm and contempt; Birotteau was plump, with a round, cheerful face. Troubert’s calm reserve masked a dullness some attributed to his subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard, while Birotteau’s jovial nature made him well liked. Neither appeared likely to rise much beyond his present station—Troubert because his restrained ambition had been smoothed into a saintly exterior by fifty, Birotteau because amiability made him complacent. Troubert’s failing health hinted at a possible vicar-generalship; Abbé Chapeloud, a respected diocesan, had quietly opposed Troubert’s elevation, yet Troubert treated him with deference despite Chapeloud’s skepticism.

During the visit Troubert asked to borrow the “Register of Bishops,” which Birotteau gladly supplied. They went downstairs together, carrying large books, and met Mademoiselle Gamard’s sharp disapproval. She scolded Birotteau for cluttering her dining room; he felt the sting of her ill temper, a jarring contrast to his usual cheerfulness.

Breakfast was strained. Mademoiselle Gamard complained about being disturbed the previous night; Birotteau, weighed by her displeasure, struggled to engage. His attempts at small talk about coffee and weather were met with disdain. Her demeanor—sharp features, abrupt movements, ungracious manner—reflected a bitterness bred from lifelong singlehood and social rejection: the mix of resentment and self-loathing that often hardens unmarried women who feel unfulfilled.

Feeling oppressed, Birotteau left the dining room and wandered in the garden, only to find Gamard and Troubert watching him, which intensified his discomfort. Distracted, he sought refuge in church duties and for a while forgot his troubles. When he returned home he discovered he had missed dinner: Mademoiselle Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting for him. This manipulation, small yet cruel, epitomized her habit of undermining his comfort while preserving an appearance of propriety.

Eight days after the troubles began, the relationship between Abbé Birotteau and Mademoiselle Gamard exposed a six-month plot. Birotteau, long blind to Gamard’s malice, finally recognized her hostility after the candlestick and clock incidents. The discovery crushed him; her presence felt suffocating. Gamard, enjoying vengeance, had carefully plotted against the unsuspecting priest, who was too absorbed in small worries to confide in friends.

Birotteau’s once-quiet life turned to torment. His anxiety about confronting Gamard worsened his health, so he sought help from Abbé Troubert, embarrassed to speak of Gamard’s provocations to someone so grave. Troubert listened coolly, suppressing a smile, and counseled patience, suggesting Gamard’s behavior stemmed from ill health.

Desperate, Birotteau thought a temporary departure might quell her hostility and planned a stay at Madame de Listomère’s country house. After ten days at the Alouette, Monsieur Caron, Gamard’s lawyer, informed him she worried about his prolonged absence. Startled, Birotteau pledged to return; Caron said Gamard wanted clarity to avoid disputes.

Back at the dining room, Birotteau told friends of his plight. Madame de Listomère suggested Troubert coveted Birotteau’s apartment. The group probed Gamard’s motives; an old landowner warned that this was only the beginning of long troubles. Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix reported the vicar-general was ill and Troubert might be appointed in his place, giving Troubert appointment power.

Madame de Listomère urged Birotteau to consider the canonry rather than endure Gamard. Friends counseled he might let Gamard and Troubert have their way if it would advance him. But the struggle escalated; they summoned Caron again. He returned with a document asking Birotteau to sign away his residence. Pressed by friends, he signed.

Birotteau’s despair deepened when he realized he had also signed over his belongings, which Gamard claimed as compensation for board differences. Confronting her, he was met with a cold reminder of the agreement. Devastated, he fled to Madame de Listomère; finding his possessions packed, he was taken back to the Alouette by Mademoiselle Salomon, who feared for his sanity. There he confided in friends about his misfortunes.

Monsieur de Bourbonne examined the deed and found it exploitative. Outraged, he urged legal action. The group debated suing versus compromising to avoid scandal, resolving to support Birotteau, though Bourbonne warned many would abandon him when the fight intensified.

Madame de Listomère’s invitation to Birotteau provoked town gossip and divided opinion; Gamard, slighted, sought to undermine his reputation. Friends began gathering evidence for a lawsuit amid political divisions and public distrust of the clergy. An expert appraisal revealed the true value of Birotteau’s belongings, suggesting Gamard’s claims were fraudulent.

As the legal battle opened, Birotteau’s situation grew precarious, fueled by gossip. The outcome remained uncertain as he faced the formidable alliance of Gamard and Troubert, determined to preserve their power and influence.

A few days after hostilities began between Birotteau and the old maid, Baron de Listomere—expecting promotion to corvette captain—received a letter warning of possible retirement. Shocked, he rushed to Paris to see the minister of the Navy, who laughed off his concerns. Further inquiries, however, revealed a document confirming his impending retirement, pending the director’s signature.

Desperate, the baron sought help from his uncle, a deputy, who learned the minister believed the baron had sided with the Radicals in Tours and had offended the influential Abbé Troubert, the vicar-general. His uncle advised reconciliation with Troubert, stressing the importance of good relations with the clergy for the baron’s career. Realizing the gravity, the baron resolved to avoid further conflict.

Three days later he told his aunt their family was precarious because of their support of Birotteau. They consulted Monsieur de Bourbonne, who counseled retreat: appease Troubert and distance themselves from Birotteau, now seen as a liability.

The baroness approached Birotteau, asking him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house. Shocked and distressed, Birotteau agreed, believing himself a burden. He requested the return of a portrait of Chapeloud that belonged to him; the baroness promised to negotiate with Gamard.

Soon Mademoiselle Gamard fell ill, and the town buzzed that Birotteau’s suit had caused her distress. The baroness visited but could not see her, then sought an audience with Troubert. They engaged in a duel of insinuations and civility, each trying to outmaneuver the other. The baroness presented Birotteau’s relinquishment; Troubert feigned indifference while secretly pleased. He later returned with Gamard’s refusal to part with Chapeloud’s portrait, which the baroness accepted to preserve peace.

Gamard died shortly after, leaving her fortune to Troubert, further complicating matters for the Listomeres. At her funeral Troubert delivered a grand eulogy that masked his true feelings. Birotteau, guilt-ridden over her death, mourned alone while the Listomeres struggled with the fallout and Troubert’s growing power.

Five months later Troubert became Bishop of Troyes; Madame de Listomere died, leaving an annuity to Birotteau. Troubert’s influence continued to threaten the baron’s career and ensured Birotteau’s legacy would be contested. In the end Birotteau was exiled to a distant parish, ill and grieving. Troubert’s ascent—marked by cunning and manipulation—reflects broader struggles within society and the Church, suggesting personal ambition often exacts a heavy cost from others.
