**The Vicar of Tours: Summary**

**Dedication**  
De Balzac dedicates "The Vicar of Tours" to sculptor David, emphasizing the enduring nature of David's work compared to the uncertain permanence of his own literary creations.

**I**  
In autumn 1826, Abbe Birotteau, a short, corpulent priest nearing sixty, is caught in the rain while returning home from a friend's house. Despite his discomfort, he feels uplifted by the prospect of being nominated as canon at the cathedral of Saint-Gatien, a long-held ambition. He reflects on his desire for a comfortable life filled with good food, clean clothes, and the coveted canonry.

Birotteau lives with Mademoiselle Gamard, an old maid who boards priests. He has long envied the apartment of his deceased friend, Abbe Chapeloud, who created a beautiful living space filled with books and comfort. After Chapeloud's death, Birotteau inherits his library and furniture, which brings him joy but also guilt over his friend's passing.

As Birotteau settles into his new life, he enjoys the comforts provided by Mademoiselle Gamard, known for her meticulous care of her lodgers. However, he soon notices a shift in her treatment towards him. Small signs of neglect, such as a lack of fire in his room and missing slippers, lead him to suspect that Mademoiselle Gamard harbors ill will against him.

Despite his good nature, Birotteau struggles to understand her hostility. He reflects on their relationship, realizing he has taken for granted the attentions he once received. Mademoiselle Gamard, feeling slighted by Birotteau's social engagements outside her home, begins to withdraw her kindness, creating tension between them.

Birotteau's longing for the canonry intensifies, making him increasingly oblivious to the conflict with Mademoiselle Gamard. His naivety prevents him from recognizing her resentment, stemming from his social aspirations and his failure to appreciate her efforts. The narrative explores themes of envy, ambition, and the fragility of happiness, illustrating how misunderstandings can lead to profound personal conflicts.

The following morning, Birotteau awoke with thoughts of his potential canonry, momentarily forgetting the ominous signs from the night before. He rang for his servant, Marianne, to light a fire, but when she did not come, Abbe Troubert entered after knocking. Troubert, a tall, lean man with a serious demeanor, contrasted sharply with the plump and jovial Birotteau. Their monthly visits were routine, and Troubert expressed concern over Birotteau's health and prospects for the canonry, unaware of the vicar's naivety regarding Troubert's ambitions.

The two men were opposites: Troubert's somber nature clashed with Birotteau's cheerful disposition. While Troubert was respected for his austere presence, Birotteau's amiability kept him in his position as vicar. Troubert, now fifty and in declining health, was expected to be promoted to vicar-general, while Birotteau's robust health made him a less appealing candidate for advancement.

Abbe Chapeloud, a sensible man, quietly opposed Troubert's rise, but Troubert maintained a facade of respect towards him. The morning after Mademoiselle Gamard had declared war on Birotteau, Troubert visited him, asking to borrow a book. Birotteau, pleased to help, felt grateful for Troubert's kindness, especially since it meant he would have a fire to dress by.

As they descended for breakfast, Mademoiselle Gamard, Birotteau's landlady, scolded him for bringing books into the dining room. She was irritable from being disturbed the previous night, souring the atmosphere. Birotteau struggled to engage in conversation, ultimately falling silent during breakfast, as his attempts at small talk were met with disdainful responses from Gamard, leaving him feeling isolated.

Gamard embodied the archetype of an old maid, marked by bitterness and unfulfilled desires. Her character reflected societal judgments against unmarried women, leading to her sharpness. Despite her harshness, Birotteau remained oblivious to the extent of Gamard's malice, caught in a cycle of trying to please her while enduring her disdain. After breakfast, he retreated to the church for solace, but upon returning home late for dinner, he discovered that Gamard had manipulated the clock to ensure he missed the meal, exemplifying her vindictive nature. Birotteau's life was increasingly overshadowed by Gamard's petty tyranny.

Eight days into the story, Abbe Birotteau discovers a plot against him by Mademoiselle Gamard, who has harbored resentment for months. Initially oblivious to her malicious intentions, Birotteau becomes tormented as unsettling incidents reveal he is under her scrutiny. His once peaceful life descends into despair as he perceives Gamard's vindictiveness as a personal attack.

Feeling isolated and ashamed to confide in friends, Birotteau seeks help from Abbe Troubert, hoping for intervention. However, Troubert dismisses his concerns as trivial, attributing Gamard's behavior to her health issues, which deepens Birotteau's sense of helplessness.

To escape, Birotteau plans a short stay at Madame de Listomere's country house, unaware this plays into Gamard's hands. At the Alouette, he is confronted by Monsieur Caron, Gamard's lawyer, who warns that his absence could lead to legal complications regarding his living arrangements. Confused and distressed, Birotteau returns to his friends, who urge him to stand firm against Gamard.

Madame de Listomere and others analyze the situation, suspecting Troubert covets Birotteau's apartment. They encourage him to resist Gamard's influence, but Birotteau's naivety leads him to sign a document relinquishing his rights to his belongings in Gamard's house, believing it to be a mere formality. This act seals his fate, as he later learns Gamard intends to keep his possessions as compensation for the difference in board prices.

Upon returning to confront Gamard, Birotteau is met with her triumphant demeanor and realizes the extent of his loss. Troubert has taken over his former apartment, and Gamard claims his furniture as part of their agreement. Devastated, Birotteau seeks solace at Madame de Listomere's, where friends promise to help him reclaim his dignity and possessions.

Mademoiselle Salomon, a devoted friend, takes Birotteau back to the Alouette, where he struggles to comprehend the betrayal he has faced. His friends, including Monsieur de Bourbonne, strategize a legal response against Gamard, believing they can expose her deceit. However, the local lawyer advises against pursuing the case, suggesting Birotteau's actions may be seen as ingratitude.

Despite this discouragement, Birotteau's friends remain committed to supporting him, recognizing the social implications of the conflict against Gamard and Troubert, who wield significant influence. The struggle escalates, drawing attention from townspeople and igniting a fierce rivalry between factions.

As the legal battle unfolds, Birotteau's friends gather evidence of the value of his possessions, which far exceed what he owed Gamard. The appraisal reveals that Birotteau's belongings are worth a substantial sum, strengthening his case against her claims. Ultimately, Birotteau's plight illustrates the complexities of human relationships and the often-hidden motivations that drive individuals, capturing the tension between personal integrity and societal expectations.

**Summary:**

A few days after hostilities began between Birotteau and the old maid, Baron de Listomere received troubling news about his potential retirement from the Navy. Concerned, he sought clarification from the minister, who dismissed his fears. However, further inquiries revealed a document confirming his retirement was pending signature. The baron turned to his uncle, a deputy, for assistance. His uncle warned that his political views had made him unpopular, especially with the influential Abbe Troubert, vicar-general and a key figure in the province. He advised the baron to reconcile with Troubert to secure his career.

Three days later, the baron returned home, anxious about the family's support for Birotteau, whom he viewed as a liability. He consulted Monsieur de Bourbonne, who suggested distancing themselves from Birotteau and appeasing Troubert. The baroness then approached Birotteau, asking him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house for family interests. Distressed, Birotteau reluctantly agreed but insisted on retrieving a portrait of Abbe Chapeloud, which belonged to him.

Madame de Listomere promised to negotiate with Mademoiselle Gamard for the portrait. Shortly after, Gamard fell ill and died, leaving her fortune to Troubert. The town buzzed with gossip, blaming Birotteau for her death. Madame de Listomere visited Troubert to negotiate peace, presenting Birotteau's relinquishment of claims. Troubert, feigning indifference, ultimately refused to part with the portrait, claiming it was a cherished memento.

Despite her efforts, Gamard's death led to Birotteau's further isolation. He was appointed curate of Saint-Symphorien, effectively exiling him from his community. Meanwhile, Troubert ascended to Bishop of Troyes, consolidating his power. Madame de Listomere passed away, leaving an annuity to Birotteau, but Troubert contested this legacy, threatening the baron's career. Ultimately, Birotteau's life deteriorated amid illness and despair, while Troubert's influence grew unchecked, illustrating the consequences of ecclesiastical politics in provincial France.
