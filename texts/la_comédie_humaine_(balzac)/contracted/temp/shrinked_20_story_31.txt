DEDICATION

To Monsieur J. B. Nacquart,
Member of the Royal Academy of Medicine.

Dear Doctor—Here is one of the most carefully hewn stones in the second course of the foundation of a literary edifice which I have slowly and laboriously constructed. I wish to inscribe your name upon it, as much to thank the man whose science once saved me as to honor the friend of my daily life.

De Balzac.

THE LILY OF THE VALLEY

ENVOI

Felix de Vandenesse to Madame la Comtesse Natalie de Manerville:

I yield to your wishes. It is the privilege of the women whom we love more than they love us to make the men who love them ignore the ordinary rules of common-sense. To smooth the frown upon their brow, to soften the pout upon their lips, what obstacles we miraculously overcome! We shed our blood, we risk our future!

You exact the history of my past life; here it is. But remember this, Natalie; in obeying you I crush under foot a reluctance hitherto unconquerable. Why are you jealous of the sudden reveries which overtake me in the midst of our happiness? Why show the pretty anger of a petted woman when silence grasps me? Could you not play upon the contradictions of my character without inquiring into the causes of them? Are there secrets in your heart which seek absolution through a knowledge of mine? Ah! Natalie, you have guessed mine; and it is better you should know the whole truth. Yes, my life is shadowed by a phantom; a word evokes it; it hovers about me and within my soul are solemn memories, buried in its depths like those marine productions seen in calm weather and which the ocean’s storms cast in fragments on the shore.

The mental labor which the expression of ideas necessitates has revived the old, old feelings which give me so much pain when they come suddenly; and if in this confession of my past they break forth in a way that wounds you, remember that you threatened to punish me if I did not obey your wishes, and do not, therefore, punish my obedience. I would that this, my confidence, might increase your love.

Until we meet,

Felix.

CHAPTER I. TWO CHILDHOODS

To what poet will belong the elegy that sings of childhoods starved of tenderness, where early blossoms are chilled by domestic frost? My own story is of such oppression: neglected as an infant, slighted by my mother and mocked by my siblings, I learned solitude and distrust instead of fraternal warmth.

Put to nurse in the country and forgotten for years, I returned to a parental house where even the servants pitied me. My brother and two sisters made my life a pastime of persecution; the unwritten compact of childhood that shields mutual faults did not exist for me. My being punished for others’ faults and excluded from protection hardened me: instead of turning to hatred, my suffering condensed into a deep inwardness that later overflowed into passionate attachment. Habitual humiliation bred a stubborn moral resistance; always expecting fresh injustice, I wore a sullen resignation that robbed childhood of its gaiety and made me appear dull—an appearance which my mother seemed to use as proof of defective nature.

When my mother spoke of educating me it chilled me; I preferred the solitude of the garden, where I played with pebbles, watched insects, and fixed my thought upon a star. Once, blamed for a prank I did not commit, I was forbidden to stay out after dark and punished for my star-gazing. I confided my pain to that star, developing the inward prattle of early thought. Long after beginning school I kept that devotion; at twelve I still watched the star with secret delight.

Charles, five years my senior and the favorite, was brawny and had a tutor; I, puny and a day-pupil at five, was taken to school by a valet with a meager lunch while others flaunted better fare. Ridicule and taunts followed me; I fought sometimes but had neither support nor protection. Once boys beat me with stones tied in a handkerchief; when the valet told my mother, she only exclaimed that I would always be a torment.

At school my gloom and aloofness confirmed my family’s judgment. When I could read and write, my mother sent me to Pont-le-Voy to the Oratorians’ class for dull lads. There I spent eight years as a kind of pariah, rarely seeing my family and receiving only three francs a month—barely enough for pens and ink. Lacking money for playthings I was excluded from games; refusing toadying kept me friendless. Under a tree, I read the library’s books and harbored countless griefs in that solitude. At prize distributions my parents never appeared; when I won, instead of joy I burst into tears and burned my crowns in the stove. The absence of my parents when other boys’ families came was a wound.

My unhappiness widened as my world did. I wrote passionate entreaties to be seen; they mocked my style and refused. At confession, driven nearly to hatred of life, my confessor pointed me to “Beati qui lugent,” and I found solace in prayer. From my first communion I plunged into religious ecstasy, which nourished dreams that strengthened imagination and turned my thoughts inward; I later attributed this to a guardian angel molding my spirit, granting me the power to see the inner soul of things.

At fifteen my father, doubting the Oratorians, sent me to an institution in the Marais under Monsieur Lepitre. Examined, I—who had reached rhetoric—was placed in a lower class. The Lepitre Academy continued my trials. My father gave no spending money; the house had a porter, Doisy, who breakfasts with the well-to-do pupils and smuggled luxuries. Long I resisted Doisy’s temptations, but eventually I yielded to café-au-lait and bread—small indulgences yet huge for a chastened boy.

Near the end of my second year in Paris my parents came to town. My brother wrote that they would dine at the Palais-Royal and go to the Theatre-Francais; eager yet ashamed because I owed Doisy a hundred francs, I pleaded with my brother to intercede. My father inclined to forgiveness; my mother raged, fearing ruin, called me a disgrace, and sent me back to school in disgrace. For twelve years this was my first interview with my mother.

When school ended, my father left me under Lepitre’s guardianship to pursue higher mathematics, law, and philosophy. Though nineteen, I remained tightly supervised; a man-of-all-work escorted me even to the law school. Paris, an Eldorado of temptations, remained beyond my reach; once at an aunt’s house I planned an escape to the Palais-Royal but was thwarted three times by fate—Lepitre’s coach, my mother’s sudden arrival—each intervening between me and the pleasures I craved.

Exhausted, study became my refuge and almost ruined my health. At twenty I was small and spare but had read deeply; knowing life metaphysically, I stood ready to feel and love. The journey from Paris to Tours was chilled by my mother’s coldness. In desperation I implored her; she called me unnatural. At Blois I even contemplated drowning; the parapet’s height saved me. Left at home with a third-story garret and no linen, I took refuge in my father’s library, reading ceaselessly. My eldest sister sometimes tried to console me, but often I wished to die.

The return of the Bourbons brought festivities in Touraine. A ball for the Duc d’Angouleme excited me; my mother, professing illness, at last had a blue coat secretly made for me. In the tent, stifled and dazzled, I felt disgust and withdrew. Mistaken for a sleepy child, a woman sat beside me; moved, I kissed her white shoulder as a child might a mother’s breast. She cried out, “Monsieur!” and her noble, saintly face struck me. Tears fell. She moved away like a queen.

That furtive kiss altered me. The star I had adored became a woman; a new soul unsheathed. Though I had seen women before, none was like her. The very thought that she lived in Touraine made the air delicious. My mother, fearing for my health, sent me to Frapesle, a château on the Indre belonging to a friend, offering the freedom I craved. Determined, I resolved to search every château on foot for her.

Walking across moors by the Indre, at midday beneath a walnut-tree I saw a château gleaming among vines—Clochegourde. Love filled the valley: the Indre ribboned through poplars and oaks, spring making the countryside seem virginal. My host at Frapesle pointed out Clochegourde. “It belongs to the Comte de Mortsauf; his wife is a demoiselle de Lenoncourt. They never leave. Madame de Mortsauf is a woman who would hold the highest position wherever she might be.” When he added she had been at the Duc d’Angouleme’s ball, I knew the woman I had kissed was here.

We reached Clochegourde in the afternoon. The modest yet effective house perched on a slope by the Indre, terraced gardens spilling toward the water, a little bell-tower and an air of genteel neglect—its whole aspect was dainty as a flower in orchards and vineyards. Warned by a watch-dog, a servant said the count had gone to Azay but Madame la comtesse was at home. My companion hesitated, but we were announced. A golden voice bade us enter; I recognized it from the ball and, blushing, almost fled. Our eyes met. She returned to her embroidery and counted stitches; embarrassed, I could not speak. Monsieur de Chessel explained that I was a young man weakened by study whom he had brought to rest. Distrustful at first, she offered me restoratives and a few kind words that gave me courage. I blushed like a girl and thanked her.

She invited my companion to dine; his worldly read of the situation gave me hope, and his suggestion that I might please her lent me power. At dinner she spoke of harvest and vintage—subjects beyond my knowledge—yet the music of her voice entranced me. Her utterance made each syllable a charm; many times I prolonged conversation only to hear her speak.

Her inattention let me study her features. A mole marked the start of her shoulder line; golden hair framed a pure neck. Her face cannot be easily captured; it required an artist to render its inner fires. Her soft fair hair brought her suffering; her brow was round and full of unuttered thought; green-brown eyes, ordinarily wan, flashed with a rare light in keen emotion. A Grecian nose, curved lips, and skin like warmed camellia completed a harmonious visage. Though mother of two, her figure retained youthful freshness. Simple, bashful, and grave through suffering, she charmed without seeking to charm; the rarity of her gestures lent solemnity to all she said. Except with her children she seldom met another’s gaze.

On that morning she wore a rose-colored gown with a wide collar, a black belt, and little boots; her hair twisted in a tortoise-shell comb. The constant emanation of her soul made Clochegourde’s life luminous: the gray-panelled salon, neat porcelain vases, and a backgammon board conveyed composed regularity. No room had ever filled me with so many impressions; many of my political and scientific ideas were born there. Through the windows the valley spread to Pont-de-Ruan and Azay; her ordered domestic life impressed itself upon everything.

Had I first met her among her husband and children I might have been different; but having stolen a kiss at the ball, having breathed her perfume, I longed for pastoral companionship—paddling the river, climbing trees, lingering in the sun. Had she asked me impossible things, I would have sought them to win other riches for her.

When she mentioned Monsieur de Mortsauf a dozen men would have felt hatred at the obstacle; I felt both hatred of the impediment and fear of the struggle with him. “Here is Monsieur de Mortsauf,” she said. The count entered with his little son. A child cried “Here’s papa!” and a pale little girl, whom I took for six but learned to be nine, came forward and sat on her mother’s lap. The children’s fragility beside their healthy mother explained something of the sadness on her brow.

The count himself was striking. Though forty-five he seemed nearer sixty. A crescent of hair fringed the back of his bald head; his face suggested a white wolf with blood about its muzzle—his nose inflamed as if by some wasting poison. His flat forehead and narrow pointed face showed wear; cheekbones were brown and prominent against pallor, and his light-yellow eye was bright but cold. His mouth showed violence and domination; his chin was flat and long. Thin and very tall, he lived on the conventional worth of his caste. Country carelessness left his dress neglected; his hands were brown and wiry, his gloves seldom, his shoes common.

Though ten years of emigration and ten more of farm-life had changed his physique, he still retained vestiges of nobility. Even the bitterest liberal would have admired his chivalric loyalty and the unshaken convictions of one who reads the Quotidienne; he was devoted to a cause, honest in his political antipathies, incapable of serving his party but very capable of injuring it, and without the slightest real knowledge of France’s affairs. The count was one of those upright men available for nothing, obstinately in the way of all; ready to die under arms at his post, but preferring to give his life rather than his money.

During dinner I noticed, in the hanging of his flaccid cheeks and the furtive glances at his children, signs of some consuming thought. Who could fail to see that he had transmitted to his children those weak bodies lacking life’s principle? If he blamed himself he denied others the right to judge him. Harsh in self-knowledge, yet without greatness of soul or charm to compensate for the misery he had caused, his private life must have been full of irascibilities shown in his angular features and restless eye. When his wife returned with the children clinging to her, I felt unhappiness, as if walking over a vault and sensing the depths below. Seeing the four together, thoughts steeped in sadness fell upon me as a gray rain dims a landscape after a clear sun.

When conversation paused, the count told his wife who I was and mentioned circumstances connected with my family unknown to me. He asked my age. When I told it, the countess echoed surprise at her daughter’s age. Perhaps she had thought me fifteen. Later I discovered this was another tie that bound her to me. Even then I read her soul. Her motherhood quivered with a tardy ray of hope. Seeing me over twenty yet so slight and delicate and nervously strong, a voice cried to her, “They too will live!” She looked at me searchingly, and in that moment I felt the barriers of ice between us melting. She seemed to have many questions but asked none.

“If study has made you ill,” she said, “the air of our valley will soon restore you.”

“Modern education is fatal to children,” remarked the count. “We stuff them with mathematics and ruin health with sciences, make them old before their time. You must stay and rest here,” he added, turning to me. “You are crushed by the avalanche of ideas. What future will this universal education bring unless we replace public schooling in the hands of religious bodies?”

These words matched a speech he later made at elections when he refused to support a man who could have served the royalist cause. “I shall always distrust men of talent,” he said.

Presently the count proposed a tour of the gardens.

“Monsieur—” said his wife.

“Well, what, my dear?” he answered, with an arrogance showing how absolute he chose to be at home.

“Monsieur de Vandenesse walked from Tours this morning,” she said. “Monsieur de Chessel, not aware, has already taken him on foot over Frapesle.”

“Very imprudent of you,” the count said, turning to me; “but at your age”—and he shook his head.

Conversation resumed. I soon saw how intractable his royalism was and how much care was needed to navigate it. The man-servant announced dinner. Monsieur de Chessel gave his arm to Madame de Mortsauf, and the count gaily seized mine to lead me into the dining-room.

The room, simple but quaintly furnished with Touraine tiles, Boulle buffets, handmade tapestry chairs, family silver, Dresden china, and flowers, delighted me. I sat at her right hand, serving her wine. In three hours my life had mingled with hers. That terrible kiss had bound us in a secret that inspired mutual shame. A glorious self-abasement possessed me. I strove to please the count, fondled the dogs, amused the children, and would gladly have been claimed by them. Love has intuitions like genius; I perceived that gloom, discontent, hostility would destroy my footing in that household.

The dinner passed with inward happiness. I ignored her obvious coldness and the count’s indifference masked by politeness. Love, like life, has an adolescence when it suffices unto itself. I made several foolish replies induced by passion, but no one perceived their cause, not even she, who knew nothing of love. The rest of my visit was a dream until by moonlight I recrossed the Indre, watching white visions embellish meadows, shores, and hills, and listening to a tree-frog’s melancholy note. Since that evening I never heard it without delight. A sense came then of the marble wall against which my feelings had dashed. Would it be always so? Unhappy events of my past rose and struggled with the personal pleasure I had enjoyed. Before reaching Frapesle I turned to look at Clochegourde and saw beneath its windows a little punt fastened to an ash-tree and swaying on the water. This punt belonged to Monsieur de Mortsauf, who used it for fishing.

“How do you mean?” I asked Monsieur de Chessel, who had remarked on my reception.

“He never, to my knowledge, received any one so well.” His speech raised my spirits.

Though inexpert socially, I was struck by Monsieur de Chessel’s feeling. His real name was Durand; ambitious, he had assumed his wife’s family name, becoming Monsieur de Chessel, later Count by Louis XVIII. His children reaped the fruits of his audacity without knowing its cost. Parvenus are like monkeys: clever in climbing, absurd at the summit. My host’s character had pettiness and envy. The peerage and he lay on diverging lines. Twice elected deputy and twice defeated, yesterday director-general, to-day nothing at all; his ups and downs had made him sour. Had he sought less he might perhaps have obtained more; but he had enough superiority to make him wish to advance.

At this time Monsieur de Chessel’s ambition had a new dawn. Royalty smiled upon him, and he affected the grand manner. Still he was kind to me, and with him I found peace and rest for the first time. The interest he showed in my affairs seemed like paternal love. His hospitable care contrasted with my usual neglect, so I felt childish gratitude to the home where no fetters bound me and where I was welcomed.

The owners of Frapesle are associated with the dawn of my life’s happiness, so I mingle them in memories I love to revive. Later I had the pleasure of rendering my host a service connected with his letters-patent. Monsieur de Chessel enjoyed wealth with an ostentation that annoyed neighbors. He could vary horses and equipages; his wife dressed exquisitely; he received on a grand scale; his servants were numerous; he was said to be aping princes. The Frapesle estate is immense. Before such luxury the Comte de Mortsauf, with one family cariole and forced by limited means to let or farm Clochegourde, was Tourangean up to the time when royal favor restored the family to a distinction unlooked for. His greeting to me, the younger son of a ruined family whose escutcheon dated to the Crusades, was meant to show contempt for a neighbor not of noble birth. Monsieur de Chessel understood this. They met politely but lacked the daily intimacy that ought to have linked Clochegourde and Frapesle, two estates separated only by the Indre.

Jealousy, however, was not the only reason for the Count de Mortsauf’s solitude. His early education, that of great families, was incomplete in knowledge but supplemented by society’s training and duties under the crown. Monsieur de Mortsauf had emigrated when the second stage of his education was to begin, and that training was lacking. He believed in the immediate restoration of the monarchy; with that conviction his exile became a long idleness. When the army of Condé, which his courage led him to join, was disbanded, he expected another post under the white flag and never sought, like other emigrants, an industry. Perhaps he lacked courage to lay aside his name and earn his living. Daily postponed hopes, a scruple of honor, and trials undermined his courage. Long tramps on insufficient food and betrayed hopes injured his health and discouraged his mind. By degrees he became utterly destitute. If misery is tonic for some, on others it acts as a dissolvent; the count was of the latter.

Reflecting on this Touraine gentleman—tramping and sleeping along Hungarian roads, sharing shepherds’ mutton and refusing service to France’s enemies—I never felt bitterness against him, even when I saw him at his worst. The natural gaiety of a Frenchman soon deserted him; he became morose, fell ill, and was charitably cared for in a German hospital. His disease was an inflammation of the mesenteric membrane, often fatal and liable to alter constitution and produce hypochondria. His love affairs, which I alone discovered, were low-lived, and not only destroyed his health but ruined his future.

After twelve years of misery he returned to France under the Emperor’s decree permitting emigrants’ return. As he crossed the Rhine and saw Strasburg’s tower, his strength gave way. “France! France!” he cried, “I see France!” Born to wealth, he was now poor; made to command a regiment or govern a province, he was without authority or future; constitutionally robust, he returned infirm and worn out. Want of money made his name a burden. His unalterable opinions, his antecedents with Condé, his trials, his recollections, his wasted health, gave him susceptibilities little spared in France, that land of jest and sarcasm. Half dead he reached Maine, where, by some civil-war accident, the revolutionary government had not sold one of his farms, which his farmer held by saying he was the owner.

The Lenoncourt family at Givry invited him to stay; they had lost immense property too. By birth Monsieur de Mortsauf was a suitable husband for the Lenoncourts’ daughter. Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt, rather than reject marriage with a feeble man of thirty-five, accepted. It gave her the opportunity of living with her aunt, the Duchesse de Verneuil, sister of the Prince de Blamont-Chauvry, who was like a mother.

Madame de Verneuil, intimate with the Duchesse de Bourbon, belonged to a devout society led by Monsieur Saint-Martin, whose disciples practised virtues taught by mystical illumination: doctrines of the spirit’s progress through reincarnations; they ordain contempt for this life’s sufferings and inspire care of the inner angel. Active prayer and pure love are elements of this faith, born of the Church but returning to primitive Christianity. Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt remained Catholic. Cruelly tried by revolutionary horrors, the Duchesse de Verneuil acquired in her last years a halo of passionate piety, which shed celestial love upon her niece.

After the duchess’s death, Madame de Mortsauf received visits at Clochegourde from Saint-Martin, who corrected his last books there. The duchess had given Clochegourde to her niece; her sudden death threw a gloom over the early days of the marriage and associated Clochegourde with sadness for the bride. The first period of her settlement was not happiest but the least troubled of her life.

After exile trials Monsieur de Mortsauf dreamed of a secure future and found some happiness in agriculture. But the birth of his first child, Jacques, was a thunderbolt that ruined past and future. The doctor declared the child lacked vitality. The count concealed this from the mother but sought other advice and was confirmed at Madeleine’s birth. These events and an inward consciousness of the cause increased his diseased tendencies. His name doomed to extinction, a pure young woman made miserable beside him and doomed to the anguish of motherhood without its joy—this uprising of his former into his present life crushed his spirit and completed its destruction.

The countess guessed the past from the present and read the future. Though nothing is so difficult as to make a man happy when he knows himself to blame, she set herself to that task, worthy of an angel. She became stoical. Descending into an abyss, whence she still could see the sky, she devoted herself to the care of one man as a sister of charity devotes herself to many. To reconcile him with himself she forgave what he could not forgive. The count grew miserly; she accepted privations. Like those whose world knowledge breeds suspicion, he feared betrayal; she lived in solitude and yielded without murmur to his mistrust. With a woman’s tact she made him will to do what was right until he fancied the ideas were his own, enjoying in his person honors never his. After experience of married life she resolved never to leave Clochegourde; she feared outbreaks that gossip would turn against her children. Thus, thanks to her, no one suspected Monsieur de Mortsauf’s real incapacity; she wrapped his ruins in a mantle of ivy. The fickle, embittered man found rest in his wife; his secret anguish was lessened by her balm.

This brief history is partly a summary forced from Monsieur de Chessel by vexation. His worldliness enabled him to penetrate several mysteries of Clochegourde. But the prescience of love could not be misled by Madame de Mortsauf’s sublime attitude. When alone in my little bedroom, a sense of the full truth made me spring from my bed; I could not bear to stay at Frapesle when I saw Clochegourde’s lighted windows. I dressed, went softly down, and left the chateau by a tower door at the foot of a winding stairway. The cool night calmed me. I crossed the Indre by the bridge at the Red Mill, took the blessed punt, and rowed before Clochegourde, where a brilliant light streamed from a window.

Again I plunged into meditation, charmed by nightingale and sedge-warbler. Ideas glided like fairies through my mind, lifting the black veil that had hidden the future. Soul and senses were charmed. With what passion my thoughts rose to her! Again and again I cried, “Will she be mine?” During preceding days the universe had enlarged to me, but in one night I found its center. On her my will henceforth fastened; I desired to be all in all to her, to heal and fill her lacerated heart.

That night beneath her windows, amid the murmur of waters, I betrothed my soul to her with the faith of a poor Castilian knight—a faith with which all love begins.

At daybreak I fled back to Frapesle; no one had seen me. After breakfast I went to the meadows but soon returned to my punt, the willows, and Clochegourde. All was silent and palpitating. Dragonflies hovered, cattle chewed cud, the vineyard earth glowed. I ran up the road, and saw the count walking beside the hedge toward a gate by the river bank.

“How are you this morning, Monsieur le comte?” I cried.

He looked at me pleasantly. “Quite well,” he answered. “You must love the country, to be rambling about in this heat!”

“I was sent here to live in the open air.”

“Then what do you say to coming with me to see them cut my rye?”

“Gladly,” I replied. “I’ll own my ignorance; I don’t know rye from wheat, nor poplar from aspen; I know nothing of farming.”

“Well, come and learn,” he cried gaily, returning upon his steps. “Come in by the little gate above.”

He showed me yards, farm buildings, pleasure-grounds, orchards, vineyards, and kitchen garden until we came to an avenue by the river where I saw Madame de Mortsauf with her children. A woman is very lovely under such light and quivering shade. The count made me admire the view, which here differs from that seen from the heights; I might have thought myself in a corner of Switzerland. We quickened as we approached Madame de Mortsauf, who dropped the book and took Jacques upon her knees as he had a violent cough.

“A sore throat,” she answered when the count asked; she seemed not to see me. She held the child, and her eyes seemed to shed life into the poor frail creature.

“You are so extraordinarily imprudent,” said the count sharply; “you expose him to the river damps and let him sit on a stone bench.”

“Why, papa, the stone is burning hot,” cried Madeleine.

“They were suffocating higher up,” said the countess.

“Women always want to prove they are right,” said the count, turning to me.

To avoid taking sides I watched Jacques, who complained of his throat. His mother carried him away, and heard her husband say: “When they have brought such sickly children into the world they ought to learn how to take care of them.” Cruel words; his self-love drove him to defend himself at his wife’s expense. The countess hurried into the portico. Monsieur de Mortsauf sat on the bench with bowed head in gloomy silence. My position was awkward; he neither spoke nor looked at me. At last he rose abruptly.

“We will put off our walk to another day, Monsieur le comte,” I said gently.

“No, let us go,” he replied. “Unfortunately, I am used to such scenes—I, who would give my life without the slightest regret to save that of the child.”

“Jacques is better, my dear; he has gone to sleep,” said a golden voice. Madame de Mortsauf suddenly appeared. She came forward without bitterness and bowed to me.

“I am glad to see you like Clochegourde,” she said.

“My dear, shall I ride over and fetch Monsieur Deslandes?” said the count.

“Don’t be worried,” she said. “Jacques did not sleep last night; he had a bad dream, and I told him stories all night. His cough is nervous; I stilled it with a lozenge, and he has gone to sleep.”

“Poor woman!” said her husband, taking her hand with a tearful look, “I knew nothing of it.”

“Why be troubled when there is no occasion?” she replied. “Now go and attend to the rye. You know if you are not there the men will let gleaners enter before the sheaves are carried away.”

“I am going to take a first lesson in agriculture, madame,” I said.

“You have a very good master,” she replied, motioning to the count, whose mouth screwed into that smile called a “bouche en coeur.”

Two months later I learned she had passed that night in great anxiety, fearing the croup; while I, rocked by thoughts of love in my boat, imagined she might see me from her window adoring the gleam of the candle lighting a brow furrowed by fears! The croup prevailed at Tours and was often fatal. When we were outside the gate, the count said in a voice of emotion, “Madame de Mortsauf is an angel!” The words staggered me. As yet I knew little of the family, and the conscience of a young soul made me exclaim inwardly: “What right have I to trouble this perfect peace?”

Glad to find a listener in a young man he could lord over, the count talked of the future the Bourbons would secure. We had desultory conversation in which I listened to childish nonsense which amazed me. He was ignorant of facts that have proof; he feared educated people, rejected superiority, and scoffed at progress. I discovered many sensitive fibres in him which required caution not to wound; a conversation with him was a strain. When I had felt his defects, I conformed with the same suppleness his wife showed in soothing him. Later I might have angered him, but now, humble as a child, supposing youth knew nothing and men in their prime knew all, I was genuinely amazed at results obtained at Clochegourde by this patient agriculturist. I besieged him with questions about prices and cultivation, and he seemed glad to answer.

“What in the world do they teach you in your colleges?” he exclaimed at last.

On that first day the count said to his wife when he reached home, “Monsieur Félix is a charming young man.”

That evening I wrote to my mother asking her to send my clothes and linen, saying I would remain at Frapesle. Ignorant of the revolution just beginning and not perceiving its influence on my fate, I expected to return to Paris to resume legal studies. The Law School did not open till November; meantime I had two and a half months.

The first part of my stay, while I studied to understand the count, was a period of painful impressions. I found him extremely irascible without cause; hasty and sometimes dangerously so. Sometimes he showed flashes of the brave gentleman of Condé’s army—bursts of will which might have made a man heroic in emergency; in presence of certain ideas his nostril contracted, his forehead cleared, and his eyes shot lightnings soon quenched. Sometimes I feared he might detect my eyes’ language and kill me. I was young then and merely tender. Will had scarcely dawned. My passionate desires shook me like throes. Death I feared not, but I would not die until I knew mutual love. I was perplexed; I hoped for some fortunate chance; I watched; I made the children love me; I tried to identify with the family.

Little by little the count restrained himself less in my presence. I came to know his sudden outbreaks of temper, deep melancholy, flashes of brutality, bitter complaints, cold hatreds, impulses of latent madness, childish moans, cries of a man’s despair, unexpected fury. The moral nature differs from the physical inasmuch as nothing is absolute in it. My position at Clochegourde, my future life, depended on this one eccentric will.

I cannot describe the distress that filled me whenever I entered Clochegourde and asked, “How will he receive me?” With what anxiety I saw clouds on that stormy brow. I lived in perpetual qui-vive. I fell under that man’s dominion; and the sufferings I endured taught me to understand Madame de Mortsauf’s. We began by exchanging looks of comprehension; tried by the same fire, I made many discoveries during those first forty days—actual bitterness, tacit joys, hopes alternately submerged and buoyant. One evening I found her pensive, watching a sunset that reddened the summits. I fancied desolation in her attitude favorable to my first appeal, and I said, “Some days are hard to bear.”

“You read my soul,” she answered; “but how have you done so?”

“We touch at many points,” I replied. “Surely we belong to the few born to highest joys and deepest sorrows; whose feeling qualities vibrate in unison and echo each other; whose sensitive natures suffer horribly among discordant surroundings, and whose happiness mounts when they meet congenial beings. Put such souls among discord and they suffer; put them among like souls and they know a fraternal comprehension. Have I not expressed our mutual sufferings?”

She shuddered, then, without removing her eyes from the sun, said, “I hear Madeleine coughing,” and left abruptly.

The countess showed no displeasure at my constant visits for two reasons. First she was pure and her thoughts wandered into no forbidden regions; next I amused the count and soothed that lion without claws. I found an excuse for my visits: Monsieur de Mortsauf proposed to teach me backgammon, and I accepted; as he did, the countess was betrayed into a look of compassion that seemed to say, “You fling yourself into the lion’s jaws.” If I did not understand this at once, three days had not passed before I knew what I had undertaken. My patience, the fruit of a miserable childhood, ripened under this trial. The count delighted in jeering at me for not practicing his rules; if I reflected he complained of slowness; if I played fast he was angry; if I forgot to mark points he declared I was always too rapid. When we played for money his winnings gave him abject delight.

A word from his wife was enough to console me. But soon I fell into unexpected misery. My money was disappearing under losses. Though the count was always present during my visits until late, I cherished hope of a moment when I might say a word to reach my idol’s heart; to obtain it I was forced to continue weary games, during which my feelings were lacerated. There were moments when we were silent, she and I, looking at sunlight on meadows or the moon on river sandbanks; saying only, “Night is beautiful!”

“Night is woman, madame.”

“What tranquillity!”

“Yes, no one can be absolutely wretched here.”

Then she returned to her embroidery. But without money, farewell to these evenings. I wrote to my mother to send some. She scolded me and sent only enough to last a week. Where could I get more? My life depended on it. Thus in the dawn of my first great happiness I found the same sufferings that assailed me elsewhere; but in Paris at college I evaded them by abstinence; at Frapesle they were active; so active that I was possessed by impulses to theft, visions of crime, furiously desperate thoughts that rend the soul and must be subdued to keep self-respect. The memory of my mother’s parsimony taught me indulgence for young men which one who has stood upon the abyss must feel. Though my rectitude was strengthened by those moments, I have never known human justice draw its blade through a criminal’s throat without thinking: “Penal laws are made by men who have never known misery.”

At this crisis I found a treatise on backgammon in Monsieur de Chessel’s library, and I studied it. My host gave me a few lessons; less harshly taught by the count I made progress and applied rules and calculations I knew by heart. Within days I could beat Monsieur de Mortsauf; but no sooner had I done so and won his money than his temper became intolerable; his eyes glittered like a tiger’s. He flung down the dice, quivered with rage, bit the dice-box, and said insulting things. Such violence came to an end as I learned to manage the games to be nearly equal to the last moment; he never admitted my skill. The countess, who understood my manoeuvres, gave me mute thanks. From that evening she always looked at me when she spoke. I cannot explain my condition when I left her. My soul had annihilated my body; I weighed nothing; I did not walk, I flew. That look I carried within me; it bathed me with light, and her last words, “Adieu, monsieur,” still sounded in my soul. I was born into a new life; I was something to her! I slept on purple and fine linen. Flames darted before my closed eyelids in dreams; her voice became palpable, an atmosphere of light and fragrance wrapping me. On the morrow her greeting expressed unuttered feeling, and from that moment I was initiated into the secrets of her voice.

That day was decisive. After dinner we walked on the heights across a barren plain stupendously lit by sunset; I held Madeleine by the hand, Madame de Mortsauf led Jacques. The count, in front, suddenly struck the earth with his cane and said to me in a dreadful tone: “Such is my life!—but before I knew you,” he added, looking at his wife. The reparation was tardy; the countess turned pale.

“But what delightful scenes are wafted here, and what a view of the sunset!” I cried. “For my part I should like to own this barren moor; there may be treasures if we dig for them. But its greatest wealth is that of being near you. Who would not pay a great cost for such a view?” She thanked me with a look.

“Bucolics!” exclaimed the count, bitterly. “This is no life for a man who bears your name.” Then he changed tone—“The bells!” he cried, “don’t you hear the bells of Azay? I hear them ringing.”

Madame de Mortsauf gave me a frightened look. Madeleine clung to my hand.

“Suppose we play a game of backgammon?” I said. “Let us go back; the rattle of the dice will drown the bells.”

We returned. In the salon an indefinable dread possessed us. The count flung himself into an armchair absorbed in reverie which his wife avoided interrupting. I also kept silence. Perhaps she thought backgammon might divert him. Nothing was harder than to make him play, which he greatly desired; like a pretty woman he required coaxing. If warned by ill-humor, I suggested a game; he would dally. “It is too late,” he would say; “besides, I don’t care for it.” Then a series of affectations left you ignorant of his real wishes.

On this occasion I pretended gaiety to induce him. He complained of giddiness and choking; then he sighed. At last he consented. Madame de Mortsauf left to put the children to bed and lead family prayers. All went well during her absence; I allowed Monsieur de Mortsauf to win, and his delight put him beside himself. This sudden change from gloom to wild joy, expressed in a crazy laugh, distressed and alarmed me. I had never seen him in so marked a paroxysm. Our intimacy bore fruits in that he no longer restrained himself before me. Day by day he endeavored to bring me under his tyranny and obtain fresh food for his temper; moral diseases seem like creatures seeking to enlarge their empire.

Presently the countess came down and sat near the table, ostensibly for light on her embroidery though anxiety led her to place her frame. A bit of ill-luck changed the count’s face; from gaiety it fell to gloom, his eyes rolled. Then worse mischance followed: Monsieur de Mortsauf made a fatal throw which decided the game. Instantly he sprang up, flung the table at me and the lamp on the floor, struck the chimney-piece with his fist and jumped about the room. The torrent of insults, imprecations, and incoherent words that rushed from his lips would have made an observer think of medieval tales of possession. Imagine my position!

“Go into the garden,” said the countess, pressing my hand.

I left before the count could notice my disappearance. On the terrace I walked and heard his shouts and moans from the adjoining bedroom. At intervals through that tempest I heard the voice of an angel, rising like a nightingale after rain. I walked under acacias in that lovely August night, waiting for the countess to join me. For several days an explanation seemed to float between us; a word would have set it gushing. Perhaps she loved, as I did, these quiverings of spirit resembling fear and numbing sensibilities while we held our lives unuttered. An hour passed. I sat on the balustrade when her steps sounded.

“Monsieur de Mortsauf is sleeping,” she said. “When he is thus I give him an infusion of poppies; the attacks are infrequent and this remedy never loses effect—Monsieur,” she continued in the most persuasive inflexion, “this accident has revealed a secret sedulously kept; promise me to bury the recollection of that scene. Do this for my sake, I beg you. I don’t ask you to swear; give me your word of honor and I shall be content.”

“Need I give it to you?” I said. “Do we not understand each other?”

“You must not judge Monsieur de Mortsauf unfavorably; you see effects of his sufferings under emigration,” she went on. “Tomorrow he will forget all that he has said and done; you will find him kind and excellent as ever.”

“Do not seek to excuse him, madame,” I replied. “I will do all you wish. I would fling myself into the Indre if I could restore his health and ensure you a happy life. The only thing I cannot change is my opinion. I can give you my life, but not my convictions; I can pay no heed to what he says, but can I hinder him from saying it? No. In my opinion Monsieur de Mortsauf is—”

“I understand you,” she interrupted hastily; “you are right. The count is as nervous as a fashionable woman,” she added, softening madness by the word. “But he is only so at intervals, once a year when the weather is very hot. Ah, what evils from the emigration! How many fine lives ruined! He would have been a great soldier—”

“I know,” I said, interrupting to show it was useless to deceive me.

She stopped, laid one hand lightly on my brow, and looked at me. “Who has sent you here into this home? Has God sent me help, a true friendship to support me?” She paused, then, as she laid her hand upon mine, “For you are good and generous—” She raised her eyes to heaven as if invoking testimony, then rested them upon me. Electrified, I was guilty by social law of want of tact, though in some natures such indelicacy means a brave desire to meet danger, avert a blow, arrest an evil; oftener, an abrupt call upon a heart to learn if it answers.

“Before we say more,” I said, my voice shaken, “suffer me to purify one memory of the past.”

“Hush!” she said quickly, touching my lips with a finger she instantly removed. She looked at me haughtily, with the glance of a woman too exalted for insult. “Be silent; I know of what you are about to speak,—the first, the last, the only outrage ever offered to me. Never speak to me of that ball. If as a Christian I have forgiven you, as a woman I still suffer from your act.”

“You are more pitiless than God himself,” I said, forcing back tears.

“I ought to be so, I am more feeble,” she replied.

“But,” I insisted, “listen to me now if only for the first, the last, the only time in your life.”

“Speak, then,” she said; “speak, or you will think I dare not hear you.”

Feeling the moment decisive, I spoke in a tone that commands attention; I told her that all women I had ever seen were nothing to me; but when I met her, a studious, not bold nature, I had been possessed by a frenzy no one who once felt it could condemn; that no heart had been so filled with passion that conquers all things even death—

“And contempt?” she asked, stopping me.

“Did you despise me?” I exclaimed.

“Let us say no more,” she replied.

“No, let me say all!” I cried. “It concerns my life, my whole being; it holds a secret you must know or I must die in despair. It also concerns you, who, unawares, are the lady in whose hand is the crown promised to the victor in the tournament!”

Then I related my childhood and youth, not with cold distance but in the language of a young man whose wounds still bleed. I described cruel details which I now spare. I spread before her the treasure of my hopes, the virgin gold of my desires, the whole of a burning heart kept alive beneath perpetual winter. When, bowed by remembered sufferings, I awaited her reply, she illumined the darkness with a look and a sentence.

“We have had the same childhood!” she said, turning a face on which the halo of martyrs shone.

After a pause, in which our souls were wedded in the thought “I am not alone in suffering,” the countess told me in the voice she kept for her little ones how unwelcome she had been as a girl when sons were wanted. She showed how her troubles as a daughter bound to her mother differed from those of a boy sent to school. My desolate neglect seemed a paradise compared to contact with a millstone under which her soul was ground until her aunt saved her. She told how, when her aunt died, she received Clochegourde and set up a home; but the duchess’s death left gloom.

“You will live happy, I must die of pain. Life is what we make of it, and mine is made forever. No power can break the heavy chain to which a woman is fastened by this ring of gold—the emblem of a wife’s purity,” she said.

We knew we were twins of one womb; she never dreamed of half-confidence between brothers. After a short sigh she told me of her married life, deceptions and disillusions, the rebirth of childhood's misery. Like me she had suffered under trifles; mighty to souls whose substance quivers to the least shock. When she married she possessed some girlish savings; these, when needed, she gave to her husband without telling him. He took no account, never regarded himself her debtor. Ah how she went from trial to trial! Monsieur de Mortsauf habitually neglected to give her money for the household. When she asked, he seemed surprised and never spared her the mortification of petitioning for necessities. What terror filled her when her husband’s disease was revealed, and she quailed under his first outburst! What bitter reflections before admitting he was a wreck! What calamities had come of bearing children! What anguish at infants born almost dead! With what courage had she said in her heart: “I will breathe the breath of life into them; I will bear them anew day by day!” Then conceive the bitterness of finding her greatest obstacle in the heart and hand from which a wife should draw succour! She saw disaster threaten him. As each difficulty was conquered, new deserts opened until the day she thoroughly understood her husband’s condition, the constitution of her children, and the character of the neighborhood; a day when she taught her feet to trample through mud and snow, trained her nerves to bullets, and all her being to passive obedience.

“You would have to live here many months,” she said, “to understand what difficulties I have met improving Clochegourde; what persuasions to make him do what was important. You cannot imagine the childish glee he shows when anything I advised was not at once successful. All that turns out well he claims for himself. Yes, I need infinite patience to bear his complaints when I am half-exhausted trying to amuse his weary hours, to sweeten his life and smooth paths he himself has strewn with stones. The reward is that awful cry: ‘Let me die, life is a burden to me!’ When visitors are here and he enjoys them, he forgets gloom and is courteous. I cannot explain that want of loyalty in a man chivalrous. He is quite capable of riding to Paris to buy me ornaments, as he did before the ball. Miserly in his household, he is lavish upon me if I wish it. I would it were reversed; I need nothing for myself, but household wants are many. In my desire to make him happy, I began by letting him treat me as a victim—I who could then, by a few caresses, have led him like a child—but I was unable to play a disgraceful part. Now, the family’s welfare requires me to be calm and stern as Justice—and yet, I too have a heart that overflows with tenderness.”

“But why,” I asked, “do you not use this influence to master him and govern him?”

“If it concerned myself only I should attempt less to conquer the dogged silence with which for days he meets my arguments, nor answer his irrational remarks and childish reasons. I have no courage against weakness, any more than against childhood; they may strike me as they will, I cannot resist. Perhaps I might meet strength with strength, but I am powerless against those I pity. If I were required to coerce Madeleine in some matter that would save her life, I should die with her. Pity relaxes my fibres and unstrings my nerves. The violent shocks of the last ten years have broken me down; my feelings, so often battered, are numb; nothing can revive them; even the courage with which I once faced troubles begins to fail me. Yes, sometimes I am beaten. For want of rest—sea-baths to recover nervous strength—I shall perish. Monsieur de Mortsauf will have killed me, and he will die of my death.”

“Why not leave Clochegourde for a few months? Surely you could take your children to the seashore.”

“In the first place, Monsieur de Mortsauf would think he were lost if I left him. Though he will not admit his condition he is aware of it. He is both sane and mad, two natures in one, explaining many irrational actions. Besides, nothing would go right if I were absent. You may have seen in me a mother watchful to protect her young; harder still are cares imposed by Monsieur de Mortsauf, who constantly cries as he follows me, ‘Where is Madame?’ I am Jacques’ tutor and Madeleine’s governess; but that is not all, I am bailiff and steward too. Working an estate here is the most fatiguing of employments. We get small returns; farms are cultivated on shares needing close supervision. If I left, not a servant would be in it in a week. So I am attached to Clochegourde as those leaden finals are to a roof. I have no reserves with you. The country-side is ignorant of our secrets, but you know them. Say nothing but what is kind and friendly, and you shall have my esteem—my gratitude. On those terms you are welcome at Clochegourde, where you will find friends.”

“Ah!” I exclaimed, “I see I have never really suffered, while you—”

“No, no!” she exclaimed, with a smile that might melt granite. “Do not be astonished at my frank confidence; it shows life as it is, not as imagination pictures it. We all have defects and good qualities. If I had married a spendthrift he would have ruined me. If I had given myself to an ardent young man perhaps I could not have retained him; he might have left me, and I should have died of jealousy. For I am jealous!” she cried. “But Monsieur de Mortsauf loves me as much as he can; all that his heart contains of affection he pours at my feet, like Magdalen’s cup of ointment. Believe me, a life of love is an exception to earthly laws; all flowers fade; great joys have a morrow of evil—if a morrow at all. Real life is anguish; yet here there are smiles in the sky, few but compensating for grief. Women who are mothers live and love more through sacrifices than pleasures. Here I draw upon myself storms I fear may break upon my children; in doing so I feel something I cannot explain which gives secret courage. Time was when my children’s condition filled me with despair; to-day as they grow they grow healthier and stronger. And then, our home is improved and beautified, our means improving. Who knows but Monsieur de Mortsauf’s old age may be a blessing? Those who stand before the Great Judge with palms in their hands, leading to Him beings who cursed their lives, they have turned sorrows into joy. If my sufferings bring about my family’s happiness, are they sufferings at all?”

“Yes,” I said, “they are; but they were necessary, as mine have been, to make us understand the true flavor of the fruit ripened on our rocks. Now, surely, we shall taste it together; surely we may admire its wonders, the sweetness of affection it has poured into our souls. Have we not followed the same star? Are we not more than brother and sister? That which heaven has joined we must not keep asunder. The sufferings you reveal are the seeds scattered by the sower for a harvest ripening in sunshine. Shall we not gather it sheaf by sheaf? What strength in me that I dare address you thus! Answer, or I will never again cross that river!”

“You have spared me the word love,” she said sternly, “but you have spoken of a sentiment I know nothing of and which is not permitted me. You are a child; and again I pardon you, but for the last time. Understand, Monsieur, that my heart is intoxicated with motherhood. I love Monsieur de Mortsauf neither from social duty nor from desire to win blessings, but from an irresistible feeling that fastens all the fibres of my heart upon him. Was my marriage a mistake? My sympathy for misfortune led to it. It is women’s part to heal woes, to comfort those who rush into the breach and return wounded. How shall I make you understand me? I have felt selfish pleasure in seeing you amuse him; is not that pure motherhood? Did I not make you see just now the three children to whom I am bound, to whom I shall never fail, on whom I strive to shed a healing dew and the light of my soul without withdrawing or adulterating a single particle? Do not embitter the mother’s milk! Though as a wife I am invulnerable, you must never again speak thus to me. If you do not respect this simple command, the door of this house will be closed to you. I believed in pure friendship, a voluntary brotherhood more real than blood. I was mistaken. I wanted a friend who was not a judge, a friend to listen in moments of weakness when reproof is killing, a sacred friend from whom I had nothing to fear. Youth is noble, truthful, capable of sacrifice; seeing your persistency I believed in some divine purpose; I thought I should find a soul that would be mine, a heart to pour my troubles into when they deluged mine. Could I but have this friend, my life precious to these children might be prolonged until Jacques grew to manhood. But that is selfish! The Laura of Petrarch cannot be lived again. I must die at my post, like a soldier, friendless. My confessor is harsh, austere, and—my aunt is dead.”

Two tears filled her eyes, gleamed in moonlight, and rolled down her cheeks; I caught them and drank them with avidity, moved by her words and the thought of ten years of secret woe, wasted feelings, and ceaseless care—years of lofty heroism. She looked at me with gentle stupefaction.

“It is the first communion of love,” I said. “Yes, I am now sharer of your sorrows. I am united to your soul as our souls are united to Christ in the sacrament. To love, even without hope, is happiness. What woman could give me a joy equal to receiving your tears! I accept the contract ending in suffering to myself. I give myself to you with no ulterior thought. I will be to you that which you will me to be—”

She stopped me with a motion and said in a deep voice, “I consent if you will promise never to tighten the bonds that bind us.”

“Yes,” I said; “but the less you grant the more evidence of possession I ought to have.”

“You begin by distrusting me,” she replied, with melancholy doubt.

“No, I speak from pure happiness. Listen; give me a name by which no one calls you; a name to be ours only, like the feeling that unites us.”

“That is much to ask,” she said, “but I will show I am not petty. Monsieur de Mortsauf calls me Blanche. One only person, my dear aunt, called me Henriette. I will be Henriette once more, to you.”

I took her hand and kissed it. She left it in mine with trustfulness that makes a woman superior to men. She leaned on the balustrade and gazed at the river.

“Are you not unwise to rush to friendship’s extremes? You have drained the cup at a draught. It is true a real feeling is never piecemeal; it must be whole. Monsieur de Mortsauf,” she added after a short silence, “is above all things loyal and brave. Perhaps for my sake you will forget what he said today; if he forgets to-morrow I will tell him what occurred. Do not come to Clochegourde for a few days; he will respect you more if you do not. On Sunday, after church, he will go to you. I know him; he will wish to undo the wrong, and he will like you better for treating him as a responsible man.”

“Five days without seeing you, without hearing your voice!”

“Do not put such warmth into your manner,” she said.

We walked twice round the terrace in silence. Then she said with a tone of command that she had taken possession of my soul, “It is late; we will part.”

I wished to kiss her hand; she hesitated, then gave it and said entreatingly: “Never take it unless I give it to you; leave me my freedom; if not, I shall be simply a thing of yours, and that ought not to be.”

“Adieu,” I said.

She opened the lower terrace gate for me. Just as she was about to close it she opened it again and offered her hand, saying: “You have been truly good to me this evening; you have comforted my whole future; take it, my friend, take it.”

I kissed her hand again and again, and when I raised my eyes I saw tears in hers. She returned to the upper terrace and I watched her from the meadow. On the road to Frapesle I saw her white robe in moonbeam; a few moments later a light burned in her bedroom.

“Oh, my Henriette!” I cried, “to you I pledge the purest love that ever shone upon this earth.”

I turned at every step as I regained Frapesle. Ineffable contentment filled me. A way was open for the devotion swelling in youth and long inert in me. Like the priest entering a new life, my vows were taken; I was consecrated. A simple “Yes” had bound me to keep my love within my soul and never to abuse our friendship by leading this woman step by step to love. Noble feelings were awakened, and I heard their murmur. Before confining myself within a room I stopped beneath an azure heaven sown with stars, listened to the ring-dove plaints of my heart, and gathered in the air emanations of that soul which henceforth must ever seek me. How grand she seemed, with absolute forgetfulness of self, her religion of mercy to wounded hearts, her declared allegiance to her legal yoke. She stood serene upon her pyre of saint and martyr. I adored her face as it shone in darkness. Suddenly I fancied a meaning in her words, a mysterious sign making her sublime. Perhaps she longed that I should be to her what she was to the little world around her. Perhaps she sought to draw from me strength and consolation, putting me within her sphere, her equal, or perhaps above her. The stars, some bold builders say, communicate light and motion. This thought lifted me to ethereal regions. I entered once more the heaven of former visions; I found a meaning for my childhood miseries in the illimitable happiness they had led me to.

Spirits quenched by tears, hearts misunderstood, saintly Clarissa Harlowe forgotten, children neglected, exiles innocent of wrong, all ye who enter life through barren ways, on whom men’s faces look coldly and ears close, cease your complaints! You alone can know the infinitude of joy in that moment when one heart opens to you, one ear listens, one look answers. A single day effaces past evil. Sorrow, despondency, despair, and melancholy passed but not forgotten are links by which the soul fastens to its mate. Woman falls heir to all our past and gives them back ennobled; she explains those former griefs as destiny’s payment for joys eternal which she brings when our souls are wedded. Angels alone can utter the new name of that sacred love, and none but women, dear martyrs, truly know what Madame de Mortsauf became to me—to me, poor and desolate.

As I read that letter I felt the maternal heart beneath my fingers; I understood why the countess had forbidden me to open it in Touraine—she feared I would fall at her feet and weep.

I met my brother Charles, until then a stranger. He displayed a haughtiness that kept us apart and prevented brotherly affection. Kindliness depends on similarity of soul; there was no point of contact between us. He preached social maxims as if I knew nothing, setting me off as a foil to his qualities. But inward solitude had sharpened my perceptions; having known Madame de Mortsauf I could see through such pretensions. I accepted my primogeniture more willingly, not as my brother’s dupe.

I frequented the Duchesse de Lenoncourt’s alone; Henriette’s name was rarely spoken there, except by the simple old duke, whose welcome suggested his daughter had commended me to his care. Just as I was overcoming the awkwardness of a youth newly entering the world and learning to use Henriette’s maxims, the events of March 20 altered everything.

My brother followed the court to Ghent; by Henriette’s advice I went there too with the Duc de Lenoncourt. The duke’s kindness became sincere protection when he saw my devotion to the Bourbons; he presented me to the king. Devotion counted at Ghent, and Louis XVIII judged men with a clear eye—I had the happiness of pleasing him.

A letter from Madame de Mortsauf, brought by a Vendean emissary, enclosed a note telling me Jacques was ill. Torn between care of her son and household obligations, the countess needed a friend to lighten her burden. Though ambitious and eager to rival my brother—then at the Congress of Vienna—I resolved, moved by the countess’s sad face, to leave Ghent and return to Clochegourde in the king’s service. Fortune favored me: the emissary could not return and the king wanted a faithful messenger. The Duc de Lenoncourt requested the mission for me; after an audience with the king I set out, able to serve my true sovereign and to go back to Henriette.

I carried the king’s instructions successfully in Paris and in the Vendée. By late May, being denounced to Bonapartist authorities, I fled, traveling on foot through parks and woods across the Bocage and Poitou. I reached Saumur, Chinon, and then the woods of Nueil, where the count found me and brought me safely to Clochegourde.

“Jacques is better,” he said. When we entered the salon Henriette exclaimed, “Felix!” We looked at one another with the hunger of two souls making up for lost months. She had prayed for my safety and prepared my room. At dinner the news of Waterloo and the Allies’ march to Paris was of political consequence, but for us the small domestic joys mattered: she told me, with shining pleasure, “You shall have ice to-day!”—an ingenuous proof of affection that stirred me. Her health and spirit revived in days; my heart opened in response. Absence, for ardent souls, strengthens love; reunion was like spring reviving a valley. Her recovery was delicate; she told me of nights by Jacques’s bedside, of having lived for three months within herself, in a dark palace, alternately listening for moans and a voice.

When she spoke of visions and said, “While I slept my heart watched,” the count interrupted harshly, calling her “half crazy.” He wounded her; she asked whether he had no indulgence for her weakness as a woman. The count’s habitual arrows pierced her, and though he regretted the murmur immediately, the pain showed the long strain of his treatment.

I tried to divert her mind to household matters. She told me of the success of her agricultural plans—the new leases, planted trees, and income—hoping that if the king returned their pension would be restored and Jacques’s fortune secured. She revealed a kind of spiritual insight: in fits of deep meditation she saw luminous or misty visions of her children, which she took as divine signs; she saw me brilliantly illuminated and heard a mental voice guiding me. I clung to her, cried that my obedience was due to none but her, and she smiled. She advised me, when the king returned, to go to Paris—“it may be degrading to beg, but dangerous to be out of the way of receiving favors.” She called me “my son” in her heart, and took my arm contentedly.

That evening I witnessed a scene that moved me: the whole household knelt in prayer, made solemn by Madeleine’s childish voice. The count presented me with backgammon; the countess brought her embroidery and said it had languished while her child was ill. That night, my love burning, I went to her door and listened to her breathing; foolish and dangerous, it was the impulse that urges some men to the brink of ruin to know if they can bear it.

I plucked flowers and made bouquets; I spent days at Clochegourde and dined at Frapesle sometimes. The new avenue to Clochegourde, the iron gate, the circular enclosure—small marks of prosperity—impressed me. I had hoped to surprise the countess, but she preferred to be prepared.

When she saw the young man I had become she blushed, was pale, yet let me kiss her hand. She feared my being lost to the world; she begged that I promise never to abandon her. She told me to be a child there and a man with the king; in effect, she wanted my devotion within safe limits.

She had even thought of silk-worm nurseries, but feared they would only increase his tyranny. She knew that weakness breeds insatiable demands: “what you yield to them to-day they exact to-morrow.” Monsieur de Mortsauf delighted in getting the better of her but deceived himself about his goodness.

She dared not decide on separation after fifteen years’ marriage; her devotion to her children forbade desertion. She resolved to consult her confessor, the new Abbé Birotteau, for courage. The boat ride, simple pleasures, and mutual attention deepened our intimacy. I reproached myself gently for not making more decisive claims, for fearing to bind what seemed to be love too tightly.

The count’s illness grew grave. I hurried to Tours for Dr. Origet and obtained a surgeon; bleeding saved him from death. The countess blamed herself, thinking she had brought on the crisis; she wept and offered acts of contrition. The doctor prescribed nursing and a long convalescence; she and I shared the watches. He warned that the moral state of patient and nurses is potent: “a single word, a fear expressed, has the effect of poison.” Origet watched us with scrutiny but, seeing our devotion, he respected us.

For fifty-two days the count hovered between life and death; Henriette and I each watched twenty-six nights. Our shared devotion and duties brought us into a new intimacy. The count’s room became tidy; we learned to serve each other in countless small attentions. She took my hand readily in service; we became, in effect, half-married in devotion. She entrusted me increasingly with household affairs so that she could nurse. In the evenings we discussed estate business and children; I sat where a husband might sit, and she encouraged me with innocent pleasure. Illness stripped the world’s conventions; in that concentrated duty we revealed our true selves.

Two small moments revealed deeper feelings, though never consummated: once she came quietly to my watch and I found her with moist, trembling hands; another time I surprised her weeping by the window. When the count showed signs of convalescence, his old habits reappeared, and he resumed quarrels over food and management, tormenting the household again. Yet the doctor’s orders gave Henriette authority; she governed him with calm firmness and sometimes yielded to pity. She had learned to be stricter, and I took joy in seeing her exercise control. But in private she would indulge him, and her compassion made me both happy and jealous.

She once asked me to accompany her to church to return thanks for the count’s recovery; I left her at the door to visit friends, and she reproached me tenderly for being “a man.” She confessed that such questions tortured her: could she abandon the old man who was the father of her children? If she failed in duty, the children would be exposed to his domination and perhaps harm. She could not sacrifice them. The thought of marrying me, of turning away from duty to embrace personal happiness, was morally impossible for her. She lived by feelings intertwined with conscience and religion. I, more impatiently passionate, could have sold eternity for a day; she resisted such compromises.

One afternoon, while the count sat in the sun and his face extinguished all hope of earthly passion, I received a letter from Tours bearing the king’s signet—my recall. The countess read it and we felt the sudden emptiness: we were necessary to one another and feared the future apart. She knelt and gave me a lock of hair—precious, symbolic—asking me to keep it. We kissed in a solemn, pure way; there was a sacrifice in the gesture. That evening I left; we parted at the walnut-tree where I had first seen her years earlier. She traveled with Madeleine as I returned to Paris.

In Paris my life resumed its duties: the work of the council and my position kept me occupied. I wrote to the countess weekly; she answered twice a month. The letters and our daily duties made our attachment a disciplined devotion—a private religion whose observances preserved feeling. My story at court spread; my reserve and loyalty attracted notice. Society was polite and women were kind; after the Duc de Berry’s marriage the court regained splendor and the city revived with the presence of European notabilities. I was drawn into salons but remained inwardly distant, sustained by the memory of Clochegourde.

Five months after leaving, in midwinter, the countess wrote despairing of Jacques’s serious illness—though he was now out of danger, precautions for his lungs troubled her. Then Madeleine fell ill: the little girl, once gay and blooming, had become pale and anemic. The countess, exhausted by Jacques’s long illness, found no courage to carry these added cares. The household relapsed into the old storms; Monsieur de Mortsauf regained power. She wrote that she felt “turned to stone by grief,” asking if I could love her now when she was “callous, ungrateful, turned to stone by grief.” She feared the loss of the children, the emptiness of life, and confessed that Clochegourde was very sad.

Throughout these events her nature remained noble and self-sacrificing. She had the courage to consult pastors and to bear the trials for her children’s sake. My love for her had been chastened into something that combined worship, tenderness, and sorrow. I remained her friend, steward, and confidant, conscious that our intimacy had grown out of faithful service rather than conquest. The count’s illness had opened to me the inner life of the one woman I loved—her conscience, her maternal heart, and her piety—a love that was at once celestial and austere, and which, in the end, prevented the consummation I desired but sanctified the devotion I offered.

It was at the height of my vitality, wrapped in morning light and the promise of crimson evenings, that I met in the salons of the Élysée-Bourbon a woman who presided over society with that rare blend of wealth, pedigree, beauty, grace, manners, and wit—the Marchioness of Dudley. Married to an old British peer, she combined the outward allure of charm with the inward authority of the “iron hand in the velvet glove.”

The English, you know, keep a cold distance: the Channel is an image of the social barrier they raise. They admit only a select few; the rest are as if nonexistent. Habit, law, and custom encase English life and polish its surface so thoroughly that Englishwomen become irresistible precisely because they are framed by appearances. Trained between extremes—virtue as a duty, social station as an obsession—they are forced into a kind of cultivated hypocrisy: outwardly delightful, inwardly constrained. That very constraint produces a particular charm: a meticulous care of person, a passionate but contained love like Juliet’s, and an enthusiasm for romantic difficulty because their daily lives are monotonous.

You who envy them know these white sirens: impenetrable yet comprehensible, who make love an end in itself and who never vary their enjoyments, so that their souls repeat a single note. My liaison with Lady Dudley acquired a disastrous reputation. At an age when the senses ruled and when I still cherished the image of the saintly Henriette de Mortsauf, I could previously resist seduction. It was perhaps the shine of that fidelity that piqued Lady Dudley’s passion. My resistance inflamed her curiosity; the more I withdrew, the more she pursued me. Many Englishwomen, starved of variety by their strict routines, adore romantic novelty and welcome the risks that make conquest glorious. Arabella desired singularity; she wanted to be the exception, the one who conquered the unyielding.

Do not excuse my fault, Natalie—observe rather the inequality in our social rules: a man has fewer legitimate means to stop a woman’s pursuit than a woman has to escape a man’s advances. Manners forbid the force that might silence a woman; modesty is a female monopoly and ridicule corrodes masculine reluctance. My pride, devotion, and sensitivity were not unaffected when Arabella sought my approval in a ballroom, when she trembled at my glance and delighted in pleasing me. Her rank put her everywhere; in the diplomatic circle she contrived to seat me beside her. She whispered of sacrifices she would make if loved as I loved Madame de Mortsauf and promised discretion. After evenings of irresistible display she came to me; scandal followed. Lady Dudley renounced place and wealth, flung aside her station in an effort to eclipse the virtue she had ruined; she took pleasure in exposing the riches of her passionate kingdom.

Listen indulgently: this is one of those crises that afflict many men. Arabella was a paradox: milky, gentle, yet with an iron nature; slender and delicate but muscular, tireless in struggle; her appetite was elemental and voracious, a desert love—violent, azure, changeless. Clochegourde, by contrast, symbolized a moist, spiritual devotion. I saw her and Henriette as representing two different realms: the mistress of the body and the wife of the soul. England, I thought, deified matter; it perfected the material comforts and left divine spirituality wanting. English luxury tempts the senses into a glossy monotony that can smother the soul. Lady Dudley gratified appetite and mind; Madame de Mortsauf nourished the infinite heart.

I loved Arabella passionately—she was magnificent in animal vitality and intellectually sparkling—but I adored Henriette. By night I wept with happiness; by morning with remorse. Some women hide jealousies under angelic kindness; Arabella, over thirty, could feel and calculate, stifle a moan with the will of a hunter. She sought to kill Henriette in my soul but was never openly jealous or complaining. She watched me like a lioness with prey; she let me write to Henriette in her presence yet rarely read my letters and never tried to know their addressee. Proud of a love that would die rather than betray, she declared she would kill herself if I left her—an English dramatism she attributed to high birth and the ruling classes.

For six months I was lost in enervating delights, caught in the intoxicating intermix of experience and artifice that older women teach the young. These pleasures, sensorial poetry revealed by a practiced hand, bind younger men to older women and harden their taste, leaving an indelible mark. In complacent sophistry I would sometimes console myself that these pleasures would elevate spirit over matter; sometimes Arabella’s presence led me to blaspheme against the angel of Clochegourde. Once a traitor, I feared any careless word to Henriette might destroy her. At times, in the midst of pleasure, Henriette’s name rose like the voice that asks Cain, “Where is thy brother?” and a horror of my treachery seized me.

At last my letters to Henriette went unanswered. Panic seized me; I decided to return to Clochegourde. Arabella did not oppose the trip; she even offered to accompany me to Tours, seeing danger in my rival’s proximity and perhaps plotting to turn the journey to her advantage. Blind with fear and guileless, I failed to see the trap. She proposed to stay near Tours, meet only at night, and avoid daylight outings. I rode to Clochegourde, stopping beneath my old walnut-tree, and saw Madame de Mortsauf in white on the terrace. When I galloped across the fields she met me with only three words—“Oh, you here!”—which revealed she knew my treachery. Her voice, once tender, was now that of someone who had been cut to the quick; life’s spring had been torn out of her.

Her mother, whose later letter she showed me, had informed her. The countess received me with a slow, princely courtesy, hiding a wound. She threatened to tell Monsieur de Mortsauf of my presence and then moved away. The whole family gathered: the count greeted me warmly, Madeleine—now a young woman—moved like a gazelle; Jacques ran about, feverish but buoyant. The count tried to smooth things, attributing the countess’s mood to whims, and urged me to question her and help. He confided in me that, for weeks, his wife’s disposition had shifted from devoted gentleness to peevishness; he feared for her health and his own failing strength. He spoke of vague stomach complaints, of a sensibility that might be killing him. This talk of illness pressed upon me the sight of Henriette’s pallor and sunken eyes in the salon. Where once her face had haloed saints, grief had hollowed her. I desired to pour happiness into her soul but realized I had helped embitter her spring.

Madeleine, having recovered from anemia, had become a developing young woman; Jacques, at seventeen, seemed fragile despite a deep voice. Henriette’s maternal joy shone when she looked at them: they were the compensation for all other affections. “When I see my children thus, happiness stills my griefs,” she said. Yet she confessed her own confusion: “If other affections fail, duties done and crowned with success are compensation.” Her domestic steadiness masked the inner havoc my betrayal caused. I watched subtle signs—candleholders moved, my room reassigned—small daggers. When I appealed to her for forgiveness she answered coldly that “Henriette” was gone; henceforth she would be Madame de Mortsauf, a devoted friend who needed time to grow accustomed to my presence. She begged me to be gentle and to let her heal, pointing to the valley she loved, which wounded her like memory.

At dinner the count probed me about Lady Dudley. I answered honestly; Madame de Mortsauf asked whether Arabella had children and other questions that betrayed her hurt and curiosity. Her restraint was now a moral barrier; she could not be alone with me. The count pressed her to confide in me, and in private she explained that life’s sufferings had taught her much. She judged that all pain had meaning, and she would meet Arabella without hatred; in the eyes of God, a woman must love the mother of the man she loves. She confessed to an inner transmutation: she could no longer be the heavenly bride I had once adored; she would be a sisterly, pure friend— “the light from above, sparkling and cold,” she said. She wished to change our dangerous attachment into something safe and sacred: the love of a sister.

The next days were torment. Her fainting, after a scene of jealous domestic quarrel with the count, revealed the depth of her exhaustion. She feared losing the virtue that made her who she was. When I implored forgiveness once more by the river, threatening to throw myself into the Indre, she replied with tenderness: “Are you not always my son?”—a phrase that spared me and returned me to a filial place. Yet the count’s jealous talks, his appeals to pity, and the countess’s fragile health made everything precarious. I grew to understand the horror of being nothing where once one was everything.

One evening we drove out in a storm; the countess and I conferred on duty and pardon. She took my hand to her heart and said that what had happened must be part of God’s ordering; she would not condemn me for a weakness of the senses but would instead become the sisterly light I had needed. She insisted that I love Arabella with all the justice I owed her, since the sensual woman and the devout mother occupied different spheres of my life.

As we crossed Charlemagne moor it was Arabella who first signaled our approach: her dog barked, and she rode up like a triumphant, athletic figure. The Englishwoman judged her rival at once and, with characteristic reserve, gave us an expression of scorn before disappearing into the underbrush. Henriette, who had told me she would see Arabella, asked me to go and propitiate her. I followed the dog into the moonlit wood and found Arabella ready to flee or to clasp me, according to her mood. She rode with utter skill and said things with striking directness: she declared that in England they die for love, that she would kill herself rather than be abandoned. She made dramatic vows and then, when we reached Saint-Cyr, flung herself upon me and kept me in her rooms. There, in the comfort of her house, she lampooned the pious morality I seemed to admire and offered instead the comforts of an English household—tea, sermonized virtues, hearty food, and practical consolations.

Arabella had the peculiar English gift of mixing ardent passion and blunt realism. She scorned a woman who set principle above the plain truth that women should give themselves or not: to refuse and moralize was, in her view, a cowardly hypocrisy. She joked about adopting the vicar and making religion a nightly entertainment; she promised to conform to any habit to please me—hair-shirt or scripture—with a frankness that was at once seduction and satire. In her hands morality became a commodity to be arranged around pleasures; in her voice there was both the danger of surrender and the endless, crude allure of possession. That night she declared herself ready to be my slave, my consolation, my refuge—if only I would choose the life she offered.

Thus I stood between two worlds: Henriette’s lofty, suffering purity, who would exchange passionate danger for sanctified sisterhood, and Arabella’s bold, material, all-consuming love. Each represented a truth about human nature: the mystic devotion that idealizes and refuses the body, and the material passion that exalts sensation and makes a religion of possession. In this sharp opposition, my weakness and my vanity were revealed; my heart was rent by choices each demanding everything I could not wholly give.
