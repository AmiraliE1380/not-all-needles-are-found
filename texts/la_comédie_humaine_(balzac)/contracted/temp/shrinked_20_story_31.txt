**DEDICATION**

To David, Sculptor:

The permanence of the work on which I inscribe your name—twice made illustrious in this century—is very problematical; whereas you have graven mine in bronze which survives nations—if only in their coins. The day may come when numismatists, discovering amid the ashes of Paris existences perpetuated by you, will wonder at the number of heads crowned in your atelier and endeavor to find in them new dynasties.

To you, this divine privilege; to me, gratitude.

De Balzac.

**THE VICAR OF TOURS**

I

In the autumn of 1826, Abbe Birotteau, the main character of this story, was caught in a rainstorm while returning home from a friend's house. He hurried across the deserted square known as “The Cloister,” located behind the cathedral of Saint-Gatien in Tours.

The Abbe Birotteau, a short, corpulent man of about sixty, had suffered from gout and was particularly averse to wet shoes. Despite this, he was in good spirits, having won money at cards with Madame de Listomere and feeling optimistic about his long-held ambition to be appointed canon of the cathedral. He was wrapped in thoughts of this promotion, which made him oblivious to the rain and his discomfort.

Once, the Cloister had housed several dignitaries of the Chapter, but after the confiscation of ecclesiastical property, it was transformed into a narrow street, the Rue de la Psalette. The left side of this street featured a single house overshadowed by the cathedral, which had always been occupied by priests and was owned by an old maid named Mademoiselle Gamard. She had taken in priests as boarders, and her devout nature allowed her to retain the property without objection during the Restoration.

Birotteau was heading to this house, where he had lived for two years. His apartment had been a source of envy and a long-held desire. To be Mademoiselle Gamard’s boarder and to become a canon were his two great ambitions, representing the aspirations of a priest who sought comfort and status in life. His desire for the apartment had become a passion, filled with obstacles and hopes.

Mademoiselle Gamard could only accommodate two lodgers, and for twelve years, she had housed the Abbe Troubert and the late Abbe Chapeloud. When Chapeloud died, Birotteau took his place, having admired the apartment and its furnishings during his visits. The late canon had been a close friend, and Birotteau’s desire for the apartment grew alongside his affection for Chapeloud.

Chapeloud had improved the apartment over the years, filling it with books and furniture that Birotteau envied. The vicar’s longing for the comforts of that space became an obsession, and he often found himself wishing for Chapeloud’s death to fulfill his desire. However, he never considered how to influence his friend’s will.

Chapeloud, aware of Birotteau’s desires, forgave him for his unspoken wishes. Just before his death, he told Birotteau, “This time you will certainly get the apartment. I feel it is all over with me now.” After Chapeloud’s passing, Birotteau inherited his library and furniture, which somewhat eased his grief. He mourned his friend but also felt a sense of relief at acquiring the comforts he had long desired.

Birotteau’s first days of mourning were spent organizing his new possessions, and he was content with his new life, even though the canonry he hoped for was given to another. Mademoiselle Gamard welcomed him as a boarder, and he enjoyed the comforts that had once belonged to Chapeloud. He marveled at the attentions Gamard provided, which he had often heard praised by Chapeloud.

However, Birotteau soon found himself longing for the canonry, which became an obsession. His thoughts of promotion consumed him, and he was so preoccupied that he left his umbrella behind in the rain. When he arrived home, he found the door locked and had to ring the bell multiple times before being let in. His servant, Marianne, informed him that Mademoiselle Gamard had assumed he was already home.

Birotteau felt a pang of annoyance at the situation, especially as he noticed a lack of the usual attentions he had come to expect from Gamard. He began to suspect that something was amiss, as he had not received his slippers or a fire in his room. These small neglects led him to believe that Gamard was intentionally slighting him.

As he settled into his room, he reflected on the changes in his treatment by Gamard, realizing that he had been subjected to a subtle form of persecution. He pondered the reasons behind her behavior, convinced that he must have displeased her in some way. Despite his good nature, he was oblivious to the underlying tensions in their relationship.

Birotteau’s innocence and lack of worldly experience left him vulnerable to Gamard’s manipulations. He failed to recognize the signs of her growing resentment, which had developed over the eighteen months he had lived with her. While he believed he had managed their relationship well, he was, in fact, the target of her quiet vengeance.

As he went to bed, he hoped for clarity in the morning, unaware that the reasons for Gamard’s hostility would remain hidden from him. His naivety and lack of self-awareness prevented him from understanding the dynamics at play, and he continued to suffer the consequences of his unintentional selfishness.

The contrast between Birotteau and Chapeloud became evident; the latter had navigated his relationship with Gamard with shrewdness, while Birotteau’s simplicity led him into a web of misunderstandings. Gamard’s desire for social recognition and her ambition to create a vibrant salon were thwarted by Birotteau’s indifference to her expectations.

Despite his initial happiness, Birotteau’s growing discontent with Gamard’s company led him to seek solace elsewhere, spending evenings with friends who offered the social engagement he craved. This shift further alienated Gamard, who felt her ambitions slipping away as Birotteau distanced himself from her.

As the vicar resumed his previous social habits, Gamard’s resentment deepened, and she began to plot her revenge. The neglect he experienced—such as the locked door and the absence of his slippers—was a manifestation of her growing animosity. Birotteau remained oblivious to the brewing storm, believing he had successfully navigated his domestic life.

The events that unfolded in the vicar’s life serve as a prologue to a deeper exploration of the complexities of human relationships, ambition, and the often unseen forces that shape our interactions. The narrative reveals how small grievances can escalate into significant conflicts, illustrating the intricate dance of social dynamics in a provincial setting.

The next morning, Birotteau awoke preoccupied with thoughts of his potential canonry, momentarily forgetting the ominous signs he had perceived the night before. He rang for Marianne to light a fire, but when she did not appear, he was about to ring again when Abbe Troubert entered after knocking. This monthly visit was expected, and Troubert immediately noticed the absence of a fire. He called for Marianne, then inquired about Birotteau’s health and any news regarding his canonry. Birotteau, unaware of Troubert's resentment towards Madame de Listomere for not inviting him to her home, shared details of his efforts to secure the position.

The two abbes were starkly different in appearance and demeanor. Troubert was tall, thin, and serious, with a face marked by wrinkles that hinted at sarcasm and contempt, while Birotteau was plump, with a round, cheerful face. Troubert’s calm exterior masked a dullness that some attributed to his subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard, while Birotteau’s jovial nature made him well-liked. Despite their differences, both men were unlikely to rise above their current positions—Troubert due to his ambition and Birotteau due to his amiable disposition.

At fifty, Troubert had managed to dispel fears about his ambitions through a saintly life, and his declining health suggested he might soon be appointed vicar-general. In contrast, Birotteau’s robust health made him appear a strong candidate for longevity. Abbe Chapeloud, a respected figure in the diocese, had quietly opposed Troubert’s elevation, but Troubert treated him with respect, despite Chapeloud’s skepticism.

As Troubert visited Birotteau, he explained that he needed to borrow the “Register of Bishops,” which Birotteau gladly offered. They went downstairs together, each carrying a large book, only to be met with Mademoiselle Gamard’s sharp disapproval. She scolded Birotteau for cluttering her dining room, and he felt the sting of her ill temper, which contrasted sharply with his usual cheerful demeanor.

During breakfast, the atmosphere was tense. Mademoiselle Gamard complained about being disturbed the previous night, and Birotteau, feeling the weight of her displeasure, struggled to engage in conversation. He attempted to break the silence with comments about the coffee and the weather, but his efforts were met with disdain. Mademoiselle Gamard’s demeanor reflected the bitterness often found in old maids, who, feeling unfulfilled and rejected, often harbor resentment towards others.

Old maids like Mademoiselle Gamard often embody a complex mix of societal rejection and self-loathing. Their lives, devoid of romantic fulfillment, lead to bitterness and a lack of charm. Mademoiselle Gamard’s physical appearance mirrored her inner turmoil—her features were sharp, her movements abrupt, and her demeanor ungracious. She had never married, which society often viewed as a failure, and this perception weighed heavily on her.

Birotteau, feeling the oppressive atmosphere, left the dining room, his heart heavy. He wandered the garden, only to find Mademoiselle Gamard and Troubert watching him, which intensified his discomfort. Distracted by their scrutiny, he sought refuge in church activities, momentarily forgetting his troubles. However, when he returned home, he discovered that he had missed dinner, as Mademoiselle Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting for him. This manipulation exemplified her malicious nature, as she took pleasure in undermining Birotteau’s comfort while maintaining an appearance of propriety.

Eight days after the events began, the dynamics between Abbe Birotteau and Mademoiselle Gamard revealed a plot that had been brewing for six months. Initially, Birotteau was oblivious to Gamard's malicious intentions, but after the incident with the candlestick and the clock, he recognized her animosity. This realization plunged him into despair, as he felt her presence suffocating him. Gamard, reveling in her vengeful emotions, meticulously executed her plan against the unsuspecting priest, who was too preoccupied with his trivial troubles to confide in friends.

Birotteau's life, once filled with mundane activities, turned into a living hell. His anxiety about confronting Gamard worsened, affecting his health. Noticing a physical decline, he decided to seek help from Abbe Troubert, hoping for intervention. When he met Troubert, he felt embarrassed discussing Gamard's provocations with someone so serious. Troubert listened coldly, suppressing a smile at Birotteau's plight, and suggested that the vicar should learn to understand Gamard better, attributing her behavior to her health issues.

Confused and desperate, Birotteau thought that leaving Gamard's house temporarily might ease her hostility. He planned to visit a country house owned by Madame de Listomere, unaware that this would only serve Gamard's interests. After ten days at the Alouette, Birotteau was approached by Monsieur Caron, Gamard's lawyer, who informed him that she was concerned about his extended absence. Birotteau, startled, insisted he had no intention of leaving, but Caron revealed that Gamard wanted clarity on his plans to avoid disputes.

Returning to the dining room, Birotteau shared his troubles with friends, who were intrigued by the drama. Madame de Listomere suggested that Troubert was after Birotteau's apartment. The group analyzed Gamard's motives, with an old landowner warning that Birotteau was only at the beginning of his troubles. Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix arrived with news that the vicar-general was ill and that Troubert was likely to be appointed in his place, which would give him significant power over appointments.

Madame de Listomere urged Birotteau to consider the canonry over his living situation with Gamard. The group rallied around him, suggesting he let Gamard and Troubert have their way, as it might lead to his advancement. However, the struggle soon escalated, and Birotteau's friends sent for Caron again to clarify the situation. Caron returned, asking Birotteau to sign a document relinquishing his residence, which he did under pressure from his friends.

Birotteau's despair deepened as he realized he had signed away his belongings, which Gamard claimed as compensation for the difference in board prices. He confronted Gamard, who coldly reminded him of the agreement. Devastated, Birotteau fled to Madame de Listomere's, where he found his belongings packed. Mademoiselle Salomon, concerned for his mental state, took him back to the Alouette, where he confided in friends about his misfortunes.

Monsieur de Bourbonne examined the deed Birotteau had signed and discovered its exploitative nature. He expressed outrage at Gamard's actions and suggested that Birotteau could pursue legal action. The group debated the merits of a lawsuit, with some advocating for a compromise to avoid scandal. They resolved to support Birotteau in his struggle against Gamard and Troubert, though Bourbonne cautioned that many would abandon him when the situation became dire.

Madame de Listomere's invitation to Birotteau to stay at her house sparked gossip and divided the town. Gamard, feeling slighted, sought to undermine Birotteau's reputation. Meanwhile, Birotteau's friends began to gather evidence for a lawsuit, which was complicated by the political climate and public sentiment against the clergy. The expert appraisal of Birotteau's belongings revealed their true value, suggesting that Gamard's claims were fraudulent.

As the legal battle unfolded, Birotteau's situation became increasingly precarious, with the town's gossip fueling the conflict. The stakes were high, and the outcome uncertain, as Birotteau faced the formidable alliance of Gamard and Troubert, who were determined to maintain their power and influence.

A few days after hostilities began between Birotteau and the old maid, Baron de Listomere, expecting a promotion to captain of a corvette, received a letter warning him of a possible retirement. Shocked, he rushed to Paris to speak with the minister of the Navy, who dismissed his concerns with laughter. However, further inquiries revealed a document confirming his impending retirement, pending the director's signature. 

Desperate, the baron sought help from his uncle, a deputy, who learned that the minister believed the baron had aligned himself with the Radicals in Tours and had offended the influential Abbe Troubert, the vicar-general. His uncle advised him to reconcile with Troubert, emphasizing the importance of maintaining good relations with the clergy for his career. The baron realized the gravity of the situation and resolved to avoid further conflict.

Three days later, the baron returned home and informed his aunt of the family's precarious position due to their support of Birotteau. He sought the counsel of Monsieur de Bourbonne, who advised them to retreat gracefully from their conflict with Troubert. The plan involved appeasing Troubert while distancing themselves from Birotteau, who was seen as a liability.

The baroness approached Birotteau, asking him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house. Birotteau, shocked and distressed, agreed, believing he was a burden. He requested the return of a portrait of Chapeloud, which belonged to him, and the baroness promised to negotiate with Gamard.

However, Mademoiselle Gamard soon fell ill, and the town buzzed with rumors that Birotteau's lawsuit had caused her distress. The baroness visited Gamard but was unable to see her. She then sought an audience with Troubert, where both parties engaged in a subtle battle of wits, each trying to outmaneuver the other while maintaining a facade of civility.

The baroness presented Birotteau's relinquishment document to Troubert, who feigned indifference but was secretly pleased. He later returned with Gamard's refusal to part with Chapeloud's portrait, which the baroness accepted, hoping to maintain peace. However, Gamard died shortly after, leaving her fortune to Troubert, which further complicated matters for the Listomeres.

At Gamard's funeral, Troubert delivered a grand eulogy, masking his true feelings about her. Birotteau, feeling guilty for her death, mourned in solitude. Meanwhile, the Listomeres navigated the fallout from Gamard's death and Troubert's growing power.

Five months later, Troubert was appointed Bishop of Troyes, while Madame de Listomere passed away, leaving an annuity to Birotteau. Troubert's influence continued to threaten the baron's career, and he manipulated the situation to his advantage, ensuring that Birotteau's legacy was contested.

In the end, Birotteau was exiled to a distant parish, suffering from illness and grief. Troubert's rise to power was marked by cunning and manipulation, reflecting the broader struggles within society and the Church. The narrative concludes with a reflection on the nature of power, individualism, and the moral complexities of human relationships, suggesting that the pursuit of personal ambition often comes at a significant cost to others.
