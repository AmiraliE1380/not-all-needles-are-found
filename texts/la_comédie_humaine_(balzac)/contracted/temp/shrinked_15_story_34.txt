MADAME FIRMIANI

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley


                              DEDICATION

                    To my dear Alexandre de Berny.
                           His old friend,

                             De Balzac.


MADAME FIRMIANI

Many tales, rich in incident or shaped by chance, carry their own setting and can be told without losing charm. But some human incidents demand the heart to give them life; certain delicate, almost anatomical touches cannot be reproduced except through an equally delicate rendering. Some portraits require the infusion of a soul; they mean nothing unless the subtlest expression of a speaking countenance is given. There are things we cannot say or do without the aid of secret harmonies produced by a day, an hour, a fortunate conjunction, or an inward tendency.

Such mysterious revelations are needed for this simple history, which should interest the grave and reflective who find sustenance in tender emotions. If the writer, like the surgeon beside his dying friend, feels a kind of reverence for his subject, should not the reader share that inexplicable feeling? Is it so difficult to be in unison with the vague, nervous sadness that casts gray tints about us—a semi-illness whose gentle sufferings are often pleasing? If the reader sometimes thinks of lost dear ones, if he is alone, if the day wanes or the night has come, let him read on; otherwise he should lay aside this book. If he has never buried a good old relative, infirm and poor, he will not understand these pages, which to some will seem redolent of musk, to others colorless and virtuous as Florian. The reader must have known the luxury of tears, felt the silent pangs of passing memory, the vision of a dear yet far-off shade—memories that bring regret for all earth has swallowed up, with smiles for vanished joys.

Believe that the writer would not, for the wealth of England, steal from poesy a single lie to embellish this narrative. The following is a true history, on which you may safely spend the treasures of your sensibility—if you have any.

In these days the French language has as many idioms and represents as many idiosyncrasies as there are varieties of men in the great family of France. It is curious to hear the different interpretations of the same thing by the various species that compose the genus Parisian—“Parisian” used merely to generalize our remark.

Ask a Practical man about Madame Firmiani and he will give an inventory: fine house in the rue du Bac, handsomely furnished salons, good pictures, a hundred thousand francs a year, husband formerly receiver-general of Montenotte. The Practical settles a person’s status by figures and acres.

The Lounger will answer that he goes to her parties; she receives Wednesdays; her house—meaning her salon—is one of the few where you can amuse yourself; the refreshments are exquisite; it is difficult to get admitted, therefore only the best society meets there. To the Lounger Madame Firmiani is almost an inn.

The Egotist despises her salons as dull and asks what use there is in a mind that frequents such places. He is the sort who would keep the universe under lock and key and permit no happiness except his own.

The Student praises her as an adorable woman who redeems Nature for all her ugly creations, and offers to secure a presentation—bold in public, timid in the boudoir.

The Fop, off to ride, summarily judges her a woman between thirty and thirty-five, faded, handsome eyes, flat figure, contralto voice worn, well dressed, a ruin still worth the trouble of a passion.

The Connoisseur speaks of her collection of pictures as something not to be missed and leaves you to his dealers. To him she is essentially a set of canvases.

A Woman, envious of other women, warns you not to visit her: dangerous, a siren; she dresses well and gives other women sleepless nights.

An Attache, with a habit of speaking in international phrases, recalls having seen her years ago in Rome or Antwerp; his manner is allusive, refined, and sometimes obscure.

The Distinguished adds that she sees much of the faubourg Saint-Germain and gives the “de” to everybody; she strives to do the “right thing” though her husband is a lawyer and she lives in the Marais.

The Duke does not know her—he recognizes only those presented at court and excuses himself as one of Napoleon’s creations.

The Ninny insists she must have sung at the Opera-house; such people prefer an answer to silence, however inaccurate.

Two old ladies, wives of former magistrates, discuss her maiden name. “She was a Cadignan,” say they—niece of the old Prince de Cadignan, cousin to the Duc de Maufrigneuse. Such a family name carries weight; a Cadignan might lack virtue, wealth, or youth, but the name survives as an active prejudice.

An Original assures you that no galoshes in her antechamber mean you can visit without compromise; if scoundrels attend, they are people of quality who come in carriages and therefore will not quarrel.

The Observer, who speaks like an oracle, describes Madame Firmiani as a beautiful woman sitting at ease by her fireplace, scarcely rising except for women, ambassadors, dukes, and great personages. She is gracious, charming in conversation, and shows indications of a passionate nature, though she has so many adorers she cannot have a favorite. If suspicion rested on two or three intimates, one might call one the “cavaliere servente,” but it does not. The lady is a mystery. She is married, though none of the world have seen her husband. Monsieur Firmiani is altogether mythical; he is like that third post-horse for which we pay though never behold it. Madame has, so say the judges, the finest contralto voice in Europe, though she has sung but two or three times since coming to Paris. She receives much company but goes nowhere.

The Observer’s words, anecdotes, and quotations pass for fact by those who pride themselves on social education; to contradict him is to risk his bitter slander. He is about forty, never dines at home, declares himself harmless to women, wears a maroon coat, reserves places at the Bouffons, and may possess a little estate in some unknown department.

The Contradictor will tell you, with certain emphasis, that Madame Firmiani was Murat’s former mistress or correct some other gossip; these men thrive on rectifying memoirs and dates, are sure of everything, talk loudly, and often have a Legion of Honor cross.

Finally, the Envious will scoff at the supposed income of a hundred thousand francs a year, call her merely a coquette who has ruined a young man and prevented his marriage, and insist that without her beauty she wouldn’t have a penny. Envy needs no fuller portrait; it endures with the perennial vigor of a vice that brings nothing in.

Thus a single name—Madame Firmiani—becomes many persons in Parisian mouths: a house, a salon, a collection of pictures, a siren, a mysterious contralto, a political or social rumor, a family badge. Each speaker translates the lady into his own idiom and prejudice, giving us a gallery of Parisian types as revealing as the lady herself.

Madame Firmiani’s identity, however, remains more than the sum of these versions. Between the gossip and the hints lies a woman who commands attention, not merely by fortune or name, but by a presence that sets tongues in motion and stirs readers of all sorts to pronounce her by their own measure. The many voices that describe her reveal less about her than about themselves—their social anxieties, ambitions, resentments, and vanities. In their utterances the reader sees Paris reflected: its practical calculations, its lounger’s pleasures, its student’s idealizing, its fop’s epigrams, its connoisseur’s tastes, its women’s jealousies, its attaché’s cosmopolitan gloss, its distinguished pretensions, its ducal exclusiveness, its silly affirmations, its elderly gossip’s reverence for lineage, its original’s oddities, the observer’s oracular certainties, the contradictor’s pedantry, and the envious man’s keen negation.

Madame Firmiani, then, is both a person and a social mirror. To understand her is to understand the society that speaks of her in such discordant keys. The anecdotes and conjectures may be manifold, but the woman they orbit exerts an inevitable fascination; she is a center of attention who never reveals all, producing that state of suspense in which Paris delights. What remains true is that the narrative which follows is founded on reality; the writer’s fidelity asks only a reader disposed to remember with tenderness what has been dear and is now gone.

In January 1824 so many differing opinions circulated about Madame Firmiani that cataloguing them would be tedious. Anyone who would not or could not visit her household could learn by rumor that she was widow or wife, silly or wise, virtuous or not, rich or poor, soulless or full of feeling, handsome or plain—there were as many Madame Firmianis as there are classes in society. Frightful reflection! We are like lithographic blocks from which different copies may be drawn; reputation depends on the balance critics strike between Truth that limps and Falsehood to which Parisian wit gives wings.

Madame Firmiani, like other noble women who make their hearts a sanctuary and disdain the world, was likely to be misjudged by Monsieur de Bourbonne, an old country magnate who had reason to think of her that winter. He belonged to the class of provincial planters—men living on estates, keeping close accounts, bargaining with peasants. Thus employed, a man becomes sagacious in spite of himself. The old gentleman had come to Paris from Touraine to satisfy his curiosity about Madame Firmiani. A man of honor and breeding, his sole heir was a nephew he greatly loved, in whose interests he planted his poplars. When a man thinks kindly about his heir while watching trees grow finer for his benefit, affection grows with every blow of the spade. Though not common, this feeling is to be met with in Touraine.

This cherished nephew, Octave de Camps, descended from the famous Abbé de Camps—well known to bibliophiles. Provincials have the habit of reprobating any young man who sells inherited estates. This antiquated prejudice interfered with the stock-jobbing the government encouraged. Without consulting his uncle, Octave had lately sold an estate to the Bande Noire, a mysterious association of speculators that bought landed estates, cut them up, and sold parcels to peasants. The château de Villaines would have been pulled down were it not for the old uncle’s remonstrances to the “Pickaxe company.” To increase the old man’s wrath, a distant relative—one of those penniless but astute cousins—had come to visit Monsieur de Bourbonne and casually told him of his nephew’s ruin.

“He has wasted his means on a certain Madame Firmiani,” the cousin said; Octave was now reduced to teaching mathematics for a living, awaiting his uncle’s death, not daring to let him know of his dissipations. This cousin, a kind of Charles Moor, was not ashamed to deliver his fatal news as the old gentleman sat by his fire after a provincial dinner.

But heirs cannot always rid themselves of uncles. The uncle refused to believe the story and, undisturbed, came to Paris unknown to Octave, resolved to make inquiries as to his nephew’s insolvency. Having many acquaintances in the faubourg Saint-Germain—among the Listomères, the Lenoncourts, and the Vandenesses—he heard so much gossip, so many facts and falsities about Madame Firmiani that he resolved to be presented to her under the name of de Rouxellay, that of his estate in Touraine. He chose an evening when he knew Octave would be engaged finishing a piece of work—this so-called lover of Madame Firmiani still went to her house; a circumstance difficult to explain. As to Octave’s ruin, that, unfortunately, was no fable, as Monsieur de Bourbonne at once discovered.

Monsieur de Rouxellay was not at all like the provincial uncle at the Gymnase. Formerly in the King’s guard, a man of the world and favorite among women, he knew how to present himself with the courteous manners of the olden time; he could make graceful speeches and understand the Charter. Though he loved the Bourbons with noble frankness, believed in God as a gentleman should, and read nothing but the Quotidienne, he was not as ridiculous as the liberals of his department would have him. He could hold his own in the court circle, provided no one talked to him of “Moses in Egypt,” nor of the drama, nor of railways. He had never got beyond Voltaire, Buffon, Payronnet, and the Chevalier Gluck.

“Madame,” he said to the Marquise de Listomère, on his arm as they entered Madame Firmiani’s salons, “if this woman is my nephew’s mistress, I pity him. How can she live in this luxury and know he is in a garret? Hasn’t she any soul? Octave is a fool to have given up such an estate as Villaines for a—”

Monsieur de Bourbonne belonged to the species Fossil and used the language of yore.

“But suppose he had lost it at play?”

“Then, madame, he would at least have had the pleasure of gambling.”

“And do you think he has had no pleasure here? See! look at Madame Firmiani.”

The old man’s brightest memories faded at the sight of his nephew’s so-called mistress. His anger died at the gracious exclamation that escaped him as he looked at her. By one of those fortunate accidents that happen only to pretty women, it was a moment when all her beauties shone with peculiar lustre—due perhaps to the wax-lights, the simplicity of her dress, and the atmosphere of elegance that surrounded her. One must have studied the transitions of an evening in a Parisian salon to appreciate the subtle lights and shades that color a woman’s face. There comes a moment when, pleased with herself and feeling queen of a salon full of remarkable men, the Parisian woman reaches a full consciousness of her grace; her beauty is enhanced by the looks she gathers—a mute homage she transfers with subtle glances to the man she loves. At such moments a woman is invested with supernatural power and becomes a magician, inspiriting the love she herself feels; her smiles and glances fascinate. If this condition can give attraction even to a plain woman, how much more does it invest a woman of natural elegance, distinguished bearing, fair, fresh, with sparkling eyes and tasteful dress.

Have you met a woman whose harmonious voice lends charm to her speech as her manners display themselves? One who knows how to speak and to be silent, whose words are happily chosen, whose language is pure, who concerns herself in your interests with delicacy? Her raillery is caressing, her criticism never wounds; she neither discourses nor argues, but likes to lead and stop a discussion at will. Her manner is affable and smiling, her politeness never forced, her readiness to serve never servile; she reduces the respect she claims to a soft shadow; she never wearies you, and you leave satisfied with her and with yourself. Her charm extends to all the things with which she surrounds herself. Everything about her pleases the eye; in her presence you breathe your native air. This woman is natural. There is no effort about her; she aims at no effect; her feelings are shown simply because they are true. Frank herself, she does not wound the vanity of others; she forgives defects and absurdities, comprehends all ages, and is vexed by nothing, having foreseen all. Tender and gay, she gratifies before she consoles. You love her so well that if this angel did wrong you would be ready to excuse her. If, for your happiness, you have met such a woman, you know Madame Firmiani.

After Monsieur de Bourbonne had talked with her ten minutes, sitting beside her, his nephew was forgiven. He perceived that whatever the actual truth might be, the relation between Madame Firmiani and Octave covered some mystery. Returning to the illusions that gild youth, and judging Madame Firmiani by her beauty, the old gentleman became convinced that a woman so innately conscious of her dignity as she appeared to be was incapable of a bad action. Her dark eyes told of inward peace; the lines of her face were so noble, the profile so pure, and the passion he had come to investigate seemed so little to oppress her heart, that he said to himself, while noting all the promises of love and virtue given by that adorable countenance, “My nephew is committing some folly.”

Madame Firmiani acknowledged to twenty-five. But the Practical people proved that having married the invisible Firmiani (then a highly respectable individual in the forties) in 1813, at sixteen, she must be at least twenty-eight in 1825. Yet the same persons also asserted that at no period had she ever been so desirable or so completely a woman. She was at an age when women are most prone to conceive passion. She possessed all that earth sells, lends, or gives. The Attaches declared there was nothing of which she was ignorant; the Contradictors said there was much she ought to learn; the Observers remarked her white hands, small feet, and movements a trifle too undulating. Nevertheless, everyone envied or disputed Octave’s happiness, agreeing, for once, that Madame Firmiani was the most aristocratically beautiful woman in Paris.

Still young, rich, a perfect musician, intelligent, witty, refined, and received by the Princesse de Blamont-Chauvry—loved by her rivals the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse (her cousin), the Marquise d’Espard, and Madame de Macumer—Madame Firmiani gratified all the vanities that feed or excite love. She was therefore sought by too many men not to fall victim to Parisian malice and its charming calumnies, whispered behind a fan or in a safe aside. It was necessary to contrast the true Firmiani with the Firmiani of society. If some women forgave her happiness, others did not forgive her propriety. Now nothing is so dangerous in Paris as unfounded suspicions—because it is impossible to destroy them.

This sketch of a woman so admirably natural gives only a faint idea of her. It would need the pencil of an Ingres to render the pride of that brow, the dignity of that glance, and the thoughts betrayed by the changing colors of her cheeks. In her were all things; poets could have found an Agnes Sorel and a Joan of Arc, also the woman unknown, the soul within that form—the soul of Eve, the knowledge of good and the riches of evil, error and resignation, crime and devotion, the Donna Julia and the Haïdee of Lord Byron.

The former guardsman stayed on after the other guests had left; Madame Firmiani found him in an armchair, determined to remain with a fly's pertinacity. The clock marked two in the morning.

“Madame,” said the old gentleman as she rose, “I am the uncle of Monsieur Octave de Camps.”

Madame Firmiani sat again, showing emotion. He could not tell whether she paled from shame or pleasure. Delicate women hide emotions; modesty betrays them. Monsieur de Bourbonne did not interpret her agitation so generously.

“Well, monsieur?” she asked with a clear glance that questioned more than it revealed.

“Well, madame,” returned the old man, “do you know what someone told me in my province? That my nephew had ruined himself for you, and that the poor fellow was living in a garret while you were in silk and gold. Forgive my rustic sincerity; it may be useful for you to know of these calumnies.”

“Stop, monsieur,” said Madame Firmiani, with an imperative gesture; “I know all that. You are too polite to continue this subject if I request you to leave it, and too gallant—not to agree that you have no right to question me. It would be ridiculous to defend myself. I trust you will hold a good opinion of my character and believe the profound contempt I feel for money. I was married, without any fortune, to a man of immense wealth. It is nothing to me whether your nephew is rich or poor; I received him because he is worthy of my friendship. My friends respect one another; I lack the philosophy to admit those I do not esteem; perhaps that seems uncharitable, but I abhor tattle and dishonesty.”

Her voice rose at first; she ended with the ease and self-possession of Celimene.

“Madame,” said Monsieur de Bourbonne, moved, “I am an old man; I am almost Octave’s father, and I ask your pardon most humbly for the question I shall now venture to put to you, giving you my word of honor that your answer shall die here,”—laying his hand on his heart—“Are these rumors true; do you love Octave?”

“Monsieur,” she replied, “to any other man I should answer that question by a look; but to you, and because you are almost the father of Monsieur de Camps, I reply by asking what you would think of a woman if to such a question she answered you? To avow our love for him we love, when he loves us—ah! that may be; but even when we are certain of being loved forever, believe me, monsieur, it is an effort for us, and a reward to him. To say to another—”

She did not finish, rose, bowed, and withdrew into her private apartments; the doors opened and closed with a language of their own to his ears.

“Ah! the mischief!” he thought; “what a woman! she is either a sly one or an angel”; and he took his hired coach, the horses stamping in the courtyard while the coachman slept on his box, cursing his tardy customer.

Next morning about eight Monsieur de Bourbonne mounted the stairs of a house in the rue de l’Observance where Octave de Camps lived. Octave was astonished to see his uncle. The door was unlocked, his lamp still burning; he had been up all night.

“You rascal!” said Monsieur de Bourbonne, sitting in the nearest chair; “since when is it fashion to laugh at uncles who have twenty-six thousand francs a year from solid acres to which we are the sole heir? In the olden time we stood in awe of such uncles. Come, speak up, what fault have you to find with me? Haven’t I been a proper uncle? Have I ever refused you money? When did I shut the door in your face on pretext that you had come to look after my health? Haven’t you had the most accommodating uncle in France? You write to me, or you don’t write—no matter, I live on pledged affection. I am making you the prettiest estate in Touraine, the envy of the department. I don’t intend to let you have it till the last moment, but that’s an excusable fancy, isn’t it? And what does monsieur himself do?—sells his property and lives like a lackey!”

“Uncle—”

“I’m not talking about uncles, I’m talking nephew. I have a right to your confidence. Come, confess at once; it is the easiest way; I know by experience. Have you been gambling? lost money at the Bourse? Say, ‘Uncle, I’m a wretch,’ and I’ll hug you. But if you tell me any lies greater than those I used to tell at your age I’ll sell my property, buy an annuity, and go back to the evil ways of my youth—if I can.”

“Uncle—”

“I saw your Madame Firmiani yesterday,” went on the old fellow, kissing the tips of his fingers. “She is charming. You have my consent and approbation, if that will do you any good. As to the sanction of the Church I suppose that’s useless—sacraments cost so much. Come, speak out, have you ruined yourself for her?”

“Yes, uncle.”

“Ha! the jade! I’d have wagered it. In my time the women of the court were cleverer at ruining a man than the courtesans of to-day; but this one—I recognized her!—it is a bit of the last century.”

“Uncle,” said Octave, with tender gravity, “you are mistaken. Madame Firmiani deserves your esteem, and all the adoration the world gives her.”

“Youth, youth! always the same!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne. “Well, go on; tell me the same old story. But remember my experience in gallantry is not of yesterday.”

“My dear kind uncle, here is a letter which will tell you nearly all,” said Octave, taking it from an elegant portfolio, her gift no doubt. “When you have read it I will tell you the rest, and you will then know a Madame Firmiani unknown to the world.”

“I haven’t my spectacles; read it aloud.”

Octave began:

“My beloved—”

“Hey, then you are still intimate with her?” interrupted his uncle.

“Why yes, of course.”

“You haven’t parted from her?”

“Parted!” repeated Octave, “we are married.”

“Heavens!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne, “then why do you live in a garret?”

“Let me go on.”

“True—I’m listening.”

Octave resumed, reading some passages with deep emotion.

  “‘My beloved Husband,—You ask me the reason of my sadness. Has it, then, passed from my soul to my face; or have you only guessed it?—but how could you fail to do so, one in heart as we are? I cannot deceive you; this may be a misfortune, for it is one of the conditions of happy love that a wife shall be gay and caressing. Perhaps I ought to deceive you, but I would not do it even if the happiness with which you have blessed and overpowered me depended on it.

  “‘Ah! dearest, how much gratitude there is in my love. I long to love you forever, without limit; yes, I desire to be forever proud of you. A woman’s glory is in the man she loves. Esteem, consideration, honor, must they not be his who receives our all? Well, my angel has fallen. Yes, dear, the tale you told me has tarnished my past joys. Since then I have felt myself humiliated in you,—you whom I thought the most honorable of men, as you are the most loving, the most tender. I must indeed have deep confidence in your heart, so young and pure, to make you this avowal which costs me much. Ah! my dear love, how is it that you, knowing your father had unjustly deprived others of their property, that YOU can keep it?

  “‘And you told me of this criminal act in a room filled with the mute witnesses of our love; and you are a gentleman, and you think yourself noble, and I am yours! I try to find excuses for you; I do find them in your youth and thoughtlessness. I know there is still something of the child about you. Perhaps you have never thought seriously of what fortune and integrity are. Oh! how your laugh wounded me. Reflect on that ruined family, always in distress; poor young girls who have reason to curse you daily; an old father saying each night: “We might not now be starving if that man’s father had been an honest man—”’”

“Good heavens!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne, interrupting, “surely you have not been such a fool as to tell that woman about your father’s affair with the Bourgneufs? Women know more about wasting a fortune than making one.”

“They know about integrity. But let me read on, uncle.”

  “‘Octave, no power on earth has authority to change the principles of honor. Look into your conscience and ask it by what name you are to call the action by which you hold your property.’”

The nephew looked at the uncle, who lowered his head.

  “‘I will not tell you all the thoughts that assail me; they can be reduced to one,—this is it: I cannot respect the man who, knowingly, is smirched for a sum of money, whatever the amount may be; five francs stolen at play or five times a hundred thousand gained by a legal trick are equally dishonoring. I will tell you all. I feel myself degraded by the very love which hitherto been all my joy. There rises in my soul a voice which my tenderness cannot stifle. Ah! I have wept to feel that I have more conscience than love. Were you to commit a crime I would hide you in my bosom from human justice, but my devotion could go no farther. Love, to a woman, means boundless confidence, united to a need of reverencing, of esteeming, the being to whom she belongs. I have never conceived of love otherwise than as a fire in which all noble feelings are purified still more,—a fire which develops them.

  “‘I have but one thing else to say: come to me poor, and my love shall be redoubled. If not, renounce it. Should I see you no more, I shall know what it means.

  “‘But I do not wish, understand me, that you should make restitution because I urge it. Consult your own conscience. An act of justice such as that ought not to be a sacrifice made to love. I am your wife and not your mistress, and it is less a question of pleasing me than of inspiring in my soul a true respect.

  “‘If I am mistaken, if you have ill-explained your father’s action, if, in short, you still think your right to the property equitable (oh! how I long to persuade myself that you are blameless), consider and decide by listening to the voice of your conscience; act wholly and solely from yourself. A man who loves a woman sincerely, as you love me, respects the sanctity of her trust in him too deeply to dishonor himself.

  “‘I blame myself now for what I have written; a word might have sufficed, and I have preached to you! Scold me; I wish to be scolded,—but not much, only a little. Dear, between us two the power is yours—you alone should perceive your own faults.’”

“Well, uncle?” said Octave, his eyes full of tears.

“There’s more in the letter; finish it.”

“Oh, the rest is only to be read by a lover,” answered Octave, smiling.

“Yes, right, my boy,” said the old man gently. “I have had many affairs in my day, but I beg you to believe that I too have loved, ‘et ego in Arcadia.’ But I don’t understand yet why you give lessons in mathematics.”

“My dear uncle, I am your nephew; isn’t that as good as saying I had dipped into the capital left me by my father? After I read this letter a revolution took place within me. I paid my whole arrearage of remorse in one day. I cannot describe the state I was in. As I drove in the Bois a voice called to me, ‘That horse is not yours’; when I ate my dinner it said, ‘You have stolen this food.’ I was ashamed. The fresher my honesty, the more intense it was. I rushed to Madame Firmiani. Uncle! that day I had pleasures of the heart, enjoyments of the soul far beyond millions. Together we made out the account of what was due to the Bourgneufs, and I condemned myself, against Madame Firmiani’s advice, to pay three per cent interest. But all I had did not suffice to cover the full amount. We were lovers enough for her to offer, and me to accept, her savings—”

“What! besides her other virtues does that adorable woman lay by money?” cried his uncle.

“Don’t laugh at her, uncle; her position obliged her to be careful. Her husband went to Greece in 1820 and died there three years later. It has been impossible thus far to get legal proof of his death, or obtain the will leaving his property to his wife. These papers were either lost or stolen during the troubles in Greece—a country where registers are not kept as in France, and where we have no consul. Uncertain whether she might be forced to give up her fortune, she has lived with the utmost prudence. As for me, I wish to acquire property that shall be mine, to provide for my wife if she loses hers.”

“But why didn’t you tell me all this? My dear nephew, you might have known I love you enough to pay all your good debts, the debts of a gentleman. I’ll play the traditional uncle now, and revenge myself!”

“Ah! uncle, I know your vengeance! but let me get rich by my own industry. If you want to do me a real service, make me an allowance of two or three thousand francs a year till I see my way to an enterprise for which I shall want capital. At this moment I am so happy that all I desire is the means of living. I give lessons so that I may not live at the cost of any one. If you only knew the happiness I had in making that restitution! I found the Bourgneufs, after a good deal of trouble, living miserably and in need of everything. The old father was a lottery agent; the two daughters kept his books and took care of the house; the mother was always ill. The daughters are charming, but they had been cruelly taught that the world thinks little of beauty without money. What a scene it was! I entered their house the accomplice in a crime; I left it an honest man who had purged his father’s memory. Uncle, I don’t judge him; there is such excitement, such passion in a lawsuit that even an honorable man may be led astray. Lawyers can make the most unjust claims legal; laws have convenient syllogisms to quiet consciences. My visit was a drama. To be Providence itself; actually to fulfill that futile wish, ‘If heaven were to send us twenty thousand francs a year’—that silly wish we all make laughing; to bring opulence to a family sitting by the light of one miserable lamp over a poor turf fire—no, words cannot describe it. My extreme justice seemed to them unjust. Well! if there is a Paradise my father is happy in it now. As for me, I am loved as no man was ever loved yet. Madame Firmiani gives me more than happiness; she has inspired me with a delicacy of feeling I lacked. So I call her my dear conscience—a love-word expressing secret harmonies within our hearts. I find honesty profitable; I shall get rich in time by myself. I’ve an industrial scheme in my head, and if it succeeds I shall earn millions.”

“Ah! my boy, you have your mother’s soul,” said the old man, his eyes filling at the thought of his sister.

Just then, in spite of the distance between Octave’s garret and the street, the young man heard the sound of a carriage.

“There she is!” he cried; “I know her horses by the way they are pulled up.”

A few moments more, and Madame Firmiani entered the room.

“Ah!” she exclaimed, with a gesture of annoyance at seeing Monsieur de Bourbonne. “But our uncle is not in the way,” she added quickly, smiling; “I came to humbly entreat my husband to accept my fortune. The Austrian Embassy has just sent me a document which proves the death of Monsieur Firmiani, also the will, which his valet was keeping safely to put into my hands. Octave, you can accept it all; you are richer than I, for you have treasures here”—laying her hand upon his heart—“to which none but God can add.” Then, unable to contain her happiness, she laid her head upon her husband’s breast.

“My dear niece,” said the old man, “in my day we made love; in yours, you love. You women are all that is best in humanity; you are not even guilty of your faults, for they come through us.”
