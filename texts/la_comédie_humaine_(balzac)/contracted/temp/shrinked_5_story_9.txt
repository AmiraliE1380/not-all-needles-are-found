The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration spirit, balancing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she maintains a virtuous facade to support his political ambitions. Some women reserve judgment until he becomes a peer, but the marquise’s conduct remains consistent with her principles.

The marquis is bland, notable for a lack of distinguishing qualities rather than vice. A silent deputy who votes correctly but lacks charisma, he is nicknamed “dull weather.” His unremarkable nature is seen as a safe match for a virtuous woman, since he is incapable of folly.

Despite her reserve, the marquise commands both respect and scorn from men who attempt to flirt; she responds with contempt. She converses with agreeable men without arousing gossip, unlike others who pursue ulterior motives. She does not play coy; she is genuinely principled.

The narrator, who admires her, describes her presence: neither plain nor conventionally pretty, she possesses a captivating air. Animated eyes reveal a deeper soul; she charms without seeking it. This quality surfaces in her recent encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man combining modesty with liveliness.

Rastignac, adept at social navigation, speaks with the marquise at a ball on matters from the opera to women’s duties. Their exchange shows mutual interest, though he abruptly leaves afterward. The next morning Rastignac finds himself preoccupied with thoughts of her.

Writing in a comfortable setting, he becomes increasingly absorbed in feelings for the marquise; his thoughts drift to the pleasures of romance before he is interrupted by his friend Horace, with whom he discusses various matters including a possible expedition to Morea.

Meanwhile the marquise receives a letter from Rastignac while dressing. It opens with affectionate terms, provoking curiosity. As she reads, her initial impulse to discard it turns to a desire to understand his sentiments. The letter’s conclusion leaves her fatigued; she reflects on Rastignac’s impertinence and the implications of his words.
