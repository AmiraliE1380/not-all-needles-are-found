The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration spirit, balancing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she preserves a virtuous façade to support her husband's political prospects. While some women await his elevation to peerage before judging him, the marquise's conduct is strictly principled.

The marquis is a bland figure, known more for faults than virtues. A silent deputy who votes correctly but lacks charisma, he has earned the nickname "dull weather." His unremarkable nature makes him a safe match for a virtuous woman, being incapable of folly.

Despite a reserved manner, the marquise elicits both respect and disdain from men who attempt to flirt with her, receiving only contempt in return. She converses with agreeable men without attracting gossip, unlike others with ulterior motives. She does not play coy; her behavior is genuinely principled.

The narrator, an admirer, sketches her appearance: neither plain nor conventionally pretty, she possesses a captivating presence. Her animated eyes reveal a deeper soul, and she exudes charm without pursuing it. This quality shows in her recent encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man combining modesty and vivacity.
