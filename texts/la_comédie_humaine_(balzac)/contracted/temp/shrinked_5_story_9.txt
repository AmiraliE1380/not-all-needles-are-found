The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration's blend of religious devotion and worldly elegance. Married seven years to an unremarkable marquis, she keeps a virtuous image to further his political aims. Nicknamed "Dull Weather," he provides a stable, comfortable home fitting her values.

Reserved yet commanding, she deflects flirtation to preserve her dignity. After dancing with Eugène de Rastignac—whose views on women intrigued her—she did not pursue him. The next morning, as Rastignac wrote letters about her, he was interrupted by his friend Horace.

The Marquise received a letter from Rastignac she meant to discard but burned, surprising her maid. At a party she awaited Rastignac and was disappointed when he did not appear. Four days later he realized he'd mistakenly sent a love letter intended for another to her. When he visited, their talk revealed tensions; Madame de Listomere openly voiced skepticism about his intentions. She soon withdrew from society, citing illness, while the marquis concealed her nervousness—underscoring the complexity of romantic misunderstanding.
