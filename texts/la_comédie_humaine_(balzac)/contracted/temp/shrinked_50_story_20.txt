Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman who married a wealthy Bordeaux heiress, became attached to her Gascon estate, the château de Lanstrac. He fled the Revolution for Martinique in 1790 and returned to find Lanstrac well managed by Mathias, a progressive notary’s clerk. After Madame de Manerville died in 1810 the old man grew miserly and deprived his only son, Paul, of means.

Paul returned from college in 1810 and endured three years of strict paternal control that stifled his character. Physically brave, he lacked the will to oppose his father and so became timid in society; confined by the mansion, he failed to connect with peers and felt out of place at his father’s royalist gatherings. To escape monotony he practiced fencing and riding and read novels.

His father’s death freed him: he inherited substantial, well-kept estates but retained an aversion to Bordeaux and Lanstrac. He invested in consols, traveled, served at embassies, and through indulgence and poor management depleted much of his fortune. Resolving to return as a country gentleman, he felt compelled to marry well, believing his foreign experience refined him. De Marsay warned marriage brings ridicule, responsibility, and loss of individuality; Paul insisted he sought emotional companionship and was ready for family duties, while de Marsay remained skeptical.

Despite earlier doubts, the Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux in winter 1821 intent on his plans. Restoring the family mansion and his Parisian manners won him a place in Bordeaux’s royalist society; an old marquise called him “the pink of fashion,” a jibe from liberals and a compliment among royalists.

Paul de Manerville enjoyed the attention. Charming, witty, attentive to women, with a touch of foppery and fastidious appearance, he preferred to receive happiness rather than bestow it. Young and averse to dull pursuits, he nonetheless felt provincial life demanded marriage and domestic variety—an inclination sharpened by a secret love for Mademoiselle Evangelista, Natalie.

Years earlier an affluent Spaniard, Evangelista, had settled in Bordeaux; his Creole wife, of an illustrious Spanish family, sustained their social standing with extravagant tastes funded by her devoted husband. After Evangelista’s death in 1813, Madame Evangelista, now a wealthy widow with a beautiful daughter, continued living lavishly even as her fortune waned. By Paul’s return Natalie was striking but unmarried, her prospects dampened by her mother’s extravagance and gossip that only a prince could afford her. Sheltered and accustomed to adoration, she knew little beyond privilege.

Six months after Paul’s arrival they met at a high-society event and were mutually charmed. Madame perceived his interest and hoped he would become her son-in-law; Paul pictured Natalie as his future wife, reassured by childhood knowledge of the Evangelista fortune and unconcerned by provincial warnings. Society soon treated him as her suitor; royalists deemed the match prudent. Madame, forty and still striking, saw Paul as a path to Paris, while Paul felt flattered.

A year later the courtship was assumed though neither pressed actively. Natalie’s reserved naivety intrigued Paul, who rationalized marriage would secure happiness and position. The narrative closes by noting that marriage negotiations—especially contracts—are pivotal in French society and will shape their future, foreshadowing forthcoming social and personal complexities.

In winter 1822 Paul de Manerville sought Natalie Evangelista’s hand through his great‑aunt, the Baronne de Maulincour. The baroness reported Natalie’s dowry would be her patrimony; her mother would contribute nothing, and advised Paul to consult notary Mathias. Paul visited Madame Evangelista, who admitted she owed Natalie a sum and could not pay; fearing he would withdraw she summoned her notary, Solonet, who promised to conceal the shortfall and arrange a favorable contract. Mathias and Solonet met—Mathias cautious, Solonet pragmatic. While Paul and Natalie shared moments, the notaries argued about selling Madame Evangelista’s house to cover debts. They compromised: Madame Evangelista would entail her property, securing Natalie and shielding Paul. Marriage could proceed, but tensions lingered, foreshadowing further conflicts of love, ambition and money.

Elie Magus, then in Bordeaux, answered Madame Evangelista’s summons expecting to discuss jewels for Comte Paul’s fiancée, Mademoiselle Natalie. Instead he valued Madame’s necklace and earrings at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” at 100,000, warning that selling them would mean a heavy loss.

That appraisal sparked staff rumors and society gossip that the marriage was unstable and Madame was selling her house. Notaries denied the claims, but public skepticism—fueled by envy of the Evangelistas’ wealth—persisted; Paul organizing a hunting party only heightened speculation. Madame de Gyas relayed the tales to Natalie and her mother, who dismissed them as self-interested.

To counter the rumors and flaunt her means, Madame planned the fête of the camellias to coincide with signing the marriage contract. As the day neared she worried the contract endangered Natalie’s fortune. At the signing—guests festive, Natalie radiant, Paul enamored—Madame learned the notaries’ warning that if Paul died first Natalie would inherit only a fraction. Determined to protect her daughter, she turned from careless spender into a shrewd strategist bent on reclaiming the family fortune and influencing Paul, setting up future conflict.

The fête had briefly distracted Paul from his anxieties, which returned that night. He feared Madame Evangelista might have deceived him without Mathias’s intervention, but comforted himself that in two days Natalie would be his and their finances settled.

Unbeknownst to him, his future had shifted. The most astute woman in his life had become an adversary, contemplating withdrawing her interests from their shared future. He failed to perceive the cunning of his Creole mother-in-law—part European intellect, part tropical passion—awakened by the prospect of losing her daughter. With a day until the marriage, Madame Evangelista realized she could shape the couple’s life: Natalie trusted her implicitly, and a single conversation might alter their fate.

Seated on Natalie’s bed the next morning, she recalled their harmony and warned that conjugal love can fade unless carefully managed. She urged Natalie to establish authority early, suggested her own presence could complicate the household, and proposed leaving to avoid conflict. She advised feigning obedience while subtly asserting dominance and outlined tactics: keep a social life, avoid constant proximity, preserve allure and independence, and keep Paul entertained.

Overwhelmed, Natalie accepted continued contact but agreed separation was necessary. When Paul arrived, Madame Evangelista offered to sell her jewelry for the couple; Paul, moved, accepted. The notary Solonet reported potential buyers for her property, and Natalie consented to the sale. Madame Evangelista resolved to step back from Parisian life and support them from afar. At the midnight wedding she played the grieving mother, extracted promises from Paul to secure her influence, and sent the newlyweds to Paris, where Natalie immediately began to assert her authority—setting the stage for a complex marriage.

Five years later, in November, Comte Paul de Manerville, cloaked and somber, arrived at his old friend Monsieur Mathias’s house in Bordeaux. The retired notary was absent; Paul waited in the late Madame Mathias’s room until evening. When Mathias returned, he showed concern and embarrassment over public notices of Paul’s ruin, blaming Paul’s marriage to Natalie Evangelista for the downfall while admitting relief that the estate titles were preserved even though the usufruct was lost.

Paul, altered but calm, confessed he had left Natalie without her knowledge and planned to sail to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune. He no longer considered himself a count and intended to go into commerce under another name. He explained enormous debts from lavish living and gifts to Natalie, the sale of assets, and heavy borrowing from usurers, which produced the catastrophe. Mathias doubted prospects—especially reliance on de Marsay—but the next day a bill of exchange from de Marsay confirmed some funds. Still, Paul remained determined, vowed to return wealthy, and boarded the ship.

At sea Paul, tormented by memories and seasickness, opened letters. Natalie begged to help, pledging her jewels and promising to economize and live at Lanstrac with her mother to pay his debts. De Marsay’s letter revealed the full extent of ruin and exposed Paul’s mother-in-law’s profiteering. Confronted by love, treachery, anger, and remorse, Paul sailed away uncertain of the future.
