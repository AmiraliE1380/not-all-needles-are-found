Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman gentleman, became a Gascon after marrying a wealthy heiress and selling his estate. He served as major of the Gate Guards until his death in 1813, having avoided the Revolution by going to Martinique. After his wife’s death in 1810, he grew miserly, limiting resources for his son, Paul.

Paul de Manerville returned from college in 1810, living under his father's strict control, which stunted his emotional growth. He felt trapped and struggled to socialize due to financial constraints. His father took him to royalist gatherings, where Paul felt out of place, turning to physical activities while his intellectual pursuits were limited to novels.

After his father's death, Paul inherited a fortune but was disillusioned with Bordeaux. He traveled Europe for six years, exhausting his capital before returning, believing his noble title would attract a partner. Now a count, he confided in de Marsay about his desire for companionship. 

In winter 1821, Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux, eager to restore his family mansion and gain favor among the elite. He recognized that marriage was essential for a fulfilling life, particularly with Mademoiselle Evangelista, a celebrated beauty. As their bond deepened, Paul idealized Natalie, overlooking potential flaws. In winter 1822, he sought to marry her, with his great-aunt warning him about Natalie's dowry and advising him to protect his interests in the marriage contract. Negotiations revealed stark differences between the notaries, hinting at future challenges.

The next day, jeweler Elie Magus appraised Madame Evangelista's diamonds for the marriage contract of Comte Paul and Mademoiselle Natalie, valuing them at 250,000 francs but offering only 75,000 for the “Discreto” diamond due to depreciation. This appraisal sparked rumors about the marriage's stability, leading to speculation that Madame Evangelista was selling her house and that the wedding might be canceled. Despite notaries denying these claims, public skepticism persisted, fueled by jealousy of the Evangelista family's wealth.

To counter the gossip, Madame Evangelista organized an extravagant "fete of the camellias" to showcase her wealth. As preparations unfolded, she grew anxious about the contract's implications for her daughter's fortune. The courtyard was transformed into a lavish tent, and as guests arrived, excitement mingled with tension. When the entailment clause was explained, Madame Evangelista realized it could financially ruin Natalie if Paul died first, making her feel trapped by her earlier manipulations.

As the contract was finalized, she confronted the notaries, expressing her frustration. Although Solonet suggested delaying the signing, she felt it would harm their reputation. Ultimately, she decided to proceed, vowing to protect her daughter's interests.

The signing marked a turning point, with Madame Evangelista determined to navigate her new reality. As festivities began, she felt a mix of triumph and resentment, aware that her daughter's happiness was tied to her ambitions. Meanwhile, Paul remained cautious, mindful of Mathias's warnings.

The next day, Madame Evangelista advised Natalie on managing her marriage, warning that love could fade if not nurtured. As the wedding approached, she proposed selling her jewelry to support Natalie, which Paul accepted, unaware of the tensions. 

Five years later, Comte Paul visited Monsieur Mathias in Bordeaux, revealing he had left Natalie to rebuild his fortune in Calcutta, citing massive debts from lavish spending. Mathias suggested living off Natalie's income, but Paul rejected this, believing women should not handle finances. 

At the quay, they faced gossip about Paul's downfall. As Paul prepared to board, Mathias signaled him with important letters, but Paul pocketed them unread. At sea, Paul reflected on his choices and, overcome by seasickness, spent days in his cabin. Eventually, he opened the letters: Natalie's anguish over his departure and a warning from de Marsay about a conspiracy against him. Confronted with these harsh realities, Paul felt anger and despair but resolved to confront his challenges and restore his honor.
