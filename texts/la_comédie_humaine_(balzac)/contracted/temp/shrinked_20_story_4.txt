In October 1800, a foreigner named Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries with his wife and young daughter. Over sixty and grave, he was deeply troubled; his wife tried to keep a calm face for their child. Passersby were drawn by their despair, but Bartolomeo’s fierce gaze discouraged sympathy.

After a moment’s thought he made a desperate decision: he handed a dagger to his wife and declared he would confront the Bonapartes. He approached the palace but was stopped by a soldier who demanded a written request. Bartolomeo persisted until Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, arrived, recognized him, and brought him inside to meet his brother, Napoleon.

Napoleon dismissed his aides to speak privately with Bartolomeo, who sought asylum and confessed he had killed the Portas—a family that had burned his vineyard and murdered his son. He explained that, in vengeance, he had slain all seven members of the Portas, a revelation that shocked Napoleon. Bartolomeo complained of the Bonaparte brothers’ earlier indifference and reminded them that he had once protected their family.

Now the leader of a nation, Napoleon felt bound by law and could not openly endorse private vengeance. Still, he acknowledged the Corsican tradition of vendetta and offered Bartolomeo a chance to live in Paris with financial assistance, warning that private justice would not be tolerated there.

Bartolomeo accepted, pledged loyalty to the Bonapartes, and appeared hopeful about his future in Paris. Afterward Lucien inquired into their finances and learned they had arrived with nothing. He gave them money and promised further help, noting that Bartolomeo’s Corsican property would not suffice to support them in the capital.

Fifteen years later, events stemming from Bartolomeo’s arrival in Paris would unfold—events that require the context of his earlier struggles and decisions.

Servin, a distinguished artist, was the first to open a studio for young girls who wished to paint. He taught about forty pupils and was married to a general’s dowerless daughter. At first mothers accompanied their daughters, but they soon trusted Servin’s principles and left them alone.

To avoid class complaints he accepted only young ladies from wealthy families and refused aspiring professional artists who would have demanded more thorough instruction. His carefulness and the quality of his teaching made him celebrated among the aristocracy; when a girl showed talent mothers recommended Servin without hesitation. He became synonymous with feminine art—Herbault for bonnets, Leroy for gowns—and, despite his ties to Parisian elites, maintained an independent, patriotic wit.

The studio itself was arranged with pupils in mind. The attic entrance above his apartment was sealed and reached by a small spiral staircase. The large room was lit through panes veiled with green linen shades; dark-gray walls were hung with caricatures and plaster models, a blend of ordered display and artistic clutter.

On a bright July day the studio buzzed with young girls at easels, each with distinct expressions and styles. Among them sat Ginevra Piombo, modestly dressed and apart, visibly anxious. The girls divided into two cliques—daughters of wealthy merchants and aristocrats—led by Amelie Thirion, vain and pretentious. Though they laughed and chatted as they worked, creating a lively atmosphere, jealousy simmered. Ginevra, most talented and beautiful, drew the aristocrats’ envy. To ostracize her, Amelie moved Ginevra’s easel away from the others, provoking whispers.

The political climate after the Bourbons’ second return sharpened these tensions. Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, faced contempt from royalist peers; Amelie’s slight was both personal and political. Yet Ginevra remained focused on her work, seemingly unaware of—or indifferent to—the growing hostility.

A mysterious sound behind a partition shifted attention. Ginevra discovered Servin was hiding a political fugitive, an officer of the Imperial Guard. The revelation brought excitement and concern for the man’s safety; Ginevra resolved to protect him, preferring to be near him rather than risk his exposure. Her calm indifference to Amelie’s insult only deepened the others’ bafflement and jealousy.

Madame Servin’s arrival interrupted the brewing conflict. She announced her husband could not attend and implied she knew of the hidden man. Sensing the tension, Ginevra began quietly humming a Corsican song to mask the officer’s breathing.

Throughout the session Ginevra stayed absorbed, sketching the officer’s head with a fervor that surprised even her. Servin praised her talent; the other girls, curious, were drawn to her sheet. Ginevra deftly deflected attention from the drawing, protecting the secret. Amelie, suspicious, tried to uncover the mystery but was foiled by Ginevra’s quickness. Their rivalry intensified: Ginevra’s talent and composure only heightened Amelie’s envy.

Servin, aware of the undercurrent, engaged each pupil but kept an eye on Ginevra. Their tacit understanding deepened a quiet bond, and Ginevra felt empowered to help the fugitive. As the day ended her resolve to keep the secret and aid the officer’s escape hardened. Outside, political turmoil loomed; inside, the girls navigated rivalries and friendships, united by their art.

When the painter and Ginevra believed they were alone, Servin knocked on the garret door, revealing a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, his arm in a sling and his face pale with pain. He recoiled at seeing Ginevra, but Servin reassured him, introducing her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo. Recognizing her patriotism, the soldier relaxed.

"You are wounded," Ginevra observed.

"It's nothing, mademoiselle," he replied. "The wound is healing."

Cries in the street announced a death sentence: "Condemned to death!" The officer turned pale at the name "Labedoyere," collapsing onto a stool. In despair he lamented that while Labedoyere faced death for their cause, he was hiding.

Ginevra blocked his path as he tried to leave. "Can you restore the Emperor?" she asked. "Do you expect to raise that giant who could not maintain himself?"

"What can I do?" he replied, feeling alone after losing his protector. He said he was willing to die, that an honest man's life was worth more than two traitors', and even contemplated selling his life to the executioner.

Alarmed, Ginevra offered her savings, explaining that her father was wealthy and would not blame her for helping a soldier. She urged him to wait until the storm passed, suggesting he could escape and serve elsewhere.

Her words, filled with maternal grace and hope, touched him. He admired her beauty and felt a rush of emotion. "You are an angel of goodness," he said, then his thoughts returned to Labedoyere.

In silence they shared a moment of understanding, their acquaintance deepening into a bond. Servin asked if he could save Labedoyere; the officer said he could only avenge him. Ginevra felt a surge at the thought of vengeance and recognized the officer's devotion to Napoleon.

As the sun set on the officer's face, Ginevra felt a magical connection, recalling childhood memories. Captivated by his presence, she smiled at him as she left, igniting a spark of hope.

The next day Ginevra returned to the studio. The officer, now called Luigi, told stories of the retreat from Moscow and the disaster at Waterloo. Ginevra listened, her admiration growing. They spoke Corsican, which deepened their bond.

Days passed and Ginevra's classmates noticed her absence from social gatherings, blaming her relationship with Luigi. Mademoiselle Thirion, worried by the rumors, withdrew from class, and attendance declined. Ginevra, lost in thoughts of Luigi, remained oblivious.

One day Laure, a fellow student, confided that her mother insisted she leave the studio because of the gossip around Ginevra and Luigi. Shocked, Ginevra reassured Laure they would meet again. Laure, sad to go, left, and Ginevra felt a pang of loss.

As Ginevra and Luigi's bond deepened, they shared unspoken love, each recognizing the other's worth. One evening Luigi signaled to Ginevra but, seeing Laure, retreated. Unaware of tension, Ginevra spoke with Laure and learned the others had left because of scandal.

Ginevra confronted Servin, revealing the young ladies had spread falsehoods about her relationship with Luigi. Servin dismissed the rumors, focused on his exhibition. Luigi entered, jokingly claiming responsibility for Servin's lost students; the painter playfully suggested marriage.

Their silence spoke volumes—the first acknowledgment of their love. Servin encouraged their union, celebrating the joy of love. Ginevra proposed to help Luigi secure a pardon, confident in her father's influence.

Meanwhile Ginevra's parents grew anxious about her late returns. Bartolomeo paced, concerned for his daughter. Though used to her late hours, he and his wife felt unease. Bartolomeo, a man of integrity who prioritized his family's well-being over appearances, reflected on their close-knit dynamic built on love and mutual respect. Ginevra's upbringing had instilled loyalty and passion, shared with her father and their political commitment to the Emperor.

When Ginevra finally arrived home, her father embraced her, relieved. The family shared a warm moment; their love was evident. Ginevra's heart swelled with affection for her parents, knowing they were her greatest supporters.

In the studio, Ginevra and Luigi's relationship blossomed amid challenges. Their love, forged in adversity, became a source of strength, uniting them against the world. As they navigated complexities, they remained steadfast in their commitment to one another, embodying the resilience of their shared ideals.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!” Ginevra exclaimed, but he set her down gently. She reassured her mother that she had only pretended to escape. The baroness recovered; Piombo, rubbing his hands in old-court fashion, looked pleased. The two elderly parents seemed revived.

“Now, to dinner!” Piombo called, taking Ginevra’s hand. “Your mother says you spend more time at the studio. Painting appears more important than us,” he teased.

“Oh, father!” she protested.

“Ginevra is preparing a surprise for us,” her mother suggested.

“A picture of your own! Will you show us?” Piombo asked.

“Yes, I’m busy at the studio,” Ginevra answered slowly. Her mother fretted. “You look pale,” she said.

“No! It shall never be said that Ginevra Piombo acted a lie,” Ginevra declared. Her parents looked astonished. “I love a young man,” she confessed, lowering her eyes.

“Is he a prince?” Piombo asked ironically, making his wife tremble.

“No, father, he is a young man without fortune,” she replied gently.

“Is he handsome?” he pressed.

“He is very unfortunate,” she said.

“What is he?” Piombo persisted.

“Labedoyere’s comrade; he was proscribed and hidden by Servin,” she explained.

“Servin is a good fellow,” Piombo acknowledged, “but you wrong to love any man but your father.”

“It does not depend on me,” Ginevra replied softly.

“I thought my Ginevra would remain faithful to me until I die,” Piombo lamented. “I did not expect our love to find a rival.”

“Did I ever reproach you for your devotion to Napoleon?” she countered. “You left me for months on missions. I endured your absence. Life has necessities.”

“You don’t love me for myself; your reproaches show your egotism,” she said.

“Dare you blame your father’s love?” he shouted.

“Father, I don’t blame you,” she answered gently. “You have grounds for your egotism, as I have for my love. I have never left your side for fifteen years; it has been my pleasure to charm your life. Is it ingratitude to desire a husband who will protect me?”

“What! You reckon benefits with your father?” Piombo asked dangerously. A dreadful silence followed. Bartolomeo cried out, “Oh! stay with us! I cannot bear to have you love another man. Ginevra, you will not have long to await your liberty.”

“But, father, I need not leave you; we will both love you. You will come to know the man to whom you bequeath me,” she said.

“Oh! Ginevra, why did you not marry when Napoleon offered?” Piombo lamented.

“They loved me to order,” she replied. “I did not wish to leave you alone.”

“You don’t wish to leave me alone, yet you marry! I know you, my daughter; you would cease to love us,” he said.

Elisa stood motionless. “We have no longer a daughter; she wishes to marry,” Piombo declared, sinking, hands raised to heaven. Ginevra, expecting a violent outburst, met instead a sudden gentleness.

“Father,” she said tenderly, “you shall never be abandoned by your Ginevra. Love me for my own sake. If you knew how he loves me! He would never make me unhappy!”

“Comparisons already!” Piombo shouted. “I cannot endure the thought of your marriage. If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me; if he does not love you, I would kill him.”

Ginevra met his gaze calmly. “Oh! to love you! What man is worthy of such a life?” he continued. “Who is worthy to be your husband?”

“He,” Ginevra replied, “he of whom I am not worthy.”

“How can he know you enough to love you?” Piombo demanded.

“Father,” she said impatiently, “whether he loves me or not, if I love him—”

“You love him?” he interrupted. She nodded.

“You love him more than you love us?” he pressed.

“The two feelings cannot be compared,” she replied.

“Is one stronger?” he asked.

“I think it is,” she admitted.

“You shall not marry him,” Piombo declared, voice shaking the window panes.

“I shall marry him,” Ginevra replied calmly.

“Oh, God!” cried her mother. Piombo, seated, wore an icy sternness. He looked at Ginevra and pleaded, “No! You will not marry him. Say nothing more tonight—let me think otherwise. Do you wish to see your father on his knees, his white hairs prostrate before you? I supplicate you—”

“Ginevra Piombo does not break her word,” she answered. “I am your daughter.”

“She is right,” the baroness said. “We are sent into the world to marry.”

“Refusing to obey an unjust order is not disobedience,” Ginevra asserted.

“No order can be unjust from your father’s lips,” he countered. “Why do you judge my actions? My repugnance is counsel from on high, perhaps to protect you from great evil.”

“The only evil would be if he did not love me,” she replied.

“Always he!” Piombo exclaimed.

“Yes, always,” she affirmed. “He is my life, my good, my thought. Even if I obeyed you, he would remain in my soul. To forbid me to marry him is to make me hate you.”

“You love us not!” Piombo shouted.

“Oh!” Ginevra shook her head.

“Then forget him; be faithful to us. After we are gone—you understand?”

“Father, do you wish me to long for your death?” she asked.

“I shall outlive you. Children who do not honor their parents die early,” he warned.

“All the more reason I should marry and be happy,” she replied. This cool reasoning troubled Piombo; Ginevra shuddered and sprang onto his knee, embracing him. “Oh, yes, yes, let me die first! I could never survive you, my father!” she cried.

“Oh! my Ginevra, my own Ginevra!” Piombo answered, his anger melting.

“It was time you ceased,” the baroness said, trembling.

“Poor mother!” Ginevra said.

“Ah! Ginevretta! mia bella Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed, playing with her as if she were a child. They had moments of joy, but Ginevra’s attempts to introduce Luigi met playful refusals from her father.

The next day she was more affectionate, thanking her father as if for consent. That evening she sang and played, saying they needed a man’s voice for the nocturne. A week later, her mother signaled her to come closer. “I have persuaded your father to receive him,” Elisa whispered.

“Oh! mother, how happy you have made me!” Ginevra replied. That day she returned home on Luigi’s arm. The officer emerged from hiding for the second time: Ginevra had appealed to the Duc de Feltre; Luigi’s name was restored on the officer’s list. Warned of her father’s potential disapproval, Luigi was anxious to meet him; Ginevra sensed his fear, which deepened her love.

“How pale you are!” she remarked.

“Oh! Ginevra, if it concerned my life only—” he began.

Though Bartolomeo had been told of the formal introduction, he remained seated, stern and intimidating.

“Father,” Ginevra said, “I bring you someone you will be pleased to see—a soldier who fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean.”

Piombo rose, casting a sidelong glance. “Monsieur is not decorated,” he remarked sarcastically.

“I no longer wear the Legion of Honor,” Luigi replied timidly.

Embarrassed by her father’s rudeness, Ginevra pulled out a chair for Luigi. Madame Piombo, noting her husband’s softening, tried to be cordial. “Monsieur’s resemblance to someone we knew in Corsica, Nina Porta, is surprising,” she observed.

“Nothing could be more natural,” Luigi replied. “Nina was my sister.”

“Are you Luigi Porta?” Piombo asked.

“Yes,” Luigi confirmed. Bartolomeo staggered as if struck; he needed support from his wife. The two old parents left the room in silence, abandoning their daughter in horror.

Luigi, bewildered, looked at Ginevra, who had turned pale and stared toward the door through which her parents had exited. The solemnity of the moment filled her with dread. “What misery in a word!” she exclaimed.

“In the name of our love, what have I said?” Luigi asked.

“My father never spoke of our tragic history, and I was too young to know,” Ginevra explained.

“Are we in vendetta?” Luigi asked, trembling.

“Yes. My mother said the Portas killed my brother and burned our house. My father then massacred their family. How did you survive? You were tied to the bed before they set fire to the house.”

“I do not know,” Luigi replied. “I was taken to Genoa at six and raised by an old man named Colonna. I was told I had enemies and advised to take Luigi as my surname to evade them.”

“Go, go, Luigi!” Ginevra urged. “Stay here; you are safe in my father’s house, but once you leave, danger awaits! My father has two Corsicans in his service, and they will hunt you.”

“Ginevra,” he said, “does this vendetta exist between us?”

She smiled sadly and nodded. “Oh, Luigi, our love must be pure and sincere to give me strength for the path ahead. But it promises happiness for our lives, does it not?”

Luigi squeezed her hand. Ginevra realized true love transcends mere words; their destiny was sealed. She foresaw a difficult struggle but felt no inclination to abandon him. With resolve, she helped him reach his modest lodging.

Returning home, Ginevra felt a calm resolve. She greeted her parents, who were preparing dinner, with gentle eyes, noticing her mother’s tear-stained face. They rose from the table in silence, unable to speak. In the gloomy salon, Piombo tried to speak but faltered; he rang for a servant. “Pietro, light the fire; I am cold,” he ordered.

Ginevra trembled, watching her father’s internal battle. Both suffered in silence, nursing wounded pride and anger. The love that had once brought them joy now felt like a burden. Ginevra’s gaze reproached her father, while Piombo’s reflected deep pain.

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother, desperate to mend the rift, gathered them in Bartolomeo’s study. Ginevra sensed her mother’s intentions and smiled sadly. At that moment two notaries arrived with witnesses. Bartolomeo eyed them warily. The older notary introduced himself and began the civil formalities for her marriage.

“Monsieur le baron, we have come to fulfill the law regarding your daughter’s marriage,” he stated.

Piombo’s anger flared. “Are there laws in France that destroy paternal authority?” he demanded. “Which tear a daughter from her father? Which deprive an old man of his last consolation?” He interrupted the notary’s calm explanations with cry after cry.

The notary’s composure only inflamed Piombo. In a sudden movement, he seized a dagger from the wall and rushed toward Ginevra. Witnesses intervened; he pushed them aside, fury raw. Seeing him approach, Ginevra knelt before him. “No, no! I cannot!” he cried, throwing the dagger away. “Have mercy! Give me Luigi! I ask for your consent upon my knees,” she pleaded, voice trembling.

Bartolomeo pushed her away. “Go. The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here,” he said, pressing his heart. “Leave my house, unhappy girl,” he added, ushering her out.

“Luigi!” Ginevra cried, rushing to his humble abode. “My Luigi, we have no fortune but our love.”

“Then I am richer than kings!” he exclaimed.

“My father and mother have cast me off,” she said sadly.

“I will love you in their place,” he promised.

“Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!” she declared, voice full of mixed joy and dread.

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father's house, she sought refuge with Madame Servin until her marriage to Luigi. Madame Servin received her coldly, resentful of the trouble Ginevra had caused her husband. Proud yet shocked by this selfishness, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her and painted a bright future that lifted her spirits despite her father's harshness.

One morning she received trunks of essentials from her mother, who included a letter urging her to reconsider the marriage. The letter, full of love and sorrow, deeply moved Ginevra. Just as she felt compelled to return home, Luigi arrived and her resolve to abandon him faded. She chose to bury her grief for the sake of their love.

On the wedding day Ginevra was alone; Luigi brought ordinary witnesses to sign the marriage certificate. Their plain presence contrasted with the usual joy of a wedding, and Ginevra's beauty silenced them, creating a somber mood. As they walked to the mayor's office, they passed other, more festive wedding parties, highlighting the difference in their situation.

Inside, they sat together while the festive atmosphere around them made their own union feel overshadowed. The mayor's clerk noted the absence of Ginevra's parents, and when the mayor inquired, the clerk confirmed their protest. The bureaucratic formality felt oppressive, a reminder of family disapproval; the ceremony seemed reduced to a legal act.

After signing the registers, Ginevra and Luigi left the office with mixed relief and sadness. Luigi expressed his gratitude, and Ginevra felt a measure of happiness despite the circumstances. They hurried to the church, where an old priest married them in a modest chapel amid grander weddings. The priest's remarks about their parents' absence stung, but a sergeant's blessing comforted Ginevra.

Once married they moved into a modest apartment. Luigi embraced Ginevra, calling this their true wedding. They explored the space, which Luigi had provisioned thoughtfully with artistic supplies for Ginevra. She was delighted by these details, and they reveled in their shared home, feeling they had found paradise despite limitations.

In the early days they indulged in their love, often losing themselves in each other. Reality soon set in and they recognized the need to work. Ginevra began copying paintings and gained clients, while Luigi, after initial struggles, used his handwriting skills to offer copying services. His efforts paid off; he eventually had enough work to hire assistants, providing a stable income for both.

This period marked the happiest time of their lives, filled with love and labor. Even though Ginevra sometimes felt pangs of remorse for the family happiness she had sacrificed, they lived contentedly. To mark an anniversary, Ginevra painted a portrait of herself for Luigi, capturing the essence of their love. Another year passed in quiet, simple happiness.

At the start of 1819, picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce original works instead of copies as competition grew. Madame Porta lamented not taking up genre painting earlier, which might have improved her standing. Ginevra experimented with portraiture but faced stiff competition from artists in greater need. Though she and Luigi had modest savings, they grew anxious about their future.

Through the winter Luigi labored relentlessly against a shrinking market for writing. Unable to afford help, he worked longer hours for the same pay. Ginevra painted several commendable works, but dealers avoided lesser-known artists; low prices failed to sell her paintings.

Their household deteriorated: love and happiness were overshadowed by looming poverty. They concealed worries from each other—Ginevra lavishing affection while Luigi hid his fears—clinging to each other and the hope that love would sustain them. One night Ginevra woke to find Luigi gone and discovered him working late to avoid burdening her. Moved, she began coloring lithographs through the night while Luigi toiled in his garret; both pretended to sleep to shield the other from anxiety.

On another evening, exhausted and feverish, Luigi opened his window and saw Ginevra's lamp across the courtyard, realizing she too was working. When he confronted her, she said she could not be idle while he suffered. They shared a tender moment, watching dawn together and feeling briefly hopeful.

Their struggles deepened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. The joy of parenthood briefly masked their financial burdens, but soon they were selling possessions and jewels. Poverty stripped them of comfort and pride, escalating their desperation. Months later Ginevra, frail and worn, struggled to care for her sickly child. Luigi, feeling helpless, sought aid but found none; he sold Ginevra's paintings and remaining furniture for a meager sum that only prolonged their hardship. Ginevra showed remarkable strength, managing household duties and caring for the boy while often starving herself to provide for Luigi.

One evening she offered Luigi her last piece of bread and urged him to eat. Realizing her sacrifice, he was consumed by despair and left to find help. In desperation he decided to sell himself as a military substitute, hoping the money would save Ginevra and their child. After securing an advance, he hurried home, only to find Ginevra asleep with their child. When he roused her, she smiled, then saw Bartolomeo cold and lifeless. Luigi, horrified, sought aid, but it was too late. In delirium, Ginevra spoke her love for Luigi and sorrow over their lost child, then died in his arms, leaving him devastated.

As Luigi mourned, Ginevra's parents sat at home reflecting on their daughter, whom they had not seen in years. Memories surged as they noticed the empty chair that had once belonged to her; Elisa Piombo spoke of hoping to rekindle Bartolomeo's love for Ginevra. Just then a man arrived with news of the tragedy. The old couple was shattered, realizing their daughter was gone; Bartolomeo’s death and Ginevra’s loss left only memories and grief.
