In October 1800 Bartolomeo di Piombo, a distraught foreigner in his sixties, stood outside the Tuileries with his wife and daughter, handed his dagger to his wife, and declared he would confront the Bonapartes. A soldier barred him for lacking a written request until Lucien Bonaparte recognized him and brought him to Napoleon. Napoleon dismissed his aides and listened as Bartolomeo sought asylum, admitting he had killed the Portas—the family that burned his vineyard and murdered his son. He confessed to avenging his son by killing all seven Portas, shocking Napoleon. Bitter at their apparent indifference, Bartolomeo reminded them he had protected the Bonapartes’ family.

Unable to condone private vengeance, Napoleon acknowledged the Corsican vendetta tradition, offered lodging in Paris and financial aid, but warned the city would not tolerate private justice. Bartolomeo accepted and pledged loyalty. Lucien found they had arrived with nothing, gave them money and promised help, noting their Corsican property could not sustain them in Paris. Fifteen years later, events would make Bartolomeo’s choices significant.

Servin, a distinguished artist of about forty, was the first to open a studio specifically for young girls who wished to paint. Morally strict and devoted to art, he was married to a general’s daughter who brought no dowry. At first mothers stayed with their daughters; reassured by Servin’s principles they soon left. To avoid social disputes and because proper training required rigor, he limited pupils to young ladies from wealthy families and refused those intent on becoming professional artists. His careful method won him a stellar reputation among the aristocracy: Servin became to feminine art what Herbault was to bonnets and Leroy to gowns; any mother who suspected talent in her daughter recommended him.

The studio—an attic above his apartment reached by a small spiral stair and sealed from below—was arranged with care: a large, light-filled room with big panes shaded by green linen, dark-gray walls hung with caricatures and plaster models, a blend of disciplined disorder and harmony.

On a bright July day the attic hummed with girls at their easels, each absorbed in different styles. Two cliques had formed: daughters of wealthy merchants and aristocrats led by the vain, pretentious Amelie Thirion, and the rest. Among them sat Ginevra Piombo, modestly dressed and anxious. Her talent and beauty provoked the aristocrats’ jealousy; Amelie, intent on ostracizing her, pushed Ginevra’s easel amid whispers. The Bourbons’ second return had sharpened political divides: Ginevra was a devoted Bonapartist and drew disdain from royalist families.

A faint sound behind a partition revealed Servin was sheltering a fugitive, an officer of the Imperial Guard. Ginevra chose proximity to protect him rather than risk exposure. Madame Servin’s arrival—hinting her husband’s absence but seeming aware of the hidden man—heightened the danger. To mask the officer’s breathing, Ginevra quietly hummed a Corsican song while sketching his head with surprising fervor. Servin praised her; the likeness drew the other girls, inflaming Amelie’s envy when her probing failed. The shared awareness of the fugitive forged a tacit bond: Ginevra helped protect the officer and resolved to aid his escape. Outside, political turmoil loomed; inside, rivalries and friendships negotiated themselves around their art.

When the painter Servin and Ginevra believed themselves alone, Servin admitted a young Imperial officer with his arm in a sling. He recoiled at Ginevra until Servin soothed him, introducing her as the daughter of the Emperor’s loyal friend, the Baron di Piombo; recognizing her patriotism, the soldier relaxed.

Ginevra noticed his wound. He brushed it aside, but shouts from the street announced a death sentence: “Condemned to death!” At the name “Labedoyere” the officer went pale and collapsed. Despairing that Labedoyere faced execution for their cause while he was in hiding, he lamented his helplessness and declared he was willing to die, convinced an honest life might outweigh traitors’ lives.

Alarmed, Ginevra offered her savings, saying her father was wealthy and would not blame her for aiding a soldier. She urged him to wait out the danger, suggesting escape and service elsewhere. Her calm compassion moved him; he admired her and called her “an angel of goodness,” though his thoughts kept returning to Labedoyere. In silence a bond formed: acquaintance deepened into understanding. When Servin asked if the officer could save Labedoyere, the officer said he could only avenge him. Ginevra felt a surge at the idea of vengeance and his devotion to Napoleon.

As the sun set, illuminating his face, Ginevra felt a near-magical connection, recalling childhood memories and leaving smiling, filled with hope.

The next day the officer—now called Luigi—shared stories of the retreats from Moscow and Waterloo. They spoke Corsican, which deepened their closeness. Ginevra listened, admiration growing.

Days passed and Ginevra’s absence from social gatherings spurred rumors linking her to Luigi. Mademoiselle Thirion withdrew from the class; attendance declined. Ginevra, absorbed with Luigi, remained oblivious. Laure, a fellow student, confided that her mother insisted she leave the studio because of the scandal; Ginevra reassured Laure they would meet again. Laure left sad, and Ginevra felt the loss.

Their bond grew into unspoken love. One evening Luigi signaled to Ginevra but retreated on seeing Laure. Ginevra confronted Servin, who dismissed the gossip and focused on his exhibition. Luigi entered, jokingly claiming responsibility for the missing students; Servin suggested they marry. Their silence acknowledged their love. Servin encouraged the union. Ginevra proposed helping Luigi obtain a pardon, confident in her father’s influence.

Her parents grew anxious about her late returns; Bartolomeo, relieved when she arrived, embraced her. In the studio, amid challenges, Ginevra and Luigi’s love, forged in adversity, became a source of strength that united them and embodied their shared ideals.

“Here she is, my Ginevra… mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old baron, embracing his daughter. Ginevra protested that he hurt her and laughed to soften the moment. The baroness revived; Piombo rubbed his hands with courtly satisfaction and the parents seemed renewed.

At dinner Piombo teased Ginevra about her studio hours and playfully resented her painting. The mood shifted when Ginevra, eyes downcast, confessed she loved a young man. Her parents were stunned: he was no prince but a man without fortune, a comrade of Labedoyere who had been proscribed and hidden by Servin. Piombo, offended, declared she should love only her father; Ginevra answered that love was not a matter of will.

Piombo insisted on his right to expect her fidelity; she reminded him she had never reproached his devotion to Napoleon despite long absences and said she wanted a husband to protect her. Accusations followed—Piombo’s possessive pride versus Ginevra’s plea for the right to choose. She promised she would not break her word: she loved this man truly. The baroness tried to soften things by observing daughters were meant to marry, but Piombo could not accept yielding to what he called an unjust order.

Tension escalated into threats. Piombo raged that if this man loved Ginevra he would kill him; if not, Piombo himself would. Ginevra maintained one cannot compare filial and romantic love; she felt both but admitted the latter might be stronger. He thundered he would not allow the marriage; she calmly replied she would marry him. The baroness entreated peace. Shaken, Piombo asked to think. Ginevra implored him to love her for herself and promised never to abandon him. Their tenderness cooled his anger; he forgave, and they returned to a playful intimacy reminiscent of earlier days.

In the following days Ginevra was more affectionate, as if thanking her parents for a reluctant consent. She sang, saying their nocturnes needed a man’s voice. A week later her mother signaled triumph: she had persuaded Piombo to receive the suitor. That day Ginevra arrived at home on Luigi’s arm.

Luigi, an officer who had reappeared after hiding, had been restored to the officer’s list thanks to Ginevra’s appeals to the Duc de Feltre. Warned of Piombo’s possible disapproval, Luigi was anxious; his pallor deepened Ginevra’s love. Inside, Piombo sat cold and stern. Ginevra presented Luigi as a soldier who had fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean. Piombo inspected him and sarcastically noted he wore no decoration. Luigi said simply he no longer bore the Legion of Honor.

The baroness, trying to bridge the moment, remarked that Luigi resembled someone they knew in Corsica—Nina Porta. Luigi answered that Nina had been his sister and then, when pressed, confirmed he was Luigi Porta.

At this name Piombo reeled. He and the baroness left the room in stunned silence; Ginevra stood pale and speechless. The revelation plunged the household into dread. The baroness had once said the Portas had killed Ginevra’s brother and burned their house; Piombo had then massacred the Portas in revenge. Luigi, bewildered, asked how he had survived. He recounted being bound to a bed before the house was set alight, then taken to Genoa at six and raised by an old man named Colonna, who warned of enemies and advised he assume the name Luigi to hide his identity. How he escaped death remained unclear.

Ginevra, horrified at the vendetta revealed in a single word, urged Luigi to stay for the moment—he was safe in her father’s house—but warned that once he left he would be hunted by Corsicans in Piombo’s service. Luigi, shaken, asked if the vendetta was indeed between them; she smiled sadly and nodded. Their love, she said, must be pure and resolute to endure what lay ahead. He squeezed her hand; they resolved their destiny was sealed. Ginevra escorted him to his modest lodging herself, determined rather than cowed.

Back home the atmosphere turned chill. Piombo fumbled for words but could not speak; he rang for a servant and ordered a fire kindled, his voice trembling. Both father and daughter paid for their pride—her reproachful eyes, his sorrowful ones—each nursing private torment the household could not bridge.

On Ginevra’s birthday, hoping to reconcile them, her mother gathered father and daughter in Piombo’s study. Ginevra read her mother’s intent and smiled sadly. At that moment two notaries arrived with witnesses to fulfill the law regarding Ginevra’s marriage to Luigi Porta. Piombo bristled, furious at what he saw as an assault on paternal authority. He demanded whether French laws could tear a daughter from her father and deprive an old man of his last consolation. The notary tried to speak, but Piombo’s agitation grew; he thrice interrupted with theatrical outrage.

When the notary continued calmly, Piombo suddenly seized a dagger from the wall and rushed toward Ginevra. Witnesses lunged to restrain him, but his fury pushed them aside. Seeing him advance, Ginevra knelt before her father and begged him to be merciful and give his consent. The sight unnerved him; he flung the dagger away and, struck by his own weakness, pushed her aside as if in shame.

Then Piombo pronounced the cruelest judgment: “Go. The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here.” He pressed his hand to his heart and bade her leave his house. The baroness, stunned, could not prevent him; he had renounced Ginevra publicly and irreversibly.

Ginevra fled to Luigi’s humble abode. She confessed the loss of parental blessing and their lack of fortune, but Luigi flung aside material concerns. “Then I am richer than kings!” he exclaimed. He vowed to love her in her parents’ place; she answered they would be happy, though joy mingled with dread at the long ordeal ahead.

Thus remained the hard facts: Ginevra’s candid declaration of love, Piombo’s possessive claim and violent renunciation, Luigi’s return thanks to Ginevra, and the catastrophic discovery that Luigi was a Porta—tied to the vendetta that had cost Ginevra’s brother and destroyed her family’s house. Piombo’s past massacre of the Portas made the union dangerous and impossible in his eyes, and his public repudiation left Ginevra only Luigi as shelter.

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father’s house she took refuge with Madame Servin until her marriage to Luigi. Madame Servin received her coldly—her husband blamed Ginevra—and the woman’s selfishness, though upsetting, left Ginevra proud yet shocked. She rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her and painted a bright future that lifted her spirits.

One morning trunks from her mother arrived with a loving, sorrowful letter pleading that she reconsider. The letter moved Ginevra and for an instant she thought of returning home. But when Luigi appeared, her resolve to abandon him dissolved; she chose to bury her grief for the sake of their love.

On the wedding day Ginevra was essentially alone; Luigi brought ordinary men as witnesses, a stark contrast to the usual gaiety. As they passed joyful processions her beauty silenced the small party. In the mayor’s office the clerk noted her parents’ absence and the mayor learned they had protested. The bureaucratic formality felt oppressive, reducing the ceremony to a legal act devoid of celebration.

After signing the registers they left with mixed relief and sadness. An old priest married them in a modest chapel; his remarks about their parents stung, but a passing sergeant’s simple blessing comforted Ginevra.

They moved into a modest apartment Luigi called their true wedding, filled with artistic supplies arranged for her. She delighted in the thoughtful touches. They copied paintings, found clients, and Luigi prospered enough to hire assistants. Evenings were shared; though she sometimes regretted the family life she had left, an anniversary portrait she painted for Luigi symbolized their contentment.

At the beginning of 1819 picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce originals instead of copies as competition grew. Madame Porta regretted not turning earlier to genre painting, and though Ginevra tried portraiture she faced stiff rivalry. With only modest savings, Luigi and Ginevra grew anxious about their future.

That winter Luigi labored relentlessly in a crowded, less lucrative writing market. He could no longer afford help and had to work longer for the same pay. Ginevra painted several commendable works, but dealers shunned lesser-known artists and low prices produced few sales.

Their household became dire; love and happiness were overshadowed by poverty. They concealed their worries: Ginevra showed affection while Luigi hid his fear, each clinging to love as sustenance. One night Ginevra woke to find Luigi gone and discovered him working late to avoid burdening her. Moved, she began coloring lithographs, laboring into the night while Luigi toiled in his garret. At dawn they pretended to sleep, each shielding the other from distress.

Another night, exhausted and feverish, Luigi opened his window and saw Ginevra's lamp across the courtyard, realizing she too was working. He confronted her and learned she could not idle while he suffered. They shared a tender moment, watched the dawn together and felt tentative hope.

Their struggle deepened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. Joy briefly masked their burdens, but soon they sold possessions and jewels. Poverty stripped them of comfort and pride. Months later Ginevra, frail and worn, struggled to care for a sickly child as Luigi, feeling helpless, sold her paintings and the remaining furniture for a pittance. Ginevra managed the household and tended their son while often starving herself to feed Luigi.

One evening she offered Luigi her last piece of bread; realizing her sacrifice, he was engulfed by despair and left to seek aid, selling himself as a military substitute to obtain an advance. He hurried home with the money, finding Ginevra asleep with their child. When he woke her she smiled, then noticed Bartolomeo cold and lifeless. Ginevra, delirious, professed her love and lamented their lost child; she died in his arms.

As Luigi mourned, Ginevra's parents sat at home remembering their daughter. Before they could reconcile a messenger arrived with the news. The old couple were shattered—the cycle of suffering had claimed another victim, leaving only memories and grief.
