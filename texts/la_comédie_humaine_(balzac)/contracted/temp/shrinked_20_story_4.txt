In the year 1800, near the end of October, a foreigner with a woman and a little girl lingered long before the Tuileries, near the ruins where the wing meant to join Catherine de’ Medici’s chateau to the Valois Louvre begins.

The man stood with folded arms and bowed head, sometimes raising it to look alternately at the consular palace and at his wife, who sat upon a stone. She seemed wholly occupied with the child of nine or ten, whose long black hair she toyed with, yet watched every glance her companion cast. Their bond was more than love; misery—perhaps the strongest tie—made them anxious together.

The stranger had a broad, serious head, thick hair like those in the Carracci paintings, jet black streaked with white. His noble, proud features were hardened; in spite of an erect figure he looked over sixty. His worn clothes were foreign. His wife’s faded, once-beautiful face betrayed deep sadness, though she forced smiles when he looked at her. The little girl, sun-tanned and Italian in her looks, had large black eyes beneath arched brows, a native nobleness, and simple grace. Passersby felt moved by the group’s plain despair; when the stranger noticed attention, he looked so savage that even bold loungers hurried away.

After standing some time he drew a dagger from his breast and gave it to his companion, saying in Italian, “I will see if the Bonapartes remember us.” He walked slowly toward the palace entrance, where a consular guard stopped him and refused to allow discussion. Seeing his obstinacy, the sentinel presented his bayonet. Fortune favored the stranger: the guard was changing, and the corporal pointed to the post commander.

“Let Bonaparte know that Bartolomeo di Piombo wishes to speak with him,” the Italian said to the captain.

The officer insisted one could not see the First Consul without a written request. Bartolomeo persisted that the soldier must go to Bonaparte. The officer refused, citing the rules. Bartolomeo frowned, casting a terrible look as if holding him responsible for what the refusal might cause. Then he remained under the portico between garden and courtyard. Those who will things intensely are often aided by chance: at that moment a carriage drew up and Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, emerged.

“Ah, Loucian, it is lucky for me I have met you!” cried the stranger in Corsican patois. Lucien recognized him and, after a word, took the Corsican away.

Murat, Lannes, and Rapp were in the First Consul’s cabinet. As Lucien entered with Piombo, the conversation ceased. Lucien took Napoleon by the arm and led him to a window recess. Napoleon signaled Murat and Lannes to retire; Rapp lingered, pretending not to see. The First Consul opened the door suddenly and found his aide-de-camp close to the wall.

“Do you choose not to understand me?” said Bonaparte. “I wish to be alone with my compatriot.”

“A Corsican!” replied the aide-de-camp. “I distrust those fellows too much to—”

Bonaparte smiled and pushed him aside. “Well, what has brought you here, my poor Bartolomeo?” he asked.

“To ask asylum and protection from you, if you are a true Corsican,” replied Bartolomeo roughly.

“What ill fortune drove you from the island? You were the richest—”

“I have killed all the Portas,” said the Corsican in a deep voice, frowning.

The First Consul stepped back in surprise.

“Do you mean to betray me?” cried Bartolomeo, casting a dark look at Bonaparte. “Do you know there are still four Piombos in Corsica?”

Lucien seized his compatriot’s arm and shook it. “Did you come here to threaten the savior of France?” he said.

Bonaparte signaled Lucien to be silent, then looked at Piombo. “Why did you kill the Portas?”

“We had been friends,” replied the man. “The Barbantis reconciled us. The day after we drank to end our quarrels, I left for Bastia. The Portas stayed in my house and set fire to my vineyard at Longone. They killed my son Gregorio. My daughter Ginevra and my wife had taken the sacrament that morning and escaped; the Virgin protected them. When I returned my house was ashes. I found Gregorio’s body by accident at night and knew the Portas had done it. I went to the woods, called together all the men who had ever served me—do you hear me, Bonaparte?—and we marched to the Portas’ vineyard. We arrived at five; at seven they were all before God. Giacomo says Eliza Vanni saved a child, Luigi. But I myself bound Luigi to his bed before setting fire to the house. I left the island with my wife and child without learning whether Luigi is alive.”

Bonaparte listened with curiosity but not surprise. Lucien asked how many there had been.

“Seven,” replied Piombo. “All of them persecuted you in olden times.”

The brothers showed no hatred. “Ha! you are no longer Corsicans!” cried Piombo in despair. “Farewell. In other days I protected you,” he added reproachfully. “Without me your mother would never have reached Marseille,” he said to Bonaparte, who stood silent and thoughtful, his elbow on a mantel-shelf.

“As a matter of duty, Piombo,” said Napoleon at last, “I cannot take you under my wing. I have become the leader of a great nation; I command the Republic; I am bound to execute the laws.”

“Ha! ha!” said Bartolomeo scornfully.

“But I can shut my eyes,” Bonaparte continued. “The tradition of the vendetta will long prevent law’s reign in Corsica,” he added to himself. “But it must be destroyed, at any cost.”

After a pause he spoke more personally. “Live here, in Paris,” he said to Bartolomeo. “We will know nothing of this affair. I will cause your property in Corsica to be bought to give you enough to live on for the present. Later, we will think of you. But remember, no more vendetta! There are no woods here to fly to. If you play with daggers you must expect no mercy. Here, the law protects all citizens; no one is allowed to do justice for himself.”

“He has made himself the head of a singular nation,” said Bartolomeo, taking Lucien’s hand and pressing it. “But you have both recognized me in misfortune, and I am yours henceforth, for life or death. You may dispose as you will of the Piombos.”

With these words his Corsican brow unbent and he looked about him pleasantly. “You are not badly off here,” he said, smiling, “You are all in red, like a cardinal.”

“Your success depends on yourself; you can have a palace too,” Bonaparte replied, watching him keenly. “It will often happen that I shall need some faithful friend in whom I can confide.”

A sigh of joy heaved the Corsican’s chest. He held out his hand to the First Consul. “The Corsican is in you still.”

Bonaparte smiled and looked at the man who seemed to bring a breath from the island that had given him support—the land he would never see again. He signaled to his brother, who then took Piombo away. Lucien asked about the family’s finances. Piombo showed his wife and Ginevra sitting on a heap of stones.

“We came from Fontainebleau on foot; we have not a single penny,” he said.

Lucien gave him his purse and told him to come the next day so arrangements could be made for their comfort. The value of Piombo’s Corsican property, if sold, would scarcely maintain him honorably in Paris.

Fifteen years elapsed between Piombo’s arrival in Paris with his family and the following event, which would be scarcely intelligible without this account of the preceding circumstances.

Servin, one of our distinguished artists, was the first to open a studio for young ladies who wished to learn painting.

About forty, upright and devoted to his art, he had married the dowerless daughter of a general. At first mothers accompanied their daughters; later they trusted him enough to send them alone, reassured by his principles and care.

He intended only well-born young women of comfortable families, to avoid complaints about class mixing. He refused those who wished to become professional artists, believing such pupils required different instruction. Gradually his prudence, skill in teaching, and marriage won him a fine reputation in society. When a girl wanted lessons, friends advised: “Send her to Servin’s.”

Servin thus became the go-to for feminine art, like Herbault for bonnets or Leroy for gowns. A student of his could judge the Musée, make a striking portrait, copy an old master, or paint a genre piece. He sufficed for the aristocracy’s educational needs. Yet despite these ties he remained independent and patriotic, keeping among them the easy, brilliant, half-ironical tone and freedom of judgment characteristic of painters.

He had taken precautions for the studio’s arrangement. The attic entrance above his rooms was walled up. To reach this sanctum—sacred as a harem—one climbed a small spiral stair within his rooms. The studio, filling most of the attic under the roof, had the vast proportions that surprise visitors who expect an artist squeezed into a garret.

The gallery was lit from above through enormous panes with green linen shades used to manage the light. On dark-gray painted walls hung caricatures and quick heads drawn with color or a knife, showing that, aside from expression, distinguished young girls share men’s fun and folly. A small stove with a large, zigzagging pipe was the room’s necessary ornament. A shelf ran round the walls with plaster models, most gray with dust. Here and there, a Niobe’s head, a smiling Venus, a pleading hand, a few yellowed écorchés, pictures, drawings, lay figures, frames without paintings, paintings without frames gave the studio that mixed physiognomy of jumble and bareness, poverty and riches, care and neglect. An atelier where everything seems small, even man, resembles an opera coulisse; ancient garments, gilded armor, fragments and machinery lie about. Yet there is something mysteriously grand there—genius and death; Diana and Apollo beside a skull; beauty and destruction, pseudo and reality—colors glowing in shadows, a whole motionless drama. Strange symbol of an artist’s head!

One brilliant July morning, two sun-rays traced gold lines in the dust of the studio. A dozen easels rose like masts. Several young girls animated the scene with varied expressions, attitudes, and dress. Strong shadows cast by the green serge curtains, arranged for each easel, produced piquant light-and-shade effects. This group was the prettiest picture in the room.

A fair young girl, simply dressed, sat apart, working bravely and seeming fearful of mishap. No one looked at or spoke to her; she was the prettiest, most modest, and apparently the least rich among them. Two main groups, distinctly separated, showed two cliques, two minds even where rank and fortune might seem forgotten.

These girls—sitting or standing amid color-boxes, playing with brushes, preparing palettes, painting, laughing, talking, singing—were a spectacle unknown to men. One, proud and capricious with black hair and fine hands, cast her glance about; another, light-hearted with chestnut hair, smiled; a third, dreamy and pale, bent like a drooping flower; her neighbor, tall and indolent with long eyes, said little and reflected, glancing at Antinous’s head. Another, like a jocoso in a Spanish play, full of wit, watched the rest with a comprehensive glance, making them laugh—she seemed to rule the first group: daughters of bankers, notaries, merchants—rich but conscious of slights from the aristocratic group.

The aristocratic group was led by Mademoiselle Amelie Thirion, the little daughter of one of the King’s ushers—silly, vain, proud of “an office at court.” She pretended to understand the master instantly, did her work as a favor, used an eyeglass, came very dressed and always late, and entreated companions to keep their voices low. In this group were several girls with exquisite figures and distinguished features but little simplicity; their attitudes were elegant but their faces lacked frankness, shaped by early training in polite manners that weakened sentiment and developed egotism.

Yet when the whole class assembled, childlike heads appeared among them—ravishingly pure faces with half-open lips and virgin smiles. Then the studio resembled not a studio, but a group of angels on a cloud.

By mid-day Servin had not appeared; he had been spending much time in another studio finishing a picture for the Exposition. Suddenly Mademoiselle Amelie Thirion spoke low to her neighbor; a great silence fell on the patrician group, and the commercial party fell equally silent in curiosity. The ultras’ secret was soon revealed.

Amelie rose, moved an easel from the aristocratic group, and placed it against a board partition that separated the studio from the attic’s extreme end where broken casts, defaced canvases, and firewood were kept. She rolled aside the stool, a box of colors, even the Prudhon copy her absent pupil had been making. After this coup d’état the Right began to work in silence; the Left discoursed at length.

“What will Mademoiselle Piombo say?” asked a girl of Mademoiselle Matilde Roguin, the lively oracle of the banking group.

“She’s not the type to say anything,” replied Matilde; “but fifty years hence she’ll remember and revenge it cruelly. She is someone I don’t want to be at war with.”

“The slight these young ladies mean to put upon her is all the more unkind,” said another, “because yesterday Mademoiselle Ginevra was very sad. Her father, they say, has just resigned. They ought not to add to her trouble, for she was considerate during the Hundred Days.”

“I have a great mind to get Mademoiselle Piombo’s easel and place it next to mine,” said Matilde. She rose, but sat down again. “With a character like hers,” she added, “you can’t tell how she’d accept civility; better wait.”

Ecco la, said the girl with black eyes, languidly.

They heard steps on the narrow stair. “Here she comes!” ran from mouth to mouth; absolute silence followed.

To understand Amelie Thirion’s ostracism add that this was late July, 1815. The second Bourbon Restoration had shaken friendships that had stood under the first Restoration. Families, almost all divided, renewed many deplorable scenes that stain history in civil wars. Children, young girls, old men shared the monarchial fever. Discord glided beneath roofs; distrust colored words and actions.

Ginevra Piombo loved Napoleon to idolatry; how then could she hate him? The emperor was her compatriot and the benefactor of her father. Baron di Piombo had been one of those devoted servants who aided the return from Elba. Incapable of denying his political faith and anxious to confess it, the old baron stayed in Paris among enemies. Ginevra made no secret of the grief the second Restoration caused her family; the only tears she had shed were at news of Napoleon’s captivity on the Bellerophon and Labedoyere’s arrest.

The studio’s aristocratic girls came from the most devoted royalist families. The Bonapartists’ exaggerations and horror were extreme then. Though petty now, Amelie’s act was a natural expression of prevailing hatred. Ginevra, one of Servin’s earliest pupils, had occupied the place now taken since her first day; the aristocratic circle had surrounded her. Driving her from a spot that in some sense belonged to her was to insult and inflict an artistic pain—for all artists have a favored spot.

But jealousy, not merely politics, fueled the Right clique. Ginevra, the ablest pupil, aroused intense envy. The master admired her talents and character; she outshone others. Without clear reason she gained a sort of prestige, like Bonaparte’s over his soldiers. The aristocracy had resolved on her fall for days; none had openly avoided her till Amelie’s decisive stroke, meant to force others to join her hatred. Though Ginevra was sincerely loved by some royalists, most were steeped in party ideas and chose feminine tactics: aloofness.

On Ginevra’s arrival she was met by profound silence. Of all who came to Servin’s studio she was the handsomest, tallest, and best made. Her carriage and demeanor had nobility and grace. Her face, instinct with intelligence, seemed to radiate light; her long hair, black eyes and lashes, and softly defined mouth gave signs of the kindliness strong beings derive from conscious strength. Yet a marble forehead expressed an almost savage pride—Corsican moral instincts—contrasting her gentle features. The rest of her person, simple and graceful in Lombard beauty, made it hard to inflict pain upon her. She inspired such attraction that her father had her accompanied to and from the studio. Her one defect: her full beauty made her look a woman; she had refused marriage out of love for her parents, feeling necessary to them. Painting had replaced passions and ordinary interests.

“You are very silent to-day, mesdemoiselles,” she said, advancing. “Good-morning, my little Laure,” she added, approaching the young girl painting apart. “That head is strong—the flesh tints a little too rosy, but the drawing is excellent.”

Laure looked up tenderly; mutual affection showed. A faint smile brightened Ginevra’s lips as she walked to her easel, greeting the first group and not noticing the unusual curiosity she excited. Like a queen in her court, she passed before the patricians without a word. Her absorption seemed such that she sat, opened her color-box, took up her brushes, and arranged herself as if oblivious to the silence. All heads in the banking group turned toward her. The Thirion camp’s sidelong glances were directed at Ginevra.

“She hasn’t noticed it!” said Mademoiselle Roguin.

Ginevra, abandoning her meditative air, turned toward the aristocrats. She measured the distance with a glance but said nothing.

“It hasn’t occurred to her they meant to insult her,” said Matilde; “she neither colored nor turned pale. How vexed those girls will be if she likes her new place as well as the old!” she added aloud.

The Italian pretended not to hear; perhaps she truly did not. She rose, walked deliberately to the partition that hid the closet, and examined the sash through which her light came. Giving importance to it, she mounted a chair to raise the green serge higher. Reaching it, her eye found a slight opening in the partition. The glance she cast through it was like a miser discovering treasure. She sprang down, returned to her place, feigned dissatisfaction with the light, pushed a table near the partition, placed a chair on it, climbed lightly, and again peered through the crevice. She cast one look into the space beyond, lit through a skylight; what she saw made her totter.

“Take care, Mademoiselle Ginevra, you’ll fall!” cried Laure.

All the girls watched the imprudent climber; fear of their coming gave her courage. “Pooh! it is more solid than a throne!” she answered, balancing. She secured the curtain, came down, moved the chair and table away, returned to her easel, and arranged it to suit the light. But her mind was not on the picture; it was bent on getting near the closet door. There she could distinctly hear a sound that had excited her curiosity the evening before: the firm, regular breathing of a man she had seen asleep. Her curiosity was satisfied beyond expectation—she recognized an Imperial eagle; upon the flitch bed lay the form of an officer of the Guard. She guessed it all. Servin was hiding a proscribed man!

She trembled lest any companion come and betray the man by a remark or closer inspection. She resolved to stay by the door, trusting her wits to prevent discovery.

“Better that I should be here,” she thought, “to prevent some luckless accident, than leave that poor man at the mercy of a heedless betrayal.”

This explained the indifference she had seemed to show to the move of her easel: the change let her gratify her curiosity naturally; she was too preoccupied to notice her removal.

Nothing is more mortifying to young girls than to see a mischief or insult miss its effect through the victim’s contempt or indifference. Hatred seems to grow as the enemy is raised. Ginevra’s behavior was an enigma; friends and foes were surprised; friends claimed for her every virtue except forgiveness. Though occasions for vengeance were rare in a studio, specimens she had shown sometimes left the impression she could be vindictive.

Mademoiselle Roguin concluded Ginevra’s silence showed grandeur of soul; the banking circle, inspired, planned to humiliate the aristocrats. Their fire of sarcasm soon humbled the Right coterie.

Madame Servin’s arrival interrupted the struggle. Amelie had noticed Madame Servin’s anxious glance toward the door near which Ginevra sat; later this glance would explain Madame Servin’s doubt and fear. “Mesdemoiselles,” she said, “Monsieur Servin cannot come to-day.” She then complimented each girl; when she reached Ginevra she showed unease she tried to hide. They nodded but said nothing; one painted, the other looked at the painting. The prisoner’s breathing could be heard, but Madame Servin appeared not to notice; her feigned deafness was obvious, and Ginevra perceived wilful blindness. The unknown man turned on his pallet.

Ginevra looked fixedly at Madame Servin, who said without change of face: “Your copy is as fine as the original; if I had to choose between the two I should be puzzled.”

“Monsieur Servin has not taken his wife into his confidence as to this mystery,” thought Ginevra, and after replying with a gentle smile she began to hum a Corsican canzonetta to cover the sound made by the prisoner.

It was so unusual to hear the studious Italian sing that all other girls looked up in surprise; later this would feed jealous theories.

Madame Servin soon left and the session ended without further events. Ginevra let companions depart and seemed to intend to work later; unconsciously she showed a desire to be left alone by impatient glances at pupils slow in leaving. Mademoiselle Thirion, cruel to the girl who excelled her, guessed a purpose hid under the rival’s industry. By watching she noticed Ginevra’s attentiveness to sounds no one else heard; that impatience was a flash of light for her.

Amelie was the last to leave; she went down to Madame Servin’s apartment and spoke a moment; then she pretended to have left her bag, ran softly back, and found Ginevra again on her frail scaffolding, absorbed, not hearing her companion’s slight steps. Amelie stepped as if on eggs, withdrew outside the door, and coughed. Ginevra quivered, turned, saw her enemy, blushed, hastened to alter the shade to explain her position, and came down leisurely. She left the studio, bearing the image of a man’s head as beauteous as Girodet’s Endymion, a masterpiece she had lately copied.

“To banish so young a man! Who can he be? for he is not Marshal Ney—” These two sentences expressed what Ginevra pondered for two days. On the third day, despite haste to be first, she found Amelie already there, having come by carriage.

Ginevra and her enemy observed each other long, faces impenetrable. Amelie had seen the handsome head but not the eagle and uniform through the crevice; she was lost in conjecture. Suddenly Servin came in much earlier than usual.

“Mademoiselle Ginevra,” he said after glancing round, “why have you placed yourself there? The light is bad. Come nearer to the rest and pull down that curtain a little.” Then he sat near Laure.

“Well, well!” he cried; “here is a head extremely well done. You’ll be another Ginevra.”

The master moved from easel to easel, scolding, flattering, jesting—his jests more dreaded than reprimands. Ginevra had not obeyed but remained at her post, resolved not to quit. She took a sheet of paper and sketched in sepia the hidden man’s head. Work done under emotion has a distinct stamp: passion often substitutes for genius. Under her circumstances an intuition lent her, for the moment, a supernatural talent. The head was dashed on the paper amid tremor she mistook for fear, in truth inspiration’s fire. From time to time she glanced furtively to hide the sketch if anyone came near. Yet at a moment she did not see Amelie’s eyeglass turned full upon the drawing from a great portfolio. Mademoiselle Thirion, recognizing the portrait of the mysterious man, sprang forward as the prisoner began to move. Ginevra knocked over her stool, spoke a few incoherent sentences, began to laugh; she had thrown the portrait into her portfolio before Amelie could reach it. The easel was now surrounded; Servin praised the beauty of the copy his favorite was making, and the whole class was duped by this stratagem—except Amelie, who slipped behind companions and attempted to open the portfolio where she had seen the sketch. Ginevra took it up without a word and placed it before her; the two girls looked at each other fixedly, in silence.

“Come, mesdemoiselles, take your places,” said Servin. “If you wish to do as well as Mademoiselle di Piombo, you mustn’t be always talking fashions and balls, trifling your time away.”

When they were reseated, Servin sat beside Ginevra.

“Was it not better that I should be the one to discover the mystery rather than the others?” the girl asked in a low voice.

“Yes,” replied the painter, “you are one of us, a patriot; but even if you were not, I should have confided the matter to you.”

They understood one another, and Ginevra no longer feared to ask: “Who is he?”

“An intimate friend of Labedoyere, who did more than any other, except the unfortunate colonel, to unite the 7th with the grenadiers of Elba. He was a major in the Imperial Guard and was at Waterloo.”

“Why not have burned his uniform and shako and given him citizen’s clothes?” she asked.

“He will have them to-night.”

“You ought to have closed the studio for some days.”

“He is going away.”

“Then they’ll kill him,” said the girl. “Let him stay here with you till the storm is over. Paris is still a place where a man can be hidden safely. Is he a friend of yours?” she asked.

“No; he has no claim on me but ill-luck. He came into my hands by my father-in-law, who returned to the army during the campaign and cleverly rescued him from those who captured Labedoyere. He came here to defend the general—a foolish fellow!”

“Do you call him that!” cried Ginevra, astonished.

“My father-in-law is too closely watched to keep him in his own house,” Servin resumed. “So he brought him to me by night about a week ago. I hoped to keep him out of sight in this corner, the only spot in the house where he could be safe.”

“If I can be useful, employ me,” said Ginevra. “I know the Marechal de Feltre.”

“Well, we’ll see,” the painter replied.

This conversation lasted long enough to draw the attention of other girls. Servin then left Ginevra, went once more round each easel, and gave such long lessons that he was still there at the hour pupils usually left.

“You are forgetting your bag, Mademoiselle Thirion,” said the professor, running after the girl now condescending to spy.

The baffled pupil returned for the bag, feigning surprise; but Servin’s act confirmed to her the presence and importance of a mystery. She noisily ran down the staircase and slammed the Servins’ apartment door to give the impression she had gone; then she softly returned and stationed herself outside the studio door.

When the painter and Ginevra thought themselves alone, Servin rapped oddly on the garret door, which opened on rusty hinges. Ginevra then saw a tall, well-made young man in an Imperial uniform; one arm was in a sling and his pallor betrayed suffering. He recoiled at the sight of an unknown woman.

Amelie, unable to look into the room with the door closed and afraid to remain, slipped away silently after hearing the garret open.

“Fear nothing,” said the painter to the officer. “Mademoiselle is the daughter of a faithful friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo.”

At once the soldier had no doubt of Ginevra’s patriotism.

“You are wounded,” she said.

“Oh! it is nothing, mademoiselle,” he answered; “the wound is healing.”

From the street came the cry of the vendors: “Condemned to death!” They all shuddered, and the soldier heard a name that blanched him.

“Labedoyere!” he cried, falling on a stool.

They sat in silence. Drops stood on the young man’s livid forehead; he seized his hair in despair and rested his elbow on Ginevra’s easel.

“After all,” he said, rising abruptly, “Labedoyere and I knew what we were doing. We were certain of the fate awaiting us, whether triumph or defeat. He dies for the Cause, and here am I hiding myself!”

He rushed to the studio door; Ginevra reached it first and barred his way.

“Can you restore the Emperor?” she asked. “Do you expect to raise that giant who could not maintain himself?”

“But what can I do?” the young man asked the two friends chance had sent him. “I have no relation in the world. Labedoyere was my protector and friend; without him I am alone. Tomorrow I myself may be condemned; my only fortune was my pay. I spent my last penny to come here and try to snatch Labedoyere from his fate; death is, therefore, a necessity for me. When a man decides to die he ought to know how to sell his life to the executioner. I was thinking just now that the life of an honest man is worth that of two traitors, and the blow of a dagger well placed may give immortality.”

This outburst alarmed Servin and even Ginevra, who understood the young man’s nature. She admired his handsome face and sweet voice, which did not lose its fervor in anger. Then she tried to soothe him.

“Monsieur,” she said, “as for your pecuniary distress, permit me to offer my savings. My father is rich; I am his only child; he loves me, and I am sure he will never blame me. Have no scruple in accepting my offer; our property comes from the Emperor; we do not own a penny that is not the result of his munificence. Is it not gratitude to assist his faithful soldiers? Take what you need as indifferently as I offer it. It is only money!” she added with contempt. “Now, as for friends—those you shall have.”

She raised her head proudly and her eyes shone.

“The head which falls tomorrow before a dozen muskets will save yours,” she went on. “Wait till the storm is over; you can then escape and take service in foreign countries if you are not forgotten here; or in the French army, if you are.”

There is always a delicacy in consolation from a woman, something maternal and complete. When words of hope and peace are spoken with graceful gesture by a beautiful benefactress, a young man does not resist. The prisoner breathed in love through all his senses. A rosy tinge colored his white cheeks; his eyes lost some of their dulled sadness and he said, in a peculiar tone:—

“You are an angel of goodness—But Labedoyere!” he added. “Oh, Labedoyere!”

At that cry they looked at one another in silence, each understanding the other’s thought. They were no longer mere acquaintances: in those moments the links of friendship knit them as if for years.

“Dear friend,” said Servin, “can you save him?”

“I can avenge him.”

Ginevra quivered. Though the stranger was handsome, what touched her more was the pity born of a woman’s heart for suffering that is not ignoble; but a cry of vengeance, a proscribed soul devoted to Napoleon, Corsican generosity—this stirred her deeply. She looked at the officer with respectful emotion that shook his heart. For the first time a man had produced a keen sensation in her. At once her sympathy deepened into something more: she put the stranger’s soul on a par with his noble beauty and the proportions she admired as an artist. Led by curiosity to pity, from pity to powerful interest, she felt such profound sensations she was uneasy at staying.

“Until tomorrow, then,” she said, giving him a gentle parting smile.

Seeing that smile illumined her features anew; the stranger forgot all else for a moment.

“Tomorrow,” he said sadly; “but tomorrow, Labedoyere—”

Ginevra put a finger to her lips and looked at him, as if saying: “Be calm, be prudent.”

The young man cried in his own language: “Ah! Dio! che non vorrei vivere dopo averla veduta?—who would not wish to live after seeing her?”

The peculiar accent made Ginevra quiver.

“Are you Corsican?” she cried, returning toward him with a beating heart.

“I was born in Corsica,” he replied; “but I was brought, while very young, to Genoa, and as soon as old enough for military service I enlisted.”

The young man’s beauty, his attachment to the Emperor, his wound and misfortunes—all blended into a new, delightful sentiment in Ginevra. This persecuted man was a child of Corsica; he spoke its cherished language. She stood motionless, held by a magical sensation; before her eyes was a living portrait to which every feeling lent vivid color. At Servin’s invitation the officer sat on a divan; the painter, after removing the sling, undid the bandages to dress the wound. Ginevra shuddered at a long, broad sabre gash across his forearm and a moan escaped her. The stranger raised his head and smiled at her. Servin lifted the lint and touched the lacerated flesh with delicate care; the wounded man’s pale, sickly face expressed, as he looked at the girl, more pleasure than suffering. An artist might have admired the contrasts: white linen and bared arm against the red and blue uniform.

A soft half-light pervaded the studio; an evening ray lit where the soldier sat, bathing his blanched face, black hair, and clothes. To Ginevra’s Italian imagination it was an omen. He seemed a celestial messenger, speaking her language and unconsciously conjuring childhood memories; in her heart dawned a feeling as fresh and pure as her innocence. For a short moment she was motionless and dreamy, then blushed, exchanged a soft glance with the wounded man, and fled with his vision before her eyes.

The next day, though not a class day, Ginevra came to the studio and the prisoner sat beside her easel. Servin, with a sketch to finish, played mentor as the two talked chiefly in Corsican. The soldier related the sufferings of the retreat from Moscow; at nineteen he had made the passage of the Beresins, and was almost the last of his regiment. He described with fiery words the disaster of Waterloo. His voice was music to the Italian girl. Brought up as a Corsican, Ginevra was, in some sense, a child of Nature; falseness was unknown to her; she gave herself up without reserve to her impressions, showing them without the coquetry of Parisian girls. That day she often sat with palette and brushes in her hands without touching a color, eyes fixed on the officer, lips slightly parted, listening as if painting a stroke never painted. She was not surprised at the softness in his eyes; she felt her own despite a resolve to remain stern. After pauses she painted diligently without raising her head, because he sat near and watched. The first time he sat by her in silence she said, after a long pause:—

“Does it amuse you to see me paint?”

That day she learned his name was Luigi. They agreed that on class days, when they could not see each other, any important political event would be signaled by Ginevra singing certain Corsican melodies.

The following day Mademoiselle Thirion informed the class, under pledge of secrecy, that Ginevra di Piombo had a lover, a young man who came during lesson hours and concealed himself in the garret.

“You, who take her part,” she said to Mademoiselle Roguin, “watch her carefully, and you will see how she spends her time.”

Ginevra was observed with malicious attention. They listened to her songs and watched her glances. At times, when she supposed herself unseen, a dozen pairs of eyes were furtively upon her. Enlightened thus, the girls interpreted truly the emotions crossing the beautiful Italian’s features—her gestures, the tones in which she hummed, and the attention with which she listened to sounds only she could hear through the partition.

By the end of a week, Laure was the only one of Servin’s fifteen pupils who had resisted peeking at Luigi through the crevice; she still defended her friend. Mademoiselle Roguin tried to make her wait on the staircase after class to prove the intimacy by entering the studio and surprising them, but Laure refused to condescend to such espionage, and thus became the object of reprobation.

Soon Mademoiselle Thirion declared it improper to attend classes with a painter whose opinions were tainted with patriotism and Bonapartism, and she ceased to attend. Though she forgot Ginevra, the harm she planted bore fruit. Little by little the other young girls told their mothers of the strange events at the studio. One day Matilde Roguin did not come; the next another was missing, and so on, until the last three or four left. Ginevra and Laure were the sole occupants of the deserted studio for several days.

Ginevra did not notice the falling off nor ask the cause. As soon as she had invented means of communicating with Luigi she lived in the studio in a delightful solitude, thinking only of the officer and the dangers that threatened him. Though a sincere admirer of noble characters who never betray their political faiths, she urged Luigi to recognize royal authority to be released and remain in France. He refused. If passions are born and nourished under romantic causes, never were so many such circumstances conjoined to unite two young souls. Their friendship progressed in a month more than a society friendship would in ten years. Adversity tested and revealed character. Ginevra studied Luigi and soon esteemed him. Older than Luigi, she found charm in being courted by a youth so grand, so tried by fate—a youth who combined manly experience with adolescent graces. Luigi felt unspeakable pleasure in being apparently protected by a woman of twenty-five. Was it not proof of love? The union of gentleness and pride, strength and tenderness in Ginevra was irresistible, and he was subjugated. In short, they loved each other so profoundly there was no need to deny or proclaim it.

One evening Ginevra heard the accustomed signal. Luigi scratched with a pin on the woodwork, making no more noise than a spider fastening its thread. The signal meant he wished to come out.

Ginevra glanced around, and not seeing Laure, opened the door; but Luigi caught sight of the pupil and abruptly retired. Surprised, Ginevra saw Laure and said as she went to her easel:—

“You are staying late, my dear. That head seems finished; you only want a highlight—see! on that knot of hair.”

“You would do me a great kindness,” said Laure in a trembling voice, “if you would give this copy a few touches; then I could carry away something to remind me of you.”

“Willingly,” said Ginevra, painting a few strokes. “But I thought it was a long way from your home, and it is late.”

“Oh! Ginevra, I am going away, never to return,” cried the poor girl sadly.

“You mean to leave Monsieur Servin!” exclaimed Ginevra, less affected than she might have been a month earlier.

“Haven’t you noticed, Ginevra, that for some days past you and I have been alone in the studio?”

“True,” said Ginevra, as if struck by recollection. “Are all those young ladies ill, or going to be married, or are their fathers on duty at court?”

“They have left Monsieur Servin,” replied Laure.

“Why?”

“On your account, Ginevra.”

“My account!” repeated the Corsican, springing up, brow threatening and eyes flashing.

“Oh! don’t be angry, my kind Ginevra,” cried Laure in distress. “My mother insists on my leaving. The young ladies say you have some intrigue, and that Monsieur Servin allows the young man you love to stay in the dark attic. I never believed these calumnies nor told my mother. But last night Madame Roguin met her at a ball and asked if she still sent me here. When my mother answered yes, Madame Roguin told her the falsehoods. Mamma scolded me severely; she said I must have known all, and that I failed in proper confidence between mother and daughter. Oh! my dear Ginevra! I, who took you for my model, how grieved I am that I can’t be your companion.”

“We shall meet again in life; girls marry—” said Ginevra.

“When they are rich,” sighed Laure.

“Come and see me; my father has a fortune—”

“Ginevra,” Laure added tenderly, “Madame Roguin and my mother are coming to see Monsieur Servin tomorrow to reproach him; hadn’t you better warn him?”

A thunderbolt could not have surprised Ginevra more.

“What matter is it to them?” she asked naively.

“Everybody thinks it very wrong. Mamma says it is immoral.”

“And you, Laure, what do you say?”

Laure looked up and their thoughts joined. She could no longer hold back tears; she flung herself on Ginevra’s breast and sobbed. At this moment Servin entered.

“Mademoiselle Ginevra,” he cried with enthusiasm, “I have finished my picture! It is now being varnished. What have you been doing meanwhile? Where are the young ladies; are they on holiday or in the country?”

Laure dried her tears, bowed to Monsieur Servin, and went away.

“The studio has been deserted for some days,” replied Ginevra, “and the young ladies are not coming back.”

“Pooh!” said the artist.

“Oh! don’t laugh,” said Ginevra. “Listen: I am the involuntary cause of the loss of your reputation—”

The artist smiled and interrupted:—

“My reputation? Why, in a few days my picture will make it at the Exposition.”

“That relates to your talent,” replied the girl. “I speak of your morality. Those young ladies have told their mothers that Luigi was shut up here, and that you lent yourself— to—our love.”

“There is some truth in that, mademoiselle,” replied the professor. “The mothers of those young ladies are foolish; had they come to me I should have explained. But I don’t care a straw! Life is short.”

And the painter snapped his fingers above his head. Luigi, who had heard part of the conversation, came in.

“You have lost all your scholars,” he cried. “I have ruined you!”

The artist took Luigi’s hand and Ginevra’s and joined them.

“Marry one another, my children,” he said with fatherly kindness.

They dropped their eyes; their silence was the first avowal of love.

“You will surely be happy,” said Servin. “There is nothing to equal the happiness of two beings like yourselves when bound together in love.”

Luigi pressed his protector’s hand and presently, with emotion, said:—

“To you I owe it all.”

“Be happy! I bless and wed you,” said the painter with comic unction, laying his hands on the lovers’ heads.

This jest ended the strained feeling. All three looked at one another and laughed. Ginevra pressed Luigi’s hand with the simple ardor of her native habits.

“Ah ca, my dear children,” resumed Servin, “you think all will go right now, but you are much mistaken.”

The lovers looked astonished.

“Don’t be anxious. I’m the only one your romance will harm. But Madame Servin is a little straitlaced; I don’t see how to settle it with her.”

“Heavens! and I forgot to tell you,” exclaimed Ginevra, “that Madame Roguin and Laure’s mother are coming here tomorrow to—”

“I understand,” said the painter.

“But you can easily justify yourself,” continued the girl, proudly. “Monsieur Luigi,” she added to him with an arch look, “will no longer object to entering the royal service. Well then,” after his smile, “tomorrow morning I will send a petition to a most influential man at the War ministry—a man who will refuse nothing to the daughter of the Baron di Piombo. We shall obtain a ‘tacit’ pardon for Captain Luigi, for, of course, they will not allow him the rank of major. And then,” she added to Servin, “you can confound the mothers of my charitable companions by telling them the truth.”

“You are an angel!” cried Servin.

While this passed at the studio, Ginevra’s parents grew anxious at her delay.

“It is six o’clock, and Ginevra not yet home!” cried Bartolomeo.

“She was never so late before,” said his wife.

The two old people looked at one another with unusual anxiety. Too restless to remain, Bartolomeo paced the salon with an active step for a man over seventy-seven. Thanks to a robust constitution he had changed little since arriving in Paris; despite his tall figure he walked erect. His white sparse hair left uncovered a broad skull suggesting character and firmness. His face, seamed with deep wrinkles, had taken with age a nobler expression, preserving pallid tones that inspire veneration. The ardor of his passions lived in the fire of his eyes; his still-not-wholly-white eyebrows retained mobility. His head’s aspect was stern but conveyed that Piombo had a right to be so. His kindness and gentleness were known only to his wife and daughter. In functions, or before strangers, he never laid aside the majesty time had impressed; the habit of frowning with heavy eyebrows and giving his eyes a Napoleonic fixity made his manner icy.

During his political life he had been so generally feared he seemed unsocial. The life, morals, and fidelity of Piombo made him obnoxious to courtiers. Despite delicate missions entrusted to him which would have been lucrative to others, he possessed an income of not more than thirty thousand francs from an investment in the Grand Livre. Considering cheap government securities under the Empire and Napoleon’s liberality toward faithful servants, the Baron di Piombo must have been a man of stern integrity. He owed his barony to the Emperor’s need to give him a title before sending him on missions to foreign courts.

Bartolomeo always professed hatred for the traitors Napoleon surrounded himself with. It is told that he took three steps to the door of the Emperor’s cabinet after advising him to get rid of three men in France on the eve of the 1814 campaign. After the second Bourbon return Bartolomeo ceased wearing the Legion of Honor. No man better represented those old Republicans, incorruptible friends to the Empire, relics of two energetic governments. Though he displeased courtiers, he had Darus, Drouots, and Carnots as friends. As for other politicians, he cared not a whiff of his cigar’s smoke for them, especially since Waterloo.

Bartolomeo had bought, for the moderate sum Madame Mère had paid him for his Corsican estates, the old Portenduere mansion, in which he made no changes. Lodged usually at government expense, he did not occupy it until after Fontainebleau. Following simple habits, the baron and his wife gave no heed to outward splendor; their furniture was what they bought with the mansion. The grand apartments, lofty, sombre, and bare, the wide mirrors in blackened gilded frames, and Louis XIV–period furniture suited Bartolomeo and his wife, personages worthy of antiquity.

Under the Empire and during the Hundred Days, while exercising well-rewarded functions, the old Corsican maintained a great establishment, more to honor his office than to shine himself. His and his wife’s lives were so frugal and tranquil that their modest fortune sufficed. To them their daughter Ginevra was more precious than all wealth. When in May, 1814, the Baron di Piombo resigned, dismissed his servants, and closed his stable, Ginevra, quiet and unpretending like her parents, saw nothing to regret. Like all great souls, she found luxury in strength of feeling and happiness in quiet and work. These three loved one another too well for externals to matter.

Often, especially after Napoleon’s second fall, Bartolomeo and his wife passed delightful evenings with their daughter, listening while she sang and played. They took vast secret pleasure in her slightest word; their eyes followed her with tender anxiety; they heard her step in the court-yard. Like lovers, the three often sat silently together, understanding better than by speech the eloquence of their souls. This profound sentiment animated their every thought. If memories of Napoleon’s benefits or public events distracted them, they could always return to this source without affecting their community of thought, for Ginevra shared their political passions. What more natural than the ardor with which they found refuge in their only child?

Until now public life had absorbed Bartolomeo’s energy; after leaving office he felt the need to cast it into the last sentiment remaining. Apart from parentage, there may have been another reason for their intense reciprocal love: it was undivided love. Ginevra’s whole heart belonged to her father, as his belonged to her; and if we are bound by defects more than virtues, Ginevra echoed her father’s youthful passions. This was the sole imperfection of their triple life. Ginevra was born unyielding, vindictive, and passionate, like her father.

The Corsican delighted in developing these savage sentiments in his daughter, as a lion teaches lion-cubs to spring at prey. But this apprenticeship to vengeance having no outlet in family life, the child turned the principle against her father; as a child she forgave him nothing, and he yielded. Piombo saw nothing more than childish nonsense in these quarrels, but Ginevra acquired a habit of ruling her parents. Amid the tempests he loved to excite, a look or word of tenderness sufficed to pacify them, and often they were nearest a kiss when threatening vehemently.

Nevertheless, for five years Ginevra, grown wiser, avoided such scenes. Her faithfulness, devotion, love filling her every thought, and admirable good sense overcame her temper. Yet a great evil resulted from her training; she lived with her parents on an equality always dangerous.

Piombo and his wife, without education, allowed Ginevra to study as she pleased. Following youthful caprices she studied many things for a time, abandoning each train of thought until painting proved her dominant passion. Ginevra would have made a noble woman had her mother guided her studies and harmonized her gifts; her defects came from the fatal education the old Corsican delighted in giving her.

After pacing and ringing the bell, a servant entered.

“Go and meet Mademoiselle Ginevra,” said his master.

“I always regret our carriage on her account,” remarked the baroness.

“She said she did not want one,” replied Piombo, looking at his wife, who, used to obedience, lowered her eyes and said no more.

Already a septuagenarian, tall, withered, pale, and wrinkled, the baroness resembled those old women Schnetz paints in Italian genre scenes. She was habitually silent, sometimes seeming another Mrs. Shandy; occasionally a word or look betrayed that her feelings retained youthful vigor. Her dress, devoid of coquetry, was often in bad taste. She usually sat passive, buried in a low sofa like a Sultana Valide, awaiting or admiring her Ginevra. All was well with her if Ginevra was happy. Her hair was white, and a few strands only showed above her wrinkled forehead and hollow cheeks.

“It is now fifteen days,” she said, “since Ginevra made a practice of being late.”

“Jean is so slow!” cried the impatient old man, buttoning his blue coat and seizing his hat, which he dashed on as he took his cane and departed.

“You will not get far,” said his wife, calling after him.

As she spoke the porte-cochere opened and shut, and the old mother heard Ginevra’s steps in the court-yard. Bartolomeo almost instantly reappeared, carrying his daughter, who struggled in his arms.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!” Ginevra laughed, and he immediately set her down with exaggerated respect. Her mother, pale with alarm, was reassured by Ginevra’s nod—the cry had been a ruse to escape him. The baroness’s color returned and gaiety brightened her face; Piombo rubbed his hands in his habitual show of joy. The two old people looked revived.

“Now, to dinner!” cried the baron, taking his daughter’s hand. Piombo teased her about spending more time at the studio than with them.

“Oh, father!” she protested.

“Ginevra is planning some surprise,” said her mother.

“A picture of your own!” the Corsican exclaimed.

“Yes, I am occupied at the studio,” Ginevra answered slowly. Her pallor brought her mother’s anxiety.

“No! it shall never be said that Ginevra Piombo acted a lie,” she added with resolution. Piombo and his wife were astonished.

“I love a young man,” Ginevra confessed, lowering her eyes.

“Is he a prince?” her father asked ironically.

“No, father. He is a young man without fortune.”

“Is he handsome?”

“He is unfortunate.”

“What is he?”

“Labedoyere’s comrade; he was proscribed, without refuge; Servin concealed him, and—”

“Servin is a good fellow,” cried Piombo, then added sourly that his daughter should love no man but her father. Ginevra insisted she could not command her feelings.

“I flattered myself,” Piombo said, wounded, “that my Ginevra would be faithful to me until I died. I did not expect our tenderness would find a rival.”

“Did I ever reproach you for your fanaticism for Napoleon?” she asked. “Have you loved any one but me? You left me months at a time on missions. I bore it. Life has necessities.”

“Ginevra!” his voice broke.

“No, you don’t love me for myself; your reproaches betray intolerable egotism.”

“You dare blame your father’s love!” Piombo cried.

“Father, I don’t blame you,” she answered gently. “I have grounds for my love as you have for your selfishness. Heaven is my witness I have fulfilled my duty. For fifteen years I have never left your wing. But is it ingratitude to desire a husband who will protect me hereafter?”

Piombo’s tone turned dangerous. Bartolomeo at last pleaded in a heart-rending voice for her to stay with him.

“But, father, remember I need not leave you; we shall be two to love you; you will learn the man to whose care you bequeath me. You will be doubly cherished—by him who is my other self, by me who am all his.”

“Oh! Ginevra, Ginevra!” cried the Corsican, clenching his fists. “Why did you not marry when Napoleon suggested counts and dukes?”

“They loved me to order,” she said. “Besides, I did not wish to leave you alone.”

“You don’t wish to leave me alone, and yet you marry! That is leaving me. I know you, my daughter; if you marry you would cease to love us.” He declared she would have no longer any daughter.

Ginevra feared a violent crisis but was disarmed by his sudden gentleness. She answered with tenderness and passion.

“Father,” she said, “no, you shall never be abandoned by your Ginevra. But love her a little for herself. If you knew how he loves me! He would never make me unhappy!”

“Comparisons already!” cried Piombo. “No, I can never endure your marriage. If he loved you as you deserve he would kill me; if he did not love you, I should put a dagger through him.” His hands and lips trembled, his eyes flashed. Ginevra matched him, her eyes equally aflame.

“To love you! What man is worthy of such life?” he raged. “To love you as a father is paradise on earth; who is there worthy to be your husband?”

“He,” said Ginevra; “he of whom I am not worthy.”

“Who is he?”

“He whom I love.”

“How can he know you enough to love you?”

“Father,” she said impatiently, “whether he loves me or not, if I love him—”

“You love him?” he cried.

She inclined her head. He demanded whether she loved him more than her parents.

“The two feelings cannot be compared,” she replied.

“Is one stronger?”

“I think it is.”

“You shall not marry him,” shouted the Corsican, shaking the windows.

“I shall marry him,” she answered calmly.

Their mother interceded in terror. The baron, striding and then sitting, begged her to say nothing more that night and appeal to pity. Ginevra refused to break her word. The baroness fell silent, terrified.

“Refusing to obey an unjust order is not disobedience,” Ginevra said.

“No order can be unjust from the lips of your father,” he retorted. He spoke of a repugnance he felt, counsel from on high, perhaps to protect her from some great evil.

“The only evil could be that he did not love me,” she answered. “He is my life, my good, my thought. Even if I obeyed you he would be ever in my soul. To forbid me to marry him is to make me hate you.”

“You love us not!” cried Piombo.

“Oh!” she shook her head.

“Then forget him; be faithful to us. After we are gone—you understand?”

“Father, do you wish me to long for your death?” she cried.

“I shall outlive you. Children who do not honor their parents die early,” he snarled.

“All the more reason why I should marry and be happy,” she replied.

Her calm reasoning enraged Piombo; his face flushed. Ginevra sprang like a bird to his knee, threw her arms about him, and caressed his white hair.

“Oh yes, yes, let me die first! I could never survive you, my father, my kind father!” she sobbed.

His anger melted. He played with her hair and frolicked, treating her like a child. She joked and coaxed to gain admission for Luigi; he refused. She sulked, coaxed, and by the end of the evening had impressed both her love for Luigi and the idea of marriage upon him.

The next day she said no more; she was more affectionate than ever, as if to thank him for a consent implied by silence. A week later her mother whispered that she had persuaded her father to receive Luigi. Ginevra was overjoyed. That day she came home on Luigi’s arm. The officer had come out of hiding a second time: Ginevra’s appeals to the Minister of War had succeeded and Luigi’s name was back on the rolls. This, she thought, was the first great step.

Warned of the difficulties with her father, Luigi braced himself; brave in battle, he trembled at meeting Piombo. Ginevra noticed and took it as proof of his love.

“How pale you are!” she said.

“Oh, Ginevra, if it concerned my life only—”

Bartolomeo, notified of the presentation, would not rise but remained seated, stern. Ginevra introduced Luigi as a soldier who had fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean. Piombo glanced at Luigi and sneered, “Monsieur is not decorated.”

“I no longer wear the Legion of Honor,” Luigi replied timidly.

Mortified by his rudeness, Ginevra set a chair forward. Madame Piombo observed that Luigi resembled a person they had known in Corsica—Nina Porta. Luigi confirmed Nina was his sister. “Are you Luigi Porta?” the old man asked. When Luigi answered yes, Bartolomeo rose, tottered, and, supported by his wife, left the room. They abandoned their daughter with a sort of horror.

Luigi, bewildered, looked at Ginevra, who had grown marble-white and stared at the door. The silence seemed solemn; fear entered her for the first time. She struck her hands and said in a voice only a lover could catch, “What misery in a word!”

“In the name of our love, what have I said?” Luigi asked.

“My father never spoke to me of our deplorable history; I was too young when we left Corsica to know anything.”

“Are we in vendetta?” Luigi asked trembling.

“Yes.” She smiled sadly, bowed, then raised her head with pride. “Oh, Luigi, our love must be pure and sincere to give me strength to tread the path I am about to enter. But it involves a happiness that will last throughout our lives, will it not?”

He smiled, squeezed her hand, their calm there foreshadowing strength. She drew him from her father’s house with desperate energy and did not leave him until he reached the lodging Servin had found.

Returning, she had regained serenity. She entered the gloomy salon smiling gently. Her mother had been weeping; the sight shook her, but she hid her emotion. No one touched dinner. When Ginevra took her place between them, Piombo tried to speak but failed; he rang for the servant and said at last, “Light the fire; I am cold.” The struggle within him was visible; his face distorted. Ginevra watched anxiously.

“Ginevra, you love the enemy of your family,” Piombo said at last.

“That is true,” she replied.

“You must choose between us. Our vendetta is part of our being. Whoso does not share my vengeance is not a member of my family.”

“My choice is made,” she answered calmly.

He softened briefly and shed the only tears he would ever shed. “I shall be his wife,” she said.

Bartolomeo recovered his coolness. “The marriage will not take place in my lifetime; I will never consent.”

Ginevra was silent.

“Ginevra, have you reflected that Luigi is the son of the man who killed your brother?” he demanded.

“He was six when that crime was committed; he was not guilty,” she replied.

“He is a Porta!” Bartolomeo cried.

“I have never shared that hatred,” she said. “You did not bring me up to think a Porta must be a monster. How could I know one you thought killed someone had survived? Is it not natural you should yield your vendetta to my feelings?”

“A Porta!” repeated Piombo. “If his father had found you in your bed you would not be living now; he would have taken your life a hundred times.”

“It may be so,” she answered; “but his son has given me life, and more than life. To see Luigi is a happiness without which I cannot live. Luigi has revealed to me the world of sentiments. I may have seen handsomer faces, but none has charmed me thus; I may have heard voices—no, never any so melodious! Luigi loves me; he will be my husband.”

“Never,” said Piombo. “I would rather see you in your coffin, Ginevra.” He paced, angry, dropping sentences. “You think you can bend my will. Undeceive yourself. A Porta shall never be my son; that is my decree. I am Bartolomeo di Piombo—do you hear me?”

“Do you attach some mysterious meaning to those words?” she asked coldly.

“They mean that I have a dagger, and that I do not fear man’s justice. Corsicans explain themselves to God.”

“And I,” said his daughter rising, “am Ginevra Piombo, and I declare that within six months I shall be the wife of Luigi Porta. You are a tyrant, my father.” He struck his fists on the mantel and muttered, “Ah! we are in Paris!” Then he sat in silence.

Having declared her will, she affected coolness: she opened the piano and played and sang nocturnes and scherzos with calm grace, triumphing over him by showing she could be merry despite his cold. This tacit insult wounded him deeply; his pride and the training he had given her made no allowance. Next day the gates were closed when she sought to leave. She told Luigi by means of a chambermaid who carried letters between them. For five days they corresponded by this crude but ingenious means.

Father and daughter spoke little. Each nursed hatred and suffered proudly and silently, trying to break ties but without success. Neither softened. Ginevra sometimes looked at her father with a savage reproach; remorse dulled her eyes at times. With Bartolomeo, hesitation from native goodness yielded to pride and Corsican rancor. They encouraged each other’s anger, each perhaps hoping the other would yield.

On Ginevra’s birthday, desperate, her mother tried to reconcile them by recalling family memories. All three sat in Bartolomeo’s study. Ginevra guessed her mother’s aim and smiled sadly. A servant announced two notaries with witnesses. Bartolomeo looked at these men whose cold faces grated on the strained souls. He gestured them to sit.

“Monsieur is, no doubt, M. le Baron di Piombo?” began the oldest.

Bartolomeo bowed. The notary, Monsieur Roguin, took a snuff-box and began stiffly to explain the law and the duties of notaries in cases of filial non-consent. He outlined the respectful summons that the law allowed a woman of age to invoke before marriage despite parental refusal and suggested, in honeyed conciliatory tones, that sensible fathers, after a final remonstrance, usually yielded.

Piombo’s face changed; his wrinkles contracted into an expression of indescribable cruelty, like a tiger’s. The witnesses trembled. Roguin, feeling the man before him, paused and then read the stamped summons. He asked Bartolomeo his answer.

“Are there laws in France which destroy paternal authority?” demanded the Corsican.

“Monsieur—” Roguin tried.

“Which tear a daughter from her father? Which deprive an old man of his last consolation? Which kill him?” Piombo’s questions cut like knives.

Notaries persisted in cool, precise reasoning amid passion. The formalities, to Piombo, seemed escaped from hell. Rage seared him; suddenly he sprang to a dagger hanging above the fireplace and rushed toward his daughter. The younger notary and a witness threw themselves before her and were knocked down; Piombo’s face glowed with madness. When Ginevra saw him approach she looked with triumph and slowly knelt.

“No, no! I cannot!” he cried, flinging the dagger away so it buried itself in the wainscot.

“Have mercy! Have pity!” she implored, kneeling. “You hesitate to be my death and refuse me life! Oh! father, never have I loved you as I do at this moment; give me Luigi! I ask your consent upon my knees: a daughter can humble herself before her father. Luigi, give me my Luigi, or I die!”

Her convulsions showed she hung between life and death. Bartolomeo roughly pushed her away.

“Go,” he said. “The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. I have not the strength to curse you, but I cast you off; you have no father. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here.” He pressed his heart. “Go, leave my house, unhappy girl.” He led her to the gate and silently shut her out.

“Luigi!” she cried, entering his humble lodging. “My Luigi, we have no other fortune than our love.”

“Then am I richer than the kings of the earth!” he cried.

“My father and my mother have cast me off,” she said sadly.

“I will love you in place of them,” he promised.

“Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!” she cried with a gayety that had something dreadful.

################################################
The day after Ginevra was driven from her father’s house she went to ask Madame Servin for asylum and protection until the period fixed by law for her marriage to Luigi.

Here began for her a hard apprenticeship to trouble. Madame Servin received her coldly, annoyed at the harm Ginevra’s affair had inflicted on her husband, and politely told her she must not count on future help. Too proud to insist yet amazed at this selfishness, the girl took a room in the lodging-house nearest Luigi’s. The son of the Portas spent all his days at the feet of his future wife; his youthful love and pure words dispersed the clouds from the mind of the banished daughter; the future he painted was so beautiful that she ended by smiling joyfully, though without forgetting her father’s severity.

One morning the lodging-house servant brought to Ginevra a number of trunks and packages containing stuffs, linen, clothes, and many articles necessary for a young wife setting up a home. In this welcome provision she recognized her mother’s foresight, and, on examining the gifts, she found a purse in which the baroness had put Ginevra’s money, adding her own savings. The purse was accompanied by a letter in which the mother implored her to forego the fatal marriage if still possible. It had cost her untold difficulty to send these few things; she entreated her daughter not to think her hard if she were henceforth forced to abandon her to want; she feared she could never again assist her; but she blessed and prayed for her happiness in this fatal marriage, if indeed she persisted, assuring her she should never cease to think of her darling child. Tears had effaced some words.

“Oh, mother!” cried Ginevra, deeply moved.

She felt the impulse to rush home, breathe the blessed air of her father’s house, fling herself at his feet, to see her mother. She was springing forward to accomplish this wish when Luigi entered. At the sight of him her filial emotion vanished; her tears stopped, and she no longer had the strength to abandon that loving and unfortunate youth. To be the sole hope of a noble being, to love him and then abandon him—such sacrifice is treachery young hearts cannot commit. Ginevra had the generosity to bury her own grief and suffering silently in her soul.

The marriage day arrived. Ginevra had no friend with her. While she was dressing, Luigi fetched the witnesses needed to sign the certificate. They were worthy men: one, a cavalry sergeant, was under obligations to Luigi contracted on the battlefield—obligations never obliterated from the heart of an honest man; the other, a master-mason, owned the house where the young couple had hired their future apartment. Each witness brought a friend, and all four, with Luigi, came to escort the bride. Little accustomed to social functions, and seeing only a matter of business, they were dressed in ordinary clothes, without luxury, and nothing about them denoted the usual joy of a marriage procession.

Ginevra herself was dressed simply, as befitted her fortunes; yet her beauty was so noble and imposing that the witnesses’ customary greetings died on their lips. They bowed with respect; she returned the bow; they did so in silence, looking at her with admiration. This reserve cast a chill over the whole party. Joy never bursts forth freely except among equals. Thus chance made all dull and grave around the bridal pair; nothing outwardly reflected the happiness within their hearts.

The church and the mayor’s office being near, Luigi and Ginevra, followed by the four required witnesses, walked the distance with a simplicity that stripped pomp from this greatest social event. They saw a crowd of waiting carriages in the mayor’s court-yard; in the great hall where civil marriages take place they found two other wedding-parties impatiently awaiting the mayor.

Ginevra sat beside Luigi at the end of a long bench; their witnesses remained standing for want of seats. Two brides, elaborately dressed in white with ribbons, laces, and pearls, crowned with orange-blossoms whose satiny petals nodded beneath their veils, were surrounded by joyous families and their mothers, to whom they looked up with content and timid eyes; all the faces reflected happiness and seemed to invoke blessings on the youthful pairs. Fathers, witnesses, brothers, and sisters went and came like a happy swarm disporting in the sun. Each seemed impressed with the value of this passing moment when the heart sits between two hopes—the wishes of the past, the promises of the future.

As she watched them, Ginevra’s heart swelled; she pressed Luigi’s arm and gave him a look. A tear rolled from the young Corsican’s eyes; never did he so well understand the joys his Ginevra was sacrificing to him. That precious tear caused her to forget all else but him—even the abandonment in which she sat. Love poured down its treasures of light upon their hearts; they saw nothing but themselves amid the joyous tumult; they were there alone in that crowd, as they were destined to be henceforth in life. Their witnesses, indifferent, conversed quietly on their own affairs.

Then they walked round the hall.

“How one loses time here,” said the mason, replacing a thick silver watch in his fob.

Luigi and Ginevra, sitting pressed to one another, seemed like one person. A poet would have admired their two heads inspired by the same sentiment, colored in the same tones, silent and saddened amid that humming happiness sparkling in diamonds and gay with flowers—a gaiety with something fleeting. The joy of those noisy, splendid groups was visible; that of Ginevra and Luigi was buried in their bosom. On one side the tumult of common pleasure, on the other the delicate silence of happy souls—earth and heaven!

But Ginevra was not wholly free from female weakness. Superstitious as an Italian, she saw an omen in this contrast, and felt a terror as invincible as her love.

Suddenly the office servant in the town livery opened a folding-door. Silence reigned, and his voice, like a dog’s yapping, called Monsieur Luigi da Porta and Mademoiselle Ginevra di Piombo. This caused some embarrassment. The celebrity of the bride’s name attracted attention, and spectators seemed to wonder that the wedding was not more sumptuous. Ginevra rose, took Luigi’s arm, and advanced firmly, followed by the witnesses. A murmur of surprise increased; a general whisper reminded her that all were wondering at the absence of her parents; her father’s wrath seemed present.

“Call in the families,” said the mayor to the clerk who read aloud the certificates.

“The father and mother protest,” replied the clerk, phlegmatically.

“On both sides?” inquired the mayor.

“The groom is an orphan.”

“Where are the witnesses?”

“Here,” said the clerk, pointing to the four men, who stood with arms folded like statues.

“But if the parents protest—” began the mayor.

“The respectful summons has been duly served,” replied the clerk, rising to lay before the mayor the papers annexed to the certificate.

This bureaucratic decision had something blighting; in a few words it contained the whole story. The hatreds of the Portas and the Piombos and their terrible passions were inscribed on this page of civil law as annals of a people are engraved on a tombstone. Ginevra trembled. Like the dove on the waters, having no resting place but the ark, she could take refuge only in Luigi’s eyes amid the cold waste around her.

The mayor assumed a stern, disapproving air, and his clerk looked up with malicious curiosity. No marriage was ever so little festal. Like other humans deprived of accessories, it became a simple act in itself, great only in thought.

After a few questions, to which the bride and bridegroom responded, and a few words mumbled by the mayor, and after signing the registers with their witnesses duly, Luigi and Ginevra were made one. Then the wedded pair walked back through two lines of joyous relations who did not belong to them, and whose only interest in their marriage was the delay caused to their own weddings by this gloomy bridal. When Ginevra finally found herself in the mayor’s court-yard, under the open sky, a sigh escaped her.

“Can a lifetime of devotion and love suffice to prove my gratitude for your courage and tenderness, my Ginevra?” said Luigi.

At these words, spoken with tears of joy, the bride forgot her sufferings; she had indeed suffered to appear before the public to obtain a happiness her parents refused to sanction.

“Why should others come between us?” she said with artless feeling that delighted Luigi.

A sense of accomplished happiness lightened the young pair’s step; they saw neither heaven nor earth nor houses; they flew, as it were, on wings to the church. When they reached a dark little chapel in one corner of the building and stood before a plain undecorated altar, an old priest married them. There, as in the mayor’s office, two other marriages were taking place, still pursuing them with pomp. The church, filled with friends and relations, echoed with the roll of carriages and the hum of beadles, sextons, and priests. Altars glowed with sacramental luxury; the wreaths of orange-flowers that crowned the Virgin were fresh. Flowers, incense, gleaming tapers, velvet cushions embroidered with gold were everywhere. When it came to hold above Luigi and Ginevra the symbol of eternal union—that yoke of satin, white, soft, brilliant, light for some, lead for most—the priest looked about in vain for the acolytes whose place it was to perform that joyous function. Two of the witnesses fulfilled it. The priest addressed a hasty homily on life’s perils and duties, throwing in an indirect reproach to Ginevra for her parents’ absence; then, after uniting them before God as the mayor had before the law, he left the married couple.

“God bless them!” said Vergniaud, the sergeant, to the mason at the church porch. “No two creatures were ever more fitted for one another. The parents are foolish. I don’t know a braver soldier than Colonel Luigi. If the whole army had behaved like him, ‘l’autre’ would be here still.”

This blessing of the old soldier, the only one bestowed on their marriage-day, shed a balm on Ginevra’s heart.

They parted with hearty handshakes; Luigi thanked his landlord.

“Adieu, ‘mon brave,’” he said to the sergeant. “I thank you.”

“I am now and ever at your service, colonel—soul, body, horses, and carriages; all that is mine is yours.”

“How he loves you!” said Ginevra.

Luigi hurried his bride to the house they were to occupy. Their modest apartment was soon reached; and there, when the door closed upon them, Luigi took his wife in his arms, exclaiming,—

“Oh, my Ginevra! for now you are mine, here is our true wedding. Here,” he added, “all things will smile upon us.”

Together they toured the three rooms of their lodging. The first served as salon and dining-room; on the right was a bedchamber; on the left a large study Luigi had arranged for his wife; in it she found easels, color-boxes, lay-figures, casts, pictures, portfolios—in short, an artist’s paraphernalia.

“So here I am to work!” she said with childlike happiness.

She looked long at the hangings and furniture, turning again and again to thank Luigi, for there was something approaching magnificence in the little retreat. A bookcase held her favorite books; a piano filled an angle. She sat on a divan, drew Luigi to her side, and said, caressing his hand,—

“You have good taste.”

“Those words make me happy,” he replied.

“But let me see all,” said Ginevra, to whom Luigi had made a mystery of the adornment.

They entered the nuptial chamber, fresh and white as a virgin.

“Oh! come away,” said Luigi, smiling.

“But I wish to see all.”

The imperious Ginevra looked at each piece of furniture with the minute care of an antiquary examining a coin; she touched the silken hangings and went over every article with the artless satisfaction of a bride inspecting the treasures of her wedding outfit.

“We begin by ruining ourselves,” she said in a half-joyous, half-anxious tone.

“True! for all my back pay is there,” replied Luigi. “I have mortgaged it to a worthy fellow named Gigonnet.”

“Why did you do so?” she asked reproachfully, though inwardly pleased. “Do you believe I should be less happy in a garret? But,” she added, “it is all charming—and it is ours!”

Luigi looked at her with such enthusiasm that she lowered her eyes.

“Now let us see the rest,” she cried.

Above these three rooms, under the roof, were Luigi’s study, a kitchen, and a servant’s room. Ginevra was pleased with her little domain, though the view from the windows was limited by a high neighboring wall, and the court-yard, from which their light came, was gloomy. But the two lovers were so happy that hope adorned their future, and they chose to see nothing but what was charming in their hidden nest. In that vast house, lost in the immensity of Paris, they were like two pearls in their shell in the depths of the ocean; to others it might seem a prison; to them it was paradise.

The first few days of their union were given to love. The effort to work at once was too difficult; they could not resist their passion. Luigi lay for hours at his wife’s feet, admiring the color of her hair, the moulding of her forehead, the enchanting sockets of her eyes, the purity and whiteness of the two arches beneath which she turned her eyes slowly, expressing the happiness of satisfied love. Ginevra caressed Luigi’s hair, never weary of gazing at his “belta folgorante,” and the delicacy of his features. She was constantly charmed by the nobility of his manners, as she herself attracted him by her grace.

They played together like children with nothings—nothings that brought them back to their love—ceasing their play only to fall into reveries of far niente. An air sung by Ginevra reproduced to their souls the lights and shadows of their passion. Together, uniting their steps as they did their souls, they roamed the country, finding their love everywhere—in flowers, in the sky, in the glowing tints of sunset; they read it in the capricious vapors that met and struggled in the ether. Each day resembled none before; their love increased because it was true. They had tested each other in what seemed only a short time; instinctively they recognized that their souls were of a kind whose inexhaustible riches promised unceasing joys.

Theirs was love in all its artlessness, with interminable conversations, unfinished speeches, long silences, oriental reposes, and ardor. Luigi and Ginevra comprehended love. Love is like the ocean: seen superficially or in haste it seems monotonous to common souls, whereas some privileged beings can pass their lives admiring it and finding ceaselessly varying phenomena that enchant them.

Soon, however, prudence and foresight drew the young couple from their Eden; it was necessary to work to live. Ginevra, with a talent for imitating old paintings, took up copying and soon found many customers among picture-dealers. Luigi, whose talents had been restricted to strategy, sought long and actively for occupation, but it was hard for a young officer in Paris.

At last, weary of vain efforts and despairing at seeing the whole burden fall on Ginevra, he made use of his excellent handwriting. With persistency he went among lawyers and notaries asking for papers to copy. The frankness of his manners and his situation interested many; he soon obtained enough work to need assistants, and this employment became little by little a regular business. The profits of his office and the sale of Ginevra’s pictures gave the young couple a competence they were justly proud of, for it was the fruit of their industry.

This, to the busy pair, was the happiest period of their lives. Days flowed rapidly, filled with occupation and the joys of love. At night, after working all day, they met with delight in Ginevra’s studio. Music refreshed their weariness. No expression of regret or melancholy clouded the young wife’s features; she never complained. She appeared to Luigi with a smile and eyes beaming. Each cherished a ruling thought that would have made them take pleasure in still severer labor: Ginevra worked for Luigi, and Luigi for Ginevra.

Sometimes, in his absence, Ginevra imagined the perfect happiness they might have had if this life of love could have been lived in her parents’ presence; the thought of remorse dropped her into melancholy; mournful pictures passed like shadows across her imagination; she saw her old father alone, or her mother weeping in secret lest the inexorable Piombo perceive her tears. The two white, solemn heads rose before her, and she felt that never again should she see them except in memory. This thought pursued her like a presentiment.

She celebrated the anniversary of her marriage by giving her husband a portrait he had long desired—the portrait of his Ginevra, painted by herself. Never had the young artist done so remarkable a work. Aside from the resemblance, the glow of her beauty, the purity of her feelings, the happiness of love were there depicted by a sort of magic. This masterpiece of her art and joy was a votive offering to their wedded felicity.

Another year of ease and comfort went by. The history of their life may be given in three words: They were happy. No event happened to them of sufficient importance to be recorded.

At the beginning of 1819 the picture-dealers demanded something other than copies; competition was such that they could not sell Ginevra’s work. Madame Porta regretted neglecting “genre” painting, which might have earned her reputation; she tried portraiture but faced many painters poorer than she. Luigi and Ginevra had saved a little, so they were not yet anxious.

Toward the end of winter Luigi worked without respite. He, too, battled rivals; payment for writing had fallen so low that he could no longer employ help and had to labor longer for the same wage. Ginevra had finished several strong pictures, but dealers bought for reputation; her works had little chance. She offered them to dealers and failed.

Household peril loomed. Their happiness floated; love overwhelmed them with its treasures, and poverty rose like a skeleton amid their joy. They hid secret anxieties: when Ginevra felt like weeping at Luigi’s worn, suffering face, she redoubled her caresses; Luigi concealed his forebodings and expressed tenderer love. Feeling was exalted to compensate; words, joys, and caresses grew frenzied. Each feared the future; each clung to hope and deceived the other with false cheer.

One night Ginevra woke and missed Luigi. A faint light on the opposite courtyard wall showed him writing in his study; he now waited till she slept to go to his garret and write. Four o’clock struck. She pretended to sleep. When Luigi returned, exhausted, she looked sadly at his worn face, where toil had begun to write wrinkles.

“It is for me he spends his nights,” she thought, weeping—then resolved to imitate him. That day, using a letter of recommendation from Elie Magus, one of her dealers, she got an order for coloring lithographs. She painted by day and colored engravings at night, sharing Luigi’s clandestine labor. They entered their nuptial bed only to deceive each other; both feigned sleep, and each left the bed as soon as the other seemed asleep.

One night Luigi, burning with feverish fatigue, opened his garret casement for air and saw the reflection of Ginevra’s lamp on the opposite wall. He went down softly and surprised her coloring engravings.

“Oh, Ginevra!” he cried.

She rose, convulsively blushing.

“Could I sleep while you wear yourself out?” she asked.

“But to me alone belongs the right to work in this way,” he answered.

“Could I be idle,” she asked with tears, “when every mouthful we eat costs a drop of your blood? I should die if I could not add my efforts to yours. All should be in common between us: pains and pleasures.”

“She is cold!” Luigi cried in despair. “Wrap your shawl closer, my Ginevra; the night is damp.”

They went to the window; she leaned on his breast, he held her around the waist, and together in deep silence they gazed at the sky brightening toward dawn. Clouds moved quickly; the East grew luminous.

“See!” said Ginevra. “It is an omen. We shall be happy.”

“Yes, in heaven,” Luigi replied with a bitter smile. “Oh, Ginevra! you who deserved all the treasures on earth—”

“I have your heart,” she said joyfully.

“Ah! I complain no more!” he answered, drawing her close and kissing her thin face, which consoled him.

“What silence!” Ginevra murmured. “I take pleasure in sitting up. The majesty of Night awes and inspires. All sleep, I wake.”

“Oh, my Ginevra,” he cried, “it is not to-night alone I feel how delicately moulded is your soul. But see, dawn is here; come and sleep.”

“Yes,” she said, “if I do not sleep alone. I suffered too much the night I first discovered you waking while I slept.”

Their courage was briefly rewarded; then an event that often crowns household happiness proved fatal. Ginevra had a son, “as beautiful as the day.” Motherhood doubled her strength. Luigi borrowed to meet confinement expenses. At first the new burden hardly seemed a burden; they surrendered to the joy of possessing a child. It was their last happiness.

Like swimmers uniting to breast a current, they struggled; sometimes apathy resembling the sleep before death overcame them. Soon they had to sell jewels. Poverty appeared—not hideous, but plainly clothed, almost tolerable; its voice neither terrifying nor raving, it made them lose comforts and dried pride’s springs. Then, before they knew it, came want in all its horror, indifferent to rags, treading underfoot human sentiments.

Seven or eight months after little Bartolomeo’s birth, it would have been hard to recognize the mother who nursed her sickly babe as the original of the beautiful portrait—the sole remaining ornament. Without fire through a hard winter, Ginevra’s graceful outlines were destroyed; her cheeks grew white as porcelain, her eyes dulled as if life’s springs were drying. Watching her shrunken, discolored child, her only suffering was his misery; Luigi had no courage to smile on his son.

“I have wandered over Paris,” he said one day. “I know no one; can I ask strangers? Vergniaud, my old sergeant, is involved in a conspiracy and imprisoned; besides, he has lent all he could. As for our landlord, he has not asked rent in over a year.”

“But we are not in want,” replied Ginevra gently, affecting calm.

“Every hour brings new difficulty,” Luigi answered in terror.

Another day Luigi sold Ginevra’s paintings, her portrait, and the few household articles they could spare for a miserable sum, prolonging their agony. In those days of wretchedness Ginevra showed the sublimity of her nature and her resignation.

Stoically she bore misery’s blows; her strong soul upheld her; she worked with an unfaltering hand beside her dying son, performed household duties with marvelous activity, and sufficed for all. She was even happy when she saw Luigi’s surprised smile at the cleanliness she produced in their one poor room.

“Dear, I kept this bit of bread for you,” she said one evening when he returned, worn out.

“And you?”

“I? I have dined, dear Luigi; I want nothing more.”

The tender look on her beseeching face urged him more than words to take the food she had denied herself.

Luigi kissed her with one of those kisses of despair given in 1793 between friends mounting a scaffold. In such moments two beings see each other heart to heart. Comprehending suddenly that his wife was starving, Luigi was seized by the fever consuming her. He shuddered and went out, pretending business called, for he would rather have drunk poison than secure life by eating the last morsel of bread left in their home.

He wandered wildly about Paris amid flaunting luxury, hurried past windows where gold glittered. At last he resolved to sell himself as a substitute for military service, hoping the sacrifice would save Ginevra and that her father would take her home during his absence.

He went to an agent and found an old officer of the Imperial guard. “It’s two days since I have eaten,” he said in a slow, weak voice. “My wife is dying of hunger and has never complained; she will die smiling, I think. For God’s sake, comrade,” he added bitterly, “buy me in advance; I am robust; I am no longer in the service, and I—”

The officer gave Luigi a sum on account. The wretched man laughed convulsively as he grasped the gold and ran breathless home, crying, “Ginevra! Oh, my Ginevra!”

It was almost night when he reached their room. He entered softly, fearing to shock his wife, whom he had left so weak. The last rays of the sun faded from Ginevra’s face as she sat sleeping in her chair, holding her child on her breast.

“Wake, my dear one,” he said, not seeing the infant, who at that moment shone with a strange pallor.

Hearing his voice, the poor mother opened her eyes, met Luigi’s look, and smiled; but Luigi cried out in horror; he scarcely recognized his wife, half mad. With a savage gesture he showed her the gold. Ginevra laughed mechanically, then cried in a dreadful voice, “The child, Luigi, he is cold!”

She looked at her son and swooned. The little Bartolomeo was dead. Luigi took his wife in his arms, the child still clasped to her; after laying her on the bed he went out to seek help.

“Oh! my God!” he cried as he met his landlord on the stairs. “I have gold, gold, and my child has died of hunger, and his mother is dying, too! Help me!”

He returned like one distraught to his wife, leaving the mason and neighbors to gather a few things for needs they had been too proud to reveal.

Luigi had cast his gold upon the floor and knelt by the bed. Ginevra, delirious, murmured, “Father! take care of my son, who bears your name.”

“Oh, my angel! be calm,” Luigi said, kissing her; “our good days are coming back.”

“My Luigi,” she said, looking at him with extraordinary attention, “listen. I feel that I am dying. My death is natural; I suffered too much; besides, such happiness as mine must be paid for. Yes, Luigi, be comforted. I have been so happy that if I lived again I would accept our fate. I am a bad mother; I regret you more than my child—My child!” she added hollowly.

Two tears escaped her dying eyes, and suddenly she pressed the little body she could not warm.

“Give my hair to my father, in memory of his Ginevra,” she said. “Tell him I have never blamed him.”

Her head fell on her husband’s arm.

“No, you cannot die!” cried Luigi. “The doctor is coming. We have food. Your father will take you home. Prosperity is here. Stay with us, angel!”

Her faithful heart, so full of love, grew cold. Ginevra turned her eyes to him she loved, though she was conscious of nothing else. Confused images passed before her; memory of earth faded. She knew Luigi was there and clasped his hand tightly as if to hold herself from some precipice.

“Dear,” she said at last, “you are cold; I will warm you.”

She tried to put his hand upon her heart, but died.

Two doctors, a priest, and neighbors came into the room, bringing what might save the poor couple and calm despair. They made some noise entering; afterwards an awful silence filled the room.

While that scene took place, Bartolomeo Piombo and his wife sat in their antique chairs at the corners of a vast fireplace in their salon. A friend had just left; the chair he had occupied remained between the two Corsicans. The clock told midnight. The old couple had lost the ability to sleep; they sat silent, like persons in their dotage, gazing about at things they did not see. Their deserted salon, filled with memories, was feebly lit by a single expiring lamp. Without the hearth’s spark they might soon be in total darkness.

Piombo cast glances at the empty chair—glances full of thoughts and remorse—for the seat was Ginevra’s. Elisa Piombo watched expressions cross her husband’s pallid face; though long used to divining his feelings, tonight they seemed threatening and melancholy, making his soul incomprehensible even to her.

Would Bartolomeo yield at last to the memories that chair awakened? Had he been shocked to see a stranger occupy it, used for the first time since his daughter left? Had the hour of his mercy struck—the hour Elisa had vainly prayed and waited for?

These thoughts shook the mother. For an instant her husband’s countenance became so terrible she trembled at having used this simple means to bring up Ginevra’s name. The wintry night blew snow sharply against the blinds; the old woman dropped her head to hide tears. Suddenly a sigh burst from the old man; he seemed crushed. She risked speaking—for the second time in three years—of his daughter.

“Ginevra may be cold,” she said softly.

Piombo quivered.

“She may be hungry,” she continued.

The old man dropped a tear.

“Perhaps she has a child and cannot suckle it; her milk is dried up!” said the mother in despair.

“Let her come! let her come to me!” cried Piombo. “Oh! my precious child, thou hast conquered me.”

The mother rose as if to fetch her daughter. At that instant the door opened noisily, and a man, his face no longer human, stood before them.

“Dead! Our two families were doomed to exterminate each other. Here is all that remains of her,” he said, laying Ginevra’s long black hair upon the table.

The old people shook as if struck by lightning.

Luigi no longer stood before them.

“He has spared me a shot, for he is dead,” Bartolomeo slowly said, gazing at the ground.
