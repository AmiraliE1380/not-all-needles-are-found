In October 1800, a foreigner named Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and young daughter. The man, appearing over sixty with a serious demeanor, was deeply troubled, while his wife, despite her sadness, tried to maintain a calm facade for their daughter. The family’s despair drew the attention of passersby, but the stranger's fierce gaze quickly deterred any sympathy.

After a moment of contemplation, Bartolomeo made a desperate decision. He handed a dagger to his wife, declaring he would confront the Bonapartes. He approached the palace but was stopped by a soldier. Despite the soldier's refusal to let him in without a written request, Bartolomeo insisted, leading to a tense standoff. Fortunately, Lucien Bonaparte, the minister of the interior, arrived and recognized Bartolomeo, taking him inside to meet his brother, Napoleon Bonaparte.

Inside, Napoleon dismissed his aides to speak privately with Bartolomeo. The Corsican sought asylum, revealing he had killed the Portas, a family that had wronged him by burning his vineyard and killing his son. He recounted how he had avenged his son’s death by killing all seven Portas, which shocked Napoleon. Bartolomeo expressed despair at the indifference of the Bonaparte brothers to his plight, reminding them of his past protection of their family.

Napoleon, now a leader of a nation, felt bound by law and could not openly support Bartolomeo’s actions. However, he acknowledged the enduring tradition of vendetta in Corsica and offered Bartolomeo a chance to live in Paris, promising to help him financially. He warned Bartolomeo that the law would not tolerate personal justice in the city.

Bartolomeo accepted Napoleon's offer, pledging loyalty to the Bonaparte brothers. He expressed gratitude for their recognition of his misfortune and seemed hopeful about his future in Paris. Napoleon, seeing Bartolomeo as a potential ally, hinted at needing trustworthy friends in the future.

After their meeting, Lucien inquired about Bartolomeo’s financial situation, learning that he and his family had arrived in Paris with nothing. Lucien generously offered him money and promised to help secure their comfort. The value of Bartolomeo’s property in Corsica would not suffice to support his family in Paris.

Fifteen years passed since Bartolomeo's arrival in Paris, leading to events that would unfold, requiring the context of his earlier struggles and decisions.

CHAPTER II. THE STUDIO

Servin, a distinguished artist, was the first to establish a studio for young girls interested in painting. At around forty years old, he was a man of high morals, devoted to his art, and married to the dowerless daughter of a general. Initially, mothers brought their daughters to the studio, but soon they felt comfortable sending them alone, reassured by Servin's principles and dedication.

Servin aimed to teach only young ladies from wealthy families to avoid complaints about class composition. He even declined to accept girls aspiring to be professional artists, as he would have to provide them with essential instructions. His careful approach and the quality of his teaching earned him a stellar reputation among the aristocracy. When a girl expressed interest in drawing, mothers would invariably recommend Servin.

He became synonymous with feminine art, much like Herbault for bonnets or Leroy for gowns. A young woman who studied under him was seen as capable of critiquing museum paintings, creating striking portraits, or painting genre scenes. Despite his connections with Paris's elite, Servin maintained an independent and patriotic stance, characterized by a witty, half-ironic tone.

The studio was meticulously arranged for his pupils. The entrance to the attic above his apartment was sealed, requiring a small spiral staircase to access it. The studio itself was expansive, with abundant light from large glass panes covered by green linen shades. The walls, painted dark gray, were adorned with caricatures and models in plaster, creating a unique blend of artistic chaos and beauty.

On a bright July day, the studio was alive with young girls at their easels, each displaying a range of expressions and styles. Among them was a modest, simply dressed girl, Ginevra Piombo, who sat apart from her peers, seemingly anxious. The group was divided into two cliques: one comprised daughters of wealthy merchants, while the other included aristocrats led by Amelie Thirion, who was vain and pretentious.

Despite their differences, the girls were engaged in their art, laughing and chatting, creating a lively atmosphere. Ginevra, the most talented and beautiful, was the target of jealousy from the aristocratic group. Amelie, seeking to ostracize Ginevra, moved her easel away from the others, igniting whispers among the girls about the insult.

The political climate of the time, following the second return of the Bourbons, heightened tensions among the girls. Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, faced disdain from her peers, who came from royalist families. Amelie’s actions were not only a personal affront but also a reflection of the political divisions of the era.

When Ginevra arrived, she was met with silence, a stark contrast to her usual warm reception. Her beauty and grace commanded respect, but her political beliefs made her a target. Despite the cold reception, she remained focused on her work, unaware of the tension surrounding her.

As the day progressed, Ginevra's attention was drawn to a mysterious sound from behind a partition. She discovered that Servin was hiding a political fugitive, an officer of the Imperial Guard. This revelation filled her with both excitement and concern for the man's safety.

Ginevra resolved to protect the fugitive, believing it was better for her to be near him than to risk his exposure. Her indifference to Amelie’s slight only fueled the jealousy of her peers, who were baffled by her calm demeanor.

Madame Servin's arrival interrupted the brewing conflict. She announced that her husband could not attend that day, and her demeanor suggested she was aware of the hidden man. Ginevra, sensing the tension, began to hum a Corsican song to mask the sound of the fugitive's breathing.

As the session continued, Ginevra remained focused on her work, sketching the officer's head with a fervor that surprised her. Servin praised her talent, and the other girls, including Amelie, were drawn to her work. Ginevra's determination to keep the fugitive's presence a secret became evident, and she skillfully deflected attention from her sketch.

Amelie, suspicious of Ginevra, attempted to uncover the mystery but was thwarted by Ginevra's quick thinking. The rivalry between the two intensified, with Ginevra's talent and composure only heightening Amelie's jealousy.

Servin, aware of the tension, engaged with each pupil, but his attention remained on Ginevra. Their shared understanding of the situation deepened their bond, and Ginevra felt empowered to assist Servin in protecting the fugitive.

As the day ended, Ginevra's resolve to keep the secret grew stronger, and she remained determined to help the fugitive escape the dangers outside. The political turmoil of the time loomed over the studio, but within its walls, the girls navigated their rivalries and friendships, all while art remained their shared passion.

When the painter and Ginevra believed they were alone, Servin knocked on the door of the dark garret, revealing a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, his arm in a sling and his face pale from pain. The officer recoiled upon seeing Ginevra, but Servin reassured him, introducing her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo. Ginevra blocked his path as he attempted to leave. "Can you restore the Emperor?" she asked. "Do you expect to raise that giant who could not maintain himself?"

"What can I do?" he replied, feeling utterly alone after losing his protector. He expressed his willingness to die, believing that an honest man's life was worth more than that of two traitors. He contemplated how to sell his life to the executioner.

Alarmed by his despair, Ginevra offered her savings, explaining that her father was wealthy and would not blame her for helping a soldier. She urged him to wait until the storm passed, suggesting he could escape and serve elsewhere.

Her words, filled with maternal grace and hope, touched the young man deeply. He admired her beauty and felt a rush of emotions. "You are an angel of goodness," he said, but his thoughts quickly returned to Labedoyere.

In silence, they shared a moment of understanding, their brief acquaintance deepening into a bond. Servin asked if he could save Labedoyere, to which the officer replied he could only avenge him. Ginevra felt a surge of emotion at the thought of vengeance, recognizing the officer's devotion to Napoleon.

As the sun set, illuminating the officer's face, Ginevra felt a magical connection to him, recalling her childhood memories. She was captivated by his presence, and as she left, she smiled at him, igniting a spark of hope.

The next day, Ginevra returned to the studio, where the officer, now named Luigi, shared stories of his experiences during the retreat from Moscow and the disaster at Waterloo. Ginevra listened intently, her heart swelling with admiration. They communicated in Corsican, forging a deeper connection.

As days passed, Ginevra's classmates began to notice her absence from social gatherings, attributing it to her relationship with Luigi. One day, Laure, a fellow student, confided in Ginevra that her mother insisted she leave the studio due to the rumors surrounding Ginevra and Luigi. Ginevra was shocked but reassured Laure that they would meet again. Laure expressed her sadness at leaving, and Ginevra felt a pang of loss.

As Ginevra and Luigi's bond deepened, they shared unspoken feelings of love, each recognizing the other's worth. One evening, Luigi signaled to Ginevra, but upon seeing Laure, he retreated. Ginevra, unaware of the tension, engaged Laure in conversation, only to learn that her classmates had left due to the scandal.

Ginevra confronted Servin about the situation, revealing that the young ladies had spread falsehoods about her relationship with Luigi. Servin dismissed the rumors, focusing instead on his upcoming exhibition. Luigi entered, jokingly claiming responsibility for Servin's lost students, and the painter playfully suggested they marry.

Their silence spoke volumes, marking the first acknowledgment of their love. Servin encouraged their union, emphasizing the joy of love. Ginevra proposed to help Luigi secure a pardon from the authorities, confident in her father's influence.

Meanwhile, Ginevra's parents grew anxious about her late return home. Bartolomeo, her father, paced the room, concerned for his daughter. He and his wife, though accustomed to her late hours, felt a sense of unease. Bartolomeo, a man of integrity and strong character, had always prioritized his family's well-being over societal expectations.

As Ginevra's parents awaited her return, they reflected on their close-knit family dynamic, built on love and mutual respect. Ginevra's upbringing had instilled in her a strong sense of loyalty and passion, traits she shared with her father. Their bond was deepened by shared political beliefs and a commitment to the Emperor.

When Ginevra finally arrived home, her father rushed to embrace her, relieved to see her safe. The family shared a moment of warmth, their love evident in their interactions. Ginevra's heart swelled with affection for her parents, knowing they were her greatest supporters.

In the studio, Ginevra and Luigi's relationship blossomed amidst the challenges they faced. Their love, forged in adversity, became a source of strength, uniting them against the world. As they navigated the complexities of their lives, they remained steadfast in their commitment to one another, embodying the resilience of their shared ideals.

CHAPTER IV. LOVE

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!” Ginevra exclaimed, but was quickly set down with respect. She assured her mother, who was startled, that it was merely a trick to escape.

The baroness regained her composure, and Piombo, rubbing his hands, showed his joy, a habit he had developed at court. The two old parents resembled a revived plant after a long drought.

“Now, to dinner!” Piombo called, offering his hand to Ginevra, who smiled in response.

“Your mother mentioned you’ve been spending more time at the studio. It seems painting is more important to you than us,” Piombo remarked.

“Oh, father!” Ginevra protested.

“Ginevra is preparing a surprise for us,” her mother suggested.

“A picture of your own! Will you show us?” Piombo asked eagerly.

“Yes, I am very busy at the studio,” Ginevra replied slowly.

“Are you alright, Ginevra? You look pale!” her mother exclaimed.

“No! It shall never be said that Ginevra Piombo acted a lie,” she declared.

Her parents exchanged astonished glances. “I love a young man,” she confessed, lowering her eyelids to hide her emotions.

“Is he a prince?” Piombo asked ironically, causing his wife to tremble.

“No, father, he is a young man without fortune,” she replied gently.

“Is he handsome?” he pressed.

“He is very unfortunate,” she said.

“What is he?” Piombo inquired.

“Labedoyere’s comrade; he was proscribed and hidden by Servin,” she explained.

“Servin is a good fellow,” Piombo acknowledged, “but you do wrong to love any man but your father.”

“It does not depend on me to love or not love,” Ginevra replied softly.

“I thought my Ginevra would remain faithful to me until I die,” Piombo lamented. “I did not expect our love to find a rival.”

“Did I ever reproach you for your devotion to Napoleon?” Ginevra countered. “You left me for months on missions. I endured your absence. Life has necessities.”

“Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed.

“You don’t love me for myself; your reproaches reveal your egotism,” she said.

“Dare you blame your father’s love?” he shouted, his eyes flashing.

“Father, I don’t blame you,” she replied gently. “You have grounds for your egotism, as I have for my love. I have never left your side for fifteen years, and it has been a pleasure to charm your life. Is it ingratitude to desire a husband who will protect me?”

“What! You reckon benefits with your father?” Piombo asked dangerously.

A dreadful silence followed. Bartolomeo finally cried out, “Oh! stay with us! I cannot bear to have you love another man. Ginevra, you will not have long to await your liberty.”

“But, father, I need not leave you; we will both love you. You will come to know the man to whom you bequeath me,” she said.

“Oh! Ginevra, why did you not marry when Napoleon offered?” Piombo lamented.

“They loved me to order,” she replied. “I did not wish to leave you alone.”

“You don’t wish to leave me alone, yet you marry! I know you, my daughter; you would cease to love us,” he said.

Elisa, the baroness, remained motionless, as if stunned. “We have no longer a daughter; she wishes to marry,” Piombo declared.

He sat down, raising his hands to heaven, bowed down by sorrow. Ginevra, touched by his restraint, expected a violent outburst but was met with gentleness.

“Father,” she said tenderly, “you shall never be abandoned by your Ginevra. Love me for my own sake. If you knew how he loves me! He would never make me unhappy!”

“Comparisons already!” Piombo shouted. “I cannot endure the thought of your marriage. If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me; if he does not love you, I would kill him.”

Ginevra alone could withstand his gaze, her own eyes aflame. “Oh! to love you! What man is worthy of such a life?” Piombo continued. “Who is worthy to be your husband?”

“He,” Ginevra replied, “he of whom I am not worthy.”

“He?” Piombo repeated. “Who is he?”

“He whom I love,” she said.

“How can he know you enough to love you?” he asked.

“Father,” she said impatiently, “whether he loves me or not, if I love him—”

“You love him?” Piombo interrupted.

Ginevra nodded softly.

“You love him more than you love us?” he pressed.

“The two feelings cannot be compared,” she replied.

“Is one stronger than the other?” he asked.

“I think it is,” she admitted.

“You shall not marry him,” Piombo declared, shaking the window panes with his voice.

“I shall marry him,” Ginevra replied calmly.

“Oh, God!” cried her mother. “How will this quarrel end? Santa Virgina! Place yourself between them!”

Piombo, now seated, wore an icy sternness. He looked at Ginevra and said gently, “No! You will not marry him. Say nothing more tonight—let me think otherwise. Do you wish to see your father on his knees, his white hairs prostrate before you? I supplicate you—”

“Ginevra Piombo does not break her word,” she replied. “I am your daughter.”

“She is right,” the baroness said. “We are sent into the world to marry.”

“Do you encourage her in disobedience?” Piombo asked, turning to his wife, who was now petrified.

“Refusing to obey an unjust order is not disobedience,” Ginevra asserted.

“No order can be unjust from your father’s lips,” Piombo replied. “Why do you judge my actions? My repugnance is counsel from on high, perhaps to protect you from great evil.”

“The only evil would be if he did not love me.”

“Always he!” Piombo exclaimed.

“Yes, always,” she affirmed. “He is my life, my good, my thought. Even if I obeyed you, he would remain in my soul. To forbid me to marry him is to make me hate you.”

“You love us not!” Piombo shouted.

“Oh!” Ginevra shook her head.

“Then forget him; be faithful to us. After we are gone—you understand?”

“Father, do you wish me to long for your death?” she asked.

“I shall outlive you. Children who do not honor their parents die early,” he warned.

“All the more reason I should marry and be happy,” she replied.

This coolness and argumentation troubled Piombo, causing blood to rush to his head. Ginevra shuddered and sprang onto his knee, embracing him. “Oh, yes, yes, let me die first! I could never survive you, my father!”

“Oh! my Ginevra, my own Ginevra!” Piombo replied, his anger melting under her affection.

“It was time you ceased,” the baroness said, trembling.

“Poor mother!” Ginevra said.

“Ah! Ginevretta! mia bella Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed, playing with her as if she were a child. They shared moments of joy, but Ginevra’s attempts to introduce Luigi were met with playful refusals from her father.

The next day, Ginevra was more affectionate toward her father, expressing gratitude as if to thank him for his apparent consent. That evening, she sang and played for him, exclaiming they needed a man’s voice for the nocturne.

A week later, her mother signaled her to come closer. “I have persuaded your father to receive him,” Elisa whispered.

“Oh! mother, how happy you have made me!” Ginevra replied.

That day, Ginevra joyfully returned home on Luigi’s arm. The officer emerged from hiding for the second time. Ginevra’s appeals to the Duc de Feltre had succeeded; Luigi’s name was restored on the officer’s list. This was a significant step forward. Warned of her father’s potential disapproval, Luigi felt anxious about meeting him. Ginevra sensed his fear, which only deepened her love.

“How pale you are!” she remarked as they reached her home.

“Oh! Ginevra, if it concerned my life only—”

Though Bartolomeo had been informed of the formal introduction, he remained seated, his stern expression intimidating.

“Father,” Ginevra said, “I bring you someone you will be pleased to see—a soldier who fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean.”

Piombo rose, casting a sidelong glance at Luigi. “Monsieur is not decorated,” he remarked sarcastically.

“I no longer wear the Legion of Honor,” Luigi replied timidly.

Ginevra, embarrassed by her father’s rudeness, pulled out a chair for Luigi. The officer’s response seemed to satisfy Piombo. Madame Piombo, noticing her husband’s demeanor softening, attempted to engage in conversation.

“Monsieur’s resemblance to someone we knew in Corsica, Nina Porta, is surprising,” she noted.

“Nothing could be more natural,” Luigi replied. “Nina was my sister.”

“Are you Luigi Porta?” Piombo asked.

“Yes,” Luigi confirmed.

Bartolomeo staggered, needing support from his wife. The two old parents left the room in silence, abandoning their daughter in a state of horror.

Luigi, bewildered, looked at Ginevra, who had turned pale, staring at the door through which her parents had exited. The solemnity of the moment filled her with fear. “What misery in a word!” she exclaimed.

“In the name of our love, what have I said?” Luigi asked.

“My father never spoke of our tragic history, and I was too young to know,” Ginevra explained.

“Are we in vendetta?” Luigi asked, trembling.

“Yes. My mother said the Portas killed my brother and burned our house. My father then massacred their family. How did you survive? You were tied to the bed before they set fire to the house.”

“I do not know,” Luigi replied. “I was taken to Genoa at six and raised by an old man named Colonna. I was told I had enemies and advised to take Luigi as my surname to evade them.”

“Go, go, Luigi!” Ginevra urged. “Stay here; you are safe in my father’s house, but once you leave, danger awaits! My father has two Corsicans in his service, and they will hunt you.”

“Ginevra,” he said, “does this vendetta exist between us?”

She smiled sadly and nodded. “Oh, Luigi, our love must be pure and sincere to give me strength for the path ahead. But it promises happiness for our lives, does it not?”

Luigi smiled and squeezed her hand. Ginevra realized that true love transcends superficial expressions. Their destiny was sealed; she foresaw a difficult struggle but felt no inclination to abandon Luigi. With determination, she pulled him from her father’s house and ensured he reached his modest lodging.

Upon returning home, Ginevra felt a calm resolve. She greeted her parents, who were preparing for dinner, with gentle eyes, noting her mother’s tear-stained face. They all rose from the table in silence, unable to speak.

In the gloomy salon, Piombo attempted to speak but faltered, his strength failing him. He rang for a servant. “Pietro, light the fire; I am cold,” he ordered.

Ginevra trembled, watching her father’s internal struggle. Both were suffering in silence, nursing their pride and anger. The love that once brought them joy now felt like a burden. Ginevra’s gaze at her father was filled with reproach, while Piombo’s eyes reflected a deep-seated pain.

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother, desperate to mend the rift, gathered them in Bartolomeo’s study. Ginevra sensed her mother’s intentions and smiled sadly.

At that moment, two notaries arrived with witnesses. Bartolomeo eyed them warily, sensing a confrontation. The older notary introduced himself and began to discuss Ginevra’s marriage to Luigi Porta.

“Monsieur le baron, we have come to fulfill the law regarding your daughter’s marriage,” he stated, but Piombo’s anger was palpable.

“Are there laws in France that destroy paternal authority?” he demanded.

“Monsieur—” the notary began.

“Which tear a daughter from her father?” Piombo pressed.

“Monsieur—”

“Which deprive an old man of his last consolation?” he continued.

“Monsieur, your daughter only belongs to you if—”

“And kill him?” Piombo shouted.

“Monsieur, permit me—”

The notary’s calm demeanor only fueled Piombo’s rage. In a sudden movement, he grabbed a dagger from the wall and rushed toward Ginevra. Witnesses intervened, but Piombo pushed them aside, his fury evident.

When Ginevra saw him approach, she knelt before him. “No, no! I cannot!” he cried, throwing the dagger away. “Have mercy! Give me Luigi! I ask for your consent upon my knees,” she pleaded, her voice trembling.

Bartolomeo pushed her away. “Go. The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here,” he said, pressing his heart. “Leave my house, unhappy girl,” he added, ushering her out.

“Luigi!” Ginevra cried, entering her lover’s humble abode. “My Luigi, we have no fortune but our love.”

“Then I am richer than kings!” he exclaimed.

“My father and mother have cast me off,” she said sadly.

“I will love you in their place,” he promised.

“Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!” she declared, her voice filled with a mix of joy and dread.

At the beginning of 1819, the picture-dealers urged Ginevra to create original works instead of copies, as competition had intensified. Madame Porta realized her earlier mistake of not pursuing genre painting, which could have enhanced her reputation. She attempted portrait painting but faced stiff competition from artists in greater financial need. Despite Luigi and Ginevra's modest savings, they began to feel anxious about their future.

As winter progressed, Luigi worked tirelessly, struggling against competition in writing, which had become less lucrative. He could no longer afford assistance and had to work longer hours for the same pay. Ginevra produced several commendable paintings, but dealers were reluctant to buy from lesser-known artists, leaving her with little success even when she offered her work at low prices.

Their household situation grew dire, with love and happiness overshadowed by the specter of poverty. They concealed their worries from each other, with Ginevra showering Luigi with affection while he hid his fears. One night, Ginevra awoke to find Luigi missing. She discovered him working late into the night, a habit he had developed to avoid burdening her with his struggles. Overcome with emotion, she resolved to contribute by coloring lithographs, working late into the night while Luigi toiled in his garret. They both pretended to sleep, each trying to shield the other from their worries.

One night, Luigi, exhausted and feverish, opened his window and saw Ginevra's lamp across the courtyard, realizing she was also working. When he confronted her, she insisted she could not be idle while he suffered. They shared a moment of tenderness, gazing at the dawn together, hopeful for the future.

Their struggles intensified when Ginevra gave birth to a beautiful son, Bartolomeo. The joy of parenthood initially masked their financial burdens, but soon they were forced to sell their possessions and jewels. Poverty crept in, stripping them of comfort and pride, leading to a desperate situation.

Months later, Ginevra, now frail and worn, struggled to care for her sickly child. Luigi, feeling helpless, searched for assistance but found none. He sold Ginevra's paintings and their remaining furniture for a meager sum, prolonging their suffering. Ginevra displayed remarkable strength, managing household duties and caring for her son, even as she starved herself to provide for Luigi.

One evening, she offered Luigi her last piece of bread, urging him to eat. Realizing her sacrifice, he was consumed by despair and left to seek help. In a moment of desperation, he decided to sell himself as a military substitute, hoping the money would save Ginevra and their child.

After securing an advance, he rushed home, only to find Ginevra asleep with their child. When he woke her, she smiled but then realized Bartolomeo was cold and lifeless. Luigi, horrified, sought help, but it was too late. Ginevra, in her delirium, expressed her love for Luigi and her regret over their lost child. She died in his arms, leaving him heartbroken.

As Luigi mourned, Ginevra's parents sat in their home, reflecting on their daughter.
