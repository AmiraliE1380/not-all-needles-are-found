In October 1800, a foreigner named Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and young daughter. The man, over sixty and somber, was deeply troubled; his wife, though sad, tried to keep calm for their child. Their despair drew onlookers, but the stranger’s fierce gaze discouraged curiosity.

After a pause, Bartolomeo made a desperate choice. He handed a dagger to his wife, declaring he would confront the Bonapartes. Approaching the palace, a soldier barred him without a written request. Bartolomeo insisted, producing a tense standoff until Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, arrived, recognized him, and brought him inside to meet his brother, Napoleon.

Napoleon dismissed his aides to speak with Bartolomeo alone. The Corsican sought asylum and admitted he had killed the Portas — the family that had burned his vineyard and murdered his son. He described avenging his son by killing all seven Portas, shocking Napoleon. Bartolomeo bitterly reminded the brothers how he had once protected their family.

Now a national leader, Napoleon felt bound by law and could not endorse private vengeance. Still, he acknowledged the Corsican tradition of vendetta and offered Bartolomeo a place to live in Paris, promising financial help while warning that the law would not tolerate private justice in the city.

Bartolomeo accepted, pledged loyalty to the Bonapartes, and expressed gratitude for their recognition of his misfortune, appearing hopeful about life in Paris. Seeing a potential ally, Napoleon hinted at needing trustworthy friends later.

Afterwards Lucien, learning the family had arrived with nothing, gave money and promised further assistance, noting that Bartolomeo’s Corsican property would not suffice to support them in Paris. Fifteen years later, events would unfold that required the context of these struggles and decisions.

Servin, a distinguished artist, was the first to open a studio for young girls who wished to paint. About forty pupils, strict in morals and devoted to art, attended his classes. He was married to the dowerless daughter of a general. At first mothers accompanied their daughters; reassured by Servin’s principles and dedication, they soon sent them alone.

Determined to avoid social disputes, he taught only young ladies from wealthy families and refused girls who wanted to become professional artists, since that would demand more thorough instruction. His careful approach and skill won him a stellar reputation among the aristocracy. When a girl showed talent, mothers invariably recommended Servin. He became synonymous with feminine art much as Herbault was with bonnets and Leroy with gowns. A young woman trained by him was assumed capable of critiquing museum pictures, painting striking portraits, or handling genre scenes. Though connected with Paris’s elite, Servin retained an independent, patriotic attitude and a witty, half‑ironic tone.

The studio itself was arranged with care. The attic above his apartment was sealed and reachable only by a small spiral stair. The large, light room had big glass panes veiled by green linen shades; dark‑gray walls displayed caricatures and plaster models, producing a curious mixture of artistic disorder and harmony.

On a bright July day the studio hummed with girls at their easels, each with different expressions and styles. Among them sat a modest, simply dressed girl, Ginevra Piombo, apart and seemingly anxious. The group split into two cliques: daughters of wealthy merchants and aristocrats, led by Amelie Thirion, vain and pretentious. Despite their differences they were absorbed in work, laughing and chatting. Ginevra, the most talented and beautiful, drew the aristocrats’ jealousy. Intent on ostracizing her, Amelie pushed her easel away, provoking whispers about the insult.

The political climate after the Bourbons’ second return sharpened tensions. Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, met disdain from royalist girls; Amelie’s act was both a personal affront and a reflection of the era’s divisions. When Ginevra arrived she found silence where she expected warmth; her beauty and grace commanded attention, but her politics made her a target. Unmoved, she focused on her work, unaware of the full extent of the surrounding tension.

During the session a faint sound behind a partition drew Ginevra’s attention. She discovered Servin was sheltering a political fugitive, an officer of the Imperial Guard. The revelation filled her with both excitement and concern for his safety. Resolving to protect him, she preferred to stay near the fugitive rather than risk his exposure. Her indifference to Amelie’s slight only increased the latter’s jealousy and baffled the other girls.

Madame Servin’s arrival interrupted the brewing conflict; she announced her husband could not attend and seemed aware of the hidden man. Sensing danger, Ginevra began humming a Corsican song to help mask the officer’s breathing. As the session continued, she sketched the officer’s head with surprising fervor. Servin praised her talent; other girls, including Amelie, were drawn to her work. Amelie, suspicious, tried to uncover the mystery but was thwarted by Ginevra’s quick thinking. Servin, attuned to the tension, engaged each pupil while keeping a close eye on Ginevra. Their shared awareness deepened their bond, and Ginevra felt empowered to assist in protecting the fugitive.

By day’s end Ginevra’s resolve to keep the secret and help the officer escape had strengthened. Political turmoil loomed outside, but within the studio the girls navigated rivalries and friendships, united by their art.

When the painter Servin and Ginevra believed they were alone, Servin admitted a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, wounded and pale. He recoiled at the sight of Ginevra, but Servin soothed him, introducing her as the daughter of Baron di Piombo, a loyal friend of the Emperor. Recognizing her patriotism, the soldier relaxed.

Ginevra noticed his wound; he dismissed it as healing. Cries in the street then announced a death sentence—“Condemned to death!”—and the officer turned ashen at the name “Labedoyere,” collapsing on a stool. In despair he lamented that while Labedoyere faced execution for their cause, he was hiding. He felt abandoned after losing his protector and believed an honest life countenanced more than traitors’ lives; he wondered how to sell his life to the executioner.

Ginevra stopped him as he tried to leave and asked whether he expected to restore the Emperor, whether he could raise “that giant who could not maintain himself.” He replied helplessly. Alarmed, she offered her savings, saying her father was wealthy and would not blame her for helping a soldier. She urged him to wait out the storm, escape, and serve elsewhere.

Her maternal grace and hopeful persuasion moved him deeply. He admired her beauty and called her an “angel of goodness,” though his thoughts kept returning to Labedoyere. A quiet understanding passed between them; their brief acquaintance deepened into a bond. Servin asked if he could save Labedoyere; the officer could only avenge him, revealing a devotion to Napoleon that roused Ginevra.

As the sun set on the officer’s face, Ginevra felt a sudden, almost magical connection, recalling childhood memories. She left smiling, a spark of hope kindled.

The next day, the officer—now named Luigi—recounted the retreat from Moscow and the disaster at Waterloo. Ginevra listened, admiration growing as they conversed in Corsican, which strengthened their intimacy. Days passed; her classmates noticed her absence from social gatherings and attributed it to Luigi. Mademoiselle Thirion, worried by the rumor, withdrew from the class, and attendance declined. Ginevra remained absorbed by thoughts of Luigi.

Laure, a fellow student, later confided that her mother forced her to leave the studio because of rumors about Ginevra and Luigi. Ginevra was shocked but reassured Laure they would meet again; Laure departed sadly. As Ginevra and Luigi’s bond deepened, they recognized each other’s worth in silence. One evening Luigi signaled to her but, spotting Laure, hesitated. Ginevra, unaware of underlying tensions, learned her classmates had left because of the scandal.

Confronting Servin, she revealed the young ladies’ falsehoods; Servin dismissed the gossip and focused on his upcoming exhibition. Luigi arrived, joking that he had caused Servin to lose students; the painter playfully suggested marriage. Their ensuing silence—more eloquent than words—marked the first public acknowledgment of their love. Servin celebrated the joy of love and urged their union. Ginevra proposed helping Luigi secure a pardon, confident in her father’s influence.

Meanwhile, Ginevra’s parents grew anxious at her late return. Bartolomeo, her father, paced, uneasy despite being used to her late hours; he had always prioritized his family’s well-being over appearances. As they waited, they reflected on their close-knit household, built on love, mutual respect, and shared political loyalty to the Emperor—values Ginevra had inherited.

When she finally returned, Bartolomeo embraced her, relieved. The family shared a warm moment that revealed their support. In the studio, Ginevra and Luigi’s relationship blossomed amid scandal and danger; their love, forged in adversity, became a source of strength, uniting them and embodying the resilience of their shared ideals.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!” Ginevra laughed, then explained to her mother it had been a trick to slip away. The baroness recovered; Piombo, rubbing his hands, showed courtly joy. The two aged parents seemed renewed.

“Now, to dinner!” Piombo called, offering his hand to Ginevra. “Your mother says you’ve been at the studio more. Painting takes you from us.”

“Oh, father!” she protested.

“Ginevra prepares a surprise,” her mother suggested.

“A picture of your own! Will you show us?” Piombo asked eagerly.

“Yes, I am busy at the studio,” she replied slowly. Her mother fretted over her pallor. “No! It shall never be said Ginevra Piombo acted a lie,” Ginevra declared, then confessed, lowering her eyelids, “I love a young man.”

Piombo’s irony and his wife’s tremor met the revelation. “Is he a prince?” the baron asked.

“No, father, a young man without fortune,” she answered gently.

“Is he handsome?”

“He is very unfortunate,” she said.

“He?” Piombo pressed. “What is he?”

“Labedoyere’s comrade; he was proscribed and hidden by Servin,” she explained.

“Servin is a good fellow,” Piombo said, “but you do wrong to love any man but your father.”

“It does not depend on me to love or not,” Ginevra replied softly.

“I thought my Ginevra would remain faithful to me until I die,” Piombo lamented. “I did not expect our love to have a rival.”

“Did I ever reproach you for devotion to Napoleon?” Ginevra countered. “You left me months on missions. I endured your absence. Life has necessities.”

“Ginevra!” he cried.

“You don’t love me for myself; your reproaches show your egotism,” she said.

“Dare you blame your father’s love?” he shouted.

“Father, I don’t blame you,” she answered. “You have grounds for your egotism, as I have for my love. I have never left your side for fifteen years; it has been my pleasure to charm your life. Is it ingratitude to desire a husband who will protect me?”

“What! You reckon benefits with your father?” Piombo asked dangerously. Silence followed. Bartolomeo finally cried, “Oh! stay with us! I cannot bear you love another man. Ginevra, you will not have long to await your liberty.”

“But, father, I need not leave you; we will both love you. You will come to know the man to whom you bequeath me,” she said.

“Oh! Ginevra, why did you not marry when Napoleon offered?” Piombo lamented.

“They loved me to order,” she replied. “I did not wish to leave you alone.”

“You don’t wish to leave me alone, yet you marry! I know you, my daughter; you would cease to love us,” he said.

Elisa, the baroness, sat motionless. “We have no longer a daughter; she wishes to marry,” Piombo declared, hands raised to heaven, bowed by sorrow. Ginevra expected a violent outburst but met gentleness.

“Father,” she said, “you shall never be abandoned by your Ginevra. Love me for my own sake. If you knew how he loves me! He would never make me unhappy!”

“Comparisons already!” Piombo shouted. “If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me; if not, I would kill him.”

Ginevra alone could withstand his gaze, her eyes aflame. “Oh! to love you! What man is worthy of such a life?” Piombo continued. “Who is worthy to be your husband?”

“He,” Ginevra replied, “he of whom I am not worthy.”

“How can he know you enough to love you?” he asked.

“Whether he loves me or not, if I love him—” she began.

“You love him?” Piombo interrupted.

Ginevra nodded.

“You love him more than you love us?” he pressed.

“The two feelings cannot be compared,” she said.

“Is one stronger?” he asked.

“Yes, I think it is,” she admitted.

“You shall not marry him,” Piombo declared, shaking the windowpanes.

“I shall marry him,” Ginevra replied calmly.

“Oh, God!” cried her mother. “How will this quarrel end?”

Piombo, now seated, tried a softer tone. “Say nothing more tonight—let me think otherwise. Do you wish to see your father on his knees, his white hairs prostrate before you? I supplicate you—”

“Ginevra Piombo does not break her word,” she answered. “I am your daughter.”

“She is right,” the baroness said. “We are sent into the world to marry.”

“Do you encourage her in disobedience?” Piombo demanded of his wife, who sat petrified.

“Refusing an unjust order is not disobedience,” Ginevra asserted.

“No order can be unjust from your father’s lips,” Piombo replied. “Why do you judge me? My repugnance may be counsel from on high, to protect you from great evil.”

“The only evil would be if he did not love me.”

“Always he!” Piombo exclaimed.

“Yes, always,” she affirmed. “He is my life, my good, my thought. Even if I obeyed you, he would remain in my soul. To forbid me to marry him is to make me hate you.”

“You love us not!” Piombo shouted.

“Oh!” Ginevra shook her head.

“Then forget him; be faithful to us. After we are gone—you understand?”

“Father, do you wish me to long for your death?” she asked.

“I shall outlive you. Children who do not honor their parents die early,” he warned.

“All the more reason I should marry and be happy,” she replied.

Piombo’s anger cooled as Ginevra sprang to his knee and embraced him. “Oh, yes, yes, let me die first! I could never survive you, my father!” she cried.

“Oh! my Ginevra, my own Ginevra!” Piombo answered, affection melting his fury. They shared playful moments, but Ginevra’s attempts to introduce Luigi were met with her father’s teasing refusals.

The next day Ginevra was more tender to her father, thanking him as if for a consent that did not yet exist. A week later, her mother motioned her close. “I have persuaded your father to receive him,” Elisa whispered. Ginevra rejoiced.

That day she returned on Luigi’s arm. The officer, who had emerged from hiding, had been helped by Ginevra’s appeal to the Duc de Feltre — his name was restored on the officers’ list. Warned that her father might disapprove, Luigi was anxious to meet him, and his fear deepened Ginevra’s love.

“How pale you are!” she said as they reached her home.

“Oh! Ginevra, if it concerned my life only—” he began. Bartolomeo sat stern and silent.

“Father,” Ginevra said, “I bring you someone you will be pleased to see—a soldier who fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean.”

Piombo rose, glancing at Luigi. “Monsieur is not decorated,” he remarked.

“I no longer wear the Legion of Honor,” Luigi replied timidly.

Embarrassed by her father’s rudeness, Ginevra drew a chair for Luigi. Madame Piombo, noting her husband’s softening, tried to engage. “Monsieur’s resemblance to someone we knew in Corsica, Nina Porta, is surprising,” she noted.

“Nothing could be more natural,” Luigi answered. “Nina was my sister.”

“Are you Luigi Porta?” Piombo asked.

“Yes,” Luigi confirmed.

The effect was instantaneous. Bartolomeo staggered, supported by his wife. The two old parents left the room in silence, abandoning their daughter in horror.

Luigi, bewildered, looked at Ginevra, who had turned pale, staring at the door through which her parents had exited. The air filled with dread. “What misery in a word!” she exclaimed.

“In the name of our love, what have I said?” Luigi asked.

“My father never spoke of our tragic history, and I was too young to know,” Ginevra explained. “My mother told me the Portas killed my brother and burned our house. My father then massacred their family. How did you survive? You were tied to the bed before they set fire to the house.”

“I do not know,” Luigi replied. “I was taken to Genoa at six and raised by an old man named Colonna. I was warned I had enemies and advised to take Luigi as my surname to evade them.”

“Go, go, Luigi!” Ginevra urged. “Stay here; you are safe in my father’s house, but once you leave, danger awaits! My father has two Corsicans in his service, and they will hunt you.”

“Ginevra,” he asked, trembling, “does this vendetta exist between us?”

She smiled sadly and nodded. “Oh, Luigi, our love must be pure and sincere to give me strength for the path ahead. But it promises happiness for our lives, does it not?”

Luigi squeezed her hand. Ginevra realized true love must endure more than words. Resolute, she escorted him to his modest lodging and ensured he reached it safely.

Returning home, she greeted her parents, who were preparing dinner, with gentle composure, noticing her mother’s tear-streaked face. They rose in silence. In the gloomy salon Piombo attempted speech but faltered; his strength seemed gone. He rang for a servant. “Pietro, light the fire; I am cold,” he ordered.

Ginevra trembled, watching his internal struggle. Both suffered in silence, nursing pride and pain. The love that had bound them was now a burden between reproach and sorrow.

On Ginevra’s birthday, in a final attempt to reconcile, her mother gathered them in Bartolomeo’s study. Ginevra divined her mother’s plan and smiled sadly. At that moment two notaries arrived with witnesses. Bartolomeo eyed them warily. The older notary began to speak of Ginevra’s marriage to Luigi Porta.

“Monsieur le baron, we have come to fulfill the law regarding your daughter’s marriage,” he stated.

Piombo erupted. “Are there laws in France that destroy paternal authority? Which tear a daughter from her father? Which deprive an old man of his last consolation?” He drove the notary silent. In a sudden movement he seized a dagger from the wall and rushed toward Ginevra. Witnesses intervened, but Piombo pushed them aside, fury driving him.

When Ginevra saw him approach she knelt before him. “No, no! I cannot!” he cried, flinging the dagger away. “Have mercy! Give me Luigi! I ask your consent upon my knees,” she pleaded, voice trembling.

Bartolomeo pushed her away. “Go. The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here,” he said, clutching his heart. “Leave my house, unhappy girl,” he added, ushering her out.

“Luigi!” Ginevra cried, entering her lover’s humble abode. “My Luigi, we have no fortune but our love.”

“Then I am richer than kings!” he exclaimed.

“My father and mother have cast me off,” she said sadly.

“I will love you in their place,” he promised.

“Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!” she declared, voice full of mingled joy and dread.

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father's house, she sought refuge with Madame Servin until her marriage to Luigi. Madame Servin received her coldly, upset by the trouble Ginevra had caused her husband. Proud yet shocked by this selfishness, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her and painted a bright future that lifted her spirits.

One morning Ginevra received trunks of essentials from her mother, who included a letter urging her to reconsider the marriage. The letter, full of love and sorrow, deeply moved Ginevra. Just as she felt compelled to return home, Luigi arrived and her resolve to abandon him faded; she chose to bury her grief for the sake of their love.

On the wedding day Ginevra was alone, and Luigi brought ordinary men as witnesses, a stark contrast to typical wedding gaiety. Ginevra's beauty silenced them, creating a somber atmosphere. As they walked to the mayor's office, they passed other joyful wedding parties, highlighting the difference in their situation.

Inside, their love was overshadowed by the festive mood around them. The mayor's clerk noted the absence of Ginevra's parents, and when the mayor inquired about their protest the clerk confirmed it. The bureaucratic formality felt oppressive, reminding Ginevra of her family's disapproval; the ceremony was reduced to a legal act devoid of celebration.

After signing the registers, Ginevra and Luigi left the office with a mix of relief and sadness. Luigi expressed his gratitude, and Ginevra felt a quiet happiness despite the circumstances. They hurried to the church, where an old priest married them in a modest chapel amid the grandeur of other weddings. The priest's remarks about their parents' absence stung, but a blessing from a sergeant comforted Ginevra.

Once married, they moved into a modest apartment where Luigi embraced Ginevra, calling it their true wedding. They explored their new home, which was filled with artistic supplies for Ginevra. She was delighted by the thoughtful arrangements, and they reveled in their shared space, feeling they had found paradise despite its limitations.

In the early days of marriage they indulged in each other’s presence, but reality soon set in and they realized they needed to work. Ginevra began copying paintings and gained clients, while Luigi used his handwriting skills to offer copying services. His efforts paid off, and soon he had enough work to hire assistants, providing a stable income for both.

This period marked the happiest time of their lives, filled with love and labor. They enjoyed evenings together; Ginevra always smiled and never complained. Each worked for the other, finding joy in shared effort. Yet Ginevra occasionally felt pangs of remorse for the happiness she had sacrificed by leaving her family, haunted by thoughts of her parents' sorrow.

To commemorate their anniversary, Ginevra painted a portrait of herself for Luigi—an embodiment of their wedded bliss. Another year passed in contentment; no significant events disrupted their quiet happiness.

At the beginning of 1819 picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce originals instead of copies as competition grew. Madame Porta regretted not pursuing genre painting earlier; she tried portraiture but faced stiffer rivals. Despite modest savings, Luigi and Ginevra worried about their future.

That winter Luigi labored relentlessly in a crowded, poorly paid writing market. He could no longer afford help and had to work longer for the same pay. Ginevra painted commendable pieces, but dealers shunned lesser-known artists, and even low prices brought few sales.

Their household grew dire; love and happiness were overshadowed by poverty. They concealed worries from each other: Ginevra lavished affection while Luigi hid his fears. One night Ginevra woke to find Luigi gone and discovered him working late to avoid burdening her. Moved, she resolved to help by coloring lithographs, working through the night while Luigi toiled in his garret. At dawn they pretended to sleep, each shielding the other from distress.

On another night Luigi, exhausted and feverish, opened his window and saw Ginevra’s lamp across the courtyard, realizing she too worked. Confronting her, he learned she could not idle while he suffered. They shared a tender moment, watching dawn together and feeling tentative hope.

Their struggle deepened when Ginevra bore a son, Bartolomeo. The joy of parenthood briefly masked their burdens, but soon they sold possessions and jewels. Poverty stripped them of comfort and pride, pushing them toward desperation.

Months later Ginevra, frail and worn, struggled to care for a sickly child. Luigi, feeling helpless, sought help and found none. He sold Ginevra’s paintings and their remaining furniture for a pittance, hardly easing their plight. Ginevra displayed remarkable strength, managing the household and tending their son while often starving herself to feed Luigi.

One evening she offered Luigi her last piece of bread and urged him to eat. Realizing her sacrifice, he was engulfed by despair and left to seek aid. In desperation he decided to sell himself as a military substitute, hoping the advance would save Ginevra and their child.

After obtaining the money, he hurried home, finding Ginevra asleep with their child. When he woke her, she smiled, then noticed Bartolomeo was cold and lifeless. Luigi, horrified, sought help, but it was too late. In delirium Ginevra professed her love for Luigi and lamented their lost child. She died in his arms, leaving him devastated.

As Luigi mourned, Ginevra’s parents sat in their home, remembering the daughter they had not seen in years. Elisa Piombo spoke of her, hoping to rekindle Bartolomeo’s affection for Ginevra. Just as Bartolomeo seemed to soften, a man entered with the tragic news of Ginevra and Luigi’s fate. The old couple were shattered, and Bartolomeo’s heart broke at the loss of his beloved child. The cycle of suffering had claimed another victim, leaving only memories and grief.
