In Baronne de Watteville's drawing-room, a hub of Besançon society, the Archbishop often visited due to her devoutness. Monsieur de Watteville, from a notorious lineage, married wealthy heiress Mademoiselle de Rupt in 1815, enhancing Madame de Watteville's piety. Their home attracted notable clergy.

During a dinner in September 1834, guests welcomed Monsieur l’Abbe de Grancey, who announced a legal victory by young lawyer Monsieur Savaron, intriguing Madame de Watteville. The Vicar-General noted Savaron's obscure origins, raising curiosity about him.

Culturally, France and England have influenced each other, with English terms like "dandy" entering French. Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, a local "lion," aspired to marry Rosalie de Watteville for her dowry, but her mother scrutinized his intentions. Despite his charm, Rosalie remained indifferent.

Monsieur de Soulas shared Albert Savaron's story, who gained recognition after defending a mentally challenged man. Rosalie found Savaron more appealing than her suitors and planned to meet him at church. Meanwhile, Savaron launched the _Eastern Review_, deepening her infatuation.

In July 1823, Rodolphe and Leopold traveled to Switzerland, where Rodolphe fell for Fanny Lovelace, learning of her modest life and hidden background. Their bond deepened as Fanny revealed her marriage to an older man, Emilio, yet Rodolphe vowed to wait for her, symbolizing their hopes amid turmoil.

For twenty days, Rodolphe was captivated by Francesca, who represented youthful imprudence and womanhood. He was intrigued by her affectionate bond with her husband, Emilio. One evening, Francesca declined to join Rodolphe in Geneva to protect Emilio’s reputation, expressing love for him but stressing the need for discretion.

After the Bergmanns left, Rodolphe went to Geneva, seeking information about Italian refugees. At Villa Diodati, he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage, revealing her aristocratic ties. Feeling inadequate, he sought an audience with her husband, Prince Gandolphini, but returned to his lodgings, preoccupied with thoughts of Francesca.

Their connection deepened at a gathering, where Francesca encouraged Rodolphe to pursue his ambitions. Her background as a well-educated heiress married to Prince Gandolphini contrasted with her revolutionary spirit, resonating with Rodolphe.

As spring approached, Rodolphe's longing and anxiety grew, leading to tension when he compared Francesca to a French noblewoman. After Francesca asked him to leave until they could meet again in Naples, he returned to Paris, discovering her portrait. Driven by ambition, he invested in a venture that failed, costing him three years. Meanwhile, Mademoiselle de Watteville's jealousy over Francesca highlighted the complexities of love and sacrifice.

In spring 1835, nineteen-year-old Rosalie aimed to thwart the Duchesse d’Argaiolo while Albert de Savarus gained political traction in Besançon for an aqueduct project. Savarus, masking his ambitions, hesitated to ally with the Wattevilles due to neutrality concerns. Visiting her family estate, Rosalie learned of her father's legal issues and suggested Savarus as their lawyer. She attempted to sabotage his political rise by informing the Prefect of his Royalist views, but he was still nominated and gained local support. As the election approached, the Prefect sought to undermine him. Meanwhile, the Abbe de Grancey urged Savarus to connect with the Wattevilles, considering a marriage alliance. At a gathering, guests were shocked to learn Savarus had left unexpectedly, and the Abbe realized Rosalie's feelings for him. The next day, news of his disappearance led to Monsieur de Chavoncourt's election. After her father's tragic drowning, Rosalie grappled with her emotions and her mother's plans. Years later, after her mother remarried, Rosalie sought closure with the Duchess regarding Savarus, who had entered a monastery, and devoted herself to religion following a steamboat accident.
