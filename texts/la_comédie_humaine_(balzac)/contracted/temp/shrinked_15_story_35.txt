The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once among the darkest, most tortuous streets near the Hôtel de Ville, twisted past the little gardens of the Paris Prefecture and ended at the Rue Martroi by a now-demolished wall. Its namesake turnstile remained until 1823, when the Municipality built a ballroom on the adjoining garden for the Duc d’Angoulême’s fête.

At its widest, where it opened into the Rue de la Tixeranderie, the Rue du Tourniquet measured under six feet. In rain the gutter ran deep, sweeping dust and refuse into corners carts could not reach; residents trusted storms to wash their miry alley. In summer the sun briefly lit this blackness but never dried the permanent damp that rose to the first story. People who lit lamps at five in June kept them burning through winter. A traveler approaching the Marais along the quays, past the Rue du Chaume and the Rues de l’Homme Armé, des Billettes, and des Deux-Portes, might think he had passed through cellars all the way.

Many streets of old Paris formed this damp labyrinth, rich in curiosities for antiquaries. On the corner where the Rue du Tourniquet met the Rue de la Tixeranderie, two iron ring clamps remained, relics of chains the watch once strung nightly. The corner house attested to such dwellings’ unhealthiness: its cellar arches rose only about two feet above ground, the entrance required three steps, and the door was crowned by a closed arch whose keystone bore a female head and time-eaten arabesques. Three windows, sills about five feet from the ground, lit a small set of rooms facing the Rue du Tourniquet, protected by strong iron bars curved outward below.

By day a passer-by could see little; only in July, when light strengthened, could one discern in the second room two beds hung with green serge side by side in an old-fashioned alcove. But by about three, when candles were lighted, the nearer pane showed an old woman on a stool tending a brazier to simmer a porter’s-wife stew; a few utensils hung against the wall. A trestle table, bare of linen, held pewter spoons and the old woman’s dish; three wretched chairs furnished the multipurpose kitchen-dining room. Above the chimney hung a small mirror, a tinder-box, three glasses, matches, and a large cracked white jug. In spite of gloom, the floor, utensils, and fireplace testified to cleanliness and thrift.

The old woman’s pale, withered face matched the street’s darkness and mustiness. Motionless as a snail in its shell, her face—alert with a vague mischief—was framed in a net cap that barely covered white hair; her fine gray eyes were quiet as the street, and wrinkles ran like cracks in the walls. Whether born to poverty or fallen from better days, she seemed resigned to melancholy.

From sunrise till dark, except when cooking or buying provisions, the old woman sat by the farther window opposite a young girl. A passer-by could see the needlewoman in an old red velvet chair bending over an embroidery frame, stitching indefatigably. The mother had a green pillow and busied herself with handmade net, but with feeble sight; an antiquated spring-grip pair of spectacles rested on her nose. By night they set a lamp between them, its light concentrated by two globe-shaped water bottles, letting the elder see the network on her pillow and the younger the pattern’s delicate details.

The window’s outward bend held a box of earth on the sill where sweet peas, nasturtiums, a sickly honeysuckle, and convolvulus twined up the iron bars. These etiolated plants bore a few pale flowers, adding sadness and sweetness to the framed picture of the two figures.

Even the most selfish observer would carry away a vivid image of working-class Parisian women’s lives: the embroideress evidently lived by her needle. Many passing through the turnstile wondered how a girl could keep her color living in a cellar. A student crossing to the Quartier Latin might liken this vegetative existence to ivy clinging to chill walls, or to peasants who toil and die unknown. A house-owner, valuing the property, would ask, “What will become of those two women if embroidery goes out of fashion?”

Among the men obliged to pass this street for the Hôtel de Ville or Palais de Justice there might have been charity. Some widower or sprightly Adonis of forty, often glimpsing these sad lives, might have reckoned on the mother and daughter’s poverty and hoped to make the innocent work-woman his at little cost—her nimble fingers, youthful figure, and pale skin (a charm due to the sunless street) drawing admiration. Perhaps some honest clerk with twelve hundred francs a year, seeing diligence and purity, waited only for better prospects to unite his life to hers, to bring a man’s arm and calm affection, pale like the window flowers, to uphold the home.

Vague hope kept life in the mother’s dim gray eyes. Each morning after a frugal breakfast she took up her pillow—chiefly for show—laid her spectacles on a little mahogany worktable as old as herself, and watched from about half-past eight till ten the regular passers, noting gait, dress, and countenance as if trying to attract attention to her daughter. This small review was as good as a play to her, perhaps her sole amusement.

The daughter rarely looked up. Modesty, or the painful consciousness of poverty, kept her eyes fixed on the frame; only some exclamation from her mother showed her small features. Then a clerk in a new coat or one appearing with a woman might catch sight of the girl’s slightly upturned nose, rosy mouth, and gray eyes, bright despite toil. Late hours left pale circles beneath each eye on her fresh cheeks.

The child seemed made for love and cheerfulness: love had drawn two perfect arches above her eyelids and given her a mass of chestnut hair she might hide under like a tent; cheerfulness widened her nostrils into quivering animation, carved dimples in her cheeks, and made her quick to forget troubles—this blossom of hope, giving strength to face life’s barren path.

Her hair was always neatly dressed. As a Paris needlewoman, she thought her toilet complete after brushing smooth and tucking the short curls at each temple against her white skin. The pretty growth at the nape and a clear brown line suggested youth and charm; an observer seeing her bent over work might deem her a coquette. This promise caught more than one young man’s eye, who turned in vain hope to glimpse that modest countenance.

“Caroline, there is a new face that passes regularly by, and not one of the old ones to compare with it.”

These words, spoken in a low voice by her mother one August morning in 1815, vanquished the young needlewoman’s indifference, and she looked out; the stranger was gone.

“Where has he flown to?” she asked.

“He will come back no doubt at four; I shall see him coming, and will touch your foot with mine. I am sure he will come back; he has been through the street regularly for the last three days; but his hours vary. The first day he came by at six o’clock, the day before yesterday it was four, yesterday as early as three. I remember seeing him occasionally some time ago. He is some clerk in the Préfet’s office who has moved to the Marais.—Why!” she exclaimed after glancing down the street, “our gentleman of the brown coat has taken to wearing a wig; how much it alters him!”

The gentleman of the brown coat seemed to close the daily procession; the old woman put on her spectacles and took up her work with a sigh, glancing at her daughter with a look that mingled admiration, gratitude, hope, and pride in such a pretty child.

At about four the old lady pushed her foot against Caroline’s, and the girl looked up quickly enough to see a new actor, whose regular advent would thenceforth lend variety to the scene. He was tall and thin, wore black, a man of about forty, with a certain solemnity; as his piercing hazel eye met the old woman’s dull gaze, he made her quake, for she felt as though he had the gift of reading hearts, or had much practice in it, and his presence seemed as icy as the air of this dank street. Was the dull, sallow complexion of that ominous face due to excess of work, or to delicate health?

The old woman supplied many answers; but next day Caroline discerned the lines of long mental suffering on a brow quick to frown. His hollow cheeks bore sorrow’s seal. Though the girl’s expression at first was one of lively but innocent curiosity, it took on gentle sympathy as the stranger receded like a last relation following a funeral.

The weather was so hot and the gentleman so absent-minded that he had taken off and forgotten his hat as he passed the squalid street. Caroline noted how his brushed hair altered his countenance. The strong, uncharming impression he made was unlike any other passenger; for the first time she felt pity for someone besides herself and her mother. She said nothing to her mother’s conjectures, drew her needle in silence, and only regretted not seeing him more closely, looking forward to the morrow to form a definite opinion.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For two months the “Black Gentleman”—as they called him—moved erratically; he did not always pass down the Rue du Tourniquet at the same hour. The old woman sometimes saw him in the evening when he had not come in the morning; he lacked the punctuality of the clerks who served Madame Crochard. Except on the first occasion, when his glance had alarmed the old mother and his eyes rested on the odd picture of the two female gnomes in the window, he seemed lost in thought, looking at the ground or straight ahead as if reading the future in the fog of the Rue du Tourniquet. There were, besides two carriage-gates and a dark ironmonger’s, only barred windows lighting staircases, so his lack of curiosity could not be blamed on rivals; Madame Crochard was piqued to see her “Black Gentleman” always absorbed. One mid-September morning, however, Caroline Crochard’s lively face, fresh among belated flowers and faded leaves, gleamed against the room’s dark background; the daily scene brightened with contrasts and the pretty needlewoman at work. The stranger, perhaps roused by the clatter her irritated mother made with bobbins, glanced up and studied this living picture.

Their eyes met briefly but meaningfully; both knew they would think of each other. When he passed again at four, Caroline heard his step and they looked steadily at one another with evident purpose; his kind expression made him smile, and Caroline blushed, which Madame Crochard noted with satisfaction. After that memorable afternoon the Gentleman in Black passed twice daily with rare exceptions, observed by mother and daughter. From his irregular hours they judged he was not a precise subordinate.

Through the first three winter months they saw each other twice a day for the few moments it took him to cross the stretch by their door and windows. Day by day the brief meetings took on a friendly, then fraternal, tone. They seemed to understand each other at once, and by studying each other’s faces learned to know them well. It became, as if, a duty of the Unknown to visit Caroline; if he failed to give the half-smile or cordial glance she expected, her day was marred—like an old man who must read his paper each morning. The charm of these meetings was mutual; neither could hide vexation or fatigue from the other.

“He must have had trouble yesterday,” Caroline would think when his features altered. “Oh, he has been working too hard!” she decided from another shade. He could tell when she had spent Sunday finishing a dress, when the quarter-day approached and her face darkened, when she had sat up late, and he marked how the gloom that dimmed her delicate features slowly lifted as their acquaintance warmed. When winter killed the window plants and the window was closed, he noted the concentration of light at Caroline’s head, the small fire and the frosty redness of the two women’s faces showing the home’s poverty; if his face ever showed pity, the girl met it with assumed cheerfulness.

Yet their feelings remained unspoken; no incident revealed how deep they were in each other. They had never heard each other speak, and both kept at a distance as if closer acquaintance might bring disaster. Was it shyness or friendship that restrained them? Fear of encountering selfishness, or the city’s mutual distrust? Each perhaps hoped to preserve an illusion: he seeming to fear a vulgar word from lips as fresh as a flower, she feeling unworthy of a mysterious person who evidently had power and wealth.

Madame Crochard, reproachful at her daughter’s indecision, now showed sulkiness to the “Black Gentleman,” on whom until then she had smiled with benignant servility. She complained of being forced to cook at her age, of catarrh and rheumatism, and could not promise the same number of ells of net as before.

By late December, when bread was dear and the 1816 corn shortage began to bite hard, the stranger saw secret sorrow in Caroline’s face which his kind smiles could not dispel. He noticed the dimness in her eyes from long hours at night. One night near month’s end he passed the Rue du Tourniquet at one in the morning. In the silence he heard Madame Crochard’s lachrymose voice and Caroline’s sadder tone mingling with sleet. He crept along slowly and, risking trouble with the police, stopped beneath the window to listen through the largest hole in the yellow muslin curtain. He saw a sheet of paper on the table by the lamp and water-filled globes—a writ. Madame Crochard wept; Caroline’s voice was thick.

“Why be so heartbroken, mother? Monsieur Molineux will not sell us up or turn us out before I have finished this dress; only two nights more and I shall take it home to Madame Roguin.”

“And supposing she keeps you waiting as usual? And will the money for the gown pay the baker too?”

The stranger, versed in reading faces, fancied the mother’s grief false while the daughter’s was genuine; he turned away, came back, and peeped again. Madame Crochard lay in bed; Caroline bent over her frame, embroidering with indefatigable diligence. On the table with the writ lay a triangular hunch of bread, placed to sustain her through the night and remind her of the reward of industry. Tremulous with pity, the stranger slipped his purse through a cracked pane so it would fall at the girl’s feet, then fled with tingling cheeks.

Next morning the shy, melancholy stranger passed with a preoccupied look, but could not avoid Caroline’s gratitude: she had opened her window and pretended to dig in the snow-buried window-box so he would not see her only through the glass. Her eyes brimmed with tears as she bowed, saying with a look, “I can only repay you from my heart.”

The Gentleman in Black affected not to understand this gratitude. That evening, when he passed, Caroline mended the window with a sheet of paper and smiled, showing her pearly teeth like a promise. Thenceforth the Stranger went another way and was no more seen in the Rue du Tourniquet.

One early May day, as Caroline watered a honeysuckle root, she caught sight of a narrow strip of cloudless blue and said gaily to her mother, “Mamma, we must go tomorrow for a trip to Montmorency!” She had hardly spoken when the Gentleman in Black passed, sadder than ever. Caroline’s innocent look might have seemed an invitation. Indeed, the next day, at the corner of the Rue du Faubourg Saint-Denis and the Rue d’Enghien, Madame Crochard, dressed in a claret merino pelisse, silk bonnet, and striped shawl, found her Unknown standing like a man waiting for his wife. A smile lit his face at sight of Caroline in plum-colored gaiters and a white dress tossed by a breeze that showed her graceful form. Her rice-straw bonnet lined with pink silk shaded a forehead white as snow; her hair parted in two brown bands gave an expression of innocence. She seemed lit from heaven; when she saw him, radiant hope brightened her. The Stranger, in doubt perhaps whether to be her escort, had his decision made by her delight. He hired a vehicle for Saint-Leu-Taverny and offered seats beside him. The mother accepted, but soon complained of the heat and her cough, making civil speeches about being an inconvenience. By Saint-Denis she seemed fast asleep; her snores seemed dubious to the Stranger, who frowned suspiciously.

“Oh, she is fast asleep,” Caroline said guilelessly; “she never ceased coughing all night. She must be very tired.”

Her companion said nothing but looked at the girl with a smile that seemed to mean, “Poor child, you little know your mother!”

Despite distrust, as they passed the poplar avenue toward Eaubonne, the Stranger, perhaps loosened by the bright sky, pure country air, and the first green scents, entered on a conversation with his young companion as aimless as the branches’ sway, as devious as butterflies in the azure air, illogical as the fields’ murmur, and full of a mysterious love. At that season the country is like a bride in her marriage robe; no heart remains unmoved on leaving the Marais for Montmorency’s smiling valley, lifting a charmed gaze to eyes as infinite and loving as the view.

He found Caroline sprightly rather than witty, affectionate but ill educated; her giddy laugh hid genuine feeling. Under his shrewd questioning she spoke with the effusiveness of the lower classes, not guarded as people of the world. Her frankness brightened his face, renewing a youthful look beneath former sadness. Gradually his sternness softened to a handsome youthfulness that made Caroline proud and happy. Her light prattle lifted the last veil from his real youth and character; he seemed to relinquish ideas that haunted him and to show the liveliness beneath his solemnity.

Their talk grew so intimate that by the time the carriage stopped at the straggling houses of Saint-Leu, Caroline was calling him Monsieur Roger. Then, for the first time, the old mother awoke.

“Caroline, she has heard everything!” Roger whispered suspiciously.

Caroline replied with an exquisite smile of disbelief, dispelling the cloud his fear of some plot had caused. Madame Crochard, amazed at nothing, approved everything and followed them into the park, where the young pair wandered through meadows and fragrant copses famed by Queen Hortense.

“How lovely!” Caroline cried when, on the green ridge where Montmorency’s forest begins, she saw the valley: combes sheltering villages, a horizon of blue hills, church towers, meadows sending a murmur like the ocean. They walked beside an artificial stream to the Swiss valley and its chalet once sheltering Hortense and Napoleon. Caroline sat with pious reverence on the mossy bench where emperors had rested. Madame Crochard wished to see a rustic bridge and left them, saying she would not go out of sight.

“What, poor child!” Roger asked, “have you never longed for wealth and luxury? Have you never wished to wear the beautiful dresses you embroider?”

“It would not be truth,” Caroline answered, “if I said I never think how happy the rich must be. Oh yes! I often fancy, especially as I fall asleep, how glad I should be to see my poor mother no longer compelled to go out, whatever the weather, to buy provisions at her age. I should like her to have a servant to bring her coffee sweetened with white sugar, and to see her wearing out her eyes over her favorite novels rather than twisting bobbins. And a little good wine. In short, I should like her comfortable—she is so good.”

“Then she has shown you great kindness?”

“Oh yes,” the girl said with conviction, then paused as they watched Madame Crochard shaking her finger at them from the bridge. Caroline added, “She sold her last silver forks to apprentice me to the old maid who taught me to embroider. And my poor father! What did she not go through to make him end his days in happiness!” She shivered at remembrance and hid her face; “Well! come! let us forget past sorrows!” she rallied, blushing when Roger too seemed moved but daring not to look at him.

“What was your father?” he inquired.

“He was an opera-dancer before the Revolution,” she replied simply, “and my mother sang in the chorus. My father, who led stage figures, was present at the siege of the Bastille. Recognized by some assailants, who asked whether he could lead a real attack, since he led on stage, he accepted, led the insurgents, and was rewarded with the rank of captain in the Sambre-et-Meuse army, where he rose to colonel. But at Lützen he was so badly wounded that, after a year’s suffering, he died in Paris. The Bourbons returned; my mother got no pension, and we sank into such misery that we had to work. She has been ailing, poor dear, and I have never known her so little resigned; she complains much, and I cannot wonder, for she once knew an easy life. I cannot pine for delights I have never known; I have but one wish.”

“And that is?” Roger asked eagerly.

“That women may continue to wear embroidered net dresses, so I may never lack work.”

Her frankness touched him and softened his look toward Madame Crochard as she slowly returned.

“Well, children, have you had a long talk?” the old woman asked with half-laughing indulgence. “When I think, Monsieur Roger, that the ‘little Corporal’ has sat where you are sitting,” she went on, “Poor man! how my husband worshiped him! Ah! Crochard did well to die; he could not have borne to think of him where they have sent him!”

Roger put his finger to his lips; the good woman went on very gravely, unhooking a bit of her bodice to show a ribbon and cross tied round her neck by black ribbon, “they shall never hinder me from wearing what he gave to my poor Crochard, and I will have it buried with me.”

At that seditious speech, Roger suddenly rose, and they returned to the village through park walks. He left briefly to order a meal at the best Taverny eating-house, then returned to fetch them and led them through the forest alleys.

Dinner was cheerful. Roger was no longer the melancholy figure of the Rue du Tourniquet but a confiding young man ready to take life as it came, like the two hardworking women who might lack bread the next day; his smile was affectionate and childlike.

After the meal ended with a few glasses of champagne, Roger proposed they join the village ball under the chestnuts, where he and Caroline danced together. Their hands met sympathetically, their hearts beat with the same hopes, and under the blue sky and slanting, rosy sunset beams, their eyes sparkled with fires that to them made heaven pale. The power of an idea and desire seemed boundless; nothing seemed impossible. In such moments the soul foresees nothing but happiness. The day had created memories neither had known before. Would the source prove more beautiful than the river, the desire more enchanting than its fulfilment?

“So the day is already at an end!” Caroline sighed when the dance finished, noticing Roger’s face shade into sadness.

“Why should you not be as happy in Paris as here?” she asked. “Is happiness only found at Saint-Leu? I think I can never be unhappy anywhere.”

Roger, struck by her unguarded words, felt what she did; for the first time since their first glance, they shared the same idea. Frightened by the mutual silence, they went to the carriage. Before entering, they playfully took hands and ran down the dark avenue in front of Madame Crochard. When they could no longer see the white net cap, Roger, in a tremulous voice, said, “Caroline!”

Startled, the girl drew back a few steps, understanding the invitation; she held out her hand, which he passionately kissed, then she withdrew it quickly, standing on tiptoe to see her mother.

Madame Crochard affected blindness, playing the supernumerary, as if by a remnant of her old stage parts.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The adventures of these two young people were not continued in the Rue du Tourniquet. To see Roger and Caroline again we must leap into modern Paris, where newly built houses contain apartments made for newlyweds on their honeymoon. There the paper and paint are as fresh as the couple, decorations in blossom like their love; everything suits youthful notions and ardent wishes.

Halfway down the Rue Taitbout, in a house with stone walls still white, where the hall columns and doorway were spotless and the inner walls shone with the neat painting the recent English fashion had brought into vogue, there was, on the second floor, a small set of rooms fitted as though the architect had known their use. A simple airy ante-room, with a stucco dado, opened into a combined drawing- and dining-room. From the drawing-room came a pretty bedroom, with a bathroom beyond. Every chimney-shelf bore a fine mirror elegantly framed; doors were crowded with tasteful arabesques and the cornices were in the best style. Any amateur would have discerned the sense of distinction and decorative fitness that marks modern French architects’ work.

For more than a month Caroline had lived in this apartment, furnished by an upholsterer guided by an artist. A short description of the principal room will show the pleasures Caroline enjoyed when Roger installed her. Gray hangings trimmed with green silk adorned her bedroom; seats covered with light-colored wool sateen were easy and fashionable; a simple inlaid chest of drawers held her toiletries; a matching writing-table served for love-letters on scented paper; an antique-draped bed, with elegant muslin hangings, suggested thoughts of love; the drab silk curtains with green fringe were half drawn to subdue the light; a bronze clock represented Love crowning Psyche; a red Gothic carpet set off the rest. A small dressing-table before a long glass was where the needlewoman sat, out of patience with Plaisir, the famous hairdresser.

“Do you think you will have done today?” she asked.

“Your hair is so long and so thick, madame,” replied Plaisir.

Caroline smiled. The man’s flattery doubtless revived the memory of Roger’s praises of her hair.

When the hairdresser finished, a waiting-maid came to counsel her about the dress Roger would like best. It was the beginning of September 1816 and cold; she chose a green grenadine trimmed with chinchilla. Dressed, Caroline flew into the drawing-room and opened a window, then stepped onto the elegant balcony that adorned the front. There she stood, arms crossed, in a charming attitude—not to display herself to passersby but to look out on the Boulevard at the foot of the Rue Taitbout. This side view, like an actor’s peephole in a stage drop-scene, let her catch glimpses of elegant carriages and crowds swept past with the rapidity of Ombres Chinoises. Not knowing whether Roger would arrive in a carriage or on foot, she watched both pedestrians and tilburies—the light cabs introduced by the English.

After a quarter of an hour, neither her keen eye nor her heart had announced him. Scorn and love swept over her face; disdain for all others showed in her features as if the passersby were ants. Her gray eyes, bright with fun, now flamed. Given over to passion, she avoided admiration as much as some cultivate it; she cared for no eyes below her, only one face and one thought. When the spotted head of a certain bay horse crossed the strip between the rows of houses, Caroline shivered and stood on tiptoe, hoping to recognize the white traces and the tilbury’s color. It was he.

Roger turned the corner, saw the balcony, whipped his horse to a gallop, and stopped at the bronze-green door he knew as well as his master did. The maid, having heard her mistress’ exclamation, opened at once. Roger rushed up, clasped Caroline to him, and embraced her with the effusive feeling natural to lovers who rarely meet. They went, by common impulse and with arms about each other, into the quiet fragrant bedroom; a settee stood ready by the fire. For a moment they looked at each other in silence, expressing happiness by clasped hands and a fond gaze.

“Yes, it is he!” she said at last. “Yes, it is you. Do you know, I have not seen you for three long days—an age!—But what is the matter? You are unhappy.”

“My poor Caroline—”

“There, you see! ‘poor Caroline’—”

“No, no, do not laugh, my darling; we cannot go to the Feydeau Theatre together this evening.”

Caroline pouted briefly, then the pout vanished.

“How absurd I am! How can I think of the play when I see you? Is not the sight of you the only spectacle I care for?” she cried, pushing her fingers through Roger’s hair.

“I am obliged to go to the Attorney-General’s. We have a knotty case. He met me in the great hall at the Palais; and as I am to plead, he asked me to dine with him. But, my dearest, you can go to the theatre with your mother, and I will join you if the meeting breaks up early.”

“To the theatre without you!” she cried in amazement; “enjoy any pleasure you do not share! O my Roger! you do not deserve a kiss,” she added, throwing her arms round his neck with artless passion.

“Caroline, I must go home and dress. The Marais is some way off, and I still have business to finish.”

“Take care what you are saying, monsieur,” she interrupted. “My mother says that when a man begins to talk about his business, he is ceasing to love.”

“Caroline! Am I not here? Have I not stolen this hour from my pitiless—”

“Hush!” she said, laying a finger on his mouth. “Don’t you see I am in jest?”

Back in the drawing-room, Roger’s eye fell on an object brought home that morning by the cabinetmaker. Caroline’s old rosewood embroidery frame, by which she and her mother had earned their bread when they lived in the Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, had been refitted and polished, and a net dress of elaborate design already stretched on it.

“Well, then, my dear, I shall do some work this evening. As I stitch, I shall fancy myself gone back to those early days when you used to pass by without a word, though not without a glance; the days when the memory of your look kept me awake all night. Oh my dear old frame—the best piece of furniture in my room, though you did not give it me!” she said, seating herself on Roger’s knees; he, overcome, had dropped into a chair. “Listen. All I can earn by my work I mean to give to the poor. You have made me rich. How I love that pretty home at Bellefeuille, less for what it is than because you gave it me! But tell me, Roger, I should like to call myself Caroline de Bellefeuille—can I? Is it legal or permissible?”

At Roger’s small affirmative grimace—for he hated the name Crochard—Caroline jumped for joy and clapped her hands.

“I feel,” she said, “as if I should more especially belong to you. Usually a woman gives up her own name and takes her husband’s—” An idea forced itself on her and made her blush. She took Roger’s hand and led him to the open piano. “Listen,” she added, “I can play my sonata now like an angel!” Her fingers were already running over the ivory keys when she felt herself seized round the waist.

“Caroline, I ought to be far from hence!”

“You insist on going? Well, go,” she said, with a pretty pout, but she smiled as she looked at the clock and exclaimed joyfully, “At any rate, I have detained you a quarter of an hour!”

“Goodbye, Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille,” he said, with the gentle irony of love.

She kissed him and saw him to the door; when his steps had died on the stairs she ran to the balcony to watch him get into the tilbury, gather up the reins, catch a parting look, hear the crack of his whip and the sound of wheels on the stones, watch the handsome horse, the master’s hat, the tiger’s gold lace, and at last stand gazing long after the dark corner of the street had eclipsed the vision.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Five years after Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille had taken up her abode in the pretty house in the Rue Taitbout, we rejoin one of those home-scenes that bind two people who love. In the middle of the blue drawing-room, before the balcony window, a four-year-old boy made a tremendous noise whipping a rocking-horse whose curved supports would not move fast enough for him; his cherubic face, ringed in fair curls, smiled up at his mother when she said from an easy-chair, “Not so much noise, Charles; you will wake your little sister.”

The curious child slipped from his horse and, treading on tiptoe as if afraid of the sound of his feet, placed a finger in his mouth and, standing in a natural, graceful, childish pose, lifted the muslin veil that hid a rosy face sleeping on his mother’s knee.

“Is Eugenie asleep, then?” he said, astonished. “Why is she asleep when we are awake?” he added, looking up with large, dark liquid eyes.

“That only God can know,” Caroline replied, smiling.

The mother and boy stared at the infant, only that morning baptized.

Caroline, now about twenty-four, showed a ripe beauty that had flowered under cloudless happiness. She had become the complete woman: delighted to obey Roger’s least wishes, she had taken pains to acquire accomplishments she previously lacked; she played the piano fairly and sang sweetly. She had not been schooled in the refinements or the talk of fashionable drawing-rooms—but she had applied herself to the knowledge necessary to be a devoted mother. Her chief ambition was to bring up her children well: to watch her boy constantly, to train him correctly from the cradle, to shelter him from evil influence, and to perform both the duties of nurse and the tender offices of mother.

From the first day she had consented to live within the enchanted sphere of her happiness, and after six years of tender intimacy she still knew her lover only by the name of Roger. A print of Psyche lighting her lamp to gaze on Love against his prohibition hung in her room and constantly recalled the condition of her happiness. During those six years her humble pleasures had never pressed upon Roger as an indiscreet ambition; he was a treasury of kindness. She never longed for diamonds or fine dress, and had repeatedly refused the luxury of a carriage he offered. To look from her balcony for Roger’s cab, to go with him to the play, to make country excursions on fine days, to long for him, to see him, and then to long again—these were the incidents of a life poor in events but rich in happiness.

While she rocked the infant, now a few months old, on her knee and sang, memory took her to happier earlier days—especially to the Septembers when Roger took her to Bellefeuille and they spent delightful days combining the charms of seasons. Nature there was lavish with flowers and fruit; evenings were mild, mornings bright, and summer would sometimes return after an autumnal spell. In the early days of their love she had attributed Roger’s calm and gentleness to the rarity of meetings and the fact they were not compelled to be together as husband and wife must be. Now she recollected that during their first stay on the little estate in the Gâtinais she had been foolishly jealous; every month since had passed like a dream of joys that never proved false. She had always met him with a tender smile, and he with one that seemed to reflect hers.

These pictures brought tears. She felt she could not love him enough and was tempted to regard her ambiguous position as Fate’s tax on love. Curiosity, however, kept returning her to the question of what events could have led so tender a heart as Roger’s to accept clandestine and illicit happiness. She invented romances to avoid the true reason she had long suspected but tried not to accept. She rose, carrying the baby to the dining-room to superintend dinner preparations.

It was the 6th of May 1822, the anniversary of the excursion to the Park of Saint-Leu that had turned her life; each year it had been celebrated. Caroline chose the linen and arranged the dessert with joy. After these little attentions she placed the infant in her pretty cot and stepped onto the balcony, where she soon saw the carriage Roger now used instead of the tilbury of his youth. After submitting to Caroline’s embraces and the kisses of their little rogue who called him papa, Roger went to the cradle, kissed the sleeping daughter’s forehead, and pulled from his pocket a document inked in black.

“Caroline,” he said, “here is the marriage portion of Mademoiselle Eugenie de Bellefeuille.”

The mother gratefully took the paper: a deed of gift of State securities.

“But why,” she asked, “have you given Eugenie three thousand francs a year, and Charles no more than fifteen hundred?”

“Charles, my love, will be a man,” he answered. “Fifteen hundred francs are enough. A man of courage is above poverty. And if by chance your son proves a nonentity, I do not wish him to be able to play the fool. If he is ambitious this small income will give him a taste for work.—Eugenie is a girl; she must have a little fortune.”

He then turned to play with his boy. The household’s cheerfulness was genuine: no shyness between father and child marred the affection, and the little family’s pure delight warmed Caroline and Roger. That evening a magic lantern delighted Charles. Later, when the boy was asleep, the baby woke for nourishment. By lamplight in the chimney corner, Roger surrendered to the domestic scene: the child clinging to Caroline’s white bosom as she sat, fresh as a newly opened lily, hair long and brown falling almost to her neck. The lamplight enhanced her grace in pictorial contrasts.

Roger was struck by her sweetness and gazed tenderly at her rosy, pouting lips, from which no harsh word had ever been heard. She glanced at him, either to enjoy the effect she produced or to measure the end of the evening—all eyes saying the same thought. He, reading her glance, affected regret. “I must be going. I have a serious case to finish, and I am expected at home. Duty before all things—don’t you think so, my darling?”

Caroline looked resigned though pained. “Good-bye, then. Go, for if you stay an hour longer I cannot so lightly set you free.”

“My dearest,” he smiled, “I have three days’ holiday, and am supposed to be twenty leagues from Paris.”

A few days after the 6th of May anniversary, Caroline hurried one morning to the Rue Saint-Louis in the Marais, hoping not to arrive too late at a house she visited weekly. An express had summoned her: her mother, Madame Crochard, was sinking under complications of illnesses brought on by chronic catarrh and rheumatism.

While the coachman whipped up his horses at Caroline’s urging, the timid old women who had been Madame Crochard’s companions brought a priest into the neat second-floor rooms the widow occupied. Madame Crochard’s maid did not know that the pretty lady who often dined at her mistress’s was her daughter; she suggested a confessor, hoping such a priest might be useful to herself as to the dying woman. Between games and walks these old companions had, by constant talk, roused scruples in the widow’s stony heart—fears of hell and hopes of forgiveness if she returned to religion. So, on this solemn morning three ancient dames had settled into the drawing-room where Madame Crochard received visitors on Tuesdays. Each in turn left her armchair to sit at the poor woman’s bedside, offering the false consolations that the elderly so often repeat.

When the end drew near and the physician could no longer promise her life, the three dames debated whether to send for Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille. Francoise, the maid, was instructed that a commissionaire should go to the Rue Taitbout to inform the young relation whose influence so troubled them; yet they hoped the messenger would be too late. The widow, evidently enjoying some annuity, would not have been so tenderly courted by this trio if none of them or Francoise had thought herself an heir. The wealth enjoyed by Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille—whom Madame Crochard never called daughter—almost justified, in their minds, a scheme of dividing the old woman’s “pickings.”

Presently the guard suggested the Abbe Fontanon be fetched: “In another two hours she will neither have the wit nor the strength to write,” said one. The old cook went for the priest. He was a small-minded man by look: flabby cheeks, double chin, powdered hair and small brown eyes under a flat forehead.

“Monsieur l’Abbe,” said Francoise, “I thank you for all your advice; but believe me, I have taken the greatest care of the dear soul.”

The servant fell silent when she saw the landing and the most insinuating of the three dowagers there to receive the confessor’s blandities. When the priest had addressed the bigoted trio he entered the sickroom to sit by Madame Crochard. Decency and a little reserve kept the three women and Francoise in the drawing-room to make the grimaces of grief perfected by wrinkled faces.

“Oh, is it not ill luck!” cried Francoise. “This is the fourth mistress I have buried. The first left me a hundred francs a year, the second fifty crowns, and the third a thousand crowns down. After thirty years’ service, that is all I have to call my own.”

She crept to a cupboard where she could still hear the priest. “I see with pleasure, daughter,” Fontanon began, “that you have pious sentiments; you have a sacred relic round your neck.”

Madame Crochard, vaguely as if her wits were slipping, produced an Imperial Cross of the Legion of Honor. The Abbe started at the Emperor’s head; he rose, went again to the penitent, whose voice fell to a whisper; for several minutes Francoise could hear nothing through the partition.

“Woe upon me!” cried the old woman suddenly. “Do not desert me. What, Monsieur l’Abbe, do you think I shall be called to account for my daughter’s soul?”

The Abbe spoke too low, the partition was thick; Francoise remained in suspense until the dying woman cried, “Alas! the wretch has left me nothing that I can bequeath. When he robbed me of my dear Caroline, he parted us, and only allowed me three thousand francs a year, of which the capital belongs to my daughter.”

“Madame has a daughter, and nothing to live on but an annuity,” Francoise burst out into the drawing-room.

The three old crones exchanged glances of dismay. One, whose nose and chin nearly met in a superior hypocrisy, winked; and when Francoise’s back was turned she nodded privately as though to say, “That slut is too knowing by half; her name has figured in three wills already.”

The Abbe came out and, at a word from him, the witches flitted down the stairs leaving Francoise alone with her mistress. Madame Crochard, suffering more, rang with a hope barely credible; Francoise pretended to search cupboards as if hunting a lost lottery ticket.

Just as this crisis reached a climax, Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille stood by her mother’s bed lavishing tender words.

“Oh my dear mother, how criminal I have been! You are ill, and I did not know it; my heart did not warn me. However, here I am—”

“Caroline—”

“What is it?”

“They fetched a priest—”

“But send for a doctor, bless me!” cried Caroline. “Francoise, a doctor! How is it that these ladies never sent for a doctor?”

“They sent for a priest—” gasped the old woman.

“She is so ill—and no soothing draught, nothing on her table!”

The mother made a vague sign which Caroline’s watchful eye understood; she fell silent to let her mother speak.

“They brought a priest—to hear my confession, as they said.—Beware, Caroline!” cried the old woman with an effort, “the priest made me tell him your benefactor’s name.”

“But who can have told you, poor mother?”

The old woman died trying to look meaningfully cunning. If Caroline had noted her mother’s face, she might have seen a smile no one else would ever see—Death laughing.

To enter into the interests behind this introduction, we must, for a moment, forget these actors and look back at some previous incidents, the last closely concerned with Madame Crochard’s death. The two parts form a whole—a story made, by a law peculiar to Paris life, of two distinct sets of actions.

Toward the close of November 1805, a young barrister of about twenty-six was descending the stairs of the hotel of the High Chancellor of the Empire at about three in the morning. Having reached the courtyard in full evening dress under keen frost, he exclaimed in mild dismay at seeing no hackney coach and hearing no sounds of Parisian coachmen. Only the occasional pawing of the Chief Justice’s horses—he had left that gentleman still at bouillotte with Cambacérès—broke the silent court, scarcely lit by carriage lamps. Suddenly a hand rested on his shoulder and, turning, he found himself face to face with the Minister, to whom he bowed. The footman let down the steps of a carriage; the great man suspected the young pleader’s dilemma.

“All cats are gray in the dark,” the Minister said kindly. “The Chief Justice cannot compromise himself by putting a pleader in the way! Especially,” he went on, “when the pleader is the nephew of an old colleague, one of the lights of the Grand Council of State which gave France the Napoleonic Code.”

At a gesture from the Chief Justice, the youth got into the carriage.

“Where do you live?” asked the great man.

“Quai des Augustins, monseigneur.”

As the horses started, the young man found himself alone with the Minister to whom he had vainly tried to speak before and after Cambacérès’ sumptuous dinner; evidently the great man had avoided him throughout the evening.

“Well, Monsieur de Granville, you are on the high road!”

“So long as I sit by your Excellency’s side—”

“I am not jesting,” said the Minister gravely. “You were called two years since, and your defence in the Simeuse and Hauteserre case raised you high in your profession.”

“I had supposed my interest with those unfortunate émigrés had done me no good.”

“You are still very young,” he said. “But the High Chancellor was greatly pleased with you this evening. Get a judgeship in the lower courts; we want men. The nephew of a man in whom Cambacérès and I take great interest must not remain in the background for lack of encouragement. Your uncle helped us tide over a stormy season, and services of that kind are not forgotten.” He paused. “Before long I shall have three vacancies in the Lower Courts and in the Imperial Court in Paris. Come to see me and take the place you prefer. Till then work hard, but do not be seen at my receptions. In the first place, I am overwhelmed with work; besides, your rivals might suspect us and do you harm.”

They stopped at the Quai des Augustins; the young lawyer thanked his generous patron and knocked loudly at his door against the cold. The old porter handed him a letter.

The young man recognized the handwriting at his bedroom candle. “From my father!” he exclaimed. He read:

“Set off by the next mail; if you can get here soon enough, your fortune is made. Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems has lost her sister; she is now an only child; and, as we know, she does not hate you. Madame Bontems can now leave her about forty thousand francs a year, besides whatever she may give her when she marries. I have prepared the way.

“Our friends will wonder to see a family of old nobility allying with the Bontems; old Bontems was a red republican of the deepest dye, owning large quantities of nationalized land that he bought cheaply. But he held nothing but convent lands, and the monks will not come back; and then, as you have, so far, derogated as to become a lawyer, I cannot see why we should shrink from another concession to the prevalent ideas. The girl will have three hundred thousand francs; I can give you a hundred thousand; your mother’s property must be worth fifty thousand crowns, more or less; so if you choose a judgeship, my dear son, you are quite in a position to become a senator as much as any other man. My brother-in-law the Councillor of State will not indeed lend you a helping-hand; still, as he is unmarried, his property will some day be yours, and if you are not senator by your own efforts you will get it through him. Then you will be perched high enough to look on events. Farewell. Yours affectionately.”

Thus young Granville went to bed full of schemes. Under the protection of the High Chancellor, the Chief Justice and his mother’s brother—one of the originators of the Code—he was poised to begin a coveted career before the Empire’s highest court, and already saw himself a member of the bench from which Napoleon appointed chief functionaries. He could also envisage a fortune sufficient to keep his rank, since the slender five thousand francs from his mother’s estate would not suffice.

To crown his ambitions he thought of Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems, the companion of his childhood. Until boyhood his parents had allowed intimacy with their neighbor’s pretty daughter; as he grew, his proud family forbade the thought of her. For ten years Granville had glimpsed but rarely the girl he sometimes still thought of as “his little wife.” Their happiest times had been country festivals where they could steal glances at each other across the crowd.

By seven next morning he took the diligence for Caen. It was with deep emotion that the young lawyer again saw the cathedral spires at Bayeux. After a welcome feast by his father he was led to a familiar house in the Rue Teinture. The Count knocked loudly at a carriage gate about four in the afternoon. A young maid curtsied and said the ladies were just out of vespers.

The Count and his son were shown into a low, conventual drawing-room of dark walnut panels and stiff chairs. A stone mantel held a discolored mirror and crooked candle-brackets of an older time. Against a panel hung an enormous crucifix surrounded by a wreath of blessed box. The room’s severe cleanliness and lack of gaiety chilled the young man. The contrast between Cambacérès’ party and this austere retreat was stark—“Living here is not life!” he thought.

The Count noticed his son’s dismay. “Listen, my boy,” he said. “Old Bontems’ widow is a frenzied bigot. She is priest-ridden; they have persuaded her it is high time to secure Saint Peter’s keys by payments in advance. She goes to Mass every day, takes communion like a pious machine, restores chapels and gifts many ornaments. She has given so much religion that at the last Corpus Christi procession as many people came to stare at the priests’ splendid robes as would look on a public execution. This house is a sort of Holy Land. I even hindered her from giving three pictures to the Church—a Domenichino, a Correggio, and an Andrea del Sarto—worth a good deal.”

“But Angélique?” asked Granville.

“If you do not marry her, Angélique is done for,” the Count said. “Our holy apostles counsel her to live a virgin martyr. I have had the utmost difficulty stirring her little heart by talking to her of you; but as soon as she is married you will carry her off to Paris. There, married life, theatres, and society will soon make her forget confessionals, fasting, hair shirts, and Masses, which are the exclusive nourishment of such creatures.”

“But the fifty thousand francs a year from Church property? Will not all that return—”

“That is the point!” exclaimed the Count. “In consideration of this marriage—for Madame Bontems’ vanity is flattered by grafting the Bontems on the Granville tree—the mother agrees to settle her fortune absolutely on the girl, reserving only a life-interest. The priesthood therefore oppose the marriage; but I have had the banns published, everything is ready, and in a week you will be free of the mother’s clutches. You will have the prettiest girl in Bayeux, a little soul who will give you no trouble because she has sound principles. She has been mortified by fasting and prayer—and,” he added low, “by her mother.”

A modest tap at the door announced a little chorister boy sent in haste to fetch offices for the procession. “Will they go on much longer?” asked the Count.

“Oh, half an hour at most,” the boy replied.

“Let us go to look on,” said the Count. “There will be some pretty women there, and a visit to the Cathedral can do us no harm.”

Granville followed, still uneasy. “What is the matter?” the Count asked.

“The matter, father, is that I am sure I am right.”

“But you have said nothing.”

“No; but I have been thinking you have still ten thousand francs a year left of your original fortune. You will leave them to me—as long a time hence as possible, I hope. But if you are ready to give me a hundred thousand francs to make a foolish match, you will surely allow me to ask only fifty thousand to save me from such a misfortune and enjoy as a bachelor a fortune equal to what your Mademoiselle Bontems would bring me.”

“Are you crazy?” cried the Count.

“No, father. The Chief Justice promised me yesterday I should have a seat on the Bench. Fifty thousand francs added to what I have, and to the pay of my appointment, will give me an income of twelve thousand francs a year. I shall then most certainly have a chance of marrying a better fortune than this alliance, which will be poor in happiness if rich in goods.”

“It is very clear,” said the Count, “that you were not brought up under the old régime. Does a man of our rank ever allow his wife to be in his way?”

“But, my dear father, in these days marriage is—”

“Bless me!” cried the Count interrupting, “then what my old émigré friends tell me is true, I suppose. The Revolution has infected all the young men with vulgar principles. You, like my Jacobin brother-in-law, will harangue me on the Nation, Public Morals, and Disinterestedness!—Good Heavens! But for the Emperor’s sisters, where should we be?”

The hale old man, whom the peasants still called Seigneur de Granville, led his son into the Cathedral porch. Despite sanctity, as he dipped fingers in holy water he hummed a tune from an opera, then stopped among the pillars to study the congregation.

The special service of the Sacred Heart was about to begin. The affiliated ladies stood near the choir; the Count and son leaned against a column where the light was least and commanded a view. Suddenly, close to Granville, a voice sweeter than possible rose into song, a nightingale among the organ pipes. Though mingled with other voices, that soprano stirred him deeply. He turned and, seeing a young figure with face partly concealed by a large white bonnet, concluded the voice belonged to her. He nudged his father.

“Yes, there she is,” said the Count, who had already noticed an elderly woman whose false eyes had detected the strangers though she seemed absorbed in devotion.

Angélique lifted her face, gazing at the altar as if to inhale the incense. In the dim light Granville saw a face that shook his resolution. A white bonnet framed features of perfect regularity: pale gold hair parted in bands, eyebrows arching with fine precision, an aquiline nose in profile, lips like two rose lines, and light-blue eyes expressing innocence. Though her face showed rigid reserve, he attributed it to devotion. Her prayerful solemnity, visible even in cold, came through like a sweet mist.

Granville leaned involuntarily toward her to breathe this diviner air. The movement caught the girl’s notice; she diverted her gaze from the altar to him dimly visible in the gloom. She recognized the companion of her youth; remembrance shone on her face; she blushed. His joy proved brief: Angélique dropped her veil, assumed calm, and sang on without betraying emotion.

When the service ended his impatience was so great that he could not leave the ladies to go home alone; he went at once to make his bow to “his little wife.” They greeted each other bashfully in the Cathedral porch before the congregation. Madame Bontems was tremulous with pride as she took the Count de Granville’s arm, though the Count, forced to offer it publicly, was vexed at his son for his imprudent haste.

Thus two scenes, separated by years and circumstances, begin the tale: one domestic and immediate in the Rue Taitbout, with Caroline’s sheltered happiness and the ominous hint revealed at her dying mother’s confession; the other forming the earlier prelude in Bayeux and Paris—Granville’s career, ambitions, and the marriage project with Angélique Bontems. Both threads—Caroline’s precarious bliss, haunted by secrets about Roger, and the intricate social and financial calculations surrounding Granville—prepare the reader for the entanglements that will follow in a Paris where private lives are bound to public fortunes, and where confessions, religion, patronage, and ambition mingle to determine destinies.

About a fortnight before their wedding, the Vicomte de Granville began frequenting the dreary drawing-room where Mademoiselle Bontems lived, devoting his visits to observing Angelique’s character; his prudence had shown after their first meeting. He always found her at a little West Indian-wood table, marking trousseau linen. Angelique never spoke first on religion. If the young lawyer toyed with her handsome rosary in its green velvet bag, or laughed at a relic attached to it, she would gently take it back and replace it without a word. When Granville made mischievous remarks about religious practices, she answered with an obstinate smile.

“You must either believe nothing, or believe everything the Church teaches,” she said. “Would you wish to have a woman without a religion as the mother of your children?—No.—What man may dare judge between disbelievers and God? And how can I then blame what the Church allows?”

Angelique spoke with fervent charity; Granville sometimes felt tempted toward her convictions by the conviction in her eyes. But he mistook desire’s enchantment for love: the young man was seduced by her beauty and the apparent harmony of heart and duty. Young men are apt to infer moral perfection from physical beauty. If Angelique had not been free to show feeling, love might have withered; hidden bigotry was hard to detect.

During that fortnight Granville read Angelique like a book. She seemed gentle; he was grateful to Madame Bontems, who had implanted religious principles that made her resilient against life’s troubles.

At the contract-signing Madame Bontems made her son-in-law solemnly promise to respect his daughter’s religious practices: allow her perfect liberty of conscience, let her take communion, attend church and confession as often as she pleased, and never control her choice of priestly advisers. Angelique looked at him with such innocent eyes that Granville gave his word. The Abbe Fontanon, pale and directing consciences in the household, smiled; Mademoiselle Bontems, by a slight nod, seemed to promise she would not take unfair advantage of this freedom. The old Count whistled an old tune.

A few days after the wedding Granville and his wife went to Paris, where his appointment as public prosecutor to the Supreme Court of the Seine circuit recalled him.

Looking for a residence, Angelique used honeymoon influence to persuade her husband to take a large ground-floor apartment at the corner of the Vieille Rue du Temple and the Rue Neuve Saint-Francois. Her chief reason was proximity to the Rue d’Orleans church and a small chapel in the Rue Saint-Louis. She pointed out that the Marais was near the Palais de Justice and the lawyers they knew; a large garden and good stables suited a young couple and children, perhaps to come. Granville had wanted the Chaussee d’Antin, near theatres and amusements, but yielded to his young wife’s first choice.

Busy with demanding duties, he devoted himself to furnishing his private study and left the house’s decoration to Angelique, gladly letting her exercise taste while ashamed of depriving her of his company. But his taste was startled by a cold, arid solemnity pervading the rooms: nothing pleased his eye. The rigid mannerism of the Bayeux sitting-room infected his home; panels hollowed in circles decorated with arabesques, heavy panel color, dark green velvet benches, an antique-pattern lamp in the hall, black-and-white marble pavement and a paper imitating mossy stone gave a gloomy first impression. A handsome but not new barometer accentuated the void. Granville saw his wife so pleased by the red braid binding the cotton curtains and the strictly decent statue on a large Gothic stove that he lacked courage to challenge her convictions. He excused her choices as provincial naivety rather than owning that dealers had likely pushed old-fashioned goods upon a tasteless little bigot.

Other rooms were worse. The school of David dominated French art then, but none of that antique luxury found favor under Madame de Granville’s roof. The square drawing-room remained Louis XV in white and tarnished gold with checkered lattice and garlands; consoles, clocks, and candelabra bore military trophies ill-matched with delicate arabesques. Bigotry, with its tiresome humility and pride, had a horror of light, cheerful colors; Angelique seemed to believe brown and purple befitted a magistrate’s dignity, and to fear luxury that might suggest evil. The husband despaired. Seeing her regret at his lack of enthusiasm, he mistook it for affection rather than wounded self-conceit.

“Happiness, dear Angelique, does not depend on a more or less elegant piece of furniture; it depends on the wife’s sweetness, gentleness, and love.”

“Why, it is my duty to love you,” she said mildly, “and I can have no more delightful duty to carry out.”

From April, when married, till early winter they lived in apparent union. Love and hard work made Granville indifferent to many household matters. If on a Friday he found Lenten fare and asked for meat, Angelique, forbidden by the Gospel to tell a lie, would cloak purpose under some pretext and even scold the cook. Young lawyers did not keep every fast and vigil; Granville did not at first notice the regularity of Lenten meals, often disguised with tasteful fish and game to cheat his palate. He unconsciously lived in strict orthodoxy.

On week-days he did not know whether his wife went to Mass; on Sundays he accompanied her as indulgently. The theatres were unbearable in summer heat, and young love makes a man less exacting about amusements. So Granville failed to perceive the coldness, dignity, and reserve of a woman who might be a bigot waiting for love in folded arms.

Granville thus believed himself happy until a fatal event: in November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux Cathedral, who had once governed Madame Bontems’ and her daughter’s consciences, came to Paris seeking a church in the capital and perhaps a bishopric. He was horrified to find Angelique already so much changed by Paris and strove to reclaim her to his chilly fold. Frightened by this priest of provincial Catholicism and inflexible bigotry, Madame de Granville did penance and returned from her Jansenist errors.

It is tedious to detail all the circumstances that insensibly wrecked the household; a few facts suffice.

The first serious misunderstanding involved society invitations. Angelique accepted solemn functions but long declined balls, excusing herself with headache. One evening Granville, impatient with these assumed indispositions, destroyed a written invitation to a Councillor of State’s ball and gave his wife only a verbal one; seeing her health sound, he took her to the entertainment. On returning he reprimanded her.

“My dear,” he said, “your position, your rank, and fortune impose duties which no divine law can relieve you from. Are you not your husband’s pride? You are required to go to balls when I go, and to appear becomingly.”

“And what is so disastrous in my dress?”

“It is your manner,” he answered. “When a young man approaches you look so serious that a spiteful person might believe you doubtful of your own virtue. You seem to fear lest a smile should undo you. The world, my dearest, is not a convent.—But as you mentioned your dress, I may confess it is a duty to conform to Society’s customs and fashions.”

“Do you wish that I should display my shape like those indecent women who wear gowns so low that impudent eyes can stare at their bare shoulders and their—”

“There is a difference, my dear,” he interrupted. “You wear three rows of net frills that cover your throat to your chin. You look as if you had desired your dressmaker to destroy the graceful line of your shoulders and bosom. Your bust is wrapped in so many folds that everyone was laughing at your affectation of prudery. You would be grieved if I repeated the ill-natured remarks about your appearance.”

“Those who admire such obscenity will not have to bear the burden if we sin,” she retorted.

“And you did not dance?” he asked.

“I shall never dance,” she replied.

“If I tell you that you ought to dance!” he said sharply. “Yes, you ought to follow fashions, to wear flowers in your hair and diamonds. Remember, my dear, that rich people—and we are rich—are obliged to keep up luxury in the State. Is it not far better to encourage manufacturers than distribute money as alms through the clergy?”

“You talk as a statesman!” said Angelique.

“And you as a priest,” he retorted.

Their argument grew bitter. Her sweet voice hid obstinacy born of priestly influence. When she appealed to rights secured by his promise, she added that her director forbade balls; he pointed out the priest overstepped Church regulations. The theological quarrel flared when Granville proposed the play, and, in defiance, Angelique referred the question in writing to Rome: could a woman wear low gowns and go to the play and balls without risking salvation?

Pope Pius VII replied strongly, condemning his daughter’s recalcitrancy and blaming the priest. His letter, a chapter on conjugal duties, might have been dictated by Fenelon: “A wife is right to go wherever her husband may take her. Even if she sins by his command, she will not be ultimately held answerable.” These sentences only made Angelique and her director accuse him of irreligion.

Before this letter arrived, Granville had discovered his wife’s strict fasting and ordered his servants to serve him meat every day. Is it not an offence to be compelled to do what one would otherwise do freely? Of all tyrannies, the most odious robs the soul of the merit of its thoughts and deeds.

Soon Granville ceased to invite friends or give dinners; the house might have been shrouded in crape. A mistress’s bigotry creates its own atmosphere. The servants, chosen for piety, wore a sanctimonious physiognomy; bigots know one another and recommend like-minded servants passed from household to household. The impious see in such homes an indescribable squalor: an appearance of avarice, mystery, and the dank scent of cold incense. This methodical meanness and narrowness of thought are summed by one word—Bigotry. In these houses bigotry is written on furniture, prints, and faces; speech and silence alike are bigoted. Everyone is ill at ease; stiffness and formality infect everything; the lady perches on a throne of ice.

One morning Granville perceived with grief that bigotry had invaded his home. Different causes produce similar effects: dulness hedges these houses with walls of brass, enclosing the desert’s horrors. The home becomes not a tomb but a worse thing—a convent. In the center of this icy sphere the lawyer studied his wife dispassionately. He saw narrow-mindedness in the low hair on her forehead, a rigid regularity in her features that soon made him hate the assumed sweetness that had bewitched him. Intuition told him those thin lips might one day say, “My dear, it is for your good!”

Angelique’s complexion took on a dull pallor and austere expression that killed joy. Was this change wrought by ascetic pharisaism rather than piety? Beauty without expression is an imposture. Her imperturbable set smile seemed a Jesuitical formula of happiness; her charity offended and her soulless beauty was monstrous. She acted on duty, not feeling.

Some faults a wife may yield to experience or a husband’s warnings, but false religious ideas are immutable. The apotheosis of egotism—Self beyond the grave—triumphs over everything. Thus even the Pope was censured at the tribunal of the priest and the young devotee. To be always in the right consumes every other feeling in these tyrannous souls.

A secret struggle had been going on, and Granville soon wearied of a battle without end. What man can endure a hypocritically affectionate face and categorical resistance to his slightest wishes? What is to be done with a wife who takes advantage of his passion to protect her coldness, who appears determined to be blandly inexorable, prepares ecstatically to play martyr, and looks on her husband as a scourge from God—a means of flagellation that may spare her purgatory? Women who make virtue hateful by defying Saint John’s “Love one another” are hard to bear.

If a bonnet condemned to remain in a milliner’s window existed, Granville was certain to see it on his wife’s head; if a hideous material was found, she would select it. Want of taste is inseparable from false pietism.

Thus in a home needing fullest sympathy Granville had no true companionship. He went alone to parties and theatres. Nothing in his house appealed to him. A huge Crucifix hung between his bed and Angelique’s, figurative of his destiny: a Man-God killed in the prime of life and beauty. The ivory of that cross was less cold than Angelique crucifying her husband under the plea of virtue. Here, one Ash Wednesday, rose the pale form of Fasting and Total Abstinence commanded in severe tones—and Granville did not deem it wise to write to the Pope and take the Consistory’s opinion on observances.

His misfortune was too great to complain: he had a pretty young wife attached to duty, virtuous—a model. She had a child every year, nursed them herself, and brought them up in high principles; the old women of her circle admired her piety and regarded her as a martyr. They blamed not the wife’s scruples but the husband’s barbarous philoprogenitiveness.

Granville, worn by work, bereft of conjugal consolation, and weary of a lonely world, by thirty-two had sunk into the Slough of Despond. He hated life. Not wishing to set an example of dissipation, he tried to deaden feeling by hard study and began a great book on Law.

But he could not find monastic peace. When Angelique saw him forsake society to work at home, she tried to convert him. She wept thinking he might die impenitent, and could not rest until she had tried to snatch him from eternal fires. Thus Granville became the object of the mean ideas, vacuous arguments, and narrow views by which she—thinking she had achieved a first victory—aimed for a second by bringing him back within the Church’s pale.

This was the last straw. What can be more intolerable than the blind struggle of a bigot’s obstinacy against a lawyer’s acumen? What more terrible than acrimonious pin-pricks to which a passionate soul prefers a dagger-thrust? Granville neglected his home. Everything was unendurable. His children, broken by their mother’s frigid despotism, dared not go with him to the play; he could never give them pleasure without punishment from their terrible mother. His loving nature withered into indifference, a selfishness worse than death. He saved his boys from this home by sending them early to school and insisting on his right to train them; he seldom interfered with daughters, resolving they should marry as soon as possible.

Had he sought violent measures he could find no justification; his wife, backed by dowagers, would have condemned him publicly. Thus Granville had no choice but to live in complete isolation; crushed by misery he avoided intimacy with worldly women, having no hope of consolation.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The improving history of this melancholy household produced no notable events between 1806 and 1825. Madame de Granville remained unchanged after losing her husband’s affection. She paid for Masses, beseeching God and the Saints to reveal what faults displeased him and to show her how to win him back; yet the more fervent her prayers, the less Granville was seen at home.

For about five years, after obtaining a high judicial post, Granville lived in the entresol to avoid his wife. Every morning a scene took place—one that, if evil tongues are believed, repeats in many households broken by incompatibility or vice. At eight a housekeeper, nunlike in appearance, rang at Granville’s door. Admitted to the room next to his study, she repeated the same message to the footman in the same tone:

“Madame would be glad to know whether Monsieur le Comte has had a good night, and if she is to have the pleasure of his company at breakfast.”

“Monsieur presents his compliments to Madame la Comtesse,” the valet would answer after speaking with his master, “and begs her to excuse him; important business compels him to be in court this morning.”

A minute later she returned to ask if she might see the Count before he went out.

“He is gone,” was always the reply, though often his carriage still waited.

This proxy dialogue became ceremonial. Granville’s servant, a favorite with his master and cause of quarrels for his irreligious, dissipated conduct, sometimes entered the master’s room when the Count was absent and returned with the same formula.

The injured wife watched for his return, standing on the steps like an embodiment of remorse. Petty aggressiveness at the root of a monastic temper formed Madame de Granville’s character; she was thirty-five and looked forty. When forced by decency to speak to her husband or dine at home, she inflicted acid-sweet remarks and the intolerable dulness of her narrow circle, trying to put him in the wrong before servants and charitable friends.

When the post of President in a provincial court—a stepping-stone to a peer’s seat—was offered to the Comte de Granville, he begged to remain in Paris. This refusal, known only to the Keeper of the Seals, provoked conjecture among the Countess’s intimates and her director. Granville, a wealthy man of a leading Norman family earning a hundred thousand francs a year, abandoning his great book on Law and the prospect of promotion, and living dissipatedly for six years, seemed inexplicable. The Countess’s confessor, who eyed a bishopric as much through family influence as service, insinuated calumnies: perhaps Granville feared provincial life because he would have to lead a regular moral life with the Countess—unless some illicit connection had alienated him. The sanctimonious woman accepted these hints, and they destroyed her remaining illusions.

Ignorant of the world and of love’s follies, Angélique could not conceive of a marriage unlike the one that had estranged her husband and believed him incapable of crime. When he ceased to demand anything of her, she supposed the calm was natural; after giving him all her love, the priest’s suggestions undermined her convictions. She defended her husband yet could not erase the suspicion so ingeniously sown.

These alarms wrecked her weak mind and made her ill; low fever consumed her. During Lent 1822 she would not omit her austerities and fell into a decline that endangered her life. Granville’s indifference added to her torment; his care was perfunctory, like a nephew obliged to an old uncle.

Though the Countess had given up nagging and tried to greet her husband with affection, the bigot’s sharpness showed through; one speech could undo a week’s progress.

By late May, spring and better food restored some strength. One morning after Mass she sat on a garden bench, the sun recalling early married days, and reviewed the years to find where she had failed as wife and mother. She was interrupted by the Abbé Fontanon in agitation.

“Has any misfortune befallen you, Father?” she asked.

“Ah! I only wish,” cried the priest, “that all the woes inflicted on you by God were visited on me; but, my admirable friend, there are trials to which you must bow.”

“Can any worse punishments await me than those with which Providence crushes me by making my husband the instrument of His wrath?”

“You must prepare yourself,” said Fontanon, “for yet worse mischief than we or your pious friends have conceived.”

“Then I may thank God,” said the Countess, “for using you as messenger of His will.”

“He measures your sufferings by the strength of your resignation and the weight of your sins.”

“Speak; I am ready to hear!” she said, looking up to heaven. “Speak, Monsieur Fontanon.”

“For seven years Monsieur Granville has lived in sin with a concubine, by whom he has two children; on this adulterous connection he has spent more than five hundred thousand francs that ought to have belonged to his legitimate family.”

“I must see it to believe it!” cried the Countess.

“Far be it from you!” the Abbé exclaimed. “You must forgive, my daughter, and wait in patience and prayer till God enlightens your husband; unless you choose to use remedies offered by human laws.”

The long conversation that followed produced an extraordinary change. She abruptly dismissed the priest, summoned her servants—alarmed by her flushed face and frantic energy—ordered her carriage and countermanded it, changed her mind repeatedly; but at last, about three, as if resolved, she went out, leaving the household amazed.

“Is the Count coming home to dinner?” she asked his servant.

“No, madame.”

“Did you go with him to the Courts this morning?”

“Yes, madame.”

“And to-day is Monday?”

“Yes, madame.”

“Then do the Courts sit on Mondays nowadays?”

“Devil take you!” cried the man, as his mistress drove off after saying to the coachman, “Rue Taitbout.”

Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille was weeping; Roger, at her side, held one of her hands. He was silent, looking at little Charles—who, not understanding his mother’s grief, stood speechless—at the cot where Eugénie slept, and at Caroline’s face, on which sorrow had fallen like rain across cheerful beams.

“Yes, my darling,” said Roger after a long silence, “that is the great secret: I am married. But some day I hope we may form one family. My wife has been given over since last March. I do not wish her dead; yet if God takes her, I believe she will be happier in Paradise than here, where she is indifferent to grief and pleasure.”

“How I hate that woman! How could she make you unhappy? Yet it is to that unhappiness I owe my happiness!” Her tears ceased.

“Caroline, let us hope,” cried Roger. “Do not be frightened by anything the priest may have said to you. Though my wife’s confessor is a man to be feared for his power in the congregation, if he should try to blight our happiness I would find means—”

“What could you do?”

“We would go to Italy: I would fly—”

A shriek from the adjoining room made Roger start and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille quake; she rushed into the drawing-room and found Madame de Granville in a dead faint. When she recovered, she sighed deeply on finding herself supported by the Count and her rival, whom she instinctively pushed away. Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille rose to withdraw.

“You are at home, madame,” said Granville, taking Caroline by the arm. “Stay.”

The Judge took his wife, carried her to the carriage, and got in with her.

“Who has brought you to the point of wishing me dead, of resolving to fly?” asked the Countess with grief and indignation. “Was I not young? You thought me pretty—what fault have you to find with me? Have I been false? Have I not been virtuous? My heart cherished no image but yours, my ears listened to no other voice. What duty have I failed in? What have I denied you?”

“Happiness, madame,” said the Count severely. “You know there are two ways of serving God. Some think that by saying set prayers, attending Mass regularly and avoiding sin they will win heaven—but they, madame, will go to hell; they have not loved God for Himself, they have not worshiped as He chooses, they have made no sacrifice. Though mild in seeming, they are hard on their neighbors; they see the law’s letter, not its spirit. This is how you have treated me—your earthly husband; you have sacrificed my happiness to your salvation. You were always absorbed in prayer when I came to you in gladness; you wept when you should have cheered my toil; you never tried to satisfy my demands.”

“And if they were wicked,” cried the Countess hotly, “was I to lose my soul to please you?”

“It is a sacrifice another, a more loving woman, has dared to make,” said Granville coldly.

“Dear God!” she cried, bursting into tears, “Thou hearest! Has he been worthy of the prayers and penance I have lived in, wearing myself out to atone for his sins and my own?—Of what avail is virtue?”

“To win Heaven, my dear. A woman cannot be both a husband’s wife and the spouse of Christ; that would be bigamy. By stripping your soul of the love and devotion God commands you to have for me, you have cherished hatred—”

“Have I not loved you?” she interposed.

“No, madame.”

“Then what is love?” the Countess involuntarily asked.

“Love, my dear,” replied Granville with a kind of ironic surprise, “you are incapable of understanding it. The cold sky of Normandy is not that of Spain. To yield to caprices, to guess them, to find pleasure in pain, to sacrifice the world’s opinion, your pride, your religion even, and still regard these offerings as mere incense burnt to an idol—that is love—”

“The love of ballet-girls!” cried the Countess in horror. “Such flames cannot last and leave only ashes and despair. A wife should bring true friendship, equable warmth—”

“You speak of warmth as negroes speak of ice,” retorted the Count with a sardonic smile. “Even the humblest daisy has more charm than the gaudiest hawthorn. At the same time,” he added, “I will do you justice. You have kept so precisely to the straight path of imagined duty that to show wherein you have failed toward me I should enter into details that would offend your dignity and seem to undermine all morality.”

“And you dare speak of morality when you have just left the house where you have dissipated your children’s fortune in debaucheries?” cried the Countess, maddened by his reticence.

“There, madame, I must correct you,” said the Count coolly. “Though Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille is rich, it is at nobody’s expense. My uncle, master of his fortune and with several heirs, in his lifetime and out of pure friendship, gave her the estate of Bellefeuille. As for anything else, I owe it to his liberality—”

“Such conduct is worthy only of a Jacobin!” said the sanctimonious Angélique.

“Madame, you forget that your own father was one of the Jacobins you scorn,” said the Count sternly. “Citizen Bontems was signing death-warrants at a time when my uncle was doing France good service.”

Madame de Granville was silenced. But the sight she had seen reawakened the jealousy nothing could kill, and she murmured, as if to herself, “How can a woman thus destroy her own soul and that of others?”

“Bless me, madame,” replied the Count, tired of the dialogue, “you yourself may one day have to answer that question.” The Countess was frightened. “You perhaps will be excused by the merciful Judge, who will weigh our sins,” he went on, “in consideration of the conviction with which you have worked out my misery. I do not hate you—I hate those who have perverted your heart and reason. You have prayed for me, just as Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille has given me her heart and crowned my life with love. You should have been my mistress and the prayerful saint by turns. Do me the justice to confess I am no reprobate, no debauchee. My life was not sordid. Alas! after seven years of wretchedness, the craving for happiness led me, by an imperceptible descent, to love another woman and make a second home. Do not imagine I am singular; thousands of husbands here live such twofold lives.”

“Great God!” cried the Countess. “How heavy the cross Thou hast laid on me to bear! If the husband Thou hast given me below can only be made happy through my death, take me to Thyself!”

“If you had always breathed such admirable sentiments and devotion, we should be happy yet,” said the Count coldly.

“Indeed,” cried Angélique, melting into a flood of tears, “forgive me if I have done wrong. Yes, monsieur, I am ready to obey you in all things; henceforth I will be all you can wish your wife to be.”

“If your purpose, madame, is to compel me to say I no longer love you, I shall find the cruel courage to tell you so. Can I command my heart? Can I wipe out in an instant traces of fifteen years of suffering?—I have ceased to love. These words contain a mystery as deep as the words ‘I love.’ Esteem, respect, friendship may be won, lost, regained; but as to love—I might school myself a thousand years and it would not blossom again, especially for a woman too old to respond to it.”

“I hope, Monsieur le Comte, I sincerely hope such words may not be spoken to you one day by the woman you love,” she said.

“Will you put on a dress a la Grecque this evening and come to the Opera?”

The shudder with which the Countess received the suggestion was a mute reply.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Early in December 1833 a man with perfectly white hair and worn features, aged more by grief than by years, walked at midnight along the Rue Gaillon. He paused before a modest two-story house to look up at an attic window piercing the roof. A dim light showed through panes, some mended with paper. He watched the wavering glimmer with the vague curiosity of a Paris idler when a young man came out. As the street lamp fell on his face, he approached the passerby with the hesitancy of one afraid of mistaking an acquaintance.

“What, is it you,” cried he, “Monsieur le Président? Alone at this hour, and so far from the Rue Saint-Lazare. Allow me the honor of giving you my arm.—The pavement is so greasy this morning that if we do not hold each other up,” he added to soothe the elder man, “we shall find it hard to escape a tumble.”

“But, my dear sir, I am no more than fifty-five, unfortunately for me,” replied the Comte de Granville. “A physician of your celebrity must know that at that age a man is still hale and strong.”

“Then you are in waiting on a lady, I suppose,” Horace Bianchon replied. “You're not in the habit of going about Paris on foot; when a man keeps such fine horses—”

“Still, when I am not visiting in the evening, I commonly return from the Courts or the club on foot,” the Count answered.

“And with large sums of money about you, perhaps!” cried the doctor. “It is a positive invitation to the assassin’s knife.”

“I am not afraid of that,” said Granville with melancholy indifference.

“But at least do not stand about,” said the doctor, leading the Count toward the boulevard. “A little more and I should believe you bent on robbing me of my last illness, and dying by some hand other than mine.”

“You caught me playing the spy,” said the Count. “Whether on foot or in a carriage, I've for some time observed at a third-floor window of your house the shadow of a person who seems to work with heroic constancy.”

The Count paused as if feeling sudden pain. “And I take as great an interest in that garret,” he went on, “as a citizen of Paris must feel in the finishing of the Palais Royal.”

“Well,” said Horace Bianchon eagerly, “I can tell you—”

“Tell me nothing,” replied Granville, cutting the doctor short. “I would not give a centime to know whether the shadow that moves across that shabby blind is that of a man or a woman, nor whether the inhabitant of that attic is happy or miserable. Though I was surprised to see no one at work there this evening, and though I stopped to look, it was solely for the pleasure of indulging in conjectures as numerous and idiotic as those of idlers who see a building left half finished. For nine years, my young—” the Count hesitated; then he waved his hand, exclaiming—“No, I will not say friend—I hate everything that savors of sentiment.—Well, for nine years past I have ceased to wonder that old men amuse themselves with growing flowers and planting trees; the events of life have taught them disbelief in all human affection; and I grew old within a few days. I will no longer attach myself to any creature but to unreasoning animals, or plants, or superficial things. I think more of Taglioni’s grace than of all human feeling. I abhor life and the world in which I live alone. Nothing, nothing,” he went on, in a tone that startled the younger man, “no, nothing can move or interest me.”

“But you have children?”

“My children!” he repeated bitterly. “Yes—well, is not my eldest daughter the Comtesse de Vandenesse? The other will, through her sister’s connections, make some good match. As to my sons, have they not succeeded? The Viscount was public prosecutor at Limoges, and is now President of the Court at Orleans; the younger is public prosecutor in Paris. My children have their own cares and business to attend to. If of all those hearts one had been devoted to me, if one had tried by entire affection to fill up the void I have here,” and he struck his breast, “well, that one would have failed in life, have sacrificed it to me. And why should he? To bring sunshine into my few remaining years—and would he have succeeded? Might I not have taken such generosity as a debt? But, doctor,” and the Count smiled with deep irony, “it is not for nothing that we teach them arithmetic and how to count. At this moment perhaps they are waiting for my money.”

“O Monsieur le Comte, how could such an idea enter your head—you who are kind, friendly, and humane! Indeed, if I were not myself a living proof of the benevolence you exercise so liberally and so nobly—”

“To please myself,” replied the Count. “I pay for a sensation, as I would to-morrow pay a pile of gold to recover the most childish illusion that would but make my heart glow.—I help my fellow-creatures for my own sake, just as I gamble; and I look for gratitude from none. I should see you die without blinking; and I beg you to feel the same with regard to me. I tell you, young man, the events of life have swept over my heart like the lavas of Vesuvius over Herculaneum. The town is there—dead.”

“Those who have brought a soul as warm and as living as yours was to such a pitch of indifference are indeed guilty!”

“Say no more,” said the Count, with a shudder of aversion.

“You have a malady which you ought to allow me to treat,” said Bianchon in a tone of deep emotion.

“What, do you know of a cure for death?” cried the Count irritably.

“I undertake, Monsieur le Comte, to revive the heart you believe frozen.”

“Are you a match for Talma, then?” asked the Count satirically.

“No, Monsieur le Comte. But Nature is as far above Talma as Talma is superior to me.—Listen: the garret you are interested in is inhabited by a woman of about thirty whose love is carried to fanaticism. The object of her adoration is a young man of pleasing appearance but endowed by some malignant fairy with every conceivable vice. This fellow is a gambler, and it is hard to say which he is most addicted to—wine or women; he has, to my knowledge, committed acts deserving punishment by law. Well, to him this unhappy woman sacrificed a life of ease, a man who worshiped her, and the father of her children.—But what is wrong, Monsieur le Comte?”

“Nothing. Go on.”

“She has allowed him to squander a perfect fortune; she would, I believe, give him the world if she had it; she works night and day; and many a time she has, without a murmur, seen the wretch she adores rob her even of the money saved to buy the children’s clothes and their food for the morrow. Only three days ago she sold her hair, the finest hair I ever saw; he came in, she could not hide the gold piece quickly enough, and he asked her for it. For a smile, for a kiss, she gave up the price of a fortnight’s life and peace. Is it not dreadful, and yet sublime?—But work is wearing her cheeks hollow. Her children’s crying has broken her heart; she is ill, and at this moment on her wretched bed. This evening they had nothing to eat; the children had not strength to cry—they were silent when I went up.”

Horace Bianchon stood still. Just then the Comte de Granville, despite himself, had put his hand into his waistcoat pocket.

“I can guess, my young friend, how it is that she is yet alive if you attend her,” said the elder man.

“O poor soul!” cried the doctor, “who could refuse to help her? I only wish I were richer, for I hope to cure her of her passion.”

“But how can you expect me to pity a form of misery whose joys to me would seem cheaply purchased with my whole fortune!” exclaimed the Count, taking his hand out of his pocket empty of the notes which Bianchon had supposed his patron to be feeling for. “That woman feels, she is alive! Would not Louis XV. have given his kingdom to rise from the grave and have three days of youth and life! And is not that the history of thousands of dead men, thousands of sick men, thousands of old men?”

“Poor Caroline!” cried Bianchon.

At the name the Count shuddered and grasped the doctor’s arm with a grip of iron, as it seemed to Bianchon.

“Her name is Caroline Crochard?” asked the President in a voice evidently broken.

“Then you know her?” said the doctor, astonished.

“And the wretch’s name is Solvet.—Ay, you have kept your word!” exclaimed Granville; “you have roused my heart to the most terrible pain it can suffer till it is dust. That emotion, too, is a gift from hell, and I always know how to pay those debts.”

By this time the Count and the doctor had reached the corner of the Rue de la Chaussée d’Antin. One of those night-birds, with a basket and crook, facetiously called the Committee of Research during the Revolution, stood by the curb where the two men stopped. This scavenger had a shriveled face worthy of those immortalized by Charlet in his caricatures of the sweepers of Paris.

“Do you ever pick up a thousand-franc note?”

“Now and then, master.”

“And you restore them?”

“It depends on the reward offered.”

“You’re the man for me,” cried the Count, giving the man a thousand-franc note. “Take this, but remember, I give it to you on condition of your spending it at the wineshop, of your getting drunk, fighting, beating your wife, blacking your friends’ eyes. That will give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist—perhaps to the police, the public prosecutor, the judge, and the prison warders. Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later.”

A draughtsman would need the pencils of Charlet and Callot, and the brushes of Teniers and Rembrandt, to render this night scene.

“Now I have squared accounts with hell, and had some pleasure for my money,” said the Count, pointing out the indescribable physiognomy of the gaping scavenger to the doctor, who stood stupefied. “As for Caroline Crochard—she may die of hunger and thirst, hearing the heartrending shrieks of her starving children and convinced of the man’s baseness. I will not give a sou to rescue her; and because you have helped her, I will see you no more—”

The Count left Bianchon standing like a statue, and walked as briskly as a young man to the Rue Saint-Lazare, soon reaching his little house, where, to his surprise, he found a carriage waiting at the door.

“Monsieur, your son, the attorney-general, came about an hour since,” said the man-servant, “and is waiting for you in your bedroom.”

Granville signed to the man to leave him.

“What motive can be strong enough to require you to infringe the order I have given my children never to come to me unless I send for them?” asked the Count of his son as he went into the room.

“Father,” the son replied in a tremulous voice and with great respect, “I venture to hope that you will forgive me when you have heard me.”

“Your reply is proper,” said the Count. “Sit down,” and he pointed to a chair, “But whether I walk up and down, or take a seat, speak without heeding me.”

“Father,” the son went on, “this afternoon, at four o’clock, a very young man who was arrested in the house of a friend of mine, whom he had robbed to a considerable extent, appealed to you.—He says he is your son.”

“His name?” asked the Count hoarsely.

“Charles Crochard.”

“That will do,” said the father with an imperious wave of the hand.

Granville paced the room in solemn silence, and his son took care not to break it.

“My son,” he began, and the words were pronounced in a voice so mild and fatherly that the young lawyer started, “Charles Crochard spoke the truth.—I am glad you came to me to-night, my good Eugène,” he added. “Here is a considerable sum of money”—and he gave him a bundle of banknotes—“you can make any use of them you think proper in this matter. I trust you implicitly, and approve beforehand whatever arrangements you may make, either now or for the future.—Eugène, my dear son, kiss me. We part perhaps for the last time. Tomorrow I shall crave my dismissal from the King, and I am going to Italy.

“Though a father owes no account of his life to his children, he is bound to bequeath the experience Fate sells him—part of their inheritance.—When you marry,” the Count went on with a little involuntary shudder, “do not undertake it lightly; that act is the most important of all which society requires of us. Remember to study at your leisure the character of the woman who is to be your partner; but consult me too, I will judge of her myself. A lack of union between husband and wife, from whatever cause, leads to terrible misfortune; sooner or later we are always punished for contravening the social law.—But I will write to you on this subject from Florence. A father who presides over a supreme court must not have to blush in the presence of his son. Good-bye.”

PARIS, February 1830–January 1842.
