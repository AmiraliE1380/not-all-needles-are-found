DEDICATION

To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini.

Madame, remembering the pleasure of your conversation in Milan, I dedicate this work to you, hoping you will shelter it with your name as you once protected a beloved writer’s tales. You have a daughter, Eugénie, whose beauty and intelligence promise a happy childhood, unlike the rigid upbringing suffered by the Eugénie of these pages. Though Frenchmen are often called fickle, I remain Italian in constancy and memory. Writing “Eugénie” recalls the laughter and warmth of our time in Milan; I now regard you as a distant, treasured masterpiece.

Your devoted servant,
De Balzac.

A DAUGHTER OF EVE

CHAPTER I. THE TWO MARIES

In a sumptuous blue-velvet boudoir on rue Neuve-des-Mathurins two young women wept by the fire. The room belonged to Ferdinand du Tillet, a rich banker who had bought social standing through marriage but lacked aristocratic ease. Madame du Tillet clasped her sister Madame Félix de Vandenesse’s hand as they exchanged confidences. Reared by a strict, pious mother and married without youthful freedoms, they entered matrimony with innocence and little experience of passion; marriage, imagined as escape, revealed unforeseen realities.

CHAPTER II. A CONFIDENCE BETWEEN SISTERS

Madame de Vandenesse, distraught by marital troubles, poured out her despair over a husband indifferent to her inner life. Madame du Tillet, equally helpless, described her own cold domesticity. Each confessed a craving for passion and excitement suppressed by society and duty; each sought consolation in the other.

CHAPTER III. THE HISTORY OF A FORTUNATE WOMAN

Félix de Vandenesse, of noble lineage, had married hoping to recreate an idealized love. The duties and constraints of his class, however, cooled the relationship. Though kind and attentive, he could not supply the romantic intensity his wife desired. Monotony awakened in her a longing for deeper emotional fulfillment and tempted thoughts of love beyond marriage.

CHAPTER IV. A CELEBRATED MAN

Raoul Nathan, an advancing literary figure, entered society with talent, audacity, and caustic wit. His unconventional, attractive manner caught Madame de Vandenesse’s attention at Madame de Montcornet’s salon; their early exchanges hinted at an unspoken attraction. Nathan, juggling a fraught career and private troubles, saw her as a potential muse, though social position and decorum threatened any open liaison.

CHAPTER V. FLORINE

Florine, a gifted actress and Nathan’s longtime companion, balanced affection with practicality: she sustained his spirits and career while depending on him for protection and status. As Nathan’s admiration for Madame de Vandenesse grew, Florine felt the shift and grew jealous.

CHAPTER VI. ROMANTIC LOVE

Nathan and Madame de Vandenesse bonded over art and literature. Their encounters grew intimate, bringing exhilaration and peril. She struggled with guilt and desire, torn between loyalty and the lure of a passionate life, aware of the social consequences.

CHAPTER VII. SUICIDE

Crushed by setbacks and inner despair, Nathan contemplated suicide. A chance meeting with Madame de Vandenesse revived his will to live; seeing his desperation, she resolved to protect and encourage him, countering his darkest impulses.

CHAPTER VIII. A LOVER SAVED AND LOST

After his crisis, she became more involved, balancing protection with her own feelings. Their secret relationship became both strength and turmoil as social risk and Florine’s jealousy complicated matters. Nathan, improving in fortune, found himself torn between Florine’s practical devotion and the countess’s inspiring influence.

CHAPTER IX. THE HUSBAND’S TRIUMPH

Félix, sensing his wife’s attachment to Nathan, moved to preserve their marriage. Determined to uncover the truth and shield her from scandal, he acted to defend her honor and their union. Love, ambition, loyalty, and expectation remain entangled, leading to a dramatic clash between desire and duty.
