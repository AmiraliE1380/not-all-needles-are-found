THE MARRIAGE CONTRACT

CHAPTER I. PRO AND CON

Monsieur de Manerville was a respectable Norman gentleman known to the Maréchal de Richelieu, who had married him to a wealthy Bordeaux heiress when the duke governed Guienne. The Norman sold his Bessin estate, became a Gascon by acquisition of the château de Lanstrac, and toward the close of Louis XV’s reign bought the post of major of the Gate Guards. He survived the Revolution and lived until 1813.

In late 1790 he went to Martinique, leaving his Guienne property to Mathias, a notary’s clerk who, though sympathetic to new ideas, managed the estates prudently. On his return the Comte de Manerville found his lands intact and well kept—the Gascon management grafted successfully onto the Norman holding.

Madame de Manerville died in 1810. Through youthful dissipations the old man had become grasping and miserly, allowing almost nothing to his only son.

Paul de Manerville returned from the college of Vendôme in 1810 and for three years lived under his father’s strict rule. The tyranny of a man of seventy shaped a young heart and unformed character. Paul, naturally Gascon in courage, dared not resist his sire and lost the faculty of resistance that breeds moral courage. His thwarted desires turned inward; later, when they clashed with the world’s maxims, he could think rightly but act wrongly. He might fight passionately for a look or word yet trembled to dismiss a servant—his timidity showing where steady will was required. Capable of great deeds to escape persecution, he would not have prevented it by steady opposition. Timid in thought, daring in action, he retained a simplicity that made him a dupe and a voluntary victim of things others revolt against. Imprisoned by his father’s mansion and without money to associate with peers, he envied their pleasures without sharing them.

Every evening his father took him in a shabby carriage to royalist houses, among the relics of parliamentary and martial nobility. Crushed by the fortunes of maritime cities, these families formed a landed aristocracy that disdained commercial display. Too young to appreciate such distinctions, Paul was bored by these ancients, unaware that such connections would later secure the social preeminence so prized in France.

He found compensation in manual exercises his father insisted upon: fencing, arms, riding, tennis, and good manners—old-nobility accomplishments said to make a gentleman. Paul took fencing lessons, rode, practiced at a pistol gallery, and spent the rest of his time reading novels; his father forbade abstruse studies thought necessary by modern educators.

A monotonous life would have crushed him but for his father’s death, which freed him when life became intolerable. Paul then inherited considerable capital amassed by paternal avarice and well-kept estates. He disliked Bordeaux; Lanstrac, his father’s summer hunting retreat, did not attract him.

Once his estate was settled, the young heir bought consols with his capital, left estate management to Mathias, and spent six years away: attached to the French embassy at Naples, secretary at Madrid, then in London—touring Europe.

After seeing the world, losing illusions, and dissipating his father’s capital, Paul drew on revenues Mathias had prudently saved. Struck by a supposed virtuous impulse, he resolved to leave Paris, return to Bordeaux, regulate his affairs, live as a country gentleman at Lanstrac, improve his property, marry, and eventually become a deputy.

As a count, nobility had matrimonial value again; he could and ought to make a good marriage. In exchange for seven hundred thousand francs squandered in six years he had gained experience: a social apprenticeship in love affairs, duels, lost bets, disillusions, toils, and pleasures. He had become “elegant.” Though extravagant, he was not a mere man of fashion; the man of elegance ranks above the mere fashionable man. Paul enjoyed and sustained this reputation. His servants were well dressed, his carriages noted, and his suppers in vogue; his bachelor establishment compared with the finest Paris houses.

He had not debased any woman; he gambled openly, led no one on, kept no love-letters. Avoiding his Guienne property encouraged a certain caution—he neither borrowed nor displayed a reckless extravagance that attracts attention. He lent foolishly to friends who deserted him. His dissipations had a method. The secret of his character lay in his father’s tyranny, which made him, as it were, a social mongrel.

One morning he told his friend de Marsay, who later became celebrated, of his plans.

“My dear fellow, life has a meaning.”

“You must be twenty-seven to find it out,” laughed de Marsay.

“Well, I am twenty-seven; because I am twenty-seven I mean to live the life of a country gentleman at Lanstrac. I’ll move my belongings to Bordeaux, and spend three months a year in Paris in this house I shall keep.”

“Will you marry?”

“I will marry.”

“I’m your friend,” said de Marsay after a pause, “and I say to you: settle down into a worthy father and husband and you’ll be ridiculous for the rest of your days. If you could be happy and ridiculous, it might be considered; but you will not be happy. You don’t have a strong wrist to manage a household. I’ll do you justice and say you are a perfect horseman; no one knows how to make a horse prance and sit firm. But marriage is another thing. I see you, led by Madame la Comtesse de Manerville, going where you would not, oftener at a gallop than a trot, and presently unhorsed—unhorsed into a ditch with your legs broken. Listen. You still have some forty-odd thousand francs a year from your Gironde property. Good. Keep your horses and servants and furnish your house in Bordeaux; you can be king of Bordeaux, promulgate our Paris edicts; be the correspondent of our stupidities. Play the rake in the provinces; commit follies; follies may win you celebrity. But don't marry. Who marries nowadays? Merchants for capital, peasants for children to work, brokers and notaries for wives' dots, miserable kings to continue dynasties. We are exempt from the pack, and you want to shoulder it! Why marry? If you marry an heiress as rich as yourself, eighty thousand francs for two is not forty for one; two soon become three or four with children. You haven't love for that silly race of Manerville that would only hamper you? Do you know what a father and mother must be? Marriage, my old Paul, is the silliest social immolation; our children alone profit and don't know its price until their horses nibble the flowers on our grave. Do you regret your father, that old tyrant who made your first years wretched? How can you be sure your children will love you? The very care you take of their education and necessary sternness will lessen their affection. Children love a weak or prodigal father, whom they will despise later. You'll live between fear and contempt. No man is a good head of a family merely by willing it. Look among all our friends and name one you would like for a son. We have known many who dishonor their names. Children, my dear Paul, are the most difficult merchandise to keep. Yours, you think, will be angels; well, so be it! Have you sounded the gulf between bachelor and married life? As a bachelor you can say: ‘I shall never exhibit more than a certain amount of the ridiculous; the public will think of me as I choose.’ Married, you'll drop into the infinitude of the ridiculous. As a bachelor you make your own happiness; you enjoy some today and forego tomorrow; married, you must take it as it comes, and the day you want it you'll go without it. Marry and you'll grow a blockhead; you'll calculate dowries; you'll talk morality, civic and religious; you'll call young men immoral; in short, you'll become a social academician. It's pitiable! The old bachelor hoarding his spoon is blessed compared with a married man. I speak not merely of annoyances, bores, irritations, coercions, tyrannies and narcotizations of marriage, that struggle of two beings always together who thought they suited; to tell you those things would repeat Boileau. Still, I'll forgive your absurd idea if you promise to marry ‘en grand seigneur’: entail your property; have two legitimate children; give your wife a house and household distinct from yours; meet her only in society, never return from a journey without sending her a courier to announce it. Two hundred thousand francs a year will suffice, and your antecedents will enable you to marry some rich Englishwoman hungry for a title. That's an aristocratic life thoroughly French; it retains respect and friendship of a woman and distinguishes a man from the crowd. Thus the Comte de Manerville may advise his epoch, place himself above the world, and be a minister or ambassador. Ridicule can never touch him; he gains social advantages of marriage while keeping bachelor privileges.”

“But, my good friend, I am not de Marsay,” said Paul; “I am Paul de Manerville, worthy father and husband, deputy of the Centre, possibly peer of France—a destiny extremely commonplace; but I am modest and I resign myself.”

“Yes, but your wife,” said the pitiless de Marsay, “will she resign herself?”

“My wife, my dear fellow, will do as I wish.”

“Ah! my poor friend, is that where you are? Adieu, Paul. Henceforth I refuse to respect you. One word more, however, for I cannot agree coldly to your abdication. Look and see where the strength of our position lies. A bachelor with only six thousand francs a year left has at least his reputation for elegance and the memory of success. Even that fantastic shadow has enormous value. Life still offers many chances to the unmarried man. Yes, he can aim at anything. But marriage, Paul, is the social ‘Thus far shalt thou go and no farther.’ Once married you can never be anything but what you then are—unless your wife deigns to care for you.”

“But,” said Paul, “you crush me with exceptional theories. I am tired of living for others; of having horses merely to exhibit them; of doing everything for what may be said; of wasting my substance to keep fools from crying, ‘Dear, dear! Paul is still driving the same carriage. What has he done with his fortune?’ Believe me, Henri, I admire your power, but I don't envy it. You judge life, act as a statesman, place yourself above ordinary laws, prejudices, and conventions; you can grasp the profits of a situation in which I should find nothing but ill luck. Your cool deductions are, to the masses, shockingly immoral. I belong to the masses. I must play by the rules of the society I live in. While putting yourself above things on peaks of ice, you still have feelings; but as for me I should freeze to death. The life of the great majority, to which I belong, is made up of emotions of which I now have need. Often a man coquets with a dozen women and obtains none. Whatever his strength and knowledge, he is crushed in convulsions. For my part, I like the peaceful chances and changes of life; I want a woman always at my side.”

“A trifle indecorous, your marriage!” exclaimed de Marsay.

Paul was not put out: “Laugh if you like; I shall feel happy when my valet says in the morning, ‘Madame is awaiting monsieur for breakfast’; happier still at night, when I return to find a heart—”

“Altogether indecorous,” said de Marsay. “You are not yet moral enough to marry.”

“—a heart in which to confide my interests and secrets. I wish a close union with a woman so our affection shall not depend on a yes or no, nor be open to love’s disillusions. I have the courage to become, as you say, a worthy husband and father. I feel fitted for family joys; I wish to put myself under society’s conditions; I desire a wife and children.”

“You remind me of a hive of honey-bees! But go your way, you’ll be a dupe all your life. Ha, ha! you wish to marry to have a wife! In other words, you wish to solve to your own profit the most difficult problem invented by bourgeois morals created by the Revolution; and, more, you mean to begin by a life of retirement. Do you think your wife won’t crave the life you say you despise? If you won’t accept the noble conjugality I’ve outlined, listen at least to my final advice. Remain a bachelor thirteen years; amuse yourself; at forty, on your first gout attack, marry a widow of thirty-six; then you may be happy. If you take a young girl now, you’ll die a madman.”

“Ah! tell me why!” cried Paul, somewhat piqued.

“My dear fellow,” replied de Marsay, “Boileau’s satire on women is commonplace. Why shouldn’t women have defects? The problem of marriage is not where Boileau puts it. Do you suppose marriage equals love and being a man suffices to make a wife love you? Have you gathered nothing in your boudoir experience but pleasant memories? Everything in our bachelor life leads to fatal errors in married life unless one is a profound observer of the human heart. In youth a man by custom is lucky; he triumphs over women ready to be triumphed over. Obstacles created by laws, sentiments and natural defenses engender mutual sensations that deceive the superficial about future married relations, where obstacles no longer exist, where the wife submits to love instead of permitting it and frequently repulses pleasure. The whole aspect of life changes. The bachelor, free and without care, need never fear repulsion; in marriage, repulsion is almost certain and irreparable. A lover can sometimes make a woman reverse an unfavorable decision, but such a change is the husband's Waterloo. Like Napoleon, the husband is condemned to victories which, despite number, cannot undo the first defeat. The woman, flattered by perseverance and delighted with ardor as a lover, calls the same things brutality in a husband. You, who talk of marrying, have you meditated on the Civil Code? I never opened that garret of gossip, the Law-school, but I see the Code’s application on society’s vitals. The Code makes woman a ward; it treats her like a minor. How must we govern children? By fear. In that word, Paul, is the curb of the beast. Now feel your pulse! Have you the strength to play the tyrant—you, so gentle, so confiding; you, at whom I have laughed, whom I love enough to reveal my science? This is science—Anthropology. Ah! if I had not solved life’s mystery by pleasure, if I did not despise those who think instead of act, I’d write a book on modern marriages and stick a lantern on that heap of sharp stones where votaries of the social ‘multiplicamini’ lie. But does humanity require my time? Besides, isn’t ink better used to snare hearts with love-letters? Well, shall you bring the Comtesse de Manerville here and let us see her?”

“Perhaps,” said Paul.

“We shall still be friends,” said de Marsay.

“If—” replied Paul.

“Don’t be uneasy; we will treat you politely, as Maison-Rouge treated the English at Fontenoy.”

Though the foregoing conversation affected the Comte de Manerville, he carried out his intentions and returned to Bordeaux in the winter of 1821.

The expense of restoring and furnishing his family mansion sustained the reputation for elegance that preceded him. Introduced by former connections to Bordeaux’s royalist society, to which his opinion, name, and fortune equally bound him, he soon obtained a fashionable pre‑eminence. His knowledge of life, manners, and Parisian accomplishments charmed the Faubourg Saint‑Germain of Bordeaux. An old marquise revived a courtly phrase, calling him “the pink of fashion.” The liberal clique used the epithet satirically, while the royalists employed it sincerely.

Paul de Manerville fulfilled his flowery title. When the public noticed him he, as many mediocre actors do, seemed to become superior. At ease, he displayed the fine qualities that accompanied his defects. His wit was mild; his manners not supercilious; his intercourse with women expressed the temperate respect they liked; his foppery merely showed a carefulness of appearance that made him agreeable. He respected rank, granted youth a certain freedom, and, though adept with sword and pistol, was noted for a feminine gentleness appreciated by others. His medium height and plumpness did not prevent him from playing the part of a Bordelais Brummell. A fair complexion, handsome hands and feet, blue eyes fringed by long lashes, black hair, graceful motions, and a chest voice that vibrated in the listener’s heart harmonized with his sobriquet. Paul was a delicate flower needing careful culture—one of those men made more to receive than to give happiness, with something of the woman in his nature—one to whom conjugal love should come as a providence.

Such a character creates private difficulties but is full of public attraction. Consequently Paul succeeded greatly in the narrow provincial circle, where his half‑tinted mind was better appreciated than it would have been in Paris.

Restoring his town house and the château de Lanstrac—into which he introduced English country‑house comfort—consumed the capital the notary had saved over six years. Reduced now to his strict income of about forty thousand a year, he believed himself prudent in regulating his household accordingly.

After flaunting his equipages, entertaining the town’s most distinguished young men, and giving hunting parties at Lanstrac, Paul saw that provincial life would not do without marriage. Too young for stinginess or for speculative improvements provincials undertake to establish children, he needed the variety that becomes the life of a Parisian. A name to preserve, property to transmit, a household to create social relations, and a weariness of irregular connections were not the chief reasons for his matrimonial desire. From his first return he had been secretly in love with Bordeaux’s queen, the great beauty Mademoiselle Evangelista.

Around the century’s beginning a rich Spaniard named Evangelista had settled in Bordeaux. His letters of recommendation and large fortune gave him entrance to noble salons. His wife, a Creole from the Casa‑Reale, lived as a great lady, ignorant of money’s value, indulging whims supplied by an adoring husband. From 1800 to 1812 Monsieur and Madame Evangelista were objects of interest in Bordeaux.

The Spaniard died in 1813, leaving Madame Evangelista a thirty‑two‑year‑old widow with an immense fortune and a prettiest little girl of eleven who became a most accomplished young woman. Clever as the mother was, the Restoration altered her position: royalist ranks cleared and old families left Bordeaux. Although lacking her husband’s direction, Madame Evangelista resolved not to change her mode of living. By squandering large sums on brilliant fêtes and a quasi‑regal life she kept up public belief in her wealth; the steady diminution of her capital remained unknown.

Natalie, now nineteen, was apparently the richest match in Bordeaux, but no marriage proposals had reached her mother. Accustomed to being gratified, she wore cashmeres and jewels and lived in a luxury that alarmed suitors in a region where sons and parents were calculating. The fatal remark “None but a prince can afford to marry Mademoiselle Evangelista” circulated in salons. Mothers jealous of Natalie’s beauty and tyrannical elegance spread treacherous warnings: her mother gave her a thousand francs a month for her toilet, she had horses and a maid, wore Mechlin laces; her morning pelerines cost six francs to “get up.” Such speeches, couched as praise, deterred many suitors.

Pampered as she was, Natalie knew nothing of the value of money or how households are maintained. She bounded through life unbridled and unshod, like a courser on his steppe.

Six months after Paul’s arrival the Pink of Fashion and the Queen of Balls met in the highest society of Bordeaux. They looked at each other with apparent coldness, each thinking the other charming. Madame Evangelista read Paul’s looks and muttered, “He will be my son‑in‑law.” Paul thought, “She will be my wife.”

The Evangelistas’ reputed wealth lingered in Paul’s mind as a memory of childhood, so their pecuniary condition was known to him from the start. When some ventured flattering remarks about Natalie that ended with cruelly practical warnings about the family’s extravagance, Paul replied with disdain that shut down provincial pettiness. He brought to Bordeaux a certain Britannic reserve and Byronic pessimism, together with English plate, boot‑polish, ponies, yellow gloves, cigars, and a habit of galloping. Thus he escaped discouragements that had previously deterred other suitors. Madame Evangelista asked him to formal dinners; he accepted, pleased to breathe the grandeur and luxury he loved.

When he rode by in his tilbury or on his English horse, young men said, “There’s a lucky man. He is rich and handsome, and is to marry, so they say, Mademoiselle Evangelista.” Society, which never does good without doing evil, credited Natalie with a dowry of a million and bestowed her, almost before consent, upon Paul. Their fortunes were said to match: Paul’s habits of luxury and his newly arranged house made him seem uniquely fitted to meet the pecuniary difficulties of marrying such a daughter of a Creole great lady. In short, the marriage seemed made. Royalist friends flattered him: “Everyone gives you Mademoiselle Evangelista. You could not find, even in Paris, a more delightful girl. Her mother is intelligent and adroit; she will be a great help in public life. She has sacrificed everything to her daughter. You must soon bring the matter to a close.”

“That is all very well,” replied Paul, anxious to keep freedom of action, “but I must be sure that the conclusion shall be a happy one.”

He now went to the Evangelistas often, partly to occupy idle hours that were harder for him than for most men. Madame Evangelista, at forty, had the beauty of a glorious summer sunset. Her spotless reputation was much discussed; her temperament combined the force and seductiveness of Spanish and Creole women. She had black eyes and hair, the swaying form, and a complexion like muslin over crimson—white suffused with color. Full and rounded, she possessed the nonchalance of strength united with vivacity, and sometimes the carriage of a queen. Men were taken by her conversation; she had the genius for schemes necessity bestows, advancing from concession to concession and retreating quickly when concessions were demanded in return. Though ignorant of facts, she had known many courts and illustrious families, which gave her a superficial but impressive education. She received society with a natural grace and dignity, and her reputed virtue lent authority to her actions and character.

Mother and daughter had a true friendship that went beyond maternal and filial bonds; they suited each other, and perpetual contact had produced no jar. Madame Evangelista’s perpetual widowhood was not explained solely by maternal devotion: she had once loved a man who recovered titles and property under the Restoration, and who courted her to marry in 1814 only to sever the connection by 1816. The widow, who believed in the power of hatred—“Odiate e aspettate”—nursed this resentment. Events seemed to prove her “jettatura” when this man began to ruin himself; she passed him on foot in the Champs‑Élysées in her brilliant equipage, and crushed him with a triumphant look. This misadventure and later pride kept her from remarrying. She had thus reached an age when women often take the mother’s part, sacrificing themselves to their children and placing their interests in another household—the last refuge of human affections.

Madame Evangelista divined Paul’s nature and concealed her own. He was precisely the man she wanted for a son‑in‑law, her instrument for achieving future power. Through his mother he belonged to the Maulincour family; his connection to the Baronne de Maulincour and her grandson Auguste’s army position made Paul an excellent introducer for the Evangelistas into Parisian society. The widow, who disliked residing in Bordeaux, longed to shine in the Restoration’s Paris, where political fortune lay. She looked to Paul as a means to this end; through him she could enjoy power indirectly. Many men serve as screens for secret feminine ambitions; Madame Evangelista had more than one interest in holding her daughter’s husband.

Paul was captivated by Madame Evangelista, who charmed him by seeming to seek no influence over him while subtly magnifying herself and her daughter in his eyes. He valued himself more for their appreciation; he fancied himself clever when his remarks pleased Natalie and flattered when the mother’s praise appeared involuntary. So kind and welcoming were they that he spent most of his time at the Hôtel Evangelista.

A year after his return, though Paul had not declared himself, his attentions made the world consider him a suitor. Neither mother nor daughter seemed intent on marriage. Natalie preserved toward Paul the reserve of a great lady who can charm without allowing intimacy—a reserve that pleased Paul, a timid man who recoiled from sudden proposals and surrendered to mild advances. He therefore committed himself inwardly all the more. The mother’s remark that superior women sometimes put ambition above earlier emotions seduced him: “That woman is fitted,” he thought, “to advance me in diplomacy before I am even made a deputy.”

A man who does not examine both things and ideas under different aspects before acting is weak and in danger of failure. At this moment Paul was an optimist; he saw everything to advantage and did not think that an ambitious mother‑in‑law might become a tyrant. He pictured himself a married man each evening and embraced the idea of wedlock cheerfully—his bachelor life, which offered nothing new, now only appeared annoying. “Marriage,” he said, “is disagreeable for people without means, but half its troubles disappear before wealth.” Every day some favorable consideration swelled his sense of advantage.

“No matter what position I attain, Natalie will always be on the level of her part,” he told himself. “How many Empire men I’ve seen who suffered horribly through their wives! It is a great condition of happiness not to feel one’s pride or vanity wounded by the companion we have chosen. A man can never be really unhappy with a well‑bred wife; she will never make him ridiculous.” He taxed his memory among Faubourg Saint‑Germain women to see Natalie as their equal; all comparisons favored her, resting on his imagination, which followed his desires. Paris would have shown other types to balance his mind; in Bordeaux Natalie had no rivals and appeared when Paul was under an age‑old tyrant: the domination of an idea.

Thus propinquity, self‑love, and a real passion whose only outlet was marriage led Paul into an irrational love that he prudently kept to himself. He tried to study Natalie, mindful of de Marsay’s warnings. But luxury breeds a certain indifference; Paul never imagined the gulf of ruin beneath the ladies’ habits. Moreover, there are no rules to predict conjugal harmony; young people display favorable weather only, oblivious to the common gray days of life. Each presents a favorable mask; later they blame marriage for evils inherent in life.

To detect in Natalie any sign of future imperfections would have required not only physiognomy but a kind of personal science few possess. Her face, like most young girls’, was impenetrable. The sculptural calm of virgin faces—Justice, Innocence—marks purity; such placid expression had not been clouded by passion or betrayal. If that expression were assumed, then no young woman remained behind it.

Natalie, closely held to her mother’s heart, received an education largely religious with a few lessons in social role from Madame Evangelista. Thus her calm expression was natural. Yet any clever analyst might find in Natalie indications of future difficulties. Her beauty arose from extreme regularity of feature and proportions—perfection that often foreshadows coldness of organization. Superior nature usually shows slight imperfections that become luminous attractions; perfect harmony often indicates a mixed, colder nature.

Natalie’s round waist signaled strength and a will that becomes obstinacy where the mind is not keen. Her statue‑like hands suggested an inclination for illogical domination, willing for will’s sake. Her meeting eyebrows were said to indicate jealousy; jealousy in superior minds becomes emulation, but in small minds turns to hatred. The “hate and wait” of her mother lived in her. Her eyes were blackish brown with orange streaks, her hair a ruddy auburn often found among those of jet black stock like her parents. The contrast of her delicate complexion with hair and eyes gave inexpressible charm, but it was mainly external; where outlines lack a certain soft roundness, beauty is not of the soul. Such roses of youth will drop their leaves, revealing hardness and dryness where nobility was once admired.

Though her face had some august outlines, her slightly full chin suggested passions that might grow violent; her astringent mouth expressed haughty pride consistent with hand, chin, brows, and figure. Her pure voice, though seductive, had metallic tones that hinted at the Duke of Alba’s temperament from whom the Casa‑Reales descended. These marks hinted at violent passions without tenderness, sudden devotions, irreconcilable dislikes, a mind not deeply intelligent, and a desire to rule natural to those who feel themselves inferior to their pretensions.

These defects, buried in Natalie like ore in a mine, would only surface under life’s shocks. Meantime the grace, freshness, distinction, sacred ignorance, and youthful sweetness gave a glamour that misled unthinking minds. Her mother taught her the art of agreeable talk, replying to arguments with clever jests and concealing the subsoil of her mind with volubility. Natalie had the charm of those who never knew suffering: frank, gay, natural, expecting pleasure from marriage and replying to objections with jest.

How could Paul, dazzled by such beauty, foresee the woman she might be at thirty? Besides, happiness in such a marriage was not impossible. Embryo defects could be stifled by proper training, especially in a young girl who loved him. But to render ductile an intractable woman the iron wrist de Marsay recommended was necessary. Fear inspired by love is an instrument to manage women; he who loves fears, and thus is nearer affection than hatred.

Had Paul the coolness, firmness, and judgment required for this struggle? Did Natalie love Paul? Like most young girls, she mistook instinct and the pleasure of his external appearance for love; she knew nothing of marriage or home demands. To her, Comte de Manerville—a rising diplomatist who knew European courts and was one of Paris’s most elegant young men—could hardly seem an ordinary man without moral force: timid though brave in some ways, energetic in adversity perhaps, but helpless against the vexations that hinder happiness. Would she have sufficient tact to distinguish his noble qualities among lesser defects? Would she not magnify the latter and forget the former, as inexperienced young wives often do? There comes a time when wives pardon defects in the husband who spares them annoyances; what conciliating power or wise experience would uphold this home? Paul, weak where even a strong man runs risks, might yield to tyranny instead of establishing his rule. Could he say “No”?

The subject here is not merely the transition from bachelor to married man—a picture that would itself offer rich drama—but the great comedy that precedes all conjugal pairing in France: the discussion before a notary about the marriage contract, played in families noble and bourgeois, where human passions are excited by small interests as by large ones. This scene, neglected by dramatists though full of wit, controlled Paul’s future life and was awaited by Madame Evangelista with terror. It is to this comedy—the notarial negotiation—that we now turn.

At the beginning of the winter of 1822, Paul de Manerville asked, through his great-aunt the Baronne de Maulincour, for the hand of Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista. The baroness lingered in Medoc until October to help him and then reported back.

“My child,” she said, “the affair is won. Madame Evangelista gives nothing of her own to her daughter; Mademoiselle Natalie’s dowry is her patrimony. Marry her. Men with a name and estate must marry to continue the family. Consult Maitre Mathias; the marriage contract is a sacred duty.”

Troubled by his aunt’s hint that Madame Evangelista was a “sly cat,” Paul retained Maitre Mathias to draw the contract. To avoid offending his future mother-in-law, he approached Madame Evangelista with hesitating courtesy. She answered that “notaries of both families usually agree,” divined his embarrassment, and though inwardly anxious kept calm. She owed Natalie one-third of Monsieur Evangelista’s fortune—about 1,156,000 francs—but lacked the means to pay it. If Paul learned the truth through his notary, he might withdraw and ruin her plan. She resolved to fight for her interests, convinced she could still secure Natalie’s happiness and find an escape if necessary.

Madame Evangelista summoned Maitre Solonet, a young, ambitious royalist dandy noted for gallantry and political zeal. He arrived flattered to be received. She explained her situation plainly.

“Mademoiselle Natalie’s dowry is her patrimony,” Solonet said. “How have you behaved toward Monsieur de Manerville? In matters like these deportment matters more than law. Is the Comte de Manerville in love with Mademoiselle Natalie?”

“He adores her,” she answered.

Solonet counseled display and charm. “Make her look her best tonight. The marriage-contract dress is half the battle,” he said. Madame Evangelista dressed Natalie herself; in white tulle with pink ribbons and hair à la Sévigné, Natalie was a picture of charm. Her mother probed her feeling for Paul.

“If you and I had to part forever would you still persist in the marriage?”

“I should give it up,” Natalie replied.

“You do not love him,” said the mother, kissing her brow.

“But I love him,” Natalie insisted.

Madame Evangelista warned that if Paul hesitated at the sight of them in their glory, she would break off the match and seek another husband. Natalie, loyal and curious, promised that if property difficulties separated them she would free Paul from blame.

Paul arrived with his habitual bouquet. He and Natalie spoke fondly of the future—Lanstrac, Paris, children—while awaiting the notaries. But the romance was soon disturbed by business. For Paul the alliance seemed simple; the legal and financial details were a minefield. The two notaries embodied different eras and temperaments.

Maitre Mathias, Paul’s notary, was an elderly professional with forty years’ experience: slight, odd in appearance, but respected for uprightness, painstaking care, and faithful service. Solonet, by contrast, was young, fashionable, mixed politics with practice, enjoyed social life, and sought profit and advancement. When they met in Madame Evangelista’s salon they presented “former times and the present day.”

Solonet, eager to secure his client’s ends, had pre-drawn a contract favoring the widow. Mathias insisted on details and clarity. He listed Paul’s property—Lanstrac (23,000 francs rental), farms and vineyards, houses in Bordeaux and Paris, and furniture—totaling substantial landed wealth. Solonet countered that the Evangelista inheritance had been invested in Five-per-cent funds and was effectively about 800,000 francs, that the Paris house added value, and that the mother could convey titles while reserving a life interest.

Mathias challenged vague claims and demanded inventories. Solonet replied that the family, being Spanish and Creole, had not followed French formalities at the father’s death and had reinvested English securities in Paris, making exact accounting difficult. When Mathias pressed the difference between land and marketable funds, the conversation turned heated. Solonet’s strategy was to push a settlement by which Madame Evangelista would preserve apparent respectability while releasing Natalie’s patrimony; Mathias sought to protect Paul’s estate and his future children.

Natalie, playing innocently with a fan, watched and then, moved by loyalty, told Paul in private that if property difficulties separated them she alone would bear the blame. Paul, not yet told of Mathias’s alarm, pressed her hand and returned to the group. Mathias, alarmed, warned Paul in a private aside that there was little ready dowry; he advised postponement to allow reflection.

Solonet quickly offered a way out: sell the house, reckon the Five-per-cents as nearly at par, and convey titles into Natalie’s name while reserving a life interest for Madame Evangelista. This would allow immediate recognition of a large patrimony and close the guardianship account. Mathias saw a trap: the scheme would acknowledge a receipt for about 1,156,000 francs, binding Paul to account to his children for that sum later while in fact the family had less secure capital. He protested that the arrangement would impoverish Paul and his heirs and leave him liable if misfortunes occurred.

Madame Evangelista, provoked, threatened to break off the marriage and leave Bordeaux; she reminded them that in Spain things were done differently and appealed to the sentimental value of her name and upbringing as a kind of dowry. The argument grew tense: Paul, torn between love and prudence, nearly yielded to emotion. Mathias, seeing Paul’s distress and Madame Evangelista’s ambition—she hoped for Paul to become a peer of France—suddenly conceived a legal remedy that could reconcile interests.

Mathias proposed that, to secure Paul’s future public prospects and protect his estate, the parties entail Lanstrac and the Paris house and include eight hundred thousand francs of Natalie’s patrimony in that entail by purchasing adjoining domains and having them included in the royal ordinance required for an entail. In short, apply the convertible portion of Natalie’s fortune to create inalienable capital that would strengthen the family’s landed estate, thus satisfying Madame Evangelista’s desire to release Natalie’s patrimony and calming Paul’s and Mathias’s fear of immediate impoverishment. This course would allow Mathias to accept the guardianship account’s deficiency.

Solonet was surprised but recognized that the entail would placate distrust: an entail is inalienable and would reassure Paul and his advisers that the capital could not be wasted. Madame Evangelista, ignorant of legal technicalities, accepted Mathias’s plan because it seemed to secure Natalie’s patrimony and her own safety. Both she and Paul agreed that if the entail were created, the marriage should proceed.

Mathias emphasized that the formalities required a royal ordinance and immediate action, but that consent to proceed would suffice for the parties to go on. Natalie, a minor, was not party to the formal bargaining beyond assent.

It was then arranged that Mathias would draw up the contract, Solonet the guardianship account and release, and both documents should be signed days before the marriage. The notaries left together, each satisfied professionally: Solonet for getting his client married under the appearance of security, Mathias for having devised a legal mechanism to protect the Manerville estate.

Left alone, Paul and Madame Evangelista reconciled their earlier sharp words. Paul was ecstatic at the prospect that, with the entail and a combined household, he and Natalie could have an income near one hundred thousand francs and he might obtain a peerage. Madame Evangelista, relieved, expressed gratitude and tenderness; she proposed to give Natalie her diamonds and assured Paul she would sell her house and find means to secure herself without injuring her daughter’s patrimony.

Natalie reacted with emotional generosity, insisting her father’s diamonds remain but accepting that her mother’s sacrifice might be required. Madame Evangelista planned to have the jewels appraised and to sell plate and other items. She spoke of moving winters to Paris, of Lanstrac as their home, and of Solonet’s aid in settling debts. She fell asleep convinced she had achieved her ends: Natalie married to a man she could influence, a household assured, and an ambitious plan for Paul’s political advancement.

Thus the first day of negotiation ended with apparent satisfaction on all sides: Paul convinced, Madame Evangelista appeased, Natalie content, Mathias relieved that a protective entail might save Manerville, and Solonet pleased at having guided the match. Yet beneath this harmony lay seeds of distrust and designs—Madame Evangelista believed herself triumphant, Mathias guarded his client’s legacy, Solonet enjoyed the victory, and Paul saw his future bright with love and ambition.

################################################
The next day Elie Magus, in Bordeaux, obeyed Madame Evangelista’s summons. Rumors that Comte Paul would marry Mademoiselle Natalie suggested she wanted jewels for a bride; instead she had Magus value the mother-in-law’s property, anticipating the diamonds might be factored into the marriage contract. She instructed him to appraise them as if for a private buyer, even though they would not be sold. Magus explained how experts distinguish Asiatic from Brazilian diamonds—Golconda and Visapur stones having superior whiteness and brilliance, Brazilian stones a yellow tinge—and valued Madame Evangelista’s necklace and earrings, composed wholly of Asiatic stones, at 250,000 francs; he called the “Discreto” one of the finest private diamonds, worth 100,000 francs. If she wished to sell immediately to a dealer, however, he could only offer 75,000 for the brilliant and 160,000 for the necklace and earrings. He described dealers’ margins: rarer gems take longer to sell and tie up capital, so purchase prices are lower than selling prices, and owning them yields no interest. He illustrated with witty calculations about the cost of wearing diamonds. When Magus asked if she would sell the rest, he valued her Brazilian diamonds, pearls, and rubies at about 100,000 to a dealer, though a dealer might resell them for 150,000.

Madame Evangelista declared she would keep the jewels; Magus warned that investing the income from capital could replace similar diamonds in five years while preserving the principal. The conference leaked. Servants overheard raised voices, gossip exaggerated practical difficulties, and society magnified the story: had Madame Evangelista sold her house? Was the marriage off? Notaries denied a collapse, blaming only delay in constituting the entail, but society persisted. Young women and thwarted provincial mothers, resentful after two decades of Evangelista magnificence, embellished the tale. Officials in the prefecture hinted that the notaries’ language implied rupture; people speculated about quibbles over the entail, indignant that a wealthy widow might lack funds to pay her daughter’s patrimony. Bets were laid; yet neither Paul nor Madame Evangelista was told.

Paul had business at Lanstrac and gave a hunting-party as a kind of farewell to bachelor life; society treated this as proof of trouble. Madame de Gyas, hoping to marry her daughter, affected concern and approached the Evangelistas with a long face. Natalie and her mother laughed at the recital of gossip but saw the motives: provincial mothers envious of Evangelista fortune invented plots. The Marquise launched into a spirited tirade about desperate mothers likened to predators and scorned public judgment of private marriage clauses. Madame Evangelista, preparing to leave the city, could safely mock both friends and enemies and enjoyed caustic remarks.

When a footman announced Paul—who had ridden twelve miles to spend an hour with Natalie—he dismissed the gossip with laughter. “These worthy people have found out, perhaps, that there will be no wedding festivities,” he said, and proposed pacifying them with a ball on the day they signed the contract: a public show to refute rumors. Madame Evangelista accepted and invited all Bordeaux to witness the contract signing; she intended the last fete to silence gossip.

Preparations for the camellia fete consumed a month. Camellias banked staircases and supper-rooms; partitions were removed to enlarge rooms; estates near Lanstrac were purchased, banns published, and doubts dissipated. For a while the earlier stormy conference faded—after all, as Madame Evangelista said, it had seemed only the notaries’ affair.

Yet on the morning of the signing a memory returned to Madame Evangelista: Mathias’s earlier words when he had yielded to Solonet’s conditions—“Questa coda non e di questo gatto”—had suggested someone would pay the cost. If Mathias had foreseen compensation, it could not be Paul. Could her daughter’s fortune be made to meet the price? She resolved to seek explanations. This day would profoundly affect Paul de Manerville’s conjugal life.

Despite winter, the courtyard was tented, shrubs and banked camellias decorated the house, and the ballroom and supper-room were fitted for the fairyland Bordeaux expected. About eight, as the final contract was discussed, a dense crowd gathered to see the ladies in full dress. The two notaries had dined with the bridal pair and their mother; Mathias’s head-clerk was present to receive signatures and guard against surreptitious readings.

No bridal toilette equalled Natalie’s; she was superb. Madame Evangelista, in cherry velvet, wore her pearl necklace clasped with the “Discreto,” a visible contradiction to calumnies of ruin. Paul and Natalie sat together by the fire, inattentive to the reading, whispering and touching; Natalie toyed with a peacock-feather fan Paul had given her. Seated by the notaries, Madame Evangelista listened as Solonet read the guardianship account: Natalie’s share of Monsieur Evangelista’s estate—an estate of over three million francs—amounted to 1,156,000 francs. “Come, listen, listen, my children; this is your marriage contract,” she said.

The clerical routine proceeded: each party’s property was stated; general deeds of gift in case of death without issue; deed of gift of a life interest and a quarter of capital allowed by the Code; constitution of a common fund; settlement of the diamonds on the wife; the library and horses on the husband; and then constitution of the entail. When all but signatures remained, Madame Evangelista asked what an entail would ultimately mean. Solonet explained generally, but Mathias bluntly defined the entail’s practical effect: if the wife died first leaving several children including a son, Monsieur de Manerville would owe those children only 360,000 francs, from which he would deduct his fourth in life-interest and his fourth in capital, reducing his debt to about 160,000 francs, excluding savings and profits from the common fund. If the husband died first leaving a male heir, Madame de Manerville’s right would be 360,000 francs plus her deeds of gift of property not in the entail, the diamonds settled on her, and profits and savings from the common fund.

The consequence struck Madame Evangelista: “My daughter is ruined,” she whispered. Both notaries overheard. Mathias, gently ironic, replied that constituting an indestructible fortune for her family could hardly be ruin. The younger notary, seeing her face, whispered bluntly that they had tried to trick them out of 300,000 francs but actually the children had secured 800,000—arguing it was a loss of 400,000 from their interests for the children’s benefit. He advised her to break the marriage immediately or carry it through.

Silence followed. Natalie, unaware she had forfeited much of her fortune, and Paul, ignorant the Manerville house had gained, continued to converse lightly. Solonet and Madame Evangelista exchanged glances—one masking indifference, the other restraining bitter feeling. Madame Evangelista’s remorse at having compromised her honor for Natalie’s interest turned to fury when she realized she would be the loser and that Mathias must have foreseen this and enlightened Paul. The thought that Mathias might be lauded for his sagacity and turn Paul against her was intolerable. She faced a dilemma: three months earlier she might have broken the match; now banns were published, friends had arrived, and postponing would bring public scandal. She felt trapped and knew she must yield.

Her anger surfaced; her chin trembled; Solonet read the birth of a mortal hatred. She turned to Mathias, accusing him of incapacity or worse, of seeing the entail’s effect and not warning her sooner. The younger notary drew her aside, thinking of his fees but speaking coolly: she had overstepped legal craft for her own sake; if she did not want Comte Paul, she need not accept him—the contract was not yet signed; she could postpone it and blame some error in Paris. He suggested she might find another count if title were the chief aim. She protested she could not chance her honor. He insisted that Mademoiselle Natalie’s happiness mattered most and argued that she, as wife, could dominate the home and lead Paul by the nose; if she played her cards right, the count’s fate was within her power. This mollified Madame Evangelista; she declared she would be grateful.

Mathias and Solonet then amended the contract to protect the wife’s interests further. The young notary proposed—and Mathias concurred—clauses to ensure that if Manerville died without children or left only daughters, the entailed property should pass under the general deed of gift between husband and wife or that the entail be declared void. Solonet added this clause in the margin. Madame Evangelista listened, dropped her eyes while Mathias read the added sentence aloud.

“We will now sign,” she said, her voice betraying violent emotion. She thought she would not allow her daughter to be ruined; she intended that Natalie should have the name, title, and fortune. If Natalie later loved another, Paul would be driven out of France. Her mind resolved on measures to keep her daughter rich and free. Mathias took Paul aside and explained the saving stipulation—that he held the documents and that the contract contained a rescript for the diamonds; Paul must ask for them and could use them toward payments. Mathias spoke practically: the first payment of 200,000 francs was due after formalities; use the diamonds; the house could be sold to pay the rest; plant vineyards to raise income. Paul pressed Mathias’s hand affectionately; Madame Evangelista noticed and suspected collusion. Rage and hatred overwhelmed her.

After verifying signatures and initials, Mathias looked at the mother-in-law. Seeing Paul say nothing of the diamonds, Mathias remarked there could be no doubt about their transfer now they were family. Solonet urged it was more regular for Madame Evangelista to make them over immediately since Monsieur de Manerville had become responsible for guardianship funds and one never knew who might live or die. Paul protested that it would be insulting; Madame Evangelista, angered at what she took as an insult masked in Mathias’s words, cried she would tear the contract up if he did not take the diamonds.

She left in fury, nearly mad with impotence. Natalie whispered to Paul to take them: her mother was angry and she would know why that night; they must pacify her. Calmed, Madame Evangelista kept the necklace and earrings she wore and brought the other jewels, which Magus had valued at 150,000 francs. Mathias and Solonet examined them and found they would meet the first payments. Solonet assured Paul he would lose nothing on the spot; Mathias said the jewels would meet the first payment on the estate purchase and the contract costs.

Hatred fed on small things; Madame Evangelista took Paul’s reluctance to accept the jewels as hypocrisy. She interpreted his embarrassment—he did not want them—as hypocrisy and flung a look that seemed to say “Take your property.” Paul, eager to end the dispute, told Natalie to lock the jewels away as her own gift. Natalie stowed them in a console drawer. At that moment carriages and voices swelled; salons filled and the fete began.

During the ball, whispers and judgments circulated among guests: speculation about the marriage’s financial and social implications, about Madame Evangelista’s supposed penury, and about Mathias’s hand in safeguarding the family. Some predicted future domestic friction; others speculated that Mademoiselle Evangelista, as a merchant’s daughter, could not hold her place socially. Card-players took tables, the young danced, supper was served, and the ball lasted till morning.

After Paul left, Madame Evangelista and Natalie retired. Though overtired, they exchanged a few words. Madame Evangelista confessed she had acted out of tender devotion and unscrupulous business ignorance in order to secure Natalie’s happiness; she had trampled her pride. Natalie, half asleep, spoke warmly of Paul’s innocence; she had no idea her mother’s fears or the implications of the contract. Madame Evangelista, in the dark, felt the first stirrings of avarice induced by passion and hate: a sudden resolve to secure her daughter’s entire fortune by reclaiming her husband’s property for Natalie. She told herself honor required it; devotion to Natalie made her shrewd and calculating. She planned to invest capital in the Funds and manage affairs carefully. Passion and hatred had transformed the once-prodigal woman into a miser and a plotter. She fell asleep muttering, “To-morrow!” Her mind, in sleep, marshalled ideas and arranged a plan to rule Paul’s life—a plan she began to put into action the next day.

Though the excitement of the fête had driven Paul’s anxieties away for the moment, they returned to torment him when he lay alone.

“It seems to me,” he thought, “that without Mathias my mother-in-law would have tricked me. And yet, is that believable? What interest could lead her to deceive me? Are we not to join fortunes and live together? Well, well, why should I worry? In two days Natalie will be my wife; our money relations are plainly defined, nothing can come between us. Vogue la galere—Nevertheless, I’ll be upon my guard. Suppose Mathias was right? Well, if he was, I’m not obliged to marry my mother-in-law.”

Unseen by Paul, the second battle over the contract had already altered his future. Of the two persons he was marrying, one—the cleverest—had become his mortal enemy, already plotting to withdraw her interests from the common fund. Incapable of discerning how a Creole temperament differs from others, Paul did not suspect her craft. The Creole nature, Madame Evangelista’s, combined European intellect, tropical passion’s illogical force, and an Eastern apathy: graceful but dangerous. She brooded over desires until she hatched them; naïveté could mask perfidy. That mixture, garnished with Spanish stubbornness and French polish, defined her.

Sixteen years of married comfort had left her energies dormant until now, when she was losing her dearest object and needed fresh activity. Natalie would be gone in three days; Madame Evangelista had one last day to sway her daughter, who believed her blindly. A few words could determine two lives. Social codes prevent violence but not crimes committed by words; the great world often uses the venom of ideas rather than the knife.

The next morning Madame Evangelista sat on Natalie’s bed; they caressed and remembered an undisturbed life.

“Poor little darling!” the mother sobbed. “How can I help being sorrowful when I think that after I have fulfilled your every wish you will, tomorrow night, belong to a man you must obey?”

“Oh, my dear mother, as for obeying!” Natalie made a graceful little gesture. “You are joking. My father always gratified your caprices. And I am loved, too.”

“Yes, Paul has a certain love for you. But conjugal love evaporates unless a wife does the right thing at the start. You need the best advice.”

“But you will be with us.”

“Possibly. Last night I reflected on the dangers of our being together. If my presence does you harm, if the little acts by which you should slowly establish your authority are attributed to me, your home will become a hell. At the first frown on your husband’s brow I, proud as I am, should instantly leave his house. If I had to leave, better never to enter at all. I would never forgive your husband for causing trouble between us. Whereas, when you have once become mistress, when your husband is to you what your father was to me, that danger is gone. Though it hurts you now, your happiness demands that you become absolute sovereign of your home.”

“Then why, mamma, did you say just now I must obey him?”

“My dear, to rule you must seem to obey. If you oppose him impulsively you might destroy your future. Paul is weak; he might fall under another’s influence and you would feel it. Rather let him be governed by you than by others.”

“Yes, certainly,” said Natalie. “I shall think only of his happiness.”

“And it is my privilege, darling, to think only of yours. I will not leave you at such a crucial moment without a compass among the reefs through which you must steer.”

“But, dearest mother, are we not strong enough, you and I, to stay together beside him? Paul loves you, mamma.”

“Oh! He fears me more than he loves me. Observe him today when I tell him I will let you go to Paris without me, and you will see his inward joy, however he hides it.”

“Why should he feel so?”

“Why? I will tell him myself, before you.”

“But suppose I marry on condition that you do not leave me?”

“Our separation is necessary,” her mother insisted. “Several considerations have changed my future. I am now poor. You will lead a brilliant life in Paris; I could not live with you suitably without spending what remains to me. If I go to Lanstrac I can take care of your property there and restore my fortune by economy.”

“You, mamma, practise economy!” Natalie laughed. “Don’t be a grandmother yet. What! do you mean to leave me for such reasons? Paul may seem a trifle stupid to you, but he is not selfish or grasping.”

“Ah!” Madame Evangelista replied with a look that tightened Natalie's heart. “Those discussions about the contract made me distrustful. I have my doubts about him—But don’t be troubled, dear child,” she added, kissing her, “I will not leave you long. Whenever I can return without making difficulty between you, whenever Paul judges me rightly, we will have again our happy life—our evening confidences—”

“Oh! mother, how can you think of living without your Natalie?”

“Because, darling, I shall live for her. My mother’s heart will be satisfied in thinking I contribute to your happiness.”

“But, my dear, adorable mother, must I be alone with Paul, here, now, all at once? What will become of me? what must I do? what must I not do?”

“Poor child! do you think I would utterly abandon you? We will write three times a week like lovers. Nothing can happen to you that I shall not know; I can then save you from misfortune. Besides, I shall always spend a month or two each year with you in Paris.”

“Alone, already alone, and with him!” cried Natalie.

“But you wish to be his wife?”

“Yes,” she said. “But tell me how I should behave—you, who did what you pleased with my father. You know the way; I’ll obey you blindly.”

Madame Evangelista had longed for this. She kissed her daughter’s forehead and began the counsel she had prepared.

“Child, my counsels must suit circumstances. Men differ morally like the lion and the frog. I can only give general advice.”

“In the first place,” she said, “the chief cause of married women’s failure—of failing both to keep their husbands’ hearts and to rule them—is perpetual intercourse. In old times intimacy was less constant; since the Revolution, bourgeois habits invaded aristocratic homes; well-bred women stay at home and family life became excessive. Perpetual contact drains love. Therefore erect between yourself and Paul the barriers of society: go to balls and the opera, dine out, receive visits. Give him only a little of your time. When two beings live only on sentiment its resources are exhausted; indifference and disgust follow. Keep yourself ever new to him. The power is to be amused and to amuse; a man always loves the woman who gives him happiness. If you make your happiness depend entirely on him, you will lose mastery.”

“Dear mother, I am listening, but I don’t understand one word you say,” Natalie admitted.

“If you love Paul to the extent of doing all he asks, all is over. You will never be mistress of your home. Instinct will guide you later; at the moment Paul desires you more than he loves you. Use that first ardor to accustom him to obedience. When he yields, choose unreasonable concessions to test his submission—victories gained over unreasonable points teach him to yield in the future. Keep a reserve in speech and action; coldness can be modified at will, and words like ‘It is not becoming in your wife to do thus’ are a talisman. The life of a woman lies in the words ‘I will not.’ To reign, make him believe you understand him; a woman can make a man think he is loved, but to make him believe he is understood is harder and more potent. Arrange from the first that there shall be but one will—and that will must be yours.”

Natalie kissed her mother’s hands, grateful but still childlike.

“Don’t frighten me, mamma,” she said. “How can I remember all these precepts?”

“You will learn by experience,” her mother answered. “We all have our destinies: a woman may be born for the salon as a man is born to be a soldier or poet. Your vocation is to please. You were made for society, not to be a steward or an early mother. If you have children delay them a few years; governesses and tutors will educate them. You must represent luxury and pleasure. But let your superiority show mainly in things that flatter a man’s self-love; hide your true superiority.”

“But how must I begin?” Natalie asked naïvely.

“Instinct will guide you,” said Madame Evangelista. “At this moment Paul desires you; be so after marriage and you shall remain beloved. A woman ardently desired can ask all things. When your husband does a foolish thing for you, you can govern him. Marriage lasts a lifetime; a husband is not like other men. Never give yourself entirely away. Many wives create their own unhappiness by changing sides. Control events instead of bearing them; that advantage compensates for difficulty.”

Natalie understood in a flash the strategy’s bearings; yet, like a spoiled child, she returned to a personal plea.

“Dear mamma,” she said, “only a few days since you spoke of promoting Paul’s advancement; why do you now abandon us?”

“I did not then know the extent of my obligations nor my debts,” the mother answered, concealing her true motive. “Besides, a year or two hence I can resume that matter. Come, let us dress; Paul will be here soon. Be as sweet and caressing as you were the night we first discussed this fatal contract; today we must save the last fragments of our fortune, and I must win for you something to which I am superstitiously attached.”

“What is it?”

“The ‘Discreto.’”

Paul arrived about four o’clock. Though he tried to look gracious, Madame Evangelista saw the traces of the morning’s counsels on his face.

“Mathias has told him!” she thought, resolving to defeat the old notary’s warning. Approaching him with tears and a theatrical confession, she told how she disliked seeing diamonds that had threatened to bring a cloud between them; she declared she would prefer to sell her pearl necklace and ear-rings to meet debts and the first payments on the purchased estates.

“They are not mine,” Paul said. “I have given them to Natalie, and when you see them on her you will forget the pain they caused you.”

Madame Evangelista seized his hand. Then she proposed a bargain: exchange the jewels she had made over to Natalie for the historic Discreto diamond and ear-rings she prized—stones tied to Philip II and the Duke of Alba. Elie Magus valued her necklace and earrings at over a hundred thousand francs. “Will you exchange?” she asked, framing it as a gift to Natalie's vanity and public happiness rather than selfishness.

“But, my dear mother, consider yourself,” Paul began.

“I,” she replied, “I want such things no longer. Yes, Paul, I am going to be your bailiff at Lanstrac. It would be folly for me to go to Paris when I ought to be here to liquidate my property and settle affairs. I shall grow miserly for my grandchildren.”

“Dear mother,” said Paul, moved, “ought I to accept this exchange without paying you the difference?”

“Good heavens! are you not my dearest interests? Do you suppose I shall not find happiness thinking, ‘Natalie is dazzling at the Duchesse de Berry’s ball’ when she wears my diamond? Nothing saddens a woman more than repressed vanity.”

Paul, who distrusted neither his wife nor mother, thought of Mathias’s warning and then accepted. Natalie, confounded, could only look on.

At this moment Solonet arrived to report two Bordeaux speculators offering for the house and gardens—three acres that could be built over. He said they offered 250,000 francs, but he could make them pay 300,000.

“My husband paid 200,000 for the place,” Natalie replied. “Therefore I consent—reserve the furniture and mirrors.”

“You are beginning to understand business,” Solonet remarked; she lamented that she must.

Madame Evangelista accompanied him to the door and, in a low voice, confided she had personal property of 250,000 francs and hoped to get 200,000 for her share of the house, a capital she needed to invest. Solonet promised to find safe merchandise investments.

Returning, she explained to Paul more fully why she must leave for Lanstrac: her presence would hamper the young couple, create obligations that would make them resentful, and perhaps breed jealousy in a young husband. Old affection, querulous and exacting, often makes mothers intolerable. She preferred to make one more sacrifice for the couple’s happiness: she had given them her fortune and now resigned her last vanities. She would be bailiff, administer the estates, and later, if needful, support their Parisian projects. “Come, Paul, be frank; my proposal suits you, does it not?”

Paul would not own it, but he was delighted to regain his liberty. The suspicions Mathias had hinted at were dissipated by her talk, and she prolonged the pleasant tone. Natalie noticed Paul’s relief and wondered why he seemed glad to know her mother was leaving—another seed of distrust. Paul’s nature was confiding and blunt; he believed in goodness and had no foresight.

Natalie was sad and thoughtful; she did not know what to do without her mother. Paul, self-confident in love, smiled at her sadness, thinking Paris and marriage would soon erase it. Madame Evangelista watched with satisfaction: she had already secured two achievements—her daughter had the diamonds that had cost Paul two hundred thousand francs, and she had contrived to leave them to themselves with no guide but their love. Her revenge was thus preparing unbeknownst to her daughter, who, in time, would become its accomplice. The question whether Natalie loved Paul remained open; if he could make her love him, his future was safe.

The next day, at midnight, after a formal dinner with the four witnesses—whom Madame Evangelista had hosted—the bridal pair, accompanied by friends, heard mass by torchlight before a curious crowd. A midnight marriage suggested an omen; light, the symbol of life and pleasure, was absent. Natalie, parted from her mother, wept. Doubts assailed her as any heart facing a new life with its pitfalls. The air and demeanor of Madame Evangelista and the couple provoked comment among the assembly.

“Solonet tells me the bride and bridegroom leave for Paris tomorrow morning, all alone.”

“Madame Evangelista was to live with them, I thought.”

“Count Paul has got rid of her already.”

“What a mistake! To shut the door on the mother of his wife is to open it to a lover. Doesn’t he know what a mother is?”

“He has been very hard on Madame Evangelista; the poor woman has had to sell her house and diamonds, and is going to live at Lanstrac.”

“Natalie looks very sad.”

“Would you like to be forced to travel the day after your marriage?”

“It is very awkward.”

“I am glad I came. I am convinced of the necessity of wedding pomp; a scene like this is bare and sad. If I may say it, this marriage seems indecent.”

Madame Evangelista took Natalie in her carriage and accompanied her alone to Paul’s house.

“Well, mother, it is done!” Natalie said.

“Remember, my dear child, my last advice, and you will be a happy woman. Be his wife, not his mistress.”

When Natalie retired, the mother performed a little provincial scene—flinging herself, tears feigned, into the arms of her son-in-law. It was the only display of the sort she allowed herself, but effective: amid tears and speeches she obtained the concessions most husbands yield. The next day she put the married pair into their carriage, accompanied them to the ferry across the Gironde, and with one look and word enabled her daughter to see that even if Paul had won the contract, her revenge had begun. Natalie was already learning to reduce her husband to perfect obedience.

Five years later, on an autumn afternoon, Comte Paul de Manerville, cloaked and with a bowed, mysterious air, presented himself at the house of his old friend Maître Mathias in Bordeaux.

Mathias, a retired notary who had sold his practice, was out. His housekeeper Josette showed the count to the late Madame Mathias’s room. Paul, spent by a rapid journey, slept until evening. When Mathias returned he climbed the stairs and watched the count sleep like a parent watching a child; Josette hovered behind him.

“It is a year to-day since I received my dear wife’s last sigh; I little thought then I should see the count half dead,” Mathias murmured.

“Poor man! he moans in his sleep,” Josette said.

Mathias, grieved in a businesslike way, muttered at the sight of Paul’s ruin. “At any rate I have saved the title to Lanstrac for him, and Ausac, Saint-Froult, and his house, though the usufruct is gone.” He counted the properties. “Five years! since his aunt, Madame de Maulincour, asked for the hand of that little crocodile of a woman who has ruined him—as I expected.”

Leaning on his cane, the gouty old man paced the garden until Paul awoke. They supped at nine. Mathias noted Paul’s calm and the premature aging in his face; Paul nevertheless seemed physically well. Paul clasped Mathias’s hand and, with forced cheer, confessed he had troubles.

“I am going to India,” Paul announced. “I sail for Calcutta in two days on the Belle‑Amélie.”

Mathias protested at such haste and at the public notices he had seen announcing seizure of Paul’s properties—farms at Grassol and Guadet, the vineyard of Belle‑Rose, and the Bordeaux house. He had bought Lanstrac for Paul’s mother when he was a young third clerk and felt ashamed to see his name on those yellow posters.

Paul explained the separation as to property between him and his wife—“separated in body” he added bitterly—and said he had left his wife unaware of his departure. Mathias was aghast. “To sail to India! You are seventy-three and have the gout, Mathias,” Paul said with a strained attempt at levity. “When I return I shall be forty and rebuild my fortune. I intend to make a noble fortune in seven years.”

“You? to undertake commerce!” Mathias blurted.

“I shall sail under the name Camille. Commerce is only a last chance. I start with a sum to redeem my future on a large scale,” Paul answered.

“Where is that money?” Mathias demanded.

“A friend will send it.”

Mathias, hardened by fifty years of notarial practice, distrusted that optimism. “I never knew a ruined man whose friend would lend him money.”

“You don’t know de Marsay. He has sold some investments already; to‑morrow you will receive from him a bill of exchange for one hundred and fifty thousand francs.”

Mathias brightened. “If that be so, cannot your friend settle your difficulties here? You could live at Lanstrac five or six years on your wife’s income and recover.”

“No arrangement could pay off one million five hundred thousand francs of debt, in which my wife is involved to the amount of five hundred and fifty thousand.”

Mathias’s eyes widened. “You cannot mean you have incurred a million and a half of debt in four years?”

“Nothing is more certain, Mathias. I gave diamonds to my wife; I spent the 150,000 I got from selling Madame Evangelista’s house on the Paris house; I used other money for payments required by the marriage contract. I sold Natalie’s forty thousand a year in the funds at eighty‑seven, so I became two hundred thousand francs in debt within a month after marriage. That left us sixty‑seven thousand francs a year; but we spent three times that every year. Add usurers’ interest and other payments and you have a million. I paid a hundred thousand for a coronet of diamond wheat‑ears. Then there are my notes protested, usurers’ claims, and the preferment of her claims when my property was mortgaged—there is no remedy.”

Mathias, practical and shocked, suggested alternatives: have the Comtesse give up her diamonds, pledge Paul’s income, sell Belle‑Rose. Paul countered that, once down, the husband’s options are limited and his wife’s claims take priority. Mathias pressed the idea of selling Belle‑Rose—the 1825 vintage still in the cellars—but Paul said he could not act without harming Natalie; she would buy Belle‑Rose in, he advised her to do so. Mathias thought he might push for seven hundred thousand francs for the estate; Paul revealed that his Bordeaux house might fetch Solonet’s higher bid, and noted speculation about Solonet and the family.

Mathias could not reconcile Paul’s behavior with his duty as a husband. “A wife should share good and evil,” he said. “Your marriage contract required you to have a son to consolidate the entail. In those noble families the wife considered it her duty to bear children.”

Paul defended his conduct romantically. “I am no longer ‘Monsieur le comte,’ dear Mathias. I have loved two women—Natalie and her mother—and that affection ruined me. How could I refuse her every caprice? I could not accept sacrifice on her part. We might live at Lanstrac and part with her diamonds, but I would sooner go to India and work for a fortune than tear Natalie from the life she enjoys. Women are angels who should not be mixed in sordid interests.”

Mathias, incredulous, tried reasoning that if Paul had children his wife would not have dissipated his fortune; a domestic family duty would have kept her. Paul urged him to stop moralizing; he preferred to go without the pain of revisiting mistakes.

The next morning a courier arrived with a bill of exchange from Henri de Marsay for one hundred and fifty thousand francs. Paul remarked that de Marsay never wrote to him but acted—“If you knew with what superiority that man can rise above sentiments and self-interest, you would be astonished,” he said.

Mathias tried one last time to deter his friend from leaving, but Paul’s resolve was fixed.

By luck the wind was fair and the Belle‑Amélie sailed the next day. The quay was lined with relations, friends, and curious onlookers; Paul’s ruin made as much spectacle as his former wealth had. Mathias accompanied him to the quay and heard the coarse comments—the Pink of Fashion now ruined, the playboy who had ruined himself by Paris, women, and the Bourse. Paul boarded composedly, defiant to the crowd, and as the sailors weighed anchor Mathias, too frail to go aboard, entrusted two letters to a sailor and begged him to give them to Paul if he came ashore.

“My friend,” Mathias said, pressing one letter into the sailor’s hand, “this packet is important; it has just arrived by courier from Paris in thirty‑five hours. Tell Monsieur le comte; it may change his plans.”

The Breton sailor, unwilling to row back, handed the letters to Paul without telling him Mathias’s message. Paul, recognizing the handwriting of his wife and of de Marsay, felt anxious but did not read them—he put them unread into his pocket, not wanting to be swayed as the ship moved away. Night and sea brought doubt. He awoke with fears for Natalie and regretted leaving. Sea‑sickness laid him low for three days; when he felt better he found the letters and opened his wife’s first.

To understand the Comtesse’s letter it is necessary to note Paul’s note he had left her when departing. In that letter Paul—ashamed to tell her directly—had explained that he was going to India to repair his shattered fortune, that he had not told her because it would have hurt her, and that he left her free to enjoy society. He begged her to trust de Marsay to liquidate his affairs, and he vowed to return wealthy, to lay riches at her feet as his father had for his mother. He closed with the lover’s images: he would never leave her; his love was a religion; he went like a man vowed to a great enterprise.

Natalie’s reply was immediate, ardent, and self‑sacrificing. She reproached him for leaving without consulting her and asked whether he had right to act as if he were freed from her. She rejected the separation as to property they had been urged to accept. She declared her admiration and pride at his resolve—“To despair of nothing and start courageously to seek fortune—only your strength could do it.” She would go to live at Lanstrac with her mother and economize to pay his debts. She implored him not to fear her fidelity and declared herself, perhaps, with child; she believed they might be expecting a son. Her mother had already sent thirty thousand francs to help. Augustine had been with her; she and her mother would use all resources and would pledge diamonds if necessary. Her mother blamed Paul for not telling her of his embarrassments, saying she would not have bought Grainrouge had she known. Madame Evangelista feared for Paul’s health in India and entreated sobriety and caution regarding women—advice that made Natalie smile at the irony. Natalie promised obedience to his wish to trust de Marsay, to seek help from him, and vowed to wait at Lanstrac with prayer and hope. She sealed her letter with tears and a kiss: “I shall live at Lanstrac with my mother and my child and await you; our love is a talisman.”

Paul read her letter again and again. It alternately strengthened and weakened him: such exclusive tenderness inspired both faith and jealousy—jealousy being the counterpart of happiness. Yet he kept returning to pure faith, sustained by Christian and lover’s conviction.

Next he opened the letter from de Marsay—the one Mathias had anticipated. Paul had written de Marsay before leaving, asking him to sign a deed to lease his house to de Marsay for six years at a nominal rent, to send a duplicate to his wife, and to lend him 150,000 francs drawn on his share of the entailed revenues and forwarded by bill of exchange to Maître Mathias, endorsed by the Comtesse. Paul explained his ruin and his resolve to go to India rather than suicide; he trusted de Marsay to guard his wife, fearing that being childless a rival might be dangerous. He asked de Marsay to act as guardian and banker in his absence.

Henri de Marsay’s reply demolished Paul’s hopes. He began with the cynical surprise that Paul had “made a wreck,” chiding him for not having confided earlier. De Marsay explained that Pauline’s notes had been protested at Lecuyer’s instigation—Lecuyer, former head clerk to Solonet—and that Solonet was acting as proxy for Madame Evangelista. Paul’s mother‑in‑law, he wrote, actually held Paul’s notes for 100,000 francs, of which she had advanced only 70,000. Solonet and accomplices had already arranged for Belle‑Rose and the farms and mortgages on the Bordeaux house to pass into Natalie’s hands via straw men. In short, de Marsay had uncovered a domestic conspiracy: the mother and daughter had calculated that, with Paul at sea, they could secure his property.

De Marsay’s tone shifted to satirical moralizing and political gossip. He would pay compliments to Madame Evangelista and say sly things about Paul at the Spanish embassy; but he warned Paul that the mission he had given him was impossible—“consummatum est.” He reproached Paul for having created his own ruin by weak indulgence. Had Paul loved his mother‑in‑law before marriage he might have been made a peer; instead he had been a dupe. Where Paul lavished gifts, de Marsay suggested, the pleasure of his wife’s social life found a rival in Felix Vandenesse, who had stirred a chord in Natalie that Paul never touched. De Marsay hinted that when a husband “shoots his rival” he becomes respectable; he proposed that Paul abandon his expatriation, return at once to Paris, and allow de Marsay to arrange a duel with Vandenesse in which Paul would take the first shot and possibly wound him. Killing the rival, he argued, would restore Paul’s honor and position; it would make the mother‑in‑law supple and the world tremble, enabling Paul to sell property with redemption rights, obtain an office, and regain his footing. De Marsay mentioned he was turning to politics and expected within five years to reach a ministry or ambassadorship; he had prospects through an advantageous marriage arranged by his mother to an English heiress, Miss Dinah Stevens, whose fortune and entail would secure him great landed income. If Paul would stay and play the political‑social game with him, de Marsay promised to help him ascend.

Finally, de Marsay offered practical immediate aid: he “held” 100,000 francs for Paul to pay principal debts. He chided Paul for being deceived into exile and urged him to profit by the chance to seize marriage and political life as instruments of recovery; his letter counseled systematic opposition to society rather than a naive expatriation.

Paul read de Marsay’s letter as if struck by a pickaxe. It demolished his hopes, illusions, and the image of Natalie he had carried. The ship had passed the Azores by then. In the ruins of his ambitions, a cold, impotent rage seized him. “What had I done to them?” he cried—the plaint of the man who sees nothing and foresees nothing. Many men might have been driven mad by such disclosure; Paul, thoroughly exhausted, went to bed and slept that heavy, stupefying sleep which follows immense disasters—the same great sleep that fell on Napoleon after Waterloo.
