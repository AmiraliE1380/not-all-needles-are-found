LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES

By Honore de Balzac

Translated by R. S. Scott

DEDICATION

To George Sand

Your name, dear George, adds a reflected radiance to my book, not for self-interest or diffidence, but to witness our enduring friendship, which has triumphed over the world's malice. This bond is unlikely to change. The names attached to my works bring me pleasure amidst the vexation of their increasing number. Each new book brings fresh annoyance, as if my pen could rival the world from which I draw my models! Would it not be wonderful if future antiquarians found only great names and generous hearts among my friends? I take pride in this possession rather than in unstable popularity. For those who know you well, it is happiness to sign myself, as I do here,

Your friend,  
DE BALZAC.  
PARIS, June 1840.

LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES

FIRST PART

I. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENEE DE MAUCOMBE. PARIS, September.

Sweetheart, I too am free! I am the first to write, unless you have already sent a letter to Blois.

Raise those great black eyes of yours, fixed on my opening sentence, and save your excitement for the letter that will tell you of my first love. By the way, why always "first?" Is there a second love?

Don't run on like this, you will say, but tell me how you escaped from the convent where you were to take your vows. My own deliverance was quite ordinary. The cries of an alarmed conscience triumphed over stern policy. The melancholy that seized me after you left hastened my aunt's decision; she did not want to see me die of decline, and my mother, whose cure for my malady was a novitiate, relented.

So I am in Paris, thanks to you, my love! If you had seen me the day I parted from you, you would have gloried in the deep impression you made on my youthful heart. We had lived so closely together, sharing dreams and letting our fancies roam, that I believe our souls became welded together, like those two Hungarian girls whose tragic story we heard from M. Beauvisage—poor man! Never was anyone better suited to the role of convent physician!

Tell me, did you not droop and sicken without your darling?

In my gloomy depression, I could only count the ties that bind us. It seemed distance had loosened them; I wearied of life, like a turtle-dove widowed of her mate. Death smiled sweetly on me, and I was quietly proceeding to die. To be at Blois, at the Carmelites, consumed by dread of taking my vows there, a Mlle. de la Valliere without her prelude, and without you! How could I not be sick—sick unto death?

How different it used to be! That monotonous existence, where every hour brings its duty, prayer, and task, with such desperate regularity that you could tell what a Carmelite sister was doing at any hour, had become for us a world of life and movement. Imagination opened her fairy realms, and our spirits roamed freely, each serving as a magic steed to the other. The world from which our bodies were shut out became our playground, where we reveled in frolicsome adventure. Even the _Lives of the Saints_ helped us understand what was left unsaid! But the day I lost your sweet company, I became a true Carmelite, like a modern Danaid, drawing an empty pitcher from Heaven knows where, thinking to find it full.

My aunt knew nothing of this inner life. How could she, who has made a paradise for herself within the two acres of her convent, understand my revolt against life? A religious life, if embraced by girls of our age, demands either extreme simplicity of soul, which we do not possess, or an ardor for self-sacrifice like my aunt's noble character. But she sacrificed herself for a devoted brother; to do the same for an unknown person or an idea is surely more than can be asked of mortals.

For the last fortnight, I have been swallowing reckless words, burying reflections in my bosom, and accumulating a store of things to tell you, fit for your ear alone. I would have suffocated without the resource of letter-writing as a poor substitute for our beloved talks. How hungry one's heart gets! I am beginning my journal this morning, picturing that yours is already started, and that soon I shall be at home in your beautiful Gemenos valley, which I know only through your descriptions, just as you will live that Paris life revealed to you only in our dreams.

Well, sweet child, know that on a certain morning—a red-letter day in my life—a lady companion and Philippe, the last of my grandmother's valets, arrived from Paris to carry me off. When my aunt summoned me to her room and told me the news, I could not speak for joy, only gazed at her stupidly.

"My child," she said, "I can see you leave me without regret, but this farewell is not the last; we shall meet again. God has placed on your forehead the sign of the elect. You have the pride that leads to heaven or hell, but your nature is too noble to choose the downward path. I know you better than you know yourself; with you, passion will be very different from what it is with most women."

She drew me gently to her and kissed my forehead. The kiss burned with the consuming fire that has turned her life to black, softened the lines around her eyes, and cast a sallow tinge over her beautiful face.

Before replying, I kissed her hands.

"Dear aunt," I said, "I shall never forget your kindness; and if it has not made your nunnery all that it ought to be for my health of body and soul, you may be sure nothing short of a broken heart will bring me back again—and that you would not wish for me. You will not see me here again till my royal lover has deserted me, and I warn you that if I catch him, death alone shall tear him from me. I fear no Montespan."

She smiled and said:

"Go, madcap, and take your idle fancies with you. There is certainly more of the bold Montespan in you than of the gentle la Valliere."

I threw my arms around her. The poor lady could not refrain from escorting me to the carriage. There her tender gaze was divided between me and the armorial bearings.

At Beaugency, night overtook me, still sunk in a stupor of mind produced by these strange parting words. What awaits me in this world for which I have so hungered?

To begin with, I found no one to receive me; my heart had been schooled in vain. My mother was at the Bois de Boulogne, my father at the Council; my brother, the Duc de Rhetore, never comes in until it is time to dress for dinner. Miss Griffith (she resembles a griffin) and Philippe took me to my rooms.

The suite belonged to my beloved grandmother, the Princess de Vauremont, to whom I owe some fortune no one has ever mentioned. As you read this, you will understand the sadness that came over me as I entered a place sacred to so many memories, finding the rooms just as she had left them! I was to sleep in the bed where she died.

Sitting on the edge of the sofa, I burst into tears, forgetting I was not alone, remembering how often I had stood there by her knees to hear her words. I had gazed upon her face, buried in brown lace, worn by age and the pangs of approaching death. The room seemed still warm with her presence. How is it that Armande-Louise-Marie de Chaulieu must be like a peasant girl, who sleeps in her mother's bed the very morrow of her death? For me, it was as though the Princess, who died in 1817, had passed away but yesterday.

I saw many things in the room that ought to have been removed. Their presence showed the carelessness with which busy people treat their own affairs, and the little thought given since her death to this grand old lady, a striking figure of the eighteenth century. Philippe seemed to sense my tears. He told me that the Princess's furniture had been left to me in her will, and that my father had allowed the larger suites to remain dismantled, as the Revolution had left them. Upon hearing this, I rose, and Philippe opened the door to the small drawing-room leading into the reception-rooms.

In these, I found all the well-remembered wreckage; the panels above the doors, which had contained valuable pictures, bare of all but empty frames; broken marbles, mirrors carried off. In old days, I was afraid to go up the state staircase and cross these vast, deserted rooms; I used to get to the Princess's rooms by a small staircase that runs under the arch of the larger one, leading to the secret door of her dressing-room.

My suite, consisting of a drawing-room, bedroom, and pretty morning-room in scarlet and gold, lies in the wing on the side of the Invalides. The house is separated from the boulevard by a wall covered with creepers and a splendid avenue of trees, mingling their foliage with that of the young elms on the sidewalk. But for the blue-and-gold dome of the Invalides and its gray stone mass, you might be in a wood.

The style of decoration in these rooms, along with their situation, indicates they were the old show suite of the duchesses, while the dukes must have had theirs in the opposite wing. The two suites are decorously separated by the two main blocks, as well as by the central one, which contained those vast, gloomy, resounding halls shown to me by Philippe, all despoiled of their splendor, as in my childhood.

Philippe grew quite confidential when he saw my surprise. In this home of diplomacy, even the servants have a reserved and mysterious air. He told me that a law would soon be passed restoring the value of property to the fugitives of the Revolution, and that my father is waiting to renovate the house until this restitution is made, the king's architect having estimated the damage at three hundred thousand livres.

This news flung me back despairing on my drawing-room sofa. Could it be that my father, instead of spending this money arranging a marriage for me, would have left me to die in the convent? This was the first thought to greet me on the threshold of my home.

Ah! Renee, what would I have given then to rest my head upon your shoulder, or to return to the days when my grandmother filled these rooms with life? You two in all the world have loved me—one away at Maucombe, and the other who survives only in my heart, the dear old lady, whose still youthful eyes opened from sleep at my call. How well we understood each other!

These memories suddenly changed my mood. What had seemed profanation now breathed of holy association. It was sweet to inhale the faint odor of the powder she loved still lingering in the room; sweet to sleep beneath the shelter of those yellow damask curtains, which must have retained something of her spirit. I told Philippe to polish the old furniture and make the rooms look lived in; I explained how I wanted everything arranged, and where to place each piece. In this way, I entered into possession, showing how an air of youth might be given to the dear old things.

The bedroom is white, a little dulled with time, harmonizing well with the faded colors of the Savonnerie tapestry, presented to my grandmother by Louis XV. The timepiece was a gift from the Marechal de Saxe, and the china ornaments on the mantelpiece came from the Marechal de Richelieu. My grandmother's portrait, painted at twenty-five, hangs in an oval frame opposite that of the King. The Prince, her husband, is conspicuous by his absence. I like this frank negligence, untinged by hypocrisy—a characteristic touch summing up her charming personality. Once, when my grandmother was seriously ill, her confessor urged that the Prince, waiting in the drawing-room, should be admitted.

"He can come in with the doctor and his drugs," was her reply.

The bed has a canopy and well-stuffed back, with curtains looped up with fine wide bands. The furniture is gilded wood, upholstered in the same yellow damask with white flowers that drapes the windows, lined with white silk that looks watered. The panels over the doors have been painted, representing a sunrise and a moonlight scene.

The fireplace is a very interesting feature. Life in the last century centered around the hearth, where great events were enacted. The copper gilt grate is a marvel of workmanship, and the mantelpiece is delicately finished; the fire-irons are beautifully chased; the bellows are a perfect gem. The tapestry of the screen comes from the Gobelins and is exquisitely mounted; charming figures run all over the frame, on the feet, the supporting bar, and the wings; the whole thing is wrought like a fan.

Dearly would I like to know who was the giver of this dainty work of art, which was such a favorite with her. How often have I seen the old lady, her feet upon the bar, reclining in the easy-chair, toying with the snuff-box, which lay upon the ledge between her box of pastilles and her silk mitts. What a coquette she was! To the day of her death, she took as much care with her appearance as though the beautiful portrait had been painted only yesterday, and she were waiting to receive the throng of exquisites from the Court! How the armchair recalls the inimitable sweep of her skirts as she sank back in it!

These women of a past generation carried off secrets typical of their age. The Princess had a certain turn of the head, a way of dropping her glance and remarks, a choice of words, which I look for in vain, even in my mother. There was subtlety and good nature; the points were made without affectation. Her talk was lengthy yet concise; she told a good story and could express her meaning in three words. Above all, she was extremely free-thinking, which has undoubtedly influenced my perspective.

From seven to ten, I never left her side; it pleased her to attract me as much as it pleased me to go. This preference caused more than one passage at arms between her and my mother, and nothing intensifies feeling like the icy breath of persecution. How charming was her greeting, "Here you are, little rogue!" when curiosity taught me to glide stealthily to her room. She felt that I loved her, and this childish affection was welcome as a ray of sunshine in the winter of her life.

I don't know what went on in her rooms at night, but she had many visitors; when I tiptoed in the morning to see if she were awake, I would find the drawing-room furniture disarranged, card-tables set out, and patches of snuff scattered about.

This drawing-room is furnished in the same style as the bedroom. The chairs and tables are oddly shaped, with claw feet and hollow moldings. Rich garlands of flowers, beautifully designed and carved, wind over the mirrors and hang down in festoons. On the consoles are fine china vases. The ground colors are scarlet and white. My grandmother was a high-spirited, striking brunette, as might be inferred from her choice of colors. I have found in the drawing-room a writing-table I remember well; the figures on it used to fascinate me; it is plaited in graven silver, a present from one of the Genoese Lomellini. Each side of the table represents the occupations of a different season, with hundreds of figures in relief.

I remained alone for two hours, while old memories rose before me, one after another, in this spot hallowed by the death of a remarkable woman among the witty and beautiful Court ladies of Louis XV.'s day.

You know how abruptly I was parted from her, at a day's notice, in 1816.

"Go and bid good-bye to your grandmother," said my mother.

The Princess received me as usual, without display of feeling, expressing no surprise at my departure.

"You are going to the convent, dear," she said, "and will see your aunt there, who is an excellent woman. I shall take care, though, that they don't make a victim of you; you shall be independent and able to marry whom you please."

Six months later, she died. Her will was given into the keeping of the Prince de Talleyrand, her most devoted friend. He contrived, while visiting Mlle. de Chargeboeuf, to intimate through her that my grandmother forbade me to take the vows. I hope to meet the Prince soon, and then I shall learn more from him.

Thus, sweetheart, if I have found no one in flesh and blood to meet me, I have comforted myself with the shade of the dear Princess and prepared to carry out one of our pledges—to keep each other informed of the smallest details in our homes and occupations. It makes such a difference to know where and how the life of one we love is passed. Send me a faithful picture of the veriest trifles around you, omitting nothing, not even the sunset lights among the tall trees.

October 19th.

It was three in the afternoon when I arrived. About half-past five, Rose came and told me that my mother had returned, so I went downstairs to pay my respects to her.

My mother lives in a suite on the ground floor, exactly corresponding to mine, in the same block. I am just over her head, and the same secret staircase serves for both. My father's rooms are in the opposite block, larger by the space occupied by the grand staircase on our side. These ancestral mansions are so spacious that my parents continue to occupy the ground-floor rooms, despite the social duties that have returned with the Bourbons, and can even receive guests there.

I found my mother, dressed for the evening, in her drawing-room, where nothing had changed. I came slowly down the stairs, speculating how I would be met by this mother who had shown so little maternal affection, from whom I had heard nothing beyond the two letters you know. Judging it unworthy to simulate an affection I could not feel, I put on the air of a pious imbecile and entered the room with many inward qualms, which soon disappeared. My mother’s tact was equal to the occasion. She made no pretense of emotion; she neither held me at arm's length nor hugged me to her bosom like a beloved daughter, but greeted me as though we had parted the evening before. Her manner was that of the kindliest and most sincere friend, addressing me like a grown person, first kissing me on the forehead.

"My dear little one," she said, "if you were to die at the convent, it is much better to live with your family. You frustrate your father's plans and mine; but the age of blind obedience to parents is past. M. de Chaulieu's intention, and in this I am quite at one with him, is to lose no opportunity of making your life pleasant and letting you see the world. At your age, I would have thought as you do, therefore I am not vexed with you; it is impossible you should understand what we expected from you. You will not find any absurd severity in me; and if you have ever thought me heartless, you will soon find out your mistake. Still, though I wish you to feel perfectly free, I think that, to begin with, you would do well to follow the counsels of a mother who wishes to be a sister to you."

I was charmed by the Duchess, who spoke gently, straightening my convent tippet as she did. At thirty-eight, she is still exquisitely beautiful. She has dark-blue eyes, silken lashes, a smooth forehead, and a complexion so pink and white that you might think she paints. Her bust and shoulders are marvelous, and her waist is as slender as yours. Her hand is milk-white and extraordinarily beautiful; the nails catch the light in their perfect polish, the thumb is like ivory, the little finger stands just a little apart from the rest, and the foot matches the hand; it is the Spanish foot of Mlle. de Vandenesse. If she is like this at forty, she will still be beautiful at sixty.

I replied, sweetheart, like a good little girl. I was as nice to her as she was to me, nay, nicer. Her beauty completely vanquished me; it seemed only natural that such a woman should be absorbed in her regal part. I told her this as simply as if I were talking to you. I daresay it surprised her to hear words of affection from her daughter's mouth, and the unfeigned homage of my admiration evidently touched her deeply. Her manner changed and became even more engaging; she dropped all formality as she said:

"I am much pleased with you, and I hope we shall remain good friends."

The words struck me as charmingly naive, but I did not let this appear, for I saw at once that the prudent course was to allow her to believe herself much deeper and cleverer than her daughter. So I only stared vacantly, and she was delighted. I kissed her hands repeatedly, telling her how happy it made me to be treated so and to feel at ease with her. I even confided my previous tremors. She smiled, put her arm around my neck, and drawing me towards her, kissed me on the forehead most affectionately.

"Dear child," she said, "we have people coming to dinner today. Perhaps you will agree with me that it is better for you not to make your first appearance in society until you have been in the dressmaker's hands; so, after you have seen your father and brother, you can go upstairs again."

I assented most heartily. My mother's exquisite dress was the first revelation to me of the world our dreams had pictured; but I did not feel the slightest desire to rival her.

My father now entered, and the Duchess presented me to him.

He became all at once most affectionate, playing the father's part so well that I could not but believe his heart was in it. Taking my two hands in his and kissing them, with more of the lover than the father in his manner, he said:

"So this is my rebel daughter!"

He drew me towards him, with his arm passed tenderly around my waist, kissing me on the cheeks and forehead.

"The pleasure with which we shall watch your success in society will atone for the disappointment we felt at your change of vocation," he said. Then, turning to my mother, "Do you know that she is going to turn out very pretty, and you will be proud of her some day?—Here is your brother, Rhetore." He introduced a fine young man who came in, "Alphonse, here is your convent-bred sister, who threatens to send her nun's frock to the deuce."

My brother came up leisurely and took my hand, which he pressed.

"Come, come, you may kiss her," said my father.

And he kissed me on both cheeks.

"I am delighted to see you," he said, "and I take your side against my father."

I thanked him, but could not help thinking he might have come to Blois when he was at Orleans visiting our Marquis brother in his quarters.

Fearing the arrival of strangers, I now withdrew. I tidied up my rooms and laid out on the scarlet velvet of my lovely table all the materials necessary for writing to you, meditating all the while on my new situation.

This, my fair sweetheart, is a true and veracious account of the return of a girl of eighteen, after an absence of nine years, to the bosom of one of the noblest families in the kingdom. I was tired from the journey and all the emotions I had been through, so I went to bed in convent fashion, at eight o'clock after supper. They have preserved even a little Saxe service which the dear Princess used when she had a fancy for taking her meals alone.

II. THE SAME TO THE SAME November 25th.

The next day, I found my rooms done out and dusted, and even flowers put in the vases by old Philippe. I began to feel at home. Only it didn't occur to anyone that a Carmelite schoolgirl has an early appetite, and Rose had no end of trouble getting breakfast for me.

"Mlle. goes to bed at dinner-time," she said, "and gets up when the Duke is just returning home."

I began to write. About one o'clock, my father knocked at the door of the small drawing-room and asked if he might come in. I opened the door; he came in and found me writing to you.

"My dear," he began, "you will have to get yourself clothes and make these rooms comfortable. In this purse, you will find twelve thousand francs, which is the yearly income I purpose allowing you for your expenses. You will make arrangements with your mother for a governess you may like, in case Miss Griffith doesn't please you, for Mme. de Chaulieu will not have time to go out with you in the mornings. A carriage and man-servant shall be at your disposal."

"Let me keep Philippe," I said.

"So be it," he replied. "But don't be uneasy; you have money enough of your own to be no burden either to your mother or me."

"May I ask how much I have?"

"Certainly, my child," he said. "Your grandmother left you five hundred thousand francs; this was the amount of her savings, for she would not alienate a foot of land from the family. This sum has been placed in Government stock, and, with the accumulated interest, now brings in about forty thousand francs a year. With this, I had purposed making an independence for your second brother, and it is here that you have upset my plans. Later, however, it is possible that you may fall in with them. It shall rest with yourself, for I have confidence in your good sense far more than I had expected.

"I do not need to tell you how a daughter of the Chaulieus ought to behave. The pride so plainly written in your features is my best guarantee. Safeguards, such as common folk surround their daughters with, would be an insult in our family. A slander reflecting on your name might cost the life of the man bold enough to utter it, or the life of one of your brothers, if by chance the right should not prevail. No more on this subject. Good-bye, little one."

He kissed me on the forehead and went out. I cannot understand the relinquishment of this plan after nine years' persistence in it. My father's frankness is what I like. There is no ambiguity about his words. My money ought to belong to his Marquis son. Who, then, has had bowels of mercy? My mother? My father? Or could it be my brother?

I remained sitting on my grandmother's sofa, staring at the purse my father had left on the mantelpiece, pleased and vexed that I could not withdraw my mind from the money. Further speculation was useless. My doubts had been cleared up, and there was something fine in the way my pride was spared.

Philippe has spent the morning rushing about among the various shops and workpeople who are to undertake the task of my metamorphosis. A famous dressmaker, by name Victorine, has come, as well as a woman for underclothing, and a shoemaker. I am as impatient as a child to know what I shall be like when I emerge from the sack that constituted the conventual uniform; but all these tradespeople take a long time; the corset-maker requires a whole week if my figure is not to be spoiled. You see, I have a figure, dear; this becomes serious. Janssen, the Operatic shoemaker, solemnly assures me that I have my mother's foot. The whole morning has gone in these weighty occupations. Even a glovemaker has come to take the measure of my hand. The underclothing woman has got my orders.

At the meal I call dinner, and the others lunch, my mother told me that we were going together to the milliner's to see some hats, so that my taste should be formed, and I might be in a position to order my own.

This burst of independence dazzles me. I am like a blind man who has just recovered his sight. Now I begin to understand the vast interval separating a Carmelite sister from a girl in society. Of ourselves, we could never have conceived it.

During this lunch, my father seemed absent-minded, and we left him to his thoughts; he is deep in the King's confidence. I was entirely forgotten; but from what I have seen, I have no doubt he will remember me when he has need of me. He is a very attractive man in spite of his fifty years. His figure is youthful; he is well made, fair, and extremely graceful in his movements. He has a diplomatic face, at once dumb and expressive; his nose is long and slender, and he has brown eyes.

What a handsome pair! Strange thoughts assail me as it becomes plain to me that these two, so perfectly matched in birth, wealth, and mental superiority, live entirely apart, having nothing in common but their name. The show of unity is only for the world.

The cream of the Court and diplomatic circles were here last night. Very soon, I am going to a ball given by the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, and I shall be presented to the society I am so eager to know. A dancing-master is coming every morning to give me lessons, for I must be able to dance in a month, or I can't go to the ball.

Before dinner, my mother came to talk about the governess with me. I have decided to keep Miss Griffith, who was recommended by the English ambassador. Miss Griffith is the daughter of a clergyman; her mother was of good family, and she is perfectly well bred. She is thirty-six and will teach me English. The good soul is quite handsome enough to have ambitions; she is Scotch—poor and proud—and will act as my chaperon. She is to sleep in Rose's room. Rose will be under her orders. I saw at a glance that my governess would be governed by me. In the six days we have been together, she has made sure that I am the only person likely to take an interest in her; while, for my part, I have ascertained that, for all her statuesque features, she will prove accommodating. She seems to me a kindly soul, but cautious. I have not been able to extract a word of what passed between her and my mother.

Another trifling piece of news! My father has this morning refused the appointment as Minister of State which was offered him. This accounts for his preoccupied manner last night. He says he would prefer an embassy to the worries of public debate.

This news was told to me at lunch, the one moment of the day when my father, mother, and brother see each other in an easy way. The servants then only come when they are rung for. The rest of the day, my brother, as well as my father, spends out of the house. My mother has her toilet to make; between two and four, she is never visible; at four o'clock, she goes out for an hour's drive; when she is not dining out, she receives from six to seven, and the evening is given to entertainments of various kinds—theatres, balls, concerts, at homes. In short, her life is so full that I don't believe she ever has a quarter of an hour to herself. She must spend considerable time dressing in the morning; for at lunch, which takes place between eleven and twelve, she is exquisite. The meaning of the things said about her is dawning on me. She begins the day with a bath barely warmed and a cup of cold coffee with cream; then she dresses. She is never, except on some great emergency, called before nine o'clock. In summer, there are morning rides, and at two o'clock, she receives a young man whom I have never yet contrived to see.

Behold our family life! We meet at lunch and dinner, though often I am alone with my mother at this latter meal, and I foresee that still oftener I shall take it in my own rooms (following the example of my grandmother) with only Miss Griffith for company, for my mother frequently dines out. I have ceased to wonder at the indifference my family has shown to me. In Paris, my dear, it is a miracle of virtue to love the people who live with you, for you see little enough of them; as for the absent—they do not exist!

Knowing as this may sound, I have not yet set foot in the streets, and am deplorably ignorant. I must wait until I am less of the country cousin and have brought my dress and deportment into keeping with the society I am about to enter, the whirl of which amazes me even here, where only distant murmurs reach my ear. So far, I have not gone beyond the garden; but the Italian opera opens in a few days, and my mother has a box there. I am crazy with delight at the thought of hearing Italian music and seeing French acting.

Already I begin to drop convent habits for those of society. I spend the evening writing to you until the moment for going to bed arrives. This has been postponed to ten o'clock, the hour at which my mother goes out, if she is not at the theatre. There are twelve theatres in Paris.

I am grossly ignorant and I read a lot, but quite indiscriminately, one book leading to another. I find the names of fresh books on the cover of the one I am reading; but as I have no one to direct me, I light on some that are fearfully dull. What modern literature I have read all turns upon love, the subject that used to bulk so largely in our thoughts, because it seemed our fate was determined by man and for man. But how inferior are these authors to two little girls, known as Sweetheart and Darling—otherwise Renee and Louise. Ah! my love, what wretched plots, what ridiculous situations, and what poverty of sentiment! Two books, however, have given me wonderful pleasure—_Corinne_ and _Adolphe_. Apropos of this, I asked my father one day whether it would be possible for me to see Mme. de Stael. My father, mother, and Alphonse all burst out laughing, and Alphonse said:

"Where in the world has she sprung from?"

To which my father replied:

"What fools we are! She springs from the Carmelites."

"My child, Mme. de Stael is dead," said my mother gently.

When I finished _Adolphe_, I asked Miss Griffith how a woman could be betrayed.

"Why, of course, when she loves," was her reply.

Renee, tell me, do you think we could be betrayed by a man?

Miss Griffith has at last discerned that I am not an utter ignoramus, that I have somewhere a hidden vein of knowledge, the knowledge we learned from each other in our random arguments. She sees that it is only superficial facts of which I am ignorant. The poor thing has opened her heart to me. Her curt reply to my question, when I compare it with all the sorrows I can imagine, makes me feel quite creepy. Once more, she urged me not to be dazzled by the glitter of society, to be always on my guard, especially against what most attracts me. This is the sum-total of her wisdom, and I can get nothing more out of her. Her lectures, therefore, become a trifle monotonous, and she might be compared in this respect to the bird which has only one cry.

III. THE SAME TO THE SAME December.

My Darling,—Here I am ready to make my bow to the world. By way of preparation, I have been trying to commit all the follies I could think of before sobering down for my entry. This morning, I have seen myself, after many rehearsals, well and duly equipped—stays, shoes, curls, dress, ornaments—all in order. Following the example of duelists before a meeting, I tried my arms in the privacy of my chamber. I wanted to see how I would look and had no difficulty discovering a certain air of victory and triumph, bound to carry all before it. I mustered all my forces, in accordance with that splendid maxim of antiquity, "Know thyself!" and boundless was my delight in thus making my own acquaintance. Griffith was the sole spectator of this doll's play, in which I was at once doll and child. You think you know me? You are hugely mistaken.

Here is a portrait, then, Renee, of your sister, formerly disguised as a Carmelite, now brought to life again as a frivolous society girl. She is one of the greatest beauties in France—Provence, of course, excepted. I don't see how I can give a more accurate summary of this interesting topic.

True, I have my weak points; but were I a man, I should adore them. They arise from what is most promising in me. When you have spent a fortnight admiring the exquisite curves of your mother's arms, and that mother the Duchesse de Chaulieu, it is impossible, my dear, not to deplore your own angular elbows. Yet there is consolation in observing the fineness of the wrist and a certain grace of line in those hollows, which will yet fill out and show plump, round, and well-modeled under the satiny skin. The somewhat crude outline of the arms is seen again in the shoulders. Strictly speaking, indeed, I have no shoulders, but only two bony blades, standing out in harsh relief. My figure also lacks pliancy; there is a stiffness about the side lines.

Poof! There's the worst out. But then the contours are bold and delicate, the bright, pure flame of health bites into the vigorous lines, a flood of life and blue blood pulses under the transparent skin, and the fairest daughter of Eve would seem a Negress beside me! I have the foot of a gazelle! My joints are finely turned, my features of Greek correctness. It is true, madame, that the flesh tints do not melt into each other; but at least, they stand out clear and bright. In short, I am a very pretty green fruit, with all the charm of unripeness. I see a great likeness to the face in my aunt's old missal, which rises out of a violet lily.

There is no silly weakness in the blue of my insolent eyes; the white is pure mother-of-pearl, prettily marked with tiny veins, and the thick, long lashes fall like a silken fringe. My forehead sparkles, and the hair grows deliciously; it ripples into waves of pale gold, growing browner towards the center, whence escape little rebel locks, which alone would tell that my fairness is not of the insipid and hysterical type. I am a tropical blonde, with plenty of blood in my veins, a blonde more apt to strike than to turn the cheek. What do you think the hairdresser proposed? He wanted, if you please, to smooth my hair into two bands and place over my forehead a pearl, kept in place by a gold chain! He said it would recall the Middle Ages.

I told him I was not aged enough to have reached the middle or to need an ornament to freshen me up!

The nose is slender, and the well-cut nostrils are separated by a sweet little pink partition—an imperious, mocking nose, with a tip too sensitive ever to grow fat or red. Sweetheart, if this won't find a husband for a dowerless maiden, I'm a donkey. The ears are daintily curled; a pearl hanging from either lobe would show yellow. The neck is long, with an undulating motion full of dignity. In the shade, the white ripens to a golden tinge. Perhaps the mouth is a little large. But how expressive! what a color on the lips! how prettily the teeth laugh!

Then, dear, there is a harmony running through all. What a gait! what a voice! We have not forgotten how our grandmother's skirts fell into place without a touch. In a word, I am lovely and charming. When the mood comes, I can laugh one of our good old laughs, and no one will think the less of me; the dimples, impressed by Comedy's light fingers on my fair cheeks, will command respect. Or I can let my eyes fall and my heart freeze under my snowy brows. I can pose as a Madonna with melancholy, swan-like neck, and the painters' virgins will be nowhere; my place in heaven would be far above them. A man would be forced to chant when he spoke to me.

So, you see, my panoply is complete, and I can run the whole gamut of coquetry from deepest bass to shrillest treble. It is a huge advantage not to be all of one piece. Now, my mother is neither playful nor virginal. Her only attitude is an imposing one; when she ceases to be majestic, she is ferocious. It is difficult for her to heal the wounds she makes, whereas I can wound and heal together. We are absolutely unlike, and therefore there could not possibly be rivalry between us, unless indeed we quarreled over the greater or lesser perfection of our extremities, which are similar. I take after my father, who is shrewd and subtle. I have the manner of my grandmother and her charming voice, which becomes falsetto when forced, but is a sweet-toned chest voice at the ordinary pitch of a quiet talk.

I feel as if I had left the convent today for the first time. For society, I do not yet exist; I am unknown to it. What a ravishing moment! I still belong only to myself, like a flower just blown, unseen yet by mortal eye.

In spite of this, my sweet, as I paced the drawing-room during my self-inspection, and saw the poor cast-off school-clothes, a queer feeling came over me. Regret for the past, anxiety about the future, fear of society, a long farewell to the pale daisies we used to pick and strip of their petals in light-hearted innocence—there was something of all that; but strange, fantastic visions also rose, which I crushed back into the inner depths, whence they had sprung, and whither I dared not follow them.

My Renee, I have a regular trousseau! It is all beautifully laid away and perfumed in the cedar-wood drawers with lacquered front of my charming dressing-table. There are ribbons, shoes, gloves, all in lavish abundance. My father has kindly presented me with the pretty gewgaws a girl loves—a dressing-case, toilet service, scent-box, fan, sunshade, prayer-book, gold chain, cashmere shawl. He has also promised to give me riding lessons. And I can dance! Tomorrow, yes, tomorrow evening, I come out!

My dress is white muslin, and on my head, I wear a garland of white roses in Greek style. I shall put on my Madonna face; I mean to play the simpleton and have all the women on my side. My mother is miles away from any idea of what I write to you. She believes me quite destitute of mind and would be dumfounded if she read my letter. My brother honors me with profound contempt and is uniformly and politely indifferent.

He is a handsome young fellow, but melancholy and given to moods. I have divined his secret, though neither the Duke nor Duchess has an inkling of it. In spite of his youth and title, he is jealous of his father. He has no position in the State, no post at Court; he never has to say, "I am going to the Chamber." I alone in the house have sixteen hours for meditation. My father is absorbed in public business and his own amusements; my mother, too, is never at leisure; no member of the household practices self-examination; they are constantly in company and have hardly time to live.

I should immensely like to know what potent charm society wields to keep people prisoner from nine every evening until two or three in the morning, forcing them to lavish strength and money. When I longed for it, I had no idea of the separations it brought about or its overmastering spell. But then, I forget, it is Paris that does it all.

It is possible, it seems, for members of one family to live side by side and know absolutely nothing of each other. A half-fledged nun arrives, and in a couple of weeks has grasped domestic details of which the master diplomatist at the head of the house is quite ignorant. Or perhaps he does see and shuts his eyes deliberately, as part of the father's role. There is a mystery here which I must plumb.

Yesterday, at two o'clock, I drove through the Champs-Elysees and the Bois de Boulogne on a beautiful autumn day reminiscent of the Loire. I finally experienced Paris! The Place Louis XV. is impressive, showcasing human artistry.

I was impeccably dressed, pensive, and poised, yet not a single smile was directed my way. The carriage moved slowly, befitting my demeanor. Only one person, a duke—my father—acknowledged me, expressing his satisfaction. My mother also sent me a butterfly kiss. Griffith, ever observant, scanned the surroundings indiscriminately. I believe a young woman should know where to focus her gaze.

I was furious. One man even inspected my carriage without noticing me, likely a carriage-maker. I misjudged my appeal; beauty, it seems, is more common in Paris than I realized. I witnessed hats being tipped to less deserving individuals, while my mother garnered much admiration. I am determined to understand this phenomenon.

The men appeared generally unattractive, with few exceptions being poor imitations of us. Their fashion is uninspired compared to previous generations—lacking elegance, color, and comfort. The hats, resembling truncated columns, do not fit the head well. It seems easier to incite a revolution than to create a stylish hat. Despite their reputation for fickleness, Frenchmen cling to these ridiculous styles.

Overall, the men were unappealing, with worn faces that reflected ambition thwarted and vanity bruised. A fine forehead was a rarity. "And these are the products of Paris!" I remarked to Miss Griffith. "Most cultivated and pleasant men," she replied. I remained silent; a thirty-six-year-old spinster's heart is a well of tolerance.

In the evening, I attended a ball, staying close to my mother, who received all the attention. I was merely a pretext for compliments directed at her. She skillfully found me partners who all commented on the heat and beauty of the ball, as if I were freezing and blind. Each one noted it was their first time seeing me.

My dazzling dress paled in comparison to the extravagant attire of the married women, each surrounded by admirers, watching each other closely. A few shone with beauty, including my mother. At a ball, a girl is merely a dancing machine, insignificant.

The men here did not impress me more than at the Champs-Elysees. They looked exhausted, their features bland and uniform. The proud, vigorous men of our ancestors seem to have vanished. However, one man stood out for his beauty, yet he failed to evoke the expected feelings in me. I did not know his works, and he lacked a noble lineage. No matter his genius, a commoner could never stir my emotions. He seemed more absorbed in himself than in others, suggesting that intellectuals view us as mere objects. When they love, they should abandon their writing; otherwise, their love is not genuine. I sensed this in his demeanor. I learned he is a professor and author, driven by ambition.

I resolved not to quarrel with society for failing to recognize my merits and instead embraced the simple joy of dancing, which I thoroughly enjoyed. I overheard much trivial gossip about unfamiliar people, but perhaps my ignorance prevented me from appreciating it, as most seemed to relish such discussions. Society is a complex web of intrigue, and I am quite perceptive. 

Returning home, I felt pleasantly tired and began sharing my experiences with my mother. She interrupted, advising me to share my thoughts only with her. "My dear child," she said, "it requires as much tact to know when to be silent as when to speak."

Her advice highlighted the nature of feelings that should remain concealed, even from a mother. I realized the vast realm of feminine duplicity. We, in our innocence, would appear as astute gossipers. A mere gesture or glance can convey profound lessons. Suddenly, I felt overwhelming shyness. Must I never again express my joy in dancing? What about deeper emotions?

I went to bed feeling sorrowful, still reeling from this clash between my candid, joyful nature and society's harsh rules. Already, the hedges of my life are flecked with white wool! Farewell, beloved.

How deeply your letter moved me, especially when I reflect on our vastly different paths! You are entering a brilliant world, while I will conclude my modest life in a peaceful retreat.

In the Castle of Maucombe, my room remains as I left it, now with a splendid view of the Gemenos valley, which I once viewed without understanding. Shortly after my arrival, my parents took me to dine with M. de l'Estorade, a wealthy old gentleman who has prospered through frugality.

M. de l'Estorade lost his only son to Bonaparte's conscription in 1813. After evading the draft, the son joined the Emperor's bodyguard but was never heard from again after the Battle of Leipzig. His father, the Baron, clung to hope, believing his son was alive, and hoarded his income for him. Eventually, I realized that the son's unexpected return was the reason for my own. Who could have imagined that my future husband was making his way back from Russia, Poland, and Germany? His luck changed in Berlin, where the French Minister facilitated his return. The Baron, a small landowner in Provence with an income of about ten thousand livres, has not gained enough fame to draw attention to his son’s adventures.

The combined income from the late Mme. de l'Estorade's estate and the father's savings amounts to around two hundred and fifty thousand livres, excluding property—quite a fortune in Provence. The Baron recently purchased a poorly managed estate, intending to plant ten thousand mulberry trees. Now that he has found his son, his sole desire is to marry him off to a girl of good family.

My parents supported this idea once they learned that Renee de Maucombe would be acceptable without a dowry, and that her future inheritance would be recognized in the marriage contract. Similarly, my younger brother, Jean de Maucombe, signed a document acknowledging an advance on his inheritance. This is how the nobles of Provence navigate the restrictive Civil Code of Bonaparte, which drives many girls into convents rather than finding husbands.

The dinner was a first meeting between your sweetheart and the exile. My father-in-law-to-be has no castle, just a simple country house at the entrance of a beautiful valley. The house is unpretentious, with four pebble walls and a roof of red tiles. The garden is a typical Provencal one, enclosed by low walls of round pebbles, showing signs of neglect.

Despite its simplicity, the house of l'Estorade made an effort for the dinner, serving us on old silver dishes. The exile, my dear, is like the railing—emaciated and pale, bearing signs of suffering. At thirty-seven, he looks much older, with streaks of white in his once-beautiful hair. He is a little deaf, reminiscent of the Knight of the Sorrowful Countenance.

Despite this, I have graciously agreed to become Mme. de l'Estorade with a dowry of two hundred and fifty thousand livres, on the condition that I can transform the grange into a park. I have requested a water grant from my father to facilitate this. In a month, I will be Mme. de l'Estorade, as I have made a good impression. After enduring Siberia, Louis de l'Estorade seems pleased to marry the fair Renee de Maucombe.

While you prepare to enjoy the vibrant life awaiting you in Paris, I, Renee, have descended from our shared dreams into the mundane realities of a simple life. I am determined to bring joy to this young man, who has known little of youth, having gone directly from his mother’s arms to war and then to the harshness of Siberia.

I will find pleasure in humble country life, aiming to enhance the oasis around my home with fine trees and greenery. I envision a park on the hillside, perhaps with a kiosk from which I can glimpse the Mediterranean. I will cultivate orange and lemon trees and embrace the poetry of nature that surrounds us. My father-in-law and the Chevalier share my religious sentiments.

My life unfolds like a smooth highway, free from the turmoil of another Bonaparte. If I have children, they will be mine to nurture and guide. If you remain true to your destiny, your children will have a devoted protector in me.

Thus, I bid farewell to the romances and adventures we once imagined. My life’s milestones will now revolve around the care of the young Masters de l'Estorade and the mischief they bring. I will embroider their caps and be cherished by a frail man in the Gemenos valley. Perhaps one day, I may visit Marseilles, but the dangers of a provincial life are minimal. We will take an interest in the silkworms whose mulberry leaves we will sell, and we will experience the ups and downs of life in Provence. Quarrels will be unlikely, as M. de l'Estorade has promised to leave household matters to me, and I will ensure he remains committed to this arrangement.

You, dear Louise, will provide the romance in my life. Please share all your adventures, your balls and parties, your attire, and the men you encounter. Your other self will always be with you, listening and feeling the excitement. If only I could enjoy some Parisian fun while you manage the household at La Crampade, the name of our grange. Poor M. de l'Estorade thinks he is marrying one woman—will he discover there are two?

I am rambling now, and since I can only be foolish through you, I should conclude. One kiss on each cheek—my lips remain untouched, as he has only taken my hand. Our propriety is quite amusing, I assure you. I must go now. Goodbye, dear.

P.S. I just opened your third letter. I have about one thousand livres to spend; please buy me pretty things that we cannot find here or even in Marseilles. While you pursue your own affairs, think of the recluse at La Crampade. Remember, neither family has anyone with taste in Paris to make purchases. I will respond to your letter later.

VI. DON FELIPE HENAREZ TO DON FERNAND PARIS, September.

The address of this letter shows you that the head of your house is safe. The massacre of our ancestors in the Court of Lions forced us into a dual identity, but it also gifted us with Arab cunning, which may have preserved my life.

Ferdinand's fear made his acting convincing enough for Valdez to believe him. Without my intervention, the Admiral would have perished. The Liberals still fail to grasp the nature of kingship. I know this Bourbon well; his repeated assurances of protection only heighten my suspicions. A true Spaniard does not need to repeat promises; excessive words often signal deceit.

Valdez departed on an English ship. As soon as I saw the cause of my beloved Spain falter in Andalusia, I instructed my steward to arrange my escape. I sent coral fishers to meet me at a coastal point, and while Ferdinand sought to capture me, I was already in my barony of Macumer, among brigands who defy all authority.

The last Hispano-Moorish family of Granada has found refuge in an African desert, and even a Saracen horse on an estate inherited from Saracens. The brigands, who once feared my authority, now protect me against the King of Spain's vendetta, proud to defend the Duc de Soria, a Henarez—the first to visit them since the Moors ruled.

I had hoped to live off the income from these vast estates, but my stay revealed the harsh truth: Queverdo's reports were accurate. The poor man had twenty-two lives at my disposal but not a single _real_; vast prairies without a house; virgin forests devoid of furniture! A million piastres and a resident master for half a century would be needed to make these lands profitable. I must address this.

The conquered have time to reflect on their plight. Witnessing my noble country reduced to a corpse for monks to exploit brought tears to my eyes; it foretells Spain's grim future.

In Marseilles, I learned of Riego's demise. It struck me painfully that my life would now be a prolonged, unnoticed martyrdom. Is existence worth living when one can neither die for their country nor live for a woman? To love and conquer—this duality is the law inscribed on our sabres and echoed in our palaces. Yet, my heart is steeled; the sabre is broken, the palace in ruins, and the living spring has dried up in the barren sand.

Here is my last will and testament.

Don Fernand, you now understand why I restrained your enthusiasm and urged you to remain loyal to the _rey netto_. As your brother and friend, I implore you to obey; as your master, I command. You will approach the King and request my dignities, property, office, and titles. He may hesitate and scowl, but tell him you are loved by Marie Heredia, and that she can marry none but a Duc de Soria. This will delight the King, as the Heredia fortune has been a barrier to my ruin. Your proposal will seem to deprive me of a final resource, and he will gladly hand over my spoils.

You will then marry Marie. The secret of your mutual love was no secret to me, and I have prepared the old Count for your ascendance. Marie and I were merely fulfilling our familial duties; everything else favors you. You are beautiful, and possess Marie's heart. I am an ill-favored grandee, for whom she harbors an unconfessed aversion. Any reluctance on her part due to my misfortunes, you will soon overcome.

As Duc de Soria, you will not regret my predecessor nor lose a maravedi. I will keep my mother's diamonds, which will suffice for my independence, as the gap they leave can be filled by Marie's jewels. You can send them with my nurse, old Urraca, the only servant I wish to retain. No one else can prepare my chocolate as she does.

During our brief revolution, my life of toil was reduced to the bare necessities, which my salary sufficed to cover. You will find the income from the last two years with your steward. This sum is mine, but a Duc de Soria cannot marry without significant expenses, so we will divide it. You will not refuse this wedding gift from your brigand brother; I intend it so.

The barony of Macumer, not being Spanish territory, remains mine. Thus, I still have a country and a name should I wish to re-enter the world.

Thank Heaven, this concludes our business, and the house of Soria is saved!

Just as I resign myself to being merely Baron de Macumer, the French cannon announce the arrival of the Duc d'Angouleme. You will understand why I must stop here.

October.

Upon my arrival, I had not ten doubloons. A poor leader would find ways to enrich himself amid calamities he could not avert. For the vanquished Moor, there remains a horse and the desert; for the Christian thwarted in his hopes, the cloister and a few gold pieces.

My resignation is mere weariness. I am not yet ready to abandon all thoughts of life. Ozalga provided me with letters of introduction, including one to a bookseller who serves our compatriots as Galignani does the English in Paris. He has found me eight pupils at three francs a lesson. I teach every other day, earning twelve francs, more than I need. When Urraca arrives, I will pass on my connection to another Spanish exile.

I lodge in the Rue Hillerin-Bertin with a poor widow who takes boarders. My room faces south, overlooking a small garden. It is quiet; I have green trees to gaze upon, and I spend one piastre a day. I am amazed at the calm pleasure I find in this life, reminiscent of Dionysius at Corinth. From sunrise until ten, I smoke and enjoy my chocolate, contemplating two Spanish plants—a broom rising from a clump of jessamine—gold on a white ground, colors that thrill any scion of the Moors. At ten, I begin lessons until four, returning for dinner, then reading and smoking until bed.

I can endure this life of work, meditation, solitude, and society for a long time. Be happy, Fernand; my abdication has brought no regrets. Five days have passed since I wrote my will; they feel like five centuries. Honor, titles, wealth—these are as if they never existed.

Now that the conventional barriers of respect have fallen, I can open my heart to you, dear boy. Though encased in gravity, my heart is full of tenderness and devotion, which have found no object, not even she who has been my destined bride since childhood. This is the source of my political enthusiasm. Spain has taken the place of a mistress and received my heart's homage. And now Spain, too, is lost! Beggared of all, I can only gaze upon the ruins of my former self and ponder the mysteries of my existence.

Why did life animate this carcass, and when will it depart? Why has that race, renowned for chivalry, infused all its virtues into this last offshoot, if the seed was never to sprout, if no stem was to rise, no flower to scatter its Eastern perfumes? What crime did I commit before birth that I inspire no love? Was I destined to be stranded, a useless hulk on a barren shore? I find in my soul the image of the deserts where my ancestors roamed, illuminated by a scorching sun that shrivels all life. Proud remnant of a fallen race, vain force, love wasted, an old man in the prime of youth, here I shall await death's last grace. Alas! under this murky sky, no spark will rekindle these ashes. Thus, my last words may echo Christ's: _My God, Thou hast forsaken me!_ A cry of agony and terror, the depths of which no mortal has yet dared to explore.

You can now understand, Fernand, the joy I find in living through you and Marie. I will watch you with the pride of a creator satisfied with his work. Love each other well and continue to do so; any discord between you would pain me more than it would you.

Our mother had a premonition that events would one day fulfill her wishes. Perhaps a mother's longing constitutes a pact with God. Was she not one of those mysterious beings who converse with Heaven and return with visions of the future? How often have I read in her forehead's lines that she coveted for Fernand the honors and wealth of Felipe! When I mentioned this to her, she would reply with tears, revealing the wounds of a heart that rightfully belonged to both her sons, yet was given to you alone by an irresistible passion.

Her spirit will joyfully hover above you as you bow at the altar. Mother, do you not have a caress for your Felipe now that he has yielded to your favorite even the girl you regretfully thrust into his arms? What I have done pleases our womenfolk, the dead, and the King; it is God's will. Do not resist, Fernand; obey and remain silent.

_P.S._ Tell Urraca to call me nothing but M. Henarez. Do not mention me to Marie. You must be the sole living soul to know the secrets of the last Christianized Moor, whose veins carry the blood of a great family that began in the desert and is now about to die out in the person of a solitary exile.

Farewell.

VII. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENEE DE MAUCOMBE

What! To be married so soon? This is unheard of. After a month, you become engaged to a stranger about whom you know nothing. He may be deaf—there are many kinds of deafness! He could be sickly, tiresome, insufferable!

Don’t you see, Renee, what they want from you? You are needed to carry on the glorious stock of the l'Estorades, that is all. You will be buried in the provinces. Are these the promises we made each other? Were I you, I would rather set off to the Hyeres islands in a caique, risking capture by an Algerian corsair and sold to the Grand Turk. Then I could become a Sultana and make a stir in the harem while I’m young—yes, and afterwards too!

You are leaving one convent to enter another. I know you; you are a coward, and you will submit to the yoke of family life with docility. But I am here to guide you; you must come to Paris. There, we shall drive men wild and hold court like queens. Your husband, sweetheart, may become a member of the Chamber in three years. I know all about members now, and I will explain it to you. You will work that machine well; you can live in Paris and become what my mother calls a woman of fashion. Oh! you needn’t think I will leave you in your grange!

Monday.

For a fortnight now, my dear, I have been living the life of society; one evening at the Italiens, another at the Grand Opera, and always a ball afterward. Ah! society is a bewitching world. The music of the Opera enchants me; while my soul is immersed in divine pleasure, I am the center of admiration and the focus of all the opera glasses. But a single glance will make the boldest youth drop his eyes.

I have seen charming young men, yet none have stirred me; not one has evoked the emotion I feel when listening to Garcia in his splendid duet with Pellegrini in _Otello_. Heavens! how jealous Rossini must have been to express jealousy so well! What a cry in "Il mio cor si divide!" I’m speaking Greek to you, for you’ve never heard Garcia, but you know how jealous I am!

What a wretched dramatist Shakespeare is! Othello loves glory; he wins battles, gives orders, struts about, while Desdemona sits at home, meekly neglected. Such a sheep deserves slaughter. Let the man I love beware of thinking of anything but loving me!

I prefer the long trials of old-fashioned chivalry. That lout of a young lord, who took offense because his sovereign lady sent him among the lions to fetch her glove, was, in my opinion, very impertinent and foolish. Doubtless, the lady had some exquisite flower of love in reserve for him, which he lost, as he well deserved—the puppy!

But here I am rambling as if I didn’t have great news to share. My father is certainly going to represent our master the King in Madrid. I say _our_ master, for I shall be part of the embassy. My mother wishes to remain here, and my father will take me to have a woman with him.

This may seem simple to you, but there are horrors behind it: in a fortnight, I have probed the house's secrets. My mother would accompany my father to Madrid if he would take M. de Canalis as secretary to the embassy. But the King appoints secretaries; the Duke cannot annoy the King, who hates opposition, nor vex my mother. The wily diplomat believes he has cut the knot by leaving the Duchess here. M. de Canalis, the great poet of the day, cultivates my mother’s society and studies diplomacy with her from three to five. Diplomacy must be a fine subject, for he is as regular as a gambler on the Stock Exchange.

The Duc de Rhetore, our elder brother, solemn, cold, and whimsical, would be extinguished by his father in Madrid, so he remains in Paris. Miss Griffith has discovered that Alphonse is in love with a ballet girl at the Opera. How can one fall in love with legs and pirouettes? We’ve noticed that my brother only attends the theatre when Tullia dances; he applauds her steps and then leaves. Two ballet girls in a family are, I think, more destructive than the plague. My second brother is with his regiment, and I have not yet seen him. Thus, I must act as the Antigone of His Majesty's ambassador. Perhaps I may marry in Spain, and perhaps my father envisions a marriage there without a dowry, like yours with that broken-down guard of honor. My father asked if I would accompany him and offered me the use of his Spanish master.

"Spain, the country for castles in the air!" I exclaimed. "Perhaps you hope it may mean marriages for me!"

He merely honored me with a knowing look. For days, he has amused himself by teasing me at lunch; he watches me, and I dissemble. In this way, I have cruelly played with him as father and ambassador _in petto_. Hadn’t he taken me for a fool? He asked my opinion of this and that young man and of some girls I had met. I replied with inane remarks about their hair color, faces, and figures. My father seemed disappointed by my crassness and inwardly blamed himself for asking.

"Still, father," I added, "don’t suppose I’m saying what I really think: mother made me afraid the other day that I had spoken too frankly about my impressions."

"With your family, you can speak quite freely," my mother replied.

"Very well, then," I continued. "The young men I’ve met so far seem too self-centered to excite interest in others; they are much more taken up with themselves than with their company. They can’t be accused of a lack of candor, at least. They put on a certain expression to talk to us, then drop it, apparently satisfied that we don’t use our eyes. The man as he converses is the lover; silent, he is the husband. The girls, again, are so artificial that it’s impossible to know what they really are, except from their dancing; their figures and movements are not a sham. But what alarms me most in this fashionable society is its brutality. The little incidents that occur when supper is announced give one an idea—comparing small things with great—of what a popular uprising might be. Courtesy is merely a thin veneer over general selfishness. I imagined society to be very different. Women count for little in it; perhaps this is a survival of Bonapartist ideas."

"Armande is coming on extraordinarily," said my mother.

"Mother, did you think I would never get beyond asking to see Mme. de Stael?"

My father smiled and rose from the table.

Saturday.

My dear, I left out one thing. Here is the tidbit I’ve reserved for you. The love we pictured must be extremely well hidden; I have seen no trace of it. True, I have caught quick exchanges of glances in drawing-rooms, but how colorless it all is! Love, as we imagined it—a world of wonders, glorious dreams, charming realities, sorrows that awaken sympathy, smiles that bring sunshine—does not exist. The bewitching words, the constant interchange of happiness, the misery of absence, the joy of the beloved’s presence—where are they? What soil produces these radiant flowers of the soul? Which is wrong? We or the world?

I have seen hundreds of men, young and middle-aged; not one has stirred the least feeling in me. No proof of admiration or devotion on their part, not even a sword drawn in my defense, would have moved me. Love, dear, is so rare that it’s possible to live a lifetime without encountering the being who can make one happy. The thought is chilling; if this being is found too late, what then?

For days, I have trembled at the thought of women’s destinies and begun to understand why so many wear sad faces beneath the flush of social excitement. Marriage is a mere matter of chance. Look at yours. A storm of wild thoughts has swept through my mind. To be loved every day the same, yet with a difference, to be loved as much after ten years of happiness as on the first day—such love demands years. The lover must languish; curiosity must be piqued and satisfied, feelings stirred and reciprocated.

Is there a law for the inner fruits of the heart, as there is for the visible fruits of nature? Can joy be made lasting? In what proportion should love mix tears with pleasures? The cold policy of the convent’s monotonous routine seems the only real life; while wealth, splendor, tears, delights, triumph, joy, and satisfaction of a love equal, shared, and sanctioned appear mere idle visions.

I see no room in this city for the gentle ways of love, for precious walks in shady alleys, the full moon sparkling on the water while the suitor pleads in vain. Rich, young, and beautiful, I have only to love, and love would become my sole occupation, my life; yet in the three months I have come and gone, eager and curious, nothing has appealed to me in the bright, covetous, keen eyes around me. No voice has thrilled me, no glance has brightened my world.

Music alone has filled my soul; it has taken the place of our friendship. Sometimes, at night, I linger by my window, gazing into the garden, summoning the future from the mystery that shrouds it. There are days when, having started for a drive, I get out and walk in the Champs-Elysees, picturing that the man who will awaken my slumbering soul is near, that he will follow and look at me. Instead, I meet only mountebanks, vendors of gingerbread, jugglers, and passers-by hurrying to their business, or lovers trying to escape notice. I am tempted to stop them, asking, "You who are happy, tell me what is love."

But the impulse is repressed, and I return to my carriage, swearing to die an old maid. Love is undoubtedly an incarnation, and how many conditions are needed before it can occur! We are not certain of never quarreling with ourselves; how much less so when there are two? This is a problem only God can solve.

I begin to think I shall return to the convent. If I remain in society, I will do things that will seem foolish, for I cannot reconcile myself to what I see. I am perpetually wounded in my sense of delicacy, my inner principles, or my secret thoughts.

Ah! my mother is the happiest of women, adored by Canalis, her great little man. My love, do you know I sometimes feel a horrible craving to know what goes on between my mother and that young man? Griffith tells me she has gone through all these moods; she has longed to lash out at women whose happiness is evident; she has blackened their characters, torn them to pieces. According to her, virtue consists of burying these savage instincts in one’s heart. But what then of the heart? It becomes a sink for the worst in us.

It is humiliating that no admirer has yet appeared for me. I am a marriageable girl, but I have brothers, a family, relations sensitive about honor. If that is what keeps men away, they are cowards.

The parts of Chimene in the _Cid_ and that of the Cid delight me. What a marvelous play! Well, goodbye.

VIII. THE SAME TO THE SAME January.

Our master is a poor refugee, forced to hide due to his role in the revolution quelled by the Duc d'Angouleme—a triumph that has led to splendid fetes. Though a Liberal and likely a man of the people, he has piqued my interest; I suspect he must have been condemned to death. I make him talk to uncover his secret, but he possesses a truly Castilian taciturnity, proud as if he were Gonsalvo di Cordova, yet angelic in his patience and gentleness. His pride is not irritable like Miss Griffith's; it belongs to his nature, compelling us to civility through his perfect manners and holding us at a distance with the respect he shows.

My father claims there is much nobility in Senor Henarez, whom he humorously calls Don Henarez. A few days ago, I took the liberty of addressing him thus. He raised his eyes, usually cast down, and flashed a look that quite abashed me; he certainly has the most beautiful eyes imaginable. I asked if I had offended him, and he replied in grand, rolling Spanish:

"I am here only to teach you Spanish."

I blushed and felt snubbed. I nearly made a sharp retort, but remembering what our dear mother in God used to say, I instead replied:

"It would be kind to tell me if you have any complaints."

A tremor passed through him, the blood rose in his olive cheeks; he replied with some emotion:

"Religion must have taught you, better than I can, to respect the unhappy. Had I been a _don_ in Spain, and lost everything in Ferdinand VII.'s triumph, your witticism would be unkind; but if I am only a poor language teacher, is it not heartless satire? Neither is worthy of a young lady of rank."

I took his hand, saying:

"In the name of religion, I beg you to pardon me."

He bowed, opened my _Don Quixote_, and sat down.

This little incident disturbed me more than a harvest of compliments, gazes, and pretty speeches on my most successful evening. During the lesson, I watched him closely, which I could do safely, as he never looks at me.

From my observations, I gathered that the tutor, whom we assumed to be forty, is actually a young man under thirty. My governess, to whom I handed him over, remarked on the beauty of his black hair and pearly teeth. His eyes are velvet and fire, but he is plain and insignificant. Though Spaniards are often described as uncleanly, this man is meticulously groomed, with hands whiter than his face. He stoops slightly and has a large, oddly-shaped head. His ugliness, which has a dash of piquancy, is accentuated by smallpox scars. His prominent forehead and shaggy eyebrows give him a harsh, frowning, plaintive look, reminiscent of a sickly child whose life depends on superhuman care, like Sister Marthe. As my father noted, his features are a shrunken version of Cardinal Ximenes. The natural dignity of our tutor's manners seems to disconcert the dear Duke, who dislikes him and is never at ease around him; he cannot bear to be in the presence of superiority.

Once my father knows enough Spanish, we will head for Madrid. When Henarez returned two days after his reprimand, I remarked to show my gratitude:

"I have no doubt you left Spain due to political events. If my father is sent there, as seems expected, we might be able to help you and obtain your pardon, should you be under sentence."

"It is impossible for anyone to help me," he replied.

"But," I said, "is that because you refuse help, or because the situation itself is impossible?"

"Both," he said, bowing, in a tone that forbade further discussion.

My father's blood boiled in my veins. I was offended by this haughty demeanor and promptly dropped Senor Henarez.

Yet, there is something admirable in this rejection of aid. "He would not accept even our friendship," I reflected while conjugating a verb. Suddenly, I stopped and told him what was on my mind, but in Spanish. Henarez replied politely that equality of sentiment was necessary between friends, which did not exist in this case, making it pointless to consider the matter.

"Do you mean equality in the amount of feeling on either side, or equality in rank?" I pressed, determined to provoke him from his gravity.

He raised those awe-inspiring eyes again, and mine fell before them. Dear, this man is a hopeless enigma. He seemed to ask whether my words implied love; the mixture of joy, pride, and agonized doubt in his glance touched my heart. It was clear that advances, which would be taken lightly in France, might lead to complications with a Spaniard, and I retreated into my shell, feeling rather foolish.

The lesson concluded, he bowed, and his eyes expressed a humble prayer: "Don't trifle with a poor wretch."

This sudden contrast to his usual grave and dignified demeanor made a significant impression on me. It seems horrible to think and say, but I can’t help believing that there are treasures of affection in that man.

All is over, my dear child, and it is Mme. de l'Estorade who writes to you. But between us, there is no change; it is only a girl the less.

Don't be troubled; I did not give my consent recklessly. My life is now mapped out, and the certainty of my path suits my disposition. A great moral power has removed chance from my life. We have property to develop and a home to beautify; I also have a household to manage and a husband to reconcile to life. I will likely have children to care for and educate.

Everyday life cannot be cast in heroic mould. While it may seem there is no room for a longing after the infinite, I can still explore that boundless sea in our familiar craft. My humble duties do allow for passion; restoring faith in happiness to those who have suffered is a noble work that can relieve the monotony of my existence. I see no room for suffering and much good to be done. My love for Louis de l'Estorade does not make my heart race, but there is nothing in him that offends me.

You may wonder what I will do with my instincts for greatness and the mental energies that connect us. This thought has troubled me, but these faculties remain ours, even if concealed and used in secret for the welfare of our family. The time for a woman to seek admiration is short; if my life has not been grand, it will at least be calm and free from shocks.

Nature has favored our sex with a choice between love and motherhood. I have made mine. My children will be my gods, and this spot of earth my Eldorado.

I can say no more today. Thank you for all you have sent me. Please look at my needs on the enclosed list. I am determined to live in an atmosphere of refinement and luxury, taking from provincial life only what adds to its charm. In solitude, a woman can never be vulgarized—she remains herself. I count on your kindness to keep me fashionable. My father-in-law is delighted and refuses me nothing, turning his house upside down. We are bringing in workers from Paris to renovate everything.

X. MLLE. DE CHAULIEU TO MME. DE L'ESTORADE January.

Oh, Renee, you have made me miserable for days! Your enchanting beauty, proud features, and graceful manner, combined with your rich soul and exquisite heart, are destined to be wasted in a dull marriage, leading to a life you will come to despise. I loathe your future children before they are even conceived; they will be monsters!

You seem to know what lies ahead, with nothing left to hope or fear. But what if a glorious morning arrives, bringing you face to face with the man who could awaken you from this slumber? The thought sends a chill through me!

At least you have a friend. You will become the guardian angel of your valley, familiarizing yourself with its beauty until it becomes part of you. As you walk with your husband and children, I can predict what you will write to me. Your valley, hills, and meadows will remind you of the monotony of your soul's life. But I will be there, a heart untouched by social pettiness, all your own.

Monday.

My dear, my Spaniard is adorably melancholic, with a calm, severe dignity that intrigues me. His solemnity and silence provoke my curiosity. How odd that a language teacher captivates me more than any nobleman or officer I’ve met.

His coldness challenges me. He maintains a distance, wrapped in pride and mystery, while I am bold. This situation amuses me, as it is trivial. What is a man, a Spaniard, a teacher to me? I regard no man as significant, not even a king. I would have made a slave of Napoleon!

Yesterday, I aimed a remark at M. Henarez that must have struck him deeply. He did not respond, and the lesson ended with a bow that suggested he would not return. This suits me; I would not want to start a new _Nouvelle Heloise_. I recently read Rousseau's work and found it distasteful. Passion that can argue and moralize seems detestable.

Clarissa is too pleased with herself and her long letter, but my father tells me Richardson's work is an admirable portrayal of English women. Rousseau's seems more like a philosophical sermon in letter form.

Love, as I see it, is a subjective poem. Everything written about it is both false and true. Since you will be an authority on marital love, I feel it is my duty to remain unmarried and pursue a grand passion, enriching our shared experience.

Tell me every detail of your first days with that strange creature called a husband. I promise to do the same if I ever find love.

Farewell, poor martyred darling.

XI. MME. DE L'ESTORADE TO MLLE. DE CHAULIEU La Crampade.

Your Spaniard and you make me shudder, my darling. I beg you to dismiss him. Everything you say about him aligns with the character of dangerous adventurers who risk everything. He cannot be your husband or lover. I will write more about my married life when my heart is free from the anxiety your last letter has caused.

XII. MLLE. DE CHAULIEU TO MME. DE L'ESTORADE February.

This morning, my father visited me. He looked more thoughtful than usual and gestured for me to sit. I sensed he wanted something.

He began by flattering me, admitting he had misjudged my intelligence. He praised my clear-sightedness and understanding, saying I was the only one capable of grasping his political concerns. He explained that France is in a critical position, understood only by the King and a few others. The King is a head without arms, and the nobles lack authority over the elected men who refuse to cooperate.

He described the Revolution as still active, embedded in law and thought, and warned that the family has ceased to exist in France. We are left with individuals, and the State treasury has replaced the family spirit. He argued that a country cannot be stable without a foundation of paternal authority.

He urged me to consider the importance of family in establishing a strong state, warning of the calamities that would ensue if our party fails. I interrupted, asking how I could help the State, as I felt no vocation for martyrdom.

He called me a little hussy, and we shared a laugh. He asked for my help in persuading me to sacrifice my inheritance for my younger brother's estate. I questioned whether I could still enjoy life if I gave up my fortune.

He insisted that my future husband would not take me without a dowry, and I suggested that perhaps an older peer might be an option. He laughed, and we parted on good terms.

I received your letter this morning, which made me reflect on the abyss you warned me about. I took precautions regarding Henarez, whose gaze unsettles me. I have hesitated to tell my father I want no more lessons, but I find myself drawn to him.

His voice is sweetly thrilling, and his manners are simple and genuine. When he left, he almost reached for my hand to kiss it but stopped, seemingly terrified. I smiled, understanding the courage it takes for a plebeian to love a noblewoman.

My smile encouraged him, and I handed him his hat with gravity. His eyes were wet with unshed tears, and in that moment, I felt a strange happiness. I cannot explain it, but he has fascinated me.

Friday.

He is truly handsome and speaks admirably, with remarkable intellectual power. He has revealed a new world of thoughts to me. He shared a story about a valet who died for a queen's glance, and I was captivated.

At a ball, I learned from M. de Vandenesse that Henarez is of noble descent, the Duke de Soria, whose titles were confiscated by King Ferdinand. This information has left me in suspense until our next lesson.

During our lesson, I confronted him, suspecting he was the Duke de Soria. He denied it, saying he was merely the servant of the King of Spain. His reticence provoked me, and I devised a practical joke to gauge his bloodline by having him translate a letter I wrote describing my ideal lover.

He was visibly moved by my words and asked to keep the letter as a memory. He left, asking to speak with my father, and I learned from him that Henarez is now the Baron de Macumer, with a property in Sardinia.

It has been two weeks since I last saw him, and I wonder what he is doing. I feel he has become necessary to me, and I am torn between love and the reality of my situation. 

Sunday.

At the Italian Opera, I felt his gaze upon me, and I was drawn to his fiery eyes. He has a genius for love. 

XIII. MME. DE L'ESTORADE TO MLLE. DE CHAULIEU LA CRAMPADE, February.

My dear Louise, I waited to write until I learned many things for your happiness. The difference between a girl and a married woman is vast; the girl cannot comprehend it, nor can the married woman return to girlhood.

I chose to marry Louis de l'Estorade rather than return to the convent. The seriousness of marriage filled me with terror, but I realized that friendship might be the key to a happy marriage. I accepted his love, believing it would not be a slavery but a reign.

We agreed to separate marriage from marital rights, allowing us to maintain independence. I watched Louis closely, wondering if suffering had softened or hardened him. I concluded that his love was a passion, and I could venture anything.

I proposed a friendship that would allow us to bear life's burdens together. I asked him to leave me free, promising to consider his feelings. He was overjoyed, and we married soon after.

I am nearly happy now, but I must conceal my happiness from him, as he is sensitive. For three months, we lived as before, and I studied our relationship closely. I saw how my presence transformed him, and I became accustomed to him.

I realized I could bring new life to my husband before bearing him children. His happiness has brightened our home, and even my father has become generous. I manage our household with economy, aiming for pleasure without excess.

I have ambitious plans for Louis, hoping to see him in politics. He has embraced my plans, showing the depth of his affection. 

To conclude, here is a letter he wrote me, expressing his love and admiration. He sees me as a treasure, and I feel a sense of pride in our relationship. 

XIV. THE DUC DE SORIA TO THE BARON DE MACUMER MADRID.

My dear brother, you did not make me Duc de Soria to betray the name. I cannot tolerate my happiness knowing you are in exile. Marie and I will not marry until you accept the money Urraca will give you.

We have prayed for your happiness, and I believe Heaven will send you the love you seek. I have taken Marie, and I will always be grateful to you for that.

You can write to us with confidence through the Nuncio. Farewell, dear brother. Be proud of the happiness you have brought us.

XV. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO MME. DE L'ESTORADE March.

Ah, my love, marriage is making a philosopher of you! Your views on life and duty are troubling. Do you think you can convert me to matrimony with your cautious arithmetic?

We left Blois innocent, armed with the weapons of meditation, and now those weapons have turned against you! If I did not know you were pure, I would think your calculations were vice. 

You have made a holocaust of your youth, becoming a miser before your time. If Louis loves you, he will never realize you are acting out of necessity. 

Love is the first principle of all virtues, and I cannot believe you are justifying your position as a married woman. You are acting a man's part, and Louis will have to play the woman!

Your letter has plunged me into thought. I see now that the convent cannot replace a mother. Please, do not turn away from my cries! 

Monday.

Last night, I looked at the stars and saw my Spaniard in a tree, lost in admiration of my window. I was overwhelmed with joy and fear. 

He blew a letter to me through the window, but I burned it without reading it. I felt it was not fitting for me to read it. 

At the opera, he was there, but I felt no agitation. He looked sad, and I learned he had performed a noble action by giving up a rich heiress for another man. 

I was wretched for burning his letter. What would he have said? To be loved in secret, to know there is a man devoted to me—how can I resist? 

When I returned home, I opened my window, hoping he would be there. He was, and I received another letter, which I read and cherished. 

XVI. THE SAME TO THE SAME March.

I am dressed in white, with camellias in my hair. I have a strange longing to delay my decision and make him wait.

What a vision of beauty! I drove my barouche, and when I saw him, I felt a thrill. I complimented him on his horse, and he shared that it was a gift from his steward.

Henarez is no longer a simple man; he is becoming more distinguished. 

Oh, my dear, do my love affairs amuse you? I am a little off my head and must stop.

XVII. MME. DE L'ESTORADE TO LOUISE DE CHAULIEU April.

My angel, you have grieved me. You have not realized the force of the word _indissoluble_ in marriage. 

Each marriage has its own laws, differing by place and character. A wife of a leader must resign herself to his guidance, while a wife of a fool must take control.

You speak of vice, but passion can be admirable when it springs from the heart. Sooner or later, you will understand that we are answerable only to God and ourselves for our happiness.

You will find that the calculation of a wife is necessary to maintain happiness. I have reflected on the duties of a wife and mother, and I believe that a sublime hypocrisy is required to attain the ideal of a perfect woman.

I persist in my principles, believing that virtue varies with surroundings. If I wished to make Louis unhappy, I would leave the helm in his hands. 

I may not have married a man of distinction, but I hope to help him on that path. Five years from now, we will see if I was mistaken.

You have taught me what a woman sacrifices in marriage. Regret is not remorse, but I feel the loss of freedom. 

You say marriage has made you a philosopher, but I feel far from it. I have read Bonald, who revealed the strength of the family. 

Farewell, my dear friend.

Well, my Renee, you are a wonderful woman, and I now agree that we can only be virtuous by cheating. Will that satisfy you? The man who loves us is our property; we can make a fool or a genius of him as we please, though the former is more common. You will make yours a genius and keep the secret—two heroic actions, if you will!

Ah! If there were no afterlife, how nicely you would be sold, for this is the martyrdom you are choosing. You want to make him ambitious and keep him in love! Child, surely the latter alone is sufficient.

Tell me, to what extent is calculation a virtue, or is virtue calculation? You won’t say? Well, we won’t quarrel over that, since we have Bonald to refer to. We are, and intend to remain, virtuous; yet at this moment, I believe that you, with all your little knavery, are a better woman than I.

Yes, I am shockingly deceitful. I love Felipe and conceal it from him with hypocrisy. I long to see him leap from his tree to my balcony—and if he did, how I would wither him with my scorn! I am frank enough with you.

What restrains me? Where is the mysterious power that prevents me from telling Felipe how supremely happy he has made me with his pure, absolute, and overflowing love?

Mme. de Mirbel is painting my portrait, which I intend to give him. What surprises me more every day is the animation love brings to life. Every hour, every action, every trifle is full of interest, creating confusion between the past, present, and future. Is it still so after reaching the heights of happiness? Oh! Tell me, what is happiness? Does it soothe or excite? I am restless; I feel lost, dragged to him despite reason and propriety. At least I can better understand your feelings.

Felipe's happiness lies in feeling himself mine; his aloofness and strict obedience irritate me, just as his profound respect did when he was my Spanish master. I want to cry out to him, "Fool, if you love me as a picture, what will it be when you know the real me?"

Oh! Renee, you burn my letters, don’t you? I will burn yours. If anyone else were to read these thoughts, I would send Felipe to silence them, perhaps even kill the owners for security.

Monday.

Oh! Renee, how can one fathom the heart of man? My father should introduce me to M. Bonald, since he is so learned; I would ask him. I envy God, who can read the heart's undercurrents.

Does he still worship? That is the question.

If I ever detect the slightest fall in the respect he used to show me as my instructor, I feel I would have the strength to end it. "Why these fine words, these grand resolutions?" you will say. Dear, I will tell you.

My charming father, who treats me with the devotion of an Italian _cavaliere servente_, had my portrait painted by Mme. de Mirbel. I managed to get a copy made for the Duke and sent the original to Felipe. I included these lines:

"Don Felipe, your single-hearted devotion is met by a blind confidence. Time will show whether this is to treat a man as more than human."

It was a big reward, suggesting both a promise and a challenge, which I intended without fully committing. If his reply includes "Dear Louise!" or even "Louise," he is done for!

Tuesday.

No, he is not done for. The constitutional minister is perfect as a lover. Here is his letter:

"Every moment away from you has been filled with ideal pictures of you, my eyes closed to the outside world, fixed on your image. Henceforth my gaze will rest upon this wondrous ivory—this talisman—since your blue eyes sparkle with life as I look, and paint passes into flesh and blood. If I delayed writing, it was because I could not tear myself away from your presence, which wrung from me all that I was bound to keep secret.

"Yes, closeted with you all last night and today, I have, for the first time, given myself up to complete happiness. Could you see yourself where I have placed you, between the Virgin and God, you might understand the agony of my night. But I would not offend you by speaking of it; for one glance from your eyes, robbed of the tender sweetness which is my life, would be full of torture for me. Queen of my life and soul, oh! that you could grant me but a fraction of the love I bear you!

"This was the burden of my prayer; doubt worked havoc in my soul as I oscillated between belief and despair. A criminal awaiting his verdict is not more racked with suspense than I, as I own my temerity. The smile on your lips, which alone can calm the storm of my dread of displeasing you, haunts me. From my birth, no one, not even my mother, has smiled on me. The beautiful girl designed for me rejected my heart for my brother. In politics, all my efforts have been thwarted. In the eyes of my king, I have read only thirst for vengeance; he has been my enemy since childhood, and the vote of the Cortes that placed me in power was seen as a personal insult.

"Less than this might breed despondency in the stoutest heart. I have no illusions; I know the gracelessness of my person and how difficult it is to do justice to the heart within this rugged shell. To be loved had ceased to be more than a dream when I met you. Thus, when I bound myself to your service, I knew that devotion alone could excuse my passion.

"But as I look upon this portrait and listen to your smile that whispers of rapture, rays of hope pierce the gloom, only to be obscured by rising mists of doubt and fear of your displeasure. It is impossible you should love me yet—I feel it; but in time, as you see the strength, constancy, and depth of my affection, you may yield me some foothold in your heart. If my daring offends you, tell me without anger, and I will return to my former part. But if you consent to try and love me, be merciful and break it gently to one who has placed his happiness in the thought of serving you."

My dear, as I read these last words, he seemed to rise before me, pale as the night when the camellias told their story and he knew his offering was accepted. These words, in their humility, were different from the usual flowery rhetoric of lovers, and a wave of feeling washed over me; it was the breath of happiness.

The weather has been atrocious; impossible to go to the Bois without raising suspicions. Even my mother, who often goes out regardless of rain, remains at home.

Wednesday evening.

I have just seen him at the Opera; he is another man. He came to our box, introduced by the Sardinian ambassador.

Having read in my eyes that this audacity was taken well, he seemed awkwardly conscious of his limbs and addressed the Marquise d'Espard as "mademoiselle." A light brighter than the chandeliers flashed from his eyes. He left with the air of a man unsure of his next move.

"The Baron de Macumer is in love!" exclaimed Mme. de Maufrigneuse.

"Strange, isn’t it, for a fallen minister?" replied my mother.

I had enough presence of mind to regard Mmes. de Maufrigneuse and d'Espard and my mother with curiosity, as if they were speaking a foreign language, but inwardly my soul sank in waves of intoxicating joy. There is only one word to express what I felt: rapture. Such love as Felipe's makes him worthy of mine. I am the very breath of his life; my hands guide his thoughts. I have a mad longing to see him clear every obstacle and stand before me, boldly asking for my hand. Then I would know whether this storm of love would calm at a glance from me.

Ah! my dear, I stopped here, and I am still trembling. As I wrote, I heard a noise outside and rose to see what it was. From my window, I saw him coming along the wall at the risk of his life. I went to the bedroom window and made him a sign; it was enough. He leaped from the wall—ten feet—and then ran along the road, showing me he was not hurt. That he thought of my fear at that moment moved me so much that I am still crying; I don’t know why. Poor ungainly man! What was he coming for? What had he to say to me?

I dare not write my thoughts and will go to bed joyful, thinking of all we would say if we were together. Farewell, fair silent one. I have no time to scold you for not writing, but it has been over a month since I heard from you! Does this mean you are finally happy? Have you lost the "complete independence" you were so proud of, which nearly played me false tonight?

At Briare, we had breakfast in the carriage, followed by a conversation reminiscent of our old talks at Blois, admiring the Loire. By half-past seven, we entered the beautiful avenue leading to Chantepleurs. We dined at eight and retired to our charming Gothic bedroom, which was comfortable with modern luxuries. Felipe, often thought ugly, appeared beautiful to me in his kindness. Throughout the journey, he felt like an old friend, though he later described the storms of passion he concealed beneath a calm exterior.

"So far, I have found nothing terrible in marriage," I said, gazing out at the moonlit park.

"Why fear it?" he replied. "Have I ever proved false to my promise? Why should I in the future?"

His words and glances were full of mastery, thrilling my heart. I mused on the nature of duty and the constraints placed on a woman's delicate feelings. What malevolent force has sought to crush a woman's sensitivity under the weight of obligation? Love should guard its own rights, blooming freely under its warmth.

Oh, my noble Renee! I understand you now. The woman who has not used marriage merely to legalize her heart's choice has only motherhood left to turn to. When earthly ties fail, the soul seeks the divine.

A hard truth emerges: only truly great men know how to love. They balance sensual and spiritual forces, while weaker men confuse the two. Great souls express natural instincts with grace, and their passion is tempered by reverence for women. The depth of feeling corresponds to the caliber of one's intellect, which is why only men of genius possess a woman's delicacy. When mind, heart, and senses unite in rapture, we soar to heaven, albeit briefly.

This is the philosophy of my first three months of marriage. Felipe is angelic, another self, and I can think aloud with him. His greatness surpasses my understanding, and our bond deepens with possession. I foresee that years of marriage will only strengthen his affection and enhance our connection. What joy!

Pleasure invigorates me, leaving behind sunshine that becomes part of my being. The intervals between ecstasies are like the brief nights separating long summer days. What happy chance has given me such a destiny? My mother's fears have proven unfounded; your fears and mine have vanished.

We spent seven and a half months at Chantepleurs, like runaway lovers in a blissful solitude. One morning, in a particularly happy moment, I reflected on my life and thought of your prosaic marriage. I grasped the meaning of your life. Oh, my sweet, why do we speak different languages? Your marriage of convenience and my love match are worlds apart. You walk the earth, while I soar in the heavens! Love crowned me queen, while you reign by duty. My heights are so lofty that a fall would shatter me.

But I won't dwell on this. I hesitate to describe the vibrant joy of love's springtime as we know it.

For ten days, we have been in Paris, staying in a lovely house in the Rue du Bac, prepared by the architect who decorated Chantepleurs. I have been listening to Rossini's divine music, which once soothed my restless heart. They say I am more beautiful now, and I take childish pleasure in being called "Madame."

Friday morning.

Renee, my dear, my happiness draws me back to you. I feel I can be more to you than ever before. I have studied your married life alongside my own, and you emerge as great, noble, and splendid in your goodness. I declare myself your humble admirer and friend. My marriage has been a joy; I would have perished had it been otherwise. And you live! What feeds your heart? I will never mock you again. Mockery stems from ignorance; we jest at what we do not understand. "Recruits laugh where veterans are grave," said the Comte de Chaulieu, whose experience has been limited to marches from Paris to Fontainebleau.

I suspect you have not shared everything. You hide wounds, and I believe you suffer. From afar, I have woven romantic theories about you. "She has merely experimented with marriage," I thought, "and what is happiness for me has brought her only pain." Your sacrifice seems devoid of reward, and you would not make it greater than necessary. The axioms of social morality cloak your disappointment. If self-denial is noble, what of my joy, sheltered by the church's canopy and witnessed by the mayor's seal? Is it unnatural?

For the honor of the law and to complete my happiness, I long to see you content. Oh, tell me you see a dawn of love for Louis, who adores you! I return to this midday blaze, fearing it may consume me.

Dear Renee, do you remember how you once said, "I love you so, Louise, that if God appeared to me, I would pray for all your sorrows to be mine"? Now, I take up your prayer, imploring Heaven to grant you a share in my happiness.

I have an idea. I suspect you are nurturing ambitious plans for Louis de l'Estorade. Very well; help him get elected deputy in the upcoming election. He will be nearly forty then, and the Chamber won't meet for six months, so he will qualify for a seat. You will come to Paris—wouldn't that be enough? My father and my future friends will come to know and admire you. If your father-in-law agrees to start a family, we could secure the title of Comte for Louis. That is something! And we shall be together.

My doctor believes that while chance governs childbirth, children born of passionate love tend to be more fortunate. Perhaps motherhood holds joys for you that I may never experience, as your love for Felipe is different from my sense of duty and obligation.

These thoughts weigh on me as I anticipate the joys of family life. Currently, I live in a state of expectation, accompanied by physical discomfort. I experience nausea and strange cravings, including an inexplicable desire for rotten fruit. While Louis brings home the finest oranges, I seek out the half-rotten ones, finding them delicious despite their smell. This craving, though odd, has alleviated my sickness and made me feel stronger, suggesting a natural process common among women.

As my condition progresses, I plan to stay within the grounds to avoid being seen. I am curious about when the sense of motherhood truly begins, hoping it won’t coincide with suffering.

This concern stems from my deep affection for you. I trust you and Felipe attend church and do good deeds together. My fears may seem primitive, but they arise from a friendship that cares deeply. You deserve happiness, and I cherish the bond we share, even in my uneventful life. God bless you!

XXX. LOUISE DE MACUMER TO RENEE DE L'ESTORADE January 1826.

Macumer just woke me with your husband's letter. Yes, we will be going to Chantepleurs at the end of April. I am excited to travel, see you, and be the godmother of your first child. I must have Macumer as godfather; sharing this role with another would be unbearable. If you could see the look he gave me, you would understand how much he cherishes me!

I am eager for our visit to La Crampade, as I might have a child there too. Can you imagine me torn between you and the infant? If I saw anything taking my place in your heart, I couldn't guarantee my reaction. Medea may have had a point! 

So, my dear, you have the fruit without the flowers; I have the flowers without the fruit. Our lives contrast, but we have enough philosophy to find meaning in it someday. Ten months married is too soon to lose hope.

We are living a joyful life, as happy people do. Society now sees the Baronne de Macumer as prettier than Louise de Chaulieu; love is a wonderful cosmetic. Driving along the Champs-Elysees on a crisp January day, I reflect on how far we've come since last year.

If I lack a mother's joys, you can share them with me; I will learn through sympathy. Yet, I cannot imagine anything surpassing the rapture of love. You may laugh at my thoughts, but I often wish to die at thirty, leaving life untainted by disappointment, having lived fully in love. To carry a young heart in an aged body, surrounded by cold faces, would be a torment.

Felipe and I had our first quarrel over this. I insisted he should have the strength to end my life in my sleep at thirty, so I could transition from one dream to another. He refused, turning pale at the thought. My dear, this distinguished statesman is surprisingly childlike. Now that I share my thoughts with him, we continually surprise each other.

Felipe and I want to send a gift to you, dear mother. We wish to choose something that will bring you joy and remind you of us. Please tell me what you would like. I thought of a special luncheon service adorned with baby figures, as lunch is our happiest meal. Let me know quickly, as we need to order it soon.

Farewell, dear nursing mother. May you enjoy all the delights of motherhood! I eagerly await your first letter detailing your experiences. Some parts of your husband's letter touched my heart. I will tell my godson how dearly he must love you. Much love, my sweet one.

XXXI. RENEE DE L'ESTORADE TO LOUISE DE MACUMER

It has been nearly five months since the baby was born, and I have not found a moment to write. When you become a mother, you will understand. Please write more often! Share your joys; I am happier than you can imagine.

I went to the parish church for the Mass of recovery from childbirth, supported by both grandfathers. I knelt before God, overwhelmed with gratitude. I have so much to share, but one memory stands out: my prayer in the church. 

Transformed into a joyful mother, I felt the Virgin on the altar bowing her head, pointing to the infant Christ, who smiled at me. With a heart full of love, I held little Armand for the priest to bless, anticipating his baptism later.

My child—how sweet that word is! In the last two months, I dragged myself through the gardens, unaware of the precious burden I carried. I was haunted by gloomy forebodings, overshadowing my curiosity about motherhood. My heart did not respond to the thought of the little one, who announced himself with lively kicks. 

When the moment came, I summoned all my strength and braced myself for suffering. I bore the agony marvelously, sinking into a stupor. I felt like two beings—my body in pain and my soul at peace. The pain formed a halo around me, and when the climax came, I screamed, finding strength against the torture. Suddenly, the cries of the newborn silenced my pain.

They laid me back in bed, and it felt like paradise, despite my exhaustion. Happy faces pointed to the child, and I exclaimed in terror, "It looks like a little monkey! Are you sure it's a child?" 

My mother, now the nurse, reassured me, "You've got the finest baby in the world. Don't worry; focus on being a mother." I fell asleep, resolved to let nature take its course.

Waking from the pain of the first days was heavenly. A ray of light pierced the darkness, and my heart and soul emerged from suffering as a flower blooms in the sun. The moment the little one latched onto my breast was pure joy—this is the dawn of motherhood!

Here is happiness, though it comes with pangs. Oh, my sweet jealous soul, you will relish this delight that exists only for us, the child, and God! For this tiny creature, all knowledge is in its mother's breast. Its thoughts revolve around this source of life, and its lips bring a sweetness beyond words. 

Nursing is a miracle of transformation. The cries go to your heart, and the child's eyes and lips speak volumes. What else matters? The father? You could kill him if he disturbed the baby! The bond between mother and child is profound, and the joy of nurturing is unparalleled.

My little one has transformed into the prettiest darling in five months. He cries little now, aware of the love surrounding him. I strive to envelop him in my affection.

Dear, my feelings for Louis have evolved into a tender fondness, perhaps even superior to love. This unselfish gratitude reflects the joy of motherhood, lighting up the future and recalling fond memories of the past.

The old l'Estorade and his son have redoubled their devotion to me; I feel like a new person. Each time they see me, it brings them joy, which touches me deeply. Louis is ecstatic about having a son and spends hours watching him sleep. He confided that he feared he would be childless, but now he works harder, driven by fatherhood.

I grow happier every moment. Each hour strengthens the bond between mother and child. My feelings are normal and enduring, unlike love, which can wane. A mother's love grows with the child's needs, encompassing passion, duty, and joy. Here lies a woman's true sphere, where self-sacrifice flourishes without jealousy.

Society aligns with nature in this regard, reinforcing the maternal instinct. A mother cherishes the child who opens up these joys and teaches her the art of motherhood. The right of the eldest child, once part of the natural order, should never have been questioned. A mother learns so much from her child, and her character flourishes in fulfilling life's duties and pleasures. A woman without children is incomplete. 

Hasten, my sweetest, to fulfill your mission. Your happiness will multiply with the wealth of my delights.

23rd.

I had to leave you because my godson was crying. I can hear him from the garden. I wanted to say farewell. I reread my letter and am horrified by how common my feelings seem! Every mother must have felt this way, but I am as happy now as I once was miserable. This place is my land of promise. The desert is past. A thousand loves, darling. Write to me, for I can now read about your happy love without tears. Farewell.

Do you know, dear, that it has been over three months since I last wrote to you? I am more at fault, as I did not respond to your last letter, but surely you don’t mind?

Macumer and I have interpreted your silence as consent regarding the baby-wreathed luncheon service, which is now on its way to Marseilles. It took six months to complete. My sweet, it’s this dreadful Paris—my excuse. What is yours? Society is a whirlpool! Didn’t I tell you that in Paris one must conform? It drives out sentiment, monopolizes your time, and can consume your heart. Moliere’s Celimene is a masterpiece of the society woman, timeless and relevant.

Without my breastplate—the love I have for Felipe—I would be lost. This morning, I told him he is my salvation. My evenings are filled with parties, but at night, my heart heals in the embrace of passionate love.

Macumer is an admirable host—witty, dignified, and courteous. No woman could help but love such a husband.

My parents have gone to Madrid. With Louis XVIII gone, the Duchess easily secured my father’s appointment as attache. My brother, the Duc de Rhetore, acknowledges me now. My younger brother, the Comte de Chaulieu, owes me gratitude for spending my fortune to secure him an estate and a marriage with Mlle. de Mortsauf, an heiress. The King will confer the de Lenoncourt and de Givry titles on him to preserve their legacy. Mlle. de Mortsauf, the sole heiress of the Duc de Lenoncourt-Givry, is expected to inherit over one hundred thousand livres a year. My father stipulated that the de Chaulieu arms appear on the de Lenoncourt escutcheon, making my brother Duc de Lenoncourt. The young de Mortsauf is gravely ill, and the marriage will occur next winter after mourning.

My father’s reasoning has proven correct, winning me compliments and explaining my marriage to society’s satisfaction. The Prince de Talleyrand, out of affection for my grandmother, has become a warm friend to Macumer. Society, which once criticized me, now admires me.

I now reign as a queen where I was once insignificant. Macumer is envied as the husband of "the most charming woman in Paris." Many women vie for that title. Men murmur sweet nothings or gaze longingly. This chorus of admiration is soothing to vanity, and I understand why women pay dearly for such fleeting privileges. This triumph is intoxicating, and I see how women can become selfish and frivolous in the face of adoration. The demands of society consume your spirit, and you give your all for smiles and jealousy, receiving only flattery in return. You know the cost, yet cannot escape the vortex.

Ah, how I crave a true friend! Felipe’s love and devotion are precious to me, and my heart longs for you! We are joyfully preparing to move to Chantepleurs, where we can escape the farce of Parisian society. After rereading your letter, I realize that no fashionable woman in Paris can be a good mother.

Goodbye for now, dear. We will be at Chantepleurs for only a week and will see you around May 10th. We will finally meet again after more than two years! How we have changed! We are both matrons now—one in love, the other in motherhood.

We will have delightful gossip and see if a child truly spoils the pattern.

Dear sister, we were all astonished at lunch when we learned you had left, especially after receiving your letter. You were happy, and there was no justification for your objections. My sentence is that you return at my first summons. Your letter didn’t mention your next stop, so I’m addressing this to Chantepleurs.

Listen, dear sister of my heart. My focus is on your happiness. Your husband commands respect, not only for his gravity but also for the power revealed in his plainness and the fire in his eyes. It took time for me to converse easily with him. He has been Prime Minister and adores you, which makes him a profound dissembler. Extracting secrets from this diplomatic soul requires skill.

You know that my role is reason, while yours is imagination. I am a simple country vicountess, ambitious for my family’s prosperity. Macumer, the Duc de Soria, has a name in the world, and you, a duchess by right, reign in Paris, where it’s hard to maintain power. You have a considerable fortune, which may double if Macumer develops his estates in Sardinia. If anyone has the right to be jealous, it’s not you. Thankfully, we both have generous hearts that place our friendship above such pettiness.

I know you, dear; you must feel ashamed of fleeing. But your flight won’t spare you from my words today. Read carefully, as this concerns you more than Macumer.

Firstly, you do not love him. In two years, you will tire of adoration. You will never see Felipe as a husband; he will always be a lover to you. You do not revere him as a woman should her god. I have studied love, and I can confidently say this is not love.

You cannot escape the fate of all queens. One day, you will long to be treated as a light-o’-love, to be swept off your feet by a strong man who won’t worship you. Macumer loves you too much to resist or criticize you. Sooner or later, you will scorn this excessive devotion. He spoils you, just as I did at the convent, for you are enchanting, and there’s no escaping your charms.

Worse, you are candid, and our happiness often relies on social hypocrisies you won’t embrace. Society won’t tolerate a wife’s power over her husband. The convention is that a man must not show himself as a lover to his wife, nor may a married woman act as a mistress. You both disregard this.

The unpardonable sin in society is to be happy. If happiness exists, it must remain hidden. More importantly, the equality between lovers should never appear in marriage, or it undermines society. It’s painful to see a man made a nonentity, and worse to see a capable man reduced to that. You will reduce Macumer to a shadow of a man, and your household will consist of one imperfect person. You will regret it, but by then, it will be too late. Women lack the qualities essential for family life. Macumer senses this; his visit to Sardinia suggests he hopes to regain his old self.

You use the power his love gives you. Your position is evident in your gestures and tone. You are the wanton your mother called you! Have you ever heard me contradict Louis? I respect his authority in public. If I want to advise him, I wait for private moments, but I never assume the role of mentor. If I didn’t remain a submissive wife publicly, he would lose confidence. The good we do for others is spoiled unless we erase ourselves so they feel no inferiority. There’s sweetness in these hidden sacrifices, and it’s a triumph when you praise Louis.

This is the sum of my observations. Right now, you love Felipe, not as a husband, but as a reflection of yourself. Your father’s words hold truth; beneath your passion lies a selfishness typical of a great lady. How I must love you to speak such bitter truths!

If you promise not to tell the Baron, I’ll share the end of our talk. We praised you, and he soon realized I love you like a sister. I ventured to say, “Louise has never struggled with life. She has been fortune’s spoiled child, and she might pay for this if you weren’t there to act as father and lover.”

“Ah! but is it possible?” he broke off, as if on the edge of a precipice. That exclamation was enough for me. If you had stayed, he would have spoken more freely.

Think of the day when your husband’s strength wanes, when pleasure turns to satiety, and he feels diminished before you. The stings of conscience will awaken remorse, painful for you, as you will feel responsible and end up despising the man you failed to respect. Remember, scorn can lead to hatred. You are too noble to forget Felipe’s sacrifices, but what more can he give when he has already served himself at the banquet? Woe to those who leave no desire unsatisfied! Women are insatiable in love.

Oh, Louise, change while there’s still time. If you treated Macumer as I treat l’Estorade, you could awaken the hero within him. Wouldn’t you feel pride in using your power to elevate a gifted man, as I do with one of lesser stature?

Had you remained, I would have written this anyway, for in conversation, you might have interrupted me. But I know you will read this thoughtfully and weigh my warnings. You have everything to be happy; don’t spoil your chances. Return to Paris as soon as Macumer returns. The demands of society, which I complain about, are necessary for both of you; otherwise, you’ll be lost in self-absorption. A married woman shouldn’t be too generous with herself. A mother should reserve sacred hours for herself; even children shouldn’t take her presence for granted.

Farewell, my dear jealous soul! Many women would be flattered by your pang of jealousy, but I can only mourn, for what isn’t mother in me is your dear friend. A thousand loves. Make whatever excuse you must for leaving; if you’re unsure of Macumer, I am sure of Louis.

XXXIX. THE BARONNE DE MACUMER TO THE VICOMTESSE DE L'ESTORADE

Your dreadful letter has reached me, forwarded by my steward as I requested. Oh, Renee... I will spare you my emotional turmoil and simply share the impact your words had on me.

We had just returned from a delightful reception hosted by the ambassador, where I shone in all my glory, and Macumer was utterly captivated by me. Then I read your harsh response to my letter, sobbing as I did so. My dear Arab fell at my feet, declaring you were mad. He took me to the balcony of our palace, where the moonlight created a magnificent scene. We now speak Italian, and his declarations of love in that beautiful language felt like poetry. He swore that even if your predictions were true, he would not trade a single moment of our love for a lifetime of conventionality. He would choose my love and death over a mundane existence with children.

While he whispered sweet nothings, a bat's cry startled us, and I fainted. But don’t worry! Though that ominous sound lingers in my mind, I am well this morning. After waking, I knelt before Felipe, gazing into his eyes, and confessed: "My love, I may be naive, and Renee might be right. Perhaps I love only your love, but I assure you, this is the only feeling in my heart. If anything about me disturbs you, please tell me. I want to follow your wishes." Macumer was too choked with emotion to respond.

I owe you thanks, Renee. Your letter revealed the depths of Macumer's love. Rome is the city of love, where passion should flourish alongside art and faith.

In Venice, we will meet the Duc and Duchesse de Soria. If you write, address it to Paris, as we leave Rome in three days after the ambassador's farewell party.

P.S. Dear, naive child, your letter shows you know nothing of love except in theory. Love is a force that can yield diverse fruits beyond any theory's grasp.

XL. THE COMTESSE DE L'ESTORADE TO THE BARONNE DE MACUMER January 1827.

My father has been elected to the Chamber, my father-in-law has died, and I am nearing my second confinement; these are the main events marking our year’s end. I mention them first to spare you the fright of the black seal.

Your letter from Rome sent chills down my spine. You and Felipe are like children. Either he is a deceitful diplomat, or his love for you resembles that of a man for a courtesan, lavishing affection while knowing she is unfaithful. Enough of this. You say I rave, so I will hold my tongue. I can only say that from our differing fates, I draw this harsh lesson—do not love if you wish to be loved.

My dear, when Louis was elected to the provincial Council, he received the cross of the Legion of Honor. Nearly three years have passed since then, and my father has asked for the rank of Officer of the Legion for his son-in-law. I would appreciate your help with the relevant bigwig to keep an eye on this matter. But please, do not get entangled in my father's affairs. The Comte de Maucombe is seeking the title of Marquis for himself; reserve your efforts for me. When Louis becomes a deputy next winter, we will come to Paris and work to secure a government position for him, allowing us to live on his salary. My father sits between the center and the right; a title will satisfy him. Our family was distinguished even in the days of King Rene, and Charles X. will likely grant a Maucombe title. My only fear is that my father may seek favors for my younger brother. If the marquisate is dangled before him, he will focus solely on himself.

January 15th.

Ah, Louise, I have been through hell. If I can bear to recount my anguish, it is because you are like a sister to me; even so, I doubt I will ever relive those five ghastly days. The mere word "convulsions" makes my heart sick. Five days felt like five centuries of torture. A mother who has not endured this suffering cannot comprehend it. I even envied you, who have never had a child!

The evening before that dreadful day, the weather was close and almost hot, and I thought my little Armand was affected. Generally sweet and affectionate, he was peevish, crying for no reason, wanting to play, and breaking his toys. I attributed his irritability to teething, as he was cutting four large teeth. I put him to bed beside me, waking frequently through the night. He was slightly feverish, but not enough to alarm me. Towards morning, he cried "Mamma!" and asked for a drink, but his voice was strange, and he was twitching. I jumped out of bed to fetch him a drink. Imagine my horror when, as I handed him the cup, he remained motionless, repeating "Mamma!" in a voice that was hardly a voice at all. His hand did not respond to my pressure; it was stiff. I offered him the cup, and he gulped down water in a convulsive manner that was terrible to witness. He clung to me desperately, and I saw his eyes roll back, revealing only the whites; his limbs turned rigid. I screamed for Louis.

"A doctor! Quick!... he is dying," I cried.

Louis vanished, and my poor Armand gasped, "Mamma! Mamma!" The next moment, he lost consciousness. The veins on his forehead swelled, and the convulsions began. For an hour before the doctors arrived, I held my lifeless baby, my pride and joy, in my arms. His eyes! I cannot think of them without horror. My pretty Armand was a mere shell—black, shriveled, misshapen.

Two doctors, brought from Marseilles by Louis, hovered about like birds of ill omen. One spoke of brain fever, while the other saw only ordinary convulsions. Our country doctor seemed the most sensible, offering no opinion. "It's teething," said the second doctor. "Fever," said the first. They finally agreed to apply leeches to his neck and ice to his head. It felt like death. To see a corpse, all purple or black, without a cry or movement—horrible!

At one point, I lost my composure and laughed hysterically at the sight of the leeches on his pretty neck and his head in an ice cap. We had to cut his lovely curls to make room for the ice. The convulsions returned every ten minutes, and the poor baby writhed, now white, now violet. His limbs clattered like wood. This unconscious flesh was the being who had smiled and prattled, who used to say "Mamma!" The thought sent waves of agony through my soul. My mother, who could have offered help, was in Paris. I believe mothers know more about convulsions than doctors do.

After four days and nights of suspense, the doctors unanimously recommended a dreadful ointment that would create open sores. Sores on my Armand! Just five days before, he had been playing and laughing. I refused the treatment, trusting in nature. Louis, who believed in doctors, scolded me. A man remains unchanged through everything. But in moments when this terrible disease mimics death, I felt that this hateful remedy was Armand's salvation. The skin was so dry and rough that the ointment wouldn’t act. I wept, my tears soaking the bedside. The doctors were at dinner!

Alone with my child, I stripped him of all medical appliances, pressed him to my bosom, and laid my forehead against his, beseeching God to grant him life. For minutes, I held him, longing to die with him so that neither life nor death could part us. Dear, I felt his limbs relax; the writhings ceased, and the ghastly colors faded! I screamed, just as I had when he fell ill; the doctors rushed in, and I pointed to Armand.

"He is saved!" exclaimed the oldest doctor.

What music those words were! Heaven opened! Two hours later, Armand returned to life, but I was utterly crushed, and only the joy of his recovery saved me from serious illness. My God! What tortures bind a mother to her child! Must the nails be driven into the quick to fasten him to my heart? Was I not mother enough before? I, who wept tears of joy over his first words and steps, who spent hours preparing for motherhood? Why these horrors for a mother who already idolized her child?

As I write, our little Armand is playing, shouting, laughing. What causes this terrible disease in children? I ponder it, especially now that I am again with child. Is it teething? A peculiar brain process? Something wrong with the nervous systems of children prone to convulsions? All these thoughts trouble me, considering both the present and the future. Our country doctor believes it is nervous trouble from teething. I would give every tooth in my head to see Armand's through. The sight of one of those little white pearls peeking from swollen gums brings a cold sweat over me. The heroism with which my little angel bore his suffering proves he will be his mother’s son. A look from him pierces my heart.

Medical science offers no satisfactory explanation for this type of tetanus, which appears and disappears rapidly, seemingly unpreventable and uncured. One thing is certain: it is hell for a mother to witness her child in convulsions. How passionately I hold him to my heart! I could walk forever with him in my arms!

To have endured this only six weeks before my confinement made it worse; I feared for the coming child. Farewell, my dear beloved. Do not wish for a child—this is the essence of my letter!

XLI. THE BARONNE DE MACUMER TO THE VICOMTESSE DE L'ESTORADE Paris.

Poor sweet, Macumer and I forgave you all your mischief upon hearing of your terrible trouble. I felt pain as I read about your double agony, and there seem to be compensations in being childless.

I write to inform you that Louis has been promoted. He can now wear the ribbon of an officer of the Legion. You are fortunate, Renee, and you will likely have a little girl, as that was your wish!

My brother's marriage to Mlle. de Mortsauf was celebrated upon our return. Our gracious King, who is extraordinarily kind, has granted my brother the reversion of the post of first gentleman of the chamber, which his father-in-law currently holds, on the condition that the Mortsauf scutcheon is placed alongside the Lenoncourt's.

"The office ought to go with the title," he remarked to the Duc de Lenoncourt-Givry.

My father is justified a hundredfold. Without my fortune, none of this would have happened. My parents came from Madrid for the wedding and will return after the reception I am hosting for the newlyweds.

The carnival will be lively. The Duc and Duchesse de Soria are in Paris, which makes me uneasy. Marie Heredia is one of the most beautiful women in Europe, and I dislike the way Felipe looks at her. Therefore, I am extra sweet and affectionate. Every look and gesture conveys what my lips do not say: "_She_ could not love like this!" Heaven knows how lovely and captivating I am! Yesterday, Mme. de Maufrigneuse said to me:

"Dear child, who can compete with you?"

I keep Felipe well entertained, making his sister-in-law seem dull by comparison. I am less sorry that a little Abencerrage is not on the way, as the Duchess will likely remain in Paris for her confinement and will no longer be a beauty. If the baby is a boy, he will be named Felipe in honor of the exile. Fate has decreed that I will serve as godmother again.

Goodbye, dear. I plan to go to Chantepleurs early this year, as our Italian tour was shockingly expensive. I will leave around the end of March to economize in Nivenais. Besides, I am tired of Paris. Felipe and I long for the beautiful tranquility of the park, our cool meadows, and the sparkling Loire, which is peerless among rivers. Chantepleurs will feel delightful after the pomp of Italy; after all, splendor becomes tiresome, and a lover's gaze is more beautiful than a _capo d'opera_ or a _bel quadro_!

We will expect you there. Do not fear that I will be jealous again. You are free to explore Macumer's heart and uncover all his doubts. I am utterly indifferent. Since that day in Rome, Felipe's love for me has deepened. He told me yesterday (he is looking over my shoulder now) that his sister-in-law, the Princess Heredia, his childhood dream, has no brains. Oh, my dear, I am worse than a ballet dancer! If you knew the joy that slighting remark brought me! I pointed out to Felipe that she does not speak French correctly. She says _esemple_ for _exemple_, _sain_ for _cinq_, _cheu_ for _je_. She is beautiful, of course, but lacks charm and wit. When complimented, she looks as if she doesn’t know what to do with such a strange thing. Felipe, being who he is, could not have lived two months with Marie after marriage. Don Fernand, the Duc de Soria, suits her perfectly. He has generous instincts but is clearly a spoiled child. I am tempted to be mischievous and make you laugh, but I will refrain. Much love, darling.

For the first time, my dear Renee, I find myself alone and crying. I sat under a willow by the Chantepleurs marsh, a charming view that feels incomplete without children. Nearly three years into my marriage, I have no child! Your quiver full of children drives me to explore my heart.

I would endure any suffering, even what you faced when my godson was born, just to have a cherub of my own, like your Athenais! I can picture her beauty, but you tell me nothing about her—just like you, Renee! I believe you sense my trouble.

Each disappointment plunges me into deep melancholy, leading me to compose sad elegies. When will I embroider little caps and sew lace for a tiny head? Will I never hear "Mamma" from my child or have my dress tugged by a beloved little despot? Will there be no wheelmarks of a carriage on the gravel, no broken toys in the courtyard? I long to witness the unfolding of a precious life—another Felipe, but dearer. I would love a son to understand how a lover can be more to one in his second self.

My park and castle feel cold and desolate. A childless woman is a monstrosity of nature; we exist to be mothers. Oh, my wise friend, how well you understand life! Barrenness is dismal. My life resembles a Gessner or Florian sheepfold, which Rivarol wished to see invaded by a wolf. I have sacrifices to make! If I cannot be a mother, I must indulge in some romantic sorrow.

I shared this with my Moor, bringing tears to his eyes. He cannot bear jokes about his love, so I let him off easily, calling him a paladin of folly.

Sometimes, I feel a desire to go on pilgrimage, to bear my longings to a shrine or a watering place. Next winter, I will seek medical advice. I am too angry with myself to write more. Goodbye.

---

A whole year has passed without a letter! What does this mean? I am a little hurt. Does your Louis, who visits me almost every other day, make up for your absence? It is not enough to know you are well; I want to know your feelings and thoughts, just as I share everything with you, risking scolding or misunderstanding. Your silence and seclusion in the country, while your husband enjoys Parliamentary honors, worry me. Your husband's eloquence has earned him a good position, and he will likely receive a fine post when the session ends. Are you spending your time writing him letters of advice?

Why did you not take the chance to see Paris? I could have enjoyed your company for four months. Louis told me you would fetch him and have your third confinement in Paris—what a terrible mother Gigogne! After bombarding Louis with questions, I learned that his grand-uncle, Athenais' godfather, is very ill. I believe you, as a careful mother, would angle for a legacy from your husband’s last remaining relative. Rest easy, my Renee; we are all working for Louis. Martignac will likely place him in the audit department. But if you won’t tell me why you hide in the country, I shall be cross.

Are you afraid that the political wisdom of the house of l'Estorade should seem to center in you? Or is it the uncle's legacy? Perhaps you fear you would be less to your children in Paris? I would love to know if you are simply too vain to show yourself in Paris for the first time in your current condition! Vain thing! Farewell.

---

You complain of my silence; have you forgotten my two little brown heads, my subjects and tyrants? I didn’t want to drag a four-year-old and a soon-to-be three-year-old to Paris while expecting again. I didn’t want to trouble you and upset your husband with such a party. I also detest life in hotels.

When I come to Paris for the session, it will be in my own house. Louis' uncle, upon hearing of his grand-nephew's rank, gifted me two hundred thousand francs to buy a house in Paris, and I have tasked Louis with finding one near you. My mother has given me thirty thousand francs for furnishing, and I will do my best not to disgrace you.

I appreciate all you have done for Louis at Court. However, I do not want him in a conspicuous position; it would commit him too much. I prefer the Audit Office for its permanence. Our affairs are in good hands, so you need not worry; once the steward masters the details, I will support Louis.

As for writing long letters, how can I? This one, in which I want to describe my daily routine, will take a week to finish. Who knows if Armand will use it to make caps for his regiments or vessels for his bath? A single day serves as a sample, as they are all alike, characterized by either the children being well or not. In this solitary grange, hours become minutes or minutes hours, depending on their health.

My delightful hours come when they are asleep, and I am no longer needed to rock one or soothe the other. When they sleep beside me, I think, "Nothing can go wrong now." Every mother spends her time imagining dangers for her children. Perhaps Armand will seize razors, or his coat will catch fire, or he might drown in a pond. A mother's life is a series of dramas, both tender and terrible. Each hour has its joys and fears.

At night, I dream of their future, which shines brightly under the watch of their guardian angel. Sometimes, Armand calls me in his sleep; I kiss his forehead and his sister's feet, watching them in their beauty. These are my joys! Just last night, I was roused by our guardian angel to Athenais' cradle; her head was too low, and Armand was uncovered, his feet purple with cold.

"Darling mother!" he cried, flinging his arms around me.

How important it is for a mother to have her children by her side at night! A nurse, however careful, cannot comfort them after a nightmare. They have their dreams, and explaining away those visions is arduous when they are scared and half-awake. I have learned to sleep lightly, seeing them stir through my eyelids. A sigh or rustle wakes me. The demon of convulsions lurks by their beds.

With the first light, my babies stir. Their chatter blends with the morning air, while Nais struggles to reach me, and Armand climbs up with monkey-like agility. They turn my bed into a playground, where I am at their mercy. The baby pulls my hair, and Armand stands guard over my breast, defending his property. Their antics and laughter banish sleep.

Then comes the dressing. I slip on my dressing gown, turn up my sleeves, and don an apron. With Mary’s help, I wash and scrub my little blossoms. I control the bath temperature, as much crying comes from mistakes here. The moment arrives for paper fleets and glass ducks, as amusement is key to getting them thoroughly washed. If you knew the diversions I invent to get them to allow a soft sponge, you would be awed by the ingenuity required in motherhood. Prayers, scoldings, and promises are all in play, and the jugglery must be deft. Providence matches a child's cunning with that of a mother. It takes little to make them laugh; a falling brush or slipping soap brings merry shouts. Our triumphs, though hard-won, are still triumphs, hidden from mortal eyes. Even their father knows nothing of it. Only God, His angels, and perhaps you can fathom the satisfaction Mary and I share when the children are finally clean amid the chaos of a true nursery.

I admit that English women excel in this department. They focus on material well-being, and their arrangements are admirable. My children will always be bare-legged and wear woolen socks. No swaddling or bandages; they will never be left alone. The helplessness of French infants means the nurse has freedom—that explains it all. A true mother is never free.

That is why I do not write. Besides managing the estate, I am raising two children.

Motherhood involves silent self-denial and devotion to minute details. I must watch the soup warming before the fire. Am I the kind of woman to shirk such cares? A child's laugh when they enjoy their food is worth a lifetime of adoration. Armand nods when pleased, and how could I leave the privilege of blowing on the spoonful of soup that is too hot for my little Nais, who still remembers my breast? To mince a chop for Nais, who has just cut her last teeth, requires patience, and only a mother can make an impatient child finish a meal.

No number of servants or English nurses can replace a mother in that daily contest, where gentleness must address the little griefs of childhood. Louise, caring for these innocent darlings engages the whole soul. A crying child condemns mother or nurse, except when the cry stems from natural pain. Now that I have two to care for (and a third on the way), they occupy all my thoughts. Even you, whom I love dearly, have become a memory.

My own dressing is often not complete by two o'clock. I have no faith in mothers whose rooms are immaculate and who look perfect. Yesterday was a lovely April day, and I wanted to take my children for a walk while I still could—time is pressing. Such an expedition is an epic for a mother! I dreamed of it the night before! Armand was to wear a little black velvet jacket, a new collar I made, and a Scotch cap; Nais was to be in white and pink with a delightful baby cap. All was laid out on my bed. The little brown heads had to be brushed, chirping like birds. Armand's hair is in curls, while Nais' is softly brought forward as a border to her cap. When they trotted off to the nursery, my heart raced. To see the children I dressed, their pure skin veined with blue, is a joy unmatched! I long for one more kiss on their white necks, which no woman can rival.

Once outside, triumphant in my efforts, I admired Armand's princely air as he helped Nais along the path. A carriage approached, and I tried to move them out of the way. They tumbled into a puddle, ruining my work! We had to return and change. I took the little one in my arms, forgetting my own ruined dress, while Mary seized Armand, and we re-entered. With a crying baby and a soaked child, what mind has a mother left for herself?

Dinner time arrives, and I have done nothing. Now comes the problem of feeding two children, putting on their bibs, turning up their sleeves, and getting them to eat. Amid these recurring cares, joys, and catastrophes, I neglect myself. If the children are naughty, I often don’t get rid of my curl papers all day. To write you these pages, I have let them cut pictures from my novels, build castles with books, and give Nais my silks to arrange, which has absorbed her completely.

Yet I have nothing to complain of. My children are strong and independent; they amuse themselves more easily than you might think. They find joy in everything; a little freedom is worth many toys. A few pebbles, small shells, and the mysteries of sand bring them pleasure. I watch Armand talking to flowers, flies, and chickens, imitating them. He is friendly with insects and never tires of admiring them. Everything small interests them. Armand is beginning to ask "why" about everything he sees. He even asks what I am saying to his godmother, whom he sees as a fairy. Strange how children hit the mark!

Alas! my sweet, I do not wish to sadden you with my joys. Let me tell you about your godson. The other day, a poor man followed us begging—beggars know a mother with a child cannot resist them. Armand, unaware of hunger and money, held out a trumpet I had just bought him, saying, "Here, take this!"

What joy could compare with that moment?

"May I keep it?" the poor man asked. "I too, madame, have had children," he added, hardly noticing the money I gave him.

I shudder at the thought of Armand going to school, knowing I have only three and a half years left to keep him with me. The flowers of his sunny childhood will fall before the scythe of a public school system; his gracious ways and candor will lose their spontaneity. They will cut the curls I have brushed and kissed so often! What will they do with the thinking being that is Armand?

And what of you? Are you still in love with Felipe? I have no worries about the Saracen. Goodbye; Nais has just tumbled, and if I continue, my letter will become a volume.

---

My sweet, tender Renee, you will have learned from the papers of the terrible calamity that has overwhelmed me. I could not write even a word. For twenty days, I never left his bedside; I received his last breath and closed his eyes, keeping vigil with the priests and repeating prayers for the dead. The cruel pangs I suffered felt like rightful punishment; yet, when I saw the calm smile on his lips, his last farewell to me, how could I believe I caused his death?

Be it so or not, he is gone, and I am left. To you, who have known us both, what more need I say? These words contain all. I would give my share of Heaven to hear that my prayers could bring him back! To see him again, even for a second, would mean I could breathe again without agony. Will you not come soon and soothe me with such promises? Is your love strong enough to deceive me?

But wait! You told me beforehand that he would suffer through me. Was it so? Yes, I had no right to his love. Like a thief, I took what was not mine, and my frenzied grasp crushed the life out of my bliss. The madness is over, but I feel alone. Merciful God! what torture can exceed the misery in that word?

When they took him away, I lay on the same bed, hoping to die. There was but a door between us, and I thought I could force it! Alas! I was too young for death; after forty days of cruel care and medical science, they slowly nursed me back to life. Now I find myself in the country, seated by my window, surrounded by lovely flowers he made bloom for me, gazing at the splendid view he loved. Ah! dear Renee, no words can express how new surroundings hurt when the heart is dead. I shiver at the sight of the moist earth in my garden, as if I walk on him! When I first went out, I trembled and could not move. It was so sad to see his flowers, and he not there!

My parents are in Spain. You know my brothers, and you are detained in the country. But do not worry about me; two angels of mercy have come to my side. The Duc and Duchesse de Soria rushed to their brother in his illness and have been everything I could wish. The last nights before the end found the three of us gathered in calm, wordless grief around the bed of this great man, rare in any age, head and shoulders above the rest of us. The patient resignation of my Felipe was angelic. His last words to me were simple and frank: "I had almost gone from life without leaving Fernand the Barony of Macumer; I must make a new will. My brother will forgive me; he knows what it is to love!"

I owe my life to my brother-in-law and his wife; they want to take me to Spain!

Ah! Renee, to no one but you can I speak freely of my grief. A sense of my own faults weighs me down, and there is solace in pouring them out to you, poor, unheeded Cassandra. My exactions, jealousy, and nagging unrest wore him to death. My love was dangerous for him because we shared the same sensitive nature; nothing was lost on him, and often my careless words struck straight to his heart. You cannot imagine how submissive he was. I only had to tell him to leave me alone, and he obeyed without a murmur. His last breath was spent blessing me, repeating that a single morning with me was more precious than a lifetime with another woman, even the Marie of his youth. My tears fall as I write.

This is my life now. I rise at midday and go to bed at seven; I linger over meals; I stand motionless for an hour before a single plant; I gaze into the trees; I take a serious interest in mere nothings; I long for shade, silence, and night. I fight through each hour, taking a gloomy pleasure in adding it to the heap of the vanquished. My peaceful park gives me all the company I care for; everything there is full of glorious images of my vanished joy, invisible to others but eloquent to me.

"I cannot away with you Spaniards!" I exclaimed one morning as my sister-in-law embraced me. "You have some nobility that we lack."

Ah! Renee, if I still live, it is because Heaven tempers the sense of affliction to the strength of those who bear it. Only a woman can know what it is to lose a love that was genuine, a passion that satisfied both spirit and flesh. How rare it is to find a man so gifted that to worship him brings no sense of degradation! If such fortune befalls us once, we cannot hope for it again. Men of true greatness, veiled by poetic grace, charm by high spiritual power—beware of love! Love will ruin you and the woman of your heart. This is my cry as I pace my woodland walks.

And he has left me no child! That love, rich in smiles and joy, has left no fruit. I am accursed. Can it be that, like the extremes of polar ice and torrid sand, the purity and vehemence of a single-hearted passion render it barren as hate? Is it only a marriage of reason, like yours, that is blessed with a family? Can Heaven be jealous of our passions? Such wild thoughts.

You are the one person whose company I could endure. Come to me; none but Renee should be with Louise in her sorrow. What a day when I first donned my widow's bonnet! When I saw myself in black, I fell back and wept until night. I weep again recalling that moment of anguish.

Goodbye. Writing tires me; thoughts crowd fast, but I have no heart to express them. Bring your children; you can nurse the baby here without making me jealous; all that is gone, he is not here, and I shall be glad to see my godson. Felipe wished for a child like little Armand.
