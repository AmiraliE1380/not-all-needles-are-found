BEATRIX

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

NOTE

Balzac, noted for detailed character studies and daily-life depictions, rarely portrayed real individuals. In “The Great Man of the Provinces in Paris” some characters echoed real figures; in “Beatrix” he borrows recognizable traits: Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin) suggests George Sand, while Beatrix, Conti, and Claude Vignon recall the Comtesse d’Agoult, Liszt, and critic Gustave Planche.

The opening scene captures customs of an old Breton family — a social state largely vanished — marking a transition from past to modernity and standing as one of Balzac’s notable depictions in the “Comedy of Human Life.”

I. A BRETON TOWN AND MANSION

In Brittany, some towns remain largely untouched by nineteenth-century change because of poor links to Paris. They view new civilization as spectacle and keep old customs. A traveler seeking to observe human nature would find remnants of the Louis XV era in Provence, Louis XIV in Pitou, and older traditions in Brittany. Many once-feudal towns declined, their histories ignored by historians focused on dates rather than cultural narratives. Yet memory of their former splendor persists, especially in Brittany, where local character ensures history is not forgotten.

Guerande is such a town: well-preserved feudal life, surrounded by walls and moats, with intact battlements and gates. The mayor’s planting of poplars along the moat met criticism—the promenade had been public for a century. The old town’s architecture remains unchanged, houses unmodernized and retaining original features. Streets, like those of four centuries ago, are now quiet, allowing a solitary traveler to wander a deserted town.

The atmosphere evokes ancient customs; even a gendarme feels out of place. The public square is alive with traditional Breton costumes, sharply contrasting with bourgeoisie and nobility. Social divisions remain stark; revolutionary spirit failed to erase rigid class distinctions. Guerande, fervently Catholic and self-contained, resists modern ideas.

Geographically, Guerande overlooks salt-marshes famed for Guerande salt, which locals claim improves their butter and sardines. It connects to France by two roads: one from Savenay and a more frequented route from Vannes. The town’s isolation is reinforced by slow government and inhabitants’ acceptance of separation. Guerande thus exists as a feudal remnant, little influenced by modernity.

The town is a picturesque blend of nature and history: salt-marshes and surrounding landscapes evoke tranquility. A carriage’s arrival is an event, drawing locals who come to barter. Guerande is known for historical incidents like a 1365 treaty and for its coastal importance.

Near the church stands a mansion of the du Guaisnic family, symbolizing the town’s past grandeur. The family, tracing roots to the du Guesclins, is ancient and noble, embodying Brittany’s history. Despite reduced fortunes, they remain significant; the Baron du Guaisnic is regarded as a prominent figure.

The mansion’s entrance has a grand archway, its gate bearing a family crest that reflects pride and antiquity. The courtyard reveals stables and a kitchen; the house, built of freestone, shows remnants of former glory. The interior is well-preserved: a grand hall with intricate woodwork and a massive fireplace.

The dining room, hung with fourteenth-century tapestries, retains historical charm; the kitchen connects through a corner tower. Upper stories contain simple family and guest accommodations, with servants sleeping above the kitchens.

The garden, about half an acre, has fruit trees and a sun-dial, tended by a devoted gardener. The mansion’s architecture shows Venetian influence and a spiral column suggesting ties to family heritage.

The mansion’s grandeur contrasts with modern, often temporary homes. The du Guaisnics have hosted friends there for generations, embodying old Brittany’s spirit. The town and mansion’s description contextualizes the family’s character, showing how environment shapes individuals. The du Guaisnics’ legacy attests to history and tradition’s endurance amid rapid change.

Early in May 1836, the Guenic family comprised Baron and Baroness du Guenic, the baron’s elder sister Mademoiselle du Guenic, and their only son, Calyste, aged twenty-one. The baron, Gaudebert-Calyste-Charles, had fought in the Vendée and Brittany uprisings alongside leaders like Charette and La Rochejaquelein. Before the war he had prudently sold his property to his sister to ensure their security. After years of resistance against Napoleon, he returned to Guérande in 1813, having fallen in love with Fanny O’Brien, an Irish noblewoman. They married, and she bore Calyste on the day Louis XVIII landed in France.

Now seventy-three, the baron bore the marks of his tumultuous life and appeared much older. His tall, wiry frame was lined with deep wrinkles, his features sunken and marred by age. Despite frailty, his presence exuded a steadfast spirit, embodying the resilience of his Breton heritage. His large, scarred hands testified to a soldier’s life, and his often-grim expression reflected proud loyalty to his beliefs.

The baron was illiterate, having read little beyond his prayer book, and his attire had not changed: sturdy shoes, ribbed stockings, and a coat adorned with the cross of Saint-Louis. His demeanor was serene, marked by a somnolence his family attributed to long years of duty. Conversations in the household revolved around the exiled Bourbons, the Catholic faith, and the future of Brittany, with Calyste the family’s sole hope.

The baron had taken Calyste under his wing, teaching him the ways of a gentleman and preparing him for potential conflict. When the Duchesse de Berry sought to reclaim the throne for her son, the baron took Calyste to join her cause, leaving his wife and sister unaware of their departure. They returned after three months, surprising the family, and the baron remarked, “The barons did not all do their duty,” reflecting on their commitment.

That evening the baron fell asleep in his armchair while his wife read the newspaper. Fanny O’Brien, the baroness, was striking, embodying the beauty and grace of her Irish heritage. At forty-two she retained a delicate loveliness, her skin glowing and features refined. She dressed elegantly, her hair in ringlets, and her demeanor radiated warmth and tenderness.

In contrast, Mademoiselle Zéphirine, the baron’s sister, sat nearby, blind and knitting. Despite her age she maintained a proud posture and cheerful disposition, refusing surgery for cataracts to avoid burdening her brother financially. The bond between the baroness and Zéphirine was strong; the latter embraced her role in the household and cared for her brother.

As the baroness reflected on her husband’s slumber, the atmosphere shifted with the setting sun, casting a warm glow over the aged furniture. Silence deepened, and Zéphirine, sensing her sister-in-law’s preoccupation, commented on her distraction. Mariotte, a servant, entered to light the lamp, while Gasselin, another loyal retainer, tended to the horses and dogs, embodying the unity and devotion of the household.

Gasselin and Mariotte, both long-time servants, shared a deep connection with the family; their lives were intertwined with the Guenics. Gasselin, a diligent gardener, took pride in his work, while Mariotte mirrored his steadfastness. Their loyalty to the family was unwavering, and they thrived under Mademoiselle Zéphirine’s careful management.

The household operated with a sense of purpose and unity, marked by affection among its members. The monotony of daily life was enriched by bonds of love and duty, creating a nurturing atmosphere that sustained them through the trials of their times.

When night fell, Gasselin entered the hall and respectfully asked his master if he needed anything further. The baron, waking, replied he could go out or to bed after prayers, unless Madame or his sister required him. The two ladies consented, and Gasselin knelt as his masters did. Mademoiselle du Guenic led the prayer, and afterward a knock sounded at the door. Gasselin opened it, suspecting Monsieur le cure, who soon entered, bowing to the company and offering the customary civility of priests.

Monsieur Grimont, a middle-aged man in a cassock, had a plump visage and calm demeanor that made him seem partly Dutch burgomaster, partly Breton peasant. His joviality suggested a clear conscience; he was well-regarded in Guerande, though he acknowledged the du Guenics' feudal superiority. He inquired about Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel's tardiness, noting the chevalier's presence at Les Touches that evening. Mariotte urged caution about discussing the chevalier's visits in front of Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, who was keen on the town’s gossip.

When Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel arrived, led by a page with a lantern, Mariotte moved her stool to converse with the boy. Thin and elderly, she dressed in a style from her youth, always carrying keys and a silver snuff-box. She belonged to the highest Brittany nobility, but her family’s legacy was waning: her sister had married a Kergarouet, who annexed her name. Managing her property with strict frugality, she was admired for avarice that drew no censure. Her modest annual expenses and frequent visits from nieces hoping to win her favor underscored her influence.

Jacqueline de Pen-Hoel, a friend of Zephirine du Guenic, planned to marry one niece to Calyste, the young chevalier, to secure ties with the du Guenics. Despite the du Guenics’ wealth, they preserved a respectful distance, valuing pride and status. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel treated Calyste with a sense of entitlement, believing her intentions gave her oversight. She held conservative views on morality and distrusted modern influences, preferring the stability of old customs.

She asked whether Monsieur du Halga would join them; the rector confirmed he had seen him walking earlier. Mention of du Halga stirred excitement as the group prepared for an evening game. The rector fetched a basket and counters, showing a childlike eagerness.

A firm knock at the gate announced Chevalier du Halga’s arrival: a frail man dressed for his health, once a competent naval officer. Now gentle and preoccupied with his little dog, he retained the remnants of former gallantry without speaking of past exploits. His health was undermined by hardships endured during the emigration, yet he remained a respected figure in Guerande, having served as mayor.

The rector, the chevalier, and Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel had spent evenings together for years at the hotel de Guenic, where the du Guenics held a prominent local position. The rector’s cough during the prayer for the king hinted at the political tensions still present in Guerande.

Mouche is a card game in which each player receives five cards and one card is turned up as trumps. Players may play or abstain; if they abstain they lose only their stake. Winning a trick earns a share of the pot, while losing makes one mouche, obliging payment of the full stake and enlarging the pot for the next deal. Players may exchange cards from the remaining deck, but only in order. The trump card belongs to the dealer, who may exchange it for any card in hand. The strongest card is Mistigris, the knave of clubs.

Despite its simple rules, mouche is engaging, exposing greed, cunning, and expression. At the hotel du Guenic, players begin with twenty counters each, representing five sous, making the total stake five farthings—a notable sum for the group. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel and Mademoiselle Zephirine treat the game seriously, likening it to a financial venture or stock speculation.

A convention from September 1825 states the game ends if a player loses ten sous and wishes to quit. The chevalier and the baron, however, sidestep this by offering counters to those who have lost, keeping the play going. The presence of Demoiselle de Kergarouet and her aunt raises the stakes further, since the young women, enamored of Calyste, care little for the small sums at stake.

These nightly games are interspersed with talks of local events and public affairs. Players occasionally forget their stakes, provoking accusations of cheating—particularly aimed at the rector. Evenings can finish abruptly, with players departing empty-handed and grumbling about the game itself. Attempts to introduce other games like whist and boston have failed; the circle prefers mouche, which outlasts newer diversions.

As the rector deals, the baroness inquires about the chevalier's health; he habitually reports new ailments, joking that they migrate about his body. Conversation turns to the recent death of the Duc de Lenoncourt and to reflections on mortality.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel announces the Comtesse de Kergarouet's marriage, prompting gossip about Mademoiselle des Touches, a woman of questionable reputation. The baroness and rector exchange concerned looks, especially as Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel holds Mistigris, silencing further commentary. The baroness's victory appalls Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, who becomes absorbed by anxieties about Calyste and Mademoiselle des Touches.

After the game the chevalier escorts Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel home. She confides worries that Calyste is infatuated with Mademoiselle des Touches, an actress. The baroness frets over Calyste's prospects, fearing this woman might lead him astray.

The rector seeks to reassure her, noting that Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel intends to leave her fortune to the niece who marries Calyste—an indication of her good intentions. He warns, however, that Mademoiselle des Touches is a dangerous influence, notorious for scandal and dubious associations. The baroness, distressed, fears for her son's future and the effects of such a liaison.

As the evening progresses, the rector expresses disbelief that a youth raised in a devout household could fall into such company. The baroness laments the erosion of her close bond with Calyste, feeling supplanted by Mademoiselle des Touches. The rector promises to investigate and learn more about Calyste's involvement with the actress.

The baroness accompanies the rector to the gate, anxiously awaiting Calyste's return; instead she hears the rector's footsteps fading into the night.

The distressed baroness returned home to find the whole town aware of her son Calyste's frequent visits to Mademoiselle des Touches, which she had thought private. She tried to entice him home earlier by taking up her needlework, but her maternal jealousy was in vain; Calyste spent more time there and returned later each night, once as late as midnight.

As she stitched, the baroness wondered what held men so captive to Mademoiselle des Touches, what secret charms could make them forget family and duty. She longed to meet the woman and judge her character, fearing modern influences that might corrupt her innocent son. Calyste, of noble Breton and Irish lineage, had been carefully raised; she had entrusted his education to the rector of Guerande, believing him shielded from impure thoughts. Yet his schooling had left him ignorant of contemporary literature and science, and he remained a fervent Catholic.

The baroness had envisioned a modest, happy life for Calyste and hoped to secure a suitable marriage with a wealthy heiress, aided by an expected inheritance. Those plans were threatened by Mademoiselle des Touches. As midnight struck she grew anxious, afraid he might stay out even longer. When he finally returned she greeted him with relief, though she noted his fatigue and signs of emotional turmoil, attributing his weariness to excitement rather than deeper trouble. Still, her thoughts grew bitter as she realized his affections lay elsewhere and feared the consequences.

The next day Calyste slept late, indulged by his mother. At dinner she noticed his attire and suspected he would dine again at Les Touches. Her sister voiced concern over his change in behavior, blaming Mademoiselle des Touches’s influence. The baroness feared the woman's morals and the impact on Calyste, who had begun reading varied books under her guidance.

Calyste defended Mademoiselle des Touches, explaining she had rejected his love and offered friendship. He insisted she was a talented artist deserving respect despite an unconventional lifestyle. The baroness remained unconvinced, fearing for her son's future and loss of his values.

As Calyste left for Les Touches, the baroness’s anxiety deepened; she worried the woman would lead him astray and ached at the thought of losing her son. The baron dismissed his wife's concerns as youthful illusions.

Meanwhile Guerande buzzed with rumors that Mademoiselle des Touches was infatuated with Calyste and exerted a mysterious influence. The rector and other townsfolk disapproved of her lifestyle, seeing her as a threat to social norms.

Mademoiselle Félicité des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, was a celebrated writer whose life and works sparked curiosity and scandal. Orphaned during the Revolution and raised by an aunt, she developed a strong intellect through extensive reading. Despite beauty and talent, she struggled with societal expectations and the limits placed on women.

Félicité’s early life was marked by tragedy, but she emerged as a formidable literary figure, known for plays and novels that challenged conventions. These experiences shaped her views on love and independence, leading her to reject traditional marriage and embrace art.

Her salon became a hub for intellectuals and artists, where she pursued passions beyond societal constraints. Drawn to Calyste, who embodied a youthful innocence she had abandoned, she found her relationships complicated by this attraction. Despite public success, she grappled with aging and the desire to maintain beauty and independence, balancing a public persona with private longing.

As her connection with Calyste deepened, Félicité confronted her feelings and the implications for both their lives. Their relationship forced her to weigh love against ambition and independence, raising stakes that threatened to upend the carefully constructed life she had built.

A few hundred yards from Guérande, Brittany's soil gives way to salt marshes and sandy dunes. We descend into a sandy desert, punctuated by ponds and a narrow arm of the sea that separates the mainland from Croisic, which is really a peninsula joined to Brittany by treacherous quicksand beaches.

At the junction of the road from Croisic to Guérande stands a country house set in a garden of wind-sculpted pines. Built of slaty stone and granite, it has a plain exterior with regular windows and a high pointed roof punctuated by gables and dormers. One facade faces the road to Guérande, the other looks over the desert toward Croisic and the sea. A brook runs from the park, crosses the road, and disappears into the sands.

The house’s gray tones harmonize with the landscape. The estate, Les Touches, yields income from the marshes and nearby farms, totaling over ten thousand francs annually. Though the Revolution stripped feudal rights, local salt workers still call it “the château.” Félicité, the owner, restored Les Touches while keeping its desolate aspect, adding only brick gate columns to mark the entrance.

The ground floor plan is typical of country houses a century old, likely built on castle ruins. Félicité transformed the entrance hall into a billiard room, which opens to a large salon and dining room. The kitchen connects to the dining room via an office. The décor is simple yet graceful, with gray-painted walls and mahogany furniture covered in green silk. The dining room displays mahogany buffets and horsehair chairs, decorated with engravings by Audran.

Upstairs, Félicité occupies a suite with a salon, a large bedroom, and two cabinets; another suite serves as guest rooms. The guest chambers are modestly furnished, while Félicité’s apartment shows luxurious taste: Gobelin tapestries, heavy silk drapes, and fine Louis XV furniture. Her study contrasts with the bedroom’s opulence, furnished in modern mahogany and filled with personal items that reveal her complex character.

Outside, the landscape—salt marshes, dunes, and the distant ocean—creates a melancholic yet uplifting atmosphere. This stark beauty captivates Camille, who prefers the desolate surroundings to Guérande’s lushness. Calyste also feels drawn to Les Touches, seeing it as refuge from Guérande’s confines. His feelings for Félicité, founded on admiration and a desire to love, persist despite her rejections.

At Les Touches, Calyste encounters the richness of art and culture, experiencing nineteenth-century music and literature that awaken dormant sensibilities. Félicité, nurturing his intellect, becomes a maternal figure, inspiring both admiration and affection.

Félicité is currently giving Calyste music lessons, and the house feels alive with creativity. The contrast between the vibrant world of art and rural dullness profoundly affects him. As he approaches the house he is filled with anticipation.

Upon entering, Calyste hears Félicité playing the piano, but the music stops when he reaches the billiard room. He suspects she is in her private salon and sits quietly to listen. Her improvisation, when it resumes, is hauntingly beautiful, expressing deep emotion that resonates with him. When he finally enters, he finds her in tears, anxious over Claude Vignon’s absence.

Calyste offers to search for Claude, but Félicité gently asks him to stay, saying her tears are a natural overflow of feeling. She reflects on the complexities of her emotions and on her relationship with Claude, revealing insecurities and desires. Despite her sadness, she finds solace in Calyste’s presence, valuing his youthful innocence.

Félicité acknowledges the age difference between herself and Calyste, admitting her rejection of his love stemmed from self-awareness and the inevitability of their separation. She expresses a longing for love and connection, lamenting her inability to find fulfillment in relationships.

As they converse, Félicité insists on being an honest woman and prepares to tell Calyste her story. She settles to tell it over tea, creating an intimate moment between them. Calyste, eager to support her, sits at her feet, unaware of the full depth of her emotions as they share this fragile connection.

“I promised you this tale of the past, and here it is,” said Camille. “The person who sent that letter yesterday, and who may be here tomorrow, is the Marquise de Rochefide. The old marquis, after marrying his eldest daughter to a Portuguese grandee, sought a noble match for his son to secure a peerage he never obtained. The Comtesse de Montcornet mentioned a young lady in Orne, Mademoiselle Béatrix-Maximilienne-Rose de Casteran, the youngest daughter of the Marquis de Casteran, who wished to marry off daughters without dowries to preserve his fortune for his son. The Casterans are of the bluest blood. Béatrix, raised at the château de Casteran, was twenty when she married in 1828. She was known for originality — independence of thought, enthusiasm, and a passion for Art. A woman following such paths risks falling into abysses, unlike men who have the strength to navigate them. Her grandmother was pleased with her marriage to a man of lesser stature. The Rochefides were satisfied: the Casterans linked them with the Verneuils and d’Esgrignons, and secured a peerage for their son in a batch made by Charles X., later revoked by the July Revolution.

The early days of marriage can be perilous. Rochefide mistook Béatrix’s ignorance for coldness, branding her among frigid women, which drove him back to his bachelor life. People said he was fortunate to have a wife who would only have head passions, content to shine and create a salon of distinguished minds. Yet Rochefide was no ordinary fool; he had the vanity of a clever man, which bred jealousy and tyranny when Béatrix feigned indifference to his infidelity. I analyze this to explain her conduct. Béatrix admired me greatly, but admiration can quickly turn to jealousy. I had a remarkable salon in Paris, and she sought to found her own, attempting to draw my circle. She attracted superficial people but lacked time to build a true society. I thought her desire for celebrity consumed her, but she possessed a grand soul, regal pride, and the capacity to grasp subjects from metaphysics to music.

Béatrix is strikingly beautiful: slender, fair, with a long, pointed face and a magnificent forehead. Her pale sea-green eyes often wear dark rings, and her features are sharp yet elegant. She has an exquisite waist and noble shoulders, though her bust is less developed. Despite such flaws, she carries herself with a natural grace revealing her birth. Dressed well, she can seem divine.

Félicité showed Calyste a fine copy of a painting by Mieris, a woman in white satin. “Fair women have a precious diversity,” Camille remarked, “while brunettes like us have only one way to charm.” She warned Calyste not to fall for Béatrix from her description, as he would be too late.

Despite her beauty, Béatrix lacks the pliant grace of her coloring; her lines are severe, and her face can appear harsh. She spent her early marriage judging men and life; the revolutionary disturbances bored her at her husband’s country estate. Upon returning to Paris, she believed the July Revolution would spur a moral upheaval, and she took an intellectual interest in the new doctrines. But she soon realized the regime offered no place for individual superiority and chose personal happiness over societal ideals.

Around this time Béatrix met Gennaro Conti, a Neapolitan composer with a brilliant mind. Though talented, he would never reach the first rank. Conti’s voice was captivating, and Béatrix fell madly in love, taking him from me. She won my esteem by confiding in me and making me arbiter of her destiny. I was devoted to Conti, but I knew his true nature. He was charming on the surface but duplicitous beneath. He portrayed himself as an artist inspired by divine forces, but his motives were self-serving.

I warned Béatrix about the gulf she was entering, but she was blinded by passion. Conti was ecstatic, and I lent him money for their journey to Italy. Béatrix left a letter for her husband and departed, where she remained for two years, writing enchanting letters to me. She expressed adoration for me, seeing me as the only woman who understood her.

At that moment Claude Vignon entered, causing a brief silence. He was imposing despite a haggard look: a high forehead and a firm mouth that hinted at sarcasm. Vignon was complex, capable of deep thought but often paralyzed by it. He criticized without constructing, and his intellect was sharp yet indifferent to the material world.

“Which way did you come?” asked Mademoiselle des Touches, surprised.

“By the door,” replied Claude dryly.

“Am I in the way?” he asked, sensing tension.

Calyste, provoked, attempted to speak but was interrupted by Claude’s sardonic remarks about sentiments. Calyste left, feeling anger at Claude’s indelicacy and pity for Félicité. He returned home, where his mother expressed joy at his arrival, and he took out Béatrix’s letter, which might disrupt their family’s peace.

In her letter Béatrix described Italy: fatigue from constant enjoyment and the strains of a new life with Conti. She felt a loss of social equality and reflected on sacrifices made for love. Béatrix acknowledged disadvantages of her position, contrasting it with Camille’s freedom. She longed for solitude and admired Italy’s beauty, while lamenting financial struggles that would drive them back to Paris.

Calyste, folding the letter, felt sadness. His mother sensed his melancholy and asked. He explained how Félicité had opened his eyes to the insufficiency of his education and the possibility of a nobler life. His aunt interrupted, suggesting he marry a good Breton girl, but Calyste rejected the notion, yearning for the freedom and passion he associated with love.

His mother, understanding his desires, embraced him and encouraged him to seek happiness. The family dynamic was tender, with mother and aunt trying to ground him while he dreamed of love and adventure.

After dinner Calyste returned to Les Touches, where Claude and Félicité remained at the table. Claude, a gourmand, enjoyed the lavish meal Félicité had prepared. Calyste presented Béatrix’s letter; Claude remarked on the fortune of Parisian women who have men of genius to adore them. Félicité defended Béatrix, insisting her grand nature would not change.

As the evening moved on, Claude and Félicité engaged in witty banter, captivating Calyste with their intellect. Claude speculated about Béatrix and Conti’s return, which stirred emotions in Camille. Calyste, lost in thought, pictured Béatrix as Félicité had described her, feeling a deep sympathy.

When he returned home his mother questioned him about his late evening. Calyste confessed his admiration for genius and lamented his lack of it. His mother reassured him, emphasizing the importance of love and happiness, encouraging him to pursue noble women if he must love.

What young man, full of restrained emotion, would not be eager to see Madame de Rochefide land at Croisic? Calyste surprised his parents by leaving early, urged by an unknown vigor as he hurried along the walls of Les Touches to avoid being seen. He felt excitement with a touch of shame, fearing the perceptiveness of Felicite and Vignon.

At the shore he found a travelers' shelter and watched two boats arrive from Croisic, loaded with luggage suggesting distinguished passengers. One boat carried a young woman in a straw bonnet, a maid, and a footman. Calyste was captivated by a chest stamped "MME. LA MARQUISE DE ROCHEFIDE," feeling an inexplicable connection. He believed he would love her, as his heart readily transferred the affection he had once felt for Camille to this new figure.

While the luggage was unloaded, he overheard the maid and footman discussing their wait for someone to fetch them from Les Touches and learned that the marquise had already been picked up. He hurried toward Guerande, skirting the servants of Les Touches, agitated and longing.

Calyste's thoughts were consumed by the marquise, and he contrasted this new eagerness with his previous relationship with Camille. He returned home late in the afternoon to find a letter from Mademoiselle des Touches inviting him to celebrate the marquise's arrival. The letter mentioned the Casteran family, to which the marquise belonged, and reassured his mother of the woman's noble lineage.

Walking to the gathering, Calyste was a tumult of emotion. He arrived at Camille's suite where company had assembled. Camille greeted him, and he was introduced to Gennaro Conti, a man with a Byron-like presence. The marquise, reclining on a divan, exceeded his imagination: more beautiful than he had pictured, hair braided with bluebells and elegantly dressed. Calyste felt an immediate, overwhelming affection that ignited both hope and fear.

At dinner Calyste was assigned to escort the marquise. His heart raced and he struggled for words, daunted by her beauty. She noticed his nervousness and asked if he were unwell. He admitted he had never seen anyone so beautiful except his mother. Mademoiselle des Touches, aware of his feelings, counselled restraint; she warned that the marquise could not return such fondness.

Conversation turned to Camille's travels, and Calyste increasingly perceived distance between himself and the marquise, who seemed absorbed by Conti. He felt insignificant next to the artist's talent. When Conti and Camille performed music, it stirred deep emotion in Calyste; he was moved to tears. The marquise, noticing his distress, turned away, while Calyste grappled with feelings of inadequacy.

As the evening progressed, his admiration for the marquise deepened even as his despair grew. The contrast between his ardor and Conti's artistic brilliance overwhelmed him. In a moment of vulnerability, he slipped away to a nearby sofa, lost in thought, wrestling with the intensity of his emotions and the painful sense of his own insignificance before such talent.

X. DRAMA

“What is it, my child?” Claude Vignon asked Calyste, who had just entered the bedroom. “You love; you think you are disdained, but it is not so. Soon, the field will be free to you, and you will reign—beloved by more than one.”

“Loved!” Calyste exclaimed, pulling Claude into the library. “Who loves me here?”

“Camille,” Claude replied.

“Camille loves me? And you—what of you?”

“I?” Claude paused, then sat, resting his head. “I am tired of life, but lack the courage to leave it. I wish I were mistaken; yet I have seen many truths lately. I did not wander the marshes for pleasure. The bitterness I felt on returning to find you with Camille sprang from wounded feelings. I will confront her soon. Two clear-sighted minds like ours cannot deceive each other. Between two such duellists, the combat cannot last long. I will leave Les Touches tomorrow, perhaps with Conti. After we are gone, strange things will happen here. I will regret not witnessing the rare conflicts of passion that will unfold. You are young to enter such dangerous waters; you interest me. Were it not for my disgust for women, I would stay and help you navigate this game. It is difficult; you may lose it. You are dealing with two extraordinary women, and you feel too much for one to use the other wisely. Beatrix is relentless; Camille has grandeur. Beware—you may be wrecked between those reefs.”

Calyste, stunned, listened like a traveler shown an abyss. Hearing that Camille loved him while he loved Beatrix was a weight he could not bear. Overwhelmed with regret, he replayed reasons for Felicite’s rejection and Claude’s arrival. Beatrix’s voice from the salon ignited a fierce desire to carry her off. If Camille loved him, how could he adore Beatrix? He saw no solution.

Silence fell; doors opened and shut. Midnight struck, and the voices of Claude and Camille roused him. Before he could rise, he heard Claude say to Felicite, “You came to Paris last year desperately in love with Calyste, but you feared the consequences: a gulf, perhaps suicide. Love must think itself eternal; you foresaw a terrible parting. You brought me to Les Touches as a screen to follow your secret adoration. The scheme was grand and ignoble; you should have chosen a common man or one so preoccupied you could deceive him. You thought me simple and easy to mislead. I am not a man of genius; I am a man of talent, and I have divined you. Your eyes were on me, but your heart throbbed for Calyste. You have never been loved, Maupin, and you never will be after rejecting the beautiful fruit chance offered you.”

“Why has love fled me?” she asked softly.

“Because you are not lovable,” he replied. “You do not bend to love; love must bend to you. Your mind is too deep; you have never been naive. Your charm is mystery, abstract, not active. Your strength repulses strong men who fear a struggle. You may please young souls like Calyste, who like to be protected; even they will tire. You are grand and sublime; bear the consequence—they fatigue.”

“What a sentence!” Camille exclaimed. “Am I not a woman? Do you think me an anomaly?”

“Possibly,” Claude replied.

“We will see!” she said, stung.

“Farewell, my dear; I leave tomorrow. I am not angry with you; I think you the greatest of women, but if I continued to serve you as a screen, you would despise me. We can part now without pain or remorse; we have neither happiness to regret nor hopes betrayed. To you, as with a few rare men, love is not what Nature made it—a need of fleeting joys. You see love as Christianity has made it—an ideal kingdom full of noble sentiments and spiritual sensations. I thought I held in you a key to that door, but you misled me. I return to Paris to my misery. Such deception, earlier in life, would have made me flee existence; today it plunges me into awful solitude.”

At that moment, Calyste appeared.

“I ought not to leave you ignorant that I am here,” he said.

Mademoiselle des Touches showed fear; a flush colored her impassible face.

“We thought you gone, Calyste,” Claude said. “This involuntary discretion on both sides will do no harm; perhaps you may be more at ease at Les Touches knowing Felicite as she is. Her silence shows me I am not mistaken about the part she meant me to play. As I told you, she loves you, but it is for yourself, not for herself—a sentiment few women can conceive; few know the voluptuous pleasure of sufferings born of longing—that emotion is magnificent and reserved to man. But she is in some sense a man,” he added sarcastically. “Your love for Beatrix will make her suffer and make her happy too.”

Tears filled Mademoiselle des Touches’ eyes as she looked at Calyste, who had raised his head abruptly. Claude enjoyed the sight of their confusion.

“You yourself have driven him to Madame de Rochefide,” Claude continued, “and he is now under her spell. You have dug your own grave. Had you confided in me, you would have escaped the sufferings that await you.”

“Sufferings!” Camille cried, taking Calyste’s head in her hands and kissing his hair, her tears falling on it. “No, Calyste; forget what you have heard; I count for nothing in all this.”

She rose, standing before the two men with an intensity that subdued them.

“While Claude was speaking,” she said, “I conceived the beauty and grandeur of love without hope; it brings us nearest God. Do not love me, Calyste; but I will love you as no woman will!”

It was the cry of a wounded eagle seeking its eyrie. Claude knelt, kissed her hand.

“Leave us now, Calyste,” she said. “It is late, and your mother will be uneasy.”

Calyste returned to Guerande heavy-hearted, glancing repeatedly toward Beatrix’s window. He felt little pity for Camille at first, but the memory of her tears and her kiss on his hair haunted him. At home, his mother sat sewing by lamp-light.

“What has happened?” Fanny asked.

Calyste took his mother in his arms, kissing her cheeks and forehead.

“It is you I love, you!” he cried. “You, who live for me; you, whom I long to make happy!”

“But you are not yourself, my child,” the baroness said. “What has happened?”

“Camille loves me, but I love her no longer,” he replied.

The next day Calyste had Gasselin watch the road to Saint-Nazaire. A carriage passed with four persons—two ladies and two gentlemen.

“Then saddle my horse and my father’s,” Calyste said.

At breakfast the household bantered about the possibility of a meeting with Charlotte. Calyste pretended indifference about marriage prospects; his family teased him. He rode briskly, secretly hoping to intercept the visitors. At Saint-Nazaire, he found Camille and the marquise waving handkerchiefs to departing passengers. Beatrix, charming in a straw hat and muslin, leaned on a parasol; Camille took Calyste’s arm, leaving Beatrix to watch the vessel.

Calyste then saw the arrival of Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, the Vicomtesse de Kergarouet, and Charlotte. Camille suggested Calyste offer the ladies seats in her carriage, and he did. The viscountess, delighted to meet Camille Maupin and to be offered a ride, accepted despite her elder sister’s objections. Charlotte spotted Calyste and greeted him eagerly. He walked with Charlotte and her aunt in embarrassed silence; Charlotte’s early hopes were dashed.

Charlotte, plain but spirited, wore shabby travel dress, painfully aware of the contrast with Camille and Beatrix. The Vicomtesse, tall and pretentious, sought to humiliate Parisians yet craved compliments. Her manners and airs amused and embarrassed her party.

“Monsieur le chevalier,” she said, “has conveyed your proposal, but we fear inconveniencing you.”

“We can very well put five in the carriage,” Mademoiselle des Touches replied. “Your maid can sit with the coachman; your luggage can go behind.”

When the viscountess learned that Madame de Rochefide was of the party she exclaimed at the marquise’s misfortunes, then compassion when Camille revealed the separation from Conti: “Poor lady! Is she legally separated?”

“No, by mutual consent,” Camille replied.

The old maid retreated with Charlotte. Calyste seized the marquise’s hand, kissed it, and left a tear upon it; Beatrix turned, wiping away tears of anger. Camille observed the effect of her calm on Calyste, and he admired her soothing manner despite recent declarations.

“What queens!” he said to Charlotte, pointing to the marquise and Camille.

They drove through Guerande to the gate; Calyste gave his arm to the two ladies and bid Camille and Beatrix adieu. He kissed Felicite’s hand but could not reach the marquise’s; she held her arms crossed. Camille whispered a friendly kiss in his ear as the carriage drove away.

Back home, the guests dined. The viscountess spoke of literature, trying to engage the provincials, but the topic sailed over most heads. After dinner there was a boisterous game of mouche; Calyste’s return to gaiety made everyone assume he was almost as good as married to Charlotte. He alone remained silent, comparing his childhood friends to the accomplished Parisian women. His mother, Fanny, alone sensed his unease and urged him to arrange the viscountess’s return in Camille’s carriage. Calyste ran back to Les Touches to settle it.

XI. FEMALE DIPLOMACY

Calyste arrived as Camille and Beatrix left the grand salon after dinner; he offered his arm to Felicite.

“So you have abandoned your viscountess and her daughter for us,” she said, pressing his arm. “We can now understand that sacrifice.”

“Are these Kergarouets related to Admiral de Kergarouet?” Madame de Rochefide asked.

“The viscountess is his great-niece,” Camille replied.

“Well, she’s a charming girl,” Beatrix said. “She will just do for you, Monsieur du Guenic.”

“The marriage will never take place,” Camille said hastily.

Calyste, mortified by the marquise’s calm about the Breton girl, remained speechless. Camille, seeing his despair and the faint flush in her own cheeks, awkwardly let him take her hand; she then seated herself at the piano and played melancholy variations.

Beatrix observed Calyste closely. After an hour, Beatrix retired to her apartments and Camille took Calyste into her chamber, closing the door.

“My child,” she said, “if you want to succeed with Beatrix, you must seem to love me still, or you will fail. You are a child; you know nothing of women; all you know is how to love. Loving and making oneself beloved are different. If you go your own way, you will fall into horrible suffering. If you rouse in her not pride but self-will—her stubbornness—she might go to Paris and rejoin Conti; what will you do then?”

“I shall love her.”

“You won’t see her again.”

“Oh! yes, I shall,” he said.

“How?”

“I shall follow her.”

“Why, you are as poor as Job,” Camille said. “Calyste, listen. You have too much candor to play a part; I don’t want to corrupt you. Yet deception is the only way to win Beatrix; I take it upon myself. In a week she shall love you.”

“Is it possible?” he cried, clasping his hands.

“Yes,” Camille replied. “You must promise blind obedience. Come here every day, devote yourself to me; avoid Beatrix. We will stay together until four; you shall study, I will smoke. You are young, but if you break our agreement, even a brief private conversation with the marquise, you will lose her forever.”

“I don’t understand,” Calyste said simply.

“If you did, you wouldn’t be the noble Calyste you are,” she replied, kissing his hand.

Calyste, moved, embraced Camille about the waist and kissed her gently in a gesture of tenderness. Mademoiselle des Touches did not restrain her tears.

“Go now,” she said. “Tell your viscountess my carriage is at her command.”

He returned home gaily, believing that in a week Beatrix would love him. At the card-table he was restored to youthful cheer; Charlotte thought her triumph assured. The household played late, the baron sleepy, the baroness stirring out of maternal concern. Mariotte served a modest festive supper; the du Guenic hospitality produced a warm, simple charm.

After Calyste left Les Touches, Beatrix returned to Camille and found her weeping on the sofa.

“What is it, Felicite?” the marquise asked.

“I am forty years old, and I love him!” Mademoiselle des Touches cried. “If you knew the tears I have shed over lost youth! To be loved out of pity! To owe happiness to perpetual care and traps laid for innocence—oh, it is infamous! If it were not that love absolves, if it did not make me feel above other women in his memory, I would—if he asked it—fling myself into the sea. Sometimes I wish he would ask it. By coming here, you have set me a hard task. I know it will be difficult to keep him against you; but you love Conti, you are noble and generous, you will help me retain Calyste. I expected your impression on him, but I have not seemed jealous; that would only add fuel. I painted you so glowing a portrait that you hardly realize it; yet you are more beautiful than ever.”

The marquise, informed by Conti and Claude of Felicite’s position, resolved to behave generously and give Calyste the cold shoulder; but she felt pleasure at being loved. Felicite coaxed and flattered her, noting a slight withering at Beatrix’s temples and wrists, and speaking of checks men do not perceive.

“Your letter told me all,” Camille replied. “Happiness ignores everything but itself. You boasted too much of yours to be really happy. I feared to have you here. My dear, Calyste is an angel; his innocent heart will not resist your eyes; already he admires you too much not to love you at the first encouragement; your coldness alone can preserve him for me. I confess cowardly that if he were taken from me, I should die. That dreadful ‘Adolphe’ tells only of the man's woes; who tells the woman’s? They would dishonor her sex. Ah! I measure the abyss by my fears. But I have one support; I know a means to keep him true to me.”

“What means?” Beatrix asked.

“That is my secret. Let me have the benefits of my age. If Claude throws me back into the gulf, I, who had climbed to a rock, will at least gather the pale flowers that grow in its depths.”

Beatrix was molded by Camille’s hands. She left piqued with curiosity, balancing jealousy and generosity, her mind full of Calyste. Alone, Camille gave way to tears, smoked opium-laced tobacco, and dwelt on the young lover’s head—thinking what a book she might write if she could express her pain.

The next morning Calyste arrived before midday and found Camille at the window, smoking and contemplating the sea and him. She instructed him to remain to dinner but to refuse with a glance at the marquise that would show regret.

When the three met in the salon and played their comedy, Calyste’s glance at Beatrix was more expressive than Felicite expected. Beatrix had dressed charmingly.

The maneuvers continued six days, during which Camille used all her skill in conversations and small deceptions, the preliminaries of a duel between two women: feints, false generousies, crafty confidences, by which one hid and the other bared her love. Camille’s treacherous words often pierced Beatrix’s heart; the marquise took offense at what she perceived as distrust. At the same time, she was secretly amused to find the great writer vulnerable to such pettiness and resolved to show Camille where her greatness ended.

“My dear, what is the excuse today for Monsieur du Guenic’s not dining with us?” Beatrix asked, looking maliciously. “Monday you said we had engagements; Tuesday the dinner was poor; Wednesday you feared his mother would be angry; Thursday you wanted a walk with me; yesterday you dismissed him. Today I mean that he shall stay.”

“Already, my dear!” Camille said with cutting irony. The marquise blushed. “Stay, Monsieur du Guenic,” Camille commanded.

Beatrix became cold and contradictory, epigrammatic and almost rude to Calyste, whom Felicite sent home to play mouche with Charlotte. Calyste, absorbed with Beatrix, paid little heed to Camille’s craft. She noted that Beatrix had written to Conti only once that week—a symptom she reported to Calyste. All his life concentrated on the daily moment he could see the marquise; that drop of water intensified his thirst. He read through nights and brought home armfuls of books. His mother, alarmed, implored him to leave Les Touches; he refused. He was about to break his promise and speak to Beatrix to read her eyes; he resolved to approach her in the garden one morning.

Mademoiselle des Touches and the marquise had walked the marshes and returned to the garden. Camille proposed taking Calyste on a trip to Croisic, praising the young man’s angelic soul and pure heart. She spoke of beauty as Nature’s ensign. Beatrix laughed but seemed pensive; Camille’s references masked deep pain.

“Ah, my dear; but you are happy,” Beatrix said, laying her hand on Camille’s arm.

“Yes, happy indeed!” Camille replied with savage bitterness.

They rested on a bench. Camille rose: “I will go hasten breakfast.”

Calyste slid into the garden path and, meeting Beatrix, could not restrain a quiver.

“Madame, in what way did I displease you yesterday?” he asked.

“But you have neither pleased me nor displeased me,” she said gently.

“Am I so indifferent to you?” he asked, tears in his eyes.

“Ought we not to be indifferent to each other?” she replied. “Have we not, each, another, and a binding attachment?”

“Oh!” Calyste cried, “if you mean Camille, I did love her, but I love her no longer.”

“Then why are you shut up together every morning?” she said, smiling. “I don’t suppose Camille prefers her cigar to you, or you your admiration for female authors.”

“Calyste!” Camille cried, appearing and interrupting him. She took his arm and drew him away. “Calyste, is this what you promised me?”

Beatrix heard and was stupefied by his assertion; she was not as strong as Claude Vignon. Camille’s part—shocking yet grand—was one of those female sacrifices that break hearts; it begins an abnegation that may lift to heaven or plunge to hell.

At breakfast the marquise resolved to stifle rising love; she was gently indifferent to Calyste, a course that tortured him. Felicite proposed an excursion to Batz and Croisic; Calyste brightened, but Beatrix refused.

“My position is delicate; I do not wish to compromise—my happiness,” she said, glancing at the young Breton. “Conti is coming back to fetch me.”

Calyste turned pale. Despite Camille’s urgings and his own entreaties, Beatrix remained inflexible, showing the obstinacy Camille had warned of. Calyste left Les Touches in a depression of love that threatened madness, pondering decisive means to explain himself to Beatrix.

When Calyste returned home, he shut himself in his room until dinner and then continued writing. His mother, worried by his seclusion, found him amid crumpled papers composing a letter to Beatrix, moved by a new distrust of Camille after the marquise’s demeanor in their recent encounter.

Calyste’s first love letter poured out his intense emotions. He told Beatrix his love had swelled since meeting her, exceeding any dream he had known. Though he lamented having nothing new to say about her beauty, he insisted on a profound connection, claiming to understand her deeply.

He acknowledged his youth and naivety, contrasting himself with ordinary lovers and comparing himself to an insect drawn to her light. Feeling unworthy, he questioned why his ardent devotion failed to warm her, seeing her disdain overshadow his worship. He contrasted his past attachment to Felicite with his present truth: Beatrix.

Calyste admitted that Beatrix had unintentionally disturbed his happiness, yet he expected no return of affection. Reflecting on love, and recalling Camille’s lesson that true love must grow and endure, he believed that if Beatrix loved him they could rise above fears that had gripped Camille.

He implored her for kindness and recognition, likening his love to a martyr’s sacrifice. He asked her to engage with him as a friend without fear, to let him accompany her outings, and to answer him. The letter continued with emotional declarations, much of it repetitive. His mother, reading it, was moved to tears—alarmed by the rawness of feeling in a love letter she had never seen from her son.

Torn about sending it, Calyste sought advice from the Chevalier du Halga on delivering the note discreetly. The chevalier suggested slipping it to Beatrix’s maid, but Calyste chose to give it to Beatrix directly. He hurried to Les Touches, found Beatrix and Camille together, and impulsively handed the letter to Beatrix, who accepted it without hesitation.

After a restless night, Calyste awaited her response. When her letter arrived, he read it in the garden. Beatrix acknowledged his noble feelings but reminded him he was still a child. She affirmed her commitment to Camille, doubting her own suitability as a partner and warning that accepting his love would be an insult. Candid about her flaws and jealous tendencies, she said she could not provide the happiness Camille offered and would likely bring Calyste unhappiness. She urged him to seek a young girl worthy of his love who could fulfill his dreams.

The baroness, moved, approached Calyste to understand his feelings and read Beatrix’s reply aloud—admiring Beatrix’s nobility while worrying about her son’s future. She urged him to consider marrying Charlotte de Kergarouet to secure the estate and their prospects.

Calyste, however, remained steadfast for Beatrix and dismissed his mother’s advice. He withdrew to write a response, reaffirming his unwavering love and commitment. Troubled by his passion, the baroness sought the Chevalier du Halga’s counsel. As she walked through Guerande, her presence stirred curiosity; she found the chevalier and asked his view. Amused by the young man’s infatuation, he offered lighthearted remarks on love and gallantry.

Returning home, the baroness discovered another letter from Calyste that revealed the depth of his obsession. He declared his happiness tied solely to Beatrix, rejecting marriage to anyone else, and even fantasized about escaping with her, convinced their love could defy society’s constraints.

Alarmed by the intensity of his feelings, the baroness prayed for his well-being. When Calyste returned, she expressed her concern and disclosed that she had read his letter. He reassured her of his love, and they planned a boat trip to Croisic the next day, hoping to see Beatrix.

Calyste’s letters showed a maturity and clarity that surprised the baroness; they exposed both the depth of his emotion and the potential consequences of his infatuation.

One of the greatest pleasures for small minds is deceiving a greater soul, and Beatrix recognized her inferiority to Camille Maupin, not only in talent but in passion. As Calyste rushed to Les Touches, thrilled by first love, Beatrix reveled in being the object of his affection even though she did not wish to reciprocate. She thought it noble to suppress her feelings, imagining herself mirroring Camille’s devotion and sacrificing for her friend. The vanities of Frenchwomen flattered her, and she felt self-approved in resisting Calyste’s allure.

The two women lounged in the salon, surrounded by flowers and a gentle breeze, their inner turmoil at odds with the serene outdoors. Camille, aware of the emotional upheaval she had caused, felt compelled to guard her secrets against Beatrix, whom she regarded as a cunning rival. To cope with her heartache, Camille sought solace in nature, finding meaning in the world around her. Calyste had become a guardian angel to her, lifting her to higher thought, but Beatrix’s triumphant manner unsettled her.

When Calyste entered with a letter, Camille watched the interaction between him and Beatrix, sensing an unspoken understanding. Beatrix now regarded Calyste as hers, while he appeared guilty and transparent. He announced plans for an excursion, and Camille, anxious, suggested they prepare for the trip. She suspected a conspiracy between them, which left her discomposed.

After Calyste left to arrange the boat, Camille’s fears intensified. Upon his return, he found that neither Camille nor Beatrix met his eye, leaving him puzzled and troubled. At home he felt the weight of despair, a stark contrast to his earlier joy; his mother noted the change, but he dismissed her concern.

Beatrix, meanwhile, grappled with feelings stirred by Calyste’s letter—emotions she had not known before. She questioned what Calyste and Camille were doing together, suspecting Camille might be manipulating the situation. This realization filled her with jealousy and anger, and she paced her room in turmoil.

When Beatrix joined Camille for dinner, the atmosphere was tense. Camille sensed a confrontation and tried to avoid Calyste’s presence during the impending clash. The dinner passed with veiled insults and a battle of glances, culminating in a direct confrontation afterward.

Beatrix accused Camille of trickery, calling her actions cruel and manipulative. Camille, unshaken, countered with observations about Beatrix’s character and motives. The exchange escalated, Beatrix denouncing what she saw as Camille’s lack of femininity and compassion.

As the argument intensified, Camille disclosed her deep feelings for Calyste, while Beatrix declared she could not accept a relationship that would burden him. Beatrix’s wish to protect Calyste from her own flawed existence led her to assert she would not be his mistress if she could not be his wife. The declaration struck Camille deeply and ignited a fiercer rivalry.

The confrontation ended with Camille declaring their friendship over and saying she would write to Conti, Beatrix’s former lover. Beatrix, feeling cornered, stood her ground and refused to back down. The night’s emotional turmoil left both women reflective, each grappling with desires and the consequences of their actions.

Ultimately the passions stirred by their conflict settled into a precarious balance as both navigated the complexities of their feelings for Calyste and the societal expectations that weighed upon them.

It was the end of August, the sky clear and the sea shimmering like molten silver. The marshes were busy with paludiers harvesting salt, dressed in white against the sun. The scene felt almost exotic as the baron and baroness admired it from the jetty, the sea's rhythmic waves a soothing backdrop.

The baron said he wanted to see the marshes one last time, prompting a paludier to joke about the Guenic family’s mortality. At that moment the party from Les Touches arrived: the marquise leading, followed by Calyste and Camille. The baroness felt a strong aversion to Beatrix, who, though attractively dressed, seemed heartless to her.

Calyste made introductions, and the baron noted Camille's significant ownership of the marshes; a paludier praised her character. The two groups parted, the baron remarking on Mademoiselle des Touches' youthful appearance, while the baroness worried about Calyste's attachment to Beatrix.

They boarded a boat to Croisic, where the marquise kept a cold demeanor. Camille had scolded Calyste for disobedience; he was torn between anger and love for Beatrix. The passage was silent and tense as they neared the port, where barefoot women unloaded salt.

The Croisic peninsula featured fantastical granite rocks sculpted by nature into unique formations. The area was a natural wonder, with a hollow basin used for bathing and a rare box bush clinging to a distant promontory—a testament to nature's resilience.

Calyste led Beatrix to a viewpoint while Camille sought solitude among the rocks, dressed for agility. She reflected on her life and the choices ahead, contemplating her future and the character of her love for Calyste. Meanwhile Calyste and Beatrix spoke of the ocean's grandeur, which steered into a deeper discussion of feelings.

Beatrix confessed an inner desolation and insisted she could never be with Calyste despite loving him. She feared the consequences of abandoning her present life and the pain of unfulfilled love. Calyste, desperate to prove his devotion, offered to sacrifice everything, but Beatrix rejected the notion of sacrifice, reaffirming her commitment to her current situation.

At the rock where the box bush grew, Calyste's emotions overwhelmed him, leading to a moment of physical intimacy that caused Beatrix to slip and fall. Calyste rushed to save her; in the ensuing confusion their feelings intensified. Gasselin and Camille arrived to help, and Beatrix, though hurt, was taken to a farmhouse for care.

Calyste stayed by Beatrix's side, despairing yet hopeful for her recovery. The doctor assured them she would heal, and Calyste took solace in remaining near her. Beatrix, moved by his devotion, began to soften, recognizing the depth of his feeling.

Playing the invalid, Beatrix enjoyed Calyste's adoration, which contrasted with her previous experiences. She relished the power she held over him while Calyste remained largely oblivious to any manipulation. Their interactions were tender, yet she kept a distance, reminding him of her commitments.

Despite her initial resistance, Beatrix found herself drawn to Calyste's steadfast affection. She disclosed past struggles, and he vowed to love her exclusively, offering understanding she had longed for. Their bond deepened, and Beatrix started to see Calyste as a source of happiness.

As they prepared to return to the rock where she had fallen, Beatrix acknowledged the joy he brought her but reiterated her obligation to another man. Calyste, full of hope, believed their love could overcome her past.

However, back at Les Touches they encountered Conti, Beatrix's former lover, which shattered the moment. The ensuing tension between the characters suggested the complexities of love, loyalty, and consequence, leaving their futures uncertain.

The marquise's inner turmoil was plain when Conti arrived unexpectedly, shifting the group's dynamics. Conti offered his arm to Beatrix, forcing her to abandon Calyste, who felt humiliated and conflicted. Though he believed in her love, Calyste was struck by her visible distress and fell silent.

Turning to Camille for insight, Calyste learned of the complex power dynamics: Beatrix could not refuse Conti because of lingering ties from their past. Camille warned Calyste to watch Conti's manipulative nature, predicting he would use insinuations to regain control over Beatrix.

Conti's return followed a failed opera, and he appeared to exploit the situation. He feigned indifference toward Beatrix's new love while subtly undermining her confidence. At dinner his jovial manner masked intent; veiled comments about women's loyalty hit painfully for Beatrix and Camille.

Afterward Beatrix confided in Camille about feeling helpless against Conti's influence, even blaming Camille for his premature return. Their talk turned bitter, exposing deep rivalry: Beatrix insisted she would always hold a place in Calyste's heart, and Camille retorted sardonically about the fleeting nature of Beatrix's reign.

Meanwhile Conti and Calyste engaged in a tense conversation. Conti claimed to have lost interest and cast himself as the victim, encouraging Calyste to pursue Beatrix and suggesting her pride would drive her to revenge. Unaware of Conti's motives, Calyste revealed details of his recent time with Beatrix, fueling Conti's jealousy.

That evening Conti's salon performance was a calculated display meant to provoke Beatrix and assert dominance. Entranced by Conti's singing, Calyste approached Beatrix with newfound confidence, not seeing the trap.

The next morning Beatrix left with Conti, leaving Calyste devastated. Camille informed him, and he sank into grief, unable to process the betrayal. Despite Camille's attempts to expose Beatrix's true nature, Calyste clung to his belief in her love.

Days became weeks as Calyste's sorrow deepened. He wandered aimlessly, haunted by memories, while his family mistook his melancholy for a passing phase, blind to the slow consumption of his heartache.

Calyste's health declined as he withdrew socially, alarming his family. They tried to distract him with plans for marriage to Charlotte, but his heart remained with Beatrix. He ultimately confessed his love, rejecting Charlotte's advances and pleading with his family to let her go.

As his condition worsened, doctors could not diagnose his ailment. The baron, fearing for the family's legacy, grew desperate. In a moment of pressure Calyste promised his father he would marry, yet his heart stayed tethered to Beatrix.

The baron's death marked a grim turning point. Calyste was left more despairing; despite his vow to marry, he slipped toward hopelessness. A chance encounter with Camille, however, sparked a flicker of hope: she offered to help him find Beatrix in Paris.

With renewed determination Calyste agreed to accompany Camille to the city, believing that reuniting with Beatrix might restore his spirit. Their journey began a new chapter as they left the shadows of the past in search of a brighter future.

Felicite’s love was paving a prosperous future for Calyste. She wrote to the Duchesse de Grandlieu about him, outlining his past and her intentions. After selling her house for two million five hundred thousand francs, she bought another for seven hundred thousand and planned to use one million to recover the du Guenic estates, settling the remainder on Sabine de Grandlieu. Aware of the duke and duchess’s plans for their five daughters—only the youngest, Sabine, being marriageable—Felicite intended to redirect Calyste’s affections from Beatrix to Sabine.

During the journey to Paris, Mademoiselle des Touches confided in the baroness, who prepared the new house for Sabine and Calyste. They were invited to stay at the hotel de Grandlieu, where the baroness received due respect for her lineage. Mademoiselle des Touches encouraged Calyste to explore Paris while she sought news of Beatrix, who was traveling abroad. The city’s vibrancy began to distract Calyste from his former obsession. Noticing a resemblance between Beatrix and the engaging Sabine, he was drawn to the latter. By the winter of 1837, Calyste, refreshed, listened to his mother’s marriage proposal for Sabine, though he concealed any indifference.

On the day of the marriage contract signing, Calyste unexpectedly refused the benefits offered by Mademoiselle des Touches, causing confusion. Sabine entered carrying a letter from Camille Maupin, which Calyste read privately. In it, Camille professed love for Calyste and declared her intention to enter a convent, urging him to accept her gifts for his future welfare. She implored him to remember her kindly and to attend to his duties as husband and father, emphasizing duty over passion.

After reading the letter, Calyste rejoined the group and the contract was signed. The marriage ceremony took place the following week at Saint Thomas d’Aquin; Calyste and Sabine departed for Brittany amid tears and congratulations. The duchess and Clotilde worried for Sabine, aware Calyste did not marry entirely of his own will.

They understood marriage as complex, requiring compatibility and harmony: a venture women faced with greater stakes than men. The Baronne du Guenic, with only one son, was tasked with looking after Sabine.

As the newlyweds traveled, they felt awkward, each conscious of the importance of their first moments together. Calyste suggested Sabine be happy in her own way and proposed they treat each other as friends and siblings. This struck Sabine as ill-fitting to romantic expectations and left her pensive. Their conversation turned to mundane topics, and Sabine pressed him about his past love for Beatrix, which he reluctantly recounted.

Sabine was hurt to learn their marriage resulted from Calyste’s rejection of another woman, yet she found solace in his assurances that he would forget Beatrix. Their relationship began to evolve; Sabine noted a gradual shift as they grew closer.

In letters to her mother, Sabine described Brittany, her longing for home, and the challenges of her new life. She acknowledged Calyste’s sorrow about the marriage and her own difficulty loving him fully, feeling simultaneously in love and affected by his past. Despite her mother’s counsel to preserve dignity and self-respect, Sabine became devoted to Calyste, experiencing a mixture of happiness and vulnerability.

Settling into their new life, Sabine found joy in her role as chatelaine, adored by Calyste’s family and tenants. She described the warmth of their welcome and the deep-rooted Breton traditions that contrasted with her Parisian upbringing. The couple signed contracts to reclaim their estates, and Sabine felt fulfillment in her responsibilities.

Visiting Mademoiselle des Touches in a convent, Sabine received counsel on preserving marital happiness. Mademoiselle des Touches advised sagely but sharply: be capricious when needed, and ensure Calyste never again sees Beatrix, warning that reviving old passions could ruin their marriage. Sabine felt conflicted; she could not feign caprice or oppose her true feelings and recognized her vulnerability.

Sabine’s letters trace her navigation of love and marriage: devotion mingled with insecurity, the struggle to reconcile affection with her husband’s lingering ties to another woman, and the challenge of balancing societal expectations with personal identity. The narrative captures the delicate interplay of love, duty, and selfhood within marriage, showing how careful management of emotion and circumstance is required to make such unions endure.

Guerande, July 1838.

To Madame la Duchesse de Grandlieu:

Ah, my dear mother! After three months, I know what jealousy is! My heart is torn between love and hatred. I feel betrayed and unloved. How fortunate I am to have you to confide in!

It’s enough for wives to hear: “Here’s a key to your palace; go everywhere, but avoid Les Touches!” to ignite our curiosity. Mademoiselle des Touches has sown bitterness in my heart. Why did she forbid me from visiting Les Touches? What happiness can I have if it hinges on a mere visit to a house in Brittany? Why should I fear? Is there anything to fear? This desire to know if my power is solid or fragile drove me to ask, “What sort of place is Les Touches?”

“Les Touches belongs to you,” my dear mother-in-law said.

“If Calyste had never set foot in Les Touches!” my aunt Zephirine exclaimed.

“He would not be my husband,” I replied.

“Then you know what happened there?” my mother-in-law asked slyly.

“It is a place of perdition!” Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel declared. “Mademoiselle des Touches committed many sins there.”

“But they saved her soul and enriched a convent,” the Chevalier du Halga interjected. “The Abbe Grimont told me she donated a hundred thousand francs to the nuns.”

“Would you like to visit Les Touches?” my mother-in-law asked. “It’s worth seeing.”

“No, no!” I hastily replied.

This little scene felt like a page from a diabolical drama, repeated under various pretexts. Eventually, my mother-in-law said, “I understand why you don’t go to Les Touches, and I think you’re right.”

You must admit, dear mother, that such an involuntary stab would compel anyone to question the stability of their happiness. To be fair, Calyste never suggested we visit that hermitage, now his property. But love makes us senseless, and his silence piqued my curiosity. So, I asked him, “What are you afraid of at Les Touches, that you never mention it?”

“Let’s go there,” he replied.

And so, I was caught, like other women who want to be caught, trusting chance to resolve my indecision. Les Touches is enchanting, profoundly artistic. I delighted in that place of horror where Mademoiselle des Touches had forbidden me to go. Poisonous flowers can be charming; the devil has flowers too. I studied Calyste, wanting to know if his passion was truly extinguished. I watched every expression on his face as he moved through the rooms, like a child searching for something hidden. Calyste seemed thoughtful, and I dared to mention Madame de Rochefide, whom I secretly called la Rocheperfide.

“She must be light indeed to have stayed there,” I laughed. Calyste remained silent, so I added, “We’ll respect the dead.”

Still, he was silent.

“Have I displeased you?” I asked.

“No; but cease to revive that passion,” he replied.

What a statement! When he saw my disappointment, he increased his care and tenderness.

August.

Alas! I was on the edge of a precipice, amusing myself like the innocent heroines of melodramas. Suddenly, a horrible thought struck me: Calyste’s love might be growing through his memories, that he was channeling the stormy emotions I stirred by reminding him of that hateful Beatrix. Just think of it! That cold, unhealthy nature dares to call itself Beatrix, Beatrice!

Already, dear mother, I am forced to keep one eye open to suspicion, even when my heart belongs entirely to Calyste. Isn’t it a catastrophe when suspicion overcomes love? It happened this way:

“This place is dear to me,” I said to Calyste one morning, “because I owe my happiness to it; so I forgive you for sometimes mistaking me for another woman.”

The loyal Breton blushed, and I embraced him. Yet, I have left Les Touches and will never return.

The very strength of my hatred for Madame de Rochefide—ah, a natural death, pleurisy, or some accident—makes me understand the depth of my love for Calyste. That woman haunts my dreams; will I ever encounter her? The postulant of the Visitation was right—Les Touches is a fatal spot. Calyste has revived his past emotions there, and they are more powerful than our joys. Please, dear mother, find out if Madame de Rochefide is in Paris; if she is, I will stay in Brittany. Poor Mademoiselle des Touches might regret her role in our marriage if she knew how much I am haunted by our odious rival! But this is degradation! I am ashamed of it all. Sometimes, I feel a frantic desire to flee from Guerande and the sands of Croisic.

August 25th.

I am determined to live in the ruins of the old chateau. Calyste, worried by my restlessness, agrees to take me. Either he knows so little about life that he suspects nothing, or he does know the cause of my flight, in which case he cannot love me. I tremble at the thought of finding the awful certainty I seek, so like a child, I cover my eyes to avoid the explosion.

Oh, mother! I am not loved as I love. Calyste is charming, it’s true! But what man, unless he were a monster, would not be amiable and gracious when receiving the affection of a pure, loving girl raised by you, as many women have said I am?

Guenic, September 18.

Has he forgotten her? That solitary thought echoes in my soul like remorse. Ah! dear mother, must all women struggle against memories as I do? Only innocent young men should marry pure young girls. But that’s a deceptive Utopia; better to have one’s rival in the past than in the future.

Ah! mother, pity me, though at this moment I am as happy as a woman who fears to lose her happiness and clings to it—one way of killing it, says that wise Clotilde.

I notice that for the last five months, I think only of myself, that is, of Calyste. Tell sister Clotilde that her melancholy wisdom often recurs to me. She is happy being faithful to the dead; she fears no rival. A kiss to my dear Athenais, about whom I see Juste is beside himself. From what you told me in your last letter, it’s evident he fears you will not give her to him. Cultivate that fear as a precious product. Athenais will be sovereign lady; but I, who fear I can never win Calyste back from himself, shall always be a servant.

A thousand tendernesses, dear mother. Ah! if my terrors are not delusions, Camille Maupin has sold me her fortune dearly. My affectionate respects to papa.

These letters reveal the secret relationship between husband and wife. Sabine envisioned a love marriage, while Calyste saw only a marriage of convenience. The joys of the honeymoon did not fully align with societal expectations.

During their stay in Brittany, the restoration and furnishing of the hotel du Guenic were overseen by the celebrated architect Grindot, under the supervision of Clotilde and the Duc and Duchesse de Grandlieu, with plans for the young couple to return to Paris in December 1838. Sabine settled into the rue de Bourbon with pleasure, not just to play the role of mistress of a grand household but to gauge her family’s opinion of her marriage.

Calyste, indifferent, allowed his sister-in-law Clotilde and mother-in-law the duchess to guide him in social matters, and they appreciated his compliance. He gained the social standing due to his name, fortune, and alliance. The success of his wife, regarded as one of the most charming women in Paris, the diversions of high society, and the duties of the season brought fresh life to their young household, creating a series of excitements and interludes. Sabine, considered happy by her mother and sister, dismissed her gloomy thoughts, hearing her lot envied by many unhappy wives, until her pregnancy provided additional assurances to this neutral union, which experienced women often viewed positively. In October 1839, the young Baronne du Guenic had a son and mistakenly decided to nurse him herself, believing that a mother should be wholly devoted to her child.

By the end of the following summer, in August 1840, Sabine was nearing the end of her nursing period. Calyste, during his two years in Paris, had shed the innocence that once adorned his early experiences in the world of passion. He now mingled with the young Duc Georges de Maufrigneuse, recently married to an heiress, Berthe de Cinq-Cygne, and the Vicomte Savinien de Portenduere, among others, fully grasping the differences between Parisian life and provincial life. Wealth brings idle hours, which Paris knows how to amuse and charm. Contact with young husbands who abandoned noble women for the pleasures of cigars, whist, and clubs gradually undermined the domestic virtues of the young Breton noble. A wife’s motherly concern, eager not to tire her husband, often supports the dissipations of young men. A wife is proud to see her husband return after granting him full freedom.

One evening in October, to escape the cries of their newly weaned child, Calyste, whose frown Sabine could not bear, went, urged by her, to the Varietes for a new play. The footman engaged a stall near the avant-scene. During the first interlude, Calyste spotted Madame de Rochefide in a proscenium box, not ten steps away. Beatrix in Paris! Beatrix in public! The shock of seeing her again after nearly three years struck him like an arrow. He had not forgotten the past; sometimes, he had even substituted Beatrix for his wife, which Sabine had noticed. Beatrix represented light, life, and the Unknown, while Sabine embodied duty, dullness, and the expected. It was like a thunderbolt striking him.

Out of loyalty, Calyste’s first instinct was to leave the theatre. As he exited the orchestra stalls, he noticed the proscenium box door ajar, and despite himself, he approached. He found Beatrix between two distinguished men, Canalis and Raoul Nathan. In the three years since he last saw her, Madame de Rochefide had changed dramatically; her transformation, while affecting her as a woman, made her even more poetic and attractive to Calyste.

“Why isn’t Conti here?” Calyste asked Canalis in a low voice after exchanging pleasantries.

The former great poet, now a cabinet minister and orator, raised a finger to his lips, signaling silence.

“I’m happy to see you,” Beatrix said demurely. “When I recognized you just now, I thought you at least would not disown me. Ah! my Calyste,” she whispered, “why did you marry?—and with such a little fool!”

When a woman whispers to a newcomer and invites him to sit beside her, men often excuse themselves from the conversation.

“Come, Nathan,” Canalis said, “Madame la marquise will allow me to speak to d’Arthez, whom I see over there with the Princesse de Cadignan; it concerns business in the Chamber tomorrow.”

This departure gave Calyste time to recover from the shock, but he nearly lost his composure again as he inhaled the entrancing yet venomous perfume of Beatrix. Now gaunt and bony, her complexion faded, her eyes hollow, she had brightened her premature ruins with clever contrivances of Parisian artifice. She had taken to adopting a virgin air, recalling the maidens of Ossian, poetically painted by Girodet.

“Why is Conti not here?” Calyste asked.

“Ah! you forget me!” she replied. “But you are married. That was one of my griefs since I last saw you. Not only do I lose love, but I lose a friendship I thought was Breton. Alas! we can bear everything. Now I suffer less, but I am broken! This is the first time I’ve poured out my heart in ages. I must seem proud before indifferent people, and I’ve lost my dear Felicite; there’s no one to whom I can say, I suffer! But to you, I can share my anguish at seeing you so near. Yes,” she said, responding to Calyste’s gesture, “it’s almost fidelity. A look, a visit, a mere nothing means everything to us. Ah! you once loved me—loved me as I deserved to be loved by him who has trampled on the treasures I poured out on him. Yet, to my sorrow, I cannot forget; I love and wish to be faithful to a past that can never return.”

After this tirade, she played with her eyes to amplify the effect of her words, which seemed to flow from her soul like a long-restrained torrent. Calyste, unable to speak, let tears gather in his eyes. Beatrix took his hand and pressed it, making him pale.

“Thank you, Calyste; that’s how a true friend responds to grief. We understand each other. No, don’t add another word; leave me now; people are watching; it might trouble your wife if someone tells her we were seen together—innocently enough, before a thousand people! There, you see I am strong; adieu—”

She wiped her eyes, making what could be called an antithesis of action.

“Let me laugh the laugh of a lost soul with the careless creatures who amuse me,” she continued. “I live among artists and writers, in the world I knew in the salon of our poor Camille—who may indeed have acted wisely. To enrich the man we love and then disappear saying, ‘I am too old for him!’ that is the best end, if one cannot die a virgin.”

She began to laugh, as if to dispel the melancholy impression she had made on her former lover.

“But,” Calyste said, “where can I see you?”

“I am hidden in the rue de Chartres opposite the Parc de Monceaux, in a little house suitable to my means; and I cram my head with literature—but only for myself, to distract my thoughts; God keep me from the mania of literary women! Now go, leave me; I must not allow the world to talk about me; what will it say on seeing us together? Adieu—oh! Calyste, my friend, if you stay another minute, I shall burst into tears!”

Calyste withdrew, after holding out his hand to Beatrix and feeling that strange sensation of a double pressure—full of seductive tingling.

“Sabine never knew how to stir my soul in that way,” he thought as he left the theatre.

The rest of the evening, the Marquise de Rochefide did not cast three straight glances at Calyste, but many sidelong looks tore at the soul of the man now wholly thrown back into his first, repulsed love.

When the baron du Guenic returned home, the splendor of his apartments reminded him of the mediocrity Beatrix had mentioned, and he hated his wealth because it could not belong to that fallen angel. When informed that Sabine had long been in bed, he rejoiced to find himself rich in the possession of a night to relive his emotions. He cursed the power of divination love had bestowed upon Sabine. When a man is adored by his wife, she reads his face like a book; she learns every quiver of its muscles, knows the source of its calmness, and questions the reason for the slightest sadness, seeking if it lies with her. She studies his eyes; for her, they reveal whether he loves or not. Calyste knew he was the object of such deep, naive, and jealous worship that he doubted his ability to maintain a cautious demeanor that wouldn’t betray his moral change.

“How shall I manage tomorrow morning?” he wondered as he fell asleep, dreading the scrutiny Sabine would impose. When they met at night, and sometimes during the day, Sabine would ask, “Do you still love me?” or “I don’t weary you, do I?” Charming questions, varied according to the cleverness of women, which hide their anxieties, whether feigned or real.

The noblest and purest hearts cannot escape the mud and slime cast up by hurricanes. The next morning, Calyste, who certainly loved his child, felt joy when he learned that Sabine was watching for signs of slight convulsions in their son, fearing the croup and not daring to leave him. The baron made a pretext of business and went out, avoiding breakfast at home. He escaped like a prisoner, happy to be free to walk along the Pont Louis XVI and the Champs Elysees to a café on the boulevard where he used to breakfast as a bachelor.

What is love? Does Nature rebel against social constraints? Does she need the impulse of life to be spontaneous, free, like an impetuous torrent foaming against rocks of opposition and coquetry, rather than a tranquil stream flowing between the banks of church and legal ceremony?

It would be hard to find a young man more sacredly raised than Calyste, of purer morals, less stained by irreligion; yet he was drawn to a woman unworthy of him, when a benign chance had given him a wife of true aristocratic beauty, keen intellect, and angelic sweetness, a love that was passionate despite marriage, like his for Beatrix. Perhaps the noblest men retain some clay in their constitutions; the slough still pleases them. If this is so, the least imperfect human being is the woman, despite her faults and lack of reason. Madame de Rochefide, amid the circle of poetic pretensions surrounding her, belonged to the highest nobility; she presented a nature more ethereal than slimy, hiding the courtesan she was meant to be beneath an aristocratic exterior. Therefore, this explanation does not fully account for Calyste’s strange passion.

Perhaps we should look for its cause in a buried vanity that moralists have yet to uncover. There are noble men like Calyste, handsome, rich, distinguished, who tire—perhaps unknowingly—of marriage with a nature like their own; beings whose own nobleness does not surprise or move them; who seek from inferior or fallen natures the seal of their superiority—if they do not openly seek praise. Calyste found nothing to protect in Sabine; she was irreproachable. The powers stagnant in his heart were now to vibrate for Beatrix. If great men have played the Saviour’s part toward the woman taken in adultery, why should ordinary men be wiser in their generation?

Calyste spent the afternoon living on one thought: “I shall see her again!”—a sentiment that has often justified a journey of two thousand miles. He went with a light step to the rue de Chartres, recognizing the house at once, though he had never seen it. Once there, he stood at the foot of the staircase, halted by the old footman’s question: “Monsieur’s name?” Calyste felt he should leave Beatrix her freedom to receive or not receive him; he waited, gazing into the garden, its walls marked by rain’s black and yellow lines on the stucco of Paris.

Madame de Rochefide, like many great ladies who break their chains, had left her fortune to her husband when she fled; she could not beg from her tyrant. Conti and Mademoiselle des Touches had spared Beatrix the petty worries of material life, and her mother frequently sent her considerable sums. Now on her own, she faced a severe economy, quite different from the luxury she was accustomed to. She had taken refuge in a “little house” formerly belonging to a great lord, standing on the street but with a charming garden, rented for eighteen hundred francs. Still served by an old footman, a maid, and a cook from Alencon, who remained loyal through her trials, her penury would have been opulence to many an ambitious bourgeoise.

Calyste ascended a well-pumiced staircase, the landings adorned with flowering plants. On the first floor, the old servant opened a double door of red velvet with lozenges of red silk studded with gilt nails. Silk and velvet furnished the rooms he passed through. The dark red carpet, garnet velvet walls with gold trimmings, and conservatory-like windows filled with flowers created a dimly lit salon. Calyste could barely see two cases of old celadon on the mantel, between which gleamed a silver cup attributed to Benvenuto Cellini, brought from Italy by Beatrix. The gilded wood furniture, magnificent consoles, and a table with Persian cloth all testified to former opulence, well applied. On a little table, Calyste noticed jeweled knick-knacks, a book in progress, with a dagger’s handle used as a paper-cutter—symbol of criticism! Finally, ten richly framed watercolors on the walls depicted the various bedrooms where Madame de Rochefide’s wandering life had led her, showcasing her superior impertinence.

The rustle of a silk dress announced the unfortunate woman, appearing in a studied outfit that would have told a roué her visitor was awaited. The gown, resembling a wrapper to showcase her white bosom, was of pearl-gray moire with large open sleeves, from which emerged arms covered with a second sleeve of puffed tulle, divided by straps and trimmed with lace. Her beautiful hair, held insecurely by a lace and flower cap, framed her elongated face.

“Already!” she said, smiling. “A lover could not have shown more eagerness. You must have secrets to tell me, have you not?”

She gracefully posed on a sofa, inviting Calyste to sit beside her. By chance (perhaps a selected chance, for women have two memories, that of angels and devils), Beatrix was redolent of the perfume she used at Les Touches during her first acquaintance with Calyste. The scent, the contact with her dress, the glance of her eyes, which gathered and returned light in the dimness, turned Calyste’s head. He was again impelled to that violence that had once nearly cost Beatrix her life; but this time, she was on the edge of a sofa, not a rock; she rose to ring the bell, placing a finger on his lips. Calyste, recalled to order, controlled himself, especially since Beatrix had no hostile intentions.

“Antoine, I am not at home—for anyone,” she said. “Put some wood on the fire. You see, Calyste, I treat you as a friend,” she continued with dignity, “therefore do not treat me as you would a mistress. I have two remarks to make. First, I would not deny myself foolishly to any man I truly loved; and secondly, I am determined to belong to no other man on earth, for I believed, Calyste, that I was loved by a species of Rizzio, who was untrammeled by any engagement, and you see to what that fatal confidence has led me. As for you, you are now under the most sacred of duties; you have a young, amiable, delightful wife; moreover, you are a father. I should be, as you are, without excuse—we would be two fools—”

“My dear Beatrix, all these reasons vanish before a single word—I have never loved anyone but you on earth, and I was married against my will.”

“Ah! a trick played upon us by Mademoiselle des Touches,” she said, smiling.

Three hours passed, during which Madame de Rochefide held Calyste to the consideration of conjugal faith, pointing out the horrible alternative of utter renunciation of Sabine. She affected to regard the sacrifice of Sabine as a small matter, knowing her so well!

“My dear child,” she said, “that’s a woman who fulfills all the promises of her girlhood. She is a Grandlieu, to be sure, but she’s as brown as her mother the Portuguese, not to say yellow, and as dry and stiff as her father. To tell the truth, your wife will never go wrong; she’s a big girl who can take care of herself. Poor Calyste! Is that the sort of woman you needed? She has fine eyes, but such eyes are very common in Italy, Spain, and Portugal. Can any woman be tender with bones like hers? Eve was fair; brown women descend from Adam, blondes come from the hand of God, which left upon Eve his last thought after creating her.”

Around six o’clock, driven to desperation, Calyste took his hat to leave.

“Yes, go, my poor friend,” she said; “don’t give her the annoyance of dining without you.”

Calyste stayed. At his age, it was easy to ensnare him on his worst side.

“What! You dare to dine with me?” Beatrix said, feigning surprise. “My poor food does not alarm you? Have you enough independence of soul to crown me with joy by this little proof of your affection?”

“Let me write a note to Sabine; otherwise, she will wait for dinner until nine o’clock.”

“Here,” Beatrix said, “this is the table at which I write.”

She lit the candles herself and took one to the table to look over what he was writing.

“My dear Sabine—”

“‘My dear’? Can you really say that your wife is still dear to you?” she asked, looking at him with a cold eye that froze the marrow in his bones. “Go—you had better go and dine with her.”

“_I dine at a restaurant with some friends._”

“A lie. Oh, fy! You are not worthy to be loved either by her or by me. Men are all cowards in their treatment of women. Go, monsieur, go and dine with your dear Sabine.”

Calyste flung himself back in his armchair, pale as death. Bretons possess a natural courage that makes them obstinate under difficulties. Presently, the young baron sat up, put his elbow on the table, his chin in his hand, and looked at the implacable Beatrix with a flashing eye. He was so superb that a Northern or Southern woman would have fallen at his feet saying, “Take me!” But Beatrix, born on the borders of Normandy and Brittany, belonged to the race of Casterans; desertion had developed in her the ferocity of the Frank, the spitefulness of the Norman; she wanted some terrible notoriety as vengeance and yielded to no weakness.

“Dictate what I ought to write,” said the unfortunate man. “But, in that case—”

“Well, yes!” she said, “you shall love me then as you loved me at Guerande. Write: _I dine out; do not expect me._”

“What next?” Calyste asked, thinking something more would follow.

“Nothing; sign it. Good,” she said, seizing the note with restrained joy. “I will send it by a messenger.”

“And now,” cried Calyste, rising like a happy man.

“Ah! I believe I have kept my freedom of action,” she said, turning away from him and going to the fireplace, where she rang the bell. “Here, Antoine,” she said when the old footman entered, “send this note to its address. Monsieur dines here.”

Calyste returned home around two in the morning. After waiting until half-past twelve, Sabine had gone to bed, overwhelmed with fatigue. She slept, though keenly distressed by the terse wording of her husband’s note. Still, she rationalized it. The true love of a woman begins by explaining everything to the advantage of the man she loves, she thought.

The next morning, the child was better; the mother’s unease subsided, and Sabine came with a smiling face, little Calyste in her arms, to present him to his father before breakfast with the silly words and gestures that young mothers often use. This scene allowed Calyste to maintain a pleasant demeanor. He was charming to his wife, thinking in his heart that he was a monster, and he played with their son too well—he overdid it; but Sabine had not reached the stage where a woman recognizes such delicate distinctions.

At breakfast, however, she suddenly asked him:

“What did you do yesterday?”

“Portenduere kept me to dinner,” he replied, “and after that, we went to the club to play whist.”

“That’s a foolish life, my Calyste,” Sabine said. “Young noblemen should busy themselves with recovering the ground lost by their fathers. It isn’t by smoking cigars, playing whist, idling away their leisure, and saying insolent things about parvenus who have driven them from their positions, nor by separating themselves from the masses whose soul and intellect they ought to embody, that the nobility will endure. Instead of being a party, you will soon be a mere opinion, as de Marsay said. Ah! if you only knew how my ideas on this subject have expanded since I have nursed and cradled your child! I’d like to see that grand old name of Guenic become historical again!” Then, suddenly locking her eyes onto Calyste’s, who listened pensively, she added, “Admit that the first note you ever wrote me was rather stiff.”

“I didn’t think of sending you word until I got to the club.”

“But you wrote on a woman’s note-paper; it had a perfume of feminine elegance.”

“Those club directors are such dandies!”

The Vicomte de Portenduere and his wife, formerly Mademoiselle Mirouet, had become very intimate with the du Guenics, sharing their box at the Opera. The two young women, Ursula and Sabine, had formed this friendship through delightful exchanges of counsel, care, and confidences regarding their first infants.

While Calyste, a novice in falsehood, thought, “I must warn Savinien,” Sabine was pondering, “I am sure that paper bore a coronet.” This thought flashed through her mind, and she scolded herself for having made it. Nevertheless, she resolved to find the paper, which she had flung into her letter-box amid her nighttime terrors.

After breakfast, Calyste went out, telling his wife he would return soon. He jumped into a low carriage, which was just beginning to replace the inconvenient cabriolet of their ancestors. He drove to the vicomte’s house, asking him to fib if Sabine questioned the vicomtesse. Then, urging his coachman to speed, he rushed to the rue de Chartres to learn how Beatrix had spent the rest of the night. He found her just after her bath, fresh, embellished, and breakfasting with a good appetite. He admired the grace with which his angel ate her boiled eggs and marveled at the beauty of the gold service, a gift from a monomaniac lord for whom Conti had composed a few ballads on his ideas, which the lord later published as his own!

Calyste listened entranced to the witty remarks of his idol, whose main goal was to amuse him, until she grew angry and wept when he rose to leave. He thought he had been there only half an hour, but it was past three when he returned home. His handsome English horse, a gift from the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu, was so bathed in sweat that it looked as if it had been driven through the sea. By one of those chances that jealous women create for themselves, Sabine was at a window overlooking the courtyard, anxious at Calyste’s absence. The condition of the horse, with its foaming mouth, surprised her.

“Where can he have come from?”

The question whispered in her ear by that power which is neither consciousness, nor devil, nor angel; which sees, forebodes, shows us the unseen, and creates belief in mental beings, creatures born of our brains, going and coming in the invisible world of ideas.

“Where do you come from, dear angel?” Sabine asked Calyste, meeting him on the first landing of the staircase. “Abd-el-Kader is nearly foundered. You told me you would be gone but a moment, and I have been waiting for you for three hours.”

“Well, well,” thought Calyste, who was improving in dissimulation, “I must get out of this with a present—”

“Dear little mother,” he said, taking her waist with more cajolery than he would have used if he hadn’t felt guilty, “I see it’s impossible to keep a secret, however innocent, from the woman who loves us—”

“Well, don’t tell secrets on the staircase,” she laughed. “Come in.”

In the salon adjoining their bedroom, she caught sight of Calyste’s face in the mirror, where he unwittingly allowed his real feelings and weariness to show.

“Now for your secret?” she said, turning around.

“You have shown such heroism as a nurse,” he said, “that the heir presumptive of the Guenics is dearer to me than ever, and I wanted to surprise you, just like any bourgeois of the rue Saint Denis. They are finishing a dressing-table for you at this moment, at which true artists have worked, and my mother and aunt Zephirine have contributed.”

Sabine clasped him in her arms, holding him tightly to her breast with her head on his neck, faint with the weight of happiness—not for the piece of furniture, but for dispelling her first dark doubt. It was one of those magnificent moments that can be counted, and which no love, however excessive, can prodigally spend, or life would soon burn out. Then, indeed, men should fall at the feet of women to adore them, for such moments are sublime, when the forces of heart and intellect gush forth like waters from sculptured nymphs.

Suddenly, as if bitten by a viper, she left Calyste, threw herself on a sofa, and fainted, for the reaction of a chill to her glowing heart nearly killed her. As she held Calyste, her nose at his cravat, abandoned to joy, she smelled the perfume of that letter paper! Another woman’s head had lain there, whose hair and face had left that adulterous odor! She had just kissed the spot where the kisses of her rival were still warm.

“What is the matter?” Calyste asked, bringing her back to consciousness with a damp cloth and salts.

“Fetch the doctor and my nurse, both! Yes, my milk has turned, I feel it. They won’t come at once unless you fetch them yourself—go!”

Calyste, alarmed, rushed out. The moment Sabine heard the closing of the porte-cochere, she sprang up like a frightened doe, walking about the salon as if beside herself, crying out, “My God! my God! my God!”

Those two words replaced all ideas. The crisis she had seized upon as a pretext truly took place. The hairs of her head felt like red-hot needles heated in the fire of a nervous fever. Her boiling blood seemed to mingle with her nerves, trying to escape from all her pores. She was blind for a moment, crying, “I am dying!”

At that terrible cry of the injured wife and mother, her maid rushed in. After she was laid upon her bed and regained both sight and mind, her first act of intelligence was to send the maid to her friend, Madame de Portenduere. Sabine felt her ideas whirl in her brain like straws in a waterspout. “I saw,” she said later, “myriads all at once.”

She rang for the footman and, in the throes of her fever, found the strength to write the following letter, driven by one mad desire—to have certainty:

To Madame la Baronne du Guenic:

Dear Mamma,—When you come to Paris, as you allow us to hope you will, I shall thank you in person for the beautiful present by which you, my aunt Zephirine, and Calyste wish to reward me for doing my duty. I was already well repaid by my own happiness in doing it. I can never express the pleasure you have given me with that beautiful dressing-table, but when you are with me, I shall try to do so. Believe me, when I array myself before that treasure, I shall think, like the Roman matron, that my noblest jewel is our little angel, etc.

She directed the letter to Guerande and gave it to the footman to post.

When the Vicomtesse de Portenduere arrived, the shuddering chill of reaction had succeeded Sabine’s first paroxysm of madness.

“Ursula, I think I am going to die,” she said.

“What is the matter, dear?”

“Where did Savinien and Calyste go after they dined with you yesterday?”

“Dined with me?” Ursula replied, surprised. “Savinien and I dined alone together and went to the Opera without Calyste.”

“Ursula, dearest, in the name of your love for Savinien, keep silence about what you have just said to me and what I shall now tell you. You alone shall know why I die—I am betrayed! at the end of three years, at twenty-two years of age!”

Her teeth chattered, her eyes dull and frozen, her face took on a greenish tinge.

“You! So beautiful! For whom?”

“I don’t know yet. But Calyste has told me two lies. Do not pity me, do not seem incensed, pretend ignorance, and perhaps you can find out who she is through Savinien. Oh! that letter of yesterday!”

Trembling, she sprang from her bed to retrieve the letter.

“See,” she said, lying down again, “the coronet of a marquise! Find out if Madame de Rochefide has returned to Paris. Am I to have a heart in which to weep and moan? Oh, dearest!—to see one’s beliefs, one’s poetry, idol, virtue, happiness, all, all in pieces, withered, lost! No God in the sky! no love on earth! no life in my heart! no anything! I don’t know if there’s daylight; I doubt the sun. I have such anguish in my soul that I scarcely feel the horrible sufferings in my body. Happily, the baby is weaned; my milk would have poisoned him.”

At that thought, tears began to flow from Sabine’s eyes, which had been dry until then.

Pretty Madame de Portenduere, holding the fatal letter, was initially stupefied by this true sorrow, shocked by this agony of love, without yet understanding it, despite Sabine’s incoherent attempts to explain the facts. Suddenly, Ursula was illuminated by one of those ideas that come only to sincere friends.

“I must save her!” she thought. “Trust me, Sabine,” she cried. “Wait for my return; I will find out the truth.”

“Ah! in my grave I’ll love you,” exclaimed Sabine.

The viscountess went straight to the Duchesse de Grandlieu, pledging her to secrecy, and then explained Sabine’s situation.

“Madame,” she said, “do you not think with me that in order to avoid some fatal illness—perhaps, I don’t know, even madness—we had better confide the whole truth to the doctor and invent some tale to clear that hateful Calyste and make him seem innocent for the time being?”

“My dear child,” the duchess replied, chilled by this confidence, “friendship has given you for the moment the experience of a woman of my age. I know how Sabine loves her husband; you are right, she might become insane.”

“Or lose her beauty, which would be worse,” the viscountess added.

“Let us go to her!” cried the duchess.

Fortunately, they arrived just before the famous accoucheur, Dommanget, the only physician Calyste had been able to find.

“Ursula has told me everything,” the duchess said to her daughter, “and you are mistaken. In the first place, Madame de Rochefide is not in Paris. As for what your husband did yesterday, my dear, I can tell you that he lost a great deal of money at cards, so that he does not even know how to pay for your dressing-table.”

“But that?” Sabine said, holding out the fatal letter.

“That!” the duchess laughed. “Why, that is written on Jockey Club paper; everybody writes nowadays on coroneted paper; even our stewards will soon be titled.”

The prudent mother threw the unlucky paper into the fire as she spoke.

When Calyste and Dommanget arrived, the duchess, having instructed the servants, was informed immediately. She left Sabine to the care of Madame de Portenduere and stopped the accoucheur and Calyste in the salon.

“Sabine’s life is at stake, monsieur,” she said to Calyste; “you have betrayed her for Madame de Rochefide.”

Calyste blushed, like a respectable girl caught in a fault.

“And,” the duchess continued, “as you do not know how to deceive, you have behaved so clumsily that Sabine has guessed the truth. But I have for the moment repaired your blunder. You do not wish my daughter’s death, I am sure—All this, Monsieur Dommanget, will put you on the track of her real illness and its cause. As for you, Calyste, an old woman like me understands your error, though she does not pardon it. Such pardons can only be earned by a lifetime of happiness. If you wish me to esteem you, you must, first, save my daughter; next, you must forget Madame de Rochefide; she is only worth having once. Learn to lie; have the courage of a criminal, and his impudence. I have just told a lie myself, and I shall have to do hard penance for that mortal sin.”

She then told the two men the lies she had invented. The clever physician, sitting at the bedside of his patient, studied her symptoms to find a remedy, while ordering measures that depended on rapid execution. Calyste, sitting at the foot of the bed, tried to convey tenderness in his gaze.

“So it was play that put those black circles around your eyes?” Sabine said to him in a weak voice.

The words made the doctor, the mother, and the viscountess tremble, and they exchanged covert glances. Calyste turned as red as a cherry.

“That’s what comes of nursing a child,” Dommanget said brutally but cleverly. “Husbands are lonely when separated from their wives, and they go to the club and play. But you needn’t worry about the thirty thousand francs Monsieur le baron lost last night—”

“Thirty thousand francs!” Ursula exclaimed, in a silly tone.

“Yes, I know it,” Dommanget replied. “They told me this morning at the house of the young Duchesse Berthe de Maufrigneuse that it was Monsieur de Trailles who won that money from you,” he added, turning to Calyste. “Why do you play with such men? Frankly, monsieur le baron, I can well believe you are ashamed of it.”

Seeing his mother-in-law, a pious duchess, the young viscountess, a happy woman, and the old accoucheur, a confirmed egotist, all three lying like a dealer in bric-a-brac, the kind and feeling Calyste understood the gravity of the danger, and two heavy tears rolled from his eyes, completely deceiving Sabine.

“Monsieur,” she said, sitting up in bed and glaring at Dommanget, “Monsieur du Guenic can lose thirty, fifty, a hundred thousand francs if it pleases him, without anyone having the right to think it wrong or lecture him. It is far better that Monsieur de Trailles should win his money than that we should win Monsieur de Trailles’.”

Calyste rose, took his wife around the neck, kissed her on both cheeks, and whispered:

“Sabine, you are an angel!”

Two days later, the young wife was thought to be out of danger, and the next day Calyste was at Madame de Rochefide’s, making a merit of his infamy.

“Beatrix,” he said, “you owe me happiness. I have sacrificed my poor little wife to you; she has discovered everything. That fatal paper on which you made me write bore your name and your coronet, which I never noticed—I saw only you! Fortunately, the ‘B’ was by chance effaced. But the perfume you left upon me and the lies I told have betrayed my happiness. Sabine nearly died from it; her milk went to her head; erysipelas set in, and she may bear the marks for the rest of her days.”

As Beatrix listened to this tirade, her face was due North, icy enough to freeze the Seine had she looked at it.

“So much the better,” she said; “perhaps it will whiten her for you.”

And Beatrix, now as hard as her bones, sharp as her voice, and harsh as her complexion, continued a series of atrocious sarcasms. There is no greater blunder than for a man to talk of his wife, if she is virtuous, to his mistress, unless it be to talk of his mistress, if she is beautiful, to his wife. But Calyste had not received that Parisian education we must call the politeness of passions. He knew neither how to lie to his wife nor how to tell his mistress the truth—two apprenticeships a man in his position must undertake to manage women. He was therefore compelled to employ all the power of passion to obtain from Beatrix a pardon, which she forced him to solicit for two hours; a pardon refused by an injured angel who raised her eyes to the ceiling to avoid seeing the guilty man, and who put forth reasons sacred to marquises in a voice quivering with tears that were furtively wiped with her handkerchief.

“To speak to me of your wife on the very day after my fall!” she cried. “Why did you not tell me she is a pearl of virtue? I know she thinks you handsome; pure depravity! I, I love your soul! For let me tell you, my friend, you are ugly compared to many shepherds on the Campagna of Rome,” etc.

Such speeches may surprise the reader, but they were part of a system profoundly meditated by Beatrix in this her third incarnation—for at each passion, a woman becomes another being and advances one step further into profligacy, the only word that properly describes the effect of experiences gained through such adventures. The Marquise de Rochefide had sat in judgment on herself before the mirror. Clever women are never deceived about themselves; they count their wrinkles, assist at the birth of their crow’s-feet, know themselves by heart, and even acknowledge it by the greatness of their efforts at preservation. Therefore, to successfully struggle against a splendid young woman, to carry away from her six triumphs a week, Beatrix resorted to the knowledge and science of courtesans. Without acknowledging to herself the baseness of this plan, led to employ such means by a Turkish passion for Calyste’s beauty, she resolved to make him think himself unpleasant, ugly, ill-made, and to behave as if she hated him. No system is more fruitful with conquering natures. To such natures, the presence of repugnance to be vanquished renews the triumph of the first day on all succeeding days. And it is something even better. It is flattery in the guise of dislike. A man then thinks to himself, “I am irresistible,” or “My love is all-powerful because it conquers her repugnance.” If you deny this principle, divined by all coquettes and courtesans throughout all social zones, you may as well reject all seekers after knowledge, all delvers into secrets, repulsed through years in their duel with hidden causes. Beatrix added to the use of contempt as a moral piston a constant comparison of her own poetic, comfortable home with the hotel du Guenic. All deserted wives who abandon themselves in despair also neglect their surroundings, so discouraged are they. On this, Madame de Rochefide counted, and soon began an underhand attack on the luxury of the faubourg Saint-Germain, which she characterized as stupid.

The scene of reconciliation, in which Beatrix made Calyste swear and reswear hatred for the wife, who, she said, was playing comedy, took place in a perfect bower where she displayed her graces amid ravishing flowers and rare plants of the costliest luxury. The science of nothings, the trifles of the day, she carried to excess. Fallen into a mortifying position through Conti’s desertion, Beatrix was determined to gain, at least, the fame that unprincipled conduct brings. The misfortune of the poor young wife, a rich and beautiful Grandlieu, should be her pedestal.

When a woman returns to ordinary life after nursing her first child, she often seems rejuvenated and charming. This phase of maternity brings freshness and vitality to young mothers. However, Sabine, on returning to her husband Calyste, finds he is not the man she married. Instead of feeling secure, she becomes anxious and seeks certainty about their relationship. That certainty comes in a letter from her mother hinting at Calyste's discontent and the presence of another woman, Beatrix.

Sabine's heart sinks as she reads the letter, which says nothing of Calyste's feelings. In despair she writes a note on the letter, signaling her awareness, and leaves it where Calyste will find it. He discovers it but ignores it, throwing the paper into the fire. Sabine is left in agony, convinced she has displeased him and humiliated her idol.

As days pass, Sabine's torment deepens. Beatrix, emboldened, writes to Calyste; Sabine, recognizing the perfume on the letter, realizes the extent of her rival's influence. In a moment of madness she dances with her child, seeking solace in motherhood's innocence, yet her heart remains heavy and she weeps alone when Calyste leaves to read Beatrix's message.

Her suffering is compounded by attempts to regain Calyste's affection. She decorates their home with flowers hoping to please him, but his indifference only deepens her despair. She becomes obsessed with understanding his feelings and comparing herself to Beatrix, trapped in jealousy and self-doubt. Her efforts to charm him with cooking and her appearance seem futile as Calyste remains unresponsive.

Despite her struggles, Sabine's love persists. She tries to embody the devoted wife's virtues, but her efforts bring disappointment. One evening, after failing to impress him, she succumbs to despair and tears off her beautiful dress in frustration.

Over time Sabine's health deteriorates under the weight of grief. The duchess, her mother, notices the decline and attempts to draw her away from the source of suffering. Sabine, however, stays silent, fearing interference in her marriage. Eventually she confides in her sister, speaking of anguish and asking that her child be cared for if she should die.

In vulnerability Sabine warns her sister Athenais about love's dangers, advising caution in marriage. She reflects that her virtues have become obstacles to happiness, lamenting that her straightforwardness and passion have not won Calyste's heart. Instead she feels forced toward the duplicity she believes necessary to retain his affection.

Concerned, the duchess seeks counsel from Abbe Brossette, a respected priest, revealing a plan to intervene in Calyste's attachment to Beatrix and restore her daughter's happiness. The abbe cautions against mixing spiritual authority with worldly intrigue but recognizes the seriousness of Sabine's plight.

The duchess proposes a scheme to persuade Beatrix to return to her husband, freeing Calyste. The abbe, intrigued, urges her to consider the moral implications and suggests that rather than deceit she should pursue a more honorable approach to resolve the situation.

Contemplating his advice, the duchess becomes determined to act for her daughter's sake, even if that means engaging in morally questionable measures. She resolves to seek a solution that will restore peace to the family and spare Sabine further suffering.

A storm was brewing for Monsieur de Rochefide, who, at that moment, was enjoying Parisian life as fully as he had with Beatrix. The duke thought it nearly impossible to disrupt such a satisfactory existence. To see how society treats men and women differently in similar situations, we must follow Rochefide's life after Beatrix left him—an example that might inspire women to remain at home and fight for their dignity, as Sabine du Guenic did.

After Beatrix's departure, Arthur de Rochefide, now an only child following his sister's death, became sole owner of the hôtel de Rochefide and inherited a large fortune from his father. His income reached a thousand francs a day, ample for his happiness. While Beatrix fulfilled maternal duties, Rochefide enjoyed his wealth without extravagance. His vanity, bolstered by good looks, let him dismiss women and borrow others' wit as his own. His military background lent a jovial tone to his conversation, making him seem witty to those around him.

As a husband he was pitied; society blamed Beatrix for leaving such a “good” man. He joined clubs and endorsed causes to distinguish himself through fashionable pursuits. His main indulgence was racehorses, managed by an English groom, costing him a sizable monthly sum. Despite his wealth, he lacked taste or intellect, relying largely on inherited fortune. After the downsides of marriage he welcomed bachelorhood again, claiming he was "born with a caul," destined for luck.

Rochefide's indifference to domestic life was evident: he rarely dined or slept at home. After several romantic escapades he married Madame Schontz, a woman of dubious reputation known in circles around Fanny Beaupré and others. That world—filled with women who often ended in poverty or early death—required a nuanced grasp of social dynamics. Madame Schontz, originally La Petite-Aurélie, emerged from a difficult upbringing, having lost her parents and been educated at Saint-Denis, a school that schooled girls but offered few prospects.

Aurelie, the daughter of Colonel Schiltz, led a tumultuous life marked by various relationships, which brought her by chance to Rochefide at Valentino’s. Captivated by her charm, Rochefide married her two years after Beatrix left, and society did not condemn him for the choice.

Rochefide's relationship with Madame Schontz unfolded in four phases. The first was courtship: he provided a modest allowance and a furnished apartment. As he learned her character he found her noble and charming; she revealed talents he admired, though ambition soon surfaced and she sought a more comfortable life.

In the second phase Madame Schontz moved to a better apartment; Rochefide lavished gifts and financial support. She ran their household efficiently, impressing him with budgeting and superior dinners. This domestic competence convinced Rochefide she loved him for himself, since she rebuffed wealthier suitors.

The third phase cast Madame Schontz as mother figure to Rochefide's son, overseeing his education and finances. She advised Rochefide on investments, producing significant gains she did not claim. Taking on more responsibilities, she became integral to his life—managing affairs and ensuring his comfort.

In the fourth phase she established herself socially, curating her guest list and preserving reputation. She amassed a small fortune through savings and investments, supplementing it by wearing and selling jewelry. Despite financial success she downplayed her wealth, framing the expenses of maintaining her lifestyle.

Through these phases Madame Schontz revealed tactical superiority, transforming the relationship into mutual benefit while navigating the complexities of their social world.

Such conduct implied a plan, and Madame Schontz had one. Jealous of Madame du Bruel for two years, she was determined to marry in both church and civil ceremonies. This ambition required a second Arthur, but no one had yet uncovered Rochefide’s secret rival. Bixiou suspected Leon de Lora, the painter thought Bixiou himself was favored. Others, like Victor de Vernisset, a poet, and Stidmann, a young sculptor, were also considered potential rivals. However, none truly understood Madame Schontz, who was focused solely on securing a good marriage.

The only man of questionable reputation in her salon was Couture, a stock-gambler who had fallen from grace. Despite his failures, Couture remained loyal, and when he introduced her to a provincial man, Fabien du Ronceret, Madame Schontz began to contemplate her options. Fabien, the son of a chief-justice, had left his judgeship in Alencon to seek fame in Paris, carrying a modest income and a desire to exploit changing social mores.

The modern era had fostered pride, vanity, and ambition, where people sought recognition at any cost. Fabien, not particularly clever, grasped this shift and aimed to make a name for himself. He believed success depended on being talked about, and he pursued attention through extravagant living and connections.

Fabien’s Paris debut was marked by ostentation: a lavish apartment and a greenhouse, not for horticulture but to attract notice. He sought association with influential figures and entry into Madame Schontz’s circle. After several failed attempts through established acquaintances, he turned to Couture, who recognized Fabien’s potential and encouraged Madame Schontz to consider him.

Madame Schontz quickly assessed Fabien and saw a suitable candidate for her ambitions. She flattered him, leading him to suppose he was the object of her affection. Fabien, eager to impress, presented himself in his finest attire, captivated by her charm. A month before a significant meeting, Madame Schontz revealed her true identity and aims to Fabien, who failed to grasp her intentions.

Realizing his misunderstanding, Madame Schontz took Fabien for a carriage ride, expressing her desire to marry a man of ambition who could appreciate her character. Despite her wealth and his encouragement to rise, Fabien hesitated at the prospect of marrying her, and she turned her attention to Couture, who quickly proposed upon learning of her fortune.

Meanwhile the Duchesse de Grandlieu sought the Comte Maxime de Trailles’s help to navigate a delicate family situation. Maxime, a seasoned political player, understood the motivations behind the duchess’s invitation and agreed to assist, but insisted on conditions, including acceptance of his future wife into the duchess’s social circle.

Maxime’s plan included removing a rival from the picture, requiring the duchess to follow his instructions closely. He acknowledged the emotional entanglements involved—especially Calyste’s deep infatuation with Beatrix—and recognized the challenges of manipulating such feelings. His understanding of love and manipulation revealed the complexities of the relationships at play as he prepared to execute his scheme.

As the duchess agreed to support Maxime’s wife, he assured her he would handle the intricacies, albeit with a hint of moral ambiguity. The conversation underscored the lengths individuals would go to secure desires, often at the expense of others’ feelings and reputations.

The next day Rochefide informed Madame Schontz of Maxime’s impending visit, prompting her to prepare her home and appearance to impress him. Maxime’s reputation as a powerful figure in Parisian society preceded him, and his presence was both a privilege and a challenge for Madame Schontz, who sought to navigate the complexities of her ambitions and relationships.

The next day Maxime de Trailles summoned Finot to arrange a breakfast at the Café Anglais with Couture and Lousteau, where they discussed Madame Schontz. Maxime, while pretending to transact with du Tillet on Tortoni’s portico, awaited the Comte de la Palferine. La Palferine wryly acknowledged his financial straits, and Maxime, admiring his spirit, offered to help him woo a woman, promising to lend twenty thousand francs for the campaign.

Maxime’s scheme centered on a well-bred marquise; he urged La Palferine to court her sincerely. They agreed to meet later at Madame Schontz’s. Meanwhile Maxime visited the duchess, who fretted over her daughter Sabine’s worry about Calyste’s absence. He asked her to keep his plans secret and requested twenty thousand francs for his maneuver, assuring her it wouldn’t require undue expense.

Madame Schontz prepared a dinner, inviting prominent guests including Claude Vignon and La Palferine. She dressed carefully to impress. Maxime praised her success with Rochefide, noting how she had cost him less than other women. Madame Schontz insisted she was an artist, not a courtesan, and expressed her desire to make a good marriage.

At dinner Maxime conferred with La Palferine, giving him the promised funds and instructions for conquering the marquise. He stressed the need for a striking impression. As the evening continued, Maxime and Madame Schontz plotted to manipulate Rochefide’s affections; Maxime vowed to secure a position for Couture in return for his cooperation.

The next day Calyste, feeling neglected, confronted Beatrix, who was torn between her genuine feelings for him and her growing attraction to La Palferine. Their relationship tightened into strain as Beatrix grappled with guilt and desire, fearful of consequences. La Palferine’s charm and wit drew her, making her question loyalty to Calyste.

Maxime intensified his scheming, arranging encounters between Beatrix and La Palferine that complicated the dynamics. Calyste, increasingly desperate, tried to reclaim Beatrix’s affection, but she remained entangled by La Palferine’s allure. Tension peaked with a public humiliation for Beatrix at the opera, where she was left exposed and alone.

Afterward Beatrix’s attachment to Calyste began to wane as she sank deeper into La Palferine’s orbit. Maxime, watching the drama unfold, perceived shifting loyalties and the emotional turmoil of all involved. He counseled Calyste to reconsider his tactics, suggesting that the pain she caused might not be worth enduring.

As events progressed, characters navigated conflicting desires and ambitions, prompting confrontations and revelations. Beatrix’s internal conflict deepened; her relationship with La Palferine grew more complicated as she struggled with identity and social expectations.

Ultimately the narrative examines love, betrayal, and the complexity of human relationships, as characters strive to reconcile passion with reality. The interplay of ambition and affection drives the plot, producing disillusionments that leave them questioning past choices and futures.
