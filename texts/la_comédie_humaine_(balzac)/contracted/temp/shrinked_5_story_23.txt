Balzac's works, rich in character and detail, don't depict real individuals. In "Beatrix" he borrows traits from contemporaries: Mademoiselle des Touches reflects George Sand, while Beatrix and Claude Vignon draw on the Comtesse d’Agoult and Liszt.

Brittany's towns, like Guerande, remain largely untouched by modernity, preserving historical customs. Guerande exemplifies this with medieval architecture and streets, where a dwindling population wears traditional Breton costumes. The town's economy depends on salt production, which sustains a nostalgic atmosphere.

The du Guaisnic mansion symbolizes the town's noble past and belongs to the ancient Guaisnic family, now weakened by debt. Despite diminished means, they keep their pride. The mansion's grand entrance, fine woodwork, and kept garden reflect the family's heritage and contrast with modern construction.

Balzac emphasizes the bond between environment and inhabitants, showing how history shapes identity. "Beatrix" captures a fading world in which remnants of feudalism and Breton culture persist amid modern change.

In May 1836 the Guenic household comprised Baron du Guenic, his wife Fanny O’Brien, sister Mademoiselle Zephirine, and son Calyste. The baron, a Vendee wars veteran, returned to Guerande in 1813. Blind but devoted, Zephirine managed the household, while family conversation centered on the exiled Bourbons. Each member embodied commitment to heritage, preserving their legacy despite mounting challenges.

As night fell, Gasselin approached the baron and was allowed to leave after prayers. The baron, his sister, and Madame du Guenic knelt in devotion and were visited by the local priest, Monsieur Grimont, who asked after the baroness's health and noted Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel's lateness. The servant Mariotte cautioned against mentioning the chevalier's visits to Les Touches in Pen-Hoel's presence to avoid gossip.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, an elderly woman in a green bonnet, ran her estate so frugally that her stinginess commanded respect. Though wealthy, the proud du Guenics had not sought her fortune; she hoped to marry her niece to Calyste du Guenic to recover lost family lands. Rector Grimont revealed she had recently donated a large sum to the royal cause, demonstrating loyalty. She treated Calyste with entitlement, assuming her designs on his future justified overseeing him. Not opposed to romance, she nonetheless disapproved of modern morals and favored traditional values.

As the evening progressed she asked whether Monsieur du Halga would attend; the rector confirmed, and the group anticipated a game of mouche, a card game that exposes human greed and strategy. Stakes rose further with the arrival of Demoiselle de Kergarouet and her aunt.

Conversation turned to personal matters: the chevalier's health and the recent death of the Duc de Lenoncourt. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel worried about Calyste's attachment to Mademoiselle des Touches, an actress of dubious reputation; the rector warned that the actress's influence might mislead Calyste, while the baroness lamented her son's apparent loss of innocence. The baroness grew anxious about his frequent absences and feared modern ideas would corrupt him. Raised under strict guidance, Calyste was expected to marry a wealthy heiress to restore the family's fortunes; his infatuation jeopardized those plans.

When Calyste returned, the baroness felt joy tinged with unease, noticing his fatigue and suspecting he concealed something. She hesitated to confront him about Mademoiselle des Touches, dreading the possible loss of her son to love. His sheltered upbringing emphasized traditional education, but his encounters with the actress had exposed him to new ideas that unsettled his family.

The next day Calyste overslept and was indulged by his mother. As they prepared for dinner the baroness pressed her concerns about his visits to Les Touches; Calyste downplayed them as youthful escapades, said Mademoiselle des Touches had rejected his advances, and defended her character. Still unconvinced, the baroness insisted a woman's conduct should meet societal expectations and worried about the gulf between appearances and propriety.

Mademoiselle Félicité des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, was a celebrated writer whose unconventional life provoked gossip. Orphaned and raised in a convent, she developed intellect and a passion for literature and rose in Parisian society. Rejecting conventional roles, Camille sought creative freedom and navigated complex relations with men; her plays challenged norms and her Paris salon became a center for artists and intellectuals. Despite independence and acclaim, she struggled with pressures around marriage and motherhood—complications that shape her bond with Calyste and embody the conflict between societal expectations and personal desire.

A few hundred yards from Guérande, Brittany gives way to salt-marshes and dunes, reaching Les Touches, the country house of Félicité. Built of slaty stone and granite, the estate overlooks the marshes and sea and yields income despite losing feudal rights in the Revolution. Félicité restored the house but preserved its desolate aspect, adding only decorative touches at the entrance.

The ground floor contains a billiard room, salon, and simply furnished dining room; upstairs is Félicité’s suite, furnished with luxurious Louis XV pieces, while guest rooms remain modest. The melancholy landscape inspires a feeling of sublime longing.

Calyste, a young man captivated by Félicité, finds solace at Les Touches, where art and music awaken him. His admiration deepens despite her rejections. She teaches him music, offering him an escape from his dull life in Guérande.

One day Calyste hears Félicité playing the piano and finds her in tears, awaiting Claude Vignon. He offers to search for Claude, but she urges him to stay, revealing her turmoil about love and relationships and lamenting her lack of deep affection. She reflects on her age difference with Calyste and on having once rejected him, admitting some selfishness. She hopes to inspire greatness in Claude and suggests Calyste test Claude’s feelings for her by intoxicating him at dinner.

Moved by her vulnerability, Calyste offers to return his fortune to her heirs if she cannot accept his love. Overwhelmed, Félicité prepares a narghile and tea, inviting an intimate moment.

The narrative then shifts to Béatrix-Maximilienne-Rose de Casteran, the Marquise de Rochefide, who struggles against social expectations and an unsatisfactory marriage. Despite talent and attractions, she feels constrained and becomes enamored of the composer Gennaro Conti, beginning a passionate affair. Traveling through Italy, she writes to Camille about her struggle for personal freedom under societal pressure. Meanwhile Calyste, torn between provincial upbringing and Parisian allure, becomes infatuated with Béatrix, complicating relations among the characters.

Eager but anxious to meet Madame de Rochefide, Calyste leaves early to avoid detection by his perceptive family. At the shore he sees boats from Croisic, one carrying luggage labeled with the marquise’s name, heightening his desire to connect with her. Overhearing a maid, he learns the marquise is already at Les Touches and hurries there, consumed by thoughts of her.

He meets the Chevalier du Halga and Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël, who speculate about a romance between him and Charlotte de Kergarouet—a notion he finds repulsive now that his heart is set on Béatrix. Returning home, his mother gives him a letter from Camille inviting him to celebrate the marquise's arrival.

At Les Touches, the company assembles in Camille’s salon. Calyste meets Gennaro Conti, a poet resembling Lord Byron, and is captivated by Béatrix, who surpasses his imagination. During dinner he struggles to engage, feeling overshadowed by Béatrix and Conti. Mademoiselle des Touches counsels him to control his emotions, but he feels hopeless about winning Béatrix’s affection.

After dinner Camille and Conti perform a duet that awakens deep emotion in Calyste, moving him to tears; Béatrix notices and becomes aware of his feelings. Overwhelmed, Calyste retreats to a sofa, brooding over his unrequited love.

Claude Vignon enters and reassures Calyste he will soon be free to pursue love, hinting that Camille has feelings for him. Conflicted between his affections for Béatrix and Camille, Calyste wrestles with his emotions. Near midnight he overhears Claude and Camille discussing her former feelings and her fear that love may lead to despair.

The next day Calyste learns Camille’s carriage has passed; he rides to Saint-Nazaire. His family speculates about his intentions, while he remains preoccupied with Béatrix. At Saint-Nazaire he sees Camille and Béatrix bidding farewell to steamer passengers, which stirs conflicting emotions.

On the return trip, Calyste feels the pressure of family expectations and his growing obsession. He decides to visit Les Touches earlier, hoping to meet Béatrix in the garden. When they finally meet, Béatrix greets him with gentle indifference, which only intensifies his desire. He confesses he no longer loves Camille, but Béatrix replies cautiously, mindful of commitments. As he begins to press his case, Camille interrupts, leaving Béatrix confused.

At breakfast Béatrix maintains an indifferent demeanor that torments Calyste. Camille proposes an excursion, but Béatrix hesitates, fearing the consequences of being seen with Calyste. Despite his eagerness, her refusal leaves him despondent. Resolving to confront Béatrix and clarify their relationship, Calyste’s desperation heightens, setting the stage for further complications.

Upon returning home, Calyste became consumed by thoughts of Beatrix and doubts about Camille. Inspired by Beatrix, he wrote a heartfelt letter declaring his admiration and unrequited devotion, saying his feelings for her had replaced those he once had for Camille, whom he now regarded as a sister.

He asked the Chevalier du Halga how to deliver the letter discreetly, then slipped it into Beatrix's hand during a visit. Days passed without a reply. When Beatrix finally answered, she was kind but refused him: she acknowledged his feelings but said she could not reciprocate because of her commitment to Camille, urging him to value Camille's devotion and find a more suitable match.

The baroness, alarmed by her son's distress after reading the letter, suggested he marry Charlotte de Kergarouet to secure the estate. Calyste, however, remained fixated on Beatrix and wrote another passionate letter, dismissing his mother's concerns.

Beatrix, feeling inferior to Camille, began to regard Calyste as hers, while Camille, sensing a threat, instructed Calyste to prepare for an excursion. Tensions rose at dinner when Beatrix confronted Camille, leading to a heated exchange that exposed their rivalry for Calyste's affection.

In late August, with the Guérande marshes heavy and the paludiers harvesting salt, Calyste introduced his parents to Beatrix and the group took a boat to Croisic. Beatrix revealed her inner conflict about love and her commitment to Conti, while Calyste professed his devotion. Their moment was interrupted when Beatrix slipped; Calyste rescued her, deepening their bond.

As Beatrix recovered, she softened toward Calyste, who remained loyal despite her past. But her commitment to Conti created strain, and Conti’s unexpected arrival forced Beatrix to leave. Conti’s presence affected Calyste deeply; at dinner he mocked Beatrix’s feelings for Calyste.

Calyste withdrew, turning down his family's plans to marry Charlotte. His health declined from heartbreak, culminating in a fever. After the baron's death his despair worsened until Camille offered to take him to Paris to search for Beatrix, rekindling his hope. The narrative examines love, loss, and the tangled emotions that make passion both ecstasy and despair.

Felicite sells her house to reclaim the du Guenic estates, hoping to secure a future for her son Calyste by marrying him to Sabine de Grandlieu, the duke’s youngest daughter. The duke and duchess have arranged marriages for their daughters, and Felicite’s plan aims to divert Calyste’s affections from Beatrix.

In Paris, Mademoiselle des Touches plans the new home for Calyste and Sabine. Calyste, encouraged to socialize, is drawn to Sabine, whose likeness to Beatrix complicates his feelings. Although still attached to Beatrix, he considers marrying Sabine. On the day the marriage contract is to be signed, Calyste unexpectedly refuses Mademoiselle des Touches’ benefits, shocking the household. Sabine brings a letter from Beatrix in which Beatrix confesses her love and urges Calyste to accept her gifts. After reading it, Calyste agrees to sign, and they marry the following week. But Sabine quickly senses his unresolved attachment to Beatrix.

In letters to her mother, Sabine describes married life as a mixture of love and insecurity. She adores Calyste but perceives that his heart remains with Beatrix. Following her mother’s advice to be patient and discreet, Sabine nonetheless struggles with feelings of inadequacy and jealousy.

While traveling through Brittany, Sabine recounts the warmth of Calyste’s family and the pleasure of being accepted by the local community, yet she feels the burden of expectations placed on a wife. Her letters trace an evolving relationship: devotion mingled with a need for self-respect and a persistent fear of Beatrix’s influence.

A visit to Mademoiselle des Touches yields counsel on keeping Calyste emotionally engaged. Torn between loving him and protecting their marriage, Sabine wrestles with insecurity as Beatrix’s presence looms. By July 1838 she writes of jealousy and obsession over Calyste’s past; despite warnings, curiosity draws her back to Les Touches, where she fears his lingering feelings. By September her anxieties intensify and she feels isolated despite efforts to preserve happiness.

In October 1839, after giving birth, Sabine hopes motherhood will bind them, but Calyste becomes absorbed in Parisian diversions. In August 1840 an encounter between Calyste and Beatrix rekindles old feelings; Sabine’s confrontation produces misunderstandings that threaten their marriage. After another pregnancy Sabine’s distress deepens; she contemplates suicide, believing love has become suffering. Her mother consults the Abbe Brossette to find a plan to restore the marriage, underscoring themes of love, jealousy, and the societal pressures on women.

Monsieur de Rochefide, recently abandoned by his wife Béatrix, discovers unexpected contentment in bachelorhood, enjoying a comfortable life on a thousand francs a day. Society pities yet favors him, a contrast to the harsher fate women in similar circumstances face.

After Béatrix leaves, Rochefide embraces freedom, navigating social life by imitation and others’ opinions. His superficial charm wins popularity, but he lacks deep intellect.

Rochefide’s life shifts when he meets Madame Schontz, a woman of doubtful reputation who has endured hardship. Once called La Petite-Aurélie, she was orphaned and educated at Saint-Denis. Her beauty and wit attract Rochefide, and their relationship unfolds in four phases. At first he supports her modestly with an apartment and allowance. As their bond deepens he grows more generous, improving her home and raising her stipend. Madame Schontz, attuned to his insecurities, manages their finances shrewdly and presents herself as a devoted partner, which enhances Rochefide’s social standing.

In the third phase she assumes a maternal role toward Rochefide’s son, prudently handling his investments and securing notable financial gains. By the final phase she is fully established as an influential figure in Rochefide’s life, cultivating a social circle that raises her status while carefully balancing power between them.

Ambitious and jealous of Madame du Bruel, Madame Schontz seeks a respectable marriage even as she subtly competes with Rochefide. Numerous men, including artists and poets, are suspected suitors, but none discern her true designs. Couture, a stock-gambler and one of her few loyal friends, proposes; she also eyes Fabien du Ronceret, a provincially raised aspirant eager for Parisian fame.

Fabien, the son of a chief-justice, has abandoned his judgeship to pursue social standing, embodying the era’s appetite for advancement. He adopts extravagant manners to ingratiate himself with figures like Couture and to impress Madame Schontz. Aware of Fabien’s ambitions, she toys with him while plotting her future.

Meanwhile the Duchesse de Grandlieu consults the calculating Comte Maxime de Trailles to resolve a family matter. Maxime agrees only if the duchess will accept his future wife into her circle—an exchange revealing the vanity and moral decay of their milieu.

As events proceed, La Palferine, a young count, receives funds from Maxime to woo a marquise, while Madame Schontz stages a lavish dinner that displays Parisian opulence. The plot advances as Béatrix wrestles with feelings for both men, culminating in a dramatic confrontation that underscores passion’s transience and the social pressures shaping their lives. Maxime’s machinations shift relationships, exposing the intricate web of connections and rivalries that define their world.
