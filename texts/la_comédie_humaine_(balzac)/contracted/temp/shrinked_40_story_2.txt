The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou, had served the Bourbon cause during the La Vendée and sacrificed much; his estate was confiscated and he was left ruined. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet, a noblewoman without fortune, and after the second revolution moved to Paris with a large family hoping for better prospects. Initially disillusioned, he unexpectedly received honors—a military promotion and royal decorations—which revived hopes for favor.

He soon felt his sacrifices little recognized as new aristocrats rose, and though he considered returning to his estate he reentered royal service in the turmoil of 1815. His loyalty was rewarded: he became a trusted adviser to Louis XVIII, valued for wit and political skill. The Fontaines began to prosper—sons won posts and daughters made advantageous, if lesser, matches.

Emilie, the youngest, was spoiled and central to Parisian attention. Beautiful and charming, she remained indifferent and scornful of suitors she deemed beneath her, provoking family conflict. The Count’s repeated attempts to secure a suitable husband were frustrated by her caprice and petty dismissals. As the political climate shifted and the King’s health declined, he pressed harder to marry her into respectability; her unreasonable standards and dismissive winter behavior left the family’s future and honor uncertain.

Nature had endowed Emilie de Fontaine with every quality suited to play Célimène. Tall and slender, she could assume dignity or coquettishness; her long neck and arched brows expressed scorn and pride. She had mastered gestures that conveyed contempt or charm and a melodious voice that could win a heart or cut off indiscretion. Her alabaster complexion remained usually calm, roused only by quick flashes of feeling. Jilted admirers accused her of acting a part; she insisted she merely inspired desire and enjoyed subjecting suitors to her whims.

Monsieur de Fontaine realized too late that constant adulation had spoiled his daughter. Praise had inflated her pride and self-confidence, fostering the selfishness of a pampered child. Her youthful charms masked these defects; his attempts to teach practical wisdom met only caprice and ironic replies, his counsel sliding off her like water from marble. He came to regard her affections as often condescending, like a child demanding attention before returning to play.

Emilie sometimes showed affection for her parents but often withdrew, jealous of any shared fondness even among siblings. She blamed solitude on fate, failing to see that happiness must originate within. Determined to avoid her sisters’ marriages, she nevertheless envied their contentment; her unpredictability led her mother to suspect a touch of madness.

Such pride is common among wealthy young beauties, who imagine mothers cannot understand youthful dreams and suspect maternal envy. These conflicts yield secret tears and rebellions against perceived tyranny, and girls devise elaborate life plans, picturing an ideal partner who must meet exacting conditions. With experience, many discover happiness unexpectedly and learn that ideals often fall short.

Emilie’s terms for a suitor were rigid: he must be a peer of France, a soldier, and bear an Order so they could be treated with respect; he must also be slender—she deemed corpulence incompatible with romance. Her disdain for those who failed her standards was clear, and Monsieur de Fontaine feared her eccentricities would invite ridicule and leave her indecisive.

During the winter of Charles X’s reign he redoubled his matchmaking, hosting gatherings to attract suitors. Though his reputation as a corrupter of legislative integrity grew, he remained earnest. After a season of efforts he felt hopeful, only to meet Emilie’s unyielding whims.

One morning he summoned her to discuss her future, arranging his study to present a serious front. Emilie greeted him with a light kiss. He spoke of marriage as essential to her happiness and urged wise choice; she interrupted, recalling their agreement about suitors. He pressed the matter, noting the family’s endeavors; she dismissed his proposals, mocking the idea of settling for anything less than a peer. Exasperated, he declared he had fulfilled his duty and that the choice was hers, warning that marital happiness rests on mutual respect rather than mere wealth or title. Emilie, touched by his sincerity, feigned warmth but kept her pride.

At a family gathering she reiterated her refusal to marry except a peer. Her uncle joked she awaited the Duc de Bordeaux’s majority; she forbade further talk.

As summer neared, at their country house Emilie sought the anonymity of the Bal de Sceaux. There she noticed a striking young man apart from the crowd, whose elegance stirred her imagination. She approached him; he remained aloof, tending instead to a pale dancer named Clara. Jealous and curious, Emilie followed them. Days later, riding with her uncle and meeting Lady Dudley and Monsieur Vandenesse, she learned the man was not what she had imagined. Disappointed yet still drawn to him, she concealed her feelings as her uncle promised an introduction. That promise intensified her infatuation: her fancy swelled, fueled by imagination and the romance of the unknown, blind to marriage’s complexities and compromises.

Before Emilie woke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse, recognized the young man he'd insulted, and greeted him with polished old‑court courtesy. He professed contrition—calling himself a vice‑admiral, blaming grog, and insisting, “Shake hands; I’d sooner endure a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than bring his family regret.” The two rode together; the admiral joked about duels, fired a pistol into a birch to show he feared none, praised the young man’s shooting, and quizzed him on gentlemanly trifles—debts, pleasures, heraldry—teasing about mistresses. Longueville answered with composed worldliness: he paid punctually, lived independently, and showed the polish of a fine education. The Count announced he would introduce him at the Pavillon Planat.

At the Villa Planat, the name “Monsieur de Longueville” shifted curiosity to attention. He arrived simply but elegantly, at ease in the Receiver‑General’s house, well spoken and informed—even on naval architecture, a subject the admiral raised. His manners suggested École Polytechnique training; pressed about his profession, he said he had renounced the Engineers to preserve his independence. He declined dinner courteously, citing a delicate sister.

Emilie, who had dressed to attract him, found his measured attention both pleasing and wounding. Usually flaunting her charms, she seemed simpler and more natural; when prodded she quipped, “Those who please everybody please nobody,” revealing pride and caution.

Over the next fortnight Longueville called repeatedly. His modesty and discretion drew Emilie—partly from pride—and he skillfully evaded questions about his family. In music and conversation he neither revealed nor flaunted his rank; once they sang a duet and company could not tell if he were professional. His reserve both tormented and deepened Emilie’s interest.

A dance was arranged to meet his sister Clara. When the siblings arrived, Emilie felt genuine pleasure for Clara but also envy: Clara combined ease with modesty and gently reproved Emilie’s earlier disdain for commoners. Clara’s frank courtesy reassured Emilie, bringing the girls closer.

With autumn, Emilie and Longueville’s attachment deepened through small intimacies—aimless conversations, sunsets, daisy‑gatherings, passionate duets—learning each other while withholding much. His silence was both balm and barrier; she cherished his attention and resented the mystery. The household watched and speculated, charmed by his discretion and talent, while unanswered questions about his rank and history lingered.
