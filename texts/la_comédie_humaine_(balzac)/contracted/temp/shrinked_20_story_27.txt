In 1828, Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac walked from Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré to the boulevard, discussing the Marquise d’Espard, whom Rastignac admired. Bianchon cautioned him against pursuing her, comparing her to a “one-eyed horse” and suggesting she was not as youthful as Rastignac thought.

Rastignac argued that the Marquise’s wealth and social status made her appealing, especially since he was financially strained after marrying off his sisters. Bianchon, a physician, expressed disdain for fashionable women, claiming they often lacked genuine character and were selfish. He warned Rastignac that the Marquise might be in debt despite her charm.

Bianchon shared his experiences with high society, describing its superficiality and moral decay. He believed that a woman of true virtue would not be fashionable, as the qualities that elevate women often stem from vices. Rastignac countered that a fashionable woman could provide significant advantages for an ambitious man, viewing her as a tool for success.

Despite Bianchon’s warnings, Rastignac remained determined to marry the Marquise, prioritizing her social connections over the virtues of a more devoted partner. Bianchon reluctantly agreed to introduce him to his uncle, Judge Jean-Jules Popinot, known for his strict adherence to the law.

The next morning, Bianchon visited Popinot, who lived in a dilapidated area of Paris. The judge was characterized by his unkempt appearance and lack of ambition, having been overlooked for promotions despite his legal skills. His life was marked by a commitment to justice, often at the expense of his own comfort and reputation.

Popinot was a diligent and compassionate judge, respected for his knowledge of criminal law but remained a supernumerary judge due to his modest nature and lack of political maneuvering. His life exemplified the struggles of a good man in a flawed system, where ambition often overshadowed merit.

As Bianchon approached Popinot’s residence, he reflected on the challenges of persuading the judge to assist Rastignac. The narrative explores themes of ambition, social status, and moral dilemmas in the pursuit of success, contrasting Bianchon’s skepticism about the Marquise with Rastignac’s aspirations.

Initially reluctant to stay in the Rue du Fouarre, he became committed to alleviating local suffering. Over five years, he evolved from a judge focused on legal outcomes to a compassionate figure, akin to Saint Vincent de Paul, dedicated to helping the poor. His charitable work drained his resources, yet he found fulfillment as a protector and benefactor.

His aide, Lavienne, helped manage the influx of desperate individuals seeking aid, filling the room from dawn until late morning. Despite the stifling atmosphere, the poor revered Popinot, who tirelessly provided food, shelter, and employment.

His private family life was supported by a few loyal servants. D’Espard’s choice to isolate his children led to misunderstandings with neighbors, who viewed him as eccentric. Gossip portrayed him as mad, fueled by his frugal lifestyle and perceived financial ruin, exacerbated by tenants’ jealousy of his noble status, deemed undeserved without wealth.

D’Espard and his wife embodied true aristocratic values, instilling dignity and pride in their children, who reflected their noble heritage. At around fifty, the Marquis appeared refined but was seen as cold due to his mannerisms. His children, Comte Clement and Vicomte Camille, were handsome, with Clement nearing sixteen and Camille still in education.

Overall, the narrative highlights the complexities of social status, isolation, and societal perceptions of declining nobility in a changing France.

On a Thursday holiday, Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard. Early that morning, his sons, Clement and Camille, played in the garden, waking their father, the Marquis, who enjoyed watching them. However, upon Popinot's arrival, the portress informed him of the Marquis' supposed madness, claiming he encouraged his children to fight.

Popinot was led to a shabby office on the third floor, where he met the Marquis, who was reading by the fire. The judge explained he was there to investigate a petition for a Commission in Lunacy filed by the Marquise d’Espard. The Marquis, surprised, asked why he had not been notified of the petition. Popinot clarified that such notifications were unnecessary for those deemed insane.

The Marquis expressed concern about how the inquiry might affect his wife. Popinot assured him of confidentiality and inquired about allegations of financial impropriety involving Madame Jeanrenaud. The distressed Marquis revealed a family secret: the Jeanrenaud fortune had been wrongfully acquired during the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes, benefiting his ancestors from the execution of a Protestant merchant. He felt compelled to repay the Jeanrenauds, whom he had located in France.

Despite his wife's lack of understanding, the Marquis committed to repaying the Jeanrenauds, straining his finances. He chose to educate his sons himself, sacrificing social standing to ensure they grew up with integrity. The judge listened intently, moved by the Marquis' noble intentions and the burden of his family's past.

As their conversation continued, the Marquis invited Popinot to his home, revealing he had rented the office for a financially successful publishing venture on China. Just then, his sons returned from riding, full of energy and affection for their father, illustrating their strong bond.

Madame Jeanrenaud soon entered, wanting to return the money given to the Marquis, fearing for their honor. Popinot intervened, explaining that the payments were legally justified and rooted in honorable motives. He reassured the Marquis that his actions would not be misinterpreted and praised him for his integrity.

Afterward, Popinot returned to his office, reflecting on the encounter and the nobility of the Marquis. The next day, he was summoned by the President of his Court, who informed him he would be removed from the case due to a potential conflict of interest stemming from his visit with Madame d’Espard. Although Popinot protested, he ultimately accepted the decision, recognizing the importance of maintaining the Court's integrity.

In summary, the narrative explores themes of honor, familial duty, and the complexities of social standing, as the Marquis d’Espard grapples with his family's dark history while striving to restore their honor through restitution and dedication to his children.
