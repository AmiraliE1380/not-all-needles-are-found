At one in the morning Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac discussed the Marquise d’Espard. Bianchon distrusted the fashionable marquise—likening her to a “one-eyed horse,” whose cosmetics could not hide age or temper—and scorned society women as insincere. Rastignac, dazzled by rank and beauty, hoped she might advance him and settle his debts. Bianchon dismissed that view but agreed to introduce Rastignac to his uncle, Judge Jean-Jules Popinot, though he doubted the rigid magistrate would be useful.

The next morning Bianchon visited Popinot’s house in a decayed quarter; the building still hinted at former grandeur. Popinot himself was unkempt and unambitious, reduced to a supernumerary judge, yet he combined firm integrity with an intuitive ability to elicit confessions without force and to detect hidden virtues in the poor. Frustrated by the legal system’s limits, he devoted himself privately to justice and charity.

Popinot’s transformation began twelve years earlier, during the 1816 famine, when he was appointed President of a Commission Extraordinary to distribute food locally. A prominent lawyer and criminal judge who once observed outcomes at a distance, direct contact with destitution awakened deep compassion. One act of charity led to another until he became a local protector of the poor, likened to a modern Saint Vincent de Paul.

He converted a storeroom into a modest parlor for the needy, furnished with wooden benches and plain chairs, and kept meticulous records of donations and requests to allocate resources fairly. His aide Lavienne handled pawn tickets and made house visits in the hardest-hit districts. From dawn until late morning the parlor filled with women, children, and the indigent; their number made heating unnecessary, and even thieves respected the household. Popinot spent mornings attending the poor, afternoons on his legal practice, and evenings on judicial duties. This close observation sharpened his judicial instinct—he learned to read subtle signs of wrongdoing and virtue alike.

His modest inherited fortune, his late wife’s savings, and a restrained judge’s salary were stretched by charitable outlays. Despite public service, he remained one of the few judges of the Court of the Seine without the Legion of Honor.

Popinot’s routine was interrupted when the President of the Second Chamber, at the Marquise d’Espard’s request, commissioned him to examine the Marquis d’Espard for a Lunacy Commission. Bianchon went to the Rue du Fouarre expecting a clash between Popinot’s rough domesticity and the Marquise’s courtly world.

The parlor was a tableau of hardship: worn faces and quiet resilience. Popinot, in a threadbare nightcap and dressing gown, sat absorbed in his ledger; Lavienne kept the petitions in order. Bianchon came with an urgent matter, but Popinot paused to help a grocer’s wife whose husband was jailed for debt, recording the transaction. By midmorning he had attended to over two hundred people; when Bianchon returned after an errand, the last of the needy had been helped.

Popinot’s home reflected his divided life—dust, scattered papers, his late wife’s things, and tokens of gratitude. As they prepared for breakfast Bianchon urged him to dress for the Marquise’s meeting. Magistrates should not accept hospitality from parties in cases they oversee; Popinot hesitated but, persuaded the visit would be revealing and kinder, agreed and began to consider the Marquis d’Espard’s mental state and its implications for the family.

The next evening at nine Bianchon climbed his uncle’s dusty staircase and found him bent over a complicated judgment. Because the tailor had not sent the coat Lavienne ordered, Popinot still wore his old stained coat; Bianchon straightened his cravat and buttoned it, but the judge’s habitual movements revealed a gap between waistcoat and trousers. Bianchon noticed the absurdity just as his uncle entered the Marquise d’Espard’s room.

A brief account of Madame d’Espard frames the interview. For seven years she had been a Parisian fashion-figure. After separating from her husband in 1815, the Marquise—about thirty-three and mother of two boys, fifteen and thirteen—kept her vogue with elaborate hair, cold baths, and strict diet; emotionally detached, she admitted neither love nor hatred and parted from M. d’Espard with satisfaction when he took the children, whom she regarded as obstacles to her ambitions.

With an income of twenty-six thousand francs a year she lived quietly at first but by 1820 reappeared socially, hosting gatherings and replacing other prominent women. She cultivated ties with duchesses and countesses, turning her drawing-room into a discreet hub for political discussion. Skilled at social maneuvering, she attracted ambitious young men while remaining vain and instrumental—using intimacy as influence without personal compromise.

Rastignac, seeking an alliance, viewed her as a useful, cunning power. Popinot’s visit confirmed the household’s opulence; she received him with feigned weakness while the Chevalier d’Espard observed silently. Perceptive and probing, Popinot questioned the husband’s supposed mental change and their separation. The Marquise offered a crafted story, but Popinot’s practical inquiries—about the children’s welfare and the household’s arrangements—exposed contradictions: claims of hardship sat uneasily with lavish living, suggesting she might be maneuvering to control her husband’s affairs for self-serving reasons.

When she tried to deflect, Popinot warned that the court would scrutinize motives and transparency and that her façade might collapse. Afterwards Bianchon and Rastignac discussed the case’s scandal potential and Popinot’s character; Bianchon feared the Marquise’s influence. Popinot resolved to investigate further and summon Madame Jeanrenaud.

The next day Madame Jeanrenaud arrived: stout, unrefined, a sharp contrast to the Marquise. She denied wrongdoing and spoke simply of concern for M. d’Espard’s well-being. Her straightforward honesty intrigued Popinot. As he began tracing M. d’Espard’s financial dealings, he realized the situation was more complex than it first appeared; untangling the truth would require careful examination of all involved.

Those who have matured know how trivial incidents can shape larger events: Popinot’s minor illness delayed a crucial visit. Feeling unwell, the judge stayed home instead of seeing the Marquis d’Espard, losing a day whose absence echoed longer historic delays.

Before following the lawyer and his clerk to the Marquis’s residence, consider the father’s home life, which his wife’s petition branded as mad. In old Paris some buildings still display a taste for permanence. The house on Rue de la Montagne-Sainte-Genevieve where M. d’Espard lived was one such stone structure: once grand, now weathered by time and revolution, formerly housing dignitaries, later partitioned and partly occupied by a printing establishment. M. d’Espard took the ground floor, likely for his children’s sake, because the garden was unusually large for the area; he paid a modest rent given the building’s decay.

The Marquis furnished his rooms in rich browns, creating a quiet, harmonious atmosphere with carefully chosen pieces. Few visitors were admitted; family life remained secluded. The household comprised the Marquis, his two sons, and a small, disciplined staff—a long-serving cook and a man-servant. To shield his children from outside influence, M. d’Espard avoided socializing with tenants, and that isolation bred gossip: neighbors branded him mad and his servants untrustworthy. Noble by birth, the family embodied increasingly rare ideals, their dignity clashing with egalitarian neighbors. The Marquis, about fifty, had a distinguished, somewhat cold appearance and wore the same coat for years. His sons, Comte Clément de Négrepelisse, nearly sixteen and fashionable, and Vicomte Camille d’Espard, were handsome. The Marquis concentrated on a broad education for them—mathematics, Eastern languages, and history—eschewing what he considered unproductive studies.

The day Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard fell on a Thursday holiday. Before their father rose, his two boys were in the garden; the younger begged to visit the shooting-gallery and urged Clément to go. The Viscount took advantage of Clément’s weakness and delighted in wrestling with him. Their quarrel roused their father, who watched amused from the window. Neighbors joked that the Marquis seemed mad to make his children fight. Noticing them, the Marquis called the boys; they leapt into his room and, after a moment’s hesitation, Clément obtained permission from Camille.

Through the house people whispered about the Marquis’s supposed new madness. When Popinot arrived near noon with his clerk, the portress led him to the third floor and remarked that M. d’Espard had made his children fight that morning and had laughed when the younger bit the elder until he bled; she added that the Marquis rarely showed himself. The landing’s muddy floor and grimy banisters lent credence to her gossip and made Popinot, though impartial, receptive to the tale.

They entered an outer office where an old man sat amid shelves of published numbers; a grating separated a private room, and a cash-desk showed the enterprise’s commercial aspect. A shopman opened the inner room to reveal the Marquis, a venerable, plainly dressed old man wearing the Cross of Saint-Louis, seated at a desk. Popinot explained they came on private business. After formalities the Marquis asked their purpose. Popinot identified himself as a judge on the Inferior Court of Appeal for the Department of the Seine, charged with examining M. d’Espard regarding a petition for a Commission in Lunacy filed by the Marquise d’Espard. The old man reddened and trembled, then steadied himself.

“How is it,” he asked, “that I have had no notice of such a petition?” Popinot explained that notice is unnecessary because those deemed subject to such a commission are presumed to lack reason; the Court must verify the petitioner’s claims. The Marquis accepted this and asked what he must do. Popinot urged him to answer fully and confidentially, especially concerning delicate accusations Madame d’Espard might make. The Marquis feared his explanations could blame his wife. Popinot promised discretion and the customary courtesy should the inquiry reveal a misunderstanding.

Popinot then confronted the major allegation: that the Marquis had dissipated his fortune for the benefit of Madame Jeanrenaud, widow of a bargemaster, or her son, Colonel Jeanrenaud, for whom he allegedly procured an appointment and exhausted influence with the King. The Marquis flushed; tears came, but pride returned quickly. He said he was in a strange dilemma: revealing his motives would mean disclosing secret wounds and trusting the family’s honor to strangers. Popinot assured him of confidentiality. Freed from his usual reserve, the Marquis spoke with clarity and passion.

After his marriage his wife had incurred heavy expenses that forced him to borrow. He could not afford a steward; most of his title-deeds had been taken to Paris by his father, who feared scrutiny. Their family name was Negrepelisse; the title d’Espard had been acquired through marriage in the time of Henri IV. An ancestor had lost the town of Negrepelisse during the religious wars and been ruined by property destruction. Though a later ancestor secured favor with Louis XIV and fortune, a stain of blood and disgrace remained, and the Marquis was determined to erase it.

He discovered that his grandfather had obtained the lands of Negrepelisse by means tied to the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes: a Protestant merchant was arrested and hanged, and the land passed to his ancestor by iniquitous means. The merchant’s family had the rightful claim; the victim’s name was Jeanrenaud. The Marquis could not bear the disgrace and resolved to find the heirs. Eventually he located M. Jeanrenaud, reduced to poverty and serving as a lieutenant under Napoleon. Believing the heir’s rights incontestable and their lack of means to sue, he offered to pay them the lands’ value at the time they were taken—eleven hundred thousand francs—payable at his convenience and without interest.

To effect restitution he lived on little for a long time. When he proposed to Madame d’Espard that they move to the country to live on half her income, she treated him as mad; her coldness convinced him she would have condoned his ancestors’ crimes. Terrified by her attitude, he resolved to leave her in control of her fortune after settling their debts, and to take charge of educating their children himself, determined to raise them as gentlemen. He invested prudently, repaid the debt sooner than expected, and had recently completed the payment of the eleven hundred thousand francs, making restitution without harming his children's prospects. These were the reasons for payments to Madame Jeanrenaud and her son.

Popinot asked whether Madame d’Espard knew his motives for retiring from public life. “Yes, monsieur,” the Marquis replied. Popinot, visibly moved, excused his clerk. He then inquired about other allegations, such as that the Marquis was engaged in a business unbecoming his rank. The Marquis declined to discuss it there and summoned Comte de Nouvion, an old family friend, explaining that he used the rooms as offices for the publication of the Picturesque History of China by a reputable firm, not as his private residence.

He showed Popinot his ground-floor apartment—a place of simple, touching harmony that struck the judge. The pleasant weather and garden convinced Popinot that a madman would hardly produce such beauty. The Marquis said he intended to remain until his younger son finished his studies, then travel with the children to European capitals to further their education and languages. He explained his China project: Comte de Nouvion, returned to France without fortune, was his partner; the Abbé Grozier, Keeper of the Books at the Arsenal, had been his tutor and taught him about China. Though enamored of China’s history, he remained a French gentleman. Financially, they had two thousand five hundred subscribers—twelve hundred in France—the book would cost about three hundred francs, and the Comte would earn six to seven thousand francs a year from it; the hundred thousand francs he hoped to make would cover his children’s education and pleasures. Twelve years of retirement to educate his children had cost him his memory at Court and political prospects, but he believed he had purified his family’s past and secured a glorious future for his children—a noble, if secret, task.

At that moment horses clattered in the courtyard. The two boys entered, robust and sunburnt, dressed for riding. They greeted their father and the lawyer. Popinot needed no further evidence of the Marquis’ bond with his sons. The boys reported shooting success and that they had ridden in the Bois, where they had seen their mother. “Why didn’t you speak to her?” the Marquis asked. “I think I noticed, father, that she doesn’t want us to speak to her in public,” Clément whispered. A cloud passed over the Marquis’ brow; Popinot felt deep sympathy at the scene of fatherly devotion. “Justice may look in here at any time,” the Marquis said. “If anyone is mad, it can only be the children, who are a little crazy about their father, and the father who is very crazy about his children—but that kind of madness rings true.”

Madame Jeanrenaud then entered, disregarding protests from the man-servant. “I take no roundabout ways!” she exclaimed. Bowing to those present, she announced she had come too late because the criminal judge was there. She declared that she and her son were of one mind to return everything, for their honor was at stake; they preferred to give back everything rather than cause trouble. “They must be as stupid as pans without handles to call you a lunatic,” she added. The boys clung to their father, alarmed.

“Silence, madame,” Popinot interposed. The Marquis sent his children to the garden. Popinot addressed Madame Jeanrenaud: the payments made by the Marquis were legally due, though prompted by an expansive sense of honesty. If every possessor of confiscated goods were compelled to make restitution every century and a half, few legitimate owners would remain in France. The law allowed M. d’Espard to dispose of his income without accounting for it. A Commission in Lunacy is appropriate only when actions lack reason; here, the remittances were driven by the highest motives. She could keep the money without remorse and let the world misinterpret a noble action. Popinot declared he would rather see the Marquis crowned than subjected to a Commission. Madame Jeanrenaud applauded his defense, calling it judge-like and warmly praising his words.

The Marquis extended his hand to Popinot, who pressed it gently; a private understanding passed between the humane judge and the noble, sublime man. “Monsieur le Marquis,” Popinot added, “from the first words of this inquiry, I regarded my clerk as unnecessary.” Leading M. d’Espard to the window, he said it was time to return home and that Madame la Marquise had likely acted under an influence the Marquis should counteract. Popinot left, the scene lingering with him; he even thought the rooms would suit him if the Marquis left them.

The next morning, having written his report the night before, Popinot went to the Palais de Justice to pursue prompt justice. As he prepared to put on his gown, the usher said the President wished to see him privately. “Good morning, my dear Popinot,” the President greeted. “I’ve been waiting.” Popinot asked if something was wrong. The President called it a silly trifle: the Keeper of the Seals, with whom he had dined, had suggested it would be best if Popinot did not sit on the case. Despite Popinot’s protest that he had left Madame d’Espard’s house before tea, the President cited propriety—“Caesar’s wife must not be suspected”—and the need to avoid any appearance of conflict that might expose the Bench to journalistic malice.

It was to be a matter of propriety, not discipline. Popinot was to signal his retirement from the case as a personal favor; in recompense, the President would secure the Cross of the Legion of Honor for him, long deserved. M. Camusot, a newly appointed judge from a provincial court, had been named in Popinot’s place. Popinot noted with ironic amusement Camusot’s pale ambition. He bowed and withdrew, choosing not to contest the implication.
