In 1828, at one in the morning, Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac left a house in the Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré and walked to the boulevard because neither had a carriage. Their conversation turned to the Marquise d’Espard. Bianchon was skeptical of her character; Rastignac, dazzled by her beauty and rank, believed she could advance his social standing and help pay his debts.

Bianchon warned that pursuing the Marquise would be a mistake, likening her to a “one-eyed horse” and suggesting she was older than she pretended. As a physician, he insisted physical signs betray truth despite cosmetics, and he expressed contempt for fashionable women, whom he portrayed as polished façades lacking genuine feeling and driven by selfishness. Rastignac replied that his motive was social power and opportunity, dismissing moral scruples: a woman of fashion, he argued, could be a useful ally for an ambitious man, offering connections and influence.

Despite misgivings, Bianchon agreed to assist by introducing Rastignac to his uncle Jean-Jules Popinot, a judge who might intervene in the Marquise’s affairs. Still, Bianchon doubted Popinot’s willingness given the judge’s reputation for inflexibility.

The next morning Bianchon went to Popinot’s residence in a poor, dilapidated quarter of Paris; the building’s decay suggested past grandeur. Popinot himself was unkempt and unambitious; his career had stalled despite ability—he had been passed over and remained a supernumerary judge. His life was defined by a rigorous commitment to justice, often to his own detriment. Known for kindness and a talent for eliciting confessions without coercion, he combined compassion with firmness and was frustrated by legal injustices.

As Bianchon approached Popinot’s door, he reflected on how difficult it would be to persuade his uncle to help Rastignac, aware of the complexities and obstacles ahead.

Twelve years before this narrative, in 1816, during a severe food shortage in France, Popinot was named President of the Commission Extraordinary to distribute food to the needy in his neighborhood. Though he had intended to leave the Rue du Fouarre, his work as a prominent lawyer and criminal judge—after five years watching legal outcomes without grasping their causes—led him to deep compassion for the destitute, turning him into a protector of the poor, almost a modern Saint Vincent de Paul.

The change was gradual. Popinot moved from one charitable act to another, becoming central in his community and active in various benevolent organizations. He strove to prevent crime, provide employment, and aid the vulnerable, while keeping a private life little known in Paris. The gratitude of those he helped was often unspoken: they were too burdened to acknowledge him.

By the end of his second year he had converted a storeroom in his home into a parlor for the needy. Simply furnished with wooden benches and basic chairs, the room became a hub where the poor sought assistance. Popinot recorded donations and requests with care to manage resources, while his aide Lavienne handled pawn tickets and visited areas hit hardest by famine.

From early morning into the day the parlor was crowded with women, children, and the destitute. The atmosphere was often stifling, but the crowd’s warmth made heating unnecessary. The poor revered Popinot and often waited outside before dawn; even criminals respected his house. He devoted mornings to the needy, afternoons to legal work, and evenings to his judicial duties.

Popinot’s keen observation let him discern hidden virtues in the impoverished and detect the faintest signs of wrongdoing in others. His modest inheritance, supplemented by his late wife’s wealth, was stretched by frugality and his charitable outlays; his judge’s salary was limited, and despite his service he remained one of the few judges in the Court of the Seine without the Legion of Honor.

At the request of the President of the Second Chamber of the Court, Popinot was charged with examining the Marquis d’Espard after the marquise, the Marquis’s wife, sought a Commission in Lunacy. The Rue du Fouarre, bustling with the needy in the early hours, would be empty by nine. Bianchon, Popinot’s nephew, rode to find his uncle amid these duties, expecting a contrast between Popinot’s unassuming presence and the marquise’s expectations.

Bianchon found the parlor full—a tableau of hardship, faces marked by despair and resilience. Popinot, in a worn nightcap and dressing gown, was engrossed in his work, a man wholly committed to easing suffering. Lavienne kept the process moving, issuing assistance efficiently.

Bianchon interrupted with urgency while Popinot continued consultations. After a brief exchange, Popinot attended a woman who needed funds to sustain her grocery business while her husband was imprisoned for debt; he gave assistance and recorded the transaction in his ledger.

As the morning wore on, Bianchon learned Popinot had seen over two hundred people that day, with more waiting. Sent away on urgent medical errands, Bianchon returned to find the parlor empty—the last of the needy helped.

Popinot’s home reflected his chaotic life: dust, disarray, papers everywhere, and remnants of his late wife’s possessions. Yet tokens of gratitude from those he aided adorned the room, poignant reminders of his impact.

Preparing for breakfast, Bianchon urged Popinot to dress properly for the meeting with the marquise. Popinot resisted, citing legal rules against magistrates accepting hospitality from interested parties. Bianchon argued it would help Popinot understand her concerns in her own environment. Conceding the point, Popinot agreed to visit the marquise, noting the appointment and preparing to examine the Marquis d’Espard’s mental state and its implications for the family.

The next evening at nine Bianchon climbed his uncle’s dusty stairs to find him bent over a complex judgment. Since the tailor had not sent the coat Lavienne ordered, Popinot wore his old stained coat, amusing those unaware of his private life. Bianchon straightened his uncle's cravat and buttoned his coat to hide the stains, but the judge’s habitual movements soon revealed a gap between waistcoat and trousers. Bianchon noted the absurdity just as his uncle entered the Marquise’s room.

A brief account of Madame d’Espard follows. For seven years she had been a prominent Parisian figure, keeping vogue despite the city’s fickleness. After separating from her husband in 1815, the Marquise—about thirty-three and mother of two boys, fifteen and thirteen—managed to preserve an appearance and social standing that usually favored youth and beauty.

She appeared far younger than her years, using elaborate coiffures, cold baths, and austere eating to maintain her looks—an effort likened to a famous Polish woman famed for retaining youthful vigor. Her complexion and figure remained attractive; emotionally she stayed detached, unfamiliar with either love or hatred. She parted from M. d’Espard without remorse, since he took the children she regarded as hindrances to her ambitions.

With an income of twenty-six thousand francs, the Marquise initially hesitated over her post-separation life. Though capable of luxurious living, she adopted a quieter existence for some years. By 1820 she re-entered society, hosting gatherings and supplanting other notable women in Paris’s elite circles.

Madame d’Espard cultivated ties with influential figures—a duchess, a countess—amplifying her political influence. Her salon became a center for political conversation; she was known for discretion and skill in navigating Parisian complexities. Ambitious young men admired her; she maintained independence while exercising power. Yet she was also indiscreet and vain, treating relationships as instruments to extend her influence rather than as genuine connections. She managed interactions to avoid personal compromise while keeping her drawing-room full of notable figures.

Rastignac, seeking to align with her, recognized her as an ally and a cunning adversary. When Popinot arrived at her household he noted its opulence. The Marquise received him with feigned frailty; the Chevalier d’Espard stood silently observing.

Though outwardly simple, Popinot was perceptive and quickly assessed the situation. He questioned the Marquise about her husband’s mental state and the separation. She offered a crafted story: a change in her husband’s temperament led to estrangement. Popinot probed the children’s welfare and financial claims, finding inconsistencies between her lavish life and assertions of hardship. He suggested her motives for seeking control over her husband’s affairs might be self-serving.

Tension rose as Popinot outlined potential consequences, emphasizing transparency and warning that the court would scrutinize her motives. The Marquise, feeling cornered, tried to deflect, but Popinot’s persistence forced her to face the risk that her carefully constructed facade could collapse.

Afterward Bianchon and Rastignac discussed Popinot’s character and the case’s implications, recognizing its complexity and scandal potential; Bianchon worried about the Marquise’s influence. Popinot resolved to investigate further, planning to summon Madame Jeanrenaud, the woman linked to M. d’Espard, to clarify matters.

The next day Madame Jeanrenaud arrived at Popinot’s office, a stout, unrefined contrast to the Marquise. She denied wrongdoing and expressed sincere concern for M. d’Espard. Intrigued by her straightforwardness, Popinot began to untangle M. d’Espard’s financial dealings, realizing the matter was more complicated than it first appeared and would require careful examination of all involved.

People who have matured know how trivial incidents can shape major events: Popinot's minor illness delayed a crucial meeting. The judge, feeling unwell, stayed home instead of visiting the Marquis d’Espard, costing a day whose loss echoed historic delays with wide consequences.

Before following the lawyer and his clerk to the Marquis’s residence, consider this father's home life, which his wife’s petition branded as mad. In old Paris, some buildings still show a taste for decoration and permanence. The house on Rue de la Montagne-Sainte-Genevieve where M. d’Espard lived was such a stone structure: once grand, now weathered and damaged by time and revolutions. Formerly home to dignitaries, it had become multi-tenant, its interior degraded by a printing establishment.

M. d’Espard took the ground floor, likely for his children’s sake, since the garden was unusually large for the area. He secured the rooms at a reasonable rent, given the building’s disrepair and the depressed market. The house, dating from the reigns of Henry III–Louis XIII, still bore traces of former glory, including a perron and the garden.

The Marquis furnished his rooms in rich browns, creating a peaceful atmosphere with carefully chosen pieces and a harmonious scheme. Few visitors were admitted to the family’s private life, which remained largely secluded. The household consisted of the Marquis, his two sons, and a small staff, including a long-serving cook and a man-servant, both contributing to the orderly quiet of the house.

M. d’Espard avoided socializing with other tenants to shield his children from outside influences. That choice, however, bred gossip and suspicion: neighbors labeled him mad and his servants untrustworthy. Tenants believed the Marquis was financially ruined, a view reinforced by his extreme frugality and habit of paying cash, avoiding credit.

The Marquis and his family were noble by birth, embodying ideals becoming rare in France. They retained a deep sense of dignity and pride in their lineage, which clashed with egalitarian sentiments among their neighbors. This disconnect fostered animosity, as tenants resented the d’Espards for perceived superiority despite apparent financial strain.

M. d’Espard, about fifty, exemplified the aristocracy of his time. He had a distinguished, somewhat cold appearance that commanded respect. A slightly crooked nose and a prominent forehead lent an eccentric air that fueled rumors of madness. Despite elegant manners, he was frugal, wearing the same coat for years.

His sons, Comte Clément de Négrepelisse and Vicomte Camille d’Espard, were handsome and displayed aristocratic grace. They inherited their father’s features and their mother’s dignified manner. The elder, nearly sixteen, had moved to more fashionable attire, while the younger still wore a round jacket. The Marquis concentrated on giving them a rounded education, emphasizing mathematics, Eastern languages, and history, while avoiding what he considered unproductive studies.

The day Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard fell on a Thursday holiday. About nine, before their father was up, the two sons were in the garden. The younger begged to visit the shooting-gallery for the first time and pressed Clément to support the request to the Marquis. The Viscount often teased Clément and delighted in wrestling with him. Their noisy quarrel roused their father, who watched amused from the window. Two neighbors, observing, commented that the Marquis seemed mad to make his children fight. Noticing them, the Marquis called the boys; they leapt into his room and, after a moment’s hesitation, Clement obtained permission to go.

All through the house people were talking about the Marquis’ supposed new madness. When Popinot arrived near noon with his clerk, the portress led him to the third floor and repeated the rumor that M. d’Espard had made his children fight that morning and had laughed when the younger bit the elder until he bled; she added that the Marquis rarely showed himself. The landing’s muddy floor and grimy banisters lent some credibility to her gossip and made Popinot, though impartial, receptive to the tale.

“This is the man who swallows enough to feed the whole neighborhood,” the porter’s wife declared. The clerk smiled; Popinot controlled his expression. They entered an outer office where an elderly man, probably the office clerk, sat amid shelves of published numbers; a grating separated a private room and a cash-desk indicated a commercial enterprise.

“M. d’Espard?” Popinot asked the man in a gray blouse. The shopman opened the next room to reveal the Marquis: a venerable, simply dressed old man wearing the Cross of Saint-Louis, seated at a desk. Popinot explained they wished to speak on private business. After formalities, the Marquis asked their purpose. Popinot introduced himself: “Monsieur le Marquis, my duties and the inquiry that brings me here require us to be alone, although such inquiries are usually public. I am a judge on the Inferior Court of Appeal for the Department of the Seine, charged with examining you regarding a petition for a Commission in Lunacy filed by the Marquise d’Espard.” The old man withdrew; the clerk sat to take notes. The Marquis reddened and trembled, then steadied himself.

“How is it,” he asked, “that I have had no notice of such a petition?” Popinot explained that notice is unnecessary because those thought subject to such a commission are presumed to lack reason; the Court must verify the petitioner’s claims. The Marquis accepted this and asked what he must do. Popinot urged him to answer fully and confidentially, especially concerning delicate accusations Madame d’Espard might make. The Marquis feared that his explanations could bring blame on his wife. Popinot promised discretion and the customary courtesy should the inquiry reveal a misunderstanding.

Popinot confronted the major allegation: that the Marquis had dissipated his fortune for the benefit of Madame Jeanrenaud, widow of a bargemaster, or her son, Colonel Jeanrenaud, for whom he allegedly procured an appointment and exhausted influence with the King. The Marquis flushed; tears came, but his pride returned quickly.

“You place me in a strange dilemma,” he said. Revealing his motives would mean disclosing secret wounds and trusting the family’s honor to strangers. Popinot assured him of confidentiality. Freed from reserve, the Marquis then spoke with clarity and passion.

After their marriage his wife had incurred heavy expenses that forced him to borrow. He could not afford a steward; most of his title-deeds had been taken to Paris by his father, who feared scrutiny. Their family name was Negrepelisse; the title d’Espard had been acquired through marriage in the time of Henri IV, when they lost the town of Negrepelisse in the religious wars and an ancestor was ruined by the destruction of his property. Though a later ancestor secured favor with Louis XIV and fortune, a stain of blood and disgrace remained, and the Marquis was determined to erase it.

He had discovered that his grandfather had obtained the lands of Negrepelisse through means tied to the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes: a Protestant merchant was arrested and hanged, and the land passed to his ancestor by iniquitous means. The merchant’s family had the rightful claim; the victim’s name was Jeanrenaud. The Marquis could not bear the disgrace and resolved to find the heirs. Eventually he located M. Jeanrenaud, reduced to poverty and serving as a lieutenant under Napoleon. Believing the heir’s rights incontestable and their lack of means to sue, he offered to pay them the lands’ value at the time they were taken—eleven hundred thousand francs—payable at his convenience and without interest.

To effect restitution he lived on little for a long time. When he proposed to Madame d’Espard that they move to the country to live on half her income, she treated him as mad; her coldness convinced him she would have condoned his ancestors’ crimes. Terrified by her attitude, he resolved to leave her in control of her fortune after settling their debts, and to take charge of educating their children himself, determined to raise them as gentlemen. He invested prudently, repaid debt sooner than expected, and had recently completed the payment of the eleven hundred thousand francs, making restitution without harming his children's prospects. These were the reasons for payments to Madame Jeanrenaud and her son.

Popinot asked whether Madame d’Espard knew his motives for retiring from public life. “Yes, monsieur,” the Marquis replied. Popinot, visibly moved, excused his clerk. He then inquired about other allegations, such as that the Marquis was engaged in a business unbecoming his rank. The Marquis declined to discuss it there and summoned Comte de Nouvion, an old family friend, explaining that he used the rooms as offices for the publication of the Picturesque History of China by a reputable firm, not as his private residence.

He showed Popinot his ground-floor apartment—a place of simple, touching harmony that struck the judge. The pleasant weather and garden convinced Popinot that a madman would hardly produce such beauty. The Marquis said he intended to remain until his younger son finished his studies, then travel with the children to European capitals to further their education and languages. He explained his China project: Comte de Nouvion, returned to France without fortune, was his partner; the Abbé Grozier, Keeper of the Books at the Arsenal, had been his tutor and taught him about China. Though enamored of China’s history, he remained a French gentleman. Financially, they had two thousand five hundred subscribers—twelve hundred in France—the book would cost about three hundred francs, and the Comte would earn six to seven thousand francs a year from it; the hundred thousand francs he hoped to make would cover his children’s education and pleasures. Twelve years of retirement to educate his children had cost him his memory at Court and political prospects, but he believed he had purified his family’s past and secured a glorious future for his children—a noble, if secret, task.

At that moment horses clattered in the courtyard. The two boys arrived, robust and sunburnt, dressed for riding. They greeted their father and the lawyer. Popinot needed no further evidence of the Marquis’ bond with his sons. The boys reported shooting success and that they had ridden in the Bois, where they had seen their mother. “Why didn’t you speak to her?” the Marquis asked. “I think I noticed, father, that she doesn’t want us to speak to her in public,” Clément whispered. A cloud passed over the Marquis’ brow; Popinot felt deep sympathy at the scene of fatherly devotion. “Justice may look in here at any time,” the Marquis said. “If anyone is mad, it can only be the children, who are a little crazy about their father, and the father who is very crazy about his children—but that kind of madness rings true.”

Madame Jeanrenaud then entered, disregarding protests from the man-servant. “I take no roundabout ways!” she exclaimed. Bowing to those present, she announced she had come too late because the criminal judge was there. She declared that she and her son were of one mind to return everything, for their honor was at stake; they preferred to give back everything rather than cause trouble. “They must be as stupid as pans without handles to call you a lunatic,” she added. The boys clung to their father, alarmed.

“Silence, madame,” Popinot interposed. The Marquis sent his children to the garden. Popinot addressed Madame Jeanrenaud: the payments made by the Marquis were legally due, though prompted by an expansive sense of honesty. If every possessor of confiscated goods were compelled to make restitution every century and a half, few legitimate owners would remain in France. The law allowed M. d’Espard to dispose of his income without accounting for it. A Commission in Lunacy is appropriate only when actions lack reason; here, the remittances were driven by the highest motives. She could keep the money without remorse and let the world misinterpret a noble action. Popinot declared he would rather see the Marquis crowned than subjected to a Commission. Madame Jeanrenaud applauded his defense, calling it judge-like and warmly praising his words.

The Marquis extended his hand to Popinot, who pressed it gently; a private understanding passed between the humane judge and the noble, sublime man. “Monsieur le Marquis,” Popinot added, “from the first words of this inquiry, I regarded my clerk as unnecessary.” Leading M. d’Espard to the window, he said it was time to return home and that Madame la Marquise had likely acted under an influence the Marquis should counteract. Popinot left, the scene lingering with him. “Those rooms would suit me perfectly,” he thought. “If M. d’Espard leaves them, I will take up his lease.”

The next morning, having written his report the night before, Popinot went to the Palais de Justice to pursue prompt justice. As he prepared to put on his gown, the usher said the President wished to see him privately. “Good morning, my dear Popinot,” the President greeted. “I’ve been waiting.” Popinot asked if something was wrong. The President called it a silly trifle: the Keeper of the Seals, with whom he had dined, had suggested it would be best if Popinot did not sit on the case. Despite Popinot’s protest that he had left Madame d’Espard’s house before tea, the President cited propriety—“Caesar’s wife must not be suspected”—and the need to avoid any appearance of conflict that might expose the Bench to journalistic malice. It was a matter of propriety, not discipline.

Popinot was to signal his retirement from the case as a personal favor; in recompense, the President would secure the Cross of the Legion of Honor for him, long deserved. M. Camusot, a newly appointed judge from a provincial court, had been named in Popinot’s place. Popinot noted with ironic amusement Camusot’s pale ambition. He bowed and withdrew, choosing not to contest the implication.
