DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

THE ILLUSTRIOUS GAUDISSART

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller, unknown to antiquity, is one of the striking figures born of our age. He may mark for future thinkers the transition that joins an era of isolated genius to one of widespread, leveling power—equalizing products, spreading them among the masses, and obeying the principle of unity. As stage-coaches carry men and things, so the commercial traveller bears ideas: he takes a handful of light from the centre and scatters it through the drowsier regions. He is a scholar without learning, a juggler who dupes himself, an unbelieving priest of mysteries he expounds the better for his want of faith.

Curious creature! He has seen everything in name, not in depth. He affects provincial familiarity while soaked in Parisian vices; he links village and capital yet is neither. His glance skims surfaces; he measures things with a little tape-line. He knows a great deal without being penetrative; much occupies him, though nothing truly does. A jester and jolly fellow, he keeps on good terms with every political opinion and is patriotic to the core. A capital mimic, he can turn on persuasive smiles or drop them for his natural expression.

By trade he must observe and probe men at a glance, guess their habits, wants, and above all their solvency. To economize time he decides quickly on his chances—becoming thereby a kind of judge. He judges theatres and discourses about Parisian and provincial stages.

He knows all the good and bad haunts of France and can pilot you to vice or virtue with equal assurance. Blessèd with the volubility of a hot-water spigot, he can run a stream of phrases to produce upon his victims a moral shower-bath. Loquacious as a cricket, he smokes, drinks, wears trinkets, overawes the common people, passes for a lord in the villages, and never allows himself to be “stumped.” He knows how to show his money at the right moment to reassure suspicious servants. His activity is astonishing: none of the hawk, the stag, or the hounds equals him in the rapidity with which he spies a commission, trips up a rival, or noses out a customer.

How many qualities must such a man possess! In every country you find these diplomats of low degree—consummate negotiators for calico, jewels, frippery, wines—often more diplomatical than many ambassadors who know only its forms. No one in France can doubt the powers of the commercial traveller, this intrepid soul who pits the genius of civilization and Parisian inventions against provincial common sense. He worms himself into the minds of the populace with a volume of words, like a file eating slowly into porphyry. To hear one of these emissaries of Parisian industry work a purse is to see language exert the utmost pressure against reluctant lucre.

“Monsieur,” said a shrewd political economist, director-cashier-manager and secretary-general of a celebrated fire-insurance company, “out of every five hundred thousand francs of policies to be renewed in the provinces, not more than fifty thousand are paid up voluntarily. The other four hundred and fifty thousand are got in by the activity of our agents, who go about among those who are in arrears and worry them with stories of horrible incendiaries until they are driven to sign the new policies. Thus you see that eloquence, the labial flux, is nine tenths of the ways and means of our business.”

To talk and make people listen is seduction itself. “A conversation of two hours ought to capture your man,” said a retired lawyer. The commercial traveller must be quick of decision, ready with phrases, and able to read the man before him.

Picture his uniform: olive-green overcoat, morocco-collared cloak, striped blue cotton shirt. In this singular figure many personalities meet: acrobat, circus, battery, tongue—all in one. Intrepid mariner, he plunges armed with a few phrases to catch five or six thousand francs in the frozen seas of the interior. Provincial fish will not rise to harpoons; they yield to seines and gentle persuasion. The traveller’s task is to extract gold from country caches by intellectual operations, pleasantly and without pain. Think of the flood of phrases that, renewed each dawn, leaps over all of sunny France.

Now the individual. In Paris lives an incomparable commercial traveller, the paragon of his race, possessing in the highest degree the qualifications for success. His speech is both vitriol and glue—glue to entangle his victim, vitriol to dissolve hard heads and close fists. Once he sold hats, but his talent in snaring the wariest provincial won him such renown that vendors of “the article Paris” courted him to take their commissions.

When in Paris he lived in perpetual festivity; in the provinces he was made much of. Welcomed, flattered, and fed, it became a novelty to breakfast alone. He lived like a sovereign, or like a journalist; in fact he was the perambulating “feuilleton” of Parisian commerce.

His name was Gaudissart, and his renown won him the surname Illustrious. Wherever he went—behind a counter, before a bar, into a salon, into a stage-coach, up to a garret, or to dine with a banker—everyone said at sight, “Ah! here comes the illustrious Gaudissart!” No name suited him better. Puns, horse-laugh, a monkish face, a Rabelaisian exterior: clothing, body, mind, and features all conspired to put devil-may-care jollity into every inch of his person. Free-handed and easy-going, he was the favorite of grisettes, leapt to the top of a stage-coach, steadied timid ladies, joked with postillions, sold caps, smiled at maids and caught them by the waist or heart; gurgled at dinner like a bottle of wine, pretended to draw corks by a whisper on his cheek, played tunes with his knife on champagne glasses, and chaffed travellers while securing the titbits for himself. Yet beneath the jest was a strong fellow who, when business required it, would fling away a cigar stump and say, with a glance at some town, “I’ll go and see what those people have got in their stomachs.”

When at work he was the slyest diplomat. All things to all men, he could accost a banker like a capitalist, a magistrate like a functionary, a royalist with pious respect, a bourgeois as one of themselves. He left Gaudissart at the door when he entered and picked him up when he came out.

Until 1830 the illustrious Gaudissart was faithful to the article Paris. Commerce had taught him the windings of the human heart. He had learned persuasive eloquence, the knack of loosening the tightest purse-strings, the art of stirring desire in husbands, wives, children, and servants; and he knew how to satisfy it. No one better inveigled a merchant with the charm of a bargain and vanished when desire reached crisis. Grateful to the hat trade, he always declared that mapping the exterior of the human head enabled him to understand its interior—he had capped and crowned so many people, he would say; jokes about hats and heads abounded.

After August and October 1830 he abandoned hats and the article Paris and rose into the higher spheres of Parisian speculation. “He forsook,” in his words, “matter for mind; manufactured products for the infinitely purer elaborations of human intelligence.” This needs explanation.

The upheaval of 1830 brought back many old ideas which clever speculators dressed in new bodies. After 1830, ideas became property. “More ideas are stolen than pocket-handkerchiefs,” said a writer too wise to publish. Perhaps in time there will be an Exchange for thought; already ideas, good or bad, have their consols, are bought, sold, and quoted like stocks. If ideas are not on hand, speculators pass off words instead and live upon them as birds live on millet. In a land where a ticket on a sack is more important than its contents, a word can be worth as much as an idea. We have seen libraries trading on “picturesque” while literature would have cut the throat of “fantastic.” Fiscal genius has guessed the right tax on intellect, estimated the profits of advertising, and registered prospectuses of quantity and value of thought, weighing intellectual property at the Stamp Office in the Rue de la Paix.

Having become an article of commerce, intellect and its products must obey the laws that bind other manufactured interests. Thus ideas, conceived by idle Parisians—who nevertheless quarrel over champagne and pheasants—are handed at birth to commercial travelers employed to spread them “urbi et orbi,” seasoned with advertisements and prospectuses that trap the departmental rodent called subscriber, sometimes stockholder, occasionally corresponding member or patron, but invariably fool.

“I am a fool!” many a poor country proprietor has said when, tempted to be first to launch a new idea, he finds he has thrown his thousand or twelve hundred francs into a gulf.

“Subscribers are fools who never can be brought to understand that to go ahead in the intellectual world they must start with more money than they need for the tour of Europe,” say the speculators.

Consequently there is endless warfare between the recalcitrant public, which refuses to pay Parisian imposts, and the tax-gatherer who, living by his receipt of custom, lards the public with new ideas, turns it on the spit of lively projects, roasts it with prospectuses, basting with flattery, and finally gobbles it up with some toothsome sauce. Since 1830 honors and emoluments have been scattered through France to stimulate the zeal and self-love of the “progressive and intelligent masses”: titles, medals, diplomas—a legion of honor for martyrs—follow each other rapidly. Speculators in manufactured intellect have a spice all their own: premiums, forestalled dividends, and the conscription of noted names levied without the knowledge of the unlucky writers whose patronymics are thus filched. Worse is the rape of ideas, torn from the parental brain before maturity and dragged half-clothed before the eyes of the public—which, failing to be amused, will cut off their heads by emptying their pockets.

This madness of our epoch reached the illustrious Gaudissart. A life-insurance company, having heard of his irresistible eloquence, offered him an unheard-of commission, which he accepted. Trained by the secretary-general in the business’s dark holes, dialect, gullible public, impromptu replies, and unanswerable arguments, Gaudissart sharpened his tongue to operate upon the life of France.

He rewarded his trainers: the company boasted of him so loudly that the proprietors of two then-prominent newspapers—the Globe, a Saint-Simonian organ, and the Movement, a republican journal—offered him cash per subscriber if he brought in a thousand or five hundred. Political journalism did not interfere with his life-insurance work; the bargain was struck. He even demanded indemnity from the Saint-Simonians for eight days spent studying their doctrines, claiming prodigious mental effort to grasp that “article,” though he asked nothing from the republicans. He inclined to republican ideas, had dipped into conspiracies, been arrested and released, and joked of needing only a certain hat and spurs to represent the Republic.

For a week he attended the Globe each morning and trained at the life-insurance company each afternoon. By April 15, the opening of the spring campaign, he was ready. Two large commercial houses begged him not to desert the article Paris and wooed him with offers; he yielded.

“Listen, my little Jenny,” he said in a hackney-coach to Jenny Courand, a pretty florist who tyrannized him with demands to prove their rumored private marriage by gifts and attentions. Gaudissart loved being dominated by her and promised to refurnish her room with all the “Children” he would get in the provinces—“Children” being the slang for subscribers to the proposed Children’s Journal. Jenny, skeptical of his promise of riches from five-sous subscriptions, teased him about politics and prison. He answered with grand ambitions: shareholder like Finot, peer of France, ambassador to London; his rhetoric parodied parliamentary bluster. Yet for the time being, he vowed devotion to capitalists, the Globe, the Movement, the Children, and his old article Paris.

Their banter revealed his vanity and self-delusion: inflated speeches about the Press as an institution, grandiose claims about personal destiny and influence, and comic political theatrics. Gaudissart, thirty-eight, stout, pear-shaped, with a round face and ruddy cheeks—an image of Abundance, Law, Force, Commerce—kissed Jenny and set out the next morning to work the towns assigned by his enterprises. After forty-five days between Paris and Blois and two weeks in Blois, he wrote Jenny of lodging two millions between Paris and Blois, selling many Ternaux shawls at Orléans, lamenting that the Movement did not move republicans, boasting of making a hundred Globes and two thousand Children subscriptions, and promising to “topple” Tours.

From Tours he went to Vouvray, a rich district where the public mind seemed susceptible. Trotting along the embankment, admiring the landscape, he little dreamed his commercial infallibility was about to perish.

A note on the Tourangian mind: stamped by Rabelais’ spirit, Touraine’s people are polished, witty, poetic, voluptuous; the softness of the climate and ease of life smother sentiment of art and enervate will. At home the Tourangian lounges, employing wit in neighborly raillery; Touraine is like Rabelais’ Abbaye of Theleme—good cheer reigns.

Gaudissart encountered in Vouvray a native wit, Monsieur Vernier, a former dyer, a leader of local gossip who especially hated Saint-Simonian doctrines. After consulting the landlord Mitouflet, Gaudissart betook himself to Vernier. He launched his patter: bankers, respectable houses, interests guaranteed—the usual phrases. Vernier feigned interest but planned a malicious joke: to fetch the town’s supposed banker—Margaritis, a half-insane man—and have Gaudissart deal with him.

Margaritis lived at the head of the Valley Coquette. Of Italian origin and half-mad, married but childless, he carried knives, hoarded flour and wine, and demanded newspapers, reading the same “Indre-et-Loire” for seven years without noticing repetition. At a certain hour he would rise mechanically and order company away. He had lucid intervals in which he advised on wine sales; at times he became dangerous. The evening before Gaudissart reached Vouvray, Madame Margaritis had to calm him and beg neighbors to help with rolling puncheons into the cellar to appease him. Vernier intended to use this scene as part of the joke.

Vernier escorted Gaudissart to La Fuye, the Margaritis house. The lunatic, seated in a yellow Utrecht-velvet arm-chair, received them. Gaudissart, turning his watch-key with a click that kept Margaritis quiet, launched into his life-insurance argument. Time is the most precious social wealth; to economize time is to become wealthy. Bankers had “capitalized” future prospects by discounting time—cashing talents and moral qualities into money—so the company would recognize and insure an artist’s future and secure his family. The insured artist could borrow on company paper at the Bourse; insurance was the exchequer of beneficence, a mutual defense against poverty. The company offered annuities better than tontines. Margaritis interrupted.

“That is usury!” he cried.

Gaudissart, undeterred, offered wine. Margaritis poured Vouvray of 1819 and expounded the etymology of capital—caput—the Head of Vouvray. He even offered to sell his puncheons. Gaudissart persisted: civilization denies bread to those who supply luxury; insurance bridges the gulf. Margaritis, amused, blustered local responses—“Twice; on foot”—pretended to comprehend Saint-Simonian doctrine, and bargained humorously, offering seven francs for the Children’s Journal and consenting to push the Globe if Gaudissart took his wine. They strolled among the vines amid laughter from the women and Vernier.

At a card-table Gaudissart wrote an order for delivery of the two casks; Margaritis read it, counted out seven francs for the Children’s Journal, and gave them to Gaudissart. Being a Norman who liked reciprocal agreements, Gaudissart demanded Margaritis sign a bond to deliver the puncheons. Margaritis signed. Gaudissart left humming triumphantly.

At the Soleil d’Or Gaudissart conversed with Mitouflet, who hinted Margaritis was a lunatic. When Vernier’s prank was revealed, Gaudissart, enraged, confronted Vernier in his salon.

“Monsieur,” he began, “you are a scoundrel and a blackguard; under pain of being thought a turn-key—you will give me satisfaction for the insult you dared to offer me in sending me to a lunatic! Do you hear me, Monsieur Vernier, dyer?”

Vernier, delighted, defended the joke: why not make fun of a man who wanted them to invest in a paper preaching a new religion that denied inheritance? Père Margaritis, he said, had spoken more sensibly.

Gaudissart attempted to slap Vernier but the crowd interposed; his wig fell on Mademoiselle Clara Vernier’s head. He declared he would be at the Soleil d’Or until morning, ready to show what giving satisfaction meant; he had “fought in July.” Vernier answered he would “fight in Vouvray” and make the traveller stay longer than he imagined.

The town was in a ferment over the duel. Madame Fontanieu and the assistant-mayor implored Mitouflet to arrange things to avoid a violent death. He took charge. In the evening Mitouflet brought pens and paper to Gaudissart.

“If you are going to fight to-morrow,” Mitouflet said, “you had better make some settlement of your affairs; perhaps you have letters to write. Are you a good swordsman? I have foils.” They practiced; Mitouflet’s experience won him sixty-two passes. He advised pistols, suggesting they load them to fire wide so both could retire with honor. Gaudissart fretted about possibly killing the man; Mitouflet calmed him.

Next morning the adversaries met by the bridge of La Cise. Vernier nearly shot a cow; they embraced after firing in the air. “Your joke was rough, but it was a good one,” said Gaudissart; Vernier announced twenty subscriptions to the Children’s Journal. They breakfasted together, for men who have fought often come to good understanding.

Back at the inn Gaudissart wanted to summon Margaritis for delivery of the casks. “But he has not got them,” said Vernier. Gaudissart demanded indemnity and insisted on pursuing the claim. Madame Margaritis, alarmed, brought thirty francs to the traveller; he accepted it and left Vouvray content with the settlement, though recognizing the region’s resistance to new ideas.

On returning from his southern trip, Gaudissart rode in a diligence and explained the enigmas of life to a young man as if to an infant. Passing Vouvray the young man exclaimed at the fine site; Gaudissart, patronizing, replied, “Yes, Monsieur, but not habitable on account of the people. You get into duels every day. Why, it is not three months since I fought one just there,” pointing to the bridge, “with a damned dyer; but I made an end of him—he bit the dust!”
