In 1828, at around one o’clock in the morning, two friends, Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac, left a house in the Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré. They decided to walk to the boulevard, as neither had a carriage available. Their conversation turned to the Marquise d’Espard, with Bianchon expressing skepticism about her character. Rastignac, however, was infatuated with her status and beauty, believing she could elevate his social standing and help him pay off debts.

Bianchon warned Rastignac that pursuing the Marquise would be a mistake, comparing her to a “one-eyed horse” and suggesting that her true age was more than what she claimed. He emphasized that a woman’s physical signs reveal her true age, regardless of cosmetics. Rastignac countered that he was drawn to the Marquise for her social power and potential to improve his life, dismissing Bianchon’s concerns about her character.

Bianchon, a physician, shared his disdain for women of fashion, arguing that they often lack genuine emotion and are driven by selfishness. He described the Marquise as a polished woman who hides her true nature behind a facade. Rastignac, however, argued that a woman of fashion could be a valuable ally for an ambitious man, providing social leverage and connections.

Despite Bianchon’s reservations, he agreed to help Rastignac by introducing him to his uncle, Jean-Jules Popinot, a judge who might assist in the Marquise’s affairs. Bianchon was skeptical about Popinot’s willingness to help, given his reputation for being unyielding.

The next morning, Bianchon set out to visit Popinot, who lived in a dilapidated building in a poor part of Paris. The description of the building highlighted its age and the contrast between its former grandeur and current state. Popinot, a judge, was characterized by his unkempt appearance and lack of ambition, which had led to his stagnation in the legal profession. Despite his intelligence and capability, he had been overlooked for promotions and remained a supernumerary judge.

Popinot’s life was marked by a commitment to justice, often at the expense of his own well-being. He was known for his kindness and ability to extract confessions without coercion, demonstrating a blend of compassion and firmness in his role. His character was further explored, revealing a man dedicated to his work, yet burdened by the injustices he witnessed in the legal system.

As Bianchon approached Popinot’s residence, he reflected on the challenge of persuading his uncle to assist Rastignac, aware of the complexities of the legal world and the obstacles that lay ahead.

Twelve years prior to this narrative, in 1816, during a severe food scarcity in France, Popinot was appointed President of the Commission Extraordinary to distribute food to the impoverished in his neighborhood. He had intended to leave the Rue du Fouarre, a place he and his wife disliked. As a prominent lawyer and criminal judge, Popinot had spent five years observing legal outcomes without understanding their causes. His experiences with the destitute led him to develop deep compassion, transforming him into a protector of the poor, akin to a modern-day Saint Vincent de Paul.

This transformation was gradual; charity, like vice, has its temptations. Popinot moved from one act of charity to another, eventually becoming a vital figure in his community, involved in various charitable organizations. He worked tirelessly to prevent crime, provide employment, and assist the vulnerable, all while maintaining a secret life that few in Paris recognized. The gratitude of those he helped was often unexpressed, as they were too burdened by their struggles to acknowledge his kindness.

By the end of his second year of charitable work, Popinot had converted a storeroom in his home into a parlor for the needy. This room, filled with wooden benches and basic furniture, served as a hub for the poor, where they could seek assistance. Popinot meticulously recorded donations and requests for aid, ensuring he could manage his resources effectively. His aide, Lavienne, assisted him by managing pawn tickets and visiting areas most affected by famine.

From early morning until late, the parlor was crowded with women, children, and the destitute seeking help. The atmosphere was often stifling, but the warmth of the crowd negated the need for heating. The poor revered Popinot, often waiting outside his door before dawn, and even criminals respected his home. He dedicated his mornings to the needy, afternoons to legal work, and evenings to his judicial duties.

Popinot's keen observational skills allowed him to discern the hidden virtues in the impoverished, as well as the faintest signs of wrongdoing in others. His modest inherited fortune was supplemented by his late wife's wealth, but his salary as a judge was limited. This financial reality necessitated his frugality, which was further compounded by his selfless dedication to charity. Despite his significant contributions, he remained one of the few judges in the Court of the Seine without the Legion of Honor.

Popinot was tasked by the President of the Second Chamber of the Court to examine the Marquis d’Espard at the request of his wife, who sought a Commission in Lunacy. The Rue du Fouarre, bustling with the needy in the early hours, would become desolate by nine o'clock. Bianchon, Popinot's nephew, rode to find his uncle amidst his charitable work, anticipating the contrast between Popinot's appearance and the Marquise d’Espard's expectations.

Upon arrival, Bianchon was greeted by the familiar sight of the parlor filled with the destitute, each person a testament to the struggles of poverty. The room was a tableau of hardship, with individuals displaying a range of emotions from despair to resilience. Popinot, dressed in a worn nightcap and dressing gown, was deeply engrossed in his work, embodying the dedication of a man committed to alleviating the suffering around him.

As Bianchon approached, he noted the peculiarities of the assembly, where the faces of the poor told stories of their struggles. Popinot, despite his disheveled appearance, was a figure of authority and compassion, diligently attending to each individual’s needs. As the morning progressed, Bianchon learned that Popinot had seen over two hundred individuals that day, with more waiting. He was instructed to attend to urgent medical matters, and upon his return, he found the parlor empty, the last of the needy having received help.

Bianchon argued that it would be beneficial for Popinot to meet her in her own environment, where she could express her concerns more comfortably.

After some persuasion, Popinot agreed to visit the Marquise, recognizing the importance of understanding her perspective in the case. To understand the ensuing interview, a brief overview of Madame d’Espard is necessary. For seven years, she had been a fashionable figure in Paris, where trends can elevate or diminish individuals. After separating from her husband in 1815, Madame d’Espard, around thirty-three years old, maintained her status as a fashionable woman, despite being a mother of two boys aged fifteen and thirteen. Her ability to remain in vogue was remarkable, as Fashion often favors youth and beauty.

The Marquise, who appeared much younger, employed various methods to maintain her looks, including elaborate hairstyles and a strict regimen of cold baths and minimal food. Her efforts mirrored those of a famous Polish lady who, despite her age, retained the vitality of youth. Madame d’Espard’s complexion was clear, and her figure retained the allure that attracted men, though she remained emotionally detached, knowing neither love nor hatred. She had parted from M. d’Espard with satisfaction, as he took their children, whom she viewed as obstacles to her ambitions.

Living on an income of twenty-six thousand francs a year, the Marquise hesitated before deciding how to manage her life post-separation. Although she had the means to live lavishly, she chose a more subdued lifestyle during the years following her separation. By 1820, she re-emerged into society, hosting gatherings and establishing herself among the elite, eventually taking the place of other prominent women in Paris.

Madame d’Espard cultivated relationships with influential figures, including a duchess and a countess, which enhanced her political clout. Her drawing-room became a hub for political discussions, and she was known for her discretion and ability to navigate the complexities of Parisian society. Among her admirers were ambitious young men, and she skillfully maintained her independence while wielding considerable influence.

Despite her apparent charm, Madame d’Espard was also known for her indiscretion and vanity. She viewed relationships as a means to an end, seeking to expand her influence rather than genuine connections. Her drawing-room was frequented by notable figures, and she adeptly managed her interactions to avoid compromising herself.

Rastignac, a young man seeking to align himself with her, recognized her potential as a powerful ally but found himself challenged by her cunning. The Marquise’s household was grand, reflecting her wealth and status, and when Popinot arrived, he noted the opulence surrounding him. The Marquise welcomed him with feigned weakness, and a third man, the Chevalier d’Espard, stood silently, observing.

Popinot, while appearing simple, was astute and quickly assessed the situation. He engaged Madame d’Espard in conversation, questioning her about her husband’s mental state and their separation. She presented a carefully crafted narrative, claiming her husband’s change in temperament led to their estrangement. Popinot, however, remained skeptical, probing deeper into the circumstances surrounding their separation and the well-being of her children.

As the conversation progressed, Popinot’s inquiries revealed inconsistencies in Madame d’Espard’s story. He noted the lavishness of her lifestyle compared to her claims of financial hardship, suggesting that her motives for seeking control over her husband’s affairs might be self-serving. The Marquise, feeling cornered, attempted to deflect his questions, but Popinot’s persistence forced her to confront the implications of her situation.

The tension in the room escalated as Popinot laid bare the potential consequences of her actions. He emphasized the need for transparency and honesty, warning her that the court would scrutinize her motives. Despite her attempts to maintain composure, the Marquise realized that her carefully constructed facade was at risk of crumbling.

After the meeting, Bianchon and Rastignac discussed Popinot’s character and the implications of the case. They recognized the complexity of the situation and the potential for scandal, with Bianchon expressing concern over the Marquise’s influence. Popinot, meanwhile, resolved to investigate further, planning to summon Madame Jeanrenaud, the woman linked to M. d’Espard, to clarify the situation.

The following day, Madame Jeanrenaud arrived at Popinot’s office, presenting a stark contrast to the Marquise. Described as stout and unrefined, she denied any wrongdoing and expressed concern for M. d’Espard’s well-being. Her honest demeanor and straightforwardness provided a stark contrast to the Marquise’s calculated charm.

Popinot, intrigued by her sincerity, began to piece together the narrative surrounding M. d’Espard’s financial dealings. He recognized that the case was more complicated than it initially appeared, with potential implications for both the Marquise and her husband. As he prepared to confront the complexities of the situation, he understood that the truth would require careful examination of all parties involved.

People who have matured understand how seemingly trivial incidents can significantly influence major events, as illustrated by Popinot's minor illness, which delayed a crucial meeting. The judge, feeling slightly unwell, chose to stay home instead of visiting the Marquis d’Espard, resulting in a lost day that echoed historical delays with far-reaching consequences.

Before following the lawyer and his clerk to the Marquis d’Espard’s residence, it is essential to consider the home life of this father, whom his wife’s petition labeled a madman. In old Paris, some buildings still reflect a desire for decoration and permanence. The house where M. d’Espard lived on Rue de la Montagne-Sainte-Genevieve was one such structure, built of stone and once grand, though now weathered and damaged by time and revolutions. Once home to dignitaries, it had become a residence for various tenants, including a printing establishment that had degraded its interior.

M. d’Espard occupied the ground floor, likely for the benefit of his children and their health, as the garden was spacious for the area. He had secured the rooms at a reasonable rent, given the state of disrepair and the low market at the time. The house, built during the reigns of Henry III, IV, and Louis XIII, still bore traces of its former glory, including a perron leading to the entrance and a garden.

The Marquis had decorated his home in rich brown tones, creating a peaceful atmosphere with carefully chosen furnishings and a harmonious color scheme. Few visitors were welcomed into the family’s private life, which was largely secluded from the public eye. The family consisted of the Marquis, his two sons, and a small staff, including a long-serving cook and a man-servant, both of whom contributed to the household's orderly and quiet environment.

M. d’Espard’s decision to avoid socializing with other tenants stemmed from his desire to protect his children from outside influences. This choice, however, led to gossip and suspicion among the other residents, who labeled him mad and his servants as untrustworthy. The tenants believed M. d’Espard was financially ruined, which was exacerbated by his extreme frugality and the fact that he paid for everything in cash, avoiding credit.

The Marquis and his family were noble by birth, embodying ideals that had become rare in France. They held a deep sense of dignity and pride in their lineage, which clashed with the egalitarian sentiments of their neighbors. This disconnect fostered animosity, as the tenants resented the d’Espards for their perceived superiority despite their financial struggles.

M. d’Espard, around fifty, exemplified the aristocracy of his time. He had a distinguished appearance, marked by a certain coldness that commanded respect. His physical traits, including a slightly crooked nose and a prominent forehead, contributed to an impression of eccentricity, which fueled rumors of madness. Despite his elegant demeanor, he was frugal, wearing the same coat for years.

His sons, Comte Clement de Negrepelisse and Vicomte Camille d’Espard, were both handsome and displayed an aristocratic grace. They inherited their father’s features and their mother’s dignified manner. The elder, nearing sixteen, had transitioned to more fashionable attire, while the younger still wore a round jacket. The Marquis focused on providing a well-rounded education for his sons, emphasizing subjects like mathematics, Eastern languages, and history, while avoiding unnecessary studies he deemed unproductive.

The day Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard was a Thursday holiday. Around nine in the morning, before their father awoke, the brothers were playing in the garden. Clement struggled to refuse his brother, who was eager to visit the shooting-gallery for the first time and begged him to support his request to the Marquis. The Viscount often took advantage of Clement's weakness and enjoyed wrestling with him. Their playful quarrel made enough noise to wake their father, who watched them from the window, amused by their antics. Two other observers commented on the Marquis’ apparent madness for making his children fight. Noticing the onlookers, the Marquis called his sons, who jumped into his room, and Clement received permission from Camille.

Throughout the house, people discussed the Marquis’ new form of insanity. When Popinot arrived around noon with his clerk, the portress led him to the third floor, mentioning that M. d’Espard had made his children fight that morning and laughed at the younger one biting the elder until he bled. She added that he rarely showed himself.

As she spoke, they reached a door covered with notices for the Picturesque History of China. The disarray of the landing, with its muddy floor and dirty banisters, aligned with the portress's claims, leading Popinot to believe them despite his impartiality.

“There you are, gentlemen,” said the porter's wife, “the man who swallows enough to feed the whole neighborhood.”

The clerk smiled at Popinot, who struggled to maintain his composure. They entered an outer room where an old man, likely the office clerk, sat. This man was the Maitre Jacques of China. Long shelves lined the walls, filled with published numbers. A wooden partition with a grating formed a private office, and a cash-desk indicated the financial side of the operation.

“M. d’Espard?” Popinot asked the man in a gray blouse.

The shopman opened the door to the next room, where they found a venerable old man, simply dressed and wearing the Cross of Saint-Louis, seated at a desk. He looked up from his work as they entered.

“The Marquis d’Espard?” Popinot inquired.

“No, monsieur,” the old man replied, rising. “What do you want with him?”

“We wish to speak with him on personal business,” Popinot said.

“D’Espard, here are gentlemen who want to see you,” the old man called, going into the furthest room where the Marquis sat reading.

This room had shabby furnishings, and the old man led Popinot and his clerk inside, pulling forward chairs as if he were the master. After the usual civilities, the Marquis asked the purpose of their visit. Popinot glanced at the old gentleman and the Marquis.

“I believe, Monsieur le Marquis,” he began, “that my functions and the inquiry that has brought me here require us to be alone, although such inquiries are generally public. I am a judge on the Inferior Court of Appeal for the Department of the Seine, tasked with examining you regarding a petition for a Commission in Lunacy filed by the Marquise d’Espard.”

The old man withdrew, and once alone, the clerk seated himself at the office table to take notes. Popinot observed the Marquis, gauging his reaction to this painful statement. The usually pale Marquis turned red with anger, trembling momentarily before regaining his composure.

“How is it, monsieur,” he asked, “that I have had no notice of such a petition?”

“Monsieur le Marquis, those subject to such a commission are not presumed to have the use of their reason, so notice is unnecessary. The Court's duty is to verify the petitioner's allegations.”

“Nothing could be fairer,” replied the Marquis. “Well, then, monsieur, what must I do?”

“Simply answer my questions fully. Speak without fear, regardless of how delicate the reasons may be that led Madame d’Espard to petition. I assure you that lawyers understand their duties and that confidentiality is paramount.”

“Monsieur,” the Marquis said, his face showing sincere pain, “if my explanations lead to blame for Madame d’Espard, what will happen?”

“The Court may add its censure to its reasons for its decision.”

“Is such censure optional? If I were to stipulate that nothing should be said to annoy Madame d’Espard if your report favors me, would the Court consider my request?”

Popinot and the Marquis exchanged sentiments of mutual respect.

“Noel,” Popinot instructed his registrar, “go into the other room. I will call you if needed. If I find that there is a misunderstanding, I can promise you, monsieur, that the Court will act with courtesy upon your application.”

“There is a significant allegation from Madame d’Espard that I must ask you to explain,” Popinot continued after a pause. “It concerns the dissipation of your fortune for the benefit of a certain Madame Jeanrenaud, the widow of a bargemaster, or rather her son, Colonel Jeanrenaud, for whom you are said to have procured an appointment and exhausted your influence with the King.”

The Marquis’ face flushed, and tears welled in his eyes, but he quickly regained his pride.

“To be honest, monsieur,” he said, “you place me in a strange dilemma. The motives of my conduct were to die with me. To reveal them would require disclosing secret wounds and placing my family's honor in your hands, which is delicate.”

“I assure you, it is possible to keep your confidences confidential,” Popinot replied.

“Some time after my marriage,” the Marquis began, “my wife incurred considerable expenses, forcing me to borrow. You know the position of noble families during the Revolution; I could not afford a steward or a man of business. Most of my title-deeds had been brought to Paris by my father, who feared the scrutiny that family documents attracted.”

“Our name is Negrepelisse; d’Espard is a title acquired through a marriage during Henri IV’s reign, which brought us estates and titles on the condition of bearing an escutcheon of pretence. At that time, we lost Negrepelisse, a town famous during the religious struggles, and my ancestor was ruined by the destruction of his property.”

“The Crown was unjust to M. de Negrepelisse; he received no reward for his loyalty. My great-grandfather, like me, was left to head the family early in life, inheriting only the d’Espard estates burdened with a jointure. He was financially strained due to his Court position, but his favor with Louis XIV. brought him fortune. However, a stain of blood and disgrace weighs on my family, which I am determined to erase.”

The Marquis spoke with clarity, free from his usual hesitations, driven by passion.

“The Revocation of the Edict of Nantes allowed many favorites to enrich themselves by seizing the lands of Protestant families. I discovered that my grandfather acquired the lands of Negrepelisse through a gift from Louis XIV., obtained through iniquitous means. The rightful owner, a Protestant merchant, was arrested and hanged, and my ancestor profited from this tragedy.”

“This unfortunate family was named Jeanrenaud,” he continued. “I could not bear the disgrace that weighed on my family. I sought to find the heirs of the victim. Eventually, I found M. Jeanrenaud, the sole heir, who was reduced to poverty. I felt their rights were clear, as they had no means to appeal against the injustices they suffered.”

“I did not want my children to think of me as I think of my ancestors. I aimed to leave them an unblemished inheritance. I discovered that the Jeanrenauds had returned to France, and I found M. Jeanrenaud, a lieutenant under Napoleon. I offered to pay them the value of the lands at the time they were taken, which we settled at eleven hundred thousand francs, to be paid at my convenience without interest.”

“I had to forego my income for a long time to make this restitution. When I proposed to Madame d’Espard that we move to the country to live on half her income, she treated me as a madman. I realized her true character; she would have condoned my grandfather’s actions. Terrified by her coldness, I decided to leave her in control of her fortune after settling our debts. I took on the education of our children myself, determined to raise them as gentlemen.”

“I invested my money wisely and repaid the debt sooner than expected. I have recently completed the payment of the eleven hundred thousand francs, achieving restitution without harming my children.”

“These are the reasons for the payments made to Madame Jeanrenaud and her son.”

“Did Madame d’Espard know your motives for retiring?” Popinot asked, controlling his emotion.

“Yes, monsieur.”

Popinot shrugged expressively and opened the door to the next room.

“Noel, you can go,” he instructed his clerk.

“Though what you have told me is enlightening, I would like to hear your explanation of other allegations in the petition. For instance, you are conducting a business not typical for a man of your rank.”

“We cannot discuss that here,” the Marquis replied, signaling for the judge to leave the room. “Nouvion,” he called to the old man, “I am going down to my rooms; the children will soon be in; dine with us.”

“Then, Monsieur le Marquis,” Popinot said on the stairs, “that is not your apartment?”

“No, monsieur; I took those rooms for the office of this undertaking. The History is being published by a reputable firm, not by me.”

The Marquis showed Popinot to his ground-floor apartment, which was filled with a tender harmony that touched the judge. The lovely weather and fresh air from the garden created a pleasant atmosphere, leading Popinot to think that a madman would hardly be capable of such beauty.

“I would like an apartment like this,” he thought. “Are you planning to leave this part of town?” he asked.

“I hope to,” the Marquis replied. “But I will stay until my younger son finishes his studies and the children’s characters are fully formed. I intend to travel with them to European capitals to broaden their horizons and ensure they become accustomed to the languages they have learned. I could not discuss the book on China with you in front of an old family friend, the Comte de Nouvion, who has returned to France without fortune and is my partner in this venture.”

“My tutor was the Abbe Grozier, appointed Keeper of the Books at the Arsenal by Charles X. He imparted his extensive knowledge of China to me, and I have always admired that nation for its rich history and achievements. However, I am a French gentleman, not a Chinaman. If you have doubts about the financial side of this undertaking, I can assure you we have two thousand five hundred subscribers, with twelve hundred in France. The book will cost about three hundred francs, and the Comte de Nouvion will earn six to seven thousand francs a year from it, which was my real motive for the undertaking. I aimed to provide my children with pleasures, and the hundred thousand francs I made will cover their education and other expenses.”

“My twelve years of retirement to educate my children have led to my being forgotten at Court. I have sacrificed my political career and lost the distinctions I could have passed on to my children, but they will achieve greatness through their own merits. While I have purified my family’s past, I have secured a glorious future for them. Is that not a noble task, even if done in secret?”

At that moment, the sound of horses echoed in the courtyard.

“Here they are!” the Marquis exclaimed. The two boys entered, dressed for riding, their faces bright with health. They greeted their father warmly before acknowledging the lawyer. Popinot felt no need to question the Marquis about his relationship with his sons.

“Did you enjoy yourselves?” the Marquis asked.

“Yes, father; I knocked down six dolls in twelve shots!” Camille replied.

“And where did you ride?” the Marquis inquired.

“In the Bois; we saw my mother,” the young Count answered.

“Did she stop?” the Marquis asked.

“We were riding so fast that she probably didn’t see us,” replied Camille.

“Why didn’t you speak to her?” the Marquis pressed.

“I think I noticed, father, that she doesn’t want us to speak to her in public,” Clement whispered.

The judge overheard this and noted the cloud that passed over the Marquis’ brow. Popinot admired the scene of the father and his sons, feeling a sense of pathos for M. d’Espard, whose expression conveyed noble honesty.

“You see, monsieur,” the Marquis said, hesitating, “Justice may look in here at any time. If anyone is mad, it can only be the children, who are a little crazy about their father, and the father who is very crazy about his children—but that kind of madness rings true.”

At that moment, Madame Jeanrenaud’s voice was heard in the ante-room as she entered, disregarding the man-servant’s protests.

“I take no roundabout ways!” she exclaimed. “Monsieur le Marquis, I want to speak to you right now,” she continued, bowing to the company. “I’m too late as it is, since Monsieur the criminal Judge is here.”

“Criminal?” the boys exclaimed.

“Good reason I didn’t find you at home, since you’re here. Well, well! The Law is always present when mischief brews. I came to tell you that my son and I are of one mind to return everything, as our honor is threatened. We’d rather give you back everything than cause you any trouble. They must be as stupid as pans without handles to call you a lunatic.”

“A lunatic! My father?” the boys cried, clinging to the Marquis. “What is this?”

“Silence, madame,” Popinot interjected.

“Children, leave us,” the Marquis ordered.

The boys exited to the garden, alarmed.

“Madame,” the judge said, “the payments made to you by Monsieur le Marquis were legally due, though given under a far-reaching theory of honesty. If all who possess confiscated goods were compelled to make restitution every hundred and fifty years, few legitimate owners would remain in France. The law allows M. d’Espard to dispose of his income without accounting for it. A Commission in Lunacy can only be granted when a man’s actions lack reason, but in this case, the remittances to you are based on the most honorable motives. You may keep it all without remorse and let the world misinterpret a noble action. In Paris, the highest virtue is often the target of foul calumny. It is unfortunate that the Marquis’ actions are seen as sublime only by comparison. I would rather see him awarded a crown than threatened with a Commission in Lunacy.”

“Now that’s well said,” Madame Jeanrenaud exclaimed. “That’s a judge! I would hug you if I weren’t so ugly; you speak like a book.”

The Marquis extended his hand to Popinot, who gently pressed it, sharing a moment of mutual understanding. The two men, one commonplace yet kind, the other noble and sublime, found harmony in their connection.

“Monsieur le Marquis,” Popinot added, “I am pleased to assure you that from the first words of this inquiry, I regarded my clerk as unnecessary.”

He led M. d’Espard to the window and said, “It’s time for you to return home, monsieur. I believe Madame la Marquise has acted under an influence you should counteract.”

Popinot departed, reflecting on the scene as one that would linger in his memory, providing solace in difficult times.

“Those rooms would suit me perfectly,” he mused as he returned home. “If M. d’Espard leaves them, I will take up his lease.”

The next day, around ten in the morning, Popinot, having written his report the previous evening, headed to the Palais de Justice to ensure prompt justice. As he prepared to don his gown, the usher informed him that the President of his Court wished to see him in his private room. Popinot complied.

“Good morning, my dear Popinot,” the President greeted him. “I’ve been waiting for you.”

“Why, Monsieur le President, is something wrong?” Popinot asked.

“A mere silly trifle,” the President replied. “The Keeper of the Seals, with whom I dined yesterday, suggested it would be best if you did not sit on this case.”

“But, Monsieur le President, I can prove I left Madame d’Espard’s house before tea was served. My conscience—”

“Yes, yes; the whole Bench knows you. I need not repeat what I said about you to his Eminence, but you know, ‘Caesar’s wife must not be suspected.’ We won’t make this a matter of discipline, just propriety. It’s not for your sake, but for the Bench’s.”

“But, monsieur, if you only knew the kind of woman—” Popinot began, trying to retrieve his report.

“I’m sure you’ve acted with the utmost independence. I’ve often shared tea with people I had to try, but the Keeper of the Seals’ mention of it means we should avoid any discussion of the matter. Any conflict with public opinion can be dangerous for a constitutional body, even when right is on its side. Journalism can say anything, and our dignity forbids us to respond. I’ve spoken to your President, and M. Camusot has been appointed in your place. It’s a family matter, so to speak. I ask you to signify your retirement from the case as a personal favor. To make amends, you will receive the Cross of the Legion of Honor, which has long been due to you. I’ll take care of that.”

Seeing M. Camusot, a recently appointed judge from a provincial Court, Popinot couldn’t suppress an ironic smile. This pale, ambitious young man seemed ready to serve any king, innocent or guilty, rather than follow the example of a noble judge.

Popinot withdrew with a bow, choosing not to deny the false accusation against him.
