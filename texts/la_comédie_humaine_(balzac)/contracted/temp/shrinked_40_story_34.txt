Many stories carry their own setting and can be told straightforwardly; but some human incidents need a delicate, almost anatomical touch of feeling to live. Certain portraits demand the infusion of a soul; some things cannot be rendered without the secret harmonies of a particular hour or inward mood. Such mysterious revelations are needed for this simple history, intended for the grave and reflective who find sustenance in tender emotions. If the writer feels a kind of reverence for his subject, should not the reader share that inexplicable feeling? If he can be in tune with the vague, nervous sadness that casts gray tints about us—a semi-illness whose gentle sufferings are often pleasing—then let him read on. If he has never buried a good old relative, never known the luxury of tears or the silent pangs of passing memory, he will not understand these pages. To some, they will smell of musk; to others, be colorless and virtuous. The writer promises no poetic lies: this is a true history.

The French language today contains as many idioms as there are varieties of Parisians. It is amusing to hear how different “species” of the Parisian genus interpret the same question—“Do you know Madame Firmiani?”—so I present their brief inventories.

The Practical: rotund, fat, usually dressed in black. He answers with figures: “Fine house in the rue du Bac, salons handsomely furnished, good pictures, one hundred thousand francs a year, husband formerly receiver-general of the department of Montenotte.” For the Practical, solidity is everything; he reduces people to incomes and acres.

The Lounger: spare, agreeable, with acquired cleverness. “Madame Firmiani?” he murmurs: “I go to her parties; receives Wednesdays; highly creditable house.” To him, Madame Firmiani is a kind of inn—one where admission is difficult, society excellent, and the refreshments superb. He acquits himself of introductions with a pinch of snuff.

The Egotist: impatient with others’ happiness, contemptuous of salons he deems dull. “Why go? What is the good of possessing a mind unless to avoid such places?” He would keep the universe under lock and key and grant permission to few.

The Student: bold in public, timid in the boudoir. “Oh, Madame Firmiani—one of those adorable women Nature uses as excuse for the ugly ones she creates. I wish I had millions so I might—” (whisper). He offers to present you.

The Fop: superficial and cruel in a flash. “Between thirty and thirty-five; faded complexion, handsome eyes, flat figure, contralto voice worn out, much dressed, rather rouged—remains of a pretty woman still worth the trouble of a passion.” He speaks with the brutality of one who has just breakfasted.

The Connoisseur: brisk, practical about art. “Magnificent collection of pictures; go and see them.” He promptly leaves for Perignon’s or Tripet’s.

A Woman of Spite: “I don’t wish you to visit her.” To her, Madame Firmiani is dangerous—a siren who gives other women sleepless nights.

The Attache: taciturn, fond of naming countries. “Isn’t she from Antwerp? I saw her ten years ago in Rome; she was very handsome then.” Press his foreign springs and his changes ring out.

The Distinguished: ever eager to place a “de.” “Madame Firmiani sees a great deal of the faubourg Saint-Germain.” She strives to know and do “the right thing,” though she lives in the Marais and her husband is a lawyer—albeit a lawyer before the Royal courts.

The Duke: aloof, recognizes only those presented at court. “I do not know her,” he says with excused hauteur.

The Ninny: quick to answer and careless of truth. “Surely she used to sing at the Opera-house.” Ninnies prefer something to silence, even if false.

Two old ladies, daughters of former magistrates: one asks her maiden name; the other, gentle and precise, replies, “She was a Cadignan, niece of the old Prince de Cadignan, consequently cousin to the present Duc de Maufrigneuse.” A Cadignan by birth remains a Cadignan regardless of virtue, wealth, or youth—an enduring prejudice.

The Original: quick with practicalities. “No galoshes in her antechamber; you can visit her without compromising yourself, and play cards there without fear; scoundrels in her salons are people of quality who come in carriages and never quarrel.”

The Observer: forty, maroon coat, oracle-like, never dines at home, claims to be no longer dangerous to women, reserved a seat at the Bouffons, and owns an unnamed small estate. He speaks as if his words must be accepted. “Call on Madame Firmiani and you will find a beautiful woman at ease by her fireplace, scarcely rising to receive you—only for women, ambassadors, dukes, and persons of distinction. She is gracious, converses well, likes many topics. There are signs of a passionate nature, yet she has so many adorers she cannot have a favorite. Her husband is mythical—Monsieur Firmiani is the third post-horse we pay for though never seen. She has the finest contralto voice in Europe, they say, but has sung scarcely two or three times since she came to Paris. She receives much company but goes nowhere.” The Observer’s authority must be accepted; otherwise you risk his social displeasure.

The Contradictor: pedantic, fond of correcting memoirs and dates. “Why, my dear fellow, she was Murat’s former mistress.” Contradictors declare certainty on everything, often are “chevaliers” of the Legion of Honor, talk loudly, and bet confidently.

The Envious: the species everyone recognizes as well as the domestic cat. “A hundred thousand francs a year? Nonsense—some people lavish millions like novelists. Madame Firmiani is a mere coquette who ruined a young man and prevents his marriage. If she were not so handsome she wouldn’t have a penny.” Envy persists with perennial vigor despite bringing nothing.

Between these character sketches arise key facts: Madame Firmiani is a Cadignan with a fine house in the rue du Bac, famous salons, a remarkable art collection, and a contralto voice seldom heard in public. She receives but rarely appears elsewhere; her husband is seldom seen, practically mythical. Opinions about her circulate through Parisian society like a prism, every class refracting her into its own idiom—wealth, entertainment, danger, art, pedigree, scandal—each speaker revealing as much about himself as about the woman discussed.

Thus, the portrait of Madame Firmiani is not a single likeness but a chorus of Parisian types, each supplying a fragment: the solid arithmetic of the Practical; the inn-like hospitality of the Lounger; the jealousy of the Envious; the snobbery of the Distinguished and the Duke; the malicious precision of the Spiteful woman; the worldly connoisseur’s focus on pictures; the Observer’s learned synthesis; the Contradictor’s historical fudge; the Student’s coquettish devotion; and the Fop’s brutal, fashionable glance. In their voices the city names her according to the concerns, prejudices, and vanities of those who speak—so that she becomes at once house, singer, coquette, noble name, danger, and enigma.

In January 1824 the town was alive with so many conflicting opinions about Madame Firmiani that it would be tedious to enumerate them. Those who did not visit her home could pick from hearsay that she was widow or wife, silly or wise, virtuous or the reverse, rich or poor, soulless or full of feeling—so many Madame Firmianis as there are social kinds. The frightful reflection was this: we are like lithographic blocks from which many prints may be taken; our reputation depends, apart from friends’ calumnies and newspaper wit, on the delicate shading chosen by our critics between a limping Truth and Falsehood given wings by Parisian jest.

Madame Firmiani, a noble woman who kept her heart a sanctuary and disdained the world, was likely to be misjudged by Monsieur de Bourbonne, an old provincial magnate whose curiosity about her that winter had real cause. He belonged to the class of provincial planters—men who live on estates, keep exact accounts, and bargain with peasants. Such occupations make a man prudently sagacious. He had come from Touraine to Paris to satisfy his curiosity because his sole heir, a nephew he cherished, Octave de Camps, had lately sold an estate to the Bande Noire, a secret association of speculators that bought and parceled up landed estates. The château de Villaines would have been wrecked had not the old man protested to the “Pickaxe company.”

To inflame him further, a distant relative—one of those canny cousins of small means—had dropped in on Monsieur de Bourbonne and casually informed him that Octave had wasted his means on a certain Madame Firmiani and was reduced to teaching mathematics, living in hope of inheriting. The report, offered as fact while the old man sat digesting a profuse provincial dinner, produced little effect at first; some shocks affect the head, others the stomach. But true to his cautious nature, the uncle—like a disciple of Saint Thomas—came to Paris incognito, resolved to make full inquiries about his nephew’s alleged insolvency.

Using the name de Rouxellay, his estate in Touraine, the astute gentleman arranged to be presented to Madame Firmiani on an evening when he knew Octave would be otherwise engaged finishing a paid piece of work. He immediately discovered that the nephew’s ruin was no fable. Monsieur de Rouxellay himself was not the provincial at the Gymnase: formerly of the King’s guard, a man of the world and a favorite in society, he had courteous breeding and could hold his own amid the court circle—provided nobody spoke to him of modern romanticism, railways, or theatrical innovations. He loved the Bourbons, believed in God as a gentleman should, and read little besides the Quotidienne, Voltaire, Buffon, Payronnet, and the Queen’s favorite musician.

Entering Madame Firmiani’s salon on the arm of the Marquise de Listomère, he murmured that if she were his nephew’s mistress he should pity Octave: how could a woman living amid luxury know that a young man loved her from a garret? How could she be so insensible? Yet the old man’s injured dignity dissolved the moment he saw her. By one of those transient salon illuminations—wax-lights, a simple dress, the full consciousness of being admired—her beauty shone with exceptional lustre. Parisian evenings have subtle transitions; there is a moment when a woman, content with her toilet and wit, delighted by a roomful of admiring tongues, becomes aware of her own charm; smiles and glances gather and rebound, lending her an unconsciously magical power. If this state, springing from the soul, can give attraction even to a plain woman, with what radiance must it invest one naturally elegant, fair, fresh, and tastefully dressed?

Madame Firmiani had all those gifts. She possessed a harmonious voice and the tact to choose words and silences with equal charm; she spoke without affectation and knew how to lead a conversation and close it at the right instant. Her raillery caressed, her criticism did not wound; she neither lectured nor argued but delighted to guide discourse. Her manner was affable, never forced; her readiness to serve was graceful, not servile; she made respect a soft shadow, never wearying those about her. Naturalness defined her: no effort for effect, feelings shown simply because they were true. Frank herself, she spared others’ vanities; she accepted men as God made them, pitying their vices, forgiving defects, and comprehending all ages. Tender and gay, she gratified before consoling. A man who had met such a woman would be ready to excuse her any fault—this was Madame Firmiani.

After ten minutes’ talk, Monsieur de Bourbonne sat beside her and found his nephew forgiven. He perceived that whatever the facts might be, the relation between Octave and Madame Firmiani involved some mystery; judging her by the dignity and peace his eye discovered, he concluded a woman so innately conscious of her dignity could not be vicious. Her dark eyes told of inward peace, the profile was noble; the supposed passion did not oppress her heart. He told himself, as he noted every promise of love and virtue given by that adorable countenance, “My nephew is committing some folly.”

Madame Firmiani had admitted to twenty-five, but practical observers proved that, having married the invisible Firmiani in 1813 at sixteen, she must be at least twenty-eight in 1825. Yet even those who counted years agreed she had hardly ever been lovelier or more completely a woman. At an age when women are most prone to passion, she seemed to possess everything earth offers: knowledge, art, refinement. The Attachés declared there was nothing she did not know; the Contradictors said there was much she ought to learn; the Observers noted her white hands, small feet, and slightly undulating movements. Still, men envied or disputed Octave’s happiness and, for once, concurred that she was the most aristocratically beautiful woman in Paris.

Young, rich, an accomplished musician, intelligent, witty, refined, and received by the Princesse de Blamont-Chauvry—the oracle of the noble faubourg—Madame Firmiani gratified the vanities that feed and excite love. Sought by many men and beloved even by rivals—the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, her cousin the Marquise d’Espard, and Madame de Macumer—she inevitably became prey to Parisian malice: whispers behind fans, safe asides, insinuations. Hence the multiplicity of Madame Firmianis described at the beginning: the public Firmiani versus the true one. Some women forgave her happiness; others could not forgive her propriety. In Paris nothing is more dangerous than unfounded suspicion, precisely because it is impossible to destroy.

This sketch, admirable in its naturalness, gives only a faint idea of her. It would need the pencil of an Ingres to render the pride of that brow, the wealth of her hair, the dignity of her glance, and the changing colors of her cheeks. In her were all things: poets might have found in her an Agnes Sorel and a Joan of Arc, the woman unknown, the soul within the form; the Eve who knows both the treasures of good and the riches of evil—error and resignation, crime and devotion, the Donna Julia and Byron’s Haïdee.

The former guardsman lingered impertinently after the other guests had left; Madame Firmiani found him still in an armchair at two in the morning, determined to remain like a fly. As she rose, the old gentleman said, “Madame, I am the uncle of Monsieur Octave de Camps.”

Madame Firmiani sat again, visibly moved. Though experienced, he could not tell whether her pallor came from shame or pleasure; delicate women often betray delicious emotions by modesty. Monsieur de Bourbonne, less generous, distrusted her.

“Well, monsieur?” she asked, a glance questioning more than it revealed.

“In my province they say my nephew has ruined himself for you and lives in a garret while you are in silk and gold,” he said bluntly. “Forgive my rustic sincerity; it may be useful for you to know of these calumnies.”

“Stop, monsieur,” she interrupted imperatively. “I know all that. You are too polite to continue and too gallant—to question me would be ridiculous. I hope you will think well of my character and believe the profound contempt I feel for money. I married, without fortune, a man of immense wealth. It is nothing to me whether your nephew is rich or poor; I receive him because I esteem him. All my friends respect each other; I have not the philosophy to admit those I do not esteem. My guardian angel preserves in me a profound aversion to tattle and dishonesty.”

Her voice rose and then settled, finishing with calm self-possession.

“I am almost Octave’s father,” the old man said humbly, “and I ask your pardon for the question I now venture to put, giving you my word of honor that your answer shall die here”—laying his hand on his heart—“Are these rumors true; do you love Octave?”

“To any other man I would answer by a look,” she replied. “But to you—since you are almost his father—I ask what you would think of a woman who answered? To avow our love for one we love—when he loves us—ah! that may be; but even when sure of being loved forever, believe me, it is an effort for us and a reward to him.” She rose, bowed, and withdrew into her private rooms, the doors closing like speech.

“Ah! the mischief!” he thought; “what a woman! she is either sly or an angel.” He left in his hired coach, the horses stamping while the coachman slept on his box, cursing his tardy passenger.

Next morning the old gentleman mounted the stairs to Octave’s garret in the rue de l’Observance. The young professor, who had been up all night, was astonished to see his uncle. Monsieur de Bourbonne reproached him in a comic, affectionate torrent: since when was it fashionable to laugh at uncles who have twenty-six thousand francs a year? Had he not been indulgent? Had he ever refused money? He warned Octave he had been the most accommodating uncle in France and at least intended to make him the prettiest estate in Touraine one day. Yet the nephew was living like a lackey, perhaps ruined by gambling or the Bourse.

“Have you been gambling? lost money?” the uncle demanded. “Say, ‘Uncle, I’m a wretch,’ and I’ll hug you. But if you lie to me I’ll sell my property, buy an annuity, and go back to the evils of my youth—if I can.”

“I saw Madame Firmiani yesterday,” he added, kissing his fingers. “She is charming. You have my consent and approbation, if that will do you any good. Have you ruined yourself for her?”

“Yes, uncle.”

“Aha! the jade! In my time women of the court were cleverer at ruining a man than the courtesans, but she—she is of the last century!”

“You are mistaken,” Octave said gravely. “Madame Firmiani deserves esteem and all adoration.”

“Youth! always the same,” the uncle cried. But when Octave produced a letter that would explain nearly all, Monsieur de Bourbonne, without spectacles, told him to read it aloud.

Octave began reading Madame Firmiani’s letter:

She addressed him tenderly, then confessed a struggle between love and conscience since learning of his father’s injustice—how he had deprived others of their property. She could not respect a man who knowingly held property obtained by deceit. A wife’s love needs reverence and esteem in the man she loves; she could not sacrifice her conscience even for love. She urged him to consult his conscience and, if the accusation were true, to make restitution. She insisted this act should spring from his own moral conviction, not from her urging; a man who truly loves a woman respects the sanctity of her trust.

Octave’s eyes filled with tears. The uncle urged him to continue; Octave said the rest was for lovers.

Monsieur de Bourbonne softened: “I too have loved: ‘et ego in Arcadia.’ But I don’t understand why you give lessons in mathematics.”

Octave explained: as his father’s heir he had been tainted by that capital. After reading the letter, a revolution took place within him. He felt shame when driving or eating, a sense that the possessions were not honestly his. He rushed to Madame Firmiani; together they calculated what was due to the Bourgneufs. Against her advice, he condemned himself to pay three percent interest, but his funds were insufficient. She offered, and he accepted, her savings.

“You’re saving money too?” the uncle said, surprised.

“Don’t laugh,” Octave answered. Her position had obliged prudence. Her husband had gone to Greece in 1820 and died three years later; legal proof of his death and the will leaving property to her had been missing—lost in the troubles of Greece, where consulates and registers were unreliable. Unsure whether she might be forced to give up her fortune, she lived austerely. Octave wanted to acquire property of his own to provide for his wife if she lost hers.

“But why didn’t you tell me all this?” the uncle asked. “I love you enough to pay your debts. I’ll be the traditional uncle and avenge myself!”

“Let me get rich by my own industry,” Octave pleaded. “If you truly want to help, make me an allowance of two or three thousand francs a year until I can undertake the enterprise I have in mind. I give lessons so I may not live at the cost of anyone. If you knew how happy I was making restitution! I found the Bourgneufs living miserably. The old father was a lottery agent; the daughters worked, the mother was ill. I entered as the accomplice in a crime and left as a man who had purged his father’s memory. The visit was like a drama. To bring sudden opulence to a family sitting by the light of a single miserable lamp—no words can describe it. My extreme justice seemed to them almost unjust. If there is a Paradise, my father is happy there now. As for me, I am loved as none was ever loved. Madame Firmiani gives me more than happiness; she has inspired in me a delicacy of feeling I lacked. I call her my dear conscience. I find honesty profitable; I hope to get rich by an industrial scheme.”

“You have your mother’s soul,” the uncle said, his eyes filling for his sister.

Just then a carriage arrived despite the narrow street. “There she is!” Octave cried. Madame Firmiani entered, mildly annoyed to see Monsieur de Bourbonne, then smiling explained she had come to entreat her husband to accept her fortune. The Austrian Embassy had just sent a document proving Monsieur Firmiani’s death and the will, which his valet had kept to give her. “Octave, you can accept it all; you are richer than I, for you have treasures here”—she laid her hand on his heart—“to which none but God can add.” Overcome with happiness, she laid her head on his breast.

“My dear niece,” said the old man, “in my day we made love; in yours, you love. You women are all that is best in humanity; you are not even guilty of your faults, for they come through us.”
