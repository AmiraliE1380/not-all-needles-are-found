The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou who had fought for the Bourbons in La Vendée, was ruined when his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet—noble but penniless—and moved to Paris with his large family seeking relief. After an initially discouraging period he received promotion and royal decorations that revived his hopes, though the later rise of new aristocrats left him feeling his sacrifices were unacknowledged. On the eve of returning to his estate he reentered service during the 1815 upheavals and became a trusted adviser to Louis XVIII, prized for his wit and political sense. The family prospered: his sons obtained important posts and his daughters made advantageous, if generally lesser, matches.

Emilie, the youngest, became the spoiled centre of Parisian society. Beautiful, exacting and capricious, she drew many suitors but remained indifferent, snubbing those she deemed inferior and rejecting matches for trivial faults. As the King’s health declined and political fortunes shifted, the Comte pressed harder to find Emilie a husband to secure the family’s standing, but her disdain through the winter left their future uncertain.

Nature had given Emilie every quality to play Célimène. Tall and slender, she could shift from dignity to coquettish affectation; her long neck and arched brows suggested scorn and impertinence, her fine black hair and melodious voice could charm or silence. Her alabaster complexion stayed composed except for fleeting betrayals of feeling. Many young men she spurned accused her of acting; she replied that she merely inspired desire only to subject it to her whims.

Monsieur de Fontaine realized too late that home adoration had spoiled his daughter. Constant admiration inflated her pride and selfishness, hidden by youthful charm but visible to a father who tried in vain to teach her wisdom. His gentle counsels slid off her like water from marble; her affection often took a condescending, childlike tone—demanding attention then returning to play. Though she sometimes showed fondness for her parents, she often withdrew, jealous when their love was shared with others, including her siblings. She blamed fate for her solitude, failing to see that happiness is inward. Determined to resist her sisters’ marriages, she nevertheless felt pangs of envy at their contentment; her unpredictability led her mother to suspect she might be a little mad.

This pride was common among beautiful daughters of affluent families: feeling misunderstood by mothers, suspecting maternal envy, shedding secret tears, and rebelling against perceived tyranny. They built elaborate ideals of the perfect partner, only to learn by experience that reality rarely matches fantasy.

Emilie’s standards were precise and unyielding. Her husband must be a peer of France and a soldier wearing an Order so they could be respected; he must be slim, for she despised corpulence and believed only the slender could be romantic. Her disdain for failing suitors was plain, and Monsieur de Fontaine feared her eccentricities would invite ridicule and that delay in the marriage market might bring practical consequences.

As winter under Charles X progressed, the Comte redoubled efforts, hosting gatherings to attract suitors despite his own reputation for compromising legislative integrity. After a season of introductions he hoped Emilie would choose, but her whims remained unchanged. One morning he summoned her to discuss her future, arranging his study to look dignified; she greeted him with a light, chilly kiss. He urged the importance of a wise choice for happiness. Emilie interrupted, mocked his suggestions, and reminded him of their prior agreement about suitors. Exasperated, he declared he had fulfilled his duty and that the choice now rested with her, warning that marital happiness depends on mutual respect rather than mere wealth or rank. Touched, Emilie feigned affection, yet her pride stayed intact.

That summer at the country house she asked to attend the Bal de Sceaux, drawn by the crowd’s anonymity. There she noticed a striking young man apart from the throng whose elegance and dignity sparked immediate attraction. Aloof, he watched another dancer, a pale girl named Clara; curious and jealous, Emilie followed them. Days later, learning he was not the noble she’d hoped, she pressed her uncle for an introduction. The promise intensified her interest; her infatuation grew, fed by imagination and youthful ideals, still unaware of the complexities love and marriage would bring.

The next morning, before Emilie woke, her uncle rode to Chevreuse. Recognizing the young man he’d insulted, the Count dropped his hauteur and adopted the civility of an old courtier. “I’m a vice-admiral,” he said, “and I think as little of dueling as of smoking a cigar. I had too much grog and ran you down. Let’s shake hands; I’d rather face a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than give his family cause for regret.” The young man accepted, and they rode into the woods; the Count, playful, fired a pistol into a birch and urged him to shoot near the mark, which won his approval.

During the ride the Count probed him about gentlemanly codes. “Do you have debts?” he asked. “No.” “What, you pay for everything?” “Punctually; otherwise, we’d lose credit and respect.” The Count teased about mistresses and extravagance, confessed his own recklessness, and promised an introduction at the Pavillon Planat, urging a dinner to meet his nephew, the Comte de Fontaine. The young man—Longueville—found the admiral odd but affable and accepted.

When the house party gathered the next afternoon and a servant announced “Monsieur de Longueville,” everyone rushed to appraise him. Simply and elegantly dressed, Longueville seemed at ease in the Receiver-General’s luxury. His manner and polished education suggested a man of the world; when the admiral mentioned naval architecture he answered aptly, prompting a lady to suspect he had attended the École Polytechnique. He accepted flattery but courteously declined dinner, saying he was “Hippocrates” to his delicate young sister—an excuse that provoked ironic speculation about his profession.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine, who had dressed to attract him, felt slighted by his lack of attention. Emilie, usually charming with newcomers, became unusually simple and natural, prompting family comment and varied interpretations—some thinking she was withholding to dazzle later. Longueville’s visit pleased everyone and intensified curiosity. The Count called him “a cunning rascal.” A sarcastic dinner-table remark and Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s warning—that universal perfection must hide a defect, “the worst fault is to have none”—kept speculation alive.

Despite efforts at indifference, Emilie’s feelings soon betrayed her. Longueville returned, and by his third visit everyone saw she was affected. Accustomed to being the center of attention, she was unsettled by someone who attracted her in turn; she tried to resist but could not dismiss him from her heart. Anxiety grew as his modesty and discretion clashed with her curiosity: he never spoke of himself or his family and deftly evaded hints. If she discussed painting, he answered like a connoisseur; if she played music, he proved a good pianist. Once they sang a duet, a moment of intimate harmony, yet he preserved the mystery of his social status.

Her uncle and the family schemed to learn more, keen to meet Longueville’s sister, Clara. They proposed an informal dance to entertain her. Emilie felt joy at the thought of another person to care for and grew gentler and more indulgent; the change pleased her family, who read it as love transforming her selfishness. She believed Longueville’s visits were chiefly for her, and though they never spoke openly of passion, she sensed his affection. She drew from him small personal details and realized he, too, was studying her and encouraging her improvement.

On the day of the dance Clara and her brother were principal guests. For the first time Emilie took pleasure in another girl’s triumph and lavished Clara with sincere attention. Clara proved deft in conversation, steering away from personal topics while charming Emilie—who felt both envious and reassured. “Mademoiselle,” Clara said, “I’ve heard so much about you from Maximilien that I wanted to know you out of affection for him.” Dismissing worries about birth and rank, Clara put Emilie at ease, and they danced warmly.

Believing him well born and seeing his grace surpass her ideal, Emilie felt satisfaction. As autumn deepened, the couple surrendered to the sweet tide of feeling. Small shared incidents—aimless conversations that masked deeper affections, admiring sunsets, gathering daisies, singing duets—knitted them closer. Each casual talk, musical exchange, and stroll deepened their intimacy and confirmed the mutual affection they had carefully concealed.
