The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou, had served the Bourbon cause in the La Vendée. Despite noble lineage and sacrifices, he was financially ruined when his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet, a noblewoman without fortune, and after the second revolution moved to Paris with his large family in search of prospects. Initially disillusioned, he received unexpected honors—a military promotion and royal decorations—that rekindled hopes for favor. He soon discovered, however, that his sacrifices were largely unrecognized and he felt marginalized as new aristocrats rose.

Though he contemplated returning to his estate, the turmoil of 1815 drew him back into service; he became a trusted adviser to Louis XVIII, valued for his wit and political acumen. The Fontaines then began to prosper: his sons obtained important posts and his daughters made advantageous marriages, if primarily into lesser nobility.

Emilie, the youngest, was spoiled and the center of Parisian attention. Her beauty and charm made her much sought-after, yet she remained indifferent, asserting superiority over suitors of lower birth. Despite the Count’s efforts to arrange matches, Emilie’s high standards and caprice provoked family conflict. She dismissed candidates for trivial flaws, repeatedly frustrating his plans.

As the political landscape shifted and the King’s health declined, the Comte intensified efforts to secure a suitable husband for Emilie to cement the family’s status. Her unreasonable expectations produced a string of rejections that left him increasingly desperate and strained relations among siblings and in-laws who were themselves adjusting to new social standing. Through the turmoil he clung to the hope that royal favor and a proper match would validate his sacrifices and restore the family’s honor, yet Emilie’s continued disdain left the Fontaine future uncertain.

Nature had given Emilie de Fontaine the exact features for playing Célimène. Tall and slender, she could adopt dignity or coquettishness at will; a long neck hinted at scorn, fine black hair and arched brows suggested pride. She had perfected feminine gestures that could signify contempt or charm, and a melodious voice that could woo or sharply silence indiscretion. Her alabaster complexion rarely betrayed emotion. Many of the young men she scorned accused her of merely performing; she answered that she inspired desire only to subject it to her caprice.

Monsieur de Fontaine recognized, too late, that adoration had spoiled his daughter. Constant praise had inflated her pride and self-confidence, nurturing the selfishness of a pampered child. Her youthful charms concealed these defects, but her father perceived them and tried to instill practical wisdom. His counsel met caprice and ironic dismissal; his gentle advice slid off her heart like water from marble. He came to think her affection often condescending, like a child demanding attention before returning to play.

Emilie sometimes showed affection for her parents but frequently withdrew, jealous of any love they shared with others, including her siblings. She blamed solitude on fate rather than seeing that happiness comes from within. Though determined to resist her sisters’ marriages, she felt pangs of envy at their contentment. Her unpredictability led her mother to suspect slight madness.

Such pride is common among beautiful young women of wealth, who imagine mothers cannot understand youthful dreams and suspect maternal envy. This breeds secret tears and revolts against perceived tyranny. In such struggles they devise elaborate life plans, picturing a perfect partner to meet exacting criteria; with experience many later find happiness unexpectedly and learn ideals rarely match reality.

Emilie’s conditions for a suitor were stern: he must be a peer of France, a soldier, decorated with an Order so they could be treated with respect. He must also be slender, for she deemed corpulence unromantic. Her disdain for those who failed these standards was plain, and Monsieur de Fontaine feared her eccentricities would invite ridicule and leave her indecisive.

During the winter of Charles X’s reign he redoubled efforts to find matches, hosting gatherings to attract suitors. His reputation as a corrupter of legislative integrity grew, yet he remained earnest. After a season of matchmaking he felt hopeful, only to meet Emilie’s unyielding whims.

One morning he summoned her to discuss her future, arranging his study to present a dignified front. Emilie greeted him with a light kiss lacking warmth. He raised the subject of marriage and urged a wise choice for her happiness. She reminded him of their prior agreement about suitors, then mocked settling for anything less than a peer and found fault with every candidate he suggested.

Exasperated, Monsieur de Fontaine declared he had fulfilled his duty and that the choice now rested with her. He warned that marital happiness depended on mutual respect rather than mere wealth or rank. Emilie, touched by his sincerity, feigned warmth to soothe him, yet her pride endured. At a family gathering she reiterated her refusal to marry unless the man were a peer; her uncle joked that she was waiting for the Duc de Bordeaux to grow up, but she forbade further talk of marriage.

As summer approached the family retired to the country house. Emilie wished to attend the Bal de Sceaux, drawn by the crowd and the anonymity it offered. The ball, lively and mixed in social class, revealed to her that aristocrats mingled with the rest. While watching the festivities she noticed a striking young man who stood apart, radiating elegance and dignity. His presence stirred her imagination and she felt immediate attraction.

She approached him, but he remained aloof. Her exaggerated gestures and laughter failed to win his attention. Frustrated, she followed his gaze and saw him watching another dancer, a pale girl named Clara. Intrigued and piqued, Emilie trailed the pair as they left the ball; though attentive to Clara, the young man twice caught Emilie’s eye, arousing a mix of jealousy and curiosity.

Days later, while riding with her uncle, Emilie met Lady Dudley and Monsieur Vandenesse and discovered the young man she admired was not what she had hoped. Disappointed, she tried to conceal her feelings but remained drawn to him. Her uncle, sensing her agitation, teased about her attachment and promised an introduction, which intensified Emilie’s interest. Fantasies bloomed: the romance of the unknown and the possibility of an ideal future consumed her.

As days passed her infatuation grew, fed by imagination and youthful yearning. She remained unaware of the complexities and compromises that love and marriage would demand, still blinded by her exacting ideals.

The next morning, before Emilie woke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse and, recognizing the young man he had insulted the day before, greeted him with the polished courtesy of the old court.

“Who would have guessed I’d meet the son or grandson of one of my best friends at seventy-three? I’m a vice-admiral; dueling’s no matter to me. I had too much grog and ran you down. Shake hands; I’d sooner endure a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than bring his family regret.”

The young man, though reserved, returned the Count’s cordiality and they rode together. The admiral joked about duels, shot a pistol into a birch to show he feared none, and praised the young man when he fired close to the mark. On the road he quizzed him about trifles that marked a gentleman—debts, pleasures, heraldry—teasing about mistresses and extravagance. Longueville replied with composed manners: he paid punctually, lived independently, and showed the polish of a fine education. The Count declared he would introduce him to society at the Pavillon Planat.

At the Villa Planat, the announcement “Monsieur de Longueville” sharpened curiosity into attention. He arrived simply but elegantly, at ease in the Receiver-General’s mansion, well-spoken and surprisingly knowledgeable—able even to discuss naval architecture when the admiral broached it. His manners suggested training at the École Polytechnique; pressed about his profession, he said he had renounced the Engineers to preserve his independence. He declined dinner courteously, explaining he was caregiver to a delicate sister.

The family was struck. He charmed without ostentation, and though he aroused Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s interest—Emilie had dressed to attract him—his measured attention left her both delighted and wounded. She usually displayed her charms for newcomers, but now seemed simpler and more guarded; when roused by her uncle she quipped that perfection often hid defects—“Those who please everybody please nobody”—a remark showing caution and pride.

Over the next fortnight Longueville called repeatedly. Emilie, used to being the center of interest, found herself drawn out of pride as an external force attracted her. He was modest and discreet: he never spoke of his family and skillfully evaded her probes. He accepted her hospitality—his knowledge of painting, his piano and voice—but revealed little about his rank. Once they sang a duet and the guests could not tell whether he was a professional. His reserve both tormented Emilie and deepened her interest.

The family wished to meet Clara Longueville, and an informal dance was proposed to welcome her. When Clara and her brother arrived, Emilie felt genuine pleasure for Clara and lavished attention, though tinged with envy: Clara combined ease and modesty and, while amiable, gently reproved Emilie’s earlier disdain for commoners. Clara confessed she had come out of affection for her brother, and her frank courtesy reassured Emilie. The exchange—Clara’s mild rebuke and Emilie’s relieved response—brought the two girls closer.

With autumn the attachment between Emilie and Longueville quietly deepened. Their life became a sequence of small intimacies: aimless conversations that skirted deeper feeling, shared sunsets and daisy gatherings, and passionate duets where music voiced what words did not. They learned each other’s ways, testing limits, revealing little and withholding more. Longueville’s silence remained both balm and barrier; Emilie cherished his attention and resented the mystery, yet the bond made her gentler, more indulgent, and less self-centered.

Throughout, the Count and household watched and speculated, charmed by Longueville’s discretion and worldliness. His blend of talent, reserve, and secrecy kept his background uncertain and his admirers hopeful. Emilie surrendered to the tide of feeling, delighting in their private intimacies, even as unanswered questions about his rank and history lingered at the edges of their romance.
