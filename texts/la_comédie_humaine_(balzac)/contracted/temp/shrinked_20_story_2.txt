The Comte de Fontaine, head of an old Poitou family, had served the Bourbon cause with intelligence and bravery during the Vendée war. Having narrowly escaped death at Les Quatre Chemins, he would wryly say, “I am one of the men who gave themselves to be killed on the steps of the throne.” Ruined by confiscation, he refused lucrative posts under Napoleon and, unwavering in aristocratic faith, married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet—poor but of an ancient Breton family—despite offers from a wealthy revolutionary parvenu.

At the second Restoration the Count, burdened with a large family and meager estate income, yielded to his wife’s wish and came to Paris. Saddened by former comrades’ greed in seeking places under the new Constitution, he was preparing to return home when a dispatch named him maréchal de camp, under a rule allowing officers of the Catholic armies to count the twenty submerged years of Louis XVIII’s reign as service. Days later he received, unsolicited, the crosses of the Legion of Honor and of Saint-Louis. Shaken and assuming these were the King’s remembrance, he no longer contented himself with taking his family to cry “Vive le Roi” in the Tuileries; he sought an audience.

In the royal drawing-room, amid powdered heads and chilly old adherents, the Count met princes he found “ADORABLE.” The King, whom the Count thought barely acquainted with him, warmly called him the most thorough Vendeen of them all. Yet no one asked about his losses or the money he had spent on Catholic regiments; he discovered, belatedly, that he had made war at his own cost. One evening he made a jocular allusion to his affairs. The King laughed, but a courtier hinted that the time for settling accounts had not yet come; older bills—the Revolution’s history—would remain. The Count withdrew, and in his hackney-cab loudly regretted that honors would not make good the three hundred thousand livres he had spent. He resolved to speak to the King privately.

When his requests for interviews went unanswered, the Count grew resentful at upstarts of the Empire obtaining offices once reserved for distinguished families. He feared the King was more revolutionary than royalist, and deplored the new constitutional order. Preparing to return to his estate and abandon claims, the events of 20 March 1815 warned of another storm. He borrowed on his lands to follow the routed Court into exile, hoping emigration might yield better returns than service at home. His calculation was sound: he was among the faithful five hundred who shared the Court’s exile at Ghent and among the fifty thousand who returned with it. During the banishment he served the King, found chances to show attachment, and amused the monarch with the vivacity of the Tuileries anecdote and discreet, well-crafted notes—little successes that stamped him on the King’s memory.

At the second Restoration he served as a special envoy with wide powers over leaders of revolt, but exercised moderation. He later sat on the Privy Council, became a deputy, spoke little, listened much, and shifted his opinions. He grew intimate enough with the King for Louis to quip that neither he nor Fontaine would keep ministerial posts on account of their opinions under representative government; the new system spared the King the trouble of dismissing Secretaries of State. The jest prefaced a rescript appointing Fontaine administrator in the Crown lands office. By listening shrewdly to the King’s sarcasms and entertaining him with political anecdote and parliamentary gossip—Louis relished such “Gouvernementabilité”—Fontaine’s name came to the King’s lips when commissions with handsome salaries were to be given.

Thanks to this royal favor, every member of his large family was, as he joked, attached like silkworms to the Pay-List. Through the King’s intervention his eldest son found a high fixed position as a lawyer. The second son, a captain before the Restoration, commanded a legion after Ghent, passed into the bodyguard, returned to a line regiment, and after the affair of the Trocadero became a lieutenant-general with a Guards commission. The youngest became sous-préfet, then a legal official and director of a municipal board in Paris, secure from legislative changes. These quiet bounties, though substantial, did not arouse envy; in the early constitutional days few understood the civil service’s breadth, where cunning favorites found equivalents for demolished abbeys. Though the Count had once professed not to have read the Charter and scoffed at courtiers’ greed, he proved as shrewd as the King about the spirit and resources of representative government. Yet, despite four official appointments and family incomes nearly equivalent to a department chief’s, Fontaine’s large household made rapid restoration of fortune unlikely.

His sons were well-positioned, but he had three daughters and feared exhausting the monarch’s benevolence. He spoke of each to the King, who, with characteristic taste and humor, arranged marriages. The eldest wed the Receiver-General Planat de Baudry by a royal speech that cost nothing and was worth millions. Another daughter married a magistrate of inferior birth but wealthy and created a Baron. When Fontaine mentioned Mademoiselle Emilie de Fontaine, the King answered with the Latin adage, “Amicus Plato sed magis amica Natio.” Days later the King amused himself with an epigram about the three daughters as a trinity—witty, but at that point their intercourse cooled. Kings enjoy contradiction: Louis’s quip, though playful, left a mark.

Emilie, the youngest and a family favourite, had been spoiled. She grew up on the estate with indulgent relations and entered Paris just as her family’s fortunes swelled; luxury and flattery became necessities. At fourteen she was still obeyed; at nineteen she had not chosen among suitors brought by her father’s politics but claimed freedom and ruled social rooms by her beauty. She was not merely pretty: well educated, painted, fluent in Italian and English, an accomplished pianist and singer, literate and witty, she dazzled the superficial and charmed deeper minds. Yet beneath the charm lay a careless heart, pride in birth and beauty, contempt for inferior birth, and impertinence toward newly-created nobility. She sought recognition of her family among Saint-Germain’s illustrious house. Such qualities provoked her father’s unease; she could stab with caustic wit, and was often implacable toward those she thought beneath her.

The Count’s acceptance of bourgeois matches for his elder daughters surprised some, given his old convictions, but it stemmed from residence in Paris and the King’s advice. Louis XVIII enjoyed persuading Fontaine to adopt ideas suited to nineteenth-century monarchy: fusion of parties and placating the third estate and creations of the Empire, while curbing the clergy’s influence. The King, as much a politician as his rival Napoleon, favored reconciliation. The Privy Councillor became a prudent leader of moderates who sought national fusion. Fontaine preached constitutional government’s costly doctrines and urged families in his drawing-room to accept that civil and military careers, in constitutional France, would be limited and that younger sons of peers would take high posts; men of the third estate would claim law and financial offices by merit. He no longer recognized nobility in France except the peerage. These views, meant to reconcile classes and secure the monarchy’s stability, led Fontaine to advise prudent, advantageous marriages.

At home, however, the Comtesse de Fontaine, tied to ancient beliefs by Rohan blood, resisted. The Count argued with arithmetic that Parisian life, entertainment, and sons’ expenses consumed their salaries. To secure the daughters’ futures and rebuild territorial fortune, they had to aim for wealthy marriages: better to settle the girls now and restore the estate later. The Countess yielded on practical grounds, though perhaps reluctantly, and insisted Emilie must marry to satisfy the pride she had cultivated.

These pragmatic unions introduced domestic discord. The two elder daughters’ husbands were objects of formality the Countess and Emilie devised. The Lieutenant-General married Mademoiselle Mongenod, a banker’s daughter; the President wed a daughter of a salt magnate; the third brother married Mademoiselle Grossetete, daughter of a Receiver-General. The new sisters-in-law and brothers-in-law, used to political salons and Faubourg Saint-Germain drawing-rooms, formed a little court around overbearing Emilie. Yet that court bred rivalry: the Lieutenant-General’s wife, a Baronne with a hundred thousand francs a year, thought herself as noble as a Kergarouet and sometimes sniped at Emilie, who in turn provoked satirical responses from her own family. Family skirmishes, though not diminishing public regard for the household, soured private feelings. The King’s earlier coolness now worried the Count; Emilie, never so high in her ambitions, had never seemed more likely to aim for elevated matches.

While this domestic warfare flared, the monarch—who had been a political helmsman through storms—fell ill and soon died. Certain then of favors to come, the Comte de Fontaine intensified efforts to gather suitors for Emilie. Those who have tried to marry off a proud, capricious girl can imagine his trouble. An advantageous match for Emilie would crown the decade of Parisian toil and secure the family’s ascent, perhaps rivaling the Habsburgs by intermarriage. The Count continued pressing suitors, but Emilie’s objections were absurdly fastidious: one’s knees too thick, another bow-legged, a third short-sighted, another limped, most were too fat, or their names unglamorous. Her coquettish rejections only made her more vivacious and attractive during the winter balls and festivities, where she criticized celebrities and encouraged proposals she invariably dismissed.

Thus the Count’s efforts, shaped by royal favor and political adaptation, produced both success and anxiety: his sons secured lucrative posts, his daughters married into wealth though not always prestige, and Emilie, the family’s spoiled idol, remained an obstacle to full consolidation of fortune and status, her caprice threatening the very royal favor the family relied upon. The narrative leaves the reader amid the family’s private battles, social maneuverings, and the fragile dependence on royal goodwill that shaped their fortunes.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

Nature had given her all the advantages needed for the part of Célimène. Tall and slight, Émilie de Fontaine could assume a dignified or frolicsome mien at will. Her somewhat long neck allowed graceful attitudes of scorn and impertinence. She had cultivated turns of the head and feminine gestures that enhanced a smile. Fine black hair and strongly arched eyebrows lent her pride, which her coquettish instincts and the mirror taught her to temper—by a stare, or by gentleness in the softness of her gaze, the curve of her lips, and the coldness or sweetness of her smile. When Émilie meant to conquer a heart, her clear voice was melodious; she could make it sharp and decisive to silence an indiscreet tongue. Her pale, alabaster brow was like a lake ruffled by a breeze, soon regaining serenity. More than one young man accused her of acting a part; she justified herself by inspiring desire and then subjecting admirers to contemptuous caprice. Among fashionable young women, none knew better how to assume reserve with a man of talent or to show that insulting politeness which treats an equal as an inferior. Wherever she went she seemed to accept homage rather than compliments; even in a princess her airs would have transformed the chair into a throne.

Monsieur de Fontaine discovered too late how utterly his daughter’s education had been warped by the tender devotion of the whole family. Early admiration had nourished Émilie’s pride and self-confidence. Universal subservience developed the selfishness natural to spoiled children, who, like kings, make playthings of everything within reach. Her graces and talents hid these faults from most, but nothing escaped a good father’s eye. Monsieur de Fontaine often tried to explain the more important pages of life to her; vainly. He had to lament his daughter’s capricious indocility and ironical shrewdness too often to persist in correcting her. He contented himself with occasional gentle advice and had the sorrow of watching his tenderest words slide from her heart as if it were marble. A father’s eyes are slow to be unsealed; it needed experience before the old Royalist perceived that his daughter’s rare caresses were given with an air of condescension. She was like a child who seems to say to her mother, “Make haste to kiss me, that I may go to play.” In short, Émilie vouchsafed to be fond of her parents. But by sudden whims she often kept aloof and scarcely appeared; she complained of having to share her parents’ hearts with too many people and was jealous even of her brothers and sisters. After creating a desert about her, she accused all nature of her unreal solitude and wilful griefs. Strong in the experience of her twenty years, she blamed fate, not knowing that the mainspring of happiness is in ourselves. She would have fled the planet to escape a marriage like her sisters’, yet was horribly jealous at seeing them married, rich, and happy. Sometimes her mother suspected a touch of madness.

Such aberrations are common enough among young women of high families and great beauty. They imagine mothers, now forty or fifty, cannot sympathize with their young souls and will dress them to eclipse them. Hence secret tears and dumb revolt against supposed tyranny; in the midst of these woes they compose schemes of life, set high standards, and cast their horoscope. After some years, by observing the world, those brilliant hues fade, and one day they find themselves happy without the nuptial poetry of their day-dreams. It was from such poetry that Mademoiselle Émilie de Fontaine had drawn a programme for suitors. Hence her disdain and sarcasm.

“Though young and of an ancient family, he must be a peer of France,” she told herself. “I could not bear not to see my coat-of-arms on the panels of my carriage, not to drive like princes down the Champs-Élysées on Longchamps days. Besides, my father says that will someday be the highest dignity in France. He must be a soldier—but I reserve the right to make him retire; and he must bear an Order, so sentries may present arms to us.”

These rare qualifications would count for nothing if he lacked amiable temper, a fine figure, intelligence, and, above all, slenderness. To be lean, a grace fugitive under a representative government, was indispensable. Émilie had an ideal standard. A young man who failed to fulfill the requisite conditions did not even get a second look.

“Good Heavens! see how fat he is!” was her utmost expression of contempt.

To her, people of respectable corpulence were incapable of sentiment, bad husbands, and unfit for civilized society. Fat seemed a misfortune in a woman; in a man, a crime. These paradoxes were amusing thanks to her lively rhetoric. The Count felt, nevertheless, that his daughter’s affections would become a subject of ridicule, and feared her eccentric notions might degenerate into bad taste. He trembled to think the pitiless world might already be laughing at a young woman who remained so long on the stage without concluding the drama she was playing. More than one suitor, disgusted by refusal, seemed to wait for the slightest misfortune to take revenge. The indifferent were beginning to weary; admiration is exhausting. The old Vendéen knew better than anyone that while there is an art in choosing the right moment to appear on the world’s boards, it is harder to quit them in time. So during the first winter after Charles X’s accession he redoubled efforts, seconded by his sons and sons-in-law, to assemble in his official rooms the best matches Paris and the departments could offer. The splendour of his entertainments and dinners, fragrant with truffles, rivalled the banquets by which ministers secured votes.

The Honorable Deputy was pointed at as an influential corrupter of legislative honesty. A whimsical result: his efforts to get his daughter married secured him splendid popularity. Certain mocking Liberals accused him of selling truffles twice over, but the Poitevin gentleman was so noble and honorable that he was not the subject of the epigrams hurled at central votes, ministers, directors-general, and official supporters of the Villele Ministry.

After this campaign, during which the Count had brought out all his forces, he believed this time the procession of suitors would not be a dissolving view in his daughter’s eyes. He felt an inward satisfaction at having fulfilled his duty. Having left no stone unturned, he hoped among so many hearts laid at Émilie’s feet there might be one to which her caprice would give preference. Incapable of repeating such effort and tired of his daughter’s conduct, one morning toward the end of Lent, when the Chamber’s business did not demand his vote, he determined to ask her views. While his valet powdered his bald head, the Count told him to tidy the room; he gave the usual orders. His anxious sinécure-holder understood the intention and prepared the study to please the eye.

The Count, wary of the saucy remarks with which his daughter answered advice, straightened his dressing-gown, wiped his nose, made the fire, pulled up his slippers, and arranged himself. He took a pinch of snuff, cleared his throat, and heard Émilie’s light step; she came humming an air from Il Barbiere.

“Good-morning, papa. What do you want with me so early?” she sang, kissing the Count with the light carelessness of one confident of pleasing.

“My dear child,” said Monsieur de Fontaine gravely, “I sent for you to talk seriously about your future. You must make such a choice of husband as may secure durable happiness—”

“My good father,” Émilie interrupted coaxingly, “it strikes me the armistice on which we agreed as to my suitors is not yet expired.”

“Émilie, we must today forbear from jesting on so important a matter. For some time the efforts of those who most truly love you have been concentrated on settling you suitably; you would be guilty of ingratitude in meeting with levity proofs of kindness lavished on you.”

Émilie set an armchair by the fire and sat facing him in mocking solemnity, crossing her arms over the frills of a pelerine à la neige. After a laughing glance at her father’s troubled face, she broke silence.

“I never heard you say, my dear father, that the Government issued its instructions in its dressing-gown. However,” she smiled, “that does not matter; the mob are probably not particular. Now, what are your proposals for legislation and your official introductions?”

“I shall not always be able to make them, headstrong girl!—Listen, Émilie. I intend no longer to compromise my reputation, which is part of my children’s fortune, by recruiting the regiment of dancers which, spring after spring, you put to rout. You have already caused dangerous misunderstandings with certain families. I hope to make you perceive the difficulties of your position and ours. You are twenty-two, my dear, and you should have been married nearly three years. Your brothers and two sisters are richly and happily provided for. But the expenses occasioned by these marriages and your mother’s required style of housekeeping have so reduced our income that I can hardly promise you a hundred thousand francs as a marriage portion. From this day I shall think only of providing for your mother. If I were taken from my family, Madame de Fontaine could not be left at anybody’s mercy. You see, my child, that the amount of your fortune bears no relation to your notions of grandeur. Even that would be a sacrifice I have not hitherto made for either child; but they have agreed not to expect future compensation for an over-favored sister.”

“In their position!” said Émilie, tossing her head.

“My dear, do not depreciate those who love you. Only the poor are generous as a rule; the rich have always reasons for not handing over twenty thousand francs to a relation. Come, do not pout, let us talk rationally.—Among the young marrying men have you noticed Monsieur de Manerville?”

“Oh, he minces his words—he says Zules instead of Jules; he is always looking at his feet because he thinks them small, and he gazes at himself in the glass! Besides, he is fair. I don’t like fair men.”

“Well, then, Monsieur de Beaudenord?”

“He is not noble! he is ill made and stout. He is dark, it is true.—If the two gentlemen could agree to combine their fortunes, and the first would give his name and figure to the second, who should keep his dark hair, then—perhaps—”

“What can you say against Monsieur de Rastignac?”

“Madame de Nucingen has made a banker of him,” she said meaningly.

“And our cousin, the Vicomte de Portenduère?”

“A mere boy, who dances badly; besides, he has no fortune. After all, papa, none of these people have titles. I want, at least, to be a countess like my mother.”

“Have you seen no one, then, this winter—”

“No, papa.”

“What then do you want?”

“The son of a peer of France.”

“My dear girl, you are mad!” said Monsieur de Fontaine, rising. Yet he suddenly lifted his eyes to heaven and found resignation in a religious thought; then, pitying his daughter who was moved, he took her hand and said: “God is my witness, poor mistaken child, I have conscientiously discharged my duty to you as a father—conscientiously, do I say? Most lovingly, my Émilie. This winter I have brought before you more than one good man, whose character, habits, and temper were known, and all seemed worthy of you. My task is done. From this day thou art the arbiter of thy fate, and I consider myself both happy and unhappy to be relieved of the heaviest of paternal functions. I know not whether you will long hear a voice which to you has never been stern; but remember conjugal happiness rests not so much on brilliant qualities and ample fortune as on reciprocal esteem. This happiness is modest, devoid of show. So now, my dear, my consent is given beforehand, whoever the son-in-law you introduce; but if you are unhappy, remember you will have no right to accuse your father. I will help you, but your choice must be serious and final. I will never again compromise the respect due to my white hairs.”

The affection expressed touched Émilie deeply, but she concealed her emotion, seated herself on her father’s knees—he had drooped into his chair again—caressed him, and coaxed until his brow cleared. As soon as she thought him over his agitation, she said gently: “I thank you for your graceful attention, dear father. You had your room set in order to receive your beloved daughter. You perhaps did not know you would find her so foolish and headstrong. But, papa, is it so difficult to get married to a peer of France? You declared they were manufactured by dozens. At least you will not refuse to advise me.”

“No, my poor child, no,—and I may have occasion more than once to cry ‘Beware!’ Remember that making peers is so recent that they have no great fortunes. Those who are rich look to becoming richer. The wealthiest of our peerage has not half the income of the least rich lord in the English Upper Chamber. Thus French peers are on the lookout for great heiresses. The necessity to marry for money will exist for at least two centuries.

“Pending such a fortunate accident as you long for—this fastidiousness may cost you the best years of your life—your attractions might work a miracle, for men often marry for love now. When experience lurks behind so sweet a face it may achieve wonders. In the first place, have you not the gift of recognizing virtue in the size and shape of a man’s body? This is no small matter! To so wise a young person as you are, I need not enlarge on the difficulties. I am sure you would never attribute good sense to a stranger because he had a handsome face or all the virtues because he had a fine figure. And I agree that sons of peers ought to have an air peculiar to themselves. Though no external sign now stamps a man of rank, those young men may have the indefinable something to reveal it. You have your heart well in hand, like a good horseman sure his steed cannot bolt. Luck be with you, my dear!”

“You are making game of me, papa. Well, I assure you I would rather die in Mlle de Condé’s convent than not be the wife of a peer of France.”

She slipped from his knees proud to be mistress of her fate, and went off singing an air from Il Matrimonio Segreto.

That day the family kept an anniversary fête. At dessert Madame Planat, the Receiver-General’s wife, spoke with enthusiasm of a young American of immense fortune who had passionately proposed to her sister.

“A banker, I think,” observed Émilie carelessly. “I do not like money dealers.”

“But, Émilie,” said the Baron de Villaine, husband of the Count’s second daughter, “you do not like lawyers either; so if you refuse men of wealth without titles, I do not see where you will find a husband.”

“Especially, Émilie, with your standard of slimness,” added the Lieutenant-General.

“I know what I want,” replied the young lady.

“My sister wants a fine name, a fine young man, prospects, and a hundred thousand francs a year,” said the Baronne de Fontaine. “Monsieur de Marsay, for instance.”

“I know, my dear,” retorted Émilie, “I do not mean to make such a foolish marriage as some I have seen. Moreover, to end these matrimonial discussions, I hereby declare I shall look on anyone who talks to me of marriage as an enemy to my peace.”

Her uncle, a vice-admiral of seventy whose fortune had risen thanks to the Act of Indemnity, feeling privileged to say hard things to his grand-niece, exclaimed:

“Do not tease my poor little Émilie; don’t you see she is waiting till the Duc de Bordeaux comes of age!”

The pleasantry drew laughter.

“Take care I don’t marry you, old fool!” replied Émilie; her last words drowned in the noise.

“My dear children,” said Madame de Fontaine to soften the saucy retort, “Émilie, like you, will take no advice but her mother’s.”

“Bless me! I shall take no advice but my own in a matter which concerns no one but myself,” said Mademoiselle de Fontaine distinctly.

All eyes turned to the head of the family. The venerable gentleman was highly regarded; happier than many fathers, he was appreciated for the solid qualities by which he had made their fortunes. Silence fell as guests looked from the spoilt girl’s proud pout to Monsieur and Madame de Fontaine’s severe faces.

“I have made my daughter Émilie mistress of her own fate,” the Count replied in a deep voice.

Relations and guests gazed with curiosity and pity. The words declared that fatherly affection was weary of contest with an incorrigible character. Sons-in-law muttered; brothers glanced at their wives with mocking smiles. From that moment everyone ceased to take interest in the haughty girl’s prospects. Her old uncle alone ventured to stand on her tack and take broadsides without returning fire.

When fine weather came and the budget was voted, the whole family—an example of parliamentary families with footing in every department—flew away to Aulnay, Antony, and Châtenay. The Receiver-General had bought a country-house near Sceaux for his wife. Though Émilie despised common folk, she was not so carried as to scorn the advantages of fortune acquired in a profession; she accompanied her sister to the villa less from affection than because fashion ordained leaving Paris in summer. The green seclusion of Sceaux answered perfectly the requirements of good style and official position.

Since the fame of the “Bal de Sceaux” may be unknown beyond the Department of the Seine, a brief account is necessary. The environs of Sceaux have a reputation for charming scenery; perhaps the fame owes partly to Parisians’ hunger for green. In the midst of a garden stands a large rotunda open on all sides with a roof on elegant pillars sheltering a dancing-floor. The most stuck-up landowners sometimes make excursions there in dashing parties or elegant carriages, hoping to meet women of fashion—or to be seen by them. The rotunda’s ballroom is crowded with the town’s shop clerks, medical disciples, and other youths, and many bourgeois marriages have begun to the band’s sound. This medley gives the Sceaux balls a spice of amusement beyond similar places: pleasure in cotton is much like pleasure in satin; middle-class girls danced as well as ladies.

Émilie was the first to wish to play at being common folk at this rural entertainment and promised herself immense pleasure in mingling with the crowd. Everyone wondered, but there is a keen pleasure to grand people in an incognito. Émilie amused herself by imagining the town-bred figures; she fancied leaving a bewitching glance on shopkeepers’ hearts, laughed at the damsels’ airs, and sharpened pencils for the scenes she would sketch in her satirical album. Sunday could not come soon enough.

The Villa Planat party set out on foot, so as not to betray the rank of those attending the ball. They dined early. May favored this aristocratic escapade with one of its finest evenings. Émilie was pleased to find quadrilles that seemed upper-class, with some young men who looked as though they had saved for a month to shine for a day; she perceived several couples whose hearty glee suggested nothing conjugal. She saw that pleasure in cotton was much like pleasure in satin; most women were simply and suitably dressed. Country folk kept politely apart. Émilie studied the mixture for satire but had not time for many remarks before she found a flower in this wide field—one figure whose splendor and coloring fascinated her.

Seated on a clumsy chair at the edge of the circular floor, she treated the living groups as a picture gallery, turning her eye-glass on people two yards away. Her eye was caught by a figure placed as if to be painted, in the best light, out of proportion with the rest.

The stranger, alone and absorbed, leaned against a column; his arms were folded and his posture elegant and unaffected. He had half turned his head and bent it a little, but without pose. His gaze followed a dancer, betraying feeling. His slender, easy frame recalled noble proportions; fine black hair curled over a high forehead. Émilie observed that his linen was fine, his gloves fresh, his feet small and well shod in Irish kid boots. He had none of the trinkets of National Guard dandies. A black ribbon, to which an eye-glass was attached, hung over a fashionable waistcoat. Long, curled lashes shaded his eyes. Melancholy and passion expressed themselves in the face; the olive complexion and a mouth ready to smile added a pathetic grace. There was too much promise to say simply “What a handsome man!” One wanted to know him. Any clear-sighted observer would take him for a clever man attracted to this rural festivity by some motive.

All these observations cost Émilie only a minute, during which the gentleman became the object of her secret admiration. She did not yet think “He must be a peer!” but rather “Oh, if only he is noble—” Without finishing the thought, she rose and, followed by her brother the General, made her way toward the column, feigning to watch the quadrille but keeping him in view. The stranger politely moved to make way and leaned against another pillar. Émilie, nettled by his politeness as she might by impertinence, began talking to her brother louder than taste enjoined; she tossed her head and laughed, less to amuse him than to attract the stranger. None of her arts succeeded. She followed the direction of his eyes and discovered the cause of his apparent indifference.

In the quadrille a pale girl was dancing whose face recalled Girodet’s Ossian divinities. Émilie fancied she recognized her as a distinguished milady living on a neighbouring estate. Her partner was a lad of fifteen, with red hands, in nankeen trousers and a blue coat. The dancer’s movements did not betray fragility, but a faint blush tinged her cheeks. Émilie went nearer to examine the girl when she returned to her place. The stranger went up to the pretty dancer and, leaning over, said in a gentle but commanding tone:

“Clara, my child, do not dance any more.”

Clara pouted, bowed, and finally smiled. When the dance was over, the young man wrapped her in a cashmere shawl with lover’s care and seated her sheltered from the wind. Soon Émilie saw them rise and walk round as if preparing to leave, and she followed under pretence of admiring the garden views. Her brother lent himself with malicious good-humour to her divagations. Émilie watched the couple get into an elegant tilbury, by which stood a groom in livery. As the young man from his high seat drew the reins, she caught a glance from his eye such as a man casts aimlessly at the crowd; then she enjoyed the feeble pleasure of seeing him turn to look at her. The young lady did the same. Was it jealousy?

“I imagine you have now seen enough of the garden,” said her brother. “We may go back to the dancing.”

“I am ready,” said she. “Do you think the girl can be a relation of Lady Dudley’s?”

“Lady Dudley may have some male relation staying with her,” said the Baron; “but a young girl—No!”

Next day Émilie expressed a wish to ride. She gradually accustomed her uncle and brothers to early rides, which she declared excellent for health. She fancied the environs of the hamlet where Lady Dudley was living and hoped to meet the stranger again. Despite her cavalry manoeuvres, she did not at once meet the Englishman who had fallen into her dreams. She went several times to the Bal de Sceaux without seeing him. Though obstacles often quicken passion, there was a time when Émilie nearly gave up the search. She might have wandered long without meeting him; Clara was not English, and the man escorting her did not dwell in Châtenay.

One evening riding with her uncle, who during fine weather had a truce from gout, she met Lady Dudley in an open carriage with Monsieur Vandenesse. Émilie recognized them, and her suppositions were dissipated. Annoyed, she touched up her horse so suddenly her uncle had difficulty following.

“I am too old, it seems, to understand youthful spirits,” said the old sailor, cantering. “What ails my niece? Now she walks at a foot-pace like a gendarme. One might fancy she wanted to outflank that worthy man who looks to me like an author with a notebook. My word, I am a great simpleton! Is not that the very young man we seek?”

At this thought the Comte de Kergarouet moderated his horse to follow without noise. He had played many pranks in earlier years when gallantry was honoured and guessed at once that by chance Émilie had met the Unknown of Sceaux. Despite age, the Count recognized signs of extreme agitation in his niece. “Ay, that’s it,” he thought. “She is following him as a pirate follows a merchantman. When she loses sight of him she will be in despair at not knowing whether he is a marquis or a shopkeeper. These young heads need an old fogy like me...”

He suddenly spurred so as to come between her and the young man, forcing the latter onto the grassy bank. Drawing up, the Count exclaimed:

“Couldn’t you get out of the way?”

“I beg your pardon, monsieur. But I did not know it lay with me to apologize because you almost rode me down.”

“There, enough of that, my good fellow!” the sailor replied harshly, touching the young man’s shoulder. “A liberal citizen is a reasoner; every reasoner should be prudent.”

The young man went up the bankside, crossed his arms, and said excitedly, “I cannot suppose, monsieur, as I look at your white hairs, that you still amuse yourself by provoking duels—”

“White hairs!” cried the sailor. “You lie in your throat. They are only gray.”

The quarrel quickly became fierce; the younger man forgot moderation. As the Comte de Kergarouet saw his niece returning in uneasiness, he told the antagonist his name and bade him keep silence before the young lady. The stranger could not help smiling as he gave a visiting card to the old man, saying he lived at a country-house at Chevreuse; then he hurried away.

“You very nearly damaged that poor young counter-jumper, my dear,” the Count said to Émilie. “Do you not know how to hold your horse? You leave me to compromise my dignity to screen your folly; whereas if you had stopped, one of your looks or pretty speeches—one of those you can make so prettily when not pert—would have set everything right.”

“But, my dear uncle, it was your horse, not mine, which caused the accident. I think you can no longer ride; you are not so good as last year. Instead of talking nonsense—”

“Nonsense, by Gad! Is it nothing to be impertinent to your uncle?”

“Ought we not to go and inquire if the young man is hurt? He is limping—look!”

“No, he is running; I rated him soundly.”

“Oh, yes, uncle; I know you there!”

“Stop,” said the Count, pulling Émilie’s horse by the bridle, “I do not see the necessity of making advances to some shopkeeper who is only too lucky to have been thrown down by a charming young lady, or the commander of La Belle-Poule.”

“Why think him common, uncle? He seems to me to have fine manners.”

“Everyone has manners nowadays, my dear.”

“No, uncle, not everyone has the air and style of drawing-rooms. I’ll bet he is of noble birth.”

“You had not long to study him.”

“No, but it is not the first time I have seen him.”

“Nor is it the first time you have looked for him,” laughed the admiral.

Émilie coloured. Her uncle amused himself with her embarrassment; then he said: “Émilie, you know I love you as my own child, precisely because you are of high birth. Who could have believed sound principles would become so rare? Well, I will be your confidant. I see he is not indifferent to you. Hush! The family would laugh if we sailed under the wrong flag. We two will keep our secret, and I promise to bring him into the drawing-room.”

“When, uncle?”

“To-morrow.”

“But, my dear uncle, I am not committed to anything?”

“Nothing whatever, and you may bombard him, set fire to him, and leave him to founder if you choose. He won’t be the first, I fancy?”

“You ARE kind, uncle!”

At home the Count put on his glasses, quietly took the card from his pocket, and read: “Maximilien Longueville, Rue de Sentier.”

“Make yourself happy, my dear niece,” he said, “you may hook him with easy conscience; he belongs to a historical family, and if he is not a peer of France, he infallibly will be.”

“How do you know so much?”

“That is my secret.”

“Then do you know his name?”

The old man bowed his gray head like a gnarled oak with a few leaves fluttering, and Émilie began to try her coquettish arts. Long familiar with cajoling her uncle, she lavished childlike caresses and tender names; she even kissed him to induce him to divulge the secret. The old man, who often paid for these scenes with presents or opera boxes, amused himself with her entreaties. As he spun out the pleasure too long, Émilie grew angry, sulked, then, urged by curiosity, recovered. The admiral extracted a solemn promise that she would be gentler, less noisy, less wilful, spend less, and tell him everything. The treaty signed by a kiss on Émilie’s brow, he led her into a corner, drew her on his knee, held the card under his thumbs to hide it, then uncovered the letters and spelled Longueville; but he firmly refused to show anything more.

This incident intensified Émilie’s secret sentiment. During most of that night she evolved brilliant pictures from dreams about the imagined wealth gilding her married life. Thanks to chance, she could now see something very unlike a chimera at the fountain-head of her imaginary fortune. Ignorant, as young girls are, of the perils of love and marriage, she was passionately captivated by externals. This birth of feeling—sweet but cruel—was like other youthful mistakes that exert fatal influence on the lives of inexperienced girls who trust only their judgment to provide for future happiness.

Next morning, before Emilie awoke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. In the courtyard of an elegant villa he recognized the young man he'd rudely affronted the night before and approached him with the pressing politeness of the old court.

“Why, my dear sir, who could have guessed I should have a brush, at seventy‑three, with the son, or grandson, of one of my best friends. I am a vice‑admiral, monsieur; is that not as much as to say I think no more of fighting a duel than of smoking a cigar? In my time no two young men were intimate till they had seen each other’s blood! But ‘sdeath, sir, last evening, sailor‑like, I had taken a drop too much grog on board, and I ran you down. Shake hands; I would rather take a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than cause his family the smallest regret.”

Though reserved, the young man could not resist the admiral’s frankness and gave his hand.

“You were going riding,” the Count said. “Do not let me detain you. But unless you have other plans, come to dinner at the Villa Planat. My nephew, the Comte de Fontaine, is a man you should know. I propose also to introduce you to five of the prettiest women in Paris. I am fond of young people and like to see them happy. Their happiness reminds me of the good times of my youth, when adventures were not lacking, any more than duels.”

“But, monsieur, are we not in the right? The sixteenth century gave religious liberty to Europe, and the nineteenth will give political lib—”

“Oh, we will not talk politics. I am a perfect old woman—ultra, you see. But I do not hinder young men from being revolutionary, so long as they leave the King at liberty to disperse their assemblies.”

When they rode into the woods, the admiral fired one pistol into a slender birch sapling at fifteen paces. “You see, my dear fellow, that I am not afraid of a duel.” The young man cocked his pistol, aimed at the admiral’s hole, and sent his bullet close to it.

“That is what I call a well‑educated man,” cried the admiral. During the ride he tested the young man on the trifles of gentlemanly life.

“Have you any debts?” he asked.

“No, monsieur.”

“What, you pay for all you have?”

“Punctually; otherwise we should lose our credit, and every sort of respect.”

“But at least you have more than one mistress?” the admiral teased when the young man blushed. He railed, jocularly, about Kantism and lawful order spoiling youth, urged the stranger’s imperfections wouldn’t hinder his introducing him at the Pavillon Planat, and reminded him of his promise to come.

Longueville thought the admiral odd yet jolly and resolved not to trust him too far.

Next day, when the house party gathered, a servant announced “Monsieur DE Longueville.” Curiosity surged: everyone wanted to see Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s face and the man who’d made such an impression. Simple yet elegant dress, ease of manner, polite conversation, and a pleasant voice won the family. He answered the admiral’s talk on naval architecture so well that one lady quipped he must have been to the École Polytechnique.

Pressed to stay to dinner, he politely refused, citing care of his young sister’s delicate health. “Monsieur is perhaps a medical man?” asked one of Emilie’s sisters‑in‑law ironically. “Monsieur has left the École Polytechnique,” Mademoiselle de Fontaine interposed; she blushed on learning Longueville’s sister was the girl from the ball.

He affirmed he had not gone into the medical profession, having even given up the Engineers to preserve his independence. The Count grumbled about doctors and dowagers, but Longueville’s visit pleased all and ended before curiosity grew impertinent. The Count called him “a cunning rascal.”

Mademoiselle de Fontaine had dressed to attract the stranger and was disappointed at first when he did not lavish attention on her. But Emilie—who usually practiced coquettish arts—found herself simple and natural in his presence. Whether smitten or charmed by his manner, she abandoned artifice and became perhaps more beautiful. Some suspected strategy; others thought she meant to conceal her merits. During dinner, as guests ascribed fresh perfections to Longueville, Emilie sat silent until an uncle’s sarcasm drew from her an epigram: such perfection must hide a defect, and she would be cautious in judging at first sight. “Those who please everybody, please nobody,” she added; “and the worst of all faults is to have none.”

Within a fortnight her family suspected she was in love. Longueville’s modesty and discretion frustrated curiosity; he never spoke of himself, pursuits, or family. If she hinted about painting he answered as a connoisseur; at the piano he proved adept; one evening he joined her in a Cimarosa duet. Bold as her uncle might be, Longueville always withdrew with tact, leaving mystery intact and curiosity within bounds.

Emilie begged to learn more from his sister. With her uncle’s aid, they coaxed Clara Longueville into the circle and arranged a light dance to draw out the shy girl. Emilie found life delicious when linked to another; love softened her—less caustic, more indulgent—and the family delighted in her change. She and Longueville walked together, admired sunsets, gathered daisies, and sang duets—petty scenes that strengthened their feeling.

At the dance, Clara’s tact and simplicity disarmed Emilie’s attempts to extract secrets. Clara’s candid remark—“I have heard so much of you from Maximilien that I had the keenest desire to know you, out of affection for him; but is not a wish to know you a wish to love you?”—pleased Emilie. Believing Longueville of good birth because of his manner and Clara’s approval, she danced with renewed spirit. Their fingertips thrilled in the dance; their understanding deepened.

Early autumn found their love strengthened by many small incidents. “Well, well; a flirtation never turned so quickly into a love match,” remarked the admiral, watching them.

The remark alarmed Monsieur and Madame Fontaine. The old Vendeen father, uneasy, went to Paris for information but found none. He warned his daughter to be prudent. Emilie answered mockingly: if she loved him she would wait for his permission before confessing. Her father retorted that they knew nothing of Longueville’s family or fortune. Emilie, defiant, pointed out there were many good families descended from illegitimate branches. The father replied with sarcasm.

The next day, the Fontaines’ last at the Pavillon Planat, Emilie awaited Longueville in a lovers’ arbor, determined to learn his origins without compromising herself. Neither had confessed love; both, proud, enjoyed the suspense. Maximilien, who had noticed Emilie’s prejudice, kept a silence meant to test her love. They had played a delicate game of reserve.

Emilie reflected that she had seen nothing vulgar in him; he seemed devoted to high interests. “Besides,” she thought, “an office clerk, a banker, or a merchant would not spare a whole season to pay addresses to me in woods and fields; wasting his time as freely as a nobleman with life before him.”

Maximilien, who had been watching, revealed himself: “Do you know it is very wrong to take a young girl thus unawares?” she smiled. “Especially when they are busy with their secrets,” he answered. “Then you really were thinking of your secrets?” “No, I was thinking of yours.” “But perhaps my secrets are yours, and yours mine,” he replied, taking her hand.

Under the sunset they walked. Overcome, Emilie trembled and asked, “Are you of noble birth?” Longueville replied, “As soon as you answer sincerely a question I will put to you. What is your object in questioning me as to my birth?” Suddenly he said, “Mademoiselle, let us go no further if we do not understand each other. I love you.” Hearing her joyful exclamation, he asked, “Why ask me if I am of noble birth?” She relented, convinced by his bearing that he could speak so only if he were a gentleman. “You thought I cared very much for dignities?” she said archly.

“I have no titles to offer my wife,” he replied half‑sportive, half‑serious. “But if I choose one of high rank, and among women used to a wealthy home, I know what such a choice requires. Love gives everything, but once married they need something more than the vault of heaven and the carpet of a meadow.”

“He is rich,” she thought. “As to titles, perhaps he only wants to try me.” Then aloud, “I assure you, monsieur, I have had extravagant ideas about life; but now I know where true riches are to be found for a wife.” Longueville believed her and added, gravely, “This winter, my dear Emilie, in less than two months perhaps, I may be proud of what I shall have to offer you if you care for the pleasures of wealth. This is the only secret I shall keep locked here,” laying his hand on his heart, “for on its success my happiness depends. I dare not say ours.”

They exchanged tender words, sang an Italian duet to applause, and parted in conventional adieus. Emilie felt bound to his fate; his dignity impressed her and deepened her love.

Alone with her father, she confessed her happiness. He asked if she had learned anything of Longueville’s family and fortune. “Yes, my dear father,” she said simply; “I am happier than I could have hoped. In short, Monsieur de Longueville is the only man I could ever marry.” The Count replied that he would act accordingly—provided the man was honorable. When the admiral was asked if Longueville was of good family, he answered he did not know from Adam or Eve. He had only observed the boy’s accomplishments—shooting, hunting, billiards, chess, fencing, riding, singing, arithmetic, drawing. He had found a card: Rue du Sentier, No. 5.

Fontaine investigated: Palma, Werbrust & Co., wholesale muslins and calicoes, maintained an interest there. Longueville the deputy had a son of thirty‑two, to whom he had given fifty thousand francs to marry a minister’s daughter; he wanted to be made a peer. The firm Palma, Werbrust & Co. was half ruined by speculation. “Any adventurer can call himself Longueville,” the Count muttered. The admiral joked about happy those whose fathers were hanged.

A few days later, on a fine November morning, Emilie and two sisters went shopping and encountered Maximilien seated behind the desk of a shop, paying out change to a workwoman and holding patterns—plain signs of trade. Emilie felt an icy shudder but concealed her rage. She went to the desk; Longueville looked up, pocketed the patterns, bowed, and came forward. “Mademoiselle,” he said to the shopgirl, “I will send to settle that account; my house deals in that way. But here,” he whispered, handing her a thousand‑franc note, “take this—it is between ourselves. You will forgive me, I trust, mademoiselle.” Turning to Emilie, “You will kindly excuse the tyranny of business matters.”

“Indeed, monsieur, it seems to me no concern of mine,” Emilie replied with sarcastic indifference.

“Do you really mean it?” he asked in a broken voice. Emilie turned away with insolence. As she bought a cape and entered the carriage, one last glance showed Maximilien standing with folded arms, superior to the disaster. Their eyes met in implacable looks; in an instant they were as far apart as if in China and Greenland.

Vanity destroyed everything. Emilie, tortured by prejudice, became feverish and for a time seriously ill; after a month physicians and family restored her. She plunged back into fashion and railed at merchants in jest—declaring, among paradoxes, that none but nobles should wear antique French costume and wishing merchants branded—but those who loved her saw melancholy behind the banter. She sometimes showed gentleness as during the summer of love; at other times she was unbearable. The family excused her mood swings as rooted in secret suffering. The admiral increased prodigality to console her.

At her first ball, the Neapolitan ambassador’s, Emilie saw Maximilien a few yards away and asked scornfully whether he was a friend. “Only my brother,” he replied; the attache also claimed to be a brother of Longueville, who had lately sacrificed his share of the family fortune to make an eldest son of his diplomat brother and was engaged in speculation that might make him a millionaire. Emilie, through quick questioning of her partner, learned details: the family had given up considerable income; Maximilien had been involved in business. Surprised, Maximilien laughed at Emilie’s blindness and urged her to make his brother happy, but civility and pride kept their enmity.

Later, in a long corridor between dances, Emilie sat near Maximilien and heard a Neapolitan duchess flutter about him. She flung a cutting remark: “She would follow him to a desert and live in a tent but not to sit in a shop,” meaning a merchant’s shop. With that sarcasm—the product of her narrow education—she slandered the idea of following a lover into trade, and once more she wrecked her prospects. Maximilien, wounded, told her solemnly he would soon leave for Italy. “You will find me married on your return,” she said coquettishly. “I hope so,” he answered. “Impertinent wretch!” she cried.

A fortnight later Maximilien left for Italy with Clara, and Emilie was prey to violent regret. The young Secretary, Auguste, took up the quarrel and avenged Emilie’s disdain by spreading the story of the “amazon who preached a crusade against bankers,” making the Court chuckle. The Count procured an appointment to Russia for Auguste to protect them from ridicule.

Not long after, the Ministry raised a levy of peers and Guiraudin de Longueville gained a peerage, the title of Vicomte. Monsieur de Fontaine also obtained a peerage for fidelity in bad times.

With age and the responsibilities of coming of age, Emilie changed. She lavished attentions on her uncle—bringing him his stick, riding with him, reading him La Quotidienne amid his smoke, learning piquet and listening to his naval stories. Though the admiral joked he knew his own longitude too well to be captured, Paris heard that Mademoiselle de Fontaine had become the Comtesse de Kergarouet by marriage. The young Countess entertained lavishly to drown thought, but beneath gaiety her face betrayed melancholy. She showed strict conduct that critics found unimpeachable; some wondered if the admiral had reserved rights over his fortune and therefore kept his niece close. People could not tell whether the Count regarded her as wife or daughter. Still, she aspired to reign in Paris and became admired in Faubourg Saint‑Germain salons; the Vicomte de Portenduere made her his idol.

Two years into her marriage, in a drawing‑room where she was praised for character of the old school, Emilie heard the Vicomte de Longueville announced. Playing piquet with the Bishop of Persepolis, she turned and saw her former lover renewed in youth’s freshness. His father’s death and his brother’s death in Saint Petersburg had left Maximilien the hereditary plumes and estate. His fortune matched his learning; the day before his eloquence had dazzled the Assembly. At that moment he stood before the Countess graced with all the advantages she had formerly required. Every mother with a marriageable daughter made advances to him, but Emilie—who had once scorned commerce—now knew better than anyone that the Vicomte de Longueville had steadfastness a wise woman sees as a guarantee of happiness. She looked at the admiral and cursed the follies of her youth.

At that moment Monsieur de Persepolis remarked with episcopal grace: “Fair lady, you have thrown away the king of hearts—I have won. But do not regret your money. I keep it for my little seminaries.”
