The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou, had served the Bourbons during the La Vendée and suffered financial ruin when his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet, noble but penniless. After the second revolution he moved to Paris with his large family seeking better prospects. Initially disillusioned by the new regime, he unexpectedly received honors — a military promotion and royal decorations — which revived hopes of favor.

Still, his sacrifices often went unrecognized as new aristocrats rose. Contemplating a return to his estate, he was drawn back into royal service during the tumult of 1815 and became a trusted adviser to Louis XVIII, prized for wit and political acumen. The Fontaine family then began to prosper: his sons obtained important posts and his daughters made advantageous marriages, typically to lesser nobility.

Emilie, the youngest, was especially spoiled and a focus of Parisian society. Her beauty and charm made her sought-after, yet she remained indifferent to suitors and looked down on those of lower birth. Despite the Count’s efforts to arrange matches, Emilie’s high standards and caprice provoked family conflict. She rejected potential husbands for trivial faults, frustrating his plans.

As the political scene shifted and the King’s health faltered, the Comte intensified attempts to secure Emilie a husband to cement the family’s standing. Her unreasonable expectations produced a series of refusals that left him increasingly desperate. Family relations strained: Emilie’s arrogance sparked friction among siblings and their spouses, all navigating their new social positions.

Throughout these tensions the Comte clung to hope that royal favor and a suitable match for Emilie would vindicate his sacrifices and restore the family’s honor. But as the winter social season advanced, her continued dismissiveness thwarted his ambitions and left the Fontaine family’s future uncertain.

Nature had endowed Emilie de Fontaine with every quality to play the part of Célimène. Tall and slender, she could be dignified or playful at will. Her long neck had an air of scorn and impertinence; fine black hair and arched brows lent pride to her features. Emilie mastered feminine gestures that could convey contempt or charm; her melodious voice won hearts or sharply silenced indiscretion. Her alabaster complexion remained calm except for fleeting emotion. Young men she scorned accused her of acting a part; she replied that she only inspired desire to subject it to her whims.

Monsieur de Fontaine realized too late that adulation at home had spoiled his daughter. Admiration inflated her pride and self-confidence, nurturing the selfishness common to pampered children. Her youthful charms concealed these flaws, but he saw them and tried to instill life’s wisdom. His gentle advice met her caprice and ironic shrewdness; it slid off her like water from marble. He slowly discerned that much of her affection was condescending, like a child demanding attention and then returning to play.

Emilie sometimes showed fondness for her parents but often withdrew, jealous of the mutual love they shared with others, including her siblings. She blamed fate for her solitude, failing to see that happiness comes from within. Determined to resist her sisters’ marriages, she yet felt pangs of envy at their happiness. Her unpredictable behavior led her mother to suspect a touch of madness.

Such pride is common among beautiful young women of wealth, who feel misunderstood by mothers and imagine maternal envy. This provokes secret tears, revolts against perceived tyranny, and elaborate notions of a perfect partner who must meet exacting criteria. With experience, many find happiness in unexpected places, learning that ideals often mismatch reality.

Emilie’s standards were precise: a suitor must be a peer of France, a soldier, and wear an Order so they'd be treated with respect. He also had to be slender; she despised corpulence, believing only the slim could be romantic. She made clear contempt for those who failed her standards, and Monsieur de Fontaine feared her eccentricities would invite ridicule and that prolonged indecision might have consequences.

As the winter of Charles X’s reign progressed, Monsieur de Fontaine intensified efforts to find matches, hosting gatherings to attract suitable suitors. His reputation as a corrupter of legislative integrity spread, yet he believed his intentions honorable. After a season of introductions he felt hopeful Emilie might choose a husband; he soon found her whims unyielding.

One morning he summoned her to discuss her future. Arranging his study to present a dignified front, Emilie greeted him with a light kiss that lacked warmth. Monsieur de Fontaine raised the subject of marriage, stressing the importance of a wise choice for her happiness. She interrupted, reminding him of their prior agreement about suitors. He urged her to take matters seriously, explaining that the family had labored to secure prospects. She dismissed his concerns, mocking the idea of settling for anything less than a peer of France, faulting the men he suggested for appearance or background.

Exasperated, Monsieur de Fontaine declared he had fulfilled his duty and that the choice now rested with her. He warned that marital happiness depends on mutual respect rather than mere wealth or rank. Moved by her father’s sincerity, Emilie feigned affection and coaxed him, yet her pride remained intact.

At a family gathering she again refused marriage, insisting she would only wed a peer. Her uncle jokingly suggested she wait for the Duc de Bordeaux, drawing laughter, but Emilie stayed resolute.

As summer approached, the family retreated to their country house, where Emilie asked to attend the Bal de Sceaux, drawn by the crowd’s anonymity. The ball was known for its lively air and mix of classes. To her surprise many upper-class guests attended. Observing the festivities, she noticed a striking young man apart from the throng, exuding elegance and dignity. His bearing sparked her imagination and she felt instant attraction.

Determined to learn more, Emilie approached him, but he remained aloof. She tried exaggerated gestures and laughter to draw him in, but he seemed indifferent. Frustrated, she followed his gaze and saw he was watching another dancer, a pale girl named Clara. Intrigued, Emilie trailed the pair as they left the ball. The young man remained attentive to Clara but still caught Emilie’s eye in their parting. She felt jealousy and curiosity, eager to learn his identity.

Days later, while riding with her uncle, Emilie met Lady Dudley and Monsieur Vandenesse and discovered the young man she admired was not who she had hoped. Disappointed, she tried to hide her feelings but remained drawn to him. Her uncle, noting her agitation, speculated about her sentiments. Insisting he must be noble, Emilie secured his promise to introduce them. This revelation intensified her interest, and her infatuation grew, fed by imagination and the allure of romance, still dazzled by youthful ideals and unaware of the complexities love and marriage would bring.

The next morning, before Emilie awoke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. Recognizing the young man he had insulted the day before, he approached with the manners of the old court.

“Who would have guessed I’d encounter the son or grandson of one of my best friends at seventy-three? I’m a vice-admiral, and I think no more of dueling than smoking a cigar. In my day, young men had to see the color of their blood before becoming friends! I had a bit too much grog last night and ran you down. Let’s shake hands; I’d rather take a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than cause his family any regret.”

Though outwardly cool, the young man could not resist the Count’s cordiality and shook his hand.

“You were going out riding,” the Count observed. “Don’t let me detain you, but I hope you’ll join us for dinner at the Villa Planat. My nephew, the Comte de Fontaine, is someone you should meet. I’ll make it up to you by introducing you to five of the prettiest women in Paris. Your brow is clearing! I enjoy seeing young people happy; it reminds me of my youth when adventures and duels were common. Nowadays, you worry over everything as if there were no history.”

“But aren’t we right to think this way? The sixteenth century gave Europe religious liberty, and the nineteenth will bring political liberty—”

“Let’s not discuss politics. I’m an old woman—ultra, you see. But I don’t stop young men from being revolutionary, as long as they let the King disperse their assemblies.”

As they rode into the woods, the Count pointed out a slender birch, took out a pistol, and shot a bullet into the tree from fifteen paces.

“You see, I’m not afraid of a duel,” he said with comic gravity.

“Nor am I,” replied the young man, promptly aiming his pistol and shooting close to the Count’s bullet.

“That’s what I call a well-educated man!” the admiral exclaimed.

Throughout the ride the Count quizzed him on the trifles his code deemed essential to a gentleman.

“Do you have any debts?” he finally asked.

“No, monsieur.”

“What, you pay for everything?”

“Punctually; otherwise, we’d lose our credit and respect.”

“But you must have more than one mistress? Ah, you blush! Manners have changed. All these notions of lawful order and liberty have spoiled young men. You have no Guimard or Duthe now, and you know nothing of heraldry. My dear young friend, you’re not fully fledged. The man who doesn’t sow his wild oats in spring will do so in winter. If I have eighty thousand francs a year at seventy, it’s because I spent my capital at thirty—honorably, of course. However, your imperfections won’t stop me from introducing you at the Pavillon Planat. Remember, you promised to come.”

“What an odd little old man!” Longueville thought. “He’s jolly and hale, but while he wants to seem friendly, I won’t trust him too far.”

The next day, around four, when the house party gathered, a servant announced “Monsieur de Longueville.” Upon hearing the old admiral’s protégé had arrived, everyone hurried to see Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s expression and judge this remarkable young man who had outshone many rivals.

Dressed simply yet elegantly, with an air of ease and polite manners, Longueville won the family’s goodwill. He seemed at home amid the Receiver-General’s luxury. His conversation showed a man of the world with a brilliant education. When the old admiral raised the trivial topic of naval architecture, he answered aptly, prompting a lady to suggest he must have attended the École Polytechnique.

“I think, madame,” he replied, “that I may regard it as an honor to have been accepted.”

Despite persistent invitations, he politely declined dinner, explaining he was the Hippocrates of his young sister, whose delicate health required care.

“Monsieur is perhaps a medical man?” one of Emilie’s sisters-in-law asked ironically.

“Monsieur has left the École Polytechnique,” Mademoiselle de Fontaine interjected, her face flushing when she learned the young lady at the ball was Longueville’s sister.

“But, my dear, he may be a doctor and still have attended the École Polytechnique—correct, monsieur?”

“There’s nothing to prevent it, madame,” he replied.

All eyes turned to Emilie, who gazed with uneasy curiosity at the captivating stranger. She breathed more easily when he added with a smile, “I do not belong to the medical profession; I even gave up going into the Engineers to preserve my independence.”

“And you did well,” the Count said.

Longueville made his visit neither too long nor too short, leaving when he saw he had pleased everyone and piqued their curiosity.

“He’s a cunning rascal!” the Count remarked after seeing him out.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine, who had dressed to attract Longueville’s attention, felt disappointed when he didn’t give her the attention she expected. The family was surprised by her sudden silence; Emilie usually displayed all her charms for newcomers, but now she seemed simple and natural, perhaps more beautiful. Some read her restraint as a refined strategy to dazzle him later.

During dinner, as everyone speculated about Longueville’s qualities, Mademoiselle de Fontaine remained silent until a sarcastic remark from her uncle prompted her to quip that such heavenly perfection must conceal a great defect, adding she would be cautious in judging such a gifted man at first sight.

“Those who please everybody please nobody,” she concluded, “and the worst fault is to have none.”

Like all girls in love, Emilie tried to hide her feelings, but after a fortnight every family member knew. When Longueville called for the third time, Emilie believed it was chiefly for her sake, which thrilled her but stung her pride. Accustomed to being the center of attention, she now recognized an outside force that attracted her, and despite attempts to resist, she could not shake the image of the young man from her heart.

Anxiety crept in as two of Longueville’s qualities—unexpected modesty and discretion—conflicted with her curiosity. He never spoke of himself or his family, skillfully evading hints. If she discussed painting, he answered as a connoisseur; if she played music, he proved a good pianist. One evening he charmed the party by singing a duet with Emilie, but when they tried to learn if he was a professional singer, he preserved the mystery of his social status.

Distracted by his reserve, Emilie hoped to extract more information from Clara, aided by her uncle’s maneuvers. The family expressed a strong desire to meet Clara Longueville, proposing an informal dance to entertain her. Despite clouds of suspicion and curiosity, joy illuminated Emilie’s soul as she felt intimately connected to someone outside herself. She began to understand life’s relationships; whether happiness made her better or she was simply too occupied to torment others, she became gentler and more indulgent.

This change enchanted her family, suggesting her selfishness was transforming into love. She eagerly awaited the arrival of her bashful admirer, and though they hadn’t spoken of passion, she sensed his affection. She led him to reveal varied information about himself, realizing he was also studying her and striving to improve her education.

The day of the dance arrived, with Clara and her brother as principal guests. For the first time, Emilie felt pleasure in another young girl’s triumph, lavishing Clara with sincere attention. However, Clara proved more adept at conversation, steering talk away from personal topics while charming Emilie, who felt both envious and judged.

“Mademoiselle,” Clara said, “I’ve heard so much about you from Maximilien that I wanted to know you out of affection for him; but isn’t wanting to know you a wish to love you?”

“My dear Clara, I feared I might have displeased you by speaking of those not of noble birth.”

“Oh, be at ease. Such discussions are pointless these days. It doesn’t affect me; I’m beside the question.”

Ambitious as her answer seemed, Emilie took it as encouragement. She danced with renewed spirit, watching Longueville, whose grace surpassed her ideal. She felt satisfaction believing him to be well-born, and her eyes sparkled as she danced, feeling a connection with him.

As autumn arrived, the couple surrendered to the tide of sweet sentiment, strengthening their bond through countless small incidents. They studied each other, as lovers do, and enjoyed aimless conversations that masked deeper feelings. They admired sunsets together, gathered daisies, and sang passionate duets, expressing secrets through music. Each little shared pleasure—the casual talk, the music, the walks—deepened their intimacy and confirmed the affection each had been careful to conceal.
