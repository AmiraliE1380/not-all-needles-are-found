The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou, had served the Bourbon cause during the La Vendee conflict. Despite his noble lineage and past sacrifices, he faced financial ruin after the confiscation of his estate. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet, a woman of noble birth but no fortune. When the second revolution occurred, he moved to Paris with his large family, hoping for better prospects. Initially disillusioned by the new regime, he received unexpected honors, including a military promotion and royal decorations, which rekindled his hopes for royal favor.

However, he soon realized that his sacrifices had gone unrecognized, and he felt increasingly marginalized as new aristocrats gained prominence. Disheartened, he contemplated returning to his estate but was drawn back into royal service during the tumultuous events of 1815. His loyalty was rewarded when he became a trusted advisor to Louis XVIII, who appreciated his wit and political acumen. As a result, Fontaine's family began to prosper, with his sons securing significant positions and his daughters marrying well, albeit to men of lesser nobility.

Emilie, the youngest daughter, was particularly spoiled and became the center of attention in Parisian society. Her beauty and charm made her a sought-after match, yet she remained indifferent to her suitors, asserting her superiority over those of lower birth. Despite her father's efforts to arrange advantageous marriages for her, Emilie's high standards and capricious nature led to conflicts within the family. The Count's attempts to secure a suitable match for her were met with frustration, as she dismissed potential suitors based on trivial flaws.

As the political landscape shifted and the King’s health declined, the Comte de Fontaine intensified his efforts to find a husband for Emilie, hoping to solidify his family's status. However, Emilie's unreasonable expectations and disdain for her suitors complicated matters, leading to a series of rejections that left her father increasingly desperate. The family's internal dynamics were strained, with Emilie's arrogance causing friction among her siblings and their spouses, who were also navigating the complexities of their newfound social standing.

In the midst of this turmoil, the Comte de Fontaine remained hopeful for royal favor, believing that securing a suitable match for Emilie would validate his efforts and restore his family's honor. Yet, as the winter season brought more social events, Emilie's dismissive attitude towards potential suitors continued to frustrate her father's ambitions, leaving the future of the Fontaine family uncertain.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Nature had endowed Emilie de Fontaine with all the qualities necessary to embody the role of Celimene. Tall and slender, she could easily adopt a dignified or playful demeanor. Her long neck allowed her to express scorn and impertinence, while her fine black hair and arched eyebrows gave her an air of pride. Emilie mastered various feminine gestures that could convey either contempt or charm, depending on her intent. When she sought to win a heart, her melodious voice captivated, but she could also wield it sharply to silence indiscretion. Her alabaster complexion mirrored a tranquil lake, disturbed only by fleeting emotions. Many young men, scorned by her, accused her of playing a role, yet she justified her behavior by inspiring their desire to please her, only to subject them to her whims.

Monsieur de Fontaine realized too late how his daughter's upbringing had been spoiled by the family's adoration. The admiration that initially surrounded Emilie had inflated her pride and self-confidence, fostering a selfishness typical of pampered children. While her youthful charms masked these flaws, her father recognized them and attempted to impart wisdom about life. However, his efforts were often met with her capriciousness and ironic shrewdness. He lamented that his gentle advice seemed to slide off her heart like water off marble. It took time for him to see that her affection was often condescending, akin to a child demanding attention before returning to play.

Emilie occasionally expressed fondness for her parents but often withdrew, feeling jealous of the love they shared with others, including her siblings. She blamed her solitude on fate, failing to realize that happiness comes from within. She was determined to avoid the marriages of her sisters, yet she felt a pang of jealousy at their happiness. Her unpredictable behavior led her mother to suspect she might be slightly mad.

Such pride is common among beautiful young women from affluent families, who often believe their mothers cannot understand their youthful dreams. They suspect that mothers, envious of their daughters, wish to overshadow them. This leads to secret tears and revolts against perceived tyranny. Amid these struggles, they often create elaborate life plans, imagining a perfect partner who must meet specific criteria. With experience, many find happiness in unexpected places, realizing that their ideals may not align with reality.

Emilie had her own stringent criteria for a suitor. He must be a peer of France, a soldier, and possess an Order, allowing them to be treated with respect. Additionally, he had to be slender, as she deemed corpulence a flaw in men. Her disdain for those who did not meet her standards was evident, and she believed that only the slim could be romantic partners. Monsieur de Fontaine feared that Emilie's eccentricities would lead to ridicule, and he worried about the consequences of her prolonged indecision in the marriage market.

As the winter of Charles X's reign progressed, Monsieur de Fontaine intensified his efforts to find suitable matches for Emilie, hosting lavish gatherings to attract potential suitors. His reputation as a corruptor of legislative integrity grew, yet he remained honorable in his intentions. After a season of matchmaking, he felt hopeful that Emilie would finally choose a husband. However, he soon realized that her whims and standards were unyielding.

One morning, he summoned her to discuss her future. As he prepared, he meticulously arranged his study, hoping to present a dignified front. When Emilie arrived, she greeted him with a light-hearted kiss, lacking the warmth of genuine affection. Monsieur de Fontaine broached the subject of her marriage prospects, emphasizing the importance of making a wise choice for her happiness.

Emilie interrupted him, reminding him of their previous agreement regarding her suitors. He urged her to take the matter seriously, explaining that her family had worked hard to secure her a suitable match. Emilie, however, dismissed his concerns, mocking the notion of settling for anything less than a peer of France. She expressed disdain for the suitors he suggested, finding fault with their appearances and backgrounds.

Monsieur de Fontaine, exasperated, declared that he had fulfilled his duty as a father and that the choice was now hers. He warned her that happiness in marriage relies on mutual respect rather than mere wealth or status. Emilie, touched by her father's sincerity, feigned affection and coaxed him, but her pride remained intact.

At a family gathering, Emilie reiterated her refusal to consider marriage, insisting that she would only marry a peer. Her uncle humorously suggested she was waiting for the Duc de Bordeaux to come of age, prompting laughter. Emilie, however, remained resolute in her standards, declaring that she would not entertain any discussions of marriage.

As summer approached, the family retreated to their country house, where Emilie expressed a desire to attend the "Bal de Sceaux," a popular local event. She was intrigued by the idea of mingling with the crowd, relishing the anonymity it offered. The ball was known for its lively atmosphere, attracting a mix of social classes.

At the ball, Emilie was surprised to find members of the upper class among the attendees. While observing the festivities, she noticed a striking young man who captivated her attention. He stood apart from the crowd, exuding elegance and dignity. His appearance and demeanor sparked her imagination, and she felt an instant attraction.

Determined to learn more about him, Emilie approached the young man, but he remained aloof. She attempted to draw his attention through exaggerated gestures and laughter, but he seemed indifferent. Frustrated, she followed his gaze and discovered he was watching another dancer, a pale girl named Clara.

Emilie, intrigued, decided to follow the pair as they left the ball. The young man, who had been attentive to Clara, caught Emilie's eye as they departed. She felt a mix of jealousy and curiosity, eager to uncover his identity.

Days later, while riding with her uncle, Emilie encountered Lady Dudley and Monsieur Vandenesse, realizing that the young man she admired was not who she had hoped. Disappointed, she tried to hide her feelings but found herself drawn to him nonetheless.

Her uncle, noticing her agitation, speculated about her feelings for the young man. Emilie insisted he must be of noble birth, and her uncle promised to introduce them. This revelation intensified Emilie's interest, and she began to fantasize about a future with him.

As the days passed, Emilie's infatuation grew, fueled by her imagination and the allure of romance. She remained unaware of the complexities and challenges that love and marriage would bring, blinded by her youthful ideals.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

The next morning, before Emilie awoke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. Upon recognizing the young man he had insulted the previous day, he approached him with the politeness characteristic of the old court. 

“Who would have guessed I’d encounter the son or grandson of one of my best friends at seventy-three? I’m a vice-admiral, and I think no more of dueling than smoking a cigar. In my day, young men had to see the color of their blood before becoming friends! I had a bit too much grog last night and ran you down. Let’s shake hands; I’d rather take a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than cause his family any regret.”

Despite his cold demeanor, the young man couldn’t resist the Count’s cordiality and shook his hand. 

“You were going out riding,” the Count noted. “Don’t let me detain you, but I hope you’ll join us for dinner at the Villa Planat. My nephew, the Comte de Fontaine, is someone you should meet. I’ll make it up to you by introducing you to five of the prettiest women in Paris. Your brow is clearing! I enjoy seeing young people happy; it reminds me of my youth when adventures and duels were common. Nowadays, you worry over everything as if there were no history.”

“But aren’t we right to think this way? The sixteenth century gave Europe religious liberty, and the nineteenth will bring political liberty—”

“Let’s not discuss politics. I’m an old woman—ultra, you see. But I don’t stop young men from being revolutionary, as long as they let the King disperse their assemblies.”

As they rode deeper into the woods, the Count pointed out a slender birch sapling, pulled out a pistol, and shot a bullet into the tree from fifteen paces away. 

“You see, I’m not afraid of a duel,” he said with comical gravity.

“Nor am I,” replied the young man, promptly aiming his pistol and shooting close to the Count’s bullet.

“That’s what I call a well-educated man!” the admiral exclaimed enthusiastically.

During their ride, the Count seized every opportunity to quiz the young man on the trifles that, according to his code, constituted an accomplished gentleman. 

“Do you have any debts?” he finally asked.

“No, monsieur.”

“What, you pay for everything?”

“Punctually; otherwise, we’d lose our credit and respect.”

“But you must have more than one mistress? Ah, you blush! Manners have changed. All these notions of lawful order and liberty have spoiled young men. You have no Guimard or Duthe now, and you know nothing of heraldry. My dear young friend, you’re not fully fledged. The man who doesn’t sow his wild oats in spring will do so in winter. If I have eighty thousand francs a year at seventy, it’s because I spent my capital at thirty—honorably, of course. However, your imperfections won’t stop me from introducing you at the Pavillon Planat. Remember, you promised to come.”

“What an odd little old man!” Longueville thought. “He’s jolly and hale, but while he wants to seem friendly, I won’t trust him too far.”

The next day, around four o’clock, when the house party was scattered in the drawing-rooms and billiard room, a servant announced, “Monsieur de Longueville.” Upon hearing the name of the old admiral’s protégé, everyone rushed in, eager to study Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s expression and judge this remarkable young man who had earned honorable mention over many rivals. 

Dressed simply yet elegantly, with an air of ease and polite manners, Longueville won the family’s goodwill. He seemed accustomed to the luxury of the Receiver-General’s mansion. His conversation revealed a man of the world, and it was clear he had received a brilliant education. He even knew the right things to say about naval architecture, a trivial topic raised by the old admiral, leading one lady to remark that he must have attended the Ecole Polytechnique.

“I think, madame,” he replied, “that I may regard it as an honor to have been accepted.”

Despite persistent invitations, he politely declined to stay for dinner, explaining that he was the Hippocrates of his young sister, whose delicate health required great care.

“Monsieur is perhaps a medical man?” one of Emilie’s sisters-in-law asked ironically.

“Monsieur has left the Ecole Polytechnique,” Mademoiselle de Fontaine interjected, her face flushing as she learned that the young lady at the ball was Longueville’s sister.

“But, my dear, he may be a doctor and still have attended the Ecole Polytechnique—correct, monsieur?”

“There’s nothing to prevent it, madame,” he replied.

All eyes were on Emilie, who gazed with uneasy curiosity at the captivating stranger. She breathed more easily when he added, with a smile, “I do not belong to the medical profession; I even gave up going into the Engineers to preserve my independence.”

“And you did well,” the Count said. “But how can you regard it as an honor to be a doctor?” the Breton nobleman added. “Ah, my young friend, such a man as you—”

“Monsieur le Comte, I respect every profession that serves a useful purpose.”

“Well, in that we agree. You respect those professions, I imagine, as a young man respects a dowager.”

Longueville made his visit neither too long nor too short, leaving when he saw he had pleased everyone and piqued their curiosity.

“He’s a cunning rascal!” the Count remarked upon returning to the drawing-room after seeing him out.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine, who had dressed carefully to attract Longueville’s attention, felt disappointed when he didn’t give her the attention she thought she deserved. The family was surprised by her sudden silence; Emilie usually displayed all her charms for newcomers, but now she seemed simple and natural, perhaps more beautiful. Some family members interpreted her behavior as a refined strategy to dazzle him later. 

During dinner, as everyone speculated about Longueville’s qualities, Mademoiselle de Fontaine remained silent. A sarcastic remark from her uncle roused her from her apathy, and she quipped that such heavenly perfection must conceal a great defect, adding that she would be cautious in judging such a gifted man at first sight.

“Those who please everybody please nobody,” she concluded, “and the worst fault is to have none.”

Like all girls in love, Emilie hoped to hide her feelings, but after a fortnight, every family member was aware of her secret. When Longueville called for the third time, Emilie believed it was mainly for her sake, which thrilled her but also pained her pride. Accustomed to being the center of attention, she recognized a force outside herself that attracted her, and despite her attempts to resist, she couldn’t shake the image of the young man from her heart.

Anxiety crept in as two of Longueville’s qualities—unexpected modesty and discretion—conflicted with her curiosity. He never spoke of himself or his family, skillfully evading her hints and traps for information. If she discussed painting, he responded as a connoisseur; if she played music, he showed he was a good pianist. One evening, he charmed the party by singing a duet with Emilie, but when they tried to discover if he was a professional singer, he skillfully maintained the mystery surrounding his social status.

Distracted by his reserve, Emilie hoped to extract more information from Clara, aided by her uncle’s skillful maneuvers. The family expressed a strong desire to meet Clara Longueville, proposing an informal dance to entertain her. Despite the clouds of suspicion and curiosity, joy illuminated Emilie’s soul as she felt intimately connected to someone outside herself. She began to understand life’s relationships, and whether happiness made her better or she was too occupied to torment others, she became gentler and more indulgent. 

This change enchanted her family, suggesting her selfishness was transforming into love. She eagerly awaited the arrival of her bashful admirer, and though they hadn’t spoken of passion, she sensed his affection. She led him to reveal varied information about himself, realizing he was also studying her and striving to improve her education. 

The day of the dance arrived, with Clara and her brother as the principal guests. For the first time, Emilie felt pleasure in another young girl’s triumph, lavishing Clara with sincere attention. However, Clara proved more adept at conversation, keeping the discussion away from personal interests while charming Emilie, who felt envious and judged by her. 

“Mademoiselle,” Clara said, “I’ve heard so much about you from Maximilien that I wanted to know you out of affection for him; but isn’t wanting to know you a wish to love you?”

“My dear Clara, I feared I might have displeased you by speaking of those not of noble birth.”

“Oh, be at ease. Such discussions are pointless these days. It doesn’t affect me; I’m beside the question.”

Ambitious as her answer seemed, it filled Emilie with joy, as she interpreted it to align with her wishes. She danced with renewed spirit, watching Longueville, whose grace surpassed her ideal. She felt satisfaction believing him to be well-born, and her eyes sparkled as she danced, feeling a connection with him.

As autumn arrived, the couple surrendered to the tide of sweet sentiment, strengthening their bond through countless little incidents. They studied each other, as lovers do, and enjoyed aimless conversations that concealed deeper feelings. They admired sunsets together, gathered daisies, and sang passionate duets, expressing their secrets through music.
