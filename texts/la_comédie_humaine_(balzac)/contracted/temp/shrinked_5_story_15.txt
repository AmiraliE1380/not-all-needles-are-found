A WOMAN OF THIRTY

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Ellen Marriage

DEDICATION

To Louis Boulanger, Painter.

A WOMAN OF THIRTY

I. EARLY MISTAKES

On a Sunday morning in April 1813, a luxurious cabriolet pulled up before the Tuileries, carrying a father and his beautiful daughter, Julie. The father, anxious and aged, took pride in the attention she drew. As they walked through the gardens, Julie was eager to see the military review commanded by Napoleon, the last of its kind before a disastrous campaign. The father noted her impatience and felt both joy and sorrow for her fleeting happiness.

They reached the gates to disappointment; the review had not begun, and the father suggested leaving. Julie insisted on staying, afraid of missing the Emperor. An officer recognized her and admitted them to a prime viewing spot. The spectacle was grand: the Old Guard prepared, the crowd buzzed, and anticipation mounted for Napoleon's arrival.

When Napoleon appeared, cheers erupted. Julie was captivated by the scene, but her attention soon shifted to a young officer, Colonel Victor d’Aiglemont, on the Emperor's staff. She felt a pang of jealousy watching him among others, though her father's anxious bearing reminded her of their precarious situation.

As the review ended, Julie's father warned her about future happiness, fearing youthful dreams would lead to disappointment. He advised against falling for a man like Victor, whom he deemed unworthy. Despite his cautions, Julie felt drawn to Victor and their connection deepened.

Nearly a year later, in March 1814, Julie and Victor were traveling together. The once vibrant girl had become a shadow, her health faltering. Victor, now a colonel, was absorbed in military duties, leaving Julie isolated. Their relationship strained; her yearning for love and connection grew.

During their travels they met an Englishman, Arthur, detained in France. Julie felt a strange kinship with him; Arthur's quiet admiration contrasted sharply with Victor's indifference, and Julie found herself drawn to his gentleness.

Despite growing feelings for Arthur, Julie remained committed to her marriage. Torn between duty and desire, she struggled with intense emotions that created deep inner conflict.

As Napoleon fell and the political landscape shifted, Victor's ambitions grew; he became more involved in the new regime. Julie continued to think of Arthur, who lingered in her thoughts as a steady presence.

The relationship reached a crisis when Victor's infidelities surfaced. Julie's heartache deepened as she grasped his selfishness. In desperation she sought solace in Arthur, though the weight of her choices pressed upon her.

Over time Julie's health declined further and she became increasingly isolated. The once vibrant woman was now a mere shadow trapped in a loveless marriage. Her longing for connection remained unfulfilled, and reality of her situation hardened around her.

At last, in a moment of clarity, Julie resolved to take control of her life. She refused to be a passive participant. With newfound determination she sought to reclaim her happiness, even at the cost of defying societal expectations.

As the narrative unfolds, Julie's journey becomes a poignant exploration of love, sacrifice, and the complexities of womanhood. Her struggles resonate with timeless themes of desire, duty, and the quest for personal fulfillment, leading her to confront the choices that define her existence.

Between the Seine and the Loing lies a flat, dreary country bordered by the Forest of Fontainebleau, with towns like Moret, Montereau, and Nemours marking its southern limits. The landscape is marked by a monotonous horizon where melancholy seems to thrive. In the center of this plain stands the old chateau of Saint-Lange, surrounded by avenues and gardens, a remnant of wealth now diminished.

In late 1820 a young woman, known in Paris for her beauty and wit, arrived at Saint-Lange, startling villagers who had not seen gentry in years. The Marquise d’Aiglemont’s presence provoked curiosity, especially as she seemed frail and withdrawn; locals speculated about financial ruin caused by her husband’s gambling in Spain and expected the estate might be sold, prompting them to consider their own interests.

The Marquise remained isolated, rarely leaving her room and only dining briefly with her daughter, whom she could not emotionally reach. Her profound sorrow and silence prompted gossip, but interest waned, leaving her to solitude. At twenty-six she was grappling with an unacknowledged grief that had extinguished maternal instincts and left her feeling like a ghost in her own life.

Her anguish sprang from a lost love, a man who had died to preserve her honor. Bound by societal expectations, she felt unable to express her pain, producing a deep internal conflict. Her beauty and youth felt meaningless as she was haunted by the absence of true love and the joy it might have brought. She reflected on her past and recognized the emptiness of her marriage, which, while socially acceptable, lacked the emotional fulfillment she desired.

Sitting in her room, the flat horizon mirrored her desolation. Seasons changed but her heart remained heavy with unfulfilled longing; she felt trapped in a life offering no hope or joy, her thoughts consumed by what might have been. Her internal struggle was compounded by guilt and the pressure to maintain a façade of happiness.

One day the village priest, M. le Curé, visited, sensing her suffering. He spoke of the universal nature of grief and the importance of faith, but she initially dismissed his comfort, feeling her pain unique and insurmountable. The priest told of a father who lost all his sons in battle, emphasizing resilience in despair. This resonated, and she found some solace in the idea that others had endured similar suffering.

Gradually she opened up to the priest, revealing feelings of isolation and the emptiness of her motherhood. She felt disconnected from her daughter Helene, whom she saw as a reminder of her lost love. The Marquise believed true motherhood required a deep emotional bond she lacked; her relationship with Helene was marked by duty rather than affection, deepening her despair.

The priest encouraged her to find strength in her maternal role, but she struggled to reconcile her inadequacy. She lamented the societal constraints dictating her life, feeling trapped in a marriage that resembled a prison. Her bitterness toward norms grew as she recognized the hypocrisy of a system that forced women into roles that stifled their true selves.

With spring came some distraction: managing the estate offered a temporary reprieve from grief. Yet her emotional turmoil remained unresolved. By the time she left Saint-Lange in October she had regained some vitality, though the scars of sorrow lingered. She encountered the curé once more, but her avoidance of him signaled a desire to move on from the painful memories tied to the chateau.

The Marquise d’Aiglemont’s journey through grief and isolation reflects the struggle of a woman caught between societal expectations and her desires. Her story is one of profound loss, the search for identity, and the complexities of motherhood, underscoring the challenges faced by women in a rigidly structured society.

Madame Firmiani was hosting a ball, and M. Charles de Vandenesse, a promising young diplomat, attended to bid farewell to Paris before his appointment at the Congress of Laybach. Although he had enjoyed the city's pleasures, he felt little regret, having grown accustomed to the transient nature of his life. At nearly thirty, he viewed existence philosophically, prioritizing ambition over romantic entanglements, and found the social scene filled with superficiality and empty intrigues.

As he surveyed the ball, he reflected on the emptiness of the Parisian elite, noting that the women and men present seemed devoid of genuine feeling, caught in a cycle of trivial pursuits. His thoughts were interrupted when Madame Firmiani introduced him to the enigmatic Marquise d’Aiglemont, a woman shrouded in mystery and known for her past romantic entanglements. Intrigued, Vandenesse was struck by her beauty and the depth of her character, which contrasted sharply with the frivolity around him.

The Marquise, at thirty, possessed a delicate beauty and an air of melancholy that drew Vandenesse in. Her demeanor suggested unfulfilled dreams and resignation to her fate. He recognized the disparity between her and her husband, a dull man who had risen to prominence through her influence. Despite her outward calm, Vandenesse sensed a profound sadness within her, which deepened his interest.

Their conversation moved from light banter to deeper themes of love and sentiment, revealing a connection that both excited and troubled Vandenesse. He found himself captivated, feeling a mix of admiration and longing. As he prepared to leave, he asked to visit her again, pulled toward her despite the complexities of her past.

In the days that followed, Vandenesse's thoughts were consumed by the Marquise. He oscillated between hope and doubt about her feelings, ultimately deciding to visit her. Their meetings became a blend of friendship and unspoken desire, with Vandenesse grappling with the implications of growing affection for a married woman.

The Marquise, too, found herself drawn to Vandenesse, yet struggled with loyalty to the memory of a deceased lover. She was aware of the societal constraints that bound them, and her heart was torn between the past and the possibility of new love. Despite reservations, she could not deny the connection they shared.

As their relationship deepened, Vandenesse became increasingly aware of the obstacles they faced. He admired the Marquise's strength and resilience, yet felt the weight of her history. Their love blossomed amid the tension of social expectations and personal memory, producing moments of joy and anguish.

One evening, during a quiet moment, their emotions culminated in a tender kiss, marking a turning point. But the intrusion of the Marquise's husband shattered the moment, forcing them to confront their reality. Vandenesse's ambitions and the Marquise's fidelity to her memories created a complex web of desire and duty, leaving both to navigate the treacherous waters of love and convention.

In the end, their connection testified to the power of love to transcend societal boundaries, yet remained fraught with the dangers of unfulfilled desire and the haunting presence of the past.

Between the Barrière d’Italie and the Barrière de la Santé, the boulevard leading to the Jardin des Plantes offers a breathtaking view of Paris that can inspire even the most jaded observer. From a higher vantage, the tree-lined boulevard reveals a deep valley filled with factories nestled among greenery and the brown streams of the Bièvre.

On the opposite slope, the Faubourg Saint-Marceau's squalor contrasts with the majestic cupola of the Panthéon and the somber dome of the Val-de-Grâce, which rise above a densely packed town. The two monuments loom like giants over small houses and tall poplars. To the left, the observatory stands out, its windows casting strange shadows, while in the distance the elegant lantern tower of the Invalides rises between the Luxembourg and Saint-Sulpice. Architecture blends with foliage, shifting with changing light and sky.

To the right, the canal Saint-Martin stretches, bordered by reddish quays and lime trees, with public granaries in a Roman style along its banks. Beyond, the smoke-laden slopes of Belleville, dotted with houses and windmills, merge with the clouds. Between that horizon and the valley's rooftops lies a vast city, hidden yet alive, echoing with sounds that declare its presence.

When sunlight floods this part of Paris, it transforms the landscape, illuminating windows, brightening tiles, and creating a magical atmosphere. The scene is idyllic, filled with the sounds of humanity and nature, a harmonious blend that captivates the soul. Beneath the cypress trees of Père-Lachaise, the entire capital lies in peaceful splendor.

One morning, as I admired this view, I reflected bitterly on those who disdain our homeland, the wealthy who travel abroad only to mock France. I gazed lovingly at modern Paris until the sound of a kiss interrupted my reverie. I saw a young woman, simply yet elegantly dressed, with a handsome young man and a charming little boy. Their joy was palpable, their connection evident in movement and expression.

A second child, a little girl, stood apart, her demeanor suggesting a deeper, more troubled nature. As I watched, the contrast between the siblings became clear. The girl, perhaps seven or eight, had dark hair and a serious expression; her younger brother was fair and carefree. Their clothing was similar, but the girl's plain collar hinted at a hidden preference from their mother.

The boy approached his sister with a small toy, but she scowled, revealing a complexity beyond her years. Her gaze was intense, as if burdened by thought. I was drawn to her, intrigued by the shadows of maturity on her young face.

As the couple continued their playful interaction, the little girl’s expression shifted from indifference to something darker. When her brother called to her, she reacted hostilely, pushing him away. In a tragic instant he stumbled and fell into the river, disappearing beneath the murky water. The scene unfolded with terrifying speed; I rushed to help, but it was too late. The mother arrived frantic; there was no hope of saving the child.

The incident left a haunting mark on the mother, who would carry the consequences of this tragedy home. The weight of her grief and the implications of her daughter’s actions loomed, a silent witness to the unfolding drama.

Years later, the Marquis de Vandenesse, now in mourning for his father, hosted a dinner with a notary present. The notary, oblivious to the tension, monopolized the conversation, unaware of the Marquise’s impatience to leave and join her children at the theater. Despite the couple’s hints to end the discussion, he persisted, oblivious.

As the clock struck nine, the Marquis finally interrupted, insisting the notary leave so he could escort his wife. The notary still failed to grasp the gravity, prolonging the talk and prompting a frustrating exchange. The Marquis, exasperated, attempted to correct the notary’s misunderstandings, but the atmosphere grew tenser.

Just then the Marquise’s husband returned unexpectedly with their children, who appeared upset. The little girl’s eyes were red from crying; the boy was fretful. The Marquise’s concern deepened as she realized the children’s distress stemmed from the play they had attended. The boy recounted a scene from the theater, revealing the source of their sudden return.

The Marquise’s face hardened as she processed the implications of her daughter’s emotional state. The notary, attempting to lighten the mood, only exacerbated matters with his comments about family dynamics and preferences, heightening tension.

Overwhelmed, the Marquise sent her daughter away, while the notary, still speaking, remained oblivious to his words’ weight. The Marquis, unable to contain his anger, confronted him and demanded he leave. Confused and flustered, the notary exited, leaving the couple to grapple with the evening’s emotional fallout.

As the notary reflected afterward, he failed to grasp the underlying issues, attributing the tension to aristocratic whims. His wife recognized the truth, advising him to stick to business in future dealings. The notary’s inability to perceive social nuance threatened further misunderstandings in his professional life.

One of Napoleon’s orderly staff-officers, called the General or the Marquis, spent the spring at Versailles. Having amassed a fortune during the Restoration and because of his court duties, he took a country house near Montreuil, seeking solitude close to Paris. The house, once a retreat for a nobleman’s fleeting romances, had expansive grounds; its entrance still faced the street, suggesting a once-quiet road.

One winter evening the family was alone; the servants had gone to Versailles for a wedding. It was Christmas time, and the revelers lingered, so the General felt unease as the clock struck eleven without their return. Outside, a deep silence lay over the countryside, broken only by the wind and distant footsteps.

Inside, the family enjoyed a cozy evening. The General, relaxed in a high-back armchair by the fire, watched his youngest son, who resisted his mother’s attempts to undress him, preferring to play with his sister. The children’s laughter filled the room; their mother, beautiful and content, observed them with affection. The General, a rugged soldier, found joy in their innocence and the warmth of family life.

At a nearby table their thirteen-year-old son read the Arabian Nights, absorbed in the tale. His sister, Helene, worked on a tapestry; her beauty was striking and her demeanor thoughtful. The family’s dynamic was rich, the children’s play contrasting with the older siblings’ more serious reflections.

As the evening wore on, the General expressed concern over the absent servants, and the Marquise, sensing tension, urged their son to bed. Then a series of hurried knocks echoed from the street, signaling urgency. The General, armed and cautious, answered the door to find a breathless man seeking refuge, claiming he was being pursued.

The stranger, desperate and disheveled, insisted on secrecy and water. The General, despite hesitation, agreed to shelter him, feeling an inexplicable pull toward the man. The stranger, with bloodied hands, explained he had been involved in a duel. As the General led him to a hidden room, he felt a mix of unease and obligation.

Meanwhile, the Marquise sensed something amiss and questioned her husband about the stranger. The General, keeping his promise of secrecy, urged the family to remain unaware. But Helene, curious and concerned, overheard their low conversation and felt compelled to investigate.

Approaching the locked room, she was struck by the presence of the stranger, who, despite his grim state, exuded a magnetic power. Their eyes met and an unspoken connection formed, leaving Helene intrigued and unsettled. She returned to the family, her mind racing with thoughts of the man she had seen.

As the night advanced, the General’s thoughts were consumed by the implications of harboring a fugitive. Tension in the household grew palpable; the Marquise, sensing her daughter’s distraction, pressed Helene to explain. Torn between loyalty to her family and fascination with the stranger, Helene struggled to articulate her feelings.

The General, now fully aware of the danger the stranger’s presence invited, resolved to protect his family at all costs. He called for the servants to return, angered as he demanded answers about their delay. The atmosphere shifted, charged with a sense of impending crisis.

Gendarmes arrived seeking the fugitive, and the General prepared to confront them, his protectiveness for his family surging. The stranger, aware of the growing tension, remained silent, contemplating his next move. The General’s resolve hardened as he faced the reality that his sheltering choice might endanger his household.

In the ensuing chaos Helene’s feelings for the stranger deepened, leading her to question her desires and the life she had known. The night culminated in a dramatic confrontation that forced the General to confront the moral complexities of his actions and their consequences for his family.

Ultimately, the General’s hospitality led to unforeseen consequences: Helene’s connection to the stranger would alter the course of their lives. What began as an idyllic evening transformed into a night of turmoil, revealing the fragility of their happiness and the weight of the choices they had made.

It was a June day in 1844 when the Marquise d’Aiglemont, a woman of about fifty, walked in the garden of her daughter’s Paris mansion. She lingered by a row of windows, sitting at times to gaze at the dome of the Invalides and her own grounds. The neighborhood was quiet; the aristocracy was only beginning to stir.

Having lost her two sons, Gustave and Abel, the Marquise had given her fortune to her daughter, the Comtesse Moïna de Saint-Héreen. Her affection for Moïna was profound, a stark contrast to her faint memories of her deceased children. Society regarded the Marquise’s devotion as a kind of superstition and largely overlooked her past and her coolness toward her daughter-in-law.

Despite her sacrifices, the Marquise was often alone; Moïna was absorbed in her social life. Her love for her daughter was clear but shaded by sadness and a sense of loss. Gossip suggested she might regret giving up her fortune, yet most praised her devotion. Rarely attending social events, she felt eclipsed by Moïna’s youthful charm when she did appear.

As she sat in the garden, the Marquise reflected on her life; her face showed the weight of experience. Beauty had faded from sorrow and time. She was aware of Moïna’s flirtation with Alfred de Vandenesse, a man she regarded with disdain, and feared it could end in heartbreak.

The Comte de Saint-Héreen had been away for months, and Moïna’s flirtation seemed perilous to the Marquise. Feeling powerless, she knew her warnings would likely be ignored. Her heart ached at the thought of her daughter on a precipice she could not prevent.

In a moment of despair she remembered the small, painful moments that had accumulated over the years, each one marking Moïna’s growing distance. She went to Moïna’s room hoping to speak, only to meet indifference. The Countess’s dismissive attitude stung, and the Marquise felt the weight of contempt.

When she finally confronted Moïna, their conversation grew tense. The Marquise warned of the risks posed by Alfred; Moïna’s flippant replies deepened the Marquise’s sorrow. Leaving the room, the Marquise felt a physical pain in her heart, realizing her daughter had lost respect for her.

Outside, overwhelmed by grief and the sense of being irretrievably estranged, she collapsed on a bench. Moïna, unaware of the gravity of the situation, believed she could repair things with a few kind gestures. The Marquise’s condition worsened; she was carried inside, lifeless.

Struck by remorse as she watched her mother decline, Moïna suddenly grasped how she had taken that love for granted. It was too late for reconciliation. As she wept, the Marquise, in her final moments, managed a smile at her daughter — a testament to a mother’s enduring love and forgiveness.

Servants rushed for a physician, but the Marquise’s fate was sealed. Moïna, now fully aware of her loss, stood before her family hollow with regret, realizing the depth of her mother’s sacrifice and devotion.
