BEATRIX

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

NOTE

Balzac, famed for detailed character studies and daily-life depictions, seldom portrayed real people. In “The Great Man of the Provinces in Paris” some figures recalled actual persons; in Beatrix he borrows recognizable traits: Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin) suggests George Sand, while Beatrix, Conti, and Claude Vignon evoke the Comtesse d’Agoult, Liszt, and critic Gustave Planche.

The opening scene captures the customs of an old Breton family — a social state largely vanished — signaling a shift from past to modernity and standing as one of Balzac’s notable depictions in the “Comedy of Human Life.”

I. A BRETON TOWN AND MANSION

In Brittany, some towns remain scarcely touched by nineteenth-century change because of poor links to Paris. They regard new civilization as spectacle and preserve old customs. A traveler seeking human types would find remnants of earlier eras across provinces: Louis XV in Provence, Louis XIV in Pitou, and older traditions in Brittany. Many feudal towns declined, neglected by historians focused on dates, yet memory of their former splendor remains, especially in Brittany, where local character keeps history alive.

Guerande is such a town: a well-preserved feudal life surrounded by walls and moats, with intact battlements and gates. The mayor’s planting of poplars along the moat sparked criticism—the promenade had been public for a century. Houses are unmodernized, retaining original features; streets resemble those of four centuries ago and are now quiet, letting a solitary traveler wander a deserted town.

The atmosphere evokes ancient customs; even a gendarme seems out of place. The public square displays traditional Breton costumes, contrasting sharply with bourgeois and noble styles. Social divisions remain stark; revolutionary spirit failed to erase rigid class distinctions. Guerande, fervently Catholic and self-contained, resists modern ideas.

Geographically, Guerande overlooks salt-marshes famed for its salt, which locals claim improves their butter and sardines. It connects to France by two roads: one from Savenay and a more frequented route from Vannes. The town’s isolation is reinforced by slow government and inhabitants’ acceptance of separation, so Guerande endures as a feudal remnant, little influenced by modernity.

The town is a picturesque blend of nature and history: salt-marshes and surrounding landscapes evoke tranquility. A carriage’s arrival is an event, drawing locals who come to barter. Guerande is noted for historical incidents like a 1365 treaty and for its coastal importance.

Near the church stands the du Guaisnic family mansion, symbolizing the town’s past grandeur. The family, tracing roots to the du Guesclins, is ancient and noble, embodying Brittany’s history. Though their fortunes have declined, they remain significant; the Baron du Guaisnic is regarded as a prominent figure.

The mansion’s entrance has a grand archway and a gate bearing a family crest reflecting pride and antiquity. The courtyard reveals stables and a kitchen; the house, built of freestone, shows remnants of former glory. The interior is well-preserved: a grand hall with intricate woodwork and a massive fireplace.

The dining room, hung with fourteenth-century tapestries, retains historical charm; the kitchen connects through a corner tower. Upper stories contain simple family and guest accommodations, with servants sleeping above the kitchens.

The garden, about half an acre, has fruit trees and a sun-dial, tended by a devoted gardener. The mansion’s architecture shows Venetian influence and a spiral column suggesting ties to family heritage.

The mansion’s grandeur contrasts with modern, often temporary homes. The du Guaisnics have hosted friends there for generations, embodying old Brittany’s spirit. The town and mansion’s description contextualize the family’s character, showing how environment shapes individuals. The du Guaisnics’ legacy testifies to history and tradition’s endurance amid rapid change.

Early in May 1836 the Guenic household consisted of Baron and Baroness du Guenic, the baron’s elder sister Mademoiselle Zéphirine, and their only son Calyste, twenty-one. The baron, Gaudebert-Calyste-Charles, had fought in the Vendée and Brittany alongside leaders like Charette and La Rochejaquelein. Before the war he had prudently transferred property to his sister to protect the family. After years opposing Napoleon he returned to Guérande in 1813, having fallen in love with Fanny O’Brien, an Irish noblewoman; they married and she gave birth to Calyste on the day Louis XVIII landed in France.

Now seventy-three, the baron showed the marks of his turbulent life and appeared much older: tall and wiry, his face deeply lined and sunken, his hands scarred like a veteran’s. Despite frailty, his presence conveyed a steadfast spirit and proud loyalty to his Breton roots. Illiterate except for his prayer book, he wore the same practical clothes—sturdy shoes, ribbed stockings, a coat bearing the cross of Saint-Louis—and moved through life with a serene, habitual somnolence his family attributed to years of duty. Conversation at home centered on the exiled Bourbons, the Catholic faith, and Brittany’s future, with Calyste their sole hope.

The baron had taken Calyste under his wing, training him as a gentleman and preparing him for conflict. When the Duchesse de Berry attempted to reclaim the throne for her son, the baron secretly took Calyste to join her cause, leaving his wife and sister unaware. They returned after three months, surprising the household; the baron observed, “The barons did not all do their duty,” a remark on their commitment.

That evening the baron slept in his armchair while his wife read the newspaper. Fanny O’Brien, the baroness, at forty-two retained a delicate Irish beauty: glowing skin, refined features, ringleted hair, elegant dress, and a warm, tender manner. By contrast Mademoiselle Zéphirine sat nearby, blind and knitting. Proud despite age, she refused cataract surgery to avoid burdening her brother financially and embraced her household role cheerfully, forming a close bond with the baroness and caring for her brother.

As the baroness reflected on her husband’s slumber the room took on the warm glow of sunset, the aged furniture softly lit and silence deepening. Zéphirine, sensing her sister-in-law’s distraction, commented on it. Mariotte, a servant, entered to light the lamp while Gasselin, another loyal retainer, tended to the horses and dogs, illustrating the household’s unity.

Gasselin and Mariotte, long-time servants, were deeply connected to the family; their lives were woven into the Guenics’. Gasselin, a diligent gardener, took pride in his work and Mariotte mirrored his steadiness. Their loyalty and the careful management by Zéphirine sustained them.

The household functioned with purpose and affection, its monotony enlivened by bonds of love and duty that created a nurturing atmosphere, helping the family endure the trials of their era.

When night fell, Gasselin entered the hall and respectfully asked his master if he needed anything further. The baron, waking, replied he could go out or to bed after prayers, unless Madame or his sister required him. The two ladies consented, and Gasselin knelt as his masters did. Mademoiselle du Guenic led the prayer, and afterward a knock sounded at the door. Gasselin opened it, expecting Monsieur le curé, who soon entered, bowing to the company and offering the customary civility of priests.

Monsieur Grimont, a middle-aged man in a cassock, had a plump visage and calm demeanor that made him seem partly Dutch burgomaster, partly Breton peasant. His joviality suggested a clear conscience; he was well-regarded in Guerande, though he acknowledged the du Guenics' feudal superiority. He inquired about Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel's tardiness, noting the chevalier's presence at Les Touches that evening. Mariotte urged caution about discussing the chevalier's visits in front of Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, who was keen on the town’s gossip.

When Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel arrived, led by a page with a lantern, Mariotte moved her stool to converse with the boy. Thin and elderly, she dressed in a fashion from her youth and always carried keys and a silver snuff-box. She belonged to the highest Brittany nobility, but her family’s legacy was waning: her sister had married a Kergarouet, who annexed her name. Managing her property with strict frugality, she was admired for an avarice that drew no censure. Her modest annual expenses and frequent visits from nieces hoping to win her favor underscored her influence.

Jacqueline de Pen-Hoel, a friend of Zephirine du Guenic, planned to marry one niece to Calyste, the young chevalier, to secure ties with the du Guenics. Despite the du Guenics’ wealth, they preserved a respectful distance, valuing pride and status. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel treated Calyste with a sense of entitlement, believing her intentions gave her oversight. She held conservative views on morality and distrusted modern influences, preferring the stability of old customs.

She asked whether Monsieur du Halga would join them; the rector confirmed he had seen him walking earlier. Mention of du Halga stirred excitement as the group prepared for an evening game. The rector fetched a basket and counters, showing a childlike eagerness.

A firm knock at the gate announced Chevalier du Halga’s arrival: a frail man dressed for his health, once a competent naval officer. Now gentle and preoccupied with his little dog, he retained the remnants of former gallantry without speaking of past exploits. His health was undermined by hardships endured during the emigration, yet he remained a respected figure in Guerande, having served as mayor.

The rector, the chevalier, and Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel had spent evenings together for years at the hôtel de Guenic, where the du Guenics held a prominent local position. The rector’s cough during the prayer for the king hinted at the political tensions still present in Guerande.

Mouche is a card game in which each player receives five cards and one card is turned up as trumps. Players may play or abstain; abstaining costs only the stake. Winning a trick earns a share of the pot, while losing makes one mouche, obliging payment of the full stake and enlarging the pot for the next deal. Players may exchange cards from the remaining deck, but only in order. The trump card belongs to the dealer, who may exchange it for any card in hand. The strongest card is Mistigris, the knave of clubs.

Despite simple rules, mouche is engaging, exposing greed, cunning, and expression. At the hotel du Guenic, players began with twenty counters each, representing five sous, making the total stake five farthings—a notable sum for the group. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel and Mademoiselle Zephirine treated the game seriously, likening it to a financial venture or stock speculation.

A convention from September 1825 stated the game ended if a player lost ten sous and wished to quit. The chevalier and the baron, however, sidestepped this by offering counters to those who had lost, keeping play going. The presence of Demoiselle de Kergarouet and her aunt raised the stakes further, since the young women, enamored of Calyste, cared little for the small sums at stake.

These nightly games were interspersed with talk of local events and public affairs. Players occasionally forgot their stakes, provoking accusations of cheating—particularly aimed at the rector. Evenings sometimes finished abruptly, with players departing empty-handed and grumbling. Attempts to introduce other games like whist and boston failed; the circle preferred mouche, which outlasted newer diversions.

As the rector dealt, the baroness inquired about the chevalier's health; he habitually reported new ailments, joking that they migrated about his body. Conversation turned to the recent death of the Duc de Lenoncourt and reflections on mortality.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel announced the Comtesse de Kergarouet's marriage, prompting gossip about Mademoiselle des Touches, a woman of questionable reputation. The baroness and rector exchanged concerned looks, especially as Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel held Mistigris, which silenced further commentary. The baroness's victory appalled Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, who became absorbed by anxieties about Calyste and Mademoiselle des Touches.

After the game the chevalier escorted Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel home. She confided worries that Calyste was infatuated with Mademoiselle des Touches, an actress. The baroness fretted over Calyste's prospects, fearing this woman might lead him astray.

The rector sought to reassure her, noting that Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel intended to leave her fortune to the niece who married Calyste—an indication of her good intentions. He warned, however, that Mademoiselle des Touches was a dangerous influence, notorious for scandal and dubious associations. The baroness, distressed, feared for her son's future and the effects of such a liaison.

As the evening progressed, the rector expressed disbelief that a youth raised in a devout household could fall into such company. The baroness lamented the erosion of her close bond with Calyste, feeling supplanted by Mademoiselle des Touches. The rector promised to investigate and learn more about Calyste's involvement with the actress.

The baroness accompanied the rector to the gate, anxiously awaiting Calyste's return; instead she heard the rector's footsteps fading into the night.

The distressed baroness returned home to find the whole town aware of her son Calyste's frequent visits to Mademoiselle des Touches, visits she had believed private. She had tried to coax him home earlier by taking up her needlework, but her maternal jealousy was in vain; Calyste spent more time there and returned later each night, once as late as midnight.

As she stitched, the baroness wondered what held men so captive to Mademoiselle des Touches, what secret charms could make them forget family and duty. She longed to meet the woman and judge her character, fearing modern influences that might corrupt her innocent son. Calyste, of noble Breton and Irish lineage, had been carefully raised; she had entrusted his education to the rector of Guerande, believing him shielded from impure thoughts. Yet his schooling had left him ignorant of contemporary literature and science, and he remained a fervent Catholic.

The baroness had envisioned a modest, secure life for Calyste, hoping to secure a suitable marriage with a wealthy heiress aided by an expected inheritance. Those plans were threatened by Mademoiselle des Touches. As midnight struck she grew anxious, afraid he might stay out still later. When he finally returned she greeted him with relief, though noting his fatigue and signs of emotional turmoil, attributing his weariness to excitement rather than deeper trouble. Still, she grew bitter as she realized his affections lay elsewhere and feared the consequences.

The next day Calyste slept late, indulged by his mother. At dinner she noticed his attire and suspected he would dine again at Les Touches. Her sister voiced concern over his change in behavior, blaming Mademoiselle des Touches’s influence. The baroness feared the woman's morals and the impact on Calyste, who had begun reading varied books under her guidance.

Calyste defended Mademoiselle des Touches, explaining she had rejected his love and offered friendship. He insisted she was a talented artist deserving respect despite an unconventional lifestyle. The baroness remained unconvinced, fearing for her son's future and loss of his values. As Calyste left for Les Touches, her anxiety deepened; she worried the woman would lead him astray and ached at the thought of losing her son. The baron dismissed his wife's concerns as youthful illusions.

Meanwhile Guerande buzzed with rumors that Mademoiselle des Touches was infatuated with Calyste and exerted a mysterious influence. The rector and other townsfolk disapproved of her lifestyle, seeing her as a threat to social norms.

Mademoiselle Félicité des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, was a celebrated writer whose life and works provoked curiosity and scandal. Orphaned during the Revolution and raised by an aunt, she developed a strong intellect through extensive reading. Despite beauty and talent, she struggled with societal expectations and the limits placed on women.

Félicité’s early life was marked by tragedy, but she emerged as a formidable literary figure, known for plays and novels that challenged conventions. These experiences shaped her views on love and independence, leading her to reject traditional marriage and embrace art. Her salon became a hub for intellectuals and artists, where she pursued passions beyond societal constraints.

Drawn to Calyste, who embodied a youthful innocence she had abandoned, she found their relationship complicated by this attraction. Despite public success, she grappled with aging and the desire to maintain beauty and independence, balancing a public persona with private longing. As her connection with Calyste deepened, Félicité confronted her feelings and the implications for both their lives, forced to weigh love against ambition and independence—stakes that threatened to upend the carefully constructed life she had built.

A few hundred yards from Guérande, Brittany’s soil gives way to salt marshes and sandy dunes. We descend into a sandy desert, punctuated by ponds and a narrow arm of the sea that separates the mainland from Croisic, a peninsula joined to Brittany by treacherous quicksand beaches.

At the junction of the road from Croisic to Guérande stands a country house set in a garden of wind-sculpted pines. Built of slaty stone and granite, it has a plain exterior with regular windows and a high pointed roof with gables and dormers. One façade faces the road to Guérande, the other looks over the desert toward Croisic and the sea. A brook runs from the park, crosses the road, and disappears into the sands.

The house’s gray tones harmonize with the landscape. The estate, Les Touches, yields income from the marshes and nearby farms, totaling over ten thousand francs annually. Though the Revolution stripped feudal rights, local salt workers still call it “the château.” Félicité, the owner, restored Les Touches while keeping its desolate aspect, adding only brick gate columns to mark the entrance.

The ground-floor plan is typical of century-old country houses, likely built on castle ruins. Félicité transformed the entrance hall into a billiard room, which opens to a large salon and dining room. The kitchen connects to the dining room via an office. The décor is simple yet graceful, with gray-painted walls and mahogany furniture covered in green silk. The dining room displays mahogany buffets and horsehair chairs, adorned with engravings by Audran.

Upstairs, Félicité occupies a suite with a salon, a large bedroom, and two cabinets; another suite serves as guest rooms. The guest chambers are modestly furnished, while Félicité’s apartment shows luxurious taste: Gobelin tapestries, heavy silk drapes, and fine Louis XV furniture. Her study contrasts with the bedroom’s opulence, furnished in modern mahogany and filled with personal items that reveal her complex character.

Outside, the salt marshes, dunes, and distant ocean create a melancholic yet uplifting atmosphere. This stark beauty captivates Camille, who prefers the desolate surroundings to Guérande’s lushness. Calyste also feels drawn to Les Touches, seeing it as refuge from Guérande’s confines. His feelings for Félicité, founded on admiration and a desire to love, persist despite her rejections.

At Les Touches, Calyste encounters rich art and culture, experiencing nineteenth-century music and literature that awaken dormant sensibilities. Félicité, nurturing his intellect, becomes a maternal figure, inspiring both admiration and affection.

Félicité is currently giving Calyste music lessons, and the house feels alive with creativity. The contrast between the vibrant world of art and rural dullness profoundly affects him. As he approaches the house he is filled with anticipation.

Upon entering, Calyste hears Félicité playing the piano, but the music stops when he reaches the billiard room. He suspects she is in her private salon and sits quietly to listen. Her improvisation, when it resumes, is hauntingly beautiful, expressing deep emotion that resonates with him. When he finally enters, he finds her in tears, anxious over Claude Vignon’s absence.

Calyste offers to search for Claude, but Félicité gently asks him to stay, saying her tears are a natural overflow of feeling. She reflects on the complexities of her emotions and on her relationship with Claude, revealing insecurities and desires. Despite her sadness, she finds solace in Calyste’s presence, valuing his youthful innocence.

Félicité acknowledges the age difference between herself and Calyste, admitting her rejection of his love stemmed from self-awareness and the inevitability of their separation. She expresses a longing for love and connection, lamenting her inability to find fulfillment in relationships.

As they converse, Félicité insists on being an honest woman and prepares to tell Calyste her story. She settles to tell it over tea, creating an intimate moment between them. Calyste, eager to support her, sits at her feet, unaware of the full depth of her emotions as they share this fragile connection.

“I promised you this tale of the past, and here it is,” said Camille. “The person who sent that letter yesterday, and who may be here tomorrow, is the Marquise de Rochefide. The old marquis, after marrying his eldest daughter to a Portuguese grandee, sought a noble match for his son to secure a peerage he never obtained. The Comtesse de Montcornet mentioned a young lady in Orne, Mademoiselle Béatrix-Maximilienne-Rose de Casteran, the youngest daughter of the Marquis de Casteran, who wished to marry off daughters without dowries to preserve his fortune for his son. The Casterans are of the bluest blood. Béatrix, raised at the château de Casteran, was twenty when she married in 1828. She was known for originality — independence of thought, enthusiasm, and a passion for Art. A woman following such paths risks falling into abysses, unlike men who have the strength to navigate them. Her grandmother was pleased with her marriage to a man of lesser stature. The Rochefides were satisfied: the Casterans linked them with the Verneuils and d’Esgrignons, and secured a peerage for their son in a batch made by Charles X., later revoked by the July Revolution.

The early days of marriage can be perilous. Rochefide mistook Béatrix’s ignorance for coldness, branding her among frigid women, which drove him back to his bachelor life. People said he was fortunate to have a wife who would only have head passions, content to shine and create a salon of distinguished minds. Yet Rochefide was no ordinary fool; he had the vanity of a clever man, which bred jealousy and tyranny when Béatrix feigned indifference to his infidelity. I explain this to account for her conduct. Béatrix admired me greatly, but admiration can swiftly turn to rivalry. I had a remarkable salon in Paris, and she sought to found her own, attempting to draw my circle. She attracted superficial people but lacked time to build a true society. I thought her desire for celebrity consumed her, but she possessed a grand soul, regal pride, and the capacity to grasp subjects from metaphysics to music.

Béatrix is strikingly beautiful: slender and fair, with a long, pointed face and a magnificent forehead. Her pale sea-green eyes often wore dark rings; her features were sharp yet elegant. She had an exquisite waist and noble shoulders, though her bust was less developed. Despite such flaws, she carried herself with a natural grace revealing her birth. Dressed well, she could seem divine.

Félicité showed Calyste a fine copy of a painting by Mieris, a woman in white satin. “Fair women have a precious diversity,” Camille remarked, “while brunettes like us have only one way to charm.” She warned Calyste not to fall for Béatrix from her description, as he would be too late.

Despite her beauty, Béatrix lacked the pliant charm of her coloring; her lines were severe, and her face could appear harsh. She spent her early marriage judging men and life; the revolutionary disturbances bored her at her husband’s country estate. Upon returning to Paris, she believed the July Revolution would spur a moral upheaval, and she took an intellectual interest in the new doctrines. But she soon realized the regime offered no place for individual superiority and chose personal happiness over societal ideals.

Around this time Béatrix met Gennaro Conti, a Neapolitan composer with a brilliant mind. Though talented, he would never reach the first rank. Conti’s voice was captivating, and Béatrix fell madly in love, taking him from me. She won my esteem by confiding in me and making me arbiter of her destiny. I was devoted to Conti, but I knew his true nature. He was charming on the surface but duplicitous beneath. He portrayed himself as an artist inspired by divine forces, but his motives were self-serving.

I warned Béatrix about the gulf she was entering, but she was blinded by passion. Conti was ecstatic, and I lent him money for their journey to Italy. Béatrix left a letter for her husband and departed, where she remained for two years, writing enchanting letters to me. She expressed adoration for me, seeing me as the only woman who understood her.

At that moment Claude Vignon entered, causing a brief silence. He was imposing despite a haggard look: a high forehead and a firm mouth that hinted at sarcasm. Vignon was complex, capable of deep thought but often paralyzed by it. He criticized without constructing, and his intellect was sharp yet indifferent to the material world.

“Which way did you come?” asked Mademoiselle des Touches, surprised.

“By the door,” replied Claude dryly.

“Am I in the way?” he asked, sensing tension.

Calyste, provoked, attempted to speak but was interrupted by Claude’s sardonic remarks about sentiments. Calyste left, feeling anger at Claude’s indelicacy and pity for Félicité. He returned home, where his mother expressed joy at his arrival, and he took out Béatrix’s letter, which might disrupt their family’s peace.

In her letter Béatrix described Italy: fatigue from constant enjoyment and the strains of a new life with Conti. She felt a loss of social equality and reflected on sacrifices made for love. Béatrix acknowledged disadvantages of her position, contrasting it with Camille’s freedom. She longed for solitude and admired Italy’s beauty, while lamenting financial struggles that would drive them back to Paris.

Calyste, folding the letter, felt sadness. His mother sensed his melancholy and asked. He explained how Félicité had opened his eyes to the insufficiency of his education and the possibility of a nobler life. His aunt interrupted, suggesting he marry a good Breton girl, but Calyste rejected the notion, yearning for the freedom and passion he associated with love.

His mother, understanding his desires, embraced him and encouraged him to seek happiness. The family dynamic was tender, with mother and aunt trying to ground him while he dreamed of love and adventure.

After dinner Calyste returned to Les Touches, where Claude and Félicité remained at the table. Claude, a gourmand, enjoyed the lavish meal Félicité had prepared. Calyste presented Béatrix’s letter; Claude remarked on the fortune of Parisian women who have men of genius to adore them. Félicité defended Béatrix, insisting her grand nature would not change.

As the evening moved on, Claude and Félicité engaged in witty banter, captivating Calyste with their intellect. Claude speculated about Béatrix and Conti’s return, which stirred emotions in Camille. Calyste, lost in thought, pictured Béatrix as Félicité had described her, feeling a deep sympathy.

When he returned home his mother questioned him about his late evening. Calyste confessed his admiration for genius and lamented his lack of it. His mother reassured him, emphasizing the importance of love and happiness, encouraging him to pursue noble women if he must love.

What young man, full of restrained emotion, would not be eager to see Madame de Rochefide land at Croisic? Calyste surprised his parents by leaving early, urged by an unfamiliar vigor as he hurried along the walls of Les Touches to avoid being seen. He felt excitement and a touch of shame, fearing the perceptiveness of Felicite and Vignon.

At the shore he found a travelers' shelter and watched two boats arrive from Croisic, loaded with luggage that suggested distinguished passengers. One boat carried a young woman in a straw bonnet, a maid, and a footman. Calyste was captivated by a chest stamped "MME. LA MARQUISE DE ROCHEFIDE," feeling an inexplicable connection. He believed he would love her, his heart readily transferring the affection he had once felt for Camille to this new figure.

While the luggage was unloaded he overheard the maid and footman discussing their wait for someone from Les Touches and learned that the marquise had already been picked up. He hurried toward Guerande, skirting the servants of Les Touches, agitated and longing.

Calyste’s thoughts were consumed by the marquise, and he contrasted this new eagerness with his previous relationship with Camille. He returned home late in the afternoon to find a letter from Mademoiselle des Touches inviting him to celebrate the marquise's arrival. The letter mentioned the Casteran family, to which the marquise belonged, and reassured his mother of the woman’s noble lineage.

Walking to the gathering, Calyste was a tumult of feeling. He arrived at Camille's suite where company had assembled. Camille greeted him, and he was introduced to Gennaro Conti, a man with a Byron-like presence. The marquise, reclining on a divan, exceeded his imagination: more beautiful than he had pictured, hair braided with bluebells and elegantly dressed. Calyste felt an immediate affection mingled with hope and fear.

At dinner Calyste was assigned to escort the marquise. His heart raced and he struggled for words, daunted by her beauty. She noticed his nervousness and asked if he were unwell. He admitted he had never seen anyone so beautiful except his mother. Mademoiselle des Touches, aware of his feelings, counseled restraint; she warned that the marquise could not return such fondness.

Conversation turned to Camille's travels, and Calyste increasingly perceived distance between himself and the marquise, who seemed absorbed by Conti. He felt insignificant beside the artist’s talent. When Conti and Camille performed music, it stirred deep emotion in Calyste; he was moved to tears. The marquise, noticing his distress, turned away, while Calyste grappled with feelings of inadequacy.

As the evening progressed, his admiration for the marquise deepened even as his despair grew. The contrast between his ardor and Conti's brilliance overwhelmed him. In a moment of vulnerability he slipped away to a nearby sofa, lost in thought, wrestling with the intensity of his emotions and the painful sense of his own insignificance before such talent.

X. DRAMA

“What is it, my child?” Claude Vignon asked Calyste, who had just entered the bedroom.

“You love; you think you are disdained, but it is not so. Soon, the field will be free to you, and you will reign—beloved by more than one.”

“Loved!” Calyste exclaimed, pulling Claude into the library. “Who loves me here?”

“Camille,” Claude replied.

“Camille loves me? And you—what of you?”

“I?” Claude paused, then sat, resting his head. “I am tired of life, but lack the courage to leave it. I wish I were mistaken; yet I have seen many truths lately. I did not wander the marshes for pleasure. The bitterness I felt on returning to find you with Camille sprang from wounded feelings. I will confront her soon. Two clear-sighted minds like ours cannot deceive each other. Between two such duellists, the combat cannot last long. I will leave Les Touches tomorrow, perhaps with Conti. After we are gone, strange things will befall here. I will regret not witnessing the rare conflicts of passion that will unfold. You are young to enter such dangerous waters; you interest me. Were it not for my disgust for women, I would stay and help you navigate this game. It is difficult; you may lose it. You are dealing with two extraordinary women, and you feel too much for one to use the other wisely. Beatrix is relentless; Camille has grandeur. Beware—you may be wrecked between those reefs.”

Calyste, stunned, listened like a traveler shown an abyss. To learn that Camille loved him while he loved Beatrix was a burden he could not bear. Overwhelmed with regret, he replayed Felicite’s rejection and Claude’s arrival. Beatrix’s voice from the salon sparked a fierce desire to carry her off. If Camille loved him, how could he adore Beatrix? He saw no solution.

Silence fell; doors opened and shut. Midnight struck, and the voices of Claude and Camille roused him. Before he could rise, he heard Claude say to Felicite, “You came to Paris last year desperately in love with Calyste, but you feared the consequences: a gulf, perhaps suicide. Love must think itself eternal; you foresaw a terrible parting. You brought me to Les Touches as a screen to follow your secret adoration. The scheme was grand and ignoble; you should have chosen a common man or one so preoccupied you could deceive him. You thought me simple and easy to mislead. I am not a man of genius; I am a man of talent, and I have divined you. Your eyes were on me, but your heart throbbed for Calyste. You have never been loved, Maupin, and you never will be after rejecting the beautiful fruit chance offered you.”

“Why has love fled me?” she asked softly.

“Because you are not lovable,” he replied. “You do not bend to love; love must bend to you. Your mind is too deep; you have never been naive. Your charm is mystery, abstract, not active. Your strength repulses strong men who fear a struggle. You may please young souls like Calyste, who like to be protected; even they will tire. You are grand and sublime; bear the consequence—they fatigue.”

“What a sentence!” Camille exclaimed. “Am I not a woman? Do you think me an anomaly?”

“Possibly,” Claude replied.

“We will see!” she said, stung.

“Farewell, my dear; I leave tomorrow. I am not angry with you; I think you the greatest of women, but if I continued to serve you as a screen, you would despise me. We can part now without pain or remorse; we have neither happiness to regret nor hopes betrayed. To you, as with a few rare men, love is not what Nature made it—a need of fleeting joys. You see love as Christianity has made it—an ideal kingdom full of noble sentiments and spiritual sensations. I thought I held in you a key to that door, but you misled me. I return to Paris to my misery. Such deception, earlier in life, would have made me flee existence; today it plunges me into awful solitude.”

At that moment, Calyste appeared.

“I ought not to leave you ignorant that I am here,” he said.

Mademoiselle des Touches showed fear; a flush colored her impassible face.

“We thought you gone, Calyste,” Claude said. “This involuntary discretion on both sides will do no harm; perhaps you may be more at ease at Les Touches knowing Felicite as she is. Her silence shows me I am not mistaken about the part she meant me to play. As I told you, she loves you, but it is for yourself, not for herself—a sentiment few women can conceive; few know the voluptuous pleasure of sufferings born of longing—that emotion is magnificent and reserved to man. But she is in some sense a man,” he added sarcastically. “Your love for Beatrix will make her suffer and make her happy too.”

Tears filled Mademoiselle des Touches’ eyes as she looked at Calyste, who had raised his head abruptly. Claude enjoyed the sight of their confusion.

“You yourself have driven him to Madame de Rochefide,” Claude continued, “and he is now under her spell. You have dug your own grave. Had you confided in me, you would have escaped the sufferings that await you.”

“Sufferings!” Camille cried, taking Calyste’s head in her hands and kissing his hair, her tears falling on it. “No, Calyste; forget what you have heard; I count for nothing in all this.”

She rose, standing before the two men with an intensity that subdued them.

“While Claude was speaking,” she said, “I conceived the beauty and grandeur of love without hope; it brings us nearest God. Do not love me, Calyste; but I will love you as no woman will!”

It was the cry of a wounded eagle seeking its eyrie. Claude knelt, kissed her hand.

“Leave us now, Calyste,” she said. “It is late, and your mother will be uneasy.”

Calyste returned to Guerande heavy-hearted, glancing repeatedly toward Beatrix’s window. He felt little pity for Camille at first, but the memory of her tears and her kiss on his hair haunted him. At home, his mother sat sewing by lamplight.

“What has happened?” Fanny asked.

Calyste took his mother in his arms, kissing her cheeks and forehead.

“It is you I love, you!” he cried. “You, who live for me; you, whom I long to make happy!”

“But you are not yourself, my child,” the baroness said. “What has happened?”

“Camille loves me, but I love her no longer,” he replied.

The next day Calyste had Gasselin watch the road to Saint-Nazaire. A carriage passed with four persons—two ladies and two gentlemen.

“Then saddle my horse and my father’s,” Calyste said.

At breakfast the household bantered about the possibility of a meeting with Charlotte. Calyste pretended indifference about marriage prospects; his family teased him. He rode briskly, secretly hoping to intercept the visitors. At Saint-Nazaire, he found Camille and the marquise waving handkerchiefs to departing passengers. Beatrix, charming in a straw hat and muslin, leaned on a parasol; Camille took Calyste’s arm, leaving Beatrix to watch the vessel.

Calyste then saw the arrival of Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, the Vicomtesse de Kergarouet, and Charlotte. Camille suggested Calyste offer the ladies seats in her carriage, and he did. The viscountess, delighted to meet Camille Maupin and to be offered a ride, accepted despite her elder sister’s objections. Charlotte spotted Calyste and greeted him eagerly. He walked with Charlotte and her aunt in embarrassed silence; Charlotte’s early hopes were dashed.

Charlotte, plain but spirited, wore shabby travel dress, painfully aware of the contrast with Camille and Beatrix. The Vicomtesse, tall and pretentious, sought to humiliate Parisians yet craved compliments. Her manners and airs amused and embarrassed her party.

“Monsieur le chevalier,” she said, “has conveyed your proposal, but we fear inconveniencing you.”

“We can very well put five in the carriage,” Mademoiselle des Touches replied. “Your maid can sit with the coachman; your luggage can go behind.”

When the viscountess learned that Madame de Rochefide was of the party she exclaimed at the marquise’s misfortunes, then compassion when Camille revealed the separation from Conti: “Poor lady! Is she legally separated?”

“No, by mutual consent,” Camille replied.

The old maid retreated with Charlotte. Calyste seized the marquise’s hand, kissed it, and left a tear upon it; Beatrix turned, wiping away tears of anger. Camille observed the effect of her calm on Calyste, and he admired her soothing manner despite recent declarations.

“What queens!” he said to Charlotte, pointing to the marquise and Camille.

They drove through Guerande to the gate; Calyste gave his arm to the two ladies and bid Camille and Beatrix adieu. He kissed Felicite’s hand but could not reach the marquise’s; she held her arms crossed. Camille whispered a friendly kiss in his ear as the carriage drove away.

Back home, the guests dined. The viscountess spoke of literature, trying to engage the provincials, but the topic sailed over most heads. After dinner there was a boisterous game of mouche; Calyste’s return to gaiety made everyone assume he was almost as good as married to Charlotte. He alone remained silent, comparing his childhood friends to the accomplished Parisian women. His mother, Fanny, alone sensed his unease and urged him to arrange the viscountess’s return in Camille’s carriage. Calyste ran back to Les Touches to settle it.

XI. FEMALE DIPLOMACY

Calyste arrived as Camille and Beatrix left the grand salon after dinner; he offered his arm to Felicite.

“So you have abandoned your viscountess and her daughter for us,” she said, pressing his arm. “We can now understand that sacrifice.”

“Are these Kergarouets related to Admiral de Kergarouet?” Madame de Rochefide asked.

“The viscountess is his great-niece,” Camille replied.

“Well, she’s a charming girl,” Beatrix said. “She will just do for you, Monsieur du Guenic.”

“The marriage will never take place,” Camille said hastily.

Calyste, mortified by the marquise’s calm about the Breton girl, remained speechless. Camille, seeing his despair and the faint flush in her own cheeks, awkwardly let him take her hand; she then seated herself at the piano and played melancholy variations.

Beatrix observed Calyste closely. After an hour, Beatrix retired to her apartments and Camille took Calyste into her chamber, closing the door.

“My child,” she said, “if you want to succeed with Beatrix, you must seem to love me still, or you will fail. You are a child; you know nothing of women; all you know is how to love. Loving and making oneself beloved are different. If you go your own way, you will fall into horrible suffering. If you rouse in her not pride but self-will—her stubbornness—she might go to Paris and rejoin Conti; what will you do then?”

“I shall love her.”

“You won’t see her again.”

“Oh! yes, I shall,” he said.

“How?”

“I shall follow her.”

“Why, you are as poor as Job,” Camille said. “Calyste, listen. You have too much candor to play a part; I don’t want to corrupt you. Yet deception is the only way to win Beatrix; I take it upon myself. In a week she shall love you.”

“Is it possible?” he cried, clasping his hands.

“Yes,” Camille replied. “You must promise blind obedience. Come here every day, devote yourself to me; avoid Beatrix. We will stay together until four; you shall study, I will smoke. You are young, but if you break our agreement, even a brief private conversation with the marquise, you will lose her forever.”

“I don’t understand,” Calyste said simply.

“If you did, you wouldn’t be the noble Calyste you are,” she replied, kissing his hand.

Calyste, moved, embraced Camille about the waist and kissed her gently in a gesture of tenderness. Mademoiselle des Touches did not restrain her tears.

“Go now,” she said. “Tell your viscountess my carriage is at her command.”

He returned home gaily, believing that in a week Beatrix would love him. At the card-table he was restored to youthful cheer; Charlotte thought her triumph assured. The household played late, the baron sleepy, the baroness stirring out of maternal concern. Mariotte served a modest festive supper; the du Guenic hospitality produced a warm, simple charm.

After Calyste left Les Touches, Beatrix returned to Camille and found her weeping on the sofa.

“What is it, Felicite?” the marquise asked.

“I am forty years old, and I love him!” Mademoiselle des Touches cried. “If you knew the tears I have shed over lost youth! To be loved out of pity! To owe happiness to perpetual care and traps laid for innocence—oh, it is infamous! If it were not that love absolves, if it did not make me feel above other women in his memory, I would—if he asked it—fling myself into the sea. Sometimes I wish he would ask it. By coming here, you have set me a hard task. I know it will be difficult to keep him against you; but you love Conti, you are noble and generous, you will help me retain Calyste. I expected your impression on him, but I have not seemed jealous; that would only add fuel. I painted you so glowing a portrait that you hardly realize it; yet you are more beautiful than ever.”

The marquise, informed by Conti and Claude of Felicite’s position, resolved to behave generously and give Calyste the cold shoulder; but she felt pleasure at being loved. Felicite coaxed and flattered her, noting a slight withering at Beatrix’s temples and wrists, and speaking of checks men do not perceive.

“Your letter told me all,” Camille replied. “Happiness ignores everything but itself. You boasted too much of yours to be really happy. I feared to have you here. My dear, Calyste is an angel; his innocent heart will not resist your eyes; already he admires you too much not to love you at the first encouragement; your coldness alone can preserve him for me. I confess cowardly that if he were taken from me, I should die. That dreadful ‘Adolphe’ tells only of the man's woes; who tells the woman’s? They would dishonor her sex. Ah! I measure the abyss by my fears. But I have one support; I know a means to keep him true to me.”

“What means?” Beatrix asked.

“That is my secret. Let me have the benefits of my age. If Claude throws me back into the gulf, I, who had climbed to a rock, will at least gather the pale flowers that grow in its depths.”

Beatrix was molded by Camille’s hands. She left piqued with curiosity, balancing jealousy and generosity, her mind full of Calyste. Alone, Camille gave way to tears, smoked opium-laced tobacco, and dwelt on the young lover’s head—thinking what a book she might write if she could express her pain.

The next morning Calyste arrived before midday and found Camille at the window, smoking and contemplating the sea and him. She instructed him to remain to dinner but to refuse with a glance at the marquise that would show regret.

When the three met in the salon and played their comedy, Calyste’s glance at Beatrix was more expressive than Felicite expected. Beatrix had dressed charmingly.

The maneuvers continued six days, during which Camille used all her skill in conversations and small deceptions, the preliminaries of a duel between two women: feints, false generosities, crafty confidences, by which one hid and the other bared her love. Camille’s treacherous words often pierced Beatrix’s heart; the marquise took offense at what she perceived as distrust. At the same time, she was secretly amused to find the great writer vulnerable to such pettiness and resolved to show Camille where her greatness ended.

“My dear, what is the excuse today for Monsieur du Guenic’s not dining with us?” Beatrix asked, looking maliciously. “Monday you said we had engagements; Tuesday the dinner was poor; Wednesday you feared his mother would be angry; Thursday you wanted a walk with me; yesterday you dismissed him. Today I mean that he shall stay.”

“Already, my dear!” Camille said with cutting irony. The marquise blushed. “Stay, Monsieur du Guenic,” Camille commanded.

Beatrix became cold and contradictory, epigrammatic and almost rude to Calyste, whom Felicite sent home to play mouche with Charlotte. Calyste, absorbed with Beatrix, paid little heed to Camille’s craft. She noted that Beatrix had written to Conti only once that week—a symptom she reported to Calyste. All his life concentrated on the daily moment he could see the marquise; that drop of water intensified his thirst. He read through nights and brought home armfuls of books. His mother, alarmed, implored him to leave Les Touches; he refused. He was about to break his promise and speak to Beatrix to read her eyes; he resolved to approach her in the garden one morning.

Mademoiselle des Touches and the marquise had walked the marshes and returned to the garden. Camille proposed taking Calyste on a trip to Croisic, praising the young man’s angelic soul and pure heart. She spoke of beauty as Nature’s ensign. Beatrix laughed but seemed pensive; Camille’s references masked deep pain.

“Ah, my dear; but you are happy,” Beatrix said, laying her hand on Camille’s arm.

“Yes, happy indeed!” Camille replied with savage bitterness.

They rested on a bench. Camille rose: “I will go hasten breakfast.”

Calyste slid into the garden path and, meeting Beatrix, could not restrain a quiver.

“Madame, in what way did I displease you yesterday?” he asked.

“But you have neither pleased me nor displeased me,” she said gently.

“Am I so indifferent to you?” he asked, tears in his eyes.

“Ought we not to be indifferent to each other?” she replied. “Have we not, each, another, and a binding attachment?”

“Oh!” Calyste cried, “if you mean Camille, I did love her, but I love her no longer.”

“Then why are you shut up together every morning?” she said, smiling. “I don’t suppose Camille prefers her cigar to you, or you your admiration for female authors.”

“Calyste!” Camille cried, appearing and interrupting him. She took his arm and drew him away. “Calyste, is this what you promised me?”

Beatrix heard and was stupefied by his assertion; she was not as strong as Claude Vignon. Camille’s part—shocking yet grand—was one of those female sacrifices that break hearts; it begins an abnegation that may lift to heaven or plunge to hell.

At breakfast the marquise resolved to stifle rising love; she was gently indifferent to Calyste, a course that tortured him. Felicite proposed an excursion to Batz and Croisic; Calyste brightened, but Beatrix refused.

“My position is delicate; I do not wish to compromise—my happiness,” she said, glancing at the young Breton. “Conti is coming back to fetch me.”

Calyste turned pale. Despite Camille’s urgings and his own entreaties, Beatrix remained inflexible, showing the obstinacy Camille had warned of. Calyste left Les Touches in a depression of love that threatened madness, pondering decisive means to explain himself to Beatrix.

When Calyste returned home he shut himself in his room until dinner and then continued writing. His mother, worried by his seclusion, found him amid crumpled papers composing a letter to Beatrix, moved by a new distrust of Camille after the marquise’s demeanor in their recent encounter.

Calyste’s first love letter poured out intense feeling. He told Beatrix his love had swelled since meeting her, exceeding any dream. Though he lamented having nothing new to say about her beauty, he insisted on a profound connection and claimed to understand her deeply. He acknowledged his youth and naivety, contrasting himself with ordinary lovers and likening himself to an insect drawn to her light. Feeling unworthy, he questioned why his ardent devotion failed to warm her, seeing her disdain overshadow his worship. He contrasted his past attachment to Felicite with his present truth: Beatrix.

He admitted that Beatrix had unintentionally disturbed his happiness yet expected no return of affection. Reflecting on love and recalling Camille’s lesson that true love must grow and endure, he believed that if Beatrix loved him they could rise above the fears that had gripped Camille. He implored her for kindness and recognition, likening his love to a martyr’s sacrifice, and asked her to treat him as a friend without fear, to let him accompany her outings, and to answer him. Much of the letter repeated these emotional declarations. His mother, reading it, was moved to tears—alarmed by the rawness of feeling in a love letter she had never seen from her son.

Torn about sending it, Calyste sought advice from the Chevalier du Halga on delivering the note discreetly. The chevalier suggested slipping it to Beatrix’s maid, but Calyste chose to give it to Beatrix directly. He hurried to Les Touches, found Beatrix and Camille together, and impulsively handed the letter to Beatrix, who accepted it without hesitation.

After a restless night, Calyste awaited her response. When her letter arrived, he read it in the garden. Beatrix acknowledged his noble feelings but reminded him he was still a child. She affirmed her commitment to Camille, doubting her own suitability as a partner and warning that accepting his love would be an insult. Candid about her flaws and jealous tendencies, she said she could not provide the happiness Camille offered and would likely bring Calyste unhappiness. She urged him to seek a young girl worthy of his love who could fulfill his dreams.

The baroness, moved, approached Calyste to understand his feelings and read Beatrix’s reply aloud—admiring Beatrix’s nobility while worrying about her son’s future. She urged him to consider marrying Charlotte de Kergarouet to secure the estate and their prospects. Calyste, however, remained steadfast for Beatrix and dismissed his mother’s advice. He withdrew to write a response, reaffirming his unwavering love and commitment.

Troubled by his passion, the baroness sought the Chevalier du Halga’s counsel. As she walked through Guerande, her presence stirred curiosity; she found the chevalier and asked his view. Amused by the young man’s infatuation, he offered lighthearted remarks on love and gallantry.

Returning home, the baroness discovered another letter from Calyste that revealed the depth of his obsession. He declared his happiness tied solely to Beatrix, rejecting marriage to anyone else, and even fantasized about escaping with her, convinced their love could defy society’s constraints. Alarmed by the intensity of his feelings, the baroness prayed for his well-being. When Calyste returned, she expressed her concern and disclosed that she had read his letter. He reassured her of his love, and they planned a boat trip to Croisic the next day, hoping to see Beatrix.

Calyste’s letters showed a maturity and clarity that surprised the baroness; they exposed both the depth of his emotion and the potential consequences of his infatuation.

One of the greatest pleasures for small minds is deceiving a greater soul, and Beatrix recognized her inferiority to Camille Maupin, not only in talent but in passion. As Calyste rushed to Les Touches, thrilled by first love, Beatrix enjoyed being the object of his affection while refusing to reciprocate. She imagined it noble to suppress her feelings, modeling herself on Camille’s devotion and sacrificing for a friend. Flattered by French vanities, she approved of resisting Calyste’s allure.

The two women lounged in the salon amid flowers and a gentle breeze, their inner turmoil at odds with the serene outdoors. Camille, aware of the upheaval she had caused, felt compelled to hide her secrets from Beatrix, whom she regarded as a calculating rival. To soothe her heartache she sought solace in nature, finding meaning beyond personal pain. Calyste had become a guardian angel to her, elevating her thoughts, yet Beatrix’s triumphant air disturbed her.

When Calyste arrived with a letter, Camille observed his interaction with Beatrix and sensed an unspoken understanding: Beatrix now treated Calyste as hers, while he seemed guilty and transparent. He announced plans for an excursion; Camille, anxious, urged preparations, suspecting a conspiracy that left her discomposed.

After Calyste left to arrange the boat, Camille’s fears grew. On his return, neither woman met his eye; he was puzzled and troubled. At home the weight of despair replaced his earlier joy; his mother noticed but he dismissed her concern.

Beatrix wrestled with feelings stirred by Calyste’s letter—emotions she had not known before. She wondered what he and Camille were doing together, suspecting manipulation by Camille. This realization filled her with jealousy and anger; she paced her room in turmoil.

At dinner the atmosphere was tense. Camille tried to avoid Calyste’s presence, dreading a confrontation. The meal passed with veiled insults and a battle of glances, which erupted afterward.

Beatrix accused Camille of trickery, calling her actions cruel and manipulative. Camille, unshaken, returned observations about Beatrix’s motives and character. The exchange escalated, Beatrix lamenting what she saw as Camille’s lack of femininity and compassion.

As the argument intensified, Camille confessed her deep feelings for Calyste; Beatrix declared she could not accept a relationship that would burden him. Wanting to protect Calyste from her flawed existence, Beatrix insisted she would not be his mistress if she could not be his wife. The declaration wounded Camille and intensified their rivalry.

The confrontation ended with Camille declaring their friendship over and saying she would write to Conti, Beatrix’s former lover. Beatrix, feeling cornered, refused to back down. The night’s emotional turmoil left both women reflective, each grappling with desires and consequences.

Ultimately, the passions the conflict stirred settled into a precarious balance as both navigated their feelings for Calyste and the societal expectations that constrained them.

It was late August, the sky clear and the sea shimmering like molten silver. The marshes were busy with paludiers harvesting salt, dressed in white against the sun. The baron and baroness admired the scene from the jetty, the sea’s rhythmic waves a soothing backdrop.

The baron said he wanted to see the marshes one last time, prompting a paludier’s joke about the Guenic family’s mortality. At that moment the party from Les Touches arrived: the marquise leading, followed by Calyste and Camille. The baroness felt a strong aversion to Beatrix, who, though attractively dressed, seemed heartless to her.

Calyste made introductions, and the baron noted Camille’s significant ownership of the marshes; a paludier praised her character. The two groups parted, the baron remarking on Mademoiselle des Touches’ youthful appearance, while the baroness worried about Calyste’s attachment to Beatrix.

They boarded a boat to Croisic, where the marquise remained cold. Camille had scolded Calyste for disobedience; he was torn between anger and love for Beatrix. The passage was silent and tense as they neared the port, where barefoot women unloaded salt.

The Croisic peninsula featured fantastical granite rocks sculpted by wind and water into unique formations. The area was a natural wonder, with a hollow basin used for bathing and a rare box bush clinging to a distant promontory—a testament to nature’s resilience.

Calyste led Beatrix to a viewpoint while Camille sought solitude among the rocks, dressed for agility. She reflected on her life and the choices ahead, contemplating her future and the character of her love for Calyste. Meanwhile Calyste and Beatrix spoke of the ocean’s grandeur, which turned into a deeper discussion of feelings.

Beatrix confessed an inner desolation and insisted she could never be with Calyste despite loving him. She feared the consequences of abandoning her present life and the pain of unfulfilled love. Calyste, desperate to prove his devotion, offered to sacrifice everything, but Beatrix rejected sacrifice, reaffirming her commitment to her current situation.

At the rock where the box bush grew, Calyste’s emotions overwhelmed him; a moment of physical intimacy caused Beatrix to slip and fall. Calyste rushed to save her; in the ensuing confusion their feelings intensified. Gasselin and Camille arrived to help, and Beatrix, though hurt, was taken to a farmhouse for care.

Calyste stayed by her side, despairing yet hopeful for her recovery. The doctor assured them she would heal, and Calyste took solace in remaining near her. Beatrix, moved by his devotion, began to soften, recognizing the depth of his feeling.

Playing the invalid, Beatrix enjoyed Calyste’s adoration, which contrasted with her previous experiences. She relished the power she held over him while he remained largely oblivious to any manipulation. Their interactions were tender, yet she kept a distance, reminding him of her commitments.

Despite her initial resistance, Beatrix found herself drawn to Calyste’s steadfast affection. She disclosed past struggles, and he vowed to love her exclusively, offering the understanding she had longed for. Their bond deepened, and Beatrix began to see Calyste as a source of happiness.

As they prepared to return to the rock where she had fallen, Beatrix acknowledged the joy he brought her but reiterated her obligation to another man. Calyste, full of hope, believed their love could overcome her past.

However, back at Les Touches they encountered Conti, Beatrix’s former lover, shattering the moment. The ensuing tension suggested the complexities of love, loyalty, and consequence, leaving their futures uncertain.

The marquise’s inner turmoil was clear when Conti arrived unexpectedly, changing the group’s dynamic. Conti offered his arm to Beatrix, forcing her to abandon Calyste; he felt humiliated and conflicted. Though he trusted her love, Calyste was struck by her visible distress and fell silent.

Turning to Camille for insight, Calyste learned the power dynamics: Beatrix could not refuse Conti because of lingering ties from their past. Camille warned him to watch Conti’s manipulative nature, predicting he would use insinuations to regain control.

Conti’s return followed a failed opera, and he seemed to exploit the moment. He feigned indifference to Beatrix’s new love while subtly undermining her confidence. At dinner his jovial manner masked intent; veiled comments about women’s loyalty stung Beatrix and Camille.

Afterward Beatrix confided in Camille about feeling powerless against Conti’s influence, even blaming Camille for his premature return. Their talk turned bitter, revealing deep rivalry: Beatrix insisted she’d always hold a place in Calyste’s heart, and Camille retorted sardonically about the fleeting nature of Beatrix’s reign.

Meanwhile Conti and Calyste engaged in a tense conversation. Conti claimed to have lost interest and cast himself as the victim, urging Calyste to pursue Beatrix and suggesting her pride would drive her to revenge. Unaware of Conti’s motives, Calyste disclosed his recent time with Beatrix, feeding Conti’s jealousy.

That evening Conti’s salon performance was a calculated display meant to provoke Beatrix and assert dominance. Entranced by Conti’s singing, Calyste approached Beatrix with newfound confidence, not seeing the trap.

The next morning Beatrix left with Conti, leaving Calyste devastated. Camille informed him; he sank into grief, unable to process the betrayal. Despite Camille’s attempts to expose Beatrix’s true nature, Calyste clung to his belief in her love.

Days became weeks as his sorrow deepened. He wandered aimlessly, haunted by memories, while his family mistook his melancholy for a passing phase, blind to the slow consumption of his heartache.

Calyste’s health declined as he withdrew, alarming his family. They tried to distract him with plans for marriage to Charlotte, but his heart remained with Beatrix. He ultimately confessed his love, rejected Charlotte’s advances, and pleaded with his family to let Beatrix go.

As his condition worsened, doctors could not diagnose his ailment. The baron, fearing for the family’s legacy, grew desperate. Under pressure Calyste promised his father he would marry, yet his heart stayed tethered to Beatrix.

The baron’s death marked a grim turning point. Calyste became more despairing; despite his vow to marry, he slipped toward hopelessness. A chance encounter with Camille, however, sparked a flicker of hope: she offered to help him find Beatrix in Paris.

With renewed determination Calyste agreed to accompany Camille to the city, believing that reuniting with Beatrix might restore his spirit. Their journey began a new chapter as they left the shadows of the past to seek a brighter future.

Felicite’s love was shaping a prosperous future for Calyste. She wrote to the Duchesse de Grandlieu about him, outlining his past and her intentions. After selling her house for two million five hundred thousand francs, she bought another for seven hundred thousand and planned to use one million to recover the du Guenic estates, settling the remainder on Sabine de Grandlieu. Aware of the duke and duchess’s plans for their five daughters — only the youngest, Sabine, being marriageable — Felicite intended to redirect Calyste’s affections from Beatrix to Sabine.

During the journey to Paris, Mademoiselle des Touches confided in the baroness, who prepared the new house for Sabine and Calyste. They were invited to stay at the hôtel de Grandlieu, where the baroness received due respect for her lineage. Mademoiselle des Touches encouraged Calyste to explore Paris while she sought news of Beatrix, who was traveling abroad. The city’s vibrancy began to distract Calyste from his former obsession. Noticing a resemblance between Beatrix and the engaging Sabine, he was drawn to the latter. By the winter of 1837, Calyste, refreshed, listened to his mother’s marriage proposal for Sabine, though he concealed any indifference.

On the day of the marriage contract signing, Calyste unexpectedly refused the benefits offered by Mademoiselle des Touches, causing confusion. Sabine entered carrying a letter from Camille Maupin, which Calyste read privately. In it, Camille professed love for Calyste and declared her intention to enter a convent, urging him to accept her gifts for his future welfare. She implored him to remember her kindly and to attend to his duties as husband and father, emphasizing duty over passion.

After reading the letter, Calyste rejoined the group and the contract was signed. The marriage ceremony took place the following week at Saint-Thomas-d’Aquin; Calyste and Sabine departed for Brittany amid tears and congratulations. The duchess and Clotilde worried for Sabine, aware Calyste did not marry entirely of his own will.

They understood marriage as complex, requiring compatibility and harmony: a venture women faced with greater stakes than men. The Baronne du Guenic, with only one son, was tasked with looking after Sabine.

As the newlyweds traveled, they felt awkward, each conscious of the importance of their first moments together. Calyste suggested Sabine be happy in her own way and proposed they treat each other as friends and siblings. This struck Sabine as ill-fitting to romantic expectations and left her pensive. Their conversation turned to mundane topics, and Sabine pressed him about his past love for Beatrix, which he reluctantly recounted.

Sabine was hurt to learn their marriage resulted from Calyste’s rejection of another woman, yet she found solace in his assurances that he would forget Beatrix. Their relationship began to evolve; Sabine noticed a gradual shift as they grew closer.

In letters to her mother, Sabine described Brittany, her longing for home, and the challenges of her new life. She acknowledged Calyste’s sorrow about the marriage and her own difficulty loving him fully, feeling simultaneously in love and affected by his past. Despite her mother’s counsel to preserve dignity and self-respect, Sabine became devoted to Calyste, experiencing a mixture of happiness and vulnerability.

Settling into their new life, Sabine found joy in her role as châtelaine, adored by Calyste’s family and tenants. She described the warmth of their welcome and the deep-rooted Breton traditions that contrasted with her Parisian upbringing. The couple signed contracts to reclaim their estates, and Sabine felt fulfillment in her responsibilities.

Visiting Mademoiselle des Touches in a convent, Sabine received counsel on preserving marital happiness. Mademoiselle des Touches advised sagely but sharply: be capricious when needed, and ensure Calyste never again sees Beatrix, warning that reviving old passions could ruin their marriage. Sabine felt conflicted; she could not feign caprice or oppose her true feelings and recognized her vulnerability.

Sabine’s letters trace her navigation of love and marriage: devotion mingled with insecurity, the struggle to reconcile affection with her husband’s lingering ties to another woman, and the challenge of balancing societal expectations with personal identity. The narrative captures the delicate interplay of love, duty, and selfhood within marriage, showing how careful management of emotion and circumstance is required to make such unions endure.

Guerande, July 1838.

To Madame la Duchesse de Grandlieu:

Ah, my dear mother! After three months, I know what jealousy is. My heart is torn between love and hatred; I feel betrayed and unloved. How fortunate I am to have you to confide in!

There’s nothing like being told, “Here’s a key to your palace; go everywhere, but avoid Les Touches!” to set a wife’s curiosity alight. Mademoiselle des Touches has sown bitterness in me. Why forbid me Les Touches? What happiness can hinge on avoiding a mere house in Brittany? My curiosity forced me to ask, “What sort of place is Les Touches?”

“Les Touches belongs to you,” my mother-in-law said.

“If Calyste had never set foot in Les Touches!” my aunt Zephirine exclaimed.

“He would not be my husband,” I replied.

“Then you know what happened there?” my mother-in-law asked slyly.

“It is a place of perdition!” Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel declared. “Mademoiselle des Touches committed many sins there.”

“But they saved her soul and enriched a convent,” the Chevalier du Halga interjected. “The Abbe Grimont told me she donated a hundred thousand francs to the nuns.”

“Would you like to visit Les Touches?” my mother-in-law asked.

“No, no!” I hastily replied.

This little scene was like a page from a diabolical drama. My husband suggested we go when I timidly probed him: “What are you afraid of at Les Touches, that you never mention it?”

“Let’s go there,” he answered.

So we went. Les Touches was enchanting and profoundly artistic—poisonous flowers can be charming; the devil has flowers too. I watched Calyste closely, testing whether his passion for Beatrix was truly past. He seemed thoughtful and uneasy when I mentioned Madame de Rochefide; he begged me not to revive that passion. Yet his silence only fed my suspicion. I left Les Touches, vowing never to return.

The depth of my hatred for Madame de Rochefide only revealed my deep love for Calyste. Calyste had revived his past emotions at Les Touches, and they outweighed our joys. Please, dear mother, find out if Madame de Rochefide is in Paris; if she is, I will stay in Brittany. Poor Mademoiselle des Touches might regret her role in our marriage if she knew how much I am haunted by our odious rival! I am ashamed of my jealousy; sometimes I long to flee Guerande.

August 25th.

I decided to live in the ruins of the old château. Calyste, worried by my restlessness, agreed to take me. Either he suspected nothing, or he did and did not love me. I trembled at the thought of confirming my worst fears, yet I was not loved as I loved: Calyste is charming, to be sure; but who, receiving so much affection from a pure young wife, would not be amiable?

Guenic, September 18.

Has he forgotten her? That solitary thought echoes in my soul. Only innocent young men should marry pure young girls; but that’s a deceptive Utopia. I am happy yet terrified of losing my happiness. For five months I have thought only of one thing—Calyste. Tell Clotilde her melancholy wisdom often recurs to me. A thousand tendernesses, dear mother. If my terrors are not delusions, Camille Maupin has sold me her fortune dearly.

These letters reveal the private unease behind a marriage outwardly acceptable. The restoration and furnishing of the hotel du Guenic were overseen by the architect Grindot under Clotilde and the Duc and Duchesse de Grandlieu; the couple planned to return to Paris in December 1838. Sabine settled into rue de Bourbon, eager to be judged by family and society. Calyste, indifferent, allowed Clotilde and the duchess to guide him; he gained his place by name, fortune, and alliance. Sabine, famed for her charm, found the social season restorative. Her pregnancy braced this neutral union; in October 1839 she had a son and, mistaken about nursing, decided to breastfeed him herself.

By August 1840 Sabine was nearly done nursing. Calyste, in two years in Paris, shed his earlier innocence and mingled with young libertines—Duc Georges de Maufrigneuse, Vicomte Savinien de Portenduere—who taught him the pleasures of idleness. A wife’s indulgence often supports such dissipations; a wife is proud to see her husband return after granting him freedom.

One October evening, to escape their newly weaned child’s cries, Sabine urged Calyste to go to the Variétés. He spotted Madame de Rochefide in a proscenium box—Beatrix in Paris, after three years. The shock struck him like an arrow. Beatrix represented light and the Unknown; Sabine embodied duty and dullness. Calyste’s instinct was to leave; he approached the open box door and found Beatrix between Canalis and Raoul Nathan. She had changed—gaunt, pale, yet poetically affected—Parisian artifice had brightened her ruins.

“Why did you marry?” she whispered. “With such a little fool!”

Canalis and Nathan withdrew. Beatrix poured out a long lament about lost love, keeping Calyste’s hand, urging him not to stay lest his wife notice. Her perfume and the memory of Les Touches overwhelmed him. He left the theatre with the sensation that Beatrix had stirred his soul in ways Sabine never had.

At home, wealth seemed poor compared to Beatrix. Sabine, devoted, scanned her husband’s face and behavior for signs of infidelity. Calyste, torn, feared the scrutiny of a wife who knows every quiver of his muscles. He left the house the next morning, ostensibly to escape her watchfulness, and wandered Paris. Nature, desire, and vanity—each pull explainable only in part—drove him toward Beatrix.

He went to the rue de Chartres and, after a moment’s hesitation at the door, was admitted. Beatrix, living in a modest house with a small garden and a reduced household, had adapted to a frugal luxury: velvet walls, gilt furniture, watercolors of her former wanderings, a silver cup attributed to Cellini. She received him in a studied déshabillé, scented as at Les Touches. The perfume, the setting, and her remembered beauty turned Calyste’s head. She insisted on a friendship’s dignity, refusing to be treated as a mistress, yet did not deny herself to any man she truly loved; she also reproached him for his duty as a husband and father.

Three hours passed in argument and seduction. Beatrix belittled Sabine as a sensible, plain Grandlieu, urging Calyste to admit his true love. He nearly left at six; she taunted him kindly, then had him write a note to his wife. When Calyste wrote, her cold question—“‘My dear’? Can you really say that your wife is still dear to you?”—shocked him. She insisted he go dine with his wife; then, with a flash of triumph, she made him sign a note falsely stating he was dining elsewhere. She sent it by messenger. Calyste left, elated yet compromised.

He returned home about two in the morning. Sabine had waited till half-past twelve and then, exhausted, gone to bed, though distressed by the terse note. The next morning, the child better, Sabine presented their son to Calyste before breakfast with natural motherly charm, which temporarily steadied him. But Sabine’s intuition nagged. At breakfast she asked about the previous night.

“Portenduere kept me to dinner,” Calyste lied, adding they played whist at the club.

Sabine reproached the idle life of young noblemen, then noted that his first note to her had been written on a woman’s paper with a coronet. Calyste, a novice at deceit, hurried off to visit Portenduere and then rushed back to rue de Chartres to learn how Beatrix had passed the night. He found her refreshed, witty, and charming at breakfast. He lingered until three; his horse returned drenched in sweat—Sabine, at a window, saw the condition of the horse and asked where he had been.

Meeting him on the stairs, she greeted him warmly. In the parlor she caught sight of him in the mirror and saw traces of something she could not name. He confessed he had a surprise: a dressing-table being finished for her with contributions from his mother and aunt. She embraced him ecstatically; the moment was sublime. But when she inhaled his cravat she fainted—the perfume of other paper, another woman’s presence, had been there. She cried for a doctor; Calyste rushed out to fetch him, while she fell into a feverish panic, convinced she was betrayed. She wrote, in a delirium, to her mother, full of gratitude for the dressing-table but unable to quell the terrible storm in her soul.

The Vicomtesse de Portenduere arrived and, seeing Sabine’s state, promised secrecy and left to consult the duchess. The duchess, cold but shrewd, arrived with Dommanget, the accoucheur, in time. She reassured Sabine that Madame de Rochefide was not in Paris and quickly invented a lie: Calyste had lost a lot of money at cards the night before. She burned the coroneted paper and explained the rest to Dommanget, who treated Sabine’s symptoms as the consequences of nervous collapse from overnursing and worry. Calyste, guilty and easily moved, shed tears that deceived Sabine. The physician’s invented story—that Calyste had lost thirty thousand francs gambling—was accepted; the duchess had repaired the blunder, at least temporarily.

Two days later, Sabine seemed out of danger. Calyste, foolhardy and in love, went to Madame de Rochefide to boast:

“Beatrix,” he said, “you owe me happiness. I have sacrificed my poor little wife to you; she nearly died. That fatal paper bore your name and coronet—though the ‘B’ was accidentally effaced. Your perfume and my lies betrayed my happiness. Sabine’s milk turned; erysipelas set in; she may bear the marks for life.”

Beatrix listened with a hard, icy face.

“So much the better,” she said. “Perhaps it will whiten her for you.”

She replied with bitter sarcasm. There is no greater mistake than for a man to speak of his virtuous wife to his mistress; Calyste lacked the Parisian education of deceit. He could neither lie to his wife properly nor be candid with his mistress. He begged Beatrix’s pardon for hours; she refused, maintaining the dignity of her rank and dignity. She declared she loved his soul and had higher standards, attacking his appearance and virtues with bitter flattery disguised as contempt. Her method was deliberate: to make him feel repulsive, to provoke him to prove his power by conquering her disgust. For conquering temperaments, being resisted renews the triumph of conquest. Beatrix combined this contempt with constant comparisons between her poetic, comfortable home and the hotel du Guenic, belittling bourgeois Faubourg Saint-Germain luxury.

She had deliberately transformed herself into a new incarnation at each passion, refining the arts of a courtesan. Fallen from Conti’s favor and proud of her name, she sought at least the fame that unprincipled conduct affords. The misfortune of the rich, beautiful, virtuous Grandlieu would be her pedestal. Thus she demanded that Calyste swear hatred for his wife and take up the role she deemed fitting for him, while she deployed every artifice—poetry, disdain, calculated emotion—to bind him to her and humiliate the rival who threatened her claims.

When a woman returns to ordinary life after nursing her first child, she often seems rejuvenated and charming, bringing freshness to young mothers. But when Sabine comes back to her husband Calyste, she finds he is not the man she married. Instead of security she feels anxiety and looks for certainty about their relationship. That certainty arrives in a letter from her mother hinting at Calyste's discontent and the presence of another woman, Beatrix.

Sabine's heart sinks as she reads the vague note, which reveals nothing explicit about Calyste's feelings. In despair she scrawls a comment on the letter to signal her awareness and leaves it where Calyste will find it. He discovers the note but ignores it, throwing the paper into the fire. Sabine is left in agony, convinced she has displeased him and humiliated her idol.

As days pass, her torment deepens. Beatrix, emboldened, writes to Calyste; Sabine, recognizing the perfume, realizes the extent of her rival's influence. In a moment of madness she dances with her child, seeking solace in motherhood's innocence, yet her heart remains heavy and she weeps alone when Calyste leaves to read Beatrix's message.

Her suffering grows with each attempt to regain Calyste's affection. She fills their home with flowers to please him, but his indifference only deepens her despair. Obsessed with understanding his feelings and comparing herself to Beatrix, she becomes trapped in jealousy and self-doubt. Efforts to charm him by cooking and tending her appearance prove futile as Calyste stays unresponsive.

Despite her struggles, Sabine's love persists. She strives to embody the devoted wife's virtues, but these efforts bring disappointment. One evening, after failing to move him, she succumbs to despair and tears off her beautiful dress in frustration.

Over time Sabine's health declines under the weight of grief. The duchess, her mother, notices and tries to draw her away from the source of suffering. Sabine stays silent, fearing interference in her marriage. Eventually she confides in her sister, telling of her anguish and asking that her child be cared for if she should die.

Vulnerable, Sabine warns her sister Athenais about love's dangers, advising caution in marriage. She reflects that her virtues have become obstacles to happiness, lamenting that straightforwardness and passion have failed to win Calyste's heart. Instead she feels pushed toward the duplicity she believes necessary to retain his affection.

Concerned, the duchess consults Abbe Brossette, a respected priest, revealing a plan to intervene in Calyste's attachment to Beatrix and restore her daughter's happiness. The abbe cautions against mixing spiritual authority with worldly intrigue but recognizes the seriousness of Sabine's plight.

The duchess, weighing his advice, becomes determined to act for her daughter's sake, even if that means resorting to morally questionable measures. She resolves to seek a solution that will restore peace to the family and spare Sabine further suffering.

A storm was brewing for Monsieur de Rochefide, who was enjoying Parisian life as fully as he had with Beatrix and thought such a satisfactory existence nearly impossible to disrupt. To see how society treats men and women differently in similar situations, we follow Rochefide's life after Beatrix left—a tale that might encourage women to stay home and defend their dignity, as Sabine du Guenic did.

After Beatrix's departure, Arthur de Rochefide, now an only child after his sister’s death, became sole owner of the hôtel de Rochefide and inherited a large fortune. His income reached a thousand francs a day, ample for his happiness. While Beatrix fulfilled maternal duties, Rochefide enjoyed his wealth without extravagance. His vanity, aided by good looks, let him dismiss women and appropriate others' wit. His military background lent a jaunty tone to his conversation, making him seem witty to acquaintances.

As a husband he was pitied; society blamed Beatrix for leaving such a “good” man. He joined clubs and championed fashionable causes to distinguish himself. His main indulgence was racehorses, run by an English groom, costing him a sizable monthly sum. Despite wealth, he lacked taste and intellect, relying largely on inherited fortune. After the downsides of marriage he welcomed bachelorhood, claiming he was "born with a caul," destined for luck.

Rochefide's indifference to domestic life was clear: he rarely dined or slept at home. After several affairs he married Madame Schontz, a woman of dubious reputation known in circles around Fanny Beaupré and others. That world—where many women ended in poverty or early death—required a nuanced grasp of social dynamics. Madame Schontz, born La Petite-Aurélie, emerged from a difficult upbringing: orphaned and educated at Saint-Denis, a school that schooled girls but offered few prospects.

Aurélie, daughter of Colonel Schiltz, led a tumultuous life of various relationships, which brought her by chance to Rochefide at Valentino’s. Captivated by her charm, Rochefide married her two years after Beatrix left, and society did not condemn him for the choice.

Rochefide's relationship with Madame Schontz unfolded in four phases. The first was courtship: he provided a modest allowance and a furnished apartment. As he learned her character he found her noble and charming; she revealed talents he admired, though ambition soon surfaced and she sought a more comfortable life.

In the second phase Madame Schontz moved to a better apartment; Rochefide lavished gifts and support. She ran their household efficiently, impressing him with budgeting and superior dinners. This domestic competence convinced Rochefide she loved him for himself, since she rebuffed wealthier suitors.

The third phase cast Madame Schontz as mother figure to Rochefide's son, overseeing his education and finances. She advised Rochefide on investments, producing significant gains she did not claim. Taking on more responsibilities, she became integral—managing affairs and ensuring his comfort.

In the fourth phase she established herself socially, curating her guest list and preserving reputation. She amassed a small fortune through savings and investments, supplementing it by wearing and selling jewelry. Despite financial success she downplayed her wealth, framing expenses as necessary to maintain her lifestyle.

Through these phases Madame Schontz demonstrated tactical superiority, transforming the relationship into mutual benefit while skillfully navigating their social world.

Such conduct implied a plan, and Madame Schontz had one. Jealous of Madame du Bruel for two years, she was determined to marry in both church and civil ceremonies. This required a second Arthur, but Rochefide’s secret rival remained unknown. Bixiou suspected Leon de Lora; the painter thought Bixiou himself was favored. Victor de Vernisset, a poet, and Stidmann, a young sculptor, were also considered. None truly understood Madame Schontz, whose focus was solely on securing a good marriage.

The only man of questionable reputation in her salon was Couture, a stock-gambler who had fallen from grace. Despite his failures he remained loyal, and when he introduced her to a provincial, Fabien du Ronceret, Madame Schontz began to consider him. Fabien, son of a chief justice, had left a judgeship in Alencon for Paris with a modest income and a desire to exploit changing social mores.

The modern era encouraged pride, vanity, and ambition; people sought recognition at any cost. Fabien, not particularly clever, grasped this shift and aimed to make a name for himself by being talked about. He pursued attention through extravagant living and connections.

His Paris debut was marked by ostentation: a lavish apartment and a greenhouse intended as a spectacle. He sought association with influential figures and entry into Madame Schontz’s circle. After failed attempts through established acquaintances, he turned to Couture, who recognized Fabien’s potential and recommended him to Madame Schontz.

Madame Schontz quickly assessed Fabien and saw a suitable candidate for her ambitions. She flattered him, leading him to suppose he was the object of her affection. Fabien, eager to impress, presented himself in his finest attire, captivated by her charm. A month before a significant meeting, Madame Schontz revealed her true identity and aims to Fabien, who failed to grasp her intentions.

Realizing his misunderstanding, she took Fabien for a carriage ride, expressing her desire to marry a man of ambition who could appreciate her character. Despite her wealth and encouragement for him to rise, Fabien hesitated at the prospect of marriage, and she then turned her attention to Couture, who quickly proposed upon learning of her fortune.

Meanwhile the Duchesse de Grandlieu sought the Comte Maxime de Trailles’s help to manage a delicate family situation. Maxime, a seasoned political operator, understood the motivations behind the duchess’s invitation and agreed to assist, but insisted on conditions, including the duchess’s acceptance of his future wife into her social circle.

Maxime’s plan included removing a rival from the picture, requiring the duchess to follow his instructions closely. He acknowledged the emotional entanglements—especially Calyste’s deep infatuation with Béatrix—and recognized the difficulty of manipulating such feelings. His grasp of love and manipulation revealed the complexities of the relationships as he prepared to execute his scheme.

As the duchess agreed to support Maxime’s wife, he assured her he would handle the intricacies, albeit with a hint of moral ambiguity. Their conversation underscored the lengths people would go to secure desires, often at the expense of others’ feelings and reputations.

The next day Rochefide informed Madame Schontz of Maxime’s impending visit, prompting her to ready her house and appearance to impress him. Maxime’s reputation as a powerful figure in Parisian society preceded him, and his presence was both a privilege and a challenge for Madame Schontz as she navigated the complexities of her ambitions and relationships.

As events progressed, characters navigated conflicting desires and ambitions, prompting confrontations and revelations. Beatrix’s internal conflict deepened and her relationship with La Palferine grew more complicated as she struggled with identity and social expectations. Ultimately the narrative examines love, betrayal, and the complexity of human relationships, where ambition and affection produce disillusionment and force characters to question past choices and future paths.
