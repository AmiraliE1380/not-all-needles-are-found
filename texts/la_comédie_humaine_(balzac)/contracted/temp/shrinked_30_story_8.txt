In September 1835 Clémentine du Rouvre, the marquis’s only daughter, married Comte Adam Laginski, a Polish exile. Her father had squandered his fortune; her mother’s settlements supplied them. Adam, from an illustrious Polish family but holding no public charge, faced Parisian prejudice: Polish nobles were scorned and often mistaken for exiled students, and the émigré split between republican and noble exiles made him a local joke.

They bought a house on rue de la Pépinière in 1834; by 1836 Adam was established in society—its mixed styles and fine décor reflecting bourgeois taste. Clémentine noted Captain Paz, a discreet factotum who managed finances and stables; Adam said Paz, from a poor branch and a Polish veteran, had refused a generous gift—proof of loyalty and pride. As they prepared to go out, Clémentine’s affection for both men suggested personal ties could transcend social norms.

Thaddeus, long a recluse in Paris, was seen riding with Comtesse Clémentine Laginska, who insisted he dine with her and Adam. He dressed for the occasion but comported himself like a subordinate—answering little, deferring to Clémentine, and prompting Adam’s urgings to be livelier. Outwardly dull, he was painfully aware of his love for Clémentine and tried to hide it.

At the Opera he remained distant; Clémentine felt slighted and Adam excused his disagreeableness. His reserve came from unrequited love, sharpened after her return from a trip. He took a bittersweet pleasure in being indispensable to their household—knowing it would fail without him even as it caused him private suffering.

Clémentine, newly confident and intent on her Parisian standing, had little room for him. Once, seeing him while driving home, she impulsively invited him along—an offer that flustered him. Their exchanges were charged with unspoken feeling: he felt gratitude and longing yet kept his distance. He found solace in managing their affairs and reflected on the depth and pain of his attachment.

When Clémentine hosted a reception his absence passed unnoticed. He retreated to solitary habits, content in silent devotion yet acutely aware that his love was both joy and torment. As Clémentine sought attention—distracted by rivals and the burdens of status—she increasingly neglected Adam. Thaddeus remained a steady presence, quietly overseeing things while wrestling with his feelings.

In May, preparing to visit her uncle’s estate, she asked him to join for dinner. Their conversation hinted at something deeper, but loyalty to Adam restrained him. Afterward she admitted dissatisfaction and a desire for more passion. Torn between love and friendship, Thaddeus suppressed confession and invented a romance with Malaga, a circus performer he supported and visited. Malaga’s despairing letter yearning for him underscored the constancy of his devotion to Clémentine and the sacrifices it demanded.

Clementine returned home more beautiful than before. After dinner the count and countess spoke of Thaddeus, who had left a letter from Malaga with Adam. Adam lamented Thaddeus’s misfortune and feared that association with a circus performer would damage his reputation; Clementine openly scorned such women. Adam defended Thaddeus, citing the sacrifices he had made for Malaga.

Paz, who had asked Adam for money to help Malaga, was seen by the countess as a threat to Adam’s stability. Though anxious, Adam trusted that Thaddeus would abandon Malaga if it suited him; the countess doubted this.

Paz felt Clementine’s chill and sank into despair, yet took quiet pleasure in admiring her at gatherings and devoted himself to her comfort and the household. His love ran deep; feeling unworthy he fell ill from anguish before recovering.

After Adam lost heavily gambling, he asked Thaddeus to cover the loss by blaming Malaga. Thaddeus reluctantly agreed, burdened by obligations. When Clementine learned of his generosity toward Paz she protested and questioned his loyalty. Overwhelmed by her expectations and the strain of managing her affairs, Thaddeus resolved to teach her household management so she could be independent.

In spring Adam fell gravely ill. Thaddeus nursed him devotedly while Clementine sought the best care. Thanks to Thaddeus’s devotion Adam slowly improved and praised him. Tormented by unrequited love and fearing Paris would prolong his pain, Thaddeus left for the Caucasus after writing Clementine a heartfelt letter confessing his feelings and sacrifices. Clementine and Adam never fully grasped his devotion; years later she wondered about his fate, his memory a bittersweet, unfulfilled love.
