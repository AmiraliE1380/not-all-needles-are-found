In September 1835 Mademoiselle du Rouvre, only daughter of the Marquis du Rouvre, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a Polish exile. Though the Marquis had squandered much of his fortune, Clémentine was a substantial heiress through her mother’s family; settlements ensured her a steady annual income after marriage.

Adam, from an illustrious Polish family, was not a charge on the French government but suffered Parisian prejudice: Polish nobility was poorly regarded, and many assumed he was merely a student enjoying Paris after political setbacks. The émigré Polish community in France was split between republican and noble exiles, and Adam initially became the butt of local humor.

In 1834 Adam had bought a house in the rue de la Pépinière, and by 1836—after falling in love with Clémentine and marrying—he was established in Parisian society. Their home, a lavish mix of architectural styles and fine decor, reflected the tastes and rising bourgeoisie.

Clémentine soon noticed Captain Paz, a discreet factotum who managed their affairs—from finances to the stables—yet remained largely invisible socially. Adam explained Paz came from a poor branch of his family, had fought for Poland, and had become a close friend through shared hardship. Paz’s loyalty, efficiency, and pride—shown when he declined a generous gift—revealed a bond of equality and mutual respect. As the couple prepared to go out, Clémentine’s affection for both men grew, illustrating how personal ties could transcend social norms.

Thaddeus lived reclusively in Paris until the Comtesse Clémentine Laginska was seen riding with him in the Bois de Boulogne. She insisted he dine with them; he dressed for the occasion but behaved like a subordinate, answering little while listening to Clémentine. Adam urged him to be more lively. Though outwardly dull, Thaddeus was acutely aware of his feelings for Clémentine and struggled to hide them.

At the Opera he remained distant; Clémentine felt slighted and Adam excused Thaddeus as disagreeable. His reserve came from unrequited love, intensified after Clémentine’s return from a trip. He derived a bittersweet joy from being indispensable to their household, knowing it would falter without him even as he suffered inwardly.

Clémentine, newly confident and focused on her Parisian standing, had little room for him, though once she noticed him while driving home and invited him along—an invitation that flustered him. Their interactions were charged with unspoken emotion: Thaddeus felt gratitude and longing yet kept his distance. Routine soon resumed; he found solace in managing their affairs while reflecting on the depth and pain of his love.

When Clémentine hosted a reception, Thaddeus’s absence went unnoticed. He retreated into solitary habits, content in silent devotion but deeply aware his love was both joy and suffering. He admired her from afar, cherishing small moments of her happiness. Clémentine’s life was a constant struggle for attention; distracted by rivals and the strain of maintaining status, she increasingly neglected Adam. Thaddeus remained a steadfast presence, quietly overseeing things while wrestling with his feelings.

In May, as Clémentine prepared to visit her uncle’s estate, she saw Thaddeus again and insisted he join them for dinner. He hesitated but tried to engage more despite shyness and duty. As the evening progressed, her interest in him grew; their conversation hinted at a deeper connection and he felt momentarily emboldened, yet loyalty to Adam restrained him.

After dinner Clémentine voiced dissatisfaction with Adam and a desire for more passion. Thaddeus, torn between love for her and friendship with Adam, felt the urge to confess but held back. To deflect his true feelings he invented a romance with Malaga, a circus performer—an exotic, simple foil to society. Visiting Malaga became an escape: he provided for her and attended to her comforts without a real relationship. Misunderstandings followed as Malaga questioned his intentions, but he stayed her benefactor while his heart remained haunted by Clémentine.

Ultimately Malaga sent a despairing letter longing for his companionship. It struck him, underlining the constancy of his devotion to Clémentine as he continued to navigate love, loyalty, and sacrifice.

Clementine returned home more beautiful than ever. After dinner the count and countess discussed Thaddeus, who had left a letter from Malaga with Adam. Adam regretted Thaddeus’s misfortune, fearing he would lose status through association with a circus performer; Clementine scorned such women, while Adam defended Thaddeus, citing his financial sacrifices for Malaga.

Paz, who had asked Adam for money to help Malaga, was seen by the countess as a threat to Adam’s stability. Although worried, Adam believed Thaddeus would abandon Malaga if it suited him; the countess doubted it.

Soon Paz noticed a change in Clementine’s manner toward him and felt her contempt, which plunged him into despair. Still, he took quiet pleasure in admiring her at gatherings and devoted himself to her comfort and the household. His love was deep, but he felt unworthy and fell ill from anguish before recovering.

Adam lost a large sum gambling and asked Thaddeus to cover it by blaming Malaga. Thaddeus reluctantly agreed, burdened by obligations. When Clementine discovered his generosity toward Paz, she expressed frustration and questioned his loyalty. Feeling overwhelmed by her expectations and by managing her affairs, Thaddeus resolved to teach her household management so she could be independent.

In spring Adam fell gravely ill. Thaddeus nursed him devotedly while Clementine sought the best medical care. Through his attention Adam was kept alive; he slowly improved and praised Thaddeus. Tormented by unrequited love and convinced staying in Paris would prolong his pain, Thaddeus decided to leave for the Caucasus. Before departing he wrote Clementine a heartfelt letter confessing his feelings and sacrifices. Clementine and Adam failed to grasp the full depth of his devotion. Years later Clementine wondered about Thaddeus’s fate; his memory remained a bittersweet, unfulfilled love.
