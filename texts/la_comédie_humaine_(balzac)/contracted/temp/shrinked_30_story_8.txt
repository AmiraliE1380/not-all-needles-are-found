In 1835 Clémentine du Rouvre, daughter of the Marquis du Rouvre, married Comte Adam Laginski, a Polish exile. Her father had squandered his fortune, but settlements from her mother provided a steady income. Adam, from an illustrious Polish family and self-sufficient, faced Parisian prejudice: Polish nobility was poorly regarded and often mistaken for exiles. The émigré community was split, and he was at first mocked. After buying a house, Adam was established in Parisian society; their home reflected rising bourgeois taste. Clémentine noticed Captain Paz, a discreet factotum who ran the household and stables. Adam said Paz came from a poor branch, had fought for Poland, and was a friend forged by hardship. Paz's refusal of a gift revealed loyalty and pride. Clémentine’s affection for both men grew, suggesting personal ties could transcend social norms.

Thaddeus lived reclusively in Paris until the Comtesse Clémentine Laginska was seen riding with him in the Bois de Boulogne. She insisted he dine with them; he dressed for the occasion but behaved like a subordinate, answering little and deferring to Clémentine. Adam urged him to be more lively. Outwardly dull, Thaddeus was acutely aware of his feelings for Clémentine and struggled to conceal them.

At the Opera he remained distant; Clémentine felt slighted and Adam excused Thaddeus as disagreeable. His reserve sprang from unrequited love, sharpened after Clémentine returned from a trip. He took a bittersweet pleasure in being indispensable to their household, conscious the household would falter without him even as his love made him suffer.

Clémentine, newly confident and intent on her Parisian standing, had little room for him. Once, noticing him while driving home, she impulsively invited him along—an invitation that flustered him. Their interactions were charged with unspoken emotion: he felt gratitude and longing but kept his distance. Routine soon resumed; he found solace in managing their affairs and reflecting on the depth and pain of his devotion.

When Clémentine hosted a reception, Thaddeus’s absence went unnoticed. He retreated into solitude, content in silent worship yet painfully aware his love was both joy and torment. He admired her from afar, cherishing small moments of her happiness. Clémentine, preoccupied with rivals and the strain of maintaining status, increasingly neglected Adam. Thaddeus remained a steadfast, quiet overseer while privately wrestling with his feelings.

In May, as Clémentine prepared to visit her uncle’s estate, she saw Thaddeus again and insisted he join them for dinner. He hesitated but tried to engage despite shyness and duty; her interest grew and conversation hinted at a deeper connection, though loyalty to Adam restrained him. After dinner she voiced dissatisfaction with Adam and a desire for passion. Torn between love and friendship, Thaddeus felt urged to confess but held back. To deflect, he invented a romance with Malaga, a circus performer; visiting her became an escape—he provided for her without a true relationship. When Malaga sent a despairing letter longing for him, it struck him, underscoring the constancy of his devotion to Clémentine and the sacrifices it demanded.

Clementine returned home more beautiful than ever. After dinner the count and countess discussed Thaddeus, who had left a letter from Malaga with Adam. Adam regretted Thaddeus’s misfortune, fearing that association with a circus performer would harm his standing; Clementine scorned such women, while Adam defended Thaddeus for the financial sacrifices he had made for Malaga.

Paz, who had asked Adam for money to help Malaga, was seen by the countess as a threat to Adam’s stability. Adam believed Thaddeus would abandon Malaga if it suited him; the countess doubted it.

Paz noticed a change in Clementine’s manner and felt her contempt, which plunged him into despair. Still he took quiet pleasure in admiring her at gatherings and devoted himself to her comfort and the household. His love was deep; feeling unworthy, he fell ill from anguish before recovering.

Adam lost a large sum gambling and asked Thaddeus to cover it by blaming Malaga. Thaddeus reluctantly agreed, burdened by obligations. When Clementine discovered his generosity toward Paz, she complained about his loyalty. Overwhelmed by her expectations and by managing her affairs, Thaddeus resolved to teach her household management so she could be independent.

In spring Adam fell gravely ill. Thaddeus nursed him devotedly while Clementine sought the best medical care. Adam slowly improved and praised Thaddeus. Tormented by unrequited love and convinced remaining in Paris would only prolong his pain, Thaddeus left for the Caucasus after writing Clementine a heartfelt letter confessing his feelings and sacrifices. Clementine and Adam never fully grasped his devotion; years later she wondered about his fate, his memory a bittersweet, unfulfilled love.
