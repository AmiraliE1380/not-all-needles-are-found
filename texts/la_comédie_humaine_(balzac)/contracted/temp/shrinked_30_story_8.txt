In 1835 Clémentine married Polish exile Comte Adam Laginski; settlements offset her father's losses. Facing Parisian prejudice, buying a house gave him bourgeois standing. She noticed Paz, the factotum. Adam said Paz was of poor origin and had fought for Poland; he refused a gift, showing pride and loyalty. She grew fond of both, suggesting ties transcended norms.

Thaddeus, long reclusive in Paris, became visible when Comtesse Clémentine Laginska was seen riding with him in the Bois de Boulogne. Invited to dine with her household, he dressed formally but behaved like a subordinate—reserved, answering little, deferring to Clémentine. Adam urged him to be livelier, unaware that Thaddeus’s coolness hid a deep, unrequited love intensified after Clémentine’s return from a trip. He took a bittersweet pleasure in being indispensable to their household, knowing they would falter without him even as his devotion caused him pain.

Clémentine, newly confident and absorbed in her Paris position, left little room for him. On an impulsive drive home she invited him along, a moment that flustered him and revealed charged, unspoken emotion: gratitude and longing met by deliberate distance. Routine resumed; he found solace in managing affairs and in silent worship, admiring small signs of her happiness while feeling the torment of his love. At a reception his absence went unnoticed; Clémentine, preoccupied with rivals and status, increasingly neglected Adam, while Thaddeus remained a steadfast, quiet overseer privately wrestling with his feelings.

In May, preparing to visit her uncle’s estate, she saw Thaddeus again and insisted he join them for dinner. He hesitated but engaged; their talk hinted at a deeper connection, yet loyalty to Adam restrained him. When she confessed a desire for passion he nearly replied in kind but held back, inventing a romance with Malaga, a circus performer. Visiting Malaga became an escape—he provided for her without love. Her despairing letter longing for him finally pierced him, underscoring the constancy and sacrifice of his devotion to Clémentine.

Clementine returned home more beautiful than ever. After dinner the count and countess discussed Thaddeus, who had left a letter from Malaga with Adam. Adam regretted Thaddeus’s misfortune, fearing that association with a circus performer would harm his standing; Clementine scorned such women, while Adam defended Thaddeus for the financial sacrifices he had made for Malaga.

Paz, who had asked Adam for money to help Malaga, was seen by the countess as a threat to Adam’s stability. Adam suspected Thaddeus might abandon Malaga if it suited him; the countess doubted it.

Paz noticed Clementine’s changed manner and felt her contempt, sinking into despair. Still he took quiet pleasure in admiring her at gatherings and devoted himself to her comfort and the household. His love was so deep he fell ill from anguish before recovering.

After Adam lost heavily gambling, he asked Thaddeus to cover the debt by blaming Malaga. Thaddeus reluctantly complied, burdened by obligations. When Clementine learned of his generosity to Paz she complained about his loyalty. Overwhelmed by expectations, Thaddeus resolved to teach her household management so she could be independent.

In spring Adam fell gravely ill. Thaddeus nursed him devotedly while Clementine sought the best medical care. Adam recovered and praised Thaddeus. Tormented by unrequited love and sure that staying in Paris would only prolong his pain, Thaddeus left for the Caucasus after writing Clementine a heartfelt letter confessing his sacrifices. Clementine and Adam never fully grasped his devotion; years later she wondered about his fate, a bittersweet, unfulfilled memory.
