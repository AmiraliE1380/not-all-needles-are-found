There are houses in some provincial towns whose aspect brings a melancholy like that of sombre cloisters or ruins. Within them silence and barrenness prevail; life seems stagnant until a pale face peers from a window at an unaccustomed step.

Such sadness marked a dwelling in Saumur at the upper end of the steep street leading to the chateau. This street—now little frequented, hot in summer, cold in winter, and dark in places—is notable for the resonance of its pebbled pavement, its narrow tortuous roadway, and the quiet of houses overtopped by the ramparts. Many are three centuries old, built of wood, their ends carved into fantastic figures, blackened by time. Slate-covered beams trace a bluish line along frail walls; rotten shingles bend beneath the years. Blackened window-sills with faint sculpture barely bear the clay pots of poor working-women. Doors studded with enormous nails show domestic hieroglyphics of a lost meaning: here a Protestant attested his belief, there a Leaguer cursed Henry IV., elsewhere a bourgeois carved the insignia of his trade—the whole history of France is written there.

Next to a tottering artisan’s house rose a country gentleman’s mansion, its stone arch still bearing battered armorial bearings. In this hilly street the ground-floors are neither shops nor warehouses; they preserve the naive simplicity of an older age. These low rooms, without show-windows, are deep, dark, and unadorned. Their doors open in two parts; the upper half is fastened back, the lower half swings continually. Air and light reach the damp den through the upper door or an open space beneath a low front wall closed each evening by solid shutters and heavy iron bars.

This wall serves as a counter. No delusive display: only samples—tubs of cod and salt, bundles of sail-cloth, cordage, copper wire, iron hoops, a few cloth pieces on shelves. Enter; a neat girl in a white kerchief drops her knitting and calls her parents, who sell phlegmatically, civilly, or arrogantly. A cooper may sit twirling his thumbs, seeming to own little beyond a few boat-ribs and laths, yet his wood-yard below supplies the cooperage trade of Anjou. A hot season makes him rich, a rainy one ruins him; puncheons worth eleven francs may fall to six in a morning.

As in Touraine, atmospheric vicissitudes control commerce. Wine-growers, proprietors, wood-merchants, coopers, inn-keepers, mariners—all watch the sun. They dread frost, rain, wind, drought; a perpetual duel goes on between the heavens and terrestrial interests. The barometer smooths, saddens, or brightens their countenances. From end to end of this street, formerly the Grand’Rue de Saumur, men call: “Here’s golden weather,” or “It rains louis,” knowing what sun or rain will bring them.

On fine Saturdays these traders, with vineyards and provisions provided, close shop and spend two days in the country. Purchases and profits arranged, they have hours for pleasure, observation, criticism, and spying. A housewife cannot buy a partridge without neighbors asking if it was cooked right; a girl is seen by idling groups if she peeps at a window. Consciences are held in the light; the dark, silent houses hide no mysteries. Life is mostly in the open air; every household sits at its threshold to breakfast, dine, and quarrel. No one passes without being examined; formerly a stranger entering a provincial town was bantered from door to door—the origin of many good stories and the nickname copieux for Angers folk who excelled in such urban sarcasm.

The ancient mansions of Saumur’s old town are at the top of this street, once occupied by neighborhood nobility. The melancholy dwelling where the following events occur is one of these venerable relics. Follow the picturesque thoroughfare and you will find the dark recess where the door of Monsieur Grandet’s house is hidden. To explain the provincial expression—the house of Monsieur Grandet—we must give his biography.

Monsieur Grandet enjoyed a reputation in Saumur whose causes and effects must be understood provincially. In 1789—still called by some le Pere Grandet—he was a master-cooper, able to read, write, and cipher. When the Republic sold church property in the arrondissement, the forty-year-old cooper had just married the daughter of a rich wood-merchant. With about two thousand louis-d’or—his own fortune and his wife’s dowry—and help from two hundred double louis given by his father-in-law to the surly republican presiding over sales, Grandet bought for a song one of the finest vineyards in the arrondissement, an old abbey, and several farms. The inhabitants, so little revolutionary, thought Pere Grandet bold and patriotic; in truth his thought was open only to vineyards. Appointed to the administration of Saumur, his pacific influence showed itself politically and commercially: he protected the ci-devant nobles and prevented, where possible, the sale of emigres’ lands; he furnished the Republican armies with thousands of puncheons of white wine and took payment in splendid fields reserved for sale.

Under the Consulate Grandet became mayor and reaped better pickings. Under the Empire he was called Monsieur Grandet; Napoleon superseded him in office but Grandet left without regret. He had built roads to his properties; his house and lands, assessed advantageously, paid moderate taxes; and his vineyards, through his care, became the “head of the country,” a local term for the finest yielders. He might well have asked for the Legion of Honor.

In 1806 Monsieur Grandet was fifty-seven, his wife thirty-six, and an only daughter, the fruit of their love, was ten. That year he inherited three fortunes—those of Madame de la Gaudiniere, née de la Bertelliere, of old Monsieur de la Bertelliere (her grandfather), and of Madame Gentillet, her grandmother—a hoard amassed by avarice. Old Monsieur de la Bertelliere watched his gold more than any other interest. The inhabitants estimated his savings according to “the revenues of the sun’s wealth,” as they said.

Monsieur Grandet thus obtained a modern sort of nobility. He became the most imposing personage in the arrondissement. He farmed a hundred acres of vineyard, which in good years yielded seven or eight hundred hogsheads of wine; he owned thirteen farms, an old abbey whose windows he had walled to save expense, one hundred and twenty-seven acres of meadow with three thousand poplars planted in 1793, and the house in which he lived. This was his visible estate; as to other property, only two men could hazard a guess at its value: Monsieur Cruchot, a notary who handled Grandet’s usurious investments, and Monsieur des Grassins, Saumur’s richest banker, in whose profits Grandet had a secret share.

Though Cruchot and des Grassins were discreet, their public respect for Grandet led observers to estimate his wealth by their obsequiousness. No one in Saumur doubted Monsieur Grandet had a private treasure, a hiding-place of louis he nightly gazed upon. Avaricious people saw proof in his eyes, which seemed tinged by yellow metal. The glance of a man accustomed to draw enormous interest acquires furtive, eager, mysterious movements—an unspoken language of passion. Grandet inspired respect as one who owed no man anything: skillful cooper and experienced wine-grower, he could tell with precision whether to make a thousand puncheons or five hundred; he never failed in speculation and always had casks when casks were dear. He could store his vintage and sell at two hundred francs when small proprietors took five louis. His famous 1811 vintage, judiciously stored and slowly sold, brought him over two hundred and forty thousand francs.

Financially, Grandet was part tiger, part boa-constrictor. He could lie in wait, spring on his prey, swallow masses of louis, and rest like a snake digesting—impassible, methodical, and cold. No one passed without admiration mingled with fear: had not many felt the rending of those polished steel claws? For one man Cruchot procured money at eleven per cent; for another des Grassins discounted bills at frightful deductions. Few days passed without Grandet’s name in market talk. To some, his fortune was an object of patriotic pride. Innkeepers and merchants would tell strangers: “We have two or three millionaire establishments; but as for Monsieur Grandet, he does not himself know how much he is worth.”

In 1816 Saumur’s best reckoners put his landed property near four millions; having made on average a hundred thousand francs a year from 1793 to 1817, it was fair to presume he held cash nearly equal to his estate. After a game of boston or talk on vines, people said: “Le Pere Grandet? le Pere Grandet must have at least five or six millions.”

“You are cleverer than I; I have never been able to find out the amount,” Cruchot or des Grassins would answer when overheard.

If some Parisian mentioned Rothschild or Lafitte, Saumur folk asked if he were as rich as Monsieur Grandet. If the Parisian affirmed, they shook their heads incredulously. Such a fortune explained all his actions. Early peculiarities that once provoked ridicule had long died away. His slightest actions, like the blinking of his eyes, were law to the countryside, where men had studied him like a naturalist studies instinct.

“It will be a hard winter,” said one; “Pere Grandet has put on his fur gloves.”

“Pere Grandet is buying quantities of staves; there will be plenty of wine this year.”

Monsieur Grandet never bought bread or meat: his farmers supplied capons, chickens, eggs, butter, and his tithe of wheat. He owned a mill; the tenant took grain as part of rent and returned flour and bran. La Grande Nanon, his only servant, baked bread every Saturday. Grandet arranged with kitchen-gardeners, his tenants, to supply vegetables. He sold most of his fruits in the market. His firewood came from hedgerows and half-rotten sheds whose planks farmers carted to town, cut, and stacked in his wood-house, receiving his thanks. His only known expenses were consecrated bread, clothing for his wife and daughter, hire of their chairs in church, La Grande Nanon’s wages, tinning saucepans, lights, taxes, repairs, and industrial costs. He had six hundred acres of woodland recently purchased, which he put under a neighbor’s care for a promised indemnity. After acquiring this, he ate game for the first time.

Monsieur Grandet’s manners were simple. He spoke little, in short sententious phrases in a soft voice. Early on he stuttered when required to speak at length; this incoherence, attributed to defects of education, was really assumed and will be explained later. Four brief sentences sufficed him: “I don’t know; I cannot; I will not; I will see about it.” He never said yes or no, and never committed himself to writing. If people talked, he listened coldly, chin in his right hand, right elbow resting on the left, forming opinions he never changed. He reflected long before any business agreement; when his opponent confidently revealed purposes, Grandet answered: “I can decide nothing without consulting my wife.” His wife, reduced to helpless slavery, was a useful screen in business. He went nowhere among friends; he neither gave nor accepted dinners; he made no stir, economizing even in motion. He never disturbed others’ things out of respect for property, yet at home coarse language and habits of his nature surfaced where he controlled himself less.

Physically, Grandet was five feet high, thick-set, square-built, with calves twelve inches in circumference, knotted knees, and broad shoulders; his face was round, tanned, pitted by small-pox; his chin straight, lips uncurved, teeth white; his eyes had the calm, devouring expression of a basilisk; his forehead showed transverse wrinkles and significant protuberances; his yellow-gray hair was mockingly called silver and gold by young jesters. His nose, thick at the end, bore a veined wen the common people said was full of malice. His whole countenance showed dangerous cunning, an integrity without warmth, and the egotism of a man long used to concentrate every feeling on avarice’s enjoyments and upon the only human being who mattered to him—his daughter and sole heiress, Eugenie. Attitude, manners, bearing, all testified to the self-belief habit of success gives.

Thus, though outwardly unctuous, Monsieur Grandet’s nature was iron. His dress never varied since 1791: stout shoes tied with thongs, thick woollen stockings, short breeches of coarse maroon cloth with silver buckles, a velvet waistcoat of yellow and puce stripes, a large maroon coat with wide flaps, a black cravat, and a quaker’s hat. His gloves, thick as a gendarme’s, lasted twenty months; to preserve them he always laid them on his hat in one particular spot. Saumur knew nothing further.

Only six persons had the right of entrance to Grandet’s house. The most important of the first three was a nephew of Monsieur Cruchot. Since his appointment as president of the Civil courts of Saumur this young man had added Bonfons to Cruchot, signing himself C. de Bonfons. Any litigant so ill-advised as to call him Monsieur Cruchot would soon feel his folly in court. The magistrate protected those who called him Monsieur le president, but favored with smiles those who addressed him as Monsieur de Bonfons. Monsieur le president was thirty-three, owned Bonfons worth seven thousand francs a year; he expected to inherit his uncle the notary’s property and that of another uncle, Abbe Cruchot, a dignitary of Saint-Martin de Tours. These three Cruchots, backed by cousins and allies, formed a party like the Medici.

Madame des Grassins, mother of a son of twenty-three, came assiduously to play cards with Madame Grandet, hoping to marry her Adolphe to Mademoiselle Eugenie. Monsieur des Grassins promoted his wife’s schemes by services constantly rendered to the old miser, arriving in time upon the field of battle. The three des Grassins had adherents and cousins. On the Cruchot side, the abbe—the family Talleyrand—backed by his brother the notary, sharply contested every inch with his female adversary and tried to obtain the heiress for his nephew the president.

This secret warfare for Eugenie’s hand kept Saumur agitated. Would Mademoiselle Grandet marry Monsieur le president or Monsieur Adolphe des Grassins? Some said Monsieur Grandet would give his daughter to neither. The old cooper, eaten with ambition, was said to seek a peer of France, to whom an income of three hundred thousand francs would make the Grandet casks acceptable. Others replied the des Grassins were nobles and rich; Adolphe was personable; unless the old man had a papal nephew at his beck, such an alliance should satisfy a man who came from nothing—whom Saumur remembered with an adze in his hand and the bonnet rouge. Certain wise heads noted Cruchot de Bonfons had the right of entry at all times whereas his rival was received only on Sundays; others trusted Madame des Grassins’ intimacy with the women. “It is diamond cut diamond,” said a Saumur wit.

The oldest, wiser inhabitants declared the Grandets knew better than to let the property go out of the family, and that Mademoiselle Eugenie Grandet of Saumur would marry the son of Monsieur Grandet of Paris, a wealthy wholesale wine-merchant. To this the Cruchotines and Grassinists replied: the two brothers had seen each other only twice in thirty years; Monsieur Grandet of Paris, mayor and deputy and colonel of the National Guard, disowned the Saumur Grandets and meant to ally with some ducal family favored by Napoleon. In short, nothing was not said of the heiress, talked of for fifty miles and even on conveyances from Angers to Blois.

At the beginning of 1811, the Cruchotines won an advantage. The estate of Froidfond, notable for its park, mansion, farms, streams, ponds, and forests, worth about three millions, was put up for sale by the young Marquis de Froidfond, who had to liquidate. Maitre Cruchot, the president, and the abbe, aided by adherents, prevented sale in small lots. The notary concluded a bargain for the whole property, payable in gold, persuading the marquis that suits against purchasers of small lots would delay collection; it was better to sell the whole to Monsieur Grandet, solvent and able to pay in ready money. The marquisate of Froidfond was accordingly conveyed down the gullet of Monsieur Grandet, who, to Saumur’s astonishment, paid for it, under proper discount, with the usual formalities.

This affair echoed from Nantes to Orleans. Monsieur Grandet took advantage of a cart returning by way of Froidfond to see his chateau. After casting a master’s eye over the property, he returned to Saumur, satisfied he had invested at five per cent, and seized by the stupendous thought of extending the marquisate by concentrating his property there. Then, to fill his coffers, now nearly empty, he resolved to thin out his woods and sell the poplars in the meadows.

One now understands what “the house of Monsieur Grandet” meant: that cold, silent, pale dwelling above the town, sheltered by ruined ramparts. The porte-cochere’s two pillars and arch were hewn from soft Loire tufa, its weathered holes giving the entrance a vermiculated, almost jail-like aspect. Above the arch a bas-relief of the four seasons, already crumbling and blackened, supported a projecting plinth where weeds—pellitory, bindweed, nettles, and even a little cherry-tree—had taken root.

The oak door beneath was brown, shrunken, split, fastened with iron bolts in symmetrical patterns; a small square grated panel, its bars red with rust, served as the knocker’s aperture. Through this grating one could see, at the far end of the dark vault, a few broken steps leading to a garden shut in by thick damp walls nourished with sickly herbage—the ramparts under which several neighboring gardens spread.

The principal room on the ground floor was a large hall entered directly from that vault. In towns of Anjou, Touraine, and Berry a hall is everything—antechamber, salon, office, boudoir, dining-room—the theatre of domestic life. There the barber came twice a year to cut Monsieur Grandet’s hair; there farmers, the cure, the under-prefect, and the miller’s boy came on business. The hall, with two street-facing windows, was entirely of wood: gray panels with ancient mouldings, exposed beams painted gray with the spaces between washed white and browned with age. An old brass clock with arabesques adorned the mantel of the ill-cut white stone chimney-piece; above hung a greenish mirror whose bevel reflected a thread of light along a gothic steel frame. Two copper-gilt candelabra, their side branches removable to make single candlesticks, stood on bluish marble pedestals. Antique chairs were covered with faded La Fontaine tapestries; colors and darning had blurred the scenes.

At the hall’s four corners were closets and buffets with dirty shelves. A marquetry card-table topped by a chessboard stood between the windows; above it an oval barometer, its gilding scoured by flies, looked as if it might yield its gilt at any moment. Opposite the chimney-piece were two pastel portraits said to represent Madame Grandet’s grandfather as a lieutenant in the French guard, and the deceased Madame Gentillet as a shepherdess. The windows were draped with red gros de Tours curtains, a luxury little in keeping with Monsieur Grandet’s habits but included in the purchase of the house with the pier-glass, tapestries, and rosewood buffets.

By the window nearest the door stood a straw chair on castors for Madame Grandet to see passers-by; a stained cherry-wood work-table filled the embrasure and Eugenie’s little armchair stood beside it. Here mother and daughter had lived peaceably for fifteen years in a round of labor from April to November; on November 1 they took their winter station by the chimney. Grandet permitted a fire only from November 1 to March 31, regardless of weather. A foot-warmer filled with embers saved mother and daughter from chill in April and October. They managed the family linen and daily tasks; if Eugenie wanted to embroider a collar she stole time from sleep and had to deceive her father to get light. For a long time the miser rationed tallow candles to his daughter and la Grande Nanon as he did bread and other necessities.

La Grande Nanon was perhaps the only person who accepted his despotism willingly. The town envied the Grandets for possessing her. She had lived with Monsieur Grandet thirty-five years and received sixty francs a year; by saving those sixty francs she had managed to invest four thousand francs in an annuity with Maître Cruchot. This seeming prosperity, the result of relentless thrift, made other servants jealous, who did not see the hard slavery behind it.

At twenty-two Nanon had been unable to find a situation because her face repelled employers—unjustly, for it would have suited a grenadier. Forced from a farm whose dwelling had burned, she came to Saumur robust and ready for work. Père Grandet, a cooper and a shrewd judge of strength, saw in her a workwoman shaped like a Hercules—firm on her feet, strong in the hips, square in the back, with cartman’s hands and unblemished honesty. He clothed and fed her, paid wages, and set her to work without cruelty. She attached herself gratefully, and from that day Grandet ruled and worked her with feudal authority.

Nanon did everything: she cooked, made lye, washed linen in the Loire and carried it home on her shoulders; she rose early, slept late, prepared food for the vine-dressers at harvest, watched market-people, protected her master’s property like a faithful dog, and obeyed his absurd exactions without murmur. In 1811, when the vintage had been especially difficult, Grandet resolved to give Nanon his old watch—the first present in twenty years; though he also turned over his old shoes, which were always worn out, to her. Necessity had made her stingy, and Grandet loved her as one loves a dog; she accepted his spiked collar of authority without complaint. If he cut bread with parsimony, she did not complain; she gladly shared the household’s severe regimen, and indeed no one was ever ill. Nanon laughed when Grandet laughed, felt gloomy or warm with him, and worked as he did. Pilfering fruit never occurred; any large yield was shared—“Come, fall-to, Nanon!” he would say when branches bent under fruit.

The cooper’s ambiguous laugh had been for her like a sunbeam. Her simple heart could hold only one feeling and one idea: gratitude to Père Grandet for having taken her from the street and set her to work. For thirty-five years she never ceased to see herself before his wood-yard barefoot, hearing him ask, “What do you want, young one?” His occasional pity—“Poor Nanon!”—was followed by a look from the servant that sealed a chain of devoted subjection. This compassion, cruel and pleasurable to the miser, was Nanon’s entire happiness. The townsfolk wondered how the Grandets had gained such devotion; she would go through fire and water for them.

Her kitchen—barred windows looking into the court—was always clean, neat, cold, a true miser’s kitchen where nothing went to waste. After locking up dinner remains and putting out her fire she spun hemp beside her masters; one tallow candle sufficed the family for the evening. She slept at the end of the passage in a closet lit by a fan-light; her robust health allowed it. She slept with one ear open like a watchdog, ready to rise.

Other parts of the house will be described as events require, but this sketch of the hall, where the household’s modest luxury appears, suggests the nakedness of the upper floors.

In 1819, on an evening in mid-November, la Grande Nanon lighted the fire for the first time that season. The autumn had been fine. This day was well known to the Cruchotines and the Grassinists: the six antagonists—Maitre Cruchot, the Abbe Cruchot, Monsieur C. de Bonfons, the des Grassins—prepared to meet at the Grandets and outdo one another in illustrated friendship. That morning everyone had seen Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet, accompanied by Nanon, on their way to Mass; it was Mademoiselle Eugenie’s birthday. Maitre Cruchot, the president, and Monsieur de Bonfons hurried to arrive before the des Grassins, each bringing enormous bouquets from their greenhouses; the president’s flowers were wound with white satin ribbon fringed with gold.

Monsieur Grandet, following the custom on Eugenie’s birthdays, went to her bedside and presented his paternal gift—regularly for thirteen years a gold piece. Madame Grandet gave her a winter or summer dress as needed. These gifts—two dresses and several gold pieces at birthdays, New Year’s, and her father’s fête—gave Eugenie a small revenue of about a hundred crowns. Grandet liked to see her amass this money; it was, in his mind, transferring coins from one strongbox to another and training his heiress’s thrift. He sometimes demanded an account of her treasure, saying, “It is to be your marriage dozen.”

The “marriage dozen” is an old custom still in parts of central France: at marriage a purse containing, according to means, twelve pieces, or twelve dozen, or twelve hundred pieces of gold is presented. Even the poorest bride receives some sort of dozen; the custom persisted in notable examples like Pope Clement VII.’s gift to Catherine de’ Medici.

During dinner, pleased to see Eugenie in a new gown, Grandet declared, “As it is Eugenie’s birthday let us have a fire; it will be a good omen.”

“Mademoiselle will be married this year, that’s certain,” said la Grande Nanon, carrying away the goose’s remains.

“I don’t see anyone suitable here,” Madame Grandet murmured, glancing timidly at her husband. Grandet, looking at his daughter, said gaily, “She is twenty-three to-day; we must soon begin to think of it.” Eugenie and her mother exchanged a knowing, silent glance.

Madame Grandet was a thin, dry woman, yellow as a quince—awkward, slow, made to be downtrodden. She had big bones, a big nose, a big forehead, big eyes; her mouth showed few blackened teeth. Yet she was a truly good woman: angelic sweetness, rare piety, an unalterable equanimity that made her pitied and respected. She had brought M. Grandet more than three hundred thousand francs through fortune and inheritances, yet felt humiliated by dependence and slavery and never asked for a penny. Grandet gave her no more than six francs at a time for personal expenses. When he sold his vintage each year he would stipulate “the wife’s pin-money,” the four or five louis visible as her annual revenue; but when he needed money he would sometimes ask, “Can you lend me a few sous?” and she, glad to help the man her confessor upheld as master, would return several crowns from that “pin-money.” When he handed Eugenie the monthly five-franc piece for small needs, he would ask, as he buttoned his pocket, “And you, mother, do you want anything?”

“My friend,” she would answer, moved by maternal dignity, “we will see about that later.” Such wasted dignity and submission fit the ironies of Providence.

After dinner Nanon fetched a bottle of black-currant ratafia from Monsieur Grandet’s bedroom and nearly stumbled on the steps.

“You great stupid!” her master said. “Are you going to tumble like other people?”

“It was that step which has given way,” she answered. Madame Grandet agreed it should have been mended; Eugenie had nearly twisted her ankle the day before. Grandet, mollified by the birthday and Nanon’s scare, filled a glass of ratafia for her and promised to mend the step. He went to the bakehouse for planks and tools, whistling songs of his youth as he worked; Nanon prepared to spin by the hearth so as not to intrude among guests.

At that moment the Cruchots arrived. Nanon opened the door and the hearth’s light let them find the way. The president, perfuming his bouquet with grandeur, kissed Eugenie’s neck with officious familiarity; the abbe kissed her hand. Maitre Cruchot, the notary, who never missed an opportunity, felt his courtship progress and hoped to be preferred to the des Grassins. Grandet removed the branches from the candelabra, fixed single tallow candles in their sockets, lit them with care, and sat down, looking alternately at friends, his daughter, and the two flames. Conversation ranged from how well fête-days were observed to inquiries about vintages.

“Have you sold your vintage?” asked de Bonfons.

“No; I shall keep it,” said the president, wishing to appear prudent and profitable.

“Is he driving some bargain?” Cruchot wondered. Grandet, whose pride swelled with talk of “all of them” when referring to his vintages, strutted about with the self-satisfaction of a man who calls his house his castle. Catching sight of Nanon by her candle in the passage he called her to join them: “Put out that fire and come sit with us. Are you not as good as the great people? They are descended from Adam, and so are you.”

While playfully parrying and boasting, the knocker announced the des Grassins. Madame des Grassins was a lively, plump little woman with pink-and-white skin, a fashion-setter in Saumur; her husband, formerly quartermaster in the Imperial Guard and wounded at Austerlitz, kept the air of an old soldier. They presented a rare Cape heather and a workbox with silver-gilt utensils from Paris; Adolphe des Grassins, a tall, blond, pale young man who had spent freely in Paris, kissed Eugenie and offered the gilt workbox monogrammed E.G. Eugenie, surprised and delighted, turned to her father as if for permission; Grandet coolly said, “Take it, my daughter.” The Cruchots felt humiliated as they perceived the bond forming between the heiress and Adolphe. Monsieur des Grassins offered snuff and the des Grassins’ ostentation irritated the notary and his circle.

Seating was arranged for loto at half-past eight; Madame des Grassins contrived to seat her son beside Eugenie. The party—two card-tables, bits of pasteboard colored and numbered, counters of blue glass—seemed trivial while each guest’s mind was on Grandet’s reputed millions. Grandet, inwardly self-conceited, watched Madame des Grassins’ feathers, the banker’s martial head, the faces of Adolphe, the president, the abbe, and the notary, thinking: “They are all after my money. Neither one nor the other shall have my daughter; but they are useful—useful as harpoons to fish with.”

This family gaiety in the old gray room, dimly lit by two tallow candles; the laughter, sincere only on Eugenie’s and her mother’s faces; the mingling of triviality and important stakes; the young girl, like certain birds sold because of their price, lured by proofs of friendship of which she was the dupe—altogether made the scene a melancholy comedy. It was a drama of all times: the figure of Grandet, playing his own game with false friendship and reaping profit, dominated the scene. The modern god, money, appeared in full force in his countenance. Tender sentiments existed but were secondary; only three pure hearts—Nanon’s, Eugenie’s, and her mother’s—were genuinely moved. Their ignorance of Grandet’s wealth made their feelings simple and innocent; in that ignorance lay their joy and their ruin.

At the moment Madame Grandet had just won a loto of sixteen sous—the largest ever pooled in that house—and while la Grande Nanon laughed, the knocker resounded on the door so loudly the women started.

“There is no man in Saumur who would knock like that,” said the notary.

“How can they bang in that way!” exclaimed Nanon. “Do they want to break in the door?”

“Who the devil is it?” cried Grandet.

Nanon took a candle and went to open the door, followed by her master.

“Grandet! Grandet!” cried his wife, alarmed, running to the room’s door.

The loto-players looked at one another.

“Suppose we all go?” said Monsieur des Grassins; “that knock seems ominous.”

A young man, accompanied by a coach-office porter with two trunks and a carpet-bag, soon appeared. Grandet turned sharply to his wife.

“Madame Grandet, go back to your loto; leave me to speak with monsieur.” Then he shut the door. The players resumed their seats but did not play.

“Is he from Saumur?” asked Madame des Grassins.

“No, a traveller,” answered Monsieur des Grassins.

“He must have come from Paris.”

“It’s nine o’clock; the Grand Bureau diligence is never late,” observed the notary.

“He looks young,” added the Abbe Cruchot.

“Nanon does not come back,” muttered Eugenie.

“It must be one of your relations,” remarked the president.

“Let us go on with our game,” Madame Grandet suggested softly. “I know from Monsieur Grandet’s tone that he is annoyed; perhaps he would not like us speaking of his affairs.”

Adolphe told his neighbor the newcomer was likely Eugenie’s cousin he had seen at the Baron de Nucingen’s ball. His mother scolded him to hold his tongue.

Grandet returned without la Grande Nanon; the porter’s steps echoed on the stairs. The traveller—whose arrival stirred all imaginations—came in, strikingly out of place among the provincial company.

“Sit near the fire,” Grandet said.

The young stranger saluted the company; the men inclined their heads, the women bowed. Madame Grandet offered that he must be cold; Grandet brusquely dismissed her, then Eugenie timidly offered something to warm him, met with her father’s curt remark that the visitor “has got a tongue.” The others were accustomed to the master’s despotism; the stranger, surprised, stood before the fire, lifted one foot to warm his boot, and thanked Eugenie, saying he had dined at Tours and was not tired.

“Has monsieur come from the capital?” asked Madame des Grassins.

The young man, called Monsieur Charles—Monsieur Grandet of Paris’s son—took an eye-glass and surveyed the room, ogling Madame des Grassins with easy impertinence. “Yes, madame. You are playing at loto, aunt. Do not let me interrupt you,” he said.

Madame des Grassins was sure he was the cousin and kept glancing between him and Eugenie, whose furtive looks at Charles betrayed melancholy forebodings. The company watched Charles with mixed curiosity and contempt; to their rustic eyes his Parisian elegance seemed absurd and yet impressive.

Charles Grandet, about twenty-two, displayed the air and trappings of a dandy: hunting-costume, best weapons, a variety of fine waistcoats and collars, a gold toilet-set from his mother, a pretty desk from a lady Annette, a little whip, and pistols—the whole apparatus of Parisian frivolity. He had travelled alone in the diligence, refreshed his curls in Tours, changed linen, put on a black satin cravat and round collar, a crossed cashmere waistcoat beneath a travelling great-coat, a short gold watch-chain, gray trousers with silk seams, gloves, and a cane with a chased gold knob. His airs were those of a young man confident in his charm.

The Cruchots, shabby and snuff-taking, with dingy shirts and twisted cravats, looked grotesque beside him. The Grassins, acquainted with fashion, cast satirical glances that seemed to say, “So this is Paris!” Yet Charles’s demeanor, with an eye-glass inspecting joists and fly-specks, made the loto-players regard him as a curiosity. Grandet, absorbed in a long letter, paid them no mind. Eugenie, unfamiliar with such perfection, breathed in the scent of his curls, desired the soft kid of his gloves, envied his small hands and refined features. To her, a girl who mended her father’s clothes, he seemed a seraphic being, an image from a keepsake. When he drew a handkerchief embroidered by a Scottish great lady, Eugenie watched to see if he would use it. His contemptuous glance at the coffer that had pleased her only enchanted her more. She dreamed of him.

The loto wound up slowly. La Grande Nanon entered and announced sheets for monsieur’s bed; Madame Grandet followed her out. Madame des Grassins suggested ending the game and each took two sous. The guests gathered around the fire.

“Have you finished your game?” Grandet asked without looking up.

“Yes, yes!” replied Madame des Grassins, seating herself near Charles.

Eugenie, moved by that young-girl impulse to care for a beloved one, left to help her mother and Nanon prepare the guest room. She was less concerned for her mother than eager to make her cousin’s room elegant. She found preparations undone and set about fixing them: insisting on a warming-pan between the sheets, covering a table with a cloth, persuading Nanon to change it daily and bring a pile of wood up the corridor without telling her father, and arranging small treasures on the chimney-piece—the lacquer tray from Monsieur de la Bertelliere, a crystal goblet, a gilt spoon, an antique flask. More ideas came to her in a quarter of an hour than ever before.

“Mamma, my cousin will never tolerate the smell of a tallow candle; shall we buy a wax one?” She produced a five-franc piece she had received for monthly expenses and urged Nanon to go. Madame Grandet protested: what would her husband say? Where would sugar come from? Nanon hesitated, aware of Grandet’s vigilance, but Eugenie, delighted at the first jest she had ever made, laughed and persuaded her. The girl’s tender, eager fluttering was a new spectacle in the austere house.

Meanwhile Madame des Grassins paid court to Charles. “You are very courageous to leave the pleasures of the capital and take up your abode in Saumur,” she cooed, suggesting the town could entertain him if he allowed it. She hinted that if he stayed too long with Monsieur Grandet, “what will become of you?”—painting the uncle as a sordid miser, the aunt as pious but dull, and the cousin Eugenie as “a little fool, without education,” destined to a life of darning towels. Charles, finding provincial flattery manageable, responded gracefully and began a light, confiding conversation with Madame des Grassins.

The notary and the president watched; the Abbe Cruchot slyly extolled Madame des Grassins’ ability to do Saumur’s honors for the young man. Adolphe reminded Charles of their meeting at a ball; Charles recognized him. The abbe, with a pinch of snuff, joked that in the provinces women of thirty could be as fresh as younger Parisian beauties and recalled the days when young men stood on chairs to see them dance, flattering Madame des Grassins. She, alert to the abbe’s insinuations, wondered what he suspected about her designs.

Charles unbuttoned his coat, put a hand into his waistcoat, and adopted the air of a dashing poet. Pere Grandet’s inattention—or the preoccupation induced by the letter he held—did not escape the notary and the president, who tried to read the miser’s face. Grandet maintained his habitual calm with difficulty as he folded and pocketed the letter. The contents, which he read by the candlelight, were momentous.

The letter, from Victor-Ange-Guillaume Grandet of Paris to his brother in Saumur, declared he could no longer survive the disgrace of bankruptcy. His broker and his notary had failed; he owed nearly four million francs while assets amounted to perhaps twenty-five percent of that sum. The wines in his warehouses had fallen in price because of the abundance and quality of the other Grandet’s vintage. Paris would soon cry him a knave; he could not endure the infamy. He bewailed the ruin he brought on his son, whom he idolized, and, foreseeing his end, begged his elder brother to be a father to Charles. He entreated that the boy be treated gently, not ripped at once from his idle life, and be given the means to try trade or seek fortune in the Indies. He confessed he had nothing to leave and begged his brother to obtain from Charles a renunciation of any right to his estate as the son’s inheritance. If the brother refused, he warned, remorse would follow; if he accepted, a dying man’s blessing would be theirs. The letter closed with a blessing and a dying father’s trust that his brother would accept guardianship.

“So you are talking?” said Pere Grandet, folding the letter and slipping it into his waistcoat with a humble, timid air that masked his feelings and calculations. “Have you warmed yourself?”

“Thoroughly, my dear uncle.”

“Well, where are the women?” Grandet asked, the letter’s heavy truths already sinking behind the businesslike mask. Eugenie and Madame Grandet returned.

“Is the room all ready?” Grandet inquired, striving to recover composure.

“Yes, father.”

“Well then, my nephew, if you are tired, Nanon shall show you your room. It isn’t a dandy’s room; but you will excuse a poor wine-grower who never has a penny to spare. Taxes swallow up everything.”

“We do not wish to intrude, Grandet,” said Monsieur des Grassins; “you may want to talk to your nephew, and therefore we will bid you good-night.”

The assembly rose and made their departures, each according to character. The notary offered his lantern and to escort the des Grassins. Madame des Grassins had not foreseen the evening’s premature end; her servant had not arrived.

“Will you do me the honor to take my arm, madame?” said the abbe.

“Thank you, monsieur l’abbe, but I have my son,” she replied.

“Ladies cannot compromise themselves with me,” said the abbe.

“Take Monsieur Cruchot’s arm,” said her husband. The abbe took the lady so quickly they soon outpaced the others.

“That is a good-looking young man, madame,” he said, pressing her arm. “Good-by to the grapes; the vintage is done. We may as well say adieu to Mademoiselle Grandet. Eugenie will belong to the dandy. Unless this cousin is enamoured of some Parisian woman, your son Adolphe will find another rival in—”

“Not at all, monsieur l’abbe. This young man cannot fail to see that Eugenie is a little fool,—a girl without the least freshness. Did you notice her to-night? She was as yellow as a quince.”

“Perhaps you made the cousin notice it?”

“I did not take the trouble—”

“Place yourself always beside Eugenie, madame, and you need never take the trouble to speak ill of her; he will make his own comparisons,” advised the abbe.

“Well, he has promised to dine with me the day after tomorrow,” she said.

“If you only would, madame—” the abbe began.

“What do you wish me to do, monsieur l’abbe? Do you mean to advise me badly? I have not reached thirty-nine without a stain upon my reputation, thank God! to compromise myself now, even for the empire of the Great Mogul. You and I know the meaning of words. For an ecclesiastic, you have ideas that are very incongruous. Fie! it is worthy of Faublas!”

“You have read Faublas?”

“No,” she replied; “I meant Les Liaisons dangereuses.”

“That book is infinitely more moral,” said the abbe, laughing. “But you make me out as wicked as a young man; I only meant—”

“Do you dare to tell me you were not thinking of putting wicked things into my head? Isn’t it clear? If this young man—who I admit is very good-looking—were to make love to me, he would not think of his cousin. In Paris, I know, good mothers devote themselves to their children’s happiness; but we live in the provinces.”

“Yes, madame.”

“And,” she continued, “I do not want, and Adolphe himself would not want, a hundred millions brought at such a price.”

“Madame, I said nothing about a hundred millions; that temptation might be too great for either of us to withstand. Only, I think an honest woman may permit herself, in all honor, certain harmless little coquetries which are part of her social duty and which—”

“Do you think so?”

“Are we not bound, madame, to make ourselves agreeable to each other? Permit me to blow my nose. I assure you, madame,” he resumed, “that the young gentleman ogled you through his glass in a more flattering manner than he looked at me; but I forgive him for doing homage to beauty in preference to old age—”

“It is quite apparent,” said the president loudly, “that Monsieur Grandet of Paris has sent his son to Saumur with extremely matrimonial intentions.”

“But in that case the cousin wouldn’t have fallen among us like a cannon-ball,” answered the notary.

“That doesn’t prove anything,” said Monsieur des Grassins; “the old miser is always making mysteries.”

“Des Grassins, my friend, I have invited the young man to dinner. You must go and ask Monsieur and Madame de Larsonniere and the du Hautoys, with the beautiful demoiselle du Hautoy, of course. I hope she will be properly dressed; that jealous mother of hers does make such a fright of her! Gentlemen, I trust you will all do us the honor to come,” Madame des Grassins added, stopping the procession to address the two Cruchots.

“Here you are at home, madame,” said the notary.

After bowing, the Cruchots went home, pondering the evening’s great event and its consequences. The Grassinists and Cruchotines, usually adversaries, found common cause: both must prevent Eugenie from loving her cousin and the cousin from being taken by her. They planned, by insinuations, soft-calumnies, and artful blandishments, to drive a wedge between them—trusting that even without overt plotting, provincial whispers and tactful comparisons could deceive the handsome Parisian and alter the destinies of the two young people.

When the four relations were left alone, Monsieur Grandet told his nephew, “We must go to bed. It is too late to talk of the matters that have brought you here; to-morrow we will take a suitable moment. We breakfast at eight; at midday we eat a little fruit or a bit of bread and drink a glass of white wine; and we dine at five, like the Parisians. That’s the order of the day. If you like to see the town and environs you are free to do so. You may perhaps hear people say that I am rich—Monsieur Grandet this, Monsieur Grandet that. I let them talk; their gossip does not hurt my credit. But I have not a penny; I work in my old age like an apprentice whose worldly goods are a bad plane and two good arms. Perhaps you’ll know what a franc costs when you’ve to sweat for it. Nanon, where are the candles?”

“I trust, my nephew, that you will find all you want,” said Madame Grandet; “but if you should need anything else you can call Nanon.”

“My dear aunt, I shall need nothing; I believe I have brought everything. Permit me to bid you good-night, and my young cousin also.” Charles took the wax candle Nanon handed him—an Anjou candle, yellow and so shop-worn it looked like tallow; Monsieur Grandet, unable to suspect such magnificence under his roof, did not perceive it.

“I will show you the way,” said Grandet. Instead of using the door under the archway he led his nephew through the passage between the hall and kitchen. A swing-door with a large oval pane shut this passage from the staircase; yet the north wind whistled in winter, and despite sandbags at the bottom of the living-room doors the house was hardly warm. Nanon bolted the outer door and loosed a wolf-dog whose strangled bark suggested laryngitis; the beast, famed for ferocity, recognized no one but Nanon.

As Charles climbed the stairs, the yellow, smoke-stained walls and worm-eaten steps under his uncle’s heavy footfall sobered his expectations. He fancied himself in a hen-roost. His aunt and cousin, used to the staircase, mistook his look of astonishment for friendliness and smiled. “Why the devil did my father send me to such a place?” he thought.

At the first landing, three Etruscan-red doors without casings stood sunk in dusty walls, bolted with iron sheaths ending in flame patterns. The first, directly above the kitchen, was evidently walled up; its only entrance was through Grandet’s bedchamber. That room served as his office and, to the family’s knowledge, his sanctum: a window barred by strong iron, a private key, a place where title-deeds, scales for weighing louis, estimates, profits, and receipts were secreted. People said he worked there like an alchemist with maps of his fruit-trees and exact calculations by vine and twig. No one—even Madame Grandet—had permission to enter.

Opposite the walled-up door was Eugenie’s chamber; at the other end of the landing were the married couple’s apartments. The master’s chamber separated from his wife’s by a partition and from the strong-room by a thick wall. Père Grandet lodged his nephew in the high mansarde attic above his own bedroom so he might hear him come and go. When Eugenie and her mother reached the middle of the landing they kissed for good-night, kissed Charles with a glance warm in the girl’s heart but cool upon the lips, and withdrew.

“Here you are in your room, my nephew,” Grandet said, opening the attic door. “If you need to go out, call Nanon; without her, beware! the dog would eat you up without a word. Sleep well. Good-night. Ha! why, they have made you a fire!” Nanon then appeared with a warming-pan. “Here’s something more!” grumbled Grandet. “Do you take my nephew for a lying-in woman? Carry off your brazier, Nanon!” Nanon pleaded the damp sheets and Charles’s delicacy. “Well, go on, as you’ve taken it into your head,” Grandet allowed, pushing her by the shoulders; “but don’t set things on fire.” He went downstairs, grumbling.

Left among his trunks, Charles inspected the attic—yellow paper sprinkled with bouquets, a chilling ribbed stone fireplace, yellow-wood chairs with varnished cane seats, a capacious open night-table, a meagre rag-carpet, and a tester whose valance shook as if eaten by moths. He asked la Grande Nanon if he was indeed in the house of Monsieur Grandet, formerly mayor of Saumur and brother to Monsieur Grandet of Paris. Nanon praised Grandet as “a very good, very kind, very perfect gentleman,” then fussed over his dressing-gown of green silk brocaded with gold flowers. “Are you going to put that on to go to bed with?” she asked. “Holy Virgin! what a beautiful altar-cloth it would make for the parish church!” Charles jested that she might have it one day. Nanon stood rooted, believing only when he promised. “Good night, Nanon.”

“What in the world have I come here for?” Charles thought as he went to sleep. “My father is not a fool; my journey must have some object. Pshaw! put off serious thought till to-morrow.” Blessed Virgin! how charming he is, my cousin! Eugenie was saying, interrupting her prayers, which that night at least were never finished. Madame Grandet had no thoughts as she went to bed; she heard the miser pacing. Like timid women, she had studied his character and “feigned dead” in storms of his temper.

Grandet, gazing at the sheet-iron door to his sanctum, cursed the legacy of his brother in letting his son to him. “What a crazy idea of my brother to bequeath his son to me! A fine legacy! I have not fifty francs to give him. What are fifty francs to a dandy who looked at my barometer as if he meant to make firewood of it!” He was perhaps more agitated than his brother had been signing the will. Nanon dreamed of the golden robe like an altar-cloth and of flowers, embroidery, damask; Eugenie dreamed of love.

In the pure monotony of young girls’ lives there comes an hour when the sun sheds its rays into the soul and a vague desire blooms. For Eugenie that hour had come. Early as provincial girls are, she rose betimes, said her prayers, and dressed with a new meaning in the act. She smoothed her chestnut hair, bound it high, washed her hands until the cold water reddened them, thought of the delicate hands of her cousin, and put on new stockings and prettiest shoes. Wishing to appear to advantage, she enjoyed the new gown. The clock struck seven; she had risen too early.

She sat by the window and looked at the courtyard, the narrow garden, high terraced walls: a dismal, hedged-in but not wholly unattractive prospect. A well near the kitchen, a vine clinging to an iron rod, logs arranged with library-like precision, a pavement stained black by lichens, walls streaked with moss, eight stone steps to the garden gate half buried beneath tall plants, a trellis of rotten wood and creepers, two stunted apple-trees flanking a latticed gate—these details became newly charming. Three parallel gravel walks edged with box, beds, a terrace of old walls with lindens, raspberry bushes and an immense walnut-tree drooping branches almost into the miser’s sanctum window: all took on a mysterious beauty under the autumn sun melting hoar-frost.

A thousand confused thoughts were born in her mind as the sun grew along the wall. She felt the vague delight that wraps the moral being as a cloud wraps the physical body. The sight of the wall where “Venus-hair” drooped lent rays of hope; the noise of falling leaves answered her secret questionings. She rose often to the glass and examined herself with the humility and fear that attend first love. “I am not beautiful enough for him!” she thought, a thought rich in suffering. Though lacking the prettiness that pleases the masses, Eugenie possessed a noble simplicity and a chastening Christian purity. Tall and strongly made, her face, softened by smallpox scars, had a gentle, modest, and almost painterly nobility. She saw in her reflection quiet lines and a world of love beneath her calm brow. Yet modesty or fear made her think, “I am too ugly; he will not notice me.”

She opened her door, listened for household noises, heard Nanon’s morning cough. Running down, she found Nanon milking the cow. “Nanon, my good Nanon, make a little cream for my cousin’s breakfast.” “Why, mademoiselle, you should have thought of that yesterday,” laughed Nanon. “I can’t make cream. Your cousin is a darling! I saw him in his dressing-gown, all silk and gold! He wears linen as fine as the surplice of monsieur le cure.” “Nanon, please make us a galette.” “And who’ll give me wood for the oven, and flour and butter?” Nanon, who in her role as Grandet’s minister sometimes assumed great importance, argued prudently: “Mustn’t rob the master to feast the cousin. You ask him for butter and flour and wood: he’s your father; perhaps he’ll give you some.” Eugenie fled into the garden, frightened as she heard the staircase shudder under her father’s step. She felt virginal consciousness and the special terror of hiding thoughts from the father who seemed master of fate.

Perceiving for the first time the cold nakedness of her father’s house, she felt rage that it did not suit her cousin’s elegance. She wanted to do something for him, though she did not know what. The sight of him had wakened natural yearnings a woman ought not to mistrust. At twenty-three, in the plenitude of intelligence and desire, she felt love’s first terrors. As she planned some stratagem for the cake, a quarrel—rare as swallows in winter—broke out between la Grande Nanon and Grandet. Armed with his keys, the master had come to dole provisions.

“Is there any bread left from yesterday?” he asked. “Not a crumb, monsieur.” Grandet took a large round loaf meant to weigh six pounds and was about to cut it when Nanon reminded him, “We are five to-day, monsieur.” “That’s true,” said Grandet. “But your loaves weigh six pounds; there’ll be some left. Besides, these young fellows from Paris don’t eat bread, you’ll see.” “Then they must eat frippe?” “No,” answered Grandet, “they eat neither bread nor frippe; they are something like marriageable girls.” After locking the closets with his usual parsimony, he was about to go to the fruit-garden when Nanon begged, “Monsieur, give me a little flour and some butter, and I’ll make a galette for the young ones.” “Are you going to pillage the house on account of my nephew?” “I wasn’t thinking more of your nephew than of your dog—not more than you think yourself; for, look here, you’ve only forked out six bits of sugar. I want eight.” “What’s all this, Nanon? I have never seen you like this. You sha’n’t have more than six.” “Well, then, how is your nephew to sweeten his coffee?” “With two pieces; I’ll go without myself.” “Go without sugar at your age! I’d rather buy you some out of my own pocket.” Sugar, still expensive in Grandet’s eyes, remained a totem of thrift learned under the Empire.

Women know how to dodge, and Nanon abandoned the sugar dispute to secure the galette. “Mademoiselle!” she called, “do you want some galette?” “No, no,” Eugenie answered, but Nanon persisted; Grandet, hearing his daughter’s voice, opened the flour cupboard and gave her a cupful and a few ounces of butter, and added wood for the oven if she would cook the whole dinner there to save fuel. “In that way you won’t need two fires,” he said, his look almost paternal to his faithful deputy. “Mademoiselle,” she cried after his back was turned, “we shall have the galette.”

Père Grandet returned from the garden with fruit and arranged a plateful on the kitchen table. “Just see, monsieur,” said Nanon, “what pretty boots your nephew has. What leather! why it smells good! What does he clean it with, I wonder? Am I to put your egg-polish on it?” “Nanon, I think eggs would injure that kind of leather. Tell him you don’t know how to black morocco; yes, that’s morocco. He will get you something himself in Saumur to polish those boots with. I have heard that they put sugar into the blacking to make it shine.” “They look good to eat,” said Nanon, putting the boots to her nose. “Bless me! if they don’t smell like madame’s eau-de-cologne.”

“Funny!” said her master. “Do you call it funny to put more money into boots than the man who stands in them is worth?” When Grandet locked the fruit-garden a second time Nanon asked, “Won’t you have the pot-au-feu put on once or twice a week on account of your nephew?” “Yes.” “Am I to go to the butcher’s?” “Certainly not. We will make the broth of fowls; the farmers will bring them. I shall tell Cornoiller to shoot some crows; they make the best soup in the world.” “Isn’t it true, monsieur, that crows eat the dead?” “You are a fool, Nanon. They eat what they can get, like the rest of the world. Don’t we all live on the dead? What are legacies?”

Having no further orders, Grandet drew out his watch and seeing half an hour before breakfast took his hat, kissed his daughter, and said, “Do you want to come for a walk in the fields, down by the Loire? I have something to do there.” Eugenie fetched her straw bonnet lined with pink taffeta; father and daughter went down the winding street to the shore. “Where are you going at this early hour?” asked Cruchot, the notary, meeting them. “To see something,” answered Grandet. When Père Grandet went to “see something,” Cruchot knew there’d be profit and joined him.

“Come, Cruchot,” said Grandet, “you are one of my friends. I’ll show you what folly it is to plant poplar-trees on good ground.” “Do you call the sixty thousand francs that you pocketed for those that were in your fields down by the Loire folly?” said Cruchot, astonished. “What luck you had! To cut down your trees when white-wood ran short at Nantes and sell them at thirty francs!” Eugenie listened without knowing she approached a solemn moment, that the notary would bring down a paternal and supreme sentence. They reached Grandet’s fields where thirty workmen were clearing the poplar rows.

“Maitre Cruchot, see how much ground this tree once took up! Jean, measure it—both ways.” “Four times eight feet.” “Thirty-two feet lost,” Grandet went on. “I had three hundred poplars in this one line, isn’t that so? Well, then, three hundred times thirty-two lost me five hundred in hay; add twice as much for the side rows—fifteen hundred; the middle rows as much more. So we may call it a thousand bales of hay—” “Very good,” said Cruchot to help; “a thousand bales are worth about six hundred francs.” “Say twelve hundred, because there’s three or four hundred francs on the second crop. Well then, calculate that twelve thousand francs a year for forty years with interest comes to—” “Say sixty thousand francs,” said the notary. “I am willing; comes to sixty thousand. Very good,” continued Grandet without stuttering. “Two thousand poplars forty years old will only yield fifty thousand. There’s a loss. I have found that myself.” He ordered holes filled except those at the river bank and planned to plant poplars bought there.

Eugenie, gazing at the Loire’s sublime scenery, caught only fragments until Cruchot suddenly said, “So you have brought a son-in-law from Paris. All Saumur is talking about your nephew. I shall soon have the marriage-contract to draw up, hey! Père Grandet?” “You got up very early to tell me that,” said Grandet, and with a motion of his wen he replied, “I would rather fling my daughter into the Loire than give her to her cousin. You may tell that everywhere—no, never mind; let the world talk.” The words dazzled Eugenie. The distant hopes newly born in her heart bloomed and were suddenly cut down. Since the previous evening she had attached herself to Charles by links of happiness that bind souls. Now her dawning love wrapped itself in mystery. She walked back trembling and felt the street’s recent joy transfigured into sadness.

A few steps from their door she waited as Grandet and Cruchot paused, Cruchot with a newspaper. “How are the Funds?” Grandet asked. Cruchot put the paper under his eyes and said, “Read that!” The paper ran an account: a respected merchant in Paris had blown his brains out after failing; frauds by his broker and notary had ruined him; he had resigned posts and then despaired. Cruchot understood that Grandet of Paris might have implored aid in vain from Grandet of Saumur. “I knew it,” said the old wine-grower to the notary. The news chilled Cruchot; he understood the state of the case and left to reassure Monsieur de Bonfons.

Entering, Grandet found breakfast ready. Madame Grandet, round whose neck Eugenie had flung her arms, was already seated knitting. “You can begin to eat,” said Nanon, coming downstairs four steps at a time; “the young one is sleeping like a cherub. Isn’t he a darling with his eyes shut? I went in and I called him: no answer.” “Let him sleep,” said Grandet; “he’ll wake soon enough to hear ill-tidings.” “What is it?” asked Eugenie, putting the two little bits of sugar—cut so sparingly by the miser—into her coffee. Madame Grandet, who did not dare to ask, looked at her husband. “His father has blown his brains out.” “My uncle?” said Eugenie. “Poor young man!” exclaimed Madame Grandet. “Poor indeed!” said Grandet; “he isn’t worth a sou!” “Eh! poor boy, and he’s sleeping like the king of the world!” said Nanon gently.

Eugenie stopped eating. Her heart was wrung; for the first time pity for one she loved overflowed her whole being. She wept. “What are you crying about? You didn’t know your uncle,” Grandet snarled, giving her one of those hungry, tigerish looks he cast upon his piles of gold. “But, monsieur,” said Nanon, “who wouldn’t feel pity for the poor young man, sleeping there like a wooden shoe, without knowing what’s coming?” “I didn’t speak to you, Nanon. Hold your tongue!” Eugenie learned then that the woman who loves must hide her feelings. She did not answer.

“You will say nothing to him about it, Ma’ame Grandet, till I return,” said the old man. “I have to go and straighten the line of my hedge along the high-road. I shall be back at noon, in time for the second breakfast, and then I will talk with my nephew about his affairs. As for you, Mademoiselle Eugenie, if it is for that dandy you are crying, that’s enough, child. He’s going off like a shot to the Indies. You will never see him again.” He put on his gloves, shoved the fingers together to fix them, and went out.

“Mamma, I am suffocating!” cried Eugenie when alone with her mother; “I have never suffered like this.” Madame Grandet, seeing her pale, opened the window. “I feel better!” said Eugenie after a moment. Her nervous excitation reacted on her mother; she looked at her daughter with the intuitive sympathy mothers have. Their life had been intimate as Hungarian sisters’: always together in the window embrasure, sleeping in the same atmosphere. “My poor child!” said Madame Grandet, taking Eugenie’s head and laying it upon her bosom. Eugenie raised her head and questioned her mother with a look, searching for her inmost thought. “Why send him to the Indies?” she said. “If he is unhappy ought he not to stay with us? Is he not our nearest relation?” “Yes, my child, it seems natural; but your father has his reasons: we must respect them.” The mother and daughter sat in silence, both taking up their work. Swelling with gratitude for her mother’s understanding, Eugenie kissed the dear hand: “How good you are, my kind mamma!” The words sent a glow into the motherly face, worn by many sorrows. “You like him?” asked Madame Grandet only with a smile. After a moment’s silence she said in a low voice, “Do you love him already? That is wrong.” “Wrong?” said Eugenie. “Why is it wrong? You are pleased with him, Nanon is pleased with him; why should he not please me? Come, mamma, let us set the table for his breakfast.”

She threw down her work and her mother did the same, half-scolding, half-sanctioning the folly by sharing it. Eugenie called Nanon. “What do you want now, mademoiselle?” “Nanon, can we have cream by midday?” “Ah! midday, to be sure you can.” “Well, let him have his coffee very strong; I heard Monsieur des Grassins say they make the coffee very strong in Paris. Put in a great deal.” “Where am I to get it?” “Buy some.” “Suppose monsieur meets me?” “He has gone to his fields.” “I’ll run, then. But Monsieur Fessard asked me yesterday if the Magi had come to stay with us when I bought the wax candle. All the town will know our goings-on.” “If your father finds it out,” said Madame Grandet, “he is capable of beating us.” “Well, let him beat us; we will take his blows on our knees.” Madame Grandet raised her eyes to heaven. Nanon put on her hood and went off.

Eugenie got out clean table-linen and fetched bunches of grapes she had hung on a string across the attic, walking softly to avoid waking her cousin and listening to his quiet breathing. “Sorrow is watching while he sleeps,” she thought. She arranged the vine-leaves and grapes coquettishly and piled pears into a pyramid mixed with leaves. She came and went, skipping and running. She would have liked to lay under contribution everything in her father’s house, but the keys were in his pocket. Nanon returned with two fresh eggs. Eugenie almost hugged her: “The farmer from Lande had them in his basket. I asked him for them, and he gave them to me, the darling, for nothing, as an attention!”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After two hours’ care, during which Eugenie rose twenty times to see if the coffee boiled or to listen to her cousin dressing, she prepared a simple breakfast—cheap, yet a departure from household custom. Midday breakfast was always standing: a slice of bread, a little fruit or some butter, and a glass of wine. As Eugenie looked at the table by the fire—an arm-chair before her cousin’s plate, two dishes of fruit, an egg-cup, a bottle of white wine, bread, and sugar piled in a saucer—she trembled at the thought of her father’s look if he should come in. She glanced often at the clock to see if Charles could eat before the master’s return.

“Don’t be troubled, Eugenie; if your father comes in, I will take it all upon myself,” said Madame Grandet.

Eugenie could not repress a tear.

“Oh, my good mother!” she cried, “I have never loved you enough.”

Charles, who had been pacing his room singing, came down at eleven. A true Parisian, he had put as much dandyism into his dress as if at a noble lady’s château and entered with the smiling, courteous manner of youth, which made Eugenie’s heart beat with mournful joy. He treated the destruction of his castles in Anjou as a joke and approached his aunt gaily.

“Have you slept well, dear aunt? and you, too, my cousin?”

“Very well, monsieur; did you?” said Madame Grandet.

“I? perfectly.”

“You must be hungry, cousin,” said Eugenie; “will you take your seat?”

“I never breakfast before midday; I never get up till then. However, I fared so badly on the journey that I am glad to eat something at once. Besides—” here he pulled out a pretty Breguet watch. “Dear me! I am early; it is only eleven o’clock!”

“Early?” said Madame Grandet.

“Yes; but I wanted to put my things in order. Well, I shall be glad to have anything to eat—anything, it doesn’t matter what, a chicken, a partridge.”

“Holy Virgin!” exclaimed Nanon, overhearing.

“A partridge!” thought Eugenie; she would gladly have given her little hoard for one.

“Come and sit down,” said his aunt.

The young dandy let himself drop into an easy-chair with the grace of a pretty woman on a sofa. Eugenie and her mother took plain chairs near the fire.

“Do you always live here?” asked Charles, finding the room uglier by daylight.

“Always,” Eugenie answered, “except during the vintage. Then we help Nanon, and live at the Abbaye des Noyers.”

“Don’t you ever take walks?”

“Sometimes on Sunday after vespers, when the weather is fine,” said Madame Grandet, “we walk on the bridge, or we go and watch the haymakers.”

“Have you a theatre?”

“Go to the theatre!” exclaimed Madame Grandet. “Why, monsieur, don’t you know it is a mortal sin?”

“See here, monsieur,” said Nanon, bringing in the eggs, “here are your chickens—in the shell.”

“Oh! fresh eggs,” said Charles, who, used to luxury, had already forgotten his partridge; “that is delicious: now, if you will give me the butter, my good girl.”

“Butter! then you can’t have the galette.”

“Nanon, bring the butter,” cried Eugenie.

Eugenie watched her cousin cut his sippets with the pleasure of a grisette at a melodrama where innocence triumphs. Brought up by a charming mother and trained by a woman of fashion, Charles had the elegant, dainty movements of a coxcomb. The compassionate sympathy and tenderness of the two women flowed toward him; he melted under their attention and gave Eugenie a bright, caressing look full of kindness, which seemed itself a smile. His eyes lingered on the harmony of her features, the grace of her attitude, the clear eyes where young love sparkled without knowing it.

“Ah! my dear cousin, if you were in full dress at the Opera, I assure you my aunt’s words would come true—you would make men commit the mortal sin of envy, and women the sin of jealousy.”

The compliment struck Eugenie’s heart, though she half understood it.

“Oh! cousin,” she said, “you are laughing at a poor little country girl.”

“If you knew me, my cousin, you would know I abhor ridicule; it withers the heart and jars upon all my feelings.” Here he swallowed his buttered sippet very gracefully. “No, I really have not enough mind to make fun of others; and doubtless it is a great defect. In Paris, when they want to disparage a man, they say: ‘He has a good heart.’ The phrase means: ‘The poor fellow is as stupid as a rhinoceros.’ But as I am rich, and known to hit the bull’s-eye at thirty paces with any kind of pistol, and even in the open fields, ridicule respects me.”

“My dear nephew, that bespeaks a good heart,” said Madame Grandet.

“You have a very pretty ring,” said Eugenie; “is there any harm in asking to see it?”

Charles loosened the ring and held out his hand; Eugenie blushed as she touched his pink nails with her fingertips.

“See, mamma, what beautiful workmanship.”

“My! there’s a lot of gold!” said Nanon, bringing in the coffee.

“What is that?” exclaimed Charles, laughing, as he pointed to an oblong brown earthenware pot, glazed inside, fringed with ashes, from which coffee-grounds bubbled up in the boiling liquid.

“It is boiled coffee,” said Nanon.

“Ah! my dear aunt, I shall at least leave one beneficent trace of my visit here. You are indeed behind the age! I must teach you to make good coffee in a Chaptal coffee-pot.”

He tried to explain the Chaptal process.

“Gracious! if there are so many things as all that to do,” said Nanon, “we may as well give up our lives to it. I shall never make coffee that way; I know that! Pray, who is to get the fodder for the cow while I make the coffee?”

“I will make it,” said Eugenie.

“Child!” said Madame Grandet, looking at her daughter.

The word recalled the sorrow that would soon fall upon the young man; the three women fell silent, looking at him with commiseration that caught his attention.

“Is anything the matter, my cousin?” he said.

“Hush!” Madame Grandet checked Eugenie, who was about to answer; “you know, my daughter, that your father charged us not to speak to monsieur—”

“Say Charles,” said young Grandet.

“Ah! you are called Charles? What a beautiful name!” cried Eugenie.

Presentiments of evil are almost always justified. At this moment Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie—each thinking with a shudder of the old man’s return—heard the knock whose echoes they knew too well.

“There’s papa!” said Eugenie.

She removed the saucer of sugar, leaving a few pieces on the tablecloth; Nanon carried off the egg-cup; Madame Grandet sat up like a frightened hare. Charles, wholly unable to understand, was amazed.

“Why! what is the matter?” he asked.

“My father has come,” answered Eugenie.

“Well, what of that?”

Monsieur Grandet entered, threw his keen eye upon the table and on Charles, and saw the whole thing.

“Ha! ha! so you have been making a feast for your nephew; very good, very good!” he said without stuttering. “When the cat’s away, the mice will play.”

“Feast!” thought Charles, ignorant of the house rules.

“Give me my glass, Nanon,” said the master.

Eugenie brought the glass. Grandet drew a horn-handled knife with a big blade from his breeches’ pocket, cut a slice of bread, took a small bit of butter, spread it carefully, and ate standing. Charles was sweetening his coffee. Père Grandet saw the bits of sugar, looked at his wife, who turned pale, made three steps forward, leaned to her ear, and asked,—

“Where did you get all that sugar?”

“Nanon fetched it from Fessard’s; there was none.”

It is impossible to picture the intense interest the three women took in this mute scene. Nanon had left her kitchen and stood looking into the room. Charles, having tasted his coffee and found it bitter, glanced for the sugar, which Grandet had already put away.

“What do you want?” said his uncle.

“The sugar.”

“Put in more milk,” answered the master; “your coffee will taste sweeter.”

Eugenie took the saucer Grandet had put away and placed it on the table, looking calmly at her father. The courage of that small act was as proud as any lover’s reward in other tales. Charles never guessed the cruel agitation in his cousin’s heart under the miser’s look.

“You are not eating your breakfast, wife.”

The poor helot came forward, cut herself a piece of bread, and took a pear. Eugenie boldly offered her father some grapes.

“Taste my preserves, papa. My cousin, you will eat some, will you not? I went to get these pretty grapes expressly for you.”

“If no one stops them, they will pillage Saumur for you, nephew. When you have finished, we will go into the garden; I have something to tell you which can’t be sweetened.”

Eugenie and her mother cast a look at Charles that he could not mistake.

“What is it you mean, uncle? Since the death of my poor mother”—his voice softened—“no other sorrow can touch me.”

“My nephew, who knows by what afflictions God is pleased to try us?” said his aunt.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta,” said Grandet. “There’s your nonsense beginning. I am sorry to see those white hands of yours, nephew”—and he showed his own shoulder-of-mutton fists—“there’s a pair of hands made to pick up silver pieces. You’ve been brought up to put your feet in the kid out of which we make the purses we keep our money in. A bad look-out! Very bad!”

“What do you mean, uncle? I’ll be hanged if I understand a single word of what you are saying.”

“Come!” said Grandet.

The miser closed his knife, drank the last of his wine, and opened the door.

“My cousin, take courage!”

The young girl’s tone struck terror into Charles, who followed his uncle, a prey to disquieting thoughts. Eugenie, her mother, and Nanon went into the kitchen, driven by curiosity to watch the scene about to take place in the garden, where at first the uncle walked silently ahead. Grandet found little difficulty in telling Charles of his father’s death; what he struggled with was compassion for a penniless nephew and finding words to soften the cruel truth. “You have lost your father” seemed slight; “you are absolutely without means” summed up all misfortunes. Grandet walked round the garden three times, the gravel crunching under his heavy step.

In crucial moments our minds fasten on the locality where sorrow overwhelms us. Charles noticed the box-borders, the yellow leaves fluttering down, the dilapidated walls, and the gnarled fruit-trees—images destined to remain with the memory of that solemn hour.

“It is very fine weather, very warm,” said Grandet, drawing a long breath.

“Yes, uncle; but why—”

“Well, my lad,” answered his uncle, “I have some bad news to give you. Your father is ill—”

“Then why am I here?” said Charles. “Nanon,” he cried, “order post-horses! I can get a carriage somewhere?” he added, turning to his uncle.

“Horses and carriages are useless,” answered Grandet, looking at Charles, who stood silent, eyes fixed. “Yes, my poor boy, you guess the truth—he is dead. But that’s nothing; there is something worse: he blew out his brains.”

“My father!”

“Yes, but that’s not the worst; the newspapers are all talking about it. Here, read that.”

Grandet, who had borrowed the fatal article from Cruchot, thrust the paper under his nephew’s eyes. The young man, still a child emotionally, burst into tears.

“That’s good!” thought Grandet; “his eyes frightened me. He’ll be all right if he weeps. That is not the worst, my poor nephew,” he added aloud, not noticing if Charles heard, “that is nothing; you will get over it: but—”

“Never, never! My father! Oh, my father!”

“He has ruined you, you haven’t a penny.”

“What does that matter? My father! Where is my father?”

His sobs resounded horribly against the dreary walls and through the echoes. The three women, filled with pity, wept too; tears are contagious. Charles, not listening further, ran through the court and up to his chamber, threw himself on the bed and hid his face in the sheets to weep in peace.

“The first burst must have its way,” said Grandet, returning to the living-room where Eugenie and her mother had resumed their seats and were sewing with trembling hands after wiping their eyes. “But that young man is good for nothing; his head is more taken up with the dead than with his money.”

Eugenie shuddered at her father’s comment on the most sacred grief. From then on she began to judge him. Charles’s muffled sobs still sounded through the house; his deep groans, seeming to come from the earth, ceased only toward evening, growing feebler.

“Poor young man!” said Madame Grandet.

Fatal exclamation! Père Grandet looked at his wife, at Eugenie, and at the sugar-bowl. He recollected the extraordinary breakfast prepared for the unfortunate youth, and took his position in the middle of the room.

“Listen to me,” he said, with his usual composure. “I hope you will not continue this extravagance, Madame Grandet. I don’t give you MY money to stuff that young fellow with sugar.”

“My mother had nothing to do with it,” said Eugenie; “it was I who—”

“Is it because you are of age,” said Grandet, interrupting her, “that you choose to contradict me? Remember, Eugenie—”

“Father, the son of your brother ought to receive from us—”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta!” exclaimed the cooper on four chromatic tones; “the son of my brother this, my nephew that! Charles is nothing at all to us; he hasn’t a farthing, his father has failed; and when this dandy has cried his fill, off he goes from here. I won’t have him revolutionize my household.”

“What is ‘failing,’ father?” asked Eugenie.

“To fail,” answered her father, “is to commit the most dishonorable action that can disgrace a man.”

“It must be a great sin,” said Madame Grandet, “and our brother may be damned.”

“There, there, don’t begin with your litanies!” said Grandet, shrugging. “To fail, Eugenie,” he resumed, “is to commit a theft which the law, unfortunately, takes under its protection. People entrusted their property to Guillaume Grandet trusting his honor; he has made away with it all, and left them nothing but their eyes to weep with. A highway robber is better than a bankrupt: the one attacks you and you can defend yourself, he risks his life; but the other—well, in short, Charles is dishonored.”

The words weighed heavily on the poor girl. Upright and delicate, ignorant of worldly sophisms, she believed her father’s atrocious explanation, unable to distinguish involuntary failure from intentional fraud.

“Father, could you not have prevented such a misfortune?”

“My brother did not consult me. Besides, he owes four millions.”

“What is a ‘million,’ father?” she asked, with childlike simplicity.

“A million? why, it is a million pieces of twenty sous each, and it takes five twenty sous pieces to make five francs.”

“Dear me!” cried Eugenie, “how could my uncle possibly have had four millions? Is there any one else in France who ever had so many millions?” Père Grandet stroked his chin, smiled, and his wen seemed to dilate. “But what will become of my cousin Charles?”

“He is going off to the West Indies by his father’s request, and he will try to make his fortune there.”

“Has he got the money to go with?”

“I shall pay for his journey as far as—yes, as far as Nantes.”

Eugenie sprang into his arms.

“Oh, father, how good you are!”

She kissed him with warmth that almost made Grandet ashamed; his conscience galled him a little.

“Will it take much time to amass a million?” she asked.

“Look here!” said the old miser, “you know what a napoleon is? Well, it takes fifty thousand napoleons to make a million.”

“Mamma, we must say a great many neuvaines for him.”

“I was thinking so,” said Madame Grandet.

“That’s the way, always spending my money!” cried the father. “Do you think there are francs on every bush?”

At that moment a muffled cry more distressing than the others echoed through the garrets and struck chill into Eugenie’s and her mother’s hearts.

“Nanon, go upstairs and see that he does not kill himself,” said Grandet. “Now, then,” he added, looking at his wife and daughter, who had turned pale at his words, “no nonsense, you two! I must leave you; I have got to see about the Dutchmen who are going away today. And then I must find Cruchot, and talk with him about all this.”

He departed. As soon as he had shut the door, Eugenie and her mother breathed more freely. Until that morning the young girl had never felt constrained in her father’s presence; but in these hours her feelings and ideas began to change.

“Mamma, how many louis are there in a cask of wine?”

“Your father sells his from a hundred to a hundred and fifty francs, sometimes two hundred—at least, so I’ve heard.”

“Then papa must be rich?”

“Perhaps he is. But Monsieur Cruchot told me he bought Froidfond two years ago; that may have pinched him.”

Eugenie, unable to understand her father’s fortune, stopped her calculations.

“He didn’t even see me, the darling!” said Nanon returning. “He’s stretched out like a calf on his bed and crying like the Madeleine, and that’s a blessing! What’s the matter with the poor dear young man!”

“Let us go and console him, mamma; if anyone knocks, we can come down.”

Madame Grandet yielded to the sweet persuasion of her daughter’s voice. Eugenie was sublime: she had become a woman. They went up to Charles’s room. The door was open. The young man saw and heard nothing; plunged in grief, he uttered inarticulate cries.

“How he loves his father!” said Eugenie in a low voice.

In that utterance it was impossible to mistake the hopes of a heart that, unknown to itself, had suddenly become passionate. Madame Grandet cast a mother’s look upon her daughter and whispered,—

“Take care, you will love him!”

“Love him!” answered Eugenie. “Ah! if you did but know what my father said to Monsieur Cruchot.”

Charles turned over and saw his aunt and cousin.

“I have lost my father, my poor father! If he had told me his secret troubles we might have worked together to repair them. My God! my poor father! I was so sure I should see him again that I think I kissed him quite coldly—”

Sobs cut off his words.

“We will pray for him,” said Madame Grandet. “Resign yourself to the will of God.”

“Cousin,” said Eugenie, “take courage! Your loss is irreparable; therefore think only of saving your honor.”

With the delicate instinct of a woman who puts her mind into all things, even while offering consolation, Eugenie sought to turn his thoughts inward.

“My honor?” exclaimed Charles, tossing aside his hair and sitting up, crossing his arms. “Ah! that is true. My uncle said my father had failed.” He uttered a heart-rending cry and hid his face in his hands. “Leave me, leave me, cousin! My God! my God! forgive my father, for he must have suffered sorely!”

There was something terribly attractive in this young sorrow, sincere without reasoning. It was a virgin grief that the simple hearts of Eugenie and her mother could comprehend, and they obeyed his sign to leave him. They went downstairs in silence and sat by the window sewing for nearly an hour without exchanging a word. Eugenie had seen, in a furtive glance around his room—the pretties of his dressing-case, the scissors, the razors embossed with gold—a gleam of luxury across his grief that made him more interesting by contrast. Never before had such a serious event touched the imaginations of these two passive beings, hitherto sunk in the stillness of solitude.

“Mamma,” said Eugenie, “we must wear mourning for my uncle.”

“Your father will decide that,” answered Madame Grandet.

They relapsed into silence. Eugenie drew her stitches with a uniform motion that revealed the teeming thoughts of her meditation. The first desire of her heart was to share her cousin’s mourning.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
About four o’clock an abrupt knock at the door struck sharply on Madame Grandet’s heart.

“What can have happened to your father?” she said to her daughter.

Grandet entered in high spirits. After removing his gloves he rubbed his hands hard; his leathery skin spared them. His secret escaped him.

“Wife,” he said without stammering, “I’ve trapped them all! Our wine is sold! The Dutch and the Belgians have gone. I loitered at the market-place before their inn, pretending not to care. That Belgian fellow—you know him—came to me. The owners of fine vineyards have kept back their vintages to wait; I didn’t hinder them. The Belgian was desperate; in a minute the bargain was struck. He takes my vintage at two hundred francs the puncheon, half down. He paid me in gold; the notes are drawn. Here are six louis for you. In three months wines will have fallen.”

Those quietly spoken, bitterly sarcastic words would have chilled Saumur’s market-place: panic would have wrecked prices.

“Did you have a thousand puncheons this year, father?”

“Yes, little one.” That term was his highest expression of joy.

“Then that makes two hundred thousand pieces of twenty sous each?”

“Yes, Mademoiselle Grandet.”

“Then, father, you can easily help Charles.”

Grandet’s rage, cold and concentrated, was fiercer than any spectacle of Babylon’s doom. He had forgotten his nephew until Eugenie put him in the account.

“What’s this? Ever since that dandy set foot in my house everything goes wrong! You behave as if you had a right to buy sugar-plums and make feasts and weddings. I won’t have that. I know my duty at my time of life! I certainly sha’n’t take lessons from my daughter, or from anybody else. I shall do for my nephew what is proper, and you have no need to poke your nose into it. As for you, Eugenie,” he added, facing her, “don’t speak of this again, or I’ll send you to the Abbaye des Noyers with Nanon, see if I don’t; and no later than to-morrow either, if you disobey me! Where is that fellow, has he come down yet?”

“No, my friend,” answered Madame Grandet.

“What is he doing then?”

“He is weeping for his father,” said Eugenie.

Grandet looked at his daughter, struck dumb; after all, he was a father. He paced, then hurried to his secret den to ponder an investment in the public Funds. The thinning of his two thousand acres had yielded six hundred thousand francs; with sales of poplars and other gains he had nine hundred thousand francs, not counting the two hundred thousand from the sale just closed. The twenty percent Cruchot promised in the Funds tempted him. He figured on the newspaper margin that had announced his brother’s death, all the while hearing his nephew’s moans but not listening. Nanon knocked to summon him to dinner. On the last step he said to himself—

“I’ll do it; I shall get eight per cent interest. In two years I shall have fifteen hundred thousand francs, and I will then draw out good gold.—Well, where’s my nephew?”

“He says he doesn’t want anything to eat,” answered Nanon; “that’s not good for him.”

“So much saved,” retorted her master.

“That’s so,” she said.

“Bah! he won’t cry long. Hunger drives the wolves out of the woods.”

Dinner was eaten in silence.

“My good friend,” said Madame Grandet when the cloth was removed, “we must put on mourning.”

“Upon my word, Madame Grandet! what will you invent next to spend money on? Mourning is in the heart, not the clothes.”

“But mourning for a brother is indispensable; the Church commands it—”

“Buy your mourning out of your six louis. Give me a hat-band; that’s enough for me.”

Eugenie raised her eyes to heaven without words. Her generous instincts, long repressed and now suddenly awakened, were galled. The evening passed as other monotonous evenings, yet was the most horrible. Eugenie sewed without lifting her head; Madame Grandet knitted; Grandet twirled his thumbs four hours, absorbed in calculations whose results would astonish Saumur the next day. No one visited; the town buzzed with news of Grandet’s sale, his brother’s failure, and the arrival of his nephew. The upper and middle-class wine-growers gossiped at Monsieur des Grassins, fulminating imprecations against the ex-mayor. Nanon spun; the whirr of her wheel was the only sound beneath the gray rafters.

“We don’t waste our tongues,” she said, showing her teeth like peeled almonds.

“Nothing should be wasted,” answered Grandet, rousing from reverie. He saw a perspective of eight millions in three years and sailed along that sheet of gold. “Let us go to bed. I will bid my nephew good-night for the rest of you, and see if he will take anything.”

Madame Grandet remained on the first landing to hear the coming conversation; Eugenie went up two stairs.

“Well, nephew, you are in trouble. Yes, weep, that’s natural. A father is a father; but we must bear our troubles patiently. I am a good uncle to you, remember that. Come, take courage! Will you have a little glass of wine?” (Wine costs nothing in Saumur.) “Why!” added Grandet, “you have got no light! That’s bad, very bad; you ought to see what you are about,” and he walked to the chimney-piece. “What’s this?” he cried. “A wax candle! How the devil did they filch a wax candle? The spendthrifts would tear down the ceilings of my house to boil the fellow’s eggs.”

Hearing this, mother and daughter slipped back into their rooms and burrowed in bed like frightened mice.

“Madame Grandet, have you found a mine?” said the man, coming into his wife’s chamber.

“My friend, wait; I am saying my prayers,” said the poor mother in a trembling voice.

“The devil take your good God!” growled Grandet.

Misers have little belief in a future life; the present is their all. This casts a terrible light on an epoch in which money sways laws, politics, and morals. Institutions, books, and men conspire to undermine belief in a future life—a belief on which the social edifice rested for centuries. The grave, once a feared transition, is now less so; the future has been imported into the present. To obtain a terrestrial paradise of luxury by any means, hardening the heart and macerating the body for fleeting possessions, has become a widespread thought—written even in laws that ask, “What do you pay?” instead of “What do you think?” When this doctrine passes from bourgeoisie to populace, where will the country be?

“Madame Grandet, have you done?” asked the old man.

“My friend, I am praying for you.”

“Very good! Good-night; to-morrow morning we will have a talk.”

The poor woman slept like a schoolboy who, unprepared, fears his master’s anger. As she drew the sheet over her to stifle sound, Eugenie, in her night-gown and with bare feet, ran to her and kissed her brow.

“Oh! my good mother,” she said, “to-morrow I will tell him it was I.”

“No; he would send you to Noyers. Leave me to manage it; he cannot eat me.”

“Do you hear, mamma?”

“What?”

“He is weeping still.”

“Go to bed, my daughter; you will take cold in your feet: the floor is damp.”

Thus passed the solemn day which would weigh upon the whole life of the rich-and-poor heiress, whose sleep was never again so calm. Certain actions seem improbable because we omit to turn psychological light upon the impulses that cause them. Eugenie’s deep passion should be traced in its fibres; it became a malady that influenced her existence. Many prefer to deny results rather than estimate the force of hidden ties. Eugenie’s past life explains her naive want of reflection and sudden emotions: the more tranquil the life, the more vivid her pity and the simpler the sentiments now developed.

Restless from the day’s events, she woke at intervals to listen for her cousin, thinking she heard his sighs. Sometimes she saw him dying or fainting from hunger. Toward morning she was certain she heard a cry. She dressed and ran, in the dawning light, to his chamber, whose door he had left open. The candle had burned to the socket. Charles, overcome, slept dressed in an armchair beside the bed, his head on the bedpost; he dreamed as men dream on an empty stomach. Eugenie might weep at her ease; she could admire the handsome face blotted with grief, the eyes swollen as if with tears. Charles felt her presence; he opened his eyes and saw her pity.

“Pardon me, my cousin,” he said, evidently not knowing the hour or place.

“There are hearts who hear you, cousin, and we thought you might need something. You should go to bed; you tire yourself by sitting thus.”

“That is true.”

“Well, then, adieu!”

She escaped, ashamed and happy. Innocence alone can be so bold. Once enlightened, virtue calculates like vice. Eugenie, who had not trembled beside her cousin, could scarcely stand when she re-entered her chamber. Her ignorant life had suddenly ended; she reproached herself.

“What will he think of me? He will think that I love him!”

That was what she most wished him to think. An honest love presages itself. To enter a young man’s chamber meant much to this solitary girl; some acts in love mean espousals to certain souls. An hour later she dressed her mother as usual. They sat before the window waiting for Grandet with the anxious dread that freezes the heart when a scene is feared. The goodman came down; he spoke to his wife absent-mindedly, kissed Eugenie, and sat to table without appearing to remember his threats.

“What has become of my nephew? The lad gives no trouble.”

“Monsieur, he is asleep,” answered Nanon.

“So much the better; he won’t want a wax candle,” said Grandet with a jeer.

This clemency and bitter gaiety struck Madame Grandet; she studied her husband. The goodman—called “goodman” in regions like Touraine and Anjou at a certain age whether kind or harsh—took his hat and gloves saying as he went out,—

“I am going to loiter about the market-place and find Cruchot.”

“Eugenie, your father certainly has something on his mind.”

Grandet, a poor sleeper, spent half his nights in preliminary calculations, giving accuracy to his schemes. All human power is a compound of time and patience. The miser’s life is constant exercise of power for self: self-love and self-interest, compact and intelligent, form egotism. From this arises persistent curiosity and the habit of trading on others. Where is the man without desire? and what social desire can be satisfied without money?

Grandet had “something on his mind.” Like all misers, he craved to play a commercial game with men and win their money legally. To impose upon others was a sign of power, proof he had the right to despise those who let themselves be preyed upon. Oh! who has ever truly understood the lamb lying peacefully at the feet of God?—the emblem of terrestrial victims. The miser fattens and slaughters the lamb, then despises it. His pasture is money and disdain. During the night his ideas took another course, explaining his sudden clemency. He hatched a plot to trick the Parisians, to decoy and snare them, to make them sweat and hope—a plot to amuse himself, the old provincial cooper, sitting under his gloomy rafters. His nephew occupied his mind. He wished to save his dead brother’s honor without spending a penny on the son or himself. His own funds he would invest for three years; he needed only to manage his Saumur property. Finding nutriment for mischief in his brother’s failure, he resolved to crush the Parisians on Charles’s behalf and to play the good brother at the cheapest terms. Family honor counted so little that his intention resembled a gambler’s interest in seeing a game well played without a stake. The Cruchots were necessary; but he would not seek them—he resolved to make them come to him and to stage, that evening, a comedy whose plot he had conceived, which would make him the town’s admired man on the morrow without costing him a penny.

In his absence Eugenie had the happiness of openly tending her cousin, spending on him fearlessly the treasures of her pity—woman’s sublime superiority, the one she desires to have recognized, the one she pardons man for letting her assume. Three or four times she went to listen to his breathing to see if he slept, then, when he rose, she turned to his breakfast—the cream, eggs, fruits, the plates and glasses—each item a special care. At length she ran up the old staircase to hear the noise he made. Was he dressing? Did he still weep? She reached the door.

“My cousin!”

“Yes, cousin.”

“Will you breakfast downstairs, or in your room?”

“Where you like.”

“How do you feel?”

“Dear cousin, I am ashamed of being hungry.”

This conversation, held through the closed door, was like an episode in a poem to Eugenie.

“Well, then, we will bring your breakfast to your own room, so as not to annoy my father.”

She flew to the kitchen.

“Nanon, go and do his room!”

That staircase, often traversed and echoing to the slightest noise, lost its decaying look in Eugenie’s eyes. It grew luminous; it had a voice and spoke to her; it was young—young like the love it was now serving. Her kind indulgent mother lent herself to the caprices of the child’s love, and after the room was put in order both went to sit with the unhappy youth and keep him company. Does not Christian charity make consolation a duty? The two women drew little sophistries from religion to justify their conduct. Charles became the object of tender and loving care. His saddened heart felt the sweetness of the gentle friendship these two souls, crushed under restraint, displayed when, for an instant, they were unfettered in the regions of suffering.

Claiming relationship, Eugenie began to fold linen and arrange the toilet articles Charles had brought; she could marvel at each luxurious bauble and knick-knack of silver and gold, holding them under pretext of examination. Charles could not see without emotion the generous interest his aunt and cousin felt; he knew Paris society and, placed as he was, had expected indifference. Eugenie thus appeared in splendor, and he admired the innocence of life and manners he had been inclined to ridicule. When Eugenie took from Nanon the bowl of coffee and cream and poured it with real feeling, giving him a kindly glance, the Parisian filled with tears; he took her hand and kissed it.

“What troubles you?” she said.

“Oh! these are tears of gratitude,” he answered.

Eugenie abruptly turned to the chimney-piece to take the candlesticks.

“Here, Nanon, carry them away!” she said.

When she looked again she blushed, but her looks could at least deceive, not betray the excess of joy that flooded her heart; yet the eyes of both expressed the same sentiment—the future was theirs. This soft emotion was precious to Charles amid heavy grief because it was wholly unexpected. The knocker recalled the women to duty; happily they ran downstairs with speed to be seated at their work when Grandet entered; had he met them under the archway his suspicions might have been roused. After breakfast, which the goodman took standing, the keeper from Froidfond, whose promised indemnity had not yet been paid, appeared with a hare and partridges from the park, eels and two pike sent as tribute by the millers.

“Ha, ha! poor Cornoiller; here he comes, like fish in Lent. Is all that fit to eat?”

“Yes, my dear, generous master; it has been killed two days.”

“Come, Nanon, bestir yourself,” said Grandet; “take these things, they’ll do for dinner. I have invited the two Cruchots.”

Nanon opened her eyes, stupefied, and looked at everybody.

“Well!” she said, “and how am I to get the lard and the spices?”

“Wife,” said Grandet, “give Nanon six francs, and remind me to get some of the good wine out of the cellar.”

“Well, then, Monsieur Grandet,” said the keeper, prepared to settle the indemnity, “Monsieur Grandet—”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta!” said Grandet; “I know what you want to say. You are a good fellow; we will see about it to-morrow, I’m too busy to-day. Wife, give him five francs,” he added to Madame Grandet as he decamped.

The poor woman was too happy to buy peace at eleven francs. She knew Grandet would let her alone for a fortnight after he thus took back, franc by franc, the money he had given her.

“Here, Cornoiller,” she said, slipping ten francs into his hand, “some day we will reward your services.”

Cornoiller could say nothing and went away.

“Madame,” said Nanon, who had donned her black coif and taken her basket, “I want only three francs. You keep the rest; it’ll go fast enough somehow.”

“Have a good dinner, Nanon; my cousin will come down,” said Eugenie.

“Something very extraordinary is going on, I am certain,” said Madame Grandet. “This is only the third time since our marriage that your father has given a dinner.”

About four o’clock, just as Eugenie and her mother had finished setting the table for six and the master had brought up a few bottles of the provincial wine they prized, Charles came down into the hall. He was pale; his gestures, expression, glance, and voice had a sadness full of grace. He truly suffered; the veil of pain gave him an air dear to women. Eugenie loved him the more for it. Sorrow had leveled the distance between them—Charles was no longer the rich, distinguished young man above her sphere, but a relation plunged into frightful misery. Misery begets equality. Women, like angels, claim suffering humanity. Charles and Eugenie spoke only with their eyes; the poor fallen dandy, orphaned and impoverished, sat apart, proud, calm, silent. From time to time the gentle caressing glance of the young girl shone upon him and drew him from sad thoughts into fields of hope, where she loved to hold him at her side.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At Saumur the talk was less about Grandet’s recent wine sale, which had offended the growers, than about the dinner he gave the Cruchots; the politic old miser could have been praised had he acted from noble motives, but convinced of his superiority to the townspeople, he cared little for public opinion.

The des Grassins, having quickly learned of Guillaume Grandet’s failure and violent death, resolved to visit that evening to commiserate and to learn why Grandet had invited the Cruchots. At five precisely Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle the notary arrived in their Sunday clothes. They ate heartily; Grandet was grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, Madame Grandet reserved. When they rose, Charles said he must retire to begin a long and painful correspondence.

Once sure Charles could not overhear, Grandet dismissed his wife and daughter and, alone with the two Cruchots, resumed the cunning that had earned him the nickname “the old dog.” He affected a stutter and slight deafness—devices to steer conversation and evade commitments—so as to hide his intentions.

Stammering, he broached the subject of bankruptcy. The president, flattered by Grandet’s awkwardly respectful address, took over and explained how a threatened merchant might avoid dishonor by liquidating rather than being declared insolvent. He described how creditors’ claims could be bought in the market at a reduction so that, if one acquired and paid off the debts by arrangement, the estate would be honorably released. Grandet pretended to be ignorant of such Paris tricks and professed he lived in Saumur attending to vines and drains. He admitted he might help his nephew but insisted he had no time or expertise to engage personally.

The president—smooth, sniffing snuff—went on about notes as merchandise, invoking Bentham’s reasoning, and suggested debts might be bought for twenty-five percent. The notary laughed; the president added practicalities: a friend could go to Paris, buy up the notes at a reduction, obtain extensions from creditors, and thus save the family’s honor. Grandet appeared distressed by the complexity and expense, repeatedly claiming he was only a poor wine-grower; but his eye betrayed eagerness.

The notary, uneasy, warned his nephew to let Monsieur Grandet explain his intentions. Before Cruchot could resume, the des Grassins arrived with expressions of condolence. The notary took the opportunity to announce Grandet’s honorable intention to liquidate his brother’s affairs and that the president had offered to go to Paris to negotiate with the creditors. Des Grassins—who had been gossiping about the old man’s avarice and had nearly accused him of fratricide—were astonished and delighted.

“Grandet is honorable to the backbone,” cried the banker, embracing him. The scene let the des Grassins present themselves as sympathetic and competent. The banker suggested that such a commercial matter required a consummate business man and offered to take charge, since he was going to Paris and could attend to it alongside his own affairs.

Dropping some assumed hesitancy, Grandet confided privately to des Grassins that he trusted the banker more than the president and had other plans. He intended to buy a few thousand francs in the Funds while the price was low; des Grassins knew the market and could make the purchase by month’s end. He signaled that none of this should be told to the Cruchots. The banker, pleased, promised to start by the mail-post and to take final directions at five o’clock, just before dinner.

They lingered; des Grassins slapped Grandet on the shoulder, murmuring it was fortunate to have such a relation. Grandet, rubbing his wen, declared his love for his brother and his intention to prove it—unless costs prevented him. Des Grassins had effectively cut the Cruchots out of the commission; the president, disappointed at being sidelined, complained to his uncle that he had begun by being “de Bonfons” and ended as nothing but Cruchot. The notary counselled modesty: let the des Grassins take this tack, and Eugenie would remain the president’s wife all the same.

News of Grandet’s magnanimity quickly spread through three houses and then the town. Everyone forgave his sale made in defiance of pledged good faith and lauded his apparent generosity; such sudden enthusiasm is a French tendency, quick to forget and quick to praise.

When Père Grandet shut the door he called Nanon and instructed her not to let the dog loose or go to bed; they had work to do. At eleven Cornoiller would bring the chariot from Froidfond; Nanon must tell him to come softly, for there must be no nocturnal racket and no neighborly curiosity. Grandet returned to his private room, moving cautiously so as not to wake his wife and daughter and, above all, not to rouse his nephew, whom he already began to condemn for his pale, despairing looks.

About midnight Eugenie, anxious for Charles, fancied she heard a cry like that of a dying person. She wrapped herself in a pelisse and, thinking of the light under her cousin’s door, was about to go out when she saw Nanon’s heavy steps and heard horses. Peering into the corridor she met her father’s vague, unnoticing gaze and was terrified. Grandet and Nanon were joined by a stout stick; a thick rope over their shoulders carried a small keg like those Grandet made in the bakehouse—filled, as Nanon complained, with heavy copper sous. Grandet cautioned them to be careful of the candlestick; the scene was lit by a single candle on the staircase.

Cornoiller had come with horses; Grandet asked if he had pistols—there was nothing to fear—and told Nanon to say to his wife that he had gone into the country and would be back to dinner. The chariot drove off with all possible secrecy; Nanon bolted the great door, let the dog loose, and went to bed with a bruised shoulder, the neighbors unaware of either the departure or its purpose. Grandet’s precautions and reticence were habitual; no one had ever seen a penny in that house, though it was filled with gold.

In the morning gossip at the port reported that exchange on gold had doubled because of military preparations at Nantes and that speculators had come to Angers to buy coin. The old wine-grower, having borrowed horses from his farmers, had seized the chance: he had gone to sell his gold and to bring back treasury notes, converting the sum he intended to put into the Funds and swelling it by advantage of the exchange.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
“My father has gone,” Eugenie thought, having heard it called from the head of the stairs. Silence fell; the rumble of the carriage died away. A cry came from her cousin’s room; a thin strip of light showed beneath the door. “He suffers!” she cried, and rushed up. A moan brought her to the landing. The door stood ajar; Charles slept with his head drooped over the old chair’s arm, his pen fallen nearly to the floor. His strained posture alarmed her; she entered.

“He must be very tired,” she thought, and glanced at a dozen sealed letters on the table—addresses to carriage-makers and a tailor—signs that he had been settling affairs to leave France. Two open letters caught her eye. The words “My dear Annette” at the head of one stunned her; her heart beat fast, her feet felt nailed. “His dear Annette! He loves! He is loved! No hope!” she cried inwardly, torn between honor and curiosity. She hesitated, then yielded: she must read.

As she read, the keen glow of first love made every feeling precious. The letter began with mournful, resolute candor. My dear Annette,—Nothing could have separated us but the great misfortune that has fallen on me. My father has killed himself; his fortune and mine are irretrievably lost. I am an orphan at an age when, by my education, I am still a child; yet I must lift myself as a man out of the abyss. If I wish to leave France an honest man—I am without a hundred francs to try my luck in the Indies or America. Yes, Anna, I must seek my fortune in those deadly climates. I cannot remain in Paris. Neither my nature nor my face are made to bear the affronts, neglect, disdain shown to a ruined man, the son of a bankrupt! Good God! think of owing two millions! I should be killed in a duel the first week; therefore I shall not return. Your love—the most tender and devoted love—cannot draw me back. I have no money to go to you, to give and receive a last kiss that might strengthen me.

Hearing those words, Eugenie’s thought was simple and immediate: “Poor Charles! I did well to read the letter. I have gold; I will give it to him.” She wiped her eyes and read on. Charles admitted he had never considered poverty’s miseries; even if he had a hundred louis for the journey, he had no outfit. He might go to Nantes and ship as a common sailor; he would begin anew in the New World like other men who started with nothing and made fortunes. He told Annette he felt more courage than might be supposed for a young man used to being petted, but the ruin aged him twenty-four hours. He spoke of Annette’s luxuries—opera-box and dress—and insisted he would never accept sacrifices. They must part; perhaps in ten years, if he returned, society or fate would have altered both their lives; sometimes daughters become companions or spies. He begged her, in memory of their four years of happiness, to be faithful if she could, but he demanded nothing: he must adapt to his new life and therefore accept the necessity of marriage if circumstances required.

“He gives her up! Blessed Virgin! What happiness!” Eugenie quivered with joy; Charles stirred in his sleep and she paused, terrified he might wake. Yet when the letter broke off abruptly, she excused him: how could an innocent girl suspect cold-heartedness in such words? Young, religiously reared girls see love’s light everywhere; they reflect their warmth on their lover and attribute to him their highest thoughts. Eugenie’s simple heart heard “My dear Annette” as the sweetest words; the tears on the young man’s lashes seemed noble.

Behind these tender effusions, however, lay more complex motives. Charles, the son of indulgent parents, had been shielded from hard reckonings by Monsieur and Madame Guillaume Grandet. Paris and Annette had taught him to calculate—he had learned the prudence, the effeminacy, the materialism of society, where to “see clear” is to suspect and weigh interests. After folly and adulation, Annette made him think soberly; with a perfumed hand among his curls she spoke to him of his future, instilling worldly prudence that effeminized and materialized him. Yet to a young girl like Eugenie, such outward appearances were convincing; she could hardly distrust a cousin whose manners and words matched youthful hopes. The fatal chance that night laid Charles’s last effusions before her: she heard the last stirrings of his conscience; she read a letter full of love and grief and vowed to love him always.

She laid down the first letter and smiled, watching him sleep; the fresh illusions of life still rested on his face. Then she glanced at the other letter, seeking more proof of his noble qualities.

My dear Alphonse,—When you receive this letter I shall be without friends; but while I doubt the friendship of the world, I have never doubted yours. I beg you to settle my affairs and get as much as you can out of my possessions. You know my situation. I have nothing left, and I intend to go at once to the Indies. I have written to those I think I owe money, and enclose a list. My books, furniture, pictures, horses, etc., ought to pay my debts. I do not wish to keep anything, except perhaps a few baubles for my outfit. I will send a power of attorney so you can make these sales. Send me all my weapons. Keep Briton for yourself; nobody would pay his value, and I would rather give him to you—like a mourning-ring bequeathed by a dying man to his executor. Farry, Breilmann, & Co. built me a travelling-carriage not yet delivered; persuade them to keep it and not ask for payment. If they refuse, do what you can and avoid anything dishonorable. I owe the British Islander six louis lost at cards; don’t fail to pay him—

This practical letter confirmed to Eugenie the gravity of his affairs and his intention to go. Her decision followed without hesitation. She ran softly to an old oak cabinet topped with the faint royal salamander, opened its drawer, and took out a large red velvet purse edged with gold fringe—a relic from her grandmother. She weighed it proudly and began counting: portugaises, genovines, Spanish quadruples, Dutch ducats, Indian rupees, and a napoleon of forty francs received the day before. Her father loved these coins for rarity; Eugenie thought only of Charles. After a few miscalculations she reckoned their intrinsic value at about five thousand eight hundred francs—nearly six thousand for collectors. She clapped her hands like a child. Father and daughter had each counted their fortune—he, to sell his gold; she, to fling hers into the sea of affection.

Carrying the candle and purse, Eugenie returned to Charles. At the threshold he woke and was speechless with surprise. She set the candle on the table.

“My cousin, I must beg pardon for a wrong I have done you; but God will pardon me—if you—will help me to wipe it out.”

“How did it happen?” Charles murmured, rubbing his eyes.

“I have read those letters,” she confessed. Charles colored.

“How came I here? Truly, I do not know. I am tempted not to regret too much that I have read them; they have made me know your heart, your soul, and—”

“And what?” asked Charles.

“Your plans, your need of a sum—”

“My dear cousin—”

“Hush, hush! not so loud; we must not wake others. See,” she said, opening her purse, “here are the savings of a poor girl who wants nothing. Charles, accept them! This morning I did not value money; you have taught me. It is but a means. A cousin is almost a brother; you can surely borrow your sister’s purse.”

Eugenie, both woman and girl, never dreamed of refusal. Charles hesitated. Her heart beat audibly; his silence mortified her. She knelt.

“I will never rise till you have taken that gold!” she cried. “My cousin, I implore you, answer me! Let me know if you respect me, if you are generous, if—”

Moved, Charles took her hands to keep her from kneeling; his tears fell on them. Eugenie sprang to the purse and poured its contents on the table.

“Ah! yes, yes, you consent?” she sobbed. “Fear nothing, my cousin, you will be rich. This gold will bring you happiness; some day you shall bring it back to me—are we not partners? I will obey all conditions.”

At last Charles could speak. “Yes, Eugenie; my soul would be small indeed if I did not accept. And yet—gift for gift, confidence for confidence.”

“What do you mean?” she asked, frightened.

“Listen, dear cousin; I have here—” He pointed to a square box covered by a leather case on the drawers. “There,” he continued, “is something as precious to me as life itself. This box was a present from my mother. All day I have thought that if she could rise from her grave, she would herself sell the gold which her love for me lavished on this dressing-case; but were I to do so, it would seem a sacrilege.” Eugenie pressed his hand.

“No,” he added after a pause with a tender look, “no, I will neither sell it nor risk its safety on my journey. Dear Eugenie, you shall be its guardian. Never did friend commit anything more sacred to another. Let me show it to you.”

He opened the case and revealed a richly wrought dressing-case with gold ornaments. “What you admire is nothing,” he said, pushing a spring that revealed a hidden drawer. He drew out two portraits, masterpieces by Madame Mirbel, richly set with pearls.

“Oh, how beautiful! Is it the lady to whom you wrote that—”

“No,” he smiled; “this is my mother, and here is my father, your aunt and uncle. Eugenie, I beg you on my knees, keep my treasure safely. If I die and your little fortune is lost, this gold and these pearls will repay you. To you alone could I leave these portraits; you are worthy to keep them. But destroy them at last, so they may pass into no other hands.” Eugenie was silent. “Ah, yes, say yes! You consent?” he added with winning grace.

She turned to him with a look of love—the first look of womanhood, half coquette, half depth. He kissed her hand. “Angel of purity! between us two money is nothing, never can be anything. Feeling and sentiment must be all henceforth.”

“You are like your mother—was her voice as soft as yours?”

“Oh! much softer—”

“Yes, for you,” she said, dropping her eyelids. “Come, Charles, go to bed; you must be tired. Good-night.” She disengaged her hand; he lighted her way to her room. At the threshold he murmured, “Ah! why am I ruined?”

“What matter?—my father is rich; I think so,” she answered.

“Poor child!” said Charles, stepping in and leaning against the wall, “if that were so, he would never have let my father die; he would not let you live in this poor way; he would live otherwise himself.”

“But he owns Froidfond.”

“What is Froidfond worth?”

“I don’t know; but he has Noyers.”

“Nothing but a poor farm!”

“He has vineyards and fields.”

“Mere nothing,” said Charles disdainfully. “If your father had only twenty-four thousand francs a year do you suppose you would live in this cold, barren room?” he added, edging forward. “Ah! there you will keep my treasures,” he said, glancing at the old cabinet.

“Go and sleep,” she said, hindering his entrance into the disordered room. They bade each other good-night with a mutual smile.

Both fell asleep in the same dream; from that moment the youth began to wear roses with his mourning. The next morning, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found her daughter in the garden with Charles. The young man was still sad, fitting for one plunged in misfortune and measuring his future. “My father will not be home till dinner-time,” Eugenie said, seeing her mother’s anxious face.

There was an unison of thought between her and her cousin. Charles spent the morning in the hall, his sadness respected. Everyone busied themselves with tasks—the cooper Grandet had left affairs unattended, and several people came on business: the plumber, mason, slater, carpenter, diggers, farmers—some to arrange repairs, others to pay or be paid. Nanon put away produce brought as tribute, always waiting for her master’s orders before deciding to use or sell. It was her custom, like many country servants, to conserve for the household rather than spend.

Toward five in the afternoon Grandet returned from Angers, having made fourteen thousand francs by the exchange on his gold, bringing treasury-notes bearing interest until he should invest them in the Funds. He had left Cornoiller at Angers to look after the horses, nearly foundered, with orders to bring them home slowly after they were rested.

“I have got back from Angers, wife,” he said; “I am hungry.”

Nanon called from the kitchen: “Haven’t you eaten anything since yesterday?”

“Nothing,” he answered. Nanon brought in the soup. Des Grassins, the banker, arrived to take his client’s orders just as the family sat down. Grandet had not even observed his nephew.

“Go on eating, Grandet,” said the banker; “we can talk. Do you know what gold is worth in Angers? They have come from Nantes after it? I shall send some of ours.”

“Don’t send any,” said Grandet; “they have got enough. We are such old friends, I ought to save you from such a loss of time.”

“But gold is worth thirteen francs fifty centimes.”

“Say was worth—”

“Where the devil have they got any?”

“I went to Angers last night,” answered Grandet in a low voice.

The banker started. A whispered conversation began between them, during which they frequently looked at Charles. Presently des Grassins gave a start of astonishment; probably Grandet was then instructing him to invest the sum that would yield him a hundred thousand francs a year in the Funds.

“Monsieur Grandet,” said the banker to Charles, “I am starting for Paris; if you have any commissions—”

“None, monsieur, I thank you,” answered Charles.

“Thank him better than that, nephew. Monsieur is going to settle the affairs of the house of Guillaume Grandet.”

“Is there any hope?” said Charles eagerly.

“What!” exclaimed his uncle with well-acted pride, “are you not my nephew? Your honor is ours. Is not your name Grandet?”

Charles rose, seized Pere Grandet, kissed him, turned pale, and left the room. Eugenie looked at her father with admiration. “Well, good-by, des Grassins; it is all in your hands. Decoy those people as best you can; lead ’em by the nose.”

The two men shook hands. The old cooper accompanied the banker to the front door. After closing it, he returned, plunged into his armchair, and said to Nanon, “Get me some black-currant ratafia.”

Too excited to sit long, he got up, looked at the portrait of Monsieur de la Bertelliere, and began to sing, performing what Nanon called his dancing steps—

“Dans les gardes francaises
J’avais un bon papa.”

Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie looked at each other in silence. The master’s hilarity always frightened them when it reached its climax. The evening soon passed. Pere Grandet chose to go to bed early, and when he went to bed everybody else was expected to do the same. Nanon, Charles, and Eugenie were as tired as the master. Madame Grandet lived always according to her husband’s will. During the two hours consecrated to digestion the cooper, more facetious than ever, uttered a number of his apothegms—one of which gives the measure of his mind. When he had drunk his ratafia he looked at his glass and said,—

“You have no sooner put your lips to a glass than it is empty! Such is life. You can’t have and hold. Gold won’t circulate and stay in your purse. If it were not for that, life would be too fine.”

He was jovial and benevolent. When Nanon came with her spinning-wheel, “You must be tired,” he said; “put away your hemp.”

“Ah, bah! then I shall get sleepy,” she answered.

“Poor Nanon! Will you have some ratafia?”

“I won’t refuse a good offer; madame makes it a deal better than the apothecaries. What they sell is all drugs.”

“They put too much sugar,” said the master; “you can’t taste anything else.”

Thus the household, thrown into motion by grief and the unforeseen consequences of a death, found itself reshaped in a night: a ruined young man resolved to leave France, a cousin’s guileless generosity converting relic-wealth into practical aid, and an old man’s sudden windfall from a shrewd operation in Angers that promised to set affairs in order. The scene closed on the intimate pledges exchanged between Eugenie and Charles and the calculated bustle of a household where money, prudence, and affection were already being recombined in new forms.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The following day the family gathered at eight for an early breakfast, presenting an image of domestic intimacy. Grief had united Madame Grandet, Eugenie, and Charles; even Nanon shared a sympathy she could not explain. The four formed one household. The old man, whose avarice and confidence that he would soon be rid of the dandy without paying more than the cost of his journey to Nantes made him almost indifferent, left the two young people—whom he called his children—to Madame Grandet’s care; he trusted her implicitly for public and religious morals. He occupied himself with straightening field boundaries, tending poplar plantations on the Loire, winter vineyard work, and affairs at Froidfond.

For Eugenie the springtime of love had arrived. Since the night she had confided her little treasure to her cousin, her heart followed it. United by the same secret, they exchanged looks and a mutual intelligence that deepened their communion, placing them above ordinary life. Their close relationship seemed to license gentleness and tender glances. Eugenie delighted in soothing Charles’s pain with the childish joys of new love. There are sweet resemblances between the birth of love and the birth of life: one rocks the babe with songs and soft looks, tells tales of a golden future, and hope spreads its wings. Childhood and first love were one for Eugenie and Charles; it was a first passion, playful and caressing because wrapped in sorrow. Struggling against the gloom of mourning, their love matched the plainness of the gray, ruined house. As they exchanged few words by the well, lingered in the garden at sunset on a mossy seat, or mused between the house and the ramparts as under church arches, Charles learned the sanctity of love; Annette had taught him only its stormy troubles. He left worldly passion for a pure love that even made him cherish the house and its customs. He rose early to speak with Eugenie before her father divided out provisions; when Grandet’s steps sounded he escaped to the garden. The small forbiddenness of these morning tête-à-têtes, which Nanon pretended not to see, lent their innocent love a charm of illicit joy.

After breakfast, when Grandet had gone to his fields, Charles stayed with the mother and daughter, finding pleasure in holding their skeins, watching them work, and listening to their quiet prattle. The simplicity of this half-monastic life, revealing the beauty of these souls, touched him. He had believed such morals possible only in Germany; even so, they seemed fabulous, like something out of La Fontaine. To him Eugenie became a Margaret of Goethe—before her fall. Day by day his words and looks enraptured the poor girl, who yielded with delicious non-resistance to the current of love; she seized her happiness as a swimmer grasps an overhanging branch to reach shore. Did dread of coming absence haunt those fleeting days? Ever some small thing reminded them of the impending parting.

Three days after des Grassins’ departure, Grandet took his nephew to the civil court, with the solemnity country people attach to legal acts, so Charles might sign a deed renouncing his rights in his father’s estate. Terrible renunciation! Charles also went before Maitre Cruchot to make two powers of attorney—one for des Grassins, another for the friend charged with selling his belongings. He took the formalities to obtain a passport; when he received mourning clothes from Paris he summoned the Saumur tailor and sold his redundant wardrobe. This last act delighted Grandet.

“Ah! now you look like a man prepared to embark and make your fortune,” he said when Charles appeared in a plain black surtout. “Good! very good!”

“I hope you will believe, monsieur,” Charles answered, “that I shall always try to conform to my situation.”

“What’s that?” Grandet asked, noticing a handful of gold Charles carried.

“Monsieur, I have collected all my buttons and rings and other superfluities which may have some value; not knowing anyone in Saumur, I wanted to ask you to—”

“To buy them?” interrupted Grandet.

“No, uncle; only to tell me of an honest man who—”

“Give them to me; I will estimate their value,” said Grandet, taking the mass of gold.

“Cousin,” said Grandet, offering Eugenie two buttons to fasten ribbons round her wrists, “that sort of bracelet is fashionable now.”

“I accept,” she answered with an understanding look.

“Aunt, here is my mother’s thimble; I have always kept it carefully,” said Charles, presenting a pretty gold thimble to Madame Grandet, who had longed for one.

“I cannot thank you; no words are possible, my nephew,” she said, tears in her eyes. “Night and morning in my prayers I shall add one for you, the most earnest of all—for those who travel. If I die, Eugenie will keep this treasure for you.”

“They are worth nine hundred and eighty-nine francs, seventy-five centimes,” said Grandet, opening the door. “To spare you the pain of selling them, I will advance the money—in livres.”

On the Loire littoral “livres” meant that crown prices of six livres were to be accepted as six francs without deduction.

“I dared not propose it,” said Charles; “but it was repugnant to me to sell my jewels to some second-hand dealer in your town. People should wash their dirty linen at home, as Napoleon said. I thank you for your kindness.”

Grandet scratched his ear; silence followed.

“My dear uncle,” resumed Charles, uneasy as if fearful of wounding him, “my aunt and cousin have accepted a trifling remembrance of me. Will you allow me to give you these sleeve-buttons, now useless? They will remind you of a poor fellow who, far away, will always think of those who are henceforth all his family.”

“My lad, my lad, you mustn’t rob yourself! Let me see, wife, what have you got?” he added, turning eagerly to her. “Ah! a gold thimble. And you, little girl? Diamond buttons? Yes, I’ll accept your present, nephew,” he answered, shaking Charles’s hand. “But—you must let me—pay—your—yes, your passage to the Indies. I wish to pay your passage because, d’ye see, my boy, in valuing your jewels I estimated only the weight of the gold; very likely the workmanship is worth something. So let us settle that I give you fifteen hundred francs—in livres; Cruchot will lend them to me. I haven’t got a copper farthing here—unless Perrotet, behindhand with his rent, should pay up. By the bye, I’ll go and see him.”

He took his hat, put on his gloves, and went out.

“Then you are really going?” Eugenie asked with a sad look mingled with admiration.

“I must,” he replied, bowing his head.

For days Charles’s bearing, manners, and speech took on the dignity of a man who, despite profound affliction, feels obligations and draws courage from misfortune. He no longer repined; he became a man. Eugenie never judged him more favorably than when she saw him in plain black that suited his pale face. That day the two women donned mourning, and all three attended a Requiem in the parish church for the late Guillaume Grandet.

At the second breakfast Charles received letters from Paris and began to read them.

“Well, cousin, are you satisfied with the management of your affairs?” Eugenie asked softly.

“Never ask such questions, my daughter,” said Grandet. “What the devil! do I tell you my affairs? Why do you poke your nose into your cousin’s? Let the lad alone!”

“Oh! I haven’t any secrets,” said Charles.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta, nephew; you’ll soon find out that you must hold your tongue in business.”

When the two lovers were alone in the garden, Charles drew Eugenie down on the old bench beneath the walnut-tree and said, “I did right to trust Alphonse; he has done famously. He has managed my affairs with prudence and good faith. I now owe nothing in Paris. All my things have been sold; he took advice of an old sea-captain and spent three thousand francs on a commercial outfit of European curiosities sure to be in demand in the Indies. He has sent my trunks to Nantes, where a ship is loading for San Domingo. In five days, Eugenie, we must bid each other farewell—perhaps forever, at least for years. My outfit and ten thousand francs, which two of my friends send me, are a very small beginning. I cannot hope to return for many years. My dear cousin, do not weigh your life in the scales with mine; I may perish; some good marriage may be offered to you—”

“Do you love me?” she asked.

“Oh, yes! indeed, yes!” he answered, with depth that revealed equal feeling.

“I shall wait, Charles—Good heavens! there is my father at his window,” she said, repulsing him as he leaned forward to kiss her.

She ran under the archway. Charles followed. At the foot of the staircase she opened the swing-door and, scarcely knowing where she was going, reached the corner near Nanon’s den, in the darkest end of the passage. There Charles caught her hand and drew her to his heart. Passing his arm about her waist, he made her lean upon him. Eugenie no longer resisted; she received and gave the purest, sweetest, most unreserved of kisses.

“Dear Eugenie, a cousin is better than a brother, for he can marry you,” said Charles.

“So be it!” cried Nanon, opening the door of her lair.

The two lovers, alarmed, fled into the hall, where Eugenie took up her work and Charles began to read the litanies of the Virgin in Madame Grandet’s prayer-book.

“Mercy!” cried Nanon, “now they’re saying their prayers.”

As soon as Charles announced his immediate departure, Grandet set himself to show interest. He became liberal of what cost him nothing; took pains to find a packer; declared the fee exorbitant; insisted on making the cases himself out of old planks; rose early to plane and nail strips into strong boxes in which he packed all Charles’s effects; he also sent them by boat down the Loire, insured them, and got them to Nantes in time.

After the kiss in the passage, hours fled for Eugenie with terrible rapidity. Sometimes she thought of following him. Those who have known that most endearing of passions—the one cut short by time, age, illness, and chance—will understand her torments. She wept as she walked in the garden, now so narrow; indeed the court, the house, the town seemed narrow. She launched in thought upon the wide sea he was about to cross. At last the eve of his departure came. That morning, in the absence of Grandet and Nanon, the case containing the two portraits was solemnly placed in the only drawer of the old cabinet that could be locked, where the now-empty velvet purse lay. This deposit was not made without many tears and kisses. When Eugenie placed the key within her bosom she had no courage to forbid the kiss with which Charles sealed the act.

“It shall never leave that place, my friend,” she said.

“Then my heart will be always there.”

“Ah! Charles, it is not right,” she said, as if reproaching him.

“Are we not married?” he asked. “I have thy promise—then take mine.”

“Thine; I am thine forever!” they said, repeating the words twice.

No promise on earth was purer. The innocent sincerity of Eugenie sanctified, for a moment, the young man’s love.

On the morrow the breakfast was sad. Nanon herself, despite the gold-embroidered robe and Jeannette cross bestowed by Charles, had tears in her eyes.

“The poor dear monsieur who is going on the seas—oh, may God guide him!”

At half-past ten the family set out to escort Charles to the diligence for Nantes. Nanon let loose the dog, locked the door, and insisted on carrying the young man’s carpet-bag. All the tradesmen in the tortuous old street were on their sills to watch the procession, which was joined in the market-place by Maitre Cruchot.

“Eugenie, be sure you don’t cry,” said her mother.

“Nephew,” Grandet said in the doorway of the inn from which the coach started, kissing Charles on both cheeks, “depart poor, return rich; you will find the honor of your father safe. I answer for that myself, I—Grandet; for it will only depend on you to—”

“Ah! my uncle, you soften the bitterness of my departure. Is it not the best gift that you could make me?”

Not understanding his uncle’s interrupted words, Charles shed tears of gratitude upon the tanned cheeks of the old miser, while Eugenie pressed her cousin’s and father’s hands with all her strength. The notary smiled, admiring the sly speech of the old man, which he alone had understood. The family stood about the coach until it started; then as it disappeared on the bridge and its rumble grew fainter, Grandet said:

“Good-by to you!”

Happily no one but Maitre Cruchot heard the exclamation. Eugenie and her mother had gone to a corner of the quay from which they could still see the diligence and wave their white handkerchiefs, to which Charles answered by displaying his. “Ah! mother, would that I had the power of God for a single moment,” Eugenie said when she could no longer see his handkerchief.

To follow the Grandet family’s affairs, one must glance at the operations the old man carried on in Paris through Monsieur des Grassins. A month after the latter’s departure from Saumur, Grandet obtained a certificate of a hundred thousand francs a year from an investment in the Funds, bought at eighty francs net. How he remitted the price and received the certificate was obscure; the inventory after his death shed no light on the methods his suspicious nature used. Maitre Cruchot thought that Nanon, perhaps unbeknownst to herself, served as the trusty instrument by which the money was transported; about this time she was absent five days under pretext of putting things to rights at Froidfond—as if the old miser were capable of leaving anything in disorder!

Regarding Guillaume Grandet’s estate, the Bank of France could offer exact information about large fortunes. The names des Grassins and Felix Grandet of Saumur were known there; they enjoyed the esteem of financial celebrities who owed their wealth to unencumbered territorial possessions. The Saumur banker’s arrival to liquidate Grandet of Paris honorably averted the shame of protested notes. The seals on the property were removed in the presence of creditors, and the notary employed by Grandet began the inventory. Des Grassins convened the creditors, who unanimously elected him, along with Francois Keller, head of a rich banking-house and a principal interested party, as liquidators, with full power to protect family honor and claimants’ interests. The credit of Grandet of Saumur and the hopes diffused by des Grassins eased the transactions. No single creditor proved recalcitrant; each said confidently, “Grandet of Saumur will pay.”

Six months passed. Parisians redeemed the notes as they became due and kept them locked—first result sought by the old cooper. Nine months after the preliminary meeting, the two liquidators distributed forty-seven percent to each creditor on his claim. This sum came from selling securities and property of the late Guillaume Grandet and was paid with scrupulous fidelity. Unimpeachable integrity seemed displayed, and creditors praised the Grandets. When praise circulated, the creditors asked for the rest. It became necessary to write a collective letter to Grandet of Saumur.

“Here it comes!” said the old man as he threw the letter into the fire. “Patience, my good friends!”

In reply, Grandet demanded that all vouchers for claims against his brother’s estate be deposited with a notary, together with acquittances for the forty-seven percent already paid; he insisted on this to sift accounts and determine the estate’s exact condition. The demand provoked difficulties. Creditors are capricious: one day lenient, another furious. Grandet had studied these variations and his brother’s creditors validated his calculations. Some were angry and refused to submit vouchers.

“Very good; so much the better,” Grandet said, rubbing his hands over des Grassins’ letter.

Others agreed only if their rights were fully guaranteed; they renounced nothing and reserved the power to force failure. Prolonged correspondence followed, ending with Grandet of Saumur agreeing to all conditions. By this concession placable creditors were able to bring the dissatisfied to reason. The deposit was then made, but not without complaint.

“Your goodman,” they said to des Grassins, “is tricking us.”

Twenty-three months after Guillaume Grandet’s death many creditors, occupied by pressing Paris business, had forgotten their claims or consoled themselves with, “I begin to believe that forty-seven percent is all I shall ever get.”

The old cooper had counted on the power of time, which, as he liked to say, is a pretty good devil. By the end of the third year des Grassins wrote that he had brought creditors to accept ten percent on the two million four hundred thousand francs still due by the house of Grandet. Grandet answered that the notary and broker whose failures caused his brother’s death were still living, that they might have recovered their credit, and should be sued to recover something toward the deficit.

By the end of the fourth year liabilities were definitively estimated at twelve hundred thousand francs. Many negotiations took place between creditors and liquidators, and between liquidators and Grandet. To make a long story short, anxious to end the affair, about the ninth month of the fourth year Grandet told the liquidators his nephew had made a fortune in the Indies and was disposed to pay his father’s debts in full; therefore he could not settle without consulting him; he had written and awaited an answer. The creditors were kept at bay until the middle of the fifth year by the promise of “payment in full,” a phrase the wily miser issued from time to time, smiling and cursing, “Those Parisians!”

But the creditors were fated to an unprecedented commercial fate; when events later brought them back into notice, they would still be in the position Grandet had resolved from the first.

As soon as the Funds reached one hundred and fifteen, Pere Grandet sold out and withdrew two million four hundred thousand francs in gold, to which he added six hundred thousand francs in compound interest. Des Grassins lived in Paris; first made a deputy, he became infatuated—despite being a married man and father—with a pretty actress at the theatre, known as Florine, and relapsed into old army habits. His conduct was considered profoundly immoral in Saumur. His wife, fortunate in having her property settled on herself and in possessing the ability to keep the Saumur banking-house, managed it in her name and repaired the breach caused by her husband’s extravagance. The Cruchotines talked of the quasi-widow’s position; she married her daughter badly and abandoned hope of an alliance between Eugenie Grandet and her son. Adolphe went to Paris and was said to be worthless. The Cruchots triumphed.

“Your husband hasn’t common sense,” Grandet said as he lent Madame des Grassins money on a note securely endorsed. “I am very sorry for you, for you are a good little woman.”

“Ah, monsieur,” she said, “who could have believed that when he left Saumur to go to Paris on your business he was going to his ruin?”

“Heaven is my witness, madame, that up to the last moment I did all I could to prevent him from going. Monsieur le president was most anxious to take his place; but he was determined to go, and now we all see why.”

Thus Grandet made it plain he felt under no obligation to des Grassins.

Women suffer more than men, for man can act, occupy himself, look forward, and find consolation in the future. Thus it was with Charles. The woman, however, stays at home; she is face to face with grief and often fills the abyss with tears and prayers. Thus did Eugenie. To feel, love, suffer, and devote oneself—this is often the sum of a woman’s life. Eugenie was to be a woman in all things except in the one thing that consoles for all. Her happiness, picked up like nails scattered on a wall—Bossuet’s simile—would never fill the hollow of her hand. Sorrows came quickly for her. The day after Charles’s departure the house resumed its ordinary aspect for everyone except Eugenie, to whom it grew suddenly empty. She wished, secretly, that Charles’s room be kept as he had left it. Madame Grandet and Nanon willingly aided this statu quo.

“Who knows but he may return sooner than we think?” she said.

“Ah, don’t I wish I could see him back!” answered Nanon. “I took to him! He was such a dear, sweet young man—pretty too, with his curly hair.” Eugenie looked at Nanon. “Holy Virgin! don’t look at me that way, mademoiselle; your eyes are like those of a lost soul.”

From that day Eugenie’s beauty took on a new character. The solemn thoughts of love that filled her soul and the dignity of the woman beloved gave her features an illumination painters render by a halo. Before her cousin, she might be compared to the Virgin before conception; after he had gone, she was like the Virgin Mother—she had given birth to love. These two Marys, so different, so well represented by Spanish art, embodied one of Christianity’s shining symbols.

Returning from Mass the morning after Charles’s departure—having vowed to attend daily—Eugenie bought a map of the world and nailed it beside her looking-glass so she might follow her cousin westward, imagine herself day by day on the ship that bore him, and ask a thousand questions: “Art thou well? Dost thou suffer? Dost thou think of me when the star, whose beauty and usefulness thou hast taught me to know, shines upon thee?” In the mornings she sat pensive beneath the walnut-tree on the worm-eaten bench where they had spoken so much, where they had built castles of a future home. She thought of the future as she looked at the small piece of sky the high walls allowed and then to the roof above his room. Her love was solitary and persistent, gliding into every thought until it became the fabric of life. When Pere Grandet’s would-be friends came in the evening for cards, she was gay and dissembling; but all morning she spoke of Charles to her mother and Nanon. Nanon had learned to pity her young mistress without failing in duty to the old master, and she would say to Eugenie,

“If I had a man for myself I’d follow him to hell; yes, I’d give my life for him; but I’ve none. I shall die and never know what life is. Would you believe, mamz’elle, that old Cornoiller (a good fellow all the same) is always round my petticoats for the sake of my money—just like the rats who come smelling after the master’s cheese and paying court to you? I see it all; I’ve got a shrewd eye, though I am as big as a steeple. Well, mamz’elle, it pleases me, but it isn’t love.”

Two months passed. Domestic life, once monotonous, quickened with the intense interest of a secret that bound the women together. For them Charles lived beneath the grim gray rafters of the hall. Night and morning Eugenie opened the dressing-case and gazed at her aunt’s portrait. One Sunday morning her mother discovered her absorbed in searching Charles’s features in his mother’s face. Madame Grandet was then for the first time drawn into the terrible secret: Charles had been given Eugenie’s treasure.

“You gave him all!” cried the poor mother, terrified. “What will you say to your father on New Year’s Day when he asks to see your gold?”

Eugenie’s eyes fixed; the two women lived through mortal terror for more than half the morning. They were so troubled they missed high Mass and only went to the military service. In three days 1819 would end. In three days a terrible drama would begin — a bourgeois tragedy without poison or dagger, but, regarding the actors, more cruel than any fabled horror in the house of Atreus.

“What will become of us?” said Madame Grandet, letting her knitting fall. She had been anxious for two months; the woollen sleeves she needed were unfinished. For want of those sleeves, a chill seized her amid a sweat caused by a terrible explosion of anger from her husband.

“I thought, my poor child, if you had confided in me we could have written to Monsieur des Grassins in Paris. He might have sent us gold pieces like yours; though Grandet knows them all, perhaps—”

“Where could we have got the money?” Eugenie answered.

“I would have pledged my property. Besides, Monsieur des Grassins would have—”

“It is too late,” said Eugenie in a broken voice. “Tomorrow morning we must go and wish him a happy New Year in his chamber.”

“But, my daughter, why not consult the Cruchots?”

“No, no; it would be delivering me up to them and putting ourselves in their power. Besides, I have chosen my course. I have done right and repent nothing. God will protect me. His will be done! Ah! mother, if you had read his letter, you would have thought only of him.”

January 1, 1820 dawned. The winter of 1819–1820 was one of the coldest of the epoch; snow lay thick on the roofs. The fear of Grandet’s inspection suggested a natural excuse to escape the formal entrance into his chamber.

Madame Grandet called when she heard him stirring. “Grandet, will you let Nanon light a fire here for me? The cold is so sharp I am freezing. Eugenie shall come and dress here; the poor child might get ill dressing in her cold room. Then we will go and wish you a happy New Year by the hall fire.”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta, what a tongue! a pretty way to begin the new year, Madame Grandet!” he answered. After a pause he consented, with a gruff jest about the Bertellieres, coughed, and entered his wife’s room fully dressed. “Yes, it is cold enough to freeze you. We shall have a fine breakfast, wife. Des Grassins has sent me a truffled pate-de-foie-gras! I am going now to get it at the coach-office. There’ll be a double napoleon for Eugenie in the package,” he whispered. “I have no gold left, wife. I had a few stray pieces—but I had to let them go in business.”

He kissed her on the forehead and went out. The two women breathed a little.

Nanon entered to light the fire. “First he said, ‘Good-morning; happy New Year, you big fool! Go and light my wife’s fire, she’s cold’; and then he gave me a six-franc piece, which isn’t worn one bit. Oh, the kind man! He is a good man, that’s a fact. He’s getting soft, improving with time, like your ratafia! He’s a good, good man—”

Grandet’s joy sprang from the complete success of his speculation. Monsieur des Grassins, after deducting what Grandet owed for discounting a hundred and fifty thousand francs in Dutch notes and for the surplus he had advanced to make up the sum required for an investment yielding a hundred thousand francs a year, had sent him thirty thousand francs in silver, the remainder of his first half-year’s interest, and informed him the Funds had risen. They were then quoted at eighty-nine; the shrewdest bought later at ninety-three. Grandet had thus gained twelve percent on his capital in two months; he had simplified accounts and would henceforth receive fifty thousand francs interest every six months without taxes or repair costs. He understood investing in public securities; at the end of five years he found himself master of six million francs capital, which increased without much effort and, joined to his land, made him enormously wealthy. The six francs for Nanon were perhaps the reward for some small service rendered unknowingly.

“Oh! oh! where’s Pere Grandet going? He has been scurrying about since sunrise,” said the tradespeople as they opened shops. When they saw him returning from the wharf followed by a porter with a barrow laden with sacks, they commented on his gold. “Silver sous,” whispered the porter. “If you want me to take care of you, keep your tongue between your teeth,” Grandet warned as they reached the door. He handed twenty sous to someone and bade them be off. The sacks were carried to his inner room, where he shut himself in. “When breakfast is ready, knock on the wall,” he said, then disappeared.

The family breakfasted at ten. “Your father will not ask to see your gold downstairs,” Madame Grandet hoped after Mass. “You must pretend to be very chilly. We may have time to replace the treasure before your fête-day.”

Grandet came down thinking of his speculation and wondering how to metamorphose his Parisian silver into solid gold; he resolved to invest everything until the Funds reached par. Fatal reverie for Eugenie. As soon as he came in, the two women wished him a happy New Year—his daughter by putting her arms round his neck and caressing him; Madame Grandet gravely.

“Ha! ha! my child,” he said, kissing both her cheeks. “I work for you, don’t you see? I think of your happiness. Must have money to be happy. Here! there’s a new napoleon for you. I sent to Paris for it. On my word of honor, it’s all the gold I have; you are the only one that has any gold. I want to see your gold, little one.”

“Oh! it is too cold; let us have breakfast,” Eugenie answered.

“Well, after breakfast, then; it will help digestion. That fat des Grassins sent me the pate. Eat as much as you like, my children, it costs nothing. Des Grassins is getting along very well. I am satisfied with him. The old fish is doing Charles a good service, and gratis too. He is making a very good settlement of that poor deceased Grandet’s business. Hoo! hoo!” he muttered with his mouth full, “how good it is! Eat, wife; that will feed you for at least two days.”

“I am not hungry. I am very poorly; you know that.”

“Ah, bah! you can stuff yourself as full as you please without danger, you’re a Bertelliere; they are all hearty. You are a bit yellow, that’s true; but I like yellow, myself.”

The expectation of ignominious and public scrutiny is perhaps less horrible to a condemned criminal than the anticipation of what was coming after breakfast to Madame Grandet and Eugenie. The more gleefully the old man talked and ate, the more their hearts shrank. Eugenie, however, had an inward prop—she drew strength through love.

“For him! for him!” she cried within her, “I would die a thousand deaths.”

At this thought she shot a glance at her mother that flamed with courage.

“Clear away,” Grandet said to Nanon about eleven, “but leave the table. We can spread your little treasure upon it,” he said, looking at Eugenie. “Little? Faith! no; it isn’t little. You possess, in actual value, five thousand nine hundred and fifty-nine francs and the forty I gave you just now. That makes six thousand francs, less one. Well, now see here, little one! I’ll give you that one franc to make up the round number. Hey! what are you listening for, Nanon? Mind your own business; go and do your work.”

Nanon disappeared.

“Now listen, Eugenie; you must give me back your gold. You won’t refuse your father, my little girl, hein?”

The two women were dumb.

“I have no gold myself. I had some, but it is all gone. I’ll give you in return six thousand francs in livres, and you are to put them just where I tell you. You mustn’t think anything more about your ‘dozen.’ When I marry you (which will be soon) I shall get you a husband who can give you the finest ‘dozen’ ever seen in the provinces. Now attend to me, little girl. There’s a fine chance for you; you can put your six thousand francs into government funds, and you will receive every six months nearly two hundred francs interest, without taxes, or repairs, or frost, or hail, or floods, or anything else to swallow up the money. Perhaps you don’t like to part with your gold, hey, my girl? Never mind, bring it to me all the same. I’ll get you some more like it—like those Dutch coins and the portugaises, the rupees of Mogul, and the genovines—I’ll give you some on your fête-days, and in three years you’ll have got back half your little treasure. What’s that you say? Look up, now. Come, go and get it, the precious metal. You ought to kiss me on the eyelids for telling you the secrets and the mysteries of the life and death of money. Yes, silver and gold live and swarm like men; they come, and go, and sweat, and multiply—”

Eugenie rose; but after a few steps toward the door she turned abruptly, looked her father in the face, and said,—

“I have not got my gold.”

“You have not got your gold!” cried Grandet, starting up like a horse that hears a cannon.

“No, I have not got it.”

“You are mistaken, Eugenie.”

“No.”

“By the shears of my father!”

Whenever the old man swore that oath the rafters trembled.

“Holy Virgin! Madame is turning pale,” cried Nanon.

“Grandet, your anger will kill me,” said the poor mother.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta! nonsense; you never die in your family! Eugenie, what have you done with your gold?” he cried, rushing upon her.

“Monsieur,” said the daughter, falling at Madame Grandet’s knees, “my mother is ill. Look at her; do not kill her.”

Grandet, frightened by his wife’s pallor, was momentarily softened. “Nanon, help me to bed,” she murmured; “I am dying—” Nanon gave her an arm, Eugenie another; with difficulty they got her upstairs; she fell with exhaustion at every step. Grandet remained. In a few moments he went up six or eight stairs and called,—

“Eugenie, when your mother is in bed, come down.”

“Yes, father.”

She soon came after reassuring her mother.

“My daughter,” Grandet said, “you will now tell me what you have done with your gold.”

“My father, if you make me presents of which I am not the sole mistress, take them back,” she answered coldly, picking up the napoleon from the chimney-piece and offering it to him.

Grandet seized the coin and slipped it into his breeches pocket. “I shall certainly never give you anything again. Not so much as that!” he said, clicking his thumb-nail against a front tooth. “Do you dare despise your father? have you no confidence in him? Don’t you know what a father is? If he is nothing for you, he is nothing at all. Where is your gold?”

“Father, I love and respect you, in spite of your anger; but I humbly ask you to remember I am twenty-three. You have told me I have attained my majority, and I do not forget it. I have used my money as I chose, and you may be sure it was put to a good use—”

“What use?”

“That is an inviolable secret,” she answered. “Have you no secrets?”

“I am the head of the family; I have my own affairs.”

“And this is mine.”

“It must be something bad if you can’t tell it to your father, Mademoiselle Grandet.”

“It is good, and I cannot tell it to my father.”

“At least you can tell me when you parted with your gold?”

Eugenie shook her head.

“You had it on your birthday, hein?”

She repeated the negative sign, crafty through love as he was through avarice.

“Was there ever such obstinacy! It’s a theft,” cried Grandet, his voice rising through the house. “What! here, in my own home, under my very eyes, somebody has taken your gold—the only gold we have—and I’m not to know who has got it! Gold is a precious thing. Virtuous girls go wrong sometimes, and give—I don’t know what; they do it among the great people, and even among the bourgeoisie. But give their gold—for you have given it to some one, hein?”

Eugenie was silent and impassive.

“Was there ever such a daughter? Is it possible that I am your father? If you have invested it anywhere, you must have a receipt—”

“Was I free—yes or no—to do as I would with my own? Was it not mine?”

“You are a child.”

“Of age.”

Dumbfounded by his daughter’s logic, Grandet turned pale and stamped. When at last he found words, he cried: “Serpent! Cursed girl! Ah, deceitful creature! You know I love you, and you take advantage of it. She’d cut her father’s throat! Good God! you’ve given our fortune to that ne’er-do-well—that dandy with morocco boots! By the shears of my father! I can’t disinherit you, but I curse you—you and your cousin and your children! Nothing good will come of it! Do you hear? If it was to Charles—but, no; it’s impossible. What! has that wretched fellow robbed me?—”

He looked at his daughter, who continued cold and silent.

“She won’t stir; she won’t flinch! She’s more Grandet than I’m Grandet! Ha! you have not given your gold for nothing? Come, speak the truth!”

Eugenie looked at her father with a sarcastic expression that stung him.

“Eugenie, you are here, in my house—in your father’s house. If you wish to stay here, you must submit to me. The priests tell you to obey me.” Eugenie bowed. “You affront me in all I hold most dear. I will not see you again until you submit. Go to your chamber. You will stay there till I give you permission to leave it. Nanon will bring you bread and water. You hear me—go!”

Eugenie burst into tears and fled to her mother. Grandet, after marching two or three times round the garden in the snow without heeding the cold, suspected his daughter had gone to her mother; only too happy to find her disobedient, he climbed the stairs with the agility of a cat and appeared in Madame Grandet’s room just as she was stroking Eugenie’s hair, the girl’s face hidden on her motherly bosom.

“Be comforted, my poor child,” she was saying; “your father will get over it.”

“She has no father!” said the old man. “Can it be you and I, Madame Grandet, who have given birth to such a disobedient child? A fine education—religious, too! Well! why are you not in your chamber? Come, to prison, to prison, mademoiselle!”

“Would you deprive me of my daughter, monsieur?” said Madame Grandet, now red with fever.

“If you want to keep her, carry her off! Clear out—out of my house, both of you! Thunder! where is the gold? what’s become of the gold?”

Eugenie rose, looked proudly at her father, and withdrew to her room. Grandet turned the key.

“Nanon,” he cried, “put out the fire in the hall.”

Then he sat down in an armchair beside his wife’s fire and said to her,—

“Undoubtedly she has given the gold to that miserable seducer, Charles, who only wanted our money.”

“I knew nothing about it,” she answered, turning away from his savage glances. “I suffer so much from your violence that I shall never leave this room, if I trust my presentiments, till I am carried out in my coffin. You ought to have spared me this suffering, monsieur—you, to whom I have caused no pain, I think. Your daughter loves you. I believe her to be as innocent as an unborn babe. Do not make her wretched. Revoke your sentence. The cold is very severe; you may give her some serious illness.”

“I will not see her, neither will I speak to her. She shall stay in her room, on bread and water, until she submits to her father. What the devil! shouldn’t a father know where the gold in his house has gone to? She owned the only rupees in France, perhaps, and the Dutch ducats and the genovines—”

“Monsieur, Eugenie is our only child; and even if she had thrown them into the water—”

“Into the water!” cried her husband; “into the water! You are crazy, Madame Grandet! What I have said is said; you know that well enough. If you want peace in this household, make your daughter confess, pump it out of her. Women understand how to do that better than we do. Whatever she has done, I sha’n’t eat her. Is she afraid of me? Even if she has plastered Charles with gold from head to foot, he is on the high seas, and nobody can get at him, hein!”

“But, monsieur—” Excited by the crisis and by her daughter’s fate, Madame Grandet suddenly observed a frightful movement of her husband’s wen, and, in replying, she changed her speech without changing tone—“But, monsieur, I have not more influence over her than you have. She has said nothing to me; she takes after you.”

“Tut, tut! Your tongue is hung in the middle this morning. Ta, ta, ta, ta! You are setting me at defiance, I do believe. I daresay you are in league with her.”

He looked fixedly at his wife.

“Monsieur Grandet, if you wish to kill me, you have only to go on like this. I tell you, monsieur—and if it were to cost me my life, I would say it—you do wrong by your daughter; she is more in the right than you are. That money belonged to her; she is incapable of making any but a good use of it, and God alone has the right to know our good deeds. Monsieur, I implore you, take Eugenie back into favor; forgive her. If you do this you will lessen the injury your anger has done me; perhaps you will save my life. My daughter! oh, monsieur, give me back my daughter!”

“I shall decamp,” he said; “the house is not habitable. A mother and daughter talking and arguing like that! Broooouh! Pouah! A fine New Year’s present you’ve made me, Eugenie.” He lectured on Charles’s worth, his morocco boots and airs, and the ruin that would come.

When the street-door was shut, Eugenie came out of her room and went to her mother.

“What courage you have had for your daughter’s sake!” she said.

“Ah! my child, see where forbidden things may lead us. You forced me to tell a lie.”

“I will ask God to punish only me.”

“Is it true,” cried Nanon, rushing in alarm, “that mademoiselle is to be kept on bread and water for the rest of her life?”

“What does that signify, Nanon?” said Eugenie tranquilly.

“Goodness! do you suppose I’ll eat frippe when the daughter of the house is eating dry bread? No, no!”

“Don’t say a word about all this, Nanon,” Eugenie begged.

“I’ll be as mute as a fish; but you’ll see!”

Grandet dined alone for the first time in twenty-four years.

“So you’re a widower, monsieur,” said Nanon; “it must be disagreeable to be a widower with two women in the house.”

“I did not speak to you. Hold your jaw, or I’ll turn you off! What is that I hear boiling in your saucepan on the stove?”

“It is grease I’m trying out.”

“There will be some company tonight. Light the fire.”

The Cruchots, Madame des Grassins, and her son arrived at the usual hour of eight, surprised to see neither Madame Grandet nor her daughter.

“My wife is not very well, and Eugenie is with her,” said the old wine-grower, whose face betrayed no emotion.

After an hour of idle conversation, Madame des Grassins, who had gone up to see Madame Grandet, came down, and all asked, “How is Madame Grandet?”

“Not at all well,” she answered; “her condition seems really alarming. At her age you ought to take every precaution, Papa Grandet.”

“We’ll see about it,” said the old man absently.

They all wished him good-night. When the Cruchots reached the street, Madame des Grassins said, “There is something going on at the Grandets. The mother is very ill without her knowing it. The girl’s eyes are red, as if she had been crying all day. Can they be trying to marry her against her will?”

When Grandet had gone to bed Nanon came softly to Eugenie’s room in her stockinged feet and showed her a pate baked in a saucepan.

“See, mademoiselle,” said the good soul, “Cornoiller gave me a hare. You eat so little that this pate will last you a full week; in such frosty weather it won’t spoil. You sha’n’t live on dry bread, I’m determined; it isn’t wholesome.”

“Poor Nanon!” said Eugenie, pressing her hand.

“I’ve made it downright good and dainty, and he never found it out. I bought the lard and the spices out of my six francs: I’m the mistress of my own money”; and she disappeared rapidly, fancying she heard Grandet.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For months the old wine-grower constantly visited his wife’s room, never mentioning his daughter, seeing her, or alluding to her. Madame Grandet stayed in her chamber and daily grew worse. Nothing softened the old man; he remained harsh, cold as granite. He carried on his business but ceased to stutter, spoke less, became more obdurate in transactions, and often erred in adding up his figures.

“Something is going on at the Grandets,” said the Grassinists and the Cruchotines.

“What has happened in the Grandet family?” became a fixed question at Saumur’s little evening-parties. Eugenie went to Mass escorted by Nanon. If Madame des Grassins spoke to her coming out of church, she answered evasively. After two months it became impossible to hide from the three Cruchots or Madame des Grassins that Eugenie was in confinement. Pretexts failed to explain her perpetual absence. Then, though it was impossible to discover who had betrayed the secret, all the town learned that since New Year’s day Mademoiselle Grandet had been kept in her room without fire, on bread and water, by her father’s orders, and that Nanon cooked little dainties and took them to her secretly at night. It was even known that the young woman could not see or care for her mother except at times when her father was out.

Grandet’s conduct was severely condemned. The town, remembering his treachery and hard-heartedness, more or less outlawed him. When he passed along the streets, people pointed and muttered. When his daughter came down the winding street, accompanied by Nanon, to Mass or Vespers, inhabitants ran to the windows and examined with intense curiosity the bearing of the rich heiress and her countenance, stamped by angelic gentleness and melancholy. Her imprisonment and her father’s condemnation were as nothing to her. Had she not a map of the world, the little bench, the garden, the angle of the wall? Did she not taste upon her lips the honey that love’s kisses left there? For a time she was unaware that the town talked about her, just as Grandet himself was. Pious and pure before God, her conscience and her love helped her suffer patiently her father’s wrath.

One deep grief silenced all others. Her mother, that gentle, tender creature, beautified by the inward light that shone as she approached the tomb, was perishing. Eugenie often reproached herself as the innocent cause of the slow malady wasting her away. This remorse, though soothed by her mother, bound her still closer to her love. Every morning, as soon as her father left the house, she went to her mother’s bedside, and Nanon brought her breakfast. The poor girl, sad and suffering through her mother’s pain, would turn her face to the old servant with a mute gesture, weeping, and yet not daring to speak of her cousin. Madame Grandet first found courage to say,—

“Where is he? Why does he not write?”

“Let us think about him, mother, but not speak of him. You are ill—you, before all.”

“All” meant “him.”

“My child,” said Madame Grandet, “I do not wish to live. God protects me and enables me to look with joy to the end of my misery.”

Every utterance of this woman was unfalteringly pious and Christian. Sometimes, during the first months of the year, when her husband came to breakfast and tramped about the room, she would speak a few religious words, always with angelic sweetness, yet with the firmness of one to whom approaching death lends courage she had lacked in life.

“Monsieur, I thank you for the interest you take in my health,” she would answer when he made some commonplace inquiry; “but if you really desire to render my last moments less bitter and ease my grief, take back your daughter: be a Christian, a husband, and a father.”

When he heard these words, Grandet would sit by the bed with the air of a man who sees rain coming and quietly shelters himself till it is over. After these touching, tender, religious supplications, he would say,—

“You are rather pale to-day, my poor wife.”

Absolute forgetfulness of his daughter seemed graven on his stony brow, on his closed lips. He was unmoved by the tears that flowed down his unhappy wife’s white cheeks as she listened to his meaningless answers.

“May God pardon you,” she said, “even as I pardon you! You will some day stand in need of mercy.”

Since Madame Grandet’s illness he had not dared to use his terrible “Ta, ta, ta, ta!” Yet his despotic nature was not disarmed by this angel of gentleness, whose ugliness daily decreased, driven out by the expression of moral qualities which shone upon her face. She was all soul. The spirit of prayer seemed to purify her and refine those homely features and make them luminous. This transformation wrought by the struggle that consumed the last shreds of her life did somewhat affect the old cooper, though feebly—for his nature was iron; if his language ceased to be contemptuous, an imperturbable silence, which saved his dignity as master, took its place and ruled his conduct.

When the faithful Nanon appeared in the market, many quips and complaints about the master whistled in her ears; but however loudly public opinion condemned Monsieur Grandet, the old servant defended him for the honor of the family.

“Well!” she would say to his detractors, “don’t we all get hard as we grow old? Why shouldn’t he? Stop telling lies. Mademoiselle lives like a queen. She’s alone, that’s true; but she likes it. Besides, my masters have good reasons.”

At last, towards the end of spring, Madame Grandet, worn out by grief even more than by illness, having failed, in spite of her prayers, to reconcile father and daughter, confided her troubles to the Cruchots.

“Keep a girl of twenty-three on bread and water!” cried Monsieur de Bonfons; “without any reason, too! That constitutes wrongful cruelty; she can contest, as much in as upon—”

“Come, nephew, spare us your legal jargon,” said the notary. “Set your mind at ease, madame; I will put a stop to such treatment to-morrow.”

Eugenie, hearing herself mentioned, came out of her room.

“Gentlemen,” she said with a proud step, “I beg you not to interfere. My father is master in his own house. As long as I live under his roof I must obey him. His conduct is not subject to the world’s approbation or disapprobation; he is accountable to God only. I appeal to your friendship to keep total silence in this affair. To blame my father is to attack our family honor. I thank you for your interest; you will do me an additional service if you put a stop to the offensive rumors current in the town, of which I am accidentally informed.”

“She is right,” said Madame Grandet.

“Mademoiselle, the best way to stop such rumors is to procure your liberty,” answered the old notary respectfully, struck with the beauty which seclusion, melancholy, and love had stamped upon her face.

“Well, my daughter, let Monsieur Cruchot manage the matter if he is so sure of success. He understands your father, and how to manage him. If you wish to see me happy for my few remaining days, you must, at any cost, be reconciled to your father.”

On the morrow Grandet, following a custom since Eugenie’s imprisonment, took a certain number of turns in the little garden; he chose the hour when Eugenie brushed and arranged her hair. When the old man reached the walnut-tree he hid behind its trunk and remained a few moments watching his daughter’s movements, hesitating between his obstinacy and his natural desire to embrace his child. Sometimes he sat on the rotten bench where Charles and Eugenie had vowed eternal love; she, too, looked at her father secretly in the mirror before which she stood. If he rose and continued his walk, she sat obligingly at the window and looked at the angle of the wall where pale flowers hung, where Venus-hair grew from crevices with bindweed and sedum. Maitre Cruchot came early and found the old wine-grower sitting in the fine June weather on the little bench, his back against the division wall, watching his daughter.

“What may you want, Maitre Cruchot?” he said, perceiving the notary.

“I came to speak on business.”

“Ah! ah! have you brought some gold in exchange for my silver?”

“No, not about money; it is about your daughter Eugenie. All the town is talking of her and you.”

“What does the town meddle for? A man’s house is his castle.”

“Very true; and a man may kill himself if he likes, or, what is worse, he may fling his money into the gutter.”

“What do you mean?”

“Why, your wife is very ill, my friend. You ought to consult Monsieur Bergerin; she is likely to die. If she does die without proper care you will not be easy, I take it.”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta! you know a deal about my wife! These doctors, if they once get their foot in, will come five and six times a day.”

“Of course you will do as you think best. We are old friends; there is no one in Saumur who takes more interest in what concerns you. I was bound to tell you this. However, happen what may, you have the right to choose your own course. Besides, that is not what brings me here. There is another thing which may have serious results for you. After all, you can’t wish to kill your wife; her life is too important to you. Think of your situation in connection with your daughter if Madame Grandet dies. You must render an account to Eugenie, because you enjoy your wife’s estate only during her lifetime. At her death your daughter can claim a division of property, and she may force you to sell Froidfond. In short, she is her mother’s heir, and you are not.”

These words fell like a thunderbolt on the old man, who was not as wise about law as in business. He had never thought of a legal division.

“Therefore I advise you to treat her kindly,” added Cruchot.

“But do you know what she has done, Cruchot?”

“What?” asked the notary, curious to hear the truth and find the cause of the quarrel.

“She has given away her gold!”

“Well, wasn’t it hers?” said the notary.

“They all tell me that!” exclaimed the old man, letting his arms fall with a movement truly tragic.

“Are you going—for a mere nothing,” resumed Cruchot, “to put obstacles in the way of concessions which you will be obliged to ask from your daughter as soon as her mother dies?”

“Do you call six thousand francs a mere nothing?”

“Hey! my old friend, do you know what the inventory of your wife’s property will cost, if Eugenie demands division?”

“How much?”

“Two, three, four thousand francs, perhaps! The property would have to be put up at auction and sold to get its actual value. Instead of that, if you are on good terms with—”

“By the shears of my father!” cried Grandet, turning pale as he suddenly sat down, “we will see about it, Cruchot.”

After a moment’s silence, full of anguish perhaps, the old man looked at the notary and said,—

“Life is very hard! It has many griefs! Cruchot,” he continued solemnly, “you would not deceive me? Swear to me upon your honor that all you’ve told me is legally true. Show me the law; I must see the law!”

“My poor friend,” said the notary, “don’t I know my own business?”

“Then it is true! I am robbed, betrayed, killed, destroyed by my own daughter!”

“It is true that your daughter is her mother’s heir.”

“Why do we have children? Ah! my wife, I love her! Luckily she’s sound and healthy; she’s a Bertelliere.”

“She has not a month to live.”

Grandet struck his forehead, went a few steps, came back, cast a dreadful look at Cruchot, and said,—

“What can be done?”

“Eugenie can relinquish her claim to her mother’s property. Should she do this you would not disinherit her, I presume?—but if you want such a settlement, you must not treat her harshly. What I am telling you, old man, is against my own interests. What do I live by, if it isn’t liquidations, inventories, conveyances, divisions of property?—”

“We’ll see, we’ll see! Don’t let’s talk any more about it, Cruchot; it wrings my vitals. Have you received any gold?”

“No; but I have a few old louis, a dozen or so, which you may have. My good friend, make it up with Eugenie. Don’t you know all Saumur is pelting you with stones?”

“The scoundrels!”

“Come, the Funds are at ninety-nine. Do be satisfied for once in your life.”

“At ninety-nine! Are they, Cruchot?”

“Yes.”

“Hey, hey! Ninety-nine!” repeated the old man, accompanying the notary to the street-door. Too agitated to stay, he went up to his wife’s room and said,—

“Come, mother, you may have your daughter to spend the day with you. I’m going to Froidfond. Enjoy yourselves, both of you. This is our wedding-day, wife. See! here are sixty francs for your altar at the Fete-Dieu; you’ve wanted one for a long time. Come, cheer up, enjoy yourself, and get well! Hurrah for happiness!”

He threw ten silver pieces of six francs each upon the bed, and took his wife’s head between his hands and kissed her forehead.

“My good wife, you are getting well, are you not?”

“How can you think of receiving the God of mercy in your house when you refuse to forgive your daughter?” she said with emotion.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta!” said Grandet in a coaxing voice. “We’ll see about that.”

“Merciful heaven! Eugenie,” cried the mother, flushing with joy, “come and kiss your father; he forgives you!”

But the old man had disappeared. He was going as fast as his legs could carry him toward his vineyards, trying to arrange his confused ideas. Grandet had entered his seventy-sixth year. During the last two years his avarice had increased, as persistent passions do at a certain age. As with other monomaniacs, his affections had fastened on one special symbol—gold. The sight and possession of gold had become a monomania. His despotic spirit had grown with his avarice, and to part with any control of his property at his wife’s death seemed to him “against nature.” To declare his fortune to his daughter, to inventory his property for division—

“Why,” he cried aloud in the midst of a field where he pretended to examine a vine, “it would be cutting my throat!”

He returned to Saumur in time for dinner, resolved to unbend to Eugenie and coax her, that he might die regally, holding the reins of his millions so long as he had breath. As he entered the house with his pass-key and climbed the stair stealthily to his wife’s room, Eugenie had brought the beautiful dressing-case from the oak cabinet and placed it on her mother’s bed. Mother and daughter, in Grandet’s absence, allowed themselves the pleasure of looking for a likeness to Charles in the portrait of his mother.

“It is exactly his forehead and his mouth,” Eugenie was saying as the old man opened the door. At the look which his glance cast upon the gold, Madame Grandet cried,—

“O God, have pity upon us!”

The old man sprang upon the box as a famished tiger might spring upon a sleeping child.

“What’s this?” he said, snatching the treasure and carrying it to the window. “Gold, good gold! All gold—it weighs two pounds! Ha, ha! Charles gave you that for your money, did he? Hein! Why didn’t you tell me so? It was a good bargain, little one! Yes, you are my daughter, I see that—” Eugenie trembled in every limb. “This came from Charles, of course, didn’t it?” continued the old man.

“Yes, father; it is not mine. It is a sacred trust.”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta! He took your fortune, and now you can get it back.”

“Father!”

Grandet took his knife to pry out some of the gold; he placed the dressing-case on a chair. Eugenie sprang forward to recover it; but her father, who had his eye on her and on the treasure, pushed her back so violently that she fell upon her mother’s bed.

“Monsieur, monsieur!” cried the mother, lifting herself.

Grandet had opened his knife and was about to apply it to the gold.

“Father!” cried Eugenie, falling on her knees and dragging herself close to him with clasped hands, “father, in the name of all the saints and the Virgin! in the name of Christ who died upon the cross! in the name of your eternal salvation, father! for my life’s sake, father—do not touch that! It is neither yours nor mine. It is a trust placed in my hands by an unhappy relation: I must give it back to him uninjured!”

“If it is a trust, why were you looking at it? To look is as bad as touching it.”

“Father, don’t destroy it, or you will disgrace me! Father, do you hear?”

“Oh, have pity!” said the mother.

“Father!” cried Eugenie in so startling a voice that Nanon ran upstairs terrified. Eugenie sprang upon a knife that was close at hand.

“Well, what now?” said Grandet coldly, with a callous smile.

“Oh, you are killing me!” said the mother.

“Father, if your knife so much as cuts a fragment of that gold, I will stab myself with this one! You have already driven my mother to her death; you will now kill your child! Do as you choose! Wound for wound!”

Grandet held his knife over the dressing-case and hesitated as he looked at his daughter.

“Are you capable of doing it, Eugenie?” he said.

“Yes, yes!” said the mother.

“She’ll do it if she says so!” cried Nanon. “Be reasonable, monsieur, for once in your life.”

The old man looked at the gold and then at his daughter alternately for an instant. Madame Grandet fainted.

“There! don’t you see, monsieur, that madame is dying?” cried Nanon.

“Come, come, my daughter, we won’t quarrel for a box! Here, take it!” he cried hastily, flinging the case upon the bed. “Nanon, go and fetch Monsieur Bergerin! Come, mother,” said he, kissing his wife’s hand, “it’s all over! There! we’ve made up—haven’t we, little one? No more dry bread; you shall have all you want—Ah, she opens her eyes! Well, mother, little mother, come! See, I’m kissing Eugenie! She loves her cousin, and she may marry him if she wants; she may keep his case. But don’t die, mother; live a long time yet, my poor wife! Come, try to move! Listen! you shall have the finest altar that ever was made in Saumur.”

“Oh, how can you treat your wife and daughter so!” said Madame Grandet in a feeble voice.

“I won’t do so again, never again,” cried her husband; “you shall see, my poor wife!” He went to his inner room and returned with a handful of louis, which he scattered on the bed. “Here, Eugenie! see, wife! all these are for you,” he said, fingering the coins. “Come, be happy, wife! feel better, get well; you sha’n’t want for anything, nor Eugenie either. Here’s a hundred louis d’or for her. You won’t give these away, will you, Eugenie, hein?”

Madame Grandet and her daughter looked at each other in astonishment.

“Take back your money, father; we ask for nothing but your affection.”

“Well, well, that’s right!” he said, pocketing the coins; “let’s be good friends! We will all go down to dinner to-day, and we’ll play loto every evening for two sous. You shall both be happy. Hey, wife?”

“Alas! I wish I could, if it would give you pleasure,” said the dying woman; “but I cannot rise.”

“Poor mother,” said Grandet, “you don’t know how I love you! and you too, my daughter!” He took her in his arms and kissed her. “Oh, how good it is to kiss a daughter when we have been angry with her! There, mother, don’t you see it’s all over now? Go and put that away, Eugenie,” he added, pointing to the case. “Go, don’t be afraid! I shall never speak of it again, never!”

Monsieur Bergerin, the celebrated doctor of Saumur, presently arrived. After an examination, he told Grandet positively that his wife was very ill; but that perfect peace of mind, a generous diet, and great care might prolong her life until the autumn.

“Will all that cost much?” said the old man. “Will she need medicines?”

“Not much medicine, but a great deal of care,” answered the doctor, who could scarcely restrain a smile.

“Now, Monsieur Bergerin,” said Grandet, “you are a man of honor, are you not? I trust to you! Come and see my wife how and when you think necessary. Save my good wife! I love her,—don’t you see?—though I never talk about it; I keep things to myself. I’m full of trouble. Troubles began when my brother died; I have to spend enormous sums on his affairs in Paris. Why, I’m paying through my nose; there’s no end to it. Adieu, monsieur! If you can save my wife, save her. I’ll spare no expense, not even if it costs me a hundred or two hundred francs.”

In spite of Grandet’s fervent wishes for his wife’s health, in spite of the consideration he now showed and the tender care Eugenie lavished, Madame Grandet rapidly approached her end. Every day she grew weaker and visibly wasted, as women of her age attacked by serious illness do. She was fragile as autumn foliage; the radiance of heaven shone through her as the sun gilds withering leaves. It was a death worthy of her life—and is not that sublime? In October, 1822, her virtues, angelic patience, and love for her daughter seemed to find special expression; then she passed away without a murmur. Lamb without spot, she went to heaven, regretting only the sweet companion of her cold and dreary life, for whom her last glance seemed to prophesy a destiny of sorrows. She shrank from leaving her ewe-lamb, white as herself, alone in the midst of a selfish world that sought to strip her of her fleece and grasp her treasures.

“My child,” she said as she expired, “there is no happiness except in heaven; you will know it some day.”

The day after his death Eugenie felt a renewed attachment to the house of her birth, where she had suffered so much and where her mother had just died. She could not look at the window or the chair on its castors without weeping. She had believed herself mistaken in thinking her old father cold, for now he lavished tender care on her. He came in the morning, gave her his arm at breakfast, looked at her kindly and brooded over her as though she were something precious. The old man trembled so before his daughter that Nanon and the Cruchotines attributed it to age and feared his faculties were failing. Yet when the family put on mourning and Maitre Cruchot (the only one privy to his secret) had been invited, the old miser’s behavior was explained.

After dinner, the old man closed the doors and began: “My dear child, you are now your mother’s heiress, and we have a few little matters to settle between us. Isn’t that so, Cruchot?” The notary outlined the issue: the estate and ready money were undivided between Eugenie and her father; to avoid inventory, taxes, and division, the father proposed that Eugenie renounce her rights to her mother’s property and leave the use and disposition, during his lifetime, to him, who would guarantee her capital. Eugenie, childlike and bewildered, asked for the deed to sign. The father, overcome, offered instead a simpler bargain: if she would simply renounce her rights and trust him, he would pay her a hundred francs a month. “See,” he argued, “you could pay for as many masses as you want for anybody—a hundred francs a month.” Eugenie agreed.

Cruchot tried to warn her she would be despoiling herself without guarantee. Grandet silenced him: “Hold your tongue, Cruchot! It’s settled, all settled,” he cried, taking his daughter’s hand, striking it, and demanding, “Eugenie, you won’t go back on your word?—you are an honest girl, hein?” He kissed and embraced her until she was nearly choked. “Go, my good child, you restore your father’s life; but you only return to him that which he gave you: we are quits. This is how business should be done. Life is a business.” To-morrow, he added to the horrified notary, the deed of relinquishment would be prepared and entered on the court records.

The next morning Eugenie signed the papers completing her own spoliation. Even so, in the first year, despite his bargain, the old man had not paid her one sou of the hundred francs. When she reminded him, he colored, went to his secret hiding-place, brought down about a third of the jewels he had taken from his nephew, and threw them into her apron. “There, little one,” he said sarcastically, “do you want those for your twelve hundred francs?” Eugenie, overcome, thanked him with childish joy. “I’ll give you as many more next year,” he said, delighted to observe her feelings, and rubbed his hands.

Though still robust, he deemed it necessary to initiate her into the secrets of his thrift. For two years he had her order the household meals in his presence and receive the rents; he taught her the names and yields of his vineyards and farms. By the third year his avaricious methods had become her habits, and he could leave the household keys in her charge without anxiety, installing her as mistress of the house.

Five years passed with no event to relieve Eugenie’s monotonous existence. The same actions were performed daily with clockwork regularity. Her deep sadness was known to all, but if others guessed the cause she never defended herself. Her only society was the three Cruchots and a few of their friends, who had gradually insinuated themselves into the Grandet household. They taught her to play whist and came every night; in 1827, perceiving his infirmities, the old man further instructed her in managing his landed property and told her to call on Maitre Cruchot in difficulty.

Towards the end of that year the old man, then eighty-two, was seized by a rapidly advancing paralysis. Dr. Bergerin gave him up. Charles was absent; Eugenie, feeling she would soon be left alone, clung more tightly to the last living link of affection. To her, love was life. She devoted all her care to him. His faculties weakened but his avarice remained instinctive. He had his chair placed facing the door of the secret room, asked for explanation of every noise, and woke at day rents or when accounts had to be settled. He made his daughter open the inner room, watched while she placed the bags of money in his secret receptacle, and fingered the key in his waistcoat pocket.

His friend the notary, sure that the heiress would marry his nephew the president if Charles did not return, redoubled his attentions: he took Grandet’s orders, ran errands to Froidfond, sold the vintages, and turned everything into gold and silver to be carried in sacks to the hiding-place.

At last the last struggle came. The old man rolled up his coverings and said to Nanon, “Put them away, lock them up, for fear they should be stolen.” So long as he could open his eyes his whole being fixed on the door behind which his treasures lay, asking in a tone of panic, “Are they there? are they there?” Nanon reassured him: “Yes, my father.” “Take care of the gold—put gold before me.” Eugenie spread coins on a table before him, and he sat for hours with his eyes fixed on them, like a child newly seeing. “It warms me!” he sometimes said. When the priest came to give the last sacraments the sight of the crucifix and silver vessels kindled his eyes; he made a frightful gesture as if to seize the cross, and the effort cost him his life. He called Eugenie, whom he did not see though she knelt beside him bathing his stiffening hand. “My father, bless me!” she entreated. “Take care of it all. You will render me an account yonder!” he said, proving by these last words that Christianity must always be the religion of misers.

Eugenie Grandet was now alone in that gray house, with none but Nanon to whom she could turn with certainty of being heard and understood—Nanon, the single being who loved her for herself and with whom she could speak of her sorrows. La Grande Nanon was a providence; not merely a servant but a humble friend. After her father’s death Eugenie learned from Maitre Cruchot that she possessed three hundred thousand francs of income from landed and personal property in the arrondissement of Saumur; six millions invested at three percent in the Funds (bought at sixty, then worth seventy-six francs); two millions in gold coin, and a hundred thousand francs in silver crown-pieces, besides unpaid interest. The sum total reached seventeen millions.

“Where is my cousin?” was her sole thought. When Maitre Cruchot handed her a clear schedule of the whole inheritance, Eugenie sat with Nanon by the vacant hall fireplace, where everything now was memory—from her mother’s chair on castors to the glass from which her cousin had drunk. “Nanon, we are alone—” she said. “Yes, mademoiselle; and if I knew where he was, the darling, I’d go on foot to find him.” Eugenie sighed: “The ocean is between us.” While she wept with the old servant in that cold, dark house that was to her the universe, the whole province from Nantes to Orléans rang with the news of her seventeen millions.

Among her first acts she settled an annuity of twelve hundred francs on Nanon, who, already possessed of six hundred more, became a rich and enviable match. Less than a month later that good soul passed from single life to wedded life under the protection of Antoine Cornoiller, appointed keeper of Mademoiselle Grandet’s estates. Madame Cornoiller, though fifty-nine, looked not more than forty; her strong features resisted time. Thanks to semi-conventual habits she laughed at old age with rosy skin and iron constitution. Perhaps she never looked so well as on her marriage-day. Big, fat, and strong, she gave an appearance of happiness that made Cornoiller’s bargain envied.

On her way to church, Nanon, beloved by the neighborhood, received many compliments. Eugenie had given her three dozen silver forks and spoons as a wedding present. Cornoiller, amazed, spoke of his mistress with tears in his eyes; he would willingly have been hacked in pieces for her. Madame Cornoiller, appointed housekeeper to Mademoiselle Grandet, took as much happiness from her new function as from her husband: she took charge of weekly accounts, locked up provisions and gave them out daily in the old manner; she ruled two servants, a cook and a maid to mend linen and make dresses. Cornoiller combined the functions of keeper and bailiff. The women-servants chosen were “perfect treasures.” Thus Mademoiselle Grandet had four devoted servants. The farmers perceived no change; the usages established by Monsieur Grandet were scrupulously carried out by Monsieur and Madame Cornoiller.

At thirty Eugenie knew none of life’s joys. Her pale, sad childhood had glided beside a mother always wounded by life’s bereavements. Her mother, leaving life joyfully, pitied her daughter and left in her some fugitive remorse and many lasting regrets. Eugenie’s first and only love had been a wellspring of sorrow. Meeting her lover for a few days she had given him her heart between two kisses; then he left and a whole world lay between them. This love, cursed by her father, had cost her mother’s life and brought Eugenie only suffering and frail hopes. Her upward spring toward happiness had wasted her strength and given nothing in exchange. The life of the soul needs to absorb another’s sentiments and assimilate them; without that respiration the heart perishes. Eugenie had begun to languish. Wealth was neither power nor consolation; she could not live except through love, religion, and faith in the future. Love explained to her the mysteries of eternity. Her heart and the Gospel taught her to know two worlds; she bathed in two infinite thoughts which may have had but one meaning. She drew back within herself, loving and believing herself beloved. For seven years her passion invaded everything. Her treasuries were not the millions but Charles’s dressing-case, the portraits above her bed, the jewels recovered from her father and spread upon wool in a drawer, the thimble of her aunt used by her mother, which she wore while embroidering—a Penelope’s web that presented that gold upon her finger.

It seemed unlikely Mademoiselle Grandet would marry during mourning. Her genuine piety was known. The Cruchots, guided by an old abbé, contented themselves with surrounding the heiress and paying affectionate attentions. Every evening the hall filled with devoted Cruchotines who sang the praises of their mistress in every key. She had her doctor in ordinary, her grand almoner, her chamberlain, her first lady of honor, her prime minister; above all, her chancellor, a chancellor who would fain have said much to her. If she had wished for a train-bearer, one would have been found. She was a queen, obsequiously flattered. Flattery never comes from noble souls; it is the gift of little minds who belittle themselves to worm into favor. So the people who nightly assembled in Mademoiselle Grandet’s house (they called her Mademoiselle de Froidfond) outdid each other in expressions of admiration. The novelty made her blush; but insensibly she grew accustomed to praise, and if any newcomer seemed to find her plain she would have felt the reproach far more than she might have eight years earlier. She ended by loving the incense she secretly laid at the feet of her idol. By degrees she grew used to being treated as a sovereign and to seeing her court press around her each evening.

Monsieur de Bonfons was the hero of the little circle: his wit, person, education, and amiability were perpetually praised. One or another remarked that in seven years he had largely increased his fortune; Bonfons brought in at least ten thousand francs a year, and was surrounded, like other possessions of the Cruchots, by the vast domains of the heiress. “Do you know, mademoiselle,” said a habitual visitor, “that the Cruchots have an income of forty thousand francs among them!” “And then, their savings!” exclaimed Mademoiselle de Gribeaucourt. “A gentleman from Paris has offered Monsieur Cruchot two hundred thousand francs for his practice,” said another. “He will sell it if appointed juge de paix.” “He wants to succeed Monsieur de Bonfons as president of the Civil courts, and is taking measures,” replied Madame d’Orsonval. “Monsieur le président will certainly be made councillor.” “Yes, he is a very distinguished man,” said another—“don’t you think so, mademoiselle?”

Monsieur de Bonfons tried to keep pace with the role he sought. In spite of forty years and withered features, he dressed in youthful fashions, toyed with a bamboo cane, avoided snuff in Mademoiselle de Froidfond’s house, and came in a white cravat and a pleated shirt frill, giving him a family resemblance to turkeys. He addressed the heiress familiarly as “Our dear Eugenie.” Except for more visitors, the change from loto to whist, and the absence of Monsieur and Madame Grandet, the scene remained as when the story opened. The pack still pursued Eugenie and her millions; but the hounds, more numerous, lay better on the scent and beset the prey more unitedly. If Charles had dropped from the Indian Isles he would have found the same people and the same interests. Madame des Grassins, to whom Eugenie was courteous, still tormented the Cruchots. Eugenie remained the central figure; and Charles, as before, would still have been sovereign. Yet there had been small progress. The flowers which the president formerly presented on her birthdays had become a daily institution. Every evening he brought the rich heiress a huge bouquet, which Madame Cornoiller placed conspicuously in a vase and secretly threw into a corner of the courtyard when the visitors had departed.

Early in spring Madame des Grassins tried to disturb the Cruchotines by speaking to Eugenie of the Marquis de Froidfond, whose ancient and ruined family might be restored if the heiress returned his estates through marriage. Madame des Grassins rang the changes on peerage and the marquise title until, mistaking Eugenie’s disdainful smile for acquiescence, she went about proclaiming that the marriage with “Monsieur Cruchot” was not as certain as people thought. “Though Monsieur de Froidfond is fifty,” she said, “he does not look older than Monsieur Cruchot. He is a widower and has children, that’s true. But then he is a marquis; he will be peer of France; and in times like these where will you find a better match? I know for a fact that Pere Grandet, when he put all his money into Froidfond, intended to graft himself upon that stock; he often told me so. He was a deep one, that old man!”

“Ah! Nanon,” Eugenie asked one night as she went to bed, “how is it that in seven years he has never once written to me?”

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles was making his fortune in the Indies. His commercial outfit sold well; he realized six thousand dollars. Crossing the Line cleared many cobwebs from his brain; he perceived that the surest way to fortune in the tropics, as in Europe, was to buy and sell men. He went to the African coast and bought Negroes, combining that traffic with other profitable merchandise. He plunged into the business tirelessly, driven by the desire to return to Paris with the prestige of a large fortune and the hope of regaining a more brilliant position than the one he had lost.

Jostling with men, traveling through many lands, and observing conflicting customs modified his ideas and made him skeptical. He ceased to have fixed principles, since what one country called crime another praised as virtue. In the perpetual struggle of selfish interests his heart grew cold. The blood of the Grandets did its work: Charles became hard and eager for prey. He trafficked in Chinamen, Negroes, bird-nests, children, artists; practiced usury on a large scale; and, by habitually defrauding custom-houses, grew less scrupulous about others’ rights. He went to St. Thomas and bought, for a song, goods captured by pirates and took them to ports where he could sell them well. If Eugenie’s pure face had accompanied him on his first voyage, like an image of the Virgin on a mast, and if he attributed his first success to her prayers, later women—blacks, mulattoes, whites, Indian dancing-girls—orgies and adventures effaced recollection of his cousin, Saumur, the house, the bench, the stolen kiss. He remembered only the little garden behind the crumbling wall, where he had learned his fate; but he rejected his family. His uncle was an old dog who had filched his jewels; Eugénie had no place in his heart, though she remained in his accounts as a creditor for six thousand francs.

Such conduct explains Charles Grandet’s silence. In the Indies, at St. Thomas, on the African coast, at Lisbon, and in the United States, the adventurer used the name Shepherd so as not to compromise his family. As Charles Shepherd he could be indefatigable, bold, grasping, and greedy, like a man resolved to snatch his fortune quibuscumque viis and get the villainy over with to live honestly thereafter.

With such methods prosperity was rapid; and in 1827 Charles returned to Bordeaux on the brig Marie Caroline, belonging to a royalist house. He brought nineteen hundred thousand francs worth of gold-dust, from which he expected another seven or eight per cent at the Paris mint. On the brig he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, a gentleman-in-ordinary to Charles X., a worthy old man who had married a fashionable woman with a fortune from the West Indies. To meet Madame d’Aubrion’s extravagance he had gone to the Indies to sell property and was now returning with his family.

Monsieur and Madame d’Aubrion, of the old southern family d’Aubrion de Buch, were reduced to about twenty thousand francs income and had an ugly daughter whom the mother was resolved to marry off without a dot—the family fortune barely sufficing for her own expenses in Paris. The mother almost despaired of placing such a girl, but she made the best of her. Mademoiselle d’Aubrion was long and spare, with a disdainful mouth and a too-long nose, thick at the end, sallow normally and very red after a meal—an unpleasant feature in a pale, dull face. Madame d’Aubrion made her daughter appear distinguished: hygienic treatment kept the nose manageable; she taught the art of dressing, charming manners, melancholy glances that interest a man, and the coquettish peep of the foot beneath a petticoat at the precise moment the nose reddened. By full sleeves, deceptive pads, puffed dresses, and tight corsets she had achieved strange feminine developments worth showing as an example to other mothers.

Charles became intimate with Madame d’Aubrion precisely because she sought his intimacy. People on the brig said the handsome Madame spared no means to secure so rich a son-in-law. Landing at Bordeaux in June 1827, Monsieur, Madame, Mademoiselle d’Aubrion, and Charles lodged at the same hotel and set out together for Paris. The hotel d’Aubrion was hampered with mortgages; Charles was to free it. The mother told him she would gladly give the ground-floor to a son-in-law. Not sharing Monsieur d’Aubrion’s prejudices about nobility, she promised Charles that she would obtain a royal ordinance from Charles X. allowing him to take the d’Aubrion name and arms and, by buying the entailed estate for thirty-six thousand francs a year, to succeed to the titles of Captal de Buch and Marquis d’Aubrion. By uniting fortunes, living together, and profiting by sinecures, the two families might occupy the hotel d’Aubrion with an income exceeding a hundred thousand francs.

“And when a man has a hundred thousand francs a year, a name, a family, and a position at court—for I will get you appointed gentleman-of-the-bedchamber—you can do what you like,” she told Charles. “You can become master of the rolls, prefect, secretary to an embassy, ambassador if you like. Charles X. is fond of d’Aubrion; they have known each other from childhood.”

Intoxicated with ambition, Charles toyed with the hopes thus presented in confidences poured from heart to heart. Believing his father’s affairs settled by his uncle, he imagined himself anchored in the Faubourg Saint-Germain—reappearing as the Comte d’Aubrion, like the Dreux reappearing in Bréze. Dazzled by the Restoration’s prosperity, which had been tottering when he left France, fascinated by aristocratic splendor, his intoxication, begun on the brig, increased after he reached Paris, and he resolved to take the course his would-be mother-in-law indicated. His cousin counted for no more than a speck in this brilliant prospect; but he went to see Annette. A woman of the world, Annette advised him to marry and promised her support in his ambitious projects. In her heart she was delighted to fasten an ugly, uninteresting girl on Charles, whose West Indies life had made him very attractive: bronzed complexion, bold, decisive manners like a man used to sharp decisions, to rule, and to succeed. Charles breathed easier in Paris, conscious of his part to play.

Des Grassins, hearing of his return, his approaching marriage, and his fortune, came to see him and inquired about the three hundred thousand francs still required to settle his father’s debts. He found Grandet conferring with a goldsmith about jewels for Mademoiselle d’Aubrion’s corbeille. Charles had brought magnificent diamonds; the setting with plate and jewelry for the new establishment amounted to over two hundred thousand francs. When des Grassins, who had already called several times, presented himself, Charles treated him with the impertinence of a young man of fashion conscious of having killed four men in as many duels in the Indies. Coldly, and without fully grasping the plea, Charles replied:

“My father’s affairs are not mine. I am much obliged, monsieur, for the trouble you have been good enough to take—by which, however, I really cannot profit. I have not earned two millions by the sweat of my brow to fling them at the head of my father’s creditors.”

“But suppose that your father’s estate were within a few days to be declared bankrupt?”

“Monsieur, in a few days I shall be called the Comte d’Aubrion; you will understand, therefore, that what you threaten is of no consequence to me. Besides, you know as well as I do that when a man has an income of a hundred thousand francs his father has never failed.” So saying, he edged Monsieur des Grassins to the door.

At the beginning of August that year, Eugénie was sitting on the little wooden bench where her cousin had sworn to love her eternally, where she usually breakfasted when the weather was fine. The poor girl was happy for the moment in the fresh summer air, letting memory recall the great and little events of her love and the catastrophes that followed. The sun had just reached the angle of the ruined wall full of chinks that no one, by the mistress’s caprice, was allowed to touch, though Cornoiller often told his wife it would fall and crush somebody one day. At that moment the postman knocked and gave a letter to Madame Cornoiller, who ran into the garden, crying:

“Mademoiselle, a letter!” She gave it to her mistress, adding, “Is it the one you expected?”

The words rang as loudly in Eugénie’s heart as they echoed from wall to wall of the court and garden.

“Paris—from him—he has returned!”

Eugénie turned pale and held the letter for a moment. She trembled and could not break the seal. La Grande Nanon stood before her, hands on her hips, her joy showing in the lines of her face.

“Read it, mademoiselle!”

“Ah, Nanon, why did he return to Paris? He went from Saumur.”

“Read it, and you’ll find out.”

Eugénie opened the letter with trembling fingers. A cheque on the house of “Madame des Grassins and Coret, of Saumur,” fluttered out. Nanon picked it up.

The letter began in a formal tone—no longer the familiar “thou” of their youth—and in those first words Eugenie felt the change. He announced the success of his enterprise, his return rich, and the recent deaths of his uncle and aunt, news he had learned from Monsieur des Grassins. He recalled with a show of sentiment the little wooden seat, the passage, the gray hall, his attic chamber, and the night when, by her delicate kindness, she had once brightened his future. These recollections, he said, had sustained him.

But he was frank: traveling through many lands had altered his views; he no longer believed in youthful illusions. He declared that love in marriage was a delusion and that in marrying one must obey social laws and meet the world’s conventional demands. He said her customs, inclinations, education, and habits did not fit Parisian life or the future he intended. Then he revealed the reason for his change—he now possessed an income of eighty thousand francs. This fortune enabled him to marry into the d’Aubrion family, whose nineteen-year-old heiress would bring him a title, the place of gentleman-of-the-bedchamber, and a brilliant position. He admitted he did not love Mademoiselle d’Aubrion, but in marrying her he secured for his children a social rank whose advantages would become incalculable: his son, as Marquis d’Aubrion with an entailed estate yielding forty thousand francs a year, could obtain any State position he chose. “We owe ourselves to our children,” he wrote.

He tried to posture this as generosity toward Eugénie: having placed his heart, hopes, and fortune before her in good faith, he declared that if she still felt their youthful bond, he was putting himself in her hands. He claimed that if she demanded it, he would renounce his social ambitions and content himself with the simple happiness she could offer. But the tone, the calculations, and his abrupt transformation belied any real intention of renouncing his prospects.

Eugénie rose as if sitting on live coals and went to the stone steps of the court. She could not read the letter without feeling the change in every line; tears gathered. The cheque that fell from the letter was a postscript’s material proof of his choice. The P.S. enclosed a cheque on the des Grassins bank for eight thousand francs payable in gold, “which includes the capital and interest of the sum you were kind enough to lend me.” He added that he expected a case from Bordeaux containing “a few things” she must accept as a mark of his gratitude, and instructed that his dressing-case could be sent by diligence to the hotel d’Aubrion, rue Hillerin-Bertin.

“By the diligence!” cried Eugénie. “A thing for which I would have laid down my life!”

The letter destroyed every dream. For some deserted women, fury or flights to extremes are the response; others bow their heads and suffer in silence. This was Eugénie’s fate. She raised her eyes to heaven, thinking of her dying mother’s last words, which had seemed prophetic: nothing remained but to unfold her wings, stretch upward, and live in prayer until deliverance.

“My mother was right,” she said, weeping. “Suffer—and die!”

Eugenie returned slowly from the garden to the house, avoiding the corridor where memories of her cousin lingered. The old hall held traces of him—the saucer and the Sèvres sugar-bowl she used each morning—and the atmosphere was heavy with the seriousness of the day. Nanon announced the parish curate, allied with the Cruchots and Monsieur de Bonfons’s interests. The old abbé had long urged the curate to speak to Mademoiselle Grandet about the duty of marriage for a woman of her station. Expecting a request for the thousand francs she gave monthly to the poor, Eugenie told Nanon to fetch the money, but the curate only smiled.

He came to speak of a “poor girl” who neglected her Christian duties. Eugenie, absorbed in her own sorrow, declared she could find refuge only in the Church and intended to withdraw from the world. The curate counseled patience: marriage was life, the religious veil, death. He argued, for both religious and practical reasons, that she owed society her example and stewardship of the fortune she held in trust; to bury herself in a convent would be selfish. He warned of the difficulties of managing vast estates alone and urged her to consider a husband as a safeguard for her property and duties.

Madame des Grassins arrived, driven by vengeance and despair. She confronted Eugenie, implying awareness of Charles’s return and his intended marriage to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion. Eugenie composed herself into the impassive demeanor inherited from her father. Madame des Grassins produced a letter recounting how Charles, returned from the Indies, had been in Paris about a month, and how the banns were published for his marriage. The marquise related that the Marquis d’Aubrion would not give his daughter to the son of a bankrupt, and that des Grassins intended to declare Guillaume Grandet bankrupt—revenge for her unpaid labors and a means to force the family into public disgrace.

Eugenie read the letter and, without outward passion, returned it unread to the woman. Nanon interrupted to collect eight thousand francs Charles had left, and Madame des Grassins complied. Eugenie asked the curate a pointed, ironic question—whether it would be sinful to remain a virgin after marriage—receiving a half-jesting promise to consult Sanchez on the matter. The curate withdrew. Eugenie retired to her father’s secret room and isolated herself all day, appearing only at the customary hour when guests arrived. Rumors of Charles’s return and treachery had spread, and the hall was full; yet Eugenie, expecting scrutiny, allowed no emotion to show, hiding her suffering beneath courtesy and a calm face.

As the evening’s company prepared to leave, an unexpected event startled Saumur. Eugenie called to Monsieur de Bonfons—the president—urging him to stay. The assembled guests, quick to pun, interpreted this as a formal engagement; provincial conventions treated such an address as a solemn promise. Left alone with the president, Eugenie spoke with emotion and candor. She asked him to swear that he would never claim from her the rights which marriage would give him; that he would leave her free throughout her life. She confessed she could offer only friendship to a husband and wished neither to wound him nor betray her own heart. But she added a condition: if he would accept the form of marriage without the claim to her person, he must perform a particular service.

Eugenie drew forth a certificate of a hundred Bank of France shares—fifteen hundred thousand francs—and commanded him to go at once to Paris. There he was to find des Grassins, learn the names of her uncle’s creditors, summon them, and pay them in full their claims, with interest at five percent from the date of each claim. He was to obtain full, legal receipts, properly notarized, and then carry those receipts and all papers to her cousin Grandet, also delivering a letter she entrusted to him. Only on his return, having secured those documents, would she keep her promise. The request was framed as a test of honor: he, a magistrate and a man of honor, would act as her agent. The president, overwhelmed, fell at her feet and vowed servitude.

Once he left, Eugenie could no longer contain herself and wept in private. The president hurried to Paris. The next morning he and des Grassins summoned the creditors to the notary’s office, where vouchers had been placed. All creditors attended; Monsieur de Bonfons, in Eugenie’s name, paid them the full amounts with interest. That refusal to stint on interest was remarkable in Parisian commerce. When receipts were registered and des Grassins received for his services the fifty thousand francs Eugenie had allowed him, the president went to the hôtel d’Aubrion and found Charles entering after a severe interview with his prospective father-in-law. The old marquis had insisted his daughter could not marry the son of a bankrupt until all her grandfather’s creditors were paid.

The president handed Charles a letter from Eugenie. In it she explained that Monsieur de Bonfons had placed in his hands acquittances for all claims and a receipt by which she acknowledged having received from Charles the sum total of those claims. She hinted at a “possible failure” and the social impossibility of a marriage between the son of a bankrupt and Mademoiselle d’Aubrion. She bade him farewell as the son of a man whose honor she would preserve; though she could not offer him worldly pleasures, she would not stand in the way of his marriage according to the social conventions he preferred. The letter closed with an assurance of continued faithful friendship.

Charles received the documents with astonishment when he learned of the sums involved. Monsieur de Bonfons observed that Eugenie’s fortune had recently been nineteen millions and was now seventeen, owing to the payments made; they estimated their combined income at seven hundred and fifty thousand francs upon marriage. Charles, recovering some audacity, suggested they might “push each other’s fortunes.” The president, with a cynical smile, noted the reduction in her wealth and delivered a small leather dressing-case entrusted to him.

Shortly afterward Madame d’Aubrion entered; the president was introduced as “his man of business,” and Mme d’Aubrion dismissed her husband’s objections. Heeding political and social appearances, the president returned to Saumur and soon announced his marriage to Eugenie. Six months later he was appointed counsellor in the Cour royale at Angers, later becoming president of the court. He conceived further ambitions—parliamentary election, a peerage—but his hopes came to little: he died eight days after his election as deputy of Saumur. His final elevation came at the height of the petty legal cleverness with which, as an exacting husband, he had drawn up the marriage contract. The contract contained a stipulation that, should the couple have no children, their entire combined property would pass to one another, “without exception or reservation, dispensing even with the formality of an inventory,” a clause designed to secure his eventual inheritance and explaining his scrupulous respect for Eugenie’s solitude.

Society, quick to find fault, pitied him and blamed Eugenie’s sorrow, excusing the president as a considerate and delicate man. Women whispered that she must be ill or capricious; others suggested she consult fashionable doctors in Paris or reproached her for denying an heir to her husband. Eugenie, meanwhile, with the clear-sightedness that solitude and suffering give, perceived the president’s motives. She divined that he coveted her death as the surest road to their fortune, heightened by the deaths of his own uncle the notary and his uncle the abbé. Her compassion for him was profound: he, constrained by selfish ambitions, respected the hopeless passion on which she wasted herself only because it served as his safeguard. To give a child to the husband would have cut off his hopes; thus his regard for her grief was rooted in self-preservation.

Providence seemed, in its inscrutable ways, to heap gold upon Eugenie—a woman indifferent to wealth and devoted to piety and secret benefactions. As Madame de Bonfons she became a widow at thirty-six. She retained a beauty sketched by sorrow: a placid, pale face, gentle voice, simple manners, saintly qualities unmarred by worldly contact, yet still bearing the rigid bearing and petty habits of an old maid shaped by provincial life. In spite of immense wealth, she lived frugally, keeping to the patterns of her childhood: the fire in her house in Saumur was never lit except by permission, and lights and comforts were arranged according to the old rules. She dressed modestly; the house remained shadowed and melancholy—an outward image of her soul. Although she appeared parsimonious, she disarmed such criticism by an honorable use of her fortune: she endowed pious and charitable institutions, founded an old-age hospital, financed Christian schools for children, and set up a public library. She contributed generously to the embellishment of Saumur’s churches. Her benefactions marked the nobility of her heart and provided tangible proof against charges of avarice.

Eugenie remained a woman “in the world but not of it,” created for domestic life yet deprived of husband, children, and family. She was spoken of with reverent respect—sometimes ironically as “mademoiselle”—and people speculated over her prospects. There were rumors that she might marry again, and talk of the Marquis de Froidfond and other suitors beginning to besiege the wealthy widow, as the Cruchots had once pursued the heiress. But such gossip overlooked the simple truth: Nanon and Cornoiller, faithful servants, had neither the mind nor the inclination for the intrigues of the world. Eugenie, whose heart beat only for the most tender emotions, continued her solitary piety, secretly stanching wounds in many families, directing her vast resources to good works, and moving through life with a saintly composure that redeemed the narrowness of her education and the petty habits of her early years.
