Railroads threatened to make many industries obsolete and to transform travel around Paris; the once-common "coucous" of 1830 were already fading by 1842, surviving mainly at suburban spectacles. In 1820 many districts still lacked regular stage-coach service, so local short-route vehicles remained essential.

The Touchards, father and son, held a near-monopoly on travel within about forty-five miles of their rue du Faubourg-Saint-Denis base, but local coucous vigorously competed on shorter routes. Parisians’ appetite for the countryside let small, familiar operators—often innkeepers who knew the roads, undercut prices, bent rules, and earned local loyalty—thrive. These coucous offered speed and convenience that challenged the Touchards until omnibuses later eclipsed them by carrying more passengers more efficiently.

One lucrative contested line ran from Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise; despite Touchard efforts to cut fares and improve equipment, stops like Saint-Denis and Saint-Brice guaranteed demand, and rivals even reached Beauvais while the Touchards extended to Chambly. By contrast, the road to Isle-Adam, prized for quarries and its picturesque terminus, discouraged speculation; only two coaches served it, giving Pierrotin and his colleague a virtual monopoly that locals often preferred.

Pierrotin, in his forties, had taken over his father’s coucou after leaving the cavalry in 1815. He married an innkeeper’s daughter, expanded the business, and combined intelligence with military precision—neat dress, stern bearing, and a tendency to regard passengers as cargo. He tried to modernize: ordering a larger coach while keeping an inherited yellow coucou (built for six but often overloaded) and a noisy four-wheeler that could hold seventeen though limited to fourteen. He paid taxes only on the coucou, using a permit for the larger coach to skirt regulations.

On an autumn Saturday in 1822, outside the Lion d’Argent, Pierrotin fretted over scarce passengers and a thousand-franc debt, mulling a loan from Monsieur Moreau, when a footman sought discreet passage for the incognito Comte de Serizy. Pierrotin seized the chance, hoping the fare would ease his burdens; the footman’s insistence on secrecy heightened his sense of responsibility for a smooth journey.

Monsieur Huguet de Serisy descends from the eminent President Huguet, ennobled under François I; the family arms and motto reflect modesty and estate. His father served as president of parliament before the Revolution. The count became councillor of State at twenty-two, noted for persuasive diplomatic memoranda. He stayed at Serizy during the Revolution, sheltered by his father; after his father’s death in 1794 he joined the Council of Five Hundred. After 18th Brumaire he won the First Consul’s favor, reentered the Council of State, and reorganized a chaotic department, helping shape much of Napoleon’s administration. In 1806 he married the widow of a prominent Republican general, improving his fortune. By 1814 ill health forced his resignation; though Napoleon mistrusted him, he retained rank. Louis XVIII later trusted him as a cabinet minister. During the Hundred Days he remained loyal to the Bourbons at Serizy; after Waterloo he resumed duties as privy councillor and vice-president of the Council of State, influencing affairs without seeking acclaim. His strict routine and many honors, including the grand cross of the Legion of Honor, concealed an unspoken sorrow.

He was passionately attached to his wife; work was his refuge from recurring heartache. He treated her with respect and freedom, yet her frequent ingratitude deeply wounded him.

A pressing dispute concerned the Presles farm, leased by tenant Léger from burgher Margueron and soon to be sold. The count instructed his notary to buy it to end conflicts. His steward Moreau—once saved by the count’s father and later spared by the count—was appointed to manage Presles but became lax and corrupt, secretly dealing with Léger. Madame de Reybert lodged detailed accusations; the count, initially doubtful, grew uneasy and sent his notary with her. What began as a routine purchase thus turned into an investigation of intrigue and possible betrayal by trusted servants.

As Pierrotin left the Café de l’Echiquier he saw Madame Clapart and her son at the Lion d’Argent searching for him. In a faded black gown and old shawl she was anxious but dignified, entrusting eighteen‑year‑old Oscar—traveling alone for the first time—to Pierrotin’s care. Oscar, in a short‑sleeved coat and patched trousers, resented her fussing over his gloves and trip to Monsieur Moreau.

Pierrotin knew them from errands for Moreau, a discreet patron who had helped the family and secured Oscar a scholarship. Once Madame Husson, Madame Clapart had been ruined by her first husband’s suicide and later married Clapart, a meager government clerk; his coldness strained the household and aggravated Oscar’s faults. Madame Clapart’s cling to past glories and overprotection fed Oscar’s vanity and entitlement, prompting Moreau to test him by placing him with the steward.

As they prepared to leave, two well‑dressed youths mocked Oscar, deepening his humiliation and desire for independence. Boarding heightened his self‑consciousness: a shabby young artist and his apprentice, Mistigris, bantered bohemianly, while the Comte de Serizy, traveling incognito as a bourgeois, altered the coach’s tone—Pierrotin even rearranged seats to oblige him, drawing complaints. The departing coach presented a cross‑section of social types, with Oscar’s struggle over identity and self‑worth quietly simmering.

Those who travel by public conveyance usually begin in silence, broken only when the most talkative passenger starts a conversation. National types are sketched briefly: English taciturn, Germans somber, Italians cautious, Spaniards seldom using public transport, Russians hampered by poor roads; France, by contrast, is lively, especially among the lower classes and bourgeois.

Georges, a clever, lively young man, quickly sized up his fellow passengers, deemed himself superior, and resolved to amuse himself at their expense. As the coach descended from La Chapelle to Saint-Denis he toyed with identities, finally posing as a Russian prince or a soldier returned from the East. When Mistigris complained about dust, Georges joked and steered talk toward the Levant, claiming service under Ali Pacha of Janina and inventing a plague scare that even made the count have the driver stop. He asserted he’d fought in 1813 and at Waterloo and praised Napoleon, spinning tales of Egypt and a supposed harem.

At Saint-Denis the party paused at a tavern famed for its cheesecakes, where the count—suspicious—found a portfolio identifying Georges as Crottat’s second clerk, casting doubt on his stories. Still, Georges paid for treats and ordered drinks, impressing Père Léger and Oscar and cultivating importance. He continued with vivid accounts of Eastern oddities and harsh pacha taxation, and capped his performance with a dramatic Dalmatian romance—Zena, a jealous pirate, poison, and a narrow escape—that captivated the travelers. Laughter, banter, and drink warmed the coach: admiration mingled with the count’s skepticism as the journey became a weave of tales and a performance of identity.

Pierrotin’s coach stopped at Saint-Brice about half past one to rest the horses. The innkeeper greeted the travelers by name and they disembarked. Georges, showy and boastful, called for a hearty breakfast and twirled his cane; Père Léger, a sturdy farmer, agreed to “break a crust.” Georges produced a straw cigar case and offered cigars to Oscar and Schinner. Oscar, flattered but awkward, puffed ineptly and became the butt of teasing: Schinner smoked with ease, Georges fumbled, and Mistigris joked about Oscar’s discomfort. Oscar watched Georges’s generosity with thinly veiled resentment, mentally tallying the bills he suspected the painter was burning through.

Georges proposed a bottle of Bordeaux with Père Léger, and the innkeeper supplied gossip: François, conductor of Touchard’s coach, had said Monsieur Margueron was invited by the Comte de Serizy to dine at Presles that day. Père Léger laughed and outlined his plan to beat aristocratic patronage: he intended to buy Les Moulineaux cheaply—using freshly minted coins and buying at once before the count could act. The innkeeper calculated returns and called the estate a sound investment; Père Léger explained he’d taken Moreau as partner and, riding royal favor, had secured the property. The scheme sounded shrewd enough even if the count intervened.

The count—listening—grew uneasy at their lack of discretion. When Pierrotin stepped out to water the horses, the count drew him aside, reminding him of a bribe: ten louis to keep quiet. Then he raised it: if Pierrotin would conceal his identity and stay with the horses rather than enter the inn, the count would give him a thousand francs next day for a new coach. He instructed Pierrotin not to speak of the matter and to wait with the horses. Pierrotin returned a curt reassurance and hurried the travelers on, insisting he must deliver fish by three to a big dinner in Stors.

Père Léger suggested they harness the horse Pierrotin meant to sell into the cabriolet so they could breakfast leisurely and overtake him. To their surprise, Pierrotin rebridled and set off; Schinner and Mistigris walked. Georges kept up talk about the hostess and the landscape; Oscar, stung by being called “the little young man,” defended his dignity: he had not been to the East, his family intended him for diplomacy, and his mother ran their household. His companions ridiculed his pretensions, tutors, and vocabulary.

Pressed to name his tutor, Oscar named the Abbé Loraux, vicar of Saint-Sulpice, and hinted at family influence that might help the abbé’s rise. Pushed further, he declared he was bound for the Château de Presles to visit the Comte de Serizy and claimed intimacy with the count’s son. His confident talk about the Serizys drew the count’s attention.

Seizing a chance, the count questioned Oscar, saying he sought information on Monsieur de Serizy to support a claim he intended to press with the American government. Oscar advised him to approach the count’s wife rather than the count: the count, he said, was madly in love with her while she secretly despised him. He described the count’s disfiguring skin disease and humiliating remedies—sulphur and steam baths, even an iron box—portraying a humiliated man, and recommended the Marquis d’Aiglemont, an old admirer of Madame de Serizy, as a better route to influence.

The young man’s ease in speaking of such intimacy shocked the party. Schinner scolded him for airing friends’ affairs in public; Mistigris professed indifference to tale-bearing. Georges pronounced a maxim: in a public coach one should know how to talk without saying anything. When the conversation turned to the Comtesse de Serizy, the count interrupted sharply, forbade disparagement, and asserted his friendship with her brother, the Marquis de Ronquerolles. He vowed that speaking ill of the countess would not go unpunished, hinting at ministers, decorations, and acquaintances with Presles’s architect. He asked Pierrotin to stop at the next inn so he could step out.

Once clear of the others, the count had Pierrotin show him aside in the yard and asked about the talkative young man. Pierrotin explained the boy was the son of Madame Husson of the rue de la Cerisaie, whom he often supplied with fruit and game from Presles. Pierrotin had driven the youngster only once and thought he might be the prince of Maffliers; the boy had told him to leave him near Maffliers, not go on to Isle-Adam. Satisfied, the count rejoined the party. Père Léger and the others exchanged awkward glances, feeling exposed; the three young men fell silent. Mistigris quipped about suffering for loose talk, and Georges reminded Oscar that public conveyances required artful speech.

They rode into the Carreau woods in a heavy hush; the count noted their quiet as if led to the scaffold. Oscar, striving to assert himself, declared he was Oscar Husson and would be famous within ten years. The companions teased him, and the count refined his name to Husson de la Cerisaie, lending his boast a veneer of dignity. The coach descended toward Presles.

Before disembarking, the count delivered theatrical farewells: the colonel should make peace with the king; Schinner’s fame was assured; Oscar needed no protection since he possessed “the secrets of statesmen;” Père Léger was urged to seize the Comte de Serizy firmly. Asking Pierrotin to set and fetch him at the same spot next day, the count left, provoking bewildered speculation about his identity. Père Léger guessed he was the count who’d leased Franconville. As they neared the château gate, Georges warned Oscar to be wary if the man truly were the Comte de Serizy—a chill reminder of his mother’s warnings. Pierrotin called out: they had arrived at Presles.

Some of the young men feigned familiarity, but Georges, not wishing to reveal his destination, pretended he would go on to Les Moulineaux. Père Léger then triumphantly disclosed he was the farmer at Les Moulineaux, and Georges was forced to admit it. Oscar left his packages with the steward and plunged toward the château, guided to the little gate by Père Léger. Georges slipped away when he realized Père Léger truly owned Les Moulineaux. Pierrotin, having obeyed the count’s injunction to keep the matter secret for now, drove in with swagger, deposited packages, and handed a small portmanteau to the concierge’s wife. Mistigris and the painter took rooms in the château itself—astonishing Oscar.

The scene closed on Pierrotin’s contentment: he had kept the count’s secret and now expected the promised reward. He anticipated a thousand francs and the splendid new coach he long desired. The coach rolled on, the passengers dispersed through the estate, and the social ambitions, pretensions, and private bargains that had surfaced settled back into the polite surface of conversation, continuing to move unseen beneath it.

Oscar hid and watched Monsieur Moreau emerge from the guard-room in a blue overcoat with a riding whip. Moreau greeted the painters Mistigris and Bridau—sent by the architect Grindot—told the concierge to show them to their rooms, invited them to dinner at five, and mentioned hunting on the estate.

Leading Oscar through the park, Moreau sent his son Jacques ahead to tell Madame Moreau of their arrival. Stern but kindly, he urged Oscar to learn riding and hunting despite his inexperience. Oscar’s mother had allowed only a two-week stay because Madame Moreau was present, yet Moreau seemed intent on extending it.

Their steward’s house, attached to the chateau and built before the Revolution, stood in a large garden and contained a salon, dining room, and a modern boudoir furnished to Madame Moreau’s taste. They lived largely on estate provisions. Madame Moreau, once a maid, preserved an air of elegance, cultivated the appearance of the chateau’s mistress, and patronized villagers. She resented the Reyberts for reminding her of her origins and hoped the visiting artist would enhance her social standing.

On the painters’ day, Madame Moreau dressed elegantly but the Paris coach brought only Mistigris’s packages, disappointing her. When Mistigris and Bridau arrived she professed a love of art; they saw her pretension and humored her. A maid then announced the count’s unexpected arrival. Flustered, Madame Moreau hurried; Oscar was scolded for his appearance, and a joking remark by Mistigris heightened the awkwardness.

The count, angry to find Moreau absent and having overheard remarks implying disloyalty, confronted him. Moreau’s explanations failed; distraught at the loss of his position and the harm caused by Oscar’s careless words, he returned home. Estelle, furious, grasped the gravity. Moreau demanded an explanation from the silent, ashamed Oscar, dragged him back to apologize, but the count dismissed the boy and doubted his promise. Humiliated, Moreau took Oscar home to face uncertain consequences for the family.

Moreau wrote to Madame Clapart, distressed that Oscar had revealed Madame de Serizy’s secrets to an incognito count; the count sent Oscar home, costing Moreau his stewardship at Presles and jeopardizing family prospects. At the Clapart house Monsieur fretted about Oscar’s character while Madame defended him; when Brochon returned with Oscar and Moreau’s letter, Madame fainted. Next day Oscar was sullen; Madame reminded him of Moreau’s past support and their precarious situation, urging discretion, hard work, and prudence, but he remained indifferent. She appealed to Uncle Cardot, a modest self-made businessman who praised Oscar and offered to fund his legal studies, advising integrity and discipline. Hope revived. Moreau later said he’d begun speculating in land and could place Oscar as a law pupil, insisting on disciplined study; Madame urged Oscar to show gratitude and heed them.

Ten days later Monsieur Moreau took Oscar to Maître Desroches, a twenty-six-year-old solicitor on the rue de Béthisy who had just opened a roomy, inexpensive office. Raised in poverty, Desroches recognized Oscar’s situation; the boy’s gaunt look and curt manners intimidated him. “We work here day and night,” Desroches declared amid a mountain of papers, placing his head clerk, Monsieur Godeschal, in charge of Oscar’s training so the youth would not lose time on the way to law school and would master the Code. Oscar was lodged in a small attic beside Godeschal’s room; Desroches plainly intended to make him a capable clerk.

For two years Oscar lived at the office under exacting, petty work. Under Godeschal’s strict oversight he kept a monk-like schedule: rising at five to prepare, running errands, studying the Code under rigorous tests, returning to office tasks after classes, and subsisting on plain meals. His social life was minimal—monthly breakfasts with his uncle Cardot and Sundays with his mother—interrupted only by the occasional outing Moreau could arrange. His efforts at elegance met his superiors’ disapproval.

Godeschal counseled a modest wardrobe and frugality—insisting a good clerk should not spend more than seven hundred francs a year—and lived those principles. He showed practical humanity: when a colleague failed, he discreetly guided him to resign rather than face public dismissal.

By twenty, Oscar was the third clerk, doing the second’s duties without pay but receiving room and board. His legal knowledge exceeded that of many licensed graduates and he began arguing cases in chambers, yet he felt constrained, envying well-dressed young men and yearning for the freedoms and pleasures he habitually suppressed. Godeschal, aware of these struggles, tried to shield him and sometimes paid for outings; Moreau supported him but could not connect as Godeschal did. “How is he getting on?” Moreau asked. “Too much vanity,” Godeschal replied, suggesting Oscar’s talents might better suit the bar than a clerkship.

In November 1825 a new clerk, Frédéric Marest, arrived—well-to-do and aiming for the magistracy. Practical jokes lightened the office: Godeschal kept a fictitious reception register filled with absurd entries. When Marest read it and then mentioned consulting a cousin who was a head clerk about his welcome, Godeschal saw his inexperience. Despite office camaraderie, Oscar’s inner conflict persisted as he navigated professional pressure and youthful expectations.

The next day Oscar recognized Georges Marest, now head-clerk to notary Hannequin; Georges recalled past mischief involving a peer of France that had caused his transfer. He invited Oscar and the clerks to the Rocher de Cancale and an evening with Madame la Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos, promising high society. Desroches, head of the office, interrupted to send Oscar to fetch an important legal document from the Palais and warned him to beware of Derville. Eager to prove himself as second clerk, Oscar accepted.

After Georges and Oscar left, Godeschal probed about the Marquise; Frederic lied that she was a wealthy widow from Mexico. In fact she was Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, a celebrated danseuse at the Gaîté; Père Cardot had taken in her and her mother and financed her career as she awaited a possible Opera engagement. With new means, Georges pursued her.

The clerks dressed up; Oscar’s mother, Madame Clapart, hoped for his advancement and warned him about Georges. After a lavish breakfast the intoxicated clerks went to Florentine’s salon. Charmed, Oscar joined a vingt-et-un game with Florentine and the actress Fanny Beaupré, quickly losing the money meant for the document. Florentine offered him a thousand francs; he accepted, then lost it all and sank into despair and stupor. The party continued into morning while Oscar slept in the boudoir, oblivious to the consequences.

The next morning loud noise wakes clerk Oscar Husson. His uncle Cardot scolds him for late-night revelry; danseuse Florentine reveals he is Cardot’s nephew and urges help. Oscar has lost gambling money of his employer, Monsieur Desroches, ashamed, he begs Cardot, who reluctantly lends 500 francs.

Mariette sends a note and money; clerk Godeschal rushes to secure Desroches’s documents. Angered, Desroches plans to dismiss Oscar despite Godeschal’s defense and his blaming Georges Marest, half-joking that Oscar might try law. At home Clapart torments his wife over their son’s failures; Madame Clapart defends Oscar. Oscar asserts his independence to his father but is ignored. Determined to be responsible, he faces conscription. Clapart hopes the army will discipline him; Oscar draws a number and, through Comte de Serizy’s influence, is drafted into the cavalry.

Madame Clapart turns to religion. In the army Oscar applies himself, rises to lieutenant-colonel, and years later decorated, he stands with his mother outside Lion d’Argent. They are changed; Georges Marest is a ruined shadow. Military service steadied Oscar; he marries Georgette Pierrotin, learns from his errors, and wins respect and security.
