Railroads promise to render many contemporary industries obsolete and will change travel around Paris; elements of the Scene will one day seem relics. The once-common "coucous" of 1830 were already fading by 1842, surviving mainly at occasional suburban spectacles. In 1820 many celebrated districts still lacked regular stage-coach service, so local short-route vehicles remained vital.

The Touchards, father and son, held a near-monopoly on travel within roughly forty-five miles from their base in the rue du Faubourg-Saint-Denis, yet they faced lively competition from local coucous on shorter routes. Parisians’ appetite for the countryside let small, familiar enterprises thrive: proprietors—often innkeepers—knew the roads, undercut prices, bent rules, and earned local loyalty. These coucous offered speed and convenience that could challenge the Touchards’ more formal coaches until omnibuses later eclipsed them by carrying more passengers more efficiently.

One lucrative contested line ran from Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise, with multiple rivals in 1822 despite Touchard efforts to cut fares and improve equipment, because profitable stops like Saint-Denis and Saint-Brice ensured demand. The Touchards even extended to Chambly while rivals reached Beauvais. By contrast, the road to Isle-Adam, prized for its quarries and picturesque terminus, was served by only two coaches; its terminus discouraged speculation, giving Pierrotin and his colleague a virtual monopoly on a route locals often preferred to the crowded Beaumont coaches.

Pierrotin, in his forties, had taken over his father’s coucou after leaving the cavalry in 1815. He married an innkeeper’s daughter, enlarged the business, and combined intelligence with military precision. His dress—sturdy boots, bottle-green trousers, a blue embroidered blouse—matched his trade and hinted at the soldier’s respect for order. Yet his daily work inclined him to regard passengers as cargo.

Sensing the industry’s future, Pierrotin tried to modernize: he ordered a larger coach from a respected maker; his fleet still included an inherited coucou painted yellow with blue trim—built for six but often overloaded—and a noisy four-wheeled coach that could hold seventeen though officially limited to fourteen. The four-wheeler was so recognizable villagers announced its approach. Pierrotin also paid taxes only on the coucou, using a special permit for the larger coach to skirt regulations.

On a Saturday morning in autumn 1822 Pierrotin stood outside the Lion d’Argent, watching coaches depart and fretting over scarce passengers and parcels. Optimistic about the new coach but burdened by a thousand-franc debt, he considered asking Monsieur Moreau, steward at Presles, for a loan. Then a footman arrived seeking a ride for his master, the Comte de Serizy, traveling incognito after countermanding his carriage. Pierrotin seized the chance, hoping the fare would ease his debts; the footman’s insistence on secrecy and the count’s mysterious purpose heightened Pierrotin’s sense of responsibility to ensure a smooth, discreet journey.

Monsieur Huguet de Serisy descends from the eminent President Huguet, ennobled under François I; the family arms and motto connect modesty with the estate’s name.

The current count’s father served as president of parliament before the Revolution. The count became a councillor of State at twenty-two, noted for persuasive diplomatic memoranda. He remained at Serizy during the Revolution, protected by his father’s standing, and after his father’s death in 1794 joined the Council of Five Hundred to distract himself from grief. After 18th Brumaire he gained the First Consul’s favor, rejoined the Council of State, and was charged with reorganizing a chaotic department—work that helped shape much of Napoleon’s administration. In 1806, at forty, he married the widow of a prominent Republican general, improving his fortune. By 1814 years of labor and ill health compelled his resignation; though Napoleon treated him with some mistrust, he retained rank. Louis XVIII later trusted him, appointing him a cabinet minister. During the Hundred Days he remained loyal to the Bourbons, staying at Serizy. After Napoleon’s final defeat he resumed duties as privy councillor and vice-president of the Council of State, influencing affairs without seeking acclaim. His routine—early to rise, early to bed—reflected relentless, quiet duty; he received many honors, including the grand cross of the Legion of Honor, yet his devotion carried an unspoken sorrow.

He had long been passionately attached to his wife; despite strains their bond endured. Work was his refuge from heartache; he treated her with respect and granted her considerable freedom. Though she acknowledged some of his virtues, her ingratitude often wounded him; only occasionally did she soothe his pain.

A pressing business matter then occupied him: a farm called Presles was encroaching on his estate. Leased by tenant Léger from a local burgher, Margueron, the lease was expiring and the property would be sold. The count intended to buy it to end disputes and instructed his notary and lawyer to negotiate quickly.

His steward, Moreau, had served faithfully for seventeen years; the count’s father had saved him during the Revolution, and the count later spared him from a death sentence. Moreau asked to manage Presles and at first was diligent, but after securing the post he grew lax and corrupt, taking bribes and arranging shady deals. He had already struck a bargain with Léger. His conduct provoked the retired officer Monsieur de Reybert’s wrath; Madame de Reybert brought the count detailed accusations of Moreau’s dishonesty. Initially skeptical of her motives, the count’s doubts grew and he ordered his notary to accompany Madame de Reybert to Presles. What began as a straightforward purchase thus became an investigation fraught with intrigue and the threat of betrayal by trusted servants.

As Pierrotin left the Café de l’Echiquier he saw Madame Clapart and her son at the Lion d’Argent searching for him. Dressed in a faded gown, she was anxious yet dignified—poverty evident but devotion clear as she entrusted eighteen-year-old Oscar, on his first trip alone, to Pierrotin’s care. Pierrotin watched them briefly with concern and pity. Oscar, in a coat and patched trousers, remained under his mother’s influence and resented her fussing over his gloves and trip to Moreau.

Pierrotin knew them from errands for Moreau, who discreetly patronized the family. Once Madame Husson, she was impoverished by misfortunes—her first husband’s suicide—and later married Clapart, a meager clerk. Moreau aided them and secured Oscar a scholarship, but the household was strained; Clapart’s coldness worsened Oscar’s faults.

Madame Clapart clung to past glories and centered her life on Oscar; her overprotection fed his vanity and entitlement. Blind to his limitations, she worried Moreau, who proposed testing Oscar by placing him with the steward. As they left, two well-dressed men mocked Oscar, deepening his humiliation, desire for independence, and sense of inadequacy.

Boarding intensified his discomfort: a shabby artist and his apprentice Mistigris, whose bohemian banter highlighted Oscar’s self-consciousness. The Comte de Serizy, travelling incognito, altered the coach’s tone; Pierrotin rearranged seats to oblige him, drawing complaints and adding tension. When the coach set off, it showed a cross-section—proud mothers, insecure youths, discreet patrons, bohemians—while Oscar’s struggle with identity and self-worth simmered, setting the journey’s stage.

Those who travel by public conveyance usually begin in silence until the most talkative passenger breaks it. National types are sketched briefly: Englishmen taciturn, Germans somber, Italians cautious, Spaniards seldom using public transport, Russians hampered by poor roads; France, by contrast, is lively, especially among the lower classes and bourgeois.

Georges, a clever, lively young man, quickly sized up his fellow passengers, deemed himself superior, and resolved to amuse himself at their expense. As the coach descended from La Chapelle to Saint-Denis he toyed with identities, finally posing as a disguised Russian prince or a soldier returned from the East. When Mistigris complained about dust, Georges joked and steered conversation toward travels in the Levant, claiming service under Ali Pacha of Janina and inventing a plague scare that alarmed the count enough to have the driver stop. He also asserted he’d fought in the 1813 campaign and at Waterloo, where he was promoted to captain.

When the count inquired about decorations, Georges said he did not wear them while traveling; the count produced his own honors, and Georges professed admiration for Napoleon, spinning further tales—of Egypt and a harem supposedly presented to him by Ali Tebelen, though he dismissed harem life as uninteresting. At Saint-Denis the party disembarked at a tavern famed for its cheese-cakes. There the count, growing suspicious, discovered a portfolio that identified Georges as Crottat’s second clerk, quickening doubts about his identity and motives.

Despite this exposure, Georges continued to impress the others—especially Père Léger and Oscar—by paying for their treats and ordering drinks, cultivating the pose of an important man. Mistigris, a painter, introduced himself modestly; the count watched the group dynamics, noting Georges’s efforts to buy admiration and elevate his status.

As the journey resumed, Georges spun more accounts of Eastern oddities: lax policing, strange farming practices, and heavy taxation under the pacha that left peasants barely surviving. The count and Mistigris compared such systems to French conditions while the count remained skeptical of Georges’s authenticity.

Georges’s storytelling culminated in a dramatic Dalmatian romance—Zena, a jealous pirate husband, poisoning, and a narrow escape from execution—that captivated the travelers; Oscar was especially envious. Mistigris added humorous anecdotes. Throughout, laughter, banter, and drink warmed the coach: Georges’s bravado and charm won admiration even as the count stayed alert to contradictions. The journey became a weave of tales, camaraderie, and intermittent skepticism—passengers united by shared narrative and an entertaining performance of identity.

Pierrotin’s coach stopped at Saint-Brice about half past one to rest the horses; the innkeeper greeted the travelers by name and they disembarked for a brief halt. Georges, ostentatious and boastful, called for a hearty breakfast and twirled his cane. Père Léger, the stout farmer, agreed to “break a crust.” Georges produced a straw case, lit a pale cigar, and offered cigars to Oscar and Schinner. Oscar, flattered then awkward, puffed ineptly and became the butt of teasing: Schinner smoked easily, Georges fumbled, and Mistigris joked about Oscar’s discomfort. Oscar watched Georges’s largesse with thinly veiled resentment, tallying the bills he suspected Georges was burning through.

Georges proposed a bottle of Bordeaux with Père Léger, and the innkeeper supplied local gossip. He’d heard François, conductor of Touchard’s coach, say Monsieur Margueron had been invited by the Comte de Serizy to dine at Presles that day. Père Léger laughed and outlined his plan to preempt such patronage: he intended to buy Les Moulineaux cheaply by using newly minted coins and snapping up the farm before the count could act. The innkeeper mused about lending twenty thousand and calculated returns; Les Moulineaux, he said, produced six thousand francs—a sound investment. Père Léger explained he’d made Moreau a partner and, taking advantage of royal favor and the pictures on coin, had secured the estate. The scheme sounded shrewd even if the count tried to intervene.

The count—listening—grew uneasy about discretion. When Pierrotin stepped out to water the horses, the count drew him aside and reminded him of a bribe: ten louis to keep the matter secret. Then he raised the inducement dramatically: if Pierrotin would conceal his name, the count would give him a thousand francs the next day for a new coach. He instructed him not to enter the inn, not to speak of the incident, and to stay with the horses. Pierrotin returned curt reassurance and hurried his customers on, insisting he must deliver fish by three to a big dinner in Stors.

Père Léger suggested harnessing the horse Pierrotin meant to sell into the cabriolet so they could breakfast calmly and overtake Pierrotin. To everyone’s surprise, Pierrotin rebridled and set off; Schinner and Mistigris walked. Georges kept up talk about the hostess and the landscape, joking about leftover cigars. Oscar, stung by being called “the little young man,” defended his dignity: he was not Mina’s aide-de-camp, had not been to the East, and his family intended him for diplomacy. This invited ridicule: his companions mocked his pretensions, tutors, and “household” language—Oscar proudly noting his mother ran their household—and taunted his social standing and vocabulary.

They goaded him into naming his tutor; Oscar named the Abbé Loraux, vicar of Saint-Sulpice, and hinted at family influence that might help the abbé’s ecclesiastical rise. Pressed for acquaintance with Abbé Frayssinous, Oscar said the abbé owed favors to his father. Then, unexpectedly, Oscar declared he was bound for the Château de Presles to visit the Comte de Serizy and claimed intimacy with the count’s son. His confident utterances about the Serizys—outlined to the small party—caught the count’s attention.

The count seized the chance to question Oscar, explaining he sought information on Monsieur de Serizy to support a claim he intended to press with the American government. Oscar counseled him to approach the count’s wife rather than the count himself: the count, he said, was madly in love with her, while she secretly despised him. He described the count’s disfiguring skin disease and the humiliating remedies—sulphur and steam baths, even an iron box—portraying a man reduced and ashamed; in Oscar’s telling, such afflictions left the count vulnerable at home. He recommended instead seeking the Marquis d’Aiglemont, an old admirer of Madame de Serizy, for influence.

The young man’s ease in speaking of intimate things shocked the company. Schinner scolded him for airing friends’ affairs in public; Mistigris professed indifference to tale-bearing. Georges preached a favored maxim: in a public coach one should know how to talk without saying anything. When the conversation turned to the Comtesse de Serizy, the count interrupted sharply and forbade any disparagement. Asserting his friendship with her brother, the Marquis de Ronquerolles, he vowed that speaking ill of the countess would not go unpunished. The painter agreed: no man should blague (mock) women. The count hinted at influence—knowing ministers, blocking unworthy men from decorations, and even acquaintanceship with the architect of Presles—and asked Pierrotin to stop at the next inn so he could step out.

Once they were clear of the others, the count had Pierrotin show him aside in the inn-yard and asked about the talkative young man. Pierrotin explained the boy was the son of a poor woman in the rue de la Cerisaie, Madame Husson, whom he often supplied with fruit and game from Presles. Pierrotin had driven the youngster only once and thought he might be the prince of Maffliers; the boy had told him to leave him near Maffliers, not to go on to Isle-Adam. The count was reassured by this account. When he rejoined the party, Père Léger and the others exchanged awkward glances, feeling exposed; the three young men shared a guilty silence. Mistigris quipped about suffering for loose talk, and Georges reminded Oscar that public conveyances required speaking artfully.

They rode on into the Carreau woods in heavy silence; the count noted their quiet as if they had been led to the scaffold. Mistigris muttered a proverb; Georges remarked on the fine weather. Oscar, striving to assert himself, declared he was Oscar Husson and would be famous within ten years. The companions teased him further, and the count refined his name to Husson de la Cerisaie, giving Oscar’s modest boast a veneer of dignity. The coach descended toward the grand château of Presles.

Before disembarking, the count delivered formal, almost theatrical farewells: the colonel should make peace with the king; Schinner’s fame was assured; Oscar, the budding diplomat, needed no protection, since he possessed “the secrets of statesmen;” Père Léger was urged to pluck the Comte de Serizy and to do so firmly. Asking Pierrotin to set and fetch him at the same spot the next day, the count left the coach to the others’ bewildered speculation about his identity. Père Léger guessed he was the count who’d leased Franconville; Schinner swore never to blague again in a public coach. Mistigris, unrepentant, shrugged with a proverb.

As they neared the château gate, Georges warned Oscar that if the man indeed had been the Comte de Serizy, Oscar should be wary—a chill reminder of his mother’s warnings. Pierrotin called out: they had arrived at Presles. Some of the young men feigned familiarity, but Georges, not wishing to reveal his destination, pretended he would continue to Les Moulineaux. Père Léger then triumphantly disclosed that he was the farmer at Les Moulineaux, forcing Georges to admit it was so.

Oscar asked Pierrotin to leave his belongings at the steward’s; he intended to go straight to the château. He plunged forward along a narrow path, uncertain where to go, and Père Léger corrected him: the little gate, not the forest path, led to the château. Guided thus, Oscar entered the courtyard. While Père Léger watched, Georges slipped away upon realizing Père Léger truly possessed Les Moulineaux. Pierrotin, having obeyed the count’s injunction to keep the matter secret for now, drove in with swagger, deposited the painter’s packages and Oscar’s bundles, and handed a small leather portmanteau to the concierge’s wife. Mistigris and the painter took rooms in the château itself—a discovery that stunned Oscar.

The scene closed on Pierrotin’s contentment: he had kept the count’s secret and now looked forward to the promised reward. With a sly peasant’s calculation, he anticipated receiving a thousand francs the next day and buying the splendid new coach he long desired. The coach rolled on, the passengers dispersed through the estate, and the social ambitions, pretensions, and private bargains that had surfaced in their talk settled back into the polite surface of conversation, continuing to move unseen beneath it.

Moreau greeted the painters Mistigris and his master—sent by architect Monsieur Grindot—told the concierge to show them to their rooms, invited them to dinner at five, and mentioned hunting on the estate.

Oscar’s mother had allowed only a two-week stay because Madame Moreau was present, yet Moreau seemed intent on extending it.

Their steward’s house, built before the Revolution and attached to the chateau, stood in a large garden and contained a salon, dining room, and a modern boudoir furnished to Madame Moreau’s taste. The Moreaus lived comfortably on estate provisions. Madame Moreau, once a maid, preserved an air of elegance and worked to be seen as the chateau’s mistress, often patronizing villagers. Tension with the Reyberts dated to a past slight when Madame de Reybert reminded her of her former status. Eager to impress, Madame Moreau hoped the visiting artist would raise her social standing.

On the day the painters were due, Madame Moreau dressed elegantly, but the Paris coach brought only Mistigris’s packages, leaving her disappointed. Ten days later Monsieur Moreau brought Oscar to Maître Desroches, a young solicitor in the rue de Béthisy who had recently opened a roomy but inexpensive office. Desroches, twenty-six and raised in poverty, recognized Oscar’s situation; the boy’s gaunt look and curt manners intimidated him. “We work here day and night,” Desroches declared amid a mountain of papers, and he placed his head clerk, Monsieur Godeschal, in charge of Oscar’s training so the youth would not lose time on the way to law school and would master the Code. Oscar was lodged in a small attic beside Godeschal’s room; Desroches plainly intended to make him a capable clerk.

For two years Oscar lived at the office under a regimen of exacting, petty work. Under Godeschal’s strict oversight he kept a monk-like schedule: rising at five to prepare for the day, running errands, studying the Code under rigorous tests, returning to office tasks after classes, and subsisting on plain meals. His social life was minimal — monthly breakfasts with his uncle Cardot and Sundays with his mother — interrupted only by the occasional outing Moreau could arrange. His efforts at elegance met the disapproval of his superiors.

Godeschal counseled a modest wardrobe and frugal living — insisting a good clerk should not spend more than seven hundred francs a year — and lived by those principles. He embodied honest, unostentatious leadership: when a colleague failed his duties, Godeschal discreetly guided him to resign rather than face public dismissal, showing practical humanity.

By twenty, Oscar had become the third clerk, doing the second’s duties without pay but receiving room and board. His legal knowledge surpassed that of many licensed graduates, and he even began arguing cases in chambers. Yet he felt constrained by a life at odds with his desires: he envied the well-dressed young men he saw on the streets and yearned for freedom and pleasure, feelings he habitually suppressed. Godeschal, aware of these struggles, tried to shield him and sometimes covered the cost of outings to give him some enjoyment; Moreau supported him but could not connect as Godeschal did. Both feared youthful follies that might imperil his future, though they hoped to steer him aright.

“How is he getting on?” Moreau asked. “Too much vanity,” Godeschal replied, noting Oscar’s taste for fine clothes and suggesting his talents might suit a barrister rather than a solicitor’s clerkship.

In November 1825 a new clerk, Frédéric Marest, arrived. Well-to-do and aiming for the magistracy, Marest claimed a desire for hands-on practice. The clerks enjoyed practical jokes: Godeschal kept a fictitious register of receptions for new clerks filled with absurd entries. When Oscar asked for it, Marest read and laughed, then said he would consult a cousin who was a head clerk about the welcome he should give — a remark that convinced Godeschal of Marest’s inexperience. Despite the office’s camaraderie, Oscar’s inner conflict persisted as he navigated professional pressures and youthful expectations.

Clapart believed military service would instill discipline. Oscar drew a number and, despite his fears, hoped for a favorable outcome. Three days later, through the Comte de Serizy’s influence, Oscar was drafted into the cavalry.

Overwhelmed by her son’s misfortunes, Madame Clapart turned to religion, seeking solace through piety. In the army Oscar applied himself and eventually rose to lieutenant-colonel.

Years later a decorated Oscar stood with his mother outside the Lion d’Argent, waiting for a coach. Both were altered from their former selves; though haunted by past mistakes, Oscar had achieved success. As they prepared to board they encountered Georges Marest, now a shadow of his former self, who failed to recognize him. On the journey passengers exchanged stories of transformed lives; military service had reshaped Oscar, taught him hard lessons, and steadied his character. He was invited to a wedding and eventually married Georgette Pierrotin, the coach proprietor’s daughter. His earlier errors became instructive lessons; through connections, discipline, and changed character he earned respect and security.
