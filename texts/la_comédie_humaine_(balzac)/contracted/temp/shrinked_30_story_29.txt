In some provincial towns a cloistered melancholy hangs over certain houses; silence seems to freeze life, broken only by a solitary figure at a window. That same air brooded over a Saumur house on a steep, seldom-used street of pebble pavement and centuries-old buildings. These wooden houses, prized by antiquarians, show carved oaken beams, slate façades, worn sills with neglected flower‑pots, and heavy studded doors marked with Protestant signs or bourgeois insignia—so the history of France appears written in wood and stone.

Beside a dilapidated dwelling stood a country gentleman’s mansion; its stone arch bore armorials eroded by the revolutions since 1789. Ground floors were plain, dark rooms where goods lay exposed and a girl might call to her parents to serve a customer. Local life and commerce bowed to the weather: merchants, wine‑growers, and coopers watched frost and rain anxiously, for a bad season could ruin them. On Saturdays vineyards drew their owners away; streets fell quiet and neighbors merely watched one another.

The most melancholy mansion belonged to Monsieur Grandet. To understand the house one must know the man. Grandet began as a master cooper in 1789 and married a rich wood‑merchant’s daughter. With his savings and his wife’s dowry he bought prime vineyards during the sale of church property, earning a reputation as protector of local nobility and supplier to Republican armies. Through shrewd investments and service as mayor in 1806, he steadily increased his fortune; that year he also inherited three fortunes, adding vast vineyards, farms, and an abbey, making him one of the region’s wealthiest men.

Townspeople speculated about his riches; his timing of sales secured maximum profit, making him both admired and feared. By 1816 his fortune was estimated at nearly four million francs, and many believed he kept as much again in cash. Frugality governed his life; he spent only on necessities. Plain and unpretentious, he spoke little and stuttered in long conversation, an awkwardness that concealed calculation; he never acted without consulting his wife, whom he kept dependent.

Only a handful entered his house, among them the young magistrate C. de Bonfons, who hoped to win Grandet’s daughter Eugénie. The town’s social life revolved around rivalry for her—chiefly between the Cruchots, who favored their nephew, and the des Grassins, who backed their son Adolphe. In early 1811 the Cruchots gained the advantage: when the estate of Froidfond was sold they arranged its purchase for Grandet, who paid in gold—an ostentatious display confirming his wealth and influence throughout Saumur.

“The house of Monsieur Grandet” sits cold and silent above the town, sheltered by ruined ramparts. Its entrance, set between two pillars under a tufa arch—soft white stone that rarely lasts two centuries—bears weathered holes that give the façade a jail‑like look. Above the arch, a crumbling bas‑relief of the four seasons is topped by a plinth where plants, even a small cherry tree, have taken root.

A solid oak door, split and iron‑bolted, has a small rusted grating through which curious visitors can peer into a dark vault leading to a garden enclosed by thick, damp walls—the remnants of ramparts dividing neighboring plots.

The principal ground‑floor room serves as antechamber, salon, office, boudoir and dining room. Wood‑paneled and gray with exposed beams, it has two street‑facing windows, a white stone chimney topped by an old brass clock and a greenish mirror, and antique chairs in faded tapestry. In each corner stand closets or buffets with dirty shelves. Between the windows sits an old card table with a barometer and two pastel portraits of Madame Grandet’s ancestors. Red gros de Tours curtains—a rare luxury—contradict Monsieur Grandet’s stinginess. A straw chair by the window lets Madame Grandet watch passersby; a work table and Eugenie’s armchair complete the room.

For fifteen years Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet’s lives have been governed by strict economy: fires lit only on specified dates, the foot‑warmer used through cold months. La Grande Nanon, the family servant for thirty‑five years, earned only sixty francs a year yet managed to invest in an annuity. Unattractive but indispensable, she cooked, washed and ran domestic affairs with tireless devotion; Grandet’s rare favors—an old watch, a pair of stockings—were treated as monumental largesse.

During the difficult 1811 grape harvest, Grandet’s relation with Nanon combined dependence and domination: he relied on her labor while maintaining firm authority, and she, grateful for employment and small kindnesses, remained devoted. Their bond was one of power and dependence rather than affection.

Nanon’s domain contrasted with the rest of the house. Her kitchen was a model of cleanliness and order; she worked late and slept in a small closet‑like space, while the upper floors remained bare compared with the hall’s modest comforts.

On a November evening in 1819, Nanon lit the fire for the first time that season, coinciding with Eugenie’s birthday. The household prepared for a gathering as local notables arrived. Grandet preserved the ritual of giving Eugenie a gold coin; Madame Grandet presented a new dress. Grandet urged Eugenie to save for her marriage, underscoring the family’s thrift and fixed habits.

Guests, including the des Grassins family—Madame des Grassins lively and pushy, her son Adolphe bearing gifts—brought compliments that masked rivalry and calculation. Attendees subtly vied for a share of Grandet’s wealth; he watched with pride and possessiveness, manipulating relationships for his benefit. The evening blended familial warmth with cold calculation—until a loud knock at the door signaled an unexpected interruption that would change the night.

Nanon lit a candle and opened the door; Monsieur Grandet followed. His wife called, the players exchanged looks, and Monsieur des Grassins suggested they leave. A young man arrived with trunks. Grandet sent his wife back and went to speak with the newcomer.

The game resumed in silence. Madame des Grassins asked if he came from Saumur; her husband said Paris. The notary checked the hour; Abbé Cruchot asked his age. Eugénie noticed Nanon’s absence; others guessed the newcomer a relative. Madame Grandet urged they continue, aware of her husband’s irritation.

Grandet returned alone; the traveler entered and received polite bows and curtsies. Madame Grandet offered warmth, but Grandet, absorbed in a letter, dismissed concern. The stranger, Charles Grandet, thanked Eugénie but said he was not tired. At twenty-two, handsome and self-assured, he displayed Parisian polish: a hunting costume, carefully arranged waistcoats, a small gold toilet set—and despite instructions to travel modestly, he had used a private coupé to show his means.

His elegance contrasted with provincial decorum. The shabby Cruchots were taken aback; locals met his nonchalance with mingled admiration and disdain. Eugénie was captivated, longing for the world he represented; enchanted by his appearance and delicate possessions, she yearned to connect.

The loto ended when Nanon returned with sheets for Charles’s bed; Madame Grandet followed. Guests began leaving. Eugénie impulsively excused herself to help prepare his room, suggesting refinements—a warming pan, a wax candle—using her birthday as pretext.

While Eugénie fussed, Madame des Grassins engaged Charles; Abbé Cruchot made sly remarks. Grandet, having read a letter from his brother about impending bankruptcy asking him to protect his son, hid his emotions and offered Charles a modest room, downplaying his means. After guests departed, talk rose that Charles might unsettle Saumur; the Cruchots and des Grassins resolved to unite against any attachment between Eugénie and her cousin and to use the situation to their advantage.

When the four relatives were left alone, Monsieur Grandet told his nephew Charles it was too late for business and set household hours: breakfast at eight, a light lunch at noon, and dinner at five. He professed poverty despite appearances and insisted his fortune came from hard work. He told Nanon to bring candles; Charles at first refused but accepted a single shabby one.

Grandet led him through the house, avoiding the cold stairwell. As they climbed Charles’s expectations fell—the staircase and decor were shabby—and he wondered why his father had sent him. On the landing Charles noticed the doors, especially one built into the wall that led to Grandet’s office, the secret room where he did business and hoarded wealth. Across from it, Eugenie sat in her room; after a brief goodnight she and her mother retired. Grandet showed Charles to his attic room, warning him to call Nanon if needed because the dog could be dangerous. Nanon returned with a warming pan, which Grandet dismissed with a grumble as he left.

Alone, Charles surveyed the shabby surroundings and questioned his father’s intentions; he felt out of place and uncertain of his purpose. Meanwhile Eugenie rose early with a new sense of beauty and longing. Dressing, she admired herself but felt inadequate beside her cousin. The garden and courtyard, once dull, now seemed charming; her heart swelled with unfamiliar emotions toward Charles and she longed to impress him.

Eugenie hurried to Nanon, asking her to prepare cream for Charles’s breakfast. Nanon reminded her they needed Grandet’s permission for provisions. When Grandet entered, he and Nanon quarreled over supplies, exposing his miserly nature; reluctantly he agreed to give some flour and butter for a galette.

After breakfast Grandet took Eugenie to the fields, where they met Cruchot, the notary. Grandet spoke of his land and the recent sale of poplars, revealing his calculating side. Cruchot mentioned gossip that Charles might be a suitor for Eugenie. Grandet reacted vehemently, declaring he would rather throw his daughter into the Loire than marry her to her cousin—words that shattered Eugenie’s hopes.

Back at the house, news arrived of Grandet’s brother’s suicide—a development with dire consequences for Charles. The subsequent meal was heavy with unspoken grief. Eugenie, moved and tearful, felt deep sympathy; Grandet remained indifferent. Despite the risk of provoking him, Eugenie and Nanon conspired to prepare Charles a better breakfast, and her affection for him deepened as she worked.

After two hours’ effort Eugenie prepared a simple meal that broke the family’s custom of a standing midday offering of bread, fruit, and wine. She set the table and placed an armchair for her cousin, anxious about her father’s reaction; Madame Grandet promised to take responsibility if he returned.

Charles arrived in Parisian fashion, cheerful despite a difficult journey. He said he seldom ate before midday but welcomed the meal and joked about wanting a partridge. His charm captivated Eugenie; she admired his elegance and felt a flutter of affection. Their fingers briefly touched when he showed her his ring, deepening the spark. The mood shifted when Nanon brought coffee: Charles called it outdated, and the women exchanged anxious glances, fearing Monsieur Grandet’s return.

When Grandet entered he surveyed the scene with suspicion, noting the breakfast and especially the sugar, and demanded its source. Charles asked for sugar for his coffee, but Grandet rebuffed him, suggesting more milk instead. Eugenie, quietly defiant, returned the sugar to the table.

Grandet’s demeanor stiffened as he spoke to Charles, then revealed devastating news: Charles’s father had died by suicide, leaving him bankrupt and penniless. The revelation shattered Charles; he broke down in tears. Eugenie and her mother felt deep compassion, while Grandet remained detached and immediately focused on the financial consequences, lecturing Eugenie about the dishonor of bankruptcy. He declared Charles must go to the West Indies to seek fortune. Eugenie offered to help; Grandet reluctantly agreed to fund the journey. The house remained heavy with grief as Charles mourned; Eugenie urged him to preserve his honor and resolved to share his sorrow—her life irrevocably changed.

About four o’clock a sharp knock startled Madame Grandet.

Grandet burst in, rubbing his hands, exulting that he had “trapped them all”: he’d sold the wine to the Dutch and Belgians at two hundred francs a puncheon, half paid down. He displayed six louis to his wife and predicted prices would fall in three months. News spread through Saumur. Eugenie, excited, asked if he had a thousand puncheons. He confirmed it; she pictured the sum in small coins and asked, “Then, father, you can easily help Charles.” The mention of the neglected nephew stung Grandet. He scolded her for presuming to advise him, insisted any duty to Charles would be on his terms, and threatened to send her to the Abbaye des Noyers with Nanon if she meddled.

When Madame Grandet said Charles had not come down, Eugenie answered that he was weeping for his father. For a moment bewildered, Grandet withdrew to his room to consider investing the proceeds in public funds. His thinning forest had already yielded six hundred thousand francs; with this sale his total reached nine hundred thousand. Though tempted by twenty percent returns, he rationalized a more modest eight percent, imagining that in two years he might withdraw fifteen hundred thousand in gold. Nanon’s call to dinner interrupted his calculations; he accepted the eight percent plan, saying hunger would bring his nephew from grief.

At table he learned Charles refused to eat; he commented curtly, and Nanon approved—“So much saved.” When Madame Grandet suggested mourning, Grandet mocked the expense and told her to buy it out of her six louis: a hat-band would suffice. Eugenie sewed in silence while he ran through arithmetic. The town buzzed about the sale; Nanon kept spinning, and Grandet repeated his maxim, “Nothing should be wasted,” as he pursued more schemes.

That night Grandet, consumed by present gain and dismissive of any future beyond material increase, revealed how money governed his laws, politics, and morals. He bargained with conscience and religion, ordered Madame Grandet to pray, and finally growled, “The devil take your good God!” He went to Charles’s dark room and reproached him in his miserly way: he permitted weeping as natural, insisted he was a “good uncle,” offered a little glass of wine, and scoffed at the theft of a small wax candle. Madame Grandet and Eugenie, overhearing, fled in fear.

Alone, Madame Grandet prayed for Grandet and fretted over the punishment he had threatened for Eugenie. Eugenie lay restless; her concern for Charles swelled into a personal disturbance. In the early hours she rose and found him asleep in an armchair, exhausted by grief; his smoothed, pale face touched her in a way she barely understood. She watched him with a feeling that made her both ashamed and secretly happy. He woke, apologized; she urged him to bed and fled, fearful of what he might think. This innocent boldness marked a turning point: she feared his judgment yet hoped he might see love in her visit.

Next morning Grandet returned distracted but outwardly affectionate, kissed Eugenie, and acted as if forgetting his threats. “What has become of my nephew? The lad gives no trouble,” he said; Nanon replied he was asleep. His unexpected softness surprised Madame Grandet but was only surface; he had spent the night calculating schemes to enlarge his fortune and dominate others, equating imposition with power.

That day he devised a way to restore his brother’s honor without spending a franc: to save Charles by manipulating public opinion in Paris. In his absence Eugenie found tender pleasure in caring for her cousin. She prepared his breakfast and carried it to his room to spare him the shame of eating before her. Her innocent devotion moved him; their unspoken understanding suggested a closeness money could not buy. Life went on around this quiet intimacy—Grandet’s calculations, Nanon’s spinning, the Cruchots’ dinner—but beneath routine Eugenie’s tenderness and Charles’s gratitude hinted at obligations and changes that would shape their lives.

Saumur was more affected by the condolence dinner Grandet gave the Cruchots than by the prior night’s sale of his vintage, which the wine-growers called a betrayal. Grandet, who fancied himself superior to those he duped, paid little heed to their censure.

When the des Grassins learned of Guillaume Grandet’s failure and death, they came that evening to offer sympathy and to learn why he had invited the Cruchots. At five, Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle, the notary, arrived in their Sunday best. The dinner was sombre: Grandet grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, Madame Grandet speaking little—a fitting meal of condolence. Afterwards Charles asked to retire to write a letter.

Once the women left, Grandet revealed the craftiness that earned him the nickname “the old dog.” Affecting a practiced stutter, he controlled and provoked his guests while steering conversation toward bankruptcy. The president of the commercial court explained that a respected merchant facing insolvency could have a receiver appointed—liquidation did not necessarily mean utter ruin. It could be managed privately: if the merchant didn’t file a schedule and no creditor appeared, family or heirs might liquidate the business; debts could be bought at a discount so that, if titles to all claims were acquired, heirs might owe nothing.

The notary urged this course to save Grandet’s reputation. Mindful of family standing and his nephew’s prospects, Grandet weighed the advantages but professed ignorance of Parisian tricks, saying he tended only vineyards. The president offered to go to Paris to deal with creditors and suggested Grandet provide some assets to facilitate negotiations.

Before committing, the des Grassins declared they would liquidate the brother’s debts honorably; the banker applauded, and Madame des Grassins even offered to go to Paris. Favoring the banker, Grandet pulled him aside to discuss investing in government bonds and asked him to say nothing to the Cruchots. They agreed to meet the next day.

As word of his supposed generosity spread, the town quickly forgave the vintage sale and admired Grandet again. Once the house closed, he ordered Nanon to keep silent and prepared a secret trip to sell gold in Angers. In the early hours, Eugenie, worried about Charles, thought she heard a cry but was distracted by her father and Nanon carrying a heavy keg. Grandet told Nanon to tell his wife he had gone to the country and hurried off, determined to reach Angers before dawn.

“My father has gone,” Eugenie thought as she listened from the top of the stairs. The house was hushed; the carriage’s rumble faded and a cry from her cousin’s room cut the silence. A strip of light from the doorway lit the staircase.

“He suffers!” she cried, running up. Charles sat in an armchair, head bowed, a pen fallen from his hand; his breathing was labored. On the table were sealed letters to tradesmen—signs he was settling affairs to leave France—and two open letters. The address “My dear Annette” struck her to the core. Rooted, she felt shame and curiosity; curiosity won. She adjusted his head, kissed his hair, and read.

Charles’s letter to Annette explained that misfortune had ruined him: his father had killed himself, their fortune was gone, and he was penniless. He could not remain in Paris where the scorn for a ruined man would be intolerable. He would not ask Annette to sacrifice comforts to keep him; better to part. He planned to try his luck in the Indies or America—perhaps to go to sea—and might return aged and altered; he urged Annette to treasure their happiness but accepted that he could not demand fidelity, for marriage or new ties might become necessities. The letter ended abruptly, as if his hand had left the page.

Eugenie excused the coldness in the words, convincing herself a girl in love might not see its selfishness. She read passion into his phrases; “My dear Annette” became music to her. She mourned his misfortune and thought, “I have gold; I will give it to him.”

The second letter, to his friend Alphonse, asked him to settle affairs: sell possessions, collect whatever could be recovered, keep his horse Briton if possible, press builders to delay delivery of an unpaid carriage, and pay off debts, including six louis lost at cards. It revealed a practical, calculating mind intent on avoiding dishonor and salvaging what remained.

Overwhelmed with compassion and admiration, Eugenie ran to her room and opened the old oak cabinet where she had hidden a red velvet purse—a grandmother’s relic. Inside were coins: various foreign gold pieces, rare rupees—about five thousand eight hundred francs in all. Joy and resolve filled her. Secret love for her cousin and pity for his plight dissolved propriety. She returned upstairs at once, candle and purse in hand.

Charles was awake, stunned at seeing her. Eugenie, trembling, confessed she had read the letters and then, without hesitation, offered him her savings. She called herself a poor girl who wanted nothing, a sister needing to help a cousin. Kneeling, she implored him to accept her gold and, by taking it, to show that he respected her and was generous. Tears fell from Charles onto her hands; moved, she poured the coins onto the table. “You will be rich,” she cried; “this gold will bring you happiness; someday you shall return it to me—are we not partners?”

Charles could not refuse. He rose, kissed her, and at last revealed he would reciprocate not with money but with an object he held dear: a leather dressing-case containing, in a hidden drawer, two portraits by Madame Mirbel, set with pearls—his mother and his father, who were Eugenie’s aunt and uncle. He declared the case more precious to him than life, and begged Eugenie to be its guardian. If she guarded it and, in the worst event his fortune vanished, the pearls and gold in the case would repay her; yet he demanded that it ultimately be destroyed to prevent its passing into other hands. This exchange of trust and sacred confidence marked a turning point; Eugenie looked at him with her first deep, womanly affection. He kissed her hand, calling her an “angel of purity,” and vowed that feeling and sentiment must be everything between them henceforth.

They exchanged gentle words and good-nights. Eugenie, practical and blushing, suggested Charles keep the treasures in her old cabinet. He sighed in his mourning but kissed her hand and, as she closed the door, he murmured the ruin of his fortunes. “What matter? My father is rich; I think so,” she answered naively. He scoffed; she retorted about Froidfond and Noyers, and he dismissed them as poor farms. The exchange was sweet and foolish: from that moment, beneath his black, Charles began to wear roses.

Next morning, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found Eugenie and Charles together in the garden. He remained sorrowful; the three women of the house, respectful of his grief, allowed him the morning in the hall while they tended chores and awaited tradesmen for neglected business. Around five, Monsieur Grandet returned from Angers, unexpectedly jubilant, having made fourteen thousand francs on an exchange of gold. He had left his groom at Angers to look after the exhausted horses.

“I am hungry,” he proclaimed upon entering. Nanon scolded him for not eating. Shortly Des Grassins arrived to take Grandet’s orders. Grandet barely registered his nephew’s presence, absorbed in his financial success and amused to baffle others. Des Grassins, the banker, informed him that gold fetched high prices in Angers and that people had come from Nantes; he talked of sending theirs. Grandet curtly refused, hinting at giving his old friend respite from wasted exertion. A startled consultation between the two men followed; they glanced often at Charles. Des Grassins learned that Grandet intended to invest a sum that might yield—so it was suggested—an enormous return; talk even floated of producing a hundred thousand francs a year, though the deputies’ figures and Grandet’s actual plans remained shrouded in his dry, cautious talk.

Des Grassins took the moment to offer to go to Paris and asked Charles if he had any commissions. Charles declined stiffly. Des Grassins rebuked the nephew for the curt reply; he reminded him of family ties and honor. Charles, pale, kissed his uncle and withdrew. Eugenie watched her father with secretly swelling admiration for his mastery over affairs.

Grandet dismissed Des Grassins with a jocular “decoy those people; lead them by the nose,” and the two men shook hands. Grandet resumed his armchair and asked Nanon for black-current ratafia. His excitement made him fidget at first and then sing snatches of a ditty, pacing in what Nanon called “his dancing steps.” The women exchanged uneasy glances at his towering contentment; his gaiety frightened them when it rose to a peak. He chose to go to bed early, expecting others to follow his lead. Madame Grandet, caught in his current, lived according to his will.

During the two hours before retiring, Grandet grew more talkative and philosophical about money, offering peculiar maxims and showing the mindset that governed his life: “You have no sooner put your lips to a glass than it is empty! Such is life. You can’t have and hold. Gold won’t circulate and stay in your purse. If it were not for that, life would be too fine.” His cheerfulness came with small generosity—offering Nanon a share of ratafia—and an undertone of the calculation that made him formidable. The evening passed under a mood blending domestic routine, latent anxieties about affairs, and the quiet bloom of a new intimacy between Eugenie and Charles, sealed by her sacrifice and his trust.

Thus the house resumed its ordinary hum: tradesmen, household tasks, and the unspoken commerce of feelings and fortunes. Eugenie’s secret gift and Charles’s confided treasures had altered their relation; Grandet’s profit from the Angers exchange had reawakened his habit of managing and tightening the household’s finances. Between these threads—Eugenie’s selfless devotion and Grandet’s thrifty triumph—Balzac sketches the beginnings of passions, calculations, and the mingling of sentiment with the sober arithmetic of money that will shape the family’s future.

The next morning the family breakfasted together in a subdued, private way. Grief drew Madame Grandet, Eugénie, and Charles closer; even Nanon felt sympathy. The old man, absorbed in thrift and intent on sending Charles away to avoid expense, stayed aloof, busy with farm work and leaving Madame Grandet to watch over Eugénie and Charles as their relationship quietly deepened.

For Eugénie, love grew from their shared secret. Their bond took on the simplicity and tenderness of childhood: soft glances, gentle words, and small consolations. Eugénie delighted in comforting Charles, soothing his pain with the sweetness of new love; Charles, hardened by worldly passions, found in her a pure, sincere attachment and prized the modest, unpretentious life they shared. While Grandet worked in the fields after breakfast, Charles savored the company of Eugénie and her mother, charmed by virtues he had once thought limited to Germany. Eugénie became his ideal; she surrendered to their love without foreseeing the looming separation.

Three days after the des Grassins left, Grandet took Charles to the civil court to renounce his claim to his father’s estate—a legal act that felt like a painful betrayal. Charles signed powers of attorney, obtained a passport, and sold his belongings to prepare for departure. Grandet admired Charles’s new appearance in mourning and regarded him as ready to seek his fortune. When Charles gathered valuables to sell, Grandet offered to buy them, valuing them at nearly a thousand francs; Charles accepted with gratitude and gave his mother’s thimble to Madame Grandet. Grandet then insisted on paying for Charles’s passage to the Indies, a generosity that paradoxically cost him little.

As Charles prepared to leave, Eugénie confessed her sorrow; he acknowledged his obligations and his coming-of-age. The family wore mourning for a Requiem Mass for Guillaume Grandet, and Charles received letters from Paris about his affairs, which he shared with Eugénie. In the garden he assured her his affairs were in order, that his effects would be sent to Nantes, and urged her not to tie her future to his uncertain fate. Still, their love held firm and they kissed passionately.

On the day of departure Grandet unexpectedly packed Charles’s things for Nantes. The family saw him off at the diligence; after he left the house felt empty. Eugénie, determined to preserve his memory, kept his room unchanged and conspired with Nanon. Her beauty grew more radiant and dignified as she followed his voyage on a map and confided her constant love only to Nanon, who, though resigned, deeply empathized.

Two months later the once-monotonous life of Eugénie and her mother was consumed by the secret that bound them: Charles filled their thoughts, and Eugénie even looked for him in her aunt’s portrait. One Sunday Madame Grandet discovered the truth about the gold Eugénie had given Charles, and panic followed. They feared Grandet’s reaction and missed Mass; Madame suggested consulting Monsieur des Grassins in Paris, but Eugénie said it was too late.

On the cold morning of January 1, 1820, Madame asked for a fire to avoid the confrontation. Grandet, unexpectedly cheerful, agreed and revealed he had received a large sum from investments, boasting over breakfast and deepening their unease. Afterward he demanded to see Eugénie’s gold and ordered its return. Cornered, she said she no longer had it. His anger flared; he accused her of deceit and threatened confinement on bread and water. Heartbroken, she fled to her mother, who pleaded for her, but Grandet remained obstinate. Nanon, fearing for Eugénie, vowed support and that evening secretly brought a hare pâté, strengthening their bond as they faced Grandet’s harshness.

For months the old wine-grower kept his ailing wife tended while confining their daughter Eugénie to her room by his orders. Townspeople learned she was given only bread and water; Nanon secretly brought extra food. The community condemned Grandet, and Eugénie’s gentle attendance at Mass aroused both sympathy and curiosity.

Eugénie’s chief sorrow was her mother’s decline, which she often blamed on herself. Madame Grandet urged reconciliation, begging her husband to restore their daughter, but he remained cold as she weakened. In spring Madame confided in the Cruchots, who were appalled and discussed possible legal consequences of Eugénie’s confinement. Eugénie overheard, feared scandal, begged them to be silent, and resolved to obey her father for the family’s honor.

When notary Maitre Cruchot warned Grandet that if Madame died Eugénie’s inheritance might force the sale of property and threaten his control, Grandet began to relent. Returning home to reconcile, he found Eugénie and her mother admiring a gold dressing-case from Charles. Possessive at the sight, he confronted her; Eugénie demanded it be returned and, in despair, threatened self-harm. Shocked, Grandet relented, allowed her to keep the case, scattered coins on the bed, and promised kindness. Despite this brief gentleness, Madame Grandet died peacefully in October 1822, urging Eugénie to seek happiness in heaven and warning of a selfish world.

After her mother’s death Eugénie clung to the childhood home; familiar objects moved her to tears. Her father’s sudden tenderness — taking her to breakfast and watching her with an almost kind gaze — made the household suspect his mind was failing. But at the mourning dinner Maitre Cruchot explained Grandet’s true plan: to avoid dividing property or paying taxes on supposed funds he needed Eugénie to renounce her rights so he could retain control. Confused, Eugénie asked for the deed to sign. Grandet, visibly moved, proposed instead that she trust him and accept a monthly allowance of one hundred francs. She agreed; the next morning she signed away her inheritance. A year later he had not paid the promised money; when she reminded him he awkwardly offered jewels.

Over the next two years Grandet trained Eugénie in household management and his frugality; by the third year she became mistress of the house, and their monotonous life continued for five years. Eugénie’s sadness was well known though she never spoke of it. Her only company was Nanon and the Cruchots’ circle, who played whist with her father.

In 1827 Grandet, now eighty-two, suffered a stroke. As he declined Eugénie devoted herself to his care; he died still fixated on hidden wealth, urging her to take care of his riches. Left alone with only Nanon, Eugénie then learned from Maitre Cruchot that she had inherited seventeen million francs. Despite the fortune, her thoughts centered on finding her cousin Charles. She secured an annuity for Nanon, who married Antoine Cornoiller and remained housekeeper while he managed the estate. At thirty Eugénie had never known joy; mourning and an unfulfilled love for Charles, who had not contacted her in seven years, left her indifferent to society’s matchmaking.

################################################
While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles prospered in the Indies. His early ventures earned six thousand dollars, and the slave trade proved most profitable. He went to Africa, bought enslaved people and other goods, and returned to Bordeaux by 1827 aboard the Marie Caroline with nearly two million francs in gold-dust, planning further profit at the Paris mint. Hardened and amoral from his experiences, he engaged in exploitative trades, customs fraud, and reselling pirate merchandise, adopting the alias “Shepherd.”

On the ship he met Monsieur d’Aubrion and his family. Once prominent, the d’Aubrions were now strained; Madame d’Aubrion, anxious for her family’s future, sought a wealthy husband for her plain daughter Annette and saw Charles as suitable, promising to help him obtain a title and income through marriage. Ambitious, Charles entertained the match. Des Grassins, aware of Charles’s return and pending marriage, asked him to settle his father’s debts; Charles refused, saying his father’s failures were no longer his problem.

In August, Eugénie—sitting where they had once pledged their love—received a trembling letter from Charles recounting his success, announcing the engagement as a pragmatic step to status, and declaring love in marriage a delusion. Devastated, she recalled her mother’s warning and resolved to a life of prayer and acceptance. The parish priest, connected to the Cruchots and allied with Monsieur de Bonfons, had been urged to speak to her about marriage; consumed by unhappiness, Eugénie told him she wished to withdraw from the world and live only for God. He warned against such a drastic retreat, reminding her of duties to society and the poor.

Madame des Grassins arrived, driven by vengeance, to reveal Charles’s return and engagement. Eugénie masked her shock as Madame des Grassins produced a letter detailing Charles’s situation and the marriage’s consequences. Tormented, Eugénie asked the priest whether remaining a virgin after marriage would be a sin, exposing her inner conflict. She secluded herself in her father’s secret room, skipped dinner, and avoided guests. Later she asked Monsieur de Bonfons—the president—to stay, an act taken as a pledge of marriage.

Privately she told the president she could give only friendship and asked him to settle her uncle’s debts in exchange for her hand. He eagerly accepted, hurried to Paris, met creditors, and arranged payment with interest. Carrying a letter from Eugénie to Charles explaining her decision and conditions, he informed Charles, who was at first excited but then shocked when he learned of Eugénie’s fortune; Madame d’Aubrion remained unaware.

The president returned to Saumur and announced his marriage to Eugénie. Though wealthy, she lived simply and devoted herself to charity. Her husband entered politics but died soon after being elected deputy. Widowed, Eugénie remained pious, dedicating her fortune to the community and rejecting worldly desires. Rumors linked her to the Marquis de Froidfond, but she stayed independent, supported by loyal servants Nanon and Cornoiller. Her story reveals the tension between wealth and personal fulfillment, showing her struggle to meet social expectations while staying true to her values.
