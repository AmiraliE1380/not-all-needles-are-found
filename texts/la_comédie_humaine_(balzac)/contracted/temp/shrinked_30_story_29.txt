In some provincial towns a cloistered melancholy hangs over certain houses; silence seems to freeze life, broken only by a solitary figure at a window. Such an air brooded over a house in Saumur, down a steep, little-used street of pebble pavement and three-century-old buildings. These wooden houses, prized by antiquarians, show carved oak beams, slate façades, worn window-sills with neglected flower-pots, and heavy studded doors bearing traces of history—Protestant marks or forgotten bourgeois insignia—so that the history of France seems written in wood and stone.

Beside a dilapidated dwelling stood a country gentleman’s mansion; its stone arch faintly showed armorial bearings eroded by revolutions since 1789. Ground floors were not modern shops but plain dark rooms where goods lay exposed; a girl knitting would call her parents to serve customers. Local commerce was governed by climate: merchants, wine-growers, and coopers watched the weather anxiously, fearing frost or rain that could ruin them. On Saturdays vineyards called their owners away, streets fell silent, and neighbors observed one another’s doings.

The most melancholy mansion belonged to Monsieur Grandet. To understand the house one must know the man. Grandet began life as a master cooper in 1789 and married the daughter of a rich wood merchant. With savings and his wife’s dowry he bought prime vineyard land during the sale of church property and earned a reputation both as protector of the local nobility and supplier to Republican armies. By shrewd investments in vineyards and estates—and by serving as mayor in 1806—he steadily increased his fortune. That year he also inherited three fortunes, adding vast vineyards, farms, and an abbey, making him one of the region’s wealthiest men.

Townspeople speculated about the extent of his riches; he timed sales to secure maximum profit. By 1816 his fortune was estimated at nearly four million francs, and many believed he kept as much again in cash. Frugality governed his life; he spent only on necessities. Plain, taciturn, and stuttering, he consulted his wife yet kept her dependent. Few entered his house, among them the young magistrate C. de Bonfons, who hoped to win Grandet’s daughter Eugénie. The town’s social life centered on the contest for her—chiefly between the Cruchots and the des Grassins. In early 1811 the Cruchots gained advantage: when Froidfond was sold they negotiated its purchase for Grandet, who paid in gold—an ostentatious display confirming his wealth and influence throughout Saumur.

“The house of Monsieur Grandet” sits cold and silent above the town, sheltered by ruined ramparts. The entrance, flanked by two pillars and a weathered tufa arch, bears holes that give the façade a jail‑like look; above the arch a crumbling bas‑relief of the four seasons is topped by a plinth where plants, even a small cherry tree, have taken root.

A solid oak door, split and bolted with iron, has a rusted grating permitting a glimpse into a dark vault that leads to a garden enclosed by thick damp walls—the remnants of the ramparts between neighboring plots.

The principal ground‑floor room serves as antechamber, salon, office, boudoir and dining room. Wood‑paneled and gray with exposed beams, it has two street‑facing windows, a white stone chimney surmounted by an old brass clock and a greenish mirror, and antique chairs in faded tapestry. In each corner stand closets or buffets with dirty shelves. Between the windows sits an old card table with a barometer and two pastel portraits of Madame Grandet’s ancestors. Red gros de Tours curtains—a rare luxury—contradict Monsieur Grandet’s stinginess. A straw chair by the window lets Madame Grandet watch passersby; a work table and Eugenie’s armchair complete the room.

For fifteen years Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet’s lives have been governed by strict economy: fires lit only on specified dates, the foot‑warmer used through the cold months. La Grande Nanon, the family servant for thirty‑five years, earned sixty francs a year yet managed to invest in an annuity. Unattractive but indispensable, she cooked, washed and ran domestic affairs with tireless devotion; Grandet’s rare favors—an old watch, a pair of stockings—were treated as monumental largesse.

During the difficult 1811 grape harvest Grandet’s relation with Nanon combined dependence and domination: he relied on her labor while enforcing strict authority, and she, grateful for employment and meager kindnesses, remained devoted. Her kitchen was model‑clean and orderly, though she slept in a small closet‑like space and the upper floors were bare compared with the hall’s modest comforts.

On a November evening in 1819 Nanon lit the fire for the first time that season, coinciding with Eugenie’s birthday. The household prepared the ritual: Grandet to give Eugenie a gold coin, Madame Grandet a new dress; Grandet urged saving for marriage. Guests—including the pushy Madame des Grassins and her son Adolphe—arrived with gifts that provoked jealousy. Compliments masked rivalry as attendees subtly vied for a share of Grandet’s wealth. He watched with pride and possessiveness, manipulating relationships for his benefit. The evening blended familial warmth with cold calculation—until a loud knock at the door signaled an unexpected interruption that would change the night.

Nanon lit a candle and opened the door; Monsieur Grandet followed. His wife called anxiously, the players exchanged uneasy looks, and Monsieur des Grassins suggested leaving. A young man arrived with a porter and trunks. Grandet brusquely sent his wife back to the game and stepped out to speak with the newcomer.

The players sat but did not resume. Madame des Grassins asked if he was from Saumur; her husband said Paris. The notary checked the hour; Abbé Cruchot inquired if the gentleman was young. Eugénie noticed Nanon’s absence; others guessed the newcomer might be a relative. Madame Grandet, aware of her husband’s irritation, urged continuation.

Grandet returned alone; the traveler entered to polite bows and curtsies. Madame Grandet offered warmth, but Grandet, absorbed in a letter, dismissed concern. The stranger—Charles Grandet—thanked Eugénie and, though not tired, surveyed the room and the loto. At twenty-two, handsome and confident, he intended to impress provincial relatives with Parisian polish: a hunting costume, several waistcoats, a gold toilet set, and, despite instructions to travel modestly, a private coupé.

His elegance surprised the provincials: the shabby Cruchots were taken aback; locals met his nonchalance with mixed admiration and disdain. Eugénie was captivated, yearning for the world he represented and enchanted by his possessions.

The loto ended when Nanon returned with sheets; guests began to leave. Eugénie promptly prepared his room, adding refinements under the pretext of her birthday. Madame des Grassins vied for Charles’s attention; Abbé Cruchot made sly remarks. Grandet, having read a letter about his brother’s impending bankruptcy and a plea to protect his son, concealed his feelings, gave Charles a modest room, and downplayed his wealth. The evening closed with talk that Charles might unsettle Saumur; the Cruchots and des Grassins resolved to unite against any attachment between Eugénie and her cousin and to exploit the situation.

When the four relatives were left alone, Monsieur Grandet told his nephew Charles it was too late for business and set household hours: breakfast at eight, a light lunch at noon, dinner at five. He professed poverty despite appearances, insisting his fortune came from hard work. He told Nanon to bring candles; Charles at first refused but accepted one so worn it looked like tallow.

Grandet led Charles through the shabby house, avoiding the cold stairwell. As they climbed, Charles’s expectations fell—the staircase and decor were mean, and he wondered why his father had sent him. On the landing he noticed many doors, especially one walled up that hid Grandet’s office, the secret room where he did business and hoarded wealth. Across from it, Eugenie sat in her room; after a brief goodnight she and her mother retired. Grandet showed Charles to his attic room, warning him to call Nanon if needed because the dog could be dangerous. Nanon returned with a warming pan, which Grandet dismissed as unnecessary as he left.

Alone, Charles surveyed the shabby surroundings and questioned his father’s intentions; he felt out of place and uncertain. Eugenie, meanwhile, woke restless. Dressing, she admired herself yet felt inadequate beside her cousin. The garden and courtyard, once dull, now seemed charming; her heart swelled with unfamiliar feelings for Charles and she longed to please him.

Eugenie hurried to Nanon, asking her to prepare cream for Charles’s breakfast. Nanon reminded her they needed Grandet’s permission. When Grandet entered, he and Nanon quarreled over supplies, exposing his miserly nature; reluctantly he consented to give some flour and butter for a galette.

After breakfast Grandet took Eugenie to the fields, where they met Cruchot, the notary. Grandet spoke of his land and the recent sale of poplars, revealing his calculating side. Cruchot mentioned gossip that Charles might be a suitor for Eugenie. Grandet reacted vehemently, declaring he would rather throw his daughter into the Loire than marry her to her cousin—words that shattered Eugenie’s hopes.

Back at the house, news arrived of Grandet’s brother’s suicide — a development with dire consequences for Charles. The subsequent meal was heavy with unspoken grief. Eugenie, moved and tearful, felt deep sympathy; Grandet remained indifferent. Despite the risk, Eugenie and Nanon conspired to prepare Charles a better breakfast, and her affection for him deepened as she worked.

Charles arrived in Parisian fashion, cheerful despite a difficult journey. His ease and charm captivated Eugenie; their conversation emphasized the contrast between his life of luxury and her modest upbringing. At breakfast Charles praised Eugenie, showed her a ring, and their fingers briefly touched. When coffee came, Grandet returned, eyed the food—especially the sugar—and demanded its source. Charles asked for sugar for his coffee, but Grandet rebuffed him, suggesting more milk instead.

Grandet then revealed the devastating news: Charles’s father had died by suicide, leaving him bankrupt. Charles broke down. Eugenie and her mother were compassionate; Grandet was immediately practical, lecturing Eugenie about the dishonor of bankruptcy and declaring Charles must go to the West Indies to seek fortune. Eugenie offered to help; Grandet reluctantly agreed to fund the journey. The house remained heavy with grief as Charles mourned and Eugenie resolved to share his sorrow—her life irrevocably changed.

About four o’clock a sharp knock startled Madame Grandet. Grandet burst in, rubbing his hands, announcing he had “trapped them all”: the wine was sold to the Dutch and Belgians at two hundred francs a puncheon, half paid down. He showed six louis for his wife and predicted prices would fall in three months. News of the sale spread panic through Saumur. Eugenie, excited, asked if he had a thousand puncheons; he confirmed it. She added up the sum and asked, “Then, father, you can easily help Charles.” The mention of his neglected nephew stung Grandet. He scolded Eugenie for presuming to advise him, insisted any duty to Charles would be discharged on his terms, and threatened to send her to the Abbaye des Noyers with Nanon if she meddled.

When Madame Grandet said Charles had not come down, Grandet asked why; Eugenie answered that he was weeping for his father. Momentarily bewildered, Grandet withdrew to consider investing the proceeds in public funds. His thinning forest had already yielded six hundred thousand francs; with this sale his total reached nine hundred thousand. Tempted by dreams of twenty percent returns, he settled on a more modest eight, imagining that in two years he might withdraw a million and a half in gold. Nanon’s call to dinner interrupted his calculations; he accepted the eight percent plan, murmuring that hunger would bring his nephew from grief.

At table he learned Charles refused to eat; he commented curtly, and Nanon approved—“So much saved.” When Madame Grandet suggested mourning, Grandet mocked the expense and told her to buy it out of her six louis: a hat-band would suffice. Eugenie sewed in silence while he ran through arithmetic. The town buzzed about the sale; Nanon kept spinning, and Grandet kept repeating his maxim, “Nothing should be wasted,” as he pursued further schemes.

That night, preoccupied with present gain and dismissive of any future life, Grandet revealed how money governed his laws, politics, and morals. He bargained with conscience and religion, ordered Madame Grandet to her prayers, and finally growled, “The devil take your good God!” He visited Charles’s dark room to chide him in his own miserly way: allowing the nephew to weep as natural, insisting he was a “good uncle,” offering a little glass of wine, and scoffing at the theft of a small wax candle. Madame Grandet and Eugenie, overhearing, fled in fear.

Alone, Madame Grandet prayed for Grandet and feared the punishment he had threatened for Eugenie. Eugenie lay restless; her concern for Charles swelled into a deeply personal disturbance. In the early hours she rose and found him asleep in an armchair, exhausted by grief; his smoothed, sorrowful face moved her in a way she barely understood. He woke, apologized; she urged him to bed and fled, ashamed yet secretly happy—a turning point in feeling and fear of judgment.

Next morning Grandet arrived distracted but outwardly affectionate, kissed Eugenie, and sat at breakfast as if forgetting his threats. “What has become of my nephew? The lad gives no trouble,” he said; Nanon reported he was asleep. His unexpected softness was only surface: he had spent the night planning schemes to enlarge his fortune and dominate others, contriving to restore his brother’s honor without spending a franc by manipulating public opinion in Paris. In his absence, Eugenie found tender pleasure in caring for Charles, bringing him breakfast to spare him shame. Her innocent devotion and his gratitude hinted at changes money alone could not govern, suggesting obligations and feelings that would shape their lives beneath Grandet’s continuing calculations and the household’s routine.

Saumur was more stirred by the dinner Grandet gave the Cruchots than by the previous night’s sale of his vintage, which the wine-growers saw as a betrayal; Grandet, who fancied himself superior to those he deceived, paid little heed to their opinion.

When the des Grassins learned of Guillaume Grandet’s failure and death, they came that evening to offer sympathy and probe why he had invited the Cruchots. At five Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle the notary arrived in their Sunday best. The dinner was sombre: Grandet grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, Madame Grandet speaking little—a fitting meal of condolence. Afterwards Charles asked to retire to write a letter.

Once the women left, Grandet showed the craftiness that earned him the nickname “the old dog.” Affecting a practiced stutter, he steered his guests toward bankruptcy. The president of the commercial court explained that a respected merchant facing insolvency might have a receiver appointed, distinguishing legal liquidation from absolute ruin; sometimes liquidation could be managed without courts: if the merchant did not file his schedule and no creditor appeared, the family or heirs could liquidate the business. Debts could also be bought at a discount so that, if titles to all debts were acquired, the heirs would owe nothing.

The notary urged this course to save Grandet’s reputation. Mindful of family standing and his nephew’s prospects, Grandet feigned ignorance of Parisian tricks. The president offered to go to Paris to negotiate with creditors and suggested Grandet provide some assets to facilitate deals. The des Grassins declared they would liquidate the debts honorably; Madame des Grassins even offered to go to Paris. Favoring the banker, Grandet pulled him aside to discuss a separate investment in government bonds, asking for discretion and arranging to meet the next day.

As word of his supposed generosity spread, the town forgave the vintage sale. Once the house was closed, Grandet ordered Nanon to keep silent and secretly prepared to sell gold in Angers while the market was favorable. In the early hours Eugenie, worried about Charles, thought she heard a cry but was distracted by her father and Nanon carrying a heavy keg; Grandet told Nanon to tell his wife he had gone to the country and hurried off, determined to reach Angers before morning.

“My father has gone,” Eugenie thought as she listened from the top of the stairs. The house was silent; the carriage’s rumble had faded and a cry from her cousin’s room cut the hush. A thin strip of light from the doorway lit the old staircase.

She ran up. Charles sat in an armchair, head bowed, a pen fallen from his hand; his breathing was labored. On the table lay sealed bills to tradesmen—evidence he was settling affairs to leave France—and two open letters. The address “My dear Annette” struck Eugenie to the core. Rooted by shame and curiosity, she yielded to the latter, adjusted his head, kissed his hair, and read.

Charles’s letter to Annette explained that misfortune had ruined him: his father had killed himself, their fortune was gone, and he was penniless. He could not remain in Paris where the scorn for a ruined man would be intolerable. He would not ask Annette to sacrifice comforts to keep him; better to part. He planned to try his fortune in the Indies or America—perhaps to go to sea—and might return aged and altered. He urged Annette to cherish their happiness but accepted that he could not demand fidelity; the letter ended abruptly, as if his hand had left the page.

Eugenie softened the harshness of these words, convincing herself that love might make a girl blind to selfishness. Young and pure, she read passion into his phrases; “My dear Annette” became music. She mourned his misfortune and thought, “I have gold; I will give it to him.”

The second letter, to his friend Alphonse, was practical: sell possessions, recover what could be saved, keep his horse Briton if possible, press builders over an unpaid carriage, and pay debts—including six louis lost at cards. It revealed a calculating mind intent on avoiding dishonor and salvaging what remained.

Overwhelmed with compassion and admiration, Eugenie ran to her room, opened an old oak cabinet where she had hidden a red velvet purse—a grandmother’s relic—and counted coins: various foreign gold pieces and rare rupees—about five thousand eight hundred francs in all. Joy and resolve filled her. Secret love for her cousin and pity for his plight overcame propriety. She returned upstairs at once, candle and purse in hand.

Charles, stunned awake, listened as Eugenie, trembling, confessed she had read the letters and, without hesitation, offered him her savings. She presented herself as a poor girl who wanted nothing, a sister bent on helping a cousin. Kneeling, she implored him to accept her gold and, by taking it, to show that he respected her and was generous. Tears fell from Charles onto her hands; moved, she poured the coins onto the table. “You will be rich,” she cried; “this gold will bring you happiness; someday you shall return it to me—are we not partners?”

He could not refuse. He rose, kissed her, and revealed he would reciprocate not with money but with an object he held dear: a leather dressing-case with a hidden drawer containing two portraits by Madame Mirbel, set with pearls—portraits of his mother and father, who were Eugenie’s aunt and uncle. He declared the case more precious to him than life and begged Eugenie to be its guardian. If she guarded it and, should his fortune vanish, the pearls and gold could repay her; yet he demanded it ultimately be destroyed to prevent its passing into other hands. This exchange of trust marked a turning point; Eugenie looked at him with her first deep, womanly affection. He kissed her hand, calling her an “angel of purity,” and vowed that feeling and sentiment must be everything between them henceforth.

Their parting was gentle. Eugenie, practical and blushing, suggested Charles keep the treasures in her old cabinet. He sighed in his mourning but kissed her hand and, as she closed the door, murmured the ruin of his fortunes. Naively she tried to reassure him by imagining family wealth elsewhere; he scoffed. They traded sweet, foolish remarks: from that moment, beneath his black, Charles began to wear roses.

Next morning, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found Eugenie and Charles together in the garden. He remained sorrowful; the three women of the house, mindful of his grief, allowed him a quiet morning in the hall while they tended chores and awaited tradesmen for neglected business. Around five, Monsieur Grandet returned from Angers, unexpectedly jubilant, having made fourteen thousand francs on an exchange of gold. He had left his groom at Angers to look after the exhausted horses.

“I am hungry,” he proclaimed upon entering. Nanon scolded him for not eating. Shortly Des Grassins arrived to take Grandet’s orders. Absorbed in his financial success and amused to baffle others, Grandet barely registered his nephew’s presence. Des Grassins, the banker, informed him that gold fetched high prices in Angers and that people had come from Nantes; talk turned to sending theirs. Grandet curtly refused, hinting at sparing his old friend wasted exertion. A startled consultation followed; they glanced often at Charles.

Des Grassins learned Grandet planned to invest a sum that might yield enormous returns—talk even floated of producing a hundred thousand francs a year—though deputies’ figures and Grandet’s actual plans remained shrouded in his dry, cautious talk. Des Grassins offered to go to Paris and asked Charles if he had any commissions. Charles declined stiffly. Des Grassins rebuked the nephew for the curt reply, reminding him of family ties and honor. Charles, pale, kissed his uncle and withdrew. Eugenie watched her father with secretly swelling admiration for his mastery over affairs.

Grandet, giddy from his gain, dismissed Des Grassins with a jocular “decoy those people; lead them by the nose,” and returned to his armchair, asking Nanon for black-current ratafia. His excitement made him fidget, then sing snatches of a ditty, pacing in his odd “dancing steps.” The women exchanged uneasy glances at his towering contentment; his gaiety frightened them when it rose to a peak. He chose to go to bed early, expecting others to follow. Madame Grandet, caught in his current, lived according to his will.

During the two hours before retiring, Grandet grew more talkative and philosophical about money, offering peculiar maxims that revealed the mindset governing his life: life’s pleasures are fleeting; gold won’t circulate and remain in your purse; if it did, life would be too fine. His cheerfulness brought small generosity—offering Nanon a share of ratafia—but an underlying calculation made him formidable. The evening passed under a mood blending domestic routine, latent anxieties about affairs, and the quiet bloom of a new intimacy between Eugenie and Charles, sealed by her sacrifice and his trust.

Thus the house resumed its ordinary hum: tradesmen, household tasks, and the unspoken commerce of feelings and fortunes. Eugenie’s secret gift and Charles’s confided treasures had altered their relation; Grandet’s profit from the Angers exchange had reawakened his habit of managing and tightening the household’s finances. Between these threads—Eugenie’s selfless devotion and Grandet’s thrifty triumph—Balzac sketches the beginnings of passion, calculation, and the mingling of sentiment with the sober arithmetic of money that will shape the family’s future.

The next morning the family breakfasted together in a private, intimate mood. Grief had drawn Madame Grandet, Eugénie, and Charles closer; even Nanon felt sympathy. The old man, absorbed in thrift and intent on sending Charles away to avoid expense, stayed aloof, busy with farm work, leaving Madame Grandet to watch over Eugénie and Charles as their relationship quietly deepened.

For Eugénie, love grew from their shared secret and took on the simplicity and tenderness of childhood: soft glances, gentle words, small consolations. She delighted in comforting Charles; he, hardened by worldly passions, found in her a pure, sincere attachment and prized their modest, unpretentious life. While Grandet worked in the fields after breakfast, Charles savored the company of Eugénie and her mother, charmed by virtues he had once thought limited to Germany. Eugénie became his ideal; she surrendered to their love without foreseeing the looming separation.

Three days after the des Grassins left, Grandet took Charles to the civil courts to renounce his claim to his father’s estate—a legal act that felt like betrayal. Charles signed powers of attorney, obtained a passport, and sold his belongings to prepare for departure. Grandet admired Charles’s new appearance in mourning and regarded him as ready to seek his fortune. When Charles gathered valuables to sell, Grandet offered to buy them, valuing them at nearly a thousand francs; Charles accepted gratefully and gave his mother’s thimble to Madame Grandet. Grandet then insisted on paying for Charles’s passage to the Indies, a generosity that paradoxically cost him little.

As Charles prepared to leave, Eugénie confessed her sorrow; he acknowledged his obligations and his coming-of-age. They wore mourning for a Requiem Mass for Guillaume Grandet, and Charles received letters from Paris about his affairs, which he shared with Eugénie. In the garden he assured her his affairs were in order, that his effects would be sent to Nantes, and urged her not to tie her future to his uncertain fate. Still, their love held firm and they kissed passionately.

On the day of departure Grandet unexpectedly packed Charles’s things for Nantes. The family saw him off at the diligence; after he left the house felt empty. Eugénie kept his room unchanged and conspired with Nanon; her beauty grew more radiant as she followed his voyage on a map and confided her constant love only to Nanon, who deeply empathized.

Two months later life was consumed by the secret: Charles filled their thoughts; Eugénie even looked for him in her aunt’s portrait. One Sunday Madame Grandet discovered the truth about the gold Eugénie had given Charles, and panic followed. They feared Grandet’s reaction and missed Mass. On the cold morning of January 1, 1820, Madame asked for a fire to avoid confrontation. Grandet, unexpectedly cheerful, revealed he had received a large sum from investments and boasted over breakfast, which increased their unease. He then demanded the gold’s return; Eugénie said she no longer had it. Angered, he accused her of deceit and threatened confinement on bread and water. Heartbroken, she fled to her mother. Nanon, fearing for Eugénie, vowed support and that evening secretly brought a hare pâté, strengthening their bond as they faced Grandet’s harshness.

For months the old wine-grower tended his ailing wife while never mentioning their daughter Eugénie, who remained confined to her room and given only bread and water—Nanon secretly supplied extra. Townspeople condemned Grandet; Eugénie’s meek attendance at Mass inspired sympathy. Her chief sorrow was her mother’s deterioration, which she often blamed on herself. Madame Grandet urged reconciliation and begged her husband to restore their daughter to the family, but he remained indifferent as she weakened.

In spring Madame confided in the Cruchots, who were appalled and discussed legal remedies. Eugénie overheard but, fearing scandal, begged them to stay silent and resolved to obey her father for the family’s honor. When notary Cruchot warned Grandet that, if Madame died, Eugénie’s inheritance might force sale of property and threaten his control, Grandet began to soften. Returning home to reconcile, he found Eugénie and her mother admiring a gold dressing-case sent by Charles. Possessive, he confronted her; she demanded it back and, in despair, threatened self-harm. Shocked, Grandet relented, allowed her to keep the case, scattered coins on the bed, and promised kindness. Despite this brief gentleness, Madame Grandet died peacefully in October 1822, urging Eugénie to seek happiness in heaven and warning of a selfish world.

Afterwards Eugénie felt a renewed attachment to the childhood home. Her father’s sudden tenderness—taking her to breakfast and watching her with an almost kind gaze—made the household suspect his mind was failing. But at the mourning dinner Cruchot revealed Grandet’s motive: to avoid dividing property or paying taxes on supposed funds, Grandet needed Eugénie to renounce her rights so he could retain control. Confused, she asked for the deed to sign; Grandet, visibly moved, proposed she trust him and accept a monthly allowance of one hundred francs. She agreed and the next morning signed away her inheritance. A year later he had not paid the promised money; when she reminded him he awkwardly offered jewels.

Over the next years Grandet trained Eugénie in running the household and his frugal ways; by the third year she was mistress of the house and their monotonous life continued. Her sadness was well known though she never spoke of it. In 1827 Grandet, now eighty-two, suffered a stroke; as he declined Eugénie devoted herself to his care. He died fixated on hidden wealth, urging Eugénie to take care of his riches.

Left alone with Nanon, Eugénie then learned from Maitre Cruchot that she had inherited seventeen million francs. Despite the fortune, her thoughts turned to finding her cousin Charles. She secured an annuity for Nanon—who married Antoine Cornoiller and remained Eugenie’s housekeeper while Cornoiller managed the estate—and at thirty Eugénie had never known joy: her childhood was shadowed by her mother’s suffering and an unfulfilled love for Charles, who had not contacted her in seven years, while local notables misread her indifference as interest.

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles prospered in the Indies. Early ventures brought him six thousand dollars, and he found the slave trade most profitable. He went to Africa, combined enslaved people with other lucrative goods, and vowed to return to Paris rich and restore his standing. Hardened and amoral, he rationalized that what one society condemned another might praise. Ruthless, he engaged in exploitative trades, customs fraud, and resold cheaply bought pirate goods. Memories of Eugénie faded; he came to view his family with contempt and her as little more than a creditor. To conceal his identity he adopted the alias “Shepherd.” By 1827 he returned to Bordeaux aboard the Marie Caroline with nearly two million francs in gold-dust, planning further profit at the Paris mint.

On the ship he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, a gentleman with royal connections who, though once prominent, was now financially strained. Madame d’Aubrion, anxious to secure her family’s future, sought a wealthy husband for her plain daughter and saw Charles as suitable, promising to help him gain a title and income through marriage. Ambitious, Charles entertained the match; Annette encouraged him. When Des Grassins asked Charles to settle his father’s debts, the newly wealthy Charles refused, declaring his father’s failures no longer his problem.

In August, Eugénie—sitting where they’d once pledged love—received Charles’s trembling letter recounting his success, announcing the engagement as a pragmatic step to status, and declaring marital love a delusion. Devastated but resigned, she recalled her mother’s warning and resolved to a life of prayer and acceptance. She spoke with the parish priest—connected to the Cruchots and allied with Monsieur de Bonfons—who cautioned her against withdrawing entirely from the world and reminded her of duties to society and the poor.

Madame des Grassins arrived, driven by vengeance, to reveal Charles’s engagement to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion and produced a letter from her husband outlining Charles’s situation, including his father’s bankruptcy; Eugénie returned the letter unread, filled with contempt and sorrow. Tormented, she asked the priest whether remaining a virgin after marriage would be a sin. She secluded herself in her father’s secret room, skipped dinner, and avoided guests. Then, unexpectedly, she asked Monsieur de Bonfons to stay—an act taken as a pledge of marriage. Privately she said she could offer only friendship and asked him to settle her uncle’s debts in exchange for her hand. He accepted, hurried to Paris, paid creditors with interest, and carried Eugénie’s letter explaining her decision. He informed Charles, who was at first excited but then shocked when he learned of Eugénie’s fortune. Madame d’Aubrion remained confident of the match.

The president returned to Saumur and announced his marriage to Eugénie. Although wealthy, she lived simply and devoted herself to charity. Her husband entered politics but died soon after being elected deputy. Widowed, Eugénie lived piously, dedicating her fortune to the community and rejecting worldly desires. Rumors linked her to the Marquis de Froidfond, but she remained independent, supported by loyal servants Nanon and Cornoiller. Her story reveals the tension between wealth and personal fulfillment, showing her struggle to meet social expectations while staying true to her values.
