**Study of a Woman**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*

**Dedication**  
To the Marquis Jean-Charles di Negro.

---

The Marquise de Listomere embodies the spirit of the Restoration, balancing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married for seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she maintains a virtuous facade, possibly to support her husband's political ambitions. The marquis is a passive figure, known for his lack of charisma and engagement, earning him the nickname "dull weather." His demeanor ensures a stable home life, which suits the Marquise's principles.

Despite her reserved nature, the Marquise commands respect and avoids the advances of flirtatious men, maintaining her dignity and reputation. She engages in conversations with agreeable men without attracting scandal, a feat not easily achieved in her social circle. Her beauty is understated; she possesses striking features that come alive when she is animated, revealing a depth of character.

Recently, she danced with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man who embodies both charm and complexity. Their conversation, which began with light topics and shifted to the roles of women, left a lasting impression on both. Rastignac, who is ambitious yet playful, found himself captivated by the Marquise, though he did not pursue her further that evening.

The next morning, Rastignac awoke in a contemplative mood, reflecting on the Marquise while writing letters. He enjoyed the comfort of his surroundings, lost in thoughts of her. His friend Horace arrived, and they discussed various topics, including a potential expedition to Morea, but the conversation was ultimately unremarkable.

Meanwhile, the Marquise received a letter from Rastignac while preparing for the day. The letter began with romantic declarations, prompting her to read on despite her initial impulse to discard it. As she absorbed the contents, she felt a mix of fatigue and intrigue, leading her to ponder the implications of such a bold approach from Rastignac.

In this moment, the Marquise's thoughts reflect the complexities of her situation—caught between societal expectations and her own desires. The letter's audacity challenges her perceptions of propriety and ignites curiosity about Rastignac's intentions. 

This encounter sets the stage for a deeper exploration of the Marquise's character and the societal dynamics at play, as she navigates the delicate balance between virtue and desire in a world where appearances often dictate reality.

Madame de Listomere decided to forbid her porter from admitting Monsieur de Rastignac and resolved to show him disdain in social settings due to his insolence. Initially considering keeping a love letter she received, she ultimately burned it. Caroline, the housemaid, expressed surprise at Madame's actions.

That evening, Madame de Listomere attended a party at the Marquis de Beauseant's, anticipating Rastignac's arrival. However, by 2 AM, she realized he was not coming, leading her thoughts to undergo a process Stendhal termed "crystallization."

Four days later, Rastignac scolded his valet, Joseph, for misplacing letters he had given him. Joseph confirmed one was for Madame de Listomere, which led Rastignac to realize he had mistakenly sent her a love letter not intended for her. This blunder prompted Rastignac to reflect on his mistakes, particularly not visiting Madame de Listomere soon after the incident, allowing her to dwell on it.

When Rastignac finally visited, the porter informed him that Madame de Listomere was out, but the marquis invited him in. Upon entering, Rastignac noticed Madame de Listomere blush at his presence, which sparked his vanity. The marquis read the newspaper, while Madame de Listomere maintained her composure despite the awkwardness of the situation.

Rastignac attempted to explain the misunderstanding regarding the letter, but Madame de Listomere, though feigning offense, seemed amused. She insisted on forgetting the incident, but Rastignac clarified that no offense had occurred. Their conversation revealed underlying tensions, with Madame de Listomere expressing skepticism about Rastignac's intentions.

As the discussion progressed, Rastignac inadvertently suggested that Madame de Nucingen was the intended recipient of the letter, causing a visible reaction in Madame de Listomere. She dismissed him coldly, pointing out the absurdity of the situation. Feeling foolish, Rastignac left after a few awkward exchanges.

Days later, Madame de Listomere withdrew from society, claiming illness, while the marquis attributed her absence to a stomach inflammation. In reality, she was experiencing a nervous attack, using it as an excuse to avoid social interactions.

The narrative highlights the complexities of social dynamics and misunderstandings in romantic pursuits, particularly among the young and inexperienced.
