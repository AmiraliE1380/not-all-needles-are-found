In September 1835 Mademoiselle Clémentine du Rouvre, only daughter of the Marquis du Rouvre and a wealthy heiress of the faubourg Saint-Germain, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a young Polish exile. Though the Marquis had dissipated much of the family wealth, Clémentine’s fortune came mainly from her maternal kin—she was niece to the Marquis de Ronquerolles and Madame de Serizy—and from the Chevalier du Rouvre on her father’s side. These relations settled on her at marriage yearly incomes that made her unexpectedly rich.

Comte Adam belonged to an old Polish family allied to princely houses, but in Paris after 1830 foreign heraldry counted for little. When he arrived in 1833 he was judged frivolous and even suspected of adventuring; Polish exiles had fallen into disrepute amid political divisions. Yet Adam’s manners, breeding, recklessness, and daring attracted attention. In 1834 he bought a house in the rue de la Pépinière and adopted an extravagant life. He had fallen in love with Clémentine at the Opera; within a year they were married. Society’s surprise faded when it became known that Adam had a solid income—his mother, a Radziwill, had mortgaged property during the insurrection to borrow heavily from Parisian bankers, and Adam enjoyed about eighty thousand francs a year. By the winter of 1836 the couple were at the height of fashion: Madame Laginska’s salon and place among elegant young women made them prominent.

Balzac frames this social scene with a domestic one that reveals a quieter nobility. After 1830 old mansions gave way to hybrid residences—palatial effects contrived on cramped lots—where artifice made a man seem grand. Adam’s hôtel in the rue de la Pépinière exemplified this: built within the garden of a former purveyor’s house, it was a small palace with kitchens and stables to either side, a porter’s lodge between coach gates, and an ambitious greenhouse off the boudoir. Decorated by accomplished artisans at great expense, it masked economy beneath veneers, mouldings, painted ceilings, and a white staircase like that of the Rothschild hôtel.

Behind the house lay a compact, luxuriant garden—an English-style lawn with exotic trees, a Chinese pagoda, climbing plants, gravel paths and palisades within twenty-five rods—an oasis where birds sang and a greenhouse filled the air with perfume. The boudoir opening on this garden was less for repose than display: Chinese carvings, inlaid ivories, mosaics, sketches, rare fabrics and collected objects set in a half-light with Persian rugs—a Parisian taste for showing rather than inwardness.

One morning in late April, two years into their marriage, Clémentine lay in the boudoir, thinking. The count smoked a narghile and the greenhouse doors stood open. Though married two years and recently returned from travel, she had become aware of an omnipresent figure in their household: Paz. Her husband invoked “Paz” to settle every practical matter—carriage, bills, the stable, the groomings—yet she had never seen him. She asked Adam for an explanation and summoned him.

Paz appeared in the gallery: a tall, well-made man with unkempt black hair, a broad white forehead, a Roman nose, jet-black eyes, and a soldierly air. He wore a polonaise and had the unconscious distinction of a man of command. The contrast between Paz’s sturdy, solemn presence and Adam’s delicate, almost effete nobility intrigued Clémentine; she perceived in the captain a hidden grandeur, the reserve and force of a past life.

Paz’s manner was modest and sincere. He addressed Adam familiarly but bowed courteously to Clémentine and declined to accept her thanks. When she asked why he served in this subordinate role, he said he liked it; he did not care for Parisian opinion and lived for himself—or, if she preferred, for the couple. He agreed politely to accompany them that day to the Bois.

Adam then told Paz’s story. Thaddeus Paz came from a noble Polish house—Adam said descended from a branch of the Pazzi of Florence that settled in Poland. Poor and orphaned, armed only with his sword, he served in the regiment of the Grand Duke Constantine and joined the Polish cause. In a desperate action before Warsaw’s fall Paz charged a Russian battery, believing his men followed; he was taken prisoner. Adam, who had been with him, recounted how he and his troop had charged and come back with Paz among the few survivors. After Warsaw capitulated they escaped through Prussia and Dantzic, then by a Dutch vessel to London, where Adam’s sick mother was. During these trials they became inseparable, bound as if by life and death.

When Adam later acquired an income he tried to secure Paz’s future by setting aside a sum producing an annual income via bearer securities. Paz reacted with dismay and returned the paper: he would not be bought off or turned into a dependent. He preferred devotion and fidelity to money. Yet in practice he contributed unostentatiously to their prosperity. Shrewd in business, Paz had found the rue de la Pépinière house at a bargain, managed funds advantageously, and thus helped pay for the hôtel. He ran the stables, bought horses with uncanny skill, chose servants—former soldiers—loyal to a fault, and saved household expenses by his prudence. When Adam lost twenty thousand francs at whist one evening, Paz paid it without reproach; his silent sigh at the expense restrained Adam more than any moral rebuke.

Clémentine responded with affectionate approval. She admired the simplicity and grand-heartedness of this man who, though ostensibly subordinate, displayed a deeper nobility. Adam lauded the hidden domain of feeling in Paz, insisting their friendship, based on mutual salvation and admiration, might be richer than love—growing rather than exhausted by giving. Moved, Clémentine promised to love Paz as an old friend; she rose to dress for a ride, pleased to go out with her husband and the enigmatic captain who had been the household’s invisible master.

Paz led so hidden a life that Parisian society wondered who he was when the Comtesse Laginska was seen in the Bois de Boulogne riding between her husband and a stranger. During that ride Clémentine insisted Thaddeus should dine with them, compelling Paz to make an evening toilette. She dressed with coquettish charm that even Adam noticed when she entered the salon where the two friends waited.

“Comte Paz,” she said, “you must go with us to the Opera.” The demand implied: refuse and we shall quarrel.

“Willingly, madame,” replied the captain, “but I have not the fortune of a count; call me captain.”

“Very good, captain; give me your arm,” she answered, taking it with the flattering familiarity of lovers.

At table Paz sat beside her and behaved like a modest sub-lieutenant at his general’s mess. He let Clémentine talk, listened deferentially as to a superior, disagreed with nothing, and spoke only when questioned. His frigid gravity and conventional respect baffled the countess’s coquetries. Adam urged him to be lively, but Thaddeus remained stolid and half asleep. At dessert he excused his dulness, saying his habits were opposite to Paris society—he went to bed at eight and rose early.

“My intention in taking you to the Opera was to amuse you, captain; but do as you prefer,” Clémentine said, piqued.

“I will go,” Paz replied.

“Duprez sings ‘Guillaume Tell,’” remarked Adam. “But perhaps you’d rather go to the ‘Variétés’?”

The captain smiled and rang for the carriage. “Tell Constantin to put the horses to the carriage instead of the coupé; we should be rather squeezed otherwise,” he told the count.

“A Frenchman would have forgotten that,” Clémentine smiled.

“Ah! but we are Florentines transplanted to the North,” answered Thaddeus with a refined accent and an air that made his earlier behavior seem assumed. The contrast surprised Clémentine, who watched him with covert glances.

Silence reigned while they took coffee, annoying Adam. Clémentine stopped trying to draw him out. The captain relapsed into military stiffness and remained so both on the way to the Opera and in the box, where he seemed asleep.

“You see, madame, I am very stupid,” he murmured during the dance in the last act of “Guillaume Tell.” “Am I not right to stick to my specialty?”

“In truth, my dear captain, you are neither a talker nor a man of the world, but perhaps you are Polish.”

“Therefore leave me to look after your pleasures, your property, your household—it is all I’m good for.”

“Tartufe! pooh!” cried Adam, laughing. “He is full of ardor; he can, if he chooses, hold his own in any salon. Clémentine, don’t believe his modesty.”

“Adieu, comtesse; I have obeyed you so far; now I will take the carriage and go home to bed,” said Paz.

Clémentine let him go without replying.

“What a bear!” she said to Adam. “You are a great deal nicer.”

Adam, unseen, pressed her hand. “Poor, dear Thaddeus,” he whispered. “He’s trying to make himself disagreeable where most men would try to seem more amiable.”

“Oh!” she said, “I am not sure but what there is some calculation in his behavior; he would have taken in an ordinary woman.”

Half an hour later, as the carriage waited, Clémentine asked where the captain lodged. “His apartments are over the coach-house,” Adam said. She noticed lights in Thaddeus’s rooms as they passed the portico—his dressing-gown, a pipe in his mouth, watching her enter the vestibule. The day had been hard for him. Seeing Mademoiselle du Rouvre at the Opera earlier had stirred him, and seeing Clémentine at her marriage had fixed his exclusive attachment. He had persuaded Adam and Clémentine to travel after their marriage to spare himself the pain; peace had lasted only while they were away.

As he sat at his window that memorable night, smoking his long wild-cherry wood pipe given him by Adam, thoughts occupied him: “I, and God, who will reward me for suffering in silence, alone know how I love her! But how shall I manage to have neither her love nor her dislike?”

He derived secret joy from the day’s deceptions. Since the couple’s return he had found satisfactions in knowing himself necessary to a household that otherwise would have been ruined. Clémentine, raised by a spendthrift father, knew nothing of household management; Adam, son of a lavish Polish lord, was not the kind to check fancies. Paz had prevented him from gambling at the Bourse, keeping them from ruin. Gratitude, the keynote of Paz’s life, held him bound; he could not leave where he alone could guard the affairs of the heedless owners. The sight of Clémentine driving in her pretty equipage stirred in him delights that did not show on his face.

How had Clémentine not perceived him for five months? Because he hid himself, carefully concealing the pains he took to avoid her. Nothing so resembles divine love as hopeless human love; a man must have depth to devote himself silently to a woman. Thaddeus had reached that height; filled with the joy of a creator he had attained a kind of grandeur. Yet he feared she might break his friendship with Adam if she took a dislike to him, or that if she coquetted with him merely for amusement he would be ruined. He scolded himself, went to bed, and resolved to await events.

The next day Clémentine breakfasted without noticing Paz’s absence. It was her reception day, when the house was semi-royally thrown open, a day that ordinarily fell on Paz to manage; she paid him little heed. He heard the last carriages drive away at two in the morning and told himself it was only a Parisian caprice that had made her seek him.

Clémentine’s Parisian life then absorbed her: winters were to fashionable women what campaigns were to soldiers—exhausting, extravagant displays spent on gowns and appearances that demanded constant attention. She found herself engaged in a struggle for social supremacy that left less time even to love her husband. Paz might well be forgotten. Yet one May day, as she drove home from the Bois before leaving Paris for Ronquerolles in Burgundy, she noticed Thaddeus sauntering in a side-path of the Champs-Élysées, delighted to see her in her elegant carriage.

“There’s the captain,” she said to her husband.

“He’s happy!” Adam answered. “He rejoices to think people envy our equipage. I see him nearly every day.”

“I wonder what he is thinking about now,” said Clémentine.

“He’s thinking this winter has cost a good deal, and it’s time we economized with your uncle Ronquerolles,” Adam replied.

Clémentine stopped the carriage near Paz and bade him take the seat beside her. Thaddeus flushed.

“I shall poison you,” he said; “I have been smoking.”

“Doesn’t Adam poison me?” she asked.

“Yes, but he is Adam,” returned the captain.

“And why can’t Thaddeus have the same privileges?” she smiled.

That smile broke through Paz’s heroic resolutions; he looked at Clémentine with the fire of his soul tempered by gratitude. Clémentine perceived a contrast in him: his fine presence and Adam’s puny frame and decay from intermarriage among the Polish aristocracy. Dangerous thoughts took hold of her; she sat silent and dreamy until they reached home.

“You will dine with us; I shall be angry if you disobey me,” she said as they turned in. “You are Thaddeus to me, as you are to Adam. I know your obligations, but I also know ours to you. Both generosities are natural—but you are generous every day and all day. My father dines here today, also my uncle Ronquerolles and my aunt Madame de Sérizy. Dress yourself,” she said, taking his hand as she stepped from the carriage.

Thaddeus dressed with a joyful dread, descended at the last moment, and again behaved at dinner like a soldier fit only for his duties as steward. But now Clémentine was not his dupe; her glance had enlightened her. The Marquis de Ronquerolles, an able diplomat known to the party, had been told by his niece of Paz’s real worth and knew how modestly he made himself steward of Laginski’s affairs.

“And why is this the first time I have the pleasure of seeing Comte Paz?” asked the marquis.

“Because he is so shy and retiring,” replied Clémentine, a look telling Paz to change his manner.

Paz, though superior in some respects to Adam, was not a man of parts. His apparent superiority owed much to misfortune; in his poverty-stricken life in Warsaw he had read and taught himself much but lacked the gift of original thought that marks a great man. Great in heart, he reached the sublime in sentiment, yet being more a man of action than of thought, he kept his feelings hidden.

After Clémentine’s speech, the Marquis and his sister exchanged a glance and embraced the niece, the count, and Paz. This rapid eye-speech, so French and Italian in its effect, meant more to Paz than to the others. “Do they think I might be loved?” he thought. “Then my happiness depends on my audacity!” For a moment love overcame gratitude and friendship; Paz became brilliant, told of the Polish insurrection with noble words when questioned by the diplomatist, and by the end of dinner Clémentine hung on his lips, regarding him as a hero and forgetting that Adam too had sacrificed much as an exile.

After coffee the principal guests left—Madame de Sérizy with Adam, the Marquis de Ronquerolles and the Marquis du Rouvre following soon. Paz and Clémentine were alone.

“I will leave you now, madame,” said Thaddeus. “You will, of course, rejoin them at the Opera?”

“No,” she answered, “I don’t like dancing; they give an odious ballet tonight.”

A pause followed.

“Two years ago Adam would not have gone to the Opera without me,” said Clémentine, not looking at Paz.

“He loves you madly,” said Paz.

“Yes, and because he loves me madly he is all the more likely not to love me tomorrow,” she replied.

“How inexplicable Parisian women are!” exclaimed Paz. “When they are loved to madness they want to be loved reasonably; and when loved reasonably they reproach a man for not loving them at all.”

“And they are quite right, Thaddeus,” she smiled. “I know Adam so well; I am not angry with him; he is volatile and above all a grand seigneur. He will always be content to have me as his wife and will never oppose my tastes, but—”

“Where is the marriage in which there are no ‘buts’?” Paz interjected gently.

Silence grew heavy. The least presumptuous man might have thought what nearly drove Paz beside himself: “If I do not tell her now that I love her I am a fool,” he kept saying. Neither spoke; the countess studied Paz; he observed her in a mirror. Buried in an armchair like a man digesting his dinner, Paz crossed his hands upon his stomach and twirled his thumbs.

“Why don’t you tell me something good of Adam?” cried Clémentine suddenly. “Tell me he is not volatile, you who know him so well.”

“Now is the time,” thought Paz, “to put an insurmountable barrier between us.” But he answered aloud, “I love him; you would not believe me; and I am incapable of telling you harm. My position is very difficult between you.”

Clémentine lowered her head and looked at his polished boots.

“You Northern men have nothing but physical courage,” she complained; “you have no constancy in your opinions.”

“How will you amuse yourself alone, madame?” Paz assumed a careless air.

“Are you not going to keep me company?”

“Excuse me for leaving you.”

“What do you mean? Where are you going?”

A heroic falsehood occurred to him. “I—I am going to the Circus in the Champs-Élysées; it opens tonight, and I can’t miss it.”

“Why not?” she asked, half angry.

“Must I confide to you what I hide from Adam, who thinks my only love is Poland?” he colored.

“Ah! a secret in our noble captain?”

“A disgraceful one—you will perhaps understand and pity,” he stammered.

“You, disgraced?” she smiled.

“Yes, I, Comte Paz; I am madly in love with a girl who travels all over France with the Bouthor family; they play at fairs. I had the director at the Cirque-Olympique engage her.”

“Is she handsome?”

“To my thinking,” said Paz, melancholy. “Malaga—that is her stage name—is strong, active, supple. When I look at her, her black hair tied with a blue ribbon, on her bare olive shoulders, dressed in white edged with gold, a living Greek statue, carrying flags to martial music, jumping through paper hoops that tear as she goes, and lighting so gracefully on the galloping horse—well, all that moves me.”

“More than a handsome woman in a ballroom?” Clémentine asked.

“Yes,” Paz answered chokingly. “Agility and grace under danger seem to me the height of triumph for a woman. Malaga is dexterity personified; her little wrist or foot can rid her of three or four men. She is the goddess of gymnastics.”

“She must be stupid—”

“Oh, no,” said Paz. “She is thoughtless as a Bohemian, says everything that comes into her head, and sometimes says grand things. She likes strong, handsome men. Her trainer used to beat her to make her supple and intrepid—”

“You are intoxicated with Malaga,” Clémentine laughed.

“She is called Malaga on the posters,” Paz retorted, piqued. “She lives in the rue Saint-Lazare in a pretty third-floor apartment, all velvet and silk, like a princess. She has two lives: the circus and the life of a pretty woman.”

“Does she love you?”

“She loves me—now you will laugh—solely because I’m a Pole. She saw an engraving of Poles charging with Poniatowski and was touched. But I am very unhappy, madame.” A tear fell from his eye and touched the countess.

“You men have such a passion for singularity,” she said.

“And you?” he asked.

“I know Adam so well that I am certain he could forget me for some mountebank like your Malaga. Where did you first see her?”

“At Saint-Cloud last September, on the fête-day. She was at a corner booth where the shows are given, silent and dumb; I thought I saw a melancholy in her face; it touched me.”

Clémentine sat pensive and somewhat melancholy.

“Poor, poor Thaddeus!” she exclaimed, then with the kindness of a true great lady and not without a malicious smile, “Go, go to your Circus.”

Thaddeus took her hand, kissed it, left a hot tear upon it, and went out.

Having invented this passion, he set about making it real. The only truth in his tale was the momentary attention he had given Malaga at Saint-Cloud; he had since seen her name on posters and, after paying a clown five francs for information, learned the rider’s real name: Marguerite Turquet, lodging in the rue des Fossés-du-Temple.

The next day Paz found the house and asked to see Mademoiselle Turquet. The portress called up the attic; a line of drying clothes snagged his hat as he climbed. He told Malaga he had seen her at the Circus and that she reminded him of a daughter he had lost; out of affection for that daughter he wished to be of service to her if she would permit it.

“Then am I to pass for your daughter?” Malaga asked slyly.

“Yes,” said Paz; “I’ll come and see you sometimes. You shall be lodged in better rooms, comfortably furnished.”

“You shall have furniture!” cried Malaga, delighted.

“And servants,” said Paz, “and all you want.”

Malaga looked suspicious. “What countryman is monsieur?” she asked.

“I am a Pole.”

“Oh! then I accept,” she said.

Paz departed, promising to return.

A month after this eccentric interview the circus-rider lived in a comfortable apartment furnished by Comte Adam’s upholsterer; Paz had judged it desirable to have his folly talked about at the hôtel Laginski. Malaga, whose adventure seemed like a leaf from the Arabian Nights, was served by Monsieur and Madame Chapuzot as friends and servants. The Chapuzots and Marguerite expected some result; but after three months none could make sense of the Pole’s caprice. Paz arrived once a week, spent an hour, sat in the salon, and never entered Malaga’s boudoir or bedroom, despite the Chapuzots’ manoeuvres. Each time he left two gold pieces of forty francs each on the mantelpiece.

“He looks as if he didn’t care to be here,” Madame Chapuzot said.

“Yes,” said Malaga, “the man’s as cold as an icicle.”

“But he’s a good fellow,” cried Chapuzot, happy in a new blue cloth suit that made him look like a minister’s servant.

The money Paz deposited weekly, joined to Malaga’s meagre salary, gave her means of a sumptuous life compared with her past. Stories circulated at the Circus about Malaga’s good fortune; vanity turned the six thousand francs Paz had spent into rumors of sixty thousand. In the demi-monde she was much talked of; some calumnies accused her of being magnetized or bewitched. These scandals distressed her.

“When I want to injure a woman,” she said, “I don’t calumniate her with magnetism; I say she is humpbacked and prove it. Why do you compromise me?”

Paz maintained a cruel silence. The Chapuzot soon discovered his name and title; then Malaga heard facts at the hôtel Laginski: Paz was a bachelor and had never been known to have had a daughter. Fear crept into her mind.

“My dear child,” Madame Chapuzot warned, “that monster”—a man who looked in slyly, not daring to speak—“is trying to get a hold upon you, and make you do something illegal and criminal. Holy Father, if you should get into the police-courts! I’ll tell you what I’d do; I’d warn the police.”

After many foolish notions fermented in Malaga’s mind, one day Paz left his customary gold on the mantelpiece; she seized it and flung it in his face, crying, “I don’t want stolen money!”

The captain gave the gold to Chapuzot, went away without a word, and did not return.

Clémentine was at her uncle’s estate in Burgundy at this time. When the Circus troupe discovered Malaga had lost her Polish count much excitement followed. Malaga’s display of honor was thought folly by some and shrewdness by others. The Pole’s conduct, even when discussed by the cleverest women, seemed inexplicable. Thaddeus received thirty-seven letters from curious women of their kind, but his reserve limited the gossip to the demi-monde.

Two weeks later the circus-rider, crippled by debt, wrote this letter to Comte Paz, which, having fallen into Adam’s hands, was read and admired by dandies:

“You, whom I still dare to call my friend, will you not pity me after all that has passed—which you have so ill understood? My heart disavows whatever may have wounded your feelings. If I was fortunate enough to charm you and keep you beside me in the past, return to me; otherwise I shall fall into despair. Poverty has overtaken me, and you do not know what horrid things it brings with it. Yesterday I lived on a herring at two sous, and one sou of bread. Is that a breakfast for the woman you loved? The Chapuzots have left me. Your desertion has shown me the bottom of all human attachments. The dog we feed does not leave us, but the Chapuzots have gone. A sheriff has seized everything on behalf of the landlord, who has no heart, and the jeweller would not wait—when we lose the confidence of such as you, credit goes too. My friend, I have taken all I have of value to my uncle; I have nothing but the memory of you, and winter is coming. I shall be fireless when it turns cold; the boulevards play only melodramas, in which I have little parts. How could you misunderstand the nobleness of my feelings for you?—for there are two ways of expressing gratitude. You, who seemed so happy in seeing me well-off, how can you leave me in poverty? Oh, my sole friend on earth, before I go back to country fairs with Bouthor’s circus where I can at least earn a living, forgive me if I wish to know whether I have lost you forever. If I were to think of you when I jump through the hoops, I should be sure to break my legs. Whatever may be the result, I am yours for life.

“Marguerite Turquet.”

“That letter,” thought Thaddeus, laughing, “is worth the ten thousand francs I have spent upon her.”

Clementine returned the next day, and Paz saw her the day after—more beautiful than ever. After dinner, once Thaddeus had gone, Adam produced Malaga’s letter, which he had taken from Thaddeus under the pretext of asking Adam’s advice.

“Poor Thaddeus!” Adam exclaimed as Paz slipped away. “What misfortune for a man of his distinction to be the plaything of that circus-rider. He’ll lose everything and sink lower and lower. Read that.” He handed the letter to his wife.

Clementine flung it aside with disgust. Adam complained that Malaga must have tricked Thaddeus. Clementine, contemptuous, pronounced that Thaddeus would forgive Malaga and that men were indulgent to such women. Adam defended him, noting Paz’s steadiness: for a Pole, he had been firm enough to spend no more than ten thousand on Malaga and to delay asking Adam for money until forced. Clementine retorted that Paz would ruin Adam. Adam, unwilling to see his friend ruined, believed Thaddeus would sacrifice Malaga if asked. They argued the morality of indulgence; Clementine’s tone kept widening a gulf of contempt between her and Adam.

Thaddeus, who had asked Adam for secrecy and a loan for Malaga, felt the sting of the countess’s disdain. Her contempt made him acutely unhappy. The Catholic ideal of elevated, noble love set his suffering apart: he bore contempt where reverence might have been. He had once lived apart from his mistress and with a quiet decency; now he sought the reward of virtue—her respect—and had lost it. His health suffered; in January he was bedridden, refusing doctors. Clementine, cruel at times, scornfully told Adam to “let him alone.” This contempt galvanized him; he left his bed, tried amusements, and recovered.

In late February Adam lost heavily at the Jockey Club and begged Thaddeus to make the loss appear in the accounts as money spent on Malaga. “If the countess finds I lost it at cards I shall be lowered in her opinion,” he pleaded. Paz sighed; he warned Adam against gambling because Adam would have children. Later Clementine discovered Adam had spent another twenty thousand on Malaga and erupted: thirty thousand francs in all—the fortune of a bourgeois—was gone, the income of which amounted only to fifteen hundred. She complained bitterly, reproaching Adam and Polish recklessness. Adam defended Paz, saying Paz had been useful—saving and investing sums a steward might have embezzled. Clementine’s harshness softened a little but she remained unforgiving toward Paz.

Not long after, Clementine summoned Paz to her boudoir. She reproached him with a cold, imperial scorn: if he loved Adam as he claimed, he would do what Adam would not ask but what she, the wife, demanded—give up Malaga. She called his behavior boyish and shameful; the woman had been made celebrated by his proceedings; many gentlemen now frequented her. Paz answered humbly that he would leave Malaga, but not immediately—he would wait until after the carnival. He acknowledged Malaga’s influence over him but insisted he had managed the countess’s household with skill and could not leave until he had confidence she would continue his work. He taught her household management and trained Constantin as major-domo. By the end of May he judged Clementine competent; she was at heart a woman of instinctive sagacity and soon could govern the household.

But fate intervened. Adam fell seriously ill. Thaddeus, instead of departing as he had promised, stayed to tend him. For forty-five days Paz watched and attended Adam without speaking of Malaga; in truth he scarcely thought of her during those nights. Clementine, half-broken with anxiety and alternation of hope and fear, summoned eminent physicians. The chief doctor warned that the patient’s recovery depended on a vigilant nursing force at a crucial moment. Thaddeus took up that role with unwearied devotion; his watchful eyes were like lamps always lit. The countess, seeing the strain, was forced to give up nursing lest she utterly break down.

On an afternoon, while the patient lay in extremis, Thaddeus, finding Clementine in the Chinese pavilion, betrayed the depth of his despair. He had never meant to aspire to be Clementine’s husband; he had sacrificed himself into a ditch of hopelessness. He confessed how much he would grieve for Adam, whom he had loved as a child, and how his life had been consumed by devotion. He admitted that if Adam died, he would himself be bereft of interest on earth, yet returned that life, though painful, might still be beautiful even for a widow of twenty-four. Clementine demurred—she loved no one; she preferred domesticated dependability to superior passion. He told her she did not yet know what love was. When she measured him with cool suspicion—finding him less lover than cupidity, and addressing him contemptuously as “Poor Malaga!”—he was crushed. He resumed his nursing, silent and relentless. He said nothing to Clementine, listened to her thanks without accepting them, and vowed to himself that she should owe Adam’s life to him.

At last, toward the end of August, Bianchon, one of the doctors, told Clementine that Adam was out of danger and that without Thaddeus they might not have saved him. Paz’s devotion had been decisive. The night after a meeting in the kiosk, the Marquis de Ronquerolles called to see his nephew on the eve of a secret mission to Russia. On the first day Adam could ride out, a messenger delivered to Paz a letter stamped with the foreign office. He read it, pocketed it, and as Adam begged to know its contents, Paz evaded. Overcome by curiosity on the road, Adam asked what was in the despatch; Paz finally confessed: he had been appointed captain in a regiment to take part in an expedition to Khiva. He meant to go, he told them—“captain I came, and captain I return”—and if he did not leave immediately for St. Petersburg he would have to go by land; he was not rich. He said he must leave Malaga some independence and confessed he had sacrificed honor for her: “That which we desire to keep at any cost in the eyes of our idol.” They passed the Circus on the Champs-Élysées and he pointed it out. He went to the Russian Embassy and the Foreign Office; by morning he had started for Havre and left for Russia.

Adam sorrowed; he felt he had lost a true friend. He could not understand why Paz had left as if a pestilence had been in the house. “We are not friends to quarrel about a woman,” he said to Clementine. She, uneasy, wondered if she had been the victim of a noble mystification. Constantin brought her a letter Paz had written during the night, which revealed the depth of his feeling and the motive for his going.

In the letter Thaddeus explained that to seek death in the Caucasus and carry with him Clementine’s contempt was unbearable. He loved her from the moment he first saw her, “as one loves a woman forever,” even though she had chosen Adam. He had been devoted as the steward; to be indispensable to her comfort and luxury was profound joy. He would groom her horse, find new-coined gold for her purse, arrange her dinners—these labors were his felicity. He confessed that when he had been eighteen in Warsaw he had once loved an ideal mistress in solitude; he had learned to love his love. He told her how, unseen, he had rejoiced in her smiles, and how from that living worship he had come to suspect, to interpret her goodwill and glances as something they might not be. In a moment of madness he had invented the Malaga story, a liaison he thought women could not forgive, as a test to reveal whether she might love him; his crime had been to suspect hope where none existed. Her contempt was his punishment.

He admitted that he had thought he might have torn Adam from death to betray him, and that thought was unendurable. He had nursed Adam when the doctors said his life depended on it; that duty had been supreme. He went on to say that he was not going to Khiva but to the Caucasus; he had the spleen and intended a pace of life that would make him—or kill him—in three years. He signed himself devotedly and departed, having taken some sixty thousand francs from Nucingen and assuring Adam their accounts were even.

For three years Paris heard nothing of any Prince Paz. The Comtesse Laginska remained interested in the Emperor Nicholas’s expeditions; she stayed, at heart, a little Russian and would ask the ambassador occasionally, with indifferent melancholy, whether anyone knew what had become of “our poor Comte Paz.” The narrative reflects on how many men like Paz pass through a woman’s life unrecognized. Noble love often appears raw and unpolished to those who expect their heroes neatly cut and set by society’s jeweler; many a genuine soul is misunderstood or misprized by the women who pass them by.

In January 1842, the Comte de La Palferine fell violently in love with Clementine. He was one of the most daring lions of the day and marked her as a guarded prize. La Palferine, aided by the jealous friend he had used to inveigle the countess, arranged a plan. Clementine, imprudently unable to suspect treachery, went with this friend to a masked ball at the Opera. About three in the morning, roused by the excitement and tempted, she consented to accept a supper and was about to enter the carriage of her faithless friend when a powerful hand seized her arm. She was taken, despite struggles, to her own carriage; as the carriage drove off she recognized Thaddeus—who vanished as mysteriously as he had appeared.

Did any woman ever have so strange a romance? Clementine was left forever hoping she might see Paz again.
