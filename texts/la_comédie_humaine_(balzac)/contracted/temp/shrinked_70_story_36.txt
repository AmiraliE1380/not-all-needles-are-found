To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, whose support has inspired my work on “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century.” 

In a small French town, the Hotel d’Esgrignon housed the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol, who remained loyal to his lands despite the Revolution. He married Mlle. de Nouastre in 1800, but she died in childbirth, leaving their son, Victurnien, as the family's last hope. The return of émigrés brought some hope, but the d’Esgrignons faced rivalry and financial struggles, becoming marginalized.

The Marquis was unaware of their financial issues, believing Victurnien would restore their status. Victurnien, spoiled and undisciplined, became embroiled in scandals and debts, aided by the loyal notary Chesnel. In Paris, he fell in love with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, accumulating debt to impress her. By late 1823, he owed 200,000 francs, leading to a confrontation with du Croisier. Mlle. Armande, heartbroken by the truth, resolved to rescue him as Victurnien faced an arrest warrant for forgery, culminating in a dramatic courtroom showdown that would change their lives.
