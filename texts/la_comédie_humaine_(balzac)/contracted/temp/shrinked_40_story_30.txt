To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron,—Your warm interest in my long, vast “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century,” and the encouragement you have given me to persevere, have made me feel entitled to associate your name with a portion of it. Are you not one of the most conscientious, studious representatives of Germany? Will not your approval secure for me the approval of others, and shield this attempt of mine? I am proud to have gained your good opinion, and have striven to deserve it by continuing my labors with the unflagging courage characteristic of your method of study and of that exhaustive documentary research by which you gave your monumental work to letters. Your sympathy with such labor as you yourself have bestowed upon the most brilliant civilization of the East has often sustained my ardor through nights of toil on the details of our modern civilization. You, whose naive kindliness can be compared only with our own La Fontaine’s, will be glad to know of this.

May this token of my respect for you and your work find you at Dobling, dear Baron, and remind you and yours of one of your most sincere admirers and friends.

DE BALZAC.

There stands a house at a corner of a street, in the middle of a town in one of the less important prefectures in France. The names of the street and town must be suppressed by convention; if a writer attempts to chronicle his own age he must touch many sore subjects, and discretion is due. The house was called the Hotel d’Esgrignon; let d’Esgrignon be a conventional name, as much a fiction as Belval or Floricour. Though the principal names will be disguised, truth will peep through contradictions and improbabilities, like shoots from a root you have thought uprooted.

The “Hotel d’Esgrignon” was simply the house of the old Marquis—Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol, Marquis d’Esgrignon. It was an ordinary house, but townspeople first joked about the name and, after twenty years, used it seriously.

The name Carol (or Karawl, as some would spell it) was glorious among Northmen chieftains who conquered Gaul and established feudalism. Their descent had been consistent: Carol never bowed before King, Commune, Church, or Finance. Charged once with guarding a French March, the marquisate in their family was a real office, not an empty title. Their fief had always been their domain. They were provincial nobles in every sense, with an unbroken line; neglected by court for two centuries, they were lords paramount locally, regarded with something like awe. The house of d’Esgrignon, buried in its remote border country, was preserved as one preserves charred piles of an ancient bridge—intact by reverence. For thirteen centuries daughters of the house married without dowries or took the veil; younger sons contented themselves with dower shares and went into the Church or the army; a cadet once became an admiral, duke, and peer of France, and died without issue. The Marquis of the elder branch would never accept a ducal title.

“I hold my marquisate as His Majesty holds the realm of France, and on the same conditions,” he told the Constable de Luynes—who seemed paltry to him then.

The d’Esgrignons lost heads on the scaffold during the troubles. The old blood showed proud even in 1789. The Marquis of that day refused to emigrate; he was answerable for his March. The countryside’s reverence saved his head, though sans-culottes’ hatred compelled him to pretend to flee, and for a while he lived in hiding. In the name of the Sovereign People, the d’Esgrignon lands were dishonored by the District, forests sold by the Nation despite the Marquis’s protest; he was forty then. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, his half-sister, saved some of the fief thanks to Chesnel, the young steward, who claimed for her the partage de présuccession—the right of a relative to a portion of an émigré’s lands. Thus the Republic made over the castle itself and some farms to Mlle. d’Esgrignon. Chesnel, faithful steward, bought in his own name the church, parsonage, castle gardens, and places dear to his patron—the Marquis advancing the money. Chesnel later became a notary.

The Terror’s slow, swift years passed, and the Marquis, esteemed in the countryside, decided he and his sister should return to the castle to improve the property Chesnel had saved from wreck. But the plundered, dismantled castle was too vast for a lord shorn of rights; too large for an estate whose woods had been sold piecemeal, yielding scarcely nine thousand francs’ income from residual estates.

In October 1800 Chesnel brought the Marquis back to the old feudal castle. The sight moved Chesnel deeply: his patron stood in an empty courtyard gazing at a filled moat and towers razed to roof level. The descendant of the Franks searched for missing Gothic turrets and weather vanes, his eyes turned to the sky as if to ask heaven why social upheaval had occurred. Only Chesnel could understand the deep anguish of the great d’Esgrignon, now called Citizen Carol. The Marquis stood long in silence, drinking in the place—his forefathers’ ancient home, the air about it—then flung out a melancholy exclamation.

“Chesnel,” he said, “we will come back again some day when the troubles are over; I could not bring myself to live here until the edict of pacification has been published; _they_ will not allow me to set my escutcheon on the wall.”

He waved to the castle, mounted his horse, and rode back beside his sister, who had driven in the notary’s shabby basket-chaise.

The Hotel d’Esgrignon in town had been demolished; factories now stood where the aristocrat’s house had been. So Chesnel spent the Marquis’s last louis on the old-fashioned building in the square—with its gables, weather-vane, turret, and dovecote. It had been the old courthouse and presidial; it had belonged to the d’Esgrignons for generations; and for five hundred louis it was made over in the title given by the Nation to its rightful lord. Half in jest, half seriously, the old house was christened the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

In 1800 little difficulty was made over removing names from the fatal list, and a few émigrés returned. Among the first were the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, ruined. M. d’Esgrignon generously offered them shelter; two months later the Baron died of grief in his house. The Nouastres were of the best provincial blood; Mlle. de Nouastre was twenty-two; the Marquis married her to continue his line. She died in childbirth, through an unskilful physician, leaving a son to bear the d’Esgrignon name. The old Marquis—only fifty-three but aged by adversity—saw the loveliest of women die, a noble figure in whom the charm of sixteenth-century femininity seemed to live. With her death joy left his old age. He stood beside her bed, kissed her forehead, drew out his watch, broke the mainspring, and hung it by the hearth. It was eleven in the morning.

“Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” he said, “let us pray God that this hour may not prove fatal yet again to our house. My uncle the archbishop was murdered at this hour; at this hour also my father died—”

He knelt and buried his face in the coverlet; his sister did likewise. Mlle. d’Esgrignon wept; the old Marquis’s eyes were dry. To stubborn Frankness he united Christian fortitude.

These events occurred in the second year of the nineteenth century. Mlle. d’Esgrignon was then twenty-seven, a beautiful woman. An ex-contractor for army forage with an income of six thousand francs, a district man, persuaded Chesnel to carry a marriage proposal to the lady. The Marquis and his sister were indignant at such presumption; Chesnel was almost heartbroken—he could not forgive himself for yielding. The Marquis’s manner toward his old servant changed; never again was their affection the same. From then on the Marquis’s gratitude wounded Chesnel; to some exalted natures gratitude seems an excessive payment—what they prefer is the equality that springs from mutual choice. Chesnel had known high friendship; the Marquis had raised him to his level. The Marquis saw his old servitor as more than a servant, less than a child—a voluntary liege man bound by affection. There were no balanced obligations; sincere affection made them unnecessary.

To the Marquis, Chesnel’s official dignity was nothing—he remained the notary in disguise. Chesnel, who felt part of the family, was furious with the presumed suitor du Croisier. He set others against Sieur du Croisier. Du Croisier, on the other hand, bore a grudge and nursed vengeance. He hated Chesnel and the d’Esgrignon family with the smothered, all-absorbing hate common in a country town. The rebuff ruined his standing among malicious provincials; in despair he courted a wealthy old maid and was refused again. He lost his hope of marrying Mlle. d’Esgrignon—access to the Faubourg Saint-Germain of the province— and his credit fell. He then married the old maid who had refused him and thereby worsened feuds.

In 1805 M. de la Roche-Guyon, an old family allied to the d’Esgrignons, proposed formally through Chesnel for Mlle. Marie Armande Clair d’Esgrignon. She declined, saying she was a mother—she had just put her nephew, little Victurnien, to bed.

The old Marquis rose, went up to his sister, returned from the cradle, kissed her hand reverently, and sat down again to say, “My sister, you are a d’Esgrignon.”

A quiver ran through the noble girl; tears stood in her eyes. M. d’Esgrignon, the father of the present Marquis, had married secondly the daughter of a tax farmer ennobled by Louis XIV—a shocking mesalliance to the family, though of no importance since a daughter was the sole child. Armande knew this; the brother, kind as he had been, still regarded her as a stranger. His words recognized her as one of the family. Her answer crowned eleven years of noble devotion: “I shall die Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” she said simply to Chesnel.

“For you there could be no fairer title,” returned Chesnel, intending a compliment. Poor Mlle. d’Esgrignon reddened.

“You have blundered, Chesnel,” the Marquis said, flattered but vexed that his sister was hurt. He discoursed on the family heraldry—“A d’Esgrignon may marry a Montmorency; their descent is not so pure as ours. The d’Esgrignons bear or, two bends, gules”—and spoke of their device, Cil est nostre, taken at a tournament in Philip Augustus’s reign, with supporters: a knight or on the right and a lion gules on the left.

“I do not remember that any woman I have ever met struck my imagination as Mlle. d’Esgrignon did,” Emile Blondet later wrote—who provides much of this history. He confesses his boyhood admiration perhaps colored by youth. If playing on the Parade and she walked by with her nephew Victurnien, seeing her thrilled him like galvanism. As a child he felt revived at sight of her. She had tawny-gold hair; a delicate down on her cheek with a silver gleam he loved to catch. Her dreamy emerald eyes flashed fire when they met his. He pretended to roll on the grass in games to admire her little feet up close; her soft whiteness, delicate features, clear forehead, and slender figure fascinated him without his fully understanding why. When she asked, in a musical voice, “What are you doing little one? Why do you look at me?” he would wriggle, bite his nails, red, and answer, “I do not know.” If she stroked his hair and asked his age, he would run and call, “Eleven!”

Every princess and fairy of his readings looked and walked like Mlle. d’Esgrignon; later when copying heads from the antique, he noticed their hair braided like hers. When fancies vanished, Mlle. Armande remained a type—he would see her as again Agnes Sorel, Marie Touchet, Gabrielle. He lent her all the love she never expressed. The angelic glimpses of her haunt him even as an adult.

Keep this portrait: it is faithful and instructive. Mlle. d’Esgrignon shows how pure goodness, without intelligence, may cause mischief.

Two-thirds of émigrés returned to France in 1804–1805; many exiles from the Marquis’s province returned. Some defected, entering Napoleon’s service or marrying upstart families; those who sided with the Empire regained fortunes and properties and mostly went to Paris. But eight or nine families remained true to the proscribed noblesse and to Bourbon monarchy: La Roche-Guyons, Nouastres, Verneuils, Casterans, Troisvilles, and others. Rich or poor, for them pedigree mattered above money. The Marquis d’Esgrignon was their acknowledged head. His house became their cenacle. There His Majesty, Emperor and King, was still “M. de Bonaparte”; “the King” meant Louis XVIII at Mittau; the Department was the Province; the prefecture the intendance.

The Marquis was honored for admirable behavior, noble loyalty, and undaunted courage; and respected in town for misfortunes, fortitude, and adherence to convictions. Such a man assumed the majestic dignity of ruined greatness. His chivalrous fair-mindedness was well known; litigants sometimes referred disputes to him. Even gentle Imperialists and authorities indulged his prejudices; but a rising section of new society—the Liberal party—laughed at an aristocratic oasis that one could not enter without proving irreproachable descent. Their animosity was all the keener because many town worthies believed the best society was to be found in the Marquis’s salon. The prefect’s wife, a Grandlieu, was sent as ambassadress.

Those excluded nicknamed the salon “The Collection of Antiquities,” and called the Marquis “M. Carol.” The receiver of taxes addressed his applications to “M. Carol (ci-devant des Grignons),” mockingly adopting an obsolete spelling.

“If I try to recall childhood,” Emile Blondet said, “the nickname always made me laugh despite my respect—my love—for Mlle. d’Esgrignon. The Hotel stood at the angle of two busy thoroughfares, not five hundred paces from the market. Two drawing-room windows looked on the street and two on the square; the room was like a glass cage: anyone passing could see through from side to side. Even at twelve I thought the salon a rare curiosity, on the border between reality and dream.”

The room, the ancient Hall of Audience, stood above cellars with grated air-holes—once prison cells of the old courthouse, now used as a kitchen. Blondet remembered the magnificent lofty hearth, covered in carved tracery like a melon. Above it an equestrian relief of Henri III., who reclaimed the ancient duchy for the crown, in a carved gilt frame. The ceiling between chestnut cross-beams bore scroll-work; remnants of gilding remained. The walls were hung with Flemish tapestry—six scenes from the Judgment of Solomon—framed with garlands, satyrs, and cupids. The parquet was laid by the present Marquis; Chesnel had acquired furniture at sale: Louis-Quatorze consoles, tables, clock-cases, andirons, sconces, and tapestry-covered chairs, which completed a stately room, large for the house. An ante-chamber, the old Salle des Pas Perdus, communicated with the magistrate’s deliberating chamber, used as dining-room.

Beneath old panelling, amid threadbare glories, eight or ten dowagers were arrayed in state in a quavering line—some palsied, others dark and shriveled, some erect, some bent—all tricked out in more or less fantastic outdated costumes with ribboned caps and faded lace. No painter or caricaturist ever caught the haunting fascination of those aged women; they come back in dreams. Whether misfortune taught me the secrets of irremediable disaster, or I grew to understand human feelings and the thoughts of Old Age and Regret, I know nowhere else have I seen the wan gray eyes or the dreadful brightness of others that I remember.

Neither Hoffmann nor Maturin ever gave Blondet such a thrill of terror as the automaton movements of those bodies sheathed in whalebone. Actors’ painted faces do not shock because the rouge is visible; but years had leveled those women’s faces and furrowed them into wrinkles, making them like carved German nutcrackers. Peering through the windows, Blondet noted battered bodies, ill-jointed limbs, lantern jaws, protuberant bones, abnormal hips; their movements as they came and went seemed as extraordinary as their sepulchral stillness at the card-tables.

The men looked gray and faded like the tapestries on the walls. Their dress conformed to the day, but their white hair, waxen faces, devastated foreheads, and pale eyes revealed kinship to the women, neutralizing any reality the costume might suggest.

The certainty of finding them at the same tables daily gave them in Blondet’s eyes a theatrical, unearthly interest. When he and schoolboys proposed to look at curiosities in the glass cage for fun, but seeing Mlle. Armande made him tremble. There was jealousy in his admiration for little Victurnien—he who belonged, they felt instinctively, to a different, higher order. It seemed strange that a fresh young creature should be in that awakened cemetery before its time. They could not explain it, but felt bourgeois and insignificant in presence of that proud court.

The disasters of 1813–1814, bringing Napoleon’s downfall, gave new life and hope to the Collection of Antiquities; but the events of 1815, the occupation, and government vacillation delayed fulfillment of their expectations. This story only begins to shape itself in 1822.

By 1822 the Marquis’s fortunes had not much improved despite Restoration changes for émigrés. Of all nobles hit by Revolutionary legislation, his case was hardest. Before 1789 the d’Esgrignons derived most income from manorial dues paid by their tenants; they had reduced holdings to swell quit-rents and heriots. Families in this position were hopelessly ruined. They were not helped by Louis XVIII’s ordinance returning unsold lands, and later indemnity laws could not restore suppressed rights; what revived was a land tax called domaines, with revenues going to the State.

The Marquis belonged to the small Royalist section that would tolerate no compromise with those they called, not Revolutionaries, but revolted subjects—or in parliamentary terms, Liberals or Constitutionnels. These Royalists, nicknamed Ultras by opposition, looked to Right-wing orators like M. de Polignac to protest the charter Louis XVIII had granted—regarded as an ill-advised, temporary concession, to be annulled when convenient. Thus the Marquis d’Esgrignon stood aloof, upholding the strictest Right until his vast fortune should be restored. He even scorned the indemnity advocated by the Villele ministry, intended to strengthen the Crown by ending lingering ownership distinctions.

The miracles of the Restoration in 1814, Napoleon’s return in 1815, their undoings, and reinstatements took the Marquis by surprise at sixty-seven. At that age many high-spirited men were worn out by long struggle; their activity in provincial retreats had hardened into immovable conviction, and they lived an enervating routine. Could anything be worse for a party than representation by old men when its ideas are already considered antiquated?

When Louis seemed firmly seated again in 1818, the Marquis asked what a man of seventy could do at court; content he was with the triumph of Monarchy and Religion while waiting for results of that indecisive victory. He continued as before, lord-paramount of his salon, the Collection of Antiquities.

But when victors of 1793 became vanquished, the nickname first given in jest was used in bitter earnest. The town, like other country towns, was full of party hatreds; du Croisier, unexpectedly, married the old maid who had refused him at first; this marriage produced a provincial war and hastened separation of great and small noblesse, of aristocratic and bourgeois elements which had briefly united under Napoleonic pressure. After the pressure lifted, class divisions revived with damage.

Vanity is the most national of French sentiments. Wounded vanity induced a thirst for Equality, although as the most ardent innovator will discover, Equality is impossible. Royalists pricked Liberals where they were most sensitive; this was sharpest in the provinces, where both sides accused the other of atrocities and employed the blackest deeds to secure public opinion. Individuals became identified with political opinions; opponents became private enemies. In a country town, theoretical disputes of Paris become personal combats—two duelists on watch for a chance to thrust steel. Each seeks to unbalance the other; political hatred becomes all-consuming. Epigram and slander are used to bring a party into discredit.

In such warfare, the nobles had the advantage of wit and delicate irony; du Croisier’s faction resorted to poisoned weapons. The Chevalier, who chose the aristocratic Mons Sacer and turned his back on mixed society, directed his jests at du Croisier’s salon, stoking fires he could not foresee. Only purists and loyal gentlemen and women entered the Hotel d’Esgrignon; they committed no indiscretions. If Liberals sought to ridicule the nobles, they fastened on political actions; meanwhile the intermediate class—officials and others who paid court to higher powers—kept nobles informed of Liberal doings, much of it laughable. Du Croisier’s adherents smarted under inferiority, which increased revenge thirst.

In 1822 du Croisier headed the province’s manufacturing interest while the Marquis headed the noblesse. Each represented a party. But du Croisier, rather than pose frankly as extreme Left, adopted positions later associated with the 221 deputies, thus keeping touch with magistrates, officials, and capitalists. His salon rivaled the d’Esgrignon one in power, larger, younger, and more energetic, felt across the countryside; the Collection of Antiquities remained inert, a passive appendage, often embarrassing central authority; Conservative pressure sometimes led the Government to fatal blunders.

The Liberals had never successfully carried a candidate. The department declined to obey them knowing du Croisier would, if elected, sit on the Left-Centre benches and lean Left. He corresponded with the Keller brothers, bankers linked to the nineteen deputies of the Left; M. Keller was related by marriage to Comte de Gondreville, a Constitutional peer still in favor. Thus the Constitutional Opposition was prepared to vote last-moment, not for its professed candidate but for du Croisier if necessary; yet du Croisier was routinely defeated by Royalists. The party leaders, taking tone from the Marquis, had thoroughly gauged him; each defeat made du Croisier more bitter. Nothing so stirs up strife as the failure of an elaborate snare.

By 1822 hostilities seemed to lull—both salons measured strengths and waited for opportunity. Ordinary folk were content with surface quiet that deceived the Government; those who knew du Croisier knew his implacable passion for revenge, especially in political ambition. Du Croisier, who used to turn white and red at the bare mention of d’Esgrignon or the Chevalier, chose an impassive countenance masking savage feeling. He smiled on enemies while hating them the more, watching them hourly. One partisan aiding him was M. du Ronceret, President of the Tribunal, a small squire who’d vainly tried to enter the Antiquities.

The d’Esgrignons’ small fortune, managed carefully by Chesnel, barely sufficed; though they lived without ostentation, they lived like nobles. The governor found an Oratorian tutor for the heir, the young Comte Victurnien d’Esgrignon, who lived with the family and drew a salary. The wages of a cook, a waiting-woman for Mlle. Armande, an old valet for the Marquis, and a few other servants, daily household expenses, and the cost of a thorough education—spared in no way—absorbed the family income despite Mlle. Armande’s economies and Chesnel’s careful management, and the servants’ affection. Chesnel had not yet begun repairs on the ruined castle; he waited for leases to fall in so he might raise farm rents—rents had been rising due to improved agriculture and currency depreciation, benefits the landlord would realize at lease expirations of 1809.

The Marquis knew nothing of his household’s management or finances. Near the end of his life he and his circle clung to the illusion that the neglected d’Esgrignon house would be revived by his handsome heir, Victurnien, who would go to Court, enter royal service, and marry into an illustrious family. Their intimates—the Troisvilles, La Roche-Guyon, the Casterans, and the Duc de Verneuil—encouraged this belief. But forty years of history could not be erased: the restored monarchy and public opinion now demanded not only noble birth but money, political relevance, and continuous prestige. Old renown could not substitute for wealth and influence. Even the Troisvilles’ daughter’s marriage to General Montcornet, far from flattering antiquated pride, nearly split social circles—evidence that times had changed.

Only Chesnel, the family’s old notary and former steward, saw the situation clearly. Devoted yet practical, he had witnessed the Revolution’s violence and the triumph of new social ideas; he understood that confiscations and deaths were binding facts and that the new order would not be overturned. His attachment to the d’Esgrignons was therefore affectionate and lucid. He tried tactfully to warn the Marquis, who met caution with a prophet’s smile and the conviction that history would repeat in their favor. Chesnel feared their superstitions and self-delusions would ruin Victurnien’s character.

Idolized by his father and aunt, Victurnien was thoroughly spoiled but in some respects justified their hopes. Mlle. Armande loved him as a mother might but lacked maternal severity and foresight; she could not foresee danger. Chesnel’s pragmatic vigilance resembled a mother’s instinct more than the aunt’s blind adoration, but the household generally paid little heed to his counsels—he had even fallen into disgrace after proposing an imprudent marriage for the family.

From infancy Victurnien was taught to assume superiority: peers among great nobles, the King his only superior, and the rest of mankind beneath him. With beauty, tact, a quick mind, and a thorough education—average height, graceful, an excellent horseman and marksman—he had every advantage to be a great man. But indulged and unopposed, he became insolent, audacious, and self-willed. The Chevalier, a charming old roue, misled the boy by glorifying the reckless manners and witty immorality of an earlier era; his jocular maxims mingled with the family’s rigid emphasis on honor. Thus good and bad counsel shaped Victurnien.

At eighteen he entered society and quickly misread appearances. Accustomed to deference at home and to unguarded talk before an elderly father, he failed to perceive the hypocrisies and practical cautions of the larger world. His reckless behavior—shooting over neighbors’ lands, amorous imprudences requiring settlements and legal cover-ups—led to lawsuits and scandals quietly hushed by Chesnel, who paid to avert public shame. Encouraged by adroit enemies—particularly Du Croisier, who plotted revenge against the family—Victurnien’s faults were fanned. President du Ronceret and other officials, while outwardly lenient, fostered liberal clamor and public suspicion to discredit nobles; Du Croisier hoped to drive the d’Esgrignons into ruin through the young man’s vices. A young instigator, Fabien du Ronceret, befriended Victurnien to lead him on.

Between eighteen and twenty-one Victurnien ran up considerable debts—nearly eighty thousand francs paid out by Chesnel, who hid this from Mlle. Armande and the Marquis. The family’s mode of life left little margin: the Marquis’s income barely covered the household. Victurnien’s tailor bills, tastes for English saddles and carriages, livery, and fashionable accessories swallowed his allowance. Chesnel, drawing on private savings of about twelve thousand livres yearly, became the young man’s secret benefactor, paying off lawsuits and bailing him out repeatedly. He hoped a sensible marriage would settle Victurnien, but the young man’s temperament—capable of lofty thought yet habitually surrendering to impulse—made him likely to repeat mistakes.

Chesnel’s patience and warnings met with shrugging complacency from the Chevalier and indulgence from the family. The Chevalier dismissed debt as par for a gentleman’s course, recalling aristocratic generosity and minimizing danger. Chesnel foresaw ruin. His fear and affection prompted a decisive plan: to have Victurnien sent to Paris and presented at court, hoping the change would sober the boy and provide opportunities befitting his rank.

One autumn evening in 1822, after cards and salon talk, the household confronted the truth about Victurnien’s debts. The Chevalier forced disclosure to the Marquis: Chesnel had lent an enormous sum—“something like a hundred thousand livres”—to the Count. The Marquis was mortified that his devoted steward would be the family’s creditor; pride and old notions of dignity made it painful to ask Chesnel for help plainly. Nevertheless, they resolved to send Victurnien to Paris to present himself to the King and secure an appointment; they accepted Chesnel’s loan rather than humiliate the family by other means.

Chesnel explained that Victurnien’s companions and enemies in town—the president, Du Croisier and his circle—were seeking vengeance by ensnaring the young man, provoking scandals to ruin the family. He had, accordingly, borrowed money himself, mortgaging Le Jard, and had made arrangements in confidence so the loan would not appear as a direct family shame. Mlle. Armande and the Marquis were moved; Chesnel kissed her hand and, with paternal tenderness, urged that Paris provide the boy with more amusement and diversion than provincial society could, while also placing him where discretion and better company might discipline him.

Preparations were hurried. The Marquis and Mlle. Armande instructed Victurnien to go to court, serve His Majesty, and there seek a career—army, navy, diplomacy, or royal household. They promised a modest provision and sent Josephin, Victurnien’s trusted valet, as companion. Chesnel wrote to an old notary friend in Paris, Maître Sorbier, begging vigilant oversight and intimating funds would be placed at Sorbier’s disposal. His letter revealed a deep emotional tie: he felt he had reared and protected the d’Esgrignons through revolution and decline, and that the young Count’s fate was intimately his concern.

Victurnien accepted a monthly allowance of two thousand francs and left amid blessings. He felt no genuine regret; provincial life had been gentle but narrow, and Paris promised to amplify his vanity and gratify his tastes. In the capital he discovered that noble names no longer guaranteed employment; money and new social forces mattered most. Undeterred, he took to luxury and fashion as necessary to command respect and assumed a posture of independence that concealed need.

He took rooms, stables, and equerries at great expense. His family’s reputation and the Faubourg Saint‑Germain’s curiosity opened many doors: old names still served as social passports. He found a circle of fashionable young men—de Marsay, Rastignac, de Ronquerolles, Maxime de Trailles—who initiated him into pleasures. The Vidame de Pamiers, a discreet source of gossip and introductions, welcomed Victurnien and took him to salons and dinners where literature and wit mixed with vanity and intrigue. There he met the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, an ethereal, calculating beauty whose pose of innocuous purity masked self-regard and power.

Thus Victurnien entered fashionable life at its center. His name, looks, and Faubourg patronage made him desirable; his vanity made him determined to sustain an image of wealth and consequence. Chesnel’s precautions were undermined by Parisian finance and notaries eager for patronage; the young Count’s expenses—lodgings, dress, horses, entertainments—quickly devoured the funds set aside. Despite Chesnel’s hopes that Paris would rescue him from provincial follies, the city offered new temptations, alliances, and risks that promised either brilliant ascent or irreversible ruin.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs, a fact neither Chesnel nor Mlle. Armande suspected. Victurnien had also taken two thousand crowns from Chesnel in small sums to conceal his sources, while sending deceiving letters to his unsuspecting father and aunt. Parisian life was quietly preparing a dreadful catastrophe for the d’Esgrignon family; only du Croisier knew, and he intended not merely to ruin but to dishonor them. He had been certain of this since discovering the dangerous growth of Victurnien’s debts.

Du Croisier’s first move was to corner his most hated enemy, the kindly notary Chesnel. The old man, living modestly in the Rue du Bercail and devoted to preserving the d’Esgrignon estates for posterity, was unprepared when Du Croisier arrived one evening and produced a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs—principal and interest—for sums advanced to Victurnien in bills of exchange drawn upon, and honored by, du Croisier. He demanded immediate payment and threatened legal proceedings against the heir-presumptive. Chesnel, distraught, surrendered the unlucky letters and begged for secrecy; du Croisier agreed to hold his peace if paid within forty-eight hours.

Pressed for money, Chesnel had already helped manufacturers and used the usual financial stratagems; he was near tears. The only way to raise such a sum would be to mortgage his lands fully. Sensing Chesnel’s incapacity, du Croisier abruptly offered to buy the notary’s property. The sale was arranged within two days. Chesnel, horrified at the thought of the d’Esgrignon heir undergoing imprisonment for debt, accepted; he was left with his practice, the sums due to him, and the house where he lived. Stripped of his lands, he paced his oak-paneled office and ceased thinking of Le Jard, thinking only of Victurnien.

“What will become of him? He ought to come back; they must marry him to some rich heiress,” he muttered, burdened by foreboding. He dreaded breaking the news to Mlle. Armande; this man who had paid the family’s debts quaked at the thought. He went to the Hotel d’Esgrignon fearing the ruin of illusions.

At the hotel Mlle. Armande had just received a charming, mendacious letter from her nephew, the happiest man alive, describing baths and travels in Italy with Mme. de Maufrigneuse—Venice, Milan, Florence—painting happiness everywhere. The poor aunt was enthralled and dreamt herself in Victurnien’s gondola, excusing a married woman’s love for her nephew. She was not an ascetic but a serene, tender figure; at that moment she allowed herself to be carried away.

Chesnel’s heavy step on the garden walk recalled her to reality. “What is it?” she cried. “All is lost!” Chesnel answered, holding out the bills and telling briefly of his recent agony. “He is deceiving us! The miserable boy!” she cried. Chesnel accepted some responsibility: “We have allowed him his own way; he needed stern guidance—he had no mother.” The Marquis, having been absorbed in his son’s bright letter, recounted ancestral glories and remained unconcerned. The two companions in distress sat together.

“You and M. le Marquis own the estate conjointly; the larger part is yours. You can raise money on it without saying a word to him,” Chesnel said. Mlle. Armande decided she must go to Paris to snatch her nephew from perdition; if anyone could carry him off, it was the woman whose maternal heart yearned over him. She meant to see the Duchess and tell her everything. For appearances she allowed it to be thought she suffered from a complaint requiring consultation with famous Parisian physicians—an excuse for the journey. She took Chesnel’s last bag of louis, her capuchine and mittens without comment. “Generous girl!” Chesnel said, delivering her to the carriage with her maid.

Du Croisier had planned his revenge with provincial meticulousness: measured, remorseless vengeance. He had concluded Victurnien’s debts growing perilous and contrived to ruin the family’s credit. He wrote to Victurnien informing him that the Kellers had been instructed to advance no more money; the letter was timed to arrive when the Duchess and the Count faced artful poverty. In that missive he left a wide blank between the formal text and his signature. It was a simple trick: one could tear off the upper part and convert the signed lower part into a bill of exchange for any amount. The sheet’s reverse was blank and its envelope concealed the manipulation.

When the letter arrived, Victurnien was already at the lowest ebb. After two years of luxurious, thoughtless living he was confronted by the absolute impossibility of procuring money. With the Duchess’ help he had extorted sums from bankers, but these loans were about to produce overdue bills and stern summonses from the Bank of France and the commercial court. For five years his purse had always been replenished; now, after Chesnel came du Croisier, and he had expected another gold-mine to pour out its wealth. He had been winning at play; fortune had spared him unpleasant necessities before. Now life became a chaotic nightmare—disorder, confusion, a frantic struggle to shut out debts and duels. He alone lived through this infernal prospect, seeing visions of a friendless outcast reading the last words on the last page—THE END.

He already imagined acquaintances enjoying his downfall. Even those who had played high could not spare a banknote for a friend. There seemed no help—Chesnel would be ruined; he had been consumed by Victurnien’s extravagance.

Sitting with the Duchess in their box at the Italiens, envied by the house but inwardly despairing, Victurnien reached such depths that he had even taken out pistols and thought of suicide. After the Opera he and the Duchess had retreated to their secret lodging; appearances were cleverly saved—their love-nest seemed a garret to outsiders but was luxuriously arranged within. Now, on the brink of ruin, he longed to flee. An evil thought crossed him: he would run away with the Duchess to some remote nook in America only if he could carry a fortune, leaving creditors to confront their bills. To carry out this plan he had only to cut off the lower portion of du Croisier’s letter bearing the signature and fill in figures to turn it into a bill, presenting it to the Kellers. There was a dreadful struggle with temptation; tears were shed; but the honor of the family triumphed—subject to one condition. Victurnien would do nothing unless he was sure of his beautiful Diane; he wanted her consent to their flight.

So he went to the Duchess in the Rue Faubourg Saint-Honoré. Mme. de Maufrigneuse was pensive but resolute. She met cares gallantly; underneath graceful helplessness lay strength, insight, rapid decision and a cold resolve. She could either rise to the highest sentiments or show selfish insensibility. Pondering her fall, she set love aside to consider safety: refuge abroad, confessing to the King, captivating a du Tillet or Nucingen, or gambling on the stock exchange—all were calmly reviewed. She could stand apart and watch a crash as a spectator rather than be buried beneath it. Such dispassionate power was formidable.

While dressing for a drive she had collected her thoughts and faced the possibilities of terrible downfall. She plucked love out of her heart to weigh necessities and was ready to reinstate passion once her position was safe. She resolved.

Victurnien entered, terror in every line. He dared not touch the cornerstone that upheld his life—Diane. He spoke, with aristocratic disdain for sordid detail, of his hopeless situation. “What is the matter?” Diane asked. “I am hopelessly in debt. I have come to the end.” “Pshaw! it is nothing,” she replied with a smile. “Money can always be arranged; nothing is irretrievable except disasters in love.” Relieved by her composure, he unrolled the bright web of his life’s recent past—the seamy side—detailing his needs.

She listened, intent and grave with love. When Victurnien proposed flight, she cried, “You are an angel!” The Duchess made no immediate answer; she weighed plans. “Good, very good,” she said at last. “But that is not the question, dear. Let us think of your affairs. Yes, we will go, and the sooner the better. Arrange it; I will follow. It is glorious to leave Paris and the world.” Her promise settled him; he would not go until it was sealed. He counted on his aunt, father, and Chesnel to hush up the affair; perhaps Chesnel might seek some compromise. Besides, obtaining money by this “business” was the only way to raise funds on the family estate. With three hundred thousand francs he and Diane would lead a hidden life in Venice.

The next day Victurnien drew a bill for three hundred thousand francs and took it to the Kellers. They advanced the money—du Croisier happened then to have a balance—but cautioned that he must not draw again without notice. Du Croisier, much astonished, asked for a statement of accounts. It was sent. Everything was explained. The day of his vengeance had arrived.

################################################
When Victurnien withdrew the money, he took it to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who locked the banknotes in her desk and proposed one last night at the Opera. Distracted by fear that his place in the Duchess’ box would cost him dearly, Victurnien thought it wiser, with the three hundred thousand francs safe, to hurry to Chesnel and confess. The Duchess, unwilling to leave her beloved nest, looked at him in a way that kept him sleepless.

The next afternoon Victurnien returned to learn her plan for that night’s escape. She scoffed at going into hiding—other women had vanished, she insisted, and they would brave the storm, certain of success. Victurnien, unnerved, hesitated. Diane reproached him with coldness; stung, he could not yield. She accused him of ingratitude after eighteen months’ tenderness and turned away. He protested, wept, implored; she toyed with her scarf, launched cruel epigrams at his rivals (Vandenesse, de Marsay, Montriveau), and kept him in suspense until, exasperated, he fled in a fury.

Near the Quai d’Orsay his horse bolted; Josephin stopped him with news: he could not go home—the authorities had a warrant for his arrest. In his agitation Victurnien imagined a criminal arrest, having forgotten the bills-of-exchange affair that had been “stirred up” into orders to pay and legal proceedings. Told to flee to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine in the Rue de Grenelle, he seized the refuge of his aunt, Mlle. Armande. At the inn he collapsed into her arms; she wept, half believing herself complicit in his ruin. That night they set out for Brest—Victurnien obsessed with his forgery, his aunt worrying over debts and bills. He suggested hiding in Chesnel’s house; she soothed him like a child.

Chance betrayed them. That evening Chesnel had been negotiating to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir’s clerk for a hundred thousand francs. His successor saw Mlle. Armande arrive at an odd hour and watched. When Chesnel opened his door and saw Victurnien, he understood at once. He ushered the Count into a room beyond his private office and, thunderstruck but devoted, wept. Refusing to admit that a forger’s scandal should reach the old man’s father, Chesnel raged and proposed desperate remedies—softening du Croisier, buying back the draft from the Kellers, hiding Victurnien in a cellar—and then set off for Paris himself. He packed, took money, left the Count locked in the room, and told his housekeeper to bar visitors.

Chesnel reached Paris in three days. At the Kellers he learned the draft had already returned to du Croisier; the document belonged to du Croisier, who could keep or return it. In despair, Chesnel went to the Duchess. By a mix of persuasion and insolence he gained admittance, told her bluntly the hundred thousand crowns she held had been gained by a forgery committed for love of her. Struck by shame and fear of implication, she handed over the notes and pledged to use her influence to save Victurnien, promising to save him “or die.” Triumphant, Chesnel left for the Rue du Bercail—but arrived an hour too late. The gendarmes had already come and taken Victurnien away.

Wild with grief, Chesnel begged the officers for mercy. Seeing the Count led off, he whispered that if Victurnien could not hush the matter he must kill himself before the indictment. Stupefied, Victurnien could not comprehend. Chesnel then dashed to the Hotel du Croisier, where du Croisier sat that night surrounded by intimates—M. du Ronceret, President of the Tribunal; M. Sauvager, deputy Public Prosecutor; M. du Coudrai; the examining magistrate M. Camusot; among others. The gathering was half political, half social; they discussed the d’Esgrignon affair as a test of social and legal principle.

Sauvager assumed the complacent tone of a zealous official. Camusot, who had authorized the warrant, was perturbed the arrest had been so prompt. They spoke of counsel from Paris—Berryer for the defense—and of the sensation this case could cause in Landernau, aware that high influence might intervene. Chesnel sought the examining magistrate in the street and implored him: support the house of d’Esgrignon and the Duchess, he hinted, and dukes and ministers would interest themselves in Camusot. He even threatened to denounce suspected corruption at du Croisier’s if the magistrate were hostile. Camusot, already sensitive to his wife’s influence and the wider stakes, listened.

Chesnel then tried to soften du Croisier. He begged, on bended knee, offering lands, service, anything if du Croisier would withdraw the charge. But du Croisier reveled in his power. He saw in prosecuting the Count a chance to assert bourgeois dignity against the aristocracy and to put nobles in their place. He delighted in the idea of exciting the Court and the public by bringing down a proud house. He demanded more than money: political advantage, social recognition, and even matrimonial leverage tying property as surety—schemes that Chesnel and Mme. du Croisier found odious.

Du Croisier framed the affair as larger than personal revenge: it was “France,” “the people,” a lesson that the bourgeoisie would not be trampled upon. He imagined victory in the Assize Court and a marriage that would cement his political aims. Desperate, Chesnel produced the hundred thousand crowns and implored Mme. du Croisier to accept them to spare the d’Esgrignons. Mme. du Croisier hesitated, torn between her husband’s will and pity. Chesnel, pressing every religious and familial plea, persuaded her to take the money and promise to swear before magistrates that she had received it on a date prior to the contested bill’s drafting—an explicit falsehood he urged as a “venial sin” to save lives and honor.

To secure her resolve, Chesnel planned to involve the Abbé Couturier and the bishop, knowing clerical counsel would strengthen Mme. du Croisier’s willingness to perjure herself. He roused Mlle. Armande to rally ecclesiastical support and went home exhausted, having mapped out the men he must influence and the political tactics required.

The narrative then widens to the provincial administration. Magistrates often begin careers in the provinces and split into two classes: the settled, torpid men resigned to local life, and the younger, ambitious men who seek promotion to Paris and look to shine at politically charged trials. In this town the bench was divided: conservative elders like President du Ronceret and old Blondet represented the resigned section; younger zealots like Camusot and his deputy formed the ambitious faction eager to make reputations by zealous prosecutions. The provincial administration leaned bourgeois and often displayed an overzealous, politicized public zeal that could overstep discretion.

President du Ronceret appears as a petty, resentful man—nominated where he had once expected more—vaunting independence but practicing inconsistency, maintaining social pretensions while nursing private rivalries. Old Blondet, by contrast, is a veteran jurist, prudent and respected though sidelined. Chesnel’s understanding of these factions—their ambitions, petty rancors, and hopes of patronage—shapes his strategy. He sees that many men, still bound to hopes of advancement, may be swayed if they think powerful patrons in Paris support them. Thus he plans to use the Duchess’s influence and court connections as leverage.

The crisis deepens: Victurnien has been arrested; Chesnel has bribed, pleaded, and schemed; the Duchess has returned the notes and promised aid; du Croisier delights in his leverage and demands political recompense; Mme. du Croisier, torn, agrees—after Chesnel’s urging and the money in hand—to give false testimony; and the local magistrates, split between ambition and caution, loom as the battleground. Chesnel lies awake mapping out the men to influence, promising to set great powers in motion through Mme. de Maufrigneuse, while the provincial world of judges, clerks, and aspiring officials gathers round the coming trial with all the pettiness and consequence of a local theater of justice.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Blondet possessed legal talent but despised his profession, devoting his ambitions, leisure, and energy to gardening. He corresponded with celebrated amateurs and sought botanical discoveries; his greenhouse, where pelargoniums reigned, was his refuge. Court business mattered little beside the inner world of fancies and emotions he cultivated among flowers. Had he been less absorbed, he might have become a deputy under the Empire and prominent in the Corps Législatif.

His marriage deepened that retreat. At forty he rashly wed an eighteen-year-old, who bore him Joseph. Three years later Mme. Blondet, then the town’s prettiest woman, inspired a lasting passion in the prefect, who became the father of her second son, Emile. The affair was public knowledge, including to old Blondet. Rather than fueling ambition, the marriage encouraged his botanical tastes; neither husband nor wife wished to change places. Unequal to contesting youth and beauty, Blondet sought consolation in his greenhouse and kept a pretty servant. Mme. Blondet spent his substance on dress; only Emile’s education interested her. She loved Emile, Blondet loved Joseph, each blinded by paternal partiality.

For twelve years Blondet bore these circumstances with eighteenth‑century grand seigneur composure, though he profoundly disliked his younger son. When his wife died in 1818 he turned Emile out, sending him to Paris on a scant allowance; Emile would have perished without help from his real father. Blondet’s house, opposite the prefecture, had a neat court and admired flowerbeds; climbing plants covered walls, honeysuckle hid piers, and cactuses perched in vases. Through glass doors one saw the garden that extended into two acres. Blondet’s income was about four thousand livres from land plus the house. He intended to leave his property and the garden bench to Joseph, making a will favoring him and setting aside money so Joseph could repurchase portions legally reserved to Emile.

Emile, turned adrift, found distinction in Paris as a man of letters and in society, aided by friendships like that of Mlle. de Troisville (later Mme. de Montcornet). Mme. Blondet had tried to link him to the Troisvilles, who later favored him in Paris. Emile’s precocious literary reputation and good looks strengthened his bond with the Countess de Montcornet; by the time adventuring pressed upon him, their attachment proved unshakeable. Old Blondet never knew of Emile’s success; convinced his son was cursed, he remained ignorant of Emile’s greatness.

Blondet’s integrity matched his passion for flowers. He knew law and botany alone, met litigants courteously, accepted seeds as gifts, and on the bench was impartial; litigants trusted his clear procedure. His steadiness made him indispensable to President du Ronceret, who, apart from matrimonial schemes, would have resisted forced retirement in favor of a son. Blondet could not foresee that Emile might have fulfilled such hopes.

Their household was frugal to parsimony: fruit saved until it spoiled, no new bread, strict fast days. The maid, Mlle. Cadot, kept keys and watched expenses; she shared Blondet’s predilection for Joseph. Blondet tried to mold Joseph into a lawyer to satisfy Mlle. Blandureau’s parents, who insisted he be an assistant judge to marry their daughter. Joseph spent evenings at the Blandureaus’, where Fabien du Ronceret also frequented. Everything was measured; the garden, however, escaped thrift. The gardener kept paths and borders immaculate, and an amphitheatre of benches held thousands of pelargoniums that drew visitors from distant departments; Empress Marie Louise once visited and Blondet received the Legion of Honor. Blondet shunned society and was unaware of the President’s machinations.

Michu, a young judge with twelve thousand livres income, cared more for pleasing high society than for routine. He did enough at the Tribunal to be useful and, with Parisian training as procuring counsel, could quickly solve legal questions that baffled others. He was socially and professionally indispensable, able to mediate delicate matters.

Crisis arrived while the public prosecutor was in Paris. The President and du Croisier seized his absence to engineer a charge of forgery against Victurnien d’Esgrignon. Normally such a case might be hushed to avoid scandal, but the President contrived to have M. Sauvager, a young Royalist deputy public prosecutor who owed his place to subservience to the Ministry, undertake the charge without consulting his absent chief. The President and du Croisier persuaded Sauvager by promising advantageous matches and future fortunes if he showed devotion, urging him to act quickly to preempt Parisian intervention. Sauvager agreed.

Camusot’s unexpected defection terrified the plotters. The examining magistrate Camusot had risen by backstairs influence: son of a silk mercer, married to Amélie Thirion (daughter of an usher of the Royal cabinet), he advanced through connections. Mme. Camusot, with Parisian instincts and impatience for provincial life, meddled in her husband’s career; she saw the d’Esgrignon affair as an opportunity to ingratiate the family with powerful circles, including the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, and secure promotion and a Paris appointment. Perceiving the President’s underhand maneuver, she advised Camusot to side with the d’Esgrignons, foreseeing advantage.

Chesnel, who knew the Tribunal’s circle, counted on the public prosecutor, Camusot, and Michu to support the d’Esgrignons; he also believed Blondet, if persuaded, would favor what he saw as justice. Before dawn he was roused by a startling visitor: the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, disguised in man’s attire, had driven from Paris to save Victurnien. She declared she had brought a hundred thousand francs from the King’s private purse to buy Victurnien’s innocence if necessary; if bribery failed, she had a vial of poison to prevent an indictment. She had sent word to the public prosecutor, who was following her.

Chesnel embraced the Duchess’s aid. They went to see Camusot early; the Duchess intended to charm Mme. Camusot and secure Camusot’s cooperation. Playing her role, she understood provincial manners and boasted of Thirion’s influence at court. She and Chesnel planned that the Duchess would speak to Mme. Camusot, and Chesnel would persuade Camusot that rescuing the d’Esgrignon was in the kingdom’s interest. The Duchess insisted on secrecy.

Mme. Camusot, quick to grasp the stakes, accompanied the Duchess to Blondet’s greenhouse, presenting the Duchess as a distinguished horticulturist. She warned Blondet of the President’s duplicity: the President aimed to thwart Blondet’s hopes for Joseph’s marriage to Mlle. Blandureau, and Fabien du Ronceret had received clandestine proposals for Mlle. Claire’s hand. Blondet, a man of flowers, was shocked. The Duchess promised that if Blondet resigned properly and awaited confirmation from the public prosecutor, his son’s appointment could be secured within a week; she urged him to consult Camusot and Michu to hush up the affair quickly, ensuring Joseph’s prospects. Blondet, overwhelmed and suspicious, hurried to seek confirmation.

At nine the three judges—Blondet, Camusot, and Michu—met secretly. Blondet, furious at the President’s trickery, insisted the matter was a trifle and argued it lacked the intent required for forgery: accounts had been squared and money allegedly deposited to meet the bill. Michu proposed removing the Count from peril by hastening the examination of du Croisier and his wife and issuing a decision the next morning. They agreed to act swiftly.

Du Croisier was summoned and, under searching interrogation by Camusot, confirmed prior dealings with the Count and that Chesnel had often remitted sums. Camusot asked whether du Croisier knew money destined to meet the bill had been deposited with him beforehand; this unnerved him. Chesnel, summoned, confirmed he had deposited the money and produced letters to prove it; Mme. du Croisier corroborated, declaring Chesnel had entrusted sums to her in her husband’s absence. With these facts on record, the Count’s innocence emerged.

Du Croisier returned home enraged at his wife’s deposition; she showed him bundles of banknotes she had kept concealed, arguing she acted for the family’s good. The town buzzed with the arrest, yet by ten the court dismissed the charge, allowing the Count to sue for libel. Chesnel paraded the exposure of du Croisier’s plot. As evening fell, the Marquis d’Esgrignon’s family fretted over debts and honor. The Duchess, who had stayed with them, advised practical measures: marry for money if necessary, since old privileges had been eroded by law. She produced the tiny phial as a jest, counseled Victurnien to take lands and a quiet life rather than Paris flirtations, and the brief romance dissolved with the crisis’s end.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Six months later Camusot became an assistant judge in Paris and later an examining magistrate. Goodman Blondet was named councillor to the Royal Court, kept the post until he secured a pension, then returned to his pretty house. Joseph Blondet took his father’s seat at the court until his death; with no hope of promotion he married Mlle. Blandereau and likely now leads, in the little flower-covered brick house, as dull a life as any carp in a marble basin. Michu and Camusot received the Cross of the Legion of Honor; Blondet became an Officer. M. Sauvager, deputy public prosecutor, was sent to Corsica, much to du Croisier’s relief; du Croisier certainly did not intend to give his niece to that functionary.

Urged by President du Ronceret, du Croisier appealed the Tribunal’s finding to the Court-Royal and lost. Liberals across the department held little d’Esgrignon guilty; Royalists spun frightful stories of plots by “that abominable du Croisier” to compass revenge. A duel followed: chance favored du Croisier; the young Count was dangerously wounded, and his antagonist stood by his words. The affair embittered party strife, and the Liberals invoked it at every opportunity. Meanwhile du Croisier never carried his election and saw no hope of marrying his niece to the Count, especially after the duel.

A month after the Tribunal’s decision was confirmed, Chesnel died, exhausted by the strain that had weakened him mentally and physically. He died at the moment of victory, like a faithful hound that brings the boar to bay and dies on its tusks. Though the great House was nearly ruined, the heir penniless and bored, and prospects of establishing himself gone, Chesnel’s exhaustion and bitter thoughts hastened his end. One comfort: at his sister’s entreaty the old Marquis restored their friendship. The great lord came to the Rue du Bercail and sat by his old servant’s bedside, unaware how much the servant had done and sacrificed. Chesnel sat up and repeated Simeon’s cry. The Marquis allowed burial in the castle chapel; they laid him crosswise at the foot of the tomb reserved for the Marquis himself, the last, in a sense, of the d’Esgrignons.

Thus died one of the last representatives of Service in its original meaning: the relation between feudal lord and servitor. Found now only in remote provinces or among a few old royal servants, it honored a noblesse that inspired affection and a bourgeoisie that could conceive it. Such magnificent devotion is no longer possible. Noble houses have no servitors; France no longer has a King, hereditary peerage, or land irrevocably bound to historic houses to perpetuate glorious names. Chesnel was not merely an obscure great man of private life; he was a great fact. There is something solemn and sublime in his sustained self-devotion, rising above a single beneficent deed or a moment’s supreme effort. Chesnel combined homely burgher virtues with heroic noble ideals enlightened by solid education — virtues belonging to classes between the people’s poverty and the aristocracy’s greatness.

Victurnien was not well regarded at Court; there was no chance of a great match or office. His Majesty refused to raise the d’Esgrignons to the peerage—the one royal favor that might have redeemed his position. He could not marry a bourgeois heiress during his father’s life, so he lived shabbily under the paternal roof, haunted by memories of two years’ splendor in Paris and the lost love of a great lady. He grew moody and depressed, vegetating with a careworn aunt and a half-broken father who blamed a wasting malady. Chesnel was gone.

The Marquis died in 1830. The great d’Esgrignon, followed by the less infirm noblesse of the Collection of Antiquities, went to wait upon Charles X at Nonancourt, swelling the meager train of the fallen king—an act then seen as heroism amid revolt. “The Gaul has conquered!” were the Marquis’ last words.

By that time du Croisier’s victory was complete. A week after his father’s death the new Marquis d’Esgrignon married Mlle. Duval. His bride brought three million francs; du Croisier and his wife settled the reversion of their fortunes upon her in the marriage contract. During the ceremony du Croisier declared the d’Esgrignon family the most honorable of all the ancient houses in France.

Someday the present Marquis will have an income exceeding a hundred thousand crowns. He comes to Paris every winter and leads a jolly bachelor life, treating his wife with more than the indifference of the grand seigneur of olden times—he takes no thought for her.

“As for Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” said Emile Blondet, source of these details, “if she is no longer like the divinely fair woman whom I saw by glimpses in my childhood, she is, at sixty-seven, the most pathetic and interesting figure in the Collection of Antiquities. She queens it among them still. I saw her on my last journey home to fetch the necessary papers for my marriage. When my father learned whom I had married he was struck dumb until I told him I was a prefect.

“‘You were born to it,’ he said, with a smile.

“As I took a walk around the town I met Mlle. Armande; she seemed taller than ever. I thought of Marius among the ruins of Carthage. Had she not outlived her creed and the beliefs that time destroyed? She is a sad and silent woman, with nothing of her old beauty left except her eyes, which shine with an unearthly light. I watched her on her way to mass, book in hand, and could not help thinking that she prayed to be taken out of the world.”

LES JARDIES, July 1837.
