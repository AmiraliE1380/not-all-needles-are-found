To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

Dear Child,—You, the joy of the household, whose pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of Wierzschovnia, how can I dedicate to you a story full of melancholy? Yet should not sorrows be spoken of to a young girl like you, since the day may come when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them? It is difficult to find a subject worthy of your eyes, so I have no choice; perhaps you will feel fortunate when you read this story sent to you by your old friend, De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

One October morning in 1827 a sixteen-year-old boy in modest clothes stood in a small square of Lower Provins, among working mills and bourgeois houses. He found the neat white-stone house he sought; its tidy air spoke of a retired petty shopkeeper’s pride. As the sun lit a calico garret curtain, the boy sang a Breton ditty for newlyweds—recalling Brittany and life’s bittersweet turns. Pierrette, at the window, signaled; he offered a yellow broom-flower. He was Brigaut, her childhood companion, newly returned and intending to settle nearby. Alarmed by a grotesque old maid below, sharp-eyed and ready to imagine scandal, Pierrette bade him go.

Overwhelmed, she reflected on her fragile childhood: illness, faded joys, and a tangled lineage. Her mother, born a Demoiselle Auffray, had endured multiple marriages and a complicated inheritance, then married Major Lorrain, who died in battle leaving poverty. Sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, Pierrette remained largely unaware of her full lineage and its financial consequences, setting the stage for the drama to follow.

Père Rogron, an indulgent, ruddy-faced innkeeper, and his unattractive, selfish wife moved from modest lodging into a dilapidated house inherited from old Auffray. Their children, Sylvie and Jérôme-Denis, were neglected and ill-bred; Rogron sent them to Paris to work with little support.

Sylvie became a forewoman on rue Saint-Denis and Jérôme a head-clerk. They pooled savings to buy the retail “Family Sister,” gaining modest success but a reputation for severity and stinginess. Sylvie ran the business shrewdly though plainly; Jérôme was less competent. Their parsimony and harsh treatment of clerks caused high turnover. Pleasant to customers, Sylvie was a sour spinster at home.

After their father’s death they learned of cousin Pierrette’s inheritance. They considered selling and returning to Provins but refused to take Pierrette in to protect their wealth, delayed replying, and left for Provins four years before Brigaut arrived.

Back home, Parisian airs faded. New wealth financed travel, politics, hunting, and house renovations aimed at aristocratic mimicry, yet they failed to enter established circles—the Julliards, Guepins, Guenées—led by Madame Tiphaine. Lacking polish, their lavish dinners drew ridicule and invitations dwindled. Sylvie’s faith that money bought status collapsed; she grew frugal and nostalgic for business. Bringing Pierrette back exposed their neglect, and bitter and sidelined, they fell in with lower bourgeois figures who exploited their wealth, revealing that provincial social codes trump money.

After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette—ashamed to live on charity—felt relieved when distant wealthy relatives were found. Brigaut, a childhood friend and her mother’s friend’s son, gave her sixty francs for the trip to Paris; she accepted without thanks, a sign of intimacy. He often visited to console her and ran after the coach in tears when she left. Penniless in Paris, a conductor briefly paid for her hotel; four days later a kinder one brought her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s, where her cousins received her with astonishment.

She arrived with a small bundle in Breton clothes. Sylvie and the maid Adèle were surprised; Pierrette, shy and overwhelmed, spoke little. Adèle and Sylvie showed her a modest room that seemed luxurious to Pierrette, and Sylvie immediately worried about her lack of clothing and resolved to buy her a new wardrobe.

In Brittany Pierrette had known affection and freedom; with the Rogrons she found coldness and constant criticism. Exploring the house and garden the next morning, she was reproved for dirty shoes. Their concern for possessions over her well‑being and continual fault‑finding sharply contrasted with the love she’d known. Pierrette’s innocence made their disdain more painful; she began to suppress her joy and became passive for fear of scorn.

The Rogrons obsessed over her appearance, treating her as a burden and stifling her spirit. When she tore a dress playing, she was devastated. Life in Provins worsened as Sylvie forbade visits to friends and pursued social ambitions encouraged by Vinet and the colonel. As her cousins plotted for status, Pierrette was increasingly confined—transformed from a lively child into a shadow of her former self, enduring harsh, self‑interested care that was not love.

At night Sylvie’s coldness made her cry; when Pierrette said her poor grandmother had been kinder, Sylvie retorted the grandmother had taken money while a rich cousin would leave her some—Pierrette wanted affection, not wealth. Accused of mischief and disobedience, she left ashamed. Rogron demanded punishment; Madame Vinet suggested school; the colonel thought instruction might keep her quiet. Vinet called her a rough diamond and hired a writing master; the lessons left ink stains and scattered papers, all blamed on Pierrette, who was told she must earn her keep.

Only Adèle showed kindness, secretly warming Pierrette’s bed. Sylvie believed in hardening children; the colonel and Vinet blamed her “Breton” nature. Vinet and Rogron invested in a local newspaper to boost their standing while Pierrette’s condition worsened.

She considered escaping to her grandparents, but a death and Rogron’s appointment as guardian prevented it. Vinet hinted at an inheritance; Rogron secured from her grandmother a promise of eight thousand francs. Preparations for Pierrette’s first communion shifted household dynamics: Sylvie insisted on the zealous priest Monsieur Habert, whose influence led Pierrette to find solace in faith and abandon escape plans. Habert’s sister offered a school place, but the Rogrons refused for fear of a convent.

Later Vinet introduced the Chargeboeufs; Bathilde attracted Rogron, and Sylvie forced Pierrette into domestic tasks. Without Adèle’s protection she was treated worse than the staff—scolded for trifles, denied praise—and over the years her health and spirit faded into a silent shadow while her cousins remained oblivious.

Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut’s arrival would provoke in the Rogron household, it is necessary to explain how he came to Provins, since he plays a significant yet passive role.

Brigaut had fled the Rogron house, shaken by Pierrette’s decline from a lively girl to a pale, sorrowful figure. From a high point in the Upper town he watched her home, consumed by concern. Their rare bond led him to better himself: he briefly went to Paris to learn a trade, then returned to Provins and found work as a journeyman cabinetmaker so he could stay near Pierrette. He kept his feelings hidden and learned more about the Rogrons after Madame Frappier revealed their dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. He often saw Pierrette at the market or near the church, where she signaled him to keep away, and worried about her frailty.

Pierrette sensed changes but did not grasp the Rogrons’ internal strife, chiefly between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste), both angling for advantageous marriages. Sylvie, forty-two, feared aging alone; Céleste, ten years younger and urged on by her priest brother, pursued a husband. A recent political shift empowering local Liberals intensified the scramble for social position.

Sylvie’s consultation with a discouraging doctor worsened her anxiety; Céleste urged delay to remove a rival. Sylvie grew jealous of the colonel’s attention to Pierrette. Vinet, a lawyer who coveted Bathilde, exploited Sylvie: he maneuvered to divert the Rogron fortune to Bathilde by encouraging the colonel to court Pierrette while secretly plotting otherwise. After overhearing Brigaut serenade Pierrette, Sylvie accused her of harboring a secret admirer; Pierrette’s frightened silence and increased toil worsened her health. Brigaut offered fleeting comfort, but love, jealousy, and ambition had become entangled, foreshadowing tragedy for all.

For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the Constitutionnel to Gouraud, so Gouraud came himself and lingered to gossip and walk with Sylvie. Eager to see him, Sylvie dressed her best; when Gouraud arrived Rogron excused himself, leaving them alone.

Gouraud’s questions about her cousin met evasiveness: the cousin was unwell. He grew uneasy—Vinet’s rising influence with the Rogrons made him feel sidelined. A half-pay officer, he feared Vinet’s control of plans and Rogron’s infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, which threatened his prospects. He even suspected Vinet of manoeuvring to push him toward Pierrette to favour Vinet and Bathilde. Alone with Sylvie he brushed off any affection for Pierrette, preferring a more mature match. Relieved, Sylvie hinted her brother had once considered Pierrette suitable; sensing her interest, Gouraud declared he would marry Sylvie, delighting Rogron. Sylvie began planning their home but watched Pierrette and the colonel with suspicion.

At dinner Sylvie scolded Pierrette for a stain but ordered her to look presentable—suspecting collusion. The Chargeboeuf ladies arrived; Bathilde, spectacularly dressed under Vinet’s guidance, treated marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone to power, using beauty and connections for independence. The evening grew tense: Bathilde’s condescension and Vinet’s remarks sharpened rivalries. At cards Pierrette’s innocent comments provoked Sylvie, and Pierrette left in tears. That night Sylvie brooded on men’s duplicity and her precarious position.

The next day Pierrette suffered a painful fall; Sylvie blamed disobedience and showed no sympathy. The week brought relentless torment. Concealing Brigaut’s arrival to avoid Sylvie’s wrath, Pierrette longed for him. He arranged clandestine correspondence—slipping a note while pretending to buy fruit and asking her to lower a string from her window. Pierrette replied at midnight, pouring out love and describing her relatives’ abuse; their hopeful, intimate exchange continued even as Sylvie’s jealousy and vigilance grew.

Under Sylvie’s scrutiny Pierrette’s health declined; she fainted one evening. Sylvie dismissed it as a ruse and then violently tried to seize Pierrette’s letters; Pierrette was hurt. Brigaut’s urgent appeals brought Madame Lorrain, who witnessed the violence, aided Pierrette, and sought medical care. As Pierrette recovered, her bond with Madame Lorrain deepened; the promise of protection and a future with her grandmother and Brigaut offered a hopeful glimmer. Pierrette remained determined to reclaim her life.

At nine o’clock Monsieur Martener told Monsieur Tiphaine about Pierrette’s mistreatment by the Rogrons. Tiphaine brought in Auffray, a notary-relative; long-standing enmities between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions—stoked by damaging truths about Madame Tiphaine’s family—gave the dispute political force.

Seeing a chance to weaken the influential royalist Rogrons, Tiphaine consulted the public prosecutor. He, Auffray, and Martener arranged to petition the courts to make Auffray Pierrette’s guardian so a Family Council, backed by medical testimony, could remove her from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday the town buzzed: neighbors had heard Pierrette’s cries and rumors spread. Doctors Bianchon and Brigaut examined her and confirmed abuse. Vinet, the Rogrons’ lawyer, warned of criminal exposure; Sylvie, initially evasive, later admitted jealousy and the truth. Exploiting fear, Vinet proposed a marriage announcement to quiet the scandal; under pressure the Rogrons signed a marriage contract that served their political aims. Vinet then filed a petition accusing Brigaut and the widow Lorrain of abducting Pierrette, trying to recast his clients as victims.

The hearing was postponed. Tiphaines rallied support while Pierrette, unaware of the turmoil, was cared for by her grandmother and Brigaut. When court reconvened, Auffray presented the physicians’ report; the judge ordered she be kept from the Rogrons until the Family Council met—a public rebuke. The Council removed Rogron as guardian and appointed Auffray; evidence led to a civil suit for neglect and abuse.

As the legal battle continued, Pierrette’s health declined. Martener and the Auffray family grew fond of her; a critical operation in March 1828 offered little hope. She forgave her cousins and soon died. Her funeral filled the town; the Rogrons, fearing outrage, stayed away. Brigaut made her coffin. Though community support hampered the Rogrons’ maneuvers and Vinet faced backlash, they largely kept political standing; only a few remembered the reality of Pierrette’s suffering. The account closes on questions of justice, accountability, and her lasting impact.
