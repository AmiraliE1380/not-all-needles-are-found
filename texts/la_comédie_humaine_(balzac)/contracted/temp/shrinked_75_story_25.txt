**Colonel Chabert**  
*By Honoré de Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage and Clara Bell*  

**DEDICATION**  
To Madame la Comtesse Ida de Bocarme née du Chasteler.

---

The story opens in a lawyer's office where a clerk, Simonnin, mocks an old man in a shabby coat, calling him "old Box-coat." The atmosphere is filled with the banter of clerks, who are busy with their work and gossiping about clients. They are dismissive of the old man, who is revealed to be Colonel Chabert, a figure believed to be dead after the battle of Eylau.

Chabert, weary and desperate, seeks to speak with M. Derville, the attorney, but the clerks refuse to take him seriously. They joke about his appearance and speculate on his past, with one clerk suggesting he might be a nobleman. Despite their mockery, Chabert insists on waiting for Derville, demonstrating his determination and the weight of his situation.

When Chabert finally reveals his identity, the clerks are taken aback. They had thought him a mere beggar, but now they realize he is a man of significance. Their laughter turns to disbelief as they process the implications of his claim. Boucard, the head clerk, tries to dismiss Chabert, suggesting he return at an inconvenient hour, further highlighting the indifference of the clerks to the plight of their clients.

As Chabert leaves, the clerks continue their banter, debating whether he is truly the colonel or just a pretender. They express skepticism about his story, particularly since Chabert's wife has remarried, leaving him in a precarious position. The clerks' attitudes reflect a broader societal disdain for those who have fallen from grace, illustrating the harsh realities of post-war France.

The scene shifts back to the clerks, who are more concerned with their own trivial matters than with the serious implications of Chabert's visit. They engage in a lively discussion about theater and bets, showcasing their youthful exuberance and lack of empathy. The office, filled with the smell of food and the clutter of legal papers, serves as a backdrop to their indifference.

Ultimately, the story captures the stark contrast between the struggles of individuals like Chabert and the callousness of the legal system and society at large. The clerks' laughter and lightheartedness serve as a poignant reminder of the disconnect between those in power and those who have been marginalized. Balzac paints a vivid picture of a world where dignity is often stripped away, leaving behind only the remnants of past glory.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At around one in the morning, Colonel Chabert arrived at the office of Maitre Derville, a young but renowned attorney in Paris. The porter informed him that Derville was not yet in, but Chabert insisted on his appointment and was shown upstairs. There, he found Derville's head clerk organizing case papers. The clerk explained that Derville often worked late into the night, using the quiet to strategize for his cases.

When Derville finally arrived, he was taken aback by Chabert's ghostly appearance. The Colonel, thin and pale, had a scarred head and an unsettling demeanor. After a brief introduction, Chabert claimed to be the Colonel who had been declared dead at the Battle of Eylau. Derville and his clerk exchanged skeptical glances, suspecting madness.

Chabert recounted his story: he had commanded a cavalry regiment at Eylau, where he was gravely injured and left for dead. Despite being reported dead, he survived, buried among the corpses. He managed to escape and was rescued by a woman who took him to a hospital. After months of recovery, he sought to reclaim his identity and fortune, only to find that his wife, the Countess Ferraud, had remarried and inherited his estate.

Derville, realizing the gravity of Chabert's situation, listened intently as the Colonel described his struggles to prove his identity and reclaim his rights. Chabert expressed his despair over being treated as a madman and his longing for vengeance against his wife, who had not acknowledged his existence since his return.

Derville, moved by Chabert's plight, offered to help him obtain the necessary documents to prove his identity. He promised to provide financial assistance while they pursued the case. Chabert, overwhelmed with gratitude, recognized Derville as a rare ally in his fight for justice.

As they discussed the legal complexities of Chabert's situation, the Colonel's demeanor shifted from despair to determination. He expressed a willingness to fight for his rights, even if it meant facing significant challenges in court. Derville assured him that he would take the case seriously and would work diligently to help him reclaim his life.

After their meeting, Chabert left with a sense of hope, clutching the money Derville had given him. For the first time in years, he felt a glimmer of possibility in his otherwise bleak existence.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Three months after an interview, Derville, a notary, is approached by Crottat, who requests the return of 600 francs given to an old soldier, Colonel Chabert. Derville expresses his reluctance to continue supporting the soldier financially, having already been misled by his sense of patriotism. He notices a letter on his desk, written in German, and instructs his clerk Boucard to have it translated. The letter reveals that documents regarding Chabert's estate will soon arrive, confirming their legitimacy and the existence of witnesses in Prussia.

Derville recalls that the settlement of Chabert’s estate was handled by Roguin, a disreputable lawyer who recently absconded with clients' money. Crottat confirms that Chabert's widow, Rose Chapotel, inherited a personal fortune of about 300,000 francs after a contested will. Derville learns that Chabert is living in poor conditions in Faubourg Saint-Marceau with a dairyman named Vergniaud. 

Upon visiting Chabert, Derville finds him living in squalor, yet the Colonel maintains a sense of dignity. Chabert expresses his desire to reclaim his life and fortune, believing he deserves recognition as a general. Derville explains the complexities of proving Chabert's identity legally, including the potential for a lengthy and costly court battle. He warns Chabert that his wife, now married to Ferraud, may contest his claims, especially since they have children together.

Chabert is disheartened by the prospect of a drawn-out legal struggle and the financial burden it entails. Derville reassures him that they will pursue a friendly suit to annul both the death certificate and the marriage, aiming to restore Chabert's rights. Chabert agrees to trust Derville with his case.

As Derville leaves, he encounters Vergniaud, who expresses concern for Chabert's well-being and requests financial help to improve the Colonel's living conditions. Derville decides to provide assistance but insists that Chabert should be the one to help Vergniaud.

Derville then plans to confront Madame Ferraud, Chabert's widow, to gauge her reaction to the news of Chabert's survival. He reflects on the political and social dynamics surrounding the Ferrauds, particularly how Ferraud's ambitions and connections could influence the outcome of Chabert's case.

When Derville meets Madame Ferraud, she initially dismisses the idea of Chabert being alive. However, Derville's serious demeanor and knowledge of the situation quickly shift the tone of the conversation. He warns her of the legal implications of Chabert's return and the potential for public opinion to turn against her if the case goes to court.

Madame Ferraud, realizing the gravity of her situation, becomes defensive but is ultimately intrigued by the possibility of leveraging Chabert's affection to negotiate a favorable outcome. Derville proposes a compromise, suggesting that they could settle the matter amicably, which would benefit both parties.

The conversation reveals the underlying tensions in Madame Ferraud's life, including her fears of losing her status and wealth. Derville's strategic approach highlights the complexities of the legal system and the personal stakes involved for all parties. Ultimately, he leaves the meeting with a sense of confidence that he can navigate the situation to Chabert's advantage.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A week after Derville's visits, Colonel Chabert and his wife, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud, set out from opposite ends of Paris to meet at Derville's office. Chabert, looking dignified in a new outfit and a wig, arrived in a cab, embodying the soldierly style he had regained. Meanwhile, the Countess, dressed elegantly, arrived shortly after. The office remained unchanged, with clerks joking about the peculiar situation.

Derville prepared a memorandum for the couple, outlining the terms of their agreement regarding Chabert's identity and the conditions for their marriage. The Countess was initially resistant to the idea of a lawsuit to annul Chabert's death certificate, insisting she wanted to avoid legal proceedings. When Chabert entered the room, the tension escalated, revealing the complexities of their relationship. The Countess feigned ignorance about Chabert's identity, but her reaction indicated otherwise.

As the discussion progressed, Chabert expressed his anger over the Countess's reluctance to acknowledge him and the financial terms she proposed. The Countess, feeling cornered, left abruptly, prompting Derville to caution Chabert about her potential traps. Despite his rage, Chabert was deeply affected by the encounter and left the office in despair.

Later, the Countess approached Chabert, leading him to her carriage. They traveled to her country house, where she expressed her emotional turmoil over their situation. The Countess revealed her love for another man, Comte Ferraud, complicating their reunion. Chabert, though hurt, offered his understanding and support, suggesting they maintain dignity and avoid public scandal.

During their time together, the Countess attempted to charm Chabert, seeking to erase the pain of their past. However, her ulterior motives became apparent as she plotted to secure her position while keeping Chabert at bay. After several days, Chabert began to sense her manipulations, leading to a confrontation with Delbecq, the Countess's secretary, who suggested exploiting Chabert's situation for financial gain.

Feeling betrayed, Chabert confronted the Countess, who attempted to manipulate him further. He ultimately decided to sacrifice his claim to his identity for her happiness, but the Countess's true intentions were revealed when she discussed putting him in an asylum if necessary. This realization shattered Chabert's trust, leading him to reject her and resolve to disappear from her life.

Chabert's subsequent life was marked by hardship, as he fell into poverty and obscurity. Derville, unaware of Chabert's fate, assumed the couple had reached a compromise. However, he later discovered Chabert imprisoned as a vagabond, stripped of his dignity. Despite his circumstances, Chabert maintained a sense of pride and contempt for societal norms.

In a final act of integrity, Chabert sought to repay Derville for his assistance, demonstrating his enduring loyalty despite the betrayal he faced. Derville, recognizing Chabert's plight, facilitated the payment owed to him by the Countess, highlighting the tragic consequences of their entangled lives. Ultimately, Chabert's story serves as a poignant reflection on identity, love, and the harsh realities of life after war.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In June 1840, attorney Godeschal visited Bicetre with Derville, his predecessor. They encountered an elderly pauper, who was drying his handkerchief in the sun. Godeschal remarked on the man's resemblance to grotesque carvings, while Derville revealed that the man was Colonel Chabert, the estranged husband of the Comtesse Ferraud. Derville recounted how Chabert had been sent to the workhouse, a fate stemming from his reminder of their humble beginnings.

Two days later, they returned to Bicetre and found Chabert drawing in the sand. When addressed, he insisted he was no longer Chabert but "Hyacinthe," and expressed a childlike innocence. Godeschal offered him money for snuff, which Chabert gratefully accepted, displaying a mix of military bravado and confusion.

An observing pauper noted that Chabert, despite his apparent childishness, was clever and philosophical. He recounted a past encounter where Chabert had impressed a Prussian officer with his military history. Derville lamented the fate of men like Chabert, who had served Napoleon only to end up in a workhouse.

Derville reflected on the disillusionment faced by priests, doctors, and lawyers, particularly the latter, who witness the darkest aspects of human nature. He shared harrowing experiences from his legal career, including familial betrayals and moral corruption, concluding that the horrors he had seen surpassed any fictional accounts. Godeschal, having seen similar cases, expressed his desire to escape the grim realities of Paris for a quieter life in the countryside.

The narrative highlights the tragic decline of Colonel Chabert and critiques societal indifference towards those who have fallen from grace, emphasizing the darker truths of human relationships and the legal profession.
