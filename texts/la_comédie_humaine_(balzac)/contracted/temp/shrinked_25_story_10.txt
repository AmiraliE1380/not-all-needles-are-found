In Paris, balls often split into two parts: a formal round of superficial pleasantries and a later, intimate meeting where genuine conversation and laughter flourish. The latter, hosted by women like Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches, preserves French wit and encourages free-flowing ideas and camaraderie.

At one evening in Mademoiselle des Touches’ salon, guests including the Minister de Marsay exchanged lively discourse. De Marsay recounted an early, passionate affair with a woman six years his senior: a secret relationship masked by outward indifference and private signals through the flowers she carried. When illness forced him to send a note canceling a meeting, jealousy drove him to visit anyway; he found her with another man. Her calm tenderness briefly quelled his doubts, but the sight ignited a storm of feeling. Confronted with betrayal, de Marsay felt the end of innocence, wrestled with thoughts of revenge, then resolved that true love must transcend vengeance. His story moved the salon to reflect on love, jealousy, social expectation, and fragile trust.

Cured of his cold and naive love, the narrator begins an affair with a charming heroine whose beauty sharply contrasts with a prior deceptive angel. He avoids quarrels with this clever woman, suspecting that true love might not match the pleasures of her skilled deceit—refined hypocrisy, he muses, can seem as good as virtue.

He visits a hair artist in the Rue Boucher to commission a keepsake. The artisan shows astonishing hair mementos and mentions passing trends like marking linen with hair. Suspicion flares when the narrator recognizes a handkerchief the artist has worked on, confirming doubts about the authenticity of women’s affections. He leaves resolved to enjoy pleasure but skeptical of love.

Two months later, in the heroine’s boudoir, he holds her hand and tests her. Charlotte professes exclusive devotion, claiming he is the only man in her world and that she sought ways to bind him. But when he bluntly asks, “When are you to marry the Duke?” her startled reaction betrays guilt. She feigns astonishment; he suggests she should accept the Duke—rich, devout, and proper.

Charlotte falls to her knees, swearing before God that she loves only him. He presses her, confessing doubts about her fidelity; she counters that he no longer loves her and tries to leave, but curiosity draws her back to ask what he has seen. He reveals he noticed her attending mass with the Duke several times; she dismisses his jealousy. He advises her to seize the Duke’s offer, since marriage would elevate her and the Duke expects propriety. In tears she laments that had he spoken sooner they might have been happy, but he insists it is too late—she has gone too far.

She fears the Duke’s suspicions; he reassures her it’s unlikely but urges her to show devotion. As she alternates demeanor and supplication, she vows never to forget him. He plays the distressed lover until she asks what will become of him; he then admits to having made advances to another woman. This surprises Charlotte, who accuses him of unfaithfulness; he replies that her wit exceeds her heart’s suffering. She proclaims disillusionment with love, asserting that women can never truly be loved; he retorts that taking love seriously in society is like romanticizing an actress. She accuses him of betrayal; he feigns submission and asks whether she will remember her grievances once she is a duchess. Her lingering bitterness convinces him he has successfully distanced himself—he has studied how successful men withdraw and believes he has mastered that art.

In the salon, the other women express sympathy for Charlotte while de Marsay smiles slightly. Conversation shifts to the erosion of social distinctions. Lady Dudley notes women often do not view their marriages as acts of inconstancy. De Marsay laments the aristocracy’s decline, mourning that duchesses and marquises are disappearing and that the Civil Code has leveled hierarchies. Blondet quips that aristocracy now begins with viscountesses; de Marsay hopes countesses may survive in some form. They recall how the last great ladies vanished with the previous century’s splendor and how the Code inadvertently created a new class of women.

The Comte de Vandenesse and Lord Dudley observe that the current order lets anyone claim nobility; unsettled property hastens the fall. Joseph Bridau laments a decline in womanly beauty; Daniel d’Arthez says the aristocracy’s retreat has deprived society of women who might have shaped it.

Discussion turns to the modern “perfect lady”: tasteful, graceful, poised but lacking the grandeur of past great ladies. Mademoiselle des Touches questions whether this is progress; Madame de Nucingen argues a woman’s worth now lies in character and presentation rather than lineage. Count Adam Laginski asks for a definition; Émile Blondet describes the perfect lady as effortlessly elegant and refined, commanding respect by bearing and demeanor across settings.

The dialogue underscores modern femininity’s complexities: the perfect lady skillfully navigates expectations with charm, blending beauty and intellect. Guests debate whether she compensates for the vanished greatness of earlier aristocratic women or simply reflects a society where merit and presentation have replaced inherited splendor.

“You will not talk for half an hour with a bourgeoise without her mentioning her husband,” Blondet began, caricaturing the modern lady. Though married, she hides her husband: at a ball a middle-aged man with a decoration bows and goes; she orders her carriage and departs. You are not the rose, but you have been with the rose; you sleep under a golden canopy of a delightful dream that may linger.

At home she sees no one before four and keeps you waiting. Her house is tastefully arranged; luxury is for daily use and constantly renewed—no dusty shades or forgotten wrappings. The staircase is warm, flowers abound—accepted gifts only from chosen people because they symbolize a promise. Fashion trinkets lie about, but not like museum pieces. She sits by the fire, not rising to greet you. At the ball she was a creditor; here she owes you the pleasure of her wit. She seeks a man to widen her circle, an object for her attentions. To draw you into her drawing-room she will be charming. All this shows how isolated women are and why they want a little world to shine in. Conversation there lives on generalities.

“Yes,” de Marsay agreed, “you have caught our age’s flaw. The epigram—a volume in a word—no longer strikes at people or things but at sordid events, fading quickly.”

“Hence a lady’s intelligence, if she has any, lies in doubting everything. Compare two women: the townswoman is virtuous; the lady hesitates and contends with her virtue. This uncertainty is one of the last graces left her. She rarely goes to church but will discuss religion; if you express free thought she will try to convert you, slipping into familiar phrases and gestures: ‘For shame! I thought you had too much sense to attack religion. Society is tottering, and you deprive it of support. Religion means you and me; it is property and the future of our children! Let us not be selfish! Individualism is the disease of the age, and religion is the only remedy; it unites families, which your laws put asunder,’ and so forth. She drifts into a neo-Christian discourse laced with political notions—neither Catholic nor Protestant—but very moral: a muddle of modern doctrines at odds with each other.”

The women laughed at Blondet’s satire.

“This sketch, Count Adam,” Blondet told the Pole, “shows that the ‘perfect lady’ embodies intellectual and political confusion, surrounded by flashy yet fleeting products of an industry that replaces its work with something new. When you leave her you think she had superior ideas because she has delicately probed your heart and asked your secrets; she feigns ignorance to learn everything. There are things she never knows, even when she does. You alone feel uneasy, knowing nothing of her heart. The great ladies of old flaunted their affairs; today’s lady arranges her passions like a musical score, with notes and rests, pauses and sharps. A merely weak woman fears compromising her love, husband, or children’s future. Name, position, and fortune no longer shield her. The aristocracy no longer protects her. Unlike the great lady of the past, she lacks lofty antagonism; she cannot crush; rather, she would be crushed. Thus she masters Jesuitical compromises, moving between propriety and anonymous passion. She fears her servants as an Englishwoman fears a divorce trial. So free at a ball, so charming on a walk, she is a slave at home; she is never independent except in privacy or theoretically. Her task is to preserve her position as a lady.

“For today, a woman repudiated by her husband, reduced to a meager allowance, without carriage, luxury, or opera box, is neither wife, maid, nor townswoman; she is adrift, a chattel. The Carmelites will not accept a married woman; it would be bigamy. Would her lover still want her? That is the question. Thus your perfect lady may give rise to calumny, but never to slander.”

“It is all so horribly true,” said the Princesse de Cadignan.

“And so,” Blondet continued, “our ‘perfect lady’ exists between English hypocrisy and eighteenth-century frankness—a bastard system symptomatic of an age where nothing that emerges resembles what has vanished; transition leads nowhere, everything is a matter of degree; all great figures fade into the background; distinction becomes purely personal. I am convinced it is impossible for a woman, even if born near a throne, to acquire before twenty-five the encyclopedic knowledge of trifles, the practice of maneuvering, the important small things, the musical tones of coloring, the angelic mischief and innocent cunning, the speech and silence, the seriousness and banter, the wit and obtuseness, the diplomacy and ignorance that make the perfect lady.”

“And where, according to your sketch,” Mademoiselle des Touches asked, “would you classify the female author? Is she a perfect lady, a woman comme il faut?”

“When she has no genius, she is a woman comme il n’en faut pas,” Blondet replied, casting a look that might praise Camille Maupin. “This epigram is not mine, but Napoleon’s.”

“You need not hold a grudge against Napoleon for that,” Canalis interjected. “He was weak in being jealous of literary genius—he had his flaws. Who can explain or depict Napoleon? He was a man of paradoxes: arms folded, he did everything; the most concentrated, caustic force, a singular genius who spread armed civilization without fixing it anywhere. He could do everything because he willed everything; he was a phenomenal will, conquering illness through battle, yet destined to die of disease in bed after living amid balls and bullets. He had a code and a sword in his brain, word and deed; a clear-sighted spirit that foresaw everything but his own fall; a capricious politician who risked men for economy yet spared three heads—Talleyrand, Pozzo de Borgo, and Metternich—whose deaths might have saved the Empire. Nature, as a rare privilege, gave a heart in a bronze frame; mirthful and kind at midnight among women, and the next morning manipulating Europe as a child might splash in her bath. Hypocritical and generous; loving tawdriness and simplicity; devoid of taste yet protecting the arts; and despite these contradictions, truly great by instinct or temperament: Caesar at twenty-five, Cromwell at thirty. He improvised public works, empires, kings, codes, verses, a romance—with more range than precision. He aimed to make all Europe France, and after changing the laws of gravitation he left us poorer; he who had taken an empire by his name lost his name on the frontier of his empire in a sea of blood and soldiers. A man of thought and action, who understood Desaix and Fouché.”

“All despotism and all justice at the right moments. The true king!” de Marsay declared.

“But do you think the treat we are giving you is common?” Joseph Bridau asked. “If you had to pay for the charms of conversation as you do for dancing or music, your fortune would be inadequate! There is no second performance of the same flash of wit.”

“And are we really so much diminished as these gentlemen think?” the Princesse de Cadignan asked, smiling at the women. “Under a regime that makes everything small, you prefer small dishes, small rooms, small pictures, small articles, small newspapers, small books—does that prove women have grown smaller? Why should the human heart change because you change your coat? In all ages passions remain the same. I know of beautiful devotion and sublime sufferings that lack the publicity that once gave luster to the errors of some women. But even if one has not saved a King of France, one is not less an Agnès Sorel. Do you believe our Marquise d’Espard is not the equal of Madame Doublet or Madame du Deffant, in whose rooms so much evil was spoken and done? Is not Taglioni a match for Camargo? Or Malibran the equal of Saint-Huberti? Are not our poets superior to those of the eighteenth century? If, at this moment, due to the Grocers who govern us, we lack a style of our own, did not the Empire have its distinguishing stamp as the age of Louis XV did, and was not its splendor fabulous? Have the sciences lost anything?”

“I completely agree, madame; the women of this age are truly great,” the Comte de Vandenesse replied. “When posterity looks back, will not Madame Récamier appear in as fine a proportion as the most beautiful women of the past? We have made so much history that historians will be lacking. The age of Louis XIV had but one Madame de Sévigné; we have a thousand now in Paris who certainly write better and do not publish their letters. Whether the Frenchwoman is called ‘perfect lady’ or great lady, she will always be the woman among women.

“Blondet has painted the fascinations of a woman of the day; but when needed this creature who bristles or preens, who echoes the ideas of this or that man, can be heroic. And your faults, mesdames, are all the more poetic because they are surrounded by greater perils. I have seen much of the world—perhaps too late; but where the illegality of your feelings might be excused, I have always observed the effects of what you may call Providence, inevitably overwhelming those we consider light women.”

“I hope,” Madame de Vandenesse said, “that we can be great in other ways——”

“Oh, let the Comte de Vandenesse preach to us!” Madame de Serizy cried.

“With all the more reason because he has preached a great deal by example,” the Baronne de Nucingen added.

“On my honor!” General de Montriveau said, “in all the dramas—a word you are very fond of,” he said, glancing at Blondet—“in which the finger of God has been visible, the most frightful I ever knew was almost by my act——”

“Well, tell us all about it!” Lady Barimore cried. “I love to shudder!”

“It is the taste of a virtuous woman,” de Marsay remarked, glancing at Lord Dudley’s lovely daughter.

“During the campaign of 1812,” General Montriveau began, “I was the involuntary cause of a terrible disaster which may interest you, Doctor Bianchon,” turning to the doctor, “since while you devote yourself to the body, you concern yourself with the mind; it may help solve some problems of the will.

“I was on my second campaign; I loved danger and laughed at everything, like the young and foolish lieutenant of artillery I was. When we reached the Beresina, the army had lost all discipline. It was a medley of men of many nations, instinctively making their way from north to south. Soldiers would drive away a ragged, barefoot general if he brought neither wood nor food. After crossing the river disorder did not lessen. I had come quietly and alone, without food, from the marshes of Zembin, wandering in search of a house where I might be taken in. Finding none or being driven away, I finally saw a wretched Polish farm, like the wooden houses of Lower Normandy or the poorest Beauce farms. These dwellings consist of a single room, one end divided by a partition as a forage store-room.

“In the twilight I saw faint smoke above the house. Hoping to find comrades more compassionate than those I had met, I approached. Inside, the table was laid. Several officers, with a woman—a common sight—were eating potatoes, some horseflesh broiled over charcoal, and frozen beetroots. I recognized artillery captains from my first regiment. I was welcomed with shouts; my comrades were warm, they had food, and the straw-strewn floor promised a night’s rest. We did not ask for much then. My comrades could be philanthropists for free—one of the commonest ways of being philanthropic. I sat to eat on a straw bundle.

“At the end of the table, by the door to the hay-filled division, sat my old colonel, one of the most extraordinary men I ever met among our mixed collection. He was Italian. Whenever southern human nature is truly fine it is sublime. My colonel was tall, admirably proportioned, perhaps a little stout but prodigiously powerful, active, and clean-limbed like a greyhound. His abundant black curls contrasted with a complexion as white as a woman’s; he had small hands, a shapely foot, a pleasant mouth, and an aquiline nose that would pinch and turn white when he was angry, which happened often. His irascibility was beyond belief; no one could remain calm in his presence. I alone perhaps was not afraid of him; he had such a fancy for me that he thought everything I did was right. When angry, his brow knit and the muscles of his forehead formed a horseshoe; his whole frame quivered, and his strength, great at rest, became almost unbounded.

“He spoke with a strong guttural roll that, when excited, lent incredible force to his commands. When calm his blue eyes were angelically sweet and his smooth brow charming. On parade or with the army of Italy, no man could compare with him. Indeed, d’Orsay himself was eclipsed by our colonel at the last review Napoleon held before the invasion of Russia.

“Everything about this man was contrast. Passion thrives on contrast. He had the irresistible influence that our nature yields to such men, yet by singular fatality he was neither a lady-killer nor indifferent to success.

“To illustrate his violence: we were dragging guns up a narrow road when we met another artillery regiment. Their colonel wanted our captain to yield but our captain refused. The other colonel ordered his battery forward; the wheel of the first gun struck our captain’s right leg, breaking it and throwing him over on the near side of his horse. Our Colonel, nearby, galloped up, calling ‘Help!’ No, our Italian colonel was no longer human! Foam rose to his lips; he roared like a lion. Incapable even of a word or cry, he made a terrifying signal, drew his sword, and in two seconds his opponent lay with his skull split. The soldiers backed away.

“The captain, nearly crushed, had an Italian wife, a beautiful Sicilian from Messina, who was not indifferent to our Colonel. This aggravated his rage. He felt pledged to protect the husband as he would the woman herself.

“In the hovel beyond Zembin where I was so well received, this captain sat opposite me, and his wife was at the other end facing the Colonel. This Sicilian, Rosina, was very dark, with the sun’s fire in her almond-shaped eyes. She was terribly thin; her face dusted like fruit exposed on a highway. Scarcely clothed in rags, exhausted by marches, her hair dishevelled under a shawl, she still had a woman’s graces; her small rose mouth, white teeth, and figure retained charms that misery had not erased. Rosina seemed fragile but wiry and springy. Her husband, a Piedmontese gentleman, had an ironical simplicity that betrayed cruelty beneath phlegm and indolence. He seemed unaware of any connection between his wife and the Colonel for three years; yet a thin, restless underlip turning down at the corners filled me with distrust.

“The conversation was not lively when I arrived. My comrades ate in silence; they asked questions, mixed reflections on the campaign, and lamented the cold. A minute after I arrived, the colonel, having finished his meager meal, wiped his mustache, bade us goodnight, shot a black look at the Italian woman, saying, ‘Rosina?’ and went into the little barn full of hay to sleep. The meaning of the call was clear. The young wife responded with an indescribable gesture, expressing the annoyance she could not feel at seeing her thralldom flaunted without decency, and the offense to her dignity as a woman and to her husband. Yet there was in her rigid features a sort of presentiment; perhaps she foresaw her fate. Rosina remained quietly in her place.

“A minute later, apparently when the Colonel was settled in straw, he repeated, ‘Rosina?’ The tone was even more brutally demanding. Rosina turned pale, rose, passed behind us, and went to the Colonel. All sat in utter silence; I, unfortunately, began to laugh, and then they all laughed too.

“‘Tu ridi?—you laugh?’ said the husband.

“‘On my honor, old comrade,’ I replied seriously, ‘I confess I was wrong; I ask your pardon a thousand times, and if you are not satisfied with my apologies, I am ready to give you satisfaction.’

“‘Oh! it is not you who are wrong, it is I!’ he replied coldly.

“Thereupon we lay down in the room, and before long all were sound asleep.

“Next morning, each one, without waking his neighbor or seeking companionship, set out again on his way, with that selfishness which made our rout one of the most horrible dramas of self-seeking, melancholy, and horror ever enacted under heaven. Nevertheless, about seven or eight hundred paces from our shelter, most of us met again and walked on together, urged by the same necessity.

“Having reached a knoll where we could still see the farmhouse, we heard sounds like a roar, mingled with a woman’s feeble scream. We looked: the farmhouse had been barricaded and was in flames. Swirls of smoke carried hoarse cries and an indescribable pungent smell. A few yards behind, the captain was quietly approaching to join our caravan; we gazed at him in silence; he, understanding our curiosity, pointed to his breast with his right forefinger and, waving his left toward the fire, said, ‘Son’io.’ We all walked on without a word to him.”

“There is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep,” de Marsay remarked.

“It would be frightful to leave with this horrible picture in our memory,” Madame de Montcornet said. “I shall dream of it——”

“And what was the punishment of Monsieur de Marsay’s ‘First’?” Lord Dudley asked, smiling.

“When the English jest, their foils have the buttons on,” Blondet replied.

“Monsieur Bianchon can tell us, for he saw her dying,” de Marsay said, turning to the doctor.

“Yes,” the doctor replied; “and her end was one of the most beautiful I ever witnessed. The Duke and I had spent the night by the dying woman’s pillow; pulmonary consumption, in its last stage, left no hope; she had taken the sacrament the day before. The Duke had fallen asleep. The Duchess, waking about four in the morning, signed to me with a friendly smile to leave him to rest, while she was about to die. She had become incredibly thin, but her face retained its sublime outline and features. Her pallor made her skin look like porcelain with a light within. Her bright eyes contrasted with this languidly elegant complexion, and her face was full of expressive calm. She seemed to pity the Duke, and this feeling sprang from a lofty tenderness that, as death approached, seemed boundless. The silence was absolute. The room, softly lit by a lamp, resembled every sickroom at the hour of death.

“At this moment the clock struck. The Duke awoke, despairing at having fallen asleep. I did not see the impatient gesture he made, expressing regret for having lost sight of his wife for a few of the last granted moments; but it is certain anyone but the dying woman might have misunderstood it. A busy statesman, always thinking of France’s interests, the Duke had a thousand odd surface ways which often lead a genius to be mistaken for a madman, explainable by the exacting needs of their intellect. He came to sit in an armchair by his wife’s side and looked fixedly at her. The dying woman reached out, took her husband’s hand and clasped it feebly; in a low but agitated voice she said, ‘My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?’ Then she died, looking at him.”

“The stories the doctor tells us,” the Comte de Vandenesse remarked, “always leave a deep impression.”

“But a sweet one,” Mademoiselle des Touches said, rising.
