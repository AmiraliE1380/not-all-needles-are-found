Doctor Monsieur Bianchon shares a chilling tale about La Grande Bretèche, a dilapidated house near Vendôme, surrounded by a wild garden that hints at its tragic history. The house's decay suggests a cursed past.

During his stay, Bianchon becomes fascinated by the house and sneaks into the garden. He meets local notary Monsieur Regnault, who reveals that the house belongs to the estate of the late Comtesse de Merret. Her will forbids entry or repairs for fifty years after her death, adding to its allure.

Regnault recounts the Comtesse's story: after her husband, the Count de Merret, died, she became reclusive, leaving the house in disarray. On her deathbed, she summoned Regnault to witness her will, intending to leave her estate to the hospital in Vendôme with strict conditions regarding the house.

Bianchon reflects on the Comtesse's life and local gossip. His landlady, Madame Lepas, reveals more about the Comtesse and her husband, hinting at a young Spanish prisoner connected to her tragic fate.

In a parallel story, a woman recounts the mysterious disappearance of Count Férédia, a Spanish Grandee. After he fails to return one night, her husband finds his clothes near the river, indicating he escaped. The woman suspects the Count's involvement in Madame de Merret's death, grappling with the moral dilemma of keeping his money. Intrigued by Rosalie, the maid who knows secrets about Madame de Merret's death, the narrator seeks the truth, leading to reflections on the moral complexities within La Grande Bretèche.
