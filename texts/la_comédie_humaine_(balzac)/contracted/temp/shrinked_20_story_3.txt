For effusive souls, twilight — that soft, silent hour between day and night — invites contemplation of art. During one such hour a young painter, absorbed in finishing a large canvas, falls from his ladder, strikes his head, and loses consciousness.

He wakes to the gentle voice of a young woman and her mother, who had rushed to his aid. As he regains his senses he is struck by the girl's beauty, precisely matching the ideal of feminine grace he has long imagined. The older woman, resembling a refined marquise, tends him kindly and advises on his recovery. They accept his thanks but, by natural reserve, depart without pressing for further acquaintance.

After they leave, the porter’s wife identifies them as Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, long-time residents of the building. The painter, Hippolyte Schinner, recently moved into the attic studio after winning some recognition. He is determined to provide better for his mother, who sacrificed much for him. Despite growing success he remains reserved and guarded about his private life.

During convalescence Hippolyte repeatedly returns in thought to the two women, especially the young girl's image, which lingers. When well enough to return to his studio he is eager to visit them, feeling a bond with their situation; he imagines they share a kind of misfortune that draws him close.

At their modest home he notes signs of poverty but feels no contempt, only empathy. Mademoiselle Leseigneur greets him politely; he is struck by the contrast between their humble surroundings and the elegance they maintain. The room contains remnants of past luxury — faded upholstery and well-worn ornament — yet everything is meticulously kept.

The conversation begins stiffly but soon turns to art, allowing Hippolyte to speak freely about his thoughts and feelings. Madame Leseigneur reveals how dear a portrait of her late husband, a naval officer, is to her, though the canvas is deteriorating. Hippolyte offers to paint a new portrait, hoping to forge a closer connection; the offer is accepted with graceful gratitude, and he senses the deep emotional weight behind it.

As they talk, Adelaide steps out briefly and returns with candles to light the room, shifting the atmosphere. Then an unexpected visitor arrives — a man who seems acquainted with Adelaide. His presence stirs jealousy in Hippolyte, who wants to know the nature of their relationship.

The newcomer is an older gentleman in a coat whose style recalls a bygone era; he carries an air mixing nobility with libertinism, suggesting a complicated past. His exchange with the women reveals layers of their lives, leaving Hippolyte to ponder the dynamics and histories hinted at by their manners and circumstances.

Throughout the evening Hippolyte struggles with feelings for Adelaide — admiration tangled with uncertainty. The contrast in social standing weighs on him, yet he is drawn to her spirit and grace, conscious of the slender hope for connection amid the shadows of their respective worlds.

A remarkable figure stood beside the “Louis XIV’s light infantry” specimen — Bonapartists’ nickname for those remnants of the Monarchy. A lean, aged man, he seemed a faded reflection of the old order: a worn coat, dull hair powder, a feebler intellect, the very image Rivarol’s quip about Champcenetz suggested.

This silent old man puzzled the company and the painter. He neither spoke nor was spoken to; his relation to the gallant host—friend, relative, or mere companion—remained unclear. His presence invited curiosity but offered no answers, especially about any link to the host’s deceased wife.

The least dilapidated of the group approached Madame de Rouville, kissed her hand, and sat beside her; another man bowed and took a seat nearby. Adelaide leaned over the old gentleman’s chair, echoing a famous painting’s pose, though his familiarity seemed to irritate her. “Are you sulky with me?” he asked, casting a shrewd glance at Schinner, the painter, as if to judge his standing.

“This is our neighbor,” Madame de Rouville introduced Hippolyte, the celebrated painter. The old man noted the artist’s red ribbon of distinction against his own hard-earned honors. Hippolyte, captivated by Adelaide’s beauty, grew lost in admiration and briefly forgot their squalor. Absorbed, he answered only shortly; the old gentleman lamented his card losses, Adelaide offered help, but he insisted she stay where he could see her. As the game ended, the gentleman complained of bad luck; the silent figure echoed the time, and Hippolyte left, entranced by the first stirrings of love.

The next day Hippolyte felt an overwhelming urge to see Adelaide. Hesitant until afternoon, he awkwardly requested the Baron’s portrait. Alone, Adelaide asked him to fetch it, revealing her modesty. Their shared glance conveyed more than words; Hippolyte took the portrait, promising its quick return.

In the following days, their relationship blossomed with laughter and shared thought. Schinner, eager to learn piquet, often lost, while an unspoken bond between Hippolyte and Adelaide deepened. They became inseparable: Adelaide’s affection grew, Hippolyte’s jealousy flared when the old Count showed undue familiarity. Adelaide worried Hippolyte’s social life harmed his health; their love matured without grand gestures, built on mutual trust and delicate exchanges. Within two months their lives were intertwined: Hippolyte visited daily, and their connection deepened.

The old gentleman’s visits waned as Hippolyte took his place at the card table, sharing in the losses. Yet Hippolyte couldn’t shake concerns over Madame de Rouville’s finances. He spent two months on the portrait, calling it one of his best, but the Baroness’s silence puzzled him. One day Adelaide visited his studio and was awestruck by the work; their charged moment overflowed with feeling. Hippolyte, overwhelmed, declared his desire to marry her, but the old vice-admiral arrived, praised the painting, and hinted at funds to pay for both portraits.

As the evening went on, Hippolyte noticed Madame de Rouville’s unusual excitement during cards and suspected a gambling habit. He began to question the nature of their relationship, wondering if deception was afoot. After a night of torment, he discovered his purse missing and suspected Adelaide and her mother of theft. Despite his heartbreak, he clung to hope of a misunderstanding. The next day a friend mentioned rumors about Adelaide and her mother, deepening his unease. Torn between love and doubt, he resolved to confront his feelings and visit Madame de Rouville.

Seeing Adelaide pale and sad, he felt ashamed of his suspicions. Their talk revealed mutual longing, and he realized his mistake. The evening turned sweet as they played cards, but his fears returned when he noticed Adelaide’s secretive glances at her mother. During the game, Adelaide discreetly placed a new purse before Hippolyte, revealing her thoughtful attempt to replace the lost one. Overwhelmed, he grasped the depth of their bond and impulsively declared his intention to marry her.

Just then the old vice-admiral arrived, announcing his promotion and expressing support for the young couple. The evening ended with a sense of unity as the families came together, dispelling lingering doubts. The tale closes on love, trust, and reconciliation after misunderstanding, promising a brighter future.
