There is a particular twilight that draws reflective artists into their work. At that hour a young painter, finishing a large canvas, falls from his ladder, strikes his head, and loses consciousness. He is awakened by a young woman and her mother who rush to his aid. As he recovers he notices the girl’s beauty—matching an ideal he had imagined—and the older woman’s refined, marquise-like air. The mother tends him with gentle advice; both are reserved and withdraw without pressing for acquaintance.

After they leave, the porter’s wife identifies them as Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother. The painter, Hippolyte Schinner, has just moved into the attic studio and begun to gain recognition. Determined to secure a better life for his own mother, who sacrificed much for him, he keeps his private affairs guarded despite modest success. During convalescence Hippolyte replays the encounter, especially the image of the girl, which lingers; he returns to the studio eager to visit, drawn by a kinship born of imagined shared misfortune.

In their modest home he sees poverty but feels only empathy, struck by the contrast between their humble surroundings and the elegance they preserve—worn finery kept meticulously clean. Conversation, after an awkward start, turns to art. Madame Leseigneur shows a decaying portrait of her late naval husband; Hippolyte offers to paint a new one, hoping to deepen their acquaintance, and they accept. Adelaide briefly leaves and returns with candles, and the mood shifts when an older gentleman in anachronistic dress arrives—his noble, libertine air and familiarity with her hint at a layered past. Throughout the evening Hippolyte wrestles with admiration and uncertainty, drawn to her spirit while aware of the fragile boundary between their worlds.

A remarkable, faded figure stood beside “Louis XIV’s light infantry,” Bonapartist sarcasm for the ragged remnants of the Monarchy: lean, aged, powdered hair dulled, intellect clouded. Rivarol’s quip about Champcenetz seemed apt. The silent old man puzzled the painter Chevalier—no one addressed him. His relation to the gallant man—friend, relative, or companion—was unclear; his presence hinted at a possible link to the gallant’s deceased wife but offered no answers.

One of the least dilapidated men approached Madame de Rouville, kissed her hand, and sat near her; another bowed and took a seat. Adelaide leaned over the old gentleman’s chair in a pose recalling a famous painting, though his familiarity seemed to irritate her. “Are you sulky with me?” he asked, casting a shrewd glance at the painter Schinner as if sizing him up.

Madame de Rouville introduced Hippolyte, the celebrated painter. The old man noticed Hippolyte’s red ribbon and compared it with his own hard-won honors. Hippolyte, captivated by Adelaide’s beauty, was briefly blind to the squalor around them; enthralled, he spoke little while the old gentleman lamented his losing streak at cards. Adelaide offered help; he insisted she stay where he could see her. When the game ended and the gentleman complained of losses, the silent figure only echoed the hour, prompting Hippolyte to take his leave, still under the spell of budding affection.

The next day Hippolyte longed to see Adelaide again. He waited until afternoon before visiting Madame de Rouville and awkwardly asked for the Baron’s portrait. Adelaide, alone in the drawing-room, sent him to fetch it, revealing modesty with a charged glance. Hippolyte took the portrait and promised to return it soon.

Their relationship blossomed over the following days with laughter and confidences. Schinner, eager to learn piquet, often lost, while the unspoken bond between Hippolyte and Adelaide deepened. They became inseparable; Adelaide’s affection grew and Hippolyte’s jealousy flared when the old Count showed familiarity with her. He feared their social life harmed his health and expected more from her. Their love unfolded without grand gestures, founded on mutual trust and subtle exchanges. Within two months Hippolyte visited daily, the old gentleman’s visits faded, and Hippolyte took his place at the card table, sharing equally in the losses.

Still, Hippolyte fretted about Madame de Rouville’s finances. He spent two months on the Baron’s portrait—considering it among his finest—but was puzzled by the Baroness’s silence about payment. One day Adelaide visited his studio and was awestruck by the portrait; moved, Hippolyte declared his desire to marry her. Their moment was interrupted by the old vice-admiral, who praised the work and hinted at financial help.

That evening Hippolyte noticed Madame de Rouville’s unusual excitement during cards. After a night of turmoil he found his purse missing and, in pain, suspected Adelaide and her mother of theft. A friend’s mention of rumors unsettled him further. Torn between love and doubt, he confronted Adelaide; struck by her pallor and sadness, he felt ashamed. During a tender evening of cards, Adelaide discreetly placed a new purse before him to replace the lost one. Overwhelmed, he declared his intention to marry her.

The old vice-admiral soon returned with news of a promotion and offered support to the young couple, praising the portrait and dispelling lingering doubts. The evening closed with renewed unity. Ultimately the tale follows characters navigating love, trust, and misunderstanding, finding solace through faith, reconciliation, and shared bonds.
