For souls given to effusiveness there is a delightful hour between day and night; twilight softens and mingles light and shadow, favoring a dreamy mood that artists especially cherish. In that hour skill and technique dissolve into illusion: a painting seems alive—figures speak and move, flesh breathes, fabrics change sheen—and the artist, withdrawn from work, surrenders to meanings that flash upon the inner eye. Illusion, a kind of mental night peopled with dreams, lifts the soul above ordinary cares.

It was at this magic hour that a young painter, who saw art as an end in itself, perched on a step-ladder to finish a large canvas. Lost in meditative judgment and self-admiration, he lingered long. Night fell; whether by misstep or believing himself on the floor, he fell, struck his head on a footstool, and lay unconscious for an indeterminate time.

A sweet voice at last roused him. Blinded by a bright lamp, he heard whispering and felt timid hands support his head. As the mist cleared he saw, by the light of an old Argand lamp, the most charming face he had ever seen—an ideal beauty corresponding to the artist’s taste: features of delicate refinement, a virginal innocence like Prudhon and Girodet’s ideals; a slight, graceful figure and a modest, neat dress giving no clue of wealth or want. A handkerchief pressed to his forehead smelled faintly of ether, used to revive him; an elderly woman held the lamp and advised the girl.

“Monsieur,” the younger woman said when he questioned them while still dazed, “my mother and I heard a noise and thought we heard a groan. After the crash the silence alarmed us. Finding the key in the latch we entered and found you motionless. My mother fetched what was needed to bathe your head and revive you. You have cut your forehead—there. Do you feel it?”

“Yes, I do now,” he replied.

“Oh, it will be nothing,” said the elder woman. “Happily your head rested against this lay-figure.”

“I feel infinitely better,” the painter answered. “I need only a hackney cab to take me home. The porter’s wife will go for one.”

He tried to thank them, but the older lady interrupted with medical admonitions: “Tomorrow, monsieur, be careful—put on leeches, or be bled, and drink something healing. A fall may be dangerous.”

The young girl stole glances at him and at the pictures; she showed perfect propriety, a curiosity tempered by reserve, and an innocent interest in another’s suffering. They seemed to forget the canvases in his mishap, and when reassured they left, looking at him with anxious kindness—neither insistent nor familiar, free from indiscreet questions or invitations. Their noble simplicity made a quiet impression on the painter; later he would be struck by those details.

On the landing the old lady gently observed, “Adelaide, you left the door open.”

“That was to hear me fall,” he replied with a grateful smile.

“You came down just now, mother,” said the young girl, blushing.

“Would you like us to accompany you downstairs? The stairs are dark.”

“No, thank you, madame; I am much better.”

“Hold tightly by the rail.”

They stayed to light him to the stairhead and listened to his footsteps.

To appreciate the effect of this scene, it helps to know that only a few days earlier the painter, Hippolyte Schinner, had set up his studio in the attics of this house in the murkier part of the Rue de Suresnes, near the Rue des Champs-Élysées and almost opposite the Madeleine. Fame had recently come to him: at the last exhibition he was hailed as an unknown genius, acknowledged by artists and rewarded with the Cross of the Legion of Honor; dealers began to pay him well. He had spared himself long walks to cheaper outskirts studios and was now enjoying his “last privations.”

Hippolyte was attractive but reserved; he did not invite acquaintances easily and guarded his personal story. He had been raised by a needy mother, Mademoiselle Schinner, a farmer’s daughter from Alsace who had never married. Her gentleness had been crushed long ago by a rich man who betrayed her; she refused his alms, renounced society, and devoted herself to maternal love, working and saving a little treasure for her son. The sacrifices of those years were amply repaid when his talent brought him honor and income. Eager to restore to his mother the pleasures denied her, Hippolyte lived for her, selecting friends among the honorable and distinguished to raise his standing still further. His boyhood solitude had left him with delicate, poetic beliefs and shy, virginal hopes that marked his character and manners: polite, modest, with a voice that stirred noble sentiments and a presence that attracted without being explainable.

These traits explain why, when he later sent the porter to fetch a cab, Hippolyte did not press the porter’s wife for information about the two women who had tended him. He answered her chatter but let it go. “They were, no doubt, Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, who have lived here four years,” she said. “We do not know exactly what they do; in the mornings an old deaf woman helps them. In the evenings two or three elderly gentlemen with ribbon loops come to see them and stay late. One arrives by carriage with servants; they say he has sixty thousand francs a year. However, they are quiet—economical—and pay promptly. It’s queer the mother’s name is not the same as the daughter’s. When they go to the Tuileries mademoiselle is very smart, always followed by young men, though she shuts the door in their faces.” The cab came; Hippolyte went home.

His mother dressed his wound and forbade his return to the studio the next day. During three enforced days at home his memory retrieved the scene of his fainting: the young girl’s profile against the dark, the mother’s faded face, Adelaide’s touch, a graceful gesture, a tone of voice—details rising like objects from deep water. When he could work again he went early to his studio, but his real motive was to visit his neighbors. Passion, when it first awakens, gives a certain immediate delight: he climbed to the fourth-floor door of Mademoiselle Leseigneur slowly, his heart beating at the thought of calling. He felt a similarity between their positions and imagined misfortunes akin to his own. After thinking, dining in his studio, he went to call at seven.

Parisian life often shows dissonances between appearance and comfort: fresh toilettes and brilliant women housed in rooms that betray doubtful means. Hip-polyte’s neighbors’ rooms influenced his feelings and hopes.

The house belonged to a proprietor who hated repairs: chimneys were left faulty, woodwork grimy and stained. Hippolyte, who had seen such frugality before, expected these signs and saw their poetical aspect. Mademoiselle Leseigneur opened the door. Recognizing him she bowed and, with Parisian tact, turned to shut a glass partition that hid laundry and a cramped household: a camp-bed, irons, a filter, kitchen crockery—utensils of a simple domestic life. Muslin curtains screened the clutter from a window overlooking a yard.

With an artist’s eye Hippolyte took in the arrangement. The room visible was divided: an ante-room/dining-room hung with patched salmon-rose paper and a faded flock border; in the center, a massive mahogany table worn at the edges; a small stove and a blocked hearth by a cupboard; carved chairs with red morocco seats, gilt nails showing former splendor. The room was a museum of the odd mingling of luxury and penury: a finely finished telescope hung over a discolored glass; a painted sideboard mimicked mahogany; slippery red quarry tiles and shabby rugs; everything hard-cleaned in a way that emphasized its age. An indescribable odor rose from the lumber-room and kitchen, while the partly open window let in dusty street air.

Adelaide opened an inner door and received him with obvious pleasure. Hippolyte, whose memories of his mother’s poverty were vivid, entered with sympathy. The old lady rose from an antique armchair and offered him a seat.

“Well, monsieur, I hope you no longer feel the effects of your fall,” she said.

“No, madame. I have come to thank you for the kind care you gave me, and above all mademoiselle, who heard me fall.”

As Hippolyte spoke with the awkwardness of first love he looked at the girl. Adelaide lit the Argand lamp, replacing a tallow candle, placed the lamp on the chimney shelf, and sat a little behind him to look at him at ease while pretending interest in the lamp. The flame sputtered in the damp chimney. A mirror on the chimney-piece reflected her, and she and the painter exchanged restrained glances, each embarrassed by the stratagem.

While talking with Madame Leseigneur, he observed the room more closely. Egyptian figures on iron fire-dogs were barely visible under cinders; two brands struggled before a sham log; a faded Aubusson carpet left the tiled floor cold. The walls were papered in a reddish imitation of figured silk; a crack outlined double doors closing a recess where Madame Leseigneur probably slept, hidden by a sofa. Above a chest of drawers hung a dim portrait of an officer; a malachite tray with a dozen painted coffee cups—perhaps Sèvres—stood on the chest. On the chimney-shelf an Empire clock—an equestrian charioteer with four horses—marked the hours; tall candlesticks bore smoke-yellowed tapers; dusty artificial flowers crowned porcelain vases.

A card-table with new packs at the center suggested either these women were industrious and honest or supported by intrigue and gambling. But Adelaide’s pure demeanor persuaded Schinner of her innocence; he preferred to ascribe the furniture’s dissonance to honorable causes.

“My dear,” the old lady said to the young one, “I am cold; make a little fire, and give me my shawl.”

Adelaide wrapped a once-costly, now-faded and darned cashmere around her mother and fetched small bundles of wood from the lumber-room to revive the fire.

Their conversation that followed was tactful and cautious. Hippolyte, mindful that pity can embarrass, avoided blunt inquiries. Women are skilled at easing the awkwardness of a first call: they asked about painting, prompting him to speak—and as they talked, his reflections and habits came out. The old lady’s face, worn by trouble but retaining an outline of refined grace and shrewd eyes, suggested the old court’s genteel bearing; she could hint either at artifice or delicacy. A woman’s face always conceals the line between frankness and duplicity; only intuition discerns it. At Schinner’s age the first impulse is to believe in goodness. Looking at Adelaide’s noble brow and soulful eyes, he sensed modest virtue. He seized the opportunity to discuss portraits and inspect a faded pastel whose chalk had faded.

“You are attached to that picture for the likeness, no doubt, mesdames, for the drawing is poor?” he said.

“It was done at Calcutta, in great haste,” replied the mother, agitated.

She looked upon the formless sketch with the absorption of one who cherishes a memory; regret showed in her face. The painter read in her attitude the mark of a once-happy life and sat down beside her.

“Madame,” he said, “in a short time the colors of that pastel will disappear. The portrait will survive only in your memory. May I reproduce the likeness on canvas? It will endure. Grant me, as a neighborly favor, the pleasure of doing you this service. Sometimes an artist enjoys a respite from greater undertakings by doing work of less lofty pretension; it will be a recreation.”

The old lady flushed at the offer; Adelaide’s glance showed deep feeling. Hippolyte wanted a tie to these neighbors, a right to mingle in their life. His proposal appealed to maternal affection, gratified his pride as an artist, and could not offend. Madame Leseigneur accepted with the composed dignity of a noble soul, aware of the delicate nature of such an obligation.

“I fancy,” the painter continued, “that the uniform is that of a naval officer.”

“Yes,” she said. “That was a captain in command. Monsieur de Rouville—my husband—died at Batavia from a wound received in a fight with an English ship off the Asiatic coast. He commanded a frigate of fifty-six guns; the Revenge carried ninety-six. The struggle was unequal, but he defended his ship bravely, held out till nightfall and got away. When I returned to France Bonaparte had not yet come to power, and I was refused a pension. When I applied again, quite lately, I was sternly told that if the Baron de Rouville had emigrated I should not have lost him; that by this time he would have been a rear-admiral; and finally his Excellency quoted degrees of forfeiture. I took the step, urged by friends, only for Adelaide’s sake. I have always hated holding out my hand as a beggar in the name of a grief that silences a woman’s voice and strength. I do not like this money valuation for blood irreparably spilt—”

“Dear mother, this subject always does you harm,” Adelaide interrupted, and the Baronne bowed in silence.

“Monsieur,” the young girl observed softly, “I had supposed a painter’s work was generally fairly quiet?” Schinner colored, remembering the noise he had made.

Their conversation was interrupted by the sound of a carriage stopping at the door. Adelaide went into her room and returned with a pair of tall gilt candlesticks with partly burnt wax candles, which she lit. Setting the lamp down, she opened the outer door. The sound of a kiss exchanged touched Hippolyte’s heart; he was impatient to see the man who treated Adelaide familiarly. The newcomers conversed in undertone with her.

At last Mademoiselle de Rouville returned, followed by two men whose costumes and appearance suggested tales of their own. The first, about sixty, wore one of those coats of the Louis XVIII. period—neither fully civil nor military—embroidered with fleurs-de-lis and gilt buttons; shoulder straps hinted at epaulettes, and his powdered hair showed no hat-crease. He might be taken for fifty and seemed robust. His frank, loyal émigré expression hinted also at libertine habits, a reckless gallantry like that of the Musketeers. His gestures and manner showed no intention of renouncing royalism, religion, or gallantry.

A really fantastic figure stood behind this specimen of “Louis XIV.’s light infantry”—a nickname the Bonapartists gave these venerable survivors of the Monarchy. To do it justice it ought to be the principal object in the picture, and yet it remained only an accessory. Imagine a lean, dry man, dressed like the first, but seeming only his reflection or his shadow. The coat, new on the one, on the other was old; the powder in his hair looked less white, the fleurs-de-lis less bright, the shoulder straps more hopeless and dog’s-eared; his intellect seemed feebler, his life nearer the end. In short, he realized Rivarol’s witticism on Champcenetz—“He is the moonlight of me.” He was simply the double of the old gallant, paler and poorer, the difference between first and last impressions of a lithograph.

This speechless old man—the Chevalier—was a mystery to the painter and always remained one. He did not speak; nobody spoke to him. Was he friend, poor relation, or the elderly companion who followed a gallant like a lady follows an older woman? Did he fill a place midway between dog, parrot, and friend? Had he saved his patron’s fortune or only his life? Was he a Trim to another Captain Toby? At the Baronne de Rouville’s he provoked curiosity without satisfying it. Who, after the Restoration, could recall the attachment that had once bound this man to his friend’s wife, twenty years dead?

The leader, least dilapidated of these wrecks, came gallantly to Madame de Rouville, kissed her hand, and sat by her. The other bowed and placed himself not far from his model, two chairs apart. Adelaide leaned her elbows on the back of the old gentleman’s armchair, unconsciously imitating the pose Guerin gave Dido’s sister.

Though the gentleman’s familiarity was paternal, his freedom seemed for a moment to annoy the young girl.

“What, are you sulky with me?” he said.

Then he shot at Schinner one of those side-glances full of shrewdness and discreet curiosity that ask, when they see a stranger, “Is he one of us?”

“This is our neighbor,” said the old lady, pointing to Hippolyte. “Monsieur is a celebrated painter; his name must be known to you despite your indifference to the arts.”

The old man read the Baronne’s mischievous intent in suppressing the name and bowed.

“Certainly,” he said. “I heard much about his pictures at the last Salon. Talent has immense privileges,” he added, observing the artist’s red ribbon. “That distinction, which we must earn by our service and blood, you gain in youth; but all glory is of the kindred,” he said, laying his hand on his Cross of Saint-Louis.

Hippolyte murmured acknowledgment and fell silent again, satisfied to admire the beautiful girl’s head that charmed him. He soon forgot the misery of the dwelling; to him Adelaide’s face stood out against a luminous atmosphere. He replied briefly to questions, as if some double consciousness allowed him to listen without attending; his mind overflowed with hope and visions of happiness.

After a short time he perceived the old lady and her daughter playing cards with the old gentleman. The silent Chevalier stood behind his friend’s chair, watching the game, answering mute inquiries by approving nods and repeating gestures.

“Du Halga, I always lose,” said the gentleman.

“You discard badly,” replied the Baronne.

“For three months now I have never won a single game,” he persisted.

“Have you the aces?” asked the old lady.

“Yes, one more to mark,” he said.

“Shall I come and advise you?” Adelaide offered.

“No, no. Stay where I can see you. By Gad, it would be losing too much not to have you to look at!”

At last the game ended. The gentleman threw two louis d’or on the table, not without temper. “Forty francs,” he cried. “Deuce take it! It is eleven o’clock.”

“It is eleven o’clock,” repeated the silent figure, looking at the painter. Hearing this, Hippolyte thought it time to retire. He found a few commonplace remarks, took leave of the Baroness, her daughter, and the two strangers, and went away wholly possessed by the first raptures of true love, without attempting to analyze the evening’s little incidents.

Next day the painter ardently desired to see Adelaide. He waited until afternoon, then blushing like a girl went downstairs to ask Mademoiselle Leseigneur to let him have the portrait of the Baron. Adelaide, who had heard him come down from the studio, summoned him in.

He followed, bashful and out of countenance; mere proximity to her was ecstasy. The old lady was not in the drawing-room. Adelaide brought a chair to stand on to take down the picture but found she could not unhook it without setting her foot on the chest of drawers; blushing, she asked Hippolyte, “I am not tall enough. Will you get it down?”—a modesty that meant more than the request. He read her soul and gave her a look of understanding that was the sweetest language of love. Adelaide dropped her eyes in maiden reserve. Hippolyte, shy, took down the picture, examined it, carried it to the window, and went away, saying only, “I will return it soon.”

In that brief moment they underwent one of those storms of agitation whose effects are like a stone flung into a deep lake—reveries that rise and follow each other, titillating the heart with repeated ripples.

Hippolyte returned to his studio bearing the portrait. His easel was ready with a fresh canvas. Till dinner he worked with the ardor artists put into whims. He went again that evening and remained from nine till eleven. The two old men arrived at the same hour, the same piquet was played, the same speeches made, Adelaide’s friend lost as much as before; Hippolyte, a little bolder, chatted with the young girl.

A week passed. In it the painter’s and Adelaide’s feelings underwent slow, delightful transformations that brought their souls to perfect understanding. Each day the look with which she welcomed him grew more intimate, more confiding, gayer; her voice and manner more eager and familiar. They laughed and talked, telling their thoughts with the artless trust of two children who have been friends for years. Schinner wished to be taught piquet, made blunders, and, like the old man, lost every game. Without speaking of love, the lovers knew they were all in all to each other. Hippolyte enjoyed his power over his gentle friend; Adelaide, timid and devoted, surrendered in small ways—false fits of temper and caprices that lovers and guileless girls play—so that familiarity between the girl and the old Count soon ceased. She understood the painter’s melancholy from the abrupt tone of his few words when the old man kissed her hands or throat.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur, for her part, expected a full account of the painter’s actions; she scolded him for absences and the painter had to give up seeing other friends. Adelaide’s natural jealousy showed when she heard he sometimes, at eleven, had visits in brilliant drawing-rooms. She told him such a life could not be proof of warm affection. Thus he came, as much from the tyranny of his passion as from the demands of a girl in love, to live almost exclusively in the little apartment where everything attracted him.

Never was there purer love. On both sides the same trustfulness and delicacy increased their passion without the usual sacrifices people use to prove affection. Between them a spontaneous affinity united their souls; two months after meeting their lives were one. From morning Adelaide knew when his steps were overhead. When he dined at his mother’s he still looked in on his neighbors; in the evening he flew there with lover’s punctuality. Adelaide tasted unmixed happiness, seeing the realization of the ideal natural to her age.

The old gentleman now came more rarely; Hippolyte, once jealous, had taken his place at the green table, sharing his constant ill luck. Yet, in the midst of happiness thoughts of Madame de Rouville’s disastrous poverty haunted him. Several times he mused, “Strange! twenty francs every evening?” and dared not confess to himself his ugly suspicions.

He spent two months on the portrait; when finished and framed, he considered it one of his best works. Madame la Baronne de Rouville had never spoken of it again. Was this indifference or pride? The painter would not analyze her silence. He plotted with Adelaide to hang the picture when the Baronne was out. One day during her usual walk in the Tuileries Adelaide came to the studio to see the portrait in its good light. She stood speechless, in ecstatic contemplation; a woman’s admiration for the man she loves suffused her. When the painter leaned forward, she held out her hand; two tears fell. Hippolyte took her hand, covered it with kisses; for a minute they looked at each other in silence, both longing to confess but not daring. The painter kept her hand, their hearts beating wildly. Adelaide, too agitated, gently drew away and said simply, “You will make my mother very happy.”

“What, only your mother?” he asked.

“Oh, I am too happy.”

The painter bent his head, frightened by the vehemence of feelings her tones stirred. Both, aware of perils, went downstairs and hung the picture in its place. Hippolyte dined for the first time with the Baroness, who, overcome and in tears, embraced him.

In the evening the old emigre, the Baron de Rouville’s comrade, paid a visit to announce he had been promoted to vice-admiral; his land voyages were counted as naval campaigns. Seeing the portrait he shook the painter’s hand, “By Gad! though my old hulk does not deserve to be perpetuated, I would give five hundred pistoles to see myself as like that is to my dear old Rouville.”

At the hint the Baroness glanced at her young friend and smiled with sudden gratitude. Hippolyte suspected the admiral meant to offer the price of both portraits while paying for his own. Pride and jealousy offended, and he replied, “Monsieur, if I were a portrait-painter I should not have done this one.”

The admiral bit his lip and sat down to cards. The painter remained near Adelaide, who proposed a dozen hands of piquet, to which he agreed. As he played he observed an excitement in Madame de Rouville unusual for her—an ardent desire to win, a keen joy in fingering the old gentleman’s gold. Evil suspicions returned: could Madame de Rouville live by gambling? Was she playing to pay a debt or rent? The old man seemed shrewd, keeping his money from being taken. What interest attracted him, who was rich, to this fourth-floor house where he lost some forty francs every evening? Why had he given up the liberties he once enjoyed with Adelaide?

These reflections made Hippolyte watch the old man and the Baroness; their looks toward Adelaide displeased him. “Am I being duped?” he thought—horrible. He resolved to stay after the old men left to confirm or dispel his suspicions. He drew his purse to pay Adelaide but, carried away by tormenting thoughts, laid it on the table and fell into reverie. Ashamed of his silence, he rose, answered a commonplace question, and moved closer to the Baronne to examine her withered features while he talked.

On leaving he felt dizzy; he had gone only a few steps when he turned back for the forgotten purse. “I left my purse here!” he said.

“No,” Adelaide said, reddening.

“I thought it was there,” he said, pointing to the card-table. Not finding it, in shame for Adelaide and the Baroness, he looked at them with amazement that made them laugh. He felt in his pockets: not there. He searched himself but did not find the purse. His memory retraced the fatal truth: he remembered the purse on the green cloth. The theft was blatant and denied with effrontery; Hippolyte no longer felt doubt as to his neighbors’ morals. His knees shook, he found himself unable to walk, struggling with the shock caused by the destruction of all his hopes. He recalled many trifling observations that now corroborated his suspicions; these suggested the character and life of the women.

Had they waited till the portrait was given them before robbing him? If so, the theft was odious. He thought perhaps the admiral had reasons for not marrying Adelaide, and the Baroness had tried to make gains—then checked himself. “If the Baroness hoped to get me to marry her daughter, they would not have robbed me,” he thought.

Clinging to illusions, he tried to find a justification in accident: “The purse must have fallen; perhaps I left it on my chair.” He searched his person but did not find it. He blamed Adelaide in his heart, yet could not admit she was base. The wretched rooms rose before him, cleansed of the poetry of love; they appeared dirty and faded, emblematic of an inner life devoid of honor, idle and vicious. Our feelings, he thought, are written upon the things about us.

Next morning he rose without having slept. Heartache had made rapid inroads. To lose a future one has dreamed of is keener than losing known happiness; hope is better than memory. The painter walked under the Tuileries’ fresh shade, absorbed.

There he met Francois Souchet, a schoolfellow and sculptor who had won the first prize and was bound for Italy.

“Why, Hippolyte, what ails you?” asked Souchet.

“I am most unhappy,” replied Hippolyte gravely.

“Nothing but a love affair can cause you grief. Money, glory, respect—you lack nothing.”

Led into confidence, Hippolyte told of his love. On hearing the Rue de Suresnes and the fourth-floor girl, Souchet laughed. “Stop. A little girl I see every morning at the Church of the Assumption; we all know her. The mother is a Baroness. Do you really believe a Baroness lives up four flights of stairs? Brrr! You are a relic of the golden age! Her face, her appearance tell everything. Have you not known her for what she is by the way she holds her bag?”

Others joined them. The sculptor repeated Hippolyte’s adventure, and jests followed—mockery common among artists. The painter’s secret, he felt, was raked through with careless laughter. Reticence made him uncomfortable as his passion was lightly handled. He pretended contradiction and asked for proofs; the jokes resumed.

“But, my dear boy, have you seen the Baroness’ shawl?” said Souchet. “Have you followed the girl to church?” asked Bridau. “The mother has a certain gray gown,” Bixiou observed.

“Come at four o’clock and study their walk,” Souchet advised. “If then you still have doubts—well, you would marry your porter’s daughter.” Torn, Hippolyte parted from his friends. He thought Adelaide and her mother must be superior to these accusations and felt remorse for having suspected them. Passing their door, he felt a pain no man can misread. He loved Adelaide so passionately that, despite the theft, he still worshiped her. He felt like Des Grieux, ready to hold his mistress pure even on the cart that bears ruined women to prison. “Why should not my love keep her the purest of women? Why abandon her to evil without trying to rescue her?”

The idea of such a mission pleased him. Love makes gains of everything; nothing tempts a young man more than to act as a good genius to a woman. There is romance in this devotion—it is the loftiest stretch of submission and service.

Hippolyte sat in his studio gazing at his picture through tears, holding his brush but touching nothing. Night fell; he met the old admiral on the stairs, looked darkly at his bow, and fled. He had intended to call on the ladies, but the sight of Adelaide’s protector froze him. For the hundredth time he wondered why this prodigal, with eighty thousand francs a year, came to this fourth story to lose about forty francs every evening; he thought he could guess.

The next days he threw himself into work to conquer passion by sudden composition. He half succeeded; study consoled him but could not smother memories of tender hours.

One evening, as he left his studio, the ladies’ door lay half open. Somebody stood in the window’s recess and the staircase made it impossible for the painter to pass unseen. He bowed coldly, with supreme indifference—but imagining the bitterness such scorn would cause in a loving heart, he shuddered. To crown a feast of mutual delight with eight days of disdain would be tragic. Perhaps the purse had been found, perhaps Adelaide had looked for him nightly. This simple thought filled him with remorse; ashamed of resisting his heart for a week, almost criminal, he called that evening.

All his suspicions vanished at the sight of Adelaide, who had grown pale and thin.

“Good heavens! what is the matter?” he asked after greeting the Baroness.

Adelaide made no reply, but gave a look of deep melancholy that pained him.

“You have, no doubt, been working hard,” said the old lady. “You are altered. We are the cause of your seclusion. That portrait delayed some pictures essential to your reputation.”

Hippolyte welcomed the excuse. “Yes. I have been busy, but I have been suffering—” he began.

At the words Adelaide raised her head; her anxious eyes showed no reproach.

“You must have thought us quite indifferent to any good or ill that may befall you?” asked the Baroness.

“I was wrong,” he replied. “Still, there are forms of pain we know not how to confide, even to a friendship older perhaps than yours.”

“The sincerity and strength of friendship are not measured by time. I have seen old friends who had not a tear to bestow on misfortune,” said the Baronne sadly.

“But you—what ails you?” Hippolyte asked Adelaide.

“Oh, nothing,” the Baroness answered. “Adelaide has sat up late finishing some little piece of woman’s work; she would not listen to me when I said a day more or less did not matter—”

Hippolyte was not listening. Seeing their noble, calm faces he blushed for his suspicions and ascribed the purse’s loss to some unknown accident. The evening was delicious. There are secrets young souls understand; Adelaide read Hippolyte’s thoughts though he did not confess. He returned more in love and affectionate, seeking tacit forgiveness. This concord was disturbed, however, by a little remark of the Baronne.

“Let us have our little game,” she said, “for my old friend Kergarouet will not let me off.”

These words revived the painter’s fears; he colored at the Baronne’s look, but saw only frank good-nature; nothing perfidious clouded it.

He sat at the card-table; Adelaide took his side, saying he did not know piquet and needed a partner. Throughout the game Madame de Rouville and her daughter exchanged looks of intelligence, which alarmed Hippolyte the more because he was winning; at last a final hand left the lovers in the old lady’s debt.

Feeling in his pocket for money Hippolyte took his hands off the table and saw before him a purse Adelaide had slipped in front of him without his noticing. The poor child had the old one in her hand, and—looking into it for the money to pay her mother—held it to him. The blood rushed to his heart; he nearly fainted.

The new purse, substituted for his own and containing his fifteen gold louis, was worked with gilt beads. The rings and tassels showed Adelaide’s taste; she had no doubt spent her little hoard on ornamenting this pretty work. It was a delicate way to say that the painter’s gift could only be repaid by some proof of affection.

Overcome with happiness, Hippolyte turned to look at Adelaide and her mother; they trembled with delight at their little ruse. He felt mean and foolish; tears rose. By an irresistible impulse he sprang up, clasped Adelaide, pressed her to his heart, and stole a kiss; then, with an artist’s hearty frankness, “I ask for her for my wife!” he exclaimed to the Baroness.

Adelaide looked at him with half-wrathful eyes; Madame de Rouville, surprised, was considering her reply when the bell rang. The old vice-admiral entered, followed by his shadow and Madame Schinner—Hippolyte’s mother. Having guessed the cause of the grief her son vainly concealed, Madame Schinner, alarmed by calumnies about Adelaide, sought out the admiral. He declared he would crop all scoundrels’ ears for slandering her.

Prompted by indignation, he explained to Madame Schinner the secret of his losing intentionally at cards; the Baronne’s pride left him no other ingenious means of assisting her.

After Madame Schinner paid her respects, the Baroness looked round—at the Comte de Kergarouet, the Chevalier du Halga, at Hippolyte and Adelaide—and, with the simple grace that comes from the heart, said, “So we are a family party this evening.”

PARIS, May 1832
