To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska: the author addresses a young girl tenderly, noting the incongruity of dedicating a melancholy tale to one who brings household joy, yet insists sorrow must sometimes be spoken of, perhaps to prepare her gentle hands for future need.

On an October morning in 1827 a sixteen-year-old boy in plain clothes stood in a small square of Lower Provins, noting working mills and modest bourgeois houses. He found the white stone house he sought—recently restored, with gray blinds and neat balconies—the neatness of a retired petty shopkeeper. As sunlight lit a calico curtain, the boy broke into a Breton ditty that recalled Brittany’s bittersweet turns. Pierrette, a frail girl in a garret, answered him with a signal; he revealed a yellow broom-flower. He was Brigaut, a childhood companion who spoke of settling nearby. Pierrette, alarmed, urged him away when a grotesque old maid below—given to sharp observations and scandalous reading of harmless scenes—appeared.

Alone, Pierrette reflected on her precarious past: her mother, born a Demoiselle Auffray, came from a tangled family and a father who remarried, leaving a confused inheritance; her mother later married Major Lorrain, who died in battle and left them poor. The Lorrains could not care for Pierrette, and she was sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, in Provins. Unaware of the full intricacies and consequences of her lineage, Pierrette’s fate is increasingly shaped by these relatives, setting the stage for ensuing drama.

Père Rogron, an indulgent, ruddy, short, fat innkeeper in Provins, and his unattractive, selfish wife lived brutishly on modest earnings. After selling the inn they moved into a dilapidated house inherited from old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother. Their children, Sylvie and Jérôme-Denis, were neglected, ill-bred, and coarse-voiced; Rogron sent them to Paris under a doctrine of harsh, minimal support.

Sylvie apprenticed on rue Saint-Denis and rose to forewoman before becoming independent; Jérôme became a head-clerk. They pooled savings to buy a retail shop, the “Family Sister.” Though modestly successful, fierce competition and their severity made them notorious for stinginess and harsh treatment, causing high clerk turnover. Sylvie, shrewd but plain, displayed a pleasing front to customers and a sour private persona. While mourning their father they received news of cousin Pierrette’s inheritance; intent on preserving their wealth, they refused to take her in, delayed answering, and later left for Provins four years before Brigaut’s arrival.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A petty shopkeeper returns from Paris; his Parisian airs quickly fade. The Rogrons use new wealth to pursue bourgeois status—remodeling their house to match the wealthy and dabbling in politics. Their renovations advertise ambition as they vie for the town’s elite. Madame Tiphaine advances her husband by hosting gatherings; the Rogrons lack polish—Sylvie learns card games while Rogron remains awkward. Lavish dinners fail; invitations stop and ridicule spreads. Sylvie’s faith that money buys status collapses; she grows frugal, and they sink into ennui and resentment for their old trade. Bringing cousin Pierrette worsens their neglect and ostracism. Bitter, they court the lower bourgeoisie, exploited by opportunists like Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet. The story shows provincial hierarchies and social codes trump wealth in determining influence.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette—ashamed to live on charity—felt relieved when wealthy relatives were found. Childhood friend Brigaut, the son of her mother’s friend, gave her sixty francs for the trip to Paris; she accepted without thanks, showing their closeness. He often visited and ran after the coach in tears. Penniless in Paris, a conductor briefly paid for her hotel; a kinder one took her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s, where her cousins received her with astonishment.

She arrived in Breton dress with a small bundle; Sylvie and Rogron were startled. Pierrette, shy and overwhelmed, spoke little. Sylvie and the maid Adèle showed her a modest room that felt luxurious, then immediately fretted over her lack of clothes and resolved to buy a wardrobe.

In Brittany she had known affection and freedom; with the Rogrons she found coldness, criticism, and obsession with appearances. Reproved for dirty shoes and treated as a burden, she suppressed joy and grew passive. Sylvie forbade visits and pursued social ambitions with Vinet and the colonel. As her cousins plotted for status, Pierrette was confined and transformed from a lively child into a shadow, enduring harsh, self‑interested care rather than love.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At the start of a new phase with her wealthy cousins, Pierrette sought a goodnight kiss but Sylvie turned away coldly, making her cry. When asked why, Pierrette said her rich cousin was not as kind as her poor grandmother; Sylvie retorted the grandmother had taken money while the cousin would leave her some—Pierrette wanted affection, not wealth.

Sylvie then accused Pierrette of mischief—breaking a watch, disobeying orders—and Pierrette left ashamed. Rogron demanded punishment, Madame Vinet urged schooling, and the colonel thought instruction might quiet her. A writing master was hired; ink stains and scattered materials were blamed on Pierrette and she was harshly told to earn her keep. Rogron’s severity and Sylvie’s indifference deepened her isolation; curiosity stifled, she grew ill and her complaints were dismissed.

Only Adèle secretly showed kindness. Plans to escape to grandparents in Brittany ended after a death and Rogron’s appointment as guardian; he secured a promise of eight thousand francs from Pierrette’s grandmother. Preparations for her first communion brought Monsieur Habert, whose fervent instruction gave Pierrette solace and led her to abandon escape plans and embrace faith. The Rogrons refused a convent school; later, introductions to the Chargeboeufs and Bathilde’s interest in Rogron prompted Sylvie to make Pierrette a servant. Without Adèle’s protection, Pierrette faded from a vibrant child into a silent shadow while her cousins remained oblivious.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut's arrival would incite, it is necessary to explain how he came to Provins, since he plays a significant yet passive role.

Brigaut fled the Rogron house, shaken by Pierrette’s alarming decline from lively girl to one consumed by illness and sorrow. From the Upper town he watched her home, torn by concern and his own feelings. Hoping to better himself for her, he briefly went to Paris to learn a trade, returned after a fortnight, and found work as a journeyman cabinetmaker so he could stay near Pierrette. He suppressed his feelings while trying to learn more about the Rogrons; Madame Frappier disclosed their dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. He often saw the frail Pierrette at the market and near the church, where she signaled him to remain hidden, fearful of some danger.

Pierrette fretted over changes since Brigaut’s return but did not perceive the Rogrons’ internal strife—chiefly between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste), rivals for advantageous marriages. Sylvie, forty-two, feared aging alone; Céleste, about ten years younger and urged on by her priest brother, actively sought a husband. A political shift encouraged local Liberals and sharpened social competition. Sylvie, alarmed, consulted a doctor whose discouraging prognosis deepened her anxiety; Céleste saw opportunity and urged delay to eliminate a rival.

Vinet, a lawyer eyeing Bathilde’s advantage, manipulated the colonel into courting Pierrette while secretly scheming to divert the Rogron fortune. The colonel’s attentions inflamed Sylvie, who—after overhearing Brigaut serenade Pierrette—mistakenly suspected the colonel, accused Pierrette, and threatened her when she remained silent. Under heavy labor, emotional strain, and relentless scrutiny, Pierrette’s health waned. Brigaut offered brief comfort, but love, jealousy, and ambition became entangled, setting the stage for the tragic events to come.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the Constitutionnel to Gouraud, who now called himself and lingered to gossip and walk with Sylvie. Eager to see the colonel, Sylvie dressed her best; when Rogron excused himself, she was left alone with Gouraud.

Noting her solemnity, he asked after her cousin’s health. Gouraud was uneasy: Vinet’s rising influence over the Rogrons threatened to sideline him. As a half-pay officer he feared Vinet’s control of their plans, Rogron’s infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, and even suspected Vinet might be steering Rogron toward Pierrette to benefit Vinet and Bathilde.

When Sylvie tentatively mentioned Pierrette, Gouraud denied any attachment, preferring a more mature wife. Relieved, Sylvie concealed her aims and, sensing her interest, Gouraud declared he would marry her. She began planning but remained wary of Pierrette.

At dinner Sylvie chastised Pierrette for a stain yet dressed her nicely, suspecting collusion. Bathilde arrived resplendent; guided by Vinet she treated marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone. Tensions rose—Bathilde’s condescension and Vinet’s remarks sharpened rivalries. At cards Pierrette’s innocent remarks drove Sylvie to anger; Pierrette left in tears. That night Sylvie brooded on men’s duplicity. The next day Pierrette’s painful fall met Sylvie’s blame, revealing a widening rift.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The week ended in torment for Pierrette under Sylvie’s escalating cruelty. Brigaut, whose arrival Sylvie hid, sustained Pierrette with memory and arranged secret letters: one morning he slipped a note while pretending to buy fruit and asked her to lower a string from her window so they could exchange notes nightly.

Pierrette received his ardent plea to escape and, despite difficulty obtaining writing materials without waking Sylvie, replied at midnight. Their correspondence brimmed with hope and plans, which only increased Sylvie’s jealousy. Under closer watch Pierrette’s health declined; she fainted one evening, alarming the colonel and others while Sylvie dismissed it as a ruse. When Pierrette cried for help and Sylvie tried to seize the letters, a violent struggle ensued and Pierrette was hurt, including an injured hand.

At Sylvie’s height of fury, Madame Lorrain—urged by Brigaut’s urgent appeals to come to Provins—arrived, found Pierrette traumatized, sought medical care, and vowed to secure her safety. As Pierrette recovered, her bond with her grandmother and hope for a life with Brigaut renewed her determination to reclaim freedom and love.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At nine o’clock Monsieur Martener told Monsieur Tiphaine about Pierrette’s mistreatment by the Rogrons. Tiphaine brought in Auffray, a notary and relative; longstanding enmities between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions—and damaging truths about Madame Tiphaine’s family—gave the dispute political charge. Seeking to weaken the Rogrons, influential royalist opponents, Tiphaine consulted the public prosecutor. He, Auffray, and Martener arranged to petition the courts to make Auffray Pierrette’s guardian so a Family Council, backed by medical testimony, could remove her from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday the town buzzed after neighbors heard Pierrette’s cries. Doctors Bianchon and Brigaut examined her and confirmed abuse. Vinet, the Rogrons’ lawyer, warned of criminal exposure; Sylvie, initially evasive, later admitted jealousy and the truth. Vinet proposed a marriage announcement to quiet the scandal; under pressure the Rogrons signed a contract that served their political interests. He then filed a petition accusing Brigaut and the widow Lorrain of abducting Pierrette, recasting his clients as victims.

The hearing was postponed and interest grew. Pierrette, cared for by her grandmother and Brigaut, was condemned by the physicians. When the court reconvened, Auffray’s medical report led the judge to bar the Rogrons from her until the Family Council met. Relatives voted to remove Rogron as guardian and appoint Auffray; a civil suit for neglect and abuse followed.

Pierrette’s health declined. Martener and the Auffray family grew fond of her; a March 1828 operation offered little hope. She forgave her cousins and soon died. Her crowded funeral saw the Rogrons absent; Brigaut made her coffin. Though community support impeded the Rogrons and Vinet suffered backlash, they largely retained political standing. Only a few—Martener, Brigaut, and like‑minded friends—remembered the reality of Pierrette’s suffering. The account closes on questions of justice, accountability, and her lasting impact.
