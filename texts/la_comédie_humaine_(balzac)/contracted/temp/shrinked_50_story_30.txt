Honoré De Balzac dedicates "Pierrette" to Mademoiselle Anna Hanska, highlighting the story's melancholy and the necessity of confronting sorrows for loved ones.

In October 1827, sixteen-year-old Jacques Brigaut admires Pierrette Lorrain's house in Lower Provins and wishes to settle nearby. Pierrette, however, urges him to flee when a noise alarms her. Alone, she reflects on her sorrowful life since her father's death and her mother's financial struggles after marrying a physician. Living with her guardians, the Rogrons, Pierrette becomes dependent on them.

The narrative explores themes of social class, familial duty, and financial struggles affecting relationships. Pierrette's fragile health and emotional turmoil reflect broader societal issues for women in precarious situations. The self-interested Rogrons complicate her life, illustrating the conflict between personal aspirations and societal constraints.

Pere Rogron, a selfish innkeeper, and his unattractive wife live modestly. After selling their inn, they move into a dilapidated house inherited from Pierrette’s grandfather. Their poorly raised children, Sylvie and Jerome-Denis, become socially inept and miserly. When the Lorrains seek to care for Pierrette, the Rogrons refuse, viewing her as a threat to their inheritance.

Sylvie invites Pierrette to live with them to regain social standing, but their plans are complicated by Pierrette's circumstances. After her grandparents are hospitalized, she feels burdened by charity until she discovers wealthy relatives. Her childhood friend Brigaut offers her money for her journey to Paris, emphasizing their bond. Upon arrival, Pierrette faces indifference from her cousins, who prioritize financial concerns over her well-being, leading to feelings of inadequacy and declining health under the Rogrons' neglect.

Jacques Brigaut's return to Provins disrupts the Rogron family, particularly affecting Pierrette, who has become frail under her cousin Sylvie's oppressive care. Concerned for Pierrette's well-being, Brigaut, a journeyman cabinet-maker, is determined to improve himself for her sake. He learns of the Rogrons' dubious acquisition of Pierrette's grandfather's property and observes her unhappiness.

Sylvie's jealousy intensifies with Brigaut's presence, fearing a romantic connection between Pierrette and Colonel Gouraud, whom she desires. This jealousy manifests in her treatment of Pierrette, whom she accuses of having a secret admirer. Pierrette, innocent and fearful, remains silent, worsening her fragile health. Sylvie's irrational jealousy culminates in a breakfast confrontation, isolating Pierrette further.

As Colonel Gouraud's visits dwindle, Sylvie manipulates Pierrette into dressing nicely, suspecting the colonel's interest. After a card game leads to a confrontation, Pierrette flees and injures herself, which Sylvie cruelly attributes to divine punishment. 

Pierrette endures torment while secretly communicating with Brigaut, who devises a plan to connect with her. When Pierrette faints, Sylvie dismisses Gouraud's concern, and tensions escalate when Sylvie discovers Pierrette's letters, leading to a violent confrontation. Pierrette's grandmother vows to seek justice against the Rogrons, but despite community efforts, Pierrette's health deteriorates, and she tragically passes away, prompting Brigaut to seek justice amid societal indifference.
