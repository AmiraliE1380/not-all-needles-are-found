Honoré De Balzac dedicates "Pierrette" to Mademoiselle Anna Hanska, highlighting the story's melancholy and the necessity of confronting sorrows for loved ones.

In October 1827, sixteen-year-old Jacques Brigaut admires Pierrette Lorrain's renovated house in Lower Provins and sings a Breton wedding song, expressing his desire to settle nearby. However, Pierrette urges him to flee when a noise alarms her.

Alone, Pierrette reflects on her life, marked by three years of sorrow since her father, Major Lorrain, died. Her mother faced financial struggles after marrying a physician, leading to further ruin. Pierrette was sent to live with the Rogrons in Provins, who became her guardians after receiving a letter from her grandparents, shaping her future and making her dependent on them.

The narrative explores themes of social class, familial duty, and the impact of financial struggles on relationships. Pierrette's delicate health and emotional turmoil reflect broader societal issues, particularly for women in precarious situations. The story foreshadows the challenges she will face with the self-interested Rogrons, examining the intersection of personal aspirations and societal constraints.

Pere Rogron, an unattractive and selfish innkeeper, lives with his equally unappealing wife on an annual income of about two thousand francs. After selling their inn, they move into a dilapidated house inherited from Pierrette’s grandfather. Their poorly raised children, Sylvie and Jerome-Denis, struggle socially, becoming miserly despite saving enough to buy a retail establishment called the “Family Sister.”

Sylvie manages the business effectively, while Jerome struggles with broader trade aspects. Their lives revolve around the shop, leaving them oblivious to the outside world. When the Lorrains seek to place Pierrette in their care, the siblings refuse, viewing her as a threat to their inheritance.

A provincial shopkeeper returning from Paris brings new ideas but soon succumbs to provincial life, transitioning from shopkeeper to bourgeois. The Rogrons remodel their old house to emulate wealthier locals, consuming their first year back in discussions about their home and attempts to integrate into society. Despite their efforts, they are perceived as vulgar and unrefined, resulting in social ostracism.

In a bid to revitalize their household, Sylvie invites Pierrette to live with them, hoping to regain social standing. However, their plans are complicated by Pierrette's unfortunate circumstances and the Rogrons' past misdeeds regarding family property. After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette felt burdened by charity until she discovered wealthy relatives. Her childhood friend Brigaut, an apprentice cabinet-maker, offered her money for her journey to Paris, which she accepted.

Upon arriving at her cousins’ home, Pierrette was met with indifference and criticism regarding her clothing. As she adjusted to her new life, she faced constant scrutiny from her cousins, who prioritized their financial investment in her clothing over her happiness. The arrival of Vinet and Colonel Gouraud, who sought to manipulate the Rogrons, deepened their resentment and neglect towards her.

As Pierrette's life in Provins progressed, she became increasingly confined and controlled, losing the joy she once enjoyed. During a family gathering, she is coldly dismissed by Sylvie, contrasting her longing for affection with her guardians' indifference. The Rogrons impose strict educational expectations, leading to feelings of inadequacy and despair. Despite having a writing master, Pierrette struggles academically and is often punished for her perceived failures.

Over time, her health declines under the oppressive atmosphere of the Rogron household. The relentless criticism and absence of affection contribute to her deterioration. The narrative starkly contrasts Pierrette's innocent nature with her relatives' coldness, highlighting the harsh realities faced by those dependent on wealthier family members. As Pierrette approaches her first communion, she finds solace in her faith, but the Rogrons remain indifferent to her spiritual growth, prioritizing their ambitions over her well-being.

To understand the turmoil in the Rogron family after Jacques Brigaut's arrival, we must first consider his journey to Provins. Troubled by Pierrette's distress, Brigaut fled her home, shaken by her transformation from a lively child to a sickly shadow. He climbed to a vantage point to observe her house, consumed with worry and realizing his deep feelings for her.

At sixteen, Brigaut was determined to improve himself for Pierrette's sake. After a brief stint in Paris, he returned to Provins and became a journeyman with Monsieur Frappier, the town's best cabinet-maker. His dedication to his trade and desire to protect Pierrette drove him to work hard, earning a modest living while keeping a watchful eye on her.

As Brigaut settled into his new life, he learned about the Rogrons' dubious acquisition of Pierrette's grandfather's property and the oppressive nature of her living situation. He often saw Pierrette in the market, where she appeared burdened and unhappy. Despite his longing to approach her, he sensed her distress and chose to keep his distance.

Meanwhile, Pierrette faced her cousin Sylvie's jealousy, which intensified after Brigaut's arrival. Sylvie, fixated on marrying Colonel Gouraud, grew envious of Pierrette, perceiving a potential romantic connection between her and the colonel. Sylvie's insecurities led her to consult a doctor regarding marriage risks, complicating her feelings further.

The dynamics within the Rogron household were tense. Sylvie's jealousy manifested in her treatment of Pierrette, whom she accused of having a secret admirer. Pierrette, innocent and unaware, remained silent, fearing repercussions. Sylvie's relentless scrutiny exacerbated Pierrette's fragile health.

As the story unfolds, Sylvie's jealousy becomes increasingly irrational, leading her to plot against Pierrette. The tension culminates in a breakfast scene where accusations reach a breaking point, leaving Pierrette feeling isolated and ill.

Colonel Gouraud ceased delivering the “Constitutionnel” to the Rogrons for a month, opting for brief visits instead. Sylvie, eager for his attention, dressed attractively and awaited his arrival. Gouraud felt uneasy about his partnership with Vinet, who seemed to dominate their political efforts. To gauge Rogron's feelings, Gouraud mentioned his interest in Bathilde, provoking jealousy from Rogron.

Sylvie's jealousy intensified as she noticed Pierrette's growing beauty and began plotting against her cousin. She sent Pierrette to dress nicely for dinner, suspecting the colonel's interest in her. Tensions rose during an evening card game, with Sylvie's jealousy flaring when she believed Pierrette had helped Gouraud win. After a confrontation, Pierrette fled, injuring her head. Sylvie cruelly attributed Pierrette's injury to divine punishment for disobedience, revealing her harshness and control over her cousin.

Pierrette suffered a week of torment from Sylvie, enduring physical and emotional pain while keeping Brigaut's arrival a secret for his safety. Thoughts of Brigaut provided her solace, reminiscent of an exile longing for home. Brigaut, aware of her struggles, devised a plan to communicate by having her lower a letter from her window at midnight. Their mutual longing allowed them to connect, and during a market visit, Brigaut slipped her a note expressing his love and urging her to escape her oppressive situation.

Moved by his words, Pierrette wrote back, revealing her suffering and desire for freedom. However, Sylvie grew suspicious of Pierrette's newfound happiness and began monitoring her closely. When Pierrette fainted at a gathering, the concerned colonel was dismissed by Sylvie. Tensions escalated when Sylvie discovered Pierrette's letters and confronted her, leading to a violent struggle that left Pierrette injured. Just then, Pierrette's grandmother arrived, alerted by Brigaut, and took her away from the oppressive environment.

Pierrette's grandmother vowed to seek medical help and take action against the Rogrons. Meanwhile, Brigaut sought a doctor for her recovery. As Pierrette recuperated, her grandmother promised a better life, emphasizing themes of love and resilience. Monsieur Martener visited Monsieur Tiphaine to address Pierrette's mistreatment, intensifying the feud between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions. They strategized to legally remove Pierrette from the Rogrons' guardianship, inciting public outrage.

As tensions rose, Vinet manipulated the Rogrons into a marriage contract to distract from potential criminal charges. Pierrette was moved to a safer environment under Auffray's care, where doctors confirmed her injuries were due to abuse. A court ruling prevented her return to the Rogrons, but her health deteriorated. Tragically, she passed away, leading to a public funeral that the Rogrons avoided. Brigaut, devastated, vowed to seek justice, while Vinet exploited the situation for political gain, critiquing societal indifference.
