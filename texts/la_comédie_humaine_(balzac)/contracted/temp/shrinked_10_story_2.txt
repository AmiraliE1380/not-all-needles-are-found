**THE BALL AT SCEAUX**

**BY HONORE DE BALZAC**

**Translated By Clara Bell**

To Henri de Balzac, his brother Honore.

**THE BALL AT SCEAUX**

The Comte de Fontaine, head of an ancient Poitou family, had served the Bourbon cause valiantly during the La Vendee war against the Republic. He often jested, “I am one of the men who gave themselves to be killed on the steps of the throne,” a remark grounded in truth, having survived the bloody battle of Les Quatre Chemins. Despite his financial ruin from confiscation, he rejected lucrative offers from Napoleon, remaining steadfast in his aristocratic beliefs. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet, a woman from an old Brittany family but without fortune.

When the second revolution struck, the Comte, burdened with a large family, reluctantly left his estate for Paris at his wife's urging. Disheartened by the greed of former comrades seeking positions under the new Constitution, he considered returning home until a ministerial dispatch announced his appointment as marechal de camp, allowing him to count the twenty years of Louis XVIII’s reign as service. Shortly after, he received the crosses of the Legion of Honor and of Saint-Louis without solicitation.

These honors stirred his ambition, leading him to seek a private audience with the King. However, the royal drawing-room was crowded with old supporters, and while he met some friends, the princes he admired did not inquire about his losses or contributions to the royal cause. He realized too late that he had fought at his own expense. Attempting a witty remark about his financial situation, he was met with royal pleasantries that hinted at the long-standing debts owed to the crown. Disheartened, he left the Tuileries, lamenting the changes at court and the inability to recover his expenses.

His requests for an audience went unanswered, and he observed the rise of upstarts in positions once reserved for nobility. In despair, he prepared to return to his estate, abandoning hopes of repayment. However, the events of March 20, 1815, heralded a new storm for the monarchy. The Comte, like a loyal servant in a storm, borrowed against his lands to support the monarchy, uncertain if this would yield better results than his previous devotion. He joined the King’s exiled companions, hoping for greater favor than those who had protested against the republic.

During the royal exile, he served Louis XVIII, showcasing his loyalty and political honesty. One evening, the King recalled a witty remark from the Tuileries, and the Comte seized the opportunity to recount his story, impressing the King. His elegant writing style also caught the monarch's attention, securing his place in the King's memory as a loyal servant.

Upon the second restoration, the Count became a special envoy with absolute jurisdiction over rebels, wielding his power judiciously. After his commission ended, he joined the Privy Council, becoming a deputy who listened more than he spoke, adapting his views significantly. His close relationship with the King led to a humorous acknowledgment of their mutual precariousness in their roles. The King appointed him as administrator of Crown lands, and his name frequently arose when commissions were formed, thanks to his tact and wit.

Through the King's favor, his eldest son secured a prominent legal position, while his second son rose through military ranks, and the youngest became a municipal director in Paris. These appointments, though discreet, provided the family with substantial incomes, yet the Count struggled to restore his family's fortunes due to the size of his household. His three sons were well-positioned, but he had three daughters to marry off, fearing to overextend the King's goodwill.

The Count decided to mention his daughters one at a time. The King arranged a marriage for the eldest with Receiver-General Planat de Baudry, and later found a husband for the second daughter, a wealthy magistrate whom he ennobled. When the Count mentioned his youngest daughter, Emilie, the King humorously remarked on the trio of daughters, leading to a less amiable relationship between them.

Emilie, the youngest, was spoiled and accustomed to luxury, having grown up in a privileged environment. At nineteen, she had yet to choose a suitor from the many young men her father introduced. Her beauty made her the center of attention, but her pride and disdain for those of lesser birth complicated her prospects. She was well-educated, talented in music and languages, and could engage in discussions on various subjects, dazzling those around her.

However, her arrogance and belief in her superiority alienated potential suitors. The Count, aware of her behavior, felt the weight of her expectations. Despite marrying off his elder daughters to men of wealth, he struggled to find a suitable match for Emilie, who dismissed suitors for trivial reasons. As the winter season approached, she immersed herself in social events, enjoying the attention while rejecting proposals, further complicating her father's efforts to secure her future.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Emilie de Fontaine was naturally suited to play the role of Celimene. Tall and slender, she could easily adopt a dignified or playful demeanor. Her long neck allowed her to express scorn or impertinence, while her fine black hair and arched eyebrows gave her an air of pride. She skillfully used her voice to charm or intimidate, and her alabaster complexion mirrored the calm surface of a lake, disturbed only by fleeting emotions. Many young men, victims of her disdain, accused her of being insincere, yet she managed to inspire their desire to please her, only to subject them to her whims. Among fashionable young women, Emilie excelled at maintaining an air of reserve around talented men, treating equals as inferiors and accepting homage rather than compliments.

Monsieur de Fontaine realized too late how his daughter's upbringing had been spoiled by the family's excessive devotion. The admiration she received from society inflated her pride and self-confidence, fostering a selfishness typical of pampered children. While her youthful charms masked these flaws, her father recognized them and attempted to impart wisdom, but his efforts were often met with her capriciousness and irony. He lamented that his affectionate advice seemed to slide off her heart like water off marble. It took him time to see that her rare displays of affection were condescending. Emilie, like many young girls from privileged backgrounds, believed her mother could not understand her youthful spirit and often felt jealous of others, including her siblings. She blamed her loneliness on fate, failing to realize that happiness comes from within. Despite her aversion to the marriages of her sisters, she envied their happiness and wealth, leading her mother to suspect she might be slightly mad.

Such pride is common among beautiful young women from high social standings, who often feel their mothers are jealous and wish to overshadow them. This leads to secret tears and revolts against perceived tyranny. They dream of a perfect life partner, often setting unrealistic standards for their future husbands. Emilie, in her youthful wisdom, had created a list of qualifications for her ideal suitor: he must be a peer of France, a soldier, and possess a noble demeanor. She also insisted on a slender physique, viewing corpulence as a sign of unworthiness in men. Her father feared that her eccentric standards would lead to ridicule, especially as time passed without a suitable match.

Monsieur de Fontaine, determined to secure a good match for Emilie, hosted lavish gatherings to attract potential suitors. His efforts, however, were met with mockery from some quarters, as he was seen as corrupting the integrity of the legislative body. Despite this, he gained popularity for his extravagant entertainments. After a winter of matchmaking, he felt it was time to discuss Emilie's future with her. One morning, he summoned her to address the matter seriously.

Emilie entered the room with a light-hearted demeanor, interrupting her father's serious tone. He expressed the importance of choosing a husband for her happiness, but she dismissed his concerns, claiming their agreement on her suitors was still in effect. Monsieur de Fontaine urged her to take the matter seriously, reminding her of the love and kindness shown by those trying to help her. Emilie, however, continued to mock the situation, displaying a lack of respect for her father's authority.

Monsieur de Fontaine, feeling the weight of his paternal duty, reminded Emilie of her age and the need for her to marry. He explained the financial strain her siblings' marriages had placed on the family, emphasizing that he could not promise her a large dowry. Emilie, dismissive of his concerns, continued to reject potential suitors, insisting on a noble title. She expressed disdain for those who did not meet her lofty standards, further frustrating her father.

Despite his efforts, Monsieur de Fontaine realized he could not force Emilie to change her mind. He expressed his resignation, stating that she was now in control of her own fate. He warned her that true happiness in marriage comes from mutual respect rather than wealth or status. Emilie, touched by her father's affection, feigned compliance while secretly determined to find a peer of France.

At a family gathering, Emilie declared her intention to avoid any discussions of marriage, asserting her independence. Her uncle, a vice-admiral, humorously suggested she was waiting for the Duc de Bordeaux to come of age, prompting laughter. Monsieur de Fontaine, weary of the ongoing struggle with his daughter's stubbornness, declared her the master of her own fate, leading to a collective sense of pity and curiosity among the family.

As summer approached, the family left Paris for their country house, where Emilie attended the "Bal de Sceaux," a popular social event. She initially sought to enjoy the festivities incognito, but soon found herself captivated by a striking young man who stood apart from the crowd. His elegance and demeanor drew her attention, and she became determined to learn more about him.

Despite her efforts to engage him, the young man remained aloof, focused on another dancer. Emilie, intrigued and frustrated, followed him discreetly, hoping to discover his identity. However, her attempts to connect were thwarted, and she began to despair of ever seeing him again.

One day, while riding with her uncle, Emilie encountered the young man again, but their meeting quickly escalated into a confrontation between her uncle and the stranger. The encounter left Emilie flustered, but she remained convinced of the young man's noble status. Her uncle, recognizing her interest, promised to introduce her to him, further fueling her hopes.

As the days passed, Emilie's infatuation grew, and she envisioned a future with the young man, unaware of the complexities and challenges that love and marriage would bring. Her youthful dreams and expectations set the stage for a tumultuous journey ahead, as she navigated the intricacies of society and her own desires.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The next morning, before Emilie awoke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. Spotting the young man he had insulted the previous day in the courtyard of a charming villa, he approached him with the politeness characteristic of the old court.

“Who would have thought I’d encounter the son or grandson of one of my best friends at seventy-three? I’m a vice-admiral, and I think no more of dueling than smoking a cigar. In my day, young men had to see the color of their blood before becoming friends! But last night, sailor-like, I had too much grog and ran you down. Let’s shake hands; I’d rather take a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than cause his family any regret.”

Despite his initial coldness, the young man couldn’t resist the Count’s cordiality and shook his hand.

“You were going out riding,” said the Count. “Don’t let me detain you. But if you have no other plans, please join us for dinner at Villa Planat. My nephew, the Comte de Fontaine, is someone you must meet. I also plan to make up for my clumsiness by introducing you to five of the prettiest women in Paris. Your brow is clearing! I enjoy seeing young people happy; it reminds me of my youth, filled with adventures and duels. Nowadays, you worry over everything as if there had never been a fifteenth or sixteenth century.”

“But, monsieur, aren’t we right to think this way? The sixteenth century brought religious liberty to Europe, and the nineteenth will bring political liberty—”

“Oh, let’s not discuss politics. I’m an ultra, you see. But I don’t stop young men from being revolutionary, as long as they let the King disperse their assemblies.”

As they rode deeper into the woods, the Count pointed out a slender birch sapling, pulled up his horse, and shot a bullet into the tree from fifteen paces away.

“You see, my dear fellow, I’m not afraid of a duel,” he said with comical gravity.

“Nor am I,” replied the young man, promptly cocking his pistol and aiming at the hole made by the Count’s bullet, landing his shot close by.

“That’s what I call a well-educated man!” exclaimed the admiral enthusiastically.

During their ride, the Count seized every opportunity to quiz the young man on the trivial knowledge he believed constituted an accomplished gentleman.

“Do you have any debts?” he finally asked.

“No, monsieur.”

“What, you pay for everything?”

“Punctually; otherwise, we’d lose our credit and respect.”

“But surely you have more than one mistress? Ah, you blush! Manners have changed. All these notions of lawful order and liberty have spoiled young men. You have no Guimard, no Duthe, no creditors—and you know nothing of heraldry. My dear young friend, you’re not fully fledged. A man who doesn’t sow his wild oats in spring will do so in winter. If I have eighty thousand francs a year at seventy, it’s because I squandered my capital at thirty—with my wife, decently and honorably. However, your imperfections won’t stop me from introducing you at Pavillon Planat. Remember, you promised to come, and I expect you.”

“What an odd little old man!” thought Longueville. “He’s so jolly and hale; though he wants to seem friendly, I won’t trust him too far.”

The next day, around four o’clock, when the house party was scattered in the drawing-rooms and billiard room, a servant announced, “Monsieur de Longueville.” Upon hearing the name of the old admiral’s protégé, everyone rushed in, eager to study Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s expression and judge this remarkable young man who had earned honorable mention at the expense of many rivals. Dressed simply yet elegantly, he exuded ease, politeness, and a pleasant voice that resonated with the family. He appeared accustomed to the luxury of the Receiver-General’s mansion. His worldly conversation revealed a brilliant education and extensive knowledge, particularly evident in a discussion on naval architecture initiated by the old admiral, prompting one lady to remark that he must have attended the Ecole Polytechnique.

“I think, madame,” he replied, “that I may regard it as an honor to have been accepted.”

Despite persistent invitations, he politely but firmly declined to stay for dinner, explaining that he was the Hippocrates of his young sister, whose delicate health required great care.

“Monsieur is perhaps a medical man?” asked one of Emilie’s sisters-in-law, with irony.

“Monsieur has left the Ecole Polytechnique,” Mademoiselle de Fontaine interjected, her face flushing as she learned that the young lady at the ball was Monsieur Longueville’s sister.

“But, my dear, he may be a doctor and still have attended the Ecole Polytechnique—correct, monsieur?”

“There’s nothing to prevent it, madame,” he replied.

All eyes turned to Emilie, who gazed with uneasy curiosity at the captivating stranger. She breathed easier when he added, smiling, “I do not belong to the medical profession; I even gave up joining the Engineers to preserve my independence.”

“And you did well,” said the Count. “But how can you regard it as an honor to be a doctor?” asked the Breton nobleman. “Ah, my young friend, such a man as you—”

“Monsieur le Comte, I respect every profession that serves a useful purpose.”

“Well, in that we agree. You respect those professions, I imagine, as a young man respects a dowager.”

Monsieur Longueville made his visit neither too long nor too short, leaving when he sensed he had pleased everyone and piqued their curiosity.

“He’s a cunning rascal!” said the Count, returning to the drawing-room after seeing him out.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine, who had dressed carefully to attract the young man’s attention, felt disappointed when he didn’t give her the attention she thought she deserved. The family was surprised by her sudden silence; Emilie usually showcased her charms for newcomers with witty banter and expressive gestures. Whether it was the young man’s captivating voice and manners that enchanted her, or if she was genuinely in love, her demeanor shifted to simplicity and naturalness, making her appear even more beautiful. Some family members interpreted this change as a strategic move to dazzle him later. Everyone was curious about Emilie’s thoughts on the stranger, but during dinner, while others speculated on Monsieur Longueville’s qualities, she remained silent. A sarcastic remark from her uncle jolted her from her reverie, prompting her to quip that such heavenly perfection must conceal a significant flaw, and she would be cautious in her judgment of such a gifted man.

“Those who please everybody, please nobody,” she added, “and the worst fault is to have none.”

Like many girls in love, Emilie hoped to hide her feelings, but by the end of two weeks, every family member was aware of her secret. When Monsieur Longueville visited for the third time, Emilie believed it was primarily for her sake. This realization brought her intoxicating pleasure, but also a painful blow to her pride. Accustomed to being the center of attention, she now recognized a force outside herself that attracted her. She tried to resist but couldn’t shake the captivating image of the young man from her heart.

Anxiety soon followed. Two of Monsieur Longueville’s qualities—unexpected modesty and discretion—were particularly vexing to Emilie. He never spoke of himself, his pursuits, or his family. The hints she dropped in conversation and the traps she set to extract information from him were deftly evaded. If she discussed painting, he responded as a connoisseur; if she played music, he demonstrated his skill as a pianist. One evening, he delighted the family by joining Emilie in a duet of Cimarosa’s charming pieces. However, when they tried to discern whether he was a professional singer, he skillfully maintained the mystery surrounding his social status, allowing him to remain the “handsome stranger” at the Villa, as curiosity never breached the bounds of good breeding.

Distracted by his reserve, Emilie hoped to gain insights from Clara, his sister. With her uncle’s help, she sought to bring Mademoiselle Clara Longueville into the spotlight. The family expressed a strong desire to meet the amiable young lady and proposed an informal dance. The ladies hoped to draw Clara out, believing she would reveal secrets.

Despite the clouds of suspicion and curiosity, joy illuminated Emilie’s soul, as she found life delightful when connected to someone beyond herself. She began to understand life’s relationships. Whether happiness made her better or she was too occupied to torment others, she became gentler and more indulgent. This transformation enchanted her family, suggesting that her selfishness was evolving into love. She eagerly anticipated the arrival of her bashful admirer. Though they hadn’t exchanged words of passion, she sensed his affection and artfully led him to share his varied knowledge. She realized he was also studying her, prompting her to remedy the flaws her education had instilled. This was her first homage to love and a bitter reproach to herself. She desired to please, and in doing so, she became enchanting; she loved, and he idolized her. Her family, aware of her pride, allowed her enough freedom to enjoy the innocent delights of first love. They often walked together in the garden, where nature resembled a woman preparing for a ball. They shared aimless conversations, admiring sunsets and gathering daisies, singing passionate duets to express their secrets.

The day of the dance arrived. Clara Longueville and her brother, whom the servants insisted on addressing with the noble “de,” were the principal guests. For the first time, Emilie felt genuine pleasure in another young girl’s triumph. She sincerely lavished attention on Clara, wanting to discover secrets. However, Clara displayed more wit than her brother, steering conversations away from personal interests while maintaining charm, provoking a hint of envy in Emilie, who dubbed her “the Siren.” Clara’s modesty and candidness prohibited any suspicion of deceit, and Emilie found herself revealing her character in responses Clara elicited. At one point, Emilie regretted a poorly judged comment about commoners that Clara had prompted.

“Mademoiselle,” Clara said sweetly, “I’ve heard so much about you from Maximilien that I was eager to meet you out of affection for him. But isn’t wanting to know you akin to wanting to love you?”

“My dear Clara, I feared I might have displeased you by speaking of those not of noble birth.”

“Oh, be at ease. Such discussions are pointless today. It doesn’t affect me; I’m beside the question.”

Though ambitious, Clara’s answer filled Emilie with joy, as she interpreted it in a way that aligned with her wishes. She danced with renewed spirit, watching Longueville, whose figure and grace surpassed her ideal. She felt satisfaction in believing him well-born, her eyes sparkling as she danced with the joy that comes from being near the one she loved. The couple understood each other better than ever, often feeling their fingertips tingle as they danced.

As early autumn enveloped the couple in country festivities, they surrendered to the tide of sweet sentiment, strengthening their bond through countless little incidents. They studied each other as lovers do.

“Well, well; a flirtation never turned so quickly into a love match,” remarked the old uncle, observing the two young people like a naturalist studying an insect.

This comment alarmed Monsieur and Madame Fontaine, who had grown less indifferent to their daughter’s prospects. The old Vendeen, uneasy about the mystery surrounding Longueville, traveled to Paris for information but found none. Concerned, he warned Emilie to behave prudently. His fatherly admonition was met with mock submission and irony.

“At least, my dear Emilie, if you love him, don’t confess it to him.”

“My dear father, I certainly do love him; but I will await your permission before I tell him so.”

“But remember, Emilie, you know nothing of his family or pursuits.”

“I may be ignorant, but I’m content to be. But, father, you wished to see me married; you left me free to choose; my choice is irrevocably made—what more is needed?”

“It’s necessary to ascertain whether your choice is the son of a peer of France,” the venerable gentleman retorted sarcastically.

Emilie fell silent for a moment, then raised her head, looking at her father anxiously. “Aren’t the Longuevilles—?”

“They became extinct with the old Duc de Rostein-Limbourg, who perished on the scaffold in 1793. He was the last representative of the last and younger branch.”

“But, papa, some good families descend from bastards. The history of France is full of princes bearing the bar sinister on their shields.”

“Your ideas have changed,” the old man smiled.

The following day marked the last the Fontaine family would spend at Pavillon Planat. Disturbed by her father’s warning, Emilie awaited Longueville’s usual visit to extract an explanation. After dinner, she walked alone through the shrubbery toward a lover’s arbor, contemplating how to discover such crucial information without compromising herself—a challenging task! Until now, no direct confession had validated the feelings binding her to this stranger. Like Maximilien, she had secretly enjoyed the sweetness of first love, but both were too proud to admit it.

Maximilien Longueville, informed by Clara of her suspicions regarding Emilie’s character, was torn between youthful passion and the desire to understand the woman to whom he would entrust his happiness. His love didn’t blind him to Emilie’s prejudices, but he wanted to be sure of her feelings before risking his own. Thus, he maintained a silence contradicted by his looks and actions.

On her side, Emilie’s self-respect, heightened by her vanity rooted in birth and beauty, prevented her from reciprocating the declaration she sometimes felt compelled to invite. The lovers instinctively understood their situation without articulating their motives. There are moments in life when such ambiguity pleases youthful minds. Because each had postponed speaking too long, they engaged in a cruel game of suspense. He sought to discover whether he was loved, while she hoped he would break his respectful silence.

Seated on a rustic bench, Emilie reflected on the enchantment of the past three months. Her father’s suspicions held no weight; she dismissed them with a few reflections typical of an inexperienced girl, which seemed conclusive to her. Above all, she was convinced she could not deceive herself. Throughout the summer, she had detected no gesture or word from Maximilien indicating a vulgar origin or occupation; his manner of discussing matters revealed a man devoted to the nation’s highest interests. “Besides,” she thought, “a clerk, banker, or merchant wouldn’t have the leisure to spend an entire season courting me in the woods and fields, wasting time like a nobleman with a carefree life ahead.”

Lost in more interesting thoughts, she was startled by a rustling in the leaves, signaling that Maximilien had been watching her for a moment, likely with admiration.

“Do you know it’s very wrong to catch a young girl unawares?” she asked, smiling.

“Especially when they’re busy with their secrets,” replied Maximilien playfully.

“Why shouldn’t I have my secrets? You certainly have yours.”

“Then you were really thinking of your secrets?” he teased.

“No, I was thinking of yours. Mine, I know.”

“But perhaps my secrets are yours, and yours mine,” he said softly, taking her hand and drawing it through his arm.

After walking a few steps under a cluster of trees wrapped in the hues of the setting sun, the moment took on a solemnity. The young man’s eager actions and the pounding of his heart, felt through Emilie’s arm, stirred emotions that were all the more intense due to their innocence. The restraint imposed on upper-class young women amplifies any emotional outburst, and encountering an impassioned lover is one of their greatest dangers. Never had Emilie and Maximilien’s eyes conveyed so much without words. Overcome by this intoxication, they forgot their pride and suspicions, expressing themselves through hand squeezes that conveyed their joyful thoughts.

After a few moments of silence, Emilie spoke. “Monsieur, I have a question to ask you,” she said, trembling. “But remember, I beg you, it’s compulsory due to my family’s peculiar position.”

A terrible pause followed her stammered words. During that minute, the proud girl dared not meet the intense gaze of the man she loved, aware of the meanness in her next words: “Are you of noble birth?”

As soon as she spoke, she wished she could disappear.

“Mademoiselle,” Longueville replied gravely, his face taking on a stern dignity, “I promise to answer you truthfully once you answer a question I will pose to you!” He released her arm, and she suddenly felt alone in the world as he asked, “What is your reason for questioning me about my birth?”

She stood motionless, cold, and speechless.

“Mademoiselle,” Maximilien continued, “let’s not proceed if we don’t understand each other. I love you,” he declared, his voice filled with deep emotion. “Well, then,” he added, hearing her joyful exclamation, “why ask me if I am of noble birth?”

“Could he speak so if he were not?” a voice within her cried, which Emilie believed came from her heart. She raised her head gracefully, finding new life in his gaze, and extended her hand as if to renew their bond.

“You thought I cared very much for titles?” she said with playful archness.

“I have no titles to offer my wife,” he replied, half-jokingly, half-seriously. “But if I choose one of high rank, among women accustomed to the luxuries of wealth, I know what such a choice requires. Love gives everything,” he added lightly, “but only to lovers. Once married, they need something more than the sky above and the meadow below.”

“He is rich,” she reflected. “As for titles, perhaps he only wants to test me. He’s been told I’m obsessed with titles and will marry only a peer’s son. My priggish sisters have played that trick on me.” “I assure you, monsieur,” she said aloud, “I’ve had extravagant ideas about life and the world; but now,” she added pointedly, looking at him distractingly, “I know where true riches lie for a wife.”

“I must believe you’re speaking from the depths of your heart,” he said with gentle gravity. “But this winter, my dear Emilie, in less than two months, I may be proud of what I can offer you if you desire the pleasures of wealth. This is the only secret I’ll keep locked here,” he said, placing his hand on his heart, “for its success depends on my happiness. I dare not say ours.”

“Yes, yes, ours!”

Exchanging sweet nothings, they slowly returned to the company. Mademoiselle de Fontaine had never found her lover more charming or witty; his light figure and engaging manners seemed more delightful than ever since their conversation had made her feel somewhat in possession of a heart worthy of envy. They sang an Italian duet with such expression that the audience applauded enthusiastically. Their farewells were conventional, concealing their happiness. This day bound Emilie more closely to the fate of the stranger. The strength and dignity he displayed during their confession of feelings likely impressed her with the respect necessary for true love.

When she was left alone with her father in the drawing-room, the old man affectionately held her hands and asked if she had learned anything about Monsieur Longueville’s family and fortune.

“Yes, my dear father,” she replied, “and I am happier than I could have hoped. In short, Monsieur de Longueville is the only man I could ever marry.”

“Very well, Emilie,” said the Count, “then I know what remains for me to do.”

“Do you know of any impediment?” she asked, alarmed.

“My dear child, the young man is entirely unknown to me; but unless he is not a man of honor, as long as you love him, he is as dear to me as a son.”

“Not a man of honor!” exclaimed Emilie. “As for that, I’m quite at ease. My uncle, who introduced him to us, will vouch for him. Say, dear uncle, has he been a filibuster, an outlaw, a pirate?”

“I knew I’d find myself in this fix!” cried the old sailor, waking up. He looked around the room, but his niece had vanished “like Saint-Elmo’s fires,” to use his favorite expression.

“Well, uncle,” Monsieur de Fontaine continued, “how could you hide from us all you knew about this young man? You must have seen how anxious we’ve been. Is Monsieur de Longueville a man of family?”

“I don’t know him from Adam or Eve,” said the Comte de Kergarouet. “Trusting that crazy child’s tact, I got him here by my own method. I know the boy shoots excellently, hunts well, plays wonderfully at billiards, chess, and backgammon; he handles the foils and rides like the late Chevalier de Saint-Georges. He knows our vintages thoroughly. He’s as good an arithmetician as Bareme, draws, dances, and sings well. The devil’s in it! What more do you want? If that’s not a perfect gentleman, find me a bourgeois who knows all this, or any man who lives more nobly than he does. Does he do anything, I ask you? Does he compromise his dignity by hanging around an office, bowing to the upstarts you call Directors-General? He walks upright. He is a man. However, I just found in my waistcoat pocket the card he gave me when he fancied I wanted to cut his throat, poor innocent. Young men are very simple-minded nowadays! Here it is.”

“Rue du Sentier, No. 5,” said Monsieur de Fontaine, trying to recall any relevant information. “What the devil can it mean? Messrs. Palma, Werbrust & Co., wholesale dealers in muslins, calicoes, and printed cotton goods, live there. Stay, I have it: Longueville the deputy has an interest in their house. But as far as I know, Longueville has only one son of thirty-two, who doesn’t resemble our man at all, and to whom he gave fifty thousand francs a year to marry a minister’s daughter; he wants to be made a peer like the rest of them. I never heard him mention this Maximilien. Does he have a daughter? What is this girl Clara? Besides, any adventurer can call himself Longueville. But isn’t Palma, Werbrust & Co. half ruined by some speculation in Mexico or the Indies? I’ll clear this up.”

“You speak a soliloquy as if you were on stage, and seem to consider me a cipher,” said the old admiral suddenly. “Don’t you know that if he’s a gentleman, I have more than one bag in my hold that will stop any leak in his fortune?”

“If he’s a son of Longueville’s, he’ll want for nothing; but,” said Monsieur de Fontaine, shaking his head, “his father hasn’t washed off the stains of his origin. Before the Revolution, he was an attorney, and the ‘de’ he has since assumed belongs to him no more than half of his fortune.”

“Pooh! pooh! happy are those whose fathers were hanged!” cried the admiral gaily.

Three or four days later, on a fine November morning, Mademoiselle de Fontaine, wrapped in a new fur cape she wished to set as a fashion, went out with two of her sisters-in-law, on whom she had often unleashed her sharpest remarks. The three women were tempted to drive, not just to try an elegant carriage and wear gowns that would set the winter trend, but to see a cape a friend had spotted in a chic lace and linen shop at the corner of Rue de la Paix. Once inside the shop, the Baronne de Fontaine tugged at Emilie’s sleeve, pointing out Maximilien Longueville seated behind a desk, engaged in paying out change to a workwoman with whom he seemed to be consulting. The “handsome stranger” held a parcel of patterns, confirming his honorable profession.

Emilie felt an icy shudder, though no one noticed. Thanks to her good breeding, she concealed her rage and replied to her sister-in-law, “I knew it,” with an intonation and decisiveness that would have made any actress envious. She approached the desk. Longueville looked up, coolly pocketed the patterns, bowed to Mademoiselle de Fontaine, and stepped forward, scrutinizing her keenly.

“Mademoiselle,” he said to the shopgirl, who appeared disturbed, “I’ll send to settle that account; my house deals in that way. But here,” he whispered, handing her a thousand-franc note, “take this—it’s between ourselves. You’ll forgive me, I trust, mademoiselle,” he added, turning to Emilie. “You’ll kindly excuse the tyranny of business matters.”

“Indeed, monsieur, it seems to me that it’s no concern of mine,” replied Mademoiselle de Fontaine, looking at him with a bold expression of sarcastic indifference that might have led anyone to believe she was seeing him for the first time.

“Do you really mean it?” asked Maximilien, his voice breaking.

Emilie turned her back on him with astonishing insolence. These words, spoken in an undertone, escaped the ears of her two sisters-in-law. After buying the cape, the three ladies returned to the carriage, and Emilie, seated with her back to the horses, couldn’t resist one last glance into the depths of the detestable shop, where she saw Maximilien standing with arms crossed, exuding the demeanor of a man superior to the disaster that had suddenly befallen him. Their eyes met, exchanging implacable looks. Each hoped to inflict a cruel wound on the other’s heart. In an instant, they felt as distant as if one were in China and the other in Greenland.

Does vanity not wither everything? Mademoiselle de Fontaine, caught in the most violent struggle that can torment a young girl’s heart, reaped the bitter harvest of anguish sown by prejudice and narrow-mindedness. Her once fresh and velvety face bore streaks of yellow and red; her cheeks occasionally turned green. Hoping to hide her despair from her sisters, she laughed as she pointed out ridiculous dresses or passersby, but her laughter was forced. She was more deeply hurt by their unspoken compassion than by any satirical comments she could have used to retaliate. Exhausting her wit, she tried to engage them in conversation, expending her fury in senseless paradoxes, heaping bitter insults on all men in trade, especially calico dealers.

Upon returning home, she suffered a fever that initially seemed serious. By the end of a month, her parents and physician restored her to her family.

Everyone hoped this lesson would sufficiently subdue Emilie’s nature, but she gradually fell back into her old habits and re-entered the fashionable world. She declared there was no disgrace in making a mistake. If she, like her father, had a vote in the Chamber, she would propose an edict branding all merchants, especially calico dealers, on the forehead, like Berri sheep, down to the third generation. She wished that only nobles should have the right to wear the antique French costume, which suited the courtiers of Louis XV. To hear her, it was a misfortune for France that there was no visible difference between a merchant and a peer of France. And a hundred more such pleasantries, easy to imagine, flowed from her whenever the subject arose.

However, those who loved Emilie could see through her banter a tinge of melancholy. It was clear that Maximilien Longueville still reigned over her heart. Sometimes she was as gentle as during the brief summer that had birthed her love; at other times, she was unbearable. Everyone excused her mood swings, which stemmed from both secret and publicly known suffering. The Comte de Kergarouet had some influence over her, thanks to his increased generosity, a consolation that rarely fails to affect a Parisian girl.

At her first ball, hosted by the Neapolitan ambassador, Emilie, taking her place in the first quadrille, spotted Maximilien Longueville a few yards away, nodding slightly to her partner.

“Is that young man a friend of yours?” she asked, scornfully.

“Only my brother,” he replied.

Emilie started. “Ah!” he continued, “and he is the noblest soul living—”

“Do you know my name?” Emilie eagerly interrupted.

“No, mademoiselle. It’s a crime, I confess, not to remember a name on every lip—rather, in every heart. But I have a valid excuse. I’ve just arrived from Germany. My ambassador, on leave in Paris, sent me here this evening to look after his amiable wife, whom you may see yonder in that corner.”

“A perfect tragic mask!” said Emilie, glancing at the ambassadress.

“And yet that’s her ballroom face!” the young man laughed. “I’ll have to dance with her! So I thought I might have some compensation.” Mademoiselle de Fontaine curtsied. “I was surprised,” the voluble young secretary continued, “to find my brother here. Upon arriving from Vienna, I heard the poor boy was ill in bed; I counted on seeing him before coming to this ball, but good policy allows us to indulge family affection. The Padrona della casa wouldn’t give me time to call on my poor Maximilien.”

“Then, monsieur, your brother is not, like you, in diplomatic employment?”

“No,” sighed the attaché, “the poor fellow sacrificed himself for me. He and my sister Clara renounced their share of my father’s fortune to make an eldest son of me. My father dreams of a peerage, like all who vote for the ministry. Indeed, it’s promised him,” he added in an undertone. “After saving a little capital, my brother joined a banking firm, and I hear he just made a speculation in Brazil that may make him a millionaire. You see me in high spirits at having contributed to his success through my diplomatic connections. I’m impatiently awaiting a dispatch from the Brazilian Legation, which will help lift the cloud from his brow. What do you think of him?”

“Well, your brother’s face doesn’t look like that of a man busy with money matters.”

The young attaché shot a scrutinizing glance at Emilie’s seemingly calm face.

“What!” he exclaimed, smiling, “can young ladies read the thoughts of love behind a silent brow?”

“Your brother is in love, then?” she asked, betraying her curiosity.

“Yes; my sister Clara, to whom he is as devoted as a mother, wrote to me that he fell in love this summer with a very pretty girl; but I’ve had no further news. Would you believe the poor boy used to get up at five in the morning, rushing off to settle his business so he could return by four o’clock to the country where the lady was? In fact, he ruined a very nice thoroughbred I had just given him. Forgive my chatter, mademoiselle; I’ve just returned from Germany. For a year, I’ve heard no decent French, been weaned from French faces, and satiated with Germans, to such a degree that, in my patriotic mania, I could talk to the chimeras on a French candlestick. If I speak with a lack of reserve unbecoming a diplomat, the fault is yours, mademoiselle. Was it not you who pointed out my brother? When he’s the theme, I become inexhaustible. I’d like to proclaim to the world how good and generous he is. He gave up no less than a hundred thousand francs a year, the income from the Longueville property.”

If Mademoiselle de Fontaine benefited from these important revelations, it was partly due to her skill in questioning her confiding partner once she discovered he was her scorned lover’s brother.

“And could you, without being grieved, see your brother selling muslin and calico?” Emilie asked after the third figure of the quadrille.

“How do you know that?” asked the attaché. “Thank God, though I pour out a flood of words, I’ve learned not to reveal more than I intend, like all the other diplomatic apprentices I know.”

“You told me, I assure you.”

Monsieur de Longueville looked at Emilie with surprise and perspicacity. A suspicion flashed upon him. He glanced from his brother to his partner, guessed everything, clasped his hands, fixed his eyes on the ceiling, and began to laugh, saying, “I’m an idiot! You’re the most beautiful person here; my brother keeps stealing glances at you; he’s dancing despite his illness, and you pretend not to see him. Make him happy,” he added, leading her back to her old uncle. “I won’t be jealous, but I’ll always shiver a little at calling you my sister—”

However, the lovers proved as inexorable to each other as they were to themselves. Around two in the morning, refreshments were served in an immense corridor, where tables were arranged like in a restaurant to allow people of the same coterie to meet freely. By one of those accidents that always happen to lovers, Mademoiselle de Fontaine found herself at a table next to that of the more important guests. Maximilien was among them. Emilie, listening attentively to her neighbors’ conversation, overheard one of those dialogues into which a young woman easily falls with a young man of Maximilien’s grace and style. The lady conversing with the young banker was a Neapolitan duchess, whose eyes shot lightning flashes, and whose skin gleamed like satin. The intimate terms Longueville affected with her stung Emilie all the more because she had just given her lover back twenty times the tenderness she had ever felt for him.

“Yes, monsieur, in my country, true love can make every kind of sacrifice,” the Duchess was saying, simpering.

“You have more passion than Frenchwomen,” said Maximilien, whose burning gaze fell on Emilie. “They are all vanity.”

“Monsieur,” Emilie interjected eagerly, “is it not very wrong to calumniate your own country? Devotion can be found in every nation.”

“Do you imagine, mademoiselle,” retorted the Italian with a sardonic smile, “that a Parisian would be capable of following her lover all over the world?”

“Oh, madame, let us understand each other. She would follow him to a desert and live in a tent, but not to sit in a shop.”

A disdainful gesture completed her meaning. Thus, under the influence of her disastrous education, Emilie once again sabotaged her budding happiness and its prospects of life. Maximilien’s apparent indifference and a woman’s smile had drawn from her one of those sarcastic remarks whose treacherous zest always led her astray.

“Mademoiselle,” said Longueville in a low voice, amid the noise of the ladies rising from the table, “no one will ever desire your happiness more ardently than I; permit me to assure you of this as I take my leave. I’m starting for Italy in a few days.”

“With a Duchess, no doubt?”

“No, but perhaps with a mortal blow.”

“Isn’t that pure fancy?” Emilie asked anxiously.

“No,” he replied. “There are wounds that never heal.”

“You are not to go,” she said imperiously, smiling.

“I shall go,” Maximilien replied gravely.

“You’ll find me married on your return, I warn you,” she said playfully.

“I hope so.”

“Impertinent wretch!” she exclaimed. “What cruel revenge!”

A fortnight later, Maximilien departed with his sister Clara for the warm, poetic scenes of beautiful Italy, leaving Mademoiselle de Fontaine consumed by vehement regret. The young Secretary to the Embassy took up his brother’s quarrel, exacting revenge on Emilie for her disdain by revealing the cause of the lovers’ separation. He repaid her past sarcasm with interest, often amusing Excellencies by describing the fair foe of the counting-house, the amazon who preached a crusade against bankers, the young girl whose love evaporated before a bale of muslin. The Comte de Fontaine had to use his influence to secure an appointment to Russia for Auguste Longueville to protect his daughter from the ridicule heaped upon her by this dangerous young tormentor.

Not long after, the Ministry, compelled to raise a levy of peers to support the aristocratic party trembling in the Upper Chamber under the lash of an illustrious writer, granted Monsieur Guiraudin de Longueville a peerage, with the title of Vicomte. Monsieur de Fontaine also received a peerage, a reward due to his fidelity in difficult times and his name, which claimed a place in the hereditary Chamber.

Around this time, Emilie, now of age, undoubtedly reflected seriously on life, as her tone and manners changed perceptibly. Instead of amusing herself with cutting remarks toward her uncle, she lavished affectionate attention on him, bringing him his stick with unwavering devotion, giving him her arm, riding in his carriage, and accompanying him on all his drives. She even persuaded him that she liked the smell of tobacco and read him his favorite paper, La Quotidienne, amid clouds of smoke he intentionally blew over her. She learned piquet to match the old count and listened patiently to his periodic tales of the Belle-Poule’s battles, the Ville de Paris’s maneuvers, M. de Suffren’s first expedition, or the battle of Aboukir.

Though the old sailor often claimed he knew his longitude and latitude too well to be captured by a young corvette, one fine morning, Paris drawing-rooms buzzed with the news of Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s marriage to the Comte de Kergarouet. The young Countess hosted lavish entertainments to drown her thoughts, but she likely found a void at the bottom of the whirlpool; luxury failed to disguise the emptiness and grief of her sorrowing soul. Despite her flashes of assumed gaiety, her beautiful face often expressed unspoken melancholy. Emilie appeared full of attentions and consideration for her elderly husband, who, upon retiring to his rooms at night, often remarked, “I do not know myself. Was I to wait until seventy-two to embark as pilot on board the Belle Emilie after twenty years of matrimonial galleys?”

The young Countess’s conduct was marked by such strictness that even the most discerning criticism found no fault. Observers speculated that the vice-admiral had reserved the right to manage his fortune to keep his wife tightly in hand; however, this notion was insulting to both the uncle and niece. Their behavior was so delicately judicious that men interested in uncovering the couple’s secrets could never decide whether the old Count regarded her as a wife or a daughter. He often claimed to have rescued his niece as a castaway after shipwreck, asserting that he had never taken mean advantage of hospitality when saving an enemy from the storm’s fury. Although the Countess aspired to reign in Paris and tried to keep pace with Mesdames the Duchesses de Maufrigneuse and du Chaulieu, the Marquises d’Espard and d’Aiglemont, the Comtesses Feraud, de Montcornet, and de Restaud, Madame de Camps, and Mademoiselle des Touches, she did not yield to the advances of the young Vicomte de Portenduere, who idolized her.

Two years after her marriage, in one of the old drawing-rooms in the Faubourg Saint-Germain, where she was admired for her character worthy of the old school, Emilie heard the Vicomte de Longueville announced. In the corner of the room where she sat playing piquet with the Bishop of Persepolis, her agitation went unnoticed. She turned her head and saw her former lover enter, fresh with youth. His father’s death, followed by that of his brother, killed by the severe climate of Saint-Petersburg, had placed the hereditary plumes of the French peer’s hat on Maximilien’s head. His fortune matched his learning and merits; just the day before, his youthful and fervent eloquence had dazzled the Assembly. At that moment, he stood before the Countess, free and graced with all the advantages she had once required of her ideal. Every mother with a daughter to marry made amiable advances to a man endowed with the virtues attributed to him, as they admired his attractive person. Yet Emilie knew, better than anyone, that the Vicomte de Longueville possessed the steadfast nature a wise woman sees as a guarantee of happiness. She looked at the admiral, who, to use his favorite expression, seemed likely to hold his course for a long time yet, and cursed the follies of her youth.

At that moment, Monsieur de Persepolis said with Episcopal grace: “Fair lady, you have thrown away the king of hearts—I have won. But do not regret your money. I keep it for my little seminaries.” 

PARIS, December 1829.
