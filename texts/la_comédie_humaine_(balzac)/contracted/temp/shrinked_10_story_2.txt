The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou who had fought for the Bourbons in La Vendée, was ruined when his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet—noble but penniless—and moved to Paris with his large family seeking relief. Though initially disheartened, he received promotion and royal decorations that revived his hopes. Soon, however, his sacrifices seemed unacknowledged as new aristocrats rose; about to return to his estate, he reentered royal service during the 1815 upheavals. His loyalty was rewarded: he became a trusted adviser to Louis XVIII, valued for his wit and political sense. The family prospered—his sons won important posts and his daughters made advantageous matches, generally with lesser nobility. Emilie, the youngest, became the spoiled center of Parisian society; beautiful and exacting, she drew many suitors but remained indifferent, snubbing those she deemed inferior and rejecting matches for trivial faults. As the political climate shifted and the King’s health declined, the Comte pressed harder to find Emilie a husband to secure the family’s status, but her disdain through the winter left their future uncertain.

Nature had given Emilie de Fontaine every quality to play the part of Célimène. Tall and slender, she could shift from dignity to playfulness; her long neck and arched brows suggested scorn and impertinence, her fine black hair and melodious voice could charm or silence. Her alabaster complexion stayed composed except for fleeting emotions. Many young men she spurned accused her of acting; she replied that she merely inspired desire to subject it to her whims.

Monsieur de Fontaine realized too late that home adoration had spoiled his daughter. Constant admiration had inflated her pride and selfishness, hidden by youthful charm but obvious to a father who tried in vain to teach her wisdom. His gentle counsels slid off her like water from marble; her affection often took a condescending tone, like a child demanding attention and then returning to play.

Though Emilie sometimes showed fondness for her parents, she often withdrew, jealous of the shared love given others, including her siblings. She blamed fate for her solitude, failing to see that happiness is inward. Determined to resist her sisters’ marriages, she nevertheless felt pangs of envy at their contentment; her unpredictability made her mother suspect she might be a little mad.

This pride was common among beautiful young women of affluent families: feeling misunderstood by mothers, suspecting maternal envy, shedding secret tears, and rebelling against perceived tyranny. They built elaborate ideals of the perfect partner, only to learn by experience that reality rarely matches fantasy.

Emilie’s standards were precise and unyielding: her husband must be a peer of France, a soldier wearing an Order so they could be respected; he must be slim, for she despised corpulence and believed only the slender could be romantic. Her disdain for failing suitors was plain, and Monsieur de Fontaine feared her eccentricities would invite ridicule and that delay in the marriage market might bring consequences.

As winter under Charles X progressed, he redoubled efforts to find matches, hosting gatherings to attract suitors despite his reputation as a corrupter of legislative integrity. After a season of introductions he hoped Emilie would choose, but her whims and standards remained unchanged.

One morning he summoned her to discuss her future, arranging his study to look dignified; she greeted him with a light, chilly kiss. He pressed the importance of a wise choice for happiness. Emilie interrupted, reminded him of their prior agreement about suitors, and mocked his suggestions for appearance or background. Exasperated, he declared he had fulfilled his duty and that the choice now rested with her, warning that marital happiness depends on mutual respect rather than mere wealth or rank. Touched by his sincerity, Emilie feigned affection and coaxed him, yet her pride stayed intact.

At a family gathering she refused to consider marriage, insisting she would only wed a peer; her uncle jokingly suggested she wait for the Duc de Bordeaux, and she remained resolute.

That summer at the country house Emilie asked to attend the Bal de Sceaux, drawn by the crowd’s anonymity. There she noticed a striking young man apart from the throng whose elegance and dignity sparked immediate attraction. Aloof, he watched another dancer, a pale girl named Clara; curious and jealous, Emilie followed them. Days later, learning he was not the noble she’d hoped, she pressed her uncle for an introduction. The promise intensified her interest; her infatuation grew, fed by imagination and youthful ideals, still unaware of the complexities love and marriage would bring.

The next morning, before Emilie awoke, her uncle went to Chevreuse. Recognizing the young man he’d insulted, he approached with the civility of an old courtier. “I’m a vice-admiral,” he said, “and I think as little of dueling as of smoking a cigar. I had too much grog and ran you down. Let’s shake hands; I’d rather face a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than give his family cause for regret.” The young man accepted the apology, and they rode into the woods. The Count, playful, fired a pistol into a birch and encouraged the young man to shoot near his mark; the man obliged and won the admiral’s approval.

During the ride the Count probed him about gentlemanly codes. “Do you have debts?” he asked. “No,” came the reply. “What, you pay for everything?” “Punctually; otherwise, we’d lose credit and respect.” The Count teased about mistresses and youthful extravagance, confessed his own past recklessness, and promised to introduce the young man at the Pavillon Planat, urging him to dinner to meet his nephew, the Comte de Fontaine. The young man—Longueville—found the old man odd but affable and accepted.

When the house party gathered the next afternoon and a servant announced “Monsieur de Longueville,” everyone rushed to appraise the newcomer. Simply and elegantly dressed, Longueville seemed at ease in the Receiver-General’s luxury. His manner and polished education suggested a man of the world; when the admiral mentioned naval architecture he answered apt ly, prompting a lady to suspect he had attended the École Polytechnique. Longueville allowed himself to be flattered but courteously declined dinner, saying he was “Hippocrates” to his delicate young sister—an explanation that provoked ironic speculation about his profession.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine, who had dressed to attract him, felt slighted when he did not give her the attention she expected. Emilie, usually charming with newcomers, became unusually simple and natural, prompting family comment and varied interpretations—some thinking she was withholding to dazzle later. Longueville’s visit was neither too long nor too short; he left having pleased everyone and intensified their curiosity. The Count called him “a cunning rascal.” Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s disappointment and Emilie’s restraint puzzled the family. During dinner, a sarcastic remark and Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s warning—that universal perfection must hide a defect, “the worst fault is to have none”—kept speculation alive.

Despite efforts at indifference, Emilie’s feelings soon betrayed her. Longueville returned, and by his third visit everyone saw she was affected. Accustomed to being the center of attention, she was unsettled by someone who attracted her in turn; she tried to resist but could not dismiss him from her heart. Anxiety grew when his modesty and discretion clashed with her curiosity: he never spoke of himself or his family and deftly evaded hints. If she discussed painting, he answered like a connoisseur; if she played music, he proved a good pianist. Once they sang a duet, a moment of intimate harmony, yet he preserved the mystery of his social status.

Her uncle and the family schemed to learn more, keen to meet Longueville’s sister, Clara. They proposed an informal dance to entertain her. Emilie felt joy at the thought of another person to care for and grew gentler and more indulgent; the change pleased her family, who read it as love transforming her selfishness. She believed Longueville’s visits were chiefly for her, and though they never spoke openly of passion, she sensed his affection. She led him into revealing small personal details and realized he, too, was studying her and encouraging her improvement.

On the day of the dance Clara and her brother were principal guests. For the first time Emilie took pleasure in another girl’s triumph and lavished Clara with sincere attention. But Clara proved more deft in conversation, steering away from personal topics while charming Emilie—who felt both envious and measured. “Mademoiselle,” Clara said, “I’ve heard so much about you from Maximilien that I wanted to know you out of affection for him; isn’t wanting to know you a wish to love you?” Emilie admitted worry about speaking of those not of noble birth; Clara dismissed such concerns with graceful ease, putting Emilie at ease, and they danced with renewed warmth.

Believing him well born and seeing his grace surpass her ideal, Emilie felt satisfaction and sparkle. As autumn deepened, the couple surrendered to the sweet tide of feeling. Small shared incidents—aimless conversations that masked deeper affections, admiring sunsets, gathering daisies, singing passionate duets—knitted them closer. Each casual talk, musical exchange, and stroll deepened their intimacy and confirmed the mutual affection they had carefully concealed.
