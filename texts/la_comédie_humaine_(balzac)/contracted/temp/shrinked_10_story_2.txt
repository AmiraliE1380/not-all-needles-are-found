The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou, had served the Bourbon cause during the La Vendée conflict. Despite noble lineage and past sacrifices he fell into financial ruin after his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet, a woman of noble birth but without fortune. When the second revolution occurred, he moved to Paris with his large family hoping for better prospects. Initially disillusioned by the new regime, he nevertheless received unexpected honors, including a military promotion and royal decorations, which rekindled hopes for royal favor.

He soon realized his sacrifices were largely unrecognized and felt marginalized as new aristocrats gained prominence. Disheartened, he contemplated returning to his estate but was drawn back into royal service during the tumult of 1815. His loyalty was rewarded when he became a trusted advisor to Louis XVIII, who valued his wit and political acumen. Consequently the Fontaine family began to prosper: his sons secured important positions and his daughters made advantageous marriages, though generally to men of lesser nobility.

Emilie, the youngest daughter, was particularly spoiled and became the center of attention in Parisian society. Her beauty and charm made her highly sought after, yet she remained indifferent to suitors, asserting superiority over those of lower birth. Despite her father’s efforts to arrange advantageous matches, Emilie’s high standards and capricious nature provoked family conflict. The Count’s attempts to secure a suitable husband were repeatedly frustrated as she dismissed potential matches for trivial flaws.

As the political landscape shifted and the King’s health declined, the Comte intensified his efforts to find Emilie a husband, hoping to solidify the family’s status. But her unreasonable expectations and disdain complicated matters, producing a string of rejections that left him increasingly desperate. Family dynamics grew strained: Emilie’s arrogance bred friction among siblings and their spouses, who were also navigating the complexities of newfound social standing.

Through these tensions the Comte remained hopeful for royal favor, convinced that a suitable match for Emilie would validate his sacrifices and restore the family’s honor. Yet as winter’s social season progressed, Emilie’s dismissive attitude toward suitors continued to frustrate his ambitions, leaving the Fontaine family’s future uncertain.

Nature had endowed Emilie de Fontaine with every quality needed to play the part of Célimène. Tall and slender, she could shift from dignified to playful at will. A long neck carried scorn and impertinence; fine black hair and arched brows lent pride to her features. Emilie had mastered feminine gestures that could convey contempt or charm. Her melodious voice could win a heart or cut short indiscretion. Her alabaster complexion remained mostly calm, disturbed only by fleeting emotion. Many young men she spurned accused her of acting a part; she answered that she merely inspired desire to subject it to her whims.

Monsieur de Fontaine realized too late that adoration at home had spoiled his daughter. The admiration surrounding Emilie swelled her pride and self-confidence, breeding the selfishness of pampered children. Her youthful charms concealed these flaws, but her father saw them and tried to teach her life’s wisdom. His gentle counsels met her caprice and ironic shrewdness; his words slid off her like water from marble. Gradually he discerned that her affection was often condescending, like a child demanding attention and then returning to play.

Emilie sometimes showed fondness for her parents but often withdrew, jealous of the affections they shared with others, including her siblings. She blamed fate for her solitude, failing to see that happiness springs from within. Determined to resist her sisters’ marriages, she nevertheless felt pangs of envy at their happiness. Her unpredictable behavior led her mother to suspect she might be a little mad.

Such pride is common among beautiful young women of affluent families, who feel misunderstood by their mothers and suspect maternal envy. This provokes secret tears, revolts against perceived tyranny, and elaborate daydreams of a perfect partner meeting exacting criteria. With experience, many find happiness in unexpected places, learning that ideals often mismatch reality.

Emilie’s standards were precise: he must be a peer of France, a soldier, and wear an Order so they could be received with respect. He also had to be slender; she despised corpulence in men and believed only the slim could be romantic. Her disdain for those who failed these tests was frank, and Monsieur de Fontaine feared her eccentricities would invite ridicule and that prolonged indecision in the marriage market might be consequential.

As the winter of Charles X’s reign progressed, Monsieur de Fontaine increased efforts to find matches, hosting gatherings to attract suitors. His reputation as a corrupter of legislative integrity spread, yet he believed his intentions were honorable. After a season of introductions he felt hopeful Emilie might choose a husband; he soon found her whims and standards unyielding.

One morning he summoned her to discuss her future. He arranged his study to present a dignified front; Emilie greeted him with a light, perfunctory kiss. Monsieur de Fontaine raised the subject of marriage, stressing that a wise choice was essential to her happiness.

Emilie interrupted, reminding him of their prior agreement about suitors. He urged seriousness, explaining that the family had worked to secure prospects. She dismissed his concern, mocking the idea of settling for less than a peer of France, faulting the men he proposed for appearance or background.

Exasperated, Monsieur de Fontaine declared he had fulfilled his duty and that the choice now rested with her. He warned that marital happiness depends on mutual respect more than wealth or rank. Touched by her father’s sincerity, Emilie feigned warmth and coaxed him, yet her pride remained intact.

At a family gathering she again refused to consider marriage, insisting she would marry only a peer. Her uncle jokingly suggested she await the Duc de Bordeaux, drawing laughter, but Emilie stayed resolute.

As summer approached, the family retired to their country house, where Emilie asked to attend the Bal de Sceaux, attracted by the anonymity of the crowd. The ball was famed for its liveliness and mix of social classes.

At the ball she was surprised by the presence of many upper-class guests. Observing the festivities, she noticed a striking young man apart from the crowd, exuding elegance and dignity. His bearing sparked her imagination and she felt instant attraction.

Determined to learn more, Emilie approached him, but he remained aloof. She tried exaggerated gestures and laughters to draw him in, but he seemed indifferent. Frustrated, she followed his gaze and saw he watched another dancer, a pale girl named Clara.

Intrigued, Emilie followed the pair as they left the ball. The young man stayed attentive to Clara but still caught Emilie’s eye as they departed. She felt jealousy and curiosity, eager to learn his identity.

Days later, while riding with her uncle, Emilie met Lady Dudley and Monsieur Vandenesse and realized the young man she admired was not what she had hoped. Disappointed, she tried to hide her feelings but remained drawn to him.

Her uncle, noting her agitation, speculated about her feelings. Insisting he must be of noble birth, Emilie secured his promise to introduce them. This revelation intensified her interest and she began to imagine a future with him.

As days passed, Emilie’s infatuation grew, fed by imagination and the allure of romance. Unaware of the complexities and compromises that love and marriage bring, she remained dazzled by youthful ideals and the dream of a perfectly matched suitor.

The next morning, before Emilie awoke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. Recognizing the young man he had insulted the day before, he approached with the courtesy of the old court.

“Who would have guessed I’d meet the son or grandson of one of my best friends at seventy-three? I’m a vice-admiral; dueling is to me what smoking a cigar is to others. In my day, young men had to see the color of their blood before becoming friends! I had a bit too much grog last night and ran you down. Let’s shake hands; I’d rather take a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than cause his family regret.”

Despite his coolness, the young man could not resist the Count’s cordiality and shook his hand.

“You were going out riding,” the Count noted. “Don’t let me detain you, but join us for dinner at the Villa Planat. My nephew, the Comte de Fontaine, is someone you should meet. I’ll make it up to you by introducing you to five of the prettiest women in Paris. Your brow is clearing! I enjoy seeing young people happy; it reminds me of my youth when adventures and duels were common. Nowadays, you worry over everything as if there were no history.”

“But aren’t we right to think this way? The sixteenth century gave Europe religious liberty, and the nineteenth will bring political liberty—”

“Let’s not discuss politics. I’m an old woman—ultra, you see. But I won’t stop young men from being revolutionary, so long as they let the King disperse their assemblies.”

As they rode into the woods, the Count pointed out a slender birch, pulled out a pistol, and shot into the tree from fifteen paces.

“You see, I’m not afraid of a duel,” he said with comic gravity.

“Nor am I,” replied the young man, promptly firing close to the Count’s bullet.

“That’s what I call a well-educated man!” the admiral exclaimed.

Throughout the ride the Count quizzed him on the trifles his code deemed essential to a gentleman.

“Do you have any debts?” he finally asked.

“No, monsieur.”

“What, you pay for everything?”

“Punctually; otherwise, we’d lose our credit and respect.”

“But you must have more than one mistress? Ah, you blush! Manners have changed. All these notions of lawful order and liberty have spoiled young men. You have no Guimard or Duthe now, and you know nothing of heraldry. My dear young friend, you’re not fully fledged. The man who doesn’t sow his wild oats in spring will do so in winter. If I have eighty thousand francs a year at seventy, it’s because I spent my capital at thirty—honorably, of course. However, your imperfections won’t stop me from introducing you at the Pavillon Planat. Remember, you promised to come.”

“What an odd little old man!” Longueville thought. “He’s jolly and hale, but while he seems friendly, I won’t trust him too far.”

The next day, around four, when the household gathered in the drawing-rooms and billiard-room, a servant announced, “Monsieur de Longueville.” Upon hearing the old admiral’s protégé had arrived, everyone rushed in, eager to observe Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s expression and judge this remarkable young man who had earned honorable mention over many rivals.

Dressed simply yet elegantly, with an air of ease and polite manners, Longueville won the family’s goodwill. He seemed accustomed to the Receiver-General’s luxury. His conversation showed a man of the world with a brilliant education. When the admiral raised the trivial topic of naval architecture, he answered aptly, prompting one lady to remark that he must have attended the École Polytechnique.

“I think, madame,” he replied, “that I may regard it as an honor to have been accepted.”

Despite persistent invitations, he politely declined dinner, explaining he was the Hippocrates of his young sister, whose delicate health required care.

“Monsieur is perhaps a medical man?” one of Emilie’s sisters-in-law asked ironically.

“Monsieur has left the École Polytechnique,” Mademoiselle de Fontaine interjected, her face flushing as she learned the young lady at the ball was Longueville’s sister.

“But, my dear, he may be a doctor and still have attended the École Polytechnique—correct, monsieur?”

“There’s nothing to prevent it, madame,” he replied.

All eyes turned to Emilie, who gazed with uneasy curiosity at the captivating stranger. She breathed more easily when he added with a smile, “I do not belong to the medical profession; I even gave up going into the Engineers to preserve my independence.”

“And you did well,” the Count said.

Longueville made his visit neither too long nor too short, leaving when he saw he had pleased everyone and piqued their curiosity.

“He’s a cunning rascal!” the Count remarked after seeing him out.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine, who had dressed to attract Longueville’s attention, felt disappointed when he didn’t pay her the attention she thought he owed. The family was surprised by her sudden silence; Emilie usually displayed all her charms for newcomers, but now she seemed simple and natural, perhaps more beautiful. Some family members read her restraint as a refined strategy to dazzle him later.

During dinner, as everyone speculated about Longueville’s qualities, Mademoiselle de Fontaine remained silent. A sarcastic remark from her uncle roused her, and she quipped that such heavenly perfection must conceal a great defect, adding she would be cautious in judging such a gifted man at first sight.

“Those who please everybody please nobody,” she concluded, “and the worst fault is to have none.”

Like all girls in love, Emilie tried to hide her feelings, but after a fortnight every family member knew. When Longueville called for the third time, Emilie believed it was chiefly for her sake, which thrilled and pricked her pride. Accustomed to being the center of attention, she now recognized an outside force that attracted her, and despite attempts to resist, she could not shake the young man from her heart.

Anxiety crept in as two of Longueville’s qualities—unexpected modesty and discretion—clashed with her curiosity. He never spoke of himself or his family, skillfully evading her hints. If she discussed painting, he answered as a connoisseur; if she played music, he proved a good pianist. One evening he charmed the party by singing a duet with Emilie, yet preserved the mystery of his social status when they tried to learn if he was a professional singer.

Distracted by his reserve, Emilie hoped to extract more information from Clara, aided by her uncle’s maneuvers. The family expressed a strong desire to meet Clara Longueville, proposing an informal dance to entertain her. Despite clouds of suspicion and curiosity, joy illuminated Emilie’s soul as she felt intimately connected to someone outside herself. She began to understand life’s relationships; whether happiness made her better or she was simply too occupied to torment others, she became gentler and more indulgent.

This change enchanted her family, suggesting her selfishness was transforming into love. She eagerly awaited the arrival of her bashful admirer, and though they had not spoken of passion, she sensed his affection. She led him to reveal varied information about himself, realizing he was also studying her and striving to improve her education.

The day of the dance arrived, with Clara and her brother as principal guests. For the first time, Emilie felt pleasure in another young girl’s triumph, lavishing Clara with sincere attention. However, Clara proved more adept at conversation, steering discussion away from personal topics while charming Emilie, who felt both envious and judged.

“Mademoiselle,” Clara said, “I’ve heard so much about you from Maximilien that I wanted to know you out of affection for him; but isn’t wanting to know you a wish to love you?”

“My dear Clara, I feared I might have displeased you by speaking of those not of noble birth.”

“Oh, be at ease. Such discussions are pointless these days. It doesn’t affect me; I’m beside the question.”

Ambitious as her answer seemed, it filled Emilie with joy; she interpreted it to fit her hopes. She danced with renewed spirit, watching Longueville, whose grace surpassed her ideal. Satisfied to believe him well-born, her eyes sparkled as she danced, feeling a connection with him.

As autumn arrived, the couple surrendered to the tide of sweet sentiment, strengthening their bond through countless small incidents. They studied each other, as lovers do, and enjoyed aimless conversations that masked deeper feelings. They admired sunsets together, gathered daisies, and sang passionate duets, expressing secrets through music. Each little shared pleasure—the casual talk, the music, the walks—deepened their intimacy and confirmed the affection each had been careful to conceal.
