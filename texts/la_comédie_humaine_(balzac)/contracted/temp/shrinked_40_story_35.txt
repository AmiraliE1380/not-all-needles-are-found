The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once one of the darkest, most tortuous lanes near the Hôtel de Ville, curved around the Préfecture de Police gardens and ended at the Rue Martroi by a wall since pulled down. A turnstile that gave the street its name stood until 1823, when the Municipality built a ballroom on the neighboring garden for the fête of the Duc d’Angoulême.

Even at its widest, where it opened onto the Rue de la Tixeranderie, the Rue du Tourniquet was under six feet across. In rain the gutter water ran deep at the house bases, sweeping dust and refuse into corners carts could not reach; inhabitants trusted storms to wash their miry alley. In summer the sun barely penetrated, never drying the damp that rose to the first story. Residents who lit lamps at five in June kept them burning all winter. A traveler approaching the Marais along the quays, past the Rue du Chaume and the Rues de l’Homme-Armé, des Billettes, and des Deux-Portes, might think he had passed through cellars all the way.

Many old Paris streets were such damp labyrinths. At the corner where the Rue du Tourniquet met the Rue de la Tixeranderie two iron ring clamps remained, relics of chains the watch once strung nightly. The corner house testified to the unhealthiness of such dwellings: its cellar arches rose about two feet above ground, the entrance required three outside steps, and the door was crowned by a closed arch whose keystone bore a female head and time-eaten arabesques. Three windows, sills about five feet from the ground, lit a small set of rooms facing the Rue du Tourniquet, protected by strong iron bars curved outward like a baker’s.

By day a passer-by could see little; only in the height of summer, when light strengthened, might one discern in the second room two beds hung with green serge side by side in an old-fashioned alcove. By about three, when candles were lighted, the nearer pane revealed an old woman on a stool tending a brazier to simmer a porter’s-wife stew; a few utensils hung against the wall in the twilight. An old trestle table, bare of linen, held pewter spoons and her dish; three wretched chairs furnished the multipurpose kitchen-dining room. Above the chimney hung a small mirror, a tinder-box, three glasses, matches, and a large cracked white jug. In spite of the gloom, the floor, utensils, and fireplace testified to cleanliness and thrift.

The old woman’s pale, withered face matched the street’s darkness and mustiness. Motionless, as inseparable from the house as a snail from its shell, her countenance—alert with a vague mischief—was framed in a net cap that barely covered white hair; her fine gray eyes were quiet as the street, and wrinkles ran like cracks in the walls. Whether born to poverty or fallen from better days, she seemed resigned to melancholy.

From sunrise till dark, except when cooking or buying provisions, she sat by the farther window opposite a young girl. At any hour a passer-by could see the needlewoman in an old red velvet chair bending over an embroidery frame, stitching indefatigably. The mother had a green pillow and busied herself with handmade net, but with feeble sight; an antiquated spring-grip pair of spectacles rested on her nose. By night they set a lamp between them, its light concentrated by two globe-shaped water bottles, letting the elder see the network on her pillow and the younger the pattern’s delicate details.

The window’s outward bend held a box of earth on the sill where sweet-peas, nasturtiums, a sickly honeysuckle, and convolvulus twined up the iron bars. These etiolated plants bore a few pale flowers, adding sadness and sweetness to the framed picture of the two figures.

Even the most selfish observer would carry away a perfect image of working-class Parisian women’s lives: the embroideress evidently lived by her needle. Many passing through the turnstile wondered how a girl could keep her color living in a cellar. A student crossing to the Quartier Latin might liken this vegetative existence to ivy clinging to chill walls, or to peasants who toil and die unknown. A house-owner, valuing the property, would ask, “What will become of those two women if embroidery goes out of fashion?”

Among the men obliged to pass this street for the Hôtel de Ville or Palais de Justice, some might have been charitable. A widower or an Adonis of forty, often glimpsing these sad lives, might reckon on the mother and daughter’s poverty and hope to make the innocent work-woman his at little cost—her nimble fingers, youthful figure, and pale skin (a charm due to the sunless street) drawing admiration. Perhaps an honest clerk with twelve hundred francs a year, seeing diligence and purity, waited only for better prospects to unite his life to hers, to bring a man’s arm and calm affection, pale like the window flowers, to uphold the home.

Vague hope kept life in the mother’s dim gray eyes. Each morning after a frugal breakfast she took up her pillow—chiefly for show—laid her spectacles on a little mahogany worktable as old as herself, and watched from about half-past eight till ten the regular passers, noting gait, dress, and countenance as if trying to attract attention to her daughter. This small review was as good as a play to her, perhaps her sole amusement.

The daughter rarely looked up. Modesty, or the painful consciousness of poverty, kept her eyes fixed on the frame; only some exclamation from her mother showed her small features. Then a clerk in a new coat or one appearing with a woman might catch sight of the girl’s slightly upturned nose, rosy mouth, and gray eyes, bright despite toil. Late hours left pale circles beneath each eye on her fresh cheeks.

The child seemed made for love and cheerfulness: love had drawn two perfect arches above her eyelids and given her a mass of chestnut hair she might hide beneath like a tent; cheerfulness widened her nostrils into quivering animation, carved dimples in her cheeks, and made her quick to forget troubles—cheerfulness, the blossom of hope, giving strength to face life’s barren path.

Her hair was always neatly dressed. As Paris needlewomen, she judged her toilet complete after brushing smooth and tucking the short curls at each temple against her white skin. The pretty growth at the nape and a clear brown line suggested youth and charm; an observer seeing her bent over work might deem her a coquette. This promise caught more than one young man’s eye, who turned in vain hope to glimpse that modest countenance.

“Caroline, there is a new face that passes regularly by, and not one of the old ones to compare with it.”

These words, spoken in a low voice by her mother one August morning in 1815, vanquished the young needlewoman’s indifference, and she looked out; the stranger was gone.

“Where has he flown to?” she asked.

“He will come back no doubt at four; I shall see him coming, and will touch your foot with mine. I am sure he will come back; he has been through the street regularly for the last three days; but his hours vary. The first day he came by at six o’clock, the day before yesterday it was four, yesterday as early as three. I remember seeing him occasionally some time ago. He is some clerk in the Préfet’s office who has moved to the Marais.—Why!” she exclaimed after glancing down the street, “our gentleman of the brown coat has taken to wearing a wig; how much it alters him!”

The gentleman of the brown coat seemed to close the daily procession; the old woman put on her spectacles and took up her work with a sigh, glancing at her daughter with a look that mingled admiration, gratitude, hope, and pride in such a pretty child.

At about four the old lady pushed her foot against Caroline’s, and the girl looked up quickly enough to see a new actor, whose regular advent would thenceforth lend variety to the scene. He was tall and thin, wore black, a man of about forty, with a certain solemnity; as his piercing hazel eye met the old woman’s dull gaze, he made her quake, for she felt as though he could read hearts, and his presence seemed as icy as the air of this dank street. His hollow cheeks bore sorrow’s seal. Though at first Caroline’s look was lively curiosity, it softened into gentle sympathy as the stranger receded like a last relation following a funeral.

The weather was so hot and the gentleman so absent-minded that he had taken off and forgotten his hat as he passed. Caroline noted how his brushed hair altered his countenance. The strong, uncharming impression he made was unlike any other passenger; for the first time she felt pity for someone besides herself and her mother. She said nothing to her mother’s conjectures, drew her needle in silence, and only regretted not seeing him more closely, looking forward to the morrow to form a definite opinion.

It was the first time a passer-by had stirred much thought in her. She usually answered her mother’s hypotheses with a smile; the old woman eyed every passer as a potential protector. If those crude suggestions did not provoke evil thoughts, Caroline’s indifference had been borne by the persistent toil consuming her youth and threatening the clearness of her eyes and the bloom of her cheeks.

For two months the “Black Gentleman”—as the neighbours called him—moved erratically along the Rue du Tourniquet, never keeping a fixed hour. The old woman and her daughter sometimes saw him in the morning or in the evening; unlike the punctual clerks who called on Madame Crochard, he seemed absent-minded, often gazing at the ground or straight ahead as if reading the future in the fog. On his first pass his glance had alarmed the mother and lingered on a quaint shop picture; thereafter he appeared lost in thought, and Madame Crochard was piqued that her mysterious visitor remained absorbed and indifferent to her chatter.

One mid-September morning, however, Caroline Crochard’s lively face—fresh among belated flowers and faded leaves—brightened the dark room and attracted the stranger’s attention. On that occasion their eyes met meaningfully; both felt they would think of one another. When he passed again at four, they looked steadily at one another with evident purpose; his kindly expression made him smile, and Caroline blushed, which her mother noted with pride. After that day the Gentleman in Black passed twice daily, observed by mother and daughter. From his irregular hours they judged he was not a mere clerk.

Through the first three winter months these twice-daily glimpses took on warmth. Their brief meetings grew friendly, then almost fraternal; by studying each other’s faces they learned their moods. It became a duty of the Unknown to visit Caroline; if he failed to give the half-smile or cordial glance she expected, her day was marred. The charm was mutual; neither could hide vexation or fatigue from the other. He could tell when she had worked late on a dress, when quarter-day bills loomed, when she had sat up the night before; she read trouble and weariness in his features. When the window was closed against winter and the little fire cast frosty redness on the two women’s faces, he noted their poverty. If pity ever showed on his face, Caroline met it with assumed cheerfulness.

Yet their feelings remained unspoken; they had never heard each other speak. Both kept a cautious distance as if intimacy might bring disaster—shyness, fear of discovering selfishness, or a city-born mutual distrust. Perhaps each hoped to preserve an illusion: he, lest vulgar words sully lips fresh as a flower; she, feeling unworthy of one who seemed to possess power and wealth.

Madame Crochard, reproachful of her daughter’s indecision, grew sulky with the “Black Gentleman,” complaining of age, ailments, and her diminished capacity to produce net. By late December, when bread was dear and the 1816 corn shortage began to bite, the stranger saw secret sorrow in Caroline’s face that his smiles could not dispel. He noted the dimness of her eyes from long nights’ work. One night near month’s end he passed the Rue du Tourniquet at one in the morning and, hearing Madame Crochard’s tears and Caroline’s sad voice mingled with sleet, crept beneath the window to listen through a hole in the yellow muslin curtain.

Inside he saw a writ on the table, water-filled globes, a lamp, and the two women: Madame Crochard weeping, Caroline’s voice thick as she pleaded that Monsieur Molineux would not distrain their goods before she could finish a dress due to Madame Roguin. The stranger, skilled at reading faces, thought the mother’s grief false while the daughter’s was genuine. Seeing on the table a triangular hunch of bread—placed to sustain Caroline through the night and remind her of the reward of industry—he trembled with pity. Risking trouble with the police, he slipped his purse through a cracked pane so that it would fall at the girl’s feet, then fled with tingling cheeks.

Next morning he returned, preoccupied but unable to avoid Caroline’s gratitude. She opened the window and pretended to dig in the snow-buried box so he would not see her but to signal thanks. Her eyes brimmed, and with a look she said, “I can only repay you from my heart.” The Gentleman in Black affected not to understand. That evening Caroline mended the window with paper and smiled; thereafter the stranger avoided the Rue du Tourniquet and was no more seen there.

One early May day, as Caroline watered a honeysuckle root and caught sight of a narrow strip of cloudless blue, she said gaily to her mother, “Mamma, we must go tomorrow for a trip to Montmorency!” At that very moment the Gentleman in Black passed, looking sadder than usual, and Caroline’s innocent mien might have seemed an invitation. The next day, at the corner of the Rue du Faubourg Saint-Denis and the Rue d’Enghien, Madame Crochard—dressed in claret merino, a silk bonnet, and a striped shawl—found her Unknown standing as if waiting for his wife. A smile lit his face at the sight of Caroline in a white dress tossed by a breeze, her rice-straw bonnet lined with pink silk, hair parted in two brown bands, and a forehead like snow. She seemed radiant; the Stranger, whether doubting whether to escort her, decided to do so at once.

He hired a vehicle for Saint-Leu-Taverny and offered seats beside him. The mother accepted but soon complained of heat and her cough, making civil speeches about being an inconvenience. By Saint-Denis she appeared asleep; her snores provoked a frown from the Stranger. Caroline, guileless, assured him she was tired from coughing all night. The Stranger’s look, half-pity, half-suspicion, hinted he distrusted the mother’s performance.

As they passed the poplar avenue toward Eaubonne, the Stranger—perhaps loosened by the bright sky and country air—entered on aimless, devious conversation with Caroline, talk as light as butterflies and full of a mysterious tenderness. In that season the country was like a bride; the view from Montmorency’s smiling valley softened hearts. Caroline was sprightly rather than witty, affectionate but uneducated; her giddy laugh hid genuine feeling. Under his gentle questions she spoke with the effusiveness of her class, unguarded like those who have never learned prudence. Her frankness brightened his face, renewing a youthful look beneath his former melancholy. Gradually his sternness softened and his real youth and character emerged; he seemed to cast off haunting ideas and show a livelier temperament beneath solemnity.

Their talk grew intimate; by the time they reached the straggling houses of Saint-Leu she was calling him Monsieur Roger. The old mother awoke, suspicious and amazed. Roger whispered that Caroline had said too much; she smiled to dispel his fear. Madame Crochard, not surprised by anything, followed them into the park, where the young pair wandered through famed meadows and fragrant copses.

At the green ridge above the valley, they paused to admire the view—combes, villages, blue hills, church towers, meadows murmuring like the ocean. They walked to the Swiss valley and its chalet once sheltering Hortense and Napoleon; Caroline sat on the mossy bench with reverent pleasure. The mother, eager to see a rustic bridge, left them, promising not to go out of sight.

Roger asked, “Have you never longed for wealth and luxury? Have you never wished to wear the beautiful dresses you embroider?” Caroline answered frankly: she sometimes dreamed of the comforts of the rich, not from selfishness but from wishing to spare her mother hardship—wanting her to be comfortable, to have a servant, sweetened coffee, and to read rather than labor. She added that her mother had sold her last silver forks to apprentice her to the old maid who taught her embroidery, and that her father had been an opera-dancer before the Revolution. Her father, once present at the siege of the Bastille, had led insurgents and been rewarded with rank, rising to colonel in the Sambre-et-Meuse army. Wounded at Lützen, he died after a year’s suffering in Paris. With the Bourbons’ return, no pension arrived; the family sank into poverty, and Caroline learned to work. She had one modest wish: that embroidered net dresses remain in fashion so she would always have work.

Her frankness moved Roger and softened his gaze toward Madame Crochard. The old woman proudly displayed a ribbon and cross tied round her neck—emblems she vowed to keep and be buried with—reminiscent of her husband’s devotion to the “little Corporal,” and she spoke of him with theatrical conviction. Roger cautioned silence, and they returned to the village through park walks. He left briefly to order a meal at the best tavern and came back to fetch them, leading them through forest alleys.

Dinner was cheerful. Roger was no longer the melancholy figure of the Rue du Tourniquet but a confiding young man willing to take life as it came, matching the two hardworking women who might lack bread the next day. His smile was affectionate and youthful. After a meal lightened by a few glasses of champagne, he proposed they attend the village ball under the chestnuts. He and Caroline danced together; their hands met sympathetically and their hearts beat with shared hopes. Under the blue sky and the rosy sunset, their eyes sparkled with feelings that to them overshadowed the world. The day had created memories neither had known before; in such moments desire and imagination made everything seem possible.

When the dance ended, Caroline sighed at the day’s close and noticed Roger’s face cloud. She asked, “Why should you not be as happy in Paris as here? Is happiness only found at Saint-Leu?” Her unguarded belief that happiness could be anywhere struck him; for the first time since their first glance they shared a mutual idea and, frightened by the silence that followed, they went toward the carriage. Before entering they playfully took hands and ran down the dark avenue in front of Madame Crochard. When they could no longer see the white net cap, Roger said in a tremulous voice, “Caroline!” Startled, the girl drew back a few steps, understanding his invitation; she extended her hand, which he passionately kissed, then withdrew it quickly, straining to see her mother. Madame Crochard affected blindness, playing the supernumerary as if by habit from her old stage life.

Thus, from furtive glances at a window to a day of frank companionship in the country, a shy, wordless familiarity had deepened into a tender intimacy. The Stranger, now revealed as Roger, had moved from melancholy reserve to youthful warmth, while Caroline’s plain honesty and devotion to her mother had won his pity and affection. The episode of the purse—his secret charity in a night of winter distress—had receded into a more candid bond formed in the sunlight of Montmorency, culminating in a first kiss that left them trembling between hope and the reality of their differing worlds.

The adventures of these two young people were not continued in the Rue du Tourniquet. To find Roger and Caroline again we must leap into modern Paris, where newly built houses contain apartments seemingly made for newlyweds on their honeymoon: fresh paper and paint, decorations in blossom like their love.

Halfway down the Rue Taitbout, in a house whose stone walls were still white and whose hall columns and inner walls shone with the neat painting recently brought into fashion by English ways, there was on the second floor a small set of rooms fitted as though the architect had known their use. A simple airy anteroom with a stucco dado opened into a combined drawing- and dining-room; from this came a pretty bedroom with a bathroom beyond. Every chimney-shelf bore a fine mirror; doors were crowded with tasteful arabesques and cornices in the best style. The sense of distinction and decorative fitness that marks modern French architects’ work was everywhere evident.

For more than a month Caroline had lived in this apartment, furnished by an upholsterer guided by an artist. A brief description of the principal room will show the pleasures Roger had given her. Gray hangings trimmed with green silk adorned the bedroom; seats covered in light-colored sateen were easy and fashionable; a simple inlaid chest of drawers held toilet treasures; a matching writing-table served for love-letters on scented paper; an antique-draped bed with muslin hangings suggested thoughts of love; drab silk curtains with green fringe were half drawn to subdue the light; a bronze clock represented Love crowning Psyche; and a red-ground Gothic carpet set off the rest. A small dressing-table before a long glass was where the needlewoman sat, out of patience with Plaisir, the famous hairdresser.

“Do you think you will have done today?” she asked.

“Your hair is so long and so thick, madame,” replied Plaisir, whose flattery revived Caroline’s memory of Roger’s praises. When the hairdresser finished, a waiting-maid came to counsel her about the dress Roger would like best. It was the beginning of September 1816 and cold; she chose a green grenadine trimmed with chinchilla. Dressed, Caroline flew into the drawing-room, opened a window, and stepped onto the elegant balcony that adorned the front. She stood with arms crossed—not to display herself to passersby but to look down at the Boulevard at the foot of the Rue Taitbout, catching glimpses of elegant carriages and silhouettes of crowds, watching both pedestrians and the new English tilburies.

After a quarter of an hour her keen eye had announced no Roger. Scorn and love swept over her face; disdain for all others was visible as if passersby were ants. Her gray eyes, bright with fun, flamed; given over to passion, she avoided admiration as much as some cultivate it; she cared for no eyes below her, only one face and one thought. When the spotted head of a certain bay horse crossed the strip between the rows of houses, Caroline shivered and stood on tiptoe, hoping to recognize the white traces and the tilbury’s color. It was he.

Roger turned the corner, whipped his horse to a gallop, and stopped at the bronze-green door he knew. The maid, having heard her mistress’ exclamation, opened at once. Roger rushed up, clasped Caroline, and embraced her with the effusive feeling natural to lovers who rarely meet. They went by common impulse into the quiet fragrant bedroom; a settee stood ready by the fire. For a moment they looked at each other in silence, expressing happiness by clasped hands and a fond gaze.

“Yes, it is you,” she said at last. “Do you know, I have not seen you for three long days—an age!—But what is the matter? You are unhappy.”

“My poor Caroline—”

“There, you see! ‘poor Caroline’—”

“No, no, do not laugh, my darling; we cannot go to the Feydeau Theatre together this evening.”

Caroline pouted briefly, then the pout vanished.

“How absurd I am! How can I think of the play when I see you? Is not the sight of you the only spectacle I care for?” she cried, pushing her fingers through Roger’s hair.

“I am obliged to go to the Attorney-General’s. We have a knotty case. He met me in the great hall at the Palais; and as I am to plead, he asked me to dine with him. But, my dearest, you can go to the theatre with your mother, and I will join you if the meeting breaks up early.”

“To the theatre without you!” she cried in amazement; “enjoy any pleasure you do not share! O my Roger! you do not deserve a kiss,” she added, throwing her arms round his neck with artless passion.

“Caroline, I must go home and dress. The Marais is some way off, and I still have business to finish.”

“Take care what you are saying, monsieur,” she interrupted. “My mother says that when a man begins to talk about his business, he is ceasing to love.”

“Caroline! Am I not here? Have I not stolen this hour from my pitiless—”

“Hush!” she said, laying a finger on his mouth. “Don’t you see I am in jest?”

Back in the drawing-room, Roger’s eye fell on an object brought home that morning by the cabinetmaker. Caroline’s old rosewood embroidery frame, by which she and her mother had earned bread in the Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, had been refitted and polished, and a net dress of elaborate design was already stretched on it.

“Well, then, my dear, I shall do some work this evening. As I stitch, I shall fancy myself gone back to those early days when you used to pass by without a word, though not without a glance; the days when the memory of your look kept me awake all night. Oh my dear old frame—the best piece of furniture in my room, though you did not give it me!” she said, seating herself on Roger’s knees; he, overcome, had dropped into a chair. “Listen. All I can earn by my work I mean to give to the poor. You have made me rich. How I love that pretty home at Bellefeuille, less for what it is than because you gave it me! But tell me, Roger, I should like to call myself Caroline de Bellefeuille—can I? Is it legal or permissible?”

At Roger’s small affirmative grimace—for he hated the name Crochard—Caroline jumped for joy and clapped her hands.

“I feel as if I should more especially belong to you. Usually a woman gives up her own name and takes her husband’s—” An idea made her blush. She took Roger’s hand and led him to the piano. “Listen,” she added, “I can play my sonata now like an angel!” Her fingers began over the ivory when he seized her round the waist.

“Caroline, I ought to be far from hence!”

“You insist on going? Well, go,” she said with a pretty pout, but she smiled and looked at the clock. “At any rate, I have detained you a quarter of an hour!”

“Goodbye, Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille,” he said, with the gentle irony of love.

She kissed him and saw him to the door; when his steps had died on the stairs she ran to the balcony to watch him get into the tilbury, gather up the reins, catch a parting look, hear the crack of his whip and the sound of wheels on the stones, watch the handsome horse, the master’s hat, the tiger’s gold lace, and at last stand gazing long after the dark corner of the street had eclipsed the vision.

Five years after Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille had settled in her pretty house in the Rue Taitbout, we rejoin a domestic scene that reveals the close affection between two people who truly love. In the blue drawing-room a four-year-old boy, Charles, thunders on his rocking-horse while his mother, Caroline, gently chides him to keep the noise down lest he wake his baby sister, Eugenie. The child tiptoes, peeks under the muslin veil that covers the infant on her mother’s knee, and asks astonished why Eugenie is asleep when they are awake. Caroline answers simply, “That only God can know,” and the two gaze at the newly baptized baby.

Caroline, now about twenty-four, has matured into a fulfilled woman. Her beauty has ripened under the steady happiness of her life. Devoted to obeying Roger’s wishes and delighted by domestic pleasures, she has acquired the accomplishments she lacked—she plays the piano and sings—and has concentrated on becoming an attentive mother. Unfamiliar with and indifferent to the arts of fashionable society, she focuses on small maternal duties: watching over Charles, training him from the cradle to love what is good and beautiful, and doing both the nurse’s work and a mother’s tender offices. For six years she has kept within the private, enchanted sphere of her affection; she still knows her lover only as “Roger.” A print of Psyche, lighting her lamp to look upon Love, hangs in her room and constantly reminds her of the fragile conditions of her happiness. She has never pushed ambitions into their relationship, has refused luxuries Roger offered, and contentment for her means simple joys: watching for Roger’s carriage, going to the play with him, and enjoying excursions together.

Rocking the infant and singing, Caroline recalls past pleasures, especially the Septembers Roger used to spend with her at Bellefeuille. Those estate visits blended the charms of summer and autumn: flowers and fruit alike, mild evenings and bright mornings. Early in their love she had sometimes worried over his even temper and the fact that he liked clandestine meetings—she had thought perhaps that their intermittent meetings preserved gentleness—but those fears proved unfounded. Each month at Bellefeuille passed as a dream of uninterrupted joy; Roger always wore a tender smile that seemed to mirror her own. Remembering this fills her with tears and with a desire to love more, though curiosity still nags at her about why Roger prefers a clandestine happiness. She invents harmless romances rather than face the possibility of a darker cause.

On the 6th of May, the anniversary of the excursion that had been the turning-point of her life, Caroline attends to household details—linen, the dessert—and, after placing the baby in her cot, goes to the balcony. She sees Roger’s carriage approach. After the usual embraces and kisses, Roger, who now uses a carriage instead of the tilbury of his youth, goes to the cradle to kiss the sleeping daughter and produces a document: a deed of gift in State funds, the marriage portion for Mademoiselle Eugenie de Bellefeuille. Caroline accepts the paper gratefully but asks why Eugenie is provided three thousand francs a year while Charles has only fifteen hundred.

Roger answers calmly: a son will be a man; fifteen hundred is sufficient and will teach him the value of work and prevent foolish extravagance. Eugenie, as a girl, must have a little fortune. He explains that twenty-seven-year-old Charles will be independent and that the smaller annuity will give him incentive. He brushes the question aside and plays with his son; the evening is happy. Later, while the little boy sleeps, the baby wakes to nurse. By lamplight Roger admires the scene—Caroline, fresh and beautiful, nursing—while she side-glances at him with that sweet, skilful look that asks what the end of the evening will be. He feigns regret and says he must go because a “serious case” awaits him, though he admits he has three days’ holiday and is supposed to be twenty leagues away. Caroline, half resigned, half pained, lets him go.

A few days after the anniversary, Caroline hurries to the Rue Saint-Louis in the Marais, summoned by news that her mother, Madame Crochard, is dying, weakened by catarrh and rheumatism. She arrives to find a small group of old women who have been Madame Crochard’s friends in later years. They had conferred with a priest because they had stirred some religious scruples in her, and they hope, by getting her to confess, to secure some benefit for themselves—indeed, Francoise, the maid, and the three old cronies have schemes to share what they imagine are the old woman’s “pickings.” Unaware that Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille is the daughter of the dying woman, the house-clusterers expect to profit should Madame Crochard die intestate or leave a small legacy. Francoise, the maid, has been stewarding an eldest woman’s household for decades and meditates aloud on the meagre sums past mistresses managed to bequeath her.

The three dowagers and Francoise have sent for the Abbe Fontanon. The Abbe is a mean-appearing little priest—low forehead, flabby cheeks, and a worldly manner. He consoles the old woman and listens to her confession. Madame Crochard, in a moment of frailty and with a touch of cunning, produces an Imperial Cross of the Legion of Honor. The sight startles the priest, and he leans nearer. In a voice scarcely audible to the servants, the old woman speaks of a wrong done to her—someone who “robbed me of my dear Caroline” and left her only three thousand francs a year, of which the capital belongs to Caroline. Francoise, lingering in a cupboard, hears enough to cry out in the drawing-room that the deceased has a daughter and “nothing to live on but an annuity.” The three old dames, expecting to inherit or otherwise profit, exchange dismay and settled back to wait.

The Abbe, more concerned with the sealing of a confession than with any worldly split, speaks with the women and then goes into the bedroom. Francoise frets about her past scant inheritances—after thirty years of service she has little to show. While the Abbe converses with Madame Crochard, Francoise hears the dying woman exclaim, “Do not desert me. What, Monsieur l’Abbe, do you think I shall be called to account for my daughter’s soul?” The Abbe’s muffled reply is unintelligible; the old woman sobs that she has nothing to bequeath and that “he” robbed her of Caroline and left her only an annuity, capital belonging to the daughter. Then, with one last effort, she cries out that she has revealed the name of Caroline’s benefactor to the priest. She dies shortly afterward, trying to look knowing; the narrative remarks that if Caroline had looked at her mother’s face at the last, she might have seen Death laughing.

This deathbed scene leads us to backtrack in the tale to earlier events closely connected to Madame Crochard’s end. In November 1805, a young barrister of six-and-twenty, the Count de Granville, descends the hotel stairs of the High Chancellor at about three in the morning. He finds no hackney coach waiting; the Chancellor’s carriage alone disturbs the silence. Suddenly the Minister—one of the heads of state, friendly in tone—places him in his carriage, having noticed the young lawyer’s abilities the previous evening. The Minister praises him for his defence in the Simeuse and Hauteserre case and hints at patronage: there will be openings in the lower courts and the Imperial Court in Paris; the High Chancellor was pleased with him. He advises the young man to take a judgeship when offered, work hard, but avoid being seen at the Minister’s receptions to prevent jealous rivals from suspecting favoritism. He promises interest on behalf of his nephew’s son and says he will have openings soon.

Back at home the young man finds a letter from his father urging him to set off at once to Bayeux. Mademoiselle Angelique Bontems has lost a sister and is now the sole heir to a fortune—forty thousand francs a year, with perhaps more on marriage. His father suggests that, with such an alliance and the Minister’s support, the young lawyer might secure a bench appointment and a comfortable fortune; his uncle, a Councillor of State, might also leave him property. The young man, under the patron’s protection and with the prospect of a wealthy match, sees rapid advancement and contentedly remembers Angelique, his childhood companion, whom in boyhood he had fancied as “his little wife.”

He sets out the next morning by diligence to Caen and arrives in Bayeux to a formal welcome. The house in the Rue Teinture is conventual—dark walnut panels, worsted chairs, a huge ebony crucifix, and sacred paintings from old Bontems. The elderly widow, Angelique’s mother, is a fanatical devotee; she lives by priests and donations, restores chapels, and fills her house with ecclesiastical finery. The Count, attempting to support his son’s hopes, explains to him that if he does not marry Angelique, the widow will press the girl to a life of religious devotion; but once married and taken to Paris, the pleasures and bustle of the capital will draw Angelique away from the restraint of confessionals and fasts. He has had the banns published: in a week the match could be done, and the Count suggests that married life in Paris will cure Angelique’s excessive piety.

The father and son, seeing the ladies return from vespers, go to the cathedral to attend a service and maybe to observe Angelique among the devout women. During the service a voice stirs the young man. He turns and beholds a young figure in a white bonnet; he believes it to be Angelique. In the dim light he perceives a face of regular features—pale gold hair, oval face, finely arched brows, a firm nose, and tender rose lips. Her light blue eyes show innocence and a strict reserve, which he attributes to devotion. When she recognizes him from childhood, she blushes, drops her veil, and continues to sing composedly. Granville, moved and impatient to speak with her, finds her in the cathedral porch after the service. Madame Bontems greets him with dignity, and the Count supports his son’s designs: Angelique is shy and devout, but a promising and pretty match. The young lawyer’s hopes of combining the Minister’s patronage with the Bontems’ fortune brighten his prospects.

Thus the tale returns to Paris and to Caroline confronting the consequences of her dying mother’s last words. Madame Crochard’s death and her revelation—under the Abbe’s questioning—that she had confessed the name of Caroline’s benefactor, together with the hints of limited provision for Caroline and generous settlements for the children, bind these two storylines. The two sets of events—the domestic happiness and the secretive, possibly compromising past, and the ambitious young lawyer’s career and pursuit of a marriage that would secure fortune and rank—are now poised to intersect, driven by secrets disclosed in confession, by patronage, by marriage contracts, and by the ambitions and dependencies that govern both private and public lives in Paris.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For about a fortnight before their wedding the Vicomte de Granville frequented the dreary drawing-room of Mademoiselle Bontems, ostensibly to study Angelique’s character; his prudence showed after their first meeting. He always found her at a small West Indian-wood table marking trousseau linen. Angelique never opened conversations about religion; if Granville toyed with her rosary or laughed at a relic, she would silently take it back with a steady, obstinate smile.

“You must either believe nothing, or believe everything the Church teaches,” she said. “Would you wish to have a woman without a religion as the mother of your children?—No.—What man may dare judge between disbelievers and God? And how can I then blame what the Church allows?” Her answers were full of fervent charity; Granville, moved sometimes by conviction in her eyes, mistook desire’s enchantment for love. Young men often infer moral perfection from beauty; so he failed to detect what would later be called hidden bigotry.

During that fortnight he read Angelique like a book: she seemed gentle, and he was grateful to Madame Bontems for implanting resilient religious principles in her. At the contract-signing Madame Bontems exacted from Granville a solemn promise to respect his daughter’s religious practices: perfect liberty of conscience, freedom to take communion and attend church and confession as she pleased, and no interference with her choice of spiritual advisers. Angelique looked at him with such innocence that he gave his word. The Abbe Fontanon, pale and directing consciences in the household, smiled; Angelique’s slight nod suggested she would not take unfair advantage of this freedom.

A few days after the wedding they moved to Paris, where Granville’s appointment as public prosecutor to the Supreme Court of the Seine circuit recalled him. Angelique used her honeymoon influence to secure a ground-floor apartment at the corner of the Vieille Rue du Temple and the Rue Neuve Saint-Francois, chiefly because it was near the Rue d’Orleans church and a small chapel in the Rue Saint-Louis. She argued the Marais was near the Palais de Justice and the lawyers they knew; a large garden and good stables suited a young couple and possible children. Granville had preferred the Chaussee d’Antin, near theatres and amusements, but yielded.

Granville, absorbed in demanding duties, furnished his study and left house decoration to Angelique. He returned to a cold, arid solemnity: panels, arabesques, dark green velvet benches, an antique lamp, black-and-white marble pavement, and paper imitating mossy stone gave a gloomy impression. Angelique was pleased by a red braid binding the cotton curtains and a strictly decent statue on a Gothic stove; Granville, ashamed of depriving her of his company, lacked courage to challenge her taste, excusing it as provincial naivety rather than admitting that dealers had likely pushed old-fashioned goods upon a tasteless little bigot.

Other rooms were worse. The fashionable school of David had no favor in the house; the square drawing-room remained Louis XV in white and tarnished gold with hideous garlands and military trophies ill-matched with delicate arabesques. Bigotry, with its tiresome humility and pride, hated light, cheerful colors; Angelique seemed to think brown and purple befitted a magistrate’s dignity and feared any luxury that might suggest evil. Granville despaired, but seeing her regret at his lack of enthusiasm, mistook it for affection rather than wounded self-conceit.

“Happiness, dear Angelique, does not depend on a more or less elegant piece of furniture; it depends on the wife’s sweetness, gentleness, and love.”

“Why, it is my duty to love you,” she replied mildly, “and I can have no more delightful duty to carry out.”

From April, when married, until early winter they lived in apparent union. Love and work made Granville indifferent to many household matters. If on a Friday he found Lenten fare and asked for meat, Angelique, scrupulous in truthfulness, would cloak refusal under some pretext and scold the cook. Young lawyers did not keep every fast; Granville did not at first notice the regularity of Lenten meals, often disguised with tasteful fish and game. Unconsciously he lived in strict orthodoxy. On weekdays he did not know whether she went to Mass; on Sundays he accompanied her more from indulgence than conviction. Young love made him less exacting about amusements, so he failed to perceive the coldness and reserve of a woman who might be a bigot waiting for love in folded arms.

He believed himself happy until November 1808, when the Canon of Bayeux, who had once governed Madame Bontems’ and Angelique’s consciences, came to Paris. Horrified to find Angelique so changed, he tried to reclaim her to his chilly fold. Frightened by this priest of inflexible Jansenist bigotry, Angelique returned to penance and renounced her Parisian ways.

It is tedious to detail every circumstance that wrecked the household; a few facts suffice. The first serious misunderstanding involved society invitations. Angelique accepted solemn functions but long declined balls, excusing herself with headaches. One evening Granville, impatient with these assumed indispositions, destroyed a written invitation to a Councillor of State’s ball and gave only a verbal one; seeing her health sound, he took her. On returning he reprimanded her.

“My dear,” he said, “your position, your rank, and fortune impose duties which no divine law can relieve you from. Are you not your husband’s pride? You are required to go to balls when I go, and to appear becomingly.”

“And what is so disastrous in my dress?”

“It is your manner,” he answered. “When a young man approaches you look so serious that a spiteful person might believe you doubtful of your own virtue. You seem to fear lest a smile should undo you. The world, my dearest, is not a convent.—But as you mentioned your dress, I may confess it is a duty to conform to Society’s customs and fashions.”

“Do you wish that I should display my shape like those indecent women who wear gowns so low that impudent eyes can stare at their bare shoulders and their—”

“There is a difference, my dear,” he interrupted. “You wear three rows of net frills that cover your throat to your chin. You look as if you had desired your dressmaker to destroy the graceful line of your shoulders and bosom. Your bust is wrapped in so many folds that everyone was laughing at your affectation of prudery. You would be grieved if I repeated the ill-natured remarks about your appearance.”

“Those who admire such obscenity will not have to bear the burden if we sin,” she retorted.

“And you did not dance?” he asked.

“I shall never dance,” she replied.

“If I tell you that you ought to dance!” he said sharply. “Yes, you ought to follow fashions, to wear flowers in your hair and diamonds. Remember, my dear, that rich people—and we are rich—are obliged to keep up luxury in the State. Is it not far better to encourage manufacturers than distribute money as alms through the clergy?”

“You talk as a statesman!” said Angelique.

“And you as a priest,” he retorted.

Their argument grew bitter. Her sweet voice hid obstinacy born of priestly influence. When she appealed to rights secured by his promise, she insisted her director forbade balls; Granville pointed out the priest overstepped Church regulations. Theological quarrels flared when Granville proposed the play; in defiance Angelique wrote to Rome: could a woman wear low gowns and go to the play and balls without risking salvation?

Pope Pius VII replied strongly, condemning his daughter’s recalcitrancy and blaming the priest. His letter, almost Fenelon-like on conjugal duties, said a wife is right to go wherever her husband takes her and that if she sins by his command she is not ultimately answerable. These words only made Angelique and her director accuse him of irreligion.

Before the papal letter arrived, Granville had discovered his wife’s strict fasting and ordered his servants to serve him meat every day. Of all tyrannies, the most odious is that which robs a man of the merit of his freely chosen deeds. Soon Granville stopped inviting friends or giving dinners; the house might as well have been shrouded. A mistress’s bigotry creates its own atmosphere: servants chosen for piety wore sanctimonious faces; bigots know one another and recommended like-minded servants from household to household. Such homes show an appearance of avarice, mystery, and the dank scent of cold incense. Methodical meanness and narrowness—Bigotry—marked every object: furniture, prints, faces; speech and silence alike were bigoted. Stiffness and formality infected everything; the lady perched on a throne of ice.

One morning Granville perceived with grief that bigotry had invaded his home. Different causes produce similar effects: dulness hedges these houses with brass walls, enclosing a desert of horrors. The home became not a tomb but a worse thing—a convent. In this icy center the lawyer studied his wife dispassionately. He saw narrow-mindedness in her low hair and a rigid regularity in her features that soon made him hate the assumed sweetness that had bewitched him. Intuition warned those thin lips might one day say, “My dear, it is for your good!”

Angelique’s complexion grew pallid and austere; beauty without expression became an imposture. Her imperturbable smile seemed a formula of happiness; her charity offended. She acted from duty, not feeling. Some faults a wife may yield to experience, but false religious ideas are immutable. The apotheosis of egotism—Self beyond the grave—triumphs over all. Thus even the Pope was censured at the priest’s tribunal by the young devotee. To be always in the right consumed every other feeling in these tyrannous souls.

A secret struggle had been going on, and Granville soon wearied of a battle without end. What man can endure hypocritical affection and categorical resistance to his slightest wishes? What is to be done with a wife who uses his passion to protect her coldness, who prepares ecstatically to play martyr, and regards her husband as a scourge from God? Women who make virtue hateful by defying Saint John’s “Love one another” are hard to bear.

If a bonnet condemned to remain in a milliner’s window existed, Granville would be certain to see it on his wife’s head; if a hideous material was found, she would select it. Lack of taste seemed inseparable from false pietism. Thus in a home needing fullest sympathy Granville had no true companionship. He went alone to parties and theatres. Nothing in his house appealed to him. A huge Crucifix hung between his bed and Angelique’s, figurative of his destiny: a Man-God killed in the prime of life and beauty. The ivory of that cross was less cold than Angelique crucifying her husband under the plea of virtue. One Ash Wednesday, the pale form of Fasting and Total Abstinence rose—and Granville did not deem it wise to appeal to Rome again.

His misfortune was too great to complain: he had a pretty, dutiful wife who was virtuous—a model. She bore a child every year, nursed them herself, and raised them in strict principles; the old women of her circle admired her piety and called her a martyr. They blamed not the wife’s scruples but the husband’s barbarous philoprogenitiveness.

Granville, worn by work and deprived of conjugal consolation, had by thirty-two sunk into despair. Not wishing to set an example of dissipation, he tried to deaden feeling by hard study and began a great book on Law. But he could not find monastic peace. When Angelique saw him forsake society to work at home, she wept thinking he might die impenitent and tried to snatch him from eternal fires, making him the object of mean ideas, vacuous arguments, and narrow views. This was the last straw. What can be more intolerable than a blind struggle between a bigot’s obstinacy and a lawyer’s acumen? Granville neglected his home. Everything became unbearable. His children, broken by their mother’s frigid despotism, dared not go with him to the play; he could never give them pleasure without punishment from their terrible mother. His loving nature withered into indifference, a selfishness worse than death. He saved his boys by sending them early to school and insisting on his right to train them; he seldom interfered with daughters, resolving they should marry as soon as possible.

Had he sought violent measures he would have found no social justification; his wife, backed by dowagers, would have condemned him publicly. Thus Granville had no choice but to live in complete isolation; crushed by misery, he avoided intimacy with worldly women and had no hope of consolation.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Between 1806 and 1825 nothing remarkable disturbed the slow decline of Madame de Granville’s household. After losing her husband’s affection, Madame remained unchanged: devout, penitent, and increasingly anguished. She paid for Masses and implored God and the saints to reveal what had displeased him and how to win him back; yet the more fervent her prayers, the less Granville was seen at home.

After securing a high judicial post, the Comte de Granville took to living in the entresol for about five years to avoid his wife. Every morning a ritual played out—so mechanical it might have been rehearsed: at eight a prim housekeeper knocked at his door, was admitted to the room next to his study, and relayed the same inquiry to the footman in the same tone: whether Monsieur le Comte had slept well and whether he would honor Madame la Comtesse with his company at breakfast. The valet, after consulting his master, returned the same ceremonious reply: Monsieur begged her excuse; important business required his attendance at court. When the woman asked to see the Count before he left, the reply was always that he had already gone—though often his carriage still waited. The formula became so routine that sometimes the servant, a favorite of Granville’s and the cause of quarrels due to his irreligious conduct, would enter the master’s room when the Count was absent and come back with the identical message.

Madame de Granville watched his comings and goings like an embodiment of remorse. She had the small aggressiveness of a monastic temperament; though thirty-five she looked forty. When decency forced her to speak to her husband or to dine at home she launched acid-sweet remarks and inflicted the intolerable dulness of her narrow circle, seeking to put him in the wrong before servants and charitable friends.

When Granville was offered the presidency of a provincial court—a post that could lead to a peerage—he begged to remain in Paris. That refusal, known only to the Keeper of the Seals, provoked conjecture among the Countess’s intimates and her confessor. Granville, a wealthy scion of a leading Norman family who earned a hundred thousand francs a year, abandoning his great treatise on Law and the prospect of promotion to live dissipatedly for six years, seemed inexplicable. The Countess’s confessor, a man who eyed a bishopric and relied on family influence as much as on service, insinuated calumnies: perhaps Granville feared provincial life because it would require a regular moral life with his wife—unless some illicit connection had alienated him. The sanctimonious Countess accepted these hints; slowly they destroyed her remaining illusions.

Ignorant of the world and of love’s follies, Angélique could not conceive that marriage might be other than what she believed. When her husband ceased to demand anything of her, she supposed the calm natural; but the priest’s suggestions undermined her convictions. She defended him but could not erase the suspicion so cleverly sown. These alarms wrecked her fragile mind and made her ill; a low fever consumed her. During Lent 1822 she would not omit her austerities and fell into a dangerous decline. Granville’s attention, when he gave any, was perfunctory—more like that of a nephew obliged to an old uncle than the care of a husband.

Although she had abandoned nagging and tried to greet him with affection, the sharpness of her bigotry could undo any progress by a single speech. By late May, spring and better food had restored some strength. One morning after Mass she sat on a garden bench, the sun recalling early married days, and reviewed the years to locate her failures as wife and mother. She was interrupted by the Abbé Fontanon, agitated.

“You must prepare yourself,” he told her. “For seven years Monsieur Granville has lived in sin with a concubine, by whom he has two children; on this adulterous connection he has spent more than five hundred thousand francs that ought to have belonged to his legitimate family.”

Angélique’s first reaction was incredulity. “I must see it to believe it!” she cried, but the Abbé insisted she must forgive and wait in patience and prayer until God enlightened her husband—unless she chose to use remedies offered by human laws. The prodigious accusation and the way it was delivered produced a sudden change in her. She dismissed the priest, summoned her servants—alarmed by her flushed face and frantic energy—ordered a carriage, countermanded it, changed her mind repeatedly; but at last, about three in the afternoon and resolved, she drove off toward Rue Taitbout.

She reached the house of Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille. There, Mademoiselle wept; Roger—who stood beside her—held one of her hands and was silent, looking from little Charles to a cot where Eugénie slept and to Caroline, whose cheer had been clouded by sorrow. Roger confessed the essential secret: he was married. His wife had been given over since the previous March; he did not wish her dead, but if God took her, he believed she would be happier in Paradise than in life, where she was indifferent to both grief and pleasure. Caroline’s initial hatred of the woman who had made Roger unhappy gave way to grateful tears—for that unhappiness had indirectly led to her own happiness. Roger, mindful of his wife’s confessor and the obstacles that priestly influence could raise, spoke of fleeing to Italy if necessary to preserve happiness.

A shriek from the adjoining room suddenly interrupted them. Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille rushed in and found Madame de Granville in a dead faint. When she revived, supported by the Count and the rival whom she instinctively repelled, she sighed and, roused, was taken by Granville to the carriage; he got in with her.

“Who has brought you to the point of wishing me dead, of resolving to fly?” she demanded, grief and indignation mingling. “Was I not young? You thought me pretty—what fault have you to find with me? Have I been false? Have I not been virtuous? My heart cherished no image but yours, my ears listened to no other voice. What duty have I failed in?”

“Happiness, madame,” said the Count severely. He then delivered a sustained reproach framed as doctrine: there are two ways of serving God. Some believe that strict prayers, regular Mass, and avoiding sin will secure Heaven; yet, he argued, they often lack true love of God and are hard on neighbors, seeing law’s letter but not its spirit. He accused her of treating him—the earthly husband—in that manner: sacrificing his happiness to what she took for salvation. He went on to say that another, a more loving woman, had dared to make the sacrifices his wife had refused.

The Countess cried out, asking whether all her prayers and penances had availed to win his love or atone for their sins. “Of what avail is virtue?” she lamented. Granville answered as if lecturing: virtue wins Heaven; but a woman cannot be both a husband’s wife and the spouse of Christ—that would be double devotion. By stripping her soul of earthly love and devotion that God commands him to have for him, she had cherished hatred instead.

“Have I not loved you?” she protested.

“No, madame,” he replied bluntly. When she asked what love was, Granville offered a bitter definition—an ironic contrast to her sanctimony: love is to yield to caprices, to guess them, to find pleasure in pain, to sacrifice the world’s opinion, pride, and even religion for the beloved—“that is love,” he declared, rhetorically distancing it from the Countess’s austere piety. She, horrified, compared such affection to the fleeting love of ballet-girls: flames that leave only ashes and despair. A wife, she insisted, should bring friendship and steady warmth; Granville retorted with sardonic contempt, comparing her spiritual coldness to a bleak Norman sky and dismissing her warmth as nonexistent.

When she accused him of dissipating his children’s fortune, he corrected her: Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille was rich by her own family’s grant; his uncle had given her the Bellefeuille estate out of friendship while alive. As for other expenditures, he owed them to his uncle’s liberality. He then struck a political note to wound her further: she had forgotten that her own father had been one of the Jacobins she scorned; at the time when her father, Citizen Bontems, signed death-warrants, his uncle had been doing France good service. The rebuke stung the sanctimonious Countess into silence, though her jealousy stirred anew as she murmured, “How can a woman thus destroy her own soul and that of others?”

The Count, tired of the dialogue, replied that she might one day have to answer why a woman destroys souls. He claimed not to hate her, but to hate those who had perverted her heart and reason. He acknowledged her prayers, acknowledged that Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille had given him her heart and crowned his life with love; Angélique should have been his mistress and prayerful saint by turns. He defended himself against charges of being a reprobate: his life had not been sordid; after seven years of wretchedness, a craving for happiness had led him, by an imperceptible descent, to love another woman and make a second home—a situation he represented as common among husbands.

Madame de Granville, overwhelmed, pleaded that if her death would make him happy, she begged God to take her. “If you had always breathed such admirable sentiments and devotion, we should be happy yet,” he said coldly. She begged forgiveness and vowed to obey and to be henceforth all he could wish. But he warned that he might find the cruel courage to tell her he no longer loved her, and that one cannot command the heart or instantly erase fifteen years of suffering: he had ceased to love. Esteem, respect, and friendship might be won back, but love, once extinguished, would not blossom again—especially not for a woman he judged too old to respond to it.

She replied that she hoped he would never hear such words from the woman he loved. He changed the subject abruptly: “Will you put on a dress à la Grecque this evening and come to the Opera?” The Countess’s shudder at that suggestion was a mute, devastating reply.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Early in December 1833 a white‑haired man whose face bore the traces of grief walked at midnight along the Rue Gaillon. At a modest two‑story house he paused to look up at an attic window where a dim, wavering light shone through panes, some patched with paper. He watched like any idle Parisian when a young man came out and, seeing him, approached.

“What, is it you, Monsieur le Président? Alone at this hour?” the young man said, offering his arm.

“I am no more than fifty‑five,” the Comte de Granville replied with bitter irony. “A physician of your fame must know a man of that age is still hale.”

“You are here to watch a lady at work, then?” Horace Bianchon teased. “You do not often go about Paris on foot.”

“Sometimes I walk when not visiting,” the Count answered. “And I have for some time observed, at that third‑floor window of your house, the shadow of a person working with heroic constancy.”

He stopped as if seized by pain. “I take as much interest in that garret as a citizen in the finishing of the Palais Royal. For nine years—no, I will not say friend. I hate everything that smells of sentiment. The events of life have taught me disbelief in all human affection; I grew old within a few days. I will no longer attach myself to any creature but unreasoning animals or plants. I abhor life and the world. Nothing can move or interest me.”

“You have children?” Bianchon asked.

“My children!” the Count repeated bitterly. “My eldest daughter is the Comtesse de Vandenesse. The others will make their matches. My sons have succeeded—one is President of the Court at Orléans, the other public prosecutor in Paris. They have their cares. If one of their hearts had been devoted to me, perhaps the void would be less. But why should they? They may only be waiting for my money.”

“O Monsieur le Comte, how could you think so—one who are kind and humane?” protested Bianchon.

“To please myself,” Granville admitted. “I pay for a sensation as I would pay to recover some childish illusion. I help my fellow creatures for my own sake, as I gamble; I look for no gratitude. I should see you die without blinking, and beg you to feel the same for me. Life has swept over my heart like Vesuvius over Herculaneum—the town is there dead.”

“You have a malady which you ought to allow me to treat,” the doctor said, earnestly.

“Do you know a cure for death?” Granville retorted.

“I undertake to revive the heart you believe frozen.”

“You are a match for Talma, then?” the Count taunted.

“No, Monsieur le Comte. Nature is. Listen: the garret you interest yourself in is inhabited by a woman of about thirty whose love has become fanatic. She adores a young man of pleasing looks but every vice—a gambler given to wine and women, who has committed acts punishable by law. To him this woman has sacrificed comfort, a man who worshiped her, and the father of her children. She has let him squander a fortune; she works night and day to feed him. Many times she has silently seen him rob her of money saved for the children’s clothes and food. Three days ago she sold her beautiful hair; he demanded the gold. For a smile, for a kiss, she gives up a fortnight’s peace. It is dreadful and yet sublime. The work is hollowing her cheeks. Her children’s crying has broken her heart; she is ill and at this moment on her wretched bed. This evening they had nothing to eat; the children were too weak to cry—they were silent when I went up.”

Bianchon stopped; the Count, despite himself, had slipped his hand into his waistcoat pocket. At the name the Count shuddered and gripped the doctor’s arm.

“Her name is Caroline Crochard?” he asked, voice broken.

“Then you know her?” Bianchon said, astonished.

“And the wretch’s name is Solvet.—Ay, you have kept your word!” Granville cried. “You have roused my heart to the most terrible pain. That emotion, too, is a gift from hell, and I always know how to pay those debts.”

They reached the corner of the Rue de la Chaussée d’Antin where a night‑walker with a basket stood by the curb. The man’s shriveled, comic face might have come from a caricature.

“Do you ever pick up a thousand‑franc note?” the Count asked.

“Now and then, master.”

“And you restore them?”

“It depends on the reward.”

“You’re the man for me,” cried Granville, thrusting the scavenger a thousand‑franc note. “Take this, but remember: I give it to you on condition you spend it at the wineshop, get drunk, fight, beat your wife, blacken your friends’ eyes. That will give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist—perhaps to the police, the public prosecutor, the judge, and the prison warders. Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you.”

Pointing out the scavenger’s hideous physiognomy to the stupefied doctor, the Count added, “Now I have squared accounts with hell, and had some pleasure for my money. As for Caroline Crochard—she may die of hunger and thirst, hearing the heartrending shrieks of her starving children, convinced of the baseness of the man she loves. I will not give a sou to rescue her; and because you have helped her, I will see you no more—”

With that he left, walking briskly to the Rue Saint‑Lazare. He soon reached his small house where a carriage waited. A man‑servant announced, “Monsieur, your son, the attorney‑general, came about an hour since and is waiting for you in your bedroom.”

Granville dismissed the servant and entered. Seeing his son, he asked sharply, “What motive can be strong enough to make you break my order that my children not come to me unless I send for them?”

“Father,” the son answered tremulously, “I venture to hope you will forgive me when you have heard me.”

“Sit down,” the Count said, pointing to a chair. “Speak without heeding me.”

“Father,” the son continued, “this afternoon, at four o’clock, a very young man who was arrested in the house of a friend of mine, whom he had robbed to a considerable extent, appealed to you. He says he is your son.”

“His name?” asked the Count.

“Charles Crochard.”

“That will do,” the father said with an imperious wave.

Granville paced the room in solemn silence; his son said nothing further. At last the Count spoke in a voice softened and fatherly, startling the young lawyer.

“Charles Crochard spoke the truth. I am glad you came to‑night, my good Eugène,” he said. He handed over a bundle of banknotes. “Here is a considerable sum; use it as you think proper in this matter. I trust you implicitly and approve any arrangements you may make. Eugène, my dear son, kiss me. We part perhaps for the last time. I shall to‑morrow crave my dismissal from the King, and I am going to Italy.

“Though a father owes no account of his life to his children, he is bound to bequeath the experience Fate sells him—that is part of their inheritance. When you marry,” he went on with an involuntary shudder, “do not undertake it lightly; study the character of the woman who is to be your partner; consult me—I will judge her myself. A lack of union between husband and wife, from whatever cause, leads to terrible misfortune; sooner or later we are always punished for contravening the social law.—But I will write to you on this subject from Florence. A father who presides over a supreme court must not have to blush in the presence of his son. Goodbye.”
