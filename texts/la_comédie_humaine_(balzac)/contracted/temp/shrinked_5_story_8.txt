Dedication
Dedicated to Comtesse Clara Maffei.

I
In September 1835 Mademoiselle du Rouvre married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a Polish exile. Although her father mismanaged the family fortune, Clémentine inherited wealth from her mother’s side. Initially dismissed as frivolous, Adam won respect after buying a house in the rue de la Pépinière, which raised their social standing.

By 1836 the couple figured prominently in Parisian society, living lavishly. Clémentine became curious about Captain Thaddeus Paz, who managed their affairs. Adam described Paz as a noble from a poor branch of his family: loyal, devoted, and instrumental in sustaining their lifestyle despite limited income. Paz prioritized Adam’s needs and their friendship crossed social boundaries; Clémentine wished to include him in the family.

Clémentine insisted Paz join them for dinner and the opera, though his reserve annoyed Adam. Paz harbored unrequited love for Clémentine while secretly stabilizing the household amid Adam’s financial carelessness. As Clémentine fulfilled social obligations, Paz tried to win her attention with stories at dinner. Aware of female rivalries in Paris, she nonetheless occasionally thought of him and, as their interactions deepened, invited him again—beginning to see him differently.

Complicating matters, Paz became infatuated with a circus performer, Malaga, inventing a tale to mask his feelings for Clémentine. Caring for Adam during an illness, he suppressed his emotions; during Carnival he proposed a night out with Malaga, knowing Clémentine might be there. After she confronted him, Clémentine demanded he end the liaison.

Paz then received a commission as captain in an expedition to Khiva and decided to leave Paris. Only after his departure did Clémentine recognize her longing; Paz disclosed his love in a heartfelt letter. Three years later she remained intrigued, the story underscoring the complexities of love, loyalty, and social expectation in 19th-century Paris.
