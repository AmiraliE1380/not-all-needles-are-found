In September 1835 Clémentine du Rouvre, the Marquis du Rouvre's only daughter, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a Polish exile. Her father had squandered his fortune, but settlements from her mother provided a steady annual income.

Adam, of an illustrious Polish family and not a charge on France, faced Parisian prejudice: Polish nobility was poorly regarded and often mistaken for exiled students. The émigré community was split between republican and noble exiles, and he was initially the butt of local humor.

After buying a house in the rue de la Pépinière in 1834, by 1836 Adam was established in Parisian society; their home's mixed architectural styles and fine decor reflected rising bourgeois tastes.

Clémentine noticed Captain Paz, a discreet factotum who ran everything from finances to the stables while remaining socially inconspicuous. Adam said Paz came from a poor branch of his family, had fought for Poland, and was a friend forged by hardship. Paz’s refusal of a generous gift revealed loyalty, pride, and mutual respect. As they prepared to go out, Clémentine’s affection for both men grew, showing how personal ties could transcend social norms.

Thaddeus lived reclusively in Paris until the Comtesse Clémentine Laginska was seen riding with him in the Bois de Boulogne. She insisted he dine with them; he dressed for the occasion but behaved like a subordinate, answering little and listening to Clémentine. Adam urged him to be more lively. Though outwardly dull, Thaddeus was acutely aware of his feelings for Clémentine and struggled to hide them.

At the Opera he remained distant; Clémentine felt slighted and Adam excused Thaddeus as disagreeable. His reserve sprang from unrequited love, intensified after Clémentine’s return from a trip. He took a bittersweet pleasure in being indispensable to their household, conscious that it would falter without him even as he suffered inwardly.

Clémentine, newly confident and focused on her Parisian standing, had little room for him. Once, noticing him while driving home, she impulsively invited him along—an invitation that flustered him. Their interactions were charged with unspoken emotion: Thaddeus felt gratitude and longing but kept his distance. Routine soon resumed; he found solace in managing their affairs while reflecting on the depth and pain of his love.

When Clémentine hosted a reception, Thaddeus’s absence went unnoticed. He retreated into solitary habits, content in silent devotion yet painfully aware that his love was both joy and suffering. He admired her from afar, cherishing small moments of her happiness. Clémentine’s life became a constant struggle for attention; distracted by rivals and the strain of maintaining status, she increasingly neglected Adam. Thaddeus remained a steadfast presence, quietly overseeing things while wrestling with his feelings.

In May, as Clémentine prepared to visit her uncle’s estate, she saw Thaddeus again and insisted he join them for dinner. He hesitated but tried to engage despite shyness and duty. As the evening progressed her interest in him grew and their conversation hinted at a deeper connection; he felt momentarily emboldened, yet loyalty to Adam restrained him.

After dinner Clémentine voiced dissatisfaction with Adam and a desire for more passion. Torn between love and friendship, Thaddeus felt urged to confess but held back. To deflect, he invented a romance with Malaga, a circus performer—an exotic, simple foil. Visiting Malaga became an escape: he provided for her and attended to her comforts without a real relationship. Misunderstandings followed, and when Malaga sent a despairing letter longing for his companionship it struck him, underscoring the constancy of his devotion to Clémentine and the sacrifices it demanded.

Clementine returned home more beautiful than ever. After dinner the count and countess discussed Thaddeus, who had left a letter from Malaga with Adam. Adam regretted Thaddeus’s misfortune, fearing association with a circus performer would cost him status; Clementine scorned such women, while Adam defended Thaddeus, citing his financial sacrifices for Malaga.

Paz, who had asked Adam for money to help Malaga, was seen by the countess as a threat to Adam’s stability. Although worried, Adam believed Thaddeus would abandon Malaga if it suited him; the countess doubted it.

Paz noticed a change in Clementine’s manner and felt her contempt, which plunged him into despair. Still, he took quiet pleasure in admiring her at gatherings and devoted himself to her comfort and the household. His love was deep; feeling unworthy, he fell ill from anguish before recovering.

Adam lost a large sum gambling and asked Thaddeus to cover it by blaming Malaga. Thaddeus reluctantly agreed, burdened by obligations. When Clementine discovered his generosity toward Paz, she expressed frustration and questioned his loyalty. Overwhelmed by her expectations and managing her affairs, Thaddeus resolved to teach her household management so she could be independent.

In spring Adam fell gravely ill. Thaddeus nursed him devotedly while Clementine sought the best medical care. Through his attention Adam was kept alive; he slowly improved and praised Thaddeus. Tormented by unrequited love and convinced staying in Paris would prolong his pain, Thaddeus left for the Caucasus after writing Clementine a heartfelt letter confessing his feelings and sacrifices. Clementine and Adam never fully grasped his devotion; years later she wondered about his fate, his memory remaining a bittersweet, unfulfilled love.
