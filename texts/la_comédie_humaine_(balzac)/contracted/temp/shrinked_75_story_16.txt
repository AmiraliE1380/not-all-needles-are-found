**The Deserted Woman**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage*  

**DEDICATION**  
To Her Grace the Duchesse d’Abrantes, from her devoted servant, Honoré de Balzac. Paris, August 1835.

---

In spring 1822, Parisian doctors sent a young man, M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil, to Lower Normandy for recovery from an illness caused by overwork or excess. He settled in Bayeux, welcomed by relatives who appreciated the novelty of his presence.

Bayeux, like many small towns, had its own social hierarchy. Gaston quickly met the local elite, characterized by a mix of ancient nobility and newly wealthy families. The first group included a family with a long but obscure lineage, whose patriarch was a proud sportsman, dismissive of modernity and the new political order. His wife, loud and traditional, believed their daughters would secure wealth through their name alone.

In contrast, a wealthier family, less noble but more fashionable, spent winters in Paris, returning with contemporary trends. The husband was politically savvy, while the wife was conscious of her status, though often behind the latest fashions. Their son, entitled to the estate, did little, while the father engaged in local politics and investments.

The local bishop, a former Vicar-General, navigated the tensions between these two families, respected for his religious position but aware of his plebeian roots. Other local figures included retired military officers and their families, who maintained a semblance of aristocracy while discussing politics and gossiping over games.

This social circle also included wealthy townspeople who had gained entry into the aristocratic fold, often patronized by older spinsters. A few ecclesiastics were welcomed for their wit, adding a bourgeois element to the otherwise noble gatherings.

The conversations among these groups were predictable, steeped in tradition and resistant to change. They critiqued Parisian society while simultaneously upholding their own rigid standards. Gaston, upon his arrival, was scrutinized and accepted based on his family’s reputation and wealth, which included an income of eighteen thousand livres and expectations of inheriting a chateau.

Despite his promising background, Gaston’s elder brother, who was consumptive and likely to die soon, was conveniently omitted from discussions about the family. Initially, Gaston found amusement in the quirks of his new acquaintances, sketching their eccentricities and enjoying their quaintness. However, he soon grew weary of their monotonous lives, feeling trapped in a cloistered existence devoid of intellectual stimulation.

As time passed, Gaston began to adapt to this stifling environment, losing touch with the vibrant life he once knew in Paris. The lack of mental engagement dulled his senses, and he risked becoming complacent, content to live among these provincial fossils, reminiscent of Ulysses’ companions, resigned to a life of mediocrity.

One evening, Gaston de Nueil found himself in a provincial drawing-room, surrounded by card tables and family portraits. As he digested a sumptuous dinner, he began to appreciate the simplicity of provincial life, contrasting it with the chaotic pleasures of Paris. His thoughts shifted when he overheard a conversation about Mme. de Beauseant, a woman of noble birth who had fallen into disgrace due to a scandal involving M. d’Ajuda-Pinto. The dowager and the local nobleman discussed her isolation, hinting at the societal disapproval she faced as a separated wife.

Intrigued, Gaston learned that Mme. de Beauseant had retreated to Normandy, shunned by society despite her noble lineage. He felt a growing fascination for her, viewing her as a symbol of romance and mystery amidst the dullness of provincial life. This newfound interest ignited dormant desires within him, contrasting sharply with the mundane company around him.

Gaston became preoccupied with thoughts of how to meet Mme. de Beauseant, recognizing the challenges posed by her seclusion and social status. He wandered near her residence, contemplating how to introduce himself without appearing foolish. Despite his self-doubt, he concocted a plan to enlist the help of M. de Champignelles, a local nobleman, to facilitate an introduction.

Gaston approached M. de Champignelles, claiming he had a delicate matter to discuss with the Vicomtesse. He asked the Marquis to inquire if she would receive him, emphasizing the importance of discretion. The Marquis, flattered by the request, agreed to help. When he relayed the message to Mme. de Beauseant, she was initially hesitant but intrigued enough to reconsider after some deliberation.

As Gaston awaited the outcome, he felt a mix of excitement and anxiety about the impending meeting. He meticulously prepared for his visit, believing that his appearance would influence her perception of him. Meanwhile, Mme. de Beauseant also took care with her appearance, wanting to make a good impression.

When Gaston finally arrived at Courcelles, he felt a rush of emotions. The moment he entered the room, he was struck by the intimacy of being alone with her, a stark contrast to the crowded gatherings he was used to. Despite his nervousness, he was determined to make a meaningful connection with the enigmatic Vicomtesse.

A fire crackled in the hearth, casting a warm glow over the room where a young woman, the Vicomtesse de Beauseant, sat gracefully in a low chair. Her movements were fluid and charming, embodying the elegance of a high-born lady. As she stirred the fire and reached for a fallen glove, her delicate beauty captivated M. de Nueil, who had recently returned from a dull exile in Normandy. In her presence, he felt transported back to the vibrancy of Paris, where her fair hair, dark eyes, and noble demeanor stood in stark contrast to the provincial women he had encountered.

Mme. de Beauseant's beauty was tinged with sadness, hinting at a past filled with joy and love now overshadowed by loneliness. She was neither a wife nor a mother, but rather an outlaw of sorts, haunted by memories of a once-brilliant life. M. de Nueil admired her deeply, feeling both humbled and awed by her charm and misfortune. However, he struggled to find words to express his feelings.

When she acknowledged him, he felt the weight of his previous deception regarding a message he was meant to deliver. Overcome with shame, he confessed his wrongdoing, but she responded with cold disdain, summoning a servant to escort him out. As he left, he realized that departing meant losing any chance of returning to her presence, and he hesitated, contemplating the implications of his actions.

In a moment of resolve, he turned back, claiming he had forgotten something. This time, he entered the salon unannounced, catching Mme. de Beauseant off guard. She invited him to sit, and he seized the opportunity, his eyes shining with hope. She, too, felt a flicker of delight at his presence, recognizing the joy he found in her company.

However, she quickly reminded him of the gravity of their situation. She questioned his motives for visiting, suspecting curiosity or pity rather than genuine interest. Her past weaknesses did not define her, and she expressed disdain for any sympathy he might offer. Instead, she sought to maintain her dignity, urging him not to return, as their association could lead to scandal.

M. de Nueil acknowledged his youthful folly and explained his longing for connection, describing the weariness of his isolation and how he had idealized her as the embodiment of his dreams. He painted a picture of a heart yearning for love, hoping to evoke her understanding and compassion. His sincerity began to soften her stance, as she recognized the genuine emotions behind his intrusion.

Their conversation unfolded with a mix of tension and vulnerability, revealing the complexities of their feelings. Mme. de Beauseant, while still guarded, found herself touched by his earnestness, and the atmosphere shifted from one of cold dismissal to a tentative connection, hinting at the possibility of deeper understanding between them.

################################################
An impassioned voice filled the chill solitude, bringing warmth and youthful charm. Mme. de Beauseant, stirred by the genuine expression of M. de Nueil, admired his noble countenance, untouched by the world's cruelties. Gaston, in the bloom of youth, captivated her with his sincerity, while she saw in him a rare man, untainted by ambition or vanity. Both felt dangerous reflections about each other, concealing their thoughts. M. de Nueil recognized the Vicomtesse as a woman whose beauty was eclipsed by her depth of feeling and goodness, while she perceived in him the ideal of a man unspoiled by the egoism of society.

As they delved into their sentiments, Gaston’s explorations were instinctive, while Mme. de Beauseant had a purpose, seeking to understand his views by presenting opposing opinions. Her wit and grace made her feel at ease, leading Gaston to exclaim, “Oh! madame, how could any man have left you?” This heartfelt remark surprised her, reviving her sense of innocence and condemning the world for her past. It offered her a chance to rise above her fall, and she reflected on the implications of their connection.

She expressed her reluctance to see him again, explaining that any suspicion of a second trespass would tarnish her reputation. She had endured a forced marriage and sought happiness, believing it was natural to pursue joy. Her past love had left her forsaken, and she had come to accept her blame, realizing that her devotion had not been enough to keep him. Pain had taught her to guard her heart, and she acknowledged the agony of her past, which had left her feeling like a slave to love.

Mme. de Beauseant, eloquent and beautiful, justified her solitude, stirring deep emotions in Gaston. He longed to express his feelings but feared rejection. Despite his internal struggle, he confessed his desire to make her forget her pain and to devote his life to her happiness. However, she reminded him that they had gone too far and that they must remain strangers, emphasizing the pain of being unable to follow the one they love through life’s phases.

As she rose to leave, she smiled, hinting at the unexpected nature of their encounter. Gaston, feeling distant from her, questioned whether her charm was merely a display of coquetry. He returned home, consumed by conflicting emotions, recognizing that Mme. de Beauseant had become essential to his existence. He spent a restless night, oscillating between despair and hope, ultimately deciding to write her a letter expressing his feelings.

In his letter, he poured out his heart, acknowledging his unworthiness and the depth of his love. He expressed a willingness to leave France if she rejected him but hoped for her friendship. He sought to spend time with her, even if it was fleeting, believing that the joy of her company would sustain him. He promised to respect her decision, even if it meant enduring heartache.

Gaston’s anxiety grew as he imagined the Vicomtesse reading his letter, fearing she would laugh at his vulnerability. He regretted his words, wishing he had expressed himself better. Overwhelmed by his emotions, he struggled to cope, feeling isolated without the support of friends who could distract him from his turmoil.

Ultimately, Gaston’s experience encapsulated the tumultuous nature of youthful love, marked by intense emotions and the fear of rejection. His longing for Mme. de Beauseant became a source of both inspiration and despair, illustrating the complexities of love and the challenges of navigating relationships in a world governed by societal expectations.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Gaston de Nueil received a letter from the Vicomtesse de Beauseant, expressing her disappointment in his youthful infatuation. She explained that while she appreciated his feelings, she could not reciprocate them due to their age difference and her own life experiences. She urged him to focus on his future and not to sacrifice his potential for an illusion of love. Despite her kindness, she firmly stated that she could not love him and advised him to remain in his own country.

In response, Gaston declared his unwavering love and refused to follow her advice, vowing that only death could absolve his feelings for her. He sent his letter to her just as she was leaving for Geneva, prompting him to follow her, driven by the hope that she might still care for him.

Upon reaching Geneva, Gaston found the Vicomtesse settled in a small house by the lake. Their reunion was filled with joy, and they spent three years together in blissful isolation, enjoying each other's company away from societal scrutiny. However, Gaston’s obligations to his family eventually called him back to France after the deaths of his father and brother. The Vicomtesse followed him, purchasing land near his estate, and they continued their life together, creating barriers against the outside world.

For nine years, they lived in happiness, but the reality of their situation loomed over them. The Vicomtesse’s husband, the Marquis de Beauseant, remained alive and healthy, making their relationship socially untenable. Gaston’s mother, a strict woman, would never accept the Vicomtesse, and the Marquise understood that this would create tension in their lives.

As time passed, a young heiress, Mlle. de la Rodiere, moved into the area, causing the Vicomtesse to question Gaston’s loyalty and future. In a heartfelt letter, she expressed her fears about their age difference and the sacrifices he had made for her. She worried that he might feel burdened by their relationship and considered the possibility that he might want to leave her for a more conventional life.

The Vicomtesse’s letter revealed her deep love for Gaston, but also her anguish over the potential harm she might cause him by holding him back. She implored him to be honest about his feelings and whether he desired freedom from their bond. Her vulnerability and love shone through as she acknowledged the pain of possibly losing him.

Meanwhile, Gaston, upon receiving her letter, felt overwhelmed by the weight of her words and the complexity of their situation. He recognized the difficulty of their love, caught between societal expectations and their profound connection. As he read her letter, he realized the depth of her feelings and the precariousness of their happiness, leaving him in a state of turmoil as he contemplated their future together.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Gaston de Nueil was torn between his mother’s wishes for him to marry Mlle. de la Rodiere and his love for Mme. de Beauseant. His mother emphasized the advantages of marrying Mlle. de la Rodiere, who had a substantial dowry, and urged him to consider his future. Despite his feelings for Mme. de Beauseant, Gaston ultimately decided to break off their relationship and pursue marriage.

As he contemplated this decision, he felt a deep sense of pity for Mme. de Beauseant, realizing the pain it would cause her. He hoped to soften the blow by gradually preparing her for their separation, intending to delay his marriage to Mlle. de la Rodiere until Mme. de Beauseant was more accepting. However, he recognized that a letter would be a painful way to communicate his intentions, especially for someone as perceptive as her.

When Jacques delivered the letter to Mme. de Beauseant, she was filled with dread. The letter contained reassurances, but she sensed the truth: Gaston was distancing himself from her. Overwhelmed by despair, she destroyed the letter, feeling betrayed and heartbroken.

After sending the letter, Gaston returned home only to receive a note from Mme. de Beauseant, expressing her unwavering love and refusal to let him go. This letter was a reminder of their past commitment, and it deeply affected him. Despite this, Gaston married Mlle. de la Rodiere within weeks.

Following his marriage, Gaston appeared apathetic, leading his mother to believe he was happy. His new wife was sweet but lacked depth, and after a few months, Gaston became increasingly withdrawn. He began to secretly communicate with Mme. de Beauseant, asking Jacques to set aside game for her, which he had shot, hoping to maintain a connection.

Eventually, Gaston attempted to reach out to Mme. de Beauseant with a letter, but it was returned unopened. In a moment of desperation, he rushed to her home, only to be met with her anguish. She threatened to throw herself from the window if he approached, and he fled in horror.

Back at home, Gaston wrote another letter, pleading for a response, but when it was returned unopened, he succumbed to despair and took his own life. His suicide shocked those who understood the depth of his love for Mme. de Beauseant, illustrating the profound impact of lost passion and the emptiness of a loveless marriage.

Mme. de Beauseant, unaware of the extent of Gaston’s despair, likely believed she alone would suffer. She maintained her dignity, refusing to accept a compromised position, which highlighted the purity of her love. The story serves as a poignant reminder of the complexities of love, loss, and the consequences of societal expectations. 

In the end, the tale reflects on the nature of true love and the devastating effects of forsaking it for material or social gain, leaving both characters in a state of profound sorrow.
