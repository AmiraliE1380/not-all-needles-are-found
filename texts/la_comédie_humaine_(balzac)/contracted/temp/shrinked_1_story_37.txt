DEDICATION

To Monsieur J. B. Nacquart,
Member of the Royal Academy of Medicine.

Dear Doctor--Here is one of the most carefully hewn stones in the second course of the foundation of a literary edifice which I have slowly and laboriously constructed. I wish to inscribe your name upon it, as much to thank the man whose science once saved me as to honor the friend of my daily life.

De Balzac.

THE LILY OF THE VALLEY

ENVOI

Felix de Vandenesse to Madame la Comtesse Natalie de Manerville:

I yield to your wishes. It is the privilege of the women whom we love more than they love us to make the men who love them ignore the ordinary rules of common-sense. To smooth the frown upon their brow, to soften the pout upon their lips, what obstacles we miraculously overcome! We shed our blood, we risk our future!

You exact the history of my past life; here it is. But remember this, Natalie; in obeying you I crush under foot a reluctance hitherto unconquerable. Why are you jealous of the sudden reveries which overtake me in the midst of our happiness? Why show the pretty anger of a petted woman when silence grasps me? Could you not play upon the contradictions of my character without inquiring into the causes of them? Are there secrets in your heart which seek absolution through a knowledge of mine? Ah! Natalie, you have guessed mine; and it is better you should know the whole truth. Yes, my life is shadowed by a phantom; a word evokes it; it hovers about me and within my soul are solemn memories, buried in its depths like those marine productions seen in calm weather and which the ocean’s storms cast in fragments on the shore.

The mental labor which the expression of ideas necessitates has revived the old, old feelings which give me so much pain when they come suddenly; and if in this confession of my past they break forth in a way that wounds you, remember that you threatened to punish me if I did not obey your wishes, and do not, therefore, punish my obedience. I would that this, my confidence, might increase your love.

Until we meet,

Felix.

CHAPTER I. TWO CHILDHOODS

To what genius fed on tears shall we some day owe that most touching of all elegies,--the tale of tortures borne silently by souls whose tender roots find stony ground in the domestic soil, whose earliest buds are torn apart by rancorous hands, whose flowers are touched by frost at the moment of their blossoming? What poet will sing the sorrows of the child whose lips must suck a bitter breast, whose smiles are checked by the cruel fire of a stern eye? The tale that tells of such poor hearts, oppressed by beings placed about them to promote the development of their natures, would contain the true history of my childhood.

What vanity could I have wounded,--I a child new-born? What moral or physical infirmity caused by mother’s coldness? Was I the child of duty, whose birth is a mere chance, or was I one whose very life was a reproach? Put to nurse in the country and forgotten by my family for over three years, I was treated with such indifference on my return to the parental roof that even the servants pitied me. I do not know to what feeling or happy accident I owed my rescue from this first neglect; as a child I was ignorant of it, as a man I have not discovered it. Far from easing my lot, my brother and my two sisters found amusement in making me suffer. The compact in virtue of which children hide each other’s peccadilloes, and which early teaches them the principles of honor, was null and void in my case; more than that, I was often punished for my brother’s faults, without being allowed to prove the injustice. The fawning spirit which seems instinctive in children taught my brother and sisters to join in the persecutions to which I was subjected, and thus keep in the good graces of a mother whom they feared as much as I. Was this partly the effect of a childish love of imitation; was it from a need of testing their powers; or was it simply through lack of pity? Perhaps these causes united to deprive me of the sweets of fraternal intercourse.

Disinherited of all affection, I could love nothing; yet nature had made me loving. If in many such hearts the crushed feelings turn to hatred, in mine they condensed and hollowed a depth from which, in after years, they gushed forth upon my life. The habit of trembling in some breeds fear and submission; but in my case perpetual tortures developed a certain strength, a habit of moral resistance. Always expecting fresh griefs, my whole being expressed a sullen resignation which smothered the grace and gaiety of childhood and gave me an appearance of idiocy that seemed to justify my mother’s prophecies. The certainty of injustice prematurely roused my pride and, perhaps, checked evil tendencies which such an upbringing might encourage.

Though my mother neglected me she sometimes spoke of my education and seemed desirous of attending to it herself. Cold chills ran through me at such times; daily intercourse with her would have been torture. I blessed the neglect that let me stay alone in the garden to play with pebbles, watch insects, and gaze at the sky. My loneliness led to reverie, but my liking for contemplation was first aroused by an incident that illustrates my early troubles. Once the governess forgot to send me to bed; I crouched under a fig-tree watching a star. My sisters’ play ceased and darkness fell. The governess, Mademoiselle Caroline, knowing where I was, falsely accused me of turning a garden faucet that drenched the other children; I denied it, was punished, and forbidden to stay in the garden after dark. Such tyrannical restraints enhance the forbidden thing’s charm; children think of nothing but what’s forbidden. I was often whipped for my star. Unable to confide in my kind, I told it all my troubles in that delicious inward prattle with which we stammer our first ideas. At twelve, long after school began, I still watched that star with indescribable delight.

My brother Charles, five years older and my parents’ favorite, was robust, with a tutor; I, puny and sickly, became a day pupil at five, taken to school by my father’s valet with a scanty lunch while others enjoyed “rillons.” My comrades teased me and showed off their food, making me feel the difference between my brother and myself. Ridiculed, I sometimes fought but was hated and had no protection. Once I was struck in the back by stones tied in a handkerchief. When the valet told my mother she exclaimed, “That dreadful child! he will always be a torment to us.”

At school I sank into distrust of myself. The boys I liked were scamps, and pride held me aloof. The master, noting my gloom and unfriendliness, confirmed my family’s verdict. When I could read and write, my mother sent me to Pont-le-Voy to the Oratorians’ “class of the Latin steps” for dull lads. There I remained eight years, seeing my family rarely, living the life of a pariah partly because I received but three francs a month pocket-money—barely enough for pens and ink. Unable to buy playthings, I lost admission to games and would not toady the rich. I lived under a tree, reading the books the librarian lent us monthly. How many griefs were in the shadow of that solitude; what genuine anguish filled my neglected life! At the distribution of prizes, where I obtained awards for theme and translation, neither of my parents was present. Instead of kissing the distributor, I burst into tears and threw myself on his breast; that night I burned my crowns in the stove. While the parents of other boys were in town, my parents, though only a few miles away, did not come.

My unhappiness grew as my social spheres widened. I wrote passionate letters to persuade my parents to see me; they mocked my style. I implored my sisters; in vain. As the distribution of prizes neared I doubled my entreaties and expected them with a beating heart. The old porter called my comrades one by one; never did he call my name. One day, accusing myself to my confessor of having cursed my life, he pointed to heaven and recalled the “Beati qui lugent.” From my first communion I dived into prayer, attracted to religious ideas; my ecstasy brought dreams that fed my imagination and strengthened my thinking powers. I attribute those visions to the guardian angel that moulded my spirit, giving me the faculty of seeing the inner soul of things.

My father, doubting the Oratorians, took me from Pont-le-Voy and sent me at fifteen to an institution in the Marais under Monsieur Lepitre. Examined, I, who had been in rhetoric, was placed in the third class. The sufferings of family and school continued in the Lepitre Academy. My father gave no money; Monsieur Lepitre, though sympathetic, could only help modestly. The Lepitre establishment, in the Joyeuse mansion, had a porter named Doisy who breakfasts with the well-to-do pupils and smuggled in luxuries. For long I resisted Doisy’s blandishments, but finally yielded to the temptations of café-au-lait and bread—small dissolutions but mighty to a boy long chastened.

About the end of the second year my parents came to Paris. My brother wrote that they would dine at the Palais-Royal and see the Theatre-Francais; I was excited but ashamed because I owed Doisy a hundred francs. I asked my brother to be Doisy’s mouthpiece. My father inclined to forgiveness, but my mother raged: “What should I be later if at seventeen I committed such follies? Did I mean to ruin my family?” Sent back to school in charge of my brother, I lost the dinner and the play. Such was my first interview with my mother after twelve years’ separation.

When school ended my father left me under Monsieur Lepitre’s guardianship to study higher mathematics, a year of law, and philosophy. Though nineteen, my father persisted in restrictive oversight. Lepitre sent a man-of-all-work to escort me to the law school. Paris alarmed my parents; a young girl would have been treated with less precaution. The Palais-Royal, an Eldorado of love, remained an asymptote to me, impossible to reach. Presented to an aunt on the Ile St. Louis, the Marquise de Listomere, I dined there under surveillance and meant once to escape to the Palais-Royal, but Lepitre’s coach and my mother’s arrival thwarted me. Fate interposed three times between me and the Palais-Royal; once my mother appeared in a post-chaise and took me away when my life there seemed poised on the verge of ruin.

Driven by repressed desires and weariness, study had become a passion and nearly fatal to my health. At twenty, small and thin, I had read and thought so much that I knew life metaphysically, poised between puberty and manhood, ready to feel and to love.

The journey from Paris to Tours was chill with my mother’s coldness. At Orleans I fell at her feet, clasped her knees, and implored her; she called me an unnatural child. At Blois I went to the parapet of the bridge to drown myself; the height prevented it. At home my sisters were initially surprised but soon showed relative kindness. I received a third-story garret; my mother left me without linen or clothes. She was tall, spare, given to cards, egotistical and insolent, like all the Listomeres. Her religion was duty; she received our homage as a priest receives incense. Despite this despotism, the sublime mistake—our love for our mother—lasted until much later when we judged her finally.

In despair I took refuge in my father’s library and read all I could. Sometimes my eldest sister tried to comfort me, but often I desired to die. The return of the Bourbons stirred Touraine; the Duc d’Angouleme’s procession excited a festival and a ball at which I longed to be present. My mother, too ill to go, angrily resisted my request, then secretly had a bottle-blue coat made for me. In that costume I sneaked into a tent to see the duke. Suffocated by heat and dazzled by the spectacle, I felt disgust and withdrew to a corner, motionless and sullen. Mistaken for a sleepy child, a woman sat beside me; my senses were arrested. I kissed her white, rounded shoulder as a child might a mother’s breast. She cried, turned, and said, “Monsieur!” Her saintly anger and noble face struck me; tears fell. She moved away like a queen.

That stolen kiss altered me. A new soul had burst its chrysalis. My star descended and became a woman. The first irruption of love in a man’s heart is strange: I had seen women before but none like her. Some conjunction made that woman unique to me at that hour. The thought that she lived in Touraine made the air delicious. My mother, fearing for my health, sent me to Frapesle, a château on the Indre belonging to a friend, offering me the freedom I craved. I determined to search every chateau on foot, convinced she might live in any one of them.

Leaving Tours, I walked across moors and by the Indre. At midday, beneath a walnut-tree, I saw the château whose white cambric dress gleamed among the vines—Clochegourde. Love filled the valley; the river Indre’s ribbon, poplars, oak-woods, and dim horizons became part of my vision. If you would see nature beautiful and virgin as a bride, go there in spring. My heart deceived me not—she lived there.

My host at Frapesle carried me over his estate and, as we ascended, I kept looking toward the pretty castle. “It is Clochegourde,” he said; “a pretty house belonging to the Comte de Mortsauf, whose wife is a demoiselle de Lenoncourt. They never leave Clochegourde. Madame de Mortsauf is a woman who would hold the highest position wherever she might be.” When he added that she had been at the Duc d’Angouleme’s ball, I exclaimed, “It was she!”

We crossed the river and reached Clochegourde at four. The building, modest yet effective, sits on a slope with a terraced garden by the Indre. Its five-windowed front, projecting end-windows, and little bell-tower with carved escutcheon give it an air of genteel neglect. The house, with its stables and greenhouses, seems dainty as a flower set in orchards and vineyards. I admired the groups of trees and felt my heart beat faster at approaching events that would change me.

Warned by the watch-dog, a servant told us the count had gone to Azay but Madame la comtesse was at home. My companion looked at me; I trembled lest he decline to see her in her husband’s absence, but he announced us. A golden voice bade us enter. I recognized her voice from the ball and, blushing, nearly fled; our eyes met. Too confused for speech, she returned to her embroidery and counted stitches while we were seated. Monsieur de Chessel explained who I was: a young man weakened by study whom he had met and led to Clochegourde to rest. Distrustful at first, Madame de Mortsauf offered me restoratives and a few kind words that gave me speech. I blushed like a girl and thanked her.

She invited my companion to dine; as a man of the world he read the shades of meaning; I believed in her sincerity and was astonished when my host later remarked, “If you do not make it all right, I may find myself on bad terms with my neighbors.” His faith that I could please her gave me power and hope.

At dinner she talked of harvest and vintage—subjects beyond me; but the music of her voice was what enthralled me. Her utterance made each syllable a charm; I stayed silent often only to hear her speak. Her way of pronouncing certain sounds was like a bird’s song; her speech led the listener into higher regions. Many times I continued a discussion to listen to the harmonies of her voice.

Her inattention let me examine her. I saw the mole where the shoulder line began, the golden hair, the pure neck. Her face cannot be fully drawn; it required an artist to paint inner fires. Her soft fair hair caused her suffering, her brow was round and full of unuttered thoughts, her green-brown eyes were wan except on rare keen emotions when they sent forth a subtle gleam. A Grecian nose and curved lips gave her face harmony; her skin was like white camellia warmed with rose. Her figure, though well developed and the mother of two, retained youthful freshness. She seemed simple, bashful, and dreamily grave through suffering; she charmed without seeking to charm. The rarity of her gestures and glances added solemnity to all she said; except when speaking to her children she seldom looked at anyone.

On this morning she wore a rose-colored gown, a wide-collared hem, a black belt, and little boots; her hair twisted in a tortoise-shell comb. Such is the imperfect sketch I promised you. The constant emanation of her soul made Clochegourde’s life luminous; the salon, panelled and painted in gray, with neat porcelain vases and a backgammon board, conveyed a calm and composed life. No apartment has given me such teeming impressions; many of my ideas in science and politics were born in that room. Through the windows the valley spread from Pont-de-Ruan to Azay; there the conventual regularity of her occupations made itself felt.

Had I first met her in the domestic gloom among her husband and children, I might have been different; but having stolen a kiss at the ball, having breathed her perfume, I longed for the companionship of the fields and nights, to paddle the river, to climb trees, to linger in the sun. Had she asked me for impossible things, I would have sought them to obtain those other riches for her.

When she mentioned Monsieur de Mortsauf I felt hatred and fear—hatred of obstacles and fear of the struggle and of her. “Here is Monsieur de Mortsauf,” she said. I rose, startled. The count entered with his little son. A child called “Here’s papa!” and a pale little girl, whom I thought six but learned was nine, came forward and sat on her mother’s lap. The child’s pallor and fragility revealed something of the grief upon the countess’s brow. Jacques, the son, showed the same weakness. Their frailty beside a mother so healthy made the cause of her silence plain. As the count bowed, his glance at me was uneasy and awkward—a distrust not born of malice but of uncertainty.

At first cold and supercilious, the count grew more polite and attentive as my name was explained. My father had rendered devoted service to the Bourbons long ago; familially, the count recognized my lineage and treated me with consideration. When the children saw us engaged in conversation they slipped away to their mother, and I heard their voices from a distance like bees around a hive.

I studied the count’s traits. Though forty-five, he seemed nearer sixty. A crescent of hair fringed the back of his bald head; his face suggested a white wolf with blood about its muzzle—his nose inflamed by long poisoning. His flat forehead and narrow pointed face bore signs of a life in the open air but not of vigorous intellect; his cheekbones were brown and prominent amid general pallor, and his light-yellow eye was bright without warmth, anxious without thought. His mouth was violent and domineering; his chin flat and long. Thin and very tall, he depended on the conventional value of his caste. The carelessness of country life had left his dress neglected; his hands brown and wiry showed he wore gloves only on rare occasions, and his shoes were common.

Though ten years of emigration and ten more of farm-life had changed his physical condition, he still retained certain vestiges of nobility. Even the bitterest liberal would have admitted his chivalric loyalty and the imperishable convictions of one who reads the "Quotidienne"; he was devoted to a cause, honest in his political antipathies, incapable of serving his party but very capable of injuring it, and without the slightest real knowledge of the affairs of France. The count was one of those upright men who are available for nothing, who stand obstinately in the way of all; ready to die under arms at the post assigned to them, but preferring to give their life rather than their money.

During dinner I detected, in the hanging of his flaccid cheeks and the covert glances he cast upon his children, traces of some wearing thought. Watching him, who could fail to understand him? Who would not have seen that he had fatally transmitted to his children those weakly bodies in which the principle of life was lacking? If he blamed himself he denied others the right to judge him. Harsh as one who knows himself in fault, yet without greatness of soul or charm to compensate for the weight of misery he had thrown into the balance, his private life was no doubt the scene of irascibilities shown in his angular features and the restlessness of his eye. When his wife returned, followed by the children clinging to her, I felt the presence of unhappiness, as if walking over the roof of a vault one becomes conscious of the depths below. Seeing these four human beings together, letting my eye pass from one to the other, thoughts steeped in sadness fell upon my heart as a fine gray rain dims a landscape after the sun has risen clear.

When the immediate subject of conversation was exhausted the count told his wife who I was, and related certain circumstances connected with my family that were wholly unknown to me. He asked my age. When I told it, the countess echoed my exclamation of surprise at her daughter’s age. Perhaps she had thought me fifteen. Later I discovered that this was another tie that bound her strongly to me. Even then I read her soul. Her motherhood quivered with a tardy ray of hope. Seeing me over twenty yet so slight and delicate and nervously strong, a voice cried to her, “They too will live!” She looked at me searchingly, and in that moment I felt the barriers of ice melting between us. She seemed to have many questions to ask, but asked none.

“If study has made you ill,” she said, “the air of our valley will soon restore you.”

“Modern education is fatal to children,” remarked the count. “We stuff them with mathematics and ruin their health with sciences, make them old before their time. You must stay and rest here,” he added, turning to me. “You are crushed by the avalanche of ideas that have rolled down upon you. What sort of future will this universal education bring unless we prevent its evils by replacing public education in the hands of the religious bodies?”

These words were in harmony with a speech he afterwards made at the elections when he refused his support to a man whose gifts would have served the royalist cause. “I shall always distrust men of talent,” he said.

Presently the count proposed a tour of the gardens.

“Monsieur--” said his wife.

“Well, what, my dear?” he said, turning to her with an arrogant harshness that showed how absolute he chose to be in his own home.

“Monsieur de Vandenesse walked from Tours this morning and Monsieur de Chessel, not aware of it, has already taken him on foot over Frapesle.”

“Very imprudent of you,” the count said, turning to me; “but at your age--” and he shook his head.

The conversation resumed. I soon saw how intractable his royalism was, and how much care was needed to swim safely in his waters. The man-servant, now in livery, announced dinner. Monsieur de Chessel gave his arm to Madame de Mortsauf, and the count gaily seized mine to lead me into the dining-room on the ground-floor facing the salon.

This room, floored with white tiles made in Touraine and wainscoted to three feet, was hung with varnished paper divided into wide panels by wreaths of flowers and fruit; the windows had cambric curtains trimmed with red; the buffets were old pieces by Boulle, and the chairs, covered by hand-made tapestry, had carved oak. The dinner, plentiful but not luxurious; family silver without uniformity, Dresden china, octagonal decanters, knives with agate handles, and lacquered trays beneath the wine-bottles were chief features, while flowers adorned the porcelain vases. I delighted in these quaint old things. My delight hid the obstacles that a uniform country life placed between her and me. I was near her, sitting at her right hand, serving her with wine. Yes, unhoped-for joy! I touched her dress, I ate her bread. In three hours my life had mingled with hers! That terrible kiss had bound us in a secret that inspired mutual shame. A glorious self-abasement took possession of me. I studied to please the count, fondled the dogs, would gladly have gratified the children, brought them hoops and marbles and played horse; I was even provoked that they did not already claim me as their own. Love has intuitions like genius; I dimly perceived that gloom, discontent, hostility would destroy my footing in that household.

The dinner passed with inward happiness. Feeling that I was under her roof, I gave no heed to her obvious coldness nor to the count’s indifference masked by politeness. Love, like life, has an adolescence during which it suffices unto itself. I made several stupid replies induced by passion, but no one perceived their cause, not even SHE, who knew nothing of love. The rest of my visit was a dream, which did not cease until by moonlight on that warm night I recrossed the Indre, watching white visions that embellished meadows, shores, and hills, and listening to the clear song, full of melancholy, of a tree-frog. Since that evening I have never heard it without delight. A sense came to me then of the marble wall against which my feelings had dashed. Would it be always so? I fancied myself under a fatal spell; unhappy events of my past rose and struggled with the personal pleasure I had enjoyed. Before reaching Frapesle I turned to look at Clochegourde and saw beneath its windows a little punt fastened to an ash-tree and swaying on the water. This punt belonged to Monsieur de Mortsauf, who used it for fishing.

“Well,” said Monsieur de Chessel, when we were out of ear-shot. “I needn’t ask if you found those shoulders; I must, however, congratulate you on the reception Monsieur de Mortsauf gave you. The devil! you stepped into his heart at once.”

These words, followed by those I have already quoted, raised my spirits. I had not yet said a word, and Monsieur de Chessel may have attributed my silence to happiness.

“How do you mean?” I asked.

“He never, to my knowledge, received any one so well.”

“I will admit that I am rather surprised myself,” I said, conscious of a certain bitterness underlying my companion’s speech.

Though inexpert in social matters, I was much struck by Monsieur de Chessel’s feeling. His real name was Durand, but he had had the weakness to discard the name of a worthy father, a merchant who had made a large fortune under the Revolution. His wife was sole heiress of the Chessels, an old parliamentary family. Ambitious, Monsieur de Chessel smothered the original Durand. He first called himself Durand de Chessel, then D. de Chessel, and that made him Monsieur de Chessel. Under the Restoration he entailed an estate with the title of count in virtue of letters-patent from Louis XVIII. His children reaped the fruits of his audacity without knowing what it cost him in sarcastic comments. Parvenus are like monkeys: clever in climbing, absurd at the summit. The reverse side of my host’s character was pettiness with envy. The peerage and he were on diverging lines. To have an ambition and gratify it shows the insolence of strength, but to live below avowed ambition is a source of ridicule to petty minds. Monsieur de Chessel did not advance straightforwardly. Twice elected deputy and twice defeated; yesterday director-general, to-day nothing at all; his successes and defeats had made him sour. Though brave and witty and capable of great things, envy—root of existence in Touraine—injured him in upper circles, where a dissatisfied man, frowning at others’ success, slow at compliments and ready at epigram, seldom succeeds. Had he sought less he might perhaps have obtained more; but he had enough genuine superiority to make him wish to advance his way.

At this time Monsieur de Chessel’s ambition had a second dawn. Royalty smiled upon him, and he affected the grand manner. Still he was kind to me, and pleased me because with him I found peace and rest for the first time. The interest, however slight, which he showed in my affairs, seemed to me an image of paternal love. His hospitable care contrasted with my usual neglect, so I felt childish gratitude to the home where no fetters bound me and where I was welcomed and even courted.

The owners of Frapesle are associated with the dawn of my life’s happiness, so I mingle them in memories I love to revive. Later, in connection with his letters-patent, I had the pleasure of doing my host some service. Monsieur de Chessel enjoyed his wealth with an ostentation that gave umbrage to neighbors. He could vary his horses and equipages; his wife dressed exquisitely; he received on a grand scale; his servants were more numerous than neighbors approved; for which he was said to be aping princes. The Frapesle estate is immense. Before such luxury the Comte de Mortsauf, with one family cariole—something between a coach without springs and a post-chaise—forced by limited means to let or farm Clochegourde, was Tourangean up to the time when royal favor restored the family to a distinction unlooked for. His greeting to me, the younger son of a ruined family whose escutcheon dated to the Crusades, was intended to show contempt for a neighbor not of noble birth. Monsieur de Chessel understood this. They always met politely; but there was none of that daily intercourse or agreeable intimacy that ought to have existed between Clochegourde and Frapesle, two estates separated only by the Indre, whose mistresses could have beckoned from their windows.

Jealousy, however, was not the sole reason for the Count de Mortsauf’s solitude. His early education was that of children of great families—an incomplete instruction as to knowledge, but supplemented by the training of society and the exercise of duties under the crown. Monsieur de Mortsauf had emigrated when the second stage of his education was about to begin, and that training was lacking. He believed in the immediate restoration of the monarchy; with that conviction his exile was a long idleness. When the army of Condé, which his courage led him to join, was disbanded, he expected to find another post under the white flag, and never sought, like other emigrants, an industry. Perhaps he lacked courage to lay aside his name and earn his living in toil he despised. His hopes, daily postponed, and perhaps a scruple of honor, kept him from offering his services to foreign powers. Trials undermined his courage. Long tramps on insufficient nourishment, and above all hopes betrayed, injured his health and discouraged his mind. By degrees he became utterly destitute. If to some misery is tonic, on others it acts as a dissolvent; the count was of the latter.

Reflecting on this Touraine gentleman, tramping and sleeping along the highroads of Hungary, sharing the mutton of Prince Esterházy’s shepherds and refusing service to France’s enemies, I never found it in my heart to feel bitterness against him, even when I saw him at his worst. The natural gaiety of a Frenchman soon deserted him; he became morose, fell ill, and was charitably cared for in some German hospital. His disease was an inflammation of the mesenteric membrane, often fatal and liable, even if cured, to change the constitution and produce hypochondria. His love affairs, which I alone discovered, were low-lived, and not only destroyed his health but ruined his future.

After twelve years of great misery he made his way to France under the Emperor’s decree permitting emigrants’ return. As the wayfarer crossed the Rhine and saw Strasburg’s tower against the evening sky, his strength gave way. “‘France! France!’ I cried. ‘I see France!’” (he said to me) “as a child cries ‘Mother!’ when hurt.” Born to wealth, he was now poor; made to command a regiment or govern a province, he was without authority or future; constitutionally robust, he returned infirm and utterly worn out. Without enough education to take part among men and affairs, broadened by events, without influence, he lived despoiled of everything. Want of money made his name a burden. His unalterable opinions, his antecedents with Condé, his trials, his recollections, his wasted health, gave him susceptibilities little spared in France, that land of jest and sarcasm. Half dead he reached Maine, where by some civil-war accident the revolutionary government had forgotten to sell one of his farms, which his farmer held by saying he was the owner.

When the Lenoncourt family at Givry heard of the Comte de Mortsauf’s arrival, the Duc de Lenoncourt invited him to stay while a house was prepared. The Lenoncourts were nobly generous, and with them he remained some months, struggling to hide his sufferings. The Lenoncourts had themselves lost immense property. By birth Monsieur de Mortsauf was a suitable husband for their daughter. Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt, instead of rejecting marriage with a feeble man of thirty-five, accepted. It gave her the opportunity of living with her aunt, the Duchesse de Verneuil, sister of the Prince de Blamont-Chauvry, who was like a mother.

Madame de Verneuil, intimate with the Duchesse de Bourbon, was a member of the devout society of which Monsieur Saint-Martin was the soul. The disciples practised virtues taught by mystical illumination: doctrines explaining existence by reincarnations through which the spirit rises; they liberate duty from legal degradation, enable serenity, ordain contempt for this life’s sufferings, and inspire care of the angel within us. It is stoicism with an immortal future. Active prayer and pure love are elements of this faith, born of the Church but returning to primitive Christianity. Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt remained in the Catholic communion, to which her aunt was bound. Cruelly tried by revolutionary horrors, the Duchesse de Verneuil acquired in her last years a halo of passionate piety, which shed celestial love upon her niece.

After the duchess’s death, Madame de Mortsauf received visits at Clochegourde from Saint-Martin, who corrected his last books there. Madame de Verneuil, wise with old age, gave Clochegourde to her niece; with grace she yielded everything, reserving one room above her old chamber for herself, which she fitted up for the countess. Her sudden death threw a gloom over the early days of the marriage and connected Clochegourde with sadness in the bride’s mind. The first period of her settlement was, I cannot say happiest, but the least troubled of her life.

After exile trials Monsieur de Mortsauf, dreaming of a secure future, had a certain recovery of mind; he breathed anew in this sweet valley and found some happiness in agricultural pursuits. But the birth of his first child, Jacques, was a thunderbolt that ruined past and future. The doctor declared the child had not vitality enough to live. The count concealed this from the mother but sought other advice and received the same fatal answer, confirmed at Madeleine’s birth. These events and an inward consciousness of the cause increased his diseased tendencies. His name doomed to extinction, a pure young woman made miserable beside him and doomed to the anguish of maternity without its joys—this uprising of his former into his present life crushed his spirit and completed its destruction.

The countess guessed the past from the present, and read the future. Though nothing is so difficult as to make a man happy when he knows himself to blame, she set herself to that task, worthy of an angel. She became stoical. Descending into an abyss, whence she still could see the sky, she devoted herself to the care of one man as a sister of charity devotes herself to many. To reconcile him with himself, she forgave what he could not forgive. The count grew miserly; she accepted his privations. Like all who know the world only to acquire suspicion, he feared betrayal; she lived in solitude and yielded without murmur to his mistrust. With a woman’s tact she made him will to do what was right until he fancied the ideas were his own, enjoying in his person honors never his. After experience of married life, she resolved never to leave Clochegourde; she saw his hysterical tendencies and feared outbreaks that gossip would turn to the injury of her children. Thus, thanks to her, no one suspected Monsieur de Mortsauf’s real incapacity; she wrapped his ruins in a mantle of ivy. The fickle, embittered nature of the man found rest in his wife; his secret anguish was lessened by the balm she shed.

This brief history is in part a summary forced from Monsieur de Chessel by vexation. His knowledge of the world enabled him to penetrate several mysteries of Clochegourde. But the prescience of love could not be misled by Madame de Mortsauf’s sublime attitude. When alone in my little bedroom, a sense of the full truth made me spring from my bed; I could not bear to stay at Frapesle when I saw Clochegourde’s lighted windows. I dressed, went softly down, and left the chateau by a tower door at the foot of a winding stairway. The cool night calmed me. I crossed the Indre by the bridge at the Red Mill, took the blessed punt, and rowed in front of Clochegourde, where a brilliant light streamed from a window looking towards Azay.

Again I plunged into my meditations, now peaceful, intermingled with the nightingale’s note and the solitary cry of the sedge-warbler. Ideas glided like fairies through my mind, lifting the black veil that had hidden the future. Soul and senses were charmed. With what passion my thoughts rose to her! Again and again I cried, like a madman, “Will she be mine?” During preceding days the universe had enlarged to me, but in a single night I found its centre. On her my will and ambition henceforth fastened; I desired to be all in all to her, that I might heal and fill her lacerated heart.

Beautiful was that night beneath her windows, amid the murmur of waters through the sluices, broken only by the clock-tower of Sache. During those hours of darkness bathed in light, I betrothed to her my soul with the faith of the poor Castilian knight in Cervantes—a faith with which all love begins.

At first gleam of day and the waking birds’ note I fled back among Frapesle’s trees and reached the house; no one had seen me, no one suspected my absence, and I slept soundly until breakfast. After the meal I went to the meadows for another sight of the Indre and its isles, the valley and slopes, of which I seemed such an admirer. But once there, by a swiftness of foot, I returned to my punt, the willows, and Clochegourde. All was silent and palpitating, as at midday in summer. The still foliage lay sharply defined on the blue sky; dragonflies and cantharides flew among reeds and ash-trees; cattle chewed the cud in shade, the vineyard earth glowed, adders glided up and down the banks. What a change in the sparkling landscape while I slept! I sprang from the boat and ran up the road round Clochegourde, for I fancied I saw the count coming out. I was not mistaken; he was walking beside the hedge, making for a gate on the road to Azay by the river bank.

“How are you this morning, Monsieur le comte?”

He looked at me pleasantly, not used to hear himself thus addressed.

“Quite well,” he answered. “You must love the country, to be rambling about in this heat!”

“I was sent here to live in the open air.”

“Then what do you say to coming with me to see them cut my rye?”

“Gladly,” I replied. “I’ll own my ignorance; I don’t know rye from wheat, nor a poplar from an aspen; I know nothing of farming nor of the methods of cultivating the soil.”

“Well, come and learn,” he cried gaily, returning upon his steps. “Come in by the little gate above.”

The count walked back along the hedge, he being within it and I without.

“You will learn nothing from Monsieur de Chessel,” he remarked; “he is altogether too fine a gentleman to do more than receive the reports of his bailiff.”

The count showed me yards and farm buildings, pleasure-grounds, orchards, vineyards, and kitchen garden, until we came to the long alley of acacias and ailanthus beside the river, at the end of which I saw Madame de Mortsauf sitting on a bench with her children. A woman is very lovely under such light and quivering shade. Surprised, perhaps, at my prompt visit, she did not move, knowing we would come. The count made me admire the view of the valley, which here differs from that seen from the heights. Here I might have thought myself in a corner of Switzerland. The meadows, furrowed with little brooks that flow into the Indre, stretch till lost in misty distance. Towards Montbazon the eye ranges over a vast plain; elsewhere it is stopped by hills and trees and rocks. We quickened as we approached Madame de Mortsauf, who dropped the book in which Madeleine was reading and took Jacques upon her knees as he had a violent cough.

“What’s the matter?” cried the count, turning livid.

“A sore throat,” answered the mother, who seemed not to see me; “but it is nothing serious.”

She held the child by head and body, and her eyes seemed to shed two rays of life into the poor frail creature.

“You are so extraordinarily imprudent,” said the count sharply; “you expose him to the river damps and let him sit on a stone bench.”

“Why, papa, the stone is burning hot,” cried Madeleine.

“They were suffocating higher up,” said the countess.

“Women always want to prove they are right,” said the count, turning to me.

To avoid agreeing or disagreeing I watched Jacques, who complained of his throat. His mother carried him away, but heard her husband say: “When they have brought such sickly children into the world they ought to learn how to take care of them.” Words cruelly unjust; his self-love drove him to defend himself at his wife’s expense. The countess hurried up the steps and into the portico, disappearing through the glass door. Monsieur de Mortsauf sat on the bench, head bowed in gloomy silence. My position was annoying; he neither spoke nor looked at me. Farewell to the walk he had proposed, in which I had hoped to fathom him. I hardly remember a more unpleasant moment. Ought I to go away, or not? How many painful thoughts must have arisen in his mind to make him forget to follow Jacques and learn his state! At last he rose abruptly and came towards me. We both turned and looked at the smiling valley.

“We will put off our walk to another day, Monsieur le comte,” I said gently.

“No, let us go,” he replied. “Unfortunately, I am accustomed to such scenes--I, who would give my life without the slightest regret to save that of the child.”

“Jacques is better, my dear; he has gone to sleep,” said a golden voice. Madame de Mortsauf suddenly appeared at the path’s end. She came forward without bitterness and bowed to me.

“I am glad to see you like Clochegourde,” she said.

“My dear, shall I ride over and fetch Monsieur Deslandes?” said the count, as if to secure her forgiveness.

“Don’t be worried,” she said. “Jacques did not sleep last night; he had a bad dream, and I told him stories all night to keep him quiet. His cough is purely nervous; I have stilled it with a lozenge, and he has gone to sleep.”

“Poor woman!” said her husband, taking her hand with a tearful look, “I knew nothing of it.”

“Why be troubled when there is no occasion?” she replied. “Now go and attend to the rye. You know if you are not there the men will let gleaners enter before the sheaves are carried away.”

“I am going to take a first lesson in agriculture, madame,” I said.

“You have a very good master,” she replied, motioning to the count, whose mouth screwed into that smile called a “bouche en coeur.”

Two months later I learned she had passed that night in great anxiety, fearing her son had the croup; while I, rocked by thoughts of love in my boat, imagined she might see me from her window adoring the gleam of the candle lighting a forehead furrowed by fears! The croup prevailed at Tours and was often fatal. When we were outside the gate, the count said in a voice of emotion, “Madame de Mortsauf is an angel!” The words staggered me. As yet I knew little of the family, and the conscience of a young soul made me exclaim inwardly: “What right have I to trouble this perfect peace?”

Glad to find a listener in a young man over whom he could lord it, the count talked of the future the Bourbons would secure. We had desultory conversation in which I listened to childish nonsense which amazed me. He was ignorant of facts susceptible of proof; he feared persons of education, rejected superiority, and scoffed at progress. I discovered many sensitive fibres in him which it required caution not to wound; a conversation with him was a strain. When I had felt his defects, I conformed with the same suppleness his wife showed in soothing him. Later I would certainly have made him angry, but now, humble as a child, supposing that I knew nothing and believing men in their prime knew all, I was genuinely amazed at results obtained at Clochegourde by this patient agriculturist. I besieged him with questions about prices and cultivation, and he seemed glad to answer in detail.

“What in the world do they teach you in your colleges?” he exclaimed at last in astonishment.

On that first day the count said to his wife when he reached home, “Monsieur Félix is a charming young man.”

That evening I wrote to my mother asking her to send my clothes and linen, saying I would remain at Frapesle. Ignorant of the great revolution that was just taking place, and not perceiving its influence upon my fate, I expected to return to Paris to resume legal studies. The Law School did not open till November; meantime I had two and a half months.

The first part of my stay, while I studied to understand the count, was a period of painful impressions. I found him extremely irascible without adequate cause; hasty in action dangerously so. Sometimes he showed glimpses of the brave gentleman of Condé’s army—parabolic flashes of will which in emergency may tear through politics like bomb-shells, and by honesty and courage make a man Elbée, Bonchamp, or Charette. In presence of certain ideas his nostril contracted, his forehead cleared, and his eyes shot lightnings soon quenched. Sometimes I feared he might detect my eyes’ language and kill me. I was young then and merely tender. Will had scarcely dawned within me. My passionate desires shook me like throes of fear. Death I feared not, but I would not die until I knew the happiness of mutual love. I was a prey to perplexity; I hoped for some fortunate chance; I watched; I made the children love me; I tried to identify with the family.

Little by little the count restrained himself less in my presence. I came to know his sudden outbreaks of temper, deep and ceaseless melancholy, flashes of brutality, bitter complaints, cold hatreds, impulses of latent madness, childish moans, cries of a man’s despair, unexpected fury. The moral nature differs from the physical inasmuch as nothing is absolute in it. My position at Clochegourde, my future life, depended on this one eccentric will.

I cannot describe the distress that filled me whenever I entered Clochegourde and asked myself, “How will he receive me?” With what anxiety I saw clouds collecting on that stormy brow. I lived in perpetual “qui-vive.” I fell under that man’s dominion; and the sufferings I endured taught me to understand Madame de Mortsauf’s. We began by exchanging looks of comprehension; tried by the same fire, how many discoveries I made during those first forty days—of actual bitterness, tacit joys, hopes alternately submerged and buoyant. One evening I found her pensively watching a sunset that reddened the summits with a ravishing glow. Did lost girlhood illusions return? Did the woman suffer an inward comparison? I fancied a desolation in her attitude favorable to my first appeal, and I said, “Some days are hard to bear.”

“You read my soul,” she answered; “but how have you done so?”

“We touch at many points,” I replied. “Surely we belong to the small number born to highest joys and deepest sorrows; whose feeling qualities vibrate in unison and echo each other; whose sensitive natures are in harmony with things. Put such beings among discordant surroundings and they suffer horribly, just as their happiness mounts when they meet congenial ideas or beings. There is a third condition, where sorrows are known only to souls affected by the same distress; in this alone is the highest fraternal comprehension. It may happen such souls find no outlet for good or evil. Then the organ within us endowed with expression is exercised in a void, expends passion without an object, utters sounds without melody, and cries lost in solitude,—terrible defeat of a soul revolting against inutility. These struggles weaken the soul; an indescribable melancholy for which the confessional has no ears. Have I not expressed our mutual sufferings?”

She shuddered, then without removing her eyes from the sun said, “How is it that, young as you are, you know these things? Were you once a woman?”

“Ah!” I replied, “my childhood was like a long illness—”

“I hear Madeleine coughing,” she cried, leaving me abruptly.

The countess showed no displeasure at my constant visits, for two reasons. First she was pure as a child, and her thoughts wandered into no forbidden regions; next I amused the count and made a sop for that lion without claws. I found an excuse for my visits which seemed plausible. Monsieur de Mortsauf proposed to teach me backgammon, and I accepted; as I did the countess was betrayed into a look of compassion, which seemed to say, “You fling yourself into the lion’s jaws.” If I did not understand this at the time, three days had not passed before I knew what I had undertaken. My patience, which nothing exhausts, the fruit of my miserable childhood, ripened under this trial. The count delighted when he could jeer at me for not practicing his rules; if I reflected he complained of slowness; if I played fast he was angry because I hurried him; if I forgot to mark points he declared I was always too rapid. It was like a schoolmaster’s tyranny. When we played for money his winnings gave him abject delight.

A word from his wife was enough to console me and recalled him to politeness. But soon I fell into unexpected misery. My money was disappearing under losses. Though the count was always present during my visits until I left, which was sometimes very late, I cherished hope of a moment when I might say a word to reach my idol’s heart; to obtain it I was forced to continue these weary games, during which my feelings were lacerated and my money lost. Still, there were moments when we were silent, she and I, looking at sunlight on meadows, clouds, misty hills, or the moon on the river sandbanks; saying only, “Night is beautiful!”

“Night is woman, madame.”

“What tranquillity!”

“Yes, no one can be absolutely wretched here.”

Then she would return to her embroidery. I came at last to hear the inward beatings of an affection which sought its object. But the fact remained—without money, farewell to these evenings. I wrote to my mother to send some. She scolded me and sent only enough to last a week. Where could I get more? My life depended on it. Thus in the dawn of my first great happiness I found the same sufferings that assailed me elsewhere; but in Paris at college I evaded them by abstinence; at Frapesle they were active; so active that I was possessed by impulses to theft, visions of crime, furiously desperate thoughts that rend the soul and must be subdued to keep self-respect. The memory of my mother’s parsimony taught me indulgence for young men which one who has stood upon the abyss must feel. Though my rectitude was strengthened by those moments when life opened and I saw rocks and quicksands beneath the surface, I have never known human justice draw its blade through a criminal’s throat without saying to myself: “Penal laws are made by men who have never known misery.”

At this crisis I found a treatise on backgammon in Monsieur de Chessel’s library, and I studied it. My host gave me a few lessons; less harshly taught by the count I made progress and applied rules and calculations I knew by heart. Within days I could beat Monsieur de Mortsauf; but no sooner had I done so and won his money than his temper became intolerable; his eyes glittered like a tiger’s, his face shrivelled, his brows knit as I never saw before. His complainings were those of a fretful child. Sometimes he flung down the dice, quivered with rage, bit the dice-box, and said insulting things to me. Such violence came to an end. When I had acquired mastery I played to suit me; I so managed that we were nearly equal up to the last moment; I allowed him to win the first half and made matters even during the last. The end of the world would have surprised him less than the rapid superiority of his pupil; but he never admitted it. The unvarying result of our games was a topic he fastened on.

“My poor head,” he would say, “is fatigued; you manage to win the last of the game because by that time I lose my skill.”

The countess, who knew backgammon, understood my manoeuvres from the first, and gave me mute thanks. From that evening she always looked at me when she spoke. I cannot explain my condition when I left her. My soul had annihilated my body; I weighed nothing; I did not walk, I flew. That look I carried within me; it bathed me with light, and her last words, “Adieu, monsieur,” still sounded in my soul. I was born into a new life; I was something to her! I slept on purple and fine linen. Flames darted before my closed eyelids in dreams; her voice became palpable, an atmosphere of light and fragrance wrapping me, a melody enfolding my spirit. On the morrow her greeting expressed fulness of unuttered feeling, and from that moment I was initiated into the secrets of her voice.

That day was decisive. After dinner we walked on the heights across a barren plain where no herbage grew; the ground was stony and arid, though a few scrub oaks and thorny bushes straggled, and instead of grass a carpet of crimped moss illuminated by sunset made our feet slip. I held Madeleine by the hand to keep her up. Madame de Mortsauf led Jacques. The count, in front, suddenly turned and striking the earth with his cane said to me in a dreadful tone: “Such is my life!--but before I knew you,” he added with a look at his wife. The reparation was tardy; the countess turned pale; what woman would not stagger?

“But what delightful scenes are wafted here, and what a view of the sunset!” I cried. “For my part I should like to own this barren moor; there may be treasures if we dig for them. But its greatest wealth is that of being near you. Who would not pay a great cost for such a view?—all harmony to the eye, with that winding river where the soul may bathe among ash-trees and alders. See the difference of taste! To you this spot is a barren waste; to me, paradise.”

She thanked me with a look.

“Bucolics!” exclaimed the count, bitterly. “This is no life for a man who bears your name.” Then he changed tone—“The bells!” he cried, “don’t you hear the bells of Azay? I hear them ringing.”

Madame de Mortsauf gave me a frightened look. Madeleine clung to my hand.

“Suppose we play a game of backgammon?” I said. “Let us go back; the rattle of the dice will drown the bells.”

We returned to Clochegourde, conversing by fits. Once in the salon an indefinable dread took possession of us. The count flung himself into an armchair absorbed in reverie which his wife, who knew his symptoms, was careful not to interrupt. I also kept silence. Perhaps she thought backgammon might divert him. Nothing was harder than to make him play, which he greatly desired. Like a pretty woman he required coaxing so he might not seem obliged. If, warned by ill-humor, I suggested a game, he would dally and demur. “It is too late,” he would say; “besides, I don’t care for it.” Then a series of affectations left you ignorant of his real wishes.

On this occasion I pretended gaiety to induce him. He complained of giddiness, his brain squeezed in a vice; he heard noises and was choking; then he sighed. At last he consented. Madame de Mortsauf left to put the children to bed and lead family prayers. All went well during her absence; I allowed Monsieur de Mortsauf to win, and his delight put him beside himself. This sudden change from gloom to wild joy expressed in a crazy laugh distressed and alarmed me. I had never seen him in so marked a paroxysm. Our intimacy bore fruits in that he no longer restrained himself before me. Day by day he endeavored to bring me under his tyranny and obtain fresh food for his evil temper; moral diseases seem like creatures seeking to enlarge their empire.

Presently the countess came down and sat near the backgammon table, apparently for better light on her embroidery though anxiety led her to place her frame. A bit of ill-luck changed the count’s face; from gaiety it fell to gloom, his eyes rolled. Then worse ill-luck followed. Monsieur de Mortsauf made a fatal throw which decided the game. Instantly he sprang up, flung the table at me and the lamp on the floor, struck the chimney-piece with his fist and jumped about the room. The torrent of insults, imprecations, and incoherent words that rushed from his lips would have made an observer think of medieval tales of satanic possession. Imagine my position!

“Go into the garden,” said the countess, pressing my hand.

I left before the count could notice my disappearance. On the terrace I slowly walked about and heard his shouts and moans from the adjoining bedroom. At intervals through that tempest I heard the voice of an angel, rising like a nightingale after rain. I walked under acacias in the loveliest night of August, waiting for the countess to join me. I knew she would come; her gesture promised it. For several days an explanation seemed to float between us; a word would have set it gushing. What timidity had delayed a full understanding? Perhaps she loved, as I did, these quiverings of the spirit resembling fear and numbing sensibilities while we held our life unuttered, hesitating to unveil its secrets with young modesty. An hour passed. I sat on the brick balustrade when the sound of her footsteps and the ripple of her gown stirred the calm night. These are sensations to which the heart suffices not.

“Monsieur de Mortsauf is sleeping,” she said. “When he is thus I give him an infusion of poppies, a cup of water with poppies steeped; the attacks are so infrequent this simple remedy never loses effect—Monsieur,” she continued, shifting tone and using the most persuasive inflexion, “this accident has revealed a secret sedulously kept; promise me to bury the recollection of that scene. Do this for my sake, I beg you. I don’t ask you to swear; give me your word of honor and I shall be content.”

“Need I give it to you?” I said. “Do we not understand each other?”

“You must not judge unfavorably of Monsieur de Mortsauf; you see effects of his sufferings under emigration,” she went on. “Tomorrow he will forget all that he has said and done; you will find him kind and excellent as ever.”

“Do not seek to excuse him, madame,” I replied. “I will do all you wish. I would fling myself into the Indre if I could restore Monsieur de Mortsauf’s health and ensure you a happy life. The only thing I cannot change is my opinion. I can give you my life, but not my convictions; I can pay no heed to what he says, but can I hinder him from saying it? No, in my opinion Monsieur de Mortsauf is—”

“I understand you,” she said, hastily interrupting me; “you are right. The count is as nervous as a fashionable woman,” she added, softening madness by the word. “But he is only so at intervals, once a year when the weather is very hot. Ah, what evils from the emigration! How many fine lives ruined! He would have been, I am sure, a great soldier, an honor to his country—”

“I know,” I said, interrupting to let her see it was useless to deceive me.

She stopped, laid one hand lightly on my brow, and looked at me. “Who has sent you here into this home? Has God sent me help, a true friendship to support me?” She paused, then, as she laid her hand upon mine, “For you are good and generous—” She raised her eyes to heaven as if to invoke testimony, and then let them rest upon me. Electrified by the look, which cast a soul into my soul, I was guilty, by social laws, of a want of tact, though in some natures such indelicacy means a brave desire to meet danger, avert a blow, arrest an evil; oftener, an abrupt call upon a heart to learn if it answers. Many thoughts rose like gleams and bade me wash out the stain that blotted my conscience at this moment when I sought understanding.

“Before we say more,” I said in a voice shaken by heart-throbbings, “suffer me to purify one memory of the past.”

“Hush!” she said quickly, touching my lips with a finger she instantly removed. She looked at me haughtily, with the glance of a woman too exalted for insult to reach her. “Be silent; I know of what you are about to speak,—the first, the last, the only outrage ever offered to me. Never speak to me of that ball. If as a Christian I have forgiven you, as a woman I still suffer from your act.”

“You are more pitiless than God himself,” I said, forcing back tears.

“I ought to be so, I am more feeble,” she replied.

“But,” I continued with persistence, “listen to me now if only for the first, the last, the only time in your life.”

“Speak, then,” she said; “speak, or you will think I dare not hear you.”

Feeling this was the turning moment of our lives, I spoke in a tone that commands attention; I told her that all women I had ever seen were nothing to me; but when I met her I, whose life was studious, whose nature not bold, had been possessed by a frenzy that no one who once felt it could condemn; that no heart had been so filled with passion that conquers all things even death—

“And contempt?” she asked, stopping me.

“Did you despise me?” I exclaimed.

“Let us say no more,” she replied.

“No, let me say all!” I replied, in the excitement of intolerable pain. “It concerns my life, my whole being; it holds a secret you must know or I must die in despair. It also concerns you, who, unawares, are the lady in whose hand is the crown promised to the victor in the tournament!”

Then I related my childhood and youth, not as told now with distance, but in the language of a young man whose wounds still bleed. My voice was like an axe in a forest. At every word the dead years fell with echoing sound, bristling with anguish. I described many cruel details which here I spare. I spread before her the treasure of my hopes, the virgin gold of my desires, the whole of a burning heart kept alive beneath perpetual winter. When, bowed by remembered sufferings related with live coal, I awaited the reply of the woman who listened with bowed head, she illumined the darkness with a look, quickened worlds terrestrial and divine with a single sentence.

“We have had the same childhood!” she said, turning a face on which the halo of martyrs shone.

After a pause, in which our souls were wedded in the thought, “I am not alone in suffering,” the countess told me in the voice she kept for her little ones how unwelcome she was as a girl when sons were wanted. She showed me how her troubles as a daughter bound to her mother differed from those of a boy cast into the world of school. My desolate neglect seemed a paradise compared to contact with a millstone under which her soul was ground until her aunt saved her. She told me of severities that had prepared her for present life. When her aunt died she received Clochegourde and set up a home; but the duchess’s death left a gloom.

“We lived in the same sphere before we met in this,” I said; “you coming from the east, I from the west.”

She shook her head.

“To you the east, to me the west,” she replied. “You will live happy, I must die of pain. Life is what we make of it, and mine is made forever. No power can break the heavy chain to which a woman is fastened by this ring of gold—the emblem of a wife’s purity.”

We knew we were twins of one womb; she never dreamed of half-confidence between brothers. After a short sigh she told me of her married life, deceptions and disillusions, the rebirth of her childhood's misery. Like me she had suffered under trifles; mighty to souls whose substance quivers to the least shock, as a lake quivers when a stone is flung in. When she married she possessed some girlish savings; these, when needed, she gave to her husband without telling him. He took no account, never regarded himself her debtor. She did not obtain the glance of thanks that would have paid for all. Ah! how she went from trial to trial! Monsieur de Mortsauf habitually neglected to give her money for the household. When she asked, he seemed surprised and never spared her the mortification of petitioning for necessities. What terror filled her when her husband’s disease was revealed, and she quailed under his first outburst! What bitter reflections she made before admitting he was a wreck! What calamities had come of her bearing children! What anguish at infants born almost dead! With what courage had she said in her heart: “I will breathe the breath of life into them; I will bear them anew day by day!” Then conceive the bitterness of finding her greatest obstacle in the heart and hand from which a wife should draw succor! She saw the disaster that threatened him. As each difficulty was conquered, new deserts opened until the day she thoroughly understood her husband’s condition, the constitution of her children, and the character of the neighborhood; a day when she taught her feet to trample through mud and snow, trained her nerves to bullets, and all her being to passive obedience.

These things, which I summarize, she told me in dark extent with piteous detail of conjugal battles lost and fruitless struggles.

“You would have to live here many months,” she said, “to understand what difficulties I have met improving Clochegourde; what persuasions to make him do what was important. You cannot imagine the childish glee he shows when anything I advised was not at once successful. All that turns out well he claims for himself. Yes, I need infinite patience to bear his complaints when I am half-exhausted trying to amuse his weary hours, to sweeten his life and smooth paths he himself has strewn with stones. The reward is that awful cry: ‘Let me die, life is a burden to me!’ When visitors are here and he enjoys them, he forgets gloom and is courteous. You ask why he cannot be so to his family. I cannot explain that want of loyalty in a man chivalrous. He is quite capable of riding to Paris to buy me ornaments, as he did before the ball. Miserly in his household, he is lavish upon me if I wish it. I would it were reversed; I need nothing for myself, but household wants are many. In my desire to make him happy, I began by letting him treat me as a victim—I who could then, by a few caresses, have led him like a child—but I was unable to play a disgraceful part. Now, the family’s welfare requires me to be calm and stern as Justice—and yet, I too have a heart that overflows with tenderness.”

“But why,” I said, “do you not use this great influence to master him and govern him?”

“If it concerned myself only I should not attempt to overcome the dogged silence with which for days he meets my arguments, nor to answer his irrational remarks and childish reasons. I have no courage against weakness, any more than I have against childhood; they may strike me as they will, I cannot resist. Perhaps I might meet strength with strength, but I am powerless against those I pity. If required to coerce Madeleine in some matter that would save her life, I should die with her. Pity relaxes my fibres and unstrings my nerves. The violent shocks of the last ten years have broken me down; my feelings, so often battered, are numb; nothing can revive them; even the courage with which I once faced troubles begins to fail me. Yes, sometimes I am beaten. For want of rest—sea-baths to recover nervous strength—I shall perish. Monsieur de Mortsauf will have killed me, and he will die of my death.”

“Why not leave Clochegourde for a few months? Surely you could take your children to the seashore.”

“In the first place, Monsieur de Mortsauf would think he were lost if I left him. Though he will not admit his condition he is aware of it. He is both sane and mad, two natures in one, a contradiction explaining many irrational actions. Besides, he would have reason for objecting. Nothing would go right if I were absent. You may have seen in me a mother watchful to protect her young; harder still are cares imposed by Monsieur de Mortsauf, who constantly cries as he follows me, ‘Where is Madame?’ I am Jacques’ tutor and Madeleine’s governess; but that is not all, I am bailiff and steward too. You will understand what that means when you see that working an estate here is the most fatiguing of employments. We get small returns; farms are cultivated on shares needing close supervision. We must sell our produce, cattle and harvests. Competitors are our own farmers who meet consumers and determine prices by selling first. I would weary you if I explained difficulties. If you think of Monsieur de Mortsauf’s defective memory and the difficulty persuading him to attend to business, you can see my burden and the impossibility of laying it down even for a day. If I left no one would obey him; his orders conflict, no one likes him, and he listens readily to inferiors. If I left, not a servant would be in it in a week. So I am attached to Clochegourde as those leaden finals are to a roof. I have no reserves with you. The country-side is ignorant of our secrets, but you know them. Say nothing but what is kind and friendly, and you shall have my esteem—my gratitude,” she added softer. “On those terms you are welcome at Clochegourde, where you will find friends.”

“Ah!” I exclaimed, “I see I have never really suffered, while you—”

“No, no!” she exclaimed, with a smile that might melt granite. “Do not be astonished at my frank confidence; it shows life as it is, not as imagination pictures it. We all have defects and good qualities. If I had married a spendthrift he would have ruined me. If I had given myself to an ardent young man, perhaps I could not have retained him; he might have left me, and I should have died of jealousy. For I am jealous!” she cried, with excitement like a passing storm. “But Monsieur de Mortsauf loves me as much as he can; all that his heart contains of affection he pours at my feet, like Magdalen’s cup of ointment. Believe me, a life of love is an exception to earthly laws; all flowers fade; great joys have a morrow of evil—if a morrow at all. Real life is anguish; its image is that nettle at the foot of the wall—no sun can reach it and it keeps green. Yet here, as in parts of the North, there are smiles in the sky, few but compensating for grief. Moreover, women naturally mothers live and love more through sacrifices than pleasures. Here I draw upon myself storms I fear may break upon my children or people; in doing so I feel something I cannot explain which gives secret courage. The resignation of night carries me through the day after. God does not leave me comfortless. Time was when my children’s condition filled me with despair; to-day as they advance they grow healthier and stronger. And then, after all, our home is improved and beautified, our means improving. Who knows but Monsieur de Mortsauf’s old age may be a blessing? Those who stand before the Great Judge with palms in their hands, leading to Him beings who cursed their lives, they have turned sorrows into joy. If my sufferings bring about my family’s happiness, are they sufferings at all?”

“Yes,” I said, “they are; but they were necessary, as mine have been, to make us understand the true flavor of the fruit ripened on our rocks. Now, surely, we shall taste it together; surely we may admire its wonders, the sweetness of affection it has poured into our souls, that inward sap which revives searing leaves—Good God! do you not understand me?” I cried, falling into mystical language. “See the paths by which we approached each other; what magnet led us through that ocean of bitterness to these springs at the foot of those hills? Have we not followed the same star? We stand before the cradle of a divine child whose carol will renew the world for us, teach us a love of life, give nights their long-lost sleep, and days their gladness. What hand is this that year by year has tied new cords between us? Are we not more than brother and sister? That which heaven has joined we must not keep asunder. The sufferings you reveal are the seeds scattered by the sower for a harvest ripening in sunshine. Shall we not gather it sheaf by sheaf? What strength in me that I dare address you thus! Answer, or I will never again cross that river!”

“You have spared me the word love,” she said sternly, “but you have spoken of a sentiment I know nothing of and which is not permitted me. You are a child; and again I pardon you, but for the last time. Understand, Monsieur, that my heart is intoxicated with motherhood. I love Monsieur de Mortsauf neither from social duty nor from desire to win blessings, but from an irresistible feeling that fastens all the fibres of my heart upon him. Was my marriage a mistake? My sympathy for misfortune led to it. It is women’s part to heal woes, to comfort those who rush into the breach and return wounded. How shall I make you understand me? I have felt selfish pleasure in seeing you amuse him; is not that pure motherhood? Did I not make you see just now the three children to whom I am bound, to whom I shall never fail, on whom I strive to shed a healing dew and the light of my soul without withdrawing or adulterating a single particle? Do not embitter the mother’s milk! Though as a wife I am invulnerable, you must never again speak thus to me. If you do not respect this simple command, the door of this house will be closed to you. I believed in pure friendship, a voluntary brotherhood more real than blood. I was mistaken. I wanted a friend who was not a judge, a friend to listen in moments of weakness when reproof is killing, a sacred friend from whom I had nothing to fear. Youth is noble, truthful, capable of sacrifice; seeing your persistency I believed in some divine purpose; I thought I should find a soul that would be mine, a heart to pour my troubles into when they deluged mine. Could I but have this friend, my life precious to these children might be prolonged until Jacques grew to manhood. But that is selfish! The Laura of Petrarch cannot be lived again. I must die at my post, like a soldier, friendless. My confessor is harsh, austere, and—my aunt is dead.”

Two large tears filled her eyes, gleamed in moonlight, and rolled down her cheeks; I caught them and drank them with avidity, moved by her words and the thought of ten years of secret woe, of wasted feelings, of ceaseless care—years of lofty heroism. She looked at me with gentle stupefaction.

“It is the first communion of love,” I said. “Yes, I am now sharer of your sorrows. I am united to your soul as our souls are united to Christ in the sacrament. To love, even without hope, is happiness. What woman could give me a joy equal to receiving your tears! I accept the contract ending in suffering to myself. I give myself to you with no ulterior thought. I will be to you that which you will me to be—”

She stopped me with a motion and said in a deep voice, “I consent if you will promise never to tighten the bonds that bind us.”

“Yes,” I said; “but the less you grant the more evidence of possession I ought to have.”

“You begin by distrusting me,” she replied, with melancholy doubt.

“No, I speak from pure happiness. Listen; give me a name by which no one calls you; a name to be ours only, like the feeling that unites us.”

“That is much to ask,” she said, “but I will show I am not petty. Monsieur de Mortsauf calls me Blanche. One only person, my dear aunt, called me Henriette. I will be Henriette once more, to you.”

I took her hand and kissed it. She left it in mine with the trustfulness that makes a woman so far superior to men; a trustfulness that shames us. She leaned on the balustrade and gazed at the river.

“Are you not unwise to rush to friendship’s extremes? You have drained the cup at a draught. It is true a real feeling is never piecemeal; it must be whole. Monsieur de Mortsauf,” she added after a short silence, “is above all things loyal and brave. Perhaps for my sake you will forget what he said to-day; if he forgets to-morrow I will tell him what occurred. Do not come to Clochegourde for a few days; he will respect you more if you do not. On Sunday, after church, he will go to you. I know him; he will wish to undo the wrong, and he will like you better for treating him as a responsible man.”

“Five days without seeing you, without hearing your voice!”

“Do not put such warmth into your manner of speaking to me,” she said.

We walked twice round the terrace in silence. Then she said with a tone of command that she had taken possession of my soul, “It is late; we will part.”

I wished to kiss her hand; she hesitated, then gave it and said entreatingly: “Never take it unless I give it to you; leave me my freedom; if not, I shall be simply a thing of yours, and that ought not to be.”

“Adieu,” I said.

She opened the lower terrace gate for me. Just as she was about to close it she opened it again and offered her hand, saying: “You have been truly good to me this evening; you have comforted my whole future; take it, my friend, take it.”

I kissed her hand again and again, and when I raised my eyes I saw tears in hers. She returned to the upper terrace and I watched her from the meadow. When on the road to Frapesle I saw her white robe in a moonbeam; a few moments later a light was in her bedroom.

“Oh, my Henriette!” I cried, “to you I pledge the purest love that ever shone upon this earth.”

I turned at every step as I regained Frapesle. Ineffable contentment filled me. A way was open for the devotion swelling in youth and long inert in me. Like the priest entering a new life, my vows were taken; I was consecrated. A simple “Yes” had bound me to keep my love within my soul and never to abuse our friendship by leading this woman step by step to love. Noble feelings were awakened, and I heard their murmur. Before confining myself within a room I stopped beneath an azure heaven sown with stars, listened to ring-dove plaints of my heart, heard again the simple tones of that ingenuous confidence, gathered in the air emanations of that soul which henceforth must ever seek me. How grand that woman seemed, with absolute forgetfulness of self, her religion of mercy to wounded hearts, her declared allegiance to her legal yoke. She stood serene upon her pyre of saint and martyr. I adored her face as it shone in darkness. Suddenly I fancied a meaning in her words, a mysterious sign making her sublime. Perhaps she longed that I should be to her what she was to the little world around her. Perhaps she sought to draw from me strength and consolation, putting me within her sphere, her equal, or perhaps above her. The stars, some bold builders say, communicate light and motion. This thought lifted me to ethereal regions. I entered once more the heaven of former visions; I found a meaning for my childhood miseries in the illimitable happiness they had led me to.

Spirits quenched by tears, hearts misunderstood, saintly Clarissa Harlowe forgotten, children neglected, exiles innocent of wrong, all ye who enter life through barren ways, on whom men’s faces look coldly and ears close, cease your complaints! You alone can know the infinitude of joy in that moment when one heart opens to you, one ear listens, one look answers. A single day effaces past evil. Sorrow, despondency, despair, and melancholy passed but not forgotten are links by which the soul fastens to its mate. Woman falls heir to all our past and gives them back ennobled; she explains those former griefs as destiny’s payment for joys eternal which she brings when our souls are wedded. Angels alone can utter the new name of that sacred love, and none but women, dear martyrs, truly know what Madame de Mortsauf became to me—to me, poor and desolate.

As I read that letter I felt the maternal heart beneath my fingers; I understood why the countess had forbidden me to open it in Touraine—she feared I would fall at her feet and weep.

I met my brother Charles, till then a stranger. He showed a haughtiness that kept us apart and prevented brotherly affection. The duke’s kindness became sincere protection when he saw my devotion to the Bourbons; he presented me to the king. Devotion counted at Ghent, and Louis XVIII judged men with a clear eye—I had the happiness of pleasing him.

A letter from Madame de Mortsauf, brought by a Vendean emissary, enclosed a note telling me Jacques was ill. Torn between care of her son and household obligations, the countess needed a friend to lighten her burden. Though ambitious and eager to rival my brother—then at the Congress of Vienna—I resolved, moved by the countess’s sad face, to leave Ghent and return to Clochegourde in the king’s service. Fortune favored me: the emissary could not return and the king wanted a faithful messenger. The Duc de Lenoncourt requested the mission for me; after an audience with the king I set out, able to serve my true sovereign and to go back to Henriette.

I carried the king’s instructions successfully in Paris and in the Vendée. By late May, being denounced to Bonapartist authorities, I fled, traveling on foot through parks and woods across the Bocage and Poitou. I reached Saumur, Chinon, and then the woods of Nueil, where the count found me and brought me safely to Clochegourde.

“Jacques is better,” he said. When we entered the salon Henriette exclaimed, “Felix!” We looked at one another with the hunger of two souls making up for lost months. She had prayed for my safety and prepared my room. At dinner the news of Waterloo and the Allies’ march to Paris was of political consequence, but for us the small domestic joys mattered: she told me, with shining pleasure, “You shall have ice to-day!”—an ingenuous proof of affection that stirred me. Her health and spirit revived in days; my heart opened in response. Absence, for ardent souls, strengthens love; reunion was like spring reviving a valley. Her recovery was delicate; she told me of nights by Jacques’s bedside, of having lived for three months within herself, in a dark palace, alternately listening for moans and a voice.

When she spoke of visions and said, “While I slept my heart watched,” the count interrupted harshly, calling her “half crazy.” He wounded her; she asked whether he had no indulgence for her weakness as a woman. The count’s habitual arrows pierced her, and though he regretted the murmur immediately, the pain showed the long strain of his treatment.

I tried to divert her mind to household matters. She told me of the success of her agricultural plans—the new leases, planted trees, and income—hoping that if the king returned their pension would be restored and Jacques’s fortune secured. She revealed a kind of spiritual insight: in fits of deep meditation she saw luminous or misty visions of her children, which she took as divine signs; she saw me brilliantly illuminated and heard a mental voice guiding me. I clung to her, cried that my obedience was due to none but her, and she smiled. She advised me, when the king returned, to go to Paris—“it may be degrading to beg, but dangerous to be out of the way of receiving favors.” She called me “my son” in her heart, and took my arm contentedly.

That evening I witnessed a scene that moved me: the whole household knelt in prayer, made solemn by Madeleine’s childish voice. The count presented me with backgammon; the countess brought her embroidery and said it had languished while her child was ill. That night, my love burning, I went to her door and listened to her breathing; foolish and dangerous, it was the impulse that urges some men to the brink of ruin to know if they can bear it.

I plucked flowers and made bouquets; I spent days at Clochegourde and dined at Frapesle sometimes. The French army held Tours; though my presence seemed to strengthen the countess, she urged me to go to Paris by Châteauroux and Orléans, to be near the king. I promised to write every night. That night I was appointed master of petitions to the Council of State and given a private place in Louis XVIII’s service—a confidential position that became the source of my prosperity. Madame de Mortsauf had judged rightly; she guided and purified me. The post brought rooms at the Château, a carriage, allowances, and proximity to the sovereign—advantages as important as talent in that world.

The king once teased me, calling me “Mademoiselle de Vandenesse” for my reserve, but he favored me. He arranged that I should have a colleague and leave for six months; I returned to Touraine a changed man—dressed with Parisian refinement and feeling the satisfaction of having repaid, in part, the woman whose counsels had shaped me. My love for her had been chastened into something that combined worship, tenderness, and sorrow. I remained her friend, steward, and confidant, conscious that our intimacy had grown out of faithful service rather than conquest. The count’s illness had opened to me the inner life of the one woman I loved—her conscience, her maternal heart, and her piety—a love that was at once celestial and austere, and which, in the end, prevented the consummation I desired but sanctified the devotion I offered.

It was at a time when I was most deeply moved, when I lived in that soul I strove to enliven with the luminous breeze of morning and the hope of crimson evenings, that I met in the salons of the Élysée-Bourbon one of those illustrious ladies who reign in society. Immensely rich, of pure lineage, married to a distinguished old British peer, these advantages enhanced her beauty, graces, manners, and wit, which dazzled before they charmed. She was the idol of the day, reigning securely because she possessed the “iron hand in the velvet glove.”

You know the peculiarities of the English: their distance, the coldness of the Channel they set between themselves and anyone not properly presented. Humanity seems an ant-hill they tread upon; they know only the few they admit. To them, the people are as if they were not. The British have an image of their island—law ruling everything, life automatic, virtue a machine run by clockwork. Fortifications of polished steel rise around the Englishwoman behind her gilded household cage, making her irresistibly attractive. No people have trained married women so carefully to hypocrisy by holding them strictly between two extremes—death or social station—allowing no middle path; for them it is all or nothing. This, joined to the scorn their customs teach, makes the Englishwoman a being apart. She is forced to be virtuous yet ready to yield, condemned to live a lie in her heart, delightful in outward appearance—everything in England rests on appearances. Hence the special charms of their women: an enthusiasm for a love that is their life, meticulous care of person, and a delicate passion, as Shakespeare rendered in Juliet.

You, who envy them, know these white sirens: impenetrable yet fathomable, who believe love suffices love, who turn enjoyments to satiety by never varying them; whose soul has one note only, voice one syllable—an ocean of love in themselves. My relations with the Marchioness of Dudley had a disastrous fame. At an age when the senses rule and when my own had been repressed, the image of the saint bearing her martyrdom at Clochegourde shone so vividly before me that I could resist seductions. It was the lustre of this fidelity that attracted Lady Dudley’s attention. My resistance stimulated her passion. What she chiefly desired, like many Englishwomen, was singularity; she wanted pepper with her heart’s food. The dull regularity of their lives leads them to adore the romantic and welcome difficulty. The more I retreated, the more impassioned Lady Dudley became. The struggle, which she gloried in, excited curiosity and thus seemed to make triumph obligatory. Ah! Read me indulgently. This concerns one of the most interesting problems of human life—a crisis to which most men are subjected. This beautiful, slender, milk-white creature, so yielding and gentle, has, in truth, an iron nature. No horse can conquer her vigorous wrist; her hand seems soft but never tires. She is slender, muscular, so strong she fears no struggle; men cannot follow her on horseback. Her body never perspires; it inhales the fire of the atmosphere and lives in water under pain of not living at all. Her love is African; her desires are whirlwinds of the desert—azure, love-laden, changeless skies. What a contrast to Clochegourde! the east and the west; one drawing moisture for herself, the other exuding her soul, wrapping her dear ones in luminous atmosphere.

Have you reflected on the meaning of English manners, customs, and morals? Is it not the deification of matter, a carefully applied Epicureanism? England is materialist—religion and morality exist, but divine spirituality is absent. England excels in that science of existence which makes women’s slippers exquisite, linens fragrant, furniture polished, tea served at fixed hours; in short, it makes matter a clean, shining pulp in which the soul expires of enjoyment and monotony, a life without contrasts that turns you into a machine.

Thus I came to know, in British luxury, a woman perhaps unique among her sex, who caught me with love excited by my indifference, to which I opposed stern continence—one of those loves with overwhelming charm, an electricity that carries us to the skies. A love monstrously ungrateful, which laughs at the bodies it kills; love without memory, cruel, like the policy of the English nation; a love to which most men yield. You see the problem? Man is matter and spirit; animality ends and the angel begins. There is the struggle between the future destiny we sense and the influence of anterior instincts—carnal love and divine love. One man combines them, another abstains; some seek satisfaction from the whole sex, others idealize one woman who is their universe; some float between matter and soul, others spiritualize the body, demanding of it what it cannot give.

If you consider the dislikes and affinities from different organisms, and the mistakes from hopes of those who live by mind, heart, or action, who think, feel, or act, you will have indulgence for sorrows to which the world is pitiless. Lady Dudley gratified the instincts, organs, appetites—the mistress of the body; Madame de Mortsauf was the wife of the soul. The mistress’ love has limits; matter is finite and saturable; often I felt a void even near Lady Dudley. Infinitude is the heart’s region; love had no limits at Clochegourde. I loved Lady Dudley passionately; she was magnificent in animality and superior in mind, her sparkling satire wide-ranging. But I adored Henriette. At night I wept with happiness, in the morning with remorse.

Some women hide jealousy under angelic kindness; they are like Lady Dudley, over thirty, knowing how to feel and calculate; they press the juices of today and think of the future. They can stifle a natural moan with the will of a huntsman. Without naming Madame de Mortsauf, Arabella sought to kill her in my soul, where she always found her, her passion increasing with consciousness of that invincible love. Intending to triumph by comparisons, she was never suspicious or complaining; like a lioness with prey she watched that nothing disturb her feast, guarding me like a captive. I wrote to Henriette under her eyes, but she never read a line; she never sought to know to whom my letters were addressed. I had liberty; she seemed to think, “If I lose him it shall be my own fault,” and proudly relied on a love that would have given me her life—she often told me she would kill herself if I left her. “In India that is a distinction reserved for the higher classes,” she said once; “we should rise to greatness by unparalleled devotions. To love with our heads up, in defiance of law; to die for the idol we have chosen; to never betray that man; these are heights common women cannot reach.”

Pride was her instrument; she flattered vanities by deifying them. She put me so high that she might live at my feet; the seductions of her spirit were expressed in attitudes of subservience and submission. How shall I describe those first six months when I was lost in enervating enjoyments, in the meshes of a love fertile in pleasures and varied with cleverness learned by experience, yet hiding that knowledge beneath transports of passion? These pleasures, the revelation of the poetry of the senses, bind young men to older women; they are the chain of the galley-slave, leaving an ineffaceable brand and filling the soul with distaste for pure, innocent love decked only with flowers.

Recalling my early dreams at Clochegourde, I found many sophistries to excuse draining that jeweled cup. Often, lost in lassitude, my soul floating far from earth, I thought these pleasures might abolish matter and let spirit soar. Sometimes Lady Dudley, profiting by my exaltation, wrung blasphemies from my lips against the angel at Clochegourde. Once a traitor, I became a scoundrel. I continued to write to Madame de Mortsauf in the tone of the lad she had first known; but her gift of second sight terrified me when I thought what ruin an indiscreet word might bring. Often, in pleasure, a sudden horror seized me; I heard Henriette’s name above me, like the voice in Scripture demanding, “Cain, where is thy brother Abel?”

At last my letters went unanswered. I was seized with anxiety and wished to leave for Clochegourde. Arabella did not oppose it; she even proposed to accompany me to Touraine, seeing the journey might detach me from my rival; blind with fear and guilelessly unsuspicious, I did not see her trap. Lady Dudley proposed humble concessions: she would stay near Tours, disguised, refrain from daylight outings, meet me only in the evening. I left Tours on horseback, choosing the road I had traversed on foot six years earlier and stopped beneath my walnut-tree. From there I saw Madame de Mortsauf in white at the terrace. Instantly I rode toward her across country. She heard the step of the swallow of the desert and when I pulled up at the terrace she said, “Oh, you here!”

Those three words blasted me. She knew my treachery. Who had told her? Her mother, whose hateful letter she later showed me. The feeble, indifferent voice, once so full of life, revealed settled grief, the breath of flowers cut and left to wither. The tempest of infidelity had torn through her soul, leaving a desert where verdure had been. I led my horse through the gate; he lay on the grass at my command and the countess came forward slowly, exclaiming, “What a fine animal!” She stood with folded arms lest I try to take her hand; I guessed her meaning.

“I will let Monsieur de Mortsauf know you are here,” she said, leaving me.

I stood confounded, watching her noble, slow figure mount the terraces of her château, pride and torture combined. I cursed Arabella in an imprecation that might have killed her had she heard. Presently the whole family came down; Jacques ran eagerly; Madeleine, a gazelle with mournful eyes, walked with her mother. Monsieur de Mortsauf came to me with open arms and kissed me on both cheeks, crying, “Felix, I know now that I owed you my life.”

Madame de Mortsauf stood with her back toward us under pretext of showing the horse to Madeleine. “Ha, the devil! that’s what women are,” cried the count; “admiring your horse!” Madeleine turned and I kissed her hand, looking at the countess who colored.

“Madeleine seems much better,” I said.

“Poor little girl!” said the countess, kissing her forehead.

“Yes, for the time being they are all well,” answered the count. “Except me, Felix; I am as battered as an old tower about to fall.”

“The general is still depressed,” I remarked to Madame de Mortsauf.

“We all have our blue devils—is not that the English term?” she replied.

The party walked toward the vineyard with a sense that something serious had happened. She had no wish to be alone with me, though I was her guest.

“But about your horse? why isn’t he attended to?” said the count.

“You see I am wrong if I think of him, and wrong if I do not,” said the countess.

“Well, yes,” said the count; “there is a time to do things, and a time not to do them.”

“I will attend to him,” I said, finding the greeting intolerable. “No one but myself can put him into his stall; my groom is coming by the coach from Chinon.”

“I suppose your groom is from England,” she said.

“That is where they all come from,” remarked the count, who brightened as his wife seemed depressed. Her coldness gave him an opportunity to oppose her, and he overwhelmed me with friendliness.

“My dear Felix,” he said, taking my hand, “pray forgive Madame de Mortsauf; women are whimsical. But it is owing to their weakness; they cannot have the evenness of temper we owe to our strength of character. She really loves you, I know it; only—”

While the count spoke Madame de Mortsauf gradually moved away to leave us alone. “Felix,” said the count softly, looking at his wife, “I don’t know what is going on in Madame de Mortsauf’s mind, but for the last six weeks her disposition has completely changed. She, so gentle, so devoted hitherto, is now extraordinarily peevish.”

Manette later told me the countess had fallen into a depression making her indifferent to the count’s provocations. No longer finding a soft surface to wound, the man became uneasy as a child whose prey ceases to move. He now needed a confidant, as the hangman a helper.

“Try to question Madame de Mortsauf,” he urged. “Find out what is the matter. A woman always has secrets from her husband; perhaps she will tell you. I would sacrifice everything to make her happy, even half my days or fortune. She is necessary to my life. If I have not that angel at my side as I grow old I shall be the most wretched of men. I do desire to die easy. Tell her I shall not be here long. Yes, Felix, my poor friend, I am going fast. I hide the truth from everyone; why worry them? The trouble is in the stomach. I have discovered the true cause: my sensibility is killing me. All our feelings affect the gastric centre.”

“Then do you mean,” I said, smiling, “that the best-hearted people die of their stomachs?”

“Don’t laugh, Felix; too keen sensibility irritates the mucous membrane of the stomach; secretions change, digestion becomes capricious; sharp pains come, worsen, and the disorder reaches a crisis like a slow poison; the mucous membrane thickens, the pylorus indurates and forms a scirrhus, of which the patient dies. I have reached that point. Look at my yellow skin, feverish eyes, excessive thinness. I am withering. I brought the seeds of the disease home from the emigration; my marriage, which might have healed, increased the wound. I have ceaseless fears for my children, domestic jars, a fortune to remake, economies that required privations. I have even worse trouble: though Blanche is an angel, she does not understand me; she is as silly as a child. When our fortune was remade, it was too late; the appetite was vitiated. Then my severe illness, mismanaged by Origet. I have not six months to live.”

I listened in terror. On meeting the countess I had noticed her yellow skin and feverish eyes. I led the count toward the house seeming to listen, but my thoughts were all with Henriette; I wanted to observe her. We found her in the salon, listening to a mathematics lesson for Jacques while showing Madeleine an embroidery stitch. Formerly she would have laid aside every occupation the day of my arrival to be with me. My love was so deep that I drove back grief and thought only of the fatal yellow tint on that celestial face, which resembled the halo painters give saints. I felt the icy wind of death pass over me. When her eyes, no longer softened by their former light, fell upon me I shuddered; I noticed changes caused by grief I had not seen outdoors. The slender lines upon her forehead had deepened; her temples with violet veins seemed burning and concave; her eyes were sunk, their circles browned—discolored like fruit in decay. I, who had sought to pour happiness into her soul, embittered the spring she hoped would refresh life and renew courage. I sat beside her and said with tears, “Are you satisfied with your health?”

“Yes,” she answered, plunging her eyes into mine. “My health is there,” she added, motioning to Jacques and Madeleine.

Madeleine, fifteen, had victoriously overcome anaemia and was now a woman. She had grown tall; roses had bloomed in her cheeks; she had lost childlike unconcern and dropped her eyes; her movements were slow and infrequent like her mother’s; her figure slim, the bust developing; an instinct of coquetry smoothed her magnificent black hair. She was like a delicate medieval statuette. Health had made her cheeks velvety and her throat silken. She was to live, the bud of a superb development. Jacques, fragile at seventeen, had a large head and feverish eyes with a deep voice, seeming to hide danger beneath roses. It was Henriette’s intellect and soul devouring a body without stamina; Jacques had the milk-white skin and high color of young English women doomed to consumption—an appearance that deceives. Seeing him drawing algebraic figures I shivered at death hidden beneath the roses and thanked my ignorance. Henriette’s maternal pleasure shone.

“When I see my children thus, happiness stills my griefs,” she said. “If other affections fail, the duties done and crowned with success are compensation. Jacques will be like you, a man of highest education; Madeleine will have a noble heart; my motherhood, once tried and tortured, is happy now. My life is full, rich. God makes my joy blossom in these sanctified affections, and turns to bitterness those that might have led me astray—”

“Good!” cried the abbé. “Monsieur le vicomte begins to know as much as I—”

Just then Jacques coughed. “Enough for today,” the countess said, “above all, no chemistry. Go for a ride, Jacques.” She let her son kiss her with tender, dignified pleasure. “Go, dear, but take care.”

“But,” I said, “do you feel ill?”

“Oh, sometimes, in my stomach. If I were in Paris I should have the honors of gastritis.”

“My mother suffers very much and often,” said Madeleine.

“Ah! does my health interest you?” she said, and my eyes counted the roses on the salon sofa.

“This situation is intolerable,” I whispered.

“Did I create it?” she asked. “Dear child,” she said aloud with cruel levity, “don’t you read history? France and England are enemies. Madeleine knows that; a broad, cold sea separates them.”

The vases on the mantel had given place to candelabra, no doubt to deprive me of filling them with flowers; I found them later in my room. When my servant arrived I went out to give orders; he had brought certain things I wished to place in my room.

“Felix,” said the countess, “do not make a mistake. My aunt’s old room is now Madeleine’s. Yours is over the count’s.”

Though guilty, I had a heart; those words were dagger thrusts coldly given at its tenderest spot. Moral sufferings depend on sensitiveness. The countess had trod each round of life’s ladder; but for that reason the kindest woman was now as cruel as she had been beneficent. I looked at Henriette, but she averted her head. I went to my room and burst into tears. Henriette heard me as she entered with a bunch of flowers.

“Henriette,” I said, “will you never forgive a wrong that is indeed excusable?”

“Do not call me Henriette,” she said. “She no longer exists, poor soul; but you may feel sure of Madame de Mortsauf, a devoted friend, who will listen and love you. Felix, we will talk later. If you have any tenderness for me let me grow accustomed to seeing you. When words will not rend my heart, if ever I recover courage, I will speak; not till then. Look at the valley,” she added, pointing to the Indre, “it hurts me, I love it still.”

“Ah, perish England and all her women! I will send my resignation to the king; I will live and die here, pardoned.”

“No, love her; love that woman! Henriette is not. This is no play, and you should know it.”

She left, betraying in her tone the extent of her wounds. I ran after her and held her, “Do you no longer love me?”

“You have done me more harm than all my other troubles put together. Today I suffer less, therefore I love you less. Be kind; do not increase my pain; if you suffer, remember that I live.”

She withdrew her hand cold and moist from mine and darted through the corridor.

At dinner the count subjected me to an unexpected torture. “So the Marchioness of Dudley is not in Paris?” he said.

I blushed but answered, “No.”

“She is not in Tours?”

“She is not divorced; she can go back to England. Her husband would be glad if she would return.”

“Has she children?” asked Madame de Mortsauf in a changed voice.

“Two sons,” I replied.

“Where are they?”

“In England, with their father.”

“Come, Felix,” interposed the count; “be frank; is she as handsome as they say?”

“How can you ask him such a question?” cried the countess. “Is not the woman you love always the handsomest?”

“Yes, always,” I said firmly, with a glance she could not sustain.

“You are a happy fellow,” said the count. “Ha! in my young days I should have gone mad over such a conquest—”

“Hush!” said Madame de Mortsauf, reminding him of Madeleine by a look.

“I am not a child,” he said.

After the table I followed the countess to the terrace. “How is it possible that some women can sacrifice their children to a man?” she exclaimed.

“Yes, and such women would give even more if they had it; they sacrifice everything.”

The world suddenly reversed before her; the grandeur of the thought struck her. She stood dumb, then rose grand and saintly.

“Love her well, Felix,” she said with tears. “She shall be my happy sister. I will forgive her the harm she has done me if she gives you what you could not have here. You are right; I have never told you that I loved you, and I never have loved you as the world loves. But if she is a mother how can she love you so?”

“Dear saint,” I answered, “I must be less moved than I am to explain how you soar above her. She is a woman of earth, daughter of decaying races; you are the child of heaven, an angel worthy of worship; you have my heart, she my flesh only. She knows this and it fills her with despair; she would change parts with you even for cruellest martyrdom. But all is irremediable: to you the soul, the thoughts, the pure love; to her the desires and joys of passing passion; to you remembrance forever, to her oblivion—”

“Tell me that again!” she cried, sitting down and bursting into tears. “If that be so, virtue, purity, a mother’s love are not mistakes. Oh, pour that balm upon my wounds! Repeat the words that bear me back to heaven.” She turned to me, praying for a look or a sacred word—she forgave me for the sufferings of the last two months.

“Henriette, there are mysteries in men’s lives of which you know nothing,” I said. “I met you at an age when heart feelings stifle desires; many scenes, the memory of which will kindle my soul to death, must have told you that age was drawing to a close, and your constant triumph still prolonged its mute delights. A love without possession is maintained by exasperated desire, but there comes a moment when all is suffering within—for in this we have no resemblance to you. We possess a power we cannot abdicate. Nature cannot long be silenced; some trifling accident awakens it to violence. No, I have not loved, but I have thirsted in the desert.”

“The desert!” she said bitterly. “How he reasons! faithful hearts are not so learned.”

“Henriette, do not quarrel with me for a phrase. No, my soul has not vacillated, but I have not been master of my senses. That woman is not ignorant you are the only one I ever loved. She plays a secondary part in my life; she knows it and is resigned. I have the right to leave her as men leave courtesans.”

“And then?”

“She tells me she will kill herself,” I answered, thinking the threat would startle her. But she smiled disdainfully. “My dear conscience,” I continued, “if you would take into account my resistance and the seductions that led to my fall you would understand the fatal—”

“Yes, fatal!” she cried. “I believed in you too much. I believed you capable of priestly virtue. All is over. I owe you much; you have extinguished in me the fires of earthly life. The worst of the way is over; age is coming on. I am ailing now, soon ill; I can never be the brilliant fairy who showers you with favors. Be faithful to Lady Dudley. Madeleine, whom I was training to be yours, ah! who will have her now? Poor Madeleine!” she repeated. “I would you had heard her say to me when you came: ‘Mother, you are not kind to Felix!’ Dear creature!”

She looked in the warm setting sun. Seized with compassion for our shipwrecks she turned back to memories of our pure past. We were silent, eyes passing from the valley to fields, from Clochegourde to Frapesle, peopling the dream with my bouquets and fragrant language. It was her last hour of pleasure, enjoyed with the purity of her Catholic soul. She believed my words and saw where I placed her—in the skies.

“My friend,” she said, “I obey God; his hand is in all this.”

I did not know until later the depth of her words. We slowly returned up the terraces. She took my arm and leaned upon it resignedly, bleeding still but with a bandage.

“Human life is thus,” she said. “What had Monsieur de Mortsauf done to deserve his fate? It proves a better world exists. Alas, for those who walk in happier ways!”

She judged life so truly that cold statements revealed her disgust for all below. At the portico she dropped my arm and said, “If God has given us the desire for happiness, ought he not to take charge of innocent souls who have found sorrow only here? Either that must be so, or God is not, and our life is a cruel jest.”

She entered quickly and I found her crouching on the sofa.

“What is the matter?” I asked.

“I no longer know what is virtue,” she replied; “I have no consciousness of my own.”

We were silent, petrified, hearing those words fall like a stone. “If I am mistaken in my life she is right in hers,” she added at last. Thus her last struggle followed her last happiness. When the count came in she complained of illness. I conjured her to tell me where she suffered; she refused and went to bed, leaving me prey to remorse. Madeleine went with her mother, and next day I heard the countess had been seized with nausea from the day’s violent excitements. Thus I, who longed to give my life for hers, was killing her.

“Dear count,” I said to Monsieur de Mortsauf, who made me play backgammon, “I think the countess very seriously ill. There is time to save her; send for Origet and persuade her to follow his advice.”

“Origet, who half killed me?” cried the count. “No, no; I’ll consult Carbonneau.”

Those days were anguish—the beginning of paralysis of the heart—my vanity mortified, soul rent. One must have been the centre of all to understand the horror of the void about me. All things were the same, but the spirit that gave life to them was extinct. I now understood lovers’ desire never to see each other again when love has flown. To be nothing where once we were everything! I came at last to envy the dismal ignorance of my youth. My despair became so great that I thought the countess felt pity for it. One day after dinner, while walking by the river, I made a last effort for forgiveness. I sent Jacques on with his sister, left the count, and took Henriette to the punt.

“Henriette,” I said, “one word of forgiveness, or I fling myself into the Indre! I have sinned—it is true; but am I not like a faithful dog? I return ashamed. Strike me, bruise me, but give me back your heart.”

“Poor child,” she said, “are you not always my son?”

She took my arm and rejoined her children, leaving me to the count, who began to talk politics. “Let us go in,” I said; “you are bare-headed and the dew may injure you.”

“You pity me, my dear Felix,” he answered; “you understand me; my wife never tries to comfort me—on principle, perhaps.”

Never would she have walked home with her husband; it was now I who had to find excuses to join her. I found her with her children, explaining backgammon rules to Jacques.

“See there,” said the count, “it is for them that I am neglected. Husbands are always suppressed. The most virtuous woman has ways of satisfying her desire to rob conjugal affection.”

She said nothing and continued. “Jacques,” he called.

Jacques objected slightly. “Your father wants you; go at once, my son,” said his mother, pushing him. “They love me by order,” the old man said, sometimes perceiving the truth.

“Monsieur,” she answered, passing her hand over Madeleine’s smooth tresses, “do not be unjust; life is not easy. Perhaps the children are the virtues of a mother.”

“My dear,” said the count, “what you say signifies that women with no children would have no virtue and would leave husbands in the lurch.”

The countess rose hastily and took Madeleine to the portico.

“That’s marriage, my dear fellow,” remarked the count. “Do you mean to imply I am talking nonsense?” he cried, taking his son and going after her with a furious look.

“On the contrary, Monsieur, you frightened me. Your words hurt me cruelly,” she replied in a hollow voice. “If virtue does not consist in sacrificing everything to our children and husband, what is virtue?”

“Sac-ri-ficing!” cried the count, each syllable a blow. “What have you sacrificed to your children? What do you sacrifice to me? Speak! Answer. What is going on here?”

“Monsieur,” she replied, “would you be satisfied to be loved for love of God, or to know your wife virtuous for virtue’s sake?”

“Madame is right,” I interposed; “the noblest thing is to attribute our virtues to those whose happiness is our work. If a woman is involuntarily subject to feelings other than those society imposes, the more irresistible that feeling, the more virtuous she is in smothering it, sacrificing herself to husband and children. This theory does not apply to me, who show the contrary, nor to you whom it will never concern.”

“You have a noble soul, Felix,” said the count, slipping his arm round his wife’s waist and drawing her to say, “Forgive a poor sick man who wants to be loved more than he deserves.”

“There are some hearts all generosity,” she said, resting her head on his shoulder. The scene made her tremble; her comb fell, her hair rolled down, and she turned pale. The count, holding her, groaned as she fainted; he caught her and carried her to the salon, and we surrounded her. She held my hand as if to tell me we alone knew the secret of that simple scene.

“I do wrong,” she said to me in a low voice when the count left to fetch orange-flower water. “I have many wrongs to repent of towards you; I wished to fill you with despair when I ought to have received you mercifully. Dear, you are kindness itself, and I alone can appreciate it. Yes, I know there is kindness prompted by passion. Men have various ways—some from contempt, others from impulse, calculation, or indolence. But you, my friend, you have been absolutely kind.”

“If that be so,” I replied, “remember that all that is good or great in me comes through you. You know well that I am of your making.”

“That word is enough for any woman’s happiness,” she said as the count reentered. “I feel better,” she added, rising; “I want air.”

We went to the acacia-scented terrace. She had taken my right arm and pressed it to her heart, expressing sad thoughts she called dear. No doubt she would gladly have been alone with me, but she feared the shadows or walking where the Indre’s course could be seen. The country calms the old but excites the young; leafed ways and moonlight stir weak resolves. Two strokes of the bell announced prayer. The countess shivered.

“Dear Henriette, are you ill?”

“There is no Henriette,” she said. “Do not bring her back. She was capricious and exacting; now you have a friend whose courage was strengthened by words heaven dictated. We will talk later. It is my day to lead prayers.”

As she begged God’s help a tone entered her voice that struck all present. Did she foresee the emotion she would endure from my forgetfulness toward an engagement with Arabella?

“We have time to make three kings before the horses are harnessed,” said the count, dragging me back to the salon. “You can drive with my wife, I’ll go to bed.”

The game was stormy. The countess heard the count either from her room or Madeleine’s. “You show strange hospitality,” she said reentering.

I looked amazed; I could not accustom myself to her change; formerly she would have protected me from the count; then it gladdened her that I shared her sufferings for love. “I would give my life,” I whispered, “if I could hear you say again, ‘Poor dear, poor dear!’”

She lowered her eyes, remembering that moment, yet turned to me beneath her lids, joyfully. The count, losing the game, said he was tired and gave it up; we walked on the lawn while awaiting the carriage. When the count left us, such pleasure shone on my face that Madame de Mortsauf questioned me by a look.

“Henriette does exist,” I said. “You love me still. You wound me with an intention to break my heart. I may yet be happy!”

“There was but a fragment of that poor woman left, and you have now destroyed even that,” she said. “God be praised, he gives me strength to bear my righteous martyrdom. Yes, I still love you, and I might have erred; the Englishwoman shows me the abyss.”

We got into the carriage and the coachman asked orders.

“Take the road to Chinon by the avenue, and come back by Charlemagne moor and the road to Saché.”

“What day is it?” I asked eagerly.

“Saturday.”

“Then don’t go that way, madame, the road will be crowded with poultry-men and their carts.”

“Do as I told you,” she said. We knew each other’s tones too well. Henriette understood all.

“You did not think of the poultry-men when you appointed this evening,” she said with irony. “Lady Dudley is at Tours, and she is coming here to meet you; do not deny it. ‘What day is it?—the poultry-men!’ Did you ever take notice of such things in our old drives?”

“It only shows that at Clochegourde I forget everything,” I answered.

“She is coming to meet you?”

“Yes.”

“At what hour?”

“Half-past eleven.”

“Where?”

“On the moor.”

“Do not deceive me; is it not at the walnut-tree?”

“On the moor.”

“We will go there,” she said, “and I shall see her.”

Hearing this I resolved to marry Lady Dudley and end the miserable struggle threatening my sensibilities. My sullen silence wounded the countess, whose grandeur I misjudged.

“Do not be angry,” she said in her golden voice. “This is my punishment. You can never be loved as you are here,” she continued, laying my hand on her heart. “I confess it; but Lady Dudley has saved me. To her the stains; to me the glorious love of angels! I have traversed tracts of thought since you returned. I have judged life. To live for this earthly life is too debasing—the selfishness of the senses reigns over spirituality. The pleasures of passion are stormy, followed by enervating anxieties that impair the soul. I came to the sea and saw these tempests; I must escape to higher regions; I would perish on the shores of this vast sea. I see in you, as in others who grieved me, the guardian of my virtue. My life has been consistent with itself. The heart which should have taken root in my first affection was closed to me; death took my aunt; Monsieur de Mortsauf, to whom I vowed myself, has repeatedly, without knowing it, smitten me; his love is the simple egotism of our children. My children are my virtues. Have you not seen how my heart was harrowed for them? When I gave my hair did I not give myself? Why did I so often dress in white? because I seemed your lily; did you not see me here all in white? I have loved my children less, for intense affection is stolen from natural affections. Felix, do you not see that all suffering has meaning? That woman is the instrument of God’s anger; I will meet her without hatred; under pain of being neither Christian, wife, nor mother, I ought to love her. If I contributed to keep your heart unsoiled, that Englishwoman ought not to hate me. A woman should love the mother of the man she loves; I am your mother. What place have I sought in your heart? that left empty by Madame de Vandenesse. Yes, you have always complained of my coldness; yes, I am indeed your mother only. Forgive me, then, the involuntary harshness with which I met you on your return; a mother ought to rejoice that her son is so well loved—”

She laid her head on my breast, repeating, “Forgive me! oh, forgive me!” in a voice new and agonizing.

“You, Felix,” she continued animatedly, “you are the friend who can do no wrong. You have lost nothing in my heart; do not blame yourself. It was selfish in me to ask you to sacrifice joys to an impossible future; to realize it a woman must abandon her children, abdicate her position, renounce eternity. Many times I thought you higher than I; you were great, I petty and criminal. Well, it is settled; I can be to you no more than a light from above, sparkling and cold, unchanging. Only let me not love the brother I have chosen without return. Love me, cherish me! The love of a sister has no dangerous tomorrow. Turn our doubtful attachment into this sweet and sacred love. I will begin tonight by taking Lady Dudley’s hand.”

She did not weep; by these words she cast aside the last veil and showed by how many ties she had linked herself to me, how many chains I had hewn apart. Our emotions were so great we did not notice the heavy rain.

“Will Madame la comtesse wait here under shelter?” asked the coachman, pointing to Ballan’s inn.

She assented and we stayed near half an hour under the vault, surprising the inn-people. When the storm became a “brouee,” letting moonlight through, the coachman drove, and to my delight turned back the way we came.

“Follow my orders,” said the countess, gently.

We crossed Charlemagne moor amid renewed rain. Halfway I heard Arabella’s dog; a horse came from under oaks, jumped a ditch, and carried Lady Dudley to the moor to meet us.

“What pleasure to meet a love thus if it can be done without sin,” said Henriette.

The dog’s barking had told Arabella I was in the carriage. When we reached her she urged her horse to the road's side with equestrian skill for which she was famed and which to Henriette seemed marvelous.

“Amedee,” she said, the name in her English pronunciation charming.

“He is here, madame,” said the countess, looking at the fantastic creature visible in moonlight.

You know how swiftly two women examine each other. The Englishwoman recognized her rival and, gloriously English, gave us a look of insular contempt and disappeared into the underbrush.

“Drive quickly to Clochegourde,” cried the countess, for that cutting look was an axe blow.

The coachman turned to the Chinon road. As we neared the moor we heard Arabella’s furious gallop. “She is going; you will lose her forever,” said Henriette.

“Let her go,” I answered, “and without regret.”

“Oh, poor woman!” cried the countess with compassionate horror. “Where will she go?”

“Back to La Grenadière, near Saint-Cyr,” I said, “where she is staying.”

Just as we entered Clochegourde’s avenue Arabella’s dog barked joyfully and bounded up to the carriage.

“She is here before us!” cried the countess; then added, “I have never seen a more beautiful woman. What a hand and figure! Her complexion outdoes the lily; her eyes are like diamonds. But she rides too well; she loves to display her strength; I think her violent, too active, too bold for our conventions. The woman who recognizes no law listens only to her caprices. Those who seek to shine do not have constancy. Love needs tranquility; I picture it like a vast lake where two beings live on a flowery isle far from the world. Still, love must take the character’s imprint. Lady Dudley is the strong woman who can traverse distances and act with a man’s vigor; she would rescue her lover and kill jailers; while others can only love with their whole souls, kneel to pray, and die. Which suits you best? That is the question. Yes, Lady Dudley must surely love; she has made many sacrifices. Perhaps she will love you when you have ceased to love her!”

“Dear angel,” I said, “let me ask: how is it you know these things?”

“Every sorrow teaches; I have suffered much and my knowledge is vast.”

My servant, hearing orders, held my horse ready in the avenue. Arabella’s dog had scented the horse, and his mistress, drawn by curiosity, followed the animal through the woods.

“Go and make your peace,” said Henriette, smiling without sadness. “Say to Lady Dudley how much she mistakes my intention; I wished to show her the treasure which has fallen to her; my heart holds none but kind feelings, neither anger nor contempt. Explain that I am her sister, not her rival.”

“I shall not go,” I said.

“Have you never discovered,” she said loftily, “that certain propitiations are insulting? Go!”

I rode to Lady Dudley to learn her mind. The dog led me to an oak and Arabella galloped up crying, “Come! away! away!” I followed her to Saint-Cyr, which we reached about midnight.

“That lady is in perfect health,” said Arabella as she dismounted, her satire meaning “I should have died!”

“I forbid you to utter any sarcasms about Madame de Mortsauf,” I said.

“Do I displease your Grace by remarking upon the perfect health of one so dear to your heart? Frenchwomen hate, I am told, even their lover’s dog. In England we love all that our masters love; we hate all they hate. Permit me therefore to love this lady as much as you love her. Only, my dear child,” she added clasping me in arms damp with rain, “if you betray me, I shall not be found lying or standing, not in a carriage with lackeys, nor on moors, nor in my bed; I shall live no longer. I was born in Lancashire, where women die for love. Know you, and give you up? I will yield you to none, not even Death.”

She led me to her rooms where comfort spread its charms. “Love her, dear,” I said warmly. “She loves you sincerely, not in jest.”

“Sincerely! you poor child!” she said, unfastening her habit. While her waiting-woman arranged her hair I tried to show Arabella Henriette’s noble character, repeating many of her great thoughts. Arabella seemed inattentive but lost nothing.

“I am delighted,” she said, “to learn your taste for pious conversation. There’s an old vicar on one estate who writes sermons better than anyone; the country-people like him. I’ll write to my father to send him by steamboat; you can meet him in Paris, and once you have heard him you will never wish to listen to any one else—he won’t give shocks but will lull you. Every evening you can satisfy your passion for sermons with dinner. English morality, I assure you, is superior to that of Touraine as our cutlery and horses are to your knives and turf. Do me the kindness to listen; promise me. I am only a woman; I can love and die for you; but I never studied at Eton or Oxford; I am no doctor of laws or reverend; I can’t preach morality; I am altogether unfit. I don’t blame your tastes; you might have others more depraved and I should conform, for I want you to find near me everything you like—pleasures of love, of food, of piety, good claret, and virtuous Christians. Shall I wear hair-cloth to-night? She is very lucky to suit you in morality. From what college did she graduate? Poor I, who can only give myself, who can only be your slave—”

“Then why did you rush away when I wanted to bring you together?”

“Are you crazy, Amedee? I could go from Paris to Rome disguised as a valet; I would do the most unreasonable thing for your sake; but how can you expect me to speak to a woman on the public road who has never been presented—and who would have preached to me? I speak to peasants; if hungry I would ask a workman to share his bread and pay him in guineas—that is proper; but to stop a carriage on the highway is not in my code. You poor child know only how to love; you don’t know how to live. Besides, I am not like you yet; I don’t like morality. Still, I am capable of great efforts; I will learn to preach; you shall have no more kisses without verses of the Bible interlarded.”

She used and abused her power when she saw my ardent eyes. She triumphed, and I complacently told myself the woman who loses all, sacrifices the future, and makes love her only virtue is far above Catholic polemics.

“So she loves herself better than she loves you?” Arabella went on. “She sets something not you above you. Is that love? How can women value anything except what you value in us? No woman, no matter how fine a moralist, is the equal of a man. Tread upon us, kill us; never embarrass your lives on our account. It is for us to die, for you to live, great and honored. For us the dagger in your hand; for you our pardoning love. Does the sun think of gnats in his beams? they stay while they can and when he withdraws they die—”

“Or fly somewhere else,” I said.

“Yes, somewhere else,” she replied with indifference that would have piqued any man. “Do you really think it is worthy of womanhood to make a man eat his bread buttered with virtue and to persuade him religion is incompatible with love? Am I a reprobate? A woman either gives herself or she refuses. But to refuse and moralize is a double wrong, contrary to the rule of the right in all lands. Here you will get excellent sandwiches prepared by your servant Arabella, whose sole morality is to imagine caresses no man has yet felt and which the angels inspire.”
