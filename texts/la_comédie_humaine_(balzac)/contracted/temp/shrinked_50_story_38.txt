*Two Poets* is the first part of a trilogy centered on Lucien, his sister Eve, and their friend David in Angoulême. The narrative unfolds during a time of traditional printing, focusing on Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, a former journeyman pressman who runs a small printing business. He obtained a printing license during the French Revolution, allowing him to operate without formal education.

After inheriting the printing house, Séchard adeptly navigated the political landscape by collaborating with M. le Comte de Maucombe to print decrees, transforming from a struggling pressman into a shrewd businessman motivated by greed and a desire to secure a future for his son, David.

David excelled academically and was sent to Paris to learn the printing trade, but his father favored practical experience over formal education. Upon returning in 1819, David was poised to take over the family business, which held a monopoly on official printing. However, competition emerged when the Cointet Brothers sought a second printing license, threatening Séchard's dominance.

As Séchard aged, his greed intensified, complicating his relationship with David, whom he viewed as both heir and rival. This tension highlighted themes of ambition, familial duty, and the harsh realities of business. *Two Poets* explores the conflict between personal ambition and familial loyalty amid societal change.

In the sitting room of the late Mme. Séchard, an inebriated Séchard brought David to review a valuation of the printing business. David was shocked to find the equipment overvalued at sixteen hundred francs, deeming the old presses unworthy. Séchard insisted on their reliability, dismissing David's preference for modern machinery.

In the workshop, Séchard demonstrated the presses' functionality, but David recognized their outdatedness and felt trapped in an unfair negotiation. Séchard, sensing David's hesitation, grew anxious and emphasized the business's profit potential. Ultimately, David agreed to terms that included a partnership allowing Séchard to retain a room and share profits until the purchase was complete.

After moving in, David faced financial strain as Séchard refused to contribute to operational costs, leading to painful realizations about his father's greed. Meanwhile, Séchard became increasingly anxious about the business's success, frequently visiting David, who struggled against competition from the Cointet Brothers.

David's friendship with Lucien Chardon, a talented but impoverished young man, flourished as they shared aspirations. David offered Lucien a position in the printing house, hoping to help him succeed. Their friendship deepened through discussions of innovative ideas, though they often neglected practicalities.

The printing house's dilapidated state mirrored Séchard's avarice, reflecting the struggles faced by David and Lucien. In May 1821, seeking refuge from their oppressive environment, David and Lucien immersed themselves in literature and art, sharing dreams of fame.

Their artistic pursuits shifted when a stranger arrived with a manuscript for printing, claiming to be an author sent by Mme. de Bargeton, the woman Lucien admired. Despite his feelings for her, Lucien recognized social barriers and vowed not to pursue her unless David was welcomed too.

Lucien wandered through Angoulême, contemplating societal divides and his aspirations to rise above his humble origins. His ambitions, influenced by Mme. de Bargeton, placed him at the center of a social conflict that could elevate or destroy him. The narrative sets the stage for Lucien's struggle against societal constraints, driven by his passion for poetry and love for Mme. de Bargeton, while David embodies loyalty and sacrifice.

M. de Bargeton, from a once-prominent Bordeaux family, faced decline due to his grandfather's extravagance, leaving only seignorial rights to their Angoumois lands, yielding ten thousand francs annually. He married Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, a noblewoman from an obscure lineage. Her miserly father depended on his wife’s estate, while Anaïs received a broad education from Abbé Niollant, who nurtured her love for music and literature.

Anaïs developed a strong sense of self-worth and disdain for others, leading to a haughty demeanor ill-suited for provincial life. After the Abbé's death in 1802, she rebelled against her father's miserly nature and marriage constraints. M. de Nègrepelisse sought a husband for her, ultimately choosing M. de Bargeton, who was older and perceived as lacking intellect.

Naïs married M. de Bargeton, hoping for freedom and social connections in Paris, but their age difference and limited income constrained their lives in Angoulême. Her vibrant qualities became absurdities, and her artistic inclinations turned into exaggerated expressions of emotion. Isolated and longing for excitement, her disappointing marriage left her yearning for love. The rise of the Empire rekindled her hopes, leading her to fall for a young officer, whose death in battle deepened her sorrow.

Mme. de Bargeton’s life became a cycle of unfulfilled dreams and artistic aspirations, leading her to seek solace in literature and the arts. Despite her gifts, she remained trapped in a narrow existence, her dignity morphing into affectation. The arrival of M. du Châtelet, a charming but superficial man, disrupted her life. He recognized her potential as a social asset, while the local bourgeoisie disapproved of him.

Châtelet cultivated a relationship with Mme. de Bargeton, seeing her as a means to elevate his status. His presence highlighted tensions within Angoulême society, where intellectual poverty coexisted with decayed gentility. Mme. de Bargeton, enamored with the Bourbons' resurgence, longed for Paris's artistic brilliance. Châtelet introduced her to Lucien, a young poet whose potential he exaggerated.

As Lucien grew closer to Mme. de Bargeton, he initially felt grateful for the nobles' attention but soon recognized their patronizing attitudes. Their bond deepened, leading to moments of intimacy that thrilled and terrified him. Mme. de Bargeton struggled with societal scrutiny and defended Lucien against backlash, arguing that genius transcends social class.

She hosted gatherings to showcase his talent, drawing mixed reactions from the local aristocracy. Feeling threatened, Châtelet sought to undermine Lucien while trying to win over Mme. de Bargeton. Lucien's talent complicated the dynamics among the three, as he became increasingly aware of the sacrifices required for success. Mme. de Bargeton encouraged him to adopt a noble name, igniting his desire for recognition.

Ultimately, Lucien's journey was marked by the tension between his aspirations and the realities of social hierarchy, with his relationship with Mme. de Bargeton serving as both inspiration and a reminder of the obstacles he faced in his pursuit of greatness.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
As Lucien descended from Angoulême, he was troubled by thoughts of Mme. de Bargeton’s potential anger and whether she would accept his friend David. He recognized the social divide between himself and the aristocracy, realizing that David's lower status could tarnish Mme. de Bargeton’s reputation. Lucien feared ridicule for David, who lacked the refinement he possessed, leading him to reconsider the introduction.

Reflecting on his simple life and love for Mme. de Bargeton, Lucien resolved to pursue success independently, inspired by Napoleon’s merit-based rise. Returning to L’Houmeau, he felt discontent with his humble surroundings, especially as he prepared to meet Mme. de Bargeton. His father’s reminders of their modest status deepened Lucien's sense of inadequacy.

When Lucien received a letter from Mme. de Bargeton expressing her willingness to help David, he felt ecstatic, interpreting it as a victory despite her prejudices against lower classes. His sister Eve teased him about his happiness, sensing a romantic connection.

Meanwhile, David, in love with Eve, struggled with feelings of unworthiness due to his humble background. Despite his talent, he felt trapped by societal expectations but remained determined to support Lucien’s ambitions.

As Lucien prepared for a public reading, David advised him to choose a different poem, fearing his biblical work might not resonate. Lucien, eager to impress, felt the weight of social dynamics, particularly with M. de Bargeton present. He felt torn between ambition and loyalty to his family and friends, reminded of their love even as he sought his identity.

At the reading, Lucien faced an indifferent audience. His initial poem received mild applause, but many were uninterested, undermining his confidence. Despite this, he pressed on, driven by a desire to connect. His passionate reading was met with indifference, leaving him feeling isolated.

Infatuated with Mme. de Bargeton, Lucien wrote an ode he believed rivaled Chénier's, but it was dismissed by the aristocratic crowd as typical youthful verse. Critics found his work obscure, and despite polite responses, the atmosphere remained hostile. Stung by rejection, Lucien vowed to prove himself worthy of high society.

In a parallel narrative, Eve prepared for a meeting with David, who expressed his feelings during a walk. He voiced concerns about Lucien’s ambitions and the potential pitfalls of his relationship with Mme. de Bargeton. However, Eve remained optimistic, believing they could support Lucien until he found success.

David proposed marriage to Eve, emphasizing that their union would enable him to help Lucien. He envisioned a future where they could provide for him, but Eve hesitated due to their differing social standings. David reassured her, promising to work hard for their future.

As the evening progressed, Lucien returned, burdened by humiliation. He shared his frustrations with Eve and David, expressing his determination to win over Mme. de Bargeton. Unbeknownst to him, their engagement posed a challenge to his aspirations, as he feared Eve's marriage would hinder his social ambitions. David's father opposed the engagement, insisting Eve's lack of wealth made her unsuitable. Despite this, David remained committed to Eve, determined to build a life together.

In Angoulême, Lucien's status soared after the Bishop's epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s response, making him a local hero. He eagerly shared news of Eve's engagement to David with Louise, who supported his ambitions. Lucien, buoyed by Louise's affection, began to see himself as significant and asked her to be his Beatrice.

As Lucien frequented the Hôtel de Bargeton, he enjoyed the luxuries of his newfound status, but rumors about his closeness with Louise began to circulate. The disparity in societal expectations became apparent, with some acting freely while others faced scrutiny.

Meanwhile, David prepared for his marriage to Eve, improving their home with his savings. Lucien, now living as M. de Rubempré, felt optimistic about his writing career, believing his family's sacrifices entitled him to success. However, rival Châtelet began spying on Lucien and Louise, hoping to catch them in a compromising situation.

Châtelet's schemes led to a scandal when he and Stanislas claimed to have seen Lucien and Louise in a compromising position. The rumor spread rapidly, with townspeople eagerly dissecting the situation. Mme. de Bargeton urged her husband to confront Stanislas for his slanderous remarks. M. de Bargeton, motivated by his wife's honor, prepared to challenge Stanislas to a duel.

The next morning, news of the duel spread quickly. Mme. Chardon informed Lucien that Bargeton had shot Stanislas, who would suffer lasting injury. Public sentiment turned against Lucien and Mme. de Bargeton. A note from Louise urged Lucien to avoid public appearances, hinting at emotional turmoil following the duel.

Preparations for Eve’s wedding continued, but Lucien felt torn between family obligations and his desire to be with Louise. Captivated by her plans to move to Paris, where they could thrive socially and artistically, she encouraged him to seize opportunities in the capital. Initially hesitant due to his sister’s marriage, Lucien was inspired by Louise’s passionate arguments about their potential future.

Despite his excitement, Lucien grappled with guilt over abandoning his family. Louise’s disdain for his hesitation fueled his internal conflict, leading to a heated exchange that left him distressed. As he prepared to leave, he sought financial support from his family, realizing their sacrifices. His family was heartbroken but recognized the necessity of his ambitions, with David offering financial help despite the strain it would cause.

Ultimately, Lucien’s departure was bittersweet. He felt the weight of his family’s sacrifices and the uncertainty of his future in Paris, filled with both hope and trepidation as he set out with David, aware of the challenges ahead but also the potential for greatness.
