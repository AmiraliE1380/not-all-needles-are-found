*Two Poets* is the first part of a trilogy centered on Lucien, his sister Eve, and their friend David in Angoulême. The narrative unfolds during the era of traditional printing, focusing on Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, a former journeyman pressman who owns a small printing business acquired during the French Revolution.

Séchard evolves from a struggling pressman to a shrewd businessman, driven by greed and the desire to secure a future for his son, David. After excelling in Paris, David returns in 1819 to take over the family business, which faces competition from the Cointet Brothers.

As Séchard ages, his greed strains his relationship with David, whom he sees as both heir and rival. A pivotal scene occurs when Séchard, intoxicated, overvalues the business, leading to a tense negotiation. David ultimately agrees to terms that allow Séchard to retain a share, but soon faces financial strain as Séchard refuses to assist.

David's friendship with Lucien deepens as they share dreams of fame, though their artistic pursuits distract them from practical challenges. In May 1821, they seek refuge from their oppressive environment, with Lucien's feelings for Mme. de Bargeton complicating his ambitions, setting the stage for his struggle against societal constraints while David remains a loyal friend.

M. de Bargeton, from a once-prominent Bordeaux family, faced decline due to his grandfather's extravagance, retaining only seignorial rights to their Angoumois lands by 1789. He married Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, a noblewoman with a strong sense of self-worth and disdain for others, which made her haughty and unsuitable for provincial life. After the Abbé Niollant's death in 1802, she rebelled against her father's constraints and married the older M. de Bargeton, perceived as lacking intellect.

In Paris, Anaïs found her marriage limiting and longed for excitement. The rise of the Empire rekindled her hopes, but the death of a young officer deepened her sorrow. The arrival of M. du Châtelet, a charming yet superficial man, disrupted her life. He recognized her potential and introduced her to Lucien, a young poet. As their bond deepened, societal scrutiny intensified, leading to gossip. Mme. de Bargeton defended Lucien, hosting gatherings to showcase his talent, while Châtelet attempted to undermine him, highlighting the tension between aspirations and social hierarchy.

As Lucien descended from Angoulême, he was troubled by his feelings for Mme. de Bargeton and the implications of his friend David's presence on her reputation. Aware of the social divide, he feared that David's lack of refinement would lead to ridicule and reconsidered the invitation.

Determined to succeed on his own merits, inspired by Napoleon, Lucien returned to L’Houmeau feeling discontented with his humble life. His father's reminders of their modest status deepened his sense of inadequacy. A letter from Mme. de Bargeton, expressing her willingness to help David but revealing her biases, thrilled Lucien, who believed David would impress her. His sister Eve teased him about his happiness, sensing a romantic connection.

David, in love with Eve, felt unworthy due to his background but was committed to supporting Lucien’s ambitions. As Lucien prepared for a public reading, David advised him to choose a different poem, fearing his biblical work might not resonate. Eve and David shared a tender bond, both hesitant to express their feelings.

At the reading, Lucien was nervous, especially with M. de Bargeton present. He struggled to connect with him, and the arrival of M. du Châtelet heightened his discomfort. Despite his passionate delivery, Lucien faced indifference from the audience, leaving him stung by rejection and determined to prove himself.

In a parallel narrative, Eve prepared for a meeting with David, who expressed his feelings and concerns about Lucien’s ambitions. David proposed marriage, believing it would help Lucien, but Eve hesitated due to their differing social standings. David reassured her of his commitment to work hard for their future.

After Lucien's humiliating experience, he confided in Eve and David about his determination to win over Mme. de Bargeton. Unbeknownst to him, their engagement posed a challenge to his aspirations. David’s father opposed the engagement due to Eve’s lack of wealth, but David remained committed.

Lucien’s status rose after the Bishop’s epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s response, making him a local hero. He shared news of Eve’s engagement with Louise, who supported his ambitions. As Lucien frequented the Hôtel de Bargeton, rumors about his closeness with Louise began to circulate. Meanwhile, David prepared for his marriage, improving their home with his savings.

Tensions escalated when Châtelet began spying on Lucien and Louise, leading to a scandal and a duel challenge after Bargeton shot Stanislas. Public sentiment turned against Lucien and Mme. de Bargeton. A note from Louise urged Lucien to avoid public appearances, hinting at emotional turmoil.

As preparations for Eve’s wedding continued, Lucien felt torn between family obligations and his desire to be with Louise. Captivated by her plans to move to Paris, he grappled with guilt over abandoning his family. Ultimately, Lucien’s departure was bittersweet, filled with hope and trepidation as he set out with David, aware of the challenges ahead but also the potential for greatness.
