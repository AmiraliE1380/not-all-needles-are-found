At the story’s outset Angoulême still used old wooden presses tied to Parisian paper mills; “the press groans” is literal: leather ink-balls and hand-inking prevailed. This antique mechanism, beloved by Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, drives the tale.

Once a journeyman pressman, Séchard survived the Requisition of 1793, became sole worker after his master’s death, obtained a master-printer’s license from a Representative of the People despite being illiterate, and bought the business cheaply. He partnered with the noble Comte de Maucombe, who helped print decrees while avoiding official notice. By 1795 Séchard hired a compositor—an Abbé who later became a Bishop—and, though uneducated, learned to run the shop: tracking costs, buying paper when prices fell, becoming his own landlord and acquiring the old printing house. His avarice grew.

He had one son, David, schooled in grammar and Paris, who excelled and finished without burdening his father. The firm held a regional monopoly on official prints, threatened when the Cointet brothers sought a second license. Now in his seventies and given to drink since his wife’s death, Séchard presents a bloated, cunning figure. He greets David warmly yet presses business like both heir and rival. The long-established shop—workroom, vine-grown yard, inky kitchen runoff and cramped, unchanged living quarters—reflects his stinginess and relentless pursuit of profit.

The sitting-room, partly modernized by the late Mme. Séchard with powder‑blue wainscoting and Oriental paper, was sparsely furnished—lyre‑backed chairs, bare arched windows and unfinished plans—Mme. Séchard having died before completing it. Jérôme‑Nicolas Séchard, practical and profit‑minded, left it as it was.

He brought in his son David, pointed to a valuation of the printing plant and had him read the inventory. David scoffed at the old presses and tossed the paper; Séchard, affronted, led him downstairs, demonstrated the presses’ efficiency and argued their superiority to newer machinery. David doubted the high price for the type; Séchard insisted it was nearly new from M. Vaflard’s foundry. The inventory totaled thirty thousand francs, including license and goodwill. David, recalling his father had once bought the business for ten thousand francs, balked.

Séchard pressed the point: the license brought steady income from advertisements and work, and attached to the valuation was a partnership deed—Séchard would rent the premises to David for twelve hundred francs a year and reserve a room for himself. Cornered, David calculated the license and goodwill’s worth and, thinking he might manage the debt, accepted the deal.

Next day Séchard had the apprentice move David’s belongings to the attic, leaving him three empty rooms and no funds to pay wages. When David asked for help, Séchard refused, saying he had already paid his share; he dismissed any claim on Mme. Séchard’s fortune as long and costly to pursue. Resolving to work, David accepted his situation. Séchard then spoke of a “treasure”—Marion, a capable country girl who ran many tasks—and departed for his Marsac vineyard, fretting over the thirty thousand francs and visiting intermittently as the Cointet Brothers gained ground.

The Cointets, allied with the clergy, monopolized the market and smeared David as a Liberal because of his father. They offered to buy the printing house; though Séchard at first urged David to keep the paper and negotiated what he called a protective price of sixty thousand francs, David was ultimately forced to sell, marking the Séchard establishment’s decline. Séchard grew detached.

David’s fortunes worsened when Lucien Chardon, an old school friend ruined by his father’s death, came seeking work and became a printer’s reader. Their friendship deepened—Lucien dreamed of being a poet, David of studying natural science; Lucien recounted his father’s schemes for refining sugar and making paper, which intrigued David. But these ambitions distracted them from the shop’s needs; while they planned, the Cointets consolidated their advantage and Séchard & Son scraped by, the workshop and household falling into disrepair.

One day in May 1821 David and Lucien waited by the window until the men went to dinner. Uncomfortable in the office, David led Lucien outside beneath the vines, where sunlight made their contrasts plain. David was robust, swarthy, weathered—a man of humble origins with a melancholy reserve. Lucien was graceful and beautiful, with a refined profile and striking blue eyes; delicate in appearance but ambitious, shrewd, and willing to use dubious means to rise.

Both were low in social standing yet nourished loftier ideals, which made them bitter when reality intervened. Lucien, impetuous and adventurous, usually led; David, stronger but more introspective and cautious, admired him and often yielded, content to play the ordinary man. For three years their friendship thrived on literature and art: they read voraciously, worked hard, and dreamed of fame as escape. When David brought a volume of André de Chénier, the verses comforted them with kinship and hope. Their reverie was broken by Cérizet, who brought a stranger to print a monograph. The visitor mentioned Mme. de Bargeton—whom Lucien admired—and Lucien, flushed, confessed his love. David warned of social barriers; Lucien hoped love could bridge them and vowed not to visit Mme. de Bargeton without David’s welcome, a pledge showing loyalty and pride.

Walking through Angoulême, Lucien reflected on the gulf between his origin and Mme. de Bargeton’s world. The city, perched on a crag, was split between an aristocratic upper town and a commercial lower town; the nobility clung to old values and scorned merchants, while the bourgeoisie resented that disdain. Despite modernization, the hierarchy remained rigid, breeding tension as the middle class pressed for recognition. In that climate Lucien’s ambition was daunting: a druggist’s son was an outsider, yet his talent, charm, and Mme. de Bargeton’s artistic tastes gave him hope. David’s admiration was mixed with concern; Lucien’s determination—and his pledge to include David—encapsulated their bond as love and ambition unfolded against entrenched divisions and shifting fortunes.

M. de Bargeton descended from a Bordeaux alderman ennobled under Louis XIII, but after his grandfather “the Waster” squandered the fortune, by 1789 the family held only entailed lands and a house in Angoulême. In 1805 he married Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse (Naïs), whose once-prominent family lived modestly: her father eked out a living on his wife’s estate, farming and distilling brandy.

Taught languages and music by the Abbé Niollant, Naïs developed independence and a haughty air; her father’s thrift and provincial seclusion reinforced her refusal to submit. Seeking a husband who wouldn’t rival her intellect, her father chose the well-bred but limited Bargeton. At thirty-six to his fifty-eight, she accepted, planning social ambitions and a move to Paris. Instead, a meager income and petty provincial life stifled her, producing theatrical affectations. Longing for love, she adored an Empire hero who died, leaving her hardened—until the charming, ambitious M. du Châtelet arrived, unsettling the town and awakening her hopes and scandal.

Political opinion was loud and exaggerated; the Quotidienne showed tepid loyalty, Louis XVIII was mocked, and society’s women were awkwardly dressed and ill-spoken. In the provinces, lingering nobility and Bourbon loyalty lent provincials misplaced pride.

Châtelet sought solace in society because of Mme. de Bargeton. Flattering many women and embodying what their husbands lacked, he endured insolence, courted the clergy, indulged the queen of Angoulême’s tastes, and circulated new literature. Mme. de Bargeton—Naïs—was enchanted by the Bourbon revival yet longed for Parisian genius; Châtelet spoke of a promising young poet in Angoulême.

That poet, Lucien, unaware of the stir, was praised by his headmaster and expected a warm reception. His sister Éve secretly supported him—saving for new clothes and coaching him in aristocratic manners—leaving him proud but anxious.

At the Hôtel de Bargeton the house was less grand than imagined, but Naïs embodied the poetry he admired. She admired his beauty and diffidence; mutual attraction promised intimacy. Châtelet, sensing a rival, asserted influence and claimed Naïs, but Lucien continued to visit, at first flattered, later resentful.

Naïs cultivated him as patroness and confidante: she encouraged his literary ambitions, introduced him to music and books, and revealed vulnerabilities. A portrait of a dead lover stirred jealousy; Lucien declared his feelings and their liaison advanced. When it became public the town buzzed. Châtelet tried to undermine him; Naïs defended the idea that genius can rise from humble origins, hosted salons, and urged Lucien to adopt the noble name Rubempré and seek courtly patronage. Exhilarated, Lucien’s egalitarian ideals gave way to social ambition; in a passionate letter he begged Naïs’s help for a friend, revealing his conflict between love, artistic sincerity, and pursuit of success.

As Lucien descended from Angoulême he worried whether the great lady was angry and if she would receive David. He sensed the vast gulf between a queen and her favorite and knew David could not easily cross that social terrain. He did not yet understand that receiving David might ruin Mme. de Bargeton: for the powerful many sins are pardoned, but breaching social law by associating with lower classes was unforgivable.

Lucien’s aristocratic instinct warned him. Noble bearing did not guarantee noble feeling; David lacked Lucien’s graces and would be ridiculed. Regretting his impulsive letter, Lucien foresaw the harsh sacrifices success demanded and preferred to win by his own efforts. Inspired by Napoleon’s rise, he resolved to pursue ambition without patronage, ashamed of his father’s shop as an obstacle.

At home M. Postel reminisced about family achievements, stoking Lucien’s resentment. In the shop Lucien found Mme. de Bargeton’s reply: she would help David but revealed prejudice toward lower status. Lucien read it as triumph, convinced David would shine in her circle. Eve noticed his elation and feared his priorities were shifting.

David, feeling unworthy, declined the invitation, affirming his commitment to being a printer and the social distance between them. His humility moved Lucien, but ambition began to eclipse gratitude. Before Lucien’s first public reading, David and Eve shared a quiet, suggestive moment. At the reading Lucien met the awkward M. de Bargeton, whose limited intellect underscored the social dynamics Lucien must navigate—setting the stage for future conflicts.

There was talk of nominating the mute M. de Bargeton for deputy, but Lucien had only briefly met him and could not fathom him. Reclining and silent, Bargeton observed everything with a cool reserve that awed Lucien; imaginative, Lucien treated him like a sphinx and behaved deferentially. “I am the first comer,” Lucien said; Bargeton’s dry reply made him self-conscious. Lucien tried to converse—guessing Mme. de Bargeton was dressing, fretting over the room and his attire—but Bargeton’s curt answers unnerved him.

The Baron du Châtelet arrived next, inspecting Lucien and dismissing his poetry as vaudeville. Mme. de Bargeton soon appeared, elaborately charming; Châtelet’s compliments pleased her while she scarcely acknowledged Lucien.

Guests followed in a parade of types: the Bishop and his Vicar-General; Mme. de Chandour and her eccentric husband, Amélie posing like a rival queen; the solemn M. de Saintot (Astolphe) with austere Élisa; M. de Bartas seeking praise for singing; the artist Alexandre de Brébian and his wife; fashion-obsessed Lolotte and Fifine; the Comte de Senonches with Zéphirine, striking despite ill health, and Francis, whose solicitous attentions to Zéphirine provoked comment. The wealthy Marquis de Pimentel and the Rastignacs drew envy beneath Mme. de Bargeton’s flattery. The Prefect and the General came last, along with a nervous country gentleman bearing a treatise on silkworms. Mme. and Mlle. du Brossard hovered, pressing social ties; M. de Séverac spoke of silkworm nurseries.

Throughout, Lucien felt scrutinized and diminished—often called M. Chardon rather than M. de Rubempré, a stinging slight. When he read, announcing newly discovered Chénier, his first poem Le Malade met mild applause; L’Aveugle fell flat amid impatience. Encouraged by Louise’s glance, he finished with Chénier’s Iambes, which finally secured attention though many missed the meaning. During the break Zéphirine sent Francis to inquire about the poems, and whispers about Lucien’s origins—Amélie’s remarks about his noble name and printing past—fueled gossip. Absorbed in the poet’s beauty, Lucien remained largely unaware of the mounting tension.

Lucien sought to immortalize his love for Mme. de Bargeton with an ode. He presented it before her; she fretted. The audience was unimpressed: the Baron called it ornate but empty; the women feigned admiration while whispering sarcasm. Francis spread a rumor that his mother wrote the verse; the Bishop’s praise only intensified aristocratic scorn. Overwhelmed, Lucien misread faces and felt alienated.

Later Mme. de Bargeton withdrew, consoling him and romanticizing suffering as the path to greatness, urging him to turn pain into work. Meanwhile Eve and her lover David debated his aims: David feared Lucien couldn’t survive high society; Eve hoped to support him. David proposed; Eve accepted despite inequality, and David vowed to support him despite his father's objections. Lucien left resolved to win Mme. de Bargeton’s love and rise above society, yet torn between ambition and reality.

In Angoulême, a Bishop’s epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s rejoinder made Lucien a local celebrity; in Beaulieu he was flattered and envied. He told Louise of his sister Eve’s impending marriage; she replied with passionate affection, praised his reading, and promised a poetic refuge. Buoyed by family and friends, Lucien’s pride grew. He teasingly asked Louise to be his Beatrice; she vowed future love and urged him toward greatness. He confided about David and Eve’s attachment; Louise said their bond transcended family ties. When M. de Bargeton arrived and invited him to dinner and a reading, Lucien—now styling himself M. de Rubempré—enjoyed intimacies, patronage, and hopes of a literary breakthrough. Eve and David postponed their wedding to support him, though David feared Lucien’s coming change.

Their closeness drew Châtelet’s attention; in the gossip-driven town, innocent acts were misread. Provincial constraints thwarted Lucien’s declarations, Louise considered fleeing, and Châtelet sowed doubt. An outburst seen by Stanislas sparked rumor; gossip spread and Châtelet seized it. Mme. de Bargeton defended her honor and pressed her husband to act; the insecure M. de Bargeton nonetheless confronted Stanislas modestly and won respect, setting the stage for a confrontation that would further entangle lives and expose the town’s fraught mix of love, honor, and social expectation.

Châtelet, suspecting Naïs’s involvement in a late-night visit to the bedbound M. de Bargeton, asked if Bargeton wished to confront Stanislas. Bargeton, relieved at having a mediator, was brought to Amélie’s room for what could be a ruinous showdown. Châtelet warned Stanislas to be decorous; instead, Bargeton demanded a retraction of remarks about Mme. de Bargeton and M. de Rubempré. Cornered, Stanislas accepted a duel and chose pistols.

The duel altered the household atmosphere: guests divided blame, Châtelet remained composed, Stanislas was shaken, and Amélie fell ill. At breakfast Mme. Chardon brought grim news—Stanislas had been shot in the neck and nearly killed after accusing Bargeton of compromising Mme. de Bargeton. Lucien defended her innocence.

Lucien then received a note from Louise urging him to avoid her home because of the duel’s fallout and to spend the day with her. David, preparing for his own marriage, suggested Lucien curb his visits to Mme. de Bargeton. Visiting her later, Lucien found her and her husband with M. de Nègrepelisse watching him. Louise confided she planned to take Bargeton to Paris to obtain a post and contain the scandal, and she pressured Lucien to join her there. He hesitated because of his sister Eve’s impending wedding; after a heated exchange and her fainting, he promised discretion and to be ready to leave the next night.

At home Eve and their mother fretted over wedding costs. David agreed to endorse a loan and assist; Lucien’s belongings were secretly sent to Paris. Châtelet sent someone to follow Mme. de Bargeton. At dawn Lucien left with David, pretending business, as Mme. de Bargeton’s carriage neared—Lucien torn between hope and dread.
