In October 1800 a foreigner, Bartolomeo di Piombo, stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and young daughter. He was over sixty and visibly distraught; his wife tried to keep calm for the child. Passersby were drawn by their despair but deterred by his fierce gaze.

After a pause Bartolomeo made a desperate choice: he handed a dagger to his wife and declared he would confront the Bonapartes. A soldier stopped him and refused entry without a written request. Bartolomeo insisted until Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, arrived, recognized him, and took him inside to see his brother Napoleon.

Napoleon sent his aides away to speak with Bartolomeo alone. The Corsican sought asylum and confessed he had killed the Portas — a family that had burned his vineyard and murdered his son. He admitted he had avenged his son by killing all seven Portas, a revelation that shocked Napoleon. Bartolomeo, bitter at the brothers’ apparent indifference, reminded them he had once protected their family.

Now a national leader, Napoleon said he was bound by law and could not approve private vengeance, yet he acknowledged the Corsican tradition of vendetta. He offered Bartolomeo a chance to live in Paris, promised financial help, and warned that the law would not tolerate private justice in the city.

Bartolomeo accepted, pledged loyalty to the Bonapartes, and expressed gratitude and hope about life in Paris. Napoleon, seeing a potential ally, hinted he would need trustworthy friends. Afterward Lucien questioned Bartolomeo about finances and learned the family had arrived in Paris with nothing. Lucien gave them money and promised assistance, noting that Bartolomeo’s Corsican property would not suffice to support them in Paris.

Fifteen years later, subsequent events would unfold that required the context of Bartolomeo’s earlier struggles and decisions.

Servin, a distinguished artist, was the first to open a studio for young girls who wished to paint. About forty pupils, strictly moral and devoted to art, attended. He was married to the dowerless daughter of a general. At first mothers accompanied their daughters; soon they sent them alone, reassured by Servin’s principles and dedication.

He taught only young ladies from wealthy families to avoid disputes about social makeup and refused girls who wanted to become professional artists, which would require more thorough instruction. His careful approach and skill earned him a stellar reputation among the aristocracy; when a girl showed talent, mothers invariably recommended Servin.

He became synonymous with feminine art, as Herbault was with bonnets and Leroy with gowns. A young woman trained by him was presumed able to critique museum pictures, paint striking portraits, or handle genre scenes. Though connected with Paris’s elite, Servin kept an independent, patriotic attitude and a witty, half-ironic tone.

The studio was arranged with care. The attic above his apartment was sealed, accessible only by a small spiral stair. The large, light-filled room had big glass panes veiled by green linen shades. Dark-gray walls displayed caricatures and plaster models, producing a curious mixture of disorder and harmony.

On a bright July day the studio hummed with girls at their easels, each showing different expressions and styles. Among them sat a modest, simply dressed girl, Ginevra Piombo, apart and seemingly anxious. The group split into two cliques: daughters of wealthy merchants and aristocrats led by Amelie Thirion, vain and pretentious.

Despite their differences the girls were absorbed in their work, laughing and chatting. Ginevra, the most talented and beautiful, drew the aristocrats’ jealousy. Amelie, intent on ostracizing her, pushed her easel away from the others, provoking whispers about the insult.

The political climate after the Bourbons’ second return sharpened tensions. Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, met disdain from girls of royalist families. Amelie’s act was both a personal affront and a reflection of the era’s political divisions.

When Ginevra arrived she found silence instead of warmth. Her beauty and grace commanded attention, but her politics made her a target. Unmoved, she focused on her work, though unaware of all the surrounding tension.

During the session a faint sound behind a partition drew Ginevra’s attention. She discovered Servin was sheltering a political fugitive, an officer of the Imperial Guard. The revelation filled her with excitement and concern for the man’s safety.

Resolving to protect him, she preferred to be near the fugitive rather than risk his exposure. Her indifference to Amelie’s slight only increased the latter’s jealousy and baffled the other girls.

Madame Servin’s arrival interrupted the brewing conflict; she announced her husband could not attend and seemed aware of the hidden man. Ginevra, sensing danger, began humming a Corsican song to help mask the fugitive’s breathing.

As the session continued, Ginevra sketched the officer’s head with surprising fervor. Servin praised her talent; the other girls, including Amelie, were drawn to her work. Her determination to keep the man’s presence secret became evident as she deftly diverted attention from her drawing.

Amelie, suspicious, tried to uncover the mystery but was thwarted by Ginevra’s quick thinking. Their rivalry intensified; Ginevra’s talent and composure only stoked Amelie’s envy.

Servin, attuned to the tension, engaged each pupil but kept a close eye on Ginevra. Their shared awareness of the situation deepened their bond, and Ginevra felt empowered to assist him in protecting the fugitive.

By day’s end Ginevra’s resolve to keep the secret and help the officer escape had only strengthened. Political turmoil loomed outside the studio, but within its walls the girls navigated rivalries and friendships, united by their art.

When the painter and Ginevra thought they were alone, Servin knocked and admitted a tall officer in an Imperial uniform, his arm in a sling and his face pale with pain. He recoiled at seeing Ginevra, but Servin soothed him, introducing her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo. Recognizing her patriotism, the soldier relaxed.

"You are wounded," Ginevra observed.

"It's nothing, mademoiselle," he replied. "The wound is healing."

Cries from the street suddenly announced a death sentence: "Condemned to death!" The officer went pale at the name "Labedoyere," and collapsed onto a stool. In despair he lamented that while Labedoyere faced death for their cause, he himself was hiding.

Ginevra blocked his path as he tried to leave. "Can you restore the Emperor?" she asked. "Do you expect to raise that giant who could not maintain himself?"

"What can I do?" he replied, feeling utterly alone after losing his protector. He said he was willing to die, believing an honest man's life was worth more than that of two traitors, and wondered how to sell his life to the executioner.

Alarmed, Ginevra offered her savings, explaining her father was wealthy and would not blame her for helping a soldier. She urged him to wait until the storm passed, suggesting he could escape and serve elsewhere.

Her words, filled with maternal grace and hope, moved the young man deeply. He admired her beauty and felt a rush of emotion. "You are an angel of goodness," he said, but his thoughts quickly returned to Labedoyere.

In silence they shared a moment of understanding; their brief acquaintance deepened into a bond. Servin asked if he could save Labedoyere; the officer said he could only avenge him. Ginevra felt a surge at the thought of vengeance, recognizing the officer's devotion to Napoleon.

As the sun set, illuminating the officer's face, Ginevra felt a sudden, almost magical connection, recalling childhood memories. She was captivated by his presence and left smiling at him, igniting a spark of hope.

The next day Ginevra returned to the studio, where the officer—now named Luigi—shared stories of the retreat from Moscow and the disaster at Waterloo. Ginevra listened, her admiration growing. They spoke Corsican, which deepened their connection.

Days passed and Ginevra's classmates noticed her absence from social gatherings, attributing it to her relationship with Luigi. Mademoiselle Thirion, worried about the rumors, withdrew from the class, and attendance declined. Ginevra remained oblivious, lost in thoughts of Luigi.

One day Laure, a fellow student, confided that her mother insisted she leave the studio because of the rumors about Ginevra and Luigi. Ginevra was shocked but reassured Laure they would meet again. Laure was sad to go, and Ginevra felt a pang of loss.

As Ginevra and Luigi's bond deepened, they shared unspoken feelings, recognizing each other's worth. One evening Luigi signaled to Ginevra but, seeing Laure, retreated. Ginevra, unaware of the tension, spoke with Laure and learned her classmates had left because of the scandal.

Ginevra confronted Servin, revealing the young ladies had spread falsehoods about her and Luigi. Servin dismissed the rumors, focused on his upcoming exhibition. Luigi entered, jokingly claiming responsibility for Servin's lost students, and the painter playfully suggested they marry.

Their silence said more than words—the first acknowledgment of their love. Servin encouraged their union, celebrating the joy of love. Ginevra proposed to help Luigi secure a pardon from the authorities, confident in her father's influence.

Meanwhile Ginevra's parents grew anxious about her late return home. Bartolomeo, her father, paced, concerned. Though used to her late hours, he and his wife felt unease. Bartolomeo, a man of integrity, had always prioritized his family's well-being over social expectations.

As they waited they reflected on their close-knit family, built on love and mutual respect. Ginevra's upbringing had instilled loyalty and passion, traits she shared with her father. Their bond was strengthened by shared political beliefs and commitment to the Emperor.

When Ginevra finally arrived, her father rushed to embrace her, relieved to see her safe. The family shared a warm moment; their love was evident. Ginevra's heart swelled with affection for her parents, knowing they were her greatest supporters.

In the studio Ginevra and Luigi's relationship blossomed amid the challenges they faced. Their love, forged in adversity, became a source of strength, uniting them against the world. As they navigated life's complexities, they remained steadfast in their commitment, embodying the resilience of their shared ideals.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!” Ginevra exclaimed, but was gently set down and told she had feigned it to escape. The baroness recovered; Piombo, rubbing his hands, showed courtly joy. The two old parents seemed renewed.

“Now, to dinner!” Piombo called, offering his hand; Ginevra smiled.

“Your mother said you’ve been at the studio more. Painting takes you from us,” he remarked.

“Oh, father!” she protested.

“Ginevra prepares a surprise,” her mother suggested.

“A picture of your own! Will you show us?” he asked eagerly.

“Yes, I am busy at the studio,” she replied slowly.

“Are you well? You look pale!” her mother cried.

“No! It shall never be said Ginevra Piombo acted a lie,” she declared.

Her parents exchanged astonished looks. “I love a young man,” she confessed, lowering her eyelids.

“Is he a prince?” Piombo asked ironically; his wife trembled.

“No, father, he is a young man without fortune,” she said gently.

“Is he handsome?” he pressed.

“He is unfortunate,” she answered.

“What is he?” Piombo inquired.

“Labedoyere’s comrade; he was proscribed and hidden by Servin,” she explained.

“Servin is a good fellow,” Piombo said. “But you do wrong to love any man but your father.”

“It does not depend on me to love or not,” Ginevra replied softly.

“I thought my Ginevra would remain faithful to me until I die,” Piombo lamented. “I did not expect our love to have a rival.”

“Did I ever reproach you for devotion to Napoleon?” Ginevra countered. “You left me months on missions. I endured your absence. Life has necessities.”

“Ginevra!” he cried.

“You don’t love me for myself; your reproaches show your egotism,” she said.

“Dare you blame your father’s love?” he shouted.

“Father, I don’t blame you,” she answered gently. “You have grounds for your egotism, as I have for my love. I have never left your side for fifteen years; it has been a pleasure to charm your life. Is it ingratitude to desire a husband who will protect me?”

“What! You reckon benefits with your father?” Piombo asked dangerously.

A dreadful silence followed. Bartolomeo finally cried, “Oh! stay with us! I cannot bear you love another man. Ginevra, you will not have long to await your liberty.”

“But, father, I need not leave you; we will both love you. You will come to know the man to whom you bequeath me,” she said.

“Oh! Ginevra, why did you not marry when Napoleon offered?” Piombo lamented.

“They loved me to order,” she replied. “I did not wish to leave you alone.”

“You don’t wish to leave me alone, yet you marry! I know you, my daughter; you would cease to love us,” he said.

Elisa, the baroness, sat motionless, stunned. “We have no longer a daughter; she wishes to marry,” Piombo declared, raising his hands to heaven, bowed by sorrow. Ginevra expected a violent outburst but met gentleness.

“Father,” she said tenderly, “you shall never be abandoned by your Ginevra. Love me for my own sake. If you knew how he loves me! He would never make me unhappy!”

“Comparisons already!” Piombo shouted. “I cannot endure marriage. If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me; if he does not love you, I would kill him.”

Ginevra alone could withstand his gaze, her own eyes aflame. “Oh! to love you! What man is worthy of such a life?” Piombo continued. “Who is worthy to be your husband?”

“He,” Ginevra replied, “he of whom I am not worthy.”

“He?” Piombo repeated. “Who is he?”

“He whom I love,” she said.

“How can he know you enough to love you?” he asked.

“Father,” she said impatiently, “whether he loves me or not, if I love him—”

“You love him?” Piombo interrupted.

Ginevra nodded.

“You love him more than you love us?” he pressed.

“The two feelings cannot be compared,” she replied.

“Is one stronger?” he asked.

“I think it is,” she admitted.

“You shall not marry him,” Piombo declared, shaking the windowpanes with his voice.

“I shall marry him,” Ginevra replied calmly.

“Oh, God!” cried her mother. “How will this quarrel end? Santa Virgina! Place yourself between them!”

Piombo, now seated, wore an icy sternness. He looked at Ginevra and said gently, “No! You will not marry him. Say nothing more tonight—let me think otherwise. Do you wish to see your father on his knees, his white hairs prostrate before you? I supplicate you—”

“Ginevra Piombo does not break her word,” she replied. “I am your daughter.”

“She is right,” the baroness said. “We are sent into the world to marry.”

“Do you encourage her in disobedience?” Piombo asked, turning to his wife, who sat petrified.

“Refusing an unjust order is not disobedience,” Ginevra asserted.

“No order can be unjust from your father’s lips,” Piombo replied. “Why do you judge me? My repugnance may be counsel from on high, to protect you from great evil.”

“The only evil would be if he did not love me.”

“Always he!” Piombo exclaimed.

“Yes, always,” she affirmed. “He is my life, my good, my thought. Even if I obeyed you, he would remain in my soul. To forbid me to marry him is to make me hate you.”

“You love us not!” Piombo shouted.

“Oh!” Ginevra shook her head.

“Then forget him; be faithful to us. After we are gone—you understand?”

“Father, do you wish me to long for your death?” she asked.

“I shall outlive you. Children who do not honor their parents die early,” he warned.

“All the more reason I should marry and be happy,” she replied.

This coolness and argument upset Piombo; blood rushed to his head. Ginevra shuddered and sprang onto his knee, embracing him. “Oh, yes, yes, let me die first! I could never survive you, my father!”

“Oh! my Ginevra, my own Ginevra!” Piombo answered, his anger melting under her affection.

“It was time you ceased,” the baroness said, trembling.

“Poor mother!” Ginevra said.

“Ah! Ginevretta! mia bella Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed, playing with her as if she were a child. They shared moments of joy, but Ginevra’s attempts to introduce Luigi were met with playful refusals from her father.

The next day she was more affectionate to her father, expressing gratitude as if to thank him for apparent consent. That evening she sang and played, saying they needed a man's voice for the nocturne.

A week later, her mother signaled her to come closer. “I have persuaded your father to receive him,” Elisa whispered.

“Oh! mother, how happy you have made me!” Ginevra replied.

That day Ginevra returned home on Luigi’s arm. The officer had emerged from hiding: Ginevra’s appeals to the Duc de Feltre had restored Luigi’s name on the officer’s list — a crucial step. Warned of her father’s potential disapproval, Luigi was anxious to meet him. Ginevra sensed his fear, which deepened her love.

“How pale you are!” she remarked as they reached her home.

“Oh! Ginevra, if it concerned my life only—” he began.

Bartolomeo had been informed but remained seated, stern.

“Father,” Ginevra said, “I bring you someone you will be pleased to see—a soldier who fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean.”

Piombo rose, glancing at Luigi. “Monsieur is not decorated,” he remarked sarcastically.

“I no longer wear the Legion of Honor,” Luigi replied timidly.

Ginevra, embarrassed by her father’s rudeness, pulled out a chair for Luigi. The officer’s response seemed to satisfy Piombo. Madame Piombo, noting her husband’s softening, tried to engage.

“Monsieur’s resemblance to someone we knew in Corsica, Nina Porta, is surprising,” she noted.

“Nothing could be more natural,” Luigi answered. “Nina was my sister.”

“Are you Luigi Porta?” Piombo asked.

“Yes,” Luigi confirmed.

Bartolomeo staggered, needing support from his wife. The two old parents left the room in silence, abandoning their daughter in horror.

Luigi, bewildered, looked at Ginevra, who had turned pale, staring at the door through which her parents had exited. The solemnity filled her with fear. “What misery in a word!” she exclaimed.

“In the name of our love, what have I said?” Luigi asked.

“My father never spoke of our tragic history, and I was too young to know,” Ginevra explained.

“Are we in vendetta?” Luigi asked, trembling.

“Yes. My mother said the Portas killed my brother and burned our house. My father then massacred their family. How did you survive? You were tied to the bed before they set fire to the house.”

“I do not know,” Luigi replied. “I was taken to Genoa at six and raised by an old man named Colonna. I was told I had enemies and advised to take Luigi as my surname to evade them.”

“Go, go, Luigi!” Ginevra urged. “Stay here; you are safe in my father’s house, but once you leave, danger awaits! My father has two Corsicans in his service, and they will hunt you.”

“Ginevra,” he said, “does this vendetta exist between us?”

She smiled sadly and nodded. “Oh, Luigi, our love must be pure and sincere to give me strength for the path ahead. But it promises happiness for our lives, does it not?”

Luigi smiled and squeezed her hand. Ginevra realized true love transcends superficial expressions. Their destiny was sealed; she foresaw a difficult struggle but felt no inclination to abandon Luigi. Resolute, she pulled him from her father’s house and ensured he reached his modest lodging.

Returning home, Ginevra felt calm resolve. She greeted her parents, who were preparing dinner, with gentle eyes, noting her mother’s tear-stained face. They rose from the table in silence, unable to speak.

In the gloomy salon, Piombo attempted to speak but faltered; strength failed him. He rang for a servant. “Pietro, light the fire; I am cold,” he ordered.

Ginevra trembled, watching her father’s internal struggle. Both suffered in silence, nursing pride and pain. The love that once brought them joy now felt burdensome. Ginevra’s gaze at her father was reproachful, while Piombo’s eyes reflected deep-seated sorrow.

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother, desperate to mend the rift, gathered them in Bartolomeo’s study. Ginevra sensed her mother’s intentions and smiled sadly.

At that moment two notaries arrived with witnesses. Bartolomeo eyed them warily, sensing confrontation. The older notary introduced himself and began to discuss Ginevra’s marriage to Luigi Porta.

“Monsieur le baron, we have come to fulfill the law regarding your daughter’s marriage,” he stated.

“Are there laws in France that destroy paternal authority?” Piombo demanded.

“Monsieur—” the notary began.

“Which tear a daughter from her father?” Piombo pressed.

“Monsieur—”

“Which deprive an old man of his last consolation?” he continued.

“Monsieur, your daughter only belongs to you if—”

“And kill him?” Piombo shouted.

The notary’s calm only fueled Piombo’s rage. In a sudden movement he grabbed a dagger from the wall and rushed toward Ginevra. Witnesses intervened, but Piombo pushed them aside, fury evident.

When Ginevra saw him approach she knelt before him. “No, no! I cannot!” he cried, throwing the dagger away. “Have mercy! Give me Luigi! I ask your consent upon my knees,” she pleaded, voice trembling.

Bartolomeo pushed her away. “Go. The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here,” he said, pressing his heart. “Leave my house, unhappy girl,” he added, ushering her out.

“Luigi!” Ginevra cried, entering her lover’s humble abode. “My Luigi, we have no fortune but our love.”

“Then I am richer than kings!” he exclaimed.

“My father and mother have cast me off,” she said sadly.

“I will love you in their place,” he promised.

“Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!” she declared, voice filled with a mix of joy and dread.

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father's house, she took refuge with Madame Servin until her marriage to Luigi. Madame Servin received her coldly, annoyed by the trouble Ginevra had caused her husband. Proud yet shocked by this selfishness, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her and painted a bright future that lifted her spirits.

One morning Ginevra received trunks of essentials from her mother, accompanied by a letter urging her to reconsider the marriage. The letter, full of love and sorrow, deeply moved Ginevra. Just as she felt compelled to return home, Luigi arrived and her resolve to abandon him faded; she chose to bury her grief for the sake of their love.

On the wedding day Ginevra was alone, and Luigi brought ordinary men as witnesses, a stark contrast to typical wedding gaiety. Ginevra's beauty silenced them, creating a somber atmosphere. As they walked to the mayor's office, they passed other joyful wedding parties, highlighting how different their situation was.

Inside, their love was overshadowed by the festive mood around them. The mayor's clerk noted the absence of Ginevra's parents, and when the mayor inquired about protest, the clerk confirmed it. The bureaucratic formality felt oppressive, reminding Ginevra of her family's disapproval; the ceremony was reduced to a legal act devoid of celebration.

After signing the registers, Ginevra and Luigi left the office with a mix of relief and sadness. Luigi expressed his gratitude, and Ginevra felt a quiet happiness despite the circumstances. They hurried to the church, where an old priest married them in a modest chapel amid the grandeur of other weddings. The priest's remarks about their parents' absence stung, but a blessing from a sergeant comforted Ginevra.

Once married, they moved into a modest apartment where Luigi embraced Ginevra, calling it their true wedding. They explored their new home, which was filled with artistic supplies for Ginevra. She was delighted by the thoughtful arrangements, and they reveled in their shared space, feeling they had found paradise despite its limitations.

In the early days of marriage they indulged in each other’s presence, but reality soon set in and they realized they needed to work. Ginevra began copying paintings and gained clients, while Luigi, after initial struggle, used his handwriting skills to offer copying services. His efforts paid off; he soon had enough work to hire assistants, providing a stable income for both.

This period marked the happiest time of their lives, filled with love and labor. They enjoyed evenings together; Ginevra always smiled and never complained. Each worked for the other, finding joy in shared effort. Yet Ginevra occasionally felt pangs of remorse for the happiness she had sacrificed by leaving her family, haunted by thoughts of her parents' sorrow.

To commemorate their anniversary, Ginevra painted a self-portrait for Luigi, capturing not just her likeness but the essence of their love. The masterpiece symbolized their wedded bliss, and another year passed in contentment.

At the beginning of 1819, picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce originals instead of copies as competition intensified. Madame Porta regretted not pursuing genre painting earlier, thinking it might have improved her standing. She tried portraiture but faced stiff rivalry from artists in greater need. Despite modest savings, Luigi and Ginevra grew anxious about their future.

That winter Luigi labored relentlessly in a crowded, less lucrative writing market. He could no longer afford help and had to work longer for the same pay. Ginevra painted several commendable works, but dealers shunned lesser-known artists, and even low prices brought little sale.

Their household deteriorated; love and happiness were overshadowed by poverty. They concealed worries from one another: Ginevra lavished affection while Luigi hid his fears. They clung to each other, hoping love would sustain them.

One night Ginevra woke to find Luigi gone and discovered him working late to avoid burdening her. Moved, she resolved to help by coloring lithographs, working into the night while Luigi toiled in his garret. At dawn both pretended to sleep, each shielding the other from distress.

On another night, Luigi, exhausted and feverish, opened his window and saw Ginevra's lamp across the courtyard, realizing she too worked. Confronting her, he learned she could not idle while he suffered. They shared a tender moment, watched dawn together and felt tentative hope.

Their struggle deepened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. The joy of parenthood briefly masked their burdens, but soon they had to sell possessions and jewels. Poverty stripped them of comfort and pride, pushing them toward desperation.

Months later Ginevra, frail and worn, struggled to care for a sickly child. Luigi, feeling helpless, sought help and found none. He sold Ginevra's paintings and their remaining furniture for a pittance, scarcely easing their plight. Ginevra displayed remarkable strength, managing the household and tending their son while often starving so Luigi could eat.

One evening she offered Luigi her last piece of bread and urged him to eat. Realizing her sacrifice, he was engulfed by despair and left to seek aid. In desperation he decided to sell himself as a military substitute, hoping the advance would save Ginevra and their child.

After obtaining the money, he hurried home, finding Ginevra asleep with their child. When he woke her, she smiled, then noticed Bartolomeo was cold and lifeless. Luigi, horrified, sought help, but it was too late. In delirium Ginevra professed her love for Luigi and lamented their lost child. She died in his arms, leaving him devastated.

As Luigi mourned, Ginevra's parents sat in their home, remembering a daughter they had not seen in years. Memories returned as they noticed an empty chair that once belonged to her. Elisa Piombo, Ginevra's mother, spoke of her daughter, hoping to rekindle Bartolomeo's affection for Ginevra. Just as Bartolomeo seemed to soften, a man entered with the tragic news of Ginevra and Luigi's fate. The old couple were shattered, recognizing their daughter was gone, and Bartolomeo's heart was broken by the loss of his beloved child. The cycle of suffering and retribution had claimed another victim, leaving only memories and grief.
