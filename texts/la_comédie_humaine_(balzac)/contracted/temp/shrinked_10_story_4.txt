**VENDETTA**

**By Honoré De Balzac**

**Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley**

---

**DEDICATION**

To Puttinati, Milanese Sculptor.

---

**CHAPTER I. PROLOGUE**

In October 1800, a foreigner stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and young daughter. The man, Bartolomeo di Piombo, appeared troubled, his features hardened by age and sorrow. His wife, though sad, tried to maintain a calm demeanor for their daughter, who was weary yet graceful. Their despair drew the attention of passersby, but the father's fierce gaze quickly deterred any sympathy.

After a moment of contemplation, Bartolomeo made a desperate decision. He handed a dagger to his wife, declaring he would confront the Bonapartes. He approached the palace but was stopped by a soldier. Despite the soldier's refusal to let him pass, Bartolomeo insisted on speaking to Bonaparte. Eventually, Lucien Bonaparte recognized him and took him inside to meet Napoleon.

Inside, Bartolomeo revealed he had killed the Portas, a family that had wronged him. He explained how they had burned his vineyard and killed his son, leading him to seek vengeance. Bonaparte, intrigued but cautious, refused to offer protection, citing the law. However, he offered Bartolomeo a chance to live in Paris, away from vendetta, promising to help him financially.

Fifteen years later, Bartolomeo's family had settled in Paris, but the past loomed over them.

---

**CHAPTER II. THE STUDIO**

Servin, a respected artist, opened a studio for young women from wealthy families. He aimed to teach them painting without compromising his reputation. His studio became a prestigious place for young ladies to learn art, and he maintained strict control over the environment.

One day, two groups of students formed in the studio, one aristocratic and the other bourgeois. Ginevra Piombo, a talented student, was ostracized by the aristocrats due to her family's political affiliations. Despite her talent, she faced jealousy and exclusion, particularly from Amelie Thirion, who led the aristocratic group.

As tensions rose, Ginevra remained unaware of the animosity directed at her. When she entered the studio, silence fell, and the aristocrats plotted to humiliate her further. Ginevra's talent and kindness had earned her respect, but her political beliefs made her a target.

The atmosphere in the studio reflected the broader societal divisions of the time, as the return of the Bourbons reignited old rivalries. Ginevra's love for Napoleon and her family's loyalty to him put her at odds with her peers.

---

**CHAPTER III. LABEDOYERE’S FRIEND**

One day, Ginevra discovered a wounded officer hiding in Servin's studio. He was Luigi Porta, a comrade of Labedoyere, who had fought for Napoleon. Ginevra felt an instant connection with him, and they began to share their stories and dreams.

As they grew closer, Ginevra learned of the vendetta between their families. Despite the danger, their love blossomed, and they found solace in each other's company. Ginevra's compassion for Luigi deepened her feelings, and she resolved to help him escape the consequences of his past.

Their love was tested by the harsh realities of their situation, but they remained committed to one another. Ginevra's determination to protect Luigi from her father's wrath only strengthened their bond.

---

**CHAPTER IV. LOVE**

Ginevra and Luigi married in secret, defying the vendetta that separated their families. They faced challenges as they built a life together, but their love provided them with strength. Ginevra's artistic talent flourished, and Luigi found work, allowing them to support each other.

Despite their happiness, the shadow of poverty loomed over them. Ginevra's health began to decline as they struggled to make ends meet. Their love remained a source of joy, but the pressures of life weighed heavily on them.

As Ginevra became pregnant, their hopes for the future grew. However, the harsh realities of their circumstances threatened to overshadow their happiness. They fought against despair, determined to create a life filled with love for their child.

---

**CHAPTER V. MARRIAGE**

The couple welcomed their son, Bartolomeo, into the world, but their joy was short-lived. Financial struggles intensified, and Ginevra's health deteriorated. They worked tirelessly to provide for their family, but the burden of poverty took its toll.

Ginevra's artistic endeavors were met with indifference, and Luigi's efforts to find work proved futile. They faced the harsh reality of their situation, but their love remained unwavering. Ginevra's strength inspired Luigi, and they continued to support one another through their trials.

As they navigated the challenges of parenthood and financial strain, their bond deepened. They found solace in their love, even as the world around them grew darker.

---

**CHAPTER VI. RETRIBUTION**

Tragedy struck when Ginevra fell ill, and their son succumbed to hunger. Luigi's desperation led him to make sacrifices, but it was too late. Ginevra's health declined rapidly, and she passed away, leaving Luigi heartbroken.

In the aftermath of her death, Luigi confronted the reality of their family's vendetta. He returned to Bartolomeo, who had been consumed by grief and regret. The cycle of vengeance had claimed another victim, and the weight of their past hung heavily over them.

As Luigi mourned Ginevra, he realized the futility of their families' hatred. The love they had shared was a testament to the power of compassion, but it had come at a terrible cost. In the end, the vendetta had destroyed not only their lives but also the possibility of reconciliation.
