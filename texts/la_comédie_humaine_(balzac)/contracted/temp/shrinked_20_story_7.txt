The incident in this sketch took place in late November 1809, at the height of Napoleon’s empire. The echoes of the Battle of Wagram still reverberated in Austria as peace talks proceeded. Kings and princes orbited Napoleon, who reveled in power and the splendor of Parisian entertainments surrounding his marriage to an Austrian archduchess. The French aristocracy’s opulence—diamonds and gold on women and uniforms—contrasted sharply with the Republic’s earlier austerity. The atmosphere was intoxicating: military leaders indulged in wealth amassed through the sacrifices of countless soldiers.

Many women adopted a carefree attitude reminiscent of the Louis XV era. Whether influenced by the fallen monarchy or the Imperial family’s example, both sexes embraced a hedonistic lifestyle that seemed to foreshadow doom. The allure of military men became a public frenzy aligned with the Emperor’s interests, as frequent calls to arms made peace treaties feel temporary. Women’s affections shifted rapidly; many moved from mistress to widow in weeks.

Soldiers’ appeal sprang from several sources: the prospect of early widowhood, the lure of historical legacy, and the romantic ideal of courage. Women were attracted to the notion that their passions might remain secret, buried on distant battlefields. Military men, seen as embodiments of honor and adventure, fueled a culture that prized bravery. The period also indulged a mania for glittering possessions—diamonds, fireworks, and jewel-adorned uniforms—following extravagant examples like Murat.

Comte de Gondreville, formerly Citizen Malin, had risen socially and postponed a grand entertainment to outdo rival courtiers and flatter Napoleon. His drawing-rooms filled with ambassadors, dignitaries, and a few princes, all awaiting the Emperor. Yet a private dispute between Napoleon and Josephine, hinting at possible divorce, kept him away; the assembled guests remained unaware of the tension.

The most beautiful women in Paris flocked to Gondreville’s event, embodying luxury and elegance. The financial elite, proud of their fortunes, sought to rival military splendor, using grand balls to introduce daughters to Napoleon’s circle and trade wealth for favor. Women confident in their beauty attended to test their allure, masking intentions behind smiles. Amid the gaiety, many trusted friends less than rivals, since the pursuit of wealth and power underpinned social interactions.

These remarks are necessary to explain the incidents surrounding the little imbroglio that is the subject of this study, reflecting the tone prevalent in Paris drawing-rooms at the time.

“Look toward the pedestal supporting that candelabrum—do you see the young lady with her hair a la Chinoise? She’s in the corner to the left, with bluebells in her chestnut curls. She’s so pale you’d think she’s ill, very small. Now she’s turning her head; her almond-shaped blue eyes seem made for tears. She’s leaning forward to see Madame de Vaudremont below the crowd.”

“I see her now. You only had to mention her lovely complexion; I would have recognized her. She has the whitest skin here, but she’s either a prude or a coquette, as her bodice barely hints at her beauty. What shoulders! What lily-whiteness!”

“Who is she?” asked the first speaker.

“I don’t know.”

“An aristocrat! Do you want to keep them all to yourself, Montcornet?”

“You of all people to tease me!” Montcornet smiled. “Do you think you can insult a poor general like me because you rival Soulanges? Or because I’m new to this Promised Land? Monsieur le Maitre des Requetes, let us glean from the field you can only hold precariously. We have a right to live! If you knew the German women, you might help me with the Parisian you love best.”

“Well, General, since you’ve noticed that lady, have you seen her dance?”

“Where have you been? There’s a triple rank of the most undaunted coquettes between her and the swarm of dancing men. It’s a miracle anyone could spot her in that corner, buried in gloom despite the candles. How could you think she’s the wife of some sous-préfet from Lippe or Dyle?”

“Oh, he will be!” exclaimed the Master of Appeals.

“I doubt it,” laughed the Colonel of Cuirassiers. “She seems as raw in intrigue as you are in diplomacy. I bet you don’t know how she got there.”

The lawyer looked at the Colonel with a mix of contempt and curiosity.

“Well,” Montcornet continued, “she probably arrived punctually at nine, the first of the company, and embarrassed the Comtesse de Gondreville, who can’t think straight. Repulsed by the mistress and driven into the corner by jealous ladies, she became a victim of their coalition against her. Each of those sweet faces would conspire to keep the men from taking her out. That’s how tender-looking women would form a coalition against the stranger without saying more than, ‘Do you know that little woman in blue?’ If you care to run the gauntlet of flattering glances and inviting questions, try to get through the rampart defending that Queen of Dyle, Lippe, or Charente. You’ll see how even the dullest woman will invent a wile to keep the plaintive stranger in the shadows. Doesn’t she look like an elegy?”

“Do you think so, Montcornet? Then she must be married?”

“Why not a widow?”

“She would be less passive,” laughed the lawyer.

“She might be the widow of a gambler,” replied the handsome Colonel.

“Of course; since the peace, there are many widows of that sort!” said Martial. “But my dear Montcornet, we are simpletons. That face is too innocent; there’s too much youth and freshness for her to be married. What splendid flesh-tints! Nothing has sunk in the modeling of her nose. Her lips and chin are as fresh as a white rosebud, though her expression is veiled by clouds of sadness. Who could make that young creature weep?”

“Women cry for little,” said the Colonel.

“I don’t know,” replied Martial; “but she’s not crying because she’s left without a partner; her grief is not new. It’s clear she beautified herself for this evening with intention. I’d wager she’s in love already.”

“Bah! She’s perhaps the daughter of some German princeling; no one talks to her,” said Montcornet.

“Dear! How unhappy a poor child may be! Can there be anything more graceful and refined than our little stranger? Yet not one of those furies around her, who think they can feel, will say a word to her. If she would only speak, we’d see if she has fine teeth.”

“Bless me, you boil over like milk at the least increase of temperature!” cried the Colonel, nettled at finding a rival in his friend.

“What!” exclaimed the lawyer, ignoring the Colonel. “Can nobody here tell us the name of this exotic flower?”

“Some lady companion!” said Montcornet.

“A companion! Wearing sapphires fit for a queen and a dress of Malines lace? Tell that to the marines, General. You wouldn’t shine in diplomacy if you jump from German princess to lady companion.”

Montcornet stopped a fat little man with iron-gray hair and clever eyes, mingling unceremoniously with various groups.

“Gondreville, my friend,” Montcornet said, “who is that charming little woman sitting under that huge candelabrum?”

“The candelabrum? Ravrio’s work; Isabey made the design.”

“Oh, I recognized your lavishness and taste; but the lady?”

“Ah! I don’t know. Some friend of my wife’s, no doubt.”

“Or your mistress, you old rascal.”

“No, on my honor. The Comtesse de Gondreville is the only one capable of inviting people no one knows.”

Despite the jibe, the little man smiled. Montcornet returned to the lawyer, who had rejoined a neighboring group, intent on asking, but in vain, for information about the fair unknown. He grasped Martial’s arm and said in his ear:

“Mind what you’re about. Madame de Vaudremont has been watching you with ominous attentiveness; she can guess by the movement of your lips what you whisper to me. Our eyes have already told her too much; she’s thinking even more than we are about the little blue lady.”

“That’s too old a trick in warfare, my dear Montcornet! However, what do I care? Like the Emperor, when I make a conquest, I keep it.”

“Martial, your fatuity cries out for a lesson. You, a civilian, and so lucky as to be the husband-designate of Madame de Vaudremont, a widow of two-and-twenty with four thousand napoleons a year—who slips such a diamond on your finger,” he added, taking the lawyer’s left hand, which the young man complacently allowed; “and you affect the Lovelace, as if you were a colonel obliged to maintain the military reputation! Fie! Just think of all you may lose.”

“At any rate, I shall not lose my liberty,” replied Martial with a forced laugh.

He cast a passionate glance at Madame de Vaudremont, who responded only with a smile of unease, having seen the Colonel examining the lawyer’s ring.

“Listen to me, Martial. If you flutter around my young stranger, I shall set to work to win Madame de Vaudremont.”

“You have my full permission, my dear Cuirassier, but you will not gain this much,” and the young Maitre des Requetes put his polished thumbnail under an upper tooth with a mocking click.

“Remember that I am unmarried,” said the Colonel; “that my sword is my whole fortune; and that such a challenge is setting Tantalus down to a banquet he cannot devour.”

“Prrr.”

This defiant sound was Martial’s only reply as he looked him over and turned away.

The fashion of the time required men to wear white kerseymere breeches and silk stockings at a ball. This costume showcased Montcornet’s fine shape. At thirty-five, his stature and handsome Cuirassier uniform made him conspicuous. His black moustache highlighted a soldierly countenance—broad forehead, aquiline nose, bright red lips. Montcornet’s manner, marked by authority from command, might please a woman sensible enough not to want a slave for a husband. He smiled as he looked at Martial, one of his favorite college friends, whose smaller figure required Montcornet to look down slightly as he replied to raillery with a friendly glance.

Baron Martial de la Roche-Hugon was a young Provencal favored by Napoleon; his fate might lead to a splendid embassy. He had won the Emperor with Italian suppleness, a talent for intrigue, and a polished knowledge of manners—good substitutes for the higher qualities of a sterling man. Though young and eager, his face had acquired the rigid brilliancy of tinned iron, a diplomat’s mask that hides emotion. Martial was one of those men who could reckon on the future amid their enjoyment; he judged the world and hid ambition under the guise of a lady-killer, cloaking talent under mediocrity as he observed rapid advancement around him.

The two friends parted with a cordial handshake. The introductory tune, signaling the ladies to form squares for a new quadrille, cleared the space they had filled while talking in Gondreville’s drawing-room. Their whispered conversation had taken place during the usual interval between dances. The chandeliers and flambeaux illuminated their faces, failing to conceal faint expressions from the keen-sighted countess or the artless stranger. Such espionage of thought is perhaps one pleasure idle people find in society.

To fully appreciate the interest of this conversation, it’s necessary to relate an incident that served as an invisible bond, drawing the actors together.

At about eleven, just as the dancers were returning to their seats, the company observed the entrance of the handsomest woman in Paris, the queen of fashion—the only person missing from the brilliant assembly. She made it a rule never to appear until the party reached its springtime of excitement. An hour later, when pleasure flagged and fatigue showed, everything was spoiled. Madame de Vaudremont never remained to be seen with drooping flowers or disorderly dress; she maintained her reputation by always leaving a ballroom as she entered—brilliant. Women whispered that she planned and wore as many different dresses as parties she attended in an evening.

On this occasion, Madame de Vaudremont was not destined to leave when she wished. Pausing on the threshold, she shot swift, observant glances at the women present, hastily scrutinizing their dresses to assure herself that her own eclipsed them all.

She entered with one of the bravest colonels of the Guards’ Artillery and the Emperor’s favorite, the Comte de Soulanges. This association had an air of mystery. Upon hearing the names announced, a few women rose, and men hurried in from side-rooms, pressing to the doorway. One jester remarked that “women have as much curiosity about seeing a man faithful to his passion as men do in studying a woman difficult to enthrall.”

The Comte de Soulanges, about thirty-two, nervy and capable of fine qualities, had a slender build and pale complexion. His black eyes revealed great vivacity, but he was taciturn in company—his future oratory under the Restoration not yet apparent.

The Comtesse de Vaudremont, tall, somewhat plump, with dazzlingly white skin and a small well-carried head, inspired love by her gracious manner. For a few minutes the pair were the center of observation, but did not long allow curiosity to exercise itself. The Colonel and the Countess seemed to understand the awkwardness of their position. Martial, watching Madame de Vaudremont with the jealous anxiety of a first flame, sensed his success might be precarious. The Countess thanked Soulanges coldly and sat down by Madame de Gondreville, relaxing Martial’s rigid face. Seeing Soulanges still near the sofa where Vaudremont sat, apparently unaware they played a ridiculous part, the Provencal smoothed his chestnut curls and watched the faces of the Countess and Soulanges while talking with his neighbors. He took the hand of Colonel Montcornet but listened absentmindedly.

Soulanges gazed calmly at the women, four ranks deep around the immense ballroom, admiring diamonds and shining hair, which almost outshone the waxlights and chandeliers. His rival’s indifference unsettled the lawyer. Unable to control his impatience, Martial approached Madame de Vaudremont with a bow. Upon seeing the Provencal, Soulanges cast a covert glance and impertinently turned away his head. A solemn silence reigned; everyone anticipated an outbreak that men of breeding avoid. Suddenly, the Count’s pale face flushed scarlet; he fixed his gaze on the floor to hide his agitation. Spotting the unknown lady humbly seated by the candelabrum’s pedestal, he moved away with a melancholy air and took refuge in one of the cardrooms. Martial and the company thought Soulanges had publicly surrendered his position out of fear of ridicule. The lawyer proudly raised his head and looked at the strange lady; then, as he sat near Madame de Vaudremont, he missed her words, spoken behind her fan:

“Martial, please don’t wear that ring you took from me. I have my reasons and will explain them when we leave. You must give me your arm to go to the Princess de Wagram’s.”

“Why did you come in with the Colonel?” asked the Baron.

“I met him in the hall,” she replied. “But leave me now; everybody is looking at us.”

Martial returned to the Colonel. It was then the little blue lady became the object of curiosity that agitated Montcornet, Soulanges, Martial, and Madame de Vaudremont.

After their challenge, the Baron quickly led Madame de Vaudremont into the most brilliant quadrille. Dancing’s intoxication and the tumult of a ball made Martial think he might yield to the charm of the fair stranger. Though he hid his glances toward the lady in blue from the Countess’s watchful eyes, he was soon caught; excusing his absent-mindedness once, he could not justify the awkward silence that followed her most insinuating question:

“Do you like me very much this evening?”

The more dreamy he grew, the more the Countess pressed and teased him.

While Martial danced, Montcornet moved from group to group seeking information about the unknown lady. After exhausting even the indifferent, he waited until the Comtesse de Gondreville seemed free and seized a small gap by the candelabrum. The dance had left several chairs vacant; the Colonel threaded through this palisade of shawls and wraps, made himself agreeable to dowagers, and finally reached the empty space next to the stranger. Braving dripping wax, he stood there, to Martial’s annoyance.

Too tactful to speak suddenly to the little blue lady, he began with a plain woman on her left.

“This is a splendid ball, madame! What luxury! What life! Every woman here is pretty! You’re not dancing—because you don’t care for it, no doubt.”

This vapid conversation aimed to draw out his right-hand neighbor; she, silent and absent-minded, paid no attention. The officer had prepared several phrases leading to, “And you, madame?”—a question from which he hoped great things. But he was surprised to see tears in the strange lady’s eyes as she gazed at Madame de Vaudremont.

“You are married, no doubt, madame?” he asked hesitantly.

“Yes, monsieur,” she answered.

“And your husband is here, of course?”

“Yes, monsieur.”

“And why, madame, do you remain in this spot? Is it to attract attention?”

The mournful lady smiled sadly.

“Allow me the honor, madame, of being your partner in the next quadrille, and I will ensure not to bring you back here. When so many are ready to ascend the throne, and Royalty is the mania of the day, I cannot imagine you will refuse the title of Queen of the Ball that your beauty may claim.”

“I do not intend to dance, monsieur.”

Her curt tone discouraged the Colonel; he withdrew. Martial, guessing what had happened and the refusal met, smiled, stroking his chin and making the diamond on his finger sparkle.

“What are you laughing at?” asked the Comtesse de Vaudremont.

“At the poor Colonel’s failure,” said Martial.

“I begged you to take your ring off,” said the Countess, interrupting.

“I did not hear you.” 

“If you can hear nothing this evening, at least you see everything, Monsieur le Baron,” said Madame de Vaudremont, vexed.

“That young man is displaying a very fine diamond,” the stranger remarked to the Colonel.

“Splendid,” he replied. “The man is the Baron Martial de la Roche-Hugon, one of my most intimate friends.”

“I thank you for telling me his name,” she said; “he seems an agreeable man.”

“Yes, but he is rather fickle.”

“She seems to be on the best terms with the Comtesse de Vaudremont?” the lady inquired.

“On the very best.”

The unknown turned pale.

“Hallo!” thought the soldier, “she is in love with that lucky devil Martial.”

“I fancied Madame de Vaudremont had long been devoted to M. de Soulanges,” the lady recovered from her grief.

“For a week past, the Countess has been faithless,” replied the Colonel. “But you must have seen poor Soulanges when he came in; he’s still trying to disbelieve in his disaster.”

“Yes, I saw him,” she said, then added, “Thank you very much, monsieur,” dismissing him.

At that moment the quadrille ended. Montcornet withdrew, telling himself, “She is married.”

“Well, valiant Cuirassier,” exclaimed the Baron, drawing the Colonel aside into a window-bay to breathe garden air, “how are you getting on?”

“She is a married woman, my dear fellow.”

“What does that matter?”

“Oh, deuce take it! I’m a decent sort of man,” replied the Colonel. “I have no intention of courting a woman I cannot marry. Besides, Martial, she expressly told me she did not intend to dance.”

“Colonel, I will bet a hundred napoleons to your gray horse that she will dance with me this evening.”

“Done!” said the Colonel, shaking Martial’s hand. “Meanwhile, I’m going to look for Soulanges; he might know the lady, as she seems interested in him.”

“You’ve lost, my good fellow,” cried Martial, laughing. “My eyes have met hers, and I know what they mean. My dear friend, you owe me no grudge for dancing with her after she has refused you?”

“No, no. Those who laugh last, laugh longest. But I’m an honest gambler and a generous enemy, Martial, and I warn you, she is fond of diamonds.”

With these words the friends parted. Montcornet made his way to the cardroom, where he saw the Comte de Soulanges at a bouillotte table. Though no close friendship linked the two soldiers beyond comradeship from war, the Colonel was alarmed to see the Coloneér, known for prudence, playing a game that could lead to ruin. Heaps of gold and notes lay on the cards, showing the frenzy of play. A circle of silent men stood around the players. Occasionally a word—pass, play, I stop, a thousand Louis—was spoken, but the five motionless men seemed to converse only with their eyes. As Montcornet, alarmed by Soulanges’ pallor, approached him, the Count was winning. Field-Marshal the Duc d’Isemberg, Keller, and a famous banker rose from the table—cleaned out of considerable sums. Soulanges looked gloomier than ever as he swept up gold and notes; he didn’t even count it; his lips curled with bitter scorn, defying fortune rather than grateful for her favors.

“Courage,” said the Colonel. “Courage, Soulanges!” He thought to help by pulling him from the gaming table. “Come with me. I have good news, but on one condition.”

“What is that?” asked Soulanges.

“That you will answer a question.”

The Comte de Soulanges rose abruptly, gathering his winnings into his handkerchief, his face so fierce that no one objected to his departure; the players seemed relieved to see him leave the light of the table.

“Those soldiers are always as thick as thieves at a fair!” remarked a diplomat who took Soulanges’ place. One tired face returned the comment with, “When we say military men, we do not mean civil, Monsieur le Ministre.”

“My dear fellow,” Montcornet said, leading Soulanges aside, “the Emperor praised you this morning, and your promotion to field-marshal is certain.”

“The Master does not love the Artillery.”

“No, but he adores the nobility, and you are an aristocrat. He said that men who married in Paris during the campaign are not in disgrace. Well?”

Soulanges looked bewildered.

“And now I hope you will tell me if you know a charming woman sitting under a huge candelabrum—”

At that the Count’s face brightened; he seized the Colonel’s hand. “My dear General,” he said, voice changing, “if anyone else had asked me that, I would have cracked his skull with this gold. Leave me, I beg you. I feel more like blowing out my brains tonight than— I hate everything I see. This gaiety, this music, these stupid faces, all laughing, are killing me!”

“My poor friend!” Montcornet replied gently, squeezing his hand. “You are too vehement. What would you say if I told you that Martial is thinking so little of Madame de Vaudremont that he is quite smitten with that little lady?”

“If he speaks to her,” Soulanges shouted, trembling with rage, “I will thrash him flat, even if he’s in the Emperor’s lap!”

He sank into an easy chair, and Montcornet slowly left, seeing Soulanges too furious for pleasantries.

Returning to the ballroom, Montcornet first noticed Madame de Vaudremont, agitated. He sat beside her.

“I wager something has vexed you?” he said.

“A mere trifle, General. I want to leave for a ball at the Grand Duchess of Berg’s, but I must first stop at the Princesse de Wagram’s. Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon is amusing himself by flirting with the dowagers.”

“That’s not the whole secret of your disturbance. I bet a hundred louis you will stay here the whole evening.”

“Impertinent man!”

“Then I’ve hit the truth?”

“Well, tell me, what am I thinking?” she asked, tapping his fingers with her fan. “I might reward you if you guess right.”

“I won’t accept the challenge; I have too much advantage.”

“You are presumptuous.”

“You are afraid of seeing Martial at the feet—”

“Of whom?” she exclaimed, feigning surprise.

“Of that candelabrum,” he replied, glancing at the fair stranger and scrutinizing the Countess.

“You have guessed it,” she said, hiding her face behind her fan. “Old Madame de Lansac, as malicious as an old monkey, just told me that Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon is flirting with that stranger, who sits here like a skeleton at a feast. I would rather see a death’s head than that face, so cruelly beautiful and pale. She is my evil genius. Madame de Lansac, who only comes to a ball to watch everything while pretending to sleep, has made me miserable. Martial shall pay dearly for this trick. Urge him, since he is your friend, not to make me unhappy.”

“I just left a man who promises to blow his brains out if he speaks to that lady. And he is a man, madame, to keep his word. But I know Martial; such threats encourage him. Besides, we have wagered—” The Colonel lowered his voice.

“Can it be true?” asked the Countess.

“On my word of honor.”

“Thank you, my dear Colonel,” Madame de Vaudremont replied, her glance inviting.

“Will you do me the honor of dancing with me?”

“Yes; but the next quadrille. During this one, I want to see what will come of this little intrigue and learn who the little blue lady is; she looks intelligent.”

Understanding Madame de Vaudremont wished to be alone, the Colonel retired, pleased to have made a good start.

At most entertainments, creatures like Madame de Lansac gather like old sailors watching younger mariners struggle with the tempest. The dowager, interested in the unfolding drama, could easily read the Countess’s agitation. The lady might fan herself, smile at the young men who bowed, and use every art to hide emotion, but the Duchess, a clear-sighted, mischievous survivor of a former age, read the heart. The faintest frown, quiver, or curl of lip was like a book to her. From her large armchair she admired the young coquette’s brave disguise. Madame de Vaudremont felt as much sorrow as she feigned cheerfulness; she had believed Martial a man of talent who could adorn her life with power, but she now realized her mistake. Her sudden passion for Martial, though recent, caused acute pain, like the amputation of a limb.

The old Duchess, watching for an opening to speak to the Countess, dismissed her Ambassador; a lover’s quarrel outshone all other interests. Madame de Lansac shot a sardonic glance at the younger woman, making the Countess fear her fate was in the dowager’s hands. There are looks between women like torches at a climax. No one who did not know the Duchess could appreciate the terror her expression inspired.

Madame de Lansac was tall; people said she must have been a handsome woman. She powdered her cheeks, wore many diamonds, and dressed with taste. Her sharp nose promised epigrams, and her ironical smile suggested a Voltairean wit. The politeness of her manners softened her mischievous mind, making her hard to accuse of spite, yet her eyes often lit with triumphant malice. A smile said, “I promised you as much!” and it sent a flush of hope to the pale young woman under the chandelier. The alliance between Madame de Lansac and the stranger did not escape the Countess’s eye; she sensed a mystery and was determined to uncover it.

At this moment the Baron de la Roche-Hugon, having questioned all the dowagers without success about the blue lady’s name, turned in despair to the Comtesse de Gondreville, who replied unsatisfactorily, “A lady whom the ‘ancient’ Duchesse de Lansac introduced to me.”

By chance, the lawyer caught the intelligent glance the Duchess sent to the stranger. Despite their bad terms, he decided to speak. The Duchess, seeing the jaunty Baron approaching, smiled with sardonic irony and looked at Madame de Vaudremont, making Montcornet laugh.

“If the old witch pretends to be friendly,” thought the Baron, “she is certainly going to play me some spiteful trick.” “Madame,” he said, “you have, I am told, undertaken the charge of a very precious treasure.”

“Do you take me for a dragon?” said the old lady. “But of whom are you speaking?” she added sweetly, reviving Martial’s hopes.

“Of that little lady, unknown to all, whom the jealousy of all these coquettes has imprisoned in that corner. You, no doubt, know her family?”

“Yes,” said the Duchess. “But what concern have you with a provincial heiress, married some time ago, a woman of good birth, whom you men do not know; she goes nowhere.”

“Why does she not dance? She is such a pretty creature. May we conclude a treaty of peace? If you will tell me all I want to know, I promise to urge a petition for the restitution of the woods of Navarreins by the Commissioners of Crown Lands on the Emperor.”

The younger branch of the house of Navarreins bears the arms of Lansac, and the Duchess’s liaison with Louis XV had earned her husband the title of duke. The lawyer’s suggestion to petition for an estate belonging to the elder branch was sheer trickery.

“Monsieur,” the old woman said with deceptive gravity, “bring the Comtesse de Vaudremont to me. I promise to reveal the mystery of the interesting unknown. You see, every man in the room has reached the same curiosity as yours. All eyes are involuntarily turned towards the corner where my protégée has modestly placed herself; she is reaping the homage the women wished to deprive her of. Happy the man she chooses for her partner!” She fixed her eyes on Madame de Vaudremont with a look plainly saying, “We are talking of you.” “I imagine you would rather learn the stranger’s name from the lips of your handsome Countess than from mine.”

There was such marked defiance in the Duchess’s attitude that Madame de Vaudremont rose, approached her, and took the chair Martial placed for her. Without acknowledging him, she said, “I can guess, madame, that you are talking of me; but I admit my lack of perspicacity; I do not know whether it is for good or evil.”

Madame de Lansac pressed the young woman’s pretty hand in her dry, wrinkled fingers and replied in a low, compassionate tone, “Poor child!”

The women looked at each other. Madame de Vaudremont understood that Martial was in the way and dismissed him with an imperious “Leave us.”

The Baron, displeased to see the Countess under the spell of the dangerous sibyl, gave one of those looks a man can give—potent over a blinded heart but ridiculous in the eyes of a woman beginning to criticize the man who attracted her.

“Do you think you can play the Emperor?” said Madame de Vaudremont, turning to give an ironical sidelong glance at the lawyer.

Martial, a man of the world with wit and acumen, would not risk breaking with a woman favored at Court, whom the Emperor wished to see married. He counted on the jealousy he meant to provoke as the surest means of discovering her coolness and withdrew willingly, as a new quadrille began.

Making room for the dance, the Baron leaned against a marble console, folded his arms, and watched the two ladies talk. He followed their glances at the stranger. Comparing the Countess with the new beauty, made attractive by mystery, the Baron fell prey to the self-interest common to adventurous suitors, hesitating between a fortune within reach and indulging his caprice. The blaze of light highlighted his anxious, sullen face against the white silk hangings, making him resemble an evil genius. Observers might remark, “There is another poor wretch who seems to be enjoying himself!”

Meanwhile, the Colonel, leaning against the doorway between the ballroom and the cardroom, laughed under his moustache. He enjoyed watching the turmoil, reading the Countess’s and Martial’s agitation, and wondering about the link between Soulanges’ gloomy looks and the fair unknown’s alternating hope and dread. Montcornet stood like the king of the feast, receiving inviting smiles from many women. As a Colonel of the Imperial Guard, he was a highly desirable match.

It was nearly midnight. Conversation, gambling, dancing, flirtations, rivalries, and scheming reached full fervor. “A fine ball!” exclaimed a young man.

“My sweet little angel,” Madame de Lansac said to the Countess, “you are now at an age when I made many mistakes. Seeing you endure a thousand deaths, I thought I might give you some charitable advice. To go wrong at two-and-twenty means spoiling your future; it is like tearing the gown you must wear. Later, you learn to go about in it without crumpling it. Go on making clever enemies and friends without sense of conduct, and you will see what a pleasant life you will lead!”

“Oh, madame, it is very hard for a woman to be happy, do you not think?” the Countess eagerly exclaimed.

“My child, at your age, you must learn to choose between pleasure and happiness. You want to marry Martial, who is not wise enough to be a good husband, nor passionate enough to remain a lover. He is in debt, my dear; he is the man to squander your fortune; still, that would be nothing if he could make you happy. Do you not see how aged he is? He must have been ill; he is making the most of what is left him. In three years, he will be a wreck. Then he will be ambitious; perhaps he may succeed. I do not think so. What is he? A man of intrigue, with a knack for gossip, too presumptuous for real merit; he will not go far. Besides—look at him. Is it not written on his brow that, at this very moment, what he sees in you is not a young and pretty woman, but the two million francs you possess? He does not love you, my dear; he is calculating you as if you were an investment. If you are bent on marrying, find an older man with an assured position who is halfway in his career. A widow’s marriage should not be a trivial love affair. Is a mouse to be caught a second time in the same trap? A new alliance should be a good speculation on your part, and in marrying again, you ought at least to hope to be addressed as Madame la Maréchale!”

As she spoke, both women naturally focused on Colonel Montcornet’s handsome face.

“If you prefer to flirt rather than marry again,” the Duchess said bluntly, “you know better than anyone how to stir up trouble and then calm it. But do not disturb families or ruin wives’ happiness. I’ve played that dangerous game. For a moment of vanity, a virtuous woman can be destroyed—there are truly virtuous women—and we can become mortally hated. I learned too late that, as the Duc d’Albe said, one salmon is worth a thousand frogs. Genuine affection brings far more happiness than fleeting passion. I came to preach to you; you are the reason for my visit to this lower-class house. Have I not just seen actors here? We used to receive them in our boudoir, but never in the drawing-room! If you want to play with men, toy only with those whose lives are unsettled and who have no duties. The others won’t forgive us for errors that make them happy. Take this from my long experience. Look at Soulanges, whom you’ve made lovesick for the past fifteen months. Do you realize what you’ve done? You’ve affected his entire life. He’s been married two years and is adored by a charming wife whom he loves but neglects; she lives in tears. Soulanges has felt remorse far worse than any pleasure you’ve given him. And you, you little minx, have abandoned him. Come see the result of your actions.”

The old lady took Madame de Vaudremont’s hand and they rose.

“There,” said Madame de Lansac, pointing to a pale, trembling figure under the candlelight, “that is my grandniece, the Comtesse de Soulanges. Today she finally yielded to my persuasion and left the sorrowful room where her child brings her little consolation. You think her charming? Imagine how she must have looked when happiness and love illuminated that now-blighted face.”

The Countess looked away, lost in sad thought.

The Duchess led her to the card-room door, then, scanning the room, said, “And there is Soulanges!”

The Countess shuddered at the sight of her husband, pale and rigid in an easy chair. His indifference and the tension in his brow revealed his suffering. The players passed him by, ignoring him as if he were dead. The image of the tearful wife and the morose husband, separated amidst festivity, struck the Countess with a sense of foreboding about her own future. Though her heart was not entirely hardened, she pressed the Duchess’s hand, thanking her with a childlike smile.

“My dear child,” the old lady whispered, “remember that we can repel a man’s attentions just as easily as we can attract them.”

“She is yours if you’re not a fool,” Madame de Lansac whispered to Colonel Montcornet while the Countess remained absorbed in her compassion for Soulanges, still loving him enough to wish to restore his happiness.

“Oh! I will talk to him!” she declared to Madame de Lansac.

“Do nothing of the sort!” the old lady exclaimed as she returned to her chair. “Choose a good husband and shut your door to my nephew. A wife cannot accept her husband’s heart as a gift from another woman; she is far happier believing she has won it back. By bringing my niece here, I believe I’ve given her a chance to regain her husband’s affection. All I need from you is to play the Colonel.” She pointed to Montcornet, and the Countess smiled.

“Well, madame, do you finally know the name of the unknown?” Martial asked the Countess when they were alone.

“Yes,” replied Madame de Vaudremont, her expression playful.

Her smile and bright eyes were like a will-o’-the-wisp leading travelers astray. Martial, believing she still loved him, adopted a charming demeanor. “Will you be annoyed if I seem to care about that name?”

“Will you be annoyed if a remnant of affection prevents me from telling you? And if I forbid you to approach that young lady? It could be dangerous.”

“To lose your favor would be worse than losing my life.”

“Martial,” the Countess said sternly, “she is Madame de Soulanges. Her husband would kill you if he knew.”

“Ha! So the Colonel can leave the man in peace who has taken his love, yet would fight for his wife! What a reversal of principles! I beg you to let me dance with her. You’ll see how little love that icy heart could feel for you; if the Colonel allows me to dance with his wife after letting me—”

“But she loves her husband.”

“An even greater obstacle I shall enjoy overcoming.”

“But she is married.”

“A whimsical objection!”

“Ah!” the Countess said with a bitter smile, “you punish us for both our faults and our repentance!”

“Do not be angry!” Martial exclaimed eagerly. “Forgive me, I beseech you. I will think no more of Madame de Soulanges.”

“You deserve that I should send you to her.”

“I’m off then,” he laughed, “and I’ll return more devoted to you than ever. You’ll see that the prettiest woman cannot capture the heart that is yours.”

“Meaning you want to win Colonel Montcornet’s horse?”

“Ah! Traitor!” he said, playfully threatening his friend. The Colonel smiled and joined them; the Baron gave him a seat near the Countess, saying with a sardonic tone, “Here, madame, is a man who boasted he could win your favor in one evening.”

He left, thinking he had cleverly piqued the Countess’ pride and done Montcornet a disservice, unaware of the irony in Madame de Vaudremont’s words, as both were unconsciously moving toward each other.

At that moment, the lawyer approached Madame de Soulanges, who sat pale and timid, seemingly alive only in her eyes. Her husband entered the ballroom, his eyes flashing with anger. The Duchess, ever watchful, rushed to her nephew, asking for his arm to find her carriage, feigning boredom to prevent a scene. Before leaving, she shot a knowing glance at her niece, signaling her to take revenge.

Madame de Vaudremont noticed the exchange and felt a sudden realization; she feared being the target of the Duchess’s cunning.

“That duplicitous Duchess,” she thought, “may be preaching morality while plotting against me.”

This thought stirred her pride more than her curiosity to unravel the intrigue. In her distraction, she lost control of herself. The Colonel, misinterpreting her embarrassment, became more ardent. The old diplomats, entertained by the unfolding drama, had never witnessed so many simultaneous intrigues. The passions of the two couples were evident in the crowded rooms, reflected in the faces around them, amplifying their own feelings of impotence.

Finally, the Baron found a seat beside Madame de Soulanges. He admired her beauty up close, captivated by her fresh neck and graceful figure. At that time, women wore sashes tied under the bosom, a style that flattered those with perfect figures. As he stole glances at the Countess, Martial was enchanted by her perfection.

“You haven’t danced once this evening, madame,” he said in soft, flattering tones. “Not for lack of a partner, I presume?”

“I never go to parties; I’m quite unknown,” Madame de Soulanges replied coldly, unaware of her aunt’s signal to attract the Baron.

Martial, adjusting the diamond on his hand, felt a spark of hope as he caught the Countess’s eye. She blushed and looked at him with an indefinable expression.

“Do you enjoy dancing?” he asked to continue the conversation.

“Yes, very much, monsieur.”

Their eyes met, and the earnestness in her voice stirred something in him. “Then, am I too bold in offering to be your partner for the next quadrille?”

Artless confusion colored her cheeks.

“But, monsieur, I have already refused one partner—a military man—”

“Was it that tall cavalry colonel over there?”

“Precisely.”

“Oh! He’s a friend of mine; don’t worry. Will you grant me the favor I hope for?”

“Yes, monsieur.”

Her tone revealed a new and deep emotion that touched Martial’s world-weary heart. Overcome with shyness, he lost his confidence, and his southern charm ignited. He tried to speak, but his words felt clumsy compared to Madame de Soulanges’ bright replies. Fortunately, the quadrille was forming, and standing beside her eased his nerves. For many men, dancing is a way to express themselves, believing they can win a woman’s heart through their physical grace. Martial likely intended to showcase all his charms, judging by his pretentious movements and gestures.

He led his conquest to the quadrille, where the most brilliant women prized dancing above all. As the orchestra sounded, the Baron surveyed the ladies forming the square with pride. Madame de Soulanges’ dress rivaled Madame de Vaudremont’s; opposite him stood the lady in blue. Eyes turned to Madame de Soulanges; the flattering murmur and the blush it produced increased her charm. When she lifted her eyes she sought her partner’s, as if to transfer the glory of admiration to him, showing she prized his opinion above all. She blended innocence and vanity, a youthful affection visible in her furtive glances. Though modest and inexperienced, she knew how to glance at Martial at just the right moments.

When a new figure devised by a dancer named Trenis brought Martial face to face with the Colonel, he joked, “I have won your horse.” Montcornet retorted, “Yes, but you have lost eighty thousand francs a year!” Martial waved it off: “What do I care? Madame de Soulanges is worth millions!”

After the quadrille whispers spread. Less attractive women made moral remarks about Martial and the Comtesse, while the more beautiful admired her apparent ease in surrendering to him. The men, puzzled by the Baron’s luck, did not regard him as remarkably charming. Some sympathetic women argued against condemning the Countess: if a mere glance or a graceful dance could compromise a woman’s reputation, young wives would be in a dire position.

Only Martial grasped the depth of his joy. During the last figure, as the ladies formed the moulinet, he clasped the Countess’s hand and felt her response through the thin glove. “Madame,” he said as the quadrille ended, “do not return to that corner where you’ve been hiding. Is admiration the only benefit you gain from your jewels? Come and enjoy the scene and yourself.”

Madame de Soulanges yielded to his invitation, and they moved through crowded rooms. She hesitated at every door, peering in before entering, calmed only by his assurance, “He is not here.” They reached a grand picture gallery where a lavish collation awaited three hundred guests. As supper neared, Martial led the Countess to an oval boudoir overlooking the garden, its blue hangings and fragrant flowers muffling the noise. Startled at first, she resisted but finally sat on an ottoman after glancing in a mirror, perhaps reassured that they might be seen.

“This room is charming,” she said, admiring the decor. “All here is love and delight!” Martial, moved, watched her face in the dim light—unease, modesty, eagerness that captivated him. She smiled, calming the struggle. Playfully she took his left hand and removed his ring, exclaiming, “What a fine diamond!”

Martial, intoxicated by her touch, regarded her with eyes bright as the gem. “Wear it,” he said, “in memory of this hour, and for the love of—” He broke off, caught by her rapture. She asked in surprise, “You give it to me?” “I wish I had the whole world to offer you!” he replied. “Will you accept only my diamond?” She pressed, “You will never take it back?” “Never,” he assured.

She placed the ring on her finger, and as Martial moved to embrace her she suddenly rose and said clearly, “I accept the diamond, monsieur, with less scruple because it belongs to me.” The Baron was taken aback. “Monsieur de Soulanges took it from my dressing-table and told me he had lost it.”

“You are mistaken, madame,” Martial said, irritated. “It was given to me by Madame de Vaudremont.” “Precisely,” she replied with a smile. “My husband borrowed it, gave it to her, and she gave it to you. This ring has made a little journey, that is all. It may reveal secrets to me.” She touched a spring in the setting, revealing a lock of M. de Soulanges’ hair, then fled into the crowded rooms, leaving Martial confounded. He noticed the Colonel and Madame de Vaudremont laughing nearby; the Colonel teased, “Will you have my horse to chase after your prize?” Martial took their banter in stride, grateful they remained silent about the evening’s events as he had, in effect, exchanged his horse for a young wife.

As the Comtesse de Soulanges drove across Paris to the Faubourg Saint-Germain, anxiety consumed her. Before leaving the Hotel Gondreville she had searched for her aunt and husband, who had departed without her. Disturbing suspicions plagued her. She had witnessed her husband’s torment since Madame de Vaudremont ensnared him and had hoped for his repentance. Now she feared she had made a grave error by agreeing to her aunt’s plan.

The evening had darkened her innocent spirit. Alarmed first by her husband’s suffering, she grew more distressed by her rival’s beauty and by the weight of societal corruption. Crossing the Pont Royal, she discarded the lock of hair from the diamond, a token of pure affection, and wept as long-standing grief rose up. She shuddered at the thought that a woman seeking peace must bury her pain without complaint. “Alas!” she thought, “What can women do when they do not love? What sustains their devotion? I cannot believe, as my aunt claims, that reason alone is enough.”

Still sighing, she arrived home and rushed upstairs, startled to find her husband by the fire. “How long has it been since you attended balls without telling me?” he asked, his voice strained. “You know a woman is out of place without her husband. You compromised yourself by hiding in the dark corner.”

“Oh, my dear Léon,” she replied coaxingly, “I couldn’t resist the joy of seeing you without your knowing. My aunt took me to the ball, and I was very happy there!”

Her words softened the Count’s sternness; he had feared she would learn of his infidelity and had been eager to criticize her first. Delighted to see him smile and grateful for his presence, the Countess looked at him tenderly, blushing and lowering her eyes. Her kindness further charmed Soulanges, particularly after the misery he had endured at the ball. He took her hand and kissed it gratefully, for gratitude often accompanies love.

“Hortense, what is that on your finger that hurt my lip?” he asked, laughing. “It’s my diamond, which you said you lost, and which I have found.”

General Montcornet did not marry Madame de Vaudremont despite their brief understanding; she later perished in the tragic fire that marred the ball hosted by the Austrian ambassador for Napoleon’s marriage to the daughter of Emperor Joseph II.
