Balzac, famed for detailed studies of character and everyday life, rarely drew directly from real persons; yet Beatrix borrows recognizable traits—Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin) suggests George Sand, while Beatrix, Conti, and Claude Vignon recall the Comtesse d’Agoult, Liszt, and critic Gustave Planche. The opening scene—an old Breton family—signals a move from past to modernity and stands as a striking portrait in Balzac’s Comedy of Human Life.

In Brittany many towns remain insulated from nineteenth‑century progress, preserving old customs and feudal traces. Guerande exemplifies this: a walled town with moats, battlements and gates intact, where a mayor’s planting of poplars along the public promenade caused scandal. Houses and streets feel centuries old and often deserted; traditional Breton costumes dominate the square, social divisions endure, and fervent Catholicism resists new ideas. The town overlooks famed salt‑marshes—locals insist their salt improves butter and sardines—and is connected to France by slow, infrequent roads from Savenay and Vannes, reinforcing isolation.

Near the church stands the du Guaisnic mansion, symbol of bygone grandeur. Tracing roots to the du Guesclins, the family retains noble status despite diminished fortunes. The house bears a crested archway, a courtyard with stables and kitchen, a grand hall with elaborate woodwork and a massive fireplace, and a dining room hung with fourteenth‑century tapestries. Simple family rooms above and a half‑acre garden with fruit trees, a sundial, Venetian touches and a spiral column preserve an enduring, ancestral spirit that contrasts with modern, transient homes.

Early May 1836 the Guenic household consisted of Baron and Baroness du Guenic, the baron’s elder sister Mademoiselle Zéphirine, and their only son Calyste, twenty-one. The baron, Gaudebert-Calyste-Charles, a veteran of the Vendée and Brittany insurrections who fought with Charette and La Rochejaquelein, had prudently sold his property to his sister before the war. After years in resistance he returned to Guérande in 1813, married Fanny O’Brien, an Irish noblewoman, and Calyste was born the day Louis XVIII landed.

Now seventy-three, the baron showed the marks of his turbulent life: tall, wiry, deeply wrinkled, with scarred hands. Illiterate except for his prayer book, he dressed plainly—his coat bearing the cross of Saint-Louis—and spoke with somnolent gravity about the exiled Bourbons, the Catholic faith, and Brittany’s future. Calyste was their sole hope; the baron trained him strictly and once took him to join the Duchesse de Berry’s cause, returning after three months with the rueful comment, “The barons did not all do their duty.”

That evening the baron slept while Fanny, forty-two, radiant and tender, read the paper. Blind Zéphirine knitted, proud and cheerful, refusing surgery to avoid burdening her brother. As Mariotte lit the lamp and Gasselin tended the horses and dogs, their steady loyalty—quietly managed by Zéphirine—reflected the household’s affectionate, duty-bound cohesion in trying times.

When night fell, Gasselin entered the hall and respectfully asked his master if he needed anything further. The baron, roused, said Gasselin could go out or to bed after prayers unless Madame or his sister required him. The two ladies consented, and Gasselin knelt with the family while Mademoiselle du Guenic led the prayer. A knock followed; Gasselin opened for Monsieur le curé.

Monsieur Grimont, a middle-aged priest in a cassock, entered with the easy civility of his office. His plump, calm visage and jovial air suggested a clear conscience; though well regarded in Guérande, he acknowledged the du Guenics’ feudal superiority. He inquired about Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel’s tardiness and mentioned the young chevalier’s presence at Les Touches that evening. Mariotte cautioned against such talk before Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, who loved town gossip.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel arrived next, thin and elderly but still in the fashion of her youth, always carrying keys and a silver snuff-box. Of high Breton nobility whose influence had waned, she ran her property frugally and attracted nieces hoping for her favor. Jacqueline de Pen-Hoel, friend to Zéphirine, meant to marry a niece to Calyste to tie the family to the du Guenics; she treated Calyste proprietarily and distrusted new influences. Inquiry about Monsieur du Halga brought expectation: the rector fetched a basket and counters for an evening game, and a firm knock announced the frail Chevalier du Halga—once a capable naval officer, now weakened by emigration hardships, gentle and absorbed with his little dog; the rector’s cough during the prayer for the king quietly hinted at lingering political tensions.

Mouche is a five-card trick game: one card is turned up as trump, players may play or abstain (abstaining costs only the stake), winners share the pot, losers become a mouche and must pay the full stake, enlarging the pot. Players may exchange cards from the deck in order; the dealer keeps the turned-up trump and may swap it for any card in hand. The highest card is the Mistigris, the knave of clubs.

Though simple, mouche proves engrossing, exposing greed and cunning. At the Hôtel du Guénic each player starts with twenty counters representing five sous, the total stake being five farthings. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël and Mademoiselle Zéphirine treat the game seriously, like speculation. An 1825 convention allows players who lose ten sous to quit, but the chevalier and the baron quietly lend counters to keep the game alive. The arrival of Demoiselle de Kergarouët and her aunt raises stakes further; the young women, enamored of Calyste, scorn such small sums.

Evenings mix play with local gossip; accusations of forgotten stakes sometimes target the rector. Attempts to introduce whist or Boston fail—the circle prefers mouche. One night, after talk of the Duc de Lenoncourt’s death and the Comtesse de Kergarouët’s marriage, gossip turns to the actress Mademoiselle des Touches. When Pen-Hoël holds the Mistigris the baroness and rector exchange worried looks. After play the chevalier escorts Pen-Hoël home; she reveals Calyste’s infatuation with the actress. The rector warns the actress is scandal-prone and notes Pen-Hoël intends to bequeath her fortune to any niece who marries Calyste. Distressed, the baroness laments her waning bond with Calyste; the rector promises to investigate and leaves her waiting at the gate.

The distressed baroness returned to find the whole town aware that her son Calyste was often visiting Mademoiselle des Touches—visits she had assumed private. She had even tried to lure him with needlework, but his evenings there grew longer, once until midnight. Puzzled and jealous, she wondered what charm held men and feared that modern influences would corrupt her carefully raised son. Calyste, of Breton and Irish nobility, had been entrusted to the rector of Guerande and sheltered from vice; his schooling left him ignorant of contemporary literature and science, and he remained a fervent Catholic.

The baroness had pictured a modest, secure future for Calyste—marriage to a wealthy heiress aided by an expected inheritance—but Mademoiselle des Touches threatened those plans. When he returned exhausted at midnight she was relieved yet bitter as she sensed his affections elsewhere. The next day he slept late; his attire suggested another dinner at Les Touches. Her sister fretted; the baroness feared the woman's morals and influence, especially after Calyste began reading a wider range of books under her guidance.

Calyste defended Mademoiselle des Touches, saying she had rejected his love and offered friendship; she was a talented artist who deserved respect despite an unconventional life. The baron dismissed such worries as youthful alarm. Meanwhile Guerande buzzed with rumors that she was infatuated with Calyste and exerted a mysterious hold—rumors the rector and townsfolk scorned.

Mademoiselle Félicité des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, was a celebrated, scandal-provoking writer. Orphaned in the Revolution and raised by an aunt, she became self-educated and cultivated a salon for artists and thinkers. Rejecting marriage to preserve her independence, she was drawn to Calyste’s innocence; their relationship stirred private longings that conflicted with her public ambition, fear of aging, and determination to maintain autonomy, forcing her to weigh love against the life she had built.

A few hundred yards from Guérande, Brittany’s soil gives way to salt marshes and dunes. We descend into a bleached landscape of ponds and a narrow arm of the sea separating the mainland from Croisic, reached by treacherous quicksand beaches.

At the road junction from Croisic to Guérande stands Les Touches, a country house set among wind‑sculpted pines. Built of slaty stone and granite, its plain exterior—regular windows, a high pointed roof with gables and dormers—faces the road on one side and the desert toward Croisic on the other; a brook runs from the park, crosses the road, and disappears into the sands. The estate, yielding over ten thousand francs a year from marshes and farms, was stripped of feudal rights by the Revolution yet still called “the château” by local salt workers. Félicité restored the house, preserving its desolate aspect and adding only brick gate columns to mark the entrance.

The ground floor, likely raised on castle ruins, follows an old country‑house plan. Félicité turned the entrance hall into a billiard room opening onto a large salon and dining room; the kitchen communicates with the dining room via an office. Décor is simple and graceful—gray walls, mahogany furniture covered in green silk; the dining room holds mahogany buffets, horsehair chairs, and engravings by Audran. Upstairs Félicité’s suite—salon, large bedroom, two cabinets—reveals luxury: Gobelin tapestries, heavy silk drapes, fine Louis XV furniture, while her study, in modern mahogany and filled with intimate objects, hints at a complex character.

Outside, marshes, dunes, and ocean create a melancholic, elevating atmosphere that Camille prefers to Guérande’s lushness. Calyste, seeking refuge from Guérande, clings to Félicité despite earlier rejection. At Les Touches he encounters music and literature that awaken him; Félicité nurtures his intellect, becomes a maternal figure, and gives him music lessons. One visit he hears her haunting piano improvisation; finding her in tears over Claude Vignon’s absence, he offers to search for the man, but she asks him to stay and prepares to tell the story of her fraught relationship with Claude—the age gap, the inevitable separation, and why she once rejected Calyste. He sits at her feet, eager but unaware of its full depth.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

“I promised you this tale of the past, and here it is,” Camille begins. The letter’s author is the Marquise de Rochefide. The old marquis, after marrying his eldest daughter to a Portuguese grandee, urged his son to wed nobly to secure a peerage. The Comtesse de Montcornet recommended Mademoiselle Béatrix-Maximilienne-Rose de Casteran, the marquis’s youngest. The Casterans, proud of lineage, would marry off daughters without dowries to preserve the estate for the male heir. Béatrix, raised at the château de Casteran, was twenty when she married in 1828.

Camille warns that Béatrix’s originality, independence, ardor, and passion for art—qualities that ennoble her—also expose a woman to abyssal risks men can more safely face. The match pleased the Rochefides and briefly helped obtain a peerage for their son under Charles X, undone by the July Revolution. Early married life proved perilous: Rochefide misread Béatrix’s reserve for frigidity, reverted to bachelor habits, and—his vanity pricked—became petty and jealous when she seemed indifferent to his infidelities. Camille explains Béatrix’s later behavior: admiration for Camille could curdle into jealousy; her attempt to found a salon produced only superficial company because she lacked time and experience. Yet beneath the missteps lay a grand soul—regal pride and an intellect able to discuss metaphysics and music.

Béatrix was striking: slender and fair, a long pointed face, noble forehead, pale sea-green eyes, severe lines; dressed well she could seem nearly divine. Camille teases young Calyste not to fall for her—by the time he notices it will be too late.

After early doubts she returned to Paris post-July Revolution, explored new doctrines, but found no room for individual superiority and chose personal happiness over abstract ideals. Then she met Gennaro Conti, a Neapolitan composer dazzling in manner and voice but duplicitous beneath his mask of genius. Camille, devoted to Conti’s talent yet clear-eyed about his selfishness, warned Béatrix; passion blinded her. Conti rejoiced; Camille even lent him money for their trip to Italy. Béatrix left her husband, stayed abroad two years, and wrote Camille adoring, grateful letters—the only woman she felt understood her.

Claude Vignon then appears, imposing despite a haggard look—high forehead, sarcastic mouth—capable of profound thought yet often immobilized by it, prone to destructive criticism. His dry remark to Mademoiselle des Touches—“By the door”—and sardonic asides unsettle the company.

Back home Calyste produces Béatrix’s letter to his mother. It speaks of Italy’s weariness amid constant enjoyment, strains with Conti, diminished social equality, sacrifices for love, longing for solitude, and financial worries likely to bring them back to Paris. Camille frames the correspondence as exaltation mixed with disillusionment.

Melancholy, Calyste confesses to his mother that Félicité has awakened him to higher possibilities beyond his narrow education. His aunt suggests a safe Breton match; he rejects prudence, yearning for the freedom and passion he associates with love. After dinner he returns to Les Touches where Claude and Félicité remain. Their witty exchange—Claude’s admiration for genius, Félicité’s defense of Béatrix’s nobility—deeply moves Calyste. His mother, understanding, embraces him, urging pursuit of happiness tempered by reason. The scene closes with encouragement, grounding wisdom, and a young man’s yearning for passion and purpose.

What young man, full of restrained feeling, would not hurry to see Madame de Rochefide? Calyste surprised his parents by slipping away early, moving briskly along Les Touches' walls to avoid being seen, excited and ashamed that Félicité or Vignon might notice.

At the shore he hid in a shelter and watched two boats arrive from Croisic, their luggage suggesting distinguished passengers. One boat held a young woman in a straw bonnet, a maid, and a footman; a chest stamped "MME. LA MARQUISE DE ROCHEFIDE" captivated him, and he felt ready to transfer the affection he had once reserved for Camille to this new figure.

Overhearing servants, he learned they awaited someone from Les Touches and that the marquise had already been taken away. Agitated, he hurried toward Guérande, consumed by thoughts of her. Returning home late, he found a letter from Mademoiselle des Touches inviting him to celebrate the marquise’s arrival and reassuring his mother of her noble lineage, mentioning the Casteran family.

At the gathering he met Gennaro Conti, a Byron-like presence, and at last saw the marquise—more lovely than imagined, her hair braided with bluebells. Assigned to escort her at dinner, he was tongue-tied, confessing she rivaled even his mother in beauty. Mademoiselle des Touches warned restraint; the marquise seemed absorbed by Conti and Camille. When Conti and Camille played, the music moved Calyste to tears; feeling inadequate and despairing, he slipped away to a nearby sofa.

Calyste returns home to find Claude Vignon waiting in his bedroom. Claude, who has already suspected more than he admits, tells Calyste the situation is not what it seems: Camille loves Calyste, while Calyste’s heart is fixed on Beatrix. Bitter and weary, Claude confesses that his brooding walks on the marshes have been the result of wounded pride; he plans to leave Les Touches—perhaps for Paris—and predicts that his absence will let passions break into tumult. He warns Calyste that the game before him is perilous: Beatrix is implacable, Camille noble and dangerous in a different way, and Calyste’s excess of feeling for one may make him misuse the other. Disgusted with women yet clear-eyed, Claude urges caution.

Stunned by the revelation that Camille loves him while he loves Beatrix, Calyste is crushed. He replays Camille’s earlier rejection, Claude’s accusations, and Beatrix’s apparent gaiety; silence and confusion overwhelm him.

At midnight Claude confronts Camille (Felicite). He accuses her of coming to Paris already in love with Calyste and of using Calyste as a screen—bringing Claude to Les Touches to hide her secret adoration. He calls her scheme both grand and ignoble, claims she misjudged him as simple when he had divined her, and asserts that she has perhaps never been truly loved because her mind and charm are too abstract and overpowering for ordinary affection. Camille, moved, asks why love eludes her; Claude replies that she is not lovable in the usual way—she fascinates but repels the strong—and says he will leave to spare her the shame of being sheltered by a man she did not love. He insists they can part without betrayed hope because they had no mutual happiness to mourn.

Calyste then reveals himself. Claude tells him plainly that Camille loves him—less as ordinary human need than as a taste for exquisite longing—and accuses her of having driven Calyste toward Madame de Rochefide. Camille, trembling, takes Calyste’s head, kisses his hair, and begs him to forget what he heard: “I count for nothing in all this,” she says. Abruptly she declares that conceiving love without hope brings one nearest to God; she commands him not to love her and yet vows, “I will love you as no woman will!” Claude kneels and kisses her hand. To spare the household worry, Camille sends Calyste away.

Calyste returns to Guerande distraught. He tells his mother, tearful and abrupt, that Camille loved him but that he no longer loves her. The next day he posts a watcher on the road and rides out. A carriage arrives with two ladies and two gentlemen: among them are Beatrix and Camille, accompanied by the Viscountess de Kergarouet, Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, and Charlotte. Camille invites the newcomers into her carriage; Charlotte, plain and conscious of her provincialness beside the Parisian women, greets Calyste. In the crowd Beatrix, elegant in straw and muslin, stands with a parasol; Camille takes Calyste’s arm. Calyste kisses Camille’s hand, leaving a tear; Beatrix, wiping away anger, looks away. Camille’s calm and grace soothe Calyste despite his turmoil.

At the evening’s card table and dinner the household plays mouche; Calyste’s momentary gaiety reassures everyone that he has recovered and that prospects with Charlotte may succeed. Yet he remains inwardly troubled, comparing the Parisians to his childhood acquaintances. Only his mother perceives his unease and urges him to offer the viscountess a ride; Calyste runs back to Les Touches to arrange it.

After dinner Camille and Beatrix leave the salon together; Camille teases Calyste about abandoning the viscountess for them. When Beatrix suggests the viscountess’s niece might suit Calyste, Camille bluntly replies that the marriage will never take place. Later, in private, Camille takes Calyste into her chamber and delivers candid counsel: if he wants to win Beatrix he must seem to love Camille still; otherwise Beatrix will be lost. Loving and making oneself beloved are different arts, she explains, and candor will not serve him. She proposes deliberate deception—he must promise blind obedience, spend every morning with her, avoid private meetings with Beatrix, and let her use her art to direct Beatrix’s feelings. Confused but desperate, Calyste agrees and embraces her; Camille weeps but does not resist.

After Calyste leaves, Camille, alone and distraught, confesses to Beatrix the depth of her feeling. She admits she is forty, in love, terrified of being loved only out of pity, and capable of extreme acts if Calyste commanded them. She foresees that Beatrix’s coldness might secure Calyste for her and pleads for the marquise’s generosity—and for a secret means of keeping him true. Beatrix, both piqued and curious, departs with mixed feelings; Camille, sinking into tears and smoking opium-laced tobacco, broods on her pain and on the young lover’s head.

For six days Camille sets about her strategy: constant companionship of Calyste, conversational feints, subtle provocations designed to wound Beatrix’s pride and kindle curiosity. Her remarks are sometimes treacherous; Beatrix alternately takes offense and is secretly amused to see the great writer caught in pettiness. Calyste lives for the daily moments before Beatrix, reads late into the night, and brings home armfuls of books. His mother begs him to leave Les Touches, but he refuses.

One morning, after an outing on the marshes, Calyste meets Beatrix in the garden. He trembles and asks if he has displeased her; she answers that he has neither pleased nor displeased her and suggests they ought to be indifferent given his binding attachment elsewhere. Calyste declares he no longer loves Camille. Camille appears, rebukes him for any broken promise, takes his arm, and draws him away—forcing a reciprocity of appearances that exposes her sacrifice: she makes herself available as a screen to preserve her hold on Calyste’s fate.

At breakfast Beatrix decides to stifle any rising warmth and behaves with gentle indifference, saying her position is delicate because Conti is coming to fetch her. Calyste leaves Les Touches pallid and near madness at the thought of losing Beatrix and plots a decisive declaration to win her.

Throughout these scenes strategy, pride, and genuine feeling are braided together. Claude remains the cynical observer predicting disaster; Camille, inwardly torn and capable of self-effacement, chooses to guide events through subtle deception; Beatrix, proud and resolute, answers with polite autonomy; Calyste, immature and ardent, is a pawn between stronger wills and uncertain consequences he cannot yet foresee.

When Calyste returned home he shut himself up and wrote feverishly; his mother found him among crumpled pages composing a letter to Béatrix, prompted by fresh distrust of Camille after the marquise’s recent behavior.

His first love letter poured out intense feeling: his love had swelled beyond any dream, he likened himself—youthful and unworthy—to an insect drawn to her light, and contrasted a former, lesser attachment to Félicité with his present, profound truth, Béatrix. Though he feared she scorned him, he invoked Camille’s teaching that true love must grow and endure and begged for kindness, recognition, or even the right to accompany her. The letter’s raw passion moved his mother to tears and alarm.

Torn over delivery, Calyste consulted the Chevalier du Halga, who suggested using Béatrix’s maid, but Calyste handed the letter to Béatrix himself at Les Touches; she accepted it. Her reply acknowledged his noble feelings but called him a child, affirmed her commitment to Camille, doubted her suitability as a partner, warned that her jealous, capricious nature would make him unhappy, and urged him to seek a young girl.

The baroness, moved and anxious, read the reply aloud and urged Calyste to marry Charlotte de Kergarouet to secure the estate, but he remained steadfast. He wrote again, even fantasizing elopement. The baroness consulted the amused Chevalier, read further letters, prayed for her son, and planned a trip to Croisic. The correspondence revealed surprising maturity and clarity in Calyste but also the dangerous intensity of his infatuation.

Beatrix delighted in outwitting those she felt inferior to, yet saw herself as less talented and passionate than Camille Maupin. While Calyste rushed to Les Touches, dazzled by first love, Beatrix enjoyed his devotion without intending to return it, priding herself on repressing feeling and imagining a noble sacrifice like Camille’s. French vanities reassured her moral uprightness.

Reclining in the salon as the calm garden contrasted their unrest, Camille—guarding a secret love and viewing Beatrix as a shrewd rival—sought refuge in nature and reflection; Beatrix’s apparent triumph disturbed her. When Calyste arrived with a letter, Camille watched an unspoken understanding: Beatrix treated Calyste as hers while he looked guilty. His plan for an excursion made Camille suspect a conspiracy and unsettled her.

After Calyste left heavy with despair, Beatrix’s letter stirred unexpected feelings and jealous doubts about Camille. Dinner was tense: veiled insults escalated into accusations of trickery and exposed motives. Beatrix attacked Camille’s lack of conventional femininity; Camille declared her deep love for Calyste. Beatrix retorted she would not be his mistress—only marriage would do—wounding Camille, who broke off the friendship and threatened to inform Conti. The night ended unresolved, both women uneasy before desire and social constraint.

It was late August; the sky was clear and paludiers in white harvested salt as the baron and baroness admired the marshes from the jetty. The baron wanted one last look, and a paludier made a wry joke about the Guenic family’s mortality. From Les Touches arrived the marquise, Calyste, and Camille; the baroness felt a strong aversion to Beatrix, who appeared tasteful but heartless to her.

After introductions—Camille noted for her marsh holdings—the groups boarded a boat for Croisic. The marquise remained cool; Camille reproached Calyste for disobedience, and he was torn between anger and love for Beatrix. The passage was tense and silent; at the port barefoot women unloaded salt.

On Croisic’s granite shore, near a hollow basin and a lone box bush, Calyste led Beatrix to a viewpoint while Camille sought solitude. Beatrix confessed an inner desolation: she loved Calyste but insisted she could never be with him, fearing to abandon her life and endure unfulfilled longing. Calyste offered to sacrifice everything; she rejected sacrifice and clung to duty. Passion overtook Calyste; Beatrix slipped and fell. He saved her; Gasselin and Camille arrived and she was carried to a farmhouse.

Calyste remained at her side as the doctor promised recovery. Beatrix softened—enjoying his devotion yet reminding him of her obligation to another man—then, moved, revealed past struggles and accepted his vow of exclusive love. Their bond deepened and she saw possible happiness, but on returning to Les Touches they encountered Conti, Beatrix’s former lover, shattering the moment and exposing love’s tangled consequences.

Beatrix’s distress was obvious when Conti arrived unexpectedly, seized her arm, and forced her to abandon Calyste, who felt humiliated though convinced of his love. Silent and conflicted, he turned to Camille, who explained that Beatrix couldn’t refuse Conti because of lingering ties and warned that Conti was manipulative, using insinuations to regain control.

Conti’s return after a failed opera was opportunistic: he feigned indifference while subtly undermining Beatrix. At dinner his jovial manner masked intent; veiled remarks about women’s loyalty wounded Beatrix and Camille and exposed a bitter rivalry. Conti then portrayed himself to Calyste as the injured party, urging Calyste to pursue Beatrix to provoke her pride. Unaware of the plot, Calyste confessed details of his intimacy with Beatrix, feeding Conti’s jealousy.

That evening Conti staged a salon performance to provoke Beatrix; entranced, Calyste approached her and fell into Conti’s trap. The next morning Beatrix left with Conti and Calyste was devastated. Weeks of wandering and grief followed; his family worried as his health and social life declined. He refused Charlotte, begged his family to release Beatrix, and after the baron’s death sank further until Camille proposed finding Beatrix in Paris—he agreed, renewed by hope.

Felicite’s love set the stage for Calyste’s future: she wrote to the Duchesse de Grandlieu, sold her house for 2,500,000 francs, bought another for 700,000, planned to use one million to recover the du Guenic estates and settled the remainder on Sabine. Knowing the duke and duchess sought advantageous marriages—only Sabine was marriageable—Felicite aimed to divert Calyste’s affections from Beatrix to Sabine.

On the trip to Paris Mademoiselle des Touches confided in the baroness, who prepared a house for Sabine and Calyste. Welcomed at the hôtel de Grandlieu, Calyste was encouraged to enjoy Paris while Mademoiselle des Touches looked abroad for news of Beatrix. The city’s bustle and Sabine’s resemblance to Beatrix softened his obsession; by winter 1837 he seemed refreshed and listened to his mother’s proposal, concealing any indifference.

On the day of the marriage contract he accepted the offered benefits but read a letter Mademoiselle des Touches had sent via Sabine. In it Camille professed love, declared she would enter a convent, urged him to accept her gifts for his welfare, and asked him to remember her kindly while placing duty above passion. He rejoined the others and the contract was signed. The wedding at Saint Thomas d’Aquin followed; the couple left for Brittany amid tears and congratulations.

The duchess and Clotilde feared for Sabine—Calyste had not wholly willed the marriage and compatibility was crucial, especially for women. The Baronne du Guenic assumed care for Sabine. On the journey the spouses felt awkward; Calyste’s suggestion that they be like friends or siblings hurt Sabine, though his promise to forget Beatrix gradually thawed her. In letters she described longing for home and difficulty loving fully. As chatelaine she found fulfillment—adored by family and tenants and engaged in estate life—but after visiting Mademoiselle des Touches, who advised caprice and never letting Calyste see Beatrix again, Sabine felt conflicted, unable to feign caprice or wholly control her feelings.

Guerande, July 1838.

To Madame la Duchesse de Grandlieu:

Ah, my dear mother! After three months, I have learned what jealousy is. My heart is torn between love and hatred; I feel betrayed and unloved. How fortunate I am to have you to confide in!

It takes so little to set a wife’s curiosity ablaze: “Here’s a key to your palace; go everywhere, but avoid Les Touches!” Mademoiselle des Touches has sown bitterness in me. Why forbid Les Touches? What happiness depends on avoiding a single house in Brittany? Why should I fear?

“My dear mother-in-law said, ‘Les Touches belongs to you.’ ‘If Calyste had never set foot in Les Touches!’ exclaimed Aunt Zéphirine. ‘He would not be my husband,’ I replied. ‘Then you know what happened there?’ she asked slyly. ‘It is a place of perdition!’ declared Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel. ‘Mademoiselle des Touches committed many sins there.’ ‘They saved her soul and enriched a convent,’ said the Chevalier du Halga. ‘Would you like to visit Les Touches?’ my mother-in-law asked. ‘No, no!’ I hastily replied.”

That little scene felt diabolical. Such involuntary stabs force anyone to question the stability of their happiness. Calyste never suggested we visit that hermitage—now his property—but his silence made me ask him about it. “Let’s go there,” he said, and so I let chance catch me.

Les Touches is enchanting and profoundly artistic—poisonous flowers can be charming; the devil has flowers too. I delighted in that place of horror and watched Calyste for signs that his passion for Mademoiselle des Touches might still live. I dared to mention Madame de Rochefide—“la Rocheperfide”—and he said nothing; when I pressed, he begged me to stop reviving that passion. The plea itself inflamed me. Still, when I spoke to him about forgiving misrecognitions tied to that place, he blushed and I embraced him. Yet I left Les Touches and vowed never to return.

My hatred of Madame de Rochefide grows as proof of my love for Calyste. That woman haunts my dreams. The postulant of the Visitation was right—Les Touches is fatal. Calyste has revived past emotions there, stronger than our joy. If Madame de Rochefide is in Paris, I shall stay in Brittany. Poor Mademoiselle des Touches might regret her role when she knew how our odious rival haunted me. But I am ashamed—sometimes I wish to flee Guerande and the sands of Croisic.

August 25th: I am determined to live in the ruins of the old chateau. Calyste, worried by my restlessness, consents. Either he suspects nothing or he knows and loves me not. I tremble at finding certainty, so like a child I cover my eyes to avoid the explosion.

Oh, mother! I am not loved as I love. Calyste is charming—what man wouldn’t be amiable when adored by a pure, loving girl? Yet suspicion intrudes. By September I oscillate between despair and clinging joy, fretting over whether his past has returned to claim him. Sister Clotilde’s melancholy wisdom recurs to me: a woman faithful to the dead fears no rival. Poor me: I fear I can never win Calyste back from himself.

A thousand tendernesses, dear mother. If my terrors are not delusions, Camille Maupin has sold me her fortune dearly. My affectionate respects to papa.

These letters reveal the private breach between husband and wife: Sabine imagined a love marriage; Calyste accepted a conventional match. Their honeymoon joys misaligned with expectations.

In Brittany, the hôtel du Guénic was restored and furnished under the famed architect Grindot, supervised by Clotilde and the Duc and Duchesse de Grandlieu. The young couple planned to return to Paris in December 1838. Sabine took pleasure in settling rue de Bourbon—both to preside over a great household and to read her family’s judgment of her marriage.

Calyste, indifferent, allowed Clotilde and the duchess to guide his social life. He earned his standing by name, fortune, and alliance. Sabine, celebrated in Paris as one of its most charming women, found diversions and duties of the season enlivening. Her pregnancy and the birth of a son in October 1839 confirmed a neutral, socially respectable union; Sabine mistakenly nursed the child herself, believing a mother should be wholly devoted.

By August 1840 Sabine was nearly done nursing. In two years in Paris, Calyste had shed the innocence of his early experience in love. He mixed with Duc Georges de Maufrigneuse, Vicomte Savinien de Portenduère, and others, learning the differences between Parisian and provincial life. Wealth brings idle hours; Paris provides amusements. Contact with young husbands who abandoned noble women for cigars, whist, and clubs gradually eroded domestic virtues. A proud wife may tolerate such dissipation as proof of her success.

One October evening, to escape the cries of their newly weaned child, Calyste went to the Variétés, urged by Sabine. He took a stall near the avant-scène. During the interlude he saw Madame de Rochefide in a proscenium box—Béatrix in Paris! The sight struck him like an arrow. He had sometimes even substituted Béatrix for his wife in thought; Béatrix embodied light, life, the Unknown; Sabine embodied duty and the expected.

Calyste’s first impulse was to leave. Yet the proscenium door was ajar and he approached. Béatrix sat between Canalis and Raoul Nathan. In three years her appearance had changed—gaunt, faded, eyes hollow—but Parisian artifice and contrivance made her instead more poetically attractive to Calyste. She greeted him with a mix of affected grief and coquettish nostalgia—complaining at his marriage and teasing him about “such a little fool.” She revived old intimacy, pressing his hand and turning tears to performative gestures. She spoke of living modestly in a rue de Chartres house “suitable to my means,” filling her head with literature to distract herself.

Calyste left with the perfume of Béatrix still in his nostrils; the rest of the evening she darted sidelong looks that tore at his soul. He returned home haunted: his apartments’ splendor reminded him of the mediocrity Béatrix had mocked, and he resented his wealth because it could not belong to her. He delighted in the possession of a night to relive emotions and feared the way Sabine could read him like a book. A woman who adores can detect every quiver, every sadness; she studies his face for signs of love or its absence.

The next morning Calyste learned Sabine watched for signs of convulsions in their son and was joyous to hear the child was better. Yet he avoided breakfast, preferring a walk to his bachelor café. Love puzzles: a man raised with sacred morals may still be drawn to a fallen, dangerous woman while his virtuous wife waits at home; reasons may lie in vanity or the thrill of triumph. Calyste was drawn to Béatrix for reasons neither philosophy nor morals fully explain.

He decided, consumed by the thought of seeing her again, to go to rue de Chartres. Her house, modest yet tasteful, retained elegant relics of a past life: garnet velvet, celadon vases, a silver cup ascribed to Benvenuto Cellini, little jewels, watercolors—traces of a former world. Béatrix, who had left fortune to her husband when she fled and now scraped by on small sums from friends and her mother, appeared in studied attire—dress revealing, hair in a lace cap—smiling as if expecting a lover. The perfume she wore recalled Les Touches; the scent, her dress, the glint in her eyes made Calyste dizzy.

She bade him sit; she treated him as a friend yet proclaimed she could belong to no man. Three hours passed with her urging him to resolve his conjugal faith; she affected to see Sabine as a stiff, dry, brown woman—good, dutiful, untempting—hardly the ideal for a man like Calyste. Pressed, he rose to leave; she teased him to dine with her. He had the impulse to write Sabine a note, and when he began “My dear Sabine—” Béatrix snapped, “‘My dear’? Can you say your wife is still dear?” She accused him of cowardice and deceit and forced him to write that he dined out; she would send the note by her messenger. He returned home at two in the morning. Sabine, who had waited until half-past twelve for him, had gone to bed exhausted. She slept, distressed by the terse note, yet made the charitable assumption that love explains everything.

The next morning the child was better. Sabine, smiling, came with the baby to present him before breakfast; Calyste charmed her, overplaying affection. In the salon she caught his face in a mirror and saw weariness. She asked, “What did you do yesterday?” He said Savinien had kept him to dinner and then they went to the club to play whist. Sabine lectured on noblesse and industry, then gently reproached the coldness of his first note to her; he claimed club directors were dandies. Small suspicions accumulated: she remembered the paper she had thrown into her letter-box—perhaps it bore a coronet.

Calyste, planning to conceal his doings, rushed to Savinien and then to rue de Chartres to see how Béatrix had passed the night. He found her after her bath, fresh and lively, breakfasting. He stayed until late; his horse returned sweating such that Sabine, watching from a window, noticed.

Meeting him on the staircase she said, “Abd-el-Kader is nearly foundered. You said you’d be gone a moment. I waited three hours.” Calyste, improving in dissimulation, tried to present the surprise of a present: a dressing-table being finished for her, with contributions from his mother and aunt. Sabine clasped him, faint with joy. Then, like a viper’s bite, she recoiled—she had smelled a perfume on his cravat: the scent of another woman’s note-paper. She had kissed where the rival’s kisses had been. Panic overcame her: “Fetch the doctor and my nurse!” she cried. Calyste fled to call them.

Sabine, seized by a chill of reaction, lay shivering, crying, “I am dying!” Her maid fetched Madame de Portenduère. In feverish haste she wrote to her mother—a letter of thanks for the dressing-table, an attempt at ordinary gratitude—but her mind raced: “My God! my God! my God!” She feared betrayal after three years; her teeth chattered, her face turned greenish. “I don’t know yet [the rival], but Calyste has told me two lies. Do not pity me. Oh! that letter of yesterday!” She seized the thrown-away paper from her letter-box: a coronet—could it belong to a marquise? She begged Ursula to find out if Madame de Rochefide had returned to Paris.

Ursula, stunned, went to the duchess, pledging secrecy. The duchess, who had seen such scenes, advised deceit for Sabine’s sake: “Friendship gives you an experience of a woman my age. I know how Sabine loves her husband; she might become insane.” “Or lose her beauty,” Ursula added. “Let us go to her.”

Fortuitously, they arrived before the accoucheur Dommanget and Calyste. The duchess told Sabine that Béatrix was not in Paris; as for Calyste’s night, she declared he had lost a great deal of money at cards and didn’t even know how to pay for the dressing-table. She scoffed at the coronet on the paper—“Everybody writes on coroneted paper now”—and burned the fatal sheet.

When Calyste and Dommanget arrived, the duchess informed them: “Sabine’s life is at stake; you have betrayed her for Madame de Rochefide.” Calyste blushed like a schoolboy. The duchess, with the physician, invented a lie to steer the diagnosis: Calyste had lost thirty thousand francs at cards. Dommanget, quick and self-interested, used the lie to explain Sabine’s fainting as reactionary hysterical symptoms due to overexertion from nursing and anxiety. The physician prescribed measures; Calyste sat at the foot of the bed, trying to look tender. Sabine, hearing the explanation, retorted that her husband could lose any sum without being lectured. Calyste embraced her and called her an angel.

Two days later, thought safe, Sabine improved. Calyste visited Madame de Rochefide, making a merit of his infamy.

“Béatrix,” he said, “you owe me happiness. I have sacrificed my poor little wife to you; she discovered everything. The fatal paper bore your name and coronet; I didn’t notice—the ‘B’ was erased. Your perfume betrayed me. Sabine nearly died; her milk went to her head; erysipelas set in; she may bear marks for life.”

Béatrix’s face froze. “So much the better,” she said; “perhaps it will whiten her for you.” Her icy cruelty continued in a series of sarcasms. There is no greater blunder than to speak of one’s virtuous wife to one’s mistress—unless it is to tell one’s wife of the mistress. Calyste had neither the Parisian training to lie to his wife nor the tact to handle his mistress. He begged Béatrix for pardon; she forced him to beg for two hours. She answered from an elevated bitterness: “To speak to me of your wife the day after my fall! Why did you not tell me she is a pearl of virtue?” She declared her love for his soul and then scorned Calyste’s looks.

This speech was part of Béatrix’s system: with each passion she became another being, advancing into profligacy. Reduced by Conti’s desertion and grateful for scraps from friends, she aimed at a terrible notoriety—vengeance through fame. To prevail over a splendid young woman, she used the arts of courtesans: contempt mixed with rare affection, comparisons meant to mortify, and a cultivated disdain to stimulate conquest. She resolved to make Calyste think himself ugly and unwelcome—thus rekindling his desire to conquer. She contrasted her poetic, comfortable home with the hôtel du Guénic’s Faubourg Saint-Germain luxury, painting the latter stupid and empty.

Thus, the scene of reconciliation—where she made him swear hatred for his wife and denounce her as playing comedy—took shape in a bower of flowers and rare plants, amid trifles and sumptuous yet carefully economical luxury. Fallen into a mortifying position by Conti’s desertion, Béatrix was determined to gain, at least, the triumphs of notoriety. The misfortune of a rich and beautiful wife like Sabine was to be her pedestal.

When Sabine returns to ordinary life after nursing her first child she seems refreshed but finds her husband Calyste changed. A letter from her mother hints at his discontent and names another woman, Beatrix, confirming Sabine’s worst fears. In despair Sabine scrawls a remark on the note and leaves it for Calyste; he finds it, burns the paper, and humiliates her. Beatrix’s influence later becomes concrete when the perfume on a letter reveals her hand. Sabine clings to motherhood’s innocence—dancing with her child—then weeps as Calyste reads Beatrix’s message. Her efforts to win him back—flowers, better cooking, improved appearance—meet only indifference, intensifying jealousy and self-doubt.

Broken, she tears off a beautiful dress and falls ill; the duchess notices and tries to distract her. Sabine eventually confides in her sister Athenais, asking that her child be cared for if she dies, and warns Athenais to be cautious in love. The duchess consults Abbe Brossette and plans to break Calyste’s attachment; the abbe warns against mixing spiritual authority with intrigue, but the duchess, determined, resolves to act even if morally questionable.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

A storm was brewing for Monsieur de Rochefide, who maintained Parisian pleasures as fully after Beatrix left as during their marriage and assumed his comfortable life was safe. The tale that follows—contrasting society’s treatment of men and women in similar situations—might encourage women to defend their dignity, as Sabine du Guenic did.

After Beatrix’s departure, Arthur de Rochefide, now sole heir after his sister’s death, took ownership of the hôtel de Rochefide and a large fortune, earning about a thousand francs a day. He enjoyed wealth without extravagance: vain, good-looking, and witty in company, he dismissed women and pursued fashionable causes and racehorses tended by an English groom. He rarely dined or slept at home and credited his luck to being “born with a caul.”

After several affairs he married Madame Schontz, La Petite-Aurélie, orphaned and schooled at Saint-Denis, who had navigated precarious social paths before catching Rochefide’s eye at Valentino’s. Society did not condemn the match. Their relationship passed through four phases: courtship, with him providing an allowance and lodgings while she displayed charm and ambition; household management after moving to better accommodations, where she rebuffed richer suitors; maternal and financial stewardship of Rochefide’s son, advising investments she never claimed; and finally, establishing their social circle, preserving reputation, and quietly amassing a modest fortune through prudent savings, investments, and selling jewelry while framing expenses as necessary.

Madame Schontz had a plan. Jealous of Madame du Bruel, she was determined to remarry properly — in church and civil ceremonies — and all her energy went into securing a good match. Rochefide’s secret rival remained unknown: Bixiou suspected Léon de Lora, the painter thought Bixiou himself favored, others named Victor de Vernisset or Stidmann; none understood that Madame Schontz’s single aim was marriage.

The salon’s only disreputable man, Couture, a fallen stock-gambler still loyal, introduced Fabien du Ronceret, a provincial son of a chief justice who had left a judgeship for Paris with small means and a flair for exploiting contemporary vanity and notoriety. Fabien cultivated attention by ostentation — a lavish apartment and a greenhouse — and sought entry into Madame Schontz’s circle. Couture recommended him; she judged him suitable, flattered him, and led him to suppose he was loved. Bewitched, he dressed his best. A month before a key meeting she revealed her aims; Fabien misread them. On a carriage ride she declared she wanted an ambitious husband who valued her character. He hesitated; she then turned to Couture, who promptly proposed when he learned of her fortune.

Meanwhile the Duchesse de Grandlieu asked Comte Maxime de Trailles to handle a delicate family matter. Maxime agreed but imposed conditions — notably that the duchess accept his future wife — and planned to remove a rival, aware of the moral complications, especially Calyste’s infatuation with Béatrix. Rochefide warned Madame Schontz of Maxime’s impending visit; she prepared, knowing his reputation promised both privilege and challenge.

The next day Maxime de Trailles summoned Finot to arrange a breakfast at the Café Anglais with Couture and Lousteau to discuss Madame Schontz. Pretending to deal with du Tillet, he waited for the Comte de la Palferine, questioned him about debts, admired his spirit, and offered a loan of twenty thousand francs to bankroll a courtship of a well-bred marquise. He urged La Palferine to woo sincerely and they agreed to meet later at Madame Schontz. Maxime also visited the duchess, who fretted over Sabine’s distress at Calyste’s absence, asked her to keep his plans secret, provided funds, and assured the campaign would not be extravagant.

Madame Schontz prepared a dinner, insisting she was an artist seeking a respectable marriage. Maxime praised her success with Rochefide, privately gave La Palferine the promised funds and advice, and schemed with Madame Schontz to manipulate Rochefide; he vowed to secure a post for Couture in return.

Meanwhile Calyste, feeling neglected, confronted Beatrix, who was torn between genuine feeling for him and attraction to La Palferine. Maxime orchestrated encounters that deepened Beatrix’s entanglement; Calyste’s attempts to reclaim her failed, culminating in her public humiliation at the opera. As Beatrix drifts into La Palferine’s orbit, loyalties shift and Maxime counsels Calyste to reconsider his tactics. The episode examines love, betrayal, ambition, and the disillusionment born of manipulation.
