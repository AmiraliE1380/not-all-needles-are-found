The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration ideal: religious devotion combined with worldly elegance. Married seven years to a bland, politically useful Marquis, she maintains a virtuous façade to support his prospects; acquaintances reserve judgment until he attains the peerage, yet she remains strictly principled. The marquis is dull—a silent deputy who votes correctly but lacks charisma—while she commands respect and sometimes disdain: she rebuffs flirtations, converses with agreeable men without inciting gossip, and is genuinely principled rather than coy.

The narrator, an admirer, sketches her as neither plain nor classically pretty but possessing a captivating presence—animated eyes suggesting depth and effortless charm. This emerged in a meeting with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever, modestly vivacious young man. At a ball he spoke with her about opera and women’s duties in a mutually interested exchange, then abruptly left. The next morning Rastignac, distracted, imagined romantic pleasures and wrote letters until his friend Horace arrived to discuss an expedition to Morea. Meanwhile the Marquise received Rastignac’s affectionate letter; intrigued at first, she read on and grew fatigued by his impertinence and implications, foreshadowing tension between her virtuous exterior and a stirring emotion.

Madame de Listomere forbade her porter to admit Rastignac after finding his insolence intolerable; she had considered keeping the letter but burned it. Caroline told the housemaid, surprised: “Madame received a fine love‑letter.” That evening the marquise went to the Marquis de Beauseant’s party expecting Rastignac; by two in the morning she realized he would not come. Stendhal called the hardening of her resentment “crystallization.”

Four days later Rastignac scolded his valet Joseph for misplacing letters; Joseph recalled one was for Madame de Listomere and another for Rastignac’s lawyer. When Rastignac learned the misplaced letter was not for Madame de Nucingen he laughed, aware his affections had wandered. His trouble began with a foolish attempt to amuse Madame de Listomere by discussing the blunder and worsened when he failed to visit for several days, letting her displeasure fester.

When he finally called, the porter said she was out but the marquis, unaware of the tension, invited him in. On the stairs Rastignac sensed social awkwardness. The marquise blushed at his appearance; he adjusted his cravat, thinking, “What! that fortress, too?” The marquis remained absorbed in his newspaper. Asked about her health, she said she was well; Eugène suggested his visit might be unwelcome because of the marquis’s intervention, and she hinted her husband did not share all her secrets.

Eugène tried to ease the mood; she promised to forget the incident while keeping a dignified reserve. When he mentioned the letter she feigned offense but could not hide amusement; he insisted it was not meant for her. The marquis interrupted, and an exchange about Madame de Nucingen shifted her demeanor: she rang for wood to signal his departure and coldly confronted him about how his pen could have written her name. Embarrassed, he left. Days later she confirmed his claim about the true recipient and then withdrew from society; the marquis blamed a stomach ailment while her physician diagnosed a nervous condition. The episode highlights social misunderstandings, emotional reactions, and rigid romantic roles.
