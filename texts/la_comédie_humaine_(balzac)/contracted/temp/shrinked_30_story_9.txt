The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration ideal: sincere religious devotion paired with worldly elegance. Seven years into a marriage with a bland but politically useful marquis, she preserves a virtuous façade to support his ambitions; acquaintances reserve judgment until he attains the peerage. Her respectable, sometimes scornful reserve rebuffs flirtations, sustains guarded conversation, and signals principle rather than coquetry.

The narrator admires her: not classically beautiful but possessing a captivating presence—animated eyes and effortless charm. This charm surfaces in her encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever, moderately vivacious young man. At a ball they talk of opera and women’s duties; he abruptly leaves. The next morning Rastignac, distracted and writing letters, imagines pleasures until his friend Horace plans an expedition. Meanwhile the Marquise reads one of Rastignac’s affectionate letters; initially intrigued, she grows offended by its impertinence and implications, a clash between outward virtue and a stirred feeling. She forbids her porter to admit Rastignac and burns the letter; Caroline, the housemaid, comments on the fine love‑letter. That evening she attends the Marquis de Beauseant’s party expecting Rastignac; by two a.m. she realizes he will not come—Stendhal calls this hardening of resentment “crystallization.”

Four days later, when Rastignac scolds his valet Joseph for misplacing letters, he learns one was for the Marquise and another for a lawyer. Aware his affections have wandered, he laughs; his trouble began when he tried to amuse her with the blunder and worsened by failing to visit. When he finally calls the marquis, unaware, admits him. The meeting is awkward: the Marquise blushes, gives guarded answers, promises to forget while remaining dignified; mention of the letter provokes feigned offense and hidden amusement, and the name Madame de Nucingen hardens her until she rings for wood to signal his departure. Embarrassed, he leaves. Days later she confirms the letter’s true recipient and withdraws from society; the marquis blames a stomach ailment, her physician calls it nervousness. The episode exposes social misunderstandings, intense but constrained feeling, and rigid romantic roles.
