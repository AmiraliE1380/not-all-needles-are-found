The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration's mix of religious devotion and worldly elegance. Married for seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she upholds a virtuous image to support his political ambitions. Known as "dull weather," the marquis provides a stable home life that aligns with her values.

Despite her reserved demeanor, the Marquise commands respect and skillfully deflects flirtatious advances, preserving her dignity. Her beauty is subtle, revealing depth when animated. Recently, she danced with Eugène de Rastignac, a charming young man whose views on women's roles left a lasting impression. Although captivated, Rastignac chose not to pursue her further that evening.

The next morning, Rastignac reflected on the Marquise while writing letters, interrupted by his friend Horace discussing an expedition. Meanwhile, the Marquise received a letter from Rastignac, which intrigued her despite her initial impulse to discard it, highlighting her internal conflict between societal expectations and personal desires.

She resolved to forbid her porter from admitting Rastignac, showing disdain for his insolence. Although she considered keeping the love letter, she ultimately burned it, surprising her maid, Caroline. At a party hosted by the Marquis de Beauseant, she anticipated Rastignac's arrival but felt disappointment when he did not come.

Days later, Rastignac learned he had mistakenly sent a love letter meant for another to Madame de Listomere. When he finally visited, the marquis invited him in, and Rastignac noticed the Marquise blush, flattering his vanity. Their conversation revealed tensions, with Madame de Listomere expressing skepticism about his intentions. Later, she withdrew from society, claiming illness, while the marquis attributed her absence to stomach issues, masking her nervous attack and highlighting romantic misunderstandings.
