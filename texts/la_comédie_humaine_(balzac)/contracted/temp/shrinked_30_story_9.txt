The Marquise de Listomere personifies the Restoration ideal: sincere religious devotion married to worldly elegance. Seven years into a marriage with the bland Marquis—useful to his political ambitions but lacking charm—she preserves a virtuous façade to support his prospects; acquaintances reserve judgment until he attains the peerage. The marquis is dull—more fault than virtue—a silent deputy who votes correctly but elicits little admiration, a fitting match for a woman of restraint. Her reserve commands respect and sometimes contempt: she rebuffs flirtations, converses with agreeable men without sparking gossip, and is genuinely principled rather than coquettish.

The narrator, an admirer, sketches her as neither plain nor classically pretty but possessing a captivating presence: animated eyes suggesting depth and effortless charm. This charm surfaced in a meeting with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever, modestly vivacious young man. At a ball they spoke about opera and women’s duties—an interested exchange cut short when he abruptly left. Next morning Rastignac, distracted, imagines romantic pleasures while writing letters until his friend Horace arrives to discuss an expedition to Morea. Meanwhile the Marquise receives one of Rastignac’s affectionate letters; intrigued at first, she reads on and grows weary of his impertinent tone and implications, foreshadowing tension between her virtuous exterior and a stirring emotion.

Offended, Madame de Listomere forbids her porter to admit Rastignac and burns the letter. Caroline tells the housemaid, surprised: “Madame received a fine love‑letter.” That evening the marquise goes to the Marquis de Beauseant’s party expecting Rastignac; by two in the morning she realizes he will not come. Stendhal calls the hardening of her resentment “crystallization.”

Four days later Rastignac scolds his valet Joseph for misplacing letters; Joseph recalls one was for Madame de Listomere and another for Rastignac’s lawyer. When Rastignac learns the misplaced letter was not for Madame de Nucingen he laughs, conscious that his affections have wandered. His trouble began by trying to amuse the marquise with talk of the blunder and worsened when he failed to visit, letting her displeasure fester.

When he finally calls, the porter says she is out, but the marquis—unaware of the tension—invites him in. On the stairs Rastignac senses awkwardness. The marquise, blushing at his appearance, gives him guarded answers; he adjusts his cravat, inwardly chafing at the barrier between them. Eugene tries to ease the mood; she promises to forget the incident while maintaining dignified reserve. When he mentions the letter she feigns offense but cannot hide amusement; he insists it was not meant for her. At the mention of Madame de Nucingen her demeanor hardens: she rings for wood to signal his departure and coldly confronts him about how his pen could have written her name. Embarrassed, he leaves. Days later she confirms the true recipient and withdraws from society; the marquis blames a stomach ailment while her physician diagnoses nervousness. The episode highlights social misunderstandings, intense but constrained feeling, and rigid romantic roles.
