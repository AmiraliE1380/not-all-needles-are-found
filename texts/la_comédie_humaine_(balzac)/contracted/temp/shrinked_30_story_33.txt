DEDICATION
To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

CHAPTER I
The commercial traveller is a modern force, shifting enterprise into intellectual influence: tying local power to markets, equalizing goods, and exporting Parisian ideas. Self-taught, he reads people, gauges solvency, discerns vice and virtue. Persuasive and adaptable, he often succeeds where ambassadors fail, negotiating deals in taverns and importing innovations. Gaudissart is this type: an acrobat of commerce who extracts provincial wealth with a torrent of phrases. Disarming in a striped shirt, he made his fortune selling hats, winning vendors and society alike. Ever festive yet businesslike, he tailors pitches to each audience. After 1830 made ideas marketable, writers and speculators thrived; Gaudissart adapted, selling life insurance and the profitable Children's Journal.

CHAPTER II
In April, after a week of Saint-Simonism, he set out despite offers to return to hats, joking with Jenny the florist about furnishing her room from his profits as he stopped in Blois.

CHAPTER III
In Vouvray townspeople’s wit and hostility thwarted him. Monsieur Vernier mocked travellers and sent him to Margaritis, a madman obsessed with wine and newspapers; Margaritis’s absurd answers and the amused crowd undermined every pitch.

CHAPTERS IV–V
Insulted, Gaudissart demanded satisfaction; Vernier refused and a duel loomed until the innkeeper arranged a breakfast reconciliation. They patched things up and Gaudissart left for Paris, reminded that eloquence does not always overcome discernment.
