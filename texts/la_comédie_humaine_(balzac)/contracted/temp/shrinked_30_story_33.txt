DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller, a modern figure, represents the shift from mere material enterprise to intellectual influence: he ties local power to universal markets, equalizes goods, and carries Parisian ideas to the provinces. Self-taught and observant, he reads people at a glance, judges habits and solvency, and must know where vice and virtue hide. Persuasive and adaptable, he often achieves by talk what ambassadors cannot, negotiating deals in taverns and importing metropolitan innovations.

Gaudissart exemplifies this type: an acrobat of commerce who extracts provincial wealth with a torrent of phrases. Notable in an olive-green overcoat and striped shirt, he is disarming, charming, and expert at courting attention. After making his fortune selling hats—winning vendors and social favor—he remains festive yet businesslike, tailoring his pitch to each audience.

After the Revolution of 1830 turned ideas into commodities, writers sold thought, speculators followed, and Gaudissart adapted. He took a commission selling life insurance, learned the trade, earned praise, and also sold newspaper subscriptions. Confident in his talents, he agreed to market both insurance and the profitable "Children’s Journal."

CHAPTER II

After a week immersed in Saint-Simonism and finance, Gaudissart prepared to travel in mid-April. Tempting offers to return to hats tried to lure him, but he left, joking with Jenny the florist about furnishing her room from his profits. She doubted his boasts; he confidently promised handsome earnings from insurance and the Journal, measuring himself against successful politicians and merchants. He set off for his assigned towns, stopping in Blois and writing home, boasting of sales.

CHAPTER III

In Vouvray Gaudissart met resistance. The Tourangians were witty, satirical, and hostile to Saint-Simonism. At the Soleil d’Or inn he encountered Monsieur Vernier, skeptical of commercial travellers and determined to humiliate him. Vernier sent Gaudissart to Margaritis, a reputed lunatic.

Margaritis, obsessed with wine and newspapers, produced absurd replies that undercut Gaudissart’s explanations of life insurance. The madman’s eccentricities and the watching townspeople thwarted every tactic.

CHAPTERS IV–V

Seated at home with wine, Margaritis ranted while two women listened amused and Vernier watched for spectacle. When the innkeeper revealed Vernier had engineered the encounter, Gaudissart, enraged at the insult, demanded satisfaction. The townspeople enjoyed the scene; Vernier’s refusal escalated toward a duel until the innkeeper suggested mediation over breakfast. They patched up the quarrel—acknowledging its absurdity—and Gaudissart left Vouvray, resolved to return to Paris and his usual work, reminded that eloquence alone cannot overcome discernment about when persuasion is futile.
