DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller, a modern figure, signals the shift from material enterprise to intellectual force: he links isolated local power to universal influence, equalizing products and spreading metropolitan ideas into the provinces. He brings the capital’s light to the countryside, a self-taught scholar and actor who knows the world superficially, a bridge between village and metropolis. Skilled at mimicry and rapid judgment, he must read habits and solvency quickly.

Travel taught him the haunts of vice and virtue across France; he persuades with polished speech, seizes opportunities, and outmaneuvers rivals. Abroad such agents often show truer diplomacy than ambassadors, negotiating commercial treaties in taverns rather than salons. The commercial traveller personifies civilization’s spirit, importing Parisian innovations into provincial life by sheer persuasiveness.

One such traveller is Gaudissart: an acrobat in commerce, extracting wealth from the provinces with a torrent of phrases. His olive-green overcoat and striped shirt mark his trade. Persuasive, disarming, adept at courting attention, he made his fortune in hats, enchanting vendors and winning social favor. Jovial and festive but businesslike when needed, he adapts his diplomacy to each audience.

Until 1830 Gaudissart stuck to hats; the upheaval that year turned ideas into commodities. Writers and thinkers began selling thought and speculators followed. Gaudissart seized the change, taking a commission on life-insurance policies and quickly learning the business. His employers praised him; newspapers sought him to sell subscriptions. Confident in his talents, he agreed to market both life insurance and periodicals.

CHAPTER II

After a week steeped in Saint-Simonism and financial talk, Gaudissart prepared to travel in mid-April. Two hat houses tempted him back with good commissions, but he set off, joking with Jenny, a florist, about refurnishing her room from his travels. She doubted such promises, prompting playful banter about his prospects. Gaudissart assured her he would earn a large income from insurance and subscriptions, measuring himself against successful politicians and merchants. The next morning he left for his assigned towns, stopping in Blois and writing to Jenny, boasting of selling life insurance and, especially, the profitable "Children’s Journal."

CHAPTER III

Arriving in Vouvray, Gaudissart expected his persuasive gifts to prevail but met a provincial mindset resistant to new ideas. The Tourangians were witty, satirical, and hostile to Saint-Simonism. At the Soleil d’Or inn he encountered Monsieur Vernier, skeptical of commercial travellers and aware of Gaudissart’s reputation. Vernier decided to humiliate him and sent Gaudissart to Margaritis, a local reputed lunatic.

Margaritis, obsessed with his wine and newspapers, began a conversation that quickly turned absurd. Despite Gaudissart’s efforts to explain life insurance, the man’s eccentricities produced nonsensical replies. Gaudissart at first kept his composure, trying to make sense of the madman’s chatter and to sell his product.

CHAPTER IV

Seated at home with wine, Margaritis spoke delusions while two women listened amused and Vernier watched, intent on making a spectacle. The lunatic’s erratic behavior—his obsession with wine, newspapers, and peculiar views—thwarted Gaudissart’s reasoning. The talk devolved into misunderstandings and incoherent exchanges. Gaudissart persisted, but the unpredictability and mockery made success impossible.

CHAPTER V

After prolonged banter, the innkeeper revealed Margaritis was indeed mad and Vernier had engineered the encounter. Enraged at the insult, Gaudissart demanded satisfaction. The townspeople relished the scene; Vernier’s refusal to apologize escalated toward a duel until the innkeeper proposed mediation over breakfast. They patched up the quarrel—recognizing its absurdity—and Gaudissart left Vouvray resolved to return to Paris and his usual work, reminded that commerce needs not only eloquence but discernment about when persuasion is futile.
