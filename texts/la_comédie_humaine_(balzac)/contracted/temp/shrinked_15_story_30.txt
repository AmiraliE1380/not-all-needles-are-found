DEDICATION

To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author
of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron,--You have shown so much interest in my extensive "History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century" and given me such encouragement that you have a right to be associated with a portion of it. You are one of the most conscientious, studious representatives of Germany; your approval will win others and protect this attempt of mine. I have sought to deserve your good opinion by continuing my labors with unflagging courage and documentary thoroughness like your own exhaustive work. Your sympathy with such labor as you have bestowed upon Eastern civilization often sustained me through nights of toil on modern civilization. May this token of my respect find you at Döbling and remind you of one of your most sincere admirers and friends.

DE BALZAC.

There stands a house at a corner of a street in a small French prefecture; the names of street and town must be suppressed. The house, called the Hotel d’Esgrignon, is only an ordinary dwelling but received that name in jest and at length in earnest. Let d’Esgrignon be deemed a fiction-name like those used on stage or in romance; the principal characters’ names will be disguised, though truth will assert itself despite the chronicler’s wish to conceal it.

The Hotel d’Esgrignon was the town house of the old Marquis, Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol, Marquis d’Esgrignon. The Carol name was glorious among Norman families who established the feudal system; theirs was a line of provincial nobles, unbending to King, Commune, Church, or Finance. Their marquisate had been a real post of honor with duties; their fief had always been their domain. For thirteen hundred years the family kept itself to rigid succession: daughters married without dowries or entered convents, younger sons sought military or ecclesiastical careers, and cadets sometimes rose at court. The elder branch would never accept a ducal title.

At the Revolution the d’Esgrignons nearly lost all. The Marquis of 1789 would not emigrate; his character and the countryside’s reverence saved his life though he was forced to hide briefly. In the name of the Sovereign People the lands were dishonored and woods sold piece by piece. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, the Marquis’s half-sister, saved portions of the fief thanks to Maitre Chesnel, the young steward, who claimed for her the partage de présuccession. Chesnel bought in his own name certain properties dear to the Marquis, advancing the money.

In October 1800 Chesnel brought the Marquis back to the ruined feudal castle. The patron stood in the empty courtyard, grieving at the filled moat, razed towers, and the loss of Gothic features. He spoke to heaven as he viewed the ruin and declared they would not live there until pacification allowed him to set his scutcheon on the wall. They returned to town, for the Hotel d’Esgrignon in the square—a former courthouse and presidial, long belonging to the family—had been bought by Chesnel for five hundred louis d'or and half in jest was named the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

By 1800 some émigrés were returning. The Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, ruined, accepted the Marquis’s hospitality; the Baron died there. Mlle. de Nouastre, a beautiful girl of twenty-two, married the Marquis to continue his line but died in childbirth due to a careless physician, leaving a son to bear the name. The grief of the Marquis—already fifty-three in spirit—was profound; he kissed his dead wife, broke his watch, and hung it above the hearth at eleven o’clock, recalling other family deaths at that hour. He combined Frankish stubbornness with Christian fortitude in his grief.

In the early 1800s an ex-contractor for army forage, a local man with six thousand francs, persuaded Chesnel to propose marriage to Mlle. Armande d’Esgrignon. The Marquis and his sister resented such presumption; Chesnel regretted his yielding. Thenceforth the Marquis’s manner toward Chesnel changed: gratitude now seemed to wound the notary, who had once been more than servant—almost part of the family. Chesnel had been raised by intimacy with the house; he felt his error bitterly and developed aversion for the unsuccessful suitor, the Sieur du Croisier. Du Croisier, mortified and scorned, nursed a lasting hatred for Chesnel and the d’Esgrignons.

In 1805 M. de la Roche-Guyon, of an old family allied by intermarriage to the d’Esgrignons, proposed formally for Mlle. Marie Armande Clair d’Esgrignon through Chesnel. She refused, saying she was a mother—she had just put her nephew Victurnien to bed. The Marquis rose and, after kissing his sister’s hand, remarked, “My sister, you are a d’Esgrignon.” Armande, whose birth had been from a second marriage of lesser rank, was deeply touched by his recognition. She answered simply that she would die Mlle. d’Esgrignon; that title was fairer to her. Her devotion to her brother and the family had been complete for years.

Emile Blondet, narrator of much of this history, recalls his childhood admiration for Mlle. Armande. Even as a boy, he found in her a thrilling presence. Her tawny-gold hair, delicate complexion, dreamy green eyes, grace of figure, and saintly dignity impressed him as something like a vision. He played near her, adored her feet and her touch, and carried an idealized image of her into later life. That childhood veneration made her for him a type of feudal ladylike grandeur and reverence. Mlle. Armande’s character is a key figure in the story—a model of goodness whose lack of worldly intelligence brings harm despite the purity of her heart.

Two-thirds of émigrés returned in 1804–05. Many entered Napoleon’s service and prospered; some stayed in Paris. Yet eight or nine families remained faithful to the proscribed noblesse and the fallen monarchy. The La Roche-Guyons, Nouastres, Verneuils, Casterans, Troisvilles and others formed an antiquarian, pedigree-obsessed circle over which Marquis d’Esgrignon presided. There the Emperor was “M. de Bonaparte” and “the King” meant Louis XVIII. Their provincial salon was called the Collection of Antiquities, sometimes derided by the Liberals as a fossilized Faubourg Saint-Germain. The Marquis’s dignity and conduct commanded respect: he was generous, inflexible in his loyalty to monarchy, and a man of arbitration to whom litigants referred disputes.

Those excluded from this circle—country bourgeois, upstarts, and the rising Liberal party—resented the nobles. Du Croisier, who had been rebuffed, married an old maid he had initially been refused by, which intensified his hatred and prolonged local factional war. The prefect, seeking favor, sent his wife as ambassadress to the d’Esgrignon salon, but many mocked the nobles, calling the house “The Collection of Antiquities” and the Marquis “M. Carol,” mocking old orthography. Provincial politics, however, were not mere theoretical debate: in small towns opinions became personal hatreds. Parties identified with individuals; slander and epigram were weapons. The nobles were witty and maintained ceremony, but the Liberals’ grievances festered and bred revenge.

The hotel’s salon sat above former prison cells, its great hearth and carved panelling, Flemish tapestries, and Louis XIV furniture collected from sales of wrecked châteaux. Around its tables sat dowagers—aged women in fantastical, out-of-fashion dress—whose gaunt, automaton movements haunted the young Blondet. Their painted, furrowed faces and stiff bodies seemed more like theatrical relics than living persons. The men were paler echoes of the same decayed aristocracy. The certainty of these figures’ regular presence gave them an unearthly theatrical quality: the salon was a glass cage open to public view.

The disasters of 1813–14 revived hopes among the nobles; Napoleon’s fall and the Restoration rekindled expectations, though the events of 1815 and foreign occupation delayed them. In 1822 things had not improved much for the Marquis. His family had been hardest hit among great nobles by Revolutionary legislation. Before 1789 they drew most income from seigneurial rights—dues, quit-rents, heriots—and had reduced peasant holdings to swell rents. After the Revolution those suppressed rights could not be fully restored; indemnity laws did not recompense everyone. A tax called domaines revived some suppressed privileges only by sending proceeds to the State.

Politically, the Marquis belonged to the uncompromising Royalists—the Ultras—who refused cooperation with Liberals or Constitutionnels. They saw the Charter as an ill-advised concession that ought to be annulled. To them, indemnities and compromises were intolerable. The Marquis, then sixty-seven, had his convictions hardened by age and experience; he remained lord-paramount of his salon, the Collection of Antiquities.

But the old nickname became a taunt. The town’s new Liberal element, represented by du Croisier, took for their camp the manufacturing and commercial interests. Du Croisier, who had worked as a forage-contractor and then entrenched himself among provincial capitalists and officials, stood for the energetic, younger society who sought influence. Although he did not always sit on the extreme Left, he associated with Liberal deputies and bankers like the Keller brothers, keeping political ties that might gain him support. The Liberals' salon grew larger and more dynamic, equal in power to the d’Esgrignon salon and influential across the countryside. The nobility’s circle remained aloof and often embarrassed authorities by its pressure on government, contributing to political blunders.

Elections seldom favored du Croisier: the department distrusted him as insufficiently Royalist, and Royalists thwarted his candidacy. Each defeat deepened his bitterness. Provincial hatreds sharpened: once united by Napoleonic rule, aristocrats and bourgeois now separated into bitter antagonists. Vanity, a national sentiment in France, made equality a rallying cry for the wounded many, while the nobles pricked sore spots among opponents in turn. In provincial life political strife became personal combat; opponents lived like duelists, waiting to wound one another’s reputation.

By 1822 a temporary lull concealed the brewing passion of revenge in du Croisier, who chose a cold, patient demeanor, smiling while he watched and plotted. His ally in local machinations was M. du Ronceret, President of the Tribunal, who had also sought admittance to the aristocratic circle and failed. The d’Esgrignon household, managed with care by Chesnel, lived as nobles without ostentation but with due ceremony. The household expenses, the salary of an elderly Oratorian tutor for the heir Victurnien, a cook, servants, and the cost of the boy’s education consumed most of the limited income despite Armande’s thrift and Chesnel’s management. Chesnel had not been able to restore the ruined castle; he waited until current leases expired so he might raise rents and benefit from improved agriculture and currency changes before undertaking repairs.

Thus in 1822 the Hotel d’Esgrignon remained at once a refuge of old dignity and a beleaguered symbol of a past order, watched with envy and malice by a rising class determined to take its revenge by political and social means.

The Marquis knew nothing of the household’s management or finances. Nearing the end of his life, he and his circle clung to the illusion that the d’Esgrignon house, ignored at Court and by government, would revive in his handsome heir, Victurnien—who would go to Court, enter royal service, and marry into some illustrious family. His intimates—the Troisvilles, La Roche-Guyon, the Casterans, and the Duc de Verneuil—encouraged him. Their faith might have seemed plausible if forty years of history could be erased; but the restored monarchy and public opinion demanded not only noble birth but money, political relevance, and uninterrupted prestige. The d’Esgrignons lacked continuity and the modern resources that mattered; their old renown could not substitute for wealth and influence. The Troisvilles’ daughter’s marriage to General Montcornet, instead of flattering antiquated pride, nearly split social circles—showing how times had changed.

Only Chesnel, the family’s old notary and former steward, saw clearly. Devoted but practical, he had witnessed the Revolution’s violence and its quieter triumph in new social ideas and vested interests. He understood that the deaths and confiscations of the Revolution had become binding facts and that the new order would not be overturned; his attachment to the d’Esgrignons was therefore affectionate and clear-sighted. He tried to warn the Marquis tactfully; the Marquis met these warnings with a prophet’s smile and the conviction that history would repeat in their favor. Chesnel feared the damage the family’s superstitions and self-delusions would do to Victurnien’s mind and habits.

Idolized by his father and aunt, Victurnien was thoroughly spoiled yet justified their hopes in many ways. Mlle. Armande, his aunt, loved him as a mother might but lacked maternal intuition and severity; she could not foresee danger as a real mother could. Chesnel’s pragmatic vigilance resembled a mother’s instinct more closely than the aunt’s blind adoration, but the household generally paid little heed to his counsels—he had even fallen into a kind of disgrace after proposing an imprudent marriage for the family.

Victurnien was taught from infancy to assume his superiority: peers among great nobles, the King as his only superior, and the rest of mankind as inferiors. With beauty, tact, a quick mind, and thorough education, he had every advantage. He was of average height, graceful, an excellent horseman and marksman, and intellectually promising—qualities that might have made him a great man. But indulged and unopposed in youth, he became insolent, audacious, and self-willed. The Chevalier, a charming old roue, misled the boy by glorifying the reckless manners and witty immorality of an earlier era, and the Chevalier’s jocular maxims mingled with the family’s rigid emphasis on honor. Thus good and bad counsel shaped Victurnien.

At eighteen, Victurnien entered society and quickly misread appearances. Accustomed to deference at home and to unguarded talk in the presence of an elderly father, he did not see the hypocrisies and practical cautions of the larger world. His reckless behavior—shooting over neighbors’ lands, amorous imprudences requiring settlements and legal cover-ups—led to lawsuits and scandals quietly hushed by Chesnel, who paid to avert public shame. Encouraged by adroit enemies—particularly Du Croisier, who plotted revenge against the family—Victurnien’s faults were fanned. President du Ronceret and other officials, while outwardly lenient, fostered liberal clamor and public suspicion to discredit the nobles; du Croisier hoped to drive the d’Esgrignons into ruin through the young man’s vices. A young instigator, Fabien du Ronceret, befriended Victurnien to lead him on.

Between eighteen and twenty-one, Victurnien ran up considerable debts—nearly eighty thousand francs paid out by Chesnel, who hid this from Mlle. Armande and the Marquis. The family’s mode of life left little margin: the Marquis’s income barely covered the household. Victurnien’s tailor bills, tastes for English saddles and carriages, livery, and fashionable accessories swallowed his allowance. Chesnel, with private savings of about twelve thousand livres yearly, had been the young man’s secret benefactor, paying off lawsuits and bailing him out repeatedly. Chesnel hoped to see Victurnien settle into a sensible marriage, but the young man’s temperament—capable of lofty thought yet habitually surrendering to impulse and pleasure—made him dangerously likely to repeat mistakes.

Chesnel’s patience and warnings met with shrugging complacency from the Chevalier and indulgence from the family. The Chevalier dismissed debt as par for a gentleman’s course, recounted extravagant examples of aristocratic generosity, and minimized the danger. Chesnel, however, foresaw ruin. His fear and affection prompted him to plan a decisive measure: to have Victurnien sent to Paris and presented at court, hoping the change would sober the boy and provide opportunities befitting his rank.

One autumn evening in 1822, after cards and salon talk, the household confronted the truth about Victurnien’s debts. The Chevalier forced the disclosure to the Marquis: Chesnel had lent an enormous sum—“something like a hundred thousand livres”—to the Count. The Marquis was mortified that his devoted steward would be the family’s creditor; pride and the old notions of dignity made it painful to ask Chesnel for help in a straightforward way. Nevertheless, they resolved to send Victurnien to Paris to present himself to the King and to secure an appointment for him; they accepted Chesnel’s loan rather than humiliate the family by other means.

Chesnel explained that the Count’s companions and enemies in town—the president, Du Croisier and his circle—were seeking vengeance by ensnaring Victurnien, provoking scandals to ruin the family. He had, accordingly, borrowed money himself, mortgaging Le Jard, and had made arrangements in confidence so the loan would not appear as a direct family shame. Mlle. Armande and the Marquis were moved; Chesnel kissed her hand and, with paternal tenderness, urged that Paris provide the boy with more amusement and distraction than provincial society could, while also placing him where discretion and better company might discipline him.

Preparations were hurried. The Marquis and Mlle. Armande instructed Victurnien that he was to go to court, serve His Majesty, and there ascertain a career—army, navy, diplomacy, or royal household. They promised a modest provision and sent Josephin, Victurnien’s trusted valet, with him as companion. Chesnel, meanwhile, wrote to an old notary friend in Paris, Maître Sorbier, begging vigilant oversight of the young man’s conduct and intimating funds would be placed at Sorbier’s disposition to support him. Chesnel’s letter revealed his deep emotional tie to the family: he felt he had reared and protected the d’Esgrignons through revolution and decline, and that the young Count’s fate was intimately his concern.

Victurnien accepted a monthly allowance of two thousand francs and left amid the household’s blessings. He felt no genuine regret; provincial life had been gentle but narrow, and Paris promised to amplify his vanity and gratify his tastes. In the capital he discovered the city’s scale and the fact that noble names no longer guaranteed power or employment; the Court’s attention was limited, and new social forces and money mattered most. Undeterred, he took to luxury and fashion as necessary to command respect and assumed a posture of independence that hid need.

In Paris, he sought to recover a position he imagined due to his birth. He bought rooms, stables, and equerries, all at great expense. His family’s reputation and the Faubourg Saint-Germain’s curiosity opened many doors: old family names still worked as social passports. He found a circle of fashionable young men—de Marsay, Rastignac, de Ronquerolles, Maxime de Trailles, and others—who moved easily in the world he coveted and initiated him into its pleasures. The Vidame de Pamiers, a discreet source of gossip and introductions in the Faubourg, welcomed Victurnien and took him to salons and dinners where he met writers and courtiers, and where the Vidame encouraged him toward conquest and social display.

At a dinner with the Vidame, de Marsay, Rastignac, and the young writer Emile Blondet, Victurnien encountered the pleasures and perils of Parisian society. The Vidame introduced him to Mlle. des Touches’ salon and to fashionable circles where literature and wit mixed with vanity and intrigue. The Vidame, who kept a finger on public taste, predicted the young Count’s rapid rise—and fall—if he pursued such extremes.

Victurnien was introduced to the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, a dazzling and complex beauty who immediately attracted him and whom others envied. The Duchesse presented herself as an ethereal, virginal figure—an affectation that masked cleverness and self-regard. She resolved to play an innocence that would grant her influence, a role she adopted to conceal and manipulate her passions. Her carefully constructed purity, full of artful glances and guarded gestures, made her all the more alluring; she used the appearance of sanctity to amplify the power of her charm.

Thus Victurnien found himself at the center of fashionable life. His family’s name, his beauty, and the patronage of the Faubourg made him a desirable figure; his own vanity made him determined to sustain an image of wealth and consequence. Chesnel’s precautions were circumvented by the mechanics of Parisian finance and the goodwill—or opportunism—of notaries eager for patronage. The young Count’s expenses mounted: his lodgings, dress, horses, and the cost of entering high society quickly devoured the money set aside. Despite Chesnel’s hopes that Paris would rescue him from provincial follies, the city offered new temptations, new alliances, and new risks that promised either brilliant social ascent or irreversible ruin.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs, a fact unknown to Chesnel and Mlle. Armande. Victurnien had also siphoned two thousand crowns from Chesnel at intervals to conceal his sources. He sent flattering, deceitful letters to his poor father and aunt, who lived happily deceived. The insidious current of Paris life was preparing a dreadful catastrophe for the d’Esgrignon house; only du Croisier knew. He passed the Antiquities rubbing his hands, confident that he could bring not merely ruin but dishonor on the family. He had been sure of it since discovering that the young Count’s debts were becoming unmanageable.

Du Croisier’s first step was to eliminate his most hated enemy, the upright old notary Chesnel. Chesnel lived in the Rue du Bercail in a modest house with a paved courtyard and small garden. At half-past five he generally sat after dinner in his black leather armchair, his stockinged legs protected by a painted pasteboard contrivance before the fire; he often ate too much and took pleasure in thinking how his savings might round out the d’Esgrignon estates. All his affections were wrapped up in that single attachment; he took pride in rebuilding the house and hoped to be remembered by future d’Esgrignons.

His old housekeeper entered one evening in alarm. “M. du Croisier wanting to speak to you,” she said.

“Du Croisier!” The name struck Chesnel like a chill; he dropped the tongs. Du Croisier entered, bowed, sat, and produced a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, principal and interest, for sums advanced to Victurnien in bills of exchange drawn upon du Croisier and honored by him. He demanded immediate payment, threatening proceedings against the heir-presumptive. Chesnel turned over the unlucky letters and begged secrecy; du Croisier agreed to keep the matter quiet if paid within forty-eight hours. Pressed for money, Chesnel had obligingly advanced manufacturers and resorted to the usual financial fictions; his eyes were dim, tears near. The only way to raise the money was to mortgage his lands to their full value.

When du Croisier learned Chesnel could not repay, he suddenly offered to buy the old lawyer’s property. The sale was completed within two days. Poor Chesnel, who could not bear the thought of the heir of the house undergoing five years’ imprisonment for debt, was left with nothing but his practice, the sums due to him, and the house in which he lived. Stripped of his lands, he paced his oak-paneled office and thought no longer of Le Jard but of Victurnien.

“What will become of him? He ought to come back; they must marry him to some rich heiress,” he murmured, with dim eyes and a heavy head. He did not know how to break the news to Mlle. Armande; the man who had paid the family’s debts quaked at the thought. He went from the Rue du Bercail to the Hotel d’Esgrignon with pulses throbbing like a girl’s, fearing the ruin of illusions.

Mlle. Armande had just received a charming, hypocritical letter. Her nephew was the happiest man alive: he had been to baths, traveled in Italy with Mme. de Maufrigneuse, and sent a journal full of love—Venice, Milan, Florence, the Apennines—painting happiness everywhere. The poor aunt was enthralled. She saw the distant country of love, sat in Victurnien’s gondola, felt the Duchess’ hand in his; she drank in the letter. Repressed passion had ripened; she loved the couple ardently and excused a married woman’s love for Victurnien; to refuse it would have seemed unpardonable. Mlle. Armande was not an angelic type but a slender, serene figure; at this moment blue buds were unfolding in her saintly eyes. She loved them as apart from life.

Even in her Venetian reverie, someone appeared on the garden walk—Chesnel. The sound of his tread and the hopelessness on his face recalled her to herself.

“What is it?” she cried.

“All is lost!” said Chesnel. “M. le Comte will bring dishonor upon the house if we do not set it in order.” He held out the bills and described the recent agony in simple, touching words.

“He is deceiving us! The miserable boy!” cried Mlle. Armande, feeling her heart surge.

“Let us say mea culpa, mademoiselle,” Chesnel replied stoutly; “we have allowed him his own way; he needed stern guidance—he had no mother.” Fate sometimes deals terribly with a decaying noble house, she thought, with tears.

The Marquis came up; he had been reading the letter sent by his son from Italy. Victurnien’s itinerary was told from an aristocrat’s point of view: he had been magnificently welcomed by the great families, due partly to his name and perhaps the Duchess. The old Marquis, with a coxcomb’s air, recounted ancestral glories. The two companions in unhappiness sat together on a garden bench, saying little, watching the father walk away in his contentment.

“What will become of him now?” Mlle. Armande asked.

“Du Croisier has sent instructions to the Kellers; he is not to be allowed to draw any more without authorization.”

“And there are debts.”

“I am afraid so.”

“If he is left without resources, what will he do?”

“I dare not answer that.”

“But he must be drawn out of that life; he must come back to us, or he will have nothing left.”

“And nothing else,” Chesnel said gloomily. Mlle. Armande could not yet understand the full force of his words.

“Is there any hope of getting him away from that woman—the Duchess? Perhaps she leads him on.”

“He would not stick at a crime to be with her,” Chesnel tried to suggest, preparing her for an intolerable thought.

“Crime!” she cried. “Oh, Chesnel, no one but you would think of such a thing!” she added with a withering look. “There is but one crime a noble can commit—the crime of high treason; and when he is beheaded, the block is covered with a black cloth, as for kings.”

“The times have changed,” said Chesnel, shaking his head. Victurnien had thinned the last of his white hairs. That thought soothed Mlle. Armande’s indignation, but she did not yet grasp Chesnel’s warning.

“Tomorrow we will decide,” she said. “It needs thought. At worst, we have our lands.”

“Yes,” Chesnel replied. “You and M. le Marquis own the estate conjointly; the larger part is yours. You can raise money on it without saying a word to him.”

That evening Mlle. Armande’s agitation was noted at whist and other games. “That poor heroic child!” said the Marquise de Casteran; “she must be suffering still. A woman never knows what her sacrifices to her family may cost her.”

The next day it was arranged that Mlle. Armande should go to Paris to snatch her nephew from perdition. If anyone could carry him off, it was the woman whose motherly heart yearned over him. She determined to go to the Duchess and tell her all. A pretext was needed for the journey; for appearances Mlle. Armande allowed it to be thought she suffered from a complaint requiring consultation with celebrated physicians. Whether the town talked of this was of no consequence; far more than her reputation was at stake. She set out with Chesnel’s last bag of louis; she took it and her capuchine and mittens without comment. “Generous girl!” he said, putting her into the carriage with her maid.

Du Croisier had contrived his revenge as provincials do—meticulously, with every contingency provided for. Had he read Victurnien’s nature so well that he foresaw how readily the young Count would lend himself to his schemes? Or was he simply exploiting a long-watched opportunity? Who warned du Croisier of the moment—the Kellers, or President du Ronceret’s son finishing law studies in Paris? He wrote to Victurnien that the Kellers had been instructed to advance no more money; the letter was timed to arrive as the Duchess and the Count faced poverty cunningly hidden. In the letter informing Victurnien the Kellers would make no further advances without security, du Croisier left a wide blank space between the formal text and the signature. It was easy to tear off the upper part and convert the lower into a bill of exchange for any amount. The missive had been enclosed in an envelope so the other side of the sheet was blank. When it arrived, Victurnien was in the lowest despair. After two years of luxurious, thoughtless life, he found himself face to face with inexorable poverty: an absolute impossibility to procure money.

There had been throes before the journey’s end. With the Duchess’ help he had extorted various sums from bankers, but those very amounts were about to mature as overdue bills, with summonses from the Bank of France and the commercial court. In the last weeks he felt the point of a sword: at every supper-party a creeping warning that the sirocco of debt was near. For five years his purse had always been replenished. After Chesnel came du Croisier (he thought), then surely another gold-mine would pour out its wealth. Besides, he was winning at play; luck had saved him unpleasant steps before; often a wild hope sent him to the Salon des Étrangers only to lose the winnings at whist. His life had become like Don Giovanni’s immortal finale—disorder, confusion, a frantic struggle to shut out debts and duels. He lived through this infernal poem alone, seeing visions of a friendless outcast reading the last words on the last page—THE END!

Yes; for him all would be at an end and soon. He already imagined the ironical eyes of acquaintances amusing themselves over his downfall. Some played high and could not spare a banknote for an intimate. There was no help—Chesnel must be ruined; he had devoured Chesnel’s living.

He sat with the Duchess in their box at the Italiens, the house envying them, and while he smiled the Furies were tearing at his heart. To give some sense of his despair: he had even taken out pistols and thought of suicide. He who would never brook insult was abusing himself in language no man hears except from himself.

He left du Croisier’s letter open on the bed. Josephin had brought it at nine. Victurnien’s furniture had been seized, but he slept none the less. After the Opera he and the Duchess had gone to a voluptuous retreat where they often spent hours after brilliant court balls. Appearances were cleverly saved; their love-nest seemed a garret to all but was luxuriously arranged within. Now on the brink of ruin, he longed to bid farewell to the dainty nest. Adversity would break the enchanted eggs; there would be no brood of white doves, no brilliant tropical birds of fancy. In three days he must be gone; his bills had been placed in the hands of money-lenders, and law proceedings had reached the last stage.

An evil thought crossed him. He would flee with the Duchess to some undiscovered nook in America, but with a fortune, leaving creditors to face their bills. To carry out the plan he had only to cut off the lower portion of du Croisier’s letter bearing the signature and fill in figures to turn it into a bill, presenting it to the Kellers. There was a dreadful struggle with temptation; tears were shed; but the honor of the family triumphed—subject to one condition. Victurnien wanted to be sure of his beautiful Diane; he would do nothing unless she consented to their flight. So he went to the Duchess in the Rue Faubourg Saint-Honoré and found her in coquettish morning dress.

Mme. de Maufrigneuse was somewhat pensive. Cares gnawed at her mind, but she met them gallantly. Of all feminine types, some combine strength and insight with prompt decision and a resolution that would shake a man’s nerves; these powers lie beneath an appearance of graceful helplessness. Such women can ascend to the highest feelings or show selfish insensibility. The Duchess, pondering her fall, put love aside to consider her safety: refuge abroad, confessing to the King, fascinating a du Tillet or a Nucingen, or gambling on the stock exchange—all were reviewed calmly. She could stand aloof and watch the crash like a spectator instead of being buried beneath it. That power was great but repulsive.

While dressing for a drive she had collected her thoughts and faced the possibilities of terrific downfall. She plucked love out of her heart to consider necessities and was ready to replace the passion when her coronet was safe. She had none of Richelieu’s hesitation or Napoleon’s early doubts; she resolved.

Victurnien entered in a state of terror; his heart beat violently; he feared to touch the cornerstone that upheld his life with Diane. Men sometimes deceive themselves to avoid humiliation. Victurnien committed himself.

“What is the matter?” Diane asked.

“I am hopelessly in debt. I have come to the end.”

“Pshaw! it is nothing,” she said, smiling. “Money can always be arranged; nothing is irretrievable except disasters in love.”

Relieved by her quick comprehension, he unrolled the bright web of his past two years and a half—the seamy side—spiced with aristocratic scorn for men and things. She listened intently, her forehead calm, gaze fixed; the concentration of her attention seemed a divine love. When Victurnien proposed flight, he cried, “You are an angel!”

The Duchess made no immediate answer; her mind was absorbed in secret plans. “Good, very good,” she said at last. “But that is not the question, dear. Let us think of your affairs. Yes, we will go, and the sooner the better. Arrange it; I will follow. It is glorious to leave Paris and the world.”

When a duchess speaks such words the sacrifice is made. Diane’s promise settled him; he could conceal sordid details because she did not inquire. Victurnien would not go till the promise was sealed; he needed courage from happiness before doing a deed that might be badly construed. He counted on his aunt and father and Chesnel to hush up the affair; Chesnel might think of some compromise. Besides, this “business” was the only way to raise money on the family estate. With three hundred thousand francs he and Diane would lead a happy hidden life in Venice.

Next day Victurnien drew a bill for three hundred thousand francs and took it to the Kellers. They advanced the money, for du Croisier happened then to have a balance; but they wrote that he must not draw again without notice. Du Croisier, much astonished, asked for a statement of accounts. It was sent. Everything was explained. The day of his vengeance had arrived.

When Victurnien had drawn "his" money, he took it to Mme. de Maufrigneuse. She locked the banknotes in her desk and proposed to bid the world farewell by going to the Opera for the last time. Victurnien was thoughtful, absent, uneasy. He began to reflect. He thought his seat in the Duchess’ box might cost him dear; that perhaps, once the three hundred thousand francs were safe, it would be better to travel post, to fall at Chesnel’s feet, and tell him all. Before they left the opera-house, the Duchess gave Victurnien a glance; her eyes shone with the desire to go back once more to bid farewell to the nest she loved. And the boy lost a night.

The next day, at three o’clock, he was back again at the Hotel de Maufrigneuse; he had come to take the Duchess’ orders for that night’s escape. "Why should we go?" she asked. "I have thought it all out. The Vicomtesse de Beauseant and the Duchesse de Langeais disappeared. If I go too, it will be commonplace. We will brave the storm. It will be far finer. I am sure of success."

Victurnien’s eyes dazzled; he felt as if his skin were dissolving and blood oozing out all over him.

"What is the matter with you?" cried the fair Diane, noticing a hesitation which a woman never forgives. A truly adroit lover will hasten to agree with any fancy Woman may take, and suggest reasons for doing otherwise, while leaving her free to change her mind and intentions as often as she pleases. Victurnien was angry for the first time, angry with the wrath of a weak man of poetic temperament; it was a storm of rain and lightning, but no thunder followed. The angel on whose faith he had risked more than his life, the honor of his house, was very roughly handled.

"So," said she, "we have come to this after eighteen months of tenderness! You are unkind, very unkind. Go away! I do not want to see you again. I thought you loved me. You do not."

"I do not love you?" he repeated, thunderstruck.

"No, monsieur."

"And yet—" he cried. "Ah! if you but knew what I have just done for your sake!"

"And how have you done so much for me, monsieur? As if a man ought not to do anything for a woman who has done so much for him."

"You are not worthy to know it!" Victurnien cried in a passion of anger.

"Oh!"

After that sublime "Oh!" Diane bowed her head on her hand and sat, still, cold, implacable as angels may be expected to be, since they share none of humanity’s passions. At the sight of the woman he loved in this terrible attitude, Victurnien forgot his danger. Had he not wronged the most angelic creature on earth? He longed for forgiveness, threw himself before her, kissed her feet, pleaded, wept. Two whole hours the unhappy young man spent in folly, only to meet the same cold face, while great silent tears, dropping one by one, were dried as soon as they fell lest the unworthy lover should try to wipe them away. The Duchess was acting a great agony, one of those hours which stamp the woman who passes through them as august and sacred.

Two more hours went by. By this time the Count had gained possession of Diane’s hand; it felt cold and spiritless. The beautiful hand, with all its treasures, might have been supple wood; there was nothing of Diane in it; he had taken it, it had not been given. As for Victurnien, the spirit had ebbed from his frame, he had ceased to think. He would not have seen the sun in heaven. What was to be done? What course should he take? What resolution should he make? Victurnien sank into a bewildered stupor; darkness settled over his brain. Visions of past rapture flitted across the misty gloom like the figures Raphael painted against a black background; to these he must bid farewell. Inexorable and disdainful, the Duchess played with the tip of her scarf. She looked in irritation at Victurnien from time to time; she coquetted with memories, spoke to her lover of his rivals as if anger had finally decided her to prefer one of them to a man who could so change in a moment after twenty-eight months of love.

"Ah! that charming young Felix de Vandenesse, so faithful as he was to Mme. de Mortsauf, would never have permitted such a scene! He can love, can de Vandenesse! De Marsay, that terrible de Marsay, such a tiger as everyone thought him, was rough with other men; but like all strong men, he kept his gentleness for women. Montriveau trampled the Duchesse de Langeais under foot, as Othello killed Desdemona, in a burst of fury which at any rate proved the extravagance of his love. It was not like a paltry squabble. There was rapture in being so crushed. Little, fair-haired, slim men loved to torment women; they could only reign over poor, weak creatures; it pleased them to have some ground for believing they were men. The tyranny of love was their one chance of asserting power. She did not know why she had put herself at the mercy of fair hair. Such men as de Marsay, Montriveau, and Vandenesse, dark-haired and well grown, had a ray of sunlight in their eyes."

It was a storm of epigrams. Her speeches, like bullets, came hissing past his ears. Every word Diane hurled at him was triple-barbed; she humiliated, stung, wounded him with an art that was all her own, as half a score of savages can torture an enemy bound to a stake.

"You are mad!" he cried finally, at the end of his patience, and out he went in God knows what mood. He drove as if he had never handled reins, locked his wheels with other vehicles, collided with the curbstone in the Place Louis-Quinze, went he knew not whither. The horse, left to its own devices, bolted for the stable along the Quai d’Orsay; but as he turned into the Rue de l’Universite, Josephin appeared to stop the runaway.

"You cannot go home, sir," the old man said, with a scared face; "they have come with a warrant to arrest you."

Victurnien thought he had been arrested on the criminal charge, albeit there had not been time for the public prosecutor to receive instructions. He had forgotten the matter of the bills of exchange, which had for days past been stirred up in the form of orders to pay, brought by officers of the court with bailiffs, men in possession, magistrates, commissaries, policemen, and other representatives of social order. Like most guilty creatures, Victurnien had forgotten everything but his crime.

"It is all over with me," he cried.

"No, M. le Comte, drive as fast as you can to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine, in the Rue de Grenelle. Mlle. Armande is waiting there for you, the horses have been put in, she will take you with her."

Victurnien, in his trouble, caught like a drowning man at the branch that came to hand; he rushed to the inn, reached the place, and flung his arms about his aunt. Mlle. Armande cried as if her heart would break; one might have thought she had a share in her nephew’s guilt. They stepped into the carriage. A few minutes later they were on the road to Brest, and Paris lay behind them. Victurnien uttered not a sound; he was paralyzed. When aunt and nephew began to speak, they talked at cross purposes; Victurnien, still laboring under the unlucky misapprehension which flung him into Mlle. Armande’s arms, thought of his forgery; his aunt had debts and bills on her mind.

"You know all, aunt," he had said.

"Poor boy, yes, but we are here. I am not going to scold you just yet. Take heart."

"I must hide somewhere."

"Perhaps.... Yes, it is a very good idea."

"Perhaps I might get into Chesnel’s house without being seen if we timed our arrival to the middle of the night?"

"That will be best. We shall be better able to hide this from my brother. Poor angel! how unhappy he is!" said she, petting the unworthy child.

"Ah! now I begin to know what dishonor means; it has chilled my love."

"Unhappy boy; what bliss and what misery!" And Mlle. Armande drew his fevered face to her breast and kissed his forehead, cold and damp though it was. Following the excellent scheme suggested by the prodigal son, he was brought by night to the quiet house in the Rue du Bercail; but chance ordered that by so doing he ran straight into the wolf’s jaws. That evening Chesnel had been arranging to sell his connection to M. Lepressoir’s head-clerk. M. Lepressoir was the notary employed by the Liberals, just as Chesnel’s practice lay among aristocratic families. The young fellow’s relatives were rich enough to pay Chesnel the considerable sum of a hundred thousand francs in cash.

Chesnel was rubbing his hands. "A hundred thousand francs will go a long way in buying up debts," he thought. "The young man is paying a high rate of interest on his loans. We will lock him up down here. I will go yonder myself and bring those curs to terms."

Chesnel, honest Chesnel, upright, worthy Chesnel, called his darling Comte Victurnien’s creditors "curs."

Meanwhile his successor was making his way along the Rue du Bercail just as Mlle. Armande’s traveling carriage turned into it. Any young man might feel curiosity if he saw a traveling carriage stop at a notary’s door in such a town and at such an hour; the young man in question was sufficiently inquisitive to stand in a doorway and watch. He saw Mlle. Armande alight.

"Mlle. Armande d’Esgrignon at this time of night!" said he to himself. "What can be going forward at the d’Esgrignons’?"

At the sight of mademoiselle, Chesnel opened the door circumspectly and set down the light he carried; when he looked out and saw Victurnien, Mlle. Armande’s first whispered word made the whole thing plain. He looked up and down the street; it seemed deserted; he beckoned, and the young Count sprang out of the carriage and entered the courtyard. All was lost. Chesnel’s successor had discovered Victurnien’s hiding place.

Victurnien was hurried into the house and installed in a room beyond Chesnel’s private office. No one could enter it except across the old man’s dead body.

"Ah! M. le Comte!" exclaimed Chesnel, no longer merely notary.

"Yes, monsieur," the Count answered, understanding the old friend’s exclamation. "I did not listen to you; and now I have fallen into the depths, and I must perish."

"No, no," the good man answered, looking triumphantly from Mlle. Armande to the Count. "I have sold my connection. I have been working for a long time now, and think of retiring. By noon to-morrow I shall have a hundred thousand francs; many things can be settled with that. Mademoiselle, you are tired," he added; "go back to the carriage and sleep. Business to-morrow."

"Is he safe?" she asked, looking at Victurnien.

"Yes."

She kissed her nephew; a few tears fell on his forehead. Then she went.

"My good Chesnel," said the Count, when they began to talk of business, "what are your hundred thousand francs in such a position as mine? You do not know the full extent of my troubles, I think."

Victurnien explained the situation. Chesnel was thunderstruck. But for the strength of his devotion, he would have succumbed to the blow. Tears streamed from eyes that might well have had no tears left. For a few moments he was a child again, bereft of senses; he stood like a man who should find his house on fire, and through a window see the cradle ablaze and hear the hiss of the flames on his children’s curls. He rose to his full height; he seemed to grow taller; he raised his withered hands and wrung them despairingly and wildly.

"If only your father may die and never know this, young man! To be a forger is enough; a parricide you must not be. Fly, you say? No. They would condemn you for contempt of court! Oh, wretched boy! Why did you not forge my signature? I would have paid; I should not have taken the bill to the public prosecutor. Now I can do nothing. You have brought me to a stand in the lowest pit in hell!--Du Croisier! What will come of it? What is to be done? If you had killed a man, there might be some help. But forgery—forgery! And time—the time is flying," he went on, shaking his fist towards the old clock. "You will want a sham passport now. One crime leads to another. First," he added after a pause, "first of all we must save the house of d’Esgrignon."

"But the money is still in Mme. de Maufrigneuse’s keeping," exclaimed Victurnien.

"Ah!" exclaimed Chesnel. "Well, there is some hope left—a faint hope. Could we soften du Croisier, or buy him over? He shall have all the lands if he likes. I will go to him; I will wake him and offer him all we have.--Besides, it was not you who forged that bill; it was I. I will go to jail; I am too old for the hulks, they can only put me in prison."

"But the body of the bill is in my handwriting," objected Victurnien, without surprise at this reckless devotion.

"Idiot!... that is, pardon, M. le Comte. Josephin should have written it," the old notary cried wrathfully. "He is a good creature; he would have taken it all on his shoulders. But there is an end of it; the world is falling to pieces," the old man continued, sinking exhausted into a chair. "Du Croisier is a tiger; we must be careful not to rouse him. What time is it? Where is the draft? If it is at Paris, it might be bought back from the Kellers; they might accommodate us. Ah! but there are dangers on all sides; a single false step means ruin. Money is wanted in any case. But there! nobody knows you are here, you must live buried in the cellar if needs be. I will go at once to Paris as fast as I can; I can hear the mail coach from Brest."

In a moment the old man recovered the faculties of his youth—his agility and vigor. He packed clothes for the journey, took money, brought a six-pound loaf to the little room beyond the office, and turned the key on his child by adoption.

"Not a sound in here," he said, "no light at night; and stop here till I come back, or you will go to the hulks. Do you understand, M. le Comte? Yes, to the hulks! if anybody in a town like this knows that you are here."

With that Chesnel went out, first telling his housekeeper to give out that he was ill, to allow no one to come into the house, to send everybody away, and to postpone business of every kind for three days. He wheedled the manager of the coach-office, made up a tale—he had the makings of an ingenious novelist—and obtained a promise that if there should be a place, he should have it, passport or no passport, and that the hurried departure should be secret. Luckily, the coach was empty when it arrived.

In the middle of the following night Chesnel was set down in Paris. At nine in the morning he waited on the Kellers, and learned that the fatal draft had returned to du Croisier three days since; but in obtaining this information he in no way committed himself. Before he went away he inquired whether the draft could be recovered if the amount were refunded. Francois Keller’s answer was that the document was du Croisier’s property, and entirely in his power to keep or return. Then, in desperation, the old man went to the Duchess.

Mme. de Maufrigneuse was not at home to any visitor at that hour. Chesnel, feeling every moment precious, sat in the hall, wrote a few lines, and succeeded in sending them to the lady by dint of wheedling, fascinating, bribing, and commanding the most insolent and inaccessible servants in the world. The Duchess was still in bed; but, to the great astonishment of her household, the old man in black knee-breeches, ribbed stockings, and shoes with buckles was shown into her room.

"What is it, monsieur?" she asked, posing in her disorder. "What does he want of me, ungrateful that he is?"

"It is this, Mme. la Duchesse," the good man exclaimed, "you have a hundred thousand crowns belonging to us."

"Yes," began she. "What does it signify—?"

"The money was gained by a forgery, for which we are going to the hulks, a forgery we committed for love of you," Chesnel said quickly. "How is it that you did not guess it, so clever as you are? Instead of scolding the boy, you ought to have had the truth out of him, and stopped him while there was time, and saved him."

At the first words the Duchess understood; she felt ashamed of her behavior to so impassioned a lover, and afraid she might be suspected of complicity. In her wish to prove she had not touched the money left in her keeping, she lost regard for appearances; and it did not occur to her that the notary was a man. She flung off the eider-down quilt, sprang to her desk, held out the notes, and went back in confusion to bed.

"You are an angel, madame." (She was to be an angel for all the world, it seemed.) "But this will not be the end. I count upon your influence to save us."

"To save you! I will do it or die! Love that will not shrink from a crime must be love indeed. Is there a woman in the world for whom such a thing has been done? Poor boy! Come, do not lose time, dear M. Chesnel; and count upon me as upon yourself."

"Mme. la Duchesse! Mme. la Duchesse!" It was all that he could say, so overborne. He cried, could have danced; but he was afraid of losing his senses, and refrained.

"Between us, we will save him," she said as he left the room.

Chesnel went straight to Josephin. Josephin unlocked the young Count’s desk and writing-table. Very luckily, the notary found letters which might be useful—letters from du Croisier and the Kellers. Then he took a place in a diligence about to start; and by dint of fees to the postilions, the lumbering vehicle went as quickly as the coach. His two fellow-passengers on the journey happened to be in as great a hurry as he, and readily agreed to take their meals in the carriage. Thus swept over the road, the notary reached the Rue du Bercail after three days of absence, an hour before midnight. And yet he was too late. He saw the gendarmes at the gate, crossed the threshold, and met the young Count in the courtyard. Victurnien had been arrested. If Chesnel had had the power, he would beyond doubt have killed the officers; as it was, he could only fall on Victurnien’s neck.

"If I cannot hush this matter up, you must kill yourself before the indictment is made out," he whispered. But Victurnien had sunk into such stupor that he stared back uncomprehendingly.

"Kill myself?" he repeated.

"Yes. If your courage should fail, my boy, count upon me," said Chesnel, squeezing Victurnien’s hand.

In spite of the anguish and tottering limbs, he stood firmly to watch the Comte d’Esgrignon go out of the courtyard between two gendarmes, with the commissary, the justice of the peace, and the clerk of the court; not until the figures had disappeared and footsteps died away into silence did he recover firmness and presence of mind.

"You will catch cold, sir," Brigitte remonstrated.

"The devil take you!" cried her exasperated master.

Never in her nine-and-twenty years’ service had Brigitte heard such words from him! Her candle fell, but Chesnel neither heeded his housekeeper’s alarm nor heard her exclaim. He hurried off towards the Val-Noble.

"He is out of his mind," said she; "after all, it is no wonder. But where is he off to? I cannot go after him. What will become of him? Suppose he should drown himself?"

Brigitte went to waken the head-clerk and send him to look along the river bank; the river had a gloomy reputation just then, for there had lately been two suicides—a promising young man and a girl, a victim of seduction. Chesnel went straight to the Hotel du Croisier. There lay his only hope. The law requires that a charge of forgery be brought by a private individual. It was still possible to withdraw if du Croisier chose to admit a misapprehension; Chesnel hoped to buy the man over.

M. and Mme. du Croisier had much more company than usual that evening. Only a few persons were in the secret. M. du Ronceret, president of the Tribunal; M. Sauvager, deputy Public Prosecutor; and M. du Coudrai, a registrar of mortgages who had lost his post by voting wrong, were the only persons supposed to know; but Mesdames du Ronceret and du Coudrai had told the news, in strict confidence, to one or two intimate friends, so that it had spread half over the semi-noble, semi-bourgeois assembly at M. du Croisier’s. Everybody felt the gravity, but no one ventured to speak openly; moreover, Mme. du Croisier’s attachment to the upper sphere was so well known that people scarcely dared mention the disaster which had befallen the d’Esgrignons or ask for particulars. The persons most interested waited till good Mme. du Croisier retired, for that lady always retreated to her room at the same hour to perform her religious exercises as far as possible out of her husband’s sight.

Du Croisier’s adherents, knowing the secret and plans of the great commercial power, looked round when the lady disappeared; but several present were untrustworthy, so they continued to play. About half past eleven all had gone save intimates: M. Sauvager, M. Camusot the examining magistrate, and his wife, M. and Mme. du Ronceret and their son Fabien, M. and Mme. du Coudrai, and Joseph Blondet, the eldest of an old judge; ten persons in all.

It is told of Talleyrand that one fatal day, three hours after midnight, he suddenly interrupted a game of cards in the Duchesse de Luynes’ house by laying down his watch on the table and asking whether the Prince de Conde had any child but the Duc d’Enghien.

"Why do you ask?" returned Mme. de Luynes, "when you know so well he has not."

"Because if the Prince has no other son, the House of Conde is now at an end."

There was a pause, and they finished the game. President du Ronceret now did something similar. Perhaps he had heard the anecdote; perhaps, in political life, little minds and great minds hit upon the same expression. He looked at his watch, and interrupted a game of boston with:

"At this moment M. le Comte d’Esgrignon is arrested, and that house which has held its head so high is dishonored forever."

"Then, have you got hold of the boy?" du Coudrai cried gleefully.

Everyone in the room, except the President, the deputy, and du Croisier, looked startled.

"He has just been arrested in Chesnel’s house, where he was hiding," said the deputy public prosecutor, with the air of a capable but unappreciated servant who ought by rights to be Minister of Police. M. Sauvager, the deputy, was a thin, tall man of five-and-twenty, with a lengthy olive-hued countenance, black frizzled hair, and deep-set eyes; wide dark rings beneath them were completed by wrinkled purple eyelids above. With a nose like the beak of a bird of prey, a pinched mouth, and cheeks worn lean with study and hollowed by ambition, he was the very type of a second-rate person ready to do anything to get on, while keeping within forms of law. His pompous expression was an admirable indication of the time-serving eloquence to be expected. Chesnel’s successor had discovered the young Count’s hiding place to him, and he took great credit to himself for his penetration.

The news seemed to shock the examining magistrate, M. Camusot, who had granted the warrant of arrest on Sauvager’s application, with no idea it would be executed so promptly. Camusot was short, fair, and fat already, though he was only about thirty; he had the flabby, livid look of officials shut up in private study or a court; his little, pale, yellow eyes were full of suspicion often mistaken for shrewdness.

Mme. Camusot looked at her spouse, as who should say, "Was I not right?"

"Then the case will come on," was Camusot’s comment.

"Could you doubt it?" asked du Coudrai. "Now they have got the Count, all is over."

"There is the jury," said Camusot. "In this case M. le Prefet is sure to take care that after the challenges from the prosecution and the defence, the jury to a man will be for an acquittal.--My advice would be to come to a compromise," he added, turning to du Croisier.

"Compromise!" echoed the President; "why, he is in the hands of justice."

"Acquitted or convicted, the Comte d’Esgrignon will be dishonored all the same," put in Sauvager.

"I am bringing an action," said du Croisier. "I shall have Dupin senior. We shall see how the d’Esgrignon family will escape his clutches."

"The d’Esgrignons will defend the case and have counsel from Paris; they will have Berryer," said Mme. Camusot. "You will have a Roland for your Oliver."

Du Croisier, M. Sauvager, and the President du Ronceret looked at Camusot, and one thought troubled them. The lady’s tone, the way she flung her proverb in the faces of the eight conspirators against the house of d’Esgrignon, caused inward perturbation, which they dissembled as provincials can, by lifelong practice in shifts of a monastic existence. Little Mme. Camusot saw their change of countenance and subsequent composure when they scented opposition from the examining magistrate. She wanted to find out how du Croisier had gained over this deputy public prosecutor, who had acted promptly and directly in opposition to the central power.

"In any case," she continued, "if celebrated counsel come down from Paris, there is prospect of a very interesting session in the Court of Assize; but the matter will be snuffed out between the Tribunal and the Court of Appeal. It is only to be expected that the Government should do what it can, below the surface, to save a young man who comes of a great family, and has the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse for a friend. So I think we shall have a ‘sensation at Landernau.’"

"How you go on, madame!" the President said sternly. "Can you suppose that the Court of First Instance will be influenced by considerations which have nothing to do with justice?"

"The event proves the contrary," she said meaningly, looking at Sauvager and the President, who glanced coldly at her.

"Explain yourself, madame," said Sauvager, "you speak as if we had not done our duty."

"Mme. Camusot meant nothing," interposed her husband.

But Mme. Camusot persisted, and the debate revealed the political stakes. A great silence followed, broken only by the dealing of the cards. M. and Mme. Camusot, sensing a chill, took their departure to leave the conspirators to talk at ease.

"Camusot," the lady began in the street, "you went too far. Why lead those people to suspect that you will have no part in their schemes? They will play you an ugly trick."

"What can they do? I am the only examining magistrate."

"Cannot they slander you in whispers, and procure your dismissal?"

At that moment Chesnel ran up against the couple. The old notary recognized the examining magistrate; with the lucidity that comes of an experience of business, he saw the fate of the d’Esgrignons lay in the young man before him.

"Ah, sir!" he exclaimed, "we shall soon need you badly. Just a word with you.--Your pardon, madame," he added, as he drew Camusot aside.

Mme. Camusot, as a good conspirator, looked towards du Croisier’s house, ready to break up the conversation if anybody appeared; but she thought, rightly, that their enemies were busy discussing this unexpected turn she had given to the affair. Chesnel meanwhile drew the magistrate into a dark corner under the wall, and lowered his voice.

"If you are for the house of d’Esgrignon," he said, "Mme. la Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, the Prince of Cadignan, the Ducs de Navarreins and de Lenoncourt, the Keeper of the Seals, the Chancellor, the King himself, will interest themselves in you. I have just come from Paris; I knew all about this; I went post-haste to explain everything at Court. We are counting on you, and I will keep your secret. If you are hostile, I shall go back to Paris to-morrow and lodge a complaint with the Keeper of the Seals that there is a suspicion of corruption. Several functionaries were at du Croisier’s house to-night, and no doubt ate and drank there, contrary to law; and besides, they are friends of his."

Chesnel would have brought the Almighty to intervene if he could. He did not wait for an answer; he left Camusot and fled like a deer towards du Croisier’s house. Camusot, meanwhile, bidden to reveal the notary’s confidences, was at once assailed with, "Was I not right, dear?"--a wifely formula used on all occasions, but more vehemently when the fair speaker is in the wrong. By the time they reached home, Camusot had admitted the superiority of his partner in life, and appreciated his good fortune in belonging to her; which confession doubtless was the prelude of a blissful night.

Chesnel met his foes in a body as they left du Croisier’s house, and began to fear du Croisier had gone to bed. In his position he was compelled to act quickly, and any delay was a misfortune.

"In the King’s name!" he cried, as the man-servant was closing the hall door. He had just brought the King on the scene for the benefit of an ambitious little official, and the word was still on his lips. He fretted while the door was unbarred; then, swift as a thunderbolt, dashed into the ante-chamber, and spoke to the servant.

"A hundred crowns to you, young man, if you can wake Mme. du Croisier and send her to me this instant. Tell her anything you like."

Chesnel grew composed as he opened the door of the brightly lighted drawing-room, where du Croisier was striding up and down. For a moment the two men scanned each other, with hatred and enmity, twenty years’ deep, in their eyes. One of the two had his foot on the heart of the house of d’Esgrignon; the other, with a lion’s strength, came forward to pluck it away.

"Your humble servant, sir," said Chesnel. "Have you made the charge?"

"Yes, sir."

"When was it made?"

"Yesterday."

"Have any steps been taken since the warrant?"

"I believe so."

"I have come to treat with you."

"Justice must take its course, nothing can stop it, the arrest has been made."

"Never mind that, I am at your orders, at your feet." The old man knelt before du Croisier, and stretched out his hands entreatingly.

"What do you want? Our lands, our castle? Take all; withdraw the charge; leave us nothing but life and honor. And over and besides all this, I will be your servant; command and I will obey."

Du Croisier sat and left the old man to kneel.

"You are not vindictive," pleaded Chesnel; "you are good-hearted, you do not bear us such a grudge that you will not listen to terms. Before daylight the young man ought to be at liberty."

"The whole town knows he has been arrested," returned du Croisier, enjoying his revenge.

"It is a great misfortune, but as there will be neither proofs nor trial, we can easily manage that."

Du Croisier reflected. He seemed to struggle with self-interest; Chesnel thought he had gained a hold on his enemy through the great motive of human action. At that supreme moment Mme. du Croisier appeared.

"Come here and help me soften your dear husband, madame?" said Chesnel, still on his knees. Mme. du Croisier made him rise with every sign of astonishment. Chesnel explained his errand; when she knew it, the generous daughter of the intendants of the Ducs de Alencon turned to du Croisier with tears in her eyes.

"Ah! monsieur, can you hesitate? The d’Esgrignons, the honor of the province!" she said.

"There is more in it than that," exclaimed du Croisier. "France is involved, M. Chesnel! It is a question of the country, of the people, of giving my lords your nobles a lesson, and teaching them that there is such a thing as justice, and law, and a bourgeoisie—a lesser nobility as good as they, and a match for them! There shall be no more trampling down half a score of wheat fields for a single hare; no bringing shame on families by seducing unprotected girls; they shall not look down on others as good as they are, and mock at them for ten years, without finding out at last that these things swell into avalanches, and those avalanches will fall and crush and bury my lords the nobles. You want to go back to the old order. You want to tear up the social compact, the Charter in which our rights are set forth—"

If Chesnel was startled by this outburst, so was Mme. du Croisier. To her this was a terrible revelation of her husband’s character, a new light not merely on the past but on the future. Any capitulation on the part of the colossus was apparently out of the question; but Chesnel in no wise retreated before the impossible.

"What, monsieur?" said Mme. du Croisier. "Would you not forgive? Then you are not a Christian."

"I forgive as God forgives, madame, on certain conditions."

"And what are they?" asked Chesnel, thinking he saw a ray of hope.

"The elections are coming on; I want the votes at your disposal."

"You shall have them."

"I wish that we, my wife and I, should be received familiarly every evening, with an appearance of friendliness at any rate, by M. le Marquis d’Esgrignon and his circle," continued du Croisier.

"I do not know how we are going to compass it, but you shall be received."

"I wish to have the family bound over by a surety of four hundred thousand francs, and by a written document stating the nature of the compromise, so as to keep a loaded cannon pointed at its heart."

"We agree," said Chesnel, without admitting the three hundred thousand francs was in his possession; "but the amount must be deposited with a third party and returned after your election and repayment."

"No; after the marriage of my grand-niece, Mlle. Duval. She will very likely have four million francs some day; the reversion of our property (mine and my wife’s) shall be settled upon her by her marriage-contract, and you shall arrange a match between her and the young Count."

"Never!"

"Never!" repeated du Croisier, quite intoxicated with triumph. "Good-night!"

"Idiot that I am," thought Chesnel, "why did I shrink from a lie to such a man?"

Du Croisier took himself off; he was pleased with himself; he had enjoyed Chesnel’s humiliation; he had held the destinies of a proud house suspended in his hand; he had set the print of his heel on the very heart of the d’Esgrignons; and, finally, he had broken off the whole negotiation on the score of his wounded pride. He went up to his room, leaving his wife with Chesnel. In his intoxication he saw his victory clear before him. He firmly believed the three hundred thousand francs had been squandered; the d’Esgrignons must sell or mortgage all to raise the money; the Assize Court was inevitable.

An affair of forgery can always be settled out of court in France if the missing amount is returned. The losers are usually well-to-do and have no wish to blight an imprudent man’s character. But du Croisier had no mind to slacken until he knew what he was about. He meditated until he fell asleep on the magnificent manner his hopes would be fulfilled by the Assize Court or by marriage. The murmur of voices below, the lamentations of Chesnel and Mme. du Croisier, sounded sweet in his ears.

Mme. du Croisier shared Chesnel’s views of the d’Esgrignons. She was deeply religious, a Royalist attached to the noblesse; the interview had been a cruel shock. She, a staunch Royalist, had heard the roaring of that Liberalism which, in her director’s opinion, wished to crush the Church. The Left benches for her meant popular upheaval and the scaffolds of 1793.

"What would your uncle, that sainted man who hears us, say to this?" exclaimed Chesnel. Mme. du Croisier made no reply, but great tears rolled down her cheeks.

"You have already been the cause of one poor boy’s death; his mother will go mourning all her days," continued Chesnel; he saw how his words told, but he would have struck harder to save Victurnien. "Do you want to kill Mlle. Armande, for she would not survive the dishonor for a week? Do you wish to be the death of poor Chesnel, your old notary? For I shall kill the Count in prison before they bring the charge against him, and take my own life afterwards, before they try me for murder in an Assize Court."

"That is enough! that is enough, my friend! I would do anything to put a stop to such an affair; but I never knew M. du Croisier’s real character until a few minutes ago. To you I can make the admission: there is nothing to be done."

"But what if there is?"

"I would give half the blood in my veins that it were so," said she, finishing with a wistful shake of the head.

As the First Consul, beaten on the field of Marengo till five in the evening, by six saw the tide turned by Desaix’s attack and Kellermann’s charge, so Chesnel in the midst of defeat saw the beginnings of victory. No one but a Chesnel, an old notary, an ex-steward, in the sudden flash of lucidity arising from despair, could rise thus, high as a Napoleon, nay, higher. This was not Marengo, it was Waterloo, and the Prussians had come up; Chesnel saw this, and was determined to beat them off the field.

"Madame," he said, "remember that I have been your man of business for twenty years; remember that if the d’Esgrignons mean the honor of the province, you represent the honor of the bourgeoisie; it rests with you, and you alone, to save the ancient house. Now, answer me; are you going to allow dishonor to fall on the shade of your dead uncle, on the d’Esgrignons, on poor Chesnel? Do you want to kill Mlle. Armande weeping yonder? Or do you wish to expiate wrongs done to others by a deed which will rejoice your ancestors, the intendants of the dukes of Alencon, and bring comfort to the soul of our dear Abbe? If he could rise from his grave, he would command you to do this thing that I beg of you upon my knees."

"What is it?" asked Mme. du Croisier.

"Well. Here are the hundred thousand crowns," said Chesnel, drawing bundles from his pocket. "Take them, and there will be an end of it."

"If that is all," she began, "and if no harm can come of it to my husband—"

"Nothing but good," Chesnel replied. "You are saving him from eternal punishment in hell, at the cost of a slight disappointment here below."

"He will not be compromised, will he?" she asked, looking into Chesnel’s face.

Then Chesnel read the depths of the poor wife’s mind. Mme. du Croisier hesitated between her two creeds; between wifely obedience to her husband as laid down by the Church, and obedience to altar and throne. Her husband, in her eyes, was acting wrongly, but she dared not blame him; she would fain save the d’Esgrignons, but was loyal to her husband’s interests.

"Not in the least," Chesnel answered; "your old notary swears it by the Holy Gospels—"

He had nothing left to lose for the d’Esgrignons but his soul; he risked it now by this horrible perjury, but Mme. du Croisier must be deceived, there was no other choice but death. Without losing a moment he dictated a receipt by which Mme. du Croisier acknowledged payment of a hundred thousand crowns five days before the fatal letter of exchange appeared; he recollected du Croisier was away superintending improvements on his wife’s property at the time.

"Now swear to me that you will declare before the examining magistrate that you received the money on that date," he said, when Mme. du Croisier had taken the notes and he held the receipt.

"It will be a lie, will it not?"

"Venial sin," said Chesnel.

"I could not do it without consulting my director, M. l’Abbe Couturier."

"Very well," said Chesnel, "will you be guided entirely by his advice in this affair?"

"I promise that."

"And you must not give the money to M. du Croisier until you have been before the magistrate."

"No. Ah! God give me strength to appear in a Court of Justice and maintain a lie before men!"

Chesnel kissed Mme. du Croisier’s hand, then stood upright, majestic as one of the prophets Raphael painted in the Vatican.

"Your uncle’s soul is thrilled with joy," he said; "you have wiped out for ever the wrong you did by marrying an enemy of altar and throne"—words that made a lively impression on Mme. du Croisier’s timorous mind.

Then Chesnel bethought himself to secure the lady’s director, the Abbe Couturier. He knew how obstinately devout souls can work for their side when once they come forward, and wished to secure the concurrence of the Church early. So he went to the Hotel d’Esgrignon, roused Mlle. Armande, gave her an account of that night’s work, and sped her to fetch the Bishop himself into the forefront of the battle.

"Ah, God in heaven! Thou must save the house of d’Esgrignon!" he exclaimed as he went slowly home. "The affair is developing into a fight in a Court of Law. We are face to face with men who have passions and interests; we can get anything out of them. This du Croisier has taken advantage of the public prosecutor’s absence; the public prosecutor is devoted to us, but since the opening of the Chambers he is in Paris. Now, what can they have done to get round his deputy? They have induced him to take up the charge without consulting his chief. This mystery must be looked into, and the ground surveyed to-morrow; and then, perhaps, when I have unraveled this web, I will go back to Paris to set great powers at work through Mme. de Maufrigneuse."

So reasoned the aged, clear-sighted wrestler, before he lay down half dead with emotion and fatigue. Yet before he fell asleep he ran a searching eye over the list of magistrates, taking all secret ambitions into account, casting about for ways to influence them, calculating his chances in the coming struggle. Chesnel’s prolonged scrutiny of consciences, condensed, will perhaps serve as a picture of the judicial world in a country town.

Magistrates and officials generally must begin their career in the provinces; judicial ambition ferments there. At the outset every man looks towards Paris; they all aspire to shine in the vast theatre where great political causes come before the courts, and the higher branches of the legal profession are closely connected with society’s palpitating interests. But few are called to that paradise of the man of law, and most sooner or later regard themselves as shelved for good in the provinces. Wherefore, every Tribunal and every Court-Royal is sharply divided in two. The first section has given up hope, and is either torpid or content; content with excessive respect paid to office in a country town, or torpid with tranquillity. The second section is made up of the younger sort, whose desire of success is untempered by disappointment, and of the really clever men urged on by ambition.

At this time the younger men were full of Royalist zeal against the Bourbons’ enemies. The most insignificant deputy official was dreaming of conducting a prosecution, and praying for one of those political cases which bring a man’s zeal into prominence, draw the attention of higher powers, and mean advancement for King’s men. Was there a member of prosecuting counsel who could hear of a Bonapartist conspiracy elsewhere without envy? Where was the man that did not burn to discover a Caron, or a Berton, or a revolt of some sort? With reasons of State and the necessity of diffusing the monarchical spirit throughout France as basis, and a fierce ambition stirred whenever party spirit ran high, these ardent politicians on promotion were lucid, clear-sighted, and perspicacious. They kept up a vigorous detective system throughout the kingdom; they did the work of spies, and urged the nation along a path of obedience from which it had no business to swerve.

Justice, thus informed with monarchical enthusiasm, atoned for ancient parliaments’ errors, and walked, perhaps, too ostentatiously hand in hand with religion. There was more zeal than discretion; but justice sinned less in machiavelism than by giving candid expression to its views when opposed to the general interests of a country that must be put safely out of reach of revolutions. Taken as a whole, there was too much bourgeois element in the administration; it was too readily moved by petty liberal agitation; and as a result, it was inevitable it should incline sooner or later to the Constitutional party, and join ranks with the bourgeoisie in the day of battle. In the great body of legal functionaries, as in other departments, there was a certain hypocrisy, or rather that spirit of imitation which leads France to model herself on the Court, and unintentionally to deceive the powers that be.

Officials of both complexions were to be found in the court where young d’Esgrignon’s fate depended. M. le President du Ronceret and an elderly judge, Blondet, represented the section shelved for good, resigned to stay where they were; while the young and ambitious party comprised the examining magistrate M. Camusot, and his deputy M. Michu, appointed through the Cinq-Cygnes’ interests, certain of promotion to the Court of Appeal of Paris at the first opportunity.

President du Ronceret held a permanent post; it was impossible to turn him out. The aristocratic party declined to give him what he considered due socially; so he declared for the bourgeoisie, glossed over his disappointment with the name of independence, and failed to realize his opinions condemned him to remain president of a court of first instance for life. Once started he placed hopes of advancement on the triumph of du Croisier and the Left. He was in no better odor at the Prefecture than at the Court-Royal. He was obliged to keep on good terms with the authorities; the Liberals distrusted him, so he belonged to neither party. He resigned his chances of election to du Croisier, exercised no influence, and played a secondary part. The false position soured him; he was tired of political ambiguity, and privately had made up his mind to come forward openly as leader of the Liberal party, and so to strike ahead of du Croisier. His behavior in the d’Esgrignon affair was the first step.

To begin with, he was an admirable representative of that section of the middle classes which allows petty passions to obscure wider interests; a class of crotchety politicians, upholding the government one day and opposing it the next, compromising every cause and helping none; helpless after they have done mischief till they brew more; unwilling to face their own incompetence, thwarting authority while professing to serve it. With a compound of arrogance and humility they demand of the people more submission than kings expect, and fret because those above them are not brought down to their level, as if greatness could be little, as if power existed without force.

President du Ronceret was tall, spare, with a receding forehead and scanty auburn hair. He was wall-eyed, his complexion blotched, his lips thin and hard, his scarcely audible voice like husky wheezings of asthma. He had a wife, great, solemn, clumsy, tricked out in the most ridiculous fashion, outrageously overdressed. Mme. la Presidente gave herself airs of a queen; she wore vivid colors, and always appeared at balls adorned with the turban, dear to the British female, and lovingly cultivated in out-of-the-way districts in France. Each had an income of four or five thousand, which with the President’s salary reached some twelve thousand. In spite of parsimony, vanity required they receive one evening in the week. Du Croisier might import modern luxury into town; M. and Mme. de Ronceret were faithful to old traditions. They had always lived in the old-fashioned house belonging to Mme. du Ronceret, and had made no changes since marriage. The house stood between a garden and a courtyard. The gray old gable end, with one window in each story, gave on the road. High walls enclosed the garden and yard, but the space beneath the chestnut-shaded walk was filled in the yard by a row of outbuildings. An old rust-devoured iron gate in the garden wall balanced the yard gateway, a huge double-leaved carriage entrance with buttresses, and a mighty shell on the top repeated over the house-door.

The whole place was gloomy, close, airless. The row of iron-gated openings in the opposite wall, as you entered, reminded one of prison windows. Every passer-by could look in through the railings to see how the garden grew; the flowers in the little square borders never seemed to thrive.

The drawing-room on the ground floor was lighted by a single window on the street side, and a French window above a flight of steps gave on the garden. The dining-room on the other side of the great ante-chamber, with windows also looking into the garden, was exactly the same size as the drawing-room, and all three apartments harmonized with the general air of gloom. It wearied your eyes to look at ceilings divided by huge painted crossbeams and adorned with a feeble lozenge pattern or a rosette in the middle. The paint was old, startling in tint, begrimed with smoke. The sun had faded the heavy silk curtains; the old Beauvais tapestry which covered the white-painted furniture had lost its color with wear. A Louis Quinze clock on the chimney-piece stood between two extravagant branched sconces filled with yellow wax candles, which the Presidente only lighted on occasions when the rock-crystal chandelier emerged from its green wrapper. Three card-tables, covered with threadbare baize, and a backgammon box, sufficed for the company’s recreations; Mme. du Ronceret treated them to refreshments like cider, chestnuts, pastry puffs, glasses of eau sucree, and home-made orgeat. For some time she had given a party once a fortnight, when tea and pitiable attempts at pastry appeared to grace the occasion.

Once a quarter the du Roncerets gave a grand three-course dinner, which made a great sensation, a Gargantuan repast lasting six hours, and by abundance the President tried to vie with du Croisier’s elegance.

And so du Ronceret’s life and its accessories were just what might have been expected from his character and false position. He felt dissatisfied at home without knowing why; but he dared not go to expense to change conditions, and was only too glad to put by seven or eight thousand every year, to leave his son Fabien a handsome private fortune. Fabien had no mind for the magistracy, the bar, or the civil service; his pronounced turn for doing nothing drove his parent to despair.

On this head there was rivalry between the President and the Vice-President, old M. Blondet. M. Blondet, for a long time past, had been sedulously cultivating acquaintance between his son and the Blandureau family. The Blandureaus were well-to-do linen manufacturers, with an only daughter, and it was on this daughter that the President had fixed his choice of a wife for Fabien. Now Joseph Blondet’s marriage with Mlle. Blandureau depended on his nomination to the post which his father, old Blondet, hoped to obtain when he retired. But President du Ronceret, in underhand ways, was thwarting the old man’s plans, and working indirectly upon the Blandureaus. Indeed, if it had not been for this affair of young d’Esgrignon’s, the astute President might have cut them out, father and son, for their rivals were richer.

M. Blondet, the victim of the machiavelian President’s intrigues, was one of the curious figures buried in the provinces like old coins in a crypt. He was then about sixty-seven, but carried his years well; he was very tall, and in frame reminded you of the canons of good old times. Smallpox had riddled his face with dints, and spoilt his nose by imparting to it a gimlet-like twist; it was a countenance not lacking in character, evenly tinted with a diffused red, lighted by a pair of bright little eyes with a sardonic look, while a certain sarcastic twitch of the purpled lips gave expression to that feature.

Before the Revolution Blondet senior had been a barrister; afterwards he became the public accuser, and one of the mildest of those formidable functionaries. Goodman Blondet, as they used to call him, deadened the force of the new doctrines by acquiescing in them all, and putting none in practice. He had been obliged to send one or two nobles to prison; but his further proceedings were marked with such deliberation that he brought them through to the 9th Thermidor with a dexterity which won respect on all sides. As a matter of fact, Goodman Blondet ought to have been President of the Tribunal, but when the courts were reorganized he had been set aside; Napoleon’s aversion for Republicans reappeared in small appointments. The qualification of ex-public accuser, written in the margin against Blondet’s name, set the Emperor inquiring of Cambaceres whether there might not be some scion of ancient parliamentary stock to appoint instead. The consequence was that du Ronceret, whose father had been a councillor of parliament, was nominated to the presidency; but Cambaceres allowed Blondet to remain on the bench, saying the old barrister was one of the best jurisconsults in France.

Blondet’s talents and legal knowledge might have taken him far, but he held a prodigious contempt for his specialty and reserved his pretensions, leisure, and capacity for a second, unrelated pursuit. He devoted himself to gardening. Corresponding with celebrated amateurs and ambitious to create new species, the old judge lived among flowers, his favorite the pelargonium. Court business and outward life mattered little beside the inward life of fancies and emotions he led in his greenhouse. Had he not been so absorbed, he would probably have been a deputy under the Empire and prominent in the Corps Législatif.

His marriage further pushed him into obscurity. At forty he rashly married an eighteen-year-old who bore him a son, Joseph. Three years later Mme. Blondet, then the town’s prettiest woman, inspired a passion in the prefect that lasted until her death; the prefect became the father of her second son, Emile. The entire town knew, and even old Blondet knew. Instead of rousing his ambition, his wife encouraged his botanical tastes; neither wished to leave their places. Blondet, unequal to contest with a young wife, sought consolation in his greenhouse and kept a pretty servant to help him. While he tended, Mme. Blondet spent his substance on dress and finery. Only Emile’s education interested her; she loved Emile and Blondet loved Joseph, blinded by paternal affection.

For twelve years Blondet bore this with eighteenth-century grand seigneur composure, but he profoundly disliked his younger son. When his wife died in 1818, he turned Emile out, sending him to Paris on a scant allowance; Emile would have perished without his real father’s help. Blondet’s house, opposite the prefecture, had a neat court and admired flowerbeds; climbing plants covered walls and honeysuckle hid piers. The brick house had green shutters and mossed roofs; through glass doors and windows one could see the garden, which extended into two acres. Blondet’s income was about four thousand livres from land, plus the house. He meant to leave his property and bench seat to Joseph, having made a will favoring him and putting by money to enable Joseph to buy back portions of the estate that could not legally be taken from Emile.

Emile, turned adrift, gained some distinction in Paris as a man of letters and in society, buoyed by friendships like that with Mlle. de Troisville (later Mme. de Montcornet). Mme. Blondet had tried to link him to the Troisvilles, who later favored him when they returned to Paris. Emile’s precocious literary reputation and good figure strengthened his bond with the Countess de Montcornet; by the time his adventurous life pressed upon him, their attachment proved unshakeable. Old Blondet never knew of Emile’s success; he had been cursed in his mind and thus remained ignorant of his son’s greatness.

Blondet’s integrity matched his passion for flowers. He knew law and botany alone. He met litigants courteously, admitted gifts of rare seeds, but on the bench he was impartial; litigants trusted his clear procedure. His steadiness made him indispensable to President du Ronceret, who, apart from matrimonial schemes, would have done all he could to prevent the vice-president from retiring in favor of his son. Blondet did not know Emile could have fulfilled his wishes quickly.

Their household was frugal to parsimony: fruit saved until it spoiled, no new bread, fast days strictly observed. The maid Mlle. Cadot kept keys and watched expenses; she shared the father’s predilection for Joseph. Blondet tried to mold Joseph into a lawyer to satisfy Mlle. Blandureau’s parents, who insisted he be an assistant judge to marry their daughter. Joseph spent evenings at the Blandureaus’, where young Fabien du Ronceret also frequented. Everything was measured; the garden, however, escaped thrift. The gardener, a single man, kept paths and borders immaculate, and an amphitheatre of benches held thousands of pelargoniums that drew visitors from far departments; Empress Marie Louise once visited and Blondet received the Legion of Honor. Blondet shunned society and was unaware of the President’s machinations.

Michu, a young judge with twelve thousand livres income, cared more for making himself agreeable to high society than for provincial tribunal routine. He did enough at the Tribunal to be useful and, with Parisian training as prosecuting counsel, could rapidly solve legal questions that stunned the President and Blondet. He was indispensable socially and professionally, able to mediate delicate matters.

At the moment of crisis the public prosecutor was away in Paris. The President and du Croisier used his absence to engineer a charge of forgery against Victurnien d’Esgrignon. Normally such a case would meet resistance, and authorities might hush up a noble’s indiscretion; here the President contrived to have M. Sauvager, a young Royalist deputy public prosecutor who owed his place to subservience to the Ministry, take up the charge without consulting his absent chief. The President and du Croisier then persuaded Sauvager by promising advantageous matches and future fortunes if he showed devotion, urging him to act against the d’Esgrignons and follow up at once so as to preempt Parisian intervention. Sauvager agreed.

Camusot’s unexpected defection from the President’s scheme terrified the plotters. Camusot, the examining magistrate, had his own background: son of a silk mercer ambitious for him, married to Amelie Thirion (daughter of an usher of the Royal cabinet), he had advanced by backstairs influence. His wife, Mme. Camusot, with Parisian instincts and impatience with provincial life, meddled in her husband’s career; she saw the d’Esgrignon affair as an opportunity to curry favor with powerful families including the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and to secure promotion and a Paris appointment for her husband. She perceived the President’s underhand maneuvers and advised Camusot to side with the d’Esgrignons, foreseeing advantage.

Chesnel, who knew the circle around the Tribunal, counted on the public prosecutor, Camusot, and Michu to support the d’Esgrignons; he also believed Blondet, if persuaded, would favor what he saw as justice for a former office-seeking cause of his lifetime. Chesnel planned to go to Blondet to avert the President’s treachery. Before dawn he was roused by a startling visitor: the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, disguised in man’s attire, had driven from Paris to save Victurnien. She declared she had brought a hundred thousand francs from the King’s private purse, to buy Victurnien’s innocence if necessary; if bribery failed, she had brought poison to prevent an indictment. She had sent word to the public prosecutor, who was following her.

Chesnel embraced the Duchess’s aid. They proceeded to see Camusot early; the Duchess would charm Mme. Camusot and secure Camusot’s cooperation. She and Chesnel repeated the plan: the Duchess would speak to Mme. Camusot, and Chesnel would use his authority to persuade Camusot that rescuing the d’Esgrignon was in the kingdom’s interest. The Duchess insisted on secrecy.

Mme. Camusot, quick to grasp the situation, accompanied the Duchess to Blondet’s greenhouse, presenting the Duchess as a distinguished horticulturist. She warned Blondet of the President’s duplicity: the President aimed to thwart Blondet’s hopes for Joseph’s marriage to Mlle. Blandureau, and Fabien du Ronceret had received clandestine proposals for Mlle. Claire’s hand. Blondet, a man of flowers, was shocked. The Duchess promised that if Blondet resigned properly and waited for confirmation from the public prosecutor, his son’s appointment could be secured within a week; she urged him to seek counsel with Camusot and Michu to hush up the affair quickly, ensuring Joseph’s prospects. Blondet, overwhelmed and suspicious, hurried to seek confirmation.

At nine the three judges—Blondet, Camusot, and Michu—met secretly. Blondet, furious at the President’s trickery, insisted the matter was a trifle and argued against construing the episode as forgery: forgery requires an intent to defraud, which seemed absent here, since Chesnel had repeatedly squared accounts and had allegedly deposited money to meet the bill. Michu proposed pulling the Count out of the mess at once by hastening examination of du Croisier and his wife and issuing a decision the next morning. They agreed to handle matters swiftly.

Du Croisier was summoned and questioned by Camusot; under searching interrogation he confirmed prior dealings with the Count and that Chesnel had often remitted amounts. Camusot then asked whether du Croisier knew the money destined to meet the bill had been deposited with him beforehand; this unnerved du Croisier. Chesnel, summoned, confirmed he had deposited the money and produced letters to prove it; Mme. du Croisier corroborated his statement, declaring Chesnel had entrusted the sum to her in her husband’s absence. With these facts on record, the Count’s innocence emerged legally.

Du Croisier returned home enraged at his wife’s deposition; she showed him bundles of banknotes she had kept concealed and argued she had acted for his good and future advantage. He threatened hasty departure and secrecy, while his wife believed she had safeguarded the family’s prospects.

The town buzzed with the Count’s arrest; yet by ten the court dismissed the charge, giving reasons that allowed the Count the right to sue for libel. Chesnel walked the street exposing du Croisier’s plot. As the day closed, the Marquis d’Esgrignon and family fretted over debts and honor. The Duchess, who had stayed with the family, advised practical measures: marry for money if necessary; the old privileges of nobility had been eroded by modern law and fortunes mattered more than lineage. She produced a tiny phial and joked about more decisive remedies; she counselled Victurnien to take lands and a quiet life rather than Paris flirtations. The romantic episode ended: the Duchess, once disguised and passionate, resumed her rank; for Victurnien and Diane, the brief romance dissolved with the crisis’s end.

Six months later Camusot was made assistant judge in Paris and became an examining magistrate. Goodman Blondet was appointed counsellor to the Royal Court; he held the post long enough to secure a pension, then returned to his pretty house. Joseph Blondet took his father’s seat at the court until his death; with no hope of promotion he married Mlle. Blandereau and now probably leads, in the little flower-covered brick house, as dull a life as any carp in a marble basin. Michu and Camusot received the Cross of the Legion of Honor; Blondet became an Officer. M. Sauvager, deputy public prosecutor, was sent to Corsica, much to du Croisier’s relief; du Croisier had certainly no intention of giving his niece to that functionary.

Urged by President du Ronceret, du Croisier appealed the Tribunal’s finding to the Court-Royal and lost. Liberals throughout the department held little d’Esgrignon guilty; Royalists spun frightful stories of plots by “that abominable du Croisier” to compass his revenge. A duel followed: chance favored du Croisier; the young Count was dangerously wounded, and his antagonist lived up to his words. The affair embittered party strife, and the Liberals raised it at every opportunity. Meanwhile du Croisier never carried his election and saw no hope of marrying his niece to the Count, especially after the duel.

A month after the Tribunal’s decision was confirmed by the Court-Royal, Chesnel died, exhausted by the dreadful strain that had broken him mentally and physically. He died in the hour of victory, like a faithful hound that brings the boar to bay and dies on its tusks. He died as happily as possible, though he left the great House nearly ruined, the heir penniless, bored by idleness, and with no hope of establishing himself. That bitter thought and his exhaustion no doubt hastened his end. One comfort amid wrecked hopes: at his sister’s entreaty the old Marquis restored their friendship. The great lord came to the Rue du Bercail and sat by his old servant’s bedside, unaware how much the servant had done and sacrificed. Chesnel sat up and repeated Simeon’s cry. The Marquis allowed burial in the castle chapel; they laid him crosswise at the foot of the tomb reserved for the Marquis himself — the last, in a sense, of the d’Esgrignons.

Thus died one of the last representatives of Service in its original meaning: the relation between feudal lord and servitor. Found now only in remote provinces or among a few old royal servants, it did honor to a noblesse that inspired affection and to a bourgeoisie that could conceive it. Such noble and magnificent devotion is no longer possible among us. Noble houses have no servitors; France no longer has a King, hereditary peerage, or land irrevocably bound to historic houses to perpetuate the nation’s glorious names. Chesnel was not merely an obscure great man of private life; he was a great fact. Is there not something indefinably solemn and sublime in his sustained self-devotion, rising above a single beneficent deed or a moment’s supreme effort? Chesnel’s virtues belonged to classes between the people’s poverty and the aristocracy’s greatness, combining homely burgher virtues with heroic noble ideals enlightened by solid education.

Victurnien was ill regarded at Court; there was no chance of a great match or office. His Majesty refused to raise the d’Esgrignons to the peerage — the one royal favor that might have rescued him from his wretched position. He could not marry a bourgeois heiress during his father’s life, so he lived shabbily under the paternal roof, haunted by memories of two years’ splendor in Paris and the lost love of a great lady. He grew moody and depressed, vegetating with a careworn aunt and a half-broken father who blamed a wasting malady. Chesnel was gone.

The Marquis died in 1830. The great d’Esgrignon, followed by the less infirm noblesse of the Collection of Antiquities, went to wait upon Charles X at Nonancourt, swelling the meager train of the fallen king — an act then seeming heroism amid enthusiastic revolt.

“The Gaul has conquered!” were the Marquis’ last words.

By that time du Croisier’s triumph was complete. A week after his father’s death the new Marquis d’Esgrignon married Mlle. Duval. His bride brought three million francs; du Croisier and his wife settled the reversion of their fortunes upon her in the marriage contract. During the ceremony du Croisier declared the d’Esgrignon family the most honorable of all the ancient houses in France.

Someday the present Marquis will have an income exceeding a hundred thousand crowns. You may see him in Paris; he comes every winter and leads a jolly bachelor life, treating his wife with more than the indifference of the grand seigneur of olden times — he takes no thought for her.

“As for Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” said Emile Blondet, source of these details, “if she is no longer like the divinely fair woman whom I saw by glimpses in my childhood, she is, at sixty-seven, the most pathetic and interesting figure in the Collection of Antiquities. She queens it among them still. I saw her on my last journey home to fetch the necessary papers for my marriage. When my father learned whom I had married he was struck dumb until I told him I was a prefect.

“‘You were born to it,’ he said, with a smile.

“As I took a walk around the town I met Mlle. Armande; she seemed taller than ever. I thought of Marius among the ruins of Carthage. Had she not outlived her creed and the beliefs that time destroyed? She is a sad and silent woman, with nothing of her old beauty left except her eyes, which shine with an unearthly light. I watched her on her way to mass, book in hand, and could not help thinking that she prayed to be taken out of the world.”

LES JARDIES, July 1837.
