DEDICATION

To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author
of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron,--You have taken such warm interest in my long, vast
“History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century,” you have
given me so much encouragement to persevere with my work, that you
have given me a right to associate your name with some portion of
it. Are you not one of the most important representatives of
conscientious, studious Germany? Will not your approval win for me
the approval of others, and protect this attempt of mine? So proud
am I to have gained your good opinion, that I have striven to
deserve it by continuing my labors with the unflagging courage
characteristic of your methods of study, and of that exhaustive
research among documents without which you could never have given
your monumental work to the world of letters. Your sympathy with
such labor as you yourself have bestowed upon the most brilliant
civilization of the East has often sustained my ardor through
nights of toil given to the details of our modern civilization.
And will not you, whose naive kindliness can only be compared with
that of our own La Fontaine, be glad to know of this?

May this token of my respect for you and your work find you at
Dobling, dear Baron, and put you and yours in mind of one of your
most sincere admirers and friends.

DE BALZAC.

There stands a house at a corner of a street, in the middle of a town,
in one of the least important prefectures in France, but the name of the
street and the name of the town must be suppressed here. Everyone will
appreciate the motives of this reticence demanded by convention;
for if a writer takes upon himself the office of annalist of his own
time, he is bound to touch on many sore subjects. The house was called
the Hotel d’Esgrignon; but let d’Esgrignon be considered a mere
fancy name, neither more nor less connected with real people than
the conventional Belval, Floricour, or Derville of the stage, or
the Adalberts and Mombreuses of romance. After all, the names of the
principal characters will be disguised; though in this history the
chronicler would prefer to conceal the facts under a mass of contradictions,
anachronisms, improbabilities, and absurdities, the truth will out
in spite of him. You uproot a vine-stock, as you imagine, and the stem
will send up lusty shoots after you have ploughed your vineyard over.

The “Hotel d’Esgrignon” was nothing more nor less than the house in
which the old Marquis lived; or, in the style of ancient documents,
Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol, Marquis d’Esgrignon. It was only an
ordinary house, but the townspeople and tradesmen had begun by calling
it the Hotel d’Esgrignon in jest, and ended after a score of years by
giving it that name in earnest.

The name of Carol, or Karawl, as the Thierrys would have spelt it, was
glorious among the names of the most powerful chieftains of the Northmen
who conquered Gaul and established the feudal system there. Never had
Carol bent his head before King or Communes, the Church or Finance.
Entrusted in the days of yore with the keeping of a French March, the
title of marquis in their family meant no shadow of imaginary office; it
had been a post of honor with duties to discharge. Their fief had always
been their domain. Provincial nobles were they in every sense of the
word; they might boast of an unbroken line of great descent; they had
been neglected by the court for two hundred years; they were lords
paramount in the estates of a province where the people looked up to
them with superstitious awe. The house of d’Esgrignon, buried in its remote
border country, was preserved as the charred piles of one of Caesar’s
bridges are maintained intact in a river bed. For thirteen hundred years
the daughters of the house had been married without a dowry or taken the
veil; the younger sons of every generation had been content with their
share of their mother’s dower and gone forth to be captains or bishops;
some had made a marriage at court; one cadet of the house became an
admiral, a duke, and a peer of France, and died without issue. Never
would the Marquis d’Esgrignon of the elder branch accept the title of
duke.

“I hold my marquisate as His Majesty holds the realm of France, and on
the same conditions,” he told the Constable de Luynes, a very paltry
fellow in his eyes at that time.

You may be sure that d’Esgrignons lost their heads on the scaffold
during the troubles. The old blood showed itself proud and high even in
1789. The Marquis of that day would not emigrate; he was answerable for
his March. The reverence in which he was held by the countryside saved
his head; but the hatred of the genuine sans-culottes was strong enough
to compel him to pretend to fly, and for a while he lived in hiding.
Then, in the name of the Sovereign People, the d’Esgrignon lands were
dishonored by the District, and the woods sold by the Nation in spite
of the personal protest made by the Marquis, then turned forty. Mlle.
d’Esgrignon, his half-sister, saved some portions of the fief, thanks to
the young steward of the family, who claimed on her behalf the partage
de presuccession, which is to say, the right of a relative to a portion
of the emigre’s lands. To Mlle. d’Esgrignon, therefore, the Republic
made over the castle itself and a few farms. Chesnel [Choisnel], the
faithful steward, was obliged to buy in his own name the church, the
parsonage house, the castle gardens, and other places to which his
patron was attached--the Marquis advancing the money.

The slow, swift years of the Terror went by, and the Marquis, whose
character had won the respect of the whole country, decided that he and
his sister ought to return to the castle and improve the property which
Maitre Chesnel--for he was now a notary--had contrived to save for them
out of the wreck. Alas! was not the plundered and dismantled castle all
too vast for a lord of the manor shorn of all his ancient rights; too
large for the landowner whose woods had been sold piecemeal, until he
could scarce draw nine thousand francs of income from the pickings of
his old estates?

It was in the month of October 1800 that Chesnel brought the Marquis
back to the old feudal castle, and saw with deep emotion, almost beyond
his control, his patron standing in the midst of the empty courtyard,
gazing round upon the moat, now filled up with rubbish, and the castle
towers razed to the level of the roof. The descendant of the Franks
looked for the missing Gothic turrets and the picturesque weather vanes
which used to rise above them; and his eyes turned to the sky, as if
asking of heaven the reason of this social upheaval. No one but Chesnel
could understand the profound anguish of the great d’Esgrignon, now
known as Citizen Carol. For a long while the Marquis stood in silence,
drinking in the influences of the place, the ancient home of his
forefathers, with the air that he breathed; then he flung out a most
melancholy exclamation.

“Chesnel,” he said, “we will come back again some day when the troubles
are over; I could not bring myself to live here until the edict of
pacification has been published; _they_ will not allow me to set my
scutcheon on the wall.”

He waved his hand toward the castle, mounted his horse, and rode
back beside his sister, who had driven over in the notary’s shabby
basket-chaise.

The Hotel d’Esgrignon in the town had been demolished; a couple of
factories now stood on the site of the aristocrat’s house. So Maitre
Chesnel spent the Marquis’ last bag of louis on the purchase of the
old-fashioned building in the square, with its gables, weather-vane,
turret, and dovecote. Once it had been the courthouse of the bailiwick,
and subsequently the presidial; it had belonged to the d’Esgrignons
from generation to generation; and now, in consideration of five hundred
louis d'or, the present owner made it over with the title given by the
Nation to its rightful lord. And so, half in jest, half in earnest, the
old house was christened the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

In 1800 little or no difficulty was made over erasing names from the
fatal list, and some few emigres began to return. Among the very first
nobles to come back to the old town were the Baron de Nouastre and his
daughter. They were completely ruined. M. d’Esgrignon generously offered
them the shelter of his roof; and in his house, two months later, the
Baron died, worn out with grief. The Nouastres came of the best blood
in the province; Mlle. de Nouastre was a girl of two-and-twenty; the
Marquis d’Esgrignon married her to continue his line. But she died in
childbirth, a victim to the unskilfulness of her physician, leaving,
most fortunately, a son to bear the name of the d’Esgrignons. The old
Marquis--he was but fifty-three, but adversity and sharp distress had
added months to every year--the poor old Marquis saw the death of the
loveliest of human creatures, a noble woman in whom the charm of the
feminine figures of the sixteenth century lived again, a charm now lost
save to men’s imaginations. With her death the joy died out of his old
age. It was one of those terrible shocks which reverberate through every
moment of the years that follow. For a few moments he stood beside the
bed where his wife lay, with her hands folded like a saint, then he
kissed her on the forehead, turned away, drew out his watch, broke the
mainspring, and hung it up beside the hearth. It was eleven o’clock in
the morning.

“Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” he said, “let us pray God that this hour may not
prove fatal yet again to our house. My uncle the archbishop was murdered
at this hour; at this hour also my father died----”

He knelt down beside the bed and buried his face in the coverlet; his
sister did the same, in another moment they both rose to their feet.
Mlle. d’Esgrignon burst into tears; but the old Marquis looked with dry
eyes at the child, round the room, and again on his dead wife. To the
stubbornness of the Frank he united the fortitude of a Christian.

These things came to pass in the second year of the nineteenth century.
Mlle. d’Esgrignon was then twenty-seven years of age. She was a
beautiful woman. An ex-contractor for forage to the armies of the
Republic, a man of the district, with an income of six thousand francs,
persuaded Chesnel to carry a proposal of marriage to the lady. The
Marquis and his sister were alike indignant with such presumption in
their man of business, and Chesnel was almost heartbroken; he could not
forgive himself for yielding to the Sieur du Croisier’s blandishments. The Marquis’ manner with his old servant changed
somewhat; never again was there quite the old affectionate kindliness,
which might almost have been taken for friendship. From that time forth
the Marquis was grateful, and his magnanimous and sincere gratitude
continually wounded the poor notary’s feelings. To some sublime natures
gratitude seems an excessive payment; they would rather have that sweet
equality of feeling which springs from similar ways of thought, and the
blending of two spirits by their own choice and will. And Maitre Chesnel
had known the delights of such high friendship; the Marquis had raised
him to his own level. The old noble looked on the good notary as
something more than a servant, something less than a child; he was the
voluntary liege man of the house, a serf bound to his lord by all the
ties of affection. There was no balancing of obligations; the sincere
affection on either side put them out of the question.

In the eyes of the Marquis, Chesnel’s official dignity was as nothing;
his old servitor was merely disguised as a notary. As for Chesnel, the
Marquis was now, as always, a being of a divine race; he believed in
nobility; he did not blush to remember that his father had thrown open
the doors of the salon to announce that “My Lord Marquis is served.”
His devotion to the fallen house was due not so much to his creed as to
egoism; he looked on himself as one of the family. So his vexation was
intense. Once he had ventured to allude to his mistake in spite of the
Marquis’ prohibition, and the old noble answered gravely--“Chesnel,
before the troubles you would not have permitted yourself to entertain
such injurious suppositions. What can these new doctrines be if they
have spoiled _you_?”

Maitre Chesnel had gained the confidence of the whole town; people
looked up to him; his high integrity and considerable fortune
contributed to make him a person of importance. From that time forth he
felt a very decided aversion for the Sieur du Crosier; and though there
was little rancor in his composition, he set others against the sometime
forage-contractor. Du Croisier, on the other hand, was a man to bear a
grudge and nurse a vengeance for a score of years. He hated Chesnel and
the d’Esgrignon family with the smothered, all-absorbing hate only to
be found in a country town. His rebuff had simply ruined him with the
malicious provincials among whom he had come to live, thinking to rule
over them. It was so real a disaster that he was not long in feeling the
consequences of it. He betook himself in desperation to a wealthy old
maid, and met with a second refusal. Thus failed the ambitious schemes
with which he had started. He had lost his hope of a marriage with Mlle.
d’Esgrignon, which would have opened the Faubourg Saint-Germain of the
province to him; and after the second rejection, his credit fell away
to such an extent that it was almost as much as he could do to keep his
position in the second rank.

In 1805, M. de la Roche-Guyon, the oldest son of an ancient family which
had previously intermarried with the d’Esgrignons, made proposals in
form through Maitre Chesnel for Mlle. Marie Armande Clair d’Esgrignon.
She declined to hear the notary.

“You must have guessed before now that I am a mother, dear Chesnel,” she
said; she had just put her nephew, a fine little boy of five, to bed.

The old Marquis rose and went up to his sister, but just returned from
the cradle; he kissed her hand reverently, and as he sat down again,
found words to say:

“My sister, you are a d’Esgrignon.”

A quiver ran through the noble girl; the tears stood in her eyes. M.
d’Esgrignon, the father of the present Marquis, had married a second
wife, the daughter of a farmer of taxes ennobled by Louis XIV. It was
a shocking mesalliance in the eyes of his family, but fortunately of no
importance, since a daughter was the one child of the marriage. Armande
knew this. Kind as her brother had always been, he looked on her as a
stranger in blood. And this speech of his had just recognized her as one
of the family.

And was not her answer the worthy crown of eleven years of her noble
life? Her every action since she came of age had borne the stamp of the
purest devotion; love for her brother was a sort of religion with her.

“I shall die Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” she said simply, turning to the notary.

“For you there could be no fairer title,” returned Chesnel, meaning to
convey a compliment. Poor Mlle. d’Esgrignon reddened.

“You have blundered, Chesnel,” said the Marquis, flattered by the
steward’s words, but vexed that his sister had been hurt. “A d’Esgrignon
may marry a Montmorency; their descent is not so pure as ours. The
d’Esgrignons bear or, two bends, gules,” he continued, “and nothing
during nine hundred years has changed their scutcheon; as it was at
first, so it is to-day. Hence our device, Cil est nostre, taken at
a tournament in the reign of Philip Augustus, with the supporters, a
knight in armor or on the right, and a lion gules on the left.”

“I do not remember that any woman I have ever met has struck my
imagination as Mlle. d’Esgrignon did,” said Emile Blondet, to whom
contemporary literature is indebted for this history among other things.
“Truth to tell, I was a boy, a mere child at the time, and perhaps my
memory-pictures of her owe something of their vivid color to a boy’s
natural turn for the marvelous.

“If I was playing with other children on the Parade, and she came to
walk there with her nephew Victurnien, the sight of her in the distance
thrilled me with very much the effect of galvanism on a dead body. Child
as I was, I felt as though new life had been given me.

“Mlle. Armande had hair of tawny gold; there was a delicate fine down on
her cheek, with a silver gleam upon it which I loved to catch, putting
myself so that I could see the outlines of her face lit up by the
daylight, and feel the fascination of those dreamy emerald eyes, which
sent a flash of fire through me whenever they fell upon my face. I used
to pretend to roll on the grass before her in our games, only to try
to reach her little feet, and admire them on a closer view. The soft
whiteness of her skin, her delicate features, the clearly cut lines of
her forehead, the grace of her slender figure, took me with a sense of
surprise, while as yet I did not know that her shape was graceful,
nor her brows beautiful, nor the outline of her face a perfect oval. I
admired as children pray at that age, without too clearly understanding
why they pray. When my piercing gaze attracted her notice, when she
asked me (in that musical voice of hers, with more volume in it, as it
seemed to me, than all other voices), ‘What are you doing little one?
Why do you look at me?’--I used to come nearer and wriggle and bite my
finger-nails, and redden and say, ‘I do not know.’ And if she chanced
to stroke my hair with her white hand, and ask me how old I was, I would
run away and call from a distance, ‘Eleven!’

“Every princess and fairy of my visions, as I read the Arabian Nights,
looked and walked like Mlle. d’Esgrignon; and afterwards, when my
drawing-master gave me heads from the antique to copy, I noticed that
their hair was braided like Mlle. d’Esgrignon’s. Still later, when the
foolish fancies had vanished one by one, Mlle. Armande remained vaguely
in my memory as a type; that Mlle. Armande for whom men made way
respectfully, following the tall brown-robed figure with their eyes
along the Parade and out of sight. Her exquisitely graceful form, the
rounded curves sometimes revealed by a chance gust of wind, and always
visible to my eyes in spite of the ample folds of stuff, revisited
my young man’s dreams. Later yet, when I came to think seriously over
certain mysteries of human thought, it seemed to me that the feeling
of reverence was first inspired in me by something expressed in Mlle.
d’Esgrignon’s face and bearing. The wonderful calm of her face, the
suppressed passion in it, the dignity of her movements, the saintly life
of duties fulfilled,--all this touched and awed me. Children are more
susceptible than people imagine to the subtle influences of ideas;
they never make game of real dignity; they feel the charm of real
graciousness, and beauty attracts them, for childhood itself is
beautiful, and there are mysterious ties between things of the same
nature.

“Mlle. d’Esgrignon was one of my religions. To this day I can never
climb the staircase of some old manor-house but my foolish imagination
must needs picture Mlle. Armande standing there, like the spirit of
feudalism. I can never read old chronicles but she appears before my
eyes in the shape of some famous woman of old times; she is Agnes Sorel,
Marie Touchet, Gabrielle; and I lend her all the love that was lost in
her heart, all the love that she never expressed. The angel shape seen
in glimpses through the haze of childish fancies visits me now sometimes
across the mists of dreams.”

Keep this portrait in mind; it is a faithful picture and sketch of
character. Mlle. d’Esgrignon is one of the most instructive figures in
this story; she affords an example of the mischief that may be done by
the purest goodness for lack of intelligence.

Two-thirds of the emigres returned to France during 1804 and 1805, and
almost every exile from the Marquis d’Esgrignon’s province came back to
the land of his fathers. There were certainly defections. Men of good
birth entered the service of Napoleon, and went into the army or held
places at the Imperial court, and others made alliances with the upstart
families. All those who cast in their lots with the Empire retrieved
their fortunes and recovered their estates, thanks to the Emperor’s
munificence; and these for the most part went to Paris and stayed there.
But some eight or nine families still remained true to the proscribed
noblesse and loyal to the fallen monarchy. The La Roche-Guyons,
Nouastres, Verneuils, Casterans, Troisvilles, and the rest were some of
them rich, some of them poor; but money, more or less, scarcely counted
for anything among them. They took an antiquarian view of themselves;
for them the age and preservation of the pedigree was the one
all-important matter. Of these families, the Marquis
d’Esgrignon was the acknowledged head. His house became their cenacle.
There His Majesty, Emperor and King, was never anything but “M. de
Bonaparte”; there “the King” meant Louis XVIII., then at Mittau;
there the Department was still the Province, and the prefecture the
intendance.

The Marquis was honored among them for his admirable behavior, his
loyalty as a noble, his undaunted courage; even as he was respected
throughout the town for his misfortunes, his fortitude, his steadfast
adherence to his political convictions. The man so admirable in
adversity was invested with all the majesty of ruined greatness. His
chivalrous fair-mindedness was so well known, that litigants many a
time had referred their disputes to him for arbitration. All gently bred
Imperialists and the authorities themselves showed as much indulgence
for his prejudices as respect for his personal character; but there was
another and a large section of the new society which was destined to
be known after the Restoration as the Liberal party; and these, with du
Croisier as their unacknowledged head, laughed at an aristocratic oasis
which nobody might enter without proof of irreproachable descent. Their
animosity was all the more bitter because honest country squires and the
higher officials, with a good many worthy folk in the town, were of the
opinion that all the best society thereof was to be found in the Marquis
d’Esgrignon’s salon. The prefect himself, the Emperor’s chamberlain,
made overtures to the d’Esgrignons, humbly sending his wife (a
Grandlieu) as ambassadress.

Wherefore, those excluded from the miniature provincial Faubourg
Saint-Germain nicknamed the salon “The Collection of Antiquities,”
and called the Marquis himself “M. Carol.” The receiver of taxes,
for instance, addressed his applications to “M. Carol (ci-devant des
Grignons),” maliciously adopting the obsolete way of spelling.

“For my own part,” said Emile Blondet, “if I try to recall my childhood
memories, I remember that the nickname of ‘Collection of Antiquities’
always made me laugh, in spite of my respect--my love, I ought to
say--for Mlle. d’Esgrignon. The Hotel d’Esgrignon stood at the angle of
two of the busiest thoroughfares in the town, and not five hundred paces
away from the market place. Two of the drawing-room windows looked upon
the street and two upon the square; the room was like a glass cage,
everyone who came past could look through it from side to side. I was
only a boy of twelve at the time, but I thought, even then, that the
salon was one of those rare curiosities which seem, when you come to
think of them afterwards, to lie just on the borderland between reality
and dreams, so that you can scarcely tell to which side they most
belong.

“The room, the ancient Hall of Audience, stood above a row of cellars
with grated air-holes, once the prison cells of the old court-house,
now converted into a kitchen. I do not know that the magnificent lofty
chimney-piece of the Louvre, with its marvelous carving, seemed more
wonderful to me than the vast open hearth of the salon d’Esgrignon when
I saw it for the first time. It was covered like a melon with a network
of tracery. Over it stood an equestrian portrait of Henri III., under
whom the ancient duchy of appanage reverted to the crown; it was a great
picture executed in low relief, and set in a carved and gilded frame.
The ceiling spaces between the chestnut cross-beams in the fine old
roof were decorated with scroll-work patterns; there was a little faded
gilding still left along the angles. The walls were covered with Flemish
tapestry, six scenes from the Judgment of Solomon, framed in golden
garlands, with satyrs and cupids playing among the leaves. The parquet
floor had been laid down by the present Marquis, and Chesnel had picked
up the furniture at sales of the wreckage of old chateaux between
1793 and 1795; so that there were Louis Quatorze consoles, tables,
clock-cases, andirons, candle-sconces and tapestry-covered chairs, which
marvelously completed a stately room, large out of all proportion to the
house. Luckily, however, there was an equally lofty ante-chamber,
the ancient Salle des Pas Perdus of the presidial, which communicated
likewise with the magistrate’s deliberating chamber, used by the
d’Esgrignons as a dining-room.

“Beneath the old paneling, amid the threadbare braveries of a bygone
day, some eight or ten dowagers were drawn up in state in a quavering
line; some with palsied heads, others dark and shriveled like mummies;
some erect and stiff, others bowed and bent, but all of them tricked out
in more or less fantastic costumes as far as possible removed from
the fashion of the day, with be-ribboned caps above their curled and
powdered ‘heads,’ and old discolored lace. No painter however earnest,
no caricature however wild, ever caught the haunting fascination of
those aged women; they come back to me in dreams; their puckered faces
shape themselves in my memory whenever I meet an old woman who puts
me in mind of them by some faint resemblance of dress or feature. And
whether it is that misfortune has initiated me into the secrets of
irremediable and overwhelming disaster; whether that I have come to
understand the whole range of human feelings, and, best of all, the
thoughts of Old Age and Regret; whatever the reason, nowhere and never
again have I seen among the living or in the faces of the dying the wan
look of certain gray eyes that I remember, nor the dreadful brightness
of others that were black.

“Neither Hoffmann nor Maturin, the two weirdest imaginations of our
time, ever gave me such a thrill of terror as I used to feel when I
watched the automaton movements of those bodies sheathed in whalebone.
The paint on actors’ faces never caused me a shock; I could see below it
the rouge in grain, the rouge de naissance, to quote a comrade at least
as malicious as I can be. Years had leveled those women’s faces, and
at the same time furrowed them with wrinkles, till they looked like the
heads on wooden nutcrackers carved in Germany. Peeping in through the
window-panes, I gazed at the battered bodies, and ill-jointed limbs
(how they were fastened together, and, indeed, their whole anatomy was
a mystery I never attempted to explain); I saw the lantern jaws,
the protuberant bones, the abnormal development of the hips; and the
movements of these figures as they came and went seemed to me no whit
less extraordinary than their sepulchral immobility as they sat round
the card-tables.

“The men looked gray and faded like the ancient tapestries on the wall,
in dress they were much more like the men of the day, but even they
were not altogether convincingly alive. Their white hair, their withered
waxen-hued faces, their devastated foreheads and pale eyes, revealed
their kinship to the women, and neutralized any effects of reality
borrowed from their costume.

“The very certainty of finding all these folk seated at or among the
tables every day at the same hours invested them at length in my eyes
with a sort of spectacular interest as it were; there was something
theatrical, something unearthly about them.

“Whenever, in after times, I have gone through museums of old furniture
in Paris, London, Munich, or Vienna, with the gray-headed custodian
who shows you the splendors of time past, I have peopled the rooms with
figures from the Collection of Antiquities. Often, as little schoolboys
of eight or ten we used to propose to go and take a look at the
curiosities in their glass cage, for the fun of the thing. But as soon
as I caught sight of Mlle. Armande’s sweet face, I used to tremble;
and there was a trace of jealousy in my admiration for the lovely child
Victurnien, who belonged, as we all instinctively felt, to a different
and higher order of being from our own. It struck me as something
indescribably strange that the young fresh creature should be there in
that cemetery awakened before the time. We could not have explained
our thoughts to ourselves, yet we felt that we were bourgeois and
insignificant in the presence of that proud court.”

The disasters of 1813 and 1814, which brought about the downfall of
Napoleon, gave new life to the Collection of Antiquities, and what was
more than life, the hope of recovering their past importance; but
the events of 1815, the troubles of the foreign occupation, and the
vacillating policy of the Government until the fall of M. Decazes,
all contributed to defer the fulfilment of the expectations of the
personages so vividly described by Blondet. This story, therefore, only
begins to shape itself in 1822.

In 1822 the Marquis d’Esgrignon’s fortunes had not improved in spite of
the changes worked by the Restoration in the condition of emigres. Of
all the nobles hardly hit by Revolutionary legislation, his case was the
hardest. Like other great families, the d’Esgrignons before 1789 derived
the greater part of their income from their rights as lords of the manor
in the shape of dues paid by those who held of them; and, naturally, the
old seigneurs had reduced the size of the holdings in order to swell the
amounts paid in quit-rents and heriots. Families in this position were
hopelessly ruined. They were not affected by the ordinance by which
Louis XVIII. put the emigres into possession of such of their lands as
had not been sold; and at a later date it was impossible that the law of
indemnity should indemnify them. Their suppressed rights, as everybody
knows, were revived in the shape of a land tax known by the very name of
domaines, but the money went into the coffers of the State.

The Marquis by his position belonged to that small section of the
Royalist party which would hear of no kind of compromise with those whom
they styled, not Revolutionaries, but revolted subjects, or, in
more parliamentary language, they had no dealings with Liberals or
Constitutionnels. Such Royalists, nicknamed Ultras by the opposition,
took for leaders and heroes those courageous orators of the Right,
who from the very beginning attempted, with M. de Polignac, to protest
against the charter granted by Louis XVIII. This they regarded as
an ill-advised edict extorted from the Crown by the necessity of the
moment, only to be annulled later on. And, therefore, so far from
co-operating with the King to bring about a new condition of things, the
Marquis d’Esgrignon stood aloof, an upholder of the straitest sect of
the Right in politics, until such time as his vast fortune should
be restored to him. Nor did he so much as admit the thought of the
indemnity which filled the minds of the Villele ministry, and formed a
part of a design of strengthening the Crown by putting an end to those
fatal distinctions of ownership which still lingered on in spite of
legislation.

The miracles of the Restoration of 1814, the still greater miracle
of Napoleon’s return in 1815, the portents of a second flight of the
Bourbons, and a second reinstatement, all these things took the Marquis by surprise at
the age of sixty-seven. At that time of life, the most high-spirited
men of their age were not so much vanquished as worn out in the
struggle with the Revolution; their activity, in their remote provincial
retreats, had turned into a passionately held and immovable conviction;
and almost all of them were shut in by the enervating, easy round of
daily life in the country. Could worse luck befall a political party
than this--to be represented by old men at a time when its ideas are
already stigmatized as old-fashioned?

When the legitimate sovereign appeared to be firmly seated on the throne
again in 1818, the Marquis asked himself what a man of seventy should
do at court; and what duties, what office he could discharge there?
The noble and high-minded d’Esgrignon was fain to be content with the
triumph of the Monarchy and Religion, while he waited for the results of
that unhoped-for, indecisive victory, which proved to be simply an armistice.
He continued as before, lord-paramount of his salon, so felicitously
named the Collection of Antiquities.

But when the victors of 1793 became the vanquished in their turn, the
nickname given at first in jest began to be used in bitter earnest.
The town was no more free than other country towns from the hatreds
and jealousies bred of party spirit. Du Croisier, contrary to all
expectation, married the old maid who had refused him at first; carrying
her off from his rival, the darling of the aristocratic quarter, a
certain Chevalier whose illustrious name will be sufficiently hidden by
suppressing it altogether. Now, not only had that marriage produced a war after the provincial
manner, in which all weapons are fair; it had hastened the separation of
the great and little noblesse, of the aristocratic and bourgeois social
elements, which had been united for a little space by the heavy weight
of Napoleonic rule. After the pressure was removed, there followed that
sudden revival of class divisions which did so much harm to the country.

The most national of all sentiments in France is vanity. The wounded
vanity of the many induced a thirst for Equality; though, as the most
ardent innovator will some day discover, Equality is an impossibility.
The Royalists pricked the Liberals in the most sensitive spots, and
this happened specially in the provinces, where either party accused the
other of unspeakable atrocities. In those days the blackest deeds were
done in politics, to secure public opinion on one side or the other, to
catch the votes of that public of fools which holds up hands for those
that are clever enough to serve out weapons to them. Individuals are
identified with their political opinions, and opponents in public life
forthwith became private enemies. It is very difficult in a country town
to avoid a man-to-man conflict of this kind over interests or questions
which in Paris appear in a more general and theoretical form, with
the result that political combatants also rise to a higher level; M.
Laffitte, for example, or M. Casimir-Perier can respect M. de Villele
or M. de Payronnet as a man. M. Laffitte, who drew the fire on the
Ministry, would have given them an asylum in his house if they had fled
thither on the 29th of July 1830. Benjamin Constant sent a copy of his
work on Religion to the Vicomte de Chateaubriand, with a flattering
letter acknowledging benefits received from the former Minister. At
Paris men are systems, whereas in the provinces systems are identified
with men; men, moreover, with restless passions, who must always
confront one another, always spy upon each other in private life, and
pull their opponents’ speeches to pieces, and live generally like two
duelists on the watch for a chance to thrust six inches of steel between
an antagonist’s ribs. Each must do his best to get under his enemy’s
guard, and a political hatred becomes as all-absorbing as a duel to the
death. Epigram and slander are used against individuals to bring the
party into discredit.

In such warfare as this, waged ceremoniously and without rancor on the
side of the Antiquities, while du Croisier’s faction went so far as to
use the poisoned weapons of savages--in this warfare the advantages
of wit and delicate irony lay on the side of the nobles. But it should
never be forgotten that the wounds made by the tongue and the eyes, by
gibe or slight, are the last of all to heal. When the Chevalier turned
his back on mixed society and entrenched himself on the Mons Sacer
of the aristocracy, his witticisms thenceforward were directed at du
Croisier’s salon; he stirred up the fires of war, not knowing how far
the spirit of revenge was to urge the rival faction. None but purists
and loyal gentlemen and women sure one of another entered the Hotel
d’Esgrignon; they committed no indiscretions of any kind; they had
their ideas, true or false, good or bad, noble or trivial, but there
was nothing to laugh at in all this. If the Liberals meant to make the
nobles ridiculous, they were obliged to fasten on the political actions
of their opponents; while the intermediate party, composed of officials
and others who paid court to the higher powers, kept the nobles informed
of all that was done and said in the Liberal camp, and much of it was
abundantly laughable. Du Croisier’s adherents smarted under a sense of
inferiority, which increased their thirst for revenge.

In 1822, du Croisier put himself at the head of the manufacturing
interest of the province, as the Marquis d’Esgrignon headed the
noblesse. Each represented his party. But du Croisier, instead of giving
himself out frankly for a man of the extreme Left, ostensibly adopted
the opinions formulated at a later date by the 221 deputies.

By taking up this position, he could keep in touch with the magistrates
and local officials and the capitalists of the department. Du Croisier’s
salon, a power at least equal to the salon d’Esgrignon, larger
numerically, as well as younger and more energetic, made itself felt all
over the countryside; the Collection of Antiquities, on the other hand,
remained inert, a passive appendage, as it were, of a central authority
which was often embarrassed by its own partisans; for not merely did
they encourage the Government in a mistaken policy, but some of its most
fatal blunders were made in consequence of the pressure brought to bear
upon it by the Conservative party.

The Liberals, so far, had never contrived to carry their candidate. The
department declined to obey their command knowing that du Croisier, if
elected, would take his place on the Left Centre benches, and as far
as possible to the Left. Du Croisier was in correspondence with the
Brothers Keller, the bankers, the oldest of whom shone conspicuous among
“the nineteen deputies of the Left,” that phalanx made famous by the
efforts of the entire Liberal press. This same M. Keller, moreover, was
related by marriage to the Comte de Gondreville, a Constitutional
peer who remained in favor with Louis XVIII. For these reasons, the
Constitutional Opposition (as distinct from the Liberal party) was
always prepared to vote at the last moment, not for the candidate whom
they professed to support, but for du Croisier, if that worthy could
succeed in gaining a sufficient number of Royalist votes; but at every
election du Croisier was regularly thrown out by the Royalists. The
leaders of that party, taking their tone from the Marquis d’Esgrignon,
had pretty thoroughly fathomed and gauged their man; and with each
defeat, du Croisier and his party waxed more bitter. Nothing so
effectually stirs up strife as the failure of some snare set with
elaborate pains.

In 1822 there seemed to be a lull in hostilities which had been kept up
with great spirit during the first four years of the Restoration. The
salon du Croisier and the salon d’Esgrignon, having measured their
strength and weakness, were in all probability waiting for opportunity,
that Providence of party strife. Ordinary persons were content with
the surface quiet which deceived the Government; but those who knew du
Croisier better, were well aware that the passion of revenge in him, as
in all men whose whole life consists in mental activity, is implacable,
especially when political ambitions are involved. About this time
du Croisier, who used to turn white and red at the bare mention
of d’Esgrignon or the Chevalier, and shuddered at the name of the
Collection of Antiquities, chose to wear the impassive countenance of
a savage. He smiled upon his enemies, hating them but the more deeply,
watching them the more narrowly from hour to hour. One of his own party,
who seconded him in these calculations of cold wrath, was the President
of the Tribunal, M. du Ronceret, a little country squire, who had vainly
endeavored to gain admittance among the Antiquities.

The d’Esgrignons’ little fortune, carefully administered by Maitre
Chesnel, was barely sufficient for the worthy Marquis’ needs; for though
he lived without the slightest ostentation, he also lived like a noble.
The governor found by his Lordship the Bishop for the hope of the house,
the young Comte Victurnien d’Esgrignon, was an elderly Oratorian who
must be paid a certain salary, although he lived with the family. The
wages of a cook, a waiting-woman for Mlle. Armande, an old valet for
M. le Marquis, and a couple of other servants, together with the daily
expenses of the household, and the cost of an education for which
nothing was spared, absorbed the whole family income, in spite of Mlle.
Armande’s economies, in spite of Chesnel’s careful management, and the
servants’ affection. As yet, Chesnel had not been able to set about
repairs at the ruined castle; he was waiting till the leases fell in to
raise the rent of the farms, for rents had been rising lately, partly
on account of improved methods of agriculture, partly by the fall in
the value of money, of which the landlord would get the benefit at the
expiration of leases granted in 1809.

The Marquis knew nothing of his household’s management or finances. Nearing the end of his life, he and his circle clung to the illusion that the d’Esgrignon house, neglected at Court and by government, would revive in his handsome heir Victurnien—who would go to Court, enter royal service, and marry into some illustrious family. Their intimates—the Troisvilles, La Roche-Guyon, the Casterans, and the Duc de Verneuil—encouraged this belief. It might have seemed plausible only if forty years of history could be erased; the restored monarchy and public opinion demanded not just noble birth but money, political relevance, and continuous prestige. The d’Esgrignons lacked the modern resources and uninterrupted prominence that now mattered; old renown could not substitute for wealth and influence. The Troisvilles’ daughter’s marriage to General Montcornet, rather than flattering antiquated pride, nearly split social circles—evidence of how times had changed.

Only Chesnel, the family’s old notary and former steward, saw the situation clearly. Devoted but practical, he had witnessed the Revolution’s violence and the triumph of new social ideas and vested interests. He understood that confiscations and deaths under the Revolution were binding facts and that the new order would not be overturned; his attachment to the d’Esgrignons was therefore affectionate and lucid. He tried tactfully to warn the Marquis, who met these cautions with a prophet’s smile and the conviction that history would repeat in their favor. Chesnel feared the damage the family’s superstitions and self-delusions would do to Victurnien’s character and habits.

Idolized by his father and aunt, Victurnien was thoroughly spoiled yet justified their hopes in some ways. Mlle. Armande loved him as a mother might but lacked maternal intuition and severity; she could not foresee danger as a real mother could. Chesnel’s pragmatic vigilance resembled a mother’s instinct more closely than the aunt’s blind adoration, but the household generally paid little heed to his counsels—he had even fallen into a kind of disgrace after proposing an imprudent marriage for the family.

Victurnien was taught from infancy to assume superiority: peers among great nobles, the King his only superior, and the rest of mankind beneath him. With beauty, tact, a quick mind, and thorough education, he had every advantage. He was of average height, graceful, an excellent horseman and marksman, and intellectually promising—qualities that might have made him a great man. But indulged and unopposed in youth, he became insolent, audacious, and self-willed. The Chevalier, a charming old roue, misled the boy by glorifying the reckless manners and witty immorality of an earlier era; the Chevalier’s jocular maxims mingled with the family’s rigid emphasis on honor. Thus good and bad counsel shaped Victurnien.

At eighteen, Victurnien entered society and quickly misread appearances. Accustomed to deference at home and to unguarded talk in the presence of an elderly father, he did not see the hypocrisies and practical cautions of the larger world. His reckless behavior—shooting over neighbors’ lands, amorous imprudences requiring settlements and legal cover-ups—led to lawsuits and scandals quietly hushed by Chesnel, who paid to avert public shame. Encouraged by adroit enemies—particularly Du Croisier, who plotted revenge against the family—Victurnien’s faults were fanned. President du Ronceret and other officials, while outwardly lenient, fostered liberal clamor and public suspicion to discredit the nobles; du Croisier hoped to drive the d’Esgrignons into ruin through the young man’s vices. A young instigator, Fabien du Ronceret, befriended Victurnien to lead him on.

Between eighteen and twenty-one, Victurnien ran up considerable debts—nearly eighty thousand francs paid out by Chesnel, who hid this from Mlle. Armande and the Marquis. The family’s mode of life left little margin: the Marquis’s income barely covered the household. Victurnien’s tailor bills, tastes for English saddles and carriages, livery, and fashionable accessories swallowed his allowance. Chesnel, with private savings (about twelve thousand livres yearly), had been the young man’s secret benefactor, paying off lawsuits and bailing him out repeatedly. Chesnel hoped to see Victurnien settle into a sensible marriage, but the young man’s temperament—capable of lofty thought yet habitually surrendering to impulse and pleasure—made him dangerously likely to repeat mistakes.

Chesnel’s patience and warnings met with shrugging complacency from the Chevalier and indulgence from the family. The Chevalier dismissed debt as par for a gentleman’s course, recounted extravagant examples of aristocratic generosity, and minimized the danger. Chesnel, however, foresaw ruin. His fear and affection prompted a decisive plan: to have Victurnien sent to Paris and presented at court, hoping the change would sober the boy and provide opportunities befitting his rank.

One autumn evening in 1822, after cards and salon talk, the household confronted the truth about Victurnien’s debts. The Chevalier forced the disclosure to the Marquis: Chesnel had lent an enormous sum—“something like a hundred thousand livres”—to the Count. The Marquis was mortified that his devoted steward would be the family’s creditor; pride and the old notions of dignity made it painful to ask Chesnel for help in a straightforward way. Nevertheless, they resolved to send Victurnien to Paris to present himself to the King and secure an appointment for him; they accepted Chesnel’s loan rather than humiliate the family by other means.

Chesnel explained that the Count’s companions and enemies in town—the president, Du Croisier and his circle—were seeking vengeance by ensnaring Victurnien, provoking scandals to ruin the family. He had, accordingly, borrowed money himself, mortgaging Le Jard, and had made arrangements in confidence so the loan would not appear as a direct family shame. Mlle. Armande and the Marquis were moved; Chesnel kissed her hand and, with paternal tenderness, urged that Paris provide the boy with more amusement and diversion than provincial society could, while also placing him where discretion and better company might discipline him.

Preparations were hurried. The Marquis and Mlle. Armande instructed Victurnien that he was to go to court, serve His Majesty, and there ascertain a career—army, navy, diplomacy, or royal household. They promised a modest provision and sent Josephin, Victurnien’s trusted valet, with him as companion. Chesnel wrote to an old notary friend in Paris, Maître Sorbier, begging vigilant oversight of the young man’s conduct and intimating funds would be placed at Sorbier’s disposition to support him. Chesnel’s letter revealed his deep emotional tie to the family: he felt he had reared and protected the d’Esgrignons through revolution and decline, and that the young Count’s fate was intimately his concern.

Victurnien accepted a monthly allowance of two thousand francs and left amid the household’s blessings. He felt no genuine regret; provincial life had been gentle but narrow, and Paris promised to amplify his vanity and gratify his tastes. In the capital he discovered the city’s scale and the fact that noble names no longer guaranteed power or employment; the Court’s attention was limited, and new social forces and money mattered most. Undeterred, he took to luxury and fashion as necessary to command respect and assumed a posture of independence that concealed need.

In Paris, he sought to recover a position he imagined due to his birth. He bought rooms, stables, and equerries, all at great expense. His family’s reputation and the Faubourg Saint-Germain’s curiosity opened many doors: old names still acted as social passports. He found a circle of fashionable young men—de Marsay, Rastignac, de Ronquerolles, Maxime de Trailles, and others—who moved easily in the world he coveted and initiated him into its pleasures. The Vidame de Pamiers, a discreet source of gossip and introductions in the Faubourg, welcomed Victurnien and took him to salons and dinners where he met writers and courtiers, and where the Vidame encouraged him toward conquest and social display.

At a dinner with the Vidame, de Marsay, Rastignac, and the young writer Emile Blondet, Victurnien encountered the pleasures and perils of Parisian society. The Vidame introduced him to Mlle. des Touches’ salon and to fashionable circles where literature and wit mixed with vanity and intrigue. The Vidame, who kept a finger on public taste, predicted the young Count’s rapid rise—and fall—if he pursued extremes.

Victurnien was introduced to the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, a dazzling and complex beauty who immediately attracted him and whom others envied. The Duchesse presented herself as an ethereal, virginal figure—an affectation that masked cleverness and self-regard. She resolved to play an innocence that would grant her influence, a role she adopted to conceal and manipulate her passions. Her carefully constructed purity, full of artful glances and guarded gestures, made her all the more alluring; she used the appearance of sanctity to amplify the power of her charm.

Thus Victurnien found himself at the center of fashionable life. His family’s name, his beauty, and the patronage of the Faubourg made him desirable; his vanity made him determined to sustain an image of wealth and consequence. Chesnel’s precautions were circumvented by the mechanics of Parisian finance and by notaries eager for patronage. The young Count’s expenses mounted: lodgings, dress, horses, and the cost of entering high society quickly devoured the money set aside. Despite Chesnel’s hopes that Paris would rescue him from provincial follies, the city offered new temptations, alliances, and risks that promised either brilliant ascent or irreversible ruin.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs, and neither Chesnel nor Mlle. Armande knew of it. He had also taken two thousand crowns from Chesnel at intervals to conceal his sources. He wrote deceitful letters to his poor father and aunt, who lived happily deceived. The insidious current of Paris life was preparing a dreadful catastrophe for the great house; only du Croisier knew. He rubbed his hands as he passed the Antiquities, hoping to secure not merely ruin but dishonor for the d’Esgrignons. He had been sure of it since discovering that the young Count’s burden of debt was growing too heavy.

Du Croisier’s first step was to rid himself of his most hated enemy, the good old notary Chesnel. The old man lived in the Rue du Bercail in a house with a little paved courtyard and a small garden. At half-past five he usually sat after dinner in his black leather-covered armchair, with a painted pasteboard contrivance protecting his stockinged legs from the fire; he often ate too much and enjoyed planning how his savings might round the d’Esgrignon estates. Chesnel’s affections were wrapped in that single attachment; he took pride in rebuilding the house and hoped to be remembered by future d’Esgrignons.

His old housekeeper entered with alarm. “M. du Croisier wanting to speak to you—” she said.

“M. du Croisier!” The name gave Chesnel a stab of cold; he dropped the tongs. Du Croisier came in, bowed, sat, and produced a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, principal and interest, for sums advanced to Victurnien in bills of exchange drawn upon du Croisier and honored by him. He demanded immediate payment, threatening proceedings against the heir-presumptive. Chesnel turned the unlucky letters over and begged secrecy; du Croisier agreed to keep the secret if paid within forty-eight hours. Pressed for money, Chesnel had obliged manufacturers and resorted to the usual financial fictions. His eyes were dim; he could scarcely keep back tears. The only way to raise the money was to mortgage his lands to their full value.

But when du Croisier learned Chesnel could not repay, he suddenly offered to buy the old lawyer’s property. The sale was completed within two days. Poor Chesnel could not bear the thought of the heir of the house undergoing five years’ imprisonment for debt; shortly nothing remained to him but his practice, the sums due to him, and the house in which he lived. Stripped of lands, he paced his oak-paneled office and thought no longer of Le Jard but of Victurnien.

“What will become of him? He ought to come back; they must marry him to some rich heiress,” he said, with dim eyes and a heavy head.

He did not know how to break the news to Mlle. Armande; the man who had paid the family’s debts quaked at the thought. He went from the Rue du Bercail to the Hotel d’Esgrignon with pulses throbbing like a girl’s, fearing the ruin of illusions.

Mlle. Armande had just received a charming, hypocritical letter. Her nephew was the happiest man alive: he had been to baths, traveled in Italy with Mme. de Maufrigneuse, and sent a journal full of love—Venice, Milan, Florence, the Apennines, Chiavari—painting happiness everywhere. The poor aunt was enthralled. She saw the far-off country of love, sat in Victurnien’s gondola, felt the Duchess’ hand in his; she drank in the letter. Repressed passion had ripened; she loved the couple ardently and excused a married woman’s love for Victurnien; to refuse it would have been unpardonable. Mlle. Armande was not an angelic type but a slender, serene figure; at this moment blue buds were unfolding in her saintly eyes. She loved them as apart from life.

Even in her Venetian reverie, someone appeared on the garden walk—Chesnel. The sound of his tread and the hopelessness on his face recalled her to herself.

“What is it?” she cried.

“All is lost!” said Chesnel. “M. le Comte will bring dishonor upon the house if we do not set it in order.” He held out the bills and described the recent agony in simple, touching words.

“He is deceiving us! The miserable boy!” cried Mlle. Armande, feeling her heart surge.

“Let us say mea culpa, mademoiselle,” Chesnel replied stoutly; “we have allowed him his own way; he needed stern guidance—he had no mother.” Fate sometimes deals terribly with a decaying noble house, she thought, with tears.

The Marquis came up; he had been reading the letter sent by his son from Italy. Victurnien’s itinerary was told from an aristocrat’s point of view: he had been magnificently welcomed by the great families, owed in part to his name and perhaps the Duchess. The old Marquis swung himself off with a coxcomb’s air, recounting ancestral glories. The two companions in unhappiness sat together on a garden bench, saying little, watching the father walk away in his contentment.

“What will become of him now?” Mlle. Armande asked.

“Du Croisier has sent instructions to the Kellers; he is not to be allowed to draw any more without authorization.”

“And there are debts.”

“I am afraid so.”

“If he is left without resources, what will he do?”

“I dare not answer that.”

“But he must be drawn out of that life; he must come back to us, or he will have nothing left.”

“And nothing else,” Chesnel said gloomily. Mlle. Armande could not yet understand the full force of his words.

“Is there any hope of getting him away from that woman—the Duchess? Perhaps she leads him on.”

“He would not stick at a crime to be with her,” Chesnel tried to suggest, preparing her for an intolerable thought.

“Crime!” she cried. “Oh, Chesnel, no one but you would think of such a thing!” she added with a withering look. “There is but one crime a noble can commit—the crime of high treason; and when he is beheaded, the block is covered with a black cloth, as for kings.”

“The times have changed,” said Chesnel, shaking his head. Victurnien had thinned the last of his white hairs. That thought soothed Mlle. Armande’s indignation, but she did not realize what Chesnel meant.

“To-morrow we will decide,” she said. “It needs thought. At worst, we have our lands.”

“Yes,” Chesnel replied. “You and M. le Marquis own the estate conjointly; the larger part is yours. You can raise money on it without saying a word to him.”

That evening players at whist and other games noticed Mlle. Armande’s agitation. “That poor heroic child!” said the old Marquise de Casteran; “she must be suffering still. A woman never knows what her sacrifices to her family may cost her.”

Next day it was arranged that Mlle. Armande should go to Paris to snatch her nephew from perdition. If any one could carry him off, it was the woman whose motherly heart yearned over him. She determined to go to the Duchess and tell her all. Some pretext was needed for the journey; for appearances Mlle. Armande allowed it to be thought she suffered from a complaint requiring consultation with celebrated physicians. Goodness knows whether the town talked of this! But she saw that far more than her reputation was at stake. She set out with Chesnel’s last bag of louis; she took it and her capuchine and mittens without comment. “Generous girl!” he said, putting her into the carriage with her maid.

Du Croisier had thought out his revenge as provincials do—meticulously, with every contingency provided for. Had he sounded Victurnien’s nature so well that he foresaw how easily the young Count would lend himself to his schemes? Or was he merely profiting on a long-watched opportunity? Who warned du Croisier of the moment—the Kellers, or President du Ronceret’s son finishing law studies in Paris?

Du Croisier wrote to Victurnien that the Kellers had been instructed to advance no more money; the letter was timed to arrive as the Duchess and the Count faced poverty cunningly hidden. In the letter informing Victurnien the Kellers would make no further advances without security, a wide blank space was left between the formal text and the signature. It was easy to tear off the upper part and convert the lower into a bill of exchange for any amount. The missive had been enclosed in an envelope so the other side of the sheet was blank. When it arrived, Victurnien was in the lowest despair. After two years of luxurious, thoughtless life, he found himself face to face with inexorable poverty: an absolute impossibility to procure money.

There had been throes before the journey’s end. With the Duchess’ help he had extorted various sums from bankers, but those very amounts were about to start up as overdue bills, with stern summonses from the Bank of France and the commercial court. All through the last weeks he had felt the point of the Commander’s sword: at every supper-party a creeping warning that the sirocco of debt was near. For five years his purse had always been replenished. After Chesnel came du Croisier (he thought), then surely another gold-mine would pour out its wealth. Besides, he was winning at play; luck had saved him unpleasant steps before; often a wild hope sent him to the Salon des Etrangers only to lose the winnings at whist. His life had become like Don Giovanni’s immortal finale—disorder, confusion, a frantic struggle to shut out debts and duels. He lived through this infernal poem alone, seeing visions of a friendless outcast reading the last words on the last page—THE END!

Yes; for him all would be at an end and soon. He already saw the ironical eyes of associates amusing themselves over his downfall. Some played high and could not spare a banknote for an intimate. There was no help—Chesnel must be ruined; he had devoured Chesnel’s living.

He sat with the Duchess in their box at the Italiens, the house envying them, and while he smiled the Furies were tearing at his heart. To give some sense of his despair: he had even taken out pistols and thought of suicide. He who would never brook insult was abusing himself in language no man hears except from himself.

He left du Croisier’s letter open on the bed. Josephin had brought it at nine. Victurnien’s furniture had been seized, but he slept none the less. After the Opera he and the Duchess had gone to a voluptuous retreat where they often spent hours after brilliant court balls. Appearances were cleverly saved; their love-nest seemed a garret to all but was luxuriously arranged within. Now on the brink of ruin, he longed to bid farewell to the dainty nest. Adversity would break the enchanted eggs; there would be no brood of white doves, no brilliant tropical birds of fancy. In three days he must be gone; his bills had been placed in the hands of money-lenders, and law proceedings had reached the last stage.

An evil thought crossed him. He would flee with the Duchess to some undiscovered nook in America, but with a fortune, leaving creditors to confront their bills. To carry out the plan he had only to cut off the lower portion of du Croisier’s letter bearing the signature and fill in figures to turn it into a bill, presenting it to the Kellers. There was a dreadful struggle with temptation; tears were shed; but the honor of the family triumphed—subject to one condition. Victurnien wanted to be sure of his beautiful Diane; he would do nothing unless she consented to their flight. So he went to the Duchess in the Rue Faubourg Saint-Honoré and found her in coquettish morning dress.

Mme. de Maufrigneuse was somewhat pensive. Cares gnawed her mind, but she met them gallantly. Of all feminine types, some combine strength and insight with a faculty for prompt decision and a resolution that would shake a man’s nerves; these powers lie beneath an appearance of graceful helplessness. Such women can ascend to the highest feelings or show selfish insensibility. The Duchess, pondering her fall, laid love aside to consider her safety: refuge abroad, confessing to the King, fascinating a du Tillet or a Nucingen, or gambling on the stock exchange—all were reviewed calmly. She could stand aloof and watch the crash as if a spectator instead of being buried beneath it. That power was great but repulsive.

While dressing for a drive she had collected her thoughts and faced the possibilities of terrific downfall. She plucked love out of her heart to consider necessities and was ready to set the passion back when her coronet was safe. She had none of Richelieu’s hesitation or Napoleon’s early doubts; she resolved.

Victurnien came in. With all his suppressed capacity he was in a state of terror; his heart beat violently; he was afraid to touch the cornerstone that upheld his life with Diane. The cleverest men sometimes deceive themselves to avoid self-humiliation. Victurnien committed himself.

“What is the matter?” Diane asked.

“I am hopelessly in debt. I have come to the end.”

“Pshaw! it is nothing,” she said, smiling. “Money can always be arranged; nothing is irretrievable except disasters in love.”

Relieved by her swift comprehension, he unrolled the bright web of his life’s past two years and a half—the seamy side—setting it off with delicate scorn for men and things. He spoke as an aristocrat; the Duchess listened.

She sat with a knee raised, elbow on knee, face in hand; her eyes never left him while a thousand thoughts flitted under their blue surface. Her forehead was calm, mouth intent—grave with love—her lips knotted by his. To have her listening thus seemed a divine love. When Victurnien proposed flight, he cried, “You are an angel!”

The Duchess made no immediate answer; her mind was absorbed in secret plans. “Good, very good,” she said at last. “But that is not the question, dear. Let us think of your affairs. Yes, we will go, and the sooner the better. Arrange it; I will follow. It is glorious to leave Paris and the world.”

When a duchess speaks such words the sacrifice is made. Diane’s promise settled him; he could conceal sordid details better because she did not inquire. Victurnien would not go till the promise was sealed; he needed courage from happiness before doing a deed that might be badly construed. He counted on his aunt and father and Chesnel to hush up the affair; Chesnel might think of some compromise. Besides, this “business” was the only way to raise money on the family estate. With three hundred thousand francs he and Diane would lead a happy hidden life in Venice.

Next day Victurnien drew a bill for three hundred thousand francs and took it to the Kellers. They advanced the money, for du Croisier happened then to have a balance; but they wrote that he must not draw again without notice. Du Croisier, much astonished, asked for a statement of accounts. It was sent. Everything was explained. The day of his vengeance had arrived.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
When Victurnien had withdrawn the money, he carried it to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who locked the banknotes in her desk and proposed they bid the world farewell by going to the Opera one last time. Victurnien was distracted; he worried that his place in the Duchess’ box might cost him dearly and thought it might be wiser, once the three hundred thousand francs were safe, to travel post to Chesnel and tell him everything. The Duchess, her eyes bright with the desire to linger once more in the nest she loved, gave him a look that made him lose a night’s rest.

At three the next day Victurnien returned to the Hotel de Maufrigneuse to learn the Duchess’ plan for that night’s escape. “Why should we go?” she asked. “The Vicomtesse de Beauseant and the Duchesse de Langeais disappeared; if I go too it will be commonplace. We will brave the storm; it will be finer. I am sure of success.” Victurnien felt as if his skin were dissolving; he hesitated.

Diane, noticing his hesitation, reproached him with the merciless coldness of a proud woman. A true tactful lover would have yielded, but Victurnien, stirred to sudden anger, could not. She accused him of ingratitude after eighteen months of tenderness and turned away, cold and implacable. He protested, urged that he had done much for her, and flung himself at her feet—pleading, weeping. For two hours she maintained a solemn agony; her silence and disdain were a deliberate, august punishment. Two more hours passed; her hand, when seized by the Count, felt as if lifeless. Victurnien fell into a stupor of despair; memories of past rapture floated by like pictures against a black background. The Duchess toyed with her scarf, spoke cruel epigrams about his rivals—Vandenesse, de Marsay, Montriveau—each example meant to wound and humiliate. Eventually, at the end of his patience, Victurnien cried, “You are mad!” and fled in a rage, driving recklessly.

As his horse bolted near the Quai d’Orsay and turned into the Rue de l’Universite, Josephin stopped him. “You cannot go home, sir,” the old servant said. “They have come with a warrant to arrest you.” In his agitation Victurnien imagined a criminal arrest, having forgotten the affair of the bills of exchange, which for days had been “stirred up” into orders to pay and legal proceedings. “It is all over with me,” he cried.

“No, M. le Comte. Drive as fast as you can to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine in the Rue de Grenelle. Mlle. Armande is waiting there, the horses are put in; she will take you with her.” Desperate, Victurnien seized this refuge. At the inn he flung himself into his aunt Mlle. Armande’s arms; she wept, half believing herself complicit in his ruin. They set out for Brest that night, and Paris slid away behind them. Both spoke at cross purposes—Victurnien thinking only of his forgery, his aunt thinking of debts and bills. He suggested sneaking into Chesnel’s house at night to hide; she soothed him like a child.

Chance, however, betrayed them. That evening Chesnel had been arranging to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir’s head-clerk—a negotiation that would bring him a hundred thousand francs. Chesnel’s successor, waiting in the Rue du Bercail, saw Mlle. Armande alight from a travelling carriage at an odd hour and, curious, watched. When Chesnel opened his door and saw Victurnien, he understood at once. He ushered the Count into a room beyond his private office—no one could enter without his consent.

“My good Chesnel,” said the Count, “you do not know the full extent of my troubles.”

Victurnien explained the forgery. Chesnel, thunderstruck but devoted, wept. He could not accept that a forger’s scandal should reach the old man’s father; he raged, suggested impossible remedies—soften du Croisier, buy back the draft from the Kellers, hide the Count in a cellar—and decided, by sheer devotion, to go to Paris himself. He packed clothes, took money, left the Count in the locked room, and told his housekeeper to keep all casual visitors away.

Chesnel reached Paris in three days by diligence. At the Kellers he learned the draft had already returned to du Croisier three days before; the document belonged to du Croisier, who could keep or return it. In despair, Chesnel went to the Duchess. She was in bed; by persuasion and insolence Chesnel had his way and was ushered into her room. He told her bluntly that the hundred thousand crowns in her keeping had been gained by a forgery committed for love of her. Struck by shame and fear of implication, she flung off the eiderdown, opened her desk, handed over the notes, and pledged to use her influence to save Victurnien. She promised to save him “or die,” and Chesnel left her room triumphant.

Back in the Rue du Bercail, despite Chesnel’s haste, he arrived an hour too late: the gendarmes had already come and taken Victurnien away. Chesnel, wild with grief, begged the officers to be merciful and, seeing the Count led out between gendarmes, whispered to him that if he could not hush the matter he must kill himself before the indictment was made. Victurnien, in stupor, could not comprehend.

Chesnel then dashed to the Hotel du Croisier. A criminal forgery charge must be brought by a private individual; du Croisier had the right to pursue it—or to withdraw. He found du Croisier surrounded that night by intimates—M. du Ronceret, President of the Tribunal; M. Sauvager, deputy Public Prosecutor; M. du Coudrai; the examining magistrate M. Camusot; and others. The gathering was half political and half social; inside, they discussed the d’Esgrignon affair with the excitement of men who saw in it a test of social and legal principle.

At du Croisier’s, the deputy public prosecutor, Sauvager, spoke with the complacent triumph of an official who thinks himself both zealous and undervalued. Camusot, the examining magistrate who had authorized the warrant, was perturbed that the arrest had been executed so promptly. The group debated whether the case would come to trial; Mme. Camusot warned that political pressure might work below the surface to influence the jury and the outcome. They spoke of counsel from Paris—Berryer for the defense—and of “a sensation at Landernau,” aware that high influence could intervene.

While the conspirators sat, Chesnel, hastily intent on saving the young house of d’Esgrignon, sought out the examining magistrate in the street and implored him: “If you are for the house of d’Esgrignon, the Duchess, the Prince de Cadignan, dukes and ministers will interest themselves in you.” He hinted that he would denounce suspected corruption at du Croisier’s if the magistrate were hostile. Camusot, won by his wife’s persuasive airs and perhaps the appeal to broader influence, listened.

Chesnel then tried to soften du Croisier. On bended knee he offered whatever was demanded: lands, service, anything if du Croisier would withdraw his charge. Du Croisier enjoyed his power. He saw in prosecuting the Count a chance to assert bourgeois dignity against the aristocracy and to teach the nobles a lesson. He delighted in the idea of exciting the Court and the public by bringing a proud house low. When Mme. du Croisier appeared and, touched by Chesnel’s plea, tried to appeal to her husband’s better judgment, du Croisier did not yield. He demanded more than money: he wanted political advantage and social recognition. He proposed that the d’Esgrignon family be bound by a surety and that the deposit be kept as leverage; he even insisted on a marriage arrangement tying up property as a future security—plans which Chesnel and Mme. du Croisier found odious.

Du Croisier pictured the affair as larger than personal revenge: it was “France,” “the people,” a lesson that the bourgeoisie would not be trampled on by nobles. He fancied his victory would be sealed in the Assize Court and by a matrimonial entanglement that would secure his political aims. Chesnel, in despair, then produced the hundred thousand crowns and implored Mme. du Croisier to accept them to spare the d’Esgrignons. Mme. du Croisier hesitated, torn between obedience to her husband and pity for the fallen family. Chesnel, pressing every religious and familial plea, persuaded her to take the money and promise to swear before magistrates that she had received it on a date prior to the drafting of the contested bill—an explicit falsehood he urged as a “venial sin” to save lives and honor.

To clinch this, Chesnel planned to involve the Abbe Couturier and the bishop, knowing that clerical counsel would strengthen Mme. du Croisier’s resolve. He roused Mlle. Armande and sent her to rally ecclesiastical support while he went home to sleep, exhausted. He had also gone to the Kellers and the Duchess earlier and had thought through the magistrates’ personal ambitions and the judicial politics of the town, calculating how to influence them in the coming struggle.

The narrative then turns to a broader picture of provincial justice. Magistrates and officials must often begin careers in the provinces; those who remain divide into two classes. Some, cul-de-sac men resigned to local life, are torpid or content; others, younger and ambitious, nurture hopes of promotion to Paris and strive to shine at politically charged trials. In this town, the bench was split: conservative, settled men like President du Ronceret and old Blondet represented the resigned section; youthful, zealous men like Camusot and his deputy comprised the ambitious group eager to make reputations by zealous prosecutions. The provincial administration leaned bourgeois, often zealous in its own political passion, ready to act like spies and to display public zeal that sometimes overstepped discretion.

President du Ronceret himself is described as a man of petty ambitions and resentments. Nominated president where he had once hoped for more, he harbors bitterness and inconsistency—willing to profess independence while opposing both sides. He and his wife maintained old-fashioned house and habits and gave boulevard soirées and occasional grand dinners to keep social standing; yet his private intrigues, even in arranging marriages for his son Fabien, betray petty rivalries. Old Blondet, by contrast, is a veteran jurist, an ex-public accuser whose conduct has been moderate and prudent; though sidelined, he is respected for judgment and steadiness.

Chesnel’s grasp of this judicial world—the factions, the ambitions, the petty rancours—shapes his strategy. He sees that many of these men, still bound to hopes of advancement, may be swayed if they think powerful patrons in Paris support them. Thus, he plans to use the Duchess’ influence and court connections as leverage. He also recognizes the dangers: du Croisier’s vindictiveness and the deputy public prosecutor’s eagerness for prominence threaten a trial that could ruin the d’Esgrignon family even if an acquittal were possible; disgrace would nonetheless endure.

Thus the crisis deepens: Victurnien is arrested; Chesnel has bribed, pleaded, and schemed; the Duchess has returned the notes and promised aid; du Croisier delights in his leverage and demands political recompense; Mme. du Croisier is torn but finally, under Chesnel’s urging and with the money in hand, agrees to a false testimony to avert dishonor; and the local magistrates, with their divided ambitions and political passions, loom as the battleground where the fate of the d’Esgrignons will be decided. Chesnel lies awake mapping out the men he must influence, promising to set great powers in motion through Mme. de Maufrigneuse, while the provincial world of judges, presidents, clerks, and aspiring officials gathers round the coming trial with all the pettiness and consequence of a local theater of justice.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Blondet’s legal talents might have carried him far, but he held his profession in contempt and reserved his ambitions, leisure, and energy for another pursuit: gardening. Corresponding with celebrated amateurs and keen on botanical discoveries, the old judge lived among flowers, his favorite the pelargonium. Court business and outward life mattered little beside the interior life of fancies and emotions he led in his greenhouse. Had he not been so absorbed, he would likely have been a deputy under the Empire and prominent in the Corps Législatif.

His marriage deepened his retreat from public life. At forty he rashly married an eighteen-year-old who bore him a son, Joseph. Three years later Mme. Blondet, then the town’s prettiest woman, inspired a passion in the prefect that lasted until her death; the prefect became the father of her second son, Emile. The whole town knew, and even old Blondet did. Rather than arousing ambition, the marriage encouraged his botanical tastes; neither husband nor wife wished to change places. Unequal to contest with youth and beauty, Blondet sought consolation in his greenhouse and kept a pretty servant to assist him. Mme. Blondet spent his substance on dress and finery; only Emile’s education interested her. She loved Emile and Blondet loved Joseph, each blinded by paternal partiality.

For twelve years Blondet bore these circumstances with eighteenth-century grand seigneur composure, though he profoundly disliked his younger son. When his wife died in 1818 he turned Emile out, sending him to Paris on a scant allowance; Emile would have perished without help from his real father. Blondet’s house, opposite the prefecture, had a neat court and admired flowerbeds; climbing plants covered walls, honeysuckle hid piers, and cactuses perched in vases. Through glass doors and windows one could see across to the garden, which extended into two acres. Blondet’s income was about four thousand livres from land, plus the house. He intended to leave his property and bench seat to Joseph, having made a will favoring him and setting aside money to enable Joseph to buy back portions of the estate that could not legally be taken from Emile.

Emile, turned adrift, found distinction in Paris as a man of letters and in society, aided by friendships like that with Mlle. de Troisville (later Mme. de Montcornet). Mme. Blondet had tried to link him to the Troisvilles, who later favored him when they returned to Paris. Emile’s precocious literary reputation and good looks strengthened his bond with the Countess de Montcornet; by the time his adventurous life pressed upon him, their attachment proved unshakeable. Old Blondet never knew of Emile’s success; he had been cursed in his mind and therefore remained ignorant of his son’s greatness.

Blondet’s integrity matched his passion for flowers. He knew law and botany alone. He met litigants courteously, accepted gifts of rare seeds, but on the bench he was impartial; litigants trusted his clear procedure. His steadiness made him indispensable to President du Ronceret, who, apart from matrimonial schemes, would have done all he could to prevent the vice-president’s retirement in favor of his son. Blondet did not know Emile could have fulfilled his wishes quickly.

Their household was frugal to parsimony: fruit saved until it spoiled, no new bread, fast days strictly observed. The maid Mlle. Cadot kept keys and watched expenses; she shared the father’s predilection for Joseph. Blondet tried to mold Joseph into a lawyer to satisfy Mlle. Blandureau’s parents, who insisted he be an assistant judge to marry their daughter. Joseph spent evenings at the Blandureaus’, where young Fabien du Ronceret also frequented. Everything was measured; the garden, however, escaped thrift. The gardener, a single man, kept paths and borders immaculate, and an amphitheatre of benches held thousands of pelargoniums that drew visitors from distant departments; Empress Marie Louise once visited and Blondet received the Legion of Honor. Blondet shunned society and was unaware of the President’s machinations.

Michu, a young judge with twelve thousand livres income, cared more for making himself agreeable to high society than for provincial routine. He did enough at the Tribunal to be useful and, with Parisian training as prosecuting counsel, could rapidly solve legal questions that baffled the President and Blondet. He was indispensable socially and professionally, able to mediate delicate matters.

When crisis came, the public prosecutor was away in Paris. The President and du Croisier seized his absence to engineer a charge of forgery against Victurnien d’Esgrignon. Normally such a case might be hushed to avoid scandal, but the President contrived to have M. Sauvager, a young Royalist deputy public prosecutor who owed his place to subservience to the Ministry, take up the charge without consulting his absent chief. The President and du Croisier persuaded Sauvager by promising advantageous matches and future fortunes if he showed devotion, urging him to act against the d’Esgrignons promptly to preempt Parisian intervention. Sauvager agreed.

Camusot’s unexpected defection from the President’s scheme terrified the plotters. Camusot, the examining magistrate, had his own background: son of a silk mercer with ambitions for him, married to Amélie Thirion (daughter of an usher of the Royal cabinet), he advanced by backstairs influence. His wife, Mme. Camusot, with Parisian instincts and impatience with provincial life, meddled in her husband’s career; she saw the d’Esgrignon affair as an opportunity to ingratiate the family with powerful circles, including the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, and to secure promotion and a Paris appointment for her husband. Perceiving the President’s underhand maneuvers, she advised Camusot to side with the d’Esgrignons, foreseeing advantage.

Chesnel, who knew the Tribunal’s circle, counted on the public prosecutor, Camusot, and Michu to support the d’Esgrignons; he also believed Blondet, if persuaded, would favor what he saw as justice for a cause that had been his lifelong hope. Chesnel planned to go to Blondet to avert the President’s treachery. Before dawn he was roused by a startling visitor: the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, disguised in man’s attire, had driven from Paris to save Victurnien. She declared she had brought a hundred thousand francs from the King’s private purse to buy Victurnien’s innocence if necessary; if bribery failed, she had brought poison to prevent an indictment. She had sent word to the public prosecutor, who was following her.

Chesnel embraced the Duchess’s aid. They went to see Camusot early; the Duchess intended to charm Mme. Camusot and secure Camusot’s cooperation. Playing her role, she understood provincial manners and boasted of the influence exerted by Thirion’s family connection to the King. She and Chesnel repeated the plan: the Duchess would speak to Mme. Camusot, and Chesnel would use his authority to persuade Camusot that rescuing the d’Esgrignon was in the kingdom’s interest. The Duchess insisted on secrecy.

Mme. Camusot, quick to grasp the situation, accompanied the Duchess to Blondet’s greenhouse, presenting the Duchess as a distinguished horticulturist. She warned Blondet of the President’s duplicity: the President aimed to thwart Blondet’s hopes for Joseph’s marriage to Mlle. Blandureau, and Fabien du Ronceret had received clandestine proposals for Mlle. Claire’s hand. Blondet, a man of flowers, was shocked. The Duchess promised that if Blondet resigned properly and awaited confirmation from the public prosecutor, his son’s appointment could be secured within a week; she urged him to consult Camusot and Michu to hush up the affair quickly, ensuring Joseph’s prospects. Blondet, overwhelmed and suspicious, hurried to seek confirmation.

At nine the three judges—Blondet, Camusot, and Michu—met secretly. Blondet, furious at the President’s trickery, insisted the matter was a trifle and argued against construing the episode as forgery: forgery requires an intent to defraud, which seemed absent here, since Chesnel had repeatedly squared accounts and had allegedly deposited money to meet the bill. Michu proposed removing the Count from peril by hastening the examination of du Croisier and his wife and issuing a decision the next morning. They agreed to act swiftly.

Du Croisier was summoned and questioned by Camusot; under searching interrogation he confirmed prior dealings with the Count and that Chesnel had often remitted sums. Camusot then asked whether du Croisier knew the money destined to meet the bill had been deposited with him beforehand; this unnerved him. Chesnel, summoned, confirmed he had deposited the money and produced letters to prove it; Mme. du Croisier corroborated his statement, declaring Chesnel had entrusted the sum to her in her husband’s absence. With these facts on record, the Count’s innocence emerged legally.

Du Croisier returned home enraged at his wife’s deposition; she showed him bundles of banknotes she had kept concealed and argued she had acted for his good and the family’s future advantage. He threatened hasty departure and secrecy, while his wife believed she had safeguarded their prospects.

The town buzzed with the Count’s arrest; yet by ten the court dismissed the charge, giving reasons that allowed the Count the right to sue for libel. Chesnel walked the streets exposing du Croisier’s plot. As the day closed, the Marquis d’Esgrignon and family fretted over debts and honor. The Duchess, who had stayed with them, advised practical measures: marry for money if necessary; the old privileges of nobility had been eroded by modern law and fortunes mattered more than lineage. She produced a tiny phial and joked about more decisive remedies; she counselled Victurnien to take lands and a quiet life rather than Paris flirtations. The romantic episode ended: the Duchess, once disguised and passionate, resumed her rank; for Victurnien and Diane, the brief romance dissolved with the crisis’s end.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Six months later Camusot became an assistant judge in Paris and later an examining magistrate. Goodman Blondet was appointed councillor to the Royal Court, kept the post until he secured a pension, then returned to his pretty house. Joseph Blondet took his father’s seat at the court until his death; with no hope of promotion he married Mlle. Blandereau and probably now leads, in the little flower-covered brick house, as dull a life as any carp in a marble basin. Michu and Camusot received the Cross of the Legion of Honor; Blondet became an Officer. M. Sauvager, deputy public prosecutor, was sent to Corsica, much to du Croisier’s relief; du Croisier certainly did not intend to give his niece to that functionary.

Urged by President du Ronceret, du Croisier appealed the Tribunal’s finding to the Court-Royal and lost. Liberals across the department held little d’Esgrignon guilty; Royalists spun frightful stories of plots by “that abominable du Croisier” to compass revenge. A duel followed: chance favored du Croisier; the young Count was dangerously wounded, and his antagonist stood by his words. The affair embittered party strife, and the Liberals invoked it at every opportunity. Meanwhile du Croisier never carried his election and saw no hope of marrying his niece to the Count, especially after the duel.

A month after the Tribunal’s decision was confirmed, Chesnel died, exhausted by the strain that had weakened him mentally and physically. He died at the moment of victory, like a faithful hound that brings the boar to bay and dies on its tusks. Though the great House was nearly ruined, the heir penniless and bored, and prospects of establishing himself gone, Chesnel’s exhaustion and bitter thoughts hastened his end. One comfort: at his sister’s entreaty the old Marquis restored their friendship. The great lord came to the Rue du Bercail and sat by his old servant’s bedside, unaware how much the servant had done and sacrificed. Chesnel sat up and repeated Simeon’s cry. The Marquis allowed burial in the castle chapel; they laid him crosswise at the foot of the tomb reserved for the Marquis himself, the last, in a sense, of the d’Esgrignons.

Thus died one of the last representatives of Service in its original meaning: the relation between feudal lord and servitor. Found now only in remote provinces or among a few old royal servants, it honored a noblesse that inspired affection and a bourgeoisie that could conceive it. Such magnificent devotion is no longer possible. Noble houses have no servitors; France no longer has a King, hereditary peerage, or land irrevocably bound to historic houses to perpetuate glorious names. Chesnel was not merely an obscure great man of private life; he was a great fact. There is something solemn and sublime in his sustained self-devotion, rising above a single beneficent deed or a moment’s supreme effort. Chesnel combined homely burgher virtues with heroic noble ideals enlightened by solid education — virtues belonging to classes between the people’s poverty and the aristocracy’s greatness.

Victurnien was not well regarded at Court; there was no chance of a great match or office. His Majesty refused to raise the d’Esgrignons to the peerage—the one royal favor that might have redeemed his position. He could not marry a bourgeois heiress during his father’s life, so he lived shabbily under the paternal roof, haunted by memories of two years’ splendor in Paris and the lost love of a great lady. He grew moody and depressed, vegetating with a careworn aunt and a half-broken father who blamed a wasting malady. Chesnel was gone.

The Marquis died in 1830. The great d’Esgrignon, followed by the less infirm noblesse of the Collection of Antiquities, went to wait upon Charles X at Nonancourt, swelling the meager train of the fallen king—an act then seen as heroism amid revolt. “The Gaul has conquered!” were the Marquis’ last words.

By that time du Croisier’s victory was complete. A week after his father’s death the new Marquis d’Esgrignon married Mlle. Duval. His bride brought three million francs; du Croisier and his wife settled the reversion of their fortunes upon her in the marriage contract. During the ceremony du Croisier declared the d’Esgrignon family the most honorable of all the ancient houses in France.

Someday the present Marquis will have an income exceeding a hundred thousand crowns. He comes to Paris every winter and leads a jolly bachelor life, treating his wife with more than the indifference of the grand seigneur of olden times—he takes no thought for her.

“As for Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” said Emile Blondet, source of these details, “if she is no longer like the divinely fair woman whom I saw by glimpses in my childhood, she is, at sixty-seven, the most pathetic and interesting figure in the Collection of Antiquities. She queens it among them still. I saw her on my last journey home to fetch the necessary papers for my marriage. When my father learned whom I had married he was struck dumb until I told him I was a prefect.

“‘You were born to it,’ he said, with a smile.

“As I took a walk around the town I met Mlle. Armande; she seemed taller than ever. I thought of Marius among the ruins of Carthage. Had she not outlived her creed and the beliefs that time destroyed? She is a sad and silent woman, with nothing of her old beauty left except her eyes, which shine with an unearthly light. I watched her on her way to mass, book in hand, and could not help thinking that she prayed to be taken out of the world.”

LES JARDIES, July 1837.
