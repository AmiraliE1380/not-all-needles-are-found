In September 1835 Clémentine du Rouvre, daughter of a spendthrift marquis, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a Polish exile. Settlements from her mother's family secured her income. Adam, not supported by France, faced Parisian prejudice—Polish nobles were scorned and the émigré community divided—so he was mocked.

He bought a house on rue de la Pépinière in 1834 and established himself in Paris; the home was lavish.

Clémentine noticed Captain Paz, a discreet factotum who ran the household and stables yet remained invisible. A poor veteran relation of Adam’s, Paz became a close friend; his refusal of a gift revealed loyalty and equality. As they prepared to go out, Clémentine's affection for both men grew, showing ties could transcend social norms.

Thaddeus lived reclusively in Paris until the Comtesse Clémentine Laginska was seen riding with him in the Bois de Boulogne and insisted he dine with her and Adam. He dressed for the occasion but acted like a subordinate, answering little while listening to Clémentine; Adam urged him to be livelier. Though outwardly dull, Thaddeus was acutely aware of his love for Clémentine and struggled to conceal it.

At the Opera he remained distant; Clémentine felt slighted and Adam excused him as disagreeable. His reserve sprang from unrequited love, deepened after her return from a trip. He took a bittersweet pleasure in being indispensable to their household—knowing it would falter without him even as he suffered inwardly. Clémentine, newly confident and intent on her Parisian standing, left little room for him, though once she noticed him while driving home and invited him along—an invitation that flustered him. Their interactions were charged with unspoken emotion: he felt gratitude and longing but kept his distance, finding solace in managing their affairs and reflecting on his pain.

When Clémentine hosted a reception his absence went unnoticed; he retreated into silent devotion. Her constant struggle for attention, rivals, and the strain of maintaining status led her to increasingly neglect Adam. In May, preparing to visit her uncle’s estate, she saw Thaddeus again and insisted he join dinner. He engaged more; her interest grew, and after dinner she confessed dissatisfaction with Adam and a desire for passion. Torn between love and loyalty, Thaddeus almost confessed but instead invented a romance with Malaga, a circus performer, as a deflection. He provided for Malaga and visited her without real intimacy; misunderstandings followed until her despairing letter longing for his companionship reminded him of the constancy of his devotion to Clémentine amid his sacrifice.

Clementine returned home more beautiful. After dinner they discussed Thaddeus and Malaga. Adam feared Thaddeus’s misfortune would harm his standing by linking him to Malaga; Clementine scorned such women, while Adam defended Thaddeus’s sacrifices.

Paz, who had asked Adam for money, was seen as a threat; he felt Clementine’s contempt, sank into despair, yet continued to admire her, care for the household, and fell ill.

When Adam lost heavily gambling he asked Thaddeus to cover the loss by blaming Malaga; Thaddeus complied reluctantly. Clementine, angered by his generosity to Paz, questioned his loyalty; overwhelmed, Thaddeus taught her household management. In spring he nursed Adam through a grave illness; Adam recovered and praised him. Tormented by unrequited love, Thaddeus left for the Caucasus after sending a confession Clementine and Adam never grasped. Years later she remembered him as a bittersweet, unfulfilled love.
