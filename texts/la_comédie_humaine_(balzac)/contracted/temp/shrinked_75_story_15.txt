**A Woman of Thirty**  
*By Honoré de Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage*  

**Dedication**

**Summary**

**I. Early Mistakes**

On a bright April morning in 1813, a cabriolet arrives at the Tuileries, where an anxious father, visibly aged, helps his beautiful daughter, Julie, out of the carriage. As they walk through the gardens, the father notes the curious glances from young men, jokingly suggesting they think she is his wife. Julie, however, is preoccupied with the upcoming military review commanded by Napoleon, which she is eager to see.

The father reflects on the impending campaign that will lead to Napoleon's downfall, while Julie's impatience grows as they are delayed from entering the review. When they finally reach the gates, they are turned away, and Julie expresses her disappointment, fearing she may never see the Emperor again. An officer, recognizing her, allows them entry, and Julie is captivated by the spectacle of the troops.

As the review begins, Julie's attention shifts to Colonel Victor d’Aiglemont, a handsome officer who captures her heart. The father observes his daughter's infatuation with concern, fearing for her future happiness. 

**II. A Year Later**

In March 1814, a year later, Julie and Victor are married, but their relationship is strained. They travel to Tours, where Julie's health declines. She feels trapped in a loveless marriage, and her father’s warnings about Victor’s character haunt her. Despite her initial joy, she realizes that Victor is selfish and lacks the depth she desires in a partner.

Julie finds solace in her daughter, Helene, born shortly after their marriage. However, her health continues to deteriorate, and she becomes increasingly isolated. Victor, preoccupied with his own ambitions, neglects her emotional needs, leading to a growing rift between them.

**III. The Englishman**

During this time, Julie encounters Lord Grenville, an Englishman who becomes infatuated with her. Their connection deepens, and Julie finds herself torn between her duty as a wife and her feelings for Grenville. She struggles with her emotions, feeling guilty for her attraction to him while still bound to Victor.

As their relationship develops, Julie grapples with her identity and the constraints of her marriage. She seeks to maintain her dignity while navigating the complexities of love and loyalty. The tension between her desires and her responsibilities intensifies, leading to moments of despair.

**IV. The Crisis**

One evening, after a confrontation with Victor, Julie confesses her feelings to Grenville. They share a passionate moment, but the reality of their situation looms over them. Julie is determined to protect her daughter from the pain of a broken home, yet she longs for the love and connection she lacks with Victor.

As the story unfolds, Julie's internal conflict escalates. She must confront the consequences of her choices and the impact they have on her family. The narrative explores themes of love, sacrifice, and the struggle for personal fulfillment within the confines of societal expectations.

**V. The Resolution**

Ultimately, Julie's journey leads her to a painful realization about her marriage and her own desires. She must decide whether to pursue her happiness at the cost of her family or to remain in a loveless union for the sake of her daughter. The story concludes with Julie facing the complexities of her life, embodying the struggles of many women of her time.

**II. A HIDDEN GRIEF**

Nestled between the Seine and the Loing, a flat, desolate landscape stretches out, bordered by the Forest of Fontainebleau and marked by the towns of Moret, Montereau, and Nemours. This dreary region, characterized by its monotonous horizon, is home to the old chateau of Saint-Lange, surrounded by stately avenues and gardens, yet steeped in melancholy.

In late 1820, a young woman, known for her beauty and wit in Paris, arrived at Saint-Lange, shocking the local villagers who had not seen gentry in years. The Marquise d’Aiglemont’s presence stirred curiosity, but her frail demeanor suggested she was not there to bring life to the area. Instead, she appeared to be retreating from a life of despair, possibly due to financial ruin linked to her husband, the Marquis, who was away in Spain.

As the Marquise isolated herself in the chateau, she rarely left her room, only dining briefly and avoiding contact with her servants. Her little girl, Helene, was often kept at a distance, as the Marquise struggled with an overwhelming grief that seemed to extinguish her maternal instincts. The villagers, initially intrigued, soon lost interest as her eccentricities became apparent.

At twenty-six, the Marquise was grappling with profound sorrow, having experienced a loss that left her feeling incomplete. Her anguish stemmed from a love that had been unfulfilled; she had lost a man she loved deeply, who had died to preserve her honor. Bound by societal expectations, she felt unable to express her grief openly, leading to a deep internal conflict.

The Marquise’s suffering was unique, as it was tied to the loss of a first love, a pain that is often unrecognized and unexpressed. This anguish, unlike other forms of grief, left her feeling hollow and disconnected from the joys of life. She reflected on her past, realizing that her marriage, though socially acceptable, was devoid of true happiness.

As she sat in her room, the flat horizon mirrored her desolation. The beauty she once possessed now felt burdensome, and she questioned the value of her existence. Memories of childhood joy contrasted sharply with her current state, leaving her feeling trapped in a life that no longer resonated with her spirit.

One day, the village priest, M. le Curé, visited her, sensing her deep sorrow. He shared a story of a father who had lost all his sons in battle, yet found the strength to live on. His compassion and understanding struck a chord with the Marquise, who began to see him as a potential confidant. However, her bitterness and despair quickly returned, overshadowing any glimmer of hope.

In subsequent visits, the curé attempted to guide her towards faith and acceptance, but the Marquise remained skeptical. She expressed her disdain for societal norms, particularly the institution of marriage, which she viewed as a form of legalized oppression for women. Her reflections revealed a deep-seated anger towards a world that had failed to provide her with the love and fulfillment she craved.

Despite the curé’s efforts, the Marquise felt increasingly isolated in her grief. She longed for a love that transcended societal expectations, lamenting the loss of a connection that could have brought her true happiness. Her relationship with her daughter, Helene, was strained, as she struggled to reconcile her feelings of motherhood with her unresolved sorrow.

As spring approached, the Marquise found some distraction in managing her estate, which helped alleviate her melancholy. By October, she had grown more composed, her initial grief transforming into a quieter sadness. However, as she departed the chateau, she encountered the curé once more, choosing to avoid him, aware that he had seen through her facade.

In the end, the Marquise’s journey reflects the complexities of grief, love, and societal expectations, illustrating how deeply personal pain can shape one’s existence and relationships.

**Summary of "At Thirty Years"**

Madame Firmiani hosted a ball, where M. Charles de Vandenesse, a promising young diplomat, attended to bid farewell to Paris before his appointment at the Congress of Laybach. Although he had enjoyed the city's pleasures, he felt detached from the frivolities of Parisian society, viewing it as a realm of superficiality and empty intrigues. At thirty, Vandenesse approached life with a philosophical mindset, prioritizing ambition over romantic entanglements.

During the ball, he was introduced to the enigmatic Marquise d’Aiglemont, a woman shrouded in mystery and known for her seclusion. Intrigued by her reputation and beauty, Vandenesse found himself captivated by her presence, which contrasted sharply with the superficiality he observed around him. The Marquise, despite her delicate appearance, exuded a depth of soul that drew him in, revealing a life marked by sorrow and unfulfilled love.

Their conversation began with light banter but soon delved into deeper themes of love and sentiment. Vandenesse expressed regret at leaving Paris, while the Marquise suggested that happiness might be more valuable than the intellectual pursuits he cherished. He requested to visit her again, feeling a strong connection that lingered in his thoughts.

As Vandenesse visited the Marquise, he became increasingly aware of her past—a love lost to tragedy and a marriage that had not brought her happiness. She confided in him about the man who had died for her honor, revealing the weight of her memories and the emptiness of her current life. This confession deepened Vandenesse's admiration for her, as he recognized the nobility of her spirit.

Despite their growing bond, the Marquise struggled with her feelings, torn between loyalty to her deceased lover and the burgeoning affection she felt for Vandenesse. She grappled with the implications of pursuing a new love, fearing it would betray her past. Vandenesse, meanwhile, found himself deeply in love, experiencing the intensity and purity of first passion.

Their relationship evolved, marked by moments of tenderness and unspoken understanding. However, societal constraints loomed over them, complicating their feelings. The Marquise's husband, General d’Aiglemont, unwittingly interrupted their intimacy, highlighting the tension between their desires and the realities of their lives.

As time passed, the Marquise acknowledged her feelings for Vandenesse, yet she remained conflicted about the potential for happiness outside societal norms. Their love, while profound, was fraught with the complexities of memory, loyalty, and the fear of societal judgment. Ultimately, the story explores the intricate dynamics of love, loss, and the struggle for personal fulfillment within the confines of social expectations.

**IV. THE FINGER OF GOD**

Between the Barrière d’Italie and the Barrière de la Santé, a picturesque view of Paris unfolds, captivating both artists and tourists. From a higher vantage point along the boulevard, one can see a valley filled with factories nestled among trees and streams, while the squalor of Faubourg Saint-Marceau lies on the opposite slope. The Panthéon and Val-du-Grace dominate the skyline, appearing as giants over the small houses below. The observatory and the elegant lantern tower of the Invalides add to the architectural beauty, which shifts with the changing light and sky.

To the right, the canal Saint-Martin stretches out, bordered by public granaries. In the distance, the smoke-covered slopes of Belleville blend with the clouds, hinting at a vast city hidden from view. This city, caught between life and death, sends forth a muffled roar, asserting its presence.

On a sunny spring morning, I admired this vibrant landscape, reflecting on the disdain some express for France while longing for its beauty. My reverie was interrupted by a scene unfolding nearby: a young woman, radiating joy, shared a tender moment with a handsome man and their charming little boy. Their happiness was palpable, contrasting sharply with the sullen demeanor of a second child, a girl named Helene, who seemed burdened by thoughts beyond her years.

As I observed, I noted the stark differences between the siblings. Helene, with her dark hair and intense gaze, appeared older than her years, while her brother, Charles, was carefree and innocent. Their mother’s subtle favoritism was evident in their clothing, hinting at deeper family dynamics. Helene’s jealousy and resentment towards her brother were palpable, culminating in a tragic moment when she pushed him, causing him to fall into the river. The scene turned chaotic as the mother rushed to the water's edge, but the child was lost in the murky depths, leaving behind a haunting silence.

Years later, the Marquis de Vandenesse mourned his father and hosted a notary at dinner. The notary, oblivious to the tension in the room, monopolized the conversation, much to the Marquise de Vandenesse's frustration. She longed to join her children at the theater, but the notary continued his rambling, unaware of the couple's growing impatience.

When the Marquis finally intervened, the notary remained oblivious, continuing to discuss business matters. Just as the Marquis attempted to dismiss him, the children returned unexpectedly, revealing that they had left the theater due to a distressing scene. The little girl, Helene, had been crying, and the atmosphere shifted dramatically.

The Marquise, recognizing the implications of the children’s return, felt a wave of dread. The notary, attempting to engage the children, inadvertently exacerbated the situation by asking about the play. Helene’s tears and the boy’s recounting of the play’s events revealed a deeper family tragedy, and the Marquise’s composure began to crack under the weight of her emotions.

In a moment of anger, the Marquis chastised the notary for his insensitivity, leading to a confrontation that left the notary bewildered. As he exited, he reflected on the evening’s events, unaware of the emotional turmoil he had stirred.

At home, the notary’s wife pointed out his blunders, emphasizing the need for discretion in social situations. Despite his confusion, he resolved to seek clarification from the Marquis, unaware that the complexities of human relationships often elude even the most astute observers.

**Summary of "Two Meetings"**

The narrative unfolds in the spring at Versailles, focusing on a character known as the General or the Marquis, a former orderly officer of Napoleon. He has established a country house near Montreuil, where he enjoys the solitude of the countryside while remaining close to Paris. The house, originally a retreat for a nobleman’s fleeting romances, features expansive grounds that provide a sense of seclusion despite its proximity to the city.

On a winter evening during Christmas, the General's family is gathered in their home, with the servants absent due to a wedding celebration in Versailles. The atmosphere is filled with warmth and joy as the family engages in playful activities. The General, content and relaxed, watches his children, particularly his youngest, Moïna, who is playfully resisting his wife’s attempts to dress him for bed. Their older children, Gustave and Helene, are also present, each absorbed in their own pursuits.

Helene, a beautiful and thoughtful girl, is depicted as being somewhat melancholic, reflecting on her life and the expectations placed upon her. The family dynamic is characterized by affection, but there are hints of underlying tensions, particularly between Helene and her mother, the Marquise. The Marquise’s strictness contrasts with Helene’s burgeoning independence and emotional depth.

As the evening progresses, a sudden commotion interrupts their domestic bliss. A stranger, pursued by unknown assailants, arrives at their door seeking refuge. The General, despite initial hesitation, decides to offer the man sanctuary, driven by a sense of honor and duty. The stranger, desperate and agitated, insists on the need for secrecy and safety, revealing that he is in grave danger.

The General leads the stranger to a secluded room, where he locks him in for safety. Meanwhile, the Marquise and Helene are left in suspense, unaware of the unfolding drama. The General’s decision to harbor the fugitive weighs heavily on him, as he grapples with the moral implications of his actions.

The tension escalates when gendarmes arrive, searching for the fugitive, who is revealed to be a murderer. The General, caught between his promise of hospitality and the law, faces a moral dilemma. The gendarmes question him about the stranger, but he maintains his composure, concealing the truth.

As the night deepens, Helene, compelled by curiosity and perhaps a sense of duty, ventures upstairs to investigate. She encounters the fugitive, who is revealed to be a complex character, embodying both danger and allure. Their interaction is charged with emotion, and Helene is drawn to him, sensing a connection that transcends the immediate circumstances.

The General, upon discovering Helene’s encounter with the fugitive, is filled with horror and rage. He confronts the stranger, demanding that he leave. The fugitive, however, challenges the General’s authority, asserting his own moral justification for his actions. This confrontation reveals the depths of Helene’s feelings and the conflict between her loyalty to her family and her burgeoning sense of self.

In a climactic moment, Helene declares her intention to leave with the fugitive, asserting her autonomy and rejecting her mother’s authority. The General, devastated by his daughter’s choice, feels a profound sense of loss. Helene’s departure marks a turning point, as she embraces a life defined by passion and danger, leaving behind the safety of her family.

The narrative shifts to the aftermath of Helene’s decision, exploring the consequences of her choice. The General, now haunted by the loss of his daughter, reflects on the fragility of familial bonds and the complexities of love and duty. The story concludes with a poignant reminder of the sacrifices made in the name of love, as the General grapples with the reality of his daughter’s new life and the irrevocable changes it brings to their family.

Ultimately, "Two Meetings" delves into themes of love, sacrifice, and the moral complexities of human relationships, set against the backdrop of a tumultuous historical period. The characters navigate their desires and responsibilities, leading to a powerful exploration of the choices that define their lives.

**Summary of "The Old Age of a Guilty Mother"**

On a June day in 1844, the Marquise d’Aiglemont, a woman of about fifty, walks through the garden of her daughter’s mansion in Paris. The Marquise has dedicated her life and fortune to her youngest child, the Comtesse Moïna de Saint-Héreen, after losing her two sons. Her affection for Moïna is profound, contrasting with her more tepid feelings for her deceased children. The Marquise's devotion to Moïna is seen as both admirable and questionable by society, as she has effectively given up her own life for her daughter’s happiness.

Moïna, beautiful and charming, has become the center of the Marquise's existence. However, the Marquise harbors deep concerns about her daughter's flirtation with Alfred de Vandenesse, a man she views as unscrupulous. Despite her fears, the Marquise feels powerless to intervene, knowing that Moïna would dismiss her warnings. This situation is compounded by the Marquise's friendship with Alfred's father, which complicates her ability to express her disapproval.

As the Marquise reflects on her life, she experiences a mix of sorrow and resignation. She recognizes the toll that her emotional struggles have taken on her health, leading to physical ailments. Two tears fall from her eyes as she contemplates Moïna's future, fearing the consequences of her daughter's choices.

When the Marquise attempts to speak to Moïna about her relationship with Alfred, she is met with resistance and dismissive behavior. Moïna's indifference to her mother's concerns deepens the Marquise's anguish. After a painful conversation, the Marquise leaves, overwhelmed by her emotions and the realization that her daughter no longer respects her.

In a moment of despair, the Marquise collapses in the garden, and her lifeless body is carried inside. Moïna, initially preoccupied with her own feelings, is struck by the gravity of her mother’s condition. As she witnesses her mother’s suffering, she begins to understand the depth of her love and sacrifices.

The story concludes with Moïna's realization of her loss, as she mourns the mother she took for granted. The Marquise's final moments reveal the profound bond between mother and daughter, underscoring themes of love, regret, and the complexities of familial relationships. 

This narrative serves as a poignant exploration of motherhood, sacrifice, and the often-unrecognized struggles that shape the lives of women.
