*Two Poets* is the first part of a trilogy focusing on Lucien, his sister Eve, and their friend David in Angoulême. The story begins in a time dominated by traditional printing, featuring Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, a former journeyman pressman who runs a small printing business. He obtained a printing license during the French Revolution, allowing him to operate without formal education.

After inheriting the printing house, Séchard skillfully navigated the political landscape by collaborating with M. le Comte de Maucombe to print decrees while avoiding dangers of the time. He transformed from a struggling pressman into a shrewd businessman, driven by greed and a desire to secure a future for his son, David.

David excelled academically and was sent to Paris to learn the printing trade, but his father dismissed formal education, favoring practical experience. Upon returning home in 1819, David was ready to take over the family business, which had a monopoly on official printing. However, competition arose when the Cointet Brothers sought a second printing license, threatening Séchard's dominance.

As Séchard aged and grew increasingly greedy, he wanted to pass the business to David while also profiting from the transaction. This dual view of his son as both heir and rival strained their relationship. Séchard's cunning overshadowed paternal affection as he prepared to negotiate the sale, complicating their dynamic.

The printing house mirrored Séchard's chaotic character, reflecting his avarice. As the story unfolds, the father-son relationship becomes more complex, highlighting themes of ambition, familial duty, and the harsh realities of business. Ultimately, *Two Poets* explores the tension between personal ambition and familial loyalty amid societal change and competition.

The sitting room of the late Mme. Séchard was only partially modernized, featuring powder blue wainscoting and Oriental wallpaper but lacking essential decor. Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, inebriated, brought his son David to review a valuation of the family printing business. David was shocked to find the equipment overvalued at sixteen hundred francs, deeming the old presses unworthy. Séchard insisted the presses were reliable and had served him well for twenty years, dismissing David's preference for modern machinery.

In the workshop, Séchard demonstrated the presses' functionality, boasting about their durability. However, David recognized the equipment's outdatedness and questioned the valuation, particularly the inflated worth of the type. Despite Séchard's insistence on the tools' quality and potential profits, David felt trapped in an unfair negotiation.

Sensing David's hesitation, Séchard grew anxious, believing a lack of argument indicated acceptance. He highlighted the value of the business license and income from advertisements, urging David to see the profit potential. Ultimately, David agreed to terms that included a partnership allowing Séchard to retain a room in the building and share profits until the purchase was fully paid.

After moving in, David faced financial strain as Séchard refused to contribute to operational costs, insisting David manage alone. This led to a painful realization for David about his father's greed and lack of support, overshadowing any paternal concern.

Meanwhile, Séchard, now living in the countryside, became increasingly anxious about the business's success. He frequently visited David, who struggled to keep the printing house afloat amidst competition from the Cointet Brothers, who were gaining market share by slandering David and monopolizing local contracts.

David's friendship with Lucien Chardon, a talented but impoverished young man, blossomed as they shared aspirations. Lucien's family had fallen into poverty after his father's death, and he was eager to escape his circumstances. David offered him a position in the printing house, hoping to help him find a path to success.

As their friendship deepened, they discussed innovative ideas for the printing business, but their intellectual pursuits distracted them from practicalities. The Cointets, recognizing David's lack of business acumen, allowed his operation to continue in a diminished capacity, ensuring it wouldn't threaten their growing enterprise.

The physical state of the printing house reflected Séchard's avarice, as he neglected repairs and maintenance. The building was dilapidated, and the workshop was cluttered and grimy, indicative of the struggles faced by David and Lucien as they navigated challenges while contending with familial and financial pressures.

In summary, the narrative explores themes of familial obligation, the clash between tradition and modernity, and the struggles of young men trying to forge their paths in a competitive world. David's relationship with his father is fraught with tension as he grapples with inherited expectations and the desire for independence, while his friendship with Lucien offers hope amidst adversity.

In May 1821, David and Lucien, two aspiring poets, sought refuge from their oppressive printing office. David, a robust yet melancholic figure, contrasted with Lucien, whose delicate beauty resembled youthful Bacchus. While David represented the struggles of obscurity, Lucien, with his Gascon temperament, was ambitious and often reflected on society's darker aspects.

Their friendship flourished over three years as they immersed themselves in literature and art, sharing dreams of fame. David admired Lucien's brilliance, while Lucien aimed to elevate both their social standings. One day, David excitedly presented a book of poetry by André de Chénier, reigniting their aspirations.

Their artistic pursuits took a turn when a stranger arrived with a manuscript for printing, claiming to be an author sent by Mme. de Bargeton, the woman Lucien admired. Despite his feelings for her, Lucien recognized the social barriers between them and vowed not to pursue her unless David was welcomed too, demonstrating his loyalty.

After Lucien left, David reflected on their bond, feeling admiration mixed with concern for Lucien's romantic ambitions. Lucien wandered through Angoulême, contemplating the societal divides between the upper town, home to the nobility, and L’Houmeau, the bustling suburb where he lived. The upper town's old families maintained strict social boundaries, viewing outsiders with disdain, which intensified during the Restoration, further alienating the bourgeoisie from the nobility.

Lucien's aspirations to rise above his humble origins were complicated by entrenched class prejudices in Angoulême. Mme. de Bargeton, an admirer of literature and the arts, significantly influenced Lucien's ambitions, placing him at the center of a social conflict that could either elevate or destroy him. The narrative sets the stage for Lucien's struggle against societal constraints, driven by his passion for poetry and love for Mme. de Bargeton, while David remains a steadfast friend, embodying loyalty and sacrifice.

M. de Bargeton, a descendant of a Bordeaux alderman ennobled under Louis XIII, witnessed his family's decline due to his grandfather's extravagance. By 1789, the family retained only seignorial rights to rents from their Angoumois lands, totaling ten thousand francs annually. M. de Bargeton married Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, a noblewoman from an old but now obscure family. Her father, M. de Nègrepelisse, was a miser reliant on his wife’s small estate, while Anaïs received a broad education from Abbé Niollant, who instilled in her a love for music and literature.

Anaïs, or Naïs, developed a strong sense of self-worth and disdain for those around her, leading to a haughty demeanor. Her education fostered a sense of superiority that made her ill-suited for provincial life. After the Abbé's death in 1802, Naïs confronted her father's miserly nature and her rebellious spirit against marriage constraints. M. de Nègrepelisse sought a suitable husband for her, ultimately choosing M. de Bargeton, who was older and perceived as lacking intellect but manageable.

Naïs married M. de Bargeton, hoping for freedom and social connections in Paris, but their age difference and limited income constrained their lives in Angoulême. Over time, Naïs's once-vibrant qualities became absurdities, and her artistic inclinations turned into exaggerated expressions of emotion. She grew increasingly isolated, longing for excitement beyond her provincial existence.

Her disappointing marriage left her yearning for love and connection. The rise of the Empire rekindled her hopes, leading her to fall for a young officer, whose death in battle deepened her sorrow. This loss cast a shadow over her life, and as she faced aging without fulfillment, her pride kept her from engaging in the mundane affairs of provincial men.

Mme. de Bargeton’s life became a cycle of unfulfilled dreams and artistic aspirations, leading her to seek solace in literature and the arts. Despite her intellectual gifts, she remained trapped in a narrow existence, her dignity morphing into affectation. The arrival of M. du Châtelet, a charming but superficial man, introduced a new dynamic to her life. He ingratiated himself into her circle, recognizing her potential as a social asset, while the local bourgeoisie disapproved of his presence.

M. du Châtelet’s background made him intriguing, and he sought to cultivate a relationship with Mme. de Bargeton, seeing in her both a source of personal gain and a means to elevate his own status. His arrival disrupted the social order, highlighting the tensions within Angoulême society, where intellectual poverty coexisted with remnants of decayed gentility.

In summary, Mme. de Bargeton’s life reflects the struggles of a woman caught between her aspirations and the limitations imposed by her environment, ultimately leading to profound isolation and unfulfilled potential.

Political discourse was often verbose and insincere, with Louis XVIII viewed as a Jacobin. Women in society were generally awkward and poorly presented, diminishing their appeal. Châtelet found some solace in this environment due to his interest in Mme. de Bargeton. Despite their flaws, the nobility had a charm and tradition that set them apart from the bourgeoisie. Their loyalty to the Bourbon dynasty resembled a form of faithfulness, and their exclusivity enhanced their perceived value.

Some women, flattered by Châtelet, admired qualities in him that their own men lacked, boosting their egos and aspirations to be close to the Imperial Highness. Châtelet endured rudeness but maintained his position by engaging with the clergy and indulging the queen of Angoulême's whims, sharing literature and poetry with her. Mme. de Bargeton, enamored with the Bourbons' resurgence, longed for Paris's artistic brilliance. Châtelet, believing he had found a local talent, introduced her to Lucien, a young poet from L’Houmeau, whose potential he exaggerated.

Lucien, unaware of social barriers, was excited about visiting the Hôtel de Bargeton. His sister Eve, supportive and resourceful, prepared him for this significant occasion with new clothes and advice on aristocratic behavior. As Lucien approached the house, he felt a mix of excitement and trepidation, envisioning a world far removed from his humble beginnings.

Upon entering, Lucien found the Hôtel de Bargeton less grand than expected, yet charming. He met Mme. de Bargeton, who was captivated by his youthful beauty and diffidence. Their initial meeting was enchanting, and Lucien was intoxicated by her presence. Mme. de Bargeton, impressed by Lucien, saw him as a poetic embodiment of her aspirations.

Châtelet, sensing a rival in Lucien, attempted to assert dominance through condescension. Despite this, Lucien continued to visit Mme. de Bargeton, growing more comfortable in her presence. Initially grateful for the nobles' attention, he soon recognized their patronizing attitudes, fueling his resentment towards the aristocracy.

Lucien's infatuation with Mme. de Bargeton deepened as she treated him with a mix of affection and aloofness. She encouraged his ambitions, presenting herself as a mentor while grappling with her own feelings. Their relationship oscillated between admiration and jealousy, with Lucien striving to prove himself worthy of her affection.

As their bond grew, Lucien began to express his feelings more openly, leading to moments of intimacy that thrilled and terrified him. Mme. de Bargeton, torn between her social standing and feelings for Lucien, struggled with the implications of their relationship. Societal scrutiny intensified, leading to gossip about their connection.

Despite challenges, Mme. de Bargeton defended Lucien against societal backlash, arguing that genius transcends social class. She hosted gatherings to showcase his talent, drawing both admiration and criticism from the local aristocracy. Lucien's rise in prominence elicited mixed reactions, with some nobles resenting his presence while others celebrated his potential.

Feeling threatened, Châtelet sought to undermine Lucien while trying to win over Mme. de Bargeton. He organized a dinner to elevate Lucien's status, hoping to manipulate the situation. However, Lucien's talent shone through, garnering praise and complicating the dynamics among the three.

As Lucien navigated this new world, he became increasingly aware of the sacrifices required for success. Mme. de Bargeton encouraged him to embrace his ambitions, suggesting he adopt a noble name to solidify his status. This idea ignited Lucien's desire for recognition and prestige, prompting him to reconsider his values and aspirations.

In a moment of vulnerability, Lucien penned a heartfelt letter to Mme. de Bargeton, expressing his hopes and dreams while seeking her support for his friend David. This letter revealed his inner turmoil and desire for validation, showcasing the complexities of his character as he balanced ambition with loyalty.

Ultimately, Lucien's journey was marked by the tension between his aspirations and the realities of social hierarchy. His relationship with Mme. de Bargeton served as both inspiration and a reminder of the obstacles he faced in his pursuit of greatness, unfolding against a backdrop of societal contradictions and challenges.

As Lucien descended from Angoulême, he was troubled by thoughts of whether the esteemed Mme. de Bargeton was angry with him and if she would receive his friend David. He recognized the vast social distance between himself and the aristocracy, realizing that David could not easily bridge that gap. Unbeknownst to Lucien, accepting David into their circle could tarnish Mme. de Bargeton’s reputation, as mingling with lower classes was frowned upon in their society.

Despite his ignorance of these social dynamics, Lucien's instincts warned him of potential ridicule directed at David, who lacked the refined appearance and manners that Lucien possessed. This realization led him to reconsider his initial impulse to introduce David to Mme. de Bargeton, and he began to regret having invited David to meet her, envisioning the consequences of such a move.

Lucien reminisced about his simple life, his family, and his love for Mme. de Bargeton. He believed it would be more admirable to achieve success through his own efforts rather than relying on a woman’s favor, inspired by Napoleon’s rise to greatness through merit. Lucien resolved to pursue his ambitions independently, feeling ashamed of his previous thoughts.

Returning to L’Houmeau, Lucien was discontented with his humble surroundings, especially as he prepared to meet Mme. de Bargeton. He longed to escape the confines of his father's pharmacy, which reminded him of his family's modest status. His father, M. Postel, often reminisced about Lucien’s late father, a chemist with a secret remedy for gout, deepening Lucien's sense of inadequacy.

When Lucien received a letter from Mme. de Bargeton, he was ecstatic. The letter expressed her willingness to help David but also revealed her prejudices against those of lower social standing. Lucien interpreted this as a victory, believing that David would shine in her company. His sister Eve noticed his joy and teased him about his apparent happiness, sensing it was connected to a romantic interest.

Meanwhile, David, deeply in love with Eve, struggled with his feelings. He admired her from afar, believing his humble background made him unworthy of her affection. Despite his talent and potential, he felt trapped by societal expectations and insecurities. David was determined to support Lucien’s ambitions, even at the cost of his own desires.

As Lucien prepared for his public reading, David advised him to choose a different poem, fearing that his biblical work might not resonate with the audience. Lucien, filled with confidence, was eager to impress both the audience and Mme. de Bargeton.

Eve and David shared a tender moment, both feeling a deep connection but hesitant to express their feelings openly. Their love blossomed quietly, marked by mutual respect and admiration. David’s modesty and Eve’s gentle nature created a bond that neither fully understood yet.

At the reading, Lucien was nervous but determined to make an impression. He was aware of the social dynamics at play, particularly the presence of M. de Bargeton, who was oblivious to the subtleties of conversation and social interaction. M. de Bargeton’s limited intellect and reliance on his wife for social navigation highlighted the disparities between the characters.

As Lucien navigated the evening, he felt torn between his ambitions and the loyalty he owed to his family and friends. David’s selflessness and Eve’s quiet strength reminded him of the love and support surrounding him, even as he sought to carve out his identity in a world that often judged based on social standing.

Ultimately, Lucien’s journey reflects the complexities of ambition, love, and societal constraints that shape individual destinies. His relationships with David and Eve illustrate the tension between personal aspirations and the bonds of family and friendship, setting the stage for the challenges he would face in his pursuit of success.

Lucien struggled to connect with the imposing M. de Bargeton, whose silent authority made their conversation awkward. M. du Châtelet's arrival intensified Lucien's discomfort, as Châtelet’s elegance highlighted Lucien’s poverty. The two men exchanged glances while M. de Bargeton attempted to ease the tension with small talk about current events and Lucien’s poetry.

As the evening progressed, notable guests like the Bishop and Mme. de Chandour contributed to the gathering's peculiar dynamics. Mme. de Bargeton, dressed elaborately, engaged little with Lucien, favoring Châtelet instead. The gathering was filled with provincial elites, each displaying their quirks, including M. de Saintot, a self-proclaimed agricultural expert, and M. de Bartas, a boastful singer. The women, including Mme. de Chandour, exhibited vanity and ambition, while the men’s discussions often strayed from Lucien’s poetry.

When Lucien finally began his reading, he faced an indifferent audience. His initial poem received mild applause, but it became clear many were uninterested. Guests conversed among themselves, undermining Lucien’s confidence. Despite the lack of engagement, Lucien pressed on, driven by a desire to connect. He read passionately, but the response remained tepid, with some guests preferring card games over poetry. The gathering underscored the contrast between Lucien’s artistic aspirations and the mundane realities of provincial life, leaving him feeling isolated amid social pretensions.

Infatuated with Mme. de Bargeton, Lucien wrote an ode he believed rivaled Chénier's. As he recited it, he felt both pride and vulnerability, while Mme. de Bargeton, though supportive, worried about the audience's reaction. The poem, rich in imagery of angels and divine love, was met with indifference and sarcasm from the aristocratic crowd, who dismissed it as typical youthful verse. Critics like the Baron and Zéphirine found Lucien's work obscure and unoriginal. Despite their polite responses, the women were frustrated by the absence of true poets. Meanwhile, Francis, a diplomat, sought to undermine Lucien's confidence by spreading rumors about his mother’s influence on his poetry, hoping to provoke a disparaging remark from the Bishop.

As Lucien navigated the elite social scene, he felt increasingly out of place, compounded by condescending remarks belittling his aspirations. Mme. de Bargeton defended him, but the atmosphere remained hostile. Stung by rejection, Lucien vowed to prove himself worthy of high society.

In a parallel narrative, Lucien's sister, Eve, prepared for a meeting with David Séchard, a printer and her love interest. David, shy yet determined, expressed his feelings for Eve during a walk by the Charente River. He voiced concerns about Lucien's ambitions and the potential pitfalls of his relationship with Mme. de Bargeton, fearing it could lead to Lucien's downfall. However, Eve remained optimistic, believing they could support Lucien until he found success.

David proposed marriage to Eve, emphasizing that their union would enable him to help Lucien. He envisioned a future where they could provide for him, but Eve hesitated due to their differing social standings. David reassured her, promising to work hard for their future, revealing plans to improve his financial situation through a potential invention in paper-making.

As the evening progressed, Lucien returned, burdened by humiliation. He shared his frustrations with Eve and David, expressing his determination to win over Mme. de Bargeton. Unbeknownst to him, their engagement posed a challenge to his aspirations, as he feared Eve's marriage would hinder his social ambitions. David's father opposed the engagement, insisting Eve's lack of wealth made her unsuitable. Despite this, David remained committed to Eve, determined to build a life together, intertwining themes of ambition, love, and the harsh realities of social class.

In Angoulême, the town buzzed with the Bishop’s epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s response, elevating Lucien to local hero status. As he walked through Beaulieu, young men envied him, and flattering remarks boosted his ego. Lucien was eager to share news of his sister Eve's upcoming marriage to David with Louise, hoping her affection would bring him happiness. Louise praised his reading and expressed her desire to create a joyful life for him, despite societal envy.

Encouraged by Louise's affection and support from family and friends, Lucien began to see himself as important. He asked Louise if she would be his Beatrice, and she playfully suggested he must prove himself worthy. Lucien then revealed Eve's love for David, which Louise accepted easily, emphasizing that their love was paramount.

As Lucien spent more time at the Hôtel de Bargeton, he enjoyed the luxuries of his new status, feeling secure in his relationship with Louise and the backing of M. de Bargeton. However, rumors about their closeness began to circulate, fueled by jealousy and the town's gossip culture. The disparity in societal expectations became evident, as some acted freely while others faced harsh scrutiny.

Meanwhile, David prepared for his marriage to Eve, using his savings to improve their home. Lucien, now living comfortably as M. de Rubempré, felt optimistic about his future as a writer, believing his literary ambitions would soon bear fruit. His family's sacrifices for his success deepened his sense of entitlement.

However, dynamics shifted when Châtelet, a rival, began spying on Lucien and Louise, hoping to catch them in a compromising situation. Despite their innocent interactions, tension grew, exacerbated by the constraints of provincial life, where privacy was scarce. Louise, feeling societal pressure, contemplated drastic measures to escape scrutiny.

Châtelet's schemes culminated in a scandal when he and Stanislas claimed to have witnessed Lucien and Louise in a compromising position. The rumor spread rapidly, with exaggerated details fueling gossip. Townspeople eagerly dissected the situation, with many women expressing shock.

Amidst the chaos, Mme. de Bargeton confided in her husband about the incident, urging him to confront Stanislas for his slanderous remarks. M. de Bargeton, motivated by his wife's honor, prepared to challenge Stanislas, aware that their actions would have lasting consequences. This unfolding drama highlighted the fragility of reputation in their small town, illustrating the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations, where personal desires and public perception can lead to both triumph and tragedy.

Châtelet recognized the implications of a late-night visit, especially with M. de Bargeton in bed, and suspected Naïs's involvement. Feeling the need to intervene, he discreetly approached M. de Bargeton to ask if he wanted to speak to Stanislas, who welcomed the mediation. Châtelet advised Stanislas to maintain decorum when addressing Bargeton about his comments regarding Naïs.

When they reconvened, Bargeton confronted Stanislas about his claims of seeing Mme. de Bargeton and M. de Rubempré in a compromising situation. Stanislas, dismissive, corrected Bargeton on the name and faced a challenge: if he did not retract his statements, Bargeton would demand a duel at dawn. Cornered, Stanislas reluctantly agreed to the duel.

As they returned to the gathering, the atmosphere shifted; Châtelet appeared calm while Stanislas looked pale, prompting whispers among the guests about the duel. The men debated Bargeton’s right to challenge Stanislas, recalling Bargeton’s past as a skilled marksman. Châtelet reassured Stanislas, emphasizing the advantage of youth in the duel.

The next morning, news of the duel spread quickly. Mme. Chardon informed Lucien that Bargeton had shot Stanislas, who would suffer lasting injury. Lucien defended Mme. de Bargeton’s innocence, but public sentiment had turned against them. A note from Louise urged Lucien to avoid public appearances and suggested they spend the day together, hinting at the emotional turmoil following the duel.

Meanwhile, preparations for Lucien’s sister Eve’s wedding were underway, but Lucien felt torn between family obligations and his desire to be with Louise. Captivated by her plans to move to Paris, where she believed they could thrive socially and artistically, she encouraged him to seize opportunities in the capital, promising a life filled with intellectual stimulation.

Initially hesitant due to his sister’s upcoming marriage, Lucien was inspired by Louise’s passionate arguments about their potential future in Paris. He envisioned escaping provincial stagnation for the vibrant culture of the city.

Despite his excitement, Lucien grappled with guilt over abandoning his family during such a significant moment. Louise’s disdain for his hesitation fueled his internal conflict. Ultimately, she insisted he prioritize their relationship over familial duties, leading to a heated exchange that left Lucien distressed.

As Lucien prepared to leave, he sought financial support from his family, realizing their sacrifices for him. His sister and mother were heartbroken at the thought of his departure but recognized the necessity of his ambitions. David, Lucien’s brother-in-law, offered to help financially, despite the strain it would place on their resources.

In the end, Lucien’s departure was bittersweet. He felt the weight of his family’s sacrifices and the uncertainty of his future in Paris. As he set out with David, he was filled with both hope and trepidation, aware that the path ahead was fraught with challenges but also ripe with potential for greatness.
