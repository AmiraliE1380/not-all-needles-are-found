At the story’s opening the modern Stanhope press and ink‑distributing roller were unknown in provincial shops. In Angoulême, tied to Paris by its paper mills, wooden presses still groaned: leather ink‑balls applied ink, and the pressman inked by hand. This old mechanism, cherished by Jérôme‑Nicolas Séchard, anchors the tale.

Séchard, once a journeyman pressman, survived the Requisition of 1793, obtained a master‑printer’s license from a Representative of the People despite being illiterate, and bought his late master’s business cheaply. He partnered with the noble Comte de Maucombe, printed decrees discreetly during the Terror, and by 1795—after hiring a compositor who later became a bishop—had learned to run the shop. Frugal to avarice, he bought paper when prices fell, became his own landlord, and acquired the old printing house outright.

He had one son, David, schooled in grammar and later sent to Paris to be a suitable successor. David excelled academically and studied economically so as not to burden his father. Séchard’s shop enjoyed a regional monopoly on official printing until the Cointet Brothers pushed for a second license, threatening that position. Confident in David, Séchard looked forward to retirement.

Now in his seventies and drinking since his wife’s death, Séchard’s bloated face, shrewd gaze, three‑cornered hat and old‑fashioned clothes matched his habits. When David returned, Séchard feigned warmth but drove business, seeing him as both heir and rival. The long‑established shop—a large room with a glass door to the workshop—drew customers who watched the processes. The vine‑grown yard, the kitchen where inky runoff mixed with household waste, and the cramped, unchanged living quarters above reflected Jérôme‑Nicolas’s stinginess and single‑minded pursuit of profit.

The sitting‑room, partly modernized by the late Mme. Séchard with powder‑blue wainscoting and Oriental paper, remained sparsely furnished: lyre‑backed chairs, bare arched windows, and incomplete plans—no clock, sconces, or mirror, because Mme. Séchard died before finishing them. Practical and profit‑minded, Jérôme‑Nicolas left it as it was.

He brought David in, showed him a valuation of the printing plant, and had him read the inventory aloud. David scoffed at the old presses and flung aside the paper; affronted, Séchard led him downstairs and briskly demonstrated the presses’ efficiency, challenging David to name an English press that could match his. He boasted they could earn enough without a foreman and dismissed newer machinery as ruinous to type. David doubted the high price placed on the type, which Séchard defended—insisting it had been cast at M. Vaflard’s foundry and, after twelve years, was nearly new. The inventory totaled thirty thousand francs, including license and goodwill. David, remembering his father had once bought the business for ten thousand francs, found the asking price exorbitant and hesitated about whether he could afford it.

Séchard, who preferred argument to silence, pressed the point: the license alone brought income from advertisements and official work, and attached to the valuation was a partnership deed—Séchard would rent the premises to David for twelve hundred francs a year and reserve a room for himself. Cornered, David calculated the license and goodwill’s worth and, thinking he might manage the debt, accepted the deal to his father’s surprise.

The next day Séchard had the apprentice move David’s belongings to the attic, leaving him three empty rooms and no reserve of funds to pay wages. When David asked for help, Séchard refused—he had already paid his share—and warned that any legal claim to Mme. Séchard’s fortune would be long and costly. Humiliated and alone, David resolved to work hard.

Séchard spoke then of a “treasure” he left behind—Marion, a capable country girl who ran many tasks in the shop—and departed for his Marsac vineyard, fretting over the thirty thousand francs and making periodic visits, suspecting David’s mismanagement as the Cointet Brothers gained ground.

David, indifferent to politics, struggled for work. The Cointets, aligned with the clergy, monopolized the market and smeared him as a Liberal and unreliable because of his father. Though Séchard at first urged David to hold out and even framed negotiations as protective—speaking of a sixty thousand franc price—David was ultimately forced to sell, marking the establishment’s decline. Séchard grew more detached, visiting less though still attached to the old shop.

David’s fortunes worsened when Lucien Chardon, an old school friend reduced to poverty after his father’s death, sought work; David made him a printer’s reader. Their friendship deepened—Lucien dreaming of poetry, David of natural science. Lucien’s accounts of his father’s schemes for refining sugar and making paper suggested other ventures. But those ambitions distracted them from immediate needs; while they indulged plans and conversation, the Cointet Brothers consolidated their advantage, Séchard & Son scraped by, and the building and workshop fell into disrepair.

One day in May 1821 David and Lucien slipped out while the men dined and sat under the vines, where sunlight emphasized their differences. David was robust, fighter-built, swarthy and weathered—shaped by humble origins and melancholy reserve. Lucien was graceful and beautiful, with a fine profile and striking blue eyes; delicate in appearance but ambitious and shrewd, willing to treat society pragmatically and even use dubious means to rise.

Both were low in social standing but nourished loftier ideals, which made them bitter when reality intruded. Lucien, impetuous and adventurous, usually led; David, stronger but more introspective and cautious, admired him and often yielded to his charm, content to be the more ordinary companion. For three years their friendship thrived on literature and art: they read voraciously, worked hard, and dreamed of fame as an escape from hardship.

David brought a new volume of André de Chénier whose verses moved them, offering solace and kinship with the poet’s struggles. Their reverie was broken by Cérizet, an apprentice who brought a stranger seeking to print a monograph. Lucien eagerly engaged the author, who mentioned Mme. de Bargeton—a woman Lucien admired. He blushed, thanked the author, and confessed to David his passionate love. David cautioned him about social barriers, but Lucien hoped love could bridge them and vowed not to visit Mme. de Bargeton without David’s welcome—a pledge that revealed loyalty and pride.

Walking through Angoulême, Lucien reflected on the gulf between his origin and Mme. de Bargeton’s world. The city, perched on a crag, was split between an upper town of nobility and a lower town of commerce. The upper class clung to old values, resisted outsiders and reform, and looked down on merchants; the bourgeoisie resented this disdain. Despite some modernization, the hierarchy remained rigid, breeding tension and the prospect of social upheaval as the middle class pressed for recognition.

In that climate, Lucien’s ambition to enter Mme. de Bargeton’s circle was daunting. His background as a druggist’s son marked him an outsider, yet his literary talent, charm, and Mme. de Bargeton’s artistic tastes gave him hope. David’s mixture of admiration and concern captured their friendship: he understood Lucien’s depth but saw the obstacles. Lucien’s determination and his pledge to include David in any future visits encapsulate their bond as love and ambition unfold against entrenched divisions.

M. de Bargeton descended from a Bordeaux alderman ennobled under Louis XIII, but the family declined after his grandfather, “the Waster,” squandered its wealth. By 1789 he held only entailed lands and a house in Angoulême with modest rents. In 1805 he married Marie-Louise-Anaïs (Naïs), taught languages and music by the Abbé Niollant during the Revolution. Her father’s thrift and provincial seclusion produced a haughty independence; she married at thirty-six to a fifty-eight-year-old M. de Bargeton hoping for Paris and an inheritance that never came. Modest means and petty provincial life stifled her until M. du Châtelet arrived—charming and ambitious, scandalizing the local elite and invigorating her salon.

Political opinion was loud and exaggerated: the Quotidienne showed tepid loyalty, Louis XVIII was mocked, society women were awkward, and in the countryside lingering nobility masked many defects while Bourbon loyalty remained a provincial badge.

Châtelet sought solace in society because of his interest in Mme. de Bargeton. He flattered women, embodied what their husbands lacked, and courted the clergy and the queen of Angoulême’s petty tastes to keep his place. Mme. de Bargeton—Naïs—was enchanted by the Bourbon revival but longed for Parisian genius; through Châtelet she learned of a promising young poet in Angoulême.

That poet, Lucien, was unaware of the stir. Praised by his headmaster, he expected a warm reception at the Hôtel de Bargeton. His sister Éve secretly supported him—saving for clothes and coaching him in aristocratic manners—leaving him proud and anxious about his prospects.

At the Hôtel de Bargeton Lucien found the house less grand than imagined, but Naïs embodied the poetry he loved. She admired his beauty and diffidence; their meeting was mutually attractive, and Lucien overlooked her faded airs, sensing intimacy.

Châtelet, sensing a rival, asserted influence over Lucien with unsolicited advice about society and claimed Naïs. Though intimidated, Lucien continued to visit—initially flattered by noble condescension, later resentful.

Naïs cultivated Lucien as a project: patroness, confidante, and encourager of his literary ambitions. She introduced him to music and books, revealed vulnerabilities, and addressed him intimately. A portrait of a dead lover provoked jealousy, yet Lucien declared his feelings and their relationship deepened.

When their liaison became public the town buzzed. Châtelet tried to undermine Lucien; Naïs defended him, arguing genius can rise from humble origins. To launch him she hosted salons and urged him to adopt the noble name Rubempré, promising courtly patronage. Exhilarated, Lucien’s aspirations widened; his earlier egalitarian ideals gave way to social ambition. In a passionate letter he asked Naïs’s help for a friend, David, exposing his conflict between love, artistic sincerity, and pursuit of recognition.

Descending from Angoulême, Lucien worried that Mme. de Bargeton might be angry and whether she would receive David. He sensed the social gulf: a queen and her favorite occupied separate worlds, and Naïs could be ruined by associating with commoners. Lucien’s aristocratic instinct noted that noble bearing did not equal noble feeling; many notable men lacked polish. David lacked graces and would be ridiculed, Lucien feared. After a poetic reverie he returned to worldly wisdom, regretting his impulsive letter and anticipating the sacrifices success demanded. He preferred to win by his own efforts, inspired by Napoleon’s rise, though ashamed of his father’s shop.

At home M. Postel praised Lucien’s grandfather, pricking Lucien’s resentment. Searching the shop, Lucien found Mme. de Bargeton’s reply: she offered to help David but revealed prejudice toward lower social standing. Lucien read this as a triumph, convinced David would shine in her circle.

Éve noticed his elation and hoped for his happiness; Lucien reassured her, though Éve sensed his priorities shifting. David, feeling unworthy, decided to decline the invitation and remain a printer—his humility moving Lucien even as ambition began to eclipse gratitude.

Preparing for his first public reading, Lucien and David chose different poems. David and Éve shared a quiet moment hinting at mutual affection. At the reading Lucien met the awkward, ineffectual M. de Bargeton, whose limited intellect and shallow pleasantries underscored the social dynamics Lucien must navigate. The evening, marked by Lucien’s ambition and strained relationships, set the stage for future conflicts and aspirations.

There was talk of nominating the mute gentleman for deputy, but Lucien, who had only briefly visited, had not yet fathomed him. M. de Bargeton, reclining and silent, watched with a cool reserve that awed Lucien; imaginative, Lucien fancied him a sphinx and behaved deferentially. “I am the first comer,” Lucien said with an exaggerated bow. “That is natural enough,” Bargeton replied; Lucien took it as a barb and grew self-conscious. When Lucien asked why those from farther away arrived earlier, Bargeton shrugged and relapsed into silence. Lucien tried to converse—imagining Mme. de Bargeton fussing over the room and his attire and lingering by a Japanese jar for a topic—but Bargeton’s curt answers and piercing scrutiny unnerved him.

The old servant announced “M. du Châtelet.” Baron Sixte entered with easy confidence, inspecting Lucien’s clothes and deepening his discomfort; he dismissed Lucien’s poetry as vaudeville. Mme. de Bargeton soon appeared, elaborately dressed and theatrically charming; Châtelet’s compliments delighted her while she scarcely acknowledged Lucien.

Guests arrived in a parade of social types: the dignified Bishop with his Vicar‑General; Mme. de Chandour and her eccentric husband with Amélie posing like a rival queen; the solemn M. de Saintot (Astolphe) and his austere, scientifically affected wife Élisa; M. de Bartas craving praise for his singing; the artist Alexandre de Brébian and his wife; fashion‑obsessed Lolotte and Fifine with their dowdy husbands. The Comte de Senonches came with Zéphirine, tall and striking despite ill health; Francis, a devoted family friend who ran their household, showed unusual attention to Zéphirine, provoking speculation. The wealthy Marquis de Pimentel and his wife arrived with the Baron and Baroness de Rastignac, drawing envy as Mme. de Bargeton lavished them with attention.

The Prefect and the General came last, followed by a nervous country gentleman who awkwardly offered a treatise on silkworms. Mme. and Mlle. du Brossard, noble but impoverished, hovered about, pressing social connections with thinly veiled desperation; M. de Séverac, a childless widower, spoke of nurseries while Mme. du Brossard pushed her daughter’s prospects.

Throughout the evening Lucien felt increasingly scrutinized and diminished. Most called him M. Chardon rather than M. de Rubempré, a stinging slight. The assembly seemed absorbed in flattery and rivalry rather than his art, leaving him isolated. When he read, announcing poems by André de Chénier provoked misunderstandings of affectation. Le Malade met mild applause; L’Aveugle fell flat amid impatience and inattention. Sweating under pressure, Lucien drew courage from Louise’s supportive glance and read Chénier’s Iambes, which finally captured some attention though many missed the meaning. During the break Zéphirine sent Francis to inquire after the poems; women whispered about Lucien’s origins. Amélie’s remarks about his noble name and printing background sparked gossip, complicating his precarious standing—yet absorbed in the poet’s beauty, Lucien remained largely unaware of the mounting tension.

Lucien tried to immortalize his love for Mme. de Bargeton with an ode he fancied rivaled Chénier’s. Confident under her protective gaze, he misread the room: the Baron dismissed the poem as ornate but empty, the women feigned admiration while whispering sarcastically, and Francis spread rumors that Lucien’s mother had written the verse. The Bishop’s sincere praise only intensified the aristocrats’ scorn, leaving Lucien overwhelmed and isolated.

After the mockery, Mme. de Bargeton withdrew with him to her boudoir, consoling him and romanticizing suffering as the road to greatness—urging him to turn pain into art. Meanwhile Lucien’s sister Eve and her lover David argued over his ambitions. David feared Lucien was unsuited to high society and might be ruined; Eve hoped they could sustain him until success. When David proposed, Eve hesitated at their unequal standings but accepted out of love and a desire to help Lucien. David vowed to provide so Lucien wouldn’t be alone. Frustrated and determined, Lucien resolved to win Mme. de Bargeton’s love and rise above his detractors.

In Angoulême the Bishop’s epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s rejoinder made Lucien a local celebrity. Walking through Beaulieu he was envied and flattered. He told Louise of Eve’s impending marriage; she greeted him passionately, praised his reading, and promised a poetic refuge despite the envy around them. Buoyed by family and friends, Lucien’s self-regard swelled. He teasingly asked Louise to be his Beatrice; she promised future love and urged him toward greatness. With M. de Bargeton’s patronage and Louise’s intimacies he felt established—calling himself M. de Rubempré—and expected a literary breakthrough. Eve and David postponed their wedding to support him, though David feared Lucien might change with status.

Their closeness drew Châtelet’s attention; in the small town innocent interactions sparked gossip and constrained behavior. An outburst, misread by Stanislas, seeded rumor that Châtelet eagerly exploited while Stanislas reveled in the drama. Alarmed, Mme. de Bargeton defended her honor and urged her husband to act. Insecure but resolute, M. de Bargeton confronted Stanislas with modesty and won respect at a tense gathering—setting the stage for a confrontation that would entangle lives and expose the town’s mix of love, honor, and social expectation.

Châtelet, alarmed by a late visit to the bedridden M. de Bargeton and suspecting Naïs’s involvement, intervened as household authority. He asked whether Bargeton wished to see Stanislas; relieved to have a mediator, Bargeton was brought to Amélie’s room for a confrontation that could make Mme. de Bargeton a widow and implicate Lucien.

Châtelet warned Stanislas that Bargeton would press him about remarks involving Naïs and urged decorum. In the room, Bargeton demanded a retraction about Mme. de Bargeton and M. de Rubempré. Cornered, Stanislas accepted a duel and chose pistols.

News of the duel depressed the guests and divided opinion. Stanislas was shaken; Amélie, sensing the strain, fell ill and was removed. The men debated consequences, some recalling Bargeton’s former marksmanship.

At breakfast the next morning Mme. Chardon arrived with alarming news: Bargeton had nearly killed Stanislas after accusing him of compromising Mme. de Bargeton. Stanislas had been shot in the neck and was injured. Lucien, hearing this, publicly defended Mme. de Bargeton’s innocence.

Lucien then received a note from Louise warning him to avoid her home because of the duel’s fallout and urging him to spend the day with her. David, preparing for his own marriage, advised Lucien to limit visits to Mme. de Bargeton. Visiting her, Lucien found her at breakfast with her husband and M. de Nègrepelisse, who watched him closely.

Louise revealed plans to go to Paris to secure a political post for Bargeton and contain the scandal, and she pressed Lucien to accompany her, praising Paris over provincial stagnation. Torn by Eve’s imminent wedding and family duties, he hesitated; after a heated exchange Louise fainted and he, desperate, promised discretion and to be ready to leave the next night.

At home Eve and their mother fretted over wedding costs. David offered help and agreed to endorse a loan; they arranged to send Lucien’s belongings to Paris covertly. Mistrustful, Châtelet sent someone to follow Mme. de Bargeton. Early the next morning Lucien left with David as Mme. de Bargeton’s carriage approached.
