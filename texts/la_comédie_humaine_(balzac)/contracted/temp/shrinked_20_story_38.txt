At the story's outset, the Stanhope press and ink-distributing roller were not yet common in small provincial printing houses. In Angoulême, closely tied to Parisian typography through its paper mills, only primitive wooden presses were in use. The phrase “the press groans” was literal, as leather ink-balls were still employed, and the pressman inked by hand. This old-world mechanism, cherished by Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, plays a significant role in this tale.

Séchard, once a journeyman pressman, had survived the Requisition of 1793, becoming the sole worker in a printing house after the master’s death. Lacking the skills to read or write, he was unexpectedly granted a master printer’s license by a Representative of the People, allowing him to buy the business at a bargain. He partnered with a nobleman, M. le Comte de Maucombe, who helped him print decrees while remaining hidden from the authorities.

By 1795, after the Terror, Séchard had to find a new compositor, hiring an Abbé who later became a Bishop. Despite his lack of education, Séchard learned to manage the business, developing a keen sense for costs and profits. His avarice grew as he recognized the potential for wealth, leading him to buy paper stock when prices fell. He became his own landlord, owning the old printing house.

Séchard had one son, David, whom he sent to grammar school with the hope of training a successor. David excelled academically, and Séchard, who had no respect for book learning, insisted he save money while studying in Paris. David completed his education without costing his father anything.

Séchard’s printing house had a monopoly on official printing in the region, but the Cointet Brothers sought a second printer’s license, threatening his business. Séchard, confident in David’s abilities, looked forward to retirement, believing his son would handle the competition.

Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, now in his seventies, had developed a fondness for alcohol, which his late wife had kept in check. His appearance reflected his drinking habits, with a bloated face and a cunning gaze. He wore a three-cornered hat and old-fashioned clothing that suited his character.

As David returned home, Séchard welcomed him with feigned warmth, eager to discuss business. Despite David’s request to postpone serious matters, Séchard was determined to negotiate the sale of the business. He viewed David not just as his son but as a potential adversary in business dealings.

The printing house, established since the reign of Louis XIV, was a large room with a glass door leading to the workshop. Customers often stumbled through the shop, distracted by the printing process. The premises included a yard with vines and a kitchen, where the inky runoff mixed with kitchen waste, creating a notorious reputation.

The living quarters above the printing office were simple and cluttered, reflecting Séchard’s greed. The dining room was sparsely furnished, and the bedroom contained old furniture, untouched since the time of Séchard’s predecessor. The state of the house mirrored the old printer’s character and his relentless pursuit of profit.
