The Marquise de Listomere belongs to that Restoration generation that mixes religious observance with elegant worldly life. She fasts, takes the sacrament, attends balls and operas, and her confessor allows her to combine sanctity with social display. Conforming to Church and society, she embodies contemporary legality: pious enough to recall a somber Maintenon-era devotion, worldly enough to adopt the gallantries of an earlier court. Married seven years to the Marquis de Listomere, a deputy hoping for a peerage, she probably considers such conduct useful to her family's ambitions. Some await the day he becomes a peer and she reaches thirty-six—when many women discover how much social rules have deceived them.

The marquis is an unremarkable man. He stands well at court; his virtues are as neutral as his faults. As deputy he never speaks but always votes RIGHT. At home he is the same—never scolding unless kept waiting. His friends call him “dull weather”: neither bright nor stormy. A woman of principle could not have fallen into better hands; it is a great thing for a virtuous wife to have married a man incapable of folly.

At balls, impertinent beaux who pressed the marquise’s hand found only her contemptuous indifference; that coolness chilled their hopes. She commands the freedom to converse at length with men she finds agreeable without becoming a subject of gossip. Some coquettish women maintain such reserves for years; to suppose similar secrecy in the marquise would be calumny.

I have had the happiness of knowing this rare woman. She talks well; I listen; thus I am admitted to her salons—my ambition achieved. Neither plain nor pretty, Madame de Listomere has dazzling skin, red lips, small graceful feet she keeps hidden, and eyes that retain a gentle glow—magical when she is animated. Beneath a cold demeanor lies a soul: when interested she reveals a charm otherwise concealed. She does not seek success, yet she wins it—an observation that became the moral of an episode then echoing through Paris salons.

A month before, the marquise danced with a young man modest yet lively, full of qualities and flaws. He was ardent yet mocked ardor, talented yet concealed it, playing the learned with aristocrats and the aristocrat with learned men. Eugene de Rastignac is one of those clever youths who try everything to probe the future; while waiting for ambition’s hour he scoffs at much. He had grace and originality—rare together. That night he talked with Madame de Listomere for nearly half an hour without any fixed intent to please. Their conversation, wandering from “Guillaume Tell” to women’s duties, caused him to look at her in a way that embarrassed her. He then left her and did not speak to her again the rest of the evening. He danced, played écarlé, lost some money, and went home to bed. Thus, precisely, the affair occurred; I neither add nor omit.

The next morning Rastignac lingered in bed in that state where heavy slumber quickens the mind: reveries of a young man gliding under silks and cashmeres. He rose without yawning, rang for his valet, ordered tea, and drank immoderately while writing letters. Seated comfortably with feet on the andirons, he wrote one letter quickly, folded and set it aside addressed later; a second took until midday and filled four closely written pages.

“That woman keeps running in my head,” he muttered as he finished and laid aside the second letter. He lounged in his dressing-gown, feet on a stool, hands in trouser pockets, staring absently at the gilded knob of his fire-shovel and ceasing even to poke the fire—a mistake, for to play with fire is one of the greatest pleasures when thinking of women. Those tiny blue flames supply speeches to our fancy; the little detonation that flings a coal or ember—called a “burgundian”—startles and contributes to our reveries. One builds and stirs the fire as the mind builds its hopes.

At that moment I entered. Rastignac started.

“Ah! there you are, dear Horace; how long have you been here?”

“Just come.”

He took the two letters, directed them, and rang for his servant. “Take these,” he said.

Joseph departed without a word—an admirable servant. We spoke of the Morea expedition, for which I hoped to be appointed physician; Eugene warned me I would lose time if I left Paris. We discussed other matters, which I will not relate.

When the Marquise de Listomere rose about half-past two that day, her maid Caroline handed a letter which the marquise read while Caroline did her hair—an imprudence many women commit. The letter began, “Dear angel of love, treasure of my life and happiness—” At those words she nearly flung it into the fire but, with a curiosity many virtuous women understand, she wished to see how a man who began in that fashion could end. She read to the fourth page and then let her arms drop, as if fatigued.

“Caroline, ask who left this letter.”

“Madame, I received it myself from the valet of Monsieur le Baron de Rastignac.”

There was a silence. “Does Madame intend to dress?” Caroline asked at last.

“No—He is certainly a most impertinent man,” the marquise reflected.

I ask all women to imagine the reflections of which that was the first.

Madame de Listomere ended hers by deciding to forbid her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac and to show him more than disdain in society; his insolence had gone beyond what the marquise would tolerate. At first she thought of keeping the letter, but on second thought she burned it.

"Madame had just received such a fine love-letter; she read it," Caroline told the housemaid.

"I should never have thought that of madame," the other answered, surprised.

That evening Madame de Listomere went to a party at the Marquis de Beauseant's, where Rastignac would likely appear; she went solely to crush him by coldness, but by two in the morning she found she was waiting in vain. Stendhal’s fanciful term "crystallization" fits the way her thoughts hardened before, during, and after that evening.

Four days later Eugene was scolding his valet.

"Ah ça! Joseph; I shall soon have to send you away, my lad."

"What is it, monsieur?"

"You do nothing but make mistakes. Where did you carry those letters I gave you Saturday?"

Joseph became stolid, like a statue in a cathedral porch, then smiled idiotically and said, "Monsieur, one was for the Marquise de Listomere, the other was for Monsieur's lawyer."

Joseph was speechless. I happened to be in Eugene's apartment and felt I must interfere.

"Joseph is right," I said.

Eugene looked at me.

"I read the addresses quite involuntarily, and—"

"And," interrupted Eugene, "one of them was not for Madame de Nucingen?"

"No, by all the devils, it was not. Consequently, I supposed, my dear fellow, that your heart was wandering from the rue Saint-Lazare to the rue Saint-Dominique."

Eugene struck his forehead and began to laugh; Joseph perceived then that the blame was not his.

There are morals young men should reflect on. First mistake: Eugene had thought it amusing to have Madame de Listomere laugh at the blunder that made her the recipient of a love-letter not meant for her. Second mistake: he failed to call on Madame de Listomere for several days after the affair, allowing her thoughts to crystallize. There were other errors which I will pass over, leaving ladies to deduce them.

At last Eugene went to call on the marquise; but as he attempted to enter, the porter stopped him, saying Madame la marquise was out. As he got back into his carriage the Marquis de Listomere came home.

"Come in, Eugene," he said. "My wife is at home."

A husband, however good, is not without fault. As they went up the staircase Rastignac perceived several blunders of worldly wisdom that had slipped into that page of his life.

When Madame de Listomere saw her husband ushering in Eugene she could not help blushing. The young baron saw that sudden color and, with youthful vanity, thought: "What! that fortress, too?" He straightened his cravat; men, if not grasping, like to acquire a new coin.

Monsieur de Listomere seized the Gazette de France from the mantelpiece and carried it to a window, as if to seek by journalistic help an opinion on the state of France.

A woman, even a prude, is not long embarrassed; she always keeps at hand the fig-leaf Eve bequeathed her. Consequently, when Eugene, interpreting his initial refusal as flattering, bowed to Madame de Listomere in a rather intentional manner, she veiled her thoughts behind one of those feminine smiles more impenetrable than a king's words.

"Are you unwell, madame? You denied yourself to visitors."

"I am well, monsieur."

"Perhaps you were going out?"

"Not at all."

"You expected someone?"

"No one."

"If my visit is indiscreet you must blame Monsieur le marquis. I had already accepted your mysterious denial when he himself came up and introduced me into the sanctuary."

"Monsieur de Listomere is not in my confidence on this point. It is not always prudent to put a husband in possession of certain secrets."

The firm, gentle tones and the imposing glance with which the marquise spoke made Rastignac aware that he had posed prematurely.

"Madame, I understand you," he said, laughing. "I ought, therefore, to be doubly thankful that Monsieur le marquis met me; he affords me an opportunity to offer you excuses which might be full of danger were you not kindness itself."

The marquise looked at him with surprise but answered with dignity: "Monsieur, silence on your part will be the best excuse. As for me, I promise you entire forgetfulness, and the pardon which you scarcely deserve."

"Madame," said Rastignac hastily, "pardon is not needed where there was no offence. The letter," he added low, "which you received, and which you must have thought extremely unbecoming, was not intended for you."

The marquise could not help smiling, though she wished to seem offended.

"Why deceive?" she said, with a disdainful air though gentle voice. "Now that I have duly scolded you, I am willing to laugh at a subterfuge not without cleverness. I know many women who would be taken in: 'Heavens! how he loves me!' they would say."

She forced a laugh, then added indulgently, "If we desire to continue friends let there be no more mistakes, of which it is impossible that I should be the dupe."

"Upon my honor, madame, you are so—far more than you think," replied Eugene.

"What are you talking about?" asked Monsieur de Listomere, who had been listening without understanding.

"Oh! nothing that would interest you," replied his wife.

Monsieur de Listomere tranquilly returned to his paper, and presently remarked, "Ah! Madame de Mortsauf is dead; your poor brother has, no doubt, gone to Clochegourde."

"Are you aware, monsieur," resumed the marquise, turning to Eugene, "that what you have just said is a great impertinence?"

"If I did not know the strictness of your principles," he answered naively, "I should think that you wished either to give me ideas I deny myself, or to tear a secret from me. But perhaps you are only amusing yourself with me."

The marquise smiled; that smile annoyed Eugene.

"Madame," he said, "can you still believe in an offence I have not committed? I hope chance may not enable you to discover the name of the person who ought to have read that letter."

"What! can it still be Madame de Nucingen?" cried Madame de Listomere, more eager to penetrate that secret than to revenge the impertinence of the young man's speeches.

Eugene colored. A man must be more than twenty-five not to blush at being taxed with a fidelity women may scoff at—perhaps to hide envy. He replied with tolerable self-possession: "Why not, madame?"

Such are the blunders we all make at twenty-five.

This speech caused a violent commotion in Madame de Listomere's bosom; but Rastignac did not yet know how to analyze a woman's face by a rapid glance. The marquise's lips paled, but that was all. She rang for wood, forcing Rastignac to rise and take his leave.

"If that be so," said the marquise, stopping Eugene with a cold, rigid manner, "you will find it difficult to explain, monsieur, why your pen should, by accident, write my name. A name, written on a letter, is not a friend's opera-hat, which you might have taken carelessly on leaving a ball."

Eugene, discomfited, looked at her with an air both stupid and conceited. Feeling ridiculous, after stammering a few juvenile phrases he left the room.

A few days later the marquise acquired undeniable proof that Eugene had told the truth. For the last fortnight she has not been seen in society.

The marquis tells all who ask the reason of this seclusion: "My wife has an inflammation of the stomach."

But I, her physician, who am now attending her, know it is really nothing more than a slight nervous attack which she is making the most of to stay quietly at home.
