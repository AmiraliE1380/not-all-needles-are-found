At the story's outset, the Stanhope press and ink-distributing roller were not yet common in small provincial printing houses. In Angoulême, tied to Parisian typography by its paper mills, only wooden presses were used. “The press groans” was literal: leather ink-balls were still employed, and the pressman inked by hand. This old mechanism, cherished by Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, is central to the tale.

Séchard, once a journeyman pressman, survived the Requisition of 1793 and became the sole worker in a printing house after the master’s death. Illiterate, he was granted a master printer’s license by a Representative of the People, allowing him to buy the business at a bargain. He partnered with a nobleman, M. le Comte de Maucombe, who helped print decrees while keeping a low profile from authorities.

By 1795, after the Terror, Séchard needed a compositor and hired an Abbé who later became a Bishop. Despite his lack of education, Séchard learned to run the business, developing a shrewd grasp of costs and profits. His avarice grew as he saw the potential for wealth: he bought paper when prices fell and became his own landlord, acquiring the old printing house.

Séchard had one son, David, whom he sent to grammar school hoping for a competent successor. David excelled academically, and Séchard—who scorned book learning—insisted he economize while studying in Paris. David completed his education without costing his father anything.

The printing house held a monopoly on official printing in the region, but the Cointet Brothers sought a second printer’s license, threatening Séchard’s position. Confident in David’s abilities, Séchard looked forward to retirement, believing his son would meet the competition.

Now in his seventies, Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard had developed a taste for drink, which his late wife had once kept in check. His appearance reflected the habit: a bloated face, cunning gaze, three-cornered hat, and old-fashioned clothing suited to his character.

When David returned home, Séchard welcomed him with feigned warmth, eager to discuss business. Despite David’s plea to postpone serious matters, Séchard was intent on negotiating the sale of the business, viewing his son not only as offspring but as a potential rival in commercial dealings.

The printing house, established since Louis XIV’s reign, was a large room with a glass door opening to the workshop. Customers often stumbled through the shop, distracted by the printing process. The premises included a yard with vines and a kitchen where inky runoff mixed with household waste, giving the place a notorious reputation.

The living quarters above the office were simple and cluttered, reflecting Séchard’s greed. The dining room was sparsely furnished, and the bedroom contained old furniture unchanged since the predecessor’s time. The state of the house mirrored the old printer’s character and relentless pursuit of profit.

Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, practical to a fault, saw no value in improvements that didn’t yield money and left it as it was.

Jérôme-Nicolas brought his son David into the room and pointed to a valuation of the printing plant on the table. David read the inventory aloud, noting the old presses and their valuation. Dismissing them as "only fit for firewood," he dropped the paper. Séchard, affronted, insisted they were time-tested and still valuable. He descended the stairs and, to David’s surprise, demonstrated the presses’ functionality with surprising agility. Proudly he asked, “Where’s the English press that can match this?” and argued the machines could bring a good income without a foreman. David, skeptical, thought the type’s price inflated.

The inventory totaled thirty thousand francs, including license and goodwill, which David feared he could not afford. Séchard preferred a heated argument to silence and extolled the virtues of old tools, claiming they were worth more than new ones. He stressed the license’s value, citing income from advertisements and other work, and attached a partnership deed: Séchard would rent the premises to David for twelve hundred francs a year and keep a room for himself. Feeling cornered and aware that his father had once bought the business for ten thousand francs, David protested that the price was exorbitant—“Father, you’re cutting my throat!”—but, recalculating the license and goodwill, decided he might manage the debt and accepted the terms, surprising his father.

The next day the apprentice moved David’s belongings to the attic, leaving him with three empty rooms and no funds to pay wages. When David asked for financial help, Séchard refused, saying he had already paid his share. Humiliated, David realized he was alone and that his father intended to profit from him rather than support him. He mentioned his mother’s fortune, but Séchard dismissed it as only her beauty and intelligence; any legal claim would demand a costly, prolonged suit. Resigned, David resolved to work hard and build his own future.

Séchard mentioned a “treasure” he was leaving David—Marion, a capable country girl who managed many tasks in the printing house—and then retired to a vineyard he had bought in Marsac. Uneasy about the thirty thousand francs and suspicious of David’s management, he frequently visited the workshop, fearing his son was failing, especially as the Cointet Brothers gained ground in the trade.

David, indifferent to politics, struggled to secure work. The Cointets, aligning themselves with the local clergy, began monopolizing the market and slandering him as a Liberal and untrustworthy because of his father’s reputation. Their advantageous connections and more aggressive business tactics undermined David’s clientele and opportunities.

As David’s business faltered, the Cointet Brothers offered to buy the printing house. Séchard intervened, insisting David keep the paper as essential for holding their Liberal readership, and negotiated a price of sixty thousand francs for the business—ostensibly to protect David’s interests. Despite these efforts, David was ultimately forced to sell the paper to the Cointets, marking the decline of the Séchard establishment. Séchard, increasingly detached, visited less often, though he still felt a residual connection to the old printing house.

David’s troubles deepened when Lucien Chardon, an old school friend reduced to poverty after his father’s death, sought his help. Lucien, struggling to find his place, accepted a position as a printer’s reader that David offered out of compassion. The two men’s friendship deepened as they exchanged dreams: Lucien aspired to be a poet, David to pursue natural science. Lucien mentioned his father’s ideas for refining sugar and making paper—ventures that intrigued David and offered a glimpse of alternative prospects.

These ambitions, however sincere, distracted them from the printing house’s urgent needs. While the Cointet Brothers thrived through clerical alliances and business savvy, Séchard & Son scraped by, earning only a fraction of what was necessary. The Cointets, recognizing David’s lack of business acumen, allowed his establishment to continue operating at a loss, ensuring it would not seriously compete. Meanwhile, the physical condition of the printing house reflected years of neglect born of Séchard’s avarice: repairs were postponed, maintenance ignored, and the building grew dilapidated. The workshop itself was cluttered and grimy, a visible testament to the persevering struggle of its occupants and the decline of their enterprise.

One day in May 1821, David and Lucien stood by the window watching the yard until the men left for dinner. Uncomfortable in the office’s atmosphere, David led Lucien outside to sit under the vines, where the sunlight revealed their contrasting looks and characters.

David had a robust, fighter’s build: broad shoulders, a swarthy face marked by struggle, thick black hair and deep-set eyes that suggested a wisdom mixed with melancholy and an awareness of social limits born of humble origins. Beside him, Lucien was graceful and beautiful, with a refined profile and striking blue eyes that held youthful innocence. His delicate features and slender frame gave him an almost feminine look, yet his ambition and sharp intellect were evident; he viewed society pragmatically and could contemplate morally dubious means to succeed.

Though both were low in social standing, they aspired to higher ideals, growing bitter when reality fell short. Lucien’s Gascon temperament made him bold and adventurous; David, stronger physically, was more introspective and cautious. Their friendship combined admiration and imbalance: David often yielded to Lucien’s charm, seeing himself as the more ordinary man.

For three years they shared a bond nourished by literature and art, immersing themselves in great writers and thinkers and dreaming of fame as an escape from harsh circumstances. One day David brought a new volume of André de Chénier’s poetry that moved them both. The verses offered solace and kinship with the poet’s struggles; as they read, the yard seemed to fill with the promise of their aspirations and the possibility of transcending their situation.

Their reverie was broken by Cérizet, an apprentice who brought a stranger seeking to print a monograph. Lucien, eager to impress, engaged the author, who mentioned Mme. de Bargeton, a woman Lucien admired. He blushed and thanked the visitor for her interest, betraying his admiration.

After the author left, David questioned Lucien about Mme. de Bargeton; Lucien confessed his passionate love. David warned him of social barriers, but Lucien hoped love could surmount them. He declared he would not visit Mme. de Bargeton again unless David was welcomed too, showing loyalty.

As Lucien walked through Angoulême, he reflected on the distance between his class and Mme. de Bargeton’s, feeling both hope and anxiety. The city, perched on a crag, was divided between the upper town of nobility and the lower town of trade. That split bred tension: the upper class looked down on merchants and tradespeople, resisting change and viewing outsiders with suspicion, creating deep animosity between classes.

Despite government efforts to modernize Angoulême, the upper town remained stagnant and attached to old values. The social hierarchy was rigid: the nobility kept their exclusivity, and the bourgeoisie resented their disdain. This animosity would later fuel social upheaval as the rising middle class sought recognition and respect.

In this climate, Lucien’s desire to enter Mme. de Bargeton’s world was a major challenge. His origins as a druggist’s son made him an outsider in her circle, yet his literary ambitions and charm set him apart. Mme. de Bargeton, a woman of artistic inclination, had a past that shaped her character and influenced Lucien’s path.

As Lucien navigated love and ambition, societal barriers loomed, but his determination to bridge the gap drove him. David’s mix of admiration and concern reflected the complexity of their friendship: he understood Lucien’s depth of feeling but saw the obstacles clearly. Lucien’s pledge to include David in any future visits underscored both loyalty and hope that friendship might soften social distance.

Their story would unfold against an Angoulême where tradition and modernity, nobility and bourgeoisie, ambition and constraint met, shaping both their fates.

M. de Bargeton was the great-grandson of a Bordeaux alderman ennobled under Louis XIII, but the family declined: his grandfather, “the Waster,” squandered the fortune. By 1789 the present Bargeton had only entailed lands and a house in Angoulême, most seignorial rights gone and income reduced to modest rents. In 1805 he married Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, a noblewoman from a once-prominent family; her father lived modestly on his wife's estate, farming and distilling brandy.

Mme. de Bargeton (Naïs) developed a love of music and literature under Abbé Niollant during the Revolution; he taught her languages and music, fostering independence and a certain haughty superiority. Her father’s thrift and provincial isolation reinforced this stance; she resisted marriage, valuing autonomy over submission.

Seeking a match who would not challenge her intellect, her father chose M. de Bargeton: a man of breeding but limited mind. Naïs accepted, intending to pursue social ambitions within that match. She was thirty-six to his fifty-eight; despite the age gap the marriage seemed mutually advantageous.

Settled in Angoulême, they awaited Naïs’s inheritance to move to Paris, but small income and provincial life stifled her intellectual yearnings and produced absurdities in her character. She became theatrical, expressing emotions with grand gestures; passions that might once have been elevated were trivialized by small-town existence.

Her life turned into a cycle of unfulfilled hopes and isolation, which intensified her longing for love. The rise of the Empire and military fame rekindled romantic dreams: she fell for a young officer whose death in battle left her heartbroken, amplifying regret.

For years she lived in solitude, her dignity hardening into affectation. Then M. du Châtelet arrived—charming, ambitious, and disruptive. He ingratiated himself into her circle, using music to win her favor and stirring a renewed sense of youth. Seeing her as a potential wealthy widow, he hoped to advance his aims through the connection.

Du Châtelet’s presence shocked the local elite, challenging the salon’s pretensions. Despite scandal, Mme. de Bargeton welcomed him; his entrance altered her social world and exposed tensions of ambition, love, and provincial expectation.

Political opinion was verbose and exaggerated; the Quotidienne showed only lukewarm loyalty, while Louis XVIII was seen as a Jacobin. Society’s women were mostly awkward and poorly dressed, lacking completeness in appearance and conversation. Châtelet found solace in company chiefly because of his interest in Mme. de Bargeton. Lucien, the poet, did not expect the attention. His headmaster had praised his work, and he thrilled at the prospect of being welcomed at the Hôtel de Bargeton. His sister Eve secretly supported him, saving money for new shoes and clothes and coaching him on aristocratic behavior. As he left, she felt a mix of pride and anxiety about his future.

At the Hôtel de Bargeton, Lucien found the house less grand than imagined but was enthralled by Mme. de Bargeton, who embodied the poetry he admired. She was struck by his beauty and diffidence; their meeting was full of mutual admiration. Lucien, enchanted by her, overlooked signs of faded grandeur and felt a promising connection.

Sensing a rival, Châtelet tried to assert influence over the young poet, offering unsolicited advice about Angoulême’s social dynamics and declaring his feelings for Mme. de Bargeton, claiming she would soon be his. Despite Châtelet's attempts to intimidate him, Lucien continued to visit Mme. de Bargeton, initially flattered by the nobility’s condescension but later resentful of it.

Lucien’s infatuation with Naïs (Mme. de Bargeton) deepened as she flattered and treated him as a project to be nurtured. Their relationship mixed patronage and tenderness: she sometimes acted as mentor and at other times showed genuine affection. Lucien experienced the tumult of first love; as their bond strengthened, Naïs addressed him more intimately, further binding them.

One evening Lucien found Naïs gazing at a portrait of a deceased lover, which sparked jealousy in him. Nevertheless, he boldly declared his feelings, and their relationship progressed. Naïs encouraged his literary ambitions, introducing him to music and literature while revealing her own vulnerabilities.

Under Naïs's influence, Lucien’s aspirations expanded: he resolved to achieve fame and success even at the cost of some principles. He wrote a heartfelt letter to Naïs, asking her to help his friend David, which revealed his deep emotional struggles and ambitions. Torn between love and recognition, Lucien navigated complex relationships while seeking to balance artistic aims with social advancement.

As Lucien descended from Angoulême he worried whether the great lady was angry and if she would receive David. David lacked the social graces Lucien possessed, and Lucien feared the ridicule his friend would face in polished circles.
