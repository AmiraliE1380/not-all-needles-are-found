**To Sofka**  
“Have you observed, mademoiselle, that the painters and sculptors of the Middle Ages, when they placed two figures in adoration, one on each side of a fair Saint, never failed to give them a family likeness? When you see your name among those dear to me, remember that touching harmony, and you will see this not so much as an act of homage but as an expression of brotherly affection from your devoted servant, DE BALZAC.”

**The Purse**

In the enchanting hour of twilight, artists often find themselves lost in contemplation, where the interplay of light and shadow inspires a dreamy mood. This magical time allows the painter to escape reality, immersing himself in the beauty of his work. One such artist, Hippolyte Schinner, a talented young painter, was engrossed in his nearly finished painting when he fell from his ladder, striking his head and losing consciousness.

He awoke to the soothing voice of a young woman, Adelaide, and her mother, who had rushed to his aid after hearing his fall. They tended to him with care, and although he was grateful, he felt a deep admiration for Adelaide's beauty and grace. The two women left after ensuring he was alright, leaving a lasting impression on Hippolyte.

Hippolyte had recently moved into a studio in a modest part of Paris, having gained some fame and financial stability through his art. He was devoted to his mother, who had sacrificed much for his success. Despite his rising status, he remained humble and reserved, often reflecting on his past struggles.

After a few days of recovery at home, Hippolyte found himself thinking about the two women who had helped him. He felt a connection to Adelaide, imagining shared misfortunes. Eager to see them again, he visited their apartment, which revealed signs of both elegance and poverty. Mademoiselle Leseigneur opened the door, and Hippolyte was struck by the contrast between their humble living conditions and the beauty of the young woman.

Inside, the atmosphere was a mix of warmth and weariness, with furniture that hinted at better days. The old lady, Madame Leseigneur, welcomed him, and they engaged in polite conversation. Hippolyte offered to paint a portrait of Madame Leseigneur, recognizing the emotional significance of preserving her likeness. She accepted, and the offer deepened their connection.

As they spoke, Madame Leseigneur revealed her past, including her late husband, a naval officer who had died in battle. The conversation turned to the challenges they faced, and Hippolyte felt a strong desire to help them, though he was careful not to express pity.

Adelaide, noticing the tension, changed the subject, and the atmosphere lightened. However, their conversation was interrupted by the arrival of two men, one of whom was an older gentleman dressed in a coat that reflected the complexities of post-revolutionary France. His presence added a layer of intrigue to the evening.

Hippolyte's admiration for Adelaide grew as he observed her interactions with the newcomers. He felt a mix of hope and uncertainty about his feelings for her, aware of the social barriers that might separate them. The evening unfolded with a blend of warmth and underlying tension, as Hippolyte navigated his emotions and the realities of their lives.

In this delicate balance of admiration, aspiration, and the complexities of social class, the story of Hippolyte and the Leseigneur family begins to unfold, revealing the intricate tapestry of love, sacrifice, and the pursuit of happiness amidst the shadows of their circumstances.

In a scene depicting the remnants of the Monarchy, a lean, aged man, referred to as a Chevalier, stands in the shadow of a more vibrant figure, reminiscent of Louis XIV’s light infantry. This old man, a mystery to those around him, is characterized by his faded attire and diminished intellect, evoking curiosity about his past connections, particularly with his friend’s deceased wife.

The narrative unfolds in the presence of Madame de Rouville and her daughter, Adelaide, who engage in a card game with the Chevalier. The atmosphere is filled with playful banter, but the Chevalier’s familiarity with Madame de Rouville seems to annoy Adelaide. As the game progresses, the Chevalier expresses his consistent bad luck, while Adelaide offers to help, revealing her affection for the old gentleman.

Hippolyte, a young painter, is captivated by Adelaide’s beauty and becomes increasingly enamored with her during his visits. Their interactions grow more intimate, and despite the backdrop of poverty in their dwelling, Hippolyte finds joy in their connection. Over the course of a week, their relationship deepens, marked by shared laughter and unspoken understanding, as they navigate the complexities of their feelings.

However, Hippolyte’s happiness is marred by suspicions regarding the financial situation of Madame de Rouville and Adelaide. He grapples with the idea that they may be living off gambling, leading to a sense of distrust. His concerns are exacerbated when he discovers that his purse, containing fifteen louis d’or, has gone missing after a visit. This incident shatters his idealized view of Adelaide and her mother, prompting him to question their integrity.

Despite his heartbreak, Hippolyte remains drawn to Adelaide, struggling to reconcile his love for her with the painful reality of the theft. He seeks solace in his art, but the memories of their time together haunt him. A chance encounter with a friend reveals the rumors surrounding Madame de Rouville, further complicating his feelings.

Determined to confront his suspicions, Hippolyte visits the ladies again, only to find Adelaide looking pale and distressed. Their reunion is bittersweet, as he attempts to mend the rift caused by his doubts. During a card game, Adelaide discreetly replaces his lost purse with a beautifully crafted one, revealing her affection and thoughtfulness. This gesture rekindles his hope and love for her.

As the evening unfolds, the arrival of the vice-admiral, an old friend of Madame de Rouville, brings unexpected revelations. He defends the Baroness against the rumors, affirming their dignity and honor. The atmosphere shifts as the characters navigate their relationships, culminating in Hippolyte’s heartfelt proposal to Adelaide, which is met with surprise and joy.

The story concludes with a sense of unity among the characters, as they embrace their intertwined fates, leaving behind the shadows of doubt and suspicion. The narrative captures the essence of love, trust, and the complexities of human relationships, set against the backdrop of societal expectations and personal struggles.
