Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman who married a wealthy Bordeaux heiress, became attached to her Gascon estate, the château de Lanstrac. He avoided the Revolution by going to Martinique in 1790 and returned to find Lanstrac well managed by Mathias, a progressive notary’s clerk. After Madame de Manerville’s death in 1810, the old man grew miserly and deprived his only son, Paul, of resources.

Paul came home from college in 1810 and endured three years of strict paternal control that stifled his character. Though physically brave, he lacked the will to oppose his father and lost moral courage; his repressed feelings made him timid in society. Confined by the old mansion, he failed to connect with peers and felt out of place at his father’s royalist gatherings among the remnants of nobility, where he was bored and isolated.

To escape monotony, Paul practiced fencing and riding—accomplishments his father insisted a gentleman possess—and read novels. His father’s death finally freed him: he inherited substantial, well-kept estates but retained an aversion to Bordeaux and Lanstrac. Seeking a new life, Paul invested in consols and traveled across Europe, serving at several embassies. Years of indulgence and poor management depleted much of his fortune, and he resolved to return to Bordeaux as a country gentleman. Conscious of rank, he felt compelled to marry well, believing his foreign experience refined him.

Despite extravagance, Paul had wronged no woman and kept a reputation for integrity; his household remained tasteful. He told his friend de Marsay of his wish to marry. De Marsay warned that marriage brings ridicule, responsibility, and loss of individuality. Paul replied he sought emotional companionship and was ready for family duties; de Marsay remained skeptical.

Despite earlier doubts, the Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux in winter 1821 determined to enact his plans. Restoring the family mansion and furnishing it in Parisian taste bolstered his reputation for elegance; his manners quickly won him a leading place in Bordeaux’s royalist society. An old marquise dubbed him “the pink of fashion,” a phrase liberals mocked and royalists admired.

Paul de Manerville enjoyed attention. Charming, witty, and solicitous toward women, he combined foppery with genuine care for his appearance. Of medium height, healthy complexion, and graceful movements, he preferred receiving happiness to bestowing it and sought in marriage the understanding and encouragement he thought he lacked. Young and averse to domestic routine, he nonetheless accepted that provincial life required matrimony and variety. His inclination was sharpened by a secret love for Mademoiselle Evangelista.

Years earlier an affluent Spaniard, Evangelista, had settled in Bordeaux and, through fortune and connections, entered noble salons. His Creole wife, from an illustrious Spanish family, sustained their social standing with extravagant tastes, indulging whims regardless of cost and backed by her devoted husband. After Evangelista’s death in 1813, Madame Evangelista—now a wealthy widow with a beautiful daughter—continued to live lavishly even as her fortune declined.

By Paul’s return Natalie was striking yet unmarried, largely because her mother’s extravagance discouraged suitors; gossip exaggerated the situation, claiming only a prince could afford her. Accustomed to luxury and adulation, she remained largely ignorant of life beyond privilege.

Six months after Paul’s arrival they met at a high-society event and found mutual charm. Madame Evangelista perceived his interest and hoped he would become her son-in-law; Paul began to picture Natalie as his future wife. He had known of the Evangelista fortune since childhood, which eased his approach. Dismissing warnings about their extravagance with Parisian disdain, he navigated provincial gossip without deterrence. Madame invited him to formal dinners, and his appetite for marriage deepened.

Spending time with the Evangelistas, he felt proud of their company; society soon treated him as Natalie’s suitor, and he relished the attention. Royalist circles called the match prudent given Natalie’s beauty and her mother’s intelligence and connections. Madame, forty and still striking, retained a reputation for virtue that enhanced her allure. Their relationship suited both: Madame saw Paul as a way to advance socially in Paris; Paul felt flattered.

A year after his return, people concluded he was courting Natalie, though neither mother nor daughter pressed for marriage. Natalie’s reserve intrigued him; he rationalized marriage as a source of happiness and security, brushing aside likely difficulties. Sheltered and naive, Natalie was attracted to Paul’s charm but inexperienced; Paul, enamored yet cautious, wondered if he could manage someone who might not fully appreciate him.

The narrative widens to note that in French society marriage is often governed by negotiations over marriage contracts—frequently overlooked in literature but crucial in shaping couples’ lives. Those impending discussions will influence Paul and Natalie’s future, foreshadowing social and personal complexities ahead.

In winter 1822 Paul de Manerville sought Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista’s hand through his great-aunt, the Baronne de Maulincour of Médoc. After discussing the dowry with Madame Evangelista, the baroness reported the match likely: Natalie’s dowry would be her patrimony, with her mother contributing nothing. She urged Paul to consult Maître Mathias to protect his interests in the contract.

Paul, anxious, visited Madame Evangelista, who privately admitted she owed her daughter a large sum from her late husband’s estate and could not pay. Fearing Paul might withdraw and damage Natalie’s prospects, she summoned Maître Solonet, who assured her they could conceal the difficulty and craft a favorable contract.

Mathias and Solonet met that evening. Mathias, cautious and traditional, sought to safeguard Paul; Solonet preferred practical, flexible solutions. While Paul and Natalie exchanged tender moments at dinner, the notaries debated financial risk: Mathias warned that Natalie's family had squandered wealth; Madame Evangelista and Solonet suggested selling her house to cover debts. They compromised reluctantly: Madame Evangelista would place an entail on her property, protecting Natalie while limiting Paul’s exposure. The marriage could proceed, but tensions remained, foreshadowing further tests of love, ambition, and money.

The next day Elie Magus, then in Bordeaux, answered Madame Evangelista’s summons expecting to discuss jewels for Comte Paul’s fiancée. Instead he appraised the mother’s possessions, valuing her necklace and earrings at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” at 100,000 francs, warning that selling them would incur heavy loss.

That conversation sparked staff rumors and society gossip about the marriage’s stability: whispers that Madame Evangelista was selling her house and the union might be jeopardized. Notaries denied the claims, but public skepticism—fueled by envy of the Evangelistas’ wealth—persisted. Paul organizing a hunting party intensified speculation. Madame de Gyas, feigning concern, relayed the tales to Natalie and her mother; they dismissed the gossip as self-interested, especially from other mothers angling for advantageous matches.

To counter the rumors and display her means, Madame Evangelista planned a grand fête—the fête of the camellias—combined with finalizing the marriage contract. As signing approached she grew uneasy about the terms, and on the day, while guests admired the festive scene and the enamored couple, the notaries revealed that if Paul died first Natalie would inherit only a fraction of her expected fortune. Struck by this, Madame Evangelista resolved—despite the personal cost—to protect her daughter’s future, shifting from careless spender to determined strategist bent on reclaiming the family fortune and influencing Paul, setting the stage for future conflict.

The fête had briefly distracted Paul from his anxieties, which returned that night. He feared Madame Evangelista might have deceived him without Mathias’s intervention, but comforted himself that in two days Natalie would be his wife and their finances settled, resolving to remain watchful. Unbeknownst to him, his future had shifted: the astute woman he trusted had become an adversary, contemplating withdrawing her interests from their shared future. He failed to perceive the cunning of his Creole mother-in-law—part European intellect, part tropical passion, part Eastern indifference—awakened by the prospect of losing her daughter. With a day until the marriage, Madame Evangelista realized she could shape the couple’s life: Natalie trusted her implicitly, and a single conversation might alter their fate.

The next morning, seated on Natalie’s bed, Madame Evangelista recalled their harmonious past and mourned the thought of her daughter bound to a man she might have to obey. Natalie dismissed obedience, expecting love to govern. Madame warned that conjugal love can fade if not carefully cultivated and urged Natalie to establish authority from the start. She proposed leaving to avoid complicating the new household and counseled Natalie to feign obedience while subtly asserting dominance.

Their talk outlined practical marital strategies: keep an active social life to hold Paul’s interest; avoid constant proximity that breeds indifference; preserve allure and independence; and keep him entertained so the relationship stays vibrant. Overwhelmed, Natalie sought guidance. Madame promised continued contact and visits but insisted that some separation was necessary for Natalie’s happiness and prospects.

When Paul arrived he tried to hide his unease; Madame noticed and offered to sell her jewelry to support the couple, framing it as a sacrifice for Natalie’s vanity and future. Paul, moved, accepted. Solonet, the notary, brought news of potential buyers for Madame Evangelista’s property, which could provide a substantial financial boost; Natalie agreed to the sale.

Determined to secure her daughter’s prospects, Madame resolved to step back from their Parisian life and support them from afar so Natalie and Paul could form their own dynamic. As the midnight wedding neared, preparations carried a foreboding air and guests wondered at her absence. After the ceremony she played the grieving mother, extracted promises from Paul to secure her influence, and sent the newlyweds to Paris. Alone with Paul, Natalie began to assert her authority, setting the stage for the complex dynamics of their marriage.

Five years later, in November, Comte Paul de Manerville, cloaked and somber, arrived at the home of his old friend Monsieur Mathias in Bordeaux. The retired notary was absent; his housekeeper led Paul to the late Madame Mathias’s room, where he stayed until evening. When Mathias returned and found Paul there on the anniversary of his wife's death, he showed concern and embarrassment over public notices of Paul’s financial ruin. Mathias blamed Paul’s marriage to Natalie Evangelista for his downfall, though he was relieved to have preserved the estate titles even as the usufruct was lost.

Paul appeared altered but calm. Over a meal, Mathias lamented the seizure of Paul’s properties. Paul then revealed he had left Natalie without her knowledge and intended to sail to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune. Mathias, shocked that a count would enter commerce, was told Paul no longer considered himself a count and planned to succeed under another name. Paul outlined enormous debts from lavish spending and gifts to Natalie; he admitted selling assets and borrowing heavily from usurers to sustain their lifestyle, which produced the present catastrophe.

Despite Mathias’s doubts and skepticism about de Marsay—who was supposed to send funds—Paul insisted India was his only chance and vowed to return wealthy by forty. The next day Mathias received de Marsay’s bill of exchange confirming some funds would arrive, but Paul remained determined to leave. Mathias lamented losing his friend as Paul boarded the ship and waved goodbye.

At sea, Paul was consumed by memories and his love for Natalie, torn between regret and resolve. Struck by seasickness, he spent days in his cabin reflecting on his choices. When he recovered, he opened letters from Natalie and de Marsay. Natalie’s letter revealed anguish and a vow to support him: she pledged her jewels to pay his debts and promised to live at Lanstrac with her mother and economize. De Marsay’s letter detailed Paul’s ruin and exposed the mother-in-law’s profiteering. Confronted with love and treachery, Paul felt anger, helplessness, and remorse as he sailed away from France uncertain of the future.
