**A Second Home**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Clara Bell*  

**DEDICATION**  
To Madame la Comtesse Louise de Turheim as a token of remembrance and affectionate respect.

---

The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once a dark and winding street near the Hotel de Ville, ended at the Rue Martroi, marked by a turnstile until its removal in 1823. The street was narrow, less than six feet wide, and often flooded with gutter water, making it perpetually muddy. In summer, the sunlight briefly illuminated the street, but the dampness remained.

Most old Paris streets resembled this gloomy labyrinth, where historical curiosities could still be found. One notable house at the corner of Rue du Tourniquet had iron rings on its walls, remnants of chains used for public safety. This ancient structure was built to combat dampness, with cellars elevated above ground level and a door accessed by three steps. The windows were high and barred, offering little light to the small rooms inside.

Inside, an old woman sat by the fireplace, nursing a fire to cook a stew. The room, though sparsely furnished with a few chairs and kitchen utensils, was clean and tidy. The old woman’s pale face matched the somber atmosphere of the street, and she seemed resigned to her life of poverty.

Her daughter, a young needlewoman, worked diligently at an embroidery frame, while her mother occupied herself with lace-making. The two women shared a modest existence, illuminated by a lamp at night, and their window displayed a few frail plants that added a touch of life to their dreary surroundings.

Passersby often wondered how the girl maintained her color in such a dark place. Some men, noticing her beauty and diligence, might have considered helping her escape her circumstances. The mother, hopeful for her daughter’s future, would watch the street for potential suitors, while the daughter remained focused on her work, occasionally glancing up at the world outside.

One day, the mother noticed a new face—a man in a brown coat—who passed by regularly. She encouraged her daughter to look out for him, believing he might be a suitable match. The girl, initially indifferent, became curious about this stranger, who seemed different from the usual passersby.

When the man appeared, he was tall and solemn, with a piercing gaze that unsettled the old woman. Caroline, the daughter, felt a mix of curiosity and sympathy for him, sensing a depth of suffering in his expression. This was the first time a man had stirred such feelings in her, as she typically focused on her work and her mother’s chatter about potential protectors.

The man’s presence sparked a new dynamic in their lives, as Caroline began to contemplate the possibility of a connection beyond her mundane existence. The old woman’s hopes for her daughter’s future intertwined with Caroline’s emerging feelings, hinting at the potential for change in their lives.

For over two months, the enigmatic figure known as the "Black Gentleman" exhibited erratic behavior, often avoiding the Rue du Tourniquet where Madame Crochard and her daughter Caroline lived. His lack of interest in the two women puzzled Madame Crochard, who was eager for his attention. However, one day in September, Caroline's vibrant presence caught his eye, prompting a brief but significant exchange of glances that established a silent connection between them. From that day forward, he began to pass by regularly, and their mutual interest grew, marked by subtle expressions of kindness and understanding.

As winter progressed, their daily encounters became a source of comfort for both. Caroline could sense when the stranger was troubled, while he noted her struggles with work and financial worries. Despite their growing bond, they remained cautious, never speaking or revealing their feelings, perhaps fearing that deeper acquaintance might lead to disappointment or heartache.

Madame Crochard, frustrated with her daughter's indecisiveness, began to show signs of bitterness, especially as the harsh winter brought financial strain. One night, the Gentleman in Black overheard a conversation between the two women, revealing their dire situation. Moved by their plight, he secretly left money for Caroline, who expressed her gratitude through a gesture that he pretended not to notice.

In May, Caroline suggested a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman, now identified as Roger, unexpectedly joined them. During the outing, he was captivated by Caroline's beauty and innocence, and their conversation flowed easily, revealing their shared hopes and dreams. Caroline spoke fondly of her mother’s sacrifices and her desire for a better life, while Roger found himself drawn to her genuine spirit.

As they explored the picturesque landscape, their connection deepened. Caroline's laughter and openness brought joy to Roger, who had previously been burdened by sadness. Their interactions became more intimate, and by the time they reached the village of Saint-Leu, Caroline affectionately called him "Monsieur Roger." 

Madame Crochard, initially skeptical, eventually joined in their enjoyment, though she remained somewhat oblivious to the budding romance between her daughter and Roger. After a delightful meal, the trio attended a village ball, where Caroline and Roger danced together, their hands and hearts intertwined in a moment of shared happiness.

As the day came to an end, Roger expressed a hint of sadness, prompting Caroline to question why happiness should be confined to their outing. This moment of mutual understanding hinted at their unspoken feelings for each other. They playfully ran down the avenue, and in a tender moment, Roger kissed Caroline's hand, solidifying their connection before they returned to the reality of their lives.

In summary, the story captures the evolving relationship between Caroline and the Black Gentleman, highlighting themes of love, social struggle, and the longing for a better life amidst the challenges they face. Their silent bond transforms into a deeper connection, suggesting hope for their future despite the uncertainties that lie ahead.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The story of Roger and Caroline continues in modern Paris, where they reside in a newly furnished apartment designed for newlyweds. The building, located on Rue Taitbout, features fresh decor and a harmonious atmosphere that reflects their youthful love. Caroline has been living in this charming space for over a month, filled with elegant furnishings and decorations that delight her senses.

As September 1816 begins, Caroline prepares for Roger's visit, choosing a beautiful green dress trimmed with chinchilla. She eagerly waits on the balcony, watching the bustling Boulevard below, hoping to catch a glimpse of him. After a quarter of an hour, her anticipation turns to frustration, but her spirits lift when she finally spots Roger approaching in a tilbury.

When Roger arrives, they embrace passionately, expressing their joy at being reunited after three long days apart. However, Roger reveals he must attend a meeting with the Attorney-General, which disappoints Caroline. Despite her initial pout, she quickly reassures him that seeing him is the only spectacle she desires. Roger suggests she go to the theater with her mother, promising to join her if he can.

Caroline playfully interrupts him, reminding him that discussing business detracts from their love. They return to the drawing-room, where Roger notices Caroline's old embroidery frame, now refurbished. She expresses her desire to contribute her earnings to the poor, feeling grateful for the life Roger has provided her. Caroline asks if she can take on the name "Caroline de Bellefeuille," and upon receiving a positive response, she joyfully embraces the idea.

As she sits on Roger's lap, she reminisces about their past and expresses her affection for their home. She excitedly leads him to the piano, eager to showcase her musical talent. However, as the time slips away, Roger reluctantly prepares to leave. They share a tender goodbye, and Caroline watches him depart, savoring the moment as he drives away, leaving her longing for his return.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Five years after Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille settled in her charming home on Rue Taitbout, we find her in a serene domestic scene. In the blue drawing-room, her four-year-old son, Charles, is energetically playing with a rocking horse, while Caroline, now twenty-four, reflects on her life filled with love and happiness. She has embraced motherhood wholeheartedly, focusing on nurturing her children and cultivating their appreciation for goodness and beauty. Despite her humble pleasures, she remains blissfully unaware of societal judgments, content in her relationship with Roger, whom she knows only by name.

On May 6, 1822, the anniversary of a pivotal day in her life, Caroline prepares for a special dinner, recalling joyful memories of their time together in Bellefeuille. Roger arrives, and they share tender moments with their children, including their newborn daughter, Eugenie. Roger presents Caroline with a marriage portion for Eugenie, explaining the financial differences between their children based on gender roles and expectations.

A few days later, Caroline rushes to visit her ailing mother, Madame Crochard, who is surrounded by friends and a priest. Unbeknownst to Caroline, her mother’s friends are scheming to benefit from her potential inheritance. As Caroline arrives, she learns that her mother has been confessing to the priest, revealing secrets that could impact Caroline's future.

The narrative shifts to a young barrister, Monsieur de Granville, who is navigating his career and personal ambitions. He receives a letter from his father urging him to marry Mademoiselle Angelique Bontems, who has recently lost her sister and stands to inherit a fortune. Granville reflects on his childhood affection for Angelique, despite his parents' disapproval of their relationship.

As Granville travels to Bayeux to see Angelique, he is struck by the contrast between his vibrant life in Paris and the somber atmosphere of her home. His father encourages him to pursue the marriage, emphasizing the benefits of the alliance. Granville, however, is conflicted about the match, believing he could achieve greater happiness and success without it.

Upon arriving, Granville is captivated by Angelique’s beauty during a church service, reigniting his feelings for her. Their brief interaction is charged with emotion, but Angelique quickly reverts to her reserved demeanor, leaving Granville yearning for a deeper connection.

The story intertwines the lives of Caroline, Roger, and Granville, exploring themes of love, ambition, and the complexities of familial relationships against the backdrop of societal expectations. As Caroline navigates her role as a mother and partner, Granville grapples with his aspirations and the weight of tradition, setting the stage for the unfolding drama of their intertwined fates.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For about two weeks following the announcement of the Vicomte de Granville's engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he visited her frequently, observing her character. Angelique, engaged in preparing her trousseau, rarely initiated discussions about religion. When Granville playfully handled her rosary or made light of religious practices, she would quietly reclaim it, asserting her belief in the Church's teachings and the importance of faith in raising children. Her conviction sometimes tempted Granville to embrace her views, but he mistakenly conflated his desire for her with love.

On the day of their marriage contract, Madame Bontems insisted that Granville respect Angelique's religious practices, which he agreed to without hesitation, charmed by her innocence. After their wedding, they moved to Paris, where Angelique persuaded Granville to choose a large ground-floor apartment near a church, emphasizing its suitability for family life. Granville, focused on his new role as public prosecutor, left the home decoration to Angelique, who filled their space with austere furnishings that reflected her provincial upbringing and strict religious views.

Granville soon found the decor cold and uninviting, contrasting sharply with the vibrant Parisian life he had hoped for. Despite his dissatisfaction, he blamed himself for not guiding Angelique better in her choices. Their early married life was marked by a superficial harmony, as Granville immersed himself in work while Angelique adhered strictly to her religious practices, often disguising her dietary restrictions under the guise of carelessness.

As time passed, Granville became increasingly aware of the constraints imposed by Angelique's beliefs, particularly when she declined invitations to social events, citing headaches. Their first significant conflict arose when Granville insisted she attend a ball, leading to a bitter argument about her obligations as his wife. Angelique's refusal to conform to societal expectations and her insistence on religious duties created a rift between them.

The situation worsened when a priest from Bayeux, who had previously guided Angelique, returned to Paris and influenced her to adopt a more rigid interpretation of her faith. This led to further misunderstandings and conflicts, particularly regarding social engagements and the nature of their marriage. Granville's attempts to assert his authority were met with resistance, culminating in Angelique seeking guidance from the Pope on her right to attend social functions.

The Pope's response, which emphasized a wife's duty to accompany her husband, only deepened the divide between them, as Angelique and her priest interpreted it as a challenge to their beliefs. Granville, frustrated by the increasing strictness of their household, began to withdraw socially, leading to a stifling atmosphere dominated by Angelique's bigotry.

As Granville observed the changes in his wife, he recognized the toll that her rigid adherence to religious principles was taking on their marriage. The once-vibrant Angelique became a figure of austere piety, her beauty dulled by her self-imposed restrictions. Granville's love turned to despair as he faced the reality of living with a woman who prioritized duty over affection.

Despite having children, Granville felt increasingly isolated, unable to share joy or companionship with his family. He sought solace in work, but even that was marred by Angelique's attempts to convert him back to her strict religious views. Their home, once a place of potential happiness, became a prison of obligation and coldness, leaving Granville to navigate the complexities of a marriage that had devolved into a struggle between love and duty.

Ultimately, Granville's efforts to maintain a semblance of normalcy in their lives were thwarted by Angelique's unwavering commitment to her beliefs, leading him to withdraw further from both his family and society. The weight of their differences became unbearable, leaving Granville to confront the reality of a life devoid of the warmth and connection he had once cherished.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Between 1806 and 1825, the Comte and Comtesse de Granville's household experienced no significant events, despite the emotional turmoil within. After losing her husband's affection, Madame de Granville remained unchanged, fervently praying for guidance to win him back, yet Granville increasingly distanced himself, occupying a separate floor of their home. Each morning, a ritual occurred where the housekeeper would inquire about Granville's well-being, only to receive polite refusals from his valet, indicating his absence due to court obligations.

Madame de Granville, now thirty-five and looking older, awaited her husband's return with a mix of hope and resentment. Her attempts to engage him during rare moments at home often resulted in tension, as she sought to assert her moral superiority while he grew more detached. When Granville declined a prestigious judicial position in the provinces, speculation arose among the Countess's friends about his motivations, leading to insinuations of infidelity that deeply troubled her.

Unaware of the true nature of her husband's life, Madame de Granville clung to the belief that he was incapable of wrongdoing. However, her health deteriorated under the strain of her suspicions, exacerbated by her religious fervor. During Lent in 1822, she fell ill, and despite her efforts to be more affectionate, her underlying bitterness often resurfaced.

In late May, after a brief recovery, she encountered the Abbe Fontanon, who delivered devastating news: Granville had been living with a mistress for seven years and had fathered two children, squandering their family's fortune. This revelation shattered her illusions, prompting a whirlwind of emotions and a determination to confront her husband.

Meanwhile, Granville's life outside the marriage continued, as he maintained a relationship with Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille, who was grieving her own circumstances. Roger, a friend, expressed his hope for a future together, despite the complications of his own marriage.

The narrative shifts back to Madame de Granville, who, upon learning of her husband's infidelity, fainted. When she regained consciousness, she confronted Granville, questioning his love and fidelity. He responded coldly, asserting that her devotion to religious duties had neglected his emotional needs, leading to his estrangement.

Granville explained that true love requires sacrifice and understanding, which he felt was absent in their marriage. He criticized her for prioritizing her spiritual obligations over their relationship, suggesting that her rigid adherence to virtue had driven him away. The Countess, in despair, pleaded for forgiveness and promised to change, but Granville remained resolute, stating that his love had faded after years of suffering.

Their conversation revealed deep-seated issues in their marriage, with Granville expressing that he could not simply rekindle feelings that had long since extinguished. The Countess's attempts to reconcile were met with skepticism, as Granville questioned whether she could truly understand the nature of love.

As the dialogue continued, Granville's indifference and the Countess's desperation highlighted the emotional chasm between them. Despite her pleas, he remained unmoved, emphasizing that love cannot be commanded or forced. The story culminates in a poignant moment where the Countess realizes the depth of her husband's disillusionment, leaving her to grapple with the reality of their fractured relationship.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In December 1833, a man with white hair and a weary face walked along the Rue Gaillon at midnight. He paused to observe a dim light in an attic window of a modest two-story house. A young man, Horace Bianchon, emerged from the house and recognized the older man as Comte de Granville. Despite Granville's insistence that he was still strong at fifty-five, Bianchon expressed concern for his safety while walking alone at night.

Granville revealed that he had been observing a shadow in the attic, intrigued by the person working there. He dismissed any personal interest, claiming he had grown indifferent to human emotions over the years. He expressed disdain for sentimentality, stating that he preferred to connect with plants and animals rather than people. Bianchon, however, urged him to consider the emotional toll of life, but Granville remained unmoved, believing that his children were too preoccupied with their own lives to care for him.

Bianchon insisted that Granville had a heart capable of feeling, and he offered to help him revive it. He then shared that the attic was occupied by a woman named Caroline Crochard, who was devoted to a man named Solvet, a gambler who exploited her love. Caroline had sacrificed her comfort and well-being for Solvet, even selling her hair to provide for him. Bianchon described her dire situation, revealing that she was ill and her children were starving.

Granville, initially indifferent, was visibly affected when he learned Caroline's name. He felt a deep pain and acknowledged that Bianchon had stirred emotions he thought were long buried. Despite this, he refused to help Caroline, choosing instead to give a large sum of money to a scavenger with the condition that he squander it on vices, thus finding a twisted sense of satisfaction in the act.

As Granville returned home, he was surprised to find his son, Eugene, waiting for him. Eugene explained that a young man named Charles Crochard, who had been arrested, claimed to be Granville's son. Granville confirmed the truth of this claim and gave Eugene money to assist in the matter. He expressed a desire to impart wisdom to his son about marriage and the importance of understanding one's partner, emphasizing the consequences of neglecting social responsibilities.

The narrative concludes with Granville preparing to leave for Italy, reflecting on the complexities of familial relationships and the burdens of his past. The story captures themes of indifference, sacrifice, and the struggle between emotional detachment and the longing for connection.
