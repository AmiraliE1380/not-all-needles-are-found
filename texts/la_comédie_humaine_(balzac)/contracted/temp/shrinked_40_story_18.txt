“HULLO! There is that old Box-coat again!”

A lawyer’s clerk—Simonnin, a gutter-jumper of thirteen or fourteen—threw a crumb like a bullet through the open window. It hit the hat of a stranger crossing the Rue Vivienne courtyard outside Maître Derville’s rooms. Boucard, the head clerk, scolded the boy: “Come, Simonnin, don’t play tricks… however poor a client may be, he is still a man!”

Simonnin belonged to Boucard’s extended household: errand-runner, billet-doux carrier, street kid by habit, pettifogger by destiny—ruthless, impudent, greedy, idle, and often sharing a miserable lodging with an old mother. He sneered at the stranger’s “box-coat” when Boucard called him “old Box-coat.”

Around the long table Godeschal, improvising mock eloquence while dictating an appeal, turned the arrival into sport. He parodied high rhetoric—“But, in his noble and beneficent wisdom, his Majesty, Louis the Eighteenth…”—and proposed tricking the visitor by saying the master saw clients only between two and three in the morning. The copying clerks laughed; Boucard muttered dates and figures. Their camaraderie—gossip, diction, and copying appeals—mingled with work.

The office was one of those Parisian law dens—large, ill-lit, with a stove-pipe slanting to a bricked-up fireplace and a marble chimney-piece littered with bread, triangles of Brie, pork cutlets, glasses, bottles, and Boucard’s chocolate. Food, an overheated stove, and old papers combined with mud and snow tracked in by the clerks. A rotating desk, piles of broutilles, yellow seizure notices, and rows of dust-encrusted bundles tied with red tape gave the place its peculiar physiognomy. It was a schoolroom for youths, a passage for clients, and a laboratory for the chief; scrupulously neglected yet carefully handed down with relics of older practice. For clients an attorney’s office was one of Paris’s most repulsive marts; inside, absorbed in law’s drama, the surroundings mattered less.

A knock interrupted their talk. Five clerks, bright-eyed and hungry, called “Come in!” Boucard kept his nose in the bills. The new-comer entered humbly, attempted to smile, but the clerks’ indifference made him lower his eyes. He addressed Simonnin: “Monsieur, is your master at home?” The messenger feigned deafness. Godeschal, with his mouth full of bread, and the others bantered as always.

“This is the fifth time I have called,” the stranger said. “I wish to speak to M. Derville.”

“M. Derville is in bed; he does no serious work till midnight,” Godeschal replied. “If you’ll lay your case before us, we could help you as well as he can—”

The stranger refused to speak to anyone but the lawyer. He looked tired; there were no chairs—attorneys seldom provided many. The inferior client stands, grumbles, and wastes no time, “which is not allowed for when the bill is taxed,” an old lawyer had said.

“I will wait till he is up,” the stranger said.

Boucard, finishing the bill, rose, sniffed his chocolate, surveyed the old man from head to foot, and grimaced at his coat. Assuming no fee could be wrung from such a client, he brusquely advised him to return at one in the morning. The clerks resumed their meal, indifferent to the old man’s embarrassment.

“I will come again to-night,” the stranger repeated, with the tenacity of the unfortunate who hopes to catch humanity at fault.

When poverty confronts Justice and Benevolence with their failures, it pleads the harder. The clerks, however, mocked him as soon as he left. “He looks as if he had been buried and dug up again,” one said. They guessed his background—colonel, retired concierge, brewer, porter—trading insults like cards. Simonnin called out from the window, and, prodded by the clerks’ mirth, the old man returned.

“Will you be kind enough to leave your name?” Boucard asked.

“Chabert.”

“The Colonel who was killed at Eylau?” Hure chimed in.

“The same, monsieur,” the old man answered with antique simplicity, and turned to go.

The clerks exploded—cat-calls, exclamations. Desroches was slapped for owing a theatre ticket. The jesting resumed with gusto: which theatre? the Opera? Madame Saqui? Godeschal, pedantic even in mockery, argued about what counts as a “play” and whether Curtius’ Waxworks could be paid for as such—applying legal habits to trivialities: define the fact, consider the point of law. The office’s speech was always legalistic, even in banter.

Godeschal doubted whether the man might be making game of them—yet admitted, “On my honor, Colonel Chabert is really and truly dead. His wife is married again to Comte Ferraud, Councillor of State. Madame Ferraud is one of our clients.” Boucard closed the interruption: business must go on. The appeal had to be copied and filed before the Fourth Chamber met that day; judgment would be given. The clerks were ordered back to work; they grumbled that nothing got done amid such distractions.

One clerk observed that, if the stranger had really been Colonel Chabert, Simonnin’s mock-deafness might have earned a boot in the right place—a more physical verdict than Godeschal’s legalistic reasoning. Eventually they agreed to go to the Français to see Talma in “Nero,” Simonnin to the pit; then they resumed dictation.

“Given in June eighteen hundred and fourteen (in words),” Godeschal read, and the copying-clerks’ pens creaked over stamped paper like trapped insects. He tried several openings—“We hope that my lords on the Bench will not be less magnanimous than the august author of the decree…”—and then hesitated: “I must read my sentence through again. I do not understand it myself.” Practicalities intruded—figures, particulars, petty scorn—and the boys went on.

The scene—the youthful camaraderie, petty malice, mixture of professional charlatanry and genuine craft, and grotesque cheerfulness amid small concerns—was one of those remembered “good times.”

At about one in the morning a man calling himself Colonel Chabert knocked at Maître Derville’s door; Derville was an attorney at the Court of First Instance in the Department of the Seine. The porter said Derville had not returned, but the visitor insisted he had an appointment and was shown upstairs to the lawyer’s rooms, where the head clerk was arranging papers for the next day’s cases. The clerk explained that Derville reserved the night for studying his cases when silence favored thought; the young lawyer earned well by this disciplined, spare life.

When Derville arrived in evening dress, both he and his clerk were struck by the man’s appearance. The Colonel was dry and lean; a wig partly hid his forehead, his eyes seemed veiled, and his pale, livid face gave a corpse-like impression. A scar ran from the nape across the skull above the right eye—an old wound that made his appearance shocking. “Monsieur,” said Derville, “to whom have I the honor of speaking?” “To Colonel Chabert.” “Which?” “He who was killed at Eylau,” answered the stranger.

Taken for mad, the man nevertheless asked to confide his position to the lawyer. Derville, used to odd clients, dismissed his clerk and asked him to be brief. The Colonel then related his story with a mixture of lucidity and the hesitation of one long disbelieved.

He commanded a cavalry regiment at Eylau and had been instrumental in Murat’s charge. In action he was struck by a Russian officer with a blow that split his skull; he fell from his horse and was trampled as regiments passed over him. Imperial surgeons reported him dead and a certificate of death was made out. Yet he had not perished. He supposed tetanus or catalepsy left him apparently lifeless when he was stripped and thrown into a common grave in the hurry of battle.

Later, at Stuttgart, an old quartermaster named Boutin—whom the Colonel had once saved—explained how his horse had fallen over him, shielding him from trampling and bullets and leaving a space that preserved his head. When he came to, he found himself buried among the dead. By feeling about he found an arm that served as a lever and worked his way through the piled bodies until he saw daylight through the snow. His head was cut open but coagulated blood and skin may have plugged it enough to keep him alive. Fainting and reviving, he shouted until he was finally dug out by a woman and her husband, who took him to their hovel. He remained for six months between life and death, speechless or delirious, then was admitted to the hospital at Heilsberg.

He lacked the documents that would prove his identity—the surgeon Sparchmann’s deposition and the notarial declaration he had made at Heilsberg—because war had separated him from them. Wandering afterward beggared and scorned, he had tried to obtain from German authorities the proofs that might restore him to France and to his rights, but had been laughed at, regarded as mad, even locked up at Stuttgart for two years. On his release with ten thalers and a key of the fields he set out to find his wife and his fortune.

He knew Derville was lawyer to the Countess Ferraud, who was indeed his wife. Chabert said that after many fruitless attempts to interest lawyers he had come to Derville because he hoped to get the German documents. He described his misery: naked when he left the hospital, wandering, begging, enduring insults, living with the hope that Boutin might find his wife. Boutin had set out for Paris to inform Madame Chabert but never reached her; he was killed at Waterloo. The Colonel arrived in Paris without money, ill and naked; after a stay at the Hôtel-Dieu he sought his house in the Rue du Mont-Blanc, only to find that it had been sold and pulled down. An old lawyer who had held his affairs was dead; his successor laughed at him when he claimed to be Chabert. He watched his wife’s house, sometimes seeing her carriage flash by after a ball; she had married M. Ferraud, had two children, and enjoyed an income of thirty thousand francs, which he asserted belonged to him. He had written her letters—she had received some—yet she gave him nothing.

Derville listened, sometimes interrupting. The Colonel’s narrative was plain and full of detail; the lawyer was struck by his candor and grateful when Derville told him he would begin inquiries and make advances. The old man, so long repudiated, was overwhelmed when the young lawyer offered to try to obtain the Heilsberg papers and to give him five francs a day meanwhile. Derville spoke with practical reserve: the case would be exceptional and difficult, might go before three tribunals, and success was not certain even if the German documents could be obtained. He added a letter to his notary authorizing fifty francs every ten days to the Colonel’s order, to be recorded as a loan rather than alms, and urged him not to accept charity.

Chabert was deeply moved. After ten years repudiated by his wife and by society, he could hardly believe the small assistance was real. Gratitude choked him; he thanked Derville warmly, saying that after the Emperor the lawyer would be the man to whom he owed most. Derville accompanied him to the stairs with a light, and, once outside by a street lamp, the Colonel took the two gold pieces out of the letter and looked at them. “I may smoke cigars!” he said to himself.

Before leaving, Derville confided to his head clerk Boucard that the tale might cost him twenty-five louis; if robbed, he would not regret the money, for he had seen “the most consummate actor of the day.”

About three months after their interview, late one night in Derville’s room, Crottat, the young notary who had advanced half-pay to Derville’s eccentric client, urged him to reclaim the six hundred francs given the old soldier. Derville, however, noticed papers Boucard had spread out and a foreign letter stamped from Prussia, Austria, Bavaria, and France; he sent Boucard to have it translated. The Berlin notary replied that the requested documents would arrive in a few days, properly witnessed and stamped—most witnesses were at Eylau, and the woman who had saved Comte Chabert was near Heilsberg.

“This looks like business,” Derville said, and turned to Crottat for the Roguin file. Crottat summarized the Chabert estate: Rose Chapotel, the widow, had remarried without a settlement so the property became common; the personalty had originally been about six hundred thousand francs. Chabert’s will had left a quarter to hospitals and another quarter to the State; after contest and settlement, the Emperor restored to the widow what would have gone to the treasury. Effectively, about three hundred thousand francs remained for any claimant after previous distributions.

Derville went to Faubourg Saint-Marceau, Rue du Petit-Banquier, to the lodging named on the lawyer’s receipt: the house of Vergniaud, an old quartermaster turned dairyman. A rutted street brought him to a shabby yard and patched hovel with barnyard smells, rabbit hutches, a cowshed, poultry and a cart-horse; tin cans and drying rags testified to daily toil. Three little boys played; when asked about Chabert they at first shrank, then laughed at being mocked.

Colonel Chabert appeared, smoking a short clay pipe, cap greasy yet bearing a military calm. He ordered the boys “Silence in the ranks!” and led Derville past a manure heap to his room: a straw bed, scraps of carpet, an earthen floor, damp walls, a reed mat, a box-coat on a nail and two pairs of boots. On the table lay reprints of the Bulletins de la Grande Armée. Despite the squalor, Chabert’s face expressed hope more than despair.

“Does the smell of the pipe annoy you?” he asked, offering a stool. Derville, aghast at the discomfort and suspecting waste, heard Chabert explain simply that the family who lodged him had fed him gratis for a year; he could not leave them while he had any money left, and he was finishing teaching their children to read. If he recovered his fortune, he would do better; meanwhile “it does very well.”

When Derville told him the woman who had dug him from the battlefield was still alive and that papers were coming, the Colonel flung his pipe to the ground, near-ecstatic in his frustration. As they walked, Derville laid out the legal landscape: Chabert’s identity and claims would have to be judicially proved; papers could be disputed; preliminary inquiries, appeals, and foreign commissions might delay matters. The Comtesse’s bigamy complicated things; the Code did not clearly settle such a case, and judges might treat it as a question of equity and favor existing family ties. Even if the law seemed to favor Chabert, litigation could be long, bitter and costly.

Chabert thought the matter simple: presumed dead and alive again, he wanted his wife and fortune returned and reinstatement on the army list—perhaps a promotion, since he had been made Colonel of the Imperial Guard the day before Eylau. Derville warned him that the facts might support his claims but that the Court could be swayed by the Comtesse and public opinion; costs would be heavy. He explained the estate’s disposition: because a quarter had been given to hospitals and the remainder had been handled after Chabert’s supposed death, some money had already gone to hospitals and to the widow; the Emperor’s decree had returned to the widow what would have gone to the Exchequer, leaving roughly three hundred thousand francs for Chabert to claim, minus costs. Derville estimated preliminary inquiries at ten or twelve thousand francs—funds he could not front, being pressed by debts from buying his business. Chabert, moved to tears by the obstacles, put himself wholly in Derville’s hands.

Derville promised to send a power of attorney and to help financially if possible. When he left, Vergniaud, who had watched from outside, told him Chabert occupied “the best room” and had often helped them with bills, wounding their pride; he asked Derville to lend a hundred crowns to furnish the room. Seeing honest poverty, Derville agreed but resolved to give the money directly to Chabert so the dairyman would not reap the charity.

As he drove away Derville began to consider their adversary: the Countess Ferraud. He resolved to conceal his hand while discovering hers and to win by exploiting a woman’s chief fear. He surveyed the Ferraud position. Comte Ferraud was the only son of an émigré councillor who had lost his fortune; under the Consulate and by marrying Rose Chapotel—who had turned her portion of the old fortune to good account—he became wealthy. By the Restoration he had estates and preferment and now held a post he hoped would lead to a peerage. Ambition consumed him.

To manage the fortune and further his rise, Ferraud attached Delbecq, a ruined attorney hungry for office, as secretary. Delbecq, tempted by the Countess, became her instrument; practical, shrewd and unscrupulous, he increased her capital and was promised a provincial judgeship for his services. Delbecq, greedy for place, trebled her capital. This partnership secured the family’s social ascent.

By 1818, with the Restoration consolidated, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud had a place at Court and comfort; privately she endured a terror. Though she had known her first husband lived and ignored him, his sudden reappearance threatened to destroy the life she had built. She had resolved to make herself so rich and well-placed that a second marriage could not be dissolved. Chabert’s return therefore posed a peril she could not explain or confide to Delbecq or the police; many women conceal such anxieties and learn to live with them.

Derville went to the Hotel Ferraud in Rue de Varennes and contrasted its luxury with Chabert’s squalor: silver, mother-of-pearl, rare plants in china jars. The Countess received him at breakfast, playful with a monkey on a pole, elegant and careless. Derville told her bluntly that Colonel Chabert was alive. She laughed it off as nonsense, claiming no letter had arrived and boasting she still drew the widow’s pension. Derville’s fixed gaze and insistence unnerved her; he suggested she had received a first letter because it contained securities, and pressed that she was “caught” in a snare laid by an attorney. She flared with anger and then went pale but could not fully recover.

“I am as much your lawyer now as I am Colonel Chabert’s,” Derville said, warning that public opinion could turn against her: her fortune came from M. le Comte Chabert, whom she had cast off, and circumstances might make her appear heartless. She protested that even if Chabert were living the law would uphold her second marriage on account of the children and she would only have to restitute some two hundred and twenty-five thousand francs. Derville replied the Bench’s view was uncertain. More dangerous, he said, was a domestic shift: if Ferraud were told his marriage was void and his wife dishonored, ambition might lead him to accept annulment, receive a peerage and remarry for title.

Derville hinted that a decorated general, restored and insisting on his rights, could be an alluring rival who might capture the Count’s wife. He had struck the Countess’s hidden wound. Pressed, she asked what to do. Derville advised compromise and suggested she might manipulate Chabert’s affection to secure terms rather than risk ruinous litigation. She conceded, offering to retain Derville as her lawyer. He left, saying he would await her orders and would either report proceedings or meet her in his office to agree terms.

################################################
A week after Derville’s two visits, on a fine June morning the estranged husband and wife, separated almost as if by a supernatural chance, set out from opposite ends of Paris to meet in the lawyer’s office engaged by both. The money Derville had advanced enabled Colonel Chabert to appear suitably: he arrived in a decent cab, wearing a wig, blue cloth and white linen, and the broad red ribbon of the higher grade of the Legion of Honor under his waistcoat. Resuming the habits of wealth restored his soldierly bearing; his grave, mysterious face reflected hope and seemed revived. He no longer resembled the Chabert of the old box-coat; a passerby would have recognized one of the noble wrecks of the old army.

When the Countess stepped from a splendid brougham, she wore a simple dress set off to display her youth; a pink-lined bonnet framed and softened her face. The office was unusually lively: Simonnin breakfasted by the open window and joked with the clerks, who noticed the two visitors—Chabert in Derville’s private room, the Countess in the main office. Derville had seated her apart, uncertain whether she wished to meet the man who claimed to be her first husband.

Derville read aloud a memorandum he had drawn up. Its preamble named Hyacinthe Chabert, Count, living in Paris, and Madame Rose Chapotel, wife of the said Comte Chabert. It proposed that she and Chabert acknowledge, before witnesses and notaries, that the person designated was her first husband; that Chabert would assert his rights only under certain circumstances—specifically, if she failed to carry out the conditions of a secret agreement; and that Chabert would accept a friendly suit to annul his certificate of death and dissolve the marriage. By a subsequent clause she pledged to secure Chabert an income of twenty-four thousand francs on government stock, to revert to her at his death.

“Too dear!” the Countess exclaimed. Derville suggested compromise; she hesitated.

“I want—I will not have a lawsuit. I want—” she began.

“You want him to remain dead?” asked Derville.

“Monsieur,” the Countess replied, “if twenty-four thousand francs are necessary, we will go to law—”

“Yes, we will go to law,” the Colonel said in a deep voice, opening the door and standing before his wife, one hand in his waistcoat, the other hanging by his side—an attitude made ominous by his adventure.

“It is he,” the Countess thought.

“Too dear!” the old soldier exclaimed. “I have given you near on a million, and you are cheapening my misfortunes. Very well; now I will have you—you and your fortune. Our goods are in common, our marriage is not dissolved—”

“But monsieur is not Colonel Chabert!” cried the Countess, feigning astonishment.

“Indeed!” the old man said with intense irony. “Do you want proofs? I found you in the Palais-Royal—”

The Countess turned pale; the remark pierced her. Chabert, touched at hurting the woman he had once loved, paused; her venomous glance made him continue abruptly: “You were with La—”

“Allow me, Monsieur Derville,” the Countess said. “You must give me leave to retire. I did not come here to listen to such dreadful things.” She left quickly; Derville rushed after her but she had gone.

Back in his private room the Colonel paced in towering rage. He reproached himself for past foolishness and trusted appearances. Derville warned him that any attempt at force would be madness—he might be arrested or worse, and the Countess might set a trap to have him confined in Charenton. He advised prudence and promised to notify her of their proceedings to protect him. The Colonel, stammering apologies, left.

Descending the dark staircase, crushed by the most cruel blow—his wife's recognition of him without acknowledgment of identity—he heard a rustle on the lowest landing and she stood before him. With a familiar gesture she took his arm. Her action and recovered gracious tone allayed his wrath, and he allowed her to lead him to the carriage. As if by magic he found himself sitting beside her.

They drove across Paris. “Monsieur,” the Countess said in a voice trembling with a rare emotion—this single word expressed reproach and pardon, hope and despair, a question and an answer—“I knew you at once.”

“Rosine,” said the old soldier, “those words contain the only balm that can help me to forget my misfortunes.” He pressed her hands with tears.

She explained why she had behaved as she did: his letters had reached her months late, opened and soiled; after obtaining Napoleon’s signature to her second marriage contract she had believed some swindler sought to make a fool of her. To avoid disturbing M. Ferraud and his family, she had taken precautions against a pretended Chabert. “Was I not right?” she asked.

“Yes, you were right. It was I who was the idiot,” he admitted. She declared she would preserve dignity and avoid scandal in Paris; though she felt bound by right to him, she was no longer his in fact, and she loved M. Ferraud. She did not blush to confess it, and she claimed a right to happiness. The Colonel accepted this with dignity; the man of honor that he was could not demand mockery of love from a wife who no longer loved him.

For a long stretch they rode without speaking. Chabert pictured their two children—he became softly paternal. The Countess sought to revive his love without awakening desire, and guided him toward the role of father and friend. She arranged for his stay at a delightful house she had prepared for him in a park near Groslay in the valley of Montmorency. Misfortune had made the Colonel more helpful and good; she displayed tender attentions, sweet melancholy, and a manner calculated to win his forgiveness and occupy his mind with memories rather than claims.

She did not yet know exactly what to do with him but meant to annihilate him socially. On the third evening, uneasy at the results of her maneuvers, she finished a letter to Delbecq, instructing him to demand the deeds from Derville in her name, copy them, and come to Groslay. She had scarcely finished when she heard the Colonel’s step; uneasy at her absence, he came to look for her. She spoke privately to her maid, sending her to Paris to deliver the letter to Delbecq and bring back a reply. Then she sat on a bench where the Colonel joined her.

“Rosine,” he asked, “what is the matter with you?”

She confessed that she feared for her position if Ferraud discovered she had been alone with a stranger. “If he should ask what I came here for, if he finds out I came here alone with a stranger, what am I to say? Decide my fate; I am resigned.” She then faltered and spoke of her children: “If I am separated from the Count, only leave me my children, and I will submit to anything...”

At that the Colonel relented utterly. He had already conceived of living humbly, in the lodging of a relation, content with little. One evening, enchanted by the domestic picture, he resolved to remain dead; he would renounce the authentication of his identity, renounce the claim that would make scandal, and accept a modest life for the children’ sake. “Do exactly as you like,” she said. “I declare I will have nothing to do with this affair.” He took this as victory of clemency and pity.

Delbecq had arrived days before and, following the Countess’s orders, had gained the Colonel's confidence. The next morning Chabert went with the intendant to Saint-Leu-Taverny, where Delbecq had a notary draw up an affidavit. After hearing it read, the Colonel rose and walked out of the office in fury.

“Turf and thunder! What a fool you must think me! Why, I should make myself out a swindler!” he cried.

“Indeed, monsieur,” said Delbecq, “I should advise you to get at least thirty thousand francs a year. Madame would pay.”

The insulted Colonel slapped the scoundrel twice and rushed off. Stung and confused, he returned to the park at Groslay by a gap in the fence and sat to meditate in a little room under a gazebo that commanded the road to Saint-Leu. There he reflected on what her words and Delbecq’s reply had revealed: the care shown him had been bait for a snare. The benevolence meant to attract him had been a prelude to entrapment. The revelation brought back all his suffering; he felt disgust for life and indecision.

At last he crept into the kiosque and found his wife seated, calm but shuddering as she saw him. He stood with folded arms, face pale, brow stern. After a gaze that compelled her to blush, he spoke with controlled scorn: he would not curse her—he scorned her. He thanked chance that had divided them; he desired no revenge and no restitution. He would no longer assert any claim to the name he might have made illustrious. He renounced his former life, telling her he would henceforth be but a poor devil named Hyacinthe, content with his share of sunshine, and bade her farewell. When she threw herself at his feet and tried to detain him, he pushed her away: “Do not touch me!”

Her expression as he left showed infernal perspicacity: she knew she might live in peace on his word and contempt. Chabert disappeared. The dairyman failed and he sank into ruin; he perhaps became a hackney-cab driver, or fell lower, lost in the mire of Paris.

Six months later, Derville, having heard nothing of Chabert or the Comtesse Ferraud, supposed they had compromised through some other lawyer. He summed the advances he had made to Chabert with costs and asked the Countess to claim the amount from M. le Comte Chabert, assuming she knew where to find her first husband.

The next day Comte Ferraud’s man of business, newly appointed President of the County Court, sent Derville a curt note: “Monsieur,—Madame la Comtesse Ferraud desires me to inform you that your client took complete advantage of your confidence, and that the individual calling himself Comte Chabert has acknowledged that he came forward under false pretences. Yours, etc., DELBECQ.”

Derville, outraged at being cheated after acting with humanity and generosity, swore at the stupidity of people who repaid legal kindness with duplicity. He worried the business would cost him dearly.

Some time after receiving the letter, Derville went to the Palais de Justice in search of a pleader employed in the Police Court. By chance he entered Court Number 6 just as the Presiding Magistrate was sentencing one Hyacinthe to two months’ imprisonment as a vagabond, followed by confinement in the Mendicity House of Detention. On hearing the name, Derville recognized the delinquent between two gendarmes as his false Colonel Chabert.

In rags and misery, the old soldier sat placid, motionless, almost absentminded. The face still showed noble pride and a stoical eye; even when a man falls into the hands of justice and becomes a mere matter of law, Chabert’s dignity remained. Derville used his privilege to follow him to the lock-up, a dark, malodorous ante-room where the wretches of the city gathered. There the sight of men reduced to wretchedness justified the desperation that pushes many toward suicide.

Chabert sat among these men, silent or speaking low, while three gendarmes paced. “Do you recognize me?” Derville asked.

“Yes, sir,” said Chabert, rising.

“If you are an honest man,” Derville whispered, “how could you remain in my debt? Has Madame Ferraud not paid you?” he continued.

“Paid me?” repeated Derville, baffled. “She wrote to me that you were a swindler,” he told Chabert.

Chabert cast up his eyes in sublime horror and imprecation, as if calling heaven to witness the new subterfuge. “Monsieur,” he said in a calm, husky voice, “get the gendarmes to allow me into the lock-up, and I will sign an order which will certainly be honored.” On Derville’s assurance, the sergeant allowed him to write. Hyacinthe wrote a few lines addressed to the Comtesse Ferraud.

“Send her that,” said the soldier, “and you will be paid your costs and the money you advanced. Believe me, monsieur, if I have not shown you the gratitude I owe you for your kind offices, it is no less sincere here,” and he laid his hand upon his heart. “But what can the unfortunate do? They live, and that is all.”

Derville went away. Returning to his office, he sent Godeschal to the Comtesse Ferraud, who, on reading the note, at once paid the sum due to Comte Chabert’s lawyer.

################################################
In late June 1840 Godeschal, now an attorney, visited Bicêtre with Derville, whose post he had taken. On the avenue they noticed an old, broken pauper beneath an elm — one of the infirmary’s many aged men, sitting on a corner-stone, methodically drying his snuffy handkerchief in the sun to save washing. He had an engaging face and wore the reddish wrapper-coat of the workhouse.

“I say, Derville,” Godeschal observed, “look at that old fellow. Isn’t he like one of those grotesque carved figures from Germany? And he is alive, perhaps happy.”

Derville, peering through his glass, called him “a whole poem, or, as romantics say, a drama,” and asked if Godeschal knew the Comtesse Ferraud. When Godeschal confirmed he did—finding her clever and agreeable but overly pious—Derville revealed that the pauper was her lawful husband, Comte Chabert, the old colonel. He suggested the Comtesse had had him put in Bicêtre because he had once reminded her, bluntly, that he had “taken her, like a hackney cab, on the street,” provoking her notorious anger.

Curious, they returned two days later to visit the supposed Chabert. Halfway up the avenue they found the old man sitting on a felled tree, drawing lines in the sand; he had been somewhere else for breakfast. Derville greeted him, and the veteran, with the simplicity of a child, denied his title. “Not Chabert! My name is Hyacinthe,” he cried. “I am no longer a man, I am No. 164, Room 7.” He asked timidly, like an old child, whether they intended to visit “the man condemned to death,” and declared, “He is not married! He is very lucky!”

Godeschal offered him snuff; Hyacinthe eagerly held out his hand. Each stranger gave him a twenty-franc piece. He thanked them with puzzled delight, calling them “brave troopers!” He paraded, saluted, pretended to take aim, shouted, “Fire! both arms! Vive Napoleon!” and flourished his stick. Derville remarked that the nature of his wound had probably made him childish. Another old pauper watching contradicted that view: Hyacinthe could be prickly and, on some days, a rogue “full of philosophy and imagination.” That morning, he had merely had his Monday treat.

The bystander then recounted a story about Hyacinthe from as far back as 1820. A Prussian officer, his chaise laboring up the hill of Villejuif, passed on foot and, to mock, remarked they had found “an old cavalry man who must have been at Rossbach.” Hyacinthe corrected him: “I was too young to be there. But I was at Jena.” The Prussian hurried on without further inquiry. The anecdote framed the old man’s odd destiny: taken from the Foundling Hospital, a veteran who had shared in Napoleon’s campaigns, now reduced to an inmate of the Infirmary for the Aged.

This provoked Derville’s philosophical turn. He reflected that in modern society three men can never think well of the world—the priest, the doctor, and the man of law—each cloaked in black, as if in mourning for every virtue and illusion. He argued that the priest is summoned by repentance and faith that elevate both supplicant and intercessor; the doctor tends to bodily illness; but the lawyer’s work is uniquely dispiriting. Lawyers confront repeated, irredeemable vice: they see fathers die in garrets after being deserted by daughters they kept, wills destroyed, mothers robbing children, wives plotting husbands’ ruin to live with lovers, women training legitimated children to fall so the illicit ones prosper. Derville had witnessed crimes that fiction could not surpass and felt justice often impotent.

“I have learned much in my profession,” he concluded, weary. “I have seen… horrors. As for me, I am going to live in the country with my wife. I have a horror of Paris.”

Godeschal replied that he had already seen many such things in Desroches’ office.
