The French often show a reluctance to travel, unlike the English, who embrace it. This aversion stems from the belief that while other countries may be beautiful, France’s unique charm—its wit, conversation, and cultural nuances—is irreplaceable, making emigration unappealing.

In 1836 two Parisians visited a palazzo in Genoa and felt Paris’s spirit among the dinner guests hosted by the French Consul-General. Present were Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin), the painter Léon de Lora, and the critic Claude Vignon. Camille had gone to Florence on business and brought Léon to see Italy; their Genoa stop honored the Consul-General. The Consul, whose look recalled Lord Byron, was married to the wealthy heiress Onorina Pedrotti. Their union was affectionate but complicated by the Consul’s melancholy, which suggested deeper unhappiness.

As the evening turned to literature and the morality of women, the Consul began a personal story about his youth. He described how his uncle, Abbé Loraux, guided him from innocence to adulthood and introduced him into the service of Comte Octave, a prominent statesman whose public success belied private sorrow. The Consul’s recollection sketched Octave as a man of exemplary public virtue yet haunted by intimate suffering, illustrating the tension between public duty and private longing.

One Monday, M. le Président de Grandville and M. de Serizy met at Comte Octave’s house to discuss political matters; I was there as secretary. The Count had secured my position as Auditor to the Council of State. We were tasked with examining important papers and the meeting ran late; a servant then reported that two priests had been waiting two hours. Despite hesitation, we invited them to dine, and the evening became a series of lively stories from the politicians present. My uncle, Abbé Loraux, offered sharp wit; Abbé Gaudron, a simple man of faith, contributed innocent curiosity. The conversation turned to adultery, highlighting the gap between civil and religious law. My uncle criticized the Code for treating adultery lightly, and the discussion broadened to marriage and women’s roles.

Comte Octave argued that because the Church held women in high regard, strict penalties for adultery were theoretically necessary; yet social practices had evolved, creating a mismatch between law and morality. He lamented dual standards and hypocrisy, which often punished appearances more than genuine misconduct. As the talk continued, the men revealed personal experiences with marriage, and Octave’s reaction to a remark about divorce suggested deep emotional pain. After the priests left, Octave confided in me that his wife had left him three years earlier, and he described her letter announcing her flight—he found its tone dignified despite the betrayal.

Octave had lived alone since then, and I perceived a complex, private drama behind his public composure. His suffering was evident, and I felt deep admiration for him. Days passed as I observed his melancholy, and one evening he finally asked me to stay, signaling readiness to disclose more about his troubles.

This is the story of Comte Octave and his wife Honorine. Returning home from college, Octave found Honorine—his father’s ward—at sixteen: beautiful, lively, and innocent. Their relationship remained pure until Octave proposed when she turned seventeen. His mother insisted on a delay; they married when he was twenty-six and she was nineteen. At first their marriage followed parental traditions, but as Octave introduced Honorine to society he assumed the role of teacher more than lover. His earnest, authoritative guidance gradually became oppressive; where a husband’s tenderness might have nurtured youth, his instructive tone stifled it. Eighteen months after Octave’s father died, Honorine left, driven by an unknown passion. She left a brief farewell letter that shattered Octave.

Despite his pain, Octave continued to love Honorine deeply; he held no anger and treated her like a child who had erred. He gradually recognized that his very devotion may have smothered her, that his excessive concern and desire to instruct had failed to meet her need for freedom and warmth. His love turned into a desperate longing and a determination to find and protect her, even though her life had deteriorated: abandoned by her lover, she fell into poverty.

Octave contrived a plan to care for Honorine from a distance, ensuring she had enough while concealing his involvement. He arranged modest lodgings and resources, persuading himself—and others—to believe she earned her living making artificial flowers. For years he watched over her invisibly, maintaining a façade of indifference in public while secretly sustaining her. His life became a struggle between his public duties as a statesman and the private obsession that governed his nights.

At one point Octave attempted direct contact: he wrote to her, but she rejected the letter without reading it. This refusal deepened his despair and reinforced the delicacy of any approach. Then came the device that sustained his vigil: he enlisted a young man, Maurice, as a liaison. Maurice moved into the gardener’s cottage next to Honorine’s house, posing as a simple florist. This arrangement enabled Octave to gather information about her life while keeping a respectful distance.

Maurice, initially cast as Octave’s instrument, soon found himself moved by Honorine’s grace and gentleness. What began as surveillance softened into real affection. Honorine, unaware of Maurice’s connection to her past and to Octave, welcomed him as a neighbor and companion. Their conversations revealed her struggles and resolve to remain independent despite hardship; she expressed a desire for freedom that resonated with Maurice. As affection grew, he was torn between loyalty to Octave and his own feelings.

The story unfolds against a Parisian social backdrop where love, betrayal, and the search for happiness intersect. Octave’s relentless, almost paternal pursuit of Honorine’s well-being, combined with Maurice’s growing tenderness, creates a complex emotional triangle. The narrative interrogates notions of duty, passion, and the cost of protecting someone by hiding one’s love.

Ultimately Honorine confronts her own feelings and social expectations. She may acknowledge affection for Octave, yet insist it cannot become what he desires or conform to his vision. The tension between duty and desire keeps her from returning to his arms, despite his willingness to accept her. The tale explores love, sacrifice, and the struggle for personal autonomy within social norms, prompting readers to question the nature of virtue and the price of happiness.
