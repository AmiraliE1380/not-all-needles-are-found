  To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

  Dear Child,--You, the joy of the household, you, whose pink or
  white pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of
  Wierzschovnia like a will-o’-the-wisp, followed by the tender eyes
  of your father and your mother,--how can I dedicate to _you_ a
  story full of melancholy? And yet, ought not sorrows to be spoken
  of to a young girl idolized as you are, since the day may come

  difficult, Anna, to find in the history of our manners and morals

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

At dawn of an October day in 1827 a sixteen-year-old youth, dressed in what modern phrase calls proletary, stood in a small square of Lower Provins. At that hour he could note the houses around the oblong space unnoticed. The mills along the river already whirred; their wheels, echoed from the Upper Town, intensified the silence so that the wheels of a diligence could be heard a league off. The two long sides of the square, separated by an avenue of lindens, were built in the simple style of bourgeois life. No signs of commerce were visible; the porte-cochères of the rich were few and seldom used, except that of Monsieur Martener, a physician with a cabriolet. Some fronts were covered by vines or roses climbing to second-story windows, scenting the air. One end of the square opens on the main street, the gardens reaching the river bank; the other opens onto a street parallel to the main road.

At that quieter end the youth recognized the house he sought: a front of white stone grooved as courses, closed gray blinds, slender iron balconies with yellow-painted rosettes. Above the ground and first floors rose three dormer windows in a slate roof; on the peak of the central one was a new weather-vane of a hunter shooting a hare. Three stone steps led to the door. A leaden pipe discharged sink-water into a gutter on one side, indicating the kitchen; on the other were two windows closely shuttered with heart-shaped openings to admit light, those of the dining-room. Vent-holes to the cellar were closed by painted iron shutters cut in open-work. Everything was new. This restored house, fresh against the older facades, suggested the paltry taste and self-satisfaction of a retired petty shopkeeper.

The young man inspected these details with a pleasure tinged with sadness; his eyes roved from the kitchen to the roof. The rosy glow of the rising sun fell on a calico curtain at one garret window; the others lacked that luxury, and his face brightened. He stepped back, leaned against a linden, and sang in the drawling tone of western France the Breton ditty, a song often sung to newly married couples:

  “We’ve come to wish you happiness in marriage,
    To m’sieur your husband
    As well as to you:

  “You have just been bound, madam’ la mariee,
    With bonds of gold
    That only death unbinds:

  “You will go no more to balls or gay assemblies;
    You must stay at home
    While we shall go.

  “Have you thought well how you are pledged to be
    True to your spouse,
    And love him like yourself?

  “Receive these flowers our hands do now present you;
    Alas! your fleeting honors
    Will fade as they.”

This native air, tender and melancholy, would awaken a Breton maiden’s memories, picturing the manners and feelings of her noble old land. As he finished the first couplet, the singer, never taking his eyes from the attic curtain, saw no sign of life. While he sang the second, the curtain stirred. At “Receive these flowers,” a face appeared; a white hand cautiously opened the casement, and a girl made a sign with her head as he closed with the melancholy thought, “Alas! your fleeting honors will fade as they.”

To her the young workman suddenly produced a yellow flower, common in Brittany and sometimes found in La Brie—the furze, or broom.

“Is it really you, Brigaut?” she said in a low voice.

“Yes, Pierrette, yes. I am in Paris. I have started to make my way; but I’m ready to settle here, near you.”

A window on the first floor beneath Pierrette’s attic creaked. The girl showed terror and whispered: “Run away!”

The lad jumped like a frightened frog to a bend in the street where a mill projected as the square opens into the main thoroughfare; but despite his agility his hob-nailed shoes echoed on the stones, easily distinguished from the mill’s music, and no doubt heard by the person who opened the window.

That person was a woman — an old maid. No man would have torn himself from a morning nap to listen to a minstrel in a jacket; only a maid wakes to songs of love. She opened her blinds with the furtive motion of a bat, looked about, saw nothing, and only heard the flying footfalls. Few sights are more dreadful than the matutinal apparition of an ugly old maid at her window. This keen-eared spinster lacked her usual embellishments; her false front and collarette were absent; she wore a little bag of black silk to cover her skull, revealed beneath a pushed-aside night-cap. This rumpled condition gave a menacing look to her head, such as painters bestow on witches. Wrinkles marked temples, ears, and nape in scarlet lines contrasting with the whiteness of her bed-gown. The gaping of that garment revealed a breast like that of an old peasant who cares little for appearance. The fleshless arm hung like a stick. At her window she seemed tall from the length and angularity of her face, recalling the exaggerated proportions of certain Swiss heads. Her countenance, lacking harmony and softened line, expressed an unfeeling spirit. Usually such features were softened by a commercial smile—a bourgeois smirk that mimicked good humor; in public she might pass for kindly. She owned the house with her brother. He slept so soundly that the orchestra of the Opera-house could not have awakened him.

The old maid stretched her neck from the window, raised cold pale-blue eyes with short lashes toward the attic, tried to see Pierrette, and, finding it useless, retreated like a tortoise drawing in her head. The blinds were then closed, and the silence of the street resumed, broken only by peasants coming in or very early persons moving about.

When there is an old maid in a house, watch-dogs are unnecessary; not the slightest event escapes her observation or comment. This trifling incident was therefore destined to excite grave suppositions and to open the way for one of those obscure family dramas—none the less terrible because secret.

Pierrette did not go back to bed. To her Brigaut’s arrival was an immense event. During the night—Eden of the wretched—she escaped the vexations and fault-findings she bore by day. Like the hero of a ballad, her sleep seemed her happy life; her waking hours a bad dream. She had her only pleasurable waking in three years. The memories of her childhood sang their melodious ditties in her soul. The first couplet was heard in a dream; the second made her spring from bed; at the third she doubted her ears; but when the fourth was sung, standing in her night-gown with bare feet at the window, she recognized Brigaut, the companion of her childhood. Ah, yes! it was truly the well-known square jacket with bobtails, pockets sticking out at the hips—the blue cloth jacket of Brittany; there, too, the printed cotton waistcoat, the linen shirt fastened by a gold heart, the large rolling collar, the earrings, stout shoes, and trousers of blue-gray drilling unevenly colored—those humble durable things of Breton peasantry. The big white-horn buttons that fastened the jacket made the girl’s heart beat. When she saw the bunch of broom her eyes filled with tears; then a dreadful fear drove back the budding happiness. She thought her cousin sleeping in the room below might have heard the noise she made in jumping to the window. The fear was just; the old maid had appeared, and Pierrette made Brigaut the terrified sign which he obeyed without understanding it. Such instinctive submission to a girl’s bidding reveals one of those innocent and absolute affections that blossom now and then in life. Whoever had seen the lad as he ran away would have loved the ingenuous chivalry of his feeling.

Jacques Brigaut was worthy of Pierrette Lorrain, who was just fifteen. Two children! Pierrette could not help crying as she watched his flight in the terror her gesture had conveyed. Then she sat in a shabby armchair before a small table topped by a mirror. Resting her elbows, head in hands, she thought for an hour, calling to memory the Marais, the village of Pen-Hoel, perilous pond voyages in a boat untied from an old willow by little Jacques; the faces of her grandfather and grandmother, her mother’s sufferings, Major Brigaut’s handsome face—in short her careless childhood. It was a dream, a luminous joy on the gloomy present.

Her beautiful chestnut hair escaped in disorder from her cap, rumpled in sleep—a cambric cap she had made. Little ringlets escaped from gray curl-papers at each side of her forehead. From the back hung a heavy braid half unplaited. Her whiteness betrayed the malady of girlhood, chlorosis, which deprives the body of color, destroys appetite, and disorders the organism. Waxy tones marked her flesh. The neck and shoulders betrayed wasted arms, flung forward and crossed upon the table. Her feet seemed enervated, the night-gown barely reached her knees, showing flaccid muscles, blue veins, and impoverished legs. The cold, which she heeded not, turned her lips violet; a sad smile drew up the corners of a sensitive mouth that showed small, ivory-white teeth. Her ears were dainty, and despite the roundness of her face she was lovely. All animation was in the eyes: the iris brown like Spanish tobacco with black flecks and golden reflections round brilliant pupils. Pierrette was made to be gay but was sad. Her lost gaiety still showed in the vivacious eye, the ingenuous grace of her brow, the smooth curve of her chin. The long eyelashes lay upon cheek-bones made prominent by suffering. Paleness rendered the lines and details purely distinct. The ear alone was a little masterpiece of modeling. Perhaps you would like to know her history, and this is it.

Pierrette’s mother was a Demoiselle Auffray of Provins, half-sister by the father to Madame Rogron, mother of the present owners of the house.

Monsieur Auffray had married at eighteen and again near sixty-nine. By his first marriage he had an only, plain daughter, married at sixteen to innkeeper Rogron; by his second he had a charming daughter, so that Madame Rogron was fifty when the half-sister was born and already had two grown children.

The youngest daughter married at eighteen a man of her choice, a Breton officer named Lorrain, captain in the Imperial Guard. The captain, anxious to rise to a colonelcy, exchanged into a line regiment. While he, then a major, and his wife enjoyed themselves in Paris on the allowance of Monsieur and Madame Auffray, or scoured Germany, old Auffray (formerly a grocer) died at eighty-eight without making a will. His property was administered by his daughter, Madame Rogron, and her husband so completely in their interests that nothing remained for the widow beyond the house she lived in on the little square and a few acres. This widow, the mother of Madame Lorrain, was thirty-eight at her husband’s death. Like many widows, she unwisely remarried. She sold the house and land to her step-daughter, Madame Rogron, and married a young physician named Neraud, who wasted her fortune. She died two years later of grief and misery.

Thus the share of her father’s property that should have come to Madame Lorrain almost entirely disappeared, reduced to a small sum of eight thousand francs. Major Lorrain was killed at the battle of Montereau, leaving his wife, then twenty-one, with a little daughter of fourteen months and only a pension and an eventual inheritance from his parents, Monsieur and Madame Lorrain, retail shopkeepers at Pen-Hoel in the Marais. These Lorrains, Pierrette’s grandparents, sold wood, slates, tiles, pipes. Their business, from incapacity or ill-luck, did poorly and gave them scarcely enough. The failure of Collinet at Nantes, caused by 1814 events and a fall in colonial products, deprived them of twenty-four thousand francs they had deposited with that house.

The arrival of their daughter-in-law was welcome. Her pension of eight hundred francs was a handsome income at Pen-Hoel. The eight thousand francs which the widow’s half-brother and sister Rogron sent her from her father’s estate were placed in the Lorrains’ business; they gave her a mortgage on a little house they owned in Nantes, let for three hundred francs and barely worth ten thousand.

Madame Lorrain the younger, Pierrette’s mother, died in 1819. The child was small, delicate, and weakly; the damp climate of the Marais did not agree with her. But the husband’s family persuaded her, to keep her with them, that in no other place could she find a healthier region. She was so petted that her death brought nothing but honor to the old Lorrains.

Some declared that Brigaut, an old Vendeen who had served under Charette and other leaders in the wars against the Republic, counted for much in the younger Madame Lorrain’s willingness to remain in the Marais. All Pen-Hoel saw him—called respectfully Major Brigaut—spending his days and evenings in the Lorrains’ parlor beside the window of the imperial major. Toward the last, the curate of Pen-Hoel urged old Madame Lorrain to have her daughter-in-law marry Brigaut, promising to have the major appointed justice of the peace through the Vicomte de Kergarouet’s influence. The young woman’s death ended the matter.

Pierrette was left with her grandparents, who owed her four hundred francs a year, interest on the little property placed in their hands. This small sum supported her. Growing less fit, they soon faced an active competitor who ruined them. With his connivance, the Lorrains borrowed on notes they could not meet, driving them into bankruptcy. Pierrette’s claim on the house in Nantes was superseded by her grandmother’s legal rights, enforced to secure daily bread. The house sold for nine thousand five hundred francs, of which one thousand five hundred went for costs. The remaining eight thousand came to Madame Lorrain, who lived on their income in a humble place at Nantes called Saint-Jacques, where the two old people had bed and board for a small payment.

As it was impossible to keep Pierrette with them, the old Lorrains wrote to her uncle and aunt Rogron in Provins. These Rogrons were dead. The letter might easily have been lost, but the post persistently traced it to Monsieur Rogron, son of the deceased, a mercer in the rue Saint-Denis in Paris; curiosity about a possible inheritance ensured its transmission, and the Treasury its fee.

These Rogrons, toward whom the old Lorrains, though dreading to part with their dear granddaughter, stretched their supplicating hands, became unexpectedly the masters of Pierrette’s destiny. It is therefore indispensable to explain both their antecedents and their character.

Pere Rogron, the innkeeper of Provins who had married old Auffray’s daughter by his first wife, had an inflamed face, a veiny nose, and cheeks marked by Bacchic redness. Short, fat, pot-bellied, with stout legs and thick hands, he had the shrewdness of a Swiss innkeeper. He was not handsome, and his wife resembled him—never was a couple better matched. Rogron liked good living and to be waited on by pretty girls. He belonged to the class of egoists whose behavior is brutal; he gave way to his vices openly. Grasping, selfish, indecent, and always gratifying his whims, he spent his earnings until his teeth failed. Selfishness remained. In his old days he sold his inn, collected as much as he could of his late father-in-law’s property, and went to live in the little house in the square of Provins, bought for a trifle from the widow of old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother.

Rogron and his wife had about two thousand francs a year from twenty-seven local lots, and twenty thousand from selling their inn. Old Auffray’s house, though dilapidated, was inhabited by the Rogrons—old rats in wrack and ruin. Rogron took to horticulture and spent savings enlarging the garden; he carried it to the river’s edge between two walls and built a stone embankment at the end, where nature showed its aquatic flora.

Early in their marriage the Rogrons had a son and a daughter, both hideous; for such human beings degenerate. Put out to nurse cheaply, these children returned after a coarse village upbringing—left to cry for hours while wet-nurses worked in the fields, shut up in damp, dark rooms. Treated thus, their features coarsened; voices grew harsh; they mortified their mother’s vanity, which she tried to correct sternly; their father’s severity made that sternness seem kindness by comparison. Generally left to run loose about stables, courtyards, or the streets, sometimes whipped, sometimes sent to their grandfather Auffray, who disliked them, the children suffered. The Rogrons justified taking the bulk of “the old scoundrel’s” property by accusing him of injustice to the children. Nevertheless Rogron sent his son to school and bought him a man, one of his cartmen, to save him from conscription. As soon as his daughter Sylvie was thirteen he sent her to Paris as an apprentice in a shop, with six hundred francs for her board; two years later he sent his son Jérôme-Denis the same way. Asked by his friends what he meant to do with his children, Pere Rogron was blunt.

“When they are old enough to understand me I shall give ’em a kick and say: ‘Go and make your own way in the world!’” he replied, emptying his glass and wiping his lips. Then, with a wink: “My father gave me three kicks; I shall only give them one; he put one louis into my hand, I shall put ten in theirs, therefore they’ll be better off. After I’m gone, what’s left will be theirs. The notaries can find them and give it to them. What nonsense to bother about children. Mine owe me their life. I’ve fed them, and I don’t ask anything from them,—I call that quits. I began as a cartman, but that didn’t prevent me marrying the daughter of that old scoundrel Auffray.”

Sylvie Rogron, apprenticed in the rue Saint-Denis to shopkeepers from Provins, was “at par” in two years; she earned her living and cost her parents nothing. By nineteen she had a salary of four hundred francs. At twenty she was second demoiselle in the Maison Julliard, wholesale silk dealers at the “Chinese Worm” rue Saint-Denis. Jérôme-Denis entered the Maison Guepin, a large wholesale mercer at the “Three Distaffs.” When Sylvie was twenty-one and forewoman at a thousand francs a year, Jérôme-Denis at eighteen was head-clerk with a salary of twelve hundred francs.

Brother and sister met Sundays and feast-days for cheap amusements; they dined out and visited Saint-Cloud, Meudon, Belleville, or Vincennes. Around the end of 1815 they pooled about twenty thousand francs in savings and bought, from Madame Guenée, the property and good-will of the “Family Sister,” a large retail establishment in the quarter. Sylvie kept the books and did the writing; Jérôme-Denis was master and head-clerk. By 1821, after five years, competition was fierce and it cost them all they had to maintain the business and its reputation.

Though Sylvie was scarcely forty, her natural ugliness, hard work, and a crabbed look made her seem fifty. At thirty-eight Jérôme presented the silliest face to customers: a retreating forehead with three folds, close-cut grizzled hair, and a dull, lifeless expression. His bluish eyes had neither flame nor thought. Short and fat like his father, his figure showed a ridiculous debility rather than brute obesity. His father’s high color had become livid flabbiness peculiar to those confined to back-shops or counting-rooms, forever tying bundles, making change, snarling at clerks, and repeating old phrases to customers.

The small amount of brains the siblings possessed had been wholly absorbed in maintaining their business, getting and keeping money, and learning the market’s laws and usages. Thread, needles, ribbons, pins, buttons, and tailors’ furnishings had taken all their capacity. Outside business they knew nothing; they were ignorant of Paris beyond the Rue Saint-Denis. Their narrow natures saw no field except the shop. They were clever at nagging clerks and young women and proving them to blame. Their happiness lay in busy counters—the glib gabble of clerks using consecrated phrases made their day fine; on really fine days when Parisians strolled caring for no goods, the day was overcast to the Rogrons. “Bad weather for sales,” they said.

Rogron’s skill at tying parcels made him admired by apprentices: he could fold, tie, and see all that happened in the street and the farthest recesses of the shop before handing a parcel back with “Here it is, madame; nothing else today?” But he would have been ruined without his sister. Sylvie had common sense and a genius for trade. She advised purchases and would send him to remote parts of France to save a trifle. Her shrewdness, not employed in the heart’s service, drifted into speculation. The necessity of a business to pay for—this thought kept the machine going and gave it infernal activity.

Rogron was really only head-clerk; he understood nothing of the business as a whole. Self-interest—the great motor of the mind—had failed to instruct him. He was often aghast when his sister ordered some article to be sold below cost to clear out the end of its fashion; later he admired her idiotically. He reasoned neither ill nor well; he was simply incapable of reasoning; but he subordinated himself to his sister because “she is my elder.” Perhaps an existence always solitary, reduced to mere needs, deprived of money and pleasures in youth, explains to physiologists the clownish face, feebleness of mind, and vacant silliness. His sister had steadily prevented him from marrying—afraid to lose her power and seeing only expense and injury in a younger, likely prettier woman.

Silliness behaves by talking or being silent. Silent silliness can be borne; Rogron’s was loquacious. He chattered to his clerks, explaining minutiae and ornamenting his talk with flat jokes—the “chaff” of shopkeeping—and had come to possess a phraseology of his own. This chatterer believed himself an orator. The necessity of explaining to customers, guessing their desires, and even giving them desires, exercises the tongues of retail shopkeepers. The petty dealer acquires the faculty of uttering words and sentences with no meaning but marked success. He explains matters of manufacture customers know nothing of; that alone gives a passing superiority; take him away from his thousand explanations and he is, relatively to thought, like a fish out of water.

Rogron and Sylvie, two mechanisms baptized by mistake, lacked the feelings that give life to the heart. Their natures were shriveled and harsh, hardened by toil, privation, and the memory of suffering during a long apprenticeship to life. Neither complained. They were not implacable so much as impracticable toward others in misfortune. To them, virtue, honor, and loyalty consisted solely in the payment of bills. Irritable and irritating, sordid in economy, they bore a dreadful reputation among the merchants of the rue Saint-Denis. Had it not been for their connection with Provins, where they went three or four times a year and could close the shop for a day or two, they would have had no clerks or young women. But old Rogron sent them all the unfortunate young people of his neighborhood, whose parents hoped to start them in Paris. He obtained these apprentices by boasting of his son’s success. Parents, attracted by the prospect of close training and perhaps eventual succession to the business, sent whichever child was most in the way. But when clerks or young women found a way to escape, they fled with rejoicing that increased the Rogrons’ bad name. New victims were supplied yearly by the indefatigable old father.

From fifteen Sylvie had two faces—the amiable seller and the sour spinster. Her acquired countenance was marvellous mimicry: all smiles and a soft, wheedling voice giving a commercial charm to business. Her real face, projecting from half-opened blinds, would have frightened the most resolute. When the Lorrains’ letter reached them they were in mourning for their father, from whom they inherited the house that had been as good as stolen from Pierrette’s grandmother, also certain lands bought by their father, and moneys acquired by usurious loans and mortgages to the peasantry. The yearly taking of stock was over. The price of the “Family Sister” had, at last, been paid in full. The Rogrons owned about sixty thousand francs’ worth of merchandise, forty thousand in bank or cash, and the value of their business. Sitting on a bench covered with striped-green Utrecht velvet behind their private counter, the siblings consulted what they should do.

All retail shopkeepers aspire to become bourgeois. By selling the good-will of their business they would have over a hundred and fifty thousand francs, not counting their father’s inheritance. By placing their available property in the public Funds they would each obtain about four thousand francs a year, and by taking the proceeds of their business when sold they could repair and improve the house they inherited, making it a good investment. They could then live in Provins. Their forewoman was the daughter of a rich farmer at Donnemarie, burdened with nine children; in five years the farmer had lost seven children, which made the forewoman interesting. Rogron had tried, unsuccessfully, to make her marry him; she showed aversion which baffled him. Mademoiselle Sylvie opposed the match; she sought instead to make the shrewd young woman their successor.

No passing observer can form an idea of the cryptic existence of a certain class of shopkeepers; one wonders on what and why they live. To discover the thin subsoil beneath their lives one must dig. The Parisian shopkeeper nurtures his soul on hope: one dreams of managing a theatre, honors of mayoralty, a country-house ten miles from Paris with a “park,” statues of tinted plaster, fountains that squirt threads of water—on which he will spend a mint; others dream of distinction in the National Guard. Provins filled the Rogrons with fanatical longings. Let it be said to La Champagne’s credit: this love is warranted. Provins rivals Frangistan and the valley of Cashmere; not only does it contain the poesy of Saadi, but it offers medicinal springs. The Provins roses are world-famous. Provins was the promised land that appeared to the two shopkeepers amid the muck of Saint-Denis.

Crossing the gray plains between La Ferté-Gaucher and Provins—a productive desert of wheat—you reach a hill. Suddenly a town watered by two rivers appears; at the rock’s feet stretches a verdant valley of enchanting lines and fugitive horizons. From Paris you pass through Provins along the highroad encumbered with beggars and blind men who follow you with pitiful voices. From Troyes you approach on the valley side. The château, old town, and ramparts terrace the hillside; the new town lies below. Upper and Lower Provins differ: the upper is airy, steep, solemn, with imposing ruins; the lower is a town of mills watered by the Voulzie and the Durtain, a town of inns, shops, retired merchants, diligences, travelling-carriages, and waggons. Its historical memories, melancholy ruins, ravines, gardens, and rivers banked with gardens excite the love of all its children, who, like Auvergnats or Savoyards, leave to make fortunes and always return. “Die in one’s form,” the proverb for hares and faithful souls, seems Provins’ motto.

Thus Jérôme and Sylvie thought constantly of their dear Provins. While Jérôme sold thread he saw the upper town; as he piled button cards he contemplated the valley; when he rolled ribbons he followed the shining rivers. Looking up at shelves he saw ravines where he had escaped his father’s anger and gathered blackberries. The little square in the lower town was his chief object: he imagined improving his house, dreaming of a new front, bedrooms, a salon, billiard-room, dining-room, and a kitchen garden turned into an English pleasure-ground with lawns, grottos, fountains, and statuary. The bedrooms they occupied in Paris were furnished with the merest necessaries, yet in fancy no one had finer furniture. Jérôme, walking, admired upholsterers’ windows and draperies he coveted for his house, and at home would tell his sister he’d found pieces “that will just do.” He bought and soon rejected each thing in favor of the next. The Budget itself could not have paid for his schemes. He wanted everything he saw, but abandoned each for the last. New balconies and ornaments he thought out of place in Paris: “Ah!” he said, “those fine things would look much better at Provins.” Leaning on his doorstep with a vacant eye, he visualized his gilded house, garden, fountain, billiard-table, and flowers.

Sylvie thought deeply, pen in hand, receiving the bourgeoisie of Provins, looking at herself in salon mirrors, admiring a cap. The siblings began to find the rue Saint-Denis atmosphere unhealthy; the market mud made them long for the fragrance of Provins roses. They were victims of genuine nostalgia and monomania, frustrated for now by the need to sell tapes and bobbins before leaving Paris. The promised land of the valley attracted them because they had really suffered crossing the sandy wastes of mercer’s business.

The Lorrains’ letter reached them amid meditations inspired by this future. They knew scarcely anything about their cousin Pierrette Lorrain. Their father had got possession of the Auffray property after they left home and said little of his affairs. They hardly remembered Aunt Lorrain. An hour of genealogical discussion was needed to make her out as the younger sister of their own mother by their grandfather Auffray’s second marriage. It struck them that this second marriage had harmed their interests by dividing the Auffray property between two daughters. In times past their father had sneered about it.

The Rogrons considered the Lorrains’ application through these recollections, which were not favorable to Pierrette. To take charge of an orphan cousin who might become their legal heir if neither married was a matter for debate. They had never seen Pierrette; what trouble would a young girl be? Wouldn’t it commit them to obligations? Could they send her away if they disliked her? Wouldn’t they have to marry her? If Jérôme found a yoke-mate among Provins heiresses they ought to preserve property for his children. A yoke-mate, according to Sylvie, meant a stupid, rich, ugly girl who would let herself be governed. They decided to refuse the Lorrain request. Sylvie agreed to write the answer. Business being urgent, she delayed, and when the forewoman offered to buy the stock and good-will of the “Family Sister,” which the Rogrons accepted, the matter left the old maid’s mind.

Sylvie Rogron and her brother departed for Provins four years before the arrival of Brigaut stirred Pierrette’s life. But the doings of the pair after reaching Provins are as necessary to relate as their life in Paris; for Provins was destined to be not less fatal to Pierrette than the commercial antecedents of her cousins.

When a petty shopkeeper returns from Paris to the provinces some Parisian notions cling for a while but soon fade, leaving only superficial scratches where the capital had touched the town. The ex-merchant’s shift into solid bourgeois life acts like an illness. No retail shopkeeper can pass at once from perpetual chatter to provincial silence, from Parisian bustle to small-town stillness, without visible change. Those who have saved a nest egg spend part of it on long-nursed desires that now absorb impulses once held in check by business: some travel, take municipal posts, hunt, fish, or speculate. The Rogrons, brother and sister, were seized by the desire to use the trowel and remodel their old house into something charming.

This fixed idea produced, under the contractor’s supervision, the front Brigaut inspected, its interior arrangements, and handsome furnishings. The builder never drove a nail without consulting them, demanding signatures on plans and explaining each item’s manufacture and price. For choice pieces he claimed provenance from Provins notables—Monsieur Tiphaine, Madame Julliard, and others—because the Rogrons wanted things like the rich bourgeois of Provins.

“Oh, if Monsieur Garceland has it, put it in,” said Mademoiselle Rogron. “His taste is good.”

“Sylvie, see, he wants ovolos in the cornice.”

“What an odd name!”

“It means eggs—from the Latin ovum.”

“What queer fellows you architects are!” cried Rogron. “It’s stepping on egg-shells to deal with you.”

“Shall we paint the corridor?” asked the builder.

“Good heavens, no!” cried Sylvie. “That would be five hundred francs more!”

“Oh, the salon and staircase are too pretty not to have the corridor decorated,” he urged. “That Madame Lesourd had hers painted last year.”

“And now her husband, as king’s attorney, must leave Provins.”

“Ah, he’ll be chief justice some day,” said the builder.

“How about Monsieur Tiphaine?”

“He’s got a pretty wife and will get on. He’ll go to Paris. Shall we paint the corridor?”

“Yes, yes,” said Rogron. “The Lesourds must see that we are as good as they.”

The first year after returning the Rogrons spent their days in such discussions, watching workmen, answering townspeople’s questions, and trying to make themselves socially intimate with Provins’ principal families.

They had never been into Parisian society; knowing no one there, they were starved for social life. In Provins they found former masters returned from Paris—Monsieur and Madame Julliard of the “Chinese Worm,” the Guepin clan whose youngest kept the “Three Distaffs,” and Madame Guenee, who had sold them the “Family Sister.” These families had spread through town: the mayor, Monsieur Garceland, was a Guepin son-in-law; the curate, Abbé Peroux, brother to Madame Julliard; the judge, Monsieur Tiphaine junior, brother to Madame Guenee.

The queen of the town was the beautiful Madame Tiphaine junior, daughter of Madame Roguin, a rich Parisian’s wife. Clever, delicate, pretty, and taken from a convent to marry in the provinces to please her mother, Mélanie Tiphaine behaved admirably in Provins. Handsomely dowered and still ambitious, her aim was to have Monsieur Tiphaine elected deputy; as deputy he would become judge in Paris, and she was determined to push him into Royal courts. To this end she courted vanities and pleased all parties; she succeeded. Twice a week she received Provins’ bourgeoisie in her Upper-town salon. At twenty she had made no blunders; she gratified every self-love and became the town’s pride. Without saying a word, every elector awaited Monsieur Tiphaine’s nomination. Every man regarded the future deputy as friend and protector; of course he would attain honors and reward Provins.

Madame Guenee had married her daughters into leading families and married Monsieur Garceland the collector herself; mother and daughters regarded Monsieur Tiphaine as the richest, ablest man around. The prosecuting attorney hoped to replace his uncle as judge. These four ladies formed a court around Madame Tiphaine, following her ideas and advice. Monsieur Julliard, eldest son of the old merchant and married to a farmer’s daughter, had fallen into a secret, disinterested passion for Madame Tiphaine. She played on him, advised him to start a journal, and acted as his Egeria. For two years Julliard had published the “Bee-hive,” filled with literary, archaeological, and medical articles by the family; advertisements paid expenses, subscriptions provided profit. Occasional incomprehensible verses addressed “TO HER!!!” united the Julliards with other clans; Madame Tiphaine’s salon naturally became the first in town. The few aristocrats had their own salon at the old Comtesse de Breautey’s.

During their first six months the Rogrons were received—first by Madame Julliard the elder and Madame Garceland, then after difficulty by Madame Tiphaine. All studied them before admitting them. Society aims to amalgamate persons of equal wealth, education, manners, accomplishments, and character. The Guepins, Guenees, and Julliards were better positioned than the Rogrons, whose father had been held in contempt for private conduct related to the Auffray property—facts known to notary Auffray.

In Madame Tiphaine’s dignified, homogeneous social life, people understood one another. The Rogrons fancied they would be accepted after being received by Mayor Garceland. Sylvie practiced boston; Rogron, unable to play, twirled his thumbs and could talk of nothing but his new house. Words choked him; he would rise, try to speak, become frightened, and sit down with a comic distortion of the lips. Sylvie betrayed herself at cards—sharp, irritable, whining when she lost, insolent when she won; nagging and quarrelsome, she became a scourge. Yet the pair possessed silly, open ambition and tried to play a part in a town already dominated by a close corporation of twelve allied families. Having spent thirty thousand francs restoring their house, and possessing at least ten thousand francs a year between them, they believed themselves wealthy and sought to impress society, which quickly measured their vulgarity, ignorance, and envy.

At their presentation to Madame Tiphaine she had already eyed them and remarked to Julliard junior, who lingered: “You all seem taken with those Rogrons.”

“No,” he said. “They bore my mother and annoy my wife. When Mademoiselle Sylvie was apprenticed to my father thirty years ago, none could endure her.”

“I have a great mind,” said Madame Tiphaine, putting her pretty foot on the fender bar, “to make it understood my salon is not an inn.”

Julliard raised his eyes to the ceiling as if to admire her wit.

“I wish my society to be select; and it certainly will not be if I admit those Rogrons.”

“They have neither heart, nor mind, nor manners,” said Monsieur Tiphaine. “If, after selling thread for twenty years, as my sister did for example—”

“Your sister, my dear,” said his wife, “cannot be out of place in any salon.”

“If,” he continued, “people are stupid enough not to throw off the shop and polish their manners, if they mistake the Counts of Champagne for the accounts of a wine-shop, as Rogron did this evening, they had better stay at home.”

“They are simply impudent,” said Julliard. “They talk as if no other handsome house in Provins but theirs existed. They want to crush us; yet they have hardly enough to live on.”

“If it was only the brother,” said Madame Tiphaine, “one might put up with him; give him a Chinese puzzle and he’d stay in a corner; but Mademoiselle Sylvie, with that voice like a hoarse hyena and those lobster-claws of hands! Don’t repeat all this, Julliard.”

When Julliard left, Madame Tiphaine told her husband, “I have aborigines enough whom I must receive; these two will kill me. I shall deprive myself of their society.”

“You are mistress in your house,” he replied, “but that will make enemies. The Rogrons will fling themselves into the opposition, which so far has no real strength in Provins. That Rogron is already intimate with Baron Gouraud and the lawyer Vinet.”

“Then,” Mélanie laughed, “they will do you service. Where there are no opponents, there is no triumph. A liberal conspiracy, an illegal cabal, a struggle of any kind, will bring you into the foreground.”

The justice looked at his wife with alarmed admiration.

Next day it was whispered the Rogrons had not succeeded in Madame Tiphaine’s salon. Her “inn” remark was much admired. A month passed before she returned Mademoiselle Sylvie’s visit; such insolence is noticed in the provinces.

During the evening Sylvie had spent at Madame Tiphaine’s, a disagreeable scene occurred between her and old Madame Julliard over boston—Sylvie accusing the old lady of cheating. The next time the hostess had the card-tables arranged before Sylvie arrived; Sylvie was reduced to wandering between tables as an onlooker, players glancing at her scornfully. At Madame Julliard senior’s they played whist, a game Sylvie did not know.

The old maid finally understood she was banned, but not why. She fancied jealousy. Invitations stopped, but the Rogrons persisted in paying evening visits. Satirical people teased them—amusingly, not spitefully—tempting them to talk absurdly about the eggs in their cornice and their wonderful wine cellar.

Before long the house was finished. The Rogrons resolved to give sumptuous dinners both to return civilities and exhibit luxury. The invited guests accepted from curiosity. The first dinner was for town leaders: Monsieur and Madame Tiphaine (who had never dined with them), Monsieur and Madame Julliard senior and junior, Monsieur Lesourd, the curate, and Monsieur and Madame Garardon. It was one of those long provincial dinners from five to nine. Madame Tiphaine had introduced the Parisian custom of leaving once coffee was served. That evening she had company and was anxious to go. The Rogrons accompanied her and her husband to the door; on returning disconcerted at not keeping them, the rest prepared to imitate Madame Tiphaine’s fashion with cruel provincial promptness.

“They won’t see our salon lighted up,” said Sylvie, “and that’s the show.”

They had counted on surprising their guests; it was the first admission to the “Rogron palace,” and the company at Madame Tiphaine’s awaited her opinion.

“Well!” cried Madame Martener. “You’ve seen the Louvre; tell us about it.”

“All? Well, it would be like the dinner—not much.”

“But describe it.”

Madame Tiphaine sketched the house: the gilded front door opening on a long corridor that divided the house unevenly; a portico and lawn with a sundial and a painted plaster statue of Spartacus; a staircase painted to imitate black marble, a walnut balustrade, cellar stairs under it; a dining-room communicating by folding doors with a salon overlooking the garden. The corridor supplied an ante-chamber. The Rogrons boasted of using only French woods: chestnut floors, chestnut sideboards and chairs. White calico curtains with red borders held by red straps on wooden rods ending in brass lion-paws. A dial hanging by a gilded napkin over a sideboard amused Madame Tiphaine only as a conceit. Lamps like restaurant counter lights, an ornate barometer Rogron adored, a white porcelain stove in a niche, crimson and gold paper like that in restaurants—everything clean, new, and harsh in tone. The dining-room, she conceded, had character but was marred by five engravings—Poniatowski, Napoleon, defence at Clichy, two Mazepas—in gilt frames of the vilest description. She preferred Madame Julliard’s Louis XV pastels and old provincial silver and china: “The provinces are provinces; they are only ridiculous when they mimic Paris.”

“As for the salon,” she said, “it is all red—the red Mademoiselle Sylvie turns when she loses at cards.”

“Sylvan-red,” said Monsieur Tiphaine, who supplied a quip that lingered in local speech.

“Window-curtains, red; furniture, red; mantelpiece, red veined yellow; heavy, common candelabra and clock mounted on bronze, Roman standards with Greek foliage; an inevitable lion decoratively holding a ball; applied mouldings on a glass over the chimney; a chandelier wrapped in green muslin in the worst taste; walls papered in red flock to imitate velvet with chromo-lithographs in stucco festooned frames; furniture in cashmere and elm in rigid uniformity—two sofas, two easy-chairs, two armchairs, six common chairs; an alabaster vase a la Medicis under glass; card-tables before the windows; an Aubusson carpet in the most vulgar pattern. The room looks as if no one lived there: no books, no engravings, no knick-knacks, no flowers—cold and barren like Mademoiselle Sylvie. Buffon says the style is the man; salons have styles.”

From this sketch one sees the Rogrons’ house and the absurdities a clever builder had foisted on them—new inventions, fantastic ornaments, systems for preventing smoke and damp, painted marquetry panels, colored glass, superfine locks—vulgarities that make a house expensive and gratify bourgeois taste.

No one chose to visit the Rogrons; their social plans failed. Invitations were refused—“already engaged” to Madame Garceland and others. The Rogrons had supposed a few dinners would secure a position. Only young men who would mock them and habitués who dined everywhere accepted.

Frightened at the loss of forty thousand francs swallowed in her “dear house,” Sylvie began to recover by economy. She gave no more dinners, sent away her cook, took a country-girl, and did her own cooking “for pleasure.”

Fourteen months after returning, they had fallen into solitude and idleness. Banished from society, Sylvie developed a strong hatred for the Tiphaines, Julliards, and the rest of the “clique”; she wanted a rival clique, but the lesser bourgeoisie comprised only small shopkeepers free only on Sundays and stained individuals like Vinet and the Bonapartist Baron Gouraud, whom Rogron thoughtlessly befriended despite warnings.

The brother and sister sat by the stove, recalling their former business, customers, and things they had wanted to forget. Ennui weighed on them by the second winter. They did not know how to pass the day: sometimes, as they went to bed, they said, “There’s another over!” They lingered in bed and dressed slowly. Rogron shaved daily, examined his face, consulted his sister on imagined changes, quarreled with the servant about hot water, wandered into the garden to inspect shrubs, the kiosk, joinery, settling walls, or fretted about a sick hen and damp spots. The barometer was his most useful possession; he tapped it like a friend. Breakfast took ages; their digestion was perfect. They read the “Bee-hive” and the “Constitutionnel,” the cost shared by Vinet and Baron Gouraud. Rogron delivered the paper to Gouraud, enjoying his long yarns and puzzling over warnings he had received—yet he foolishly repeated “the clique’s” suspicions to the colonel, who in turn talked about Madame Tiphaine and the ministerialists.

About two o’clock Rogron took a walk, happy if a shopkeeper stopped him to ask news. He would gather gossip, go to the Upper town or ravines, meet old men, and sometimes learned scholars. His bewilderment at hearing a deputy-judge, Desfondrilles, speak to Monsieur Martener about Provins’ springs and catacombs was great. Desfondrilles thought Provins should rival Spa; Martener explained towns and houses rarely recover from ruin. Rogron learned Provins had been a capital in the twelfth century and that the Upper town stood on extensive catacombs like cathedral naves—an archaeological hobby that occupied Rogron for days and gave topics for evenings.

Rogron’s walks gave him bits of information he narrated to Sylvie. He would ask the same person the same day, “Well, what’s the news?” and then fling himself on the sofa as if exhausted. Dinner came after he had gone to the kitchen twenty times and compared clocks. When they stayed home, the evenings were desert-like. Passersby sometimes heard unearthly sounds that were only yawns. The two, having nothing to grind between their rusty wheels, creaked at each other. Rogron spoke of marrying in despair; the thought of a woman frightened him. Sylvie, realizing the need for a third person, remembered the little cousin about whom no one had inquired—perhaps presumed dead—Pierrette Lorrain.

Sylvie, too careful to lose anything, pretended to have found the Lorrains’ letter to raise the prospect of Pierrette coming. She answered Madame Lorrain’s letter half affectionately, half commercially, blaming delay on change of abode, hinting Pierrette might inherit twelve thousand francs a year if Jérôme did not marry.

Like caged beasts, the siblings impatiently awaited the cousin. Three days after writing, they already wondered when she would arrive. Sylvie perceived in this spurious benevolence a way to recover social standing. She went to call on Madame Tiphaine to mention Pierrette’s coming, deploring the girl’s misfortune and posing as eager to succor her.

“You have been rather long in discovering her,” said Madame Tiphaine with sarcasm.

A few words by Madame Garceland during cards recalled the shameful conduct of old Rogron over the Auffray property; the notary explained the iniquity. Monsieur Tiphaine asked, “Where is the little girl now?”

“In Brittany,” said Rogron.

“Brittany is a large place,” remarked Monsieur Lesourd.

“Her grandfather and grandmother Lorrain wrote to us—when was that?” Rogron asked his sister.

Sylvie, asking Madame Garceland where she had bought her gown’s stuff, answered hastily, “Before we sold the business.”

“And have you only just answered the letter, mademoiselle?” the notary asked.

Sylvie turned red. “We wrote to the Institution of Saint-Jacques,” Rogron said.

“That is an almshouse for old people,” said Desfondrilles, knowing Nantes. “They receive no one under sixty.”

“She is there, with her grandmother,” Rogron insisted.

“Her mother had a little fortune, the eight thousand francs your grandfather left to her,” the notary said, making the blunder intentionally.

“Ah!” said Rogron, stupidly missing the sarcasm.

“Then you know nothing about your cousin’s position or means?” asked Monsieur Tiphaine.

“If Monsieur Rogron had known it,” the deputy-judge said, “he would never have left her so long in such an establishment. I remember a Nantes house belonging to Monsieur and Madame Lorrain was sold under court order, and Mademoiselle Lorrain’s claim was swallowed up. I was commissioner at the time.”

The notary spoke of Colonel Lorrain, who, had he lived, would have been amazed his daughter was in such an institution. The Rogrons retreated, blaming malicious world. Sylvie saw her benevolence had missed its effect; she had lost ground. Thereafter the Rogrons no longer concealed their hatred of that class. The brother told Sylvie the scandals Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet had suggested about the Tiphaines, Guenees, Garcelands, and Julliards: accusing Madame Tiphaine’s family of fraud and claiming her fortune was stolen—“and such people set up to be proud!” he fumed.

Once they began to declaim against “the clique,” they set out, unknowingly, to have a society of their own: their house was to become a rendezvous for the floating elements of the liberal party. Colonel Gouraud and the lawyer Vinet, who felt ostracized and shared opinions, had watched the Rogrons’ launch with curiosity. Both professed patriotism—partly to become of consequence. The Liberals in Provins were confined to one old soldier who kept a cafe, an innkeeper, Monsieur Cournant the notary, Doctor Neraud, and a few scattered farmers. The colonel and the lawyer, delighted to find a fool whose money could aid their schemes and whose house could serve as a meeting-ground, exploited the Rogrons’ ill-will toward the upper class. They already shared a subscription to the “Constitutionnel”; it would not be hard for the colonel to convert the ex-mercer into a Liberal, though Rogron knew so little of politics he might take Sergeant Mercier’s exploits for those of a brother shopkeeper.

Pierrette’s expected arrival made the duo’s selfish ideas ripen. Gouraud, a veteran accustomed to horrors, felt little repelled by the thought of a poor cousin and began eyeing Sylvie’s fortune. The imperial colonel—short and fat with enormous ear-rings and bushy tufts, sparse grizzled whiskers called “fins,” a jolly red face discolored by campaigns, a sabre-cut on his nose, and thick nasal speech—had the Legion of Honor; his emoluments and retired pay gave him about three thousand francs a year.

The lawyer Vinet, tall and thin, had liberal opinions instead of talent and earned meagerly. In Provins lawyers plead their own cases; the court was unfavorable to Vinet because of his opinions, so agrarian Liberals preferred judges’ congenial lawyers. Vinet was disfavored also for seducing a rich girl near Coulommiers and forcing her parents to marry her to him. Madame Vinet was a Chargeboeuf, an old La Brie family; her parents had arranged a fortune perhaps to provide for his sister’s children. Vinet’s first bold efforts failed. Driven by poverty and ashamed of failing to give his wife a fitting appearance, he had tried public life but the Chargeboeufs refused influence. Repulsed, filled with hatred for his wife’s family and the government that denied him a place, Vinet became a Liberal, hoping opposition triumph would raise him. He lived miserably in the Upper town; his wife seldom left. Madame Vinet had no defenders except an old Madame de Chargeboeuf in Troyes. Vinet’s conscience grew elastic; his gall increased. His viperish face, slit mouth, gleaming eyes behind glasses, and sharp rasping voice frightened courtroom acquaintances. His muddy green-yellow skin showed jaundice born of balked ambition. He could talk, argue, and was shrewd; accustomed to view everything for personal advantage, he was fitted for politics; a man who shrinks from nothing so long as it’s legal is strong—Vinet’s strength lay there.

This future parliamentary athlete had a terrible influence on Pierrette’s fate. At present he aimed to found a newspaper at Provins. After studying the Rogrons from a distance (with the colonel’s aid), he concluded the brother might be made useful. He was not mistaken: Vinet’s days of poverty were ending after seven wretched years. The day Gouraud told him the Rogrons had quarrelled with the Upper-town bourgeois aristocracy, he nudged the colonel and said, with a knowing look:—

“One woman or another—handsome or ugly—you don’t care; marry Mademoiselle Rogron and we can organize something at once.”

“I have thought of it,” replied Gouraud, “but they’ve sent for Colonel Lorrain’s daughter; she’s their next of kin.”

“You can get them to make a will in your favor. Ha! you’d get a very comfortable house.”

“As for the little girl—well, let’s see her,” said the colonel, leering wickedly, showing how little respect he felt for any girl.

After her grandparents were placed in the kind of hospital where they expected to end their days, Pierrette, young and proud, suffered so at living on charity that she was grateful when she heard she had rich relations. Brigaut, the son of her mother’s friend the major and her childhood companion, apprenticed as a cabinet-maker at Nantes, hearing of her departure offered the money to pay her way to Paris in the diligence—sixty francs, the total of his tips as an apprentice—which she accepted with the indifference of true affection.

Brigaut used to go every Sunday to Saint-Jacques to play with Pierrette and console her. The vigorous young workman delighted in bestowing devoted protection on the object chosen by his heart. More than once, sitting in a garden corner, they had embroidered the veil of the future with youthful projects: the apprentice, armed with his plane, scouring the world to make their fortune while Pierrette waited.

In October, 1824, when she had just turned eleven, the two old people and Brigaut, all sorrowful, entrusted her to the conductor of the diligence from Nantes to Paris, begging him to put her safely on the diligence from Paris to Provins and take good care of her. Poor Brigaut ran like a dog after the coach as long as he could; when exhausted his tear-wet eyes still followed her. She, too, cried when she could no longer see him, putting her head out of the window until the lumbering vehicle disappeared.

The Lorrains and Brigaut knew so little of life that the girl had not a penny when she arrived in Paris. The conductor, to whom she had mentioned her rich friends, paid her hotel expenses and made the conductor of the Provins diligence pay him, telling him to take good care of the girl and to see that the charges were paid by the family, treating her like a parcel of goods. Four days after leaving Nantes, about nine on a Monday night, a kind old conductor of the Messageries-royales took Pierrette by the hand, and while porters discharged packages and passengers for Provins in the Grand’Rue, he led the little girl, whose only luggage was a bundle with two dresses, two chemises, and two pairs of stockings, to Mademoiselle Rogron’s house, pointed out by the director at the coach office.

“Good-evening, mademoiselle and the rest of the company. I’ve brought you a cousin, and here she is; and a nice little girl too. You have forty-seven francs to pay me, and sign my book.”

Mademoiselle Sylvie and her brother were dumb with pleasure and amazement.

“Excuse me,” said the conductor, “the coach is waiting. Sign my book and pay me forty-seven francs, sixty centimes, and whatever you please for myself and the conductor from Nantes; we’ve taken care of the little girl as if she were our own; paid for her beds and food, her fare to Provins, and other little things.”

“Forty-seven francs, twelve sous!” said Sylvie.

“You are not going to dispute it?” cried the man.

“Where’s the bill?” said Rogron.

“Bill! look at the book.”

“Stop talking, and pay him,” said Sylvie. “You see there’s nothing else to be done.”

Rogron fetched the money and gave the man forty-seven francs, twelve sous.

“And nothing for my comrade and me?” said the conductor.

Sylvie took two francs from her old velvet key-bag.

“Thank you, no,” said the man; “keep ’em yourself. We would rather care for the little one for her own sake.” He picked up his book and departed, saying to the servant-girl: “What a pair! it seems there are crocodiles out of Egypt!”

“Such men are always brutal,” said Sylvie, who overheard the words.

“They took good care of the little girl, anyhow,” said Adele with her hands on her hips.

“We don’t have to live with him,” remarked Rogron.

“Where’s the little one to sleep?” asked Adele.

Thus Pierrette Lorrain arrived in her cousins’ home, gazed at with stolid eyes; she was tossed to them like a package, with no intermediate state between the wretched chamber at Saint-Jacques and their dining-room, which seemed a palace. She was shy and speechless. To any but the Rogrons the little Breton girl would have seemed enchanting in her coarse blue flannel petticoat, pink cambric apron, thick shoes, blue stockings, white kerchief, and red worsted mittens edged with white bought by the conductor. Her dainty Breton cap, washed in Paris for the journey, formed a halo round her happy little face. The cap of fine lawn, trimmed with stiffened lace, produced a soft shadow that gave her virginal grace. Beneath the fluted frame of light sparkled a white, rosy, artless face glowing with vigorous health; the warmth of the room brought blood to cheeks, ears, lips, and the end of the delicate nose, making the complexion whiter still.

“Well, are you not going to say anything? I am your cousin Sylvie, and that is your cousin Rogron.”

“Do you want something to eat?” asked Rogron.

“When did you leave Nantes?” asked Sylvie.

“Is she dumb?” said Rogron.

“Poor little dear, she has hardly any clothes,” cried Adele, who had opened the child’s handkerchief-bound bundle.

“Kiss your cousin,” said Sylvie.

Pierrette kissed Rogron. “Kiss your cousin,” said Rogron. She kissed Sylvie.

“She is tired out with her journey, poor little thing; she wants to go to sleep,” said Adele.

Pierrette was overcome with a sudden, invincible aversion for her two relatives—a feeling no one had ever before excited in her. Sylvie and the maid took her up to bed in the room where Brigaut afterward noticed the white cotton curtain. It held a little bed with a blue pole and a calico curtain, a walnut bureau without a marble top, a small table, a looking-glass, a common night-table without a door, and three chairs. The walls sloped and were hung with shabby blue paper with black flowers. The tiled floor, stained red and polished, was icy. There was only a strip of carpet at the bedside. The mantelpiece of common marble bore a mirror, two candelabra, and a vulgar alabaster cup with pigeon handles.

“You will be comfortable here, my little girl?” said Sylvie.

“Oh, it’s beautiful!” said the child in her silvery voice.

“She’s not difficult to please,” muttered the stout servant. “Sha’n’t I warm her bed?” she asked.

“Yes,” said Sylvie, “the sheets may be damp.”

Adele brought one of her own night-caps when she returned with the warming-pan, and Pierrette, who had slept only in coarse linen sheets, was amazed at the fineness and softness of the cotton ones. When she was tucked up, Adele and Sylvie went downstairs, and Adele could not refrain from saying, “All she has isn’t worth three francs, mademoiselle.”

Ever since Sylvie’s economical regime began she had compelled the maid to sit in the dining-room so one fire and one lamp could do for all, except when Colonel Gouraud and Vinet came, on which occasions Adele was sent to the kitchen.

Pierrette’s arrival enlivened the rest of the evening.

“We must get her some clothes to-morrow,” said Sylvie; “she has absolutely nothing.”

“No shoes but those she had on, which weigh a pound,” said Adele.

“That’s always so, in their part of the country,” remarked Rogron.

“How she looked at her room! though it isn’t handsome enough for a cousin of yours, mademoiselle.”

“It is good enough; hold your tongue,” said Sylvie.

“Gracious, what chemises! coarse enough to scratch her skin off; not a thing can she use here,” said Adele, emptying the bundle.

Master, mistress, and servant were busy till past ten, deciding what cambric to buy for chemises, how many pairs of stockings, how many under-petticoats, and what material, and reckoning the cost of Pierrette’s outfit.

“You won’t get off under three hundred francs,” said Rogron.

“Three hundred francs!” cried Sylvie.

“Yes, three hundred. Add it up.”

They recalculated and found the cost would be fully three hundred francs, not counting the making.

“Three hundred francs at one stroke!” said Sylvie to herself as she got into bed.

Pierrette was one of those children of love whom love endows with tenderness, vivacity, gaiety, nobility, and devotion. Nothing had so far wounded a heart delicate as a fawn’s, but now it was painfully repressed by the cold greeting of her cousins. If Brittany had been full of outward misery, at least it was full of love. The old Lorrains were the most incapable merchants, but they were loving, frank, caressing friends, like the incautious and uncalculating. At Pen-Hoel the little granddaughter had received no education but that of nature. Pierrette went where she liked—on a boat on the pond or roaming the village and fields with Jacques Brigaut, her comrade—exactly as Paul and Virginia might have done. Petted by everybody, free as air, they chased the joys of childhood: in summer watching the fishing, catching insects, gatherings flowers, gardening; in winter sliding, building snow-men or huts, pelting each other with snowballs. Welcomed by all, they met smiles wherever they went.

When education began, disasters came, too. Jacques, left without means at his father’s death, was apprenticed and fed by charity, as Pierrette soon would be at Saint-Jacques. Until the little girl was taken with her grandparents to that asylum she had known nothing but fond caresses and protection. Accustomed to confide in so much love, she missed in these eagerly desired rich relations the kindly looks and ways which all the world, even conductors, had bestowed on her. Her bewilderment, already great, was increased by the moral atmosphere she had entered. The heart turns cold or hot like the body. The poor child wanted to cry without knowing why; but being very tired she went to sleep.

Next morning, waking early as country children do, she dressed softly and looked out at the little square. She started downstairs and was struck with the beauties of the staircase: the painted walls, the brasses, the ornaments, the window fixtures. Then she went to the garden-door but couldn’t open it, and returned to wait until Adele stirred. As soon as the woman went to the kitchen Pierrette flew to the garden, took possession of it, ran to the river, marvelled at the kiosk, and sat there; truly, she had enough to see and wonder at until her cousins rose. At breakfast Sylvie said:

“Was it you, little one, trotting over my head by daybreak and making that racket on the stairs? You woke me so I couldn’t get to sleep. You must be very good and quiet, and amuse yourself without noise. Your cousin doesn’t like noise.”

“And you must wipe your feet,” said Rogron. “You went into the kiosk with your dirty shoes and tracked the floor. Your cousin likes cleanliness. A great girl like you ought to be clean. Weren’t you clean in Brittany? But I recollect when I went down there to buy thread it was pitiable to see the folks,—they were like savages. At any rate she has a good appetite,” he added, looking at his sister; “one would think she hadn’t eaten anything for days.”

Thus from the start Pierrette was hurt by their remarks—hurt, she knew not why. Her straightforward, open nature, hitherto left to itself, was not given to reflection. Incapable of thinking her cousins hard, she was fated to find it out slowly through suffering. After breakfast the brother and sister, pleased with Pierrette’s astonishment at the house and anxious to enjoy it, took her to the salon to show its splendors and teach her not to touch them. Many celibates, driven by loneliness, substitute factitious affections for natural ones; they love dogs, cats, canaries, servants, or their confessor. Rogron and Sylvie had come to love immoderately their house and furniture, which had cost them so dear. Sylvie began by helping Adele in the mornings to dust and arrange the furniture, under pretext that she did not know how to keep it looking as new. This dusting became a desired occupation, and the furniture, instead of losing value, became ever more precious. To use things without hurting or soiling them was the problem that became the mania of the old maid’s life. Sylvie had a closet full of bits of wool, wax, varnish, and brushes, used with the dexterity of a cabinet-maker; she had feather dusters and dusting-cloths and rubbed away without fear of hurting herself—she was so strong. Her cold blue eyes roved over the furniture and under it; you could as soon have found a tender spot in her heart as a bit of fluff under the sofa.

After the remarks at Madame Tiphaine’s, Sylvie dared not flinch from the three hundred francs for Pierrette’s clothes. During the first week her time and Pierrette’s were taken up with frocks to order and try on, chemises and petticoats to cut out and have made by a seamstress who worked by the day. Pierrette did not know how to sew.

“That’s pretty bringing up!” said Rogron. “Don’t you know how to do anything, little girl?”

Pierrette, who knew nothing but how to love, made a pretty, childish gesture.

“What did you do in Brittany?” asked Rogron.

“I played,” she answered naively. “Everybody played with me. Grandmamma and grandpapa told me stories. Ah! they all loved me!”

“Hey!” said Rogron; “didn’t you take it easy!”

Pierrette opened her eyes wide, not comprehending.

“She is as stupid as an owl,” said Sylvie to Mademoiselle Borain, the best seamstress in Provins.

“She’s so young,” said the workwoman, looking kindly at Pierrette, whose delicate little face was turned up with a coaxing look.

Pierrette preferred the sewing-women to her relations. She watched their work and made those pretty speeches that seem like the flowers of childhood, which her cousin had already silenced, for that gaunt woman loved to impress those under her with salutary awe. The sewing-women were delighted with Pierrette. Their work, however, was not carried on without many and loud grumblings.

“That child will make us pay through the nose!” cried Sylvie to her brother.

“Stand still, my dear, and don’t plague us; it is all for you and not for me,” she would say to Pierrette when the child was being measured. Sometimes, when Pierrette asked the seamstress a question, Sylvie rebuked her: “Let Mademoiselle Borain do her work, and don’t talk to her; it is not you who are paying for her time.”

“Mademoiselle,” said Mademoiselle Borain, “am I to back-stitch this?”

“Yes, do it firmly; I don’t want to be making such an outfit as this every day.”

Sylvie put the same emulative spirit into Pierrette’s outfit as she had into the house. She was determined her cousin should be as well dressed as Madame Garceland’s little girl. She bought fashionable boots of bronzed kid like the Tiphaine children wore, fine cotton stockings, a corset by the best maker, a dress of blue reps, a pretty cape lined with white silk—all so Sylvie might hold her own against those women. The underclothes matched; Sylvie feared the examining eyes of the various mothers. Pierrette’s chemises were of fine Madapolam calico. Mademoiselle Borain had mentioned that the sub-prefect’s little girls wore cambric drawers, embroidered and trimmed in the latest style. Pierrette had the same. Sylvie ordered for her a charming little drawn bonnet of blue velvet lined with white satin, precisely like Dr. Martener’s little daughter’s.

Thus attired, Pierrette was the most enchanting little girl in Provins. On Sunday after church all the ladies kissed her; Mesdames Tiphaine, Garceland, Galardon, Julliard and the rest fell in love with the sweet Breton child. This enthusiasm flattered Sylvie’s self-love; she regarded it as less due to Pierrette than to her own benevolence. Yet she ended by being affronted by her cousin’s success. Pierrette was constantly invited out, and Sylvie allowed her to go, always to triumph over “those ladies.” Pierrette was much in demand for games or little parties and dinners with their children. She had succeeded where the Rogrons had failed; Mademoiselle Sylvie soon grew indignant that Pierrette was asked to other children’s houses when those children never came to hers. The artless little thing did not hide her pleasure in these visits, whose affectionate manners contrasted with her cousins’ harshness. A mother would have rejoiced in the child’s happiness, but the Rogrons had taken Pierrette for their own sakes, not for hers; their feelings were dyed in selfishness and a sort of commercial calculation.

The handsome outfit, the fine Sunday dresses, and the every-day frocks were the beginning of Pierrette’s troubles. Like children free to amuse themselves, unused to restraint, she was hard on her clothes, her shoes, and above all on the embroidered drawers. A mother reproves thinking only of the child; her voice is gentle and she does not raise it unless driven to extremities. But here the cousins’ money was the first consideration; their interests, not the child’s, were to be thought of. Children have the perceptions of the canine race for the sentiments of those who rule them; they know instinctively whether they are loved or only tolerated. Pure and innocent hearts are distressed by shades of difference more than contrasts; a child does not understand evil, but it knows when the instinct of good and beauty is shocked. The lectures Pierrette drew on propriety, modesty, and economy were merely the corollary of the one theme: “Pierrette will ruin us.”

These perpetual fault-findings, destined to a fatal result for the poor child, brought the two celibates back to their old shop-keeping habits, from which their move to Provins had parted them, and in which their natures now expanded. Accustomed to rule, make inquisitions, and reprove clerks sharply, Rogron and his sister had suffered for lack of victims. Little minds need to practise despotism to relieve their nerves, just as great souls thirst for equality in friendship to exercise their hearts. Narrow natures expand by persecuting as others do by beneficence; they prove power over fellows by cruel tyranny as others by loving kindness. Add self-interest and you may read the enigma of most social matters.

Thenceforth Pierrette became a necessity to her cousins’ lives. From the day of her coming their minds were occupied—first with her outfit, then with the novelty of a third presence. But every new thing, a sentiment and even a tyranny, is moulded into fresh shapes. Sylvie began by calling Pierrette “my dear,” or “little one.” Then she abandoned gentler terms for “Pierrette” only. Her reprimands, at first cross, became sharp and angry; once on the path of fault-finding, the brother and sister advanced rapidly. They were no longer bored to death! It was not deliberate wickedness; it was the blind instinct of an imbecile tyranny. The pair believed they were doing Pierrette a service, as they had thought their harshness a benefit to apprentices.

Pierrette, whose true, noble, extreme sensibility was the antipodes of the Rogrons’ hardness, dreaded being scolded; it wounded her so that tears instantly started in her beautiful, pure eyes. She struggled to repress the enchanting sprightliness that had made her a favorite elsewhere. After a time she displayed it only in the homes of her little friends. By the end of the first month she had learned to be passive in her cousins’ house—so much that Rogron one day asked if she were ill. At that question she ran to the end of the garden and stood crying by the river, into which her tears may have fallen as she herself was about to fall into the social torrent.

One day, despite all care, she tore her best reps frock at Madame Tiphaine’s, where she had been happy. The child burst into tears, foreseeing the cruel things that would be said at home. Questioned by friends, she let fall a few words about her terrible cousin. Madame Tiphaine happened to have some reps exactly like the frock and put in a new breadth herself. Mademoiselle Rogron found out the trick, as she called it, which the little devil had played her. From that day she refused to let Pierrette go to any of “those women’s” houses.

Pierrette’s life in Provins was divided into three phases. The first, already shown, in which she had some joy mingled with the cold kindness and sharp reproaches of her cousins, lasted three months. Sylvie’s refusal to let her go to Madame Tiphaine’s, backed by the necessity of beginning her education, ended the first phase of her Provins life—the only period when it was bearable.

These events at the Rogrons were watched by Vinet and the colonel with the caution of foxes preparing to enter a poultry-yard. They called from time to time—but seldom, lest they alarm the old maid; they talked with Rogron under pretexts and made themselves masters of his mind with an affectation of reserve and modesty which even Tartuffe might have respected. The colonel and the lawyer were spending the evening with Rogron on the day Sylvie had bitterly refused to let Pierrette go to Madame Tiphaine’s. Told of this refusal, the colonel and the lawyer looked at each other as if to say they knew Provins well.

“Madame Tiphaine intended to insult you,” said the lawyer. “We have long warned Rogron of what would happen. There’s no good to be got from those people.”

“What can you expect from the anti-national party!” cried the colonel, twirling his moustache. “But, mademoiselle, if we had tried to warn you you might have supposed we had malicious motives. If you like a game of cards in the evening, why not have it at home; why not play your boston here, in your own house? Is it impossible to fill the places of those idiots, the Julliards and all the rest? Vinet and I know how to play boston, and we can easily find a fourth. Vinet might present his wife to you; she is charming, and, what is more, a Chargeboeuf. You will not be so exacting as those apes of the Upper town; you won’t require a good little housewife, compelled by family meanness to do her own work, to dress like a duchess. Poor woman, she has the courage of a lion and the meekness of a lamb.”

Sylvie showed her long yellow teeth as she smiled on the colonel, who bore it heroically and assumed a flattered air.

“If we are only four we can’t play boston every night,” said Sylvie.

“Why not? What do you suppose an old soldier of the Empire like me does with himself? And as for Vinet, his evenings are always free. Besides, you’ll have plenty of other visitors; I warrant you that,” he added with a rather mysterious air.

“What you ought to do,” said Vinet, “is take an open stand against the ministerialists of Provins and form an opposition salon. You would soon be popular; you would have a society at once. The Tiphaines would be furious. Well, why not laugh at others, if others laugh at you?—and they do; the clique doesn’t mince matters in talking about you.”

“How’s that?” demanded Sylvie.

In the provinces gossip leaks from one social set to another. Vinet knew all the slurs cast upon the Rogrons in the salons from which they were excluded. The deputy-judge and archaeologist Desfondrilles belonged to neither party. With other independents like him, he repeated what he heard on both sides and Vinet made the most of it. The lawyer’s spiteful tongue put venom into Madame Tiphaine’s speeches, and by showing Rogron and Sylvie the ridicule they had brought on themselves he roused an undying hatred in those bitter natures, which needed an object for their petty passions.

A few days later Vinet brought his wife, a well-bred woman, neither pretty nor plain, timid, very gentle, and deeply conscious of her false position. Madame Vinet was fair, faded by the cares of her poor household, and simply dressed. No woman could have pleased Sylvie more. Madame Vinet endured her airs and bent before them like one accustomed to subjection. On the poor woman’s rounded brow and timid cheek and in her slow, gentle glance were traces of deep reflection, of those perceptive thoughts women accustomed to suffer bury in silence.

The influence of the colonel, now displaying courtier graces in marked contrast to his usual brusqueness, together with the astute Vinet’s, was soon to harm the Breton child. Shut up in the house, no longer allowed to go out except in company with her old cousin, Pierrette, that pretty little squirrel, was at the mercy of the incessant cry, “Don’t touch that, child, let that alone!” She was perpetually lectured on carriage and behavior; if she stooped or rounded her shoulders her cousin would call to her to be as erect as herself (Sylvie was rigid as a soldier presenting arms); sometimes the ill-natured old maid enforced the order with slaps on the back to make the girl straighten up.

Thus the free and joyous little child of the Marais learned by degrees to repress all liveliness and to make herself, as best she could, an automaton.

One evening early in Pierrette’s second stay at her cousins’ house, the child, unseen all evening by the three guests, came in to kiss them good-night. Sylvie turned her cheek coldly to avoid kissing her; the motion was so cruel that Pierrette burst into tears.

“Did you prick yourself, little girl?” said the atrocious Vinet.

“What is the matter?” asked Sylvie, severely.

“Nothing,” said the child, going up to Rogron.

“Nothing? That’s nonsense; nobody cries for nothing,” Sylvie snapped.

“What is it, my little darling?” Madame Vinet asked.

“My rich cousin isn’t as kind to me as my poor grandmother was,” sobbed Pierrette.

“Your grandmother took your money,” Sylvie retorted, “and your cousin will leave you hers.”

The colonel and the lawyer exchanged glances.

“I would rather be robbed and loved,” said Pierrette.

“Then you shall be sent back whence you came.”

“What has the dear little thing done?” Madame Vinet asked.

Vinet gave his wife the stern look with which men enforce dominion. The hapless child, repeatedly punished for lacking that which was wanted of her—a fortune—took up her cards. Sylvie, throwing up her head so violently that the yellow wall-flowers in her cap nodded, declared, “She is always looking about to annoy us. She opened my watch to see the inside, meddled with the wheel and broke the mainspring. Mademoiselle pays no heed to what is said to her. I am all day long telling her to take care of things, and I might just as well talk to that lamp.”

Ashamed before strangers, Pierrette crept from the room.

“I am thinking all the time how to subdue that child,” Rogron said.

“Isn’t she old enough to go to school?” Madame Vinet suggested, but was silenced by a look from her husband, who had concealed his own and the colonel’s schemes.

“This is what comes of taking charge of other people’s children!” cried the colonel. “You may yet have some of your own. Why don’t you both marry?”

Sylvie smiled at the colonel; for the first time in her life she met a man for whom the idea she might marry did not seem absurd.

“Madame Vinet is right,” Rogron said; “perhaps teaching would keep Pierrette quiet. A master wouldn’t cost much.”

“If you will be security for that opposition journal I mentioned,” Vinet said, “you will find an excellent master in the managing editor; we intend to engage that poor schoolmaster deprived by the clergy’s encroachments. My wife is right; Pierrette is a rough diamond needing polishing.”

While Vinet explained how the paper would benefit them all, Pierrette dissolved in tears. She blamed her cousin and believed charity should be whole. She hated her handsome frocks and their cost, and resolved to behave so no fault could be found. She recalled Brigaut’s gift of his savings, thought her troubles could not worsen, unaware further misfortunes were being planned in the salon.

Soon Pierrette had a writing-master to teach reading, writing, and ciphering. This brought the usual complaints: ink-spots on tables, copy-books and pens abandoned, sand scattered; torn books. She was told to earn her living and not be a burden; such remarks choked her. She restrained tears or was scolded for weeping as an insult to her cousins’ kindness. Rogron found his mode—he scolded her as he scolded clerks, summoned her from play to study, made her repeat lessons, becoming almost savage. Sylvie taught her the little she knew of women’s work. Neither had softness. Their narrow minds, finding pleasure in worrying the child, passed from outward kindness into severity, justified by what they called Pierrette’s self-will. They could not adapt instruction to the pupil’s intelligence—a difference between public and private education. Pierrette took an age to learn the rudiments and was called stupid, clumsy, awkward for trifles. Abused incessantly in words and by harsh looks, she acquired the submissive habits of a sheep, daring nothing of her own impulse, awaiting her cousins’ orders and sheltering her mind in passive obedience. Her brilliant colors faded. At times she complained of feeling ill; when asked “Where?” she answered, “Everywhere.”

“Nonsense! who ever heard of suffering everywhere?” Sylvie scoffed. Rogron lectured that “everywhere” meant nowhere—“you’re complaining for complaining’s sake.”

Pierrette fell silent, for her girlish remarks were met with ignorant commonplaces she knew were ridiculous.

“You complain,” Rogron said, “but you’ve got the appetite of a monk.”

Only Adele, the fat servant, did not bruise the little flower. She warmed Pierrette’s bed on the sly after Sylvie scolded her. Sylvie’s belief was firm: children should be hardened—they had been. “You’ll make Pierrette a peakling,” she said, using their word for a puny, suffering being.

The child’s natural affection was treated as dissimulation. Her fresh devotion was pitilessly crushed. If she tried to soften their ferocity, they accused her of ulterior motives. “Tell me at once what you want?” Rogron would say, “you are not coaxing me for nothing.” Neither brother nor sister believed in affection; Pierrette’s whole being was affection. Colonel Gouraud, anxious to please Sylvie, approved all she did. Vinet encouraged them, attributing her so-called misdeeds to Breton obstinacy as incurable. Flattered, Rogron agreed to go security for the “Courrier de Provins,” and Sylvie invested five thousand francs.

The colonel and lawyer then took the field. They got a hundred shares of five hundred francs among farmers and the “independents” and buyers of national-domain lands—whose fears they exploited. Each shareholder subscribed to the paper; the judicial advertisements were split between the “Bee-hive” and the “Courrier.” The paper’s first issue contained a pompous eulogy of Rogron, presenting him as Provins’ Laffitte. The public, nudged thus, expected contested elections. Madame Tiphaine, dreaming of taking her husband to Paris as deputy, despaired when an article in the new paper attacked her and Julliard junior: “There is always a scoundrel close to a dupe,” she said.

As the “Courrier” spread, Vinet bought new clothes and adopted the gray slouch hat of liberals; his wife took a servant and appeared as the wife of a prominent man. Vinet proved grateful out of policy. He and his ally Cournant, a liberal notary opposed to the ministerial Auffray, became close advisers to the Rogrons, performing services: leases made by old Rogron in 1815 were expiring, and horticulture and gardening had developed near Provins. The lawyer and notary worked to increase the Rogrons’ rentals. Vinet won two suits involving planting trees and five hundred poplars; the proceeds, with the Rogrons’ savings—who for three years had set aside six thousand francs a year at high interest—were invested in improved lands. Vinet also carried out the ejectment of peasants who had defaulted on debts secured by the elder Rogron. The cost of the fine house was thus partly recouped. Their lands, chosen by their father’s innkeeper’s eye, were divided into small holdings, the largest not exceeding five acres, rented to safe tenants whose other holdings secured leases. By 1826 these investments brought in five thousand francs a year; taxes charged to tenants, no buildings needed insurance or repairs.

By the end of Pierrette’s second period in Provins, life had become so harsh—cold indifference, silly fault-finding, total absence of affection—that she felt a chill like a grave creeping round her. She thought of escaping on foot to Brittany to her grandparents. Two events prevented it: old Lorrain died, and Rogron was appointed her guardian. Had the grandmother died first, Rogron, advised by Vinet, would have claimed Pierrette’s eight thousand francs and reduced the old man to penury.

“You may, perhaps, inherit from Pierrette,” Vinet said with a horrid smile. “Who knows who may live and who may die?”

Illuminated by that remark, Rogron pressured Madame Lorrain until she secured for Pierrette the reversion of the eight thousand francs at her death.

Deeply shocked and on the point of making her first communion—a reason to abandon escape—Pierrette’s ceremony led to changes at the Rogrons’. Sylvie learned that Monsieur le curé Peroux taught the Julliards, Lesourds, Garcelands; so she insisted Pierrette be instructed by the vicar, Monsieur Habert, a priest thought to belong to the Congregation—zealous, feared, austere, ambitious. His sister, an unmarried woman about thirty, kept a school for young ladies; both siblings were thin, yellow, black-haired.

Like a true Breton girl, rooted in Catholic practice and poetry, Pierrette opened to the priest’s words. Suffering predisposes to devotion; many young girls are inclined to mysticism, and the priest found good soil. He changed the current of her thoughts: Pierrette loved Jesus as taught at first communion—as a celestial bridegroom—giving meaning to her sufferings; she saw God’s finger in events. Her soul, hurt though she could not accuse her cousins of outright wrong, took refuge in Faith, Hope, Charity. She abandoned thoughts of escape. Surprised by the priest’s effect on Pierrette, Sylvie too became pious under his influence. Jerome Rogron, however, who could not be gripped by this so-called Jesuit (then the late Constitutionnel was a stronger influence), remained faithful to Colonel Gouraud, Vinet, and Liberalism.

Mademoiselle Rogron befriended Mademoiselle Habert; the two spinsters loved each other as sisters. Mademoiselle Habert offered to take Pierrette to her school to spare Sylvie the annoyance of education, but the Rogrons declared the house would be too lonely without Pierrette; their attachment seemed excessive.

When Gouraud and Vinet learned of Mademoiselle Habert’s appearance, they feared the priest had matrimonial designs for his sister like the colonel’s plans for Sylvie. “Your sister wants to get you married,” Vinet told Rogron. “With whom?” Rogron asked. “With that old sorceress of a schoolmistress,” the colonel sneered. Rogron naively replied that she hadn’t spoken of it. Sylvie was certain an old maid like Mademoiselle Habert would make good progress toward salvation, increasing the priest’s influence, potentially converting Rogron and sending Pierrette to a convent. The two liberals, alarmed, saw in the priest and his sister adversaries as strong as themselves. Monsieur and Mademoiselle Habert visited the Rogrons nightly and could play whist and boston; their assiduity matched that of the colonel and Vinet. Each side suspected the other, and the salon turned into a battlefield, each wanting the whole prey.

The liberals made the first move. Vinet, pursuing self-interest, contacted Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf and her mother, living in poverty in Troyes on two thousand francs a year. Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, who believed in love-based marriage until twenty-five, was persuaded by Vinet’s promise that, joining means with his, she could live in Provins and marry the fool Rogron, taking her place in the best society.

Their arrival was a great reinforcement for the liberal party and a shock to Provins’ aristocrats and the Tiphaine clique. Madame de Breautey, horrified that two women of rank should be misled, begged them to come to her and railed against the selfishness degrading France—the consequence, she said, of materialism and the laws’ emphasis on money: nobility and beauty no longer mattered. Such creatures as the Rogrons, Vinets, could now contend with the King of France.

Bathilde de Chargeboeuf had not only beauty but dress; she was dazzlingly fair. At twenty-five her figure was developed; her throat, hair, smile, carriage, features, eyes, and motions were noble and high-bred. Her hands and feet were beautiful; health gave her perhaps too much the look of a handsome barmaid—“but that can’t be a defect in the eyes of a Rogron,” Madame Tiphaine sighed. Bathilde’s dress on first appearing at the Rogrons was simple: a brown merino gown edged with green embroidery, low-necked, with a tulle fichu carefully drawn down though opening a little in front, caught with a sevigne. She removed her velvet bonnet and shawl to reveal gold ear-drops and wore a black velvet ribbon with a heart—tricks of a girl seeking marriage. She entreated Rogron to fasten a cuff-button to show him her wrist; the dazzled fool refused, masking his emotions with indifference. Sylvie and Celeste Habert were deceived by his manner; Vinet was not.

The colonel, courting Sylvie as Bathilde courted Rogron, put on a clean shirt each evening, velvet stocks, a new blue surtout, a red rosette, even refrained from smoking. He adopted the air of a party leader preparing to drive out the Bourbons.

Vinet and the colonel played a crueler trick: they insinuated the priest sympathized with their ideas; soon Provins called him a liberal ecclesiastic. When the bishop heard, Monsieur Habert was summoned and admonished to stop visiting the Rogrons; his sister persisted. Thus the Rogron salon became established and powerful.

Before the year ended political intrigues matched the matrimonial plots. The Villele ministry fell in 1826; Vinet, the Liberal candidate who had borrowed money to buy a domain to qualify, nearly defeated Monsieur Tiphaine, who saved the election by two votes. The Rogrons’ salon was Liberal headquarters; habitués included notary Cournant and Doctor Neraud, whose stormy youth gave way to study and, Liberals claimed, more capability than the aristocratic Martener. The Rogrons themselves hardly understood their present triumph.

Bathilde, to whom Vinet had portrayed Pierrette as an enemy, was disdainful of the girl. It seemed everyone’s schemes demanded humiliation of that poor victim. Madame Vinet could do little for Pierrette, crushed as she was by her husband’s will. Pierrette often pressed against Madame Vinet as if divining her thoughts, asking to be shown a stitch or taught embroidery. Treated gently, she could learn and succeed marvellously. But after the arrival of Madame and Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, Madame Vinet ceased to visit the Rogrons.

Sylvie, now thinking of marrying, considered Pierrette an obstacle. Nearly fourteen, the pallid whiteness of her skin—symptomatic of illness overlooked by the ignorant old maid—made her exquisitely lovely. Sylvie thought to balance Pierrette’s cost by making her a servant. The habitués advised sending away Adele: why not have Pierrette keep the house and cook? If needed, Sylvie could employ the colonel’s woman-of-all-work. The lawyer counseled every girl should learn to keep house and know prices. Pierrette, generous and self-devoted, offered willingly, pleased to earn the bitter bread she ate. Adele was sent away, and Pierrette lost the only protector she had.

Crushed physically and mentally, she was treated with less consideration than a servant—she belonged to them. She was scolded for the smallest dust on a glass globe or mantelpiece. The ornaments she once admired became odious. No matter how she strove to do right, her cousins always found cause to reproach her. In two years she received no praise or kindly word; happiness was simply not being scolded. She bore with angelic patience the morose ill-humor of two celibates to whom tenderness was unknown and who daily made her feel her dependence.

Such life, pressed between two vise-like jaws, worsened her health. She began to feel violent internal distresses, secret pangs undermining her strength and arresting development. Slowly, through dreadful hidden sufferings, the poor child came to the state in which the companion of her childhood had found her when he sang the Breton ditty at dawn on that October day.

Before relating the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut’s coming provoked in the Rogron family, it is best to explain how the lad came to be in Provins; he is a somewhat mute personage on the scene.

When he ran from the house Brigaut was not only frightened by Pierrette’s gesture, he was horrified by the change he saw in his little friend. He could scarcely recognize the voice, the eyes, the gestures that had once been lively, gay, and tender. When he had gained some distance his legs trembled; hot flushes ran down his back. He had seen Pierrette’s shadow, but not herself. Climbing to the Upper town, he found a spot from which he could see the square and the house where Pierrette lived. He gazed at it mournfully, lost in thoughts that rent his heart, revealing to his own soul the extent of his love for his little adopted sister.

Passion between two children of opposite sexes is rare. The charming story of Paul and Virginia, like that of Pierrette and Brigaut, does not answer that strange moral fact. Modern history shows only a few such examples. Such was the sudden perfect affection in the Breton workman. Pierrette and he had often protected each other; he had blissfully given her money for her journey, had almost killed himself running after the diligence when she left. Pierrette had known nothing of this; for him, the recollection warmed his hard life. For Pierrette’s sake he had struggled to improve himself; he had learned his trade for her; he had come to Paris to make his fortune for her. After a fortnight in the city he could not resist seeing her, and he had walked from Saturday night to Monday morning. He intended to return to Paris, but the sight of his little friend nailed him to Provins. A wonderful, unconscious magnetism acted upon him. Tears rolled from his eyes when they rose in hers. If to her he was Brittany and childhood, to him she was life itself.

At sixteen Brigaut had not yet learned to draw or model a cornice; ignorant of much, he had nevertheless earned, by piece-work during his apprenticeship, some four or five francs a day. On this he could live in Provins and be near Pierrette; he chose the best cabinet-maker in town and meant to learn the rest by working for him, thus keeping watch over his darling.

Brigaut’s mind was made up. He went back to Paris, fetched his certificate, tools, and baggage, and three days later was a journeyman in the establishment of Monsieur Frappier, the best cabinet-maker in Provins. Active, steady workmen, not given to junketing, are rare, and masters hold to young men like Brigaut. By the end of the month he was made foreman and was fed and lodged by Frappier, who taught him arithmetic and line drawing. The house and shop were in the Grand’Rue, not a hundred feet from the little square where Pierrette lived.

Brigaut buried his love and committed no imprudence. He made Madame Frappier tell him all she knew about the Rogrons, including how their father had laid hands on the property of old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandfather. He obtained other information about the brother and sister. He met Pierrette sometimes in the market with her cousin and shuddered to see the heavy basket she carried. On Sundays he went to church to look for her, dressed in his best clothes. There, for the first time, he became aware that Pierrette was Mademoiselle Lorrain. Pierrette saw him and made a hasty sign to keep out of sight. To him there was a world in that little gesture, as there had been earlier in the sign from her window telling him to run away. Ah! what a fortune he must make in the coming ten years to marry his little friend, to whom, he was told, the Rogrons were to leave their house, a hundred acres of land, and twelve thousand francs a year, not counting their savings!

The persevering Breton set about thoroughly educating himself in his trade; for principles of the craft he could learn in Provins as well as Paris and thus remain near Pierrette, to whom he became anxious to explain his projects and the protection he could offer. He was determined to know the reason for her pallor and the debility beginning to show in the eyes; he would also discover the cause of the sufferings that made her look as if death might take her at any moment. The two signs—the gestures not denying friendship but imploring caution—alarmed him. Evidently Pierrette wished him to wait; otherwise there was danger. As she left the church she gave him one look, and he saw her eyes full of tears. But he could as soon have squared the circle as have guessed what had happened in the Rogrons’ house during the fortnight since his arrival.

It was not without keen apprehension that Pierrette came downstairs the morning after Brigaut had invaded her dreams. She thought Sylvie must have heard the song, or she would not have risen and opened her window; but Pierrette was ignorant of the powerful reasons that made the old maid so alert. For the last eight days strange events and bitter feelings had agitated the minds of the Rogron salon’s chief personages. These hidden matters, carefully concealed by all concerned, were destined to fall like an avalanche on Pierrette. Such secret schemes and calculations lie at the base of great revolutions, whether political, social, or domestic; their subtle significance cannot be rendered by a simple narrative. To set down all circumlocutions, oratorical precautions, protracted conversations, and honeyed words that gloss venom would make as long a book as Clarissa Harlowe.

Mademoiselle Habert and Mademoiselle Sylvie were equally desirous of marrying, but Celeste Habert was ten years older and could hope her children would inherit all the Rogron property. Sylvie was forty-two, an age at which marriage is perilous. In confiding to each other their ideas, Celeste, instigated by her vindictive brother the priest, enlightened Sylvie as to the risks. Sylvie trembled; she was terribly afraid of death, an idea that shakes all celibates. But at that time the Martignac ministry came to power—a Liberal victory overturning Villele. The Vinet party carried their heads high in Provins. Vinet himself became a personage, the Liberals prophesying his advancement to deputy and attorney-general. The colonel would be made mayor. Ah, to reign as Madame Garceland reigned! Sylvie could not resist that hope; she determined to consult a doctor, though it would cover her with ridicule. Consulting Monsieur Neraud, the Liberal physician and rival of Martener, would be a blunder. Celeste offered to hide Sylvie in her dressing-room while she herself consulted Monsieur Martener on this delicate matter. Whether Martener was Celeste’s accomplice need not be discovered; at any rate he told Celeste that even at thirty the danger, though slight, did exist. “But,” he added, “with your constitution, you need fear nothing.”

“But how about a woman over forty?” asked Celeste.

“A married woman who has had children has nothing to fear.”

“But I mean an unmarried woman, like Mademoiselle Rogron, for instance?”

“Oh, that’s another thing,” said Martener. “Successful childbirth is then one of those miracles God sometimes allows, but rarely.”

“Why?” asked Celeste.

The doctor gave a terrifying pathological account: the elasticity of youthful muscles and bones does not exist later, especially in sedentary women. “So you think an unmarried woman ought not to marry after forty?”

“Not unless she waits some years,” replied the doctor. “But then, of course, it is not marriage; it is only an association of interests.”

The interview’s clinical result was clear: an unmarried woman would make a great mistake marrying after forty. When the doctor departed Celeste found Sylvie in a frightful state, green and yellow, pupils dilated.

“Then you really love the colonel?” asked Celeste.

“I still hoped,” replied Sylvie.

“Well, then, wait!” cried Mademoiselle Habert, Jesuitically, aware that time would rid her of the colonel.

Sylvie’s new devotion to the church warned her that the morality of such a marriage might be doubtful. She sounded her conscience in the confessional. The stern priest explained Church opinions, which see marriage primarily as propagation and rebuke second marriages and all passion without social purpose. Sylvie’s perplexities were great. These internal struggles gave extraordinary force to her passion, investing it with that attraction which, from Eve’s day, the forbidden has for women. Mademoiselle Rogron’s perturbation did not escape the lynx-eyed lawyer.

One evening, after the game had ended, Vinet approached Sylvie, took her hand, and led her to a sofa.

“Something troubles you,” he said.

She nodded sadly. He let the others depart; Rogron walked home with the Chargeboeufs, and when Vinet was alone with the old maid he wormed the truth from her.

“Cleverly played, abbé!” he thought. “But you’ve played into my hands.”

The foxy lawyer, more decided even than the doctor, advised marriage in ten years. Inwardly he vowed the whole Rogron fortune should go to Bathilde. He rubbed his hands and hurried home. The influence exercised by Monsieur Habert, physician of the soul, and by Vinet, doctor of the purse, balanced each other. Rogron had no piety; the black-robed pair were fairly matched.

On discovering Celeste’s victory in her anxiety to marry Rogron over Sylvie, the lawyer saw his chance to drive the colonel from the field. He knew Rogron well enough to be certain he could marry him to Bathilde; Jerome had already inwardly succumbed to her charms, and Vinet knew the first time the pair were alone the marriage would be settled. Rogron had reached the point of keeping his eyes fixed on Celeste to avoid looking at Bathilde. Vinet had now learned Sylvie’s secrets and saw the force of her love for the colonel. He fully understood the struggle of such passion in an old maid gripped by religion and saw a way to rid himself of Pierrette and the colonel alike by making each the cause of the other’s overthrow.

The next day, after court, Vinet met the colonel and Rogron walking their daily walk. Whenever the three men were seen in company the town talked. This triumvirate, held in horror by the sub-prefect, the magistracy, and the Tiphaine clique, was a pride to the Liberals. Vinet, sole editor of the “Courrier,” was head of the party; the colonel, its arm; Rogron, by his purse, its nerves. The Tiphaines declared the three men always plotted evil to the government; the Liberals admired them as defenders of the people. When Rogron turned to go home, recalled by dinner-hour, Vinet stopped the colonel by taking Gouraud’s arm.

“Well, colonel,” he said, “I am going to take a fearful load off your shoulders; you can do better than marry Sylvie; if you play your cards properly you can marry that little Pierrette in two years’ time.”

He then related the Jesuit’s manoeuvre and its effect on Sylvie.

“What a skulking trick!” cried the colonel; “and spreading over years, too!”

“Colonel,” said Vinet gravely, “Pierrette is a charming creature; with her you can be happy for the rest of your life; your health is so sound that the difference in ages won't seem disproportionate. But you mustn’t think it easy to change a dreadful fate to a pleasant one. To turn a woman who loves you into a friend and confidant is as perilous as crossing a river under fire. You must study the position and manoeuvre your forces with the same wisdom you have displayed and which has won us our present position. If I get to be attorney-general you shall command the department. Oh! if you had been an elector we should be further advanced; I would have bought votes by threatening men with the loss of their places, and we should have had a majority.”

The colonel had long thought about Pierrette but concealed his feelings with utmost dissimulation. His roughness to the child was only a mask. She could not understand why the man who claimed to be her father’s old comrade usually treated her so ill when sometimes, if alone with her, he would chuck her under the chin and give a friendly kiss. After Vinet’s conversation about Sylvie’s fears, Gouraud began to seek opportunities to find Pierrette alone; the rough colonel made himself as soft as a cat. He told her how brave her father was and what a misfortune it had been that she lost him.

A few days before Brigaut’s arrival Sylvie had suddenly come upon Gouraud and Pierrette talking together. Jealousy rushed into her heart with monastic violence. Jealousy, credulous and suspicious, gives fancy freedom but not intelligence; it filled her with fantastic ideas. When she heard Brigaut’s ditty a few mornings later she jumped to the conclusion that the man who had used the words “Madam’ le mariee,” addressing them to Pierrette, must be the colonel. She was certain, for she had noticed a change in his manners for a week. He was the only man who, in her solitary life, had ever paid her attention. Consequently she watched him with all her eyes and mind; by hope and despair she brought the matter to enormous proportions and saw all things in a mental mirage. By dint of staring she saw nothing. Alternately she repelled, admitted, and conquered the idea of rivalry. She compared herself with Pierrette: forty-two with gray hair, versus Pierrette delicately fair with soft eyes. She had heard men of fifty apt to love young girls of that kind. Before the colonel came to the house Sylvie had heard in the Tiphaines’ salon strange stories of his life and morals. Old maids preserve exaggerated Platonic sentiments; they hold to fixed doctrines like those with little experience of life. The mere thought of being jilted by the colonel was torture to Sylvie. She lay in bed going over her desires, Pierrette’s conduct, and the song that had awakened her with the word “marriage.” Foolishly, instead of looking through the blinds to see the lover, she opened her window without reflecting that Pierrette would hear her. If she had had the instinct of a spy she would have seen Brigaut, and the fatal drama begun would never have taken place.

It was Pierrette’s duty, weak as she was, to unbar and open the wooden shutters of the kitchen, then the glass door from corridor to garden. She fetched various brooms and utensils with a care and particularity no servant, not even a Dutchwoman, gives her work. She hated reproof. Happiness for her was seeing her cousin’s cold blue eyes—not satisfied but calm—after glancing about with the look of an owner. Pierrette’s skin was moist with labor when she returned to put the kitchen in order and light the stove so she might carry up hot water to her two cousins (a luxury she never had), and to prepare the dining-room fire. For these she fetched wood from the cellar, leaving warm rooms for damp air; such sudden transitions, made quickly to escape harsh words, aggravated her condition. She did not know she was ill, yet she suffered. She began to have strange cravings; she liked raw vegetables and salads and ate them secretly. The innocent child was unaware her condition was serious and required utmost care. If Neraud, the Rogrons’ doctor, had told this to Pierrette before Brigaut’s coming she would have only smiled; life was so bitter she could smile at death. But now her feelings changed; the child, whose physical suffering was increased by Breton homesickness, was suddenly content to be in Provins. The sight of that yellow flower, the song, the presence of her friend revived her like rain on a parched plant. Unconsciously she wanted to live and even thought she did not suffer.

Pierrette slipped timidly into her cousin’s bedroom, made the fire, left the hot water, said a few words, and went to wake Rogron and do the same for him. Then she went down to take in the milk, bread, and other provisions. She stood some time on the door-sill hoping Brigaut would come; by then he was already on his way to Paris.

She had finished arranging the dining-room and was busy in the kitchen when she heard Sylvie coming down. Mademoiselle Rogron appeared in a brown silk dressing-gown with a cap, false front awry, night-gown showing above the silk, slippers down at heel. She cast an eye about and came straight to Pierrette, who awaited orders.

“Ha! here you are, lovesick young lady!” said Sylvie mockingly.

“What is it, cousin?”

“You came into my room like a sly cat and crept out the same way, though you knew I had something to say to you.”

“To me?”

“You had a serenade this morning, as if you were a princess.”

“A serenade!” exclaimed Pierrette.

“A serenade!” mimicked Sylvie. “And you’ve a lover, too.”

“What is a lover, cousin?”

Sylvie avoided answering. “Do you dare tell me a man did not come under your window and talk to you of marriage?”

Persecution had taught Pierrette the wariness of slaves; she answered bravely: “I don’t know what you mean—”

“Who means?—your dog?” said Sylvie sharply.

“I should have said ‘cousin,’” replied the girl humbly.

“And didn’t you get up and go in your bare feet to the window?—which will give you an illness; and perhaps you didn’t talk to your lover, either?”

“No, cousin.”

“You have many faults, but I did not think you told lies. You will have to explain this affair to your cousin and to me, or I shall take severe measures.”

The old maid, exasperated with jealousy and curiosity, meant to frighten the girl. Pierrette, who endured more than she had strength for, kept silence. Silence is the weapon by which such victims sometimes conquer; it baffles envy’s charges and suspicion’s skirmishings and is absolute. Sylvie watched Pierrette narrowly. The girl colored, but the color came out in patches on her cheekbones—burning and significant spots. A mother would have changed tone at once, taken the child on her lap, and questioned her; she would have seen the signs of weakness and known that digestive disturbance and other infirmities threatened the lungs. But an old maid who never awakened family instincts or knew the precautions required for adolescence had neither indulgence nor the compassionate intelligence of a mother; Pierrette’s sufferings only hardened her.

“She blushes, she is guilty!” thought Sylvie.

Pierrette’s silence thus was turned to her injury.

“Pierrette,” Sylvie continued, “before your cousin comes down we must have some talk together. Come,” she said softer, “shut the street door; if any one comes they will ring and we shall hear them.”

In spite of the damp mist rising from the river, Sylvie took Pierrette along the winding gravel path across the lawn to the rock terrace—an ornamental quay covered with iris and aquatic plants. She changed tactics, thinking firmness might catch Pierrette tripping; the hyena became a cat.

“Pierrette,” she said, “you are no longer a child; you are nearly fifteen; it is not surprising you should have a lover.”

“But, cousin,” said Pierrette, raising her eyes with angelic sweetness to Sylvie’s cold face, “what is a lover?”

It would have been impossible for Sylvie to define a lover truthfully and decently. Instead of seeing adorable innocence, she considered the question insincere.

“A lover, Pierrette, is a man who loves us and wishes to marry us.”

“Ah,” said Pierrette, “when that happens in Brittany we call the young man a suitor.”

“Well, remember that in owning your feelings for a man you do no wrong, my dear. The wrong is in hiding them. Have you pleased some of the men who visit here?”

“No.”

“Do you love any of them?”

“No.”

“Certain?”

“Quite certain.”

“Look at me, Pierrette.”

Pierrette looked.

“A man called to you this morning in the square.”

Pierrette lowered her eyes.

“You went to your window, you opened it, and you spoke to him.”

“No cousin, I went to look out and I saw a peasant.”

“Pierrette, you have much improved since your first communion; you have become pious and obedient, you love God and your relations; I am satisfied with you. I don’t say this to puff you up with pride.”

The horrible creature had mistaken submission and the silence of wretchedness for virtues.

Pierrette raised grateful eyes to her cousin, feeling she could almost forgive the suffering she had caused.

“But if it is all hypocrisy, if I find you a serpent that I have warmed in my bosom, you will be a wicked girl, an infamous creature!”

“I think I have nothing to reproach myself with,” said Pierrette, painfully shocked by the sudden change from praise to hyena tones.

“You know that to lie is a mortal sin?”

“Yes, cousin.”

“Well, you are now under the eye of God,” said the old maid, with a solemn gesture to the sky; “swear to me that you did not know that peasant.”

“I will not swear,” said Pierrette.

“Ha! he was no peasant, you little viper.”

Pierrette rushed away like a frightened fawn. Sylvie called her in a dreadful voice.

“The bell is ringing,” she answered.

“Artful wretch!” thought Sylvie. “She is depraved in mind; now I am certain the little adder has wound herself round the colonel. She has heard us say he was a baron. To be a baroness! little fool! Ah! I’ll get rid of her; I’ll apprentice her out, and soon too!”

Sylvie was so lost in thought she did not notice her brother coming down the path lamenting the frost’s injury to his dahlias.

“Sylvie! what are you thinking about? I thought you were looking at the fish; sometimes they jump out of the water.”

“No,” said Sylvie.

How did you sleep?” he began to tell his dreams. “Don’t you think my skin is getting tabid?”—a Rogron word.

Ever since Rogron had desired to marry Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf he had been uneasy about himself and his health. At this moment Pierrette came down and called that breakfast was ready. At sight of her, Sylvie’s skin turned green and yellow; her bile was in commotion. She looked at the corridor floor and declared Pierrette ought to rub it.

“I will rub it now if you wish,” said the little angel, unaware of the injury such work may do a girl.

The dining-room was irreproachably in order. Sylvie pretended through breakfast to want this and that—which she would never have thought to ask in a quieter mood—only to make Pierrette rise again and again just as she began to eat. But mere teasing was not enough; she wanted fault and was angry with herself for not finding one. She scarcely answered her brother’s remarks yet kept her eyes on him and avoided Pierrette. Pierrette, deeply conscious, brought milk mixed with cream in a large silver goblet after heating it in a bain-marie. The cousins poured coffee. When Sylvie carefully prepared hers she saw an atom of coffee-grounds floating on the surface. On this the storm broke.

“What is the matter?” asked Rogron.

“The matter is that mademoiselle has put dust in my milk. Do you suppose I am going to drink coffee with ashes in it? Well, I am not surprised; no one can do two things at once. She wasn’t thinking of the milk! a blackbird might have flown through the kitchen today and she wouldn’t have seen it! and then it was my coffee, ha! that didn’t signify!”

As she spoke she laid aside the coffee-grounds that had run through the filter.

“But, cousin, that is coffee,” said Pierrette.

“Oh! then it is I who tell lies, is it?” cried Sylvie, blasting her with a fearful flash from her eyes.

Organizations not exhausted by emotion often have much vital fluid at their service. This extreme clearness of eye in anger was marked in Mademoiselle Rogron because she often exercised it in her shop to inspire dependents with fear.

“You had better dare to give me the lie!” continued Sylvie; “you deserve to be sent from the table to eat by yourself in the kitchen.”

“What’s the matter with you two?” cried Rogron, “you are as cross as bears this morning.”

“Mademoiselle knows what I have against her,” said Sylvie. “I leave her to make up her mind before speaking to you; for I mean to show her more kindness than she deserves.”

Pierrette looked out the window to avoid her cousin’s eyes, which frightened her. “Look at her! she pays no attention to what I am saying than if I were that sugar-basin! And yet mademoiselle has a sharp ear; she can hear and answer from the top of the house when some one talks to her from below. She is perversity itself,—perversity, I say; and you needn’t expect any good of her; do you hear me, Jerome?”

“What has she done wrong?” asked Rogron.

“At her age, too! to begin so young!” screamed Sylvie.

Pierrette rose to clear the table to give herself something to do; she could hardly bear the scene. Though such language was not new, she had never grown used to it. Her cousin’s rage seemed to accuse her of a crime. She imagined what fury would be if they knew about Brigaut; perhaps they would send him away and she would lose him. All the rapid thoughts of a slave came to her, and she resolved to keep absolute silence about a circumstance her conscience told her was innocent. But the cruel words and wounding suspicion so shocked her that as she reached the kitchen she was taken with a convulsion of the stomach and turned deadly sick. She dared not complain; she was not sure anyone would help her. When she returned to the dining-room she was white as a sheet and, saying she was not well, went to bed, dragging herself up step by step by the baluster and thinking she was going to die. “Poor Brigaut!” she thought.

“The girl is ill,” said Rogron.

“She ill! That’s only shamming,” replied Sylvie loudly that Pierrette might hear. “She was well enough this morning, I can tell you.”

This last blow struck Pierrette to the earth; she went to bed weeping and praying to God to take her out of this world.

For a month past Rogron had ceased to carry the “Constitutionnel” to Gouraud; the colonel came obsequiously to fetch his paper, gossip a little, and take Rogron off to walk if the weather was fine. Sure of seeing the colonel and able to question him, Sylvie dressed as coquettishly as she could. She fancied herself attractive in a green gown, a yellow shawl with a red border, and a white bonnet with straggling gray feathers. About the hour when the colonel usually came Sylvie stationed herself in the salon with her brother, whom she kept at home in dressing-gown and slippers.

“It is a fine day, colonel,” said Rogron, when Gouraud with his heavy step entered the room. “But I’m not dressed; my sister wanted to go out, and I was going to keep the house. Wait for me; I’ll be ready soon.”

So saying, Rogron left Sylvie alone with the colonel.

“Where were you going? you are dressed divinely,” said Gouraud, noticing a certain solemnity on the pock-marked face of the old maid.

“I wanted very much to go out, but my little cousin is ill, and I cannot leave her.”

“What is the matter with her?”

“I don’t know; she had to go to bed.”

Gouraud’s distrust was constantly excited by the results of his alliance with Vinet. Vinet controlled the paper, reigned as master, and took the revenues; the colonel, the responsible editor, earned little. Vinet and Cournant had done the Rogrons great services; Gouraud, a half-pay colonel, could do nothing. Who was to be deputy, chief authority, or consulted by the liberals? Vinet. Moreover, the colonel knew as well as Vinet the extent of Rogron’s passion for the beautiful Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, now intense; Bathilde’s voice made him tremble. Absorbed, Rogron hid his desires and dared not hope. When the colonel hinted he might ask for her hand, Rogron turned pale and since then had shown coldness and even hatred to Gouraud.

Thus Vinet reigned supreme in the Rogron household while the colonel had no hold there except by the hypothetical tie of his mendacious affection for Sylvie, whose feelings were unclear. When the lawyer told him of the priest’s manoeuvre and advised breaking with Sylvie to marry Pierrette, he flattered Gouraud’s foible; but after analyzing the advice, the colonel suspected his ally intended to separate him from Sylvie and, by exploiting her fears, throw the Rogron property into Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf’s hands.

Therefore, when left alone with Sylvie the colonel’s perspicacity immediately caught signs of her uneasiness. He saw she was on guard and had planned to see him alone. Suspecting Vinet of a trick, he attributed the conference to the lawyer and went instantly on guard, like in enemy territory—alert, listening, hand ready. The colonel never believed a woman’s word; when Sylvie said Pierrette had gone to bed before midday, he concluded Sylvie had locked her up in jealousy.

“She is getting to be quite pretty, that little thing,” he said with an easy air.

“She will be pretty,” replied Mademoiselle Rogron.

“You ought to send her to Paris and put her in a shop,” continued the colonel. “She would make her fortune. The milliners all want pretty girls.”

“Is that really your advice?” asked Sylvie, in a troubled voice.

“Good!” thought the colonel. “I was right. Vinet advised me to marry Pierrette just to spoil my chance with the old harridan. But,” he said aloud, “what else can you do with her? There’s that beautiful girl Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, noble and well-connected, reduced to single-blessedness—nobody will have her. Pierrette has nothing, and she’ll never marry. As for beauty, what is it? To me, for example, youth and beauty are nothing; haven’t I been a captain of cavalry in the imperial guard, carried my spurs into all the capitals of Europe, and known all the handsomest women of those cities? Don’t talk to me; youth and beauty are common and silly. At forty-eight,” he added a few years to match Sylvie’s, “after surviving the retreat from Moscow and that terrible campaign of France, a man is broken down; I’m nothing but an old fellow now. A woman like you would pet me and care for me, and her money, joined to my poor pension, would give me ease in my old days. I should prefer such a woman to a little minx who would worry me, thirty when I should be sixty with rheumatism. At my age a man calculates. Between ourselves, I should not wish to have children.”

Sylvie’s face was an open book to the colonel during this tirade, and her next question proved Vinet’s perfidy.

“Then you don’t love Pierrette?” she said.

“Heavens! are you out of your mind, my dear Sylvie?” he cried. “Can those who have no teeth crack nuts? Thank God I’ve got some common-sense and know what I’m about.”

Sylvie thus reassured resolved not to show her own hand, and thought herself very shrewd in putting her own ideas into her brother’s mouth.

“Jerome,” she said, “thought of the match.”

“How could your brother take up such an incongruous idea? Why, it is only a few days ago that, in order to find out his secrets, I told him I loved Bathilde. He turned as white as your collar.”

“My brother! does he love Bathilde?” asked Sylvie.

“Madly,—and yet Bathilde is only after his money.” (“One for you, Vinet!” thought the colonel.) “I can’t understand why he should have told you that about Pierrette. No, Sylvie,” he said, taking her hand and pressing it in a certain way, “since you have opened this matter” (he drew nearer to her), “well” (he kissed her hand), “let me tell you that I desire no wife but you. Though such a marriage may look like one of convenience, I feel, on my side, a sincere affection for you.”

“But if I wish you to marry Pierrette? if I leave her my fortune--eh, colonel?”

“But I don’t want to be miserable in my home, and in less than ten years see a popinjay like Julliard hovering round my wife and addressing verses to her in the newspapers. I’m too much of a man to stand that. No, I will never make a marriage that is disproportionate in age.”

“Well, colonel, we will talk seriously of this another time,” said Sylvie, casting a glance upon him which she supposed to be full of love, though, in point of fact, it was a good deal like that of an ogress. Her cold, blue lips of a violet tinge drew back from the yellow teeth, and she thought she smiled.

“I’m ready,” said Rogron, coming in and carrying off the colonel, who bowed in a lover-like way to the old maid.

Gouraud determined to press on his marriage with Sylvie, and make himself master of the house; resolving to rid himself, through his influence over Sylvie during the honeymoon, of Bathilde and Celeste Habert. So, during their walk, he told Rogron he had been joking the other day; that he had no real intention of aspiring to Bathilde; that he was not rich enough to marry a woman without fortune; and then he confided to him his real wishes, declaring that he had long chosen Sylvie for her good qualities,—in short, he aspired to the honor of being Rogron’s brother-in-law.

“Ah, colonel, my dear baron! if nothing is wanting but my consent you have it with no further delay than the law requires,” cried Rogron, delighted to be rid of his formidable rival.

Sylvie spent the morning in her room considering how the new household could be arranged: to build a second storey for her brother and furnish the rest for herself and her husband; but, true to old-maid suspicion, she resolved to test the colonel’s heart and morals and ensure Pierrette had no private intercourse with him. She was still suspicious, and wanted to make sure that Pierrette had no private intercourse with the colonel.

Pierrette came down before the dinner-hour to lay the table. Sylvie, forced to cook the dinner, had sworn at that “cursed Pierrette” for a spot she had made on her gown,—wasn’t it plain that if Pierrette had done her own work Sylvie wouldn’t have got that grease-spot on her silk dress?

“Oh, here you are, peakling? You are like the dog of the marshal who woke up as soon as the saucepans rattled. Ha! you want us to think you are ill, you little liar!”

That idea—“You did not tell the truth about what happened in the square this morning, therefore you lie in everything”—was a hammer with which Sylvie battered the head and heart of the poor girl incessantly.

To Pierrette’s astonishment Sylvie sent her to dress in her best clothes after dinner. Suspicion can make an old maid invent endlessly; Sylvie determined to consult Vinet after searching for evidence, and kept Pierrette near to judge by her face whether the colonel had told the truth.

On this particular evening the Chargeboeuf ladies were the first to arrive. At Vinet’s advice Bathilde dressed more elaborately in blue velveteen, a transparent fichu, garnet pendants, ringlets, a coquettish jeannette at her throat, black satin slippers, gray silk stockings, and suede gloves, with queenly manners and girlish coquetry aimed at Rogron. Her mother, calm and dignified, shared an aristocratic insolence that preserved their caste spirit. Bathilde was intelligent, a fact Vinet had discovered during the ladies’ two-month stay at his house. When he had fathomed the mind of the girl, wounded by the fruitlessness of her beauty and youth and contemptuous of an age that worshipped money, Vinet exclaimed—

“If I could only have married you, Bathilde, I should to-day be Keeper of the Seals. I should call myself Vinet de Chargeboeuf, and take my seat as deputy of the Right.”

Bathilde had no vulgar idea in her marriage intentions. She did not marry to be a mother or merely to possess a husband; she married for freedom, to gain a responsible position, to be called “madame,” and to act as men act. Rogron was nothing but a name to her; she expected to make something of the fool—a voting deputy whom she would instigate; moreover, she longed to avenge herself on her family, who had taken no notice of a girl without money. Vinet had enlarged and strengthened her ideas by admiring and approving them.

“My dear Bathilde,” he said, explaining women’s influence and the sphere in which she ought to work, “do you suppose that Tiphaine, a man of the most ordinary capacity, could ever get to be a judge of the Royal court in Paris by himself? No, it is Madame Tiphaine who has got him elected deputy, and it is she who will push him when they get to Paris. Her mother, Madame Roguin, is a shrewd woman, who does what she likes with the famous banker du Tillet, a crony of Nucingen, and both of them allies of the Kellers. The administration is on the best of terms with those lynxes of the bank. There is no reason why Tiphaine should not be judge, through his wife, of a Royal court. Marry Rogron; we’ll have him elected deputy from Provins as soon as I gain another precinct in the Seine-et-Marne. You can then get him a place as receiver-general, where he’ll have nothing to do but sign his name. We shall belong to the opposition if the Liberals triumph, but if the Bourbons remain—ah! then we shall lean gently, gently towards the centre. Besides, you must remember Rogron can’t live forever, and then you can marry a titled man. In short, put yourself in a good position, and the Chargeboeufs will be ready enough to serve us. Your poverty has no doubt taught you, as mine did me, to know what men are worth. We must make use of them as we do of post-horses. A man, or a woman, will take us along to such or such a distance.”

Vinet ended by making Bathilde a small edition of Catherine de Medicis. He left his wife at home and every night visited the Rogrons with Madame and Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, arriving in the trappings of success: gold spectacles, a silk waistcoat, a white cravat, black coat, thin boots, and a gold watch. In place of the former pale, snarling Vinet, the present Vinet bore himself with the air and manner of a man of importance, certain of success, with the peculiar show of security belonging to lawyers who know the hidden places of the law. His sly little head was well-brushed, his chin well-shaved, giving him a mincing though frigid look that made him agreeable in a style akin to Robespierre. The bitterness and hatred that formerly actuated him had now turned into soft-spoken perfidy; the poison was transformed into anodyne.

“Good-evening, my dear; how are you?” said Madame de Chargeboeuf, greeting Sylvie.

Bathilde went straight to the fireplace, took off her bonnet, looked at herself in the glass, and placed her pretty foot on the fender that Rogron might admire it.

“What is the matter with you?” she said to him, looking directly in his face. “You have not bowed to me. Pray why should we put on our best velvet gowns to please you?”

She pushed past Pierrette to lay down her hat, which the latter took from her hand and held exactly as though she were a servant. No beast equals the gentle cruelty and disdain of a young woman who thinks herself superior in birth or fortune. The “Thank you, mademoiselle,” which Bathilde said to Pierrette was a poem in many strophes. She was named Bathilde, the other Pierrette. She was a Chargeboeuf, the other a Lorrain. Pierrette was small and weak, Bathilde tall and full of life. Pierrette lived on charity, Bathilde on her means. Pierrette wore a stuff gown with a chemisette, Bathilde made velvet undulate. Pierrette had skin and bone shoulders, Bathilde the finest shoulders in the department. Pierrette was Cinderella, Bathilde the fairy. Pierrette was to die a maid, Bathilde to be adored. The proud little Breton girl understood this tragic poem.

“Good-evening, little girl,” said Madame de Chargeboeuf, in the tone of condescending grandeur which her pinched nose gave her.

Vinet put the last touch to this sort of insult by looking fixedly at Pierrette and saying, in three keys, “Oh! oh! oh! how fine we are to-night, Pierrette!”

“Fine!” said the poor child; “you should say that to Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, not to me.”

“Oh! she is always beautifully dressed,” replied the lawyer. “Isn’t she, Rogron?” he added, turning to the master of the house, and grasping his hand.

“Yes,” said Rogron.

“Why do you force him to say what he does not think?” said Bathilde; “nothing about me pleases him. Isn’t that true?” she added, going up to Rogron and standing before him. “Look at me, and say if it isn’t true.”

Rogron looked at her from head to foot, and gently closed his eyes like a cat whose head is being scratched.

“You are too beautiful,” he said; “too dangerous.”

“Why?”

Rogron looked at the fire and was silent. Just then Mademoiselle Habert entered the room, followed by the colonel.

Celeste Habert, now the common enemy, could only reckon Sylvie on her side; nevertheless, everybody present showed her the more civility and amiable attention because each was undermining her. Her brother, though no longer able to be on the scene of action, was well aware of what was going on, and as soon as he perceived that his sister’s hopes were killed he became an implacable and terrible antagonist to the Rogrons.

Every one will immediately picture Mademoiselle Habert when they know that if she had not kept an institution for young ladies she would still have had the air of a school-mistress. School-mistresses have a way of their own in putting on their caps. Just as old Englishwomen have acquired a monopoly in turbans, school-mistresses have a monopoly of these caps. Flowers nod above the framework, flowers that are more than artificial; lying by in closets for years the cap is both new and old, even on the day it is first worn. These spinsters make it a point of honor to resemble the lay figures of a painter; they sit on their hips, never on their chairs. When any one speaks to them they turn their whole busts instead of simply turning their heads; and when their gowns creak one is tempted to believe that the mechanism of these beings is out of order. Mademoiselle Habert, an ideal of her species, had a stern eye, a grim mouth, and beneath her wrinkled chin the strings of her cap, always limp and faded, floated as she moved. Two moles, rather large and brown, adorned that chin, and from them sprouted hairs which she allowed to grow rampant like clematis. And finally, to complete her portrait, she took snuff, and took it ungracefully.

The company went to work at their boston. Mademoiselle Habert sat opposite Sylvie, with the colonel at her side opposite Madame de Chargeboeuf. Bathilde was near her mother and Rogron. Sylvie placed Pierrette between herself and the colonel; Rogron had set out a second card-table, in case other company arrived. Two lamps were on the chimney-piece between the candelabra and the clock, and the tables were lighted by candles at forty sous a pound, paid for by the price of the cards.

“Come, Pierrette, take your work, my dear,” said Sylvie, with treacherous softness, noticing that the girl was watching the colonel’s game.

She usually affected to treat Pierrette well before company. This deception irritated the honest Breton girl, and made her despise her cousin. She took her embroidery, but as she drew her stitches she still watched Gouraud’s play. Gouraud behaved as if he did not know the girl was near him. Sylvie noticed this apparent indifference and thought it extremely suspicious. Presently she undertook a grande misere in hearts, the pool being full of counters, besides containing twenty-seven sous. The rest of the company had now arrived; among them the deputy-judge Desfondrilles, who for the last two months had abandoned the Tiphaine party and connected himself more or less with the Vinets. He was standing before the chimney-piece, with his back to the fire and the tails of his coat over his arms, looking round the fine salon of which Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf was the shining ornament; for it really seemed as if all the reds of its decoration had been made expressly to enhance her style of beauty. Silence reigned; Pierrette was watching the game, Sylvie’s attention was distracted from her by the interest of the grande misere.

“Play that,” said Pierrette to the colonel, pointing to a heart in his hand.

The colonel began a sequence in hearts; the hearts all lay between himself and Sylvie; the colonel won her ace, though it was protected by five small hearts.

“That’s not fair!” she cried. “Pierrette saw my hand, and the colonel took her advice.”

“But, mademoiselle,” said Celeste, “it was the colonel’s game to play hearts after you began them.”

The scene made Monsieur Desfondrilles smile; his was a keen mind, which found much amusement in watching the play of all the self-interests in Provins.

“Yes, it was certainly the colonel’s game,” said Cournant the notary, not knowing what the question was.

Sylvie threw a look at Mademoiselle Habert,—one of those glances which pass from old maid to old maid, feline and cruel.

“Pierrette, you did see my hand,” said Sylvie fixing her eyes on the girl.

“No, cousin.”

“I was looking at you all,” said the deputy-judge, “and I can swear that Pierrette saw no one’s hand but the colonel’s.”

“Pooh!” said Gouraud, alarmed, “little girls know how to slide their eyes into everything.”

“Ah!” exclaimed Sylvie.

“Yes,” continued Gouraud. “I dare say she looked into your hand to play you a trick. Didn’t you, little one?”

“No,” said the truthful Breton, “I wouldn’t do such a thing; if I had, it would have been in my cousin’s interests.”

“You know you are a story-teller and a little fool,” cried Sylvie. “After what happened this morning do you suppose I can believe a word you say? You are a—”

Pierrette did not wait for Sylvie to finish her sentence; foreseeing a torrent of insults, she rushed away without a light and ran to her room. Sylvie turned white with anger and muttered between her teeth, “She shall pay for this!”

“Shall you pay for the misere?” said Madame de Chargeboeuf.

As she spoke Pierrette struck her head against the door of the passage which some one had left open.

“Good! I’m glad of it,” cried Sylvie, as they heard the blow.

“She must be hurt,” said Desfondrilles.

“She deserves it,” replied Sylvie.

“It was a bad blow,” said Mademoiselle Habert.

Sylvie thought she might escape paying her misere if she went to see after Pierrette, but Madame de Chargeboeuf stopped her.

“Pay us first,” she said, laughing; “you will forget it when you come back.”

The remark, based on the old maid’s trickery and her bad faith in paying her debts at cards, was approved by the others. Sylvie sat down and thought no more of Pierrette,—an indifference which surprised no one. When the game was over, about half past nine o’clock, she flung herself into an easy chair at the corner of the fireplace and did not even rise as her guests departed. The colonel was torturing her; she did not know what to think of him.

“Men are so false!” she cried, as she went to bed.

Pierrette had given herself a frightful blow on the head, just above the ear, at the spot where young girls part their hair when they put their “front hair” in curlpapers. The next day there was a large swelling.

“God has punished you,” said Sylvie at the breakfast table. “You disobeyed me; you treated me with disrespect in leaving the room before I had finished my sentence; you got what you deserved.”

“Nevertheless,” said Rogron, “she ought to put on a compress of salt and water.”

“Oh, it is nothing at all, cousin,” said Pierrette.

The poor child had reached a point where even such a remark seemed to her a proof of kindness.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The week ended as it had begun: in continual torture. Sylvie grew inventive, finding refinements of tyranny with savage cruelty. Pierrette dared not complain of her vague sufferings or the real pains in her head; with Breton obstinacy she kept silence. Her thoughts turned to Brigaut, fearing danger for him if he were discovered, yet longing to have him near and joyful that he was in Provins. That single glimpse of him had sustained her like an exile’s look at his country or a martyr’s lift to heaven.

The major’s son had understood Pierrette’s look; as he planed planks or took his measures he sought a way to communicate. He chose a simple plan: at a certain hour Pierrette must lower a letter by a string from her window. In the midst of her sufferings she was sustained by the hope of communicating with Brigaut. At every shock to her heart she said to herself, “Brigaut is here!” and that thought enabled her to live without complaint.

One morning in the market Brigaut, lying in wait, got near her. Though he saw her tremble and turn pale, he kept his head, calmly bought fruit of the market-woman with whom Sylvie was bargaining, and managed to slip a note to Pierrette while joking with the woman as one accustomed to such manoeuvres. Cool in action though his blood hummed, he had firmness without roughness and the hesitation of innocence within him.

Pierrette’s inward commotion matched Brigaut’s. She slipped the note into her apron pocket; the hectic spots on her cheekbones turned cherry-scarlet. These two children felt more than ordinary loves; that moment in the market-place left in their souls a well-spring of passion. Sylvie, who did not recognize the Breton accent, took no notice, and Pierrette went home safely with her treasure.

Their letters, later to become documents in a terrible inquiry, began like this. Pierrette read at night:

My dear Pierrette,—At midnight, when everybody is asleep but me, who am watching you, I will come every night under your window. Let down a string long enough to reach me; it will not make any noise; you must fasten to the end of it whatever you write. I will tie my letter the same way. I hear they have taught you to read and write—those wicked relations who were to do you good, and have done you so much harm. You, Pierrette, daughter of a colonel who died for France, reduced by those monsters to be their servant! That is where your pretty color and health have gone. My Pierrette, what has become of her? why are you not the same, not happy? Oh! Pierrette, let us go back to Brittany. I can earn enough now; you yourself can earn three francs a day and I four or five; thirty sous is all I want to live on. I have a hundred and fifty francs; I can send them up by the string. Ah, dear Pierrette, it is a long time since I have had two hours’ happiness. When I saw you the other morning, looking like a shadow, I could not reach you; that hag of a cousin came between us. At least we can pray together every Sunday; perhaps God will hear us all the more when we pray together.

Not good-by, my dear Pierrette, but to-night.

This so affected Pierrette that she sat for more than an hour reading and re-reading and gazing at it. Then she remembered, with anguish, that she had nothing to write with. Summoning courage she made the difficult journey from her garret to the dining-room, obtained pen, paper, and ink, and returned without waking her terrible cousin. A few minutes before midnight she finished the following letter:

My Friend,—Oh! yes, my friend; for there is no one but you, Jacques, and my grandmother to love me. God forgive me, but you are the only two I love. I was too little to know my dear mamma; but you, Jacques, and my grandmother and my grandfather—God grant him heaven, for he suffered much from his ruin—are the two who are left; I love you both. To know how much I love you you must know how much I suffer; but I don’t wish that, it would grieve you. They speak to me as we would not speak to a dog; they treat me like the worst of girls; yet I do examine myself before God and cannot find that I do wrong by them. Before you sang the marriage song I saw the mercy of God in my sufferings; I had prayed him to take me from the world, and I felt so ill I said to myself, “God hears me!” But, Jacques, now you are here, I want to live and go back to Brittany, to my grandmamma who loves me, though they say she stole eight thousand francs of mine. Jacques, is that so? If they are mine could you get them? But it is not true, for if my grandmother had eight thousand francs she would not live at Saint-Jacques.

I don’t want to trouble my kind, good grandmamma with the knowledge of my troubles; she might die of it. Ah! if she knew they made her grandchild scrub pots and pans—she who used to say to me, “Don’t touch that, my darling; you will spoil your pretty fingers.” Ah! my hands are never clean now. Sometimes I can hardly carry the basket from market; it cuts my arm. Still I don’t think my cousins mean to be cruel; it is their way always to scold, and it seems I have no right to leave them. My cousin Rogron is my guardian. One day when I wanted to run away because I could not bear it and told them so, my cousin Sylvie said the gendarmes would go after me, for the law was my master. Oh! I know now that cousins cannot take the place of father or mother.

My poor Jacques, what would I do with your money? Keep it for our journey. Oh! how I think of you and Pen-Hoel, and the big pond—where we had our only happy days. I shall have no more, for I feel I am going from bad to worse. I am very ill, Jacques. I have dreadful pains in my head, and in my bones and back, which kill me, and I have no appetite except for horrid things—roots and leaves. Sometimes I cry when I am alone, for they won’t let me do anything I like if they know it, not even cry. I hide to offer my tears to Him to whom we owe the mercies we call afflictions. It must have been He who gave you the blessed thought to come and sing the marriage song beneath my window. Ah! Jacques, my cousin heard you, and she said I had a lover. If you wish to be my lover, love me well. I promise to love you always, as I did in the past, and to be

Your faithful servant,
Pierrette Lorrain.

You will love me always, won’t you?

She brought a crust of bread from the kitchen, made a hole for the letter, and fastened it as a weight to her string. At midnight she opened the window cautiously and lowered the letter; Brigaut pulled the string, broke it, and crept softly away. In the starlight she could just see him; he saw her in the candlelight. They stood thus over an hour—she making him sign to go, he starting, she remaining—until she closed the window, went to bed, and blew out the candle. Once in bed she fell asleep, happy in heart though suffering in body, with Brigaut’s letter under her pillow.

Next day Pierrette rose light and joyous as a lark. Such change did not escape Sylvie, who now watched her with the scrutiny of a magpie. “What reason is there for such happiness?” she thought, jealous rather than tyrannical. If the colonel had not been in her mind she would have only scolded as before; but now she resolved to spy upon Pierrette as only old maids can spy. The day was still and gloomy.

“You don’t appear to be ill now, mademoiselle,” Sylvie said at dinner. “Didn’t I tell you she put it on to annoy us?” she cried, addressing her brother, not waiting for Pierrette.

“On the contrary, cousin, I have a sort of fever—”

“Fever! what fever? You are as gay as a lark. Perhaps you have seen someone again?”

Pierrette trembled and dropped her eyes. Sylvie ordered her to stay in the dining-room with a candle and do her sewing, excluding her from the salon. Pierrette made no sign.

Rogron, not understanding his sister’s anger, urged Pierrette to please her cousin. Pierrette bore everything; Brigaut would come at midnight and bring an answer, and that hope sustained her. But she was using up her strength. At last midnight sounded; she opened the window with a string made by tying bits of twine, heard Brigaut’s step, and drew up this letter, which filled her with joy:

My dear Pierrette,—As you are so ill you must not tire yourself by waiting for me. You will hear me if I cry like an owl. Happily my father taught me to imitate their note. So when you hear the cry three times you will know I am there, and then you must let down the cord. But I shall not come again for some days. I hope then to bring you good news.

Oh! Pierrette, don’t talk of dying! All my heart shook at the mere idea. No, my Pierrette, you must not die; you will live happy, and soon you shall be delivered from your persecutors. If I do not succeed in what I am undertaking for your rescue, I shall appeal to the law, and I shall speak out before heaven and earth and tell how your wicked relations treat you. I am certain you have not many more days to suffer; have patience, my Pierrette! Jacques is watching over you as in the old days when we slid on the pond and I pulled you out of the hole where we nearly drowned together.

Adieu, my dear Pierrette; in a few days, if God wills, we shall be happy. Alas, I dare not tell you the only thing that may hinder our meeting. But God loves us! In a few days I shall see you at liberty, without troubles, without any one to hinder my looking at you—for, ah! Pierrette, I hunger to see you. Yes, Pierrette, I will be your lover when I have earned the fortune you deserve; till then I will be to you only a devoted servant whose life is yours to do with as you please.

Jacques Brigaut.

The major’s son sent another letter, without telling Pierrette, to Madame Lorrain at Nantes:

Madame Lorrain,—Your granddaughter will die, worn out with ill-treatment, if you do not come to fetch her. I could scarcely recognize her; to show you the state of things I enclose a letter I have received from Pierrette. You are thought here to have taken the money of your granddaughter, and you ought to justify yourself. If you can, come at once. We may still be happy; but if delay Pierrette will be dead.

I am, with respect, your devoted servant,
Jacques Brigaut.
At Monsieur Frappier’s, Cabinet-maker, Grand’Rue, Provins.

Brigaut feared the grandmother was dead.

Though his letter was almost enigmatical to Pierrette, she believed it with virgin faith. Her heart felt like a traveler who sees palm-trees round a well. In a few days her misery would end—Jacques said so. Yet a dreadful thought came: “Poor Jacques; he does not know the hole into which I have now fallen!”

Sylvie had heard Pierrette and had seen Brigaut under the window. She jumped out of bed, rushed to the window, and saw a man hurrying toward the colonel’s house. She went upstairs, found Pierrette’s candle, looked through the keyhole, and—astonished—returned. “Pierrette, are you ill?” she asked.

“No, cousin,” said Pierrette.

“Why is your candle burning at this time of night? Open the door; I must know what this means.”

Pierrette went barefoot to the door; Sylvie entered and saw the cord Pierrette had forgotten. She seized it.

“What is that for?” she asked.

“Nothing, cousin.”

“Nothing!” she cried. “Always lying; you’ll never get to heaven that way. Go to bed; you’ll take cold.”

She asked no more and left Pierrette terrified by this unusual clemency. Instead of exploding, Sylvie had decided to surprise Pierrette and the colonel together, to seize their letters and confound the lovers. Pierrette, sensing danger, sewed the letters into her corset and covered them with calico.

Thus ended the secret loves of Pierrette and Brigaut.

Pierrette rejoiced that Jacques would not come for some nights; she thought Sylvie’s suspicions would be quieted. Sylvie, however, spent the next three nights on watch. Each evening she watched the colonel without discovering anything that betrayed an understanding. She sent Pierrette to confession and seized that moment to search the child’s room with the penetration of a spy. She found nothing. Her fury reached a peak. Had Pierrette been present she would have struck her remorselessly. To Sylvie, jealousy was more occupation than feeling; she lived in it and watched with a vigilance that made the slightest sound keep her on edge.

“That miserable little wretch will kill me,” she said.

Her severity increased, and Pierrette’s condition worsened. She had fever regularly, and the pains in her head became intolerable. By the end of the week even visitors noticed her suffering face. Doctor Neraud, perhaps on Vinet’s advice, did not come that week. The colonel, knowing himself suspected by Sylvie, feared to risk his marriage by showing solicitude for Pierrette. Bathilde explained the change by the girl’s natural growth. At last, one Sunday evening in the salon, Pierrette’s sufferings overcame her and she fainted. The colonel caught her and carried her to a sofa.

“She did it on purpose,” Sylvie said, looking at those playing boston.

“I assure you your cousin is very ill,” the colonel said.

“She seemed well enough in your arms,” Sylvie whispered to him with a savage smile.

Madame de Chargeboeuf urged sending for a doctor; everyone had noticed Pierrette’s appearance. Pierrette whispered, “I am dying.” Desfondrilles told Sylvie to unfasten the girl’s gown. Sylvie approached, saying it was only a tantrum. She unfastened the gown and touched the corset; Pierrette, roused, sat up with superhuman strength, exclaiming, “No, no, I will go to bed.”

Sylvie had felt papers in the corset. She let Pierrette go, announcing to the company that the illness was a pretence. Later, furious and seeking vengeance, Sylvie was grossly rude to the colonel. Vinet kept his counsel when Sylvie ordered him to remain. Alone with him she declared she would never marry the colonel. Vinet, who owed Rogron, promised friendship and power: when in the Chamber he could make Rogron receiver-general; he advised Sylvie to be patient, to marry her brother to Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf and leave her property to nephews and nieces—vexing Gouraud and Pierrette. His plan was to get the Chargeboeufs as allies and to try for revenge on the colonel. Sylvie, obstinate, accepted the scheme as her own.

The lawyer found the colonel in the square and they argued bitterly. The colonel demanded Vinet find him a wife with a hundred and fifty thousand francs before the elections, else threaten repercussions. Vinet promised he would.

About one o’clock three clear, sharp cries of an owl, well imitated, echoed through the square. Pierrette, in fevered sleep, awoke, opened her window, and flung down a ball of silk to Brigaut, who fastened a letter. Sylvie, restless and awake, heard the owl.

“Ah, bird of ill-omen!” she thought. When she heard the attic open she rushed to her window and then upstairs to Pierrette’s. She found the girl unwinding silk and freeing the letter.

“Ha! I’ve caught you!” cried Sylvie, rushing to the window and seeing Jacques running at full speed. “Give me that letter.”

“No, cousin,” said Pierrette, who, inspired by a desperate courage, resolved to resist.

Sylvie advanced with hatred and fury. Pierrette fell back to put her letter in her hand, which she clenched with unnatural force. Sylvie gripped the delicate white hand with cruel strength and tried to open it. It was a frightful struggle over the girl’s sole treasure—the mind, the secret. Pierrette’s eyes flashed the defiant look of innocence under torture; Sylvie answered in malignant gleams. The clenched hand resisted like steel. Sylvie twisted Pierrette’s arm, tried to force the fingers open, stuck her nails into the flesh, and in her madness set her teeth into the wrist, trying to conquer by pain. Pierrette still defied her. Sylvie’s fury blinded her; she seized Pierrette’s arm and struck the closed fist upon the window-sill and then upon the marble mantelpiece.

“Help! help!” cried Pierrette, the blood flowing.

At that instant loud knocks sounded at the front door. Exhausted, the two women paused.

Rogron, awakened, frightened at his sister’s absence, went down and opened the door, nearly overturned by Brigaut, followed by a sort of phantom. Sylvie’s eyes fell on Pierrette’s corset; she remembered the papers. Releasing the girl’s wrist she sprang upon the corset like a tiger, producing it with a smile of triumph.

“I am dying,” said Pierrette, falling on her knees. “Oh, who will save me?”

“I!” said a woman with white hair and an aged parchment face, her gray eyes glittering.

“Ah! grandmother, you have come too late,” cried the poor child, bursting into tears.

Pierrette collapsed, half-dead from the exhaustion after such a struggle. The tall gray woman lifted her as a nurse lifts a child and went out, followed by Brigaut, without a word to Sylvie, on whom she cast one glance of majestic accusation.

The apparition of that august Breton woman, in her coif, accompanied by Brigaut, appalled Sylvie; she fancied she saw death. She went downstairs, listened to the front door closing, and met her brother, who exclaimed, “Then they haven’t killed you?”

“Go to bed,” said Sylvie. “To-morrow we will see what we must do.”

She returned to her bed, ripped open the corset, and read Brigaut’s two letters, which confounded her. She went to sleep in greatest perplexity, not foreseeing the terrible consequences to which this conduct would lead.

The letters Brigaut had sent to old Madame Lorrain found her in a moment of ineffable joy, soon troubled. The poor old woman had grieved living without Pierrette but consoled herself with the thought of sacrifice for her grandchild’s interest. She had an ever-young heart upheld by sacrifice. Her old husband had mourned Pierrette; every day he seemed to look for her—an old man’s grief.

The news of a generous reparation altered everything. The head of the house of Collinet, whose failure in 1814 had cost the Lorrains twenty-four thousand francs, had gone to America with his children, worked and triumphed, returning to restore his position. He found Madame Lorrain of Pen-Hoel at Saint-Jacques and witnessed her resignation. He made over to the widow both capital and accrued interest, amounting to about forty-two thousand francs; he promised to pay off the husband’s debts to the amount of forty thousand francs more. When the Bourse of Nantes heard of this they sought to honor Collinet, but he preferred to wait for the court.

Madame Lorrain had received the money only the day before Brigaut’s letter arrived, enclosing Pierrette’s. Her first thought was to live with Pierrette and marry her to Brigaut, who would prosper with her help. The moment she read the letters she made instant preparations to start for Provins. She left Nantes that night by mail, went to Paris, took the diligence for Troyes that passed through Provins, and by half-past eleven reached Frappier’s, where Brigaut, shocked at her despairing looks, told her of Pierrette’s state and promised to bring the girl instantly.

When Pierrette screamed, the horror of that cry struck the grandmother as it did Brigaut. Together they would have roused the neighborhood if Rogron had not opened the door. The scream of the young girl at bay gave the grandmother sudden strength; she bore Pierrette in her arms to Frappier’s, where Madame Frappier hastily arranged Brigaut’s room for them. There, on a half-made bed, the sufferer was deposited; she fainted, holding her hand still clenched, wounded, bleeding, nails deep in the flesh. Brigaut, Frappier, his wife, and the old woman stood looking in silence, amazed.

“Why is her hand bloody?” the grandmother asked.

Pierrette, exhausted and dimly conscious she was safe, let her fingers unbend. Brigaut’s letter fell from them. “They tried to take my letter from her,” said Brigaut, kneeling and picking up the lines in which he had told her to come away. He kissed the martyr’s hand with pious love.

The tall gray woman, a sublime spectre, stood beside her grandchild’s pillow, terror and vengeance in the wrinkles of her yellow skin. She read, with an old intuition, Pierrette’s life and divined the illness and the danger. Two big tears rose in her wan eyes and rolled down her cheek without wetting it.

“They have killed her!” she said, clasping her hands, and fell on her knees, making a vow perhaps to Saint Anne d’Auray.

“A doctor from Paris,” she commanded Brigaut. “Go and fetch one.”

She pushed him out, then, recalling him, unfastened her gown and drew forty-two bank-bills from her bosom. “Take what is necessary, and bring back the greatest doctor in Paris,” she said.

“Keep those,” Frappier replied; “he can’t change thousand-franc notes now. I have money; the diligence will pass presently; he can find a place on it. But first consult Doctor Martener to tell us the best physician in Paris. We have time.”

Brigaut woke Monsieur Martener and brought him at once. The doctor was surprised to find Mademoiselle Lorrain at Frappier’s. Brigaut told him of the scene at the Rogrons’; but Martener did not at first grasp the full horror. He examined the bruised hand.

“She could not have given these wounds herself,” he said.

“No; the horrible woman to whom I entrusted her was murdering her,” the grandmother said. “My poor Pierrette was screaming ‘Help! help! I’m dying’—enough to touch an executioner.”

“But why was it?” asked the doctor. Pierrette’s pulse showed she was very ill. Martener, after feeling her head and questioning her, found an abscess above the left ear. “You must tell us all,” he said, so they might cure her. He would await the Paris doctor’s arrival and consult the hospital surgeon. Meanwhile he would send a quieting draught so the girl might sleep; she needed sleep.

Left alone with her granddaughter, the old Breton woman made her tell everything; she promised Pierrette comfort and that Brigaut should live with them. Pierrette admitted her martyrdom, not foreseeing the events her admissions would provoke. The monstrosity of two beings without affection or family feeling opened to the grandmother a world of woe unknown to her.

The arrival of her grandmother and the certainty of living with her soothed Pierrette’s mind as the sleeping draught soothed her body. The old woman watched the darling, kissing her forehead, hair, and hands as if laying her in a sacred repose.

That morning Monsieur Martener went to Monsieur Tiphaine and recounted the scene between Pierrette and Sylvie, the moral and physical tortures the Rogrons had inflicted, and the illnesses their cruelty had produced. Tiphaine summoned Auffray the notary, one of Pierrette’s maternal relatives.

The feud between the Vinet and Tiphaine parties was fierce. Rogron’s followers had spread scandalous stories about Madame Tiphaine’s mother’s liaison with the banker du Tillet and her father’s bankruptcy; such wounds cut deep. In Provins, private grudges readily became public, partisan conflicts.

Tiphaine saw in Pierrette’s case an opportunity to humiliate the Rogrons, hosts of the salon where anti-monarchist plans and an opposition journal had arisen. The public prosecutor was notified; with Auffray and Martener a secret consultation decided to have Auffray appointed guardian so he could convene a Family Council and, backed by three doctors’ testimony, demand Pierrette’s release from the Rogrons. The procureur, Lesourd, would push the matter toward a criminal inquiry.

By midday the town buzzed with news of the Rogron household. Pierrette’s faint cries were magnified into horror. A crowd gathered at Frappier’s shop as he described how Pierrette had been brought to him with broken fingers and a bloody hand. Doctor Bianchon’s post-chaise arrived about one; Martener and the hospital surgeon were summoned. The Rogrons were accused of deliberate ill-treatment and nearly killing their cousin.

Vinet, at the law courts, rushed to the Rogrons. Sylvie, evasive about the night, admitted nothing until pressed. Vinet bluntly warned they might face criminal charges depending on Pierrette’s injuries. Under questioning Sylvie confessed jealousy and, painfully, that her suspicions were baseless. Vinet used their terror, insisting he needed a strong motive to represent them publicly; they contrived Rogron’s immediate marriage to Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, to be announced that day with banns posted Sunday. Sylvie agreed to appear in the marriage contract as assigning her capital to her brother, retaining the income. Vinet urged antedating the contract to bind the Chargeboeufs publicly and preserve their visits. He promised to take their defense if they signed.

By noon the lawyer had full authority to draw the contract and defend the Rogrons. At twelve a petition was made to Monsieur Tiphaine, sitting in chambers, against Brigaut and the widow Lorrain for abducting the minor Pierrette from her guardian’s house—turning the defenders into complainants. The judge postponed the hearing till four; Tiphaine wanted Auffray at the hearing with the doctors’ report.

The marriage announcement and Sylvie’s sacrifices alienated two supporters, Mademoiselle Habert and the colonel, who ostentatiously stayed with the Rogrons but intended to hurt them. When Martener mentioned Pierrette’s head injury, Celeste and the colonel related a blow Pierrette had given herself the evening she was forced to leave the salon and recounted Mademoiselle Rogron’s heartless remarks. Vinet had foreseen this opposition, but he had secured the Rogrons’ fortune for Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf and planned to use that alliance to dominate Provins socially and politically.

From midday to four the Tiphaine circle checked on Pierrette. Suffering, she was unaware of the tumult and found ineffable happiness in being reunited with her grandmother and Brigaut. Bianchon protested such barbarity and insisted all town physicians examine the case; Dr. Neraud, friend of the Rogrons, was present. The unanimous report explained Pierrette’s malady, a sadly common but horrifying condition.

At four President Tiphaine took his seat. Madame Lorrain, Auffray, Brigaut and others entered the court; Vinet alone faced them. He claimed two strangers had forced entry into the Rogron home and abducted the minor. Auffray, as surrogate guardian, presented the physicians’ report—signed by a Paris authority and all Provins doctors—asserting that Pierrette’s near-fatal state resulted from ill-treatment by Rogron and his sister. Auffray asked that Pierrette not be returned to the guardian’s household but remain with a family member designated by the judge and proposed convening a Family Council to consider deposing the guardian.

Vinet objected that he had not seen the report to rebut it. The judge replied that the report might have been submitted to the procureur du roi; the case was heard. Tiphaine’s order forbade returning Pierrette to her guardian until the Family Council met, commissioned the attending physician and the hospital surgeon to inspect her, and stipulated that if her injuries became alarming the criminal courts would take up the matter, without prejudice to Auffray’s civil suit.

Vinet scoffed, calling it fuss over a girl supposedly in an intrigue with an apprentice. He left to rally his party, arguing the Tiphaines made an unjust spectacle of Rogron. To his supporters, Sylvie’s intervention to seize letters and her supposed severity were natural; Rogron bore limited responsibility. Vinet framed the dispute as partisan.

Frappier’s house was later judged unsuitable for Pierrette; she was moved to Auffray’s with care and ostentation. Laid on a stretcher, accompanied by a Gray Sister and her grandmother, she passed under many windows, her pallor advantaging the Auffrays’ case. Installed on the second floor, with Martener and the hospital surgeon attending, Pierrette received tender care.

That evening exaggerations grew on both sides. The Rogron salon was crowded; Vinet had rallied the Liberals, the Chargeboeuf ladies dined with the Rogrons to sign the contract. He downplayed the Pierrette episode, confident the Royal courts would vindicate them; to some the Chargeboeuf alliance wiped the Rogrons’ slate clean and painted Pierrette as depraved. In Madame Tiphaine’s salon, vengeance for past slanders was taken out on the Rogrons, now monsters to be tried. Journalists offered opposing accounts: one article suggested no case existed; the Rogron paper hesitated, fearing slander, and urged patience for the law.

The Family Council, selected by the juge de paix, included Rogron and the two Auffrays as nearest relatives, Monsieur Ciprey (maternal nephew), Mademoiselle Habert, Pierrette’s confessor, and Colonel Gouraud, a friend of her father. The judge’s choice seemed impartial, Habert and Gouraud being considered Rogron friends. Rogron requested Vinet for the council, thereby postponing it until late December when Tiphaine would be in Paris, a tactic by Vinet to delay and influence the outcome.

Vinet spoke three hours before the council, arguing an alleged intrigue between Pierrette and Brigaut to justify Sylvie’s severity, and portraying the guardian’s delegation of duties as natural. Despite his efforts the Council unanimously removed Rogron's guardianship; Auffray became guardian and Ciprey surrogate. Testimony included Adele the servant against her former masters, Mademoiselle Habert recounting Mademoiselle Rogron’s cruel remarks, and Brigaut’s letter from Pierrette describing her ill-treatment. The council concluded Pierrette’s illness resulted from neglect by the guardian, and the case would proceed publicly. Rogron, advised by Vinet, opposed the council’s report being accepted by the court. Authorities intervened as Pierrette’s condition worsened; the trial, placed on the docket, was postponed until March 1828.

Meanwhile Rogron married Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf; Sylvie moved to the second floor to share with her. Madame Rogron supplanted Madame Tiphaine socially. Tiphaine, backed by Nucingen and du Tillet, advanced to civil judge and moved to Paris; his son inherited and sold the Provins house—evidence, in Vinet’s narrative, of Tiphaine’s contempt for the town. The promotion did little for the Vinet faction, but Vinet exploited the events politically. Thus the brutal martyrdom Pierrette endured—leading to the trepanning performed by Martener under Bianchon’s advice—was mired in legal delay by an unscrupulous lawyer while Pierrette languished in agonizing pain.

Martener and the Auffrays grew fond of Pierrette and her grandmother, whose Roman severity of feeling impressed them. Martener, who had begun his career in Paris but returned to provincial life, devoted himself to curing her, treating her as a poem of suffering he would not publicize. His dedication influenced the Auffrays’ household to be reverent and quiet; even the children kept silence for her sake. The Auffray house, on a terrace beneath the chateau ruins overlooking the valley, became her refuge. Pierrette’s room above Auffray’s study allowed her to see the valley, a landscape she had rarely known.

Brigaut, unable to work, visited thrice daily, sorrowful and faithful. The grandmother, concealing her despair to comfort Pierrette, made her a Breton cap recalling the girl’s earlier happier life. Pierrette’s delicate features, pale skin and serious expression embodied melancholy; all served her with devotion. Madame Martener sent a piano to amuse her; music transfixed her as she listened, resigned yet touching. Her religious comforters admired her saintly resignation, the kind of serene departure that convinces even skeptics.

Martener corresponded with Bianchon and sought to drain the abscess; periodic improvements—a better appetite, some color—cheered them, but the head disease remained grave. Bianchon came from Paris with the celebrated Desplein, who warned Martener that only a miracle could save the child: caries of the bone had begun and, at her age, bones were fragile. The operation was performed in early March 1828 by the great surgeon, but Desplein cautioned the odds. Martener had even proposed a daring procedure akin to lithotrity—passing a hollow instrument into the head—that Desplein refused. Returning from Paris, Martener announced to the Auffrays, Madame Lorrain, the priests and Brigaut that science could do no more; recovery was in God’s hands. They responded with masses and vows.

While Pierrette lay dying, Vinet calumniated her in court. The judge accepted the Family Council’s report; Vinet appealed. The procureur du roi required further evidence, including testimony from Pierrette. When Desfondrilles came to the Auffrays to receive it, Pierrette was dying, with her confessor near for extreme unction. She begged forgiveness for her cousins, saying judgment belonged to God; she told her grandmother to leave all to Brigaut and to give Adele a thousand francs, insisting if Adele had stayed with the Rogrons she would not be dying.

At three on the Tuesday of Easter week, on a bright day, Pierrette died. Her grandmother kept vigil through the night and sewed her shroud with stiff fingers. Brigaut went to Frappier’s to make the coffin. Frappier watched as Brigaut planed, his plane cutting ribbons of wood wet with tears. He fashioned the coffin alone and lined it with lead; by chance the lead cost exactly the sum Pierrette had given Brigaut for her journey—the coincidence overwhelmed him. He burned his tools, settled his accounts, and bid farewell, his grief making him part of the awful finale of the Rogrons’ tyranny.

At the Auffray home, the room where Pierrette lay was crowded. Rogron’s emissaries stood near the body, and the surgeon in a dissecting apron prepared instruments. Madame Lorrain, on her knees, cried out; she believed they intended to open the body and cut into her head as they had when she was alive. Brigaut, who had just brought the coffin, seized a chisel, threatened the men, and swore he would kill any who touched her. Auffray and the curate tried to restrain him; the lawyers and doctors left. The plumber soldered the lead to make the coffin less likely to be opened. With Pierrette dead, the guardianship suit collapsed; Rogron did not dare attend her funeral.

Brigaut left Provins and walked to Paris, petitioned the Dauphiness to join the Royal guard and was admitted. When the Algerian expedition opened he sought service and rose: he became sub-lieutenant, then advanced; he distinguished himself and rose to major, later receiving a commission as an officer—always taciturn, solitary, trusted, and never wounded. He inherited forty-six thousand francs from old Madame Lorrain, who died in Paris in 1829.

In public life, Vinet rose: elected deputy in 1830, later procureur-general; his influence secure, he remained in power. Rogron became receiver-general in the same town where Vinet held legal functions; Tiphaine, after aligning with the July dynasty, became president of the Royal court there. The ex-beautiful Madame Tiphaine and Madame Rogron lived amicably; Vinet was close with Tiphaine. Rogron, declining in health and witless, made absurd remarks; Sylvie managed his property, lent money at high interest, and lived frugally.

General Baron Gouraud married Mademoiselle Matifat, daughter of a druggist, as Vinet had arranged; he became a departmental commander and was later made peer of France for his conduct during the riots under Casimir Perier, rewarded for seizing Saint-Merry. None of those involved in Pierrette’s death showed remorse. Desfondrilles, to further his prospects, was installed as president of the Provins court through Vinet’s efforts. Sylvie maintained a small circle.

Over time locals downplayed the scandal. Travelers returning from Paris who had visited Mademoiselle Rogron heard laments of a supposed slanderous story about a ward; officials like Desfondrilles dismissed it as prejudice and told softened versions that blamed poor constitution or an imprudent liaison. Frappier was mocked as given to his wine; Habert’s motives were dismissed as personal.

Two men remembered Pierrette daily: Doctor Martener and Major Brigaut; they alone knew the hideous truth. To give full scale to that truth one thinks of Beatrice Cenci—another young woman condemned by intrigues like those that destroyed Pierrette. As in those cases, legality serves scoundrelism unless one believes in a higher justice.
