At Paris balls, two gatherings commonly occur: the formal party of superficial pleasantries, and a later, more intimate meeting where genuine conversation and laughter flourish. The latter, hosted by women like Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches, preserves the old spirit of French wit and allows free exchange of ideas and camaraderie.

One memorable evening at Mademoiselle des Touches' salon gathered notable figures, including the Minister de Marsay, in an atmosphere of wit and ease. Anecdotes and confessions wove a lively tapestry of social interaction until the talk turned to de Marsay's recollections of early love—particularly a passionate affair with a woman six years his senior. He described youthful infatuation marked by intense devotion, secrecy, and a practiced indifference to shield their liaison from society.

He portrayed their relationship as a careful dance of appearances and feeling: riding past her carriage, exchanging silent signals through the flowers she carried, reveling in mutual admiration and the thrill of concealment, all while remaining unaware of any betrayal. The turning point came when, ill, he sent a note to cancel a meeting but, consumed by jealousy, went to her nonetheless. There he found her with another man, which ignited a storm of emotion; yet her calm demeanor and tender concern briefly soothed his doubts.

The narrative darkened as de Marsay confronted the reality of betrayal. He felt innocence end and a more cynical view of love begin, wrestling with thoughts of revenge and the tangled nature of passion, and ultimately deciding that true love must rise above the urge for vengeance. His account moved those present, prompting reflection on love, jealousy, and the social expectations that shape relationships—reminding the salon of trust’s fragility and love’s transformative power.

Cured of my cold and my pure love, I began an affair with a charming heroine whose beauty sharply contrasted with that of my deceptive angel. I avoided quarrels with this clever woman, doubting whether true love could match the delights of her skilled deceit. Such refined hypocrisy can be as good as virtue.

I sought a hair artist in the Rue Boucher to make a keepsake for my new angel. He specialized in hair mementos and showed me work surpassing fairy tales, mentioning trends like marking linen with hair. Suspicion rose when I recognized a handkerchief he had worked on, confirming doubts about the authenticity of women’s affections. I left the shop believing in pleasure but skeptical about love.

Two months later, in the boudoir of the ethereal being, I held her hand as we explored the depths of sentiment. In a moment of tenderness, I seized the chance to draw out her most delightful lies. Charlotte professed love, insisting I was the only man in her world and that she had been seeking ways to bind me to her eternally.

The women listening to de Marsay seemed offended by his portrayal of their airs. Just as I might have believed her sweet falsehoods, I asked, “When are you to marry the Duke?” The directness startled her; her reaction revealed guilt. She feigned astonishment, but I pressed on, suggesting she should marry him, as he was rich and devout.

Charlotte fell to her knees, insisting she loved no one but me. I urged her to be truthful, and she swore before God. I confessed doubts about her fidelity, which she quickly turned against me, claiming I no longer loved her. She sought to escape the confrontation, but curiosity compelled her to ask what I had seen. I revealed that I had noticed her attending mass with the Duke several times; she dismissed it as jealousy.

I explained that her marriage to the Duke would elevate her status and advised her to embrace the role, as he was virtuous and would expect propriety. Charlotte, in tears, lamented that if I had only spoken sooner we could have been happy. I maintained it was too late and she had gone too far with the Duke.

She worried about the Duke’s suspicions; I reassured her he likely had none but that she needed to prove devotion. As she paced the boudoir, she shifted demeanor, held out her hand, and declared I was noble and charming, vowing never to forget me.

Her tactics were admirable, and I played the distressed lover. She then asked what would become of me, and I revealed I had made advances to another woman. This startled Charlotte, who accused me of being unfaithful. I countered that she had too much wit for her heart to suffer.

Charlotte expressed disillusionment with love, claiming women could never be truly loved. I acknowledged her anger but pointed out that taking love seriously in society was like romanticizing an actress. She accused me of betrayal; I feigned submission and asked if she would remember her grievances once she became a duchess.

Her bitterness confirmed she still felt wronged, and I realized I had successfully distanced myself. I had studied the lives of successful men with women and believed I had mastered the art of retreat.

The women in the room reacted with sympathy for Charlotte, while de Marsay maintained a slight smile. Conversation shifted to the decline of social distinctions. Lady Dudley noted that women often did not see their marriages as acts of inconstancy. De Marsay lamented the erosion of the aristocracy, saying duchesses and marquises were disappearing and the Civil Code had leveled social hierarchies.

Blondet remarked that aristocracy now began with viscountesses, while de Marsay observed that countesses might survive in some form. He reflected on how the last great ladies had vanished with the splendor of the previous century, and the Code had inadvertently created a new class of women.

The Comte de Vandenesse commented on how the current structure allowed anyone to claim nobility, while Lord Dudley noted property had not been organized, accelerating the aristocracy’s decline.

Joseph Bridau observed that the beauty of womanhood had diminished and the great lady’s fan was broken. Daniel d’Arthez said the aristocracy had retreated, leaving behind women who might otherwise have shaped society.

The discussion turned to the modern “perfect lady,” a product of the era, embodying good taste and grace but lacking the grandeur of past great ladies. Mademoiselle des Touches questioned the notion of progress, while Madame de Nucingen explained that a woman’s worth was no longer tied to lineage but to character and presentation.

Count Adam Laginski asked what defined a “perfect lady.” Émile Blondet described her as exuding elegance and refinement, effortlessly commanding respect and admiration. He depicted her as graceful in bearing, charming in conversation, composed at carriage and table, and able to captivate without theatricality.

The dialogue highlighted modern femininity’s complexities: the “perfect lady” navigates societal expectations with skill and charm, blending beauty and intellect to captivate those around her. Guests considered whether this new type of woman compensated for the vanished greatness of earlier aristocratic women, or merely reflected a reshaped society where merit and presentation replaced inherited splendour.

“You will not talk for half an hour with a bourgeoise without her mentioning her husband,” Blondet began, gravely observing the ways of contemporary women. Even when a lady is married she artfully conceals her husband; he appears only as a briefly noticed middle-aged man who bows and leaves. At a ball she can be radiant and unattached; at home she is prudent and exacting. Blondet sketched the modern lady’s household with care: she receives no one before four, keeps her house warm and orderly, renews her luxury so nothing looks neglected, arranges flowers as deliberate gifts, and surrounds herself with fashionable trifles that are for living use, not museum display. At home she sits by the fire in a low chair and lets the visitor do the honors; playful at a ball, she becomes the hostess whose wit you owe. She creates an intimate world where she can shine, supported by conversation that often skirts generalities.

De Marsay agreed that Blondet had detected a flaw of the age: the epigram now indexes momentary events rather than striking at enduring people or objects. Blondet went on: a modern lady’s intelligence, if present, shows itself chiefly in skepticism. The townswoman is steady in virtue; the fashionable lady hesitates and debates her virtue. That uncertainty, Blondet suggested, is among the few graces left by the confused moral climate of the time. She may rarely go to church yet will debate religion; if you dissent, she will try to convert you with the pious, politically slanted phrases typical of the class—claims that religion preserves society and family against selfish individualism, couched in a neo-Christian morality that is neither clearly Catholic nor Protestant but very “moral” in a muddled, modern way.

The women laughed at Blondet’s satire, but he pressed the point: the “perfect lady” of the day is a mixture of intellectual and political confusion, surrounded by ostentatious yet ephemeral goods produced by an industry that constantly replaces its wares. She feigns curiosity and ignorance to extract confidences, arranging her passions discreetly, like notes on a musical score, with rests and pauses. Whereas the great ladies of old displayed their affairs boldly, the modern woman stages them with calculated restraint. Name, position, and fortune no longer provide immunity; the aristocracy no longer shields indiscretions. Rather than being aggressively triumphant, she navigates compromises; she fears servants as an Englishwoman fears a divorce trial. Free and dazzling in public, she becomes a slave at home—independent only in private or in theory. Her first duty is to preserve her position as a lady. In a society where a repudiated wife, stripped of carriage or opera box, becomes neither wife, maid, nor townswoman but an adrift chattel, a woman must weigh potential ruin and the uncertainties of lovers and convents. Thus the modern lady may provoke gossip but rarely slander.

Blondet summarized the era as producing a system between English hypocrisy and eighteenth-century frankness—a transitional, bastard regime that yields no clear new model. He doubted that any woman, however high-born, could acquire before twenty-five the encyclopedic familiarity with social nuance, the practiced maneuvering, the delicate combination of artifice and candor needed to be “perfect.” When Mademoiselle des Touches asked where the female author would fit in this scheme, Blondet replied, with a teasing epigram attributed to Napoleon, that a woman without genius is a woman “comme il n’en faut pas”—not the right sort. Canalis interposed, defending literary genius and segueing into a vivid encomium of Napoleon.

Canalis painted Napoleon as a phenomenon of will and contradiction: a man of action and thought, the most concentrated force of his age, capable of creating empires, laws, and public works while also improvising. He juxtaposed Napoleon’s military and political genius with his human contradictions—hypocrisy and generosity, love of both simplicity and pomp, protection of the arts despite poor taste, a strong practical genius that nevertheless failed to secure his final position. Canalis admired Napoleon’s capacity to do everything he willed, likening him in youthful vigor to Caesar and Cromwell but emphasizing his tragic, improvisational mastery that ultimately left Europe altered and himself undone. De Marsay lauded Napoleon as both despot and dispenser of justice, the genuine sovereign of decisive moments.

This appreciation of strong, decisive characters led the company to remark on the value of conversation itself—how invaluable those brilliant flashes of wit are, like rare performances that cannot be repeated. Joseph Bridau observed that if one had to pay for the charms of such conversation as one pays for music, one’s fortune would be inadequate. The Princesse de Cadignan, skeptical of claims that the age had diminished women, defended contemporary passions and capabilities: public splendor may have receded under a regime run by grocers, but the human heart remains unchanged. She argued that devotion and magnificent suffering still exist without the old publicity; that her contemporaries—marquises, dancers, singers, poets—match or exceed the glories of the past; that the Empire’s age, like Louis XV’s, had its own stamp and splendor. The Comte de Vandenesse chimed in, agreeing that present women can rival the heroines and beauties of earlier ages and that Paris boasts a thousand great women who write better than the solitary Madame de Sévigné, although they publish not. Blondet’s portrait of the modern lady, Vandenesse conceded, described many fascinations but did not preclude heroism where needed. He had seen providential effects even amid the casualness of women deemed light by society.

The group’s banter and compliments—Madame de Vandenesse’s hopes, Madame de Serizy’s teasing, Baron de Nucingen’s relish for the talk—led to General Montriveau’s turn. He prefaced his tale with a solemn note: in his campaigns he had, involuntarily, been the cause of a frightful disaster, a drama useful to Doctor Bianchon, who studied the mind as well as the body, for it bore on the will.

General Montriveau recounted the disordered retreat from the Beresina in 1812. The army had dissolved into a mixed, suffering mass. On a bleak evening he sought shelter and found a wretched Polish farmhouse where officers were gathered to share meager food—potatoes, horseflesh, frozen beets. He sat down among comrades and remembered an extraordinary Italian colonel of his regiment: tall, powerfully built, with abundant black curls and a complexion so fair as to be almost womanly, and with a strikingly irascible temperament. The colonel’s face could be angelic one moment and terrifying the next; his voice had a guttural roll that lent force to every command. He was a man of contrasts, gifted with an authority and presence that made women yield to him as glass yields to a blower’s pipe. Yet he was neither a louche seducer nor indifferent to female influence—rather, he enacted a fierce, possessive, almost primitive brutality when roused.

Montriveau recounted a scene where the colonel, witnessing a quarrel between two batteries whose collision had crushed one captain’s leg, had turned suddenly ferocious and, in two seconds of outburst, killed the other colonel by splitting his skull. The victimized captain’s Sicilian wife, Rosina, was beautiful despite hardship—dark, small, with almond eyes and the remnants of female grace. She had captured the colonel’s attention, nurturing a dangerous liaison; her husband, an impassive Piedmontese gentleman, appeared oblivious to their connection but betrayed a cruel, restless underlip that made Montriveau distrust him.

That night at the farm the colonel called for “Rosina,” and the little wife, after distressed gestures, went to him. Montriveau, with a laugh that broke the tense air, apologized to the husband for some perceived impropriety, and the men slept. Next morning they resumed marching. Some distance on, as they looked back toward the farmhouse, they heard terrifying sounds—the din of fire and a woman’s weak scream. The farmhouse had been barricaded and burned. The captain, who had been walking calmly among the column, pointed to his breast and toward the fire with a single gesture: “It was I.” None asked further; they resumed their march in silence, as the Englishman’s phrase in the group remarked: there is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep. The image of Rosina’s fate—the burning of the house and the dreadful explanation—haunted them. Madame de Montcornet confessed she would dream of it.

Blondet and others turned to the medical testimony. De Marsay suggested Bianchon, who had seen the dying woman, should speak. Bianchon affirmed the scene’s tragic beauty: the Duke and he had spent a night by a dying Duchess’s pillow. She was consumed by pulmonary disease, but retained a sublime, pallid serenity. The Duke for a moment fell asleep; upon waking he sat beside his wife, and she, with a last tenderness, took his hand and murmured, “My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?” and died looking at him. The company noted the profound, sweet impression such intimate deathbed scenes left, and Mademoiselle des Touches rose, moved.

An appended catalogue followed, listing the principal characters and where else they appear in Balzac’s Human Comedy: Bianchon; the Blondets; Bridau; Canalis; Lord Dudley; the Marquise d’Espard; Comte Adam Laginski; Marsay; the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse; General Montriveau; the Nucingens; the Portenduere vicomtesse; Rastignac; the Ronquerolles; the Serizy comtesse; the imaginary mistress saga; Mademoiselle des Touches; and Comte Félix de Vandenesse—each with references to other novels and tales in which Balzac revisits them, indicating the interwoven unity of his social panorama.

Throughout the conversation and narratives, the recurring themes are clear: the modern woman’s performance of virtue and sentiment; the precarious social position that forces delicate public artifice and private constraint; the decline of old aristocratic protections; the moral muddle of a society caught between English prudery and eighteenth-century frankness; the place of genius and heroism amid triviality; and the brutal realities of war that expose character and precipitate tragedy. The dialogue alternates satire, admiration, and melancholy—satire of contemporary manners, admiration for strength and talent (as in Napoleon), and melancholy at examples of human suffering and the small cruelties of social life. The General’s anecdote and the doctor’s deathbed scene provide poignant counterpoints to the conversation’s lighter wit, grounding the assembled company’s reflections in the human costs of passion, pride, and misfortune.
