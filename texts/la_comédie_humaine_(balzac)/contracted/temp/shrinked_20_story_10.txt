In Paris, balls split into a formal round of pleasantries and a later, intimate meeting where genuine conversation and laughter flourish. The latter, hosted by women like Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches, preserves French wit and encourages candid exchange.

At one such evening in Mademoiselle des Touches’ salon, the Minister de Marsay recalled an early affair with a woman six years his senior: a secret relationship disguised by outward indifference and private signals in the flowers she carried. When illness forced him to cancel a meeting, jealousy drove him to visit anyway; he found her with another man. Her calm tenderness briefly quelled his doubts, but the sight ignited a storm of feeling. Confronted with betrayal, de Marsay felt the end of innocence, wrestled with revenge, then resolved that true love must transcend vengeance. His tale prompted reflections on love, jealousy, social expectation, and fragile trust.

Cured of naive passion, the narrator begins an affair with a charming heroine whose beauty contrasts sharply with the earlier deceptive angel. He avoids quarrels with this clever woman, suspecting that true love might not match the pleasures of her practiced deceit—refined hypocrisy can seem nearly as satisfying as virtue.

He visits a hair artist in the Rue Boucher to commission a keepsake. The artisan displays astonishing hair mementos and mentions passing trends like marking linen with hair. Suspicion flares when the narrator recognizes a handkerchief the artist has worked on, confirming doubts about the authenticity of women’s affections. He leaves resolved to enjoy pleasure but skeptical of love.

Two months later, in the heroine’s boudoir, he tests her. Charlotte professes exclusive devotion, claiming he is the only man in her world and that she sought ways to bind him. But when he bluntly asks, “When are you to marry the Duke?” her startled reaction betrays guilt. She feigns astonishment; he suggests she should accept the Duke—rich, devout, and fitting.

Charlotte falls to her knees, swearing before God that she loves only him. He presses her, confessing doubts about her fidelity; she counters that he no longer loves her and tries to leave, but curiosity draws her back to ask what he has seen. He reveals he noticed her attending mass with the Duke several times; she dismisses his jealousy. He advises her to seize the Duke’s offer, since marriage would elevate her and the Duke expects propriety. In tears she laments that had he spoken sooner they might have been happy, but he insists it is too late—she has gone too far.

She fears the Duke’s suspicions; he reassures her it’s unlikely but urges her to show devotion. As she alternates between supplication and composed entreaty, she vows never to forget him. He plays the distressed lover until she asks what will become of him; he then admits to having made advances to another woman. This surprises Charlotte, who accuses him of unfaithfulness; he replies that her wit exceeds her heart’s suffering. She proclaims disillusionment with love, asserting that women can never truly be loved; he retorts that taking love seriously in society is like romanticizing an actress. She accuses him of betrayal; he feigns submission and asks whether she will remember her grievances once she is a duchess. Her lingering bitterness convinces him he has successfully distanced himself—he has learned how successful men withdraw.

Back in the salon, the women express sympathy for Charlotte while de Marsay smiles slightly. Conversation turns to the erosion of social distinctions. Lady Dudley notes women often do not view marriages as acts of inconstancy. De Marsay laments the aristocracy’s decline, blaming legal and social changes for leveling hierarchies. Blondet quips that aristocracy now begins with viscountesses; de Marsay hopes countesses may survive.

The Comte de Vandenesse and Lord Dudley observe that the current order lets anyone claim nobility; unsettled property hastens the fall. Joseph Bridau laments a decline in womanly beauty; Daniel d’Arthez says the aristocracy’s retreat has deprived society of women who might have shaped it. Discussion turns to the modern “perfect lady”: tasteful, graceful, poised but lacking the grandeur of past great ladies. Mademoiselle des Touches questions whether this is progress; Madame de Nucingen argues worth now lies in character and presentation rather than lineage. Émile Blondet describes the perfect lady as effortlessly elegant and refined, commanding respect by bearing and demeanor across settings.

The dialogue highlights modern femininity’s complexities: the perfect lady skillfully navigates expectations with charm, blending beauty and intellect, compensating for—or reflecting—the vanished splendor of inherited rank.

Blondet began with a jest about a certain modern married woman who speaks of her husband without revealing him: at a ball she bows to a middle-aged man, then retires to her carriage; at home she admits no one before four and keeps callers waiting. Her house is tastefully arranged and lived in—no dusty relics, warm staircases, abundant flowers; gifts are accepted only from chosen people because they imply a promise. Fashion trinkets lie about as part of life. At a ball she is the creditor of pleasure; at home she owes you the pleasure of her wit. She cultivates charm to draw men into a private world where she can shine—a world of general conversation and carefully managed attention. Blondet used this portrait to explain how isolated women are and why they crave a little sphere in which to be admired.

De Marsay agreed that epigrams now skewer transient events more than lasting qualities. Blondet added that intelligent modern women adopt doubt as a chief faculty: unlike straightforwardly virtuous townswomen, the “lady” disputes virtue itself, making perpetual uncertainty one of her last graces. She rarely goes to church but will debate religion, slipping into a muddled, sentimental moralizing that satisfies neither dogma nor free thought but sounds respectable.

The women laughed, and Blondet continued. The “perfect lady” embodies intellectual and political confusion and is surrounded by flashy, ephemeral products of modern industry. She will seem to possess superior ideas because she has probed others and feigned ignorance to learn everything; she is skilled in concealment. Where great ladies of old flaunted affairs, today’s women arrange passions with artful restraint—notes and rests, pauses and sharps. A weak woman fears compromising love, husband, or children; name, position, and fortune no longer shield her, and aristocracy no longer protects. Lacking the old grandeur that could crush, the modern lady masters Jesuitical compromises and moves between propriety and anonymous passion. She fears servants as an Englishwoman fears divorce trials; free at a ball and charming on a walk, she is a slave at home—independent only in privacy or theory. Her task is to preserve her position as a lady.

Blondet sketched the desperate in-betweenness of a woman repudiated by her husband and reduced to a meager allowance: neither wife, maid, nor townswoman—adrift, a chattel. The Carmelites will not accept a married woman; it would be bigamy. Will her lover still desire her? The perfect lady may inspire calumny, but not the coarse slander of past times. Blondet argued that the perfect lady is a product of an age in which everything is transitional and indistinct; great figures fade into the background and distinction becomes merely personal. Near a throne it is impossible, even by twenty-five, to acquire the nuanced arts—maneuvering of tone, silence, that angelic mischief—that made the great lady of the past.

Mademoiselle des Touches asked where the female author fits: perfect lady or woman comme il faut? Blondet quipped that a female author without genius is a woman comme il n’en faut pas, a remark attributed to Napoleon, which prompted Canalis to defend Napoleon against jealousy toward literary genius and to offer an expansive, paradoxical portrait of him.

Canalis described Napoleon as a man of contradictions: folded arms yet constant action, concentrated and caustic, a prodigious will who spread armed civilization without settling it. He could conquer illness by will yet was fated to die in bed; he had a mind of law and a mind of arms, foresight and blindness to his fall. Capricious in politics, he mixed grand calculations with petty mercies—spared three heads whose deaths might have saved the Empire. Nature placed a heart in his brazen frame: mirthful and tender among women at midnight, and the next morning manipulating Europe like a child at play. He loved both tawdriness and simplicity, had no refined taste yet protected the arts, improvised empires and codes, and ultimately sought to make Europe French—leaving brilliance without permanence. He combined action and thought, used subordinates like Desaix and Fouché, and in moments of power exercised both despotism and justice; to Canalis, these qualities made him a true king.

De Marsay summed him up as “all despotism and all justice at the right moments.” Joseph Bridau interrupted to note that the company’s conversation was priceless—like music whose charm could not be repeated for money.

The Princesse de Cadignan defended contemporary women. Despite an era that makes everything small, people still love great passions; changing fashions do not alter the human heart. There is beautiful devotion and sublime suffering without the old public spectacle. Modern women—Madame Récamier, the Marquise d’Espard, Taglioni, Malibran—have their equivalents among historical beauties and artists. Though grocers govern and there is no unified style, posterity will still find our women proportionate and our poets able. The Comte de Vandenesse agreed: historians will find many Mme de Sévignés among Parisian women; Frenchwomen will remain “the woman among women.” Blondet’s type could, when needed, be heroic; the petty faults of contemporary women are rendered poetic by the larger perils of the age. Providence often compensates apparent lightness with overwhelming effects, Vandenesse said.

The women teased Vandenesse and praised General Montriveau, who, urged by the ladies, confessed to having been involuntarily the cause of a terrible disaster during the 1812 campaign—a story he told with Doctor Bianchon present because it concerned will and mind.

Montriveau’s tale: on his second campaign he embraced danger like a reckless young artillery lieutenant. At the Berezina the army had broken discipline: a chaotic migration of men of many nations. He found, beyond the marshes of Zembin, a wretched Polish farm—one room with a partitioned forage corner—where life showed by evening smoke. A table was set; officers and a woman ate potatoes, some horseflesh, and frozen beetroots. Artillery captains welcomed him; the straw on the floor promised rest. Among them sat his old colonel, an extraordinary Italian of strength, charm, and furious temper.

The colonel was tall, powerful, with abundant black curls; his small hands and aquiline nose became white with rage. His voice rolled gutturally and his fits of fury were famous. Yet when calm his blue eyes were sweet. He commanded by force of character rather than gallantry, capable of sublimity but consumed by jealous affections and honor.

Montriveau recalled another episode of the colonel’s violence: two artillery regiments met on a narrow road; after an insult to a captain the Italian colonel sprang, drew his sword, and split his rival colonel’s skull in two seconds. The men recoiled.

At the farmhouse, a captain sat near the door and his wife—Rosina, a pale but still-charming Sicilian—sat facing the colonel. Rosina, dark with almond eyes and a small mouth, had not lost grace despite poverty. Her husband, a Piedmontese gentleman, was phlegmatic but cruel beneath the mask. The colonel’s attention to Rosina had been plain. After the meal he called “Rosina?” and slept in the hay; his intent was understood. Rosina made a slight, indignant gesture but stayed. The colonel called again more brutal; Rosina, pale, rose and went behind them to him. The room fell silent; Montriveau began to laugh, others joined, the husband challenged, younger men joked the awkwardness away, and they all lay down and slept, pretending normalcy.

Next morning they set off, each seeking his own survival. Some distance away, many men rejoined out of necessity. From a knoll they saw the farmhouse barricaded and in flames, noises like a roar, mingled with feeble screams and the pungent smell of burning. A few yards behind, the captain they had seen earlier approached to rejoin the caravan. He pointed to his breast with his right forefinger and, waving his left toward the fire, said, “Son’io” (I am the one). The men said nothing; they walked on in silence. Montriveau left motives to their imagination: perhaps, in revenge for the colonel’s affront or for other private reasons, the captain had set his home ablaze, sacrificing shelter and occupants rather than bear the insult. The scene—a mix of private honor, revenge, and collective self-interest—left a somber image of men moving on amid horror and selfishness. “There is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep,” de Marsay remarked; Madame de Montcornet said the picture would haunt her dreams.

Conversation lightened briefly with an English jest about foils, but returned to tragic intimacy. De Marsay called on Doctor Bianchon to recount a noble death he had witnessed: the Duchess’s end. Bianchon described being called to a consumption case in which he and the Duke spent the night by the dying woman’s pillow. The Duchess, having taken the sacrament the day before, was very thin but had a sublime facial outline; her pallor made her skin seem like luminous porcelain, her bright eyes calm and expressive. The Duke, asleep more than might please a man of his power, had been absent a little; awakening, he sat by her side. The Duchess, reaching out in a faintly agitated voice, took his hand and said, “My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?” Then she died looking at him. Bianchon noted the scene’s majesty and tenderness; Mademoiselle des Touches observed the story left a sweet, deep impression.

Thus the company’s talk ranged from ironic social anatomy to grand historical paradox and private tragedy: Blondet’s satire of the modern “perfect lady”; Canalis’s encomium to Napoleon’s contradictory genius; the Princesse’s defense of contemporary women’s greatness and Vandenesse’s praise of their historical role; Montriveau’s wartime moral tableau—the farmhouse burning—and Bianchon’s quiet noble deathbed scene.

PARIS, June 1839–42.
