In Paris, balls often split into two gatherings: an official party of superficial pleasantries and a more intimate follow-up where genuine conversation and laughter flourish. The latter, often hosted by women like Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches, preserves the old spirit of French wit and permits free-flowing ideas and camaraderie.

One evening at Mademoiselle des Touches' salon, notable figures including the Minister de Marsay gathered for lively discourse. The charged atmosphere showcased Parisian conversation: anecdotes and confessions wove a vivid social tapestry. Conversation turned to de Marsay's early experiences with love—particularly a passionate affair with a woman six years his senior. He recalled youthful infatuation marked by intense devotion and secrecy; to conceal their relationship he kept a façade of indifference. Their careful dance of appearances and emotions—riding past her carriage and exchanging silent signals through the flowers she carried—thrived on mutual admiration and the thrill of concealment, while he remained blind to possible betrayal.

A turning point came when he fell ill and, after canceling a meeting by note, nevertheless visited her and saw her with another man, which ignited a storm of jealousy. Yet her calm demeanor and tender concern briefly soothed his doubts when they met. His narrative then darkened as he grappled with betrayal and felt a profound internal shift—the end of innocence and the rise of a more cynical view of love. He reflected on revenge and passion’s complexities, ultimately asserting that true love should transcend the desire for vengeance. De Marsay's story resonated with his audience, prompting reflection on trust’s fragility and love’s transformative power and leaving a lasting impression.

Cured of my cold and my idealized love, I set out with a charming heroine whose beauty sharply contrasted with that of my deceptive angel. I avoided quarrels with this clever woman, suspecting that true love might not equal the pleasures of her practiced deceit; refined hypocrisy, I thought, could pass for virtue.

I visited a hair-worker in the Rue Boucher to have a keepsake made for my new angel. He monopolized hair mementos and displayed work beyond fairy tales, explaining fashionable uses of hair in linen and accessories. When I recognized a handkerchief he had embroidered, my doubts about the sincerity of women’s affections hardened. I left still fond of pleasure but skeptical about love.

Two months later I was in Charlotte’s boudoir, holding her hand as we explored sentiment. In a tender moment I sought to draw out her most beguiling falsehoods. Charlotte professed love, declaring I was the only man in her world and that she had sought ways to bind me to her forever.

When I bluntly asked, “When are you to marry the Duke?” her startled reaction betrayed guilt. She feigned astonishment, then, as I persisted and suggested she should marry him because he was rich and pious, she fell to her knees swearing before God that she loved no one but me. I admitted my doubts; she immediately turned the charge on me, saying I no longer loved her. She tried to escape, but curiosity made her ask what I had seen. I told her I had spotted her attending mass with the Duke several times; she dismissed it as jealousy.

I argued that marrying the Duke would elevate her and that he, being virtuous, would expect a serious wife. Charlotte, weeping, lamented that if I had spoken sooner we might have been happy. I maintained it was too late; she had gone too far with the Duke. She fretted over the Duke’s suspicions; I suggested he likely had none, though she would need to prove her devotion. As she paced, her demeanor shifted: she extended her hand, praised my nobility and charm, and vowed never to forget me.

Her tactics were admirable; I played the distressed lover. Then she asked what would become of me, and I disclosed advances from another woman. This shocked Charlotte, who accused me of unfaithfulness; I countered that she had too much wit for her heart to suffer. She declared disillusionment with love, claiming women could never be truly loved. I acknowledged her anger but argued treating love as a solemn social bond was like romanticizing an actress. She accused me of betrayal; I feigned submission and asked if she would remember her grievances once she became a duchess. Her bitterness confirmed she still felt wronged, and I realized I had successfully detached myself. I had studied the lives of successful men with women and believed I had mastered the art of retreat.

The women in the room sympathized with Charlotte, while de Marsay wore a slight smile. Conversation turned to the decline of social distinctions. Lady Dudley observed that women often did not see marriages as acts of inconstancy. De Marsay lamented the erosion of aristocracy: duchesses and marquises were disappearing, and the Civil Code had leveled hierarchies. Blondet noted that aristocracy now often began with viscountesses; de Marsay thought countesses might survive in a reduced form. He reflected that the last great ladies had vanished with the vanished splendors of the previous century, and the Code had inadvertently created a new class of women.

The Comte de Vandenesse remarked that under the current structure anyone could claim nobility; Lord Dudley added that property had not been organized, hastening the aristocracy’s decay. Joseph Bridau declared that the beauty of womanhood had dimmed and that the great lady’s fan was broken. Daniel d’Arthez observed the aristocracy’s retreat left behind women who once might have shaped society. The talk turned to the modern “perfect lady,” a product of the new era: she embodied good taste and grace but lacked the grandeur of former great ladies.

Mademoiselle des Touches questioned whether this was progress, while Madame de Nucingen argued that a woman’s worth was no longer tied solely to lineage but to character and presentation. Count Adam Laginski asked what defined a “perfect lady.” Émile Blondet described her as a woman of effortless elegance and refinement, who commands respect and admiration—graceful and poised across social settings.

The discussion underscored the complexities of modern femininity: the “perfect lady” must navigate societal expectations with skill and charm, blending beauty and intellect to captivate those around her, even as the old orders and grandeurs fade.

“Now, you will not talk for half an hour with a bourgeoise without her mentioning her husband,” Blondet observed gravely. “Yet a married lady will hide her husband so skillfully you might never find him. You may only see her glance at a middle-aged man with a decoration who bows and goes; she has ordered her carriage and departs.

“You are not the rose, but you have been with the rose, and you go to bed under a golden canopy of a delightful dream that may linger even after Sleep has opened the ivory gates of dreams.

“At home she sees no one before four; she keeps you waiting. Her house is tasteful; her luxury is daily and renewed. There are no dusty glass shades or rags—warm stairs, flowers everywhere—flowers being the only permissible gift, and only from certain people since they mean a promise. Fashionable trifles are scattered, but not museum-fashion. She sits by the fire in a low chair and does not rise to greet you. At the ball she was our creditor; at home she owes you the pleasure of her wit. These are her arts. She seeks in you someone to expand her sphere, an object for her attention. To lure you into her drawing-room she is enchantingly charming. This shows how isolated women are and why they want a little world where they can shine like constellations. Conversation tends to generalities.

“Yes,” de Marsay agreed, “you have found the flaw of our age. The epigram—an entire volume in a word—no longer attacks people or things but petty events that fade quickly.”

“Hence,” Blondet went on, “a lady’s intelligence, if she has any, lies in casting doubt on everything. Here is the contrast between two women: the townswoman is virtuous, the lady hesitates and wrestles with virtue. That uncertainty is one of the last graces left her. She hardly goes to church but will discuss religion; if you show free thought, she’ll try to convert you with a stock of platitudes understood by these women: ‘For shame! I thought you had too much sense to attack religion. Society totters, and you deprive it of support. Religion is property and the future of our children! Individualism is the disease of the age, religion the only remedy; it unites families your laws sunder,’ and so on. She then glides into a neo-Christian, politically tinged discourse that is neither Catholic nor Protestant—but very moral—a muddle of modern doctrines at odds with each other.”

The women laughed at Blondet’s satire.

“This explanation, Count Adam,” Blondet told the Pole, “shows that the ‘perfect lady’ embodies both intellectual and political muddle, surrounded by flashy, ephemeral products of an industry that replaces itself. When you leave her you think: she certainly has superior ideas! You believe it because she has probed your heart delicately and asked your secrets; she feigns ignorance to learn everything. There are things she never knows even when she does. You alone feel uneasy, knowing nothing of her heart. The great ladies of old flaunted affairs; today’s lady arranges her passions like a musical score, with rests and notes, avoiding anything that would compromise love, her husband, or her children’s future. Name, place, and fortune no longer shield her; the aristocracy no longer protects. She lacks the lofty antagonism of the past; instead of crushing, she risks being crushed. She masters Jesuitical compromises between propriety and anonymous passion. She fears her servants as an Englishwoman fears a divorce trial. So free at a ball, charming in public, she is a slave at home; she is never independent except in secrecy or in theory. Her task is to remain a lady.

“For today, a repudiated woman, reduced to a meager allowance without a carriage, luxury, or opera box is neither wife nor maid nor townswoman; she drifts into chattelhood. The Carmelites will not take a married woman; it would be bigamy. Would her lover want her then? That is the question. Thus your perfect lady may invite calumny but not slander.”

“It is all horribly true,” said the Princesse de Cadignan.

“And so,” Blondet continued, “our ‘perfect lady’ stands between English hypocrisy and eighteenth-century frankness—a bastard system symptomatic of an age where nothing that emerges resembles what has vanished, where transition leads nowhere and everything is graduated; the great figures fade into the background, and distinction becomes merely personal. I doubt any woman, even near a throne, can, before twenty-five, acquire that encyclopedic knowledge of trifles, the practice of maneuvering, the small yet important arts—the musical tones and harmony of dress, angelic mischief and innocent cunning, speech and silence, wit and obtuseness, diplomacy and ignorance—that make a perfect lady.”

“And where,” Mademoiselle des Touches asked, “would you place the female author? Is she a perfect lady, a woman comme il faut?”

“When she has no genius, she is a woman comme il n’en faut pas,” Blondet replied, looking in a way that might praise Camille Maupin. “That epigram is not mine but Napoleon’s.”

“You need not hate Napoleon for that,” Canalis interposed. “He was weakly jealous of literary genius—he had his faults. Who can explain Napoleon? A man with arms folded who did everything—the greatest force, most concentrated, most caustic; a singular will who spread armed civilization without fixing it. He could do everything because he willed everything: a prodigious phenomenon of will, conquering illness by battle yet destined to die sick in bed; a man with a code and a sword in his brain, word and deed; a clear spirit seeing everything but his own fall; a capricious politician risking men, yet sparing three diplomats—Talleyrand, Pozzo, Metternich—whose deaths might have saved the Empire. Nature gave him, as a rare gift, a heart in a bronze frame; mirthful and kind among women at midnight, the next morning he moved Europe as a girl might splash water. Hypocritical and generous; liking tawdriness and simplicity; without taste, yet protecting the arts; truly great in everything by temperament: Caesar at twenty-five, Cromwell at thirty. Then, like my grocer in Père Lachaise, a good husband and father. He improvised public works, empires, kings, codes, verses, a romance—with breadth over precision. He aimed to make all Europe France; having changed laws of gravitation, he left us poorer. He who took an empire by his name lost it at its frontier in blood. A man of thought and action who understood Desaix and Fouché.”

“All despotism and all justice at the right moments. The true king!” de Marsay declared.

“Ah! what a pleasure to digest what you talk about,” Baron de Nucingen remarked.

“But is our banquet of talk a common one?” Joseph Bridau asked. “If you paid for the charms of conversation as for music or dancing, your fortune would be inadequate! There is no second performance of the same flash of wit.”

“And are we really so diminished as these gentlemen think?” the Princesse de Cadignan asked the women with a skeptical smile. “Because under a regime that makes everything small you prefer small dishes, small rooms, small pictures, small articles, small newspapers, small books, does it follow that women have grown smaller? Why should the human heart change because you change your coat? Passions remain the same in all ages. I know of beautiful devotion and sublime sufferings that lack the publicity—the glory—that once lent luster to some women’s errors. But even without saving a king one is no less an Agnès Sorel. Do you think our Marquise d’Espard is inferior to Madame Doublet or Madame du Deffand, in whose rooms so much evil was done? Is not Taglioni a match for Camargo? Malibran for Saint-Huberti? Are our poets not superior to those of the eighteenth century? If now, under merchant rule, we lack a defining style, did the Empire not have its stamp as Louis XV’s age did, and was its splendor not fabulous? Have the sciences lost anything?”

“I agree, madame; the women of this age are truly great,” Comte de Vandenesse replied. “When posterity looks back, will not Madame Récamier appear as fine as any past beauty? We have made so much history that historians will be lacking. The age of Louis XIV had but one Madame de Sévigné; we have a thousand in Paris who write better privately than she published. Whether called ‘perfect lady’ or great lady, she will always be the woman among women.

“Blondet painted the fascinations of a woman of the day; but when needed, this creature who bristles or shows off, who echoes Mr. This and Mr. That, can be heroic. Your faults, mesdames, are more poetic because they are surrounded by greater perils. I have seen much—perhaps too late—but when the illegality of your feelings might be excused, I always saw what you call Providence inevitably overwhelming those we deem light women.”

“I hope,” Madame de Vandenesse said, “that we can be great in other ways—”

“Oh, let the Comte preach to us!” Madame de Serizy cried.

“With all the more reason because he has preached by example,” the Baronne de Nucingen added.

“On my honor!” General de Montriveau said, “in all the dramas—a word you like,” he added, glancing at Blondet—“where God’s finger has been visible, the most frightful I ever knew was almost by my act—”

“Tell us!” Lady Barimore cried. “I love to shudder!”

“It is the taste of a virtuous woman,” de Marsay observed, looking at Lord Dudley’s lovely daughter.

“During the 1812 campaign,” General de Montriveau began, “I was the involuntary cause of a terrible disaster which may interest Doctor Bianchon, since while you study the body, you care for the mind; it may help solve will’s problems.

“I was on my second campaign, enjoying danger like a young foolish lieutenant. At the Beresina the army lost discipline and obedience; it was a medley of nations making their way south. Men would drive off a general in rags if he brought no wood or food. After crossing the river, disorder worsened. I had wandered from Zembin’s marshes seeking shelter. Finding none, I finally saw a wretched little Polish farm—think of the meanest wooden houses of Lower Normandy. These dwellings were a single room, one end partitioned as a forage store.

“In twilight I saw faint smoke; hoping to find kinder comrades, I approached. Inside, the table was laid. Several officers and a woman—common enough—were eating potatoes, some horseflesh grilled, frozen beetroots. I recognized artillery captains from my regiment. I was welcomed with shouts that would have amazed me on the other side of the Beresina; but at that moment the cold was less keen; my comrades had food, warmth, and straw; we asked for little then. I sat on a bundle of straw.

“At the table’s end, by the door to the hay partition, sat my old colonel—one of the most extraordinary men I met among the mixed ranks. He was Italian. When the South is truly fine, it is sublime. His fairness under lamplight was exquisite. My colonel, like many officers borrowed from Eugene’s army, was tall—well proportioned, perhaps stout, but prodigiously powerful and greyhound-like. His abundant black curls contrasted with a white, almost feminine complexion; small hands, a shapely foot, a pleasant mouth, and an aquiline nose that would turn white with anger, which happened often. His irascibility was beyond belief; no one could remain calm near him. He had taken a fancy to me and thought all I did was right. When angry his brow knit into a horseshoe delta; the muscles quivered and his strength became boundless.

“He spoke with a guttural roll that, though sometimes a grace, conveyed incredible force when excited. When calm his blue eyes were angelic and his brow charming. In parade he eclipsed others; even d’Orsay paled before him. Passion thrives on contrast, so you need not ask whether he was irresistible to women—he could be—but singularly he was not a lady-killer.

“To show his violence: we once were dragging guns up a narrow road when another artillery regiment crossed us. Their colonel wanted our captain to give way; our captain refused. Their nearest gun wheel struck our captain’s right leg, breaking it and throwing him off his horse. Our Italian colonel, nearby, sensed a quarrel; he galloped among the guns, risking everything, and reached the captain shouting ‘Help!’ No, our colonel was no longer human—foam rose, he roared. Incapable of speaking, he beckoned his opponent into the wood and drew his sword. In two seconds the other colonel lay with his skull split. The soldiers of the opponent’s regiment backed away—yes, quickly.

“The injured captain had an Italian wife, a beautiful Sicilian from Messina, who was not indifferent to our colonel. This aggravated his rage; he felt pledged to protect the husband as he would the woman.

“In the Zembin hovel where I was received that night the captain sat opposite me; his wife sat at the other end facing the Colonel. Rosina was small, dark, with almond eyes full of Southern fire; though dust-covered and ragged, exhausted, she retained womanly graces—rose mouth, white teeth, an outline suggesting love despite misery. Rosina’s frame was fragile-looking but wiry. Her husband, a Piedmontese gentleman, had an ironical simplicity on his face that betrayed a quiet cruelty; a thin, restless underlip that turned down at the corners made me uneasy.

“The conversation was flat when I arrived. We exchanged campaign misfortunes. After the Colonel finished he wiped his mustache, gave a black look at the Italian woman, said ‘Rosina?’ and went into the hay to sleep. The meaning was clear. The young wife’s gesture expressed the annoyance she could not show—an offense to her dignity and to her husband—but also a presentiment. She stayed seated.

“A minute later, apparently when the Colonel was snug, he repeated, ‘Rosina?’ The tone was more brutally demanding. Rosina turned pale, rose, passed behind us, and went to him. We all sat in absolute silence; I began to laugh, and then they laughed too. ‘Tu ridi?—you laugh?’ said the husband. ‘On my honor, old comrade,’ I replied, serious again, ‘I confess I was wrong; I ask your pardon a thousand times, and if you are not satisfied with my apologies I am ready to give you satisfaction.’ ‘Oh! it is not you who are wrong; it is I!’ he answered coldly. We all lay down and slept.

“Next morning each set out alone, with the selfishness that made our rout one of the most horrible dramas of self-seeking, melancholy, and horror. Yet about seven or eight hundred paces away we met again and walked on together like geese. Necessity drove us all.

“At a knoll where we could still see the farmhouse we heard sounds like lions and bulls—a noise like no known cry—and a woman’s feeble scream. We looked around in terror; we no longer saw the house, only a huge bonfire. The farmhouse was barricaded and ablaze. Swirls of smoke carried hoarse cries and a pungent smell. A few yards behind, the captain quietly joined our caravan; we gazed at him in silence; he pointed to his breast with his right forefinger and, waving his left toward the fire, said, ‘Son’io.’ We all walked on without asking a word.”

“There is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep,” de Marsay remarked.

“It would be frightful to leave with that vision in mind,” Madame de Montcornet said. “I shall dream of it—”

“And what was the punishment of Monsieur de Marsay’s ‘First’?” Lord Dudley asked, smiling.

“When the English jest, their foils have the buttons on,” Blondet replied.

“Monsieur Bianchon can tell us, for he saw her dying,” de Marsay said, turning to me.

“Yes,” I answered; “and her death was one of the most beautiful I ever witnessed. The Duke and I had spent the night by the dying woman’s pillow; pulmonary consumption in its last stage left no hope; she had taken the sacrament the day before. The Duke fell asleep. The Duchess woke about four, and with a friendly smile signed to me to let him rest while she faced death. She had grown incredibly thin, but her face kept its sublime outline; the pallor made her skin like porcelain lit from within. Her bright eyes contrasted with this languid beauty; her countenance showed expressive calm and pity for the Duke, a lofty tenderness that death seemed only to enlarge. The silence was complete; the room, softly lamp-lit, resembled any sickroom at the hour of death.

“At that moment the clock struck. The Duke awoke, despairing at having slept through a few of the last moments; his gesture might have been misread by any but the dying woman. A busy statesman, always thinking of France’s interests, had strange odd ways that make genius seem mad to the unseeing. He sat by her, looked fixedly. The dying woman took his hand weakly and said in a low, agitated voice, ‘My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?’ Then she died looking at him.”

“The stories the doctor tells always leave a deep impression,” Comte de Vandenesse remarked.

“But a sweet one,” Mademoiselle des Touches said, rising.
