The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once a dark, winding lane by the Hôtel de Ville ending at the Rue Martroi, was shut by a turnstile until 1823. Less than six feet wide at its broadest, it often filled with gutter water and mud; summer sunlight touched it briefly while dampness lingered.

Residents trimmed their lamps at five in June and kept them burning through winter. A passerby approaching the Marais felt as if moving through cellars, as with many old Paris streets. The corner house at Rue du Tourniquet and Rue de la Tixeranderie bore its age: iron rings from chains used for public safety still protruded from the walls.

Built to fight dampness, the house had cellars raised above ground and an entrance reached by three steps. The door formed a closed arch topped by a keystone carved with a female head. Three high windows, barred with iron, admitted light to a small dwelling. Curious passersby could glimpse two beds draped in green serge and an old woman tending a brazier, preparing the simple stew typical of porters’ wives. The kitchen-dining room was sparsely furnished but kept clean and thrifty.

The old woman, pale and wrinkled, seemed as much a fixture as the walls. She sat by the hearth with a look that mixed mischief and resignation while her daughter worked at an embroidery frame. The mother, with slow fingers and antiquated spectacles, glanced occasionally out the window; the daughter bent over her work with a modest, downcast gaze.

Their modest home mirrored the lives of working-class women in Paris. Despite the surroundings, the girl retained youthful charm—rosy cheeks and bright eyes suggesting a spirit not yet dimmed by hardship. The mother speculated about passersby and dreamed of a better future for her daughter, while the girl remained absorbed in her embroidery.

One August day in 1815 the mother noticed a new face among the regular passersby: a tall, thin man in black whose solemn manner intrigued her. She guessed he was a clerk from the Prefect’s office, and her excitement was obvious. Caroline, roused from indifference by her mother’s remarks, barely saw him before he passed on.

The old woman eagerly awaited his return, convinced he would come again. When he did, Caroline caught sight of him and felt an unfamiliar sympathy, noticing suffering marked in his face. It was the first time a passerby had pierced her usual indifference; normally she was absorbed by work and daily routine. As she resumed her embroidery, she found herself looking forward to seeing him again, intrigued by the possibility that this stranger might become a connection able to alter the course of her life.

For over two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved erratically, not always passing the Rue du Tourniquet at the same hour. Madame Crochard noticed his distracted air—often lost in thought and rarely noticing her or her daughter, Caroline. One mid-September morning Caroline’s lively presence caught him; he paused to watch her at the window. That moment began a silent connection of furtive glances suggesting a deeper understanding.

In the weeks that followed he began to pass twice daily, and their brief encounters became a routine both anticipated. Their quiet exchanges conveyed mutual concern: Caroline sensed when he was troubled, and he knew when she was anxious about work. Yet both remained cautious, fearing a closer bond might bring complications or heartbreak.

Madame Crochard, frustrated by her daughter’s indecision, grew displeased with the Gentleman, who had once drawn her benign attention. As winter approached, the hardships of their life pressed on Caroline, and the Gentleman observed how it wore on her spirit. One night he overheard a conversation between mother and daughter that revealed severe financial strain. Touched, he secretly left money for them; Caroline found it the next day, and a moment of silent gratitude passed between them.

In early May Caroline suggested a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—more sombre than usual—unexpectedly accompanied them. On the carriage ride Madame Crochard pretended to sleep, giving Caroline and the Gentleman space for a light-hearted conversation that unveiled their growing fondness. The countryside’s beauty and the warmth of their exchange cheered him, and he began to view Caroline differently.

Their bond deepened as they traded personal stories: Caroline spoke of family sacrifices and her mother’s efforts; the Gentleman, later called Roger, was drawn to her honesty and resilience. The day in Montmorency brimmed with laughter and joy, a sharp contrast to their earlier somber interactions. As evening approached Caroline voiced the happiness she felt, prompting Roger to consider the possibility of joy beyond that day. Preparing to leave, they playfully held hands—a discreet, significant gesture Madame Crochard, though pretending not to notice, was aware of. The moment marked an important step in their relationship, full of unspoken promises and hope for a brighter future together.

The adventures of Roger and Caroline continue in modern Paris, where new apartments suit honeymooners. In a pristine building on Rue Taitbout, Caroline has lived for over a month in a charming second-floor apartment, tastefully furnished and reflecting English-influenced trends.

Her bedroom has gray hangings trimmed with green silk, comfortable seating, and a writing table for love letters. A bronze clock showing Love crowning Psyche and a Gothic-patterned carpet complete the cozy scene. As she readies for Roger's visit, her hairdresser flatters her, reminding her of Roger's admiration.

On a chilly September day in 1816, Caroline dons a green grenadine gown trimmed with chinchilla and steps onto the balcony, heart racing as she watches for Roger. After a quarter-hour of waiting her impatience grows, then lifts when she finally sees him approaching.

Roger arrives and they embrace passionately, rejoicing in their reunion after three days apart. He then tells her he must attend a meeting with the Attorney-General, disappointing Caroline, who playfully protests the thought of enjoying the theatre without him and voices her deep affection.

Despite the setback, Caroline focuses on the joy of being with Roger. They share tender moments; she declares her wish to take the name Caroline de Bellefeuille, signaling her commitment, and tells him she plans to use her earnings to help the poor, emphasizing her gratitude for their life together.

Her excitement rises as she plays a sonata, but Roger, mindful of his duties, prepares to leave. They exchange a playful farewell; she watches him drive away, savoring their parting and longing for his return.

Five years after Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille settled on Rue Taitbout, we find her in a peaceful moment with her four-year-old son Charles, playing with a rocking horse. Caroline, now twenty-four, bears a beauty shaped by contentment. She has embraced motherhood, dedicating herself to nurturing her children and cultivating their taste for goodness and beauty. She enjoys simple pleasures and remains indifferent to luxury or social ambition.

Rocking her infant daughter Eugénie, Caroline muses on the past, particularly the happy visits to Bellefeuille with Roger. Their love has prospered in the quiet simplicity of their life together, yet Caroline wonders why Roger seeks happiness in secrecy.

On May 6, 1822—the anniversary of a pivotal day—Caroline prepares for dinner, anticipating Roger’s arrival. He brings a document detailing Eugénie’s marriage portion, sparking a discussion about unequal financial provisions for their children. Roger argues that Charles, as a boy, should learn self-reliance, while Eugénie, as a girl, ought to be provided for more generously.

Their evening is warm and lively, ending with a lantern show for Charles. When Roger must leave, Caroline feels the familiar pang of separation; he promises three days off, but duty soon intervenes.

Shortly after this anniversary, Caroline rushes to her mother, Madame Crochard, who is gravely ill. The widow’s acquaintances—unaware that Caroline is her daughter—have summoned a priest for last rites. Caroline arrives amid confusion and deceit: the old women hope to profit from Madame Crochard’s wealth. Despite their scheming, Caroline’s devoted care shines through.

In her final moments, Madame Crochard regrets their estrangement and reveals that the priest pressed her to confess about Caroline’s benefactor—hinting at a web of relationships and secrets to come.

To clarify these connections, the narrative turns back to events before Madame Crochard’s death. In November 1805, a young barrister, Monsieur de Granville, finds himself in a delicate position after leaving a gathering at the High Chancellor’s. An unexpected opportunity to advance his career appears, aided by his uncle’s influence.

Granville receives a letter from his father urging a swift return: a proposed marriage to Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems could secure his future. The letter details the financial advantages despite the Bontems family’s social stigma. Torn between ambition and affection, Granville recalls a childhood fondness for Angélique, whom he has not seen in years.

He travels the next day to Bayeux, where his father escorts him to the Bontems’ home. The household atmosphere is oppressive, dominated by the widow’s strict piety. Granville dreads the marriage, feeling his father’s pressure keenly.

At a church service he glimpses Angélique; her beauty revives his feelings. Their brief exchange rekindles his attachment, even as family constraints weigh on him. As the service ends, his impatience drives him to approach her, setting the stage for a reunion that may reshape their lives.

The intertwined stories of Caroline and Granville reveal the tensions of love, duty, and social expectation, as both confront personal desire and familial obligation.

For about two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he visited her dreary drawing-room and watched Angelique as she marked her trousseau. She never led religious conversation; when Granville playfully handled her rosary or mocked practices, she quietly returned it to its bag with a serene smile. She firmly believed in the Church, insisting a mother must have a religion and questioning how one could judge between disbelievers and God. Granville, captivated by her conviction, sometimes felt tempted to adopt her beliefs, mistaking his desire for her as love. Angelique, reconciling feeling with duty, left him unsure of his own sentiments. He was grateful to Madame Bontems for instilling religion in Angelique, thinking it would sustain her through life’s trials.

At the marriage-contract signing Madame Bontems required Granville to promise to respect Angelique’s religious practices and freedom of conscience; the Abbe Fontanon approved. Shortly after, they married and moved to Paris, where Granville became public prosecutor. Angelique persuaded him to take a spacious ground-floor apartment in the Marais, near church and chapel, arguing it would be good for future children. Granville, who had preferred a more fashionable district, yielded. He concentrated on his study and left the decoration to Angelique, who delighted in furnishing their new home. He soon found the apartment cold and solemn, mirroring Angelique’s austere upbringing.

Though he tried to understand her choices, Granville grew increasingly frustrated by the lack of warmth and charm at home and attributed the sober decor to her Bayeux background. He attempted to comfort Angelique, believing happiness would come from her love and gentleness rather than material comforts. From April through the winter their life seemed outwardly harmonious: Granville kept busy with work, accompanied her to church on Sundays, and failed to notice many of the strict religious observances she enforced—Lenten meals and other austerities she passed off as carelessness.

In November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux, who had once guided Angelique, arrived in Paris alarmed by what he perceived as moral decline and sought to reclaim her from a lax life. Angelique adopted stricter practices that led to misunderstandings about social obligations; she made frequent excuses to avoid events, especially balls, which infuriated Granville. After a tense evening he confronted her about her withdrawn demeanor and dress, urging her to fulfill her social role. Arguments escalated; Angelique cited prohibitions from her director against attending balls, and Granville accused her priest of overstepping.

Their dispute became theological and culminated in Angelique writing to the Pope. The Pope’s reply sided with Granville, stating a wife should accompany her husband even if it risked compromising her virtue. Rather than reconciling them, this deepened the rift: Angelique and her director dismissed the Pope’s advice. Frustrated by her strict observances, Granville began asserting his own preferences—ordering meat daily despite her objections—and withdrew from social life, feeling weighed down by what he saw as her bigotry.

The household atmosphere grew oppressive and gloomy, affecting servants as well as family. Angelique’s beauty was overshadowed by austerity; duty replaced affection, and her devotion left little room for love. The children suffered under the strict regime, prompting Granville to send his sons to school early and resolve to marry off his daughters as soon as possible. Seeking solace in work, he tried to write a book on law while Angelique attempted to convert him back to her views. Their relationship deteriorated, leaving him increasingly alienated from his wife and their home, which had become a place of coldness and rigidity rather than warmth and companionship.

Between 1806 and 1825 the de Granville household experienced little outward drama, but a deep domestic estrangement. Madame de Granville, unchanged after losing her husband's affection, devoted herself to Masses and prayer in hopes of restoring their relationship. Granville, now a prominent judge, withdrew to the entresol of their house to avoid her; each morning a housekeeper shuttled messages between them while he habitually declined breakfast, citing urgent court business. Madame, thirty-five and looking older, waited to engage him with her narrow-minded remarks. When he refused the presidency of a provincial court, the Countess’s friends and her confessor suspected an illicit affair, a notion she found inconceivable.

In Lent of 1822 her health worsened under stress and a low fever, compounded by Granville’s indifference. She tried to be more affectionate, but her piety and bigotry often undermined her efforts. By late May, feeling better, she consulted the Abbe Fontanon, who delivered a crushing revelation: Granville had lived in sin with a concubine for seven years, fathering two children and squandering much of their fortune.

Stunned and divided between faith and betrayal, Madame struggled to accept the news. The Abbe urged forgiveness and patience, hoping Granville would one day be enlightened, but she was consumed by turmoil and left home determined to confront him.

At that moment Roger and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille were in a tense exchange: she declared her hatred for Roger’s wife, whom she blamed for their mutual unhappiness; Roger tried to reassure her and speak of a future together. Their conversation was interrupted when Madame de Granville, overcome, fainted. Upon reviving she confronted Granville, demanding to know whether he loved her and had been faithful. Granville retorted that she had sacrificed his happiness to piety and failed in her duty as a loving wife.

Their confrontation exposed the gulf between them. Granville argued that love requires sacrifice and understanding and accused her of neglecting the marital bond in favor of religious devotion. Madame clung to moral certainty, torn between jealousy and righteous anger. Granville admitted he had sought happiness elsewhere and acknowledged his affection for another, leaving her devastated. Despite her tears and entreaties, he remained firm: love could not be commanded or rekindled by will after years of neglect.

The exchange ended in a stark acknowledgment of a failed marriage. Madame de Granville was left to face the consequences of their choices and an uncertain future, stripped of illusions and options.

Early in December 1833, a white-haired, worn man, aged by grief rather than years, walked the Rue Gaillon at midnight. He paused before a modest two-story house; a dim light flickered through attic windows, some patched with paper. As he watched, a young man emerged. Recognizing him, the young man approached hesitantly.

“What, is it you, Monsieur le Président? Alone at this hour, so far from the Rue Saint-Lazare. Allow me to give you my arm. The pavement is so greasy this morning that if we do not hold each other up, we shall find it hard to escape a tumble.”

The Comte de Granville replied, “But, my dear sir, I am no more than fifty-five, unfortunately for me. A physician of your celebrity must know that at that age a man is still hale and strong.”

“Then you are in waiting on a lady, I suppose,” said Horace Bianchon. “You are not in the habit of going about Paris on foot. When a man keeps such fine horses—”

“Still, when I am not visiting in the evening, I commonly return from the Courts or the club on foot,” Granville answered.

“And with large sums of money about you, perhaps!” the doctor exclaimed. “It is a positive invitation to the assassin’s knife.”

“I am not afraid of that,” Granville said with melancholy indifference.

“Then do not stand about,” Bianchon urged, leading the Count toward the boulevard. “A little more and I shall believe you are bent on robbing me of your last illness, dying by some other hand than mine.”

“You caught me playing the spy,” Granville admitted. “For some time past I have observed at a window on the third floor of your house the shadow of a person who seems to work with heroic constancy.”

Granville paused, as if in pain. “I take as great an interest in that garret as a citizen of Paris must feel in the finishing of the Palais Royal.”

“Well,” Bianchon said eagerly, “I can tell you—”

“Tell me nothing,” Granville interrupted. “I would not give a centime to know whether the shadow that moves across that shabby blind is that of a man or a woman, nor whether the inhabitant of that attic is happy or miserable. Though I was surprised to see no one at work there this evening, I stopped to look solely for the pleasure of indulging in conjectures as numerous and as idiotic as those of idlers who see a building left half-finished. For nine years I have ceased to wonder that old men amuse themselves with growing flowers and planting trees; the events of life have taught them disbelief in all human affection. I grew old within a few days. I will no longer attach myself to any creature but to unreasoning animals, plants, or superficial things. I think more of Taglioni’s grace than of all human feeling. I abhor life and the world in which I live alone. Nothing, nothing can move or interest me.”

“But you have children?” Bianchon asked.

“My children!” Granville repeated bitterly. “Yes—my eldest daughter is the Comtesse de Vandenesse. The other will, through her sister’s connections, make a good match. My sons have succeeded; the Viscount was public prosecutor at Limoges and is now President of the Court at Orléans; the younger is public prosecutor in Paris. My children have their own cares, their own anxieties. If one of those hearts had been devoted to me, if one had tried to fill the void I have here,” he said, striking his breast, “that one would have failed in life, sacrificing it to me. And why should he? To bring sunshine into my few remaining years—and would he have succeeded? Might I not have accepted such generosity as a debt? But, doctor,” he added with deep irony, “it is not for nothing that we teach them arithmetic and how to count. At this moment perhaps they are waiting for my money.”

“O Monsieur le Comte, how could such an idea enter your head—you who are kind, friendly, and humane! If I were not myself a living proof of the benevolence you exercise so liberally—”

“To please myself,” Granville replied. “I pay for a sensation, as I would pay a pile of gold to recover the most childish illusion that would make my heart glow. I help my fellow-creatures for my own sake, just as I gamble; I look for gratitude from none. I should see you die without blinking; and I beg of you to feel the same with regard to me. The events of life have swept over my heart like the lavas of Vesuvius over Herculaneum. The town is there—dead.”

“Those who have brought a soul as warm and living as yours to such a pitch of indifference are indeed guilty!” Bianchon exclaimed.

“Say no more,” Granville said, shuddering with aversion.

“You have a malady which you ought to allow me to treat,” Bianchon insisted.

“What, do you know of a cure for death?” Granville cried irritably.

“I undertake, Monsieur le Comte, to revive the heart you believe to be frozen.”

“Are you a match for Talma, then?” Granville asked satirically.

“No, Monsieur le Comte. But Nature is as far above Talma as Talma is superior to me. Listen: the garret you are interested in is inhabited by a woman of about thirty, and her love is carried to fanaticism. The object of her adoration is a young man of pleasing appearance but endowed with every conceivable vice. This fellow is a gambler, and it is hard to say which he is more addicted to—wine or women; he has committed acts deserving punishment by law. To him, this unhappy woman sacrificed a life of ease, a man who worshiped her, and the father of her children.”

“What is wrong, Monsieur le Comte?” Granville asked.

“Nothing. Go on,” he replied.

“She has allowed him to squander a perfect fortune; she would give him the world if she had it; she works night and day; and many times she has seen the wretch she adores rob her even of the money saved for her children’s clothes and food. Only three days ago she sold her hair, the finest I ever saw; he came in, and she could not hide the gold piece quickly enough. For a smile, for a kiss, she gave up the price of a fortnight’s life and peace. Is it not dreadful, and yet sublime? But work is wearing her cheeks hollow. Her children’s crying has broken her heart; she is ill, and at this moment on her wretched bed. This evening they had nothing to eat; the children have not strength to cry; they were silent when I went up.”

Bianchon stood still. Granville, despite himself, put his hand into his waistcoat pocket.

“I can guess, my young friend, how it is that she is yet alive if you attend her,” Granville said.

“O poor soul!” Bianchon cried. “Who could refuse to help her? I only wish I were richer, for I hope to cure her of her passion.”

“But how can you expect me to pity a form of misery of which the joys to me would seem cheaply purchased with my whole fortune!” Granville exclaimed, withdrawing his hand empty of the notes Bianchon had assumed he was feeling for. “That woman feels; she is alive! Would not Louis XV. have given his kingdom to rise from the grave and have three days of youth and life? And is not that the history of thousands of dead men, thousands of sick men, thousands of old men?”

“Poor Caroline!” Bianchon lamented.

At the name Granville shuddered and grasped the doctor’s arm tightly.

“Her name is Caroline Crochard?” he asked, his voice breaking.

“Then you know her?” Bianchon replied, astonished.

“And the wretch’s name is Solvet. Ay, you have kept your word!” Granville exclaimed. “You have roused my heart to the most terrible pain it can suffer until it is dust. That emotion, too, is a gift from hell, and I always know how to pay those debts.”

They reached the corner of the Rue de la Chaussée-d’Antin. A night scavenger, with a shriveled face, stood by the curb.

“Do you ever pick up a thousand-franc note?” Granville asked.

“Now and then, master,” the scavenger answered.

“And you restore them?”

“It depends on the reward offered.”

“You’re the man for me.” Granville handed him a thousand-franc note. “Take this, but remember, I give it to you on condition of your spending it at the wineshop, getting drunk, fighting, beating your wife, blacking your friends’ eyes. That will give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist—perhaps to the police, the public prosecutor, the judge, and the prison warders. Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later.”

A draughtsman would need the pencil of Charlet and Callot, the brush of Teniers and Rembrandt to capture the night scene accurately.

“Now I have squared accounts with hell and had some pleasure for my money,” Granville said, pointing out the scavenger’s grotesque face to Bianchon, who stood stupefied. “As for Caroline Crochard—she may die of hunger and thirst, hearing the heartrending cries of her starving children, convinced of the baseness of the man she loves. I will not give a sou to rescue her; and because you have helped her, I will see you no more—”

Granville left Bianchon standing like a statue and walked briskly to the Rue Saint-Lazare, where a carriage waited at his door.

“Monsieur, your son, the attorney-general, came about an hour ago,” the servant said. “He is waiting for you in your bedroom.”

Granville dismissed the servant and entered the room. “What motive can be strong enough to require you to infringe the order I have given my children never to come to me unless I send for them?” he asked his son.

“Father,” the younger man replied tremulously, “I hope you will forgive me when you have heard me.”

“Your reply is proper,” Granville said. “Sit down,” he gestured to a chair. “But whether I walk up and down or take a seat, speak without heeding me.”

“Father,” the son continued, “this afternoon, at four o’clock, a very young man who was arrested in the house of a friend of mine, whom he had robbed, appealed to you. He says he is your son.”

“His name?” Granville asked hoarsely.

“Charles Crochard.”

“That will do,” Granville said, waving his hand imperiously.

He paced in silence; his son remained quiet.

“My son,” he began in a mild, fatherly voice, causing the young lawyer to start, “Charles Crochard spoke the truth. I am glad you came to me tonight, my good Eugène.” He handed him a bundle of banknotes. “Here is a considerable sum; use it as you see fit in this matter. I trust you implicitly and approve whatever arrangements you may make, now or later. Eugène, my dear son, kiss me. We part perhaps for the last time. I shall tomorrow crave my dismissal from the King, and I am going to Italy.

“Though a father owes no account of his life to his children, he is bound to bequeath to them the experience Fate sells him dearly—is it not a part of their inheritance? When you marry,” he continued, involuntarily shuddering, “do not undertake it lightly; that act is the most important of all which society requires of us. Remember to study the character of the woman who is to be your partner; consult me too, I will judge of her myself. A lack of union between husband and wife, from whatever cause, leads to terrible misfortune; sooner or later we are always punished for contravening the social law. But I will write to you on this subject from Florence. A father who presides over a supreme court of justice must not have to blush in the presence of his son. Good-bye.”
