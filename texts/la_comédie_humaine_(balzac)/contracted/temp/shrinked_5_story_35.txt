AN OLD MAID

CHAPTER I. ONE OF MANY CHEVALIERS DE VALOIS

Across France there were many Chevaliers de Valois, including one in Alençon who is the subject of this tale. Though sharing the name, these chevaliers mostly did not know one another; they accepted the Bourbon monarchy, acknowledging that Henri IV. took the throne because the Valois line had no male heir. The Alençon chevalier, like the others, was an elderly gentleman, tall and thin, whose youth in Paris and romantic pursuits had been interrupted by the Revolution.

Well regarded among the local aristocracy, he was known for manners, wit, and anecdotes from Louis XV’s reign. A regular at social gatherings, his charm and discretion made him a favorite. At fifty-eight he claimed to be fifty, preserving a youthful appearance despite age. He had a large nose and a pale complexion that reddened on one side from a self-explained “hot liver,” an excuse he used to justify his voracious appetite.

Though seemingly frail, the chevalier was vigorous and passionate; his face bore marks of a life lived fully. He groomed himself carefully, projecting an elegance that belied his years. His attire, though worn, was always clean, reflecting his commitment to aristocratic standards. He lived modestly in a rented room, dined out daily, and depended on a small annuity.

Beloved in Alençon for card-playing and amiability, he was a master of conversation, engaging with all ranks; his presence was sought at events. Despite his charm, curiosity swirled around his private life, including rumors of a secret marriage to a woman named Césarine.

Residing above Madame Lardot’s laundry, the chevalier balanced social engagement with discretion. His finances were precarious, yet he maintained dignity and the reputation of a gentleman by navigating provincial life with grace.

CHAPTER II. SUSANNAH AND THE ELDERS

One spring morning the chevalier was interrupted by Suzanne, a young girl from Madame Lardot’s laundry. She brought news that might bring both pleasure and pain, though she feigned indifference to his rumored link with Césarine. Ambitious and clever, Suzanne wanted to leave Alençon for Paris, believing she could improve herself there.

The chevalier, struck by her charm and ambition, encouraged her in a paternal tone and advised her to court Monsieur du Bousquier, a wealthy bachelor who had shown interest. He understood social dynamics and how Suzanne might use beauty and wit to secure a favorable match.

The narrative then shifts to du Bousquier, a man of dubious repute who had once enjoyed wealth and influence. Now back in Alençon, he hoped to marry Mademoiselle Cormon, a rich old maid, to restore his fortunes. His past as a purveyor during the Revolution had tarnished his standing, yet he remained determined to regain status.

Du Bousquier contrasts with the chevalier: the latter embodies the elegance of the old regime, the former the brusque opportunism of the new order. Their rivalry over Mademoiselle Cormon is subtle but intense, each seeking her favor while navigating social position.

As the story unfolds, Suzanne’s ambitions and the machinations of both men intertwine, revealing the intricate dance of desire, ambition, and societal expectation in provincial Alençon. Their interactions reflect broader themes of class, gender, and the pursuit of happiness in a changing world.

Madame Granson, a widow living on a modest pension and her own property, struggled to support her son Athanase, who contributed a small salary from the mayor’s office. Their provincial home was simple and marked by poverty, which shaped their lives and morals. Athanase, a pale, introspective twenty-three-year-old, harbored a deep passion for his cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he believed to be wealthy. His feelings were complicated by social expectations and the ridicule he might face for loving an older woman.

Athanase's love for Mademoiselle Cormon began as a practical consideration to secure a better life for himself and his mother, but it grew into genuine affection. He admired her from afar, feeling both drawn to her and constrained by his circumstances. His talent and potential went unrecognized in their provincial setting, where ambition often clashed with harsh realities.

Madame Granson, aware of her son’s feelings, saw the potential benefits of a match with Mademoiselle Cormon despite Athanase's lack of fortune and the cousin's age. She remained hopeful and patient, waiting for the right moment to act. Meanwhile, Athanase’s thoughts were consumed by Mademoiselle Cormon; he often fantasized about a future together despite barriers that seemed insurmountable.

The arrival of Suzanne, a young woman from a less fortunate background, introduced a new dynamic. She felt an immediate connection to Athanase, sensing his struggles and aspirations. Yet her own ambitions pushed her toward deception as a means to a better future. After a brief encounter with Madame Granson, Suzanne left town with money and new clothes, while the community lamented her departure.

As Athanase prepared for a dinner with Mademoiselle Cormon, his mother urged him to present himself well, hoping to improve his chances. Athanase, unaware of the full extent of his mother’s plans, navigated his feelings of love and ambition under the constraints of provincial life. The evening promised to be pivotal as the fates of Athanase, Mademoiselle Cormon, and Suzanne began to unfold, each driven by desire and societal pressure.

In the end, Suzanne's departure to Paris marked a significant turning point; she sought to redefine her life away from past constraints. Her journey reflected the struggles of many women of the time, caught between social expectations and personal aspirations. Athanase remained in Alençon, grappling with unfulfilled dreams and the weight of his love for Mademoiselle Cormon, as the complexities of their lives continued to intertwine against the backdrop of provincial society.

In many second-class prefectures of France there exists a salon that gathers influential—but not elite—members of the community. Its hosts are prominent locally and invited to events, yet they do not mix with the highest social echelons, who prefer distance and communicate by formal invitations.

This salon, where lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates convene, wields considerable local influence. Its members are indifferent to luxury and fashion, concerned with practical matters such as land acquisition. They resist outside influences, particularly from Paris, and their gossip often assumes the weight of judgment. Young women are seldom present; when they appear it is usually to seek validation.

In Alençon, the upper classes gathered at the house of an old maid, Mademoiselle Rose-Marie-Victoire Cormon, whose fortune was coveted by relatives and two old bachelors. She lived with her uncle, a former grand-vicar, and her family had long served the nobility. Through marriages with noble families they acquired a semblance of aristocracy despite a fundamentally middle-class status.

Mademoiselle Cormon's house, dating from the time of Henri IV, provoked envy by its prime location and careful upkeep. The mansion featured a courtyard with a flowerbed, a mark of her meticulous care and insistence on cleanliness. Its robust architecture and decorative elements recalled its historical significance.

Inside, the house was arranged for reception: a salon hung with family portraits and a dining room exuding provincial charm. The salon was a hub for social life—card games and conversations about local affairs took place there. Mademoiselle Cormon's circle numbered about 150 people, mostly mature, who rarely traveled and had long been rooted in provincial life.

Wednesdays were special: she hosted dinners that drew a larger assemblage. The women dressed modestly and the atmosphere was one of familiarity and routine. Conversations typically centered on local gossip, land disputes, and judgments about aristocratic behavior, reflecting the community's concerns and values.

Despite her wealth and standing, Mademoiselle Cormon remained unmarried, largely because she wished to marry a nobleman and because of the era’s constraints. The political upheaval from 1788 to 1815 made suitable matches scarce—many eligible men were too old or too young, or lacked the qualities she sought. Fear of being married for her fortune made her set subtle tests for suitors, seeking to expose mercenary intentions without their knowledge.

As she aged, her longing for love intensified, yet she remained oblivious to some genuine affections, including those of Athanase Granson, one of her suitors. Her innocence and naivety prevented her from recognizing sincere attachment, leading to missed opportunities. Over time her character grew more rigid and the weight of social expectations pressed on her.

Although pious and virtuous, she became a figure of local ridicule; her unmarried condition turned into a proverb. She lived modestly, shunning ostentation and keeping a lifestyle in tune with provincial values. Her considerable fortune did not exempt her from scrutiny, and attempts at marriage were viewed skeptically.

As years passed her hopes for a suitable husband dwindled and she became increasingly isolated in her desires. The return of disbanded soldiers and officers offered occasional glimmers of hope, but none met her standards. Her yearning for companionship and motherhood turned into a source of despair as she confronted the realities of her situation.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s story thus illuminates the complexities of love, social expectation, and the particular challenges faced by women in her position. Her life—marked by a deep desire for connection and a struggle against restrictive social norms—serves as a poignant commentary on the nature of happiness and fulfillment in a world governed by rigid class conventions.

To complete the picture of Mademoiselle Cormon’s household, we must consider her and the Abbé de Sponde alongside their loyal servants: Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette, who all contributed to the comfort of the uncle and niece.

Jacquelin, a short, stout man with a ruddy complexion, had served the household for twenty-two years, doing everything from waiting at table to grooming the mare Pénélope, cherished by the family. He was in love with Josette, a thirty-year-old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if she married him. The couple quietly saved their wages, hoping Mademoiselle’s marriage would free them to wed. Josette, plump and practical, was said to rule her mistress. Mariette, the cook, had been with the family for fifteen years and was skilled in preparing local delicacies.

Pénélope, the old brown-bay mare, attracted affection and conversation, since Mademoiselle Cormon, lacking children, directed maternal instincts toward the animal. The servants and the mare maintained a routine reflecting their long-standing roles. Mademoiselle, however, grew increasingly difficult to please, her idle mind fixating on trivial matters. She fretted over small details—household linens, the timing of Pénélope’s meals—turning minor disputes into a sense of purpose.

Her life revolved around the household, her uncle, and her religious duties, which magnified the minutiae of existence. Concerned about her health, she obsessed over minor ailments and dietary choices. Each season she visited her estate at Prébaudet to oversee apple trees and prepare for harvest, marking the occasion with a farewell dinner for friends, a notable event in Alençon.

On the day of her departure the town buzzed with gossip about her and potential suitors, especially M. du Bousquier, whom she had rejected. The farewell dinner was full of social maneuvering; Mademoiselle Cormon tried to engage guests but often struggled to find the right words. Her attempts at amiability earned mixed reactions, her lack of wit amusing the sharper guests.

As the evening progressed conversation turned to local politics and the controversial du Bousquier, rumored to be involved with a young woman named Suzanne. In trying to defend du Bousquier, Mademoiselle made a remark that unexpectedly drew laughter and admiration, showing she could contribute to the evening’s entertainment, albeit unintentionally.

The dinner closed with light banter about du Bousquier’s character, revealing a town divided in opinion. Mademoiselle’s innocence and naivety were plain as she navigated social complexities and tensions around marriage and morality. When the guests departed, talk continued of her prospects and the implications of du Bousquier’s behavior. The evening left her contemplating her role in the community and her own desires, setting the stage for future developments. Love, ambition, and societal expectations intertwined, creating relationships that would continue to evolve in Alençon.

The following day Mademoiselle Cormon, packed into her old carriole with Josette, set off for Prebaudet, unaware that an unexpected event would hasten her marriage. The morning after her arrival, while having breakfast, she was interrupted by Jacquelin with a letter from her uncle, the Abbé de Sponde. The letter announced that Monsieur de Troisville, a grandson of one of the abbé’s friends, wished to stay in Alençon and needed hospitality. Alarmed by the urgency, Mademoiselle Cormon decided to return to town immediately to help her uncle prepare for the guest.

Despite the rain she insisted on a quick departure, driven by excitement and the prospect of marriage. Their arrival in Alençon caused a stir, with townspeople speculating about the reason. Josette and Jacquelin were left to manage the household while Mademoiselle Cormon concentrated on preparing for Monsieur de Troisville’s visit.

News of the viscount’s impending arrival spread quickly and the town buzzed with gossip. Hopeful, she worried about the state of her home and her appearance, fearing her old-fashioned decor might not impress him; she even sacrificed some prized liqueurs to make a better impression.

When Monsieur de Troisville arrived he was greeted by the abbé and Mademoiselle Cormon. He looked distinguished and she felt an immediate attraction. Their dinner conversation flowed: the viscount complimented her home and expressed admiration for the region, making her feel at last noticed and desirable.

The evening shifted when the Chevalier de Valois and other guests, drawn by curiosity about the newcomer, arrived. During introductions the viscount casually mentioned he was married, which devastated Mademoiselle Cormon. She fainted; Du Bousquier, a local suitor, caught her and carried her to her room while the townspeople, watching eagerly, were left to speculate about the implications.

As she recovered Mademoiselle Cormon realized her hopes for marriage had been dashed. The next day news of her fainting and the viscount’s marital status swept through Alençon, prompting a mixture of laughter and sympathy. Feeling the weight of her situation she pondered her options as both Du Bousquier and the Chevalier sought her attention, each hoping to win her favor.

Ultimately Mademoiselle Cormon accepted Du Bousquier’s proposal as a way to regain standing in the community. She required him to agree to a lie about their engagement, which he readily accepted. As they prepared to announce it the Chevalier de Valois arrived, unaware of the developments. The evening ended with Mademoiselle Cormon feeling a blend of triumph and uncertainty as the town buzzed with news of her engagement, marking a new chapter in her life.

The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage devastated Athanase Granson, though he concealed his turmoil. He first heard it at Chief Justice du Ronceret’s house, where his mother was playing cards. Athanase had long pinned his hopes on Mademoiselle Cormon; the announcement felt catastrophic. Shaped by an Imperial education that bred fatalism, he drifted into indifference and despair. His mother noticed his pallor but assumed he was merely preoccupied with his work.

After the marriage was announced, Athanase wandered the picturesque Sarthe region, finding a bitter solace in its overlooked beauty. He withdrew from politics and society, becoming a figure of little interest to townspeople who were unaware of the depth of his suffering. His mother worried and speculated he might be contemplating a major project, not grasping the real cause of his withdrawal.

Suzanne, a young woman inspired by romantic tales and adventure, returned to Alençon intending to help Athanase financially, though their paths did not meet. The town buzzed with gossip about Mademoiselle Cormon’s match, with conflicting reports about its legitimacy. Eventually the marriage contract was signed and the wedding took place amid ominous signs—a priest accidentally read from the De Profundis—setting an uneasy tone.

The marriage of Mademoiselle Cormon and du Bousquier lacked celebration; the couple soon retreated to Prebaudet. Du Bousquier, intent on modernizing the Cormon estate, began renovations that alarmed locals who feared losing their provincial charm. Athanase, consumed by grief, ultimately took his own life, leaving a letter his mother misread as evidence of newfound independence rather than the despair it expressed.

Athanase’s suicide shocked Alençon. Madame Granson was left to mourn privately, grappling with the full weight of her loss that others barely comprehended. The Chevalier de Valois revealed Athanase’s unrequited love for Mademoiselle Cormon, deepening the town’s sense of tragedy. Madame Granson’s grief was profound; she alone understood the true nature of her son’s struggles and bore them in silence.

Madame du Bousquier returned to Alençon and encountered Madame Granson in a meeting heavy with unspoken words. Madame Granson sought solace in the church and implored the rector for a proper burial for her son; the ceremony was conducted discreetly to avoid scandal, reflecting the community’s desire to contain the episode.

As time passed, Madame du Bousquier’s marriage became both gossip and cautionary tale. At first she appeared content, but the reality was different. Du Bousquier, outwardly successful and politically ambitious, proved a tyrant at home. The marriage brought social advantage but left her feeling powerless and unfulfilled; they had no children, and her longing for motherhood deepened her pain.

The Chevalier de Valois, embittered by the match, sought revenge against du Bousquier, whose conduct made him a figure of ridicule. Public mockery at Mademoiselle Cormon’s misfortune gave way to growing sympathy for Madame du Bousquier as the true hardships of her life became known. The chevalier’s schemes only widened the rift between du Bousquier and the local aristocracy, intensifying animosity rather than restoring honor.

Madame du Bousquier lived amid contradictions: she performed the duties of a devoted wife while inwardly resenting her husband’s oppression. The marriage, beneficial in status, left her emotionally desolate; she clung to duty even as she mourned the life she might have had.

The Chevalier de Valois’s death coincided with sweeping political changes in France, marking an end of an era and creating a void for those who had known him. Suzanne, seeking to honor the past, purchased a memento from the chevalier’s estate, a small symbol of lost connections and vanished possibilities.

Ultimately, Madame du Bousquier’s life illustrates the complexities of love, duty, and social expectation. She remained both pitied and admired—enduring the consequences of her choices and reflecting on what might have been. The narrative offers a poignant exploration of personal desires constrained by society, showing how private suffering and public reputation intertwine.
