AN OLD MAID

CHAPTER I. ONE OF MANY CHEVALIERS DE VALOIS

Most persons have encountered, in certain provinces in France, a number of Chevaliers de Valois. One lived in Normandy, another at Bourges, a third (with whom we have here to do) flourished in Alencon. The number of the Valesian tribe is of no consequence to this tale. They were all so little known to one another that it was not advisable to speak of one with regard to the other. They were willing to leave the Bourbons in tranquil possession of the throne; after all, Henri IV. had become king for want of a male Orleans heir. If any Valois remain they descend from Charles de Valois, Duc d’Angouleme; the male line ended, until proof to the contrary, in the Abbe de Rothelin. The Valois-Saint-Remy, descending from Henri II., also came to an end in the Lamothe-Valois implicated in the Diamond Necklace affair.

Each of these chevaliers, like the Chevalier of Alencon, was an old gentleman—tall, thin, withered, and moneyless. The Chevalier of Alencon had spent his youth in Paris, where the Revolution overtook him at thirty amid conquests and gallantries. Accepted by the highest provincial aristocracy as a genuine Valois, he distinguished himself by excellent manners. He dined out every day, played cards every evening, and told many anecdotes of the reign of Louis XV. and the beginnings of the Revolution; when heard for the first time they seemed witty. He never repeated his own bons mots, never spoke of his love-affairs, and used his privilege as a Voltairean noble to stay away from mass; his royalist devotion earned him indulgence for irreligion.

He had an air and manner—imitated perhaps from Mole—in taking snuff from a gold box adorned with the portrait of the Princess Goritza, a Hungarian celebrated in Louis XV.’s later years; for her he had once fought a duel. At fifty-eight he owned to only fifty, and his thin, fair figure helped him retain a youthful look. He had one enormous nose that divided a pale face into two halves: one side reddened with digestion while the other remained white. The incandescence was on the left. He ate like an ogre and declared he had a “hot liver,” perhaps to excuse his appetite; in a region given to four-hour meals of thirty or forty dishes, his stomach seemed providential. Heat on the left side, doctors said, denotes a prodigal heart; his gallantries confirmed it.

Despite these symptoms his constitution was vigorous. His face bore the furrows of pleasure—crow’s-feet and marches-du-palais—signs of passion. He was fastidious in his ablutions and dress: white, fine linen; a clean though half-worn coat; maroon coat with gilt buttons; half-tight breeches of poult-de-soie; white waistcoat without embroidery; a tight cravat showing no shirt-collar; square-buckled shoes; two parallel watch-chains. His hair and brows were combed to preserve youth; a part of his skull shone like ivory. His hands were delicate and well cared for. Had it not been for his magisterial nose, he might have seemed too dainty.

Yet he was not without littlenesses: he put cotton in his ears and wore two little diamond ear-rings representing negroes’ heads, which he claimed cured his headaches. These oddities were pardoned because of his other graces. He labored to conceal his age and give pleasure. He comported himself with all the old-marquis grace now lost since Mole’s disappearance.

His private life appeared open but was mysterious. He lived modestly on the second floor of Madame Lardot’s house in the rue du Cours, paying one hundred francs a year for two rooms. He dined out daily and returned only to bed; his sole expense at home was breakfast—chocolate, bread and butter, fruit in season. He made no fire except in the coldest weather. Between eleven and four he walked, read the papers, and paid visits. He claimed poverty—his whole fortune an annuity of six hundred francs, the remains of old opulence—and visited the man of business quarterly. In truth a banker paid him one hundred and fifty francs every three months, sent by Monsieur Bordin of Paris, the last of the procureurs du Chatelet. The chevalier demanded secrecy about such details.

Monsieur de Valois was a welcome guest in Alencon’s most distinguished houses. His talents at cards, his stories, his amiability, and his approving grimace made gatherings feel incomplete without him. Young women prized his compliments; his old-fashioned diminutives—“my heart,” “my jewel,” “my little pet”—came with a grace few could resist. At cards he never complained, praised adversaries when they lost, and by a snuff-box gesture—worthy of Mole—silenced tedious dissertations on play. He accepted poor players and made the best of them. His equability earned him admiration: “I do admire the Chevalier de Valois!”

His manner to grisettes was paternal and flattering; he supplied them with trifles—chocolate, bonbons, ribbons—and they reciprocated with secret attachments and discretion. In the mornings, while breakfasting, he amused himself with their gossip, his “petticoat gazettes.” Despite constant winnings—he left salons with about six francs—he remained the darling of the town.

To explain his existence: after the “glorious” sad days of July, some discovered his nightly winnings were about one hundred and fifty francs every three months and that he had the pluck to send himself his annuity so as not to appear entirely without resources. Friends contested this, defending him as respectable and calumniated. In any case, after about fifteen years he had amassed ten thousand and some odd hundred francs. On the Bourbon return, an old friend, the Marquis de Pombreton, allegedly repaid twelve hundred pistoles lent long ago; this windfall led him to consult moneyed friends and invest on the Grand-Livre at an opportune moment. He also obtained, through unnamed influential persons, a pension of three hundred francs from the king’s privy purse and the cross of Saint-Louis, permitting him to take the rank of retired colonel for his service in the Catholic armies of the West.

Thus, besides his fiction of an annuity, he had an honest income of a thousand francs. He made little change in lifestyle; the red ribbon of the cross completed his physiognomy. After 1802 he sealed letters with an old family seal and bore arms: Party of France, with mascles and a cross; motto: “Valeo.” Many envied his tranquil life—whist, boston, backgammon, reversi, piquet; snuff gracefully inhaled; tranquil walks. Yet he had an ambition: to marry a certain rich old maid, using her as a stepping-stone to the court. There lay the secret of his royal bearing and residence in Alencon.

CHAPTER II. SUSANNAH AND THE ELDERS

One Wednesday morning in mid-spring, in the year—such was his mode of reckoning—the chevalier, dressing in his old green-flowered damask gown, heard the light step of a young girl run up the stair. Three taps on the door, and without waiting for response a handsome girl slipped into his room.

“Ah! is it you, Suzanne?” said the chevalier, still stropping his razor. “What have you come for, my dear little jewel of mischief?”

“I have come to tell you something which may perhaps give you as much pleasure as pain.”

“Is it anything about Cesarine?”

“Cesarine! much I care about your Cesarine!” she answered saucily.

This Suzanne was a work-girl at Madame Lardot’s. The wash-rooms occupied the ground floor; the tiny courtyard served to dry the fine linen of the town’s best families. The chevalier, claiming to know love-affairs from the linen’s condition, was discreet; he was never betrayed into epigrams that might close to him a salon. He passed the work-room door often, bestowing trifles on his “little ducks,” who adored him. Women have an instinct for men who love them and demand no payment for gallantries; the chevalier’s gallant protection, a remnant of seigneurs’ ways, made him beloved. He could no longer ruin himself for a mistress, so he gave toffees instead of bank-bills; they accepted them gladly. They made him seem older and more respectable in public than he was, but among themselves they perched on his shoulders like parrots. He enjoyed their household gossip—what he called his “talking feuilletons”—and in the mornings while breakfasting was as amused as the saints in heaven.

Suzanne was one of his favorites—clever, ambitious, handsome—the stuff of a Sophie Arnould. She had a Norman, rubenesque beauty.

“Well, my child, tell me your great or little adventure,” said the chevalier.

He listened with a paternal manner to grisettes; they in turn confided. Though his room was shabby—shabby tapestry, tavern-paper profiles of Louis XVI. and members of his family—he exhaled an essence of the eighteenth century; libertine graces of his youth reappeared. He draped Suzanne before him, holding her legs between his knees; she let him but, in the street, she could be offish to other men.

“We slander ourselves,” he said craftily; “we are as virtuous as that biblical girl whose name we bear; we can always marry as we please, but we are thirsty for Paris. We want to go to see if the great capital hasn’t some young Chevalier de Valois with a carriage, diamonds, an opera-box.” He counseled her to go to Paris at the cost of an old bachelor—“an old bachelor is the natural money-box of a young girl”—but warned: “don’t drag me into it. I haven’t a penny to get you out of trouble. If I marry Mademoiselle Cormon and become rich, of course I would prefer you.”

“Monsieur le chevalier, I assure you, you are mistaken,” she began, blushing; the chevalier had already fathomed her plan.

“Yes, yes! I understand: you want me to believe it. Well! I do believe it. But take my advice: go to Monsieur du Bousquier. Don’t you take linen there? He’s an old bachelor and very rich; if you’re clever you can go to Paris at his expense. Twist him round your finger; he fears scandal and is ambitious.” At these words Suzanne, eager, hurried off—to du Bousquier.

Du Bousquier’s biography: of an old Alencon family, he had gone to Paris, taken part in public business, and made a fortune as commissary of provisions to the armies from 1793 to 1799. He lived magnificently, was intimate with Barras and Fouche, expected to be minister by supporting a secret party until Marengo. His speculations failed when Kellermann and Desaix altered events; Napoleon disfavored contractors who had speculated on his defeat, and du Bousquier was ruined. From his past he saved only twelve hundred francs a year placed in the Grand Livre. He returned to Alencon, furious with Bonaparte, and was well received among royalists though not by the leading nobility. He had offered himself to Mademoiselle Armande and been refused. Now middle-aged, he was a man of vigor—plump, strong calves, broad chest—but his voice was stifled, like a saw cutting moist wood. He favored the liberal party in Alencon, manipulating elections and doing harm to the Restoration; his hatred of the royalists was patient and inexhaustible.

The chevalier had turned Suzanne upon du Bousquier because the two old bachelors were secret rivals for the hand of Mademoiselle Cormon. Though they kept their designs hidden from the town, both hoped for this wealthy old maid. The chevalier had never asked her hand; du Bousquier had and been refused. Their war was courteous in the chevalier’s manner, less so in du Bousquier’s, but both met nearly every evening on the same ground.

Suzanne tripped from the rue du Cours to du Bousquier’s house in the rue du Cygne, where he had bought a grey Jura-stone house about five years earlier. It preserved some furniture and decorations of his former splendor; yet provincial neglect made the whole a jumble—magnificence marred by dirt. He lived as a man of leisure, with a single Breton servant, Rene, a ninny trained to rub floors and black boots; Rene was a glutton and a trusty confidant.

“’Tisn’t your day. We haven’t any linen for the wash,” Rene grunted when Suzanne entered. She left him and went upstairs.

Du Bousquier, in bed, listened as Suzanne sat swinging curtain-rings.

“What is it, my charmer?” he said, sitting up.

“Monsieur,” she said gravely, “I know how ridiculous it is in a poor girl to nag a man. But I can be of use to you if you’ll attach yourself to me as I am to you. If we went to Paris you’d see what I could do with a man of your mind and capacity; they are remaking the government. Perhaps this misfortune is a piece of luck. Who and what are you working for now?”

“For myself, of course!” cried du Bousquier.

“Monster! you’ll never be a father!” she retorted.

Du Bousquier, torn between joy, distrust, and calculation, had long planned to marry Mademoiselle Cormon for her property and social standing. That marriage would have advanced his political career; but Suzanne’s sudden offer bewildered him. Without it, he might have married her and led the liberal party openly; with it, he hesitated. He rubbed his night-cap and thought. Finally: “I’m not to be taken in with such bosh, not I!”—the proud remark of a man who prided himself on never being duped by women.

“Remember, Monsieur du Bousquier,” Suzanne replied, “I have offered you my hand with dignity. I shan’t beg. I can find other husbands. But I cannot stay in Alencon: my mother would beat me, Madame Lardot would turn me off. If I go to Paris I must have money for the journey and for—you know what.”

This struck du Bousquier more than the chevalier; had she truly been desperate she would have died a thousand deaths before asking. “Will you really go to Paris, then?” he asked.

“Yes, monsieur.”

Du Bousquier lamented his debts—payments on the house, bills for painter and upholsterer. He offered three hundred francs. Suzanne pretended to depart.

“Stop, where are you going?” said du Bousquier uneasily. “The devil take me if I ever did anything more than rumple her collar, and, lo and behold! she makes THAT a ground to put her hand in one’s pocket!”

“I’m going to Madame Granson, treasurer of the Maternity Society,” Suzanne said. “They save many poor girls in my condition from suicide.”

“Madame Granson!” Du Bousquier, alarmed, gave her a key and told her to take from his secretary a bag containing about six hundred francs—“it is all I possess.”

“If you deceive me, Suzanne,” he cried as she handled the sack.

“Monsieur,” she answered impertinently, “wouldn’t you have given me money if I had asked for it?” Recalled to gallantry, du Bousquier kissed her; she left, cursing his stinginess. She hid the sack in an osier gamebag, already thinking that the Maternity Society, presided over by Mademoiselle Cormon, might be induced to complete the sum she had calculated for Paris—one thousand francs—and, in revenge for du Bousquier’s stinginess, perhaps to entangle him in scandal. With a mind quick to mischief, she went to Madame Granson, composing an expression of deepest dejection.

Madame Granson, widow of a lieutenant-colonel of artillery killed at Jena, lived on a meagre pension of nine hundred francs a year and three hundred francs from a small property, her savings eaten up by the education and support of a son. Their lodging in the rue du Bercail was one of those modest ground-floor apartments of a provincial town: a street door above three steep steps, a passage to an interior courtyard, a gallery-covered staircase, a dining-room and kitchen on one side, and a multifunctional salon and the widow’s bedroom on the other.

Athanase Granson, twenty-three, slept in an attic and added six hundred francs to his mother’s income by a post in the mayor’s office, obtained through his relation Mademoiselle Cormon; he was placed in charge of the archives. Madame Granson, in her plain bourgeois dress, sat in the salon beneath a portrait of the lieutenant-colonel, watching the street and preserving the air of strict provincial morals. At the moment, mother and son breakfasted simply—coffee, bread and butter, radishes. To understand how much Suzanne’s visit would mean to Madame Granson, certain secret interests of both mother and son must be explained.

Athanase was thin, pale, of medium height, with a hollow face and two black eyes that looked like bits of coal—features that suggested repressed talent. Irregular lines, a prominent chin, a well-shaped forehead and a melancholy cast from poverty’s struggle hinted at an imprisoned spirit. In any place but Alençon his appearance might have drawn help from those who recognize ability in obscurity. He had a contemplative nature; timidity and the chill of provincial life stifled action. Poverty intensified a savage pride in him—noble but obstructive at the outset of life. He belonged to the sort of talent that doubts and is easily discouraged: more a thinker than an actor, liable at times to sudden resolves that others might call madness.

The contempt shown to poverty was crushing to Athanase; solitude enervated him; without encouragement his powers languished. His library work passed unnoticed; he kept his ambitions hidden, fearing they might harm him. Under all lay a secret passion that hollowed his cheeks: he loved his distant cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon, whom the Chevalier de Valois and du Bousquier also pursued. His love began in calculation—Cormon was thought rich—and he imagined marriage would provide the steady livelihood to free him for fame and secure his mother’s comfort. That motive degraded his feeling in his own eyes; he also feared ridicule for a young man of twenty-three loving an “old maid” of forty.

Yet in provincial life such a marriage could be genuine. Where manners are fixed and opportunities few, marriage is often a necessity; improvident liaisons alarm provincial parents and ruin a young man’s prospects. A poor artist or scholar cannot rely on the provinces to marry on any purpose other than material comfort; the aim to combine love and sustenance is usually denied. Athanase’s initial practical motive turned into real passion as he studied Cormon and, by the charm of habit, began to see only her perfections.

For a young man the senses are powerful; in Athanase’s isolation Mademoiselle Cormon was the single figure presented to him, concentrating all light upon her. His secret feeling broadened and deepened silently, swelling hour by hour, and with it his timidity.

Madame Granson suspected the truth and calculated candidly. She saw advantages in the match: a talented young husband would honor the family and neighborhood. She judged the obstacles—his poverty and Cormon’s age—formidable but surmountable by patience. Like other provincials, she watched for propitious circumstances and quietly plotted. She distrusted du Bousquier but concealed her tactics from her son.

Thus Suzanne’s lie, confided to Madame Granson, became a powerful weapon in the widow’s hands. As treasurer of the Maternity Society, Madame Granson could spread news under the guise of charity while gathering support for “the chaste Suzanne,” using the scandal to wound du Bousquier.

At breakfast Athanase, leaning on his elbow, twirled his spoon and looked over the poor room—red brick floor, straw chairs, a painted wooden buffet, and chequered curtains. His pallid face, framed in black hair and lit by the window, was the first thing the entering Suzanne saw. Suzanne, a grisette familiar with misery and heartache, felt instantly an electric attraction—an inexplicable flash that convinces one, “It is he!”—a presentiment of shared love. Often reason quells such moments, but here Suzanne was struck by a genuine feeling that dispersed the cynicism of her dissipated life. What had seemed a joke the night before now seemed to her a grave misdeed; she recoiled at her own readiness to deceive. Yet practical considerations—the young man’s poverty, hopes of enriching herself, perhaps destiny—checked this sentiment.

She asked for a private interview with Madame Granson, who led her to the bedchamber. When Suzanne emerged and looked at Athanase again, tears came to her eyes. Madame Granson was radiant: she had at last a terrible weapon against du Bousquier and promised the seduced girl the support of charitable ladies, foreseeing a storm over the guilty man’s head. The Chevalier de Valois anticipated the affair’s turn but could not foresee the scandal it would become.

“My dear child,” said Madame Granson to her son, “we dine to-night with Mademoiselle Cormon; do take pains with your appearance. Put on that handsome frilled shirt and your green coat of Elbeuf cloth. I have my reasons.” She urged him to study the Chevalier de Valois’s easy manners and to soften his stiff air. Athanase listened, surprised but obedient; he rose, took his cap, and went to the mayor’s office, wondering if his mother suspected his secret.

He walked past the rue du Val-Noble, where Mademoiselle Cormon lived—a daily pleasure—and fancied himself beside her: how little she knew that a young man loved her, would be faithful, let her manage her fortune, and yet could not speak out. “Suppose I were to speak to her this evening?” he thought.

Suzanne returned to her mother’s, thinking of Athanase, and, like many who long to help an adored man beyond their powers, considered making her body a stepping-stone for his advancement. Her falsehood had achieved its purpose: a few days later she left town with money and good clothes, including a green reps dress and a green bonnet lined with pink given by Monsieur de Valois—a gift she preferred even to money—and set off joyfully for Paris. Alençon lamented her misfortunes as the ladies of the Charity and Maternity Societies showed lively sympathy. Though Suzanne belonged to the class absorbed by Paris, she remained in the more decent strata of gallantry; in her time, as Monsieur de Valois said, “Woman no longer existed,” yet she styled herself “Madame du Val-Noble.” One distinguished Restoration writer, a journalist who shapes public opinion, took her under his protection and might marry her.

In nearly all the second-class prefectures of France there exists one salon which is the meeting-ground of considerable and well-considered persons of the community who are, nevertheless, not the cream of the best society. The master and mistress of such an establishment are counted among the leading persons of the town; they are received wherever it may please them to visit; no fete is given, no formal or diplomatic dinner takes place, to which they are not invited. But the chateau people, heads of families possessing great estates, in short, the highest personages in the department, do not go to their houses; social intercourse between them is carried on by cards from one to the other, and a dinner or soiree accepted and returned.

This salon, in which the lesser nobility, the clergy, and the magistracy meet together, exerts a great influence. The judgment and mind of the region reside in that solid, unostentatious society, where each man knows the resources of his neighbor, where complete indifference is shown to luxury and dress,—pleasures which are thought childish in comparison to that of obtaining ten or twelve acres of pasture land,—a purchase coveted for years, which has probably given rise to endless diplomatic combinations. Immovable in its prejudices, good or evil, this social circle follows a beaten track, looking neither before it nor behind it. It accepts nothing from Paris without long examination and trial; it rejects cashmeres as it does investments on the Grand-Livre; it scoffs at fashions and novelties; reads nothing, prefers ignorance, whether of science, literature, or industrial inventions. It insists on the removal of a prefect when that official does not suit it; and if the administration resists, it isolates him, after the manner of bees who wall up a snail in wax when it gets into their hive.

In this society gossip is often turned into solemn verdicts. Young women are seldom seen there; when they come it is to seek approbation of their conduct,—a consecration of their self-importance. This supremacy granted to one house is apt to wound the sensibilities of other natives of the region, who console themselves by adding up the cost it involves, and by which they profit. If it so happens that there is no fortune large enough to keep open house in this way, the big-wigs of the place choose a place of meeting, as they did at Alencon, in the house of some inoffensive person, whose settled life and character and position offers no umbrage to the vanities or the interests of any one.

For some years the upper classes of Alencon had met in this way at the house of an old maid, whose fortune was, unknown to herself, the aim and object of Madame Granson, her second cousin, and of the two old bachelors whose secret hopes in that direction we have just unveiled. This lady lived with her maternal uncle, a former grand-vicar of the bishopric of Seez, once her guardian, and whose heir she was. The family of which Rose-Marie-Victoire Cormon was the present representative had been in earlier days among the most considerable in the province. Though belonging to the middle classes, she consorted with the nobility, among whom she was more or less allied, her family having furnished, in past years, stewards to the Duc d’Alencon, many magistrates to the long robe, and various bishops to the clergy. Monsieur de Sponde, the maternal grandfather of Mademoiselle Cormon, was elected by the Nobility to the States-General, and Monsieur Cormon, her father, by the Tiers-Etat, though neither accepted the mission. For the last hundred years the daughters of the family had married nobles belonging to the provinces; consequently, this family had thrown out so many suckers throughout the duchy as to appear on nearly all the genealogical trees. No bourgeois family had ever seemed so like nobility.

The house in which Mademoiselle Cormon lived, built in Henri IV.’s time by Pierre Cormon, the steward of the last Duc d’Alencon, had always belonged to the family; and among the old maid’s visible possessions this one was particularly stimulating to the covetous desires of the two old lovers. Yet, far from producing revenue, the house was a cause of expense. But it is so rare to find in the very centre of a provincial town a private dwelling without unpleasant surroundings, handsome in outward structure and convenient within, that Alencon shared the envy of the lovers.

This old mansion stands exactly in the middle of the rue du Val-Noble. It is remarkable for the strength of its construction,—a style of building introduced by Marie de’ Medici. Though built of granite,—a stone which is hard to work,—its angles, and the casings of the doors and windows, are decorated with corner blocks cut into diamond facets. It has only one clear story above the ground-floor; but the roof, rising steeply, has several projecting windows, with carved spandrels rather elegantly enclosed in oaken frames, and externally adorned with balustrades. Between each of these windows is a gargoyle presenting the fantastic jaws of an animal without a body, vomiting the rain-water upon large stones pierced with five holes. The two gables are surmounted by leaden bouquets,—a symbol of the bourgeoisie; for nobles alone had the privilege in former days of having weather-vanes. To right of the courtyard are the stables and coach-house; to left, the kitchen, wood-house, and laundry.

One side of the porte-cochere, being left open, allowed the passers in the street to see in the midst of the vast courtyard a flower-bed, the raised earth of which was held in place by a low privet hedge. A few monthly roses, pinkes, lilies, and Spanish broom filled this bed, around which in the summer season boxes of paurestinus, pomegranates, and myrtle were placed. Struck by the scrupulous cleanliness of the courtyard and its dependencies, a stranger would at once have divined that the place belonged to an old maid. The eye which presided there must have been an unoccupied, ferreting eye; minutely careful, less from nature than for want of something to do. An old maid, forced to employ her vacant days, could alone see to the grass being hoed from between the paving stones, the tops of the walls kept clean, the broom continually going, and the leather curtains of the coach-house always closed. She alone would have introduced, out of busy idleness, a sort of Dutch cleanliness into a house on the confines of Bretagne and Normandie,—a region where they take pride in professing an utter indifference to comfort.

Never did the Chevalier de Valois, or du Bousquier, mount the steps of the double stairway leading to the portico of this house without saying to himself, one, that it was fit for a peer of France, the other, that the mayor of the town ought to live there.

A glass door gave entrance from this portico into an antechamber, a species of gallery paved in red tiles and wainscoted, which served as a hospital for the family portraits,—some having an eye put out, others suffering from a dislocated shoulder; this one held his hat in a hand that no longer existed; that one was a case of amputation at the knee. Here were deposited the cloaks, clogs, overshoes, umbrellas, hoods, and pelisses of the guests. It was an arsenal where each arrival left his baggage on arriving, and took it up when departing. Along each wall was a bench for the servants who arrived with lanterns, and a large stove, to counteract the north wind, which blew through this hall from the garden to the courtyard.

The house was divided in two equal parts. On one side, toward the courtyard, was the well of the staircase, a large dining-room looking to the garden, and an office or pantry which communicated with the kitchen. On the other side was the salon, with four windows, beyond which were two smaller rooms,—one looking on the garden, and used as a boudoir, the other lighted from the courtyard, and used as a sort of office.

The upper floor contained a complete apartment for a family household, and a suite of rooms where the venerable Abbe de Sponde had his abode. The garrets offered fine quarters to the rats and mice, whose nocturnal performances were related by Mademoiselle Cormon to the Chevalier de Valois, with many expressions of surprise at the inutility of her efforts to get rid of them. The garden, about half an acre in size, is margined by the Brillante, so named from the particles of mica which sparkle in its bed elsewhere than in the Val-Noble, where its shallow waters are stained by the dyehouses, and loaded with refuse from the other industries of the town. The shore opposite to Mademoiselle Cormon’s garden is crowded with houses where a variety of trades are carried on; happily for her, the occupants are quiet people,—a baker, a cleaner, an upholsterer, and several bourgeois. The garden, full of common flowers, ends in a natural terrace, forming a quay, down which are several steps leading to the river. Imagine on the balustrade of this terrace a number of tall vases of blue and white pottery, in which are gilliflowers; and to right and left, along the neighboring walls, hedges of linden closely trimmed in, and you will gain an idea of the landscape, full of tranquil chastity, modest cheerfulness, but commonplace withal, which surrounded the venerable edifice of the Cormon family. What peace! what tranquillity! nothing pretentious, but nothing transitory; all seems eternal there!

The ground-floor is devoted wholly to the reception-rooms. The old, unchangeable provincial spirit pervades them. The great square salon has four windows, modestly cased in woodwork painted gray. A single oblong mirror is placed above the fireplace; the top of its frame represented the Dawn led by the Hours, and painted in camaieu (two shades of one color). This style of painting infested the decorative art of the day, especially above door-frames, where the artist displayed his eternal Seasons, and made you, in most houses in the centre of France, abhor the odious Cupids, endlessly employed in skating, gleaning, twirling, or garlanding one another with flowers. Each window was draped in green damask curtains, looped up by heavy cords, which made them resemble a vast dais. The furniture, covered with tapestry, the woodwork, painted and varnished, and remarkable for the twisted forms so much the fashion in the last century, bore scenes from the fables of La Fontaine on the chair-backs; some of this tapestry had been mended. The ceiling was divided at the centre of the room by a huge beam, from which depended an old chandelier of rock-crystal swathed in green gauze. On the fireplace were two vases in Sevres blue, and two old girandoles attached to the frame of the mirror, and a clock, the subject of which, taken from the last scene of the “Deserteur,” proved the enormous popularity of Sedaine’s work. This clock, of bronze-gilt, bore eleven personages upon it, each about four inches tall. At the back the Deserter was seen issuing from prison between the soldiers; in the foreground the young woman lay fainting, and pointing to his pardon. On the walls of this salon were several of the more recent portraits of the family,—one or two by Rigaud, and three pastels by Latour. Four card tables, a backgammon board, and a piquet table occupied the vast room, the only one in the house, by the bye, which was ceiled.

The dining-room, paved in black and white stone, not ceiled, and its beams painted, was furnished with one of those enormous sideboards with marble tops, required by the war waged in the provinces against the human stomach. The walls, painted in fresco, represented a flowery trellis. The seats were of varnished cane, and the doors of natural wood. All things about the place carried out the patriarchal air which emanated from the inside as well as the outside of the house. The genius of the provinces preserved everything; nothing was new or old, neither young nor decrepit. A cold precision made itself felt throughout.

Tourists in Normandy, Brittany, Maine, and Anjou must all have seen in the capitals of those provinces many houses which resemble more or less that of the Cormons; for it is, in its way, an archetype of the burgher houses in that region of France, and it deserves a place in this history because it serves to explain manners and customs, and represents ideas. Who does not already feel that life must have been calm and monotonously regular in this old edifice? It contained a library; but that was placed below the level of the river. The books were well bound and shelved, and the dust, far from injuring them, only made them valuable. They were preserved with the care given in these provinces deprived of vineyards to other native products, desirable for their antique perfume, and issued by the presses of Bourgogne, Touraine, Gascogne, and the South. The cost of transportation was too great to allow any but the best products to be imported.

The basis of Mademoiselle Cormon’s society consisted of about one hundred and fifty persons; some went at times to the country; others were occasionally ill; a few travelled about the department on business; but certain of the faithful came every night (unless invited elsewhere), and so did certain others compelled by duties or by habit to live permanently in the town. All the personages were of ripe age; few among them had ever travelled; nearly all had spent their lives in the provinces, and some had taken part in the chouannerie. The latter were beginning to speak fearlessly of that war, now that rewards were being showered on the defenders of the good cause. Monsieur de Valois, one of the movers in the last uprising (during which the Marquis de Montauran, betrayed by his mistress, perished in spite of the devotion of Marche-a-Terre, now tranquilly raising cattle for the market near Mayenne),—Monsieur de Valois had, during the last six months, given the key to several choice stratagems practised upon an old republican named Hulot, the commander of a demi-brigade stationed at Alencon from 1798 to 1800, who had left many memories in the place. [See “The Chouans.”]

The women of this society took little pains with their dress, except on Wednesdays, when Mademoiselle Cormon gave a dinner, on which occasion the guests invited on the previous Wednesday paid their “visit of digestion.” Wednesdays were gala days: the assembly was numerous; guests and visitors appeared in fiocchi; some women brought their sewing, knitting, or worsted work; the young girls were not ashamed to make patterns for the Alencon point lace, with the proceeds of which they paid for their personal expenses. Certain husbands brought their wives out of policy, for young men were few in that house; not a word could be whispered in any ear without attracting the attention of all; there was therefore no danger, either for young girls or wives, of love-making.

Every evening, at six o’clock, the long antechamber received its furniture. Each habitue brought his cane, his cloak, his lantern. All these persons knew each other so well, and their habits and ways were so familiarly patriarchal, that if by chance the old Abbe de Sponde was lying down, or Mademoiselle Cormon was in her chamber, neither Josette, the maid, nor Jacquelin, the man-servant, nor Mariette, the cook, informed them. The first comer received the second; then, when the company were sufficiently numerous for whist, piquet, or boston, they began the game without awaiting either the Abbe de Sponde or mademoiselle. If it was dark, Josette or Jacquelin would hasten to light the candles as soon as the first bell rang. Seeing the salon lighted up, the abbe would slowly hurry to come down. Every evening the backgammon and the piquet tables, the three boston tables, and the whist table were filled,—which gave occupation to twenty-five or thirty persons; but as many as forty were usually present. Jacquelin would then light the candles in the other rooms.

Between eight and nine o’clock the servants began to arrive in the antechamber to accompany their masters home; and, short of a revolution, no one remained in the salon at ten o’clock. At that hour the guests were departing in groups along the street, discoursing on the game, or continuing conversations on the land they were covetous of buying, on the terms of some one’s will, on quarrels among heirs, on the haughty assumption of the aristocratic portion of the community. It was like Paris when the audience of a theatre disperses.

Certain persons who talk much of poesy and know nothing about it, declaim against the habits of life in the provinces. But put your forehead in your left hand, rest one foot on the fender, and your elbow on your knee; then, if you compass the idea of this quiet and uniform scene, this house and its interior, this company and its interests, heightened by the pettiness of its intellect like goldleaf beaten between sheets of parchment, ask yourself, What is human life? Try to decide between him who scribbles jokes on Egyptian obelisks, and him who has “bostoned” for twenty years with Du Bousquier, Monsieur de Valois, Mademoiselle Cormon, the judge of the court, the king’s attorney, the Abbe de Sponde, Madame Granson, and tutti quanti. If the daily and punctual return of the same steps to the same path is not happiness, it imitates happiness so well that men driven by the storms of an agitated life to reflect upon the blessings of tranquillity would say that here was happiness enough.

To reckon the importance of Mademoiselle Cormon’s salon at its true value, it will suffice to say that the born statistician of the society, du Bousquier, had estimated that the persons who frequented it controlled one hundred and thirty-one votes in the electoral college, and mustered among themselves eighteen hundred thousand francs a year from landed estate in the neighborhood.

The town of Alencon, however, was not entirely represented by this salon. The higher aristocracy had a salon of their own; moreover, that of the receiver-general was like an administration inn kept by the government, where society danced, plotted, fluttered, loved, and supped. These two salons communicated by means of certain mixed individuals with the house of Cormon, and vice-versa; but the Cormon establishment sat severely in judgment on the two other camps. The luxury of their dinners was criticised; the ices at their balls were pondered; the behavior of the women, the dresses, and “novelties” there produced were discussed and disapproved.

Mademoiselle Cormon, a species of firm, as one might say, under whose name was comprised an imposing coterie, was naturally the aim and object of two ambitious men as deep and wily as the Chevalier de Valois and du Bousquier. To the one as well as to the other, she meant election as deputy, resulting, for the noble, in the peerage, for the purveyor, in a receiver-generalship. A leading salon is a difficult thing to create, whether in Paris or the provinces, and here was one already created. To marry Mademoiselle Cormon was to reign in Alencon. Athanase Granson, the only one of the three suitors for the hand of the old maid who no longer calculated profits, now loved her person as well as her fortune.

To employ the jargon of the day, is there not a singular drama in the situation of these four personages? Surely there is something odd and fantastic in three rivalries silently encompassing a woman who never guessed their existence, in spite of an eager and legitimate desire to be married. And yet, though all these circumstances make the spinsterhood of this old maid an extraordinary thing, it is not difficult to explain how and why, in spite of her fortune and her three lovers, she was still unmarried. In the first place, Mademoiselle Cormon, following the custom and rule of her house, had always desired to marry a nobleman; but from 1788 to 1798 public circumstances were very unfavorable to such pretensions. Though she wanted to be a woman of condition, as the saying is, she was horribly afraid of the Revolutionary tribunal. The two sentiments, equal in force, kept her stationary by a law as true in ethics as it is in statics. This state of uncertain expectation is pleasing to unmarried women as long as they feel themselves young, and in a position to choose a husband. France knows that the political system of Napoleon resulted in making many widows. Under that regime heiresses were entirely out of proportion in numbers to the bachelors who wanted to marry. When the Consulate restored internal order, external difficulties made the marriage of Mademoiselle Cormon as difficult to arrange as it had been in the past. If, on the one hand, Rose-Marie-Victoire refused to marry an old man, on the other, the fear of ridicule forbade her to marry a very young one.

In the provinces, families marry their sons early to escape the conscription. In addition to all this, she was obstinately determined not to marry a soldier: she did not intend to take a man and then give him up to the Emperor; she wanted him for herself alone. With these views, she found it therefore impossible, from 1804 to 1815, to enter the lists with young girls who were rivalling each other for suitable matches.

Besides her predilection for the nobility, Mademoiselle Cormon had another and very excusable mania: that of being loved for herself. You could hardly believe the lengths to which this desire led her. She employed her mind on setting traps for her possible lovers, in order to test their real sentiments. Her nets were so well laid that the luckless suitors were all caught, and succumbed to the test she applied to them without their knowledge. Mademoiselle Cormon did not study them; she watched them. A single word said heedlessly, a joke (that she often was unable to understand), sufficed to make her reject an aspirant as unworthy: this one had neither heart nor delicacy; that one told lies, and was not religious; a third only wanted to coin money under the cloak of marriage; another was not of a nature to make a woman happy; here she suspected hereditary gout; there certain immoral antecedents alarmed her. Like the Church, she required a noble priest at her altar; she even wanted to be married for imaginary ugliness and pretended defects, just as other women wish to be loved for the good qualities they have not, and for imaginary beauties. Mademoiselle Cormon’s ambition took its rise in the most delicate and sensitive feminine feeling; she longed to reward a lover by revealing to him a thousand virtues after marriage, as other women then betray the imperfections they have hitherto concealed. But she was ill understood. The noble woman met with none but common souls in whom the reckoning of actual interests was paramount, and who knew nothing of the nobler calculations of sentiment.

The farther she advanced towards that fatal epoch so adroitly called the “second youth,” the more her distrust increased. She affected to present herself in the most unfavorable light, and played her part so well that the last wooers hesitated to link their fate to that of a person whose virtuous blind-man’s-buff required an amount of penetration that men who want the virtuous ready-made would not bestow upon it. The constant fear of being married for her money rendered her suspicious and uneasy beyond all reason. She turned to the rich men; but the rich are in search of great marriages; she feared the poor men, in whom she denied the disinterestedness she sought so eagerly. After each disappointment in marriage, the poor lady, led to despise mankind, began to see them all in a false light. Her character acquired, necessarily, a secret misanthropy, which threw a tinge of bitterness into her conversation, and some severity into her eyes. Celibacy gave to her manners and habits a certain increasing rigidity; for she endeavored to sanctify herself in despair of fate. Noble vengeance! she was cutting for God the rough diamond rejected by man. Before long public opinion was against her; for society accepts the verdict an independent woman renders on herself by not marrying, either through losing suitors or rejecting them. Everybody supposed that these rejections were founded on secret reasons, always ill interpreted. One said she was deformed; another suggested some hidden fault; but the poor girl was really as pure as a saint, as healthy as an infant, and full of loving kindness; Nature had intended her for all the pleasures, all the joys, and all the fatigues of motherhood.

Mademoiselle Cormon did not possess in her person an obliging auxiliary to her desires. She had no other beauty than that very improperly called la beaute du diable, which consists of a buxom freshness of youth that the devil, theologically speaking, could never have,—though perhaps the expression may be explained by the constant desire that must surely possess him to cool and refresh himself. The feet of the heiress were broad and flat. Her leg, which she often exposed to sight by her manner (be it said without malice) of lifting her gown when it rained, could never have been taken for the leg of a woman. It was sinewy, with a thick projecting calf like a sailor’s. A stout waist, the plumpness of a wet-nurse, strong dimpled arms, red hands, were all in keeping with the swelling outlines and the fat whiteness of Norman beauty. Projecting eyes, undecided in color, gave to her face, the rounded outline of which had no dignity, an air of surprise and sheepish simplicity, which was suitable perhaps for an old maid. If Rose had not been, as she was, really innocent, she would have seemed so. An aquiline nose contrasted curiously with the narrowness of her forehead; for it is rare that that form of nose does not carry with it a fine brow. In spite of her thick red lips, a sign of great kindliness, the forehead revealed too great a lack of ideas to allow of the heart being guided by intellect; she was evidently benevolent without grace. How severely we reproach Virtue for its defects, and how full of indulgence we all are for the pleasanter qualities of Vice!

Chestnut hair of extraordinary length gave to Rose Cormon’s face a beauty which results from vigor and abundance,—the physical qualities most apparent in her person. In the days of her chief pretensions, Rose affected to hold her head at the three-quarter angle, in order to exhibit a very pretty ear, which detached itself from the blue-veined whiteness of her throat and temples, set off, as it was, by her wealth of hair. Seen thus in a ball-dress, she might have seemed handsome. Her protuberant outlines and her vigorous health did, in fact, draw from the officers of the Empire the approving exclamation,—

“What a fine slip of a girl!”

But, as years rolled on, this plumpness, encouraged by a tranquil, wholesome life, had insensibly so ill spread itself over the whole of Mademoiselle Cormon’s body that her primitive proportions were destroyed. At the present moment, no corset could restore a pair of hips to the poor lady, who seemed to have been cast in a single mould. The youthful harmony of her bosom existed no longer; and its excessive amplitude made the spectator fear that if she stooped its heavy masses might topple her over. But nature had provided against this by giving her a natural counterpoise, which rendered needless the deceitful adjunct of a bustle; in Rose Cormon everything was genuine. Her chin, as it doubled, reduced the length of her neck, and hindered the easy carriage of her head. Rose had no wrinkles, but she had folds of flesh; and jesters declared that to save chafing she powdered her skin as they do an infant’s.

This ample person offered to a young man full of ardent desires like Athanase an attraction to which he had succumbed. Young imaginations, essentially eager and courageous, like to rove upon these fine living sheets of flesh. Rose was like a plump partridge attracting the knife of a gourmet. Many an elegant deep in debt would very willingly have resigned himself to make the happiness of Mademoiselle Cormon. But, alas! the poor girl was now forty years old. At this period, after vainly seeking to put into her life those interests which make the Woman, and finding herself forced to be still unmarried, she fortified her virtue by stern religious practices. She had recourse to religion, the great consoler of oppressed virginity. A confessor had, for the last three years, directed Mademoiselle Cormon rather stupidly in the path of maceration; he advised the use of scourging, which, if modern medical science is to be believed, produces an effect quite the contrary to that expected by the worthy priest, whose hygienic knowledge was not extensive.

These absurd practices were beginning to shed a monastic tint over the face of Rose Cormon, who now saw with something like despair her white skin assuming the yellow tones which proclaim maturity. A slight down on her upper lip, about the corners, began to spread and darken like a trail of smoke; her temples grew shiny; decadence was beginning! It was authentic in Alencon that Mademoiselle Cormon suffered from rush of blood to the head. She confided her ills to the Chevalier de Valois, enumerating her foot-baths, and consulting him as to refrigerants. On such occasions the shrewd old gentleman would pull out his snuff-box, gaze at the Princess Goritza, and say, by way of conclusion:—

“The right composing draught, my dear lady, is a good and kind husband.”

“But whom can one trust?” she replied.

The chevalier would then brush away the snuff which had settled in the folds of his waistcoat or his paduasoy breeches. To the world at large this gesture would have seemed very natural; but it always gave extreme uneasiness to the poor woman.

The violence of this hope without an object was so great that Rose was afraid to look a man in the face lest he should perceive in her eyes the feelings that filled her soul. By a wilfulness, which was perhaps only the continuation of her earlier methods, though she felt herself attracted toward the men who might still suit her, she was so afraid of being accused of folly that she treated them ungraciously. Most persons in her society, being incapable of appreciating her motives, which were always noble, explained her manner towards her co-celibates as the revenge of a refusal received or expected. When the year 1815 began, Rose had reached that fatal age which she dared not avow. She was forty-two years old. Her desire for marriage then acquired an intensity which bordered on monomania, for she saw plainly that all chance of progeny was about to escape her; and the thing which in her celestial ignorance she desired above all things was the possession of children. Not a person in all Alencon ever attributed to this virtuous woman a single desire for amorous license. She loved, as it were, in bulk without the slightest imagination of love. Rose was a Catholic Agnes, incapable of inventing even one of the wiles of Moliere’s Agnes.

For some months past she had counted on chance. The disbandment of the Imperial troops and the reorganization of the Royal army caused a change in the destination of many officers, who returned, some on half-pay, others with or without a pension, to their native towns,—all having a desire to counteract their luckless fate, and to end their life in a way which might to Rose Cormon be a happy beginning of hers. It would surely be strange if, among those who returned to Alencon or its neighborhood, no brave, honorable, and, above all, sound and healthy officer of suitable age could be found, whose character would be a passport among Bonaparte opinions; or some ci-devant noble who, to regain his lost position, would join the ranks of the royalists. This hope kept Mademoiselle Cormon in heart during the early months of that year. But, alas! all the soldiers who thus returned were either too old or too young; too aggressively Bonapartist, or too dissipated; in short, their several situations were out of keeping with the rank, fortune, and morals of Mademoiselle Cormon, who now grew daily more and more desperate. The poor woman in vain prayed to God to send her a husband with whom she could be piously happy: it was doubtless written above that she should die both virgin and martyr; no man suitable for a husband presented himself. The conversations in her salon every evening kept her informed of the arrival of all strangers in Alencon, and of the facts of their fortunes, rank, and habits. But Alencon is not a town which attracts visitors; it is not on the road to any capital; even sailors, travelling from Brest to Paris, never stop there. The poor woman ended by admitting to herself that she was reduced to the aborigines. Her eye now began to assume a certain savage expression, to which the malicious chevalier responded by a shrewd look as he drew out his snuff-box and gazed at the Princess Goritza. Monsieur de Valois was well aware that in the feminine ethics of love fidelity to a first attachment is considered a pledge for the future.

But Mademoiselle Cormon—we must admit it—was wanting in intellect, and did not understand the snuff-box performance. She redoubled her vigilance against “the evil spirit”; her rigid devotion and fixed principles kept her cruel sufferings hidden among the mysteries of private life. Every evening, after the company had left her, she thought of her lost youth, her faded bloom, the hopes of thwarted nature; and, all the while immolating her passions at the feet of the Cross (like poems condemned to stay in a desk), she resolved firmly that if, by chance, any suitor presented himself, to subject him to no tests, but to accept him at once for whatever he might be. She even went so far as to think of marrying a sub-lieutenant, a man who smoked tobacco, whom she proposed to render, by dint of care and kindness, one of the best men in the world, although he was hampered with debts.

But it was only in the silence of night watches that these fantastic marriages, in which she played the sublime role of guardian angel, took place. The next day, though Josette found her mistress’ bed in a tossed and tumbled condition, Mademoiselle Cormon had recovered her dignity, and could only think of a man of forty, a land-owner, well preserved, and a quasi-young man.

The Abbe de Sponde was incapable of giving his niece the slightest aid in her matrimonial manoeuvres. The worthy soul, now seventy years of age, attributed the disasters of the French Revolution to the design of Providence, eager to punish a dissolute Church. He had therefore flung himself into the path, long since abandoned, which anchorites once followed in order to reach heaven: he led an ascetic life without proclaiming it, and without external credit. He hid from the world his works of charity, his continual prayers, his penances; he thought that all priests should have acted thus during the days of wrath and terror, and he preached by example. While presenting to the world a calm and smiling face, he had ended by detaching himself utterly from earthly interests; his mind turned exclusively to sufferers, to the needs of the Church, and to his own salvation. He left the management of his property to his niece, who gave him the income of it, and to whom he paid a slender board in order to spend the surplus in secret alms and gifts to the Church.

All the abbe’s affections were concentrated on his niece, who regarded him as a father, but an abstracted father, unable to conceive the agitations of the flesh, and thanking God for maintaining his dear daughter in a state of celibacy; for he had, from his youth up, adopted the principles of Saint John Chrysostom, who wrote that “the virgin state is as far above the marriage state as the angel is above humanity.” Accustomed to reverence her uncle, Mademoiselle Cormon dared not initiate him into the desires which filled her soul for a change of state. The worthy man, accustomed, on his side, to the ways of the house, would scarcely have liked the introduction of a husband. Preoccupied by the sufferings he soothed, lost in the depths of prayer, the Abbe de Sponde had periods of abstraction which the habitues of the house regarded as absent-mindedness. In any case, he talked little; but his silence was affable and benevolent. He was a man of great height and spare, with grave and solemn manners, though his face expressed all gentle sentiments and an inward calm; while his mere presence carried with it a sacred authority. He was very fond of the Voltairean chevalier. Those two majestic relics of the nobility and clergy, though of very different habits and morals, recognized each other by their generous traits. Besides, the chevalier was as unctuous with the abbe as he was paternal with the grisettes.

Some persons may fancy that Mademoiselle Cormon used every means to attain her end; and that among the legitimate lures of womanhood she devoted herself to dress, wore low-necked gowns, and employed the negative coquetries of a magnificent display of arms. Not at all! She was as heroic and immovable in her high-necked chemisette as a sentry in his box. Her gowns, bonnets, and chiffons were all cut and made by the dressmaker and the milliner of Alencon, two hump-backed sisters, who were not without some taste. In spite of the entreaties of these artists, Mademoiselle Cormon refused to employ the airy deceits of elegance; she chose to be substantial in all things, flesh and feathers. But perhaps the heavy fashion of her gowns was best suited to her cast of countenance. Let those laugh who will at this poor girl; you would have thought her sublime, O generous souls! who care but little what form true feeling takes, but admire it where it is.

Here some light-minded person may exclaim against the truth of this statement; they will say that there is not in all France a girl so silly as to be ignorant of the art of angling for men; that Mademoiselle Cormon is one of those monstrous exceptions which commonsense should prevent a writer from using as a type; that the most virtuous and also the silliest girl who desires to catch her fish knows well how to bait the hook. But these criticisms fall before the fact that the noble catholic, apostolic, and Roman religion is still erect in Brittany and in the ancient duchy of Alencon. Faith and piety admit of no subtleties. Mademoiselle Cormon trod the path of salvation, preferring the sorrows of her virginity so cruelly prolonged to the evils of trickery and the sin of a snare. In a woman armed with a scourge virtue could never compromise; consequently both love and self-interest were forced to seek her, and seek her resolutely. And here let us have the courage to make a cruel observation, in days when religion is nothing more than a useful means to some, and a poesy to others. Devotion causes a moral ophthalmia. By some providential grace, it takes from souls on the road to eternity the sight of many little earthly things. In a word, pious persons, devotes, are stupid on various points. This stupidity proves with what force they turn their minds to celestial matters; although the Voltairean Chevalier de Valois declared that it was difficult to decide whether stupid people became naturally pious, or whether piety had the effect of making intelligent young women stupid. But reflect upon this carefully: the purest catholic virtue, with its loving acceptance of all cups, with its pious submission to the will of God, with its belief in the print of the divine finger on the clay of all earthly life, is the mysterious light which glides into the innermost folds of human history, setting them in relief and magnifying them in the eyes of those who still have Faith. Besides, if there be stupidity, why not concern ourselves with the sorrows of stupidity as well as with the sorrows of genius? The former is a social element infinitely more abundant than the latter.

So, then, Mademoiselle Cormon was guilty in the eyes of the world of the divine ignorance of virgins. She was no observer, and her behavior with her suitors proved it. At this very moment, a young girl of sixteen, who had never opened a novel, would have read a hundred chapters of a love story in the eyes of Athanase Granson, where Mademoiselle Cormon saw absolutely nothing. Shy herself, she never suspected shyness in others; she did not recognize in the quavering tones of his speech the force of a sentiment he could not utter. Capable of inventing those refinements of sentimental grandeur which hindered her marriage in her early years, she yet could not recognize them in Athanase. This moral phenomenon will not seem surprising to persons who know that the qualities of the heart are as distinct from those of the mind as the faculties of genius are from the nobility of soul. A perfect, all-rounded man is so rare that Socrates, one of the noblest pearls of humanity, declared (as a phrenologist of that day) that he was born to be a scamp, and a very bad one. A great general may save his country at Zurich, and take commissions from purveyors. A great musician may conceive the sublimest music and commit a forgery. A woman of true feeling may be a fool. In short, a devote may have a sublime soul and yet be unable to recognize the tones of a noble soul beside her. The caprices produced by physical infirmities are equally to be met with in the mental and moral regions.

This good creature, who grieved at making her yearly preserves for no one but her uncle and herself, was becoming almost ridiculous. Those who felt a sympathy for her on account of her good qualities, and others on account of her defects, now made fun of her abortive marriages. More than one conversation was based on what would become of so fine a property, together with the old maid’s savings and her uncle’s inheritance. For some time past she had been suspected of being au fond, in spite of appearances, an “original.” In the provinces it was not permissible to be original: being original means having ideas that are not understood by others; the provinces demand equality of mind as well as equality of manners and customs.

The marriage of Mademoiselle Cormon seemed, after 1804, a thing so problematical that the saying “married like Mademoiselle Cormon” became proverbial in Alencon as applied to ridiculous failures. Surely the sarcastic mood must be an imperative need in France, that so excellent a woman should excite the laughter of Alencon. Not only did she receive the whole society of the place at her house, not only was she charitable, pious, incapable of saying an unkind thing, but she was fully in accord with the spirit of the place and the habits and customs of the inhabitants, who liked her as the symbol of their lives; she was absolutely inlaid into the ways of the provinces; she had never quitted them; she imbibed all their prejudices; she espoused all their interests; she adored them.

In spite of her income of eighteen thousand francs from landed property, a very considerable fortune in the provinces, she lived on a footing with families who were less rich. When she went to her country-place at Prebaudet, she drove there in an old wicker carriole, hung on two straps of white leather, drawn by a wheezy mare, and scarcely protected by two leather curtains rusty with age. This carriole, known to all the town, was cared for by Jacquelin as though it were the finest coupe in all Paris. Mademoiselle valued it; she had used it for twelve years,—a fact to which she called attention with the triumphant joy of happy avarice. Most of the inhabitants of the town were grateful to Mademoiselle Cormon for not humiliating them by the luxury she could have displayed; we may even believe that had she imported a caleche from Paris they would have gossiped more about that than about her various matrimonial failures. The most brilliant equipage would, after all, have only taken her, like the old carriole, to Prebaudet. Now the provinces, which look solely to results, care little about the beauty or elegance of the means, provided they are efficient.

To complete the picture of the household’s internal habits, it is necessary to place about Mademoiselle Cormon and the Abbé de Sponde the figures of Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette, the cook, who provided for the uncle and niece’s comfort.

Jacquelin, about forty, short, fat, ruddy, with a Breton-sailor face, had been in their service twenty-two years. He waited at table, groomed the mare, gardened, blacked the abbé’s boots, ran errands, chopped wood, drove the carriole, fetched oats, straw, and hay from Prébaudet, and sat in the antechamber evenings, where he slept like a dormouse. He was in love with Josette, a thirty-year-old short, plump woman, whose face, “like a dirty apricot,” showed sense and character; it was said she ruled her mistress. Mademoiselle would have dismissed Josette had she married Jacquelin, so the pair, resigned, saved their wages and loved silently, awaiting—like Jews awaiting the Messiah—some distant change. Mariette, the cook of fifteen years’ standing, knew how to prepare the most esteemed dishes of Alençon.

The fat old Norman mare, Penelope, drawn to Prébaudet by Mademoiselle Cormon, deserves mention: she had served the family eighteen years and was treated with the same affectionate mania as any human servant. Kept carefully and fed regularly, Mademoiselle and Jacquelin hoped to use her ten years more. The household’s four dependents—raised and lowered in their mutual subservience to the beast—moved each day with mechanical accuracy. As they said, “they had eaten their white bread first.” Mademoiselle Cormon, driven by a fixed idea, grew hard to please, petty, and nagging—less by nature than in order to occupy her active mind. Without husband or children, she fell back on minute details: napkins marked “Z” before the “O’s,” whether Penelope had her oats at two o’clock, a neglected dust-layer, a poorly made toast, Josette’s delay in closing blinds that faded furniture. Small things became grounds for serious quarrels. Once Josette gave her the “Journée du Chrétien” instead of the “Quinzaine de Pâques”; Mademoiselle left church and returned home in embarrassment; the town heard of the calamity. She would admonish Josette gently: “Such a thing must never happen again.”

These little quarrels served as catharses for Mademoiselle Cormon, and in secret she was steadied by them. Josette and Jacquelin accepted the mood swings as weather-changes: “It is fine to-day,” or “It rains,” without arraigning the heavens. Each morning they wondered in what humor Mademoiselle would rise, as farmers wonder at dawn mists.

She had ended, naturally, contemplating herself in the infinite pettinesses of life: herself and God, her confessor and the weekly wash, her preserves and church services, and her uncle to care for, occupied her feeble intellect. Her perfect health magnified the slightest digestive trouble. She lived under the iron rod of medical practice, taking quarterly precautionary doses strong enough, it was said, to have killed Penelope but seeming to rejuvenate her mistress. A pimple on her satiny shoulder occasioned endless inquiries into the week’s alimentary articles until Josette triumphantly attributed it to a high hare or to Mariette’s over-seasoning: “No doubt, no doubt, it was the hare!”

Four times a year she spent a number of days at her estate, Prébaudet. In mid-May she liked to see how her apple-trees had “snowed” (the fallen blossoms resembling snow promised an abundant cider harvest), attend winter repairs, order the digging of flower-beds and the vegetable garden that supplied her table. Every season had its business. Before departing she always gave a farewell dinner to intimate friends, though she expected to see them again within weeks. Her departure was the town’s news: shopkeepers repeated it from door to door—“So Mademoiselle Cormon is going to Prébaudet!”—with all manner of provincial commentary.

The Wednesday Suzanne chose to reveal her scandal happened to be such a farewell Wednesday, when Mademoiselle was driving Josette distracted packing. Madame Granson had spent the morning visiting houses, and the malicious Chevalier de Valois was playing piquet with Mademoiselle Armande, who governed aristocratic salons. The scene was set: the chevalier and Madame Granson wanted to see how Mademoiselle Cormon, in her double capacity as marriageable woman and president of the Maternity Society, would receive the news. Du Bousquier, the innocent suitor, walked on the promenade, beginning to suspect Suzanne had tricked him.

On gala days Mademoiselle Cormon’s table was laid about half-past three; the fashionable dined at four. One of her chief pleasures was seeing herself dressed as mistress of the house to receive guests. When fully equipped, a ray of hope would glide into her heart; she felt that nature had not provided in vain and that some brave man would present himself. She revelled in inspecting the dining-room, the oblong table set with a snow-white cloth and twenty covers, the squadron of bottles with honorable labels, the names on little paper bits in the trembling hand of the abbé—the only duty he assumed. After these preliminaries she would go in her finery to her uncle, who took his walk on the terrace overlooking the Brillante and the birds in the coppice. In waiting she never joined the Abbé de Sponde without asking some ridiculous question to rouse him from his meditative silence; she regarded it as her duty to talk and make herself agreeable, a practice her spiritual director, the Abbé Couturier, had advised. She perspired in her corset thinking up ideas; her efforts produced such absurdities as: “No one can be in two places at once—unless it is a little bird,” which somehow provoked a debate on the apostles’ ubiquity. Such talk made the beaux esprits call her “that good Mademoiselle Cormon”—a polite way of saying she was rather a fool; but many of her peers took it literally, answering, “Yes; oh yes! Mademoiselle Cormon is an excellent woman.”

When conversation flagged the Chevalier de Valois, who loved to assist women, would transform her sayings into wit by paradoxically sustaining them; once, when she could not distinguish an ox from a bull she was rescued by the chevalier claiming the only difference was as between sheep and lamb. He often covered her retreats so well that she seemed never to have said anything silly. She took his help as proof of her own adequacy and grew complacent in her ignorance, acquiring a self-possession that gave her speeches a solemnity like patriotic absurdities.

Approaching her uncle one day, she tried to draw him into a matrimonial discussion.

“Uncle,” she said, leaning on his arm, “if everything here below happens according to the will of God, there must be a reason for everything.”

“Certainly,” replied the abbé.

“Then if I remain unmarried—supposing that I do—God wills it?”

“Yes, my child.”

“And yet, as nothing prevents me from marrying to-morrow if I choose, His will can be destroyed by mine?”

“That would be true if we knew what was really the will of God,” replied the former prior of the Sorbonne. “Observe, my daughter, that you put in an if.”

Such childish logic perplexed her. “But, uncle, God did not make women intending them not to marry; otherwise they ought all to stay unmarried; if not, they ought all to marry. There’s great injustice in the distribution of parts.”

“You are blaming the Church, which declares celibacy the better way to God,” replied the abbé.

“But if the Church is right, and all the world were good Catholics, wouldn’t the human race come to an end, uncle?”

“You have too much mind, Rose; you don’t need so much to be happy.”

That remark pleased her and confirmed her good opinion of herself.

Guests arrived and the familiarities between servants and company began: Mariette telling Monsieur du Ronceret she had cooked cauliflowers au gratin “expressly for you, for Mademoiselle knows how you like them”; Josette informing Madame Granson that “Mademoiselle has thought of you, and there’s fish for dinner”; the Chevalier de Valois, in his easy, condescending tone, asking Mariette if there was some dainty he should reserve himself for. Du Bousquier was not invited. Mademoiselle Cormon, faithful to her ambivalence, treated the fifty-year-old suitor ill though she felt inexplicable sentiments toward him. She had refused him yet sometimes repented; a presentiment haunted her that she might yet marry him, and terror prevented her desiring it. Madame Granson and the chevalier, observing her naive glances toward du Bousquier, resolved to ruin his hopes.

Two tardy guests—Monsieur du Coudrai, recorder of mortgages, and Monsieur Choisnel, the notary—kept dinner waiting. As they arrived, Jacquelin announced “They are all in the garden.” The company strolled the terrace above the river, looking at water-plants, mosaics of currents, houses across the Sarthe with old wooden galleries and little gardens where clothes dried; a charming provincial landscape.

The chevalier scanned faces; he had introduced his firebrand into the chief houses. The scandal about Suzanne and du Bousquier had not yet been openly discussed: provincials distil gossip, waiting the right moment. Whispers circulated: “You have heard?” “Yes.” “Du Bousquier?” “And that handsome Suzanne.” “Does Mademoiselle Cormon know of it?” “No.” “Ha!”

The chevalier, noting Madame Granson’s triumphant look, suspected not only her joy but self-interest. He watched Athanase and saw, in a furtive look at Mademoiselle Cormon, a flash that set his plans awry. He offered his arm to Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he revered. She, secretly wishing for the alliance, admired the name de Valois as a social object, though his seeming purity and indifference worried her; she wanted a husband a little dissipated—someone with experience to purify. Observers have remarked pious women’s leaning toward scamps; Mademoiselle’s wish shows this: she would have liked a man to combine the chevalier’s nobility with du Bousquier’s vigor.

To explain what followed, two matters agitated the town, matters in which du Bousquier was implicated. One concerned the rector of Alençon, who had once taken the constitutional oath but was winning Catholics by high virtues. He had supporters among the middle classes rallied by du Bousquier, while Mademoiselle Cormon and the Abbé de Sponde, belonging to the uncompromising “little Church,” refused to receive him. The second was du Bousquier’s idea of building a theatre in Alençon; Athanase Granson and other young clerks championed the project. The chevalier urged Mademoiselle Cormon to save Athanase from this influence.

“Mademoiselle,” he said, “you have so much sense and judgment in social proprieties and influence with his mother; ought you not to use it to save him from perdition? He is irreligious, liberal, agitating for a theatre, the dupe of that disguised republican du Bousquier.”

Mademoiselle responded that his mother said he had so much mind, “and yet he can’t say two words; he stands planted before me as mum as a post—”

“Which doesn’t think at all!” cried the recorder, and the chevalier led Mademoiselle to a flower-pot to speak privately. He warned that lads raised in lycées had bad morals, were marched about in uniform, and, he implied, Athanase’s shyness and pale looks foretold a sad end. He urged her to warn the mother that her son might lose his place in the mayor’s office for such associations.

Madame Granson, who had heard part of the talk, followed Mademoiselle, hoping to learn the outcome. But Jacquelin announced dinner in, and the recorder of mortgages, sensing his opportunity, offered his arm and cut off Monsieur de Valois. The chevalier then addressed Madame Granson directly, warning that Athanase’s theatre intrigues and rector sympathies could cost his job; she gratefully promised to enlighten him.

The chevalier had long seen Athanase’s unyielding republican convictions—rooted in artistic sorrows and a bitter view of society—and knew he would persist. To remain faithful to the Left in Alençon was to earn Mademoiselle Cormon’s aversion.

Thus this outwardly peaceful society was internally agitated by craft and passion like any diplomatic circle. The guests sat and ate as provincials, with appetites undisturbed by Parisian sumptuary fads. Toward the end of the first course Mademoiselle Cormon made the celebrated remark that would be repeated for two years: in the debate against du Bousquier—whom the chevalier and others depicted as a pernicious intriguer—she had innocently said, “As for me, I thought that Monsieur du Bousquier cared chiefly for childish things.” The phrase had enormous success: it undermined the Princess Goritza’s dignity and astonished the chevalier into silent admiration; he applauded with fingers as they do at the Opera.

Prompted by the chevalier, du Bousquier was caricatured as a monster, a Pere Gigogne who had supplied the Foundling Hospital for fifteen years, his immoral habits explained as the cause of Parisian revels. The chevalier, leading the cancan, speculated that a du Bousquier might marry a “Suzette,” invoking Madame du Barry and the Emperor Joseph; Mademoiselle Cormon corrected him with naïve historicism. The Abbé de Sponde, touched with charitable solemnity, urged succor for the poor girl now unlikely to marry. The judge, Madame Granson, and others volleyed puns and jests until the Abbe de Sponde’s grave “Nor can he be a foster-father” quieted them.

Usually a man is ignorant of town rumors; du Bousquier remained superbly unaware of Suzanne’s revelations and was jaunty at coffee. Mademoiselle Cormon, embarrassed, chose to lecture Athanase with platitudes about royalist politics and morality. Athanase listened with a stupid air, gazing at her enormous bust and swelling in his love, which transformed her shrill voice into a soft murmur and her flat remarks into witticisms. Love, the narrator notes, is a maker of false coin, turning copper into gold.

“Will you promise me?” she asked Athanase, and the words pierced his absorbed mind. Had he spoken then, he might have revealed his passion; but his shy absorption ruined him.

“What have you been saying to Mademoiselle Cormon?” demanded his mother.

“Nothing.”

Madame Granson, who believed du Bousquier forever lost in Mademoiselle’s esteem after that evening, thought little more of the remark. The salon later filled: four tables set for piquet, others for backgammon; the group gathered by the fire in evening finery, each repeating their farewell: “So you are going tomorrow to Prébaudet?” “Yes, I really must.”

Mademoiselle Cormon appeared preoccupied; Madame Granson noticed she was thinking.

“What are you thinking of, cousin?” she asked.

“I am thinking,” Mademoiselle replied, “of that poor girl. As president of the Maternity Society, I will give you fifty francs for her.”

“Fifty francs!” cried Madame Granson in astonishment. Mademoiselle never gave so much, but her sudden generosity showed du Bousquier had advanced in her estimation.

“Du Bousquier is not only a monster, he is a villain,” said Madame Granson. “When a man has done a wrong like that, he ought to pay the indemnity. Isn’t it his place rather than ours to look after the girl?—who, to tell you the truth, seems rather questionable.”

Mademoiselle, who could not quite grasp how hearts work, said innocently that perhaps one could be calumniated for loving Athanase, yet be loved truly. Madame Granson, exasperated by her cousin’s naiveté, tried to explain the physical side of things: “It is impossible for Suzanne to love du Bousquier. And if the heart counts for nothing in this affair—” “But, cousin, what do people love with if not their hearts?” inquired the old maid, widening her eyes in honest puzzlement.

Madame Granson advised a show of displeasure: not receiving du Bousquier in her house until he married would manifest exemplary morals. Mademoiselle promised to consult her uncle and the Abbé Couturier upon her return from Prébaudet.

The lights, the women in best clothes, the solemn assembly made Mademoiselle Cormon proud: to many it was the best of Parisian society. Du Bousquier, whistling the pastoral air, was the centre of curiosity. Some declared him strong and misjudged; others saw danger in him. The chevalier, going home, planned to spread a rumor of his marriage to Mademoiselle Cormon to secure attention and advantage, to use his connections to become mayor and then deputy, dreaming of peerage and power, imagining how the marriage would serve his ambitions.

When Madame Granson reached home she fiercely argued with her son Athanase, who could not see the link between his political opinions and his love—a quarrel the household had never known before.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

The next day, Mademoiselle Cormon, packed into the old carriole with Josette and looking like a pyramid on a sea of parcels, drove up the rue Saint-Blaise toward Prebaudet, where an event hurried on her marriage—an event entirely unlooked for by Madame Granson, du Bousquier, Monsieur de Valois, or Mademoiselle Cormon herself. Chance is the greatest of artificers.

The day after her arrival at Prebaudet, about eight o’clock, she was listening at breakfast to reports from her keeper and gardener when Jacquelin burst into the dining-room.

“Mademoiselle,” he cried, out of breath, “Monsieur l’abbé sends you an express, the son of Mère Grosmort, with a letter. The lad left Alençon before daylight; he ran like Penelope! Can’t I give him a glass of wine?”

“What can have happened, Josette? Do you think my uncle can be—”

“He couldn’t write if he were,” said Josette, guessing her mistress’s fears.

“Quick! quick!” cried Mademoiselle Cormon, on reading the first lines. “Tell Jacquelin to harness Penelope—Get ready, Josette; pack up everything in half an hour. We must go back to town—”

“Jacquelin!” called Josette, excited by the look on her mistress’s face.

Jacquelin entered to say, “But, mademoiselle, Penelope is eating her oats.”

“What does that signify? I must start at once.”

“But, mademoiselle, it is going to rain.”

“Then we shall get wet.”

“The house is on fire!” muttered Josette, piqued at her mistress’s silence about the letter, which she read and reread.

“Finish your coffee at any rate, mademoiselle; don’t excite your blood; just see how red you are.”

“Am I red, Josette?” she said, going to a mirror, from which the quicksilver was peeling, and which presented her features upside down.

“Good heavens!” thought Mademoiselle Cormon. “Suppose I should look ugly! Come, Josette; dress me at once; I want to be ready before Jacquelin has harnessed Penelope. If you can’t pack my things in time, I will leave them here rather than lose a single minute.”

If you have comprehended the monomania to which the desire of marriage had brought Mademoiselle Cormon, you will share her emotion. The worthy uncle announced in this sudden missive that Monsieur de Troisville, of the Russian army during the Emigration, grandson of one of his best friends, desired to retire to Alençon and asked the abbe’s hospitality on the ground of his friendship for the Vicomte de Troisville. Alarmed at the responsibility, the old abbe entreated his niece to return instantly and help him receive this guest; the viscount’s letter had been delayed, and he might descend upon his shoulders that very night.

After reading this, could there be a question of Prebaudet’s demands? The keeper and gardener, witnesses to Mademoiselle Cormon’s excitement, stood aside and awaited orders. But as she was about to leave, they asked for instructions, and for the first time the despotic old maid, who oversaw Prebaudet with minute care, said, to their stupefaction, “Do what you like.” This from a mistress who even counted her fruits and marked them to order their consumption!

“I believe I’m dreaming,” thought Josette, as she watched her mistress fly down the staircase like an elephant made to fly.

In spite of a driving rain, Mademoiselle Cormon drove away from Prebaudet, leaving her factotums with the reins on their necks. Jacquelin dared not hasten the usual little trot of the peaceable Penelope, who seemed to make as many steps backward as forward. Impatient, mademoiselle ordered Jacquelin sharply to drive at a gallop, whip if necessary, so afraid was she of not having time to arrange the house suitably to receive Monsieur de Troisville. She calculated the grandson was probably about forty; a soldier just from service was undoubtedly a bachelor; with her uncle’s aid she resolved not to let Monsieur de Troisville quit their house as he entered it. Though Penelope galloped, Mademoiselle Cormon, absorbed in thoughts of her trousseau and wedding-day, declared again and again that Jacquelin made no way. She twisted in the carriole, unresponsive to Josette’s questions, talking to herself as if revolving important designs.

The carriole at last reached Alençon’s main street. If her departure had made noise, her sudden return in a pouring rain created an uproar: Penelope at a full gallop, Jacquelin’s grin, the early hour, the parcels half-packed, and Mademoiselle Cormon’s impatience were remarked.

The Troisville property lay between Alençon and Mortagne. Josette knew the family branches. A word dropped by her mistress put Josette on the scent; they settled that the expected de Troisville must be between forty and forty-two, a bachelor, neither rich nor poor. Mademoiselle Cormon beheld herself soon Vicomtesse de Troisville.

“And to think my uncle told me nothing! thinks of nothing! inquires nothing! That’s my uncle all over. He’d forget his own nose if it wasn’t fastened to his face.”

Under such circumstances old maids become keen-witted, fierce, bold, and promissory—and like inebriate clerks no longer in awe of anything.

Immediately Alençon, from the far end of the rue Saint-Blaise to the gate of Seez, was perturbed. Cooks, shopkeepers, street-passers told the news; it rose to upper circles. Soon the words: “Mademoiselle Cormon has returned!” burst like a bombshell into all households. At that moment Jacquelin was descending from his polished wooden seat on the carriole and opened the great green gate, round at the top, and closed it in sign of mourning; during Mademoiselle Cormon’s absence the evening assemblies had not taken place. The faithful invited the Abbe de Sponde to their houses; Monsieur de Valois repaid the debt by inviting him to dine at the Marquis d’Esgrignon’s. Jacquelin, having opened the gate, called to Penelope, who turned in of herself and circled the courtyard to avoid the flower-bed. He then took her bridle and led the carriage to the portico.

“Mariette!” cried Mademoiselle Cormon.

“Mademoiselle!” exclaimed Mariette, closing the gate.

“Has the gentleman arrived?”

“No, mademoiselle.”

“Where’s my uncle?”

“He is at church, mademoiselle.”

Jacquelin and Josette were by this time on the portico steps, ready to help their mistress out of the carriole as she clung to the curtains. She threw herself into their arms; for two years she had dared not risk her weight on the iron step, affixed by a clumsy mechanism.

At the portico level she surveyed her courtyard with satisfaction.

“Come, come, Mariette, leave that gate alone; I want you.”

“There’s something in the wind,” whispered Jacquelin, as Mariette passed.

“Mariette, what provisions have you in the house?” asked Mademoiselle Cormon, sitting on the bench in the antechamber like one overcome with fatigue.

“I haven’t anything,” replied Mariette, hands on hips. “Mademoiselle knows that during her absence Monsieur l’abbé dines out every day. Yesterday I went to fetch him from Mademoiselle Armande’s.”

“Where is he now?”

“At church; he won’t be in before three o’clock.”

“He thinks of nothing! he ought to have told you to go to market. Mariette, go at once; and without wasting money, don’t spare it; get all there is that is good and delicate. Go to the diligence office and see if you can send for pâtés; and I want shrimps from the Brillante. What o’clock is it?”

“A quarter to nine.”

“Good heavens! Mariette, don’t stop to chatter. The person my uncle expects may arrive at any moment. If we had to give him breakfast, where should we be with nothing in the house?”

Mariette turned back to Penelope in a lather, and looked at Jacquelin as if to say, “Mademoiselle has put her hand on a husband this time.”

“Now, Josette,” continued the old maid, “let us see where we had better put Monsieur de Troisville to sleep.”

With what joy she said, “Put Monsieur de Troisville to sleep.” How many ideas in those few words! The old maid was bathed in hope.

“Will you put him in the green chamber?”

“The bishop’s room? No; that’s too near mine. All very well for monseigneur; he’s a saintly man.”

“Give him your uncle’s room.”

“Oh, that’s so bare; it is indecent.”

“Well, then, why not arrange a bed in your boudoir? It is easily done; and there’s a fireplace. Moreau can find a bed to match the hangings.”

“You are right, Josette. Go yourself to Moreau; consult with him what to do; I authorize you to get what is wanted. If the bed could be put up to-night without Monsieur de Troisville observing it, I should like it. If Moreau won’t engage to do this, then I must put Monsieur de Troisville in the green room, though he would be very near to me.”

Josette was departing when recalled.

“Stop! explain to Jacquelin,” she cried. “Tell him to go to Moreau; I must be dressed! Fancy if Monsieur de Troisville surprised me as I am now! and my uncle not here to receive him! Oh, uncle, uncle! Come, Josette; dress me at once.”

“But Penelope?” said Josette.

“Always Penelope! Penelope this, Penelope that! Is Penelope the mistress of this house?”

“But she is all of a lather, and she hasn’t had time to eat her oats.”

“Then let her starve!” cried Mademoiselle Cormon; “provided I marry,” she thought.

Hearing this, Josette stood speechless, then ran down the portico steps.

“The devil is in her, Jacquelin,” she murmured.

Thus all things conspired on this fateful day to produce the effect which decided Mademoiselle Cormon’s future. The town was already agitated by five extraordinary circumstances accompanying her return: the pouring rain; Penelope at a gallop in a lather; the early hour; parcels half-packed; and the excited old maid. But when Mariette raided the market for the best things; when Jacquelin sought a bed at the principal upholsterer two doors from the church—there was matter for the gravest conjectures. These events were discussed everywhere; they even occupied Mademoiselle Armande herself, with Monsieur de Valois. Within two days Alençon had been agitated as in the critical days of 1814 and 1815. Some good women feared the world was ending. The question resolved into: “What is happening at the Cormons?”

The Abbe de Sponde, adroitly questioned when he left Saint-Leonard’s, replied that he expected the Vicomte de Troisville, a nobleman in the service of Russia during the Emigration, returning to Alençon to settle. From two to five o’clock a labial telegraphy went on; all learned that Mademoiselle Cormon had at last found a husband by letter, and was about to marry the Vicomte de Troisville. Some said “Moreau has sold them a bed.” The bed was six feet wide in that quarter; four feet in Madame Granson’s; reduced to a simple couch at Monsieur du Ronceret’s. The lesser bourgeoisie declared its cost eleven hundred francs. Others thought rumor counted chickens before they were hatched. It was said Mariette’s market raid had raised the price of carp. At the end of the rue Saint-Blaise Penelope had dropped dead. This was doubted at the house of the receiver-general; but at the Prefecture Penelope’s death was authenticated as she turned into the Cormon courtyard, so swiftly had the old maid flown. The harness-maker even called to learn if anything had happened to the carriage. From Saint-Blaise to the Bercail, it was then known that, thanks to Jacquelin’s devotion, Penelope still lived, though suffering.

Along the road to Brittany the Vicomte de Troisville was stated to be a younger son without a penny, the Perche estates belonging to the Marquis de Troisville, who had children; the marriage would be great luck for a poor émigré. The aristocracy approved. But among the bourgeoisie the Vicomte was a Russian general who had fought against France and now returned with a great fortune from Saint-Petersburg; he was a foreigner, one of those allies hated by the liberals; the Abbe de Sponde had slyly negotiated this marriage. All who had a right to call upon Mademoiselle Cormon determined to do so that very evening.

During this commotion, which made Suzanne’s affair almost forgotten, Mademoiselle was not less agitated; new emotions seized her. She looked at her salon, dining-room, and boudoir with cruel apprehensions. A demon sneered at her old-fashioned luxury. The handsome things she had admired since youth she suddenly suspected of age and absurdity. She feared the viscount’s smile of contempt might reflect age upon her; she would gladly have spent half her savings to refit the house if a fairy wand could do it.

“Madame la Vicomtesse de Troisville,” she said to herself; “a noble name! Our property will go to a good family, at any rate.”

She fell prey to an irritation that made every nerve quiver. Her blood, lashed by hope, was in motion; she felt able to converse, if necessary, with Monsieur de Troisville.

It is needless to relate with detail the activity of Josette, Jacquelin, Mariette, Moreau, and his agents. It was like ants about their eggs. All routine care was again gone over, scrubbed and brushed. The china of ceremony saw the light; the damask linen marked “A, B, C” was drawn from depths beneath triple wrappings and pins. Above all, Mademoiselle Cormon's sacrifices included three bottles of Madame Amphoux’s famous liqueurs, dear to gourmets. Thanks to her lieutenants, she was soon ready. Furniture, cookery, provisions, all munitions of war, and reserve forces were prepared. Jacquelin, Mariette, and Josette had orders to appear in full dress. The garden was raked. The old maid would have liked to arrange the nightingales’ finest trills.

At about four o’clock, as the Abbe de Sponde returned home and Mademoiselle considered she had in vain set her table with the best plate and linen, the click-clack of a postilion was heard in the Val-Noble.

“’Tis he!” she said, the whip’s snap echoing in her heart.

A post-chaise with a single gentleman made such sensation descending rue Saint-Blaise into rue du Cours that boys and grown persons followed it and stood about the Cormon gate. Jacquelin, who foresaw his own marriage in his mistress’s, opened the gates. The postilion, a friend, made a fine turn and drew up before the portico. The abbe greeted his guest, whose chaise was emptied with highwayman speed; the chaise was rolled into the coach-house, gates closed, and all signs of Monsieur de Troisville’s arrival vanished. Never did two chemicals blend more rapidly than the hotel Cormon absorbed the Vicomte de Troisville.

Mademoiselle, whose heart beat like a lizard caught, sat heroically on her salon sofa. Josette opened the door; the Vicomte de Troisville, followed by the Abbe de Sponde, presented himself to the spinster.

“Niece, this is Monsieur le Vicomte de Troisville, the grandson of one of my old schoolmates; Monsieur de Troisville, my niece, Mademoiselle Cormon.”

“Ah! that good uncle; how well he does it!” thought Rose-Marie-Victoire.

The Vicomte de Troisville was, to paint him in two words, du Bousquier ennobled. Between them there was the difference separating vulgar from noble style. The viscount’s strength had elegance; his figure preserved magnificent dignity. He had blue eyes, black hair, an olive skin, and looked about forty-six. One might think him a handsome Spaniard preserved in the ice of Russia. His manner, carriage, and attitude denoted a diplomat who had seen Europe. His dress was that of a well-bred traveller. As he seemed fatigued, the abbe offered to show him his room, and was amazed when his niece threw open the boudoir door, transformed into a bedroom.

Mademoiselle Cormon and her uncle left the stranger to attend to his affairs, aided by Jacquelin bringing luggage, and went to walk beside the river until the guest had made his toilet. Although the Abbe de Sponde was even more absent-minded than usual, Mademoiselle was not less preoccupied. They walked in silence. The old maid had never met any man as seductive as this Olympean viscount. She might have said, as the Germans, “This is my ideal!” Instead she felt bound from head to foot and could only think “Here’s my affair!” Then she flew to Mariette to know if the dinner could be delayed without loss.

“Uncle, your Monsieur de Troisville is very amiable,” she said on return.

“Why, niece, he hasn’t as yet said a word.”

“But you can see it in his ways, his manners, his face. Is he a bachelor?”

“I’m sure I don’t know,” replied the abbe, thinking of a discussion on mercy lately begun between him and the Abbe Couturier. “Monsieur de Troisville wrote he wanted to buy a house here. If he was married, he wouldn’t come alone on such an errand,” he added carelessly, not conceiving his niece might think of marriage.

“Is he rich?”

“He is a younger son of the younger branch,” said her uncle. “His grandfather commanded a squadron, but this young man’s father made a bad marriage.”

“Young man!” exclaimed the old maid. “It seems to me, uncle, that he must be at least forty-five.” She wanted to equate their years.

“Yes,” said the abbe; “but to a poor priest of seventy, Rose, a man of forty seems a youth.”

All Alençon now knew Monsieur de Troisville had arrived. The traveller rejoined his hosts and admired the Brillante, garden, and house.

“Monsieur l’abbe,” he said, “my whole ambition is to have a house like this.” The old maid fancied a declaration lurked in that speech and lowered her eyes. “You must enjoy it very much, mademoiselle,” added the viscount.

“How could it be otherwise? It has been in our family since 1574, when one ancestor, steward to the Duc d’Alencon, acquired the land and built the house,” replied Mademoiselle Cormon. “It is built on piles,” she added.

Jacquelin announced dinner. Monsieur de Troisville offered his arm to the happy woman, who endeavored not to lean too heavily; she feared to seem forward.

“Everything is so harmonious here,” said the viscount, as he sat.

“Yes, our trees are full of birds, which give us concerts for nothing; no one ever frightens them; and the nightingales sing at night,” said Mademoiselle Cormon.

“I was speaking of the interior of the house,” remarked the viscount, not troubling to observe Mademoiselle Cormon, and therefore not perceiving her dulness. “Everything is so in keeping,—the tones of color, the furniture, the general character.”

“But it costs a great deal; taxes are enormous,” responded the excellent woman.

“Ah! taxes are high, are they?” said the viscount, preoccupied.

“I don’t know,” replied the abbe. “My niece manages the property of each of us.”

“Taxes are not of much importance to the rich,” said Mademoiselle Cormon, not wishing to be thought miserly. “As for the furniture, I shall leave it as it is, and change nothing,—unless I marry; and then, of course, everything here must suit the husband.”

“You have noble principles, mademoiselle,” said the viscount with a smile. “You will make one happy man.”

“No one ever made to me such a pretty speech,” thought the old maid.

The viscount complimented her on the excellence of her service and the admirable arrangements of the house, remarking he had supposed the provinces behind the age; on the contrary, he found them, as the English say, “very comfortable.”

“What can that word mean?” she thought. “Oh, where is the chevalier to explain it to me? ‘Comfortable’—there seem to be several words in it. Well, courage!” she said. “I can’t be expected to answer a foreign language—But,” she continued aloud, feeling eloquence under the pressure to be agreeable, “we have a very brilliant society here, monsieur. It assembles at my house, and you shall judge of it this evening, for some of my faithful friends have no doubt heard of my return and your arrival. Among them is the Chevalier de Valois, a seigneur of the old court, a man of infinite wit and taste; then there is Monsieur le Marquis d’Esgrignon and Mademoiselle Armande, his sister”—(she bit her tongue)—“a woman remarkable in her way. She resolved to remain unmarried in order to leave all her fortune to her brother and nephew.”

“Ah!” exclaimed the viscount. “Yes, the d’Esgrignons,—I remember them.”

“Alençon is very gay,” continued the old maid. “There’s much amusement: the receiver-general gives balls; the prefect is an amiable man; and Monseigneur the bishop sometimes honors us with a visit—”

“Well, then,” said the viscount with a smile, “I have done wisely to come back, like the hare, to die in my form.”

“Yes,” she said. “I, too, attach myself or I die.”

The viscount smiled.

Conversation continued in generalities. By some mysterious faculty, Mademoiselle Cormon found under the pressure to be agreeable all the phrases and opinions of the Chevalier de Valois. It was like being aimed by the devil. Never was any adversary better aimed. The viscount, well-bred, did not speak of the dinner’s excellence; his silence was praise. As he drank the wines Jacquelin served, he seemed to feel he was with friends and met them with pleasure; for the true connoisseur enjoys, he does not applaud. He inquired the price of land and houses, made Mademoiselle Cormon describe the confluence of the Sarthe and the Brillante; he expressed surprise that the town lay so far from the river, and seemed much interested in the topography.

The silent abbe left his niece to throw the dice of conversation; and she truly felt she pleased Monsieur de Troisville, who smiled and had committed himself more during dinner than her eager suitors had done in ten days. Imagine the little attentions: she watched his every look and served him as if he were a cherished lover's return. She did not depreciate herself; instead she set every sail, assumed the bearing of the queen of Alençon, and boasted of her preserves. She fished for compliments, seeing that she pleased; her eager desire had transformed her almost into a woman.

At dessert she heard sounds denoting the arrival of her usual guests. She called the abbe’s and the viscount’s attention as proof of affection, whereas it was really curiosity. Impatient to show herself, Mademoiselle Cormon told Jacquelin to serve coffee and liqueurs in the salon, where he displayed a magnificent Dresden liqueur-stand seen only twice a year. This was noted by the company ready to gossip.

“The deuce!” muttered du Bousquier. “Madame Amphoux’s liqueurs, only served at the four church festivals!”

“Undoubtedly the marriage was arranged a year ago by letter,” said the chief-justice du Ronceret. “The postmaster tells me his office has received letters postmarked Odessa for more than a year.”

Madame Granson trembled. The Chevalier de Valois, though he had dined with the appetite of four men, turned pale on the left of his face. Feeling he might betray himself, he said hastily,—

“Don’t you think it is very cold today? I am almost frozen.”

“The neighborhood of Russia, perhaps,” said du Bousquier.

The chevalier looked at him as if to say, “Well played!”

Mademoiselle Cormon appeared radiant and triumphant; the company thought her handsome. Since early morning her blood had been whirling and nerves agitated by the presentiment of a crisis. These combined made her so unlike herself. With joy she presented the viscount to the chevalier, the chevalier to the viscount, and all Alençon to Monsieur de Troisville.

By accident the viscount and chevalier, aristocrats by nature, came instantly into unison; they recognized each other as men of the same sphere and conversed before the fireplace. A circle formed and listened in silence. Picture Mademoiselle Cormon pouring coffee for her suitor with her back to the fireplace.

Monsieur de Valois. “Monsieur le vicomte has come, I am told, to settle in Alençon?”

Monsieur de Troisville. “Yes, monsieur, I am looking for a house.” [Mademoiselle Cormon, cup in hand, turns round.] “It must be a large house” [Mademoiselle Cormon offers him the cup] “to lodge my whole family.” [The old maid’s eyes are troubled.]

Monsieur de Valois. “Are you married?”

Monsieur de Troisville. “Yes, for the last sixteen years, to a daughter of the Princess Scherbellof.”

Mademoiselle Cormon fainted; du Bousquier, who saw her stagger, sprang forward and received her in his arms; someone opened the door and allowed him to pass with his burden. The fiery republican, instructed by Josette, found strength to carry the old maid to her bedroom, where he laid her on the bed. Josette, with scissors, cut the terribly tight corset. Du Bousquier flung water on Mademoiselle Cormon’s face and bosom, which, freed from the corset, overflowed like the Loire in flood. The poor woman opened her eyes, saw du Bousquier, and gave a cry of modesty. Du Bousquier retired, leaving six women, headed by Madame Granson, radiant with joy, to care for the invalid.

What had the Chevalier de Valois been doing? Faithful to his system, he had covered the retreat.

“That poor Mademoiselle Cormon,” he said to Monsieur de Troisville, gazing at the assembly whose laughter was repressed by his aristocratic glances, “her blood is horribly out of order; she wouldn’t be bled before going to Prebaudet, and see the result!”

“She came back this morning in the rain,” said the Abbe de Sponde, “and she may have taken cold. It won’t be anything; it is only a little upset she is subject to.”

“She told me yesterday she had not had one for three months, adding that she was afraid it would play her a trick at last,” said the chevalier.

“Ha! so you are married?” thought Jacquelin as he looked at Monsieur de Troisville, who quietly sipped his coffee.

The faithful servant espoused his mistress’s disappointment; he divined it, and promptly carried away Madame Amphoux’s liqueurs, offered to a bachelor and not to the husband of a Russian woman.

All these details were noticed and laughed at. The Abbe de Sponde knew Monsieur de Troisville’s object, but, absent-minded, forgot and did not suppose his niece could be interested. The viscount, preoccupied with his own errand and like many husbands not eager to talk of his wife, had had no occasion to say he was married; he would naturally suppose Mademoiselle Cormon knew.

Du Bousquier reappeared and was questioned. One of the six women came down and announced Mademoiselle Cormon was much better, that the doctor had come. She intended to stay in bed, as bleeding was necessary. The salon was full. The ladies discussed the tragi-comic scene—embellished, extended, embroidered—destined on the morrow to occupy all Alençon.

“That good Monsieur du Bousquier! how well he carried you!” said Josette to her mistress. “He was really pale at the sight of you; he loves you still.”

That speech closed the solemn evening.

Next morning every circumstance of the comedy was known, and—let it be to the town’s shame—they caused inextinguishable laughter. But on that day Mademoiselle Cormon, much benefited by bleeding, would have seemed sublime had scoffers seen her dignity and Christian resignation as she gave her arm to her involuntary deceiver for breakfast. Cruel jesters! why not have seen her say to the viscount,—

“Madame de Troisville will have difficulty in finding a suitable house; do me the favor, monsieur, of accepting the use of mine while you seek yours.”

“But, mademoiselle, I have two sons and two daughters; we should greatly inconvenience you.”

“Pray do not refuse me,” she said earnestly.

“I made you the same offer in the answer I wrote to your letter,” said the abbe; “but you did not receive it.”

“What, uncle! then you knew—”

The poor woman stopped. Josette sighed. Neither the viscount nor the abbe observed anything amiss. After breakfast the Abbe de Sponde carried off his guest, as agreed, to show him houses in Alençon.

Left alone in the salon, Mademoiselle Cormon said to Josette, distressed, “My child, I am now the talk of the whole town.”

“Well, then, mademoiselle, you should marry.”

“But I am not prepared to make a choice.”

“Bah! if I were you, I should take Monsieur du Bousquier.”

“Josette, Monsieur de Valois says he is so republican.”

“They don’t know what they say, your gentlemen: sometimes they declare that he robbed the republic; he couldn’t love it if he did that,” said Josette, departing.

“That girl has an amazing amount of sense,” thought Mademoiselle Cormon, who remained alone, prey to perplexities.

She saw plainly that a prompt marriage was the only way to silence the town. This mortifying checkmate would drive her to extreme action; for persons deficient in mind find difficulty in changing course.

Each of the two old bachelors understood the situation; each resolved that morning to call and in bachelor fashion “press his point.” Monsieur de Valois considered such an occasion demanded a painstaking toilet; he took a bath and groomed with extraordinary care. For the first and last time Cesarine saw him apply a suspicion of rouge. Du Bousquier, coarse and brisk-willed, paid no attention to dress, and arrived first.

Little things decide fortunes. Kellerman’s charge at Marengo, Blucher’s arrival at Waterloo, Louis XIV.’s disdain for Prince Eugene—all these turning points show how small neglects alter fate. A touch of rouge destroyed the Chevalier de Valois’s hopes; he had lived by the Graces and was doomed to fail by them. While he made this last touch, the rough du Bousquier entered the desolate old maid’s salon. This entrance produced a thought favorable to the republican, though in other respects the chevalier had advantages.

“God wills it!” she said, piously, seeing du Bousquier.

“Mademoiselle, you will not, I trust, think my eagerness importunate. I could not trust my stupid René to bring news of your condition, and therefore I have come myself.”

“I am perfectly recovered,” she replied, with emotion. “I thank you, Monsieur du Bousquier,” she added after a pause and in a significant tone, “for the trouble you have taken, and for that which I gave you yesterday—”

She remembered being in his arms, and that again seemed an order from heaven. She had been seen for the first time by a man with her laces cut, her treasures forced from their casket.

“I carried you with such joy that you seemed to me light.”

Mademoiselle Cormon looked at du Bousquier as she had never looked at any man. Encouraged, the purveyor cast upon the old maid a glance that reached her heart.

“I would,” he said, “that that moment had given me the right to keep you forever”—she listened delighted—“as you lay fainting upon that bed, you were enchanting. I have never in my life seen a more beautiful person,—and I have seen many handsome women. Plump ladies are superb to look upon; they have only to show themselves and they triumph.”

“I fear you are making fun of me,” said the old maid, “and that is not kind when all the town will misinterpret what happened yesterday.”

“As true as my name is du Bousquier, mademoiselle, I have never changed in my feelings toward you; and your first refusal has not discouraged me.”

Her eyes lowered. A moment of cruel silence followed, and then Mademoiselle Cormon decided. She raised her eyelids; tears flowed, and she gave du Bousquier a tender glance.

“If that is so, monsieur,” she said trembling, “promise me to live in a Christian manner, and not oppose my religious customs, but to leave me the right to select my confessors, and I will grant you my hand”; she held it out.

Du Bousquier seized the good fat hand and kissed it solemnly.

“But,” she added, “one thing more I must require of you.”

“If it is possible, it is granted,” replied the purveyor.

“Alas!” returned the old maid. “For my sake, I must ask you to take upon yourself a sin which I feel to be enormous,—for to lie is one of the capital sins. But you will confess it, will you not? We will do penance together.” They looked at each other tenderly. “Besides, it may be one of those lies which the Church permits as necessary—”

“Can she be as Suzanne says she is?” thought du Bousquier. “What luck! Well, mademoiselle, what is it?” he asked.

“That you will take upon yourself to—”

“What?”

“To say this marriage has been agreed upon between us for the last six months.”

“Charming woman,” said the purveyor, willing to devote himself, “such sacrifices can be made only for a creature adored these ten years.”

“In spite of my harshness?” she asked.

“Yes, in spite of your harshness.”

“Monsieur du Bousquier, I have misjudged you.”

Again she held out the fat red hand, which he kissed again.

At that moment the door opened; the betrothed pair looked round to see who entered and beheld the delightful, but tardy, Chevalier de Valois.

“Ah!” he said on entering, “I see you are about to be up, fair queen.”

She smiled at the chevalier, feeling a weight upon her heart. Monsieur de Valois, remarkably young and seductive, had the air of a Lauzun reentering the apartments of the Grande Mademoiselle in the Palais-Royal.

“Hey! dear du Bousquier,” said he in a jaunty tone, sure of success, “Monsieur de Troisville and the Abbe de Sponde are examining your house like appraisers.”

“Faith!” said du Bousquier, “if the Vicomte de Troisville wants it, it is his for forty thousand francs. It is useless to me now. If mademoiselle will permit—must soon be known—Mademoiselle, may I tell it?—Yes! Well, then, be the first, my dear Chevalier, to hear of the honor that mademoiselle has done me, the secret I have kept for some months. We shall be married in a few days; the contract is drawn, and we shall sign it to-morrow. You see, therefore, my house in the rue du Cygne is useless to me. I have been secretly seeking a purchaser; and the Abbe de Sponde, who knew that, naturally took Monsieur de Troisville to see the house.”

This falsehood seemed true and the chevalier believed it. That “my dear chevalier” was du Bousquier’s revenge for a thousand little past shafts. In triumph, he ran his hands through his hair and knocked aside his false front.

“I congratulate you both,” said the chevalier with agreeable air; “and I wish that the marriage may end like a fairy tale: They were happy ever after, and had—many—children!” So saying, he took snuff. “But, monsieur,” he added satirically, “you forget—you are wearing a false front.”

Du Bousquier blushed. The false front was hanging half a dozen inches from his skull. Mademoiselle Cormon raised her eyes, saw the skull in its nudity, and lowered them, abashed. Du Bousquier cast upon the chevalier the most venomous look a toad ever darted.

“Dogs of aristocrats who despise me,” he thought, “I’ll crush you some day.”

The chevalier thought himself recovered. But Mademoiselle Cormon did not understand the connection the chevalier intimated between his wish and the false front. Besides, her word was passed and her hand given. Monsieur de Valois saw all was lost. The innocent woman, with the two now silent men before her, wished in duty to amuse them.

“Why not play a game of piquet together?” she said artlessly.

Du Bousquier smiled and went to fetch the piquet table as future master of the house. Whether the chevalier lost his head or wanted to study his disaster, he allowed himself to be led like a lamb to the slaughter. He had received the most violent blow a man might suffer; any noble would have lost his senses for less.

The Abbe de Sponde and Monsieur de Troisville soon returned. Mademoiselle Cormon rose, hurried into the antechamber, and took her uncle aside to tell him her resolution. Learning that the rue du Cygne house suited the viscount, she begged her future husband to tell him her uncle knew it was for sale. She dared not confess the lie to the absent-minded abbe. The lie prospered better than a virtuous action. That evening all Alençon heard the news. The town had had as much to think of as during the fatal years 1814–1815. Some laughed; others admitted the marriage. The middle classes rejoiced, viewing it as a victory. The next day the Chevalier de Valois said cruelly among friends,—

“The Cormons end as they began; there’s only a hand’s breadth between a steward and a purveyor.”

CHAPTER VII. OTHER RESULTS


The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s choice stabbed poor Athanase Granson to the heart; yet he showed no outward sign. He heard of the marriage at the house of the chief-justice, du Ronceret, where his mother was playing boston. Madame Granson thought him pale, but he had been so all day from a rumor already reaching him.

Mademoiselle Cormon was the card on which Athanase had staked his life; a cold presentiment of catastrophe weighed on him. When the imagination magnifies a misfortune beyond endurance, a long-cherished hope balked, faith failing in self and future, the man is lost. Athanase, a product of the Imperial system and its fatalistic schooling, sat still, his eyes fixed on Madame du Ronceret’s cards in a stupor that passed for indifference; his mother was deceived. This apparent coldness explained his refusal to make a sacrifice for the marriage of his liberal opinions—the word “liberal” having recently been revived by the discussion of Alexander and by writers like Madame de Stael and Benjamin Constant.

After that fatal evening the young man wandered the picturesque regions of the Sarthe—the windmills, gay river meadows, well-grouped trees—solitude he loved. Provincials care little for a fine view; they lack poesy. Athanase sat beneath a poplar, vacant-eyed; neighbors said to Madame Granson, “Something is the matter with your son.” She hinted he meditated some great work.

He ceased to have political opinions and no longer took part in politics; he sometimes seemed gay in a satirical way that insulted a world with individual scorn. He interested few; no friend or woman came to dry his tears; they fell into the Sarthe. Had the gorgeous Suzanne crossed his path, many young miseries might have been born; they would surely have loved.

Suzanne did come. Her ambition had been fired by the tale of the Marquis de Montauran’s adventure with Marie de Verneuil at the tavern of the More (see “The Chouans”). The power of beauty and the story fascinated Suzanne; she longed to grow up to play upon men. Months after her hasty departure she passed through her native town with an artist, wanting to see Fougeres and the scene of the Marquis’s tragedy. She fancied arriving in Alencon so well equipped none would recognize her; she hoped to put her mother beyond necessity and to send Athanase, delicately, a sum of money.

A month passed in uncertainties about Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage. Unbelievers denied it; believers affirmed it. The sale of du Bousquier’s house to the Marquis de Troisville seemed to show otherwise, but that too was explained. Choisnel, Mademoiselle Cormon’s notary, heard nothing about a contract; yet on the twentieth day Monsieur Lepressoir, the notary of the liberals, went to her house and the contract was signed.

This was the first of numerous sacrifices Mademoiselle Cormon would make to her husband. Du Bousquier bore deep hatred toward Choisnel, whom he blamed for a prior refusal of Mademoiselle Armande’s hand—an event he thought had influenced Mademoiselle Cormon’s delay. Mademoiselle received anonymous letters and learned to her astonishment that Suzanne was, as to du Bousquier, as truly a virgin as herself; the seducer with the false toupet had never been hero of any such adventure. Mademoiselle Cormon wrote to Suzanne, and Suzanne, who had heard of the proposed marriage, acknowledged her trick, sent a thousand francs to the Maternity Society, and did what harm she could to the old purveyor. The society resolved henceforth to assist only misfortunes that had happened, not those about to.

Despite gossip, the banns were published, and Athanase prepared the deeds. As propriety demanded, the bride retired to Prebaudet, where du Bousquier, with sumptuous bouquets, visited every morning and returned home for dinner.

On a dull rainy June morning, Mademoiselle Cormon and the Sieur du Bousquier were married at noon in the parish church of Alencon, in sight of the whole town. They drove from the mayor’s office to the church in an open caleche secretly sent from Paris—the loss of the old carriole being viewed as a local calamity. People feared the triumph of Parisian luxury, a rise in rents and provisions, and invasion by foreign furniture. Jacquelin charged ten sous to show the new vehicle. A pair of horses bought in Normandie alarmed the Ronceret circle, who argued, however absurdly, that buying horses themselves would prevent outsiders from taking their money. Penelope succumbed to pleurisy six weeks before the marriage.

Madame du Bousquier entered the church with her left foot—a dreadful omen as “Left” was gaining political meaning. The priest opened his book by chance at the De Profundis. No wedding party followed; the pair departed immediately for Prebaudet. Parisian customs, the community said, were about to triumph over time-honored provincial ways. Jacquelin and Josette’s wedding, gay and heartening, alone refuted the sinister prophecies.

Du Bousquier used proceeds of his former residence to restore and modernize the hotel Cormon, decided to remain two seasons at Prebaudet, and took the Abbe de Sponde with them. The town feared “comfort.” One morning the bridegroom drove in from Prebaudet in a fine tilbury with Rene livery at his side; he had deposited his wife’s savings on the Grand-Livre and, by playing for a rise, made himself a personal fortune nearly as large as his wife’s.

These forebodings were superseded by a still more fatal event connected with the marriage.

On the evening of the ceremony, Athanase and his mother sat before a little fagot fire. Madame Granson proposed they go to the du Roncerets’; Athanase, in a voice like childhood, asked his mother to stay a little longer, calling her “my dear mamma.” She consented, preferring to listen to his projects rather than play boston.

He clung to her with desperate tenderness, kissed her eyes and gray hair. Though he tried to deceive her about a fatal resolution, he revealed he wished to leave Alencon, to suffer away from her. She refused to be parted from him; he insisted. She offered money hidden in her petticoat for his outfit.

After a brief outing to du Ronceret’s, where his mother said he had “great independence of heart,” Athanase left for the Sarthe, sat beneath his poplar on two heavy stones, reviewed the glorious future that would no longer come, and, in a resolute, nearly soundless act, glided into the deep pool he had long selected.

Madame Granson returned at half-past nine to find a letter: “My good mother, I have departed; don’t be angry with me.” She thought he had gone off cleverly and went to bed in peace.

The Sarthe, having risen suddenly the previous morning, had brought eels and, with them, the fisherman’s net at the very place Athanase had flung himself. At six o’clock the fisherman drew in his net, and with it the young body. Friends prepared the mother for the remains. The news of the suicide caused great excitement in Alencon: the poor young man of genius had no protector the night before and a thousand voices cried, “I would have helped him.” It is easy and convenient to be charitable in hindsight.

The Chevalier de Valois explained the suicide in a spirit of revenge, revealing Athanase’s sincere love for Mademoiselle Cormon. Madame Granson, guided by the chevalier, recalled many details that confirmed him. The story became touching; many women wept. Madame Granson’s grief was silent, concentrated, and little understood. Mothers mourn in two ways—public mourning for sons admired by society, and the deeper private mourning known only to the one who has loved and shared a child’s innermost life.

Before Madame du Bousquier returned to town, Madame du Ronceret had driven to Prebaudet to fling this corpse upon the roses of the bride’s joy and sober her bridal month. On the road back to Alencon, Madame du Bousquier met Madame Granson at the corner of rue Val-Noble; the mother’s dying look, full of maledictions and reproach, struck the poor woman to the heart.

The evening after the catastrophe Madame Granson, though hostile to the town’s rector, sought him out. After placing her son in his shroud with her own hands, she found the modest priest putting away flax and yarns in an outer room—charity that saved many who could not beg. He took her to his dining-room; she noticed his frugal living.

“Monsieur l’abbé,” she sobbed, “I have come to implore you—”

“I know what brings you,” he replied. “I must trust you and your relation Madame du Bousquier to pacify Monseigneur the Bishop at Seez. Yes, I will pray for your unhappy child; yes, I will say the masses. But avoid scandal; give no opportunity for evil-judging persons. I alone, without other clergy, at night—”

“Yes, as you think best; if only he may lie in consecrated ground,” she said, kissing his hand.

Toward midnight a coffin was clandestinely borne to the parish church by four young men, comrades of Athanase. A few friends, veiled women, and half a dozen intimates attended. The rector, assisted by a discreet choirboy, said the mortuary mass. The body was noiselessly carried to a corner of the cemetery; a black wooden cross without inscription marked its place. Athanase lived and died in shadow. No voice blamed the rector; the bishop was silent. The piety of the mother redeemed the impiety of the son’s last act.

Months later, half beside herself with grief, Madame Granson resolved to see the spot where her son had drowned—perhaps to recover thoughts of him beneath that poplar or to see what his eyes had seen last. As she stood there she saw a woman approach, weeping: it was Suzanne. Told of the catastrophe that morning at the hotel du More, Suzanne had planned to send Athanase several thousand francs under a false pretense had he lived. Seeing Madame Granson, she whispered as she passed, “I loved him!”

Suzanne did not leave Alencon without changing the bride’s orange blossoms to rue; she declared Madame du Bousquier would always be Mademoiselle Cormon, revenging poor Athanase and her dear chevalier with one cutting tongue.

Alencon then witnessed a slower suicide: the Chevalier de Valois died in life for fourteen years. Three months after the du Bousquier marriage, his linen was frayed, his hair unkempt; his elegance collapsed. He ate, snuffed, lost teeth; wrinkles and pallor marked him. The cavalier who had once maintained appearances with meticulous care now showed the power of the soul over the body; with hope deserted, he grew old overnight. His nose, once delicate, smeared with tobacco; his vanity gone, he revealed the pains he had formerly taken. His clever sayings grew rare, but the appetite remained; he languidly took pinches of snuff and alarming dinners. His intercourse with the Princess Goritza lessened.

One day he appeared in Mademoiselle Armande’s salon with his calf of leg on his shin-bone; that bankruptcy of grace struck Alencon with horror. The chevalier, who had waited and watched for Mademoiselle Cormon for ten years and missed his game, frightened society by his decay. A mortified grisette revealed his mornings’ details, and he now passed for a libertine; liberals blamed him for foundlings once attributed to du Bousquier. The faubourg Saint-Germain pitied him and gathered him closer, bringing back a few smiles; but enmity piled on du Bousquier. Eleven persons deserted the Cormon salon for the d’Esgrignons.

The old maid’s marriage defined two parties in Alencon. The salon d’Esgrignon represented upper aristocracy (the returning Troisvilles attached themselves to it); the Cormon salon under du Bousquier represented that class of opinions which, without being truly liberal or resolutely royalist, had given birth to votes like the famous 221. The salon du Ronceret, secretly allied to Cormon, was boldly liberal.

The Abbe de Sponde, after returning from Prebaudet, suffered inwardly and told no word to his niece but to Mademoiselle Armande he confessed he would have preferred the Chevalier de Valois to Monsieur du Bousquier. The abbe mourned the loss of his little grove and lindens—symbols of his home and prayers—cut down for an English garden. Du Bousquier, acting on the Abbe Couterier’s authorization, had committed wrongs to please his wife, but the restorations destroyed the venerable dignity and patriarchal air of the old house. The salon’s bourgeois dignity no longer existed when turned to white and gold with mahogany and blue satin; columns in stucco, glass doors, Greek moldings—a mélange of styles—produced magnificence out of season.

The town gabbled at first, but months later took pride in the luxury; rich manufacturers restored houses, modern furniture arrived, and astral lamps were seen.

The Abbe de Sponde perceived the secret unhappiness this marriage imposed on his niece. The character of noble simplicity that had ruled their lives was lost the first winter, when du Bousquier gave two balls every month; the abbe prayed on his knees while the revels lasted. Political tone in the salon was perverted. The abbe fathomed du Bousquier; he shuddered at his imperious tone and saw his niece lose control of her property—left only the linen and table to manage—while Jacquelin became coachman, Rene groom, and the Parisian chef took over. The triumph of the will is intoxicating to narrow minds; one must have been a dismissed minister to know the bitter pain of losing power.

At first du Bousquier constrained himself out of respect for the abbe and to avoid vexing him, but it was his hand that dealt the blow sending the old priest to his grave. If you view "intolerance" as firmness of principle, and do not condemn the Catholic soul of the Abbe de Sponde, you can understand his sorrow when he saw in his niece’s salon the apostate priest reconciled with the rector—the renunciation of orthodoxy that to him was treachery. Du Bousquier, secretly ambitious to lay down the law to the town, reconciled the minister of Saint-Leonard with the parish rector; his wife thought he had accomplished peace, but the abbe saw treachery. The bishop visited du Bousquier; orthodoxy seemed to expire in the diocese. The abbe died when orthodoxy thus expired in the diocese.

In 1819 the abbe’s property increased Madame du Bousquier’s income to twenty-five thousand francs, not counting Prebaudet or the Val-Noble house. Du Bousquier returned to his wife the capital of her savings, and made her use it to buy lands contiguous to Prebaudet, enlarging the domain considerably. He passed for one of the department’s richest men—a constant candidate of the liberals, missing electoral victories by slim margins, then ostentatiously repudiating liberals by seeking ministerial royalist election. This rancorous republican, mad with ambition, resolved to rival and ruin the royalism and aristocracy of Alencon when they regained power. He strengthened himself with the Church by a deceitful feint of piety: accompanying his wife to mass, giving to convents, aiding congregations, siding with clergy. Secretly supported by liberals, protected by the Church, calling himself a constitutional royalist, he kept beside the aristocracy to ruin it—he succeeded.

Among other benefits he gave money to revive point d’Alencon manufacture, renewed the linen trade, and the town gained a factory. Insulating himself upon popular interests, he risked little—backed by fortune he could wait. He posed as a banker, a partner in enterprises taking security; he promoted insurance companies, public conveyances, petitions for roads and bridges. The government, warned, considered this encroachment, and a struggle began injudiciously; authorities yielded as community interest demanded. Du Bousquier embittered provincial nobility against court nobility and the peerage; he brought constitutional royalists to side with the “Journal des Debats” and M. de Chateaubriand against the throne—an ungrateful opposition based on ignoble interests that was one cause of journalism’s triumph in 1830.

Thus du Bousquier, common to his class, watched royalty’s funeral. After 1830 he became receiver-general, relying on relations with the Duc d’Orleans and Monsieur de Folmon, receiving about eighty thousand francs a year. In the public eye he was respectable, steady, upright, and obliging. Alencon owed him its industrial connection and the sudden proliferation of private carriages. Burghers and landowners, alarmed at first that prices might rise, later found revenues helped by increased style of living. Du Ronceret’s remark, “Du Bousquier is a very strong man,” was adopted by the countryside.

But unhappily for his wife, that saying had a double meaning. The public politician did not resemble the domestic husband. This great citizen, generous to the world, was a despot at home, utterly devoid of conjugal affection. Astute, hypocritical, sly—this Cromwell of the Val-Noble—behaved as he did to aristocrats: velvet glove on iron hand. His wife bore no children; Suzanne’s remark and the chevalier’s insinuations were thus seemed justified. The liberal bourgeoisie and church party blamed Madame du Bousquier, saying she was too old when she married and that pregnancy would be dangerous. Friends praised du Bousquier to his wife, saying she was lucky, he’d make her queen of the department. Misjudged by society, the pious woman found opportunity at home to display virtues. She lived in tears but presented a placid face. She told herself she had loved the Chevalier de Valois but had married du Bousquier; Athanase’s death pursued her like remorse. Her uncle’s death made her life more sorrowful; sorrow spread slow as oil, imbibing into her being.

The Chevalier de Valois contrived to reveal du Bousquier’s baseness to her. The chevalier, expert in love, divined the married man. Du Bousquier’s salon was closed to him and to those who had slighted the Cormon mansion; he was impervious to ridicule, with vast fortune and power. To please her husband, Madame du Bousquier had broken off relations with the d’Esgrignon household, visiting only briefly when her husband left for Paris. Three years after her marriage, at the Abbe de Sponde’s death, Mademoiselle Armande led Madame du Bousquier to Mr. d’Esgrignon’s. Finding the Chevalier de Valois there, he took her hand and in a tender, fatherly manner questioned her happiness. Surrounded by Mademoiselle Armande, Madame de Troisville, and the marquise, they gently contrived to draw from her, in naïve confession, the secrets of her house—her inability to have children and the deceptions of her marriage. The chevalier thus rendered du Bousquier for a time ridiculous and odious; ridicule waned, gossip died, but the incident envenomed du Bousquier’s hatred for the house of Esgrignon.

By way of reprisals, du Bousquier, who had created the journal "Courrier de l’Orne," inserted: “Bonds to the amount of one thousand francs a year will be paid to any person who can prove the existence of one Monsieur de Pombreton before, during, or after the Emigration.” He turned vengeance into public pen.

Although her marriage was essentially negative, Madame du Bousquier saw some advantages: a remarkable man about her was preferable to solitude. Du Bousquier was preferable to a dog or canaries. He showed for his wife a sentiment more real and less selfish than that felt by servants or hopeful heirs. Later she regarded her husband as an instrument of divine wrath for past desires: she had brought sorrow on Madame Granson and hastened her uncle’s death; obedient to religion that bids one kiss the rod, she praised him publicly but wept in prayer.

With feelings bruised and immolated, compelled to make her husband happy and attached to him by habit and a dutiful affection, her life became perpetual contradiction. She had married a man she hated but whom she must care for. Often she walked with angels when du Bousquier ate her preserves with satisfaction. She watched to see his slightest wish satisfied, seeing in his small attentions proofs of his oblivious duty. When he kissed her forehead and called her “a good woman,” tears came to her eyes. Du Bousquier likely made some concessions, enough to earn her respect; Catholic virtue did not require total dissimulation. Often she sat mutely and listened as men she admired ranged against the Church; she shuddered at the ruin she foresaw. A stupid word from her was cut short by his glance.

The worries stupefied her; she found it easier and more dignified to concentrate on inner thoughts and resign herself to a life purely animal—slave-like submission as meritorious. She gave herself to severe religious practices and ceased thinking of Satan’s vanities. She presented to the world a union of Christian virtue; du Bousquier was certainly one of the luckiest men in France.

“She will be a simpleton to her last breath,” said the former collector, who nevertheless dined with her twice a week.

This history would be incomplete without noting the coincidence of the Chevalier de Valois’s death with that of Suzanne’s mother. The chevalier died with the monarchy, in August, 1830. He had joined Charles X.’s cortege at Nonancourt and piously escorted it to Cherbourg with the Troisvilles and others. He had carried fifty thousand francs—the sum of his savings—offering it to a faithful friend of the king for transmission, speaking of his approaching death and declaring the money came originally from the king’s goodness. Whether his zeal moved the abandoned Bourbon is unknown; Cesarine, residuary legatee, received only six hundred francs a year from his estate. The chevalier returned to Alencon, weakened by grief and fatigue, and died on the very day Charles X. arrived on a foreign shore.

Madame du Val-Noble and her protector, fearing liberal vengeance, lingered in the village where Suzanne’s mother died. At the chevalier’s sale, Suzanne bought the famous snuff-box’s portrait of Princess Goritza for a thousand francs; two years later a collector obtained it from Madame du Val-Noble. If the dead could know what happens after them, the chevalier’s head would blush upon its left cheek.

If this history pricks possessors of relics to make codicils and bequeath such souvenirs to loving hands, it will serve the romantic portion of the community. It contains a higher moral: the need for a new species of education, chairs of anthropology—Germany outstripping France here. Modern myths are even less understood than ancient ones; myths press us from every point, serving all theories. If Mademoiselle Cormon had been a reader or student, had there been in the Orne a professor of anthropology, or even if she had read Ariosto, the frightful disasters of her conjugal life might never have occurred. She would probably have known why Angelica prefers Medoro to Orlando—Medoro as the mythic courtier, Orlando the myth of impotent revolutions. We publish this opinion of a pupil of Monsieur Ballanche without assuming responsibility.

No information reached us about the fate of the negroes’ heads in diamonds. You may see Madame du Val-Noble every evening at the Opera; thanks to the chevalier’s education she has almost the air of a well-bred woman.

Madame du Bousquier still lives; is not that as much as to say she still suffers? After sixty—the age women allow confessions—she told Madame du Coudrai she had never been able to endure the idea of dying an old maid.
