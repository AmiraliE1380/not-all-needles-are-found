**A Daughter of Eve**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati. This work is a token of gratitude for the joy your conversation brought to a traveler reminiscing about Paris. Your daughter Eugenie embodies the gifts of womanhood, unlike the Eugenie in these pages, who faced a rigid upbringing. My thoughts often return to our cherished moments in Milan, and I hope this book finds its way to you as a sign of my friendship.

---

**Chapter I: The Two Maries**  
In a luxurious boudoir in Paris, two sisters, Madame du Tillet and Madame Felix de Vandenesse, are in distress. Their father, the Comte de Granville, and their mother, the Comtesse de Granville, raised them under strict religious and social constraints, leading to their marriages without true understanding of love or life. The sisters share a deep bond, having grown up in a repressive environment that stifled their emotions and desires. 

Madame du Tillet, married to a wealthy banker, Ferdinand du Tillet, feels trapped in a loveless marriage, while Madame de Vandenesse enjoys a more affectionate relationship with her husband, Comte Felix de Vandenesse. However, both women are ultimately dissatisfied with their lives, longing for the freedom and passion they have been denied.

Their childhood was marked by strict religious education, leaving them innocent yet naive. They were married off without truly knowing themselves or the world, leading to a sense of loss and yearning for something more fulfilling.

---

**Chapter II: A Confidence Between Sisters**  
Madame de Vandenesse confides in her sister about her unhappiness, revealing the emptiness of her luxurious life. She feels trapped by societal expectations and her husband's political ambitions. Madame du Tillet, despite her own struggles, tries to comfort her sister, but both women feel the weight of their circumstances.

The sisters discuss their past and the constraints placed upon them by their upbringing. They reflect on their innocence and the lack of genuine love in their marriages. Madame du Tillet expresses a desire to help her sister, but feels powerless against the societal norms that bind them.

---

**Chapter III: The History of a Fortunate Woman**  
Comte Felix de Vandenesse, a man of noble character, marries Madame de Vandenesse, hoping to provide her with a loving and supportive home. He is aware of the oppressive upbringing she endured and seeks to nurture her spirit. Their early years of marriage are filled with happiness, but as time passes, Madame de Vandenesse begins to feel a sense of monotony in her life.

She becomes increasingly aware of her desires and the limitations of her marriage. The count, while kind and attentive, fails to recognize the changes in his wife's heart. As she seeks fulfillment, she finds herself drawn to the idea of romantic love, which complicates her feelings for her husband.

---

**Chapter IV: A Celebrated Man**  
Raoul Nathan, a rising literary figure, enters the social scene, capturing the attention of Madame de Vandenesse. Their initial interactions are filled with tension and unspoken attraction. Nathan, aware of his growing feelings for the countess, struggles with his ambitions and the demands of his career.

As he navigates the complexities of his life, Nathan's relationship with Florine, an actress, becomes strained. He is torn between his love for Madame de Vandenesse and his obligations to Florine, who has supported him throughout his struggles.

---

**Chapter V: Florine**  
Florine, a talented actress, has built her career with Nathan's support. Their relationship is complicated, as she relies on him for her success while also feeling possessive of him. Despite their bond, Nathan's ambitions pull him away from her, leading to tension and jealousy.

Florine's life is filled with the chaos of the theatrical world, and she grapples with her feelings for Nathan as he becomes increasingly involved with Madame de Vandenesse. Their relationship is tested as Nathan's ambitions grow, and Florine fears losing him to the countess.

---

**Chapter VI: Romantic Love**  
Madame de Vandenesse and Nathan's relationship deepens, filled with passion and longing. The countess finds herself captivated by Nathan's artistic spirit, while he is drawn to her innocence and beauty. Their love blossoms in secret, but the societal pressures surrounding them loom large.

As they navigate their feelings, both characters experience a mix of joy and anxiety. The countess is torn between her duty to her husband and her desire for Nathan, while he grapples with the implications of their love on his career and reputation.

---

**Chapter VII: Suicide**  
Nathan's struggles with debt and societal expectations lead him to a dark place. Overwhelmed by his circumstances, he contemplates suicide, believing it to be the only escape from his troubles. The countess, unaware of his despair, continues to nurture her feelings for him.

When she learns of his plight, she rushes to save him, demonstrating the depth of her love and commitment. Their reunion is fraught with emotion, as they confront the reality of their situation and the sacrifices they must make for each other.

---

**Chapter VIII: A Lover Saved and Lost**  
The countess's determination to save Nathan leads her to take bold actions, including seeking financial assistance from her family. As she navigates the complexities of her love for Nathan, she faces societal judgment and personal turmoil.

Despite her efforts, Nathan's situation remains precarious, and the pressures of their respective lives threaten to pull them apart. The countess's love for Nathan is tested as she grapples with the consequences of their relationship.

---

**Chapter IX: The Husband’s Triumph**  
In the aftermath of their struggles, the countess and Nathan's relationship faces new challenges. The count, aware of the growing bond between his wife and Nathan, takes steps to protect his family's honor while also seeking to understand the depth of their connection.

As the countess confronts her feelings for Nathan, she must also navigate her responsibilities to her husband and the expectations of society. The tension between love and duty reaches a climax, forcing the characters to make difficult choices that will shape their futures.

---

**Addendum**  
The characters in "A Daughter of Eve" appear in various other works by Balzac, illustrating the interconnectedness of his literary universe. Each character's journey reflects the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations in 19th-century France.
