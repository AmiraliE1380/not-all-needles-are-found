The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, a narrow and often flooded street near the Hotel de Ville, ended at the Rue Martroi, once marked by a turnstile until 1823. Many old Paris streets resembled this gloomy labyrinth, where historical curiosities remained. A notable house at the corner had iron rings on its walls, remnants of chains for public safety, and featured elevated cellars with high, barred windows.

Inside, an old woman tended a fire to cook stew in a clean, sparsely furnished room. Her pale face reflected her life of poverty, while her daughter, Caroline, worked diligently at an embroidery frame. They shared a modest existence, illuminated by a lamp at night, with a few frail plants in the window. Passersby often wondered how Caroline maintained her color in such darkness. The mother, hopeful for her daughter’s future, watched for potential suitors. One day, she noticed a man in a brown coat who passed regularly. Encouraged by her mother, Caroline grew curious about him, marking the beginning of a new dynamic in their lives.

For over two months, the "Black Gentleman" avoided the Rue du Tourniquet, puzzling Madame Crochard. However, one September day, Caroline's vibrant presence caught his eye, leading to a significant exchange of glances that established a silent connection. He began to pass by regularly, and their mutual interest grew through subtle acts of kindness.

As winter progressed, their daily encounters became a source of comfort. Caroline sensed when the stranger was troubled, while he noted her struggles. Despite their growing bond, they remained cautious, avoiding conversation. Madame Crochard, frustrated with her daughter's indecisiveness, showed signs of bitterness, especially as winter brought financial strain. One night, the Gentleman overheard their dire situation and secretly left money for Caroline, who expressed her gratitude through a gesture he pretended not to notice.

In May, Caroline suggested a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman, now identified as Roger, unexpectedly joined them. During the outing, he was captivated by Caroline's beauty and innocence, and their conversation flowed easily. As they explored the picturesque landscape, their connection deepened, and by the time they reached the village of Saint-Leu, Caroline affectionately called him "Monsieur Roger."

Madame Crochard, initially skeptical, eventually joined in their enjoyment, though she remained somewhat oblivious to the budding romance. After a delightful meal, they attended a village ball, where Caroline and Roger danced together, their hands intertwined. As the day ended, Roger expressed a hint of sadness, prompting Caroline to question why happiness should be confined to their outing. In a tender moment, Roger kissed Caroline's hand, solidifying their connection.

The story captures the evolving relationship between Caroline and the Black Gentleman, highlighting themes of love and social struggle. Their silent bond transforms into a deeper connection, suggesting hope for their future despite uncertainties ahead.

The narrative shifts to modern Paris, where Caroline and Roger live in a newly furnished apartment on Rue Taitbout. As September 1816 begins, Caroline prepares for Roger's visit, choosing a beautiful green dress. Upon his arrival, they embrace passionately, but Roger must attend a meeting, disappointing Caroline. He suggests she go to the theater with her mother, promising to join if he can.

Five years later, Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille enjoys a peaceful life with her four-year-old son, Charles. On May 6, 1822, she prepares for a special dinner, reminiscing about joyful moments. A few days later, Caroline rushes to visit her ailing mother, Madame Crochard, who is surrounded by friends and a priest. Unbeknownst to Caroline, her mother’s friends are scheming to benefit from her potential inheritance.

The narrative also introduces Monsieur de Granville, a young barrister navigating his career and personal ambitions. He receives a letter from his father urging him to marry Mademoiselle Angelique Bontems, who stands to inherit a fortune. Granville reflects on his childhood affection for Angelique, feeling conflicted about the marriage, believing he could find greater happiness without it. Captivated by Angelique’s beauty during a church service, Granville yearns for a deeper connection, intertwining the lives of Caroline, Roger, and Granville against societal expectations.

For two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville's engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he observed her character as she prepared her trousseau. Although Angelique rarely discussed religion, she emphasized its importance in raising children, leading Granville to agree to respect her practices on their wedding day. After their marriage, they moved to Paris, where Angelique chose a large ground-floor apartment near a church, reflecting her strict upbringing.

Granville, focused on his role as public prosecutor, left home decoration to Angelique, who filled their space with austere furnishings. He soon found the decor cold and uninviting, contrasting with the vibrant Parisian life he desired. Their early married life appeared harmonious, with Granville immersed in work while Angelique adhered strictly to her religious practices.

However, Granville soon noticed the constraints of Angelique's beliefs, particularly when she declined social invitations. Their first major conflict arose when Granville insisted she attend a ball, leading to a bitter argument about her obligations as his wife. Angelique's refusal to conform to societal expectations created a rift between them, worsened by the influence of a priest who encouraged her rigid interpretation of faith.

Granville's attempts to assert authority were met with resistance, culminating in Angelique seeking guidance from the Pope, which deepened their divide. Granville grew frustrated with the increasing strictness of their household, leading to a stifling atmosphere dominated by Angelique's bigotry. Despite having children, Granville felt isolated, unable to share joy with his family, as Angelique's unwavering commitment to her beliefs left him confronting a life devoid of warmth.

Between 1806 and 1825, the Comte and Comtesse de Granville's household was marked by emotional strife. Madame de Granville, feeling her husband's affection diminish, remained unchanged, praying for his love while Granville distanced himself. Each morning, the housekeeper would inquire about Granville's well-being, only to receive polite refusals from his valet.

At thirty-five, Madame de Granville awaited her husband's return with hope and resentment. Her attempts to engage him often led to tension, as she sought to assert her moral superiority while he grew more detached. When Granville declined a prestigious judicial position, speculation among her friends led to troubling insinuations of infidelity.

Unaware of her husband's true life, Madame de Granville clung to the belief that he was incapable of wrongdoing. However, her health deteriorated under suspicion, worsened by her religious fervor. In 1822, she fell ill during Lent, and despite efforts to be more affectionate, her bitterness resurfaced.

In late May, after a brief recovery, she learned from the Abbe Fontanon that Granville had been living with a mistress for seven years and had fathered two children. This revelation shattered her illusions, prompting her to confront Granville, who coldly asserted that her devotion to religious duties had neglected his emotional needs.

Granville explained that true love requires sacrifice and understanding, which he felt was absent in their marriage. He criticized her for prioritizing her spiritual obligations over their relationship, suggesting that her rigid adherence to virtue had driven him away. Despite her pleas for forgiveness, Granville remained resolute, stating that his love had faded.

In December 1833, a weary Comte de Granville walked along Rue Gaillon and noticed a dim light in an attic window. Young Horace Bianchon expressed concern for Granville's safety. Granville claimed indifference to human emotions, preferring connections with plants and animals. Bianchon revealed that the attic housed Caroline Crochard, a woman devoted to a gambler named Solvet. Initially indifferent, Granville was affected upon hearing Caroline's name but refused to help her.

Upon returning home, Granville found his son, Eugene, waiting for him. Eugene informed him that a young man named Charles Crochard, recently arrested, claimed to be Granville's son. Granville confirmed this and gave Eugene money to assist, wishing to impart wisdom about marriage and the importance of understanding one’s partner.

The narrative concludes with Granville preparing to leave for Italy, reflecting on familial complexities and the burdens of his past, encapsulating themes of indifference, sacrifice, and the struggle between emotional detachment and the desire for connection.
