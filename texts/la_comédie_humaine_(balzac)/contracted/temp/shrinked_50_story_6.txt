The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, a narrow street near the Hotel de Ville, housed Madame Crochard and her daughter Caroline, who lived modestly. Despite their poverty, Madame Crochard hoped for a better future for Caroline, who caught the eye of the "Black Gentleman." For two months, he avoided their street until Caroline's presence drew him in, leading to silent exchanges that provided comfort amid their struggles. Frustrated by Caroline's indecision, Madame Crochard felt bitterness, especially during winter's financial strain. Moved by their situation, the Gentleman secretly left them money, which Caroline appreciated.

In May, Caroline suggested a trip to Montmorency, where the Gentleman, now revealed as Roger, joined them. Their shared hopes deepened their bond, and by the day's end, Caroline affectionately called him "Monsieur Roger." At a village ball, their connection blossomed, culminating in a tender kiss on her hand.

The narrative shifts to modern Paris, where Caroline and Roger live on Rue Taitbout. As September 1816 begins, Caroline prepares for Roger's visit but is disappointed by his meeting. She expresses a desire to help the poor, adopting the name "Caroline de Bellefeuille" with Roger's support. Five years later, she enjoys life with their son, Charles, while rushing to visit her ailing mother, only to find friends scheming for her inheritance. Meanwhile, Monsieur de Granville contemplates marrying Mademoiselle Angelique Bontems, reflecting on childhood feelings. The intertwined lives of Caroline, Roger, and Granville explore themes of love, social struggle, and the pursuit of happiness amidst societal pressures.

For two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville's engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he observed her character as she prepared her trousseau. Although Angelique rarely discussed religion, she emphasized its importance in raising children, and Granville agreed to respect her practices on their wedding day. After moving to Paris, Angelique chose a large ground-floor apartment near a church, reflecting her strict upbringing.

Granville, focused on his role as public prosecutor, left home decoration to Angelique, who filled their space with austere furnishings. He soon found the decor cold and uninviting, contrasting with the vibrant Parisian life he desired. Their early married life seemed harmonious, but Granville grew frustrated as Angelique declined social invitations, leading to conflict when he insisted she attend a ball. Her refusal to conform to societal expectations created a rift, worsened by a priest's influence on her beliefs. Granville's attempts to assert authority were met with resistance, culminating in Angelique seeking guidance from the Pope, deepening their divide.

As Granville observed the changes in Angelique, he recognized the toll her rigidity took on their marriage. Despite having children, he felt isolated and unable to share joy with his family. Between 1806 and 1825, emotional strife marked the Granville household. Madame de Granville, feeling her husband's affection diminish, prayed for his love while he distanced himself. In 1822, she learned from the Abbe Fontanon that Granville had a mistress and two children, shattering her illusions.

Upon confronting Granville, he coldly asserted that her devotion to religious duties had neglected his emotional needs. Despite her pleas for forgiveness, Granville remained resolute, stating that his love had faded. In December 1833, Granville encountered young Horace Bianchon, who expressed concern for his safety. Granville claimed indifference to emotions, preferring connections with plants and animals. He later learned of Caroline Crochard, devoted to a gambler, but refused to help her. Upon returning home, Granville found his son Eugene, who informed him that a young man named Charles Crochard claimed to be Granville's son. Granville confirmed this and gave Eugene money to assist, wishing to impart wisdom about marriage and understanding one’s partner.

The narrative concludes with Granville preparing to leave for Italy, reflecting on familial complexities and the burdens of his past, encapsulating themes of indifference, sacrifice, and the struggle between emotional detachment and the desire for connection.
