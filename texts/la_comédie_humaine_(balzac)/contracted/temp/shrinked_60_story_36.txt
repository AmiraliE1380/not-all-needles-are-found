In a small French town, the Hotel d’Esgrignon housed the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol, a nobleman from an ancient family. Despite the Revolution's upheaval, he remained loyal to his lands, facing the loss of his estate but retaining some properties through his sister, Mlle. d’Esgrignon, and their steward, Maitre Chesnel. 

In 1800, the Marquis married Mlle. de Nouastre, who died in childbirth, leaving their son, Victurnien, as the last hope for the family. The return of emigres brought some hope, but the d’Esgrignons, nicknamed “The Collection of Antiquities,” faced rivalry from men like du Croisier. By 1822, financial struggles and political tensions left them marginalized, caught between their past and a changing world.

The Marquis d’Esgrignon was unaware of his estate's financial issues, believing his son Victurnien would restore their family's status. Surrounded by nostalgic friends like notary Chesnel, the Marquis ignored the political changes threatening their nobility. Chesnel, loyal yet aware of the Revolution's impact, tried to warn him, but his advice was ignored.

Victurnien, spoiled and undisciplined, felt superior due to his looks and lineage but struggled socially. The Chevalier romanticized nobility for him, distorting his view. As he entered society, he quickly became embroiled in scandals and debts, often aided by Chesnel, who tried to mitigate the fallout. Despite his charm, Victurnien's reckless behavior alienated him from the townspeople and clashed with the new social order.

A turning point came when Victurnien went to Paris to seek favor at court. He fell deeply in love with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, blinded by her beauty, but overlooked her financial troubles. To impress her, he indulged in a lavish lifestyle, accumulating debt and relying on loans from Chesnel and du Croisier, who aimed to exploit him.

By late 1823, Victurnien had borrowed 200,000 francs from the Kellers without informing Chesnel or Mlle. Armande. Du Croisier sought to ruin them, confronting Chesnel with a bill for 227,000 francs owed by Victurnien. Chesnel mortgaged his property to save Victurnien, ultimately losing everything except his practice.

Mlle. Armande, enchanted by Victurnien's letters, was heartbroken when Chesnel revealed the truth. She resolved to go to Paris to rescue him. Meanwhile, Victurnien faced mounting debts and considered fleeing with the Duchess but hesitated. When he confided in her, they began planning their escape, leading to emotional turmoil for Victurnien.

Eventually, Victurnien learned of an arrest warrant for forgery. Mlle. Armande helped him hide, while Chesnel vowed to assist, even considering taking the blame. As the trial approached, tensions mounted, reflecting broader societal struggles. The courtroom became a battleground for personal loyalties and societal expectations, culminating in a dramatic climax that would alter their lives and the fate of the d’Esgrignon family.
