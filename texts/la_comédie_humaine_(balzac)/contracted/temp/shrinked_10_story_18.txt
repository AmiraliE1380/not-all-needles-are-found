I have always wanted to tell a simple, true story that would terrify two young lovers and drive them to seek refuge in each other’s hearts, like children clinging together at the sight of a snake. Announcing this intention may spoil the tale and make me seem vain.

I played a part in this almost ordinary tragedy, so if it fails to engage you, the fault is partly mine and partly the mundane nature of reality. Life often lacks interest; the artist’s task is to select from reality those elements with poetic potential.

In 1819 I was traveling from Paris to Moulins and, for want of money, took an outside seat on the coach. An Englishman, who seemed better off, joined me, and we quickly bonded over our love of fresh air and the landscape. Conversation turned to women and love, and we soon admired “the woman of a certain age,” specifically those between thirty-five and forty. A poet would have found our exchanges rich with passion and tender confidences. Our shy fears and slight interruptions showed a youthful charm I no longer possess.

We agreed a birth certificate was meaningless and that some women were younger at forty than girls at twenty. This belief opened a boundless exploration of love. As we described our mistresses—young, charming, devoted—my companion revealed his lady was thirty-eight; I confessed to adoring a woman of forty. This freed us to share romantic follies, each trying to outdo the other in sentiment.

One of us had traveled six hundred miles for a brief meeting; the other had risked danger to meet his lady in secret. We recounted our joys and perils, reliving those moments of delight. My friend’s countess had lighted a cigar for him; mine made chocolate and wrote daily when we were apart. Our countesses were adored by their husbands, whose simplicity provided just enough danger to heighten our pleasure. But soon, our laughter was swept away by the wind.

At Pouilly I observed my new friend closely. He was well-proportioned, with an expressive face, dark hair, blue eyes, and a pallor suggesting recent illness. He had shapely hands, seemed well-informed and clever, and was worthy of a countess. He was a Vicomte with a respectable income; many a girl would have desired him.

About a league out of Pouilly the coach overturned. My companion jumped to escape but miscalculated and was crushed beneath the vehicle. We carried him to a peasant’s cottage, where, in agony, he entrusted me with a sacred task—delivering the news of his death to his mistress. He feared the shock she would feel if she learned of his death from a newspaper. He asked me to retrieve a key he wore around his neck, which I found embedded in his flesh. After giving me directions to his home in La Charite-sur-Loire for his love letters, he grew speechless and died, leaving me with the burden of his last wish.

The coachman said it was partly my friend’s fault, but I was consumed by the task ahead. At La Charite I fulfilled his wishes, but his mother was away, leaving me to comfort an old servant who fainted at the news. I took the sealed letters, burdened by the sorrow of a woman who had lost her last love.

The Countess’s chateau lay eight leagues beyond Moulins, and I had little money for the journey. Nevertheless, youthful enthusiasm propelled me to walk as fast as possible, eager to deliver the bad news before anyone else. As I neared the Chateau de Montpersan, unease about my role as the bearer of such tragic news increased.

When I reached the chateau, the open doors unsettled me. I entered and encountered barking dogs and a servant-maid who directed me to the park where Madame was. I felt lost and unsure how to find her. A little girl overheard my inquiry and, with a playful air, led me through the winding paths of the park, calling for her mother.

I paused behind the last shrub on the avenue, adjusting my shabby clothes so the lady would not mistake me for a local rate collector. Thinking back now amuses me.

I soon spotted Juliette and her husband among the flowers; the lady's pace quickened at her child's ambiguous remark. Surprised by my presence, she regarded me coolly, disappointment visible. I fumbled for the elegant phrases I had prepared.

That hesitation let her husband approach. I awkwardly asked if they were indeed M. le Comte and Mme. la Comtesse de Montpersan, using the questions to size them up. The husband was a typical provincial nobleman: big shoes leaving deeper impressions than his worn coat and trousers, a mixture of self-importance and bad manners, led by his wife while fancying himself master.

The Countess was the opposite: petite, graceful, a delicate figure inspiring desire. Dressed in white muslin with rose-colored accents, she seemed youthful and lively, reminding me of the Comtesse de Lignolles and the Marquise de B---- — types any young man who has read Louvet's romance would recognize.

Choosing diplomacy, I took the Count aside with an air of mystery and requested a private word. He obliged, confident his wife could learn his secrets at will, and left her to wander off.

I told the Count of my traveling companion's death, noting his genuine affection. He expressed concern for his wife and insisted on caution in delivering the news. I explained I could not approach the Countess without first informing him, emphasizing the sacredness of the message entrusted to me. Flattered, he allowed me to proceed.

As we neared the house, Juliette glanced at us with a mix of curiosity and disdain, likely due to my appearance and the unexpected guest. I recalled her beauty in the sunlit alley and sighed.

"Alas, madame, I have made a difficult journey solely for you," I began. Her face paled.

"Is he ill?" she asked, voice dropping.

"Yes, but control yourself. He entrusted me with secrets concerning you; I am a discreet messenger."

"What is wrong with him?" she pressed.

"What if he no longer loves you?" I replied, and she reacted immediately.

"That is impossible!" she exclaimed, a faint smile flickering. Panic followed. "Is he alive?"

Her question hit me; I only nodded. Distress overwhelmed her.

"Give me an answer!" she cried.

"Yes, madame."

"Is it true? Tell me the truth! Any pain would be better than this suspense."

I could only respond with tears; she leaned against a tree, crying out in despair.

"Your husband is coming!" I warned.

"Do I have a husband?" she fled from sight.

The Count called me to dinner; I followed him into a dining room set with a lavish meal, though the Countess was notably absent. The canon of Saint-Denis inquired after her, but the Count shrugged and hastily served dinner.

After a while, a maid reported the Countess missing. Alarmed, I rushed out with the canon and the maid to search the garden and park. As we called, I recounted the tragic accident; the maid's concern for her mistress was evident.

At last we heard a muffled cry from a granary. Inside we found Juliette buried in hay, sobbing. The maid and I helped her to her room; I instructed that she not be disturbed, claiming she had a headache.

Returning to the dining room, I was surprised the Count still ate, seemingly unfazed. His indifference was explained: a serious medical condition had left him ravenous, overshadowing his concern for Juliette.

The evening was somber. I was exhausted; the canon pondered the cause of Juliette's tears while the Count, preoccupied with his meal, accepted the maid's vague explanation. We retired early.

Passing the Countess's room, I asked the servant for news. Juliette called me in, tried to speak but could not. I left, feeling the weight of her agitation.

Later that night I was awakened by the sound of curtain rings. The Countess sat at the foot of my bed, pale and drawn.

"Is it true?" she asked, voice trembling. "I don't know how I can live after this blow, but I want to know everything."

She had lost her youthful glow; her eyes were red and swollen. I briefly recounted the accident, avoiding distressing details. She listened intently, leaning forward to catch every word.

When I related the dying man's fears and the message he had entrusted to me, her tears dried into a deep pallor. I handed her letters from him; she took them mechanically, lamenting that she had burned all his other letters and now had nothing.

"I cut this lock of his hair," she said, revealing a precious token. I gave it to her; her tears fell on my hands, a moment of profound gratitude and connection.

"Ah! You love! May you always be happy," she said, clutching the lock and fleeing with her treasure.

The next morning felt surreal. I searched under my pillow for the letters but found nothing. I spent the day with Juliette, witnessing her noble spirit and selflessness.

That evening the Comte de Montpersan accompanied me to Moulins and asked if I could deliver a sum of money to a contact in Paris. I agreed, taking a rouleau of twenty-five louis d'or, which covered my journey back. On arrival I appreciated the delicate way Juliette had arranged the loan, revealing the depth of her love and thoughtfulness. What a joy to share this adventure with a woman who cherished me, saying, "Oh, my dear, not you! You must not die!"
