DEDICATION

To Louis Boulanger, Painter.

A WOMAN OF THIRTY

I. EARLY MISTAKES

It was a Sunday morning in early April 1813, a day of bright promise when Parisians first saw dry pavements and a cloudless sky. Before noon a luxurious cabriolet drawn by two spirited horses turned from the Rue de Castiglione into the Rue de Rivoli and stopped behind carriages before the newly opened barrier on the Terrasse de Feuillants. The carriage owner looked anxious and out of health; the thin hair on his sallow temples already showed gray. He flung the reins to a servant and lifted a young girl from the carriage—her dainty beauty drawing the eyes of loungers. She stood on the step, yielding graciously as he took her by the waist and set her down without ruffling the trimming of her green rep dress. No lover would have been so careful; the stranger could only be her father, who took her arm familiarly and hurried into the Tuileries Gardens.

The old man noted the curious stares and smiled for a moment, vain of such illusory delights. “They think you are my wife,” he whispered, and walked with slow steps which filled his daughter with despair.

He seemed to play the coquette’s part for her; perhaps he took the greater gratification in the attention. Her dress and trim figure, low-cut bodice and embroidered chemisette, plum-colored shoes, and open-work silk stockings caught many a glance. Her brown tresses framed an almond-shaped face, with liquid bright eyes shaded by long lashes and arched brows. Youth and life glowed in the petulant face and graceful bust.

Yet the young lady seemed indifferent to admiration, fixing anxious eyes on the Tuileries Palace, the goal of her promenade. It was the thirteenth Sunday of 1813. In two days Napoleon would set out on the campaign that led to Lutzen, Bautzen, and the disaster of Leipsic. The Emperor's magnificent review was to be the last of such pageants. The throng came with a sense of sadness and the consciousness that they were witnessing a farewell.

“Do let us go more quickly, father; I can hear the drums,” she urged, half-teasing.

“They are marching into the Tuileries,” he answered.

“Or out of it—everybody is coming away,” she pouted, and he smiled.

They reached the peristyle under the tower where the tricolor still waved, but the sentries forbade entrance through the arcade between the Tuileries Gardens and the Place du Carrousel.

“We were too late, father,” she fretted.

“Very well, Julie—let us go. You dislike a crush.”

“Do let us stay, father. Even here I may catch a glimpse of the Emperor; he might die in the campaign, and then I should never have seen him.”

Her selfish words made the old man shudder. He detected tears in her voice and perhaps saw them in her lowered eyelids. Suddenly Julie cried out; an officer within the barrier, hearing, recognized her and set aside his own order. He drew her and her father toward the Place du Carrousel, securing them a good place. Julie’s joy was evident; she bent her head graciously as the officer bowed and left.

The old man stood a little behind, watching both the spectacle and, furtively, the officer whom neither had yet noticed intimately. The Place du Carrousel was full: a dense living mass of spectators, the regiments of the Old Guard in blue columns, the Triumphal Arch, the bands and lancers—all arranged with military symmetry. Sunlight flashed from bayonets; a breeze ruffled plumes; the scene was framed by the towering walls of the Tuileries and filled with an enthusiasm that seemed almost religious. The soldiers were France’s last hope; many in the crowd felt as if they were taking farewell of the Emperor.

At half-past twelve Napoleon appeared: short, somewhat stout in green uniform, with his cocked hat and the red ribbon of the Legion of Honor. Drums and bands struck up; the crowd cried “Long live the Emperor!” He sat resolute on his horse, his presence setting life into the silent ranks.

Julie’s attention, having been split between Napoleon and the troops, now fixed wholly on a young orderly officer moving among the lines, galloping to and from the Emperor. He rode a splendid black horse; his sky-blue uniform and gold lace set him apart. When the manoeuvres ended he galloped near Julie—Colonel Victor d’Aiglemont, not yet thirty, tall, slender, courageous, the model of an imperial hero. She watched his soldierly splendor with increasing interest and, when his frightened horse reared, cried out in alarm. The officer regained control and rode off at Napoleon’s command. Julie, dizzy with sights, clung to her father’s arm with a convulsive grip that revealed her secret. The old man saw it all and, with foreboding, led her away into the Gardens.

“Why, father, there are still regiments to be passed,” she protested.

“No, child, all the troops are marching out.”

“But I think he told them to advance—”

“I feel ill; I do not care to stay.”

“You talk of death again,” she reproached. He lamented how devoted parents are repaid by children’s indifference, and, seeing her blush, declared he believed she loved the colonel. He warned her that soldiers like Victor, with their gaiety and carelessness, were poor husbands—spendthrifts, selfish, without the delicacy of heart which cherishes a woman’s happiness. Julie, half-amused, half-irritated, promised to say no more about Victor for the present, and the old man, moved, pressed her hand.

Nearly a year later, in early March 1814, a calèche rolled from Amboise to Tours. As the carriage crossed the bridge over the Cise near the Loire, the horses halted: a trace had broken. The break gave the occupants, Julie and Colonel d’Aiglemont, a leisurely view of Touraine—meandering rivers, green meadows, the cliffs of Vouvray with vine-dressers’ caves, and Tours itself rising like a city out of the islands. It was a landscape to haunt the memory.

The postilion’s repairs done, the Colonel stretched and woke the young woman in a sable traveling cap and fur pelisse. The frivolous, lively girl who had skipped to the Tuileries was scarcely recognizable in Julie d’Aiglemont, for she had grown pallid, her face wan, her eyes glittering through violet shadows. She displayed no curiosity for the Loire scenery, and when a young Englishman rode by, the Colonel scowled at the foreigner’s insolence. The stranger—one of the British subjects detained by Napoleon—glanced at Julie and looked away. The Countess scarcely noticed. The carriage reached Tours, and they stopped at the old mansion of the Marquise de Listomère-Landon.

The Marquise, with white hair and an experienced air, welcomed them warmly. Julie’s melancholy impressed her, and while the Colonel hurried off, the aunt detected that her niece’s sadness was not simply widowhood or separation; she suspected secrets of the heart. The Marquise, curious and warm, soon won Julie’s confidence by kindly humor, and the two grew close. Within a month Julie’s singular sadness drew the Marquise into a deep attachment; the young Countess became her companion.

Julie, in solitude, wrote a letter to her friend Louisa revealing her disillusion with marriage. The Marquise found and read it. The letter recalled the days when the two girls had promised to reveal the mysteries of marriage to each other; Julie’s youthful trust had been broken. She confessed that her marriage to Colonel Victor d’Aiglemont had changed her—her joy had faded into a sense of oppression. The dowager, experienced and ironic, guessed the cause: Victor’s barrack-gaiety and ignorance of women. She comforted Julie, offering counsel to correct the divergence between them and avoid mutual hatred.

Julie, comforted, slept under the Marquise’s care. Next morning the light of the Englishman’s frequently passing horse roused comment. The Marquise evoked his story: Arthur Ormand, Lord Grenville, detained in Montpellier years earlier, had cured himself by studying medicine and had become a solitary, proud figure in Tours; he had evidently taken a liking to Julie. The Marquise teased, and Julie, with repugnance rather than triumph, revealed she loved no one, which both puzzled and amused the aunt.

Soon a change: Napoleon fell, the Bourbons returned, and Victor, now warmly received by the Comte d’Artois, obtained a commission in the Life Guards. He urged Julie to join him in Orléans; the Marquise would go to Paris to help them. Julie left under escort. During the journey an Englishman—Lord Grenville—followed them with determined attention; he later intervened at Orléans to secure Julie’s safe passage, earning her thanks. She reached Paris and found Victor, who had been promoted; but the Marquise de Listomère-Landon died of joy and gout when the Duc d’Angoulême returned to Tours—an irreparable loss to Julie, who had lost her protector and guide.

Victor d’Aiglemont, like many superficially competent men, masked incapacity with rank, ceremony, and vanity. He had been taken for a soldier of dash and had become a Marquis and Lieutenant-General under the Restoration, cultivating an air of wisdom though he was essentially shallow. At home he felt his wife superior and yielded to her influence; Julie, forced into the advisory role, felt humiliated and burdened. She protected her husband’s honor and household while bearing inner misery. Her health declined—an inflammatory, nameless complaint whispered about in salons. Doctors ordered rest; she became more of an invalid, going out only in a closed carriage. Yet she had beauty, a famous voice, and occasional society successes.

In 1817 a daughter, Hélène, was born; Julie nursed her and found brief respite in motherhood. For two years the child softened life’s hardships. But by 1819 the marriage grew worse. The husband’s indifference crystallized into separation of minds. Julie’s tact and prudence walled off her sufferings, but the thought of her child’s future made her resolve to live. She feared her husband’s susceptibility to unbridled passion and dreaded the ruinous consequences for their daughter.

Memory often took her back to the review at the Tuileries; Arthur’s face appeared nobler in recollection than before. His shy, silent love had been the single sweetness since marriage. She sometimes vowed to seek happiness at any cost; often she sank into stupor. Conscience and piety saved her again and again. Her instinct to shield herself and her child led to resolve and occasional strategic behavior. She resolved to win back Victor by dazzling society, to captivate him again. But her nature was too noble; she could not long maintain the artifice of coquetry.

She ventured into society at Mme. de Sérizy’s concert, and triumphed—her singing drew the rooms. But Arthur’s presence among the audience unnerved her; a momentary falter at the piano precipitated whisper and speculation. D’Aiglemont, who sarcastically declared his marriage ornamental, admitted he had no ground for jealousy—so he supposed. Arthur, more bluntly, proposed to cure Julie of her ailment through a regimen of travel, money, and medical care; he promised to consult the Marquis and do nothing without consent. The offer scandalized and tempted; in the end Julie accepted.

Montcontour, an old chateau on the Loire with vineyards and rock-hewn cellars, became the scene of convalescence. One August evening in 1821, Julie and Lord Grenville climbed the scented hillside together. She was transformed: health and vitality returned; her eyes sparkled with life. Arthur led her gently, finding smooth paths and pointing out the landscape where years before fate had first set them on this course. They sat in silence before the wide Loire country; gratitude spilled from Julie’s lips. He, having watched over her for a year—moving her for health among Aix and La Rochelle and tending her like a gardener tending a rare flower—was moved to tears by her thanks.

Their close sympathy deepened into love. Julie acknowledged the gift of life and joy he had given. Yet she insisted on honor: she could not be his. At a poignant moment, with D’Aiglemont and the child visible below, she told Arthur that he must leave France; they must part to avoid temptation and preserve virtue. Both yielded to the sacrifice: Arthur agreed; they would part and never meet. They walked by the river in dying light and then separated that evening—he left next morning.

On the journey back, Victor showed renewed ardor; Julie received his attentions with guarded composure. She had won a short-lived reinvigoration of his interest, but her inner resolution held: she would live blamelessly and yet be dead to him in feeling. She asserted rules: she would obey society’s code, keep her duties, and preserve herself and her daughter’s honor. Thus husband and wife began to live more apart, meeting frequently in the world but rarely at home—a kind of genteel separation.

Two years passed. One evening at home with Mme. de Wimphen, the Marquise spoke of the boar-hunt and Victor’s absences. She confided to her friend that she took laudanum to sleep and spent only part of each day awake with her child. Then a letter arrived: Arthur had written; he had been in Paris and had seen Julie; he was dying and wanted to say farewell. He came in person. Lord Grenville, thin and haggard, entered—his emotion stunned the women. He confessed that he had come in desperation; his pistol accidentally fell from his pocket. He had meant to die, perhaps with her, but seeing her he renounced the design and vowed to suffer in silence.

Julie, overcome, took him to the child’s bedside; Hélène awoke and cried “Mamma,” and maternal instincts conquered passion. Arthur offered to take the child with them; she recoiled at the thought of taking the child from her father and the world. The lovers were interrupted: Victor returned unexpectedly. Arthur hid in a dressing-room; his hand was caught in the door and crushed as it closed. The General, unaware, asked for a handkerchief. Julie, cool and composed, explained the noise away. The details of that night remain obscure, but the next day Julie was in bed for days with shock, and the rumor spread about what had happened.

Soon Lord Grenville’s funeral filled Parisian tongues. Ronquerolles asked d’Aiglemont about the strange event. The Marquis casually reported the story: the curtains of Hélène’s cot had caught fire, his wife had been so shocked she would not recover for a year according to the doctor; yet when asked about Lord Grenville’s death, he made the cold jest that Englishmen die in romantic fashions and hinted that the poor Arthur had perished because of the woman—never suspecting, or wishing not to suspect, the larger truth.

Between the Seine and the little river Loing lies a wide flat country,
skirted on one side by the Forest of Fontainebleau and bounded to the
south by Moret, Montereau, and Nemours. It is a dreary region; knolls
appear only rarely, a coppice here and there affords game, and beyond on
every side stretches the endless gray or yellowish horizon of Beauce,
Sologne, and Berri.

In the plain’s center, equidistant from Moret and Montereau, stands the
old chateau of Saint-Lange amid dignified surroundings—magnificent
avenues of elms, great gardens surrounded by a moat, and walls about a
huge manorial pile that represents the profits of the maltôte, the gains
of farmers-general, legalized malversation, and the fortunes of great
houses now brought low by the Civil Code.

An artist or dreamer straying along the rutted roads or over the heavy
land would wonder how this romantic old place came to be set in a
savanna of corn-land, a desert of chalk and sand where gaiety dies and
melancholy is a product of the soil. The voiceless solitude and the
monotonous horizon weigh on the spirits; these negative beauties suit a
sorrow that refuses consolation.

Hither, at the close of 1820, came a young woman, well known in Paris for
her charm, fair face, and wit; to the astonishment of the nearby village
a mile away, this woman of high rank and fortune took up her abode at
Saint-Lange.

From time immemorial the estate had been managed by a steward and lived
in by old servants; farmers and laborers had seen no gentry at the
chateau. The appearance of the lady of the manor caused a sensation.

A group gathered at the little wineshop at the road fork to watch the
carriage pass. It went slowly; the Marquise had come from Paris with her
own horses. Observers had ample opportunity to note a waiting-maid with
a little dreamy-looking girl on her knee, and the child’s mother in the
carriage, looking like a dying woman sent to the country by her doctors.
Village politicians were displeased: any stir would be distasteful to an
invalid.

That evening, drinking in a private room of the wineshop, the village
notables concluded that such depression could mean only one thing: the
Marquise was ruined. Her husband, the Marquis, was said to be away in
Spain with the Duc d’Angoulême; perhaps she had come to retrench after
losses on the Bourse—he was known as a great gambler. Maybe the estate
would be cut up and sold, and the villagers schemed how to secure their
share.

They sought to learn the truth through the servants, but none could
explain why their mistress had come to the old chateau in winter instead
of choosing a cheerful country house.

The mayor called to pay respects but was not admitted; the land-steward
tried and failed. Madame la Marquise kept her room, only moving to the
small adjoining drawing-room where she dined—if that could be called
dining, since she only looked with disgust at the dishes and took the
precise minimum of nourishment. After the meal she returned to the
old-fashioned low chair in the embrasure of the one window that lit her
room.

She saw her little girl for only a few minutes daily during the
dismal dinner, and seemed scarcely able to bear the child’s presence.
Such suppression of a mother’s love could indicate unheard-of anguish.

No servants were permitted near; her own woman was the only one she kept
about her. The chateau must be perfectly quiet, the child must play at
the other end of the house. The slightest sound had grown intolerable;
even the child’s voice jarred upon her.

At first the countryside was deeply interested in these eccentricities,
but time passed, every hypothesis had been advanced, and peasants thought
no more of the invalid lady.

So the Marquise was left to herself. She might live on perfectly silent
amid the silence she had created; there was nothing to draw her from the
tapestried chamber where her grandmother had died and whither she herself
had come to die gently, without witnesses, importunate solicitude, or
insincere demonstrations of egoism that double the agony of death in
cities.

She was twenty-six. At that age, still full of romantic illusions,
the mind often dwells on death when it seems a friend. But youth is coy:
death draws near and retreats, and youth plunges back into living men to
find pain more pitiless than death. This woman refused to live; she
would know the bitterness of reprieves and the moral apprenticeship to
the egoism that takes the bloom from the heart.

This severe teaching is the usual outcome of early sorrows. For the
first time in her life, the Marquise d’Aiglemont was truly suffering. It
is an error to suppose such feeling can be reproduced exactly: once
developed, the power to feel is always there, but the first storm yields
most intensity. Pain’s sting lessens through repeated crises either by
habituation or by a self-preserving inertia.

What kind of suffering counts as anguish? Nature prepares us for the
loss of parents; physical suffering passes or ends in death; the young
mother who loses her firstborn often finds consolation in another
child. Such troubles are wounds and bruises that do not sap life’s
springs. Great pain that becomes anguish must be so deadly that it
includes past, present, and future in its grip, leaving no part of life
whole; never again can one think as before. Anguish engraves itself on
the soul, leaving seeds of disgust for all things. To be measureless it
should strike when body and soul are at full strength, making great
scars. Survivors return to the world with an actor’s countenance, knowing
where to hide or play; life holds no inscrutable dark places for them.

For young women like the Marquise, the first poignant pain usually springs
from love. A young, beautiful woman stakes her life; if that life fails
her, the pangs are cruel, since first love is loveliest. This catastrophe
has no painter or poet because the anguish eludes analysis and defies
colors; it is never confessed. To console such a sufferer one must
divine the past she clings to in bitterness like a remorse; the loss is
an avalanche that laid all waste before settling.

The Marquise suffered from such anguish, unknown to the world because it
condemns it while sentiment cherishes it, and a true woman’s conscience
justifies it. Her tragedy was aggravated by circumstance: the man she
had loved was young and generous; she had refused him in obedience to
social laws, and he had died to save a woman’s honor, as the world
calls it. To whom could she speak? Her tears would offend her husband,
the origin of the tragedy. By all laws she was bound to silence. Such
grief can only weep freely in solitude; she must consume it or be
consumed—die or kill something within herself.

Day after day she sat gazing at the flat horizon—her life to come,
visible at a glance. It presented outwardly the chill desolation that
gnawed at her heart. Misty mornings, pale bright skies, low clouds under
a gray dome matched her soul-sickness. Her youth seemed slowly turned to
stone by an intolerable barren anguish. She suffered through and for
herself; how could it end except in self-absorption? Ugly torturing
thoughts probed her conscience: she felt double—one self that felt and
one that thought; one that suffered and one that would suffer no more.
Her mind traveled back to childhood joys, which she had not known how
to value; scenes crowded up to show the deception of a marriage that
was outwardly respectable but inwardly wretched. What had the delicate
pride of young womanhood won? Love, she felt, had been foregone. Her
grace, smile, and charm remained, but her beauty had become intolerable
as a useless thing. She shrank at the thought of remaining incomplete:
had not the inner self lost the power of receiving impressions with the
zest that gives life its savor? Future impressions would be effaced as
soon as received, and many thoughts that once moved her would move her
no more.

After the creature’s childhood comes the heart’s childhood; this second
infancy was over—her lover had taken it into the grave. Longings
remained; she was young yet, but the completeness of youth had gone, and
so much of life’s value and savor had gone. She would always bear seeds
of sadness and mistrust ready to rob emotion of its fervor. Conscious
that nothing now could give her the happiness she had dreamed, she
believed herself guilty for having missed a union of body and soul,
which would have lessened her pain. Something of the mortification of
an actress cheated of her part mingled with the pain in every fiber of
her heart and brain. Her nature had been thwarted, her vanity wounded,
her woman’s generosity cheated of self-sacrifice. Exhausted by questions
and conflicting ideas, she was powerless to seize a single thought.

Sometimes, as mists fell, she would throw her window open and stand
motionless, breathing damp air unheedingly, her mind seemingly blank,
the ceaseless hum of sorrow leaving her deaf to earth’s harmonies.

One noon, when the sun shone, her maid came without summons.

“This is the fourth time M. le Curé has come to see Mme. la Marquise;
to-day he is so determined about it we did not know what to tell him.”

“He has come for money for the poor, no doubt; take him twenty-five
louis from me.”

The woman returned. “M. le Curé will not take the money, my lady; he
wants to speak to you.”

“Then let him come!” said Mme. d’Aiglemont with an involuntary shrug.

Mme. d’Aiglemont had lost her mother in early childhood and had been
brought up in the relaxed notions of the Revolution; she had adopted her
father’s philosophism and practised little religion. A priest seemed a
civil servant of doubtful utility. In her position, religious teaching
could only poison her wounds, and she meant to put the curate in his
place, perhaps by bestowing a benefit to end his importunity.

At first sight the curé provoked no inclination to change her mind: a
stout little man with a wrinkled, elderly face that awkwardly tried to
smile, a bald square forehead furrowed with lines, scanty white hair,
thick lips, a slightly curved nose, and a chin hidden in a double fold
of wrinkles. Yet his voice was sweet. Looking more closely she saw tears
in his eyes and an impress of sorrow that marked him.

“Madame la Marquise, the rich only come in trouble,” he said. “It is
easy to see that the troubles of a young, beautiful, and wealthy woman
who has lost neither children nor relatives are caused by wounds whose
pangs religion alone can soothe. Your soul is in danger, madame. I do
not speak now of the hereafter; it is my duty to open your eyes to your
future life here on earth. You will pardon an old man for importunity
which has your happiness for its object?”

“There is no more happiness for me, monsieur. I shall soon be, as you
say, in your province; but it will be forever.”

“Nay, madame. You will not die of this pain. If you had been destined
to die of it you would not be here at Saint-Lange. A definite regret is
not so deadly as hope deferred. I have known others pass through more
intolerable anguish and yet live.”

The Marquise looked incredulous.

“Madame, I know a man whose affliction was so sore that your trouble
would seem light compared with his.” Perhaps loneliness had left her
willing to listen; she gave the curé a questioning glance.

He told of an old man who lost wife and children: three sons in the
army who all fell in the battles of the Hundred Days and Waterloo. The
father waited anxiously for tidings, and in a night learned their
fate. Yet he still lived, taking refuge in the sanctuary—he became a
priest, consecrated by his tears.

Silence fell. The Marquise and the curé looked out at the foggy
landscape. “Not a priest in a city, but a simple country curé,” he
added.

“At Saint-Lange,” she said, drying her eyes.

“Yes, madame.”

The words sank into her heart with infinite sorrow. The curé’s deep
voice, charged with plangent vibration, was the voice of one who had
suffered.

“And if I do not die, monsieur, what will become of me?” she asked
almost reverently.

“Have you not a child, madame?”

“Yes,” she said stiffly.

The curé gave her a doctor’s glance. He resolved to do all in his power
to combat the evil spirit that had seized her.

“We must live on with our sorrows—religion alone offers us real
consolation. Will you permit me to come again to speak to you as a man
who can sympathize with every trouble?”

“Yes, monsieur, come back again. Thank you for thinking of me.”

This visit relaxed the strain; the priest’s words had a soothing
influence. For a moment she felt a prisoner’s relief at a neighbor’s
knock on the wall. But soon she sank back into bitterness, thinking a
companion in misfortune could neither lighten her bondage nor her
future.

The curé returned two days later determined to make progress toward
religion. “Have you given thought to the mass of human suffering?
Have you raised your eyes above our earth and seen the immensity of the
universe?” he asked.

“No, monsieur. I cannot rise to such heights. Our social laws lie too
heavily upon me and rend my heart with poignant anguish. Ah! the world!”

“Madame, we must obey both. Law is doctrine, custom the practice of
society.”

“Obey society?” cried the Marquise. “Monsieur, it is the source of our
woes. God laid down no law to make us miserable; mankind has perverted
God’s work. Civilization deals harder measure to us women than nature.
Nature imposes physical suffering; civilization has developed feelings
which you cheat continually. We must give up our lives to you; you give
us a few moments of yours. A man makes a choice, we blindly submit.
Marriage in these days seems to me legalized prostitution. This is the
cause of my wretchedness. Among so many miserable creatures I alone am
bound to be silent; my marriage was my own doing.”

She stopped; bitter tears fell.

“In the depths of my wretchedness I found some sands on which to set
foot and suffer at leisure. A great tempest swept everything away. I
am helpless and alone, too weak to cope with storms.”

“We are never weak while God is with us,” said the priest. “If your
cravings for affection cannot be satisfied here on earth, have you no
duties?”

“Duties continually!” she exclaimed. “But where are the sentiments to
give strength to perform them? Nothing from nothing; this is one of
nature’s laws. Would you make trees break into leaf without sap? It is
the same with human nature; in me the sap is dried up.”

“I speak not of religious sentiments from which resignation is born,
but of motherhood, madame—”

“Stop, monsieur! I will be sincere with you. Alas! in future I can be
sincere with no one; I am condemned to falsehood. The world requires
grimaces. There are two kinds of motherhood. Only half of me has become
a mother; it were better I had not been a mother at all. Helene is not
his child! Oh, do not start. At Saint-Lange there are volcanic depths
whence lurid gleams and shocks shake fragile laws not based on nature.
I have borne a child; I am a mother in the eyes of the law. But you,
monsieur, with your compassionate soul, perhaps understand this cry of
an unhappy woman who has suffered no lying illusions. God will judge me,
but I obeyed His laws by giving way to affections He set in me.

“What is a child but the image of two beings, the fruit of blended
sentiments? Unless owned by every fibre of the body and chord of
tenderness in the heart—unless it recalls the bliss of love—unless it
embodies the poetic dreams of a double life, it is an incomplete
creation.

“My poor little Helene is her father’s child, the offspring of duty and
chance. In me she finds nothing but instinctive affection. Socially I am
above reproach. Have I not sacrificed my life to my child? Her cries go
to my heart; if she fell into water I should save her, but she is not
in my heart.

“Ah! love set me dreaming of a motherhood more complete. In a vanished
dream I held in my arms a child conceived in desire before it was
begotten. For his child I could have acquiesced in any lot. He who
died bearing the burden of the world will forgive this thought. The
world, however, is merciless. My words are blasphemies in its ears. I
would wage war on it and refashion its laws! Has it not turned my
thoughts and feelings into sources of pain? My child is and is not.

“When Helene speaks to me I wish her voice were different; when she
looks at me I wish her eyes were other. She keeps me mindful of what
should have been. I cannot bear her near me. I smile, try to make up
for the affection she is defrauded of. I am wretched, too wretched to
live. And I am supposed to be a pattern wife. I have committed no sins.
I have fought down forbidden love; but if I kept the letter of the law,
have I kept it in my heart? There has never been but one here,” she
said, laying her hand on her breast, “one and no other; and my child
feels it. Certain looks and tones mould a child’s nature; my poor one
feels no thrill when I take her up. There are times I shudder to think
that some day she may be my judge. Heaven grant she may not hate me!
Rather let the tomb open for me—rather let me end my days here at
Saint-Lange! I want to go back to the world where I shall find my other
soul and become wholly a mother. Ah! forgive me, sir, I am mad. Those
words choked me; now they are spoken. You are weeping—”

She heard the child return from a walk. “Helene, my child, come here!”
she called. The words sounded like a cry.

The little girl ran in, laughing with a butterfly she had caught; at
her mother’s tears she grew quiet and received a kiss on her forehead.

“She will be very beautiful one day,” said the priest.

“She is her father’s child,” said the Marquise, kissing the child with
eager warmth as if to pay a debt of affection.

“How hot you are, mamma!”

“There, go away, my angel,” said the Marquise.

The child went; she did not look back, perhaps glad to escape the sad
face. A mother’s love speaks in smiles; the Marquise could not smile. She
flushed under the curé’s eyes. She had hoped to act a mother’s part
before him, but neither could deceive him. When a woman loves sincerely,
a kiss breathes a soul; the formal kiss lacks that unction. The priest
felt the difference and, with a keen glance, said:

“You are right, madame; it would be better for you if you were dead—”

“Ah!” she cried, “then you know all my misery; I see you do, Christian
priest if you can guess my determination to die and sanction it. Yes, I
meant to die, but lacked courage. The spirit was strong, the flesh weak;
when my hand did not tremble the spirit wavered. I will confess my
weakness. At night I would go to the lake; at the brink cowardice shrink
me from self-destruction. Once I took laudanum; I was ill, but did not
die. I thought I had emptied the phial but had taken only half the dose.”

“You are lost, madame,” the curé said gravely with tears. “You will go
back into the world and deceive it. You will seek compensation and then
will come a day of reckoning for your pleasures—”

“Do you think I shall bestow my heart’s treasures on the first impostor
who can play passion’s comedy? That I would pollute my life for a moment
of doubtful pleasure? No; the flame to consume my soul shall be love
and nothing but love. Not every man has the soul that satisfies our
nature. Such a soul is not found twice. A woman is nothing without love;
beauty is nothing without pleasure. Even if happiness were offered again
would the world not frown? I owe my daughter an honored mother. I am
condemned to an iron circle with one shameful escape. The round of
family duties is thankless and irksome. I shall curse life, but my child
shall have a fair semblance of a mother. I will give her treasures of
virtue for treasures of love I defraud her.

“I have not the mother’s desire to live to enjoy a child’s happiness.
What will Helene’s fate be? My own, beyond doubt. How can a mother
ensure that the man she gives her daughter will be the husband of her
heart? You pour scorn on miserable creatures who sell themselves for a
few coins; while another union, horrible for different reasons, is
tolerated—nay encouraged—by society, and a young girl is married to a
man she has scarcely met. She is sold for her lifetime. If you allow no
compensation for her sorrows you might at least respect her; the most
virtuous cannot escape calumny. Open prostitution and shame; secret
prostitution and unhappiness. As for portionless girls, they may die or
go mad without pity. Beauty and virtue are not marketable in the bazaar
where souls and bodies are bought. Why not disinherit daughters? Then
people might choose companions by inclination.”

“Madame, you show that neither family spirit nor religion appeals to
you. Why hesitate between social selfishness and personal selfishness?”

“The family—does such a thing exist? I decline to recognize as family
a knot of individuals bidden by society to divide property and go their
separate ways. A family should be a lasting association passed down by
will and tradition. Our laws have broken up homes and estates; I see
wreck and ruin.”

“Madame, you will only return to God when His hand has been heavy upon
you, and I pray you have time enough to make your peace. Instead of
looking to heaven for comfort you fix your eyes on earth. Philosophism
and self-interest have invaded your heart; like children of the
eighteenth century you are deaf to religion’s voice. The pleasures of
this life bring only misery. You are about to make an exchange of
sorrows.”

She smiled bitterly. “I will falsify your predictions,” she said. “I
shall be faithful to him who died for me.”

“Sorrow is not likely to live long save in souls disciplined by
religion,” he answered and lowered his eyes lest she read his doubts.
Her energy had grieved him; he had seen her underlying self and despaired
of softening a heart seared by affliction. The divine seed could not
take root in such soil, and his gentle voice was drowned by self-pity.
Yet he returned repeatedly with apostolic persistence, hoping to lead so
noble a soul to God—until he discovered she only talked because it was
sweet to speak of him who was no more. He refused to condone passion and
confined conversation to generalities.

Spring came; with it the Marquise found distraction and busied herself
with estate improvements for amusement. By October she left the old
chateau. In the leisure of Saint-Lange she had recovered from the first
violent paroxysms of grief, which had slackened into melancholy. Melancholy
is made of successive oscillations from despair to the border between
pain and pleasure; in youth it is a twilight of dawn.

As she drove through the village in her traveling carriage she met the
curé returning from church. She bowed but with lowered eyes and averted
face. She did not wish to see him again. The village curé had judged
this poor Diana of Ephesus only too well.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Madame Firmiani was giving a ball. M. Charles de Vandenesse, a young man of promise, bearer of one of those historic names still associated with France’s glory, had received letters of introduction to some of the great lady’s friends in Naples and had come to thank the hostess and take his leave.

Vandenesse had acquitted himself creditably on several diplomatic missions; now appointed attache to a plenipotentiary at the Congress of Laybach, he wished to study Italy en route. This ball was a farewell to Paris and its amusements, to the eddying intellectual centre and maelstrom of pleasure. Charles had little to regret, accustomed for three years to salute capitals and turn his back at a diplomatist’s bidding. Women no longer made much impression on him; perhaps he thought real passion would play too large a part in a diplomatist’s life, or that frivolous amusements were too empty for a man of character.

Charles, scarcely thirty, looked at life philosophically, concerned with theories and means and ends while others his age thought of pleasure and sentiment. He forced back the generosity and enthusiasms of youth, trying to convert natural warmth into gracious manners and attractive arts; it is the task of an ambitious man to play a part to gain “a good position.”

He had been dancing and now gave a farewell glance over the rooms, moved partly by the feeling that keeps a theatre-goer in his box for the final tableau. He also wished to carry away a picture of the light and laughter and faces at this Parisian fete to compare with the novel scenes that awaited him in Naples. Thoughts, somewhat poetical though hackneyed, crossed his brain—a desire in the depths of a heart fastidious rather than jaded, vacant rather than seared.

“These are the wealthiest and most fashionable women and the greatest ladies in Paris,” he thought. “These are the great men of the day—artists and men in power—and yet it seems as if there were nothing but petty intrigues and still-born loves, meaningless smiles and causeless scorn, eyes lighted by no inner flame, brain-power running aimlessly to waste. All those faces are here not so much for enjoyment as to escape dulness. None of the emotion is genuine. If you desire simply to skim the surface of life, here is your world. I abhor stale intrigues ending in sub-prefectures and marriages; or if love comes, in stealthy compromises. Not a single eloquent face tells of a soul absorbed by one idea. There is no passion left, because we have no individuality. Italy at least is a land of sharp contrasts—woman there is a dangerous, unreasoning siren...”

Mme. Firmiani interrupted these vague musings. “I want to introduce you to some one who has the greatest wish to make your acquaintance, after all she has heard of you,” she said, taking his arm, and led him to a woman by the hearth.

“Who is she?” Charles asked.

“You have heard her name more than once. She lives in seclusion—a perfect mystery,” Mme. Firmiani said.

“If ever you have been merciful, tell me her name.”

“She is the Marquise d’Aiglemont.”

“I will take lessons from her; she made a peer of France of that eminently ordinary man her husband. Pray tell me, did Lord Grenville die for her sake, do you think, as some say?”

“Possibly. Since that adventure, real or imaginary, she is much changed, poor thing! She has not gone into society since. Four years of constancy—that is something in Paris. If she is here to-night—” Mme. Firmiani broke off, adding with a mysterious expression, “I must say nothing. Go and talk with her.”

Charles stood motionless, leaning against the doorway, absorbed in scrutinizing a woman who had become famous for reasons nobody could exactly say. Mme. d’Aiglemont’s reputation was no more extraordinary than many other great reputations: men always in travail of some great work that never sees the light, politicians living on a newspaper article, men of letters and artists whose performances are never given. “A man with a special subject” might have been invented for such species.

He gazed longer than he intended, vexed with himself for feeling so interested; the lady’s appearance refuted his ballroom generalizations.

The Marquise had reached thirty. She was beautiful despite a fragile form and delicate look. Her charm lay in a still face revealing unfathomed depths. Some haunting thought veiled the full brilliance of eyes that told of a fevered life and deep resignation. So seldom did she raise her eyelids soberly that it seemed the fire in her eyes was reserved for some occult contemplation. Any man of genius and feeling would be strangely attracted by her gentleness and silence. If one sought to explain a constant inward turning from present to past, one would find a proud soul bowed by memory. Everything about her fitted this impression. Like many women with long hair, she was very pale; the fineness of her skin indicated quick sensibility seen in her features—minute delicacy as in a Chinese artist’s figure. Perhaps her neck was rather long, but such necks belong to a graceful type, suggesting affinities between a woman’s head and the magnetic curve of the serpent. An observer of human nature reads character in little movements of head and throat.

Her dress harmonized with the haunting thought that informed her. Her hair was gathered up into a tall coronet of broad plaits, without ornament; she seemed to have bidden farewell to elaborate toilettes. Nor did the small arts of coquetry that spoil many women appear. Her bodice, modest though it was, did not wholly conceal a dainty figure; the gown looked rich from the distinction of its fashion, and the numerous straight folds invested her with dignity. Perhaps she took minute care with hand and foot, but any affectation was absolved by the naturalness of her gestures.

All these little signs combine to make up a woman’s charm or lack of it, but with Mme. d’Aiglemont a dominating personality gave coherence to the details. Her manner matched her beauty and dress. Only certain women at a certain age put language into their attitude. Is it joy or sorrow that teaches a woman of thirty the secret of eloquence of carriage, so that she must always remain an enigma each interprets by his hopes and desires?

The way she leaned both elbows on the arm of her chair, the toying of interlaced fingers, the curve of her throat, the indolent lines of her body—her unstudied pose and utter lassitude—all suggested a woman for whom life had lost interest, who had known love only in dreams, bowed by memories, despairing of the future and of herself, an unoccupied woman who took her emptiness for the nothingness of life.

Charles saw and admired the picture as an artistic success beyond ordinary powers. He knew d’Aiglemont and at once saw a disproportionate marriage, an incompatibility so great it was impossible that the Marquise loved her husband. Yet her life was above reproach, and the mystery was the more interesting. After the first surprise, Vandenesse cast about for a way to approach her, deciding on a piece of clumsy diplomacy to disconcert her.

“Madame,” he said, seating himself near her, “through a fortunate indiscretion I have learned that I have had the good fortune to attract your notice. I owe you thanks because I have never been so honored before. At the same time, you are responsible for one of my faults, for I mean never to be modest again—”

“You will make a mistake, monsieur,” she laughed; “vanity should be left to those who have nothing else to recommend them.”

Their conversation ranged over art, literature, politics, men and things, till insensibly they spoke of love, sentiment, and women.

“We are bond-slaves.”

“You are queens.”

These formulas summed the usual discourse. “Madame,” Charles said under his breath, “you have made me bitterly regret that I am leaving Paris. In Italy I shall not pass hours in intellectual enjoyment such as this has been.”

“Perhaps, monsieur, you will find happiness, and happiness is worth more than all the brilliant things—true and false—that are said every evening in Paris.”

Before Charles left he asked permission to pay a farewell call, and he felt lucky when his phrase was used in all sincerity; that night and the next day he could not put the Marquise from his mind.

At times he wondered why she had singled him out. He supplied an inexhaustible commentary of conjectures—sometimes it meant everything, sometimes nothing. He finally resolved not to yield to inclination and went to call on Mme. d’Aiglemont.

There are thoughts that determine conduct while we do not suspect their existence. For a young man a woman of thirty has irresistible attractions. There is a heart-deep tie between such a woman and a man like Charles. A girl has too many young illusions; the instinct of sex counts for too much in her love for a young man to feel flattered by it. A woman of thirty knows the self-surrender involved; she acts with integrity of sentiment. The first yields; the second makes deliberate choice. That choice flatters—a woman armed with experience seems to give more than herself; while the inexperienced girl, unable to compare, can appreciate nothing at its just worth. A woman is a counselor and guide when we love to be guided; a girl meets us with naive curiosity rather than womanly tenderness. She affords a single triumph; a woman presents resistance upon resistance.

A girl cannot play the mistress’s part unless corrupt; a woman has a thousand ways of preserving power and dignity; she has risked so much for love that she must put him through transformations. A girl’s coquetry is simple; a woman’s is endless, shrouding herself in veil after veil, satisfying every demand of vanity. And there are terrors and hesitations—storm in a woman’s love not found in a young girl’s. At thirty a woman asks her lover to give back the esteem she may have forfeited for his sake; she lives for him and thinks of his future; she bids him succeed; she can obey, entreat, command, humble herself, or rise in pride. With all advantages of position she can be a girl again, assume bashfulness, grow fairer.

Between these feminine types lies the immeasurable difference separating foreseen from unforeseen, strength from weakness. A woman of thirty satisfies every requirement; a young girl must satisfy none. Such ideas in a young man’s mind strengthen the strongest of passions, blending spontaneous feeling with the artificial sentiment created by manners.

The most decisive step in a woman’s life is the one she regards as insignificant. After marriage she is no longer her own mistress; she is queen and bond-slave of the domestic hearth. The sanctity of womanhood is incompatible with social liberty; for a woman, emancipation means corruption. If you give a stranger entry into the sanctuary of home, you put yourself at mercy. Or if she bids him in, is not that an offence? Society in France has chosen a middle course—looking on and laughing when offences come—condoning theft and punishing clumsiness. ‘Tis an appalling punishment to have neighbors point the finger. Women are tenacious of respect; without esteem they cannot exist. The most corrupt seeks to pledge the future to buy absolution for the past and strives to make her lover understand that only irresistible bliss can barter the respect the world will refuse.

Such reflections cross a woman’s mind when she for the first time and alone receives a visit from a young man, especially if he is handsome or clever. And similarly many young men base secret hopes on ideas that justify the instinct that attracts them to a beautiful, witty, unhappy woman like Mme. d’Aiglemont.

She felt troubled when Charles was announced; he was almost confused despite the assurance of his trade. The Marquise took refuge in the friendliness women use as defence—an easy manner that admits no afterthought and paves the way to sentiment. In that ambiguous place where roads to indifference, respect, wonder, and passion meet, a woman may stay as long as she pleases, but only at thirty does she understand the possibilities. Laughter, tenderness, jest are all permitted; she has tact to find responsive chords in a man’s nature, judging the sounds she draws forth. Her silence is as dangerous as her speech. You will never read her at that age; she gives the right to a game of fence and by a glance or a gesture can close the combat, turning from you with your secret in her keeping, free to interest or mock you.

Mme. d’Aiglemont took up this neutral ground, preserving a high womanly dignity. The sorrows she never spoke of hung over her assumed gaiety like a cloud. When Vandenesse left after a surprisingly enjoyable conversation, he felt this conquest would be costly for his aspirations.

“It would mean sentiment from here to yonder,” he thought, “and correspondence enough to wear out a deputy second-clerk on his promotion. And yet if I really cared—”

Luckless phrase that has ruined many. In France the way to love lies through self-love. Charles went back to Mme. d’Aiglemont, imagining she showed pleasure in his conversion. Instead of yielding like a boy to falling in love, he tried to play a double role: act passion and keep cool enough to analyze the flirtation—lover and diplomatist at once; but youth and hot blood and analysis ended one way, over head and ears in love; the Marquise was his match. Each time he left her he scrutinized the progress of the affair.

“To-day she gave me to understand that she has been very unhappy and lonely,” he said to himself after the third visit, “and that but for her little girl she would have longed for death. She was perfectly resigned. Now as I am neither her brother nor her spiritual director, why should she confide in me? She loves me.”

Two days later he blamed modern manners. “Love takes on the hue of every age. In 1822 love is a doctrinaire. Instead of proving love by deeds, we take to argument and rhetoric. Women’s tactics are threefold: declare we cannot love as they do; grow pathetic to appeal to generosity or self-love; and have a craze for virginity. She must have thought me credulous. My good faith is like to be an excellent speculation.”

But suspicion wore out. He asked whether the Marquise was not sincere; could so much suffering be feigned? She lived in seclusion, drinking silently from a cup of sorrow guessed only by a chance exclamation in a controlled voice. From that moment Charles felt keen interest. Though his visits were recognized and tacitly arranged, he still thought her clever rather than sincere. “Decidedly, she is an uncommonly clever woman,” he said to himself.

When he came in she lay in her favorite melancholy attitude and looked at him with a glance like a smile. Her manner meant confidence and sincere friendship, but of love there was no trace. He sat and had nothing to say, troubled by a sensation for which no language exists.

“What is the matter with you?” she asked softly.

“Nothing.... Yes; I am thinking of something of which, as yet, you have not thought at all.”

“What is it?”

“Why—the Congress is over.”

“Well,” she said, “and ought you to have been at the Congress?”

A direct answer would have been the most eloquent declaration; but Charles did not make it. Before her candid friendship his calculations of vanity and diplomatist’s doubts died. She did not suspect—or seemed not to suspect—his love; he admitted he had done nothing to warrant such a belief. For him that evening she was simple and friendly, sincere in sorrow, glad to have a friend, proud to find a nature responsive to her own—nothing more. It had not entered her mind that a woman could yield twice; love lay bleeding still in her heart; she believed not merely in intellect but in soul; for her love was not a simple attraction but a noble binding.

Charles became a young man again, enthralled by a lofty nature. He wished for fuller initiation into the secret history of a life blighted rather by fate than by fault. Mme. d’Aiglemont heard him ask the cause of her overwhelming sorrow; she gave him one searching look like a seal on some solemn compact.

“Ask no more,” she said. “Four years ago, on this very day, the man who loved me, for whom I would have given up everything, even my own self-respect, died, and died to save my name. That love was young and full of illusions when it ended. Before I gave way to passion—I had been drawn into the mistake that ruins many a girl’s life, a marriage with a man whose agreeable manners concealed his emptiness. Marriage plucked my hopes one by one. Now I have forfeited happiness through marriage, as well as the happiness styled criminal, and I have known no happiness. Nothing is left to me. If I could not die, at least I ought to be faithful to my memories.”

No tears came. Her eyes fell and her fingers twisted as usual. The short telling, with that gesture for comment, the might of anguish in a fragile woman, the dark depths masked by a fair face, the tears of four years’ mourning fascinated Vandenesse; he sat diminished before her greatness, seeing not merely physical beauty but a soul great in power to feel. He had found the ideal of his imaginings—the ideal invoked by those who view life as raw material for a passion many seek and die before grasping.

His thoughts seemed poor beside her words and beauty. Powerless to find words lofty enough, he took refuge in platitudes about women’s destiny.

“Madame, we must either forget our pain, or hollow out a tomb for ourselves.”

Reason cuts a poor figure beside sentiment; where one must feel, reasoning is limited. Vandenesse held his peace, sat with eyes on her, then came away, prey to thoughts exalting woman as a painter who suddenly confronts a noble antique.

He was deeply in love—loyal, fervent, and simple as a first passion, which carries ineffable charm. Delicious first passion of youth is often savored by the woman who calls it forth; at thirty a woman can look over love’s course—back to the past, forward to the future—knowing the price to be paid and enjoying bliss with the dread of losing it. Her soul is still fair though youth wanes, and passion strengthens from the dismay at coming days.

“This is love,” he said, “and for my misfortune I love a woman wedded to her memories. It is hard to struggle against a dead rival, never present to make blunders and fall out of favor, nothing left but his better qualities. What is it but treason against the Ideal to attempt to break the charm of memory, to destroy hopes that survive a lost lover who only awakened longings?”

Such reflections, the last efforts of diplomatic reasoning, expired. Thenceforward he knew no afterthoughts; he was the plaything of love and lost himself in the inexplicable happiness nourished by a chance word, a silence, or a vague hope. He tried to love Platonically, breathed the air she breathed, became almost part of her house, and went everywhere with her—slave to a passion compounded of egoism and devotion. Love has its instinct finding the way to the heart as an insect finds its flower; if feeling is sincere, its destiny is not doubtful. Let a woman begin to think her life depends on the sincerity of her lover and is there not sufficient thought to drag her through tortures of dread? It is impossible for a wife or mother to be secure from a young man’s love; she can refuse to see him, but this is too decided a step at an age when marriage has become a prosaic yoke and conjugal affection tepid. Everything is a snare. The seclusion in which Greeks and Orientals kept their women—and an example followed in modern England—is the only safeguard of domestic morality; but under it there is an end to the charm of social intercourse and refinement becomes impossible. Nations must take their choice.

So months went by and Mme. d’Aiglemont discovered her life closely bound to this young man’s, with something like pleasure that she shared his tastes and thoughts. Had she adopted Vandenesse’s ideas or he hers? She did not inquire. Swept into the current of passion, she still told herself with misgiving: “Ah! no. I will be faithful to him who died for me.”

Pascal said the doubt of God implies belief in God; similarly a woman parleying has surrendered. A day came when the Marquise admitted to herself she was loved, and with that admission a time of wavering among conflicting thoughts. Superstitions of experience spoke: should she be happy? Was it possible to find happiness outside social laws? Her cup had been full of bitterness; might happiness be bought too dear? Curiosity remained on the lover’s side. The secret tribunal sat till Vandenesse’s presence put reason to flight.

If such are the transformations through which sentiment passes in a young man and a woman of thirty, there comes a moment when reasonings end and desire absorbs thought. The longer resistance, the mightier love. From this moment every day adds color, clothes the dry bones with living flesh and the charm of youth, until they glow with sentiment’s persuasions and life’s loveliness.

Charles found Mme. d’Aiglemont absorbed, and to his “What is it?” she at first did not reply. The delicious question bore witness to unity of spirit; the Marquise felt that to speak of her sorrow would be to make an advance; she read herself with lucid glance and was silent.

“I am not feeling well,” she said at last.

“Madame,” said Charles, voice tender but unsteady, “soul and body depend on each other. If you were happy, you would be young and fresh. Why refuse to ask of love all that love has taken from you? You think your life is over when it is only beginning. Trust yourself to a friend’s care. It is so sweet to be loved.”

“I am old already,” she said; “there is no reason I should not continue to suffer. ‘One must love,’ do you say? Well, I must not and cannot. Your friendship has put sweetness into my life, but beside you I care for no one. A friend I accept; I should fly from a lover. Besides, would it be generous to exchange a withered heart for a young one; to smile on illusions I cannot share, to cause happiness in which I should either have no belief or tremble to lose? I should perhaps respond with egoism, weigh and deliberate while he felt; my memory would resent the poignancy of his happiness. No, if you love once, that love is never replaced. Indeed, who would have my heart at that price?”

There was a tinge of coquetry in the words, the last effort of discretion. “If he loses courage, well and good, I shall live alone and faithful.” The thought came from deep within her, a slender twig caught in vain by a swimmer swept out by the current.

Vandenesse’s involuntary shudder at her dictum pleaded for him more eloquently than all his assiduity. Nothing moves a woman so much as discovering a gracious delicacy in a man; she learned the strength of his affection from the intensity of his pain.

“Perhaps you are right,” he said coldly. “New love, new vexation.”

Then he changed the subject, spoke of indifferent matters; but visibly moved, he concentrated his gaze as if seeing her for the last time.

“Adieu, madame,” he said with emotion.

“Au revoir,” she answered with subtle coquetry.

He made no reply and went.

When Charles was gone, regrets flocked in and she found fault with herself. Passion advances as soon as a woman persuades herself she has failed in generosity or hurt a noble nature. Vandenesse stayed away for several days. Every evening the Marquise sat expectant in remorseful impatience. She could not write—that would be a declaration—and instinct told her he would come. On the sixth day he was announced and she heard his name with delight that frightened her.

“You have punished me well,” she said.

Vandenesse gazed in astonishment.

“Punished?” he echoed. “And for what?” He understood and meant to be avenged for all his past suffering as soon as she suspected it.

“Why have you not come to see me?” she asked, smiling.

“Then you have seen no visitors?” he parried.

“Yes. M. de Ronquerolles and M. de Marsay and young d’Escrignon came and stayed for nearly two hours, the first two yesterday, the last this morning. And besides, I have had calls, I believe, from Mme. Firmiani and your sister, Mme. de Listomère.”

Here was new torture—a monstrous jealousy, a perpetual desire to be the sole influence. “She has seen visitors, talked with happy creatures while I was unhappy and alone,” he thought, burying annoyance and consigning love to the depths like a coffin in the sea. His brow clouded; Mme. d’Aiglemont, by instinct, shared his sadness without understanding it. She had hurt him unwittingly. He discussed his position with her as if his jealousy were a hypothetical loved case. Then the Marquise understood and was so deeply moved she could not keep back tears—and so these lovers entered the heaven of love.

Heaven and Hell are imaginative conceptions of Joy and Sorrow—poles about which life revolves. Heaven now covers an infinite of human feeling; Hell, our power to suffer tortures so diverse that of pain it is possible to make art, since no two sorrows are alike.

One evening the two lovers sat alone, silently watching a fair transformation of the sky, a cloudless heaven taking pale gold and purple from the last rays. With the fading light, soft stirrings of passion awaken and a mysterious sense of trouble amid calm. Nature’s spectacle bids us enjoy or lament. In such moments speech is fraught; silence leaves eyes with the power of the heavens reflected. A least word has irresistible might.

Vandenesse and Julie—for she had allowed him to call her Julie—sat talking but had drifted far from their original subject; their spoken words grew meaningless, and they delighted in unspoken thoughts. Her hand lay in his; she abandoned it without thinking. They leaned forward to look on a majestic cloud country of snows and glaciers, a picture of sharp contrasts. As they leaned Julie’s hair brushed Vandenesse’s cheek. She shuddered, he more; they had reached one of those crises when quiet sharpens perception until the slightest shock fills the melancholy heart with tears or raises joy to ecstasy. Almost involuntarily Julie pressed his hand. That pressure gave courage to his timidity. The present joy and hopes of the future blended in the bashful trembling kiss he placed upon her cheek. The slighter the concession, the more insinuating it was. For both it was a sincere revelation. Two noble natures had met and blended, drawn by natural laws but held apart by ordinance.

General d’Aiglemont came in at that moment. “The Ministry has gone out,” he said. “Your uncle will be in the new cabinet. So you stand an uncommonly good chance of an embassy, Vandenesse.”

Charles and Julie looked at each other and flushed. That blush was one more tie; there was thought and remorse in both; between lovers guilty of a kiss there is a bond as strong and terrible as between two robbers who have murdered. Something had to be said.

“I do not care to leave Paris now,” Charles said.

“We know why,” the General replied with the knowing air of a man who discovers a secret. “You do not like to leave your uncle, because you do not wish to lose your chance of succeeding to the title.”

The Marquise took refuge in her room and passed a pitiless verdict on her husband: “His stupidity is really beyond anything!”

Between the Barrière d’Italie and the Barrière de la Santé, along the
boulevard leading to the Jardin des Plantes, you have a view of Paris
that could send even the most blasé artist or tourist into ecstasies.
Reach the slightly higher ground where the line of boulevard, shaded by
tall, spreading trees, curves with the grace of a green, silent forest
avenue, and you see spread at your feet a deep valley populous with
factories that look almost countrified among green trees and the brown
streams of the Bièvre or the Gobelins.

On the opposite slope, beneath thousands of roofs packed like heads
in a crowd, lurks the squalor of the Faubourg Saint-Marceau. The
imposing cupola of the Panthéon and the grim dome of the Val-de-Grâce
tower proudly above a whole town built amphitheatre-wise; every tier
is grotesquely represented by a crooked line of street, so the two
monuments look like giants dwarfing the poor little houses and the
tallest poplars in the valley. To your left a daylighted observatory,
light pouring athwart its windows and galleries, produces fantastical
effects that make the building seem a black spectral skeleton. Further
yet rises the elegant lantern tower of the Invalides, soaring between the
bluish pile of the Luxembourg and the gray towers of Saint-Sulpice. From
this standpoint architecture blends with green leaves and gray shadows,
changing with every aspect of the heavens, every alteration of light.
Afar, the skyey spaces themselves seem full of buildings; near, serpentine
curves of waving trees and green footpaths wind.

Away to your right, through a great gap in this singular landscape,
you see the canal Saint-Martin, a long pale stripe edged with reddish
stone quays and fringed by a lime avenue. The long rows of buildings
beside it, in genuine Roman style, are the public granaries.

Beyond, on the last plane, rise the smoke-dimmed slopes of Belleville
covered with houses and windmills, their outlines blending with cloud.
And still, between that vague horizon and the serried range of roofs in
the valley, a whole city lies out of sight: a huge city engulfed, as it
were, in a hollow between the pinnacles of the Hôpital de la Pitié and the
ridge line of the Cimetière de l’Est, between suffering on one hand and
death on the other; a city sending up a smothered roar like ocean grumbling
at a cliff, as if to say, “I am here!”

When sunlight pours like a flood over this strip of Paris, purifying and
etherealizing outlines, kindling lights in window panes, brightening red
tiles, flaming about golden crosses, whitening walls and transforming the
atmosphere into a gauzy veil, calling up rich contrasts of light and
fantastic shadow; when the sky is blue and earth quivers in the heat and
bells peal, then you shall see one of the eloquent fairy scenes that
stamp themselves on the imagination—scenes that find as fanatical worshipers
as the views of Naples and Byzantium or the isles of Florida. Nothing is
lacking to complete the harmony: the murmur of the world of men and the
idyllic quiet of solitude, the voices of a million human creatures and
the voice of God. There lies a whole capital beneath the peaceful cypresses
of Père-Lachaise.

The landscape lay in all its beauty, sparkling in spring sunlight, as I
stood looking out one morning, my back against a huge elm that flung its
yellow flowers to the wind. At the sight of the rich view before me I
thought bitterly of the scorn with which even in our literature we affect
to hold this land, and cursed the pitiable plutocrats who fall out of
love with fair France, spending gold to acquire the right to sneer at
their country by galloping through Italy and inspecting that desecrated
land through opera-glasses. I cast loving eyes on modern Paris. I was
beginning to dream when a kiss disturbed the solitude and put philosophy
to flight. Down the sidewalk, along the steep bank above the rippling
water, I saw beyond the Pont des Gobelins the figure of a woman, dressed
with the daintiest simplicity; she seemed still young, and the gladness
of the landscape was reflected in her sweet face. Her companion, a
handsome young man, had just set down a little boy. A prettier child
has never been seen, and to this day I do not know whether it was the
child or his mother who received the kiss. In their young faces, eyes,
smiles, every movement, you could read the same deep tender thought.
Their arms interlaced with such swift gladness; they drew close with such
marvelous unanimity of impulse that, conscious only of themselves, they
did not even see me. A second child, however—a little girl who had
turned her back in sullen discontent—threw me a glance, and the
expression in her eyes startled me. She was as pretty and engaging as
the little brother she left to run about by himself; but her charm was
less childish, and as she stood mute and motionless, her attitude and
demeanor suggested a torpid snake. There was something indescribably
mechanical in the way the pretty woman and her companion paced up and
down. In absence of mind, probably, they were content to walk between the
little bridge and a carriage waiting nearby, turning, stopping, looking
into each other’s eyes, or breaking into laughter as their casual talk
grew lively or languid, grave or gay.

I watched this delicious picture awhile from my hiding-place by the
elm, and would have respected their privacy had I not made a chance
discovery. In the brooding, silent elder child I saw traces of thought
overdeep for her age. When her mother and the young man turned and came
near, her head was often lowered; the furtive sidelong glances she gave
them and the child were extraordinary. Sometimes the pretty woman or her
friend would stroke the little boy’s fair curls, or lay a caressing
finger against his throat or white collar as he played keeping step;
and no words can describe the shrewd subtlety, ingenuous malice, fierce
intensity that lit that pallid little face with faint circles round the
eyes. Truly there was a man’s power of passion in the strange-looking,
delicate little girl. Here were traces of suffering or of thought; which
is the more certain token of death when life is in blossom—physical
suffering, or the malady of too-early thought preying on a soul in bud?
Perhaps a mother knows. For my part, I know nothing more dreadful than
an old man’s thoughts on a child’s forehead; even blasphemy from girlish
lips is less monstrous.

The almost stupid stolidity of this child who had begun to think,
her rare gestures, everything about her, interested me. I scrutinized
her. Then the common whim of the observer drew me to compare her with
her brother, and to note their likeness and unlikeness.

Her brown hair and dark eyes and look of precocious power contrasted with
the little one’s fair curls and sea-green eyes and winning helplessness.
She perhaps was seven or eight; the boy was about four. Both were dressed
alike; but a slight difference revealed much: the little brown-haired maid
wore a linen collar with a plain hem; her brother’s was edged with dainty
embroidery—yet another tacit preference a child can read in a mother’s
soul. The fair-haired child, careless and glad, looked almost like a girl;
his skin so fair and fresh, his movements so graceful, his look so sweet.
The older sister, despite energy and beauty, looked like a sickly little
boy. In her bright eyes there was none of the humid softness that lends
children’s faces charm; they seemed, like courtiers’ eyes, dried by an
inner fire; and in her pallor there was a swarthy olive tint, the sign
of vigorous character. Twice her brother came to her, holding out a tiny
hunting-horn with a touching, winning look—“Here, Hélène, will you
take it?” so persuasively spoken—but she only scowled. The little girl,
so sombre and vehement beneath apparent indifference, shuddered and even
flushed red when he came near; but he seemed not to notice her dark mood,
and his unconscious earnestness marked a final character difference:
the child’s brow was overclouded already by the gloom of a man’s
knowledge and cares.

“Mamma, Hélène will not play,” cried the little one, seizing an
opportunity to complain while the two stood silent on the Pont des
Gobelins.

“Let her alone, Charles; you know very well she is always cross.”

Tears sprang to Hélène’s eyes at the words so thoughtlessly uttered by
her mother as she turned abruptly to the young man beside her. The child
devoured the speech in silence, but she gave her brother one of those
sagacious looks that seemed inexplicable to me, glancing with a sinister
expression from the bank to the Bièvre, then at the bridge and the view,
and then at me.

I was afraid my presence might disturb the happy couple; I slipped away
and took refuge behind a thicket of elder that completely screened me.
Sitting quietly on the summit of the bank, I watched the landscape and the
fierce-looking little girl; with my head almost level with the boulevard I
could still see her through the leaves. Hélène seemed uneasy over my
disappearance; her dark eyes searched the alley and behind the trees with
indefinable curiosity. What was I to her? Then Charles’ baby laughter rang
like a bird. The tall young man, with the same fair hair, danced him in
his arms, showering kisses upon him, and the meaningless baby words of
the little language that rises when we play with children rose to our
ears. The mother looked on smiling, now and then putting in some low word
from the heart, for her companion would stop in full happiness, the
blue eyes turned toward her full of light, love, and worship. Their
voices, blending with the child’s, reached me like a caress. The three
figures, charming in themselves, composed a lovely scene in a glorious
landscape, filling it with a pervasive grace: a delicately fair woman,
radiant with smiles; a child of love; a young man with irresistible charm;
a cloudless sky—nothing in nature lacked to complete a perfect harmony.
I found myself smiling as if their happiness had been my own.

The clocks struck nine. The young man gave a tender embrace, and went
toward the tilbury which an old servant drove slowly to meet him. The
lady had grown grave and almost sad. The child’s prattle sounded through
the last farewell kisses. Then the tilbury rolled away, and the lady
stood motionless, listening to the wheels, watching the little cloud of
dust raised by its passage. Charles ran down the green path back to the
bridge to join his sister. I heard his silver voice calling to her.

“Why did you not come to say good-bye to my good friend?” he cried.

Hélène looked up. Never surely did such hatred gleam from a child’s
eyes as hers when she turned them on the brother beside her. She gave him
an angry push. Charles lost his footing on the steep slope, stumbled over
a tree root, and fell headlong, dashing his forehead on the sharp-edged
stones, covered with blood, and disappeared over the edge into the muddy
river. The turbid water closed over a fair, bright head with a shower of
splashes; one sharp shriek after another rang in my ears; then the sounds
were stifled by the thick stream, and the poor child sank with a dull
sound as if a stone had been thrown in. The accident happened with
more than lightning swiftness. I sprang down the footpath, and Hélène,
stupefied with horror, shrieked again and again:

“Mamma! mamma!”

The mother was at my side. She had flown to the spot like a bird. But
neither a mother’s eyes nor mine could find where the little one had
gone under. There was a wide space of black hurrying water, and below a
bed of ten feet of mud. There was not the smallest possibility of saving
the child. No one was stirring at that hour on a Sunday morning; there
are neither barges nor anglers on the Bièvre. There was not a creature in
sight, not a pole to plumb the filthy stream. What need to explain how
the ugly accident had happened—accident or misfortune, whichever it
might be? Had Hélène avenged her father? Her jealousy surely was the
sword of God. And yet when I looked at the mother I shivered. What fearful
ordeal awaited her when she should return to her husband, the judge before
whom she must stand all her days? And here with her was an inseparable,
incorruptible witness. A child’s forehead is transparent, a child’s face
hides no thoughts, and a lie, like a red flame set within, glows red and
colors the eyes. But the unhappy woman had not thought yet of the
punishment awaiting her at home; she stared into the Bièvre.

Such an event must inevitably send ghastly echoes through a woman’s life,
and here is one of the most terrible reverberations that troubled Julie’s
love from time to time.

Several years had gone by. The Marquis de Vandenesse wore mourning for
his father and succeeded to his estates. One evening, after dinner, a
notary was present in his house. This was no pettifogging lawyer after
Sterne’s pattern, but a solid notary of Paris, one of those estimable men
who do a stupid thing pompously, stamp on your private corn, and then
ask what there is to cry about. If they know the enormity, “Upon my
word,” they cry, “I hadn’t a notion!” He was a well-intentioned ass who
saw nothing in life but deeds and documents.

Mme. d’Aiglemont had dined with M. de Vandenesse; her husband had
excused himself before dinner was over, for he was taking his two
children to the play—some Boulevard theatre, the Ambigu-Comique or the
Gaieté, melodrama judged harmless and suitable pabulum for childhood
because innocence triumphs in the fifth act. The boy and girl had
teased their father to be there before the curtain rose, so he had left
before dessert.

But the notary, imperturbable and incapable of asking why Mme. d’Aiglemont
should have allowed husband and children to go without her, sat on as if
screwed to his chair. Dinner was over, dessert prolonged by discussion,
and coffee delayed. All these things consumed time, and drew impatient
movements from that charming woman; she looked like a thoroughbred pawing
the ground before a race; but the man of law, to whom horses and women
were equally unknown quantities, simply thought the Marquise a lively
personage. So delighted to be in the company of a woman of fashion and a
political celebrity, he exerted himself to shine in conversation, taking
the lady’s forced smile for approbation, and talked on with unflagging
spirit till the Marquise was almost out of patience.

The master of the house, in concert with the lady, had more than once
maintained eloquent silence when the lawyer expected a civil reply;
these pauses were employed by the talkative nuisance in looking for
anecdotes in the fire. M. de Vandenesse had recourse to his watch; the
charming Marquise tried the experiment of fastening her bonnet strings,
and made as if to go. But she did not go, and the notary, blind and deaf,
delighted with himself, was convinced his conversational powers could
keep her.

“I shall certainly have that woman for a client,” he said to himself.

Meanwhile the Marquise stood, putting on her gloves, twisting her
fingers, looking from the impatient Marquis to the lawyer, still
pounding away. At every pause the pair heaved a sigh of relief, their
looks saying plainly, “At last! He is really going!”

Nothing of the kind. It was a nightmare that could only exasperate the
two passionate creatures, on whom the lawyer had some of the fascinating
effect of a snake on a pair of birds; before long they would be driven to
cut him short.

The notary was giving them the history of the discreditable ways in
which one du Tillet (a stockbroker then much in favor) had built his
fortune; all the ins and outs of the disgraceful business were accurately
put before them; and at the very middle of his tale M. de Vandenesse heard
the clock strike nine. Then it became clear to him his legal adviser was
an idiot who must be sent away. He stopped him with a gesture.

“The tongs, my lord Marquis?” queried the notary, handing the object.

“No, monsieur, I am compelled to send you away. Mme. d’Aiglemont wishes
to join her children, and I shall have the honor of escorting her.”

“Nine o’clock already! Time goes like a shadow in pleasant company,”
said the man of law, who had talked on end for the past hour.

He looked for his hat, planted himself before the fire with a suppressed
hiccough; and, without heeding the Marquise’s withering glances, spoke
once more to his impatient client:

“To sum up, my lord Marquis. Business before all things. To-morrow
we must subpoena your brother; we will proceed to make out the inventory,
and faith, after that----”

So ill had the lawyer understood directions that his impression was
the opposite of what was intended. It was a delicate matter, and
Vandenesse, in spite of himself, began to put the thick-headed notary
right. The discussion that followed took up time.

“Listen,” the diplomatist said at a sign from the lady, “You are
puzzling me; come back to-morrow, and if the writ is not issued by noon
to-morrow, the days of grace will expire, and then—”

As he spoke, a carriage entered the courtyard. The poor woman turned
sharply away at the sound to hide the tears in her eyes. The Marquis
rang to tell the servant his master was not at home; but before the
footman could answer the bell, the lady’s husband returned unexpectedly
from the Gaieté, holding both children by the hand. The little girl’s
eyes were red; the boy was fretful and cross.

“What can have happened?” asked the Marquise.

“I will tell you by and by,” said the General, and catching sight
through an open door of newspapers on the table in the adjoining
sitting-room, he went off. The Marquise, at the end of her patience,
flung herself on the sofa in desperation. The notary, thinking it
incumbent to be amiable with the children, spoke to the little boy in an
insinuating tone:

“Well, my little man, and what is there on at the theatre?”

“The Valley of the Torrent,” said Gustave sulkily.

“Upon my word,” declared the notary, “authors nowadays are half crazy.
The Valley of the Torrent! Why not the Torrent of the Valley? It is
conceivable a valley might be without a torrent; now if they had said
the Torrent of the Valley, that would have been something clear,
something precise and comprehensible. But never mind that. Now, how is
the drama to take place in a torrent and in a valley? You will tell me
that in these days the principal attraction lies in the scenic effect,
and the title is a capital advertisement.—And did you enjoy it, my
little friend?” he continued, sitting down before the child.

When the notary pursued his inquiries about a drama in the bed of a
torrent, the little girl turned slowly away and began to cry. Her mother
did not notice this in her intense annoyance.

“Oh! yes, monsieur, I enjoyed it very much,” said the child. “There is
a dear little boy in the play, and he was all alone in the world, because
his papa could not have been his real papa. And when he came to the top
of the bridge over the torrent, a big, naughty man with a beard, dressed
all in black, came and threw him into the water. And then Hélène began
to sob and cry, and everybody scolded us, and father brought us away
quick, quick—”

M. de Vandenesse and the Marquise looked on in dull amazement, as if
all power to think or move had been paralyzed.

“Do be quiet, Gustave!” cried the General. “I told you that you were not
to talk about anything that happened at the play, and you have forgotten
already.”

“Oh, my lord Marquis, your lordship must excuse him,” cried the notary.
“I ought not to have asked questions, but I had no idea—”

“He ought not to have answered them,” said the General, looking sternly
at the child.

It seemed the Marquise and the master both perfectly understood why the
children had come back so suddenly. Mme. d’Aiglemont looked at her
daughter and rose as if to go to her, but a terrible convulsion passed
over her face, and all that could be read in it was relentless severity.

“That will do, Hélène,” she said. “Go into the other room, and stop
crying.”

“What can she have done, poor child!” asked the notary, thinking to
appease the mother and stop Hélène’s tears in one stroke. “So pretty as
she is, she must be as good as can be; never anything but a joy to her
mother, I will be bound. Isn’t that so, my little girl?”

Hélène cowered, looked at her mother, dried her eyes, struggled for
composure, and took refuge in the next room.

“And you, madame, are too good a mother not to love all your children
alike. You are too good a woman, besides, to have any of those
lamentable preferences which have such fatal effects, as we lawyers have
only too much reason to know. Society goes through our hands; we see its
passions in that most revolting form, greed. Here it is the mother of a
family trying to disinherit her husband’s children to enrich the others
whom she loves better; or it is the husband who tries to leave all his
property to the child who has done his best to earn his mother’s hatred.
And then begin quarrels, fears, deeds, defeasances, sham sales, trusts,
and all the rest; a pretty mess, in fact, it is pitiable, upon my honor,
pitiable! There are fathers that will spend their whole lives cheating
their children and robbing their wives. Yes, robbing is the only word.
We were talking of tragedy; oh! I can assure you that if we were at
liberty to tell the real reasons of some donations I know, modern
dramatists would have material for sensational bourgeois dramas. How
the wife manages to get her way, as she invariably does, I cannot think;
for in spite of appearances and of their weakness, it is always the women
who carry the day. Ah! by the way, they don’t take me in. I always know
the reason at the bottom of those predilections the world politely calls
‘unaccountable.’ But in justice to husbands, I must say they never
discover anything. You will tell me that this is a merciful dispens—”

Hélène had come back to the drawing-room with her father and was
listening. So well she understood all that was said that she gave her
mother a frightened glance, feeling with a child’s quick instinct that
these remarks would aggravate the punishment hanging over her. The
Marquise turned her white face to Vandenesse and, with terror in her
eyes, indicated her husband, who stood with his eyes fixed absently on
the carpet’s pattern. The diplomatist, man of the world though he was,
could no longer contain his wrath; he gave the man of law a withering
glance.

“Step this way, sir,” he said, and went hurriedly to the ante-chamber;
the notary left his sentence half finished and followed, quaking, and the
husband and wife were left together.

“Now, sir,” said the Marquise de Vandenesse—he banged the drawing-room
door and spoke with concentrated rage—“ever since dinner you have done
nothing but make blunders and talk folly. For heaven’s sake, go. You will
make the most frightful mischief before you have done. If you are a
clever man in your profession, keep to your profession; and if by any
chance you go into society, endeavor to be more circumspect.”

With that he returned to the drawing-room, and did not even wish the
notary good-evening. For a moment the worthy man stood dumfounded, then,
when the buzzing in his ears subsided, he thought he heard someone
moaning in the next room. Footsteps came and went, and bells were
violently rung. He was by no means anxious to meet the Marquis again,
and found the use of his legs to make good his escape, only to run
against a hurrying crowd of servants at the door.

“Just the way of all these grand folk,” he said to himself in the
street as he looked for a cab. “They lead you on to talk with compliments,
and you think you are amusing them. Not a bit of it. They treat you
insolently; put you at a distance; even put you out at the door without
scruple. After all, I talked very cleverly, I said nothing but what was
sensible, well turned, and discreet; and, upon my word, he advises me to
be more circumspect in future. I will take good care of that! Eh! the
mischief take it! I am a notary and a member of my chamber!--Pshaw! it
was an ambassador’s fit of temper, nothing is sacred for people of that
kind. To-morrow he shall explain what he meant by saying I had done
nothing but blunder and talk nonsense in his house. I will ask him for
an explanation—that is, I will ask him to explain my mistake. After
all is said and done, I am in the wrong perhaps—Upon my word, it is
very good of me to cudgel my brains like this. What business is it of
mine?”

So the notary went home and laid the enigma before his spouse, with a
complete account of the evening’s events.

And she replied, “My dear Crottat, His Excellency was perfectly right
when he said you had done nothing but blunder and talk folly.”

“Why?”

“My dear, if I told you why, it would not prevent you from doing the
same thing somewhere else to-morrow. I tell you again—talk of nothing
but business when you go out; that is my advice to you.”

“If you will not tell me, I shall ask him to-morrow—”

“Why, dear me! the veriest noodle is careful to hide a thing of that
kind, and do you suppose an ambassador will tell you about it? Really,
Crottat, I have never known you so utterly devoid of common sense.”

“Thank you, my dear.”

V. TWO MEETINGS

One of Napoleon’s orderly staff-officers, who shall be known in this
history only as the General or the Marquis, had come to spend the spring
at Versailles. He made a large fortune under the Restoration; and as
his place at Court would not allow him to go very far from Paris, he had
taken a country house between the church and the barrier of Montreuil,
on the road that leads to the Avenue de Saint-Cloud.

The house had been built originally as a retreat for the short-lived
loves of some grand seigneur. The grounds were large; the gardens on
either side extended from the first houses of Montreuil to the thatched
cottages near the barrier, so that the owner could enjoy solitude with
the city almost at his gates. By contradiction, the whole front of the
house itself, with the principal entrance, gave directly upon the street.
Perhaps in time past it was a tolerably lonely road; not far away, on
this same road, Louis Quinze built a summer villa for Mlle. de Romans,
and the wayside casinos recall traditions of the ingenious taste our
ancestors displayed in debauchery, seeking to invest it with secrecy.

One winter evening the family were by themselves in the lonely house.
The servants had received permission to go to Versailles to celebrate
the wedding of one of their number. It was Christmas time, and the
holiday makers, presuming upon the double festival, did not scruple to
outstay their leave; yet, as the General was well known to be a man of
his word, the culprits felt some twinges of conscience as they danced
on after the hour of return. The clocks struck eleven, and still there
was no sign of the servants.

A deep silence prevailed over the country-side, broken only by the
northeast wind whistling through the black branches and along the
corridors. The hard frost had purified the air and held the earth in its
grip; the roads gave back every sound with the metallic ring which
strikes us with surprise; the heavy footsteps of some belated reveler
or a cab returning to Paris could be heard for a long distance with
unusual distinctness. Out in the courtyard a few dead leaves set a-dancing
by an eddying gust found a voice for the night which fain had been
silent. It was one of those sharp, frosty evenings that wring barren
expressions of pity from our selfish ease for wayfarers and the poor,
and fill us with a luxurious sense of the comfort of the fireside.

But the family party in the salon at that hour gave not a thought to
absent servants nor houseless folk, nor to the gracious charm with which
a winter evening sparkles. Secure in the protection of an old soldier,
women and children gave themselves up to the joys of home life; talk and
play and glances were bright with frankness and affection.

The General sat, or rather lay buried, in the depths of a huge, high-
back armchair by the hearth. The heaped-up fire burned scorching clear
with the cold of the night. The good father leaned his head slightly to
one side against the back of the chair, in the indolence of perfect
serenity and a glow of happiness. The languid, half-sleepy droop of his
outstretched arms completed his expression of placid content. He was
watching his youngest, a boy of five or thereabouts, who, half clad as
he was, declined to allow his mother to undress him. The little one fled
from the night-gown and cap with which he was threatened now and again,
and stoutly declined to part with his embroidered collar, laughing when
his mother called to him, for he saw that she too was laughing at this
declaration of infant independence. The next step was a romping game
with his sister. She was older by two years, mischievous, and could
speak distinctly, whereas his inarticulate words and confused ideas were
a puzzle even to his parents. Little Moïna’s playfulness provoked
inextinguishable laughter, explosions of merriment like fireworks for no
apparent cause. As they tumbled about before the fire, unconcernedly
displaying plump bodies and delicate contours, their dark and golden
curls mingled on rosy cheeks dimpled with childish glee. A father must
have understood those little souls, and a mother certainly saw character
and the power of passion already developed for their eyes. As the
cherubs frolicked on the soft carpet, its flowers looked pale beside
the glowing white and red of their cheeks and the brilliant color of
their shining eyes.

On the sofa by the fire, opposite the great armchair, the children’s
mother sat among a heap of scattered garments, with a little scarlet
shoe in her hand. She seemed to have given herself up completely to the
enjoyment of the moment; wavering discipline had relaxed into a sweet
smile. At the age of thirty-six, or thereabouts, she was still a
beautiful woman, by reason of the rare perfection of the outlines of
her face, and at this moment light and warmth and happiness filled it
with brightness.

Again and again her eyes wandered from her children, and their tender
gaze was turned upon her husband’s grave face; and now and again the eyes
of husband and wife met with a silent exchange of happiness and thoughts
from some inner depth.

The General’s face was deeply bronzed, a stray lock of gray hair scored
shadows on his forehead. The reckless courage of the battlefield could
be read in the lines carved in his hollow cheeks, and gleams of rugged
strength in the blue eyes; clearly the red ribbon at his button-hole had
been paid for by hardship and toil. An inexpressible kindliness and
frankness shone out of the strong, resolute face which reflected his
children’s merriment; the gray-haired captain found it not so very hard
to become a child again. Is there not always a little love of children
in the heart of a soldier who has seen enough of life’s seamy side to
know something of the piteous limitations of strength and the
privileges of weakness?

At a round table rather further away, in a circle of bright lamplight,
sat a boy of thirteen, rapidly turning the pages of a thick volume,
undisturbed by the shouts of the children. There was curiosity in his
face. From his lycéens uniform he was evidently a schoolboy, and the book
he was reading was the Arabian Nights. Small wonder he was absorbed.
He sat perfectly still, elbow on the table, hand propping his head—the
white fingers contrasting with the brown hair into which they were
thrust. With the light full upon his face, and the rest of his body in
shadow, he looked like one of Raphael’s dark portraits—bent head and
intent eyes filled with visions of the future.

Between the table and the Marquise a tall, beautiful girl sat at her
tapestry frame; sometimes she drew back from her work, sometimes she
bent over it, and her hair, picturesque in its ebony smoothness and
darkness, caught the lamp light. Hélène was a picture in herself. In her
beauty there was a rare character of power and refinement. Though her
hair was gathered up from her face, so thick and abundant was it that it
sprang back in curl-tendrils to the nape of her neck. Her eyebrows were
evenly marked upon her pure forehead. On her upper lip, beneath the
Grecian nose, lay a faint swarthy shadow, a sign of courage; but the
enchanting roundness of contour, the innocent expression of her other
features, the transparence of her delicate carnations, the softness of
the lips, and the flawless oval of her face, and, more than all, the
saintlike expression in her girlish eyes, gave to her vigorous loveliness
the touch of feminine grace, that modesty we look for in these angels of
peace and love. Yet there was no fragility about her; with so grand a
woman’s frame, so attractive a face, she must possess corresponding
warmth of heart and strength of soul.

She was as silent as her schoolboy brother. Prey to maiden meditations
which baffle a father’s penetration and a mother’s sagacity, it was
impossible to be certain whether lamplight cast the shadows that flitted
over her face like thin clouds, or whether they were passing shades of
secret and painful thoughts.

Husband and wife had quite forgotten the two older children, though now
and again the General’s questioning glance traveled to that second mute
picture; a larger growth, a gracious realization, as it were, of the
hopes embodied in the baby forms rioting in the foreground. Their faces
made up a kind of living poem, illustrating life’s phases. The luxurious
background of the salon, the different attitudes, the strong contrasts
of coloring in the faces differing with age, the modeling brought into
high relief by the light—altogether it was a page of human life,
richly illuminated beyond the art of painter, sculptor, or poet. Silence,
solitude, night and winter lent a final touch of majesty to complete
this simplicity and sublimity of nature’s contriving. Married life is
full of these sacred hours, perhaps owing their charm to some vague
memory of a better world. A divine radiance surely shines upon them,
the destined compensation for some portion of earth’s sorrows, the
solace enabling man to accept life. We seem to behold a vision of an
enchanted universe; social life pleads for its laws by bidding us look
to the future.

Yet despite the tender glances Hélène gave Abel and Moïna; despite the
gladness in her face whenever she looked at her father, a deep melancholy
pervaded her gestures, attitude, and eyes veiled by long lashes. Those
white, strong hands through which the light passed, tinting them with a
diaphanous red—those hands were trembling. Once only did the eyes of
mother and daughter clash without shrinking, and the two read each
other’s thoughts in a look, cold, wan, and respectful on Hélène’s part,
sombre and threatening on her mother’s. At once Hélène’s eyes were
lowered to her work; she plied her needle swiftly, and it was long
before she raised her head, bowed as if by a weight of thought too heavy
to bear. Was the Marquise over harsh? Did she think this needful? Was she
jealous of Hélène’s beauty? Or had Hélène, like many girls at a clairvoyant
age, read secrets which this wife, so religiously faithful in appearance,
believed buried in her own heart?

Hélène had reached an age when purity of soul inclines to over-rigid
judgments. A certain order of mind is apt to exaggerate transgression
into crime; imagination reacts upon conscience, and a young girl is a
hard judge because she magnifies the seriousness of offence. Hélène
seemed to think herself worthy of no one. Perhaps there was a secret in
her past, something unintelligible then but with growing significance to
a mind susceptible to religious influences; something that lately seemed
to have degraded her in her own eyes. This change dated from the day of
reading Schiller’s Wilhelm Tell in a new translation. Her mother scolded
her for letting the book fall, and then remarked to herself that the
passage which moved Hélène was the scene where Tell, who spilled the
blood of a tyrant to save a nation, fraternizes in some sort with John
the Parricide. Hélène had grown humble, dutiful, and self-contained; she
no longer cared for gaiety. Never had she made so much of her father,
especially when the Marquise was not by to watch her girlish caresses.
And yet, if Hélène’s affection for her mother had cooled at all, the
change was so slight the General could not have noticed it. No masculine
insight could have sounded the depths of those two feminine natures;
the one was young and generous, the other sensitive and proud; the first
had indulgence, the second craft and love. If the Marquise made her
daughter’s life a burden by a woman’s subtle tyranny, it was invisible
to all but the victim; conjectures on the matter must remain conjectures.
Until this night no accusing flash had escaped either of them, but an
ominous mystery was growing between them, known only to themselves
and God.

“Come, Abel,” called the Marquise, seizing the moment when the children
were tired and still. “Come, child; you must be put to bed.”

With a glance that must be obeyed she caught him up and took him on her
knee.

“What!” exclaimed the General. “Half-past ten o’clock, and not one of
the servants has come back! The rascals!--Gustave,” he added, turning
to his son, “I allowed you to read that book only on condition that you
should put it away at ten o’clock. You ought to have shut it up and gone
to bed, as you promised. If you mean to make your mark in the world,
you must keep your word; let it be a second religion to you, and a
point of honor. Fox, one of the greatest English orators, was remarkable
for the beauty of his character, and the first of his qualities was the
scrupulous faithfulness with which he kept his engagements. When he was
a child, his father gave him a lesson which he never forgot. Like most
rich Englishmen, Fox’s father had a country house and a park. In the
park there was an old summer-house, and orders had been given that it
was to be pulled down and put up elsewhere. Fox was about your age and
asked to stay to see it taken down; his father said he must go back to
school. Fox’s mother took the boy’s part. The father promised the summer-
house would stay. Fox went back to school; the father had it pulled down
and put in a new place. The persistent youngster complained, and the
father, ashamed, restored it to the old place and took it down again
for his son to see. Let this be a lesson to you, Gustave.”

Gustave, listening, closed the book at once. There was silence while the
General took possession of Moïna, whose languid head fell back on her
father’s breast, and in a moment she was asleep, wrapped in golden curls.

Just then hurrying footsteps rang on the pavement, followed by three
knocks on the street door, waking echoes through the house. The blows
told plainly that a man was flying for his life. The house dog barked.
A thrill of excitement ran through Hélène and Gustave and the General
and his wife; but neither Abel, with the night-cap strings just tied,
nor Moïna awoke.

“The fellow is in a hurry!” exclaimed the General. He put the little
girl down on the chair and hastened out of the room, heedless of his
wife’s entreating cry, “Dear, do not go down—”

He stepped into his own room for a pair of pistols, lighted a dark
lantern, sprang down the staircase, and in another minute reached the
house door, his oldest boy following.

“Who is there?” demanded he.

“Let me in,” panted a breathless voice.

“Are you a friend?”

“Yes, friend.”

“Are you alone?”

“Yes! But let me in; they are after me!”

The General had scarcely set the door ajar before a man slipped into the
porch with the uncanny swiftness of a shadow. Before the master could
prevent him, the intruder had closed the door with a kick and set his
back against it resolutely, as if determined it should not be opened.
In a moment the General had his lantern and pistol at the stranger’s
breast, and beheld a man of medium height in a fur-lined pelisse. It
was an old man’s garment, both too large and too long. Chance or
caution slouched the hat over his eyes.

“You can lower your pistol, sir,” said this person. “I do not claim to
stay in your house against your will; but if I leave it, death is
waiting for me at the barrier. And what a death! You would be answerable
to God for it! I ask for your hospitality for two hours. And bear this
in mind, sir, that, suppliant as I am, I have a right to command with
the despotism of necessity. I want the Arab’s hospitality. Either I
and my secret must be inviolable, or open the door and I will go to my
death. I want secrecy, a safe hiding-place, and water. Oh! water!” he
cried, with a rattle in his throat.

“Who are you?” demanded the General, taken aback by the stranger’s
feverish volubility.

“Ah! who am I? Good, open the door, and I will put a distance between
us,” retorted the other, with diabolical irony.

Dexterously the Marquis passed the lantern over the man’s face; he
could only see the lower half, and that did not prepossess him in favour
of this claimant of hospitality. The cheeks were livid and quivering,
the features dreadfully contorted. Under the hat-brim a pair of eyes
gleamed like flames; the feeble candlelight looked dim in comparison.
Some answer must be made.

“Your language, sir, is so extraordinary that in my place you yourself—”

“My life is in your hands!” the intruder broke in. The sound of his
voice was dreadful.

“Two hours?” said the Marquis, wavering.

“Two hours,” echoed the other.

Then, with a desperate gesture, he pushed back his hat and left his
forehead bare, and, as if to try a final expedient, he gave the General
a glance that seemed to plunge like a vivid flash into his very soul.
That electrical discharge of intelligence and will was swift as lightning
and crushing as a thunderbolt; for there are moments when a human being
is invested for a brief space with inexplicable power.

“Come, whoever you may be, you shall be in safety under my roof,” the
master said gravely at last, acting on one of those intuitions a man
cannot explain.

“God will repay you!” said the stranger, with a deep sigh.

“Have you weapons?” asked the General.

For answer the stranger flung open his pelisse, scarcely giving time for
a glance before wrapping it again. To all appearance he was unarmed and
in evening dress. Swift as the soldier’s scrutiny had been, he saw
something, however, which made him exclaim:

“Where the devil have you been to get yourself in such a mess in such
dry weather?”

“More questions!” said the stranger haughtily.

At the words the Marquis caught sight of his son, and his own homily on
keeping a word came to mind. In vexation he exclaimed, not without anger:

“What! little rogue, you here when you ought to be in bed?”

“Because I thought I might be of some good in danger,” answered Gustave.

“There, go up to your room,” said his father, mollified.—“And you,” he
added to the stranger, “come with me.”

The two men grew as silent as gamblers watching each other’s play.
The General himself was troubled with ugly presentiments. The strange
visit weighed upon him like a nightmare; but he had passed his word,
there was no help, and he led the way along passages and stairways to a
large room on the second floor above the salon. This was an empty room
where linen was dried. It had but the one door, and for decoration one
solitary shabby looking-glass above the chimney-piece left by the
previous owner, and a great pier-glass placed provisionally opposite
the fireplace. The four yellowish walls were bare. The floor had never
been swept. The attic was icy-cold, and the furniture consisted of a
couple of rickety straw-bottomed chairs. The General set the lantern
upon the chimney-piece. Then he spoke:

“It is necessary for your safety to hide you in this comfortless attic.
And, as you have my promise to keep your secret, you will permit me to
lock you in.”

The other bent his head in acquiescence.

“I asked for nothing but a hiding-place, secrecy, and water,” returned
he.

“I will bring you some directly,” said the Marquis, shutting the door
cautiously. He groped down into the salon for a lamp before going to the
kitchen to look for a carafe.

“Well, what is it?” the Marquise asked quickly.

“Nothing, dear,” he returned coolly.

“But we listened, and we certainly heard you go upstairs with somebody.”

“Helène,” said the General, and he looked at his daughter, who raised
her face, “bear in mind that your father’s honor depends upon your
discretion. You must have heard nothing.”

The girl bent her head in answer. The Marquise was confused and smarting
inwardly at the way her husband had silenced her.

Meanwhile the General fetched the bottle and tumbler and returned. His
prisoner was leaning against the chimney-piece, head bare, hat flung on
a chair. Evidently he had not expected such bright light, and he frowned
and looked anxious as he met the General’s keen eyes; but his face softened
as he thanked his protector. When the latter placed the bottle and glass
on the mantel-shelf, the stranger’s eyes flashed again; and when he
spoke, it was in musical tones with no sign of the previous convulsion,
though his voice was unsteady with repressed emotion.

“I shall seem to you to be a strange being, sir, but you must pardon the
caprices of necessity. If you remain in the room, I beg that you will
not look at me while I am drinking.”

Vexed at this continual obedience to a man he disliked, the General
sharply turned his back. The stranger drew a white handkerchief and
wound it about his right hand. Then he seized the carafe and emptied it
at a draught. The Marquis, staring vacantly into the tall mirror,
saw the stranger’s figure reflected and saw a red stain suddenly appear
through the folds of the white bandage. The man’s hands were steeped in
blood.

“Ah! you saw me!” cried the other. He had drunk the water and wrapped
himself again in his cloak, now scrutinizing the General suspiciously.
“It is all over with me! Here they come!”

“I don’t hear anything,” said the Marquis.

“You have not the same interest that I have in listening for sounds in
the air.”

“You have been fighting a duel, I suppose, to be in such a state?”
queried the General, disturbed by the dark patches staining his visitor’s
cloak.

“Yes, a duel,” said the other, and a bitter smile flitted over his
lips.

As he spoke a sound rang along the road, the faint dawn of galloping
horses. The General’s trained ear recognized the advance of a troop of
regulars.

“That is the gendarmerie,” said he.

He glanced at his prisoner to reassure him after his involuntary
indiscretion, took the lamp, and went down to the salon. He had scarcely
laid the key of the room above upon the chimney-piece when the hoof
beats sounded louder and nearer. The horses stopped at the house door;
a few words were exchanged and one man dismounted and knocked loudly.
There was no help for it; the General opened. He could scarcely conceal
perturbation at sight of half a dozen gendarmes, the metal rims of their
caps gleaming like silver in the moonlight.

“My lord,” said the corporal, “have you heard a man run past towards the
barrier within the last few minutes?”

“Towards the barrier? No.”

“Have you opened the door to any one?”

“Now, am I in the habit of answering the door myself—”

“I ask your pardon, General, but just now it seems to me that—”

“Really!” cried the Marquis wrathfully. “Have you a mind to try joking
with me? What right have you—?”

“None at all, none at all, my lord,” cried the corporal, putting in a
soft answer. “You will excuse our zeal. We know, of course, that a peer
of France is not likely to harbor a murderer at this time of night;
but as we want any information we can get—”

“A murderer!” cried the General. “Who can have been—”

“M. le Baron de Mauny has just been murdered. It was a blow from an
axe, and we are in hot pursuit of the criminal. We know for certain that
he is somewhere in this neighborhood, and we shall hunt him down. By
your leave, General,” and the man swung himself into the saddle as he
spoke. It was well he did, for a corporal trained to observation would
have had suspicions if he had caught sight of the General’s face. Everything
that passed through the soldier’s mind was faithfully revealed in his
frank countenance.

“Is it known who the murderer is?” asked he.

“No,” said the other, now in the saddle. “He left the bureau full of
banknotes and gold untouched.”

“It was revenge, then,” said the Marquis.

“On an old man? pshaw! No, the fellow hadn’t time to take it, that was
all,” and the corporal galloped after his comrades, who were almost out
of sight.

For a few minutes the General stood, a victim to perplexities; but he
heard the servants returning, their voices raised in some dispute at
the cross-roads of Montreuil. When they came in, he gave vent to his
feelings in an explosion of rage, his wrath fell upon them like a
thunderbolt. In the midst of the storm his own man brought out an
excuse; they had been stopped by the gendarmerie at the gate of Montreuil,
a murder had been committed, and the police were in pursuit. In a
moment the General’s anger vanished; then, bethinking himself of his
position, he drily ordered them all to bed at once.

While these incidents took place in the yard, a trifling occurrence had
changed the relative positions of three characters in this story. The
Marquis had scarcely left the room before his wife looked first toward
the key on the mantel-shelf, and then at Hélène; after some wavering,
bent toward her daughter and said in a low voice, “Hélène your father has
left the key on the chimney-piece.”

The girl looked up in surprise and glanced timidly at her mother. The
Marquise’s eyes sparkled with curiosity.

“Well, mamma?” she said, her voice troubled.

“I should like to know what is going on upstairs. If there is anybody
up there, he has not stirred yet. Just go up—”

“I?” cried the girl, with something like horror.

“Are you afraid?”

“No, mamma, but I thought I heard a man’s footsteps.”

“If I could go myself, I should not have asked you to go, Hélène,” said
her mother with cold dignity. “If your father were to come back and did
not see me, he would go to look for me perhaps, but he would not notice
your absence.”

“Madame, if you bid me go, I will go,” said Hélène, “but I shall lose
my father’s good opinion—”

“What is this!” cried the Marquise in a sarcastic tone. “But since you
take a thing said in joke in earnest, I now order you to go upstairs and
see who is in the room above. Here is the key, child. When your father
told you to say nothing, he did not forbid you to go up to the room.
Go at once—and learn that a daughter ought never to judge her mother.”

The last words were spoken with the severity of a justly offended mother.
The Marquise took the key and handed it to Hélène, who rose without a
word and left the room.

“My mother can always easily obtain her pardon,” thought the girl; “but
as for me, my father will never think the same of me again. Does she
mean to rob me of his tenderness? Does she want to turn me out of his
house?”

These thoughts set her imagination in ferment as she went down the dark
passage to the mysterious door at the end. When she stood before it,
her mental confusion grew. Feelings forced down into inner depths crowded
up. Perhaps she'd never believed in a happy life before, but now her
despair was complete. She shook convulsively as she set the key in the
lock; so great was her agitation she stopped a moment and laid her hand
on her heart, as if to still the heavy throbs in her ears. Then she
opened the door.

The creaking of the hinges sounded in vain on the murderer’s ears.
Acute as were his powers of hearing, he stood as if lost in thought,
motionless as if glued to the wall. In the circle of semi-opaque darkness,
dimly lit by the bull’s-eye lantern, he looked like the shadowy figure
of some dead knight, standing in his mortuary niche. Drops of cold sweat
trickled over the sallow forehead. An incredible fearlessness showed on
every tense feature. His eyes of fire were fixed and tearless; he seemed
to be watching some struggle in the darkness beyond him. Stormy thoughts
passed swiftly across a face whose firm decision spoke of uncommon
character. His whole bearing suggested a tameless spirit. The man looked
power and strength personified; he stood facing the darkness as if it
were the image of his own future.

These physical characteristics had made no impression upon the General,
familiar with the powerful faces of the group about Napoleon; speculative
curiosity filled his mind; but Hélène, with feminine sensitiveness to
surface impressions, was struck by the chaos of light and darkness,
grandeur and passion, suggesting a likeness between this stranger and
Lucifer recovering from his fall. Suddenly the storm in his face stilled
as if by magic; the power to sway others spread about him like a flood.
A torrent of thought rolled away from his brow as his face resumed its
ordinary expression. Perhaps it was the strangeness of the meeting, or
the mystery into which she had penetrated, that held the girl spellbound
in the doorway, so that she could look at a face pleasant to behold and
full of interest. For some moments she stood in the magical silence;
a trouble had come upon her never known before. Perhaps some exclamation
broke from Hélène, perhaps she moved unconsciously; however it was, he
turned his head and saw dimly in the shadow a noble face and queenly
form, which he must have taken for an angel.

“Monsieur...” a trembling voice cried.

The murderer trembled.

“A woman!” he cried under his breath. “Is it possible? Go,” he cried,
“I deny that any one has a right to pity, to absolve, or condemn me. I
must live alone. Go, my child,” he added with an imperious gesture,
“I should ill requite the service done me by the master of the house
if I allowed a single creature under his roof to breathe the same air
with me. I must submit to be judged by the laws of the world.”

The last words were lower. Even as he realized all the misery awakened
by that melancholy thought, the glance he gave Hélène had something of
the serpent, stirring a whole dormant world. To her that glance was like
a light revealing unknown lands. She was stricken with strange trouble,
helpless, quelled by a magnetic power exerted unconsciously. Trembling
and ashamed, she went out and returned to the salon. She had scarcely
entered when her father came back, so she had not time to say a word to
her mother.

The General was wholly absorbed in thought. He folded his arms and
paced silently to and fro between the windows which looked out upon the
street and the garden. His wife lay the sleeping Abel on her knee, and
little Moïna slept in the low chair like a bird in its nest. His older
daughter stared into the fire, a skein of silk in one hand, a needle in
the other.

Deep silence prevailed, broken only by lagging footsteps as the servants
crept away to bed; there was an occasional burst of stifled laughter
from the wedding festivity, doors opened and shut. A smothered sound came
from the bedrooms, a chair fell, the old coachman coughed, then all was
silent.

In a little while the dark majesty of sleeping earth brought all things
under its sway. No lights shone but the stars. The frost gripped the
ground. The crackling of the fire only made the depth of silence more
fully felt.

The church clock of Montreuil had just struck one, when an almost
inaudible sound of a light footstep came from the second flight of stairs.
The Marquis and his daughter, both believing M. de Mauny’s murderer was
a prisoner above, thought one of the maids had come down; no one was
surprised to hear the door open in the ante-chamber. Quite suddenly the
murderer appeared in their midst. The Marquis himself was sunk in deep
musing, the mother and daughter were silent from curiosity and astonishment,
so that the visitor was almost half-way across the room when he spoke.

“Sir, the two hours are almost over,” he said in a voice strangely calm
and musical.

“You here!” cried the General. “By what means—” he gave wife and
daughter a formidable questioning glance. Hélène grew red as fire.

“You!” he went on, in a tone filled with horror. “You among us! A
murderer covered with blood! You are a blot on this picture! Go, go out!”
he added in a burst of rage.

At that word “murderer,” the Marquise cried out; as for Hélène, it
seemed to mark an epoch in her life. She looked as if she had been
waiting for this—for him. Those vast thoughts of hers found a meaning.
The punishment reserved by Heaven for her sins flamed out before her.
In her own eyes she was as great a criminal as this murderer; she
confronted him with her quiet gaze; she was his fellow, his sister.
It seemed to her that in this accident the command of God had been made
manifest. If she had been older, reason would have disposed of her
remorse, but at this moment she was like one distraught.

The stranger stood impassive and self-possessed; a scornful smile spread
over his face and the thick red lips.

“You appreciate the magnanimity of my behavior very badly,” he said
slowly. “I would not touch with my fingers the glass of water you
brought me; I did not wash my blood-stained hands under your roof; I am
going away, leaving nothing of my crime but memory; I have tried to
leave no trace of my presence in this house. Indeed, I would not even
allow your daughter to—”

“My daughter!” cried the General, horror-stricken at Hélène. “Vile wretch,
go, or I will kill you—”

“The two hours are not yet over,” said the other; “if you kill me or
give me up, you must lower yourself in your own eyes—and in mine.”

At these words the General stared in dumb amazement; he could not endure
the intolerable light in those eyes which for the second time disorganized
him. He was afraid of showing weakness once more, conscious his will was
weaker already.

“An old man! You can never have seen a family,” he said, with a father’s
glance at his wife and children.

“Yes, an old man,” echoed the stranger, frowning slightly.

“Fly!” cried the General, but he did not dare to look at his guest. “Our
compact is broken. I shall not kill you. No! I will never be purveyor to
the scaffold. But go out. You make us shudder.”

“I know that,” said the other patiently. “There is not a spot on French
soil where I can set foot and be safe; but if man’s justice, like God’s,
took all into account, if man’s justice inquired which was the monster
—the murderer or his victim—then I might hold up my head among my
fellows. Can you not guess that other crimes preceded that blow from an
axe? I constituted myself his judge and executioner; I stepped in where
man’s justice failed. That was my crime. Farewell, sir. Bitter though
you have made your hospitality, I shall not forget it. I shall always
bear in my heart a feeling of gratitude towards one man in the world, and
you are that man.... But I could wish that you had showed yourself more
generous!”

He turned toward the door, but in the same instant Hélène leaned to
whisper something in her mother’s ear.

“Ah!...”

At the cry that broke from his wife, the General trembled as if he had
seen Moïna lying dead. There stood Hélène, and the murderer had turned
instinctively, with anxiety about these folk in his face.

“What is it, dear?” asked the General.

“Hélène wants to go with him.”

The murderer’s face flushed.

“If that is how my mother understands an almost involuntary exclamation,” 
Hélène said in a low voice, “I will fulfil her wishes.” She glanced about
with fierce pride; then her eyes fell, and she stood, admirable in her
modesty.

“Hélène, did you go up to the room where—?”

“Yes, father.”

“Hélène” (and his voice shook), “is this the first time you have seen
this man?”

“Yes, father.”

“Then it is not natural that you should intend to—”

“If it is not natural, father, at any rate it is true.”

“Oh! child,” said the Marquise, lowering her voice, but not so much but
that her husband could hear, “you are false to all the principles of
honor, modesty, and right I have tried to cultivate. If until this
fatal hour your life has been one lie, there is nothing to regret. It
can hardly be the moral perfection of this stranger that attracts you?
I have too good an opinion of you to suppose that—”

“Oh, suppose everything, madame,” Hélène said coldly.

Though her force of character sustained the ordeal, her flashing eyes
could scarcely hold the tears. The stranger, watching her, guessed the
mother’s language and turned his eagle glance on the Marquise. An
irresistible power constrained her to look at this terrible seducer; as
her eyes met his bright, glittering gaze she felt a shiver run through
her frame, such a shock as at sight of a reptile or the contact of a
Leyden jar.

“Dear!” she cried to her husband, “this is the Fiend himself. He can
divine everything!”

The General rose and went to the bell.

“He means ruin for you,” Hélène said to the murderer.

The stranger smiled, stepped forward, grasped the General’s arm, and
compelled him to endure a steady gaze which benumbed the soldier’s brain
and left him powerless.

“I will repay you now for your hospitality,” he said, “and then we shall
be quits. I will spare you the shame by giving myself up. After all,
what should I do now with my life?”

“You could repent,” answered Hélène, and her glance conveyed such hope as
only glows in a young girl’s eyes.

“I shall never repent,” said the murderer in a sonorous voice, raising
his head proudly.

“His hands are stained with blood,” the father said.

“I will wipe it away,” she answered.

“But do you so much as know whether he cares for you?” said her father,
not daring now to look at the stranger.

The murderer came nearer. Some light seemed to glow through Hélène’s
beauty, coloring and bringing into relief the least details of her face.
The stranger, with that terrible face blazing in his eyes, gave one
tender glance to her loveliness, then spoke, revealing how deeply he
had been moved.

“And if I refuse to allow this sacrifice of yourself, and so discharge
my debt of two hours of existence to your father; is not this love,
love for yourself alone?”

“Then do you too reject me?” Hélène’s cry rang painfully.

“Farewell, then, to you all; I will die.”

“What does this mean?” asked the father and mother.

Hélène gave her mother an eloquent glance and lowered her eyes.

Since the General and his wife first contested the intruder’s presumption,
from their first experience of the power of those glittering eyes, a
mysterious torpor crept over them, their faculties struggled in vain
with the preternatural influence. The air seemed heavy; they could
scarcely breathe; a voice within told them this magnetic presence was
the cause. In this moral agony it flashed across the General that he
must make every effort to overcome the influence on his daughter’s
reeling brain; he caught her by the waist and drew her into the embrasure
of a window, as far as possible from the murderer.

“Darling,” he murmured, “if some wild love has been born in your heart, I
cannot believe you have not the strength to quell the mad impulse; your
innocent life, your pure and dutiful soul, has given me too many proofs
of your character. There must be something behind. Well, this heart of
mine is full of indulgence; you can tell everything to me; even if it
breaks, dear child, I can be silent about my grief, and keep your
confession a secret. What is it? Are you jealous of our love for your
brothers or your little sister? Is it some love trouble? Tell me the
reasons that urge you to leave your home, to rob it of its greatest
charm, to leave your mother and brothers and your little sister?”

“I am in love with no one, father, and jealous of no one, not even of
your friend the diplomatist, M. de Vandenesse.”

The Marquise turned pale; her daughter saw this and stopped.

“Sooner or later I must live under some man’s protection, must I not?”

“That is true.”

“Do we ever know the human being to whom we link our destinies? Now, I
believe in this man.”

“Oh, child,” said the General, “you have no idea of all the misery that
lies in store for you.”

“I am thinking of his.”

“What a life!” groaned the father.

“A woman’s life,” the girl murmured.

“You have a great knowledge of life!” exclaimed the Marquise.

“Madame, my answers are shaped by the questions; but if you desire it, I
will speak more clearly.”

“Speak out, my child... I am a mother.”

Mother and daughter looked each other in the face, and the Marquise said
no more. At last she said:

“Hélène, if you have any reproaches to make, I would rather bear them
than see you go away with a man from whom the whole world shrinks in
horror.”

“Then you see yourself, madame, that but for me he would be quite
alone.”

“That will do, madame,” the General cried; “we have but one daughter
left to us now,” and he looked at Moïna, who slept on. “As for you,”
he added, turning to Hélène, “I will put you in a convent.”

“So be it, father,” she said in calm despair, “I shall die there. You
are answerable to God alone for my life and for his soul.”

A deep sullen silence fell. The on-lookers during this strange scene,
so at variance with ordinary life, shunned each other’s eyes.

Suddenly the Marquis glanced at his pistols. He caught up one, cocked
it, and pointed it at the intruder. At the click the other turned his
piercing gaze full upon the General; the soldier’s arm slackened and
fell heavily. The pistol dropped to the floor.

“Girl, you are free,” said he, exhausted by the struggle. “Kiss your
mother, if she will let you kiss her. For my part, I wish never to see
nor to hear of you again.”

“Hélène,” the mother began, “only think of the wretched life before you.”

A rattling sound came from the intruder’s deep chest; all eyes were turned
to him. Disdain was visible in his face.

The General rose. “My hospitality has cost me dear,” he cried. “Before
you came you had taken an old man’s life; now you are dealing a deadly
blow at a whole family. Whatever happens, there must be unhappiness in
this house.”

“And if your daughter is happy?” asked the other, gazing steadily.

The father made a superhuman effort. “If she is happy with you,” he
said, “she is not worth regretting.”

Hélène knelt timidly before her father.

“Father, I love and revere you,” she said, “whether you lavish all the
treasures of your kindness upon me, or make me feel the rigor of
disgrace.... But I entreat that your last words of farewell shall not be
words of anger.”

The General could not trust himself to look at her. The stranger came
nearer; there was something half-diabolical, half-divine in the smile
he gave Hélène.

“Angel of pity, you who do not shrink from a murderer, come, since you
persist in entrusting your life to me.”

“Inconceivable!” cried her father.

The Marquise looked strangely at her daughter, opened her arms, and
Hélène fled to her in tears.

“Farewell,” she said, “farewell, mother!” The stranger trembled as
Hélène, undaunted, made sign she was ready. She kissed her father’s
hand; and, as if performing a duty, gave a hasty kiss to Moïna and Abel,
then she vanished with the murderer.

“Which way are they going?” exclaimed the General, listening to the
footsteps of the fugitives.—“Madame,” he turned to his wife, “I think I
must be dreaming; there is some mystery behind all this, I do not
understand it; you must know what it means.”

The Marquise shivered.

“For some time past your daughter has grown extraordinarily romantic and
strangely high-flown. In spite of the pains I have taken to combat these
tendencies—”

“This will not do—” began the General, but hearing footsteps in the
garden, he flung open the window.

“Hélène!” he shouted.

His voice was lost in the darkness like a vain prophecy. The utterance
of that name broke the charm and set him free from the enchantment. It
was as if some spirit passed over his face. He now saw clearly what had
taken place, and cursed his weakness. A shiver of heat rushed from his
heart to head and feet; he became himself again, terrible and thirsting
for revenge. He raised a dreadful cry.

“Help!” he thundered, “help!”

He rushed to the bell-pull, pulled till the bells rang with a strange
clamor, pulled till the cord gave way. The whole house was roused. Still
shouting, he flung open the windows that looked upon the street, called
for the police, caught up his pistols, and fired them to hurry patrols,
the servants, and the neighbors. The dogs barked; the horses neighed and
stamped. The quiet night was suddenly filled with hideous uproar. The
General on the staircase, in pursuit of his daughter, saw the scared
faces of the servants flocking from all parts.

“My daughter!” he shouted. “Hélène has been carried off. Search the
garden. Keep a lookout on the road! Open the gates for the gendarmerie!
Murder! Help!”

With fury he snapped the chain and let loose the great house-dog.

“Hélène!” he cried.

The dog sprang out like a lion, barking furiously, and dashed into the
garden, leaving the General far behind. A troop of horses came at a
gallop, and he flew to open the gates himself.

“Corporal!” he shouted, “cut off the retreat of M. de Mauny’s murderer.
They have gone through my garden. Quick! Put a cordon of men to watch
the ways by the Butte de Picardie.—I will beat up the grounds, parks,
and houses.—The rest of you keep a lookout along the road,” he ordered,
“form a chain between the barrier and Versailles. Forward, every man!”

He caught up the rifle his man had brought and dashed into the garden.

“Find them!” he called to the dog.

An ominous baying came in answer, and he plunged toward the sound.

It was seven o’clock in the morning; all the search by gendarmes,
servants, and neighbors had been fruitless, and the dog had not come
back. The General entered the salon, empty now for him though the other
three children were there; he was worn out with fatigue, and looked old
already with that night’s work.

“You have been very cold to your daughter,” he said, turning his eyes
on his wife.—“And now this is all that is left to us of her,” he added,
indicating the embroidery frame, and the flower just begun. “Only just
now she was there, and now she is lost... lost!”

Tears followed; he hid his face in his hands; he could not bear the sight
of the room, which so short a time ago had made a setting to a picture
of the sweetest family happiness. The winter dawn struggled with the
dying lamplight; the tapers burned down and flared out; all matched the
father’s despair.

“This must be destroyed,” he said after a pause, pointing to the
tambour-frame. “I shall never bear to see anything that reminds us of
her!”

The terrible Christmas night when the Marquis and his wife lost their
oldest daughter, powerless to oppose the mysterious influence exercised
by the man who stole Hélène from them, was like a warning. The Marquis
was ruined by the failure of his stock-broker; he borrowed money on his
wife’s property, and lost it trying to retrieve his fortunes. Driven to
desperate expedients, he left France. Six years went by. His family
seldom had news of him; but a few days before Spain recognized the
independence of the American Republics, he wrote that he was coming home.

So, one fine morning, several French merchants were on board a Spanish
brig a few leagues out from Bordeaux, impatient to reach their native
land with wealth acquired by long toil and perilous adventures in
Venezuela and Mexico.

One of the passengers, a man aged by trouble rather than years, leaned
against the bulwark netting, apparently unaffected by the sight from
deck. The bright day, the safe voyage, had brought all passengers above
to greet land. Most insisted they could see the houses and lighthouses
on the coast of Gascony and the Tower of Cardouan, melting into white
cloud along the horizon. The sea was calm. The sky was clear, the dark
blue vaulted above paled until it blended with the bluish water; the
sunlight caught myriads of facets on the ocean, so the plains of salt
water looked more full of light than the fields of sky.

The brig had set all her canvas. The snowy sails, swelled by the soft
wind, the labyrinth of cordage, and the yellow flags flying at the masthead,
stood out clear against the background of space; there was nothing to
alter the color but the shadow cast by the great sails.

A glorious day, a fair wind, and the fatherland in sight: the scene was
full of harmony. Solitude and bustle, silence and sound, were brought
together; no human voice broke the divine spell.

The Spanish captain, the crew, and the French passengers sat or stood in
a mood of devout ecstasy. There was idleness in the air. The beaming
faces told of forgetfulness of past hardships. Yet, from time to time
the elderly passenger, leaning over the bulwark, looked with uneasiness
at the horizon. Distrust of Fate could be read in his face; he seemed
to fear he would not reach the coast in time. This was the Marquis.
Fortune had not been deaf; after five years of endeavor he was wealthy
again. In his impatience to reach home and to bring good news to his
family, he had embarked with some French merchants on a Spanish vessel.
Now, tired of forebodings, his fancy traced lovely pictures of past
happiness. In that far brown line he seemed to see his wife and children.
He sat in his place by the fireside; they crowded about him; he felt
their caresses. Moïna had grown to be a young girl, beautiful and tall.
The fancied picture grew real, tears filled his eyes, and to hide his
emotion he turned his face to the sea-line.

“There she is again.... She is following us!” he said.

“What?” cried the captain.

“There is a vessel,” muttered the General.

“I saw her yesterday,” answered Captain Gomez. He looked at his
interlocutor as if to ask what he thought; then he added, “She has been
chasing us all along.”

“Then why has she not come up with us, I do not know,” said the General,
“for she is a faster sailor than your damned Saint-Ferdinand.”

“She will have damaged herself, sprung a leak—”

“She is gaining on us!” the General broke in.

“She is a Columbian privateer,” the captain said in his ear, “and we are
still six leagues from land, and the wind is dropping.”

“She is not going ahead, she is flying, as if she knew that in two
hours’ time her prey would escape her. What audacity!”

“Audacity!” cried the captain. “Oh! she is not called the Othello for
nothing. Not so long back she sank a Spanish frigate that carried thirty
guns! This is the one thing I feared.”

The Othello was scarce three leagues away; passengers and crew, attracted
by a sail, came to that side of the vessel. They took the privateer for
a merchantman until a sailor shouted with energy:

“By Saint-James, it is all up with us! Yonder is the Parisian captain!”

At that name dismay and panic spread through the brig. The Spanish
captain’s orders put energy into the crew; he set all studding sails and
crowded every stitch of canvas. But this was not done at once; men did
not work with the unanimity of a man-of-war. The Othello, trimmed by
experts, flew over the water like a swallow; yet she seemed to make so
little headway that the Frenchmen hoped. At last, after unheard-of
efforts, the Saint-Ferdinand sprang forward when the man at the tiller,
steering at random, swung the vessel round. The wind struck athwart the
beam; the sails shivered; the booms were carried away; the vessel was
out of hand. The captain’s face grew white with rage. He sprang upon the
man at the tiller, drove his dagger at him, missed, and hurled the weapon
overboard. Gomez took the helm and strove to right her. Tears of despair
rose; it is harder to lose the fruit of careful plans through treachery
than to face death. But the more he swore, the less the men worked, and
he fired the alarm-gun, hoping to be heard on shore. The privateer,
gaining upon them, replied with a cannon-shot striking the water ten
fathoms away.

“Thunder of heaven!” cried the General, “that was a close shave! They
must have guns made on purpose.”

“Oh! when that one speaks, look you, you must hold your tongue,” said a
sailor. “The Parisian would not be afraid to meet an English man-of-war.”

“It is all over with us,” the captain cried; he had pointed his telescope
landwards and saw no sign from shore. “We are further from the coast
than I thought.”

“Why despair?” asked the General. “All your passengers are Frenchmen;
they have chartered your vessel. The privateer is a Parisian, you say?
Run up the white flag, and—”

“And he would run us down,” retorted the captain. “He can be anything
when he wants to seize rich booty!”

“Oh! if he is a pirate—”

“Pirate!” said the ferocious sailor. “Oh! he always has the law on his
side, or he knows how to be on the same side as the law.”

“Very well,” said the General, “let us make up our minds,” and he kept
back tears.

Hardly had he spoken before a second cannon-shot came crashing through
the hull. “Heave to!” cried the captain gloomily.

A sailor who had commended the Parisian showed skill at working a ship.
The crew waited in agony. The Saint-Ferdinand had four millions of
piastres on board, the whole fortunes of five passengers, and the
General’s eleven hundred thousand francs. At length the Othello lay not
ten gunshots away, so those on the Saint-Ferdinand could look into the
muzzles of her loaded guns. The vessel seemed borne by a devil-sent breeze,
but the expert would have discovered the secret of her speed—the rake of
her stern, her long narrow keel, tall masts, cut of sails, and light
rigging. Everything gave the impression of swift intelligence. The
privateer crew stood silent, ready to shatter the merchantman, which
remained motionless like a schoolboy caught in flagrant delict.

“We have guns on board!” cried the General, clutching the Spanish captain.
But Gomez’s courage was of despair.

“Have we men?” he said.

The Marquis looked round at the crew, and a chill ran through him. There
stood four merchants pale with fear, while the crew gathered about some
of their own who appeared to be arranging to go over to the enemy. They
watched the Othello with greed and curiosity. The captain, the Marquis,
and the mate exchanged glances; they were the only three who thought of
any but themselves.

“Ah! Captain Gomez, when I left my home my heart was half dead with the
bitterness of parting, and now must I bid it good-bye once more when I
bring back happiness for my children?”

The General turned toward the sea, with tears of rage—then saw the
steersman swimming out to the privateer.

“This time it will be good-bye for good,” said the captain, and the
dazed look in the Frenchman startled the Spaniard.

By this time the two vessels were almost alongside, and at sight of the
enemy’s crew the General saw Gomez’s gloomy prophecy was true. The three
men at each gun might have been bronze statues, athletes with rugged
features; their eyes glittered like sparks with infernal glee and courage.
Perfect silence on the upper deck, black with men, bore witness to
rigorous discipline.

The captain of the Othello stood with folded arms at the main mast; an
axe lay on the deck beside him. His face was hidden by a broad hat. The
men looked like dogs crouching before their master. Gunners, soldiers,
and ship’s crew turned their eyes first on his face, then on the merchant
vessel.

The two brigs came up alongside, and the shock roused the privateer
captain from his musings; he spoke a word to the lieutenant.

“Grappling-irons!” shouted the latter, and the Othello grappled the
Saint-Ferdinand with quickness. The captain gave orders in a low voice;
the men, told off in succession, went on the Saint-Ferdinand like
seminarists going to mass. They bound crew and passengers hand and foot
and seized the booty. In a twinkling, provisions and barrels of piastres
were transferred; the General thought he must be dreaming when he,
bound, was flung down on a bale as if part of the cargo.

A brief conference took place between the privateer captain, his
lieutenant, and a sailor who seemed the mate; then the mate gave a
whistle, and the men jumped aboard the Saint-Ferdinand and dismantled
her with the nimble dexterity of soldiers stripping a dead comrade.

“It is all over with us,” said the Spanish captain coolly. He had eyed
the three chiefs and saw the sailors were proceeding to pull his vessel
to pieces.

“Why so?” asked the General.

“What would you have them do with us?” returned the Spaniard. “They have
just decided they will scarcely sell the Saint-Ferdinand in any French
or Spanish port, so they are going to sink her. As for us, do you suppose
they will feed us when they don’t know what port they will put into?”

The words were scarcely out before a hideous outcry rose, followed by a
dull splashing as several bodies were thrown overboard. The four merchants
were gone; eight ferocious gunners still stood with arms raised. He
shuddered.

“What did I tell you?” coolly asked the captain.

The Marquis sprang to his feet. The sea was smooth again; he could not
see where his unhappy fellow-passengers had disappeared.

Only a few paces away, the treacherous steersman and the sailor who
had boasted of the Parisian’s power fraternized with the Othello crew,
pointing out those among their number worthy to join the privateer.
Then the boys tied the rest by the feet. It was soon over; the eight
gunners seized the doomed men and flung them overboard without ado,
watching their contortions and last agony with malignant curiosity. For
them it was ordinary; the older men instead looked with grim smiles at
the casks of piastres about the main mast.

The General and Captain Gomez, seated on a bale, consulted with
hopeless looks; they were, in a sense, the sole survivors, for the seven
men pointed out by the spies were transformed into Peruvians.

“What atrocious villains!” cried the General. Loyal indignation silenced
prudence and pain.

“They do it because they must,” Gomez answered coolly. “If you met one
of those fellows, you would run him through, would you not?”

The lieutenant now came up.

“Captain,” said he, “the Parisian has heard of you. He says you are the
only man who really knows the passages of the Antilles and Brazilian
coast. Will you—”

The captain cut him short with scorn.

“I shall die like a sailor,” he said, “a loyal Spaniard and a Christian.
Do you hear?”

“Heave him overboard!” shouted the lieutenant, and a couple of gunners
seized Gomez.

“You cowards!” roared the General, seizing the men.

“Don’t get too excited, old boy,” said the lieutenant. “If your red
ribbon has made some impression upon our captain, I myself do not care—
you and I will have our talk.”

A smothered sound, with no cry, told the General the gallant captain
had died “like a sailor,” as he had said.

“My money or death!” cried the Marquis in rage.

“Ah! now you talk sensibly!” sneered the lieutenant. “That is the way
to get something—”

Two men bound the Frenchmen’s feet, but with boldness he snatched the
lieutenant’s cutlass and struck like a cavalry officer.

“Brigands that you are! You shall not chuck one of Napoleon’s troopers
over a ship’s side like an oyster!”

At the sound of pistol shots fired point blank at the Frenchman, “the
Parisian” looked round from superintending the transfer of rigging.
He came up behind the brave General, seized him, dragged him to the
side, and was about to fling him over with no more concern than if the
man were a broken spar. They were at the edge when the General looked
into the tawny eyes of the man who had stolen his daughter. The recognition
was mutual.

The captain of the privateer, his arm still upraised, suddenly swung it
and set him down at the main mast. A murmur rose but the captain glanced
round and the deck fell silent.

“This is Hélène’s father,” said the captain in a clear voice. “Woe to any
one who meddles with him!”

A hurrah of joy went up; lads swung aloft in the rigging, caps flew,
gunners pounded, there was an outburst of oaths and shrill cries. The men
cheered like fanatics; the General’s misgiving deepened; he grew uneasy;
there seemed some horrible mystery in such transports.

“My daughter!” he cried. “Where is my daughter?”

The captain gave him a searching glance which threw the bravest man
into confusion. He led the way down a staircase and flung open a cabin
door.

“There she is,” he said, and disappeared, leaving the General in a
stupor.

Hélène cried out and sprang up from the sofa. So changed was she that
none but a father’s eyes would have recognized her. The tropic sun had
brought warmer tones into the once pale face; there was Oriental charm
in the coloring; a certain grandeur about her and a profound sentiment
impressed the coarsest nature. Her long hair fell in large curls about
her throat, adding power to the proud face. The consciousness of that
power shone out in every line. The rose-tinted nostrils dilated with
triumph; the serene happiness of her life left tokens in the full
development of her beauty. A virginal grace met the pride of a woman who
is loved. This was a slave and a queen, a queen who would fain obey that
she might reign.

Her dress was magnificent; India muslin was the sole material, but her
sofa and cushions were of cashmere. A Persian carpet covered the floor,
and her four children played at her feet building castles of gems and
pearl necklaces. The air was full of the scent of rare flowers in Sevres
vases; tiny South American birds hovered among jessamines and camellias.
A pianoforte stood in the room, and on the paneled walls hung pictures
by great painters—Hippolyte Schinner beside a Terburg, a Raphael
Madonna beside a Gericault sketch, while a Gerard Dow eclipsed the
painters of the Empire. On a lacquered table stood a golden plate of
fruit. Indeed, Hélène might have been the sovereign lady of some country,
and this cabin her boudoir where her crowned lover had brought together
earth’s treasure to please his consort. The children gazed with keen eyes
at their grandfather. Accustomed to a life of battle and storm, they
recalled the Roman children in David’s Brutus, watching fighting with
curious interest.

“What! is it possible?” cried Hélène, catching her father’s arm as if
to assure herself this was no vision.

“Hélène!”

“Father!”

They fell into each other’s arms; the old man’s embrace was not so
close as Hélène’s.

“Were you on board that vessel?”

“Yes,” he answered sadly, looking at the little ones gathered about him.

“I was about to perish, but—”

“But for my husband,” she broke in. “I see how it was.”

“Ah!” cried the General, “why must I find you again like this, Hélène?
After all the many tears, must I still groan for your fate?”

“And why?” she asked, smiling. “Why should you be sorry to learn I am the
happiest woman under the sun?”

“Happy?” he cried with surprise.

“Yes, happy, my kind father,” and she caught his hands, covered them
with kisses, and pressed them to her heart. Her caresses and the carriage
of her head were plain tokens in the joy sparkling in her eyes.

“And how is this?” he asked, bewildered.

“Listen, father; I have for lover, husband, servant, and master one
whose soul is as great as the boundless sea, as infinite in kindness as
heaven, a god on earth! Never during these seven years has a chance look
or word jarred in the divine harmony of his talk, his love, his caresses.
His eyes have never met mine without a gleam of happiness; there has
always been a bright smile; on deck his voice rises above thunder; but
here below it is soft and melodious—as Rossini’s music—for he has
Rossini’s music sent for me. I have everything a woman can imagine. My
wishes are more than fulfilled. I am a queen on the seas; I am obeyed—
he is never more than a few paces from me—he cannot wander further than
from stern to prow,” she added with mischief. “Seven years! A love that
outlasts seven years of continual joy—oh no! it is something better.”
A torrent of tears fell from her burning eyes. The children cried and
flocked about their mother. The oldest boy struck the General with a
threatening look.

“Abel, darling,” said Hélène, “I am crying for joy.”

She took him on her knee; the child fondled her, putting his arms about
her neck like a lion’s whelp with a lioness.

“Do you never weary of your life?” asked the General, bewildered.

“Yes,” she said, “sometimes, when we are on land; yet even then I never
part from my husband.”

“But you need music and balls and fetes.”

“His voice is music for me; for fêtes I devise toilettes for him. When
he likes my dress, it is as if all the world admired me. Simply for that
I keep the diamonds and jewels, the flowers and masterpieces he heaps on
me—he says, ‘Hélène, as you live out of the world, I will have the world
come to you.’ But for that I would fling them all overboard.”

“But there are others on board, wild, reckless men whose passions—”

“I understand, father. Do not fear for me. Never was empress encompassed
with more observance. The men are very superstitious; they look upon me
as a tutelary genius, the luck of the vessel. But he is their god; they
worship him. Once only one crewman showed disrespect in words,” she
added laughing; “but before Victor knew it the others flung the offender
overboard, although I forgave him. They love me as their good angel; I
nurse them when ill; several times I have been fortunate enough to save
a life. Poor fellows, they are giants, but children at the same time.”

“And when there is fighting overhead?”

“I am used to it now; I quaked at the first engagement, but never
since.—I am used to peril, and—I am your daughter,” she said; “I love
it.”

“But how if he should fall?”

“I should die with him.”

“And your children?”

“They are children of the sea and danger; they share the life of their
parents. We have but one life, our names written on the same page of
Fate, one skiff bears us and our fortunes, and we know it.”

“Do you so love him that he is more to you than all beside?”

“All beside?” echoed she. “Let us leave that mystery. Yet stay! this
little one—well, this too is he,” and straining Abel she kissed him.

“But I can never forget he has just drowned nine men!” muttered the
General.

“There was no help for it, doubtless,” she said, “for he is generous and
humane. He sheds as little blood as may be, and only in the interests of
the little world he defends and the sacred cause for which he is
fighting. Talk to him about anything that seems wrong and he will
convince you.”

“There was that crime of his,” muttered the General.

“But how if that crime was a virtue?” she asked with dignity. “How if
man’s justice failed to avenge a great wrong?”

“But a private revenge!” exclaimed her father.

“But what is hell,” she cried, “but eternal revenge for a wrong done
in a day?”

“Ah! you are lost! He has bewitched you. You are talking wildly.”

“Stay with us one day, father, and if you listen to him and see him you
will love him.”

“Helène, France lies only a few leagues away,” he said gravely.

Hélène trembled; then she went to the porthole and pointed to the
savannas of green water spreading far and wide.

“There lies my country,” she said, tapping the carpet.

“But are you not coming with me to see your mother and sister and
brothers?”

“Oh! yes,” she cried with tears, “if he is willing, if he will come
with me.”

“So,” the General said sternly, “you have neither country nor kin now,
Hélène?”

“I am his wife,” she answered proudly. There was something noble in her
tone. “This is the first happiness in seven years that has not come to
me through him,” she said—then, kissing her father’s hand—“and this
is the first word of reproach I have heard.”

“And your conscience?”

“My conscience; he is my conscience!” she cried, trembling. “Here he
is! Even in the thick of a fight I can tell his footstep among all the
others on deck,” she cried.

A crimson flushed her cheeks, her eyes lighted up; there was joy and
love in every fibre. That soft quiver softened the General.

It was as she had said. The captain came in, sat in an easy-chair, took
up his oldest boy, and began to play. There was a moment’s silence;
the cabin and playing children seemed like a nest of halcyons floating
on the waves between sky and sea, safe in protection of this man who
steered among perils. He gazed admiringly at Hélène—a dreamlike vision
of a sea goddess, gracious and rich in happiness; all the treasures
about her grew poor beside her nature.

The captain broke the silence.

“Am I in the way?” he asked his wife.

“No,” said the General. “Hélène has told me all. I see she is lost to
us—”

“No,” the captain put in quickly; “in a few years the statute of
limitations will allow me to go back to France. When the conscience is
clear, and a man has broken the law in obedience to—” he stopped, as if
scorning to justify himself.

“How can you commit new murders without remorse?”

“We had no provisions,” the privateer retorted calmly.

“But if you had set the men ashore—”

“They would have given the alarm and sent a man-of-war after us, and we
should never have seen Chili again.”

“Before France would have warned the Spanish admiralty—” began the
General.

“But France might take it amiss that a man, with a warrant out,
should seize a brig chartered by Bordeaux merchants. And for that matter,
have you never fired a shot or two too many in battle?”

The General shrank under the other’s eyes. He said no more, and his
daughter looked at him half sadly, half triumphant.

“General,” the privateer continued, “I have made it a rule to abstract
nothing from booty. But even so, my share will be larger than your
fortune. Permit me to return it to you in another form—”

He drew a pile of banknotes from the piano, and without counting handed
a million francs to the Marquis.

“You can understand,” he said, “that I cannot spend time watching
vessels pass to Bordeaux. So unless the dangers of our Bohemian life
attract you, unless you care to see South America and the tropics, and
a bit of fighting for the pleasure of helping win a triumph for a young
nation, or for the name of Simon Bolivar, we must part. The long boat
manned with a trustworthy crew is ready for you. And now let us hope our
third meeting will be completely happy.”

“Victor,” said Hélène, “I should like to see a little more of my father.”

“Ten minutes may bring up a French frigate. However, so be it, we shall
have a little fun. The men find things dull.”

“Oh, father, go!” cried Hélène, “and take these keepsakes to my sister
and brothers and—mother,” she added. She caught up a handful of jewels,
folded them in an Indian shawl, and timidly held it out.

“But what shall I say to them from you?” he asked. Her hesitation on the
word “mother” struck him.

“Oh! can you doubt me? I pray for their happiness every day.”

“Hélène,” he began, watching her closely, “how if we should not meet
again? Shall I never know why you left us?”

“That secret is not mine,” she answered gravely. “Even if I had the right
to tell it, perhaps I should not. For ten years I was more miserable
than words can say—”

She broke off and gave the presents. The General, softened by a soldier’s
ease about booty, took Hélène’s gifts and comforted himself with the
reflection that the Parisian captain would wage war against the Spaniards
honorably, under Hélène’s pure nature. His passion for courage carried
all before it. He shook hands cordially with his captor, kissed Hélène
with a soldier’s expansiveness, letting a tear fall on her proud face.
The Parisian, moved, brought the children for his blessing. The parting
was over; the last goodbye was a long farewell look, with tender regret.

A strange sight to seaward met the General’s eyes. The Saint-Ferdinand
was blazing like a bonfire. The men setting out to sink the Spanish brig
had found a cargo of rum and, as the Othello was already supplied, had
lighted a floating bowl in jest. As the General went over the side into
the long-boat manned by six rowers, he looked at the burning vessel and
at the daughter who stood by her husband on the Othello’s stern. He saw
Hélène’s white dress flutter like another sail; he saw the tall figure
against a sea background, queenly even before Ocean; memories crowded
his mind, and with a soldier’s recklessness he forgot he was borne over
the grave of brave Gomez.

A vast column of smoke spread like a brown cloud, pierced by shafts of
sunlight. It was a second sky reflecting the flames; the ordinary blue
soared above. The smoke’s strange hues blurred and blended, shrouding
the burning vessel. The hissing flames licked the rigging; the burning
rum sent up blue lights. But in overpowering sunlight the colors were
scarcely visible.

The Othello used the little wind to fly on her course. Swaying like a
stag beetle on the wing, the vessel beat to windward on a zigzag flight
south. Sometimes hidden by the smoke, she then shot clear; Hélène,
catching sight of her father, waved one more farewell.

A few minutes, and the Saint-Ferdinand went down with a bubbling turmoil.
Nothing was left but a smoke cloud. The Othello was far; the long-boat
neared land; through a break in the smoke the General caught the last
glimpse of Hélène. Her dress and handkerchief stood out against murk.
Then she was an imperceptible speck, a faint line, an angel, a memory.

The Marquis had retrieved his fortunes when he died, worn out with toil.
A few months after his death, in 1833, the Marquise was obliged to take
Moïna to a watering-place in the Pyrenees, for the child wished to see
the mountains. They left the baths, and on their way home the following
tragical incident occurred.

“Dear me, mother,” said Moïna, “it was very foolish not to stay among the
mountains a few days longer. It was nicer there. Did you hear that horrid
child moaning all night, and that wretched woman gabbling in patois? I
could not understand a word. What kind of people in the next room? This
was one of the horridest nights.”

“I heard nothing,” said the Marquise, “but I will see the landlady, dear,
and engage the next room, and then we shall have the suite to ourselves.
How do you feel this morning? Are you tired?”

As she spoke the Marquise rose and went to Moïna’s bedside.

“Let us see,” she said, feeling for the girl’s hand.

“Oh! let me alone, mother,” said Moïna; “your fingers are cold.”

She turned her head on the pillow, pettishly, but with engaging grace.
Just then a wailing cry echoed through the next room, a faint prolonged
cry that would go to any woman’s heart.

“Why, if you heard that all night, why did you not wake me? We should
have—”

A deeper moan interrupted the Marquise.

“Some one is dying there,” she cried, and hurried out.

“Send Pauline to me!” called Moïna. “I shall get up and dress.”

The Marquise hurried downstairs and found the landlady in the courtyard
with a little group about her.

“Madame, you have put some one in the next room who seems very ill—”

“Oh! don’t talk to me!” cried the mistress. “I have just sent for the
mayor. Imagine: a woman, a poor unfortunate, came last night on foot from
Spain with her baby on her back; the child was dying. I could not refuse.
I saw her this morning; she looked dreadfully. ‘Madame,’ she said, pulling
a gold ring from her finger, ‘this is all I have; take it in payment; I
shall not stay long. Poor little one! we shall die together soon!’ she
said, looking at the child. I took her ring. I asked her who she was;
she never told me her name.... I have sent for the doctor and M. le Maire.”

“Why, you must do all you can,” cried the Marquise. “Good heavens! perhaps
it is not too late! I will pay for everything necessary—”

“Ah! my lady, she looks uncommonly proud, and I don’t know she would
allow it.”

“I will go to see her at once.”

The Marquise went up to the stranger’s room, not thinking of the shock
widow’s weeds might give a dying woman. At sight of that suffering
woman the Marquise turned pale. In spite of the changes wrought by
suffering, she recognized her eldest daughter.

But Hélène, when she saw a woman dressed in black, sat upright with a
shriek of horror. Then she sank back; she knew her mother.

“My daughter,” said Mme. d’Aiglemont, “what is to be done? Pauline!...
Moïna!...”

“Nothing now for me,” said Hélène faintly. “I had hoped to see my father
once more, but your mourning—” she broke off, clutched her child to her
heart as if to give it warmth, and kissed its forehead. Then she turned
her eyes on her mother, and the Marquise met the old reproach in them,
tempered with forgiveness, but still reproach. She saw it, and would not
see it. She forgot that Hélène was the child conceived amid tears and
despair, the child of duty, the cause of one of her greatest sorrows.
She stole to her eldest’s side, remembering only that Hélène was her
firstborn who taught her motherhood. The mother’s eyes filled with tears.
“Hélène, my child!...” she cried, with her arms about her.

Hélène was silent. Her own babe had just drawn its last breath on her
breast.

Moïna came into the room with Pauline, the landlady and the doctor. The
Marquise held her daughter’s ice-cold hand and gazed at her in despair;
but the widowed woman who had escaped shipwreck with but one of her
children spoke in a voice dreadful to hear. “All this is your work,” she
said. “If you had been for me all that—”

“Moïna, go! Go out of the room, all of you!” cried Mme. d’Aiglemont,
her shrill tones drowning Hélène’s voice.—“For pity’s sake,” she
continued, “let us not begin these miserable quarrels again now—”

“I will be silent,” Hélène answered with a preternatural effort. “I am
a mother; I know Moïna ought not... Where is my child?”

Moïna came back, impelled by curiosity.

“Sister,” said the spoiled child, “the doctor—”

“It is all of no use,” said Hélène. “Oh! why did I not die as a girl of
sixteen when I meant to take my life? There is no happiness outside the
laws. Moïna... you...”

Her head sank till her face lay against the little one; in her agony she
strained her babe to her breast, and died.

“Your sister, Moïna,” said Mme. d’Aiglemont, bursting into tears when
she reached her room, “your sister meant no doubt to tell you that a girl
will never find happiness in a romantic life, in living as nobody else
does, and, above all, far away from her mother.”

VI. THE OLD AGE OF A GUILTY MOTHER

It was one of the earliest June days of the year 1844. A lady of fifty
or thereabouts, for she looked older than her actual age, was pacing up
and down one of the sunny paths in the garden of a great mansion in
the Rue Plumet in Paris. It was noon. The lady took two or three turns
along the gently winding garden walk, careful never to lose sight of a
certain row of windows, to which she seemed to give her whole attention;
then she sat down on a bench, a piece of elegant semi-rusticity made of
branches with the bark left on the wood. From the place where she sat
she could look through the garden railings along the inner boulevards to
the dome of the Invalides rising above the crests of a forest of
elm-trees, and see the less striking view of her own grounds ending in
the gray stone front of one of the finest hotels in the Faubourg
Saint-Germain.

Silence lay over the neighboring gardens, and the boulevards stretching
away to the Invalides. Day scarcely begins at noon in that aristocratic
quarter, and masters and servants are all alike asleep, or just
awakening, unless some young lady takes it into her head to go for an
early ride, or a gray-headed diplomatist rises betimes to redraft a
protocol.

The elderly lady stirring abroad at that hour was the Marquise
d’Aiglemont, the mother of Mme. de Saint-Héreen, to whom the great house
belonged. The Marquise had made over the mansion and almost her whole
fortune to her daughter, reserving only an annuity for herself.

The Comtesse Moïna de Saint-Héreen was Mme. d’Aiglemont’s youngest
child. The Marquise had made every sacrifice to marry her daughter to
the eldest son of one of the greatest houses of France; and this was
only what might have been expected, for the lady had lost her sons,
first one and then the other. Gustave, Marquis d’Aiglemont, had died of
the cholera; Abel, the second, had fallen in Algeria. Gustave had left
a widow and children, but the dowager’s affection for her sons had been
only moderately warm, and for the next generation it was decidedly
tepid. She was always civil to her daughter-in-law, but her feeling
towards the young Marquise was the distinctly conventional affection
which good taste and good manners require us to feel for our relatives.
The fortunes of her dead children having been settled, she could devote
her savings and her own property to her darling Moïna.

Moïna, beautiful and fascinating from childhood, was Mme. d’Aiglemont’s
favorite; loved beyond all the others with an instinctive, involuntary
love, a fatal drawing of the heart, which sometimes seems inexplicable,
sometimes, and to a close observer, only too easy to explain. Her
darling’s pretty face, the sound of Moïna’s voice, her ways, her manner,
her looks and gestures, roused all the deepest emotions that can stir
a mother’s heart with trouble, rapture, or delight. The springs of the
Marquise’s life, of yesterday, to-morrow, and to-day, lay in that young
heart. Moïna, with better fortune, had survived four older children.
As a matter of fact, Mme. d’Aiglemont had lost her eldest daughter, a
charming girl, in a most unfortunate manner, said gossip; nobody knew
exactly what became of her; and then she lost a little boy of five by a
dreadful accident.

The child of her affections had, however, been spared to her, and
doubtless the Marquise saw the will of Heaven in that fact; for those
who had died, she kept but very shadowy recollections in some far-off
corner of her heart; her memories of her dead children were like the
headstones on a battlefield, scarcely seen for the flowers that have
sprung up about them since. Of course, if the world had chosen, it might
have said some hard truths about the Marquise, might have taken her to
task for shallowness and an overweening preference for one child at the
expense of the rest; but the world of Paris is swept along by the full
flood of new events, ideas, and fashions, and it was inevitable the Mme.
d’Aiglemont should in some sort be allowed to drop out of sight. So
nobody thought of blaming her for coldness or neglect which concerned no
one, whereas her quick, apprehensive tenderness for Moïna was found
interesting by not a few who respected it as a sort of superstition.
Besides, the Marquise scarcely went into society at all; and the few
families who knew her thought of her as a kindly, gentle, indulgent
woman, wholly devoted to her family. What curiosity, keen indeed,
would seek to pry beneath the surface with which the world is quite
satisfied? And what would we not pardon to old people, if only they
will efface themselves like shadows, and consent to be regarded as
memories and nothing more!

Indeed, Mme. d’Aiglemont became a kind of example complacently held up
by the younger generation to fathers of families, and frequently cited
to mothers-in-law. She had made over her property to Moïna in her own
lifetime; the young Countess’ happiness was enough for her, she only
lived in her daughter. If some cautious old person or morose uncle here
and there condemned the course with--“Perhaps Mme. d’Aiglemont may be
sorry some day that she gave up her fortune to her daughter; she may
be sure of Moïna, but how can she be equally sure of her
son-in-law?”--these prophets were cried down on all sides, and from all
sides a chorus of praise went up for Moïna.

“It ought to be said, in justice to Mme. de Saint-Héreen, that her
mother cannot feel the slightest difference,” remarked a young married
woman. “Mme. d’Aiglemont is admirably well housed. She has a carriage at
her disposal, and can go everywhere just as she used to do--”

“Except to the Italiens,” remarked a low voice. (This was an elderly
parasite, one of those persons who show their independence--as they
think--by riddling their friends with epigrams.) “Except to the
Italiens. And if the dowager cares for anything on this earth but her
daughter--it is music. Such a good performer she was in her time! But
the Countess’ box is always full of young butterflies, and the Countess’
mother would be in the way; the young lady is talked about already as a
great flirt. So the poor mother never goes to the Italiens.”

“Mme. de Saint-Héreen has delightful ‘At Homes’ for her mother,” said a
rosebud. “All Paris goes to her salon.”

“And no one pays any attention to the Marquise,” returned the parasite.

“The fact is that Mme. d’Aiglemont is never alone,” remarked a coxcomb,
siding with the young women.

“In the morning,” the old observer continued in a discreet voice, “in
the morning dear Moïna is asleep. At four o’clock dear Moïna drives
in the Bois. In the evening dear Moïna goes to a ball or to the
Bouffes.--Still, it is certainly true that Mme. d’Aiglemont has the
privilege of seeing her dear daughter while she dresses, and again at
dinner, if dear Moïna happens to dine with her mother. Not a week ago,
sir,” continued the elderly person, laying his hand on the arm of the
shy tutor, a new arrival in the house, “not a week ago, I saw the poor
mother, solitary and sad, by her own fireside.--‘What is the matter?’ I
asked. The Marquise looked up smiling, but I am quite sure that she had
been crying.--‘I was thinking that it is a strange thing that I should
be left alone when I have had five children,’ she said, ‘but that is
our destiny! And besides, I am happy when I know that Moïna is enjoying
herself.’--She could say that to me, for I knew her husband when he was
alive. A poor stick he was, and uncommonly lucky to have such a wife; it
was certainly owing to her that he was made a peer of France, and had a
place at Court under Charles X.”

Yet such mistaken ideas get about in social gossip, and such mischief
is done by it, that the historian of manners is bound to exercise his
discretion, and weigh the assertions so recklessly made. After all, who
is to say that either mother or daughter was right or wrong? There is
but One who can read and judge their hearts! And how often does He wreak
His vengeance in the family circle, using throughout all time children
as His instruments against their mothers, and fathers against their
sons, raising up peoples against kings, and princes against peoples,
sowing strife and division everywhere? And in the world of ideas, are
not opinions and feelings expelled by new feelings and opinions, much
as withered leaves are thrust forth by the young leaf-buds in the
spring?--all in obedience to the immutable Scheme; all to some end which
God alone knows. Yet, surely, all things proceed to Him, or rather, to
Him all things return.

Such thoughts of religion, the natural thoughts of age, floated up
now and again on the current of Mme. d’Aiglemont’s thoughts; they were
always dimly present in her mind, but sometimes they shone out clearly,
sometimes they were carried under, like flowers tossed on the vexed
surface of a stormy sea.

She sat on a garden-seat, tired with walking, exhausted with much
thinking--with the long thoughts in which a whole lifetime rises up
before the mind, and is spread out like a scroll before the eyes of
those who feel that Death is near.

If a poet had chanced to pass along the boulevard, he would have found
an interesting picture in the face of this woman, grown old before her
time. As she sat under the dotted shadow of the acacia, the shadow the
acacia casts at noon, a thousand thoughts were written for all the world
to see on her features, pale and cold even in the hot, bright sunlight.
There was something sadder than the sense of waning life in that
expressive face, some trouble that went deeper than the weariness of
experience. It was a face of a type that fixes you in a moment among a
host of characterless faces that fail to draw a second glance, a face
to set you thinking. Among a thousand pictures in a gallery, you are
strongly impressed by the sublime anguish on the face of some Madonna
of Murillo’s; by some Beatrice Cenci in which Guido’s art portrays the
most touching innocence against a background of horror and crime; by the
awe and majesty that should encircle a king, caught once and for ever
by Velasquez in the sombre face of a Philip II., and so is it with some
living human faces; they are tyrannous pictures which speak to you,
submit you to searching scrutiny, and give response to your inmost
thoughts, nay, there are faces that set forth a whole drama, and Mme.
d’Aiglemont’s stony face was one of these awful tragedies, one of such
faces as Dante Alighieri saw by thousands in his vision.

For the little season that a woman’s beauty is in flower it serves her
admirably well in the dissimulation to which her natural weakness and
our social laws condemn her. A young face and rich color, and eyes that
glow with light, a gracious maze of such subtle, manifold lines and
curves, flawless and perfectly traced, is a screen that hides everything
that stirs the woman within. A flush tells nothing, it only heightens
the coloring so brilliant already; all the fires that burn within
can add little light to the flame of life in eyes which only seem the
brighter for the flash of a passing pain. Nothing is so discreet as a
young face, for nothing is less mobile; it has the serenity, the surface
smoothness, and the freshness of a lake. There is not character in
women’s faces before the age of thirty. The painter discovers nothing
there but pink and white, and the smile and expression that repeat the
same thought in the same way--a thought of youth and love that goes no
further than youth and love. But the face of an old woman has expressed
all that lay in her nature; passion has carved lines on her features;
love and wifehood and motherhood, and extremes of joy and anguish,
having wrung them, and left their traces in a thousand wrinkles, all
of which speak a language of their own; then it is that a woman’s face
becomes sublime in its horror, beautiful in its melancholy, grand in its
calm. If it is permissible to carry the strange metaphor still further,
it might be said that in the dried-up lake you can see the traces of
all the torrents that once poured into it and made it what it is. An old
face is nothing to the frivolous world; the frivolous world is shocked
by the sight of the destruction of such comeliness as it can understand;
a commonplace artist sees nothing there. An old face is the province of
the poets among poets of those who can recognize that something which
is called Beauty, apart from all the conventions underlying so many
superstitions in art and taste.

Though Mme. d’Aiglemont wore a fashionable bonnet, it was easy to see
that her once black hair had been bleached by cruel sorrows; yet her
good taste and the gracious acquired instincts of a woman of fashion
could be seen in the way she wore it, divided into two bandeaux,
following the outlines of a forehead that still retained some traces of
former dazzling beauty, worn and lined though it was. The contours
of her face, the regularity of her features, gave some faint idea of
that beauty of which surely she had once been proud; but those traces
spoke still more plainly of the anguish which had laid it waste,
of sharp pain that had withered the temples, and made those hollows in
her cheeks, and empurpled the eyelids, and robbed them of their lashes,
and the eyes of their charm. She was in every way so noiseless; she
moved with a slow, self-contained gravity that showed itself in her
whole bearing, and struck a certain awe into others. Her diffident
manner had changed to positive shyness, due apparently to a habit now of
some years’ growth, of effacing herself in her daughter’s presence. She
spoke very seldom, and in the low tones used by those who perforce must
live within themselves a life of reflection and concentration. This
demeanor led others to regard her with an indefinable feeling which was
neither awe nor compassion, but a mysterious blending of the many ideas
awakened in us by compassion and awe. Finally, there was something in
her wrinkles, in the lines of her face, in the look of pain in those
wan eyes of hers, that bore eloquent testimony to tears that never had
fallen, tears that had been absorbed by her heart. Unhappy creatures,
accustomed to raise their eyes to heaven, in mute appeal against the
bitterness of their lot, would have seen at once from her eyes that she
was broken in to the cruel discipline of ceaseless prayer, would have
discerned the almost imperceptible symptoms of the secret bruises which
destroy all the flowers of the soul, even the sentiment of motherhood.

Painters have colors for these portraits, but words, and the mental
images called up by words, fail to reproduce such impressions
faithfully; there are mysterious signs and tokens in the tones of the
coloring and in the look of human faces, which the mind only seizes
through the sense of sight; and the poet is fain to record the tale
of the events which wrought the havoc to make their terrible ravages
understood.

The face spoke of cold and steady storm, an inward conflict between a
mother’s long-suffering and the limitations of our nature, for our human
affections are bounded by our humanity, and the infinite has no place
in finite creatures. Sorrow endured in silence had at last produced an
indefinable morbid something in this woman. Doubtless mental anguish had
reacted on the physical frame, and some disease, perhaps an aneurism,
was undermining Julie’s life. Deep-seated grief lies to all appearance
very quietly in the depths where it is conceived, yet, so still and
apparently dormant as it is, it ceaselessly corrodes the soul, like the
terrible acid which eats away crystal.

Two tears made their way down the Marquise’s cheeks; she rose to her
feet as if some thought more poignant than any that preceded it had cut
her to the quick. She had doubtless come to a conclusion as to Moïna’s
future; and now, foreseeing clearly all the troubles in store for her
child, the sorrows of her own unhappy life had begun to weigh once
more upon her. The key of her position must be sought in her daughter’s
situation.

The Comte de Saint-Héreen had been away for nearly six months on a
political mission. The Countess, whether from sheer giddiness, or in
obedience to the countless instincts of woman’s coquetry, or to essay
its power--with all the vanity of a frivolous fine lady, all the
capricious waywardness of a child--was amusing herself, during her
husband’s absence, by playing with the passion of a clever but heartless
man, distracted (so he said) with love, the love that combines readily
with every petty social ambition of a self-conceited coxcomb. Mme.
d’Aiglemont, whose long experience had given her a knowledge of life,
and taught her to judge of men and to dread the world, watched the
course of this flirtation, and saw that it could only end in one way,
if her daughter should fall into the hands of an utterly unscrupulous
intriguer. How could it be other than a terrible thought for her that
her daughter listened willingly to this roue? Her darling stood on the
brink of a precipice, she felt horribly sure of it, yet dared not hold
her back. She was afraid of the Countess. She knew too that Moïna would
not listen to her wise warnings; she knew that she had no influence
over that nature--iron for her, silken-soft for all others. Her mother’s
tenderness might have led her to sympathize with the troubles of a
passion called forth by the nobler qualities of a lover, but this was
no passion--it was coquetry, and the Marquise despised Alfred de
Vandenesse, knowing that he had entered upon this flirtation with Moïna
as if it were a game of chess.

But if Alfred de Vandenesse made her shudder with disgust, she was
obliged--unhappy mother!--to conceal the strongest reason for her
loathing in the deepest recesses of her heart. She was on terms of
intimate friendship with the Marquis de Vandenesse, the young man’s
father; and this friendship, a respectable one in the eyes of the world,
excused the son’s constant presence in the house, he professing an old
attachment, dating from childhood, for Mme. de Saint-Héreen. More than
this, in vain did Mme. d’Aiglemont nerve herself to come between Moïna
and Alfred de Vandenesse with a terrible word, knowing beforehand that
she should not succeed; knowing that the strong reason which ought to
separate them would carry no weight; that she should humiliate herself
vainly in her daughter’s eyes. Alfred was too corrupt; Moïna too clever
to believe the revelation; the young Countess would turn it off and
treat it as a piece of maternal strategy. Mme. d’Aiglemont had built
her prison walls with her own hands; she had immured herself only to
see Moïna’s happiness ruined thence before she died; she was to look on
helplessly at the ruin of the young life which had been her pride and
joy and comfort, a life a thousand times dearer to her than her
own. What words can describe anguish so hideous beyond belief, such
unfathomed depths of pain?

She waited for Moïna to rise, with the impatience and sickening dread
of a doomed man, who longs to have done with life, and turns cold at the
thought of the headsman. She had braced herself for a last effort, but
perhaps the prospect of the certain failure of the attempt was less
dreadful to her than the fear of receiving yet again one of those
thrusts that went to her very heart--before that fear her courage ebbed
away. Her mother’s love had come to this. To love her child, to be
afraid of her, to shrink from the thought of the stab, yet to go
forward. So great is a mother’s affection in a loving nature, that
before it can fade away into indifference the mother herself must die
or find support in some great power without her, in religion or another
love. Since the Marquise rose that morning, her fatal memory had called
up before her some of those things, so slight to all appearance, that
make landmarks in a life. Sometimes, indeed, a whole tragedy grows out
of a single gesture; the tone in which a few words were spoken rends a
whole life in two; a glance into indifferent eyes is the deathblow of
the gladdest love; and, unhappily, such gestures and such words were
only too familiar to Mme. d’Aiglemont--she had met so many glances that
wound the soul. No, there was nothing in those memories to bid her hope.
On the contrary, everything went to show that Alfred had destroyed her
hold on her daughter’s heart, that the thought of her was now associated
with duty--not with gladness. In ways innumerable, in things that were
mere trifles in themselves, the Countess’ detestable conduct rose
up before her mother; and the Marquise, it may be, looked on Moïna’s
undutifulness as a punishment, and found excuses for her daughter in the
will of Heaven, that so she still might adore the hand that smote her.

All these things passed through her memory that morning, and each
recollection wounded her afresh so sorely, that with a very little
additional pain her brimming cup of bitterness must have overflowed. A
cold look might kill her.

The little details of domestic life are difficult to paint; but one or
two perhaps will suffice to give an idea of the rest.

The Marquise d’Aiglemont, for instance, had grown rather deaf, but she
could never induce Moïna to raise her voice for her. Once, with the
naivete of suffering, she had begged Moïna to repeat some remark which
she had failed to catch, and Moïna obeyed, but with so bad a grace, that
Mme. d’Aiglemont had never permitted herself to make her modest request
again. Ever since that day when Moïna was talking or retailing a
piece of news, her mother was careful to come near to listen; but this
infirmity of deafness appeared to put the Countess out of patience,
and she would grumble thoughtlessly about it. This instance is one
from among very many that must have gone to the mother’s heart; and yet
nearly all of them might have escaped a close observer, they consisted
in faint shades of manner invisible to any but a woman’s eyes. Take
another example. Mme. d’Aiglemont happened to say one day that the
Princesse de Cadignan had called upon her. “Did she come to see you!”
Moïna exclaimed. That was all, but the Countess’ voice and manner
expressed surprise and well-bred contempt in semitones. Any heart,
still young and sensitive, might well have applauded the philanthropy of
savage tribes who kill off their old people when they grow too feeble
to cling to a strongly shaken bough. Mme. d’Aiglemont rose smiling, and
went away to weep alone.

Well-bred people, and women especially, only betray their feelings
by imperceptible touches; but those who can look back over their own
experience on such bruises as this mother’s heart received, know also
how the heart-strings vibrate to these light touches. Overcome by her
memories, Mme. d’Aiglemont recollected one of those microscopically
small things, so stinging and so painful was it that never till this
moment had she felt all the heartless contempt that lurked beneath
smiles.

At the sound of shutters thrown back at her daughter’s windows, she
dried her tears, and hastened up the pathway by the railings. As she
went, it struck her that the gardener had been unusually careful to rake
the sand along the walk which had been neglected for some little time.
As she stood under her daughter’s windows, the shutters were hastily
closed.

“Moïna, is it you?” she asked.

No answer.

The Marquise went on into the house.

“Mme. la Comtesse is in the little drawing-room,” said the maid, when
the Marquise asked whether Mme. de Saint-Héreen had finished dressing.

Mme. d’Aiglemont hurried to the little drawing-room; her heart was too
full, her brain too busy to notice matters so slight; but there on the
sofa sat the Countess in her loose morning-gown, her hair in disorder
under the cap tossed carelessly on her head, her feet thrust into
slippers. The key of her bedroom hung at her girdle. Her face, aglow
with color, bore traces of almost stormy thought.

“What makes people come in!” she cried, crossly. “Oh! it is you,
mother,” she interrupted herself, with a preoccupied look.

“Yes, child; it is your mother----”

Something in her tone turned those words into an outpouring of the
heart, the cry of some deep inward feeling, only to be described by the
word “holy.” So thoroughly in truth had she rehabilitated the sacred
character of a mother, that her daughter was impressed, and turned
towards her, with something of awe, uneasiness, and remorse in her
manner. The room was the furthest of a suite, and safe from indiscreet
intrusion, for no one could enter it without giving warning of approach
through the previous apartments. The Marquise closed the door.

“It is my duty, my child, to warn you in one of the most serious crises
in the lives of us women; you have perhaps reached it unconsciously, and
I am come to speak to you as a friend rather than as a mother. When you
married, you acquired freedom of action; you are only accountable to
your husband now; but I asserted my authority so little (perhaps I was
wrong), that I think I have a right to expect you to listen to me, for
once at least, in a critical position when you must need counsel. Bear
in mind, Moïna that you are married to a man of high ability, a man of
whom you may well be proud, a man who--”

“I know what you are going to say, mother!” Moïna broke in pettishly. “I
am to be lectured about Alfred--”

“Moïna,” the Marquise said gravely, as she struggled with her tears,
“you would not guess at once if you did not feel--”

“What?” asked Moïna, almost haughtily. “Why, really, mother--”

Mme. d’Aiglemont summoned up all her strength. “Moïna,” she said, “you
must attend carefully to this that I ought to tell you--”

“I am attending,” returned the Countess, folding her arms, and affecting
insolent submission. “Permit me, mother, to ring for Pauline,” she added
with incredible self-possession; “I will send her away first.”

She rang the bell.

“My dear child, Pauline cannot possibly hear--”

“Mamma,” interrupted the Countess, with a gravity which must have struck
her mother as something unusual, “I must--”

She stopped short, for the woman was in the room.

“Pauline, go yourself to Baudran’s, and ask why my hat has not yet
been sent.”

Then the Countess reseated herself and scrutinized her mother. The
Marquise, with a swelling heart and dry eyes, in painful agitation,
which none but a mother can fully understand, began to open Moïna’s eyes
to the risk that she was running. But either the Countess felt hurt
and indignant at her mother’s suspicions of a son of the Marquis de
Vandenesse, or she was seized with a sudden fit of inexplicable levity
caused by the inexperience of youth. She took advantage of a pause.

“Mamma, I thought you were only jealous of the father--” she said,
with a forced laugh.

Mme. d’Aiglemont shut her eyes and bent her head at the words, with a
very faint, almost inaudible sigh. She looked up and out into space,
as if she felt the common overmastering impulse to appeal to God at the
great crises of our lives; then she looked at her daughter, and her eyes
were full of awful majesty and the expression of profound sorrow.

“My child,” she said, and her voice was hardly recognizable, “you have
been less merciful to your mother than he against whom she sinned; less
merciful than perhaps God Himself will be!”

Mme. d’Aiglemont rose; at the door she turned; but she saw nothing but
surprise in her daughter’s face. She went out. Scarcely had she reached
the garden when her strength failed her. There was a violent pain at her
heart, and she sank down on a bench. As her eyes wandered over the path,
she saw fresh marks on the path, a man’s footprints were distinctly
recognizable. It was too late, then, beyond a doubt. Now she began to
understand the reason for that order given to Pauline, and with these
torturing thoughts came a revelation more hateful than any that had
gone before it. She drew her own inferences--the son of the Marquis
de Vandenesse had destroyed all feeling of respect for her in her
daughter’s mind. The physical pain grew worse; by degrees she lost
consciousness, and sat like one asleep upon the garden-seat.

The Countess de Saint-Héreen, left to herself, thought that her mother
had given her a somewhat shrewd home-thrust, but a kiss and a few
attentions that evening would make all right again.

A shrill cry came from the garden. She leaned carelessly out, as
Pauline, not yet departed on her errand, called out for help, holding
the Marquise in her arms.

“Do not frighten my daughter!” those were the last words the mother
uttered.

Moïna saw them carry in a pale and lifeless form that struggled for
breath, and arms moving restlessly as in protest or effort to speak; and
overcome by the sight, Moïna followed in silence, and helped to undress
her mother and lay her on her bed. The burden of her fault was greater
than she could bear. In that supreme hour she learned to know her
mother--too late, she could make no reparation now. She would have them
leave her alone with her mother; and when there was no one else in the
room, when she felt that the hand which had always been so tender for
her was now grown cold to her touch, she broke out into weeping. Her
tears aroused the Marquise; she could still look at her darling
Moïna; and at the sound of sobbing, that seemed as if it must rend the
delicate, disheveled breast, could smile back at her daughter. That
smile taught the unnatural child that forgiveness is always to be found
in the great deep of a mother’s heart.

Servants on horseback had been dispatched at once for the physician and
surgeon and for Mme. d’Aiglemont’s grandchildren. Mme. d’Aiglemont the
younger and her little sons arrived with the medical men, a sufficiently
impressive, silent, and anxious little group, which the servants of the
house came to join. The young Marquise, hearing no sound, tapped gently
at the door. That signal, doubtless, roused Moïna from her grief, for
she flung open the doors and stood before them. No words could have
spoken more plainly than that disheveled figure looking out with haggard
eyes upon the assembled family. Before that living picture of Remorse
the rest were dumb. It was easy to see that the Marquise’s feet were
stretched out stark and stiff with the agony of death; and Moïna,
leaning against the door-frame, looking into their faces, spoke in a
hollow voice:

“I have lost my mother!”
