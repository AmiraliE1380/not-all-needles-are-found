Railroads promised to make many contemporary industries obsolete and transform travel around Paris; elements of the Scene were already becoming relics. The once-common "coucous" of 1830 were fading by 1842, surviving mainly at occasional suburban spectacles. In 1820, many districts still lacked regular stage-coach service, so short-route local vehicles remained essential.

The Touchards, father and son, held a near-monopoly on travel within roughly forty-five miles of their rue du Faubourg-Saint-Denis base, yet they faced brisk competition from local coucous on shorter routes. Parisians’ appetite for the countryside let small familiar enterprises—often run by innkeepers who knew the roads, undercut fares, and earned local loyalty—thrive. These coucous offered speed and convenience that could challenge the Touchards’ more formal coaches until omnibuses later eclipsed them by carrying more passengers more efficiently.

One lucrative contested line ran from Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise; in 1822 multiple rivals operated despite Touchard attempts to cut fares and improve equipment, because profitable stops like Saint-Denis and Saint-Brice ensured steady demand. The Touchards even extended to Chambly while rivals reached Beauvais. By contrast, the road to Isle-Adam, prized for its quarries and scenic terminus, was served by only two coaches; its terminus discouraged speculation, giving Pierrotin and a colleague a virtual monopoly on a route locals often preferred to the crowded Beaumont coaches.

Pierrotin, in his forties, had taken over his father’s coucou after leaving the cavalry in 1815. He married an innkeeper’s daughter, expanded the business, and combined practical intelligence with military precision. His sturdy boots, bottle-green trousers, and blue embroidered blouse matched his trade and hinted at a soldier’s respect for order; his daily work inclined him to regard passengers as cargo.

Sensing the industry’s future, Pierrotin tried to modernize: he ordered a larger coach from a respected maker even as his fleet still included an inherited yellow coucou built for six but often overloaded, and a noisy four-wheeler that could hold seventeen though officially limited to fourteen. He paid taxes only on the coucou, using a permit for the larger coach to skirt regulations.

On a Saturday morning in autumn 1822 Pierrotin stood outside the Lion d’Argent, fretting over scarce passengers and parcels. Optimistic about the new coach but burdened by a thousand-franc debt and contemplating a loan from Monsieur Moreau, steward at Presles, he seized a chance when a footman sought a discreet ride for his master, the Comte de Serizy—whose countermanded carriage and the footman’s insistence on secrecy heightened Pierrotin’s sense of responsibility, and the hope that the fare might ease his debts.

Monsieur Huguet de Serizy descends from the eminent President Huguet, ennobled under François I; the family arms and motto link modesty to the estate’s name.

The current count’s father was president of parliament before the Revolution. The count became a councillor of State at twenty-two, noted for persuasive diplomatic memoranda. He remained at Serizy during the Revolution, protected by his father’s standing; after his father’s death in 1794 he joined the Council of Five Hundred to occupy himself. After 18th Brumaire he gained the First Consul’s favor, rejoined the Council of State, and reorganized a chaotic department, shaping much of Napoleon’s administration. In 1806, at forty, he married the widow of a prominent Republican general, improving his fortune. By 1814 ill health forced his resignation; though Napoleon mistrusted him he retained rank. Louis XVIII later trusted him as a cabinet minister. During the Hundred Days he stayed loyal to the Bourbons at Serizy. After Napoleon’s final defeat he returned as privy councillor and vice-president of the Council of State, influencing affairs without ostentation. His regimen—rising early, retiring early—reflected relentless, quiet duty; he accepted many honors, including the grand cross of the Legion of Honor, yet his devotion carried an undercurrent of private sorrow.

He had long been passionately attached to his wife; despite strains their bond endured. Work was his refuge from heartache; he treated her with respect and allowed her considerable freedom. Though she acknowledged some of his virtues, her ingratitude often wounded him; only occasionally did she soothe his pain.

A pressing business matter then preoccupied him: a farm called Presles was encroaching on his estate. Leased by tenant Léger from a local burgher, Margueron, the lease was expiring and the property would be sold. The count intended to buy it to end disputes and instructed his notary and lawyer to negotiate quickly.

His steward, Moreau, had served faithfully for seventeen years; the count’s father had saved him during the Revolution, and the count later spared him from a death sentence. Moreau asked to manage Presles and at first was diligent, but after securing the post he grew lax and corrupt, taking bribes and arranging shady deals—already bargaining with Léger. His conduct provoked the retired officer Monsieur de Reybert’s wrath; Madame de Reybert brought the count detailed accusations. Initially skeptical of her motives, the count’s doubts grew and he ordered his notary to accompany Madame de Reybert to Presles. What began as a straightforward purchase thus became an investigation fraught with intrigue and the threat of betrayal by trusted servants.

As Pierrotin left the Café de l’Echiquier he saw Madame Clapart and her son at the Lion d’Argent searching for him. In a faded gown she was anxious yet dignified; poverty was visible but devotion clear, as she entrusted eighteen-year-old Oscar, on his first trip alone, to Pierrotin’s care. Oscar, in patched clothes, remained dominated by his mother and resented her fussing. Once Madame Husson, she had been impoverished by misfortunes and a first husband’s suicide, later marrying the meager clerk Clapart. Moreau aided them and secured Oscar a scholarship, but household strain and Clapart’s coldness worsened Oscar’s faults. Overprotected, Oscar’s vanity and entitlement grew; Moreau proposed testing him by placing him with the steward. As they left, two well-dressed men mocked Oscar, deepening his humiliation.

Boarding heightened his discomfort: a shabby artist and his apprentice, Mistigris, mocked him. The Comte de Serizy, travelling incognito, altered the coach’s tone; Pierrotin rearranged seats for him, drawing complaints. When the coach set off, its mixed passengers—proud mothers, insecure youths, discreet patrons, bohemians—framed Oscar’s simmering struggle with identity, setting the stage for the journey.

Those who travel by public conveyance usually begin in silence until the most talkative passenger breaks it. National types are sketched briefly: the English taciturn, Germans somber, Italians cautious, Spaniards seldom using public transport, Russians hampered by poor roads; France is lively, especially among the lower classes and bourgeois.

Georges, a clever young man, quickly sized up his fellow passengers, deemed himself superior, and resolved to amuse himself at their expense. As the coach descended from La Chapelle to Saint-Denis he toyed with identities, finally posing as a disguised Russian prince or a soldier returned from the East. When Mistigris complained about dust, Georges joked and steered conversation toward travels in the Levant, claiming service under Ali Pacha of Janina and inventing a plague scare that alarmed the count enough to have the driver stop. He also said he’d fought in the 1813 campaign and at Waterloo, where he’d been promoted to captain.

The count asked about decorations; Georges said he did not wear them while traveling, but when the count produced his own honors a portfolio found at Saint-Denis identified Georges as Crottat’s second clerk, quickening doubts about his identity. Nevertheless Georges continued to impress—especially Père Léger and Oscar—by paying for treats and cultivating importance. Mistigris, a modest painter, introduced himself; the count watched as Georges spun more Eastern oddities and a dramatic Dalmatian romance—Zena, a jealous pirate husband, poisoning, and a narrow escape—that captivated the coach with laughter and skepticism.

Pierrotin’s coach stopped at Saint-Brice about half past one to rest the horses. The innkeeper greeted the travelers by name and they stepped out. Georges, showy and boastful, ordered a large breakfast, flourished his cane, produced a straw cigar case, lit a pale cigar, and offered cigars to Oscar and Schinner. Oscar, flattered but awkward, puffed ineptly and became the butt of teasing: Schinner smoked with ease, Georges fumbled theatrically, and Mistigris mocked Oscar’s discomfort. Oscar watched Georges’s largesse with thinly veiled resentment, mentally tallying the bills he suspected Georges was burning through.

Georges proposed a bottle of Bordeaux with Père Léger, and the innkeeper supplied local gossip: François, conductor of Touchard’s coach, had heard that Monsieur Margueron had been invited by the Comte de Serizy to dine at Presles that day. Père Léger laughed and sketched a scheme to outmaneuver such patronage: he intended to buy Les Moulineaux cheaply by using new coins and snapping up the farm before the count could act. The innkeeper speculated about lending twenty thousand and calculated returns; Les Moulineaux, he said, yielded six thousand francs—a sound investment. Père Léger explained he’d made Moreau a partner and, leveraging royal favor and the pictures on coins, had secured the estate. The plan sounded shrewd even if the count tried to intervene.

The count—who had been listening—grew uneasy about discretion. When Pierrotin stepped out to water the horses, the count drew him aside and reminded him of a bribe: ten louis to keep the matter secret. Then he raised the inducement dramatically: if Pierrotin would conceal his name, the count would give him a thousand francs the next day for a new coach. He instructed him not to enter the inn, not to speak of the incident, and to stay with the horses. Pierrotin returned curt reassurance and hurried his customers along, insisting he must deliver fish by three to a big dinner in Stors.

Père Léger suggested harnessing the horse Pierrotin meant to sell into the cabriolet so they could breakfast calmly and overtake Pierrotin. To everyone’s surprise, Pierrotin rebridled and set off; Schinner and Mistigris walked. Georges kept up talk about the hostess and the landscape, joking about leftover cigars. Oscar, stung by being called “the little young man,” defended his dignity: he was not Mina’s aide-de-camp, had not been to the East, and his family intended him for diplomacy. This prompted ridicule: his companions mocked his pretensions, tutors, and “household” language—Oscar proudly noting his mother ran their household—and taunted his social standing and vocabulary.

They goaded him into naming his tutor; Oscar named the Abbé Loraux, vicar of Saint-Sulpice, and hinted at family influence that might aid the abbé’s ecclesiastical rise. Pressed for acquaintance with Abbé Frayssinous, Oscar said the abbé owed favors to his father. Then, unexpectedly, Oscar declared he was bound for the Château de Presles to visit the Comte de Serizy and claimed intimacy with the count’s son. His confident talk about the Serizys—outlining family relations and entanglements—caught the count’s attention.

The count seized the chance to question Oscar, explaining he sought information on Monsieur de Serizy to support a claim he intended to press with the American government. Oscar counseled him to approach the count’s wife rather than the count himself: the count, he said, was madly in love with her, while she secretly despised him. He described the count’s disfiguring skin disease and humiliating remedies—sulphur and steam baths, even an iron box—portraying a man reduced and ashamed; such afflictions left the count vulnerable at home. He recommended instead seeking the Marquis d’Aiglemont, an old admirer of Madame de Serizy, for influence.

The young man’s ease in discussing intimate matters shocked the company. Schinner scolded him for airing friends’ affairs in public; Mistigris professed indifference. Georges offered his maxim: in a public coach one should know how to talk without saying anything. When the conversation turned to the Comtesse de Serizy, the count interrupted sharply and forbade disparagement. Asserting his friendship with her brother, the Marquis de Ronquerolles, he vowed that speaking ill of the countess would not go unpunished. The painter agreed: no man should blague women. The count hinted at influence—knowing ministers, blocking unworthy men from decorations, and even acquaintanceship with the architect of Presles—and asked Pierrotin to stop at the next inn so he could step out.

Once they were clear of the others, the count had Pierrotin show him aside in the inn-yard and asked about the talkative young man. Pierrotin explained the boy was the son of a poor woman in the rue de la Cerisaie, Madame Husson, whom he often supplied with fruit and game from Presles. Pierrotin had driven the youngster only once and thought he might be the prince of Maffliers; the boy had told him to leave him near Maffliers, not to go on to Isle-Adam. The count was reassured by this account. When he rejoined the party, Père Léger and the others exchanged awkward glances, feeling exposed; the three young men shared a guilty silence. Mistigris quipped about suffering for loose talk, and Georges reminded Oscar that public conveyances required speaking artfully.

They rode on into the Carreau woods in heavy silence; the count noted their quiet as if they had been led to the scaffold. Mistigris muttered a proverb; Georges remarked on the fine weather. Oscar, striving to assert himself, declared he was Oscar Husson and would be famous within ten years. The companions teased him further, and the count refined his name to Husson de la Cerisaie, giving Oscar’s modest boast a veneer of dignity. The coach descended toward the grand château of Presles.

Before disembarking, the count delivered formal, almost theatrical farewells: the colonel should make peace with the king; Schinner’s fame was assured; Oscar, the budding diplomat, needed no protection, since he possessed “the secrets of statesmen;” Père Léger was urged to pluck the Comte de Serizy and to do so firmly. Asking Pierrotin to set and fetch him at the same spot the next day, the count left the coach to the others’ bewildered speculation about his identity. Père Léger guessed he was the count who’d leased Franconville; Schinner swore never to blague again in a public coach. Mistigris, unrepentant, shrugged with another proverb.

As they neared the château gate, Georges warned Oscar that if the man indeed had been the Comte de Serizy, Oscar should be wary—a chill reminder of his mother’s warnings. Pierrotin called out: they had arrived at Presles. Some of the young men feigned familiarity, but Georges, not wishing to reveal his destination, pretended he would continue to Les Moulineaux. Père Léger then triumphantly disclosed he was the farmer at Les Moulineaux, forcing Georges to admit it was so.

Oscar asked Pierrotin to leave his belongings at the steward’s; he intended to go straight to the château. He plunged forward along a narrow path, uncertain where to go, and Père Léger corrected him: the little gate, not the forest path, led to the château. Guided thus, Oscar entered the courtyard. While Père Léger watched, Georges slipped away upon realizing Père Léger truly possessed Les Moulineaux. Pierrotin, having obeyed the count’s injunction to keep the matter secret for now, drove in with swagger, deposited the painter’s packages and Oscar’s bundles, and handed a small leather portmanteau to the concierge’s wife. Mistigris and the painter took rooms in the château itself—a discovery that stunned Oscar.

The scene closed on Pierrotin’s contentment: he had kept the count’s secret and now looked forward to the promised reward. With a sly peasant’s calculation, he anticipated receiving a thousand francs the next day and buying the splendid new coach he long desired. The coach rolled on, the passengers dispersed through the estate, and the social ambitions, pretensions, and private bargains that had surfaced in their talk settled back into the polite surface of conversation, continuing to move unseen beneath it.

Oscar hid among the trees and watched Monsieur Moreau emerge from the guard-room in a blue overcoat with a riding whip. Moreau greeted the painters Mistigris and his master—sent by architect Monsieur Grindot—told the concierge to show them to their rooms, invited them to dinner at five, and mentioned hunting on the estate. He sent his son Jacques ahead to inform Madame Moreau of their arrival and, stern but kindly, urged Oscar to learn to ride and hunt despite his inexperience. Oscar’s mother had allowed only a two-week stay because Madame Moreau was present, yet Moreau seemed intent on extending it.

Their steward’s house, attached to the chateau and older than the Revolution, sat in a large garden and contained a salon, dining room, and a modern boudoir furnished to Madame Moreau’s taste. The Moreaus lived comfortably on estate provisions. Madame Moreau, once a maid, preserved an air of elegance and worked to be treated as the chateau’s mistress, often patronizing villagers. Tension with the Reyberts dated to an old slight when Madame de Reybert reminded her of her former status. Eager to impress, Madame Moreau hoped the visiting artist would raise her social standing.

On the day the painters were due, Madame Moreau dressed carefully, but the Paris coach brought only Mistigris’s packages, leaving her annoyed. When Mistigris and Bridau arrived, she welcomed them and professed her love of art; the two artists quickly recognized her pretentiousness and decided to humor her with polite conversation about painting. Their talk was cut short when a maid announced the count’s arrival, and Madame Moreau grew flustered. Oscar, feeling out of place, was fetched by his younger brother and scolded for his appearance; Mistigris’s joking remark about Oscar’s future increased the awkwardness. The maid then said the count wanted dinner promptly.

The count had come unexpectedly and was upset to learn Moreau was absent. Having overheard remarks that suggested Moreau’s disloyalty, he confronted him, angered that private matters had been discussed—especially in front of Oscar. Moreau’s attempts to explain failed; he returned home distraught over the loss of his position and the damage caused by Oscar’s careless words. Estelle, his wife, was furious and grasped the gravity of their predicament. Moreau demanded an explanation from Oscar, who remained silent from fear and shame. In anger Moreau dragged him back to the count to apologize; the count dismissed Oscar’s behavior and doubted his promise. Humiliated, Moreau took Oscar home to face uncertain consequences for their family.

Moreau wrote to Madame Clapart, distraught that Oscar had revealed Madame de Serizy’s secrets to an incognito count; the count sent Oscar away, costing Moreau the stewardship at Presles and imperiling the family’s prospects. At the Clapart home Monsieur fretted about Oscar’s character while Madame defended him. When Brochon returned with Oscar and Moreau’s letter, Madame Clapart fainted at the thought of her son’s future.

The next day she found Oscar sullen, reminded him of Moreau’s past support and their precarious position without the stewardship, and urged discretion, hard work, and prudence; he remained largely indifferent. As a last hope she appealed to Uncle Cardot, a self-made, modest businessman who generously supported his children and had been unaware of their plight. Cardot received them kindly, praised Oscar’s promise and—moved by Madame Clapart—offered to fund his legal studies, insisting on integrity, discipline, and effort. Madame Clapart felt renewed hope. Moreau later visited, saying he had begun speculating in land and could place Oscar as a pupil in a law office; he reiterated that disciplined study was essential to repair Oscar’s reputation and secure his future, and Madame Clapart urged Oscar to show gratitude by heeding these admonitions.

Ten days later Monsieur Moreau brought Oscar to Maître Desroches, a young solicitor on the rue de Béthisy who had just opened a roomy, inexpensive office. Desroches, twenty-six and himself from poverty, recognized Oscar’s plight; the boy’s gaunt look and curt manners intimidated him. “We work here day and night,” Desroches declared, and put his head clerk, Monsieur Godeschal, in charge of Oscar’s training so he would not lose time on the way to law school and would master the Code. Oscar was lodged in a small attic beside Godeschal’s room; Desroches intended to make him a capable clerk.

For two years Oscar lived at the office under exacting, petty discipline. Under Godeschal’s strict oversight he kept a monk-like schedule: rising at five to prepare for the day, running errands, studying the Code by rigorous tests, returning to office tasks after classes, and subsisting on plain meals. His social life was limited to monthly breakfasts with his uncle Cardot, Sundays with his mother, and rare outings arranged by Moreau. Attempts at elegance brought disapproval from his superiors.

Godeschal counseled a modest wardrobe and frugal living, insisting a good clerk should not spend more than seven hundred francs a year — a rule he himself followed. He combined rigid expectations with practical humanity: when a colleague failed, Godeschal discreetly guided him to resign rather than face public dismissal, protecting both the man and the firm’s reputation.

By twenty, Oscar was the third clerk, often performing the second’s duties without pay but receiving room and board. His legal knowledge surpassed many licensed graduates, and he began arguing cases in chambers. Yet he felt constrained by a life at odds with his desires: he envied well-dressed young men and longed for freedom and pleasure, feelings he habitually suppressed. Godeschal, aware of these struggles, tried to shield him and sometimes paid for outings; Moreau supported him but could not connect as Godeschal did. Both feared youthful follies might imperil his future, though they hoped to steer him aright. Godeschal even suggested Oscar’s talents might suit a barrister more than a solicitor’s clerkship.

In November 1825 a new clerk, Frédéric Marest, arrived. Well-to-do and aiming for the magistracy, Marest professed a desire for hands-on practice. The clerks enjoyed practical jokes: Godeschal kept a fictitious register of receptions for new clerks filled with absurd entries. When Oscar asked for it, Marest read and laughed, then said he would consult a cousin who was a head clerk — a remark that confirmed his inexperience to Godeschal. Despite office camaraderie, Oscar’s inner conflict persisted as he navigated professional pressures and youthful expectations.

The next day Georges Marest, now head-clerk to the notary Hannequin, recalled mischief that had led to his transfer and invited Oscar and the clerks to the Rocher de Cancale and an evening with Madame la Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos, promising high society. Desroches interrupted to assign Oscar the crucial task of fetching a legal document from the Palais and warned him to beware of Derville, who might obstruct the errand. Eager to prove himself as second clerk, Oscar accepted.

At the lavish breakfast that followed — flirtatious and intoxicated — Oscar played vingt-et-un with Florentine (in truth Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, a celebrated danseuse supported by Père Cardot) and Fanny Beaupré. He lost the money meant for the legal document, accepted Florentine’s offered thousand francs, and then lost everything. Despairing, he fell into stupor and slept in the boudoir while the party continued into the early morning.

A loud noise woke clerk Oscar Husson the next morning. His uncle Cardot scolded him for late-night revelry; the danseuse Florentine revealed Oscar as Cardot’s nephew and urged the uncle to help. Oscar admitted he had lost his employer Monsieur Desroches’s gambling money; Cardot reluctantly lent five hundred francs, warning it would be the last aid.

Mariette sent a note and money while Godeschal, another clerk, hurried to fetch documents for Desroches. Enraged, Desroches resolved to dismiss Oscar despite Godeschal’s defense. Godeschal recounted Oscar’s past mistakes and blamed the bad influence of Georges Marest; Desroches half-joked that Oscar might take up law to flatter his vanity.

At home, Clapart berated his wife about their son’s failures; Madame Clapart defended him. Oscar confronted his father asserting independence but was ignored. Determined to take responsibility, he faced conscription—Clapart believed military service would instill discipline. Oscar drew a number and, three days later through the Comte de Serizy’s influence, was drafted into the cavalry.

Overwhelmed, Madame Clapart turned to religion. In the army Oscar applied himself; the rigors of service steadied his character and he gradually rose in rank to lieutenant‑colonel. Years later a decorated Oscar stood with his mother outside the Lion d’Argent; they encountered Georges Marest, a shadow of his former self. Oscar married Georgette Pierrotin, his earlier errors having become instructive lessons that, with connections and discipline, brought respect and security.
