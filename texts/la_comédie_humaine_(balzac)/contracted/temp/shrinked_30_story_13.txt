**LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES**  
**By Honoré de Balzac**  
**Translated by R. S. Scott**  

**DEDICATION**  
To George Sand, whose friendship has been a source of joy amidst my literary challenges. May this work reflect the esteemed company of great names and noble hearts that accompany my writings.

**LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES**

**FIRST PART**

**I. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENEE DE MAUCOMBE. PARIS, September.**  
My dearest Renee, I am free! I hope you have escaped the convent as well. My release came from my aunt's concern for my health after your departure left me despondent. In Paris, I feel our souls are intertwined. Without you, I felt trapped, and my aunt cannot understand my longing for life outside the convent.

Upon leaving, my aunt's parting words were both ominous and affectionate. Arriving in Paris, I found no one waiting for me, and the emptiness of my late grandmother's home overwhelmed me. Philippe, her servant, informed me that my inheritance was intact, but my father had not restored the family home, filling me with despair over my future.

As I settled in, I found comfort in my grandmother's memories and resolved to make the space my own. I long to hear about your life and hope you are well.

**October 19th.**  
I met my mother for the first time since my return. She treated me as an equal, expressing her desire to be a friend rather than a strict mother. My father was equally affectionate, and I felt a sense of belonging. I am preparing for my debut into society, with a generous allowance and a governess, Miss Griffith, to guide me.

**II. THE SAME TO THE SAME November 25th.**  
I am starting to feel at home. My father’s allowance allows me to acquire new clothes for my social debut, and I am eager to embrace society. Miss Griffith is a good companion, and my family seems busy with their own lives, leaving me to discover my path.

I have not yet ventured into Paris but look forward to experiencing the city. My mother is often out, giving me time to reflect on my new circumstances.

**III. THE SAME TO THE SAME December.**  
I am ready to enter society! After much preparation, I feel confident in my appearance. I plan to present myself as charming and innocent, hoping to win favor. Despite my excitement, I feel regret for my past life in the convent. The transition is overwhelming, and my family remains distant, absorbed in their own affairs.

I am curious about the allure of society and how it captivates people, keeping them from truly connecting. I hope to uncover the mysteries of my family and the society I am about to enter.

On December 15th, I drove through the Champs-Elysees and Bois de Boulogne on a beautiful autumn day. Despite my carefully curated appearance, I received little attention from passersby, except for my approving parents. Griffith scanned the crowd indifferently while I grew frustrated that one man inspected my carriage without noticing me, realizing that beauty in Paris is more common than I expected.

The men I observed were generally unattractive, lacking elegance in their fashion, particularly their hats. I commented to Miss Griffith about their looks, but she defended them as cultivated.

At an evening ball, I stayed close to my mother, who attracted all the attention while I was merely a pretext for compliments. My dress, which I thought stunning, paled in comparison to the married women’s finery. The men at the ball did not impress me; they appeared worn and lacked the proud bearing of their ancestors. One man stood out for his beauty and intellect but seemed self-absorbed, suggesting that intellectuals often prioritize ambition over genuine connections.

Determined not to let society's indifference affect me, I focused on enjoying the dance. Upon returning home, I shared my experiences with my mother, who advised me to be discreet about my feelings, highlighting the complexities of feminine duplicity.

A fortnight after my arrival at the Castle of Maucombe, my family dined with M. de l'Estorade, a wealthy neighbor whose son had been lost during Bonaparte's reign. After evading conscription, he joined the Emperor's bodyguard but vanished after the Battle of Leipzig. Eventually, the son returned from travels through Russia, Poland, and Germany, aided by the French Minister in Berlin. M. de l'Estorade has amassed a fortune of about 250,000 livres.

My parents support a match between me and Louis de l'Estorade, especially since I would be acceptable without a dowry. The dinner marked my first meeting with Louis, who, despite his humble background, is now my intended. The dinner was simple, and Louis appeared worn and silent. Despite this, I have agreed to marry him, contingent upon my ability to improve his estate.

While you prepare for the excitement of Parisian life, I embrace a simpler existence. I am determined to beautify my new home, envisioning a life filled with nature and family, free from past turmoil. My future seems straightforward, with no more Bonaparte to disrupt it.

I jest about my new life, but I must embrace this role. P.S. I have about 1,000 livres to spend; please buy me lovely things unavailable here or in Marseilles. Remember the recluse at La Crampade. I will respond to your letter soon.

In his letters to Don Fernand Paris, Don Felipe Henarez reassures his brother of his safety amid Spain's political chaos, reflecting on their ancestors' massacre that forced them into survival. Despite Ferdinand's promises of protection, Felipe remains skeptical, believing true Spaniards do not need reassurances. He recounts his escape from Spain, aided by former foes, and laments the decline of his estates, feeling trapped between life and death, unable to fight for his country or pursue love.

Felipe urges Fernand to claim his titles and properties from the King, using his affection for Marie Heredia as leverage. He acknowledges their mutual feelings and encourages their union while resigning himself to obscurity. He plans to keep his mother’s diamonds for independence and offers Fernand a wedding gift from his limited savings, concluding with a sense of finality as he prepares to embrace life as the Baron de Macumer.

In a subsequent letter, Felipe reflects on his modest life in France, teaching Spanish and finding solace in nature. He expresses no regrets about his abdication, feeling detached from his former wealth. He channels his unexpressed tenderness into his love for Spain and hopes Fernand and Marie will cherish each other, fulfilling dreams he could not. He emphasizes the importance of keeping his identity as Henarez a secret from Marie to preserve their family legacy.

Louise de Chaulieu writes to Renee de Maucombe, expressing disbelief at her quick engagement and warning against the constraints of marriage. She shares her disillusionment with society and men while remaining hopeful for love. In her second letter, she reveals her interest in Don Henarez, noting his dignified demeanor amidst the superficiality of her social circle. Louise grapples with her emotions and societal expectations, recognizing the rarity of genuine affection and the challenges of finding true love.

In a Monday update, Mlle. de Chaulieu shares her fascination with M. Henarez, a melancholic Spanish teacher whose enigmatic nature captivates her. Despite their social differences, she feels drawn to him, contemplating that genuine passion should exist beyond marriage.

Mme. de L'Estorade cautions Mlle. de Chaulieu against pursuing M. Henarez, labeling him a dangerous adventurer and hinting at her own marital difficulties.

In February, Mlle. de Chaulieu reflects on her father's political ambitions and France's instability, revealing the tension between her personal desires and family obligations.

In a Friday update, she acknowledges her growing feelings for M. Henarez, noting his charm and intellect, which intensify her attraction.

Mme. de L'Estorade shares her marriage experiences, highlighting the contrasts between single and married life, and the importance of friendship and independence within marriage.

The Duc de Soria advises his brother to accept family financial support, emphasizing the value of family ties despite challenges.

Mlle. de Chaulieu critiques Mme. de L'Estorade's pragmatic views on marriage, insisting that love should guide decisions while reflecting on the joy and turmoil of her feelings for M. Henarez.

In a Monday update, she recounts a romantic encounter with M. Henarez, feeling both excitement and apprehension, ultimately deciding to burn a letter from him, symbolizing her emotional conflict.

As she prepares for a significant evening adorned with white camellias, she contemplates love's power and the simplicity of expressing emotions through flowers. The evening culminates in a deepening connection with M. Henarez, reinforcing her resolve to embrace her feelings.

Mlle. de Chaulieu expresses her excitement about a new carriage and her affection for M. Henarez, feeling empowered by love. In response, Mme. de L'Estorade emphasizes the seriousness of marriage, arguing that love must be balanced with responsibility and reflecting on her own marriage's complexities.

Dear Renee, you are remarkable, and I believe we can only be virtuous through deception. The man who loves us is ours to shape, yet we often make fools of them. You seek to inspire ambition in yours while keeping him in love, but is love alone sufficient?

Without an afterlife, your self-sacrifice would be tragic. You want to balance his ambition and love, but where is the line between virtue and calculation? Despite my deceit, I confess my love for Felipe, which I hide behind hypocrisy. I long for him, yet I would scorn him if he entered my life.

What stops me from revealing my happiness to Felipe? His love is pure, yet I feel a strange pull toward him, despite my better judgment. I resonate with your feelings as I navigate this emotional turmoil.

Felipe's happiness seems tied to his devotion, but his respectful distance frustrates me. I want to shout that if he loves me as an image, he will be even more captivated by the real me.

Oh, Renee, please burn my letters, as I will yours. If anyone else read our thoughts, I would have Felipe silence them.

On Monday, I ponder the heart's complexities. I wish my father would introduce me to M. Bonald for answers. Does Felipe still worship me? If I sense any change in his respect, I would walk away.

On Tuesday, I received his perfect response, expressing his deep happiness and vulnerability. The weather has been dreadful, preventing me from going out.

On Wednesday, I saw Felipe at the Opera, transformed and nervous. His eyes sparkled, and others noticed his change, which elated me. I yearn for him to boldly ask for my hand, so I can gauge his love. I am left wondering what he wanted to say. I will go to bed joyful, dreaming of our conversations. Farewell, dear friend. It has been over a month since I heard from you. Are you finally happy? Have you given up your cherished independence?

In her letter, Renee expresses dissatisfaction with marriage, questioning why love is often overlooked. She feels that marriage imposes obligations on women, forcing them into subservience. Renee reflects on her transformation from an individual to a possession, lamenting her emotional sacrifices and longing for a love that transcends societal expectations, hoping for fulfillment through motherhood.

Renee observes that her husband, Louis, is unaware of her struggles, enjoying the benefits of her sacrifices without recognizing the effort behind them. She yearns for a child to channel her energy and emotions, believing motherhood could bring her joy.

Louise shares her turmoil as she contemplates a secret meeting with Felipe, a man she desires. Torn between societal norms and her instincts, she wants to test Felipe's character and commitment. Driven by a longing for adventure, Louise plans a clandestine encounter in her garden, hoping to explore their feelings away from prying eyes.

As she prepares, Louise reflects on the thrill and anxiety of the situation, wishing for Renee's company to share in the excitement. When they meet, their conversation is charged with unspoken emotions. Louise expresses concerns about the risks of their relationship, while Felipe, overwhelmed with joy, vows to prove his worthiness.

Their exchange reveals a deepening connection, with Louise acknowledging the complexities of love and the balance of power within relationships. Both women navigate love, duty, and personal fulfillment, seeking to reconcile their aspirations with societal realities.

In her correspondence with Felipe, Louise expresses disappointment, stating that if he does not find Racine's _Berenice_ moving, they lack a true connection. She criticizes his self-satisfied demeanor, feeling that if he does not see her as unique, she must be less than a woman in his eyes. Louise reflects on love's nature, asserting she will not forgive serious transgressions and warns that any sign of diminished passion from Felipe would lead her to despair.

Louise implores Felipe not to make her envy her past self, who was blissfully loved. Doubt has pierced her heart, and if he cannot meet her emotional needs, she prefers he leave. She values his intellect and character, desiring him to embody noble qualities while hoping for a deeper connection.

Felipe expresses remorse for upsetting Louise, interpreting her jealousy as love. He longs for a closer connection and fears she no longer loves him. However, her letter brings him joy and hope, emphasizing his humility and commitment.

Louise shares her experiences with Felipe, revealing her desire to test his love. During a garden walk, she observes his unwavering loyalty and hints at societal pressures regarding her father's marriage plans. At the opera, she appreciates Felipe's calm demeanor amidst rumors about her potential marriage, signaling him for an intimate moment. Louise declares her love for Felipe and suggests marriage, emphasizing her commitment despite societal constraints.

Later, she recounts a conversation with her parents about her impending marriage, acknowledging her mother's wisdom on the realities of married life. Renee responds with a contrasting account of her mundane life with Louis, expressing concern over the romantic ideals Louise holds and warning of potential disillusionment after marriage. Despite her worries, she expresses a desire to meet Felipe and share in Louise's happiness.

In a later letter, Louise shares the joy of her engagement to Felipe, who has accepted her family's wishes regarding her fortune. She reflects on her mother's supportive role and acknowledges the complexities of love and marriage, recognizing the need for patience and understanding. As the wedding approaches, she feels excitement and anxiety, affirming her identity as Louise de Macumer while cherishing her past as Louise de Chaulieu. 

Through these letters, themes of love, loyalty, societal expectations, and the transition from maidenhood to womanhood are explored, highlighting the emotional depth and complexity of relationships during this period.

Dear friend, it has been nearly eight months since our marriage, and your silence is inexcusable.

We traveled to the Castle of Chantepleurs, purchased by Macumer in Nivernais, sixty leagues from Paris. Our servants arrived ahead, and we reached the castle the following evening. I slept during the journey, comforted by Felipe's tender presence. His affectionate gestures deeply touched me, and I awoke to find his passionate gaze unchanged. Despite his calm demeanor, he later revealed intense emotions he had kept hidden.

As we settled into our new life, I found that marriage was not as daunting as I expected. Felipe reassured me of his fidelity, and I felt a mix of joy and complexity in love. I began to understand the depth of my feelings and our bond.

True happiness in marriage is often unspoken, overshadowed by duty. I admire your strength, Renee, and see that those who marry for love, like me, experience a different reality than those who marry for convenience, like you.

We spent seven and a half blissful months at Chantepleurs, akin to lovers escaping the world. Yet, I often thought of you and your more pragmatic marriage. Now, we are in Paris, enjoying a lovely home in the Rue du Bac. I feel more beautiful and content than ever.

I wish to understand your heart, as I suspect you hide your own wounds. I long for you to find joy with Louis, who adores you. Remember our youthful vows to share burdens? I now pray for your happiness as fervently as you once prayed for mine.

I have an idea: encourage Louis to pursue a political career. If he becomes a deputy, we can be together in Paris, and perhaps he will gain a title. This could be a new beginning for both of us.

My dear Louise, your letter filled me with joy and a touch of envy. As I sit by the Mediterranean, nurturing climbing plants, I feel the stirrings of motherhood, transforming my understanding of sacrifice into strength. While you experience love with Felipe, my love is directed toward nurturing future children. Though I feel detached from the excitement of pregnancy, I recognize that each woman's journey is unique.

I grapple with strange cravings, particularly for rotten fruit, which oddly alleviates my discomfort. I wonder when the true sense of motherhood will awaken within me.

As I close this letter, I urge you to remain humble in your happiness. Your love for Felipe seems to challenge fate, and I worry that such joy may attract misfortune. Remember to keep faith and practice kindness, as I have come to value spirituality more since my marriage.

We are thrilled to announce that Renee has given birth to a healthy boy, Armand-Louis de l'Estorade, whom we hope you will be godmother to. Renee showed remarkable patience during childbirth, and we look forward to welcoming you soon. Your assistance in obtaining a title for my father would greatly benefit our son.

Yours sincerely,  
LOUIS DE L'ESTORADE.

In January 1826, Louise de Macumer writes to her friend Renee de l'Estorade, excited about her upcoming visit to Chantepleurs in April and her role as godmother to Renee's first child. She insists her husband, Felipe, must be the godfather. Louise reflects on their different lives—Renee as a mother and herself as childless—finding joy in their roles despite her desire for motherhood and fear of jealousy.

Louise describes her vibrant life with Felipe, noting society's perception of her beauty since their marriage. She longs for intense love, even contemplating dying young to preserve her happiness. Their first quarrel arises when she wishes Felipe to end her life at thirty, which he refuses, revealing his innocence.

Renee responds nearly five months after giving birth to her son, Armand, apologizing for her delayed reply. She shares her emotional journey during childbirth and the overwhelming love she feels for her child, contrasting it with her initial fears. Renee highlights the deep bond created through nursing and notes her feelings for her husband, Louis, have shifted to profound gratitude. She reflects on motherhood's fulfillment and encourages Louise to embrace her future as a mother, believing it will enhance her happiness. The letters illustrate the contrasting yet complementary experiences of two friends navigating love and motherhood.

**Summary of Correspondence Between Mme. de Macumer and Mme. de L'Estorade (March - July 1826)**

**Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (March 1826)**  
I apologize for my delayed response; it has been over three months. Felipe and I assumed your silence meant consent regarding the baby-wreathed luncheon service, which is now on its way to Marseilles. I miss our conversations. Life in Paris is overwhelming, filled with societal pressures that leave little room for genuine connection. My love for Felipe feels like my only refuge.

I am busy with social gatherings and have gained a fashionable reputation, which pleases Felipe. My parents are in Madrid, and my brother is marrying an heiress, securing our family's legacy. Society's initial criticism of my marriage has turned to admiration, granting me influence. However, the constant attention can be intoxicating, making me understand why some women become selfish in such an environment.

I long for a true friend and cherish Felipe's love. We plan to move to Chantepleurs for a break from Parisian life, and I look forward to seeing you in May after two years apart.

**Mme. de L'Estorade to Mme. de Macumer**  
Dear friend, motherhood has kept me busy, and I am fully dedicated to my son, Armand-Louis. My life is fulfilling, and I eagerly await your visit, hoping you find my home welcoming despite the challenges of a teething baby.

**Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (April 1826)**  
I’m pleased to inform you that your father-in-law is now Comte de l'Estorade, which I ensured before leaving Paris.

**Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (July 1826)**  
I must confess I left abruptly due to jealousy. Felipe’s attention to you during our visit was hard for me to bear. Your beauty and joy of motherhood highlighted my insecurities, and I felt envious of your lively home.

Despite this, I recognize your happiness with L'Estorade, who seems respectable. I’m returning to Chantepleurs, hoping to find fulfillment in my life. I know I’m being foolish, but I needed to escape the discomfort I felt around you.

**Mme. de L'Estorade to Mme. de Macumer**  
Your sudden departure surprised us, and I was taken aback by your letter. You had no reason to feel jealous; our conversations were innocent and focused on your happiness. I urge you to return, as Felipe respects you deeply.

I’ve observed that your relationship lacks true partnership. If you continue to dominate, you risk feeling unfulfilled. Genuine love requires balance; embrace your role as a supportive wife to maintain Felipe’s dignity.

Consider my words carefully. You have the potential for true happiness; do not squander it. Return to Paris when Felipe does, as society can help you both grow.

Farewell, my dear friend. I hope you find peace and clarity. 

My beloved, I’m thrilled to be in Italy with Macumer, whose Sardinia plans are on hold. This enchanting country could convert any Protestant. Macumer is well-received here, and I’m favored at court. 

We’ll soon be in Florence, then spend a month in Tuscany and Naples before reaching Rome in November. Our journey feels like a honeymoon, revitalizing our passion. Apologies for not writing more; I’m fully immersed in this experience.

The Vicomtesse de L'Estorade writes to the Baronne de Macumer about a reply waiting for her at Chantepleurs. She expresses concern over the government's impending dissolution, which may hinder the Crown's power consolidation. Her father will stand for election before resigning and shares joy over her godson's upcoming weaning in December, concluding with affectionate remarks about the Baronne's influence in Italy.

I received your distressing letter, forwarded by my steward. Renee, I won’t dwell on my hurt feelings but will share the impact of your words.

We had just returned from a lovely reception hosted by the ambassador, where I felt radiant, and Macumer was swept away in love. Upon reading your harsh response, I sobbed, risking my composure. Felipe, my dear Arab, declared you were mistaken. He took me to the balcony, professing his love in Italian, insisting that even if your predictions were true, he would not trade our moments together for a mundane existence with children.

While he whispered sweet nothings, a bat's cry startled me, and I fainted. But I am well now. This morning, I knelt before Felipe, confessing my fears and asking him to guide me if I ever strayed from his desires. Your letter frightened me, for you are a true friend.

Thank you, Renee. Your words revealed the depth of Macumer's love. Rome is the city of love, where passion thrives alongside art and faith.

We will soon meet the Duc and Duchesse de Soria in Venice. If you write, please address it to Paris, as we leave Rome shortly.

P.S. Your letter shows you know little of love beyond theory. Love produces diverse outcomes, defying any single explanation.

My father has been elected to the Chamber, my father-in-law has passed away, and I am nearing my second confinement. I mention these events to avoid alarming you with the black seal.

Your letter from Rome unsettled me. You and Felipe seem like children. His love for you may resemble a man’s affection for a courtesan—lavish yet knowing she is unfaithful. I draw a harsh lesson from our differing fates: do not love if you wish to be loved.

Louis received the cross of the Legion of Honor upon his election to the provincial Council nearly three years ago. My father has requested the rank of Officer of the Legion for him, and I hope you can assist in this matter without entangling yourself in my father's ambitions.

Ah, Louise, I have endured hell. If I recount my anguish, it is because you are like a sister to me. Five days of torment felt like centuries. A mother who has not experienced this suffering cannot comprehend it.

The night before the crisis, the weather was close, and my son Armand was unusually irritable. I attributed it to teething. In the morning, he cried for water, but his voice was strange, and he became stiff. I called for Louis, fearing he was dying. The doctors arrived, but their opinions varied. They suggested leeches and ice, which felt like death to me.

For an hour, I held my lifeless child, watching in horror as he convulsed. I felt utterly helpless, and my mother was far away. After four days of fear, the doctors recommended a painful ointment. I refused, trusting nature instead. In a moment of desperation, I held Armand close, praying for his life. Miraculously, he began to recover, but I was left utterly drained.

Now, as I write, Armand is playing, but I remain troubled by the cause of his illness. Is it teething or a nervous issue? I fear for my unborn child. The agony of seeing a child in convulsions is indescribable.

Dear Renee, Macumer and I forgave your earlier naughtiness upon hearing of your distress. Your double agony resonated with me, and I see some compensation in being childless.

I write to inform you that Louis has been promoted to Officer of the Legion. You are fortunate, and perhaps you will have a daughter, as you wished.

My brother married Mlle. de Mortsauf upon our return. The King has granted him the reversion of a prestigious post, contingent on a family emblem arrangement.

My parents visited for the wedding and will return to Madrid after my reception tomorrow. The carnival will be lively, especially with the Duc and Duchesse de Soria in Paris, which makes me uneasy. I am lavish with affection towards Felipe to keep his attention away from Marie Heredia, who is stunning but lacks charm.

I plan to retreat to Chantepleurs after March to economize, as our Italian tour was costly. Felipe and I long for the tranquility of the countryside after the extravagance of Italy. We look forward to your visit, and I assure you, I will not be jealous. You are free to explore Macumer's heart. Since our time in Rome, his love for me has deepened.

With love, dear friend.

My daughter, Jeanne-Athenais, is two months old, with my mother and Louis' grand-uncle as her godparents. I plan to visit Chantepleurs soon, as you’re not worried about a nursing mother. Your godson can now say your name, calling it _Matoumer_. He’s growing quickly, but I worry about him while I recover from childbirth, as the doctors have advised special precautions. My father noticed Felipe looks thin, and I sense something is troubling you, as your letter lacks warmth. My nurse is upset with me for writing, and Mlle. Athenais is hungry. Please write me a long letter.

For the first time, I find myself alone and crying under a willow by the Chantepleurs marsh. Married for nearly three years without a child, I yearn for motherhood, imagining the joy of a child like your Athenais. Each disappointment deepens my melancholy, prompting me to compose elegies and dream of motherhood—embroidering caps, choosing baby clothes, and hearing "Mamma" from my own child. My home feels desolate without children, and I feel unnatural as a childless woman. I long to express my sorrow, and even my Moor was moved to tears by my plight. I sometimes feel the urge to go on a pilgrimage for solace. Next winter, I plan to consult a doctor about my situation.

A year has passed without a letter from you, which pains me. Your husband Louis visits often, but I miss your thoughts and feelings. Your seclusion in the country during a time when you could enjoy Paris and your husband's political success worries me. I hope you will come soon, as I want to know more about your life and family.

You complain of my silence, but my children keep me busy. I didn’t want to bring them to Paris while expecting another child. I plan to buy a house in Paris with money from Louis' uncle and my mother. My days revolve around caring for my children, and I find joy in their sleep, planning their futures. Motherhood requires self-denial and devotion; I cannot trust anyone else to care for them as I do.

I am overwhelmed with grief after losing Felipe. I stayed by his side until the end, and now I am left alone, haunted by memories. I regret the jealousy I placed on him and live in a haze of sorrow, questioning if our passion is destined to be barren. I invite you to visit me, as your presence would bring comfort.

Dear Renee,

By the time you read this, I will be near you. Louis must remain in Provence for the elections due to Liberal plots against him. I come not to comfort but to share my heart. Weep, for tears bring joy in reuniting with him. Each duty done brings you closer. In my arms, you will rise, pure and noble, ready to do good in his name. I write amidst preparations, often interrupted by the children, especially Armand, who longs to see you.

I have sold my house and properties, but I assure you, I am neither mad nor ruined. After settling everything, I have about twelve hundred thousand francs left. I invested a million in the Three per Cents at fifty, yielding an income of sixty thousand francs, which is better than the thirty thousand from my properties. Managing farms was overwhelming for a widow of twenty-seven. Now, I receive my income directly from the state, which is simpler and more secure.

You may wonder about the risk of national bankruptcy, but I believe it’s unlikely, and even if it happened, I would still be stable. My friend, the Comte de l'Estorade, reassures me of my decision.

The real reason for my actions is love. I am to be married soon in a secret ceremony near Paris. I love deeply and am loved in return. My beloved, Marie Gaston, has been courting me for nearly two years. He is twenty-three and has debts of thirty thousand francs, which I have paid off. He is a poet struggling to make a name for himself, and I admire his passion.

Despite our age difference and his financial struggles, I am ready to end our long restraint. This is not naive; I am confident in my feelings and his. I have never met a man with such noble feelings, and I am excited about our future.

Gaston is the illegitimate son of Lady Brandon, raised by his brother Louis, who has since disappeared. He has faced many hardships, believing that genius would lead him to success, but he has only accumulated more debt. I felt an immediate connection when we met, though he is unaware of my love.

I am certain of my happiness in marriage, but I fear the potential for loss. I worry about aging and not meeting his expectations. If our love were to fade, I would take the blame. I ask you, should that happen, to care for my children as my legacy.

I plan to keep our marriage private. I have purchased a secluded property near Ville d'Avray, designed for our solitude. The chalet is beautifully constructed and hidden from view, ensuring our privacy.

I have arranged for a small, trustworthy staff to help maintain our home, and I have moved my cherished belongings there. The tranquility of the valley is perfect for our life together.

In two days, I will become Madame Gaston. I am filled with both joy and sorrow, knowing my love for Gaston is immense, yet I will miss you dearly. Please address future correspondence to me as Madame Gaston, Poste Restante, Versailles, to keep our lives a secret.

Farewell, my dear friend. The pain of losing you is as profound as my love for Gaston.

Dear Daniel,

I need you and Joseph Bridau as witnesses for my wedding tomorrow. My future wife prefers seclusion, which is why we’ve seen little of each other. After the wedding, we will be apart for a while, but I trust our friendship can endure this absence. She has sacrificed her closeness with a childhood friend for me, and I must do the same.

Our love is profound and genuine, built on mutual understanding. However, I feel a burden of shame; she is wealthy, while I am not. Your support has been crucial during my struggles, and I feel humiliated by our circumstances. This shame reflects my love, as I willingly accept it.

Despite this, my dreams are fulfilled. She is noble, intelligent, and beautiful, embodying the ideal woman. At twenty-seven, she is a widow without children, possessing both energy and moments of melancholy. Our love is pure and has flourished without succumbing to physical desire, reflecting our mutual respect. We have both faced disappointments, and she acknowledges her previous marriage as a mistake.

You, my friend, are a giant among men, and I hope you can appreciate the happiness that awaits me.
