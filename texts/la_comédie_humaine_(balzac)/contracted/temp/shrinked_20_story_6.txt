The Rue du Tourniquet‑Saint‑Jean, a dark, winding lane by the Hôtel de Ville that ended at the Rue Martroi, was closed by a turnstile until 1823. Less than six feet wide at its broadest, it carried gutter water, stayed perpetually muddy, and received sunlight only briefly in summer; dampness lingered. Residents lit lamps as early as five in June and kept them burning through winter. Approaching the Marais felt like passing through cellars, and the corner house at the Rue du Tourniquet and Rue de la Tixeranderie still bore iron rings from chains once used for public safety.

Built against the damp, the house raised its cellars above ground. Three steps led to a closed arched doorway topped by a keystone carved as a female head. Three high iron‑barred windows lit a small dwelling where two beds draped in green serge stood, while an old woman tended a brazier and prepared a porter’s‑wife stew. The kitchen‑dining room was spare but plainly clean and thrifty.

The old woman’s pale, wrinkled face seemed part of the house; she sat by the fire with mischief mingled with resignation as her daughter, Caroline, worked at an embroidery frame. With slow fingers and antiquated spectacles the mother occasionally glanced out the window; the daughter bent over her work with modest concentration. Their humble home reflected the lives of working‑class Parisian women. Despite poverty, Caroline kept youthful charm—rosy cheeks and bright eyes—and her spirit was not wholly dimmed. The mother speculated about passersby and hoped for a better future for her daughter; Caroline usually kept her gaze down, absorbed in embroidery.

One August day in 1815 the mother noticed a new face among the regular passersby: a tall, thin man in black whose solemn bearing intrigued her. When he passed again, Caroline, roused from indifference, felt an unfamiliar sympathy at the traces of suffering on his face and wondered if he might alter her life.

For two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved erratically, no longer passing the Rue du Tourniquet at the same hour. Madame Crochard noticed his distracted manner—often lost in thought and seldom acknowledging her or her daughter Caroline. One mid‑September morning Caroline’s lively presence caught his eye; their fleeting glances hinted at a silent understanding.

Weeks later he resumed his twice‑daily walks, and their brief encounters became a guarded routine. Their wordless exchanges showed concern: Caroline sensed when he was troubled, and he knew when she worried about work. Both remained cautious, fearing intimacy might bring trouble.

Annoyed by Caroline’s indecision, Madame Crochard turned her displeasure on the Gentleman, who had once won her mild approval. That winter, after overhearing a conversation about the family’s finances, he was moved and secretly left money for them; Caroline found it the next day, and they shared a silent moment of gratitude.

In early May Caroline proposed a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—more somber than usual—unexpectedly joined. Madame Crochard pretended to sleep, letting Caroline and the Gentleman speak freely. The countryside and easy talk lifted his spirits; sharing family hardships and personal stories deepened their bond. The day brimmed with laughter, and as evening fell Caroline voiced her newfound happiness. Roger—by that name now—playfully held her hand before leaving, sealing an unspoken promise of a brighter future.

Their story continues in Paris. Caroline has spent a month in a second‑floor flat on Rue Taitbout, furnished in an English‑influenced style: grey hangings trimmed with green silk, comfortable chairs, a writing table for love letters, a bronze clock of Love crowning Psyche, and a Gothic‑pattern carpet. As she readies for Roger’s visit, her hairdresser flatters her.

On a chilly September day in 1816, dressed in a green grenadine gown trimmed with chinchilla, she watches from the balcony, heart racing. After fifteen minutes impatience gives way to joy when he arrives; they embrace passionately after three days apart. He must attend a meeting with the Attorney‑General, which disappoints her; she objects to his theatre visits without her and declares her deep affection. Perched on his lap, she says she wishes to take his name—Caroline de Bellefeuille—and use her earnings to help the poor. She plays a sonata as he leaves, watching him drive away, longing for his return.

Five years after settling in her Rue Taitbout home, Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille sits contentedly with her four‑year‑old son Charles, who plays with a rocking horse. Now twenty‑four, her beauty reflects a happy, fulfilled life. She has embraced motherhood, devoted to raising her children and fostering their goodness. Satisfied with simple pleasures, she is indifferent to society’s expectations and seeks neither luxury nor status.

Rocking her infant daughter Eugénie, Caroline recalls joyful visits to Bellefeuille with Roger. Their love has deepened in the simplicity of shared life, free from social complication, yet she still wonders why Roger insists on secrecy and what their exact arrangement is.

On May 6, 1822—the anniversary of a pivotal day—Caroline prepares for dinner, expecting Roger. He brings a document detailing Eugénie’s marriage portion and insists that Charles, as a boy, must learn self‑sufficiency while Eugénie, as a girl, deserves a fortune. Their evening is warm, ending with a lantern show for Charles. But as night deepens Roger must leave; he promises three days off, and Caroline is left with the familiar ache of separation.

Soon after she hurries to her mother, Madame Crochard, now gravely ill. The widow’s acquaintances, unaware Caroline is her daughter, have summoned a priest for last rites. Arriving amid confusion and opportunism—local women hoping to gain from the widow’s wealth—Caroline tends her mother. Madame Crochard laments their estrangement and reveals that the priest compelled a confession about Caroline’s benefactor, hinting at a more complex web of relationships and secrets.

The narrative then backtracks to November 1805: a young barrister, Monsieur de Granville, precarious after a High Chancellor’s gathering, is urged to return for a proposed marriage to Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems that could secure his future despite the family’s stigma. In Bayeux he finds a household stifled by the widow’s piety; at church he glimpses Angélique, whose beauty rekindles a childhood affection, setting the stage for a reunion that may alter both their lives. The intertwined stories probe love, duty, and social expectation as each navigates desire against familial obligation.

For about two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems he frequented her dull drawing-room and watched Angélique prepare her trousseau. She never argued about religion: when he playfully handled her rosary or mocked practices she calmly returned it to its bag. Firm in Church teaching, she insisted a mother must have a religion and asked how one could judge between disbelievers and God.

Granville, moved by her conviction, sometimes mistook desire for love and was tempted to adopt her beliefs. Angélique reconciled feeling with duty, leaving him unsure of his own emotions. On the day of the marriage contract Madame Bontems exacted a promise from Granville to respect Angélique’s practices and allow her freedom of conscience; Angélique’s gentle look convinced him and the Abbé Fontanon approved. After their wedding they moved to Paris, where Granville became public prosecutor.

Angélique persuaded him to take a ground-floor apartment in the Marais near a church and chapel, arguing it would be better for their future children. Granville, who preferred a more fashionable quarter, yielded. He set up his study and left furnishing to Angélique, who took pleasure in arranging the home. He was dismayed by the cold solemnity she created—a reflection of her austere upbringing. He tried to comfort her that happiness came from love and gentleness, not material things, yet grew frustrated by the lack of warmth.

For a time the couple seemed harmonious; Granville stayed absorbed in work and remained largely oblivious to Angélique’s strict observances—he passed off her Lenten austerities as singular zeal and went to church with her on Sundays without grasping her fervor. He did not fully see it until a disruptive event. In November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux, Angélique’s spiritual director, arrived in Paris alarmed by what he saw as her moral decline and aimed to reclaim her from a lax lifestyle. Angélique adopted stricter practices that sparked misunderstandings about social obligations. Granville was irritated by her frequent excuses to avoid events, especially balls. After a tense evening he confronted her about her demeanor and dress, urging her to fulfill her social role. Arguments escalated: Angélique cited her director’s prohibitions; Granville argued the priest was overreaching. She even wrote to the Pope for guidance.

The Pope’s reply sided with Granville, saying a wife should accompany her husband even at some personal cost. Rather than reconcile them, the decision deepened the divide: Angélique and her director dismissed it. Frustrated, Granville began asserting his preferences more forcibly—ordering meat daily despite her objections—and retreated from social life, weighed down by what he saw as her bigotry.

Their home grew oppressive and joyless; the rigid piety affected everyone, including the servants. Angélique’s austerity dimmed her beauty; duty replaced warmth. Granville felt trapped in a marriage that left little room for affection. Their children suffered: he sent his sons away to school early and planned to marry off his daughters as soon as possible. Increasingly isolated, he sought refuge in work and legal study, while Angélique’s attempts to reconvert him only worsened their estrangement, turning the household into a cold, rigid place rather than a home of companionship.

The Granville household looked calm, but Madame suffered, seeking Mass while Granville lived apart.

She grew bitter after he refused a provincial presidency; her confessor suspected it. Stress and fever ruined her health, worsened by his indifference.

Abbé Fontanon revealed Granville had kept a concubine seven years, sired two children, and ruined their fortune. She meant to confront him, but an argument between Roger and Bellefeuille—who blamed her as Roger spoke of hopes—left Madame fainting. She confronted him; he blamed her piety, admitted the misconduct, refused to mend it; the marriage collapsed, leaving her devastated.

Early one December night in 1833 a white‑haired man whose face showed grief more than age paced the Rue Gaillon and stopped before a modest two‑story house with a dim attic light. A young doctor, Horace Bianchon, came out; recognizing him, he offered his arm and joked about the hour and the slippery pavement. The visitor—Comte de Granville—answered with weary pride that at fifty‑five he was still hale and often walked home from the Courts or the club when not visiting.

Bianchon teased that a man of his station courted robbery by walking alone. Granville shrugged off danger with melancholy, admitting he had been spying on a shadow in the third‑floor window of Bianchon’s house. He took a morbid interest in that garret, likening it to the idle pleasure some take in watching the Palais‑Royal being finished. When Bianchon offered to tell him who lived there, Granville refused: he would not pay to learn whether the shadow was man or woman. Nine years earlier he had ceased to be surprised that old men found pastime in small things; life’s events had taught him to distrust attachment. “I grew old within a few days,” he said. He had renounced ties to living beings, clinging only to “unreasoning animals, or plants, or superficial things”—even the grace of Taglioni moved him more than human feeling. He abhorred life and insisted nothing could interest him.

Bianchon probed: did he have children? Granville answered bitterly, listing them—the eldest daughter, the Comtesse de Vandenesse; another daughter married suitably through that sister; sons respectable in law: a viscount who had been public prosecutor at Limoges and now President of the Court at Orléans, and a younger son a public prosecutor in Paris. Each had their cares; none had devoted themselves to him. If one had tried to fill the void in his breast, that child would have ruined his life by sacrificing itself to him—and why should it? Perhaps they now waited for his money. Bianchon protested; Granville, sarcastic and self‑aware, explained his philanthropy as self‑indulgence: he paid for sensations the way he gambled, seeking illusions that might warm his heart while expecting no gratitude. He would see Bianchon die without blinking and asked that Bianchon feel the same toward him. Life’s events had flowed over his heart like lava over Herculaneum—the town remained, dead.

Unable to leave him so, Bianchon insisted he could treat Granville’s malady and then, reluctantly, described the garret’s occupant. The picture was stark: a woman of about thirty whose love had become fanaticism. She adored a young man of pleasing appearance but every vice—Solvet—a gambler given to wine and women, guilty of acts the law might punish. This woman had abandoned a comfortable life and an adoring husband, the father of her children, for him. She worked day and night; he had stolen from her, even funds saved for the children’s food and clothes. Three days earlier she had sold her hair—the finest Bianchon had ever seen—and when the lover returned she could not hide the gold quickly enough. For a smile or a kiss she gave away the price of a fortnight’s subsistence. The toil had hollowed her cheeks; the children’s cries had broken her heart; she lay ill on her wretched bed that night. When Bianchon had visited, the family had nothing to eat; the children were so weak they could not even cry.

Granville, despite himself, slid his hand toward his waistcoat pocket as if to help; Bianchon begged him instead to pity the woman and help cure her passion. Granville recoiled: how could he pity a misery whose joys he might once have prized with his whole fortune? Did not thousands of dead men, thousands of sick and old men, long to barter everything for a return of youth and feeling? Bianchon murmured “Poor Caroline!” At the name Granville shuddered, gripped the doctor’s arm, and asked hoarsely whether the woman’s name was Caroline Crochard. When Bianchon confirmed it, Granville’s reaction was violent: he accused Bianchon of awakening the worst pain his heart could endure, an emotion he called a gift from hell. He vowed he knew how to pay such debts.

They had reached the Rue de la Chaussée d’Antin when a shriveled night scavenger stood by the curb. Granville asked if the scavenger ever found thousand‑franc notes and, hearing that sometimes he did, handed him such a note with a grotesque injunction: spend it in the wine shop, get drunk, start fights, beat your wife, blacken your friends’ eyes—give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist, perhaps to the police, public prosecutor, judge, and prison wardens. “Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later,” Granville said. He pointed out the scavenger’s corrupt face to Bianchon and declared he had thus “squared accounts with hell” and found a perverse pleasure in the money. As for Caroline Crochard, he pronounced coldly that she might die of hunger and thirst listening to the cries of her starving children, convinced of her lover’s baseness; he would not give a sou to rescue her. Because Bianchon had helped her, he said, he would see him no more.

Granville left Bianchon stunned and walked to the Rue Saint‑Lazare where a carriage waited. A servant informed him that his son, the attorney‑general, had been to see him an hour earlier and was waiting in his bedroom. Granville dismissed the servant, then confronted his son—in a tone at once imperious and unexpectedly tender—about coming after being ordered not to visit unless summoned.

Eugène, the younger man, spoke with tremor: that afternoon a very young man arrested in the house of a friend he had robbed had appealed to Granville, claiming to be his son. “His name?” Granville asked. “Charles Crochard,” Eugène answered. Granville waved him down: that sufficed. He stood a moment, then spoke as a father. “Charles Crochard spoke the truth. I am glad you came to me tonight, my good Eugène.” He handed his son a bundle of banknotes—“a considerable sum”—and authorized him to use it as he saw fit in the affair. He trusted him implicitly and approved whatever arrangements Eugène might make now or later.

The mood shifted. Granville’s last words of parental counsel and farewell were almost tender. He warned Eugène about the weight of marriage—“that act is the most important society requires of us”—and urged him not to undertake it lightly, to study the character of the woman he would partner with and to consult him. A lack of union between husband and wife, Granville warned—with involuntary shudder—led to terrible misfortunes and social punishment. He promised to write on the subject from Florence. Then, with a quietness that suggested this might be their last parting, he asked his son to kiss him and announced his plan: tomorrow he would beg dismissal from the King and go to Italy. The conversation closed on his wish to preserve his dignity as a father and as a former presiding officer of a supreme court of justice; he would not wish to blush before his son.

Thus the scene shows two faces of Granville: the man in the street hardened into cruelty and misanthropy, throwing a thousand francs to corrupt a scavenger and delighting in the chaos it might cause; and the father in his bedroom, handing money and last counsels to his son, preparing to depart and to write about marriage from Florence. Between those moments stands Bianchon’s account of Caroline Crochard—a portrait of sacrifice, degradation, and the brutal cost of passion—and the Comte’s icy rejection of duty toward her, even as the name awakens a torment proving he is not wholly dead to feeling. The narrative ends with Granville resolved to quit Paris and cut off a friend who had stirred his pity, while secretly acknowledging familial ties he will attempt to leave behind with money and admonition.
