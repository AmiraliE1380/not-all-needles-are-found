The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once one of the darkest and most tortuous lanes near the Hôtel de Ville, wound about the little gardens of the Paris Prefecture and ended at the Rue Martroi by an old wall since pulled down. A turnstile that gave the street its name remained until 1823, when the Municipality built a ballroom on the adjoining garden for the Duc d’Angoulême’s fête.

Even at its widest, where it opened into the Rue de la Tixeranderie, the Rue du Tourniquet was less than six feet across. In rain the gutter water ran deep at the houses’ bases, sweeping dust and refuse into corners carts could not reach; the inhabitants trusted storms to wash their miry alley. In summer the sun touched the blackness briefly, never drying the permanent damp that rose to the first story. Residents who lit lamps at five in June kept them burning through winter. A traveler coming to the Marais along the quays, past the Rue du Chaume and the Rues de l’Homme-Armé, des Billettes, and des Deux-Portes, might think he had passed through cellars all the way.

Many old Paris streets were such damp labyrinths, where antiquaries find curiosities. On the corner where the Rue du Tourniquet met the Rue de la Tixeranderie, two iron ring clamps remained, relics of chains the watch once strung nightly. The house itself testified to the unhealthiness of such dwellings: cellar arches rose about two feet above ground, the entrance required three outside steps, and the door was crowned by a closed arch whose keystone bore a female head and time-eaten arabesques. Three windows, with sills about five feet from the pavement, lit a small set of rooms facing the Rue du Tourniquet, protected by strong iron bars curved outward like a baker’s.

By day a passer-by could see little; only in July, when light strengthened, could one discern in the second room two beds hung with green serge side by side in an old-fashioned alcove. But by about three, when candles were lighted, the nearer pane showed an old woman on a stool tending a brazier to simmer a porter’s-wife stew; a few utensils hung against the wall in the twilight. An old trestle table, bare of linen, held pewter spoons and the old woman’s dish; three wretched chairs furnished the multipurpose kitchen-dining room. Above the chimney hung a small mirror, a tinder-box, three glasses, matches, and a large cracked white jug. In spite of the gloom, the floor, utensils, and fireplace testified to cleanliness and thrift.

The old woman’s pale, withered face matched the street’s darkness and mustiness. Sitting motionless, as inseparable from the house as a snail from its shell, her alert, vaguely mischievous countenance was framed in a net cap that barely covered white hair; her fine gray eyes were as quiet as the street, and wrinkles like cracks in the walls marked her brow. Whether born to poverty or fallen from better days, she seemed resigned to melancholy.

From sunrise till dark, except when cooking or buying provisions, she sat by the farther window opposite a young girl. At any hour a passer-by could see the needlewoman in an old red velvet chair, bent over an embroidery frame and stitching indefatigably. The mother sat with a green pillow and busied herself with handmade net, but with feeble sight; a spring-grip pair of antiquated spectacles rested on her nose. By night they set a lamp between them, its light concentrated by two globe-shaped water bottles, letting the elder see the network on her pillow and the younger the pattern’s delicate details.

The window’s outward bend held a box of earth on the sill where sweet peas, nasturtiums, a sickly honeysuckle, and convolvulus twined up the iron bars. These etiolated plants bore a few pale flowers, adding sadness and sweetness to the framed picture of the two figures.

Even the most selfish observer would carry away a perfect image of working-class Parisian women’s lives: the embroideress evidently lived by her needle. Many who passed the turnstile wondered how a girl could keep her color living in a cellar. A student crossing to the Quartier Latin might liken this vegetative existence to ivy clinging to chill walls, or to peasants who toil and die unknown. A house-owner, valuing the property, would ask, “What will become of those two women if embroidery goes out of fashion?”

Among the men obliged to pass this street for the Hôtel de Ville or Palais de Justice there might have been charitable souls and others with baser hopes. Some widower or dandy of forty, often glimpsing these sad lives, might have reckoned on the mother and daughter’s poverty and hoped to make the innocent workwoman his at little cost—her nimble fingers, youthful figure, and pale skin (a charm due to the sunless street) drawing admiration. Perhaps some honest clerk with a modest income, seeing diligence and purity, waited only for better prospects to unite his life to hers, to bring a man’s arm and calm affection, pale like the window flowers, to uphold the home.

Vague hope kept life in the mother’s dim gray eyes. Each morning after a frugal breakfast she took up her pillow—chiefly for show—laid her spectacles on a little mahogany worktable as old as herself, and watched from about half-past eight till ten the regular passers, noting gait, dress, and countenance as if trying to attract attention to her daughter. This small review was as good as a play to her, perhaps her sole amusement.

The daughter rarely looked up. Modesty, or the painful consciousness of poverty, kept her eyes fixed on the frame; only some exclamation from her mother showed her small features. Then a clerk in a new coat or one appearing with a woman might catch sight of the girl’s slightly upturned nose, rosy mouth, and gray eyes, bright despite toil. Late hours left pale circles beneath each eye on her fresh cheeks.

She seemed made for love and cheerfulness: two perfect arches above her eyelids, a mass of chestnut hair she might hide under like a tent, dimples in her cheeks, and a quick vivacity that was the blossom of hope. Her hair was always neatly dressed; as Paris needlewomen usually considered their toilet complete after brushing smooth and tucking the short curls at each temple against white skin. The pretty growth at the nape and a clear brown line suggested youth and charm; an observer seeing her bent over work might deem her a coquette. This promise caught more than one young man’s eye, who turned in vain hope to glimpse that modest countenance.

“Caroline, there is a new face that passes regularly by; not one of the old ones to compare with it.”

These words, spoken low by her mother one August morning in 1815, vanquished the young needlewoman’s indifference, and she looked out; the stranger was gone.

“Where has he flown to?” she asked.

“He will come back no doubt at four; I shall see him coming, and will touch your foot with mine. I am sure he will come back; he has passed the street regularly for the last three days; but his hours vary. The first day he came at six, the day before yesterday at four, yesterday as early as three. I remember seeing him occasionally some time ago. He is some clerk in the Préfet’s office who has moved to the Marais.—Why!” she exclaimed after glancing down the street, “our gentleman of the brown coat has taken to wearing a wig; how much it alters him!”

The gentleman of the brown coat seemed to close the daily procession; the old woman, spectacles on, took up her work with a sigh, glancing at her daughter with a look that mingled admiration, gratitude, hope, and pride in such a pretty child.

At about four the old lady pushed her foot against Caroline’s, and the girl looked up quickly enough to see a new actor, whose regular advent would lend variety to the scene. He was tall and thin, wore black, a man of about forty, with a certain solemnity; his piercing hazel eye met the old woman’s dull gaze and made her quake, for she felt as though he had the gift of reading hearts, or much practice in it, and his presence seemed as icy as the air of this dank street. Was the dull, sallow complexion of that ominous face due to excess of work, or to delicate health?

The old woman supplied many answers; but the next day Caroline discerned lines of long mental suffering on a brow quick to frown. His hollow cheeks bore sorrow’s seal. Though the girl’s expression at first was one of lively but innocent curiosity, it took on gentle sympathy as the stranger receded like a last relation following a funeral.

The weather was so hot and the gentleman so absent-minded that he had taken off and forgotten his hat as he passed the squalid street. Caroline noted how his brushed hair altered his countenance. The strong, uncharming impression he made was unlike any other passenger; for the first time she felt pity for someone besides herself and her mother. She said nothing to her mother’s absurd conjectures, drew her needle in silence, and only regretted not seeing him more closely, looking forward to the morrow to form a definite opinion.

Indeed it was the first time a passer-by had stirred much thought in her. She usually replied to her mother’s hypotheses with a smile; her indifference had been borne by persistent toil consuming her youth and threatening the clearness of her eyes and the bloom of her cheeks.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For two months the “Black Gentleman”—as they called him—moved erratically along the Rue du Tourniquet; he did not always pass at the same hour. The old woman sometimes saw him in the evening when he had not come in the morning. Except on the first occasion, when his glance had alarmed the mother, his eyes never rested on the odd picture in the window; he seemed lost in thought, looking at the ground or straight ahead as if reading the future in the fog. Beside two carriage-gates and a dark ironmonger’s, there were only barred windows lighting staircases, so his lack of curiosity could not be blamed on rivals; Madame Crochard was piqued to see her “Black Gentleman” always absorbed.

One mid-September morning, Caroline Crochard’s roguish face, fresh among belated flowers and faded leaves, gleamed against the room’s dark background; the scene brightened with contrast and the pretty needlewoman at work. The stranger, perhaps roused by the clatter her irritated mother made with bobbins, glanced up and studied this living picture. Their eyes met briefly but meaningfully; both knew they would think of each other. When he passed again at four, Caroline heard his step and they looked steadily at one another with evident purpose; his kind expression made him smile, and Caroline blushed, which Madame Crochard noted with satisfaction. After that afternoon the Gentleman in Black passed twice daily with rare exceptions, observed by mother and daughter. From his irregular hours they judged he was not a precise subordinate.

Through the first three winter months they saw each other twice a day for the few moments it took him to cross the stretch by their door and windows. Day by day the brief meetings took on a friendly, then fraternal, tone. They seemed to understand each other at once; by studying each other’s faces they learned them well. It became, as if, a duty of the Unknown to visit Caroline; if he failed to give the half-smile or cordial glance she expected, her day was marred. The charm of these meetings was mutual; neither could hide vexation or fatigue from the other.

“He must have had trouble yesterday,” Caroline would think when his features altered. “Oh, he has been working too hard!” she decided from another shade. He could tell when she had spent Sunday finishing a dress, when the quarter-day approached and her face darkened, when she had sat up late; he noted how the gloom that dimmed her delicate features slowly lifted as their acquaintance warmed. When winter killed the window plants and the window was closed, he observed the concentration of light at Caroline’s head, the small fire and the frosty redness of the two women’s faces showing the home’s poverty; if his face ever showed pity, the girl met it with assumed cheerfulness.

Yet their feelings remained unspoken; no incident revealed how deep they were in each other. They had never heard each other speak, and both kept at a distance as if closer acquaintance might bring disaster. Was it shyness or friendship that restrained them? Fear of encountering selfishness, or the city’s mutual distrust? Each perhaps hoped to preserve an illusion: he seeming to fear a vulgar word from lips as fresh as a flower, she feeling unworthy of a mysterious person who evidently had power and wealth.

Madame Crochard, reproachful at her daughter’s indecision, now showed sulkiness to the “Black Gentleman,” on whom until then she had smiled with benignant servility. She complained of being forced to cook at her age, of catarrh and rheumatism, and could not promise the same number of ells of net as before.

By late December, when bread was dear and the 1816 corn shortage began to bite hard, the stranger saw secret sorrow in Caroline’s face which his kind smiles could not dispel. He noticed the dimness in her eyes from long hours at night. One night near month’s end he passed the Rue du Tourniquet at one in the morning. In the silence he heard Madame Crochard’s lachrymose voice and Caroline’s sadder tone mingling with sleet. He crept along slowly and, risking trouble with the police, stopped beneath the window to listen through the largest hole in the yellow muslin curtain. He saw a sheet of paper on the table by the lamp and water-filled globes—a writ. Madame Crochard wept; Caroline’s voice was thick.

“Why be so heartbroken, mother? Monsieur Molineux will not sell us up or turn us out before I have finished this dress; only two nights more and I shall take it home to Madame Roguin.”

“And supposing she keeps you waiting as usual? And will the money for the gown pay the baker too?”

The stranger, versed in reading faces, fancied the mother’s grief false while the daughter’s was genuine; he turned away, came back, and peeped again. Madame Crochard lay in bed; Caroline bent over her frame, embroidering with indefatigable diligence. On the table with the writ lay a triangular hunch of bread, placed to sustain her through the night and remind her of the reward of industry. Tremulous with pity, the stranger slipped his purse through a cracked pane so it would fall at the girl’s feet, then fled with tingling cheeks.

Next morning the shy, melancholy stranger passed with a preoccupied look, but could not avoid Caroline’s gratitude: she had opened her window and pretended to dig in the snow-buried window-box so he would not see her only through the glass. Her eyes brimmed with tears as she bowed, saying with a look, “I can only repay you from my heart.”

The Gentleman in Black affected not to understand this gratitude. That evening, when he passed, Caroline mended the window with a sheet of paper and smiled, showing her pearly teeth like a promise. Thenceforth the Stranger went another way and was no more seen in the Rue du Tourniquet.

One early May day, as Caroline watered a honeysuckle root, she caught sight of a narrow strip of cloudless blue and said gaily to her mother, “Mamma, we must go tomorrow for a trip to Montmorency!” She had hardly spoken when the Gentleman in Black passed, sadder than ever. Caroline’s innocent look might have seemed an invitation. Indeed, the next day, at the corner of the Rue du Faubourg Saint-Denis and the Rue d’Enghien, Madame Crochard, dressed in a claret merino pelisse, silk bonnet, and striped shawl, found her Unknown standing like a man waiting for his wife. A smile lit his face at sight of Caroline in plum-colored gaiters and a white dress tossed by a breeze showing her graceful form. Her rice-straw bonnet lined with pink silk shaded a forehead white as snow; her hair parted in two brown bands gave an expression of innocence. She seemed lit from heaven; when she saw him, radiant hope brightened her. The Stranger, in doubt perhaps whether to be her escort, had his decision made by her delight. He hired a vehicle for Saint-Leu-Taverny and offered seats beside him. The mother accepted, but soon complained of the heat and her cough, making civil speeches about being an inconvenience. By Saint-Denis she seemed fast asleep; her snores seemed dubious to the Stranger, who frowned suspiciously.

“Oh, she is fast asleep,” Caroline said guilelessly; “she never ceased coughing all night. She must be very tired.”

Her companion said nothing but looked at the girl with a smile that seemed to mean, “Poor child, you little know your mother!”

Despite distrust, as they passed the poplar avenue toward Eaubonne, the Stranger perhaps loosened by the bright sky, pure country air, and the first green scents, entered on a conversation with his young companion as aimless as the branches’ sway, as devious as butterflies in the azure air, illogical as the fields’ murmur, and full of a mysterious love. At that season the country is like a bride in her marriage robe; no heart remains unmoved on leaving the Marais for Montmorency’s smiling valley, lifting a charmed gaze to eyes as infinite and loving as the view.

He found Caroline sprightly rather than witty, affectionate but ill educated; her giddy laugh hid genuine feeling. Under his shrewd questioning she spoke with the effusiveness of the lower classes, not guarded as people of the world. Her frankness brightened his face, renewing a youthful look beneath former sadness. Gradually his sternness softened to a handsome youthfulness that made Caroline proud and happy. Her light prattle lifted the last veil from his real youth and character; he seemed to relinquish ideas that haunted him and to show the liveliness beneath his solemnity.

Their talk grew so intimate that by the time the carriage stopped at the straggling houses of Saint-Leu, Caroline was calling him Monsieur Roger. Then, for the first time, the old mother awoke.

“Caroline, she has heard everything!” Roger whispered suspiciously.

Caroline replied with an exquisite smile of disbelief, dispelling the cloud his fear of some plot had caused. Madame Crochard, amazed at nothing, approved everything and followed them into the park, where the young pair wandered through meadows and fragrant copses famed by Queen Hortense.

“How lovely!” Caroline cried when, on the green ridge where Montmorency’s forest begins, she saw the valley: combes sheltering villages, a horizon of blue hills, church towers, meadows sending a murmur like the ocean. They walked beside an artificial stream to the Swiss valley and its chalet once sheltering Hortense and Napoleon. Caroline sat with pious reverence on the mossy bench where emperors had rested. Madame Crochard wished to see a rustic bridge and left them, saying she would not go out of sight.

“What, poor child!” Roger asked, “have you never longed for wealth and luxury? Have you never wished to wear the beautiful dresses you embroider?”

“It would not be truth,” Caroline answered, “if I said I never think how happy the rich must be. Oh yes! I often fancy, especially as I fall asleep, how glad I should be to see my poor mother no longer compelled to go out, whatever the weather, to buy provisions at her age. I should like her to have a servant to bring her coffee sweetened with white sugar, and to see her wearing out her eyes over her favorite novels rather than twisting bobbins. And a little good wine. In short, I should like her comfortable—she is so good.”

“Then she has shown you great kindness?”

“Oh yes,” the girl said with conviction, then paused as they watched Madame Crochard shaking her finger at them from the bridge. Caroline added, “She sold her last silver forks to apprentice me to the old maid who taught me to embroider. And my poor father! What did she not go through to make him end his days in happiness!” She shivered at remembrance and hid her face; “Well! come! let us forget past sorrows!” she rallied, blushing when Roger too seemed moved but daring not to look at him.

“What was your father?” he inquired.

“He was an opera-dancer before the Revolution,” she replied simply, “and my mother sang in the chorus. My father, who led stage figures, was present at the siege of the Bastille. Recognized by some assailants, who asked whether he could lead a real attack, since he led on stage, he accepted, led the insurgents, and was rewarded with the rank of captain in the Sambre-et-Meuse army, where he rose to colonel. But at Lützen he was so badly wounded that, after a year’s suffering, he died in Paris. The Bourbons returned; my mother got no pension, and we sank into such misery that we had to work. She has been ailing, poor dear, and I have never known her so little resigned; she complains much, and I cannot wonder, for she once knew an easy life. I cannot pine for delights I have never known; I have but one wish.”

“And that is?” Roger asked eagerly.

“That women may continue to wear embroidered net dresses, so I may never lack work.”

Her frankness touched him and softened his look toward Madame Crochard as she slowly returned.

“Well, children, have you had a long talk?” the old woman asked with half-laughing indulgence. “When I think, Monsieur Roger, that the ‘little Corporal’ has sat where you are sitting,” she went on, “Poor man! how my husband worshiped him! Ah! Crochard did well to die; he could not have borne to think of him where they have sent him!”

Roger put his finger to his lips; the good woman went on very gravely, unhooking a bit of her bodice to show a ribbon and cross tied round her neck by black ribbon, “they shall never hinder me from wearing what he gave to my poor Crochard, and I will have it buried with me.”

At that seditious speech, Roger suddenly rose, and they returned to the village through park walks. He left briefly to order a meal at the best Taverny eating-house, then returned to fetch them and led them through the forest alleys.

Dinner was cheerful. Roger was no longer the melancholy figure of the Rue du Tourniquet but a confiding young man ready to take life as it came, like the two hardworking women who might lack bread the next day; his smile was affectionate and childlike.

After the meal ended with a few glasses of champagne, Roger proposed they join the village ball under the chestnuts, where he and Caroline danced together. Their hands met sympathetically, their hearts beat with the same hopes, and under the blue sky and slanting, rosy sunset beams, their eyes sparkled with fires that to them made heaven pale. The power of an idea and desire seemed boundless; nothing seemed impossible. In such moments the soul foresees nothing but happiness. The day had created memories neither had known before.

“So the day is already at an end!” Caroline sighed when the dance finished, noticing Roger’s face shade into sadness.

“Why should you not be as happy in Paris as here?” she asked. “Is happiness only found at Saint-Leu? I think I can never be unhappy anywhere.”

Roger, struck by her unguarded words, felt what she did; for the first time since their first glance, they shared the same idea. Frightened by the mutual silence, they went to the carriage. Before entering, they playfully took hands and ran down the dark avenue in front of Madame Crochard. When they could no longer see the white net cap, Roger, in a tremulous voice, said, “Caroline!”

Startled, the girl drew back a few steps, understanding the invitation; she held out her hand, which he passionately kissed, then she withdrew it quickly, standing on tiptoe to see her mother.

Madame Crochard affected blindness, playing the supernumerary, as if by a remnant of her old stage parts.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The adventures of these two young people were not continued in the Rue du Tourniquet. To see Roger and Caroline again we must leap into modern Paris, where newly built houses contain apartments seemingly made for newlyweds on their honeymoon. There the paper and paint are as fresh as the couple, and the decorations are in blossom like their love; everything suits youthful notions and ardent wishes.

Halfway down the Rue Taitbout, in a house whose stone walls were still white, where the hall columns and doorway were spotless and the inner walls shone with the neat painting our recent intimacy with English ways had brought into fashion, there was, on the second floor, a small set of rooms fitted as though the architect had known their use. A simple airy antechamber, with a stucco dado, opened into a combined drawing- and dining-room. From the drawing-room came a pretty bedroom, with a bathroom beyond. Every chimney-shelf bore a fine mirror elegantly framed; doors were crowded with tasteful arabesques and the cornices were in the best style. Any amateur would have discerned the sense of distinction and decorative fitness that marks modern French architects’ work.

For more than a month Caroline had lived in this apartment, furnished by an upholsterer guided by an artist. A short description of the principal room will show the pleasures Caroline enjoyed when Roger installed her. Gray hangings trimmed with green silk adorned her bedroom; seats covered with light-colored wool sateen were easy and fashionable; a simple inlaid chest of drawers held toilet treasures; a matching writing-table served for love-letters on scented paper; an antique-draped bed, with elegant muslin hangings, suggested thoughts of love; the window curtains, drab silk with green fringe, were half drawn to subdue the light; a bronze clock represented Love crowning Psyche; and a Gothic red-ground carpet set off the rest. A small dressing-table before a long glass was where the needlewoman sat, out of patience with Plaisir, the famous hairdresser.

“Do you think you will have done today?” she asked.

“Your hair is so long and so thick, madame,” replied Plaisir.

Caroline smiled; the man’s flattery doubtless revived the memory of Roger’s praises of her hair.

When the hairdresser finished, a waiting-maid came to counsel her about the dress Roger would like best. It was the beginning of September 1816 and cold; she chose a green grenadine trimmed with chinchilla. Dressed, Caroline flew into the drawing-room and opened a window, then stepped onto the elegant balcony that adorned the front. There she stood, arms crossed, in a charming attitude—not to display herself to passersby but to look out on the Boulevard at the foot of the Rue Taitbout. This side view, like the actor’s peephole in a stage drop-scene, let her catch glimpses of elegant carriages and crowds swept past with the rapidity of Ombres Chinoises. Not knowing whether Roger would arrive in a carriage or on foot, she watched both pedestrians and tilburies—the light cabs introduced by the English.

After a quarter of an hour, neither her keen eye nor her heart had announced him. Scorn and love swept over her face; disdain and indifference for all others showed as if the passersby were ants. Her gray eyes, bright with fun, now flamed. Given over to passion, she avoided admiration as much as some cultivate it; she cared for no eyes below her, only one face and one thought. When the spotted head of a certain bay horse crossed the strip between the rows of houses, Caroline shivered and stood on tiptoe, hoping to recognize the white traces and the tilbury’s color. It was he.

Roger turned the corner, saw the balcony, whipped his horse to a gallop, and stopped at the bronze-green door he knew as well as his master did. The maid, having heard her mistress’ exclamation, opened at once. Roger rushed up, clasped Caroline to him, and embraced her with the effusive feeling natural to lovers who rarely meet. They went, by common impulse and with arms about each other, into the quiet fragrant bedroom; a settee stood ready by the fire. For a moment they looked at each other in silence, expressing happiness by clasped hands and a fond gaze.

“Yes, it is he!” she said at last. “Yes, it is you. Do you know, I have not seen you for three long days—an age!—But what is the matter? You are unhappy.”

“My poor Caroline—”

“There, you see! ‘poor Caroline’—”

“No, no, do not laugh, my darling; we cannot go to the Feydeau Theatre together this evening.”

Caroline pouted briefly, then the pout vanished.

“How absurd I am! How can I think of the play when I see you? Is not the sight of you the only spectacle I care for?” she cried, pushing her fingers through Roger’s hair.

“I am obliged to go to the Attorney-General’s. We have a knotty case. He met me in the great hall at the Palais; and as I am to plead, he asked me to dine with him. But, my dearest, you can go to the theatre with your mother, and I will join you if the meeting breaks up early.”

“To the theatre without you!” she cried in amazement; “enjoy any pleasure you do not share! O my Roger! you do not deserve a kiss,” she added, throwing her arms round his neck with artless passion.

“Caroline, I must go home and dress. The Marais is some way off, and I still have business to finish.”

“Take care what you are saying, monsieur,” she interrupted. “My mother says that when a man begins to talk about his business, he is ceasing to love.”

“Caroline! Am I not here? Have I not stolen this hour from my pitiless—”

“Hush!” she said, laying a finger on his mouth. “Don’t you see I am in jest?”

Back in the drawing-room, Roger’s eye fell on an object brought home that morning by the cabinetmaker. Caroline’s old rosewood embroidery frame, by which she and her mother had earned their bread when they lived in the Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, had been refitted and polished, and a net dress of elaborate design already stretched on it.

“Well, then, my dear, I shall do some work this evening. As I stitch, I shall fancy myself gone back to those early days when you used to pass by without a word, though not without a glance; the days when the memory of your look kept me awake all night. Oh my dear old frame—the best piece of furniture in my room, though you did not give it me!” she said, seating herself on Roger’s knees; he, overcome, had dropped into a chair. “Listen. All I can earn by my work I mean to give to the poor. You have made me rich. How I love that pretty home at Bellefeuille, less for what it is than because you gave it me! But tell me, Roger, I should like to call myself Caroline de Bellefeuille—can I? Is it legal or permissible?”

At Roger’s small affirmative grimace—for he hated the name Crochard—Caroline jumped for joy and clapped her hands.

“I feel,” she said, “as if I should more especially belong to you. Usually a woman gives up her own name and takes her husband’s—” An idea forced itself on her and made her blush. She took Roger’s hand and led him to the open piano. “Listen,” she added, “I can play my sonata now like an angel!” Her fingers were already running over the ivory keys when she felt herself seized round the waist.

“Caroline, I ought to be far from hence!”

“You insist on going? Well, go,” she said, with a pretty pout, but she smiled as she looked at the clock and exclaimed joyfully, “At any rate, I have detained you a quarter of an hour!”

“Goodbye, Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille,” he said, with the gentle irony of love.

She kissed him and saw him to the door; when his steps had died on the stairs she ran to the balcony to watch him get into the tilbury, gather up the reins, catch a parting look, hear the crack of his whip and the sound of wheels on the stones, watch the handsome horse, the master’s hat, the tiger’s gold lace, and at last stand gazing long after the dark corner of the street had eclipsed the vision.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Five years after Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille settled into her pretty house in the Rue Taitbout, we find her in a domestic scene that binds two people deeply in affection. In the blue drawing-room, a four-year-old boy, Charles, beat furiously on a rocking-horse, then tiptoed over to lift the muslin veil from his baby sister Eugenie, who slept on their mother’s knee. Caroline, now about twenty-four, was the very picture of matured, contented beauty. She had blossomed under a life of serene happiness. Devoted to obeying her beloved Roger and to the duties of motherhood, she had acquired accomplishments—playing the piano and singing—yet had not been absorbed by the empty polish of fashionable society. Her chief delight was to keep close to her children, to give them the strict, minute, loving training she believed essential.

Caroline had accepted the limited, secret nature of her relation with Roger from the first. Even after six years of intimacy she still privately called him only “Roger,” living within the small enchanted circle in which all her happiness lay. A print of Psyche lighting her lamp, a reminder of the condition set for her happiness, hung in her room; she had never pressed Roger for more or for a public life. She had refused luxuries he offered, content with the pleasures of balcony-watching for his cab, shared theatre nights, and country excursions. Her days were poor in incident but rich in joy.

On the anniversary of their first decisive outing, the 6th of May, Caroline prepared the house with small attentions and placed the infant in her cot. When Roger arrived in his carriage, he received lovingly affectionate greetings. He kissed his daughter’s forehead and drew from his pocket a deed of gift covering securities in State funds—the marriage portion for Mademoiselle Eugenie de Bellefeuille. Caroline gratefully accepted the paper but remarked that Eugenie’s endowment of three thousand francs a year was double Charles’s fifteen hundred. Roger explained without hesitation: a son would become a man and should be spurred to industry by modest means; a daughter must have a small fortune. Later that evening, as the household enjoyed a magic-lantern show for Charles, the infant awoke for nourishment, and in the lamplight Roger lingered, admiring Caroline’s beauty and the domestic scene. Yet he would leave, feigning work to be done elsewhere—“duty before all things”—and Caroline submitted with gentle sorrow, still bound to the quiet sacrifice her lot required. Roger added, with something like mischief, that he had days’ holiday and was supposed to be far from Paris.

A few days after the May observance, Caroline hurried to the Rue Saint-Louis in the Marais upon being summoned to her mother, Madame Crochard, who was failing from complications of catarrh and rheumatism. In the neat second-floor rooms, three old women who had long been companions of the widow had already arranged for a priest; they had, between games and walks, persuaded the infirm woman into scruples and penitential hopes. Their motives were mingled with piety and a practical interest: none of them knew for certain whether the widow had an heir, and the thought of a legacy had led them to scheme. Francoise, the maid, and the three old cronies had agreed to fetch a confessor in time, treating the dying woman with the kind of indulgent attentions that also serve private ends.

When Caroline arrived breathless, the scene was in progress. The Abbe Fontanon, a small, complacent priest with mean features and a soft air of self-satisfaction, sat with Madame Crochard. Francoise hovered, torn between faithful service and petty fretting about past mistresses’ legacies. The three dowagers waited in the sitting-room, exchanging covert glances about the potential “pickings” of the widow’s estate. Fontanon encouraged pious sentiment and accepted the woman’s confession. At one point the old widow drew forth the Imperial Cross of the Legion of Honor, an odd relic the priest recoiled from. Through feeble speech she warned Caroline: the priest had been led to ask her name by someone. She gasped that the priest had made her confess the benefactor’s name, cried out that she had nothing to leave, and with a last twitch of cunning died. Francoise announced that Madame Crochard had a daughter and no capital—only an annuity—news that threw the three old crones into private alarm and scheming. Their disappointment and their nods of mutual suspicion showed their petty, grasping natures. The household’s small hypocrisies and private calculations framed the dying woman’s last hours; death itself seemed to mock the contrivances and greed of those around her.

To enter the main interests of the tale, the narrative shifts back to events that form the other half of the story. In November 1805, a young barrister—about twenty-six—was leaving the High Chancellor’s hotel at three in the morning, ill-at-ease without a coach to take him home in the frost. He was Monsieur de Granville, nephew of an influential man and an up-and-coming lawyer whose recent defenses had raised his reputation. Finding himself aided unexpectedly by the Chief Justice, who saw to his return in the Chancellor’s carriage, he had an encouraging conversation: high officials, including the Chancellor and Cambacérès, meant to promote him to a judgeship in the lower courts or in the Imperial Court. They urged him to work, to avoid public patronage that might provoke rivals, and promised their support. As he stepped into his lodging afterward, he received a letter from his father that would redirect his life.

The epistle announced a chance to marry advantageously: Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems had become an only child and might inherit some forty thousand francs a year; the father proclaimed that he had prepared the way. He calculated dowries and inheritances, offered a hundred thousand francs as his own contribution, and suggested that with such a match and the judgeship he might climb to be a senator, nourished by the political protection he could count on. Granville lay awake with ambitious visions. The young man, who had been thinking also of Angélique as “his little wife” since childhood, found the prospect seductive: position, fortune, and the possibility of a political career.

By seven next morning he left for Bayeux by diligence. The sight of the cathedral spires and the familiar town moved him; yet on arriving with his father at the Bontems’ austere house in the Rue Teinture, he felt the chill of a life ruled by pious ritual and convent-like restraint. The house’s dark panels, polished floor, austere religious images, and methodical cleanliness offended the metropolitan habitus he had taken to; the contrast between Parisian opulence and this puritanical quiet was sharp. The Count de Granville explained that Madame Bontems was a devout bigot, priest-ridden and extravagant in her religious benefactions—restoring chapels, donating vestments, and displaying reliquaries—so that her house was a kind of “Holy Land.” Yet the Count also hinted he had prevented the loss of valuable pictures to the Church. He told his son that unless he married Angélique, the girl might be lost to clerical influence and forced into a kind of lifelong ascetic martyrdom. The Count had arranged banns and the marriage settlement; the old woman’s vanity enjoyed the idea of linking to noble Granville blood. He advised marriage as the only way to rescue the girl from the confessor’s grip and to carry her to Paris where life and society would cure her excessive devotion.

Granville was torn. He recognized another course: to accept the judgeship promised by the Chancellor and wait to secure a better or more independent marriage later; fifty thousand francs more from his father could free him to remain a bachelor with an income comparable to that the Bontems would provide. Yet his father replied with the complacent scorn of the old emigre milieu at any talk of modern civic notions; he found the son’s prudence strangely lacking in worldly sense.

A quiet page announced that the ladies of the Congregation of the Sacred Heart would process that evening; the Count, thinking of diverting his son and of the prospect of seeing pretty women, suggested they go. Inside the cathedral, in the dim light and slow incense, a voice so sweet as to stand out among the choir’s many voices struck Granville. He recognized that voice and followed it with his eyes: under a white bonnet, a young figure sang with a purity that transfixed him. Angelique’s pale gold hair, delicate features, and restrained expression captivated him; she seemed at once modest and noble. When she raised her face she blushed slightly upon catching his gaze, then resumed singing with composed devotion.

By the end of the service Granville could not keep from presenting himself to “his little wife.” In the porch he and his father bowed to Madame Bontems; the mother swelled with pride at the alliance. The Count’s impatience and his son’s ardor were balanced against the reality of the austere household where the girl had been raised. Yet the scene in the cathedral, the sight of Angelique’s sweetness, and the father’s encouragement conspired to sharpen Granville’s wish to secure both the match and the benefit it promised.

Thus the two threads are set: Caroline’s simple, secret domestic happiness and the consequences of a relationship in which her mother’s estate and the small annuity are objects of others’ greed; and the ambitious young Granville, propelled by patronage and parental calculation toward a marriage that promises fortune but confronts religious zeal and family pressure. The narrative places these intimate domestic tableaux against the wider currents of social manoeuvring—clerical influence, patronage at the highest level of the Imperial government, and old aristocratic instinct to maintain rank through advantageous marriage. The deaths, confessions, and private calculations in Caroline’s neighborhood show how financial considerations and social respectability around a small estate can twist human relations, while Granville’s upward career illustrates how patronage, family ambitions, and personal desires combine to shape destinies.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For about a fortnight before their wedding the Vicomte de Granville frequented the gloomy drawing-room where Mademoiselle Bontems lived, bent on testing Angelique’s character; after their first meeting his prudence governed him. He always found her at a little West Indian-wood table marking trousseau linen. Angelique never spoke first on religion. If the young lawyer toyed with her handsome rosary in its green velvet bag or laughed at a relic attached to it, she would quietly take it back and replace it without remark. When Granville made mischievous comments about religious observances she met them with a steady, obstinate smile.

“You must either believe nothing, or believe everything the Church teaches,” she answered him once. “Would you wish to have a woman without a religion as the mother of your children? No. What man may dare to judge between disbelievers and God? How then can I blame what the Church allows?”

She spoke with fervent charity; the conviction in her eyes sometimes tempted Granville toward her beliefs. But he misread desire’s enchantment for love’s depth: beauty and apparent unity of conscience seduced him. Young men often infer moral perfection from physical beauty; had Angelique shown warmer feeling, love might have withered less quickly. Hidden rigour was hard to detect.

During that fortnight Granville read Angelique like a book and came to admire her gentleness; he thanked Madame Bontems for implanting religious principles that made the daughter resilient. At the contract-signing Madame Bontems exacted from her son-in-law a solemn promise to respect his wife’s religious practices: to allow her perfect liberty of conscience, let her take communion, attend church and confession as often as she wished, and never control her choice of spiritual advisers. Angelique’s innocent look and the Abbe Fontanon’s pale, approving smile secured the pledge; the old Count whistled an old tune.

A few days after the wedding Granville and his wife went to Paris, his appointment as public prosecutor at the Supreme Court calling him. Angelique used honeymoon influence to prevail on him to rent a large ground-floor apartment at the corner of the Vieille Rue du Temple and Rue Neuve Saint-Francois, chiefly because it was near the Rue d’Orleans church and a small chapel in Rue Saint-Louis. The Marais was near the Palais de Justice and familiar lawyers; the house had a large garden and good stables for a young couple and children. Granville had preferred the Chaussee d’Antin nearer theatres and amusements, but yielded.

Busy with heavy duties he gave himself to furnishing a private study and left the house’s decoration to his wife, ashamed to deprive her of taste though he rarely shared it. He was startled, however, by an arid solemnity pervading the rooms: panels carved with hollowed circles and arabesques, heavy coloring, dark green velvet benches, a paper imitating mossy stone, black-and-white marble pavement, an antique-pattern lamp in the hall — all made a gloomy first impression. A handsome though not new barometer emphasized the void. He observed his wife so pleased by a red braid binding the cotton curtains and a strictly decent statue on a large Gothic stove that he lacked courage to contradict her. He excused her choices as provincial naivety rather than admit dealers had likely foisted old-fashioned goods on a tasteless little devotee.

Other rooms were worse. The prevailing academic style in French art had no favour here; the square drawing-room remained Louis XV in white and tarnished gold with hideous garlands; consoles, clocks, and candelabra bore military trophies at odds with delicate arabesques. Angelique’s bigotry, with its tiresome humility and pride, recoiled from light, cheerful colours; she seemed to believe brown and purple befitted a magistrate’s dignity and feared any luxury that might suggest vice. Granville despaired; seeing her regret at his lack of enthusiasm he mistook it for affection rather than wounded self-conceit.

“Happiness, dear Angelique, does not depend on a more or less elegant piece of furniture; it depends on the wife’s sweetness, gentleness, and love,” he protested.

“Why, it is my duty to love you,” she answered mildly, “and I can have no more delightful duty to carry out.”

From April, when they married, until early winter their life was outwardly one of perfect union. Love and heavy work made Granville indifferent to many household matters. If on a Friday he craved meat, Angelique, forbidden by the Gospel to lie, would cloak her intention under pretext and even scold the cook. Young lawyers did not keep every fast and vigil; Granville at first did not notice the regularity of Lenten meals, often disguised with tasteful fish and game. On weekdays he did not know whether his wife went to Mass; on Sundays he accompanied her indulgently. Theatres were unbearable in summer heat, and new love made him less exacting about amusements. So he failed to perceive the coldness, dignity and reserve of a woman who might well be a bigot waiting for love in folded arms.

Granville considered himself happy until November 1808, when a decisive event occurred. The Canon of Bayeux Cathedral, a man who had once governed Madame Bontems’ and her daughter’s conscience, came to Paris seeking a church and perhaps a bishopric. He was alarmed to find Angelique already so altered by Paris and strove to bring her back to his austere fold. Frightened by this provincial priest of inflexible rigour, Madame de Granville did penance and returned from what he and her old companions viewed as her Jansenist errors.

The particulars that insensibly wrecked the household are tedious to recount in full; a few facts suffice. The first serious misunderstanding concerned society invitations. Angelique accepted solemn functions but long declined balls, excusing herself with headache. One evening, impatient with these habitual refusals, Granville destroyed a written invitation to a Councillor of State’s ball and gave his wife only a verbal one; her apparent health that night encouraged him to take her to the entertainment. On returning he reproached her.

“My dear,” he said, “your position, your rank, and fortune impose duties from which no divine law can relieve you. Are you not your husband’s pride? You are required to go to balls when I go, and to appear becomingly.”

“And what is so disastrous in my dress?” she asked.

“It is your manner,” he answered. “When a young man approaches you look so serious that a spiteful person might think you doubtful of your own virtue. You seem to fear lest a smile should undo you. The world, my dear, is not a convent. As to dress, you wear three rows of net frills that cover your throat to your chin. You look as if you had instructed your dressmaker to destroy the graceful line of your shoulders. Your bust is wrapped in so many folds that people laughed at your affectation of prudery. You would be grieved if I repeated the ill-natured remarks.”

“Those who admire such obscenity will not have to bear the burden if we sin,” she retorted.

“And you did not dance?” he asked.

“I shall never dance,” she replied.

“If I tell you you ought to dance!” he said sharply. “Yes, you ought to follow fashions, to wear flowers and diamonds. Remember, we are rich and obliged to keep up the luxury that encourages manufacturers rather than handing money to clergy as alms.”

“You talk as a statesman!” she mocked.

“And you as a priest,” he retorted.

Their argument grew bitter. Angelique’s sweet voice masked an obstinacy born of clerical influence. When he reproached her, she appealed to the rights secured by his promise and claimed her spiritual director forbade balls; he insisted the priest overstepped Church regulations. The quarrel turned theological when Granville proposed the theatre; in defiance, Angelique referred the question in writing to Rome: could a woman wear low gowns and attend plays and balls without risking salvation?

Pope Pius VII replied strongly, condemning his daughter’s recalcitrancy and rebuking the meddling priest. His letter, a treatise on conjugal duties that might have been dictated by Fenelon, affirmed a wife should go where her husband takes her and that even if she sins by his command she would not be ultimately held answerable. These words only inflamed Angelique and her director, who accused the Pope of laxity.

Before this letter arrived Granville had discovered his wife’s strict fasting and countermanded it by ordering his servants to serve him meat daily. It is a particular offence to be compelled to do freely what one might otherwise do voluntarily; of all tyrannies the most odious robs the soul of the merit of its thoughts and deeds.

Soon Granville ceased inviting friends or giving dinners; the house might have been shrouded in crape. A mistress’s bigotry creates its own atmosphere, and in such houses the servants, chosen for piety, wore sanctimonious faces; bigots recommend like-minded servants to one another. Outsiders sensed an indescribable squalor: an air of avarice, secrecy, and the dank scent of cold incense. Methodical meanness and narrowness of thought are summed in one word—Bigotry. In these homes bigotry is written on furniture, prints, and faces; speech and silence alike are infected. Stiffness and formality pervade everything; the lady perches on a throne of ice.

One morning Granville perceived with grief that bigotry had invaded his home. Different causes produce similar effects; dulness builds walls of brass around these houses, enclosing the desert’s horrors. Home became not a tomb but a worse thing—a convent. Standing at the center of this icy sphere the lawyer studied his wife dispassionately. He saw narrow-mindedness in the low hair on her forehead, a rigid regularity in her features that turned his earlier enchantment to hatred. Intuition told him those thin lips might one day say, “My dear, it is for your good!”

Angelique’s complexion took on a dull pallor and austere expression that killed joy. Had ascetic pharisaism, not Christian piety, wrought this change? Beauty without expression is an imposture. Her imperturbable smile seemed a ritual formula of contentment; her charity offended because it was mechanical, and her soulless beauty became monstrous. She acted on duty, not feeling.

Some faults a wife may yield to experience or a husband’s gentle warnings, but false religious ideas are immutable. The apotheosis of egotism—Self beyond the grave—triumphs in such souls. Thus even the Pope was censured at the tribunal of the priest and the young devotee. To be always in the right consumes every other feeling in these tyrannous spirits.

A secret struggle had been going on, and Granville soon wearied of a battle without end. What man can endure a hypocritically affectionate face and categorical resistance to his slightest wishes? What is to be done with a wife who takes advantage of his passion to shelter her coldness, who prepares ecstatically to play martyr and looks on her husband as a scourge from God—a means of flagellation that may spare her purgatory? Women who make virtue hateful by defying Saint John’s “Love one another” are hard to bear.

If a bonnet condemned to remain in a milliner’s window existed, Granville was certain to see it on his wife’s head; if a hideous fabric were found, she would select it. Want of taste seemed inseparable from false pietism.

Thus in a home needing the fullest sympathy Granville had no true companionship. He went alone to parties and theatres; nothing in his house appealed to him. A huge crucifix hung between his bed and Angelique’s, figurative of his destiny: a Man-God killed in the prime of life and beauty. The ivory of that cross was less cold than Angelique crucifying her husband under the plea of virtue. One Ash Wednesday the pale form of Fasting and Total Abstinence rose and remained a daily presence—and Granville did not deem it wise to write to the Pope and take the Consistory’s opinion on observances.

His misfortune prevented complaint: he had a pretty young wife attached to duty and virtuous—admirable in public. She had a child every year, nursed them herself, and brought them up in strict principles; the old matrons of her circle admired her piety and regarded her as a martyr. They blamed not her scruples but the husband’s barbarous philoprogenitiveness.

Granville, worn by work and bereft of conjugal consolation, by thirty-two had sunk into the Slough of Despond. He hated life. Not wishing to set an example of dissipation, he sought to deaden feeling by hard study and began a great book on law. But he could not find monastic peace. When Angelique saw him forsake society to work at home, she tried to convert him. She wept thinking he might die impenitent and could not rest until she had tried to snatch him from eternal fires. Thus Granville became the object of the mean ideas, vacuous arguments, and narrow views by which she—thinking she had achieved a victory—aimed to draw him back within the Church’s pale.

This final effort was the last straw. What can be more intolerable than the blind struggle of a bigot’s obstinacy against a lawyer’s acumen? What more terrible than acrimonious pin-pricks to which a passionate soul prefers a dagger-thrust? Granville neglected his home. Everything was unendurable. His children, broken by their mother’s frigid despotism, dared not accompany him to the play; he could never give them pleasure without punishment from their terrible mother. His loving nature withered into indifference, a selfishness worse than death. He saved his boys from this atmosphere by sending them early to school and insisting on his right to train them; he seldom interfered with daughters, resolving they should marry as soon as possible.

Had he sought violent measures he could find no justification; his wife, backed by dowagers, would have condemned him publicly. Thus Granville had no choice but to live in complete isolation; crushed by misery, he avoided intimacy with worldly women, having no hope of consolation.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The improving history of this melancholy household produced no notable events between 1806 and 1825. Madame de Granville remained the same after losing her husband’s affection. She paid for Masses, beseeching God and the Saints to reveal what faults displeased her husband and to show her how to win him back; yet the more fervent her prayers, the less Granville was seen at home.

For about five years, having obtained a high judicial post, Granville lived in the entresol to avoid his wife. Every morning a little scene took place—one that, if evil tongues be believed, is repeated in many households broken by incompatibility or vice. At eight a housekeeper, nunlike in appearance, rang at Granville’s door. Admitted to the room next to his study, she always repeated the same message to the footman, always in the same tone:

“Madame would be glad to know whether Monsieur le Comte has had a good night, and if she is to have the pleasure of his company at breakfast.”

“Monsieur presents his compliments to Madame la Comtesse,” the valet would answer after speaking with his master, “and begs her to hold him excused; important business compels him to be in court this morning.”

A minute later the woman returned and asked whether she might see the Count before he went out.

“He is gone,” was always the reply, though often his carriage still waited.

This dialogue by proxy became ceremonial. Granville’s servant, a favorite with his master and the cause of quarrels for his irreligious, dissipated conduct, sometimes even entered the master’s room when the Count was absent and came back with the same formula.

The injured wife watched for her husband’s return, standing on the steps like an embodiment of remorse. The petty aggressiveness at the root of a monastic temper formed Madame de Granville’s character; she was thirty-five and looked forty. When forced by decency to speak to her husband or to dine at home, she inflicted her company with acid-sweet remarks and the intolerable dulness of her narrow circle, trying to put him in the wrong before servants and charitable friends.

When the post of President in a provincial court was offered to the Comte de Granville—an appointment that would have been a stepping-stone to a peer’s seat—he begged to remain in Paris. This refusal, known only to the Keeper of the Seals, provoked extraordinary conjectures among the Countess’s intimates and her director. Granville, a wealthy man of a leading Norman family, earning a hundred thousand francs a year, abandoning his great book on Law and the prospect of promotion, and living dissipatedly for six years, seemed inexplicable. The Countess’s confessor, who eyed a bishopric as much through family influence as service to his association, sought to insinuate calumnies: perhaps Granville feared provincial life because he would have to lead a regular, moral life and live with the Countess—unless some illicit connection had alienated him. The sanctimonious woman accepted these hints, and they destroyed her remaining illusions.

Ignorant of the world and of love’s follies, Angélique could not conceive of a marriage unlike the one that had estranged her husband; she believed him incapable of crime. When he ceased to demand anything of her, she supposed the calm was natural; having given him all the love of her heart, the priest’s suggestions undermined her convictions. She defended her husband, yet could not erase the suspicion so ingeniously sown.

These alarms wrecked her weak mind and made her ill; low fever consumed her. These events occurred during Lent 1822; she would not omit her austerities and fell into a decline that endangered her life. Granville’s indifference added to her torment; his care was perfunctory, like that of a nephew obliged to an old uncle.

Though the Countess had given up nagging and tried to greet her husband with affection, the bigot’s sharpness showed through; one speech could undo a week’s progress.

By late May, spring and more nourishing food restored a little of her strength. One morning after Mass she sat on a garden bench, where the sun recalled early days of married life, and she reviewed the years to find where she had failed as wife and mother. She was interrupted by the Abbé Fontanon in obvious agitation.

“Has any misfortune befallen you, Father?” she asked.

“Ah! I only wish,” cried the priest, “that all the woes inflicted on you by the hand of God were visited on me; but, my admirable friend, there are trials to which you can but bow.”

“Can any worse punishments await me than those with which Providence crushes me by making my husband the instrument of His wrath?”

“You must prepare yourself,” said Fontanon, “for yet worse mischief than we or your pious friends have conceived.”

“Then I may thank God,” said the Countess, “for using you as messenger of His will.”

“He measures your sufferings by the strength of your resignation and the weight of your sins.”

“Speak; I am ready to hear!” she said, looking up to heaven. “Speak, Monsieur Fontanon.”

“For seven years Monsieur Granville has lived in sin with a concubine, by whom he has two children; and on this adulterous connection he has spent more than five hundred thousand francs, which ought to have belonged to his legitimate family.”

“I must see it to believe it!” cried the Countess.

“Far be it from you!” the Abbé exclaimed. “You must forgive, my daughter, and wait in patience and prayer till God enlightens your husband; unless, indeed, you choose to use the remedies offered by human laws.”

The long conversation that followed produced an extraordinary change in the Countess. She abruptly dismissed the priest, summoned her servants—who were alarmed by her flushed face and frantic energy—ordered her carriage and countermanded it, changed her mind twenty times in an hour; but at last, about three, as if resolved, she went out, leaving the household amazed at the transformation.

“Is the Count coming home to dinner?” she asked his servant, whom she would never otherwise speak to.

“No, madame.”

“Did you go with him to the Courts this morning?”

“Yes, madame.”

“And to-day is Monday?”

“Yes, madame.”

“Then do the Courts sit on Mondays nowadays?”

“Devil take you!” cried the man, as his mistress drove off after saying to the coachman, “Rue Taitbout.”

Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille was weeping: Roger, at her side, held one of her hands. He was silent, looking by turns at little Charles—who, not understanding his mother’s grief, stood speechless at her tears—at the cot where Eugénie lay sleeping, and at Caroline’s face, on which sorrow had fallen like rain across cheerful beams.

“Yes, my darling,” said Roger after a long silence, “that is the great secret: I am married. But some day I hope we may form but one family. My wife has been given over ever since last March. I do not wish her dead; yet if it pleases God to take her, I believe she will be happier in Paradise than here, where she is indifferent to both grief and pleasure.”

“How I hate that woman! How could she bear to make you unhappy? And yet it is to that unhappiness I owe my happiness!”

Her tears ceased.

“Caroline, let us hope,” cried Roger. “Do not be frightened by anything that priest may have said to you. Though my wife’s confessor is a man to be feared for his power in the congregation, if he should try to blight our happiness I would find means—”

“What could you do?”

“We would go to Italy: I would fly—”

A shriek from the adjoining room made Roger start and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille quake; she rushed into the drawing-room and found Madame de Granville in a dead faint. When she recovered, she sighed deeply upon finding herself supported by the Count and her rival, whom she instinctively pushed away. Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille rose to withdraw.

“You are at home, madame,” said Granville, taking Caroline by the arm. “Stay.”

The Judge took up his wife, carried her to the carriage, and got in with her.

“Who has brought you to the point of wishing me dead, of resolving to fly?” asked the Countess, looking at him with grief and indignation. “Was I not young? You thought me pretty—what fault have you to find with me? Have I been false? Have I not been virtuous and well-conducted? My heart cherished no image but yours, my ears listened to no other voice. What duty have I failed in? What have I denied you?”

“Happiness, madame,” said the Count severely. “You know there are two ways of serving God. Some Christians think that by saying set prayers, attending Mass regularly and avoiding sin they will win heaven—but they, madame, will go to hell; they have not loved God for Himself, they have not worshiped as He chooses, they have made no sacrifice. Though mild in seeming, they are hard on their neighbors; they see the law’s letter, not its spirit. This is how you have treated me—your earthly husband; you have sacrificed my happiness to your salvation. You were always absorbed in prayer when I came to you in gladness; you wept when you should have cheered my toil; you never tried to satisfy my demands.”

“And if they were wicked,” cried the Countess hotly, “was I to lose my soul to please you?”

“It is a sacrifice another, a more loving woman, has dared to make,” said Granville coldly.

“Dear God!” she cried, bursting into tears, “Thou hearest! Has he been worthy of the prayers and penance I have lived in, wearing myself out to atone for his sins and my own?—Of what avail is virtue?”

“To win Heaven, my dear. A woman cannot be both a husband’s wife and the spouse of Christ; that would be bigamy. For future advantage you have stripped your soul of the love and devotion God commands you should have for me; you have cherished hatred—”

“Have I not loved you?” she interposed.

“No, madame.”

“Then what is love?” the Countess involuntarily asked.

“Love, my dear,” replied Granville with a kind of ironic surprise, “you are incapable of understanding it. The cold sky of Normandy is not that of Spain. To yield to caprices, to guess them, to find pleasure in pain, to sacrifice the world’s opinion, your pride, your religion even, and still regard these offerings as mere incense burnt to an idol—that is love—”

“The love of ballet-girls!” cried the Countess in horror. “Such flames cannot last and leave only ashes and despair. A wife should bring true friendship, equable warmth—”

“You speak of warmth as negroes speak of ice,” retorted the Count with a sardonic smile. “Even the humblest daisy has more charm than the gaudiest hawthorn. At the same time,” he added, “I will do you justice. You have kept so precisely to the straight path of imagined duty that to show wherein you have failed toward me I should enter into details that would offend your dignity and seem to undermine all morality.”

“And you dare speak of morality when you have just left the house where you have dissipated your children’s fortune in debaucheries?” cried the Countess, maddened by his reticence.

“There, madame, I must correct you,” said the Count coolly. “Though Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille is rich, it is at nobody’s expense. My uncle, master of his fortune and with several heirs, in his lifetime and out of pure friendship, gave her the little estate of Bellefeuille. As for anything else, I owe it to his liberality—”

“Such conduct is worthy only of a Jacobin!” said the sanctimonious Angélique.

“Madame, you forget that your own father was one of the Jacobins you scorn,” said the Count sternly. “Citizen Bontems was signing death-warrants at a time when my uncle was doing France good service.”

Madame de Granville was silenced. But the sight she had just seen reawakened the jealousy nothing could kill, and she murmured, as if to herself, “How can a woman thus destroy her own soul and that of others?”

“Bless me, madame,” replied the Count, tired of the dialogue, “you yourself may one day have to answer that question.” The Countess was frightened. “You perhaps will be excused by the merciful Judge, who will weigh our sins,” he went on, “in consideration of the conviction with which you have worked out my misery. I do not hate you—I hate those who have perverted your heart and reason. You have prayed for me, just as Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille has given me her heart and crowned my life with love. You should have been my mistress and the prayerful saint by turns. Do me the justice to confess I am no reprobate, no debauchee. My life was not sordid. Alas! after seven years of wretchedness, the craving for happiness led me, by an imperceptible descent, to love another woman and make a second home. Do not imagine I am singular; thousands of husbands here live such twofold lives.”

“Great God!” cried the Countess. “How heavy the cross Thou hast laid on me to bear! If the husband Thou hast given me below can only be made happy through my death, take me to Thyself!”

“If you had always breathed such admirable sentiments and devotion, we should be happy yet,” said the Count coldly.

“Indeed,” cried Angélique, melting into a flood of tears, “forgive me if I have done wrong. Yes, monsieur, I am ready to obey you in all things; henceforth I will be all you can wish your wife to be.”

“If your purpose, madame, is to compel me to say I no longer love you, I shall find the cruel courage to tell you so. Can I command my heart? Can I wipe out in an instant traces of fifteen years of suffering?—I have ceased to love. These words contain a mystery as deep as the words ‘I love.’ Esteem, respect, friendship may be won, lost, regained; but as to love—I might school myself a thousand years and it would not blossom again, especially for a woman too old to respond to it.”

“I hope, Monsieur le Comte, I sincerely hope such words may not be spoken to you one day by the woman you love,” she said.

“Will you put on a dress a la Grecque this evening and come to the Opera?”

The shudder with which the Countess received the suggestion was a mute reply.

Early in December 1833 a man with white hair and a face aged by grief rather than years was walking at midnight along the Rue Gaillon. Reaching a modest two-story house, he paused and looked up at an attic window whose panes were patched, a dim light trembling behind a shabby blind. As he watched, a young man emerged. Seeing the elder, he approached hesitantly.

They recognized one another: the elder was the Comte de Granville, and the young man Dr. Horace Bianchon. Bianchon offered his arm in mock gallantry against the greasy pavement; Granville protested that at fifty-five he was still vigorous. They spoke lightly until Granville confessed he had been watching the attic window for some time, remarking on the persistent shadow he had seen there. He denied any real curiosity about the occupant’s sex or fortunes, only the idle pleasure of conjecture; then, with bitter irony, he explained how the events of life had made him old in a few days. He had renounced attachment to human beings, preferring animals, plants, and superficial interests; no longer could anything move or interest him.

Bianchon, shocked, urged him that this indifference could be cured by compassion. Granville retorted with sarcasm, then disclosed his belief that his children were perhaps waiting only for his money. Bianchon, for whom Granville’s reputation for benevolence was real, protested. Granville acknowledged his charity as self-serving—“I pay for a sensation”—and declared himself unmoved by sentimental debt. Bianchon pleaded that Granville’s desolation was a moral wrong; the Count shuddered and rebuffed him.

Bianchon then spoke earnestly of the garret. He described its occupant: a woman of about thirty, consumed by a fanatical love for a worthless young man, a gambler and debauchee named Solvet. She had sacrificed comfort, a devoted husband, and the father of her children to this man; she worked day and night to feed him and her children while he spent, lied, and robbed. Bianchon had tended her; she had even sold her beautiful hair for a coin, which Solvet immediately seized. Recently the children had cried from hunger; that evening they had had nothing to eat and lay exhausted. Bianchon had found her ill on her wretched bed; she and her children were near starvation.

Hearing this, Granville—despite his professed indifference—was visibly afflicted. Bianchon exclaimed “Poor Caroline!” The name struck the Count with a physical recoil. He seized Bianchon’s arm and, in a broken voice, asked if the woman was Caroline Crochard. When Bianchon confirmed the name and the lover’s name, Solvet, Granville’s restraint broke. He accused Bianchon of rousing the most terrible pain in him, pain he called a gift from hell, and said he always knew how to pay such debts.

They reached the corner of the Rue de la Chaussée d’Antin where a coarse night scavenger stood. In a malicious, theatrical gesture Granville gave the man a thousand-franc note, but with a curse: spend it in the wine-shop, get drunk, pick a fight, make mischief that would set the city’s petty offices—watch, surgeon, druggist, police, prosecutor, judge, and prison warders—to work. “I have squared accounts with hell,” he said. He declared he would not lift a finger to save Caroline Crochard even if she died of hunger hearing her children’s cries; and because Bianchon had helped her, he would see the doctor no more.

Left stunned, Bianchon watched Granville stride away briskly toward the Rue Saint-Lazare. When the Count reached his little house, he found a carriage waiting and a servant who said his son had been there for an hour, waiting in the bedroom. Granville admitted surprise and a degree of displeasure: he had forbidden his children from coming to him unless summoned. Still, he went to the room.

His son, the public prosecutor (now attorney-general), spoke in trembling respect. Earlier that afternoon a very young man arrested in the house of a friend—taken for robbery—had claimed to be the Count’s son. His name was Charles Crochard. Granville listened, gave a curt “That will do,” and paced the room in silence. Then, in unexpectedly mild and fatherly tones, he announced that Charles Crochard had spoken the truth: he was indeed the Count’s son. He told his son Eugène that he was glad the matter had been brought to him, and handed over a bundle of banknotes, giving Eugène full discretion to use the money as he thought fit to resolve whatever arrangements were necessary. He expressed trust in Eugène and approved whatever steps he might take.

Granville then, with melancholy dignity, prepared for a parting that might be final. He implied he would ask to be dismissed from his royal office and go to Italy. He spoke to Eugène as a father passing along hard-won experience: when the son married he should not act lightly; marriage is the most important of social obligations, and a lack of harmony between spouses brings terrible punishments. He asked Eugène to consult him in choosing a wife and warned that a father who presides over a supreme court should not have reason to blush before his son. He kissed him, bade him farewell, and promised to write from Florence.
