The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once a dark, winding lane near the Hôtel de Ville ending at Rue Martroi, was marked by a turnstile until 1823. Less than six feet wide, it often filled with gutter water and was perpetually muddy; summer sunlight barely reached it, and dampness lingered.

Residents lit their lamps at five and kept them burning through winter. Approaching the Marais, a passerby felt he was moving through cellars, as many Paris streets did. The corner house at Rue du Tourniquet and Rue de la Tixeranderie showed its age: iron rings from chains still marked its walls.

Built to fight dampness, the house had cellars raised above ground and an entrance reached by three steps. The door formed a closed arch crowned by a keystone carved with a female head. Three high windows, protected by iron bars, admitted light to the small dwelling within. Curious passersby could glimpse two beds draped in green serge and an old woman tending a brazier, preparing a stew for porters’ wives. The kitchen-dining room was sparsely furnished but kept clean and thrifty.

The old woman, pale and wrinkled, seemed as much a fixture as the walls. She sat by the hearth with a look that mixed mischief and resignation, while her daughter worked at an embroidery frame. The mother, with slow fingers and spectacles, occasionally glanced out the window; the daughter bent over her work with a downcast gaze.

Their modest home mirrored the lives of working-class women in Paris. Despite the surroundings, the girl—Caroline—retained youthful charm: rosy cheeks and bright eyes suggested a spirit not yet dimmed by hardship. The mother speculated about passersby and dreamt of a better future for her daughter, while Caroline remained absorbed in her embroidery.

One August day in 1815 the mother noticed a new face among the regular passersby: a tall, thin man in black whose solemn demeanor intrigued her. She guessed he was a clerk from the Prefect’s office, and her excitement was evident. Caroline, usually indifferent, stirred at her mother’s remarks, but the stranger had already gone.

The old woman eagerly awaited his return, certain he would pass again. When he did, Caroline caught sight of him and felt an unfamiliar sympathy, noticing traces of suffering on his face. This was the first time a passerby had pierced her indifference; usually she was absorbed by work and daily grind.

The man’s presence lingered in Caroline’s mind, distinct among the many faces she barely registered. For the first time she felt compassion for someone beyond her own struggles. As she resumed her embroidery, she found herself looking forward to seeing him again, intrigued by the possibility that this stranger might alter the course of her life.

For over two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved erratically, not always passing the Rue du Tourniquet at the same hour. Madame Crochard noticed his distracted air—lost in thought and hardly observing her or her daughter, Caroline. One mid-September morning Caroline’s liveliness caught him; he paused to watch her at the window, and that pause began a quiet exchange of furtive glances implying a growing understanding.

In the weeks that followed he began to pass twice daily, and their brief encounters became an anticipated routine. Their quiet exchanges showed mutual concern: Caroline sensed when he was troubled, and he knew when she fretted over work. Still, both remained cautious, fearing a closer bond might bring complications or heartbreak.

Madame Crochard, frustrated by her daughter’s indecision, grew displeased with the Gentleman, who had once won her benign attention. As winter neared, hardships weighed on Caroline, and the Gentleman observed how they wore on her spirit. One night he overheard a mother-and-daughter conversation that revealed severe financial strain. Touched, he secretly left money for them; Caroline found it the next day, and a moment of silent gratitude passed between them.

In early May Caroline suggested a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—more sombre than usual—unexpectedly accompanied them. On the carriage ride Madame Crochard pretended to sleep, giving Caroline and the Gentleman space for a light-hearted conversation that revealed their growing fondness. The countryside’s beauty and their warm exchange cheered him, and he began to see Caroline differently.

Their bond deepened as they shared personal stories: Caroline spoke of family sacrifices and her mother’s efforts; the Gentleman, later called Roger, was drawn to her honesty and resilience. The day in Montmorency brimmed with laughter, a sharp contrast to earlier reserve. As evening fell, Caroline voiced her happiness and Roger entertained the possibility of something more. Preparing to leave, they playfully held hands—discreet, significant. Madame Crochard, though pretending not to notice, was aware. The moment marked a step forward, full of unspoken promises and hope.

The adventures of Roger and Caroline continue in modern Paris, where new apartments suit honeymooners. In a pristine building on Rue Taitbout, Caroline has lived for over a month in a charming second-floor apartment, carefully furnished with fresh paint and elegant, English-influenced decor.

Her bedroom features gray hangings trimmed with green silk, comfortable seating, and a writing table for love letters. A bronze clock depicting Love crowning Psyche and a Gothic-patterned carpet complete the cozy atmosphere. As she prepares for Roger’s visit, her hairdresser flatters her about his admiration.

On a chilly September day in 1816, Caroline dons a green grenadine gown trimmed with chinchilla and steps onto the balcony, heart racing as she watches for Roger. After fifteen minutes her impatience grows, but her spirits lift when she finally sees him approaching.

They embrace passionately, rejoicing in their reunion after three days apart. Roger then tells her he must attend a meeting with the Attorney-General, which disappoints Caroline; she protests the idea of enjoying the theatre without him and expresses deep affection. Still, she quickly focuses on the joy of being together. She tells him she wishes to take the name Caroline de Bellefeuille, sits on his lap, and vows to use her earnings to help the poor, grateful for their life together.

Her excitement swells as she plays a sonata, but Roger, mindful of obligations, prepares to leave. They exchange a playful farewell; she watches him drive away, savoring the parting and longing for his return.

Five years after settling on Rue Taitbout, Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille sits calmly with her four‑year‑old son Charles playing with a rocking horse. Now twenty‑four, she reflects a beauty shaped by contentment. She has embraced motherhood, dedicating herself to raising her children and fostering in them a love of goodness and beauty. Comfortable with simple pleasures, she is indifferent to social ambitions or luxury.

Rocking her infant daughter Eugenie, Caroline thinks back on joyful visits to Bellefeuille with Roger, where their love grew in the simplicity of private life, free from social entanglements. She wonders why Roger seeks happiness in secrecy and what that secrecy means for them.

On May 6, 1822, the anniversary of a pivotal day, Caroline prepares dinner anticipating Roger. When he arrives, he produces a document outlining Eugenie’s marriage portion, prompting a discussion about unequal provisions for their children. Roger argues that Charles, being a boy, should learn self‑reliance, while Eugenie, as a girl, ought to receive a substantial fortune. Their evening is warm and lively—ended with a lantern show for Charles—but the night brings the familiar ache of parting. Roger promises three days’ leave, yet duty soon summons him away.

Shortly after this anniversary, Caroline hurries to the bedside of her mother, Madame Crochard, who is gravely ill. The household acquaintances, not recognizing Caroline as the daughter, have summoned a priest for last rites; the scene is marked by confusion and opportunism. The old women around the widow seem more intent on securing a share of her wealth than on genuine care. Despite their motives, Caroline’s devotion is evident as she tends her mother.

In her final moments Madame Crochard expresses regret for their estrangement and reveals that the priest pressured her into confessing information about Caroline’s benefactor—an admission that hints at a more complicated network of relationships and secrets to be unraveled.

To clarify these connections, the narrative returns to events preceding Madame Crochard’s death. In November 1805 a young barrister, Monsieur de Granville, leaves a gathering at the High Chancellor’s and finds his prospects suddenly precarious and yet promising thanks to his uncle’s influence. He receives a letter from his father urging him to return home quickly: a proposed marriage to Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems could secure his career. The letter details financial advantages despite the social stigma attached to the Bontems family.

Torn between ambition and feeling, Granville recalls a childhood attachment to Angélique, whom he has not seen for years. He travels to Bayeux the next day; his father brings him to the Bontems household, a stifling place dominated by a widow’s strict piety. At church Granville glimpses Angélique—her beauty stirs old feelings—and, impatience overcoming restraint, he approaches her after the service, setting the stage for a reunion that may complicate duty, desire, and social expectation.

For about two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he frequented her gloomy drawing-room. He watched Angélique prepare her trousseau; she never brought up religion, and when Granville playfully handled her rosary or mocked practices she quietly returned it to its bag with a serene smile. She believed firmly in the Church, insisting a mother must have a religion and questioning how one could judge between disbelievers and God.

Captivated by her conviction, Granville sometimes felt tempted to adopt her beliefs, mistaking his desire for her as love. Angélique, torn between feeling and duty, left him uncertain of his own sentiments. He was grateful to Madame Bontems for instilling religion in Angélique, thinking it would steady her in life’s trials. At the marriage-contract signing Madame Bontems required Granville to promise respect for Angélique’s religious practices and freedom of conscience; their spiritual guide, the Abbé Fontanon, approved, and the couple soon married and moved to Paris, where Granville became public prosecutor.

Angélique persuaded him to take a large ground-floor apartment in the Marais, near a church and chapel, arguing it would benefit future children. Granville, who preferred a more fashionable district, yielded. He concentrated on his study and left decoration to Angélique, who delighted in furnishing their home, but he was dismayed by the apartment’s cold, solemn atmosphere, reflecting her austere upbringing.

Despite trying to understand her choices, he grew increasingly frustrated by the lack of warmth and charm at home, attributing the sober decor to her Bayeux origins. He tried to comfort Angélique, believing happiness would come from her love and gentleness rather than material comforts. From spring through winter their life seemed outwardly harmonious: Granville kept busy with work and accompanied her to church on Sundays, yet he remained unaware of the strict religious observances she enforced—Lenten meals and other austerities she disguised as carelessness—until a disruptive event.

In November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux, who had earlier guided Angélique, arrived in Paris alarmed by what he perceived as her moral decline and sought to reclaim her from a lax life. Angélique adopted stricter practices, which led to misunderstandings about social obligations. Granville grew irritated by her frequent excuses to avoid social events, especially balls. After a tense evening he confronted her about her demeanor and attire, urging her to embrace her social role. Arguments escalated; Angélique cited prohibitions from her director against attending balls. Granville accused her priest of overstepping, and their dispute turned theological, culminating in Angélique writing to the Pope for guidance.

The Pope’s reply sided with Granville, asserting a wife should accompany her husband even if it risked compromising her virtue. Rather than resolve matters, this deepened their divide; Angélique and her director dismissed the Pope’s advice. Granville, exasperated by her strict observances, began asserting his own preferences—ordering meat daily despite her objections.

As the household atmosphere grew oppressive, Granville withdrew from social life, feeling weighed down by Angélique’s rigidity. The home became stifling and gloomy, affecting everyone, including the servants. He recognized their house had turned into a place devoid of joy, marked by rigid piety. Angélique’s beauty was increasingly overshadowed by austerity; duty replaced affection and her devotion left little room for love. The children, raised under strict rules, suffered, prompting Granville to send his sons to school early and resolve to marry off his daughters as soon as possible.

Increasingly isolated, Granville sought solace in work—attempting to write on law—while Angélique tried to convert him back to her views. Their relationship deteriorated, leaving him alienated from his wife and their home, now a place of coldness and rigidity rather than warmth and companionship.

Between 1806 and 1825 the de Granville household experienced little outward upheaval, but its inner life was barren. After losing her husband's affection, Madame de Granville continued to pay for Masses to discern her faults and restore their relationship. Granville, now a prominent judge, took to living in the entresol to avoid her; each morning a housekeeper relayed messages, and he habitually declined breakfast, citing pressing court business.

At thirty-five, looking older, Madame de Granville awaited her husband's return, eager to engage him with her narrow-minded remarks. When Granville refused the presidency of a provincial court, the Countess’s friends and her confessor suspected an illicit affair. The suggestion horrified Madame de Granville, who could not conceive her husband capable of such sin.

During Lent in 1822 her health declined from stress and low fever, worsened by Granville's indifference. Though she tried to be more affectionate, her bigotry often undermined her attempts. By late May, as she slightly recovered, she sought counsel from the Abbe Fontanon, who delivered devastating news: for seven years Granville had lived with a concubine, fathering two children and squandering their fortune.

At first she could not believe it, torn between faith and betrayal. The Abbe urged forgiveness and patience for a hoped-for enlightenment, but she was consumed by a storm of emotions. After a tumultuous day she left home determined to confront her husband.

At that moment Roger and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille were in a tense exchange: she proclaimed hatred for his wife, whom she blamed for their unhappiness, while Roger reassured her with hopes for their future. Their conversation was cut short when Madame de Granville suddenly fainted. Upon recovering she confronted Granville directly, questioning his love and fidelity.

Granville retorted that she had sacrificed his happiness to piety and had failed to fulfill the role of a loving wife. Their dialogue exposed deep-rooted faults: he argued that true love requires sacrifice and understanding, while she clung to moral righteousness. He admitted seeking happiness elsewhere because, he said, her religious austerity and coldness drove him away.

Madame de Granville’s jealousy and anger surfaced, and she faced the painful realization of an irreconcilable gulf between them. Granville’s indifference and his assertion that he could not rekindle a faded love left her devastated. The exchange ended in a stark acknowledgement of a failed marriage: love, he insisted, cannot be commanded or revived at will, and she was left to confront the consequences and an uncertain future.

Early in December 1833, a white-haired, worn man, aged by grief more than years, walked the Rue Gaillon at midnight. He paused before a modest two-story house; a dim light flickered in the attic windows, some patched with paper. A young man emerged. Recognizing him, the young man approached hesitantly.

“What, is it you, Monsieur le Président? Alone at this hour, so far from the Rue Saint-Lazare. Allow me to give you my arm. The pavement is greasy this morning; if we do not hold each other up, we shall tumble.”

The Comte de Granville replied, “I am no more than fifty-five, unfortunately for me. A physician of your fame must know that at that age a man can be hale and strong.”

“Then you are in waiting on a lady, I suppose,” said Horace Bianchon. “You do not go about Paris on foot unless—”

“Still, when I am not visiting in the evening I often return from the Courts or the club on foot,” Granville answered.

“And with large sums of money about you, perhaps!” the doctor exclaimed. “It is an invitation to the assassin’s knife.”

“I am not afraid of that,” Granville said with melancholy indifference.

“Then do not stand about,” Bianchon urged, leading him toward the boulevard. “A little more and I shall believe you are bent on robbing me of your last illness—dying by some other hand than mine.”

“You caught me playing the spy,” Granville admitted. “For some time I have observed at a window on the third floor the shadow of a person who seems to work with heroic constancy.”

He paused, as if in pain. “I take as much interest in that garret as a citizen must feel in the finishing of the Palais Royal.”

“Well,” Bianchon began eagerly—

“Tell me nothing,” Granville interrupted. “I would not give a centime to know whether the shadow that moves across that shabby blind is a man or a woman, or whether the inhabitant is happy or miserable. Though I was surprised to see no one at work there this evening, I stopped only for the pleasure of indulging idle conjectures. For nine years I have ceased to wonder that old men amuse themselves with flowers and trees; life has taught me disbelief in human affection. I grew old within a few days. I will no longer attach myself to any creature but animals, plants, or superficial things. I think more of Taglioni’s grace than of all human feeling. I abhor life and the world in which I live alone. Nothing can move or interest me.”

“But you have children?” Bianchon asked.

“My children!” Granville repeated bitterly. “Yes—my eldest daughter is the Comtesse de Vandenesse. The other will, by her sister’s connections, make a suitable match. My sons have succeeded: the Viscount was public prosecutor at Limoges and is now President of the Court at Orléans; the younger is public prosecutor in Paris. My children have their own cares, their own anxieties. If one of those hearts had been devoted to me, had tried to fill the void I have here”—he struck his breast—“that one would have failed in life, sacrificing it to me. And why should he? To bring sunshine into my few remaining years—and would he have succeeded? Might I not have accepted such generosity as a debt? But, doctor,” he added with deep irony, “it is not for nothing that we teach them arithmetic. At this moment perhaps they are waiting for my money.”

“O Monsieur le Comte, how could such an idea enter your head—you who are kind, friendly, humane! If I were not myself a living proof of your benevolence—”

“To please myself,” Granville replied. “I pay for a sensation, as I would pay to recover the most childish illusion that would make my heart glow. I help my fellow-creatures for my own sake, like gambling; I expect no gratitude. I would see you die without blinking; and I beg of you to feel the same about me. Life has swept over my heart like lava over Herculaneum. The town is there—dead.”

“Those who have brought a soul as warm as yours to such indifference are indeed guilty!” Bianchon exclaimed.

“Say no more,” Granville said, shuddering.

“You have a malady which you ought to allow me to treat,” Bianchon insisted.

“What, do you know a cure for death?” Granville cried irritably.

“I undertake, Monsieur le Comte, to revive the heart you think frozen.”

“Are you a match for Talma, then?” Granville asked satirically.

“No, Monsieur le Comte. But Nature is above Talma. Listen: the garret you are interested in is inhabited by a woman of about thirty, and her love is fanatic. The object of her adoration is a young man of pleasing appearance but possessed of every conceivable vice. He is a gambler—perhaps more addicted to wine or women; he has committed acts punishable by law. To him this unhappy woman sacrificed a life of ease, a man who worshiped her, and the father of her children.”

“What is wrong?” Granville asked.

“Nothing. Go on,” he replied.

“She has allowed him to squander a fortune; she would give him the world if she had it; she works night and day; many times she has seen the wretch rob her even of money saved for her children’s clothes and food. Only three days ago she sold her hair, the finest I ever saw; he came in, and she could not hide the gold quickly enough. For a smile, for a kiss, she gave up the price of a fortnight’s subsistence. Is it not dreadful, and yet sublime? Work has worn her cheeks hollow. Her children’s crying has broken her heart; she is ill, and at this moment on her wretched bed. This evening they had nothing to eat; the children had no strength to cry; they were silent when I went up.”

Bianchon stood still. Granville, despite himself, put his hand into his waistcoat pocket.

“I can guess, my young friend, how she is yet alive if you attend her,” Granville said.

“O poor soul!” Bianchon cried. “Who could refuse to help her? I only wish I were richer, for I hope to cure her of her passion.”

“But how can you expect me to pity a misery whose joys would seem cheaply bought with my whole fortune!” Granville exclaimed, withdrawing his hand empty of the notes Bianchon had assumed he was feeling for. “That woman feels; she is alive! Would not Louis XV have given his kingdom to rise from the grave and have three days of youth and life? Is not that the history of thousands of dead men, thousands of sick men, thousands of old men?”

“Poor Caroline!” Bianchon lamented.

At the name Granville shuddered and grasped the doctor’s arm tightly.

“Her name is Caroline Crochard?” he asked, voice breaking.

“Then you know her?” Bianchon replied, astonished.

“And the wretch’s name is Solvet. Ay, you have kept your word!” Granville exclaimed. “You have roused my heart to the most terrible pain it can suffer until it is dust. That emotion, too, is a gift from hell, and I always know how to pay those debts.”

They reached the corner of the Rue de la Chaussée-d’Antin. A night scavenger, with a shriveled face, stood by the curb.

“Do you ever pick up a thousand-franc note?” Granville asked.

“Now and then, master,” the scavenger answered.

“And you restore them?”

“It depends on the reward.”

“You’re the man for me.” Granville handed him a thousand-franc note. “Take this, but remember: I give it to you on condition you spend it at the wineshop, get drunk, fight, beat your wife, black your friends’ eyes. That will give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist—perhaps to the police, the public prosecutor, the judge, and the prison warders. Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later.”

A draughtsman would need the pencil of Charlet and Callot, the brush of Teniers and Rembrandt to capture the grotesque night scene.

“Now I have squared accounts with hell and had some pleasure for my money,” Granville said, pointing to the scavenger’s face. “As for Caroline Crochard—she may die of hunger, hearing the heartrending cries of her starving children, convinced of the baseness of the man she loves. I will not give a sou to rescue her; and because you have helped her, I will see you no more—”

Granville left Bianchon like a statue and walked briskly to the Rue Saint-Lazare, where a carriage waited at his door.

“Monsieur, your son, the attorney-general, came about an hour ago,” the servant said. “He is waiting for you in your bedroom.”

Granville dismissed the servant and entered the room. “What motive can be strong enough to make you infringe the order I gave my children never to come to me unless I send for them?” he asked his son.

“Father,” the younger man replied tremulously, “I hope you will forgive me when you have heard me.”

“Your reply is proper. Sit down,” Granville gestured. “But whether I walk up and down or take a seat, speak without heeding me.”

“Father,” the son continued, “this afternoon, at four o’clock, a very young man who was arrested in the house of a friend of mine, whom he had robbed, appealed to you. He says he is your son.”

“His name?” Granville asked hoarsely.

“Charles Crochard.”

“That will do,” Granville said, waving his hand.

He paced in silence; his son remained quiet.

“My son,” he began in a mild, fatherly voice that surprised the young lawyer, “Charles Crochard spoke the truth. I am glad you came to me tonight, my good Eugène.” He handed him a bundle of banknotes. “Here is a considerable sum; use it as you see fit in this matter. I trust you implicitly and approve whatever arrangements you may make, now or later. Eugène, my dear son, kiss me. We part perhaps for the last time. I shall to-morrow crave my dismissal from the King, and I am going to Italy.

“Though a father owes no account of his life to his children, he is bound to bequeath to them the experience Fate sells him dearly—is it not part of their inheritance? When you marry,” he continued, involuntarily shuddering, “do not undertake it lightly; that act is the most important society requires of us. Remember to study the character of the woman who is to be your partner; consult me too—I will judge her myself. A lack of union between husband and wife, from whatever cause, leads to terrible misfortune; sooner or later we are always punished for contravening the social law. But I will write to you on this subject from Florence. A father who presides over a supreme court of justice must not have to blush in the presence of his son. Good-bye.”
