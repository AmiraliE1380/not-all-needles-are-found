The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once a dark, winding lane near the Hôtel de Ville, ended at the Rue Martroi and was closed by a turnstile until 1823. Less than six feet wide at its broadest, it often pooled gutter water and remained muddy and damp; sunlight barely reached it except briefly in summer. Lamps were lit by five in June and kept burning through winter. Approaching the Marais felt like passing through cellars, as many old Paris streets did. A corner house at Rue du Tourniquet and Rue de la Tixeranderie showed its age: iron rings remained from chains once used for public safety.

Built to combat humidity, the house had cellars raised above ground and an entrance reached by three steps. The door had a closed arch with a keystone carved as a female head. Three high windows, protected by iron bars, lit a small dwelling. Inside, two beds draped in green serge and an old woman tending a brazier preparing a porter’s-wife stew were visible. The kitchen-dining room was sparsely furnished but kept with evident cleanliness and thrift.

The old woman’s pale, wrinkled face seemed part of the house itself. She sat by the fireplace with an expression of mischief mixed with resignation while her daughter worked at an embroidery frame. With slow fingers and antiquated spectacles, the mother occasionally glanced out the window; the daughter bent over her work with focused modesty.

Their modest home reflected the lives of working-class Parisian women. Despite poverty, the girl retained youthful charm—rosy cheeks and bright eyes that suggested a spirit not wholly dimmed by hardship. The old woman speculated about passersby and hoped for a better future for her daughter; the girl, absorbed in embroidery, usually kept her gaze down.

One August day in 1815 the mother noticed a new face among the regular passersby: a tall, thin man in black whose solemn bearing intrigued her. She fancied he was a clerk from the Préfet’s office, and her excitement was plain. Caroline, roused from indifference by her mother’s remarks, glimpsed the stranger as he passed.

The old woman eagerly awaited his return, convinced he would come by again. When he did, Caroline caught another glimpse and felt an unfamiliar sympathy, noting traces of suffering on his face. This was the first time a passerby had stirred such feeling in her; she typically regarded passersby with indifference, focused only on work and routine. The man’s presence left a lasting impression, and as she resumed her embroidery Caroline found herself looking forward to another sight of him, intrigued by the possibility that this new acquaintance might alter her life.

For two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved erratically, varying the time he passed the Rue du Tourniquet. Madame Crochard noticed his distracted manner—often lost in thought and seldom acknowledging her or Caroline. One mid-September morning Caroline’s lively presence caught his eye; their brief, silent glances suggested a quiet understanding.

Weeks later he began passing twice daily; their meetings became a routine they both anticipated. Their silent exchanges conveyed mutual concern: Caroline sensed when he was troubled, and he could tell when she was anxious about work. Both remained cautious, fearing greater intimacy might bring complications.

Madame Crochard, annoyed by her daughter’s indecision, turned her displeasure toward the Gentleman, whom she had once mildly approved. As winter neared, life’s hardships wore on Caroline, and the Gentleman observed how they affected her spirit. One night he overheard a mother-and-daughter conversation that revealed their financial struggles. Touched, he secretly left money for them; Caroline found it the next day, and a moment of silent gratitude passed between them.

In early May Caroline proposed a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—more somber than usual—unexpectedly joined. During the journey Madame Crochard pretended to sleep, giving Caroline and the Gentleman a chance for light-hearted talk that revealed their growing affection. The countryside’s beauty and their warm exchange lifted his spirits; he began to see Caroline differently.

Their bond deepened as they shared personal stories. Caroline spoke of her family’s hardships and her mother’s sacrifices. The Gentleman, now called Roger, was drawn to her honesty and resilience. The day in Montmorency brimmed with laughter and joy, a sharp contrast to their earlier meetings.

As evening approached, Caroline voiced her newfound happiness, and Roger reflected on the possibility of more than a single bright day. Preparing to leave, they playfully held hands—an unspoken promise and a hopeful step toward a brighter future together.

The adventures of Roger and Caroline continue in modern Paris, where newly built apartments suit honeymooners. In a pristine building on Rue Taitbout, Caroline has lived for over a month in a charming second-floor apartment, furnished with care and style. The rooms, freshly painted and elegantly appointed, reflect current trends influenced by English design.

Her bedroom has gray hangings trimmed with green silk, comfortable chairs, and a writing table for love letters. A bronze clock showing Love crowning Psyche and a Gothic-patterned carpet complete the cozy setting. As she prepares for Roger’s visit, her hairdresser flatters her, reminding her of his admiration.

On a chilly September day in 1816, Caroline wears a green grenadine gown trimmed with chinchilla and eagerly steps onto the balcony to watch for Roger; her heart races as she anticipates his arrival. After a quarter-hour of waiting, impatience replaces excitement, but she brightens when she finally sees him approaching.

Roger arrives and they embrace passionately, rejoicing in their reunion after three long days apart. He then tells her he must attend a meeting with the Attorney-General, which disappoints Caroline; she playfully objects to the idea of his enjoying the theatre without her and declares her deep affection.

Despite the setback, Caroline returns to the joy of being with Roger. She expresses her wish to take his name—Caroline de Bellefeuille—and, perched on his lap, speaks of using her earnings to help the poor, underscoring her gratitude for their life together. As she plays a sonata, Roger, mindful of obligations, prepares to leave. They exchange a playful farewell; she watches him drive away, savoring the moment and longing for his return.

Five years after Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille settled in her Rue Taitbout home, she sits serenely with her four-year-old son Charles, who plays with a rocking horse. Now twenty-four, Caroline’s beauty reflects happiness and fulfillment. She has embraced motherhood, devoted to raising children and cultivating their appreciation of goodness and beauty. Content with simple pleasures, she remains indifferent to social ambition, never craving luxury or status.

Rocking her infant daughter Eugenie, Caroline recalls joyful visits to Bellefeuille with Roger. Their love has flourished in the simplicity of shared life, free from social complication; yet she wonders why Roger seeks happiness in secrecy and what binds him to such arrangements.

On May 6, 1822, the anniversary of a pivotal day for them, Caroline prepares for dinner expecting Roger. He arrives with a document detailing Eugenie’s marriage portion and they discuss different provisions for their children. Roger argues Charles, as a boy, should learn self-sufficiency, while Eugenie, as a girl, deserves a fortune. Their evening is warm and lively, ending with a magical lantern show for Charles. As night deepens, Roger must leave; Caroline feels the familiar pain of separation. He reassures her he has three days off, but duty still looms.

Soon after that anniversary, Caroline hastens to her mother, Madame Crochard, who is gravely ill. The old widow’s acquaintances, unaware that Caroline is her daughter, have summoned a priest to offer last rites. Caroline arrives amid confusion and deceit: the old women hope to benefit from Madame Crochard’s wealth. Despite their intentions, Caroline’s love shows as she cares for her mother.

In her final moments Madame Crochard expresses regret over their estrangement and reveals that the priest has compelled her to confess about Caroline’s benefactor—an admission that hints at a complex web of relationships and secrets to follow.

To explain those connections, the narrative backtracks to events before Madame Crochard’s death. In November 1805 a young barrister, Monsieur de Granville, finds himself precariously placed after leaving a gathering at the High Chancellor’s residence and gets a chance to advance his career through his uncle’s influence. He receives a letter from his father urging a swift return: a possible marriage to Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems could secure his future. The letter details the financial advantages despite the social stigma attached to the Bontems family.

Torn between ambition and feeling, Granville recalls childhood affection for Angélique, whom he has not seen in years. The next day he travels to Bayeux, greeted by his father and taken to the Bontems’ home, stifled under the widow’s strict religious observances. Granville dreads the match, yet his father’s insistence weighs on him.

During a church service Granville glimpses Angélique; her beauty captivates him anew. Their brief interaction rekindles his feelings, while family constraints remain. As the service ends his impatience drives him to approach her, setting the stage for a reunion that could alter both their lives.

The intertwined stories of Caroline and Granville reveal the tensions of love, duty, and social expectation as each navigates personal desire against familial obligation and ambition.

For about two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he visited her regularly in her dreary drawing-room. He watched Angelique marking her trousseau; she never initiated religious discussion. When Granville playfully handled her rosary or mocked religious practices, she quietly returned it to its bag with a serene smile. She was firmly committed to the Church’s teachings, insisting a mother should have a religion and questioning how one could judge between disbelievers and God.

Granville, captivated by her conviction, sometimes mistook his desire for her as a wish to share her beliefs. Angelique reconciled feeling and duty with ease, leaving Granville unsure of his own emotions. He was grateful to Madame Bontems for instilling those principles, believing they would help Angelique face life’s hardships.

On the day of their marriage contract, Madame Bontems made Granville promise to respect Angelique’s religious practices and allow her freedom of conscience. Angelique’s innocent gaze convinced him to agree; the Abbe Fontanon, who guided their consciences, approved. Shortly after their wedding the couple moved to Paris, where Granville became public prosecutor.

Angelique persuaded him to take a large ground-floor apartment in the Marais, near a church and chapel, arguing it would benefit their future children. Granville, who had preferred a more fashionable neighborhood, yielded. He set up his study and left the home decoration to Angelique, who delighted in furnishing their new house. But Granville was dismayed by the cold, solemn atmosphere she created, a reflection of her austere upbringing.

Despite trying to understand her choices, he grew increasingly frustrated by the lack of warmth and charm. He blamed her Bayeux background for a less refined taste and tried to comfort her by saying happiness came from her love and gentleness rather than material things.

From April through winter the couple appeared harmonious; Granville stayed busy with work and remained largely unaware of Angelique’s strict observances—Lenten abstinence she disguised as carelessness. He accompanied her to church on Sundays but did not grasp her fervor until a significant event disrupted their lives.

In November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux, Angelique’s spiritual guide, arrived in Paris alarmed by what he saw as her moral decline and sought to reclaim her from a lax lifestyle. Angelique adopted stricter practices, causing misunderstandings, particularly over social obligations. Granville grew irritated by her frequent excuses to avoid events, especially balls.

After a tense evening he confronted her about her demeanor and attire, urging her to fulfill her social role. Arguments escalated: Angelique cited her director’s prohibitions; Granville insisted her priest was overstepping. The dispute turned theological and led Angelique to write to the Pope for guidance.

The Pope’s reply affirmed Granville’s view, saying a wife should accompany her husband even if it meant compromising her virtue. Rather than settle the matter, this deepened the divide; Angelique and her director dismissed the Pope’s advice. Frustrated by her strict observances, Granville began to assert his preferences more forcefully—ordering meat daily despite her objections.

As the home grew oppressive, Granville withdrew from social life, weighed down by Angelique’s bigotry. The household became stifling and gloomy, affecting everyone including servants. Angelique’s beauty was overshadowed by austerity; duty had replaced love, leaving little room for affection. Their children suffered under strict rules: Granville sent his sons to school early and resolved to marry off his daughters as soon as possible.

Increasingly isolated, he sought solace in work and attempted to write a book on law, but Angelique’s attempts to reconvert him only added to his frustration. The relationship deteriorated: Granville felt alienated from his wife and their home, now cold and rigid rather than warm and companionable.

The history of the de Granville household between 1806 and 1825 was outwardly uneventful. Madame de Granville, having lost her husband's affection, persisted in paying for Masses and scrutinizing her faults in hopes of restoring their marriage. Granville, now a prominent judge, avoided her by living in the entresol. Each morning a housekeeper relayed messages; he continually declined breakfast invitations, citing important court business.

At thirty-five and looking older, Madame de Granville waited for his return, ready with narrow remarks. When Granville refused the presidency of a provincial court, friends and her confessor suspected an illicit affair; the refusal seemed to signal a liaison, which horrified her because she believed him incapable of such sin.

During Lent in 1822 her health declined from stress and fever, made worse by his indifference. Her attempts at affection were often undone by recurring bigotry. By late May, as she recovered, she sought guidance from the Abbe Fontanon, who delivered devastating news: Granville had lived with a concubine for seven years, fathering two children and squandering their fortune.

In disbelief, she wrestled with faith and betrayal. The Abbe urged forgiveness and patience, predicting Granville’s eventual enlightenment, but she was consumed by turmoil. After a tumultuous day she left home, determined to confront him.

Meanwhile Roger and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille engaged in a tense exchange: she declared hatred for his wife, whom she blamed for their unhappiness, and he reassured her of hopes for a shared future. Their conversation was interrupted when Madame de Granville suddenly fainted. On regaining consciousness she confronted Granville, questioning his love and fidelity. He accused her of sacrificing his happiness to piety, claiming she had failed to be a loving wife.

Their ensuing exchange exposed deep faults. Granville argued that true love requires sacrifice and understanding; he said he had sought happiness elsewhere because she had neglected their relationship through religious devotion. Madame de Granville clung to moral righteousness, her jealousy and anger revealing irreconcilable differences. His coldness and admission of loving another devastated her. Despite her tears and pleas for forgiveness, he remained resolute, saying he could not rekindle a love faded by years of neglect.

The dialogue ended in a stark acknowledgment of their failed marriage: love cannot be commanded or revived at will. Madame de Granville was left to face the reality of his emotional detachment and the consequences of their choices.

Early in December 1833 a white‑haired man, his features worn more by grief than years, walked the Rue Gaillon at midnight. He paused before a modest two‑story house whose attic windows showed a dim, flickering light, some patched with paper. A young man emerged; recognizing him, he approached with a jesting courtesy.

“What, is it you, Monsieur le Président? Alone at this hour, so far from the Rue Saint‑Lazare. Allow me to give you my arm. The pavement is so greasy this morning that if we do not hold each other up, we shall find it hard to escape a tumble.”

The Comte de Granville answered dryly that at fifty‑five he was still hale and strong.

“You must be in waiting on a lady,” said Horace Bianchon. “You are not in the habit of walking Paris. When a man keeps such fine horses—”

“Still, when I do not visit in the evening, I commonly return from the Courts or the club on foot,” Granville returned.

“And with large sums of money about you, perhaps!” Bianchon exclaimed. “It is a positive invitation to the assassin’s knife.”

“I am not afraid of that,” Granville said with melancholy indifference.

“Then do not stand about,” urged Bianchon, drawing him toward the boulevard. “A little more and I shall believe you are bent on robbing me of your last illness, dying by some other hand than mine.”

“You caught me playing the spy,” Granville admitted. “For some time I have watched at a third‑floor window of your house the shadow of a person who seems to work with heroic constancy.”

He paused as if in pain. “I take as great an interest in that garret as a citizen of Paris must in the finishing of the Palais‑Royal.”

“Well,” Bianchon began eagerly—

“Tell me nothing,” Granville interrupted. “I would not give a centime to know whether the shadow is that of a man or a woman, nor whether the inhabitant is happy or miserable. Though I was surprised to see no one at work there this evening, I stopped only to indulge in the idle conjectures of an idler. For nine years I have ceased to wonder at old men who amuse themselves with growing flowers and planting trees; life has taught them disbelief in all human affection. I grew old within a few days. I will no longer attach myself to any creature but unreasoning animals, plants, or superficial things. I think more of Taglioni’s grace than of all human feeling. I abhor life and the world in which I live alone. Nothing, nothing can move or interest me.”

“But you have children?” Bianchon asked.

“My children!” Granville repeated bitterly. “My eldest daughter is the Comtesse de Vandenesse. The other, through her sister’s connections, will make a good match. My sons have succeeded; the Viscount was public prosecutor at Limoges and is now President of the Court at Orléans; the younger is public prosecutor in Paris. My children have their own cares, their own anxieties. If one of those hearts had been devoted to me, if one had tried to fill the void I have here”—he struck his breast—“that one would have failed in life, sacrificing it to me. And why should he? To bring sunshine into my few remaining years—and would he have succeeded? Might I not have accepted such generosity as a debt? But, doctor,” he added with bitter irony, “it is not for nothing that we teach them arithmetic and how to count. At this moment perhaps they are waiting for my money.”

“O Monsieur le Comte, how could such an idea enter your head—you who are kind, friendly, humane! If I were not myself a living proof of the benevolence you exercise so liberally—”

“To please myself,” Granville replied. “I pay for a sensation, as I would pay a pile of gold to recover the most childish illusion that would make my heart glow. I help my fellow‑creatures for my own sake, just as I gamble; I look for gratitude from none. I should see you die without blinking; and I beg of you to feel the same with regard to me. The events of life have swept over my heart like the lavas of Vesuvius over Herculaneum. The town is there—dead.”

“Those who have brought a soul as warm and living as yours to such a pitch of indifference are indeed guilty!” Bianchon exclaimed.

“Say no more,” Granville said, shuddering with aversion.

“You have a malady which you ought to allow me to treat,” Bianchon insisted.

“What, do you know of a cure for death?” Granville cried irritably.

“I undertake, Monsieur le Comte, to revive the heart you believe to be frozen.”

“Are you a match for Talma, then?” Granville asked satirically.

“No, Monsieur le Comte. But Nature is as far above Talma as Talma is superior to me. Listen: the garret you are interested in is inhabited by a woman of about thirty, and her love is carried to fanaticism. The object of her adoration is a young man of pleasing appearance but endowed with every conceivable vice. He is a gambler, addicted to wine and women, and has committed acts deserving legal punishment. To him this unhappy woman sacrificed a life of ease, a man who worshiped her, and the father of her children.”

“What is wrong, Monsieur le Comte?” Granville asked.

“Nothing. Go on,” he replied.

“She has allowed him to squander a perfect fortune; she would give him the world if she had it; she works night and day; and many times she has seen the wretch rob her even of the money saved for her children’s clothes and food. Only three days ago she sold her hair—the finest I ever saw; he came in, and she could not hide the gold quickly enough. For a smile, for a kiss, she gave up the price of a fortnight’s life and peace. Is it not dreadful, and yet sublime? But work is wasting her; her cheeks are hollow. Her children’s crying has broken her heart; she is ill, and at this moment on her wretched bed. This evening they had nothing to eat; the children had not strength to cry; they were silent when I went up.”

Bianchon stood still; Granville, despite himself, put his hand into his waistcoat pocket.

“I can guess, my young friend, how it is that she is yet alive if you attend her,” Granville said.

“O poor soul!” Bianchon cried. “Who could refuse to help her? I only wish I were richer, for I hope to cure her of her passion.”

“But how can you expect me to pity a form of misery of which the joys to me would seem cheaply purchased with my whole fortune!” Granville exclaimed, withdrawing his hand empty of the notes Bianchon had assumed he was feeling for. “That woman feels; she is alive! Would not Louis XV have given his kingdom to rise from the grave and have three days of youth and life? And is not that the history of thousands of dead men, thousands of sick men, thousands of old men?”

“Poor Caroline!” Bianchon lamented.

At the name Granville shuddered and gripped the doctor’s arm.

“Her name is Caroline Crochard?” he asked, voice breaking.

“Then you know her?” Bianchon replied, astonished.

“And the wretch’s name is Solvet. Ay, you have kept your word!” Granville exclaimed. “You have roused my heart to the most terrible pain it can suffer until it is dust. That emotion, too, is a gift from hell, and I always know how to pay those debts.”

They had reached the corner of the Rue de la Chaussée‑d’Antin. A night scavenger with a shriveled face stood at the curb.

“Do you ever pick up a thousand‑franc note?” Granville asked.

“Now and then, master,” the scavenger replied.

“And you restore them?”

“It depends on the reward offered.”

“You're the man for me,” Granville said, handing him a thousand francs. “Take this, but remember, I give it to you on condition of your spending it at the wineshop, getting drunk, fighting, beating your wife, blacking your friends’ eyes. That will give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist—perhaps to the police, the public prosecutor, the judge, and the prison warders. Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later.”

Granville pointed out the scavenger’s grotesque face to Bianchon, who stood stupefied. “Now I have squared accounts with hell and had some pleasure for my money,” he said. “As for Caroline Crochard—she may die of hunger and thirst, hearing the heartrending cries of her starving children, convinced of the baseness of the man she loves. I will not give a sou to rescue her; and because you have helped her, I will see you no more—”

He left Bianchon standing like a statue and walked briskly to the Rue Saint‑Lazare, where a carriage waited at his door.

“Monsieur, your son, the attorney‑general, came about an hour ago,” the servant said. “He is waiting for you in your bedroom.”

Granville dismissed the servant and entered. “What motive can be strong enough to make you infringe the order I have given my children never to come to me unless I send for them?” he asked his son.

“Father,” the younger man replied tremulously, “I hope you will forgive me when you have heard me.”

“Your reply is proper,” Granville said. “Sit down.” Then, after a pause: “But whether I walk up and down or take a seat, speak without heeding me.”

“Father,” the son continued, “this afternoon, at four o’clock, a very young man who was arrested in the house of a friend of mine, whom he had robbed, appealed to you. He says he is your son.”

“His name?” Granville asked hoarsely.

“Charles Crochard.”

“That will do,” Granville said, waving his hand imperiously.

He paced in silence; his son waited.

“My son,” he began, voice suddenly mild and fatherly—startling the young lawyer—“Charles Crochard spoke the truth. I am glad you came to me tonight, my good Eugène.” He handed him a bundle of banknotes. “Here is a considerable sum of money; you can make any use of it in this matter. I trust you implicitly and approve whatever arrangements you may make, either now or in the future. Eugène, my dear son, kiss me. We part perhaps for the last time. I shall tomorrow crave my dismissal from the King, and I am going to Italy.

“Though a father owes no account of his life to his children, he is bound to bequeath to them the experience Fate sells him dearly—is it not part of their inheritance? When you marry,” he continued, involuntarily shuddering, “do not undertake it lightly; that act is the most important society requires of us. Remember to study the character of the woman who is to be your partner; consult me too, I will judge of her myself. A lack of union between husband and wife, from whatever cause, leads to terrible misfortune; sooner or later we are always punished for contravening the social law. But I will write to you on this subject from Florence. A father who presides over a supreme court of justice must not have to blush in the presence of his son. Good‑bye.”
