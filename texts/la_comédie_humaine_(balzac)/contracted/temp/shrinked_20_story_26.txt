DEDICATION

To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy.

My dear Nodier, I present a book of domestic deeds hidden from legal action behind closed household doors; in such affairs the finger of God, often called chance, takes the place of human justice, and the moral — though sometimes voiced by a scoffer — remains striking and instructive.

These tales teach much about Family and Maternity. We may yet see, perhaps too late, the consequences of the diminution of paternal authority. That authority, which once held sway until a father’s death, was the only human tribunal to which domestic crimes could be brought; kings sometimes aided its execution. However tender a mother may be, she cannot fulfil the patriarchal function any more than a woman can sit on a throne. Here I have shown plainly how essential to European society is the indissoluble marriage bond, how fatal feminine weakness can be, and how dangerous unrestrained selfish interests are. May a society that worships wealth shudder to see the impotence of the law in a system that deifies success and forgives any means to attain it. May it return to the Catholic religion for purification of its masses and an education other than that of a lay university.

In the “Scenes from Military Life” I shall set forth many noble self-devotions; allow me to point out the depraving effects of war’s necessities on minds who then act at home as if on the battlefield.

You have cast a sagacious glance over our time; your philosophy shines in many bitter reflections. You have perceived the havoc wrought by the existence of four distinct political systems. I cannot more fitly dedicate this history than under your protection. Your name may defend my work against the criticisms sure to follow it—for where is the patient who keeps silence when the surgeon lifts the dressing from his wound?

To the pleasure of dedicating this Scene to you I add the pride of making known your friendship for one who subscribes himself

Your sincere admirer,
De Balzac
Paris, November, 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792, Issoudun had a physician, Doctor Rouget, whom the townspeople deemed consummately malignant. Rumor held that he made his wife miserable, though she had once been the beauty of the neighborhood. Whether she was foolish or merely timid, friends and enemies never fully exposed that household. Rouget was the sort of man people found “not pleasant to deal with,” and townsmen treated him with civility. His wife, a demoiselle Descoings, had been weak in health as a girl — supposedly the reason the doctor married her. She bore a son, Jean-Jacques, and, ten years later, a daughter, Agathe, whose birth surprised even her father.

These facts matter: without them Rouget would look like a monster, when in truth he embodied the malignity many shelter under the axiom that “men should have strength of character,” a belief that has caused much female misery.

The elder Descoings, his in-laws, were commission merchants in the wool trade: they bought for manufacturers, sold for producers, and pocketed commissions on both sides. They grew rich and miserly. Their son Descoings disliked Issoudun, went to Paris, and set up as a grocer in the rue Saint-Honoré, where, by love, patience, and a little money from his parents, he married his predecessor’s widow, Madame Bixiou.

In 1792 Descoings seemed prosperous. The old Descoings retired and bought forfeited estates. Doctor Rouget, foreseeing his wife’s death, sent Agathe to Paris to his brother-in-law, partly to let her see the capital and partly to execute a design: he judged that the childless brother-in-law and sister might grow fond of the girl. Agathe, whose beauty did not resemble either parent, had caused a breach between Rouget and his friend Lousteau, a former sub-delegate. Madame Rouget poured her griefs into Madame Hochon, Lousteau’s sister and Agathe’s godmother; Hochon later told most of Issoudun what it knew of Madame Rouget.

When Rouget told his wife he would send Agathe to Paris, her first words were, “I shall never see my daughter again.” Madame Hochon thought she was right. The poor mother grew yellow with grief; tongues said the doctor was killing her by inches. The behavior of her foolish son Jean-Jacques, who owed her little attention or respect, added to her misery. Jean-Jacques resembled his father in the worst ways; the doctor himself was at best unsatisfactory.

Agathe’s arrival did not bring happiness to her uncle. About the same time Descoings was arrested on a hint from Robespierre. He had doubted the alleged famine to customers in his shop, and citoyenne Duplay, Robespierre’s housekeeper, took offense and denounced him for “accaparation.” His wife’s ill-judged interventions only made matters worse. She appealed to Bridau, secretary to Roland, who sought to enlighten the arbiters, but they only rebuked him: “Why do you meddle?” Bridau advised patience, and his sensible efforts were wasted. A small gift to citoyenne Duplay might have saved Descoings; the lesson was that it is as dangerous to employ honest men as scoundrels — one must rely on oneself. Descoings perished on the scaffold with André Chénier. At the time his execution made more sensation than Chénier’s; posterity has since judged differently.

That shop near Robespierre’s lodging seemed accursed: César Birotteau later failed there as well. During Bridau’s visits to the widow, he was struck by Agathe’s cold, calm, innocent beauty and married her with the consent of her father. Rouget, eager to secure his plan, hurried to Paris to draw the marriage contract to his advantage. Bridau’s ardent and disinterested love gave carte blanche to the perfidious doctor, who took full advantage of his son-in-law’s blindness, as this history will show.

Madame Rouget — or rather Rouget — inherited the elder Descoings’ property; within two years both parents had died, and Rouget, after his wife’s death in 1799, acquired vineyards, farms, and ironworks. Jean-Jacques proved incapable of anything; the father destined him for a life of proprietorship and permitted him to grow up in wealth and silliness. After 1799 the cipherers of Issoudun credited the doctor with at least thirty thousand francs’ income. From his wife’s death he led a debauched life, kept within his house; this man of “strength of character” died in 1805. Jean-Jacques remained a bachelor for reasons partly his father’s fault, as will appear.

It is worth asking the result of the private vengeance the doctor took on a daughter he did not recognize as his own, though legitimately his. Agathe bore a strong likeness to the doctor’s mother; resemblances sometimes skip generations. In turn, Agathe’s eldest child, who resembled her physically, possessed the moral qualities of his grandfather, Doctor Rouget.

Agathe’s beauty was of a pure, placid kind: oval face, clear complexion, golden hair, composed mouth, long lashes, and tender dark-blue eyes. Bridau’s portrait of her hung in his atelier. She was the ideal provincial housekeeper, pious though not sanctimonious, educated by the Church. In ordinary terms she was an accomplished wife; yet her ignorance of the world made her vulnerable. The Roman epitaph “She did needlework and kept the house” suited her.

Under the Consulate Bridau adored Napoleon. In 1804 he became head of a department at the ministry of the interior, with a salary of twelve thousand francs and handsome emoluments. From 1804 to 1808 the Bridaus lived in a handsome suite on the Quai Voltaire, near the ministry and the Tuileries. A cook and a footman were their only servants. Agathe rose early, went to market with her cook, prepared breakfast; Bridau seldom left for the ministry before eleven. When together, she prepared his breakfast, saw him off at the window, cleared the table, dressed, played with the children, and later sat knitting by his writing table when he worked late. Sometimes they attended the theatre or used a ministerial box; Agathe enjoyed the occasional restaurant as any provincial new to Paris. Bridau received friends on Thursdays and gave an annual Shrove-Tuesday ball.

Their conjugal life had just three notable events: the births of two children three years apart, and Bridau’s death in 1808, killed by overwork just as Napoleon was about to appoint him director-general, a count, and councillor of state. The effort to finish work before the Emperor’s departure caused an inflammatory fever that killed him. Touched by Bridau’s devotion, Napoleon planned an order to reward civil services, the Order of the Reunion, to be worn with a blue ribbon, though Providence thwarted that profanation. After Bridau’s death the Emperor inquired into his widow’s circumstances: both sons received scholarships at the Imperial Lyceum paid from the Emperor’s privy purse, and Madame Bridau was given a pension of four thousand francs.

From her marriage to her husband’s death Agathe had no communication with Issoudun. Her mother died as she was about to bear her youngest son; when her father died, the coronation of the Emperor kept Bridau busy and Agathe unwilling to leave him. Her brother Jean-Jacques had not written. Once a year she received a letter from her godmother Madame Hochon, to which she replied in commonplace fashion and paid little heed to the pious advice therein.

Some time before Doctor Rouget’s death Madame Hochon warned Agathe she would get nothing from her father’s estate unless she gave a power of attorney to Monsieur Hochon. Agathe, reluctant to harass her brother, was passive. Whether because Bridau thought the spoliation legal or because he shared his wife’s magnanimity, he would not listen to Roguin, his notary, who urged him to use his ministerial position to contest the deeds by which the father had deprived his daughter. Thus husband and wife tacitly sanctioned the transfer. Roguin, however, forced Bridau to reflect that if he died first Agathe would be left without property. Reviewing their finances, Bridau found that between 1793 and 1805 they had used nearly thirty thousand of the fifty thousand francs that old Rouget had given to Agathe at her marriage. He had invested the remaining twenty thousand in public funds then quoted at forty, producing about two thousand francs a year. As a widow she could live suitably on an income of six thousand francs. With provincial good sense she planned to change residence, dismiss the footman, and keep only a cook; but her intimate friend Madame Descoings, insisting on being considered her aunt, sold her own establishment and came to live with Agathe, taking Bridau’s study as her bedroom.

The two widows pooled their revenues, jointly possessing about twelve thousand francs a year. Such simple, “natural” measures often deserve attention; fools excuse wrongs as natural. Behold the serpent amid flowers.

In 1809 Madame Descoings, who never told her age, was sixty-five. Once called a beauty, she had kept her looks by constitution: medium height, plump, fresh, with fine shoulders and a rosy complexion; her blond hair showed no gray despite her husband’s fate. She loved good cheer, the theatre, and a secret vice — lottery shares. Like women lucky enough to stay young, she spent on dress and was a pleasant companion, never opposing anyone and exchanging jests. If she did not remarry, it was no doubt the times: under the Empire men found young women too easily.

Madame Descoings often cheered Madame Bridau with theatre and drives, prepared excellent dinners, and even tried to marry her to her own son by her first husband, Bixiou. To this end she revealed a family secret kept by her, her late husband, and their notary: her son Bixiou, thirty-five and a widowed major who later perished at Lützen, had a son — a grandson she secretly saw and claimed as the child of her first wife. That boy, educated with Agathe’s sons at the Imperial Lyceum on a half-scholarship, proved clever, witty, and gifted as a draughtsman and designer.

Agathe, who lived only for her children, declined to remarry out of fidelity to her husband and good sense. But being a good mother is harder than being a good wife; a widow must both love and command her children. Few women are firm enough for this double duty. Thus Agathe, despite many virtues, became the innocent cause of grave unhappiness. Through her lack of intelligence and blind confidence, she fell victim to Madame Descoings, who nursed a combination of three numbers in a lottery and used the joint household money to pay stakes, hoping at last to win and enrich her grandson, Agathe, and the little Bridaus.

When the debts reached ten thousand francs she increased her stakes; failing to win, the debt rolled up. At twenty thousand francs she lost her head, still hoping for a trey. She attempted to mortgage her life-interest, but Roguin, the notary, showed that the late Doctor Rouget had secured her only a life-interest by disinheriting Madame Descoings in favor of his son Jean-Jacques. No money-lender would advance twenty thousand francs to a sixty-six-year-old on an annuity of about four thousand. One morning Madame Descoings fell at Agathe’s feet and, sobbing, confessed the state of things. Agathe reproached her not; she dismissed the footman and cook, sold all but the bare necessities of her furniture, sold three-fourths of her government funds, paid the debts, and bade farewell to her appartement.

One of the worst corners in Paris was the section of the rue Mazarin between the rue Guenegard and the rue de Seine, behind the Institute. The high gray walls of the college and Cardinal Mazarin’s library cast chill shadows there; the sun seldom shone and the north wind blew. The poor ruined widow, Madame Bridau, took a third-floor lodging in a shallow house at that damp, dark corner. Opposite were the Institute buildings with the studios of young artists—rapins—then still there. From Madame Bridau’s new windows one could peer into those melancholy barred cages. Looking north the view closed on the dome of the Institute; up the street the only movement was a file of hackney-coaches. She planted boxes of earth at the windows, cultivating illicit aerial gardens whose vegetables improved the air. The staircase wound round upon itself; the third floor was the last. Three windows opened to three rooms—dining-room, a small salon, and a chamber on one side of the landing; on the other a little kitchen and two single rooms; above, an immense garret without partitions. She chose this lodging because it cost only four hundred francs a year on a nine-year lease, because it was near her sons’ school, and because she was used to the quarter.

The interior matched the outside. The dining-room, hung with yellow paper patterned with small green flowers and floored with unglazed tiles, contained only a table, two sideboards, and six chairs brought from the other apartment. The salon had an Aubusson carpet given to Bridau when the ministry was refurnished, and a common mahogany sofa with Egyptian heads covered in green silk. Over it hung an amateur pastel portrait of Bridau that drew the eye: the noble, obscure brow, serene yet proud eyes, prudent lips, and frank smile suggested a man of duty—“Justum et tenacem,” as the Emperor had said. On the opposite wall, over a card-table, flashed a picture of the Emperor in brilliant colors by Vernet, Napoleon riding rapidly, attended by his escort.

Agathe had given herself two large birdcages—canaries and Java sparrows—and adopted this childish fancy since her husband’s loss. After three months the widowed chamber had become a litter: cats domiciled on the sofa; canaries, occasionally let loose, left droppings on the furniture. She scattered millet and chickweed and put tidbits for the cats in broken saucers. Garments lay everywhere. Everything of Bridau’s was scrupulously preserved: his desk implements were kept with the care one might give a knight’s armor; she had wrapped and sealed the last pen he used and marked it so. The cup from which he drank his last draught stood on the fireplace. After his death no trace of coquetry or ordinary self-care remained in the young widow of thirty-five. Parted from the only man she had known and loved—one who had never caused her unhappiness—she seemed to have renounced her womanhood. Some people, through love, transfer the self to another; when that other dies, no life of their own remains.

Agathe now lived only for her children and was infinitely sad at the privations their ruin would bring. From the move to the rue Mazarin a melancholy took her face. She hoped a little in the Emperor; he could do no more than he already was doing—giving three hundred francs a year to each child from his privy purse, besides scholarships.

The brilliant Descoings occupied an apartment on the second floor, similar to her niece’s above. She had assigned Madame Bridau three thousand francs out of her annuity; Roguin, the notary, arranged it, but it would take seven years of slow repayment to make good the loss. The Descoingses, reduced to an income of twelve hundred francs, lived modestly with her niece. They employed a woman-of-all-work for mornings only; Madame Descoings liked to cook. In evenings a few old friends—persons at the ministry who owed their places to Bridau—came for cards with the two widows. Madame Descoings still cherished her trey, declaring it obstinate about turning up; she hoped by one stroke to repay the loan she’d made upon her niece. She was dear to the Bridaus partly from a feeling of wrong she had done them and partly because her niece never reproached her under privation. So Philippe and Joseph were cosseted; the old gambler of the Imperial Lottery cooked them nice dinners with sweets. Later, they could extract small sums from her—one for pencils and paper, the elder for tennis-shoes and marbles. Madame Descoings’s passion forced her to be content with fifty francs a month for domestic expenses so she could gamble with the rest.

Madame Bridau, by motherly love, kept her expenses to the same sum. As penance for former overconfidence she cut off little enjoyments. Timid and distrusting, she exaggerated risk into virtue. She feared the Emperor might die or forget them; she shuddered at the risk her children ran of being left without means. Unable to understand Roguin’s assurance that in seven years Descoings’s assignment would restore the sold sum, she trusted neither notary, aunt, nor government; she trusted only herself and practiced privation. By laying aside three thousand francs every year from her pension she fancied she would have thirty thousand in ten years, yielding fifteen hundred a year to her children. At thirty-six she might live twenty years more; if she kept to the plan she could leave each child enough for bare necessities.

Thus the two widows passed from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty—one through a vice, the other through the purest virtue. These petty details matter: the view into student dens below, the tumult of the rapins, the need to look up at the sky to escape the damp street, the presence of that portrait full of soul, the sight of rich old colors in the calm home, the mother’s preference for her elder child and opposition to the younger’s tastes—these facts prefaced this history and perhaps explain Joseph Bridau, one of the greatest painters of the modern French school.

Philippe, the elder, strikingly resembled his mother. Blond and blue-eyed, he had a daring look taken for intrepidity and courage. Old Claparon, who had entered the ministry with Bridau and was one of the whist players, tapped Philippe’s cheek and prophesied he’d “stand to his guns.” Stimulated, the boy assumed a resolute manner, became adroit at bodily exercises; fights at the Lyceum taught endurance and contempt for pain, laying the foundation of military valor. He acquired a distaste for study; public education failed to develop mind and body together.

Agathe believed physical resemblance implied moral likeness; she confidently expected delicacy of feeling combined with manly vigor. Philippe was fifteen when the family moved to rue Mazarin; his winning ways confirmed maternal belief. Joseph, three years younger, resembled his father but only in defective ways. His thick black hair was always disorderly; Philippe’s was neat. By some fatality Joseph could not keep his clothes clean; dress him in new clothes and he made them look old. The elder took care of his things out of vanity. Unconsciously Agathe scolded Joseph and held up his brother as example; she never treated them alike. Fetching them from school she worried especially about Joseph. Such trifles drove her into maternal preference.

No one among the ordinary circle—Du Bruel, Claparon, Desroches, or even the Abbé Loraux—noticed Joseph’s faculty for observation. Absorbed in his own tastes, the future colorist paid no attention to himself; during childhood this torpor alarmed his father. His face, which later gained originality, wore a sullen expression. The features were pinched, contracted by the close attention Joseph paid to his surroundings. Philippe flattered his mother with clever sayings and lively answers, prompting hope. Joseph was taciturn, a dreamer. The mother hoped great things of Philippe and expected nothing of Joseph.

Joseph’s leaning to art began simply. During Easter holidays of 1812, returning from a walk in the Tuileries with his brother and Madame Descoings, he saw a pupil drawing a caricature of a professor on the wall of the Institute and stopped in admiration. The next day he stood at the window watching pupils enter by the rue Mazarin door; then slipped into the long courtyard with statues, busts, half-finished marbles, plasters, and baked clays; he gazed feverishly—his instinct awakened, his vocation stirred. He entered a ground-floor room with a half-open door where a dozen young men drew from a statue; they began to make fun of him.

“Hi! little one,” cried the first, scattering crumbs.

They mocked his ugliness. For a quarter hour Joseph bore the teasing in Chaudet’s atelier. But his persistency and expression struck the pupils; when asked what he wanted, Joseph said he wished to learn to draw. They encouraged him. Won by friendliness, he said he was Madame Bridau’s son.

“Oh! if you are Madame Bridau’s son,” they cried, “you will certainly be a great man!” They teased and tested him—one pupil set up a “telegraph trial,” stretching Joseph’s arms and daring him to hold them so. Joseph, at thirteen, stood motionless for five minutes amid shouts; at ten minutes sweat stood on his brow. Then a bald-headed, pale, sickly man entered—Chaudet. He lowered Joseph’s arms, patted him, and asked what brought him. Hearing Joseph wanted to be an artist and was Madame Bridau’s son, Chaudet, struck by recollection of Bridau’s service, welcomed him. He gave him a board, paper, and chalks, sent a pupil for cakes and sugar-plums, and petted the child. The scene—rough, warm-hearted, and real—impressed Joseph. Chaudet, for whom the Emperor’s protection earlier opened prospects later cut short by premature death, appeared like a vision. Joseph told his mother nothing, but he spent two hours every Sunday and Thursday in Chaudet’s atelier. Madame Descoings, who humored the boy’s fancies, supplied pencils, red chalks, prints, and paper. At school he sketched masters, drew comrades, and showed surprising assiduity in drawing-class. Lemire, his drawing-master, reported Joseph’s faculty to Madame Bridau. She, like a true provincial with little knowledge of art, was terrified. When Lemire left, she wept.

“What is to become of me!” she cried to Madame Descoings. “Joseph, whom I meant to make a government clerk—protected by his father’s memory at the ministry—wants to be a painter, a vagabond! I always knew that child would give me nothing but trouble.”

Madame Descoings confessed she had encouraged Joseph’s passion, taking him to the Salon where his interest in pictures was, she declared, miraculous.

“If he understands painting at thirteen,” she said, “your Joseph will be a man of genius.”

“Yes; and see what genius did for his father—killed him with overwork at forty!” Agathe answered.

As Joseph entered his fourteenth year, Agathe, against Madame Descoings’s entreaties, went to Chaudet and requested he cease to “debauch” her son. She found the sculptor in a blue smock modeling his last statue, working with spasmodic jerks born of passion. He received the widow roughly at first but, moved by Bridau’s memory and by devotion to craftsmanship, he burst into holy wrath at her plea.

“I was under obligations to your deceased husband; I wished to help his son, to watch his first steps in the noblest of careers,” he cried. “A great artist is a king—happier, independent; he reigns in the world of fancy. Your son has a glorious future. Faculties like his are rare; they are disclosed at his age in beings like Giottos, Raphaels, Titians. In my opinion he will make a better painter than sculptor. If I had such a son, I should be as happy as the Emperor is to have given himself the King of Rome. You are mistress of his fate. Make him a miserable quill-driver, tie him to a desk, and you’ve murdered him! But a true vocation is stronger than obstacles. Vocation means a call—God’s election. You will make your child unhappy, that’s all.” He flung the clay aside and dismissed the day’s work.

Agathe, alarmed by the sight of a nude woman in a corner, left horrified. Chaudet told his pupils she was not to bring the little Bridau any more; they jeered as she closed the door. When the students learned she opposed the Institute, their happiness turned to the project of getting Joseph among them. He often slipped into Regnauld the painter’s studio where he was encouraged to paint. When the widow complained the bargain was not kept, Chaudet’s pupils insisted they were not Chaudet and composed a long mocking song about her.

That evening Agathe refused cards and sat drowned in tears.

“What is the matter, Madame Bridau?” asked old Claparon.

“She thinks her boy will have to beg his bread because he has the bump of painting,” Madame Descoings said. The men offered their usual advice: let the boy follow his bent; if not a genius, he could later be placed in an office. Desroches spoke of keeping a son hard at work to make a man of him; Du Bruel warned against letting a boy choose a profession at his age. Claparon and others counselled resignation to Joseph’s tastes. Their unanimity only increased Agathe’s anxieties. She feared the atelier’s naked women; Madame Descoings joked about “good fires.”

Soon after, the disasters of the retreat from Moscow became known; Napoleon returned to Paris to organize troops. Philippe, tired of school, wanted to serve the Emperor; he saw a review at the Tuileries—the last Napoleon ever held—and became infatuated with soldier’s life. Military splendor and uniforms seduced him. Without his mother’s knowledge he wrote a petition to the Emperor asking to enter the army. Within twenty-four hours the Emperor sent him to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr; six months later, in November 1813, he was appointed sub-lieutenant in a cavalry regiment. He spent that winter in cantonments, learned to ride, and was dispatched to the front. During the campaign in France he was made lieutenant after an outpost affair where his bravery saved his colonel’s life. The Emperor named him captain at La Fere-Champenoise and took him on his staff. Inspired, Philippe won the cross at Montereau, witnessed Napoleon’s farewell at Fontainebleau, refused to serve the Bourbons, and returned in July 1814 to find his mother ruined.

Joseph’s scholarship was withdrawn after the holidays; Madame Bridau vainly entreated that it be inscribed on the ministry rolls. Joseph, more devoted than ever to painting, begged to go to Regnauld and promised to earn his living, saying he was advanced enough in the second class to do without rhetoric. Philippe, a captain at nineteen and decorated, who had served the Emperor as aide-de-camp in two battles, flattered the mother immensely. Coarse and blustering, without merit beyond cavalry bravery, he was to her a man of genius; whereas Joseph, puny, sickly, unkempt, and dreaming an artist’s glory, would only worry her.

The winter of 1814–1815 favored Joseph. Secretly encouraged by Madame Descoings and Bixiou, a pupil of Gros, he worked in that celebrated atelier, forming an intimacy with Schinner. The return from Elba came; Captain Bridau joined the Emperor at Lyons, accompanied him to the Tuileries, and commanded a squadron in the Guard’s dragoons. After Waterloo—where he was slightly wounded and took the officer’s Legion of Honor—he was near Marshal Davoust at Saint-Denis and, through Davoust’s intercession, retained his cross and rank though placed on half-pay.

Joseph, anxious about his future, studied with ardor through these turbulent times, sometimes to the point of illness.

“It is the smell of the paints,” Agathe said to Madame Descoings. “He ought to give up a business so injurious to his health.”

Agathe’s anxieties remained for her lieutenant-colonel son. When he returned in 1816 reduced from a nine-thousand-franc commander’s salary to half-pay, she fitted attic rooms for him and spent her savings. Philippe belonged to the faithful Bonapartes of café Lemblin; he acquired the habits and airs of a half-pay officer, exaggerated them, spurned the Bourbons, and refused infantry opportunities. To his mother he seemed noble. “The father himself could have done no more,” she said.

Philippe’s half-pay sufficed; he cost little at home, whereas Joseph’s expenses were paid by the widows. From then Agathe’s preference for Philippe was openly shown. Up to that time it had been secret; but the colonel’s persecution for the Emperor and his wound drew forth maternal tenderness. “He is unfortunate” explained everything to her. Joseph, simple in the artist’s way and brought up to admire his big brother, was not hurt by maternal preference and shared his worship of the hero who had carried Napoleon’s orders. How could he doubt the superiority of the brother he had seen in green and gold, commanding on the Champ de Mars?

Agathe, notwithstanding preference, was an excellent mother. She loved Joseph, though she could not understand him. Joseph adored his mother; Philippe let her adore him. Toward her the dragoon softened his military roughness yet never concealed contempt for Joseph—expressed in a patronizing, almost friendly way. He nicknamed the weak, shrunken seventeen-year-old “Cub.” Philippe’s patronizing manners would have wounded anyone less indifferent than Joseph, who believed soldiers hid goodness beneath brutal exteriors. Joseph did not yet know that soldiers of genius can be gentle and courteous; all genius is alike.

“Poor boy!” said Philippe to his mother, “we mustn’t plague him; let him do as he likes.” To Agathe, the colonel’s contempt seemed fraternal affection. “Philippe will always love and protect his brother,” she thought.

In 1816 Joseph obtained his mother’s permission to convert the garret adjoining his attic into an atelier; Madame Descoings contributed money for his necessities. The two widows treated painting as a trade, but Joseph arranged the humble studio; Madame Descoings induced the owner to add a skylight, Joseph painted the hall chocolate and hung sketches, and Agathe reluctantly installed an iron stove so her son could work at home without abandoning Gros’s or Schinner’s studios.

Meanwhile the constitutional party, backed by half-pay officers and Bonapartists, stirred emeutes about the Chamber of Deputies and rumors of conspiracies circulated. Philippe, implicated in these circles, was arrested and freed for lack of proof; the minister of war restored him to the active list, ending his half-pay. France seemed unsafe; Agathe feared provocateurs. Philippe spent time in disaffected cafés, drinking and picking up bad habits while Agathe lived in dread. The Grecian sages still visited Madame Bridau nightly. Claparon, retained at the ministry though cautious, whispered Moniteur news. Desroches, retired after old Du Bruel, still fought for his pension. Their friends urged Agathe to send Philippe abroad.

“They talk of conspiracies, and your son will be certain to fall a victim,” they warned.

“Philippe is cut from the wood the Emperor made into marshals,” Du Bruel replied; “he mustn’t give up his profession. Let him serve in the East, in India—”

“Think of his health,” Agathe answered.

“Why doesn’t he get some place, some business?” Desroches asked. “There are private offices to be had.” Agathe insisted Philippe was a soldier and wouldn’t want anything else. Du Bruel suggested royal favor could make him lieutenant-colonel, though Madame Descoings doubted his prospects without noble birth.

To please his mother, Philippe took up the notion of joining General Lallemand in the United States and helping found the Champ d’Asile, one of the most disastrous swindles run under the name of a national subscription. Agathe gave ten thousand francs and saw him off at Havre. By the end of 1817 she lived on the six hundred francs left and, by prudent investment, secured seven percent on savings. Joseph emulated her frugality: he dressed plainly, denied himself comforts, and lived on bread, milk, and Brie; only Madame Descoings and Bixiou showed sympathy.

Joseph later recalled how the coming summer saved him the cost of charcoal. Now a colorist as capable as Gros, he consulted his master only occasionally. He was already planning a revolt against classical traditions, aiming to free art to represent Nature — a struggle that began with his first Salon exhibition and never ceased.

1818 proved terrible. Roguin, Madame Descoings’s notary, absconded with seven years’ withheld annuity funds. Three days later a bill of exchange for a thousand francs arrived from New York: Philippe had lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. The letter told of debts contracted and comrades who had indorsed for him. Agathe, blaming herself, wept. The two women faced selling silver, linen, and furniture. Joseph, alarmed, told Gérard, who procured an order for two royal copies of Louis XVIII’s portrait at five hundred francs each and supplied materials. Joseph painted four panels in ten days, sold them, and brought his mother the thousand francs to meet Philippe’s draft. Then a later letter announced Philippe’s imminent return on a packet whose captain had trusted him for passage; he would need a thousand francs at Havre.

Joseph promised his mother the money after finishing the copies. Agathe, ecstatically grateful, kissed him and called him “his father all over.” Madame Bridau rushed to Havre and watched beyond the round tower daily for the packet. The sight of home softened Philippe, but his misfortunes in Texas and New York had brought out his worst traits: he had grown brutal, selfish, rude, given to drink and smoking, and imagined himself persecuted. Such men either live by honest virtues or treat every want as necessity; Philippe had drifted to the latter, becoming cunning and self-centered while keeping an air of frank soldierly simplicity. His figure was stouter, face bronzed, speech terse and decisive; exposed to hardship he had become a man who makes himself feared. To his mother he remained a hero; in truth he had become, bluntly, a blackguard.

At Havre Agathe bought him clothes and could not at first restrain his indulgence. The scheme of conquering Texas with imperial remnants had failed from treachery and poor management; the Liberals had used the enterprise selfishly, abandoning devoted men. Agathe, a household manager with shrewd common sense, saw through the political chicane and recognized the subscription’s failure; she could not make her son see he had been duped rather than persecuted. During the costly week at Havre she could not persuade him to make terms with the royal government. She brought him back to Paris before her funds gave out.

On arrival, those awaiting them noted how much Agathe had aged. Philippe greeted Madame Descoings coolly. Joseph, now twenty-one and confident, was hurt by his brother’s contempt. At a family dinner the friends — Du Bruel, Claparon, Desroches, and the Abbé Loraux — observed the exile’s roughened manners and coarse voice. While Joseph prepared the card tables, the intimate friends asked Agathe what she intended for Philippe. She could not get him to serve the Bourbons; Du Bruel suggested other roles like writing plays, as his son had once done. Philippe, Bixiou, and young Desroches played écarté. Madame Descoings, eager to celebrate the prodigal’s return, provided funds for play; Joseph secretly slipped twenty francs to Philippe.

The evening grew lively. Philippe, a poor player, ended owing fifty francs. He showed bad temper and quarreled with young Desroches and then with Desroches senior, who forbade further play though his son was right. Madame Descoings silenced her grandson’s barbed witticisms for fear of provoking the colonel. Philippe’s temper and gambling continued into the night.

The next morning the two widows, counting resources, resolved to economize: Madame Descoings proposed selling furniture and giving up apartments to save seven hundred francs a year and pay Philippe fifty francs monthly until he found something. They pooled incomes — about five thousand three hundred francs, four thousand from Madame Descoings as a life annuity — and had consumed their savings. Philippe promised to find a situation and asked only for board and lodging. Agathe kissed him; Madame Descoings slipped him a hundred francs to cover his losses.

Within ten days the furniture was sold and the household rearranged. Philippe’s habits hardened: he left after breakfast, returned at dinner, and spent evenings in cafés drinking brandy, reading newspapers, playing billiards at the Café Minerve, smoking many cigars, and gambling at night. He boasted of a regular life and, while often winning, returned home talking of smashing the ultras and the Bourbon bodyguard.

He dined often with army comrades who spoke freely of hopes based on plots and inventions like a submarine to restore the Emperor. He particularly liked an old dragoons’ captain, Giroudeau, whose company led Philippe into further dissipation and into seeing women as resources. Giroudeau, a bluff old roue, introduced Philippe to the theatre and to a young dancer, Florentine, and to another, Marie Godeschal — Mariette — who was beautiful, cold, and ambitious. Mariette, orphaned and poor, had a devoted brother who clerked in Derville’s law office; she resolved to use her beauty and talent to help him. Poorly dressed yet striking, she provoked Philippe’s passion. To Mariette, Philippe was a Guard staff-officer, and a liaison with him would elevate her above rivals. Florentine and Giroudeau encouraged a kind of morganatic connection between Philippe and Mariette.

Giroudeau promised to speak to his nephew Finot at a theatrical journal and to get Philippe a place on a newspaper. Philippe revealed his poverty, but Giroudeau reassured him: his nephew could put him on a paper or help him get patronage. Mariette, thinking Philippe would write for a newspaper and help her career, accepted him.

The next day Philippe met Giroudeau at the subscription office where the veteran lived in a cramped room labeled “Subscription Office.” There they encountered Coloquinte, a one-armed man, and then climbed to Giroudeau’s nephew Finot’s attic, where Finot, cool and sardonic, eyed Philippe. Giroudeau praised the colonel; Finot mocked the Texan affair a little, then gave Philippe a plan: exploit his position as a victim of the Liberals and demand an account of the Champ d’Asile subscription. A new Opposition journal was starting; Finot could make Philippe its cashier with a salary of three thousand francs if Philippe found someone to go security for twenty thousand francs. “Threaten loudly,” Finot advised, “and we’ll silence you with a place.” Philippe was grateful and impressed by Finot’s worldly cleverness.

That night at cafés Philippe fulminated against the Liberals, declaring he would demand an accounting of the moneys collected. He went to Mariette to tell her of the expected post on a newspaper with ten thousand subscribers and how he would advance her claims.

Agathe and Madame Descoings waited anxiously for Philippe one night amid the political alarm following the assassination of the Duc de Berry. He returned with good news: he had been promised the cashiership of a new journal at three thousand francs on condition they could go security for twenty thousand francs by depositing their shares in the Funds as guarantee while drawing the interest. The friends urged Agathe to go security; she and Madame Descoings, overjoyed at the prospect of providing for Philippe, transferred her shares — representing thirteen hundred francs in interest — to secure him. Philippe promised to pay one hundred francs monthly for board and lodging and was acclaimed the best of sons. Those who had doubted him congratulated Agathe and admitted they had been unjust. Joseph, not to be outdone, resolved to pay for his own support and succeeded.

Three months after Philippe obtained the modest pay that made him, in his mother’s eyes, “easy in mind,” the colonel’s appetite and taste for luxury began to press on the household. He nitpicked at the food and forced the widows to spend beyond their means, though he had not yet contributed a penny. His mother and Madame Descoings, delicate women, did not remind him of his promise; Philippe eased his conscience by seldom dining at home.

A feuilleton edited by Vernou and championed by Finot put Mariette, the celebrated dancer, in a leading position at the Porte-Saint-Martin. Among the directors was a wealthy, indulgent general in love with an actress; he and other acquaintances knew Philippe, and with the help of Giroudeau and others arranged Mariette’s debut. Bixiou, mischievous and indiscreet, soon told Agathe Bridau and Madame Descoings that Philippe, their hero of heroes, was in love with Mariette. The widows were thunderstruck; Agathe, devout, regarded actresses as morally lost, and both imagined they lived off gold and squandered fortunes.

Joseph Bridau assured his mother Philippe would not be such a fool as to spend their money; yet Bixiou reassured them further, saying Mariette would be engaged at the Opera and leave Philippe to the diplomatic admirers who frequented the theatre. Bixiou also dropped gossip about the death of old Claparon and his son the banker’s parsimony, tacked on as an aside.

Philippe, driven partly by mercenary motives, proposed to Mariette, but she, on the verge of an engagement at the Grand Opera and liking her independence, refused—either suspecting his motive or valuing her autonomy. After that she left for London in May, while Philippe, who had already begun to love her despite her infidelities, could not follow. For the next year and a half Philippe led the life of a Parisian pleasure-seeker: dinners, suppers, theatres, journalists’ camaraderie. From four in the afternoon, when his work ended, until midnight he was at the theatre or at social gatherings arranged the previous night. He kept company with singers and actresses, with Giroudeau, Finot, Lousteau, Vernou, and Bixiou; his life glittered with diversions.

This carnival did not last without consequence. Mariette’s début at the Opera in January 1821 attracted a duke’s passion; Philippe tried to compete but by April, driven by passion and bad luck, had dipped into the funds he managed as a cashier. By May he had taken eleven hundred francs. Forsaken by Mariette to other admirers, she nonetheless retained a particular gratitude to Philippe for smoothing the path of her career, but she had never regarded him as anything more than a stepping-stone. Left behind and desperate, Philippe returned to his attic room in his mother’s flat. He realized that the married world Mariette provided—its constant novelty, its dinners, the company, the gossip—had become to him a necessity as pressing as tobacco or brandy. The thought of living without that atmosphere, rather than the deficit about to be found, drove him toward despair and even mentioned suicide.

The next morning, in Joseph’s studio, Philippe’s sense of ruin burst out. Joseph, copying pictures for dealers and planning his own exhibition, was surprised when Philippe asked how long it would take to paint his portrait. Philippe, with forced gaiety, said he had only one day and then declared he intended never to return—he would blow his brains out. Joseph, shocked, revealed himself steady and discreet, promised to tell no one, and asked if Philippe were serious. Philippe confessed he had taken eleven hundred francs from the funds he held; his accounts were due and half his security was lost; their poor mother would be reduced to six hundred francs a year. He could not live with dishonor. Joseph, fraternal and practical, urged him to repay rather than die; Philippe fled.

Joseph immediately told Madame Descoings, who let fall a saucepan and sat stunned. Agathe, summoned and throwing herself into religious horror, lamented the fall of her son: “He! to fail in honor! the son of Bridau to take the money that was trusted to him!” She imagined the worst: Philippe dead in the Seine. Madame Descoings tried to soothe her, recognizing that passions ruin good people and pointing out that a single fault did not make a man a villain. She urged pity rather than condemnation.

While the family oscillated between hope and despair, Philippe audaciously presented accounts to his office explaining that he had taken eleven hundred francs home for safekeeping. Then, at five o’clock, he stole five hundred more from the desk and went to a gambling-house he had avoided since his paper connection, betting on the slender chance of a fortune. At first he won, gaining as much as six thousand francs, and tempted by the dream of solving everything in one stroke, rushed into a reckless stake: first nearly winning five thousand, then losing when chance turned. He lost all.

That night Philippe, having not really intended to kill himself, returned home in a daze. His mother, Madame Descoings, and Joseph greeted him with tears and relief. Philippe played the part of the contrite son well: he took his mother in his arms, let her press his hands to her heart, and accepted her pardon. A good dinner, two bottles of old wine, and a cigar, which the widows endured out of a belief that conceding him small comforts would keep him at home, calmed the household. Agathe slept not a wink that night and awoke aged by terror; the idea of living on six hundred francs was intolerable, yet she resolved to be a nurse rather than be supported by her children. Madame Descoings, who had twice sacrificed large sums for the Bridau family, planned to put saving schemes in motion and to spare no effort to preserve family honor.

At daybreak Madame Descoings proposed a solution: have an old, shrewd friend, Desroches, spin a tale to the newspapers—suggest that Philippe had trusted a friend and become the victim of his weakness, so that Philippe could resign rather than be dismissed for dishonor. Desroches, who had owed Bridau favors, obliged. He visited, dined with the family, persuaded Agathe to sign the transfer of funds to obtain her pension of six hundred francs, and forced Philippe to sign a petition to the minister of war asking reinstatement. Desroches promised to follow the petition at the war office and secure the influence of the Duc de Maufrigneuse, who might get Philippe back into service. He predicted Philippe would be lieutenant-colonel in the duke’s regiment within three months. The household breathed. As for the journal that had promoted Mariette, it soon folded; the public never knew of Philippe’s crime and Agathe’s belief in her son remained painfully shaken but not entirely destroyed.

When, after a fortnight, things resumed their placid course, Philippe relapsed into the life of ease and dissipation: cafés, billiards, amusements, nightly visits to the gambling-table, where he gambled recklessly but modestly enough to appear economical at home. He paraded shabby clothes to deceive his mother—greasy hat, patched boots, shabby overcoat that made the Legion of Honor ribbon nearly invisible. Mariette was the object of his passion; her betrayal had hardened him. When flush, or dining with friends, he affected contempt for other women, but usually he breakfasted and dined at home, returning about one in the morning. Three months of this inconsistent life restored some confidence in Agathe.

Joseph, meanwhile, lived in his atelier, working on the grand picture that would later bring him fame. Supported by the affectionate patronage of Madame Descoings, who took his breakfasts and attended to little things, Joseph spent his days reading, perfecting his craft, waiting for the Salon. He was known only among painters then, but he had support—Schinner got him commissions at a château and dealers supplied copying work. He hoped to earn eighteen hundred to two thousand francs a year beyond expenses; he had a serious picture ready for the exhibition that could make his fortune.

Madame Descoings, meanwhile, had her own passion: the lottery. She nursed a “trey” for nearly twenty-one years, certain that when it finally turned up she would secure all their fortunes. She economized and hid savings inside her mattress, planning to stake everything in the last drawing. The narrator pauses to note the social function of the lottery: a poor person’s opiate, it gave five days’ delight for a small sacrifice, a fairy of hope that civilized pleasures otherwise inaccessible could be imagined. For Madame Descoings, the trey became an obsession: she consulted cards, interpreted dreams, pinned her faith to the single number that had always been missing and intended to bet ruinously on the last drawing.

This gambler’s passion explained much of her behavior: the fat woman’s face, her secretiveness, her gluttonous gestures—traits that made her an excellent model for an aging woman in Joseph’s planned painting. Joseph used her expression, mixed with other references, to compose a remarkable picture of a young courtesan led by an old woman to a Venetian Doge—a painting that would later be mistaken by Gros for a Titian and pave the way for Joseph’s acclaim at the Salon of 1823. Even as he worked on this masterpiece, economic necessity forced Joseph to copy old masters for dealers; in doing so he learned their techniques and trained his brush to excellence. He prudently began to conceal the modest savings he had accumulated from his mother and Madame Descoings, knowing each had their own ruinous peril: Agathe’s indulgence in Philippe and Madame Descoings’s lottery mania.

Joseph had bought an antique chest and used it to hide money, placing the small sums for daily expenses in a skull-shaped compartment. Since Philippe’s return, Joseph noticed discrepancies: the hundred francs a month disappeared faster than accounted for. At first he joked, then became concerned; after several months of missing sums, he confided in Madame Descoings. She, loving him tenderly as a second son, shared his alarm. Philippe alone could have taken the money. “Why didn’t he ask me?” Joseph wondered. “Is it likely I would refuse him?” But theft was theft. Madame Descoings suggested hiding a special sum in silver and watching the studio to see who entered. The next day, proof arrived: Philippe entered the studio when Joseph was absent and took what he needed. Joseph’s anger and fear for their future mounted.

Joseph, practical and slightly sardonic, expressed frustrations. He believed Philippe a dead weight, forever falling into schemes and demanding rescue—sent to America at family expense or living wildly, and always returning at cost to his mother. He feared Philippe would drag Agathe to beggary. He did not resent personal privations but saw his brother’s thoughtless selfishness as a threat to the family’s honor and comfort. Bixiou agreed: Philippe was, in his view, a rogue. Joseph’s barbs were mingled with a deep affection for his brother and mother.

The chapter closes with Joseph’s contempt, mingled with concern and irony, at the splendid contrast between Philippe’s handsome appearance—the “heavenly blue eyes” and Bayard look—and the scoundrel he had become. Philanthropy, indulgence, religious devotion, and the poker-faced diplomacy of friends like Desroches had for a time saved the family from ruin and preserved appearances. But the twin dangers remained: Philippe’s restless passions and gambling, and Madame Descoings’s lottery obsession. Joseph, the true artist, continued to work and hope, sheltering his modest hoard from both. The household’s fragile peace balanced on their illusions and small deceptions: Philippe’s ability to play contrite son when necessary, Desroches’s promises to reinstate him, and Madame Descoings’s faith in a trey that might at any moment deliver them from anxiety. Meanwhile, the thefts from Joseph’s studio signaled that the old threats were far from over.

In spite of the coolness and discretion with which Philippe played his nightly trifling game, he was sometimes what gamblers call “cleaned out.” Driven by the necessity of having his evening stake of ten francs, he plundered the household, taking his brother’s money and whatever Madame Descoings or Agathe left about. The poor mother had a dreadful vision in her first sleep: Philippe entered the room and took from her gown every coin he could find. Agathe pretended to sleep but spent the night crying. “One wrong act is not a vice,” Madame Descoings had once said; after so many repetitions, vice was unmistakable. Agathe could doubt no longer: her beloved son had neither delicacy nor honor.

The morning after that frightful vision, before Philippe left after breakfast, Agathe drew him into her chamber and begged him to ask her for whatever money he needed. After that, his applications were so numerous that in two weeks Agathe was drained of all her savings. She was without a penny and began to think of working. She and Madame Descoings had discussed taking in worsted work from a shop called the “Pere de Famille,” which paid about twenty sous a day. Madame Descoings guessed the motive of this desire despite Agathe’s silence. Her appearance changed: the fresh face withered, skin clung to temples and cheekbones, deep lines cut her forehead; her eyes lost their clearness; an inward fire consumed her; she wept most nights.

A chief cause of this ruin was the need to hide her anguish. She never slept until Philippe came in; she listened for his step, learned the inflections of his voice, the variations of his walk, even the sound of his cane on pavement. Nothing escaped her. She knew how drunk he was by the way he stumbled and the state of his boots. When he had drunk and won, his voice was gruff and his cane dragged; when he had lost, his step was sharp and angry, he hummed in a clear voice and carried his cane as if presenting arms. At breakfast, if he had won, he was gay and affectionate; gloomy when he had lost, his brusque speech, hard glance, and depression frightened them. Debauchery and liquor debased a once-handsome countenance: veins swelled, features coarsened, eyelashes fell away, eyes became hard and dry. No longer careful of his person, Philippe smelled of taverns and muddy boots.

“You ought,” said Madame Descoings to Philippe in late December, “to get yourself new clothes from head to foot.”

“And who is to pay for it?” he answered sharply. “My poor mother hasn’t a sou; and I have five hundred francs a year. It would take my whole pension; besides I have mortgaged it for three years—”

“What for?” asked Joseph.

“A debt of honor. Giroudeau borrowed a thousand francs from Florentine to lend me. I am not gorgeous, that’s a fact; but when one thinks that Napoleon is at Saint Helena, and has sold his plate for the means of living, his faithful soldiers can manage to walk on their bare feet,” he said, showing his boots without heels, as he marched away.

“He is not bad,” said Agathe, “he has good feelings.”

“You can love the Emperor and yet dress properly,” said Joseph. “If he would take any care of himself and his clothes, he wouldn’t look so like a vagabond.”

“Joseph! have some indulgence for your brother,” cried Agathe. “You do as you like, while he is certainly not in his right place.”

“What did he leave it for?” demanded Joseph. “What can it matter to him whether Louis the Eighteenth’s bugs or Napoleon’s cuckoos are on the flag, if it is the flag of his country? France is France! For my part, I’d paint for the devil. A soldier ought to fight, if he is a soldier, for the love of his art. If he had stayed quietly in the army, he would have been a general by this time.”

“You are unjust to him,” said Agathe. “Your father, who adored the Emperor, would have approved of his conduct. However, he has consented to re-enter the army. God knows the grief it has caused your brother to do what he considers treachery.”

Joseph rose to return to his studio, but his mother took his hand and begged him to be good to his brother; he was so unfortunate.

Back in his painting-room, followed by Madame Descoings, Joseph found Philippe there, to their amazement.

“Joseph, my boy,” Philippe said off-hand, “I want some money. Confound it! I owe thirty francs for cigars at my tobacconist’s, and I dare not pass the cursed shop till I’ve paid it. I’ve promised to pay it a dozen times.”

“Well, I like your present way best,” said Joseph; “take what you want out of the skull.”

“I took all there was last night, after dinner.”

“There was forty-five francs.”

“Yes, that’s what I made it,” replied Philippe. “I took them; is there any objection?”

“No, my friend, no,” said Joseph. “If you were rich, I should do the same by you; only, before taking what I wanted, I should ask you if it were convenient.”

“It is humiliating to ask,” Philippe remarked; “I would rather see you taking as I do, without a word; it shows more confidence. In the army, if a comrade dies, and has a good pair of boots, and you have a bad pair, you change, that’s all.”

“Yes, but you don’t take them while he is living.”

“Oh, what meanness!” shrugged Philippe. “Well, so you haven’t got any money?”

“No,” said Joseph, determined not to show his hiding-place.

“In a few days we shall be rich,” said Madame Descoings.

“Yes, you; you think your trey is going to turn up on the 25th at the Paris drawing. You must have put in a fine stake if you think you can make us all rich.”

“A paid-up trey of two hundred francs will give three millions, without counting the couplets and the singles.”

“At fifteen thousand times the stake—yes, you are right; it is just two hundred you must pay up on!” cried Philippe.

Madame Descoings bit her lips; she knew she had spoken imprudently. Philippe, as he went downstairs, thought: “That old witch! where does she keep her money? It is as good as lost; I can make a better use of it. With four pools at fifty francs each, I could win two hundred thousand francs, and that’s much surer than the turning up of a trey.”

He tried to think where she might have hidden it. On festival days Agathe went to church and stayed long; she might prepare for communion. It was the day before Christmas; Madame Descoings would certainly go out to buy revelry dainties and perhaps pay up her stake. The Paris lottery was drawn on the twenty-fifth of each month, and the lists closed on the twenty-fourth at midnight. Philippe studied these points and set himself to watch. He came home at midday; the Descoings had gone out and taken the key of the apartment. That was no difficulty. Philippe pretended to have forgotten something and asked the concierge to fetch a locksmith, who came at once and opened the door. The villain’s first thought was the bed; he uncovered it, passed his hands over the mattress and felt pieces wrapped in paper at the lower end. He ripped the ticking, picked out twenty napoleons, and, without sewing up the mattress, remade the bed neatly so Madame Descoings would suspect nothing.

The gambler stole off, resolving to play three times, three hours apart, each time for only ten minutes—a regimen seasoned players adopted after past ruin. But before reaching that experience they lost fortunes. The whole science of gambling houses rests on the impassibility of the bank, drawn games where half the money goes to the bank, and the bad faith allowed by government refusing to hold or pay players’ stakes except optionally. The gambling-house, which refuses the game of a cool rich player, devours the fortune of the obstinate fool, carried away by the rapid motion of the table; the croupiers at “trente et quarante” move nearly as fast as the ball.

Philippe had acquired the sang-froid of a commanding general, enabling him to keep his eye clear and mind prompt amid tumult. He had reached that statesmanship of gambling which in Paris is the livelihood of those strong enough to look nightly into an abyss without vertigo. With four hundred francs, he resolved to make his fortune that day. He put aside two hundred francs in his boots and kept the other two hundred in his pocket. At three o’clock he went to the gambling-house at the Palais-Royal, where the bank accepted large sums. He came out half an hour later with seven thousand francs. He then paid Florentine the five hundred francs he owed her, proposed supper at the Rocher de Cancale, and returned to play. By six he had won twenty-five thousand francs and stopped after ten minutes as promised. That night, by ten o’clock, he had won seventy-five thousand francs. After the magnificent supper, drunk and confident, he returned to the table at midnight. In defiance of his rule, he played for an hour and doubled his fortune. The bankers, from whom he had extracted one hundred and fifty thousand francs by his system, watched with curiosity. “Will he go away now, or will he stay?” they signalled. “If he stays he is lost.”

Philippe thought luck had struck and stayed. Towards three in the morning the hundred and fifty thousand francs were back at the bank. The colonel, having imbibed much grog while playing, left drunk, and a waiter from the gambling-house found him and took him to one of those terrible lodging-houses. The waiter paid for the ruined gambler who was put to bed, where he remained till Christmas night. The managers of gambling-houses show some consideration for high players. Philippe awoke about seven in the evening, mouth parched, face swollen, in the grip of a nervous fever. His constitution’s strength enabled him to get home on foot, where meanwhile he had, without willing it, brought mourning, desolation, poverty, and death.

The evening before, when dinner was ready, Madame Descoings and Agathe expected Philippe. They waited until seven. Agathe always went to bed at ten; she wished to be present at midnight mass and lay down after dinner. Madame Descoings and Joseph remained in the little salon; the old woman asked the painter to add up the amount of her great stake on the famous trey, which she was to pay that evening at the Lottery office. She wished to put in for doubles and singles, to seize all chances. After feasting on the poetry of her hopes and relating dreams that seemed to demonstrate certain success, she felt only the difficulty of waiting until ten the next day when winning numbers were declared. Joseph, who knew nothing of the four hundred francs required to pay up the stakes, asked about them. The old woman smiled and led him into her former salon, now her bed-chamber.

“You shall see,” she said.

Madame Descoings hastily unmade the bed and searched for scissors to rip the mattress; she put on spectacles, looked at the ticking, saw the hole, and let fall the mattress. Hearing a sigh from the depths of her breast, as though strangled, Joseph instinctively held out his arms and placed the poor creature fainting in a chair, calling for his mother. Agathe ran in, applied ordinary remedies—eau-de-cologne, cold water, a burnt feather under the nose—and presently her aunt revived.

“They were there this morning; HE has taken them, the monster!” she said.

“Taken what?” asked Joseph.

“I had twenty louis in my mattress; my savings for two years; no one but Philippe could have taken them.”

“But when?” cried the mother, overwhelmed, “he has not been in since breakfast.”

“I wish I might be mistaken,” said the old woman. “But this morning in Joseph’s studio, when I spoke before Philippe of my stakes, I had a presentiment. I did wrong not to go down and take my little all and pay for my stakes at once. I meant to, and I don’t know what prevented me. Oh, yes—my God! I went out to buy him some cigars.”

“But,” said Joseph, “you left the door locked. Besides, it is so infamous. I can’t believe it. Philippe couldn’t have watched you, cut open the mattress, done it deliberately—no, no!”

“I felt them this morning, when I made my bed after breakfast,” repeated Madame Descoings.

Agathe, horrified, went downstairs and asked if Philippe had come in during the day. The concierge related the tale of his return and the locksmith. The mother, heart-stricken, returned a changed woman. White as her chemise, she walked as a spectre, slowly and noiselessly, moved as if mechanically. She held a candle whose light fell full upon her face and showed her eyes fixed with horror. Unconsciously her hands had dishevelled the hair about her brow; Joseph stood rooted in awe at the apparition of that remorse—the vision of a statue of terror and despair.

“My aunt,” she said, “take my silver forks and spoons. I have enough to make up the sum; I took your money for Philippe’s sake; I thought I could put it back before you missed it. Oh! I have suffered much.”

She sat down. Her dry, fixed eyes wandered.

“It was he who did it,” whispered the old woman to Joseph.

“No, no,” cried Agathe; “take my silver plate, sell it; it is useless to me; we can eat with yours.”

She went to her room, took the box that contained the plate, felt its light weight, opened it, and saw a pawnbroker’s ticket. The poor mother uttered a dreadful cry. Joseph and the Descoings ran to her, saw the empty box; her noble falsehood was of no avail. All three were silent and avoided looking at each other; then, by an almost frantic gesture, Agathe laid her finger on her lips as if to entreat secrecy no one desired to break. They returned to the salon and sat beside the fire.

“Ah! my children,” cried Madame Descoings, “I am stabbed to the heart: my trey will turn up, I am certain of it. I am not thinking of myself, but of you two. Philippe is a monster,” she continued, addressing her niece; “he does not love you after all you have done for him. If you do not protect yourself against him he will bring you to beggary. Promise me to sell out your Funds and buy a life-annuity. Joseph has a good profession and he can live. If you will do this, dear Agathe, you will never be an expense to Joseph. Monsieur Desroches has just started his son as a notary; he would take your twelve thousand francs and pay you an annuity.”

Joseph seized his mother’s candlestick, rushed to his studio, and returned with three hundred francs.

“Here, Madame Descoings!” he cried, giving her his little store; “it is no business of ours what you do with your money; we owe you what you have lost, and here it is, almost in full.”

“Take your poor little all—the fruit of those privations that have made me so unhappy! are you mad, Joseph?” cried the old woman, torn between faith in the coming trey and the sacrilege of accepting such a sacrifice.

“Oh! take it if you like,” said Agathe, moved to tears by this action of her true son.

Madame Descoings took Joseph by the head and kissed him on the forehead: “My child,” she said, “don’t tempt me. I might only lose it. The lottery, you see, is all folly.”

No more heroic words were ever uttered in the hidden dramas of domestic life. It was affection triumphant over inveterate vice. At that instant the clocks struck midnight.

“It is too late now,” said Madame Descoings.

“Oh!” cried Joseph, “here are your cabalistic numbers.”

The artist sprang at the paper and rushed down the staircase to pay the stakes. When he was no longer present, Agathe and Madame Descoings burst into tears.

“He has gone, the dear love,” cried the old gambler; “but it shall all be his; he pays his own money.”

Unhappily, Joseph did not know the way to any of the lottery offices, which then were as familiar to most people as the cigar-shops to a smoker now. He ran along, reading street names on lamps. When he asked passers-by to show a lottery office, he was told they were all closed except the one under the Palais-Royal portico which was sometimes kept open a little later. He flew there: the office was shut.

“Two minutes earlier, and you might have paid your stake,” said a vendor under the portico, where he cried: “Twelve hundred francs for forty sous,” offering tickets all paid up.

By the glimmer of a street lamp and the lights of the Café de la Rotonde, Joseph examined these tickets to see if any bore the Descoings’s numbers. He found none and returned home grieved at having done his best in vain. Agathe and her aunt went together to midnight mass at Saint-Germain-des-Prés. Joseph went to bed. The collation did not take place. Madame Descoings had lost her head; in Agathe’s heart was eternal mourning.

They rose late on Christmas morning. Ten o’clock had struck before Madame Descoings began to stir; breakfast was not ready until half-past eleven. At that hour the oblong frames with winning numbers are hung over lottery doors. If Madame Descoings had paid and held her ticket she would have gone by half-past nine to learn her fate at a building near the Ministry of Finance. On drawing days a curious crowd of old women, cooks, and old men gathered about that door.

“Well, here you are, rolling in wealth!” said old Desroches, coming in just as the Descoings took her last drop of coffee.

“What do you mean?” cried poor Agathe.

“Her trey has turned up,” he said, producing the list of numbers on a bit of paper.

Joseph read. Agathe read. The Descoings read nothing; she was struck as by a thunderbolt. At the change in her face and the cry she gave, old Desroches and Joseph carried her to bed. Agathe fetched a doctor. The poor woman was seized with apoplexy and only recovered consciousness at four in the afternoon; old Haudry then said that, despite improvement, she ought to settle her affairs and think of salvation. She uttered only two words: “Three millions!”

Old Desroches, with caution, related many instances where fortunes escaped those who forgot to pay stakes; but he understood how fatal this blow could be after twenty years’ perseverance. About five, as a deep silence reigned in the little apartment and the sick woman lay watched by Joseph and his mother, the sound of Philippe’s step and cane resounded on the staircase.

“There he is! there he is!” cried the Descoings, sitting up in bed and suddenly able to use her paralyzed tongue.

Agathe and Joseph were deeply impressed by the effect on the old woman. Their suspense ended when Philippe entered, convulsed and purple-faced, staggering, his eyes deeply sunken and haggard; he had a strong chill and his teeth chattered.

“Starvation in Prussia!” he cried, looking about him. “Nothing to eat or drink?—and my throat on fire! Well, what’s the matter? The devil is always meddling in our affairs. There’s my old Descoings in bed, looking at me with her eyes as big as saucers.”

“Be silent, monsieur!” said Agathe, rising. “At least, respect the sorrows you have caused.”

“_Monsieur_, indeed!” he cried, looking at his mother. “My dear little mother, that won’t do. Have you ceased to love your son?”

“Are you worthy of love? Have you forgotten what you did yesterday? Go and find another home; you cannot live with us any longer,—that is, after to-morrow,” she added; “for in the state you are in now it is difficult—”

“To turn me out—is that it?” he interrupted. “Ha! are you going to play the melodrama of ‘The Banished Son’? Well done! is that how you take things? You are all a pretty set! What harm have I done? I’ve cleaned out the old woman’s mattress. What the devil is the good of money kept in wool? Do you call that a crime? Didn’t she take twenty thousand francs from you? We are her creditors, and I’ve paid myself as much as I could get—that’s all.”

“My God! my God!” cried the dying woman, clasping her hands and praying.

“Be silent!” exclaimed Joseph, springing at his brother and putting his hand before his mouth.

“To the right about, march! brat of a painter!” retorted Philippe, laying his strong hand on Joseph’s head and twirling him round, flinging him on a sofa. “Don’t dare to touch the moustache of a commander of a squadron of the dragoons of the Guard!”

“She has paid me back all she owed me,” cried Agathe, rising and turning an angry face to her son; “and besides, that is my affair. You have killed her. Go away, my son,” she added, with a gesture that took all her remaining strength, “and never let me see you again. You are a monster.”

“I kill her?”

“Her trey has turned up,” cried Joseph, “and you stole the money for her stake.”

“Well, if she is dying of a lost trey, it isn’t I who have killed her,” said the drunkard.

“Go, go!” said Agathe. “You fill me with horror; you have every vice. My God! is this my son?”

A hollow rattle sounded in Madame Descoings’s throat, increasing Agathe’s anger.

“I love you still, my mother—you who are the cause of all my misfortunes,” said Philippe. “You turn me out of doors on Christmas-day. What did you do to grandpa Rouget, to your father, that he should drive you away and disinherit you? If you had not displeased him, we should all be rich now, and I should not be reduced to misery. What did you do to your father—you who are a good woman? You see by yourself, I may be a good fellow and yet be turned out of house and home—I, the glory of the family—”

“The disgrace of it!” cried the Descoings.

“You shall leave this room, or you shall kill me!” cried Joseph, springing on his brother with the fury of a lion.

“My God! my God!” cried Agathe, trying to separate the brothers.

At that moment Bixiou and Doctor Haudry entered. Joseph had just knocked his brother over and stretched him on the ground.

“He is a regular wild beast,” he cried. “Don’t speak another word, or I’ll—”

“I’ll pay you for this!” roared Philippe.

“A family explanation,” remarked Bixiou.

“Lift him up,” said the doctor, looking at him. “He is as ill as Madame Descoings; undress him and put him to bed; get off his boots.”

“That’s easy to say,” cried Bixiou, “but they must be cut off; his legs are swollen.”

Agathe took scissors. When she had cut down the boots, which were worn outside the clinging trousers, ten pieces of gold rolled on the floor.

“There it is—her money,” murmured Philippe. “Cursed fool that I was, I forgot it. I too have missed a fortune.”

He was seized with a horrible delirium of fever and began to rave. Joseph, assisted by old Desroches and Bixiou, carried him to his room. Doctor Haudry was obliged to write to the Hopital de la Charité and borrow a strait-waistcoat; the delirium ran so high he feared Philippe might kill himself. At nine o’clock calm was restored. The Abbé Loraux and Desroches endeavored to comfort Agathe, who never ceased to weep at her aunt’s bedside. She listened in silence and obstinately shook her head; Joseph and the Descoings alone knew the depth of her inward wound.

“He will learn to do better, mother,” said Joseph, when Desroches and Bixiou had left.

“Oh!” cried the widow, “Philippe is right—my father cursed me: I have no right to—Here, here is your money,” she said to Madame Descoings, adding Joseph’s three hundred francs to the two hundred found on Philippe. “Go and see if your brother does not need something,” she said to Joseph.

“Will you keep a promise made to a dying woman?” asked Madame Descoings, who felt her mind failing.

“Yes, aunt.”

“Then swear to me to give your property to young Desroches for a life annuity. My income ceases at my death; and from what you have just said, I know you will let that wretch wring the last farthing out of you.”

“I swear it, aunt.”

The old woman died on the thirty-first of December, five days after the terrible blow old Desroches had so innocently given her. The five hundred francs—the only money in the household—were barely enough for her funeral. She left a small amount of silver and some furniture, the value of which Madame Bixiou paid over to her grandson Bixiou. Reduced to an eight hundred franc annuity paid by young Desroches, who had bought a business without clients and himself took the capital of twelve thousand francs, Agathe gave up her third-floor apartment and sold all superfluous furniture. When, after a month, Philippe seemed convalescent, his mother coldly explained that the costs of his illness had taken all her ready money, that she would be obliged to work for her living, and urged him with the utmost kindness to re-enter the army and support himself.

“You might have spared me that sermon,” said Philippe, looking at his mother with indifference. “I have seen all along that neither you nor my brother love me. I am alone in the world; I like it best!”

“Make yourself worthy of our affection,” answered the poor mother, struck to the heart, “and we will give it back to you—”

“Nonsense!” he cried, interrupting her.

He took his old hat, rubbed white at the edges, stuck it over one ear, and went downstairs, whistling.

“Philippe! where are you going without any money?” cried his mother, who could not repress her tears. “Here, take this—”

She held out a hundred francs in gold, wrapped in paper. Philippe came up the stairs and took the money.

“Well; won’t you kiss me?” she said, bursting into tears.

He pressed his mother in his arms, but without the warmth that would have given value to the embrace.

“Where shall you go?” asked Agathe.

“To Florentine, Giroudeau’s mistress. Ah! they are real friends!” he answered brutally.

He went away. Agathe turned back with trembling limbs, failing eyes, and aching heart. She fell on her knees, prayed God to take her unnatural child into His keeping, and abdicated her woeful motherhood.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
By February 1822 Madame Bridau had taken the attic room over the kitchen of her old apartment. The painter’s studio and bedroom faced it across the stair. Seeing his mother so reduced, Joseph resolved to make her comfortable. After his brother left he refurnished the garret with an artist’s touch: a rug, a simple monastic bed, modest glazed cotton on the walls, a double door with a portiere, and a blind to soften the light. Though Agathe’s life was stripped to the plainest circumstances in Paris, Joseph made her as decent as he could.

To spare her frugal housekeeping, Joseph took Agathe daily to dine at a table-d’hôte on the rue de Beaune frequented by well-bred women, deputies, and the titled; dinners there cost ninety francs a month. Agathe, paying only for breakfast, resumed old habits from her married life. Alarmed when she learned the dinner cost nearly a hundred francs a month, and not imagining Joseph earned money by painting naked women, she obtained through her confessor, the Abbé Loraux, a place worth seven hundred francs a year in a lottery office belonging to the Comtesse de Bauvan. Lottery offices often sufficed to support a manager’s family; the Comtesse promised the manager’s place when it vacated; meanwhile Agathe took an assistant’s post at six hundred francs. Her hours were long: at the office by ten, scarcely time for dinner, then back by seven to midnight. For two years Joseph fetched her every night, often taking her to dinner; friends saw him leave the opera or a brilliant salon to be punctual at midnight at the rue Vivienne office.

Agathe soon adopted the monotonous regularity that supports those who have suffered. Mornings she tidied her room—no cats or birds now—prepared breakfast and carried it into the studio to eat with Joseph, arranged his bedroom, put out her own fire, and sat sewing by the iron stove, retreating when a comrade or model entered. She understood nothing of art and made no pretense; amazed at the fuss about color, composition, and drawing, she later examined canvases and found nothing to justify the painters’ disputes. She made Joseph’s shirts, mended stockings, cleaned his palette, supplied rags, and kept the studio in order. Joseph, noticing her care over trivial things, lavished attentions on her. Their bond was tenderness rather than shared art.

One morning, while Joseph sketched a large picture, Agathe, petting him, spoke of Philippe as if casually: Had he suffered? Had poverty changed him? Joseph denied it, saying Philippe kept carnival in New York as he did in Paris, and that if he were suffering he’d gladly give him money but did not want to see him—“he killed our poor Descoings.” Agathe hoped Joseph would paint Philippe’s portrait to have one of him; Joseph consented for his mother’s sake. She pressed him tenderly, their mutual attachment plain: “He is your passion,” Joseph said.

That afternoon Joseph went to the rue du Sentier and found Philippe, who had taken Giroudeau’s place at a weekly journal that Finot’s nephew ran; the old dragoon had been promoted to cashier. Finot, though still owner of another paper, favored his nephew Lousteau (connected to the Bridaus’ past), and to please him had cut Philippe’s salary. Philippe earned six hundred francs and five hundred from his cross—sufficient for a warm office day and free theatre passes at night—needing little but food and a place to sleep. Coloquinte, the office boy, watched Philippe, who oddly behaved well. When Joseph arrived, Philippe proposed dinner, the opera, and introductions; Joseph declined, saying he would take his mother to dine at the table d’hôte. Philippe agreed to sit for his portrait in uniform; though he worked in a cramped “hen-coop” from nine to five, he said two Sundays would suffice to sit.

When Joseph told Agathe of Philippe’s position on their way to the rue de Beaune, she trembled with joy and breathed as if relieved of a weight. She showed gratitude by decorating the studio with flowers and buying two plant stands. On the first Sunday Philippe sat, the ex-dragoon sending his uniform the night before; Agathe, hiding behind a screen, prepared a charming breakfast and cried silently. Philippe posed for two hours before and two after breakfast. At three he dressed ordinary and proposed dining at the Palais-Royal, jingling gold. Joseph, uneasy, feared seeing gold about Philippe; Agathe insisted on going. Philippe gave a splendid dinner at the Rocher de Cancale costing a hundred francs. Drunk and boasting a run of luck, he took them to the theatre later, but Agathe feigned illness and refused; back at rue Mazarin she sank into gloom.

On the next sitting Sunday Agathe was visibly present. She served breakfast and questioned Philippe, learning he lodged with journalists, actresses, and booksellers as a cashier and lived on kirsch before posing, boasting of future greatness but silent about his means. Joseph, saying the Salon was near and he needed money for frames and to finish a copy of a Rubens ordered by dealer Elie Magus (the original on loan from a Swiss banker for ten days), postponed the sitting till the following Sunday. That evening Joseph prepared a stratagem: when Pierre Grassou, a fellow painter, came to see the finished copy, Joseph varnished it and placed it where the original had been, setting the original on his easel. Grassou was taken in and delighted. Magus did not come as agreed. Joseph left the key with the concierge and dined at his table d’hôte with Grassou.

An hour later Philippe arrived, told the concierge he was to sit that evening, and the concierge gave him the key. Philippe went upstairs, took the copy thinking it the original, returned the key claiming he’d forgotten something, and hurried off to sell the Rubens for three thousand francs. He had sent Magus a note asking him not to call till the next day. When Joseph returned that night with his mother, the concierge told him Philippe’s trick. Joseph feared ruin and ran to the studio. Relieved to find the original still there, he cursed Philippe as a “vile scoundrel.” Agathe, bewildered by his words, wept when told. Joseph insisted they warn the concierge and keep the keys themselves; he vowed to paint his brother’s blackguard face from memory. Agathe, heart-broken, asked him to leave the portrait as it was—she could not bear to look at it.

Philippe knew that the money from the Rubens would be used to prevent his brother’s ruin, yet still took the copy. After this crime Agathe never mentioned him; her face became cold, concentrated, bitter. One thought possessed her: “Some day we shall hear of a Bridau in the police courts.”

Two months later an old officer—Giroudeau—calling himself Philippe’s friend, came to their studio on urgent business. His appearance—fishy gray eyes, piebald mustache, greasy hair, a butter-colored skull, an overlarge stomach in a shabby iron-gray coat with a faded Legion of Honor ribbon, gold rings in his ears—gave him a debauched, libidinous look. Joseph thought him a “noceur,” a loose companion. Giroudeau told them Philippe could no longer work at the office; he was lodged with Mademoiselle Florentine in a miserable attic and was dying; unless they paid for doctor and medicines he would be taken to the Capuchins’ hospital. He had pledged his pension of the cross and had literally nothing. Giroudeau and Florentine would pay half the cost of keeping Philippe where he was if Agathe paid the rest—three hundred francs for a nurse and two months’ care. Agathe thanked him but said the son was banished from her heart and she had no money—she was an assistant in a lottery office to avoid burdening Joseph.

Giroudeau pressed Joseph too; Joseph blurted that Giroudeau had come to swindle them on false pretences. He refused to give anything; Giroudeau left mortified and disappointed. Three months later, in July, as Agathe crossed the Pont Neuf, she saw a man along the Quai de l’Ecole who looked like Philippe in the last stages of poverty: ragged neck-cloth, scurfy hat, patched boots, threadbare coat, dirty collar, black hands, a waistcoat in place of a shirt. His nearly bald head, sallow skin, sunken face showed he had just left the Hopital du Midi. His blue overcoat, whitened at the seams, still bore the ribbon of his cross; passersby noticed the rosette with curiosity and commiseration.

Agathe trembled. Forgetting past crimes—stealing, gambling, debauchery—she saw only a man recovering from illness and likely to die of starvation. Impulsively she crossed the quay, thrust her purse into Philippe’s hand, and fled as if guilty. For two days she ate nothing, haunted by the image of her son dying in Paris and thinking Giroudeau had not deceived them; at forty-seven she grew to look seventy. Her eyes were dimmed with tears and prayers. But worse was to come: a conspiracy among officers was discovered, and Moniteur articles hawked lists of arrests. Agathe, hearing Philippe’s name in her lottery office, fainted; the manager, understanding, gave her two weeks’ leave.

Joseph went to Desroches to consult about Philippe. While the artist confided the matter to the younger Desroches—now reputed a clever lawyer—Giroudeau called on the widow Desroches and persuaded Agathe that twelve thousand francs would secure Philippe’s release by buying the silence of two witnesses. Agathe, desperate, promised to get the money without knowing how. She wrote to her godmother, Madame Hochon, begging her to ask Jean-Jacques Rouget to send the sum or to lend it and she would repay in two years. Madame Hochon’s reply, mailed by return courier, refused. She explained that her brother Hochon, with an income of at least forty thousand francs and savings estimated at over six hundred thousand, would give nothing to nephews he had never seen. She could not act without her husband’s signature and said Rouget would refuse; she could not dispose of money while her husband lived. She hinted that Agathe’s presence in Issoudun might neutralize the concubine’s influence over Jean-Jacques Rouget and suggested Agathe come and stay with her; she promised to try to make her husband consent and hinted at using a will to coerce him if necessary. She urged Agathe to employ a good lawyer and to come to Issoudun at once.

The letter impressed Agathe, who showed it to Joseph after telling him of Giroudeau’s demand. Joseph advised telling everything to Desroches. The next morning at six they went to find Desroches in the rue de Bussy. The lawyer, cold, stern, with a cracked voice, rough skin, implacable eyes and a fox-like visage, bounded like a tiger when he heard of Giroudeau’s proposal and scornfully warned Agathe not to be duped. He urged her not to give a farthing; he’d answer for Philippe and would rather see him convicted—brought before the Court of Peers—if that would save his future. He advised securing the property at Issoudun and getting evidence of undue influence if Jean-Jacques had made a will favoring the woman; he offered to go to Issoudun himself in the holidays if needed. His briskness alarmed Joseph; Desroches winked and detained Joseph a moment to tell him Philippe was a “great scoundrel” and might have been the cause of the conspiracy’s discovery—either a fool or a traitor—and would be under police surveillance.

“Go to Issoudun with your mother,” Desroches urged. “Try to save the property.” Joseph, having sold two pictures to fund the trip, told his mother they would start for Berry. After writing to Madame Hochon, Agathe and Joseph left that evening for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to his fate. On the road, Agathe, reflecting on the Luxembourg prison to which Philippe had been transferred, said, “If it were not for the Allies he would never be there!” Joseph, gallant, embraced his mother and praised her maternal devotion: “You are a mother just as Raphael was a painter. And you will always be a fool of a mother!”

Agathe’s mind, distracted by the journey, turned to the task. She re-read Madame Hochon’s letter, struck by the words “concubine” and “slut” used by a septuagenarian pious as she and by “imbecile” applied to Rouget. She wondered how her presence could save the inheritance. Joseph, ignorant of the Code, admitted he was at a loss. He recalled Desroches had said to gather evidence of undue influence if Jean-Jacques had made a will favoring the woman. “Don’t trouble your heads uselessly,” Agathe said; “when we get to Issoudun my godmother will tell us what to do.” Their naivety in the task Desroches set them was plain as they entered the Sologne.

Returning to Issoudun after thirty years’ absence, Agathe was about to find changes in its manners that require a portrait of the town. Without it the reader would scarcely understand Madame Hochon’s heroism in helping her goddaughter, or the strange position of Jean-Jacques Rouget. That a brother should for thirty years give no sign of life to a sister was extraordinary and suggested peculiar circumstances; any other sister and nephew would long since have inquired. There is, moreover, a connection between Issoudun’s condition and the Bridau family’s interests, which will appear as the story goes on.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Issoudun, without disparaging Paris, ranks among France’s oldest cities. Tradition makes Probus the Noah of the Gauls, and Caesar praises Champ-Fort wine (“de Campo Forti”), still among Issoudun’s best. Rigord notes its once-great population and commerce, but Armand Peremet’s excavations revealed older layers: beneath the celebrated tower lay a fifth-century basilica, likely unique in France, whose stones came from a Roman temple once on the same site, preserving signs of an earlier civilization.

Peremet argues that town names ending in “-dun” point to autochthonous, Druidical, or Celtic origins. Beneath the Gaulish dun likely stood a Roman temple to Isis, hence Chaumon’s name Issous-Dun—“Is” for Isis. Richard Coeur-de-lion probably built the famous tower—where he coined money—over the fifth-century basilica, enclosing the church in feudal fortifications for protection. Issoudun once hosted the brief power of routiers and cottereaux, adventurers Henry II sent against his son Richard during the Poitou rebellion.

The unwritten history of Aquitaine may remain obscure with the Benedictines gone. Another sign of Issoudun’s ancient importance is the Tournemine’s conversion into a canal, a stream raised above the Theols that surrounds the town—undoubtedly Roman engineering. A northern suburb traversed by a rue de Rome is inhabited by people who call themselves Romans, mostly vine-growers with distinct features and customs, possibly descendants of those who exterminated the cottereaux and routiers on the plain of Charost in the twelfth century.

After the 1830 insurrection, national agitation overshadowed Issoudun’s vine-growers’ uprising; a terrible affair there went unpublished for good reasons. The bourgeois refused military entry, invoking a medieval claim to govern their city. The government yielded to this sturdy people backed by seven or eight thousand vine-growers, who burned archives and offices of indirect taxation and dragged a customs officer through the streets, shouting at every lantern, “Let us hang him here!” The national guard saved him by taking him to prison under pretense of drafting an indictment. The general entered only after compromise; when he appeared at the hotel-de-ville a man from the faubourg de Rome swung a volant—a huge pruning-hook—round his neck, crying, “No more clerks, or there’s an end to compromise!” A leader promised suppression of excisemen and intervened to save the general.

In the fourteenth century Issoudun still had sixteen or seventeen thousand inhabitants, vestiges of a larger medieval population. Charles VII owned a mansion known into the eighteenth century as the Maison du Roi. Once a center of the woollen trade, the town supplied much of Europe with wool—for blankets, hats, and Chevreautin gloves. Under Louis XIV., Issoudun, birthplace of Baron and Bourdaloue, was famed for elegance and correct speech; the curate Poupard praised its subtlety and wit. Today wit and splendor have faded. Issoudun now has barely twelve thousand inhabitants, including vine-dressers of four large suburbs—Saint-Paterne, Vilatte, Rome, and Alouette—that are almost small towns. The bourgeoisie are spread through the streets. Issoudun still holds Berry’s fleeces market, a commerce threatened by the limited introduction of improved stock-breeding.

The vineyards produce a wine drunk across two departments and could rival Burgundy or Gascony if cultivated differently. Yet conservative habits prevail: vine-growers leave grape refuse in the juice during fermentation, making the wine detestable though it could be a source of wealth. They claim the refuse’s bitterness lessens with age, so a vintage will keep a century—an excuse echoed by Guillaume le Breton in his “Phillippide.”

Issoudun’s decline stems from sluggish torpor. When a highroad between Paris and Toulouse was planned, the natural line from Vierzon to Chateauroux would have passed through Issoudun, shorter than the present route via Vatan. But leading citizens and the city council demanded it go by Vatan, arguing a road through Issoudun would raise provisions and force them to pay thirty sous for a chicken—short-sighted parochialism similar to resistance that forced a Sardinian highway to make an eight-mile detour.

Falling wine prices please the bourgeoisie seeking cheap provisions but threaten vine-growers with ruin as costs and taxes rise—just as the woollen trade suffers from lack of improvement in sheep breeding. Country folk fear change; a peasant may prefer poverty to being pointed at for adopting a new habit. Many bourgeois express this sentiment with inward pride.

In town, social stagnation has set in. Families live for themselves; society lacks the class distinctions that give manners character. Nobility has vanished—cottereaux, routiers, jacquerie, religious wars, and the Revolution abolished them, a triumph Issoudun applauds. The town repeatedly refused a garrison, claiming cheap provisions, thus losing intercourse with the age and the profits troops bring; before 1756 it had been a delightful garrison town. A judicial feud between a lieutenant-general and the Marquis de Chapt—whose son, a dragoon, was put to death—deprived the town of a garrison. The forty-fourth demi-brigade’s sojourn during the civil war did not reconcile inhabitants to soldiers.

Bourges, likewise declining, suffers the same malady. Vitality leaves such communities. The government is to blame: administration should find and heal wounds by sending energetic men with power to change things, but instead it approves fatal tranquillity. Who would accept being buried in such arrondissements, where useful work lacks glory? Any ambitious outsider soon sinks into local inertia; Issoudun would have benumbed Napoleon.

In 1822 the arrondissement’s administrators were all locals. Administration became nullity or farce—except in rare cases. The procureur du roi, Monsieur Mouilleron, was cousin to much of the community; his substitute from a town family. The court judge, before his seat, became infamous for a provincial saying: ending a summing-up, he told the accused, “My poor Pierre! the thing is as plain as day; your head will be cut off. Let this be a lesson to you.” The commissary of police, in office since the Restoration, had relations throughout the arrondissement. Religion’s influence was weak; even the curate was held in little esteem.

This radical, ignorant bourgeoisie—fond of annoying others—spread tales, some comic, about Jean-Jacques Rouget and his servant-woman. Still, children went to Sunday-school, schools were kept up, mass said, taxes paid (the sole thing Paris extracts from the provinces), and the mayor passed resolutions. But these acts were routine; the laxity of local government matched the moral and intellectual state of the governed. Subsequent events show effects of this condition, common in many provinces, especially the South. The state to which bourgeois ascendency has reduced Issoudun may spread across France, even to Paris, if the bourgeois continue to rule domestic and foreign policy.

Topography: Issoudun stretches north-south along a hillside curving toward the Chateauroux road. At the hill’s foot a canal, now called the “Riviere forcee,” fed from the Theols, was built when the town flourished—to supply manufactories or flood rampart moats. The “Riviere forcee” is an artificial arm of the Tournemine, joining other streams beyond the Rome suburb. These threads of water irrigate wide meadows, enclosed by yellowish or white terraces dotted with dark specks—the vineyards’ appearance for many months. Vine-growers cut plants annually to ugly stumps, unsupported, sheltered by barrels. A traveller from Vierzon, Vatan, or Chateauroux, weary of monotony, is pleasantly surprised by Issoudun’s meadows—an oasis supplying vegetables across thirty miles. Below the Rome suburb lies a vast tract of kitchen-gardens, upper and lower Baltan. A long poplar avenue leads from town across the meadows to the ancient convent of Frapesle, whose English gardens, grandly named Tivoli, are a Sunday rendezvous for lovers.

Traces of ancient grandeur appear in town divisions. The chateau, once almost a town with walls and moats, remains a distinct quarter entered by ancient gateways via three bridges across two river arms, resembling an old city. Ramparts show formidable foundations on which houses have sprung. Above the chateau stands the famous tower, once the citadel. Conquering the city, which lay around these two fortified points, still left the tower and castle to be won.

The Saint-Paterne suburb, a palette-shaped extension beyond the tower encroaching on meadows, was likely part of the ancient city. In 1822 this seemed certain because of the then-existence of the charming church of Saint-Paterne, recently pulled down by the heir of the buyer from the nation. This Romanesque church, one of France’s finest, perished without a single drawing of its preserved portal. The lone voice raised to save it found no echo in town or department. Though the castle appears like an old town with narrow streets and mansions, the main city, repeatedly captured and burned—especially during the Fronde—has a modern air. Spacious streets and well-built houses contrast with the citadel’s aspect, earning Issoudun the epithet “pretty” in some geographies.

In such a town, lacking activity, taste for art, or learned occupations, many young men after the Restoration in 1816 had no career until marriage or inheritance. Bored at home and lacking distraction, they “shed their cuticle” by sowing wild oats at the town’s expense. Avoiding daytime escapades to prevent recognition and arraignment before police courts, they chose the night. Thus, amid traces of lost civilizations, a last flare of drollery burst forth.

These young men amused themselves as courts and soldiers once did, banding together for mutual help and tricks. They became an organized conspiracy—the “Knights of Idleness.” By day they played saints, sleeping late after nights of mischief. The Knights began with commonplace pranks—unhooking and changing signs, ringing bells, flinging casks into neighbors’ cellars, rousing inhabitants with crashes suggesting explosions. In Issoudun, cellars are entered by openings near the door, covered by a wooden scuttle with iron hinges and padlock.

In 1816 such pranks persisted. But in 1817 the Order acquired a Grand Master and, until 1823, Maxence Gilet, called Max, led mischief that spread something like terror in Issoudun and kept artisans and bourgeois perpetually uneasy.

Maxence Gilet’s background, energy, and bearing suited him for the role. Town rumor supposed him the natural son of the sub-delegate Lousteau—the brother of Madame Hochon whose gallantries were remembered—and some said he might be Doctor Rouget’s son; in truth his father was a dragoon officer garrisoned at Bourges. Because Rouget and Lousteau had been close friends, both claimed paternity, leaving the matter undecided. Max’s mother, wife of a poor sabot-maker in the Rome suburb, was strikingly beautiful—a Trasteverine beauty she transmitted to her son. Madame Gilet, pregnant with Max in 1788, enjoyed the town’s attributions of his paternity, perhaps to set reputed fathers against each other; she colluded with old Gilet to keep protectors ignorant. Madame Hochon paid sixty francs a year for the lad’s schooling—a liberality attributed to her brother at Sancerre.

Doctor Rouget, unlucky in sons, seeing Max’s beauty, paid the youngster’s seminary board until 1805. Lousteau died in 1800, and the doctor continued payments, leaving paternity unresolved. Max, the butt of jests, was soon forgotten when in 1806, a year after Rouget’s death, he began a venturesome life. Gifted with strength and agility, he plotted with Monsieur Hochon’s grandsons to vex grocers, gathered fruit before owners could pick it, scaled walls, excelled at physical exercises, and loved hunting. He spent the doctor’s money on powder and shot for a pistol old Gilet had given him. In autumn 1806, at seventeen, Max committed an involuntary homicide: startling a pregnant woman in dusk while stealing fruit, her death was blamed on him. Threatened with the guillotine by Gilet, Max fled to Bourges, enlisted with a regiment bound for Egypt, and nothing came of the woman’s death.

In three campaigns Max distinguished himself and rose to captain; his lack of formal education did not impede him. In Portugal, 1809, he was left for dead in an English battery. Taken prisoner, he was sent to the Spanish hulks at Cabrera, the most horrible prison station. Friends petitioned for the Legion of Honor and major’s rank, but the Emperor, campaigning in Austria and displeased with Portuguese events, withheld favors from those taken prisoner. Max was at Cabrera from 1810 to 1814. There he was demoralized: the hulks were like galleys, full of crime and infamy. To maintain will and protect against corruption, he killed in duels seven bullies among his fellow-prisoners, ridding the island of tyrants. He then reigned in his hulk by skill with weapons, strength, and cunning.

Though deprived of good example, he returned innocent when peace came in April 1814. He found his parents dead in the almshouse and destitute. At Napoleon’s landing at Cannes he went to Paris to ask for rank and decoration. A marshal then minister of war, recalling Max’s conduct in Portugal, put him in the Guard as captain—giving him the grade of major in infantry—but could not secure the cross. “The Emperor says that you will know how to win it at the first chance,” said the marshal. Indeed, Napoleon put Max on the decoration list the evening after Fleurus, where he distinguished himself.

After Waterloo Max retreated to the Loire. At disbandment Marshal Feltre refused to recognize his grade or cross. Enraged, Max declared he would not serve without rank or decoration. The war-office deemed his demands presumptuous; he resigned, losing half-pay. Issoudun was roused at seeing the brave young man left with only twenty napoleons; the mayor gave him a six-hundred-franc post, which Max held a few months before giving it up. Captain Carpentier, a faithful Napoleonic officer, replaced him.

By now Max was grand master of the Knights of Idleness and lived in ways that lost him the bourgeoisie’s goodwill, though none openly told him, for he was feared, even by officers who, like him, had refused to serve the Bourbons and returned to Berry. Issoudun, small and unimportant, contained more Bonapartists than any town in France; these men became, as is known, nearly all Liberals.

In Issoudun and vicinity a dozen officers shared Max’s position. They admired and followed him, except Captain Carpentier and Major Mignonnet. Carpentier, a cavalry officer risen from the ranks, had married into the town’s best family; Mignonnet, from the École Polytechnique, served in a corps that held itself superior. In the Imperial armies two types existed: a majority who despised civilians, and others—particularly artillerymen—more republican and not adopting the military-civil dichotomy. Thus Major Potel and Captain Renard befriended Max, while Mignonnet and Carpentier sided with the bourgeoisie, judging his conduct dishonorable.

Major Mignonnet, lean and dignified, busied himself with steam-engine problems and lived modestly with Monsieur and Madame Carpentier. His gentle manners and scientific pursuits won town respect; he and Carpentier were often said to be “quite another thing” from Potel, Renard, Max, and the cafe Militaire habitués, who retained soldierly manners and the Empire’s defective morals.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun, Max was excluded from society. He showed self-respect by not appearing at the club or complaining, though he was the town’s handsomest, best-dressed man, spent much money, and kept a horse—a rarity at Issoudun as Byron’s horse in Venice. We shall see how Max, poor and apparently without means, became the town’s dandy. His disgrace, earning bourgeois and religious contempt, connected with interests that brought Agathe and Joseph to Issoudun.

Judging by his audacious bearing and face, Max cared little for public opinion; he expected revenge and to lord it over those who condemned him. Although the bourgeoisie thought ill of him, the common people admired his courage, daring, and decision—his degradations were mostly unknown, even to the bourgeoisie. Max played a role like the blacksmith in the Fair Maid of Perth: champion of Bonapartism and Opposition, counted on in great occasions.

An incident illustrates his heroism. In 1819 a battalion commanded by royalist officers, fresh from the Maison Rouge, passed through Issoudun to Bourges. They lingered at the cafe Militaire—rendezvous of Bonapartists—where they asked for royalist papers. The fat landlady refused, offering the “Commerce” (the “Constitutionnel”). A captain tore it up, spat on it, and cried, “Bring dominos!” Word spread: “Let us tell Max!” Max entered the cafe with Major Potel and Captain Renard, followed by thirty young men. After a little comedy about the newspaper, Max, enraged, slapped the nearest officer and fought. They fought in the allée de Frapesle, three against three; Max killed his man. Major Potel wounded another who died in hospital; the third escaped. The battalion left that night. The affair spread through Berry and firmly established Max as a hero.

The Knights of Idleness, all young—the eldest not more than twenty-five—admired Max. Some envied his position. Under such a leader they accomplished great mischief. After May 1817, rarely a week passed without uproar. Max imposed conditions; statutes were drawn. These youths trained like pupils of Amoros—bold, agile, clever and strong as criminals. They practised roof-climbing, scaling houses, noiseless walking, mixing mortar, walling up doors, and collected ropes, ladders, tools, and disguises. They perfected invention and execution of pranks, achieving a Panurge-like genius for evil that provoked laughter while covering victims with ridicule and silencing complaints. Being sons of good families, they had household information facilitating outrages.

Sometimes they lay in ambush along the Grand’rue or the Basse rue, towns’ arteries. Crouched in wall angles at the hour households slept, they called along the town in tones of terror: “What’s the matter?” “What is it?” repeated cries woke citizens who hurried out in shirts and caps with lights, asking questions and showing queer faces.

A poor old bookbinder who believed in hobgoblins worked in a small basement shop. The Knights, disguised as devils, invaded at night, put him in his cutting-press, and left him shrieking as if among hell's souls. He roused the neighbors with tales of Lucifer and was nearly driven insane.

In a severe winter the Knights took down the collector of taxes’ chimney and rebuilt it overnight so noiselessly that they left no trace, but arranged the flue to send all smoke into the house. The collector suffered two months before discovering the cause and had to rebuild a new chimney.

Another time they put three trusses of hay dusted with brimstone and oiled paper down the chimney of a pious old woman friend of Madame Hochon. In the morning, lighting her fire, she thought she had started a volcano. Fire-engines came; the population rushed to help; Knights among the firemen deluged her house, frightening her as much with water as with the “fire.” She grew ill with fear.

To keep someone on guard and in terror, they wrote anonymous letters warning of an impending robbery, then stole softly around his house or under his windows, whistling as if to call each other.

A famous performance was sending letters to all heirs of a miserly old woman likely to leave large property, announcing her death and requesting they be present when the seals were affixed. Eighty persons arrived from neighboring towns in deep mourning—widows, sons, children, some in carrioles or carts. Imagine the scene between servants and arrivals and the notaries’ consultations; it created a sort of riot.

At last the sub-prefect waked to the intolerable state, made worse by the difficulty of discovering perpetrators. Suspicion fell on several youths; but the National Guard was a mere name, there was no garrison, and the lieutenant of police had only eight gendarmes—no patrols—so proof was impossible. The sub-prefect was made an object of mockery. He breakfasted on two fresh eggs, kept chickens, boiled eggs himself with watch in hand, boasting his egg-boiling skill. For two years he had boiled eggs successfully, earning many witticisms. Then, for a month, each night his eggs were taken and replaced by hard-boiled ones, ruining his reputation as the “sous-préfet à l’oeuf.” He lost his egg-boiling laurels and never suspected the Knights, who had planned instead. Max greased the sub-prefect’s stoves nightly with fetid oil, making the house unbearable. His wife, going to mass one morning, found her shawl glued inside by a tenacious substance and had to go without it. The sub-prefect finally requested transfer. His cowardly submissiveness helped establish the Knights’ weird and comic authority.

Beyond the rue des Minimes and the place Misere lay a quarter enclosed between an arm of the “Riviere forcee” and the ramparts, from the place d’Armes to the pottery market. This square was filled with poor houses crowded together, divided by streets so narrow two abreast could not pass. This quarter, a sort of cour des Miracles, housed poor people and lowly trades—hovels and “blind houses.” It has been, from earliest ages, an accursed quarter, haunt of evil-doers; one thoroughfare is called “the street of the Executioner.” For five centuries it’s been customary for the executioner to have a red door. The executioner of Chateauroux’s assistant is said to live there, seen rarely; vine-dressers alone keep intercourse with him, for he inherits the craft of curing wounds and fractures. In Issoudun’s heyday women wandered this quarter. It was home to second-hand sellers and old-clothes dealers whose wares infected the air; in short, the rendezvous of that apocryphal population found in such city parts, where two or three Jews sometimes gain ascendancy.

At a corner in the livelier half of the quarter existed, from 1815 to 1823 and perhaps later, a public-house kept by a woman called Mère Cognette. The house was well built of white stone with ashlar and cement infill, one storey with an attic; over the door hung a huge pine branch like a Florentine bronze. A blue poster showed, above the words “Good Beer of Mars,” a soldier pouring foam in an arched jet toward a very décolletée woman—an image to stir the imagination.

The ground floor was an immense hall serving as kitchen and dining-room, from whose beams provisions hung on huge nails. A winding staircase led to the upper storey; at the foot a door opened into a low, long room lit from a narrow, sunken provincial court like a chimney flue. Hidden by a shed and walls, this low room was where the Bad Boys held their plenary court. Ostensibly Pere Cognet boarded country folk on market-days; secretly he was landlord to the Knights. Pere Cognet, formerly a groom in a rich household, had married La Cognette, a cook in a good family. In the Rome suburb the town follows the Latin custom of placing a feminine termination on the husband’s name and giving it to the wife.

By uniting savings Pere Cognet and his spouse bought their house. La Cognette, about forty, tall and plump, with a Roxelane nose, swarthy skin, jet-black hair, lively brown eyes and a mirthful intelligence, was chosen by Max as the Order’s Leonarde for her character and cookery. Pere Cognet, about fifty-six, thick-set and under his wife’s rule—“he only sees things with a good eye,” she joked, being blind of the other—kept absolute silence about nightly gatherings. From 1816 to 1823 neither revealed what went on. Their devotion was absolute, but self-interest secured their affection and silence. Whenever the Knights knocked a certain way Pere Cognet would get up, light the fire and candles, open the door, and fetch a particular wine laid in for the Order; La Cognette prepared an excellent supper, eaten before or after expeditions planned the previous evening or day.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
While Joseph and Madame Bridau were traveling from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness pulled off one of their best pranks. An old Spaniard, a former prisoner who traded grain near Issoudun, left his empty cart at the foot of the town’s tower. Maxence, who had come to the rendezvous for the Knights, proposed they first haul the cart up onto the embankment around the tower. The tower itself, erected by Richard Cœur-de-Lion on the ruins of a basilica that covered a Roman temple and a Celtic dun, stands on a high conical mound approached only by steps; its pedestal toward the town was some eighty feet high. In an hour the Knights had taken the cart apart, hauled the pieces up, and reassembled it on top of the embankment—an effort likened to soldiers carrying artillery over a mountain pass. Hungry and thirsty, they then returned to Mère Cognette’s to anticipate Fario’s dismay when he came to fetch his barrow in the morning.

The Knights did not prank nightly; three clever men could not sustain three hundred and sixty-five tricks a year. Their gatherings varied with weather, tempers, and duties—sometimes they hunted, helped with vintages, or skated in winter. Among the twenty youths in the circle, some were closer to Maxence and looked to him as their leader. François Hochon and Baruch Borniche, grandsons of Madame Hochon, were his devoted followers. Having accepted local gossip about Lousteau’s left-handed parentage, they regarded Max as kin. Max lent them money, took them hunting, and exercised greater influence over them than their grandfather did. Both youths were orphans under Monsieur Hochon’s guardianship for reasons to be explained later.

On this night François and Baruch sat one on each side of Max at a table lit by four tallow candles; eleven Knights were present and a dozen to fifteen bottles had been emptied. Baruch, whose name showed the persistence of Calvinism in Issoudun, whispered that Madame Hochon had received a letter from her goddaughter, Madame Bridau, saying she and her son were coming and that she was preparing two rooms. François feared that if Madame Bridau, sister of Rouget, arrived with her son, she might secure Rouget’s property when he died, jeopardizing Max’s hopes.

Max reacted to this suggestion with a mixture of indignation and restraint. He had never allowed his attachment to Mademoiselle Flore Brazier—the servant-mistress of Jean-Jacques Rouget, called by the town the “Rabouilleuse”—to be turned into town gossip. His friends knew better than to jest about Flore, for Max’s temper made the subject dangerous. Still, pressed by their gossip and fear for his future, they speculated that Flore intended to secure Rouget’s property for herself, that Max planned to marry her when the old man died, and that the Bridaus’ arrival threatened those plans.

Max denied weakness and scorned the contemptuous nickname his friends used for Flore, reminding them of their bond and forbidding mockery. He then addressed the Knights: he would not poison or kill anyone, he was no fool, but if the Bridaus succeeded and Flore retained nothing, he would be content. He loved her enough to prefer her to Mademoiselle Fichet, the town’s richest heiress. His frankness won the men over; they rose and pledged to stand by him. Max’s leadership was partly strategic—by befriending youths from the best families and doing them services he cultivated a support base to recover position later.

They joked about Fario’s cart, but Max promised to settle that business and asked them to be in the market early and to tell him when the old man came for his cart. The Knights dispersed at half-past three in the morning. Max walked slowly back to the place Saint-Jean, concealing his alarm. The news had unsettled him: his experience on the hulks at Cabrera had taught him deep dissimulation. Above all, the forty thousand francs a year from Rouget’s lands underpinned Max’s attachment to Flore. By Flore’s assurance of interest in Rouget’s property, Max had built a financial future. The arrival of legitimate heirs now shook his confidence in that future. As he passed homeward he muttered at Flore’s silence about a sister and nephews during all their years together; seven hundred and fifty thousand francs in savings spread among notaries at Bourges, Vierzon, and Châteauroux could not be transferred into government funds overnight without the whole town noticing. The urgent task was to get rid of these relations and then secure the property; he would think it over and let himself in with a pass-key, putting off action until daylight.

To understand Max’s alarm, it is necessary to relate how Flore Brazier came to be called the “Rabouilleuse” and how she became quasi-mistress of Jean-Jacques Rouget’s house. As old Doctor Rouget advanced in years he saw his son Jean-Jacques as incompetent and treated him harshly. One day while returning from his rounds the doctor encountered a charming little girl at the edge of some fields along the avenue de Tivoli. She had been wading and catching crabs—“rabouiller,” a local term meaning to disturb the water to bring crabs to the surface—and wore rags and a paper hat; yet beneath the poverty was a strikingly lovely face and figure. With her was her uncle Brazier, a peasant with a weathered look.

The doctor questioned the girl and her uncle and learned she was from Vatan, worked for five sous a day while the season lasted, gleaned during harvest, and spun in winter. The uncle had been drinking and had no means to protect her. Doctor Rouget, moved—partly by compassion, partly by self-interest, and perhaps by private motives—offered to take the child into his service: she would be well fed, dressed, shod, and taught. He proposed that the uncle bring her to his house in place Saint-Jean; he would pay wages, and he suggested the uncle might keep them as guardian.

The uncle and niece came to Rouget’s house that evening. They admired the doctor’s hall with wooden carvings, a handsome marble chimney-piece, and a large gilded mirror. The house Rouget had inherited from the Descoings family stood in the middle of the long square of place Saint-Jean and contained a large hall, kitchen, stairs to upper floors and attic, a wood-shed, wash-house, stable and coach-house. The hall’s panelled walls held pictures rescued from religious houses: an Albano Holy Family, Domenichino’s Saint Jerome, a Head of Christ by Bellini, a Virgin of Leonardo, a Bearing of the Cross by Titian, a Lazarus by Veronese, a Marriage of the Virgin by Genois, two church paintings attributed to Rubens, probable replicas by Perugino or Raphael, two Correggios, and an Andrea del Sarto. The Descoings had culled these works from three hundred church pictures, often for their frames and glass. The furniture suggested luxury—a Boulle clock between two silver candlesticks, carved oak armchairs, a rich console with a China jar for tobacco—but Rouget and his household took little care of the place, letting it show signs of neglect.

Rouget welcomed his guests. He ordered Fanchette, his cook, to prepare wine and told Brazier to bring the child into his service, offering three hundred francs in wages, and even proposing to pay a year in advance. Brazier, touched by the promise and the chance to better Flore’s lot—indeed by the idea of two years’ wages—consented. The doctor arranged lodgings for Flore; she would sleep in the room above his. The uncle kissed her and sent her off with advice to obey and be good.

That evening the whole town of Issoudun gossiped about the sudden appearance of the little rabouilleuse in Doctor Rouget’s house; the nickname fit and stuck. The doctor perhaps intended a protégée relationship like Louis XV’s with Mademoiselle de Romans, but being older, his designs remained ambiguous. From twelve to fourteen Flore lived in comfort and favor. She had fine clothes, a gold watch, jewels given by Rouget to encourage study, and a tutor to teach reading, writing and ciphering. But the peasant girl disliked the hard discipline of study, and Rouget ceased her education when she showed reluctance.

Town gossip speculated wildly about the doctor’s motives. Some thought the old man meant to marry Flore; others, mindful of Rouget’s past and advanced age, scoffed at such a notion. Brazier’s boasting when drunk and other rumors provided fodder for malicious tongues. But in 1802, when Catholic worship in the provinces had barely recovered from the Revolution and priests remained scarce, moral strictures and public scandals were less clearly policed; marriage was sometimes a legal formality more than a sacral bond, and revolutionary notions had altered local mores. Thus, in Issoudun no serious reproach attached to Flore beyond the town’s satirical nickname. She was considered “wide awake”—shrewd and resourceful—and Uncle Brazier’s roughness left her disposition to fend for herself.

Rouget’s favor continued into the years when Flore matured, though the doctor grew colder in his last years. He had acquired no children of his own and disliked his son Jean-Jacques’s incompetence. People read his actions variously: some saw paternal protection, others a cynical calculation. When the doctor’s death approached, his notary urged him to make provision for Flore, who was then seventeen. Rouget’s response—“So I do. My death sets her at liberty”—came off as a bitter, ironic dismissal, revealing a nature that cloaked ill will in wit. He refused to leave anything to the girl outright, saying instead that her beauty would make her rich enough. Those words, and his general harshness, convinced many that, far from indulging a romantic fancy, Rouget had hardened into a man who would use irony to hide selfish resentment.

Thus Flore, the Rabouilleuse, arrived at Rouget’s house as a rescued child transformed into a comfortable yet ambiguous ward. The town’s gossip fussed over motives and possible futures, while Maxence, already dependent on the anticipated income from Rouget’s lands, tied his fortune to Flore’s influence. This web of affection, protecting friends, local satire, and looming inheritances set the scene for the anxiety and plotting that would follow the news of Madame Bridau’s coming.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Jean-Jacques Rouget did not mourn his father; Flore Brazier did. The old doctor had made his son miserable since Jean-Jacques came of age in 1791, but he had provided Flore, the peasant girl, with the comfortable life country folk value. After the funeral, when Fanchette was clearing the table and asked Flore what would become of her now that the doctor was dead, Jean-Jacques’s eyes brightened and, for the first time, his dull face showed feeling.

“Leave the room,” he said to Fanchette.

At seventeen Flore still had the delicate features that had attracted the doctor, though the ease of domestic life had begun to plump her. Her bust and shoulders were developed; her chin and profile remained faultless.

“Flore,” Jean-Jacques said in a trembling voice, “you feel at home in this house?”

“Yes, Monsieur Jean.”

He began to declare himself but fell silent, troubled by memories of his father and uncertain how far the old man’s indiscretions had gone. Flore, unable to suspect his simplicity, waited; Jean-Jacques left the conversation unfinished. It would be long before she understood his character.

At his father’s death, Jean-Jacques—then thirty-seven—was as timid and submissive to paternal discipline as a child of twelve. This timidity explains his life. There are timorous minds and timorous nerves; when both unite a man becomes a cipher, though sometimes fine qualities hide behind such infirmity. Jean-Jacques’s timidity sprang from a torpor of his faculties; a strong hand might have roused him. In him, as in cretins, the feeling of love was stronger than his intellect, though he had enough sense for ordinary affairs. Passion, stripped of romantic idealism, only heightened his fear. He had never courted in Issoudun: small, awkward, with a flattened face, pallid skin and large pale green eyes, he looked prematurely old. A woman’s presence stultified him; desire petrified his speech. Paralyzed between passion and lack of ideas, he sought solitude.

The doctor, late in life, tried to manage his heir. Wishing to marry his son but unwilling to surrender control of the estate, he trained Jean-Jacques in habits of thrift and mechanical routine, keeping estates in good order and long-leased to spare him management. He tried to instill avarice and fixed notions about investing revenues. What he did not perceive was that Jean-Jacques’s timidity hid a passionate attachment to the Rabouilleuse. Flore was the only woman in the bachelor’s home; he secretly contemplated her constantly and rejoiced in the education his father gave her, believing it would make her easier to win. Passion that can reflect gives even ninnies a kind of intelligence; animal instinct persisted with the force of thought.

The day after the funeral Flore, expecting a declaration, waited; Jean-Jacques hovered near her, glanced at her with passion but could not speak. At last, during dessert, he awkwardly asked if she liked life there and whether she was honest. Flore answered readily. He asked if she was still the barefoot girl her uncle had brought; she blushed. Encouraged, Rouget stumbled, turned red, and could only say, “Are you the same?” She answered on her honor; he took her hand and, unable to say more, hinted that she could stay and be mistress of the house.

This odd situation lasted three weeks. Once Flore found Jean-Jacques crouched like a dog on the landing; she thought, “He loves me,” and tolerated his oddities. During another meal she told him the truth about his father: the old man had been a joker, a worthy man who might have meant harm to Jean-Jacques. Rouget, taking her hand, said, “If your father was nothing to you you can stay here with me, can’t you?” She admitted she would if he wished it. He confessed he had loved her since she came barefoot. Flore made no direct answer at first; when silence grew embarrassing, Jean-Jacques used a practical argument: “Wouldn’t it be better than returning to the fields?” She assented.

Yet he could not press his claim. Men of his nature want certainty; the effort to avow love costs too much and they quail. Ten months after his father’s death Jean-Jacques changed: his leaden face cleared and he breathed happiness. Flore insisted he take better care of himself and watched him go out. The town noticed the transformation.

“Have you heard?” people said. “Jean-Jacques inherits everything, even the Rabouilleuse. She’s sly! She’s very handsome and will make him marry her.” Some thought he ought to marry a woman of family; others praised Flore for devotion to the poor fellow. In this way Flore obtained management of the Rouget household—from father to son, as young Goddet had said. It is necessary to sketch that management for the edification of old bachelors.

Fanchette, the cook, alone thought it wrong that Flore should be queen over Jean-Jacques. She protested with injured virtue, but was humiliated by a child mistress. Fanchette owned three hundred francs a year in the Funds and had an annuity from the doctor; she could live without working and quit the house nine months after the funeral, April 15, 1806. That date, the narrator suggests, may mark when Flore ceased to be an honest girl.

Foreseeing Fanchette’s departure, the Rabouilleuse resolved to do without a servant. For six months she had studied Fanchette’s culinary operations. In choice living doctors rival bishops; the late doctor, trained under Rouelle in Paris, had applied chemistry to cooking and improved provincial techniques. He taught subtleties—how to treat omelettes, the use of a “cagnard,” methods to prevent browned butter’s tartness—secrets honed in his kitchen.

Flore, a born fryer and roaster, soon surpassed Fanchette and became a cordon-bleu with care for Jean-Jacques’s comfort. She polished furniture, kept everything clean, managed the wash and mended linen with thrift. She learned the family business, consulted the notary Monsieur Heron, and advised Jean-Jacques well. Two months before the doctor’s death Flore’s uncle Brazier had died after a fall, leaving a wine-shop where he had spent most of his time. Flore had also lost her father; thus she served her master with an orphan’s gratitude.

This period was paradise for poor Jean-Jacques. He acquired gentle habits of an animal, trained into monastic regularity. Flore woke him with breakfast ready; after a morning walk he read papers at three, spent much time at dinner, and went to bed early to save fire and candles—provincial economies that encourage empty-mindedness.

For nine years their life remained regular. Jean-Jacques lent at five percent on first mortgages with release of the wife’s rights if married, never more than a third of a property’s value, and required notes for an extra two and a half percent—his father’s rules. Usury, that clog on peasant ambition, had destroyed rural regions; Rouget’s seven and a half percent levy seemed reasonable and gave him the choice of investments; notaries reaped commissions.

During these nine years Flore obtained absolute control. At first she treated him familiarly; then, surpassing him in mind and force, she became effectively his superior and he the servant of his servant. He met this mastery halfway by letting her mother him as a son clings to a mother’s protection. Flore kept the house and managed all business; Jean-Jacques left everything to her so completely that life without her seemed impossible. She indulged his fancies and understood him thoroughly. Her brightness was his chief pleasure; when it clouded his state betrayed her power, which she sometimes exercised. Secure in her power, she did not think to make him marry her.

Towards the close of 1815 Flore, then twenty-seven, had reached full beauty: plump, fresh, white-skinned, the ideal “buxom housewife,” likened to a handsome barmaid raised in type. In 1816 she saw Maxence Gilet and fell in love at first sight. Maxence, a penniless officer, perceived her value once he learned of her and Jean-Jacques’s positions. He took up abode at the Rougets’, recognizing the old bachelor’s weakness. Flore’s passion affected household affairs: she grew sullen and gave Jean-Jacques flashes of temper like a wife suspecting infidelity. He asked why; her eyes were full of hatred and her voice contemptuous.

“You have neither heart nor soul!” she cried. “Sixteen years I have spent my youth in this house, and I have only just found out you have a stone there” (striking her breast). “For two months you have seen that brave captain, a victim of the Bourbons, cut out for a general and down in poverty, hunted into a hole where there’s no way to make a penny! He’s forced to sit on a stool all day in the mayor’s office to earn—what? Six hundred miserable francs—and here are you, with six hundred and fifty-nine thousand well invested, and sixty thousand francs’ income—thanks to me, who never spend more than three thousand a year—and you never think of offering him a home here, though the second floor is empty! You’d rather rats and mice run riot than put a human being there—and he’s a lad your father always allowed to be his own son! Do you want to know what you are? A fratricide! And I know why. You see I take an interest in him, and that provokes you. Well, yes! I do take an interest in him.”

Jean-Jacques, flustered, could only plead ignorance. She answered bitterly that there ought to be a man in the house for safety and reputation, and that Max could swallow three burglars as a flash. People might gossip; the proper story would be that his father commanded care of the poor Max. For nine years she had eaten his bread and refused many suitors; she had always replied she would not abandon the “poor innocent.” terrified at losing her, Jean-Jacques promised to invite Maxence.

He did so politely, before witnesses. A reconciliation occurred; but Jean-Jacques noticed that Flore’s affections had wholly shifted. For weeks she complained to tradespeople about Rouget’s alleged stinginess in inviting his natural brother. No one was deceived: they saw Flore as clever and artful. Old Rouget, however, found himself comfortable after Max became master of his house; he gained a companion who paid him attention without servility. Gilet talked, discussed politics, and sometimes walked with Rouget. After Max’s arrival Flore stopped cooking, saying it spoiled her hands. Mere Cognette, grand master of idleness, produced a relative, an old maid who had lost a curate master and promised devotion: an excellent cook whose fidelity would be rewarded with an annuity of three hundred francs after ten years. This Vedie, scarred by smallpox and ugly, took over the kitchen.

Flore now adopted the title Madame Brazier. She wore corsets, silk or fine woollen and cotton dresses, expensive neckerchiefs, embroidered caps, collars, lace ruffles and boots. She renewed her youth through richness and elegance, trimming a rough diamond into worth. She wished to honor Max. In 1817 she brought a horse, called English, from Bourges for the weary cavalry captain. Max had found in Issoudun an old lancer of the Imperial Guard, a Pole named Kouski, who, now poor, asked to quarter at Rouget’s as the captain’s servant. Max idolized Kouski, especially after the duel with three royalists. Thus from 1817 the household numbered five persons, three of whom were masters, and expenses rose to about eight thousand francs a year.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At the time Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun—Maitre Desroches said—to claim an inheritance, Jean-Jacques Rouget had become nearly semi-vegetative. After Max’s arrival, Flore set the household firmly under her control. Surrounded by better food, Rouget ate more but did not gain strength; he grew daily more feeble, his eyes ringed with dark circles. When asked, he said he had never been better; thought mentally deficient, people failed to notice his failing faculties. His love for Flore kept him alive; he existed only for her, obeying her slightest look and watching her like a dog. As Madame Hochon noted, at fifty-seven he looked older than Monsieur Hochon, an octogenarian.

Everyone assumed, rightly, that Max’s apartment matched his charm. In six years the captain had improved and adorned it for himself and Flore: colored tiles, somewhat elegant wallpaper, mahogany furniture, gilt mirrors, muslin curtains with red borders, a canopy bed, provincial draperies—luxury for Issoudun though commonplace in Paris. One unusual thing caused talk: rush-matting on the stairs to muffle footsteps. Max came in at daybreak without waking anyone; Rouget did not suspect his guest was an accomplice in the Knights of Idleness’ nocturnal performances.

About eight the next morning Flore, in a cotton dressing-gown, lace cap, and furred slippers, opened Max’s door softly and found him asleep. He woke briskly.

“You are sleepy; I’ll go,” she said.

“No, stay; there’s something serious going on,” he answered. “It concerns you, me, and that old fool. You never told me he had a family! Well, his family are coming here—no doubt to turn us out.”

“Ah! I’ll shake him well,” Flore answered.

“Mademoiselle Brazier,” said Max gravely, “things are too serious for giddiness. Send me my coffee; I’ll think about what to do. Come back at nine and we’ll talk. Meanwhile, act as if you’ve heard nothing.”

Frightened, Flore went to make coffee; a quarter-hour later Baruch burst in, crying to the grand master, “Fario is hunting for his barrow!”

Max dressed and strolled to the tower embankment, where a crowd had gathered. Fario, a withered, bitter Spaniard known as “goodman Fario,” complained his cart had vanished. The horse stood harnessed some distance away; the cart, mysteriously atop the embankment near the tower, seemed to have flown up. People laughed at the supposed witchcraft; Fario swore and feared mischief.

“Don’t lose heart. We’ll go up and see how your barrow got there,” said Max, instructing Francois to keep the crowd back at the foot of the embankment. Max and a few knights climbed. At the top it seemed impossible the cart had reached there; Fario, panic-stricken, fretted about getting it down. Max, taking advantage of the Spaniard’s stupefaction, lifted the barrow by its shafts and flung it down, shouting, “Look out there, below!” The crowd, obeying Francois, had retired. The cart dashed to pieces in a very picturesque manner.

“Ah, brigands! ah, scoundrels!” cried Fario, furious and threatening revenge unless someone gave him a new one. Max mocked him, offering to buy a replacement if Fario handed over two hundred and fifty francs, a jest that cooled the Spaniard. Max raised his fist at Fario, but Baruch restrained him. “If I, by accident, broke your barrow, and you slander me, we are quits,” Max said. Fario muttered he would remember, then rode away amid hurrahs, though his complaint spread gossip throughout the province. The affair reached Berry and then three departments; people speculated about Max and the Rabouilleuse, and rumors reached Bourges, Vatan, Vierzon, and Chateauroux. Maxence Gilet knew how venomous such talk would become.

“We can’t stop their tongues,” he said.

“Ah! I did a foolish thing!” Francois warned. “They are coming to-night.”

“They! Who?” Max asked.

“The Bridaus. My grandmother has had a letter from her goddaughter.”

“Listen, my boy,” said Max low. “Neither Flore nor I ought to seem opposed to the Bridaus. If these heirs are to be got rid of, it’s for you Hochons to drive them out. Find out what sort of people they are. Tomorrow at Mere Cognette’s, after I’ve taken their measure, we can decide what to do, and how to set your grandfather against them.”

“The Spaniard found the flaw in Max’s armor,” Baruch told Francois as they watched Max enter his door.

While Max was occupied, Flore, despite counsel, exploded upon the poor bachelor. When Jean-Jacques incurred her anger, his little attentions and vulgar fondlings—his simple joy—were suppressed. Flore sent him into disgrace: no tender words—“my kitten,” “my old darling”—only a cold, sharp, ironic “you,” which cut to the heart. Instead of helping him with his toilet, anticipating his wants, or admiring him—“You look as fresh as a rose!”—she left him to dress alone. If he called she answered from the foot of the stairs, “I can’t do everything at once; how can I look after your breakfast and wait upon you? Aren’t you big enough to dress yourself?”

“Oh, dear! what have I done to displease her?” he asked after a rebuff when seeking shaving-water.

“Vedie, take up the hot water,” cried Flore.

“Vedie!” exclaimed the poor man, stupefied. Vedie, pretending concern, told him he might search all the women and not find the equal of Madame; Rouget dropped into an armchair and stared into vacancy, forgetting to shave. These alternations of tenderness and severity affected him like a disease; he existed only through his love for Flore.

“Well, haven’t you shaved yet?” she said, appearing suddenly at his door.

Her presence made him start; from pale and cast down he grew red but dared not complain.

“Your breakfast is waiting,” she added. “You can come down as you are, in dressing-gown and slippers; for you’ll breakfast alone, I can tell you.”

To breakfast alone was his worst punishment; he loved to talk to her as he ate. At the foot of the stairs he had a coughing fit; emotion aggravated his catarrh.

“Cough away!” said Flore in the kitchen, indifferent. “Confound the old wretch! He is able enough to get over it without bothering others. If he coughs up his soul, it will only be after—”

These were the amenities the Rabouilleuse addressed to Rouget when angry. He sat distressed at a corner of the table, looking at his old furniture and pictures with a disconsolate air.

“You might at least have put on a cravat,” said Flore. “Do you think it pleasant for people to see such a neck as yours, redder and more wrinkled than a turkey’s?”

“But what have I done?” he asked, lifting his big light-green eyes full of tears, trying to meet her hard countenance.

“What have you done? As if you didn’t know! Your sister Agathe—who is as much your sister as I am sister of the tower of Issoudun, if one believes your father, and who has no claim at all on you—is coming from Paris with her son, a miserable two-penny painter, to see you.”

“My sister and my nephews coming to Issoudun!” he said, bewildered.

“Oh, yes! play surprised; try to make me believe you didn’t send for them! Don’t fret; we won’t trouble your Parisians—before they set foot in this house we shall have shaken the dust off ours. Max and I will be gone, never to return. As for your will, I’ll tear it in quarters under your nose and to your very beard—do you hear? Leave your property to your family if you don’t think we are your family; and then see if you’ll be loved for yourself by people who have not seen you for thirty years—who in fact have never seen you! Is that the sort of sister who can take my place? A pinchbeck saint!”

“If that’s all, my little Flore,” said the old man, “I won’t receive my sister, or my nephews. I swear this is the first I heard of their coming. It is all got up by Madame Hochon—a sanctimonious old—”

Max, overhearing, entered and said in a masterly tone, “What’s all this?”

“My good Max,” Rouget said, glad of his protector, “I swear by all that is most sacred, I now hear this news for the first time. I never wrote to my sister; my father made me promise not to leave her any property—to leave it to the Church rather. Well, I won’t receive my sister Agathe or her sons—”

“Your father was wrong, my dear Jean-Jacques, and Madame Brazier is still more wrong,” answered Max. “Your father may have had reasons, but he is dead and his hatred should die with him. Your sister is your sister, and your nephews your nephews. You owe it to yourself to welcome them, and you owe it to us as well. What would people say in Issoudun? Thunder! I’ve enough on my shoulders, without hearing people say we shut you up and don’t allow you a will of your own, or that we influence you against your relations and are trying to get hold of your property. The devil take me if I don’t pull up stakes if calumny is flung at me! the other is bad enough! Let’s eat our breakfast.”

Flore, now mild, helped Vedie set the table. Old Rouget, full of admiration for Max, took him by both hands and led him to a window recess, saying low, “Ah! Max, if I had a son, I couldn’t love him better than I love you. Flore is right: you two are my real family. You are a man of honor, Max, and what you have just said is true.”

“You ought to receive and entertain your sister and her son, but not change your arrangements about property,” Max advised. “In that way you will do right in the world’s eyes and yet keep your promise to your father.”

“Well! my dear loves!” cried Flore gayly, “the salmi is getting cold. Come, my old rat, here’s a wing for you,” she said, smiling on Jean-Jacques.

At these words the long-drawn face lost its cadaverous tints, a smile flickered; but he had another violent coughing fit. Flore rose, pulled a little cashmere shawl from her shoulders, and tied it round his throat, exclaiming: “How silly to put yourself in such a way about nothing. There, you old goose, that will do you good; it has been next my heart—” She fetched a black velvet cap for his nearly bald head.

“As good as she is beautiful,” Max said, “but she is quick-tempered, like all people who carry their hearts in their hands.”

This scene, played again and again with variations, typifies how women act when interest draws them from obedience to power: the pouting of a fine lady is like the violence of a Rabouilleuse. At all levels, bitter sayings, ironical jests, cold contempt, hypocritical complaints, and false quarrels win as much success as lower outbursts.

Max told the story of Fario and his barrow with humor, making Rouget laugh; Vedie and Kouski in the kitchen exploded; Flore laughed convulsively. After breakfast, while Rouget read the newspapers, Max carried Flore to his quarters.

“Are you sure he has not made any other will since the one leaving property to you?” Max asked.

“He hasn’t anything to write with,” she answered.

“He might have dictated it to some notary,” Max said. “We must look out for that. Therefore be cordial to the Bridaus, and at the same time turn those mortgages into money. The notaries will be glad to make the transfers; it is grist to their mill. The Funds are rising; we shall conquer Spain, deliver Ferdinand VII and the Cortez, and they will be above par. You and I could make a good thing by putting the old fellow’s seven hundred and fifty thousand francs into the Funds at eighty-nine. Only you must try to get it done in your name; it will be so much secured anyhow.”

“A capital idea!” said Flore.

“And as there will be an income of fifty thousand francs from eight hundred and ninety thousand, we must make him borrow one hundred and forty thousand francs for two years, to be paid back in two instalments. In two years, we shall get one hundred thousand francs in Paris, and ninety thousand here, and risk nothing.”

“If it were not for you, my handsome Max, what would become of me now?” she said.

“Oh! tomorrow night at Mere Cognette’s, after I have seen the Parisians, I shall find a way to make the Hochons themselves get rid of them.”

“Ah! what a head you’ve got, my angel! You are a love of a man.”

The place Saint-Jean lies at the center of a long street called, at its upper end, rue Grand Narette, and at its lower, rue Petite Narette. “Narette” in Berry denotes a steep street. The Grand Narette rises rapidly from place Saint-Jean to the port Vilatte. Opposite Jean-Jacques Rouget’s was the house of old Monsieur Hochon. From Madame Hochon’s usual sitting-room it was easy to see what went on at the Rouget household and vice versa. The Hochon house resembled Rouget’s; doubtless the same architect built both.

Monsieur Hochon, formerly tax-collector at Selles, born at Issoudun, had returned home and married the sister of the sub-delegate, the gay Lousteau, exchanging his office for one at Issoudun. Having retired before 1787, he escaped the Revolution’s dangers and adhered to its principles like other “honest men” who side with winners. He had the reputation of a miser; one episode illustrates him. At his daughter’s wedding, needed to give a dinner to the Borniche family, the cook asked Hochon for twine to truss the turkey. The old man produced an end of string already used to tie a parcel and, before she left, called, “Gritte, mind you give it back to me!” Year by year Hochon grew more petty and penurious; at eighty-five he belonged to those who stop mid-conversation to pick up a pin, remarking as they stick it in their coat sleeve, “There’s the wife’s stipend.” He complained of cloth quality and how his coat had lasted ten years. Tall, gaunt, thin, sallow, saying little, reading little, doing nothing to fatigue himself, observant of forms, he enforced at home strict abstemious discipline, weighing and measuring food and drink for his family: his wife née Lousteau, his grandson Borniche with a sister Adolphine, heirs of old Borniche, and his other grandson Francois Hochon.

Hochon’s eldest son, taken by the 1813 draft and killed at Hanau, had married early a rich woman to avoid conscription; after enrollment he wasted his substance and died. His wife, who followed the army at a distance, died at Strasbourg in 1814, leaving debts Hochon refused to pay—answering creditors with an axiom: “Women are minors.”

The house, though large, was scantily furnished; on the second floor two rooms suited Madame Bridau and Joseph. Old Hochon regretted having kept them, though they contained two beds, an old armchair of raw wood covered with needlework, and a walnut table with a wide-mouthed water-pitcher in a basin. He stored winter apples, pears, medlars, and quinces on heaps of straw in these rooms where rats and mice ran, exhaling a mixed odor of fruit and vermin. Madame Hochon directed everything be cleaned; she fastened wallpaper with wafers and decorated windows with curtains pieced from old hoards. Denied a strip of drugget, she laid her bedside carpet for little Agathe—“Poor little thing!” she said—who was now over forty-seven. Madame Hochon borrowed two night-tables from a neighbor and hired two chests of drawers with brass handles from a second-hand dealer next to Mere Cognette. She had preserved two candlesticks carved in choice woods by her father, who liked turning; from 1770 to 1780 it was fashionable for some rich people to learn a trade, and Monsieur Lousteau had been a turner. These candlesticks were ornamented with circlets from roots of rose, peach, and apricot trees. Madame Hochon risked using her relics. These preparations and sacrifices increased old Hochon’s anxiety; until now he had not believed the Bridaus’ arrival.

The morning of the Fario trick Madame Hochon said after breakfast, “I hope, Hochon, you will receive my goddaughter, Madame Bridau, properly.” Then, making sure her grandchildren were out of hearing, she added, “I am mistress of my own property; don’t oblige me to make up to Agathe in my will for any incivility on your part.”

“Do you think, madame,” answered Hochon mildly, “that at my age I don’t know forms of decent civility?”

“You know very well what I mean, you crafty old thing! Be friendly to our guests, and remember I love Agathe.”

“And you love Maxence Gilet also, who is getting the property away from your dear Agathe! Ah! you’ve warmed a viper in your bosom; but after all, the Rouget money is bound to go to a Lousteau.”

Having said this about Max and Agathe, Hochon turned to leave; but Madame Hochon, still erect and spare, wearing a round cap with ribbon knots, powdered hair, a taffeta petticoat of changeable colors, tight sleeves, and high-heeled slippers, placed her snuff-box on a little table and said, “Really, Monsieur Hochon, how can a man of your sense repeat absurdities which cost my poor friend her peace and Agathe the property she ought to have had from her father. Max Gilet is not the son of my brother, whom I often advised to save the money he paid for him. You know as well as I do that Madame Rouget was virtue itself—”

“And the daughter takes after her; for she strikes me as uncommonly stupid. After losing all her fortune, she brings her sons up so well that here is one in prison and likely to be indicted before the Court of Peers for a conspiracy worthy of Berton. As for the other, he is worse—a painter. If your protégés stay here till they have extricated that fool Rouget from Gilet and the Rabouilleuse, we shall eat a good deal more than half a measure of salt with them.”

“That’s enough, Monsieur Hochon; you had better wish they may not have two strings to their bow.”

Monsieur Hochon took his hat and cane with an ivory knob and went away stunned; he had no idea his wife could show such resolution. Madame Hochon took her prayer-book to say the service; her age prevented daily church attendance; she went on Sundays. Since receiving her goddaughter’s letter she had added a petition to her prayers, supplicating God to open Jean-Jacques Rouget’s eyes, bless Agathe, and prosper the expedition she had drawn her into. Concealing the plan from her grandchildren, whom she accused of being “parpaillots,” she had asked the curate to say a mass for Agathe’s success during a neuvaine held by her granddaughter Adolphine Borniche, thus making prayers by proxy.

Adolphine, then eighteen, who for seven years had sat sewing by her grandmother in that cold household of monotony and method, undertook the neuvaine hoping to inspire some feeling in Joseph Bridau, whose oddities her grandfather so castigated.

All the old and sensible people of the town and heads of families approved Madame Hochon’s conduct in receiving her goddaughter; their good wishes matched secret contempt for Maxence Gilet’s conduct. Thus the news of Rouget’s sister and nephew split Issoudun into two parties: the higher, older bourgeoisie, who offered good wishes and watched without assisting, and the Knights of Idleness and Max’s partisans, who were capable of high-handed outrages against the Parisians.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales office in the Place Misere at three. Though tired, Madame Bridau felt youth revive at sight of her native town; every step stirred girlish memories. News of the Parisians spread instantly through Issoudun; their arrival was known within ten minutes. Madame Hochon, Agathe’s godmother, met her at the door and kissed her like a daughter. After seventy-two years of a barren life, marked by the graves of three unhappy children, she cherished this early friendship and took a deep interest in Bridau. Agathe was led into the salon where Monsieur Hochon sat, cool as a tepid oven.

Introductions followed: Baruch Borniche, twenty-two; François Hochon, twenty-four; and little Adolphine, blushing and awkward, anxious not to look at Joseph Bridau, who was closely examined by the two young men and old Hochon. The miser thought the new guest would be as hungry as a convalescent; the youths plotted lively entertainment. Agathe presented her son with maternal pride.

“He looks ill,” said Madame Hochon.

“No, madame,” Joseph answered bluntly, “I am like my father, and very ugly at that.”

Madame Hochon pressed Agathe’s hand, understanding; she recalled that the late Madame Descoings had written favorably about Joseph’s talent. “Talent!” cried Joseph; not yet, he said, though with time he might win fame and fortune. The old man sneered at painting. Adolphine was sent to see about dinner, while Joseph offered to tend the trunks and inspected the attic rooms. Word of the Bridau arrival spread fast; Baruch rushed into town to describe Agathe’s dress and especially Joseph’s haggard, brigand-like face. That evening Joseph was the talk of every household.

“Looks like a baboon,” whispered one. “A brigand,” said another. “He seems capable of anything; perhaps he ruined his brother.” The town’s curiosity flared; many resolved to call that night and examine the Parisians. Their arrival struck Issoudun like dropping a beam among frogs.

The Hochon house itself was plainly, almost meanly, furnished: damp walls, bare staircase and drawers with only necessities. Joseph felt the sharp transition from poetic Paris to arid province. Seeing Monsieur Hochon methodically slicing bread for each person, he thought of Molière’s Harpagon. Dinner confirmed his fears: quantity over quality. A watery soup, ceremonious bouilli garnished with parsley, vegetables served separately, hard-boiled eggs on sorrel, a nut-oil salad, and tiny custards flavored with burnt oats—plus butter, radishes, pickled gherkins, and horseradish. Madame Hochon approved, nodding contentedly; her husband shrugged as if to remind everyone of the sacrifices he made.

After Monsieur Hochon sliced the bouilli as thin as a dancing shoe sole, he replaced it with three pigeons. The wine was the 1811 country vintage. Adolphine had decorated the table ends with flowers. Joseph ate like a man who had had an execrable breakfast earlier, and when he asked for more bread, Monsieur Hochon produced a tiny portion with the silence and gravity of an old soldier meeting death. The gesture made Joseph understand that asking for more was forbidden. Conversation continued: Agathe learned her birthhouse had been bought by the Borniches; she wished to see it.

Gritte, the sixty-year-old servant, brought dessert—the famous ripe cheese of Touraine and Berry, mold-patterned like vine-leaves—along with nuts and time-honored biscuits. Joseph laughed openly at the provincials’ habit of putting out only slightly rotten fruit. He teased Monsieur Hochon into fetching peaches, pears and Saint Catherine plums; Adolphine got grapes. Joseph regarded the two young men as if to say, “Is this the fare that keeps your healthy faces?” Baruch smiled; he and his cousin frequented Mère Cognette’s for feasts. Baruch had been told that the grand master had convoked the Order at midnight for a magnificent supper and a great enterprise; the Hochon’s hospitality explained why the two young fellows would be joining nocturnal repasts.

After dinner, Madame Hochon moved to the salon to take the liqueur, asking Joseph for his arm. As they walked out she whispered, “This dinner won’t give you indigestion; I had hard work to get it for you. It is always Lent here; you’ll get enough to live on, and no more. So bear it patiently.” Her kindness pleased Joseph. She confided she had lived fifty years with that man—Monsieur Hochon—“without ever hearing half-a-dozen gold pieces chink in my purse,” but she prayed to survive it. Joseph, moved by the piety and modest heroism of this unrecognized noble soul, asked to paint her portrait, but she declined, “too weary of life to wish to remain here on canvas.” She then produced a flask of ratafia she made herself from a nuns’ recipe and poured in little engraved glasses; Agathe remarked, “It seems as though my father’s turn were coming round!” The customs of the province, immutable, returned them to childhood memories.

Hochon said he would go to his club to read the papers, leaving the three women and Joseph alone in the salon. The room was exactly as when Agathe left—only swept but unchanged by Monarchy, Revolution, Empire or Restoration. Agathe, surprised to see a stuffed canary on the mantel among the old clock and silver candlesticks, exclaimed at how cruelly tried her life had been compared to her godmother’s. The old woman reminded her that trials are in the heart and that greater need for resignation makes the struggle harder. Then she prudently turned the conversation to Agathe’s affairs, pointing out that the Rouget windows directly faced them—an enemy within sight.

Adolphine, destined for a cloister, looked constantly out the window at Jean-Jacques Rouget, hoping to hear more about Maxence Gilet, the Rabouilleuse, and the slanders circulating. Madame Hochon told her granddaughter to leave them alone until visitors arrived; they would have ten or twelve inquisitive people that night. She then recounted Maxence’s influence over Jean-Jacques and the Rabouilleuse’s doings, with all the embroidered town gossip. Soon the Borniches, Beaussier, Lousteau-Prangin, Fichet, Goddet-Hérau families—fourteen persons in all—arrived, determined to examine the Parisians.

When the fourteen were seated and compliments exchanged, Madame Hochon presented Agathe and Joseph. Joseph sat in his armchair all evening, slyly studying sixty faces that, from five until half-past nine, posed for him gratis. The town appreciated neither his sardonic glances nor his sinister looks: they went home ruffled by his sarcasm, uneasy under his smile, uneasy at his fierce face—an appearance that frightened a class unable to recognize genius.

After ten, when the household slept, Madame Hochon kept her goddaughter in her chamber until midnight. The two women exchanged sorrows. As Agathe listened to the last echoes of a soul that had missed its destiny, she perceived the immensity of the desert in which this noble, unrecognized soul had been wasted; she realized that, with the small joys of city life, perhaps she was not the most unhappy. “You who are so pious,” she said, “explain my shortcomings; tell me what God punishes in me.” “He is preparing us, my child,” answered the old woman, “for the striking of the last hour.”

At midnight the Knights of Idleness gathered like shadows under the Boulevard Baron’s trees. François and Baruch thought Max merely meant to give them supper; the consensus was that their grand master, Maxence Gilet, had concocted some scheme against the Parisians and that driving the Bridau family away would be a good joke. Max appeared and called them to Mère Cognette’s, and the cry rang through the town; many later asked what those frightful cries at one in the morning had been. The supper at La Cognette brightened the faces of twenty-two guests; the whole Order was present.

At two, after sipping, Max rose to speak. He complained that the honour of his grand master was grossly attacked that morning by a vile peddler—“a Spaniard (oh, Cabrera!)”—and he resolved to revenge himself within the rules. He proposed a trick to drive the peddler crazy while “feeding the sacred animals of the Egyptians”—i.e., rats. He ordered each man to procure twenty rats, starve them so they would be ravenous, and bring them clandestinely within three days. Invitations to plunder Fario’s grain-store were made: let four hundred rats loose in the old Capuchin church where Fario stores his grain and cause a deficit. He allowed mice and field-mice as substitutes. He assigned roles: Kouski hunted, Mademoiselle Flore Brazier set mouse-traps, Kouski and others would provide animals. Goddet boasted he could find animals worth forty rats alone. Someone suggested a squirrel, another a little monkey—both rejected because they might betray who put the beasts there. Young Beaussier proposed catching pigeons from different farms and using them to attract more. Max appointed Beaussier director of the pigeons, ordered them to turn soles backward on their list shoes when approaching the store, and to make the church watchman drunk if necessary. He promised his shotgun, a treasure from Versailles, to whoever found a trick to get the Bridaus into difficulties with the Hochons so the Parisians would be sent off—or forced to leave—without harming the respectable relatives of his friends Baruch and François. If the inventor preferred not to take the gun, Max promised his horse. After that night twenty brains worked to carry out the plan against Agathe and her son. But success required devil or chance; conditions made it nearly impossible.

The next morning, before the second breakfast at ten, Agathe and Joseph came down. Hochon’s first breakfast was a cup of milk and a slice of bread and butter, often taken in bed. Joseph noticed Jean-Jacques Rouget standing squarely at his door across the street; Agathe could hardly recognize her brother’s face, so altered was he. Beside him stood Flore Brazier, decked out: hair dressed, snowy shoulders and a dazzling bosom in a gauze neckerchief trimmed with lace; her grenadine dress had leg-of-mutton sleeves, bracelets at the wrists, a gold chain over her bosom, little satin slippers, and expensive stockings—obvious display. She leaned forward to give Jean-Jacques his black silk cap; the scene seemed staged.

“There’s a fine woman, rare!” cried Joseph, who, artist that he was, saw in Flore a magnificent Titian-like model. Agathe signaled she was used to such jargon. “So you think a creature who is depriving you of your property handsome?” asked Madame Hochon. Joseph allowed that this did not prevent her being a splendid model. Agathe warned him about speaking before Adolphine. Monsieur Hochon, who had come in, said if Jean-Jacques were not an idiot he would have married long ago and had children—and then Agathe would have had no chance at the property. “The sooner the matter is decided,” he added, “the sooner I will rid myself of the guests.” The miser’s interest was clear: a large fortune might be at stake, and he feared Agathe might bequeath some legacy to her goddaughter; the possibility of inheriting her fortune kept him alert. Madame Hochon, knowing threats of a will could be used to obtain concessions, had always resorted to them.

In the middle of breakfast, Kouski brought a letter from Jean-Jacques Rouget to his sister. Monsieur Hochon read it aloud: he learned of their arrival, guessed why they preferred the Hochons’ house to his, yet offered a cordial reception and invited Joseph to dine the next day, adding that young men are less sensitive than women about company and inviting Baruch and François to accompany him. Monsieur Hochon, pleased that his own hand might have penned that courteous note, gave a sly look to his wife and Agathe and suggested Jean-Jacques was capable of it only as much as he himself could be capable of giving away twenty-five louis—meaning, the soldier’s or adventurer’s boldness. The remark implied Jean-Jacques might be playing a role.

Madame Hochon, worried, said they must reply that Agathe and Joseph would go, and that Joseph must dine at Rouget’s. But old Hochon intervened; he feared Agathe might be favored or that her nephews might triumph and be given an influence over the Rouget property. He believed, with a sense of social justice, that the property should go to natural heirs rather than be pillaged by outsiders. He urged Joseph to try to obtain a private talk with his uncle and to “pull the wool from his eyes” about the situation put upon him by Maxence and Flore. He advised telling Jean-Jacques frankly about the nephews and their claims. Madame Hochon supported this plan; the woman’s affection for Agathe and the potential fortune combined to awaken the old miser’s intelligence.

By midday the household—Monsieur and Madame Hochon, Agathe, and Joseph—had concocted an answer to Jean-Jacques, a carefully worded letter meant to preempt Max and Flore. Agathe’s reply explained her long absence from Issoudun by referring to “strange and false ideas” her father conceived about her and the joys and sorrows of life in Paris. She mentioned her son Philippe, accused and imprisoned for his devotion to the Emperor, and her own humble position working at a lottery office to live. She defended Joseph’s choice of painting as a career that requires sacrifice and prolonged study, promising glory precedes fortune. She reminded Jean-Jacques that he had two nephews—one, Philippe, who carried the Emperor’s orders at Montereau and served in the Guard at Waterloo and now lies under accusation; the other, Joseph, seized by natural gifts for art from his youth. She accepted Jean-Jacques’s invitation for herself and allowed Joseph to dine there, adding a sisterly embrace.

“There’s the matter started,” said Monsieur Hochon. “Now, when you see him, you must speak plainly to him about his nephews.” Gritte returned ten minutes later with village gossip. Since yesterday the Rabouilleuse—Flore Brazier—had left the salon and Roger’s part of the house in a pitiable state, but now the rooms were put in order, glittering; Kouski had gone to fetch provisions and come back at nine with abundance. “It’s going to be a grand dinner,” she said. “Monsieur pokes into everything; he said, ‘I want to do honor to my nephew,’ and he is delighted to find his sister amiable. Oh! she had on such a dress! Two diamonds in her ears—Vedie says three thousand francs apiece; such lace! rings and bracelets! you’d think she was a shrine; a silk dress like an altar cloth. Little black satin slippers and stockings—flowers in silk and openwork—and a lovely little apron Vedie says cost more than two years’ wages.” The description magnified the suspicion that Flore and Maxence aimed to astonish and to assert influence through ostentatious display.

“Well done! We shall have to dress up,” laughed Joseph. The Hochons were now resolved to arm Agathe with words to sway her brother. Monsieur Hochon, who sat with his head in his hands, then declared: “You have to do with a Maître Bonin!”—i.e., a crafty schemer. “With your ideas, young man,” he added to Joseph, “you haven’t force enough to struggle with a practised scoundrel like Maxence Gilet. No matter what I say, you will commit some folly. But tell me everything you see, hear, and do tonight. Go, and God be with you! Try to get alone with your uncle. If, despite your genius, you can’t manage it, that in itself will throw light on their scheme. But if you do get a moment alone, out of earshot, pull the wool from his eyes about the situation those two have put him in, and plead your mother’s cause.”

Thus equipped with counsel, a plan, and the stern expectations of the Hochons, Joseph prepared to confront his uncle at dinner the next day, while in Issoudun the Knights of Idleness had already set in motion their mischievous plot—rats, pigeons, and a shotgun promised to the inventor of a humiliating trick—designed to harry any interlopers and to settle the local claims by wiles and noise rather than law. The town, its gossips, the miserly household, the scheming youths and the determined Parisians were now all lodged around a single question: who would control the Rouget property, and what means would be used to decide that fate?

At four o’clock Joseph crossed the avenue of weak lindens from the Rouget house to the Hochon house. Kouski announced him. The table was set in the large hall; Joseph, who could easily distinguish his uncle, went up, kissed him, and bowed to Flore and Max.

“We have not seen each other since I came into the world, my dear uncle,” the painter gayly announced.

“You are very welcome, my friend,” said the old man, looking at his nephew in a dull way.

“Madame,” Joseph said to Flore with an artist’s vivacity, “this morning I was envying my uncle the pleasure of admiring you every day.”

“Isn’t she beautiful?” the old man murmured; his dim eyes brightened.

“Beautiful enough to be the model of a great painter.”

“Nephew,” said Rouget, nudged by Flore, “this is Monsieur Maxence Gilet; he served the Emperor, like your brother, in the Imperial Guard.”

Joseph rose and bowed.

“Your brother was in the dragoons, I believe,” Maxence said. “I was only a dust-trotter.”

“On foot or on horseback,” said Flore, “you both risked your skins.”

Joseph and Max inspected one another. Max, a dandy, had an eager face and the gray eyes of his father. The painter marked him with an artist’s eye: “My uncle must be a fearful bore, and that handsome girl has compensations. It is a triangular household.”

At that instant, Baruch and François entered.

“Have you seen the tower of Issoudun?” Flore asked Joseph. “No? Then, if you’d like a walk before dinner in an hour, we’ll show you the great curiosity of the town.”

“Gladly,” said the artist, incapable of seeing any impropriety.

While Flore went to put on her bonnet, gloves, and shawl, Joseph jumped up to look at the pictures.

“Ah! you have pictures, indeed, uncle!” he cried at one that struck him.

“They came to us from the Descoings, who bought them during the Revolution, when the convents and churches in Berry were dismantled,” the old man answered.

Joseph was lost in admiration.

“Magnificent! Oh, what painting! That fellow didn’t spare his canvas. Better and better, as at Nicolet’s—”

“There are seven or eight very large ones up in the garret, kept on account of the frames,” said Gilet.

“Let me see them!” cried the artist; Max took him upstairs.

Joseph returned wildly enthusiastic. Max whispered to the Rabouilleuse, who drew the old man to a window embrasure where Joseph heard her say, “Your nephew is a painter; you don’t care for those pictures; be kind, and give them to him.”

“It seems,” said Jean-Jacques, leaning on Flore’s arm to reach Joseph by an Albano, “—it seems that you are a painter—”

“Only a ‘rapin,’” said Joseph.

“What may that be?” asked Flore.

“A beginner,” replied Joseph.

“Well,” continued Jean-Jacques, “if these pictures can be of any use to you in your business, I give them to you,—but without the frames. Oh! the frames are gilt, and besides, they are very funny; I will put—”

“Well done, uncle!” cried Joseph, enchanted; “I’ll make you copies of the same dimensions, which you can put into the frames.”

“But that will take your time, and you will want canvas and colors,” said Flore. “You will have to spend money. Come, Père Rouget, offer your nephew a hundred francs for each copy; here are twenty-seven pictures, and I think there are eleven very big ones in the garret which ought to cost double,—call the whole four thousand francs. Oh, yes,” she added, turning to Joseph, “your uncle can well afford to pay you four thousand francs for the copies, since he keeps the frames—but bless me! you’ll want frames; and they say frames cost more than pictures; there’s more gold on them. Answer, monsieur.” She shook the old man’s arm. “Hein? It isn’t dear; your nephew will take four thousand francs for new pictures in place of the old ones.”

“Well, nephew, I will pay you four thousand francs for the copies—”

“No, no!” said the honest Joseph; “four thousand francs and the pictures, that’s too much; the pictures, don’t you see, are valuable—”

“Accept, simpleton!” said Flore; “he is your uncle, you know.”

“Very good, I accept,” said Joseph, bewildered; for he had recognized a Perugino.

The artist beamed as he went out with the Rabouilleuse on his arm, which suited Maxence’s plans. Neither Flore, nor Rouget, nor Max, nor anyone in Issoudun knew the true value of the pictures, and Max reckoned he had secured Flore’s triumph cheaply as she paraded the town, leaning on the arm of her master’s nephew. People flocked to their doors to see the crab-girl’s triumph. This sensation helped Max; when they returned at five, everyone talked of the cordial understanding between Max, Flore, and the nephew. The incident of the pictures and the four thousand francs circulated. The dinner, with Lousteau, one of the court judges, and the Mayor, was splendid and lasted five hours. By nine o’clock, at dessert, Joseph, seated opposite his uncle and between Flore and Max, had fraternized with the soldier and thought him a good fellow. Joseph returned home at eleven somewhat tipsy. Rouget was carried to bed dead-drunk; he had soaked up the wine like sand.

“Well,” Max said to Flore when alone, “isn’t this better than making faces at them? The Bridaus are well received, given presents, smothered with attentions; they will sing our praises and leave us in peace. Tomorrow you and I and Kouski will take down the pictures and send them over to the painter; we’ll put the frames in the garret and cover the walls with one of those varnished papers representing scenes from Telemachus.”

“Oh, that will be much prettier!” said Flore.

Next morning Joseph did not wake till midday. From bed he saw the pictures, brought in while he slept, leaning against the opposite wall. While he examined them anew, recognizing each masterpiece and searching for signatures, his mother had gone to see and thank her brother, urged by old Hochon, who, having heard of the painter’s follies the night before, despaired of the Bridau cause.

“Your adversaries have the cunning of foxes,” he said to Agathe. “In all my days I never saw a man carry things with such a high hand as that soldier; they say war educates young men! Joseph has let himself be fooled. They shut his mouth with wine, those miserable pictures, and four thousand francs! Your artist hasn’t cost Maxence much!”

The long-headed old man instructed Madame Bridau about a line of conduct: enter into Maxence’s ideas, cajole Flore, set up intimacy, and thus obtain a few moments’ interview with Jean-Jacques alone. Madame Bridau was warmly received by her brother, to whom Flore had taught her lesson. The old man was ill from his excesses. Agathe, under the circumstances, could scarcely at once speak of family matters; Max thought it proper and magnanimous to leave brother and sister alone. The calculation worked: poor Agathe found her brother so ill she would not deprive him of Madame Brazier’s care.

“Besides,” she said to the old bachelor, “I wish to know a person to whom I am grateful for the happiness of my brother.”

These words pleased the old man, who rang for Madame Flore. Flore, not far off, showed servile attentions and tenderness to her master, fussing over him like a new bride, and the poor creature received it with feeling.

“We owe you much gratitude, mademoiselle,” said Agathe, “for the attachment you have long shown my brother, and for the way you watch over his happiness.”

“That is true, my dear Agathe,” said the old man; “she has taught me what happiness is; she is a woman of excellent qualities.”

“And therefore, my dear brother, you ought to have recompensed Mademoiselle by making her your wife. Yes! I am too sincere in my religion not to wish to see you obey the precepts of the church. You would each be more tranquil in mind if you were not at variance with morality and the laws. I have come here, dear brother, to ask for help in my affliction; but do not suppose that we wish to make any remonstrance as to the manner in which you may dispose of your property—”

“Madame,” said Flore, “we know how unjust your father was to you. Monsieur here can tell you,” she went on, looking at Jean-Jacques, “that the only quarrels we have ever had were about you. I have always told him that he owes you part of the fortune he received from his father, and your father, my benefactor,—for he was my benefactor.” She added tears: “I shall ever remember him! But your brother, madame, has listened to reason.”

“Yes,” said the old man, “when I make my will you shall not be forgotten.”

“Don’t talk of these things, my dear brother; you do not yet know my nature.”

After such a beginning, it is easy to imagine how the visit went on. Rouget invited his sister to dinner on the next day but one.

During these three days the Knights of Idleness captured an immense quantity of rats and mice, kept them half-famished, then let four hundred and thirty-six into a grain storage one night, including some breeding mothers. They also put a dozen pigeons into the old church roof. These animals revelled while the watchman, enticed away by drink, took no care of his master’s property.

Madame Bridau believed, contrary to old Hochon, that her brother had as yet made no will; she intended to ask about Mademoiselle Brazier as soon as she could take a walk with him alone—a hope Flore and Maxence constantly dangled and disappointed.

Meantime the Knights searched for a way to put the Parisians to flight, finding none practicable.

At the end of a week—half the time the Parisians were to stay—the Bridaus had made no progress. “Your lawyer does not understand the provinces,” old Hochon told Madame Bridau. “What you have come to do can’t be done in two weeks, nor in two years; you ought never to leave your brother, but live here and try to give him some ideas of religion. You cannot countermine the fortifications of Flore and Maxence without getting a priest to sap them. That is my advice, and it is high time to set about it.”

“You certainly have very singular ideas about the clergy,” said Madame Hochon to her husband.

“Bah!” the old man exclaimed, “that’s just like you pious women.”

“God would never bless an enterprise undertaken in a sacrilegious spirit,” said Madame Bridau. “Use religion for such a purpose! Why, we should be more criminal than Flore.”

This conversation took place at breakfast with François and Baruch listening.

“Sacrilege!” exclaimed old Hochon. “If some good abbé, keen as I have known many, knew what a dilemma you are in, he would not think it sacrilege to bring your brother’s lost soul back to God, force him to send away the woman who causes the scandal (with a proper provision, of course), and show him how to set his conscience at rest by giving a few thousand francs a year to the seminary and leaving his property to the rightful heirs.”

The passive obedience the old miser exacted from his children and grandchildren prevented Baruch and François from showing surprise, but they exchanged glances about how dangerous such a scheme would be to Max’s interest.

“The fact is, madame,” said Baruch, “that if you want to secure your brother’s property, the only sure way will be to stay in Issoudun for the necessary length of time—”

“Mother,” said Joseph hastily, “you had better write to Desroches about all this. As for me, I ask nothing more than what my uncle has already given me.”

After recognizing the great value of his thirty-nine pictures, Joseph had carefully unnailed the canvases, covered them with paper, gummed the edges, and laid them one above another in an enormous wooden box, which he sent to Desroches by the carrier’s wagon, proposing to write him a letter by post. The precious freight had been sent off the night before.

“You are satisfied with a pretty poor bargain,” said Monsieur Hochon.

“I can easily get a hundred and fifty thousand francs for those pictures,” replied Joseph.

“Painter’s nonsense!” exclaimed old Hochon.

“Mother,” said Joseph, “I am going to write to Desroches and explain the state of things here. If he advises you to remain, you had better do so. As for your situation, we can always find another like it.”

“My dear Joseph,” said Madame Hochon, following him from the table, “I don’t know anything about your uncle’s pictures, but they ought to be good, judging by the places from which they came. If they are worth only forty thousand francs—a thousand apiece—tell no one. Though my grandsons are discreet, they might, without intending harm, speak of this windfall; it would be known all over Issoudun; and it is very important that our adversaries should not suspect it. You behave like a child!”

In fact, before evening many persons in Issoudun, including Max, had heard this estimate, which caused a search for old paintings and the appearance of many execrable daubs. Max repented having driven Rouget into giving away the pictures, and his rage against the heirs, after hearing old Hochon’s ecclesiastical scheme, increased. The influence of religion on such a feeble creature as Rouget was the one fear. The news decided Maxence to turn Rouget’s investments into money and to borrow on his landed property to buy into the Funds as soon as possible; but he considered it even more important to get rid of the Parisians at once.

Flore, by Max’s advice, pretended Monsieur was too feeble to take walks and ought to have a carriage. This pretext avoided inquiry when they went to Bourges, Vierzon, Châteauroux, Vatan, and other places where withdrawing investments obliged Max and Flore to be with Rouget. At the close of the week, all Issoudun was amazed that the old man had gone to Bourges to buy a carriage—a step the Knights regarded as favorable to the Rabouilleuse. Flore and Max selected a hideous “berlingot,” twenty-two years old, a sort of demi-fortune carriage. It only cost four hundred and fifty francs; Max bought a stout mare and repainted the carriage dark brown, buying a tolerable harness at a bargain. The town awaited Pere Rouget’s equipage; at its first appearance every household was on its doorstep.

The second time the old bachelor went out he drove to Bourges, went before a notary, and signed a power of attorney in favor of Maxence Gilet to make all the transfers enumerated. Flore reserved to herself the business of making Monsieur sell investments in Issoudun and its neighborhood. The principal notary in Bourges was requested by Rouget to get him a loan of one hundred and forty thousand francs on his landed estate. Nothing was known at Issoudun of these secret proceedings. Maxence, a good rider, went with his horse to Bourges and back between five in the morning and five in the afternoon. Flore never left the old bachelor. Rouget consented without objection to the actions Flore dictated; but he insisted that the investment in the Funds producing fifty thousand francs a year should stand in Flore’s name as holding a life-interest only, and in his as owner of the principal. His tenacity in this domestic dispute worried Max; he thought it the result of reflections inspired by seeing the natural heirs.

Amid these movements, which Max concealed, he forgot the Spaniard and his granary. Fario returned to Issoudun to deliver his corn after various trips to raise the price of cereals. The morning after his arrival he noticed the church roof black with pigeons. He hurried to the storehouse, where he found half his grain devoured. Thousands of mice and rat marks showed further ruin. The church was a Noah’s ark. But anger turned the Spaniard white when he found the grain at the bottom sprouting from water introduced by tin tubes into the pile. The pigeons and rats could be instinct, but the wetting showed human hand.

Fario sat on the chapel steps with his head between his hands. After half an hour he spied the squirrel Goddet had sent him playing on a cross-beam. He rose, turned to his watchman with a calm face, made no complaint, but hired laborers to gather sound grain and spread the moist to dry, saving as much as possible; after estimating his losses at about three fifths, he attended to filling his orders. But his previous manipulations had raised the price of cereals, and he lost on the three fifths he had to buy to fill orders; his losses were really over half. The Spaniard, with no enemies, at once attributed this revenge to Gilet. He was convinced Maxence and others were the authors of the nocturnal mischief and intended to ruin him. It was a matter of over three thousand francs—nearly his whole capital since the peace. Driven by the desire for vengeance, the man displayed the persistence of a detective. Hiding at night in different parts of Issoudun, he soon acquired proof of the Knights’ proceedings: he saw them, counted them, watched their rendezvous, and knew of their suppers at Mère Cognette’s; he lay in wait, witnessed their deeds, and thus became well informed of their nocturnal habits.

In spite of Max’s journeys and preoccupations, he did not neglect his nightly employments—first, because he did not wish his comrades to suspect his operations with Pere Rouget’s property; second, to keep the Knights in hand. They were convened for a prank to be talked of for years: poisoned meat was to be thrown to every watchdog in town and environs. Fario overheard them congratulating each other after a supper on the probable success of this massacre and laughing over the general mourning it would cause.

“It will make people forget Fario’s cart,” said Goddet.

Fario needed no further proof; his mind was made up.

After three weeks in Issoudun, Agathe and Madame Hochon were convinced of the old miser’s observation that it would take years to destroy Max and Flore’s influence. She had made no progress in Jean-Jacques’s confidence and was never left alone with him. On the other hand, Mademoiselle Brazier triumphed openly by taking Agathe to drive in the caleche, sitting beside her on the back seat while Monsieur Rouget and his nephew occupied the front. Mother and son impatiently awaited an answer to the confidential letter they had written to Desroches. The day before the night on which the dogs were to be poisoned, Joseph, nearly bored to death in Issoudun, received two letters: one from the great painter Schinner and the second from Desroches.

Schinner’s letter, from Beaumont-sur-Oise, said Joseph was recommended for the chateau de Presles for the Comte de Serizy; Schinner was off to Italy; Joseph should take his place at the Salon if need be. Desroches’s letter gave practical advice:

My dear Joseph,—Your Monsieur Hochon strikes me as an old man full of common-sense, and you give me a high idea of his methods; he is perfectly right. My advice, since you ask it, is that your mother should remain at Issoudun with Madame Hochon, paying a small board—say four hundred francs a year—to reimburse her hosts. Madame Bridau ought, in my opinion, to follow Monsieur Hochon’s advice in everything; for your excellent mother will have many scruples in dealing with persons who have no scruple at all, and whose behavior to her is a masterstroke of policy. That Maxence, you are right enough, is dangerous. He is another Philippe, but of a different calibre. The scoundrel makes his vices serve his fortunes, and gets his amusement gratis; whereas your brother’s follies are never useful to him. All that you say alarms me, but I could do no good by going to Issoudun. Monsieur Hochon, acting behind your mother, will be more useful to you than I. As for you, you had better come back here; you are good for nothing in a matter which requires continual attention, careful observation, servile civilities, discretion in speech, and a dissimulation of manner and gesture wholly against the grain of artists.

If they have told you no will has been made, you may be sure they have one. But wills can be revoked, and as long as your uncle lives he is susceptible of being worked upon by remorse and religion. Your inheritance will be the result of a combat between the Church and the Rabouilleuse. There will come a time when that woman will lose her grip and religion will be all-powerful. So long as your uncle makes no gift of the property during his lifetime, and does not change his estate, all may come right when religion gets the upper hand. For this reason you must beg Monsieur Hochon to keep an eye on the condition of your uncle’s property. It is necessary to know if the real estate is mortgaged, and if so, where and in whose name the proceeds are invested. It is so easy to terrify an old man with fears about his life, in case you find him despoiling his own property, that almost any heir with a little adroitness could stop the spoliation at its outset. But how should your mother, with ignorance of the world and religious ideas, manage such an affair? I cannot throw more light. All you have done probably gave the alarm, and your adversaries may already have secured themselves—

“That is what I call an opinion in good shape,” exclaimed Monsieur Hochon, proud of being appreciated by a Parisian lawyer.

“Oh! Desroches is a famous fellow,” answered Joseph.

“It would be well to read that letter to the two women,” said the old man.

“There it is,” said Joseph, giving it to him; “as to me, I want to be off to-morrow; and I am now going to say good-by to my uncle.”

“Ah!” said Monsieur Hochon, “I see that Monsieur Desroches tells you in a postscript to burn the letter.”

“You can burn it after showing it to my mother,” said the painter.

Joseph dressed, crossed the little square, and called on his uncle, who was finishing breakfast. Max and Flore were at table.

“Don’t disturb yourself, my dear uncle; I have only come to say good-by.”

“You are going?” said Max, exchanging glances with Flore.

“Yes; I have work at the chateau of Monsieur de Serizy, and I am all the more glad because his arm is long enough to do a service to my poor brother in the Chamber of Peers.”

“Well, well, go and work,” said old Rouget, with a silly air. Joseph thought him extraordinarily changed within a few days. “Men must work—I am sorry you are going.”

“Oh! my mother will be here some time longer,” remarked Joseph.

Max made a movement with his lips which Flore observed, meaning: “They are going to try the plan Baruch warned me of.”

“I am very glad I came,” said Joseph, “for I have had the pleasure of making your acquaintance and you have enriched my studio—”

“Yes,” said Flore, “instead of enlightening your uncle on the value of his pictures, which is now estimated at over one hundred thousand francs, you have packed them off in a hurry to Paris. Poor dear man! he is no better than a baby! We have just been told of a little treasure at Bourges,—what did they call it? a Poussin,—which was in the choir of the cathedral before the Revolution and is now worth, all by itself, thirty thousand francs.”

“That was not right of you, my nephew,” said Jean-Jacques, at a sign from Max, which Joseph could not see.

“Come now, frankly,” said the soldier, laughing, “on your honor, what should you say those pictures were worth? You’ve made an easy haul out of your uncle! and right enough, too—uncles are made to be pillaged. Nature deprived me of uncles, but damn it, if I’d had any I should have shown them no mercy.”

“Did you know, monsieur,” said Flore to Rouget, “what your pictures were worth? How much did you say, Monsieur Joseph?”

“Well,” answered the painter, red as a beetroot,—“the pictures are certainly worth something.”

“They say you estimated them to Monsieur Hochon at one hundred and fifty thousand francs,” said Flore; “is that true?”

“Yes,” said the painter, with childlike honesty.

“And did you intend,” said Flore to the old man, “to give a hundred and fifty thousand francs to your nephew?”

“Never, never!” cried Jean-Jacques, on whom Flore had fixed her eye.

“There is one way to settle all this,” said the painter, “and that is to return them to you, uncle.”

“No, no, keep them,” said the old man.

“I shall send them back to you,” said Joseph, wounded by Max and Flore’s offensive silence. “There is something in my brushes which will make my fortune, without owing anything to any one, even an uncle. My respects to you, mademoiselle; good-day, monsieur—”

Joseph crossed the square in a state of irritation artists can imagine. The entire Hochon family were in the salon. Seeing Joseph gesticulate and talking to himself, they asked him what was the matter. The painter, as open as the day, related before Baruch and François the scene; two hours later it was the town’s talk, embroidered with various circumstances. Some persons insisted the painter was maltreated by Max; others that he had misbehaved to Flore and been turned out.

“What a child your son is!” said Hochon to Madame Bridau; “the booby is the dupe of a scene they have been keeping for the last day of his visit. Max and the Rabouilleuse have known the value of those pictures for the last two weeks—ever since he foolishly told it before my grandsons, who blurted it out to all the world. Your artist had better have taken himself off without taking leave.”

“My son has done right to return the pictures if they are really so valuable,” said Agathe.

“If they are worth, as he says, two hundred thousand francs,” said old Hochon, “it was folly to put himself in a way to be obliged to return them. You might have had that, at least, out of the property; whereas, as things are going now, you won’t get anything. And this scene with Joseph is almost a reason why your brother should refuse to see you again.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Between midnight and one the Knights of Idleness began their nightly rounds distributing food to the dogs, an expedition that lasted until three when they supped at Cognette’s. At half-past four, after creeping back, Max turned from the rue l’Avenier into the Grande rue and was struck in the heart by Fario, who fled toward Vilatte, wiping the knife on his handkerchief. The Spaniard washed the handkerchief in the Riviere forcée and returned quietly to his lodgings at Saint-Paterne, entering by an open window and going to bed; later a watchman found him asleep.

Max uttered a fearful cry as he fell. Lousteau-Prangin, the judge’s son and a distant relative of the sub-delegate, and young Goddet of the lower Grande rue ran up, shouting, “They are killing Max! Help!” The town, accustomed to false alarms, remained largely asleep. They found Max fainting and roused Monsieur Goddet, the surgeon. Max had recognized Fario; but when revived among bystanders and realising the wound was not mortal, he seized the opportunity to exploit the attack. Whispering to Flore, he said, “I think I recognized that cursed painter!”

Lousteau-Prangin ran to his father, the judge. Max was carried home by Cognette, young Goddet, and two others, while Mere Cognette and Monsieur Goddet walked beside the stretcher. Those who carried him glanced at Monsieur Hochon’s door and saw Hochon’s servant sweeping the steps; Gritte, like most provincial households, was early astir. The few words Max had spoken aroused Monsieur Goddet’s suspicions. He asked the woman if Monsieur Joseph Bridau was in bed.

“He went out at half-past four,” she answered. “He walked up and down his room all night.”

Such an answer produced exclamations of horror; Gritte, curious, came and saw Max in his bloody shirt half-fainting on a mattress. Artists could guess what had kept Joseph restless all night: he had dreaded the gossip at Issoudun, imagined the town would say he had fleeced his uncle and been deceitful. Early at dawn he had gone to the poplar avenue to walk and calm himself.

While Joseph comforted himself by vowing never to return to Issoudun, and Max prepared a theatrical outrage to appeal to the town’s passions, Monsieur Goddet, after probing the wound and finding that a small pocket-book had deflected the knife so the blow was serious but not mortal, did what country surgeons do to add drama: he “did not answer for the patient’s life,” dressed him, pronounced a grave verdict to the Rabouilleuse, Jean-Jacques Rouget, Kouski, and the Vedie, then left. The Rabouilleuse wept over Max; Kouski and the Vedie declared the captain likely to die. Nearly two hundred people gathered about Place Saint-Jean and the two Narettes.

Max, lying and feverish, told Flore, “I sha’n’t be a month in bed; and I know who struck the blow. We’ll profit by it to get rid of the Parisians. I said I thought I recognized the painter; pretend I’m expected to die and try to have Joseph Bridau arrested. Let him taste prison for a couple of days and his mother will be off to Paris when she gets him out. Then we needn’t fear the priests they talk of setting on the old fool.”

When Flore came downstairs the crowd was ready to take the impression she meant to give. She went out in tears and, sobbing, alleged that the painter, “who had just the face for that sort of thing,” had been angry with Max the previous night about some pictures he had “wormed out” of Père Rouget.

“That brigand—look at him!—thinks that if Max were dead his uncle would leave him his fortune,” she cried. “The old one told me Max is Doctor Rouget’s son!”

“Ah! he meant to do the deed before he left Issoudun; he chose his time, for he was going away today,” said one of the Knights of Idleness.

“Max hasn’t an enemy in Issoudun,” said another.

“Besides, Max recognized the painter,” said the Rabouilleuse.

“Where’s that cursed Parisian? Let us find him!” they cried.

“Find him? Why, he left Monsieur Hochon’s at daybreak.”

A Knight of Idleness ran to Monsieur Mouilleron. The crowd thickened; commotion threatened. Groups filled the Grande-Narette and gathered before Saint-Jean; a mass assembled at Porte Vilatte. Lousteau-Prangin, Monsieur Mouilleron the commissary of police, the lieutenant of gendarmes, and two men struggled through hedges of people already prejudiced against the Parisian—circumstances, however, pointed against him.

After conferring with Max, Monsieur Mouilleron sent the commissary and a sergeant with one gendarme to inspect the “theatre of the crime.” Messieurs Mouilleron and Lousteau-Prangin, with the gendarme lieutenant, crossed to the Hochon house, now guarded by two gendarmes in the garden and two at the door. The crowd swelled; the whole town surged in the Grande rue.

Gritte rushed, terrified, to inform Monsieur Hochon: “Monsieur, we shall be pillaged! The town is in revolt; Monsieur Maxence Gilet has been assassinated; they say Monsieur Joseph did it!”

Monsieur Hochon, alarmed for his gold, dressed quickly, peered out, then bolted the house. He learned from Gritte that his guest had left at daybreak—after pacing his room all night—and not yet returned. Madame Hochon, already awake from the noise, heard the frightful tidings.

“He is innocent, of course,” she said.

“Before his innocence can be proved, the crowd may pillage us,” replied Hochon, white with fear.

“Where is Agathe?”

“Sound asleep.”

“So much the better,” said Madame Hochon. “A shock might kill the poor child.”

But Agathe woke half-dressed, disturbed by evasive answers and the turmoil. She found Madame Hochon pale, weeping at a salon window beside her husband.

“Courage, my child. God sends us our afflictions,” the old lady said. “Joseph is accused—”

“Of what?”

“Of a bad action he could never have committed,” Madame Hochon answered.

Seeing the lieutenant of gendarmes enter with the magistrates, Agathe fainted.

“There now!” said Monsieur Hochon. “Carry off Madame Bridau; women are only in the way at these times. Take her to her room and stay there, both of you. Sit down, gentlemen. The mistake to which we owe your visit will soon, I hope, be cleared up.”

“Even if it should be a mistake,” said Monsieur Mouilleron, “the crowd is so exasperated I fear for the accused’s safety. I should like to arrest him; that might pacify these people.”

“Who would have believed Monsieur Maxence Gilet inspired so much affection?” asked Lousteau-Prangin.

“One of my men says twelve hundred more are coming from the faubourg de Rome,” said the lieutenant; “they threaten to kill the assassin.”

“Where is your guest?” asked Mouilleron of Hochon.

“He went to walk in the country, I believe.”

“Call Gritte,” said the judge gravely. “The crime was committed not far from here.”

The functionaries exchanged looks.

“I never liked that painter’s face,” said the lieutenant.

When Gritte appeared trembling, Lousteau-Prangin asked if she had seen Monsieur Joseph leave that morning. She confirmed it: “Just as I was getting up; he walked about all night, and was dressed when I came downstairs.” Lousteau ordered warrants fetched. Monsieur Hochon pleaded caution: Joseph may have been agitated for other reasons—he meant to return to Paris that day for business in which Gilet and Mademoiselle Brazier had questioned his honor. Mouilleron added that the quarrel over the pictures yesterday had been hot; with artists it can be a word and a blow. Lousteau declared no one in Issoudun had cause to kill Gilet. Hochon noted, “But what was Monsieur Gilet doing in the streets at four in the morning?”

A clamour rose from the other end of town: “Here he is! Here he is—he’s arrested!” Joseph, returning quietly past the mill at Landrole to breakfast, had been seen at the Place Misere. Two gendarmes ran in time to snatch him from faubourg de Rome inhabitants, who had already pinioned him and threatened him with death.

“Give way! Give way!” cried the gendarmes as they formed a protective escort.

“You see, monsieur,” one who held the painter said, “it concerns our skin as well as yours. Innocent or guilty, we must protect you. The people adore Monsieur Gilet and want to take justice into their own hands. Didn’t we see them in 1830 dusting the jackets of the tax-gatherers?”

Joseph, pale, forced himself to go on. Amid hoots, insults, and stones, he was led from Place Misere to Place Saint-Jean; the gendarmes drew sabres, one was wounded, and Joseph received missiles on his legs, shoulders, and hat.

“We must disperse the crowd; I see but one way,” said the lieutenant to the magistrates. “Take Monsieur Bridau to the Palais accompanied by all of you; my gendarmes will make a circle. One can’t answer for anything before a furious crowd of six thousand.”

“You are right,” said Monsieur Hochon, trembling for his gold.

“Do you wish your friend’s house pillaged?” asked the lieutenant. “Could we beat back a crowd pushed from behind by an angry populace that knows nothing of legal forms?”

“That will do; let us go; we can explain later,” said Joseph, who had recovered composure.

“Give way, friends! He is arrested, and we are taking him to the Palais,” the lieutenant cried. “Respect the law!” said Mouilleron.

“Wouldn’t you prefer to see him guillotined?” jeered a gendarme at a group.

“Yes, yes, they shall guillotine him!” one madman shouted; the women echoed, “They are going to guillotine him!” By the time they reached the end of the Grande-Narette the crowd cried: “They are taking him to the guillotine! They found the knife upon him! That’s what Parisians are! He carries crime on his face!”

Though blood rushed to his head, Joseph walked from Place Saint-Jean to the Palais with calm. In Monsieur Lousteau-Prangin’s private office he declared his innocence: he had been out past five, stood at half-past five before Saint-Cyr’s facade, talked with the sexton, crossed the vegetable market where women had assembled, passed Place Misere, went as far as the mill of Landrole by the Pont aux Anes and watched the ducks five or six minutes—miller’s men must have seen him—and then followed the avenue to Tivoli where he spoke with the gardener. He urged the magistrates to verify these facts and begged not to be arrested, promising to remain in the office until cleared.

These sensible, confident statements impressed the magistrates.

“Yes, summon all those persons,” said Mouilleron; “but it is more than a day’s affair. Make up your mind to be imprisoned in the Palais.”

“Provided I can write to my mother to reassure her—oh, you can read the letter,” Joseph added.

This request was granted, and Joseph wrote to his mother assuring her the mistake could be rectified and that he expected to be free by evening or tomorrow.

Madame Bridau, seized by a nervous attack and medicated by Monsieur Goddet without relief, read the letter and was soothed into the usual depressed state. When Goddet returned, he found her regretting leaving Paris.

“How is Monsieur Gilet?” asked Madame Hochon of Goddet.

“His wound, though serious, is not mortal,” the doctor replied. “With a month’s nursing he will be all right. I left him writing to Monsieur Mouilleron to request Joseph’s release,” he added. “Oh! Max is a fine fellow. He then remembered something proving the assassin was not your son; the man wore list shoes, whereas it is certain Monsieur Joseph left in boots—”

“Aha!” exclaimed Madame Hochon. That detail mattered.

Earlier that night a man had left Max a typed note: “Captain Gilet ought not to let an innocent man suffer. He who struck the blow promises not to strike again if Monsieur Gilet will have Monsieur Joseph Bridau set at liberty,”—without naming the attacker. After reading and burning this letter, Max wrote to Monsieur Mouilleron relating the list-shoe detail from Goddet and begged Joseph’s release, asking Mouilleron to visit so he might explain more.

By the time Max’s letter arrived, Lousteau-Prangin had verified Joseph’s account through the bell-ringer, market-women, washerwomen, and the miller’s men. Max’s note clinched Joseph’s innocence, and Monsieur Mouilleron himself escorted him back to the Hochons’. His mother greeted him with overflowing tenderness; the poor misunderstood son was grateful; misfortune had shown him affection.

“Oh,” said Monsieur Mouilleron with self-satisfaction, “I knew by your look before the crowd that you were innocent; but the only way to protect you was to arrest you as we did. You carried your head high.”

“I was thinking of something else,” replied Joseph. “An officer once told me he had been stopped in Dalmatia under similar circumstances returning from a walk. The recollection made me think of painting a revolt of 1793. Besides, I kept saying, Blackguard that I am! I deserve this for coming to look after an inheritance instead of painting in my studio.”

“If you allow me a word of advice,” said the procureur du roi, “take the postmaster’s carriage to-night and return to Paris by the diligence from Bourges.”

“That is my advice too,” said Monsieur Hochon, burning to see them go.

“My most earnest wish is to get away,” said Agathe, kissing Madame Hochon’s hand. “When shall I see you again?”

“Ah, my dear, never until we meet above. We have suffered enough here below,” the old woman answered.

Shortly after, Monsieur Rouget surprised them with a farewell visit; Jean-Jacques came to bid his sister good-by and offer his cabriolet for the drive to Bourges. “Ah! your pictures have been a great evil to us,” said Agathe. “Keep them, my sister,” said Rouget, who still doubted their value. He let them kiss him and went away without manifesting feeling. Baruch, at a hint from his grandfather, had seen the postmaster. At eleven that night the two Parisians, ensconced in a wicker cabriolet drawn by one horse and a postilion, left Issoudun. Adolphine and Madame Hochon parted with tears; they alone regretted Joseph and Agathe.

“They are gone!” said Francois Hochon, going with the Rabouilleuse into Max’s bedroom.

“Well done! The trick succeeded,” answered Max, now tired and feverish.

“What did you say to old Mouilleron?” asked Francois.

“I told him I had given my assassin some cause to waylay me; that he was dangerous and likely, if I pursued the affair, to kill me before capture. Consequently, I begged Mouilleron and Prangin to make an ostensible active search but really to let the assassin go in peace, unless they wished to see me dead.”

“I do hope, Max,” said Flore, “that you will be quiet at night for some time to come.”

“At any rate, we are delivered from the Parisians!” cried Max. “The fellow who stabbed me had no idea what a service he was doing us.”

The next day the Parisians’ departure was celebrated by nearly everyone in Issoudun as a provincial victory over Paris, except for the sober household of the Hochons. A few of Max’s friends spoke harshly of the Bridaus.

“Do those Parisians think we are all idiots?” cried one. “They came to fleece and have been shorn themselves,” said another. “They actually consulted a lawyer in Paris—what plan had they?” “A plan to get possession of old Rouget. But the Parisians were not clever enough; that lawyer can’t crow over us Berrichons!” “How abominable!” “That’s Paris for you!” “The Rabouilleuse knew they came to attack her, and she defended herself.” “She did gloriously right!”

To the townspeople the Bridaus were Parisians and foreigners; they preferred Max and Flore.

Joseph and Agathe returned to their lodging in the rue Mazarin with relief. On the journey Joseph recovered spirits lost by arrest and a day’s confinement; but his mother could not be cheered. The Court of Peers was about to try the military conspirators, which kept Agathe anxious. Philippe’s conduct, despite a clever defender recommended by Desroches, raised suspicions unfavorable to his character. To escape hearing about the trial—lasting twenty days—Joseph, after briefing Desroches fully on Issoudun, set off with Mistigris for the Comte de Serizy’s chateau.

Contemporary histories need not be repeated here: whether Philippe had played an agreed part or was truly an informer, he was condemned to five years’ surveillance by the police and ordered to leave Paris that day for Autun, the director-general’s pick for his exile. The punishment resembled parole, with a town for a prison. Learning that the Comte de Serizy, one of the peers on the court-martial, was employing Joseph to decorate his chateau at Presles, Desroches sought an audience and found Monsieur de Serizy favorably disposed; they had met. Desroches explained the brothers’ financial plight, recalled their father’s services and neglect under the Restoration, and urged the minister to induce the director-general of police to change Philippe’s residence from Autun to Issoudun and to grant a dole of sixty francs a month to the former lieutenant-colonel—a matter the minister of war would be ashamed to deny.

“I will obtain all you ask of me, for I think it just,” replied the count.

Three days later Desroches, armed with authority, fetched Philippe from the Court of Peers’ prison and took him to his house in the rue de Bethizy. There the young barrister read Philippe a plain, unanswerable lecture in which lawyers reduce clients’ sentiment to blunt terms. He humbled the former staff-officer by reproaching his reckless dissipations, his mother’s misfortunes, and the death of Madame Descoings, then explained Issoudun’s situation and probed Maxence Gilet and the Rabouilleuse’s characters. Philippe, who grasped such matters keenly, listened more to this part than to the reproaches.

“Under these circumstances,” Desroches continued, “you can repair some of the injury you have done—so far as possible; you cannot restore life to the poor mother you have nearly killed. But you alone can—”

“What can I do?” asked Philippe.

“I have obtained a change of residence for you from Autun to Issoudun.”

Philippe’s sunken, almost sinister face brightened.

“And,” Desroches went on, “you alone can recover your uncle’s inheritance; half may already be in Gilet’s hands. I suggest no plan; everything depends on local circumstances. You have to deal with a strong, astute man. The way he tried to recover the pictures he’d given to Joseph, and the audacity with which he laid the crime on your brother, shows he’s capable of anything. Be prudent; behave properly out of policy if not decency. Without telling Joseph, whose pride as an artist would revolt, I have sent the pictures to Monsieur Hochon, telling him to give them up to no one but you. By the way, Maxence Gilet is a brave man.”

“So much the better,” said Philippe; “I count on his courage for success; a coward would leave Issoudun.”

“Consult Monsieur Hochon,” urged Desroches. “You have your orders from the police; I have taken your place in the Orleans diligence for half-past seven tonight. I suppose your trunk is ready; come and dine.”

“I own nothing but what I have on my back,” said Philippe, opening his threadbare blue overcoat; “but I only need three things, which you must tell Giroudeau, the uncle of Finot, to send me—my sabre, my sword, and my pistols.”

“You need more than that,” said the lawyer, shuddering at his client’s appearance. “You will receive a quarterly stipend which will clothe you decently.”

“Bless me! are you here, Godeschal?” cried Philippe, recognising in Desroches’s head-clerk the brother of Mariette.

“Yes, I have been with Monsieur Desroches for two months.”

“And he will stay with me till he gets business of his own,” said Desroches.

“How is Mariette?” asked Philippe, moved.

“She is getting ready for the opening of the new theatre.”

“It would cost her little trouble to get my sentence remitted,” said Philippe. “However, as she chooses!”

After a meagre dinner—Desroches boarded his head-clerk—the two lawyers put the political convict in the diligence and wished him good luck.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
On the second of November, All-Souls’ day, Philippe Bridau presented himself at the Issoudun police to register and, on advice, took rooms in rue l’Avenier. News that an officer banished for a recent conspiracy had come to town spread fast and caused more stir when people learned he was the brother of the painter wrongly accused. Maxence Gilet, recovered from his wound, had converted Père Rouget’s mortgages into cash and placed the proceeds in the “grand-livre.” The 140,000-franc mortgage on Rouget’s real estate caused a scandal in the provinces. Monsieur Hochon, worried for the Bridaus and still trembling from the thought of his house being ransacked, questioned old Monsieur Heron, the Bourges notary, about it.

“If not for me,” Heron declared, “old Rouget would have kept the 50,000-franc income in Maxence Gilet’s name. I advised Mademoiselle Brazier to rely on the will, and cautioned Maxence and his mistress to let this sudden change be forgotten.” Hochon urged protection for the Bridaus.

Maxence and Flore, secure against attack, welcomed another nephew; they knew they could have old Rouget sign a power of attorney transferring funds to Max or Flore if danger threatened. If the will leaving Flore the principal were revoked, the 50,000-franc income and the newly extracted 140,000-franc mortgage would be consolation.

The day after arriving, Philippe called on his uncle around ten, in shabby clothes. Flore shivered at his repulsive appearance; Gilet felt the same chill that warns of a hidden enemy. Philippe’s ruined greatcoat was buttoned to the throat; his trousers were ragged, his boots leaked mud, his hat greasy, his cane dull. His face had the gambler’s exhaustion: sallow skin, sunken cheeks, red-rimmed lids and a threatening brow; bald except for a fringe, his brilliant eyes were cold steel-blue. He introduced himself hoarsely as Philippe Bridau, a lieutenant-colonel ill-treated by the Bourbons, and complained that by police order he had to live on sixty francs a month. He made a point of disdain for relying on his uncle.

“Good-morning, uncle,” he said, offering his hand; Rouget took it. He declared he would sooner cut off his hand than beg from his uncle after how his mother and brother had been treated in Issoudun, but he had felt it proper to pay respects. Flore invited him to breakfast; he declined, saying he had already eaten. He hinted he must safeguard the Bridaus’ honor, spoke curtly about pictures at Monsieur Hochon’s for identification, and left. The violence of his tone and the way he slammed the door shocked Flore and Max more than his looks.

Max and Flore watched him cross to the Hochons’. Gilet, though furious, kept temper; he knew the need for political prudence. Flore urged Rouget not to admit him again.

Later Philippe entered the Hochon house where curiosity was thick. Madame Hochon shuddered at the father of Agathe’s griefs and the supposed murderer of Madame Descoings; others felt unease. Philippe politely explained he had come to introduce himself and manage living on sixty francs. He spoke well, admitted past faults, and promised reformation. He praised Lousteau the journalist as a rara avis, winning the old lady’s favor. Madame Hochon advised him gently; he said prison had taught him much and he would live another life.

Outside, he took Monsieur Hochon aside. In a whisper he warned that the town’s gossip could be ruinous and they must avoid being suspected of advising him publicly. Desroches had instructed Philippe to use Hochon’s counsel discreetly; they agreed to meet “accidentally” in open places when needed. Philippe asked for names of veterans in Issoudun who were not Maxence’s friends—he wanted allies. Hochon named Mignonnet, an artillery captain of the Guard, and Captain Carpentier, a cavalry officer who had replaced Gilet as mayoral clerk and befriended Mignonnet. They discussed Philippe’s need for work; a mutual insurance agency might pay fifty francs a month—enough.

Within a week Philippe had bought on credit a decent suit, boots, gloves, and a hat. Giroudeau sent linen and weapons and a letter to Carpentier, who welcomed him and introduced him to Mignonnet, recommending him as a man of merit. Philippe narrated parts of the conspiracy to them—framed to win admiration. He presented the recent affair as a continuation of earlier army efforts against the Bourbons: a northern operation and a Paris diversion. Betrayed, the leaders had seen the Paris plot reduced to a barrack affair; when the larger scheme failed Philippe said he broke links, letting only the smaller plot be exposed. His apparent misery and ruined costume served to minimize the conspiracy’s size in the authorities’ eyes. He professed to have played both sides, preserving favor with influential figures while waiting to join whichever party served his interests best. Carpentier and Mignonnet, impressed by the account and his seeming devotion, brought him into their circle.

They recommended him for the insurance post Hochon had mentioned, requiring only three hours a day. Philippe frequented their club; his manners and the officers’ praise earned him respect. Determined to seem sober and respectable, and to lull Gilet’s suspicions, he feigned simple-mindedness while quietly observing his uncle’s property and plotting. Hochon had given him details of Rouget’s wealth, which stirred his covetousness. He and Hochon agreed not to alarm Maxence; if Rouget were spirited off to Bourges the game would be lost.

Philippe dined once a week with Mignonnet, once with Carpentier, and every Thursday with Hochon; soon he had engagements most nights. He never spoke of his uncle or his brother and mother except in connection with their stay in Issoudun. The three officers, decorated men in town, walked together daily; Philippe wore a rosette and carried himself like a man of consequence. To Max’s friends Philippe’s demeanor and story made him seem a sabreur, a brave front of the old army.

“His conduct before the Court of Peers shows him either a dupe or a spy,” said Max; others defended him. Philippe, wishing to conceal details of his situation, lived in a secluded house in the faubourg Saint-Paterne with a garden; here he fenced with Carpentier, reviving his skills and learning new tricks. He practiced pistols with Mignonnet and Carpentier, ostensibly for sport but really to make Max believe he preferred pistols in a duel. When he met Gilet he waited for a bow and returned it with a cavalier touch to the hat, as an officer to a private. Gilet gave no sign of impatience and never spoke of Bridau at the Cognettes’, though the pranks of the Order of Idleness were temporarily suspended since Fario’s attack.

Philippe’s contempt for Gilet and Gilet’s restrained bearing became the talk of Issoudun. Some thought Philippe was setting a trap. Potel suggested Max might be baiting him; others speculated about what might grow from the enmity. Rumor set the two up as adversaries.

Philippe had investigated the circumstances of his brother’s arrest and Gilet’s and the Rabouilleuse’s past, and he was brought into contact with Fario the Spaniard. Finding Fario reliable, Philippe made him a confidant and promised indemnity for past losses—Fario, a willing henchman, put his knowledge of the Knights of Idleness and his services at Philippe’s disposal. The town, abuzz with gossip, expected a deadly clash between the two men who intensely despised one another.

Toward the end of November, Philippe told Hochon he had discovered that Hochon’s grandsons Baruch and François were intimate with Maxence and had spied in his house, informing Max of what was said there. He claimed to have overheard them and learned from Fario of their nocturnal visits to drinking-shops with Maxence; they owed Maxence over three thousand francs. Philippe suspected Max and the Rabouilleuse intended to secure the 50,000-franc income and then run away and marry. They must learn what was happening under Rouget’s roof, Philippe said, but he did not know how to proceed. Hochon promised to think.

Jean-Jacques Rouget suffered as never before since Philippe’s first visit. Flore, fearing evil to Max, planned to leave Issoudun and marry Max in Paris after getting the Funds’ income transferred. Rouget refused to transfer, insisting Flore be sole heir—he believed she would abandon him if made rich. She tried cajolery and then coldness; Vedie found Rouget one morning with swollen eyes from weeping. For a week he breakfasted alone, perhaps on meager fare.

The day after Philippe’s talk with Hochon, he went to Rouget’s house again. Flore played tender and solicitous in his presence; Gilet stayed away. Philippe, discerning danger, judged it time to strike. He forced Flore to look him in the face and demanded to know if Rouget was free to walk alone with him. Flore stammered that he was. Philippe insisted Rouget accompany him on foot, implying that if he didn’t, the town’s claim that Rouget was under Flore’s dominion would be confirmed. Seeing Rouget hesitate, Philippe pretended to go; he caught Flore making a menacing gesture at her uncle, and told Rouget to meet him at the door in ten minutes or he would take other measures. He left for the Hochons’.

At nine that night, Hochon, Madame Hochon, Monsieur Heron the notary, and the grandchildren gathered. Hochon, enraged, denounced Baruch and François for their nocturnal life with Maxence and for having informed the Knights of Idleness; he accused them of betraying secrets for money and even of causing past crimes by transmitting plans. He read the guardianship accounts: the Borniche children’s net fortune was 70,000 francs, part of their mother’s dowry, but Hochon, as creditor to his daughter, controlled much of it. Baruch’s portion was twenty thousand francs. Hochon told them he would release their property to them but no longer tolerate their conduct; he discharged his guardianship and made their futures contingent on strict behavior. Baruch and François were stunned.

After Heron read the accounts, Hochon told Baruch he would be sent to Paris to study banking under Mongenod; François, who was in debt, would have only six hundred francs a year and would go to Poitiers to study law. Hochon gloated over his quick vengeance: he would watch them and test them by years of conduct. Having settled the legal business and obtained receipts, Heron was preparing to leave when Philippe arrived. Madame Hochon withdrew with her grandsons to see what effect the scene had produced.

Philippe and Hochon spoke in the window embrasure. Hochon had consulted Heron and learned that the security for the 50,000-franc income could not be sold without the owner’s signature or a power of attorney; Rouget had not given any power before a notary since his arrival. If Rouget attempted to give one in Issoudun they would know instantly; if he left town to sign, they would discover it through registration. Therefore, if Rouget left, they would follow him and learn his movements. Philippe reported seeing his uncle cross the street and detailed his last visit. He told Hochon that Fario and a man named Benjamin Bourdet—a soldier Philippe had helped in Vatan—were watching; they would follow Rouget secretly. “If they let him walk with you,” Hochon mused, “Maxence must believe he has found some means to win the game.” Philippe said Maxence was afraid of him but could not evade him; Fario had stirred public censure against Maxence and the gossip was now in motion.

When Philippe left, Flore hurried to Max’s room with a full account of his boldness. Max resolved that before resorting to fighting him they would play a bold card. He told Flore to send at once to Père Cognette for a horse and a char-à-banc; pack their things and go to Vatan pretending to stay, taking Vedie and the twenty thousand francs in Rouget’s drawer. If Philippe brought Rouget to Vatan, Flore must refuse to return unless Rouget signed a power of attorney. As soon as they obtained it, Max would slip off to Paris while she came back to Issoudun. When Jean-Jacques found her gone he would be desperate and likely follow; Max planned to give him a stern talking-to when he did.

While the foregoing plot unfolded, Philippe walked arm in arm with his uncle along the boulevard Baron, plotting to save Rouget’s fortune and to tame Flore Brazier. Watching the colonel march off with his uncle, Monsieur Hochon mused that two great tacticians were coming to close quarters. Philippe, whose Parisian phraseology showed, told his uncle bluntly that Flore adored Max and wanted Rouget out of the way so she and Max could enjoy his money. Rouget acknowledged the danger, but confessed he loved Flore despite it.

Philippe swore by his mother’s soul to make Flore “as supple as a glove” as she had been before that scoundrel in the Imperial Guard took up with her in Rouget’s house. He proposed a ruthless plan: kill Max as he would a dog—on one condition. Rouget must not sign the power of attorney they wanted before December third; delay it, Philippe urged, for his enemies only sought the signature so they could sell the fifty thousand francs a year in the Funds and run to Paris to pay for wedding festivities out of his millions. Rouget admitted his weakness whenever Flore looked at him; Philippe insisted he promise only to defer signing and let Philippe know the night before he intended to do it. Philippe assured him that Max would not be his proxy unless Max first killed him; if Philippe killed Max, he would take Max’s place and make Flore obedient to Rouget. He even boasted he would thrash her if necessary—though Rouget recoiled at the notion of hurting her.

Philippe framed the dispute in blunt terms to Mignonnet and Carpentier: either Colonel Bridau must kill Captain Gilet or Captain Gilet must kill Colonel Bridau. He planned to sit opposite Max at the Emperor’s coronation banquet and expected Mignonnet and Carpentier to be his seconds. He asked them to arrange the seating so he’d sit opposite Gilet. They agreed, naming him to preside and sit themselves on either side; Max would sit opposite as vice-president. Philippe mocked Max’s supporters Potel and Renard but counted on the duel to resolve who would control Rouget’s fortune and Flore.

For two hours the two soldiers tried to comfort Rouget and bolster the plan. Philippe promised that within days Rouget would be living happily with Flore—after a brief period of tears and protestations, she would yield. He advised Rouget to trust him and to delay any signature; he claimed to know women, having been taught lessons by a costly Parisian mistress. They returned to Rouget’s house about half-past two; Kouski opened the door in tears—he announced that Madame had gone away and taken Vedie with her. The blow prostrated Rouget; he searched the house and fell into despair, sobbing, “Where is she? Oh! where is Max?”

Kouski, under Max’s orders, feigned weeping. Gilet was out sauntering: he had decided being visible would sharpen Rouget’s sense of desertion and make him more pliant. Flore had taken twenty thousand francs in gold from Rouget’s drawer, telling Vedie that Rouget showed her no affection and that she would not return. Max felt he could find her and reconcile Rouget with her if necessary; Rouget begged Max to bring Flore back and said he would do everything she asked him to. Max, who did not want to directly oppose Bridau, kept an air of indifference, though Rouget warned that Bridau had threatened to kill him. Max laughed it off.

The town had begun to turn against Max. Reports of the Knights of Idleness and particularly Joseph Bridau’s arrest, now explained, had disgraced Max and made him a subject of public censure. Many who would previously have greeted him avoided him. Captain Potel, distressed that the Guard should be split against itself—Bridau had incited the bourgeoisie against Gilet—urged Max to attend the banquet and not appear a coward. Max muttered that he would have the money first if a fight came; he would not allow it to go to Philippe. He planned to have a power of attorney drawn in Baruch’s name, send him to Paris, and covertly secure the funds. Calm outwardly though boiling inwardly, Max was as cool as ever; he was a born leader reduced to plotting to preserve his income before a duel.

When Max returned to Rouget’s weeping salon, he pretended surprise at Flore’s departure and questioned Kouski, who said Flore had taken twenty thousand francs and left for Vatan. Rouget, desperate, said Philippe would help find her; Max replied that he did not wish to oppose Bridau. Rouget insisted Max must bring Flore back and promised to do whatever she desired. Max ordered Kouski to make inquiries and report by dessert which road Mademoiselle Brazier had taken. This calmed Rouget temporarily.

Max himself rode through town, feeling keenly the disapproving glances. He resolved to secure the money before the duel: if he died, it would not go to Philippe. He meant the power of attorney to be in Baruch’s name and to have Baruch transfer funds on his order. To Rouget, Max kept up the air of nonchalance and volunteered to accompany the old man to Vatan that night to make up with Flore.

Philippe, informed by Monsieur Hochon of Flore’s departure, rose from dinner and guessed what was intended. If Max and Rouget went to Vatan, Max would likely promise reconciliation to obtain the signature; if Max went ahead to teach Flore a lesson, he might be trapped. Philippe sent Fario to watch the Rouget stable and summon Benjamin; if the carriage went toward Vatan, he would need time to react—Rouget couldn’t sign at night when notaries were asleep. He returned to Mignonnet’s to fetch Carpentier. Hochon, curious, watched Max ride off and then pursued him.

At this time a lad brought a letter for Baruch to the Hochon house. The two Borniche grandsons had behaved penitently since the morning’s events and promised reform, prompted by Madame Hochon’s promise that the debt to Max would be repaid. Baruch, who had the most to lose, advised surrendering to Hochon. Baruch took the letter to old Hochon: it was from Maxence requesting that Baruch be at Vatan to receive a power of attorney from Rouget and offering to send him to Paris with expenses, hinting that Max might leave Issoudun on December third. Hochon rejoiced—Rouget’s property would be saved from “the claws of the devil.” He instructed Baruch to accept the power, go to Vatan and then to Paris, but to stop at Orléans and wait for further orders, to lodge incognito.

But Hochon’s curiosity was piqued when he saw Rouget and Colonel Bridau return together in the carriage, Carpentier and Benjamin following. Hochon found Rouget in his room writing, at Philippe’s dictation, a blunt letter to Flore threatening to revoke his will if she did not return immediately and warning that Gilet could not remain his guest after staying with her at Vatan; he sent the letter by Captain Carpentier. Philippe explained to Hochon that he had intercepted the scheme: his uncle had been about to run into a trap where Flore and Max would get Rouget’s signature and then desert him. Philippe had persuaded Rouget to use the letter to bring Flore back that night. He claimed he would make Flore pliant if Rouget allowed him to replace Max—Flore would adore the man who held her purse by his side.

Hochon urged Rouget to open his eyes and see they were being robbed. Rouget, bewildered and weeping, clung to the hope Philippe offered. The plan worked: the carriage returned at half-past eleven and Rouget’s face changed from agony to exaltation when he saw Flore and Vedie. Philippe helped Flore down and informed Kouski that he was relieved of service; Benjamin would take his place. Philippe seized Flore’s hand and led her out into the place Saint-Jean to browbeat her: either Max would kill him or Philippe would kill Max and take his place; if Philippe survived, Flore would have to comport herself and keep Rouget happy—or be replaced by younger Paris girls Philippe knew. During this lecture Flore trembled.

Rouget, ignoring Hochon’s protests, came out and clasped Flore as a miser clutches his treasure, drew her into his room and shut the door. The Pole Kouski, only pretending to be faithful, had gone to Vatan to warn Max and Flore of the adversary’s move; after fulfilling his mission he left for the hotel de la Poste to join Max. Benjamin took Kouski’s post.

The next morning the town buzzed. The story of Philippe’s intervention spread; everyone wondered what would happen at the banquet between Max and Bridau. Philippe, with few words to Vedie, offered her six hundred francs’ annuity or dismissal to secure her neutrality. Flore, aware that Max’s life might be at risk, became more affectionate toward Rouget than she had been before; her devotion, though self-interested, appeared more agreeable than truth and thus more effective. She came down to breakfast on Rouget’s arm, tearful at the sight of the terrible man who had replaced Max at the table.

Philippe greeted his uncle coldly, and Flore could not endure the idea of his fighting Maxence. Philippe said he didn’t particularly desire to kill Gilet: if Max would leave Issoudun and go to America, Philippe would be the first to recommend it and even outfit him. The colonel argued it was better that Max “kick his legs about” in a free country than rot in a pine box in France; yet he warned that perhaps Max might be a dangerous shot. When Flore asked to speak to Max, Philippe allowed it only as a pretext for Max to collect his belongings and leave; she sent Vedie to the hotel to summon him. Max politely refused to come; he asked Vedie to send his things to the hotel.

Asked to allow Vedie to take his belongings to Max, Rouget consented, but Philippe demanded that if Flore were not back by midday Rouget give him a power of attorney to attend to the property. He told Flore to take Vedie to save appearances and to begin thinking of Rouget’s honor. Flore could obtain nothing from Max: humbled by being routed in public by the imposing colonel, Max was too proud to run away and refused to back down. He insisted on fighting Philippe to restore his honor. The Rabouilleuse proposed running off together to America, but Max, who wanted Flore’s money and not Flore without it, refused; he declared he had killed nine men already and intended to add Bridau to the list, preferring to confront the colonel with the sabre. He boasted confidently that he would run Philippe through.

The town reacted with lively gossip. Some praised Philippe’s conduct as masterly and diplomatic, remembering his performance before the Court of Peers; others deplored the Guard’s fracture: the Guardsmen were being disgraced by internecine quarrels. Captain Potel, indignant at the Guard being opposed to itself and the bourgeoisie watching, lamented that Bridau had set the townspeople on Gilet and that the honor of the Guard was at stake. For most townsfolk the question remained: who would win and what would become of Rouget’s property? Rumors persisted that Gilet had been laying hands on fifty thousand a year.

Throughout Issoudun Philippe’s star rose; the townsfolk offered prayers for the honorable Colonel Bridau, admiring the way he had “rolled together like a couple of thunder-clouds” with Max. The duel seemed inevitable and the victory over Max not only a personal triumph for Philippe but a reclaiming of Rouget’s fortune and honor.

Towards four o’clock the following day the veteran officers of the old army in Issoudun loitered about the place du Marché, in front of Lacroix’s eating-house, expecting Colonel Philippe Bridau. The coronation banquet was to begin at five. Groups exchanged gossip about Max’s disgrace and his expulsion from old Rouget’s house; the officers would dine at Lacroix’s while the common soldiers planned a meeting at a nearby wine-shop. Only Potel and Renard defended Max among the officers.

Max joined the veterans and was greeted with marked silence. Potel and Renard linked arms with him and spoke supportively, but when Philippe arrived in full dress their reception contrasted sharply: Bridau was warmly grasped and congratulated by Mignonnet, Carpentier and others. That contrast ended whatever remaining prudence Flore’s pleadings had awakened in Max.

“We shall fight,” Max told Renard. “And to the death. Don’t talk to me; let me play my part well.”

Potel then led the party into Lacroix’s long dining-hall, and at Philippe’s request the two foes were seated opposite each other. A few town youths lingered outside to watch, each convinced a duel was inevitable. Throughout the meal both men kept an alert cheerfulness. At dessert Philippe proposed the first toast, “To the Grand Army!” and the company answered. Mignonnet claimed a second toast “to those who attempted to restore his son.” All but Maxence Gilet bowed to Philippe. Max rose, bowed ironically to Bridau, and quipped, “May we all meet again at this time next year,” his voice level but charged. There was tense expectation; whispers circulated, but outwardly the conversation remained ordinary and the stillness profound.

Philippe warned the soldiers that the affair was for the Old Guard only; they would obey. The antagonists and their seconds arranged the duel formally: it should not appear a brawl but a gentlemanly combat with proper stakes of honor. At ten that night they agreed on sabres; the rendezvous was set for eight the next morning behind the chancel of the Capuchins’ church. Goddet, a former army surgeon, would attend. The seconds bound the duel to last only ten minutes.

At eleven that night Monsieur and Madame Hochon found Philippe in his rooms; the tearful Madame implored him to say prayers and pressed upon him a relic—a tooth of Sainte Solange mounted on black velvet—to wear for protection. Philippe received it with an affected jest and had Benjamin sew it into his trousers; Benjamin complied for the pay and commission rather than faith. Madame Hochon left comforted.

At eight the next morning, under cloudy skies on the little meadow around the church apse, the duelists and their seconds took their places. Potel and Mignonnet paced off twenty-four feet and attendants marked lines at each end: no man might retreat beyond his line under pain of dishonor. They stripped to shirts; the cold did not make their muscles tremble. Goddet and two soldiers observed their composure.

Just as the signal was given Max caught sight of Fario’s sinister face peering through a hole in the church roof; those eyes, full of hatred and revenge, momentarily dazed him. Philippe adopted the inside guard of a skilled duellist. Max felt inferior. He attempted a moulinet to bewilder Philippe, but at their first clash he discovered Philippe’s wrist iron and spring-like. Where two men were so equal, victory would hinge on a flash of calculation and a single error. On a bad parry by Max, Philippe sent his sabre spinning from his hand.

“Pick it up,” Philippe said; “I am not the man to kill a disarmed enemy.”

That magnanimous phrase, which the onlookers took as deliberate superiority, cooled Max’s nerve. When he resumed position he confronted again Philippe’s raised guard and, determined to redeem his shame, rushed with both hands on his sabre. He managed a slash that cut Philippe’s forehead and part of his face; but Philippe, with a terrible oblique moulinet, cleft Max’s head. Two savage blows ended the combat in the ninth minute. Fario descended to gloat over Max in convulsions; Philippe was carried back to his uncle’s house.

Thus died a man who might have excelled in different circumstances. Nature had given Max courage, calm, and political sagacity, but not the nobility of conduct that makes greatness. Given the perfidy by which he had been brought to disrepute, he was not much regretted. His death ended the exploits of the Order of Idleness to Issoudun’s relief; Philippe’s conduct in the duel was praised, and his victory meant he had little to fear as his bravery was lauded.

Flore Brazier’s position would have been awkward had she not fallen into a grave condition after Max’s death. A brain fever, aggravated by inflammation from her escapade to Vatan, seized her; instead of fleeing the house where Max lay dead and Philippe convalesced, she hovered between life and death for three months, attended by Goddet, who nursed both combatants.

As soon as he could write, Philippe sent letters to shape the narrative and his future. To Monsieur Desroches he reported he had “killed the most venomous of the two reptiles,” noted his own head had been cut, and suggested the remaining “viper” must be made to come to terms because his uncle clung to her. He claimed to have won Goddet and others to his side; Goddet preached resignation and religion to Flore and promised to keep her in bed for three months. Philippe had bought over the cook, who spread insinuations that Max would have ill-treated Flore. “All goes well so far,” he wrote; “my uncle, by old Hochon’s advice, has destroyed his will.”

To Monsieur Giroudeau, he requested a girl—Cesarine, if available—or a ballet-girl who could present as the respectable daughter of a brave soldier and be ready for him; he promised fifty thousand francs if she served his purpose on his uncle’s death. He described the duel and hinted at lively times to come.

Madame Hochon told Agathe Bridau, who rushed to Issoudun. Philippe’s mother, given her maternal tenderness, took heart from the town’s praises.

“After all, my child,” said Madame Hochon, “youth must have its fling. Thanks to your son, Issoudun now breathes and sleeps in peace. He has come to his senses, and if he can be kept from Paris he will end by being a comfort to you.”

Philippe performed saintly attentions toward his mother because he needed her. He did not want to use Cesarine unless Flore remained an object of horror to Mademoiselle Brazier; instead he preferred to keep Flore in place because she was essential to his uncle’s life and thus to the property he coveted. He conceived the idea of making his uncle legally marry Flore, thereby legitimizing her position and keeping the estate within reach. He asked his mother to visit Flore and treat her sisterly.

“Circumstances have compelled me to give you pain,” he told Flore once she recovered sufficiently to receive callers. “It is I who have long advised your marriage with my uncle; if you consent, it will take place as soon as you are recovered. Wealth, respect, and a family position are worth more than what you have lost. You would not have been that fellow’s wife long after my uncle’s death—he meant to get rid of you. Come, my dear, let us understand each other and live happily. You shall be my aunt and nothing more. You will see how well I will have you treated in the marriage contract. Think it over; all sensible people, indeed the whole town, urge you to put an end to your illegal position; no one will blame you for receiving me. By the day of your marriage you will be handsomer than ever. On my honor, if my uncle did not love you so madly, you should be the wife of Colonel Bridau.”

Philippe’s soft talk—calculated kindness—moved her; he even hinted that children would be a means of revenge against Max’s family. In this scene he revealed himself as a manipulator: calculation masked as sentiment, the powerful lever of interest over feeling.

By early April 1823 Rouget’s hall hosted a brilliant dinner for the signing of the marriage contract between Flore Brazier and the old bachelor. Guests included various notables—Mignonnet, Carpentier, Hochon, Goddet, the mayor and curate—Agathe Bridau and Madame Hochon among them. The bride, much impressed by Philippe’s protection and the two ladies’ advocacy, was radiant. The curate was to allow her first communion the next day; the local press hailed the marriage as a revival of religion and a moral correction, printing accounts that lauded Philippe’s supposed disinterestedness.

By the contract Rouget settled on Flore a dower of one hundred thousand francs and a life annuity of thirty thousand. After the wedding Agathe returned to Paris triumphant and wrote the news to Joseph and Desroches. Desroches, skeptical of Philippe, predicted he would keep his hand on the inheritance by artifice; Madame Bridau defended her son’s conduct as statesmanlike.

The morning after the marriage, Philippe took Madame Rouget aside to set terms. He spoke like a man who had turned affection into leverage: now that the law had tied the knot, they must “play above board.” He showed her a letter from the theatrical set—Mariette and friends—which outlined schemes to provide a trained actress to entrap Rouget and secure the property for Philippe, listing the girl’s arts and the circle ready to exploit any fortune, and promising Parisian pleasures. The letter shook Madame Rouget; terror and dependency betrayed her.

“I trust you,” he said, “but I expect some return. I have made you my aunt intending to marry you some day. You are worth more to me than Esther in managing my uncle. In a year from now we must be in Paris. I shall return to the army and become a general, and you will be a great lady. There’s our future; now work for it. But I must have a pledge. You are to give me, within a month, a power of attorney from my uncle, to relieve him of business; a month later, a special power to transfer the income in the Funds. When that stands in my name, you and I have an equal interest in marrying. I can marry my aunt at the end of a year’s widowhood; but I could not marry a disgraced girl.”

He left before she could answer. Vedie, clearing the table, found her mistress pale and trembling; Flore felt as if she had fallen into a pit. The future seemed black; a voice told her she deserved to have him as master. She was helpless.

The married household changed Flore’s condition: she had once had a room of her own as Rouget’s servant-mistress; now Madame Rouget belonged legally to her husband and lost the freedom she had once wielded. In this horrible new constraint she briefly clung to the hope of having a child, only to find that impossible under Philippe’s constant surveillance. Benjamin served as Philippe’s innocent spy; Vedie trembled under his presence. Flore felt deserted and utterly helpless; she feared that if Philippe suspected pregnancy her doom would be sealed. The tone of his voice, the veiled glitter of his gambler’s eye, the soldier’s small civilities masking brutality, made her shudder.

Philippe’s demanded power of attorney he obtained as soon as he wanted it; Flore fell under his dominion as decisively as France had once fallen under Napoleon. Rouget, weakened and dissipating, surrendered his remaining strength. In his decline the nephew remained as cold and impassive as a diplomat in convulsed times: he watched, he planned, he took advantage.

Meanwhile Philippe, now skeptical even of Napoleon II., wrote through intermediaries to the minister of war to offer his services to the King. He explained that Napoleon was gone; having been faithful, he was now free to serve the monarch. He asked for consideration and reinstatement at his rank, professed submission, and assured the minister of his fidelity. He enclosed endorsements from the mayor, the sub-prefect and the commissary of police of Issoudun, praising his conduct and citing newspaper articles about Rouget’s marriage. Two weeks later he received permission and a letter informing him that by order of the King he was, as a preliminary favor, reinstated lieutenant-colonel in the royal army.

Thus Philippe consolidated both domestic and public advantages: he had legally bound Flore into a marriage that put him close to Rouget’s fortune and had secured royal favor and reinstatement—moves that combined family intrigue and political maneuvering to ambitious effect.

Lieutenant-Colonel Philippe Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the feeble Rouget. Three days later he took them to the Treasury, where Jean-Jacques assigned his income to Philippe. Exhausted and bewildered, the old man and the Rabouilleuse were soon plunged by Philippe into the dissipations of actresses, journalists, artists, and dubious women among whom Philippe had wasted his youth. Rouget’s extravagant amusements, incited by Giroudeau, ended in death after a brilliant supper at Florentine’s, where Lolotte, an Opera dancer urged on by Giroudeau, served as his amiable assassin; the death was publicly ascribed to indigestion. Madame Rouget adapted to scandalous society but, placed under Mariette’s supervision by Philippe, avoided gross excesses.

In October 1823 Philippe returned to Issoudun with a power of attorney from his aunt to settle his uncle’s estate. By March 1824 he was back in Paris with sixteen hundred thousand francs—the net proceeds of Rouget’s property, excluding the paintings still with Monsieur Hochon. On Hochon’s assurance of solidity, Philippe deposited the funds in Mongenod and Sons at six percent with three months’ notice. He visited his mother and arranged his marriage, witnessed by Giroudeau, Finot, Nathan, and Bixiou. By the contract the widow Rouget’s million-franc portion would belong to Philippe should she die childless.

Philippe married discreetly but with plans. He lodged his wife in a ready-furnished rue Saint-Georges apartment bought from Lolotte—she thought it delightful though he rarely visited. Secretly he bought a magnificent hotel in the rue de Clichy for 250,000 francs, paying 150,000 down and taking two years to finish the purchase, and lavished his income on its renovation and furnishings, including restored pictures then valued at about 300,000 francs. Effectively, he invested two years’ income in establishing the appearance of a great lord.

The accession of Charles X favored the Duc de Chaulieu’s family, whose eldest son, the Duc de Rhetore, frequented Tullia’s salon where Philippe was known. The elder branch, confident under Charles X, sought to draw former Imperial soldiers into its orbit; Philippe, likely useful for revealing conspiracies of 1820 and 1822, was made lieutenant-colonel in the Duke of Maufrigneuse’s regiment. The appointment—partly a reward, partly a fashioning of the Dauphin’s circle with a hint of liberalism—placed Philippe as a sort of equerry and introduced him to the Dauphin and Dauphine, who favored brusque ex-soldiers. Philippe adopted the pose required by court life and leveraged his new position to be aid-de-camp to a marshal of influence.

In January 1827 Philippe, now lieutenant-colonel in the Royal Guard, sought ennoblement. Nobility under the Restoration was granted to “roturiers” serving in the Guard; Colonel Bridau bought the estate of Brambourg and sought to entail it under a countship. The favor granted, he adopted all the trappings of rank—horses, carriages, livery—and frequented the Comte de Soulanges. Ambitious and backed by influential mistresses, he aimed to be one of the Dauphin’s aides-de-camp and married into the higher circles. He played the sycophant role expertly, copied the respectability he had once admired in others, excluded old companions whose past might compromise him, and refused to intercede for Giroudeau despite their shared complicity in past crimes. He was unrelenting to those whose vulgarity he feared might stain his new position.

Philippe hoped to marry Amélie de Soulanges and rise to command a Guard regiment. To be free for such a match, he dismissed his wife to a slow death: he told the Duke of Maufrigneuse that she was a cook by taste and was ruined by drink, implying he expected to inherit her fortune. Yet the marriage contract, and the million francs, were in Mongenod and Sons; he counted on a million and the entailed Brambourg estate to secure his path to higher rank. He collected decorations—Commander of the Legion of Honor and Saint Louis—and paraded at court in splendid uniforms, indifferent to the family he had abandoned.

Meanwhile, Agathe Bridau, his mother and Joseph’s guardian, prospered modestly: after the Salon of 1823 a leading painter got her a lottery-office near the Markets, later exchanged for an office in the rue de Seine above Joseph’s atelier. She hired an agent and no longer imposed on Joseph financially, though she doubted his artistic fame and resented his constant struggles. Joseph, a great painter whose genius made extraordinary demands, earned too little to meet the social and material expectations imposed by his relations and artistic circle. Despite the support of friends and some recognition—decoration after the Salon of 1827—he received few commissions; his imaginative tendencies produced inequality that critics used to deny him. Agathe compared this unstable career with Philippe’s apparent success and favored Philippe in her pride: a parade of public honors made his earlier misery vanish from her mind; she felt supported and proud to have such a son.

Yet Joseph’s debts and need troubled Agathe, who, though contemptuous of his profession, resolved to help him. She kept house with the lottery-office’s proceeds and expected Philippe, now rich and fashionable, to assist. After three years of hoping for his visits, she wrote to him asking for money for Joseph:

  My dear Philippe,—You have not given the least little word of remembrance to your mother for five years... Joseph is in need of money, and you are floating in wealth; you now possess, all to yourself, the property of my brother... Come and see Joseph; slip into the skull a few thousand-franc notes. Philippe, you owe them to us.

Two days later a brief, brutal reply arrived:

  A man does not marry a Mademoiselle Amelie de Soulanges without the purse of Fortunatus... Your son, Philippe Bridau.

The letter shattered Agathe. Joseph found her collapsed, sent for Bianchon, and when she revived she revealed the exchange. Joseph, stunned, did not speak of Philippe during Agathe’s three-week decline. Bianchon, bluntly honest, told Joseph that at their mother’s age and condition the hope was only to ease death. Agathe, sensing a moral failure, asked for Abbé Loraux, her confessor. Alone with him she confessed confusion and sorrow: she had lavished her pride and tenderness on the eldest son and misunderstood the true difference between her boys. The priest, gently but firmly, told her that she had misdirected her love, prized the ambitious son, and neglected the one who had always served her faithfully; this realization prodded her to repentance.

Joseph, who had been out borrowing money to pay urgent bills, returned and found his mother asking forgiveness, saying she had not loved him as deserved. Joseph answered with tears and tenderness, and Agathe, consoled by his forgiveness, found peace. For two weeks, each exchange of affection seemed to sum a lifetime; friends—D’Arthez, Michel Chrestien, Fulgence Ridal, Pierre Grassou, and Bianchon—kept Joseph company. Joseph discerned in his mother’s moments an unspoken wish to see Philippe before dying. He implored Bixiou, who moved in circles still frequented by Philippe, to persuade the count to visit, at least to feign affection for a few hours.

Bixiou undertook the errand. Philippe, ensconced in a bedroom hung with yellow damask, laughed at the request. He declared the woman whose death would release him from a burden should die quickly; her presence at his marriage would be awkward. He bragged of being a parvenu and said that his son must be more fortunate; if his past were exposed he would be ashamed of it. He refused to play a part in comforting his mother for appearances’ sake. When Bixiou related this to Joseph, the painter was frozen with despair.

The day after Bixiou’s visit, Agathe asked whether Philippe knew of her illness; Joseph suffocated, left, and later found that the Abbé Loraux’s gentle remark to Agathe—“Alas! my child, you have never had but one son”—had pierced her. She died twenty hours later, murmuring in delirium, “Whom does Philippe take after?” Joseph forbade Philippe’s attendance at the funeral, having written a note accusing him of murder by letter and instructing him to feign illness—he would not suffer the “assassin” at his mother’s side.

Grief emptied Joseph of the will to paint. Two weeks after the funeral a letter arrived for Joseph from a woman calling herself Comtesse Flore de Brambourg—the count’s estranged wife—asking for help. The desperate appeal enraged Joseph: he saw in it further proof of Philippe’s villainy. Bixiou and others urged inquiry; Joseph and his friends summoned the woman’s messenger, an impoverished nurse named Madame Gruget, who explained that the countess had been abandoned, could not pay rent, owed debts for sixty days’ nursing, and was threatened with eviction. Flore, once beautiful, now lived in the attic in the rue du Houssay, ravaged by disease and destitution. Joseph, Bianchon, Bixiou, and Desroches hurried to see her, bringing what money Joseph had left.

At the attic they found a skeleton of a woman—green, nearly hairless, with hollow eyes and a once-graceful frame reduced to bone. Flore wept on seeing Joseph and declared Philippe guilty: she believed he had driven her to ruin and caused Max’s death who had advised her towards extremes. Bianchon, moved to act, proposed transferring her to Despleins’ care and then to Dubois’s hospital for operation and treatment; Desroches vowed to summon Philippe for restoration of the conjugal home after her recovery. They quickly removed Flore to Dubois’s private hospital. Bianchon hoped surgery could save her from a disease aggravated by alcoholism and neglect. After an operation and three weeks of care, however, the patient died—more of exhaustion from poverty and starvation than from surgical failure.

Soon after Flore’s death Philippe, in conspicuous deep mourning, called on the Comte de Soulanges to inform him of his loss. Gossip tied Philippe to a possible match with a daughter of Soulanges, seeing his grief as a matter of form. Rumors spread in fashionable circles that the count would wed advantageously and rise further. At the Rocher de Cancale, De Marsay told Rastignac of rumors that Philippe would win a rich match and promotion. But Bixiou, who had been insulted by Philippe’s earlier disdain (at the hotel de Brambourg Philippe had told him he might visit only once he held office), had set himself to vengeance: he disguised himself and, pretending to be an emissary, told the Comte de Soulanges the sordid truth he had heard from Bianchon—Flore’s abandonment, Agathe’s death triggered by Philippe’s cruelty, old Rouget’s suspicious demise, and other scandals. His revelations unsettled Soulanges.

Philippe continued to keep up appearances and gave a supper to du Tillet, Nucingen, Eugène de Rastignac, Maxime de Trailles, and Henri de Marsay. At it he boasted of his fortunes—claiming three million francs besides his house and entailed estate—and plotted financial moves. Du Tillet and Nucingen, bankers central in political and financial circles, pretended to do business with him but harbored other plans. In July 1830 they helped him make a profit of fifteen hundred thousand francs—encouraging his faith in the Restoration and tempting him to risk heavily on the presumed triumph of the Ordonnances. Unbeknownst to Philippe, both men were playing a different game: confident of revolutionary stirrings, they secretly played against him and orchestrated his ruin. Philippe, who had backed the Restoration and scorned civilians, bet all on a return he believed secure. When the ordonnances drove open the July crisis, he fought as if with all his fortune at stake, but he was saved from death at the barricades only because he had been ordered to Saint-Cloud with the Duc de Maufrigneuse on July 25th; otherwise, he would have been in the streets and likely murdered by Giroudeau’s forces.

Within a month the Comte de Brambourg’s vast fortune had melted away, leaving only house, furniture, estate, and the pictures from Issoudun. He remained loyal to the Restoration’s elder branch until 1834, then re-entered the military service from jealousy and ambition. In 1835 he obtained a command in Algeria and remained three arduous years, always craving general’s epaulets. There he grew brutal and excessively severe, disliked despite personal bravery reminiscent of Murat. In early 1839, during a desperate retreat, he rashly charged Arabs with a single company, became isolated, and fell amid a murderous mêlée. Surrounded and wounded, he cried “Your colonel! to me! a colonel of the Empire!” but his men could not rescue him. The Arabs hacked him to pieces with scimitars and cut off his head.

Joseph, married about that time through Comte de Serizy to the daughter of a wealthy farmer, inherited Philippe’s Paris house and the Brambourg estate per the entail—something Philippe might have tried to prevent had he foreseen it. Joseph’s chief pleasure in the inheritance was the celebrated collection of pictures from Issoudun. He acquired an income of some sixty thousand francs, while his father-in-law continued to support him financially. Although Joseph painted magnificent works and rendered important services to artists, he had not yet gained membership in the Institute. Because of the entail’s clause, he now bore the title Comte de Brambourg—an ironic prestige that often made him laugh among his atelier friends.

Many characters in this tale recur throughout Balzac’s Human Comedy. Among those who appear in other novels are:

He also narrated Another Study of Woman and La Grande Bretèche.

- Cesar Birotteau — Cesar Birotteau; At the Sign of the Cat and Racket.

- Jean-Jacques Bixiou — The Purse; The Government Clerks; Modeste Mignon; Scenes from a Courtesan’s Life; The Firm of Nucingen; The Muse of the Department; Cousin Betty; The Member for Arcis; Beatrix; A Man of Business; Gaudissart II.; The Unconscious Humorists; Cousin Pons.

- Comte de Brambourg (title of Philippe Bridau, later Joseph) — appears in The Unconscious Humorists.

- Philippe Bridau — Scenes from a Courtesan’s Life.

- Joseph Bridau — The Purse; A Distinguished Provincial at Paris; A Start in Life; Modeste Mignon; Another Study of Woman; Pierre Grassou; Letters of Two Brides; Cousin Betty; The Member for Arcis.

- Giroudeau — A Distinguished Provincial at Paris; A Start in Life.

- Lousteau (Étienne) — A Distinguished Provincial at Paris; Scenes from a Courtesan’s Life; A Daughter of Eve; Beatrix; The Muse of the Department; Cousin Betty; A Prince of Bohemia; A Man of Business; The Middle Classes; The Unconscious Humorists.

- Desplein and Desroches — The Atheist’s Mass; Cousin Pons; Lost Illusions; The Thirteen; The Government Clerks; Pierrette; The Seamy Side of History; Modeste Mignon; Scenes from a Courtesan’s Life; Colonel Chabert; A Start in Life; A Woman of Thirty; The Commission in Lunacy; The Government Clerks; A Distinguished Provincial at Paris; The Firm of Nucingen; A Man of Business; The Middle Classes.
