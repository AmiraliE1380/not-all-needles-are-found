Bianchon, a physician renowned for his theoretical physiology, initially trained in surgery under the illustrious Desplein, a celebrated French surgeon. Desplein's genius, while acknowledged, was ultimately personal and did not extend to a legacy that would influence future generations. His remarkable ability to diagnose and operate was coupled with a strong atheism, shaped by his experiences dissecting the human body without ever encountering a soul, which he believed was absent from the physical form.

Despite his brilliance, Desplein's life was marked by contradictions that his detractors seized upon to undermine his reputation. His eccentricities, such as fluctuating between lavish and indifferent dress, and his complex personality, which oscillated between kindness and harshness, led to mixed judgments about him. He was a man of profound insight, capable of understanding human nature deeply, yet he often displayed a disdain for humanity, having observed its follies closely.

Among his students, Horace Bianchon stood out, having endured poverty while studying medicine. Bianchon was characterized by his integrity, loyalty, and a cheerful disposition despite his financial struggles. His relationship with Desplein blossomed as the surgeon recognized Bianchon's potential, taking him under his wing and introducing him to the intricacies of medical practice and the social dynamics of Paris.

One day, Bianchon informed Desplein about a destitute water-carrier suffering from a debilitating illness. Desplein, moved by compassion, rushed to help the man, demonstrating a softer side to his otherwise stern demeanor. This act of kindness was not an isolated incident; Desplein had a particular fondness for helping the less fortunate, especially those from his native Auvergne.

Bianchon later witnessed Desplein entering a church, which surprised him given the surgeon's outspoken atheism. Curious, he followed Desplein and observed him attending mass, an act that seemed contradictory to his beliefs. During a dinner conversation, Desplein dismissed the mass as a farce, yet Bianchon remained puzzled by his master's actions.

Months later, another physician questioned Desplein about his church visit, to which Desplein provided a seemingly innocuous explanation. Bianchon, however, remained skeptical and decided to investigate further. He returned to the church on the same day the following year and found Desplein attending mass again, confirming a pattern. The sacristan revealed that Desplein had been attending this mass regularly for twenty years and had even founded it.

This revelation deepened the mystery surrounding Desplein's beliefs and actions, suggesting a complexity in his character that defied simple categorization. Bianchon was left to ponder the implications of a man who, while professing atheism, engaged in rituals that seemed to contradict his convictions, highlighting the intricate relationship between belief, practice, and the human experience.

Some time passed before Doctor Bianchon, despite their friendship, found a moment to discuss a significant incident in Desplein's life. Their meetings, whether in consultation or social settings, rarely allowed for private conversation. Finally, seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, Bianchon spotted Desplein entering Saint-Sulpice and knelt beside him without surprise. They attended a mass together.

"Can you explain your sudden piety?" Bianchon asked as they left the church. "You, who do not believe in God, attending mass? You owe me an explanation."

"I am like many devout people—outwardly religious but inwardly skeptical," Desplein replied, launching into a critique of certain political figures.

"That doesn't answer my question," Bianchon insisted. "Why did you found this mass?"

"Well, my dear boy," Desplein said, "I am nearing the end of my life; I can share the beginnings of it."

They stood in the Rue des Quatre-Vents, a grim street in Paris. Desplein gestured to a building where he had lived for two years. "I lived up there," he said, recalling the hardships of his early life. "I endured hunger, poverty, and isolation. I had no money for books or medical training, and my irritable nature made it hard to connect with others. I was alone, struggling to rise from the depths of poverty."

Desplein recounted his meager meals and the sacrifices he made to pursue his studies. "I worked tirelessly, often without sympathy or support. I had no friends to share my burdens, and I felt the weight of my circumstances deeply."

He expressed frustration at those born into wealth who could not understand his struggles. "They ask why I got into debt, as if it were a choice. They have no idea what it means to be truly destitute."

Desplein continued, explaining how his early experiences shaped his view of humanity. "If I seem hard or bitter, it's because I've witnessed the selfishness and envy of those around me. In Paris, mediocrity often seeks to undermine those who strive for greatness."

He shared a pivotal moment when he faced extreme poverty and considered enlisting in the military. He had hoped for a box of linen from a relative, but when it arrived, he found it had cost too much for the porter to release it. Despairing, he encountered his neighbor, Bourgeat, a water-carrier who offered to help.

Bourgeat, despite his own struggles, paid Desplein's debts and helped him find new lodgings. "He devoted his savings to my future," Desplein said, visibly moved. "He understood my mission and supported me selflessly."

Desplein described Bourgeat as a nurturing figure, who cared for him like a mother. "He took pride in my success and never sought recognition for his sacrifices. When I became a house surgeon, I felt a deep pain leaving him behind, but he was proud of my achievements."

Desplein eventually repaid Bourgeat by buying him a horse and barrel, fulfilling a dream Bourgeat had cherished. "He was furious I had spent my money on him, yet he was delighted. He never claimed credit for my success, even though I owed him everything."

When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein dedicated himself to his care, but ultimately, Bourgeat succumbed. "He died in my arms, leaving me everything he had. He had a strong faith, and I honored his wishes by having masses said for him after his death."

Desplein concluded, "I can only offer this as a tribute to Bourgeat's faith. I hope that if there is a paradise, he finds peace there. I would give anything for the faith he had."

Bianchon, who witnessed Desplein's last days, could not definitively say whether the great surgeon died an atheist. He pondered whether Bourgeat might have opened the gates of Heaven for Desplein, just as he had opened the doors to the earthly temple they had shared.

PARIS, January 1836.
