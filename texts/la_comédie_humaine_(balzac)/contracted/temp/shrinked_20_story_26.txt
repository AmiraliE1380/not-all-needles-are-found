Bianchon, a physician known for his theoretical physiology, first trained in surgery under the celebrated French surgeon Desplein. Desplein’s diagnostic and operative skill was extraordinary, yet his genius remained personal rather than institutional. His atheism—strengthened by dissecting bodies and perceiving no soul—was a central part of his outlook.

Critics used his contradictions to diminish him: eccentric habits of lavish then indifferent dress, a temperament swinging from kindness to harshness, and a contemptuous attitude born of close observation of human folly. Among his students, Horace Bianchon stood out for enduring poverty to study medicine; noted for integrity, loyalty, and cheerfulness, he won Desplein’s recognition and was introduced to medical practice and Parisian society by his mentor.

When Bianchon told Desplein about a destitute water-carrier with a crippling illness, Desplein rushed to help, revealing a compassionate side beneath his stern exterior and a recurring sympathy for the less fortunate, especially fellow Auvergnats.

Bianchon later saw Desplein enter a church—a surprising sight given the surgeon’s outspoken unbelief. Curious, he followed and observed Desplein at mass; at a subsequent dinner Desplein dismissed the service as a farce, leaving Bianchon puzzled. Months later, after another physician questioned Desplein, Bianchon resolved to investigate. Returning a year later, he again found Desplein at mass; the sacristan disclosed that Desplein had attended that service regularly for twenty years and had even founded it.

The discovery intensified the mystery of Desplein’s beliefs and behavior: a man who publicly professed atheism yet privately participated in religious ritual. These episodes reveal a multifaceted character—skillful, skeptical, compassionate—whose public declarations and private practices resist simple labels and highlight the complex relationship between belief, practice, and human contradiction.

Some time passed before Doctor Bianchon, despite their friendship, found an opportunity to discuss a significant incident in Desplein's life. Their meetings, whether professional or social, rarely allowed privacy. Finally, seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, Bianchon saw Desplein entering Saint-Sulpice and knelt beside him without surprise. They attended mass together.

"Can you explain your sudden piety?" Bianchon asked as they left the church. "You, who do not believe in God, attending mass? You owe me an explanation."

"I am like many devout people—outwardly religious but inwardly skeptical," Desplein replied, then launched into a critique of certain political figures.

"That doesn't answer my question," Bianchon insisted. "Why did you found this mass?"

"Well, my dear boy," Desplein said, "I am nearing the end of my life; I can tell you its beginnings."

They stood in the Rue des Quatre-Vents, a grim Paris street. Desplein pointed to a building where he had lived for two years. "I lived up there," he said, recalling the hardships of his early life. He had endured hunger, poverty, and isolation, with no money for books or medical training, and an irritable nature that made it hard to connect with others. He was alone, struggling to rise from the depths of poverty.

He recounted meager meals and the sacrifices he made for study. He worked tirelessly, often without sympathy or support, with no friends to share his burdens and feeling the full weight of his circumstances.

He expressed frustration at those born into wealth who could not grasp his struggles. "They ask why I got into debt, as if it were a choice. They have no idea what it means to be truly destitute."

Desplein explained how early experiences shaped his view of humanity. "If I seem hard or bitter, it's because I've witnessed the selfishness and envy around me. In Paris, mediocrity often seeks to undermine those who strive for excellence."

He recalled a pivotal moment when he faced utter poverty and considered enlisting. He had hoped for a box of linen from a relative, but when it arrived the porter demanded an exorbitant fee to release it. Despairing, he met his neighbor, Bourgeat, a water-carrier who offered help.

Bourgeat, despite his own hardships, paid Desplein's debts and helped him find new lodgings. "He devoted his savings to my future," Desplein said, visibly moved. "He understood my mission and supported me selflessly."

Desplein described Bourgeat as a nurturing figure who cared for him like a mother. "He took pride in my success and never sought recognition for his sacrifices. When I became a house surgeon, I suffered leaving him, but he was proud of me."

Desplein later repaid Bourgeat by buying him a horse and barrel, fulfilling a long-held dream. "He was furious I had spent my money on him, yet he was delighted. He never claimed credit for my success, though I owed him everything."

When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein dedicated himself to his care, but Bourgeat died in his arms. "He left me everything he had. He had a strong faith, and I honored his wishes by having masses said for him after his death."

Desplein concluded, "I can only offer this as a tribute to Bourgeat's faith. I hope that if there is a paradise, he finds peace there. I would give anything for the faith he had."

Bianchon, who had witnessed Desplein's last days, could not definitively say whether the great surgeon died an atheist. He pondered whether Bourgeat might have opened the gates of Heaven for Desplein, just as he had opened the doors to the earthly temple they had shared.

PARIS, January 1836.
