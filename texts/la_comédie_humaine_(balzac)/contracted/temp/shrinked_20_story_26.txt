**The Atheist's Mass**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Clara Bell*  

**Dedicated to Auguste Borget by his friend De Balzac**

**Summary:**

Bianchon, a prominent Parisian physician, trained under the celebrated but ultimately fleeting genius Desplein. Despite his acclaim, Desplein struggled to leave a lasting legacy and embraced atheism, feeling isolated in his beliefs. He viewed the world skeptically, rejecting the existence of a soul, which he never found in his dissections.

Desplein's life was marked by contradictions that his critics exploited. His eccentricities, such as his varying dress style and complex personality, led to mixed judgments. He was both generous and miserly, often expressing disdain for humanity after observing their behaviors.

Among his students, Bianchon stood out, having faced poverty while studying medicine. Known for his integrity and selflessness, he was a loyal friend. Desplein recognized Bianchon's potential and mentored him in medical practice and Parisian society.

Bianchon was surprised when he saw Desplein enter a church, given his staunch atheism. This incident sparked Bianchon's curiosity about Desplein's beliefs. During a dinner conversation, Desplein dismissed the significance of the mass, criticizing its historical implications and the bloodshed associated with its establishment.

Months later, when another physician asked about Desplein's church visit, he offered a vague excuse. Intrigued, Bianchon decided to observe Desplein's behavior. The following year, he confirmed Desplein's attendance at mass again, discovering that Desplein had founded the very service he attended.

This revelation deepened the mystery surrounding Desplein, highlighting the complexity of a man who, despite his atheistic convictions, engaged in rituals that contradicted his beliefs, leaving Bianchon pondering the enigmatic nature of Desplein's actions.

Doctor Bianchon took seven years to recount a pivotal incident in his friend Desplein's life. After the Revolution of 1830, amidst the chaos in Paris, Bianchon saw Desplein entering Saint-Sulpice and joined him for mass. Intrigued by Desplein's attendance, given his atheism, Bianchon sought an explanation.

Desplein shared that he had faced extreme poverty in his early years, living in a dilapidated building where hunger and isolation plagued him as he struggled to finance his medical education. He described his life as a relentless battle against despair, lacking support from family or friends. His meals were often limited to stale bread soaked in milk, and he frequently lacked proper clothing or companionship.

Despite these hardships, Desplein was determined to succeed. He immersed himself in his studies, driven by the desire to escape his dire circumstances. He felt misunderstood by those who had never experienced true poverty, likening their ignorance to a princess suggesting the hungry should simply buy cake.

Desplein's bitterness arose from witnessing the selfishness and envy of others in the competitive Parisian environment. He expressed deep mistrust of humanity, feeling that mediocrity constantly undermined those striving for greatness.

During this challenging time, Desplein faced eviction due to unpaid rent. His neighbor, Bourgeat, a humble water-carrier, offered assistance by paying Desplein's debts and sharing his aspirations of buying a horse and barrel for work. In return, Bourgeat supported Desplein's education, providing food and encouragement, treating him like a son.

Their bond deepened over time, with Bourgeat taking on domestic responsibilities and nurturing Desplein's ambitions. When Desplein became a house surgeon, he felt a profound loss leaving Bourgeat behind but remained committed to repaying his kindness.

When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein dedicated himself to his care, trying every means to save him. Despite his efforts, Bourgeat passed away, leaving Desplein heartbroken. He honored Bourgeat's wishes by arranging masses for his soul, reflecting on the man's unwavering faith and love.

Desplein's relationship with Bourgeat profoundly impacted his life, leading him to reconcile skepticism with gratitude. In the end, Bianchon, who witnessed Desplein's final days, pondered whether Bourgeat's faith might have opened the gates of Heaven for his friend, highlighting themes of sacrifice, gratitude, and the complexities of belief.
