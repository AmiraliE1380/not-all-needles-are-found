Dedication  
To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati. This work thanks you for the pleasure your conversation gave a traveler recalling Paris. Your daughter Eugénie embodies womanly gifts, unlike the Eugénie here, who suffered a rigid upbringing. I recall our time in Milan and offer this book as a token of friendship.

Chapter I: The Two Maries  
In a Parisian boudoir, sisters Madame du Tillet and Madame Félix de Vandenesse are distraught. Reared by strict parents, they entered loveless marriages without understanding love. Madame du Tillet feels confined in her marriage to banker Ferdinand du Tillet; Madame de Vandenesse, though more affectionately matched with Comte Félix, remains unfulfilled. Their naive upbringing leaves them yearning for deeper fulfillment.

Chapter II: A Confidence Between Sisters  
Madame de Vandenesse confides her unhappiness, lamenting the emptiness beneath her luxuries and the suffocating social expectations. Madame du Tillet, despite her own troubles, comforts her, and both mourn the absence of genuine love.

Chapter III: The History of a Fortunate Woman  
Comte Félix marries her hoping to give a loving home. At first content, she soon finds life monotonous and discovers desires her marriage does not satisfy, complicating her feelings.

Chapter IV: A Celebrated Man  
Raoul Nathan, an ascending literary figure, attracts Madame de Vandenesse. Their exchanges brim with tension and attraction. Nathan is torn between artistic ambitions and his relationship with Florine, an actress who has supported him.

Chapter V: Florine  
Florine, a talented actress dependent on Nathan, grows possessive. As Nathan’s ambitions and his involvement with Madame de Vandenesse increase, jealousy strains their partnership.

Chapter VI: Romantic Love  
Madame de Vandenesse and Nathan fall into a secret, passionate affair. She is captivated by his artistic spirit; he by her innocence. Their love deepens in private, while social pressure and moral constraints complicate and endanger it.

Chapter VII: Suicide  
Crushed by debt and despair, Nathan contemplates suicide. The countess, at first unaware of his plight, nurtures her feelings; when she discovers his desperation she rushes to save him, revealing the intensity of her attachment.

Chapter VIII: A Lover Saved and Lost  
The countess seeks financial aid for Nathan, enduring societal judgment and inner turmoil. Despite her efforts his situation remains precarious, testing her devotion and forcing her to confront their relationship’s consequences.

Chapter IX: The Husband’s Triumph  
The count, aware of the bond, acts to protect his family’s honor while trying to understand it. The countess must face her feelings, reconcile duties to her husband, and navigate societal expectations—decisions that will determine their futures.

Addendum  
Characters from A Daughter of Eve recur across Balzac’s works, reflecting his interconnected literary universe. Their journeys probe love, ambition, and social constraint in 19th-century France.
