DEDICATION

To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati.

Madame, remembering the joy your conversation gave a traveler in Milan, I lay this work at your feet, hoping you will protect it with your name as you once shielded the tales of a beloved writer. You have a daughter, Eugenie, whose beauty and intelligence promise a joyful childhood, unlike the rigid upbringing of the Eugenie in these pages. While Frenchmen are often thought fickle, I remain Italian in my constancy and memory. Writing “Eugenie” recalls the laughter and warmth of our time in Milan; I now regard you as a distant, treasured masterpiece.

Your devoted servant,
De Balzac.

A DAUGHTER OF EVE

CHAPTER I. THE TWO MARIES

In a luxurious boudoir in the rue Neuve-des-Mathurins, two young women sat weeping before a fireplace. The room, hung in blue velvet and furnished with elegance, belonged to Ferdinand du Tillet, a wealthy banker who had married the daughter of a prominent magistrate. Despite his fortune, du Tillet lacked true social standing, having married into the aristocracy for a dowry.

Madame du Tillet, holding her sister Madame Félix de Vandenesse’s hand, listened to intimate confessions. The two sisters, raised by a strict, pious mother, had married without ever enjoying the freedoms of youth. Their upbringing was so severe that they entered marriage utterly naive about life and love.

They shared a deep bond forged in a repressive household where their mother’s control stifled their spirits. Forbidden the wider world, their education limited to religion and needlework, they retained innocence at the cost of understanding life’s complexities. As they confided, they reflected on the oppressive atmosphere and how marriage had seemed their only escape, unaware of the challenges awaiting them.

CHAPTER II. A CONFIDENCE BETWEEN SISTERS

Madame de Vandenesse sought solace in her sister’s company, overwhelmed by marital troubles. She confessed despair over a husband seemingly indifferent to her happiness. Madame du Tillet, feeling helpless, listened as her sister lamented. Their sisterly love was evident, transcending their husbands’ indifference.

Madame du Tillet revealed her own marriage’s emptiness, describing her husband’s coldness and the flatness of her life, contrasting it with their shared warmth. They both voiced desires for passion and excitement, recognizing the limits society and marriage imposed. Each encouraged the other to seek happiness within those constraints.

CHAPTER III. THE HISTORY OF A FORTUNATE WOMAN

Félix de Vandenesse, of noble lineage, had married Madame de Vandenesse hoping to recreate an idealized love. He had experienced love in its purest form and expected marriage to mirror that happiness. Over time, however, the constraints of society and duty stifled their relationship.

Madame de Vandenesse, initially content, began to feel the monotony and lack of passion. She longed for excitement and adventure, feeling confined by a life that had become predictable. Her husband, kind and attentive, could not satisfy her craving for deeper emotional connection, and she grew aware of the allure of romance outside marriage.

CHAPTER IV. A CELEBRATED MAN

Raoul Nathan, an emerging literary figure, entered society with talent and audacity. Known for sharp wit and caustic humor, he both charmed and alienated. His unconventional appearance and manner set him apart, making him a captivating presence to women.

At a gathering hosted by Madame de Montcornet, Nathan attracted Madame de Vandenesse’s notice. Their first interactions carried unspoken attraction, a mutual magnetic pull. As Nathan managed a complex career and personal life, he became increasingly drawn to her, seeing her as a muse for his creative work. Yet society’s norms and their circumstances threatened their budding connection.

CHAPTER V. FLORINE

Florine, a talented actress and Nathan’s long-time companion, embodied another side of his life. Their relationship mixed affection and practicality: she supported his spirits and career while relying on him for her own success. Their bond was complicated, both navigating public demands and private needs.

As Nathan’s ambitions grew, he found himself torn between loyalty to Florine and growing feelings for Madame de Vandenesse. The actress, aware of his infatuation, felt threatened by the countess and the possible shift in Nathan’s affections.

CHAPTER VI. ROMANTIC LOVE

Madame de Vandenesse and Nathan’s relationship deepened, fueled by shared passion for art and literature. Their encounters grew more intimate, and both felt exhilarated by their connection. But societal expectations and their roles cast long shadows, creating tension.

Madame de Vandenesse wrestled with guilt and desire, torn between loyalty to her husband and attraction to Nathan. The emotional turmoil intensified her longing for a life of passion, even as she feared the consequences.

CHAPTER VII. SUICIDE

Nathan, under mounting pressure in career and life, sank into despair. The weight of ambition and fear of failure drove him to contemplate suicide. A chance encounter with Madame de Vandenesse, however, rekindled his will to live.

Seeing his desperation, the countess resolved to help. She recognized the depth of his struggle and sought to encourage him, offering protection from his darkest impulses and from the destructive side of his ambitions.

CHAPTER VIII. A LOVER SAVED AND LOST

After Nathan’s near-suicide, Madame de Vandenesse became more involved in his life, balancing protection and her own feelings. Their relationship became a source of strength and turmoil as they faced the moral and practical realities of love amid social scrutiny.

As Nathan’s work began to flourish, he remained torn between Florine’s practical attachment and the countess’s inspiring influence. Florine, feeling endangered, grew increasingly possessive, heightening tensions among the three.

CHAPTER IX. THE HUSBAND’S TRIUMPH

Félix de Vandenesse, aware of his wife’s attachment to Nathan, acted to preserve his marriage. Determined to uncover the truth and shield her from scandal, he sought to protect her honor and well-being. His resolve to defend their union showed his love and commitment.

The story follows characters entangled by love, ambition, and societal expectation, leading to a dramatic conclusion that highlights the conflicts of desire and duty.
