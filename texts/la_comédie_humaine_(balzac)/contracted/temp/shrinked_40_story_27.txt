The commercial traveller, unknown to antiquity, is one of the striking figures of our age. He marks a transition from an epoch of individual creative power to one of universal, levelling influence—equalizing products, spreading them among the masses, and governed by the principle of unity, the final expression of modern social organization. We seem to pass from the saturnalia of popular thought to a dead level in which civilizations’ treasures are concentrated and redistributed.

He is to ideas what stage-coaches are to people and goods: their vehicle. From the luminous centre he takes a handful of light and scatters it among drowsier regions. This human pyrotechnic is a scholar without learning, a juggler deceived by his own tricks, an unbelieving priest who expounds mysteries better for his lack of faith. Curious being: he has seen everything, claims all the ways of the world, soaked in Parisian vices yet affecting provincial familiarity. He links village and capital—neither Parisian nor provincial, but a traveller. He sees nothing to the core; he knows men and places by name, surveys things at the surface, measures with a little tape-line. His glance skims all and penetrates none. Busy with much, he is occupied by nothing truly profound.

A jester and bon vivant, he courts every political opinion and professes patriotism to the marrow. A master mimic, he can switch smiles of persuasion, satisfaction, and good-humour and drop them to reveal his ordinary face. In his trade he must be a certain observer: probing men with a glance to guess habits, wants, and, above all, solvency. To economize time he makes quick decisions about his chances—practice that turns him into a shrewd judge; on that basis he sets up as critic of theatres, discoursing of both Parisian and provincial stages.

He knows all the haunts of France “de actu et visu.” He can pilot you equally to vice or virtue. With an eloquence like a hot-water spigot, he can open or check a stream of phrases kept on tap, producing upon his victims a moral shower-bath. Loquacious as a cricket, he smokes, drinks, wears trinkets, overawes the common people, passes for a lord in villages, and never allows himself to be “stumped”—a bit of his own slang. He knows how to slap his pockets at the right moment and make money jingle if he fears suspicious servants think him a thief. Activity is among his most astonishing qualities: no hawk, stag, or hound can rival his rapid dart for a commission, his agility to trip up rivals, or his keenness in scenting a customer and where his wares will sell.

What great qualities such a man must possess! In all countries one finds these low diplomats: consummate negotiators selling calico, jewels, frippery, wines, often showing truer diplomacy than ambassadors who know mostly its forms. No one in France doubts the powers of the commercial traveller: that intrepid soul who boldly brings the genius of civilization and Parisian inventions to reckon with the plain common sense of remote villages. Who can forget the manoeuvres by which he worms himself into the minds of the populace, bringing a volume of words to bear upon the refractory, like a slow file eating into porphyry? Would you know the force of language, the pressure a phrase can exert against rebellious lucre and the miserly country proprietor—listen to one of these ambassadors of Parisian industry revolve, work, and suck like an intelligent piston of the steam-engine called Speculation.

“Monsieur,” said a shrewd political economist, director-cashier-manager and secretary-general of a celebrated fire-insurance company, “out of every five hundred thousand francs of policies to be renewed in the provinces, not more than fifty thousand are paid up voluntarily. The other four hundred and fifty thousand are got in by the activity of our agents, who go among those in arrears and worry them with stories of horrible incendiaries until they are driven to sign the new policies. Thus you see that eloquence, the labial flux, is nine tenths of the ways and means of our business.”

To talk and make people listen—that is seduction itself. A nation with two Chambers, a woman who lends both ears, are soon lost. Eve and her serpent are the everlasting myth of an hourly fact that began, and may end, with the world itself. “A conversation of two hours ought to capture your man,” said a retired lawyer.

Walk round the commercial traveller and look at him well: olive-green overcoat, cloak with morocco collar, striped blue cotton shirt. In this original figure—so distinct it cannot be rubbed out—how many personalities we meet! What an acrobat, circus, battery all at once! Intrepid mariner, he plunges armed with a few phrases to catch five or six thousand francs in the frozen seas of France’s interior, among the red Indians of the countryside. Provincial fish will not rise to harpoons; they are taken with seines, nets, and gentle persuasions. The traveller’s business is to extract gold from country caches by a purely intellectual operation, pleasantly and without pain. Think of the flood of phrases which, renewed each dawn, leaps over the length and breadth of sunny France.

Now take an individual example.

In Paris there lived an incomparable commercial traveller, the paragon of his race, possessing the necessary qualifications to success in the highest degree. His speech was both vitriol and glue—glue to catch and entangle, vitriol to dissolve hard heads, close fists, and close calculations. His line was once the hat; his skill at snaring the wariest provincial made him a celebrity, so that all vendors of “article Paris” sought him and begged him to take their commissions.

When he returned to Paris between provincial triumphs, he lived in perpetual festivity: weddings and suppers. In smaller towns correspondents made much of him; in Paris the great houses feted him. Welcomed, flattered, and fed wherever he went, to breakfast or dine alone became novelty. He lived like a sovereign—or a journalist; in fact he was the perambulating “feuilleton” of Parisian commerce.

His name was Gaudissart; flatteries won him the surname Illustrious. Wherever he went—behind a counter or before a bar, into a salon or atop a stage-coach, up to a garret or dining with a banker—everyone said, “Ah! here comes the illustrious Gaudissart!” Similia similibus—he believed in homoeopathy. Puns, horse-laugh, monkish face, friar’s skin—clothing, body, mind, and features—combined to give devil-may-care jollity to every inch of him. Free-handed and easy-going, he was the grisettes’ favourite: he jumped to the top of stage-coaches, helped timid ladies down, joked with postillions and sold them caps, smiled at maids and caught them by the waist or heart; gurgled at dinner like a bottle of wine, pretended to draw a cork by a brisk blow on his cheek, played tunes on champagne glasses, dared the company to copy him; chaffed the timid traveller, contradicted the knowing one, lorded it at table and managed to get the titbits. Strong as he was, he could throw aside nonsense and mean business when he flung away a cigar stump and said, with a glance at some town, “I’ll go and see what those people have got in their stomachs.”

Buckled down to work he became the slyest and cleverest diplomat. All things to all men, he could accost a banker like a capitalist, a magistrate like a functionary, a royalist with pious sentiments, a bourgeois as one of themselves. Where he went he was what he ought to be; he left Gaudissart at the door when he entered, and picked him up when he came out.

Until 1830 the illustrious Gaudissart was faithful to the article Paris. Close contact with humanity’s caprices taught him the windings of the human heart. He learned persuasive eloquence, the knack of loosening the tightest purse-strings, the art of rousing desire in husbands, wives, children, and servants—and how to satisfy it. No one better inveigled a merchant by the charms of a bargain and vanished at the crisis of desire. Grateful to the hat trade, he always declared that capping heads had enabled him to understand interiors; he had “capped and crowned” so many people that he felt at liberty to fling himself at their heads. His jokes about hats and heads were irrepressible.

Yet after August and October 1830 he abandoned the hat trade and the article Paris, tearing himself from visible wares to mount into the higher spheres of Parisian speculation. “He forsook,” in his words, “matter for mind; manufactured products for the infinitely purer elaborations of human intelligence.” This needs explanation.

The upheaval of 1830 brought old ideas recomposed by clever speculators. After 1830 ideas became property. A writer, too wise to publish, once said, “more ideas are stolen than pocket-handkerchiefs.” Perhaps we may one day have an Exchange for thought; even now ideas, good or bad, have their consols, are bought, imported, exported, sold, and quoted like stocks. If ideas are not on hand, speculators pass off words in their stead and live on them as a bird lives on millet. Do not laugh: a word is worth as much as an idea in a land where the ticket on a sack is more important than its contents. We have seen libraries working off the word “picturesque” when literature would have killed “fantastic.” Fiscal genius has guessed the proper tax on intellect; it has estimated the profits of advertising and registered a prospectus of thought’s quantity and worth—intellectual property weighed at the Stamp Office in the Rue de la Paix.

Intellect and its products, once turned into articles of commerce, must submit to the same laws as manufactured goods. Ideas conceived by idle Parisians—who zealously fight moral battles over champagne and pheasants—are handed at birth to commercial travellers charged with spreading them “urbi et orbi,” seasoned with advertisement and prospectus. These agents trap the departmental rodent called subscriber—sometimes stockholder, occasionally corresponding member or patron, but invariably fool. Many a poor provincial proprietor, seduced by the prospect of being the first to launch a new idea, has cried “I am a fool!” after sinking a thousand or twelve hundred francs into a void. Speculators sneer that subscribers should know that to succeed in the intellectual world they must start with more money than for a tour of Europe.

Since 1830 honors and emoluments have been lavishly distributed across France to stimulate what are called the “progressive and intelligent masses”: titles, medals, diplomas, a sort of legion of honor for martyrs follow one another. Speculators in manufactured intellect have their own spice—premiums, forestalled dividends, and the conscription of famous names without the writers’ consent, who find themselves associated with more enterprises than days in the year; the law does not consider theft of a patronymic. Worse still is the rape of ideas: these caterers for the public mind, like slave-merchants, tear unborn notions from the parental brain and parade them half-clothed before the terrible public, which, if not amused, will cut off their heads by curtailing ingots and emptying pockets.

This commercial madness reached the illustrious Gaudissart. Noted for his irresistible eloquence, he was offered an unprecedented commission by a life-insurance company, which trained him thoroughly—the secretary-general schooling him in the business, its dialect, the gullible public, impromptu replies, and unanswerable arguments—sharpening the file of a tongue about to operate upon the life of France. Gaudissart rewarded their pains by bringing in subscribers; the insurers boasted of him so loudly that managers of two newspapers—the Globe (Saint-Simonian) and the Movement (republican)—conceived employing him to obtain subscribers. Each proposed to pay ten francs per head if he brought in a thousand, but only five francs if he fetched five hundred. The bargain did not interfere with his life-insurance work; he even claimed indemnity from the Saint-Simonians for eight days spent studying their doctrines, asserting prodigious memory and intellect were necessary to grasp them, though he asked nothing from the republicans.

Gaudissart inclined to republican ideas, had dabbled in conspiracies, had once been arrested and released for lack of proof; lately he had grown a mustache and believed a hat of certain shape and a pair of spurs would make him a fit representative of the Republic. For a week he went each morning to be Saint-Simonized at the Globe and every afternoon to the life-insurance company. His aptitude and memory were prodigious; by April 15, the spring campaign’s opening, he was ready. Two large commercial houses, alarmed at declining business, persuaded him not to abandon the commodity “Paris” and tempted him with large offers. The king of travellers yielded; enormous premiums and old friendships swayed him.

Gaudissart delighted in letting himself be tyrannized by Jenny, a pretty florist he kept in a fourth-floor house in the Rue d’Artois, reputedly privately married to. She required innumerable attentions and ordered him to write from every town, rendering minute accounts of his proceedings. He delighted in fantasizing that subscriptions would refurnish her room: “I consecrate to the adornment of your room all the ‘Children’ I shall get in the provinces,” he told her—“Children” being his shorthand for subscriptions to a proposed newspaper for children. He imagined that five sous per subscriber would make her rich, and himself wealthy: instead of earning eight thousand francs a year, he would bring back twenty thousand at least from each trip. He planned to become a shareholder in newspapers like his friend Finot, son of a hatter, who had amassed thirty thousand francs income and sought a peerage. Why not Gaudissart as Baron, peer of France, ambassador in London, nonplussing the English? For the present, though, he must give himself wholly to capitalists: the Globe, the Movement, the Children’s Journal, life-insurance, and some of his old Article Paris.

He rehearsed the grandiloquent political rhetoric he would adopt in the Chamber—declaring the Press an institution to be judged politically, debating whether it should be taxed, free, or encouraged—all in a Napoleonic pose that amused Jenny and himself. He was thirty-eight, of medium height, stout from stage-coach life, with a round, ruddy face epitomizing Abundance, Law, Force, Commerce. After a glorious breakfast with Jenny, Gaudissart departed on horseback to canvass the chief towns in his district for his multiple enterprises.

After forty-five days between Paris and Blois, he stayed two weeks at Blois to write correspondence and make short visits. The night before leaving for Tours he wrote Jenny a gloating letter boasting of lodging two millions between Paris and Blois, selling Ternaux shawls at Orléans, lamenting that the Movement did not move republicans, recounting making a hundred Globes, and securing two thousand Children subscriptions. He bragged of cajoling a dynastic diner into subscribing to the Children’s Journal to avoid quarrel and promised to take Amboise in two days and “topple” Tours.

From Tours he went to Vouvray, a rich district where the public mind seemed susceptible. Trotting along an embankment, admiring the landscape, he little dreamed his commercial infallibility would perish there. A note on the Tourangian mind: stamped by Rabelais’ spirit, Touraine’s people are polished, witty, poetic, voluptuous; their impulse subsides quickly. Atmosphere, ease, and jovial manners smother sentiment of art and enervate will. At home Tourangians sit at ease, employ wit to mock neighbors, and make merry—Rabelais’ Abbaye of Theleme is their emblem.

In Vouvray Gaudissart met one of its native wit-masters: Monsieur Vernier, a former dyer, a social leader among small proprietors, and a merry-maker. The Tourangians especially hated Saint-Simonian doctrines and treated them with superb disdain. After arriving and consulting landlord Mitouflet, Gaudissart betook himself to Vernier to solicit help. He began his patter about bankers, respectability, guarantees—drawing his string of phrases. Vernier let him talk, apparently interested, but plotted a prank: to bring before him the town’s supposed banker—Margaritis, a half-insane man—and have Gaudissart sell to him.

Margaritis lived at the head of the Valley Coquette with his devoted wife. Of Italian origin and half-mad, he carried two long-bladed knives, sometimes threatened his wife, kept a sack of flour and two puncheons of wine in the cellar, and for seven years read the same Indre-et-Loire newspaper without perceiving the repetition. He moved mechanically to say, “Go away!” yet had lucid intervals when he managed wine sales and gave useful advice. He stole delicacies and once flew into fury when told he did not hear. The evening before Gaudissart’s arrival Madame Margaritis had struggled to deceive her husband about the wine; Vernier had arranged to use Benoîte Fontanieu as witness to the joke.

Vernier escorted Gaudissart to La Fuye, Margaritis’s modest house. Margaritis sat in a yellow Utrecht-velvet arm-chair, somewhat inert. Vernier introduced the traveller, telling him to “put away your mathematics and talk.” Margaritis rose and invited Gaudissart to sit, and the show began: Gaudissart discoursed on life-insurance, time as the most precious social wealth, and capitalizing future prospects—“cashing” talents by discounting time. He argued that the company would recognize a man’s intellectual capital; by paying a small premium the insured would secure his family and be freed from anxieties that distract from success. Insurance, he insisted, was the exchequer of beneficence, mutual insurance against poverty, enabling men to borrow on their paper at the Bourse and among bankers; civilization denies bread to those who provide luxury, and insurance bridges the gulf. The company also offered annuities superior to tontines. These arguments, delivered with the traveller’s articulate confidence, aimed to show the practical benefits of insuring nascent talent.

Margaritis reacted unpredictably. He cried, “That is usury!” then was mollified when Gaudissart offered wine; he poured Vouvray of 1819 and dubbed it Head of Vouvray—a pun with caput, caputal—and even offered to sell his puncheons. He adopted local touches and humorously bargained: he would not pay eighty francs for the Globe but offered seven francs for the Children’s Journal and consented to push the Globe if Gaudissart took his wine. The two men strolled among the vines; the ladies and Vernier watched, laughing at the strange spectacle of the urbane traveller and the lunatic engaged in earnest commercial negotiation. At length Gaudissart, who required reciprocal agreements, wrote an order for delivery of the two casks and demanded Margaritis sign a bond to deliver them. Margaritis did so and counted out seven francs for the Children’s Journal; Gaudissart left humming triumphantly.

The next day, however, the joke was revealed. At the Soleil d’Or Gaudissart heard from Mitouflet that Margaritis might be a lunatic. Furious at having been duped, he marched to Vernier’s salon where Vernier was laughing with friends. “Monsieur,” he began, calling him a scoundrel and blackguard for sending him to a lunatic and threatening to make him give satisfaction—reflecting the provincial code of honor. Vernier, delighted at the stir, defended his prank as justifiable mockery of a man trying to feather his nest by promoting a paper that preached a religion denying inheritance. Gaudissart, enraged, lunged to slap Vernier but was restrained; his wig fell on Mademoiselle Clara Vernier’s head. He declared he would stay at the Soleil d’Or until morning, ready to fight; Vernier answered he would “fight in Vouvray” and make Gaudissart stay longer than he thought.

The town became agitated over the duel. Madame Fontanieu and the assistant-mayor implored Mitouflet to avert a violent death; he took charge. In the evening Mitouflet brought pens and paper to the traveller, advising him to settle business affairs if a duel loomed. He asked if Gaudissart was a good swordsman and offered foils; his experience won him sixty-two passes in practice. He advised pistols instead, loaded to fire wide so both might retire with honor. Gaudissart fretted about possibly killing the man; Mitouflet calmed him.

The adversaries met by the bridge of La Cise next morning. Vernier nearly shot a cow in the scrambling; they fired in the air and embraced—recognizing that a rough joke had passed into a good one. Back at the inn, Gaudissart wanted to summon Margaritis for delivery of the casks. Vernier confessed he had none. Gaudissart demanded indemnity and refused to be outwitted without pursuing his claim. Madame Margaritis, alarmed at the fracas, brought thirty francs to compensate the traveller; Gaudissart accepted, seeing himself indemnified, and left Vouvray content with the settlement—though convinced that the region resisted new ideas.

On his return from the southern departments, Gaudissart rode in a diligence and explained life’s enigmas to a young man like an infant. Passing Vouvray the youth admired the fine site; Gaudissart patronizingly replied, “Yes, Monsieur, but not habitable on account of the people. You get into duels every day. Why, it is not three months since I fought one just there,” pointing to the bridge, “with a damned dyer; but I made an end of him—he bit the dust!” The boast blended bravado with self-promotion.

Throughout, the tale satirizes the commercialization of ideas, the manipulative machinery of advertisement and prospectus, the easy enlistment of famous names, the hasty plundering of nascent thought, and the provincial theater where urban pretensions meet local wit. Gaudissart, a consummate salesman—eloquent, ambitious, and vain—serves as both perpetrator and victim of this marketplace: he peddles life-insurance and newspapers, vows political ambition and social ascent, wins and loses subscriptions, is pranked by provincial satire, provokes a duel, and leaves paid off yet unconvinced of the provincial capacity for intellect. The story exposes a social economy where intellect becomes a commodity and the traveller an instrument of its distribution, often to comedic and tragic effect.
