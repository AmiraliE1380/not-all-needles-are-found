In 1828, Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac walked from Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré to the boulevard, discussing Rastignac's admiration for the Marquise d’Espard. Bianchon cautioned him against pursuing her, likening her to a “one-eyed horse” and suggesting she was not as youthful as he believed.

Rastignac argued that her wealth and status were appealing, especially since he was financially strained after marrying off his sisters. Bianchon criticized fashionable women for lacking genuine character and warned that the Marquise might be in debt despite her charm.

He highlighted high society's superficiality, asserting that true virtue is rare among fashionable women. Rastignac countered that such women could offer significant advantages for an ambitious man.

Determined to marry the Marquise, Rastignac prioritized her social connections. Bianchon reluctantly agreed to introduce him to his uncle, Judge Jean-Jules Popinot, known for his strict legal adherence.

The next morning, Bianchon visited Popinot, who lived in a dilapidated area of Paris. Despite his skills, the judge was overlooked for promotions, illustrating the struggles of a good man in a flawed system where ambition often overshadows merit. Bianchon contemplated the challenges of persuading Popinot to assist Rastignac, reflecting on ambition and moral dilemmas in the pursuit of success.

Twelve years earlier, in 1816, Popinot was appointed President of the Commission Extraordinary to distribute food during a severe scarcity. Initially hesitant, he became dedicated to alleviating local suffering, evolving from a judge focused on legal outcomes to a compassionate figure akin to Saint Vincent de Paul. His charitable work drained his resources, yet he found fulfillment as a protector and benefactor.

By the end of his second year, Popinot had transformed his storeroom into a parlor for the needy, meticulously recording donations and requests for assistance. His aide, Lavienne, helped manage the influx of desperate individuals seeking aid, filling the room from dawn until late morning. Despite the stifling atmosphere, the poor revered Popinot, who tirelessly provided food, shelter, and employment.

Living frugally on a modest inherited fortune, Popinot was known for his absent-mindedness regarding personal appearance, often linked to his intellectual pursuits. He remained unrecognized by his peers, having not received the Legion of Honor.

The narrative shifts to a case involving the Marquis d’Espard, whose wife sought a Commission in Lunacy for him. Bianchon, a doctor and Popinot's nephew, visits the Rue du Fouarre, where he finds his uncle amidst a crowd of impoverished individuals.

Popinot's disheveled attire reflects his preoccupation with charity as he engages with petitioners. Bianchon expresses concern about the Marquis, and Popinot reads the petition detailing the Marquis's alleged mental decline and the influence of a woman named Jeanrenaud. While skeptical, Popinot acknowledges the need for further investigation and reflects on the Marquise's potential ulterior motives. He agrees to visit Madame d’Espard for more information.

The following evening, Bianchon finds Popinot absorbed in a complex judgment. They enter the Marquise’s room, where she sits elegantly, flanked by Rastignac and the silent Chevalier d’Espard. Madame d’Espard, notable since her separation from her husband in 1815, maintains a youthful appearance and social connections.

As the conversation unfolds, Madame d’Espard recounts her version of events, portraying herself as a victim. Popinot questions her motives, noting her luxurious lifestyle, which seems at odds with her claims of financial distress. Tension escalates as he hints that her request might be driven by self-interest rather than genuine concern for her children. 

After their meeting, Popinot resolves to investigate further, planning to summon Madame Jeanrenaud, linked to M. d’Espard. The next day, Madame Jeanrenaud arrives, presenting herself as an unassuming figure, far from the seductive temptress implied by the Marquise. She expresses concern for M. d’Espard, intriguing Popinot about the true dynamics at play.

As the story unfolds, it becomes clear that the Marquise’s polished exterior masks a web of ambition and manipulation, while Popinot must navigate these treacherous waters with caution. The case promises to reveal not only the truth about M. d’Espard’s mental state but also the lengths individuals will go to protect their interests in Parisian society.

The text examines the life of M. le Marquis d’Espard, a nobleman living in a dilapidated Paris mansion, illustrating how minor events can lead to significant consequences. A minor illness delayed an important meeting with a judge, reflecting historical instances where small incidents had major repercussions.

D’Espard's once-grand home symbolizes the decline of French nobility. Chosen for its garden to benefit his children's health, the mansion, despite its condition, exuded a peaceful atmosphere with modest decor. His private family life was supported by a few loyal servants. D’Espard’s choice to isolate his children led to misunderstandings with neighbors, who viewed him as eccentric. Gossip portrayed him as mad, fueled by his frugal lifestyle and perceived financial ruin, exacerbated by tenants’ jealousy of his noble status, deemed undeserved without wealth.

D’Espard and his wife embodied true aristocratic values, instilling dignity and pride in their children, who reflected their noble heritage. At around fifty, the Marquis appeared refined but was seen as cold due to his mannerisms. His children, Comte Clement and Vicomte Camille, were handsome, with Clement nearing sixteen and Camille still in education. The narrative highlights the complexities of social status, isolation, and societal perceptions of declining nobility in a changing France.

On a Thursday holiday, Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard. Early that morning, his sons played in the garden, waking their father, who enjoyed watching them. Upon Popinot's arrival, the portress informed him of the Marquis' supposed madness, claiming he encouraged his children to fight. Popinot met the Marquis in a shabby office, where he explained he was investigating a petition for a Commission in Lunacy filed by the Marquise d’Espard. The Marquis expressed concern about how the inquiry might affect his wife and revealed a family secret regarding the Jeanrenaud fortune, which had been wrongfully acquired. He felt compelled to repay the Jeanrenauds, straining his finances.

As their conversation continued, the Marquis invited Popinot to his home, revealing he had rented the office for a successful publishing venture. Just then, his sons returned, illustrating their strong bond. Madame Jeanrenaud soon entered, wanting to return the money given to the Marquis, fearing for their honor. Popinot intervened, explaining that the payments were legally justified.

Afterward, Popinot reflected on the encounter and the nobility of the Marquis. The next day, he was informed he would be removed from the case due to a potential conflict of interest stemming from his visit with Madame d’Espard. Although Popinot protested, he accepted the decision, recognizing the importance of maintaining the Court's integrity. The narrative explores themes of honor, familial duty, and the complexities of social standing as the Marquis d’Espard grapples with his family's dark history while striving to restore their honor through restitution and dedication to his children.
