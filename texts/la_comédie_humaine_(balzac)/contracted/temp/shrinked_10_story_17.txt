La Grenadiere is a quaint house on the right bank of the Loire, about a mile downstream from the bridge of Tours. The broad river, dotted with green islands, runs between cliffs terraced for warm-climate fruit; cliffs are crowned with white stone country houses, gardens and vineyards. A church spire marks nearby Saint-Cyr and, further on, the Choisille joins the Loire. The house stands halfway up the hillside, a hundred paces from the church.

Typical of Touraine and two to three hundred years old, La Grenadiere is reached by a fissure in the rock that provides steps down to the dike—the embankment that keeps the Loire in its bed and serves as the Paris–Nantes causeway. A gate opens to a narrow footpath between terraces; trees partly conceal the house until the river view widens. A crumbling Gothic archway leads into a small garden hewn from the rock, with a wooden summer-house draped in jessamine and honeysuckle. Trellised vines and pomegranate trees (the source of its name) surround the house; vine-covered steps lead to cellars carved into the rock.

The façade has two large windows flanking a rustic door, three dormers in a steep slate roof, yellowwashed walls and green shutters. Inside, a crooked staircase rises to a lobby; to the right, a dining room with white tile floors; to the left, a sitting room with saffron walls and exposed walnut rafters. The first floor holds two simple whitewashed bedrooms with stone chimneypieces. A later timber-framed kitchen, with a rustic pump and well, suggests the house began as a vintner’s retreat.

Two acres of vineyard slope steeply at the back; a vinedresser’s cottage leans against the western gable and stone walls with espaliers maximize the land while neglected corners fill with figs, wildflowers and strawberries. Modest yet dignified, La Grenadiere yields varied grapes, figs, peaches and melons; Spanish broom and Italian oleanders add an exotic note. The Loire flows below, evening breezes bring a sea-scent, and changing clouds reveal the south bank, Tours and châteaux—an owned retreat for lovers and dreamers where the river’s sounds invite quiet contemplation among perennial flowers and fruit.

If an Englishman rents La Grenadiere for the summer, he pays a thousand francs for six months, excluding vineyard produce; orchard fruit doubles the rent, and the vintage doubles it again. With its stone staircase, triple terrace, two acres of vineyard, flowering roses, and well-head, it seems priceless. Unsold since its 1690 sale for forty thousand francs, it commands a view over three valleys of Touraine and cathedral towers; its health and beauty are beyond valuation.

In a bright spring during the Restoration, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, thirteen and eight, and took La Grenadiere—perhaps for its distance from town. She converted the drawing-room into her bedroom, gave the children the two rooms above, and had the housekeeper sleep in a closet. The dining-room served as sitting and drawing room; plainly furnished in walnut, the house’s charm lay in neatness and harmony rather than luxury.

Mme. Willemsens resisted easy classification: her simple dress invited assumptions, while polished manners suggested higher rank. Tall, slender, and melancholy, with dark eyes that occasionally revealed secret anguish and premature wrinkles, she always wore a long black gown and an English bonnet, signaling mourning. Her health seemed fragile; she walked with her children to the bridge of Tours for air and sunsets.

She visited Tours only twice—to arrange the children’s lessons—and signed the lease as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, a fact whispered only in business circles. Curious townspeople found her untouchable; suitors were shy, and she burned her letters. The three tutors admired the close household bond: Louis-Gaston, dark and robust, protective of his younger, almost golden-haired Marie-Gaston. Well-mannered and devout, the boys rose early, prayed, and attentively watched for small signs of their mother’s failing strength.

Mme. Willemsens dressed for her children’s early breakfasts and play so she would be a pleasing sight for them. From ten until three she listened to their lessons, breaking for lunch in the summer-house where they all ate together; afterward the children played while she rested on a sofa, enjoying the Touraine landscape.

They roamed the orchard and garden, chasing lizards, studying plants and insects, and returning with eager questions. During lessons Mme. Willemsens embroidered but followed attentively, concentrating on her eldest, Louis—encouraging his ambition while asking little about Marie. She walked the tutor to the gate and candidly asked about Louis’s progress; tutors answered honestly. Work and play were balanced, leaving little room for quarrels. Her steady love smoothed difficulties, taught her sons moderation and submission, and she often wept for joy watching them.

The garden-house hours were precious and full of laughter; the children spoke English and French thanks to their English nurse, and their mother guided their learning with books about great historical figures to inspire ambition. Little Marie often fell asleep on her knee. Afterwards Mme. Willemsens grew pensive and tearful, confessing fears of dying and leaving them without resources. Louis promised to care for Marie; she said love kept her alive and hoped Touraine’s air would help.

Her talk of death made Louis more focused and quietened the household. Annette watched as Mme. Willemsens grew frail but strove to preserve her beauty. By autumn she spent nearly all her time with the children, while Louis—encouraged by his mother and by a retired naval officer’s tales at the bridge of Tours—took long walks, climbed trees, learned to swim, and worked late preparing for the École Polytechnique; his sunburned face showed a new purpose.

When October arrived, Mme. Willemsens could only rise at noon. The Loire’s warmth made the air almost like the Bay of Naples, and the faculty had recommended the area. At midday she sat in the shade with her two children, Louis and Marie, who never left her side. With lessons over they sought a life of closeness: the elder child lying beside her, basking in her affection, while restless Marie gathered flowers. Mme. Willemsens, with tired eyes and languid movements, smiled at her vibrant children—a poignant scene touched by autumn’s melancholy.

The doctor eventually ordered her to remain in her room, which was brightened daily by her favorite flowers; her children stayed near. One early November day she sat at the piano for the last time, fingers moving with passionate feeling. When the doctor arrived he insisted she stay in bed; she was stunned. After he left she asked Louis to take her to the terrace for a final view. Outside, she watched the clouds, then, anguished, clutched her children’s hands and lamented their uncertain future, fearing how they would judge her life when older. Seeing Louis and Marie kneel beside her, she returned to bed, never to rise again.

Days blurred with Annette and Louis keeping vigil. On the fifth day the doctor banned flowers—an ominous sign. One Sunday night she told Louis the next day would be her last, dictating a letter to Lord Brandon and hesitating before signing “Louis-Gaston.” She revealed twelve thousand francs saved and urged him to safeguard it. Louis vowed to care for Marie, send her to school, and apprentice at sea so he could return successful.

She received the sacrament the next morning and died surrounded by loved ones. Annette and the vinedresser’s wife prepared the body; only a few mourners attended. Marie cried as they lowered the coffin and a simple cross marked the grave. The children returned to La Grenadiere, then went with Annette to her cousin’s in Tours. Louis entrusted Annette with the papers and money, saw Marie to school, and left to apprentice on a warship. A month later, watching the French coast fade, he embraced life at sea determined to honor his mother, comforted by an old sailor’s words.
