If the French have as great an aversion to traveling as the English have a propensity for it, both may have reasons. Something better than England is to be found everywhere; the peculiar charm of France—the life of the brain, the talent for conversation, the subtle half-speech so familiar in Paris—is rarely elsewhere. A Frenchman whose raillery finds little comprehension abroad would wither like an uprooted tree; emigration opposes the French instinct. This prelude explains the joy French travellers feel in finding a little Paris abroad: not the exact cookery or the most fashionable pleasantry, but wines, a critical atmosphere, and that wit which makes Paris unique.

In 1836, when the Sardinian Court was at Genoa, two Parisians could fancy themselves in Paris while in a palazzo on the last fold of the Apennines near the gate of San Tomaso and the lighthouse. This palazzo, one of Genoa’s magnificent villas, stood above the city; after a morning’s torrents of rain, a clear evening there—sea and sky matching, the quay and villa groves silent, marble heads spouting mysterious water, stars and Mediterranean waves—offered a Boccaccian hour known only on Mediterranean shores: perfumed air, voluptuousness in the chair, an ice or sorbet spoon, town at your feet and a fair woman opposite.

Round the table sat the Marquis di Negro and the Marquis Damaso Pareto, two Frenchmen masquerading as Genoese, the Consul-General with his wife and two sleeping children, the French Ambassador and his wife, a secretary who fancied himself crushed and mischievous, and two Parisians come to bid farewell to the Consul’s wife at a splendid dinner. The predominant figure, upon whom eyes dwelt, was a celebrated woman—Camille Maupin, known in letters as Mademoiselle des Touches—the heroine of this improvised festival.

The two Parisians were the landscape painter Leon de Lora and the critic Claude Vignon. Camille had been to Florence on business and had brought Leon to show him Italy, extending to Rome for the Campagna. Returning by the Cornice road to Marseilles, she stopped again at Genoa for Leon’s sake. The Consul-General, attentive to a woman of her position and talent, had wished to honor her; she, who knew Genoa intimately, had left her painter to the diplomat and the two marquises and had only a few minutes to spare. Though the Ambassador, a distinguished man of letters, had pressed his advances, Camille, fearing exhibition, initially refused; she consented to a farewell day at the Consul’s villa. Leon said her presence was the only return he could make to those hosting him, so she sacrificed one of the rare free days enjoyed by those watched by the world.

Etiquette was banished and curiosity abounded among women eager to see whether Camille’s manly talents impaired her feminine grace. After dinner, conversation—gay and grave, enlivened by Leon’s sallies and by good taste—had scarcely touched literature. But talk turned, as it must among Frenchmen and women, to woman’s sin: when a woman falls, is the blame hers or the man’s? The three respectable women present—the Ambassadress, the Consul’s wife Onorina, and Camille—were merciless to the woman. The men argued that some virtues might survive a woman’s fall. Leon, impatient, broke the dispute, and the Consul rose to put his children to bed and to send a little black pocket-book by Gina—an action showing his wife’s confidence and their mutual tenderness.

“I will tell you a story in which I played a part,” the Consul-General said, “and after that we can discuss it. Begin by dissecting a corpse.” All prepared to listen; it was then, after much talk, that he began his tale.

He was a man of thirty-four, married about six years, and bore the living portrait of Lord Byron—poetical in look, but genuinely so. His handsome face and delightful nature had captivated a Genoese heiress, Onorina Pedrotti, the sole child of a banker without male heirs. Though daughters rarely inherit in Genoa, Onorina was the exception. The Consul had seemed unwilling to marry, but after two years and the Ambassador’s encouragement, the marriage was decided; the town attributed the Consul’s melancholy reserve to passion. Onorina gave her love as consolation; Signor Pedrotti had no reason to complain of the choice. Powerful protectors in Paris watched the young diplomat. The Ambassador secured for the Consul a title and honors; Signor Pedrotti’s daughter’s dowry was a million francs and the Casa Pedrotti’s fortune passed to the young couple when the old Count Pedrotti died in January 1831.

Onorina Pedrotti was one of those Genoese beauties whose type inspired Michelangelo. A full, singularly placed bosom, long hair, dark complexion, dreamy eyes, and a long white dress embroidered with flowers: picture Michelangelo’s Night re-dressed for modern life, and you have Onorina, with a handsome boy of six and a little girl of four—childhood’s type sought by sculptors. This beautiful family was the object of Camille’s secret study. She noted that the Consul seemed too absent-minded for perfect happiness and wondered why a man of his intellect and a hundred thousand francs a year remained at Genoa. Yet she saw, in small details women perceive, that the husband was faithfully devoted. She alternately told herself, “What is wrong?—Nothing is wrong,” following the misleading symptoms of his demeanor; he had the calmness of consummate diplomats.

The dinner’s talk had moved to the perennial subject—woman’s sin—when the Consul’s story began:

At twenty-two, having taken his degree in law, the Consul’s uncle, the Abbe Loraux, seventy-two and Cure of the White Friars, sought a protector and position for his nephew. One morning in 1824 the Abbe told him to dress quickly: he was to be introduced to a man willing to engage him as secretary—Comte Octave (the Consul uses only the Christian name out of discretion). The Abbe explained that the Count, a high legal authority in the confidence of the Dauphiness and newly a State Minister, would offer the young man a salary and rooms, not admitting him to the Count’s table lest servants take charge, but promising that the secretary’s work would be summons to high posts later. The Abbe urged discretion.

The narrator described himself: educated at the College Saint-Louis and under a virtuous headmaster, reared in his uncle’s house, pious and with modest means, he had a thirst for literature and the theatre. Entering Comte Octave’s service meant independent life: a hundred louis a year, society, and perhaps a protectress to rescue him from youthful danger. The Abbe, seeing the young man’s restlessness, would give him twenty francs and a smile; a maternal care that kept him from excess.

At the Count’s house—an enormous place like the Hotel Carnavalet, with a hall like Versailles and a marble staircase wide enough for eight—he saw grandeur yet slight neglect: cobblestones hidden in grass, blackened walls, a vast library and study opening into a great garden. Labranche, the footman, announced them: “Monsieur le Cure of the White Friars, and his nephew, Monsieur de l’Hostal.” Comte Octave, dressed in long trousers and gray flannel, rose from his desk, took the Abbe’s hands, and made the young man feel welcome. The Count offered a room and explained the office: the young man would work hard and learn much.

At first the narrator felt like an insect near an eagle, but he soon perceived Comte Octave’s extraordinary personality. The Count, though veiled in an austere, hermit-like life, showed depths: a harsh yet refined face, a large mouth suggesting both irony and kindness, a broad forehead, small turquoise-blue eyes as keen and expressionless as Talleyrand’s. His hair, streaked black and white, and his white, woman-like hands suggested delicate habit. The narrator withheld certain details of rank and office out of discretion, though the Count’s public life and fortune were as he described.

Living with the Count, the narrator quickly found not plain peacefulness but an ice-buried youth, sorrow and fatigue under intense study—youth entombed under grief and suppressed passion. At moments, when the Count smiled for the Abbe, his age seemed forty, but otherwise the face had been transfigured by some blow. He was pious, regularly attending early mass at Saint-Paul’s for workmen and servants, yet his piety was private, almost a vice of secrecy. The narrator sensed subterranean caverns of sorrow; the Count evaded inquiry like a sharpshooter seeking cover. He often absented himself at odd hours and left the young man to carry on work entrusted to him.

The Count loved flowers and owned a garden and study full of rare plants, often buying them half-withered—perhaps, the narrator thought, because they reflected his own faded state. He devoted himself to public interest with almost frantic zeal, as if to cheat some other passion. His struggle was not that of a cynic; he was religious, rigorous, a silent, concentrated man who pitied and admired without transient enthusiasm. The narrator detected long, harrowing internal battles and supposed at first that perhaps the Count loved a woman or lived with some unworthy mistress. He noticed oddities: the Count once conversed eagerly with an old woman, provoking suspect thoughts, and the Count never seemed to invest his considerable savings. Yet he was a masterful, serious statesman and judge—a man whose work and manner combined stoic duty with secret wounds.

Over months, the Count and his secretary studied each other. The Count, whom the narrator later learned to be thirty-seven, had an exterior of quiet propriety born of duty, but beneath lay an almost volcanic passion, constrained by will. He attended to his duties and charities; his life was one of relentless labor, interrupted by moments when sorrow broke through and he became like a child—tears, excitement, long solitary stares at a goldfish basin, the senator’s mask dissolving. The young man felt affection like a son’s for this mysterious figure; curiosity, tempered by respect, drove him to watch for the cause of the Count’s unhappiness.

The house had been mismanaged through neglect; the Count, despite an income of about 160,000 francs, spent recklessly on servants and was in need of reorganization. The narrator set about reforming the household: getting rid of worthless servants, securing honest ones, improving food and household management. Within two years the Count enjoyed modern comforts: good horses, competent cookery, and reasonable housekeeping at about 30,000 francs a year. The Count, grateful, joked about servants becoming rich restaurant owners. In time, their relationship deepened; the Count took the young man for a son, called him to confidence in practical matters, and introduced him into the highest circles, often sending his carriage and servants to represent him.

Once, when the narrator, unlucky at play, lost two thousand francs and refused to touch his savings, he asked the Count for help. The Count, with simple kindness, gave him six thousand francs and said they would henceforth go halves: since the young man represented him publicly, his self-respect must not suffer. This liberality reinforced their mutual trust, yet the Count never disclosed his secret sorrows, and the narrator never demanded to know their cause.

What could the Count be doing in the evenings? The narrator wondered: vices, a jealous attachment, or some other infirmity? He once caught the Count in eager talk with an old woman, which nourished suspicion. The Count never seemed to invest savings; was he spending secretly? Sometimes in the garden he walked like a man racked by melancholy, rubbing his hands raw, and his face would shift from hardness to velvety softness. He would take the narrator by the arm and ask, “What have you come to ask?” without revealing his own joy. More often he would sit for hours staring at the goldfish in the marble basin, as if that mechanical occupation soothed his secret pain.

Thus the Consul described the drama of Comte Octave’s second life: a great mind bowed by some private catastrophe, carrying out public duties with martyr-like faith while hidden passions ravaged him. The narrator had become attached to him as to a father and had altered the house for the better, bringing order and comfort. Yet beneath this domestic improvement the mystery remained: deep disquiet, tears, sudden surliness, then gracious humility. The Count’s melancholy and hidden struggles had become an absorbing problem, and the narrator’s affection was matched by a desire to know and to aid.

This is as far as the Consul had told his tale at the pause in the evening by the Genoese villa table.

“On a certain Monday,” he resumed, “M. le Président de Grandville and M. de Serizy (then Vice‑President of the Council of State) met at Comte Octave’s house. They formed a committee of three; I was the secretary. The Count had already secured my appointment as Auditor to the Council of State. All documents for their inquiry into the political matter privately submitted to them lay on a long table in the library. They had trusted the Count to examine the papers preliminarily. To avoid carrying all the papers to M. de Serizy as president, they decided to meet first in the Rue Payenne. The Cabinet at the Tuileries attached great importance to this work; its chief burden fell on me—and to it I owed my appointment that year as Master of Appeals.

“Though the Comtes de Grandville and de Serizy, whose habits resembled my patron’s, never dined away, we were still discussing the matter late when the servant told me, ‘MM. the Cures of Saint‑Paul and of the White Friars have been waiting in the drawing‑room for two hours.’

It was nine o’clock.

“‘Well, gentlemen, you find yourselves compelled to dine with priests,’ said Comte Octave. ‘I do not know whether Grandville can overcome his horror of a priest’s gown—’

“‘It depends on the priest.’

“‘One of them is my uncle, and the other is the Abbé Gaudron,’ said I. ‘Do not be alarmed; the Abbé Fontanon is no longer second priest at Saint‑Paul—’

“‘Well, let us dine,’ replied the Président de Grandville. ‘A bigot frightens me, but there is no one so cheerful as a truly pious man.’

We went into the drawing‑room. The dinner was delightful. Men of real information—politicians whose business gives consummate experience and practice of speech—are admirable storytellers. With them there is no medium: they are either heavy or sublime. In this sport Prince Metternich equals Charles Nodier. A statesman's fun, faceted like a diamond, is sharp, sparkling, and full of sense. Confident the proprieties would be observed, my uncle let his wit play—refined, gentle, penetrating, elegant, like men used to concealing thoughts under a black robe. There was nothing vulgar nor idle in this light talk, which I compare, for its effect, to Rossini’s music.

The Abbé Gaudron was, as M. de Grandville said, more Saint Peter than Saint Paul: a peasant full of faith, square on his feet and tall; his ignorance of the world and literature enlivened conversation with guileless amazement and unexpected questions. They began discussing a plague‑spot of social life—adultery. My uncle noted the contradiction the legislators of the Code, still shaken by the Revolution, had set between civil and religious law, which he said was at the root of the mischief.

“‘In the eyes of the Church,’ said he, ‘adultery is a crime; in those of your tribunals it is a misdemeanor. Adultery drives to the police court in a carriage instead of standing at the bar to be tried. Napoleon’s Council of State, too tender toward erring women, was inefficient. Should they not have harmonized civil and religious law, and sent the guilty wife to a convent, as of old?’

“‘To a convent!’ said M. de Serizy. ‘They must first have created convents, and in those days monasteries were being turned into barracks. Besides, think of what you say, M. l’Abbé—give to God what society would have none of?’

“‘Oh!’ said the Comte de Grandville, ‘you do not know France. They were obliged to leave the husband free to take proceedings: well, there are not ten cases of adultery brought up in a year.’

“‘M. l’Abbé preaches for his own saint, for it was Jesus Christ who invented adultery,’ said Comte Octave. ‘In the East, the cradle of the human race, woman was merely a luxury, regarded as a chattel; no virtues were demanded of her but obedience and beauty. By exalting the soul above the body, the modern family in Europe—a daughter of Christ—invented indissoluble marriage, and made it a sacrament.’

“‘Ah! the Church saw the difficulties,’ exclaimed M. de Grandville.

“‘This institution has given rise to a new world,’ the Count went on with a smile. ‘But the practices of that world will never be those of a climate where women are marriageable at seven years of age, and more than old at five‑and‑twenty. The Catholic Church overlooked the needs of half the globe.—So let us discuss Europe only.

“‘Is woman our superior or our inferior? That is the real question so far as we are concerned. If woman is our inferior, by placing her on so high a level as the Church does, fearful punishments for adultery were needful. And formerly that was what was done. The cloister or death sums up early legislation. But since then practice has modified the law, as is always the case. The throne became a hotbed for adultery, and its increase marks the decline of Catholic dogma. Today, where the Church demands only sincere repentance, society marks rather than executes. The law still condemns but no longer terrifies. Thus two moral standards exist: the world and the Code. Where the Code is weak, as I admit with our dear Abbé, the world is audacious and satirical; few judges would not have committed the faults they thunder against. The world, which contradicts law in its rejoicings, habits, and pleasures, is severer than Code or Church: it punishes a blunder after encouraging hypocrisy. The whole marriage law needs reconstruction. French law would be perfect if it excluded daughters from inheriting.’

“‘We three among us know the question very thoroughly,’ said the Comte de Grandville with a laugh. ‘I have a wife I cannot live with. Serizy has a wife who will not live with him. As for you, Octave, yours ran away from you. So we three represent every case of the conjugal conscience, and, no doubt, if ever divorce is brought in again, we shall form the committee.’

Octave’s fork dropped on his glass, breaking it and his plate. He turned as pale as death and flashed a thunderous glare at M. de Grandville, hinting at my presence, which I caught.

“‘Forgive me, my dear fellow. I did not see Maurice,’ the Président went on. ‘Serizy and I, after being the witnesses to your marriage, became your accomplices; I did not think I was committing an indiscretion in the presence of these two venerable priests.’

M. de Serizy changed the subject, listing all he had done to please his wife without success. He concluded that human sympathies cannot be regulated; social law is best when nearest natural law. Nature disregards affinities of souls; her aim is propagation. Hence the Code was wise to leave latitude to chance. Preventing daughters from inheriting while male heirs existed was an excellent provision—whether to hinder degeneration, or to make households happier by abolishing scandalous unions and favoring moral qualities and beauty.

“‘But then,’ he exclaimed, lifting his hand with a gesture of disgust, ‘how are we to perfect legislation in a country which insists on bringing together seven or eight hundred legislators!—After all, if I am sacrificed,’ he added, ‘I have a child to succeed me.’

“‘Setting aside all the religious question,’ my uncle said, ‘I would remark to your Excellency that Nature only owes us life, and that it is society that owes us happiness. Are you a father?’ my uncle asked.

“‘And I—have I any children?’ said Comte Octave in a hollow voice, and his tone made such an impression that there was no more talk of wives or marriage.

After coffee, the two Counts and two priests slipped away, seeing poor Octave had fallen into melancholy and did not notice. My patron sat in an armchair by the fire, like a man crushed.

“‘You now know the secret of my life,’ he said when we were alone. ‘After three years of marriage, one evening I found a letter in which the Countess announced her flight. The letter had dignity; women preserve virtues even when committing that sin. The story now is that she went abroad on a ship that wrecked; she is supposed dead. I have lived alone seven years. Enough for this evening, Maurice. We will speak of my situation when I have grown used to speaking of it. Chronic disease needs time to accustom to improvement—which often seems another aspect of the complaint.’

I went to bed agitated; the mystery seemed more obscure. I foresaw some strange drama, for there could be no vulgar difference between the woman the Count could select and his character. The events driving the Countess from so noble, amiable, perfect, loving a man must have been singular. M. de Grandville’s remark was like a torch thrown into caverns I had long walked; though the flame dimly lit them, I perceived their extent. I could imagine the Count’s sufferings without knowing their depths. His sallow face, parched temples, overwhelming studies, moments of absentmindedness, the smallest details of this married bachelor’s life stood out in luminous relief during the hour of mental questioning—the twilight before sleep—into which I gave myself.

Oh, how I loved my poor master! He seemed sublime. I read a melancholy poem; I saw perpetual activity in a heart I had accused of torpor. Must not supreme grief at last stagnate? Had this judge, with so much power, ever revenged himself? Was he feeding on her long agony? Is it not remarkable in Paris to keep anger seething for ten years? What had Octave done since this misfortune—the separation of husband and wife is a great misfortune in our day, when domestic life has become a social question unlike before?

We allowed days to pass on watch, for great sorrows have a diffidence of their own; but at last, one evening the Count said in a grave voice:

“‘Stay.’

“This, as nearly as may be, is his story.

“My father had a ward, rich and lovely, who was sixteen when I came back from college to live in this old house. Honorine, brought up by my mother, was just awakening to life. Full of grace and childish ways, she dreamed of happiness as one dreams of jewels; perhaps happiness was to her the jewel of the soul. Her piety had a puerile side; religion itself was poetry to her ingenuous heart. She looked to the future as a perpetual fête. Innocent and pure, no delirium had disturbed her dream. Shame and grief had never tinged her cheek. She did not even inquire into the cause of her involuntary emotions on a fine spring day. She felt weak and destined to obedience, and awaited marriage without wishing for it. Her smiling imagination knew nothing of the corruption which literature depicts; she was ignorant of the world and its dangers. The dear child had suffered so little that she had not developed courage. Her guilelessness would have led her to walk among serpents like the ideal Innocence painters invent. We lived together like two brothers.

“After a year, by the basin in the garden, throwing crumbs to the fish, I asked her, ‘Would you like us to be married? With me you could do whatever you please, while another man would make you unhappy.’

“‘Mamma,’ she said to my mother, ‘Octave and I have agreed to be married—’

“‘What! at seventeen?’ my mother protested. ‘No, you must wait eighteen months; if eighteen months hence you like each other, well, your birth and fortunes are equal; you can have a suitable marriage and a love match.’

“When I was six-and-twenty and Honorine nineteen, we were married. Respect for my parents—old folks of the Bourbon Court—kept us from making the house fashionable; we lived as children. I went into society; I initiated my wife into fashion; I regarded it as my duty to instruct her.

“I recognized afterwards that marriages under such conditions carry a rock against which many affections are wrecked. The husband becomes a pedagogue and love perishes beneath the rod that sooner or later gives pain; a young, handsome wife, discreet and laughter-loving, will not accept any superiority beyond her natural grace. Perhaps I was wrong. During the difficult beginnings of a household I perhaps assumed a magisterial tone; or I trusted too entirely to that artless nature. I kept no watch over the Countess, in whom revolt seemed impossible. Alas! neither in politics nor domestic life has it been settled whether empires and happiness are wrecked by too much confidence or too much severity. Perhaps I failed to perceive Honorine’s girlish dreams? Who can tell while happy days last what precepts he has neglected?

“I remember only the broad outlines of his self-reproaches, made in the good faith of an anatomist seeking a cause missed by his brethren; his indulgence struck me as worthy of Jesus rescuing the woman taken in adultery.

“It was eighteen months after my father’s death—my mother followed in a few months—when the fearful night came which surprised me with Honorine’s farewell letter. What poetic delusion had seduced my wife? Was it sensual? Misfortune’s magnetism? Genius? I could not know. The blow was so terrible that for a month I remained stunned. Afterwards reflection counselled me to continue in ignorance; Honorine’s misfortunes have since taught me too much. So far, Maurice, the story is commonplace enough; but one word will change it all: I love Honorine; I have never ceased to worship her. From the day she left me I have lived on memory; one by one I recall the pleasures for which Honorine no doubt had no taste.

“Oh!” he cried, seeing my amazement, “do not make a hero of me; do not think me such a fool as the Colonel would say, as to have sought no diversion. Alas, my boy! I was either too young or too much in love; I have not in the whole world met another woman. After frightful struggles I tried to forget; money in hand, I stood on the threshold of infidelity, but the memory of Honorine rose before me like a white statue. I recalled the infinite delicacy of that exquisite skin, the ingenuous face, the heavenly fragrance, the light in her eyes, the prettiness of her movements, and I fled as one would from violating a tomb. At consultations, in Court, by night, I dream incessantly of Honorine; only by excessive strength of mind do I attend to business. This is the secret of my labors.

“I felt no more anger with her than a father feels seeing his child in danger. I understood I had made a poem of my wife, a poem I delighted in so intoxicatedly that I fancied she shared the intoxication. Ah! Maurice, indiscriminating passion in a husband is a mistake that may lead to any crime in a wife. I had left all the faculties of this child, loved as a child, unemployed; I had perhaps wearied her with my love before the hour of loving had struck for her. Too young to see that in the constancy of the wife lies the germ of the mother’s devotion, she mistook this first test of marriage for life itself, and the refractory child cursed life, unknown to me, out of modesty perhaps. In that cruel position she was defenceless against any man who stirred her deeply. I, so wise a judge, who have a kind heart but whose mind was absorbed, understood too late these unwritten laws of the woman’s code: I read them by the light of the fire that wrecked my roof. Then I constituted my heart a tribunal; by the law the husband is judge: I acquitted my wife and condemned myself. Love seized me as a mean, despotic passion which comes over some old men. To this day I love the absent Honorine as a man of sixty loves a woman he must possess, and yet I have the strength of a young man. I have the insolence of the old man and the reserve of a boy. Society laughs at such a desperate conjugal predicament. Where it pities a lover, it ridicules a husband who cannot keep his wife under the canopy of the Church and the Maire’s scarf. I had to keep silence.

“Serizy is happy. His indulgence allows him to see his wife; he can protect and defend her; as he adores her, he knows all the joys of a benefactor. ‘I remain married only for my wife’s sake,’ he told me.

“But I—I have nothing; I have not even to face ridicule, I who live solely on a love which is starving! I who can never find a word to say to a woman of the world! I who loathe prostitution! I who am faithful under a spell! But for my religious faith, I should have killed myself. I have thrown myself into hard work as into a gulf, and come out alive, fevered, sleepless!

“I cannot repeat all his words; passion gave him an eloquence above the pleader’s, and I, like him, felt my cheeks wet with tears. After a pause, he finished with this revelation:

“This is the drama of my soul, but it is not the living drama acted in Paris. The interior drama interests nobody. I know it; one day you will admit it. No one can burden his heart with another’s pain; the measure of suffering is in ourselves. You can imagine me calming despair by contemplating a miniature in which I see and kiss her brow and lips, breathe the whiteness of her skin; or leaping with hope and then writhing under despair, trampling Paris to quell irritation by fatigue. My life is a continual paroxysm of fear, joy, and dejection.

“As to the drama—it is this. You think I am occupied with Council of State, Chamber, Courts, politics. Seven hours at night suffice for all that; my faculties are over-wrought. Honorine is my real concern. To recover my wife is my only study: to guard her in her cage without her suspecting she is in my power; to satisfy her needs, supply the little pleasures she allows herself, to be about her like a sylph without letting her know, for if she knew the future would be lost—that is my life. For seven years I have never gone to bed without seeing the light of her night-lamp, or her shadow on the curtains.

“She left my house taking nothing but the dress she wore that day. The child carried magnanimity to folly. Eighteen months after her flight her lover deserted her, appalled by the cold, cruel aspect of poverty—the coward! The man doubtless had counted on the easy life abroad. Honorine had sixty thousand francs a year of her own. The wretch left the dear creature expecting an infant and without a penny. In November 1820 I found means to get the best accoucheur to pose as a humble suburban apothecary. I induced the parish priest to supply her needs as charity. To hide my wife, secure her against discovery, find a devoted housekeeper to be my confidante—this was a task worthy of Figaro! You may suppose that to find where my wife had taken refuge I only had to make up my mind.

“After three months of desperation rather than despair, I resolved to devote myself to Honorine with God only in my secret. Love must have its daily food. Ought I not to protect this child, whose guilt flowed from my imprudence, against fresh disaster—to fulfil my part as guardian angel? At seven months the infant died, happily for her and me. For nine months more my wife lay between life and death, deserted when she most needed a manly arm; but this arm,” he said, holding out his, “was extended over her head. Honorine was nursed as she would have been at home. When, on recovery, she asked by whom she had been assisted, she was told ‘By the Sisters of Charity, by the Maternity Society, by the parish priest.’

“This woman, whose pride is almost a vice, has shown resistance in misfortune, which on some evenings I call obstinacy. Honorine was bent on earning her living. For five years I have lodged her in the Rue Saint-Maur in a charming little house where she makes artificial flowers and articles of fashion. She believes she sells them to a shop, paid so well that she makes twenty francs a day; in six years she suspected nothing. She pays about a third of true cost, so on six thousand francs a year she lives as if she had fifteen thousand. She is devoted to flowers, and pays a gardener a hundred crowns, who costs me twelve hundred in wages, and sends me a bill for two thousand francs every three months. I have promised him a market-garden on land I hold in the name of a clerk. The smallest indiscretion would ruin him. Honorine has a little house, a garden, and a splendid hothouse for five hundred francs a year. There she lives under the name of her housekeeper, Madame Gobain, an old woman of discretion I found, whose affection Honorine has won. But their zeal is kept hot by the promise of reward. The porter and his wife cost me dreadfully dear for the same reasons. However, for three years Honorine has been happy, believing she owes her comfort to her own toil.

“Yes,” he cried, reading the question in my eyes, “I have made the attempt. My wife had been in the Faubourg Saint-Antoine. One day, when I thought of reconciliation, I wrote a letter, re-written twenty times! I went from the Rue Payenne to the Rue de Reuilly like a condemned man. It was dark and foggy; I met Madame Gobain who was to tell me of my wife. Honorine, on recognizing my writing, had thrown the letter into the fire without reading it. What a dagger-stroke to a man who delights in the trickery by which he gets fine Lyons velvet cheaply, a pheasant, a fish, fruit, for a tenth of their value, for a woman ignorant enough to think two hundred and fifty francs ample wages for a cook fit for a bishop.

“You have sometimes found me rubbing my hands in a kind of happiness. Well, I had just succeeded in some ruse worthy of the stage—I had sent her an Indian shawl by a purchaser as the property of an actress who had hardly worn it. My life can be summed up in two words: I love and I wait! I have in Madame Gobain a faithful spy on the heart I worship. I go every evening to chat with the old woman to hear all Honorine has done; a single exclamation might betray the secrets of that soul which is wilfully deaf and dumb. Honorine is pious; she attends church and prays, but she has never been to confession or taken Communion; she foresees what a priest would tell her. She will not listen to advice or an injunction to return to me. This horror of me overwhelms me; I have never done her the smallest harm. I have been kind. Granting I may have been hasty in teaching her, that my man’s irony may have hurt her girlish pride, is that reason for persisting in a determination only implacable hatred could inspire? Honorine has never told Madame Gobain who she is; she keeps absolute silence as to her marriage, so the worthy woman can never speak a word in my favor. The others know nothing; they live in awe of the Prefect of Police and their respect for a Minister’s power. Hence it is impossible for me to penetrate that heart; the citadel is mine, but I cannot get in. An act of violence would ruin me for ever.

“How can I argue against reasons I do not know? Should I write a letter and have it copied and laid before Honorine? That would risk a third removal. The last cost me fifty thousand francs. The purchase was made in the name of the secretary whom you succeeded. The unhappy man, who did not know how lightly I sleep, was detected by me opening a box with the private agreement; I coughed, and he panicked; next day I compelled him to sell the house to the man in whose name it now stands, and I turned him out.

“If it were not that I feel my noblest faculties satisfied—if my part were not that of divine fatherhood; if I did not drink in delight by every pore—there are moments when I should believe I was a monomaniac. Sometimes at night I hear the jingling bells of madness. I dread the violent transitions from faint hope to complete despair. A few days since I was considering the horrible end of Lovelace and Clarissa Harlowe, and saying to myself, if Honorine were the mother of a child of mine, must she not necessarily return under her husband’s roof?

“I have such faith in a happy future that ten months ago I bought and paid for one of the handsomest houses in the Faubourg Saint-Honoré. If I win her back I will not allow her to see that house again nor the room from which she fled. I mean to place my idol in a new temple. That house is being made a marvel of taste. I have been told of a poet who, mad with love for an actress, bought the handsomest bed without knowing how she would reward him. Well, one of the coldest of lawyers was stirred to the depths by that anecdote. The orator of the Legislative Chamber can understand the poet who feeds his ideal on material possibilities. I love as a poet—as an emperor!”

“As I heard the last words, I believed Count Octave’s fears were realized; he rose and walked, gesticulating, then stopped as if shocked by his own vehemence. ‘I am very ridiculous,’ he added after a long pause, looking at me as if craving pity.

“‘No, monsieur, you are very unhappy.’

“‘Ah yes!’ he went on. ‘From my speech you must believe in the intensity of a physical passion which for nine years has absorbed me; but that is nothing compared with the worship I feel for the soul, mind, and heart in that woman. By retrospection I now see the graces of Honorine’s mind and heart, which I paid little heed to while happy. Day by day I appreciate the extent of my loss, discovering the exquisite gifts of that capricious and refractory young creature who has grown so strong and proud under poverty and the shock of cowardly desertion. And that heavenly blossom is fading in solitude and hiding! The law we spoke of,’ he went on with bitter irony, ‘is a squad of gendarmes—my wife seized and dragged away by force! Would that be to triumph over a corpse? Religion has no hold on her; she craves its poetry and prays, but she does not listen to the Church’s commandments. I have exhausted mercy, kindness, love; I am at my wits’ end. Only one chance of victory is left—the cunning and patience with which bird-catchers at last entrap the wariest birds. Hence, Maurice, when M. de Grandville’s indiscretion betrayed to you the secret of my life, I regarded that incident as a decree of fate. Have you enough affection for me to show me romantic devotion?’

“‘I see what you are coming to, Monsieur le Comte,’ said I; ‘I guess your purpose. Your first secretary tried to open your deed box. I know the heart of your second—he might fall in love with your wife. Can you devote him to destruction by sending him into the fire? Can any one put his hand into a brazier without burning it?’

“‘You are a foolish boy,’ replied the Count. ‘I will send you well gloved. It is no secretary of mine that will be lodged in the Rue Saint-Maur in the little garden-house I have at his disposal. It is my distant cousin, Baron de l’Hostal, a lawyer high in office...’

“After a moment the gate bell rang, and a carriage came in. The footman announced Madame de Courteville and her daughter. The Count’s cousin, the widow of a judge, had a daughter and no fortune. What could a woman of nine-and-twenty be in comparison with a young girl of twenty as lovely as an ideal mistress?

“‘Baron, Master of Appeals till you get something better, and this old house settled on her—would you not have enough reasons for not falling in love with the Countess?’ he whispered, taking my hand and introducing me to Madame de Courteville and her daughter.

“I was dazzled, not so much by these advantages as by Amélie de Courteville’s beauty and her costuming designed to secure a marriage.

“But I will not talk of myself,” the Consul resumed.

“Three weeks later I went to live in the gardener’s cottage, cleaned, repaired, and furnished with the celerity explained by three words: Paris; French workmen; money! I was as much in love as the Count could desire as a security. Would prudence in a young man of five-and-twenty be equal to the part I undertook, involving a friend’s happiness? I confess I counted on my uncle’s advice, for I had been authorized by the Count to take him into confidence if I deemed it necessary. I engaged a garden; I devoted myself to horticulture; I worked frantically, turning up the soil, appropriating the ground to flowers. Like maniacs of Holland or England, I gave out I was devoted to one flower and especially grew dahlias. You will understand that my conduct, even in small details, was laid down by the Count, whose intellect was directed to the trifling incidents of the tragi-comedy in Rue Saint-Maur. As soon as the Countess had gone to bed, at about eleven, Octave, Madame Gobain, and I sat in council. I heard the old woman’s report: everything the Countess had done. He inquired into her meals, occupations, frame of mind, the flowers she aimed to imitate. I understood what love in despair may be when it is the threefold passion of heart, mind, and senses. Octave lived only for that hour.

“During two months, while my garden work lasted, I never set eyes on the little house where my fair neighbor dwelt. I had not inquired whether I had a neighbor, though the Countess’ garden was divided from mine by a paling, along which she had planted cypress trees already four feet high. One morning Madame Gobain announced as disastrous news the intention, expressed by an eccentric neighbor, to build a wall between the gardens at the end of the year. I will say nothing of the curiosity which consumed me to see the Countess! The wish almost extinguished my budding love for Amélie. A wall would cut away three-quarters of the hatchet-shaped plot and leave Honorine’s garden a narrow alley.

“The Countess was in despair.

“‘My good Gobain,’ she said, ‘what sort of man is this florist?’

“‘I do not know whether it is possible to tame him. He seems to have a horror of women. He is the nephew of a Paris curé. I have seen the uncle once; a fine old man of sixty, very ugly but amiable. It is possible the priest encourages his nephew’s love of flowers, as they say in the neighborhood, that nothing worse may happen—’

“‘Well?’ asked the Countess.

“‘He is a little cracked,’ said Gobain, tapping her head.

“A harmless lunatic is the only man no woman distrusts in matters of sentiment. You will see how wise the Count had been in choosing this disguise for me.

“‘What ails him then?’ asked the Countess.

“‘He has studied too hard,’ replied Gobain; ‘he has turned misanthropic. If you want to know all that is said about him—’

“‘Madmen frighten me less than sane folks; I will speak to him myself! Tell him I beg him to come here. If I do not succeed, I will send for the curé.’

“The day after, as I walked my graveled path, I caught sight of the half-open curtains on the Countess’ first-floor window and a woman’s face peeping out. Gobain called me. I glanced at the Countess’ house and, by a rude shrug, expressed, ‘What do I care for your mistress?’

“‘Madame,’ said Gobain, ‘the madman bid me leave him in peace, saying that even a charcoal seller is master in his own premises, especially when he has no wife.’

“‘He is right,’ said the Countess.

“‘Yes, but he ended by saying, “I will go,” when I told him he would distress a lady living in retirement who found her solace in growing flowers.’

“The next day a signal from Gobain informed me that I was expected. After the Countess’ breakfast, when she walked before her house, I broke out some palings and went towards her. I had dressed as a countryman in old gray flannel trousers, heavy wooden shoes, shabby shooting coat, a peaked cap, a ragged bandana, hands soiled with mould, and a dibble in my hand.

“‘Madame,’ said the housekeeper, ‘this good man is your neighbor.’

“The Countess was not alarmed. I saw at last the woman whose conduct and her husband’s confidences had made me so curious. It was early May. The air was pure, the spring verdure and fragrance a fitting setting for this creature of sorrow. As I saw Honorine I understood Octave’s passion.

“Her pallor struck me by its peculiar tone of white—for there are as many tones of white as of red. Her skin showed the blood in blue veins; at the slightest emotion a rosy flush came. The sunshine through the acacias shed on Honorine a pale gold like that Raphael and Titian enwrap their Virgins in. Her brown eyes expressed tenderness and vivacity; their brightness seemed reflected through long lashes. By lifting her eyelids she could spellbind; there was feeling, dignity, terror, or contempt in her way of raising or dropping those veils of the soul. She could freeze or give life by a look. Her light-brown hair, carelessly knotted, outlined a poet’s brow, high, powerful, dreamy. The mouth was voluptuous. All the lines and forms of the head had a stamp of nobleness defying time.

“Though slight, Honorine was not thin; her figure might revive love when it believed itself exhausted. She perfectly represented the word mignonne—pliant like a kitten. Her small feet made a light inherent sound that harmonized with the rustle of her dress, producing a feminine music stamped on the heart. Her gait bore the quarterings of her race with pride, so even a working man would make way in the street. Gay and tender, haughty and imposing, she remained a child. But a child who might be as strong as an angel; once hurt, she would be implacable.

“Coldness on that face must be death to those she had smiled on. Inhaling the perfume of violets about her, I understood how the memory of this wife arrested the Count on the threshold of debauchery, and how impossible to forget a creature who was a flower to the touch, the eye, and the soul. Honorine inspired chivalrous devotion irrespective of reward. A man seeing her must say to himself: ‘Think, and I will divine your thought; speak, and I will obey. If my life sacrificed in torments can procure you one day’s happiness, take my life; I will smile like a martyr.’

“Many women study expression and succeed in producing similar effects; in her it was all outcome of delightful nature. If I tell you this, it is because her soul, thoughts, and heart’s exquisiteness are what we are concerned with, and you would blame me if I had not sketched them.

“I was near forgetting my part as a half-crazy lout, clumsy and hardly chivalrous.

“‘I am told, madame, that you are fond of flowers?’

“‘I am an artificial flower-maker,’ she said. ‘After growing flowers I imitate them, like a mother painting her children. That is enough to say I am poor and unable to pay for the concession I desire from you.’

“‘But how,’ said I, grave as a judge, ‘can a lady of such rank ply so humble a calling? Have you, like me, good reasons for employing your hands so as to keep your brain from working?’

“‘Let us stick to the question of the wall,’ she said with a smile.

“‘We have begun at the foundations,’ I replied. ‘Must I not know which of us ought to yield to the other in behalf of our suffering, or, if you choose, our mania? Oh! what a charming clump of narcissus!’

“She had made a perfect museum of flowers and shrubs, arranged as an artist. The retired garden breathed comforting scents and suggested sweet thoughts and graceful pictures. It showed the sign-manual of our true character stamped on all when nothing compels hypocrisy. I looked at the narcissus and at the Countess, pretending to be more in love with the flowers than with her to keep up my part.

“‘So you are fond of flowers?’ she asked.

“‘They are the only beings that never disappoint our care and affection.’ I delivered a diatribe comparing botany and the world, ending miles from the dividing wall. The Countess naturally brought me back to the point; women not in love have the cold blood of an experienced attorney.

“‘If you insist on my leaving the paling,’ I said, ‘you will learn all the secrets of gardening I want to hide; I am seeking a blue dahlia, a blue rose; I am crazy for blue flowers. Is not blue the favorite color of superior souls? We are neither of us at home; we might as well make a door of open railings to unite our gardens. You too are fond of flowers; you will see mine, I shall see yours. If you receive no visitors, I have none but my uncle, the Curé of the White Friars.’

“‘No,’ she said, ‘I will give you the right to come into my garden at any hour. You will always be admitted as a neighbor with whom I hope to keep good terms. But I like my solitude too well to burden it with loss of independence.’

“‘As you please,’ said I, and leaped the paling.

“‘Now, of what use would a door be?’ I cried from my domain, mocking her with a madman’s grimace.

“For a fortnight I seemed not to notice my neighbor. Towards the end of May, one lovely evening we were both walking opposite sides of the paling and exchanged civil words. Finding me in deep dejection she spoke to me of hope in short, nurse-like sentences. I leaped the fence and found myself at her side. The Countess led me into the house, wishing to subdue my sadness. So I penetrated the sanctuary where everything harmonized with the woman I had tried to describe.

“Exquisite simplicity reigned. The interior was a dainty box of eighteenth-century art: the dining-room frescoed with garlands, staircase in monochrome, a faded drawing-room hung with tapestry panels, a bath, and upstairs a single bedroom with dressing-room and a library she used as a workroom. The kitchen was in the basement. The balcony’s garlands hid the roof; only lead cornices were visible. In this retreat one was a hundred leagues from Paris.

“But for a bitter smile that occasionally played on those red lips, one might have believed the violet hidden in her thicket of flowers happy. In a few days we reached intimacy from close neighborhood and the Countess’ conviction I was indifferent to women. A look would have spoilt all, and I never allowed thought of her to appear in my eyes. Honorine regarded me as an old friend; her manner was pity. Her looks, voice, and words showed she was far from coquetry. She soon gave me the right to enter the pretty workshop where she made flowers, a retreat full of books and curiosities, as smart as a boudoir where elegance emphasized the vulgarity of her tools. She had poetized a manufacture.

“Perhaps of all the work a woman can do, making artificial flowers lets her display the most grace. Coloring prints requires stooping; embroidery brings consumption or curvature; engraving music is laborious; sewing earns little. But making flowers and light articles needs a variety of movements and ideas that keep a pretty woman in her sphere; she may chat, laugh, sing, or think.

“There was feeling for art in how she arranged the myriad-colored petals on her long table. The saucers of color were clean and orderly; a little cabinet held tiny moulds; a fine Japanese bowl the paste; wire lay in a drawer. A Venetian glass held the living model. She had a passion for achievement, attempting difficult racemes and corollas. Her hands were swift as her thoughts; her fingers seemed fairy-like in the twisting, fitting, and pressure required. She adapted each motion with the lucidity of instinct.

“I could not tire of admiring her shaping a flower, padding the wire, adjusting leaves. She displayed the genius of a painter, copying faded flowers and yellowing leaves, struggling even with wildflowers, artless yet elaborate.

“‘This art,’ she said, ‘is in its infancy. If Parisian women had some of the genius the harem slavery draws out in Oriental women, they would lend a complete language of flowers to their wreaths. To please my taste I have made drooping flowers with leaves of Florentine bronze for a young woman whose life is a failure—poetical fitness. How many things a woman might express by her headdress! Are there not flowers for drunken Bacchantes, flowers for stern bigots, pensive flowers for bored women? Botany may express every sensation and thought, even the most subtle.’

“She employed me to stamp leaves, cut material, and prepare wires. My affected desire for occupation made me skilful. We talked as we worked; when idle I read new books to her, keeping up my part as a man weary of life, gloomy and misanthropic. My person led to banter as to my physical resemblance—save his club-foot—to Lord Byron. It was tacitly admitted that her troubles outweighed mine, though my causes for misanthropy might have satisfied Job.

“I will not speak of the shame which tormented me as I inflicted false wounds on my heart to excite her compassion. I soon appreciated my devotedness by estimating the baseness of a spy. Her sympathy would have comforted any great grief. This charming creature, weaned from the world and alone for years, offered kindnesses with childlike effusiveness and such compassion as would fill with bitterness any profligate who fell in love with her; for alas, it was all charity and pity. Her renunciation of love and dread of what is called happiness, she proclaimed with equal vehemence. These days proved to me that a woman’s friendship is far superior to her love.

“I suffered my revelations being dragged from me with grimaces as a young lady allows before sitting at the piano. The Countess strengthened our intimacy; she found in me a counterpart of her antipathy to love, and I fancied she was content with being a Man Friday on her desert island. Solitude perhaps weighed on her. There was nothing coquette; she told me she had no heart except in the ideal world. I compared these two lives: his, activity and agitation; hers, inaction and quiescence. My misanthropy let me utter cynical sallies against men and women, hoping to bring her to confidence; she was not to be caught, and I began to understand the mulish obstinacy commoner among women than assumed.

“‘The Orientals are right,’ I said one evening, ‘when they shut you up and regard you as playthings. Europe has been punished for admitting you to society and accepting you on an equal footing. Woman is the most dishonorable and cowardly being, and that is where her charm lies. Where would be pleasure in hunting a tame thing? Once a woman inspires a man’s passion, she is for him forever sacred. Men’s gratitude for past delights is eternal, though the woman may forget. You all have a feeling a popular calumny ascribes to the Lady of Nesle: “What a pity we cannot live on love as on fruit, and that when we have had our fill, nothing survives but remembrance!”’

“‘God has, no doubt, reserved such perfect bliss for Paradise,’ she said. ‘But if your argument is witty, it is false. What can those women be who give themselves up to a succession of loves?’ she asked, looking at me like the Virgin in Ingres’ picture.

“‘You are an actress in good faith,’ I said. ‘Still, lovely as you are, you have loved; ergo, you forget.’

“‘I!’ she cried, evading. ‘I am not a woman. I am a nun, and seventy-two years old!’

“‘Then how can you assert you feel more keenly than I? Sorrow has one form for women; the only misfortunes they regard are disappointments of the heart.’

“She looked at me sweetly and, like many women caught between truth’s issues, persisted in her wilfulness.

“‘I am a nun,’ she said, ‘and you talk to me of a world where I shall never again set foot.’

“‘Not even in thought?’ I asked.

“‘Is the world so desirable?’ she replied. ‘Oh! my mind wanders higher. The angel of perfection, the beautiful Gabriel, often sings in my heart. If I were rich I should work to stop myself soaring on many-tinted wings. There are meditations which ruin us women. I owe much peace of mind to my flowers, though sometimes they fail. On some days an idea possesses me that some great event is impending and life will change; I stare into darkness, dislike my work, and after many fatigues find everyday life again. Is this a warning from heaven? I ask myself—’

“After three months of this struggle between two diplomats—youthful melancholy and a woman whose disgust of life made her invulnerable—I told the Count it was impossible to drag this tortoise out of her shell; it must be broken. The evening before, in our last friendly discussion, the Countess had exclaimed: ‘Lucretia’s dagger wrote in letters of blood the watchword of woman’s charter: Liberty!’

“From that moment the Count left me free to act.

“‘I have been paid a hundred francs for the flowers and caps I made this week!’ Honorine exclaimed gleefully one Saturday evening when I visited her in the ground-floor sitting-room which the unavowed proprietor had had regilt.

“It was ten o’clock. July twilight and a glorious moon lent a misty light. Gusts of perfume soothed the soul; the Countess clinked five gold pieces given to her by a suppositious dealer in frippery, another of Octave’s accomplices, M. Popinot.

“‘I earn my living by amusing myself,’ she said; ‘I am free, when men armed with laws have tried to make us slaves. I have transports of pride every Saturday! I like M. Gaudissart’s gold pieces as much as Lord Byron liked Mr. Murray’s.’

“‘This is not becoming in a woman,’ I said.

“‘Pooh! Am I a woman? I am a boy with a soft soul; a boy whom no woman can torture—’

“‘Your life is the negation of your being,’ said I. ‘What? you, on whom God lavished His choicest treasures, do you never wish—’

“‘For what?’ she asked, disturbed by a speech that for the first time had contradicted my assumed part.

“‘For a pretty little child, with curling hair, running among the flowers, calling you mother!’

“A prolonged silence revealed the effect of my words. Leaning on her sofa, the Countess did not faint but froze under a nervous attack she afterwards compared to an insidious poison. I called Madame Gobain, who laid her mistress on the bed, unlaced and undressed her, restoring not life but consciousness of dreadful suffering. I walked the path behind the house weeping and doubting. I wished to give up this bird-catcher’s part I had rashly assumed. Madame Gobain came down and found me with my face wet with tears, then hurried up to say:

“‘Monsieur Maurice is crying like a child.’

“Roused by the evil interpretation our mutual behavior might attract, the Countess summoned strength to put on a wrapper and come down to me.

“‘You are not the cause of this attack,’ she said. ‘I am subject to these spasms, a sort of cramp of the heart—’

“‘And will you not tell me of your troubles?’ I asked, wiping my tears. ‘Have you not told me you were a mother and unhappy at losing your child?’

“‘Marie!’ she called. Gobain came in.

“‘Bring lights and some tea,’ she ordered, with the calm decision of a Mylady in armor of pride.

“When the housekeeper had lighted tapers and closed shutters, the Countess showed a mute countenance; pride and gravity reasserted themselves. She said:

“‘Do you know why I like Lord Byron so much? It is because he suffered as animals do. Of what use are complaints when they are not elegy, mockery like Don Juan, or reverie like Childe Harold? Nothing shall be known of me. My heart is a poem I lay before God.’

“‘If I chose—’ I began.

“‘If?’ she repeated.

“‘I have no interest in anything,’ I replied, ‘so I cannot be inquisitive; but if I chose, I could know all your secrets by to-morrow.’

“‘I defy you!’ she exclaimed with ill-disguised uneasiness.

“‘Seriously?’

“‘Certainly,’ she said, tossing her head. ‘If such a crime is possible I ought to know it.’

“‘First, madame,’ I went on, pointing to her hands, ‘those pretty fingers show they were not made for toil. Then you call yourself Madame Gobain, yet you said to Marie: “Here, this is for you?” Marie is the real Madame Gobain; you conceal your name behind your housekeeper’s. Fear nothing from me. You have in me the most devoted friend you will ever have: a friend I give the sacred and pathetic meaning to—the friend who will defend you against everything, and only wishes you to be as happy as such a woman ought to be. Who can tell whether the pain I caused was not voluntary?’

“‘Yes,’ she replied with threatening audacity, ‘I insist on it. Be curious and tell me all you can find out about me; but,’ and she held up her finger, ‘you must also tell by what means you obtain your information. The preservation of the small happiness I enjoy here depends on the steps you take.’

“‘That means you will fly—’

“‘On wings!’ she cried, ‘to the New World—’

“‘Where you will be at the mercy of brutal passions you would inspire,’ I interrupted. ‘Is it not the essence of genius and beauty to shine and excite desires? Paris is a desert with Bedouins; it is the only place to hide among those who work for a livelihood. What have you to complain of? Who am I? An additional servant—M. Gobain, that is all. If you have to fight a duel you may need a second.’

“‘Never mind; find out who I am. I have already said I insist. Now I beg that you will,’ she added with the grace all ladies can command.

“‘Well then, to-morrow, at the same hour, I will tell you what I may have discovered,’ I replied. ‘But do not therefore hate me! Will you behave like other women?’

“‘What do other women do?’

“‘They lay upon us immense sacrifices, and when we have made them they reproach us later as if for an injury.’

“‘They are right if the thing required appears to be a sacrifice!’ she replied pointedly.

“‘Instead of sacrifices say efforts and—’

“‘It would be an impertinence,’ she said.

“‘Forgive me,’ I replied. ‘I forget that woman and the Pope are infallible.’

“‘Good heavens!’ she said after a long pause, ‘only two words would be enough to destroy the peace so dearly bought, which I enjoy like a fraud—’

“She rose. ‘Where can I go? What is to become of me? Must I leave this quiet retreat that I arranged to end my days in?’

“‘To end your days!’ I exclaimed. ‘Has it never struck you that a time would come when you could no longer work, when competition will lower the price of flowers and articles of fashion—?’

“‘I have already saved a thousand crowns,’ she said.

“‘Heavens! what privations such a sum must represent!’ I cried.

“‘Leave me,’ she said, ‘till to-morrow. This evening I am not myself; I must be alone. Must I not save my strength in case of disaster? If you should learn anything, others would be informed, and then—Good-night,’ she added shortly, dismissing me with an imperious gesture.

“The battle is tomorrow,” I repeated with a smile to keep up my air of indifference. As I walked down the avenue the words ran through my mind.

Octave’s anxiety matched Honorine’s. The Count and I stayed together until two in the morning, pacing by the Bastille trenches like generals on the eve of a fight, measuring chances and searching for a decisive moment. These two beings, split inwardly, would each lie awake—one hoping, the other dreading reunion. True drama is in feeling, in the Spiritual World. Octave and Honorine lived there.

I was punctual. At ten I was shown into a charming white-and-blue bedroom—the nest of this wounded dove. The Countess looked at me and, struck dumb by my respectful demeanour, was about to speak.

“Madame la Comtesse,” I said gravely. She sank into her chair, grief stamped on her face in a pose I could have wished painted.

“You are the wife of a noble, respected man; great in the world and greater in his conduct to you. You and he are lofty natures. Where do you suppose you live?”

“In my own house,” she answered, astonished.

“In Count Octave’s,” I said. “You have been deceived. M. Lenormand, the usher, is only a screen for your husband. The seclusion you enjoy is the Count’s doing; the money you spend comes from him; his protection extends to every detail. He has saved you before the world; he invented reasons for your disappearance—the Cécile wreck, the journey to Havana for an aunt’s fortune; he sent agents and received hopeful letters. He takes precautions to hide you as you hide yourself; he obeys you.”

“That is enough,” she said. “From whom do you know all this?”

“My uncle got a penniless youth a place as secretary to the Commissary of Police. That young man told me everything. If you leave this house tonight, your husband will know where you have gone—his care will follow you. How could you believe shopkeepers buy flowers at their sale price? Ask a thousand crowns for a bouquet and you will get it. No mother’s ingenuity equaled your husband’s. The porter told me the Count often comes behind the fence at night to see the glimmer of your lamp. Your cashmere shawl cost six thousand francs—your old-clothes-seller brings you fresh things from the best makers. You live like Venus in Vulcan’s toils; you are alone in a prison fashioned by sublime magnanimity for seven years.”

The Countess trembled like a trapped swallow. She studied me defiantly, her dry eyes glittering; then she wept—not from touch, but from helplessness. She had been proud of her independence; marriage pressed on her like a cell.

“I will go!” she cried through her tears. “He forces me; I will go where no one will surely follow.”

“What? You would kill yourself? Madame, you must have powerful reasons to refuse Comte Octave.”

“Certainly I have.”

“Tell them to me, to my uncle. We will be devoted advisers. My uncle is a priest in the confessional, but a man in company. We will hear you; perhaps we can clear a misapprehension. If you are guiltless, we will say so; if you have wronged, your fault may be expiated. In me you have a sincere friend. If you wish to evade the Count’s tyranny, I will find you means—he shall not find you.”

“There is always a convent,” she said.

“Yes. But the Count, as Minister, can procure your rejection from any convent. Even so, I will save you—only when you prove you cannot and ought not to return. Do not fear that you will escape his power only to fall into mine. You shall have peace, solitude, independence, as if you were an ugly old maid. I myself would never see you without your consent.”

“And how? By what means?”

“That is my secret. I am not deceiving you. Prove this is the only life you can lead, better than being Comtesse Octave—rich, admired, in the finest house, beloved by your husband, a happy mother—and I will decide for you.”

“But will there never be a man who understands me?”

“No. And that is why I appeal to religion to decide. The Cure of the White Friars is a saint of seventy-five. My uncle is not a Grand Inquisitor; he is Saint John—Fénelon to you.”

“No, monsieur, the convent is my last hope. There is none but God who understands me. No man, not Augustine, could enter into the scruples of my conscience, which form Dante’s circles, whence there is no escape. Another than my husband has had all my love. No, he did not take it; I gave it as a mother gives a child a toy it breaks. For me there cannot be two loves. In some natures love is not a trial; it is, or it is not. That eighteen months for me were like eighteen years; I poured myself into them, and they left me exhausted. The cup of happiness is broken. I have no weapons left. I had but one name—Honorine—and one heart. My husband had the young girl; a worthless lover had the woman—nothing is left. Then let myself be loved! That is the idea you mean to offer. But I rebel at being a prostitute! I could imagine surrendering to another man’s love, but to Octave’s? No, never.”

“You love him.”

“I esteem, respect, venerate him; he has done me no harm; he is kind and tender; but I can never love him. We will talk no more. I will put my thoughts in writing; I am suffocating, feverish; all I see reminds me of things I wish to forget. I must fly as I fled my home.”

“Where will you go? Can a woman exist unprotected? At thirty, in glory and beauty and tenderness to give, are you prepared to live where I could hide you? Be at ease. The Count, who for nine years has never shown himself here, will never go there without your permission. Your nine years of his devotion guarantee your tranquillity. Discuss the future with my uncle and me. My uncle weighs the hearts of kings; he will be as tender as your flowers.”

I left the Countess at midnight; she seemed calm but depressed, with a purpose none could guess. I found the Count a few paces off in the Rue Saint-Maur. Drawn by irresistible attraction, he had quit the rendezvous on the Boulevards.

“What a night my poor child will suffer!” he cried when I finished. “Suppose I went to her! Suppose she saw me suddenly?”

“At this moment she might throw herself out of the window,” I replied. “The Countess is one of those Lucretias who could not survive such violence, even into the arms of a man she could embrace.”

“You are young,” he said. “In a soul tossed by such alternatives the will is like a lake lashed by storm; the wind changes and the waves drive alternately to each shore. Tonight it is as likely that on seeing me she might rush into my arms as that she would throw herself out.”

“And you would accept those chances?”

“Well, come,” he said, “I have at home a dose of opium from Desplein to send me to sleep without risk.”

Next day Gobain brought word that the Countess had taken a soothing draught and slept from six.

This is her letter, of which I kept a copy—for you, mademoiselle,” said the Consul to Camille. “Literature may simulate pathos, but nothing is so terrible as truth. Here is the letter written by anguish:

MONSIEUR MAURICE,—

I know what your uncle would say; he is not better informed than my conscience. Conscience is God’s interpreter to man. I know that if I do not reconcile with Octave I shall be damned; that is religious law. Civil law condemns me to obey. If my husband does not reject me, the world will regard me as pure whatever I have done. Marriage has this sublimity; society ratifies the husband’s forgiveness, but forgiveness must be accepted. Religiously, legally, socially I ought to return. Keeping to the human aspect, is it not cruel to refuse him happiness, to deprive him of children, to blot his name from the Golden Book and the peers? My selfish suffering ought to be sacrificed to family. I would be a mother; the caresses of my child would wipe tears; I would be looked up to. So God, law, world are in accord.

“What are you rebelling against?” heaven and the pulpit and the judge ask. Your uncle would speak of celestial grace that floods the heart when one does duty.

God, law, world, and Octave desire my life. If there is no other difficulty, my reply cuts the knot: I will not live. I will become white and innocent in death. This is not mulish obstinacy. That obstinacy in a woman results from confidence, from a vision of the future. Though my husband may forget all, I shall not. Does forgetfulness depend on will? When a widow remarries love makes a girl; but I cannot love Count Octave. Each time my eyes met his I should see my sin, even when his eyes are full of love. His generosity would measure my crime. My eyes would read an invisible condemnation. Marriage cannot give me the cruel rapture of passion. I would kill my husband by coldness, by comparisons he might guess. I might be misled by suspicion; I might die, perhaps by my own oversusceptibility or by believing some innocent sign a reproach.

What torture for both! Octave would doubt me, I doubt him. I would give him a rival unworthy of him, a man I despise, with whom I had raptures branded like fire—my shame, which I cannot forget.

Have I shown my heart enough? No one can convince me love may be renewed; I neither can nor will accept love from anyone. A young bride is a plucked flower; a guilty wife is a flower trodden underfoot. You, florist, know whether a broken stem can be restored, whether bruised corolla can be smoothed. If a botanist could remake a flower, he would be God! God alone can remake me. I drink my bitter cup of expiation; but expiation is not annihilation.

In my little house, alone, I eat bread soaked with tears; but no one sees me weep. If I go back to Octave I must hide my tears—they would offend him. How many virtues must a woman trample, not to give herself, but to restore herself to a betrayed husband? God alone can count them. A woman has courage if the husband knows nothing; she deceives to secure him double happiness. But common knowledge is degrading. If I could exchange humiliation for ecstasy, would Octave feel my consent was sheer depravity? Marriage rests on esteem, on mutual sacrifices; but neither Octave nor I could esteem each other after reunion. He would love me like an old man loves a courtesan; I should forever feel shame, a chattel instead of a lady. I would represent pleasure, not virtue. These are the bitter fruits of sin. I have made a bed of burning coals, a sleepless pillow.

Here, when I suffer, I bless my sufferings. But in my husband’s house I should be full of terror, tasting joys I have no right to. All this is feeling of a soul hollowed by seven years of suffering. Religion gives answers: this suffering is my punishment, and God will give me strength. This argument suits pious souls with energy I lack. I have chosen between this hell, where God does not forbid my blessing Him, and the hell that awaits me under Count Octave’s roof.

One word more. If I were still a girl, with my experience, my husband would be the man I should choose; that is why I refuse. I could not bear to blush before him. What! always on my knees, he standing upright; if we reversed positions I should scorn him! I will not be better treated by him because of my sin. The angel who could venture on liberties permissible when both are blameless is not on earth. Octave is full of delicate feeling, but even in his generous soul there is no guarantee for the new life I should lead with him.

Come then and tell me where you will find the solitude and peace you promised.

I had copied the letter and went to the Rue Payenne. Anxiety had conquered opium. Octave paced his garden like a madman.

“Answer that!” I said, handing him the letter. “Try to reassure the modesty of experience. It’s harder than conquering the curiosity of ignorance.”

“She is mine!” cried the Count, joy flooding his face as he read. He signed me away, to be alone. I understood extreme happiness and extreme pain obey the same laws. I went to receive Madame de Courteville and Amélie, who dined with the Count that day. Seeing Amélie, I reflected on love’s three aspects. The woman who could inspire perfect love is rare. Comparing Amélie with Honorine, the erring wife seemed more attractive than the pure girl. To Honorine fidelity had been inevitable; Amélie would solemnly promise without grasping the weight of promise. The crushed woman seemed sublime; she demanded all a man’s generosity and strength, giving zest to happiness; Amélie, chaste and confiding, would settle into peaceful motherhood without struggle or victory.

Of the plains of Champagne and the storm-swept Alps, what young man would choose the chalky level? Such comparisons are wrong on the threshold of marriage. Experience teaches that marriage excludes passion; a family cannot rest on tempests of love. At twenty-five I did not trust myself; I took a manful resolution.

I returned to the Count to announce his relations’ arrival and found him young again in hope.

“What ails you, Maurice?” he asked, struck by my changed mien.

“Monsieur le Comte—”

“No longer Octave? You to whom I owe my life—”

“My dear Octave, if you succeed in bringing back the Countess, she must never see me again; she must never know Maurice was your secretary. Never mention my name, or all will be undone. You gave me an appointment as Maître des Requêtes—get me instead a diplomatic post abroad, a consulship, and don’t think of my marrying Amélie. I will play my part to the end.”

“Poor boy!” he said, pressing my hand, holding back tears.

We then agreed my role that evening at Honorine’s house, where I returned. It was August; the day had been hot. She sat on a wooden bench under an arbor in white muslin with blue bows, hair unadorned, feet on a small stool, and did not rise; she motioned me to sit.

“Now, is not life at a deadlock for me?” she said.

“Life as you have made it,” I answered. “But not the life I propose. If you choose, you may be very happy.”

“How?” she asked, her whole being a question.

“Your letter is in the Count’s hands.” She started like a frightened doe, paced the garden, then returned to the drawing-room where I joined her after giving time to the pain.

“You—a friend? Say rather a traitor! A spy sent by my husband.”

“Instinct is keen in women. You wanted an answer, and there was but one man in the world who could write it. You must read his reply, and if after reading it you still think your life a deadlock, the spy will prove a friend; I will place you in a convent beyond the Count’s power. But consider the other side. There is a law, divine and human, which even hatred obeys—do not condemn the accused without hearing his defence. Till now you have been a child, condemning with ears stopped. The devotion of seven years has claims. You must read the Count’s answer, sent through my uncle. He will bring it. In his presence and mine, out of respect for your dignity, you must read it; you must make this sacrifice to the world, to the law, and to God.”

Seeing no attack on her resolve, she consented. Months of work had built to this moment. The Count had set his hopes on it and had reached it.

I remember nothing more formidable than my uncle’s entrance into that Pompadour drawing-room at ten. His fine head, silver hair against black dress, and calm face had a magical effect. The Countess felt balm on her wounds and beamed at his reflected virtue.

“Monsieur the Cure of the White Friars,” said Gobain.

“Are you come with a message of happiness and peace?” I asked.

“Happiness and peace are found in obedience to the Church,” replied my uncle, and he handed the Countess the following letter:

MY DEAR HONORINE,—

If you had read the letter I wrote five years ago you would have spared yourself five years of useless labor and privations. In it I proposed an arrangement to relieve your fears and make domestic life possible. I have much to reproach myself with; in seven years of sorrow I discovered errors. I misunderstood marriage and failed to scent danger when it threatened you. An angel was in the house. The Lord bade me guard it, and punished me for my audacity.

You cannot lash yourself without striking me. Have mercy, my dear Honorine. I so appreciated your susceptibilities that I would not bring you back to the Rue Payenne where I can live without you, but I am decorating another house in the Faubourg Saint-Honoré to which, in hope, I will conduct not a wife secured by law but a sister who will allow me to press on her brow a father’s kiss.

Will you deprive me of the right I have conquered—the right to watch over your needs, pleasures, life? Women always have their mother’s heart; you never knew any mother but mine. My affection is neither mean nor grasping; it is one that never lets annoyance last. What can you think of the companion of your childhood if you believe him capable of accepting trembling kisses, of living between delight and anxiety? Do not fear supplicant passion; I would not want you back until certain of my strength to leave you free.

Your solitary pride has exaggerated difficulties. You may look on life as brother or father without suffering; you will find neither mockery nor indifference. The warmth of our life will be equable, without tempests. If later you desire pleasures or amusements, you can expand their circle. The tenderness of a mother knows neither contempt nor pity. In me admiration shall hide every sentiment you might see as an offence.

We may be magnanimous: the kindness of a sister and the thoughtfulness of a friend will satisfy him who wishes to be your companion. Neither of us will be jealous of the past; we will look only forward.

You will be at home in my new house as in Rue Saint-Maur—unapproachable, alone, free; but with the legitimate protection and consideration of a woman, and the fortune to do good. When you long for absolution, ask for it; it will not be forced by Church or Law; it will wait on your pride. If this compact does not suit you, I have begged the saintly man who takes charge of this letter not to speak for me. I will not owe your return to terrors of the Church or the Law. I will not accept simple happiness from anyone but yourself. If you persist in solitude, my will yields to yours. You shall be no more troubled than before. I will get rid of the crazy fellow who meddled in your affairs and perhaps annoyed you...

“Madame,” said Honorine, folding the letter and placing it at her heart, “thank you. I will avail myself of Monsieur le Comte’s permission to remain here—”

“Ah!” I cried.

My exclamation made my uncle look uneasy and threw a mischievous glance from the Countess that enlightened me as to her motive. She had wanted to test whether I was an actor, a spy; and my cry, from the heart, deceived her. “Ah, Maurice,” she said, “you know how to love.” The light in my eyes was another answer, and the Count found me useful to the end.

Honorine finished the Count’s letter. My uncle signed to me to go. “Let us leave the Countess,” he said. “You are going already, Maurice?” she asked, without looking. She rose, still reading, and followed us to the door. On the threshold she took my hand, pressed it affectionately, and said, “We shall meet again—”

“No,” I replied, wringing her hand; she cried out. “You love your husband. I leave tomorrow.” I rushed away, leaving my uncle, who, with a look meaning “He is mad,” completed my part.

Six days later I set out with an appointment as vice-consul in Spain, where I could rise toward a consulship. After I had settled, I received this letter from the Count:

MY DEAR MAURICE,—

If I were happy I should not write, but I have entered a new life of suffering. I have grown young again in desire, with the impatience of forty and the prudence of a diplomat. When you left I had not yet been admitted to the pavilion in the Rue Saint-Maur, but a letter promised permission—the mild, melancholy letter of a woman who dreaded agitation. After waiting more than a month I called and had Gobain inquire whether I could be received. I sat in a chair in the avenue near the lodge, my head in my hands, for an hour.

“Madame had to dress,” said Gobain, to hide her hesitancy under a pride of appearance that flattered me.

For a quarter of an hour we both trembled like speakers on a platform, exchanging sacred phrases as those surprised who “make believe” conversation.

“You see, Honorine,” said I, tearful, “the ice is broken; I am so tremulous with happiness that you must forgive my incoherence. It will be so for a long time.”

“There is no crime in being in love with your wife,” she said with forced smile.

“Do me the favor,” I said, “no longer to work as you do. I have heard you have lived on savings; you have sixty thousand francs a year. If you cannot give me your heart, at least do not abandon your fortune to me.”

“I have long known your kindness,” she said.

“Though you prefer to remain here and preserve independence, do not toil.”

I gave her three certificates for twelve thousand francs each; she took them, opened them languidly, and gave me only a look. She understood I was offering freedom, not money.

“I am conquered,” she said, holding out her hand, which I kissed. “Come and see me as often as you like.”

So she had done violence to herself in receiving me. Next day she showed affected high spirits; habit took two months before I saw her true character. Then it was like May, a spring of love giving ineffable bliss; she was no longer afraid and studied me. Alas! when I proposed she go to England and then return openly to my house, she was seized with alarm.

“Why not live always as we are?” she said.

I submitted and asked myself, “Is she making an experiment?” On my way from my house to the Rue Saint-Maur thought of love swelled in my heart and I said, like a young man, “This evening she will yield.”

All my force was blown away by a smile, by a command from proud, calm eyes untouched by passion. I remembered the words you once quoted, “Lucretia’s dagger wrote in letters of blood the watchword of woman’s charter—Liberty!” They froze me. I felt the necessity of her consent and how impossible to wring it from her. Could she guess my storms as I left?

At last I painted my situation in a letter, giving up speaking. Honorine made no answer and was so sad I pretended not to have written. I was grieved at the thought of distressing her; she read my heart and forgave me. Three days ago she received me, for the first time, in her blue-and-white room—bright with flowers and light. Honorine was in a gown that made her bewitching, hair framing her face with Cape heath. She was a bride of old days. My joy chilled; her face was grave, fires beneath the ice.

“Octave,” she said, “I will return as your wife when you will. But understand this submission has dangers. I can be resigned—” I stirred.

“Yes,” she went on. “Resignation offends you; you want what I cannot give—Love. Religion and pity led me to renounce solitude; you are here!” She paused.

“At first,” she continued, “you asked no more. Now you demand your wife. Here I give you Honorine, such as she is, without deceiving you. What shall I be? A mother? I hope it. Try to change me; you have my consent; but if I should die, do not curse my memory; do not call obstinacy what I might call worship of the Ideal, or of something divine! The future will be your concern.”

She sat calm and watched me pale. Seeing the effect she took my hands and said, “Octave, I do love you, but not as you wish. I love your soul. Still, I love you enough to die in your service like an Eastern slave, without regret. It will be my expiation.”

She knelt before me and added, “And perhaps I shall not die!”

For two months I have struggled. What shall I do? My heart is too full; I seek a friend and cry, “What shall I do?”

I did not answer. Two months later the papers announced the Comtesse Octave’s return on an English vessel, restored to her family after adventures invented with sufficient probability to avoid contradiction.

When I moved to Genoa I received a formal announcement of the birth of a son to the Count and Countess. I held that letter for two hours on this bench. Two months later, urged by Octave, M. de Grandville, and Monsieur de Sérizy, and broken by my uncle’s death, I agreed to marry.

Six months after the July revolution I received this letter, which concludes this couple’s story:

MONSIEUR MAURICE,—I am dying though I am a mother—perhaps because I am a mother. I have played my part as a wife well; I have deceived my husband. I have had happiness as genuine as an actress’s tears. I am dying for society, for the family, for marriage, as early Christians died for God! I do not know of what I die; I try to find out, for I am not perverse; I am bent on explaining my malady to you—you who brought that heavenly physician, your uncle, at whose word I surrendered. He was my director; I nursed him in his last illness; he showed me the way to heaven, bidding me persevere in duty.

And I have done my duty.

I do not blame those who forget; I admire them as strong natures; but I have the malady of memory! I could not twice feel the love that identifies a woman with her man. To the last, as you know, I cried to your heart and to my husband, “Have mercy!” But there was no mercy. I am dying, dying with stupendous courage. No courtesan was ever gayer. My poor Octave is happy; I let his love feed on illusions. I throw all my powers into this terrible masquerade; the actress is applauded, feasted, smothered in flowers; but the invisible rival comes every day to seek its prey—a fragment of my life. I am rent and I smile. I smile on two children, but the elder, the dead one, will triumph. I told you so. The dead child calls me, and I go to him.

Marriage without love degrades my soul hourly. I can never weep or dream except when alone. The exigencies of society, care of my child, and Octave’s happiness leave me no moment to refresh myself as in solitude. Constant watchfulness starts my heart with alarms. I have not implanted the sharp-eared vigilance that lies with facility; it is not the lip of one I love that cools my burning eyes. My soul is acting a part, and perhaps that is why I die! I lock up my grief so carefully nothing shows; it eats into me and attacks my life.

I said to the doctors, “Make me die of some plausible complaint, or I shall drag my husband with me.”

So it is understood by M. Desplein, Bianchon, and me that I am dying of softening of some bone. Octave believes I adore him. I am afraid lest he follow me. I now beg you be the little Count’s guardian. You will find a codicil in which I express my wish; do not produce it except in need. My devotion may leave Octave inconsolable but willing to live. Poor Octave! I wish him a better wife than I have been; he deserves to be well loved.

Since my spiritual spy is married, I bid him remember what the florist of Rue Saint-Maur bequeaths: May your wife soon be a mother! Fling her into vulgar materialism of household life; hinder her from cherishing in her heart the mysterious flower of the Ideal, that enchanted blossom whose perfume disgusts us with reality. I am a Saint-Theresa who could not live on ecstasy in a convent with the Holy Infant.

You saw me happy among my flowers. I did not tell you all: I saw love budding under your affected madness, and I concealed my thoughts, my poetry; I did not admit you to my kingdom of beauty. Well, you will love my child for love of me if he should lose his father. Keep my secrets as the grave keeps them. Do not mourn for me; I have been dead this many a day, if Saint Bernard was right that where there is no more love there is no more life.

And the Countess died,” said the Consul, putting away the letters and locking his pocket-book.

“Is the Count still living?” asked the Ambassador, “for since July he has disappeared politically.”

“Do you remember, Monsieur de Lora,” said the Consul-General, “seeing me go to the steamboat with—”

“A white-haired man? an old man?” the painter asked.

“An old man of forty-five, going to Southern Italy for health and amusement. That was my poor friend, my patron, passing through Genoa to take leave and place his will in my hands. He appoints me guardian of his son. I had no need to tell him of Honorine’s wishes.”

“Does he suspect himself of murder?” asked Mademoiselle des Touches of the Baron de l’Hostal.

“He suspects the truth,” replied the Consul, “and that is killing him. I remained on the packet till it put to sea; a small boat brought me back. We sat some time saying farewell—for ever, I fear. God only knows how much we love the confidant of our love when she who inspired it is no more.

“That man,” said Octave, “holds a charm and wears an aureole.” The Count went to the prow and looked down on the Mediterranean. Moved, he said: “Ought we not, for human nature’s sake, inquire what irresistible power leads us to sacrifice an exquisite creature to the most fugitive pleasures despite reason? I heard cries in my conscience. Honorine was not alone in her anguish. And yet I would have it... I am consumed by remorse. In Rue Payenne I died of joys I had not; now I shall die in Italy of joys I have had.... Where lay the discord between two natures, equally noble?”

For some minutes the terrace was silent.

Then the Consul asked the two women, “Was she virtuous?”

Mademoiselle des Touches rose, took his arm, and said, “Are not men wrong too when they make a young girl a wife while cherishing at heart some angelic image, comparing us to those unknown rivals and always finding us wanting?”

“Mademoiselle, you would be right if marriage were based on passion; and that was the mistake of those two. Marriage with heart-deep love on both sides would be Paradise.”

She turned and was joined by Claude Vignon, who whispered, “A bit of a coxcomb is M. de l’Hostal.”

“No,” she whispered back, “for he has not guessed that Honorine would have loved him.—Oh!” seeing the Consul’s wife approach, “his wife was listening! Unhappy man!”

Eleven struck by all the clocks, and the guests walked home along the seashore.

“Still, that is not life,” said Mademoiselle des Touches. “That woman was one of the rarest and most extraordinary intellects—a pearl! Life is incidents, pain and pleasure alternating. Dante’s Paradise is found only in the soul; to ask it of life is a luxury nature protests. To such souls the six feet of a cell and the kneeling chair are all they need.”

“You are right,” said Léon de Lora; “but good-for-nothing as I may be, I admire a woman who could live by a studio, under a painter’s roof, never coming down nor seeing the world.”

“Such a thing has been known—for a few months,” said Claude Vignon with irony.

“Comtesse Honorine is not unique,” replied the Ambassador. “A man, a politician, a bitter writer, was the object of such passion; the pistol shot that killed him struck not him alone; the woman who loved lived like a nun thereafter.”

“Then there are yet some great souls in this age!” said Camille Maupin, leaning on the quay balustrade.
