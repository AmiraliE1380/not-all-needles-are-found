**La Grenadière** by Honoré de Balzac, translated by Ellen Marriage, depicts a charming house on the right bank of the Loire River, just below the bridge of Tours. The area features picturesque cliffs with white stone country houses, gardens, and vineyards, benefiting from a favorable climate for fruit cultivation.

La Grenadière is located about a hundred paces from the village church of Saint-Cyr, halfway up a hillside. A series of steps leads down to the embankment, which serves as a causeway. The property boasts terraced gardens, trellises, and a variety of flowers, creating a vibrant environment. A Gothic archway opens into a garden filled with roses and a wooden summer-house covered in climbing plants.

The house itself is an old structure with rustic charm, featuring large windows, a slate roof, and yellow-washed walls with green shutters. Inside, a crooked staircase leads to a dining room with tiled floors and a sitting room adorned with saffron-colored wallpaper. The first floor contains two simple bedrooms, and a modern kitchen connects to the main house, indicating that La Grenadière was originally a vintage retreat for townsfolk from Tours.

The property includes a small vineyard and a vinedresser’s cottage, surrounded by stone walls and fruit trees, maximizing land use. The landscape is rich with various fruits, flowers, and diverse plants, showcasing the beauty of Touraine. The terrace offers a breathtaking view of the Loire, with its changing colors and distant hills and châteaux.

La Grenadière embodies a humble yet majestic dwelling, rich in natural beauty and tranquility. It serves as a retreat for lovers and dreamers, inviting reflection and appreciation of life’s simple pleasures, making it a cherished haven for all who visit.

An Englishman renting La Grenadiere for the summer pays a thousand francs for six months, plus extra for orchard fruit and vintage. This estate, cherished since 1690, boasts picturesque views of Touraine's valleys and cathedral towers, making it invaluable for health and happiness.

In the spring of the Restoration, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, seeking a home. She chose La Grenadiere, possibly attracted by its distance from town. The house was simply furnished, reflecting her modest lifestyle, leaving her social status ambiguous—whether upper middle or higher class. Her plain dress and reserved demeanor sparked local curiosity about her background.

Tall and slender, Mme. Willemsens had delicate features and a melancholic expression. Her dark eyes hinted at hidden anguish, yet she exuded quiet strength. Dressed in mourning attire, she seemed to retreat from the world. Despite her frail health, she took evening walks with her children along the Loire, captivating Tours' residents.

During her stay at La Grenadiere, she ventured into Tours only twice: once to inquire about schools for her children and again to arrange lessons. Her rare appearances on the bridge piqued local interest, but little was known about her true status. The landlord referred to her as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, suggesting a noble background, though this remained largely within business circles.

Mme. Willemsens' life was a mystery, marked by her natural grace and deep bond with her children, enchanting those around her. Young men were drawn to her, but her sadness made them hesitant to approach. She discarded letters from admirers, preferring to focus on her life at La Grenadiere.

Her two sons, Louis-Gaston and Marie-Gaston, also attracted attention. Louis-Gaston, the elder, was robust and energetic, while Marie-Gaston was more delicate and gentle. Their close relationship was evident, with Louis often caring for his younger brother. The boys embodied their mother's nurturing upbringing, reflected in their behavior and appearance.

Each morning, the boys began their day with a prayer, a ritual instilled in them since childhood. They maintained a meticulous routine, ensuring they presented themselves well for their mother. Their interactions were filled with affection, laughter, and a deep understanding of each other's needs. Mme. Willemsens fostered a loving environment where her children felt secure and cherished.

The bond among the three was profound, characterized by mutual respect and empathy. If Mme. Willemsens was quiet, her sons mirrored her mood; Louis, perceptive and protective, sensed her struggles. He often redirected their activities to ensure she was not overwhelmed, demonstrating maturity beyond his years.

In this idyllic yet isolated life, the family thrived on shared experiences and emotional connections. Mme. Willemsens' love and dedication shaped her children's lives, creating a nurturing atmosphere that allowed them to flourish despite challenges. Their existence at La Grenadiere illustrated the power of love and the importance of familial bonds, showcasing a life of simplicity, beauty, and profound connection.

Mme. Willemsens dressed beautifully for her children, wanting to be a pleasing presence in their lives. She was always ready to hear their lessons from ten to three, with lunch in the summer-house. After lunch, while the children played, she relaxed on a sofa, enjoying the picturesque landscape of Touraine.

The children explored the orchard and garden, engaging with nature and frequently seeking their mother’s guidance. During lessons, Mme. Willemsens embroidered quietly, focusing on Louis to inspire his ambition, while being less concerned about Marie. After lessons, she discussed Louis’ performance with his tutors to assist him effectively.

Their days balanced work and play, fostering a harmonious environment. Mme. Willemsens instilled values of moderation and resilience in her children, nurturing their spirits with praise. Watching them play often brought tears of joy to her eyes, as they had never caused her distress. These moments represented her ideal of happiness, akin to a vision of Heaven.

In the summer evenings, she read inspiring biographies to Louis while Marie slept, using these stories to teach him about perseverance and success. However, her happiness was tinged with sadness, as she feared for their future without her. One evening, she confided in Louis about her illness and the inevitability of her death, expressing her deep love and concern for their well-being. She urged him to be strong and promised to guide him in caring for Marie.

As summer progressed, Mme. Willemsens’ health declined, and the atmosphere at La Grenadiere grew somber. The once vibrant household became quiet, with the children adapting to their mother’s condition. Louis, feeling a strong connection to her, focused more on his studies, showing remarkable progress.

Despite her illness, Mme. Willemsens cherished her time with her children, often seen walking with them in the gardens. The local community noticed her decline but admired her grace and dignity. As autumn approached, she remained devoted to her children, savoring each moment as if it were her last.

Unbeknownst to his mother, Louis worked diligently at night, excelling in his studies and preparing for the entrance examination to the Ecole polytechnique. He sought adventure and knowledge from a naval officer he met, eager to learn about military life. His mother encouraged his pursuits, relieved he was engaging in physical activity to balance his studies.

Through these experiences, Louis matured, developing a sense of purpose and determination, while his mother’s love and teachings continued to guide him.

In October, Mme. Willemsens could only rise at noon, enjoying the warm atmosphere reminiscent of the Bay of Naples. She spent her days with her two children, Louis and Marie, cherishing their time together without outside worries. The elder boy adored her, while Marie brought her flowers, creating a serene yet melancholic scene amidst the autumn leaves.

Eventually, the doctor confined Mme. Willemsens to her room, where she remained surrounded by flowers and her children. One day in early November, she played the piano for the last time, filled with emotion as she reflected on her life. When the doctor ordered her to stay in bed, she asked Louis to take her to the terrace for one last view of her homeland. Overcome with anguish, she expressed her fears about their uncertain future.

As her health declined, Louis and Marie stayed by her side, witnessing her suffering. The doctor prohibited flowers, signaling the end was near. On a quiet Sunday night, Mme. Willemsens called Louis to her, revealing that she would soon die. She instructed him to retrieve important papers from her table, including their birth certificates and a letter to her estranged husband, Lord Brandon, forgiving him.

She shared her limited financial resources with Louis, urging him to keep the money close, as everything would be sealed after her death. Louis vowed to care for Marie, planning to send her to school while he pursued a career at sea. Mme. Willemsens found solace in her son’s determination, feeling a sense of peace before her passing.

The next morning, she received the sacrament surrounded by her children and Annette, their caretaker. As her breathing slowed, the room filled with sorrow, and she eventually passed away, leaving her children in grief. They prepared her body for burial, marking her grave with a simple cross inscribed with a poignant message about her life.

After the funeral, Louis and Marie returned home for a final farewell before leaving with Annette. Louis entrusted Marie’s birth certificate and their limited funds to Annette, then took Marie to school, offering her heartfelt advice as they parted ways.

A month later, Louis-Gaston, now an apprentice on a warship, left France behind. As he gazed at the receding coastline, he felt the weight of solitude and responsibility. An old sailor comforted him, reminding him of God’s presence, and Louis accepted his new life at sea, determined to honor his mother’s memory. 

Thus, the story concludes with Louis embracing his future, embodying the resilience and hope instilled by his mother.
