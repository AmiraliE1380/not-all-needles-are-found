**ALBERT SAVARUS**  
*By Honore de Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage*  

**DEDICATION**  
To Madame Emile Girardin  

In the drawing-room of the Baronne de Watteville, a key figure in Besançon society, the Archbishop of Besançon often visited due to her devout nature. Monsieur de Watteville, a descendant of a notorious historical figure, was a gentle, unremarkable man married to the wealthy heiress Mademoiselle de Rupt. Their 1815 marriage united their families' fortunes, with Madame de Watteville growing increasingly pious over time.

Monsieur de Watteville, a turner by trade, also collected geological specimens, a hobby some found eccentric. Their home, reflecting their noble heritages, was filled with fine furnishings and exquisite food, showcasing their social status. Madame de Watteville’s close ties with the Archbishop attracted notable clergy to their gatherings.

During a ceremonial dinner in September 1834, guests welcomed Monsieur l’Abbe de Grancey, who announced a surprising legal victory regarding a lawsuit. He credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron, for his success against a formidable Parisian opponent. This revelation intrigued the guests, particularly Madame de Watteville, who inquired about Savaron’s background. The Vicar-General noted that Savaron lived nearby, but his origins were unknown, raising further curiosity.

The conversation underscored Savaron's remarkable abilities and the church's unexpected triumph over liberal opposition, setting the stage for a deeper exploration of his character and the unfolding events in Besançon.

Throughout history, France and England have playfully exchanged cultural influences, particularly in language and fashion, often bypassing customs regulations. Styles labeled "English" in Paris are seen as "French" in London, showcasing a mutual fascination. For example, the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli, while hoop skirts that became fashionable in Paris were introduced by an Englishwoman but originated from a French invention.

Recently, England has added new terms to the French lexicon, such as "dandy" and "lion." The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset's poetry, describes a fashionable man with flamboyance. This trend spread beyond Paris, influencing provincial towns like Besançon, where Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas emerged as a local "lion." Amedee, a young man of Spanish descent, sought to elevate his status in a town known for its military and conservative culture.

Living modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment, Amedee maintained a lavish lifestyle with the help of his young servant, Babylas, whom he dressed stylishly. Despite his extravagant appearance, Amedee struggled financially, living on a limited income from his father's estate. He was seen as a spendthrift but cleverly balanced his expenses, prioritizing appearances over substance.

Amedee's social life revolved around impressing the local aristocracy, particularly aiming to win the affection of Rosalie de Watteville, a young woman from a wealthy family. Rosalie, raised in a strict household, was largely sheltered from the outside world, receiving a limited education focused on religious and domestic skills. At eighteen, she embodied modesty and virtue, yet harbored a strong spirit beneath her reserved exterior.

The dynamic between Amedee and Rosalie was complicated by their social standings and familial expectations. Amedee's aspirations to marry Rosalie were fueled by her substantial dowry, which he believed would secure his financial future. Meanwhile, Rosalie's mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective, scrutinizing Amedee's intentions and controlling Rosalie's interactions.

Despite Amedee's efforts to charm Rosalie and her mother, he faced challenges in gaining their approval. His attempts to present himself as a cultured gentleman were met with skepticism, as Rosalie remained indifferent to his advances. The tension between them was exacerbated by Madame de Watteville's rigid expectations and Rosalie's internal struggles with her feelings.

As Amedee navigated the complexities of social life in Besançon, he became a figure of intrigue among the local elite. His fashionable attire and social maneuvers earned him a reputation as the most charming young man in town, yet his true motivations remained obscured. Ultimately, Amedee's pursuit of Rosalie was not merely a quest for love but a strategic move to elevate his social standing and secure a prosperous future.

In summary, the interplay of fashion, social status, and personal ambition shaped the lives of Amedee de Soulas and Rosalie de Watteville in the conservative milieu of Besançon, reflecting broader themes of aspiration, societal expectations, and the complexities of human relationships in a changing cultural landscape.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Monsieur de Soulas shared with the Baroness the story of Albert Savaron, who came to Besançon from Paris, rented an apartment, and registered as a barrister. Mademoiselle de Watteville noted the Savaron family's prestigious lineage, but Amedee de Soulas clarified that Savaron was a common Frenchman who had added a bar-sinister to his name to suggest nobility.

Initially, Savaron was overlooked by the local legal community, who found his presence puzzling. He worked diligently, rising early and keeping to himself, which led to his being forgotten. He attended early mass but remained largely a mystery. Monsieur de Soulas learned about him through his servant and local gossip.

Savaron's reputation shifted when he successfully defended a mentally challenged man accused of forgery, impressing the court and attracting clients. The Vicar-General, Abbe de Grancey, noted Savaron's striking appearance and eloquence, highlighting his calm demeanor despite a passionate nature. His modest living conditions contrasted with his impressive intellect and oratory skills.

Madame de Chavoncourt expressed curiosity about Savaron's motivations for coming to Besançon and why he had not integrated into the community. The Abbe speculated that Savaron would eventually make a significant impact.

Rosalie de Watteville, intrigued by Savaron's character, found herself drawn to him, contrasting him with the more superficial suitors she knew. Determined to meet him, she devised a plan to encounter him at church. After a brief confession with the Abbe, she glimpsed Savaron during mass and felt an intense connection.

As Rosalie's fascination grew, she encouraged her father to build a Belvedere in their garden to observe Savaron's private life. The project attracted attention in Besançon, with many speculating about its lavishness.

Meanwhile, Savaron launched a fortnightly publication, the _Eastern Review_, aimed at promoting Besançon's interests and competing with larger cities. He managed the publication discreetly, gaining support from local merchants.

Rosalie's father subscribed to the _Eastern Review_, allowing her to read its contents, including a tale attributed to Savaron. This captivated Rosalie, as she sought to uncover more about Savaron's life and emotions through his writing, deepening her infatuation with him.

In summary, the narrative follows Albert Savaron's arrival in Besançon, his initial obscurity, and eventual rise to prominence through his legal and literary achievements, all while capturing the attention and affection of Rosalie de Watteville, who becomes enchanted by his mysterious persona.

**AMBITION FOR LOVE’S SAKE - Summary**

In July 1823, two young men, Rodolphe and Leopold, embarked on a holiday in Switzerland to explore the scenic beauty of the Four Cantons. While traveling by boat, Rodolphe became enchanted by a house in Gersau, where he spotted Fanny Lovelace looking out. He decided to stay in Gersau, while Leopold continued their journey.

Rodolphe and Leopold were close friends with contrasting backgrounds. Leopold was destined for a career as a notary in Paris, while Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, was raised by a devoted mother who sacrificed everything for his education. Rodolphe was passionate and sensitive, driven by his desires and dreams.

After breakfast at the Swan Inn, Rodolphe found lodging with the Stopfer couple, who offered him a room overlooking the lake. He learned more about Fanny, who had moved to Gersau with her elderly father for his health. They lived modestly, relying on Fanny's work as a translator and writer. Rodolphe was captivated by her beauty and musical talent.

Eager to meet Fanny, Rodolphe sought permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens, where she and her mute servant, Gina, lived. Initially met with suspicion, he eventually gained access. While exploring, he noticed Gina's unusual appearance and suspected she had a hidden background.

One evening, while listening to Fanny sing, Rodolphe overheard her speaking Italian with Gina, leading him to suspect they were Italian refugees. When he approached them, Gina attacked him, mistaking him for a threat. Fanny and Gina helped him recover, deepening Rodolphe's attraction to Fanny's beauty and kindness.

As Rodolphe healed, he learned of his mother’s death, which plunged him into despair. Leopold returned to France, leaving Rodolphe in Gersau, where he found solace in Fanny's company. Their bond deepened as they shared their sorrows and dreams. Fanny revealed she was married to an older man, Emilio, but expressed hope for a future together.

Rodolphe's feelings for Fanny intensified, and he admired her strength and independence. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential. Their conversations revealed a shared passion for freedom and liberal ideals, further connecting them.

One day, Fanny received news that her family was no longer in danger and would soon return to Italy. This news brought joy to Fanny and Gina but left Rodolphe feeling abandoned. He struggled with jealousy and insecurity as Fanny's past and her relationship with Emilio became clearer.

Despite the challenges, Rodolphe remained devoted to Fanny, vowing to wait for her. Their love blossomed against the stunning Swiss landscape, symbolizing both their hopes and the obstacles they faced. The story explores themes of ambition, love, and the complexities of human relationships amid political turmoil and personal sacrifice.

For twenty days, Rodolphe stayed with the Bergmanns, captivated by Francesca, who embodied youthful imprudence and the complexities of womanhood. He observed her relationship with her husband, Emilio, characterized by gratitude and a fatherly affection, which intrigued him and drew him closer to Francesca.

Their interactions were filled with secret joys and occasional quarrels that Rodolphe found delightful. One evening, Francesca expressed her longing for luxury, likening it to her love for art and beauty. She refused to accompany Rodolphe to Geneva, fearing gossip that could tarnish her husband’s reputation. While she declared her love for Rodolphe, she emphasized the need for discretion to protect their bond.

The next day, upon learning that the Bergmanns had left, Rodolphe departed Gersau for Geneva. He settled in Eaux-Vives and inquired about Italian refugees, particularly a bookseller named Lamporani. His curiosity led him to the Villa Diodati, where he encountered Francesca in an elegant carriage, revealing her connection to the aristocratic Colonna family.

Rodolphe was struck by the realization of Francesca’s true identity, grappling with feelings of inadequacy for loving a princess disguised as a commoner. He sought an audience with Prince Gandolphini, Francesca’s husband, who welcomed him warmly. Despite his desire to see Francesca, Rodolphe returned to his lodgings, consumed by thoughts of their future.

When Rodolphe finally saw Francesca at a gathering, her beauty captivated him. Their eyes met, igniting a profound connection that transcended their surroundings. Francesca’s subtle acknowledgment of their bond amidst the social elite deepened Rodolphe’s feelings, solidifying his love for her.

As they spent time together, Francesca revealed her understanding of Rodolphe’s ambition and desire for greatness, encouraging him to pursue his dreams while remaining youthful. Their conversations reflected a shared commitment to love faithfully, despite the challenges they faced.

Francesca’s background as a well-educated heiress, initially destined for a cloistered life, added depth to her character. Her marriage to Prince Gandolphini was a strategic move to preserve family status, yet her revolutionary spirit aligned with Rodolphe’s aspirations.

Winter passed quickly for Rodolphe in Geneva, but as spring approached, he struggled with feelings of longing and anxiety. Francesca’s calm demeanor contrasted with his emotional turmoil, leading him to question her feelings. Their discussions revealed the complexities of their love, with Francesca asserting her Italian identity and pride.

One day, tension arose when Rodolphe compared Francesca to a French noblewoman, inadvertently hurting her feelings. Sensing her distress, he sought to comfort her, but she maintained her dignity, instructing him to leave until they could meet again in Naples.

This separation marked a pivotal moment in their relationship, highlighting the challenges they faced as lovers from different worlds. Francesca’s strength and resolve left a lasting impression on Rodolphe, who was determined to prove himself worthy of her love.

Upon returning to Paris, Rodolphe found a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner, who had surprisingly agreed to paint it. Francesca's charm led the artist to create an original for Rodolphe and a duplicate for Emilio, fostering regular correspondence between them. Driven by ambition and love, Rodolphe invested all his resources in a venture that ultimately failed, costing him three years. After the Villele ministry fell, he sought solace in Naples, enjoying time at the Villa Gandolphini. Just as he was poised for success, the July 1830 revolution struck, thwarting his plans. Despite setbacks, Rodolphe perseveres, supported by love.

As Mademoiselle de Watteville finished her reading, she was consumed by rage and jealousy over Francesca Colonna, the woman she believed Albert loved. This passionate narrative ignited a fierce desire in her to win Albert's affection, yet she doubted her worthiness, thinking, “He will never love me!”

That night, Rosalie's imagination ran wild with schemes to connect with Albert, from sneaking into his garden to writing him a letter. She even considered a lawsuit to bring him into her social circle. While gazing at Albert's window, she noticed a suspicious scene involving Jerome, Albert's servant, and Mariette, the maid. This discovery sparked a dangerous curiosity in Rosalie, who realized that a single moment could unravel her mother's strict upbringing.

The next day, Rosalie confronted Mariette about her relationship with Jerome, offering her silence in exchange for access to letters Albert sent through Jerome. Mariette, relieved, agreed, deepening Rosalie's obsession with Albert's life and feelings.

Rosalie learned from her father that a copy of the _Eastern Review_ was sent to the Duchesse d’Argaiolo in Italy, further fueling her jealousy. She later intercepted a letter from Albert to his friend Leopold, revealing his noble lineage and ambitions. This letter painted a picture of Albert's struggles and aspirations, intensifying Rosalie's infatuation and leading her to view him as a heroic figure.

As Albert's legal career flourished, Rosalie's feelings morphed into a dangerous obsession. She felt compelled to read another letter from Albert to the Duchesse, detailing his hopes and affections for her. The letters revealed Albert's deep love for the Duchesse, leaving Rosalie heartbroken yet determined to respect their bond.

Despite her turmoil, Rosalie resolved to step back, believing she should sacrifice her desires for Albert and the Duchesse's happiness. She grappled with her feelings of unrequited love, questioning when she would find someone who truly loved her. Ultimately, Rosalie's internal conflict highlighted the complexities of love, jealousy, and the sacrifices made in the name of affection.

In the spring of 1835, nineteen-year-old Rosalie fantasized about overcoming the Duchesse d’Argaiolo, driven by her solitude and secret desires. Meanwhile, Albert de Savarus made political gains in Besançon, securing support from influential figures, including a wealthy contractor, as he aimed to restore an aqueduct to supply water from Arcier, benefiting both his political ambitions and the contractor's interests.

Savarus, who had successfully represented local priests in several lawsuits, was motivated by a desire for success, concealing his true intentions behind a facade of disinterest. In October 1834, he arranged financing for a merchant, inadvertently positioning himself for election. The Abbe de Grancey recognized Savarus's potential but noted his hesitation to align with the Wattevilles, fearing it would compromise his neutrality.

Determined to introduce Savarus to her social circle, Rosalie planned a trip to les Rouxey, her family estate. There, she learned of her father's legal troubles over land disputes with the village of Riceys and suggested hiring Savarus as their lawyer. She believed that securing his services would protect her inheritance and strengthen their connection.

While plotting to keep Savarus in Besançon, Rosalie also sought to undermine his political ambitions by informing the Prefect of his Royalist leanings. Despite her efforts, Savarus was nominated as a candidate, and his eloquence during a meeting won the townsfolk's support.

However, the political landscape shifted as the Prefect and his allies worked to weaken Savarus's backing. The election process revealed the complexities of local politics, with various factions vying for influence. Despite initial enthusiasm, Savarus faced increasing challenges as the election approached, creating a tense atmosphere filled with uncertainty.

In a moment of despair, Savarus reflected on his unrequited love for Francesca, feeling the weight of his ambitions and election pressures. Just when he felt most defeated, the Abbe de Grancey arrived with hopeful news, urging him to connect with the Wattevilles, particularly Madame de Chavoncourt, whose daughter Sidonie could provide crucial support.

The Abbe's strategic advice led Savarus to consider a marriage alliance with Sidonie, which would secure the votes he needed to win the election. As he prepared to navigate this new political landscape, he recognized the sacrifices he would need to make for his ambitions, including a potential marriage that could alter his future.

Ultimately, the intertwining of personal desires and political aspirations set the stage for a dramatic confrontation between love and ambition, as Savarus sought to balance his heart's yearnings with the demands of his political career.

The following evening, Madame la Baronne de Watteville hosted a gathering of Besançon’s aristocracy to discuss the election, particularly regarding the daughter of the Rupts. The former Master of Appeals was to be presented, with attendees including Madame de Chavoncourt and her daughters. The Abbe de Grancey mingled among the guests, offering sharp insights on the political situation.

By nine o’clock, Albert de Savarus had not arrived, raising concern. Madame de Chavoncourt suggested his delay might be trivial, but Rosalie, the Baroness's daughter, suspected otherwise. As time passed, frustration grew, and the Abbe de Grancey speculated that Albert might be dead. They learned from a servant that he had left in a post-chaise, prompting mixed reactions of shock and relief.

As the evening continued, only a few guests remained, including Madame de Chavoncourt and the Abbe de Godenars. The Abbe de Grancey received a letter that visibly affected Rosalie, revealing her feelings for Albert. He deduced she was in love with him and discovered that Albert had withdrawn from the election. Rosalie, desperate to keep this news secret, realized that if the election ended, so would the prospect of her sister Sidonie marrying Albert.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

In the morning, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette about the mysterious circumstances surrounding Albert Savaron's sudden disappearance. An old gentleman from Paris had visited Savaron, leaving him pale and agitated. After their meeting, Savaron quickly arranged for a journey, leaving behind his keys and instructing his servant to find another job. His departure stirred commotion in Besançon, leading to the election of Monsieur de Chavoncourt.

As time passed, Albert was forgotten by most, except for a few deeply affected by his absence. Rosalie, aware of the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo's death, felt increasingly troubled. The Baroness de Watteville expressed concern for Rosalie, suggesting she marry Monsieur de Soulas, but Rosalie adamantly refused, insisting she was content as she was.

The tension between Rosalie and her mother escalated, leading to a summer at les Rouxey, where Rosalie's refusal to marry became public knowledge. After Mariette and Jerome's marriage, the Baron improved les Rouxey, which Rosalie took pride in, further aggravating her mother.

Tragedy struck when the Baron drowned while retrieving a shell from the lake. His death, attributed to Rosalie's defiance, deepened the rift between mother and daughter. Grief-stricken, Rosalie felt responsible and contemplated confessing her role in Albert's fate, having intercepted letters between him and the Duchess to manipulate their correspondence.

The Vicar-General, upon learning of Rosalie's actions, expressed horror at her moral crimes and urged her to seek redemption. Rosalie resolved to write to Albert's friend, Monsieur Leopold Hannequin, to uncover his fate.

Hannequin's response revealed that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery, seeking peace after the heartbreak caused by the Duchess's marriage. The Vicar-General wrote to Albert, who confirmed his decision to remain in the monastery, forgiving Rosalie and wishing her well.

As the years passed, Rosalie's mother married Monsieur de Soulas, and Rosalie, now of age, was encouraged to marry as well. Despite her mother's attempts to control her, Rosalie remained resolute in her feelings for Albert. She sought to confront the Duchess, determined to make her aware of the pain she had caused.

At a ball, Rosalie publicly confronted the Duchess, revealing the truth about Albert's letters and their tragic separation. The encounter left the Duchess shaken, and Rosalie returned to Besançon, feeling a sense of closure.

Ultimately, Rosalie's journey was marked by loss, regret, and a quest for redemption as she navigated the complexities of love, family, and the consequences of her actions.

Mademoiselle de Watteville's quirks include annual visits to the Grande Chartreuse, possibly inspired by her grand-uncle's legacy. In 1841, she left Besancon to marry but returned severely injured from a steamboat accident, losing her right arm and left leg, and suffering disfigurement. Now, she leads a life devoted to religious practices at les Rouxey.
