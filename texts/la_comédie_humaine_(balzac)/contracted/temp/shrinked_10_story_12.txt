In the Restoration the Archbishop of Besançon frequented Baronne de Watteville’s salon for her piety. A descendant of Watteville, she married Mlle de Rupt (arranged 1802, formalized 1815). After her grandparents' deaths she became devout and influential.

He was a turner and collector; admired for humility, he accepted her prominence and focused on work. Rupt house displayed their status; her fortune exceeded his estate. Their ties attracted abbés. At a September 1834 dinner Abbé de Grancey announced a favorable suit; the Vicar‑General credited Savaron, a nearby lawyer of obscure origin, intriguing guests. Amedée de Soulas proved significant.

France and England have long traded cultural influences—what is "English" in Paris becomes "French" in London. Examples include the English anthem "God Save the King," said to have been composed by Lulli for a French play, and hoop skirts and fashions mocked in Paris after 1815.

In the 19th century England contributed "dandy" and "lion" (popularized by Alfred de Musset), sparking a provincial trend of stylish young men. In 1834 Besançon’s "lion" was Amedee‑Sylvain de Soulas, of Spanish descent, ill at ease in the dull military town. He kept a spare apartment, cultivated an ostentatious appearance, and maintained a fashionable young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger," despite scarce means. He economized—often dining out—to sustain a polished façade and worldly tales to impress the local aristocracy and win heiress Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville. Besançon’s conservative "Colony" used his quirks to seem modern to Parisians. Rosalie, modest and resolute, was fiercely protected by her mother, complicating Amedee’s pursuit. His story shows social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraint.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
“Madame,” Monsieur de Soulas began while his soup cooled, “a Parisian gentleman took rooms on the first floor of Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron and registered at the Mairie as ‘Albert Savaron.’ He left his card with the local officials.”

“The Savaron name is well known,” Mademoiselle de Watteville observed.

“He’s a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amedee de Soulas replied. “If he claims the Savaron arms he must add a bar-sinister.” Rosalie noted that even a bastard of a Comte de Savarus remains noble, but the Baroness cut her off. The Vicar-General went on: in a town like Besançon, Albert was carefully catalogued. He sent cards to judges rather than calling, hired Jérôme, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant who could cook, and kept mostly to himself.

“Does he go to mass?” asked Madame de Chavoncourt.

“He attends early service at Saint-Pierre,” Monsieur de Soulas answered. “He rises between one and two in the morning, works until eight, breakfasts, works again, walks in his garden, dines, and sleeps early.” He watched Albert from Rue Neuve; his servant spoke with Jérôme; townsfolk supplied the rest.

The Baroness inquired why the Vicar-General had engaged him. He had been assigned to defend a simple peasant accused of forgery; Savaron proved the man innocent, exposed the true culprits, secured arrests, and later won a merchant’s difficult suit. Monsieur Berryer recommended him, and the Vicar-General’s confidence was justified.

“Is he extraordinary?” asked Madame de Chavoncourt.

“Certainly,” replied the Abbe de Grancey, who painted a striking portrait. He first found Savaron in a room lined with law books and furnished simply: an old table, six armchairs, gray curtains and a green carpet. Younger than expected, Savaron wore a black dressing gown with red accents. The Abbe pronounced him devilishly dressed but magnificent in head and countenance: thick black hair streaked with gray, a white throat, a forehead furrowed by thought, an olive complexion, a square nose, fiery, eagle-like eyes—passionate yet calm, with a sweet voice that could thunder. He was of average height, neither stout nor thin, with hands like a prelate’s.

Subsequent visits found him in a shabby bedroom or having a frugal breakfast in his library. The Abbe sensed something heavy burdening Savaron—a man not merely learned but marked by secret weight. He wondered why such a man left Paris for Besançon and predicted he would make a significant impact.

Rosalie, the Vicar-General’s daughter, was captivated. The Abbe’s portrait offered her a figure far removed from the commonplace suitors she’d known. She replayed his words—“What a fine forehead!”—and compared him in her mind to the men at the table. When pressed by her mother, she confessed she had lost her appetite thinking of the prelate-like hands and the window she had glimpsed from her bed, now knowing it belonged to him. “I must see him,” she decided.

That night she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse his windows. The sight of candlelight and a man at work while the world slept filled her with a sacred thrill. The matter, she knew, raised the broader issue of educating girls: how much should they know? Too much knowledge forces premature maturity; too little leaves them stunted. Rosalie’s sudden inner stirring—transforming the ordinary into the extraordinary—was a consequence of that precarious balance.

The next morning she saw Albert walking in his garden and resolved to enter his house, uncover his mystery, hear his voice, and glimpse his eyes. She embroidered with the absorbed air of someone outwardly idle but inwardly intense. She arranged confession at half-past seven before the eight o’clock service, hoping to meet him.

She suggested to her father a grotto belvedere in the garden as a pretext. He delighted in carving larger pieces and liked the conceit; she cautioned him not to tell her mother. She pictured an observatory from which to spy on Albert’s private room. Young girls, she reflected, often contrive elaborate plans to adore from afar.

Sunday she dressed carefully and arrived at church before Mass. She watched for the Abbe and positioned herself to see Albert. To her newly curious eye, no man could look unattractive. Albert’s appearance astounded her: solemn, burdened, with a deep, intense gaze like an eagle’s revealing hidden ardor. In a bold moment she stood in his path and exchanged glances; a rush of warmth followed. After confession, Mass ended and Albert was gone. She left convinced the Vicar-General had been right: he was unhappy, an “eagle” in exile. Her desire to know everything about him hardened into resolve.

Rosalie masked her absorption with innocent industry and, with practiced air, supported the belvedere plan. Madame de Watteville approved, inspected columns, and chose a site commanding a view into Savaron’s rooms. Builders were engaged to construct a grotto planted with periwinkles, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle and Virginia creeper; the interior would be rustic—mirror, ottoman, inlaid bark table—and Monsieur de Watteville suggested an asphalt floor while Rosalie wanted a rustic chandelier. Rumors spread in Besançon about the Wattevilles’ new garden feature and its cost—“a thousand crowns!” exclaimed Madame de Chavoncourt. No one guessed Rosalie’s true motive: a foolish-sounding scheme to peer into Albert’s study.

Meanwhile Albert’s legal triumphs were quickly resented by jealous local lawyers. He remained secluded, avoiding society, and continued to win challenging cases, gaining four prominent merchant clients. More importantly, he launched a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review, funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each from his first ten clients. The Review aimed to promote Besançon as a center of trade and enlightenment to rival Strasbourg and Dijon. Savaron kept his name out of the foreground, letting Monsieur Boucher handle finances while he retained editorial control and a share of the profits.

The Review, modestly priced, quickly secured five hundred subscribers. To avoid offending local pride, Savaron gave the literary management to Alfred Boucher, a junior eager to shine, while he subtly guided the content. With two hundred more subscribers, shareholders might see dividends; the editor, for now, remained unpaid. The third issue gained notice among French publications and contained a tale attributed to Savaron that stirred comment in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room and drew ridicule from the town’s more conservative circles.

Rosalie, hearing of the Review, persuaded her father to subscribe and keep it in his room—her mother would not have allowed her to read it—so he did. That night Rosalie devoured the tale—the first story she had ever read. Given her sheltered upbringing, it affected her deeply; she was convinced the narrative reflected Savaron himself, a confession of his life and feeling. Others likewise suspected that Albert drew from his own experience in his writing.

In 1823 two young law students left Lucerne to tour the lake of the Four Cantons. Passing Gersau, Rodolphe was so taken with a house and a woman at the window that he stayed while Leopold continued. Leopold was steady, bound for a Parisian notary’s life; Rodolphe, an illegitimate nobleman’s son, was troubled and impulsive. His mother had sacrificed for him.

He lodged with a couple and learned the woman was Fanny Lovelace, who had moved there with her father and supported him by translating. He admired her beauty and music. After reluctantly gaining permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens, he noticed Gina, a deaf-mute of mysterious origin. The Bergmanns guarded Fanny. He later heard her sing; when he finally met her an unknown assailant wounded him, and Fanny and Gina nursed him back.

During convalescence he learned his mother had died, plunging him into despair, yet he found solace in Fanny. He confessed his love and learned she was married to Emilio; she later said the marriage had been coerced and he was not so old. Their intimacy grew, complicated by Rodolphe’s jealousy—especially when Tito, a former lover, reappeared—and by ambition, duty, and social constraints. They dreamed of freedom, but love, ties and sacrifice kept them conflicted, the landscape echoing their passions.

For twenty days Rodolphe stayed with the Bergmanns, watching Francesca with fascination and puzzlement. He perceived both youthful imprudence and a resolute, sometimes resistant character. The old man treated her like a father; her gratitude suggested an innate nobility that deepened her enigma.

Those days mixed secret joys and melancholy, quarrels as sweet as harmony. Rodolphe grew increasingly captivated by Francesca’s constant tenderness and jealous over trifles. One evening, when she said she wished to leave Gersau for its lack of luxuries, he asked if she valued luxury. She answered passionately, equating a love of luxury with appreciation for art and beauty, while insisting she had endured hardship without complaint. Her playful claim that ordinary people aspire to greatness only added to her charm.

Francesca then insisted Rodolphe not accompany them to Geneva, citing gossip and the need to protect her husband’s reputation. She professed love for Rodolphe but demanded secrecy, kissed him, and left him astonished.

The next day Rodolphe went to Vevay and Geneva, renting a room in Eaux-Vives and asking after Italian refugees—hoping for a bookseller named Lamporani—but found only Prince and Princess Colonna and Prince and Princess Gandolfini. At Villa Diodati he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage and felt dizzy: the intimacy he had felt collided with her noble rank, and he feared he had been a whim.

He left his card with Prince Gandolfini, attended a concert by invitation, and that night his passion intensified and his ambition to rise socially burned. At the concert they met; she was radiant, and he believed she had remained true. Introduced to the Colonnas, she teasingly called herself “Princess Rodolphini,” acknowledged his faithful affection, and he confessed his desire to achieve greatness so she could be proud.

Francesca’s life was full of contradiction: born for the cloister, family tragedies forced her marriage to Prince Gandolfini to preserve status, yet she prized independence and liberal ideas. Winter in Geneva passed quickly, but in spring Rodolphe grew restless; her calm clashed with his need for more demonstrative feeling. On a terrace he bitterly lamented others’ happiness, wounding her; finding her in tears, he kissed her. She rebuffed him, asserted her dignity, ordered him to leave—vowing they would not meet again until Naples—and he complied.

Rodolphe saw Schinner’s portrait of Gandolphini; Francesca got the artist to paint one for him and a duplicate for Emilio, starting correspondence.

Ambition and love led him to stake all on a deceitful venture costing three years. Villele’s ministry fell, ruining him; after months at Gandolphini he turned to politics. His talents won a promised post, but the July 1830 upheaval thwarted him. Only Francesca and God knew his heart. Lovers, pray for him!

When Mademoiselle de Watteville finished her tale, Rosalie flushed and wept. The novel—an early example of a man narrating his love—was her first encounter with that voice and made her jealous, convinced Francesca Colonna was Albert’s beloved.

Despite insecurity about her looks and lack of musical talent, Rosalie resolved to win Albert or at least learn the truth, contriving schemes—sneaking into his garden, writing, visiting her father’s land—to force a meeting. From Albert’s window she saw Jerome helped over the wall by Mariette; Mariette’s plainness explained Jerome’s interest and showed Rosalie that servants could bypass her mother’s restrictions.

The next morning Rosalie accompanied Mariette to Mass and bargained for access to the letters Albert sent by Jerome; Mariette, relieved, agreed. A letter to Leopold revealed Albert’s noble lineage, struggles, ambition, and steadfast devotion to Francesca. As she read, Rosalie felt inadequate and wrong for invading privacy but tried to justify it as proof of love. On reading a letter meant for Francesca that confirmed Albert’s hopes, she stepped back, recognizing the nobility of their bond and longing for such selfless, enduring love.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil; by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo and nurtured private passion and dark plans. The story is offered as a cautionary example of such women.

That winter Albert de Savarus advanced his position in Besançon, pressing for the Chamber’s dissolution and winning backing from a wealthy contractor tied to moderates. He promoted restoring the Arcier aqueduct—arguing Doubs water was unfit—even though the town relied on the Roman works; the local Review echoed him and “Roman” water became accepted across parties. Hidden interests drove the debate, opponents were dismissed as old-fashioned, and after eighteen months Savarus had stirred the stagnant town and gained influence without broad popularity. He also won lawsuits for local priests and, though he affected indifference to personal gain, his ambition sought larger rewards: in October 1834 he aided a struggling merchant with Léopold Hannequin’s funds, improving his election prospects. The Abbé de Grancey, sensing Savarus’s designs, called him remarkable but misdirected.

Meanwhile Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the Hôtel de Rupt’s society, initially content to observe. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, produced income but was poorly managed by the inattentive Baron and steward Modinier; the family’s heirs pressed a dubious claim on the village of Riceys, whose inhabitants long held rights. After the Baron's death his descendants continued asserting usurpation.

In May 1835 Rosalie went to les Rouxey, enchanted by its lake and linking it to love. On a boat her father revealed legal troubles over Riceys; Rosalie urged hiring Savarus to secure the Dent de Vilard, wanting the lawsuit—and Savarus—at les Rouxey to fix her future and create gardens. The Abbé visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles; Savarus hesitated, fearing political compromise. Rosalie even wrote to the préfet exposing his ambitions, but was ignored.

As the election neared, influential men unexpectedly nominated Savarus; surprised, he was cautioned by the Abbé to be transparent about his past. Savarus’s passionate speech impressed many, yet the préfet worked to undermine him and the first vote disappointed him. In despair he reflected on Francesca, until the Abbé assured behind-the-scenes support, promised the Rouxey case would be settled, and suggested marriage to Sidonie de Chavoncourt to consolidate votes. Savarus, reluctantly seeing the advantage, prepared to navigate love and politics.

By nine the next evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms filled with Besançon’s aristocracy to debate the surprise nomination of the Rupts’ daughter, linked to a former Master of Appeals and secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch. Madame de Chavoncourt came with her younger daughter Sidonie; her elder daughter, secure in her relationship, stayed away. The Abbe de Grancey steered talk, probing an elderly statesman about whom the Elder Branch might support; they feared Berryer, noted the Duc de Fitz-James’s Toulouse nomination, and worried that backing Monsieur Savarus could drive Republicans toward them rather than the Moderates.

By nine-thirty Albert had not arrived; Madame de Watteville took his lateness as an insult while Madame de Chavoncourt counselled patience. Rosalie, watching, hinted at a possible match between Albert and Sidonie. By ten impatience grew; some gentlemen played cards. The Abbe, uneasy, went to the window where Rosalie had withdrawn and cried, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville, and Rosalie went to Albert’s dark rooms; the servant Jérôme said Albert had left in a post-chaise. The Abbe exclaimed ambiguously, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” and Rosalie’s face betrayed joy.

Back in the drawing-room the Baroness announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By half-past eleven only about fifteen guests remained. A servant brought a letter for the Abbe; he read it, recognized the handwriting, folded it, and declared, “She is in love with Albert Savarus!” Rosalie then confessed the letter was from Albert and begged him to keep quiet about his withdrawal from the election, knowing it would destroy any hope of her sister’s marrying him.

On her way to Mass de Watteville learned from Mariette that Albert vanished. A Parisian visited Monsieur Savaron; he grew pale, met him, left with a package, hastened to the Prefecture, resigned his post, secured a carriage, and obtained a passport. His abrupt departure inflamed local prejudice against outsiders. Most soon forgot Albert—except Girardet, the Vicar‑General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was no news. The Baroness pressed Rosalie to marry; the Baron promised les Rouxey. A match with Monsieur de Soulas was arranged; Rosalie refused. That summer tensions rose. Rosalie confessed to the Vicar‑General she had sabotaged Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess, by intercepting their letters. A clerk for Albert later revealed he had entered a monastery. The Vicar‑General obtained Albert’s letter: he forgave Rosalie and chose solitude. Urged to reconcile and accept de Soulas, she refused. After the Vicar‑General’s death Madame de Watteville married de Soulas. Now of age and independent, Rosalie confronted the Duchess in Paris, confessed her part in Albert’s downfall, and returned to Besançon bearing the cost of love, betrayal, and ambition.

Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone on her les Rouxey estate—riding, hunting, and improving the land—making her notable in the Eastern provinces. Madame de Soulas, mother of two, looks younger than her years; Monsieur de Soulas has aged and told young Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

Eccentric and capricious, Mademoiselle de Watteville visits the walls of the Grande Chartreuse annually, perhaps following a grand‑uncle who once broke monastery walls to gain his freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon, ostensibly to marry though her motive was unclear; she returned after a steamboat boiler explosion on the Loire that cost her her right arm and left leg and scarred her face. With failing health and constant pain she now devotes herself to religious practices at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
