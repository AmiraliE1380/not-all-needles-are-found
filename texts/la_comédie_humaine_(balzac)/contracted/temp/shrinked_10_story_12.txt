During the Restoration the Archbishop of Besançon frequently attended Baronne de Watteville’s drawing-room because of her devoutness.

Monsieur de Watteville, descended from the infamous Watteville, was gentle and unassuming. After a quiet life in La Franche-Comté he married Mademoiselle de Rupt, heiress of the Rupt family; the match was arranged in 1802 and formalized in 1815 after the second Restoration. Following her grandparents’ deaths, Madame de Watteville became more devout, adopting a pious life that influenced Besançon’s upper circles.

Monsieur de Watteville, thin and unremarkable in appearance, occupied himself as a turner and collector of local geological specimens. Many noted a certain nobility of soul in him: realizing early he would not dominate his wife, he immersed himself in work and hobbies.

The Rupt residence reflected both families’ standing—grand rooms, elegant furnishings, and fine cuisine—with Monsieur sometimes acting as his own butler. Madame de Watteville’s fortune was considerable while her husband’s estate was modest. Their intimacy with the Archbishop drew several notable abbés to their home.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834 guests warmly received Monsieur l’Abbé de Grancey, who announced a favorable verdict in a lawsuit. The Vicar-General explained the success had been unexpected given the political climate and credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron, for his decisive role.

Curious about Savaron, Madame de Watteville inquired into his background. The Vicar-General said Savaron lived nearby but that his origins were unknown, which piqued the guests’ curiosity. Amedée de Soulas, present at the gathering, would later prove significant to the story that followed.

In all ages France and England have exchanged cultural influences, especially in language and fashion, often skirting customs. What is "English" in Paris is "French" in London, reflecting mutual borrowing. For example, the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli for a French play, and hoop skirts, first derided, became a dominant European fashion for decades. By 1815 English long waists were mocked in Paris, yet French styles soon shifted in response.

More recently England supplied French with terms like "dandy" and "lion." The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset's poetry, sparked a trend of stylish young men in Paris that spread to the provinces. In 1834 Besançon had its own "lion" in Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, a young man of Spanish descent who felt ill at ease in the dull, military town. He lived modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment but maintained an ostentatious appearance, keeping a young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger." Amedee dressed Babylas fashionably and lavished expense on his own wardrobe while subsisting on scant income.

Though seen as a spendthrift, Amedee was adept at economizing, often dining out to save on household meals. His costly grooming and clothes were a façade aimed at impressing the local aristocracy and winning Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a wealthy heiress. His travels and Parisian knowledge helped him cultivate a reputation as a well-informed young man despite financial strain.

Besançon was conservative and resistant to change; the local aristocracy, called "the Colony," kept strict social hierarchies. Amedee’s unusual fashions—like wearing trouser-straps—set him apart and bolstered his status as a "lion." The town’s nobles wished to appear modern to visiting Parisians, and Amedee enabled that image.

Amedee’s chief ambition was to marry Rosalie, who had been raised in a strict, religious household with little contact with the wider world. At eighteen she was modest and unassuming, educated under her mother’s watchful eye and instructed more in domestic skills than social polish. Sheltered from modern influences, she lacked awareness of the social expectations surrounding courtship.

Despite a passive exterior, Rosalie had a resolute spirit inherited from her lineage. Her mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective and controlling, convinced her daughter’s honor was at stake. This dynamic created tension as Amedee courted Rosalie: the Baroness’s strictness led Rosalie to suppress any feelings for him, producing a complex relationship marked by maternal jealousy and Rosalie’s uncertainty.

As Amedee pursued Rosalie, he navigated Besançon’s social intricacies to secure a favorable match while protecting his reputation. The town’s elite regarded him as charming, and gossip about a possible engagement circulated among the nobility. Yet Rosalie’s upbringing and her mother’s influence complicated his efforts; she remained largely unaware of the romantic expectations around her. Amedee de Soulas’s life in Besançon thus illustrates the interplay of social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraints as he strives to win Rosalie de Watteville’s heart amid rigid social structures.

“Madame,” said Monsieur de Soulas to the Baroness as he waited for his soup to cool, “one morning a gentleman from Paris arrived at the Hotel National and took the first floor of Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron. He registered at the Mairie as ‘ALBERT SAVARON’ and left his card with local officials.”

“The name Savaron is well-known,” Mademoiselle de Watteville interjected, “one of the oldest and richest families in Belgium.”

“He’s a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amedee de Soulas replied. “If he claims the Savaron arms, he must add a bar-sinister. The only remaining Brabant is a rich heiress, Mademoiselle de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister marks a bastard,” Rosalie added, “but a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble.”

“Enough, that will do, mademoiselle!” the Baroness interposed.

“You insisted she learn heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville remarked, “and she knows it well.”

“Please continue, Monsieur de Soulas.”

“In a town like Besancon, where everything is catalogued, Albert Savaron was accepted without fuss by the local lawyers. They wondered who had sent him and what he hoped to achieve—he sent his card to judges instead of calling. Three days later, nobody noticed him. He hired Jerome, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who could cook a little. Savaron remained overlooked because no one saw him.”

“Does he not go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends the early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He rises between one and two, works until eight, breakfasts, works again, walks in his garden, dines, and goes to bed between six and seven.”

“How do you know all this?” Madame de Chavoncourt inquired.

“I live on Rue Neuve, overlooking his house, and my servant communicates with Jerome.”

“And you gossip with Babylas?”

“What else is there to do while riding?”

“How did you come to hire him for your defense?” the Baroness asked the Vicar-General.

“The President of the Court assigned him to defend a half-witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man innocent and exposed the real culprits, leading to arrests. His defense impressed the Court and jury. A merchant then gave him a difficult case, which he won. Given our situation, Monsieur Berryer advised we employ Savaron, predicting success. Upon meeting him, I felt confident—and was right.”

“Is he truly extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” the Vicar-General replied.

“Tell us more,” urged Madame de Watteville.

“The first time I met him,” the Abbe de Grancey recounted, “he received me in an outer room lined with law books and old oak. The room was simply furnished: an old writing table, six armchairs, gray curtains. I did not expect my advocate to be young. His appearance matched the setting; he wore a black dressing gown with a red cord, red slippers, a red waistcoat, and a red smoking cap.”

“The devil’s colors!” exclaimed Madame de Watteville.

“Yes,” the Abbe continued, “but he has a magnificent head. Black hair streaked with gray, thick and curly; a round white throat, a splendid forehead scored by deep lines of thought, an olive complexion, a square nose, and fiery eyes. Despite passion, he has a calm manner and a sweet voice that can thunder when required.”

“Monsieur Albert Savaron is of average height, neither stout nor thin, with hands like a prelate.”

“The second time I visited, he received me in his shabby bedroom. The third time, he had a frugal breakfast in his library; I observed him closely. He is no ordinary man; there’s much behind that terrible yet gentle face. He seems to carry a heavy burden.”

“Why would such an eloquent man leave Paris for Besancon?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. “Doesn’t he know how little chance a stranger has here?”

“After studying his head,” the Abbe replied, “and hearing him answer a prominent Parisian lawyer, I believe he will eventually make a significant impact.”

“Why discuss him? You’ve won your case and paid him,” Madame de Watteville remarked, noting her daughter’s rapt attention.

The conversation moved on, but the Vicar-General’s portrait of Savaron captivated Rosalie. For the first time she encountered someone exceptional, unlike the mundane Count who had courted her. Albert Savaron presented enigmas. “Albert Savaron de Savarus,” she mused, longing to see him, replaying the Abbe’s words and fixating on his description. “What a fine forehead!” she thought, comparing him to the men at the table.

“Rosalie, why aren’t you eating?” her mother asked.

“I’m not hungry, mamma,” she replied, lost in thought about the prelate’s hands. She recalled a lighted window seen from her bed and realized it belonged to him. “I must see him!” she decided.

“Monsieur de Grancey, is the Chapter’s lawsuit settled?” Rosalie asked during a lull.

“What does that matter to you?” her mother replied, sensing her daughter’s unusual interest.

“It might go to the Court of Appeal, but our adversaries will hesitate,” the Abbe answered.

“I never thought Rosalie would think about a lawsuit during dinner,” Madame de Watteville remarked.

“Nor I,” Rosalie said dreamily, prompting laughter. “But Monsieur de Grancey was so engrossed that I became interested.”

After dinner, Rosalie listened for any mention of Albert Savaron, but aside from congratulations to the Abbe, he was not discussed. Eager for bed, she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse Albert’s windows. When the hour arrived, she felt a thrill watching candlelight through the trees. She saw him writing and imagined the color of his furniture.

“While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought.

The education of girls poses serious challenges, since the future of a nation rests on mothers. The University of France long avoided this issue: Should girls be informed about everything? Should their minds be restrained? Religion often imposes restraint. Enlightening them too much brings premature maturity, while suppressing thought can produce explosive outbursts, as in Moliere’s Agnes. Rosalie’s crisis was triggered by the Abbe’s portrait of Savaron.

The next morning, while dressing, Rosalie looked out to see Albert walking in the garden next to the Hotel de Rupt. “What if he lived elsewhere? At least I can see him here. What is he thinking?” she wondered.

Having seen him, Rosalie resolved to enter his house, uncover his mystery, hear his voice, and glimpse his eyes. She planned to achieve this.

Throughout the day she embroidered with the concentration of someone who appears idle yet is deeply reflective. She decided to go to confession. The next morning, after Mass, she arranged to confess at half-past seven, just before the eight o’clock service, hoping to encounter Albert.

She visited her father in his workshop, suggesting he turn larger pieces and build a grotto in the garden to showcase his work. As he delighted in the idea, she cautioned him not to tell her mother, fearing a scolding.

“Don’t worry!” he replied, sharing her frustration with his wife.

Rosalie envisioned a charming observatory from which she could see into Albert’s private room. Young girls often go to great lengths for the sake of a man, even if he remains unaware.

The eagerly awaited Sunday arrived. Rosalie dressed with such care that her maid Mariette smiled.

“It’s the first time I’ve seen you so fidgety,” Mariette remarked.

“Perhaps you too are more particular on certain days,” Rosalie replied, blushing.

As she crossed the courtyard, her heart raced. She had never walked the streets like this before, feeling as if her mother could read her thoughts and would forbid her confession. She felt invigorated, as if walking on fire. She arrived at church before Mass, prayed briefly, and checked if the Abbe Giroud was in his confessional, positioning herself to see Albert as he entered.

No man could seem unattractive to Rosalie in her curious state. Albert Savaron, strikingly handsome, made a profound impression. His solemn demeanor suggested a heavy burden, and his deep gaze conveyed intense thought. Rosalie now fully understood the Vicar-General’s words. Those eagle-like eyes revealed a hidden ardor. In a bold moment she stood in his path to exchange glances, igniting a rush of warmth within her.

After Albert took a seat, Rosalie found a place to observe him during her confession. When Mariette announced Monsieur Giroud’s arrival, the time in the confessional felt fleeting. By the time she emerged, Mass had ended, and Albert had gone.

“The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besancon? I must know everything! But how?”

With this new desire, Rosalie focused intently on her embroidery, masking her thoughts with an innocent demeanor to deceive her mother.

From that Sunday, after their glance, Rosalie eagerly supported the plan for the Belvedere.

“Mamma,” she said one day, “my father has a unique idea for a Belvedere in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of everything your father does,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly. “Why should I object to something that amuses him?”

“Because from there we’ll see into Monsieur de Soulas’ rooms, and he’ll see us—perhaps remarks will be made—”

“Do you presume to guide your parents, Rosalie, thinking you know more about life and propriety than we do?”

“I’ll say no more, mamma. Besides, my father mentioned a cool room in the grotto where we can take coffee.”

“Your father has had an excellent idea,” Madame de Watteville said, going to inspect the columns.

She approved the design, choosing a spot that could not be seen from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows but would allow views into Albert Savaron’s rooms. A builder was commissioned to construct a grotto with a path lined with periwinkles, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper. The interior was to be rustic, with a mirror, an ottoman, and a table of inlaid bark. Monsieur de Soulas suggested an asphalt floor, while Rosalie proposed a rustic chandelier.

“The Wattevilles are creating something lovely in their garden,” rumors spread in Besancon.

“They’re wealthy and can afford such whims—”

“A thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed.

“Yes, a thousand crowns,” young Monsieur de Soulas replied. “A man from Paris is coming to rusticate the interior, and it will be very pretty. Monsieur de Watteville is carving the chandelier himself.”

“Berquet is making a cellar beneath it,” added an Abbe.

“No,” the young man corrected, “he’s raising the kiosk on a concrete foundation to prevent dampness.”

“You know the least about what happens in that house,” Madame de Chavoncourt remarked sourly, glancing at her daughter, who had been waiting to marry for a year.

Mademoiselle de Watteville felt a flush of pride at the Belvedere’s success, sensing superiority over others. No one suspected that the girl thought a witless goose had devised the plan to get a closer look at Albert Savaron’s private study.

Albert Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon forgotten amid envy from other lawyers. He remained secluded, avoiding social gatherings, increasing the likelihood of being overlooked as a stranger in Besancon. Nevertheless, he successfully argued three complex cases at the Commercial Tribunal, gaining four prominent merchants as clients who recognized his legal skill.

On the day the Watteville family inaugurated the Belvedere, Savaron was also launching a new venture. Through quiet connections among local merchants, he was starting a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review, funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each from his first ten clients, convinced of the need to promote Besancon as a trade hub.

To compete with Strasbourg, Besancon needed to become a center of enlightenment and commerce. Albert’s vision was to challenge the literary dominance of Strasbourg and Dijon and bring light to Eastern France. His ideas appealed to merchants eager to believe in their own enterprise.

Savaron wisely kept his name out of the spotlight, letting his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, handle finances while he retained editorial control and a share of the profits. The Review appealed to various regions, and with a low subscription price it quickly garnered five hundred subscribers.

To avoid offending local pride, Albert invited the Boucher family’s eager son, Alfred, to manage the literary side. Albert maintained control, subtly guiding Alfred’s contributions while letting him think he was gaining. The commercial community, pleased with the Review’s success, only had to pay a fraction of their shares. With two hundred more subscribers, the publication would yield dividends for shareholders, while the editor remained unpaid.

After the third issue, the Review gained recognition among French publications, which Albert began reading at home. The third number featured a tale attributed to him, sparking discussion in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room, though higher circles of Besancon dismissed it for liberal views.

“Papa,” Rosalie said, “there’s a Review published in Besancon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room, as mamma wouldn’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.”

Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please Rosalie who had shown him so much affection, subscribed and lent her the four issues already published. That night Rosalie devoured the tale—the first she had ever read. Given her limited experience, the impact on her cannot be judged by ordinary standards. She hoped to find in it the sentiments and perhaps the life of Albert. From the first pages, she became convinced it reflected him, as critics noted Albert had drawn from his own life in this confession.

In 1823 two young men set out from Lucerne for a holiday in Switzerland to tour the lake of the Four Cantons. The journey offered striking views. As they passed Gersau, one friend, Rodolphe, was captivated by a newly built house and a woman at the window and decided to stay while his friend Leopold continued the tour.

Rodolphe and Leopold had studied law together. Leopold, calm and steady, was destined to be a notary in Paris. Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a more troubled background: his mother had sacrificed everything for his education, hoping to secure his future. Passionate and sensitive, Rodolphe was often driven by desire, in contrast to Leopold’s temperamental restraint.

In Gersau Rodolphe lodged with a couple who informed him the woman was Fanny Lovelace, who had moved there with her elderly father for his health. They were of modest means; Fanny supported her father by translating and writing. Rodolphe was struck by her beauty and musical talent.

The next day Rodolphe sought permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens, who rented to the Lovelaces. After hesitation he was allowed to explore. He noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, who seemed to have an unusual background. Eager to meet Fanny, he encountered resistance from the protective Bergmanns.

That evening Rodolphe heard Fanny singing and was enchanted. He later met Fanny and Gina, but their conversation was cut short when Rodolphe was attacked and wounded by an unknown assailant. Fanny and Gina aided him and he was taken back to safety.

While he recovered Rodolphe received devastating news: his mother had died. The loss plunged him into deep depression, but he found solace in Fanny’s company. They bonded over shared interests, and Rodolphe confessed his feelings, only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite this, her affection for Rodolphe grew and they shared intimate moments.

Fanny later revealed her husband was not as old as he had first appeared and that she had married him under duress. Rodolphe was torn between his feelings and the reality of her marriage. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing he had great potential.

Their relationship deepened, yet Rodolphe struggled with jealousy and insecurity, especially when Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past, sent news and then arrived. Tito’s presence stirred rivalry and fear in Rodolphe of losing Fanny’s affection.

Despite these challenges Rodolphe and Fanny’s connection remained strong. They shared dreams of freedom and a future together, but the complexities of their circumstances—Rodolphe’s ambition, Fanny’s loyalty to her husband, and social constraints—loomed over them. As they navigated their feelings Rodolphe grappled with the tensions between love, ambition, and the sacrifices required for happiness. The beauty of their surroundings mirrored the intensity of their emotions even as life’s realities threatened to overshadow their dreams.

Francesca, with her blend of gaiety and tenderness, was remarkable. Born destined for a cloister, family tragedies forced her into marriage with Prince Gandolphini to preserve their status. Rodolphe spent the winter in Geneva, where time flew in the company of the intelligent, beautiful Francesca. Yet as spring neared he felt inner turmoil: Francesca’s calm contrasted with his unrest, and he longed for more demonstrative feeling, believing Italian women to be more expressive.

One day on the terrace Rodolphe voiced bitterness about others’ happiness, particularly that of a noblewoman. Francesca, wounded, drew back; Rodolphe followed and found her in tears. In a moment of passion he kissed her, but she rebuffed him and asserted her dignity.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil, but by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. In solitude that struggle fueled her passion and dark plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist; this story is a cautionary tale for them.

That winter Albert de Savarus advanced his position in Besançon, eager for the Chamber’s dissolution. He had secured support from a wealthy contractor linked to the moderate party. The Romans had long been credited with supplying town water; Besançon drew its supply from Arcier, a distant hill. Restoring an aqueduct to drink “Roman” water seemed absurd given the Doubs river, yet persuading citizens would authorize large expenditures benefiting the contractor.

Savarus argued the river water was unfit and promoted Arcier water. The local Review echoed him, and townspeople across parties agreed on drinking the Romans’ water. The issue became a focal point in Besançon, driven by hidden interests. Opponents were dismissed as old-fashioned while Savarus’s projects dominated discussion. After eighteen months he had stirred the stagnant town, gaining influence without popularity.

Throughout the winter he won several lawsuits for local priests, and ambition consumed him. His apparent disinterest in personal gain masked a form of moral usury: he anticipated a much larger reward.

In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with funds from Léopold Hannequin, which also qualified him for election. The Abbé de Grancey, who saw Savarus’s designs, remarked on his remarkable nature and suggested he had taken a wrong turn in life.

Meanwhile Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hôtel de Rupt, initially content to observe him. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, produced ten thousand francs a year but was poorly managed by the Baron, who left its oversight to an old servant, Modinier. The Baron's indifference stemmed from his wife's substantial income, and they often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque spot developed by the famous Watteville.

Watteville had constructed a dam and lake, enhancing the area’s beauty, but his heirs maintained a questionable claim over the village of Riceys, which had long enjoyed rights to the land. The Baron's death left a legacy of usurpation that his descendants continued to assert.

Rosalie’s maneuvers were subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey, captivated by the lake and associating it with love. She suggested a boat trip, during which her father confided about legal troubles with Riceys over land rights. Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus as lawyer, stressing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for their estate.

She argued that a lawsuit would add excitement and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey. Her determination to secure her future there was clear; she envisioned creating beautiful gardens.

Four days later the Abbé de Grancey visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing alignment would compromise his neutrality. The Abbé urged him to consider political advantages, especially potential votes.

Rosalie, determined to keep Savarus in Besançon and prevent his election, wrote to the préfet revealing Savarus’s ambitions, but the préfet disregarded her. She understood the political landscape and sought to manipulate it to her advantage.

As the election approached a meeting of influential men in Besançon decided to nominate Savarus, much to his surprise. He had been unaware of the political machinations. The Abbé, observing the situation, warned Savarus of potential betrayals and advised him to be transparent about his past.

Savarus delivered a passionate speech outlining his vision, captivating his audience. However, he faced challenges as the préfet worked to undermine him, eroding his voter base. Despite his efforts, the political tides shifted against him, and he struggled to maintain his position.

On the day of the first voting the results disappointed Savarus; he felt the weight of his ambitions and the election’s pressure. He returned home in despair, reflecting on his love for Francesca and the uncertainty of his future.

Just as he neared hopelessness, the Abbé arrived with news of hope. The priest had labored behind the scenes to secure support, urging Savarus to visit the Wattevilles and promising their case would be resolved in time for the election.

The Abbé revealed that winning the Rouxey lawsuit could secure crucial votes, and he encouraged Savarus to pursue a marriage with Sidonie de Chavoncourt to further solidify his standing. Savarus was initially resistant but recognized the potential benefits.

The Abbé’s guidance proved invaluable. Savarus prepared to navigate the complexities of both love and politics, aware that his future depended on choices he would make in the coming days.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
By nine o’clock the next evening, Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms held Besançon’s aristocracy, gathered over the unexpected decision to go to the polls for the daughter of the Rupts. It was known the former Master of Appeals, a secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch, would be presented. Madame de Chavoncourt arrived with her younger daughter Sidonie, elegantly dressed; her elder sister, secure in her relationship, had not bothered — in a small town such details are noticed. The Abbe de Grancey moved through the groups, listening and offering sharp comments that steered the talk.

“If the Elder Branch returns,” he asked an elderly statesman, “what politicians would they find?” The statesman said Berryer, isolated on his bench, was uncertain and might upset the Government if he rallied support. They discussed the Duc de Fitz-James’s nomination in Toulouse and how voting for Monsieur Savarus might align Republicans with them rather than with the Moderates.

By nine-thirty Albert had still not appeared, and Madame de Watteville took his tardiness as an insult. Madame de Chavoncourt advised her not to let small matters distract from serious issues, suggesting Albert might be detained by a consultation. Rosalie, watching her mother’s exchange with Madame de Chavoncourt, noted her leniency toward Albert and hinted at a possible match between Sidonie and him.

At ten Albert remained absent and impatience grew. Some gentlemen began to play cards; the Abbe de Grancey, perplexed, went to the window where Rosalie had withdrawn, exclaiming, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville, and Rosalie went into the garden to check Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent.

“Jérôme!” Rosalie called to the servant below. When he confirmed that Albert had left in a post-chaise, the Abbe de Grancey declared, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” Rosalie’s face betrayed a sudden joy, which the Vicar-General noticed but pretended not to, wondering about her involvement.

Back in the drawing-room, Monsieur de Watteville announced the shocking news of Albert’s unexplained departure. By half-past eleven only fifteen guests remained: Madame de Chavoncourt, the Abbe de Godenars, the two Chavoncourt girls, Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbe de Grancey, Rosalie, Amédée de Soulas, and a retired magistrate who had supported Albert’s election. The Abbe de Grancey stationed himself near the Baroness to observe Rosalie, whose normally pale face now showed agitation.

“What could have happened to Monsieur de Savarus?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. At that moment a servant brought a letter for the Abbe de Grancey on a silver tray. The Baroness urged him to read it. As he did, he noticed Rosalie turn pale; he recognized the handwriting. He folded the letter and discreetly pocketed it, having exchanged glances with Rosalie that hinted at her feelings.

“She is in love with Albert Savarus!” he concluded. As he prepared to leave, Rosalie stopped him. “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!” When he questioned how she knew the writing, her passionate reply revealed her: “I love him! What is the matter?” He told her that Albert was withdrawing from the election. Rosalie, stunned, begged him to keep the news secret, realizing that if the election ended, so would the prospect of her sister’s marriage to Albert.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
On her way to Mass Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette how Albert had vanished. An elderly gentleman from Paris had visited Monsieur Savaron at the Hotel National. Savaron turned pale at the card; they met over an hour, and the visitor left with a large package. Savaron, distraught, went to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage, ordered post horses, left his keys, and told his servant to seek other work.

Savaron’s abrupt departure shook Besançon; the Prefect promptly granted him a passport. The affair inflamed local prejudice against outsiders, and Albert was soon forgotten by most—except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness de Watteville urged Rosalie to marry, observing how much she had changed. The Baron agreed to give Rosalie the family estate, les Rouxey, though the Baroness fretted over finances. A match with Monsieur de Soulas was arranged; Rosalie rejected it, insisting she was content. Conflict between Rosalie and her mother escalated, and they endured a tense summer at les Rouxey. After Mariette and Jerome wed, the Baron invested in the estate—moves the Baroness resented. As tensions mounted, the Vicar-General sought to mediate.

One day Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General that she had sabotaged Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo: she had intercepted letters, causing their separation. Horrified at the moral gravity of her deed, he urged her to seek forgiveness and to grasp the consequences.

Rosalie’s guilt deepened when a clerk from Paris, acting for Albert, came to sell his belongings and revealed that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received a letter from Albert expressing complete forgiveness for Rosalie and a desire to remain in solitude.

When the Vicar-General shared this, he pressed Rosalie to reconcile with her mother and accept Monsieur de Soulas. She remained defiant, unwilling to sacrifice what she perceived as her mother’s happiness. The Vicar-General’s death left a void; soon after, Madame de Watteville married Monsieur de Soulas, believing she had Rosalie’s support.

Now of age, Rosalie encouraged her mother to marry, securing her own financial independence. With a substantial fortune she entered Parisian society, resolved to confront the Duchess d’Argaiolo. At a ball she publicly revealed her part in Albert’s downfall, leaving the Duchess shaken.

Rosalie returned to Besançon after exposing the truth, but the emotional cost weighed heavily. The tale of love, betrayal, and ambition’s consequences left a lasting impact on all involved.

Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone on her les Rouxey estate, riding, hunting, and improving the land, making her notable in the Eastern provinces. Madame de Soulas, mother of two, appears younger, while her husband, Monsieur de Soulas, has aged considerably. He remarked to young Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

Mademoiselle de Watteville is known for her eccentric “vagaries.” Each year she visits the walls of the Grande Chartreuse, perhaps inspired by a grand-uncle who once broke through monastery walls to gain his freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon, reportedly to marry, though the true reason remains unclear. She returned after a steamboat boiler explosion on the Loire that cost her her right arm and left leg and scarred her face. Her health deteriorated; in constant pain, she now devotes herself to religious practices at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
