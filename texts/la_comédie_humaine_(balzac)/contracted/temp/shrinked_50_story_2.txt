The Comte de Fontaine, a royalist who fought in La Vendée, was ruined when his estate was confiscated. He married Mlle de Kergarouet, noble yet penniless, and moved his family to Paris. Royal honors briefly raised hopes, but new aristocrats overshadowed him. Nearly returning home, he reentered service in 1815 and became Louis XVIII's trusted adviser. The family prospered: sons won posts, daughters married advantageously, if modestly.

Emilie, youngest, spoiled and socially central, rebuffed suitors with snobbery, rejecting matches for petty faults. As the King's health failed, the Comte pressed to find her a husband to secure their standing, but her winter disdain caused repeated refusals and left their future uncertain.

Nature had endowed Emilie de Fontaine with every quality needed to play the part of Célimène. Tall and slender, she could assume dignity or playfulness at will; her long neck and arched brows suggested scorn and impertinence, her fine black hair and melodious voice could charm or silence. Her alabaster complexion betrayed little except fleeting emotion. Many young men she scorned accused her of acting; she said she merely inspired desire to subject it to her whims.

Monsieur de Fontaine realized too late that home adoration had spoiled his daughter. Constant admiration inflated a pride and selfishness that his gentle counsels could not pierce; affection from her often took a condescending tone, like a child demanding attention. Emilie sometimes showed fondness but frequently withdrew, jealous of shared family affection and blaming fate for her solitude, blind to the inward source of happiness. Determined to resist her sisters’ marriages, she nevertheless envied their happiness; her mother suspected she might be a little mad.

This pride, the narrator suggests, was common among beautiful young women of affluent families: feeling misunderstood by mothers, imagining maternal envy, nursing secret tears, and rebelling against perceived tyranny. They build elaborate ideals of the perfect partner, only to learn that experience rarely matches fantasy.

Emilie’s standards were precise and unyielding: her husband must be a peer of France, a soldier wearing an Order so they could be respected; he must be slim, for she despised corpulence and equated slenderness with romance. Her disdain for inadequate suitors was plain, and Monsieur de Fontaine feared ridicule and the risks of delay.

During the waning months of Charles X’s reign he hosted gatherings to attract matches, but her whims persisted. One morning he summoned her, urged a wise choice, and warned that mutual respect, not rank alone, makes marital happiness. Moved, Emilie feigned affection yet kept her pride.

At a family gathering she again insisted she would only marry a peer; an uncle joked she should wait for the Duc de Bordeaux. Later, at the Bal de Sceaux, she noticed a striking young man apart from the throng—aloof, elegant—and, seeing him watch a pale dancer named Clara, followed out of jealousy. Learning he was not the noble she hoped, she pressed for an introduction; her imagination and infatuation deepened, still ignorant of love’s complexities.

The next morning, before Emilie awoke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. Recognizing the young man he’d insulted the day before, he approached with courtly civility. “I’m a vice-admiral,” he said, apologizing for his drunken outburst and preferring a handshake to family quarrel. The young man accepted, and they rode into the woods, where the Count, half-jesting about duels, fired a pistol into a birch and invited the young man to shoot near his mark. The man obliged and won the admiral’s approval.

During the ride the Count tested the young man about gentlemanly codes—debts, pay, mistresses—and teased his own past recklessness. He promised to introduce the young man at the Pavillon Planat and urged him to dinner to meet his nephew, the Comte de Fontaine. The newcomer—Longueville—found the old man odd but affable and accepted.

When the house party gathered and “Monsieur de Longueville” was announced, everyone rushed to appraise him. Simply but elegantly dressed, he moved comfortably in the Receiver-General’s luxury. His polished manners and apt answers—on naval architecture and the arts—suggested a worldly, educated man; some suspected École Polytechnique training. Longueville accepted flattery but declined dinner, saying he was “Hippocrates” to his delicate young sister—an explanation that provoked ironic speculation about his profession.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine, who had dressed to attract him, felt slighted by his apparent indifference. Emilie, usually charming with newcomers, adopted a simpler, more natural manner that puzzled the family; some thought she was withholding to dazzle later. Longueville pleased everyone without revealing himself, prompting the Count to call him “a cunning rascal.” A sarcastic remark at dinner and Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s warning—that universal perfection must hide a defect—kept conjecture alive.

Despite attempts at indifference, Emilie’s feelings soon betrayed her. Longueville’s modesty and discretion deepened her anxiety: he spoke little of himself or his family yet revealed connoisseurship in painting and music. Once they sang a duet, their intimacy touched a private chord even as his social status remained mysterious.

Her uncle and family schemed to learn more and to meet Longueville’s sister, Clara, arranging an informal dance. Emilie welcomed another person to care for and grew gentler, a change read as love transforming selfishness. On the day of the dance, Clara and Maximilien were principal guests. Clara charmed Emilie with graceful ease—“I’ve heard so much about you from Maximilien; I wanted to know you out of affection for him”—putting her at ease. Believing him well born and seeing his grace meet her ideal, Emilie glittered with satisfaction.

As autumn deepened, the couple surrendered to feeling. Small shared incidents—aimless conversations masking deeper affection, admiring sunsets, gathering daisies, singing duets—knit them closer, each casual exchange confirming the mutual love they carefully concealed.
