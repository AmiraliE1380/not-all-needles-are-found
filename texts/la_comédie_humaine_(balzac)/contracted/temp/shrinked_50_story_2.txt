The Comte de Fontaine, a royalist from Poitou, supported the Bourbon cause during the French Revolution but faced financial ruin when his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet from a noble yet impoverished family and moved to Paris with his large family, seeking royal favor. Initially promoted to brigadier-general, he soon felt unrecognized by the monarchy.

Disillusioned by former comrades pursuing wealth, he considered returning to his estate but supported the monarchy again after March 20, 1815, hoping for better fortunes. He became a loyal servant to Louis XVIII during the king's brief exile, securing positions that helped his sons advance in civil service and military roles.

Despite their newfound status, the Comte struggled with the marriage prospects of his three daughters, especially the youngest, Emilie. While his elder daughters married well, Emilie's high standards and spoiled nature made her difficult to please. She enjoyed Parisian luxuries and was admired for her beauty but remained indifferent to her suitors.

Emilie’s upbringing fostered a sense of superiority, leading her to dismiss potential husbands over trivial flaws. Her father's eagerness for a suitable match faced challenges as her haughty demeanor alienated many suitors. As the political climate shifted and the king's health declined, the Comte intensified his efforts to find a husband for Emilie, but her unreasonable expectations thwarted his plans.

Emilie, embodying the role of Celimene, alienated potential suitors with her pride. Monsieur de Fontaine realized too late that her upbringing had fostered entitlement. Despite his guidance, Emilie's indifference frustrated him. At a social event, she met Maximilien Longueville, whose elegance intrigued her.

Emilie’s uncle invited Longueville to dinner, where he impressed the family but declined due to his sister's health. As Longueville visited more frequently, Emilie's affection grew, though he remained mysterious. With her uncle's help, she organized a dance to meet his sister, Clara, leading to a competitive bond between the two women.

As autumn festivities continued, Emilie and Longueville's connection deepened, but her father grew concerned about Longueville's unclear background. Emilie dismissed his worries, believing love transcended social status. However, when she found Longueville working in a shop, her idealized image shattered, leading to emotional turmoil.

Despite attempts to move on, Emilie remained drawn to Longueville, especially upon hearing hints of his continued affection. Their final exchange before his departure for Italy was filled with unspoken emotions, leaving both heartbroken. Months later, Emilie married the Comte de Kergarouet but felt regret. Years later, she encountered Maximilien again, realizing she still held feelings for him, haunted by memories of their love.
