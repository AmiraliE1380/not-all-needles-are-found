One of the few drawing-rooms where the Archbishop of Besançon was seen during the Restoration was that of the Baronne de Watteville, closely associated with him because of her devout nature.

Monsieur de Watteville, descended from the infamous Watteville, was gentle and unassuming compared to his ancestor. After living quietly in La Franche-Comté, he married the heiress of the Rupt family, Mademoiselle de Rupt, who brought a substantial income. Their union, arranged in 1802, was formalized in 1815 after the second Restoration. Following the death of her grandparents, Madame de Watteville grew more devout, leading a pious life that influenced Besançon’s upper circles.

Monsieur de Watteville, thin and unremarkable, spent his days as a turner and collector of local geological specimens. Some observed a noble soul in him: he recognized early that he would never dominate his wife, so he immersed himself in work and hobbies.

The Rupt residence was grand, reflecting both families’ status. It featured elegant furnishings and exquisite food, with Monsieur de Watteville often acting as his own butler. Madame de Watteville’s wealth was considerable, while her husband’s estate was modest. Their close ties with the Archbishop attracted several notable abbés to their home.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834, guests welcomed Monsieur l’Abbé de Grancey, who announced a favorable verdict in a lawsuit. The Vicar-General said the victory was unexpected in the political climate and credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron, for its success.

Curious, Madame de Watteville asked about Savaron. The Vicar-General said he lived nearby but his origins were unknown, heightening interest. Amedee de Soulas, present that evening, would later prove significant.

In all ages France and England have exchanged cultural influences, especially in language and fashion, often by-passing customs. What is called "English" in Paris is "French" in London, reflecting mutual influence. For example, the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli for a French play, and hoop skirts, once ridiculed, became dominant in Europe. By 1815 English long waists were mocked in Paris, yet French styles soon evolved.

Recently England contributed terms to French such as "dandy" and "lion." The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset’s poetry, inspired a vogue for stylish young men in Paris that spread to provincial towns. In 1834 Besançon had its own "lion" in Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, a young man of Spanish descent who felt out of place in the dull military town. He lived modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment but maintained a lavish appearance, employing a young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger." Amedee dressed Babylas fashionably and spent extravagantly on his wardrobe while surviving on a limited income.

Though seen as a spendthrift, Amedee was adept at budgeting, often dining out to save on household costs. His grooming and clothes were a façade designed to impress the local aristocracy and win the favor of Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a wealthy heiress. His travels and knowledge of Parisian culture earned him a reputation as well-informed despite financial strain.

Besançon was conservative and resistant to change; the local aristocracy—"the Colony"—maintained strict social hierarchies. Amedee’s distinctive fashions, like trouser-straps, set him apart and reinforced his status as a "lion." The town’s aristocrats, eager to appear modern to visiting Parisians, welcomed his cosmopolitan touch.

Amedee aimed to marry Rosalie, who had been raised in a strict religious household with little exposure to the wider world. At eighteen she was modest and unassuming, educated under her mother’s watchful eye; domestic skills were emphasized over social graces and she was sheltered from contemporary influences.

Despite a passive demeanor, Rosalie had a strong spirit from her family lineage. Her mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective and controlling, convinced her daughter’s honor was at stake. This produced tension as Amedee courted Rosalie: the Baroness’s strictness caused Rosalie to suppress any feelings, creating a complex relationship marked by maternal jealousy and uncertainty.

As Amedee pursued Rosalie he navigated Besançon’s social intricacies, aiming for a favorable match while preserving his reputation. The town’s elite found him charming, and talk of an engagement circulated among the nobility, yet Rosalie’s upbringing and her mother’s influence complicated his efforts, since she remained largely unaware of the romantic expectations around her.

Amedee de Soulas’s life in Besançon thus exemplifies the interplay of social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraint as he strives to win Rosalie de Watteville amid rigid social structures.

“Madame,” said Monsieur de Soulas to the Baroness as he waited for his soup to cool, “one morning a gentleman from Paris arrived at the Hotel National and took the first-floor rooms in Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron. He registered at the mairie and had his card, ‘ALBERT SAVARON,’ put on the list of barristers at the Court.”

“The Savaron name is famous,” Mademoiselle de Watteville observed.

“He’s a Frenchman, not of the Brabant line,” Amedee de Soulas replied. “If he claims the Savaron arms he must add a bar-sinister. The only remaining Brabant heir is a rich Miss de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister denotes a bastard, but even a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble,” Rosalie added.

“Enough,” the Baroness interrupted. “You insisted she learn heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville said with a smile. “She knows it well.”

“Please continue,” the Baroness encouraged Monsieur de Soulas.

“In a town like Besançon everything is catalogued,” he went on. “Albert Savaron was received without fuss by local lawyers, who wondered who had sent him and what he hoped to do—especially since he left his card with judges instead of calling. Three days later no one paid him much mind. He hired Jerome, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who could cook a little. Savaron kept to himself; no one had seen much of him.”

“Does he go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends the early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He rises very early, works until eight, breakfasts, walks in his garden—fifty or sixty turns—dines, and is in bed by six or seven.”

“How do you know all this?” Madame de Chavoncourt wanted to know.

“I live on Rue Neuve, opposite his house; my servant speaks with Jerome.”

“And you gossip with Babylas?” someone laughed.

“How did you come to hire a stranger for your defense?” the Baroness asked the Vicar‑General.

“The President of the Court assigned Savaron to defend a half-witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man innocent and exposed the real culprits, who were arrested. That defense impressed the Court and the jury. Then a merchant put a difficult case in his hands, which he won. Monsieur Berryer advised us to employ him, promising success. I met him and had confidence in him—and I was right.”

“Is he extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” the Vicar‑General replied.

“Tell us more,” urged Madame de Watteville.

“The first time I met him,” the Abbe de Grancey recounted, “he received me in an outer room lined with books, modestly furnished. I had not expected a young advocate. His dress was odd—black dressing gown, red cord, red slippers, red waistcoat, red smoking cap.”

“The devil’s colors!” Madame de Watteville exclaimed.

“Yes,” the Abbe continued, “but he has a magnificent head. Thick black hair streaked with gray, a round white throat, a broad forehead marked by thought, an olive complexion, a square nose, and eyes like an eagle. He is passionate yet calm; his voice is sweet but can thunder.”

“Monsieur Savaron is of average height, with hands like a prelate,” the Abbe added.

“The second time I saw him he received me in his shabby bedroom; the third I found him having a frugal breakfast in his library. He is no ordinary man; there is something heavy on him. His face is terrible and gentle at once; he seems to carry a great burden.”

“Why would such an eloquent man leave Paris for Besançon?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. “Doesn’t he know how little a stranger can do here?”

“After seeing his head and hearing him reply to a leading Paris lawyer,” the Abbe said, “I believe he will yet make a name.”

“Why speak of him? You’ve paid your bill,” Madame de Watteville remarked, noting her daughter’s rapt attention.

The conversation moved on, but the Abbe’s portrait of Savaron had taken root in Rosalie. For the first time she encountered someone who seemed truly exceptional, in stark contrast to the dull Count who had courted her. He presented an enigma: eloquent, serious, isolated, and apparently burdened. She replayed the Abbe’s words in her mind, particularly the description of his forehead and hands, and felt a new, restless curiosity.

“Rosalie, why aren’t you eating?” her mother asked.

“I’m not hungry, mamma,” she answered, lost in thought.

She realized she had seen a lighted window from her bed that belonged to him. “I must see him,” she decided.

“Is the Chapter’s lawsuit settled?” she asked the Abbe, trying to appear casual.

“It might go to the Court of Appeal, but our adversaries will hesitate,” he said.

After dinner, Rosalie listened for any mention of Savaron, but there was little more than congratulations to the Abbe. Eager for bedtime, she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse Savaron’s windows. When the hour came she felt a thrill watching the candlelight through the trees and thought of the furniture and color of his rooms. “While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God,” she imagined.

The question of educating girls—the tension between informing them and restraining them—was briefly noted: too much enlightenment risks precociousness; too much suppression breeds rebellion. For Rosalie, the Abbe’s portrait had torn aside the usual restraint and kindled a desire—part romantic, part intellectual—to know this man.

The next morning, while dressing, she saw Albert walking in the garden beside the Hotel de Rupt. “At least I can see him here,” she thought. Determined to enter his house and hear his voice, she planned to engineer an opportunity.

She spent the day embroidering with the air of someone distracted. She decided to go to confession, timing herself to leave just before the eight o’clock mass, hoping to encounter Albert. She visited her father in his workshop and suggested he carve larger pieces and build a grotto in the garden to display his work. He delighted in the idea; she warned him not to tell her mother.

She imagined the grotto with an observatory that would give her a view into Albert’s private room. Young girls often contrive elaborate lengths for the sake of a man, even if he never knows.

When Sunday arrived Rosalie dressed with care; her maid Mariette smiled at her nervousness. She arrived at church before mass and took a place where she might see Albert enter. He had a solemn, burdened air; his gaze was intense. To Rosalie, everything about him now seemed filled with meaning. Boldly she stood in his path to exchange glances, and a warmth surged through her.

After confession she found a seat to watch him; by the time Mass ended he had gone. “The Vicar‑General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besançon? I must know everything!” With renewed resolve she turned her thoughts to the grotto plan.

“Mamma,” she said one day, “my father has an idea for a Belvedere in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of whatever amuses your father,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly.

“From there we’ll be able to see into Monsieur de Soulas’ rooms, and he may see us—perhaps remarks will be made—”

“Do you presume to guide your parents, Rosalie?” her mother snapped.

“I’ll say no more, mamma,” she answered. But the plan pressed on. Monsieur de Watteville and their carpenter devised a grotto hidden from one neighbor yet giving a view toward Savaron’s windows. Periwinkles, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper would frame a rustic interior with a mirror, ottoman, and bark inlay table. A chandelier carved by Monsieur de Watteville himself completed the idea. The garden project was commissioned and rumors spread: the Wattevilles were building a lovely belvedere—“a thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed.

Rosalie felt a flush of pride. No one suspected that the supposed simple-witted girl had planned the arrangement chiefly to see Savaron’s private study.

Meanwhile Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon forgotten amid envy from other lawyers. He remained secluded, avoiding social gatherings, which increased his anonymity. Yet he kept winning complex cases: three successes at the Commercial Tribunal brought him four prominent merchant clients who recognized his legal skill.

On the day the Wattevilles inaugurated their Belvedere, Savaron was launching an enterprise of his own. Quietly, through connections among local merchants, he planned a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review. It was financed by forty shares of five hundred francs each subscribed by his first ten clients, who saw the need to promote Besançon as a trade center.

To compete with Strasbourg and Dijon, Besançon needed to become a center of commerce and enlightenment. Savaron wanted to challenge the literary dominance of the West and bring light to Eastern France. His ideas appealed to merchants who fancied they were furthering their own interests.

Savaron kept his name out of the public foreground. He let his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, handle accounts, while Savaron retained editorial control and a share of any profits. The Review invited contributions from various regions and, with a low subscription price, quickly gained five hundred subscribers.

To avoid bruising local pride, Savaron invited Alfred Boucher, Monsieur Boucher’s eager son, to manage the literary side. Savaron subtly guided Alfred’s contributions while letting him believe he was in charge. The merchants were pleased: with a few more subscribers the publication would yield dividends for shareholders, while the editor remained unpaid.

After the third issue the Review began to be noticed by other French publications; Savaron read them at home. The third number contained a tale attributed to him that stirred talk in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room, though some of Besançon’s higher circles dismissed it for its liberal sentiments.

“Papa,” Rosalie said, “there’s a Review published in Besançon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room—mamma won’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.”

Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please Rosalie, subscribed and lent her the four issues. That night she devoured the tale—the first she had ever read. Given her limited reading experience, its effect on her was profound. She hoped to find in it the sentiments or even the life of Albert. From the opening pages she felt convinced it reflected him; critics would later note that the author had drawn from his own experiences as if confessing.

In 1823 two young men left Lucerne for a holiday in Switzerland to tour the lake of the Four Cantons. The journey offered striking mountain, river, and meadow views. Passing the hamlet of Gersau, one friend, Rodolphe, was captivated by a newly built house and a woman at the window and decided to stay; his friend Leopold continued the trip.

Rodolphe and Leopold had studied law together: Leopold was destined to be a notary in Paris, while Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a more unsettled background. His mother had sacrificed everything to secure his education, hoping to improve his prospects. Passionate and sensitive, Rodolphe was often led by desire, a contrast to Leopold’s calm demeanor.

In Gersau Rodolphe lodged with the Bergmanns, who had taken him in. He learned the woman at the window was Fanny Lovelace, who had moved there with her elderly father for his health. Moderately well off, they lived simply; Fanny supported her father by translating and writing. Rodolphe was taken by her beauty and musical talent.

The next day he sought permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens; though initially hesitant they allowed him to explore. There he noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, who suggested a distinctive past. Eager to meet Fanny, Rodolphe encountered the Bergmanns’ protectiveness.

That evening he heard Fanny singing and was enchanted. He later met Fanny and Gina, but their conversation was cut short when an unknown assailant attacked and wounded him. Fanny and Gina aided him and he was taken to safety.

While recovering, Rodolphe received devastating news: his mother had died. The loss plunged him into deep depression, but he found solace in Fanny’s company. They bonded over shared interests, and Rodolphe confessed his feelings, only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite this, Fanny’s affection for Rodolphe grew and they shared intimate moments.

Fanny revealed that her husband was not as old as he seemed and that she had married him under duress. Rodolphe was torn between his love for her and the reality of her marriage. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential.

Their relationship deepened, yet Rodolphe struggled with jealousy and insecurity, especially when news arrived of Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past. Tito’s presence provoked rivalry in Rodolphe, who feared losing her.

Despite these challenges, Rodolphe and Fanny’s connection remained strong. They shared dreams of freedom and a future together, but the complexities of their circumstances—Rodolphe’s ambition and Fanny’s loyalty to her husband—created persistent tension. Rodolphe wrestled with the demands of love, ambition, and the sacrifices required for happiness. The beauty of their surroundings mirrored the intensity of their emotions, yet the realities of life threatened to overshadow their dreams.

For twenty days Rodolphe stayed at the Bergmanns’, watching Francesca almost unconsciously. He perceived in her the youthful imprudence and unbroken spirit of a woman who sometimes resisted love and sometimes yielded. The old man treated her like a father; her gratitude awakened a native nobility that intrigued Rodolphe and made her an enigma.

Those days mixed secret joys and melancholy, their quarrels as delightful as their reconciliations. Rodolphe grew captively fond of Francesca’s steady tenderness, which often showed a jealous exactness over trifles.

One evening, when Francesca declared she wanted to leave Gersau because it lacked luxury, Rodolphe asked if she cared for luxury. She answered passionately, equating her fondness for luxury with a love of art and beauty, and insisting she had never complained during hardships. With a mischievous air she argued that it was natural for ordinary people to aspire to greatness, revealing her charming character.

She then begged Rodolphe not to accompany them to Geneva, citing town gossip and her wish to shield her husband’s reputation. Though she affirmed her love for Rodolphe, she demanded it remain concealed; she kissed him and left him astonished.

The next day the Bergmanns’ lodgers departed early. Restless, Rodolphe went to Vevay and then to Geneva at the end of October, taking a long route. To avoid the town’s discomforts he rented a room in Eaux-Vives. His first inquiry was after Italian refugees, especially a bookseller named Lamporani; the landlord replied that the only new arrivals were Prince and Princess Colonna and Prince and Princess Gandolphini.

On his first walk to the Villa Diodati the beauty of the place struck him. Approaching, he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage with an old lady and felt dizzy with surprise. Learning they were at the Colonna residence, a veil seemed to lift: the implications of their past interactions became clear. He feared he had been a mere whim for Francesca, struggling with the reality of her noble standing.

He sent his card to Prince Gandolphini and was received by the false Lamporani, who invited him to a concert that evening. Though curious, Rodolphe refrained from requesting an audience with the Princess and returned to Eaux-Vives with mingled anticipation and anxiety. As the evening approached his passion deepened and he felt compelled to elevate his social position to match hers.

When he finally saw her at the concert she was dazzling; their eyes met in an electric instant. Francesca’s beauty and grace captivated him and he felt a profound connection, convinced she had remained faithful to their past despite her rank. After the concert he was introduced to the Colonnas and to Francesca, who playfully styled herself “Princess Rodolphini.” The jest revived memories of their time in Gersau; he was overwhelmed by the beauty of her voice and the intimacy of their proximity.

In the ensuing days Francesca perceived Rodolphe’s deep love, which for her felt natural. She said their affection should be faithful and enduring, whatever the distance. Rodolphe, however, was driven by ambition; he wanted to achieve greatness so Francesca could be proud of him.

Francesca herself combined gaiety and tenderness. Once destined for a cloister, family misfortunes had compelled her to marry Prince Gandolphini to preserve her family’s standing. Yet despite her noble birth she had acquired a taste for independence and liberal ideas, inclining her toward revolutionary sentiments.

Rodolphe spent the winter in Geneva where time flew in the company of the intelligent and beautiful Francesca. But with spring came inner turmoil: Francesca’s calmness contrasted with his emotional unrest. He wished she would show more feeling, believing Italian women to be more expressive. One day on the terrace he voiced a bitter complaint about the happiness of others, singling out a woman of noble lineage; his words wounded Francesca, and she withdrew. Rodolphe followed and found her in tears. In a moment of passion he kissed her, but she repelled him and asserted her dignity.

Francesca then ordered him to leave, declaring they would not meet again until Naples. Her command was firm; Rodolphe, recognizing the strength of her will, complied.

As Mademoiselle de Watteville finished her narrative, Rosalie's cheeks burned with rage and she wept. The novel, told through a man's experiences, was her first encounter with such a love story and stirred deep emotion. Her agitation sprang from jealousy of Francesca Colonna, whom she believed to be Albert's beloved.

Consumed by curiosity and a fierce desire to win Albert, Rosalie—despite insecurities about her looks and lack of musical talent—feared he would never love her and became determined to learn the truth. Her imagination ran wild with schemes typical of a young woman raised under strict rules: sneaking into Albert's garden, writing to him, or contriving encounters at her father's land dispute to draw him into her drawing-room.

Watching Albert's window, she observed something that might reveal his secrets: Jérôme, Albert's servant, being helped over the wall by Mariette, the maid. Rosalie noted Mariette's plainness but also her steadiness and means, which explained Jérôme's interest. The incident taught Rosalie a dangerous lesson: her mother's rigid upbringing could be undermined by a servant's choices.

The next morning Rosalie accompanied Mariette to Mass and seized the chance to question her about Jérôme. She confronted Mariette about their secret meetings and, to her surprise, offered support in return for access to the letters Albert passed through Jérôme. Relieved, Mariette agreed.

Rosalie's obsession deepened as she read a letter from Albert to his friend Léopold revealing his noble lineage and past struggles. She was enthralled by his ambitions and the complexity of his character, intensifying her feelings. The letter portrayed a man striving for success while devoted to the woman he loved—Francesca.

As she continued to read Albert's correspondence, Rosalie became more aware of her inadequacy and the moral cost of spying. She wrestled with guilt over invading his privacy but rationalized it as proof of love. Her passion slid into dangerous infatuation, prompting penances and self-imposed suffering intended to demonstrate devotion.

One evening Mariette brought a letter meant for Francesca, plunging Rosalie into jealousy and doubt. She hesitated but yielded to the urge to read it. The letter confirmed Albert's enduring love for Francesca, outlining his ambitions and hopes for a shared future.

Rosalie's heart ached as she absorbed its contents and grasped the depth of his feeling for another. Although she had once resolved to respect their relationship, she found herself torn between admiration for their fidelity and her own longing. In a clear moment she chose to step back, seeing the nobility of their bond and acknowledging her shortcomings.

Preparing to return the letter with Mariette, Rosalie reflected on her feelings and the nature of love. She felt sorrow and resignation, recognizing that true love often requires sacrifice, and began to accept the reality of her unrequited affection.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil; by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. In solitude that imagined struggle fed her passion and dark thoughts, prompting numerous plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist, and this story functions as a cautionary tale.

That winter Albert de Savarus made notable advances in Besançon, eagerly awaiting the Chamber’s dissolution. He’d secured the backing of a wealthy contractor tied to the moderate party. The Romans had invested heavily in town water supplies, and Besançon drew water from Arcier, a distant hill. Restoring an aqueduct to drink the Romans’ water seemed absurd where the Doubs flowed, but persuading citizens would produce large expenses benefiting the contractor.

Savarus argued the river water was undrinkable and promoted Arcier water. Local Review articles echoed him, and townspeople across political lines bought the romance of drinking like the Romans. The issue became a focal point driven by hidden interests. Opponents were dismissed as old-fashioned while Savarus’s projects dominated discussion. After eighteen months he had stirred the stagnant town and gained influence without real popularity.

He also won several lawsuits for local priests over the winter, and his ambition consumed him. His apparent indifference to money masked a form of moral usury: he expected a far greater reward. In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with funds from Léopold Hannequin, which also qualified him for election. The Abbé de Grancey, who saw Savarus’s ambition clearly, remarked on his remarkable nature and hinted he’d chosen a wrong turn in life.

Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hôtel de Rupt, initially content to observe him from afar. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, produced ten thousand francs a year, but management was neglected by the Baron, who left it to an old servant, Modinier. The Baron’s indifference sprang from his wife’s substantial income; the family often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque spot shaped by the famous Watteville.

Watteville had built a dam and lake, enhancing the estate, but his heirs maintained a dubious claim over the village of Riceys, which historically enjoyed rights to the land. The Baron’s actions left a legacy of usurpation, and his descendants continued to assert control over the village.

Rosalie’s efforts were complex and subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey; the lake’s beauty captivated her and she associated it with love. During a suggested boat trip her father confided about legal troubles with Riceys over land rights. Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus as their lawyer, stressing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for the estate. She argued a lawsuit would enliven their lives and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey, revealing her determination to secure her future there and to create beautiful gardens.

Four days later the Abbé de Grancey visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing such alignment would compromise his neutrality. The Abbé urged him to weigh the political advantages of backing the Wattevilles, especially for votes.

Rosalie, meanwhile, was determined to keep Savarus in Besançon and prevent his election. She wrote to the préfet, revealing Savarus’s ambitions, but the préfet disregarded her. Rosalie understood the political landscape and sought to manipulate it to her advantage.

As the election approached, a meeting of influential men in Besançon decided to nominate Savarus as a candidate, much to his surprise; he had been initially unaware of these machinations. The Abbé, who had been watching, warned Savarus of potential betrayals and advised him to be transparent about his past.

Savarus delivered a passionate speech outlining his vision and ambitions, captivating his audience. Yet he faced challenges: the préfet worked to undermine his support, and his voter base began to erode. Despite efforts, the political tide shifted against him and he struggled to maintain his position.

On the day of the first voting the results were disappointing for Savarus; he felt the weight of his ambitions and the pressure of the election, returned home in despair, and reflected on his love for Francesca and the uncertainty of his future.

Just as hope faded, the Abbé de Grancey arrived with encouraging news. The priest had worked behind the scenes to secure support for Savarus, urging him to visit the Wattevilles and promising their case would be resolved in time for the election. The Abbé revealed that winning the Rouxey lawsuit could secure crucial votes and encouraged Savarus to pursue a marriage with Sidonie de Chavoncourt to solidify his political standing. Initially resistant, Savarus recognized the potential benefits.

The Abbé’s guidance proved invaluable, and Savarus prepared to navigate the complexities of both love and politics, understanding that his future depended on choices he would have to make in the coming days.

By nine o’clock the next evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were crowded with Besançon’s aristocracy, assembled over the unusual decision to go to the polls for the daughter of the Rupts. The man to be presented was the former Master of Appeals, a secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch. Madame de Chavoncourt had come with her younger daughter Sidonie, elegantly dressed; her elder daughter, confident in her relationship, had made no effort with her appearance—an omission immediately noticed in a small town. The Abbe de Grancey moved among the groups, listening and offering incisive comments that steered the conversation.

“If the Elder Branch returns,” he asked an elderly statesman, “what politicians would they find?” They spoke of Berryer, isolated on his bench and liable to unsettle the Government, of the Duc de Fitz-James in Toulouse, and how voting for Monsieur Savarus might draw Republicans toward them rather than the Moderates.

By half past nine Albert had not arrived, and Madame de Watteville took his lateness as a personal insult. Madame de Chavoncourt urged calm, suggesting he might be detained by a consultation. Rosalie, watching the two women, observed her mother’s indulgence toward Albert, which hinted at the possibility of a match between him and Sidonie.

At ten the delay had become embarrassing; some gentlemen began to play cards. The Abbe de Grancey, perplexed, went to a window where Rosalie stood and exclaimed, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville, and Rosalie went into the garden to check Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent.

“Jerome!” Rosalie called to the servant. When he confirmed Albert had left in a post-chaise, the Abbe cried, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” Rosalie’s face, however, betrayed a joy the Vicar-General noticed and pretended to ignore, wondering if she was implicated.

Back in the drawing-room Monsieur de Watteville announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By eleven-thirty only about fifteen guests remained: Madame de Chavoncourt, the Abbe de Godenars, the two Chavoncourt girls, Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbe de Grancey, Rosalie, Amedee de Soulas—a retired magistrate who had backed Albert’s election—and a few others. The Abbe took up a post near the Baroness to watch Rosalie, whose normally pale face showed agitation.

“What could have happened to Monsieur de Savarus?” Madame de Chavoncourt wondered aloud. At that moment a servant brought a letter for the Abbe de Grancey on a silver tray. The Baroness urged him to read it. As he did, he saw Rosalie turn pale and recognized the handwriting; he folded the letter and slipped it into his pocket after exchanging a look with her that betrayed feeling.

“She is in love with Albert Savarus!” he concluded inwardly. As he prepared to leave, Rosalie intercepted him. “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!” she cried. When he asked how she knew, her passionate reply—“I love him! What is the matter?”—laid bare her feeling. He told her that Albert was withdrawing from the election. Stunned, Rosalie begged him to keep the news secret, realizing that if the election ended, so too might her sister’s prospect of marrying Albert.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In the morning, on her way to Mass, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette how Albert had suddenly disappeared. An elderly gentleman from Paris had arrived at the Hotel National and called on Monsieur Savaron. The lawyer turned pale at the gentleman’s card; they met for over an hour. Afterwards the gentleman left with a large package, and Savaron was distraught. He went to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage, ordered post-horses, left his keys, and told his servant to find other employment.

The next day Savaron’s flight caused a stir in Besançon; the Prefect quickly granted him a passport. The affair fed local prejudice against outsiders, and Albert was soon forgotten by most — except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness de Watteville urged Rosalie to marry, noting how much she had changed. The Baron agreed to give Rosalie the family estate, les Rouxey, but the Baroness fretted over finances. She and the Baron arranged a match with Monsieur de Soulas, which Rosalie firmly rejected, insisting she was content as she was.

The conflict between Rosalie and her mother escalated, and a summer at les Rouxey made Rosalie’s refusal public. After Mariette and Jerome married, the Baron made improvements to the estate, which the Baroness resented. As tensions rose, the Vicar-General tried to mediate.

One day Rosalie confessed to him that she had interfered in Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo: she had intercepted letters and caused their separation. He was horrified, stressing the moral gravity of her actions and urging her to seek forgiveness and to grasp the consequences she had set in motion.

Rosalie’s guilt deepened when she learned of Albert’s fate. A Paris clerk representing Albert came to sell his effects, revealing that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received a letter from Albert expressing complete forgiveness for Rosalie and his wish to remain in solitude.

When the Vicar-General shared this, he urged reconciliation with her mother and acceptance of Monsieur de Soulas. Rosalie remained defiant, unwilling to sacrifice her mother’s happiness. The Vicar-General’s death left a void; soon after, Madame de Watteville married Monsieur de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support.

Now legally of age, Rosalie encouraged the marriage and thus secured her independence. With a substantial fortune she entered Parisian society intent on confronting the Duchess d’Argaiolo. At a ball she publicly revealed her role in Albert’s downfall, leaving the Duchess shaken.

Rosalie returned to Besançon, having exposed the truth, yet the emotional toll of her actions weighed heavily. The tale of love, betrayal, and the consequences of ambition unfolded, leaving a lasting impact on all involved.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone on her estate of les Rouxey, riding, hunting, and improving the land, which made her well known in the Eastern provinces. Madame de Soulas, mother of two, appears younger, while her husband, Monsieur de Soulas, has grown old. He told young Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

Famed for eccentric “vagaries,” Mademoiselle de Watteville annually visited the walls of the Grande Chartreuse, perhaps following a grand-uncle who once broke through monastery walls to seek freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon—said to be for marriage, though the true reason was unclear—and returned after a tragic Loire steamboat boiler explosion. The blast so gravely injured her that she lost her right arm and left leg and was left with disfiguring facial scars. Her health declined; she endured constant pain and now leads a life given to religious observance at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
