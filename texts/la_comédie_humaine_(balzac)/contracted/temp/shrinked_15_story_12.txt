One of the few drawing-rooms where the Archbishop of Besançon appeared during the Restoration was that of the Baronne de Watteville, closely connected to him by her devoutness.

Monsieur de Watteville, descended from the notorious Watteville, was mild and unassuming by contrast. After a quiet life in La Franche-Comté, he married the heiress Mademoiselle de Rupt, bringing substantial income. Their match, arranged in 1802, was formalized in 1815 after the second Restoration. Following her grandparents’ death, Madame de Watteville grew increasingly pious, and her devotion influenced Besançon’s upper circles.

Thin and unremarkable, Monsieur de Watteville occupied himself as a turner and collector of local geological specimens, especially fossils. Some perceived a noble soul in him: recognizing he would not dominate his wife, he threw himself into work and hobbies.

The Rupt residence was grand, reflecting both families’ standing, with elegant furnishings and ceremonious meals; Monsieur often acted as his own butler. Madame’s wealth was considerable, while her husband’s estate was modest. Their close ties with the Archbishop drew several notable abbés to their home.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834, guests welcomed Monsieur l’Abbé de Grancey, who announced a favorable verdict in a lawsuit over family property. The Vicar-General said the victory was unexpected in the political climate and credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron.

Curious, Madame de Watteville asked about Savaron’s background. The Vicar-General said he lived nearby but his precise origins were unknown, which heightened interest. Amedee de Soulas, present, would later prove significant to the unfolding story.

In all ages France and England have exchanged cultural influences, especially in language and fashion, often bypassing customs. The label "English" in Paris is "French" in London, reflecting mutual influence. For example, the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli for a French play, and hoop skirts, once ridiculed, became dominant in Europe. By 1815 English long waists were mocked in Paris, but French styles soon evolved.

England contributed terms to French usage, such as "dandy" and "lion." The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset’s poetry, inspired a vogue for stylish young men in Paris that spread to provincial towns. In 1834 Besançon had its own "lion" in Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, a young man of Spanish descent who felt out of place in the dull military town. He lived modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment but maintained a lavish appearance, employing a young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger." Amedee dressed Babylas fashionably and spent extravagantly on his wardrobe while surviving on limited income.

Though seen as a spendthrift, Amedee was skilled at budgeting, often dining out to save on household costs. His expensive grooming and clothes were a façade to impress the local aristocracy and win the favor of Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a wealthy heiress. His travels and knowledge of Parisian culture earned him a reputation as well-informed despite financial strain.

Besançon was conservative and resistant to change; the local aristocracy—called "the Colony"—maintained strict hierarchies. Amedee’s distinctive fashion, like trouser-straps, set him apart and reinforced his status as a "lion." The town’s aristocrats were eager to appear modern to visiting Parisians, and Amedee provided that cosmopolitan touch.

Amedee’s ambitions included marrying Rosalie, who had been raised in a strict religious household with little exposure to the wider world. At eighteen she was modest and unassuming, educated under her mother’s watchful eye, trained in domestic skills rather than social graces, and sheltered from contemporary influences.

Despite a passive demeanor, Rosalie possessed a strong spirit inherited from her family. Her mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective and controlling, convinced her daughter’s honor was at stake. This dynamic produced tension as Amedee courted Rosalie: the Baroness’s strictness caused Rosalie to suppress feelings, creating a complex relationship marked by maternal jealousy and the daughter’s uncertainty.

As Amedee pursued Rosalie he navigated Besançon’s social intricacies, aiming for a favorable marriage while preserving his reputation. The town’s elite found him charming, and talk of an engagement circulated among the nobility. Yet Rosalie’s upbringing and her mother’s influence complicated his efforts, since she remained largely unaware of the romantic expectations around her.

Amedee de Soulas’s life in Besançon thus exemplifies the interplay of social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraints as he strives to win Rosalie de Watteville amid rigid social structures.

“Madame,” said Monsieur de Soulas to the Baroness as he waited for his soup to cool, “a gentleman from Paris arrived at the Hotel National and took the first floor of Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron. He registered at the Mairie and had his card entered on the list of barristers under the name ALBERT SAVARON.”

“The Savaron name is famous,” Mademoiselle de Watteville interrupted, “one of Belgium’s oldest and richest families.”

“He’s French and not of that family,” Amedee de Soulas replied. “If he claims the Savaron arms he must add a bar-sinister. The only Brabant left is a wealthy heiress, Mademoiselle de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister marks a bastard, but a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble,” Rosalie chimed in.

“Enough, mademoiselle!” the Baroness said.

“You taught her heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville remarked. “She knows it well.”

“Go on, Monsieur de Soulas.”

“In a small town like Besançon, where everything’s catalogued, Albert Savaron was welcomed by the lawyers. They wondered who sent him and what he aimed for, especially since he sent his card to judges instead of calling personally. Three days later they forgot about him. He hired Jerome, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who could cook a little. No one really noticed him.”

“Does he go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He rises between one and two, works until eight, breakfasts, continues working, walks in his garden in long circles, dines, and goes to bed between six and seven.”

“How do you know all this?” she pressed.

“I live on Rue Neuve, opposite his house. My servant speaks with Jerome.”

“And you gossip with Babylas?”

“What else to do while riding?”

“How did you come to hire a stranger for your defense?” the Baroness asked the Vicar-General.

“The President of the Court assigned him to defend a half-witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man innocent and exposed the real culprits, who were arrested. His defense impressed Court and jury. A merchant then trusted him with a hard case, which he won. Monsieur Berryer advised us to employ Savaron given our situation; I met him and felt confident— I was right.”

“Is he so remarkable?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” the Vicar-General said.

“Tell us more,” urged Madame de Watteville.

“The first time I met him,” the Abbe de Grancey recalled, “he received me in an outer room panelled like oak and lined with law books. The furniture was plain: a writing table, six armchairs, gray curtains, green carpet. I did not expect my advocate to be young. He matched his rooms: a black dressing gown with red accents. He had a magnificent head—black hair streaked with gray, thick curls, a white throat, a broad forehead furrowed by thought, an olive complexion, a square nose, and fiery eyes. He can be passionate yet calm; his voice is sweet but can thunder.”

“Monsieur Albert Savaron is of average height, neither stout nor thin, with hands like a prelate,” the Abbe added.

“The second time I saw him he received me in his shabby bedroom; the third, I caught him having a frugal breakfast in his library. There’s much behind that terrible yet gentle face; he seems to carry a heavy burden.”

“Why would such an eloquent man leave Paris for Besançon?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. “Doesn’t he know how little chance a stranger has here?”

“After seeing his head and hearing him answer a prominent Parisian lawyer,” the Abbe replied, “I believe he could make a great mark.”

“Why discuss him? You’ve won your case and paid him,” Madame de Watteville said, watching her daughter listen with rapt attention.

The conversation moved on, but the Vicar-General’s portrait of Savaron transfixed Rosalie. For the first time she encountered someone exceptional, a contrast to the mundane Count who had courted her. Albert Savaron became an enigma she wanted to unravel.

“Albert Savaron de Savarus,” she mused, longing to see him. She replayed the Abbe’s words—his forehead, his hands—and found nothing like him at the table.

“Rosalie, why aren’t you eating?” her mother asked.

“I’m not hungry, mamma,” she answered, lost in thoughts of the prelate’s hands. She recalled a lighted window she had once glimpsed from her bed and realized it belonged to him. “I must see him!” she decided.

“Monsieur de Grancey, is the Chapter’s lawsuit settled?” Rosalie asked during a lull.

“What does that matter to you?” her mother said.

“It might go to the Court of Appeal,” the Abbe answered.

“I never thought Rosalie would think about a lawsuit at dinner,” Madame de Watteville laughed.

“Nor I,” Rosalie said dreamily, prompting more amusement. The rest of the meal passed without further mention of Savaron, but the idea of him lingered with Rosalie. She planned to wake between two and three to glimpse his windows that night. When she did, she thrilled to see candlelight moving behind the trees and imagined his furniture and rooms.

“While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought.

The education of girls is delicate; mothers shape the nation's future, and schools or religious systems must decide how much girls should know. Enlightening them too much risks premature seriousness; suppressing thought can cause sudden outbreaks, as in Molière’s Agnes. Rosalie’s crisis was set off by the Abbe’s portrayal of Savaron.

Next morning, while dressing, Rosalie saw Albert walking in the garden behind the Hotel de Rupt. “What if he lived elsewhere? At least I can see him here. What is he thinking?” she wondered. Determined to enter his house, hear his voice, and see his eyes, she resolved to act.

She stitched through the day with a focused air that concealed deep thought. She decided to go to confession and arranged to confess after Mass at half-past seven, hoping to encounter Albert.

She visited her father in his workshop and suggested he make large turnings and build a grotto in the garden to showcase his work. Pleased, he promised not to tell her mother, sharing her desire to surprise her. Rosalie pictured an observatory from which to watch Albert’s private study. Young girls often contrive much for the sake of a man, even if he is unaware.

Sunday arrived and Rosalie dressed more carefully than usual. Her maid Mariette noticed her fidgeting.

“It’s the first time I’ve seen you so nervous,” Mariette said.

“Perhaps you’re more particular on certain days,” Rosalie replied, blushing.

She crossed the courtyard with a pounding heart and went to church early, praying briefly, then checking the confessional to see if Abbe Giroud was in place. She positioned herself to see Albert enter. No man seemed unattractive to her now; Albert Savaron, strikingly handsome, made a deep impression. His solemn air suggested a heavy burden; his deep gaze hinted at intense thought. She understood the Vicar-General’s words fully: those eagle-like eyes revealed hidden ardor. Boldly, she intercepted his path to exchange glances, and warmth flooded her.

After Albert sat, Rosalie found a spot to watch him during her confession. When Mariette announced Monsieur Giroud, her time in the confessional felt swift. By the time she emerged, Mass had ended and Albert had left.

“The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besançon? I must know everything! But how?”

Resolved, she redoubled her attention to embroidery to hide her distraction from her mother.

From that Sunday, after their glance, Rosalie threw herself into promoting the Belvedere her father planned. “Mamma,” she said, “my father has an idea for a Belvedere in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of everything he does,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly. “Why should I object to something that amuses him?”

“Because from there we’ll see into Monsieur de Soulas’ rooms and he might see us,” Rosalie warned.

“Do you presume to guide your parents, Rosalie?” her mother rebuked.

“I’ll say no more, mamma. Besides, my father mentions a cool room in the grotto for coffee.”

Madame de Watteville approved the design and chose a spot unseen from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows but with a view into Albert Savaron’s rooms. A builder was hired to construct a grotto with paths of periwinkle, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper. The interior would be rustic: a mirror, ottoman, and a table of inlaid bark. Monsieur de Soulas suggested an asphalt floor; Rosalie proposed a rustic chandelier. Rumors of the Wattevilles’ project circulated.

“They’re wealthy and can afford whims—” people said.

“A thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed.

“Yes, a thousand crowns,” young Monsieur de Soulas confirmed. “A man from Paris will decorate the interior; it will be pretty. Monsieur de Watteville is carving the chandelier himself.”

“Berquet is making a cellar under it,” an Abbe added.

“No,” young de Soulas corrected, “it’s on a concrete foundation to avoid damp.”

“You know least of what happens in that house,” Madame de Chavoncourt snapped, glancing at her daughter Rosalie, who had waited a year to marry.

Mademoiselle de Watteville felt proud of the Belvedere’s success, pleased at outshining others. No one suspected that the ostensible plan to enjoy family leisure was actually Rosalie’s scheme to get a closer look at Albert Savaron’s study.

Meanwhile, Albert’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon eclipsed by jealousy. He remained secluded and avoided society, which helped him slip by as a stranger in Besançon. Still, he argued three complex cases at the Commercial Tribunal and won four prominent merchants as clients who appreciated his skill.

On the day the Watteville family inaugurated the Belvedere, Savaron was launching a new venture. Quietly, he had gathered merchants to found a fortnightly paper called the Eastern Review, funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each from his first ten clients, who believed Besançon needed promotion as a trade center.

To compete with Strasbourg, Besançon must become a center of enlightenment and commerce. Savaron’s aim was to challenge the literary dominance of Strasbourg and Dijon and illuminate eastern France. Merchants, feeling their own interests at stake, found his ideas attractive.

Savaron kept his name from the limelight. He let his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, manage finances while he kept editorial control and a share of profits. The Review appealed across regions; at a low subscription price it quickly gained five hundred subscribers.

To avoid offending local pride, Savaron enlisted the Boucher family’s eager son, Alfred, to handle the literary side. Albert guided the content subtly while allowing Alfred to think he was gaining recognition. The commercial backers happily paid only a fraction of their shares. With two hundred more subscribers the publication would pay dividends; the editor would remain unpaid.

By the third issue the Review had earned recognition among French publications. Albert read the other reviews at home. The third number included a tale attributed to him, which stirred discussion in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room even as higher circles in Besançon dismissed it for its liberal bent.

“Papa,” Rosalie said, “there’s a Review published in Besançon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room, for mamma wouldn’t let me read it, but you could lend it to me.”

Monsieur de Watteville, anxious to please Rosalie, who had shown him much affection, subscribed to the Eastern Review and lent her the four issues already out. That night Rosalie devoured the tale—the first she had ever read. Given her limited experience, its impact was profound. She expected to find in it the sentiments and perhaps the life of Albert; from the first pages she believed it reflected his experiences, convinced that the tale was a confession drawn from his life.

In 1823 two young men left Lucerne for a holiday in Switzerland to tour the Lake of the Four Cantons. The journey offered striking mountain, river, and meadow views. Passing the hamlet of Gersau, one friend, Rodolphe, was captivated by a newly built house and a woman at the window and decided to stay; his friend Leopold continued the trip.

Rodolphe and Leopold had studied law together: Leopold was bound for a notary’s post in Paris, while Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a more unsettled background. His mother had sacrificed everything to secure his education, hoping to improve his prospects. Passionate and sensitive, Rodolphe was often led by desire, a contrast to Leopold’s calm demeanor.

In Gersau Rodolphe lodged with a couple who took him in. He learned the woman at the window was Fanny Lovelace, who had moved there with her elderly father for his health. They were modestly off, and Fanny supported her father by translating and writing. Rodolphe was struck by her beauty and musical talent.

The next day he sought permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens; the Bergmanns rented land to the Lovelaces and were initially hesitant but allowed him to explore. There he noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, whose presence hinted at a distinctive past. Eager to meet Fanny, Rodolphe encountered resistance from the protective Bergmanns.

That evening he heard Fanny singing and was enchanted. He later met Fanny and Gina in person, but their conversation was cut short when an unknown assailant attacked and wounded him. Fanny and Gina aided him and he was taken to safety.

While recovering, Rodolphe received devastating news: his mother had died. The loss plunged him into deep depression, but he found solace in Fanny’s company. They bonded over shared interests, and Rodolphe confessed his feelings, only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite this, Fanny’s affection for Rodolphe grew and they shared intimate moments.

Fanny disclosed that her husband was not as old as he appeared and that she had married him under duress. Rodolphe was torn between his love for her and the reality of her marriage. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential.

Their relationship deepened, yet Rodolphe struggled with jealousy and insecurity, especially when news arrived of Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past. Tito’s appearance provoked rivalry in Rodolphe, who feared losing her affection.

Despite these challenges, Rodolphe and Fanny’s connection remained strong. They shared dreams of freedom and a future together, but the complexities of their circumstances—Rodolphe’s ambition and Fanny’s ties to her husband—created persistent tension. As they navigated their feelings, Rodolphe wrestled with the demands of love, ambition, and the sacrifices required for happiness; the beauty of their surroundings mirrored the intensity of their emotions, even as reality threatened their dreams.

For twenty days Rodolphe stayed at the Bergmanns’, watching Francesca. He saw in her the youthful imprudence and unbroken spirit of a woman who sometimes resisted love and sometimes yielded to it. The old man treated her like a father; her gratitude stirred a native nobility that intrigued Rodolphe and made her an enigma.

Those days mingled secret joys and melancholy, their quarrels as delightful as their reconciliations. Rodolphe became increasingly captivated by Francesca’s steady tenderness, growing jealous over trifles.

One evening, when Francesca said she wanted to leave Gersau because it lacked luxury, Rodolphe asked whether she cared for luxury. She answered passionately, equating her fondness for luxury with a love of art and beauty and insisting she had never complained through hardships. Playfully she argued that it was natural for ordinary people to aspire to greatness, revealing her charming character.

She then insisted Rodolphe not come with them to Geneva, citing town gossip and her wish to protect her husband’s reputation. She affirmed her love for Rodolphe but demanded it remain concealed. She kissed him and left him astonished.

The next day the Bergmanns’ lodgers departed early, and restless, Rodolphe went to Vevay and then to Geneva by the end of October, taking a longer route. To avoid the town’s discomforts he rented a room in Eaux-Vives. His first inquiry was after Italian refugees, especially a bookseller named Lamporani, but the landlord said the only new arrivals were Prince and Princess Colonna and Prince and Princess Gandolphini.

Rodolphe’s first walk took him to the Villa Diodati, whose beauty struck him. Approaching, he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage with an old lady and felt dizzy with surprise. Learning they were at the Colonna residence, a veil seemed to lift: the implications of their past interactions became clear.

He feared he had been a mere whim for Francesca, struggling with the reality of her noble standing. He sent his card to Prince Gandolphini and was received by a false Lamporani, who invited him to a concert that evening. Though curious, Rodolphe refrained from requesting an audience with the Princess and returned to Eaux-Vives with mingled anticipation and anxiety.

As evening approached his passion deepened and he felt compelled to elevate his social position to match hers. When he finally saw her at the concert she was dazzling; their eyes met with immediate recognition. Francesca’s beauty and grace captivated him and he felt a profound connection, convinced she had remained faithful to their past despite her rank.

After the concert Rodolphe was introduced to the Colonnas and to Francesca, who playfully styled herself “Princess Rodolphini.” The jest revived memories of their time in Gersau; he was overwhelmed by the beauty of her voice and the intimacy of their proximity.

In the ensuing days Francesca perceived Rodolphe’s deep love, which for her felt natural. She said their affection should be faithful and enduring, whatever the distance. Rodolphe, however, was driven by ambition; he wanted to achieve greatness so Francesca could be proud of him.

Francesca was a remarkable blend of gaiety and tenderness. Once destined for a cloister, family misfortunes had compelled her to marry Prince Gandolphini to preserve her family’s standing. Yet despite her noble birth she had acquired a taste for independence and liberal ideas, inclining her toward revolutionary sentiments.

Rodolphe spent the winter in Geneva where time flew in the company of the intelligent and beautiful Francesca. But with spring came inner turmoil: Francesca’s calmness contrasted with his emotional unrest; he wished she would show more feeling, believing Italian women to be more expressive.

One day on the terrace he voiced a bitter complaint about the happiness of others, singling out a woman of noble lineage; his words wounded Francesca, and she withdrew. Rodolphe followed and found her in tears. In a moment of passion he kissed her, but she repelled him and asserted her dignity.

Francesca then ordered him to leave, declaring they would not meet again until Naples. Her command was firm; Rodolphe, recognizing the strength of her will, complied.

Rosalie’s heart ached as she absorbed its contents and grasped the depth of his feeling for another. Though she had once resolved to respect their relationship, she found herself torn between admiration for their fidelity and her own longing. In a clear moment she chose to step back, recognizing the nobility of their bond and acknowledging her shortcomings.

Preparing to return the letter with Mariette, Rosalie reflected on her feelings and the nature of love. She felt sorrow and resignation, recognizing that true love often demands sacrifice. Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil; by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. Alone, that struggle fueled a dark passion and drove her to devise plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist; her story reads as a cautionary tale.

That winter Albert de Savarus made significant advances in Besançon, eagerly awaiting the Chamber’s dissolution. He’d secured the backing of a wealthy contractor influential in the moderate party. The Romans had once invested heavily in town water supplies, and Besançon drew water from Arcier, a distant hill. Restoring an aqueduct to drink the Romans’ water seemed absurd in a town already served by the Doubs, but convincing citizens would generate substantial expenses benefiting the contractor.

Savarus argued the river water was unfit and promoted Arcier water. Articles in the local Review echoed this, and townspeople across political lines agreed on the desirability of drinking like the Romans. The issue became a focal point, driven by hidden interests: opponents were dismissed as old-fashioned while Savarus’s projects dominated discussion. After eighteen months he had stirred the stagnant town and gained influence without popularity.

Throughout the winter he won several lawsuits for local priests, and his ambition consumed him. His apparent indifference to personal profit masked a form of moral usury: he anticipated a far greater reward.

In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with funds from Léopold Hannequin, which also gave him a qualification for election. The Abbé de Grancey, who perceived Savarus’s ambitions, remarked on his remarkable nature and suggested he’d taken a wrong turn in life.

Meanwhile Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hôtel de Rupt, at first content to observe him from afar. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, yielded ten thousand francs a year, but its management was neglected by the Baron, who left it to an old servant named Modinier. The Baron’s indifference sprang from his wife’s substantial income; they often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque spot enhanced by the famous Watteville.

Watteville had built a dam and lake, improving the area’s beauty, but his heirs maintained a questionable claim over the village of Riceys, which had long enjoyed rights to the land. The Baron’s death left a legacy of usurpation, and his descendants continued to assert control over the village.

Rosalie’s maneuvers were subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey, where the lake’s beauty captivated her and she associated it with love. On a boat trip her father confided about legal troubles with Riceys over land rights; Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus as their lawyer, stressing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for the estate.

She argued a lawsuit would enliven their lives and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey. Determined to secure her future there, she envisioned creating beautiful gardens.

Four days later the Abbé de Grancey visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing such an alignment would compromise his neutrality. The Abbé urged him to consider the political advantages of supporting the Wattevilles, especially given potential votes.

Rosalie, meanwhile, sought to keep Savarus in Besançon and prevent his election. She wrote to the préfet to reveal Savarus’s ambitions, but the préfet disregarded her. Rosalie understood the political landscape and tried to manipulate it to her advantage.

As the election approached, a meeting of influential men in Besançon decided to nominate Savarus as a candidate, much to his surprise. He had been unaware of the political machinations. The Abbé, watching closely, warned Savarus of potential betrayals and advised him to be transparent about his past.

Savarus delivered a passionate speech outlining his vision and ambitions, captivating his audience. Yet he faced challenges as the préfet worked to undermine his support and his voter base began to decline. Despite his efforts, the political scene shifted against him and he struggled to maintain his position.

On the day of the first voting the results were disappointing for Savarus; he felt the weight of his ambitions and the pressure of the election. He returned home in despair, reflecting on his love for Francesca and the uncertainty of his future.

Just as hope faded, the Abbé de Grancey arrived with encouraging news. The priest had been working behind the scenes to secure support for Savarus, urging him to visit the Wattevilles and promising their case would be resolved in time for the election.

The Abbé revealed that winning the Rouxey lawsuit could secure crucial votes and encouraged Savarus to consider a marriage with Sidonie de Chavoncourt to solidify his political standing. Initially resistant, Savarus realized the potential benefits of such a union.

The Abbé’s guidance proved invaluable. Savarus prepared to navigate the complexities of both love and politics, understanding that his future depended on choices he would soon have to make.

By nine o’clock the following evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were filled with Besançon’s aristocracy, assembled because of the unusual decision to go to the polls for the daughter of the Rupts. The former Master of Appeals, a secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch, was to be presented. Madame de Chavoncourt came with her younger daughter Sidonie, elegantly dressed; her elder sister, confident in her relationship, had made no effort—an omission remarked on in small towns. The Abbe de Grancey moved through the groups, listening and offering incisive comments that steered the conversation.

“If the Elder Branch returns,” he asked an elderly statesman, “what politicians would they find?” The statesman said Berryer, isolated on his bench, was uncertain and might disrupt the Government if supported. They discussed the Duc de Fitz-James in Toulouse and how voting for Monsieur Savarus could draw Republicans to them instead of the Moderates.

By nine-thirty Albert had still not arrived, and Madame de Watteville found his lateness disrespectful. Madame de Chavoncourt advised her not to let trifles affect serious business, suggesting Albert might be delayed by a consultation. Rosalie, watching her mother and Madame de Chavoncourt, observed her leniency toward Albert, hinting at a possible match between Sidonie and him.

At ten Albert’s absence provoked growing irritation; some gentlemen began playing cards. The Abbe de Grancey, perplexed, went to a window where Rosalie stood and exclaimed, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville and Rosalie went into the garden to check Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent.

“Jerome!” Rosalie called to the servant. When he confirmed Albert had left in a post-chaise, the Abbe cried, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” Rosalie’s face showed joy, which the Vicar-General noticed but pretended not to, wondering at her involvement.

Back in the drawing-room, Monsieur de Watteville announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By half-past eleven only fifteen guests remained: Madame de Chavoncourt, the Abbe de Godenars, the two Chavoncourt girls, Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbe de Grancey, Rosalie, Amedee de Soulas, a retired magistrate who had supported Albert’s election, and a few others. The Abbe de Grancey stationed himself near the Baroness to observe Rosalie, whose usually pale face now showed agitation.

“What could have happened to Monsieur de Savarus?” Madame de Chavoncourt wondered. At that moment a servant brought a letter for the Abbe de Grancey on a silver tray. The Baroness urged him to read it. As he did he saw Rosalie turn pale and recognized the handwriting. He folded the letter and slipped it into his pocket, exchanging a glance with Rosalie that hinted at her feeling.

“She is in love with Albert Savarus!” he concluded. As he prepared to leave, Rosalie intercepted him. “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!” she cried. When he asked how she knew the writing, she answered passionately, “I love him! What is the matter?” He told her that Albert was withdrawing from the election. Rosalie, stunned, begged him to keep the news secret, realizing that if the election ended so too would the prospect of her sister’s marriage to Albert.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In the morning, on her way to Mass, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette about Albert’s sudden disappearance. An elderly gentleman from Paris had arrived at the Hotel National and called on Monsieur Savaron. The lawyer turned pale at the gentleman’s card; they met for over an hour. Afterwards the gentleman left with a large package, and Savaron was distraught. He went to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage, ordered post-horses, left his keys, and told his servant to seek other employment.

The next day Savaron’s flight caused a stir in Besançon; the Prefect quickly granted him a passport. The affair fed local prejudice against outsiders, and Albert was soon forgotten by most—except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness de Watteville urged Rosalie to marry, noting how much she had changed. The Baron agreed to give Rosalie the family estate, les Rouxey, but the Baroness fretted over finances. She and the Baron arranged a match with Monsieur de Soulas, which Rosalie firmly rejected; she insisted she was content as she was.

Conflict between Rosalie and her mother escalated, and a summer at les Rouxey made Rosalie’s refusal public. After Mariette and Jerome married, the Baron improved the estate, provoking the Baroness’s resentment. As tensions rose, the Vicar-General tried to mediate.

One day Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General that she had interfered in Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo: she had intercepted letters and caused their separation. The Vicar-General was horrified, stressing the moral gravity of her deception and urging her to seek forgiveness and to recognize the consequences she had set in motion.

Rosalie’s guilt deepened when a Paris clerk arrived to sell Albert’s effects, revealing that he had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received a letter from Albert expressing complete forgiveness for Rosalie and his wish to remain in solitude.

When the Vicar-General shared this, he urged reconciliation with her mother and acceptance of Monsieur de Soulas. Rosalie remained defiant, unwilling to sacrifice her mother’s happiness. The Vicar-General’s death left a void; soon after, Madame de Watteville married Monsieur de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support.

Now legally of age, Rosalie encouraged the marriage and thus secured her independence. With a substantial fortune she entered Parisian society intent on confronting the Duchess d’Argaiolo. At a ball she publicly revealed her role in Albert’s downfall, leaving the Duchess shaken.

Rosalie returned to Besançon, having exposed the truth, yet the emotional toll of her actions weighed heavily. The tale of love, betrayal, and ambition unfolded, leaving a lasting impact on all involved.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone on her estate of les Rouxey, riding, hunting, and improving the land, and was well known in the Eastern provinces. Madame de Soulas, who has two children, appears younger; her husband, Monsieur de Soulas, has grown old. He remarked to young Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

Mademoiselle de Watteville was known for eccentric vagaries. Each year she visited the walls of the Grande Chartreuse, perhaps following a grand-uncle who once broke through monastery walls to seek freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon, ostensibly for marriage, though the true reason was unclear. She returned after a Loire steamboat boiler explosion that so grievously injured her she lost her right arm and left leg and was left with disfiguring facial scars. Her health declined; in constant pain, she now leads a life of religious observance at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
