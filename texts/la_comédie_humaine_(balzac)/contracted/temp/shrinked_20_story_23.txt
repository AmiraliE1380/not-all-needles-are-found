BEATRIX

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

NOTE

Balzac, celebrated for acute character studies and depictions of daily life, rarely painted real people. In “The Great Man of the Provinces in Paris” some figures recalled actual persons; in Beatrix he borrows traits from recognizable figures: Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin) hints at George Sand, while Beatrix, Conti, and Claude Vignon evoke the Comtesse d’Agoult, Liszt, and critic Gustave Planche.

The opening scene captures an old Breton family’s customs — a social state largely vanished — signaling a shift from past to modernity and standing as one of Balzac’s notable portrayals in the “Comedy of Human Life.”

I. A BRETON TOWN AND MANSION

Some Breton towns remained little touched by nineteenth‑century change due to poor links with Paris. New civilization is for them a spectacle; older customs persist. A traveler seeking bygone human types would find in the provinces echoes of former eras: Louis XV in Provence, Louis XIV in Pitou, and still older traditions in Brittany. Many feudal towns faded, neglected by historians, yet memory of their former splendor lingers where local character preserves history.

Guerande exemplifies this: a well‑preserved feudal town enclosed by walls and moats, with intact battlements and gates. When the mayor planted poplars along the moat he was criticized—locals insisted the promenade had been public for a century. Houses remain unmodernized, their streets resembling those of four centuries ago and now often deserted, so a solitary traveler may wander through a place that seems suspended in time.

The atmosphere evokes ancient customs; even the gendarme appears out of place. Traditional Breton costume dominates the square, creating a sharp contrast with bourgeois and noble fashions. Social divisions remain stark; revolutionary spirit failed to erase rigid class distinctions. Guerande is devoutly Catholic, inward, and resistant to modern ideas.

Geographically, Guerande overlooks salt‑marshes famed for their salt, which locals claim improves their butter and sardines. It connects to France by two roads—one from Savenay and a busier one from Vannes—yet its isolation is reinforced by slow government and the inhabitants’ acceptance of separation, allowing Guerande to endure as a feudal remnant.

The town is a picturesque blend of nature and history—salt‑marshes, tranquil landscapes, and a coastline of some historical note, including a 1365 treaty. A carriage’s arrival is an event that draws barterers from the marshes.

Near the church stands the du Guaisnic family mansion, symbolizing the town’s past grandeur. The family, tracing roots to the du Guesclins, is ancient and noble; though fortunes declined, they remain prominent. The mansion’s grand archway and crest signal pride and antiquity. Inside, a courtyard opens to stables and kitchen; the freestone house retains a grand hall with intricate woodwork, a massive fireplace, and a dining room hung with fourteenth‑century tapestries. Upper rooms serve family and guests; servants sleep above the kitchens.

A half‑acre garden with fruit trees and a sun‑dial is tended by a faithful gardener. Architectural details—Venetian influences and a spiral column—suggest lineage and taste. The mansion’s enduring grandeur, its hospitality across generations, and the town’s preserved life illustrate how environment shapes character and testify to history’s stubborn endurance amid rapid change.

Early in May 1836 the Guenic household consisted of Baron and Baroness du Guenic, the baron’s elder sister Mademoiselle Zéphirine, and their only son, Calyste, twenty-one. The baron, Gaudebert-Calyste-Charles, had fought in the Vendée and Brittany with leaders like Charette and La Rochejaquelein. Before the war he had prudently transferred his property to his sister for security. After years of resistance to Napoleon he returned to Guérande in 1813, having fallen in love with Fanny O’Brien, an Irish noblewoman; they married, and she bore Calyste on the day Louis XVIII landed in France.

Now seventy-three, the baron showed the marks of his turbulent life: a tall, wiry frame lined with deep wrinkles, sunken features, and large, scarred hands that testified to a soldier’s existence. Though frail and often grave, his presence conveyed a steadfast spirit and proud loyalty to his Breton roots. Largely illiterate beyond his prayer book, he still wore the same sturdy shoes, ribbed stockings, and a coat bearing the cross of Saint-Louis. His family attributed his habitual somnolence to long years of duty. Conversations at home centered on the exiled Bourbons, the Catholic faith, and Brittany’s future, with Calyste regarded as the family’s sole hope.

The baron had personally guided Calyste in gentlemanly conduct and prepared him for possible conflict. When the Duchesse de Berry attempted to restore the throne for her son, the baron took Calyste to join her cause, leaving his wife and sister unaware; they returned three months later, and the baron dryly observed, “The barons did not all do their duty,” a remark reflecting their commitment.

That evening the baron slept in his armchair while his wife read the newspaper. Fanny O’Brien, the baroness, at forty-two retained delicate beauty and Irish refinement: glowing skin, elegant dress, ringleted hair, and a warm, tender manner. Nearby sat Mademoiselle Zéphirine, blind and knitting. Despite her age she kept a proud, cheerful bearing and had refused cataract surgery to avoid burdening her brother financially. Her bond with the baroness was strong; she embraced her domestic role and cared for her brother.

As the sun set, warming the old furniture, silence deepened. Zéphirine, sensing the baroness’s preoccupation, remarked on it. Mariotte, a servant, entered to light the lamp while Gasselin tended the horses and dogs. Both long-standing retainers were deeply connected to the family: Gasselin a diligent gardener proud of his work, Mariotte steady and devoted. Under Zéphirine’s careful management their lives were intertwined with the Guenics’, and their loyalty was unwavering.

The household functioned with unity and purpose, its daily routine sustained by affection and duty. The monotony of life was softened by these bonds, creating a nurturing atmosphere that supported them through the trials of their time.

When night fell, Gasselin entered the hall and respectfully asked his master if anything further was needed. The baron, waking, replied he could go out or to bed after prayers, unless Madame or his sister required him. The two ladies consented; Gasselin knelt as his masters did. Mademoiselle du Guenic led the prayer, and afterward a knock sounded. Gasselin opened, expecting Monsieur le curé, who soon entered, bowing and offering the customary civility.

Monsieur Grimont, a middle-aged curate in a cassock, had a plump, calm visage that suggested part Dutch burgomaster, part Breton peasant. His jovial air implied a clear conscience; he was well-regarded in Guerande, though he acknowledged the du Guenics’ feudal superiority. He inquired about Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel's tardiness and noted the chevalier had been at Les Touches that evening. Mariotte cautioned against discussing the chevalier’s visits in Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel’s hearing, knowing her taste for town gossip.

When Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel arrived, a page carried a lantern. Mariotte moved her stool to speak with the boy. Thin and elderly, she dressed in the fashion of her youth and always carried keys and a silver snuff-box. Born to the highest Brittany nobility, her family’s legacy was waning: a sister’s marriage to a Kergarouet had annexed her name. She managed her property with strict frugality, admired for an avarice that drew no censure; her modest annual expenses and the frequent visits of nieces hoping to win her favor underscored her influence.

Jacqueline de Pen-Hoel, friend of Zephirine du Guenic, intended to marry one niece to Calyste, the young chevalier, to secure ties with the du Guenics. Though wealthy, the du Guenics preserved a respectful distance, valuing pride and status. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel treated Calyste with entitlement, believing her designs gave her oversight. She held conservative views on morality and distrusted modern influences, preferring old customs.

She asked if Monsieur du Halga would join them; the rector confirmed seeing him earlier. Mention of du Halga provoked excitement as the group prepared for an evening game; the rector fetched a basket and counters with childlike eagerness. A firm knock announced Chevalier du Halga’s arrival: a frail man dressed for his health, once a competent naval officer, now gentle and preoccupied with his little dog. Hardened by emigration hardships, his health suffered, yet he remained a respected figure in Guerande, having served as mayor. The rector, the chevalier, and Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel had long spent evenings at the hôtel de Guenic, where the du Guenics held local prominence. The rector’s cough during the prayer for the king hinted at the lingering political tensions in Guerande.

Mouche is a card game in which each player receives five cards and one card is turned up as trumps. Players may play or abstain; if they abstain they lose only their stake. Winning a trick earns a share of the pot; losing makes one mouche, obliging payment of the full stake and enlarging the pot for the next deal. Players may exchange cards from the remaining deck, but only in order. The trump card belongs to the dealer, who may exchange it for any card in hand. The strongest card is Mistigris, the knave of clubs.

Despite simple rules, mouche is engaging, exposing greed, cunning, and expression. At the Hôtel du Guenic, players begin with twenty counters each, representing five sous, making the total stake five farthings—a notable sum for the group. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel and Mademoiselle Zéphirine take the game seriously, likening it to a financial venture or stock speculation.

A convention from September 1825 states the game ends if a player loses ten sous and wishes to quit. The chevalier and the baron, however, keep play going by offering counters to those who have lost. The presence of Demoiselle de Kergarouet and her aunt raises stakes further: the young women, enamored of Calyste, care little for the small sums at stake.

The nightly games are punctuated by talk of local events and public affairs. Players occasionally forget their stakes, prompting accusations of cheating—particularly aimed at the rector. Evenings can finish abruptly, with players departing empty-handed and grumbling. Attempts to introduce other games like whist and boston have failed; the circle prefers mouche, which outlasts newer diversions.

As the rector deals, the baroness inquires about the chevalier's health; he jokes about migrating ailments. Conversation shifts to the recent death of the Duc de Lenoncourt and reflections on mortality.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel announces the Comtesse de Kergarouet's marriage, provoking gossip about Mademoiselle des Touches, a woman of questionable reputation. The baroness and rector exchange concerned looks, especially when Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel holds Mistigris, which stifles further commentary. The baroness's victory appalls Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, who becomes absorbed by anxieties about Calyste and Mademoiselle des Touches.

After the game the chevalier escorts Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel home. She confides fears that Calyste is infatuated with Mademoiselle des Touches, an actress. The baroness frets over Calyste's prospects, fearing this woman might lead him astray. The rector reassures her that Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel intends to leave her fortune to the niece who marries Calyste—evidence of good intentions—but warns that Mademoiselle des Touches is a dangerous influence, notorious for scandal and dubious associations.

Distressed, the baroness laments the erosion of her close bond with Calyste, feeling supplanted by the actress. The rector promises to investigate Calyste's involvement. The baroness accompanies him to the gate, anxiously awaiting Calyste's return; instead she hears the rector's footsteps fading into the night.

The distressed baroness returned home to discover the whole town aware of her son Calyste’s frequent visits to Mademoiselle des Touches, visits she had supposed private. She had tried to draw him back by taking up her needlework, but her maternal jealousy was futile; Calyste spent more time there and returned later each night, once as late as midnight.

As she stitched, the baroness wondered what could so captivate men about Mademoiselle des Touches and make them neglect family and duty. She longed to meet the woman and judge her character, fearing modern influences that might corrupt her innocent son. Calyste, of noble Breton and Irish descent, had been carefully raised; she had entrusted his education to the rector of Guerande, believing him sheltered from impure thoughts. Yet his schooling left him ignorant of contemporary literature and science, and he remained a fervent Catholic.

The baroness had pictured for Calyste a modest, secure life and hoped to arrange a suitable marriage with a wealthy heiress, aided by an expected inheritance. Those plans looked threatened by Mademoiselle des Touches. When he finally returned at midnight she greeted him with relief, noting his fatigue and emotional agitation, which she attributed to excitement rather than deeper trouble. Still, realizing his affections lay elsewhere made her bitter and fearful of the consequences.

The next day Calyste slept late, indulged by his mother. At dinner she noted his dress and suspected he would dine again at Les Touches. Her sister voiced concern over his changed behavior, blaming Mademoiselle des Touches’s influence and fearing the moral impact on Calyste, who had begun reading varied books under her guidance.

Calyste defended Mademoiselle des Touches, insisting she had rejected his love and offered only friendship. He praised her as a talented artist deserving respect despite an unconventional lifestyle. The baroness remained unconvinced, worrying for her son’s future and loss of values. As he left for Les Touches, her anxiety deepened; she feared the woman would lead him astray and lamented the possible loss of her son. The baron dismissed his wife’s concerns as youthful illusions.

Meanwhile Guerande buzzed with rumors that Mademoiselle des Touches was infatuated with Calyste and exerted a mysterious influence. The rector and other townspeople disapproved of her lifestyle, seeing her as a threat to social norms.

Mademoiselle Félicité des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, was a celebrated writer whose life and works provoked curiosity and scandal. Orphaned during the Revolution and raised by an aunt, she cultivated a strong intellect through extensive reading. Despite beauty and talent, she struggled against societal expectations and the limits placed on women.

Félicité’s early tragedy shaped her into a formidable literary figure—her plays and novels challenged conventions and informed her views on love and independence. Rejecting traditional marriage, she embraced art and made her salon a hub for intellectuals and artists. Drawn to Calyste’s youthful innocence, she found their relationship complicated by personal attraction. Despite public success, she wrestled with aging and the desire to preserve beauty and autonomy. As her connection with Calyste deepened, Félicité confronted the tension between love, ambition, and independence—stakes that threatened her carefully constructed life.

A few hundred yards from Guérande, Brittany’s soil yields to salt marshes and sandy dunes. We descend into a sandy desert punctuated by ponds and a narrow arm of the sea separating the mainland from Croisic, a peninsula linked to Brittany by treacherous quicksand beaches.

At the road junction from Croisic to Guérande stands a country house in a garden of wind-sculpted pines. Built of slaty stone and granite, it has a plain exterior with regular windows and a high pointed roof pierced by gables and dormers. One facade faces the road to Guérande, the other looks over the desert toward Croisic and the sea. A brook runs from the park, crosses the road, and vanishes into the sands.

The house’s gray tones harmonize with the landscape. The estate, Les Touches, earns income from marshes and nearby farms—over ten thousand francs a year. Though the Revolution removed feudal rights, local salt workers still call it “the château.” Félicité, the owner, restored Les Touches while preserving its desolate aspect, adding only brick gate columns to mark the entrance.

The ground floor follows a typical century-old country-house plan, likely built on castle ruins. Félicité converted the entrance hall into a billiard room opening onto a large salon and dining room; the kitchen connects to the dining room via an office. The décor is simple and graceful: gray-painted walls, mahogany furniture covered in green silk, mahogany buffets, horsehair chairs, and engravings by Audran.

Upstairs, Félicité occupies a suite with a salon, a large bedroom, and two cabinets; another suite serves as guest rooms. Guests’ chambers are modest, while Félicité’s apartment shows luxurious taste—Gobelin tapestries, heavy silk drapes, and Louis XV furniture. Her study contrasts with the bedroom’s opulence: modern mahogany and personal items that reveal a complex character.

Outside, the salt marshes, dunes, and distant ocean create a melancholic yet uplifting atmosphere. This stark beauty captivates Camille, who prefers the desolate surroundings to Guérande’s lushness. Calyste also feels drawn to Les Touches as a refuge from Guérande’s confines; his feelings for Félicité, founded on admiration and a desire to love, persist despite her rejections.

At Les Touches, Calyste encounters rich art and culture—nineteenth-century music and literature that awaken dormant sensibilities. Félicité, nurturing his intellect, becomes a maternal influence, inspiring both admiration and affection. She is giving him music lessons, and the house feels alive with creativity. The contrast between that vibrant world and rural dullness profoundly affects him, and he approaches the house filled with anticipation.

Upon entering, Calyste hears Félicité at the piano; the music stops as he reaches the billiard room. Suspecting she is in her private salon, he sits quietly to listen. When her improvisation resumes, it is hauntingly beautiful, expressing deep emotion that resonates with him. On entering, he finds her in tears, anxious over Claude Vignon’s absence.

Calyste offers to search for Claude, but Félicité gently asks him to stay, saying her tears are a natural overflow of feeling. She reflects on the complexities of her emotions and her relationship with Claude, revealing insecurities and desires. Despite her sadness, she finds solace in Calyste’s presence, valuing his youthful innocence. Acknowledging their age difference, she admits rejecting his love from self-awareness and the inevitability of separation, yet laments her inability to find fulfilling connection.

As they converse, Félicité insists on honesty and prepares to tell Calyste her story. She settles to speak over tea, creating an intimate moment; Calyste, eager to support her, sits at her feet, unaware of the full depth of her emotions as they share this fragile connection.

“I promised you this tale of the past, and here it is,” said Camille. The letter’s sender was the Marquise de Rochefide. The old marquis, after marrying his eldest daughter to a Portuguese grandee, wanted a noble match for his son to secure a peerage he had failed to obtain. The Comtesse de Montcornet mentioned a young lady in Orne, Mademoiselle Béatrix-Maximilienne-Rose de Casteran, the youngest daughter of the Marquis de Casteran, who sought to marry off daughters without dowries to preserve his fortune for his son. The Casterans were of the bluest blood. Béatrix, raised at the château de Casteran, was twenty when she married in 1828. She was original—independent in thought, enthusiastic, and passionately attached to Art. Such paths, Camille warns, expose women to dangers that men with stronger resources can better navigate. Her grandmother approved of her marriage to a man of lesser rank. The Rochefides were pleased: the Casterans linked them to the Verneuils and d’Esgrignons and helped secure a peerage for their son in a batch from Charles X, later revoked by the July Revolution.

Early married life proved perilous. Rochefide mistook Béatrix’s ignorance for coldness and branded her among frigid women, driving him back to bachelor habits. Society assumed she would be content to shine socially rather than interfere with his pleasures. Yet Rochefide was not a fool; his clever vanity turned into jealousy and tyranny when Béatrix feigned indifference to his infidelities. Camille analyzes this to explain Béatrix’s conduct. Béatrix admired Camille greatly, but admiration can turn to jealousy. Camille had a notable Paris salon; Béatrix sought to found her own and tried to draw Camille’s circle. She attracted superficial people but lacked the time to cultivate a true society. Camille initially thought Béatrix sought mere celebrity, but soon recognized her grand soul, regal pride, and intellectual scope—from metaphysics to music.

Béatrix’s beauty was striking: slender, fair, with a long, pointed face and a magnificent forehead. Her pale sea-green eyes, often ringed darkly, and sharp yet elegant features gave her a severe line; her waist and noble shoulders were exquisite though her bust less developed. Despite imperfections, she carried herself with an instinctive grace revealing her birth; well dressed, she could seem divine.

Félicité showed Calyste a fine copy of a Mieris painting of a woman in white satin. “Fair women have a precious diversity,” Camille commented, noting brunettes lack that variety. She warned Calyste not to fall for Béatrix, suggesting he would be too late.

Though beautiful, Béatrix lacked the pliant charm of her coloring; her severity could seem harsh. She spent her early marriage judging men and life; the revolutionary disturbances bored her at her husband’s country estate. Returning to Paris after the July Revolution, she hoped the upheaval would produce moral change and took an intellectual interest in new doctrines. She soon saw the regime left no room for personal superiority and chose private happiness over public ideals.

Around this time Béatrix met Gennaro Conti, a Neapolitan composer of talent unlikely to rank first among greats. Conti’s voice and demeanor captivated Béatrix; she fell madly in love and took him from Camille. Béatrix confided in Camille, making her arbiter of her destiny. Camille had been devoted to Conti but knew his true nature: superficially charming yet duplicitous, presenting himself as an artist inspired by divine forces while driven by self-interest.

Camille warned Béatrix about the chasm she was crossing, but passion blinded her. Conti was ecstatic; Camille lent him money for their journey to Italy. Béatrix left a letter for her husband and departed, staying away two years and writing enchanting letters to Camille that expressed adoration and gratitude for being the only woman who understood her.

At that moment Claude Vignon entered, prompting a brief silence. He looked imposing despite a haggard air: a high forehead and a firm mouth hinting sarcasm. Vignon was complex—capable of deep thought yet often paralyzed by it—keen to criticize without always building. His intellect was sharp but indifferent to the material world.

“Which way did you come?” Mademoiselle des Touches asked, surprised.

“By the door,” Vignon replied dryly.

“Am I in the way?” sensing tension, he asked.

Calyste, provoked, attempted to speak but was interrupted by Claude’s sardonic remarks about sentiments. Feeling anger at Claude’s indelicacy and pity for Félicité, Calyste left and returned home where his mother joyfully received him. He took out Béatrix’s letter, which threatened family peace.

In her letter Béatrix described Italy: the fatigue of constant enjoyment and the strains of a new life with Conti; the loss of social equality and the sacrifices made for love. She compared her constrained position to Camille’s freedom, longing for solitude and admiring Italy’s beauty while lamenting financial struggles that would force their return to Paris.

Folding the letter, Calyste felt sadness. His mother noticed and asked. He explained how Félicité had opened his eyes to the insufficiency of his education and the possibility of a nobler life. His aunt suggested a prudent marriage to a good Breton girl, but Calyste rejected this, yearning for the freedom and passion he associated with love. His mother, understanding, embraced him and encouraged him to seek happiness. The family dynamics were tender: mother and aunt attempted to ground him while he dreamed of love and adventure.

After dinner Calyste returned to Les Touches, where Claude and Félicité remained at table. Claude, a gourmand, enjoyed Félicité’s lavish meal. Calyste presented Béatrix’s letter; Claude remarked on the fortune of Parisian women who have geniuses to adore. Félicité defended Béatrix, asserting her grand nature would remain unchanged.

As evening wore on, Claude and Félicité engaged in witty banter, captivating Calyste with their intellect. Claude speculated about Béatrix and Conti’s return, stirring Camille. Calyste, lost in thought, pictured Béatrix as Félicité had described, feeling deep sympathy.

Back home his mother questioned his lateness. Calyste confessed admiration for genius and lamented his lack of it. His mother reassured him, emphasizing the importance of love and happiness and encouraging him, if he must love, to pursue noble women.

What young man, full of restrained emotion, would not be eager to see Madame de Rochefide land at Croisic? Calyste surprised his parents by slipping away early, urged by an unfamiliar vigor as he hurried along the walls of Les Touches to avoid being seen, fearing the perceptiveness of Félicité and Vignon.

At the shore he found a travelers' shelter and watched two boats arrive from Croisic, their luggage suggesting distinguished passengers. One boat carried a young woman in a straw bonnet, a maid, and a footman. Calyste was captivated by a chest stamped "MME. LA MARQUISE DE ROCHEFIDE," feeling an inexplicable connection; he believed he would love her, his heart readily transferring the affection he had once felt for Camille to this new figure.

While the luggage was unloaded he overheard the maid and footman discussing their wait for someone from Les Touches and learned the marquise had already been picked up. Agitated and longing, he hurried toward Guérande, skirting the servants of Les Touches.

Thoughts of the marquise consumed him; he contrasted this eagerness with his former, quieter relationship with Camille. He returned home late to find a letter from Mademoiselle des Touches inviting him to celebrate the marquise's arrival. The letter mentioned the Casteran family, to which the marquise belonged, and reassured his mother of the woman's noble lineage.

Walking to the gathering, Calyste was a tumult of feeling. He arrived at Camille's suite where company had assembled. Camille greeted him, and he was introduced to Gennaro Conti, a man with a Byronic presence. The marquise, reclining on a divan, exceeded his imagination: more beautiful than he had pictured, hair braided with bluebells and elegantly dressed. Calyste felt immediate affection mingled with hope and fear.

At dinner he was assigned to escort the marquise. His heart raced and he struggled for words, daunted by her beauty. She noticed his nervousness and asked if he were unwell; he admitted he had never seen anyone so beautiful except his mother. Mademoiselle des Touches, aware of his feelings, counseled restraint and warned that the marquise could not return such fondness.

Conversation turned to Camille's travels, and Calyste increasingly perceived distance between himself and the marquise, who seemed absorbed by Conti. He felt insignificant beside the artist’s talent. When Conti and Camille performed, their music stirred him deeply; he was moved to tears. The marquise, noticing his distress, turned away, while Calyste grappled with inadequacy.

As the evening progressed, his admiration deepened even as despair grew. The contrast between his ardor and Conti's brilliance overwhelmed him. In a moment of vulnerability he slipped away to a nearby sofa, lost in thought, wrestling with the intensity of his emotions and the painful sense of his own insignificance before such talent.

X. DRAMA

“What is it, my child?” Claude Vignon asked Calyste as he entered the bedroom.

“You love; you think you are disdained, but it is not so. Soon, the field will be free to you, and you will reign—beloved by more than one.”

“Loved!” Calyste exclaimed, pulling Claude into the library. “Who loves me here?”

“Camille,” Claude replied.

“Camille loves me? And you—what of you?”

“I?” Claude paused, then sank into a seat. “I am tired of life, but lack the courage to leave it. I wish I were mistaken; yet I have seen many truths lately. I did not wander the marshes for pleasure. The bitterness I felt on returning to find you with Camille sprang from wounded feelings. I will confront her soon. Two clear-sighted minds like ours cannot deceive each other. Between two such duellists, the combat cannot last long. I will leave Les Touches tomorrow, perhaps with Conti. After we are gone, strange things will befall here. I will regret not witnessing the rare conflicts of passion that will unfold. You are young to enter such dangerous waters; you interest me. Were it not for my disgust for women, I would stay and help you navigate this game. It is difficult; you may lose it. You are dealing with two extraordinary women, and you feel too much for one to use the other wisely. Beatrix is relentless; Camille has grandeur. Beware—you may be wrecked between those reefs.”

Calyste, stunned, felt as if shown an abyss. That Camille loved him while he loved Beatrix was unbearable. Regret over Felicite’s rejection and Claude’s discovery pressed on him. Beatrix’s voice from the salon rekindled his desire to carry her off. If Camille loved him, how could he adore Beatrix? He saw no solution.

Silence fell; doors opened and shut. Midnight; Claude and Camille’s voices rose and roused him. Before he could move he heard Claude tell Felicite bluntly that she had come to Paris last year desperately in love with Calyste but had used Claude as a screen to follow her secret adoration. Claude accused her of grand and ignoble scheming, of thinking him simple and easy to mislead; he said he had divined her. He told her she had never been loved and argued that love fled her because she was not lovable—too profound, too self-contained, repulsing strong men. He said she might charm young souls like Calyste, who enjoy protection, but they would tire. He condemned her as sublime and exhausting.

“You have never been naive,” he said. “Your charm is mystery, abstract, not active. Your strength repulses strong men who fear a struggle.”

Camille, stung, protested, asking whether she was an anomaly.

Claude, gentle yet resigned, announced his imminent departure. He declared he would not continue to serve as her screen; he could not remain the instrument of her deception. He would go back to Paris, to misery and solitude, rather than continue living a lie.

At that moment Calyste appeared. “I ought not to leave you ignorant that I am here,” he said.

Felicite flushed; Claude enjoyed their confusion. He told Calyste plainly that Felicite loved him, but that her love was of a peculiar sort—more a relish for suffering and longing than a woman’s ordinary passion. He teased that she was “in some sense a man.” He also warned that Calyste’s love for Beatrix would both make Felicite suffer and make her happy.

Tears filled Camille’s eyes as she looked at Calyste. Claude, delighting in the scene, added that Felicite had driven Calyste to Madame de Rochefide and dug her own grave by failing to confide in him. Camille, impassioned, sprang forward, kissed Calyste’s hair and begged him to forget Claude’s words. Then, in a bold, almost painful cry, she told him not to love her—yet declared she would love him as no woman would: “Do not love me, Calyste; but I will love you as no woman will!” It was a wounded, lofty avowal that struck both men; Claude knelt and kissed her hand. She dismissed Calyste, sending him home heavy-hearted, his mind alternately full of Beatrix’s allure and haunted by Camille’s tears and kiss.

At home, his mother Fanny sat by lamplight. Calyste embraced her and, moved, cried that he loved her—he loved the one who lived for him and whom he longed to make happy. The baroness, puzzled, asked what had happened. He confessed, “Camille loves me, but I love her no longer,” and was restless with the conflicting emotions.

The next day he had Gasselin watch the road to Saint-Nazaire. A carriage passed; Calyste hurried to saddle horses and intercept it. At Saint-Nazaire he found Camille and the marquise among departing passengers. Beatrix, charming in straw and muslin, leaned on her parasol while Camille took Calyste’s arm. Calyste saw the arrival of Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, the Vicomtesse de Kergarouet, and Charlotte. Camille proposed offering the viscountess and her daughter seats in her carriage, and the viscountess accepted, despite her sister’s objections. Charlotte greeted Calyste eagerly; his family’s teasing and the household banter about marriage prospects followed, but he was inwardly mortified by the thought of Beatrix possibly being matched with Charlotte.

The viscountess, pretentious and eager to show herself superior, accepted Camille’s offer with airs; Charlotte felt the contrast with Camille and Beatrix acutely. When the viscountess learned Madame de Rochefide was present she expressed pity for the marquise’s misfortunes; Camille revealed the separation from Conti and corrected inquiries into its legality: “No, by mutual consent,” she said. The women retreated, Calyste seized the marquise’s hand and kissed it, leaving a tear; Beatrix turned away, wiping at tears of anger. Camille noted Calyste’s response and admired his absorption.

They drove through Guerande; Calyste gave his arm to the ladies and bade Camille and Beatrix farewell. He kissed Felicite’s hand but could not reach the marquise’s, who kept her arms crossed. Camille whispered a friendly kiss in his ear as her carriage pulled away.

Back home, the guests dined. The viscountess tried to discuss literature, but it sailed over most heads. After dinner they played mouche; the general cheer obscured Calyste’s inner turmoil, which his mother alone suspected. Fanny urged him to organize the viscountess’s return in Camille’s carriage; he ran back to Les Touches to arrange it.

XI. FEMALE DIPLOMACY

Calyste arrived as Camille and Beatrix left the grand salon after dinner; he offered his arm to Felicite, who gently chided him: by preferring them to the viscountess he had shown his sacrifice. Madame de Rochefide asked about the Kergarouets’ relation to the admiral; Camille answered. Beatrix, with a half-jest, suggested Charlotte would suit him, and Camille hastily declared the marriage impossible—an announcement that mortified Calyste.

Camille, seeing his dismay and feeling her own blush, let him take her hand and then sat at the piano, playing melancholy variations. Beatrix watched him closely. After an hour Camille took Calyste into her chamber and closed the door.

“My child,” she said, “if you want to succeed with Beatrix, you must seem to love me still, or you will fail. You are a child; you know nothing of women; all you know is how to love. Loving and making oneself beloved are different. If you go your own way, you will fall into horrible suffering. If you rouse in her not pride but self-will—her stubbornness—she might go to Paris and rejoin Conti; what will you do then?”

“I shall love her,” he said.

“You won’t see her again,” Camille pronounced. “Why, you are as poor as Job. Listen. You have too much candor to play a part; I don’t want to corrupt you. Yet deception is the only way to win Beatrix; I take it upon myself. In a week she shall love you.”

“Is it possible?” he cried.

“Yes,” Camille replied. “You must promise blind obedience. Come here every day, devote yourself to me; avoid Beatrix. We will stay together until four; you shall study, I will smoke. You are young, but if you break our agreement, even a brief private conversation with the marquise, you will lose her forever.”

Calyste, simple and moved, pledged obedience. Camille kissed his hand; he embraced her, and she let her tears fall. She sent him off to tell the viscountess her carriage was at her disposal. He returned home buoyant, convinced that in a week Beatrix would love him. At the card-table his gaiety reassured everyone; Charlotte felt her triumph secure. The household enjoyed a modest supper; Calyste’s renewed cheer concealed the complexity of his entanglement.

After he left Les Touches, Camille confessed to Beatrix, weeping: she was forty and loved him desperately—she had shed tears over lost youth and resented the thought of being loved out of pity. She feared traps laid for innocence and the dependence that would follow. She implored Beatrix—who loved Conti—to help her retain Calyste by playing generosity and coldness. Camille urged that Beatrix’s apparent indifference might protect Calyste from rashness; she pleaded for the marquise’s aid, urging her to use restraint rather than jealousy. Beatrix, piqued and curious, left balancing jealousy and generosity, thinking of Calyste. Alone, Camille wept, smoked opium-laced tobacco, and dwelt on the young man, composing in her mind the tragedy of her love.

The next morning Calyste arrived and found Camille at the window, smoking and contemplating the sea and him. She instructed him to stay to dinner but to meet Beatrix with a look of regret and refusal. In the salon, when Calyste glanced at Beatrix with more expression than Felicite expected, Beatrix noted his attention and dressed charmingly.

The maneuvering continued for six days: Camille performed feints and false confidences, delicately wounding Beatrix, who resented the implied distrust yet was secretly amused to see such a great woman exposed to pettiness. Beatrix attempted to show where Camille’s greatness ended. At one pointed moment she asked, with a malicious turn, why Calyste had been kept from dining with them. Camille’s cutting irony and command that he stay produced a blush on the marquise—Beatrix became cold, contradictory, even almost rude to Calyste, whom Felicite then sent away to play mouche with Charlotte. Calyste, absorbed in Beatrix, noticed little of Camille’s craft. Camille reported to him that Beatrix had written to Conti only once that week—an observation that intensified Calyste’s longing. He read through nights for distraction and brought home armfuls of books. His mother, alarmed, implored him to leave Les Touches; he refused.

He was on the verge of breaking his promise to speak openly with Beatrix to read her eyes. One morning, planning to do so, he found Beatrix in the garden after the marsh walk. Camille had suggested a trip to Croisic, praising Calyste’s angelic soul and extolling beauty. Beatrix, outwardly light, seemed pensive.

“Ah, my dear; but you are happy,” Beatrix said, placing her hand on Camille’s arm.

“Yes, happy indeed!” Camille replied, with bitter intensity.

They paused on a bench; Camille rose to hasten breakfast. Calyste slipped into the garden path and, meeting Beatrix, could not restrain a quiver.

“Madame, in what way did I displease you yesterday?” he asked.

“But you have neither pleased me nor displeased me,” she answered gently.

“Am I so indifferent to you?” he asked, tears in his eyes.

“Ought we not to be indifferent to each other? Have we not, each, another, and a binding attachment?” she said.

“Oh!” Calyste cried. “If you mean Camille, I did love her, but I love her no longer.”

“Then why are you shut up together every morning?” she said, smiling. “I don’t suppose Camille prefers her cigar to you, or you your admiration for female authors.”

“Calyste!” Camille cried as she appeared, interrupting him and taking his arm. “Is this what you promised me?”

Beatrix heard and was stupefied by his assertion. Camille’s performance—shocking yet grand—was one of those female sacrifices that can either elevate or destroy hearts; it began an abnegation that might lift to heaven or plunge to hell.

At breakfast the marquise resolved to choke down any rising love; she acted gently indifferent, a stance that tormented Calyste. Felicite proposed a trip to Batz and Croisic; Calyste brightened, but Beatrix refused.

“My position is delicate; I do not wish to compromise—my happiness,” she said, glancing at the young Breton. “Conti is coming back to fetch me.”

Calyste turned pale. Despite Camille’s urging and his own entreaties, Beatrix remained inflexible, showing the obstinacy Camille had warned of. Calyste left Les Touches in a depression of love that threatened madness, pondering decisive means to explain himself to Beatrix.

When Calyste returned home he shut himself in his room until dinner and then continued writing. His mother, alarmed by his seclusion, found him among crumpled papers composing a letter to Beatrix, moved by a fresh distrust of Camille after the marquise’s recent demeanor.

Calyste’s first love letter poured out intense feeling. He told Beatrix his love had swelled since meeting her, exceeding any dream. Though he complained of having nothing new to say about her beauty, he insisted on a profound connection, claiming to understand her deeply. He acknowledged his youth and naivety, likening himself to an insect drawn to her light and feeling unworthy that his ardent devotion seemed to cool her, her disdain overshadowing his worship. He contrasted a past attachment to Félicité with his present truth: Beatrix.

He admitted Beatrix had unintentionally disturbed his happiness but expected no return of affection. Reflecting on love and recalling Camille’s lesson that true love must grow and endure, he believed that if Beatrix loved him they could rise above the fears that had gripped Camille. He implored her for kindness and recognition, likening his love to a martyr’s sacrifice, asking her to engage with him without fear, to let him accompany her outings, and to answer him. Much of the letter repeated the same emotional appeals. The baroness, reading it, was moved to tears—alarmed by the rawness of feeling she had never seen in him.

Torn about sending it, Calyste sought the Chevalier du Halga’s advice on delivering the note discreetly. The chevalier suggested slipping it to Beatrix’s maid, but Calyste chose to give it to Beatrix directly. He hurried to Les Touches, found Beatrix and Camille together, and impulsively handed the letter to Beatrix, who accepted it without hesitation.

After a restless night, Calyste awaited her response. When her letter arrived, he read it in the garden. Beatrix acknowledged his noble feelings but reminded him he was still a child. She affirmed her commitment to Camille, doubting her suitability as a partner and warning that accepting his love would be an insult. Candid about her flaws and jealous tendencies, she said she could not provide the happiness Camille offered and would likely bring Calyste unhappiness. She urged him to seek a young girl worthy of his love who could fulfill his dreams.

The baroness, moved, approached Calyste to understand his feelings and read Beatrix’s reply aloud—admiring Beatrix’s nobility while worrying about her son’s future. She urged him to consider marrying Charlotte de Kergarouet to secure the estate and their prospects. Calyste, however, dismissed his mother’s advice, remained steadfast for Beatrix, and withdrew to write a response reaffirming his unwavering love.

Troubled by his passion, the baroness sought the Chevalier du Halga’s counsel. As she walked through Guérande, her presence stirred curiosity; she found the chevalier, who, amused by the young man’s infatuation, offered lighthearted remarks on love and gallantry. Returning home, the baroness discovered another letter from Calyste revealing the depth of his obsession: he declared his happiness tied solely to Beatrix, rejected marriage to anyone else, and even fantasized about escaping with her, convinced their love could defy society’s constraints.

Alarmed by the intensity of his feelings, the baroness prayed for his well-being. When Calyste returned, she expressed her concern and disclosed she had read his letter. He reassured her of his love, and they planned a boat trip to Croisic the next day, hoping to see Beatrix. Calyste’s letters showed a surprising maturity and clarity, exposing both the depth of his emotion and the potential consequences of his infatuation.

One of the greatest pleasures for small minds is deceiving a greater soul, and Béatrix recognized her inferiority to Camille Maupin, not only in talent but in passion. As Calyste rushed to Les Touches, thrilled by first love, Béatrix reveled in being the object of his affection even though she did not wish to reciprocate. She considered it noble to suppress her feelings, imagining herself mirroring Camille’s devotion and sacrificing for her friend. The vanities of Frenchwomen flattered her, and she felt self-approved in resisting Calyste’s allure.

The two women lounged in the salon, surrounded by flowers and a gentle breeze, their inner turmoil at odds with the serene outdoors. Camille, aware of the upheaval she had caused, felt compelled to guard her secrets against Béatrix, whom she regarded as a cunning rival. To cope with her heartache, Camille sought solace in nature, finding meaning in the world around her. Calyste had become a guardian angel to her, lifting her to higher thought, but Béatrix’s triumphant manner unsettled her.

When Calyste entered with a letter, Camille watched the interaction between him and Béatrix, sensing an unspoken understanding. Béatrix now regarded Calyste as hers, while he appeared guilty and transparent. He announced plans for an excursion, and Camille, anxious, suggested they prepare for the trip. She suspected a conspiracy between them, which left her discomposed.

After Calyste left to arrange the boat, Camille’s fears intensified. Upon his return, he found that neither woman met his eye, leaving him puzzled and troubled. At home he felt the weight of despair, a stark contrast to his earlier joy; his mother noticed the change, but he dismissed her concern.

Béatrix, meanwhile, grappled with feelings stirred by Calyste’s letter—emotions she had not known before. She questioned what Calyste and Camille were doing together, suspecting Camille might be manipulating the situation. This realization filled her with jealousy and anger, and she paced her room in turmoil.

When Béatrix joined Camille for dinner, the atmosphere was tense. Camille sensed a confrontation and tried to avoid Calyste’s presence during the impending clash. The dinner passed with veiled insults and a battle of glances, culminating in a direct confrontation afterward.

Béatrix accused Camille of trickery, calling her actions cruel and manipulative. Camille, unshaken, countered with observations about Béatrix’s character and motives. The exchange escalated, Béatrix denouncing what she saw as Camille’s lack of femininity and compassion.

As the argument intensified, Camille disclosed her deep feelings for Calyste, while Béatrix declared she could not accept a relationship that would burden him. Béatrix’s wish to protect Calyste from her own flawed existence led her to assert she would not be his mistress if she could not be his wife. The declaration struck Camille and ignited a fiercer rivalry.

The confrontation ended with Camille declaring their friendship over and saying she would write to Conti, Béatrix’s former lover. Béatrix stood her ground. The night’s turmoil left both women reflective, their passions settling into a precarious balance as they navigated desire and social expectations.

It was the end of August; the sky was clear and the sea shimmered like molten silver. The marshes were busy with paludiers harvesting salt, dressed in white against the sun. The baron and baroness admired the scene from the jetty; when the baron said he wanted to see the marshes one last time a paludier joked about the Guenic family’s mortality. Then the party from Les Touches arrived: the marquise leading, followed by Calyste and Camille. The baroness felt a strong aversion to Beatrix, who, although attractively dressed, seemed heartless to her.

Calyste made introductions. The baron noted Camille's large ownership of the marshes, and a paludier praised Camille’s character. The groups parted with casual remarks about youth and propriety; the baroness worried about Calyste's attachment to Beatrix. They boarded a boat to Croisic, where the marquise remained cold. Camille scolded Calyste for disobedience; he was torn between anger and love for Beatrix. The passage was silent and tense as they neared the port, where barefoot women unloaded salt.

The Croisic peninsula presented fantastical granite rocks shaped by wind and sea into unique formations: a hollow basin used for bathing and a lone box bush clinging to a distant promontory—a small, wild testament to nature’s resilience. Calyste led Beatrix to a viewpoint while Camille sought solitude among the rocks, dressed for agility and reflecting on her life and the choices ahead, questioning the nature of her love for Calyste.

Calyste and Beatrix spoke of the ocean's grandeur, which turned into a deeper talk of feelings. Beatrix confessed an inner desolation and insisted she could never be with him despite loving him. She feared the consequences of abandoning her present life and the pain of unfulfilled love. Calyste, desperate to prove his devotion, offered to sacrifice everything, but she rejected sacrifice and reaffirmed her commitment to her current situation.

At the rock where the box bush grew, Calyste’s emotion overwhelmed him and a moment of physical intimacy led to Beatrix slipping and falling. Calyste rushed to save her; in the ensuing confusion their feelings intensified. Gasselin and Camille arrived to help, and Beatrix, though hurt, was taken to a farmhouse for care. Calyste stayed at her side, despairing yet hopeful. The doctor assured them she would heal, and Beatrix, moved by his devotion, began to soften.

Playing the invalid, she enjoyed Calyste's adoration—a contrast to her previous relationships—and relished the power it gave her, even as she kept a distance and reminded him of her obligations. Despite her resistance, she found herself drawn to his steadfast affection; she disclosed past struggles, and he vowed exclusive love. Their bond deepened and she began to see Calyste as a source of happiness. As they prepared to return to the rock, she acknowledged the joy he brought but reiterated her duty to another man. Calyste, full of hope, believed their love could overcome the past.

Back at Les Touches they encountered Conti, Beatrix's former lover. The meeting shattered the fragile moment and underscored the complex tensions of love, loyalty, and consequence, leaving their futures uncertain.

The marquise's distress was obvious when Conti arrived unexpectedly and offered his arm to Beatrix, forcing her to leave Calyste, who felt humiliated and conflicted. Though he trusted her love, Calyste was shaken by her visible suffering and fell silent.

Calyste turned to Camille, who explained the complicated power between Beatrix and Conti: lingering ties made it impossible for Beatrix to refuse him. Camille warned that Conti was manipulative and would use insinuations to regain control.

Conti had returned after a failed opera and seemed to exploit the situation. He feigned indifference to Beatrix’s new attachment while subtly undermining her confidence. At dinner his jovial manner concealed intent; veiled remarks about women’s loyalty wounded Beatrix and Camille deeply.

Later Beatrix confided in Camille that she felt helpless against Conti’s influence and even blamed Camille for his premature return. Their conversation grew bitter, revealing rivalry: Beatrix claimed she would always hold a place in Calyste’s heart, and Camille retorted coldly about the fleeting nature of Beatrix’s reign.

Meanwhile Conti and Calyste had a tense exchange. Conti professed disinterest and cast himself as the victim, urging Calyste to press his suit and predicting Beatrix’s pride would spur revenge. Unaware of Conti’s scheme, Calyste disclosed intimate details of his time with Beatrix, which inflamed Conti’s jealousy.

That evening Conti staged a calculated salon performance to provoke Beatrix and assert dominance. Entranced by Conti’s singing, Calyste approached Beatrix with renewed confidence, failing to see the trap.

The next morning Beatrix left with Conti, and Calyste was devastated. Camille informed him; he sank into grief, unable to process the betrayal. Despite Camille’s attempts to reveal Beatrix’s nature, Calyste clung to his belief in her love.

Days became weeks as his sorrow deepened. He wandered aimlessly, haunted by memories, while his family misread his melancholy as a passing mood, blind to the slow consumption of his heart. His health declined; he withdrew socially and alarmed his family. They tried to distract him with plans for a marriage to Charlotte, but his heart stayed with Beatrix. He confessed his love, rejected Charlotte, and pleaded with his father to let him be.

Doctors could not diagnose his malady, and the baron, fearing for the family’s future, grew desperate. Under pressure Calyste promised to marry, though his heart remained elsewhere. The baron’s death marked a grim turn; Calyste sank further into despair. A chance meeting with Camille rekindled a flicker of hope: she offered to help find Beatrix in Paris. Renewed, Calyste agreed to go, believing reunion might restore him, and they set out to leave the past behind in search of a brighter future.

Félicité’s love was paving a prosperous future for Calyste. She wrote to the duchesse de Grandlieu about him, outlining his past and her intentions. After selling her house for two million five hundred thousand francs, she bought another for seven hundred thousand and reserved one million to recover the du Guenic estates, settling the remainder on Sabine de Grandlieu. Aware the duke and duchess planned marriages for their five daughters—only the youngest, Sabine, being marriageable—Félicité intended to redirect Calyste’s affections from Béatrix to Sabine.

During the journey to Paris, Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin) confided in the baronne, who prepared a new house for Sabine and Calyste. The couple were invited to stay at the hôtel de Grandlieu, where the baronne received due respect for her lineage. Mademoiselle des Touches encouraged Calyste to explore Paris while she sought news of Béatrix, who was traveling abroad. The city’s vibrancy began to distract him from his former obsession; noticing a resemblance between Béatrix and the engaging Sabine, he grew drawn to the latter. By the winter of 1837 Calyste, refreshed, listened to his mother’s marriage proposal for Sabine, though he concealed any indifference.

On the day of signing the marriage contract, Calyste unexpectedly refused the benefits offered by Mademoiselle des Touches, causing confusion. Sabine then entered carrying a letter from Camille Maupin. In it Camille confessed love for Calyste and declared her intention to enter a convent, urging him to accept her gifts for his future welfare. She implored him to remember her kindly and to attend to his duties as husband and father, emphasizing duty over passion.

After reading the letter, Calyste rejoined the group and the contract was signed. The marriage took place the following week at Saint-Thomas d’Aquin; Calyste and Sabine departed for Brittany amid tears and congratulations. The duchess and Clotilde worried for Sabine, aware Calyste had not entirely chosen this marriage of his own free will. They acknowledged marriage’s complexity—requiring compatibility and harmony—and recognized that women faced greater stakes than men. The baronne du Guénic, with only one son, was tasked with looking after Sabine.

As newlyweds they felt awkward, each conscious of the importance of their first moments together. Calyste suggested they be happy in their own way and treat each other as friends or siblings. Sabine found this ill-fitting to romantic expectations and was pensive. Their conversation turned to mundane topics, and Sabine pressed him about his past love for Béatrix, which he reluctantly recounted. Hurt to learn their marriage resulted from his rejection of another woman, she nevertheless took solace in his assurances that he would forget Béatrix. Gradually their relationship began to evolve.

In letters to her mother Sabine described Brittany, her longing for home, and the challenges of her new life. She acknowledged Calyste’s sorrow about the marriage and her own difficulty loving him fully—feeling simultaneously in love and affected by his past. Despite her mother’s counsel to preserve dignity and self-respect, Sabine became devoted to Calyste, experiencing a mixture of happiness and vulnerability.

Settling into their new life, Sabine found joy as châtelaine, adored by Calyste’s family and tenants. She described the warm welcome and the deep-rooted Breton traditions that contrasted with her Parisian upbringing. The couple signed contracts to reclaim their estates, and Sabine felt fulfillment in her responsibilities.

Visiting Mademoiselle des Touches in a convent, Sabine received blunt counsel on preserving marital happiness: be capricious when needed, and ensure Calyste never again sees Béatrix—warning that reviving old passions could ruin their marriage. Sabine felt conflicted; she could not feign caprice or entirely suppress her true feelings and recognized her vulnerability.

Sabine’s letters trace her navigation of love and marriage: devotion mingled with insecurity, the struggle to reconcile affection with her husband’s lingering ties to another woman, and the challenge of balancing societal expectations with personal identity. The narrative captures the delicate interplay of love, duty, and selfhood within marriage, showing how careful management of emotion and circumstance is required for such unions to endure.

Guerande, July 1838.

To Madame la Duchesse de Grandlieu:

Ah, my dear mother! After three months I know what jealousy is. My heart is torn; I feel betrayed and unloved. How fortunate I am to have you to confide in!

It is enough for wives to hear: “Here’s a key to your palace; go everywhere, but avoid Les Touches!” to kindle curiosity. Mademoiselle des Touches has planted bitterness in me. Why forbid Les Touches? What happiness could depend on avoiding a mere house in Brittany? My curiosity forced me to ask, “What sort of place is Les Touches?”

“Les Touches belongs to you,” my mother-in-law said.

“If Calyste had never set foot in Les Touches!” Aunt Zephirine cried.

“He would not be my husband,” I answered.

“Then you know what happened there?” my mother-in-law asked slyly.

“It is a place of perdition!” Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel declared. “Mademoiselle des Touches committed many sins there.”

“But they saved her soul and enriched a convent,” the Chevalier du Halga objected. “The Abbe Grimont told me she gave a hundred thousand francs to the nuns.”

“Would you like to visit Les Touches?” my mother-in-law asked.

“No, no!” I replied hastily.

That little scene felt like a damnable drama, repeated in different guises. Finally my mother-in-law conceded, “I understand why you don’t go to Les Touches, and I think you’re right.”

You must see, dear mother, that such an involuntary stab forces one to question one’s happiness. Calyste never suggested we visit that hermitage, now his property. But love makes us blind; his silence inflamed me. When I asked, “What are you afraid of at Les Touches?” he simply said, “Let’s go there.”

So I was caught, like other women who wish to be caught, letting chance decide. Les Touches is enchanting, artistically accomplished. I delighted in that place of horror where Mademoiselle des Touches had forbidden me to go. Poisonous flowers can be charming; even the devil has flowers. I watched Calyste, testing whether his passion was truly dead. He seemed thoughtful; when I mentioned Madame de Rochefide, whom I call la Rocheperfide, and laughed, he grew silent and asked me to “cease to revive that passion.” When he saw my disappointment, his tenderness increased.

August.

I was on the edge of a precipice, amusing myself like an ingénue in melodrama. A horrible thought struck me: Calyste’s love might be fed by memory; by reviving the storm I once stirred, I could be rekindling him. That cold, unhealthy Beatrix—already I keep one eye open to suspicion, even while my heart belongs to Calyste. To be suspected by the one I love! It happened like this:

“This place is dear to me,” I said one morning, “because I owe my happiness to it; so I forgive you for sometimes mistaking me for another woman.”

The loyal Breton blushed, and I embraced him. Yet I left Les Touches and vowed never to return.

My hatred for Madame de Rochefide—ah, may she die of pleurisy or accident!—shows the depth of my love for Calyste. That woman haunts my dreams; will I ever meet her? The postulant at the Visitation was right—Les Touches is a fatal spot. Calyste has revived past emotions there, more powerful than our joys. Please, dear mother, find out if Madame de Rochefide is in Paris; if she is, I will stay in Brittany. Poor Mademoiselle des Touches might repent her role in our marriage if she knew how haunted I am by our odious rival! I am ashamed and sometimes wish to flee Guerande and the sands of Croisic.

August 25th.

I am resolved to live in the ruins of the old château. Calyste, concerned by my restlessness, agrees. Either he suspects nothing, or he knows and cannot love me. I tremble at finding the awful certainty I seek.

Oh, mother! I am not loved as I love. Calyste is charming—but who would not be amiable when receiving affection from a pure, loving wife raised by you? Guenic, September 18.

Has he forgotten her? That solitary thought echoes like remorse. Ah, must all women struggle against memories as I do? Only innocent young men should marry pure young girls—that is a deceptive Utopia. Better a rival in the past than in the present.

At this moment I am as happy as a woman who fears to lose her happiness and clings to it. I think only of myself—of Calyste. Tell sister Clotilde her melancholy wisdom often returns to me. A thousand tendernesses, dear mother. If my terrors are not illusions, Camille Maupin has sold me her fortune dearly. My respects to papa.

These letters reveal the secret between husband and wife. Sabine expected a love marriage; Calyste accepted something more conventional. Their honeymoon joys did not fully align with hope.

While in Brittany the hotel du Guenic was restored by the architect Grindot under Clotilde and the Duc and Duchesse de Grandlieu; the young couple planned to return to Paris in December 1838. Sabine settled rue de Bourbon with pleasure, eager to be judged by her family. Calyste allowed his sister-in-law Clotilde and mother-in-law to guide him in society; they appreciated his compliance. He gained standing from his name, fortune, and alliance. Sabine, now one of the most charming women in Paris, enjoyed society and the season’s duties; her pregnancy and the birth of their son in October 1839 reassured her that this neutral union had advantages. Mistakenly convinced that a mother should nurse, Sabine nursed the child herself.

By August 1840, Sabine was finishing nursing. Two years in Paris had stripped Calyste’s naïveté. He mixed with Duc Georges de Maufrigneuse, the Vicomte Savinien de Portenduere, and others, learning how Paris amuses the idle rich. Contact with men who abandoned domestic virtues for cigars, whist, and clubs gradually undermined the young Breton’s domestic habits. A wife’s indulgence often supports such dissipation; she is proud to see him return after granting freedom.

One October evening, to escape their newly weaned child’s cries, Calyste, urged by Sabine, went to the Variétés for a new play. During the first interlude he saw Madame de Rochefide in a proscenium box. Beatrix in Paris! The shock after nearly three years struck him like an arrow. She had not been forgotten; sometimes he had even substituted Beatrix for his wife, as Sabine had noticed. Beatrix represented light, life, the Unknown; Sabine embodied duty and the expected. It was a thunderbolt.

Out of loyalty, Calyste first wanted to leave. He noticed the proscenium box door ajar and approached. He found Beatrix between Canalis and Raoul Nathan. In three years she had changed—gaunt, faded, eyes hollow, but heightened by Parisian artifice; more poetic, even more attractive to him.

“Why isn’t Conti here?” Calyste asked Canalis softly.

The former poet, now a minister, signaled silence.

“I’m happy to see you,” Beatrix said demurely. “When I recognized you I thought you would not disown me. Ah! my Calyste, why did you marry—and with such a little fool!”

A woman whispering to an old acquaintance and inviting him to sit often sends men away.

“Come, Nathan,” Canalis said; “Madame la marquise will allow me to speak to d’Arthez.”

Left alone, Beatrix poured her heart out: she had lost love and friendship, was “broken,” yet could still feel fidelity to a past. Calyste, unable to speak, shed tears; she took his hand and pressed it, making him pale.

“Leave me now; people are watching; it might trouble your wife if someone tells her we were seen together—innocently,” she murmured, then pretended strength. She laughed and affected comic resignation at aging.

“But where can I see you?” he asked.

“In rue de Chartres opposite the Parc Monceaux, a little house; I cram my head with literature to distract myself,” she replied. “Now go—if you stay I shall burst into tears!”

Calyste withdrew with the strange sensation of renewed temptation. He thought, “Sabine never knew how to stir my soul so.” The rest of the evening Beatrix sent him sidelong looks that tore his soul.

At home the splendor of his apartments reminded him of the mediocrity Beatrix had mentioned; he hated his wealth because it could not belong to her. Learning Sabine was in bed, he rejoiced at a night to relive emotion. A wife who adores reads her husband like a book; she knows every quiver and questions every sadness. Calyste feared Sabine’s scrutiny.

The next morning, Calyste found comfort in the child’s improved condition and left the house, fleeing breakfast as if escaping prison. What is love? Why does Nature rebel against social constraints? Calyste, raised sacredly, was drawn to a woman unworthy of him. Beatrix’s poetic airs masked courtesan instincts, yet she appealed to something dormant in him. Perhaps some buried vanity sought superiority over a fallen woman; perhaps he needed a nature unlike his own. He found nothing to challenge him in Sabine; his dormant powers vibrated for Beatrix.

He lived on the thought, “I shall see her again!” and went to rue de Chartres. Though never there, he recognized the house. He waited on the stairs, feeling he should leave her freedom to receive him. The old footman’s question, “Monsieur’s name?” stopped him. He admired the little garden’s walls streaked by rain.

Beatrix had left her fortune to her husband when she fled and could not beg from him. Conti and Mademoiselle des Touches had helped her; her mother sent sums. She now lived in a “little house” of a formerly great lord—eighteen hundred francs rent—served by an old footman, maid, and cook from Alençon: penury compared to her past, but luxury to many.

Calyste ascended a well-pumiced staircase; silk and velvet furnished the rooms. A dim salon with garnet velvet walls and conservatory-like windows held celadon, a silver cup said to be by Benvenuto Cellini, gilded furniture, and ten watercolors of places where Beatrix had lived. On a table lay a manuscript half-written, a dagger handle used as a paper-cutter—symbolic!

Her rustling dress announced her. Her gown, arranged to display her bosom, her cap, her elongated face: she smiled, “Already! A lover could not have shown more eagerness. You must have secrets to tell me.”

She posed, inviting him to sit. By chance—or choice—Beatrix wore the scent from Les Touches. The perfume, the dress, her eyes in the dimness turned his head. He was again seized by an impulse that once nearly cost her life; but she rose to ring the bell and placed a finger on his lips to restore order.

“Antoine, I am not at home—for anyone,” she said. “I treat you as a friend—do not treat me as a mistress. Two things: I would not deny myself foolishly to a man I truly loved; and I will belong to no other man on earth. You are under sacred duties: a young, amiable wife, a father. We would be two fools.”

“My dear Beatrix, all these reasons vanish before a single word—I have never loved anyone but you, and I was married against my will.”

“Ah! a trick by Mademoiselle des Touches,” she said with a smile.

Three hours passed. Beatrix counselled conjugal faith and disparaged Sabine’s merits: “Your wife will never go wrong; she is a Grandlieu, but a brown, dry woman—Eve was fair; brown women descend from Adam, blondes from God,”—one of her petty jibes.

About six, Calyste took his hat to leave.

“Yes, go, my poor friend,” she said; “don’t give her the annoyance of dining without you.”

He stayed. At his age, it was easy to ensnare him.

“What! You dare to dine with me?” she feigned shock. “My poor food—have you enough independence to crown me by dining here?”

“Let me write a note to Sabine; otherwise she will wait for dinner until nine.”

“Here,” she said, “this is the table I write at.”

She looked over what he wrote.

“My dear Sabine—”

“‘My dear’? Can you still call your wife ‘dear’?” she asked coldly. “Go—better dine with her.”

“_I dine at a restaurant with some friends._”

“A lie. Oh, fy! You are not worthy to be loved by her or me. Go, monsieur, go.”

Calyste recoiled, then sat up magnificent and resolute. He glowed with Breton obstinacy; a Northern or Southern woman might have fallen at his feet, but Beatrix, hard and vindictive, belonged to a race of rancor. She wanted notoriety as revenge.

“Dictate what I ought to write,” said Calyste.

“You shall love me as at Guerande. Write: I dine out; do not expect me.”

“Very well,” he answered.

She seized the note, calling Antoine to send it. Calyste returned home about two a.m. Sabine, having waited until half past twelve, had gone to bed, exhausted and distressed by the terse note. She reasoned: true love explains everything in the man’s favor.

Next morning the child was better; Sabine, smiling, brought him to his father before breakfast. Calyste behaved like a model husband; he overdid the tenderness. Yet subtle distinctions eluded Sabine.

At breakfast she suddenly asked, “What did you do yesterday?”

“Portenduere kept me to dinner,” he lied, “and after that we went to the club.”

“That’s foolish, my Calyste,” she said, launching into a lecture on nobility’s duty and the loss of national ground—ideas that had expanded while nursing. “Admit the first note you wrote me was stiff.”

“I didn’t think of writing until I got to the club.”

“But you wrote on a woman’s note-paper; it had feminine perfume.”

“The club directors are dandies.”

Portenduere and his wife were intimate with the du Guenics; the vicomtesse and Sabine had become friends through their infants. While Calyste, novice in falsehood, thought, “I must warn Savinien,” Sabine resolved to find the paper she had thrown into her letter-box the night before.

After breakfast, Calyste left, went to the vicomte’s to arrange Savinien’s fib, then urged his coachman to rue de Chartres to learn of Beatrix. He found her after her bath, fresh and merry, breakfasting heartily. He admired the grace with which she ate boiled eggs from gold service—gifts from a fool who had appropriated Conti’s ballads.

He listened entranced until she wept as he left. He thought he had been there half an hour; it was past three when he returned. His English horse was drenched in sweat. By chance Sabine watched from a window; the horse’s condition alarmed her.

“Where can he have come from?” she whispered, that premonition neither angel nor devil, but the mind’s intuition.

On the landing she met him. “Abd-el-Kader is nearly foundered. You told me you would be gone but a moment; I have waited three hours.”

“Dear little mother,” he soothed, taking her waist with more cajolery than before, “they are finishing a dressing-table for you, true artists have worked, and my mother and aunt have contributed.”

Sabine clung to him, faint with joy—not for the furniture but for dispelling doubt. It was a sublime moment. Then, as if bitten by a viper, she snatched away, threw herself on a sofa, and fainted. The perfume on the paper had betrayed another woman’s presence; she had kissed where the rival’s kisses had been. She cried that her milk had turned and insisted he fetch the doctor and nurse.

Calyste rushed out. Sabine, beside herself, rang for her maid and sent her to Madame de Portenduere. She felt her ideas whirl; she wrote a letter to you, dear mother, to thank you for the dressing-table—in a frenzy—and gave it to the footman.

When the vicomtesse arrived, the chill of reaction had set in.

“Where did Savinien and Calyste go after they dined with you yesterday?” Sabine asked.

“Savinien and I dined alone and went to the Opera without Calyste,” Ursula replied.

“Ursula, in the name of your love for Savinien, keep silence about what I shall tell you. You alone shall know why I die—I am betrayed at the end of three years, at twenty-two!”

She shuddered, accusing fate. She held out the fatal letter. “See,” she said, “the coronet of a marquise! Find if Madame de Rochefide has returned to Paris. Am I to weep? My faith, my poetry, my idol, my virtue, my happiness—lost! No God! no love! no life!” At that thought tears flowed—tears she had not shed earlier.

Ursula, first stunned, then struck by the truth of her friend's agony, resolved to save her. She went to the duchess, swore secrecy, and explained Sabine’s situation.

“Madame,” she said, “do you not think we should inform the doctor and invent a tale to clear Calyste for the moment, to avoid sickness or madness?”

“My dear child,” the duchess replied, chilled but prudent, “friendship gives you experience for now. I know how Sabine loves him; you are right—she might become insane.”

“Or lose her beauty,” Ursula added.

“Let us go to her!” cried the duchess.

Fortunate for Sabine, they arrived before the great accoucheur Dommanget and before Calyste returned. The duchess had the servants ready; she caught Calyste and Dommanget in the salon.

“Sabine’s life is at stake,” she told Calyste; “you have betrayed her to Madame de Rochefide.”

Calyste blushed like a girl caught. The duchess explained that Sabine had guessed the truth. “You must save my daughter and forget Madame de Rochefide. Learn to lie; have the courage of a criminal. I have told a lie myself and will do penance for this mortal sin.”

She then invented a lie: Calyste had lost a great deal of money at cards. Dommanget, sitting at Sabine’s bedside, used that lie to treat her symptoms. Calyste tried to show tenderness.

“So it was play that put those black circles under your eyes?” Sabine asked weakly.

The doctor, mother, and vicomtesse trembled and exchanged glances. Calyste turned red as a cherry. “That’s what comes of nursing a child,” Dommanget said, brusquely but cleverly. “Husbands are lonely and go to the club. But you needn’t worry about the thirty thousand francs monsieur lost last night.”

“Thirty thousand francs!” Ursula gasped.

“Yes,” Dommanget said. “They told me Monsieur de Trailles won that money.”

Seeing the three respected people lie like shopkeepers to save Sabine, Calyste understood the danger; tears rolled down his face, deceiving her. She sat up, fierce. “Monsieur du Guenic can lose thirty, fifty, a hundred thousand francs if he pleases; no one has the right to lecture him.” Calyste embraced her and whispered, “Sabine, you are an angel!”

Two days later she was thought out of danger. The next day Calyste was at Madame de Rochefide’s, making a merit of his infamy.

“Beatrix,” he said, “you owe me happiness. I sacrificed my poor little wife to you; she discovered everything—your coronet on the note, the perfume, the lies. Sabine nearly died; her milk went to her head; erysipelas set in; she may bear the marks for life.”

Beatrix listened, cold as the north wind. “So much the better,” she said; “perhaps it will whiten her for you.” Her voice was harsh; she answered with atrocious sarcasm.

Calyste’s mistake was to speak of his virtuous wife to his mistress; he neither knew how to lie to his wife nor how to tell his mistress the truth—two arts the world teaches. He begged Beatrix for pardon for two hours; she refused, raised her eyes to the ceiling to avoid seeing him, and in a trembling voice recited reasons sacred to marquises, wiping furtive tears.

“To speak to me of your wife on the day after my fall!” she cried. “Why not tell me she is a pearl of virtue? I know she thinks you handsome; pure depravity! I love your soul! You are ugly compared to many shepherds on the Campagna.” Such speeches fit into Beatrix’s system: at each passion she became another being, advancing into profligacy. Clever women know themselves; they count wrinkles, preserve themselves, and use every artifice to triumph. Fallen by Conti’s desertion, Beatrix resolved at least to win fame that unprincipled conduct can bring. She would make the misfortune of the rich and beautiful Sabine her pedestal.

To conquer Calyste she adopted contempt as a moral lever, constantly comparing her poetic, comfortable home to the hotel du Guenic and attacking the Saint-Germain luxury as stupid. She sought to make him feel he had conquered a repugnance—flattery in the guise of dislike—which appeals to conquering natures. In the perfect bower of her rare plants and ravishing flowers she made him swear hatred of his wife, persuaded him to renounce Sabine, and displayed the trivial sciences of luxury to captivate him. Thus, having been mortified, she aimed at a new triumph: to be the celebrated victor over a noble and charming young wife.

When a woman returns to ordinary life after nursing her first child, she often seems rejuvenated and charming, bringing freshness to other young mothers. But when Sabine comes back to her husband Calyste, she discovers he is not the man she married. Instead of security she feels anxiety and craves certainty about their relationship. That certainty arrives in a letter from her mother hinting at Calyste's discontent and the presence of another woman, Béatrix.

Sabine's heart sinks reading the vague note, which reveals nothing of Calyste's feelings. In despair she writes a comment on the letter to signal her awareness and leaves it where Calyste will find it. He sees the note but ignores it, throwing the paper into the fire. Sabine is left agonizing, convinced she has displeased and humiliated her idol.

As days pass, her torment deepens. Béatrix, emboldened, writes to Calyste; Sabine, recognizing the perfume, realizes how extensive her rival's influence is. In a moment of madness she dances with her child, seeking solace in motherhood's innocence, yet her heart remains heavy and she weeps alone when Calyste departs to read Béatrix's message.

Her suffering grows with each attempt to win Calyste's affection. She decorates their home with flowers to please him, but his indifference increases her despair. Obsessed with understanding his feelings and comparing herself to Béatrix, she is trapped by jealousy and self-doubt. Efforts to charm him with cooking and appearance prove futile as Calyste stays unresponsive.

Despite her struggles, Sabine's love persists. She tries to embody devoted-wifely virtues, but these efforts bring disappointment. One evening, after another failure to move him, she succumbs to despair and tears off her beautiful dress in frustration.

Over time Sabine's health declines under the weight of grief. The duchess, her mother, notices the deterioration and attempts to draw her away from the source of suffering. Sabine remains silent, fearing interference in her marriage. Eventually she confides in her sister, asking that their child be cared for should she die, and warns Athenais about love's dangers and the need for caution in marriage. She reflects that her virtues have become obstacles to happiness: her straightforwardness and passion have not won Calyste, and she feels pushed toward the duplicity she believes necessary to retain him.

Concerned, the duchess seeks counsel from Abbé Brossette, a respected priest, revealing a plan to intervene in Calyste's attachment to Béatrix and restore her daughter's happiness. The abbé cautions against mixing spiritual authority with worldly intrigue but recognizes Sabine's plight. The duchess proposes a scheme to persuade Béatrix to return to her husband, thereby freeing Calyste. Contemplating his advice, the duchess becomes determined to act for her daughter's sake, even if it means engaging in morally questionable measures, resolved to restore peace to the family and spare Sabine further suffering.

A storm was brewing for Monsieur de Rochefide, who enjoyed Parisian life as fully as he had with Beatrix and thought such a satisfactory existence nearly impossible to disrupt. To see how society treats men and women differently in similar situations, we follow Rochefide after Beatrix left—a tale that might encourage women to stay home and defend their dignity, as Sabine du Guenic did.

After Beatrix's departure, Arthur de Rochefide, now an only child after his sister’s death, became sole owner of the hôtel de Rochefide and inherited a large fortune. His income reached a thousand francs a day, ample for his happiness. While Beatrix fulfilled maternal duties, Rochefide enjoyed his wealth without extravagance. His vanity, aided by good looks, let him dismiss women and appropriate others' wit. His military background lent a jaunty tone to his conversation, making him seem witty to acquaintances.

As a husband he was pitied; society blamed Beatrix for leaving such a “good” man. He joined clubs and championed fashionable causes to distinguish himself. His main indulgence was racehorses, run by an English groom, costing him a sizable monthly sum. Despite wealth, he lacked taste and intellect, relying largely on inherited fortune. After the downsides of marriage he welcomed bachelorhood, claiming he was "born with a caul," destined for luck.

Rochefide's indifference to domestic life was clear: he rarely dined or slept at home. After several affairs he married Madame Schontz, a woman of dubious reputation known in circles around Fanny Beaupré and others—an environment where many women ended in poverty or early death and where subtle social skill was essential. Madame Schontz, originally La Petite-Aurélie, emerged from a difficult upbringing, orphaned and educated at Saint-Denis, a school that schooled girls but offered few prospects.

Aurelie, daughter of Colonel Schiltz, led a tumultuous life of various relationships, which brought her by chance to Rochefide at Valentino’s. Captivated by her charm, Rochefide married her two years after Beatrix left, and society did not condemn him for the choice.

Their relationship unfolded in four phases. First, courtship: he provided a modest allowance and a furnished apartment. He discovered her noble charm and admired talents, though ambition soon surfaced and she sought a more comfortable life. Second, she moved to a better apartment; Rochefide lavished gifts and support while she ran the household efficiently, impressing him with budgeting and superior dinners—convincing him she loved him for himself by rebuffing wealthier suitors.

Third, she became mother figure to Rochefide's son, overseeing his education and finances and advising Rochefide on investments that yielded significant gains she did not claim. Taking on responsibilities, she managed affairs and ensured his comfort. Fourth, she established herself socially, curating guests and preserving reputation, accumulating a small fortune through savings, investments, and selling jewelry while downplaying her wealth.

Through these phases Madame Schontz demonstrated tactical superiority, transforming the relationship into mutual benefit while skillfully navigating their social world.

Such conduct implied a plan, and Madame Schontz had one. Jealous of Madame du Bruel for two years, she was determined to marry in both church and civil ceremonies and thus needed a second Arthur. Rochefide’s secret rival remained unknown; Bixiou suspected Leon de Lora, the painter thought Bixiou himself was favored, and Victor de Vernisset and Stidmann were also considered. None truly understood Madame Schontz, whose sole aim was securing a good marriage.

The only man of questionable reputation in her salon was Couture, a stock-gambler who had fallen from grace but remained loyal. When he introduced her to a provincial, Fabien du Ronceret, Madame Schontz began to take him seriously. Fabien, son of a chief justice, had left a judgeship in Alençon for Paris with a modest income and the ambition to exploit changing social mores.

The modern era encouraged pride, vanity, and the desire to be talked about; Fabien, not particularly clever, seized on this and aimed to make a name by ostentation. His Paris debut was theatrical: a lavish apartment and a greenhouse meant to dazzle. He sought association with influential figures and entry into Madame Schontz’s circle. After failed attempts through established acquaintances, he turned to Couture, who recognized Fabien’s potential and recommended him.

Madame Schontz quickly assessed Fabien as suitable for her ambitions. She flattered him until he supposed he was the object of her affection. Eager to impress, he presented himself in his finest attire, captivated by her charm. A month before a significant meeting, Madame Schontz disclosed her identity and aims to Fabien, who misunderstood her intentions. Realizing his mistake, she took him for a carriage ride and spoke plainly of wanting a man of ambition who could appreciate her character. Despite her wealth and encouragement to rise, Fabien hesitated at marriage, and she then turned to Couture, who promptly proposed upon learning of her fortune.

Meanwhile the Duchesse de Grandlieu sought the Comte Maxime de Trailles’s help with a delicate family matter. Maxime, a seasoned political operator, read the duchess’s motives and agreed to assist only on terms, including her acceptance of his future wife into her social circle. His plan required removing a rival, and he demanded the duchess follow his instructions closely. He acknowledged the emotional entanglements—especially Calyste’s deep infatuation with Béatrix—and the difficulty of manipulating such feelings, revealing the moral ambiguity of his methods.

The duchess agreed to support Maxime’s wife, and he assured her he would handle the intricacies, hinting at the lengths people would go to secure desires at others’ expense. The next day Rochefide informed Madame Schontz of Maxime’s impending visit, prompting her to ready her house and appearance to impress him. Maxime’s reputation as a powerful figure in Parisian society preceded him, and his presence posed both a privilege and a challenge for Madame Schontz as she navigated her ambitions and relationships.

The next day Maxime de Trailles summoned Finot to arrange a breakfast at the Café Anglais with Couture and Lousteau, where they discussed Madame Schontz. Maxime then loitered on Tortoni’s portico under the pretense of transacting with du Tillet while waiting for the Comte de la Palferine. When La Palferine arrived, Maxime questioned him about his debts; the Comte admitted his straits, and Maxime, admiring his spirit, offered to fund a courtship with a loan of twenty thousand francs.

Maxime’s scheme targeted a well-bred marquise; he urged La Palferine to woo her sincerely, and they agreed to meet later at Madame Schontz’s. Meanwhile Maxime visited the duchess, who fretted over her daughter Sabine’s distress at Calyste’s absence. He asked the duchess to keep his plans secret and requested twenty thousand francs for his maneuver, assuring her it would not be costly.

Madame Schontz prepared a dinner, inviting notable guests including Claude Vignon and La Palferine; she dressed carefully to impress. Maxime praised her success with Rochefide, noting she had cost him less than other conquests. Madame Schontz insisted she was an artist rather than a courtesan and expressed a desire for a respectable marriage.

At dinner Maxime gave La Palferine the promised funds and detailed instructions for making a striking impression on the marquise, emphasizing the need for an unforgettable display. As the evening continued, Maxime and Madame Schontz plotted to manipulate Rochefide’s affections; Maxime vowed to secure a position for Couture in return for his cooperation.

The next day Calyste, feeling neglected, confronted Beatrix, who was torn between genuine feeling for him and a growing attraction to La Palferine. Their relationship strained as Beatrix wrestled with guilt and desire, fearful of consequences. La Palferine’s charm increasingly drew her, making her question loyalty to Calyste.

Maxime intensified his scheming, arranging encounters between Beatrix and La Palferine that further complicated matters. Calyste, increasingly desperate, tried to reclaim her affection, but she remained entangled by La Palferine’s allure. The tension peaked with a public humiliation for Beatrix at the opera, where she was left exposed and alone.

Afterward Beatrix’s attachment to Calyste waned as she sank deeper into La Palferine’s orbit. Maxime, observing the drama, perceived shifting loyalties and emotional turmoil and counseled Calyste to reconsider his tactics, suggesting the pain might not be worth persisting.

The narrative sketches conflicting desires and ambitions, portraying love, betrayal, and the complexity of human relations, where ambition and affection yield disillusionment and force characters to question past choices and future paths.
