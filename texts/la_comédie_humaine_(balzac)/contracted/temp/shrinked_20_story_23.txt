BEATRIX

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

NOTE

Balzac, known for his detailed character studies and depictions of daily life, has never been accused of portraying real individuals in his extensive body of work. In “The Great Man of the Provinces in Paris,” some characters were inspired by real figures, but in “Beatrix,” he draws on recognizable traits of certain people, such as Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin) resembling George Sand, and Beatrix, Conti, and Claude Vignon reflecting the Comtesse d’Agoult, Liszt, and critic Gustave Planche.

The opening scene captures the customs of an old Breton family, representing a social state that has largely vanished, marking a transition from the past to modernity, and is one of Balzac’s notable depictions in the “Comedy of Human Life.”

I. A BRETON TOWN AND MANSION

In Brittany, some towns remain untouched by the changes of the nineteenth century due to poor communication with Paris. These towns view new civilization as a spectacle, remaining loyal to their old customs. A traveler seeking to observe human nature would find remnants of the Louis XV era in Provence, Louis XIV in Pitou, and even older traditions in Brittany. Many towns, once centers of feudal power, have declined, their histories overlooked by historians focused on dates rather than cultural narratives. Yet, the memory of their former splendor persists, particularly in Brittany, where the local character ensures that history is not forgotten.

Guerande is one such town, a well-preserved example of feudal life, surrounded by walls and moats, with intact battlements and gates. The mayor's decision to plant poplars along the moat was met with criticism, as the promenade had been a public space for a century. The architecture of the old town remains unchanged, with houses that have not been altered or modernized, retaining their original characteristics. The streets, reminiscent of four centuries ago, are now quiet, reflecting a social stagnation that allows a solitary traveler to wander through a deserted town.

The town's atmosphere evokes thoughts of ancient customs, with even the presence of a gendarme feeling out of place. The public square is alive with traditional Breton costumes, contrasting sharply with the bourgeoisie and nobility. The social divisions remain stark, with the revolutionary spirit failing to alter the rigid class distinctions. Guerande, fervently Catholic and self-contained, remains a town apart, resistant to modern ideas.

Geographically, Guerande overlooks a salt-marsh known for its Guerande salt, which locals believe enhances the quality of their butter and sardines. It is connected to the rest of France by two roads, one from Savenay and another from Vannes, with the latter being the more frequented route. The town's isolation is compounded by the slow pace of government and the inhabitants' acceptance of their separation from the rest of France. Guerande, therefore, exists as a remnant of feudalism, a place where modernity has little influence.

The town is a picturesque blend of nature and history, with its salt-marshes and surrounding landscapes evoking a sense of tranquility. The arrival of a carriage is a significant event, drawing the attention of locals who come to barter goods. Guerande is known for its historical significance, including the signing of a treaty in 1365, and its reputation as a key coastal town.

Near the church stands a mansion belonging to the du Guaisnic family, a symbol of the town's past grandeur. This family, with roots tracing back to the du Guesclins, is ancient and noble, embodying the history of Brittany. Despite their diminished fortunes, they remain significant in Guerande, where the Baron du Guaisnic is regarded as a prominent figure.

The mansion's entrance features a grand archway, indicative of its historical significance. The gate, adorned with a family crest, reflects the family's pride and antiquity. The courtyard reveals stables and a kitchen, while the house itself is constructed of freestone, showcasing remnants of its former glory. The interior is well-preserved, with a grand hall featuring intricate woodwork and a massive fireplace.

The dining room, adorned with tapestries from the fourteenth century, retains its historical charm, while the kitchen connects to the dining area through a corner tower. The upper stories contain simple accommodations for the family and guests, with servants sleeping above the kitchens.

The garden, a half-acre of well-maintained land, features fruit trees and a sun-dial, cared for by a dedicated gardener. The mansion's architecture, influenced by Venetian design, showcases a blend of styles that reflect its historical significance. The presence of a spiral column suggests a connection to the family's heritage.

The grandeur of the mansion stands in stark contrast to modern times, where homes are often temporary and lack the permanence of the past. The du Guaisnics have hosted friends in their home for generations, embodying the spirit of old Brittany. The description of the town and mansion provides context for understanding the family's character, illustrating how the environment shapes individuals. The legacy of the du Guaisnics is a testament to the enduring nature of history and tradition in a rapidly changing world.

Early in May 1836, the Guenic family comprised Baron and Baroness du Guenic, the baron's elder sister Mademoiselle du Guenic, and their only son, Calyste, aged twenty-one. The baron, Gaudebert-Calyste-Charles, had fought in the Vendee and Brittany uprisings, aligning with notable leaders like Charette and La Rochejaquelein. Before the war, he prudently sold his property to his sister, ensuring their security. After years of resistance against Napoleon, he returned to Guerande in 1813, having fallen in love with Fanny O’Brien, an Irish noblewoman. They married, and she bore him Calyste on the day Louis XVIII landed in France.

Now seventy-three, the baron bore the marks of his tumultuous life, appearing much older. His tall, wiry frame was lined with deep wrinkles, and his once-vibrant features were now sunken and marred by age. Despite his frailty, his presence exuded a steadfast spirit, embodying the resilience of his Breton heritage. His hands, large and strong, bore the scars of a soldier's life, and his expression, though often grim, reflected a proud loyalty to his beliefs.

The baron was illiterate, having read little beyond his prayer book, and his attire remained unchanged: sturdy shoes, ribbed stockings, and a coat adorned with the cross of Saint-Louis. His demeanor was serene, marked by a somnolence that his family attributed to his long life of duty. Conversations in the household revolved around the exiled Bourbons, the Catholic faith, and the future of Brittany, with Calyste being the family's sole hope.

The baron had taken Calyste under his wing, teaching him the ways of a gentleman and preparing him for potential conflict. When the Duchesse de Berry sought to reclaim the throne for her son, the baron took Calyste to join her cause, leaving his wife and sister unaware of their departure. They returned after three months, surprising their family, and the baron remarked, “The barons did not all do their duty,” reflecting on their commitment to their cause.

That evening, the baron fell asleep in his armchair while his wife read the newspaper. Fanny O’Brien, the baroness, was a striking woman, embodying the beauty and grace of her Irish heritage. At forty-two, she retained a delicate beauty, her skin glowing and her features refined. She dressed elegantly, her hair styled in ringlets, and her demeanor radiated warmth and tenderness.

In contrast, Mademoiselle Zephirine, the baron’s sister, sat nearby, blind and knitting. Despite her age, she maintained a proud posture and a cheerful disposition, refusing to undergo surgery for her cataracts to avoid burdening her brother financially. The bond between the baroness and Zephirine was strong, with the latter embracing her role in the household and caring for her brother.

As the baroness reflected on her husband’s slumber, the atmosphere in the room shifted with the setting sun, casting a warm glow over the aged furniture. The silence deepened, and Zephirine, sensing her sister-in-law’s preoccupation, commented on her distraction. Mariotte, a servant, entered to light the lamp, while Gasselin, another loyal retainer, tended to the horses and dogs, embodying the unity and devotion of the household.

Gasselin and Mariotte, both long-time servants, shared a deep connection with the family, their lives intertwined with the Guenics. Gasselin, a diligent gardener, took pride in his work, while Mariotte mirrored his steadfastness. Their loyalty to the family was unwavering, and they thrived in the harmonious environment created by Mademoiselle Zephirine’s careful management.

The household operated with a sense of purpose and unity, marked by the affection shared among its members. The monotony of daily life was enriched by the bonds of love and duty, creating a nurturing atmosphere that sustained them through the trials of their times.

When night fell, Gasselin entered the hall and respectfully asked his master if he needed anything further. The baron, waking up, replied he could go out or to bed after prayers, unless Madame or his sister required him. The two ladies consented, and Gasselin knelt as his masters did. Mademoiselle du Guenic led the prayer, and afterward, a knock sounded at the door. Gasselin opened it, suspecting it was Monsieur le cure, who soon entered, bowing to the occupants and greeting them with the customary civility of priests.

Monsieur Grimont, a middle-aged man in a cassock, had a plump visage and a calm demeanor, embodying both the Dutch burgomaster and the Breton peasant. His joviality suggested a clear conscience, and he was well-regarded in Guerande, though he acknowledged the du Guenics' feudal superiority. He inquired about Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel's tardiness, noting the chevalier's presence at Les Touches that evening. Mariotte urged caution about discussing the chevalier's visits in front of Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, who was eager to know the town's gossip.

As Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel arrived, accompanied by a page with a lantern, Mariotte moved her stool to converse with the boy. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel was a thin, elderly woman, dressed in a style reminiscent of her youth, with a collection of keys and a silver snuff-box always at hand. She belonged to the highest nobility of Brittany, and her family’s legacy was dwindling, as her sister had married a Kergarouet, who added her name to his own. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, managing her property with frugality, was admired for her avarice, which was never criticized. She had a modest yearly expense and was often visited by her nieces, who hoped to win her favor.

Jacqueline de Pen-Hoel, a friend of Zephirine du Guenic, had plans to marry one of her nieces to Calyste, the young chevalier, to secure a connection with the du Guenics. Despite her wealth, the du Guenics maintained a respectful distance, valuing their pride and status. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel treated Calyste with a sense of entitlement, believing her intentions granted her oversight. She held traditional views on morality and was wary of modern influences, preferring the stability of old customs.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel inquired if Monsieur du Halga would join them that evening. The rector confirmed he had seen him out walking. The mention of du Halga brought excitement, as the group prepared for their evening game. The rector retrieved a basket and counters for the game, displaying a childlike eagerness.

A firm knock at the gate announced the arrival of Chevalier du Halga, a frail man dressed for his health, who had once been a competent naval officer. Now, he was gentle and concerned about his little dog, embodying the remnants of his former gallantry without mentioning his past exploits. His health issues were compounded by the hardships he faced during the emigration, but he remained a respected figure in Guerande, having served as mayor.

The rector, chevalier, and Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel had spent their evenings together for years at the hotel de Guenic, where the du Guenics held a prominent position in the local nobility. The rector's cough during the prayer for the king indicated the political tensions still present in Guerande.

_Mouche_ is a card game where each player receives five cards, and one card is turned over as trumps. Players can choose to play or abstain; if they abstain, they lose only their stake. Winning a trick earns them a share of the pot, while losing makes them _mouche_, requiring them to pay the full stake, which increases the pot for the next game. Players can exchange cards from the remaining deck, but only in order. The trump card belongs to the dealer, who can exchange it for any card in their hand. The most powerful card is Mistigris, the knave of clubs.

Despite its simplicity, _mouche_ is engaging, revealing human greed, cunning, and expressions. At the hotel du Guenic, players each start with twenty counters, representing five sous, making the total stake five farthings, a significant amount for this group. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel and Mademoiselle Zephirine take the game seriously, viewing it as a financial venture akin to stock trading.

A convention from September 1825 dictates that the game ends if a player loses ten sous and wishes to leave. However, the chevalier and the baron have found ways to circumvent this rule, offering counters to players who have lost, allowing the game to continue. The presence of Demoiselle de Kergarouet and her aunt elevates the stakes, as the young women, enamored with Calyste, are less concerned about the small amounts involved.

Over the years, these nightly games have been punctuated by discussions about local events and public affairs. Players sometimes forget their stakes, leading to accusations of cheating, particularly against the rector. The evening can end unexpectedly, with players leaving without any gains, leading to complaints about the game itself. Despite attempts to introduce other games like whist and boston, the players prefer _mouche_, which triumphs over modern games.

As the rector deals, the baroness inquires about the chevalier's health, who always claims to have new ailments. He humorously notes that his troubles shift locations in his body. The conversation turns to the recent death of the Duc de Lenoncourt, prompting reflections on mortality among the players.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel shares news of the Comtesse de Kergarouet's marriage, which leads to gossip about Mademoiselle des Touches, a woman of questionable reputation. The baroness and rector exchange concerned glances, especially as Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel has Mistigris, making it difficult to continue the conversation. The baroness's victory in the game horrifies Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, who becomes preoccupied with thoughts of Calyste and Mademoiselle des Touches.

After the game, the chevalier walks Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel home, discussing the evening's play. She confides her worries about Calyste's affection for Mademoiselle des Touches, an actress. The baroness expresses her anxiety about Calyste's future, fearing he may be led astray by this woman.

The rector reassures her, noting that Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel has good intentions for Calyste, planning to leave her fortune to whichever niece marries him. However, he warns that Mademoiselle des Touches is a dangerous influence, known for her scandalous lifestyle and connections to dubious characters. The baroness is distressed, fearing for her son's future and the impact of Mademoiselle des Touches on his life.

As the evening progresses, the baroness and rector discuss Calyste's situation, with the rector expressing disbelief that a young man raised in a devout household could fall into such a lifestyle. The baroness laments the loss of her close relationship with Calyste, feeling replaced by Mademoiselle des Touches. The rector promises to investigate further, hoping to uncover the truth about Calyste's involvement with her.

The baroness accompanies the rector to the gate, anxiously awaiting Calyste's return, but only hears the sound of the rector's footsteps fading into the night.

The distressed baroness returned home to find that the entire town was aware of her son Calyste's frequent visits to Mademoiselle des Touches, which she had thought were private. She hoped to entice him home earlier by engaging in her needlework, but her maternal jealousy was in vain as Calyste continued to spend more time with Mademoiselle des Touches, returning home later each night. The night before, he had come home at midnight.

As she stitched, the baroness pondered the allure that Mademoiselle des Touches held over men, wondering what secrets she possessed that could make them forget their families and responsibilities. She longed to meet this woman and assess her character, fearing the modern influences that might corrupt her innocent son. Calyste, a product of noble Breton and Irish lineage, had been raised with care, and the baroness had entrusted his education to the rector of Guerande, believing he was shielded from impure thoughts. However, his education had left him ignorant of modern literature and sciences, and he remained a fervent Catholic.

The baroness envisioned a modest but happy life for Calyste, hoping to secure a suitable marriage with a wealthy heiress. She had anticipated an inheritance that would help her achieve this goal, but her dreams were threatened by Mademoiselle des Touches. As midnight struck, she grew anxious, fearing Calyste might stay out longer than usual. When he finally returned, she rushed to greet him, relieved yet still troubled.

Calyste's appearance brought her joy, but she noticed his fatigue and the signs of emotional turmoil. Despite her worries, she attributed his weariness to excitement rather than deeper issues. The baroness's thoughts turned bitter as she realized that Calyste's affections were directed elsewhere, and she feared the consequences of his passion.

The next day, Calyste slept late, indulged by his mother, who allowed him to skip the usual meal times. The family gathered for dinner, and the baroness noted Calyste's attire, suspecting he would dine again at Les Touches. Her sister expressed concern over Calyste's change in behavior, attributing it to Mademoiselle des Touches's influence. The baroness shared her fears about the woman's morals and the impact on Calyste, who had begun to read various books under her guidance.

Calyste defended Mademoiselle des Touches, explaining that she had rejected his love and offered him friendship instead. He insisted that she was a talented artist deserving of respect, despite her unconventional lifestyle. The baroness, however, remained unconvinced, fearing for her son's future and the potential loss of his values.

As Calyste left for Les Touches, the baroness's anxiety deepened. She worried that Mademoiselle des Touches would lead him astray, and her heart ached at the thought of losing her son to this woman. The baron, dismissing his wife's concerns, suggested that Calyste was merely enjoying youthful illusions.

Meanwhile, the town of Guerande buzzed with rumors about Mademoiselle des Touches, believing she was infatuated with Calyste and wielding a mysterious influence over him. The rector and other townsfolk expressed their disapproval of her lifestyle, viewing her as a threat to societal norms.

Mademoiselle Felicite des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, was a celebrated writer whose life and works had sparked curiosity and scandal. Orphaned during the Revolution, she had been raised by her aunt and had developed a strong intellect through extensive reading. Despite her beauty and talent, she struggled with societal expectations and the limitations placed on women.

Felicite's early life was marked by tragedy, but she emerged as a formidable figure in literature, known for her plays and novels that challenged conventional norms. Her experiences shaped her views on love and independence, leading her to reject traditional marriage and embrace her artistic pursuits.

As Felicite navigated her complex relationships, she found herself drawn to Calyste, who represented a youthful innocence she had long abandoned. Her salon became a hub for intellectuals and artists, where she could explore her passions without the constraints of societal expectations.

Despite her success, Felicite grappled with the realities of aging and the desire to maintain her beauty and independence. Her life was a blend of artistic ambition and personal longing, as she sought to balance her public persona with her private desires.

In the midst of her emotional turmoil, Felicite's connection with Calyste deepened, leading her to confront her feelings and the implications of their relationship. As she navigated the complexities of love and ambition, the stakes grew higher, threatening to upend the carefully constructed life she had built.

A few hundred yards from Guerande, Brittany's soil gives way to salt-marshes and sandy dunes. We descend into a sandy desert, marked by ponds and a narrow arm of the sea separating the mainland from Croisic, which is more accurately a peninsula connected to Brittany by difficult quicksand beaches.

At the junction of the road from Croisic to Guerande stands a country house surrounded by a garden with uniquely shaped pine trees, victims of harsh weather. The house, built of slaty stone and granite, has a plain exterior with regular windows. Its high pointed roof features gables and dormer windows, with one facade facing the road to Guerande and the other overlooking the desert leading to Croisic and the sea. A brook flows from the park, crossing the road and disappearing into the sands.

The house's gray tones blend well with the surrounding landscape. The estate, known as Les Touches, generates an income from the marshes and nearby farms, amounting to over ten thousand francs annually. Although the Revolution stripped it of its feudal rights, the local salt workers still refer to it as “the chateau.” Felicite, the owner, restored Les Touches while maintaining its desolate exterior, only adding brick columns at the gate to enhance its entrance.

The ground floor layout is typical of country houses from a century ago, likely built on the ruins of an old castle. Felicite transformed the entrance hall into a billiard room, leading to a large salon and dining room. The kitchen connects to the dining room through an office. The decor is simple yet elegant, with gray-painted walls and mahogany furniture covered in green silk. The dining room features mahogany buffets and horsehair chairs, adorned with engravings by Audran.

Upstairs, Felicite occupies a suite with a salon, large bedroom, and two cabinets, while the other suite serves as guest accommodations. The guest rooms are furnished modestly, while Felicite's apartment showcases luxurious decor, including Gobelin tapestries, heavy silk drapes, and exquisite furniture from the Louis XV period. Her study contrasts with the opulence of her bedroom, filled with modern mahogany furniture and personal items that reflect her multifaceted character.

The landscape outside is striking, with salt marshes, sandy dunes, and the distant ocean, creating a melancholic yet uplifting atmosphere. This stark beauty captivates Camille, who finds herself drawn to the desolate surroundings rather than the lush landscapes of Guerande. Calyste, too, feels a magnetic pull toward Les Touches, viewing it as a refuge from the confines of Guerande. His feelings for Felicite, rooted in admiration and a desire to love, persist despite her rejections.

At Les Touches, Calyste is introduced to the richness of art and culture, experiencing the music and literature of the nineteenth century that awaken his dormant sensibilities. Felicite, nurturing his intellect, becomes a maternal figure to him, inspiring admiration and affection.

Felicite is currently giving Calyste music lessons, and the atmosphere of the house feels alive with creativity. The contrast between the vibrant world of art and the dullness of rural life leaves a profound impact on him. As he approaches the house, he is filled with anticipation.

Upon entering, Calyste hears Felicite playing the piano, but when he reaches the billiard room, the music stops. He suspects she is playing in her private salon and quietly sits down to listen. Felicite's improvisation is hauntingly beautiful, expressing deep emotions that resonate with Calyste. When he finally enters, he finds her in tears, revealing her anxiety over Claude Vignon's absence.

Calyste offers to search for Claude, but Felicite gently insists he stay, explaining that her tears are a natural expression of her emotions. She reflects on the complexities of her feelings and the nature of her relationship with Claude, revealing her insecurities and desires. Despite her sadness, she finds solace in Calyste's presence, appreciating his youthful innocence.

Felicite acknowledges the age difference between herself and Calyste, admitting that her rejection of his love stemmed from self-awareness and the inevitability of their separation. She expresses a longing for love and connection, lamenting her inability to find fulfillment in her relationships.

As they converse, Felicite reveals her desire to be an honest woman and shares her intention to tell Calyste a story. She prepares to recount her history while enjoying tea, creating a moment of intimacy between them. Calyste, eager to support her, sits at her feet, unaware of the depth of her emotions as they share this connection.

“I promised you this tale of the past, and here it is,” said Camille. “The person from whom I received that letter yesterday, and who may be here tomorrow, is the Marquise de Rochefide. The old marquis, after marrying his eldest daughter to a Portuguese grandee, sought a noble alliance for his son to secure a peerage he never obtained. The Comtesse de Montcornet mentioned a young lady in Orne, Mademoiselle Beatrix-Maximilienne-Rose de Casteran, the youngest daughter of the Marquis de Casteran, who wished to marry off his daughters without dowries to preserve his fortune for his son. The Casterans are of the bluest blood. Beatrix, raised at the chateau de Casteran, was twenty when she married in 1828. She was known for her originality, which is simply independence of thought, enthusiasm, and a passion for Art. A woman following such paths risks falling into abysses, unlike men who have the strength to navigate them. Her grandmother was pleased with her marriage to a man of lesser stature. The Rochefides were satisfied with the Casterans, who connected them with the Verneuils and d’Esgrignons, and secured a peerage for their son in a batch made by Charles X., later revoked by the July Revolution.

The early days of marriage can be perilous. Rochefide mistook Beatrix’s ignorance for coldness, classifying her among frigid women, which led him back to his bachelor life. People told him he was lucky to have a wife who would only have head passions, content to shine and create a salon filled with distinguished minds. However, Rochefide was not an ordinary fool; he had the vanity of a clever man, leading to jealousy and tyranny when Beatrix feigned indifference to his infidelity. I analyze this to explain her conduct. Beatrix admired me greatly, but admiration can quickly turn to jealousy. I had a remarkable salon in Paris, and she sought to create her own, attempting to draw away my circle. She attracted superficial people but lacked time to build a real society. I thought her desire for celebrity consumed her, but she possessed a grand soul, regal pride, and a remarkable ability to grasp various subjects, from metaphysics to music.

Beatrix is strikingly beautiful, slender, and fair, with a long, pointed face and a magnificent forehead. Her pale sea-green eyes often have dark rings, and her features are sharp yet elegant. She has an exquisite waist and beautiful shoulders, though her bust is less developed. Despite her flaws, she carries herself with a natural grace that reveals her noble birth. When she dresses elegantly, she can appear divine. 

Felicite showed Calyste a fine copy of a painting by Mieris, depicting a woman in white satin. “Fair women have a precious diversity,” Camille remarked, “while brunettes like us have only one way to charm.” She warned Calyste not to fall in love with Beatrix from her description, as he would be too late.

Despite her beauty, Beatrix lacks the grace of her color; her lines are severe, and her face can appear harsh. She was busy judging men and life during her early years of marriage, and the revolutionary disturbances bored her at her husband’s country estate. Upon returning to Paris, she believed the July Revolution would lead to a moral upheaval, and she took an intellectual interest in the new doctrines emerging at that time. However, she soon realized that the new regime offered no place for individual superiority and chose personal happiness over societal ideals.

Around this time, Beatrix met Gennaro Conti, a Neapolitan composer with a brilliant mind. Although he had talent, he would never reach the first rank. Conti’s voice was captivating, and Beatrix fell madly in love with him, taking him from me. She won my esteem by confiding in me and making me the arbiter of her destiny. I was devoted to Conti, but I knew his true nature. He was charming on the surface but duplicitous beneath. He represented himself as an artist inspired by divine forces, but his true motivations were self-serving.

I warned Beatrix about the gulf she was entering, but she was blinded by love. Conti was ecstatic, and I lent him money for their journey to Italy. Beatrix left a letter for her husband and departed, where she remained for two years, writing enchanting letters to me. She expressed her adoration for me, seeing me as the only woman who understood her.

At this moment, Claude Vignon entered the room, causing a brief silence. He was imposing despite his haggard appearance, with a high forehead and a firm mouth that hinted at sarcasm. Vignon was a complex character, capable of deep thought but often paralyzed by it. He criticized without constructing, and his intellect was sharp yet indifferent to the material world.

“Which way did you come?” asked Mademoiselle des Touches, surprised.

“By the door,” replied Claude dryly.

“Am I in the way?” he asked, sensing the tension.

Calyste, feeling provoked, attempted to speak but was interrupted by Claude’s sardonic remarks about sentiments. Calyste left, feeling anger towards Claude for his indelicacy and pity for Felicite. He returned home, where his mother expressed joy at his arrival, and he pulled out Beatrix’s letter, which could potentially disrupt their family’s happiness.

In her letter, Beatrix described her experiences in Italy, expressing fatigue from constant enjoyment and the challenges of her new life with Conti. She felt a loss of social equality and reflected on the sacrifices she made for love. Beatrix acknowledged the disadvantages of her position, contrasting it with Camille’s freedom. She expressed her longing for solitude and the beauty of Italy, while lamenting their financial struggles that would bring them back to Paris.

Calyste, folding the letter, felt sadness. His mother sensed his melancholy and inquired about it. He explained how Felicite had opened his eyes to the insufficiency of his education and the potential for a noble life. His aunt interrupted, suggesting he marry a good Breton girl, but Calyste rejected the idea, yearning for the freedom and passion he associated with love.

His mother, understanding his desires, embraced him and encouraged him to seek happiness. The family dynamic was tender, with his mother and aunt trying to keep him grounded while he dreamed of a life filled with love and adventure.

After dinner, Calyste returned to Les Touches, where Claude and Felicite were still at the table. Claude, a gourmand, enjoyed the lavish meal prepared by Felicite. Calyste presented Beatrix’s letter, and Claude remarked on the fortune of Parisian women who have men of genius to adore them. Felicite defended Beatrix, asserting her grand nature would not allow her to change.

As the evening progressed, Claude and Felicite engaged in witty banter, captivating Calyste with their intellect. Claude speculated about Beatrix and Conti’s arrival, which stirred emotions in Camille. Calyste, lost in thought, envisioned Beatrix as described by Felicite, feeling a deep connection to her.

When he returned home, his mother questioned him about his late evening. Calyste expressed his admiration for genius and lamented his lack of it. His mother reassured him, emphasizing the importance of love and happiness, encouraging him to pursue noble women if he must love.

One boat carried a young woman in a straw bonnet, accompanied by a maid and a footman. Calyste was captivated by a chest marked with the name "MME. LA MARQUISE DE ROCHEFIDE," feeling an inexplicable connection to her. He believed he would love her, as his heart clung to the first woman who appeared before him, inheriting the affection he had once felt for Camille.

As he watched the unloading of luggage, he overheard the maid and footman discussing their wait for someone to fetch them from Les Touches. Calyste learned that Madame la marquise was already there, having been picked up earlier. He hurried toward Guerande, avoiding the servants of Les Touches, feeling a mix of agitation and longing.

Calyste's thoughts were consumed by the marquise, contrasting his feelings with his previous relationship with Camille. He returned home late in the afternoon, where his mother handed him a letter from Mademoiselle des Touches, inviting him to celebrate the marquise's arrival. The letter mentioned the Casteran family, to which the marquise belonged, and reassured his mother of her noble lineage.

As Calyste walked, he felt a tumult of emotions, a mix of excitement and anxiety. He arrived at Camille's suite, where the company was gathered. Camille greeted him, and he was introduced to Gennaro Conti, a man resembling Lord Byron. The marquise, reclining on a divan, was even more beautiful than he had imagined, adorned with blue-bells in her hair and dressed elegantly. Calyste was struck by her beauty, feeling an overwhelming love that ignited his hopes and fears.

Dinner commenced, and Calyste was tasked with escorting the marquise. His heart raced, and he struggled to find words, overwhelmed by her beauty. The marquise noticed his nervousness and asked if he was unwell. He confessed that he had never seen anyone as beautiful as her, except for his mother. Mademoiselle des Touches, aware of Calyste's feelings, advised him to control his admiration, as the marquise could not reciprocate.

During dinner, the conversation turned to Camille's travels, and Calyste felt increasingly distant from the marquise, who seemed preoccupied with Conti. Despite his admiration, he felt insignificant compared to the artist's talent. The music performed by Conti and Camille stirred deep emotions within Calyste, leaving him in tears. The marquise, noticing his distress, turned away, while Calyste grappled with feelings of inadequacy.

As the evening progressed, Calyste's admiration for the marquise grew, but so did his sense of despair. He felt overwhelmed by the contrast between his feelings and the artistic brilliance of Conti. In a moment of vulnerability, he slipped away to a nearby sofa, lost in thought, grappling with the intensity of his emotions and the realization of his own insignificance in the face of such talent.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

X. DRAMA

“What is it, my child?” Claude Vignon asked Calyste, who had just entered the bedroom. “You love; you think you are disdained, but it is not so. Soon, the field will be free to you, and you will reign—beloved by more than one.”

“Loved!” Calyste exclaimed, pulling Claude into the library. “Who loves me here?”

“Camille,” Claude replied.

“Camille loves me? And you—what of you?”

“I?” Claude paused, then sat down, resting his head on a cushion. “I am tired of life, but I lack the courage to leave it. I wish I were mistaken about what I just told you; however, I have seen many vivid truths lately. I did not wander the marshes for pleasure. The bitterness I felt upon returning to find you with Camille was due to wounded feelings. I intend to confront her soon. Two clear-sighted minds like ours cannot deceive each other. Between two such skilled duellists, the combat cannot last long. Therefore, I must tell you that I will leave Les Touches tomorrow, perhaps with Conti. After we are gone, strange things will happen here. I will regret not witnessing the rare and dramatic conflicts of passion that will unfold. You are young to enter such dangerous waters; you interest me. Were it not for my profound disgust for women, I would stay and help you navigate this game. It is difficult; you may lose it. You are dealing with two extraordinary women, and you feel too much for one to use the other wisely. Beatrix is relentless; Camille has grandeur. You may be wrecked between those reefs, drawn in by the waves of passion. Beware!”

Calyste’s shock at these words allowed Claude to speak without interruption and leave him, like a traveler shown the depth of an abyss. Hearing from Claude that Camille loved him, just as he felt he loved Beatrix for life, was a weight too heavy for his untested soul. Overwhelmed with regret and despair, he sat lost in thought, seeking reasons for Felicite’s rejection of his love and Claude’s arrival from Paris. The voice of Beatrix occasionally reached him from the salon, igniting a savage desire to rush in and carry her off. What would become of him? If Camille loved him, how could he adore Beatrix? He saw no solution to these difficulties.

Silence fell over the house, and he heard the opening and shutting of doors. Suddenly, midnight struck, and the voices of Claude and Camille roused him from his contemplation. Before he could rise, he heard Claude say to Felicite, “You came to Paris last year desperately in love with Calyste, but you were horrified at the thought of the consequences of such a passion at your age; it would lead you to a gulf, to hell, perhaps to suicide. Love cannot exist unless it thinks itself eternal, and you foresaw a horrible parting. You thought of ‘Adolphe,’ the dreadful finale of the loves of Madame de Stael and Benjamin Constant, who were closer in age than you and Calyste. You brought me to Les Touches to mask your real feelings and leave you safe to follow your secret adoration. The scheme was grand and ignoble; but to carry it out, you should have chosen either a common man or one so preoccupied with noble thoughts that you could easily deceive him. You thought me simple and easy to mislead as a man of genius. I am not a man of genius; I am a man of talent, and I have divined you. When I praised women of your age yesterday, do you think I took your fascinated glance to myself? Had I not read into your soul? Your eyes were on me, but your heart throbbed for Calyste. You have never been loved, my poor Maupin, and you never will be after rejecting the beautiful fruit that chance offered you at the portals of that hell of woman.”

“Why has love fled me?” she asked softly.

“Because you are not lovable,” he replied. “You do not bend to love; love must bend to you. You may have yielded to youthful follies, but there was no youth in your heart; your mind is too deep. You have never been naive and artless, and you cannot begin to be so now. Your charm comes from mystery; it is abstract, not active. Your strength repulses strong men who fear a struggle. Your power may please young souls like Calyste, who like to be protected; though, even they will tire of it in the long run. You are grand and sublime; bear the consequence of those qualities—they fatigue.”

“What a sentence!” Camille exclaimed. “Am I not a woman? Do you think me an anomaly?”

“Possibly,” Claude replied.

“We will see!” she said, stung.

“Farewell, my dear Camille; I leave tomorrow. I am not angry with you; I think you the greatest of women, but if I continued to serve you as a screen, you would despise me. We can part now without pain or remorse; we have neither happiness to regret nor hopes betrayed. To you, as with a few rare men of genius, love is not what Nature made it—a need to which she attaches fleeting joys. You see love as Christianity has created it—an ideal kingdom full of noble sentiments, grand weaknesses, and spiritual sensations, where two creatures as one angel fly on the wings of pleasure. This is what I hoped to share; I thought I held in you a key to that door, closed to many, by which we may advance toward the infinite. You were there already. In this, you have misled me. I return to my misery—to my vast prison of Paris. Such a deception, had it come to me earlier in life, would have made me flee from existence; today it plunges me into an awful solitude.”

At that moment, Calyste appeared.

“I ought not to leave you ignorant that I am here,” he said.

Mademoiselle des Touches showed fear; a sudden flush colored her impassible face. 

“We thought you gone, Calyste,” Claude said. “But this involuntary discretion on both sides will do no harm; perhaps you may be more at ease at Les Touches knowing Felicite as she is. Her silence shows me I am not mistaken about the part she meant me to play. As I told you before, she loves you, but it is for yourself, not for herself—a sentiment few women can conceive and practice; few know the voluptuous pleasure of sufferings born of longing—that is a magnificent passion reserved for man. But she is in some sense a man,” he added sarcastically. “Your love for Beatrix will make her suffer and make her happy too.”

Tears filled Mademoiselle des Touches’ eyes as she looked at Calyste, who had raised his head abruptly. Claude Vignon enjoyed the sight of their confusion.

“You yourself have driven him to Madame de Rochefide,” Claude continued, “and he is now under her spell. You have dug your own grave. Had you confided in me, you would have escaped the sufferings that await you.”

“Sufferings!” Camille cried, taking Calyste’s head in her hands and kissing his hair, on which her tears fell. “No, Calyste; forget what you have heard; I count for nothing in all this.”

She rose, standing before the two men, subduing them with the intensity of her gaze.

“While Claude was speaking,” she said, “I conceived the beauty and grandeur of love without hope; it is the sentiment that brings us nearest God. Do not love me, Calyste; but I will love you as no woman will!”

It was the cry of a wounded eagle seeking its eyrie. Claude knelt, took Camille’s hand, and kissed it.

“Leave us now, Calyste,” she said. “It is late, and your mother will be uneasy.”

Calyste returned to Guerande with heavy steps, turning back repeatedly to see the light from Beatrix’s window. He was surprised to find how little pity he felt for Camille. But soon, he felt the weight of the scene, the tears she had left on his hair; he suffered with her, imagining he heard her moans, so beloved and desired just days before.

When he opened the door of his home, where silence reigned, he saw his mother sewing by the light of a lamp. Tears filled his eyes as he looked at her.

“What has happened?” Fanny asked, sensing his emotion.

Calyste took his mother in his arms, kissing her cheeks, forehead, and hair with passionate affection. 

“It is you I love, you!” he cried. “You, who live for me; you, whom I long to make happy!”

“But you are not yourself, my child,” the baroness said, looking at him intently. “What has happened?”

“Camille loves me, but I love her no longer,” he replied.

The next day, Calyste instructed Gasselin to watch the road to Saint-Nazaire and inform him if Mademoiselle des Touches’ carriage passed. Gasselin reported that it had.

“How many persons were in it?” Calyste asked.

“Four—two ladies and two gentlemen.”

“Then saddle my horse and my father’s.”

Gasselin left.

“My nephew, what mischief is in you now?” Aunt Zephirine asked.

“Let the boy amuse himself, sister,” the baron said. “Yesterday he was dull as an owl; today he is gay as a lark.”

“Did you tell him that our dear Charlotte was to arrive today?” Zephirine asked her sister-in-law.

“No,” the baroness replied.

“I thought perhaps he was going to meet her,” Mademoiselle du Guenic said slyly.

“If Charlotte is to stay three months with her aunt, he will have plenty of opportunities to see her,” his mother said.

“Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel wants me to marry Charlotte to save me from perdition,” Calyste laughed. “I was on the mall when she and the Chevalier du Halga were discussing it. She can’t see that it would be greater perdition for me to marry at my age—”

“It is written above,” the old maid interrupted, “that I shall not die tranquil or happy. I wanted to see our family continued, and some of the estates brought back; but it is not to be. What can you, my fine nephew, put in the scale against such duties? Is it that actress at Les Touches?”

“What?” the baron asked. “How can Mademoiselle des Touches hinder Calyste’s marriage when it becomes necessary for us to make it? I shall go and see her.”

“I assure you, father,” Calyste said, “that Felicite will never be an obstacle to my marriage.”

Gasselin appeared with the horses.

“Where are you going, chevalier?” his father asked.

“To Saint-Nazaire.”

“Ha, ha! And when is the marriage to be?” the baron joked, believing Calyste was eager to see Charlotte. “It is high time I was a grandfather. Spare the horses,” he continued, going to the portico with Fanny to see Calyste mount. “Remember that they have more than thirty miles to go.”

Calyste started with a tender farewell to his mother.

“Dear treasure!” she said, watching him lower his head to ride through the gateway.

“God keep him!” the baron replied. “For we cannot replace him.”

The words made the baroness shudder.

“My nephew does not love Charlotte enough to ride to Saint-Nazaire after her,” the old blind woman said to Mariotte, who was clearing the breakfast table.

“No; but a fine lady, a marquise, has come to Les Touches, and I’ll warrant he’s after her; that’s the way at his age,” Mariotte said.

“They’ll kill him,” Mademoiselle du Guenic said.

“That won’t kill him,” Mariotte replied, pleased with Calyste’s behavior.

The young man rode at a brisk pace until Gasselin asked if he was trying to catch the boat, which was not his intention. He had no wish to see Conti or Claude again; he expected to be invited to drive back with the ladies, leaving Gasselin to lead his horse. He was cheerful, thinking, “_She_ has just passed here; _her_ eyes saw those trees! What a lovely road!” 

“_Tiens_! Here comes the coach from Nazaire,” Gasselin exclaimed.

“Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel and her niece will be in it. Let us hide,” Calyste said.

“Hide! Are you crazy, monsieur? We are on the moor!”

The coach, coming up the sandy hill above Saint-Nazaire, was full, and to Calyste’s surprise, there were no signs of Charlotte.

“We had to leave Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, her sister, and niece; they are dreadfully worried; but all my seats were engaged by the custom-house,” the conductor told Gasselin.

“I am lost!” Calyste thought. “They will meet me down there.”

When he reached the little esplanade surrounding the church of Saint-Nazaire, overlooking Paimboeuf and the magnificent Mouths of the Loire, he found Camille and the marquise waving handkerchiefs as a last adieu to two passengers on the departing steamer. Beatrix was charming, her features softened by the shadow of a rice-straw hat adorned with scarlet ribbon. She wore a muslin gown with a floral pattern, leaning on a slender parasol. Nothing is finer than a woman poised on a rock like a statue on its pedestal. Conti could see Calyste from the vessel as he approached Camille.

“I thought,” he said, “that you would probably come back alone.”

“You have done right, Calyste,” she replied, pressing his hand.

Beatrix turned, saw her young lover, and gave him her most imperious look. A smile from Camille made her aware of the vulgarity of such conduct, and she said to Calyste, smiling, “Are you not guilty of a slight impertinence in supposing that I should bore Camille if left alone with her?”

“My dear, one man to two widows is none too much,” Mademoiselle des Touches said, taking Calyste’s arm and leaving Beatrix to watch the vessel until it disappeared.

At that moment, Calyste heard the approaching voices of Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, the Vicomtesse de Kergarouet, Charlotte, and Gasselin, all talking at once. The old maid questioned Gasselin about what brought him and his master to Saint-Nazaire; the carriage of Mademoiselle des Touches had already caught her eye. Before Calyste could escape, Charlotte spotted him.

“Why, there’s Calyste!” she exclaimed eagerly.

“Go and offer them seats in my carriage,” Camille told Calyste. “The maid can sit with the coachman. I saw those ladies lose their places in the mail-coach.”

Calyste, unable to resist, carried the message. When Madame de Kergarouet learned that the offer came from the celebrated Camille Maupin, and that the Marquise de Rochefide was of the party, she was surprised at the objections raised by her elder sister, who refused to profit from what she called the devil’s carryall. In Nantes, which boasted more civilization than Guerande, Camille was read and admired; she was thought to be the muse of Brittany and an honor to the region. The viscountess, eager to see her, dragged her old sister forward, ignoring her complaints.

“Good morning, Calyste,” Charlotte said.

“Oh! good morning, Charlotte,” he replied, not offering his arm.

Both were confused; she by his coldness, he by his cruelty, as they walked up the ravine, which is called a street in Saint-Nazaire, following the two sisters in silence. The little girl of sixteen saw her castle in Spain, built and furnished with romantic hopes, reduced to ruins. She and Calyste had played together so much in childhood that she had quietly assumed her future was secure; now, swept along by thoughtless happiness, she was stopped in her flight, unable to imagine the obstacle.

“What is the matter, Calyste?” she asked, taking his hand.

“Nothing,” he replied, releasing himself with cruel haste as he remembered the projects of his aunt and her friend.

Tears filled Charlotte’s eyes. She looked at the handsome Calyste without ill humor, but a first spasm of jealousy seized her, and she felt the dreadful madness of rivalry when she came in sight of the two Parisian women, suspecting the cause of his coldness.

Charlotte de Kergarouet was of ordinary height, with commonplace coloring; she had a round face enlivened by sparkling black eyes, abundant brown hair, a round waist, thin arms, and the curt, decided manner of a provincial girl who did not want to be seen as foolish. She was the favored child of the family due to her aunt’s preference. At this moment, she wore a large plaid Scotch merino mantle lined with green silk, which she had worn on the boat. Her traveling dress, made of common fabric with a chemisette body and pleated collar, appeared horrible compared to the fresh outfits of Beatrix and Camille. She was painfully aware of her soiled stockings from jumping off the boat and her shabby leather shoes, chosen to avoid spoiling better ones on the journey—a fixed principle among provincials.

The Vicomtesse de Kergarouet typified a provincial woman: tall, hard, withered, full of pretensions that only emerged when mortified, talking excessively, and catching a few ideas to gain a reputation for wit. She sought to humiliate Parisians whenever she met them, fishing for compliments she never caught. Her clothes were exaggerated in style yet poorly cared for; she mistook a lack of good manners for dignity and tried to embarrass others by ignoring them. She refused what she desired to have it offered again, concerned about matters others had moved on from, and surprised at not being in fashion. Her manners, language, and ideas had descended to her four daughters. Knowing Camille Maupin and Madame de Rochefide would be a future topic for a hundred conversations, she advanced toward the church as if to take it by assault, waving her handkerchief to display the heavy corners of domestic embroidery trimmed with flimsy lace. Her gait was bold and cavalier, which was of no consequence in a woman of forty-seven.

“Monsieur le chevalier,” she said to Camille and Beatrix, pointing to Calyste, who was mournfully following with Charlotte, “has conveyed to me your friendly proposal, but we fear—my sister, my daughter, and I—to inconvenience you.”

“Sister, I shall not put these ladies to inconvenience,” Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel said sharply. “I can find a horse in Saint-Nazaire to take me home.”

Camille and Beatrix exchanged a glance, which Calyste intercepted, annihilating all memories of his childhood and beliefs in the Kergarouets and Pen-Hoels, ending forever the projects of the three families.

“We can very well put five in the carriage,” Mademoiselle des Touches replied, “even if we were inconvenienced, which cannot be the case with your slender figures. Besides, I should enjoy the pleasure of doing a little service to Calyste’s friends. Your maid, madame, can sit with the coachman, and your luggage can go behind the carriage; I have no footman with me.”

The viscountess was overwhelming in her thanks, complaining that her sister Jacqueline had been in such a hurry to see her niece that she would not give her time to come properly in her own carriage with post-horses, though the post-road was longer and more expensive. She had to return almost immediately to Nantes, where she had left three other little kittens anxiously awaiting her. Here she put her arm around Charlotte’s neck. Charlotte, in reply, raised her eyes to her mother with the air of a little victim, giving onlookers the impression that the viscountess bored her four daughters by dragging them on the scene like Corporal Trim producing his cap in “Tristram Shandy.”

“You are a fortunate mother and—” Camille began, stopping short as she remembered that Beatrix must have parted from her son when she left her husband’s house.

“Oh, yes!” the viscountess said. “If I have the misfortune of spending my life in the country, and especially at Nantes, I have at least the consolation of being adored by my children. Have you children?” she asked Camille.

“I am Mademoiselle des Touches,” Camille replied. “Madame is the Marquise de Rochefide.”

“Then I must pity you for not knowing the greatest happiness that there is for us poor, simple women—is not that so, madame?” the viscountess said, turning to Beatrix. “But you, mademoiselle, have so many compensations.”

Tears filled Madame de Rochefide’s eyes, and she turned away toward the parapet to hide them. Calyste followed her.

“Madame,” Camille said softly to the viscountess, “are you not aware that the marquise is separated from her husband? She has not seen her son for two years and does not know when she will see him.”

“You don’t say so!” Madame de Kergarouet exclaimed. “Poor lady! Is she legally separated?”

“No, by mutual consent,” Camille replied.

“Ah, well! I understand that,” the viscountess said boldly.

Old Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, furious at being dragged into the enemy’s camp, retreated with her dear Charlotte. Calyste, ensuring no one could see him, seized the marquise’s hand, kissed it, and left a tear upon it. Beatrix turned, her tears dried by anger; she was about to utter a terrible word, but it died on her lips as she saw the grief on the angelic face of the youth, deeply touched by her present sorrow.

“Good heavens, Calyste!” Camille said in his ear as he returned with Madame de Rochefide. “Are you to have _that_ for a mother-in-law, and the little one for a wife?”

“Because her aunt is rich,” Calyste replied sarcastically.

The whole party moved toward the inn, and the viscountess felt obliged to make Camille a speech on the savages of Saint-Nazaire.

“I love Brittany, madame,” Camille replied gravely. “I was born at Guerande.”

Calyste admired Mademoiselle des Touches, who, by her tone, tranquility, and quiet manner, put him at ease despite the terrible declarations of the previous night. She seemed a little fatigued; dark circles under her eyes showed she had not slept, but her brow dominated the inward storm with cold placidity.

“What queens!” he said to Charlotte, drawing her attention to the marquise and Camille as he gave the girl his arm, to Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel’s great satisfaction.

“What an idea your mother has had,” the old maid said, taking her niece’s other arm, “to put herself in the company of that reprobate woman!”

“Oh, aunt, a woman who is the glory of Brittany!” Charlotte replied.

“The shame, my dear. Mind that you don’t fawn upon her in that way.”

“Mademoiselle Charlotte is right,” Calyste said. “You are not just.”

“Oh, you!” Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel replied. “She has bewitched you.”

“I regard her,” Calyste said, “with the same friendship that I feel for you.”

“Since when have the du Guenics taken to telling lies?” the old maid asked.

“Since the Pen-Hoels have grown deaf,” Calyste replied.

“Are you not in love with her?” the old maid demanded.

“I have been, but I am so no longer,” he said.

“Bad boy! Then why have you given us such anxiety? I know very well that love is only foolishness; there is nothing solid but marriage,” she remarked, looking at Charlotte.

Charlotte, somewhat reassured, hoped to recover her advantages by recalling childhood memories. She leaned affectionately on Calyste’s arm, who resolved to have a clear explanation with the little heiress.

“Ah! what fun we shall have at _mouche_, Calyste!” she said. “What good laughs we used to have over it!”

The horses were put in; Camille placed Madame de Kergarouet and Charlotte on the back seat. Jacqueline having disappeared, she herself, with the marquise, sat forward. Calyste was obliged to relinquish the pleasure he had counted on of driving back with Camille and Beatrix, but he rode beside the carriage all the way; the horses, tired from the journey, went slowly enough to allow him to keep his eyes on Beatrix.

History must lose the curious conversations that went on between these four persons whom accident had so strangely united in this carriage, for it is impossible to report the hundred and more versions that circulated in Nantes about the remarks, replies, and witticisms that the viscountess heard from the lips of the celebrated Camille Maupin herself. She was, however, careful not to repeat, nor even to comprehend, the actual replies made by Mademoiselle des Touches to her absurd questions about Camille’s authorship—a penance to which all authors are subjected, often making them expiate the few and rare pleasures they win.

“How do you write your books?” she began.

“Much as you do your worsted-work or knitting,” Camille replied.

“But where do you find those deep reflections, those seductive pictures?”

“Where you find the witty things you say, madame; there is nothing so easy as to write books, provided you will—”

“Ah! Does it depend wholly on the will? I shouldn’t have thought it. Which of your compositions do you prefer?”

“I find it difficult to prefer any of my little kittens.”

“I see you are _blasee_ on compliments; there is really nothing new that one can say.”

“I assure you, madame, that I am very sensible to the form you give to yours.”

The viscountess, anxious not to seem to neglect the marquise, remarked, looking at Beatrix with a meaningful air, “I shall never forget this journey made between Wit and Beauty.”

“You flatter me, madame,” the marquise laughed. “I assure you that my wit is but a small matter, not to be mentioned by the side of genius; besides, I think I have not said much as yet.”

Charlotte, who keenly felt her mother’s absurdity, looked at her, trying to stop its course; but Madame de Kergarouet went bravely on in her tilt with the satirical Parisians.

Calyste, trotting slowly beside the carriage, could only see the faces of the two ladies on the front seat, and his eyes expressed painful thoughts. Forced by her position to be looked at, Beatrix constantly avoided meeting his gaze, practicing a maneuver exasperating to lovers; she held her shawl crossed and her hands over it, appearing deeply meditative.

In a shaded, dewy, verdant part of the road, where the wheels of the carriage scarcely sounded, and the breeze brought down balsamic odors, Camille called Madame de Rochefide’s attention to the harmonies of the place, pressing her knee to make her look at Calyste.

“How well he rides!” she said.

“Oh! Calyste does everything well,” Charlotte said.

“He rides like an Englishman,” the marquise said indifferently.

“His mother is Irish—an O’Brien,” Charlotte added, feeling insulted by such indifference.

Camille and the marquise drove through Guerande with the viscountess and her daughter, astonishing the town’s inhabitants. They left the mother and daughter at the end of the lane leading to the Guenic mansion, where a crowd nearly gathered, attracted by such an unusual sight. Calyste rode on to announce the arrival of the company to his mother and aunt, who expected them for dinner, postponed until four o’clock. Then he returned to the gate to give his arm to the two ladies and bid Camille and Beatrix adieu.

He kissed Felicite’s hand, hoping to do the same to the marquise; but she still kept her arms crossed resolutely, and he cast moist glances of entreaty at her in vain.

“You little ninny!” Camille whispered, lightly touching his ear with a kiss full of friendship.

“Quite true,” Calyste thought as the carriage drove away. “I am forgetting her advice—but I shall always forget it, I’m afraid.”

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel (who had returned to Guerande on a hired horse), the Vicomtesse de Kergarouet, and Charlotte found dinner ready, treated with utmost cordiality, if lacking luxury, by the du Guenics. Mademoiselle Zephirine had ordered the best wine from the cellar, and Mariotte had surpassed herself with Breton dishes.

The viscountess, proud of her trip with the illustrious Camille Maupin, endeavored to explain the present condition of modern literature and Camille’s place in it. But the literary topic met the fate of whist; neither the du Guenics, nor the abbe, nor the Chevalier du Halga understood a word of it. The rector and the chevalier arrived just in time for the liqueurs at dessert.

As soon as Mariotte, assisted by Gasselin and Madame de Kergarouet’s maid, had cleared the table, there was a general and enthusiastic cry for _mouche_. Joy reigned in the household. All assumed Calyste was free of his late entanglement and almost as good as married to little Charlotte. The young man alone remained silent. For the first time, he compared his lifelong friends with the two elegant women, witty, accomplished, and tasteful, who must be laughing heartily at the provincial mother and daughter, judging by the look he intercepted between them.

He sought an excuse to leave his family and go to Les Touches, when Madame de Kergarouet mentioned regretting not having accepted Mademoiselle des Touches’ offer of her carriage for the return journey to Saint-Nazaire, which she felt compelled to make the following day for the sake of her three other “dear kittens.”

Fanny, who alone saw her son’s uneasiness and the little hold Charlotte’s coquetries and her mother’s attentions were gaining on him, came to his aid.

“Madame,” she said to the viscountess, “you will, I think, be very uncomfortable in the carrier’s vehicle, especially at having to start so early in the morning. You would certainly have done better to take the offer made to you by Mademoiselle des Touches. But it is not too late to do so now. Calyste, go up to Les Touches and arrange the matter; but don’t be long; return to us soon.”

“It won’t take me ten minutes,” Calyste cried, kissing his mother violently as she followed him to the door.

XI. FEMALE DIPLOMACY

Calyste ran lightly to Les Touches, reaching the portico just as Camille and Beatrix were leaving the grand salon after dinner. He had the sense to offer his arm to Felicite.

“So you have abandoned your viscountess and her daughter for us,” she said, pressing his arm. “We can now understand the full merit of that sacrifice.”

“Are these Kergarouets related to the Portendueres and to old Admiral de Kergarouet, whose widow married Charles de Vandenesse?” Madame de Rochefide asked.

“The viscountess is the admiral’s great-niece,” Camille replied.

“Well, she’s a charming girl,” Beatrix said, gracefully settling into a Gothic chair. “She will just do for you, Monsieur du Guenic.”

“The marriage will never take place,” Camille said hastily.

Mortified by the calm air with which the marquise considered the Breton girl as the only suitable match for him, Calyste remained speechless.

“Why so, Camille?” Beatrix asked.

“Really, my dear,” Camille said, seeing Calyste’s despair, “you are not generous; did I advise Conti to marry?”

Beatrix looked at her friend with surprise mingled with indefinable suspicions.

Calyste, unable to understand Camille’s motive but sensing her assistance and seeing the faint color in her cheeks indicating violent emotion, awkwardly took her hand. But she left him and seated herself carelessly at the piano, like a woman so sure of her friend and lover that she can afford to leave him with another woman. She played variations, improvising them on themes chosen by the impulse of her mind; they were extremely melancholy.

Beatrix seemed to listen to the music, but she was really observing Calyste, who, too young and artless for the part Camille intended him to play, remained in rapt adoration before his true idol.

After about an hour, during which Camille continued to play, Beatrix rose and retired to her apartments. Camille immediately took Calyste into her chamber and closed the door, fearing to be overheard; for women have an amazing instinct of distrust.

“My child,” she said, “if you want to succeed with Beatrix, you must seem to love me still, or you will fail. You are a child; you know nothing of women; all you know is how to love. Now loving and making oneself beloved are two very different things. If you go your own way, you will fall into horrible suffering, and I wish to see you happy. If you rouse not the pride, but the self-will, the obstinacy that is a strong feature in her character, she is capable of going off at any moment to Paris and rejoining Conti; and what will you do then?”

“I shall love her.”

“You won’t see her again.”

“Oh! yes, I shall,” he said.

“How?”

“I shall follow her.”

“Why, you are as poor as Job, my dear boy.”

“My father, Gasselin, and I lived for three months in Vendee on one hundred and fifty francs, marching night and day.”

“Calyste,” Mademoiselle des Touches said, “now listen to me. I know you have too much candor to play a part, too much honesty to deceive; and I don’t want to corrupt such a nature as yours. Yet deception is the only way to win Beatrix; I take it upon myself. In a week from now, she shall love you.”

“Is it possible?” he said, clasping his hands.

“Yes,” Camille replied, “but it will be necessary to overcome certain pledges she has made to herself. I will do that for you. You must not interfere in the arduous task I shall undertake. The marquise has a true aristocratic delicacy of perception; she is keenly distrustful; no hunter could meet with game more wary or difficult to capture. You are wholly unable to cope with her; will you promise me blind obedience?”

“What must I do?” he asked.

“Very little,” she said. “Come here every day and devote yourself to me. Come to my rooms; avoid Beatrix if you meet her. We will stay together until four o’clock; you shall employ the time in study, and I in smoking. It will be hard for you not to see her, but I will find you many interesting books. You have read nothing yet of George Sand. I will send one of my people this very evening to Nantes to buy her works and those of other authors you ought to know. We will spend the evenings together, and I permit you to make love to me if you can—it will be for the best.”

“I know, Camille, that your affection for me is great and rare, making me wish I had never met Beatrix,” he replied with simple good faith. “But I don’t see what you hope from all this.”

“I hope to make her love you.”

“Good heavens! It cannot be possible!” he cried, clasping his hands toward Camille, who was greatly moved by the joy she gave him at her own expense.

“Now listen to me carefully,” she said. “If you break the agreement between us, if you have—not a long conversation—but a mere exchange of words with the marquise in private, if you let her question you, if you fail in the silent part I ask you to play, which is certainly not very difficult, I assure you,” she said seriously, “you will lose her forever.”

“I don’t understand what you are saying,” Calyste said, looking at Camille with adorable naivete.

“If you did understand it, you wouldn’t be the noble and beautiful Calyste that you are,” she replied, taking his hand and kissing it.

Calyste then did what he had never done before; he took Camille around the waist and kissed her gently, not with love but with tenderness, as he would kiss his mother. Mademoiselle des Touches did not restrain her tears.

“Go now,” she said. “My child; and tell your viscountess that my carriage is at her command.”

Calyste wanted to stay longer, but he was forced to obey her imperious gesture.

He returned home gaily, believing that in a week the beautiful Beatrix would love him. The players at _mouche_ found him once more the Calyste they had missed for the last two months. Charlotte attributed this change to herself. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel was charming to him. The Abbe Grimont endeavored to discern what was passing in the mother’s mind. The Chevalier du Halga rubbed his hands. The two old maids were as lively as lizards. The viscountess lost one hundred sous by accumulated _mouches_, exciting Zephirine’s cupidity, making her regret not being able to see the cards, and even speaking sharply to her sister-in-law, who acted as the proxy of her eyes.

The party lasted until eleven o’clock. Two defections occurred: the baron and the chevalier fell asleep in their respective chairs. Mariotte had made galettes of buckwheat, and the baroness produced a tea-caddy. The illustrious house of du Guenic served a little supper before the departure of its guests, consisting of fresh butter, fruits, and cream, in addition to Mariotte’s cakes. For this festal event, a silver teapot and beautiful old English china sent to the baroness by her aunts were brought out. This appearance of modern splendor in the ancient hall, along with the exquisite grace of its mistress, who made and poured out tea like a true Irish lady, created a charming atmosphere. The most exquisite luxury could never achieve the simple, modest, noble effect produced by joyful hospitality.

A few moments after Calyste’s departure from Les Touches, Beatrix, who had heard him go, returned to Camille, finding her with humid eyes lying back on her sofa.

“What is it, Felicite?” the marquise asked.

“I am forty years old, and I love him!” Mademoiselle des Touches said, her voice filled with agony, her eyes hard and brilliant. “If you knew, Beatrix, the tears I have shed over the lost years of my youth! To be loved out of pity! To know that one owes one’s happiness only to perpetual care, to the slyness of cats, to traps laid for innocence and all youthful virtues—oh, it is infamous! If it were not that one finds absolution in the magnitude of love, in the power of happiness, in the certainty of being forever above all other women in his memory, the first to carve on that young heart the ineffaceable happiness of absolute devotion, I would—yes, if he asked it—I would fling myself into the sea. Sometimes I wish he would ask it; it would then be an oblation, not a suicide. Ah, Beatrix, by coming here, you have unconsciously set me a hard task. I know it will be difficult to keep him against you; but you love Conti, you are noble and generous, you will not deceive me; on the contrary, you will help me retain Calyste’s love. I expected the impression you would make upon him, but I have not committed the mistake of seeming jealous; that would only have added fuel to the flame. On the contrary, before you came, I described you in such glowing colors that you hardly realize the portrait, although you are, it seems to me, more beautiful than ever.”

This vehement elegy, mingling truth with deception, completely duped the marquise. Claude Vignon had told Conti the reasons for his departure, and Beatrix was informed. She resolved to behave generously and give Calyste the cold shoulder; but at the same time, she felt a quiver of joy at being loved. The love a woman inspires in any man’s heart is flattering without hypocrisy, and it is impossible for some women to forego it; but when that man belongs to a friend, his homage gives more than pleasure—it gives delight. Beatrix sat beside her friend, coaxing her prettily.

“You have not a white hair,” she said. “You haven’t even a wrinkle; your temples are just as fresh as ever; whereas I know more than one woman of thirty who is obliged to cover hers. Look, dear,” she added, lifting her curls, “see what that journey to Italy has cost me.”

Her temples showed an almost imperceptible withering of the delicate skin. She raised her sleeves, revealing the same slight withering of her wrists, where the transparent tissue allowed the blue network of swollen veins to be visible, and three deep lines formed a bracelet of wrinkles.

“There, my dear, are two spots which—as a certain writer ferreting for the miseries of women has said—never lie,” she continued. “One must have suffered to know the truth of his observation. Happily for us, most men know nothing about it; they don’t read us like that dreadful author.”

“Your letter told me all,” Camille replied. “Happiness ignores everything but itself. You boasted too much of yours to be really happy. Truth is deaf, dumb, and blind where love truly exists. Consequently, seeing plainly that you have your reasons for abandoning Conti, I have feared to have you here. My dear, Calyste is an angel; he is as good as he is beautiful; his innocent heart will not resist your eyes; already he admires you too much not to love you at the first encouragement; your coldness alone can preserve him for me. I confess to you, with the cowardice of true passion, that if he were taken from me, I should die. That dreadful book of Benjamin Constant, ‘Adolphe,’ tells us only of Adolphe’s sorrows; but what about those of the woman? The man did not observe them enough to describe them; and what woman would have dared to reveal them? They would dishonor her sex, humiliate its virtues, and pass into vice. Ah! I measure the abyss before me by my fears, by these sufferings that are those of hell. But, Beatrix, I will tell you this: in case I am abandoned, my choice is made.”

“What is it?” Beatrix cried, her eagerness making Camille shudder.

The two friends looked at each other with the keen attention of Venetian inquisitors; their souls clashed in that rapid glance, striking fire like flints. The marquise lowered her eyes.

“After man, there is nought but God,” the celebrated woman said. “God is the Unknown. I shall fling myself into that as into some vast abyss. Calyste has sworn to me that he admires you only as he would a picture; but alas! you are but twenty-eight, in the full magnificence of your beauty. The struggle thus begins between him and me by falsehood. But I have one support; happily I know a means to keep him true to me, and I shall triumph.”

“What means?” Beatrix asked.

“That is my secret, dear. Let me have the benefits of my age. If Claude Vignon, as Conti has doubtless told you, flings me back into the gulf, I, who had climbed to a rock I thought inaccessible—I will at least gather the pale and fragile, but delightful flowers that grow in its depths.”

Madame de Rochefide was molded like wax in those able hands. Camille felt an almost savage pleasure in entrapping her rival. She sent her to bed that night piqued by curiosity, floating between jealousy and generosity, but most assuredly with her mind full of the beautiful Calyste.

“She will be enchanted to deceive me,” Camille thought as she kissed her goodnight.

When she was alone, the author, the constructor of dramas, gave way to the woman, and she burst into tears. Filling her hookah with tobacco soaked in opium, she spent most of the night smoking, dulling the sufferings of her soul, and seeing through the clouds around her the beautiful young head of her late lover.

“What a glorious book to write, if I were only to express my pain!” she said to herself. “But it is written already; Sappho lived before me. And Sappho was young. A fine and touching heroine, truly, a woman of forty! Ah! my poor Camille, smoke your hookah; you haven’t even the resource of making a poem of your misery—that’s the last drop of anguish in your cup!”

The next morning, Calyste arrived before midday and slipped upstairs into Camille’s room, where he found the books. Felicite sat before the window, smoking, contemplating the marshes, the sea, and Calyste, occasionally saying a few words about Beatrix. At one point, seeing the marquise strolling in the garden, she raised a curtain to attract her attention and throw a band of light across Calyste’s book.

“To-day, my child, I shall ask you to stay to dinner; but you must refuse, with a glance at the marquise, which will show her how much you regret not staying.”

When the three actors met in the salon, and this comedy was played, Calyste felt for a moment his equivocal position, and the glance he cast on Beatrix was far more expressive than Felicite expected. Beatrix had dressed charmingly.

“What a bewitching toilet, my dearest!” Camille said when Calyste had departed.

These maneuvers lasted six days, during which many conversations, into which Camille Maupin put all her ability, took place, unknown to Calyste, between herself and the marquise. They were like the preliminaries of a duel between two women—a duel without truce, in which the assault was made on both sides with snares, feints, false generosities, deceitful confessions, crafty confidences, by which one hid and the other bared her love; and in which the sharp steel of Camille’s treacherous words entered the heart of her friend, leaving its poison there. Beatrix eventually took offense at what she perceived as Camille’s distrust; she considered it out of place between them. At the same time, she was enchanted to find the great writer a victim to the pettiness of her sex, and she resolved to enjoy the pleasure of showing her where her greatness ended and how even she could be humiliated.

“My dear, what is to be the excuse today for Monsieur du Guenic’s not dining with us?” she asked, looking maliciously at her friend. “Monday you said we had engagements; Tuesday the dinner was poor; Wednesday you were afraid his mother would be angry; Thursday you wanted to take a walk with me; and yesterday you simply dismissed him without a reason. Today I shall have my way, and I mean that he shall stay.”

“Already, my dear!” Camille said with cutting irony. The marquise blushed. “Stay, Monsieur du Guenic,” Camille said, in the tone of a queen.

Beatrix became cold and hard, contradictory in tone, epigrammatic, and almost rude to Calyste, whom Felicite sent home to play _mouche_ with Charlotte de Kergarouet.

“_She_ is not dangerous at any rate,” Beatrix said sarcastically.

Young lovers are like hungry men; kitchen odors will not appease their hunger; they think too much of what is coming to care for the means that bring it. As Calyste walked back to Guerande, his soul was full of Beatrix; he paid no heed to the profound feminine cleverness Felicite displayed on his behalf. During this week, the marquise had only written once to Conti, a symptom of indifference that did not escape Camille’s watchful eyes, who imparted it to Calyste. All Calyste’s life concentrated on the short moment of the day when he was allowed to see the marquise. This drop of water, far from quenching his thirst, only intensified it. The magic promise, “Beatrix shall love you,” made by Camille, was the talisman with which he strove to restrain the fiery ardor of his passion. But he did not know how to consume the time; he could not sleep, spending the hours of the night reading; every evening he brought back cartloads of books, as Mariotte remarked.

His aunt cursed Mademoiselle des Touches; but his mother, who had gone to his room several times seeing his light burning late, knew by this time the secret of his conduct. Though for her, love was a sealed book, and she was unaware of her own ignorance, Fanny rose through maternal tenderness into certain ideas about it; but the depths of such sentiment being dark and obscured by clouds to her mind, she was shocked at the state in which she saw him; the solitary, uncomprehended desire of his soul, evidently consuming him, terrified her. Calyste had but one thought; Beatrix was always before him. In the evenings, while cards were played, his abstraction resembled his father’s somnolence. Finding him so different from when he loved Camille, the baroness became aware, with a sort of horror, of the symptoms of real love—a possession that had seized upon her son—a love unknown within the walls of that old mansion.

Feverish irritability and constant absorption in thought made Calyste almost doltish. Often he would sit for hours with his eyes fixed on some figure in the tapestry. One morning, his mother implored him to give up Les Touches and leave the two women forever.

“Not go to Les Touches!” he cried.

“Oh! yes, yes, go! Do not look so, my darling!” she cried, kissing him on the eyes that flashed such flames.

Under these circumstances, Calyste often came near losing the fruit of Camille’s plot through the Breton fury of his love, of which he was ceasing to be the master. Finally, he swore to himself, despite his promise to Felicite, to see Beatrix and speak to her. He wanted to read her eyes, bathe in their light, examine every detail of her dress, breathe its perfume, listen to the music of her voice, watch the graceful composition of her movements, embrace at a glance her whole figure, and study her as a general studies the field where he means to win a decisive battle. He willed as lovers will; he was seized by desires that closed his ears and darkened his intellect, throwing him into an unnatural state in which he was conscious of neither obstacles, distances, nor even the existence of his own body.

One morning, he resolved to go to Les Touches earlier than agreed and endeavor to meet Beatrix in the garden. He knew she walked there daily before breakfast.

Mademoiselle des Touches and the marquise had gone to see the marshes and the little bay with its fine sandy margin, where the sea penetrates and lies like a lake among the dunes. They had just returned and were walking up a garden path beside the lawn, conversing.

“If the scenery pleases you,” Camille said, “we must take Calyste and make a trip to Croisic. There are splendid rocks, cascades of granite, little bays with natural basins—charmingly unexpected and capricious things—besides the sea itself, with its store of marble fragments—a world of amusement. You will also see women making fuel with cow dung, which they nail against the walls of their houses to dry in the sun, after which they pile it up as we do peat in Paris.”

“What! Will you really risk Calyste?” the marquise cried, laughing, proving that Camille’s ruse had worked.

“Ah, my dear,” she replied, “if you knew the angelic soul of that dear child, you would understand me. In him, mere beauty is nothing; one must enter that pure heart, which is amazed at every step it takes into the kingdom of love. What faith! What grace! What innocence! The ancients were right in the worship they paid to sacred beauty. Some traveler, I forget who, relates that when wild horses lose their leader, they choose the handsomest horse in the herd for his successor. Beauty, my dear, is the genius of things; it is the ensign Nature hoists over her most precious creations; it is the truest of symbols as it is the greatest of accidents. Did anyone ever suppose that angels could be deformed? Are they not necessarily a combination of grace and strength? What makes us stand for hours before some picture in Italy, where genius has striven through years of toil to realize but one of those accidents of Nature? Come, call up your sense of the truth of things and answer me; is it not the Idea of Beauty which our souls associate with moral grandeur? Well, Calyste is one of those dreams, those visions realized. He has the regal power of a lion, tranquilly unsuspicious of its royalty. When he feels at ease, he is witty; and I love his girlish timidity. My soul rests in his heart away from all corruptions, all ideas of knowledge, literature, the world, society, politics—those useless accessories under which we stifle happiness. I am what I have never been—a child! I am sure of him, but I like to play at jealousy; he likes it too. Besides, that is part of my secret.”

Beatrix walked on pensively, in silence. Camille endured unspeakable martyrdom, casting a sidelong look at her companion that resembled flame.

“Ah, my dear; but _you_ are happy,” Beatrix said presently, laying her hand on Camille’s arm like a woman wearied by some inward struggle.

“Yes, happy indeed!” Camille replied with savage bitterness.

The two women dropped onto a bench from exhaustion. No creature of her sex was ever played upon like an instrument with more Machiavellian penetration than the marquise throughout this week.

“Yes, you are happy, but I!” she said—“to know of Conti’s infidelities and have to bear them!”

“Why not leave him?” Camille asked, seeing the hour had come to strike a decisive blow.

“Can I?”

“Oh! poor boy!”

Both gazed into a clump of trees with stupefied expressions.

Camille rose.

“I will go and hasten breakfast; my walk has given me an appetite,” she said.

“Our conversation has taken away mine,” Beatrix remarked.

The marquise, in her morning dress, was outlined in white against the dark greens of the foliage. Calyste, who had slipped through the salon into the garden, took a path along which he sauntered as if meeting her by accident. Beatrix could not restrain a quiver as he approached her.

“Madame, in what way did I displease you yesterday?” he asked after the first commonplace sentences were exchanged.

“But you have neither pleased me nor displeased me,” she said gently.

The tone, air, and manner in which the marquise said these words encouraged Calyste.

“Am I so indifferent to you?” he asked, troubled, tears coming to his eyes.

“Ought we not to be indifferent to each other?” the marquise replied. “Have we not, each of us, another, and a binding attachment?”

“Oh!” Calyste cried, “if you mean Camille, I did love her, but I love her no longer.”

“Then why are you shut up together every morning?” she said with a treacherous smile. “I don’t suppose that Camille, in spite of her passion for tobacco, prefers her cigar to you, or that you, in your admiration for female authors, spend four hours a day reading their romances.”

“So then you know—” began the guileless young Breton, his face glowing with happiness at being face to face with his idol.

“Calyste!” Camille cried angrily, suddenly appearing and interrupting him. She took his arm and drew him away. “Calyste, is this what you promised me?”

Beatrix heard Mademoiselle des Touches’ words as she disappeared toward the house, stupefied by the young man’s assertion, unable to comprehend it; she was not as strong as Claude Vignon. In truth, the part played by Camille Maupin, as shocking as it was grand, is one of those wicked grandeurs women only practice when driven to extremity. By it, their hearts are broken; in it, the feelings of their sex are lost; it begins an abnegation that ends by either plunging them to hell or lifting them to heaven.

During breakfast, which Calyste was invited to share, the marquise, whose sentiments could be noble and generous, made a sudden return upon herself, resolving to stifle the germs of love rising in her heart. She was neither cold nor hard to Calyste, but gently indifferent—a course that tortured him. Felicite proposed they make an excursion into the curious and interesting country between Les Touches, Croisic, and the village of Batz the day after next. She asked Calyste to hire a boat and sailors to take them across the little bay, undertaking to provide horses, provisions, and everything necessary for a pleasure party with no fatigue. Beatrix, however, stopped the matter short, saying she did not wish to make excursions around the country. Calyste’s face, which had beamed with delight at the prospect, suddenly clouded.

“What are you afraid of, my dear?” Camille asked.

“My position is so delicate I do not wish to compromise—I will not say my reputation, but my happiness,” she said meaningfully, glancing at the young Breton. “You know how suspicious Conti can be; if he knew—”

“Who will tell him?”

“He is coming back here to fetch me,” Beatrix said.

Calyste turned pale. Despite all Camille could urge, despite Calyste’s entreaties, Madame de Rochefide remained inflexible, showing what Camille had called her obstinacy. Calyste left Les Touches, the victim of one of those depressions of love that threaten, in certain men, to turn into madness. He began to consider decisive means of coming to an explanation with Beatrix.

When Calyste returned home, he isolated himself in his room until dinner, and afterward continued writing. His mother, concerned about his absence, found him surrounded by crumpled papers, composing a letter to Beatrix, driven by a newfound distrust of Camille. The marquise's demeanor during their brief encounter had inspired him.

Calyste's first love letter was a heartfelt outpouring, reflecting the intensity of his emotions. He expressed to Beatrix that his love for her had grown immensely upon meeting her, surpassing the dream he once had. He lamented that he had nothing new to say about her beauty, yet he felt a profound connection, believing he understood her deeply.

He acknowledged his youth and naivety, contrasting his feelings with those of common lovers. He felt unworthy of her, likening himself to an insect drawn to her light. Despite his devotion, he questioned why his ardent love did not seem to warm her heart, feeling that her disdain overshadowed his worship. He compared his past love for Felicite to his current feelings for Beatrix, declaring that she was his truth.

Calyste recognized that Beatrix had unknowingly disrupted his happiness, yet he felt no expectation of reciprocation. He reflected on the nature of love, learning from Camille that true love must grow and endure. He believed that if Beatrix loved him, they could soar together, unburdened by the fears that plagued Camille.

He implored Beatrix to be kind and to acknowledge his feelings, likening his love to a martyr's sacrifice. He asked her to engage with him as a friend, without fear, and expressed a desire to accompany her on outings, hoping for a response.

The letter continued with emotional declarations, but much of it was repetitive. Calyste's mother, upon reading it, was moved to tears, realizing her son was not happy. The letter's raw emotion frightened her, as it was her first encounter with such a love letter.

Calyste, torn about sending the letter, eventually sought advice from the Chevalier du Halga on how to deliver it discreetly. The chevalier suggested slipping it to Beatrix's maid, but Calyste decided to give it to her directly. He rushed to Les Touches, where he found Beatrix and Camille together. In a moment of impulsive love, he handed the letter to Beatrix, who accepted it without hesitation.

After a restless night filled with thoughts of Beatrix, Calyste awaited her response. When he finally received her letter, he read it in the garden. Beatrix acknowledged his noble feelings but reminded him that he was still a child. She emphasized her commitment to Camille, who loved him deeply, and expressed her own doubts about being a suitable partner. Beatrix felt that accepting his love would be an insult and that she could not betray Camille.

She candidly shared her struggles, stating that she would not be able to provide the happiness that Camille could. Beatrix recognized the depth of her own flaws and the potential for jealousy, asserting that she would only bring Calyste unhappiness. She encouraged him to seek a young girl worthy of his love, someone who could fulfill his dreams.

The baroness, moved by the letter, approached Calyste, seeking to understand his feelings. She read the letter aloud, both admiring Beatrix's nobility and expressing concern for her son's future. She urged him to consider marrying Charlotte de Kergarouet, suggesting that it would secure their estate and future.

Calyste, however, was resolute in his feelings for Beatrix, dismissing his mother's advice. He retreated to his room to write a response to Beatrix, expressing his unwavering love and commitment. The baroness, troubled by her son's passion, sought counsel from the Chevalier du Halga, who offered his insights on love and relationships.

As the baroness walked through Guerande, her presence sparked curiosity among the townspeople. She eventually found the chevalier and sought his perspective on Calyste's situation. The chevalier, amused by the young man's infatuation, offered lighthearted commentary on love and gallantry.

Upon returning home, the baroness discovered another letter from Calyste to Beatrix, revealing the depth of his obsession. In it, he declared that his happiness was tied solely to her, dismissing any thoughts of marrying anyone else. He expressed a desire to escape with her, believing that their love could transcend societal constraints.

The baroness, alarmed by the intensity of Calyste's feelings, prayed for his well-being. When Calyste returned, she expressed her concern, revealing that she had read his letter. He reassured her of his love, and they planned to take a boat to Croisic the next day, hoping to see Beatrix.

Calyste's letters reflected a maturity and clarity that surprised the baroness, as they revealed the depth of his emotions and the potential consequences of his infatuation.

XIII. DUEL BETWEEN WOMEN

One of the greatest pleasures for small minds is deceiving a greater soul, and Beatrix recognized her inferiority to Camille Maupin, not just in talent but in passion. As Calyste rushed to Les Touches, filled with the excitement of first love, Beatrix reveled in being the object of his affection, though she did not wish to reciprocate. She believed it noble to suppress her feelings, thinking she was mirroring Camille’s devotion and sacrificing for her friend. The vanities of Frenchwomen flattered her, and she felt a sense of self-approval in resisting Calyste’s allure.

The two women lounged in the salon, surrounded by flowers and a gentle breeze, while their inner turmoil contrasted sharply with the serene nature outside. Camille, aware of the emotional turmoil she had stirred, felt the need to guard her secrets against Beatrix, whom she viewed as a cunning rival. To cope with her own heartache, Camille sought solace in nature, finding meaning in the world around her. Calyste had become a guardian angel to her, leading her to higher realms of thought, but Beatrix’s triumphant demeanor unsettled her.

As Calyste entered, letter in hand, Camille observed the interaction between him and Beatrix, sensing an unspoken understanding. Beatrix now regarded Calyste as her own, while he appeared guilty and transparent. He announced plans for an excursion, and Camille, feeling a wave of anxiety, suggested they prepare for the trip. She sensed a conspiracy between them, which left her feeling discomposed.

After Calyste left to arrange for the boat, Camille’s fears grew. Upon returning, he found that neither Camille nor Beatrix could see him, leaving him puzzled and troubled. At home, he felt the weight of despair, contrasting sharply with the joy he had previously experienced. His mother noticed his change in demeanor, but he dismissed her concerns.

Meanwhile, Beatrix was grappling with her emotions after reading Calyste’s letter, which stirred feelings she had not experienced before. She questioned what Calyste and Camille were doing together, suspecting that Camille might be manipulating the situation. This realization filled her with jealousy and anger, leading her to pace her room in turmoil.

When Beatrix finally joined Camille for dinner, the atmosphere was tense. Camille sensed a confrontation was imminent and sought to avoid Calyste’s presence during the impending conflict. The dinner was marked by veiled insults and a battle of glances, culminating in a direct confrontation after the meal.

Guerande, July 1838.

To Madame la Duchesse de Grandlieu:

Ah, my dear mother! After three months, I know what jealousy is! My heart is torn between love and hatred. I feel betrayed and unloved. How fortunate I am to have you to confide in!

It’s enough for wives to hear: “Here’s a key to your palace; go everywhere, but avoid Les Touches!” to ignite our curiosity. Mademoiselle des Touches has sown bitterness in my heart. Why did she forbid me from visiting Les Touches? What happiness can I have if it hinges on a mere visit to a house in Brittany? Why should I fear? Is there anything to fear? This desire to know if my power is solid or fragile drove me to ask, “What sort of place is Les Touches?”

“Les Touches belongs to you,” my dear mother-in-law said.

“If Calyste had never set foot in Les Touches!” my aunt Zephirine exclaimed.

“He would not be my husband,” I replied.

“Then you know what happened there?” my mother-in-law asked slyly.

“It is a place of perdition!” Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel declared. “Mademoiselle des Touches committed many sins there.”

“But they saved her soul and enriched a convent,” the Chevalier du Halga interjected. “The Abbe Grimont told me she donated a hundred thousand francs to the nuns.”

“Would you like to visit Les Touches?” my mother-in-law asked. “It’s worth seeing.”

“No, no!” I hastily replied.

This little scene feels like a page from a diabolical drama, repeated under various pretexts. Eventually, my mother-in-law said, “I understand why you don’t go to Les Touches, and I think you’re right.”

You must admit, dear mother, that such an involuntary stab would compel anyone to question the stability of their happiness. To be fair, Calyste never suggested we visit that hermitage, now his property. But love makes us senseless, and his silence piqued my curiosity. So, I asked him, “What are you afraid of at Les Touches, that you never mention it?”

“Let’s go there,” he replied.

And so, I was caught, like other women who want to be caught, trusting chance to resolve my indecision. Les Touches is enchanting, profoundly artistic. I delighted in that place of horror where Mademoiselle des Touches had forbidden me to go. Poisonous flowers can be charming; the devil has flowers too. I studied Calyste, wanting to know if his passion was truly extinguished. I watched every expression on his face as he moved through the rooms, like a child searching for something hidden. Calyste seemed thoughtful, and I dared to mention Madame de Rochefide, whom I secretly called la Rocheperfide. 

“She must be light indeed to have stayed there,” I laughed. Calyste remained silent, so I added, “We’ll respect the dead.”

Still, he was silent.

“Have I displeased you?” I asked.

“No; but cease to revive that passion,” he replied.

What a statement! When he saw my disappointment, he increased his care and tenderness.

August.

Alas! I was on the edge of a precipice, amusing myself like the innocent heroines of melodramas. Suddenly, a horrible thought struck me: Calyste’s love might be growing through his memories, that he was channeling the stormy emotions I stirred by reminding him of that hateful Beatrix. Just think of it! That cold, unhealthy nature dares to call itself Beatrix, _Beatrice!_

Already, dear mother, I am forced to keep one eye open to suspicion, even when my heart belongs entirely to Calyste. Isn’t it a catastrophe when suspicion overcomes love? It happened this way: 

“This place is dear to me,” I said to Calyste one morning, “because I owe my happiness to it; so I forgive you for sometimes mistaking me for another woman.”

The loyal Breton blushed, and I embraced him. Yet, I have left Les Touches and will never return.

The very strength of my hatred for Madame de Rochefide—ah, a natural death, pleurisy, or some accident—makes me understand the depth of my love for Calyste. That woman haunts my dreams; will I ever encounter her? The postulant of the Visitation was right—Les Touches is a fatal spot. Calyste has revived his past emotions there, and they are more powerful than our joys. Please, dear mother, find out if Madame de Rochefide is in Paris; if she is, I will stay in Brittany. Poor Mademoiselle des Touches might regret her role in our marriage if she knew how much I am haunted by our odious rival! But this is degradation! I am ashamed of it all. Sometimes, I feel a frantic desire to flee from Guerande and the sands of Croisic.

August 25th.

I am determined to live in the ruins of the old chateau. Calyste, worried by my restlessness, agrees to take me. Either he knows so little about life that he suspects nothing, or he does know the cause of my flight, in which case he cannot love me. I tremble at the thought of finding the awful certainty I seek, so like a child, I cover my eyes to avoid the explosion.

Oh, mother! I am not loved as I love. Calyste is charming, it’s true! But what man, unless he were a monster, would not be amiable and gracious when receiving the affection of a pure, loving girl raised by you, as many women have said I am?

Guenic, September 18.

Has he forgotten her? That solitary thought echoes in my soul like remorse. Ah! dear mother, must all women struggle against memories as I do? Only innocent young men should marry pure young girls. But that’s a deceptive Utopia; better to have one’s rival in the past than in the future.

Ah! mother, pity me, though at this moment I am as happy as a woman who fears to lose her happiness and clings to it—one way of killing it, says that wise Clotilde.

I notice that for the last five months, I think only of myself, that is, of Calyste. Tell sister Clotilde that her melancholy wisdom often recurs to me. She is happy being faithful to the dead; she fears no rival. A kiss to my dear Athenais, about whom I see Juste is beside himself. From what you told me in your last letter, it’s evident he fears you will not give her to him. Cultivate that fear as a precious product. Athenais will be sovereign lady; but I, who fear I can never win Calyste back from himself, shall always be a servant.

A thousand tendernesses, dear mother. Ah! if my terrors are not delusions, Camille Maupin has sold me her fortune dearly. My affectionate respects to papa.

These letters reveal the secret relationship between husband and wife. Sabine envisioned a love marriage, while Calyste saw only a marriage of convenience. The joys of the honeymoon did not fully align with societal expectations.

During their stay in Brittany, the restoration and furnishing of the hotel du Guenic were overseen by the celebrated architect Grindot, under the supervision of Clotilde and the Duc and Duchesse de Grandlieu, with plans for the young couple to return to Paris in December 1838. Sabine settled into the rue de Bourbon with pleasure, not just to play the role of mistress of a grand household but to gauge her family’s opinion of her marriage.

Calyste, indifferent, allowed his sister-in-law Clotilde and mother-in-law the duchess to guide him in social matters, and they appreciated his compliance. He gained the social standing due to his name, fortune, and alliance. The success of his wife, regarded as one of the most charming women in Paris, the diversions of high society, and the duties of the season brought fresh life to their young household, creating a series of excitements and interludes. Sabine, considered happy by her mother and sister, dismissed her gloomy thoughts, hearing her lot envied by many unhappy wives, until her pregnancy provided additional assurances to this neutral union, which experienced women often viewed positively. In October 1839, the young Baronne du Guenic had a son and mistakenly decided to nurse him herself, believing that a mother should be wholly devoted to her child.

By the end of the following summer, in August 1840, Sabine was nearing the end of her nursing period. Calyste, during his two years in Paris, had shed the innocence that once adorned his early experiences in the world of passion. He now mingled with the young Duc Georges de Maufrigneuse, recently married to an heiress, Berthe de Cinq-Cygne, and the Vicomte Savinien de Portenduere, among others, fully grasping the differences between Parisian life and provincial life. Wealth brings idle hours, which Paris knows how to amuse and charm. Contact with young husbands who abandoned noble women for the pleasures of cigars, whist, and clubs gradually undermined the domestic virtues of the young Breton noble. A wife’s motherly concern, eager not to tire her husband, often supports the dissipations of young men. A wife is proud to see her husband return after granting him full freedom.

One evening in October, to escape the cries of their newly weaned child, Calyste, whose frown Sabine could not bear, went, urged by her, to the Varietes for a new play. The footman engaged a stall near the _avant-scene_. During the first interlude, Calyste spotted Madame de Rochefide in a proscenium box, not ten steps away. Beatrix in Paris! Beatrix in public! The shock of seeing her again after nearly three years struck him like an arrow. He had not forgotten the past; sometimes, he had even substituted Beatrix for his wife, which Sabine had noticed. Beatrix represented light, life, and the Unknown, while Sabine embodied duty, dullness, and the expected. It was like a thunderbolt striking him.

Out of loyalty, Calyste’s first instinct was to leave the theatre. As he exited the orchestra stalls, he noticed the proscenium box door ajar, and despite himself, he approached. He found Beatrix between two distinguished men, Canalis and Raoul Nathan. In the three years since he last saw her, Madame de Rochefide had changed dramatically; her transformation, while affecting her as a woman, made her even more poetic and attractive to Calyste. 

“Why isn’t Conti here?” Calyste asked Canalis in a low voice after exchanging pleasantries.

The former great poet, now a cabinet minister and orator, raised a finger to his lips, signaling silence. 

“I’m happy to see you,” Beatrix said demurely. “When I recognized you just now, I thought you at least would not disown me. Ah! my Calyste,” she whispered, “why did you marry?—and with such a little fool!”

When a woman whispers to a newcomer and invites him to sit beside her, men often excuse themselves from the conversation. 

“Come, Nathan,” Canalis said, “Madame la marquise will allow me to speak to d’Arthez, whom I see over there with the Princesse de Cadignan; it concerns business in the Chamber tomorrow.”

This departure gave Calyste time to recover from the shock, but he nearly lost his composure again as he inhaled the entrancing yet venomous perfume of Beatrix. Now gaunt and bony, her complexion faded, her eyes hollow, she had brightened her premature ruins with clever contrivances of Parisian artifice. She had taken to adopting a virgin air, recalling the maidens of Ossian, poetically painted by Girodet. 

“Why is Conti not here?” Calyste asked.

“Ah! you forget me!” she replied. “But you are married. That was one of my griefs since I last saw you. Not only do I lose love, but I lose a friendship I thought was Breton. Alas! we can bear everything. Now I suffer less, but I am broken! This is the first time I’ve poured out my heart in ages. I must seem proud before indifferent people, and I’ve lost my dear Felicite; there’s no one to whom I can say, _I suffer!_ But to you, I can share my anguish at seeing you so near. Yes,” she said, responding to Calyste’s gesture, “it’s almost fidelity. A look, a visit, a mere nothing means everything to us. Ah! you once loved me—loved me as I deserved to be loved by him who has trampled on the treasures I poured out on him. Yet, to my sorrow, I cannot forget; I love and wish to be faithful to a past that can never return.”

After this tirade, she played with her eyes to amplify the effect of her words, which seemed to flow from her soul like a torrent long restrained. Calyste, unable to speak, let tears gather in his eyes. Beatrix took his hand and pressed it, making him pale.

“Thank you, Calyste; that’s how a true friend responds to grief. We understand each other. No, don’t add another word; leave me now; people are watching; it might trouble your wife if someone tells her we were seen together—innocently enough, before a thousand people! There, you see I am strong; adieu—”

She wiped her eyes, making what could be called an antithesis of action.

“Let me laugh the laugh of a lost soul with the careless creatures who amuse me,” she continued. “I live among artists and writers, in the world I knew in the salon of our poor Camille—who may indeed have acted wisely. To enrich the man we love and then disappear saying, ‘I am too old for him!’ that is the best end, if one cannot die a virgin.”

She began to laugh, as if to dispel the melancholy impression she had made on her former lover.

“But,” Calyste said, “where can I see you?”

“I am hidden in the rue de Chartres opposite the Parc de Monceaux, in a little house suitable to my means; and I cram my head with literature—but only for myself, to distract my thoughts; God keep me from the mania of literary women! Now go, leave me; I must not allow the world to talk about me; what will it say on seeing us together? Adieu—oh! Calyste, my friend, if you stay another minute, I shall burst into tears!”

Calyste withdrew, after holding out his hand to Beatrix and feeling that strange sensation of a double pressure—full of seductive tingling.

“Sabine never knew how to stir my soul in that way,” he thought as he left the theatre.

The rest of the evening, the Marquise de Rochefide did not cast three straight glances at Calyste, but many sidelong looks tore at the soul of the man now wholly thrown back into his first, repulsed love.

When the baron du Guenic returned home, the splendor of his apartments reminded him of the mediocrity Beatrix had mentioned, and he hated his wealth because it could not belong to that fallen angel. When informed that Sabine had long been in bed, he rejoiced to find himself rich in the possession of a night to relive his emotions. He cursed the power of divination love had bestowed upon Sabine. When a man is adored by his wife, she reads his face like a book; she learns every quiver of its muscles, knows the source of its calmness, and questions the reason for the slightest sadness, seeking if it lies with her. She studies his eyes; for her, they reveal whether he loves or not. Calyste knew he was the object of such deep, naive, and jealous worship that he doubted his ability to maintain a cautious demeanor that wouldn’t betray his moral change.

“How shall I manage tomorrow morning?” he wondered as he fell asleep, dreading the scrutiny Sabine would impose. When they met at night, and sometimes during the day, Sabine would ask, “Do you still love me?” or “I don’t weary you, do I?” Charming questions, varied according to the cleverness of women, which hide their anxieties, whether feigned or real.

The noblest and purest hearts cannot escape the mud and slime cast up by hurricanes. The next morning, Calyste, who certainly loved his child, felt joy when he learned that Sabine was watching for signs of slight convulsions in their son, fearing the croup and not daring to leave him. The baron made a pretext of business and went out, avoiding breakfast at home. He escaped like a prisoner, happy to be free to walk along the Pont Louis XVI and the Champs Elysees to a café on the boulevard where he used to breakfast as a bachelor.

What is love? Does Nature rebel against social constraints? Does she need the impulse of life to be spontaneous, free, like an impetuous torrent foaming against rocks of opposition and coquetry, rather than a tranquil stream flowing between the banks of church and legal ceremony? 

It would be hard to find a young man more sacredly raised than Calyste, of purer morals, less stained by irreligion; yet he was drawn to a woman unworthy of him, when a benign chance had given him a wife of true aristocratic beauty, keen intellect, and angelic sweetness, a love that was passionate despite marriage, like his for Beatrix. Perhaps the noblest men retain some clay in their constitutions; the slough still pleases them. If this is so, the least imperfect human being is the woman, despite her faults and lack of reason. Madame de Rochefide, amid the circle of poetic pretensions surrounding her, belonged to the highest nobility; she presented a nature more ethereal than slimy, hiding the courtesan she was meant to be beneath an aristocratic exterior. Therefore, this explanation does not fully account for Calyste’s strange passion.

Perhaps we should look for its cause in a buried vanity that moralists have yet to uncover. There are noble men like Calyste, handsome, rich, distinguished, who tire—perhaps unknowingly—of marriage with a nature like their own; beings whose own nobleness does not surprise or move them; who seek from inferior or fallen natures the seal of their superiority—if they do not openly seek praise. Calyste found nothing to protect in Sabine; she was irreproachable. The powers stagnant in his heart were now to vibrate for Beatrix. If great men have played the Saviour’s part toward the woman taken in adultery, why should ordinary men be wiser in their generation?

Calyste spent the afternoon living on one thought: “I shall see her again!”—a sentiment that has often justified a journey of two thousand miles. He went with a light step to the rue de Chartres, recognizing the house at once, though he had never seen it. Once there, he stood at the foot of the staircase, halted by the old footman’s question: “Monsieur’s name?” Calyste felt he should leave Beatrix her freedom to receive or not receive him; he waited, gazing into the garden, its walls marked by rain’s black and yellow lines on the stucco of Paris.

Madame de Rochefide, like many great ladies who break their chains, had left her fortune to her husband when she fled; she could not beg from her tyrant. Conti and Mademoiselle des Touches had spared Beatrix the petty worries of material life, and her mother frequently sent her considerable sums. Now on her own, she faced a severe economy, quite different from the luxury she was accustomed to. She had taken refuge in a “little house” formerly belonging to a great lord, standing on the street but with a charming garden, rented for eighteen hundred francs. Still served by an old footman, a maid, and a cook from Alencon, who remained loyal through her trials, her penury would have been opulence to many an ambitious bourgeoise.

Calyste ascended a well-pumiced staircase, the landings adorned with flowering plants. On the first floor, the old servant opened a double door of red velvet with lozenges of red silk studded with gilt nails. Silk and velvet furnished the rooms he passed through. The dark red carpet, garnet velvet walls with gold trimmings, and conservatory-like windows filled with flowers created a dimly lit salon. Calyste could barely see two cases of old celadon on the mantel, between which gleamed a silver cup attributed to Benvenuto Cellini, brought from Italy by Beatrix. The gilded wood furniture, magnificent consoles, and a table with Persian cloth all testified to former opulence, well applied. On a little table, Calyste noticed jeweled knick-knacks, a book in progress, with a dagger’s handle used as a paper-cutter—symbol of criticism! Finally, ten richly framed watercolors on the walls depicted the various bedrooms where Madame de Rochefide’s wandering life had led her, showcasing her superior impertinence.

The rustle of a silk dress announced the unfortunate woman, appearing in a studied outfit that would have told a _roué_ her visitor was awaited. The gown, resembling a wrapper to showcase her white bosom, was of pearl-gray moire with large open sleeves, from which emerged arms covered with a second sleeve of puffed tulle, divided by straps and trimmed with lace. Her beautiful hair, held insecurely by a lace and flower cap, framed her elongated face. 

“Already!” she said, smiling. “A lover could not have shown more eagerness. You must have secrets to tell me, have you not?”

She gracefully posed on a sofa, inviting Calyste to sit beside her. By chance (perhaps a selected chance, for women have two memories, that of angels and devils), Beatrix was redolent of the perfume she used at Les Touches during her first acquaintance with Calyste. The scent, the contact with her dress, the glance of her eyes, which gathered and returned light in the dimness, turned Calyste’s head. He was again impelled to that violence that had once nearly cost Beatrix her life; but this time, she was on the edge of a sofa, not a rock; she rose to ring the bell, placing a finger on his lips. Calyste, recalled to order, controlled himself, especially since Beatrix had no hostile intentions.

“Antoine, I am not at home—for anyone,” she said. “Put some wood on the fire. You see, Calyste, I treat you as a friend,” she continued with dignity, “therefore do not treat me as you would a mistress. I have two remarks to make. First, I would not deny myself foolishly to any man I truly loved; and secondly, I am determined to belong to no other man on earth, for I believed, Calyste, that I was loved by a species of Rizzio, who was untrammeled by any engagement, and you see to what that fatal confidence has led me. As for you, you are now under the most sacred of duties; you have a young, amiable, delightful wife; moreover, you are a father. I should be, as you are, without excuse—we would be two fools—”

“My dear Beatrix, all these reasons vanish before a single word—I have never loved anyone but you on earth, and I was married against my will.”

“Ah! a trick played upon us by Mademoiselle des Touches,” she said, smiling.

Three hours passed, during which Madame de Rochefide held Calyste to the consideration of conjugal faith, pointing out the horrible alternative of utter renunciation of Sabine. She affected to regard the sacrifice of Sabine as a small matter, knowing her so well!

“My dear child,” she said, “that’s a woman who fulfills all the promises of her girlhood. She is a Grandlieu, to be sure, but she’s as brown as her mother the Portuguese, not to say yellow, and as dry and stiff as her father. To tell the truth, your wife will never go wrong; she’s a big girl who can take care of herself. Poor Calyste! Is that the sort of woman you needed? She has fine eyes, but such eyes are very common in Italy, Spain, and Portugal. Can any woman be tender with bones like hers? Eve was fair; brown women descend from Adam, blondes come from the hand of God, which left upon Eve his last thought after creating her.”

Around six o’clock, driven to desperation, Calyste took his hat to leave.

“Yes, go, my poor friend,” she said; “don’t give her the annoyance of dining without you.”

Calyste stayed. At his age, it was easy to ensnare him on his worst side.

“What! You dare to dine with me?” Beatrix said, feigning surprise. “My poor food does not alarm you? Have you enough independence of soul to crown me with joy by this little proof of your affection?”

“Let me write a note to Sabine; otherwise, she will wait for dinner until nine o’clock.”

“Here,” Beatrix said, “this is the table at which I write.”

She lit the candles herself and took one to the table to look over what he was writing.

“_My dear Sabine—_”

“‘My dear’? Can you really say that your wife is still dear to you?” she asked, looking at him with a cold eye that froze the marrow in his bones. “Go—you had better go and dine with her.”

“_I dine at a restaurant with some friends._”

“A lie. Oh, fy! You are not worthy to be loved either by her or by me. Men are all cowards in their treatment of women. Go, monsieur, go and dine with your dear Sabine.”

Calyste flung himself back in his armchair, pale as death. Bretons possess a natural courage that makes them obstinate under difficulties. Presently, the young baron sat up, put his elbow on the table, his chin in his hand, and looked at the implacable Beatrix with a flashing eye. He was so superb that a Northern or Southern woman would have fallen at his feet saying, “Take me!” But Beatrix, born on the borders of Normandy and Brittany, belonged to the race of Casterans; desertion had developed in her the ferocity of the Frank, the spitefulness of the Norman; she wanted some terrible notoriety as vengeance and yielded to no weakness.

“Dictate what I ought to write,” said the unfortunate man. “But, in that case—”

“Well, yes!” she said, “you shall love me then as you loved me at Guerande. Write: _I dine out; do not expect me._”

“What next?” Calyste asked, thinking something more would follow.

“Nothing; sign it. Good,” she said, seizing the note with restrained joy. “I will send it by a messenger.”

“And now,” cried Calyste, rising like a happy man.

“Ah! I believe I have kept my freedom of action,” she said, turning away from him and going to the fireplace, where she rang the bell. “Here, Antoine,” she said when the old footman entered, “send this note to its address. Monsieur dines here.”

Calyste returned home around two in the morning. After waiting until half-past twelve, Sabine had gone to bed, overwhelmed with fatigue. She slept, though keenly distressed by the terse wording of her husband’s note. Still, she rationalized it. The true love of a woman begins by explaining everything to the advantage of the man she loves, she thought.

The next morning, the child was better; the mother’s unease subsided, and Sabine came with a smiling face, little Calyste in her arms, to present him to his father before breakfast with the silly words and gestures that young mothers often use. This scene allowed Calyste to maintain a pleasant demeanor. He was charming to his wife, thinking in his heart that he was a monster, and he played with their son too well—he overdid it; but Sabine had not reached the stage where a woman recognizes such delicate distinctions.

At breakfast, however, she suddenly asked him: 

“What did you do yesterday?”

“Portenduere kept me to dinner,” he replied, “and after that, we went to the club to play whist.”

“That’s a foolish life, my Calyste,” Sabine said. “Young noblemen should busy themselves with recovering the ground lost by their fathers. It isn’t by smoking cigars, playing whist, idling away their leisure, and saying insolent things about parvenus who have driven them from their positions, nor by separating themselves from the masses whose soul and intellect they ought to embody, that the nobility will endure. Instead of being a party, you will soon be a mere opinion, as de Marsay said. Ah! if you only knew how my ideas on this subject have expanded since I have nursed and cradled your child! I’d like to see that grand old name of Guenic become historical again!” Then, suddenly locking her eyes onto Calyste’s, who listened pensively, she added, “Admit that the first note you ever wrote me was rather stiff.”

“I didn’t think of sending you word until I got to the club.”

“But you wrote on a woman’s note-paper; it had a perfume of feminine elegance.”

“Those club directors are such dandies!”

The Vicomte de Portenduere and his wife, formerly Mademoiselle Mirouet, had become very intimate with the du Guenics, sharing their box at the Opera. The two young women, Ursula and Sabine, had formed this friendship through delightful exchanges of counsel, care, and confidences regarding their first infants.

While Calyste, a novice in falsehood, thought, “I must warn Savinien,” Sabine was pondering, “I am sure that paper bore a coronet.” This thought flashed through her mind, and she scolded herself for having made it. Nevertheless, she resolved to find the paper, which she had flung into her letter-box amid her nighttime terrors.

After breakfast, Calyste went out, telling his wife he would return soon. He jumped into a low carriage, which was just beginning to replace the inconvenient cabriolet of their ancestors. He drove to the vicomte’s house, asking him to fib if Sabine questioned the vicomtesse. Then, urging his coachman to speed, he rushed to the rue de Chartres to learn how Beatrix had spent the rest of the night. He found her just after her bath, fresh, embellished, and breakfasting with a good appetite. He admired the grace with which his angel ate her boiled eggs and marveled at the beauty of the gold service, a gift from a monomaniac lord for whom Conti had composed a few ballads on his ideas, which the lord later published as his own!

Calyste listened entranced to the witty remarks of his idol, whose main goal was to amuse him, until she grew angry and wept when he rose to leave. He thought he had been there only half an hour, but it was past three when he returned home. His handsome English horse, a gift from the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu, was so bathed in sweat that it looked as if it had been driven through the sea. By one of those chances that jealous women create for themselves, Sabine was at a window overlooking the courtyard, anxious at Calyste’s absence. The condition of the horse, with its foaming mouth, surprised her.

“Where can he have come from?”

The question whispered in her ear by that power which is neither consciousness, nor devil, nor angel; which sees, forebodes, shows us the unseen, and creates belief in mental beings, creatures born of our brains, going and coming in the invisible world of ideas.

“Where do you come from, dear angel?” Sabine asked Calyste, meeting him on the first landing of the staircase. “Abd-el-Kader is nearly foundered. You told me you would be gone but a moment, and I have been waiting for you for three hours.”

“Well, well,” thought Calyste, who was improving in dissimulation, “I must get out of this with a present—”

“Dear little mother,” he said, taking her waist with more cajolery than he would have used if he hadn’t felt guilty, “I see it’s impossible to keep a secret, however innocent, from the woman who loves us—”

“Well, don’t tell secrets on the staircase,” she laughed. “Come in.”

In the salon adjoining their bedroom, she caught sight of Calyste’s face in the mirror, where he unwittingly allowed his real feelings and weariness to show.

“Now for your secret?” she said, turning around.

“You have shown such heroism as a nurse,” he said, “that the heir presumptive of the Guenics is dearer to me than ever, and I wanted to surprise you, just like any bourgeois of the rue Saint Denis. They are finishing a dressing-table for you at this moment, at which true artists have worked, and my mother and aunt Zephirine have contributed.”

Sabine clasped him in her arms, holding him tightly to her breast with her head on his neck, faint with the weight of happiness—not for the piece of furniture, but for dispelling her first dark doubt. It was one of those magnificent moments that can be counted, and which no love, however excessive, can prodigally spend, or life would soon burn out. Then, indeed, men should fall at the feet of women to adore them, for such moments are sublime, when the forces of heart and intellect gush forth like waters from sculptured nymphs.

Suddenly, as if bitten by a viper, she left Calyste, threw herself on a sofa, and fainted, for the reaction of a chill to her glowing heart nearly killed her. As she held Calyste, her nose at his cravat, abandoned to joy, she smelled the perfume of that letter paper! Another woman’s head had lain there, whose hair and face had left that adulterous odor! She had just kissed the spot where the kisses of her rival were still warm.

“What is the matter?” Calyste asked, bringing her back to consciousness with a damp cloth and salts.

“Fetch the doctor and my nurse, both! Yes, my milk has turned, I feel it. They won’t come at once unless you fetch them yourself—go!”

Calyste, alarmed, rushed out. The moment Sabine heard the closing of the porte-cochere, she sprang up like a frightened doe, walking about the salon as if beside herself, crying out, “My God! my God! my God!”

Those two words replaced all ideas. The crisis she had seized upon as a pretext truly took place. The hairs of her head felt like red-hot needles heated in the fire of a nervous fever. Her boiling blood seemed to mingle with her nerves, trying to escape from all her pores. She was blind for a moment, crying, “I am dying!”

At that terrible cry of the injured wife and mother, her maid rushed in. After she was laid upon her bed and regained both sight and mind, her first act of intelligence was to send the maid to her friend, Madame de Portenduere. Sabine felt her ideas whirl in her brain like straws in a waterspout. “I saw,” she said later, “myriads all at once.”

She rang for the footman and, in the throes of her fever, found the strength to write the following letter, driven by one mad desire—to have certainty:

To Madame la Baronne du Guenic:

Dear Mamma,—When you come to Paris, as you allow us to hope you will, I shall thank you in person for the beautiful present by which you, my aunt Zephirine, and Calyste wish to reward me for doing my duty. I was already well repaid by my own happiness in doing it. I can never express the pleasure you have given me with that beautiful dressing-table, but when you are with me, I shall try to do so. Believe me, when I array myself before that treasure, I shall think, like the Roman matron, that my noblest jewel is our little angel, etc.

She directed the letter to Guerande and gave it to the footman to post.

When the Vicomtesse de Portenduere arrived, the shuddering chill of reaction had succeeded Sabine’s first paroxysm of madness.

“Ursula, I think I am going to die,” she said.

“What is the matter, dear?”

“Where did Savinien and Calyste go after they dined with you yesterday?”

“Dined with me?” Ursula replied, surprised. “Savinien and I dined alone together and went to the Opera without Calyste.”

“Ursula, dearest, in the name of your love for Savinien, keep silence about what you have just said to me and what I shall now tell you. You alone shall know why I die—I am betrayed! at the end of three years, at twenty-two years of age!”

Her teeth chattered, her eyes dull and frozen, her face took on a greenish tinge.

“You! So beautiful! For whom?”

“I don’t know yet. But Calyste has told me two lies. Do not pity me, do not seem incensed, pretend ignorance, and perhaps you can find out who _she_ is through Savinien. Oh! that letter of yesterday!”

Trembling, she sprang from her bed to retrieve the letter.

“See,” she said, lying down again, “the coronet of a marquise! Find out if Madame de Rochefide has returned to Paris. Am I to have a heart in which to weep and moan? Oh, dearest!—to see one’s beliefs, one’s poetry, idol, virtue, happiness, all, all in pieces, withered, lost! No God in the sky! no love on earth! no life in my heart! no anything! I don’t know if there’s daylight; I doubt the sun. I have such anguish in my soul that I scarcely feel the horrible sufferings in my body. Happily, the baby is weaned; my milk would have poisoned him.”

At that thought, tears began to flow from Sabine’s eyes, which had been dry until then.

Pretty Madame de Portenduere, holding the fatal letter, was initially stupefied by this true sorrow, shocked by this agony of love, without yet understanding it, despite Sabine’s incoherent attempts to explain the facts. Suddenly, Ursula was illuminated by one of those ideas that come only to sincere friends.

“I must save her!” she thought. “Trust me, Sabine,” she cried. “Wait for my return; I will find out the truth.”

“Ah! in my grave I’ll love you,” exclaimed Sabine.

The viscountess went straight to the Duchesse de Grandlieu, pledging her to secrecy, and then explained Sabine’s situation.

“Madame,” she said, “do you not think with me that in order to avoid some fatal illness—perhaps, I don’t know, even madness—we had better confide the whole truth to the doctor and invent some tale to clear that hateful Calyste and make him seem innocent for the time being?”

“My dear child,” the duchess replied, chilled by this confidence, “friendship has given you for the moment the experience of a woman of my age. I know how Sabine loves her husband; you are right, she might become insane.”

“Or lose her beauty, which would be worse,” the viscountess added.

“Let us go to her!” cried the duchess.

Fortunately, they arrived just before the famous _accoucheur_, Dommanget, the only physician Calyste had been able to find.

“Ursula has told me everything,” the duchess said to her daughter, “and you are mistaken. In the first place, Madame de Rochefide is not in Paris. As for what your husband did yesterday, my dear, I can tell you that he lost a great deal of money at cards, so that he does not even know how to pay for your dressing-table.”

“But _that?_” Sabine said, holding out the fatal letter.

“That!” the duchess laughed. “Why, that is written on Jockey Club paper; everybody writes nowadays on coroneted paper; even our stewards will soon be titled.”

The prudent mother threw the unlucky paper into the fire as she spoke.

When Calyste and Dommanget arrived, the duchess, having instructed the servants, was informed immediately. She left Sabine to the care of Madame de Portenduere and stopped the _accoucheur_ and Calyste in the salon.

“Sabine’s life is at stake, monsieur,” she said to Calyste; “you have betrayed her for Madame de Rochefide.”

Calyste blushed, like a respectable girl caught in a fault.

“And,” the duchess continued, “as you do not know how to deceive, you have behaved so clumsily that Sabine has guessed the truth. But I have for the moment repaired your blunder. You do not wish my daughter’s death, I am sure—All this, Monsieur Dommanget, will put you on the track of her real illness and its cause. As for you, Calyste, an old woman like me understands your error, though she does not pardon it. Such pardons can only be earned by a lifetime of happiness. If you wish me to esteem you, you must, first, save my daughter; next, you must forget Madame de Rochefide; she is only worth having once. Learn to lie; have the courage of a criminal, and his impudence. I have just told a lie myself, and I shall have to do hard penance for that mortal sin.”

She then told the two men the lies she had invented. The clever physician, sitting at the bedside of his patient, studied her symptoms to find a remedy, while ordering measures that depended on rapid execution. Calyste, sitting at the foot of the bed, tried to convey tenderness in his gaze.

“So it was play that put those black circles around your eyes?” Sabine said to him in a weak voice.

The words made the doctor, the mother, and the viscountess tremble, and they exchanged covert glances. Calyste turned as red as a cherry.

“That’s what comes of nursing a child,” Dommanget said brutally but cleverly. “Husbands are lonely when separated from their wives, and they go to the club and play. But you needn’t worry about the thirty thousand francs Monsieur le baron lost last night—”

“Thirty thousand francs!” Ursula exclaimed, in a silly tone.

“Yes, I know it,” Dommanget replied. “They told me this morning at the house of the young Duchesse Berthe de Maufrigneuse that it was Monsieur de Trailles who won that money from you,” he added, turning to Calyste. “Why do you play with such men? Frankly, monsieur le baron, I can well believe you are ashamed of it.”

Seeing his mother-in-law, a pious duchess, the young viscountess, a happy woman, and the old _accoucheur_, a confirmed egotist, all three lying like a dealer in bric-a-brac, the kind and feeling Calyste understood the gravity of the danger, and two heavy tears rolled from his eyes, completely deceiving Sabine.

“Monsieur,” she said, sitting up in bed and glaring at Dommanget, “Monsieur du Guenic can lose thirty, fifty, a hundred thousand francs if it pleases him, without anyone having the right to think it wrong or lecture him. It is far better that Monsieur de Trailles should win his money than that we should win Monsieur de Trailles’.”

Calyste rose, took his wife around the neck, kissed her on both cheeks, and whispered: 

“Sabine, you are an angel!”

Two days later, the young wife was thought to be out of danger, and the next day Calyste was at Madame de Rochefide’s, making a merit of his infamy.

“Beatrix,” he said, “you owe me happiness. I have sacrificed my poor little wife to you; she has discovered everything. That fatal paper on which you made me write bore your name and your coronet, which I never noticed—I saw only you! Fortunately, the ‘B’ was by chance effaced. But the perfume you left upon me and the lies I told have betrayed my happiness. Sabine nearly died from it; her milk went to her head; erysipelas set in, and she may bear the marks for the rest of her days.”

As Beatrix listened to this tirade, her face was due North, icy enough to freeze the Seine had she looked at it.

“So much the better,” she said; “perhaps it will whiten her for you.”

And Beatrix, now as hard as her bones, sharp as her voice, and harsh as her complexion, continued a series of atrocious sarcasms. There is no greater blunder than for a man to talk of his wife, if she is virtuous, to his mistress, unless it be to talk of his mistress, if she is beautiful, to his wife. But Calyste had not received that Parisian education we must call the politeness of passions. He knew neither how to lie to his wife nor how to tell his mistress the truth—two apprenticeships a man in his position must undertake to manage women. He was therefore compelled to employ all the power of passion to obtain from Beatrix a pardon, which she forced him to solicit for two hours; a pardon refused by an injured angel who raised her eyes to the ceiling to avoid seeing the guilty man, and who put forth reasons sacred to marquises in a voice quivering with tears that were furtively wiped with her handkerchief.

“To speak to me of your wife on the very day after my fall!” she cried. “Why did you not tell me she is a pearl of virtue? I know she thinks you handsome; pure depravity! I, I love your soul! For let me tell you, my friend, you are ugly compared to many shepherds on the Campagna of Rome,” etc.

Such speeches may surprise the reader, but they were part of a system profoundly meditated by Beatrix in this her third incarnation—for at each passion, a woman becomes another being and advances one step further into profligacy, the only word that properly describes the effect of experiences gained through such adventures. The Marquise de Rochefide had sat in judgment on herself before the mirror. Clever women are never deceived about themselves; they count their wrinkles, assist at the birth of their crow’s-feet, know themselves by heart, and even acknowledge it by the greatness of their efforts at preservation. Therefore, to successfully struggle against a splendid young woman, to carry away from her six triumphs a week, Beatrix resorted to the knowledge and science of courtesans. Without acknowledging to herself the baseness of this plan, led to employ such means by a Turkish passion for Calyste’s beauty, she resolved to make him think himself unpleasant, ugly, ill-made, and to behave as if she hated him. No system is more fruitful with conquering natures. To such natures, the presence of repugnance to be vanquished renews the triumph of the first day on all succeeding days. And it is something even better. It is flattery in the guise of dislike. A man then thinks to himself, “I am irresistible,” or “My love is all-powerful because it conquers her repugnance.” If you deny this principle, divined by all coquettes and courtesans throughout all social zones, you may as well reject all seekers after knowledge, all delvers into secrets, repulsed through years in their duel with hidden causes. Beatrix added to the use of contempt as a moral piston a constant comparison of her own poetic, comfortable home with the hotel du Guenic. All deserted wives who abandon themselves in despair also neglect their surroundings, so discouraged are they. On this, Madame de Rochefide counted, and soon began an underhand attack on the luxury of the faubourg Saint-Germain, which she characterized as stupid.

The scene of reconciliation, in which Beatrix made Calyste swear and reswear hatred for the wife, who, she said, was playing comedy, took place in a perfect bower where she displayed her graces amid ravishing flowers and rare plants of the costliest luxury. The science of nothings, the trifles of the day, she carried to excess. Fallen into a mortifying position through Conti’s desertion, Beatrix was determined to gain, at least, the fame that unprincipled conduct brings. The misfortune of the poor young wife, a rich and beautiful Grandlieu, should be her pedestal.

When a woman returns to ordinary life after nursing her first child, she often appears rejuvenated and charming. This phase of maternity brings a freshness and vitality to young mothers. However, Sabine, upon returning to her husband Calyste, realizes he is not the same man she married. Instead of feeling secure in her happiness, she becomes anxious and seeks certainty about their relationship. This certainty arrives in the form of a letter from her mother, which hints at Calyste's discontent and the presence of another woman, Beatrix.

Sabine's heart sinks as she reads her mother's letter, which lacks any mention of Calyste's feelings. In a moment of despair, she writes a note on the letter, indicating her awareness of the situation, and leaves it for Calyste to find. He discovers it but chooses to ignore it, throwing the letter into the fire. Sabine is left in agony, feeling she has displeased him and fearing she has humiliated her idol.

As the days pass, Sabine's torment deepens. Beatrix, emboldened, writes to Calyste, and Sabine, recognizing the perfume on the letter, realizes the extent of her rival's influence. In a moment of madness, she dances with her child, seeking solace in the innocence of motherhood. However, her heart is heavy with sorrow, and she weeps alone when Calyste leaves to read Beatrix's letter.

Sabine's suffering is compounded by her attempts to regain Calyste's affection. She decorates their home with flowers, hoping to please him, but his indifference only deepens her despair. She becomes obsessed with understanding his feelings and comparing herself to Beatrix, leading to a cycle of jealousy and self-doubt. Sabine's efforts to impress him with her cooking and appearance seem futile, as Calyste remains unresponsive.

Despite her struggles, Sabine's love for Calyste persists. She tries to embody the virtues of a devoted wife, but her efforts are met with disappointment. One evening, after a failed attempt to impress him, she succumbs to despair, tearing off her beautiful dress in frustration.

As time passes, Sabine's health deteriorates under the weight of her grief. Her mother, the duchess, notices her decline and attempts to draw her away from the source of her suffering. Sabine, however, remains silent about her pain, fearing interference in her marriage. Eventually, she confides in her sister about her anguish, expressing a desire for her child to be cared for if she were to die.

In a moment of vulnerability, Sabine warns her sister Athenais against the dangers of love, advising her to be cautious in her marriage. She reflects on how her virtues have become obstacles to her happiness, lamenting that her straightforwardness and passion have not won Calyste's heart. Instead, she feels compelled to adopt the duplicity she believes is necessary to retain his affection.

The duchess, concerned for her daughter, seeks advice from the Abbe Brossette, a respected priest. She reveals her plan to intervene in Calyste's relationship with Beatrix, hoping to restore her daughter's happiness. The abbe cautions her against mixing spiritual matters with worldly intrigues but acknowledges the seriousness of Sabine's situation.

The duchess proposes a scheme to persuade Beatrix to return to her husband, thereby freeing Calyste. The abbe, intrigued, encourages her to consider the moral implications of her actions. He suggests that rather than resorting to deceit, she should focus on a more honorable approach to resolve the situation.

As the duchess contemplates her plan, she becomes determined to act for the sake of her daughter's happiness, even if it means engaging in morally questionable actions. She resolves to seek a solution that will restore peace to her family and save Sabine from her suffering.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A storm was brewing for Monsieur de Rochefide, who, at that moment, was enjoying a Parisian life as much as he had with Beatrix. The duke believed it nearly impossible to disrupt such a satisfactory existence. To understand the vast differences in how society treats men and women in similar situations, we must explore Rochefide's life after Beatrix left him. This contrast may inspire women to remain in their homes and fight for their dignity, as Sabine du Guenic did.

After Beatrix's departure, Arthur de Rochefide, now an only child due to his sister's death, became the sole owner of the hotel de Rochefide and inherited a substantial fortune from his father. His total income reached a thousand francs a day, which, for a man like him, was sufficient for happiness. While Beatrix fulfilled her maternal duties, Rochefide enjoyed his wealth without spending it extravagantly. His vanity, bolstered by his good looks, allowed him to dismiss women and rely on the wit of others, which he regurgitated as his own. His military background added a joviality to his conversations, making him appear witty to those around him.

As a husband, he was pitied, and society blamed Beatrix for leaving such a good man. He was a member of various clubs and subscribed to numerous causes, seeking to distinguish himself through fashionable pursuits. His primary indulgence was his collection of racehorses, managed by an English groom, costing him a significant monthly sum. Despite his wealth, he lacked personal taste or intellect, relying on inherited fortune. After experiencing the downsides of marriage, he was relieved to return to bachelorhood, claiming he was "born with a caul," or destined for good luck.

Rochefide's indifference to domestic life was evident; he rarely dined or slept at home. After several romantic escapades, he married Madame Schontz, a woman of dubious reputation known in the circles of Fanny Beaupre and others.
