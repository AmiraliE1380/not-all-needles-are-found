For souls given to effusiveness there is a delightful hour between day and night; twilight softens lights and reflections and invites a dreamy mood where light and shade play. The prevailing silence makes it especially loved by artists, who become contemplative, step back from pictures they can no longer work on, and judge, rapt by meanings that flash on the inner eye. He who has never stood pensive by a friend in such an hour can hardly grasp its soothingness. Favored by the clear-obscure, material skill used to produce illusion disappears. If the work is a painting, the figures seem to speak and walk; shade is shadow, light is day; flesh breathes, eyes move, blood flows, fabrics change sheen. Imagination heightens every realistic detail and sees only the work’s beauties. At that hour illusion rules; it wakens a mind’s night, peopled by dreams, bears the soul to a world of fancies where the artist forgets the real world—yesterday, tomorrow, all miseries and goods alike.

At this magic hour a young, talented painter who saw in art nothing but Art itself was perched on a step-ladder working on a large, nearly finished painting. Criticising and admiring himself, floating on the current of his thoughts, he lost himself in a meditative mood that elevated and soothed him. His reverie lasted long. Night fell. Whether he meant to come down or made some ill-judged movement—he did not know—the painter fell, struck his head on a footstool, lost consciousness, and lay motionless for an unknown time.

A sweet voice roused him. When he opened his eyes a bright light made him close them again; through the mist he heard two women whispering and felt two timid hands supporting his head. He soon recovered; by the light of an old-fashioned Argand lamp he saw the most charming face he had seen, a head that for an artist realized the ideal beauty he had formed: features recalling Prudhon’s delicacy and Girodet’s poetic sentiment—fresh temples, regular eyebrows, pure outline, virginal innocence on every feature. Her figure was slight and graceful; her dress simple and neat, showing neither wealth nor penury.

As he came to, the painter expressed admiration and fumbling thanks. A handkerchief pressed to his forehead; amid the studio smell he recognized the strong odor of ether used to revive him. He saw an old woman, looking like a marquise of the old school, holding the lamp and advising the younger woman.

“Monsieur,” said the young woman in answer to a few vague questions, “my mother and I heard the noise of your fall and fancied we heard a groan. The silence after the crash alarmed us, and we hurried up. Finding the key in the latch, we entered and found you lying motionless. My mother fetched what was needed to bathe your head and revive you. You have cut your forehead—there. Do you feel it?”

“Yes, I do now,” he replied.

“Oh, it will be nothing,” returned the elder. “Happily your head rested against this lay-figure.”

“I feel infinitely better,” replied the painter. “I need nothing further but a hackney cab to take me home. The porter’s wife will go for one.”

She tried to repeat his thanks, but the elder interrupted him at every sentence: “Tomorrow, monsieur, pray be careful to apply leeches, or to be bled, and drink a few cups of something healing. A fall may be dangerous.”

The young girl stole glances at the painter and the pictures. Her expression revealed perfect propriety; her curiosity seemed a gentle absence of mind, and her eyes spoke the natural interest women feel in suffering. The two strangers seemed to forget his works in his mishap. When he reassured them they left, looking at him with anxious kindness—free from insistence or familiarity, without indiscreet questions or invitations. Their noble and simple manners made a quiet impression on the painter; later, recalling the incident, he was greatly struck by them.

On the landing the old lady gently observed, “Adelaide, you left the door open.”

“That was to hear me fall,” said the painter with a grateful smile.

“You came down just now, mother,” replied the young girl with a blush.

“Would you like us to accompany you downstairs? The stairs are dark.”

“No, thank you, madame; I am much better.”

“Hold tightly by the rail.”

The two women remained to light him, listening to his steps.

To appreciate the interest this scene aroused, one must know something of the painter. A few days earlier he had established his studio in the attics of a house in the darker, muddier part of the Rue de Suresnes, near the Rue des Champs-Elysees and almost opposite the Church of the Madeleine. Fame had recently made him one of the artists most dear to his country; he was beginning to feel free from want and was, as he put it, enjoying his last privations. Instead of working in cheaper studios near the city gates he had spared himself the long walk and the loss of time now more valuable than before.

No man inspired more interest than Hippolyte Schinner, though he did not lightly reveal the secrets of his life. He was the idol of a necessitous mother who had raised him amid severe privations. Mademoiselle Schinner, daughter of an Alsatian farmer, had never married. Her gentle soul had been cruelly crushed long ago by a rich man who showed no delicacy in his love affairs. The day she lost her illusions she embraced reflection, religious thought, and resignation. She refused the betrayer’s alms, renounced the world, and made a glory of her shame. She devoted herself to maternal love, sought in it her joys instead of social pleasures, worked, and saved a treasure for her son. In after years one day amply repaid her sacrifices.

At the last exhibition her son received the Cross of the Legion of Honor. Newspapers hailed an unknown genius, artists acknowledged Schinner as a master, and dealers covered his canvases with gold. At twenty-five Hippolyte, to whom his mother had transmitted her woman’s soul, understood his standing. Eager to restore to his mother the pleasures society had denied her, he lived for her, hoping that fame and fortune would one day make her happy, rich, respected, and surrounded by people of mark. Schinner chose friends among the honorable and distinguished; fastidious in selection, he desired to raise further the position his talent had placed him in. His solitary work from boyhood had left him with beautiful beliefs that grace early days. His adolescent soul was not closed to the shy emotions that make a young man an apart being, full of poetry and virginal hopes—puerile to the worldly but deep in their singleness.

He had gentle, polite manners that spoke to the soul and fascinated even those who did not understand them. His voice, from the heart, stirred noble sentiments and testified to his modesty by ingenuous tones. Those who saw him felt an attraction of moral nature they could not analyze; they might call it galvanism or attempt to express it in formulas.

These details help explain why, in the absence of the porter whom he had sent to fetch a coach, Hippolyte did not question the porter’s wife closely about the two women who had tended him. He answered her natural inquiries about his accident and the fourth-floor tenants but could not stop her chatter, led by lodge policy and gossip.

“Ah,” she said, “they were, no doubt, Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, who have lived here four years. We do not know exactly what these ladies do; in the morning an old woman who is half deaf comes to help them; in the evening, two or three old gentlemen with ribbon loops, like you, monsieur, come to see them and often stay very late. One comes in a carriage with servants; they say he has sixty thousand francs a year. However, they are very quiet tenants, as you are, monsieur; and economical! they live on nothing, and pay as soon as a letter is brought. It is queer, monsieur, the mother’s name is not the same as the daughter’s. Ah, but when they go for a walk in the Tuileries, mademoiselle is very smart, and she never goes out without being followed by a lot of young men; but she shuts the door in their face, and she is quite right. The proprietor would never allow—”

The coach came; Hippolyte heard no more and went home. His mother dressed his wound and would not permit him to return to the studio the next day. After advice and treatments, Hippolyte remained at home three days. During this enforced idleness his fancy recalled, bit by bit, the scene of his fainting fit. The young girl’s profile returned clearly; he saw the mother’s faded features again, felt Adelaide’s touch. He remembered some gesture whose grace memory enhanced; then an attitude, a tone of voice rising like objects from deep waters to the surface.

When he could resume work he went early to his studio; really he hurried to visit his neighbors. When passion wakes, inexplicable pleasures are felt; he mounted the fourth-floor stairs slowly, and his heart throbbed as he neared the humble brown door of Mademoiselle Leseigneur. He fancied similarity between their positions, imagined misfortunes of birth akin to his own. He lingered at the studio to think of them, dined there, then about seven o’clock went to call.

No painter of manners has sufficiently told the curious privacy of certain Parisian existences: fresh elegant toilettes and brilliant women whose homes betray doubtful comfort. If my picture is sometimes bold, blame the truth; the appearance of his neighbors’ rooms greatly influenced Hippolyte Schinner’s feelings and hopes.

The house belonged to one of those proprietors who abhor repairs and decoration, who regard being a Paris homeowner as business. Between miser and usurer, such men are allergic to moving a cupboard or opening an air-hole; when chimneys fall they deprive themselves of theatre nights “on account of repairs.” Hippolyte, who had seen a comical scene about decorative repairs, was not surprised by dark greasy paint, oily stains, and other signs of neglect. These stigmata of poverty are not without a sort of poetry to an artist.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur opened the door. Recognizing the young artist she bowed and, with Parisian tact and pride, turned to shut a glass partition through which Hippolyte might have seen linen hung over patent ironing stoves, an old camp-bed, charcoal and irons, a filter, household crockery—utensils of a small household. Muslin curtains screened this capharnaum, lighted by windows facing a neighboring yard.

With an artist’s quick eye Hippolyte saw the uses, furniture, and general condition of this cut-in-half first room. The more honorable half, serving as ante-room and dining-room, was hung with old salmon-rose paper with a flock border patched with wafers. Prints of the battles of Alexander in gilding-rubbed frames were arranged on the walls. In the middle stood a massive, old-fashioned mahogany table worn at the edges. A small stove with a thin pipe stood before the chimney, whose hearth was blocked by a cupboard. Chairs showed remains of former splendor—carved mahogany, red morocco seats, gilt nails—scarred like a veteran.

This room was a museum of objects that show both luxury and penury: a splendidly finished telescope hung over a discolored glass on the chimney. Between chimney and partition was a wretched painted sideboard pretending to be mahogany. Slippery red quarries, shabby rugs, and all the furniture shone with hard cleanliness that accentuated oldness. An indescribable odor mixed exhalations from the lumber-room, dining-room vapors, and stair smells, though the window was partly open. Street air fluttered dusty curtains, carefully drawn to hide a window bay where former tenants left ornamental additions—a sort of domestic fresco.

Adelaide hastened to open the inner room door and announced the painter with obvious pleasure. Hippolyte, who had earlier seen similar poverty in his mother’s home, noted these signs with an impression vivid as earliest memories and entered with sympathy rather than scorn.

“Well, monsieur, I hope you no longer feel the effects of your fall,” said the old lady, rising from an antique armchair and offering a seat.

“No, madame. I have come to thank you for the kind care you gave me, and above all mademoiselle, who heard me fall.”

As he spoke—words stamped with the exquisite awkwardness of first love—Hippolyte looked at the young girl. Adelaide lit the Argand lamp, replacing a tallow candle in a large copper candlestick, then placed the lamp on the chimney shelf and sat a little behind the painter to look at him at her ease while appearing interested in the lamp. The flame sputtered in the damp chimney. Seeing the large mirror on the chimney-piece, Hippolyte fixed his eyes on it to admire Adelaide; her stratagem only embarrassed them both.

While talking with Madame Leseigneur—Hippolyte called her so—he examined the room unobtrusively. Egyptian figures on iron fire-dogs were scarcely visible under cinders; two brands struggled before a sham log. An old Aubusson carpet, faded and mended, left the tiled floor cold. Walls were hung with reddish paper imitating figured silk. Opposite the windows he saw a crack and the paper outline of double doors closing a recess where Madame Leseigneur likely slept, poorly disguised by a sofa. Above a mahogany chest of drawers hung a portrait of an officer of rank, so dim he could not judge it well; from what he saw it must have been painted in China. Window-curtains of red silk were as faded as the furniture in red-and-yellow worsted work. On the chest’s marble top was a costly malachite tray with a dozen magnificently painted coffee cups, perhaps Sevres. On the chimney shelf stood an Empire clock—a warrior driving four horses on a chariot whose wheel bore the hours. Tall candlesticks held smoke-yellowed tapers; porcelain vases crowned with dusty artificial flowers stood in the corners.

In the middle of the room a card-table with new packs suggested a painful dilemma: either these two women were perfectly honest or they lived by intrigue and gambling. Yet in looking at Adelaide, so pure-minded, Schinner could not but believe in her innocence and attribute the furniture’s incoherence to honorable causes.

“My dear,” said the old lady to the young one, “I am cold; make a little fire, and give me my shawl.”

Adelaide fetched a cashmere shawl—once costly, now faded and darned—and wrapped her mother in it. She then brought a bundle of small wood from the lumber-room and threw it on the fire to revive it.

It is hard to reproduce the ensuing conversation. Guided by the tact born of early hardship, Hippolyte did not remark on his neighbors’ poverty; any direct question would be indiscreet. The simplest pity can offend, so his thoughts and words diverged. The two ladies began by talking of painting; women easily guess the embarrassment of a first call and supply devices to end it. Questioning him about his art emboldened him to talk, and the chat’s light nothings led Hippolyte to reflections that revealed his habits and mind. Trouble had prematurely faded the old lady’s face; only prominent features remained, an outline suggesting shrewdness with grace in the eyes—an expression of the old Court that can hint either at artifice or refined delicacy.

A woman’s face hides mystery: the line between frankness and duplicity, intrigue and heart, is inscrutable. The old lady’s face, like her room, made it hard to tell whether the squalor covered vice or highest virtue. But at Schinner’s age the heart’s first impulse is to believe in goodness. As he studied Adelaide’s noble, almost haughty brow and soulful eyes, he breathed the modest fragrance of virtue. He seized an opportunity to discuss portraits and thus examine the faded pastel whose chalk had fallen away.

“You are attached to that picture for the sake of the likeness, no doubt, mesdames, for the drawing is dreadful?” he said, looking at Adelaide.

“It was done at Calcutta, in great haste,” replied the mother, her voice agitated.

She gazed at the formless sketch with the absorbed melancholy of someone recalling happier days; in her face were traces of perennial regret. The painter interpreted her attitude as the mark of a once-happy life. He then sat down beside her.

“Madame,” he said, “in a very short time the colors of that pastel will disappear. The portrait will only survive in your memory. Will you allow me to reproduce the likeness on canvas? It will be more permanently recorded. Grant me, as a neighborly favor, the pleasure of doing you this service. There are times when an artist is glad of a respite from greater undertakings by doing work of less lofty pretension; it will be a recreation for me.”

The old lady flushed at the offer, and Adelaide shot a glance of deep feeling. Hippolyte wanted some tie to his neighbors, to gain a right to mingle in their life. His offer appealed to the heart’s affections, gratified his pride as an artist, and could not wound the ladies. Madame Leseigneur accepted, without eagerness or reluctance, with the self-possession of a noble soul, aware of the delicate nature of such an obligation.

“I fancy,” said the painter, “that the uniform is that of a naval officer.”

“Yes,” she said, “that of a captain in command. Monsieur de Rouville—my husband—died at Batavia from a wound received in a fight with an English ship off the Asiatic coast. He commanded a frigate of fifty-six guns; the Revenge carried ninety-six. The struggle was unequal, but he defended his ship bravely, held out till nightfall and got away. When I returned to France Bonaparte was not yet in power, and I was refused a pension. When I applied again, quite lately, I was sternly informed that if the Baron de Rouville had emigrated I should not have lost him; that by this time he would have been a rear-admiral; finally, his Excellency quoted degrees of forfeiture. I took this step, urged by friends, only for Adelaide’s sake. I have always hated holding out my hand as a beggar in the name of a grief that deprives a woman of voice and strength. I do not like this money valuation for blood irreparably spilt—”

“Dear mother, this subject always does you harm.”

The Baronne Leseigneur bowed and was silent.

“Monsieur,” said the young girl, “I had supposed a painter’s work was generally fairly quiet?”

Schinner colored, remembering the noise he had made. Adelaide rose at the sound of a carriage stopping at the door. She went into her own room and returned with a pair of tall gilt candlesticks with partly burnt wax candles, which she quickly lit. Without waiting for the bell she opened the outer door where she set the lamp down. The sound of a kiss given and received touched Hippolyte’s heart. His impatience to see the man who treated Adelaide familiarly was not immediately gratified; the newcomers conversed in undertone with the young girl.

At last Mademoiselle de Rouville returned, followed by two men whose costumes and countenances needed a long tale. The first, about sixty, wore one of those coats fashioned for Louis XVIII.: neither civil nor military, it might pass for either. Fleurs-de-lis embroidered the lapels and gilt buttons bore the same. Shoulder straps begged for useless epaulettes; a buttonhole blossomed with many colored ribbons. He probably always carried his three-cornered hat with a gold cord, for his powdered hair showed no traces of pressure. He might be taken for fifty and seemed robust. His frank, loyal emigre expression hinted also at libertine habits, the reckless gallantry of the Musketeers. His gestures and manner showed no intention of renouncing royalism, religion, or love affairs.

A fantastic figure stood behind this specimen of “Louis XIV.’s light infantry”—the Bonapartists’ nickname for these venerable Monarchy survivors. He ought to have been the picture’s principal subject, but served only as an accessory. A lean, dry man dressed like the first, seeming his reflection or shadow: his coat older, his powder less white, the fleurs-de-lis dimmer, his shoulder-straps more hopeless and dog’s-eared; his intellect feebler, his life nearer its end. He realized Rivarol’s quip about Champcenetz, “He is the moonlight of me.” He was simply a paler double, the difference between a first and last impression of a lithograph.

The painter found this speechless old Chevalier a mystery. He did not speak; nobody spoke to him. Was he a friend, a poor relation, a companion who followed at the old gallant’s heels? Did he fill a place between dog, parrot, and friend? Had he saved his patron’s fortune, or only his life? Was he the Trim to a Captain Toby? At the Baronne de Rouville’s he provoked curiosity without satisfying it. Who after the Restoration could recall an attachment that, before the Revolution, had bound him to his friend’s wife, dead twenty years?

The leader, least dilapidated of these wrecks, gallantly kissed Madame de Rouville’s hand and sat down by her. The Chevalier bowed and placed himself a couple of chairs away from his model. Adelaide stood behind the old gentleman’s armchair, leaning her elbows on the back in an attitude unconsciously like Guerin’s depiction of Dido’s sister.

Though the gentleman’s familiarity was fatherly, his freedom seemed to annoy the girl. “What, are you sulky with me?” he said, then shot at Schinner one of those shrewd, diplomatic side-looks that ask, when they see a stranger, “Is he one of us?”

“This is our neighbor,” said the Baronne, pointing to Hippolyte. “Monsieur is a celebrated painter; his name must be known to you despite your indifference to the arts.”

The old man saw the intent in suppressing the name and bowed to the young man. “Certainly,” he said. “I heard much about his pictures at the last Salon. Talent has immense privileges,” he added, observing the artist’s red ribbon. “That distinction, which we must earn at the cost of our blood and long service, you win in your youth; but all glory is of the same kindred,” he said, laying his hand on his Cross of Saint-Louis.

Hippolyte murmured thanks and fell silent, satisfied to admire the beautiful girl’s head. He soon forgot the extreme misery of the dwelling; Adelaide’s face stood out against a luminous atmosphere. He answered questions briefly, his attention divided by that inward double consciousness when soul and ear attend different things. Hope poured out before him a thousand visions of happiness; he refused to analyze the evening’s incidents.

After a while the Baroness and her daughter began to play cards with the old gentleman. The Chevalier, faithful to his shadow role, stood behind his friend’s chair watching the game, answering mute inquiries with approving nods and repetition of gestures.

“Du Halga, I always lose,” said the gentleman.

“You discard badly,” replied the Baronne.

“For three months now I have never won a single game,” he said.

“Have you the aces?” asked the old lady.

“Yes, one more to mark,” he answered.

“Shall I come and advise you?” said Adelaide.

“No, no. Stay where I can see you. By Gad, it would be losing too much not to have you to look at!”

At last the game was over. The gentleman pulled out his purse and, throwing two louis-d’or on the table, not without temper, “Forty francs,” he exclaimed, “the exact sum.—Deuce take it! It is eleven o’clock.”

“It is eleven o’clock,” repeated the silent figure, looking at the painter.

Hippolyte, hearing this more distinctly, thought it time to retire. He found a few commonplace remarks to make, took leave of the Baroness, her daughter, and the two strangers, and went away wholly possessed by the first raptures of true love, without attempting to analyze the evening’s little incidents.

The next day the painter felt an ardent desire to see Adelaide again. He might have presented himself at six when he went to his studio, but waited until the afternoon. Blushing like a girl, he shyly asked Mademoiselle Leseigneur, who came to let him in, for the portrait of the Baron.

“But come in,” said Adelaide, who had no doubt heard him come down from the studio.

The painter entered, tongue-tied; happiness dulled his wit. To see Adelaide, hear the rustle of her skirt after longing all morning to be near her, was an intensity that made trifles precious. The old lady was not in the drawing-room. Adelaide brought a chair to stand on to take down the picture; finding she could not reach it without stepping on a chest of drawers, she turned to Hippolyte and, blushing, said, “I am not tall enough. Will you get it down?”

Her modesty was the true motive; he read this and returned one of those looks of intelligence that are the sweetest language of love. Adelaide cast down her eyes with maidenly reserve. Hippolyte, feeling almost timid, took down the picture, examined it in the window light, and then went away saying only, “I will return it soon.”

During that brief moment both felt a storm of agitation, as if a stone flung into a deep lake had set in motion long ripples in their hearts.

Hippolyte returned to his studio with the portrait. His easel awaited a fresh canvas; his palette set, brushes cleaned, the light chosen. He worked with the ardor artists throw into their whims until dinner, then returned that evening to the Baronne’s and stayed from nine till eleven. The same topics, the same arrivals, the same game, the same loss by the old man; only Hippolyte, a little bolder, ventured to chat with the young girl.

A week passed, and their feelings underwent the slow, delightful transformations that bring two souls to understanding. Every day Adelaide’s greeting grew more intimate, her voice more eager, her manner more familiar. They laughed and talked with the simplicity of children newly made friends, as if they had known one another for years. Schinner wished to be taught piquet; being a novice, he blundered and lost almost every game. Without a word of love the lovers knew they were everything to each other. Hippolyte enjoyed his power over his gentle little friend; Adelaide, timid and devoted, allowed many concessions, playing small caprices like a spoilt child. Familiarity with the old Count soon stopped: she perceived the painter’s melancholy from the abrupt tone of his words when the old man unceremoniously kissed her hands or throat.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur began to expect an account of the painter’s actions; she scolded him so effectually for absences that he gave up other friends and went nowhere else. Adelaide’s jealousy showed when she heard that sometimes at eleven he still had visits to pay in Paris’s brilliant drawing-rooms. She declared such a life bad for his health and argued passionately that a man who expended time and charms on several women at once could not love warmly. So the painter, driven by passion and the exactions of a girl in love, lived exclusively in the little apartment that enchanted him.

Never was there a purer or more ardent love. Both trusted and were delicate; their passion grew without the need for sacrifices. A spontaneous affinity made their union close. Two months after their first meeting their lives had become one: in the morning Adelaide could tell by footsteps that “He is there.” When Hippolyte went home to his mother at dinner he always looked in on his neighbors; in the evening he flew back with lover’s punctuality. Adelaide enjoyed the realization of the ideal she naturally dreamt of.

The old gentleman came less often; Hippolyte, who had been jealous, had taken his place at the green table and shared his constant ill-luck. Yet sometimes, amid his happiness, thought of Madame de Rouville’s desperate poverty haunted him. “Strange! twenty francs every evening?” he asked himself, and dared not confess his odious suspicions.

He spent two months on the portrait; when finished, varnished, and framed, he deemed it one of his best works. Madame de Rouville had not spoken of it again. Was this indifference or pride? He would not account for the silence. Joyfully plotting with Adelaide, he planned to hang it when the Baroness was out. One day, while her mother walked in the Tuileries, Adelaide went up to Hippolyte’s studio to see the portrait in its light. She stood speechless in ecstatic contemplation, all a woman’s feeling merged in admiration for the man she loved. The painter, uneasy, leaned forward; she held out her hand, unable to speak, and two tears fell. Hippolyte kissed her hand; for a minute they stood mute, longing to confess their love and not daring. The painter kept her hand in his; their hearts beat wildly. Overcome, Adelaide drew away and said simply, “You will make my mother very happy.”

“What, only your mother?” he asked.

“Oh, I am too happy.”

Frightened by the vehemence stirred in his heart, the painter bent his head. Both, sensing danger, went downstairs and hung the picture in its place. Hippolyte dined for the first time with the Baroness, who, overcome and in tears, embraced him.

That evening the old emigre, the Baron de Rouville’s comrade, paid a visit to announce he had just been promoted to vice-admiral; his land travels through Germany and Russia had been counted as naval campaigns. Seeing the portrait, he shook the painter’s hand and exclaimed, “By Gad! though my old hulk does not deserve to be perpetuated, I would gladly give five hundred pistoles to see myself as like as that is to my dear old Rouville.”

At this hint the Baroness looked at her young friend and smiled with sudden gratitude. Hippolyte suspected the admiral meant to offer the price for both portraits while paying for his own. His pride as an artist, and his jealousy, took offense; he replied, “Monsieur, if I were a portrait-painter I should not have done this one.” The admiral bit his lip and sat down to cards.

The painter stayed near Adelaide, who proposed a dozen hands of piquet, to which he agreed. As he played he noticed an unexpected eagerness in Madame de Rouville to win and finger the old gentleman’s gold. Evil suspicions troubled Hippolyte. Could the Baroness be living by gambling? Was she playing to pay a debt, to meet the rent? Why would this rich old man frequent a fourth-story room where he lost about forty francs each evening? Why had he been so familiar with Adelaide and then given up the rights he had acquired?

These reflections led him to watch the old man and the Baroness more closely. Their sidelong glances at Adelaide displeased him. “Am I being duped?” was his last, horrible thought. He resolved to stay after the departure of the two old men to confirm or dispel his suspicions. He drew out his purse to pay and, carried away by thought, laid it on the table and fell into a brief reverie; then, ashamed of his silence, he rose, answered some commonplace question, and went close to the Baroness to examine her withered features as she talked.

He left, racked by doubts. A few steps down, he turned back to fetch the forgotten purse. “I left my purse here!” he said to Adelaide.

“No,” she answered, reddening.

“I thought it was there,” he said, pointing to the card-table. Not finding it, shamed for Adelaide and the Baroness, he looked at them with blank amazement that made them laugh; he checked himself, felt his waistcoat, and said, “I must have made a mistake. I have it somewhere no doubt.”

In the purse were fifteen louis d’or and some small change. The theft seemed flagrant and denied with effrontery; Hippolyte no longer felt doubt about his neighbors’ morals. He stood on the stairs, legs shaking, dizzy, in a cold sweat, unable to walk, struggling with the shock of his destroyed hopes. He recalled various trifling observations that now corroborated his frightful suspicions and opened his eyes to the character and life of these two women.

Had they waited until the portrait was given them before robbing him? If so, the theft was more odious. He remembered Adelaide’s seeming curiosity at the worn silk netting—might she have been counting coins in his purse while making innocent jests to watch for a moment to steal? He briefly fancied other motives—perhaps the admiral hesitated to marry Adelaide—but checked himself with the thought that if the Baroness hoped to make him marry her daughter, they would not have robbed him.

Clinging to his illusion and the love already rooted, he tried to find an accident: “The purse must have fallen on the floor,” he told himself. He searched himself hastily but did not find it. His memory retraced the truth: he distinctly saw the purse on the green cloth. Even so, he excused Adelaide, thinking misfortune might explain a degrading action; there must be some secret. He refused to admit that that proud face could be a lie.

The next morning he had not slept. The heartache that follows the loss of unrealized bliss made rapid inroads. To lose the future we dreamed of, to renounce it, is a keener pang than losing known happiness. Hope can be better than memory. His spirit was plunged into a shoreless sea; such partial death can lead, in delicate natures, to ruin. Thus Hippolyte wandered under the Tuileries’ fresh shade, absorbed and forgetful.

There he met Francois Souchet, a school-fellow and studio-mate about to set out for Italy. “Why, Hippolyte, what ails you?” asked the sculptor.

“I am most unhappy,” replied Hippolyte gravely.

“Nothing but a love affair can cause you grief. Money, glory, respect—you lack nothing.”

Led imperceptibly into confidences, Hippolyte confessed his love. When Souchet heard “Rue de Suresnes, a young girl on the fourth floor,” he laughed and began to recall what all the young men knew: the mother’s appearance and the air that betrayed her station. “Do you not know her by the way she holds her bag?” he said. Several acquaintances joined; jokes and mockery, lively but painful, followed. Hippolyte’s reticence made him suffer as his heart’s secret was handled so carelessly. He pretended contradiction and asked for proofs, but the jesting resumed. “Come at four o’clock and study their walk,” Souchet advised. Torn by conflicting feelings, Hippolyte parted from his friends, still convinced Adelaide must be superior to such accusations and filled with remorse for suspecting her. He passed the door of their rooms, feeling a pain none could misread. His love was such that, despite the theft, he still worshiped her. He thought of playing the good genius who rescues a woman whose love others have abandoned—romantic and grand to a highly-strung soul.

He sat in his studio, gazed at his picture without working, tears in his eyes, brush in hand but not touching. Night fell; roused by darkness, he went downstairs, met the old admiral, looked darkly at him as he bowed, and fled. He had planned to see the ladies, but the admiral’s presence froze him. For the hundredth time he wondered what interest brought this prodigal, with eighty thousand francs a year, to a fourth story where he lost forty francs every evening; he imagined motives.

In the next days Hippolyte threw himself into his work to conquer passion by idea and composition. Study consoled him, though memories of tender hours with Adelaide persisted. One evening, leaving his studio, he saw the door of the ladies’ rooms half open and Adelaide standing in the window recess. The door and staircase made it impossible not to be seen. He bowed coldly, with a glance of supreme indifference; yet thinking of the pain such coldness would inflict, he shuddered inwardly. That week of disdain would have been terrible; perhaps the purse had been found and she had sought him nightly. This thought filled him with remorse; he asked whether the signs of attachment Adelaide showed did not deserve inquiry and at least justification. Ashamed of resisting his heart for a week, he called that evening on Madame de Rouville.

All his suspicions vanished at the sight of Adelaide, pale and thin. “Good heavens! what is the matter?” he asked, after greeting the Baroness.

Adelaide made no reply, but gave him a look of deep melancholy that pained him. “You must have thought us indifferent?” said the Baroness.

“I was wrong,” he replied. “There are forms of pain we know not how to confide to any one.”

“The sincerity and strength of friendship are not to be measured by time,” the Baroness observed sadly.

“What ails you?” he asked Adelaide.

“Oh, nothing,” said the Baroness, explaining Adelaide had sat up late finishing some woman’s work and would not listen when told a day more or less did not matter.

Hippolyte, blushing with shame at his suspicions, ascribed the loss of his purse to some accident. The evening was delicious to both. Adelaide could read his thoughts; though he could not confess his wrongs, he had come back more in love and affectionate, seeking tacit forgiveness. Yet at Madame de Rouville’s suggestion to play—“Let us have our little game; my old friend Kergarouet will not let me off”—his fears revived. He sat at the card-table; Adelaide said he needed a partner because he did not know piquet.

Throughout the game the Baroness and her daughter exchanged looks of intelligence, alarming Hippolyte more because he was winning. At last a final hand left the lovers in the old lady’s debt. Reaching for some money he saw before him a purse Adelaide had slipped in front of him without his noticing it; the poor child held the old one in her hand and was, to keep her countenance, looking into it for the money to pay her mother. The sight made the blood rush to his heart; he was near fainting.

The substituted purse contained his fifteen gold louis. It was worked with gilt beads; rings and tassels betrayed Adelaide’s taste and the small hoard she had spent on it. It was an impossible, delicate way to show the painter that his gift could only be repaid by some proof of affection.

Overcome, Hippolyte turned to Adelaide and her mother, saw their trembling pleasure, felt mean and foolish; tears rose. He sprang up, clasped Adelaide, pressed her to his heart, and stole a kiss; then, with the simple heartiness of an artist, “I ask for her for my wife!” he exclaimed, looking to the Baroness.

Adelaide looked half-wrathful, and Madame de Rouville, somewhat astonished, considered her reply when a bell rang. The old vice-admiral entered, followed by his shadow and Madame Schinner. Alarmed by calumnies against Adelaide, Hippolyte’s mother had inquired and reported them to the admiral, who declared he would crop all the scoundrels’ ears for them.

Prompted by his wrath, he told Madame Schinner his secret of losing intentionally at cards, because the Baroness’s pride left him no means but these ingenious ones to help her.

After Madame Schinner had paid respects to Madame de Rouville, the Baroness looked at the Comte de Kergarouet, the Chevalier du Halga—friend of the departed Comtesse de Kergarouet—Hippolyte, and Adelaide, and said with a gracious, heartfelt tone, “So we are a family party this evening.”

PARIS, May 1832
