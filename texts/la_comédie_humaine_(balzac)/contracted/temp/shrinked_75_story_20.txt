**Chapter I: Pro and Con**

Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman gentleman, married a wealthy heiress from Bordeaux and became a Gascon after selling his estate. He served as major of the Gate Guards until his death in 1813, having avoided the Revolution by going to Martinique. Upon his return, he found his estate well-managed by Mathias, a notary’s clerk who embraced new ideas. After Madame de Manerville's death in 1810, Monsieur de Manerville became increasingly miserly, depriving his only son, Paul, of resources.

Paul de Manerville, returning from college in 1810, lived under his father's strict control for three years. This oppressive environment stunted his emotional growth, making him timid and unable to assert himself. He felt trapped in his father's old mansion, unable to socialize with peers due to financial constraints. His father took him to royalist gatherings, where Paul felt out of place among the aging nobility. To cope, he engaged in physical activities like fencing and horseback riding, while his intellectual pursuits were limited to novels.

After his father's death, Paul inherited a substantial fortune and estates but was disillusioned with Bordeaux and Lanstrac. He spent six years traveling Europe, eventually exhausting his capital and relying on his estate's revenues. Realizing he needed to settle down, Paul decided to return to Bordeaux, manage his affairs, and marry, believing that his noble title would attract a suitable partner.

Paul, now a count, recognized the value of marriage in society. He had gained worldly knowledge through his experiences, which he believed would enhance his prospects. Despite his extravagant lifestyle, he maintained a reputation for integrity and elegance, avoiding the pitfalls of gambling and deceit.

One morning, Paul confided in his friend de Marsay about his desire to marry and live as a country gentleman. De Marsay, however, warned him against marriage, arguing that it would lead to a loss of freedom and happiness. He criticized the institution, suggesting that marriage was a burden primarily benefiting children, and questioned whether Paul truly wanted to take on such responsibilities.

De Marsay painted a bleak picture of marriage, emphasizing the societal expectations and potential for ridicule that come with it. He advised Paul to enjoy his bachelorhood and consider marrying later in life, perhaps a widow, to avoid the complications of a young bride.

Paul defended his desire for a family, expressing a longing for companionship and stability. He envisioned a life where he could share his joys and secrets with a wife, rejecting de Marsay's cynical view of marriage. He believed he had the courage to be a good husband and father, seeking a deep connection with a partner.

De Marsay remained skeptical, arguing that Paul’s romanticized view of marriage was naive. He warned that the dynamics of love and attraction change once a couple is married, often leading to disappointment. He cautioned Paul about the legal and emotional complexities of marriage, suggesting that a husband must exert control over his wife, which Paul might not be capable of.

Despite de Marsay's warnings, Paul remained resolute in his desire for a family, dismissing the notion that he would become a mere shadow of himself in marriage. He expressed a yearning for a life filled with emotional connections, rather than the superficiality he associated with bachelorhood.

De Marsay continued to challenge Paul’s perspective, arguing that the realities of marriage often clash with the ideals of love. He suggested that Paul’s experiences with women might lead to misconceptions about marital relationships, where the initial excitement can fade into disillusionment.

Ultimately, Paul’s determination to marry stemmed from a desire for genuine companionship and a stable family life, contrasting sharply with de Marsay’s cynical outlook on marriage. The conversation highlighted the tension between societal expectations and personal desires, as Paul sought to navigate the complexities of love and commitment in a changing world.

**CHAPTER II. THE PINK OF FASHION**

Despite the impact of recent conversations, Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux in the winter of 1821, determined to restore his family mansion and maintain his reputation for elegance. His connections within the royalist society of Bordeaux quickly elevated him to a position of fashionable prominence, earning him the nickname “the pink of fashion.” His charm, manners, and Parisian sophistication captivated the local elite, and he navigated social interactions with a blend of respect and ease.

Paul de Manerville’s success in provincial society was marked by his attention to personal appearance and a gentle demeanor, which endeared him to both men and women. However, he soon realized that marriage was essential for a fulfilling life in the provinces. Although he was too young to engage in the mundane pursuits of provincial life, he felt a deep longing for companionship, particularly for Mademoiselle Evangelista, the celebrated beauty of Bordeaux.

Mademoiselle Evangelista was the daughter of a wealthy Spanish merchant who had established himself in Bordeaux. After her father's death, Madame Evangelista maintained a lavish lifestyle, raising her daughter in luxury. By the time Paul returned, Natalie was a stunning young woman, yet rumors about her extravagant upbringing made potential suitors hesitant. The prevailing sentiment was that only a prince could afford to marry her, which further complicated her prospects.

When Paul and Natalie first met, they were immediately drawn to each other, each secretly envisioning a future together. Paul was aware of the Evangelista wealth and felt no need for inquiries that would typically deter a suitor. He dismissed the warnings of others regarding the family's extravagance, confident in his ability to manage the financial implications of marrying Natalie.

As Paul frequented the Evangelista household, he became increasingly enchanted by both mother and daughter. Madame Evangelista, at forty, was still strikingly beautiful and possessed a commanding presence. She was adept at navigating social circles and had ambitions for her daughter to rise in Parisian society. Paul, appreciating the warmth and charm of the Evangelistas, found himself spending more time with them, leading society to speculate about his intentions toward Natalie.

Despite the growing attention, neither Paul nor Natalie openly acknowledged their feelings. Natalie maintained a reserved demeanor, which intrigued Paul, while Madame Evangelista subtly encouraged their connection. Paul began to see marriage as a means to secure his social standing and fulfill his ambitions, believing that wealth would mitigate the challenges of married life.

As their relationship developed, Paul idealized Natalie, overlooking potential flaws in her character. He was captivated by her beauty and charm, failing to recognize the complexities that might arise in their future together. The societal pressures and expectations surrounding their union added to the allure, as both families seemed to endorse the match.

However, beneath the surface, Natalie’s upbringing and her mother’s ambitions posed potential challenges. While Paul was drawn to the idea of a life with Natalie, he remained unaware of the deeper dynamics at play, including the influence of Madame Evangelista’s past and her desire for power through her daughter's marriage.

As Paul contemplated the prospect of marriage, he grappled with the realities of commitment and the potential for conflict. He was caught between his romantic ideals and the practicalities of a life intertwined with Natalie and her mother. The narrative foreshadows the complexities of their impending union, highlighting the societal expectations and personal ambitions that would shape their lives.

Ultimately, the chapter sets the stage for the exploration of marriage as a social contract, revealing the intricate dance of desires, ambitions, and the often-unseen challenges that accompany the pursuit of love and status in a society governed by appearances and expectations.

**CHAPTER III. THE MARRIAGE CONTRACT--FIRST DAY**

In the winter of 1822, Paul de Manerville sought the hand of Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista through his great-aunt, Baronne de Maulincour. The baroness stayed longer than usual in Medoc to assist Paul in this endeavor. After discussing the matter with Madame Evangelista, she returned to inform Paul that the marriage was likely to succeed, but warned him about the dowry situation: Natalie’s fortune was tied to her father’s patrimony, and her mother would not contribute anything of her own.

The baroness advised Paul to consult their notary, Maitre Mathias, to ensure the marriage contract was properly drawn up, emphasizing the importance of protecting his interests for the sake of future children. This left Paul anxious and confused about the implications of the marriage contract and whether he needed to defend his interests against potential deceit from his future mother-in-law.

Following his aunt's advice, Paul approached Madame Evangelista to discuss the marriage contract. She suggested that both families’ notaries should meet to negotiate the terms. Paul, feeling nervous, agreed, relieved that the notaries would handle the details.

Madame Evangelista, however, was under pressure as she owed her daughter a significant sum from her late husband’s estate, which she could not pay. She feared that if Paul learned the truth, he might withdraw his proposal, jeopardizing Natalie’s future. This led her to resolve to manipulate the situation to her advantage.

The next day, she summoned her notary, Maitre Solonet, who was ambitious and eager to please. He assured her that they could navigate the marriage negotiations favorably. Madame Evangelista expressed her desire to maintain control over her daughter’s fortune while ensuring a good match for Natalie.

As the evening approached, both notaries met to discuss the marriage contract. Solonet proposed terms that would benefit Madame Evangelista while Mathias insisted on protecting Paul’s interests. The discussions revealed the stark differences in their approaches: Mathias represented traditional values, while Solonet embodied modern, opportunistic strategies.

During the dinner, Paul and Natalie shared tender moments, but the underlying tension regarding the marriage contract loomed. Paul was enamored with Natalie, but Mathias warned him about the financial implications of marrying into a family that had already squandered much of its wealth.

As the negotiations continued, Mathias expressed concern over the potential ruin of Paul’s fortune if he accepted unfavorable terms. He urged Paul to be cautious, but Paul, blinded by love, was eager to proceed with the marriage.

Madame Evangelista, sensing the precariousness of her position, attempted to reassure Paul of her intentions. However, the discussions became heated, revealing the underlying tensions and distrust between the parties involved. 

Ultimately, a compromise was reached where Madame Evangelista would secure her daughter’s inheritance while also ensuring her own financial stability. The notaries agreed on terms that would allow for the creation of an entail on the family estate, which would protect Paul’s interests and provide for future generations.

As the evening concluded, both families felt a sense of relief, believing they had successfully navigated the complexities of the marriage contract. However, the seeds of distrust had been sown, foreshadowing potential conflicts in the future. 

In the end, all parties left the negotiations feeling satisfied, but the underlying tensions hinted at the challenges that lay ahead in their intertwined lives.

**CHAPTER IV. THE MARRIAGE CONTRACT--SECOND DAY**

The following day, Elie Magus, a jeweler in Bordeaux, was summoned by Madame Evangelista, expecting to discuss jewelry for the upcoming marriage of Comte Paul and Mademoiselle Natalie. Instead, he was asked to appraise the value of her mother’s property, including her diamonds, which were integral to the marriage contract. Magus valued Madame Evangelista's necklace and earrings at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” diamond at 100,000 francs. However, he offered only 75,000 francs for the diamond and 160,000 for the jewelry if sold, explaining that the value of diamonds decreases when they are owned rather than sold.

This conversation sparked rumors among the household staff and the wider community, leading to speculation about the marriage's stability. Gossip suggested that Madame Evangelista was selling her house and that the marriage might not happen. Despite the notaries denying these claims, public opinion remained skeptical, fueled by jealousy from those who envied the Evangelista family's wealth and status.

As the rumors spread, Paul organized a hunting party, which further fueled speculation about the marriage's future. Madame de Gyas, eager to confirm the gossip, visited Natalie and her mother, feigning concern while relaying the circulating rumors. Madame Evangelista, aware of the motivations behind the gossip, dismissed the concerns, asserting that the marriage contract was a private matter.

In the lead-up to the wedding, Madame Evangelista planned an extravagant celebration called the "fete of the camellias," intending to showcase her wealth and counter the rumors. Preparations took over a month, during which the legalities of the marriage contract were finalized, and the community buzzed with anticipation.

On the day of the contract signing, Madame Evangelista experienced a moment of realization regarding the implications of the contract. She recalled her earlier discussions with the notaries and feared that her daughter's fortune might be compromised. Despite her initial confidence, she resolved to demand clarity on the contract's terms.

The lavish preparations for the fete included transforming the courtyard into a tent filled with camellias, creating an atmosphere of opulence. As guests arrived, the excitement heightened, but the signing of the contract loomed ominously. Paul and Natalie, blissfully unaware of the underlying tensions, engaged in playful banter while the notaries read the contract details.

Madame Evangelista listened intently, but when the entailment clause was explained, she realized the potential financial ruin for her daughter. The entail would limit Natalie’s inheritance, leaving her vulnerable should Paul die first. This revelation struck Madame Evangelista deeply, leading her to conclude that her daughter was at risk of losing her fortune.

In a moment of despair, she recognized that breaking off the marriage was no longer an option; the public nature of the preparations and the expectations of both families made it impossible. She felt trapped, realizing that her earlier manipulations had backfired, and now her daughter’s future was at stake.

As the contract was finalized, Madame Evangelista's emotions boiled over. She confronted the notaries, expressing her anger and frustration at the situation. Solonet, one of the notaries, suggested that they could delay the signing, but Madame Evangelista felt that doing so would only worsen their reputation.

Despite her internal turmoil, she ultimately decided to proceed with the marriage, vowing to protect her daughter’s interests. In a moment of clarity, she resolved to regain control over her daughter’s fortune, planning to invest wisely and ensure Natalie’s security.

The signing of the contract marked a turning point, with Madame Evangelista determined to navigate the complexities of her new reality. As the festivities began, she felt a mix of triumph and resentment, knowing that her daughter’s happiness was intertwined with her own ambitions and fears. The night unfolded with dancing and celebration, but beneath the surface, tensions simmered, setting the stage for future conflicts.

**CHAPTER V. THE MARRIAGE CONTRACT--THIRD DAY**

Despite the festive atmosphere surrounding his upcoming marriage, Paul found himself troubled by doubts when alone. He questioned whether his mother-in-law, Madame Evangelista, might deceive him, but reassured himself that their financial arrangements were clear and that he would soon marry Natalie. However, he resolved to remain vigilant, considering Mathias's warnings.

Unbeknownst to Paul, his future had shifted dramatically. His mother-in-law, once a supportive figure, was now a potential adversary, contemplating how to withdraw her interests from their shared life. Paul failed to recognize the complexities of Madame Evangelista's Creole nature, which combined intellect, passionate volatility, and a certain indifference. This nature, dormant during her years of marital happiness, was now awakening as she faced the impending loss of her daughter.

The next day, Madame Evangelista sat with Natalie, reminiscing about their harmonious life together. She expressed sorrow over Natalie’s impending marriage, warning her that love could quickly fade if not carefully managed. She advised Natalie to establish her authority as a wife from the start, emphasizing the importance of appearing to obey Paul while subtly maintaining control.

Natalie, initially resistant, was persuaded by her mother’s wisdom. Madame Evangelista cautioned her against the dangers of constant proximity in marriage, suggesting that social engagements would help maintain her allure and keep Paul interested. She stressed that a woman must never fully surrender her power, advising Natalie to cultivate a sense of mystery and reserve.

As they prepared for the wedding, Madame Evangelista revealed her intention to sell her jewelry to support her daughter, proposing an exchange that would benefit Natalie. Paul, moved by his mother-in-law's sacrifice, agreed to the arrangement, unaware of the underlying tensions.

When Paul arrived, he appeared troubled, prompting Madame Evangelista to suspect that Mathias had influenced him. She proposed selling her jewelry to ensure Natalie had valuable pieces, emphasizing the importance of a woman's vanity in maintaining happiness. Paul accepted, and they discussed the sale of their property, with Natalie showing signs of understanding business matters.

Madame Evangelista, determined to step back from their lives, insisted that her presence might hinder their marriage. She expressed a desire to manage her own affairs at Lanstrac, believing that her absence would allow Natalie and Paul to bond without her influence. Paul, relieved by this arrangement, felt a sense of freedom.

As the wedding approached, Natalie felt anxious about her new life without her mother. Despite her fears, she was determined to be a good wife. The ceremony took place at midnight, a time often associated with ominous omens. Natalie, now separated from her mother, was filled with doubt and apprehension about her future.

The guests noted the unusual circumstances of the wedding, with some criticizing Paul for excluding Madame Evangelista. They speculated about the implications of such a decision, suggesting it might invite trouble into their marriage. Meanwhile, Madame Evangelista, having played her part, left Natalie with a final piece of advice: to be a wife, not a mistress.

After the ceremony, she feigned despair to extract promises from Paul, ensuring he would remain committed to Natalie. As they departed for Paris, Natalie signaled to her mother that Paul had won the initial battle, but she was already beginning to assert her influence over him.

**CHAPTER VI. CONCLUSION**

Five years later, in November, Comte Paul de Manerville arrived at the home of his old friend, Monsieur Mathias, in Bordeaux. Mathias, now retired from his notary practice, was absent but his housekeeper led Paul to the room of the late Madame Mathias. Exhausted from his journey, Paul slept until evening. When Mathias returned, he found Paul looking changed but calm. They shared a meal, during which Mathias expressed his sorrow over the public notices of Paul's financial troubles, which he had nurtured for years.

Mathias lamented the loss of Paul's estate, which had been in the family for generations. He was particularly distressed by the public nature of the seizures, feeling personally shamed by the announcements. Paul revealed that he had left his wife, Natalie, without her knowledge, and was sailing to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune under the name Camille. Mathias was shocked at Paul's decision to enter commerce, believing it beneath his dignity.

Paul explained that he had incurred massive debts, largely due to lavish spending and the costs associated with his marriage. He had given Natalie expensive gifts and had to sell her investments to maintain their lifestyle. Mathias was incredulous at the scale of Paul's debts, which amounted to over a million and a half francs. Paul insisted that he could not burden Natalie with their financial troubles and preferred to separate their assets.

Mathias suggested that Paul could live off Natalie's income while recovering, but Paul rejected this idea, preferring to work for his fortune rather than rely on her. He believed that women should not be involved in financial matters. Mathias, however, felt that a wife should share both the joys and burdens of marriage.

The next day, Mathias received a bill of exchange for 150,000 francs from de Marsay, a friend of Paul’s. Despite Mathias's concerns about Paul's plans, he ultimately accepted Paul's determination to leave for India. The ship, "Belle-Amelie," was set to sail, and Mathias accompanied Paul to the quay, where they were met with curiosity and gossip about Paul's downfall.

As Paul prepared to board, Mathias signaled him with a handkerchief, indicating he had important letters. However, the sailor delivering the letters did not convey the urgency of the message. Paul, wanting to avoid being swayed by their contents, pocketed the letters unread.

Once at sea, Paul reflected on his life and the choices that led him to this point. He felt a mix of grief and uncertainty about leaving Natalie behind. Overcome by seasickness, he spent days in his cabin, contemplating his situation. Eventually, he opened the letters from Natalie and de Marsay.

Natalie's letter expressed her anguish over Paul's departure and her desire to support him. She felt betrayed by his decision to leave without consulting her and vowed to economize and pay off his debts. She also revealed that she believed she was pregnant, which filled her with hope for their future.

In contrast, de Marsay's letter detailed the extent of Paul's financial ruin, revealing that Natalie's mother had orchestrated much of the situation. De Marsay warned Paul of the conspiracy against him and urged him to return to Paris instead of going to India. He suggested that Paul could regain his honor by confronting his rivals and reclaiming his position.

As Paul absorbed the harsh realities of his situation, he felt a mix of anger and despair. He questioned what he had done to deserve such betrayal and turmoil. Ultimately, he resolved to confront his challenges head-on, determined to rebuild his life and restore his honor.
