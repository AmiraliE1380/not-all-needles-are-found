Railroads threaten to make certain transportation services around Paris obsolete. The Touchards, a father-son team, previously dominated travel within a 45-mile radius but faced competition from local services, particularly on shorter routes. In 1822, they contended with three rivals on the lucrative Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise route, despite extending their service to Chambly.

The Isle-Adam route had less competition, with only two coaches operating smoothly. Pierrotin, a popular coachman, coordinated schedules with his rival to benefit customers from the “Lion d’Argent” inn, managed by the innkeeper’s wife.

On a Saturday morning in 1822, Pierrotin awaited passengers outside the inn, needing to raise a thousand francs for a new coach due to financial strain. A footman approached, seeking a ride for the Comte de Serizy, who wished to travel incognito. This chapter highlights themes of ambition and social dynamics in early 19th-century France.

The count, a prominent figure with a complex marriage, faced issues regarding a farm encroaching on his estate. He planned to invite the owner, Margueron, to dinner to finalize the sale, unaware of conspiracies against him. Meanwhile, Pierrotin encountered Madame Clapart and her son Oscar, who struggled with feelings of inadequacy as they prepared for their journey, which included the disguised Comte de Serizy.

After meeting Maitre Desroches, Oscar lived a disciplined life preparing for law school, facing rigorous examinations while longing for freedom. In November 1825, after passing his exams, he began working as a clerk, participating in lighthearted office pranks amidst societal expectations.

The next day, Georges Marest, now head-clerk of notary Hannequin, invited Oscar and his colleagues to a lavish gathering at the Rocher de Cancale. Oscar's superior, Desroches, tasked him with retrieving a crucial legal document and warned him about Derville, who might hinder their efforts. Eager to prove himself, Oscar accepted.

At the event, the clerks were excited to mingle with high society. The Marquise was actually Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, a successful danseuse. At the Rocher de Cancale, Oscar met various guests and became intoxicated. He lost significant money in a game of vingt-et-un, including five hundred francs from Desroches, and accepted a thousand francs from Florentine to recover but lost even more.

Awakened by his uncle Cardot, who scolded him for his gambling, Oscar begged for help and received five hundred francs, with a warning it would be the last time. Desroches decided to dismiss him. Feeling the weight of his mistakes, Oscar resolved to change and enlisted in the cavalry. He excelled, earning honors and maturing into a respectable man, eventually marrying Georgette Pierrotin and becoming a collector, reflecting on his past lessons.
