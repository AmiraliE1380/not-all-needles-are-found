In October 1800, Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and young daughter, their sadness stemming from shared misery rather than love. Bartolomeo, an older man in tattered clothes, had a serious demeanor, while his distressed wife tried to calm their weary daughter, who still exhibited Italian grace.

As they lingered, Bartolomeo's intense gaze drew attention, but his fierce demeanor deterred sympathy. After contemplation, he made a desperate decision, handing a dagger to his wife and declaring his intention to confront the Bonapartes. He approached the palace but was stopped by a soldier, who refused him entry without an audience request, leading to a tense standoff.

Fortunately, Lucien Bonaparte recognized Bartolomeo and took him to see Napoleon, who was meeting with Murat, Lannes, and Rapp. After dismissing the others, Napoleon asked Bartolomeo why he had come. Bartolomeo revealed that he had killed the Portas, a family responsible for his son’s death and the destruction of his home after they set fire to his vineyard.

Surprised, Napoleon questioned Bartolomeo's motives. Bartolomeo expressed despair over the Bonaparte brothers' lack of support, feeling they had forgotten their Corsican roots. Napoleon explained he could not protect Bartolomeo but offered him a chance to live in Paris, away from vendetta culture.

Bartolomeo accepted, pledging loyalty to the Bonaparte family and expressing gratitude for their recognition of his plight. Napoleon promised financial assistance, emphasizing the importance of adhering to the law in Paris, where personal justice was not tolerated. Fifteen years later, Bartolomeo's life in Paris set the stage for future events intertwined with themes of vengeance and familial loyalty.

Servin, a distinguished artist, established a studio for young girls interested in painting. At around forty, he was dedicated to his art and married to a general's daughter. Initially, mothers brought their daughters to the studio, but soon they felt comfortable sending them alone, reassured by Servin's integrity and quality instruction.

To maintain a harmonious class composition, Servin only accepted pupils from wealthy families, even refusing aspiring professional artists. His careful approach and assurance that only well-bred girls attended his classes earned him a stellar reputation, making him synonymous with feminine art education.

The studio was meticulously arranged, with a walled-off entrance leading to a spacious attic filled with light from large glass panes. The walls were adorned with caricatures and models, creating an atmosphere that blended artistic chaos with elegance. On a bright July day, the studio buzzed with activity as young girls painted, laughed, and formed distinct cliques based on social status.

Among them was Ginevra Piombo, the most beautiful and modest girl, who sat apart from her peers. The aristocratic group, led by Amelie Thirion, often looked down on the wealthier but less refined girls from the banking sector. Tensions simmered as Amelie orchestrated a slight against Ginevra, moving her easel away from its usual spot, which sparked whispers of jealousy and rivalry.

Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, faced ostracism from the royalist girls, influenced by the political climate following the Bourbons' return. Despite her beauty and talent, she became a target of jealousy, particularly from Amelie, who sought to undermine her.

When Ginevra arrived, she was met with silence, a stark contrast to the usual camaraderie. Her presence commanded attention, yet she remained oblivious to the tension. As she settled into her work, she sensed a mysterious presence behind a partition—a man hidden away, breathing quietly. This revelation filled her with curiosity and concern for his safety.

Ginevra's indifference to the slight against her only fueled her peers' animosity, perplexed by her calm demeanor. Meanwhile, Amelie and her group attempted to humiliate Ginevra further, but their efforts were thwarted by her apparent indifference.

Madame Servin's arrival interrupted the brewing conflict, announcing that her husband could not attend that day. Ginevra and Madame Servin exchanged glances, both aware of the hidden man. As the session continued, Ginevra focused on sketching the officer's head, inspired by the urgency of the moment.

Servin, noticing her talent, praised her work, drawing the attention of the other girls, especially Amelie, who was determined to uncover Ginevra's secret. The atmosphere shifted as Ginevra's skill became evident, and Servin's admiration heightened the tension.

As the day progressed, Ginevra and Servin shared a private conversation about the hidden officer, revealing the political stakes involved. Ginevra offered her assistance, demonstrating her commitment to protecting the man and her loyalty to Servin.

The dynamics in the studio shifted as the girls became increasingly aware of Ginevra's focus and the mystery surrounding her. Amelie, driven by jealousy, attempted to spy on Ginevra, but the latter remained resolute in her purpose, determined to keep the officer safe while navigating the complexities of her relationships with her peers.

In a dim garret, painter Servin and Ginevra were startled by a knock, revealing a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, his arm in a sling and face pale from pain. He recoiled upon seeing Ginevra, but Servin introduced her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, Baron di Piombo. The officer, visibly shaken, revealed he was wounded and horrified by Labedoyere's death sentence, collapsing onto a stool.

He expressed despair over his cowardice for hiding while Labedoyere faced execution. Ginevra urged him to consider his life and offered her savings, insisting it was merely money from the Emperor’s generosity. Her words inspired hope in the officer, named Luigi, who admired her beauty and kindness but remained troubled by Labedoyere’s fate.

As they spoke, Ginevra felt a deep connection to Luigi. They shared a moment of silence, realizing their bond had grown quickly. Servin asked if Luigi could save Labedoyere, to which he replied he could only avenge him. Ginevra, moved by his passion, felt emotions she had never experienced before.

The next day, Ginevra returned to the studio, where Luigi recounted his harrowing experiences during the retreat from Moscow and the disaster at Waterloo. Ginevra listened intently, captivated by his stories. Their friendship blossomed, and they devised a secret communication system through Corsican songs.

However, rumors began to circulate among Ginevra’s classmates about her relationship with Luigi, leading to their gradual departure from the studio. Ginevra remained oblivious to the gossip, focused solely on her feelings for Luigi. Despite her encouragement for him to submit to royal authority for safety, he refused, remaining loyal to his beliefs.

One evening, Ginevra learned from her friend Laure that the other girls had left due to rumors about her and Luigi. Laure’s mother insisted she stop attending the studio, believing Ginevra was involved in an inappropriate relationship. Shocked, Ginevra remained steadfast in her feelings.

Servin entered the studio, unaware of the turmoil. Ginevra confided in him about the rumors, and he dismissed them, valuing their bond over societal judgment. Luigi, overhearing their conversation, felt guilty for the trouble he had caused Servin.

As the three shared a moment of camaraderie, Ginevra and Luigi’s love became evident, though unspoken. Servin playfully suggested they marry, and the couple exchanged silent affirmations of their feelings. Ginevra proposed to help Luigi secure a pardon, leveraging her father’s influence.

Meanwhile, Ginevra’s parents grew anxious over her late return home. Bartolomeo di Piombo, her father, was a stern yet loving figure known for his integrity and loyalty to Napoleon. When Ginevra finally arrived, he carried her in his arms, showcasing their protective family dynamic. This chapter explores themes of loyalty, love, and societal judgment through the relationships between Ginevra, Luigi, and their families, setting the stage for the unfolding drama surrounding their fates.

**CHAPTER IV. LOVE**

“Here she is, my Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed, embracing his daughter. Ginevra, startled, reassured her mother with a nod. The baroness's initial fright faded, and Piombo's joy was evident as he rubbed his hands, a habit from his court days. They seemed like a revived plant after a long drought.

“Now, to dinner!” Piombo called, referring to Ginevra as “Signora Piombellina.” As they sat down, he noted her long hours at the studio, suggesting painting had become more important than family. Ginevra, however, was preoccupied and eventually confessed, “I love a young man.”

Her parents were taken aback. When asked about him, she revealed he was without fortune and a comrade of Labedoyere, who had been proscribed. Piombo reacted with disbelief, insisting that Ginevra should love no one but him. She countered, asserting that love was not a choice.

“I thought you would remain faithful to me,” Piombo lamented, but Ginevra reminded him of his own absences for political missions. “Life has necessities,” she defended her feelings.

Piombo’s anger flared as he struggled with the idea of losing his daughter to another man. Ginevra pleaded for understanding, promising her love would not diminish her affection for him. “You will learn to know the man to whom you bequeath me,” she insisted.

Piombo could not accept this. “If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me,” he declared, his rage palpable. Ginevra maintained that her love was pure and sincere.

The tension escalated, with Piombo declaring he would never allow the marriage. Ginevra stood firm, stating, “I am Ginevra Piombo, and I declare that within six months I shall be the wife of Luigi Porta.”

Piombo’s fury intensified, warning her of the consequences of marrying a Porta, the family responsible for her brother’s death. Ginevra argued that Luigi was innocent, having been just a child during the vendetta.

Despite her father’s threats, Ginevra remained resolute. “I will be his wife,” she asserted, leading to a confrontation where Piombo, in a fit of rage, nearly attacked her but ultimately hesitated.

The next day, Ginevra found herself barred from leaving the house. She communicated with Luigi through a chambermaid, and their love deepened despite the growing rift with her father.

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother attempted to mediate, but the arrival of two notaries to discuss her marriage to Luigi escalated tensions. Piombo confronted the notaries, who were merely doing their duty.

In desperation, Ginevra knelt before her father, pleading for his consent. “Give me Luigi, or I die!” she cried, but Piombo, overwhelmed, rejected her. “You have no father,” he declared, casting her out.

Ginevra entered Luigi’s modest lodging, declaring, “My father and my mother have cast me off.” Luigi promised to love her in place of her parents, and Ginevra, despite her sadness, resolved to find happiness with him. “Then let us be happy,” she said, her spirit unbroken.

**CHAPTER V. MARRIAGE**

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father’s house, she sought refuge with Madame Servin, who coldly refused to help, irritated by the trouble Ginevra's situation caused her husband. Hurt yet proud, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her, painting a hopeful future that lifted her spirits despite her father's harshness.

One morning, Ginevra received trunks filled with essentials from her mother, who included a letter urging her to reconsider the marriage. The letter expressed her mother's love and concern, deeply moving Ginevra. Just as she felt compelled to return home, Luigi arrived, and her resolve to abandon him faded. She chose to support him instead, burying her own grief.

On their wedding day, Ginevra had no friends present. Luigi brought two witnesses: a cavalry sergeant and a master mason, both dressed simply, reflecting the somber nature of the occasion. Ginevra, though beautiful, wore modest attire, and the atmosphere was grave, lacking the usual joy of a wedding.

As they walked to the mayor’s office, they encountered other wedding parties filled with happiness, contrasting sharply with their own situation. Ginevra felt a mix of emotions, especially when the mayor’s clerk noted her parents' absence, heightening her anxiety. The bureaucratic ceremony felt cold, overshadowing their love with familial disapproval.

After a brief ceremony, Ginevra and Luigi were married, but they left the mayor’s office feeling the weight of their families' absence. Outside, Luigi expressed gratitude, and Ginevra felt relief despite the challenges ahead. They hurried to their modest apartment, where Luigi embraced her, declaring their true wedding had begun.

Inside their new home, Ginevra was delighted by the thoughtful arrangements Luigi had made, including a studio for her art. They explored their small but charming space, filled with love and hope for the future. Their initial days together were consumed by passion, often forgetting the outside world.

However, reality soon set in, and they realized they needed to work to sustain themselves. Ginevra began copying paintings, quickly gaining clients, while Luigi struggled until he decided to use his excellent handwriting for copying services. His efforts paid off, and he eventually established a successful business, allowing them to support themselves comfortably.

This period marked the happiest time in their lives, filled with love and shared labor. They enjoyed evenings together, filled with music and laughter, and neither expressed regret about their choices. Yet, Ginevra occasionally felt sorrow for her parents, imagining their loneliness over her absence.

To commemorate their anniversary, Ginevra painted a portrait of herself for Luigi, capturing not just her likeness but the essence of their love. This masterpiece symbolized their happiness and commitment to one another. Another year passed, and their life together continued in blissful harmony, marked by their enduring love.

In early 1819, Ginevra's picture-dealers encouraged her to create original works due to increasing competition. Acknowledging her earlier neglect of genre painting, she attempted portraiture but faced fierce competition from struggling artists. Despite some savings, Ginevra and Luigi's future remained uncertain.

As winter progressed, Luigi worked tirelessly, grappling with declining writing fees that forced him to labor longer hours without help. Ginevra produced several commendable paintings, but the market was unkind, leaving her efforts largely unsold. Their financial situation worsened, yet they masked their anxieties with love, fearing poverty would strip away their happiness.

One night, Ginevra awoke to find Luigi missing. She discovered him working late and, feeling guilty for his sacrifices, resolved to help by coloring lithographs. They both pretended to sleep, only to rise again to work, driven by love and desperation.

One evening, exhausted, Luigi opened his window and saw Ginevra’s lamp across the courtyard, realizing she too was working. When he confronted her, she insisted on sharing their burdens. They shared a tender moment, gazing at dawn, hopeful despite their hardships.

Their situation worsened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. Initially, the joy of parenthood masked their financial troubles, but soon they were forced to sell possessions and jewels. Poverty crept in, stripping them of comfort and pride.

Months later, Ginevra, now frail and worn, struggled to care for her sickly child. Luigi, desperate for help, found himself alone in a city where he knew no one. He sold Ginevra’s remaining paintings and furniture for meager sums, but their situation continued to deteriorate. Ginevra displayed remarkable strength, caring for her son and maintaining their home despite her suffering.

One evening, Ginevra saved a piece of bread for Luigi, who was horrified to realize she was starving. In despair, he decided to sell himself as a military substitute to secure money for her. He approached an old officer, who provided him with an advance. Luigi rushed home, hopeful, but found Ginevra in despair, holding their lifeless child.

In shock, Ginevra cried out for help, and Luigi, frantic, sought assistance from neighbors. As they gathered supplies, Ginevra expressed her love for Luigi and her regret over their lost child. Despite Luigi's pleas for her to hold on, Ginevra succumbed to exhaustion and died in his arms, leaving him devastated. 

Meanwhile, Ginevra's parents, Bartolomeo and Elisa, sat in their home, haunted by memories of their daughter. Just as Bartolomeo expressed a desire to see her again, Luigi appeared at their door, broken and holding Ginevra’s hair, revealing the tragic fate of their families. The revelation shattered the old couple, who realized the depth of their loss and the consequences of their estrangement.
