Bianchon, a notable Parisian physician, trained under the brilliant but troubled Desplein, who struggled to establish a lasting legacy and embraced atheism, feeling isolated in his beliefs. Desplein rejected the existence of a soul, which he never found in his dissections, leading to mixed judgments about his eccentric personality.

Bianchon, who faced poverty while studying medicine, was known for his integrity and loyalty. Desplein recognized his potential and mentored him in both medical practice and Parisian society. Bianchon was intrigued when he saw Desplein enter a church, given his atheism. During a dinner, Desplein dismissed the significance of mass, criticizing its historical implications.

Later, when questioned about his church visit, Desplein provided a vague excuse. Bianchon discovered that Desplein had founded the service he attended, deepening the mystery of a man who engaged in rituals contrary to his beliefs.

After the 1830 Revolution, Bianchon joined Desplein at Saint-Sulpice for mass, seeking an explanation for his attendance. Desplein recounted his early years of extreme poverty, living in a dilapidated building and struggling to finance his medical education. He described his life as a battle against despair, often lacking food and companionship.

Despite these hardships, Desplein was determined to succeed, feeling misunderstood by those who had never experienced true poverty. His bitterness stemmed from witnessing the selfishness of others in competitive Paris. 

During this time, his neighbor Bourgeat, a humble water-carrier, helped Desplein by paying his debts and supporting his education. Their bond deepened, and when Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein cared for him until his death. Desplein honored Bourgeat's faith by arranging masses for his soul, reflecting on the impact of their relationship and the complexities of belief.
