Bianchon, a physician schooled in theoretical physiology, first trained in surgery under the celebrated Desplein. Desplein excelled diagnostically and operatively but left no lasting school; his declared atheism—strengthened by dissecting corpses without sensing a soul—shaped his outlook. Critics pointed to contradictions: eccentric habits (alternating lavish and indifferent dress) and a mercurial temperament that ranged from kindness to harshness, provoking mixed judgments. Horace Bianchon, a poor but loyal and cheerful student, won Desplein’s mentorship and entry into practice and Parisian society. Anecdotes—Desplein rushing to aid a destitute water‑carrier and secretly attending and founding a Mass for twenty years—reveal a skilled, skeptical, compassionate, paradoxical man.

Some time passed before Dr. Bianchon, despite their friendship, found an opening to ask about a pivotal episode in Desplein’s life. Meetings rarely allowed private talk, but seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, Bianchon saw Desplein at Saint-Sulpice; they attended mass together.

“Can you explain your sudden piety?” Bianchon asked. Desplein first deflected—outwardly religious but inwardly skeptical—and criticized politicians. Pressed about the mass he had founded, he spoke plainly: nearing life’s end, he could trace its beginnings. Pointing to a building in the Rue des Quatre-Vents where he had lived two desperate years, he recalled hunger, poverty, isolation, an irritable temper that drove away friendships, no money for books or medical training, and a solitary struggle to rise from destitution.

He described meager meals, tireless work without sympathy, and how the well-to-do could not grasp his plight, asking why he had incurred debt as if it were voluntary. Early hardship taught him to expect selfishness and envy, and to see how mediocrity sought to undermine excellence.

A turning point came at the depth of his poverty, when, having been stranded by a porter who demanded payment for a relative’s linen, he considered enlisting. His neighbor Bourgeat, a humble water-carrier, changed everything: though poor himself, Bourgeat paid Desplein’s debts, secured lodgings, devoted his savings and understanding to Desplein’s future, and supported him selflessly. Bourgeat was nurturing, proud of Desplein’s success yet seeking no recognition. When Desplein became house surgeon he left Bourgeat, who remained proud; later Desplein bought him a horse and a barrel, fulfilling a dream. Bourgeat, angry that Desplein had spent money on him, was nonetheless delighted and never claimed credit, though Desplein felt he owed him everything.

When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein cared for him until he died in his arms, leaving him all he had. A man of strong faith, Bourgeat’s wishes were honored with masses; the mass Desplein founded was a tribute to that faith. Desplein said he would give anything for such belief. Bianchon, who had witnessed Desplein’s final days, could not say whether the great surgeon died an atheist; he wondered whether Bourgeat might have opened Heaven’s gates for Desplein as he had opened the doors to the earthly temple they shared.

PARIS, January 1836.
