Bianchon, a notable Parisian physician, trained under the troubled Desplein, who grappled with his legacy and embraced atheism, feeling isolated in his beliefs. Desplein, who denied the existence of a soul, faced mixed judgments about his eccentricity. 

Bianchon, who experienced poverty during his medical studies, was mentored by Desplein, who recognized his potential. Bianchon was intrigued when he saw Desplein enter a church, given his atheism. At dinner, Desplein dismissed the significance of mass, criticizing its historical implications. 

Later, Bianchon learned that Desplein had founded the service he attended, adding to the mystery. After the 1830 Revolution, Bianchon sought an explanation from Desplein, who recounted his extreme poverty and struggles to finance his education. Despite hardships, Desplein was determined to succeed, feeling misunderstood by those who had never faced true poverty. His neighbor Bourgeat, a humble water-carrier, supported his education, and their bond deepened. Desplein cared for Bourgeat until his death, honoring him by arranging masses for his soul.
