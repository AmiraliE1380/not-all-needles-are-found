Bianchon, a physician known for his theoretical physiology, trained in surgery under the celebrated French surgeon Desplein. Desplein’s genius lay in diagnostic and operative skill, but it remained personal rather than a broader legacy shaping future generations. His atheism—reinforced by dissecting bodies without perceiving any soul—formed a core of his outlook and informed his skepticism.

Despite his brilliance, Desplein's life was full of contradictions critics used to diminish him. His eccentricities—alternating lavish and indifferent dress—and a temperament swinging from kindness to harshness produced mixed judgments. He understood human nature but often displayed contempt after observing its follies.

Among his students, Horace Bianchon endured poverty while studying medicine. Noted for integrity, loyalty, and cheerfulness despite financial hardship, Bianchon was mentored by Desplein, who introduced him to medical practice and Parisian social dynamics. His compassion, often private, contrasted with public harshness, surprising those who knew him superficially.

Once Bianchon told him of a destitute water-carrier with a debilitating illness. Moved, Desplein rushed to help, revealing compassion beneath his stern exterior. This sympathy for the less fortunate, especially fellow Auvergnats, recurred.

Bianchon later saw Desplein enter a church—odd for an outspoken atheist. Curious, he followed and observed him at mass—at odds with his declared disbelief. At a later dinner Desplein dismissed the mass as a farce, but Bianchon was puzzled.

Months later another physician questioned Desplein; he gave a matter-of-fact explanation, but Bianchon resolved to investigate. He returned a year later and again found Desplein at mass, confirming a pattern. The sacristan disclosed Desplein had attended that mass for twenty years and had founded it.

This deepened the mystery, revealing a complexity that resisted labels. Bianchon confronted a man who professed atheism yet engaged in ritual—an apparent contradiction highlighting the complex relation between belief, practice, and behavior. The episodes suggested Desplein's public declarations and private practices sprang from a multifaceted character shaped by skill, skepticism, compassion, and paradox.

Some time passed before Doctor Bianchon, despite their friendship, found an opportunity to discuss a significant incident in Desplein's life. Their meetings, professional or social, rarely allowed private conversation. Finally, seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, Bianchon saw Desplein entering Saint-Sulpice and knelt beside him without surprise. They attended mass.

"Can you explain your sudden piety?" Bianchon asked as they left. "You, who do not believe in God, attending mass? You owe me an explanation."

"I am like many devout people—outwardly religious but inwardly skeptical," Desplein replied, then launched into a critique of certain political figures.

"That doesn't answer my question," Bianchon insisted. "Why did you found this mass?"

"Well, my dear boy," Desplein said, "I am nearing the end of my life; I can tell you its beginnings."

They stood in the Rue des Quatre-Vents, a grim Paris street. Desplein pointed to a building where he had lived for two years. "I lived up there," he said, recalling the hardships of his early life. He had known hunger, poverty, and isolation—no money for books or medical training, an irritable temperament that kept him from friends, and a constant struggle to rise from the depths of destitution. He described meager meals and tireless work without sympathy or support.

He expressed frustration at those born into wealth who could not grasp his struggles. "They ask why I got into debt, as if it were a choice. They have no idea what it means to be truly destitute."

Early experiences shaped his view of humanity. "If I seem hard or bitter, it's because I've witnessed the selfishness and envy around me. In Paris, mediocrity often seeks to undermine those who strive for excellence."

He recalled a pivotal moment when, facing utter poverty and contemplating enlisting, he had hoped for a box of linen from a relative. When it arrived, the porter demanded too much to release it. Despairing, he met his neighbor, Bourgeat, a water-carrier who offered help.

Bourgeat, despite his own hardships, paid Desplein's debts and helped him find new lodgings. "He devoted his savings to my future," Desplein said, visibly moved. "He understood my mission and supported me selflessly."

He described Bourgeat as a nurturing figure who cared for him like a mother. "He took pride in my success and never sought recognition for his sacrifices. When I became a house surgeon, I suffered leaving him, but he was proud of me."

Desplein later repaid Bourgeat by buying him a horse and barrel, fulfilling a long-held dream. "He was furious I had spent my money on him, yet he was delighted. He never claimed credit for my success, though I owed him everything."

When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein devoted himself to his care, but Bourgeat died in his arms. "He left me everything he had. He had a strong faith, and I honored his wishes by having masses said for him after his death."

Desplein concluded, "I can only offer this as a tribute to Bourgeat's faith. I hope that if there is a paradise, he finds peace there. I would give anything for the faith he had."

Bianchon, who had witnessed Desplein's last days, could not definitively say whether the great surgeon died an atheist. He pondered whether Bourgeat might have opened the gates of Heaven for Desplein, just as he had opened the doors to the earthly temple they had shared.

PARIS, January 1836.
