DEDICATION

To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron, your encouragement of my History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century led me to associate your name with my work. As a representative of studious Germany, your approval gives weight to my efforts. Your devotion to research sustained me in writing about modern civilization. Accept this token of respect from your sincere admirer and friend.

DE BALZAC.

THE COLLECTION OF ANTIQUITIES

In a small French town stood the Hôtel d’Esgrignon, home of the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol. The name matched a family proud of ancient Northman lineage and stubborn provincial dignity—marriages without dowries, refusal of titles, local reverence despite neglect by court and state.

The Revolution threatened them. The Marquis avoided emigration by hiding; family lands were sold. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, with the steward Chesnel, saved some property. After the Terror the Marquis returned to a ruined castle; the old hôtel was demolished. Chesnel used the family’s last funds to buy a modest new dwelling, wryly renamed the Hôtel d’Esgrignon.

Around 1800 émigrés returned, including the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, whom the Marquis married; she died in childbirth, leaving a son. Grief compounded their reduced circumstances. A local contractor’s proposal to marry Mlle. d’Esgrignon offended the siblings; Chesnel was reproached for negotiating. Chesnel was more than a servant—confidant, manager, protector—faithful to a declining house. Politically nobles split between Bonapartists and monarchists; the d’Esgrignons remained royalist. By 1804 returns had not restored fortunes. The Marquis became a local emblem of old nobility, presiding over a salon mockingly called the “Collection of Antiquities.” By 1822 the Restoration brought little relief: finances strained, life modest, yet they clung to ideals and a lingering legacy.

The Marquis neglected estate management, and most believed the d’Esgrignons could not regain their former glory. Many hoped Victurnien, the handsome heir, would marry into nobility to restore prestige, but after forty years the family lacked the connections, money, and stability to reclaim status. Only Chesnel, the notary, recognized this reality and urged adaptation; the Marquis preferred to trust fate or divine intervention.

Victurnien, adored and indulged by his father and aunt, had never faced opposition. Spoiled by the Chevalier’s romantic tales of past grandeur and constant flattery, he became selfish and reckless, seeing himself above common people. His escapades alienated townspeople and led to legal troubles Chesnel discreetly managed. Encouraged by companions who enjoyed his company, his arrogance hardened; Du Croisier, a rival, resolved to exploit these weaknesses to hasten the family’s ruin.

Vanity drove Victurnien into gambling and frivolity, sustained by Chesnel’s financial sacrifices. Sent to Paris to restore the name, he found the court indifferent and instead adopted an extravagant lifestyle. His looks opened doors, and he became infatuated with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, blind to her debts and convinced their passion was pure. As pleasures mounted, so did debts; friends egged his spending while Chesnel repeatedly propped him up. This clash between pretension and dire finances foreshadowed downfall, illustrating the dangers of vanity and the illusion of inherited status.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien 200,000 francs without Chesnel or Mlle. Armande knowing. Victurnien had also taken 2,000 crowns from Chesnel to conceal his sources and wrote flattering, deceitful letters home; his father and aunt remained unaware. Only Du Croisier knew, delighting not only in ruining the d’Esgrignons but in dishonoring them as debts mounted.

Du Croisier first targeted Chesnel, who lived modestly on Rue du Bercail. He presented a bill for 227,000 francs, demanding payment or threatening exposure. Overwhelmed, Chesnel saw he could raise money only by mortgaging his lands. Du Croisier seized the chance, bought the property within two days, and left Chesnel with only his practice and house, consumed by worry for Victurnien.

Chesnel dreaded telling Mlle. Armande, who had just received a charming letter from Victurnien about travels in Italy with the Duchesse and remained enchanted. His news shattered her reverie: all was lost, and Victurnien risked dishonor. She resolved to go to Paris—pretending it was for medical reasons—to confront the Duchesse; Chesnel gave her his last bag of louis.

Du Croisier then moved to cut Victurnien off, arranging for the Kellers to demand security before advancing more funds. Pressed and briefly contemplating suicide, Victurnien instead plotted to flee with the Duchesse and forged a bill of exchange; when she agreed and he presented a bill for 300,000 francs, the Kellers advanced the sum unknowingly. Du Croisier, seeing the deception and its effect, realized his scheme was succeeding.

Victurnien gave the money to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who hid it and persuaded him to return to the Opera. He nearly confessed to Chesnel but, dazzled by the Duchess’s look, stayed at her request. The next day, when he planned to flee, she resolved to face the consequences; torn between admiration and despair, he recognized the depth of his love. After a quarrel Diane’s coldness left him distraught; his plea for forgiveness met indifference, worsening his anguish and clouding his judgment.

When a warrant was issued, Josephin urged him to go to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine where Mlle. Armande waited. Reeling, Victurnien and his aunt discussed hiding and slipping into Chesnel’s house—a plan that proved a trap. Chesnel, preparing to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir (who hoped to profit from Victurnien’s misfortune), was devastated. He considered taking the blame for the forgery and went to confront Victurnien’s creditor, du Croisier, to seek a settlement.

Du Croisier, relishing his ascendancy over the d’Esgrignon family, treated the affair as an opportunity to assert dominance and settle aristocratic scores. At his salon news of Victurnien’s arrest spread and guests debated the impending trial. Chesnel tried to influence the examining magistrate, M. Camusot, by appealing to his ambitions and leaned on Mme. de Maufrigneuse—who, once she grasped Victurnien’s love and the stakes, felt compelled to help.

Negotiations with du Croisier were fraught: he demanded money, political favors, and social recognition—terms Chesnel could scarcely meet. Desperate, Chesnel offered a large sum and even contemplated perjury, but du Croisier’s pride resisted compromise. The legal struggle came to symbolize a broader clash between aristocracy and bourgeoisie, where loyalty, love, honor, and ambition would decide more than one fate.

Meanwhile Blondet, more devoted to gardening than his bench, neglected career prospects amid a troubled marriage; he favored his illegitimate son Émile in Paris while grooming his legitimate son Joseph for the law. Ambitious younger lawyers outshone the elders. Under pressure from the Tribunal’s president and his wife, a politically astute prosecutor was drawn into a scheme against the d’Esgrignons. The Duchess and Chesnel funded efforts to free Victurnien; the trial ultimately exonerated him and exposed du Croisier’s plots. Though socially scrutinized, the d’Esgrignons preserved their honor; Victurnien’s future remained uncertain, highlighting shifting loyalties and ambitions in a changing France.
