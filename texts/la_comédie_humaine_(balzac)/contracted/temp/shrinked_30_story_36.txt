DEDICATION

To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Baron, your encouragement of my “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century” led me to associate your name with my work. As a representative of studious Germany, your approval gave weight to my efforts; your devotion to research sustained me while writing on modern civilization. Receive this token of respect from your sincere admirer and friend.

DE BALZAC.

THE COLLECTION OF ANTIQUITIES

In a small French town stands the Hotel d’Esgrignon, seat of the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol. Proud of Northman lineage, the family treated its name as a badge of stubborn provincial nobility, refusing dowries, titles, or concessions to court and state, and retaining local reverence despite neglect.

The Revolution threatened them: the Marquis hid instead of emigrating and family lands were sold. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, through the steward Chesnel, rescued some property. After the Terror the Marquis returned to a ruined castle; the old hotel was demolished and Chesnel used the family’s last funds to buy a new dwelling, wryly named the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

Around 1800 émigrés returned, including the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, whom the Marquis married; she died in childbirth, leaving a son. Grief and reduced circumstances deepened the family’s decline. When a local contractor proposed marrying Mlle. d’Esgrignon—an offer she and her brother deemed insulting—a rift grew and Chesnel, who had negotiated with the contractor, was blamed.

Chesnel was more than a servant: confidant, manager, protector as the house decayed. Politically, nobles split between Bonapartists and monarchists; the d’Esgrignons remained staunch royalists. By 1804 many émigrés had returned but fortunes did not revive. The Marquis became a local emblem of old nobility, presiding over a salon mockingly called the “Collection of Antiquities.” As the bourgeoisie rose and rivals like du Croisier undermined them, the 1822 Restoration brought little relief: finances strained, life modest, yet they clung to their ideals and lingering legacy.

The Marquis neglected estate management, and most believed the d’Esgrignons could not regain their former glory. People hoped the handsome heir Victurnien might marry into nobility to restore the family’s prestige, but after forty years the family lacked connections, finances, and continuity. Only Chesnel, the notary, urged the Marquis to adapt to post-Revolution realities; the Marquis instead trusted fate or divine intervention.

Victurnien, adored and indulged by his father and aunt, had never been opposed, which made him selfish and reckless. The Chevalier, a relic of the old order, fed his extravagance with romantic tales of past grandeur, reinforcing a sense of superiority. Surrounded by flattery and exempt from ordinary restraints, Victurnien felt above common people, worsening both moral and practical defects. In society his behavior alienated townspeople; escapades produced legal troubles Chesnel discreetly managed. Encouraged by companions who liked his company, his arrogance hardened into excess, assuming lineage would shield him from consequences. Du Croisier, a rival, sought to exploit these weaknesses to hasten the d’Esgrignons’ ruin.

Sent to Paris to revive the family name, Victurnien found court life self-interested and indifferent but adopted the extravagant ways of the elite. His charm and looks opened doors, and he became infatuated with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, blind to her debts and convinced their passion was pure. As pleasures mounted so did obligations: friends egged his spending while Chesnel kept propping him up. The tension between noble pretension and dire finances foreshadowed collapse, illustrating the dangers of vanity, excess, and the illusion of inherited status.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs without Chesnel or Mlle. Armande knowing. Victurnien had also taken two thousand crowns from Chesnel to conceal his sources and wrote deceitful, flattering letters to his father and aunt, who remained unaware of the looming disaster. Only Du Croisier knew; he delighted not only in ruining the d’Esgrignons but in dishonoring them as Victurnien’s debts mounted.

Du Croisier first targeted Chesnel, who lived modestly on the Rue du Bercail. After dinner he presented a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, demanding payment or threatening to expose Victurnien’s liabilities. Overwhelmed, Chesnel realized he could raise the money only by mortgaging his lands. Du Croisier seized the chance: he proposed to buy Chesnel’s property and completed the purchase within two days. Stripped of his lands, Chesnel was left with only his practice and his house, consumed by worry for Victurnien’s future.

Chesnel dreaded telling Mlle. Armande, who had just received a romantic letter from Victurnien describing travels in Italy with the Duchesse and remained enchanted. Chesnel’s revelation shattered her reverie: all was lost, and Victurnien risked dishonoring their house. Devastated, she resolved to rescue him—going to Paris under the pretense of a medical consultation and taking Chesnel’s last bag of louis to confront the Duchesse.

Meanwhile Du Croisier planned to cut Victurnien off from further support, informing the Kellers they would advance no more without security. Victurnien, facing ruin, briefly contemplated suicide but instead plotted to flee with the Duchess, forging a bill of exchange; when she agreed and he presented a bill for three hundred thousand francs, the Kellers advanced the sum unknowingly. Du Croisier, learning of the transaction, saw his scheme succeeding.

Victurnien entrusted the money to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who hid it and persuaded him to return to the Opera. He nearly confessed to Chesnel but, mesmerized by the Duchess, stayed at her request. Planning to flee the next day, he returned to find her resolved to face the consequences. Torn between admiration and despair, Victurnien recognized the depth of his love; after a quarrel Diane’s coldness left him distraught and clouded his judgment.

Things worsened when a warrant issued for his arrest. Josephin urged him to go to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine, where Mlle. Armande waited. Reeling, Victurnien and his aunt plotted to hide and slip into Chesnel’s house — a scheme that became a trap.

Chesnel, preparing to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir (who hoped to profit from Victurnien’s misfortune), was devastated by the young man’s plight. He considered taking responsibility for the forgery and resolved to confront Victurnien’s creditor, du Croisier, to negotiate a settlement. Du Croisier, relishing his ascendancy over the d’Esgrignon family, treated the affair as an opportunity to assert dominance and settle aristocratic scores. At a gathering in his house, news of Victurnien’s arrest spread and guests debated the implications for the impending trial.

Recognizing the stakes, Chesnel tried to influence the examining magistrate, M. Camusot, by appealing to his ambitions and connections, and pressed Mme. de Maufrigneuse—who, once she grasped Victurnien’s love and the consequences, felt compelled to help. Negotiations with du Croisier proved thorny: he demanded money, political favors, and social recognition—terms Chesnel could scarcely meet. Desperate, Chesnel offered a large sum, but du Croisier’s pride resisted compromise. As the trial approached both sides hardened; Chesnel even contemplated lying under oath. The legal battle came to symbolize a broader clash between aristocracy and bourgeoisie, where loyalty, love, honor, and ambition would decide more than one man’s fate.

Blondet, a competent judge, was distracted by his passion for gardening and domestic complications: a much younger wife who had an affair with the local prefect. Though he had a legitimate son, Joseph, Blondet favored his illegitimate son, Émile, and after his wife’s death sent Émile to Paris with little support. Émile prospered in Paris with help from his mother’s family and Mlle de Troisville, gaining literary recognition unknown to his father. Joseph was groomed for the bench, while Michu, a young lawyer, moved through circles and often outshone older judges.

A shrewd public prosecutor was drawn into a political scheme against the d’Esgrignons that produced the forgery charge. Pressured by the Tribunal’s president and du Croisier’s ambitions, the prosecution unfolded amid political maneuvering. The Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and Chesnel intervened, funding efforts to free Victurnien; the trial ultimately exonerated him and exposed du Croisier’s plots. Though socially scrutinized, the d’Esgrignons kept their honor; Victurnien’s future remained uncertain, illustrating shifting nobility, loyalty, and ambition in a changing France.
