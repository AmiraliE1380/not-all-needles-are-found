DEDICATION

To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron, your encouragement of my “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century” led me to associate your name with my work. As a representative of studious Germany, your approval gives weight to my efforts. Your devotion to research sustained me in writing about modern civilization. Accept this token of respect from your sincere admirer and friend.

DE BALZAC.

THE COLLECTION OF ANTIQUITIES

In a small French town stood the Hotel d’Esgrignon, home of the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol. The name began as a jest but suited a family proud of its ancient Northman lineage. Proud provincial nobles who neither bowed to kings nor commoners, the d’Esgrignons kept a stubborn dignity: marriages without dowries, refusals of titles, and a local reverence that persisted despite neglect by court and state.

The Revolution imperiled them. The Marquis avoided emigration but hid; family lands were sold. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, with the steward Chesnel, saved some property. After the Terror the Marquis returned to a ruined castle; the old hotel had been demolished. Chesnel used the family’s last funds to buy a new dwelling, wryly named the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

Around 1800 émigrés returned, including the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, whom the Marquis married; she died in childbirth, leaving a son. Grief deepened the family’s reduced circumstances. When a local contractor proposed to marry Mlle. d’Esgrignon—a proposal she and her brother found insulting—a rift grew and Chesnel, who had negotiated with the contractor, was reproached.

Chesnel was more than a servant: confidant, manager, protector of the family’s interests, struggling with fidelity to a declining house even as the Marquis regarded him as a loyal companion. Politically the era split nobles between Bonapartists and monarchists; the d’Esgrignons remained staunchly royalist. By 1804 many émigrés had returned, yet fortunes did not revive. The Marquis became a local emblem of old nobility, presiding over a salon mockingly called the “Collection of Antiquities,” as the bourgeoisie rose and rivals sought to undermine them. By 1822 Restoration brought little relief: finances were strained, life modest, but the d’Esgrignons clung to their ideals and lingering legacy.

The Marquis neglected estate management, and most considered it absurd that the d’Esgrignons could regain their former glory. Many hoped Victurnien, the handsome heir, would marry into nobility to restore the family’s prestige, but the family lacked the political connections, money, and continuity to reclaim status after forty years.

Only Chesnel, the notary, recognized the futility of such hopes. He understood the post-Revolution social changes and, despite loyalty, urged the Marquis to adapt. The old noble, however, trusted fate or divine intervention to revive their fortunes.

Victurnien, adored by his father and aunt, had been spoiled and never faced opposition. His indulgent upbringing made him selfish and reckless; the Chevalier, a relic of the old order, fed his extravagance with romantic tales of past grandeur, reinforcing a sense of superiority. Surrounded by flattery and exempt from ordinary restraints, he began to regard himself as above the common people.

When he entered society, the contrast with modern realities became clear. His behavior alienated townspeople; escapades led to legal troubles Chesnel discreetly managed. Encouraged by companions who relished his company, his arrogance hardened into excess, assuming lineage would shield him from consequences. Du Croisier, a rival, sought to exploit these weaknesses to hasten the d’Esgrignons’ ruin.

Victurnien’s vanity drove him into gambling and frivolity, sustained by Chesnel’s financial sacrifices, which he accepted as his due. Sent to Paris with high expectations, he found the court mostly self-interested and indifferent to reviving the family name. Undeterred, he adopted the extravagant lifestyle of Parisian elites; his charm and looks opened doors, and he became captivated by the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, blind to her debts and convinced their passion was pure.

As pleasures mounted, so did debts. Friends egged his spending while Chesnel kept propping him up. The tension between noble pretension and dire finances foreshadowed a downfall, illustrating the dangers of vanity, excess, and the illusion of inherited status in a changing world.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs without Chesnel or Mlle. Armande knowing. Victurnien had also taken two thousand crowns from Chesnel to conceal his sources and wrote flattering, deceitful letters to his father and aunt, who remained unaware of the disaster looming over their house. Only du Croisier knew; he delighted not only in ruining the d’Esgrignons but in dishonoring them as Victurnien’s debts mounted.

Du Croisier first targeted Chesnel, who lived modestly on the Rue du Bercail. One afternoon he presented a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, demanding payment or threatening to expose Victurnien’s liabilities. Overwhelmed, Chesnel realized he could raise the money only by mortgaging his lands. Du Croisier seized the chance, proposed to buy the property, and completed the purchase within two days. Stripped of his lands, Chesnel was left with only his practice and his house, consumed by worry for Victurnien’s future.

Chesnel dreaded telling Mlle. Armande, who had just received a charming, hypocritical letter from Victurnien about his travels in Italy with the Duchesse and remained enchanted. Chesnel’s arrival shattered her reverie: all was lost, and Victurnien risked bringing dishonor on their house. Devastated, she realized they had given him too much freedom.

The Marquis, having read Victurnien’s letter, remained unaware of the crisis. Mlle. Armande and Chesnel resolved to rescue the young Count. She decided to go to Paris—pretending it was for a medical consultation to avoid gossip. Chesnel gave her his last bag of louis, and she set off to confront the Duchesse.

Du Croisier, meanwhile, planned to cut Victurnien off from further support. He wrote that the Kellers would advance no more money without security, timing the letter to Victurnien’s growing desperation. Once living in luxury and buoyed by occasional gambling wins, Victurnien now faced ruin. After briefly contemplating suicide, he rejected it and plotted to flee with the Duchess, forging a bill of exchange; when she agreed and he presented a bill for three hundred thousand francs, the Kellers advanced the sum unknowingly. Du Croisier, learning of the transaction, realized his scheme was succeeding.

Victurnien gave the money to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who locked it away and persuaded him to attend the Opera again. He nearly confessed to Chesnel but, despite misgivings, lingered at the Duchess’s request, captivated by her look. The next day, planning to flee, he found the Duchess resolved to stay and face the consequences. Torn between admiration and despair, Victurnien recognized the depth of his love. After their quarrel Diane’s coldness left him distraught; his plea for forgiveness met indifference, deepening his anguish and clouding his judgment.

When a warrant was issued for his arrest, Josephin urged him to go to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine, where Mlle. Armande waited. Reeling, Victurnien and his aunt discussed hiding and slipping into Chesnel’s house — a plan that led him into a trap. Chesnel, preparing to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir (who hoped to profit from Victurnien’s misfortune), was devastated by the young man’s plight. He even considered taking the blame for the forgery and resolved to confront Victurnien’s creditor, du Croisier, to negotiate a settlement.

Du Croisier, relishing his ascendancy over the d’Esgrignon family, treated the affair as an opportunity to assert dominance and settle aristocratic scores. At a gathering in his house the news of Victurnien’s arrest spread and guests debated the implications for the impending trial. Realizing the stakes, Chesnel tried to influence the examining magistrate, M. Camusot, by appealing to his ambitions and connections, and pressed Mme. de Maufrigneuse—who, once she grasped Victurnien’s love and the consequences, felt compelled to help.

Negotiations with du Croisier were fraught: he demanded money, political favors, and social recognition—terms Chesnel could scarcely meet. Desperate, Chesnel offered a large sum to withdraw the charges, but du Croisier’s pride resisted compromise. As the trial neared both sides hardened; Chesnel even contemplated lying under oath to save Victurnien. The legal battle came to symbolize a wider clash between aristocracy and bourgeoisie, where loyalty, love, honor, and ambition would decide more than one man’s fate.

Meanwhile Blondet, a competent judge, was eclipsed by his passion for gardening—especially pelargoniums—which absorbed his time. His marriage to a much younger woman, who had an affair with the local prefect, further clouded his career. Though he had a legitimate son, Joseph, Blondet preferred his illegitimate son, Émile, and after his wife’s death sent Émile to Paris with little support. Émile prospered with help from his mother’s family and Mlle de Troisville, gaining literary recognition, while Joseph was groomed for the bench. Michu, a young lawyer, often outshone older judges.

A shrewd public prosecutor was drawn into a political scheme against the d’Esgrignons, pressured by the President of the Tribunal and his wife to produce a forgery charge. The Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and Chesnel intervened, funding efforts to free Victurnien; the trial ultimately exonerated him and exposed du Croisier’s plots. Though socially scrutinized, the d’Esgrignons kept their honor; Victurnien’s future remained uncertain, underscoring shifting loyalties and ambitions in a changing France.
