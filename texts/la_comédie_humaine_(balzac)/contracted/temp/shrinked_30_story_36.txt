DEDICATION

To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron, your encouragement of my “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century” led me to associate your name with my work. As a representative of studious Germany, your approval lends weight to my efforts. Your devotion to research sustained me while writing on modern civilization. Receive this token of respect from your sincere admirer and friend.

DE BALZAC.

THE COLLECTION OF ANTIQUITIES

In a small French town stands the Hotel d’Esgrignon, seat of the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol. Proud of his Northman lineage, the family wore its name almost as a jest, emblematic of a stubborn provincial nobility that refused to bow to kings or commoners: marriages without dowries, refusals of titles, and local reverence despite neglect by court and state.

The Revolution threatened them. The Marquis avoided emigration by hiding; family lands were sold. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, through the steward Chesnel, rescued some property. After the Terror the Marquis returned to a ruined castle; the old hotel had been demolished. Chesnel used the family’s last funds to buy a new dwelling, wryly named the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

Around 1800 émigrés returned, including the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, whom the Marquis married; she died in childbirth, leaving a son to bear the name. Grief deepened the family’s reduced circumstances. When a local contractor proposed marrying Mlle. d’Esgrignon—an offer she and her brother deemed insulting—a rift grew, and Chesnel, who had negotiated with the contractor, was blamed.

Chesnel was more than a servant: confidant, manager, protector as the house declined. Politically the era split nobles between Bonapartists and monarchists; the d’Esgrignons remained staunchly royalist. By 1804 many émigrés had returned, yet fortunes did not revive. The Marquis became a local emblem of old nobility, presiding over a salon mockingly called the “Collection of Antiquities,” gathering those clinging to the past. As the bourgeoisie rose and rivals like du Croisier undermined them, the 1822 Restoration brought little relief: finances strained, life modest, but the d’Esgrignons clung to their ideals and lingering legacy.

The Marquis neglected estate management, and most considered it unrealistic that the d’Esgrignons could recover their former glory. People hoped Victurnien, the handsome heir, might marry into nobility to restore the family’s prestige, but after forty years the family lacked the connections, finances, and continuity to reclaim its status.

Only Chesnel, the notary, recognized that clinging to old hopes was futile. He urged the Marquis to adapt to the social changes since the Revolution; the Marquis, however, trusted fate or divine intervention to revive their fortunes. Victurnien, adored by his father and aunt, had been indulged and never opposed. This upbringing made him selfish and reckless. The Chevalier, a relic of the old order, encouraged his extravagance with romantic tales of past grandeur, reinforcing a sense of superiority. Surrounded by flattery and exempt from ordinary restraints, Victurnien felt above the common people, worsening both moral and practical defects.

When he entered society, the contrast with modern realities became plain. His behavior alienated townspeople; escapades led to legal troubles Chesnel discreetly managed. Encouraged by companions who enjoyed his company, his arrogance hardened into excess; he assumed lineage would shield him from consequences. Du Croisier, a rival, sought to exploit these weaknesses to accelerate the d’Esgrignons’ ruin. Victurnien’s vanity drove him into gambling and frivolity, sustained by Chesnel’s financial sacrifices, which he accepted as his due.

Sent to Paris to revive the family name, Victurnien found the court self-interested and indifferent. Undeterred, he adopted the extravagant lifestyle of the Parisian elite; his charm and looks opened doors, and he became infatuated with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, blind to her debts and convinced their passion was pure. As pleasures mounted, so did obligations. Friends egged his spending while Chesnel kept propping him up. The tension between noble pretension and dire finances foreshadowed collapse, illustrating the dangers of vanity, excess, and the illusion of inherited status.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs without Chesnel or Mlle. Armande knowing. Victurnien had also taken two thousand crowns from Chesnel to conceal his sources and wrote flattering, deceitful letters to his father and aunt, who remained unaware of the disaster looming over their house. Only du Croisier knew; he delighted not only in ruining the d’Esgrignons but in dishonoring them, relishing revenge as Victurnien’s debts mounted.

Du Croisier first targeted Chesnel, who lived modestly on the Rue du Bercail. After dinner he presented a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, demanding payment or threatening to expose Victurnien’s liabilities. Overwhelmed, Chesnel realized he could raise the money only by mortgaging his lands. Du Croisier seized the chance: he proposed to buy Chesnel’s property and completed the purchase within two days. Stripped of his lands, Chesnel was left with only his practice and his house, consumed by worry for Victurnien’s future.

Chesnel dreaded telling Mlle. Armande, who had just received a romantic letter from Victurnien describing travels in Italy with the Duchesse and remained enchanted. Chesnel’s revelation shattered her reverie: all was lost, and Victurnien risked bringing dishonor on their house. Devastated, she realized they had given him too much freedom. The Marquis, having read Victurnien’s letter, remained unaware of the crisis. Armande and Chesnel resolved to rescue the young count: she decided to go to Paris—pretending a medical consultation to avoid gossip—taking Chesnel’s last bag of louis to confront the Duchesse.

Du Croisier meanwhile planned to ensure Victurnien was cut off from further support, informing the Kellers they would advance no more without security. Victurnien, once living in luxury and buoyed by gambling, now faced ruin. After briefly contemplating suicide, he rejected it and plotted to flee with the Duchess, forging a bill of exchange; when she agreed and he presented a bill for three hundred thousand francs, the Kellers advanced the sum unknowingly. Du Croisier, learning of the transaction, realized his scheme was succeeding.

Victurnien entrusted the money to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who hid it and persuaded him to return to the Opera. He nearly confessed to Chesnel but, mesmerized by the Duchess, stayed at her request. Planning to flee the next day, he came back to find her resolved to face the consequences. Torn between admiration and despair, Victurnien recognized the depth of his love; after a quarrel Diane’s coldness left him distraught and clouded his judgment.

Things worsened when a warrant was issued for his arrest. Josephin urged him to go to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine, where Mlle. Armande waited. Reeling, Victurnien and his aunt plotted to hide and slip into Chesnel’s house — a plan that led him into a trap.

Chesnel, preparing to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir (who hoped to profit from Victurnien’s misfortune), was devastated by the young man’s plight. He considered taking the blame for the forgery and resolved to confront Victurnien’s creditor, du Croisier, to negotiate a settlement. Du Croisier, relishing his ascendancy over the d’Esgrignon family, treated the affair as an opportunity to assert dominance and settle aristocratic scores. At a gathering in his house, news of Victurnien’s arrest spread and guests debated the implications for the impending trial.

Recognizing the stakes, Chesnel tried to influence the examining magistrate, M. Camusot, by appealing to his ambitions and connections, and pressed Mme. de Maufrigneuse—who, once she grasped Victurnien’s love and the consequences, felt compelled to help. Negotiations with du Croisier proved thorny: he demanded money, political favors, and social recognition—terms Chesnel could scarcely meet. Desperate, Chesnel offered a large sum, but du Croisier’s pride resisted compromise. As the trial approached both sides hardened; Chesnel even contemplated lying under oath. The legal battle came to symbolize a broader clash between aristocracy and bourgeoisie, where loyalty, love, honor, and ambition would decide more than one man’s fate.

Blondet, a competent judge, was distracted by his passion for gardening and by domestic complications: a much younger wife who had an affair with the local prefect. Though he had a legitimate son, Joseph, Blondet favored his illegitimate son, Émile, and after his wife’s death sent Émile to Paris with little support. Blondet planned to leave his estate to Joseph, disinheriting Émile, who nevertheless prospered in Paris with help from his mother’s family and Mlle de Troisville, gaining literary recognition unknown to his father. Joseph was being groomed for the bench, while Michu, a young lawyer, moved through circles and often outshone older judges.

A shrewd public prosecutor was drawn into a political scheme against the d’Esgrignons, producing the forgery charge. The Tribunal’s president, seeking advantage for du Croisier and urged by his wife, pressured the prosecutor. The Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and Chesnel intervened, funding efforts to free Victurnien; the trial ultimately exonerated him and exposed du Croisier’s plots. Though socially scrutinized, the d’Esgrignons kept their honor; Victurnien’s future remained uncertain, illustrating shifting nobility, loyalty, and ambition in a changing France.
