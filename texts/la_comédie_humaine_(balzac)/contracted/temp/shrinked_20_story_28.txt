PIERRE GRASSOU

Have you ever felt unease and sadness while viewing the crowded galleries of art exhibitions since the revolution of 1830? The true Salon has vanished, overtaken by a multitude of artists. In the past, the Salon showcased only the finest works, granting honor to those selected. The public could engage in passionate discussions about the art, and even criticism contributed to the fame of artists like Delacroix and Ingres. Today, however, the excitement has faded; the public is overwhelmed by the sheer volume of works, leading to a lack of engagement and a swift closure of exhibitions. Before 1817, only a select few paintings were displayed, but now the galleries overflow with countless pieces, diluting the quality and impact of individual works.

The essence of the Salon has been lost in this chaos. Instead of a refined selection, we now face a tumultuous bazaar where great artists are overshadowed. For instance, Decamps' notable works would have gained more recognition if displayed among a limited number of quality pieces rather than being lost in a sea of mediocrity. Ironically, despite the influx of artists, there is now more talk of unrecognized genius than ever before. When the Academy's selection was more stringent, true talent was acknowledged. Now, with no judgment, the quality of art suffers, and without the Academy's selection, the Salon—and art itself—may perish.

Among the many names in the ever-expanding catalogue of artists, one stands out: Pierre Grassou, known simply as "Fougeres" among his peers. In 1832, he lived in a cramped apartment in the rue de Navarin, with a studio overlooking Montmartre. His studio was modestly furnished, reflecting his tidy nature and humble means. As a genre painter, he avoided the complexities that burden historical painters, focusing instead on easel painting.

One December morning, while preparing to work, Grassou was visited by Elie Magus, a picture dealer. Magus, who bought Grassou's paintings for modest sums, proposed a business arrangement: Grassou would paint portraits of a bourgeois family, the Vervelles, who were eager to enter the art world. The daughter had a substantial dowry, and Magus suggested that if Grassou married her, he would benefit greatly.

Grassou, who had a simple and solitary life, was taken aback by the idea of marriage. However, the prospect of painting three portraits for the family intrigued him, as it could elevate his status as a portrait artist. Magus left to find the Vervelles, leaving Grassou to reflect on the potential impact of this opportunity.

To understand Grassou's situation, we must look back at his life. He studied under various masters, including Servin and Schinner, but never quite grasped the secrets of their techniques. His education was marked by a lack of recognition; he was often teased for his mediocrity. Despite his gentle nature and dedication, he struggled to stand out among his peers, who were more flamboyant or eccentric.

Grassou's first attempt at the Salon in 1819—a painting of a village wedding—was rejected. Rather than succumbing to despair, he sought feedback from Schinner, who kindly advised him to abandon painting. However, Grassou was determined to improve. He worked tirelessly to refine his rejected piece, only to sell it to Magus for a meager sum. This marked the beginning of his long and challenging journey in the art world, characterized by perseverance despite the odds stacked against him. 

As Grassou navigated his career, he remained committed to his craft, embodying the struggles and aspirations of many artists of his time. His story reflects the broader themes of ambition, recognition, and the often harsh realities of the art world, where talent can be overshadowed by circumstance.

For several days, Pierre Fougeres lingered near Elie's shop, hoping for attention to his painting, but it went unnoticed. After a week, the painting vanished, prompting him to casually approach the dealer. Elie Magus informed him that he had sold the piece, and offered to buy more of his work if he created Flemish interiors, anatomy lessons, and landscapes. Overjoyed, Fougeres felt validated in his talent, believing there were those who appreciated his art. 

Despite his excitement, he remained oblivious to the irony in Elie's demeanor, which a more cynical artist might have noticed. He met his friend Joseph Bridau, but was too consumed with thoughts of painting to enjoy the evening. Returning home, he sketched furiously, inspired by his newfound confidence. He bought supplies and hired models, dedicating himself to his craft.

After two months, he completed four new paintings and sought advice from Schinner and Bridau. They criticized his work as derivative, suggesting he explore literature instead. Undeterred, Fougeres retouched his paintings and sold them to Elie for twenty-five francs each, barely breaking even. He noticed a strange mist over his paintings when he visited the shop, but attributed it to his imagination.

Over seven years, Fougeres honed his skills, producing work that was competent but not exceptional. Elie sold his paintings, allowing him to earn a modest living. At the 1829 Exhibition, his friends helped him gain entry for a powerful piece depicting a young man in prison, inspired by Gerard Douw. The painting garnered significant attention, even from Charles X, leading to its purchase by Madame and a commission for more work.

Fougeres' success stemmed from his ability to imitate rather than innovate, a lesson he learned well. Despite criticism, his modesty and perseverance won him support, and he earned a reputation for his dedication. With his earnings, he furnished a studio and completed commissioned works with punctuality, a rarity among artists.

By thirty-seven, he had produced around two hundred paintings, achieving a style that appealed to the bourgeoisie. His friends valued his character over his artistic talent, often lamenting his choice of profession. Nevertheless, he offered sound advice and maintained a keen appreciation for beauty.

After the July Revolution, he continued to submit work to the Salon, living frugally and managing his finances with the help of his notary, Cardot. He dreamed of a comfortable life as an artist, aspiring to join the Institute and gain recognition.

One day, a wealthy family, the Vervelles, visited his studio for portraits. The father, a former bottle-dealer, was impressed by Fougeres' cross of the Legion of Honor. The family expressed admiration for his work, and Pierre found himself charmed by their daughter, Virginie. As they returned for subsequent sittings, the Vervelles grew more familiar with him, discussing their art collection and financial stability.

During the sessions, Pierre flattered the family, which endeared him to them. The father boasted of their valuable art collection, while the notary praised Fougeres' character, emphasizing his financial prudence and ability to provide for a family. This reputation solidified the Vervelles' interest in him, as they began to see him as a suitable match for their daughter. 

As the sittings progressed, Pierre's feelings for Virginie deepened, and he began to envision a future with her, despite the initial focus on his art. The Vervelles' admiration and their discussions about his success further fueled his aspirations, intertwining his personal and professional life in unexpected ways.

The day Fougeres began painting Mademoiselle Virginie, he was effectively part of the Vervelle family. The Vervelles felt an inexplicable attraction to the clean, artistic studio, which had become a second home for them. Toward the end of the sitting, the door burst open, and Joseph Bridau entered like a whirlwind, his disheveled appearance startling the Vervelles. He quickly scanned the studio and approached Grassou, struggling to button his coat.

"Wood is dear," he remarked, before asking for five hundred francs to fend off creditors. Grassou agreed to write him a note, while Bridau criticized his painting style, suggesting he should embrace nature's imperfections.

As Bridau left, Madame Vervelle expressed concern about the impact of his presence on Virginie's portrait. Grassou reassured her that Bridau had only improved it. The Vervelles, intrigued by the artist's genius, invited Fougeres to their country house the following Sunday, hoping to impress him with their hospitality and collection of paintings.

Grassou, flattered by the invitation, traveled to Ville d'Avray, where he was warmly welcomed by the Vervelles. They proudly showcased their well-kept grounds and prepared a lavish dinner, treating him like a celebrated artist. After the meal, they led him to their picture gallery, eager for his opinion on their collection, which they believed rivaled that of King Louis-Philippe.

As Grassou examined the paintings, he was shocked to recognize many of them as his own works, sold to the dealer Elie Magus. Vervelle boasted about the collection's value, claiming it was worth three hundred thousand francs. Grassou, realizing the irony, whispered to Vervelle that he had sold those paintings for far less.

Vervelle, impressed, promised to double his daughter's dowry if Grassou could prove his claim. Grassou's revelation only enhanced his standing with the Vervelles, who continued to regard him as a great artist. When he completed the portraits of the family, he gifted them, further solidifying their bond.

Today, Pierre Grassou is recognized as a fine portrait painter, earning around twenty thousand francs a year. He lives comfortably with his wife, who has a six thousand franc dowry, and they enjoy a happy life together. Grassou remains within the bourgeois circle, where he is considered a leading artist, painting family portraits for five hundred francs each. His reputation is bolstered by his financial success, and he has even been honored with the Legion of Honor for his contributions during civil unrest.

Despite his achievements, Grassou harbors insecurities, believing that other artists mock his work and that he is overlooked by critics. Nevertheless, he continues to strive for recognition, aiming for the Academy while secretly replacing his lesser works with those of more celebrated artists in his collection at Ville d'Avray. Grassou's mediocrity is tempered by his kindness and generosity, making him a complex figure in the artistic community.
