For several days, Pierre Fougeres lingered near Elie's shop, hoping for recognition of his painting, which ultimately vanished. When he inquired, Elie informed him it had sold and offered to buy more of his work. Overjoyed, Fougeres felt validated despite skepticism from others. Inspired, he returned to his studio and produced four new paintings, though they were critiqued as imitative.

Despite the criticism, Elie bought the paintings for a modest price, allowing Fougeres to live frugally while continuing to create art. After years of hard work, he gained recognition at the 1829 Exhibition, where a painting of a condemned man garnered significant attention and was purchased by Madame la Dauphine. This success marked a turning point in his career, leading to further commissions and a modest income.

Now financially stable, Fougeres furnished a studio and continued to produce art, remaining humble and dedicated to his craft. His friends admired his perseverance, even if they found his work lacking in originality. He maintained a simple lifestyle, investing his earnings with a notary, Cardot, while dreaming of greater artistic achievements.

One day, the wealthy Vervelle family approached Fougeres for portraits. Their admiration flattered him, especially as he became drawn to their daughter, Virginie. As he painted their portraits, he grew enchanted by her, despite initial reservations about their social status. The Vervelles, impressed by his reputation and stability, began to consider him a potential match for their daughter.

As the sittings progressed, the Vervelles grew more familiar with Fougeres, who reciprocated their enthusiasm. Their discussions about art solidified their interest in him, and the notary's positive remarks about his character further enhanced their favorable view, making them more inclined to see him as a suitable partner for Virginie. Caught up in the charm of the Vervelle family, Fougeres envisioned a future with her, rationalizing that marrying into the family could fulfill his dreams of love and success.

On the day Fougeres began painting Mademoiselle Virginie, he felt at home with the Vervelle family. During a sitting, Joseph Bridau burst in, frantic and disheveled, seeking 500 francs from Grassou, who was painting Virginie, claiming creditors were after him. The Vervelles were shocked by his chaotic entrance, but Grassou reluctantly agreed to help.

As Bridau critiqued Grassou's work, he urged him to embrace nature in his painting. After Bridau left, Madame Vervelle worried about the impact of his visit on Virginie's portrait, but Grassou reassured her it had improved. Eager to impress, Vervelle invited Fougeres to their country house.

Grassou accepted and was warmly welcomed at their villa in Ville d'Avray, where they showcased their beautiful grounds and prepared a lavish dinner. In their picture gallery, Grassou was astonished to find many of his own paintings misattributed to masters like Rubens and Rembrandt.

Feigning admiration, Grassou realized the extent of the deception. Vervelle boasted about the collection's value, while Grassou noted he had sold those paintings to dealer Elie Magus for a fraction of their worth. Impressed, Vervelle promised to double his daughter's dowry if Grassou could prove his claims.

Despite the absurdity, Grassou's reputation grew, and he became known as a fine portrait painter, earning a comfortable living and marrying into the Vervelle family. He lived happily with them, maintaining a successful career while grappling with the insecurity of being mocked by fellow artists. Aspiring to join the Academy, he secretly replaced his lesser works in the Vervelle collection with those of more renowned artists, navigating the complexities of mediocrity and ambition in the art world.
