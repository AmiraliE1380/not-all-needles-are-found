Have you felt unease and sadness in the crowded galleries since the revolution of 1830? Balzac argues the Salon has been swamped by quantity. Where careful selection once showcased the best and provoked debate—helping form reputations like Delacroix and Ingres—exhibitions now drown in numbers. Before 1817 only a few paintings were shown; today galleries overflow, individual works lose impact, shows close quickly, and the Salon’s role as arbiter of taste has become a tumultuous bazaar. Great artists are obscured by mediocrity, and talk of unrecognized genius grows when the Academy’s discerning judgment is absent.

Amid this swelling catalogue stands Pierre Grassou, nicknamed “Fougeres.” In 1832 he lives in a cramped rue de Navarin apartment with a modest studio overlooking Montmartre. Tidy and careful, Grassou is a genre painter who avoids grand historical subjects, producing easel-sized works suited to his means.

One December morning the dealer Elie Magus visits. Habitually buying Grassou’s pictures cheaply, Magus proposes a commission: portrait the bourgeois Vervelle family, who wish to enter the art world. Their daughter's substantial dowry and Magus’s hint that marriage might secure Grassou’s future startle him; he sees the commission as a chance to become a portraitist and improve his status.

Balzac traces Grassou’s past: he studied under Servin and Schinner but never mastered their techniques; his training brought little recognition and frequent ridicule. After a rejected 1819 Salon attempt Schinner advised him to quit; Grassou refined the piece and sold it to Magus for a meager sum, launching a long career of perseverance. His story embodies many contemporaries’ plight: ambition checked by limited talent, and the reality that circumstance and market forces often outweigh true merit.

For days Pierre Fougeres (later called Grassou) waited near Elie Magus’s shop hoping his painting would attract notice, but it was ignored. A week later it disappeared; when he asked Elie, the dealer said he had sold it and would buy more if Fougeres produced Flemish interiors, anatomy lessons, and landscapes. Overjoyed, Fougeres felt validated and missed the ironic distance a more cynical artist might have kept.

He met his friend Joseph Bridau but, absorbed in painting, could not enjoy the evening. Back home he sketched, bought supplies, hired models, and devoted himself to his craft. After two months he produced four canvases. Schinner and Bridau called them derivative and urged literature, but Fougeres retouched the works and sold them to Elie for twenty-five francs apiece, barely breaking even. He noticed a strange mist over his paintings in the shop but dismissed it.

Over seven years he developed competent, if unremarkable, work. Elie sold the pieces and Fougeres earned a modest living. At the 1829 Exhibition, with friends’ help, he entered a striking piece—a young man in prison inspired by Gerard Dou—which drew attention even from Charles X. Madame bought it and commissioned more.

Grassou’s success rested more on imitation than innovation; yet his modesty, perseverance, punctuality, and steadiness won supporters. He furnished a studio, completed commissions reliably, and by thirty-seven had produced around two hundred paintings in a style that pleased the bourgeoisie. Friends often preferred his character to his art and regretted his career choice, though he offered sound advice and kept a keen appreciation for beauty.

After the July Revolution he continued submitting to the Salon, lived frugally, and managed finances with his notary Cardot. He dreamed of a comfortable artistic life and aspired to join the Institute. One day the wealthy Vervelle family came for portraits; the father admired Grassou’s Legion of Honor cross, the family liked his work, and Pierre was charmed by their daughter Virginie. They began to regard him as a suitable match, blending personal hopes with professional ambitions.

While painting Virginie, Grassou became part of the Vervelle household. Near the end of a sitting Bridau burst in, disheveled, begged five hundred francs to meet creditors, and criticized Grassou’s style, urging acceptance of nature’s imperfections. Bridau left; Madame Vervelle feared he had spoiled the portrait, but Grassou assured her he had improved it.

Flattered, Grassou accepted an invitation to the Vervelles’ country house at Ville-d’Avray. They showed off their gallery, boasting its value at three hundred thousand francs. Grassou was shocked to find many canvases were his own works, long sold to Elie. Vervelle promised to double his daughter’s dowry if Grassou could prove the claim. The revelation raised Grassou’s standing; when he later completed their portraits he gifted them, securing their favor.

Today Pierre Grassou is a competent portrait painter, earning about twenty thousand francs a year, living on his wife’s six-thousand-franc dowry, painting bourgeois portraits for five hundred francs apiece, and wearing the Legion of Honor. Yet he remains insecure—sure other artists mock him and critics ignore him—secretly swapping weaker works in the Ville-d’Avray gallery for those by celebrated painters. His mediocrity, tempered by kindness and generosity, makes him oddly sympathetic.
