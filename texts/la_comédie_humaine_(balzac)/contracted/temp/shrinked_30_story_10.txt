In Paris, balls split into a formal round of pleasantries and a later, intimate meeting where genuine conversation and laughter flourish. The latter, hosted by women like Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches, preserves French wit and encourages candid exchange.

At one such evening in Mademoiselle des Touches’ salon, the Minister de Marsay recalled an early affair with a woman six years his senior: a secret relationship disguised by outward indifference and private signals in the flowers she carried. When illness forced him to cancel a meeting, jealousy drove him to visit anyway; he found her with another man. Her calm tenderness briefly quelled his doubts, but the sight ignited a storm of feeling. Confronted with betrayal, de Marsay felt the end of innocence, wrestled with revenge, then resolved that true love must transcend vengeance. His tale prompted reflections on love, jealousy, social expectation, and fragile trust.

Cured of naive passion, the narrator begins an affair with a charming heroine whose beauty contrasts sharply with the earlier deceptive angel. He avoids quarrels with this clever woman, suspecting that true love might not match the pleasures of her practiced deceit—refined hypocrisy can seem nearly as satisfying as virtue.

He visits a hair artist in the Rue Boucher to commission a keepsake. The artisan displays astonishing hair mementos and mentions passing trends like marking linen with hair. Suspicion flares when the narrator recognizes a handkerchief the artist has worked on, confirming doubts about the authenticity of women’s affections. He leaves resolved to enjoy pleasure but skeptical of love.

Two months later, in the heroine’s boudoir, he tests her. Charlotte professes exclusive devotion, claiming he is the only man in her world and that she sought ways to bind him. But when he bluntly asks, “When are you to marry the Duke?” her startled reaction betrays guilt. She feigns astonishment; he suggests she should accept the Duke—rich, devout, and fitting.

Charlotte falls to her knees, swearing before God that she loves only him. He presses her, confessing doubts about her fidelity; she counters that he no longer loves her and tries to leave, but curiosity draws her back to ask what he has seen. He reveals he noticed her attending mass with the Duke several times; she dismisses his jealousy. He advises her to seize the Duke’s offer, since marriage would elevate her and the Duke expects propriety. In tears she laments that had he spoken sooner they might have been happy, but he insists it is too late—she has gone too far.

She fears the Duke’s suspicions; he reassures her it’s unlikely but urges her to show devotion. As she alternates between supplication and composed entreaty, she vows never to forget him. He plays the distressed lover until she asks what will become of him; he then admits to having made advances to another woman. This surprises Charlotte, who accuses him of unfaithfulness; he replies that her wit exceeds her heart’s suffering. She proclaims disillusionment with love, asserting that women can never truly be loved; he retorts that taking love seriously in society is like romanticizing an actress. She accuses him of betrayal; he feigns submission and asks whether she will remember her grievances once she is a duchess. Her lingering bitterness convinces him he has successfully distanced himself—he has learned how successful men withdraw.

Back in the salon, the women express sympathy for Charlotte while de Marsay smiles slightly. Conversation turns to the erosion of social distinctions. Lady Dudley notes women often do not view marriages as acts of inconstancy. De Marsay laments the aristocracy’s decline, blaming legal and social changes for leveling hierarchies. Blondet quips that aristocracy now begins with viscountesses; de Marsay hopes countesses may survive.

The Comte de Vandenesse and Lord Dudley observe that the current order lets anyone claim nobility; unsettled property hastens the fall. Joseph Bridau laments a decline in womanly beauty; Daniel d’Arthez says the aristocracy’s retreat has deprived society of women who might have shaped it. Discussion turns to the modern “perfect lady”: tasteful, graceful, poised but lacking the grandeur of past great ladies. Mademoiselle des Touches questions whether this is progress; Madame de Nucingen argues worth now lies in character and presentation rather than lineage. Émile Blondet describes the perfect lady as effortlessly elegant and refined, commanding respect by bearing and demeanor across settings.

The dialogue highlights modern femininity’s complexities: the perfect lady skillfully navigates expectations with charm, blending beauty and intellect, compensating for—or reflecting—the vanished splendor of inherited rank.

Blondet opened with a jest about a certain modern married woman who manages always to speak of her husband without really revealing him. At a ball she bows to a middle-aged man and then, after bidding, orders her carriage and departs. She is not ostentatiously the rose, but has been with the rose; she sleeps under the golden canopy of a pleasant illusion. At home she admits no one before four and keeps callers waiting. Her house is tastefully arranged; luxury is for constant, daily use—no dusty shades or forgotten wrappings. The staircase is warm, flowers abound, and gifts are accepted only from chosen people because they imply a promise. Fashion trinkets lie about, not like museum pieces. She sits by the fire and does not rise to greet you. At a ball she is the creditor of pleasure; at home she owes you the pleasure of her wit. She cultivates charm to draw men into her drawing-room, seeking a man to widen her circle, an object for her attention. Conversation there feeds on generalities. All this, Blondet said, explains how isolated women are and why they crave a little world in which to shine.

De Marsay agreed: the epigram of the age—sharp words in miniature—now attacks transient events rather than enduring people or things. Blondet added that the intelligent modern woman adopts doubt as her chief faculty. Compare two women: a townswoman is straightforwardly virtuous; the “lady” doubts and contends with virtue. This perpetual uncertainty is one of her last graces. She rarely goes to church but will debate religion; when free thought is expressed she will don the language of conversion and moral concern—“For shame! I thought you had too much sense to attack religion. Society is tottering; religion means you and me; it is property and the future of our children!”—slipping into a muddle of neo-Christian, political, and sentimental platitudes that satisfy neither Catholic nor Protestant orthodoxy but sound very moral.

The women laughed at Blondet’s satire. He continued: the “perfect lady” embodies intellectual and political confusion, surrounded by flashy but ephemeral products of modern industry. She will seem to have had superior ideas because she has artfully probed you and feigned ignorance to learn everything; she is skilled in concealment. The great ladies of old flaunted affairs; today’s woman arranges passions like a musical score—notes and rests, pauses and sharps. A merely weak woman fears compromising her love, husband, or children. Name, position, and fortune no longer shield her; aristocracy no longer protects. Lacking the old grandeur that could crush, she masters Jesuitical compromises and moves between propriety and anonymous passion. She fears servants as an Englishwoman fears divorce trials; free at a ball and charming on a walk, she is a slave at home—never independent except in privacy or theory. Her task is to preserve her position as a lady.

Blondet painted the desperate in-betweenness of a woman repudiated by her husband and reduced to a meager allowance: she is neither wife, maid, nor townswoman—adrift, a chattel. The Carmelites will not accept a married woman; it would be bigamy. Will her lover still desire her? The perfect lady may give rise to calumny, but never to the coarse slander of past times.

“This sketch,” Blondet told Count Adam Mitgislas, “shows that the perfect lady is a product of an age in which everything is transitional and indistinct; all great figures fade into the background; distinction becomes merely personal. It is impossible, even near a throne, to acquire before twenty-five that encyclopedic practice in trifles—manœuvring, small but vital arts of tone and silence, the angelic mischief and innocent cunning—that made the great lady of the past.”

Mademoiselle des Touches asked where the female author would stand in Blondet’s taxonomy: perfect lady or woman comme il faut? Blondet replied that when she has no genius she is a woman comme il n’en faut pas—a quip he ascribed to Napoleon, prompting Canalis to defend Napoleon from jealousy toward literary genius and to offer a paradoxical, expansive portrait of him.

Canalis described Napoleon as a man of contradictions—arms folded yet acting, concentrated and caustic, a phenomenal will who spread armed civilization without settling it. He could conquer illness by the force of will, yet was fated to die of disease in bed. He had a code and a sword in his brain, did works of law and arms; he foresaw many things yet could not foresee his fall. Capricious in politics, he mingled grand calculations with petty mercies—spared three heads whose deaths might have saved the Empire. Nature placed a heart in his brazen frame: mirthful and tender among women at midnight, and the next morning manipulating Europe like a child at play. He loved both tawdriness and simplicity, had no taste yet protected the arts, improvised empires and codes, and ultimately aimed to make Europe French—leaving brilliance without permanence. A man of action and thought who both understood and used subordinates like Desaix and Fouché, and whose despotism and justice at propitious moments made him, to Canalis, a true king.

De Marsay declared him “all despotism and all justice at the right moments.” Joseph Bridau interrupted with the observation that conversation here was priceless—if you had to pay for the charm of a repartee as for music, one’s fortune would be insufficient: no twinkling wit repeats itself.

The Princesse de Cadignan defended contemporary women. Under a regime that makes everything small, people still love great passions; changing fashions do not alter the human heart. There is beautiful devotion and sublime suffering without the old public spectacle. Our modern women—Madame Récamier, Marquise d’Espard, Taglioni, Malibran—have their equivalents among historical beauties and artists. Though the Grocers govern and we lack a style of our own, posterity will still find our women proportionate and our poets able. The Comte de Vandenesse agreed: when historians look back, Paris will show a thousand Mme de Sévignés; Frenchwomen will continue to be “the woman among women.” Blondet’s portrait had captured a type that could, when needed, be heroic; the petty faults of contemporary women are rendered poetic by the larger perils of the age. Providence, said Vandenesse, often compensates apparent lightness with overwhelming effects.

The women teased Vandenesse and praised his example, and General Montriveau, who had recently spoken of dramas touched by Providence, now declared he had been involuntarily the cause of a terrible disaster during the 1812 campaign—an incident he recounted at the ladies’ urging, calling in Doctor Bianchon because the story touched on the will and mind.

General Montriveau’s tale: on his second campaign he loved danger like a young, reckless artillery lieutenant. When they reached the Beresina the army had lost discipline: a chaotic migration of men of many nations. He had wandered from the marshes of Zembin in search of shelter and found a wretched Polish farm—one room, a partitioned forage corner—where smoke showed life at twilight. Inside, a table was set; officers and a woman were eating potatoes, some horseflesh, and frozen beetroots. Artillery captains from his old regiment welcomed him with shouts and bread; the straw on the floor promised rest. Among them sat his old colonel, an extraordinary Italian man of strength, charm, and fits of furious temper.

The colonel was tall, powerful, with abundant black curls and a white complexion, small hands and an aquiline nose that turned white with rage. His irascibility was legendary; his voice rolled gutturally and could make orders thunder. Yet when calm his blue eyes were sweet and his brow smooth. Everything about him was a contrast—passion based on contrast—and he possessed an irresistible influence not through gallantry but through force of character. He could be sublime but was also a man consumed by rage, jealous affections, and a sense of personal honor.

Montriveau recalled an earlier episode that illustrated the colonel’s violence: two artillery regiments met on a narrow road. When a rival colonel ordered forward and struck his captain’s leg, breaking it, the Italian colonel sprang, drew his sword, and split the other colonel’s skull in two seconds. The soldiers backed away.

At the farmhouse beyond Zembin, a captain sat near the door and his wife—Rosina, a pale but still-charming Sicilian—sat facing the colonel. Rosina was dark, with almond eyes and a small mouth; poverty had thinned but not erased her grace. Her husband, a Piedmontese gentleman, had a phlegmatic manner masking cruelty. The colonel’s attention to Rosina had been obvious in prior months. When the colonel, after finishing his meal, called “Rosina?” and went to sleep in the hay, the listener understood his intent. Rosina made a slight gesture expressing indignation at this flaunted thralldom, but kept her place. The colonel called again more brutally; Rosina, pale, rose and went behind them to the colonel. The room fell silent; Montriveau, unfortunate, began to laugh and was soon joined by the others. The husband challenged, but the younger men joked away the awkwardness, and finally all lay down and slept ignoring tensions.

Next morning they set off, each selfishly seeking his own survival. Some distance from the farmhouse, most of the men rejoined in company from necessity. From a knoll they saw the farmhouse barricaded and in flames, noises like a roar, mingled with feeble screams and the pungent smell of burning. A few yards behind, the captain whom they had seen earlier approached quietly to rejoin the caravan. He pointed to his breast with his right forefinger and, with his left hand waving toward the fire, said, “Son’io” (I am the one). None of the men spoke to him; they walked on silent. The implication was that in revenge for the colonel’s affront—or perhaps for other private reasons—the captain had set his own home ablaze, sacrificing his shelter and its occupants rather than bear the insult. Montriveau left the detail to their imaginations, concluding with the somber image of men moving on amid horror and self-seeking. “There is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep,” de Marsay remarked; Madame de Montcornet said the picture would haunt her dreams.

The conversation lightened briefly with an English jest about foils, but soon returned to the theme of tragic intimacy. De Marsay called on Doctor Bianchon, who had witnessed a noble death, to tell of the Duchess’s end. Bianchon described being called to a consumption case in which the Duke and he had spent the night by the dying woman’s pillow. The Duchess, having taken the sacrament the day before, was very thin but with a sublime outline to her face; her pallor made her skin seem like luminous porcelain, her bright eyes full of expressive calm. The Duke, asleep more than might please a man near power, had been left a little while; waking, he came to sit by her side. The Duchess, reaching out in faintly agitated voice, took his hand and said, “My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?” Then she died looking at him. Bianchon noted the scene’s majesty and tenderness; the story left a deep impression but a sweet one, Mademoiselle des Touches observed.

The company’s talk had thus ranged from ironic social anatomy to grand historical paradox and private tragedy: Blondet’s satire of the modern “perfect lady,” Canalis’s encomium to Napoleon’s contradictory genius, the Princesse’s defense of contemporary women’s greatness, Vandenesse’s praise of their place in history, and Montriveau’s vivid wartime moral tableau culminating in the farmhouse-burning and in the quiet, noble deathbed scene described by Bianchon.

PARIS, June 1839–42.
