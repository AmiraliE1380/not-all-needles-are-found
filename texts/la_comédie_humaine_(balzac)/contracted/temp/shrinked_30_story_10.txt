In Paris, balls often split into a formal round of polite small talk and a later, intimate salon where genuine conversation and wit flourish—hosted by women like Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches. At one such evening, guests including the Minister de Marsay shared lively discourse.

De Marsay told of a youthful, passionate affair with a woman six years older who outwardly seemed indifferent but privately signaled affection with the flowers she carried. When illness made him cancel a meeting, jealousy drove him there anyway and he found her with another man. Her calm tenderness briefly soothed him, but the discovery ignited a storm: his innocence felt ended, he wrestled with thoughts of revenge, and concluded that true love must rise above vengeance—prompting reflection on love, jealousy, social expectation, and fragile trust.

Freed from naïveté, the narrator began a new affair with a charming heroine whose practiced beauty contrasted with his earlier deceptive angel. He avoided quarrels, suspecting refined hypocrisy could masquerade as virtue and fearing genuine love might not match the pleasures of her practiced deceit. Visiting a hair artisan in the Rue Boucher to commission a keepsake, he saw astonishing hair mementos and learned of passing trends. Suspicion flared when he recognized a handkerchief the artisan had worked on, confirming doubts about the authenticity of women’s affections. He left resolved to pursue pleasure but skeptical of love.

Two months later in the heroine’s boudoir he tested her. Charlotte professed exclusive devotion and claimed he was the only man in her world, but when he bluntly asked, “When are you to marry the Duke?” her startled reaction betrayed guilt. He suggested she should accept the Duke—rich, devout, proper. Kneeling, she swore before God she loved only him; he pressed her, revealing he'd seen her attend mass with the Duke. She alternately supplicated and raged, lamenting that had he spoken sooner they might have been happy. He insisted it was too late—that she had gone too far.

She feared the Duke’s suspicions; he counseled her to show devotion while he feigned the distressed lover until she asked his fate. Then he admitted making advances to another woman, discomfiting Charlotte, who accused him of unfaithfulness. He retorted that taking love seriously in their society was like romanticizing an actress. When she could not shake bitterness even as a potential duchess, he felt he had successfully distanced himself—having learned how successful men withdraw.

Back in the salon, the women sympathized with Charlotte while de Marsay smiled. Conversation turned to the erosion of social distinctions: Lady Dudley observed that women often did not view marriages as inconstancy; de Marsay lamented the aristocracy’s decline—duchesses and marquises fading as the Civil Code leveled hierarchies and created a new class. The Comte de Vandenesse and Lord Dudley noted anyone could claim nobility under the new order; unsettled property hastened the fall. Joseph Bridau lamented a decline in womanly beauty; Daniel d’Arthez argued society lost women who might have shaped it.

They debated the modern “perfect lady”: tasteful, graceful, poised but lacking the grandeur of past great ladies. Mademoiselle des Touches questioned whether this was progress; Madame de Nucingen argued a woman’s worth now lay more in character and presentation than lineage. Émile Blondet defined the perfect lady as effortlessly elegant and refined, commanding respect by bearing and demeanor. The dialogue captured modern femininity’s complexity—how charm, intellect, and presentation now often replace inherited splendor.

Blondet begins with a caustic portrait of the modern “perfect lady”: married yet effectively concealing her husband, she performs delight rather than possession. At a ball a decorated middle-aged man bows and departs; she orders her carriage and leaves, leaving an impression of brightness instead of conjugal solidarity. At home she keeps strict hours, receives no one before afternoon, and stages her life—warm staircases, fresh flowers (accepted only from favored suitors as tokens), tasteful comforts, and ever-renewed trifles. She sits by the fire and lets visitors come to her; in public she dazzles, in private she calculates. Her salon becomes a tiny world where conversation clings to safe generalities because women are isolated and hungry for a sphere in which to shine.

Blondet contends that a woman’s intelligence in this milieu often shows as cultivated doubt: uncertainty becomes a social grace. The straightforward townswoman is openly virtuous; the lady doubts and negotiates virtue, debates religion without practicing it, and invokes moral rhetoric when it suits social aims. Her speech becomes a tangle of modern political and religious catchphrases—neo-Christian, neither fully Catholic nor Protestant—moralistic and self-contradictory. Women laugh at Blondet’s satire, but he persists: the “perfect lady” feigns ignorance to extract confidences and arranges her passions like a musical score—careful pauses, modulations, anonymous compromises—because exposure threatens ruin. Name, rank, and wealth no longer guarantee safety; the old aristocratic grandeur that let great ladies dominate has faded. The modern woman must master subtleties and Jesuitical compromises, living between public propriety and private liberties. She is seldom independent except in secrecy or theory; her chief task is to preserve her status.

Blondet imagines a repudiated wife deprived of carriage, opera box, and social role: neither wife, maid, nor townswoman—adrift and vulnerable to calumny, with religious orders rejecting her as scandalous. Thus the modern lady may provoke gossip but seldom outright slander because her cautious arrangements offer more protection than frankness ever did. Blondet judges the contemporary blend of English hypocrisy and eighteenth-century frankness a degraded transitional system: present manners resemble nothing that has truly vanished. Great figures are receding; distinctions have become merely personal. He doubts that any woman, even of high birth, can before twenty-five acquire the encyclopedic refinements—subtlety of manner, wit, innocence, mischief, silence, speech, diplomacy, and artful balance—that made the perfect lady of the past.

Mademoiselle des Touches asks where the female author fits. Blondet replies with a stinging quip—an ungenial female author is “a woman of whom there should not be one,” an echo of Napoleon. Canalis interrupts to defend Napoleon against the epigram, recognizing his paradoxical genius: a phenomenal will who created empires, laws, public works, and literature, yet remained capricious, contradictory, and humanly weak. Canalis portrays Napoleon as brute force coupled with subtle intellect—able to found codes and crowns, to seize Europe, yet unable to secure lasting civilization—and admires the extraordinary, if inconsistent, greatness.

De Marsay calls him “all despotism and all justice” at the right moments, “the true king.” Joseph Bridau observes that witty conversation is a rare, fleeting luxury—each flash unique and unrepeatable, like music one could not afford if sold by the note. The Princesse de Cadignan challenges Blondet’s caricature: fashions and small forms do not mean the human heart has shrunk. Passions and sublime devotions persist, though less publicly staged. The lack of a grand public theater does not diminish depth. Contemporary women may rival historical figures—Madame Récamier beside Mme. de Sévigné; Marquise d’Espard with Madame Doublet or du Deffand; Taglioni and Malibran with Camargo and Saint-Huberti; modern poets matching or exceeding eighteenth-century ones. The Empire had its own splendor. Comte de Vandenesse amplifies this defense, praising Frenchwomen’s enduring significance and asserting that Blondet’s “perfect lady” can, when necessary, become heroic. Providence sometimes directs events where feelings cross legal bounds, and Vandenesse has seen women rise to greatness beyond their faults.

This praise provokes convivial banter and then leads to General Montriveau’s grim story, which reveals the brutal realities behind honor, passion, and conscience. He recounts an episode during the 1812 retreat near the Beresina, when discipline collapsed and survivors sought shelter. Montriveau, cold and starving, found a poor Polish farmhouse—one room piled with hay and a small meal laid out. Among the officers gathered there was a colonel who dominates the tale: Italian by temperament, tall, powerful, dark-haired, alternately tender-eyed and ferociously irascible, able to inspire both fear and devotion. His passions erupted in swift violence: when another colonel’s battery struck their captain and badly wounded him, Montriveau watched the Italian colonel lose control—foaming, roaring, and in two seconds split the other man’s skull with his sword. The soldiers were stunned.

Present also was the wounded captain’s wife, Rosina, a striking but ragged Sicilian. Her husband—a phlegmatic Piedmontese—had seemed oblivious to scandal, but Montriveau sensed tension. At the farmhouse the colonel summoned Rosina with brusque possessiveness; she responded with a gesture of humiliation or foreboding. When he summoned her again more brutally, she rose and went to him amid awkward silence; Montriveau’s nervous laugh drew a rebuke from the husband. They slept that night and resumed the dreadful march next morning. A short distance on, they saw the farmhouse in flames, barricaded, the air full of smoke and cries. The captain returned to the caravan and, with a pointed gesture to his breast and a wave toward the burning house, said “Son’io” (I am the one). He walked on; no one pressed him. Montriveau’s account leaves the precise deed implied but unstated—an act of vengeance or desperate protest—illustrating how easily dignity is crushed and how humiliation can provoke savage reactions. De Marsay comments bitterly that “there is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep,” an image that haunts the company.

Conversation lightens with laughter at English foibles and then closes on a softer note from Doctor Bianchon. He recounts a moving bedside scene: a duchess dying of consumption, thin and luminous, having taken the sacrament. The Duke—an immense public figure, tired and complex—had fallen asleep at her bedside; when he awoke about dawn, despairing at his lapse and fearing he had missed her last moments, the dying duchess, in a tenderly altered voice, took his hand and said, “My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?” She died looking at him. Bianchon calls her end one of the most beautiful he has seen; the Duke’s sorrow and the serenity of the death reveal a private, sacramental tenderness that outlasts display.

The assembled company reflects on these vignettes: Blondet’s satire, Canalis’s defense of Napoleon, the Princesse’s praise of women, Montriveau’s wartime violence, and Bianchon’s bedside charity together sketch a society where appearances, passions, public roles, and private heroics interweave. Women, constrained by manners and rank, still have depth and capacity for sublimity; men of genius display paradoxes of grandeur and triviality; sudden violence and rare tenderness punctuate public life. The dialogues and episodes portray contemporary Parisian life—its hypocrisies, private loyalties, sudden cruelties, and occasional nobility.
