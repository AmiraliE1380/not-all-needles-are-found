Doctor Monsieur Bianchon recounts the haunting tale of La Grande Bretèche, a dilapidated house near Vendôme, surrounded by a wild garden that hints at its former beauty. The house's decay suggests a tragic history, and Bianchon imagines it as a refuge for a horticulturally passionate gentleman. The overgrown garden symbolizes lost joy, while the house's ruin raises questions about its abandonment.

During his stay in Vendôme, Bianchon becomes captivated by the house and often sneaks into the garden, allowing his imagination to weave stories of its past. One evening, he meets local notary Monsieur Regnault, who reveals that the house belongs to the estate of the late Comtesse de Merret. Regnault explains that her will forbids anyone from entering or repairing the house for fifty years after her death, deepening its mystery.

Regnault shares the Comtesse's tragic story: after her husband, the Count de Merret, died, she became reclusive, leaving La Grande Bretèche in disarray, leading to rumors about her mental state. On her deathbed, she summoned Regnault to witness her will, intending to leave her estate to the hospital in Vendôme with strict conditions regarding the house.

Bianchon reflects on the Comtesse's life and the fate of La Grande Bretèche, becoming engrossed in local gossip. His landlady, Madame Lepas, reveals more about the Comtesse and her husband, portraying a once-happy couple whose relationship soured due to the Count's temper and the Comtesse's beauty. She also shares a secret about a young Spanish prisoner who stayed at her inn during the war, hinting at connections to the Comtesse's fate.

The narrative explores themes of love, loss, and memory, prompting readers to consider how the past shapes the present. In a parallel story, a woman recounts the mysterious disappearance of Count Férédia, a Spanish Grandee. After he fails to return one night, she finds a letter and valuables he left behind, intended for masses of gratitude for his escape. Her husband searches for him and discovers the Count's clothes near the river, suggesting he escaped.

The story darkens as the woman suspects the Count's involvement in Madame de Merret's death, grappling with the moral dilemma of keeping his money. Intrigued by Rosalie, the maid who knows secrets about Madame de Merret's death, the narrator seeks the truth. Rosalie reveals that Madame de Merret had been ill, and her husband confronted her one night, leading to a tragic outcome. Believing in her purity, Monsieur de Merret chose not to open a cupboard where a man was hidden, ultimately confining his wife to her room for twenty days, leaving her in despair. The story concludes with the women reflecting on the moral complexities and emotional turmoil within La Grande Bretèche.
