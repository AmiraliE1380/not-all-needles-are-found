“Ah! madame,” said the doctor, “I have appalling stories; but each has its proper hour—Chamfort told the Duc de Fronsac, ‘Between your sally and the present moment lie ten bottles of champagne.’”

“But it is two in the morning, and Rosina’s story has prepared us,” said the mistress of the house.

“Tell us, Monsieur Bianchon!” cried everyone.

The doctor bowed and began.

About a hundred paces from Vendôme, on the Loir, stands an old brown house with very high roofs, isolated without even a tannery or tavern nearby. In front is a garden sloping to the river; neat box borders and espaliered fruit trees have run wild; willows in the stream half hide the house. An arbor’s wreck and a table remain. One wall bears a sundial with the motto “Ultimam cogita.” The roof is dilapidated; shutters closed; balconies hung with swallows’ nests; doors forever shut. Weeds and rust mark the stonework; moon and weather have eaten the wood. Only birds, cats, polecats, rats, and mice disturb the silence. The whole scene seems stamped with a single word: Mystery.

From the street a gate riddled with holes reveals a courtyard of cracked walls, disjointed steps, and broken gutters. What calamity befell it? What curse rests here? The empty house is an enigma.

Once a small fief called La Grande Bretèche, it became the doctor’s secret pleasure during his stay in Vendôme. While attending a rich patient he found the unrevealed thought of the dwelling irresistible. He often climbed the wild hedge into the ownerless garden, scratched by briars, lingering for hours, inventing romances and melancholy to match the scene; had he known the real cause of the neglect the unwritten poetry would have been lost. The house suggested provincial life, contemplative and grave-like; sometimes he imagined lepers there, sometimes the Atridae; he wept there; he never laughed.

Often involuntary terrors seized him—a wood-pigeon’s hum sounding ominous; damp earth and reptiles requiring caution; an icy chill settling as if a hand had lain on his shoulder. One evening a weathercock’s creak made him shudder; finishing a gloomy drama, he returned to his inn lost in shadowy thought. After supper his hostess entered with an air of mystery and announced, “Monsieur, here is Monsieur Regnault.”

“Who is Monsieur Regnault?” he asked.

The hostess left and a tall, slim man in black appeared—hat in hand, with a receding forehead and a colorless face. His coat was shabby, but he wore a diamond in his shirt frill and gold rings in his ears.

“Monsieur,” the doctor asked, “whom have I the honor of addressing?”

“Dear me, it is very cold. I am Monsieur Regnault,” the man answered, rubbing his hands. “I am notary at Vendôme.”

Regnault warned the doctor that walking in La Grande Bretèche’s garden was trespass and, as executor under the will of the late Comtesse de Merret, begged him to discontinue the practice. Bound to the testatrix’s directions, he could not allow repairs or entry. He had never set foot in the house since the will was read; they had merely verified doors and windows to assess taxes. “Ah! my dear sir,” he said, “her will made a great commotion in the town.”

Once a head-clerk in a Paris office ruined by bankruptcy, Regnault had come to Vendôme after failing to buy a Paris practice. He married into local ties; three months after being licensed he was summoned one evening by Madame la Comtesse de Merret to her château. She had been living at Merret after the Count went to Paris and died miserably two months before Regnault arrived. The Count and Countess had behaved eccentrically for months, admitting no visitors; Madame lived on the ground floor and Monsieur on the first; she was little seen except at church and had become altered. People whispered she was not quite right. When she left La Grande Bretèche for Merret she had dismantled the house and, it was said, burned furniture.

Curiosity brought Regnault to the château that night. The maid had told him the Countess had received the Sacrament and might not live through the night. He crossed great, lofty, dark, cold rooms to the state bedroom. The chamber was old-fashioned, papered in brown, diabolically cold and thick with dust; a single old Argand lamp lighted it and a high tester bed hung with chintz dominated the scene. A small table bore an “Imitation of Christ” and the lamp; a deep armchair and two small chairs completed the scant furnishing. There was no fire. The room seemed a stage for a funeral.

Under the lamp, Madame de Merret lay in the bed. She was yellow as wax and narrow as two folded hands; her lace cap showed abundant but white hair. Sitting up with difficulty, her large black eyes were dimmed by fever; her forehead clammy; her fleshless hands like bones beneath soft skin—veins and muscles visible. She had once been handsome; now she was a phantom. Those who wrapped her later said none had ever lived so emaciated. Her pale violet lips barely moved when she spoke.

Although Regnault had often tended the dying, the lonely, silent woman in her vast château affected him deeply. He stood motionless, absorbed in her. At last her large eyes moved; with great effort she tried to raise her right hand and murmured in a breath, “I have waited for you with the greatest impatience.” A bright flush rose on her cheeks; speaking was a great effort.

The housekeeper whispered, “Do not speak; Madame la Comtesse is not in a state to bear the slightest noise.” Regnault sat. After a few moments the Countess summoned strength to slide her right hand under the bolster and, with a last effort, withdrew a sealed paper, drops of perspiration on her brow. “I place my will in your hands—Oh! God! Oh!” Her hand seized a crucifix; she lifted it to her lips and died.

The expression in her eyes haunted Regnault; joy had been in her last glance. He brought away the will. When opened, it named him executor and left the whole property to the Vendôme hospital except for a few legacies. As for La Grande Bretèche, it ordered that the house be left, for fifty years from her death, in the state it then occupied: absolutely no entry into the apartments, no repairs of any sort, and watchmen to enforce the decree if necessary. After fifty years, if her will had been faithfully executed, the house would become the property of Regnault’s heirs; otherwise it reverts to legal heirs only after complying with conditions in a codicil sealed until the expiration of the fifty years. The will had not been contested.

Regnault’s recital made the doctor feel he still saw the dying woman. When asked whether he knew the motives behind the testatrix’s strange directions, Regnault, with comic reticence, refused to criticize a benefactor who had given him a diamond. Still, the doctor soon coaxed from him the town’s gossip—contradictory and diffuse opinions that nearly put him to sleep. The notary left pleased, making a wry joke on the stairs about living forty-five years longer and taking a pinch of snuff.

Later, the innkeeper, Madame Lepas, a buxom Flemish woman with an innkeeper’s quick scrutiny, asked if Regnault had told the story of La Grande Bretèche. When the doctor repeated the tale, she leaned forward eagerly. He suspected she knew more than she first let on; she confessed she had a secret that tormented her conscience and wished to consult him, believing he was a man of judgment and incapable of betraying her. Though she swore she would not tell what could compromise him, she asked him to listen.

Madame Lepas then related another thread. When the Emperor sent Spaniards and prisoners to the region, she had been required to lodge, at the government’s expense, a young Spaniard on parole who had to present himself daily to the sub-prefect. He was a Spanish grandee—Bagos de Férédia, she recorded his name in her books. He was a handsome little man, barely five-foot-two or three, well made, with small, delicate hands he groomed carefully; thick black hair, a flame in his eye, a somewhat coppery complexion she admired despite prejudice against Spanish looks. He wore the finest linen she had ever seen—even finer than that of the nobles and princes she had lodged. He ate little but had polite, amiable ways. He spoke little and rarely answered, which she attributed to a Spanish mannerism.

He read his breviary like a priest and attended mass regularly, always taking a place within two yards of Madame de Merret’s chapel. At first no one suspected a purpose. He never looked up from his book. In the evenings he walked among the ruins of the old castle on the hill—his only amusement, which reminded him, people said, of his native Spain. “They say that Spain is all hills!” she concluded.

One evening soon after he arrived the Spaniard stayed out late. I was uneasy when he didn’t return until midnight, but we had learned to accept his whims; he took the key and we didn’t wait. He lived in a house we owned on Rue des Casernes. One of our stable-boys thought he had seen the Spanish Grandee swimming in the river. When he did come back, I warned him about the weeds, and he seemed annoyed at being seen.

Eventually, one morning we discovered he hadn’t returned at all. Searching his belongings, I found a note in his drawer and fifty doubloons—about five thousand francs—plus a sealed box of diamonds worth ten thousand francs. The note said that if he didn’t come back, he left this money and the diamonds to fund masses in gratitude for his escape.

My husband went looking for him and, curiously, returned with the Spaniard’s clothes, found under a stone on a breakwater near La Grande Bretèche. He went early, unseen. After reading the letter, he burned the clothes and, following Count Férédia’s wishes, we announced the Spaniard had escaped.

The sub-prefect sent the constabulary after him, but he was never caught. Lepas suspected the Spaniard had drowned. I, however, thought he was linked to the affair of Madame de Merret, especially since Rosalie told me the crucifix her mistress had cherished and been buried with was ebony and silver; early in his stay Monsieur Férédia had a similar crucifix, which I never saw again. Now, monsieur, should I feel remorse about the Spaniard’s fifteen thousand francs? Aren’t they truly mine?

“Certainly. But have you ever questioned Rosalie?” I asked.

“Oh, I have, sir. But what can be done? That girl is like a wall. She knows something, but it’s impossible to make her talk.”

After a brief talk my hostess left me with vague sinister thoughts, romantic curiosity, and a religious dread—the feeling one has in entering a dark church at night, with a faint light under a high vault. La Grande Bretèche—with its overgrown grass, shuttered windows, rusty ironwork, and empty rooms—appeared vividly. I felt compelled to explore that mysterious house to reach the heart of this solemn story, this drama that had claimed three lives.

Rosalie became the most intriguing person in Vendôme to me. Watching her, I sensed something inward beneath her robust health: an element of sorrow or hope, as if her soul held a secret. Yet she was simple and clumsy, her vacant smile free of criminality; she looked innocent in her large checked kerchief and lilac-and-white-striped gown. I resolved not to leave Vendôme without learning La Grande Bretèche’s full story. To do so I would make advances with Rosalie if necessary.

“Rosalie!” I called one evening.

“Your servant, sir?”

“You’re not married?” She flinched.

“Oh! There’s no shortage of men if I ever fancy being miserable!” she laughed, quickly regaining composure.

“Yes; you’re attractive enough not to lack suitors! But tell me, Rosalie, why did you become an inn-servant after leaving Madame de Merret? Didn’t she leave you a small annuity?”

“Oh yes, sir. But my position here is the best in all Vendôme.”

Her reply seemed evasive. Rosalie appeared central to this romance; she seemed bound to its core. It wasn’t an ordinary courtship affair—this girl held the last chapter of a romance. From that moment my attention fixed on her. By studying her I noticed many admirable qualities; she was clean, neat, and undeniably attractive, soon embodying all the charms desire can give a woman, whatever her station. A fortnight after the notary’s visit, one morning I said to Rosalie:

“Come, tell me all you know about Madame de Merret.”

“Oh!” she replied, “I will tell you, but keep it a secret.”

“Of course, my child; I will guard your secrets with a thief’s honor, the most loyal known.”

“If it’s all the same to you,” she said, “I’d prefer it be with your own.”

She adjusted her head-kerchief and settled to tell the tale; one needs a certain attitude to tell a story. The best tales are told at a proper hour—just as we are here at table. No one tells a story well standing or fasting.

If I reproduced Rosalie’s diffuse eloquence, a whole volume would scarce suffice. Since the event she recounted sits between the notary’s gossip and Madame Lepas’s account, I will summarize it succinctly.

Madame de Merret’s bedchamber at La Grande Bretèche was on the ground floor. A small built-in cupboard about four feet deep served as her wardrobe. Three months before the evening in question, Madame de Merret had been so ill that her husband had begun sleeping on the first floor. One evening he returned two hours later than usual from the club, delayed by animated discussions and losses at billiards. His wife assumed he was in bed. For some time Monsieur de Merret had relied on Rosalie to tell him if his wife was in bed; her affirmative replies led him to his room, trusting her completely. That evening, however, he went to check on his wife to share his misfortunes and perhaps find comfort; he had noticed her especially lovely at dinner and thought her recovery had enhanced her beauty.

Upon entering, he thought he heard the closet door shut, but when he entered, Madame de Merret stood alone by the fireplace. He felt a suspicion, and noticed anxiety in her eyes.

“You’re very late,” she said, her voice slightly husky.

Monsieur de Merret remained silent as Rosalie entered, which startled him. He began pacing with folded arms.

“Have you received bad news, or are you unwell?” his wife asked timidly while Rosalie helped her undress. He did not answer.

“You can go, Rosalie,” Madame de Merret said; “I can manage my curl-papers myself.” She wanted to be alone with her husband, sensing disaster. Once Rosalie left—or was thought to have left, for she lingered in the passage—Monsieur de Merret confronted his wife, declaring coldly, “Madame, there’s someone in your cupboard!” She calmly replied, “No, monsieur.”

This “No” pierced Monsieur de Merret’s heart; he did not believe it, yet his wife looked more saintly than ever. He moved toward the closet door, but Madame de Merret took his hand and stopped him. In strange emotion she said, “Remember, if you find no one there, everything must end between us.”

The dignity of her stance filled him with deep respect and inspired a resolve that could be described as fierce.

“No, Josephine,” he said, “I won’t open it. Regardless of the outcome, we would be forever parted. I know your soul’s purity; you wouldn’t commit a deadly sin to save your life.” At these words Madame de Merret looked haggard. “Here is your crucifix,” he continued. “Swear to me before God that there’s no one in that closet; I will believe you—I will never open that door.”

Madame de Merret took the crucifix and swore, “I swear it.”

“Louder,” he insisted; “repeat: ‘I swear before God that there is nobody in that closet.’” She repeated the words without flinching.

“That will do,” said Monsieur de Merret coldly. After a pause he added, “You have a fine piece of work there, which I’ve never seen before,” examining the ebony-and-silver crucifix.

“I found it at Duvivier’s; last year when that troop of Spanish prisoners passed through Vendôme he bought it from a Spanish monk.”

“Indeed,” said Monsieur de Merret, hanging the crucifix back on its nail, and he rang for Rosalie.

He waited for her to come. “Go to bed, all of you,” he told the household, and called Jean, his coachman and confidential servant, close. He whispered, “When they’re all asleep—mind, asleep—you understand?—come down and tell me.”

Monsieur de Merret kept his gaze on his wife as he gave orders, returning to her at the fireside to discuss billiards and the club. When Rosalie returned she found them conversing amiably.

Not long before, Monsieur de Merret had had new ceilings put in all the ground-floor reception rooms. Plaster was scarce in Vendôme, and he had ordered a large quantity, knowing he could sell any surplus. This circumstance supplied the means for his plan.

“Gorenflot is here, sir,” Rosalie whispered.

“Tell him to come in,” he said aloud.

Madame de Merret paled at the sight of the mason.

“Gorenflot,” her husband said, “go fetch some bricks from the coach-house; bring enough to wall up the cupboard door; you can use the leftover plaster as cement.” Pulling Rosalie and the mason close, he whispered, “Listen, Gorenflot, you’ll sleep here tonight; tomorrow morning I’ll give you a passport to go abroad to a place I’ll specify. I’ll give you six thousand francs for your journey. You must live in that town for ten years; if you don’t like it you may settle elsewhere, but it must be in the same country. Go through Paris and wait there until I join you. I’ll give you an agreement for six thousand francs more, paid on your return if you fulfill the conditions. In return, you must keep silent about what you do tonight. Rosalie, I’ll secure ten thousand francs for you, payable on your wedding day, provided you marry Gorenflot; but to marry, you must stay silent. If not, no wedding gift!”

“Rosalie,” Madame de Merret said, “come and brush my hair.”

Her husband paced quietly, watching the door, the mason, and his wife without revealing suspicion. Gorenflot made some noise. Madame de Merret caught a moment when he was unloading bricks to whisper to Rosalie: “My dear child, I’ll give you a thousand francs a year if you tell Gorenflot to leave a crack at the bottom.” Then she added casually, “You’d better help him.”

Monsieur and Madame de Merret kept silent while Gorenflot began walling the cupboard. Their silence was deliberate; the husband did not want to give his wife any ambiguous chance. From her side it was either pride or prudence. When the wall was half built, the crafty mason, taking advantage of his master’s back being turned, broke one of the two panes at the top of the door with his pick. Madame de Merret understood Rosalie had urged this. They all then saw the face of a dark, gloomy man with black hair and fiery eyes.

Before her husband turned around, the poor woman nodded to the stranger, signaling “Hope.”

At four o’clock, as dawn broke in September, the work was finished. The mason was placed in charge of Jean, and Monsieur de Merret slept in his wife’s room.

The next morning he mentioned casually, “Oh, by the way, I must go to the Maire for the passport.” He donned his hat, took a few steps toward the door, paused, and grabbed the crucifix. His wife trembled with joy; “He will go to Duvivier’s,” she thought.

As soon as he left, Madame de Merret rang for Rosalie and cried in a terrifying voice, “The pick! Bring the pick! We must work. I saw how Gorenflot did it yesterday; we have time to make a gap and build it up again.”

In an instant Rosalie brought her mistress a cleaver; with a frenzy she began demolishing the wall. She had removed a few bricks when, turning to deliver a stronger blow, she saw Monsieur de Merret behind her. She fainted.

“Lay madame on her bed,” he said coldly.

Foreseeing what would happen in his absence, he had set a trap; he had only written to the Maire and summoned Duvivier. The jeweler arrived just as the disorder was being repaired.

“Duvivier,” Monsieur de Merret asked, “didn’t you buy any crucifixes from the Spaniards who passed through town?”

“No, monsieur.”

“Very well; thank you,” he said, casting a fierce glance at his wife. “Jean,” he added, turning to his valet, “you can serve my meals here in Madame de Merret’s room. She is ill, and I won’t leave her until she recovers.”

The cruel man remained in his wife’s room for twenty days. In the early days, when there were noises from the cupboard and Josephine wished to intercede for the man dying inside, he silenced her: “You swore on the Cross that there was no one there.”

After this story all the ladies rose from the table, breaking the spell Bianchon had cast over them. Some of them had shivered at those final words.
