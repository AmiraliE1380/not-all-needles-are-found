Doctor Monsieur Bianchon shares a chilling tale about La Grande Bretèche, a dilapidated house near Vendôme, surrounded by a wild garden that hints at its tragic history. The house's decay evokes mystery, suggesting a cursed past.

During his stay, Bianchon becomes fascinated by the house and sneaks into the garden, imagining its former glory. He meets local notary Monsieur Regnault, who reveals that the house belongs to the estate of the late Comtesse de Merret. Her will forbids anyone from entering or repairing the house for fifty years after her death, adding to its allure.

Regnault recounts the Comtesse's story: after her husband, the Count de Merret, died, she became reclusive, leaving the house in disarray. On her deathbed, she summoned Regnault to witness her will, intending to leave her estate to the hospital in Vendôme with strict conditions regarding the house.

Bianchon reflects on the Comtesse's life and the fate of La Grande Bretèche, engrossed in local gossip. His landlady, Madame Lepas, reveals more about the couple, hinting at a young Spanish prisoner connected to the Comtesse's tragic fate.

In a parallel story, a woman recounts the mysterious disappearance of Count Férédia, a Spanish Grandee. After he fails to return one night, her husband discovers the Count's clothes near the river, indicating he escaped. The woman suspects the Count's involvement in Madame de Merret's death, grappling with the moral dilemma of keeping his money. Intrigued by Rosalie, the maid who knows secrets about Madame de Merret's death, the narrator seeks the truth, ultimately revealing the tragic outcome of a confrontation between the Count and the Comtesse. The story concludes with reflections on the moral complexities within La Grande Bretèche.
