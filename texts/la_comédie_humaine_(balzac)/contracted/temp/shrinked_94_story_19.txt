**Summary of GOBSECK**

The story begins in the winter of 1829-30 in the salon of the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu, where her daughter Camille is warned about her interest in the young Comte de Restaud, whose mother is a notorious usurer. The Vicomtesse expresses concern that Restaud's family background could jeopardize Camille's future. Derville, a family friend and attorney, interjects, suggesting he has a story that may alter their perception of Restaud.

Derville recounts his past encounter with Gobseck, a usurer with a sallow face and a meticulous lifestyle. Gobseck, originally named Jean-Esther Van Gobseck, is a man of mystery, having lived a life filled with adventure and wealth accumulation through lending money at exorbitant rates. He is described as cold and calculating, with a singular focus on gold as the ultimate power.

Derville describes how he became acquainted with Gobseck while living in a damp house in Paris. Gobseck, despite his wealth, lives frugally and is indifferent to human emotions. Derville learns that Gobseck has a grandniece, La belle Hollandaise, who is embroiled in a murder case, but Gobseck shows little concern for family ties.

As Derville's career progresses, he becomes Gobseck's advisor and eventually borrows money from him to buy a law practice. Gobseck lends him the money at a high interest rate, but Derville pays off the loan quickly and marries Fanny Malvaut, a seamstress he admires.

The narrative shifts to the Comte de Restaud, who is in a precarious financial situation due to his gambling habits and the influence of his mother, the Comtesse. Derville observes the dynamics between the Count and Countess, noting the tension and the Count's declining health. The Countess, desperate to secure her children's future, becomes increasingly manipulative.

As the Count's health deteriorates, he becomes paranoid and distrustful of his wife, believing she is plotting against him. The Countess, in turn, is determined to regain control over her husband's wealth. Their relationship is marked by betrayal, love, and desperation.

In a climactic scene, the Count dies, leaving behind a complex web of financial entanglements. The Countess's attempts to secure her children's inheritance lead to a confrontation with Gobseck, who reveals his ownership of the family estate. The Countess's actions are driven by a mix of maternal instinct and self-preservation, showcasing the moral ambiguities of her character.

Derville, witnessing the fallout from the Count's death, reflects on the nature of greed, love, and the consequences of one's choices. Gobseck's legacy is one of avarice, and his influence extends beyond his death, affecting the lives of those around him.

The story concludes with Derville contemplating the impact of wealth and the human condition, leaving readers to ponder the intricate relationships between power, money, and morality in society.
