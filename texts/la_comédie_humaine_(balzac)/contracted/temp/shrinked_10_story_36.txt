DEDICATION

To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron, your encouragement of my “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century” led me to associate your name with my work. As a representative of studious Germany, your approval lends credibility to my efforts. Your dedication to research has sustained me in my labor on modern civilization. I hope this token of respect finds you well in Dobling and reminds you of your sincere admirer and friend.

DE BALZAC.

THE COLLECTION OF ANTIQUITIES

In a small French town stands the house known as the Hotel d’Esgrignon, residence of the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol. What began as a jest in naming became earnest through time. The d’Esgrignon family, descended from powerful Northmen, kept a proud lineage, never bowing to kings or commoners. As provincial nobles, they were locally revered, preserving status despite neglect by the court. Their history featured marriages without dowries and a steadfast refusal of titles that would diminish their nobility.

During the Revolution the d’Esgrignons were imperiled. The Marquis, respected in the countryside, avoided emigration but had to hide. Family lands were sold, yet Mlle. d’Esgrignon managed to save some property through the family steward, Chesnel. After the Terror the Marquis returned to a dilapidated castle, mourning lost heritage. The old Hotel d’Esgrignon had been demolished; Chesnel used the last of the Marquis’s money to purchase a new home for the family, wryly named the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

Around 1800 émigrés began returning, including the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, whom the Marquis married. She died in childbirth, leaving a son to carry the family name. Grief burdened the Marquis, now confronting diminished circumstances. A proposal from a local contractor to Mlle. d’Esgrignon — deemed insulting by her and her brother — drove a wedge between them and Chesnel, who had acted for the contractor.

Chesnel, a respected notary, had always been more than a servant: confidant, manager, and protector of the family’s interests. The altered dynamics troubled him; the Marquis saw Chesnel as a loyal companion, while Chesnel felt the strain of fidelity to a declining house. Politically, the era shifted as some nobles aligned with Napoleon and others remained monarchist. The d’Esgrignons held fast to their principles.

By 1804 many émigrés returned, yet the d’Esgrignons’ fortunes did not recover. The Marquis became a symbol of loyalty and old nobility, a leader among remaining royalists. His salon, nicknamed the “Collection of Antiquities,” gathered those clinging to the past. What began as a playful sobriquet assumed a more solemn tone as political tides moved.

The family faced challenges from the rising bourgeoisie, epitomized by du Croisier, who sought to undermine their standing. Rivalry intensified as du Croisier’s faction gained strength while the d’Esgrignons remained a bulwark of old-world values. Political life in the town grew polarized; du Croisier adopted an increasingly aggressive stance against the nobility.

By 1822 the d’Esgrignons’ situation remained precarious. The Marquis, now elderly, was uncertain of his role in a changing society. The Restoration had not delivered expected relief; financial struggles continued. The family lived modestly, their income barely meeting needs, while political tensions simmered.

As society evolved, the d’Esgrignons clung to their ideals, navigating a world increasingly hostile to their way of life. The Collection of Antiquities stood as a testament to a bygone era and the enduring legacy of the d’Esgrignon family.

The Marquis was oblivious to managing his estate, and the idea that the d’Esgrignons could recover their former glory struck others as absurd. People pinned hopes on Victurnien, the handsome heir, marrying into nobility to restore the family's prestige. In truth the d’Esgrignons lacked the political connections and finances to reclaim status, and their continuity had been broken by events of the past forty years.

Only Chesnel, the notary, perceived the futility of such hopes. He understood the social transformations wrought by the Revolution and the altered political landscape; despite his loyalty, he could not ignore their predicament. He tried gently to persuade the Marquis that adaptation was necessary, but the old noble remained convinced that fate or divine intervention would restore their fortunes.

Victurnien, adored by his father and aunt, was a spoiled young man who had never faced real opposition. Indulgence left him selfish and reckless. The Chevalier, a relic of the old order, fed his extravagance with romantic tales of past grandeur, inflating the young man’s sense of superiority. Surrounded by flattery and exempt from ordinary constraints, Victurnien came to regard himself as above the common people, worsening his moral and practical flaws.

When he entered society, the gap between his upbringing and modern realities became apparent. His behavior alienated townspeople, and escapades produced legal troubles Chesnel managed discreetly. Encouraged by companions who enjoyed his company, his arrogance hardened into a life of excess; he assumed lineage alone would shield him from consequences.

Du Croisier, a rival, sought to exploit Victurnien’s weaknesses and hasten the d’Esgrignons’ ruin. Meanwhile Victurnien’s vanity and appetite for pleasure led him into gambling and frivolity, sustained by Chesnel’s financial sacrifices, which he accepted as his due. Sent to Paris with high expectations, he quickly found the court far less welcoming than imagined; the nobility he met were largely self-interested and indifferent to reviving the d’Esgrignon name.

Undeterred, he tried to secure a place by adopting the extravagant lifestyle of Parisian elites. His charm and looks opened doors, and he became a fixture in high society, especially captivated by the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse. Enchanted by her beauty and the glitter of her world, he fell intensely in love, blind to her debts and convinced their passion was pure.

As he indulged in Parisian pleasures his debts mounted beyond his means. Friends who enjoyed his company also recognized his precarious state and egged his spending, entertained by the prospect of his fall. Victurnien’s naivety and lack of foresight led him to believe he could sustain lavish living without consequence.

Chesnel, ever loyal, continued to prop him up financially but grew increasingly alarmed. Despite warnings, Victurnien remained absorbed in fantasy, oblivious to reality. The tension between his noble pretensions and dire finances would precipitate his downfall.

Victurnien’s passage through Parisian high society exposes the dangers of vanity, excess, and the illusion of nobility. His entanglements—most notably with the Duchesse—become enmeshed in deceit and financial ruin, underscoring the fragility of status and the consequences of living beyond one’s means. The narrative is a cautionary tale about pride’s perils and the need to recognize one’s limits in a rapidly changing world.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs, without Chesnel or Mlle. Armande knowing. He had also received two thousand crowns from Chesnel to conceal his sources. Victurnien wrote deceitful letters to his father and aunt, who remained unaware of the disaster looming over their house. Only du Croisier knew, and he delighted not merely in ruining the d’Esgrignons but in dishonoring them; he sensed his revenge as Victurnien’s debts mounted.

Du Croisier’s first move targeted Chesnel, who lived modestly in the Rue du Bercail. One afternoon, after dinner, Chesnel’s housekeeper announced du Croisier. The old lawyer’s heart sank. Du Croisier presented a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, demanding payment or threatening to expose Victurnien’s debts. Overwhelmed, Chesnel realized he could raise the money only by mortgaging his lands. Du Croisier seized the opportunity, proposed to buy Chesnel’s property—and did so within two days. Stripped of his lands, Chesnel was left with only his practice and his house, consumed by worry for Victurnien’s future.

Chesnel dreaded telling Mlle. Armande about the family’s ruin. She had just received a charming, hypocritical letter from Victurnien describing his travels in Italy with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and remained enchanted and oblivious. Chesnel’s arrival shattered her reverie: all was lost, and Victurnien risked bringing dishonor on their house. Devastated, she realized they had given Victurnien too much freedom.

The Marquis, having read Victurnien’s letter, remained unaware of the crisis. Mlle. Armande and Chesnel shared a silent understanding of their concern for the young Count. They discussed intervening to save him from his reckless lifestyle, fearing he would be left destitute. Mlle. Armande resolved to go to Paris to rescue Victurnien, masking the trip as a medical consultation to avoid gossip.

Chesnel gave her his last bag of louis, and she set off, determined to confront the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse. Du Croisier, meanwhile, had planned his revenge to ensure Victurnien would be cut off from support. He wrote to Victurnien, informing him that the Kellers would advance no more money without security, timing the letter to coincide with Victurnien’s increasing desperation.

Victurnien, until then living in luxury, suddenly faced the reality of his debts. Despite previous gambling wins, he could not procure funds and felt the weight of impending ruin. In despair he briefly considered suicide but rejected it. Instead, he thought of fleeing with the Duchess and forging a bill of exchange to finance their escape, though he hesitated, wanting the Duchess’s agreement first.

When Victurnien confessed his financial troubles to the Duchess, she assured him money could be found. Encouraged, he proposed they flee together; she agreed, eager to break free from their current lives. Buoyed by her promise, Victurnien resolved to forge the bill.

The next day he presented a bill for three hundred thousand francs to the Kellers, who advanced the money, unaware of the deceit. Du Croisier, learning of the transaction, realized his plan for revenge was bearing fruit.

When Victurnien received his money, he gave it to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who locked it away and suggested one last visit to the Opera. He considered confessing to Chesnel but, despite doubts, lingered at the Duchess's behest; her enchanting glance kept him longer than he intended.

The next day he returned to the Hotel de Maufrigneuse to plan an escape, only to find the Duchess resolved to stay. She thought it more remarkable to face the storm than to flee, and Victurnien, torn between admiration and despair, realized how deeply he loved her.

After their argument Diane's coldness left him distraught. He begged forgiveness, but she remained unyielding—an angelic indifference that intensified his anguish. As time passed he felt increasingly lost and unable to think clearly.

Meanwhile the Count took Diane's hand and found it lifeless. Victurnien’s emotional turmoil was compounded by the discovery that his financial misdeeds had placed him in serious trouble. When Josephin told him a warrant for his arrest had been issued, he panicked, convinced he was ruined.

Josephin urged him to flee to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine, where Mlle. Armande waited. Clinging to his determined aunt, Victurnien discussed hiding and the possibility of slipping into Chesnel’s house unnoticed.

Executing the plan, he unwittingly walked into a trap. Chesnel, meanwhile, was selling his practice to a rival notary, M. Lepressoir, who hoped to profit from Victurnien's troubles. Learning of Victurnien's plight devastated Chesnel; fearing for the young man’s future, he even contemplated taking the blame for the forgery himself.

Chesnel resolved to confront du Croisier, Victurnien's creditor, and negotiate a settlement. Du Croisier, however, reveled in his power over the d’Esgrignon family, seeing the affair as an opportunity to assert dominance and exact revenge on the aristocracy. At a gathering at his house, news of Victurnien's arrest spread among guests who were shocked and intrigued; the atmosphere was tense as allies weighed the implications and a possible trial.

Realizing the gravity of the situation, Chesnel tried to influence the examining magistrate, M. Camusot, by appealing to his ambitions and connections, hoping Camusot's support might mitigate damage to Victurnien's reputation. As legal proceedings unfolded, Chesnel intensified his efforts and sought Mme. de Maufrigneuse’s assistance. Initially unaware of the forgery's full extent, she felt compelled to help once she understood Victurnien's love and the consequences he faced.

Negotiations with du Croisier proved difficult; he demanded not only money but political favors and social recognition—demands Chesnel could scarcely satisfy. In desperation Chesnel offered a substantial sum to have the charges withdrawn, but du Croisier’s pride and ambition made him resistant. Both sides were unwilling to yield, and the conflict escalated as the trial approached.

Chesnel’s determination to save Victurnien pushed him toward morally questionable options, including lying under oath. He understood that the d’Esgrignon family’s honor hung in the balance and that dishonor could be devastating. The legal battle thus became emblematic of broader tensions between aristocracy and bourgeoisie: its outcome would decide Victurnien's fate and signal shifting power dynamics.

Throughout, Chesnel’s unwavering loyalty to the d’Esgrignon family and his willingness to navigate provincial politics highlighted the complexities of love, honor, and ambition in a world where social status could dictate destiny.

Blondet, a knowledgeable judge, was overshadowed by his passion for gardening—particularly pelargoniums—which consumed his time and ambition. His marriage to a much younger woman, who had an affair with the local prefect, further stalled his career. Though he had a legitimate son, Joseph, Blondet favored his illegitimate son, Emile, sending Emile to Paris with little support after his wife’s death.

Blondet’s modest brick house and well-tended garden reflected his horticultural devotion. He intended to leave his estate to Joseph, having disinherited Emile, whom he regarded with disdain. Emile, however, prospered in Paris thanks to his mother’s family connections—especially Mlle. de Troisville, who promised support and helped secure his literary recognition—achievements Blondet never learned of, absorbed as he was in legal work and gardening. The household ran on strict frugality, enforced by a maid who managed daily life.

Joseph Blondet was being groomed for the bench and spent time with the Blandureau family. Michu, a young lawyer in those circles, often outshone the older judges. Meanwhile, a shrewd public prosecutor became involved in a plot against the d’Esgrignons, leading to a forgery charge against Victurnien d’Esgrignon, a young nobleman.

The President of the Tribunal, pursuing personal advantage, manipulated events to benefit du Croisier, a rival of the d’Esgrignons. Influenced by the President’s wife, the prosecutor pursued the case against Victurnien despite likely political fallout.

As the trial approached, the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse intervened, bringing funds to secure Victurnien’s release. Her efforts, with Chesnel the family notary’s support, aimed to restore the d’Esgrignon name. The trial ultimately revealed the truth, exonerating Victurnien and exposing du Croisier’s machinations.

Afterward the d’Esgrignons endured social scrutiny, but their honor was preserved. Victurnien’s future remained uncertain as he grappled with his past and his family’s expectations. The story reflects on the changing nature of nobility and the enduring bonds of loyalty and service—embodied by characters like Chesnel—examining ambition, familial loyalty, and the complexities of social standing in a transforming France.
