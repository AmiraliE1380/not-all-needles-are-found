To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron, your encouragement for my “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century” led me to associate your name with my work. Your approval lends weight. Your devotion to research sustained my writing on civilization. Receive this token of respect from your admirer.

DE BALZAC.

In a small French town the Hotel d’Esgrignon houses the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol. The name suits a family proud of its ancient Northman lineage. Proud provincial nobles, the d’Esgrignons refused to bow to kings or commoners: marriages without dowries, refusal of titles, and a local reverence persisted despite neglect by court.

The Revolution threatened them: the Marquis hid, lands were sold, and Mlle. d’Esgrignon, through the steward Chesnel, rescued property. After the Terror the Marquis returned to a ruined castle; the old hotel was demolished. Chesnel used the family’s remaining funds to buy modest residence, naming it Hotel d’Esgrignon.

Around 1800 émigrés returned. The Marquis married the Baron de Nouastre’s daughter, who died in childbirth leaving a son. Reduced circumstances and grief deepened. When a contractor proposed to Mlle., she and her brother were insulted; Chesnel, who had negotiated, was blamed. Chesnel was more than a servant: confidant, manager, protector, torn between fidelity and the house’s decline. The family remained royalist while the bourgeoisie rose and rivals like du Croisier sought to undermine them. By 1822 the Restoration brought little relief; finances stayed strained, but the “Collection of Antiquities” salon endured as a testament to their bygone ideals and legacy.

The Marquis neglected the estate, and many thought the d’Esgrignons' restoration fanciful. Hopes rested on Victurnien, the handsome heir, marrying into nobility, but the family lacked political connections, money, and forty years of continuity.

Only Chesnel, the notary, saw clearly: he grasped post‑Revolution social shifts and gently urged the Marquis to adapt, but the old noble clung to a belief in fate or divine revival.

Indulged by his father and aunt and never opposed, Victurnien became spoiled, selfish, and reckless, inflamed by the Chevalier’s romantic tales of past grandeur. Surrounded by flatterers and exempt from restraint, he came to see himself above common people.

In town his conduct alienated locals; escapades caused legal troubles Chesnel discreetly managed. Companions encouraged his arrogance while Du Croisier, a rival, sought to exploit him. He sank into gambling and frivolity, sustained by Chesnel’s sacrifices. Sent to Paris with high hopes, he found the court indifferent yet adopted its extravagant ways. His charm opened society’s doors and the infatuation of the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse—despite her debts—while his liabilities mounted. Friends egged his spending; Chesnel kept propping him up. The clash between pretension and ruined finances foreshadows downfall, exposing the dangers of vanity, excess, and the illusion of inherited status.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had secretly advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs; he had also taken two thousand crowns from Chesnel and written flattering, deceitful letters to his father and aunt. Only du Croisier knew, and he delighted in both ruining and dishonoring the d’Esgrignons as Victurnien’s debts grew.

Du Croisier’s first move targeted Chesnel, who lived modestly in the Rue du Bercail. Calling one afternoon, he produced a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, demanding payment or threatening exposure. Chesnel, able to raise money only by mortgaging his lands, was forced to sell; du Croisier bought the property within two days, leaving Chesnel with only his practice and house and consumed by fear for Victurnien’s future.

Chesnel dreaded telling Mlle. Armande, who had just received a flattering letter from Victurnien about travels with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and remained enchanted. The news shattered her; she saw they had given him too much freedom. The Marquis, though he had read Victurnien’s letter, was unaware of the crisis. Chesnel and Mlle. Armande silently resolved to save the young count; she set off for Paris—pretending a medical visit to avoid gossip—with Chesnel’s last bag of louis to confront the Duchesse.

Du Croisier ensured the Kellers would advance no more without security. Cornered, Victurnien rejected suicide and plotted to flee with the Duchesse, forging a bill for three hundred thousand francs which the Kellers advanced unknowingly. Du Croisier, learning of the transaction, realized his scheme was succeeding.

When Victurnien received the money he gave it to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who locked it away and persuaded him to visit the Opera. He nearly confessed to Chesnel but, despite doubts, stayed at the Duchess’s request, captivated by her.

He returned to the Hotel de Maufrigneuse intending to flee, only to find the Duchess resolved to face the storm. Torn between admiration and despair, Victurnien recognized his love. Diane’s coldness after their quarrel left him distraught; her indifference to his plea for forgiveness intensified his anguish.

Worse followed: a warrant was issued for his arrest. Josephin urged him to go to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine, where Mlle. Armande waited. He and his aunt plotted to hide and slip into Chesnel’s house, but they walked into a trap.

Chesnel, preparing to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir, was devastated by Victurnien’s plight and considered taking the blame for the forgery. He resolved to confront the creditor du Croisier, who relished his ascendancy over the d’Esgrignon family. At du Croisier’s gathering news of the arrest spread and debate about the trial flared.

Chesnel tried to influence the magistrate M. Camusot and enlisted Mme de Maufrigneuse’s help. Negotiations with du Croisier proved thorny—he demanded money, favors, and recognition. Chesnel offered a sum and contemplated perjury, but du Croisier’s pride resisted compromise. The looming trial became emblematic of a wider clash between aristocracy and bourgeoisie, with love, honor, ambition, and Chesnel’s loyalty at stake.

Blondet, a judge, was eclipsed by his passion for gardening—pelargoniums—which consumed his time and ambition. His marriage to a younger woman, who had an affair with the local prefect, further clouded his career. Though he had a legitimate son Joseph, Blondet preferred his illegitimate Émile and, after his wife's death, sent Émile to Paris with little support.

Blondet planned to leave his estate to Joseph, disinheriting Émile. Émile, however, prospered in Paris with his mother's family and Mlle. de Troisville's patronage, gaining literary recognition unknown to his absorbed father. Joseph was groomed for the bench and mingled with the Blandureaus; Michu, a young lawyer, outshone the older judges.

A shrewd prosecutor, urged by the President to benefit du Croisier, pursued a forgery charge against Victurnien d’Esgrignon. The Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and Chesnel funded his defense; the trial exonerated him and exposed du Croisier's plots. The d'Esgrignons preserved their honor, though Victurnien’s future remained uncertain amid shifting loyalties in a changing France.
