“HULLO! There is that old Box-coat again!”

A lawyer’s messenger, Simonnin — a gutter-jumper of some thirteen or fourteen, accustomed to running errands and carrying papers — called out this greeting as he chewed a piece of dry bread and flicked a crumb like a bullet through the open window. The pellet struck the hat of a stranger crossing the Rue Vivienne courtyard outside Maître Derville’s office and rebounded nearly to the pane. The head clerk reproved the boy with a good-natured impatience: however poor a client might be, he was still a man.

In Paris law-offices the messenger is a sort of street lad attached to the managing clerk’s authority; rude, greedy, idle, but often supporting an old mother on a small pittance. Simonnin’s taunt—“If he is a man, why do you call him old Box-coat?”—earned him a laugh while the clerks went back to their work.

The room where the clerks labored was full of cheap camaraderie and professional mockery. Godeschal, the extemporizing clerk, paused his dictation of an appeal only to mimic pompous official phrases and to contrive a joke on the stranger outside. The copying clerks — neophytes from the provinces — followed his lead, practicing courtly rhetoric and absorbing the slang and habits of their office. They joked over a blunder when one novice wrote “by Gingo” for “by Jingo,” and the others laughed at his provinciality. Boucard, the head clerk, scolded them: a judge who taxed a bill would not tolerate such jokes. The banter served both to amuse and to keep the engine of copying moving.

The clerks’ office was characteristic: a large, dim room with a diagonal stove-pipe, a bricked-up fireplace, and a marble chimney-piece cluttered with bread, pieces of Brie, pork cutlets, glasses, bottles, and Boucard’s cup of chocolate. Dust, mud, and the smell of old paper mixed with the odor of food and an overheated stove. The floor bore traces of the city’s weather; the dirty window-panes let in little light, so that one could not write without lamplight before ten in February. The walls were plastered with huge yellow posters announcing seizures, sales, and judgments—an attorney’s glory. Behind Boucard, an immense room heaped with bundles of papers and yellowed cardboard boxes labeled with clients’ names testified to cases cooking slowly, like stews. The office possessed a history of reused, handed-down furniture and implements; legal offices were places of routine, where clients passed and clerks learned the craft, often treating the place as both schoolroom and laboratory. To visitors the room appeared repellent: one of the most loathsome marts of Paris, where the business of souls was bartered amid mould and neglect.

Within this setting the clerks continued their familiar sparring. Godeschal resumed dictation — “given in—Are you ready?” — and the clerks answered dutifully. The appeal and the gossip intertwined. They argued over trivialities as much as they worked: the precise date to write, the formulation of a sentence, whether a waxworks show could be counted a “play.” Godeschal could spin a few words of logic into a wager that would decide a night at the theatre, and the others joined in his mock-legal dialect, drawing distinctions so that a few lines of banter could be inflated into an elaborate game of reasoning.

A knock at the office door broke the current of their jokes. Five clerks raised their heads and shouted “Come in!” while Boucard kept his face buried in a pile of broutilles and went on compressing a bill of costs. The newcomer was an old man in a greasy box-coat; he shut the door with a humble air, the humility of one who has fallen on hard times. He tried to smile to the indifferent faces before him and addressed the messenger politely: “Monsieur, is your master at home?” Simonnin, who preferred to be insolent, only patted his ear.

Godeschal, with a mouthful of bread and a flourish of his knife, asked whether the visitor wanted business. The stranger said he had been there several times and wished to speak only with M. Derville. The clerks, confident of their profession, but lazy in courtesy, offered to take the case; an attorney’s clerks believe they can replace the master in many matters. The stranger, timid and evidently tired, resisted: he insisted he could only explain to Derville and would wait.

Boucard, finishing his bill and sniffing his chocolate, rose to peer at the visitor. He scrutinized the man’s coat and boots, made a grimace at his unpromising appearance, and then, perhaps knowing it would be impossible to squeeze a fee from him, put the old man off with a mordant civility: Derville only worked at night; if the business was important, the visitor should return at one in the morning. The clerks resumed their noisy eating, making as much racket as horses over a manger; their indifference stung the old man, who stood motionless and bewildered. At length he said, with that tenacious hope of the desperate, “I will come again to-night.”

Their indifference to the call of poverty was both irony and habit. Poverty, when it confronts social denial, still expects mercy. The clerks, however, took their chance to mock. Simonnin quipped about whether the man was a colonel or a porter, and the others volleyed guesses: a buried-and-dug-up fellow, a colonel seeking arrears, a retired concierge, a nobleman reduced to pulling door-latches. Boucard and Godeschal wagered on his identity: Boucard insisted he had been a brewer in 1789 and a colonel in the Republic; Godeschal called him no soldier, predicting a bet lost or won; all this elicited a storm of cat-calls and laughter.

But the old man returned. Boucard stepped forward and asked for his name so that Derville might be informed. The visitor replied simply: “Chabert.” Hure, who had been quiescent, piped up: “The Colonel who was killed at Eylau?” The old man answered with candor and antique simplicity, “The same, monsieur.” He then left. The clerks exploded into derisive exclamations of triumph, as if a prank had been surer than a ghost.

In the bustle that followed, a variety of jibes and threats were repeated. Hure slapped a colleague recollecting unpaid theatre tickets. The clerks decided which theatre to attend that night; Boucard proposed the first boxes of the Français to see Talma in “Nero.” Godeschal argued, at amusing length and with mock forensic gravity, about the definition of a “play” — whether the waxworks or other spectacles could qualify as theatre — turning a bet into an elaborate syllogism to settle where they would spend their evening. Simonnin, warned not to make mischief, might be relegated to the pit. The bickering amused them more than their work. Yet amid the chatter, they kept one eye on their tasks: the appeal had to be handed in before the Fourth Chamber’s sitting that day.

Godeschal resumed dictation with theatrical flourishes: “Given in June eighteen hundred and fourteen (in words) … and we hope that my lords on the Bench will not be less magnanimous than the august author of the decree…” His language oscillated between genuine legal phrase-making and parody. The novice scribes’ pens creaked on the stamped paper like trapped beetles in paper cages. The little messenger interrupted to ask whether Godeschal wanted a glass of water; Boucard, exasperated at the boy’s antics, sent him off on errands to the Invalides. The clerks juggled responsibilities: Godeschal wanted to add interest for “Madame la Vicomtesse de Grandlieu,” but Boucard forbade him, accusing him of overreaching; he told him to set aside his copies for another case and demanded that he attend himself to a petition he would draw up. The office might be full of noise and small quarrels, but when something important presented itself the head clerk asserted authority and would choose personally to handle the weighty task.

All this petty life and bustle — the jokes, the copying, the bargains for theatre tickets, the noise of pens and mouths and boots — was emblematic of the profession. The clerks’ behavior showed the mixture of laziness, mendacity, and comic camaraderie that seasoned their trade. They were neither wholly heartless nor kindly: they were practised in the art of making light of misfortune, of turning suffering into opportunity for mockery. Yet in the midst of it, a human drama had briefly intruded — an old man saying he was Colonel Chabert, thought dead, who had come back to seek some redress. The clerks shrugged at such a claim and returned to their appeals and wagers. The chief, seeing the office thrown into confusion and the appeal needing correction, stepped in to do the job himself, muttering about the proper allowance of “inasmuch,” and planning to go to the Courts.

The tableau ends with a wry reflection: this scene, with its noisy trivialities, its petty malice and small pleasures, is one of the thousand delights of youth that, on recollection, make men say, “Those were good times.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At about one in the morning a man calling himself Colonel Chabert knocked at Maître Derville’s door; Derville was an attorney to the Court of First Instance in the Department of the Seine. The porter said Derville had not yet returned, but the visitor insisted he had an appointment and was shown upstairs to the lawyer’s rooms, where the head clerk was arranging papers for the next day’s cases. The clerk explained that Derville reserved the night for studying his cases when silence favored thought; the young lawyer earned well by this disciplined, spare life.

The visitor sat motionless. When Derville arrived in evening dress, both he and his clerk were struck by the man’s appearance. Colonel Chabert was dry and lean; a wig partly hid his forehead, his eyes seemed veiled, his face pale and livid like the dead. A tight black silk stock, the shadow of his hat, and the stillness of his pose added to the corpse-like impression. When he removed his hat his wig stuck to the greasy leather lining and came away, exposing a horrible scar from the nape across the skull over the right eye—an old wound that made the sight of him shocking.

“Monsieur,” said Derville, “to whom have I the honor of speaking?”

“To Colonel Chabert.”

“Which?”

“He who was killed at Eylau,” answered the stranger.

Taken for mad, the man nevertheless asked to confide his position to the lawyer. Derville, used to odd clients, dismissed his clerk and asked him to be brief. The Colonel then related his story with a mixture of lucidity and the hesitation of one long disbelieved.

He commanded a cavalry regiment at Eylau and had been instrumental in Murat’s charge. In action he had been struck by a Russian officer with a blow that split his skull; he fell from his horse and was trampled as regiments passed over him. Surgeons sent by the Emperor reported him dead and a certificate of death was made out. Yet he had not perished. He supposed that tetanus or catalepsy left him apparently lifeless when he was stripped and thrown into a common grave in the hurry of battle.

Later, at Stuttgart, an old quartermaster named Boutin—whom the Colonel had once saved—explained how his horse had fallen over him, shielding him from trampling and bullets and leaving a space that saved his head. When he came to, he found himself buried among the dead. By feeling about he found an arm that served as a lever and worked his way through the piled bodies until he saw daylight through the snow. His head was cut open but coagulated blood and skin may have plugged it enough to keep him alive. Fainting and reviving, he shouted until he was finally dug out by a woman and her husband, who took him to their hovel. He remained for six months between life and death, speechless or delirious, then was admitted to the hospital at Heilsberg.

He lacked the documents that would prove his identity—the surgeon Sparchmann’s deposition and the notarial declaration he had made at Heilsberg—because war had separated him from them. Wandering afterward beggared and scorned, he had tried to obtain from German authorities the proofs that might restore him to France and to his rights, but had been laughed at, regarded as mad, even locked up at Stuttgart for two years. On his release with ten thalers and a key of the fields he set out to find his wife and his fortune.

He knew Derville was lawyer to the Countess Ferraud, who was indeed his wife. Chabert said that after many fruitless attempts to interest lawyers he had come to Derville because he hoped to get the German documents. He described his misery: naked when he left the hospital, wandering, begging, enduring insults, living with the hope that Boutin might find his wife. Boutin had set out for Paris to inform Madame Chabert but never reached her; he was killed at Waterloo. The Colonel arrived in Paris without money, ill and naked; after a stay at the Hôtel-Dieu he sought his house in the Rue du Mont-Blanc, only to find that it had been sold and pulled down. An old lawyer who had held his affairs was dead; his successor laughed at him when he claimed to be Chabert. He watched his wife’s house, sometimes seeing her carriage flash by after a ball; she had married M. Ferraud, had two children, and enjoyed an income of thirty thousand francs, which he asserted belonged to him. He had written her letters—she had received some—yet she gave him nothing.

Derville listened, sometimes interrupting. The Colonel’s narrative was plain and full of detail; the lawyer was struck by his candor and grateful when Derville told him he would begin inquiries and make advances. The old man, so long repudiated, was overwhelmed when the young lawyer offered to try to obtain the Heilsberg papers and to give him five francs a day meanwhile. Derville spoke with practical reserve: the case would be exceptional and difficult, might go before three tribunals, and success was not certain even if the German documents could be obtained. He added a letter to his notary authorizing fifty francs every ten days to the Colonel’s order, to be recorded as a loan rather than alms, and urged him not to accept charity.

Chabert was deeply moved. For ten years repudiated by his wife and by society, he could hardly believe the small assistance was real. Gratitude choked him; he thanked Derville warmly, saying that after the Emperor the lawyer would be the man to whom he owed most. Derville accompanied him to the stairs with a light, and, once outside by a street lamp, the Colonel took the two gold pieces out of the letter and looked at them. “I may smoke cigars!” he said to himself.

Before leaving, Derville confided to his head clerk Boucard that the tale might cost him five and twenty louis; if robbed, he would not regret the money, for he had seen “the most consummate actor of the day.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
About three months after their interview, late one night in Derville’s room, the young notary Crottat—recently promoted from head clerk after his chief fled a bankruptcy—came to ask Derville to refund the six hundred francs advanced to the eccentric old soldier. Derville, amused, thanked him for the reminder and then noticed a stamped letter among the papers Boucard had laid out, postmarked through Prussia, Austria, Bavaria and France. He had Boucard translate it: it was from Berlin. The Berlin notary replied that the documents requested would arrive in a few days, duly witnessed and stamped; most witnesses still lived at Eylau, and the woman who had saved Comte Chabert was in a suburb of Heilsberg.

“That looks like business,” Derville said when Boucard reported the substance. Turning to Crottat, he asked about a certain Roguin.

“We’ll say ‘unfortunate, ill-used Roguin,’” Crottat laughed, and recounted what he knew: years earlier Roguin had absconded with eight hundred thousand francs of clients’ money, ruined families, and arranged the settlement of Chabert’s estate. Crottat had been third clerk and remembered Rose Chapotel, wife and widow of Hyacinthe called Chabert, Count of the Empire. They had married without settlement, so property was common; the personalty was valued at about six hundred thousand francs. Before marrying, Colonel Chabert had made a will leaving a quarter to hospitals and a quarter to the State; the will had been contested, there was a forced sale, and the Emperor later restored by decree to the widow what would otherwise have gone to the treasury. Crottat concluded that Chabert’s personal fortune was thus only three hundred thousand francs.

Derville set out on foot to the Faubourg Saint-Marceau, Rue du Petit-Banquier, lodging with an old quartermaster turned dairyman, Vergniaud. The address led to a shabby yard and hovel built of scraps: mismatched windows, thatched roofs, a puddle of refuse, rabbit hutches, a cowhouse, fowls and a dog. The yard showed the haste of preparing Paris’s daily meals—tin cans, cream pots, rags on lines. Derville surveyed the squalor in astonishment: the man who had helped decide victory at Eylau lodged here.

Three little boys played in the yard. The children did not answer when asked about M. Chabert, but then the Colonel himself emerged from a low room near the dairy. He smoked a short clay pipe, wore a greasy cap, and preserved an air of calm military authority; calling to the boys, “Silence in the ranks!” he restored order. He led Derville past the manure heap and into his room: a straw bed, fragments of carpet, an earthen floor, saltpeter-stained walls with a reed mat nailed to protect from damp, a box-coat on a nail, two pairs of boots, no linen, and reprinted Bulletins de la Grande Armée on the table. Despite the squalor, his countenance was composed; hope gleamed in his face when he saw the lawyer.

“Why did you not write to me?” he asked.

Derville replied at once with professional distrust about the origin and use of the money he had advanced, but softened by sympathy. Chabert explained that the Vergniauds had fed him gratis for a year; he could not leave them after saving their household, and he had not finished teaching their children to read. When Derville suggested he might live better in Paris for the same money, Chabert preferred his scant but honest lodging. He did not resent his poverty; he resented the loss of his name and rights.

Derville then outlined the legal difficulties they faced. Although Chabert was plainly alive, judicial proof of his identity and rank would be demanded by opponents; documents would be disputed and preliminary inquiries—perhaps even a commission to Prussia—would be required. Even if a court recognized him, questions of the Comtesse Ferraud’s subsequent marriage might be treated as equity: judges would weigh the wife’s present position and her children by Ferraud against an elderly husband who no longer held a place in society. The marriage involved novel points of law; outcomes would be uncertain, delayed, and costly. Moreover, because Chabert had made a prior will leaving a quarter to hospitals, the estate’s forced valuation at six hundred thousand and the Emperor’s subsequent decree complicated claims: Chabert’s share might now be reduced to three hundred thousand francs, and costs would further diminish recoverable sums.

Chabert listened incredulously. “And you call that justice!” he exclaimed.

Derville answered soberly: though a compromise might be wisest, the law would demand months of pleadings and resources. He estimated the preliminary inquiries and attendant expenses would total ten or twelve thousand francs—money neither he nor Chabert possessed. Without funds to pay pleaders and copy documents, the case could not proceed. Chabert, unversed in legal processes, despaired; he spoke impetuously of the Vendôme Column and of proclaiming himself there, but Derville warned that would end in a madhouse. The old soldier collapsed under the weight of the bureaucracy and the thought of Charenton. Seeing Chabert’s despair, Derville asked for full confidence: “Take courage; the end of the business cannot fail to be in your favor. Only, consider whether you can give me your whole confidence and blindly accept the result I may think best for your interests.”

“I put myself entirely in your hands,” Chabert answered.

They agreed Derville would try a friendly suit to annul the death certificate and the marriage, to put Chabert in possession of his rights and possibly restore his name on the army list as general, obtaining a pension. Derville promised to send a power of attorney and help if Chabert needed money.

As Derville left, Louis Vergniaud, the dairyman—an old soldier turned farmer, tanned and simple—took him by the arm. He explained how Chabert’s scruples had offended their household: the Colonel had quietly paid Vergniaud’s bills, thereby running the household into debt and depriving Vergniaud and his wife of tobacco and small comforts. The dairyman asked to borrow a hundred crowns to buy clothes for the Colonel and furnish his room, swearing he would repay. Derville, moved, promised the money—not to Vergniaud but to Chabert directly when the Colonel was rich enough to enjoy giving it. Vergniaud rejoiced.

Derville, meanwhile, planned his next move: he must approach the Countess Ferraud, but not show all his hand; he needed to discover her position and frighten her into compromise. He reflected on what frightens women and on the Countess’s likely vulnerabilities. A brief survey of the Ferraud household explained his strategy.

Comte Ferraud was the sole son of an émigré councillor who had lost fortune during the Terror. Returning under the Consulate, Ferraud remained faithful to Louis XVIII and belonged to the Faubourg Saint-Germain circle. Napoleon had tried to win him, promising restoration of title and offices. When Comte Chabert was supposed dead, Ferraud—some twenty-six and penniless—married the wealthy widow Rose Chapotel to secure fortune and social position. The Countess parlayed her gain into about forty thousand francs a year and became an admired woman. Her marriage to Ferraud pleased vanity and passion alike; under the Restoration his prospects improved, and he became a Councillor of State and Director-General, seeing his present place as the first step of a career.

Ambition dominated Ferraud. The Countess entrusted her affairs to Delbecq, a ruined but clever attorney, who cultivated patronage by managing their private fortune and hoping for an appointed judgeship in return. Delbecq multiplied her capital, aided by her lack of scruple. The Countess, though outwardly triumphant in Court life, harbored a moral fear: she had known her first husband lived and had married again when she thought him dead. Ferraud, despite adoration, had at times shown regrets about the marriage; a remark about another marriage by a contemporary hinted that, had circumstances been different, he might not have married her. That offhand coldness could prey on a woman’s conscience. The Countess, fearing discovery and scandal, had not confided in Delbecq or the police; she had learned to live with a private dread while enjoying public pleasures and wealth.

Derville arrived at the Hotel Ferraud in the Rue de Varennes and reflected that if Ferraud remained unmade a peer, the Countess’s marriage might be key: if the marriage were annulled, Ferraud could be free to marry into a peer’s family and quickly gain title—a powerful threat to a woman who feared losing her husband’s position. Derville resolved to touch that hidden wound.

He found the Countess at breakfast in an elegant room, playing with a monkey. She was careless and coquettish; the contrast between her luxury and Chabert’s squalor rankled Derville. He opened bluntly: he had come on a very serious matter and would prefer not to have the Count present.

She suggested Delbecq’s involvement; Derville curtly dismissed it. “Colonel Chabert is alive,” he told her. She laughed and denied it; Derville’s fixed gaze silenced her. He warned her of the danger: the evidence of Chabert’s identity was indisputable and full; if she resisted proceedings to show the death certificate false, they would likely win that preliminary case and then everything else would follow. He hinted at letters she had allegedly received from her first husband, securities, and other proofs.

The Countess first feigned indignation—no letters, she had always warded off Chaberts who came making claims, and she still drew the pension granted to Bonaparte’s widow—but Derville’s calm logic and insinuations unnerved her. He said he was as much her lawyer as Chabert’s: he would not lose a valuable client, but circumstances might turn public opinion against her. If she appeared in disgraceful colors before judges, the result could be worse than she imagined. He added a final sting: though Ferraud adored her now, if he learned the marriage might be void he could be tempted to desert her in favor of marrying the daughter of a peer of France—thereby acquiring a peerage by royal patent. The Countess blanched.

Derville thus pressed the Countess’s most vulnerable point—her place in society and her husband’s ambition—and made the problem personal and political rather than merely legal. “Compromise,” he proposed. The Countess, shaken, asked if Chabert still loved her; Derville replied that he believed so. Seeing in that love a lever, she conceded: she would have no lawyer but Derville. He arranged to receive her orders and to consult further, leaving with the sense that by framing the matter as a threat to her social position and Ferraud’s ambitions he had cornered her into negotiation.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A week after Derville’s two visits, on a fine June morning the husband and wife, separated by an almost supernatural chance, set out from opposite ends of Paris to meet in the lawyer’s office employed by both. The supplies Derville had advanced enabled Colonel Chabert to dress suitably; the dead man arrived in a decent cab, wearing a wig that suited him, blue cloth and white linen, and the broad red ribbon of the higher grade of the Legion of Honor under his waistcoat. Resuming the habits of wealth had restored his soldierly bearing; his grave, mysterious face reflected happiness and hope and seemed revived. He no longer resembled the Chabert of the old box-coat; a passerby would have recognized one of the noble wrecks of the old army.

When the Count jumped out of his carriage to go into Derville’s office, he did it as lightly as a young man. Hardly had his cab moved off when a smart brougham drove up, splendid with coats-of-arms. Madame la Comtesse Ferraud stepped out in a simple dress arranged to show how youthful her figure was; a drawn bonnet lined with pink framed her face and softened its outlines.

The office remained unchanged. Simonnin, breakfasting by the open window, stared at the blue sky above the courtyard.

“Ah, ha! Who will bet an evening at the play that Colonel Chabert is a General, and wears a red ribbon?” cried the little clerk.

“The chief is a great magician,” said Godeschal.

“Then there is no trick to play on him this time?” asked Desroches.

“His wife has taken that in hand, the Comtesse Ferraud,” said Boucard.

“Is Comtesse Ferraud required to belong to two men?” Godeschal joked.

“Here she is,” answered Simonnin.

Chabert pinched the gutter-jumper’s ear, to the clerks’ amusement. He was in Derville’s private room when the Countess entered the office.

“I have placed you apart,” Derville told her, “not knowing whether it would be agreeable to you to meet M. le Comte Chabert. If, however, you wish it—”

“It is an attention for which I am obliged to you.”

Derville had drawn up a memorandum of an agreement for them to discuss. He read aloud: the preamble named Hyacinthe Chabert, Count, living in Paris, and Madame Rose Chapotel, wife of the said Comte Chabert; it proposed that she and Chabert acknowledge, before witnesses and notaries, that the person designated is her first husband, and that Chabert would assert his rights only under certain circumstances—failure to carry out the conditions of their secret agreement. Chabert would accept a friendly suit to annul his certificate of death and dissolve the marriage. By a subsequent clause she pledged to secure Chabert an income of twenty-four thousand francs on government stock, to revert to her at his death.

“But it is much too dear!” exclaimed the Countess.

“Can you compromise it?” asked Derville.

“Possibly. But what do you want, madame?”

“I want—I will not have a lawsuit. I want—”

“You want him to remain dead?” interrupted Derville.

“Monsieur,” said the Countess, “if twenty-four thousand francs are necessary, we will go to law—”

“Yes, we will go to law,” said the Colonel in a deep voice, opening the door and standing before his wife, one hand in his waistcoat, the other hanging by his side—an attitude made ominous by his adventure.

“It is he,” the Countess thought.

“Too dear!” the old soldier exclaimed. “I have given you near on a million, and you are cheapening my misfortunes. Very well; now I will have you—you and your fortune. Our goods are in common, our marriage is not dissolved—”

“But monsieur is not Colonel Chabert!” cried the Countess with feigned astonishment.

“Indeed!” said the old man with intense irony. “Do you want proofs? I found you in the Palais-Royal—”

The Countess turned pale. Chabert paused, touched by the pain he caused the woman he had once loved; but her venomous glance made him continue abruptly: “You were with La—”

“Allow me, Monsieur Derville,” said the Countess. “You must give me leave to retire. I did not come here to listen to such dreadful things.”

She left. Derville rushed after her, but she had already gone.

Back in his private room, the Colonel paced in a towering rage.

“In those times a man took his wife where he chose,” he said. “But I was foolish and chose badly; I trusted appearances. She has no heart.”

“Madness! you will be caught and executed like any common wretch,” Derville warned. “Besides you might miss! A man must not miss his shot when he wants to kill his wife. Let me set things straight; go now. Take care of yourself; she may set a trap and shut you up in Charenton. I will notify her of our proceedings to protect you.”

The Colonel obeyed and went away, stammering apologies. He slowly descended the dark staircase, crushed by the most cruel blow—his wife's recognition of him without acknowledgment of identity—when he heard a rustle on the lowest landing and she stood before him.

“Come, monsieur,” she said, taking his arm with a familiar gesture. Her action and recovered gracious tone allayed his wrath, and he allowed her to lead him to the carriage.

“Well, get in!” she said when the footman let down the step.

As if by magic he found himself sitting beside her.

“Where to?” asked the servant.

“To Groslay,” she answered.

They drove across Paris. “Monsieur,” said the Countess in a voice trembling with a rare emotion—this single word expressed reproach and pardon, hope and despair, a question and an answer—“I knew you at once.”

“Rosine,” said the old soldier, “those words contain the only balm that can help me to forget my misfortunes.”

Two hot tears rolled onto her hands, which he pressed to show his paternal affection.

“Could you not have guessed what it cost me to appear before a stranger in a position so false?” she asked. “If I have to blush for it, at least let it be in privacy. You will forgive me, I hope. I received your letters, but they did not reach me till thirteen months after the battle of Eylau. They were opened, dirty, the writing unrecognizable; after obtaining Napoleon’s signature to my second marriage contract, I believed some swindler wanted to make a fool of me. To avoid disturbing Monsieur Ferraud and family ties, I took precautions against a pretended Chabert. Was I not right?”

“Yes, you were right. It was I who was the idiot,” he admitted. “But where are we going?” noticing they had reached La Chapelle.

“To my country house near Groslay, in the valley of Montmorency. There we will consider the steps to be taken. Though I am yours by right, I am no longer yours in fact. Can you wish that we should become the talk of Paris? Let us preserve our dignity. You still love me,” she said sadly, “but have I not been authorized to form other ties? A secret voice bids me trust your kindness. Be judge and party to the suit. I love M. Ferraud. I believed I had a right to love him. I do not blush to confess it; I do not disgrace us. When fate made me a widow, I was not a mother.”

The Colonel waved her silence away; for a long stretch they rode without speaking. Chabert imagined the two little ones before him.

“Rosine.”

“Monsieur?”

“The dead are very wrong to come to life again.”

“Oh, monsieur, no! Do not think me ungrateful. You find me a lover, a mother, while you left me merely a wife. Though it is no longer in my power to love, I know how much I owe you, and I can still offer you all the affection of a daughter.”

“Rosine,” he said in a softened tone, “I no longer feel resentment. We will forget. I have not so little delicacy as to demand the mockery of love from a wife who no longer loves me.”

The Countess gave him a look of deep gratitude that overwhelmed him. Some men have a soul strong enough for such self-devotion; his whole reward was the assurance he had made her happy.

“My dear friend, we will talk all this over later when our hearts have rested,” she said.

They conversed, recalling their former life and the Empire, and she lent a tinge of melancholy that kept the talk serious. She revived his love without awakening desire and allowed him to see her mental resources while guiding him to moderate his pleasure to paternal affection. He found in her a Countess of the Restoration.

By a cross-road they reached the entrance to a large park in the valley between Margency and Groslay. The Countess had a delightful house prepared for his stay. Misfortune had made the Colonel more helpful and good; though trusting, he could not help asking, “Then you felt quite sure you would bring me here?”

“Yes,” she said, “if I found Colonel Chabert among Derville’s clients.”

Her plausible reply calmed his slight remaining suspicion. For three days she was charming, with tender attentions and unfailing sweetness, apparently anxious to wipe out his sufferings and earn forgiveness for wrongs she confessed to have caused innocently. She delighted in showing the charms he liked while assuming a kind of melancholy to appeal to his feelings and take possession of his mind.

She did not yet know precisely what to do with him but meant to annihilate him socially. On the third evening uneasy at the results of her maneuvers, she finished a letter to Delbecq, instructing him to demand the deeds from Derville in her name, copy them, and come to Groslay. She had scarcely finished when she heard the Colonel’s step; uneasy at her absence, he came to look for her.

“Alas! I wish I were dead! My position is intolerable...”

“Why, what is the matter?” he asked.

“Nothing,” she replied, and went to speak privately to her maid, sending her to Paris to deliver the letter to Delbecq and bring it back as soon as read. The Countess then sat on a bench in sight to be joined by the Colonel. He hurried and sat beside her.

“Rosine,” he said, “what is the matter with you?”

She did not answer. The June evening was calm and lovely; distant children’s voices added sweetness. “You do not answer me?” he said.

“My husband—” the Countess broke off, blushed, and asked, “What am I to say when I speak of M. Ferraud?”

“Call him your husband,” Chabert replied gently. “Is he not the father of your children?”

“If he should ask what I came here for, if he finds out I came here alone with a stranger, what am I to say? Decide my fate; I am resigned.”

“My dear,” said the Colonel, taking her hands, “I have made up my mind to sacrifice myself entirely for your happiness—”

“That is impossible!” she cried, convulsively. “Remember you would have to renounce your identity, in an authenticated form.”

“Is not my word enough?” he asked. The demand for authentication pierced him and aroused distrust; he looked at her in a way that made her color.

A child cried in the distance. “Jules, leave your sister in peace,” the Countess called. “What, are your children here?” he asked.

“Yes, but I told them not to trouble you.”

He understood the delicacy and took her hand to kiss it. “But let them come,” he said. Two children ran up, complaining and holding out their hands. The Countess, no longer restraining her tears, cried, “Poor little things! I shall have to leave them. To whom will the law assign them? A mother’s heart cannot be divided; I want them.”

“Are you making mamma cry?” Jules asked, glaring at the Colonel.

“Silence, Jules!” said the mother. The children stood speechless, curious.

“If I am separated from the Count, only leave me my children, and I will submit to anything...” she cried.

“That is decisive,” said Chabert. “I must return underground. I had told myself so already.”

“Can I accept such a sacrifice?” she asked. “To sign that you are not Colonel Chabert, to acknowledge yourself an impostor, to live a lie every hour! But for my children I would have fled with you to the other end of the world.”

“Cannot I live here in your little lodge as one of your relations? I am as worn as a cracked cannon; I want nothing but tobacco and the Constitutionnel.”

The Countess melted; a contest of generosity ensued between them, and the soldier prevailed. One evening, bewitched by the domestic picture, he resolved to remain dead, and, no longer frightened by the authentication of a deed, asked what he could do to secure the family’s happiness.

“Do exactly as you like,” she said. “I declare I will have nothing to do with this affair.”

Delbecq had arrived days before and, following the Countess’s orders, had gained the Colonel’s confidence. The next morning Chabert went with the intendant to Saint-Leu-Taverny, where Delbecq had a notary draw up an affidavit. After hearing it read, the Colonel started up and walked out of the office.

“Turf and thunder! What a fool you must think me! Why, I should make myself out a swindler!” he cried.

“Indeed, monsieur,” said Delbecq, “I should advise you to get at least thirty thousand francs a year. Madame would pay.”

The insulted Colonel slapped the scoundrel’s cheeks twice and rushed off. Stung and confused, he returned to the park at Groslay by a gap in the fence and sat to meditate in a little room under a gazebo that commanded the road to Saint-Leu. The Countess, in the upper room, did not hear him approach. She and Delbecq discussed how to get him into Charenton if necessary.

The Colonel, recovering youthful elasticity, leaped the haha and stood before Delbecq, giving him two fine slaps. His rage spent, he no longer felt vigorous enough to leap back; he had seen the truth in its nakedness. The Countess’ words and Delbecq’s reply had exposed the conspiracy intended to entrap him. The care shown him had been bait for a snare. The revelation brought back all his suffering; he felt disgust for life and indecision.

At last he went up into the kiosque and found his wife seated, calm but shuddering slightly when she saw him. He stood with folded arms, face pale, brow stern.

“Madame,” he said after a moment’s gaze that compelled her to blush, “I do not curse you—I scorn you. I can thank the chance that divided us. I do not desire revenge; I no longer love you. I want nothing from you. Live in peace on my word; it is worth more than the scrawl of all the notaries in Paris. I will never assert my claim to the name I perhaps made illustrious. I am henceforth but a poor devil named Hyacinthe, who asks no more than his share of sunshine. Farewell!”

The Countess threw herself at his feet and would have detained him by his hands, but he pushed her away with disgust: “Do not touch me!”

Her expression when he left showed an infernal perspicacity: she knew she might live in peace on his word and contempt.

Chabert disappeared. The dairyman failed and became a hackney-cab driver. The Colonel perhaps took up some similar industry, or fell from ledge to ledge, lost in the mire of rags of Paris.

Six months later, Derville, hearing nothing of Chabert or the Comtesse Ferraud, supposed they had compromised through some other lawyer. One morning he summed the advances he had made to Chabert with costs, and asked the Countess to claim the amount from M. le Comte Chabert, assuming she knew where to find her first husband.

The next day Comte Ferraud’s man of business, newly appointed President of the County Court, sent Derville a note:

“Monsieur,—Madame la Comtesse Ferraud desires me to inform you that your client took complete advantage of your confidence, and that the individual calling himself Comte Chabert has acknowledged that he came forward under false pretences. Yours, etc., DELBECQ.”

“One comes across people who are, on my honor, too stupid by half,” cried Derville. “They don’t deserve to be Christians! Be humane, generous, philanthropical, and a lawyer, and you are bound to be cheated! There is a piece of business that will cost me two thousand-franc notes!”

Some time after receiving this letter, Derville went to the Palais de Justice in search of a pleader employed in the Police Court. By chance he entered Court Number 6 just as the Presiding Magistrate was sentencing one Hyacinthe to two months’ imprisonment as a vagabond, followed by confinement in the Mendicity House of Detention. On hearing the name, Derville recognized the delinquent between two gendarmes as his false Colonel Chabert.

The old soldier sat placid, motionless, almost absentminded. In rags and misery, his face still showed noble pride and a stoical eye. When a man falls into the hands of justice, he becomes a mere matter of law.

Taken back to the lock-up to be removed with the other vagabonds at the bar, Derville used his privilege to follow him. The lock-up was a dark, malodorous ante-room where the wretches of the city gathered. The sight justified, in the eyes of any observer, the desperation that pushes many to suicide.

Colonel Chabert sat among men in the livery of misery, silent or speaking low, while three gendarmes paced. “Do you recognize me?” Derville asked, standing before him.

“Yes, sir,” said Chabert, rising.

“If you are an honest man,” Derville whispered, “how could you remain in my debt?”

The old soldier blushed as a young girl accused of a clandestine affair might.

“What! Madame Ferraud has not paid you?” Derville cried.

“Paid me?” said Derville. “She wrote to me that you were a swindler.”

Chabert cast up his eyes in sublime horror and imprecation, as if calling heaven to witness to the new subterfuge.

“Monsieur,” he said in a calm, husky voice, “get the gendarmes to allow me into the lock-up, and I will sign an order which will certainly be honored.”

On Derville’s word the sergeant allowed it, and Hyacinthe wrote a few lines addressed to the Comtesse Ferraud.

“Send her that,” said the soldier, “and you will be paid your costs and the money you advanced. Believe me, monsieur, if I have not shown you the gratitude I owe you for your kind offices, it is no less sincere here,” and he laid his hand on his heart. “But what can the unfortunate do? They live, and that is all.”

Derville went away. Returning to his office, he sent Godeschal to the Comtesse Ferraud, who, on reading the note, at once paid the sum due to Comte Chabert’s lawyer.

In late June 1840 Godeschal, an attorney, went to Bicêtre with Derville, whom he had succeeded. On the avenue they saw beneath an elm an old broken pauper who, like the poor women at La Salpêtrière, had earned among beggars the Marshal’s staff. He sat on a corner-stone, intent on drying his snuffy handkerchief to avoid washing it. He had an attractive countenance and wore the reddish wrapper-coat the workhouse gives its inmates, a horrid livery.

“I say, Derville,” Godeschal said, “look at that old fellow. Isn’t he like those grotesque carved figures we get from Germany? And he is alive, perhaps happy.”

Derville peered through his eyeglass. “That old man is a whole poem, or, as romantics say, a drama. Did you ever meet the Comtesse Ferraud?”

“Yes; she is clever and agreeable, but rather too pious,” said Godeschal.

“That Bicêtre pauper is her lawful husband, Comte Chabert, the old Colonel. She has had him sent here, no doubt. If he is in this workhouse instead of living in a mansion, it is solely because he reminded the pretty Countess that he had taken her, like a hackney cab, on the street. I remember the tiger’s glare she shot at him then.”

This piqued Godeschal’s curiosity; Derville related the story that follows.

Two days later, returning to Paris on Monday morning, they looked again at Bicêtre and Derville proposed visiting Colonel Chabert. Halfway up the avenue they found the old man sitting on the trunk of a felled tree, drawing lines in the sand; they noticed he had breakfasted elsewhere.

“Good-morning, Colonel Chabert,” said Derville.

“Not Chabert! not Chabert! My name is Hyacinthe,” replied the veteran. “I am no longer a man, I am No. 164, Room 7,” he added, looking at Derville with timid anxiety, the fear of an old man and a child. “Are you going to visit the man condemned to death? He is not married! He is very lucky!”

“Poor fellow!” said Godeschal. “Would you like something to buy snuff?”

With the simplicity of a street Arab, Hyacinthe eagerly held out his hand; each stranger gave him a twenty-franc piece. He thanked them with a puzzled look, saying:

“Brave troopers!”

He ported arms, pretended to take aim, and shouted:

“Fire! both arms! Vive Napoleon!” Then he flourished his stick in the air.

“The nature of his wound has no doubt made him childish,” said Derville.

“Childish! he?” said another old pauper looking on. “Why, there are days you would better not tread on his corns. He is an old rogue, full of philosophy and imagination. But to-day—he has had his Monday treat. He was here, monsieur, as long ago as 1820. In 1820 a Prussian officer, whose chaise was crawling up the hill of Villejuif, came by on foot. We two were together, Hyacinthe and I, by the roadside. The officer, as he walked, was talking to another, a Russian, or some such, and when the Prussian saw the old boy, just to make fun, he said to him, ‘Here is an old cavalry man who must have been at Rossbach.’—‘I was too young to be there,’ said Hyacinthe. ‘But I was at Jena.’ And the Prussian made off pretty quick, without asking any more questions.”

“What a destiny!” exclaimed Derville. “Taken from the Foundling Hospital to die in the Infirmary for the Aged after helping Napoleon between whiles to conquer Egypt and Europe. Do you know, there are in modern society three men who can never think well of the world—the priest, the doctor, and the man of law? They wear black robes, as if mourning every virtue and every illusion. The most hapless of the three is the lawyer. When a man seeks the priest, he is prompted by repentance and beliefs that elevate and comfort; the priest purifies, repairs, and reconciles. But we lawyers see the same evil feelings repeated; nothing can correct them; our offices are sewers that can never be cleansed.

“How many things I have learned in my profession! I have seen a father die in a garret, deserted by two daughters to whom he had given forty thousand francs a year. I have known wills burned; I have seen mothers robbing their children, wives killing their husbands, women working on a husband’s love to make him idiotic or mad, that they might live in peace with a lover. I have seen women teaching the child of their marriage tastes that must bring it to the grave so the child of an illicit affection may benefit. I could not tell you all I have seen, for I have witnessed crimes against which justice is impotent. All the horrors romancers suppose they invent are below the truth. You will know something of these pretty things; as for me, I am going to live in the country with my wife. I have a horror of Paris.”

“I have seen plenty of them already in Desroches’ office,” replied Godeschal.

PARIS, February–March 1832.
