“HULLO! There is that old Box-coat again!”

A lawyer’s clerk—called a gutter-jumper in French offices—threw a crumb like a bullet through the open window. It struck the hat of a stranger crossing the Rue Vivienne courtyard outside Maître Derville’s rooms. The head clerk, pausing over a bill of costs, scolded the boy, “Come, Simonnin, don’t play tricks… however poor a client may be, he is still a man!”

The messenger, Simonnin, a lad of thirteen or fourteen, belonged to the managing clerk’s extended household: errand-runner, billet-doux carrier, street kid in habit, pettifogger by destiny—ruthless, impudent, greedy, idle, yet often sharing a miserable lodging with an old mother. He sneered at the stranger’s “box-coat” when the head clerk called him “old Box-coat.”

Around the long table of clerks, Godeschal, improvising mock-eloquence as he dictated an appeal, turned the arrival into an occasion for jest. He parodied high rhetoric—“But, in his noble and beneficent wisdom, his Majesty, Louis the Eighteenth—when he resumed the reins of Government—understood the high mission…”—and proposed a prank: tell the visitor the master only saw clients between two and three in the morning. The copying clerks laughed; Boucard, the head clerk, muttered “June 1814” when asked the date for a decree. Their camaraderie mingled with their work: gossip, diction, and the copying of appeals went on together.

The office itself was one of those Parisian law dens—large, ill-lit, with a stove-pipe slanting to a bricked-up fireplace and a marble chimney-piece littered with bread, triangles of Brie, pork cutlets, glasses, bottles, and Boucard’s cup of chocolate. The smell of food, the overheated stove, and old papers blended with mud and snow tracked in by the clerks. A rotating desk, piles of broutilles, yellow posters announcing seizures and judgments, rows of dust-encrusted bundles tied with red tape—all gave the place its peculiar physiognomy. The space was a schoolroom for youths, a passage for clients, and a laboratory for the chief; it was scrupulously neglected yet carefully handed down, with remnants of parchment-twisting machines and prosecutors’ bags that dated back to the Châtelet. For clients an attorney’s office was one of the most repulsive marts of Paris; yet for those inside, absorbed in law’s drama, accessories mattered less.

A knock interrupted their talk. Five clerks, with bright eyes and hungry jaws, called “Come in!” Boucard kept his nose buried in the bills. The new-comer entered humbly. He tried to smile, but the clerks’ indifference made him lower his eyes. He addressed Simonnin politely: “Monsieur, is your master at home?” The messenger affected deafness. Godeschal, with his mouth full of bread, and the others bantered as always.

“This is the fifth time I have called,” the stranger said. “I wish to speak to M. Derville.”

“M. Derville is in bed; he does no serious work till midnight,” Godeschal replied. “If you’ll lay your case before us, we could help you as well as he can—”

The stranger refused to speak to anyone but the lawyer. He looked tired; there were no chairs—attorneys seldom provided many. The inferior client stands, grumbles, and wastes no time, as an old lawyer said, “which is not allowed for when the bill is taxed.”

“I will wait till he is up,” the stranger said.

Boucard, finishing the bill at his table, rose, sniffed his chocolate, surveyed the old man from head to foot, and made a grimace at his coat. He assumed no fee could be wrung from such a client and advised him briskly to return at one in the morning. The clerks resumed their meal, indifferent to the old man’s embarrassment.

“I will come again to-night,” the stranger repeated, with the tenacity of the unfortunate who hopes to catch humanity at fault.

When poverty confronts Justice and Benevolence with their failures, it has the irony of pleading harder. The clerks, however, mocked him as soon as he left. “He looks as if he had been buried and dug up again,” one said. They guessed his background—colonel, retired concierge, brewer, porter—trading insults like cards. Simonnin called out from the window, and, prodded by the clerks’ mirth, the old man returned.

“Will you be kind enough to leave your name?” Boucard asked.

“Chabert.”

“The Colonel who was killed at Eylau?” Hure chimed in.

“The same, monsieur,” the old man answered with an antique simplicity, and turned to go.

The clerks exploded—cat-calls, exclamations. Desroches was slapped for owing a theatre ticket. The jesting resumed with gusto: which theatre? the Opera? Madame Saqui? Godeschal, pedantic even in mockery, argued about what counts as a “play” and whether Curtius’ Waxworks could be paid for as such—an absurd litigiousness that was comic because they applied to trivialities the habits of their trade: define the fact, consider the point of law, etc. The office’s speech was always legalistic, even in banter.

Godeschal also doubted whether the man might not be making game of them—yet he admitted, “On my honor, Colonel Chabert is really and truly dead. His wife is married again to Comte Ferraud, Councillor of State. Madame Ferraud is one of our clients.” Boucard closed the interruption: business must go on. The appeal had to be copied and handed in before the Fourth Chamber met that day; judgment would be given. The clerks were ordered back to work; they groused that nothing got done amid such distractions.

One clerk observed that, if the stranger had really been Colonel Chabert, Simonnin’s mock-deafness might have earned a boot in the right place—a more physical verdict than Godeschal’s legalistic reasoning. Eventually they agreed to go to the Français to see Talma in “Nero,” Simonnin to the pit; then they resumed dictation.

“Given in June eighteen hundred and fourteen (in words),” Godeschal read, and the copying-clerks’ pens creaked over stamped paper like trapped insects. He tried several openings—“We hope that my lords on the Bench will not be less magnanimous than the august author of the decree…”—and then hesitated, “I must read my sentence through again. I do not understand it myself.” Practicalities intruded: “Forty-six and three forty-nines,” Boucard muttered, and the boys went on.

The little messenger offered water; Boucard sent him to the Invalides with a packet. Godeschal wanted to tack on the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu’s interest “(at full length),” provoking Boucard’s scorn. “What! are you thinking of drawing up an appeal in the case of Vicomtesse de Grandlieu against the Legion of Honor—a case for the office to stand or fall by?…Have the goodness to put aside your copies and notes; you may keep that for the case of Navarreins against the Hospitals. It is late. I will draw up a little petition myself, with a due allowance of ‘inasmuch,’ and go to the Courts myself.”

The scene—the youthful camaraderie, the petty malice, the mixture of professional charlatanry and genuine craft, the grotesque cheerfulness amid petty concerns—was one of those remembered “good times.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At about one in the morning a man calling himself Colonel Chabert knocked at Maître Derville’s door; Derville was an attorney at the Court of First Instance in the Department of the Seine. The porter said Derville had not yet returned, but the visitor insisted he had an appointment and was shown upstairs to the lawyer’s rooms, where the head clerk was arranging papers for the next day’s cases. The clerk explained that Derville reserved the night for studying his cases when silence favored thought; the young lawyer earned much by this disciplined, spare life.

The visitor sat motionless. When Derville arrived in evening dress, both he and his clerk were struck by the man’s appearance. Colonel Chabert was dry and lean; a wig partly hid his forehead, his eyes seemed veiled, his face pale and livid like the dead. A tight black silk stock, the shadow of his hat, and his stillness added to the corpse-like impression. When he removed his hat his wig stuck to the greasy leather lining and came away, exposing a horrible scar from the nape across the skull over the right eye—an old wound that made the sight of him shocking.

“Monsieur,” said Derville, “to whom have I the honor of speaking?”

“To Colonel Chabert.”

“Which?”

“He who was killed at Eylau,” answered the stranger.

Taken for mad, the man nevertheless asked to confide his position to the lawyer. Derville, used to odd clients, dismissed his clerk and asked him to be brief. The Colonel then related his story with a mixture of lucidity and the hesitation of one long disbelieved.

He commanded a cavalry regiment at Eylau and had been instrumental in Murat’s charge. In action he had been struck by a Russian officer with a blow that split his skull; he fell from his horse and was trampled as regiments passed over him. Surgeons sent by the Emperor reported him dead and a certificate of death was made out. Yet he had not perished. He supposed that tetanus or catalepsy left him apparently lifeless when he was stripped and thrown into a common grave in the hurry of battle.

Later, at Stuttgart, an old quartermaster named Boutin—whom the Colonel had once saved—explained how his horse had fallen over him, shielding him from trampling and bullets and leaving a space that saved his head. When he came to, he found himself buried among the dead. By feeling about he found an arm that served as a lever and worked his way through the piled bodies until he saw daylight through the snow. His head was cut open but coagulated blood and skin may have plugged it enough to keep him alive. Fainting and reviving, he shouted until he was finally dug out by a woman and her husband, who took him to their hovel. He remained for six months between life and death, speechless or delirious, then was admitted to the hospital at Heilsberg.

He lacked the documents that would prove his identity—the surgeon Sparchmann’s deposition and the notarial declaration he had made at Heilsberg—because war had separated him from them. Wandering afterward beggared and scorned, he had tried to obtain from German authorities the proofs that might restore him to France and to his rights, but had been laughed at, regarded as mad, even locked up at Stuttgart for two years. On his release with ten thalers and a key of the fields he set out to find his wife and his fortune.

He knew Derville was lawyer to the Countess Ferraud, who was indeed his wife. Chabert said that after many fruitless attempts to interest lawyers he had come to Derville because he hoped to get help to obtain the German documents. He described his misery: naked when he left the hospital, wandering, begging, enduring insults, living with the hope that Boutin might find his wife. Boutin had set out for Paris to inform Madame Chabert but never reached her; he was killed at Waterloo. The Colonel arrived in Paris without money, ill and naked; after a stay at the Hôtel-Dieu he sought his house in the Rue du Mont-Blanc, only to find that it had been sold and pulled down. An old lawyer who had held his affairs was dead; his successor laughed at him when he claimed to be Chabert. He watched his wife’s house, sometimes seeing her carriage flash by after a ball; she had married M. Ferraud, had two children, and enjoyed an income of thirty thousand francs, which he asserted belonged to him. He had written her letters—she had received some—yet she gave him nothing.

Derville listened, sometimes interrupting. The Colonel’s narrative was plain and full of detail; the lawyer was struck by his candor and grateful when Derville told him he would begin inquiries and make advances. The old man, so long repudiated, was overwhelmed when the young lawyer offered to try to obtain the Heilsberg papers and to give him five francs a day meanwhile. Derville spoke with practical reserve: the case would be exceptional and difficult, might go before three tribunals, and success was not certain even if the German documents could be obtained. He added a letter to his notary authorizing fifty francs every ten days to the Colonel’s order, to be recorded as a loan rather than alms, and urged him not to accept charity.

Chabert was deeply moved. For ten years repudiated by his wife and by society, he could hardly believe the small assistance was real. Gratitude choked him; he thanked Derville warmly, saying that after the Emperor the lawyer would be the man to whom he owed most. Derville accompanied him to the stairs with a light, and, once outside by a street lamp, the Colonel took the two gold pieces out of the letter and looked at them. “I may smoke cigars!” he said to himself.

Before leaving, Derville confided to his head clerk Boucard that the tale might cost him twenty-five louis; if robbed, he would not regret the money, for he had seen “the most consummate actor of the day.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
About three months after their interview, at night in Derville’s room, Crottat, the young notary who had been asked to advance half-pay on Derville’s account to his eccentric client, came to plead that the six hundred francs given the old soldier be refunded.

“Are you amusing yourself with pensioning the old army?” Crottat laughed.

“My philanthropy will not carry me beyond twenty-five louis,” Derville replied. He noticed papers Boucard had laid out and a letter bearing stamps from Prussia, Austria, Bavaria, and France.

“It is the last act of the comedy; now we shall see if I have been taken in!” He opened the German letter and sent Boucard to have it translated. The Berlin notary answered that the requested documents would arrive in a few days, properly witnessed and stamped. Most witnesses were at Eylau, and the woman who had saved Comte Chabert was in a suburb of Heilsberg.

“This looks like business,” cried Derville when Boucard summarized the letter. Addressing Crottat, he asked about the Roguin affair.

“We will say that unfortunate, ill-used Roguin,” Crottat laughed. He, formerly a clerk, had copied the Ferraud file. He summarized the Chabert estate: Rose Chapotel, widow of Hyacinthe (Comte Chabert), had married without settlement so property was common. The personalty was about six hundred thousand francs. Before marriage Chabert had made a will leaving one-quarter to hospitals and another quarter to the State; the will was contested, forced sale occurred, and at settlement the man ruling France restored to the widow what would have gone to the treasury.

“So Comte Chabert’s personal fortune was no more than three hundred thousand francs?”

“Consequently so it was, old fellow!” Crottat said. “You lawyers are sometimes clear-headed.”

Derville went to the Faubourg Saint-Marceau, Rue du Petit-Banquier, where the lawyer’s receipt had listed Chabert’s address: lodging with Vergniaud, an old quartermaster turned dairyman. The coachman would not drive on the rutted street, so Derville walked. The sign “Vergniaud, dairyman” hung on a crossbeam. A gate opened onto a wretched yard and a hovel-like house patched from demolition scraps; the ground floor sat unevenly; rabbit hutches, a cowhouse, and poultry made the scene almost rural.

The yard bore signs of daily meal preparation—tin cans and cream pots, drying rags, a cart-horse, a goat, a cat on cream jars, fowls, a barking dog. Three little boys played. When asked if M. Chabert lived there they did not answer; Derville mocked them and they laughed. Then Colonel Chabert appeared, smoking a short clay pipe, wearing a greasy cap, calmly military despite the squalor. He called the boys to order with “Silence in the ranks!”

“Why did you not write to me?” he asked. “Go round by the cowhouse; the path’s paved there.” He led the way, avoiding a manure heap.

His room was barely fit to live in: a straw bed with scraps of carpet, earthen floor, saltpetre-stained cracked walls with a reed mat nailed to the damp side. A box-coat hung on a nail; two pairs of boots lay in a corner; no linen. On the table lay reprints of the Bulletins de la Grande Armée. Despite the squalor Chabert’s face was calm; Derville saw hope there.

“Does the smell of the pipe annoy you?” Chabert asked, offering a stool.

“But, Colonel, you are dreadfully uncomfortable here!” Derville blurted, suspecting waste of funds, as lawyers often do.

“To be sure, monsieur, we are not distinguished for luxury here. It is a camp lodging, tempered by friendship,” the soldier replied. “I have done no one wrong, I have never turned my back on anybody, and I sleep in peace.”

Derville’s legal instinct assumed the Colonel had squandered money on pleasures. He did not press for an account, but asked why Chabert would not come to Paris, where he could live better at similar cost.

“Why,” Chabert said, “the good folks with whom I live fed me gratis for a year. How could I leave them when I had a little money? Besides, the father of those three pickles is an Egyptian—”

“An Egyptian?”

“We call those who went to Egypt ‘Egyptians.’ We who returned are brothers; Vergniaud was in my regiment. We shared draughts in the desert; and besides, I have not yet finished teaching his brats to read.”

“He might have lodged you better for your money,” Derville said.

“Bah! his children sleep on the straw as I do. He and his wife have no better bed; they are very poor. They have taken a bigger business than they can manage. But if I recover my fortune... However, it does very well.”

“Colonel, tomorrow or the next day I shall receive your papers from Heilsberg. The woman who dug you out is still alive!”

“Curse the money! To think I haven’t got any!” he cried, flinging his pipe to the ground—an almost generous gesture.

Derville explained the legal complexity as they walked. Chabert thought the matter simple: he had been thought dead and here he was; he wanted his wife and fortune back and to have the rank of General reinstated, as he had been made Colonel of the Imperial Guard the day before Eylau.

“Things are not done so in the legal world,” Derville cautioned. Chabert’s identity and claims would have to be proved judicially; papers would be disputed; preliminary inquiries, appeals, and possibly foreign commissions could delay matters. Even if justice recognized him, the bigamy committed by the Comtesse Ferraud complicated things. The point was not clearly covered by the Code and would be treated like an equity question. Judges might favor the existing family ties; two important persons—the Countess and M. le Comte Ferraud—might influence the Bench. Thus the case might be prolonged, giving the Colonel time for bitter regrets.

“And my fortune?”

“Do you suppose you had a fine fortune?”

“Had I not thirty thousand francs a year?”

“My dear Colonel, in 1799 you made a will leaving one-quarter to hospitals... When you were reported dead, valuation and sale were necessary; your wife likely omitted ready money and jewels and possibly undervalued furniture—the valuation stood at six hundred thousand francs. Your wife, having a right to half, bought in much of it; she got something, and the hospitals received their seventy-five thousand. Then, because you had not mentioned your wife in the will, the Emperor restored by decree the residue that would have gone to the Exchequer. So now you have three hundred thousand francs, and costs will be deducted.”

“And you call that justice!” the Colonel exclaimed.

“It is the legal view,” said Derville. “Perhaps a compromise would be best. You might gain more than you can prove.”

“That would be to sell my wife!”

“With twenty-four thousand francs a year you could find a woman who would suit you better and make you happier. I propose seeing the Comtesse Ferraud to sound her; but I would not act without warning you.”

“Let us go together.”

“What, just as you are? No—your presence might lose the case on the spot.”

“Can I possibly gain it?”

“On every count,” Derville answered. “But I am not rich; the preliminary inquiries will cost ten or twelve thousand francs. I have not so much to lend you—I am crushed by interest on money I borrowed to buy my business. And you? Where will you find it?”

Tears gathered in Chabert’s eyes. The legal and social obstacles seemed like a nightmare. He thought of shouting his name at the foot of the Vendôme column; Derville warned that would lead to Charenton. Despair almost conquered him. Derville then asked for absolute trust, promising to pursue a friendly suit to annul both the death certificate and the marriage so the Colonel might regain rights and perhaps be reinserted on the army list as general, with pension.

“Proceed then,” said Chabert. “I put myself entirely in your hands.”

Derville promised to send a power of attorney and to help financially if needed. Chabert warmly clasped his hand and, drained, could only watch him leave.

In the street an old man in a blue waistcoat—Louis Vergniaud—had watched and now took Derville aside. He said he had lodged Comte Chabert and had a few words: the Colonel “had the best room,” Vergniaud would have given his own. He explained they were poor; Chabert had helped them pay bills when they were short, paying what they could not, thereby “hurting their feelings” because it put them in debt. He then asked Derville to lend them a hundred crowns to buy clothes and furnish the Colonel’s room.

Derville looked at the dairyman and the squalor, and thought it characteristic of virtue to have nothing to do with riches. “All right, you shall have your hundred crowns, and more,” he said, but would give the money to the Colonel so Vergniaud would not have the pleasure of charity.

As he got into his cab Derville reflected on their opponent: the Countess Ferraud. He determined not to show his hand but to discover hers and to win the game at one stroke. He knew a woman’s main fear and how to use it.

A brief survey of the Ferraud position explained his strategy. Monsieur le Comte Ferraud was the only son of a former councillor who emigrated in the Terror and lost his fortune. He returned under the Consulate, remained faithful to Louis XVIII, and belonged to the Faubourg Saint-Germain circle resisting Napoleon. The Emperor had tried to win him with promises of title and office. At the time of Comte Chabert’s supposed death, M. Ferraud was about twenty-six, handsome, without fortune, adopted by the Faubourg Saint-Germain. Madame la Comtesse Chabert had turned her share of the old fortune to good account; after eighteen months of widowhood she had about forty thousand francs a year and made an advantageous marriage to Ferraud. When the Restoration came, Ferraud’s advancement was slow; he gained some estates and a place as Councillor of State and Director-General, and saw his present position as the first step in his career.

Ambition dominated him; he attached to himself as secretary Delbecq, a ruined attorney expert in legal resources. Delbecq cared for the Count’s fortune and, hoping for office, became the Countess’ instrument. The Countess, practical and unscrupulous, used Delbecq to increase her private wealth and promised him a modest provincial judgeship if he devoted himself to her interests. Delbecq, greedy for place, trebled her capital. This partnership made the turning point of the story.

By 1818 the Restoration seemed secure; Madame la Comtesse Ferraud took her place at Court, rich and married to a rising Count. Yet she suffered a mental strain: though she had known her first husband lived and had ignored him, his sudden reappearance was a source of secret fear. She had resolved to make herself so rich that her second marriage could not be dissolved. Now Chabert’s return posed a danger she could neither explain nor confide in Delbecq or the police; many women live with such secret fears and learn to endure.

Derville’s cab halted at the Hotel Ferraud in the Rue de Varennes. He noted that Ferraud, so rich and favored by the King, was not yet a peer. If his marriage were annulled, he might receive a peerage—this, Derville suspected, would frighten the Countess most, so he meant to use it.

She received him in a pretty winter dining-room at breakfast, playing with a monkey on a pole, in an elegant wrapper, hair carelessly pinned, giving an arch air. Silver, gilding, and mother-of-pearl shone; rare plants grew in china jars. Seeing her luxury while the old soldier lived among beasts, Derville thought a pretty woman would never acknowledge a man in an old box-coat.

“Good-morning, Monsieur Derville,” she said.

“Madame,” he replied sharply, “I have come to speak with you on a very serious matter.”

“I am so grieved, M. le Comte is away—”

“Luckily. It would be grievous if he were present. I understand you manage your own affairs, M. Delbecq told me so.”

“Then I will send for Delbecq,” she said.

“He would be of no use; listen. One word will make you grave. Colonel Chabert is alive.”

“Is it by telling me such nonsense you think you can make me grave?” she laughed. Derville’s fixed gaze silenced her.

“Madame,” he said coldly, “you know not the extent of the danger. I need not detail the indisputable evidence or the fulness of proof testifying to Comte Chabert’s identity. I am not the man to take up a bad cause. If you resist our suit contesting the death certificate, you will lose that case; and then we shall gain every point.”

“What then do you wish to discuss with me?” she asked.

“Neither the Colonel nor yourself,” he said. “I need not allude to clever briefs or to letters you may have received from your first husband.”

“It is false,” she cried. “I never had a letter from Comte Chabert; and if someone pretends to be the Colonel, he is some swindler, some returned convict. Bonaparte sent an aide-de-camp to inquire for me on his death, and to this day I draw the pension of three thousand francs granted to this widow by the Government. I have been right to turn away the Chaberts who came.”

“Happily we are alone, madame. We can tell lies at our ease,” Derville said coolly, enjoying how her anger might betray her. “You received that first letter because it contained securities—”

“Oh, as to securities—that it certainly did not.”

“Then you received the letter,” said Derville, smiling. “You are caught, madame, in the first snare laid by an attorney, and you fancy you could fight Justice—”

The Countess colored and then paled, hiding her face. She regained composure and retorted with hauteur, but Derville insisted.

“Madame, I am as much your lawyer now as I am Colonel Chabert’s. Do you suppose I want to lose so valuable a client as you are? But you are not listening.”

“Nay, speak on, monsieur,” she said more graciously.

“Your fortune came to you from M. le Comte Chabert, and you cast him off. Your fortune is immense, and you leave him to beg. An advocate can be eloquent when a cause is eloquent; circumstances here might turn public opinion strongly against you.”

“But, monsieur,” she protested, “even if your Monsieur Chabert is living, the law will uphold my second marriage on account of the children, and I shall get off with restitution of two hundred and twenty-five thousand francs to M. Chabert.”

“It is impossible to foresee the Bench’s view,” Derville answered. “If on one side you have a mother and children, and on the other an old man crushed by sorrow, will judges contravene the law? Your marriage to Chabert has priority. But if you appear under disgraceful colors, you might have an unlooked-for adversary.”

“And who is he?” she demanded.

“Comte Ferraud.”

“Monsieur Ferraud has too great affection for me, too much respect for the mother of his children—”

“Do not talk such absurdities to lawyers, who read hearts to the bottom. At this instant M. Ferraud has not the slightest wish to annul your union; he adores you. But if someone told him his marriage is void—that his wife will be called before the bar of public opinion as a criminal—”

“He would defend me, monsieur.”

“No, madame.”

“What reason could he have for deserting me, monsieur?”

“That he would be free to marry the only daughter of a peer of France, whose title would be conferred on him by patent from the King.”

The Countess turned pale—Derville had hit the hidden wound. He pressed on: besides, a man covered with glory—a General, Count, Grand Cross of the Legion of Honor—might seem an attractive alternative; if that man insisted on his wife’s return—

“Enough, enough, monsieur!” she cried. “I will never have any lawyer but you. What is to be done?”

“Compromise,” Derville said.

“Does he still love me?” she asked.

“Well, I do not think he can do otherwise.”

At that a flash of hope shone in her eyes: perhaps she could manipulate Chabert’s affection. Derville left her saying he would await her orders, to report proceedings or see her in his office to agree terms.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A week after Derville’s two visits, on a fine June morning the estranged husband and wife, separated by an almost supernatural chance, set out from opposite ends of Paris to meet in the lawyer’s office engaged by both. The money Derville had advanced enabled Colonel Chabert to appear suitably: he arrived in a decent cab, wearing a wig, blue cloth and white linen, and the broad red ribbon of the higher grade of the Legion of Honor under his waistcoat. Resuming the habits of wealth had restored his soldierly bearing; his grave, mysterious face reflected hope and seemed revived. He no longer resembled the Chabert of the old box-coat; a passerby would have recognized one of the noble wrecks of the old army.

When the Count jumped from his carriage to go into Derville’s office, he did so lightly. A splendid brougham drove up; Madame la Comtesse Ferraud stepped out in a simple dress arranged to display her youthful figure; a drawn bonnet lined with pink framed her face and softened its outlines.

The office remained the same. Simonnin, breakfasting by the open window, stared at the blue sky above the courtyard and joked with the clerks. They noted the visitors—Chabert in Derville’s private room and the Countess entering the office. Chabert pinched the gutter-jumper’s ear to the clerks’ amusement. Derville had placed the Countess apart, unsure whether she would wish to meet M. le Comte Chabert; she accepted the attention.

Derville read aloud a memorandum of an agreement he had drawn up for them to discuss. The preamble named Hyacinthe Chabert, Count, living in Paris, and Madame Rose Chapotel, wife of the said Comte Chabert. It proposed that she and Chabert acknowledge, before witnesses and notaries, that the person designated was her first husband, and that Chabert would assert his rights only under certain circumstances—failure to carry out the conditions of their secret agreement. Chabert would accept a friendly suit to annul his certificate of death and dissolve the marriage. By a subsequent clause she pledged to secure Chabert an income of twenty-four thousand francs on government stock, to revert to her at his death.

“But it is much too dear!” exclaimed the Countess.

“Can you compromise it?” asked Derville.

“Possibly. But what do you want, madame?”

“I want—I will not have a lawsuit. I want—”

“You want him to remain dead?” interrupted Derville.

“Monsieur,” answered the Countess, “if twenty-four thousand francs are necessary, we will go to law—”

“Yes, we will go to law,” said the Colonel in a deep voice, opening the door and standing before his wife, one hand in his waistcoat, the other hanging by his side—an attitude made ominous by his adventure.

“It is he,” the Countess thought.

“Too dear!” the old soldier exclaimed. “I have given you near on a million, and you are cheapening my misfortunes. Very well; now I will have you—you and your fortune. Our goods are in common, our marriage is not dissolved—”

“But monsieur is not Colonel Chabert!” cried the Countess with feigned astonishment.

“Indeed!” said the old man with intense irony. “Do you want proofs? I found you in the Palais-Royal—”

The Countess turned pale. Chabert paused, touched by the pain he caused the woman he had once loved; but her venomous glance made him continue abruptly: “You were with La—”

“Allow me, Monsieur Derville,” said the Countess. “You must give me leave to retire. I did not come here to listen to such dreadful things.”

She left. Derville rushed after her, but she had already gone.

Back in his private room, the Colonel paced in towering rage.

“In those times a man took his wife where he chose,” he said. “But I was foolish and chose badly; I trusted appearances. She has no heart.”

“Madness! you will be caught and executed like any common wretch,” Derville warned. “Besides you might miss! A man must not miss his shot when he wants to kill his wife. Let me set things straight; go now. Take care of yourself; she may set a trap and shut you up in Charenton. I will notify her of our proceedings to protect you.”

The Colonel obeyed and went away, stammering apologies. He slowly descended the dark staircase, crushed by the most cruel blow—his wife's recognition of him without acknowledgment of identity—when he heard a rustle on the lowest landing and she stood before him.

“Come, monsieur,” she said, taking his arm with a familiar gesture. Her action and recovered gracious tone allayed his wrath, and he allowed her to lead him to the carriage.

“Well, get in!” she said when the footman let down the step.

As if by magic he found himself sitting beside her.

“Where to?” asked the servant.

“To Groslay,” she answered.

They drove across Paris. “Monsieur,” said the Countess in a voice trembling with a rare emotion—this single word expressed reproach and pardon, hope and despair, a question and an answer—“I knew you at once.”

“Rosine,” said the old soldier, “those words contain the only balm that can help me to forget my misfortunes.”

Two hot tears rolled onto her hands, which he pressed to show his paternal affection.

“Could you not have guessed what it cost me to appear before a stranger in a position so false?” she asked. “If I have to blush for it, at least let it be in privacy. You will forgive me, I hope. I received your letters, but they did not reach me till thirteen months after the battle of Eylau. They were opened, dirty, the writing unrecognizable; after obtaining Napoleon’s signature to my second marriage contract, I believed some swindler wanted to make a fool of me. To avoid disturbing Monsieur Ferraud and family ties, I took precautions against a pretended Chabert. Was I not right?”

“Yes, you were right. It was I who was the idiot,” he admitted. “But where are we going?” noticing they had reached La Chapelle.

“To my country house near Groslay, in the valley of Montmorency. There we will consider the steps to be taken. Though I am yours by right, I am no longer yours in fact. Can you wish that we should become the talk of Paris? Let us preserve our dignity. You still love me,” she said sadly, “but have I not been authorized to form other ties? A secret voice bids me trust your kindness. Be judge and party to the suit. I love M. Ferraud. I believed I had a right to love him. I do not blush to confess it; I do not disgrace us. When fate made me a widow, I was not a mother.”

The Colonel waved her silence away; for a long stretch they rode without speaking. Chabert imagined the two little ones before him.

“Rosine.”

“Monsieur?”

“The dead are very wrong to come to life again.”

“Oh, monsieur, no! Do not think me ungrateful. You find me a lover, a mother, while you left me merely a wife. Though it is no longer in my power to love, I know how much I owe you, and I can still offer you all the affection of a daughter.”

“Rosine,” he said in a softened tone, “I no longer feel resentment. We will forget. I have not so little delicacy as to demand the mockery of love from a wife who no longer loves me.”

The Countess gave him a look of deep gratitude that overwhelmed him. Some men have a soul strong enough for such self-devotion; his whole reward was the assurance he had made her happy.

“My dear friend, we will talk all this over later when our hearts have rested,” she said.

They conversed, recalling their former life and the Empire, and she lent a tinge of melancholy that kept the talk serious. She revived his love without awakening desire and guided him toward paternal affection. He found in her a Countess of the Restoration.

By a cross-road they reached the entrance to a large park in the valley between Margency and Groslay. The Countess had a delightful house prepared for his stay. Misfortune had made the Colonel more helpful and good; though trusting, he could not help asking, “Then you felt quite sure you would bring me here?”

“Yes,” she said, “if I found Colonel Chabert among Derville’s clients.”

Her plausible reply calmed his slight remaining suspicion. For three days she was charming, with tender attentions and unfailing sweetness, apparently anxious to wipe out his sufferings and earn forgiveness for wrongs she confessed to have caused innocently. She delighted in showing the charms he liked while assuming a kind of melancholy to appeal to his feelings and take possession of his mind.

She did not yet know precisely what to do with him but meant to annihilate him socially. On the third evening, uneasy at the results of her maneuvers, she finished a letter to Delbecq, instructing him to demand the deeds from Derville in her name, copy them, and come to Groslay. She had scarcely finished when she heard the Colonel’s step; uneasy at her absence, he came to look for her.

“Alas! I wish I were dead! My position is intolerable...”

“Why, what is the matter?” he asked.

“Nothing,” she replied, and spoke privately to her maid, sending her to Paris to deliver the letter to Delbecq and bring it back as soon as read. The Countess then sat on a bench in sight to be joined by the Colonel. He hurried and sat beside her.

“Rosine,” he said, “what is the matter with you?”

She did not answer. The June evening was calm and lovely; distant children’s voices added sweetness. “You do not answer me?” he said.

“My husband—” the Countess broke off, blushed, and asked, “What am I to say when I speak of M. Ferraud?”

“Call him your husband,” Chabert replied gently. “Is he not the father of your children?”

“If he should ask what I came here for, if he finds out I came here alone with a stranger, what am I to say? Decide my fate; I am resigned.”

“My dear,” said the Colonel, taking her hands, “I have made up my mind to sacrifice myself entirely for your happiness—”

“That is impossible!” she cried, convulsively. “Remember you would have to renounce your identity, in an authenticated form.”

“Is not my word enough?” he asked. The demand for authentication pierced him and aroused distrust; he looked at her in a way that made her color.

A child cried in the distance. “Jules, leave your sister in peace,” the Countess called. “What, are your children here?” he asked.

“Yes, but I told them not to trouble you.”

He understood the delicacy and took her hand to kiss it. “But let them come,” he said. Two children ran up, complaining and holding out their hands. The Countess, no longer restraining her tears, cried, “Poor little things! I shall have to leave them. To whom will the law assign them? A mother’s heart cannot be divided; I want them.”

“Are you making mamma cry?” Jules asked, glaring at the Colonel.

“Silence, Jules!” said the mother. The children stood speechless, curious.

“If I am separated from the Count, only leave me my children, and I will submit to anything...” she cried.

“That is decisive,” said Chabert. “I must return underground. I had told myself so already.”

“Can I accept such a sacrifice?” she asked. “To sign that you are not Colonel Chabert, to acknowledge yourself an impostor, to live a lie every hour! But for my children I would have fled with you to the other end of the world.”

“Cannot I live here in your little lodge as one of your relations? I am as worn as a cracked cannon; I want nothing but tobacco and the Constitutionnel.”

The Countess melted; a contest of generosity ensued between them, and the soldier prevailed. One evening, bewitched by the domestic picture, he resolved to remain dead, and, no longer frightened by the authentication of a deed, asked what he could do to secure the family’s happiness.

“Do exactly as you like,” she said. “I declare I will have nothing to do with this affair.”

Delbecq had arrived days before and, following the Countess’s orders, had gained the Colonel’s confidence. The next morning Chabert went with the intendant to Saint-Leu-Taverny, where Delbecq had a notary draw up an affidavit. After hearing it read, the Colonel started up and walked out of the office.

“Turf and thunder! What a fool you must think me! Why, I should make myself out a swindler!” he cried.

“Indeed, monsieur,” said Delbecq, “I should advise you to get at least thirty thousand francs a year. Madame would pay.”

The insulted Colonel slapped the scoundrel’s cheeks twice and rushed off. Stung and confused, he returned to the park at Groslay by a gap in the fence and sat to meditate in a little room under a gazebo that commanded the road to Saint-Leu. The Countess, in the upper room, did not hear him approach. She and Delbecq discussed how to get him into Charenton if necessary.

The Colonel, recovering youthful elasticity, leaped the haha and stood before Delbecq, giving him two fine slaps. His rage spent, he no longer felt vigorous enough to leap back; he had seen the truth in its nakedness. The Countess’ words and Delbecq’s reply had exposed the conspiracy intended to entrap him. The care shown him had been bait for a snare. The revelation brought back all his suffering; he felt disgust for life and indecision.

At last he went up into the kiosque and found his wife seated, calm but shuddering slightly when she saw him. He stood with folded arms, face pale, brow stern.

“Madame,” he said after a moment’s gaze that compelled her to blush, “I do not curse you—I scorn you. I can thank the chance that divided us. I do not desire revenge; I no longer love you. I want nothing from you. Live in peace on my word; it is worth more than the scrawl of all the notaries in Paris. I will never assert my claim to the name I perhaps made illustrious. I am henceforth but a poor devil named Hyacinthe, who asks no more than his share of sunshine. Farewell!”

The Countess threw herself at his feet and would have detained him by his hands, but he pushed her away with disgust: “Do not touch me!”

Her expression when he left showed infernal perspicacity: she knew she might live in peace on his word and contempt.

Chabert disappeared. The dairyman failed and became a hackney-cab driver. The Colonel perhaps took up some similar industry, or fell from ledge to ledge, lost in the mire of rags of Paris.

Six months later, Derville, hearing nothing of Chabert or the Comtesse Ferraud, supposed they had compromised through some other lawyer. One morning he summed the advances he had made to Chabert with costs, and asked the Countess to claim the amount from M. le Comte Chabert, assuming she knew where to find her first husband.

The next day Comte Ferraud’s man of business, newly appointed President of the County Court, sent Derville a note:

“Monsieur,—Madame la Comtesse Ferraud desires me to inform you that your client took complete advantage of your confidence, and that the individual calling himself Comte Chabert has acknowledged that he came forward under false pretences. Yours, etc., DELBECQ.”

“One comes across people who are, on my honor, too stupid by half,” cried Derville. “They don’t deserve to be Christians! Be humane, generous, philanthropical, and a lawyer, and you are bound to be cheated! There is a piece of business that will cost me two thousand-franc notes!”

Some time after receiving this letter, Derville went to the Palais de Justice in search of a pleader employed in the Police Court. By chance he entered Court Number 6 just as the Presiding Magistrate was sentencing one Hyacinthe to two months’ imprisonment as a vagabond, followed by confinement in the Mendicity House of Detention. On hearing the name, Derville recognized the delinquent between two gendarmes as his false Colonel Chabert.

The old soldier sat placid, motionless, almost absentminded. In rags and misery, his face still showed noble pride and a stoical eye. When a man falls into the hands of justice, he becomes a mere matter of law.

Taken back to the lock-up to be removed with the other vagabonds at the bar, Derville used his privilege to follow him. The lock-up was a dark, malodorous ante-room where the wretches of the city gathered. The sight justified, in the eyes of any observer, the desperation that pushes many to suicide.

Colonel Chabert sat among men in the livery of misery, silent or speaking low, while three gendarmes paced. “Do you recognize me?” Derville asked, standing before him.

“Yes, sir,” said Chabert, rising.

“If you are an honest man,” Derville whispered, “how could you remain in my debt?”

The old soldier blushed as a young girl accused of a clandestine affair might.

“What! Madame Ferraud has not paid you?” Derville cried.

“Paid me?” said Derville. “She wrote to me that you were a swindler.”

Chabert cast up his eyes in sublime horror and imprecation, as if calling heaven to witness to the new subterfuge.

“Monsieur,” he said in a calm, husky voice, “get the gendarmes to allow me into the lock-up, and I will sign an order which will certainly be honored.”

On Derville’s word the sergeant allowed it, and Hyacinthe wrote a few lines addressed to the Comtesse Ferraud.

“Send her that,” said the soldier, “and you will be paid your costs and the money you advanced. Believe me, monsieur, if I have not shown you the gratitude I owe you for your kind offices, it is no less sincere here,” and he laid his hand on his heart. “But what can the unfortunate do? They live, and that is all.”

Derville went away. Returning to his office, he sent Godeschal to the Comtesse Ferraud, who, on reading the note, at once paid the sum due to Comte Chabert’s lawyer.

In late June 1840, Godeschal, now an attorney, went to Bicêtre with Derville, whose position he had taken. On the avenue they saw beneath an elm one of those old, broken paupers who, like poor women at La Salpêtrière, have earned among beggars the Marshal’s staff. The man, one of two thousand poor lodged in the infirmary for the aged, sat on a corner-stone and concentrated all his intelligence on the common operation among pensioners: drying his snuffy pocket-handkerchief in the sun to save washing it. He had an attractive face and wore the reddish wrapper-coat the workhouse gives its inmates.

“I say, Derville,” Godeschal said, “look at that old fellow. Isn’t he like those grotesque carved figures we get from Germany? And he is alive, perhaps happy.”

Derville peered through his eyeglass and answered: “That old man, my dear fellow, is a whole poem, or, as romantics say, a drama. Did you ever meet the Comtesse Ferraud?”

“Yes; she is clever and agreeable, but rather too pious,” said Godeschal.

“That Bicêtre pauper is her lawful husband, Comte Chabert, the old Colonel. She has had him sent here, no doubt. If he is in this workhouse instead of living in a mansion, it is solely because he reminded the pretty Countess that he had taken her, like a hackney cab, on the street. I remember the tiger’s glare she shot at him then.”

This piqued Godeschal’s curiosity, and Derville related the story that follows.

Two days later, returning to Paris on Monday morning, they looked again at Bicêtre and Derville proposed visiting Colonel Chabert. Halfway up the avenue they found the old man sitting on the trunk of a felled tree, amusing himself by drawing lines in the sand; they noticed he had breakfasted elsewhere than at Bicêtre.

“Good-morning, Colonel Chabert,” said Derville.

“Not Chabert! not Chabert! My name is Hyacinthe,” replied the veteran. “I am no longer a man, I am No. 164, Room 7,” he added, looking at Derville with timid anxiety, like an old child. “Are you going to visit the man condemned to death? He is not married! He is very lucky!”

“Poor fellow!” said Godeschal. “Would you like something to buy snuff?”

With the simplicity of a street urchin, Hyacinthe eagerly held out his hand; each stranger gave him a twenty-franc piece. He thanked them with a puzzled look, saying:

“Brave troopers!”

He ported arms, pretended to take aim, and shouted with a smile:

“Fire! both arms! Vive Napoleon!” Then he flourished his stick in the air.

“The nature of his wound has no doubt made him childish,” said Derville.

“Childish! he?” said another old pauper looking on. “Why, there are days you would better not tread on his corns. He is an old rogue, full of philosophy and imagination. But to-day—what can you expect?—he has had his Monday treat. He was here, monsieur, as long ago as 1820. At that time a Prussian officer, whose chaise was crawling up the hill of Villejuif, came by on foot. We two were together, Hyacinthe and I, by the roadside. The officer, as he walked, was talking to another, a Russian, or some animal of the same species, and when the Prussian saw the old boy, just to make fun, he said to him, ‘Here is an old cavalry man who must have been at Rossbach.’—‘I was too young to be there,’ said Hyacinthe. ‘But I was at Jena.’ And the Prussian made off pretty quick, without asking any more questions.”

“What a destiny!” exclaimed Derville. “Taken from the Foundling Hospital to die in the Infirmary for the Aged after helping Napoleon between whiles to conquer Egypt and Europe. Do you know, my dear fellow,” he went on after a pause, “there are in modern society three men who can never think well of the world—the priest, the doctor, and the man of law. They wear black robes, perhaps because they are in mourning for every virtue and every illusion. The most hapless of the three is the lawyer. When a man seeks the priest, he is prompted by repentance and beliefs which elevate him and comfort the intercessor; the priest purifies, repairs, and reconciles. But we lawyers see the same evil feelings repeated; nothing can correct them; our offices are sewers that can never be cleansed.

“I have learned much in my profession. I have seen a father die in a garret, deserted by two daughters to whom he had given forty thousand francs a year. I have known wills burned; I have seen mothers robbing their children, wives killing their husbands, women working on a husband’s love to make him idiotic or mad so they might live in peace with a lover. I have seen women train the child of a marriage toward ruin so a child of illicit affection may benefit. I could not tell you all I have seen, for I have witnessed crimes against which justice is often impotent. All the horrors romancers suppose they invent are below the truth. You will know something of these pretty things; as for me, I am going to live in the country with my wife. I have a horror of Paris.”

“I have seen plenty of them already in Desroches’ office,” replied Godeschal.

PARIS, February–March 1832.
