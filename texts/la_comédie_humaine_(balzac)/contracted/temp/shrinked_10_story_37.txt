THE LILY OF THE VALLEY

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

To Monsieur J. B. Nacquart, Member of the Royal Academy of Medicine.

De Balzac thanks the doctor whose science once saved him and honors the friend of his daily life by inscribing this work among the stones of his literary foundation.

ENVOI

Felix de Vandenesse to Madame la Comtesse Natalie de Manerville:

At your insistence I yield and tell you my past. Women we love may make us ignore common sense; you have the right to demand my history even though I resist. You have guessed the phantom that haunts me; you require its confession. In obeying you I overcome a deep reluctance and hope my confidence will increase your love. Until we meet, Felix.

CHAPTER I. TWO CHILDHOODS

What genius, nourished by tears, will one day write the truest elegy for childhoods starved of affection? A life whose tender roots find only stone at home moves more surely than any public tragedy. Such was my childhood.

From infancy I endured neglect interrupted by brief scrutiny. Left with a nurse for three years, I returned to a household dominated by indifference; servants, urged by a favored child and fearing our mother's temper, often persecuted me. Regular childhood alliances were absent. I was punished for my brother’s faults and forbidden to prove my innocence. These injustices did not corrupt me but hardened me: constant expectation of new blows bred sullen resignation and a premature pride that tempered the ill effects of neglect.

Sometimes my mother spoke of educating me herself; the idea chilled me, and I blessed the neglect that let me wander in gardens, watch insects, and fix my mind on a star. Once, crouched beneath a fig-tree absorbed by a star, I was falsely accused by our governess, Mademoiselle Caroline, of tampering with a garden faucet and punished severely; still, the star remained mine. Forbidden pleasures grew more desirable, and that glowing point in my imagination sustained me through boyhood.

Charles, five years my senior, was the household favorite—handsome, robust, indulged. I, puny and sickly, was sent at five to a town school as a day pupil with a scanty lunch. The contrast between my hunger and my classmates' plenty wounded me deeply. Boys taunted me over rillons and dainties I did not possess; a cruel trick—the promised slice withdrawn—left me ashamed and resolved. Once I was stoned; my mother heard and only lamented my supposed dangerous temperament. Rejected by family and schoolmates, I learned to distrust myself.

At twelve I went to Pont-le-Voy, an Oratorian school for dull boys, where for eight years I lived a pariah’s life. My three-franc monthly allowance barely covered necessities; I could not afford games and was ostracized except by humiliating flattery of the rich. I took refuge under a tree with borrowed books and thought. When I won prizes for composition and translation and my parents did not attend the theatre, I burned my crowns in tears, feeling the deeper ache of being forsaken.

The sense of injustice drove me to religion. At first communion I sought consolation in prayer and martyrology; youthful zeal fed sublime visions that strengthened the imagination and made me sensitive to inner life—qualities that later tempered my talent. At fifteen my father, uneasy about the Oratorians, placed me in a Paris institution, the Lepitre Academy. There neglect continued: meager pocket-money, strict discipline, and social restrictions. Monsieur Lepitre, given to Bourbon sympathies, contrived a small subvention but not enough.

In Lepitre’s house the porter Doisy smuggled comforts for the better-off pupils; resisting him was heroic for a hungry boy. My parents' arrival in Paris once promised relief: my brother planned to take us to the Palais-Royal and to a theatre. But I owed a hundred francs to Doisy; my mother, infuriated, refused indulgence and reproached me with future ruin. Sent back to Lepitre, I lost the dinner and the play—the first meeting with my mother after twelve years ended in reproach.

At nineteen, still under Lepitre's guardianship but longing for Paris, I grew desperate. My aunt, the Marquise de Listomère, ceremonious and cold on the Ile Saint-Louis, would not offer money and shamed me for my youth. Several attempts to slip to the Palais-Royal were thwarted by unlucky coincidences: Lepitre’s coach, my mother’s unexpected arrival, the post-chaise that brought her. My family's anxieties—political and moral—kept me restrained.

Study was both refuge and peril; absorbed in books I delayed the normal stirrings of youth. In Tours my mother’s coldness crushed me; on a journey home I threw myself at her feet, begged for affection, and was mocked as playing at "comedy." At Blois I once went to the bridge intending to throw myself into the Loire but could not commit suicide. Back at home I found only the slight mercies of my sisters and poor clothing. My mother's ironical speeches and punctilious sense of duty made affection an unreliable thing.

Yet Tours had public excitement. The Duc d'Angouleme passed in triumph; the town celebrated, and I longed to attend a ball in his honor. Despite my mother's mockery about the request, secret preparations were made: a bottle-blue coat, silk stockings, and a frilled shirt—my first finery. The ball overwhelmed me with heat, music, a crush of people, the military band and cries for the duke. Initially the spectacle disgusted me, but then a strange ambition stirred—an appetite for glory and power over the masses.

Among the crowd I saw a woman who altered me. Misjudged as a sleepy lad, I leant against a woman's shoulders I took for a mother; she rebuked me with a single word, "Monsieur!" Tears sprang to my eyes; the look that followed—noble, angry, forgiving—awoke in me a passion I had never known. She moved with queenly grace, carrying a purity and brightness that transformed my life: my star had become flesh and blood. From then the sky seemed bluer and I sought her everywhere in Touraine.

My mother, seeing my melancholy, sent me for a few weeks to Frapesle, a château on the Indre, hoping country air would restore my health. For a young man newly in love but ignorant of the object's name or station, this was opportunity. Walking the countryside, I scanned every house and old tower as if she might live there. The landscape—moors, woods, the valley of the Indre—enchanted me; beauty seemed intrinsic to the air and matched my inner mood.

From a walnut-tree where I later loved to pause I first saw the castle that housed the woman I sought: a white spot among vines, cambric fluttering in an arbor. The castle, Clochegourde, belonged to the Comte de Mortsauf and his wife, a Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt—an ancient, proud, secluded house with limited fortune and fixed habits. My host, Monsieur de Chessel, knowing the count and countess, suggested we call; his teasing revealed how transparent my feelings were.

Clochegourde was modest but tasteful: a façade of five windows, terraced gardens, acacias, vines, neat outbuildings. From the road the ground floor seemed an upper story; a tourelle and carved crest testified to lineage. The house fit the valley like a jewel; approaching it quickened my heart with foreboding and delight.

We were announced after the bell for breakfast; the count was at Azay but the countess, whose voice I had recognized at the ball, welcomed us. My agitation nearly betrayed me; she returned to her embroidery and spoke with a calm dignity that both concealed and revealed feeling. Monsieur de Chessel explained my fatigue; Madame de Mortsauf offered restoratives and gentle courtesy. Her glance, at first severe, softened when she saw my distress. Her invitation to dine, accepted, seemed a promise: if I pleased her, my introducer might keep favor with the count.

Madame de Mortsauf, reserved yet radiant, enchanted me. At dinner she spoke of harvests and local affairs; her cadence, the brevity of her glances, the rare luminous intonations moved me more than artifice. I prolonged conversations and accepted reproach merely to hear the melody of her voice and breathe her presence.

Up close she retained the luminous appeal that had struck me at the ball. Her hair was fair, her brow broad and thoughtful; her eyes green tinged with brown, usually wan but capable of sudden gleams. A Grecian nose and graceful lips gave her spiritual beauty; her figure combined strength with softness. She seemed both mother and maiden—grave from suffering, yet childlike in feeling. Her movements, her rare glances, her plain dress formed a charm that made men dream: virginal purity and moral elevation without ambition.

More than features, it was the constant emanation of her soul—tenderness to family, cheerfulness in serenity, resignation amid storms—that impressed me. Clochegourde’s salon, panelled and modest, matched her spirit: gray paint, porcelain vases, cambric curtains, an embroidery frame. From its windows the valley unrolled—Pont-de-Ruan, château d'Azay, Sache—images that nourished my ideas and changed my mind. Many of my earliest thoughts in science and politics seemed first to live there, in that composed atmosphere.

When Monsieur de Mortsauf returned I watched him closely. A little girl, Madeleine, frail and pale, entered and clouded the mother’s brow; Jacques, her brother, was similarly weak. The children’s frailty suggested family sorrow; Madame de Mortsauf’s health and spirit bore the cost. Monsieur de Mortsauf, about forty-five but aged by care, offered a polite, uneasy courtesy; he seemed a man whose distrust sprang from inability to understand rather than malice. Learning my name and family connections, his manner warmed—a respectful recognition of lineage in a man of old family.

Thus began days at Clochegourde that would shape me. The valley, the house, the maternal presence, and that first vivid encounter set in motion a passion combining gratitude, reverence, and the hunger of first love. My lonely childhood had prepared me to adore with an intensity that neglect can create. The domestic world of the Mortsaufs—humble and dignified—provided the soil in which my youthful devotion took root.

I learned the subtleties of Madame de Mortsauf’s manner: the economy of her speech, the weight of her silences, glances scarce as jewels. Her conversation, even about trivial matters, carried an undertone of feeling revealing depth. Her benevolence and austere tenderness drew me where admiration and self-sacrifice mingled. The simplicity of the household—the neat salon, embroidery, the garden paths—spoke of moral discipline and inner warmth.

The contrast between modest means and noble air left an impression: pride without display, ancestral dignity humbled by circumstance. This image of noble poverty deepened my reverence. In the strangeness of my past—neglected by my mother, punished unjustly, driven inward by study and prayer—the spectacle of such domestic devotion opened me like a flower. Clochegourde, its valley, and above all its countess became the scene and cause of a passion that would shape my destiny.

Our visit ended with impressions words cannot fully render: Madame de Mortsauf’s presence, the music of her voice, her discreet tenderness for the children, and the quiet dignity of the home remained with me. I left with mingled hope and helplessness—hope from an attentive hour in her presence; helplessness because I was too young, too dependent, and too ignorant of the world to win her beyond the simple worship of a distant admirer.

Thus closes the account of my early years to the point where life after twenty begins under influences both tender and decisive. From neglect to the sanctuary of the valley, my character was formed—partly by solitude and hard study, partly by the sudden appearance of a woman whose moral and physical beauty became my guiding star. The meeting at the ball and at Clochegourde introduced an affection equal parts devotion and pain, opening the way to all that followed.

Though ten years of emigration and farm life had altered his body, the Comte de Mortsauf still bore traces of nobility: chivalric loyalty, steadfast convictions, honest politics, but ignorance of France’s practical affairs. He was the upright sort who, unable to be useful to his party, might hurt it; ready to sacrifice his life rather than his money. At dinner I noticed his flaccid cheeks and furtive looks at his children—signs of a burdened mind and a frail constitution he had passed on. He blamed himself but refused to be judged; his harshness, born of self-awareness, magnified the misery his faults had brought the family. When his wife returned with the children, unhappiness settled over the house.

After the conversation shifted he introduced me to his wife and, with curious intimacy, revealed family details I had not known. He asked my age; the countess, surprised, inspected me with maternal curiosity and hope for her children. Some barriers between us dissolved. She said, “If study has made you ill, the air of our valley will soon restore you.” The count, however, denounced modern education—burdening children with mathematics and sciences, aging them prematurely—and urged a return of education to religious hands. He added later, explaining his political stance, “I shall always distrust men of talent,” which clarified why he had refused to support a promising candidate.

He proposed a tour of the gardens; a brief interruption from Madame—revealing his desire to control the household—preceded a remark about Monsieur de Vandenesse’s arrival from Tours and Monsieur de Chessel’s escort of him on foot. I sensed then that navigating the count’s royalism required care. The manservant announced dinner; Monsieur de Chessel offered his arm to Madame de Mortsauf, while the count took mine.

The dining room, though unpretentious, had charm: white tiles, varnished floral paper, family silver, mismatched china, flowers on the table. Dinner was modest but plentiful. I sat beside the countess, served her wine, and felt a profound connection. A kiss had already bound us in a secret that filled me with joy and shame. I lavished affection on the dogs and engaged the children, hoping to be accepted. Any gloom would have jeopardized my place in their lives.

Dinner passed in a haze of happiness: I reveled in being under her roof, ignoring her apparent reserve and the count’s polite indifference. Love, like life, has an adolescence, and I was content in my feelings. Foolish remarks, born of passion, went unnoticed; even she seemed oblivious to love. I returned across the Indre enchanted by moonlight and the sounds of nature; that evening marked a turning point—past struggles clashed with newly experienced joy.

Approaching Frapesle I saw Clochegourde and a small boat tied to a tree—Monsieur de Mortsauf’s fishing boat. Once out of earshot, Monsieur de Chessel congratulated me on the warm reception I had received, telling me I had stepped into the count’s heart at once. His praise lifted me, though I sensed underlying bitterness.

Monsieur de Chessel—actually Durand—had abandoned his father’s merchant name to adopt an old parliamentary one. Ambition led him to claim a count’s title under Louis XVIII, a title his children enjoyed without understanding the sacrifices. He was petty and envious; his recent royal favor made him ostentatious, yet he was kindly to me in a paternal way I welcomed after years of neglect. His ostentation annoyed some neighbors while Mortsauf, with limited means, struggled to maintain his estate. Their relations were polite but never intimate.

Jealousy did not fully explain the Comte de Mortsauf’s isolation. His education had been shallow; emigration interrupted the deepening of his learning, and long idleness followed. Hopes for a restored monarchy kept him from seeking work; life’s trials eroded his spirit, natural gaiety faded into moroseness, and he became inclined to hypochondria. Hidden love affairs further harmed his health and prospects.

After twelve years he returned under a decree allowing emigrants back. Crossing the Rhine, he cried “France! France!” like a child calling for its mother. Once wealthy, he came back impoverished and stripped of influence; his name offered no protection. His rigid opinions and past made him vulnerable to ridicule. He eventually settled in Maine on a forgotten farm that provided some stability. The Lenoncourt family, having lost fortune, welcomed him; he stayed with them while preparing a home. Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt accepted his proposal and lived with her aunt, the Duchesse de Verneuil, who profoundly influenced the countess’s devout character. The duchess’s death cast a shadow over their early married years, though Saint-Martin later visited Clochegourde to correct his works.

The birth of the count’s first child, Jacques, brought despair: the doctor pronounced the child unviable, a truth the count concealed from his wife. Subsequent births confirmed his fears and deepened his sense of failure. The countess, sensing her husband’s turmoil, devoted herself to his care, accepting his miserliness and mistrust and guiding him toward what was right—often at the expense of her own happiness. She resolved to remain at Clochegourde, fearing that leaving would harm their children. Her efforts masked his incapacity and allowed him some peace in her presence.

My own understanding of Clochegourde deepened as I spent time there. One evening I found the countess watching a sunset; we acknowledged a shared sorrow, only to be interrupted by the children. She welcomed my visits, seeing in me an amusement for her husband. I often played backgammon with the count, enduring his harshness. To keep peace I learned to balance the game—letting him win at first, then evening the score—an unspoken strategy the countess recognized with a grateful look that filled me with joy.

Once, after an intense game, the count erupted in rage. I waited in the garden; when she joined me she revealed her husband’s condition and begged me to forget the scene. “Promise me to bury the recollection,” she implored, asking only for my word of honor. I pledged to do all she wished, though I could not change my opinion of him. She asked that I respect her freedom—never take her hand unless she gave it—and to refrain from undue warmth. As I departed I felt a profound connection, pledging my love in silence.

In the days that followed small moments deepened my affection. I resolved to support her, be a source of strength, and honor the bond we had formed. Our shared sufferings had forged a rare, precious understanding; beneath the stars I vowed to cherish and protect that love through life’s trials.

This scene occurred on a Tuesday; I had waited until Sunday and had not crossed the river. Much had happened at Clochegourde during those five days. The count received his brevet as general of brigade, the cross of Saint-Louis, and a pension of four thousand francs. The Duc de Lenoncourt-Givry was restored as peer of France, reclaimed two forests and his place at court; his wife recovered unsold property that had once been imperial crown lands. As a result the Comtesse de Mortsauf became an heiress: her mother arrived at Clochegourde with a hundred thousand francs saved at Givry—the dowry the count had never demanded despite earlier poverty. With his own savings he bought two neighboring estates producing about nine thousand francs a year. Their son would inherit the grandfather’s peerage; the count thought to entail the estate upon him without harming Madeleine, and the duc could likely help secure a good marriage.

These events calmed the count. The duchesse’s presence at Clochegourde affected the neighborhood. I reflected gloomily that her caste spirit, hidden from me by my love for Henriette, now revealed itself. Who was I—poor, insignificant, with only courage and faculties? On Sunday morning, seated in the private chapel with Monsieur and Madame de Chessel and the Abbé de Quelus, I stole a glance toward the duchess’s pew and saw her with her daughter, the count and his children, at prayer. Henriette’s broad straw hat did not tremble; her apparent unconcern at my presence bound me to her more than our past. She prayed with such abandonment she seemed a religious statue; it moved me profoundly.

After mass vespers followed. Madame de Chessel proposed going to Frapesle rather than cross the Indre twice in the heat. Monsieur de Chessel offered his arm to the duchess, Madame de Chessel took the count’s, and I offered mine to the countess, feeling her arm against mine for the first time. Walking through the woods of Sache, where light filtered through foliage, sensations and proud thoughts overwhelmed me; my heart beat violently.

“What is the matter?” she asked.

“I have heard of your good fortune,” I said. “Will your new dignities change you and lessen your friendship?”

“Change me!” she cried. “Oh, fie! Another such idea and I shall—not despise you, but forget you forever.”

I looked at her in ecstasy.

“We profit by new laws which we have neither made nor sought,” she said. “We are neither place-hunters nor beggars; neither Monsieur de Mortsauf nor I can leave Clochegourde. By my advice he declined the command at the Maison Rouge. We are content my father should have it. This forced modesty has benefited our son. The king—whose household my father joins—will favor Jacques’ education. He must represent both Lenoncourt and Mortsauf. I can have no ambition except for him; he must live and be made worthy. Who will keep him safe in Paris? My friend,” she added, voice breaking, “be some day his guardian and sponsor. Go to Paris; if your family will not support you, ours—especially my mother, who rules society—will help you. Put your desires into a noble ambition—”

“I understand,” I interrupted. “Ambition will be my mistress. But I will not be rewarded for obedience here by favors there. I will go and make my own way. From you, I would accept everything; from others, nothing.”

“Child!” she murmured, pleased.

“Besides, I have taken my vows,” I continued. “I am resolved to bind myself to you by ties that can never be broken.”

She trembled and stopped.

“What do you mean?” she asked.

“First tell me how you wish me to love you.”

“Love me as my aunt loved me,” she answered. “I gave you her rights when I permitted you to call me by her chosen name.”

“Then I must love without hope and with absolute devotion. I will enter a seminary, become a priest, and educate your son. Jacques shall be myself in his own form; political conceptions, energy, patience—I will give him all. I will live near you; my love, enclosed in religion, can never be suspected of evil. I will consume myself in the flame and love you with a purified love.”

She turned pale. “Félix, do not bind yourself in a way that might be an obstacle. I should die of grief. Do you think despairing love a life’s vocation? Wait for life’s trials. Marry neither the Church nor a woman—marry not at all—I forbid it. Remain free. You are twenty-one—My God! can I have mistaken him? I thought two months sufficed to know some souls.”

“What hope have you?” I cried.

“Accept our help, rise in life, make your fortune, and you shall know my hope. And,” she added as if whispering, “never release the hand you are holding at this moment.”

She bent to my ear as she said it.

“Madeleine!” I exclaimed. “Never!”

We neared the wooden gate into Frapesle’s park. At that instant the thought of the count’s death flashed through me, and I said, “I understand you.”

“I am glad of it,” she answered, surprised.

Her purity provoked tears of admiration mingled with the bitterness of passion. I thought she did not love me enough even to wish me liberty; love that recoils from crime seemed limited. She did not love me, I thought. To hide my pain, I kissed Madeleine’s hair. “I am afraid of your mother,” I said.

“So am I,” she answered playfully. “Don’t forget to call her Madame la duchesse and speak of her in the third person. Young people have lost polite manners; you must learn them for my sake. It is good taste to respect women, no matter their age, and to recognize social distinctions. The respect shown to established superiority guarantees what is due to you. You have drunk the milk of the Revolution in your academy; but as you advance, you will find crude principles of liberty powerless to create happiness. Before considering what an aristocracy ought to be, my common sense tells me societies can exist only through a hierarchy. Be on our side—especially now,” she added, laughing, “when it triumphs.”

I was moved by her combination of political feeling and affection. In defending her husband she foresaw criticisms I might make of the servility he showed her mother. Monsieur de Mortsauf, king in his castle, had dignity; yet I was astonished at the obsequious distance he put between himself and the duchess. Humiliated by his degradation, I understood the martyrdom of generous women yoked to petty men. I treated the duchess with respect because of my youth; where others saw a duchess, I saw Henriette’s mother, and that sanctified my homage.

At Frapesle the count presented me to the duchess, who examined me coldly. Madame de Lenoncourt was fifty-six, wonderfully preserved, erect and imposing, with a hard face and courtly manner reminiscent of my own mother. I behaved well; as I passed the countess she said softly, “You are perfect.”

The count took my hand. “You are not angry with me, Félix, are you? If I was hasty, pardon an old soldier. We shall probably stay for dinner, and I invite you on Thursday, the evening before the duchess leaves. I must go to Tours tomorrow. Don’t neglect Clochegourde. My mother-in-law is an acquaintance I advise you to cultivate. Her salon will set the tone for the faubourg St. Germain. She has all the traditions of the great world and immense social knowledge.”

The count’s changed fortunes softened him. The duchess was not patronizing; Monsieur and Madame de Chessel accepted the invitation. She questioned me about my family and, learning that the Vandenesse in diplomacy was my brother, grew almost affectionate. She told me my great-aunt, the old Marquise de Listomere, was a Grandlieu, and that by a provision of the Charter my father was once more Marquis de Vandenesse.

“I am but one thing, the serf of Clochegourde,” I answered.

The Restoration’s transformations bewildered our generation. To me, the revolution meant nothing; the least word from Madame de Mortsauf was the single important event. I was ignorant of councils and politics; my sole ambition was to love Henriette better than Petrarch loved Laura. This indifference made the duchess take me for a child. A large company assembled at Frapesle, and to a young man unused to the world, seeing the woman he loves admired by all is intoxicating. The count, delighted, seemed almost young; his wife hoped for change; I amused myself with Madeleine, whose cleverness made others laugh. It was a happy day; a word, a hope awakened in the morning and illuminated nature. Seeing me joyous, Henriette was joyful too.

“That happiness smiling on my gray and cloudy life seems good,” she said to me the next day.

I spent that day at Clochegourde; the count left early for Tours. A serious disagreement arose between mother and daughter. The duchess wanted the countess to move to Paris, where she promised a place at court and where the count might accept a position. Henriette, concealing her husband’s incapacity and her own sufferings, opposed leaving Clochegourde because its air calmed his excitements and soothed the children’s health. She had persuaded him to go to Tours so as to hide his condition from the duchess. I alone knew Clochegourde’s dark secret.

Henriette saw that the duchess cared little for Jacques and Madeleine—a painful discovery. The duchess brooked no opposition; she used sweetness, then coldness, then sarcasm to force compliance. In those ten days Henriette endured the struggles a young woman must face to assert independence. The mother and daughter had nothing in common; the duchess could not imagine the reasons Henriette stayed. Tortured and wounded, Henriette loved the one who did not strike her, who would build a shield around her. I was happy when she rested upon my heart; she told me of her troubles. Day by day I learned what she meant by “Love me as my aunt loved me.”

“Have you no ambition?” the duchess asked me at dinner.

“Madame,” I replied, “I have enough in me to conquer the world; but I am only twenty-one, and I am all alone.”

She looked at her daughter in astonishment. The duchess’s visit meant constraint. The countess begged me to be cautious. The Thursday of ceremony was wearying, but the duchess returned to court and Clochegourde resumed its ordinary life.

My quarrel with the count had made me more at home; I could go there at all times. I clung like a climbing plant to the beautiful soul that had opened the world of shared emotions. Our fraternal marriage, based on trust, grew firmer; Henriette enfolded me with maternal care, while my love for her burned when away. I loved her with a double love—seraphic in her presence, searing when absent. At my age I was not yet man enough to torment her; she feared harm to her children and sat beside them when her husband allowed her a little rest. The mere sound of ardor shook her; she needed veiled love—support mingled with tenderness—the devotion she gave others. This brought languor and secret immolation. Her conscience and self-devotion awed me; the piety that bound her virtues filled the air with spiritual incense. She gave me only the back of her hand to kiss, never the palm, as though limiting sensuality. No two souls ever clasped each other with such ardor while remaining unbound. Later I understood why: in youth we love the whole woman—her children and interests—as our own.

I became part of the household and knew infinite sweetness—the privilege of being the mistress’s friend in a strange family. Servants treated me kindly; the children divined my affection and were discreet. True passions bloom more charmingly in barren soil.

But I also saw Monsieur de Mortsauf’s failings: perpetual scolding about nothing, complaints of imaginary ills, an inward dissatisfaction that demanded victims. On walks he chose the way yet complained whichever way we went; at home he blamed others. He took pleasure in touching his wife’s sensitive chords. Sometimes he fell into gloomy silence that alarmed her and brought tender attentions. Like a spoiled child, he let her serve him as she did the children. I discovered his petty tyranny in household details and realized how it stifled the family, hindered management, and impeded improvement of the estate. The countess had been noble in describing these daily struggles. My admiration for her rose above my love; I yielded to be a voluntary victim to come nearer her. She allowed me a place beside her and permission to share her sorrows; eager to die in her arena, I became a repentant apostate seeking heaven with my brethren.

“Were it not for you, I must have succumbed under this life,” Henriette said when the count had been more venomous than usual.

He had gone to bed. Henriette and I stayed under the acacias with the children playing in the setting sun. Our few words revealed mutual thoughts; when language failed our silence served, and our souls seemed to enter one another. We luxuriated in pensive languor and asked by a look, “Among all our days on earth, will there be one for us?”

Despite the evening’s poetry, desire crept over me. After three months of repression I could not accept my assigned fate. I took Henriette’s hand and caressed it. She withdrew at once; tears from my eyes fell and she offered her hand to my lips.

“You must know,” she said, “that this will cause me grief. A friendship that asks such a favor is dangerous.”

Then I lost control; I reproached her, spoke of my suffering and of a slight alleviation I dared request. I told her that at my age the soul had a sex and that I could meet death but not silence my cries. She forced me to silence with a proud glance. Ever since the gate of Frapesle, when I had taken her hope to be born from a grave, I had shunned the thought of staining her soul. She said she could never be wholly mine; if I could not be to her somewhat as her old confessor—less than a lover yet more than a brother—she must never see me again. She could die and offer her sufferings to God.

“I gave you,” she said, “more than I ought, so that nothing might be left to take, and I am punished.”

I calmed her, promised never to cause pain, and to love her as an old man loves his youngest child.

The next day I came early; there were no flowers in her gray salon. I rushed into the fields and vineyards to make two bouquets; as I gathered them it occurred to me that color and foliage have meanings, and by melody awaken memory. I enlisted Jacques and Madeleine and arranged two bouquets on the portico steps: white roses and lilies with blue flowers intermingled, symbolizing two purities—the child and the martyr. The countess discerned the sentiment and gave me a poignant glance, reward enough. I pressed Père Castel’s theories into service and recovered that lost science: the language of flowers. What charm in expressing our sensations through these daughters of the sun! I communed with the flora, seeking varieties that would complete my messages.

Twice a week I pursued this poetic enterprise, walking long distances to find flowers, taking pleasure in the world of feeling rather than form. Nature offered scenes rising to high moral conceptions: heather with dew like diamonds, mossy rocks like savage cries, anemone pulsatilla like my pure idol alone in her valley, sheets of water with floating life, a cottage garden as humble existence’s image, a forest path like a cathedral nave. I gathered these images and poured them into bouquets.

In September and October no bouquet cost less than three hours’ search. These floral symphonies expressed what no declaration could; my baffled desires found expression as Beethoven finds it in notes. In their presence Madame de Mortsauf was my Henriette. She looked at them often; they nourished her spirit. These neutral pleasures soothed me: abstinence exhausts, and a few crumbs of heavenly comfort keep one from disaster. Sometimes I found her standing before the bouquets, lost in agitation; she would lift her head and murmur, “Ah, how beautiful!”

Throughout the month I often saw her at the window when I arrived. If I was late, she wandered on the terrace and said she had come to meet me and tend her youngest child.

Backgammon had ended; the count’s purchases occupied him in inspecting his new properties. He was neither arrogant nor offensive, and our walks together were mutual joys. Once, the count, wishing to know what we talked about, asked; Henriette answered with words having a double meaning that satisfied him. Madeleine laughed. Our spiritual union continued.

The days passed with new joys. The grape harvest—the vintage—began in late September. The vintage brings abundance and hilarity: coopers and carts, girls and panniers. Classes are equal for the day; everyone shares the harvest. Jacques and Madeleine, who had never seen a vintage, were full of delight. We went to Villaines for baskets; none of us were to eat too many grapes. Jacques made me swear to stay at Clochegourde. The day in the vineyard was glorious: the children’s chatter, their racing about, the mother laughing with the joy of youth; she passed a hand round my neck and tapped my cheek—the only caress she ever gave me. I filled my basket and emptied it into the vat, moving slowly in regulated motion that kept my spirit free. I found the pleasure of external labor that regulates passion; I learned monastic discipline.

For the first time in weeks the count was neither surly nor cruel. His son’s rosy face rejoiced him. He had promised a dance in front of Clochegourde to celebrate the Bourbons’ return. As we returned, the countess leaned on my arm and whispered, “You bring us happiness.”

To me, burdened with knowledge of her sleepless nights and cares, those words were more than any other woman could give.

“The terrible monotony of my life is broken; all things are radiant with hope,” she said. “Oh, never leave me! Be the elder son, the protector of the younger.”

This is not romantic; in youth passion restrained by obstacles can fill the crater of a volcano with pure water.

We had another fête. The countess, wishing to accustom her children to practical life, gave Jacques the produce of the walnut trees and Madeleine that of the chestnuts. Gathering chestnuts and walnuts, seeing the children take pride in their small granaries, made charming scenes of innocent happiness amid autumn’s fading colors. Jacques’ walnuts suffered from rain; I advised him to hold back and sell later. His father urged prudence; the mother rejoiced to see Jacques listen. These homely scenes attached me ever more closely to Henriette.

I learned how, over ten years, the countess had changed cultivation—rotating crops and dividing the estate into four farms on share leases. She induced the count to cultivate a model farm and rebuild two farmhouses to relet for money rents afterward. Fearing to die before her husband, she sought to secure his income and her children’s property. Fruit trees planted in the last ten years were bearing; hedges and elms grew well. With the new purchases and farming system, Clochegourde—divided into four farms—could bring in forty thousand francs a year, not counting vineyards and woodlands. The new road to Chinon would attract tenants. The countess wished to invest fifteen thousand francs in each new estate to improve houses for better rents; this required thirty thousand francs, which she and her husband argued about. The fear “If I die tomorrow, what will become of them?” drove her. The count objected, but common sense won. That day we went to the Cassine and the Rhetorière to decide on buildings; she spoke gently, confident of success. A carrier wanted a large farm on the new route; another honest man was ready to take la Baude; the soil would attract tenants. In two years Clochegourde might yield twenty-four thousand francs. Gravelotte in Maine was rented for seven thousand a year; his pension was four thousand. Later additions might let her go to Paris and arrange Jacques’ education.

With what feeling she said “Paris!” I knew she wished not to be separated from her friend. I told her I would finish my education and work to be Jacques’ tutor. She looked grave.

“No, Félix,” she said. “That cannot be, any more than your priesthood. I thank you as a mother, but as a woman who loves you, I can never allow you to be the victim of your attachment. Such a position would be a social discredit to you. My father would never forgive your taking such a humble place; he would refuse you his protection. You must accept society as it is; never fly in its face.”

“Love,” I whispered.

“No, charity,” she said, controlling tears. “This wild idea enlightens me as to your character; your heart will be your bane. I shall claim the right to teach you sometimes. Let my woman’s eye see for you. From the solitudes of Clochegourde I will share in your successes. As to a tutor, do not fear; we shall find some good old abbé, my father will devote sums to the boy’s education. Jacques is my pride; he is eleven years old.”

At the Cassine Jacques, Madeleine, and I followed her like children. I left to speak with Martineau and saw how beloved she was. A laborer told me she was good and knew our hardships and treated the people well. I gave that man all the money I had.

A pony arrived for Jacques; the father, an excellent horseman, would accustom him gradually. The morning of the first lesson, with Madeleine shouting and all the household clustered round, was a maternal festival. Jacques wore a blue collar embroidered by his mother and rode fearlessly. The count smiled for the first time in months; the mother burst into tears and leaned on my arm, “I think I have never suffered. Do not leave us today.” The child jumped into her arms; I and Madeleine arranged bouquets for dinner. She said, “The fifteenth of October is certainly a great day for me. Jacques has taken his first riding lesson, and I have just set the last stitch in my furniture cover.”

“Then, Blanche,” the count laughed, “I must pay you for it.”

He offered her his arm; an open carriage her father had sent awaited with two English horses. As we returned, she whispered anxiously, “I am too happy; happiness is like illness—it overwhelms me; I fear it may vanish.”

I loved her so passionately I was jealous. She asked me what my eyes said; I told her. She was more touched than by any gift, then poured comfort into my heart. “Love me as my aunt loved me,” she said. “If I take your life, must I not be grateful?”

“It was time I finished my tapestry,” she added. “Men find occupations a resource against troubles; we women need physical exercise to control inward sufferings. Each stitch rocked my thoughts; to every stitch I confided my secrets. While doing my last chair I thought much of you. What you put into your bouquets I have said in my embroidery.”

Dinner was lovely. Jacques offered his garland to his mother with grace. In the afternoon we played backgammon; the count was charming. They accompanied me to Frapesle in the tranquil twilight. It was a day of days for the poor woman.

Soon the riding lessons became a source of contention. The countess feared the father’s harsh reprimands; Jacques grew thin and wore dark circles. I advised him to tell his father when tired, but the measure failed, and the old huntsman took over. Angry reproaches resumed. Then the cost of the Cassine and Rhetorière buildings exceeded estimates by half. The news, given first to Monsieur de Mortsauf instead of to his wife, sparked a quarrel that grew bitter until the count’s malady displayed itself.

That day I had gone from Frapesle to search for flowers with Madeleine. Returning, I could not find her and heard cries in the house. Madeleine ran out: “The general,” she cried, “is scolding mamma; go and defend her.”

I entered the salon unseen. Monsieur de Mortsauf was in a violent tirade.

“Never marry, Félix,” he said. “A woman is led by the devil; the most virtuous would invent evil if it did not exist; they are all vile.”

He harked back to old troubles, cursed the new farming system, declared he could have been twice as rich had he managed Clochegourde differently. He sprang about, displacing furniture; then stopped, moaned his marrow was on fire, and accused his wife of ruining him.

“Yes, Blanche,” he cried, “you are my executioner; you are killing me; you are a monster of hypocrisy. She is smiling! Do you know why she smiles, Félix?”

I kept silence.

“That woman denies me all happiness; she is no more to me than to you, and yet she pretends to be my wife! She fulfills none of the duties of laws, human and divine; she lies to God and man. She puts all her art into keeping me away; she has made me mad through privations; she is killing me by degrees, and she thinks herself a saint and takes the sacrament every month!”

Henriette wept bitterly, answering only, “Monsieur! monsieur! monsieur!”

His words burned me with shame for him and anger because they struck at chastity and delicacy—the warp and woof of first love.

“She is virgin at my expense,” cried the count.

At this the countess cried, “Monsieur!”

“And what mean you by your imperious ‘Monsieur!’?” he shouted. “Am I not your master? Must I teach you that I am?”

He advanced, face white, eyes savage. Henriette slid from her chair to avoid a blow, fell full-length, and lost consciousness. The count stopped bewildered. I took her in my arms; the count opened her bedroom door. We laid her, still dressed, on the bed. When she revived she motioned to unfasten her belt; the count found scissors; I gave her salts and she opened her eyes. The count left the room, ashamed. Two hours passed in silence. Henriette’s hand lay in mine; she pressed it but could not speak. Once she rose on her elbow and whispered, “Unhappy man!—ah! if you did but know—” then sank back. The memory of past sufferings and the present shock threw her into convulsions I calmed by an instinctive magnetism. I smoothed her hair—the first time I touched it—and sat looking at the simple room: chintz curtains, a crucifix, her aunt’s portrait, the children’s drawings with locks of hair. What renunciations of luxury for such a woman—the chamber of a married nun, filled with holy resignation. Painful as the sight was, it exposed hidden, irreparable woe.

When the children and Manette came in I left. The count waited for me; he seemed to seek me as mediator. He caught my hands, exclaiming, “Stay, stay with us, Félix!”

“Unfortunately,” I said, “Monsieur de Chessel has a party, and my absence would cause remark. But after dinner I will return.”

He left the house with me and took me to the lower gate.

Soon after I reencountered my brother Charles, haughty and distant; his condescension made me awkward, though my deep love kept me from feeling foolish. Childhood sorrows and solitude had sharpened my sense of affection, and since returning from Clochegourde I could better recognize genuine attachment.

I often visited the Duchesse de Lenoncourt, where Henriette's name was seldom spoken. Only the old duke, whose welcome eased my shyness, seemed aware of our bond. Just as I began enjoying society again, the events of March 20th forced a change. At Henriette's advice I followed my brother to Ghent with the Duc de Lenoncourt. The duke’s amiability protected me when he perceived my loyalty to the Bourbons; he introduced me to the king, who valued my steadfastness. A letter from Madame de Mortsauf told of Jacques' illness; her distress compelled me to help. Putting rivalry with my brother aside, I left court to serve the sovereign as a reliable messenger, eager to return to Clochegourde.

After carrying out the king's orders I evaded Bonapartist authorities, travelling through the Vendée to Clochegourde. Jacques was better; the count welcomed me, and in the salon I met Henriette. Her surprise and joy were immediate: she prepared my room, the count saw to my food, and at dinner we learned of Waterloo and Napoleon’s flight. More than the political news, I noticed Henriette’s tenderness—she had even built an ice‑house for me—and absence had only intensified my love.

Henriette spoke of long nights at Jacques' bedside, prayers and exhausted vigilance, and the count’s harshness that made her words fall flat. Still she clung to duty, urging her children to respect their father and managing their farm with practical wisdom she sometimes called divine insight. She encouraged me to be available to the king and to pursue opportunities in Paris. The tension in her marriage haunted us both; she feared the count’s behaviour might harm the children and hesitated to assert herself. I urged firmness.

When the count fell ill we alternated caring for him, which drew us closer; his slow recovery, however, revived his difficult ways. Henriette repeatedly sacrificed her own happiness for her children. Eventually I returned to Paris, faithful to her. Our letters revealed her love and burden — Jacques' relapse later intensifying her pain. Throughout, her resilience and maternal devotion shone, and my loyalty grew with her suffering; our love remained a rare, guiding light I hoped would endure.

During a deeply emotional period I met a remarkable woman at the Élysée-Bourbon salons: immensely wealthy, of noble lineage and married to a distinguished British peer. Her beauty, grace, and wit made her the idol of Parisian society; she ruled with a firm yet charming demeanor.

The English, distant and reserved, live by strict laws and rigid virtues, producing an Englishwoman who, though delicate and charming, is trapped in hypocrisy and compelled to maintain appearances; her passion is intense but constrained. You may envy these women, but they are complex and convinced that love alone suffices.

My liaison with the Marchioness of Dudley became notorious. At a time when my better feelings were subdued, I was drawn to her; my fidelity to Madame de Mortsauf only increased her desire. Lady Dudley craved the chase, and my resistance inflamed her passion. Her mocking remark—“I am wearied to death of these turtle-dove sighings”—should have warned me.

Men have fewer means to resist women than women have to escape men. Social codes keep men from appearing harsh; women are expected to be coy. Though I loved Madame de Mortsauf, I was not immune to Lady Dudley’s beauty and rank. Her attention stirred me; she often hinted she would be my mistress. After one beguiling evening she made her intentions clear, provoking scandal in the British aristocracy.

Seemingly fragile, Lady Dudley possessed a strong will and remarkable courage—she could outpace any horse—and pursued her desires relentlessly. By contrast, Madame de Mortsauf was nurturing and expansive; her love was spiritual and boundless. Lady Dudley embodied a materialist Englishness: compelling physically but leaving an emptiness I did not feel with Madame de Mortsauf.

Lady Dudley’s jealousy sought to erase Madame de Mortsauf from my heart. My letters to Madame de Mortsauf went unanswered, and when I returned to Clochegourde her coldness revealed the harm my infidelity had caused. Her beauty was dimmed by sorrow; the count, unaware of the cause, welcomed me, while she remained distant. The count confessed his fears and ill health; I was tormented that I had contributed to her decline. Seeking forgiveness, I was rebuffed. Torn between Lady Dudley’s passionate allure and Madame de Mortsauf’s devoted love, I was left to confront the true nature of love and sacrifice as the weight of my actions pressed upon me.

I know nothing more destructive than the wit of an Englishwoman; it carries the pompous certainty with which the British mask their prejudices. French wit is lace; English wit is corrosive. Arabella wielded her power like a sultan, cutting down rivals with words.

She spoke to me in a mixture of passion and flattery meant to erase the impressions left by Henriette’s dignified love. “My angel,” she said, arguing that her love was not a crime but pious, that God created beauty to inspire adoration, and that the real sin would be not to love me. She contrasted her weakness with another woman’s strength, declared me above moral laws, and closed by claiming me forever. Her speech seduced and denigrated Henriette as prudish and unworthy. She demanded assurances, wept at the thought of losing me, and, after extracting pledges, dried her eyes and released me to return to Henriette if I willed—because loving me by choice would prove my love truer.

Under Arabella’s influence I went back to Clochegourde. In the moment I did not feel the falsity of my position; refusing would have let Lady Dudley triumph and taken me to Paris. But leaving then was fatal to my genuine love. I realized, belatedly, that any truly loving woman would rather see her lover die than know him happy with another; my departure made Madame de Mortsauf’s illusions burst.

When I arrived that morning she was pale and sleepless, distance between us like a precipice. Henriette’s illusions were broken; she refused to look at me, denying me the light that had warmed me for six years. She treated me with a kind of brotherly care—ostentatious attention that humiliated me. Our long intimacy yielded nothing now; we had nothing to say, concealing intolerable pain. Henriette called herself my sister; our forced silence intensified my shame. I cursed the pleasure Arabella had given me and wished Henriette would demand my blood. I could not attack Arabella before Henriette; had I mentioned her, Henriette would have despised me. After five years of intimacy we could not bridge the gulf.

Her manner grew cold; I resolved to leave. At the grove marking the estate boundary she stopped me. “Farewell, my friend,” she said, laying her head on my chest and embracing me. She declared she could foresee the future; she had seen me turn my back on her. She asked me to take the place of the mother she might lose, to care for Jacques and Madeleine. Startled by the fire in her eyes, I ordered her to swear to live and obey her doctor. She misread me, cried that she would do all I asked, and at my seeming submission I kissed her eyelids. The look we exchanged erased Arabella’s memory. For a moment I was once more the lover of an angel, torn between two irreconcilable passions: one virtuous and selfless, the other ostentatious and corrosive.

Yet I remained with Arabella in Paris, perhaps hoping the count’s death would restore me to Henriette, perhaps seduced by the corrupting spirit of the court. Arabella and I grew intimate quickly; we discarded prudence and displayed our union. She wanted to exhibit me to Paris as her “sposo,” keeping me under her roof by flaunting our liaison. At first this life enthralled me; then despair set in. I saw my life bound to a path that offended Henriette’s principles and advice. From then on I lived in a rage that ate at me. My letters to Henriette, expressing the moral malady I endured, did her infinite harm; she answered only once, pleading she wished my happiness even at so many treasures lost. But I was not happy; happiness leaves no room for comparisons. When the first ardor cooled, character’s asperities reasserted themselves, and natural antipathies resurfaced.

I began to examine Arabella critically. She lacked the qualities of mind that make Frenchwomen so transforming in love. A Frenchwoman, when truly in love, becomes mother, adviser, doctor; she adorns conversation and action with coquetry and self-abandonment, making love a varying grace. Arabella, by contrast, lived two lives: passionate in private, cold in public. She could set her love aside like a garment; society could govern her feelings; fashion often reigned over pleasures. Where Madame de Mortsauf’s smallest movements were constant caresses and faithful signs of devotion, Arabella’s mind existed for the world and for sarcasm. She loved to wound and jest; she exhibited affection but kept an impenetrable English reserve in public. Her ambivalence—ostentation plus dignity, love plus coldness—wounded me. In private she mixed tenderness with cutting sarcasm; in public she preserved a composed, ambassador-like aloofness that outraged my heart.

The contrast extended beyond manners to inward generosity and domestic feeling. Henriette studied my prospects, engaged ardently in my permitted ambitions, and took hold of my life with modest ardor. Lady Dudley showed only the reticence of an acquaintance; she never inquired into my affairs and separated self-interest from love. She was prodigal for herself but not generous in the way that nourishes a man’s life. To save me a pang, Henriette would have labored for me in secret; Lady Dudley would not. French luxury expresses the man’s spirit and reproduces his ideas between lovers; English luxury, initially alluring, proved mechanical and cold. The thousand attentions at Clochegourde expressed care and soul; Arabella treated them as servants’ business. Her candid selfishness, her theatrical crimson draperies over an iron nature, gradually revealed a stony rock where my tender seeds were wasted.

When weariness of Arabella and longing for Clochegourde’s serenity possessed me, I received a blow whose echo has not ceased. While I was working in the king’s cabinet the Duc de Lenoncourt entered and told the king that my poor daughter—Henriette—was dying. I begged leave at once. The court’s cold tone, the king’s ironic look when we crossed paths, and the pomp that seemed to trample my beloved—all this provoked bitter reflections on the world’s insensitivity. On the road beyond Tours I met Monsieur Origet, who told me that Henriette was dying of inanition: her stomach had rejected nourishment for forty days. He explained that her illness was essentially grief made physical—an inertia of an organ as necessary as the heart. This accusation pierced me.

I hurried to Clochegourde filled with remorse. Jacques, Madeleine, and the Abbé Dominis knelt at a wooden cross; the old huntsman stood apart. The house was dumb with grief. The count, white-headed, alternated between despair and accusation. The abbé explained how, for months, Henriette’s suffering had increased despite medical care; the mind must be healed before the body. Her words had grown bitter and contradictory, torn between wanting to live and wanting release. Despair gripped me as I approached the house.

They warned me not to enter unprepared; the countess was greatly changed. Her room had been given a tenderness of decoration. She sat in white before the fire, flowers around her; her face had a greenish pallor, hollow eyes, and features altered by prolonged suffering, but still a forehead in proportion and, at times, inward fires of feeling. She knew she had been changed and met me with bitterness and irony. When I took her hand, she spoke of death as the end of love and reproached me with coming to surprise her in such a state. She alternately hoped to recover and disparaged life. She kissed me, clung to me, and fainted; the doctor noted it had been forty-two days since she had eaten or drunk. The abbés and the doctor feared for her; they gave her opium to ease pain and prepared for sacraments. At moments she seemed to rally in a strange composure, speaking of life to come and Italy, yet the danger was constant.

Cast into stupefaction and self-reproach, I hid alone to suffer. I asked whether I ought not to have led the simple, faithful life she had proposed. Had she not asked me to become great to avoid shameful entanglements? Made more acute by the sight of her children’s aversion, my self-condemnation deepened. Madeleine avoided me, returning within the house with a look of hatred; Jacques sat expressionless, his handkerchief stained. The children seemed to perceive their mother’s murderer and recoiled.

At sunset, the countess fell into a long, opium-assisted sleep that seemed both annihilation and a triumph of the soul. Her suffering face cleared into a white, heavenly beauty that made those present kneel and weep. The abbés read the signs as angelic presence. The doctor said this sleep was the hour without pain and that they should administer the last sacraments. At nine o’clock she awoke with gentle eyes and the beauty we had known. Madeleine cried that life and health would return; Henriette, smiling, said she would yet live in her daughter. She tried to absolve, to reconcile, to be tender toward me. She asked me to stay for her last scene and to respect her weakness. In confession she uttered words that pierced me: though she had been a faithful wife, she feared she had sometimes failed and had given an outside friendship more affectionate care than to her husband. She wanted his blessing with a clean heart. She begged him to grant as a last wish a pledge she had written—making me her adopted son, a token of affection, and asking my friend to secure her husband’s happiness.

These final sacrifices unveiled depths of tenderness and forgiveness that dissolved my egotism. Henriette had, in death, shown a greater charity than I had imagined. Her last acts—burning letters, arranging her affairs, composing a testament of love—were meant to place me under the obligation of taking charge of the household and of Madeleine’s welfare. She surrendered to death with dignity, pressing my hand, asking me not to be absent from the least pain in which I was concerned, and reminding us of the valley where she would rest.

From that dreadful day my world dimmed. Those who die are sometimes buried within us: Henriette’s soul entered mine; when any good was done by me thereafter, it felt like her act. I ceased writing; my recollections were too bitter. Henriette’s letter and her last wishes indicated a way to redeem myself: to live at Clochegourde with Madeleine and consecrate myself to her. But first I had to face Madeleine’s feelings.

On the terrace, when the count fetched his daughter at my request, I appealed to Madeleine for a sisterly welcome, urging her not to expel me from Clochegourde in her mother’s name. She answered with cold dignity that she knew my life and thoughts and that she would rather fling herself into the Indre than ally herself to me. In her voice was a resolute hatred. She refused my plea that my life was bound to that place and that her mother’s memory should preserve me there. Her young, searching eye had discovered her mother’s secret. Her hatred, perhaps augmented by a sense of complicity, was implacable.

Thus the house I loved turned against me. Madeleine condemned me without weighing cause or victim; the edifice of my happiness lay in ruins. Henriette had been known fully only to me; her nobility had no historian to measure its depths. Mothers, husbands, and friends seldom fathom the inner life; many families shelter a Henriette unknown to the world. When Madeleine withdrew, I left, my heart broken, and departed toward Paris along the Indre.
