In September 1835 Mademoiselle du Rouvre, the only daughter of the Marquis du Rouvre and one of the wealthiest heiresses of the faubourg Saint-Germain, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a young Polish exile. The du Rouvre fortune, despite the Marquis’s dissipation, derived largely from Clémentine’s maternal kin: she was niece to the Marquis de Ronquerolles and Madame de Serizy, and on her father’s side to the Chevalier du Rouvre. Those three relations agreed to settle on her, at marriage, yearly incomes that made her unexpectedly rich.

Comte Adam, though an exile, belonged to an ancient Polish family allied to princely houses. Heraldry, however, counted for little in Paris under Louis-Philippe. When Adam first appeared in 1833 in fashionable circles he was judged frivolous and even suspected of being an adventurer. Polish exiles then suffered a fall from French esteem: political reactions and divisions among them—between republican followers of Lelewel and the noble party around Czartoryski—had made Poles a subject of satire. Still, Adam’s manners, breeding, recklessness, and daring won attention; Parisian dandies mistook his light-heartedness for superficiality.

In 1834 Adam bought a house in the rue de la Pépinière and adopted an extravagant life. He had fallen in love with Clémentine at the Opera, and within a year they were married. Society’s surprise at the match subsided when it emerged that Adam had a solid income: his mother, a Radziwill, had mortgaged property during the insurrection to secure a large loan from two Jewish bankers in Paris, and Comte Adam now enjoyed about eighty thousand francs a year. By the winter of 1836 he and his wife were at the height of fashion; Madame Laginska’s salon and her place among the young, elegant women of Paris made them prominent.

This setting frames what follows: a domestic scene revealing a noble action—one of those quiet sacrifices easy to lose amid society’s noise. Balzac seizes on architecture as a mirror of manners. After the revolution of 1830, old mansions gave way to hybrid residences—palatial effects contrived on cramped lots—where a peer might live with an air of grandeur produced by artifice. Adam’s hôtel in the rue de la Pépinière exemplified this: built within the garden of a former purveyor’s house, it was a small palace with kitchens and stables to either side, a porter’s lodge between coach gates, and an ambitious greenhouse off the boudoir. The house, decorated by accomplished artisans and costing nearly a million and a quarter francs, combined imitation and display; it masked economy beneath veneers, mouldings, painted ceilings, and a white staircase like that of the Rothschild hôtel.

Behind the house lay a compact but luxuriant garden with English-style lawn, exotic trees, a Chinese pagoda, climbing plants, gravel paths and palisades contained within twenty-five rods—an oasis where birds sang and a greenhouse filled the air with perfume. The boudoir opening on this garden held pretty exotica—Chinese carvings, inlaid ivories, mosaics, sketches, rare fabrics and objects collected or bought as bargains. The room was less for repose than tasteful display—half-light, embroideries, Persian rugs—reflecting a Parisian penchant for showing rather than introspection.

One morning in late April, two years into their marriage, Clémentine lay on a couch in the boudoir, thinking. The count smoked a narghile and the greenhouse doors stood open. Married two years and recently returned from travel in Italy, Switzerland, and Germany, Clémentine had become aware, at home, of a ubiquitous presence in her household: Paz. Her husband always invoked “Paz” to settle every practical matter—carriage, bills, the stable, the groomings—yet she had never seen him. She asked Adam for an explanation and summoned him.

Paz presented himself in the gallery: a tall, well-made man with unkempt black hair, a broad white forehead, a Roman nose, jet-black eyes, and a robust soldierly air. He wore a polonaise and had the unconscious distinction of a man of command. The contrast between Paz’s sturdy, solemn presence and Adam’s delicate, almost effete nobility intrigued the countess; she perceived in the captain a kind of hidden grandeur, the reserve and force of a past life.

Paz’s manner was modest and sincere. He addressed Adam familiarly but bowed courteously to Clémentine and declined to accept her thanks for his services. When she asked why he served in this subordinate role, he replied that it gave him pleasure; he did not care for Parisian opinion and lived for himself or, if she preferred, for the couple. He agreed to accompany them that day to the Bois, politely and simply.

Adam explained Paz’s history. Paz—Thaddeus by name—came from a noble Polish house, descended, Adam said, from a branch of the Pazzi of Florence that settled in Poland. He had been poor, orphaned, and only armed with his sword when he served in the regiment of the Grand Duke Constantine and joined the Polish cause. In a last desperate action before Warsaw’s fall, Paz charged a Russian battery thinking his men followed, and was taken prisoner. Adam, who had been with him in the action, recounted how he and his troop had saved Paz: they’d charged and come back with Paz among the few survivors. After Warsaw capitulated, Adam and Paz escaped through Prussia and Dantzic, then by a Dutch vessel to London, where Adam’s sick mother was. During these trials they became inseparable—so much so that Adam spoke of a bond of life and death.

When Adam later found himself with an income, he sought to secure Paz’s future. He offered to set aside a sum producing an annual income for Paz by producing securities payable to bearer; Paz, however, reacted with dismay, refusing to be bought off. Thaddeus returned the paper, asking whether Adam wished to be rid of him; he could not accept a relation based on such a transaction. The exchange revealed Paz’s generous spirit: he preferred devotion and fidelity to money, yet he nonetheless contributed unostentatiously to their prosperity. Adam described how Paz, shrewd in business, had found the rue de la Pépinière house at a bargain, sold and repurchased funds advantageously, and thus helped pay for the hôtel. Paz managed the stables, bought horses with uncanny skill, chose servants—former soldiers—loyal to a fault, and saved household expenses through his prudence. When Adam lost twenty thousand francs at whist one evening, Paz paid it without reproach; his silent sigh at the expense, Adam said, was more restraining than any moral rebuke.

Clémentine reacted with affectionate approval. She admired the simplicity and grand-heartedness of the man who, though apparently a subordinate, displayed a deeper nobility. Adam lauded the hidden domain of feeling in Paz, the man who in Paris was “Captain” but possessed the soul of a noble. He told his wife that he and Paz were as close as brothers—he had been saved by Paz and saved Paz in turn—and that their friendship, based on mutual needs and admiration, might be richer than love, growing rather than exhausted by giving.

Moved, Clémentine promised to love Paz as an old friend; the idea amused and pleased her. She rose to dress for a ride, delighted that the day was fine and eager to go out with her husband and the enigmatic captain who had been the household’s invisible master.

Paz led so hidden a life that Parisian society wondered who he was when the Comtesse Laginska was seen in the Bois de Boulogne riding between her husband and a stranger. During that ride Clémentine insisted Thaddeus should dine with them. This caprice forced Paz to make an evening toilet. Clémentine dressed with a coquettish charm that even Adam appreciated when she entered the salon where the two friends waited.

“Comte Paz,” she said, “you must go with us to the Opera.”

The tone meant: “Refuse and we shall quarrel.”

“Willingly, madame,” replied the captain, “but I have not the fortune of a count; call me captain.”

“Very good, captain; give me your arm,” she answered, taking it with the flattering familiarity that enchants lovers.

At table the captain sat beside her and behaved like a modest sub-lieutenant at his general’s mess. He let Clémentine talk, listened deferentially as if to a superior, disagreed with nothing, and waited to be questioned before he spoke. His frigid gravity and conventional respect baffled the countess’s coquetries. Adam urged him to be lively, but Thaddeus remained heavy and half asleep. At dessert he excused his dulness by saying his habits were opposite to Paris society—he went to bed at eight and rose early.

“My intention in taking you to the Opera was to amuse you, captain; but do as you prefer,” Clémentine said, piqued.

“I will go,” Paz replied.

“Duprez sings ‘Guillaume Tell,’” remarked Adam. “But perhaps you’d rather go to the ‘Variétés’?”

The captain smiled and rang for the carriage. “Tell Constantin to put the horses to the carriage instead of the coupé; we should be rather squeezed otherwise,” he said to the count.

“A Frenchman would have forgotten that,” Clémentine smiled.

“Ah! but we are Florentines transplanted to the North,” answered Thaddeus with a refined accent and a look that made his earlier behavior seem assumed. The contrast surprised Clémentine, who watched him with covert glances.

Silence reigned while they took coffee, a silence annoying to Adam. Clémentine stopped trying to draw him out. The captain relapsed into military stiffness and remained so both on the way to the Opera and in the box, where he seemed asleep.

“You see, madame, I am very stupid,” he murmured during the dance in the last act of “Guillaume Tell.” “Am I not right to stick to my specialty?”

“In truth, my dear captain, you are neither a talker nor a man of the world, but perhaps you are Polish.”

“Therefore leave me to look after your pleasures, your property, your household—it is all I’m good for.”

“Tartufe! pooh!” cried Adam, laughing. “He is full of ardor; he can, if he chooses, hold his own in any salon. Clémentine, don’t believe his modesty.”

“Adieu, comtesse; I have obeyed you so far; now I will take the carriage and go home to bed,” said Paz.

Clémentine let him go without replying.

“What a bear!” she said to Adam. “You are a great deal nicer.”

Adam, unseen, pressed her hand. “Poor, dear Thaddeus,” he whispered. “He’s trying to make himself disagreeable where most men would try to seem more amiable.”

“Oh!” she said, “I am not sure but what there is some calculation in his behavior; he would have taken in an ordinary woman.”

Half an hour later, as the carriage waited, Clémentine asked where the captain lodged. “His apartments are over the coach-house,” Adam said. She noticed lights in Thaddeus’s rooms as they passed the portico of the house—his dressing-gown, a pipe in his mouth, watched her enter the vestibule. The day had been hard for him. Seeing Mademoiselle du Rouvre at the Opera earlier had stirred him, and seeing Clémentine at her marriage had fixed his exclusive attachment. He had persuaded Adam and Clémentine to travel after their marriage to spare himself the pain; peace had lasted only while they were away.

As he sat at his window that memorable night, smoking his long wild-cherry wood pipe given him by Adam, thoughts occupied him:

“I, and God, who will reward me for suffering in silence, alone know how I love her! But how shall I manage to have neither her love nor her dislike?”

He derived secret joy from the deceptions of the day. Since the couple’s return he had found ineffable satisfactions in knowing himself necessary to a household that otherwise would have been ruined. Clémentine, raised by a spendthrift father, knew nothing of household management; Adam, son of a lavish Polish lord, was not the kind to check fancies. Paz had prevented him from gambling at the Bourse, and thus had kept them from ruin. Gratitude, the keynote of Paz’s life, held him bound; he could not leave where he alone could guard the affairs of the heedless owners. The sight of Clémentine driving in her pretty equipage stirred in him delights that showed not on his face.

How had Clémentine not perceived him for five months? Because he hid himself, carefully concealing the pains he took to avoid her. Nothing so resembles divine love as hopeless human love; a man must have depth to devote himself silently to a woman. Thaddeus had reached that height; filled with the joys of a creator he had attained a kind of grandeur. Yet he feared she might break his friendship with Adam if she took a dislike to him, or that if she coquetted with him merely for amusement he would be ruined by it. He scolded himself, went to bed, and resolved to await events.

The next day Clémentine breakfasted without noticing Paz’s absence. It was her reception day, when the house was semi-royally thrown open, a day that ordinarily fell on Paz to manage; she paid him little heed. He heard the last carriages drive away at two in the morning and told himself it was only a Parisian caprice that had made her seek him.

Clémentine’s Parisian life then absorbed her: winters were to fashionable women what campaigns were to soldiers—exhausting, extravagant displays spent on gowns and appearances that demanded constant attention. She found herself engaged in a struggle for social supremacy that left less time even to love her husband. Paz might well be forgotten. Yet one May day, as she drove home from the Bois before leaving Paris for Ronquerolles in Burgundy, she noticed Thaddeus sauntering in a side-path of the Champs-Élysées, delighted to see her in her elegant carriage.

“There’s the captain,” she said to her husband.

“He’s happy!” Adam answered. “He rejoices to think people envy our equipage. I see him nearly every day.”

“I wonder what he is thinking about now,” said Clémentine.

“He’s thinking this winter has cost a good deal, and it’s time we economized with your uncle Ronquerolles,” Adam replied.

Clémentine stopped the carriage near Paz and bade him take the seat beside her. Thaddeus flushed.

“I shall poison you,” he said; “I have been smoking.”

“Doesn’t Adam poison me?” she asked.

“Yes, but he is Adam,” returned the captain.

“And why can’t Thaddeus have the same privileges?” she smiled.

That smile broke through Paz’s heroic resolutions; he looked at Clémentine with the fire of his soul tempered by gratitude. Clémentine perceived a contrast in him: his fine presence and Adam’s puny frame and decay from intermarriage among the Polish aristocracy. Dangerous thoughts took hold of her; she sat silent and dreamy until they reached home.

“You will dine with us; I shall be angry if you disobey me,” she said as they turned in. “You are Thaddeus to me, as you are to Adam. I know your obligations, but I also know ours to you. Both generosities are natural—but you are generous every day and all day. My father dines here today, also my uncle Ronquerolles and my aunt Madame de Sérizy. Dress yourself,” she said, taking his hand as she stepped from the carriage.

Thaddeus dressed with a joyful dread, descended at the last moment, and again behaved at dinner like a soldier fit only for his duties as steward. But now Clémentine was not his dupe; her glance had enlightened her. The Marquis de Ronquerolles, an able diplomatist known to the party, had been told by his niece of Paz’s real worth and knew how modestly he made himself steward of Laginski’s affairs.

“And why is this the first time I have the pleasure of seeing Comte Paz?” asked the marquis.

“Because he is so shy and retiring,” replied Clémentine, a look telling Paz to change his manner.

Paz, though superior in some ways to Adam, was not a man of parts. His apparent superiority owed much to misfortune; in his poverty-stricken life in Warsaw he had read and taught himself much but lacked the gift of original thought that marks a great man. Great in heart, he reached the sublime in sentiment, yet being more a man of action than of thought, he kept his feelings hidden.

After Clémentine’s speech, the Marquis and his sister exchanged a glance and embraced the niece, the count, and Paz. This rapid eye-speech, so French and Italian in its effect, meant more to Paz than to the others. “Do they think I might be loved?” he thought. “Then my happiness depends on my audacity!” For a moment love overcame gratitude and friendship; Paz became brilliant, told of the Polish insurrection with noble words when questioned by the diplomatist, and by the end of dinner Clémentine hung on his lips, regarding him as a hero and forgetting that Adam too had sacrificed much as an exile.

After coffee the principal guests left—Madame de Sérizy with Adam, the Marquis de Ronquerolles and the Marquis du Rouvre following soon. Paz and Clémentine were alone.

“I will leave you now, madame,” said Thaddeus. “You will, of course, rejoin them at the Opera?”

“No,” she answered, “I don’t like dancing; they give an odious ballet tonight.”

A silence followed.

“Two years ago Adam would not have gone to the Opera without me,” said Clémentine, not looking at Paz.

“He loves you madly,” said Paz.

“Yes, and because he loves me madly he is all the more likely not to love me tomorrow,” she replied.

“How inexplicable Parisian women are!” exclaimed Paz. “When they are loved to madness they want to be loved reasonably; and when loved reasonably they reproach a man for not loving them at all.”

“And they are quite right, Thaddeus,” she smiled. “I know Adam so well; I am not angry with him; he is volatile and above all a grand seigneur. He will always be content to have me as his wife and will never oppose my tastes, but—”

“Where is the marriage in which there are no ‘buts’?” Paz interjected gently.

The least presumptuous man might have thought what nearly drove Paz beside himself: “If I do not tell her now that I love her I am a fool,” he kept saying. Neither spoke; the silence grew heavy with thought. The countess studied Paz; he observed her in a mirror. Buried in an armchair like a man digesting his dinner, Paz crossed his hands upon his stomach and twirled his thumbs.

“Why don’t you tell me something good of Adam?” cried Clémentine suddenly. “Tell me he is not volatile, you who know him so well.”

“Now is the time,” thought Paz, “to put an insurmountable barrier between us.” But he answered aloud, “I love him; you would not believe me; and I am incapable of telling you harm. My position is very difficult between you.”

Clémentine lowered her head and looked at his polished boots.

“You Northern men have nothing but physical courage,” she complained; “you have no constancy in your opinions.”

“How will you amuse yourself alone, madame?” Paz assumed a careless air.

“Are you not going to keep me company?”

“Excuse me for leaving you.”

“What do you mean? Where are you going?”

A heroic falsehood occurred to him.

“I—I am going to the Circus in the Champs-Élysées; it opens tonight, and I can’t miss it.”

“Why not?” she asked, half angry.

“Must I confide to you what I hide from Adam, who thinks my only love is Poland?” he colored.

“Ah! a secret in our noble captain?”

“A disgraceful one—you will perhaps understand and pity,” he stammered.

“You, disgraced?” she smiled.

“Yes, I, Comte Paz; I am madly in love with a girl who travels all over France with the Bouthor family; they play at fairs. I had the director at the Cirque-Olympique engage her.”

“Is she handsome?”

“To my thinking,” said Paz, melancholy. “Malaga—that is her stage name—is strong, active, supple. When I look at her, her black hair tied with a blue ribbon, on her bare olive shoulders, dressed in white edged with gold, a living Greek statue, carrying flags to martial music, jumping through paper hoops that tear as she goes, and lighting so gracefully on the galloping horse—well, all that moves me.”

“More than a handsome woman in a ballroom?” Clémentine asked.

“Yes,” Paz answered chokingly. “Agility and grace under danger seem to me the height of triumph for a woman. Malaga is dexterity personified; her little wrist or foot can rid her of three or four men. She is the goddess of gymnastics.”

“She must be stupid—”

“Oh, no,” said Paz. “She is thoughtless as a Bohemian, says everything that comes into her head, and sometimes says grand things. She likes strong, handsome men. Her trainer used to beat her to make her supple and intrepid—”

“You are intoxicated with Malaga,” Clémentine laughed.

“She is called Malaga on the posters,” Paz retorted, piqued. “She lives in the rue Saint-Lazare in a pretty third-floor apartment, all velvet and silk, like a princess. She has two lives: the circus and the life of a pretty woman.”

“Does she love you?”

“She loves me—now you will laugh—solely because I’m a Pole. She saw an engraving of Poles charging with Poniatowski and was touched. But I am very unhappy, madame.” A tear fell from his eye and touched the countess.

“You men have such a passion for singularity,” she said.

“And you?” he asked.

“I know Adam so well that I am certain he could forget me for some mountebank like your Malaga. Where did you first see her?”

“At Saint-Cloud last September, on the fête-day. She was at a corner booth where the shows are given, silent and dumb; I thought I saw a melancholy in her face; it touched me.”

Clémentine sat pensive and somewhat melancholy.

“Poor, poor Thaddeus!” she exclaimed, then with the kindness of a true great lady and not without a malicious smile, “Go, go to your Circus.”

Thaddeus took her hand, kissed it, left a hot tear upon it, and went out.

Having invented this passion, he set about making it real. The only truth in his tale was the momentary attention he had given Malaga at Saint-Cloud; he had since seen her name on posters and, after paying a clown five francs for information, learned the rider’s real name: Marguerite Turquet, lodging in the rue des Fossés-du-Temple.

The next day Paz found the house and asked to see Mademoiselle Turquet. The portress called up the attic; a line of drying clothes snagged his hat as he climbed. He told Malaga he had seen her at the Circus and that she reminded him of a daughter he had lost; out of affection for that daughter he wished to be of service to her if she would permit it.

“Then am I to pass for your daughter?” Malaga asked slyly.

“Yes,” said Paz; “I’ll come and see you sometimes. You shall be lodged in better rooms, comfortably furnished.”

“You shall have furniture!” cried Malaga, delighted.

“And servants,” said Paz, “and all you want.”

Malaga looked suspicious. “What countryman is monsieur?” she asked.

“I am a Pole.”

“Oh! then I accept,” she said.

Paz departed, promising to return.

A month after this eccentric interview the circus-rider lived in a comfortable apartment furnished by Comte Adam’s upholsterer; Paz had judged it desirable to have his folly talked about at the hôtel Laginski. Malaga, whose adventure seemed like a leaf from the Arabian Nights, was served by Monsieur and Madame Chapuzot as friends and servants. The Chapuzots and Marguerite expected some result; but after three months none could make sense of the Pole’s caprice. Paz arrived once a week, spent an hour, sat in the salon, and never entered Malaga’s boudoir or bedroom, despite the Chapuzots’ manoeuvres. Each time he left two gold pieces of forty francs each on the mantelpiece.

“He looks as if he didn’t care to be here,” Madame Chapuzot said.

“Yes,” said Malaga, “the man’s as cold as an icicle.”

“But he’s a good fellow,” cried Chapuzot, happy in a new blue cloth suit that made him look like a minister’s servant.

The money Paz deposited weekly, joined to Malaga’s meagre salary, gave her means of a sumptuous life compared with her past. Stories circulated at the Circus about Malaga’s good fortune; vanity turned the six thousand francs Paz had spent into rumors of sixty thousand. In the demi-monde she was much talked of; some calumnies accused her of being magnetized or bewitched. These scandals distressed her.

“When I want to injure a woman,” she said, “I don’t calumniate her with magnetism; I say she is humpbacked and prove it. Why do you compromise me?”

Paz maintained a cruel silence. The Chapuzot soon discovered his name and title; then Malaga heard facts at the hôtel Laginski: Paz was a bachelor and had never been known to have had a daughter. Fear crept into her mind.

“My dear child,” Madame Chapuzot warned, “that monster”—a man who looked in slyly, not daring to speak—“is trying to get a hold upon you, and make you do something illegal and criminal. Holy Father, if you should get into the police-courts! I’ll tell you what I’d do; I’d warn the police.”

After many foolish notions fermented in Malaga’s mind, one day Paz left his customary gold on the mantelpiece; she seized it and flung it in his face, crying, “I don’t want stolen money!”

The captain gave the gold to Chapuzot, went away without a word, and did not return.

Clémentine was at her uncle’s estate in Burgundy at this time. When the Circus troupe discovered Malaga had lost her Polish count much excitement followed. Malaga’s display of honor was thought folly by some and shrewdness by others. The Pole’s conduct, even when discussed by the cleverest women, seemed inexplicable. Thaddeus received thirty-seven letters from curious women of their kind, but his reserve limited the gossip to the demi-monde.

Two weeks later the circus-rider, crippled by debt, wrote this letter to Comte Paz, which, having fallen into Adam’s hands, was read and admired by dandies:

“You, whom I still dare to call my friend, will you not pity me after all that has passed—which you have so ill understood? My heart disavows whatever may have wounded your feelings. If I was fortunate enough to charm you and keep you beside me in the past, return to me; otherwise I shall fall into despair. Poverty has overtaken me, and you do not know what horrid things it brings with it. Yesterday I lived on a herring at two sous, and one sou of bread. Is that a breakfast for the woman you loved? The Chapuzots have left me. Your desertion has shown me the bottom of all human attachments. The dog we feed does not leave us, but the Chapuzots have gone. A sheriff has seized everything on behalf of the landlord, who has no heart, and the jeweller would not wait—when we lose the confidence of such as you, credit goes too. My friend, I have taken all I have of value to my uncle; I have nothing but the memory of you, and winter is coming. I shall be fireless when it turns cold; the boulevards play only melodramas, in which I have little parts. How could you misunderstand the nobleness of my feelings for you?—for there are two ways of expressing gratitude. You, who seemed so happy in seeing me well-off, how can you leave me in poverty? Oh, my sole friend on earth, before I go back to country fairs with Bouthor’s circus where I can at least earn a living, forgive me if I wish to know whether I have lost you forever. If I were to think of you when I jump through the hoops, I should be sure to break my legs. Whatever may be the result, I am yours for life.

“Marguerite Turquet.”

“That letter,” thought Thaddeus, laughing, “is worth the ten thousand francs I have spent upon her.”

Clementine returned the next day, and Paz saw her the day after, more beautiful and graceful than ever. After dinner, when Thaddeus had gone, a little scene occurred between the count and his wife. Thaddeus had, on the pretense of asking Adam’s advice, left Malaga’s tobacco-scented letter with him.

“Poor Thaddeus!” said Adam as Paz slipped away. “What a misfortune for a man of his distinction to be the plaything of that circus-rider. He’ll lose everything and sink lower and lower. Read that,” he added, handing the letter to his wife.

Clementine read it, flung it aside with disgust, and Adam continued, “He must have found out something; Malaga tricked him.”

“But he goes back to her,” said Clementine, “and will forgive her! It is for such horrible women you men are indulgent.”

“Well, they need it,” Adam answered.

“Thaddeus used to have decency—he used to live apart from us,” she added.

“Oh, my dear angel, that’s too much,” Adam said; he did not wish the man’s ruin.

Paz, who knew Adam well, had asked him to secrecy and to lend a few thousand francs for Malaga. “He is a very firm fellow,” Adam said.

“How so?” asked Clementine.

“For having spent no more than ten thousand on her, and letting her send that letter before he would ask me for enough to pay her debts. For a Pole, that’s firm.”

“He will ruin you,” Clementine said sharply.

“Oh, I know him,” Adam replied; “he will sacrifice Malaga if I ask him.”

“We shall see,” the countess remarked.

“If it is best for his happiness, I sha’n’t hesitate to ask him to leave her. Constantin says that since Paz has been with her he, sober as he is, has sometimes come home excited. If he takes to intoxication I shall be grieved as if he were my own son.”

“Don’t tell me more,” cried the countess, disgusted.

Two days later the captain noticed the change in the countess—contempt had opened a gulf between her and Adam. He was plunged into distress, thinking, “I have made her despise me!” His folly stung him. Life became a burden; the sun itself turned gray. Yet amid bitterness he had moments of pure joy. He devoted himself to admiration for his mistress, silent at her parties, attentive to every gesture and tone when she sang. He lived in her life, groomed her horse, managed her household, taking delight in its smallest interests. These secret pleasures were buried deep, like a mother’s love unknown to the child. His love was more perfect than Petrarch’s for Laura. Such emotions Paz enjoyed daily—without dying, and without immortality as reward.

Still, despite these delights, Paz was unhappy. The Catholic ideal had elevated Love with respect and nobility; it is rare where contempt exists. Thaddeus had suffered wounds of his own making. His former life—living beside his mistress, unknown but generous—had been better. He wanted the reward of his virtue—her respect—and he had lost it. He grew thin and feverish; in January he was bedridden though he refused a doctor. Adam was uneasy; the countess remarked cruelly, “Let him alone; it’s only some Olympian trouble.” Hearing this, Thaddeus found courage in despair: he left his bed, tried amusements, and recovered.

At the end of February Adam lost a large sum at the Jockey-Club and, afraid of his wife, begged Thaddeus to make it appear in the accounts as spent on Malaga.

“There’s nothing surprising in your spending that sum on the girl,” Adam said. “But if the countess finds out I lost it at cards I shall be lowered in her opinion.”

“Ha! this, too!” sighed Thaddeus.

“If you will do me this service we shall be quits,” Adam urged.

“Adam, you will have children; don’t gamble,” Paz replied.

Some time later the countess discovered Adam’s generosity and cried, “So Malaga has cost us another twenty thousand francs! First ten thousand, now twenty more—thirty thousand! The income of which is fifteen hundred! The cost of my box at the Opera, and the whole fortune of many a bourgeois. Oh, you Poles!” She asked why Clementine was not furious with him.

“Poor Paz is—” Adam began.

“Poor Paz, indeed!” she interrupted. “What good does he do us? I shall take over the household management myself. You can give him the allowance he refused and let him settle it as he likes with his Circus.”

“He is very useful,” Adam replied. “He has saved over forty thousand this last year and put a hundred thousand with Nucingen, which a steward would have pocketed.”

Clementine softened yet remained hard toward Thaddeus. A few days later she asked him to come to the boudoir where, a year earlier, she had compared him to her husband. This time she received him alone.

“My dear Paz,” she said with condescending familiarity, “if you love Adam as you say, you will do what he will not ask of you, but which I, his wife, demand.”

“About Malaga?” Thaddeus asked bitterly.

“Well, yes,” she said; “if you wish to remain here and be friends with us, you must give her up. How can a man of your distinction allow himself to be caught like a boy? Your proceedings have made that woman celebrated. My uncle wanted to see her, and he did. Malaga receives many gentlemen. I thought you had a noble soul. For shame! Will she be such a loss that you can’t replace her?”

“Madame, if I knew any sacrifice I could make to recover your esteem I would; but to give up Malaga is not one—”

“In your position, that is what I should say if I were a man,” Clementine replied. “Well, if I accept it as a great sacrifice there can be no ill-will between us.”

Paz left fearing he might commit a folly. He walked in the cold, burning with shame. “I thought you had a noble soul,” her words rang in his ears. “A year ago she thought me a hero who could fight the Russians single-handed!” He thought of leaving the Laginski household, joining the spahis, getting killed in Africa, but fear stopped him. “Without me, what would become of them? They would be ruined. Poor countess! What a horrible life if reduced to thirty thousand francs a year. No, since all is lost for me in this world—courage! I will keep on as I am.”

Since 1830 the Paris carnival had become a European, burlesque, lively affair—collective amusements, cheap salons without hostesses or manners. March was full of balls where dancing, coarse fun, grotesque figures, and Parisian wit produced extravagant effects. These carnival follies had their center in the rue Saint-Honoré and their Napoleon in Musard, a small man whose noisy orchestra set the galop that ended in the grand galop of “Gustave,” a finale that seemed to symbolize a dreamlike epoch in which things hurried by like a vision.

Now the grave Thaddeus, with one pure image in his heart, proposed to Malaga, queen of the carnival dances, to spend an evening at Musard; he knew the countess, disguised, intended to come there with two friends, accompanied by their husbands, to watch.

On Shrove Tuesday, 1838, at four in the morning, the countess, wrapped in a black domino and sitting low on the platform steps in the Babylonian hall, saw Thaddeus, as Robert Macaire, threading the galop with Malaga in a savage costume, plumes in her hair, bounding through the crowd like a will-o’-the-wisp.

“Ah!” said Clementine to her husband, “you Poles have no honor at all! I believed in Thaddeus. He gave me his word he would leave that woman; he did not know I should be here, unseen.”

A few days later she asked Paz to dine with them. After dinner Adam left them alone, and Clementine reproved Paz, making plain she did not wish him to live in her house any longer.

“Yes, madame,” Paz said humbly, “you are right; I am a wretch; I gave you my word. But I put off leaving Malaga till after the carnival. Besides, that woman exerts an influence over me—”

“An influence!—a woman who ought to be turned out of Musard’s!” Clementine cried.

“I agree; I accept condemnation and I’ll leave your house. But you know Adam. If I give up managing your property you must show energy. I may be to blame about Malaga, but I have taken the whole charge of your affairs, managed your servants, and looked after the least details. I cannot leave until I see you prepared to continue my management. You’ve been married three years; you’re safe from honeymoon extravagance. I see Parisian women manage fortunes and households; as soon as I’m certain of your perseverance I shall leave Paris.”

“It is Thaddeus of Warsaw, not that Circus Thaddeus who speaks,” Clementine said. “Go, and come back cured.”

“Cured! never,” Paz answered, eyes fixed on her pretty feet. “You do not know, countess, what charm she has.” Then, feeling his courage fail, he added, “There is not a woman in society, with her mincing airs, worth the honest nature of that young animal.”

“At any rate, I want nothing of the animal about me,” the countess snapped.

After that evening Thaddeus showed Clementine the exact state of her affairs; he made himself her tutor, taught her property management, proper prices, and how to avoid being cheated by servants. He told her she could rely on Constantin and make him major-domo; he had trained the man thoroughly. By the end of May he judged Clementine competent. She was one of those instinctive, far-sighted women who have an innate genius as mistress of a household.

This arrangement did not end without further trials; his sufferings were not as sweet as he tried to make them. He forgot to reckon on hazard. Adam fell seriously ill, and Thaddeus, instead of leaving, stayed to nurse his friend. His devotion was unwearied; a perceptive woman might have seen in this devotion a noble soul punishing itself to suppress involuntary evil thoughts—but women, lovers or not, often see all or nothing.

For forty-five days Paz watched and tended Adam without hinting at Malaga because he truly did not think of her. Clementine, feeling Adam near death though he did not die, summoned Paris’s leading doctors.

“If he comes safely out of this,” said the chief, “it will be by an effort of nature. For those who nurse him must watch the moment to second nature. The count’s life is in his nurses’ hands.”

Thaddeus told Clementine this. He found her in the Chinese pavilion, partly to rest, partly to leave the field to the doctors. As he climbed the winding path he felt an abyss. He had never dreamed of becoming Clementine’s husband; now he had drowned himself in a ditch. His face convulsed; despair was visible.

“Is he dead?” Clementine asked.

“They have given him up to nature,” Thaddeus said. “Do not go in; they are still there, and Bianchon is changing the dressings.”

“Poor Adam! I ask myself if I have not sometimes pained him,” she said.

“You have made him very happy,” said Thaddeus. “You ought to be easy on that score; you have shown every indulgence.”

“My loss would be irreparable.”

“But, dear, you judged him justly.”

“I was never blind to his faults,” she said, “but I loved him as a wife should.”

“Then you ought, if you lose him,” Thaddeus said in a voice she had not heard before, “to grieve less than if you lost a man who was your pride and love as some men are to you women. Surely you can be frank with a friend like me. I shall grieve too; long before your marriage I had made him my child; I had sacrificed my life to him. If he dies I shall be without an interest on earth; but life is still beautiful for a widow of twenty-four.”

“Ah! but you know that I love no one,” she said impatiently.

“You don’t yet know what it is to love,” he answered.

“Oh, as husbands are, I have sense enough to prefer a child like Adam to a superior man. It is now over a month we have said to each other, ‘Will he live?’ and these alternations have prepared me for this loss. Well, I would give my life to save Adam. What is a woman’s independence in Paris? The freedom to let herself be taken in by ruined or dissipated men who pretend to love her. I pray God to leave me this husband who is so kind and so obliging, and who is beginning to stand in awe of me.”

“You are honest, and I love you the better for it,” said Thaddeus, taking her yielded hand and kissing it. “In solemn moments there is satisfaction in finding a woman without hypocrisy. Let us look to the future. Suppose God does not grant your prayer—and no one cries to him more than I do. Yes, these fifty nights have not weakened me; if thirty more days and nights are needed I can give them. I will tear him from death if nursing can save him. But suppose, in spite of us, the count dies—well, then, if you were loved, oh adored, by a man of a heart worthy of you—”

“I may have wished for such love, foolishly, but I have never met it.”

“Perhaps you are mistaken—”

Clementine looked fixedly at Thaddeus, imagining less love than cupidity; her eyes measured him, poured contempt, then crushed him with “Poor Malaga!” in tones only a great lady can find. She rose, leaving him half unconscious, slowly re-entered the boudoir, and went back to Adam’s chamber.

An hour later Paz returned to the sick-room and resumed his care, death in his heart. From that moment he said nothing. He struggled with the patient and managed him in a way that excited the doctors’ admiration. At all hours his watchful eyes were like lamps always lit. He showed no resentment to Clementine and listened to her thanks without accepting them; he seemed dumb and deaf. To himself he said, “She shall owe his life to me,” and wrote the thought on the walls of Adam’s room. On the fifteenth day Clementine was forced to give up nursing lest she utterly break down. Paz was unwearied. At last, toward the end of August, Bianchon told Clementine Adam was out of danger.

“Ah, madame, you are under no obligation to me,” Bianchon said; “without his friend, Comte Paz, we could not have saved him.”

The day after the meeting in the kiosk, the Marquis de Ronquerolles came to see his nephew; he was about to start for Russia on a secret mission. Paz took the chance to say a few words to him. The first day Adam could drive out with his wife and Thaddeus, a gentleman entered the courtyard as the carriage was about to leave and asked for Comte Paz. Thaddeus, sitting front, took a letter stamped with the ministry of Foreign Affairs. Having read it, he pocketed it so Clementine and Adam could not speak of it. Nevertheless, by the time they reached the porte Maillot, Adam, curious, said, “There’s no indiscretion between brothers who love each other—tell me what there is in that despatch; I’m in a fever of curiosity.”

Clementine glanced at Thaddeus with vexation. “He has been so sulky with me for the last two months that I shall never ask him anything again,” she remarked.

“Oh, as for that,” Paz replied, “I can’t keep it out of the newspapers, so I may as well tell you at once. The Emperor Nicholas has appointed me captain in a regiment to take part in the expedition to Khiva.”

“You are not going?” cried Adam.

“Yes, I shall go, my dear fellow. Captain I came, and captain I return. We shall dine tomorrow for the last time. If I don’t start at once for St. Petersburg I shall have to go by land, and I am not rich, and I must leave Malaga some independence. I ought to think of the only woman who has been able to understand me; she thinks me grand, superior. I dare say she is faithless, but she would jump—”

“Through the hoop, for your sake and come down safely on the back of her horse,” said Clementine sharply.

“Oh, you don’t know Malaga,” said the captain bitterly; the look made Clementine uneasy.

“Good-by to the young trees of this beautiful Bois which we Parisians love,” he said. “On the borders of Asia, fighting for the Emperor, promoted perhaps by courage and risking my life, I may regret these Champs-Elysées where I have driven beside you. I shall grieve for Malaga’s hardness—the Malaga of whom I now speak.”

This made Clementine tremble. “Then you do love Malaga very much?” she asked.

“I have sacrificed for her the honor that no man should ever sacrifice.”

“What honor?”

“That which we desire to keep at any cost in the eyes of our idol.”

After that he was silent until, as they passed a wooden building on the Champs-Elysées, he pointed and said, “That is the Circus.”

He went to the Russian Embassy before dinner, then to the Foreign Office, and the next morning he had started for Havre before the count and countess were up.

“I have lost a friend,” said Adam with tears when he heard Paz had gone. “A friend in the true sense. I don’t know what made him abandon me as if a pestilence were in my house. We are not friends to quarrel about a woman,” he said, looking at Clementine. “You heard what he said about Malaga. Well, he has never so much as touched the little finger of that girl.”

“How do you know that?” said Clementine.

“I had the natural curiosity to see Mademoiselle Turquet, and the poor girl can’t explain the absolute reserve of Thad—”

“Enough!” the countess said, retreating into her bedroom. “Can it be that I am the victim of some noble mystification?” she asked herself. Constantin then brought her a letter Thaddeus had written during the night:

“Countess,—To seek death in the Caucasus and carry with me your contempt is more than I can bear. A man should die untainted. When I saw you first I loved you as one loves a woman forever, even if she be unfaithful. I loved you thus—I, the friend of the man you had chosen; I, poor, the steward—a voluntary service.

“In this misfortune I found a happy life. To be indispensable to your comfort and luxury was profound joy. If these enjoyments were great when I thought only of Adam, think what they were when the woman I loved was the mainspring. I have known the pleasures of maternity in my love. I built myself a hut on the borders of your domain, though I never sought to approach you. Poor and lonely, struck blind by Adam’s good fortune, I was nevertheless the giver. You were surrounded by a love as pure as a guardian-angel’s; it waked while you slept; it caressed you with a look as you passed; it was happy in its own existence—you were the sun to me, poor exile.

“When I was eighteen, having no one to love, I took for my ideal mistress a charming woman in Warsaw to whom I confided all my thoughts. She knew nothing; why should she? I loved my love.

“You can imagine, madame, how happy I was to live in the sphere of your existence: to groom your horse, to find new-coined gold for your purse, to prepare the splendor of your dinners, to see you eclipse those of greater fortune, all by my management. Ah! with what ardor I ransacked Paris when Adam said, ‘She wants this or that.’ It was a joy I can never express. When I saw you unseen, smiling among your flowers, I could forget that no one loved me. On some nights, when happiness turned my head, I kissed the spot where your feet had left their traces.

“I must tell you these things to explain the presumption of my involuntary thoughts. You will recall those days when you wished to know the man who performed household miracles. I thought—pardon me—I believed you might love me. Your goodwill and glances, interpreted by me a lover, seemed dangerous. I invented the story of Malaga, knowing it was the sort of liaison women cannot forgive. I did it in a moment when my love might be revealed. Despise me; crush me with contempt—yet I am sure that if, on that evening when your aunt took Adam away, I had said what I now write, I should—”

He breaks off: midnight—he could not go on; the memory was too living. He had been maddened. Was there hope in her eyes? then victory would have flamed in his. His crime was to think so; perhaps wrongly. Her contempt was his punishment. You have shown me there is no return from loathing. I love you madly. I should have gone had Adam died; I must go because he lives. A man does not tear his friend from death to betray him. Besides, my going is my punishment for the thought that I would let him die when the doctors said his life depended on nursing.

“Adieu, madame; in leaving Paris I lose all, but you lose nothing in my being gone.

“Your devoted

“Thaddeus Paz.”

“If my poor Adam says he has lost a friend, what have I lost?” thought Clementine, sinking into a chair.

The following letter Constantin was to give privately to the count:

“My dear Adam,—Malaga has told me all. In the name of all your future happiness, never let a word escape to Clementine about your visits to that girl; let her think Malaga has cost me a hundred thousand francs. I know Clementine’s character; she will never forgive you losses at cards or visits to Malaga.

“I am not going to Khiva, but to the Caucasus. I have the spleen; at the pace I intend I shall be either Prince Paz in three years, or dead. Good-by; though I have taken sixty thousand francs from Nucingen, our accounts are even.

“Thaddeus.”

“Idiot that I was,” Adam thought; “I came near to cutting my throat just now, talking about Malaga.”

It is three years since Paz went away. The papers have said nothing of any Prince Paz. The Comtesse Laginska is intensely interested in the Emperor Nicholas’s expeditions; she is Russian to the core and reads news from that distant land avidly. Once or twice every winter she asks the Russian ambassador, with indifference, “Do you know what has become of our poor Comte Paz?”

Alas! most Parisian women, though they think themselves clever, pass and repass beside a Paz and fail to recognize him. Many a Paz is unknown and misconceived; worse, some are misconceived even though loved. The simplest women exact a conventional polish from great men: noble love signifies nothing if rough and unpolished; it needs the jeweller’s cutting and setting to be valued.

In January, 1842, the Comtesse Laginska, with her gentle melancholy, inspired a violent passion in the Comte de La Palferine, one of the most daring lions of the day. La Palferine knew conquering a guarded woman would be difficult, but he thought he could inveigle her with the aid of a friend already jealous of her.

Quite incapable, despite her intelligence, of suspecting treachery, the Comtesse imprudently went with this friend to a masked ball at the Opera. About three in the morning, led by excitement, Clementine, whom La Palferine had seduced, consented to accept a supper and was about to enter the carriage of her faithless friend. At that moment her arm was seized by a powerful hand and she was taken, despite struggles, to her own carriage, the door of which stood open though she had not known it was there.

“He has never left Paris!” she exclaimed as she recognized Thaddeus, who disappeared when the carriage drove away.

Did any woman ever have so strange a romance? Clementine constantly hopes she may see Paz again.
