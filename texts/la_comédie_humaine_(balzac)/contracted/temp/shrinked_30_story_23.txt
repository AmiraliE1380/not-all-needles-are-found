Balzac, famed for detailed character studies and everyday life, seldom based characters on real people. In “The Great Man of the Provinces in Paris” some figures recall actual persons; in Beatrix he borrows recognizable traits: Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin) suggests George Sand, while Beatrix, Conti, and Claude Vignon evoke the Comtesse d’Agoult, Liszt, and critic Gustave Planche. The opening scene captures the customs of an old Breton family—a vanished social order—marking a shift to modernity and standing out within Balzac’s Comedy of Human Life.

I. A BRETON TOWN AND MANSION

Some Breton towns remained little touched by nineteenth‑century progress because poor links to Paris insulated them. They regard new civilization as spectacle and preserve older customs; a traveler seeking bygone types would find traces of different eras across the provinces. Many feudal towns declined and escaped historians, yet their memory persists.

Guerande exemplifies preserved feudal life: walled and moated, with intact battlements and gates. When the mayor planted poplars along the moat there was outcry—the promenade had long been public. Unmodernized houses and quiet streets recall past centuries. The public square still shows traditional Breton costumes, sharply contrasting bourgeois and noble styles; social divisions remain rigid and revolutionary fervor failed to erase them. Fervently Catholic and self‑contained, Guerande resists modern ideas.

Overlooking famed salt‑marshes, and linked to France by two slow roads, the town’s slow governance reinforces isolation. Near the church stands the du Guaisnic mansion: crested archways, stables, a carved hall with a massive fireplace, a dining room hung with fourteenth‑century tapestries, modest family rooms, and a walled garden—testimony to enduring heritage.

Early in May 1836 the du Guenic household comprised Baron and Baroness du Guenic, the baron’s elder sister Mademoiselle Zéphirine, and their only son Calyste, twenty-one. The baron, Gaudebert-Calyste-Charles, was a veteran of the Vendée and Brittany insurrections who had fought with Charette and La Rochejaquelein; prudently he had sold his property to his sister before the war. After years in the resistance he returned to Guérande in 1813, married Fanny O’Brien, an Irish noblewoman, and Calyste was born the day Louis XVIII landed.

Now seventy-three, the baron still bore the marks of his turbulent life: tall, wiry, deeply wrinkled, with scarred hands. Illiterate except for his prayer book, he dressed plainly, wore the cross of Saint-Louis, and combined serenity with somnolent gravity. Household talk centered on the exiled Bourbons, the Catholic faith, and Brittany’s future; Calyste was their sole hope. The baron trained his son strictly and once took him to join the Duchesse de Berry’s attempted restoration, returning after three months to remark, “The barons did not all do their duty,” a blend of pride and lingering obligation.

That evening the baron dozed in his armchair while his wife read the paper. Fanny O’Brien, forty-two, retained an Irish beauty and warmth—refined, elegant, ringleted—and a tender manner that softened her husband’s austerity. Nearby, blind Mademoiselle Zéphirine knitted; proud and cheerful, she refused cataract surgery to avoid burdening her brother and shared a close bond with the baroness. As the room warmed at sunset, Mariotte lit the lamp and Gasselin tended the horses and dogs—longtime retainers whose steady loyalty, quietly overseen by Zéphirine, embodied the household’s duty-bound cohesion.

When night fell, Gasselin asked if he was needed. The baron, roused, told him he could go out or to bed after prayers unless Madame or his sister required him. The two ladies consented, and Gasselin knelt with the family while Zéphirine led the prayer. A knock followed; Gasselin opened for Monsieur le curé.

Monsieur Grimont, a middle-aged priest in a cassock, arrived with the civility of his office. His plump, calm face and jovial air suggested a clear conscience; though well regarded in Guérande, he acknowledged the du Guenics’ feudal superiority. He inquired about Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel’s tardiness and mentioned the young chevalier’s presence at Les Touches that evening. Mariotte cautioned against speaking of the chevalier’s visits in front of Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, who loved town gossip.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel arrived next, thin and elderly, dressing in the fashion of her youth and carrying keys and a silver snuff-box. Of the highest Breton nobility, her family’s influence had waned; she ran her estate with strict frugality, her avarice attracting nieces eager for favor. Jacqueline de Pen-Hoel, a friend of Zéphirine, intended to marry one niece to Calyste to bind the families. As the rector fetched a basket and counters for the evening game, a firm knock announced the Chevalier du Halga: frail, dressed for his health, once a competent naval officer, now gentle and preoccupied with his little dog. The rector’s cough during the prayer for the king quietly hinted at the political tensions that still lingered in the town.

Mouche is a five-card trick-taking game: each player receives five cards and one card is turned up as trump. Players may play or drop out; dropping costs only the stake. Winning a trick shares the pot; failing makes one a mouche, who must pay the full stake and enlarge the pot. Cards may be exchanged from the remaining deck but only in order; the dealer keeps the turned-up trump and may swap it for any card in hand. The highest card is Mistigris, the knave of clubs.

Simple yet absorbing, mouche at the Hôtel du Guénic reveals greed, cunning, and expression. Players start with twenty counters each—five sous apiece—so the stake is five farthings. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël and Mademoiselle Zéphirine treat the game gravely, likening it to finance or speculation. A convention from September 1825 allows a player who loses ten sous to quit, but the chevalier and the baron undercut this by lending counters to keep play going. The arrival of Demoiselle de Kergarouët and her aunt raises stakes: the young women, besotted with Calyste, pay little heed to small sums.

Evenings are punctuated by local and public talk. Players sometimes forget stakes, provoking accusations of cheating—especially aimed at the rector. Nights can end abruptly, with departures empty-handed and grumbling. Attempts to introduce other games like whist and Boston fail; the circle prefers mouche, which outlasts newer diversions.

One night, while the rector deals, conversation moves from the chevalier’s health to the Duc de Lenoncourt’s death and mortality. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël announces the Comtesse de Kergarouët’s marriage and sparks gossip about Mademoiselle des Touches, an actress of questionable repute. The baroness and the rector exchange concerned looks—muted when Pen-Hoël holds Mistigris.

After the game the chevalier escorts Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël home. She confides that Calyste seems besotted with Mademoiselle des Touches; the baroness fears he will be led astray. The rector notes Pen-Hoël intends to leave her fortune to the niece who marries Calyste and warns that the actress is a dangerous, scandal-prone influence. Distressed, the baroness laments her eroding bond with Calyste; the rector promises to investigate. She accompanies him to the gate, anxiously awaits Calyste’s return, and instead hears the rector’s footsteps fade into the night.

Returning home, the baroness finds the town already aware that Calyste frequently visits Mademoiselle des Touches—visits she had assumed private. She had even tried to entice him by taking up needlework, but maternal jealousy failed; Calyste spent more time there and once came home as late as midnight. She wonders what secret charm holds men captive to Mademoiselle des Touches, making them forget family and duty, and longs to meet her and judge her character.

Calyste, of Breton and Irish lineage, was carefully raised; the baroness entrusted his education to the rector of Guérande, believing him sheltered from impure thoughts. Yet his schooling left him ignorant of contemporary literature and science, and he remains a fervent Catholic. The baroness had envisioned a modest, secure life for Calyste—a suitable marriage to a wealthy heiress aided by an expected inheritance. Those plans now seem threatened by Mademoiselle des Touches. When he returns at midnight she notes his fatigue and emotional turmoil, which she attributes to excitement rather than deeper trouble, though bitterness grows as she realizes his affections lie elsewhere.

The next day Calyste sleeps late; at dinner his attire suggests another visit to Les Touches. The baroness and her sister voice concern at his changed behavior, blaming the actress’s influence and morals. Calyste defends her: she rejected his romantic advances and offered friendship, and he insists she is a talented artist deserving respect. The baron dismisses his wife’s fears as youthful alarm. Meanwhile Guérande buzzes with rumors that Mademoiselle des Touches exerts a mysterious hold over Calyste; the rector and townsfolk disapprove.

Mademoiselle Félicité des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, is a celebrated writer whose life provokes curiosity and scandal. Orphaned during the Revolution and raised by an aunt, she cultivated a keen intellect through wide reading. Rejecting traditional marriage to preserve her independence, she made her salon a hub for artists and thinkers. Drawn to Calyste’s youthful innocence, their relationship awakens private longings that conflict with her public ambition and determination to remain autonomous; she must weigh love against the life she has built.

A few hundred yards from Guérande, Brittany’s soil gives way to salt marshes and dunes. The road drops into a pale landscape of ponds and a narrow arm of sea separating the mainland from Croisic, a peninsula reached by treacherous quicksand beaches.

At the junction to Guérande stands a country house among wind-sculpted pines. Built of slaty stone and granite, its plain exterior—regular windows and a high roof—faces the road on one side and the desert toward Croisic on the other. A brook runs from the park, crosses the road, and disappears into the sands.

Les Touches, harmonizing in gray with its surroundings, yields over ten thousand francs a year from marshes and farms. It lost feudal rights in the Revolution yet is still called “the château” by local salt workers. Félicité restored the house while preserving its desolate aspect, marking the entrance only with brick gate columns.

The ground floor follows the plan of many old country houses: Félicité turned the entrance hall into a billiard room that opens to a large salon and dining room; the kitchen connects to the dining room through an office. Décor is simple and graceful—gray-painted walls, mahogany furniture draped in green silk. The dining room holds mahogany buffets, horsehair chairs, and engravings by Audran.

Upstairs, Félicité occupies a suite with a salon, a large bedroom, and two cabinets; another suite serves guests. Guest rooms are modest, but her apartment reveals luxurious taste—Gobelin tapestries, heavy silk drapes, and fine Louis XV furniture. Her study, furnished in modern mahogany and filled with intimate objects, contrasts with the bedroom’s opulence and hints at her complex character.

Outside, salt marshes, dunes, and the distant ocean create a melancholic yet elevating atmosphere; Camille prefers Les Touches’ desolation to Guérande’s lushness. Calyste, seeking refuge from Guérande’s confinements, persistently clings to Félicité—rooted in admiration and a longing to love—despite her earlier rejection.

At Les Touches Calyste encounters rich art and nineteenth-century music and literature that awaken dormant sensibilities. Félicité nurtures him like a maternal figure, giving music lessons and filling the house with creativity. On one visit he hears her improvising at the piano; the music stops as he approaches and resumes when he finds her in tears, anxious over Claude Vignon’s absence. He offers to search for Claude, but she asks him to stay, calling her tears a natural overflow and preparing to tell of their fraught relationship—its age difference, the inevitability of separation, and why she once rejected Calyste. He sits at her feet, eager to support her yet unaware of the full depth she will reveal.

“I promised you this tale of the past, and here it is,” begins Camille. The letter’s author is the Marquise de Rochefide. The old marquis, after marrying his eldest daughter to a Portuguese grandee, urged his son to secure a peerage by wedding a noble wife. The Comtesse de Montcornet proposed Mademoiselle Béatrix-Maximilienne-Rose de Casteran, the marquis’s youngest, of the ancient, dowry-less Casteran line, preserved for the male heir. Béatrix, raised at the château de Casteran, was twenty when in 1828 she married. Camille warns that Béatrix’s originality, independence, ardor, and love of art ennoble her but also expose a woman to risks men face with less danger.

The match pleased the Rochefides: it tied them to an old family and briefly helped secure a peerage for their son under Charles X, later undone by the July Revolution. Early married life proved perilous. Rochefide mistook Béatrix’s reserve for frigidity and resumed bachelor habits; society praised him for having a wife who ornamented his salon without demanding attention. His vanity bred jealousy and a petty tyranny when Béatrix seemed indifferent to his infidelities. Camille uses this to explain Béatrix’s later behavior: admiration could curdle to jealousy, and her failed attempt to found a salon yielded shallow company because she lacked time and experience to form a true circle. Beneath those missteps lay a grand soul—regal pride and an intellect versed in metaphysics and music.

Béatrix was striking: slender, fair, with a long pointed face, noble forehead, pale sea-green eyes and severe lines that, when well dressed, could seem nearly divine. Camille teases young Calyste not to be taken in—by the time he notices her charms it will be too late.

She spent her early marriage quietly appraising men and life. Revolutionary disturbances bored her at her husband’s country estate. Returning to Paris after the July Revolution, she explored new doctrines but found the regime left no place for individual superiority; she chose personal happiness over abstract ideals. Then she met Gennaro Conti, a Neapolitan composer whose voice, manner, and appearance suggested genius. Conti proved duplicitous and self-serving beneath the mask of talent. Camille, devoted to art yet clear-eyed about Conti’s character, warned Béatrix. Blinded by passion, Béatrix was undeterred. Conti rejoiced; Camille even lent him money for their journey to Italy. Béatrix left a note for her husband, departed with Conti, spent two years abroad, and wrote enchanting letters to Camille, full of adoration for the only woman she felt understood her.

At this point Claude Vignon appears and the company falls quiet. Imposing despite a haggard look—high forehead, firm sarcastic mouth—Claude is complex: capable of deep original thought yet often paralyzed by it, inclined to criticism that destroys rather than builds; his intellect is sharp and indifferent to worldly life. His dry, brusque remarks unsettle the group. When Mademoiselle des Touches asks how he came, he replies simply, “By the door.” His sardonic asides about sentiment offend Calyste, who leaves angry and pitying Félicité.

Back home Calyste’s mother is delighted by his arrival. He produces Béatrix’s letter, which threatens domestic calm. In it Béatrix describes Italy’s weariness amid constant enjoyment, the strain of life with Conti, a sense of diminished social equality, and the sacrifices made for love. She contrasts her constrained marital position with Camille’s freedom, longs for solitude, admires Italy’s beauty, and laments financial worries likely to bring them back to Paris. Camille presents the correspondence as a blend of exaltation and disillusionment.

Folding the letter, Calyste is melancholy. His mother notices and questions him. He confesses that Félicité has opened his eyes to the insufficiency of his education and shown him the possibility of a nobler life. His aunt proposes a safe match with a good Breton girl; Calyste rejects such prudence, yearning instead for the freedom and passion he associates with love. His mother, understanding rather than scolding, embraces him and counsels: pursue happiness but temper your dreams with prudence and a moral aim.

After dinner Calyste returns to Les Touches, where Claude and Félicité remain. Claude and Félicité’s witty exchange—his cynicism against her generosity—captivates Calyste. Claude’s speculation about Béatrix and Conti’s return stirs Camille, and Calyste, imagining Béatrix as Félicité described her, feels deep sympathy. At home his mother reassures him, urging noble love. The scene closes on a tender family moment: encouragement blended with grounding wisdom, and a young man’s yearning for passion and purpose that will shape his future choices.

What young man of restrained feeling would not hurry to see Madame de Rochefide land at Croisic? Calyste slipped out early, ashamed to be seen, and hid on the shore. He watched two boats; one bore a woman and a chest stamped "MME. LA MARQUISE DE ROCHEFIDE," which fixed his attention. A sudden attraction rose to replace his earlier ardor for Camille.

He overheard servants mention someone from Les Touches and learned the marquise had already been collected. Agitated, he hurried to Guérande, turning over the new passion against the old. Returning late, he found a letter from Mademoiselle des Touches inviting him to celebrate the marquise’s arrival and reassuring his mother of the visitor’s noble birth.

Calyste arrived in turmoil. In Camille’s suite he encountered Gennaro Conti, a Byronic presence. The marquise reclined on a divan, surpassing his imagination: her hair braided with bluebells, exquisitely dressed. Hope and fear mingled in him. At dinner, charged to escort her, he fumbled; she noticed and he confessed he had never seen anyone so beautiful except his mother. Mademoiselle des Touches counseled restraint, warning the marquise could not return such fondness. The marquise seemed absorbed by Conti; when Conti and Camille performed, their music moved Calyste to tears. She turned away, and he slipped to a sofa, overwhelmed and conscious of his inadequacy.

Calyste returned to find Claude Vignon waiting. Claude, who had seen more than he admitted, told Calyste the situation was not as it seemed: he believed Camille loved Calyste. Stunned, Calyste asked about Claude’s own feelings. Claude confessed wounded pride—his melancholy walks had come from imagining Calyste preferred Camille. He planned to leave Les Touches, perhaps for Paris with Conti, warning that his departure would free passions to break. He cautioned Calyste that the choice before him was perilous: Beatrix was relentless, Camille noble and abstruse, and Calyste’s headlong feeling might prevent a prudent use of either. Disgusted with women yet clear-eyed, Claude advised caution: between those reefs Calyste might be wrecked.

Learning that Camille loved him while he loved Beatrix crushed Calyste. He replayed Mademoiselle des Touches’ earlier rejection, Claude’s return, and Beatrix’s salon laughter, silenced by the contradiction. At midnight Claude confronted Camille (Félicité), accusing her of arriving in Paris already in love with Calyste and of using Claude to hide her devotion. He called her scheme grand and ignoble, claiming she misjudged him as simple when he had divined her. Camille asked why love fled her; Claude answered that she was not lovable in the ordinary way—her mind too deep, her charm abstract and overpowering, repelling strong men. She might briefly please youth like Calyste, but such force fatigued others. He suggested she was an anomaly and resolved to leave to spare her the shame of his presence as a screen, insisting they could part without pain because they had had no mutual happiness to mourn.

Calyste then revealed himself. Claude told him plainly that Camille loved him, not in an ordinary way but for the exquisite pain of longing—an affection Claude cynically likened to masculine asceticism. Camille’s tears proved the truth. Claude accused her of driving Calyste toward Madame de Rochefide, saying Camille had dug her own grave and that if she had confided in him the suffering might have been avoided. Camille, moved, took Calyste’s head, kissed his hair, and begged him to forget what he had heard: “I count for nothing in all this,” she urged. Rising, she declared that conceiving love without hope brought one nearest God; she commanded him not to love her, yet vowed, “I will love you as no woman will!” Claude knelt and kissed her hand. She dismissed Calyste to spare his mother concern.

Calyste returned to Guerande distraught, glancing at Beatrix’s window on the way. That night he told his mother, tearful and abrupt, that Camille loved him but that he no longer loved her. The next day he posted a watcher on the road to Saint-Nazaire and rode out. A carriage arrived with two ladies and two gentlemen: Beatrix and Camille, with Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, the Vicomtesse de Kergarouet, and Charlotte. Camille invited the visitors into her carriage; the viscountess, pleased to meet Camille Maupin, accepted despite her elder sister’s misgivings. Charlotte, plain beside the Parisian women, greeted Calyste.

In the crowd Beatrix, elegant in straw and muslin, leaned on a parasol; Camille took Calyste’s arm. The viscountess expressed pity for Madame de Rochefide’s misfortunes; Camille explained her separation from Conti was by mutual consent, prompting sympathy. Calyste kissed Camille’s hand, leaving a tear; Beatrix, wiping anger from her eyes, looked away. Camille’s calm and grace soothed Calyste despite the earlier revelations.

At home the visitors dined amid lively games and mouche; Calyste’s sudden gaiety made everyone assume his troubles were over and that a match with Charlotte might prosper. Yet he remained inwardly troubled, comparing the Parisians to his childhood acquaintances. Only his mother Fanny noticed his unease and urged him to offer the viscountess a ride in Camille’s carriage; Calyste ran back to Les Touches to arrange it.

After dinner Camille teasingly accused him of abandoning the viscountess for them. When Beatrix suggested the viscountess’s niece might suit Calyste, Camille demurred: “The marriage will never take place.” Mortified by the marquise’s indifference to the Breton girl, Calyste stayed silent. Camille, seeing his discomfort and feeling herself stirred, yielded him her hand briefly and then sat at the piano to play melancholy variations.

Later, in private, Camille took Calyste into her chamber and gave candid counsel. She told him that if he wanted to win Beatrix he must appear to love Camille still; otherwise Beatrix would be lost. Loving and making oneself beloved were different arts, she said, and candor would not serve him. She proposed deliberate deception: promise blind obedience, spend every day with her until four, avoid private encounters with Beatrix, and let her use her art to direct the marquise’s feelings. Camille trusted her ability to fashion the marquise’s heart; in a week she boldly claimed Beatrix would love him. Desperate and naive, Calyste agreed and embraced Camille, who wept but did not stop him.

He left gaily, convinced of success, and returned to the card table where Charlotte believed victory assured. After he departed, Camille, alone and distraught, confessed to Beatrix the depth of her feeling: she was forty and in love; she had mourned youth and feared being loved only from pity. She admitted she might fling herself into the sea if Calyste asked it—an image of despair. Foreseeing how Beatrix’s coldness could secure Calyste for her, she pleaded with the marquise’s generosity and warned she too feared losing him. Yet she possessed a secret means to keep him and begged Beatrix’s help. Beatrix, piqued and curious, left with mixed feelings; Camille sank into tears and smoked opium-laced tobacco, brooding on the young lover’s head and her own pain.

Next morning Calyste returned before midday; Camille, pacing and watching the sea, told him to stay to dinner but to show Beatrix a look of regret when she passed—a signal to conceal his devotion. For six days Camille employed every conversational art and subtle deception—the feints and false magnanimities of a duel of hearts—aiming to conceal her love while revealing enough to inflame Beatrix’s pride and curiosity. Her remarks sometimes wounded Beatrix, who took offense at perceived distrust, yet Beatrix was secretly amused to find such a great writer caught in pettiness and resolved to show where Camille’s greatness ended.

Camille’s tactics included assigning Calyste to study, keeping him constantly in her company, and hinting at the marquise’s supposed indifference. She reported that Beatrix had written only once to Conti that week, implying a thaw. Calyste lived for the daily moments before Beatrix and read late into the night, bringing home armfuls of books. His mother, alarmed, implored him to leave Les Touches, but he refused. He nearly broke his promise and resolved to approach Beatrix privately in the garden one morning to read her eyes.

After a marsh outing, Calyste met Beatrix on a garden path. He trembled and asked if he had displeased her. She replied faintly that he had neither pleased nor displeased her. When he implored whether he was indifferent to her, she hinted indifference was proper given a “binding attachment” elsewhere. Calyste, stunned, declared that he had once loved Camille but loved Beatrix now. Beatrix, hurt yet composed, noted their constant mornings together and suggested Camille did not prefer a cigar to his company. Camille then appeared and rebuked Calyste: was this what he had promised? She took his arm and drew him away. The scene exposed Camille’s sacrifice: she forced Calyste into a reciprocity of appearances—an abnegation that could be read as noble or ruinous.

At breakfast Beatrix resolved to stifle any rising love and behaved with gentle indifference, following Camille’s counsel. Félicité proposed excursions, but Beatrix refused, saying her position was delicate and she would not jeopardize her happiness because Conti was coming to fetch her. Calyste took the news with pallor, left Les Touches depressed and near madness at the thought of losing Beatrix, and spent his time pondering a decisive explanation to win her.

Throughout these scenes strategy, pride, and genuine feeling intertwine. Claude, preparing to leave, remains the cynical observer who recognizes motives and predicts calamities. Camille, inwardly torn and capable of self-effacement, chooses to guide events through subtle deception, convinced she can shape Beatrix’s heart and preserve her claim to Calyste. Beatrix, proud and resolute, answers with polite aloofness that may hide deeper sentiment but insists on autonomy. Calyste, immature and ardent, is trapped between youthful passion and artful manipulation, a pawn in the hands of stronger wills whose consequences he cannot yet foresee.

When Calyste returned home he locked himself away and wrote feverishly; his mother found him amid crumpled pages composing a letter to Béatrix, spurred by renewed distrust of Camille after the marquise’s conduct.

His first letter poured out intense feeling: meeting Béatrix had awakened a love beyond any dream. He called himself young and unworthy, likened himself to an insect drawn to her light, contrasted this passion with his former attachment to Félicité, invoked Camille’s teaching that true love must grow and endure, and begged for kindness, recognition, or even the simple right to accompany her. The raw ardor moved his mother to tears and alarm.

Unsure how to deliver it, Calyste consulted the Chevalier du Halga—who suggested using Béatrix’s maid—but Calyste chose to give it to Béatrix himself. At Les Touches he found Béatrix and Camille together and impulsively handed her the letter; she accepted it without hesitation.

After a restless night he awaited her reply in the garden. Béatrix acknowledged his noble feelings but reminded him he was still a child and affirmed her commitment to Camille. She doubted she could be his partner, warned that accepting his love would insult Camille, and candidly admitted her jealous, capricious nature would likely make him unhappy, urging him to seek a young girl more fitting for his devotion.

The baroness, moved, questioned Calyste and read Béatrix’s reply aloud—admiring Béatrix’s nobility but anxious for her son’s future. She urged him to marry Charlotte de Kergarouet to secure the estate, but Calyste remained steadfast and dismissed her counsel, even fantasizing about elopement. Troubled, the baroness consulted the amused Chevalier and prayed for him. Calyste reassured her, and they planned a boat trip to Croisic to see Béatrix. The correspondence revealed surprising maturity yet also the intensity and probable consequences of his infatuation.

One of Beatrix’s chief pleasures was outwitting those she felt superior to, though she admired Camille Maupin’s talent and passion. While Calyste hurried to Les Touches intoxicated by first love, Beatrix enjoyed being adored without intending to return it. She prided herself on repressing feeling, imagining a noble sacrifice like Camille’s; French vanities flattered her and she felt morally upright in resisting Calyste.

The two women reclined in the salon, the calm garden outside at odds with their inner unrest. Camille, aware of the disturbance she’d caused, guarded her secret love and regarded Beatrix as a shrewd rival, seeking refuge in nature and reflection. Calyste seemed almost a guardian angel to her—yet Beatrix’s apparent triumph unnerved Camille.

When Calyste arrived with a letter, Camille watched and perceived an unspoken understanding: Beatrix now treated Calyste as hers while he looked guilty and transparent. He announced plans for an excursion; Camille, suspecting a conspiracy, grew anxious. After he left to arrange the boat and later returned, neither woman met his eye; he departed for home heavy with despair, a stark contrast to his earlier joy—his mother noticed but he dismissed it. Beatrix found the letter stirring unexpected feelings; doubts about a plot with Camille provoked jealousy and anger.

Dinner was tense. Veiled insults and hostile looks culminated in confrontation: Beatrix accused Camille of trickery and cruelty; Camille exposed Beatrix’s motives. Beatrix attacked Camille’s lack of conventional femininity; Camille declared her deep love for Calyste. Beatrix retorted she would not be his mistress—only marriage would do. The remark wounded Camille, who broke off the friendship and threatened to write to Conti. Beatrix refused to yield. The night left both women reflective, their conflict unresolved and balanced precariously between desire and social constraint.

Late August brought clear skies and a shimmering sea. Paludiers harvested salt while the baron and baroness admired the marshes from the jetty. A party from Les Touches arrived: the marquise, followed by Calyste and Camille. The baroness felt a strong aversion to Beatrix, who, though tastefully dressed, seemed heartless. The marquise remained cool; Camille chastised Calyste for disobedience, and he was torn between anger and love for Beatrix.

On the wind-sculpted Croisic peninsula, Calyste led Beatrix to a viewpoint while Camille sought the rocks to reflect. Beatrix confessed an inner desolation and insisted she could never be with Calyste despite loving him: she feared abandoning her life and the pain of unfulfilled love. Calyste, desperate, offered to sacrifice everything, but she rejected sacrifice and reaffirmed her commitment to her situation.

At a rock crowned by a lone box bush, passion overwhelmed Calyste; in a moment of intimacy Beatrix slipped and fell. He rushed to save her; amid the confusion their feelings intensified. Help arrived, and Beatrix was carried to a farmhouse for care. Calyste stayed by her; moved by his devotion, Beatrix softened and accepted his vow of exclusive love—yet on returning to Les Touches they encountered Conti, shattering the moment and revealing the tangled complexities of love and loyalty.

The marquise’s distress was clear when Conti arrived, seized Béatrix’s arm, and pulled her from Calyste, who felt humiliated and stunned. Convinced of his love, he was shaken by her visible anguish and fell silent.

Camille explained the tangled power dynamics: Béatrix could not refuse Conti because of lingering obligations, and Conti, opportunistic after a failed opera, used insinuations to regain control. He feigned indifference to Béatrix’s attachment while subtly undermining her confidence. At dinner his jovial manner masked intent; veiled remarks about women’s loyalty wounded both Béatrix and Camille. Béatrix later confessed to Camille that she felt helpless and even blamed Camille for Conti’s return, exposing deep rivalry. Béatrix insisted she would always occupy Calyste’s heart; Camille countered that such reigns were fleeting.

Conti and Calyste exchanged tense words. Conti posed as the injured party, professing disinterest and urging Calyste to pursue Béatrix so her pride would retaliate. Unaware of Conti’s design, Calyste confessed details of his relationship with Béatrix, inflaming Conti’s jealousy.

That evening Conti staged a salon performance to provoke Béatrix and assert dominance. Entranced by Conti’s singing, Calyste approached Béatrix with renewed confidence—walking into Conti’s trap. The next morning Béatrix left with Conti; Calyste was devastated. Camille informed him and tried to expose Béatrix’s true nature, but he clung to hope.

Weeks passed and his sorrow deepened. He wandered aimlessly; his family assumed melancholy was temporary and worried as his health and social life declined. Pressed to marry, he refused Charlotte, confessed his love for Béatrix, and begged that she be released. After the baron’s death Calyste sank further until Camille proposed helping him search for Béatrix in Paris; renewed by action, he agreed.

Félicité’s devotion had set the stage for Calyste’s future. She praised him to the Duchesse de Grandlieu, sold a house for 2,500,000 francs, bought another for 700,000, and planned to use one million to recover the du Guenic estates, placing the remainder on Sabine de Grandlieu. Aware the duke and duchess sought suitable matches for their daughters and that only the youngest, Sabine, was marriageable, Félicité aimed to redirect Calyste’s affections toward her.

On the trip to Paris, Mademoiselle des Touches confided with the baroness, who prepared a new house for Sabine and Calyste. Welcomed at the hôtel de Grandlieu, they received proper respect. Mademoiselle des Touches urged Calyste to enjoy Paris while she searched for Béatrix abroad. The city’s bustle eased his obsession; noticing Sabine’s likeness to Béatrix, he grew drawn to her. By winter 1837 he seemed refreshed and listened to his mother’s proposal for Sabine, though he concealed any reluctance.

On the day of the marriage contract, Calyste surprised everyone by accepting Mademoiselle des Touches’s benefits but then reading a letter she had sent through Sabine. In it Camille confessed love for him, declared her intention to enter a convent, urged him to accept her gifts for his future welfare, and asked him to remember her kindly while placing duty above passion.

After reading the letter Calyste rejoined the others and the contract was signed. The wedding at Saint-Thomas-d’Aquin followed the next week; Calyste and Sabine departed for Brittany amid tears and congratulations. The duchess and Clotilde worried for Sabine, knowing Calyste had not married wholly by his own will and that a woman’s happiness in marriage was precarious. The Baronne du Guenic, with only one son, accepted responsibility for Sabine’s care.

On the journey the newlyweds felt awkward, each conscious of their first moments together. Calyste suggested they be like friends or siblings, unsettling Sabine. He reluctantly recounted his former love for Béatrix; Sabine was hurt that their union had arisen from his rejection of another, though she found some comfort in his promise to forget Béatrix. Gradually their relationship warmed.

In letters to her mother Sabine described Brittany, her longing for home, and the difficulty of loving Calyste fully. As châtelaine she felt fulfilled—adored by family and tenants—and embraced Breton duties. A convent visit to Mademoiselle des Touches yielded blunt advice: be capricious when necessary and never let Calyste see Béatrix again. Sabine felt conflicted—unable to feign caprice or fully control her feelings—trying to balance devotion, insecurity, social expectation, and identity to make the marriage endure.

Guerande, July 1838.

To Madame la Duchesse de Grandlieu:

Ah, my dear mother! After three months I know what jealousy is. My heart is torn between love and hatred; I feel betrayed and unloved. How fortunate to have you to confide in!

A few words from others were enough to kindle my curiosity and poison my peace: “Here’s the key to your palace; go everywhere, but avoid Les Touches!” Mademoiselle des Touches—Madame de Rochefide—has sown bitterness in me. Why forbid Les Touches? What happiness hinges on avoiding one house in Brittany? My mother-in-law said, “Les Touches belongs to you.” Aunt Zéphirine cried, “If Calyste had never set foot in Les Touches!” Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël called it “a place of perdition.” The Chevalier du Halga said they had “saved her soul and enriched a convent.” My curiosity was tormented.

Calyste never proposed visiting the hermitage—now his property—but when I asked he said, “Let’s go,” and chance caught me. Les Touches is enchanting and deeply artistic—poisonous flowers can charm. I delighted in the place of horror, watching Calyste for signs that his old passion for Mademoiselle des Touches survived. I mentioned Madame de Rochefide; he begged me to stop reviving that passion. The plea inflamed me. He blushed when I spoke of forgiving past misrecognitions; I embraced him. Still, I left Les Touches and vowed never to return.

My hatred of Madame de Rochefide proves my love for Calyste. She haunts my dreams. The Visitation postulant was right—Les Touches is fatal. Calyste has revived past emotions there stronger than our joy. If Madame de Rochefide is in Paris, I shall stay in Brittany. Poor Mademoiselle des Touches might regret the role she played when she knew how our odious rival haunted me. I am ashamed—sometimes I wish to flee Guerande.

August 25: I am determined to live in the ruins of the old château. Calyste consents, either suspecting nothing or loving me not. I tremble at finding out. Oh, mother! I am not loved as I love. Calyste is charming—what man wouldn’t be amiable when adored by a pure, loving girl? Yet suspicion intrudes. By September I oscillate between despair and clinging joy, fretting whether his past has returned to claim him. Sister Clotilde’s melancholy wisdom haunts me: a woman faithful to the dead fears no rival. Poor me: I fear I shall never win Calyste back from himself.

A thousand tendernesses, dear mother. If my terrors are not delusions, Camille Maupin has sold me her fortune dearly. My affectionate respects to papa.

These letters reveal the private breach between husband and wife: Sabine imagined a marriage of mutual passion; Calyste accepted a conventional match. Their honeymoon joys misaligned with expectations.

In Brittany the hôtel du Guénic was restored and furnished under architect Grindot, supervised by Clotilde and the Duc and Duchesse de Grandlieu. The young couple planned to return to Paris in December 1838. Sabine took pleasure in settling rue de Bourbon—presiding over a great household and reading her family’s judgment of her marriage.

Calyste, indifferent, let Clotilde and the duchess guide his social life. He owed his standing to name, fortune, and alliance. Sabine, celebrated in Paris as one of its most charming women, found the season’s diversions and duties enlivening. Her pregnancy and the birth of a son in October 1839 confirmed a neutral, socially respectable union; Sabine mistakenly nursed the child herself, believing a mother should be wholly devoted.

By August 1840 Sabine was nearly finished nursing. In two years in Paris Calyste had shed the innocence of his early experience of passion. He associated with Duc Georges de Maufrigneuse, Vicomte Savinien de Portenduère, and others, keen to learn the differences between Parisian and provincial life. Wealth brings idle hours; Paris provides amusements. Contact with husbands who abandoned their wives for cigars, whist, and clubs eroded domestic virtues. A proud wife may tolerate such dissipation as proof of her success.

One October evening, to escape the cries of their newly weaned child, Calyste went to the Variétés, urged by Sabine. He took a stall near the avant-scène. During the interlude he saw Madame de Rochefide in a proscenium box—Béatrix in Paris! The sight struck him like an arrow. He had sometimes even substituted Béatrix for his wife in thought; Béatrix embodied light, life, the Unknown; Sabine embodied duty and the expected.

Calyste’s first impulse was to leave. Yet the proscenium door was ajar and he approached. Béatrix sat between Canalis and Raoul Nathan. In three years her appearance had changed—gaunt, faded, eyes hollow—but Parisian artifice made her more poetically attractive to him. She greeted him with affected grief and coquettish nostalgia—complaining at his marriage and teasing him about “such a little fool.” She revived old intimacy, pressing his hand and turning tears into performance. She spoke of living modestly in a rue de Chartres house “suitable to my means,” filling her head with literature.

Calyste left with Béatrix’s perfume in his nostrils; for the rest of the evening she cast sidelong looks that tore at his soul. He returned home haunted: his apartments’ splendor reminded him of the things she had mocked, and he resented his wealth because it could not belong to her. He delighted in possessing a night to relive emotions and feared Sabine’s easy read of him—she loved so purely she could detect every quiver.

The next morning Calyste learned Sabine had watched the child through the night and was joyous to hear the child better. Yet he avoided breakfast, preferring a walk to his bachelor café. A man raised with sacred morals may still be drawn to a fallen, dangerous woman while his virtuous wife waits at home; vanity, the thrill of triumph, and the desire to revive a conquering youth can all play a part. Calyste was drawn to Béatrix for motives neither philosophy nor morals fully explain.

Consumed by the thought of seeing her again, he went to rue de Chartres. The house was familiar though he had never been there; the old footman asked his name. Calyste waited, gazing into a garden marked by rain-streaked stucco.

Béatrix, who had left fortune to her husband when she fled and now relied on small sums from her mother and friends, lived modestly yet tastefully in a house formerly owned by a lord, with servants and frugal comfort. Calyste entered rooms whose elegant relics—garnet velvet, celadon vases, a silver cup ascribed to Benvenuto Cellini—seemed like tokens of a past life. A little table held jeweled trinkets and a work-in-progress: a dagger handle used as a paper-cutter; watercolors showed bedrooms of her wandering life.

She appeared in studied attire, a gown revealing her bosom, hair in a lace cap, smiling as though expecting a lover. The perfume she wore recalled Les Touches; the scent, her dress, the glint in her eyes made Calyste dizzy. She bade him sit; she said she treated him as a friend and would be no one’s honest mistress, yet she spoke of the impossibility of belonging to any man because of what she had once expected of him. Three hours passed with her urging him to resolve his conjugal faith; she affected to see Sabine as a stiff, dry, brown woman—good, dutiful, untempting—hardly the ideal for a man like Calyste.

Pressed, Calyste rose to leave; she teased him to dine with her. He had the impulse to write Sabine a note, and when he began “My dear Sabine—” Béatrix snapped, “‘My dear’? Can you say your wife is still dear?” She accused him of cowardice and deceit and forced him to write that he dined out; she would send the note by her messenger. He returned home at two in the morning. Sabine, who had waited until half-past twelve for him, had gone to bed exhausted. She slept, disturbed by the terse note, yet made the charitable assumption that love explains everything.

The next morning the child was better. Sabine, smiling, came with the baby before breakfast; Calyste overplayed affection. In the salon she caught his face in a mirror and saw weariness. She asked, “What did you do yesterday?” He lied that Savinien had kept him to dinner and they went to the club to play whist. Sabine gently reproached the coldness of his first note; he blamed dandified club directors. Small suspicions accumulated: she remembered a paper she had thrown into her letter-box—perhaps it bore a coronet.

Calyste, planning to conceal his doings, rushed to Savinien and then back to rue de Chartres to see how Béatrix had passed the night. He found her after her bath, fresh and lively, breakfasting on eggs with wit and candor. He stayed until late; his horse returned sweating so that Sabine, watching from a window, noticed.

Meeting him on the staircase she said, “Abd-el-Kader is nearly foundered. You said you’d be gone a moment. I waited three hours.” Calyste, trying to recover, presented as a surprise a dressing-table being finished for her, with contributions from his mother and aunt. Sabine clasped him, faint with joy. Then, like a viper’s bite, she recoiled—she had smelled a perfume on his cravat: the scent of another woman’s note-paper. She had kissed where the rival’s kisses had been. Panic seized her: “Fetch the doctor and my nurse!” Calyste fled to call them.

Sabine, seized by a chill, lay shivering, crying, “I am dying!” Her maid fetched Madame de Portenduère. In feverish haste she wrote to her mother—a letter of thanks for the dressing-table, an attempt at ordinary gratitude—but her mind raced: “My God! my God! my God!” She feared betrayal after three years and twenty-two years of age; her teeth chattered, her face turned greenish. “I don’t know yet [the rival], but Calyste has told me two lies. Do not pity me. Oh! that letter of yesterday!” She seized the thrown-away paper from her letter-box: a coronet—could it belong to a marquise? She begged Ursula to find out if Madame de Rochefide had returned to Paris.

Ursula, stunned, went to the duchess, pledging secrecy. The duchess, who had seen such scenes, advised deceit for Sabine’s sake: “Friendship gives you an experience of a woman my age. I know how Sabine loves her husband; she might become insane.” “Or lose her beauty,” Ursula added. “Let us go to her.”

Fortuitously, they arrived before the accoucheur Dommanget and Calyste. The duchess told Sabine that Béatrix was not in Paris; as for Calyste’s night, she declared he had lost a great deal of money at cards and didn’t even know how to pay for the dressing-table. She scoffed at the coronet—“Everybody writes on coroneted paper now”—and burned the fatal sheet.

When Calyste and Dommanget arrived, the duchess informed them: “Sabine’s life is at stake; you have betrayed her for Madame de Rochefide.” Calyste blushed like a schoolboy. The duchess, with the physician, invented a lie to steer the diagnosis: Calyste had lost thirty thousand francs at cards. Dommanget, quick and self-interested, used the lie to explain Sabine’s fainting as hysterical symptoms due to overexertion from nursing and anxiety. The physician prescribed measures; Calyste sat at the foot of the bed, trying to look tender. Sabine, hearing the explanation, retorted that her husband could lose any sum without being lectured. Calyste embraced her and called her an angel.

Two days later, thought safe, Sabine improved. Calyste visited Madame de Rochefide, making a merit of his infamy.

“Béatrix,” he said, “you owe me happiness. I have sacrificed my poor little wife to you; she discovered everything. The fatal paper bore your name and coronet; I didn’t notice—the ‘B’ was erased. Your perfume betrayed me. Sabine nearly died; her milk went to her head; erysipelas set in; she may bear marks for life.”

Béatrix’s face froze. “So much the better,” she said; “perhaps it will whiten her for you.” Her icy cruelty continued in a series of sarcasms. There is no greater blunder than to speak of one’s virtuous wife to one’s mistress—unless it is to tell one’s wife of the mistress. Calyste had neither the Parisian training to lie to his wife nor the tact to handle his mistress. He begged Béatrix for pardon; she forced him to beg for two hours. She answered from an elevated bitterness: “To speak to me of your wife the day after my fall! Why did you not tell me she is a pearl of virtue?” She declared her love for his soul and then scorned Calyste’s looks.

This speech was part of Béatrix’s system: with each passion she became another being, advancing into profligacy. She had judged herself by the mirror, counting wrinkles and crow’s-feet; she knew her resources and used them. Reduced by Conti’s desertion and grateful for scraps from friends, she aimed at a terrible notoriety—vengeance through fame. To prevail over a splendid young woman, she used the arts of courtesans: contempt mixed with rare affection, comparisons meant to mortify, and a cultivated disdain to stimulate conquest. She resolved to make Calyste think himself ugly and unwelcome—thus rekindling his desire to conquer. She contrasted her poetic, comfortable home with the hôtel du Guénic’s Faubourg Saint-Germain luxury, painting the latter stupid and empty.

Thus the scene of reconciliation—where she made him swear hatred for his wife and denounce her as playing comedy—took shape in a bower of flowers and rare plants, amid trifles and sumptuous yet carefully economical luxury. Fallen into a mortifying position by Conti’s desertion, Béatrix was determined to gain, at least, the triumphs of notoriety. The misfortune of a rich and beautiful wife like Sabine was to be her pedestal.

When Sabine returns to ordinary life after nursing her first child she seems refreshed but finds her husband Calyste changed. A letter from her mother hints at his discontent and names another woman, Beatrix, confirming Sabine’s fears. The vague note offers no explanation; in despair she scrawls a remark on it and leaves it where Calyste will see it. He finds the comment, ignores it, and burns the paper, humiliating her and deepening her distress.

Beatrix’s influence soon becomes tangible: perfume on a later letter betrays her presence. Recognizing it, Sabine feels the rival’s hold. In a febrile moment she dances with her child, clinging to motherhood’s innocence, then weeps as Calyste departs to read Beatrix’s message. Her attempts to win him back—decorating the house with flowers, improving her cooking and appearance—meet only indifference, intensifying jealousy and self-doubt.

Despite her suffering, Sabine’s love persists; she remains a devoted wife, but these virtues prove ineffective. After another failed effort to move Calyste she collapses, tears off a beautiful dress, and her health falters. The duchess, her mother, notices and tries to distract her. Sabine, fearing interference, at first stays silent but eventually confides in her sister Athenais, describes her anguish, and asks that her child be cared for if she dies.

Warned by experience, Sabine counsels Athenais to be cautious in love, lamenting that her straightforwardness and passion now obstruct happiness and may force her toward duplicity. Alarmed, the duchess consults Abbe Brossette and reveals a plan to break Calyste’s attachment and restore Sabine. The abbe cautions against mixing spiritual authority with worldly intrigue, but the duchess, determined to spare her daughter further suffering, resolves to act even if it means morally questionable measures.

Meanwhile a storm brews for Monsieur de Rochefide, who continued to enjoy Parisian life after Beatrix left, confident such existence was nearly impossible to disrupt. The narrative contrasts how society treats men and women in similar situations, implying women must guard their dignity at home as Sabine did.

After Beatrix’s departure, Arthur de Rochefide, now an only child after his sister’s death, became sole owner of the hôtel de Rochefide and inherited a large fortune. His income reached about a thousand francs a day, ample for his tastes. While Beatrix had fulfilled maternal duties, Rochefide enjoyed his wealth without great extravagance. His vanity, aided by good looks and a jaunty military air, let him dismiss women and borrow others’ wit; acquaintances found him amusing if shallow. As a husband he was pitied; society blamed Beatrix for leaving such a “good” man. Seeking distinction, he joined clubs and championed fashionable causes; his chief indulgence was racehorses, kept by an English groom at considerable cost.

Rochefide’s indifference to domestic life was clear: he rarely dined or slept at home. After several affairs he married Madame Schontz, known as La Petite-Aurélie, an orphan who had navigated precarious social worlds. Captivated, he married her two years after Beatrix left. Their relationship passed through courtship, domestic improvement, motherhood and financial management, and social consolidation—Aurélie modestly preserving their reputation and quietly amassing a small fortune through savings, investments, and the sale of jewelry, turning the match into mutual benefit.

Madame Schontz had a plan born of two years’ jealousy of Madame du Bruel: she resolved to win a respectable, church-and-civil marriage. Her secret rival was unclear—Bixiou guessed Léon de Lora, others named Victor de Vernisset or Stidmann—but none realized Madame Schontz’s sole aim was an honest match.

The dubious regular in her salon was Couture, a fallen stock-gambler loyal to her. When he introduced Fabien du Ronceret, a flashy provincial, Madame Schontz took notice. Fabien, son of a chief justice, had abandoned a judgeship in Alençon for Paris with a modest income and a knack for theatrical self-promotion: a showy apartment and a greenhouse arranged as spectacle to create talk. Seeking entry into Madame Schontz’s circle, he first tried known acquaintances, then relied on Couture’s recommendation. She flattered him and let him imagine he was the object of her interest. Bewitched, he appeared in his finest clothes. A month before a key meeting she disclosed her identity and aims; Fabien misread her intentions. When she corrected him during a carriage ride—declaring she wanted a husband with ambition who would value her character—Fabien hesitated. Couture, learning of her fortune, promptly proposed.

Meanwhile the Duchesse de Grandlieu engaged Comte Maxime de Trailles to resolve a delicate family matter. Maxime agreed but imposed conditions—chiefly that the duchess accept his future wife into her circle—and planned to eliminate a rival, demanding her obedience. He noted Calyste’s infatuation with Béatrix and the moral murkiness of manipulating it. Rochefide informed Madame Schontz of Maxime’s impending visit; she prepared, aware his reputation could be both asset and threat.

Maxime summoned Finot to organize a breakfast at the Café Anglais with Couture and Lousteau to discuss Madame Schontz. Posing as having business with du Tillet, he waited for the Comte de la Palferine, questioned him about debts and, admiring his spirit, offered to bankroll a courtship with a loan of twenty thousand francs. Maxime targeted a well-bred marquise, urged La Palferine to woo her sincerely, and arranged a meeting at Madame Schontz. He visited the duchess—who fretted over Sabine’s distress at Calyste’s absence—promising funds and secrecy.

At Madame Schontz’s dinner, which included Claude Vignon and La Palferine, she insisted she was an artist seeking respectable marriage, not a courtesan. Maxime lauded her success with Rochefide, privately gave La Palferine the promised funds and sharp advice, and schemed with Madame Schontz to manipulate Rochefide’s affections, vowing a post for Couture in return.

Calyste, feeling neglected, confronted Béatrix, who was torn between genuine feeling for him and attraction to La Palferine. Maxime arranged encounters that deepened Béatrix’s entanglement; Calyste’s attempts to reclaim her failed, culminating in her public humiliation at the opera. As she drifts into La Palferine’s orbit, loyalties shift. The episode examines love, betrayal, and ambition—how manipulation and desire breed disillusionment and force characters to re-evaluate choices and futures.
