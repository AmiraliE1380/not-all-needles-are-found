BEATRIX

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

NOTE

Balzac, famous for detailed character studies and everyday life, seldom portrayed real people. In “The Great Man of the Provinces in Paris” some figures recalled actual persons; in Beatrix he borrows recognizable traits: Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin) suggests George Sand, while Beatrix, Conti, and Claude Vignon evoke the Comtesse d’Agoult, Liszt, and critic Gustave Planche.

The opening scene captures the customs of an old Breton family — a social state largely vanished — signaling a shift from past to modernity and stands as a notable depiction in Balzac’s “Comedy of Human Life.”

I. A BRETON TOWN AND MANSION

In Brittany some towns remain little changed by nineteenth-century progress because poor links to Paris leave them insulated. They view new civilization as spectacle and preserve old customs. A traveler seeking bygone human types would find them across the provinces: traces of Louis XV in Provence, Louis XIV in Pitou, and older traditions in Brittany. Many feudal towns declined, overlooked by historians, yet their memory and character persist.

Guerande is such a town: a preserved feudal life within walls and moats, with intact battlements and gates. The mayor’s planting of poplars along the moat caused local outcry—the promenade had long been public. Houses remain unmodernized; streets resemble those of four centuries past and are now quiet, letting a solitary traveler wander a deserted town.

The atmosphere evokes ancient customs; even a gendarme seems out of place. The public square displays traditional Breton costumes, contrasting sharply with bourgeois and noble styles. Social divisions are rigid; revolutionary spirit failed to erase them. Guerande, fervently Catholic and self-contained, resists modern ideas.

Guerande overlooks famed salt-marshes, whose salt locals claim improves their butter and sardines. It connects to France by two roads: one from Savenay and a more frequented route from Vannes. Slow government and residents’ acceptance of separation reinforce the town’s isolation.

Near the church stands the du Guaisnic family mansion, symbolizing past grandeur. Tracing roots to the du Guesclins, the family is ancient and noble; though fortunes declined, they remain significant. The mansion’s grand archway bears a crest; the courtyard reveals stables and a kitchen; inside, a grand hall with intricate woodwork and a massive fireplace survives. The dining room, hung with fourteenth-century tapestries, and the simple family rooms above preserve historical charm. A half-acre garden with fruit trees, a sundial, Venetian touches and a spiral column show the family’s heritage.

The mansion’s enduring grandeur contrasts with modern, often temporary homes; the du Guaisnics, hosting friends for generations, embody old Brittany’s spirit and the persistence of tradition.

Early in May 1836 the Guenic household comprised Baron and Baroness du Guenic, the baron’s elder sister Mademoiselle Zéphirine, and their only son Calyste, aged twenty-one. The baron, Gaudebert-Calyste-Charles, a veteran of the Vendée and Brittany insurrections who fought with Charette and La Rochejaquelein, had prudently sold his property to his sister before the war. After years in resistance he returned to Guérande in 1813, married Fanny O’Brien, an Irish noblewoman, and Calyste was born the day Louis XVIII landed.

Now seventy-three, the baron bore the marks of his turbulent life: tall, wiry, deeply wrinkled, with sunken features and large scarred hands. Illiterate except for his prayer book, he still dressed plainly, wearing sturdy shoes, ribbed stockings, and a coat bearing the cross of Saint-Louis. His manner combined serenity and somnolent gravity, and conversations in the household centered on the exiled Bourbons, the Catholic faith, and the future of Brittany. Calyste was their sole hope.

The baron rigorously trained Calyste in gentlemanly ways and, when the Duchesse de Berry tried to restore her son, took him to join the cause—leaving his wife and sister uninformed. They returned after three months, and the baron observed, “The barons did not all do their duty,” a remark reflecting both pride and a lingering sense of duty.

That evening the baron slept in his armchair while his wife read the newspaper. Fanny O’Brien, forty-two, retained delicate Irish beauty and warmth: refined features, elegant dress, ringleted hair, and a tender, radiant manner that softened her husband’s austerity. Nearby, blind Mademoiselle Zéphirine knitted; proud and cheerful despite her age, she refused cataract surgery to avoid burdening her brother and shared a close bond with the baroness.

As the room warmed at sunset, Mariotte lit the lamp and Gasselin tended horses and dogs—longtime retainers whose steady loyalty and service, managed quietly by Zéphirine, exemplified the household’s affectionate, duty-bound cohesion and nurturing atmosphere amid trying times.

When night fell, Gasselin entered the hall and respectfully asked his master if he needed anything further. The baron, roused, said Gasselin could go out or to bed after prayers unless Madame or his sister required him. The two ladies consented, and Gasselin knelt with the family while Mademoiselle du Guenic led the prayer. A knock followed; Gasselin opened for Monsieur le curé.

Monsieur Grimont, a middle-aged priest in a cassock, entered with the easy civility of his office. His plump, calm visage and jovial air suggested a clear conscience; though well regarded in Guérande, he acknowledged the du Guenics’ feudal superiority. He inquired about Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel’s tardiness and mentioned the young chevalier’s presence at Les Touches that evening. Mariotte cautioned against speaking of the chevalier’s visits in front of Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, who loved town gossip.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel arrived next, led by a page with a lantern. Thin and elderly, she still dressed in the fashion of her youth and always carried keys and a silver snuff-box. Of the highest Breton nobility, her family’s influence had waned after a sister’s marriage to a Kergarouet annexed the name; she ran her property with strict frugality. Her admired avarice drew nieces eager to win her favor.

Jacqueline de Pen-Hoel, a friend of Zéphirine du Guenic, intended to marry one niece to Calyste, the young chevalier, to bind the family to the du Guenics. Despite the du Guenics’ wealth, they kept a respectful distance, valuing pride and status. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel treated Calyste with a proprietary air, believing her plans granted oversight; she held conservative views and distrusted new influences.

She asked whether Monsieur du Halga would join them; the rector confirmed seeing him earlier. Mention of du Halga stirred expectation as the group prepared for an evening game; the rector fetched a basket and counters with childlike eagerness. A firm knock announced Chevalier du Halga’s arrival: frail, dressed for his health, once a competent naval officer, now gentle and preoccupied with his little dog. Hardships during the emigration had undermined his health, yet he remained respected in Guérande, having served as mayor. The rector’s cough during the prayer for the king quietly hinted at the political tensions still present in the town.

Mouche is a card game where each player gets five cards and one is turned up as trump. Players may play or abstain; abstaining costs only the stake. Winning a trick shares the pot; losing makes one a mouche, obliging payment of the full stake and enlarging the pot for the next deal. Players may exchange cards from the remaining deck, but only in order. The trump card belongs to the dealer, who may swap it for any card in hand. The strongest card is Mistigris, the knave of clubs.

Despite simple rules, mouche proves engrossing, revealing greed, cunning, and expression. At the Hôtel du Guénic players begin with twenty counters each, representing five sous, making the total stake five farthings—a notable sum for the group. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël and Mademoiselle Zéphirine treat the game gravely, likening it to a financial venture or stock speculation.

A convention from September 1825 allows a player who loses ten sous to quit the game; the chevalier and the baron, however, subvert this by lending counters to losers to keep play going. The arrival of Demoiselle de Kergarouët and her aunt raises stakes further: the young women, enamored of Calyste, care little for such small sums.

Evenings at mouche are punctuated by talk of local events and public affairs. Players sometimes forget stakes, provoking accusations of cheating—especially directed at the rector. Nights can end abruptly, with departures empty-handed and grumbling. Attempts to introduce other games like whist and Boston have failed; the circle prefers mouche, which outlasts newer diversions.

One night, as the rector deals, the baroness inquires after the chevalier’s health; he jokes about migrating ailments. Conversation turns to the Duc de Lenoncourt’s recent death and mortality. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël announces the Comtesse de Kergarouët’s marriage, prompting gossip about Mademoiselle des Touches, an actress of questionable repute. The baroness and the rector exchange concerned looks—muted when Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël holds Mistigris.

After the game the chevalier escorts Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël home. She confides that Calyste seems infatuated with Mademoiselle des Touches. The baroness fears Calyste will be led astray. The rector notes Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël intends to leave her fortune to the niece who marries Calyste, but warns that the actress is a dangerous, scandal-prone influence. Distressed, the baroness laments her eroding bond with Calyste; the rector vows to investigate. She accompanies him to the gate, anxiously awaiting Calyste’s return, and instead hears the rector’s footsteps fade into the night.

The distressed baroness returned home to find the whole town aware of her son Calyste's frequent visits to Mademoiselle des Touches—visits she had assumed private. She had even tried to lure him earlier by taking up her needlework, but her maternal jealousy was in vain; Calyste spent more time there and came home later each night, once as late as midnight.

As she stitched, she wondered what held men so captive to Mademoiselle des Touches, what secret charm could make them forget family and duty. She longed to meet the woman and judge her character, fearing modern influences that might corrupt her innocent son. Calyste, of noble Breton and Irish lineage, had been carefully raised; she had entrusted his education to the rector of Guerande, believing him shielded from impure thoughts. Yet his schooling left him ignorant of contemporary literature and science, and he remained a fervent Catholic.

The baroness had envisioned a modest, secure life for Calyste, planning a suitable marriage with a wealthy heiress and aided by an expected inheritance. Those plans seemed threatened by Mademoiselle des Touches. When he finally returned at midnight she greeted him with relief, noting his fatigue and signs of emotional turmoil, which she attributed to excitement rather than deeper trouble. Still, bitterness grew as she realized his affections lay elsewhere and feared the consequences.

The next day Calyste slept late, indulged by his mother. At dinner she noticed his attire and suspected he planned to dine again at Les Touches. Her sister voiced concern over his changed behavior, blaming Mademoiselle des Touches’s influence. The baroness feared the woman's morals and the impact on Calyste, who had begun reading varied books under her guidance.

Calyste defended Mademoiselle des Touches: she had rejected his love and offered friendship. He insisted she was a talented artist deserving respect despite an unconventional lifestyle. The baron dismissed his wife's worries as youthful alarm.

Meanwhile Guerande buzzed with rumors that Mademoiselle des Touches was infatuated with Calyste and exerted a mysterious hold over him. The rector and other townsfolk disapproved of her lifestyle, seeing her as a threat to social norms.

Mademoiselle Félicité des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, was a celebrated writer whose life and works provoked curiosity and scandal. Orphaned during the Revolution and raised by an aunt, she developed a keen intellect through extensive reading. Despite beauty and talent, she struggled with the limits society imposed on women. Rejecting traditional marriage to preserve independence, she made her salon a hub for artists and thinkers. Drawn to Calyste’s youthful innocence, their relationship awakened private longings that conflicted with her public ambition, fear of aging, and determination to maintain autonomy; she had to weigh love against the carefully constructed life she had built.

A few hundred yards from Guérande, Brittany’s soil gives way to salt marshes and dunes. We descend into a bleached landscape of ponds and a narrow arm of the sea separating the mainland from Croisic, a peninsula reached by treacherous quicksand beaches.

At the road junction from Croisic to Guérande stands a country house set in a garden of wind-sculpted pines. Built of slaty stone and granite, its plain exterior—regular windows, a high pointed roof with gables and dormers—faces the road on one side and the desert toward Croisic on the other. A brook runs from the park, crosses the road, and disappears into the sands.

Les Touches harmonizes in gray with its surroundings. The estate, which yields income from the marshes and nearby farms—over ten thousand francs a year—was stripped of feudal rights by the Revolution yet still called “the château” by local salt workers. Félicité, its owner, restored the house while preserving its desolate aspect, adding only brick gate columns to mark the entrance.

The ground floor follows the plan of many old country houses, probably raised on castle ruins. Félicité turned the entrance hall into a billiard room that opens to a large salon and dining room; the kitchen communicates with the dining room via an office. Décor is simple and graceful: gray-painted walls, mahogany furniture covered in green silk. The dining room holds mahogany buffets and horsehair chairs, adorned with engravings by Audran.

Upstairs, Félicité occupies a suite with a salon, a large bedroom, and two cabinets; another suite serves guests. Guest rooms are modest, but Félicité’s apartment reveals luxurious taste—Gobelin tapestries, heavy silk drapes, fine Louis XV furniture. Her study, in modern mahogany and filled with intimate objects, contrasts with the bedroom’s opulence and hints at her complex character.

Outside, the salt marshes, dunes, and distant ocean create a melancholic yet elevating atmosphere. Camille prefers Les Touches’ desolation to Guérande’s lushness. Calyste, drawn to the estate as a refuge from Guérande’s confines, persists in his attachment to Félicité—rooted in admiration and a desire to love—despite her earlier rejection.

At Les Touches Calyste encounters rich art and culture—nineteenth-century music and literature that awaken dormant sensibilities. Félicité, who nurtures his intellect and becomes a maternal figure, now gives him music lessons; the house feels alive with creativity, a vivid contrast to rural dullness.

One visit, Calyste hears Félicité playing the piano; the improvisation is haunting. She stops when he reaches the billiard room, then resumes. He finds her in tears, anxious over Claude Vignon’s absence. He offers to search for him, but she asks him to stay, calling her tears a natural overflow and preparing to tell him the story of her fraught relationship with Claude—the age difference, the inevitability of separation, and why she once rejected Calyste. He sits at her feet, eager to support her yet unaware of the full depth she will reveal.

“I promised you this tale of the past, and here it is,” Camille begins. The letter’s author is the Marquise de Rochefide. The old marquis, having married his eldest daughter to a Portuguese grandee, pressed his son to take a noble wife to secure a peerage he himself never obtained. The Comtesse de Montcornet recommended a young woman from Orne: Mademoiselle Béatrix-Maximilienne-Rose de Casteran, the marquis’s youngest daughter. The Casterans, proud of their ancient lineage, were willing to marry off daughters without dowries to preserve the estate for the male heir. Béatrix, raised at the château de Casteran, was twenty when she married in 1828. Camille warns that Béatrix’s originality, independence, ardor, and passion for art—qualities that ennoble her—also expose a woman to abyssal risks men can more safely face.

The match pleased the Rochefides: it linked them to old families and helped secure, briefly, a peerage for their son under Charles X, which the July Revolution would undo. Early married life, however, was perilous. Rochefide misinterpreted Béatrix’s reserve for frigidity and reverted to bachelor habits; society approved, calling him fortunate to have a wife content to ornament his salon. But his vanity bred jealousy and a petty tyranny when Béatrix seemed indifferent to his infidelities. Camille offers this analysis to explain Béatrix’s subsequent behavior: admiration for Camille could curdle into jealousy, and Béatrix’s attempts to found her own salon produced only superficial company because she lacked the time and experience to build true society. Yet beneath the missteps lay a grand soul—regal pride and an intellect capable of discussing everything from metaphysics to music.

Béatrix was striking: slender, fair, with a long pointed face, a noble forehead, pale sea-green eyes, severe lines, and an appearance that could verge on harshness; dressed well she could seem nearly divine. Camille teases that fair women have a variety of charms and warns young Calyste not to fall for Béatrix—by the time he notices it will be too late.

Béatrix spent her early marriage evaluating men and life. Revolutionary disturbances bored her at her husband’s country estate. Returning to Paris after the July Revolution, she explored the era’s new doctrines intellectually but soon found the regime left no place for individual superiority; she chose personal happiness over abstract ideals. Then she met Gennaro Conti, a Neapolitan composer of brilliant surface gifts who would never reach the highest rank. Conti’s voice, manner, and apparent inspiration captivated Béatrix. He was duplicitous and self-serving beneath the mask of genius. Camille, devoted to Conti’s talent yet clear-eyed about his nature, warned Béatrix of the gulf she was entering; Béatrix, blinded by passion, was undeterred. Conti rejoiced, and Camille even lent him money for their journey to Italy. Béatrix left a note for her husband, departed with Conti, stayed abroad two years, and wrote enchanting letters to Camille, full of adoration and gratitude to the only woman she felt understood her.

At this point Claude Vignon appears, and the room grows quiet. He is imposing despite a haggard look—high forehead, firm sarcastic mouth—and complex: capable of deep thought yet often paralyzed by it, inclined to criticism that destroys rather than builds, his intellect sharp and indifferent to worldly life. His dry, brusque remarks unsettle the company. When Mademoiselle des Touches asks how he came, he replies dryly, “By the door.” His sardonic asides about sentiment offend Calyste, who leaves angry and pitying Félicité.

Back home, Calyste’s mother is delighted by his arrival. He produces Béatrix’s letter, which threatens domestic calm. In it, Béatrix describes Italy’s weariness amid constant enjoyment, the strains of life with Conti, a sense of diminished social equality, and the sacrifices made for love. She contrasts her constrained marital position with Camille’s freedom, longs for solitude, admires Italy’s beauty, and laments financial worries that will likely bring them back to Paris. Camille presents Béatrix’s correspondence as a mixture of exaltation and disillusionment.

Folding the letter, Calyste is melancholy. His mother notices and questions him. He confesses that Félicité has opened his eyes to the insufficiency of his education and shown him the possibility of a nobler life. His aunt proposes a safe match with a good Breton girl; Calyste rejects such prudence, yearning instead for the freedom and passion he associates with love. His mother, understanding, embraces him, urging the pursuit of happiness while offering maternal counsel to temper his dreams.

After dinner Calyste returns to Les Touches, where Claude and Félicité remain. Claude, fond of fine dining, enjoys Félicité’s cooking. Calyste presents Béatrix’s letter; Claude muses on the fortune of Parisian women to have men of genius to adore, while Félicité defends Béatrix’s endurance and nobility of spirit. The evening’s witty exchange between Claude and Félicité captivates Calyste. Claude’s speculation about Béatrix and Conti’s return stirs Camille, and Calyste, imagining Béatrix as Félicité described her, feels deep sympathy. At home he explains his late hour; his mother reassures him, emphasizing love and happiness and urging him, if he must love, to seek what is noble. The scene closes on a tender family moment: encouragement blended with grounding wisdom and a young man’s yearning for passion and purpose.

What young man, full of restrained emotion, would not be eager to see Madame de Rochefide land at Croisic? Calyste surprised his parents by leaving early, moved by an unfamiliar vigor as he hurried along Les Touches' walls to avoid being seen, excited and ashamed that Felicite or Vignon might notice.

At the shore he hid in a travelers' shelter and watched two boats arrive from Croisic, loaded with luggage suggesting distinguished passengers. One boat carried a young woman in a straw bonnet, a maid, and a footman. Calyste was captivated by a chest stamped "MME. LA MARQUISE DE ROCHEFIDE," and felt an immediate, inexplicable attraction, ready to transfer the affection he once held for Camille to this new figure.

While the luggage was unloaded he overheard the servants say they were waiting for someone from Les Touches and learned the marquise had already been collected. Agitated and longing, he hurried toward Guerande, skirting the servants.

Consumed by thoughts of the marquise, he contrasted this fresh ardor with his earlier feeling for Camille. Returning home late, he found a letter from Mademoiselle des Touches inviting him to celebrate the marquise's arrival; it mentioned the Casteran family and reassured his mother of the woman’s noble lineage.

At the gathering Calyste arrived in a tumult of feeling. In Camille's suite he was introduced to Gennaro Conti, a man with a Byron-like presence. The marquise, reclining on a divan, surpassed his imagination: more beautiful than he had pictured, her hair braided with bluebells and elegantly dressed. His affection mingled with hope and fear.

At dinner he was assigned to escort her; his heart raced and he stumbled for words, daunted by her beauty. She noticed and asked if he were unwell; he confessed he had never seen anyone so beautiful except his mother. Mademoiselle des Touches counseled restraint, warning the marquise could not return such fondness.

Conversation turned to Camille's travels, and Calyste felt marginalized as the marquise seemed absorbed by Conti. When Conti and Camille performed, their music stirred him to tears; the marquise turned away. Overwhelmed by admiration and despair, he slipped away to a nearby sofa, lost in thought and painfully aware of his own inadequacy.

X. DRAMA

Calyste returned to the house to find Claude Vignon waiting in his bedroom. Claude, who had perceived more than he admitted, told Calyste the situation was not what it seemed: he believed Camille loved Calyste. Calyste, bewildered, asked about Claude himself. Claude confessed a weariness with life and bitterness at recent events; his wanderings on the marshes were not for pleasure but because he felt wounded at the thought Calyste favored Camille. He planned to leave Les Touches the next day—perhaps for Paris with Conti—and predicted that once he was gone passions here would become tumultuous. He warned Calyste that the game before him was perilous: Beatrix was relentless, Camille noble, and Calyste’s excess of feeling for one might prevent prudent use of the other. Disgusted with women yet clear-eyed, Claude advised caution: between those reefs he might be wrecked.

Stunned to learn that Camille loved him while he loved Beatrix, Calyste felt crushed. He replayed Mademoiselle des Touches’ earlier rejection, Claude’s return, and Beatrix’s laughter in the salon. If Camille loved him, how could he continue to adore Beatrix? Silence settled over him.

At midnight Claude confronted Camille (Felicite). He accused her of having come to Paris last year already in love with Calyste and of using Calyste as a screen—bringing Claude to Les Touches to hide her secret adoration. He called her scheme grand and ignoble, said she had misjudged him as simple when he had divined her, and declared she had never really been loved and likely never would be after refusing chance. Camille asked softly why love fled her; Claude answered that she was not lovable in the ordinary way—her mind too deep, her charm abstract and overpowering, repelling strong men. She might please youth like Calyste briefly, but such strength fatigued others. He suggested she was an anomaly and said he would leave to spare her the shame of his continuing presence as a screen. He insisted they could part without the pain of betrayed hope because they had no mutual happiness to mourn. Love for Camille, he argued, was an ideal she mistook for normal human need; she had misled him. He would return to Paris to his misery.

Calyste then revealed himself. Claude told him plainly that Camille loved him, not in the ordinary way but for the exquisite pain of longing—an affection Claude sarcastically deemed in some sense masculine. Camille’s tears and agitation proved the truth. Claude accused her of driving Calyste toward Madame de Rochefide, saying Camille had dug her own grave and that if she had confided in him the suffering might have been avoided. Camille, moved, took Calyste’s head, kissed his hair, and begged him to forget what he had heard; “I count for nothing in all this,” she urged. Rising abruptly, she declared that conceiving love without hope brought one nearest God; she commanded him not to love her, yet vowed, “I will love you as no woman will!” Claude knelt and kissed her hand. She dismissed Calyste to spare his mother concern.

Calyste returned to Guerande distraught, glancing at Beatrix’s window on the way. That night he told his mother, tearful and abrupt, that Camille loved him but that he no longer loved her. The next day he placed a watcher on the road toward Saint-Nazaire and rode out with two horses. A carriage arrived with two ladies and two gentlemen; among them were Beatrix and Camille, along with Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, the Vicomtesse de Kergarouet, and Charlotte. Camille invited the new ladies to ride in her carriage; the viscountess, delighted at meeting Camille Maupin, accepted despite her elder sister's misgivings. Charlotte recognized Calyste and greeted him, conscious of her plainness beside the Parisian women.

In the crowd Beatrix, elegant in straw and muslin, leaned on a parasol; Camille took Calyste’s arm. The viscountess expressed pity for Madame de Rochefide’s misfortunes; Camille explained her separation from Conti was by mutual consent, prompting sympathy. Calyste kissed Camille’s hand, leaving a tear; Beatrix, wiping anger from her eyes, looked away. Camille’s calm and grace affected Calyste strongly; she had soothed his agitation despite the earlier revelations.

Back home the visitors dined amid lively games. The household played mouche; Calyste’s momentary gaiety made everyone assume the worst was over and that prospects with Charlotte might succeed. Yet Calyste remained inwardly troubled, comparing the Parisians to his childhood acquaintances. Only his mother, Fanny, perceived his unease and urged him to offer the viscountess a ride in Camille’s carriage; Calyste ran back to Les Touches to arrange it.

XI. FEMALE DIPLOMACY

Calyste arrived as Camille and Beatrix left the salon after dinner. Camille teasingly accused him of abandoning the viscountess and her daughter for them. When Beatrix suggested the viscountess’s niece might suit Calyste, Camille immediately demurred: “The marriage will never take place.” Calyste, mortified by the marquise’s indifference toward the Breton girl, stayed silent. Camille, seeing his discomfort and her own flushed face, yielded him her hand briefly and then sat at the piano to play melancholy variations.

Beatrix watched him closely. Later, in private, Camille took Calyste into her chamber and delivered hard but candid counsel. She told him that if he wanted to win Beatrix he must seem to love Camille still; otherwise Beatrix would be lost. Loving and making oneself beloved were different skills, and candor would not serve him. She proposed a deliberate deception: he should promise blind obedience, spend every day with her until four, avoid private encounters with Beatrix, and allow her to use her art to direct Beatrix’s feelings. Camille trusted her ability to fashion the marquise’s heart; in a week Beatrix would love him, she boldly claimed. Calyste, naive and desperate, agreed and embraced Camille, who wept but did not stop him.

He left gaily, convinced of success, and returned to the card table where Charlotte believed victory assured. After Calyste departed Les Touches that evening, Camille, alone and distraught, confessed to Beatrix the depth of her feeling: she was forty and in love; she had mourned youth and feared being loved only out of pity. She admitted she might fling herself into the sea if Calyste asked it—an image of her despair. Camille, foreseeing how Beatrix’s coldness could secure Calyste for her, pleaded with the marquise’s generosity and warned that she, too, feared losing him. Yet she had a secret means to keep him true and begged Beatrix’s help. Beatrix, piqued and curious, left with mixed feelings; Camille sank into tears and smoked opium-laced tobacco, brooding on the young lover’s head and the pain of her situation.

Next morning Calyste returned before midday; Camille, pacing and watching the sea, told him to stay to dinner but to show Beatrix a look of regret when she passed—a signal to conceal his devotion. For six days Camille employed every conversational art and subtle deception—the feints and false magnanimities of a duel of hearts—aiming to conceal her love while revealing enough to inflame Beatrix’s pride and curiosity. Her remarks, sometimes treacherous, often wounded Beatrix, who took offense at perceived distrust. Yet Beatrix could not help being secretly amused to find such a great writer caught in pettiness and resolved to show where Camille’s greatness ended.

Camille’s tactics included assigning Calyste to study, keeping him in her company, and hinting at the marquise’s supposed indifference to his visits. She reported that Beatrix had written only once to Conti that week, implying a thaw. Calyste lived for the daily moments before Beatrix and read late into the night, bringing home armfuls of books. His mother, alarmed, implored him to leave Les Touches, but he refused. He nearly broke his promise and resolved to approach Beatrix privately in the garden one morning to read her eyes.

After a marsh outing, Calyste met Beatrix on a garden path. He trembled and asked if he had displeased her. She replied faintly that he had neither pleased nor displeased her. When he implored whether he was indifferent to her, she asked if they should not be indifferent given their “binding attachment” elsewhere. Calyste, stunned, declared that he had once loved Camille but loved her no longer. Beatrix, hurt yet composed, noted that Calyste and Camille spent every morning together and suggested that Camille did not prefer a cigar to his company. Camille then appeared and rebuked Calyste: was this what he had promised? She took his arm and drew him away. The scene displayed Camille’s sacrifice: she forced Calyste into a reciprocity of appearances—an abnegation that could be interpreted as noble or ruinous.

At breakfast Beatrix determined to stifle any rising love and behaved with gentle indifference, following Camille’s advice. Felicite proposed excursions, but Beatrix refused, saying her position was delicate and she would not jeopardize her happiness because Conti was coming to fetch her. Calyste took the news with pallor, left Les Touches depressed and near madness at the thought of losing Beatrix, and spent his time pondering a decisive explanation to win her.

Throughout these scenes strategy, pride, and genuine feeling intertwine. Claude, preparing to leave, remains the cynical observer who recognizes motives and predicts calamities. Camille, inwardly torn and capable of self-effacement, chooses to guide events through subtle deception, convinced she can shape Beatrix’s heart and preserve her own claim to Calyste. Beatrix, proud and resolute, answers with politeness that may hide deeper sentiment but insists on autonomy. Calyste, immature and ardent, is trapped between youthful passion and artful manipulation, a pawn in the hands of stronger wills whose consequences he cannot yet foresee.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
When Calyste returned home he shut himself up and wrote feverishly. His mother found him among crumpled pages composing a letter to Béatrix, driven by fresh distrust of Camille after the marquise’s recent behavior.

Calyste’s first love letter poured out intense feeling. He said his love had swelled since meeting Béatrix, beyond any dream, and compared himself—youthful and unworthy—to an insect drawn to her light. He contrasted a former, lesser attachment to Félicité with his present, profound truth: Béatrix. Though he felt she scorned him, he insisted his devotion was real, invoked Camille’s teaching that true love must grow and endure, and begged for kindness, recognition, and even the simple right to accompany her or be treated as a friend. Much of the letter reiterated these themes; his mother, reading it, was moved to tears and alarmed by his raw passion.

Torn about how to deliver the note, Calyste consulted the Chevalier du Halga, who suggested giving it to Béatrix’s maid for discretion. Calyste, however, chose to hand it to Béatrix himself. At Les Touches he found Béatrix and Camille together and impulsively gave her the letter, which she accepted without hesitation.

After a restless night he awaited her reply in the garden. Béatrix acknowledged his noble feelings but reminded him he was still a child and affirmed her commitment to Camille. She doubted her own suitability as a partner, warned that accepting his love would insult Camille, and candidly admitted her jealous, capricious nature would likely make Calyste unhappy. She urged him to seek a young girl worthy of his devotion.

The baroness, moved, questioned Calyste and read Béatrix’s reply aloud—admiring Béatrix’s nobility but anxious for her son’s future. She pressed him to consider marrying Charlotte de Kergarouet to secure the estate and prospects, but Calyste remained steadfast for Béatrix and dismissed his mother’s counsel.

He withdrew to write again, reaffirming his unwavering love and even fantasizing about elopement, convinced love could defy social constraints. Troubled, the baroness sought the Chevalier’s view; amused by the infatuation, he made light remarks. Returning home she found yet another letter revealing the depth of Calyste’s obsession and prayed for him. When he returned she confessed she had read his letters; he reassured her. They planned a boat trip to Croisic to see Béatrix. The letters revealed surprising maturity and clarity in Calyste but also the intensity of his emotion and the potential consequences of his infatuation.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
One of Beatrix’s chief pleasures was outwitting someone she felt superior to, and she considered herself inferior to Camille Maupin in both talent and passion. While Calyste sped off to Les Touches, intoxicated by first love, Beatrix enjoyed being the object of his affection without intending to return it. She prided herself on repressing feeling, imagining a noble sacrifice like Camille’s; French vanities flattered her and she felt morally upright in resisting Calyste.

The two women reclined in the salon, the calm garden outside contrasting with their inner unrest. Camille, conscious of the disruption she’d caused, guarded her secret love and viewed Beatrix as a shrewd rival. She sought refuge in nature and higher reflection—Calyste had become almost a guardian angel—yet Beatrix’s triumph unsettled her.

When Calyste arrived with a letter, Camille observed their exchange and perceived an unspoken understanding: Beatrix now treated Calyste as hers while he appeared guilty and transparent. He announced plans for an excursion; Camille, suspecting a conspiracy between them, grew anxious and discomposed.

After Calyste left to arrange the boat and later returned, neither woman met his eye; he left for home heavy with despair, a stark contrast to earlier joy—his mother noticed but he dismissed it. Beatrix, meanwhile, found the letter stirring unexpected feelings; doubts about a possible plot with Camille provoked jealousy and anger.

Dinner was tense. Camille avoided direct contact; veiled insults and hostile looks culminated in a confrontation. Beatrix accused Camille of trickery and cruelty; Camille retorted by exposing Beatrix’s motives. Beatrix attacked Camille’s lack of traditional femininity and compassion. Camille then declared her deep love for Calyste; Beatrix answered that she would not be his mistress—she could not accept a relationship that would burden him, only marriage would do. The remark wounded Camille and escalated their rivalry. Camille broke off the friendship and threatened to write to Conti, Beatrix’s former lover; Beatrix refused to yield.

The night left both women reflective: their conflict unresolved, settling into a precarious balance as they confronted desire and social constraint.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
It was late August; the sky was clear and the sea shimmered. Paludiers in white harvested salt as the baron and baroness admired the marshes from the jetty. The baron wanted one last look, prompting a paludier’s wry joke about the Guenic family’s mortality. A party from Les Touches then arrived: the marquise, followed by Calyste and Camille. The baroness felt a strong aversion to Beatrix, who, though tastefully dressed, seemed heartless to her.

Calyste introduced the visitors; the baron noted Camille’s large marsh holdings, and a paludier praised her. The groups separated and boarded a boat for Croisic, where the marquise kept a cool demeanor. Camille chastised Calyste for disobedience; he was torn between anger and love for Beatrix. The passage was tense and silent; at the port barefoot women unloaded salt.

The Croisic peninsula presented wind-sculpted granite and a hollow basin used for bathing; a lone box bush clung to a distant promontory, a small marvel of resilience. Calyste led Beatrix to a viewpoint while Camille sought solitude among the rocks, reflecting on her life and feelings toward Calyste. Calyste and Beatrix spoke of the ocean and then their emotions.

Beatrix confessed an inner desolation and insisted she could never be with Calyste despite loving him; she feared abandoning her present life and the pain of unfulfilled love. Calyste, desperate, offered to sacrifice everything, but Beatrix rejected sacrifice and reaffirmed her commitment to her situation. At the rock with the box bush, passion overwhelmed Calyste; in a moment of intimacy Beatrix slipped and fell. He rushed to save her; amid the confusion their feelings intensified. Gasselin and Camille arrived to help, and the injured Beatrix was carried to a farmhouse for care.

Calyste stayed by her, alternating despair and hope as the doctor assured them she would recover. Beatrix, moved by his devotion, began to soften. Playing the invalid, she enjoyed his adoration—an unusual power over him—while keeping distance and reminding him of her obligation to another man. Despite that resistance, she was drawn to his steadfast affection, disclosed past struggles, and accepted his vow of exclusive love. Their bond deepened and Beatrix began to see him as a possible source of happiness.

They prepared to return to the rock; Beatrix acknowledged the joy he brought but reiterated her duty. Calyste believed love could overcome her past. Back at Les Touches, however, they encountered Conti, Beatrix’s former lover, and the moment shattered—revealing the tangled complexities of love, loyalty, and uncertain consequences.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The marquise’s inner turmoil was plain when Conti arrived unexpectedly, took Beatrix’s arm, and forced her to abandon Calyste, who felt humiliated and conflicted. Though convinced of his love, he was struck by her distress and fell silent.

Calyste turned to Camille, who explained the tangled power dynamics: Beatrix could not refuse Conti because of lingering ties from their past. Camille warned that Conti was manipulative and would use insinuations to regain control.

Conti’s return after a failed opera seemed opportunistic. He feigned indifference to Beatrix’s new attachment while subtly undermining her confidence. At dinner his jovial manner masked intent; veiled comments about women’s loyalty wounded both Beatrix and Camille. Afterwards Beatrix told Camille she felt helpless and even blamed Camille for Conti’s premature return, exposing a deep rivalry. Beatrix insisted she would always hold a place in Calyste’s heart; Camille retorted that Beatrix’s reign was fleeting.

Meanwhile Conti and Calyste exchanged tense words. Conti cast himself as the injured party, claiming to have lost interest and urging Calyste to pursue Beatrix, suggesting her pride would spur revenge. Unaware of Conti’s designs, Calyste confessed details of his time with Beatrix, inflaming Conti’s jealousy.

That evening Conti staged a salon performance to provoke Beatrix and assert dominance. Entranced by Conti’s singing, Calyste approached Beatrix with new confidence, not perceiving the trap. The next morning Beatrix left with Conti, and Calyste was devastated. Camille informed him; he sank into grief, clinging to his belief in Beatrix despite Camille’s attempts to reveal her true nature.

Weeks passed and Calyste’s sorrow deepened. He wandered aimlessly, his family mistaking melancholy for a passing phase and worrying as his health and social withdrawal worsened. Pressured to marry, he refused Charlotte, confessed his love, and begged his family to release Beatrix. After the baron’s death Calyste slid further into despair until Camille proposed helping him find Beatrix in Paris. Renewed, he agreed, hoping reunion might restore him.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Felicite’s love had set the stage for a prosperous future for Calyste. She wrote to the Duchesse de Grandlieu about him, sold her house for 2,500,000 francs, bought another for 700,000, and planned to use one million to recover the du Guenic estates, settling the remainder on Sabine de Grandlieu. Knowing the duke and duchess wanted advantageous matches for their daughters—only the youngest, Sabine, was marriageable—Felicite aimed to redirect Calyste’s affections from Beatrix to Sabine.

On the trip to Paris, Mademoiselle des Touches confided in the baroness, who prepared a new house for Sabine and Calyste. Welcomed at the hôtel de Grandlieu, they received due respect. Mademoiselle des Touches encouraged Calyste to enjoy Paris while she sought news of Beatrix abroad. The city’s bustle eased his obsession; noticing a resemblance between Beatrix and the lively Sabine, he grew drawn to her. By winter 1837 he appeared refreshed and listened to his mother’s proposal for Sabine, though he concealed any indifference.

On the day the marriage contract was to be signed, Calyste surprised everyone by accepting the benefits offered by Mademoiselle des Touches but then reading a letter she had sent through Sabine. In it Camille professed love, declared her intention to enter a convent, urged him to accept her gifts for his future welfare, and asked him to remember her kindly while placing duty above passion.

After reading the letter, Calyste rejoined the others and the contract was signed. The wedding at Saint Thomas d’Aquin followed the next week; Calyste and Sabine left for Brittany amid tears and congratulations. The duchess and Clotilde worried for Sabine, aware Calyste had not married wholly by his own will and understanding that marriage required true compatibility—a greater risk for women. The Baronne du Guenic, with only one son, took responsibility for Sabine’s care.

On the journey the newlyweds felt awkward, each conscious of their first moments together. Calyste suggested they be like friends or siblings, unsettling Sabine and leaving her pensive. He reluctantly recounted his former love for Beatrix; Sabine was hurt that their union stemmed from his rejection of another, but she found some comfort in his promise to forget Beatrix, and gradually their relationship thawed.

In letters to her mother Sabine described Brittany, her longing for home, and the difficulty of loving Calyste fully. As chatelaine she felt fulfilled—adored by his family and tenants—and embraced Breton traditions and estate work. A convent visit to Mademoiselle des Touches yielded blunt advice: be capricious when necessary and never let Calyste see Beatrix again. Sabine felt conflicted—unable to feign caprice or fully control her feelings—tracing the delicate navigation of devotion, insecurity, social expectation, and identity required to make such a marriage endure.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

Guerande, July 1838.

To Madame la Duchesse de Grandlieu:

Ah, my dear mother! After three months, I have learned what jealousy is. My heart is torn between love and hatred; I feel betrayed and unloved. How fortunate I am to have you to confide in!

It takes so little to set a wife’s curiosity ablaze: “Here’s a key to your palace; go everywhere, but avoid Les Touches!” Mademoiselle des Touches has sown bitterness in me. Why forbid Les Touches? What happiness depends on avoiding a single house in Brittany? Why should I fear?

“My dear mother-in-law said, ‘Les Touches belongs to you.’ ‘If Calyste had never set foot in Les Touches!’ exclaimed Aunt Zephirine. ‘He would not be my husband,’ I replied. ‘Then you know what happened there?’ she asked slyly. ‘It is a place of perdition!’ declared Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel. ‘Mademoiselle des Touches committed many sins there.’ ‘They saved her soul and enriched a convent,’ said the Chevalier du Halga. ‘Would you like to visit Les Touches?’ my mother-in-law asked. ‘No, no!’ I hastily replied.”

That little scene felt like a diabolical drama. Such involuntary stabs force anyone to question the stability of their happiness. Calyste never suggested we visit that hermitage—now his property—but his silence made me ask him about it. “Let’s go there,” he said, and so I let chance catch me.

Les Touches is enchanting and profoundly artistic—poisonous flowers can be charming; the devil has flowers too. I delighted in that place of horror and watched Calyste for signs that his passion for Mademoiselle des Touches might still live. I dared to mention Madame de Rochefide—“la Rocheperfide”—and he said nothing; when I pressed, he begged me to stop reviving that passion. The plea itself inflamed me. Still, when I spoke to him about forgiving misrecognitions tied to that place, he blushed and I embraced him. Yet I left Les Touches and vowed never to return.

My hatred of Madame de Rochefide grows as proof of my love for Calyste. That woman haunts my dreams. The postulant of the Visitation was right—Les Touches is fatal. Calyste has revived past emotions there, stronger than our joy. If Madame de Rochefide is in Paris, I shall stay in Brittany. Poor Mademoiselle des Touches might regret her role when she knew how our odious rival haunted me. But I am ashamed—sometimes I wish to flee Guerande and the sands of Croisic.

August 25th: I am determined to live in the ruins of the old chateau. Calyste, worried by my restlessness, consents. Either he suspects nothing or he knows and loves me not. I tremble at finding certainty, so like a child I cover my eyes to avoid the explosion.

Oh, mother! I am not loved as I love. Calyste is charming—what man wouldn’t be amiable when adored by a pure, loving girl? Yet suspicion intrudes. By September I oscillate between despair and clinging joy, fretting over whether his past has returned to claim him. Sister Clotilde’s melancholy wisdom recurs to me: a woman faithful to the dead fears no rival. Poor me: I fear I can never win Calyste back from himself.

A thousand tendernesses, dear mother. If my terrors are not delusions, Camille Maupin has sold me her fortune dearly. My affectionate respects to papa.

These letters reveal the private breach between husband and wife: Sabine imagined a love marriage; Calyste accepted a conventional match. Their honeymoon joys misaligned with expectations.

In Brittany, the hotel du Guenic was restored and furnished under the famed architect Grindot, supervised by Clotilde and the Duc and Duchesse de Grandlieu. The young couple planned to return to Paris in December 1838. Sabine took pleasure in settling rue de Bourbon—both to preside over a great household and to read her family’s judgment of her marriage.

Calyste, indifferent, allowed Clotilde and the duchess to guide his social life. He earned his standing by name, fortune, and alliance. Sabine, celebrated in Paris as one of its most charming women, found diversions and duties of the season enlivening. Her pregnancy and the birth of a son in October 1839 confirmed a neutral, socially respectable union; Sabine mistakenly nursed the child herself, believing a mother should be devoted wholly to her child.

By August 1840 Sabine was nearly done nursing. Calyste, in two years in Paris, had shed the innocence of his early experience in passion. He mixed with Duc Georges de Maufrigneuse, Vicomte Savinien de Portenduere, and others, learning the differences between Parisian and provincial life. Wealth brings idle hours; Paris provides amusements. Contact with young husbands who abandoned noble women for cigars, whist, and clubs gradually eroded domestic virtues. A proud wife may tolerate such dissipation as proof of her success.

One October evening, to escape the cries of their newly weaned child, Calyste went to the Variétés, urged by Sabine. He took a stall near the avant-scène. During the interlude he saw Madame de Rochefide in a proscenium box—Beatrix in Paris! The sight struck him like an arrow. He had sometimes even substituted Beatrix for his wife in thought; Beatrix embodied light, life, the Unknown; Sabine embodied duty and the expected.

Calyste’s first impulse was to leave. Yet the proscenium door was ajar and he approached. Beatrix sat between Canalis and Raoul Nathan. In three years her appearance had changed—gaunt, faded, eyes hollow—but Parisian artifice and contrivance made her instead more poetically attractive to Calyste. She greeted him with a mix of affected grief and coquettish nostalgia—complaining at his marriage and teasing him about “such a little fool.” She revived old intimacy, pressing his hand and turning tears to performative gestures. She spoke of living modestly in a rue de Chartres house “suitable to my means,” filling her head with literature to distract herself.

Calyste left with the perfume of Beatrix still in his nostrils; the rest of the evening she darted sidelong looks that tore at his soul. He returned home haunted: his apartments’ splendor reminded him of the mediocrity Beatrix had mocked, and he resented his wealth because it could not belong to her. He delighted in the possession of a night to relive emotions and feared the way Sabine could read him like a book. A woman who adores can detect every quiver, every sadness; she studies his face for signs of love or its absence.

The next morning Calyste learned Sabine watched for signs of convulsions in their son and was joyous to hear the child was better. Yet he avoided breakfast, preferring a walk to his bachelor café. Love puzzles: a man raised with sacred morals may still be drawn to a fallen, dangerous woman while his virtuous wife waits at home; the reasons may be buried vanity—men sometimes seek to assert superiority by conquering what is beneath them—or the thrill of triumph in winning a repugnant or repellent object. Calyste was drawn to Beatrix for reasons neither philosophy nor morals fully explain.

He decided, consumed by the thought of seeing her again, to go to rue de Chartres. The house was familiar to him though he had never been there; the old footman asked his name. Calyste waited, gazing into a garden marked by rain-streaked stucco.

Beatrix, who had left fortune to her husband when she fled and relied on small sums from her mother and friends, lived in a modest yet tasteful house formerly owned by a lord, with servants and frugal comfort. Calyste entered richly furnished rooms—garnet velvet, celadon vases, a silver cup ascribed to Benvenuto Cellini—elegant relics of a past life. A little table held jeweled trinkets and a work-in-progress, a dagger handle used as a paper-cutter: symbol of critique; watercolors showed the bedrooms of her wandering life.

She appeared in studied attire, a gown revealing her bosom, hair in a lace cap, smiling as though expecting a lover. The perfume she wore recalled Les Touches; the scent, her dress, the glint in her eyes made Calyste dizzy. She bade him sit; she said she treated him as a friend and would be no one’s honest mistress, yet she spoke of the impossibility of belonging to any man on earth because of what she had once expected of Calyste. Three hours passed with her urging him to resolve his conjugal faith; she affected to see Sabine as a stiff, dry, brown woman—good, dutiful, untempting—hardly the ideal for a man like Calyste.

Calyste, pressed, rose to leave; she teased him to dine with her. He had the impulse to write Sabine a note, and when he began “My dear Sabine—” Beatrix snapped, “‘My dear’? Can you say your wife is still dear?” She accused him of cowardice and deceit and forced him to write that he dined out; she would send the note by her messenger. He returned home at two in the morning. Sabine, who had waited until half-past twelve for him, had gone to bed exhausted. She slept, distressed by the terse note, yet made the charitable assumption that love explains everything.

The next morning the child was better. Sabine, smiling, came with the baby to present him before breakfast; Calyste charmed her, overplaying affection. In the salon she caught his face in a mirror and saw weariness. She asked, “What did you do yesterday?” He said Savinien had kept him to dinner and then they went to the club to play whist. Sabine lectured on noblesse and industry, then gently reproached the coldness of his first note to her; he claimed club directors were dandies. Small suspicions accumulated: she remembered the paper she had thrown into her letter-box—perhaps it bore a coronet.

Calyste, planning to conceal his doings, rushed to Savinien and then to rue de Chartres to see how Beatrix had passed the night. He found her after her bath, fresh and lively, breakfasting on eggs with wit and candor. He stayed until late; his horse returned sweating such that Sabine, watching from a window, noticed.

Meeting him on the staircase she said, “Abd-el-Kader is nearly foundered. You said you’d be gone a moment. I waited three hours.” Calyste, improving in dissimulation, tried to present the surprise of a present: a dressing-table being finished for her, with contributions from his mother and aunt. Sabine clasped him, faint with joy. Then, like a viper’s bite, she recoiled—she had smelled a perfume on his cravat: the scent of another woman’s note-paper. She had kissed where the rival’s kisses had been. Panic overcame her: “Fetch the doctor and my nurse!” she cried. Calyste fled to call them.

Sabine, seized by a chill of reaction, lay shivering, crying, “I am dying!” Her maid fetched Madame de Portenduere. In feverish haste she wrote to her mother—a letter of thanks for the dressing-table, an attempt at ordinary gratitude—but her mind raced: “My God! my God! my God!” She feared betrayal after three years and twenty-two years of age; her teeth chattered, her face turned greenish. “I don’t know yet [the rival], but Calyste has told me two lies. Do not pity me. Oh! that letter of yesterday!” She seized the thrown-away paper from her letter-box: a coronet—could it belong to a marquise? She begged Ursula to find out if Madame de Rochefide had returned to Paris.

Ursula, stunned, went to the duchess, pledging secrecy. The duchess, who had seen such scenes, advised deceit for Sabine’s sake: “Friendship gives you an experience of a woman my age. I know how Sabine loves her husband; she might become insane.” “Or lose her beauty,” Ursula added. “Let us go to her.”

Fortuitously, they arrived before the accoucheur Dommanget and Calyste. The duchess told Sabine that Beatrix was not in Paris; as for Calyste’s night, she declared he had lost a great deal of money at cards and didn’t even know how to pay for the dressing-table. She scoffed at the coronet on the paper—“Everybody writes on coroneted paper now”—and burned the fatal sheet.

When Calyste and Dommanget arrived, the duchess informed them: “Sabine’s life is at stake; you have betrayed her for Madame de Rochefide.” Calyste blushed like a schoolboy. The duchess, with the physician, invented a lie to steer the diagnosis: Calyste had lost thirty thousand francs at cards. Dommanget, quick and self-interested, used the lie to explain Sabine’s fainting as reactionary hysterical symptoms due to overexertion from nursing and anxiety. The physician prescribed measures; Calyste sat at the foot of the bed, trying to look tender. Sabine, hearing the explanation, retorted that her husband could lose any sum without being lectured. Calyste embraced her and called her an angel.

Two days later, thought safe, Sabine improved. Calyste visited Madame de Rochefide, making a merit of his infamy.

“Beatrix,” he said, “you owe me happiness. I have sacrificed my poor little wife to you; she discovered everything. The fatal paper bore your name and coronet; I didn’t notice—the ‘B’ was erased. Your perfume betrayed me. Sabine nearly died; her milk went to her head; erysipelas set in; she may bear marks for life.”

Beatrix’s face froze. “So much the better,” she said; “perhaps it will whiten her for you.” Her icy cruelty continued in a series of sarcasms. There is no greater blunder than to speak of one’s virtuous wife to one’s mistress—unless it is to tell one’s wife of the mistress. Calyste had neither the Parisian training to lie to his wife nor the tact to handle his mistress. He begged Beatrix for pardon; she forced him to beg for two hours. She answered from an elevated bitterness: “To speak to me of your wife the day after my fall! Why did you not tell me she is a pearl of virtue?” She declared her love for his soul and then scorned Calyste’s looks.

This speech was part of Beatrix’s system: with each passion she became another being, advancing into profligacy. She had judged herself by the mirror, counting wrinkles and crow’s-feet; she knew her resources and used them. Reduced by Conti’s desertion and grateful for scraps from friends, she aimed at a terrible notoriety—vengeance through fame. To prevail over a splendid young woman, she used the arts of courtesans: contempt mixed with rare affection, comparisons meant to mortify, and a cultivated disdain to stimulate conquest. She resolved to make Calyste think himself ugly and unwelcome—thus rekindling his desire to conquer. She contrasted her poetic, comfortable home with the hotel du Guenic’s Faubourg Saint-Germain luxury, painting the latter stupid and empty.

Thus, the scene of reconciliation—where she made him swear hatred for his wife and denounce her as playing comedy—took shape in a bower of flowers and rare plants, amid trifles and sumptuous yet carefully economical luxury. Fallen into a mortifying position by Conti’s desertion, Beatrix was determined to gain, at least, the triumphs of notoriety. The misfortune of a rich and beautiful wife like Sabine was to be her pedestal.

When Sabine returns to ordinary life after nursing her first child, she seems refreshed but finds her husband Calyste changed. A letter from her mother hints at his discontent and mentions another woman, Beatrix, confirming Sabine’s worst fears. The vague note offers no clear explanation; in despair Sabine scrawls a comment on it and leaves it where Calyste will see it. He finds the remark but ignores and burns the paper, humiliating her and deepening her distress.

Beatrix’s influence becomes concrete when a perfume on a later letter reveals her hand. Sabine, recognizing it, sees the rival’s hold. In a febrile moment she dances with her child, clinging to motherhood’s innocence, then weeps alone as Calyste departs to read Beatrix’s message. Her efforts to win him back—decorating the house with flowers, improving her cooking and appearance—meet only indifference, intensifying jealousy and self-doubt.

Despite her suffering, Sabine’s love persists; she strives to be the devoted wife but finds these virtues ineffective. After another failed attempt to move Calyste she collapses in despair and tears off a beautiful dress. Grief saps her health; the duchess, her mother, notices and attempts to distract her. Sabine, fearing interference in her marriage, initially remains silent but eventually confides in her sister Athenais, speaking of anguish and asking that her child be cared for should she die.

Warned by her experience, Sabine counsels Athenais to be cautious in love, lamenting that her straightforwardness and passion now obstruct happiness and feel driven toward duplicity. Alarmed, the duchess consults Abbe Brossette and reveals a plan to break Calyste’s attachment and restore Sabine’s happiness. The abbe cautions against mixing spiritual authority with worldly intrigue and urges a more honorable solution, but the duchess, determined to spare her daughter further suffering, resolves to act even if it means morally questionable measures.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A storm was brewing for Monsieur de Rochefide, who enjoyed Parisian life as fully after Beatrix left as he had during their marriage, and assumed such a satisfactory existence was nearly impossible to disrupt. The tale that follows.—contrasting how society treats men and women in similar situations—might encourage women to stay home and defend their dignity, as Sabine du Guenic did.

After Beatrix’s departure, Arthur de Rochefide, now an only child after his sister’s death, became sole owner of the hôtel de Rochefide and inherited a large fortune. His income reached about a thousand francs a day, ample for his tastes. While Beatrix had fulfilled maternal duties, Rochefide enjoyed his wealth without extravagance. His vanity, aided by good looks and a jaunty military tone, let him dismiss women and appropriate others’ wit; acquaintances found him witty enough. As a husband he was pitied; society blamed Beatrix for leaving such a “good” man. Seeking distinction, he joined clubs and championed fashionable causes. His main indulgence was racehorses, kept by an English groom at considerable monthly cost. Despite wealth, he lacked taste and intellect, relying largely on inherited fortune and bachelor freedoms, claiming he was “born with a caul,” destined for luck.

Rochefide’s indifference to domestic life was plain: he rarely dined or slept at home. After several affairs he married Madame Schontz, known as La Petite-Aurélie, daughter of Colonel Schiltz. Orphaned and schooled at Saint-Denis, Aurelie had been launched into precarious social worlds where many women fell into poverty or early death. Her turbulent relationships eventually led her to Rochefide at Valentino’s; captivated by her charm, he married her two years after Beatrix left—a choice society did not condemn.

Their relationship unfolded in four phases. First, courtship: he provided an allowance and furnished lodgings; she showed charm and talent but also ambition. Second, she moved them to better accommodations; he lavished gifts while she managed the household with superior dinners and budgeting, rebuffing richer suitors. Third, she became a mother figure to Rochefide’s son, oversaw education and finances, and advised profitable investments she never claimed. Fourth, she established their social circle, preserved reputation, and quietly amassed a modest fortune through savings, investments, and selling jewelry—presenting expenses as necessary to maintain their lifestyle. Through these phases Madame Schontz demonstrated tactical superiority, turning the relationship into mutual benefit while skillfully navigating their social world.

Such conduct implied a plan, and Madame Schontz had one. Jealous of Madame du Bruel for two years, she was determined to marry in both church and civil ceremonies — in short, to find a second Arthur. Rochefide’s secret rival remained unknown: Bixiou suspected Léon de Lora, the painter thought Bixiou himself was favored, and others named Victor de Vernisset or Stidmann. None truly understood Madame Schontz, whose single focus was securing a good marriage.

The only man of dubious reputation in her salon was Couture, a stock-gambler fallen from grace but still loyal. When he introduced Fabien du Ronceret, a provincial, Madame Schontz took interest. Fabien, son of a chief justice, had left a judgeship in Alençon for Paris with a small income and a taste for exploiting modern social mores. He grasped the era’s new currency — pride, vanity, notoriety — and aimed to be talked about, cultivating attention through ostentation.

His Paris debut was conspicuous: a lavish apartment and a greenhouse designed as spectacle. Seeking influential associations and entry into Madame Schontz’s circle, he first tried established acquaintances, then turned to Couture, who recognized his potential and recommended him. Madame Schontz quickly assessed Fabien as suitable for her ambitions, flattered him, and led him to suppose he was the object of her affection. Bewitched by her charm, he presented himself in his finest attire.

A month before a significant meeting Madame Schontz disclosed her identity and aims; Fabien failed to grasp her intentions. Realizing his misunderstanding, she took him for a carriage ride and declared she wanted a husband of ambition who could value her character. Despite her wealth and encouragement to rise, Fabien hesitated at marriage; she then turned to Couture, who promptly proposed upon learning of her fortune.

Meanwhile the Duchesse de Grandlieu sought Comte Maxime de Trailles’s help with a delicate family matter. Maxime, a seasoned political operator, agreed but imposed conditions — notably that the duchess accept his future wife into her circle — and planned to eliminate a rival, requiring her obedience. He acknowledged the emotional complications, especially Calyste’s infatuation with Béatrix, and the moral ambiguity of manipulating such feelings. The next day Rochefide told Madame Schontz of Maxime’s impending visit; she readied her house and appearance, aware his reputation promised both privilege and challenge.

The next day Maxime de Trailles summoned Finot to arrange a breakfast at the Café Anglais with Couture and Lousteau to discuss Madame Schontz. While lingering under the pretense of dealing with du Tillet, he waited for the Comte de la Palferine, questioned him about his debts, and—admiring the Comte’s spirit—offered to bankroll a courtship with a loan of twenty thousand francs.

Maxime’s target was a well-bred marquise; he urged La Palferine to woo her sincerely and they agreed to meet later at Madame Schontz’s. He also visited the duchess, who fretted over her daughter Sabine’s distress at Calyste’s absence, asking the duchess to keep his plans secret while providing the funds and assuring her the campaign would not be extravagant.

Madame Schontz prepared a dinner, inviting guests including Claude Vignon and La Palferine; she insisted she was an artist, not a courtesan, and expressed a desire for a respectable marriage. At dinner Maxime praised her success with Rochefide—claiming she had cost him less than other conquests—then privately gave La Palferine the promised funds and precise advice on making a memorable impression on the marquise. Maxime and Madame Schontz schemed to manipulate Rochefide’s affections, and Maxime vowed to secure a post for Couture in return for cooperation.

Meanwhile Calyste, feeling neglected, confronted Beatrix, who was torn between genuine feeling for him and a growing attraction to La Palferine. Their relationship frayed as Maxime arranged encounters that deepened Beatrix’s entanglement; Calyste’s attempts to reclaim her failed, culminating in Beatrix’s public humiliation at the opera. As Beatrix drifts into La Palferine’s orbit, loyalties shift and Maxime counsels Calyste to reconsider his tactics. The episode explores love, betrayal, and ambition—how manipulation and desire breed disillusionment and force characters to re-evaluate past choices and futures.
