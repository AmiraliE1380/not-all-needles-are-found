In October 1800, a foreigner named Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and young daughter. The man, over sixty and grave, was deeply troubled; his wife, though sorrowful, tried to keep a calm face for their child. Their despair attracted passersby, but the stranger's fierce gaze quickly deterred sympathy.

After a moment’s thought, Bartolomeo made a desperate decision. He handed a dagger to his wife, declaring he would confront the Bonapartes. He approached the palace but was stopped by a soldier who refused entry without a written request. Bartolomeo persisted, creating a tense standoff until Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, arrived, recognized him, and took him inside to meet his brother, Napoleon Bonaparte.

Inside, Napoleon dismissed his aides to speak privately with Bartolomeo. The Corsican sought asylum, revealing he had killed the Portas, a family who had wronged him by burning his vineyard and killing his son. He recounted how he avenged his son’s death by killing all seven Portas, a revelation that shocked Napoleon. Bartolomeo complained of the Bonaparte brothers' indifference, reminding them of his past protection of their family.

Napoleon, now the leader of a nation, felt bound by law and could not openly endorse Bartolomeo’s actions. Yet he acknowledged the enduring tradition of vendetta in Corsica and offered Bartolomeo a chance to live in Paris, promising financial assistance. He warned that the law would not tolerate personal justice in the city.

Bartolomeo accepted Napoleon's offer, pledging loyalty to the Bonaparte brothers. He thanked them for recognizing his misfortune and seemed hopeful about his future in Paris. Napoleon, seeing Bartolomeo as a possible ally, hinted that he might need trustworthy friends in the future.

After their meeting, Lucien asked about Bartolomeo’s finances and learned the family had arrived in Paris with nothing. Lucien generously gave him money and promised to help secure their comfort; the value of Bartolomeo’s Corsican property would not suffice to support them in Paris.

Fifteen years passed since Bartolomeo's arrival in Paris, leading to events that would unfold and required the context of his earlier struggles and decisions.

CHAPTER II. THE STUDIO

Servin, a distinguished artist, was the first to establish a studio for young girls wishing to paint. About forty, upright in character and devoted to his art, he was married to a general’s dowerless daughter. At first mothers accompanied their daughters, but soon they trusted Servin’s principles enough to send them alone.

He taught only young ladies from wealthy families to avoid complaints about class, and refused aspiring professional artists who would require more thorough instruction. His carefulness and the quality of his teaching won him a stellar reputation among the aristocracy. When a girl expressed an interest in drawing, mothers recommended Servin without hesitation.

He became synonymous with feminine art, like Herbault for bonnets or Leroy for gowns. A young woman trained by him was presumed able to critique museum paintings, paint striking portraits, or produce genre scenes. Despite his connections with Parisian elites, Servin maintained an independent, patriotic stance, expressed with a witty, half-ironic tone.

The studio was arranged with pupils in mind. The attic entrance above his apartment was sealed, reached by a small spiral staircase. The studio itself was large, lit by broad panes veiled with green linen shades. Dark-gray walls were hung with caricatures and plaster models, a mix of artistic chaos and order.

On a bright July day the studio buzzed with young girls at easels, each with distinct expressions and styles. Among them sat Ginevra Piombo, modestly dressed and apart, seeming anxious. The group split into two cliques: daughters of wealthy merchants, and aristocrats led by Amelie Thirion, vain and pretentious.

Despite differences, the girls worked, laughing and chatting, creating a lively atmosphere. Ginevra, the most talented and beautiful, drew jealousy from the aristocratic group. Amelie, aiming to ostracize her, moved Ginevra’s easel away from the others, provoking whispers about the insult.

The political climate after the Bourbons’ second return intensified tensions. Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, faced disdain from royalist peers. Amelie’s act was both personal slight and reflection of political divisions.

When Ginevra arrived she encountered cold silence rather than her usual warm reception. Her beauty and grace commanded respect, but her politics made her a target. She remained focused on her work, apparently unaware of the growing hostility.

As the session progressed, a mysterious sound behind a partition drew Ginevra’s attention. She discovered Servin was hiding a political fugitive, an officer of the Imperial Guard. The revelation brought excitement and concern for the man’s safety.

Ginevra resolved to protect the fugitive, preferring to be near him rather than risk his exposure. Her indifference to Amelie’s slight only intensified jealousy among the girls, who were baffled by her calm.

Madame Servin’s arrival interrupted the brewing conflict. She announced her husband could not attend that day, and her manner suggested she knew of the hidden man. Sensing the tension, Ginevra began humming a Corsican song to mask the sound of the fugitive’s breathing.

Throughout the session Ginevra stayed absorbed in her work, sketching the officer’s head with a fervor that surprised her. Servin praised her talent, and the others, including Amelie, were drawn to her sheet. Ginevra’s determination to keep the fugitive’s presence secret became evident as she deftly deflected attention from her sketch.

Amelie, suspicious, tried to uncover the mystery but was foiled by Ginevra’s quick thinking. Their rivalry intensified: Ginevra’s talent and composure only heightened Amelie’s envy.

Servin, aware of the undercurrent, engaged each pupil but kept an eye on Ginevra. Their shared understanding of the situation deepened a tacit bond, and Ginevra felt empowered to help protect the fugitive.

As the day ended, Ginevra’s resolve to keep the secret grew. She remained determined to aid the officer’s escape from the dangers outside. Political turmoil loomed over the studio, but within its walls the girls navigated rivalries and friendships, united by their art.

When the painter and Ginevra believed they were alone, Servin knocked on the garret door, revealing a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, his arm in a sling and his face pale with pain. He recoiled at seeing Ginevra, but Servin reassured him, introducing her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo. Recognizing her patriotism, the soldier relaxed.

"You are wounded," Ginevra observed.

"It's nothing, mademoiselle," he replied. "The wound is healing."

Cries in the street announced a death sentence: "Condemned to death!" The officer turned pale at the name "Labedoyere," collapsing onto a stool. In despair he lamented that while Labedoyere faced death for their cause, he was hiding.

Ginevra blocked his path as he tried to leave. "Can you restore the Emperor?" she asked. "Do you expect to raise that giant who could not maintain himself?"

"What can I do?" he replied, feeling alone after losing his protector. He said he was willing to die, that an honest man's life was worth more than two traitors', and contemplated selling his life to the executioner.

Alarmed, Ginevra offered her savings, explaining that her father was wealthy and would not blame her for helping a soldier. She urged him to wait until the storm passed, suggesting he could escape and serve elsewhere.

Her words, filled with maternal grace and hope, touched him. He admired her beauty and felt a rush of emotion. "You are an angel of goodness," he said, then his thoughts returned to Labedoyere.

In silence they shared a moment of understanding, their acquaintance deepening into a bond. Servin asked if he could save Labedoyere; the officer said he could only avenge him. Ginevra felt a surge at the thought of vengeance and recognized the officer's devotion to Napoleon.

As the sun set on the officer's face, Ginevra felt a magical connection, recalling childhood memories. Captivated by his presence, she smiled at him as she left, igniting a spark of hope.

The next day Ginevra returned to the studio. The officer, now called Luigi, told stories of the retreat from Moscow and the disaster at Waterloo. Ginevra listened, her admiration growing. They spoke Corsican, which deepened their bond.

Days passed and Ginevra's classmates noticed her absence from social gatherings, blaming her relationship with Luigi. Mademoiselle Thirion, worried by the rumors, withdrew from class, and attendance declined. Ginevra, lost in thoughts of Luigi, remained oblivious.

One day Laure, a fellow student, confided that her mother insisted she leave the studio because of the gossip around Ginevra and Luigi. Shocked, Ginevra reassured Laure they would meet again. Laure, sad to go, left, and Ginevra felt a pang of loss.

As Ginevra and Luigi's bond deepened, they shared unspoken love, each recognizing the other's worth. One evening Luigi signaled to Ginevra but, seeing Laure, retreated. Unaware of tension, Ginevra spoke with Laure and learned the others had left because of scandal.

Ginevra confronted Servin, revealing the young ladies had spread falsehoods about her relationship with Luigi. Servin dismissed the rumors, focused on his exhibition. Luigi entered, jokingly claiming responsibility for Servin's lost students; the painter playfully suggested marriage.

Their silence spoke volumes—the first acknowledgment of their love. Servin encouraged their union, celebrating the joy of love. Ginevra proposed to help Luigi secure a pardon, confident in her father's influence.

Meanwhile Ginevra's parents grew anxious about her late returns. Bartolomeo paced, concerned for his daughter. Though used to her late hours, he and his wife felt unease. Bartolomeo, a man of integrity who prioritized his family's well-being over appearances, reflected on their close-knit dynamic built on love and mutual respect. Ginevra's upbringing had instilled loyalty and passion, shared with her father and their political commitment to the Emperor.

When Ginevra finally arrived home, her father embraced her, relieved. The family shared a warm moment; their love was evident. Ginevra's heart swelled with affection for her parents, knowing they were her greatest supporters.

In the studio, Ginevra and Luigi's relationship blossomed amid challenges. Their love, forged in adversity, became a source of strength, uniting them against the world. As they navigated complexities, they remained steadfast in their commitment to one another, embodying the resilience of their shared ideals.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!” Ginevra exclaimed, but was quickly set down with respect. She assured her mother, who was startled, that it was merely a trick to escape.

The baroness regained her composure, and Piombo, rubbing his hands, showed his joy, a habit he had developed at court. The two old parents resembled a revived plant after a long drought.

“Now, to dinner!” Piombo called, offering his hand to Ginevra, who smiled in response.

“Your mother mentioned you’ve been spending more time at the studio. It seems painting is more important to you than us,” Piombo remarked.

“Oh, father!” Ginevra protested.

“Ginevra is preparing a surprise for us,” her mother suggested.

“A picture of your own! Will you show us?” Piombo asked eagerly.

“Yes, I am very busy at the studio,” Ginevra replied slowly.

“Are you alright, Ginevra? You look pale!” her mother exclaimed.

“No! It shall never be said that Ginevra Piombo acted a lie,” she declared.

Her parents exchanged astonished glances. “I love a young man,” she confessed, lowering her eyelids to hide her emotions.

“Is he a prince?” Piombo asked ironically, causing his wife to tremble.

“No, father, he is a young man without fortune,” she replied gently.

“Is he handsome?” he pressed.

“He is very unfortunate,” she said.

“What is he?” Piombo inquired.

“Labedoyere’s comrade; he was proscribed and hidden by Servin,” she explained.

“Servin is a good fellow,” Piombo acknowledged, “but you do wrong to love any man but your father.”

“It does not depend on me to love or not love,” Ginevra replied softly.

“I thought my Ginevra would remain faithful to me until I die,” Piombo lamented. “I did not expect our love to find a rival.”

“Did I ever reproach you for your devotion to Napoleon?” Ginevra countered. “You left me for months on missions. I endured your absence. Life has necessities.”

“Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed.

“You don’t love me for myself; your reproaches reveal your egotism,” she said.

“Dare you blame your father’s love?” he shouted, his eyes flashing.

“Father, I don’t blame you,” she replied gently. “You have grounds for your egotism, as I have for my love. I have never left your side for fifteen years, and it has been a pleasure to charm your life. Is it ingratitude to desire a husband who will protect me?”

“What! You reckon benefits with your father?” Piombo asked dangerously.

A dreadful silence followed. Bartolomeo finally cried out, “Oh! stay with us! I cannot bear to have you love another man. Ginevra, you will not have long to await your liberty.”

“But, father, I need not leave you; we will both love you. You will come to know the man to whom you bequeath me,” she said.

“Oh! Ginevra, why did you not marry when Napoleon offered?” Piombo lamented.

“They loved me to order,” she replied. “I did not wish to leave you alone.”

“You don’t wish to leave me alone, yet you marry! I know you, my daughter; you would cease to love us,” he said.

Elisa, the baroness, remained motionless, as if stunned. “We have no longer a daughter; she wishes to marry,” Piombo declared.

He sat down, raising his hands to heaven, bowed down by sorrow. Ginevra, touched by his restraint, expected a violent outburst but was met with gentleness.

“Father,” she said tenderly, “you shall never be abandoned by your Ginevra. Love me for my own sake. If you knew how he loves me! He would never make me unhappy!”

“Comparisons already!” Piombo shouted. “I cannot endure the thought of your marriage. If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me; if he does not love you, I would kill him.”

Ginevra alone could withstand his gaze, her own eyes aflame. “Oh! to love you! What man is worthy of such a life?” Piombo continued. “Who is worthy to be your husband?”

“He,” Ginevra replied, “he of whom I am not worthy.”

“He?” Piombo repeated. “Who is he?”

“He whom I love,” she said.

“How can he know you enough to love you?” he asked.

“Father,” she said impatiently, “whether he loves me or not, if I love him—”

“You love him?” Piombo interrupted.

Ginevra nodded softly.

“You love him more than you love us?” he pressed.

“The two feelings cannot be compared,” she replied.

“Is one stronger than the other?” he asked.

“I think it is,” she admitted.

“You shall not marry him,” Piombo declared, shaking the window panes with his voice.

“I shall marry him,” Ginevra replied calmly.

“Oh, God!” cried her mother. “How will this quarrel end? Santa Virgina! Place yourself between them!”

Piombo, now seated, wore an icy sternness. He looked at Ginevra and said gently, “No! You will not marry him. Say nothing more tonight—let me think otherwise. Do you wish to see your father on his knees, his white hairs prostrate before you? I supplicate you—”

“Ginevra Piombo does not break her word,” she replied. “I am your daughter.”

“She is right,” the baroness said. “We are sent into the world to marry.”

“Do you encourage her in disobedience?” Piombo asked, turning to his wife, who was now petrified.

“Refusing to obey an unjust order is not disobedience,” Ginevra asserted.

“No order can be unjust from your father’s lips,” Piombo replied. “Why do you judge my actions? My repugnance is counsel from on high, perhaps to protect you from great evil.”

“The only evil would be if he did not love me.”

“Always he!” Piombo exclaimed.

“Yes, always,” she affirmed. “He is my life, my good, my thought. Even if I obeyed you, he would remain in my soul. To forbid me to marry him is to make me hate you.”

“You love us not!” Piombo shouted.

“Oh!” Ginevra shook her head.

“Then forget him; be faithful to us. After we are gone—you understand?”

“Father, do you wish me to long for your death?” she asked.

“I shall outlive you. Children who do not honor their parents die early,” he warned.

“All the more reason I should marry and be happy,” she replied.

This coolness and argumentation troubled Piombo, causing blood to rush to his head. Ginevra shuddered and sprang onto his knee, embracing him. “Oh, yes, yes, let me die first! I could never survive you, my father!”

“Oh! my Ginevra, my own Ginevra!” Piombo replied, his anger melting under her affection.

“It was time you ceased,” the baroness said, trembling.

“Poor mother!” Ginevra said.

“Ah! Ginevretta! mia bella Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed, playing with her as if she were a child. They shared moments of joy, but Ginevra’s attempts to introduce Luigi were met with playful refusals from her father.

The next day, Ginevra was more affectionate toward her father, expressing gratitude as if to thank him for his apparent consent. That evening, she sang and played for him, exclaiming they needed a man’s voice for the nocturne.

A week later, her mother signaled her to come closer. “I have persuaded your father to receive him,” Elisa whispered.

“Oh! mother, how happy you have made me!” Ginevra replied.

That day, Ginevra joyfully returned home on Luigi’s arm. The officer emerged from hiding for the second time. Ginevra’s appeals to the Duc de Feltre had succeeded; Luigi’s name was restored on the officer’s list. This was a significant step forward. Warned of her father’s potential disapproval, Luigi felt anxious about meeting him. Ginevra sensed his fear, which only deepened her love.

“How pale you are!” she remarked as they reached her home.

“Oh! Ginevra, if it concerned my life only—”

Though Bartolomeo had been informed of the formal introduction, he remained seated, his stern expression intimidating.

“Father,” Ginevra said, “I bring you someone you will be pleased to see—a soldier who fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean.”

Piombo rose, casting a sidelong glance at Luigi. “Monsieur is not decorated,” he remarked sarcastically.

“I no longer wear the Legion of Honor,” Luigi replied timidly.

Ginevra, embarrassed by her father’s rudeness, pulled out a chair for Luigi. The officer’s response seemed to satisfy Piombo. Madame Piombo, noticing her husband’s demeanor softening, attempted to engage in conversation.

“Monsieur’s resemblance to someone we knew in Corsica, Nina Porta, is surprising,” she noted.

“Nothing could be more natural,” Luigi replied. “Nina was my sister.”

“Are you Luigi Porta?” Piombo asked.

“Yes,” Luigi confirmed.

Bartolomeo staggered, needing support from his wife. The two old parents left the room in silence, abandoning their daughter in a state of horror.

Luigi, bewildered, looked at Ginevra, who had turned pale, staring at the door through which her parents had exited. The solemnity of the moment filled her with fear. “What misery in a word!” she exclaimed.

“In the name of our love, what have I said?” Luigi asked.

“My father never spoke of our tragic history, and I was too young to know,” Ginevra explained.

“Are we in vendetta?” Luigi asked, trembling.

“Yes. My mother said the Portas killed my brother and burned our house. My father then massacred their family. How did you survive? You were tied to the bed before they set fire to the house.”

“I do not know,” Luigi replied. “I was taken to Genoa at six and raised by an old man named Colonna. I was told I had enemies and advised to take Luigi as my surname to evade them.”

“Go, go, Luigi!” Ginevra urged. “Stay here; you are safe in my father’s house, but once you leave, danger awaits! My father has two Corsicans in his service, and they will hunt you.”

“Ginevra,” he said, “does this vendetta exist between us?”

She smiled sadly and nodded. “Oh, Luigi, our love must be pure and sincere to give me strength for the path ahead. But it promises happiness for our lives, does it not?”

Luigi smiled and squeezed her hand. Ginevra realized that true love transcends superficial expressions. Their destiny was sealed; she foresaw a difficult struggle but felt no inclination to abandon Luigi. With determination, she pulled him from her father’s house and ensured he reached his modest lodging.

Upon returning home, Ginevra felt a calm resolve. She greeted her parents, who were preparing for dinner, with gentle eyes, noting her mother’s tear-stained face. They all rose from the table in silence, unable to speak.

In the gloomy salon, Piombo attempted to speak but faltered, his strength failing him. He rang for a servant. “Pietro, light the fire; I am cold,” he ordered.

Ginevra trembled, watching her father’s internal struggle. Both were suffering in silence, nursing their pride and anger. The love that once brought them joy now felt like a burden. Ginevra’s gaze at her father was filled with reproach, while Piombo’s eyes reflected a deep-seated pain.

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother, desperate to mend the rift, gathered them in Bartolomeo’s study. Ginevra sensed her mother’s intentions and smiled sadly.

At that moment, two notaries arrived with witnesses. Bartolomeo eyed them warily, sensing a confrontation. The older notary introduced himself and began to discuss Ginevra’s marriage to Luigi Porta.

“Monsieur le baron, we have come to fulfill the law regarding your daughter’s marriage,” he stated, but Piombo’s anger was palpable.

“Are there laws in France that destroy paternal authority?” he demanded.

“Monsieur—” the notary began.

“Which tear a daughter from her father?” Piombo pressed.

“Monsieur—”

“Which deprive an old man of his last consolation?” he continued.

“Monsieur, your daughter only belongs to you if—”

“And kill him?” Piombo shouted.

“Monsieur, permit me—”

The notary’s calm demeanor only fueled Piombo’s rage. In a sudden movement, he grabbed a dagger from the wall and rushed toward Ginevra. Witnesses intervened, but Piombo pushed them aside, his fury evident.

When Ginevra saw him approach, she knelt before him. “No, no! I cannot!” he cried, throwing the dagger away. “Have mercy! Give me Luigi! I ask for your consent upon my knees,” she pleaded, her voice trembling.

Bartolomeo pushed her away. “Go. The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here,” he said, pressing his heart. “Leave my house, unhappy girl,” he added, ushering her out.

“Luigi!” Ginevra cried, entering her lover’s humble abode. “My Luigi, we have no fortune but our love.”

“Then I am richer than kings!” he exclaimed.

“My father and mother have cast me off,” she said sadly.

“I will love you in their place,” he promised.

“Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!” she declared, her voice filled with a mix of joy and dread.

Madame Servin received her coldly, resentful of the trouble Ginevra had caused her husband. Proud yet shocked by this selfishness, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her, painting a bright future that lifted her spirits despite her father's harshness.

This period marked the happiest time of their lives, filled with love and labor. They enjoyed evenings together, with Ginevra always smiling and never complaining. To commemorate an anniversary, Ginevra painted a portrait of herself for Luigi, capturing not just her likeness but the essence of their love. The masterpiece symbolized their wedded bliss, and another year passed in contentment. No significant events disrupted their happiness; they were simply happy.

At the beginning of 1819, picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce original works instead of copies as competition intensified. Madame Porta regretted not taking up genre painting earlier, which might have improved her standing. Ginevra tried portraiture but faced stiff competition from artists in greater need. Despite modest savings, she and Luigi grew anxious about their future.

As winter went on, Luigi labored relentlessly against a shrinking market for writing. Unable to afford help, he worked longer hours for the same pay. Ginevra painted several commendable works, but dealers avoided lesser-known artists, and even low prices failed to sell her paintings.

Their household situation deteriorated: love and happiness were overshadowed by looming poverty. They concealed worries from each other—Ginevra lavished affection while Luigi hid his fears—clinging to each other and the hope that love would sustain them.

One night Ginevra woke to find Luigi gone and discovered him working late, a habit to avoid burdening her. Moved, she decided to help by coloring lithographs, laboring through the night while Luigi toiled in his garret. Both pretended to sleep to shield the other from their anxieties.

On another night, feverish and exhausted, Luigi opened his window and saw Ginevra's lamp across the courtyard, realizing she too was working. When he confronted her, she said she could not be idle while he suffered. They shared a tender moment, watching the dawn together and feeling hopeful.

Their struggles deepened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. The joy of parenthood briefly masked their financial burdens, but soon they began selling possessions and jewels. Poverty stripped them of comfort and pride, escalating their desperation.

Months later, Ginevra, frail and worn, struggled to care for her sickly child. Luigi, feeling helpless, sought aid but found none; he sold Ginevra's paintings and remaining furniture for a meager sum, which only prolonged their hardship. Ginevra showed remarkable strength, managing household duties and caring for the boy while often starving herself to provide for Luigi.

One evening she offered Luigi her last piece of bread and urged him to eat. Realizing her sacrifice, he was consumed by despair and left to find help. In desperation he decided to sell himself as a military substitute, hoping the money would save Ginevra and their child.

After securing an advance, he hurried home, only to find Ginevra asleep with their child. When he roused her, she smiled, then saw Bartolomeo cold and lifeless. Luigi, horrified, sought aid, but it was too late. In delirium, Ginevra spoke her love for Luigi and sorrow over their lost child. She died in his arms, leaving him devastated.

As Luigi mourned, Ginevra's parents sat at home reflecting on their daughter, whom they had not seen in years. Memories surged as they noticed the empty chair that had once belonged to her. Elisa Piombo, Ginevra's mother, spoke of her hoping to rekindle Bartolomeo's love for Ginevra. Just as Bartolomeo seemed to soften, a man arrived and revealed the tragic fate of Ginevra and Luigi.

The old couple was shattered, realizing their daughter was gone; Bartolomeo's heart was broken by the loss of his beloved child. The cycle of suffering and retribution had claimed another victim, leaving only memories and grief.
