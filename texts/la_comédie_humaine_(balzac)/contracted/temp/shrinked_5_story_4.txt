In October 1800 Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries with his wife and daughter, weighed down by hardship. His wife, distressed, tried to soothe their weary child while Bartolomeo, desperate, handed her a dagger intending to confront the Bonapartes. A soldier stopped him at the palace; the tense standoff ended when Lucien Bonaparte recognized him and brought him to Napoleon. Bartolomeo confessed he had killed the Portas, who was responsible for his son’s death. Napoleon, surprised, probed his motives; Bartolomeo lamented the Bonaparte brothers’ failure to support him. Napoleon offered him refuge in Paris, away from vendetta culture; Bartolomeo accepted and pledged his loyalty to the Bonaparte family.

Fifteen years later Bartolomeo’s life in Paris reflected the same strands of vengeance and allegiance. The painter Servin had opened a reputable studio for young girls of wealthy families, specializing in feminine art education. Among his pupils was Ginevra Piombo: beautiful, modest, and coldly disregarded by royalist classmates. Her indifference to their slights provoked particular enmity from Amelie, who sought to humiliate her.

Ginevra’s curiosity was piqued when she sensed a hidden man behind a partition. Despite Amelie’s attempts to embarrass her, Ginevra remained focused on her work. Madame Servin’s arrival interrupted the brewing conflict; Ginevra and the teacher exchanged glances, both aware of the concealed presence. Ginevra sketched the officer’s head, earning Servin’s praise and intensifying the girls’ tension. Later Ginevra confided in Servin about the hidden officer and vowed to protect him.

In a dim garret, Servin and Ginevra met the young officer at the center of the secret: Luigi. He recoiled on seeing Ginevra, confessing despair over Labedoyere’s death sentence and his own cowardice. Ginevra, moved, offered her savings; the act inspired hope and quickly deepened their bond. They developed a clandestine communication using Corsican songs, but gossip about their relationship spread, forcing them to leave the studio.

Ginevra learned from her friend Laure that the other girls had departed because of rumors. Shocked, she sought reassurance from Servin, who dismissed the whispers; Luigi, overcome with guilt, overheard. Their unspoken love became unmistakable, and Ginevra proposed using her father’s influence to seek Luigi a pardon. Meanwhile her parents grew anxious over her late returns; Bartolomeo remained a stern yet loving father. The chapter follows loyalty, love, and societal judgment through the entwined lives of Ginevra, Luigi, and their families.

“Here she is, my Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed, embracing his daughter and chiding her long hours in the studio as if painting had become more important than family. Ginevra shocked him by confessing, “I love a young man,” naming Luigi Porta, a comrade of the proscribed Labedoyere. Piombo insisted she should love no one but him; Ginevra replied that love was not a choice.

Piombo lamented her supposed lack of faithfulness; she reminded him of his own long absences on political missions. As his anger flared at the thought of losing her, Ginevra promised her love would not diminish her affection for him and urged her father to learn to know the man she loved. Piombo declared he would never allow the marriage. Ginevra stood firm: “I am Ginevra Piombo, and I declare that within six months I shall be the wife of Luigi Porta.” Piombo, furious, warned her of the Porta family’s responsibility for her brother’s death; Ginevra argued Luigi’s innocence, noting he had been a child during the vendetta.

Despite threats, she remained resolute. Piombo nearly struck her but hesitated. The next day she was barred from leaving the house; she and Luigi communicated through a chambermaid, deepening their attachment.

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother tried to mediate, but the arrival of two notaries to discuss her marriage escalated tensions. Ginevra knelt before her father, pleading, “Give me Luigi, or I die!” Overwhelmed, Piombo rejected her and said, “You have no father,” effectively casting her out.

Ginevra found Luigi in his lodging and declared, “My father and my mother have cast me off.” Luigi vowed to love her as her parents could not, and she resolved to seek happiness with him: “Then let us be happy.” The day after her expulsion she sought help from Madame Servin, who coldly refused. Hurt but proud, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi. He devoted himself to her, painting a hopeful future.

One morning Ginevra received trunks from her mother filled with essentials and a letter urging her to reconsider. For a moment she considered returning, but Luigi’s arrival renewed her resolve to stay. Their wedding was spare and grave, with no friends present; after a brief, cold ceremony they left feeling the weight of their families’ absence. In their new home, Ginevra delighted in Luigi’s thoughtful arrangements, including a studio for her art. Their early days were passionate; reality soon arrived and they began to work to sustain themselves.

This period was the happiest of their marriage, full of love and shared labor. Ginevra felt occasional sorrow imagining her parents’ loneliness, but she commemorated their first anniversary with a self-portrait for Luigi, symbolizing their happiness and commitment. Another year passed in blissful harmony, yet the future remained uncertain.

In early 1819 picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce original works amid rising competition. Despite some savings, their prospects dimmed. As winter progressed, Luigi worked tirelessly amid declining writing fees, while Ginevra painted commendable pieces that largely failed to sell. Their finances worsened, though they concealed anxieties with affection. One night Ginevra found Luigi working late and took to coloring lithographs to help; they pretended to sleep then rose again to toil, driven by love and desperation.

Their situation deteriorated further when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. Joy was short-lived as money ran out; they sold possessions and jewels. One evening Ginevra saved a scrap of bread for Luigi, revealing she was starving. In despair he resolved to sell himself as a military substitute for money. Rushing home with that plan, he found Ginevra in utter despair, holding their lifeless child. Despite his pleas, Ginevra succumbed to exhaustion and died in his arms, leaving him devastated.

Ginevra’s parents were later haunted by memories of her. When Luigi, broken, appeared at their door and revealed the tragedy, it shattered them; they were overwhelmed by grief over their lost and estranged daughter.
