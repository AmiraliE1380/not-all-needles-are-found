The Comte de Fontaine, a royalist from Poitou, supported the Bourbon cause during the French Revolution but was ruined when his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet, of a noble but impoverished family, and moved to Paris with his large household to seek royal favor. Promoted to brigadier-general, he soon felt overlooked by the monarchy.

Disillusioned by former comrades pursuing wealth, he considered returning home but renewed his loyalty after March 20, 1815, hoping for better fortune. During Louis XVIII’s brief exile he served faithfully, securing positions that advanced his sons in civil and military service and improving the family's standing.

Despite this improved status, the Comte struggled to arrange marriages for his daughters, especially the youngest, Emilie. While her elder sisters married well, Emilie—spoiled, proud, and used to Parisian luxury—set impossibly high standards. Admired for her beauty, she remained indifferent to suitors, dismissing men for trivial faults. Her upbringing, in part theatrical and affectatious—she played the role of Célimène—fostered a sense of superiority that alienated many potential husbands. As the political climate shifted and the king’s health declined, the Comte intensified efforts to find a match, but Emilie’s unreasonable expectations thwarted him.

At a social event Emilie met Maximilien Longueville; his elegance intrigued her. Her uncle invited him to dinner, where he impressed the family but left abruptly because his sister was ill. He visited more often, maintaining a mysterious air that deepened Emilie’s affection. With her uncle’s help she arranged a dance to meet his sister, Clara, and a competitive bond formed between the two women.

As autumn’s festivities continued, Emilie and Longueville grew closer, yet the Comte worried about Maximilien’s obscure background. Emilie dismissed these concerns, believing love transcended rank. Her idealized image of him collapsed when she found him working in a shop—an encounter that devastated her. Though she tried to move on, she remained drawn to him, especially after hints of reciprocal feeling surfaced. Their final exchange before his departure for Italy was full of unspoken emotion, leaving both heartbroken.

Months later Emilie married the Comte de Kergarouet but felt regret. Years afterward she encountered Maximilien again and realized she still harbored feelings, haunted by memories of their brief but profound attachment.
