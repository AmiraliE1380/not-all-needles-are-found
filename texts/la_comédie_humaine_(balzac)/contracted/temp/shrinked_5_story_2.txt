The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou, had served the Bourbon cause during the La Vendée conflict. Despite noble lineage and past sacrifices, he faced financial ruin after his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet, a woman of noble birth but no fortune. When the second revolution occurred, he moved to Paris with his large family, hoping for better prospects. Initially disillusioned by the new regime, he received unexpected honors, including a military promotion and royal decorations, which rekindled hopes for royal favor.

He soon realized, however, that his sacrifices went largely unrecognized, and he felt marginalized as new aristocrats gained prominence. Disheartened, he contemplated returning to his estate but was drawn back into royal service during the tumult of 1815. His loyalty was rewarded when he became a trusted advisor to Louis XVIII, who valued his wit and political acumen. Consequently the Fontaine family began to prosper: his sons secured significant positions and his daughters made good marriages, though to men of lesser nobility.

Emilie, the youngest daughter, was especially spoiled and became the center of attention in Parisian society. Her beauty and charm made her a sought-after match, yet she remained indifferent to suitors, asserting superiority over those of lower birth. Despite her father's efforts to arrange advantageous marriages, Emilie’s high standards and capricious nature provoked family conflict. The Count’s attempts to secure a suitable husband were met with frustration as she dismissed potential matches for trivial flaws.

As the political landscape shifted and the King’s health declined, the Comte intensified his efforts to find Emilie a husband, hoping to solidify the family’s status. But her unreasonable expectations and disdain complicated matters, causing a string of rejections that left him increasingly desperate. Family dynamics grew strained: Emilie’s arrogance bred friction among siblings and their spouses, who were also navigating the complexities of newfound social standing.

Through these tensions the Comte remained hopeful for royal favor, convinced that a suitable match for Emilie would validate his sacrifices and restore the family’s honor. Yet as winter’s social season progressed, Emilie’s dismissive attitude toward potential suitors continued to frustrate his ambitions, leaving the Fontaine family’s future uncertain.

Nature had endowed Emilie de Fontaine with all the qualities necessary to embody the role of Célimène. Tall and slender, she could adopt a dignified or playful demeanor at will. Her long neck allowed her to express scorn and impertinence; her fine black hair and arched eyebrows gave her an air of pride. Emilie mastered feminine gestures that could convey contempt or charm. When she sought to win a heart, her melodious voice captivated; she could also use it sharply to silence indiscretion. Her alabaster complexion mirrored a tranquil lake, disturbed only by fleeting emotions. Many young men, scorned by her, accused her of playing a part, while she justified herself by inspiring desire only to submit it to her whims.

Monsieur de Fontaine realized too late that his daughter's upbringing had been spoiled by family adoration. The admiration that surrounded Emilie had inflated her pride and self-confidence, fostering a selfishness typical of pampered children. While her youthful charms masked these flaws, her father recognized them and tried to impart wisdom about life. His efforts were often met with her capriciousness and ironic shrewdness; his gentle advice seemed to slide off her heart like water off marble. It took time for him to see that her affection was often condescending, like a child demanding attention before returning to play.

Emilie sometimes expressed fondness for her parents but often withdrew, jealous of the love they shared with others, including her siblings. She blamed solitude on fate, failing to see that happiness comes from within. Determined to avoid her sisters’ marriages, she nevertheless felt pangs of envy at their happiness. Her unpredictable behavior led her mother to suspect she might be a little mad.

Such pride is common among beautiful young women of affluent families, who often feel misunderstood by their mothers and suspect maternal envy. This leads to secret tears and revolts against perceived tyranny and to elaborate life plans imagining a perfect partner who must meet specific criteria. With experience, many find happiness in unexpected places, learning that ideals may not match reality.

Emilie had stringent criteria for a suitor: he must be a peer of France, a soldier, and possess an Order, so they could be treated with respect. Additionally, he had to be slender; she deemed corpulence a flaw in men and believed only the slim could be romantic partners. Her disdain for those who failed her standards was clear, and Monsieur de Fontaine feared her eccentricities would lead to ridicule and that her prolonged indecision in the marriage market could have consequences.

As the winter of Charles X's reign progressed, Monsieur de Fontaine intensified efforts to find suitable matches, hosting gatherings to attract potential suitors. His reputation as a corruptor of legislative integrity grew, yet he believed his intentions honorable. After a season of matchmaking he felt hopeful Emilie would choose a husband; he soon discovered her whims and standards were unyielding.

One morning he summoned her to discuss her future. As he arranged his study to present a dignified front, Emilie greeted him with a light-hearted kiss that lacked genuine warmth. Monsieur de Fontaine broached the subject of marriage prospects, emphasizing the importance of a wise choice for her happiness.

Emilie interrupted, reminding him of their previous agreement about suitors. He urged her to take the matter seriously, explaining that the family had worked hard to secure matches. She dismissed his concerns, mocking the notion of settling for anything less than a peer of France, finding fault with the suitors he suggested for appearance or background.

Exasperated, Monsieur de Fontaine declared he had fulfilled his duty and the choice was now hers. He warned that happiness in marriage relies on mutual respect rather than mere wealth or status. Emilie, touched by her father's sincerity, feigned affection and coaxed him, yet her pride remained intact.

At a family gathering she reiterated her refusal to consider marriage, insisting she would only marry a peer. Her uncle jokingly suggested she was waiting for the Duc de Bordeaux to come of age, drawing laughter, but Emilie remained resolute against entertaining marriage talks.

As summer approached, the family retreated to their country house, where Emilie expressed a desire to attend the Bal de Sceaux, attracted by the anonymity of the crowd. The ball was known for its lively atmosphere and its mix of social classes.

At the ball, Emilie was surprised to find members of the upper class among the attendees. Observing the festivities, she noticed a striking young man who captivated her: apart from the crowd, exuding elegance and dignity. His appearance and demeanor sparked her imagination and she felt an instant attraction.

Determined to learn more, Emilie approached him, but he remained aloof. She attempted to draw his attention through exaggerated gestures and laughter, but he seemed indifferent. Frustrated, she followed his gaze and saw he was watching another dancer, a pale girl named Clara.

Intrigued, Emilie decided to follow the pair as they left the ball. The young man, attentive to Clara, still caught Emilie’s eye as they departed. She felt jealousy and curiosity, eager to uncover his identity.

Days later, while riding with her uncle, Emilie met Lady Dudley and Monsieur Vandenesse and realized the young man she admired was not who she had hoped. Disappointed, she tried to hide her feelings but found herself drawn to him nonetheless.

Her uncle, noticing her agitation, speculated about her feelings. Emilie insisted he must be of noble birth, and her uncle promised to introduce them. This revelation intensified her interest and she began to fantasize about a future with him.

As days passed, Emilie's infatuation grew, fueled by imagination and the allure of romance. She remained unaware of the complexities and challenges that love and marriage would bring, still blinded by youthful ideals.

The next morning, before Emilie awoke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. Recognizing the young man he had insulted the previous day, he approached with the politeness of the old court.

“Who would have guessed I’d encounter the son or grandson of one of my best friends at seventy-three? I’m a vice-admiral, and I think no more of dueling than smoking a cigar. In my day, young men had to see the color of their blood before becoming friends! I had a bit too much grog last night and ran you down. Let’s shake hands; I’d rather take a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than cause his family any regret.”

Despite his cold demeanor, the young man couldn’t resist the Count’s cordiality and shook his hand.

“You were going out riding,” the Count noted. “Don’t let me detain you, but I hope you’ll join us for dinner at the Villa Planat. My nephew, the Comte de Fontaine, is someone you should meet. I’ll make it up to you by introducing you to five of the prettiest women in Paris. Your brow is clearing! I enjoy seeing young people happy; it reminds me of my youth when adventures and duels were common. Nowadays, you worry over everything as if there were no history.”

“But aren’t we right to think this way? The sixteenth century gave Europe religious liberty, and the nineteenth will bring political liberty—”

“Let’s not discuss politics. I’m an old woman—ultra, you see. But I don’t stop young men from being revolutionary, as long as they let the King disperse their assemblies.”

As they rode deeper into the woods, the Count pointed out a slender birch sapling, pulled out a pistol, and shot a bullet into the tree from fifteen paces away.

“You see, I’m not afraid of a duel,” he said with comical gravity.

“Nor am I,” replied the young man, promptly aiming his pistol and shooting close to the Count’s bullet.

“That’s what I call a well-educated man!” the admiral exclaimed enthusiastically.

Throughout the ride the Count took every opportunity to quiz the young man on the trifles his code deemed essential to a gentleman.

“Do you have any debts?” he finally asked.

“No, monsieur.”

“What, you pay for everything?”

“Punctually; otherwise, we’d lose our credit and respect.”

“But you must have more than one mistress? Ah, you blush! Manners have changed. All these notions of lawful order and liberty have spoiled young men. You have no Guimard or Duthe now, and you know nothing of heraldry. My dear young friend, you’re not fully fledged. The man who doesn’t sow his wild oats in spring will do so in winter. If I have eighty thousand francs a year at seventy, it’s because I spent my capital at thirty—honorably, of course. However, your imperfections won’t stop me from introducing you at the Pavillon Planat. Remember, you promised to come.”

“What an odd little old man!” Longueville thought. “He’s jolly and hale, but while he wants to seem friendly, I won’t trust him too far.”

The next day, around four o’clock, when the house party gathered in the drawing-rooms and billiard-room, a servant announced, “Monsieur de Longueville.” Upon hearing the name of the old admiral’s protégé, everyone rushed in, eager to study Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s expression and judge this remarkable young man who had earned honorable mention over many rivals.

Dressed simply yet elegantly, with an air of ease and polite manners, Longueville won the family’s goodwill. He seemed accustomed to the luxury of the Receiver-General’s mansion. His conversation showed a man of the world who had enjoyed a brilliant education. When the old admiral raised the trivial topic of naval architecture, he answered aptly, prompting one lady to remark that he must have attended the Ecole Polytechnique.

“I think, madame,” he replied, “that I may regard it as an honor to have been accepted.”

Despite persistent invitations, he politely declined dinner, explaining he was the Hippocrates of his young sister, whose delicate health required great care.

“Monsieur is perhaps a medical man?” one of Emilie’s sisters-in-law asked ironically.

“Monsieur has left the Ecole Polytechnique,” Mademoiselle de Fontaine interjected, her face flushing as she learned that the young lady at the ball was Longueville’s sister.

“But, my dear, he may be a doctor and still have attended the Ecole Polytechnique—correct, monsieur?”

“There’s nothing to prevent it, madame,” he replied.

All eyes turned to Emilie, who gazed with uneasy curiosity at the captivating stranger. She breathed more easily when he added with a smile, “I do not belong to the medical profession; I even gave up going into the Engineers to preserve my independence.”

“And you did well,” the Count said.

Longueville made his visit neither too long nor too short, leaving when he saw he had pleased everyone and piqued their curiosity.

“He’s a cunning rascal!” the Count remarked upon returning to the drawing-room after seeing him out.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine, who had dressed carefully to attract Longueville’s attention, felt disappointed when he didn’t give her the attention she thought she deserved. The family was surprised by her sudden silence; Emilie usually displayed all her charms for newcomers, but now she seemed simple and natural, perhaps more beautiful. Some family members interpreted her behavior as a refined strategy to dazzle him later.

During dinner, as everyone speculated about Longueville’s qualities, Mademoiselle de Fontaine remained silent. A sarcastic remark from her uncle roused her from her apathy, and she quipped that such heavenly perfection must conceal a great defect, adding that she would be cautious in judging such a gifted man at first sight.

“Those who please everybody please nobody,” she concluded, “and the worst fault is to have none.”

Like all girls in love, Emilie hoped to hide her feelings, but after a fortnight every family member was aware of her secret. When Longueville called for the third time, Emilie believed it was mainly for her sake, which thrilled her but also stung her pride. Accustomed to being the center of attention, she now recognized a force outside herself that attracted her, and despite attempts to resist, she could not shake the image of the young man from her heart.

Anxiety crept in as two of Longueville’s qualities—unexpected modesty and discretion—conflicted with her curiosity. He never spoke of himself or his family, skillfully evading her hints and traps for information. If she discussed painting, he answered as a connoisseur; if she played music, he proved a good pianist. One evening he charmed the party by singing a duet with Emilie, but when they tried to discover if he was a professional singer, he preserved the mystery surrounding his social status.

Distracted by his reserve, Emilie hoped to extract more information from Clara, aided by her uncle’s maneuvers. The family expressed a strong desire to meet Clara Longueville, proposing an informal dance to entertain her. Despite the clouds of suspicion and curiosity, joy illuminated Emilie’s soul as she felt intimately connected to someone outside herself. She began to understand life’s relationships; whether happiness made her better or she was simply too occupied to torment others, she became gentler and more indulgent.

This change enchanted her family, suggesting her selfishness was transforming into love. She eagerly awaited the arrival of her bashful admirer, and though they hadn’t spoken of passion, she sensed his affection. She led him to reveal varied information about himself, realizing he was also studying her and striving to improve her education.

The day of the dance arrived, with Clara and her brother as principal guests. For the first time, Emilie felt pleasure in another young girl’s triumph, lavishing Clara with sincere attention. However, Clara proved more adept at conversation, keeping the discussion away from personal interests while charming Emilie, who felt envious and judged by her.

“Mademoiselle,” Clara said, “I’ve heard so much about you from Maximilien that I wanted to know you out of affection for him; but isn’t wanting to know you a wish to love you?”

“My dear Clara, I feared I might have displeased you by speaking of those not of noble birth.”

“Oh, be at ease. Such discussions are pointless these days. It doesn’t affect me; I’m beside the question.”

Ambitious as her answer seemed, it filled Emilie with joy; she interpreted it to align with her wishes. She danced with renewed spirit, watching Longueville, whose grace surpassed her ideal. She felt satisfaction believing him to be well-born, and her eyes sparkled as she danced, feeling a connection with him.

As autumn arrived, the couple surrendered to the tide of sweet sentiment, strengthening their bond through countless little incidents. They studied each other, as lovers do, and enjoyed aimless conversations that concealed deeper feelings. They admired sunsets together, gathered daisies, and sang passionate duets, expressing their secrets through music. Each small shared pleasure—the casual talk, the music, the walks—deepened their intimacy and confirmed the affection each had been careful to conceal.
