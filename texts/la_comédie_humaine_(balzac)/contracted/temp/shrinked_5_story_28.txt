PIERRE GRASSOU

Have you felt unease and sadness in the crowded galleries since the revolution of 1830? The true Salon has vanished, overwhelmed by a multitude of artists. Once the Salon displayed only the finest works, honoring a chosen few and provoking passionate public debate; criticism even helped make names like Delacroix and Ingres. Now the excitement has faded: the public is numbed by sheer volume, exhibitions close quickly, and individual works lose impact. Before 1817 only a select number of paintings were shown; now galleries overflow, diluting quality and attention.

The Salon’s essence is lost in this chaos. Instead of refined selection we face a tumultuous bazaar where great artists are overshadowed. Decamps’ notable works, for example, would have stood out more among fewer high-quality pieces than they do amid mediocrity. Ironically, despite more artists, talk of unrecognized genius has increased. When the Academy’s selection was strict, true talent was acknowledged; without that judgment, art suffers and the Salon risks perishing.

Among the swelling catalogue of names stands Pierre Grassou, known as “Fougeres” to his acquaintances. In 1832 he lived in a cramped rue de Navarin apartment with a studio overlooking Montmartre. Modest and tidy, his studio reflected his careful nature and limited means. As a genre painter he avoided the burdens of historical subjects, concentrating on easel painting.

One December morning Grassou, preparing to work, was visited by Elie Magus, a picture dealer. Magus bought Grassou’s paintings cheaply and proposed a business arrangement: Grassou would paint portraits of the bourgeois Vervelle family, eager to enter the art world. Their daughter had a substantial dowry, and Magus hinted that marriage could benefit Grassou financially.

Grassou, living simply and solitary, was startled by talk of marriage. The idea of painting three family portraits, however, intrigued him as a chance to establish himself as a portraitist. Magus left to find the Vervelles, leaving Grassou to consider the opportunity’s potential.

To understand Grassou’s situation we must look back. He studied under various masters—Servin and Schinner among them—but never fully grasped their techniques. His education was marked by a lack of recognition; he was often mocked for mediocrity. Gentle and diligent, he nevertheless failed to stand out among flashier or more eccentric peers.

His first Salon attempt, a village-wedding scene in 1819, was rejected. Rather than despair, he sought Schinner’s critique, who bluntly advised him to give up painting. Still determined to improve, Grassou labored to refine the rejected piece, only to sell it to Magus for a meager sum. That sale began a long, difficult career characterized by perseverance against discouragement.

As Grassou navigated his art life he remained committed to his craft, embodying the struggles and hopes of many contemporaries. His story reflects broader themes of ambition, recognition, and the harsh realities of the art world, where circumstance often overshadows talent.

For several days Pierre Fougeres lingered near Elie's shop, hoping his painting would attract attention, but it went unnoticed. After a week the painting vanished; when he casually approached the dealer, Elie Magus told him he had sold it and offered to buy more if Fougeres produced Flemish interiors, anatomy lessons, and landscapes. Overjoyed, Fougeres felt validated, believing others appreciated his art.

He missed the irony in Elie's manner that a more cynical artist might have seen. He met his friend Joseph Bridau but was too consumed with painting to enjoy the evening. Back home he sketched furiously, inspired by newfound confidence, bought supplies, hired models, and devoted himself to his craft.

After two months he produced four paintings. Schinner and Bridau criticized them as derivative and suggested he try literature; undeterred, Fougeres retouched the canvases and sold them to Elie for twenty-five francs each, barely breaking even. He noticed a strange mist over his paintings in the shop but dismissed it as imagination.

Over seven years he honed his skills, producing competent but not exceptional work. Elie sold the paintings, letting Fougeres earn a modest living. At the 1829 Exhibition, with friends' help, he entered a powerful piece—a young man in prison inspired by Gerard Douw—which drew significant attention, even from Charles X. Madame purchased the painting and commissioned more work.

Fougeres' success came from his ability to imitate rather than innovate, a lesson he learned well. Despite criticism, his modesty and perseverance won him supporters, and he gained a reputation for dedication. With his earnings he furnished a studio and completed commissions punctually, a rarity among artists.

By thirty-seven he had produced around two hundred paintings, achieving a style that appealed to the bourgeoisie. His friends preferred his character to his art and often regretted his career choice. Nevertheless, he gave sound advice and maintained a keen appreciation for beauty.

After the July Revolution he continued submitting work to the Salon, living frugally and managing finances with his notary Cardot. He dreamed of a comfortable life as an artist, aspiring to join the Institute and gain recognition.

One day the wealthy Vervelle family visited his studio for portraits. The father, once a bottle-dealer, was impressed by Fougeres' Legion of Honor cross. The family admired his work, and Pierre was charmed by their daughter Virginie. On subsequent sittings the Vervelles grew familiar, discussing their art collection and financial stability.

During sessions Pierre flattered the family, endearing himself to them. The father boasted of their valuable collection, while the notary praised Fougeres' character, stressing his financial prudence and ability to provide for a family. This reputation strengthened the Vervelles' interest, and they began to see him as a suitable match for Virginie.

As sittings progressed Pierre's feelings for Virginie deepened and he began to envision a future with her, despite initially focusing on his art. The Vervelles' admiration and talk of his success further fueled his aspirations, intertwining personal hopes with professional ambitions.

The day Fougeres began painting Mademoiselle Virginie, he was effectively part of the Vervelle family. The Vervelles were drawn to the clean, artistic studio, which had become a second home. Toward the end of the sitting, the door burst open and Joseph Bridau entered like a whirlwind, his disheveled appearance startling the Vervelles. He quickly scanned the studio and approached Grassou, struggling to button his coat.

"Wood is dear," he remarked, then asked for five hundred francs to fend off creditors. Grassou agreed to write a note, while Bridau criticized his painting style, urging him to embrace nature's imperfections.

As Bridau left, Madame Vervelle fretted about his effect on Virginie's portrait. Grassou reassured her that Bridau had only improved it. Intrigued by the artist's genius, the Vervelles invited Fougeres to their country house the following Sunday, hoping to impress him with their hospitality and paintings.

Flattered, Grassou traveled to Ville d'Avray, where he was warmly received. They proudly showed their well-kept grounds and prepared a lavish dinner, treating him like a celebrated artist. After the meal, they led him to their picture gallery, eager for his opinion on a collection they believed rivaled King Louis-Philippe's.

As Grassou examined the paintings, he was shocked to recognize many as his own works, long sold to the dealer Elie Magus. Vervelle boasted the collection was worth three hundred thousand francs. Grassou, aware of the irony, whispered that he had sold those paintings for far less.

Vervelle, impressed, promised to double his daughter's dowry if Grassou could prove his claim. Grassou's revelation enhanced his standing with the family, who continued to regard him as a great artist. When he completed their portraits, he gifted them, further solidifying their bond.

Today Pierre Grassou is recognized as a fine portrait painter, earning around twenty thousand francs a year. He lives comfortably with his wife, who brought a six thousand franc dowry, and they enjoy a happy life. Grassou remains within the bourgeois circle as a leading artist, painting family portraits for five hundred francs each. His reputation is bolstered by his financial success, and he has even been honored with the Legion of Honor for his conduct during civil unrest.

Despite these achievements, Grassou harbors insecurities, convinced other artists mock his work and that critics overlook him. Nevertheless he continues to strive for recognition, aiming for the Academy while secretly replacing his poorer works with those of more celebrated artists in the Ville d'Avray collection. His mediocrity is tempered by kindness and generosity, making him a complex figure in the artistic community.
