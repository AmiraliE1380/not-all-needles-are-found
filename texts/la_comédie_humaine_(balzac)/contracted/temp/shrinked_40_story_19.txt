Bianchon, celebrated for theoretical physiology and early recognition at the Paris medical school, began as a surgeon before practicing medicine. His teacher Desplein, one of France’s greatest surgeons, shone with a unique, incommunicable method yet left no school. Like many geniuses, Desplein’s brilliance was immediate and personal rather than systematic; he possessed a godlike eye and an intuition—whether innate or acquired—that let him read patients and disease, choose the exact moment to operate, and account for temperament and circumstance. He seemed to perceive the human frame through time, attending to present needs. But he did not found a lasting scientific tradition; everything in him was intensely individual, and his egoism limited his posthumous fame. His glory, heroic in action, proved transient.

Desplein’s genius shaped unorthodox beliefs. He imagined the atmosphere as a generative envelope and the earth as an egg in a shell; unable to decide which came first, he rejected both antecedent animals and any surviving spirit. From dissecting the living and the dead, he found no evidence of an individual soul; observing cerebral and nervous centers and what he called a center for blood aeration, and detecting two “souls” in man, he embraced a confident atheism, dying impenitent as many great intellects do.

His greatness was shadowed by contradictions his enemies called meannesses. Envious critics exploit inconsistencies, but even a man whose deeds vindicate his methods can be misunderstood. Desplein’s moods invited attack: sometimes elegant, sometimes indifferent; rough at times, kind at others. He could display ostentatious covetousness yet offer his whole fortune to exiled patrons, provoke conflicting judgments by carrying a prayer-book at court while privately mocking religion. After studying humanity in every light, he felt profound contempt for men, seeing their true nature in both lofty and base acts.

Genius often federates talent; great insight brings moral perception—one who sees a flower sees the sun. Desplein’s repartee and precise medical judgment made his claims credible to pupils, who accepted his pretensions and even imagined he might have shone in politics. Balzac notes one of Desplein’s puzzles and resolves it later, vindicating him against foolish charges.

Of all Desplein’s pupils, Horace Bianchon was most devoted. Before becoming house surgeon at the Hôtel-Dieu, Bianchon had been a poor medical student lodging at the Maison Vauquer in the Latin Quarter. Poverty, Balzac suggests, refines talent: tested by passions, such men acquire honesty and the steady labor needed to survive life’s battles. Horace was upright, incapable of dishonor, direct, ready to stake his cloak for a friend, lend his time and nights, and expect nothing in return while trusting time to repay him. Friends respected his modest virtue and feared his censure. Neither pedant nor preacher, he could swear gracefully, give counsel, and enjoy good company. Frank, open, and content, he was loyal—the Pylades to many Oresteses—undaunted by creditors and soberly active.

His fortune changed when Desplein noticed both his virtues and faults. Under a leading clinician, doors open: Desplein made Bianchon his assistant in affluent houses where the student received fees and glimpses of Parisian life’s mysteries. He kept him at consultations, occupied him with cases, and sometimes sent him with rich patients to spas—effectively building a practice. The tyrant of surgery thus gained a devoted ally. The two men—one at the summit of honor, the other humble and poor—became intimate. Desplein confided in Bianchon, who knew which women had sat near the master, who had slept on his famous surgical couch, and the temperament—part lion, part bull—that swelled until causing the man’s death by heart degeneration. Bianchon observed schemes of sordid avarice and the politician behind the man of science; he foresaw the mortifications awaiting the solitary sentiment hidden in a steeled heart.

One episode shows Desplein’s complex character. Bianchon mentioned a poor water-carrier from Saint-Jacques with a horrible disease brought on by fatigue and want; the man had eaten only potatoes all winter of 1821. Desplein left his rounds, risking his horse to rush with Bianchon to remove the man to a sick house founded by Dubois in the Faubourg Saint-Denis. After curing him, Desplein gave the man money to buy a horse and barrel. The Auvergnat later returned to bring a sick friend, saying, “I could not have borne to let him go to any one else!” Desplein clasped his hand and said, “Bring them all to me.” Bianchon noted his chief’s predilection for Auvergnats and water-carriers, but since Desplein prided himself on cures at the Hôtel-Dieu, the pupil found nothing odd.

The strangest puzzle involved Desplein’s religious behavior. One morning, Bianchon saw Desplein slip into Saint-Sulpice and was astonished to find the great atheist kneeling seriously in the Lady Chapel, remaining through mass and giving alms. Later at dinner Desplein railed against the mass as a papal invention that had cost Christendom more blood than wars and leeching, recounting its violent controversies with Voltairean satire. Bianchon was puzzled: had he misread the scene?

Three months passed. A colleague asked Desplein about Saint-Sulpice; he said he’d seen a priest with a diseased knee recommended by the Duchesse d'Angoulême. Unsatisfied, Bianchon vowed to watch the next year. Though no longer house surgeon, he saw Desplein again, creeping along Saint-Sulpice’s wall to attend mass at the Virgin’s altar. The sacristan informed him, “For twenty years that I have been here, M. Desplein has come four times a year to attend this mass. He founded it.” The paradox—how an outspoken atheist could establish and regularly attend a mass—remained as startling to Bianchon as any theological puzzle, closing Balzac’s portrait of a man whose contradictions made him both great and inscrutable.

Some time passed before Doctor Bianchon, though intimate with Desplein, could find an hour to ask about a private episode in the surgeon’s life. Meetings in consultations or society offered no solitude for secrets. Seven years after the Revolution of 1830, when mobs invaded the Archbishop’s house and Republican agitators urged the destruction of church ornaments, Bianchon again saw Desplein entering Saint‑Sulpice. He followed, knelt beside him, and they attended the mass Desplein had founded.

Outside the church Bianchon pressed him: why this “fit of monkishness”? Desplein, whom everyone knew as an atheist, had been seen going to mass three times. At first he parried with jest and philosophy, but finally, on the verge of death, he confessed he could safely speak of the beginning of his life and related the story that explained the mass.

He led Bianchon to a sixth‑floor garret in the Rue des Quatre‑Vents—the sort of garret Arthez once occupied—and began. The mass, he said, sprang from events in that small room, from a clothesline and a pot of flowers. His early years in Paris had been so harsh he could claim the palm of suffering. He had known hunger and cold, lack of clothes and shoes, the shame of rags and the constant pinch of poverty. He worked through winters with his head steaming from the heat of a lamp while his body shivered from want; he had no money for books or formal training, no friends among students, and a temper misread as vice that was really the distress of a man striving from the bottom.

His family sent a scant allowance. Breakfast meant a cheap roll crumbled into milk for two sous; he dined only every other day at a household where a full meal cost sixteen sous. He guarded his clothes and shoes with the fanatic care of a man for whom a tear meant humiliation; he drank only water; the café and its sociability seemed an inaccessible promised land. He dreamed of coffee with milk and a game of dominoes. Yet he channeled his misery into labor—working by the lamp through endless nights, paying more for light than for bread to gain the knowledge that would give him worth and place.

He spoke of the peculiar sting of begging: the contraction of the throat, the ball rising to the larynx. Men born to wealth could not grasp the arithmetic of want; they criticize debt as if bread were bought with cakes. He wondered what a rich man, suddenly penniless and forced to work with his hands, would do. Paris, he said, is a field where mediocrity wages ceaseless warfare against talent: when some are about to mount, others pull their coat‑tails, loosen buckles, steal shoes, or fire pistols; virtue is turned into vice, success into suspicion, help into accusation.

Having sketched his general bitterness at human ingratitude and obstruction, he turned to the critical incident. He had expected a box of linen from his family—an auntly present of shirts meant to help a nephew supposed to live well on little. The box arrived while he was away at the schools; its carriage had cost forty francs. The porter, a German shoemaker who lodged in a loft, had advanced the money and kept the trunk. Desplein sought ways to recover his things without paying forty francs he did not have, certain he could quickly sell the linen. Like many surgeons, he admitted, he was resourceful in the craft but not in intrigue; chance, not cunning, governed him.

That night he returned to find, as Bourgeat, his fellow lodger—a water‑carrier from Saint‑Flour—told him, that the landlord had turned them out for unpaid rent. Desplein spent the most miserable night of his life, weeping over how to find a messenger, how to pay the porter, where to go. He fell into the peculiar sort of poverty that produces “heavenly slumbers full of beautiful dreams,” temporary escapes into sleep.

Next morning Bourgeat proposed a plan. A foundling from Saint‑Flour, without parents, he had a hand‑cart for hire at two sous an hour; they could move together and seek lodgings. He even produced a greasy purse of savings. Bourgeat paid their arrears and the porter, loaded their furniture and linen into the cart, and set off, stopping to inquire about rooms. By midday they were still wandering; the price of rooms kept them searching. At evening they found two garrets “at the very top of a house next the roof” in the Cour de Rohan, Passage du Commerce—rooms renting at sixty francs a year each.

Bourgeat, who usually earned about fifty sous a day, had been saving a hundred crowns to buy a barrel and a horse. On hearing Desplein’s desperate plight—extracted with a quiet craftiness that moved Desplein—he abandoned his plan temporarily and devoted his savings to help. For twenty‑two years he had carried water; now he put his fortune and his life into another man. He paid Desplein’s examination fees, bought books, watched him at work, and took a mother’s care that the student might have wholesome, abundant food instead of the meager fare to which he had been condemned. Bourgeat called him his boy, lent him money, kept house and performed tasks with the pride of one who felt everything ennobled by the aim. He woke Desplein at night, trimmed his lamp, cleaned the landing, sawed wood—doing all with a simplicity that magnified his dignity.

Desplein clasped Bianchon’s arm remembering this: Bourgeat had understood that Desplein’s mission and intellectual needs were larger than the water‑carrier’s own hopes. He loved him for himself and for what he might become. Bourgeat had a homely, medieval face and a prominent brow, with affections formerly devoted to a poodle that had accompanied him to church for twelve years; he would even ask whether masses could be said for the repose of a dog’s soul. His love was of the lower classes transferred nobly upward—loyal, practical, and self‑forgetting.

When Desplein left him to be house surgeon at the Hôtel‑Dieu, Bourgeat bore the separation with a dull pain but comforted himself with the hope of saving enough to help Desplein take his degree. Desplein dedicated his thesis to him. Later, when he could afford it, Desplein bought Bourgeat the barrel and the horse. Bourgeat was furious to learn his protégé had denied himself to do it, yet delighted in seeing his wish fulfilled; he laughed, scolded, admired the new barrel and horse and wiped away a tear. He also insisted on buying for Desplein a case of instruments mounted in silver—the pride of every young surgeon. Bourgeat never boasted; his simple devotion had saved Desplein from dying of want. He had eaten coarse bread rubbed with garlic so that Desplein might have coffee to work by.

Bourgeat fell ill. Desplein nursed him through nights, pulling him back once, but two years later the water‑carrier relapsed and died despite the utmost care and exertions of science. “No king was ever nursed as he was,” Desplein said. He had used every resource to prolong Bourgeat’s life long enough to show gratitude and complete his duty. Bourgeat died in his arms. He left everything to Desplein by a will beforehand made with a public scrivener in the year they had gone to the Cour de Rohan.

Bourgeat’s faith was perfect and ardent. He loved the Virgin as a man might love a wife. Though deeply religious, he never pressed his beliefs on Desplein. In his last hours he implored Desplein to spare no expense for every possible ecclesiastical benefit. Desplein had a mass said for him every day and often, at night, Bourgeat fretted that his life had not been saintly enough to secure Paradise. He received the last sacraments like a saint; his death matched the dignity of his life. Desplein alone followed him to the grave and then sought to honor his burial wishes: Bourgeat, without kin, had asked simply for masses for the repose of his soul.

As soon as Desplein had money, he paid Saint‑Sulpice for four masses every year. “As the only thing I can do for Bourgeat is thus to satisfy his pious wishes,” he told Bianchon, “on the days when that mass is said at the beginning of each season, I go for his sake and say the required prayers; and I say with the good faith of a skeptic—‘Great God, if there is a sphere which Thou hast appointed after death for those who have been perfect, remember good Bourgeat; and if he should have anything to suffer, let me suffer it for him, that he may enter all the sooner into what is called Paradise.’”

He added with a touch of rueful tenderness that although he professed no faith, he envied Bourgeat’s simple belief and would gladly give his fortune if such faith could enter his mind. Bianchon, who attended Desplein in his last illness, would not assert that the great surgeon died an atheist. Those who wish to believe may imagine that the humble Auvergnat opened Heaven’s gate for his friend, as he had once opened the literal door of a temple inscribed “A grateful country to its great men.”
