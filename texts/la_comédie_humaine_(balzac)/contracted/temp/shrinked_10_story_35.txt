**CHAPTER I. ONE OF MANY CHEVALIERS DE VALOIS**  

In various provinces of France, many Chevaliers de Valois exist, including one in Alencon, the focus of this tale. These chevaliers, sharing a name, were largely unaware of each other and accepted the Bourbon monarchy without question. The Alencon chevalier, now fifty-eight but claiming to be fifty, was a tall, thin gentleman of modest means, known for his excellent manners and social skills. He dined out daily and was a witty storyteller, often sharing anecdotes from the reign of Louis XV and the early Revolution. 

Despite his age, he maintained a youthful appearance, aided by a vigorous constitution and a notable nose that divided his pale face. His legendary appetite was attributed to a "hot liver," justifying his voracious eating habits. The chevalier's charm lay in his delicate grooming and refined manners, endearing him to the local aristocracy. He was known for his impeccable linen and a maroon coat that, while worn, was always clean. 

Living modestly in a second-floor room rented from Madame Lardot, the town's washerwoman, the chevalier's life revolved around social engagements and card games. His only income was a modest annuity, supplemented by occasional winnings at cards. Despite financial struggles, he was beloved in Alencon for his kindness and discretion. 

However, beneath this charming exterior lay a secret ambition: the chevalier aspired to marry a wealthy old maid to elevate his social standing, a desire that influenced his behavior and interactions within the community.

---

**CHAPTER II. SUSANNAH AND THE ELDERS**  

One spring morning, as the chevalier prepared for the day, a young girl named Suzanne entered his room. She was a work-girl at Madame Lardot's and had come to share news that could impact both their lives. Ambitious and clever, Suzanne sought to escape her provincial life and was considering a move to Paris. 

The chevalier, with his characteristic charm, encouraged her ambitions while maintaining a paternal demeanor. He advised her to pursue a wealthy man, suggesting Monsieur du Bousquier, a local bachelor with a dubious past. The chevalier recognized the rivalry between himself and du Bousquier, both vying for the affections of Mademoiselle Cormon, a wealthy old maid.

Eager to leave Alencon, Suzanne saw an opportunity to manipulate du Bousquier into supporting her move to Paris. Presenting herself as a victim of circumstance, she leveraged her situation to extract money from him. Caught off guard by her boldness, du Bousquier ultimately agreed to provide her with funds, revealing his own insecurities and ambitions.

As Suzanne left, she felt empowered by her ability to navigate the complexities of her relationships with men, contrasting sharply with the chevalier's genteel approach. Her cunning and ambition set the stage for a deeper exploration of the social dynamics in Alencon, where both men and women sought to secure their futures through marriage and social maneuvering.

**CHAPTER III. ATHANASE**

Madame Granson, a widow living on a modest pension and her own property, struggled to support her son, Athanase, who contributed a small salary from the mayor’s office. Their simple home in a provincial town was marked by poverty, shaping their lives and morals.

Athanase, a 23-year-old with a thin, pale appearance and a contemplative nature, felt stifled by his surroundings. Despite his potential, he lacked the encouragement and opportunities to flourish. His pride and timidity hindered his ambitions, leaving him with unfulfilled dreams and a deep love for his cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he believed to be wealthy. Initially motivated by financial stability, his affection evolved into genuine love, complicated by societal expectations and his insecurities.

In this provincial setting, marriage was often seen as a means of securing material comfort, complicating Athanase's aspirations amid rivals like du Bousquier and the Chevalier de Valois. Societal norms dictated that a young man of limited means could not pursue a wealthy woman without facing ridicule.

Athanase's love for Mademoiselle Cormon grew quietly, fueled by his isolation and limited options. His mother, aware of his feelings, recognized the potential benefits of such a match but understood the obstacles posed by their financial situation and Cormon's age. She remained hopeful, waiting for the right moment to advocate for her son.

Meanwhile, Suzanne, a young woman from a different social class, entered the scene with her own ambitions. After a brief encounter with Athanase, she felt an inexplicable connection to him, igniting her desire to help him rise above his circumstances. However, her aspirations were clouded by her struggles and societal pressures.

As the narrative unfolds, the characters converge at a dinner hosted by Mademoiselle Cormon, where tensions and hidden motivations emerge. Madame Granson, armed with knowledge of Suzanne's predicament, saw an opportunity to leverage the situation against du Bousquier, whom she viewed as a rival for her son's affections.

Athanase, unaware of the machinations surrounding him, prepared for the dinner with a mix of hope and anxiety. He longed to express his feelings to Cormon but was burdened by his circumstances. Meanwhile, Suzanne, having made a bold decision to leave her past behind, set her sights on a new life in Paris, hoping to find success and perhaps rekindle her connection with Athanase.

This chapter highlights the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations in a provincial setting, illustrating how the characters navigate their desires and the constraints imposed by their environment.

**CHAPTER IV. MADEMOISELLE CORMON**

In many second-class prefectures of France, salons serve as gathering places for influential yet non-elite community members. The hosts, respected figures, are invited to social events but do not mingle with the upper echelons of society, who prefer formal communication.

These salons, frequented by lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates, wield significant influence. Members prioritize land acquisition over luxury and resist outside influences, particularly from Paris, often dictating local administrative changes and isolating disapproved officials.

Gossip carries weight in this society, and young women are seldom present unless seeking validation. The dominance of one household can incite envy, prompting gatherings at more neutral homes when no suitable host is available.

For years, the upper classes of Alencon gathered at Mademoiselle Rose-Marie-Victoire Cormon's home, an old maid whose fortune was coveted by relatives and two bachelors. Living with her uncle, a former grand-vicar, she belonged to a family historically connected to nobility, producing stewards and magistrates. The Cormon family had a long-standing reputation through noble marriages.

Mademoiselle Cormon's residence, built during Henri IV's reign, is a notable structure in Alencon, admired for its architecture and cleanliness, reflecting her meticulous nature. The house features a courtyard with a flowerbed, a well-kept garden, and a layout typical of provincial homes, embodying the calm monotony of provincial life.

The salon is modestly decorated, with a large dining room and a salon filled with family portraits and furniture that reflects provincial spirit. The atmosphere is routine and familiar, with regular gatherings for card games among a group that controls significant electoral power and wealth in the region.

Despite her social standing, Mademoiselle Cormon remains unmarried, complicated by her desire to marry a nobleman and societal pressures. The political climate from 1788 to 1815 made finding a suitable match difficult, as she was wary of marrying a soldier or an older man. Her insistence on being loved for herself led her to set traps for potential suitors, resulting in disappointment and growing distrust of men.

As she aged, her fears of being married for her wealth intensified, and her character became increasingly rigid. Public opinion turned against her, speculating on her reasons for remaining single, while she remained unaware of affections directed toward her, particularly from Athanase Granson, one of her suitors.

Mademoiselle Cormon's physical appearance, once vibrant, began to fade as she approached her forties. Her attempts to maintain youth through religious practices deepened her despair. Despite longing for companionship and children, she found herself isolated, as suitable men were either too young, too old, or lacked the qualities she sought.

Her uncle, the Abbe de Sponde, was oblivious to her desires, focused on his spiritual life. Mademoiselle Cormon's lack of awareness regarding her suitors' affections, especially Athanase's, complicated her situation. She remained steadfast in her principles, refusing to compromise her values for marriage.

Over time, her reputation as an old maid became a source of ridicule in Alencon, despite her charitable nature and alignment with local customs. Mademoiselle Cormon's life exemplified the struggles of a woman caught between societal expectations and personal desires, ultimately leading to profound loneliness and unfulfilled aspirations.

**CHAPTER V. AN OLD MAID’S HOUSEHOLD**

To understand Mademoiselle Cormon's household dynamics, we must consider her and the Abbe de Sponde alongside their loyal servants: Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette.

Jacquelin, a stout forty-year-old who had served for twenty-two years, performed various tasks, from waiting at table to grooming the beloved mare, Penelope. He was in love with Josette, a thirty-year-old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if they married. They quietly saved their wages, hoping Mademoiselle’s marriage would free them to wed. Josette, plump and practical, was said to have a strong influence over Mademoiselle. Mariette, the cook, had been with the family for fifteen years and was skilled in local delicacies.

Penelope, the old brown-bay mare, was a focal point of affection, having served the family for eighteen years. Mademoiselle Cormon, childless and single, projected her maternal instincts onto the mare, which became a source of constant conversation and care among the household members.

The four servants operated with mechanical precision, but Mademoiselle Cormon, agitated by her fixed ideas, became increasingly difficult to please. She fixated on trivial matters, such as napkin arrangements or Penelope’s meal timings, leading to petty quarrels that provided her with a sense of purpose. These disputes, while seemingly trivial, were cathartic for her, allowing her to vent frustrations.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s life revolved around her household, her uncle, her health, and her religious duties. Her obsession with well-being led her to frequently consult doctors, humorously attributing minor ailments to her diet, which sparked amusing exchanges with her servants.

Every season, Mademoiselle Cormon visited her estate at Prebaudet, tending to her apple trees and preparing for harvest. Her departures were significant events in Alencon, drawing attention and speculation from townsfolk, particularly regarding her marital status and her relationship with Monsieur du Bousquier, a rejected suitor.

On the day of her farewell, the town buzzed with gossip, especially concerning the scandal involving Suzanne and du Bousquier. Mademoiselle Cormon, preoccupied with packing, was unaware of the brewing scandal.

During her dinner parties, Mademoiselle Cormon took pride in her role as hostess, hoping to attract a suitable husband. She often struggled to engage in meaningful conversation, relying on her limited wit and the assistance of the Chevalier de Valois, who deftly turned her awkward remarks into humor. Despite her lack of intellectual depth, she was regarded as kind-hearted, though often ridiculed by more astute guests.

As the evening progressed, conversation turned to the local theater and the rector of Alencon, with du Bousquier at the center of gossip. Mademoiselle Cormon attempted to defend him, inadvertently highlighting her own naivety. Her innocent remarks sparked laughter and further gossip among the guests.

The atmosphere shifted as guests discussed du Bousquier’s character and his rumored relationship with Suzanne. Mademoiselle Cormon, still oblivious to the true nature of the situation, expressed concern for Suzanne, revealing her growing awareness of the implications of du Bousquier’s actions.

As the night wore on, Mademoiselle Cormon contemplated her future and the possibility of marriage, particularly her feelings for Athanase, a young man connected to her through familial ties. Her musings were interrupted by the arrival of du Bousquier, who remained blissfully unaware of the gossip surrounding him.

The evening concluded with guests departing, leaving Mademoiselle Cormon to reflect on the night’s events. The discussions revealed the complexities of social dynamics in Alencon, where personal relationships intertwined with societal expectations and gossip.

In the aftermath, Madame Granson engaged in a heated discussion with her son about the connection between love and political beliefs, highlighting tensions within their household. The chapter closes with uncertainty regarding Mademoiselle Cormon’s future and the relationships that defined her life.

**CHAPTER VI. FINAL DISAPPOINTMENT AND ITS FIRST RESULT**

The next day, Mademoiselle Cormon, traveling back to Prebaudet with her servant Josette, was unexpectedly propelled toward marriage by a letter from her uncle, the Abbe de Sponde. The letter revealed that Monsieur de Troisville, a Russian army officer and grandson of a family friend, sought refuge in Alencon and needed hospitality. Alarmed by the urgency, Mademoiselle Cormon hastily prepared to return to town, disregarding the rain and her usual meticulousness.

Upon her return, the town buzzed with gossip about her sudden arrival and the potential marriage. Filled with hope, Mademoiselle Cormon envisioned herself as the Vicomtesse de Troisville and was determined to impress her guest, whom she assumed to be a bachelor around forty. As she prepared for his arrival, she felt a mix of excitement and anxiety about her home’s appearance and her own readiness.

When Monsieur de Troisville finally arrived, he was greeted by the Abbe de Sponde and Mademoiselle Cormon. He was distinguished and charming, resembling a refined gentleman rather than a soldier. Their dinner conversation flowed smoothly, with Mademoiselle Cormon eager to showcase her hospitality and the beauty of her home.

However, the evening took an unexpected turn when Monsieur de Troisville casually mentioned his wife, revealing he had been married for sixteen years. Mademoiselle Cormon fainted at this revelation, prompting local suitor du Bousquier to rush to her aid. This incident sparked a flurry of gossip throughout Alencon, with townsfolk speculating about her relationship with the viscount.

As Mademoiselle Cormon recovered, she realized her hopes for marriage had been dashed. The town's curiosity about her situation only intensified her embarrassment. In the aftermath, both du Bousquier and the Chevalier de Valois sought her attention, each hoping to win her favor. Ultimately, Mademoiselle Cormon decided to accept du Bousquier's proposal, seeing it as a way to regain her dignity and silence the town's chatter.

In a moment of vulnerability, she agreed to marry du Bousquier, asking him to lie about the timeline of their engagement to avoid scandal. Their engagement was soon announced, and the town buzzed with the news, marking a significant shift in Mademoiselle Cormon's life. Despite the circumstances, she found a sense of resolution in her decision, even as the specter of her earlier hopes lingered.

**CHAPTER VII. OTHER RESULTS**

The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage devastated Athanase Granson, though he concealed his turmoil. He learned of the engagement while at Chief Justice du Ronceret's home, where his mother was playing cards. Athanase had long hoped for Mademoiselle Cormon, and the news felt catastrophic. His Imperial education instilled a sense of fatalism, leading to deep despair.

Afterward, Athanase wandered the picturesque Sarthe region, finding solace in its beauty, though he appeared increasingly withdrawn. His political engagement faded, and he became indifferent in Alencon, with no one to share his grief. The arrival of Suzanne, inspired by tales of romance, briefly stirred his heart, but she remained unaware of his suffering.

Rumors about Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage circulated, with some doubting its validity until the contract was signed. The marriage was marked by ominous signs, including a lack of celebration and the bride entering the church with her left foot, interpreted as a bad omen. The couple quickly retreated to Prebaudet, where du Bousquier began modernizing the Cormon estate, alarming townspeople with his lavish spending.

Athanase’s despair deepened, leading him to contemplate leaving Alencon. He shared his feelings with his mother, who was unaware of his true intentions. After a heartfelt conversation, he left her for the Sarthe, where he ultimately took his own life, believing it would spare her further pain.

Madame Granson received the news of her son’s death with disbelief, later learning of his love for Mademoiselle Cormon through the Chevalier de Valois. The community reacted with shock, expressing regret for not supporting him during his struggles. Madame Granson’s grief was profound and private, contrasting with the public mourning for a young man whose potential was cut short.

In the wake of Athanase’s death, Madame du Bousquier returned to Alencon, where she encountered Madame Granson. The two women shared a moment of silent understanding, marked by the weight of their respective losses. Meanwhile, du Bousquier’s marriage began to reveal its darker side, as he exerted control over his wife and household, diminishing her autonomy.

As time passed, Madame du Bousquier’s life became increasingly constrained by her husband’s ambitions and societal expectations. Despite her initial happiness, she found herself trapped in a marriage devoid of affection, leading to a gradual decline in her spirit. The Chevalier de Valois, observing these changes, sought to expose du Bousquier’s true nature, complicating the dynamics within Alencon’s social circles.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage had significant repercussions, dividing the town into factions and altering the political landscape. Du Bousquier, now a prominent figure, leveraged his wealth and influence to reshape Alencon, but his personal life remained fraught with tension and dissatisfaction.

Madame du Bousquier’s internal conflict grew as she reconciled her duty to her husband with lingering feelings for the Chevalier. Societal pressures and her moral compass forced her into a life of quiet suffering, maintaining a facade of contentment while grappling with her past choices.

Ultimately, the story illustrates the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations, revealing how personal tragedies intertwine with broader social dynamics. The characters’ lives reflect the struggles between individual desires and societal constraints, leading to a poignant exploration of human relationships and the consequences of unfulfilled dreams.
