In provincial France, notably Alençon, many Chevaliers de Valois exist; one notable chevalier is central to this tale. At fifty-eight he insists he is fifty, presenting as a tall, thin gentleman of modest means, celebrated for excellent manners and social ease. He dines out daily, trading anecdotes from Louis XV’s reign and the early Revolution. Despite his age he seems youthful, thanks to a vigorous constitution and a prominent nose. His legendary appetite is blamed on a “hot liver,” explaining his voraciousness.

He lives modestly in a room rented from Madame Lardot, the town washerwoman, and revolves around social engagements and card games, supported by a small annuity and occasional winnings. Beloved for his kindness, he secretly hopes to marry a wealthy old maid to improve his standing.

One spring morning Suzanne, a work-girl for Madame Lardot, brings news that could change lives. Ambitious and clever, she plans to escape provincial life for Paris. The chevalier encourages her, advising she seek financial aid from the local bachelor Monsieur du Bousquier.

Suzanne intends to manipulate du Bousquier, presenting herself as a victim to extract funds for Paris. Her boldness unsettles him, yet he ultimately agrees, exposing his insecurities. As she leaves, Suzanne feels empowered by her ability to navigate relationships, contrasting with the chevalier’s genteel tactics and highlighting Alençon’s social dynamics, where men and women alike aim to secure futures through marriage.

Madame Granson, a widow on a modest pension, struggles to support her son Athanase, who earns a small salary at the mayor’s office. Their simple home reflects poverty that shapes their morals and prospects. Athanase, twenty-three, feels stifled, lacking encouragement and opportunity. His love for his cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon, which began as attraction to her perceived wealth, matures into genuine feeling complicated by social expectations.

In this provincial setting marriage is often a means to material comfort, complicating Athanase’s hopes amid rivals like du Bousquier and the Chevalier de Valois. His mother sees potential benefits in a match with Cormon but knows their finances are an obstacle.

Suzanne, from another social class, feels a sudden kinship with Athanase after a brief encounter and wishes to help him rise above his circumstances. The narrative converges at a dinner hosted by Mademoiselle Cormon, where tensions and hidden motives surface. Madame Granson hopes to leverage Suzanne’s situation against du Bousquier, whom she views as a rival for her son’s affection.

Athanase prepares for the dinner with anxious hope, longing to confess his feelings to Cormon while burdened by his social position. This chapter emphasizes the tangled interplay of love, ambition, and societal pressure in a provincial town.

In many second-tier prefectures, salons function as gathering places for the local notables excluded from the elite. Gossip carries weight and young women rarely appear unless seeking validation. Mademoiselle Cormon’s home has long hosted the upper class, despite her unmarried status and desire to wed a nobleman. Growing older, her fear of being married for her fortune hardened her character. Public opinion turned against her, speculating about her singleness, while she remained oblivious to admirers like Athanase. Her life embodies the loneliness and thwarted aspirations of a woman trapped between social expectation and personal desire.

To understand Mademoiselle Cormon’s household dynamics, consider her and the Abbé de Sponde alongside their steady servants: Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette.

Jacquelin, a stout man of forty who had served twenty-two years, handled many tasks—from waiting at table to grooming the mare Pénélope. He loved Josette, a practical thirty-year-old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if they married. The couple saved their wages, hoping Mademoiselle’s eventual marriage would free them to wed. Mariette, the cook, had been with the family for fifteen years and excelled at regional dishes.

Pénélope, the old brown-bay mare, was central to the household’s affections, having served the family for eighteen years. Childless and single, Mademoiselle Cormon projected maternal instincts onto the mare, which became a constant topic and object of care among household members.

The servants moved with machine-like precision, yet Mademoiselle Cormon, driven by fixed ideas, grew increasingly hard to please. She fixated on trifles—napkin folds, the mare’s feeding times—creating petty quarrels that gave her life structure. These disputes, though trivial, were cathartic outlets for her bottled-up anxieties.

Her life revolved around the house, her uncle, her health, and her devotions. Obsessed with well-being, she consulted doctors at the slightest complaint, humorously blaming minor ailments on diet and sparking amused exchanges with her servants.

Each season she visited her estate at Prébaudet to tend apple trees and prepare the harvest. Her comings and goings drew town attention, fueling speculation about her marital prospects and her relationship with Monsieur du Bousquier, a rejected suitor whose attentions were widely discussed.

On the day she left for Prébaudet, the town buzzed with gossip—particularly about a scandal involving Suzanne and du Bousquier—but Mademoiselle Cormon, preoccupied with packing, remained unaware.

At her dinner parties she cherished the role of hostess, hoping to attract a husband. She often failed at serious conversation, relying on the Chevalier de Valois to turn awkward remarks into amusement. Kind-hearted but intellectually limited, she was gently ridiculed by sharper guests.

That evening talk turned to the local theater, the rector of Alençon, and du Bousquier’s rumored conduct. Mademoiselle Cormon tried to defend du Bousquier, unwittingly revealing her naiveté and eliciting laughter that fed the gossip. As guests dissected his character and his alleged liaison with Suzanne, Mademoiselle Cormon, gradually sensing the implications, expressed concern for Suzanne, showing a dawning awareness of the situation.

Later she considered her own future and the possibility of marriage, and thought of Athanase, a young man connected to her family. Her reflections were interrupted by du Bousquier’s arrival, who seemed oblivious to the town’s whispers. The night ended with the guests leaving and Mademoiselle Cormon reflecting on how personal relationships, gossip, and social expectations entwined in Alençon.

The next day, returning to Prébaudet with Josette, she was suddenly driven back to town by a letter from the Abbé de Sponde. Monsieur de Troisville—a Russian army officer and grandson of a family friend—sought refuge in Alençon. Alarmed, she rushed back, ignoring rain and her usual meticulousness.

Her return intensified town chatter about a possible marriage. Imagining herself Vicomtesse de Troisville, Mademoiselle Cormon prepared to impress what she assumed would be a bachelor of about forty, feeling anxious about her home and appearance.

Monsieur de Troisville arrived, greeted by the Abbé and Mademoiselle Cormon. He was a polished, courtly gentleman rather than a soldier, and dinner conversation flowed pleasantly as she displayed her hospitality. Then, unexpectedly, he mentioned his wife—he had been married sixteen years. The revelation floored Mademoiselle Cormon and she fainted; du Bousquier rushed to help her, and the episode ignited fresh gossip in Alençon.

Recovering, she recognized that her hopes for marriage had been ruined. Town curiosity compounded her embarrassment. Both du Bousquier and the Chevalier sought her attention after the incident, but she ultimately accepted du Bousquier’s proposal, seeing marriage as a way to regain dignity and silence rumor. She asked him to lie about when their engagement began to avoid scandal—a choice that marked a major change in her life.

The news devastated Athanase Granson. He hid his turmoil but felt it as a catastrophe, his despair deepened by the fatalism instilled during his Imperial education. He learned of the engagement at Chief Justice du Ronceret’s home while his mother played cards; this blow intensified his isolation.

Athanase wandered the picturesque Sarthe to seek solace, but withdrew further from political and social life. Suzanne’s arrival and tales of romance briefly stirred him, but she remained unaware of his suffering.

Rumors about Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage circulated until the contract was signed. The wedding bore ominous signs: scant celebration and superstitions about the bride entering the church with her left foot, seen as a bad omen. The couple quickly returned to Prébaudet, where du Bousquier embarked on modernizing the estate, alarming townspeople with extravagant changes and spending.

Athanase’s despair grew until he contemplated leaving Alençon. He confided in his mother—who hadn’t grasped his depth of feeling—then went to the Sarthe and took his own life, convinced it would spare her further pain.

Madame Granson received news of her son’s death in stunned disbelief and later learned of his love for Mademoiselle Cormon from the Chevalier. The community reacted with regret and a sense of failure for not having supported him; Madame Granson’s grief was profound and private in contrast to public expressions of shock.

In the aftermath, Madame du Bousquier returned to Alençon and met Madame Granson; the two shared a moment of silent understanding shaped by their losses. Meanwhile, du Bousquier’s marriage began to show a darker side: he increasingly controlled his wife and household, eroding her autonomy.

Over time Madame du Bousquier’s life narrowed under her husband’s ambitions and social expectations. What began with expectation and comfort turned into a constrained existence lacking affection, and her spirit gradually dimmed. The Chevalier de Valois observed these changes and sought to expose du Bousquier’s true nature, complicating Alençon’s social dynamics.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage split the town and altered its political landscape. Du Bousquier, empowered by wealth and influence, reshaped Alençon but remained personally dissatisfied and domineering. Madame du Bousquier wrestled with duty to her husband while nursing lingering feelings for the Chevalier; societal pressure forced a facade of contentment atop quiet suffering.

Ultimately the story reveals the interplay of love, ambition, and social expectation: how private tragedies—failed hopes, a death, a stifling marriage—ripple through a community and expose the costs of unfulfilled dreams and compromised dignity.
