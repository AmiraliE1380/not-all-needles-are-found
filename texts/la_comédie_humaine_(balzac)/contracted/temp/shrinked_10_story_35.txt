Across France there were many Chevaliers de Valois; the one in Alençon is this tale’s subject. Though sharing the name, these chevaliers mostly did not know one another; they accepted the Bourbon monarchy because the Valois line had no male heir. The Alençon chevalier was an elderly, tall, thin gentleman whose youth in Paris and romantic pursuits had been interrupted by the Revolution.

Well regarded among the local aristocracy, he was known for his manners, wit, and anecdotes from Louis XV’s reign. A regular at social gatherings, his charm and discretion made him a favorite. At fifty-eight he claimed to be fifty, preserving a youthful air despite age. He had a prominent nose and a pale complexion that reddened on one side—his excuse for the flush, and for a voracious appetite, was a “hot liver.”

Though seemingly frail, he remained vigorous and passionate; his face bore the marks of a lived life. He groomed himself carefully and kept his clothes clean though worn, maintaining aristocratic standards. Living modestly in a rented room above Madame Lardot’s laundry, he dined out daily on a small annuity. Beloved in Alençon for card-playing and amiability, he was a master of conversation who mingled with all ranks and whose presence was sought. Yet curiosity attended his private life, including persistent rumors of a secret marriage to a woman named Césarine. Financially precarious, he managed provincial life with dignity.

One spring morning Suzanne, a young girl from Madame Lardot’s laundry, interrupted him with news that might bring pleasure or pain; she feigned indifference to his rumored link with Césarine. Ambitious and clever, Suzanne aspired to leave Alençon for Paris, convinced she could better herself. The chevalier, struck by her charm and drive, encouraged her in a paternal tone and advised courting Monsieur du Bousquier, a wealthy bachelor who had shown interest—recognizing how beauty and wit could secure a favorable match.

Du Bousquier, by contrast, was of dubious repute: once wealthy and influential, his role as a purveyor during the Revolution had tarnished him. Returned to Alençon, he hoped to marry Mademoiselle Cormon, a rich old maid, to restore his fortunes. His brusque opportunism contrasts with the chevalier’s elegance; their rivalry over Mademoiselle Cormon, and Suzanne’s ambitions, set in motion an intricate dance of desire, ambition, and societal expectation that reflects broader themes of class, gender, and the pursuit of happiness in a changing world.

Madame Granson, a widow living on a modest pension and a small property, struggled to support her son Athanase, who added only a meager salary from the mayor’s office. Their provincial home was simple and poor, shaping their lives and morals. Athanase, a pale, introspective twenty-three-year-old, secretly loved his cousin Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he believed to be wealthy. His passion was complicated by social expectations and the possible ridicule of loving an older woman.

What began as a practical hope to secure a better life for himself and his mother became genuine affection. He admired Mademoiselle Cormon from afar, constrained by circumstances that smothered his talent and ambition. Madame Granson, aware of his feelings, recognized the match’s advantages despite Athanase’s lack of fortune and the cousin’s age; she remained hopeful and patient, waiting for the right moment while Athanase dreamed of a future together despite daunting barriers.

The arrival of Suzanne, a young woman from an even poorer background, altered the dynamics. She felt an immediate connection to Athanase and understood his struggles, yet her ambitions pushed her toward deception as a way out of poverty. After a brief meeting with Madame Granson, Suzanne left town with money and new clothes; the community lamented her departure.

As Athanase prepared for a dinner with Mademoiselle Cormon—prompted by his mother to present himself well, unaware of her full plans—their fates began to unfold. Suzanne’s departure for Paris marked a turning point as she sought to remake her life, while Athanase remained in Alençon, burdened by unfulfilled dreams and provincial pressures.

In many second‑class French prefectures there was a salon that gathered influential—but not elite—members of the community. Hosted by locally prominent figures, it included lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates who, while invited to events, did not mix with the highest echelons; those above kept their distance and communicated formally. The salon wielded considerable local influence: its members valued practicality over luxury, focused on land and local concerns, resisted Parisian fashions, and treated gossip as a form of judgment. Young women rarely appeared except to seek validation.

In Alençon the upper classes convened at the house of Mademoiselle Rose‑Marie‑Victoire Cormon, an old maid whose fortune was coveted by relatives and two old bachelors. Living with her uncle, a former grand‑vicar, she descended from a family long in service to the nobility and, through marriages, had gained a semblance of aristocracy despite middle‑class origins. Her house, dating from the time of Henri IV, provoked envy by its prime location and careful upkeep—its courtyard, flowerbed, solid architecture, and period decor signaled meticulousness and historical standing. Inside, rooms were arranged for reception: a portrait‑hung salon where card games and local conversation took place, and a dining room that exuded provincial charm. Her circle numbered about 150 people, mostly mature, rooted in provincial life and rarely traveling.

Wednesdays were special: dinners drew larger assemblages where modestly dressed women conversed in a familiar, routine atmosphere about local gossip, land disputes, and judgments of aristocratic behavior—topics reflecting the community’s concerns and values.

Despite wealth and standing, Mademoiselle Cormon remained unmarried. She had hoped to wed a nobleman, but the political upheavals of 1788–1815 left few suitable matches; many were too old, too young, or otherwise unsuitable. Fear of being married for her fortune led her to set subtle tests for suitors. As she aged her longing for love intensified even as she missed genuine affections—most notably from Athanase Granson—revealing her innocence and naivety. Pious and virtuous, she stiffened under social expectation and became a figure of local ridicule, her unmarried state proverbial. Occasional returns of disbanded soldiers offered hope, but none met her standards. Her yearning for companionship and motherhood turned into despair, illustrating the complexities of love and social expectation for women constrained by rigid class conventions.

To complete the picture of Mademoiselle Cormon’s household, we must include her, the Abbé de Sponde, and their loyal servants: Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette, who ensured the uncle and niece’s comfort.

Jacquelin, a short, stout man with a ruddy face, had served twenty-two years, doing everything from waiting at table to grooming the family mare Pénélope. He loved Josette, a thirty-year-old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if she married him; the couple quietly saved, hoping Mademoiselle’s eventual marriage would free them to wed. Josette, plump and practical, was said to rule her mistress. Mariette, the cook of fifteen years, prepared the local delicacies.

Pénélope, the old brown-bay mare, drew Mademoiselle’s maternal affections: childless, she directed her care toward the animal. The servants and mare kept a steady routine while Mademoiselle grew harder to please. With an idle mind she fixed on trifles—household linens, the timing of Pénélope’s meals—turning small disputes into purpose. Her life centered on the household, her uncle, and religious duties, which magnified everyday minutiae; concerned about her health, she obsessed over minor ailments and diet.

Each season she visited her estate at Prébaudet to inspect the apple trees and prepare for harvest, marking the occasion with a farewell dinner that was an Alençon event. Town gossip swirled about suitors, notably M. du Bousquier, whom she had rejected. At the dinner Mademoiselle tried to engage but often lacked wit, amusing sharper guests. When talk turned to du Bousquier’s rumored involvement with Suzanne, her clumsy defense produced a remark that unexpectedly drew laughter and admiration, leaving her to ponder her place amid love, ambition, and social expectation in Alençon.

The next day Mademoiselle Cormon, packed into her old carriole with Josette, set off for Prebaudet, unaware an event would hasten her marriage. The morning after her arrival, while breakfasting, Jacquelin brought a letter from her uncle, the Abbé de Sponde: Monsieur de Troisville, a grandson of one of the abbé’s friends, wished to stay in Alençon and needed hospitality. Alarmed by the urgency, she returned to town to help her uncle receive the guest.

Despite the rain she insisted on leaving at once, driven by excitement and the prospect of marriage. Their arrival provoked town gossip; Josette and Jacquelin managed the household while Mademoiselle Cormon prepared for the viscount. News of his visit spread, and she fretted about her old-fashioned home and appearance, even sacrificing prized liqueurs to make a better impression.

When Monsieur de Troisville arrived he was greeted by the abbé and Mademoiselle Cormon. He looked distinguished and she felt an immediate attraction. Their dinner conversation flowed: the viscount praised her home and the region, making her feel noticed and desirable.

The evening shifted when the Chevalier de Valois and other guests arrived. During introductions the viscount casually mentioned he was married, devastating Mademoiselle Cormon, who fainted. Du Bousquier caught her and carried her to her room while townspeople watched and speculated.

As she recovered she realized her hopes for marriage were gone. News of her fainting and the viscount’s marital status swept Alençon, met with laughter and sympathy. To restore her standing—and with both Du Bousquier and the Chevalier seeking her favor—she accepted Du Bousquier’s proposal on the condition he consent to a public falsehood about their engagement, which he did. As they prepared to announce it the Chevalier arrived, unaware of the plan. The evening closed with Mademoiselle Cormon feeling a mix of triumph and uncertainty as the town buzzed about her engagement, marking a new chapter.

The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage devastated Athanase Granson, though he hid his turmoil. He first heard it at Chief Justice du Ronceret’s house while his mother played cards. Having long fixed his hopes on Mademoiselle Cormon and schooled in an Imperial fatalism, he slipped into indifference and despair. His mother noticed his pallor but assumed he was merely preoccupied with work.

After the announcement, Athanase wandered the Sarthe countryside, finding a bitter solace in its overlooked beauty. He withdrew from politics and society, becoming a figure of little interest to townspeople who did not grasp the depth of his suffering. His mother fretted and speculated he might be planning some major undertaking, unaware of the true cause of his withdrawal.

Suzanne, moved by romantic tales of adventure, returned to Alençon determined to help Athanase financially, though they never met. The town buzzed with gossip about Mademoiselle Cormon’s match, with conflicting reports about its legitimacy. Eventually the marriage contract was signed and the wedding took place amid ominous signs—a priest accidentally read from the De Profundis—setting an uneasy tone.

The union of Mademoiselle Cormon and du Bousquier lacked celebration; the couple soon retreated to Prébaudet. Du Bousquier set about modernizing the Cormon estate, alarming locals who feared losing their provincial charm. Consumed by grief, Athanase ultimately took his own life, leaving a letter that his mother misread as evidence of newfound independence rather than the despair it revealed.

Athanase’s suicide shocked Alençon. Madame Granson was left to mourn privately, carrying a grief others barely comprehended. The Chevalier de Valois disclosed Athanase’s unrequited love for Mademoiselle Cormon, deepening the town’s sense of tragedy. Madame Granson bore the true weight of her son’s struggles in silence.

Madame du Bousquier returned to Alençon and met Madame Granson in a tense, wordless encounter. Madame Granson enlisted the rector for a discreet burial to avoid scandal; the ceremony was quietly conducted, reflecting the community’s wish to contain the episode.

Over time, Madame du Bousquier’s marriage became both gossip and cautionary tale. Though she initially appeared content, she was powerless and unfulfilled. Du Bousquier, outwardly successful and politically ambitious, proved a domestic tyrant. The marriage brought social advantage but no children, deepening her longing for motherhood.

The Chevalier de Valois, embittered by the match, sought revenge against du Bousquier; his schemes only widened the rift between du Bousquier and the local aristocracy, intensifying animosity rather than restoring honor. Public mockery of Mademoiselle Cormon shifted toward sympathy for Madame du Bousquier as the true hardships of her life emerged.

She lived amid contradictions: fulfilling the duties of a devoted wife while inwardly resenting her husband and mourning the life she might have had. The Chevalier’s death, together with sweeping political changes in France, marked an end of an era. Suzanne, honoring the past, purchased a small memento from the chevalier’s estate—a symbol of lost connections and vanished possibilities. Madame du Bousquier’s life thus illustrates the complexities of love, duty, and social expectation: pitied and admired, she endures the consequences of choices made within constraining social limits.
