Across France there were many Chevaliers de Valois; the Alençon chevalier is this tale’s subject. Though sharing the name, these men mostly did not know one another and accepted the Bourbons after the Valois left no male heir. The Alençon chevalier was an elderly, tall, thin gentleman whose Parisian youth and romances were cut short by the Revolution.

Well regarded among the local aristocracy, he was famed for manners, wit, and anecdotes of Louis XV’s reign. A regular at social gatherings, his charm and discretion made him a favorite. At fifty-eight he claimed fifty, preserving a youthful air despite a prominent nose and a pale face that reddened on one side—his excuse for the flush and appetite was a “hot liver.” Seemingly frail but vigorous, he kept immaculate though worn clothes and lived modestly in a rented room above Madame Lardot’s laundry, dining on a small annuity. Beloved for card-playing and conversation, he mingled with all ranks; rumors persisted of a secret marriage to a Césarine.

One spring morning Suzanne, a clever laundry girl who dreamed of Paris, interrupted him with news. Touched by her ambition, the chevalier advised her paternally to attract Monsieur du Bousquier, a wealthy suitor. Du Bousquier, once prosperous but sullied by Revolutionary dealings, sought to marry rich Mademoiselle Cormon to restore his fortunes. His brusque opportunism and the chevalier’s elegance set up a rivalry—along with Suzanne’s aspirations—that exposes class, gender, and the pursuit of happiness in a changing world.

Madame Granson, a widow on a modest pension with a small property, struggled to support her son Athanase, who added a meager salary from the mayor’s office. Their poor provincial home shaped their lives and morals. Athanase, a pale, introspective twenty-three-year-old, secretly loved his cousin Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he believed wealthy. What began as a practical hope to secure a better life for himself and his mother became genuine affection, complicated by social expectations and the awkwardness of loving an older woman. Madame Granson, aware of his feelings, saw the match’s advantages despite his lack of fortune and waited patiently.

The arrival of Suzanne, a poorer newcomer, changed things: she felt an instant bond with Athanase but, driven by ambition, resorted to deception to escape poverty. After a brief meeting with Madame Granson she left town with money and new clothes, to the community’s regret. As Athanase prepared for a dinner with Mademoiselle Cormon at his mother’s urging—unaware of all her plans—Suzanne departed for Paris to remake her life, while Athanase remained in Alençon, weighed down by unfulfilled dreams and provincial pressures.

In many second‑class French prefectures a local salon gathered influential—but not elite—figures: lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates who kept a formal distance from elites. These salons, hosted by locals, wielded influence on practical concerns like land acquisition, resisting Parisian fashions, and treating gossip as communal judgment. Young women seldom appeared to seek validation.

In Alençon the upper classes met at the house of Mademoiselle Rose‑Marie‑Victoire Cormon, an unmarried heiress living with her uncle, a former grand‑vicar. Though of middle‑class origin, her family’s service to the nobility and advantageous marriages gave her an aristocratic veneer. Her well‑kept Henri IV house, with prized location, courtyard, flowerbed and furnishings, signaled historical standing. Reception rooms—a portrait‑hung salon for cards and local talk and a provincial dining room—served a circle of about 150 mostly mature, locally rooted people who seldom traveled.

Wednesdays brought larger dinners where modestly dressed women discussed gossip, land disputes, and judgments of aristocratic behavior. Cormon never married: the upheavals of 1788–1815 thinned matches; fearing a fortune‑marriage she tested suitors. Her longing for love, for Athanase Granson, became disappointment. Pious and prudent, she was ridiculed; dashed hopes from returning soldiers left her yearning and despair, revealing the harsh limits class expectations placed on women.

Completing Mademoiselle Cormon’s household were the Abbé de Sponde and servants Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette. Jacquelin had served twenty-two years, waiting at table and grooming the mare Pénélope, and loved Josette, a thirty-year-old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if she married him; the couple saved, hoping her eventual marriage would free them to wed. Josette, plump and practical, ruled her mistress. Mariette, cook of fifteen years, prepared local delicacies.

Childless, Mademoiselle poured maternal care into Pénélope and centered her life on the household, her uncle, and religion, becoming exacting and fixated on trifles—linens, meal times, minor ailments. Each season she inspected Prébaudet’s apple trees, returning with a farewell dinner, an Alençon event. Town gossip about suitors, notably the rejected M. du Bousquier, surfaced there; her awkward defense of him over rumors involving Suzanne won laughter and admiration, leaving her to ponder her place amid love, ambition, and social expectation.

Mademoiselle Cormon and Josette set off for Prebaudet, unaware events would hasten her marriage. The next morning Jacquelin brought a letter from her uncle, the Abbé de Sponde: Monsieur de Troisville, a grandson of his friend, wished to stay in Alençon and needed hospitality. Eager for a match, she hurried back to town despite the rain to receive him.

Their arrival provoked gossip; Josette and Jacquelin managed the household while Mademoiselle Cormon fretted over her old home and appearance, even sacrificing prized liqueurs. Monsieur de Troisville arrived distinguished and flattering, and she felt immediate attraction as their dinner warmed.

The mood changed when the Chevalier de Valois arrived with guests and the viscount casually revealed he was married. Devastated, she fainted and was carried to her room by Du Bousquier amid speculation. Determined to recover her standing—and with both Du Bousquier and the Chevalier courting her—she accepted Du Bousquier’s proposal on condition of a public sham engagement. The town buzzed as the announcement neared, leaving her triumphant yet uncertain.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage shattered Athanase Granson, though he concealed his turmoil. He first learned of it at Chief Justice du Ronceret’s while his mother played cards. Long nourished by hope and shaped by an Imperial fatalism, he slid into indifference and despair; his mother noticed only a pale, distracted son and blamed work.

Athanase wandered the Sarthe countryside, finding bitter solace in neglected beauty. He withdrew from politics and society, becoming of little interest to townspeople who could not fathom his pain. Meanwhile Suzanne, inspired by romantic tales, returned to Alençon resolved to help Athanase financially, though they never met. The town buzzed with gossip about the legitimacy of the match. When the marriage contract was signed and the wedding held, ominous signs—most notably a priest accidentally reading from the De Profundis—set an uneasy tone.

The union lacked celebration; the couple retreated to Prébaudet. Du Bousquier set about modernizing the estate, alarming locals protective of provincial customs. Consumed by grief, Athanase ultimately took his life, leaving a letter his mother misread as proof of independence rather than despair. His suicide stunned Alençon. Madame Granson mourned privately; only the Chevalier de Valois disclosed Athanase’s unrequited love, deepening the town’s sense of tragedy.

Madame du Bousquier returned to Alençon and had a tense, silent encounter with Madame Granson. Seeking to avoid scandal, Madame Granson arranged a discreet burial. Over time the marriage became both gossip and cautionary tale: socially advantageous yet personally barren. Du Bousquier, outwardly successful and ambitious, proved a domestic tyrant; his wife, childless and unfulfilled, performed duty while inwardly resenting him. The Chevalier’s vendetta and broader political changes widened the rift around them. Suzanne’s purchase of a small memento from the Chevalier’s estate symbolized lost connections. Madame du Bousquier’s life thus embodies the painful collisions of love, duty, and social expectation.
