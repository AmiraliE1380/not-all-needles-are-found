At the story's outset, the Stanhope press and ink-distributing roller were not yet common in small provincial printing houses. In Angoulême, tied to Parisian typography by its paper mills, only primitive wooden presses were used. “The press groans” was literal: leather ink-balls were still employed, and the pressman inked by hand. This old-world mechanism, cherished by Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, is central to the tale.

Séchard, once a journeyman pressman, survived the Requisition of 1793 and became the sole worker in a printing house after the master’s death. Illiterate, he was unexpectedly granted a master printer’s license by a Representative of the People, allowing him to buy the business at a bargain. He partnered with a nobleman, M. le Comte de Maucombe, who helped print decrees while staying hidden from authorities.

By 1795, after the Terror, Séchard needed a new compositor and hired an Abbé who later became a Bishop. Despite his lack of education, Séchard learned to manage the business, developing a keen sense for costs and profits. His avarice grew as he recognized the potential for wealth; he bought paper when prices fell and became his own landlord, owning the old printing house.

Séchard had one son, David, whom he sent to grammar school hoping to train a successor. David excelled academically, and Séchard—who scorned book learning—insisted he economize while studying in Paris. David completed his education without costing his father anything.

The printing house held a monopoly on official printing in the region, but the Cointet Brothers sought a second printer’s license, threatening Séchard’s business. Confident in David’s abilities, Séchard looked forward to retirement, believing his son would meet the competition.

Now in his seventies, Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard had developed a fondness for alcohol, which his late wife had once kept in check. His appearance reflected his drinking: a bloated face, cunning gaze, three-cornered hat, and old-fashioned clothing suited his character.

When David returned home, Séchard welcomed him with feigned warmth, eager to discuss business. Despite David’s request to postpone serious matters, Séchard was set on negotiating the sale of the business, viewing his son not only as offspring but as a potential adversary in business dealings.

The printing house, established since Louis XIV’s reign, was a large room with a glass door opening to the workshop. Customers often stumbled through the shop, distracted by the printing process. The premises included a yard with vines and a kitchen, where inky runoff mixed with kitchen waste, giving the place a notorious reputation.

The living quarters above the printing office were simple and cluttered, reflecting Séchard’s greed. The dining room was sparsely furnished, and the bedroom contained old furniture unchanged since the predecessor’s time. The state of the house mirrored the old printer’s character and relentless pursuit of profit.
