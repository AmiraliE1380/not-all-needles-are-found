To Monsieur J. B. Nacquart, Member of the Royal Academy of Medicine.

Dear Doctor—Here is one of the most carefully hewn stones in the second course of the foundation of a literary edifice which I have slowly and laboriously constructed. I wish to inscribe your name upon it, as much to thank the man whose science once saved me as to honor the friend of my daily life.

De Balzac.

Felix de Vandenesse to Madame la Comtesse Natalie de Manerville:

I yield to your wishes. It is the privilege of the women whom we love more than they love us to make the men who love them ignore the ordinary rules of common sense. To smooth the frown upon their brow, to soften the pout upon their lips, what obstacles we miraculously overcome! We shed our blood, we risk our future!

You exact the history of my past life; here it is. But remember this, Natalie; in obeying you I crush under foot a reluctance hitherto unconquerable. Why are you jealous of the sudden reveries which overtake me in the midst of our happiness? Why show the pretty anger of a petted woman when silence grasps me? Could you not play upon the contradictions of my character without inquiring into the causes of them? Are there secrets in your heart which seek absolution through a knowledge of mine? Ah! Natalie, you have guessed mine; and it is better you should know the whole truth. Yes, my life is shadowed by a phantom; a word evokes it; it hovers vaguely above me and about me; within my soul are solemn memories, buried in its depths like those marine productions seen in calmest weather and which the storms of ocean cast in fragments on the shore.

The mental labor which the expression of ideas necessitates has revived the old, old feelings which give me so much pain when they come suddenly; and if in this confession of my past they break forth in a way that wounds you, remember that you threatened to punish me if I did not obey your wishes, and do not, therefore, punish my obedience. I would that this, my confidence, might increase your love.

Until we meet,

Felix.

To what genius nourished by tears shall we one day owe the most touching elegy—the tale of torments silently borne by souls whose tender roots find stony ground at home? The true history of a childhood stunted by neglect and coldness would move pity more surely than public tragedies. Such was my childhood.

From infancy I knew a curious blend of neglect and occasional scrutiny. After three years with a nurse in the country I returned to a household where indifference reigned; even the servants, kinder than my blood relations, seemed more solicitous. My elder brother and sisters, encouraged by our mother’s unequal temper and favoritism for the eldest, persecuted me. The usual compact of children protecting one another was annulled in my case: often punished for a brother’s faults and forbidden to plead my innocence, I learned mistrust early. These wrongs did not make me servile; they hardened me. Constant expectation of fresh blows bred a sullen resignation and a premature pride that tempered the ill effects of my upbringing.

Sometimes my mother spoke of educating me herself; the mere thought chilled me, and I blessed the neglect that let me wander through the garden, watch insects, and fix my attention on a star. One evening, crouched under a fig-tree and absorbed by a star, I was falsely accused by our governess, Mademoiselle Caroline, of mischief with a garden faucet and severely punished. The star, however, remained mine; forbidden pleasures grew more desirable, and I kept that glowing point in my imagination through boyhood.

Charles, five years my senior, was the household favorite: handsome, robust, indulged. I, puny and sickly, at five was sent as a day pupil to a town school with only a scanty lunch. The contrast between my hunger and my companions’ plenty wounded me deeply. Boys taunted me over rillons and delicacies I did not possess; a cruel trick—withdrawal of a promised slice—left me ashamed and more resolved. Once I was stoned; my mother heard and only lamented what she called my dangerous temperament.

Rejected by family and classmates, I distrusted myself and others. At twelve I was sent to Pont‑le‑Voy, an Oratorian school where dull boys languished. For eight years I lived a pariah’s life; my three francs a month barely covered study needs; I could not afford games and was excluded from playground life except by humiliating flattery of the rich. I took refuge beneath a tree with borrowed books and thought much. When I won prizes for composition and translation and my parents did not appear at the theatre, I burst into tears, burned my crowns, and felt the deeper ache of being forsaken.

The sense of injustice drove me toward religion. At first communion I sought consolation in prayer and martyrology; youthful zeal fed sublime visions that strengthened imagination and deepened my inner life—qualities that would later temper any talent I might have. My father, uneasy about the Oratorians, sent me to Paris to the Lepitre Academy at fifteen. There, as before, neglect continued: meager pocket-money, strict discipline, and social restrictions. Monsieur Lepitre—old, ceremonious, given to Bourbon sympathies—managed a small subvention for me but never enough.

In Lepitre’s house the porter Doisy, who provided breakfasts and small luxuries for favored pupils, made resisting temptation a daily struggle for a hungry boy. Once my parents’ arrival in Paris promised escape: my brother planned to take us to dinner in the Palais‑Royal and to a theatre. But I owed a hundred francs to Doisy; my mother, infuriated, refused indulgence and reproached me with future ruin. Sent back to Lepitre’s charge, I lost the dinner and the play—the first meeting with my mother after twelve years apart ended in reproach.

At nineteen, still under Lepitre’s guardianship and constrained by lack of money and supervision, I longed for the city. My aunt, the Marquise de Listomère, on the Île Saint‑Louis, was ceremonious and cold; she would not offer money and made me feel ashamed of my youth. Several attempts to slip away to the Palais‑Royal were thwarted by unlucky coincidences: once Monsieur Lepitre’s coach appeared, another time my mother unexpectedly arrived, and a third time a post-chaise caught me. My family’s anxieties, political as well as moral, kept me under restraint.

Study became both refuge and peril; absorbed in books, I delayed the normal stirrings of youth. In Tours, my mother’s coldness crushed me; on a journey home I threw myself at her feet, begged for affection, and was mocked as playing at “comedy.” Once at Blois I stood on a bridge intending to throw myself into the Loire but could not commit the act. Back at home I found only the slight mercies of my sisters and poor clothing. My mother’s ironical speeches and punctilious sense of duty made affection a painful, unreliable thing.

Yet life at Tours had public excitement. The Duc d’Angoulême passed in triumph; Touraine celebrated, and I longed to attend a ball given in his honor. Despite my mother’s mockery, secret preparations were made: a bottle‑blue coat, silk stockings, and a frilled shirt—my first finery. The ball overwhelmed me: heat, music, a crush of people, military bands, and cries for the duke. At first the spectacle disgusted me, but then a strange ambition stirred—an appetite for glory and influence over the masses.

Amid the crowd I saw a woman who changed me. Mistaking her for my mother’s shoulders, I was rebuked by a single drawn word, “Monsieur!” Tears sprang to my eyes, and the look that followed—noble, angry, forgiving—awoke a passion I had never known. She moved with queenly grace and a purity that transformed my life: my star had become flesh. From then on the sky seemed bluer and I sought her everywhere in Touraine.

My mother, seeing my melancholy, sent me for a few weeks to Frapesle, a château on the Indre, hoping country air would restore me. For a young man newly in love but ignorant of his beloved’s name or station, this was an opportunity. Walking the country, I examined every house and old tower as if she might be there. The landscape—moors, woods, the valley of the Indre—enchanted me; in that region beauty is intrinsic to the air and my soul felt in tune with it.

From a walnut‑tree where I later loved to pause, I first saw the castle that housed the woman I sought. A white spot among the vines, cambric fluttering in an arbor, convinced me she was there. The castle, Clochegourde, belonged to the Comte de Mortsauf and his wife, a Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt—an ancient house, proud and secluded, their fortune limited and habits fixed. My host, Monsieur de Chessel, who knew the count and countess, proposed we cross the river and call; his teasing reading of my enthusiasm revealed how transparent my feelings had become.

Clochegourde was modest but tasteful: five windows on the façade, terraced gardens, acacias, vines, and neat outbuildings. From the road the ground‑floor seemed an upper story; a little tourelle and carved crest over the portico testified to lineage. The house sat in the valley like a jewel in green; approaching it quickened my heart with foreboding and delight.

We were announced shortly after the bell for breakfast; the count was at Azay but the countess—whose voice I had heard at the ball—welcomed us. My agitation nearly betrayed me; she returned to her embroidery and spoke with a calm dignity that both concealed and revealed feeling. Monsieur de Chessel explained my fatigue from walking and my being a Touraine native newly returned. Madame de Mortsauf offered hospitality with gentle courtesy; her glance, severe at first, softened when she saw my distress. She gave restoratives, words of comfort, and after consultation with Monsieur de Chessel invited us to dine. That invitation—accepted—seemed a promise: if I pleased her, my introducer might remain in the count’s good graces.

Madame de Mortsauf, though reserved, possessed a voice and manner that entranced me. At dinner she spoke of harvests and local affairs; at first she seemed to shut me out, but I discovered the charm of listening to her. The cadence of her speech, the brevity of her glances, the rare but luminous intonations moved me more than any artifice. I would prolong conversations and accept reproach merely to hear the melody of her voice and drink the subtle air of her presence.

Her appearance—white shoulders, gauze veiling her bosom, braids above a soft throat—had impressed me at the ball; up close, in everyday dress, she retained that luminous appeal. Her face defied easy description: an artist could best render the inward fires and restraint that made her expression unique. Her hair was fair and soft; her brow broad and thoughtful; her eyes green tinged with brown—usually wan but capable of sudden gleams touched by sorrow or joy. A Grecian nose and graceful lips gave spiritual beauty; her figure combined strength with unusual softness. She seemed both mother and maiden—grave from suffering, yet childlike in feeling. Her movements, rare glances, and simplicity of dress formed a charm that made men dream: she revealed, without ambition, a virginal purity and moral elevation that made others thoughtful.

Beyond physical features, it was the constant emanation of her soul—tenderness to family, cheerfulness in serenity, resignation amid storms—that impressed me most. Clochegourde’s salon, panelled and modestly ornamented, matched her spirit: gray paint, porcelain vases with Cape heather, cambric curtains, and an embroidery frame. From its windows the valley unrolled—Pont‑de‑Ruan, château d’Azay, Sache—images that would nourish my ideas and transform my mind. Many of my thoughts in science and politics seemed first to live there, in that composed, reflective atmosphere. The salon’s quiet regularity deepened the impression of a domestic epic lived in a small and noble sphere.

When Monsieur de Mortsauf returned, my curiosity about him vied with fear that he might obstruct my hopes. A little girl, Madeleine, entered—fragile and pale—and the mother’s brow clouded. Madeleine, though thin and wan, preserved a spirit; Jacques, her brother, similarly weak, followed the count. The children’s frailty suggested family sorrow; Madame de Mortsauf’s health and spirit bore the cost. Monsieur de Mortsauf, about forty‑five but aged by care, offered a polite, uneasy courtesy. His manner concealed more than it revealed, but the domestic picture—motherly care, frail children, the reserve of the house—revealed a life of tenderness restrained by trial.

As the visit continued, I grew more attentive to the count’s attitudes. Though outwardly civil, he was awkward and uneasy, like a man whose distrust springs from inability to understand rather than malice; yet his demeanor warmed when he learned my name and family connections. There was a respectful recognition of lineage: my father had served the Bourbons, and such devotion could not be disregarded by a man of old family like Mortsauf. The count’s complexity hinted at tragedies and virtues entangled by history—a man whose public reserve sheltered private tenderness.

Thus began a sequence of days at Clochegourde that would shape my life. The valley, the house, the maternal presence, and that first vivid encounter set in motion a passion that combined gratitude, reverence, and the consuming hunger of a first love. My lonely childhood had prepared me to adore and worship with an intensity only such neglect can generate. The domestic world of the Mortsaufs—humble and dignified—provided the soil into which my youthful devotion sank its roots.

I learned quickly the subtleties of Madame de Mortsauf’s manner: the economy of her speech, the weight of her silences, the glances scarce as jewels. Her conversation, even on trivial matters, carried an undertone betraying a depth of soul. Her benevolence and austere tenderness drew me into a state where admiration and self‑sacrifice mingled. I noted the simplicity of the household, the English tidiness of the salon, the embroidery, the backgammon board, the garden, the interlaced paths. Each small thing seemed to speak of moral discipline and inner warmth.

The more I saw of Clochegourde’s family life, the clearer it became that respect would come as easily as passion; here was a woman whose humanity compelled love. The contrast between their modest means and noble air made an impression: pride without display, ancestral dignity humbled by circumstance. This image of noble poverty added to my reverence.

In the strangeness of my past—neglected by my mother, punished unjustly, driven inward by study and prayer—the spectacle of such domestic devotion opened me like a flower. It is not only beauty that wins the heart, but constancy, sacrifice, and the gentle influence of a soul that turns small duties into holy acts. Thus Clochegourde, its valley, and above all its countess, became the scene and cause of a passion that would shape my destiny.

Our visit ended with impressions words could not fully render. Madame de Mortsauf’s presence, the music of her voice, her discreet tenderness for the children, and the quiet dignity of the home remained with me. I left with a mingled feeling of hope and helplessness—hope because I had shared an attentive hour in her presence; helplessness because I was too young, too dependent, and too ignorant of the world to know how to win her friendship beyond the simple worship of a distant admirer.

Thus closes the account of my early years to the point where my life after twenty begins under influences both tender and decisive. From the neglect of my family to the sanctuary of the valley, my character was formed—partly by solitude and hard study, partly by the sudden appearance of a woman whose moral and physical beauty made of her my guiding star. The meeting at the ball and at Clochegourde introduced an affection equal parts devotion and pain, opening the way to all that followed.

Though ten years of emigration and farm life had altered him, the count retained traces of nobility. Even liberal onlookers could admire his chivalric loyalty and steadfast convictions: devoted to a cause and honest in politics, yet ignorant of France’s practical affairs. Upright and self-sacrificing, he was the type who, unable to serve his party, might inadvertently harm it—ready to sacrifice his life rather than his money.

At dinner his flaccid cheeks and furtive glances at his children revealed a burdened mind and a frail constitution he had passed to them. He might blame himself but refused to be judged; his self-aware harshness magnified his family’s misery. When his wife returned with the children, unhappiness settled over the house.

Conversation shifted and the count introduced me to his wife, revealing curious intimacies about my family. The countess, surprised by my age, displayed a maternal instinct and a cautious curiosity. She asked many questions but spoke little. “If study has made you ill,” she said, “the air of our valley will soon restore you.” The count, suspicious of modern education, declared that it burdens children with ideas and should return to religious hands. Later he added, bluntly, “I shall always distrust men of talent.”

He proposed a tour of the gardens. When the countess began to mention Monsieur de Vandenesse’s long walk and Monsieur de Chessel’s escort, the count interrupted, calling it “very imprudent,” revealing his desire to control his household and the rigid royalism that governed his judgments. A servant announced dinner; Monsieur de Chessel offered his arm to Madame de Mortsauf, the count took mine, and we went to the dining room.

The dining room, modest but charming, had a white-tiled floor, varnished floral paper, simple but ample fare, family silver, and mismatched china. These quaint details delighted me and briefly lifted me above the difficulties of country life. Seated beside her, serving her wine, I felt a profound connection. A kiss had bound us in a secret that filled me with both joy and shame. Wanting to please the count, I lavished affection on the dogs and engaged the children, hoping they would accept me as one of their own. I sensed any sign of gloom would jeopardize my place in their lives.

Dinner passed in a haze of happiness: I reveled in being under her roof, ignoring her apparent coldness and the count’s polite indifference. Love, like life, has an adolescence, and I was content in my feelings. I made several foolish remarks, driven by passion, but no one noticed their cause; even she appeared oblivious to love. The rest of my visit felt dreamlike as I returned across the Indre, enchanted by moonlight and nature’s sounds. That evening marked a turning point; I felt past struggles yield to the joy I had just experienced.

Approaching Frapesle, I saw Clochegourde and a small boat tied to a tree—Monsieur de Mortsauf’s fishing boat. Once out of earshot, Monsieur de Chessel murmured that I had stepped into the count’s heart at once, lifting my spirits though I sensed a trace of bitterness.

Monsieur de Chessel, whose family name was Durand, had abandoned his merchant name to revive an old parliamentary one and had been given a count’s title under Louis XVIII. Ambition had made him ostentatious; his children enjoyed honors without knowing the sacrifices behind them. He combined pettiness and envy with a recent rekindling of royal favor that fed his pretensions. Yet he treated me kindly, with a paternal hospitality that contrasted with the neglect I knew, offering a peace I had longed for.

The owners of Frapesle were central to my awakening of happiness, and I cherished those memories. Monsieur de Chessel’s ostentation—fine horses and lavish entertaining—annoyed neighbors while the Comte de Mortsauf, with limited means, struggled to maintain his estate. Their relations were polite but lacked intimacy.

Jealousy did not alone explain the count’s isolation. His education had been shallow; he emigrated just when his learning should have deepened and then lived through long idleness, clinging to hopes of a restored monarchy instead of seeking work. Life’s trials eroded his spirit; natural gaiety browned into moroseness and a disposition toward hypochondria. Hidden affairs and the strain of exile further impaired his health and prospects.

After twelve years of hardship he returned to France under a decree allowing emigrants back. Crossing the Rhine he cried “France! France!” like a child calling for its mother. Once wealthy, he came back ruined, stripped of authority, burdened by his name. His unbending opinions and past left him vulnerable to ridicule in a society that often mocked weakness. He finally settled in Maine on a forgotten farm that offered some stability.

The Lenoncourt family, ruined themselves, welcomed him; he lived with them while preparing a home. Mademoiselle de Lenoncourt accepted his proposal and lived with her aunt, the Duchesse de Verneuil, whose devout influence shaped the countess. After the duchess’s sudden death, Madame de Mortsauf received visits from Saint-Martin, who corrected his last works at Clochegourde; the duchess’s death cast a shadow over the early years of the marriage.

Monsieur de Mortsauf found solace in the valley, but the birth of his first child, Jacques, brought despair: the doctor pronounced the child unviable—a truth the count concealed from his wife. Subsequent births confirmed his fears, deepening his sense of failure. The countess, sensing her husband’s turmoil, devoted herself to his care, striving to reconcile him with himself. She accepted his miserliness and mistrust and guided him toward what was right, often at the expense of her own happiness. She resolved to remain at Clochegourde, fearing that leaving would harm their children. Her efforts masked the count’s incapacity and allowed him limited peace in her presence.

As I spent time there, my understanding deepened and the weight of unspoken truths grew. One evening I found the countess watching a sunset and sensed a shared sorrow. “Some days are hard to bear,” I remarked. “You read my soul,” she replied. “We touch at many points,” I said. She shuddered and left abruptly when a child coughed.

The countess welcomed my visits, seeing in me an amusement for her husband. I often played backgammon with the count, enduring his harshness. As I improved, his temper flared when I began to win; I learned to manage our matches—letting him win at first, then evening the score. The countess understood my strategy and thanked me with a grateful gaze that filled me with joy.

One evening, after an intense game, the count’s mood shifted into rage. I left the room and waited in the garden. When she joined me, she revealed her husband’s condition and asked me to forget the incident. “Promise me to bury the recollection of that scene,” she implored. “I don’t ask you to swear it; just give me your word of honor.” “I will do all you wish,” I replied, “but I cannot change my opinion of him.” She acknowledged my feelings and urged understanding: “Perhaps for my sake, you will forget what he said to you today. He will wish to undo the wrong he did.”

We walked in silence; she eventually said, “It is late; we will part.” I wished to kiss her hand; she hesitated before allowing it. “Never take it unless I give it to you,” she warned. “Leave me my freedom.” As I departed I felt a profound connection, pledging my love in silence.

The next day I reflected on our bond, feeling purpose and commitment to her. I recognized her sacrifices and strength. Our shared sufferings had forged a connection beyond friendship, binding us in a deep understanding of each other’s pain and resilience. In the days that followed, small moments deepened my affection. I was determined to support her, to be a source of strength, and to honor the bond we had formed—an affection meant to endure through life’s trials. Beneath the stars I vowed to honor her spirit and nurture the love that had blossomed between us.

This scene begins on a Tuesday, though I had waited until Sunday and had not yet crossed the river. Much had changed at Clochegourde in the intervening days. The count was made general of brigade, received the cross of Saint‑Louis and a pension of four thousand francs. The Duc de Lenoncourt‑Givry was restored as a peer of France, recovered two forests and his place at court; his wife regained some crown property. Because of this recovery, the Comtesse de Mortsauf became an heiress: her mother arrived with the hundred thousand franc dowry the count had never demanded in their earlier poverty. With his own savings the count bought two neighboring estates yielding some nine thousand francs a year. Their son was to inherit his grandfather’s peerage; the count intended to entail the estate on him without harming Madeleine, and the Duc might arrange a good match.

These events soothed the count and changed the atmosphere at Clochegourde. The duchess’s arrival revealed her caste pride, something my love for Henriette had hidden from me. I felt my poverty and insignificance keenly. On Sunday morning, seated in the private chapel with Monsieur and Madame de Chessel and the Abbé de Quelus, I stole a glance at the duchess’s pew and saw her with her daughter, the count and his children, praying. Henriette, calm and composed beneath a broad straw hat, seemed almost a religious statue; her abandoned prayer moved me deeply.

After mass came vespers. Madame de Chessel proposed going to Frapesle to avoid crossing the Indre twice in the heat. Monsieur de Chessel offered his arm to the duchess; Madame de Chessel took the count’s; I offered mine to the countess and felt her arm for the first time. In the woods of Sache, with light filtering through leaves, sensation and thought overwhelmed me; my heart beat violently.

I asked if the new dignities would change them. Henriette protested—she and the count had not sought these favors; they were neither place‑hunters nor beggars. She explained that she had counselled moderation—declining a command at the Maison Rouge had preserved their son’s prospects and earned royal favor; Jacques must represent both Lenoncourt and Mortsauf. Turning to me, she begged me to be Jacques’ guardian and sponsor, to go to Paris; if my family would not support me, theirs—especially her mother—would help. “Put your desires into a noble ambition,” she urged.

I answered that ambition would be my mistress but promised I would not accept favors there in exchange for obedience here. “From you I would accept everything; from others, nothing.” I told her I had taken vows and would bind myself to her by ties that could never be broken.

She trembled. I pressed my meaning: I must love her as her aunt had been loved; therefore I would love without hope and with absolute devotion. I would enter a seminary, become a priest, educate Jacques—making him my likeness in energy and political conception. I would live near her; cloaked by religion, my love could never be suspected of wrong. I would consume myself in a purified flame.

Henriette implored me not to bind myself in a way that might be an obstacle. “Do you think despairing love a life’s vocation?” she asked, telling me to remain free and wait for life’s trials: marry neither Church nor woman—marry not at all, she seemed to say. She counseled me to accept their help, rise in life and fortune, and then perhaps know her hope. As she spoke, she bent to my ear and whispering, “never release the hand you are holding at this moment.” I swore I would not.

We neared the wooden gate into Frapesle’s park; at that instant I felt I understood her. Her purity stirred tears—admiration mixed with the bitterness of passion. I kissed her hair to hide my pain. She teased me about etiquette and the necessity of respecting rank: “Be on our side—especially now, when it triumphs.” I was moved by the union of political feeling and affection in her counsel.

At Frapesle the count presented me to the duchess, who examined me with the cold eye of fifty‑six years, preserved and courtly. I behaved well; the countess murmured, “You are perfect.” The count, softened by his change in fortune, invited me to dinner and to come the evening before the duchess left. He advised me to cultivate Madame la duchesse’s acquaintance; her salon would set the tone in the faubourg St. Germain.

The Restoration’s transformations bewildered our generation. I cared nothing for politics; my sole ambition was to love Henriette as Petrarch loved Laura. The duchess therefore saw me as a child. The gathering at Frapesle, where the woman I loved was admired, intoxicated me. The count seemed almost young, and I enjoyed Madeleine’s wit. It was a happy day; a morning hope lit nature and made Henriette smile: “That happiness smiling on my gray and cloudy life seems good,” she told me.

The count left for Tours; a serious disagreement then arose between mother and daughter. The duchess wanted the countess to move to Paris—where she promised a court position and where the count might accept a post. Henriette opposed leaving Clochegourde, which soothed her husband and preserved the children’s health. She had persuaded him to go to Tours to hide his condition from the duchess. I alone knew Clochegourde’s dark secret. Henriette discovered, painfully, how little the duchess cared for Jacques and Madeleine. The duchess brooked no opposition, using sweetness, then coldness, then sarcasm to force compliance. In those ten days Henriette endured the struggles of a young woman asserting independence; she endured what a generous woman must when yoked to a petty man. I warmed to her cause and sought to be her protector.

“Have you no ambition?” the duchess asked me at dinner.

“Madame,” I answered, “I have enough in me to conquer the world; but I am only twenty‑one, and I am all alone.” The duchess looked at her daughter in astonishment. The countess begged me to be cautious. The Thursday of ceremony exhausted everyone; the duchess returned to court and Clochegourde settled back into its ordinary life.

My quarrel with the count made me ever more welcome; I clung like a vine to the soul that had opened the world of shared feeling. Our fraternal marriage, based on trust, strengthened; Henriette wrapped me in maternal care, and my love burned in our separations. I loved her with a double love—seraphic when near, searing when away. She was not the kind of woman to be tormented by a young man; she feared harm to her children and could not endure ardor. She needed veiled love—support mingled with tenderness—and gave herself in charity to others. This combination of piety and self‑devotion filled the air with spiritual incense. She gave me only the back of her hand to kiss and never the palm, as though limiting sensuality. No two souls ever clasped so ardently while remaining unbound; in youth we love the whole woman—her children and interests—as our own.

I became part of the household; servants treated me kindly, children divined my affection and were discreet. True passions bloom sweeter in barren soil. But I also saw Monsieur de Mortsauf’s faults: perpetual complaining, petty tyranny in trifles, a temperament that demanded victims—an inner dissatisfaction that punished his family. On walks he chose the direction and then complained whichever path was taken; at home he blamed others. Sometimes he fell into gloomy silence that alarmed Henriette and called forth her tender attentions. He let her serve him as she would the children. I discovered that his petty tyranny stifled management and blocked improvements to the estate—daily struggles Henriette had described nobly. My admiration for her grew; I became a voluntary victim to come nearer her, a repentant apostate seeking heaven in a humble role beside her.

“Were it not for you, I must have succumbed under this life,” she said to me after a particularly venomous attack.

Once, after the count had gone to bed, we stayed under the acacias while the children played at sunset. Our few words revealed mutual thoughts; when language failed, our silence sufficed. We asked each other, “Among all our days on earth, will there be one for us?” Despite the evening’s poetry, desire came upon me. After months of restraint I could not accept the assigned fate: I took Henriette’s hand and caressed it. She withdrew and tears fell from my eyes. She offered her hand to my lips but warned that such a request would cause her grief; a friendship that asked such a favor was dangerous. I lost control, complained of my suffering and begged for slight alleviation. She forced me into silence with a proud glance: she had given more than she should, and if I could not be to her something between confessor and brother, she must never see me again. She could die and offer her sufferings to God. I calmed her and promised never to cause pain, to love her as an old man loves his youngest child.

The next day I came early and, finding no flowers in her gray salon, rushed into fields and vineyards to make two bouquets. I found color and foliage could express meaning; melody awakens memory, and flowers their own language. I enlisted Jacques and Madeleine, and arranged white roses and lilies with blue flowers—symbols of two purities: the child and the martyr. The countess understood and gave me a poignant look; it was reward enough. Twice a week I engaged in this pursuit, walking long distances for blooms, taking pleasure in sensation and form. Nature offered images rising to moral conceptions: dew‑glistened heather, mossy rocks, anemones like a pure idol alone in her valley, sheets of water, woods like cathedral naves. In September and October each bouquet demanded hours of search. These floral symphonies expressed what no declaration could; my baffled desires found refuge as Beethoven finds it in notes. In their presence the countess was my Henriette. Such neutral pleasures soothed me: abstinence exhausts, and little crumbs of heaven stave off disaster.

Throughout the month I often saw her at the window when I arrived. If late, she would wander on the terrace to meet me and tend her youngest child. The harvest began in late September: the vintage brought abundance and hilarity, coopers and carts, girls with panniers. For the day classes were equal; everyone shared the harvest. Jacques and Madeleine, who had never seen a vintage, were delighted. We went to Villaines for baskets; Jacques made me swear to stay at Clochegourde. The day in the vineyard was glorious—the children’s chatter, the mother’s laughter. She passed an arm around my neck and tapped my cheek—the only caress she had ever given me. I filled my basket and emptied it into the vat, moving slowly in regulated motion that kept my spirit steady. I found the pleasure of external labor that regulated passion; I learned monastic discipline.

For a while the count was neither surly nor cruel. His son’s rosy face pleased him; he had promised a dance to celebrate the Bourbons’ return. Returning, the countess leaned on my arm and whispered, “You bring us happiness.” Burdened with knowledge of her sleepless nights and care, that was the supreme praise. “The terrible monotony of my life is broken; all things are radiant with hope,” she said. “Oh, never leave me! Be the elder son, the protector of the younger.”

We had another fête to accustom the children to practical life: Jacques was given the walnuts’ proceeds, Madeleine the chestnuts. Such innocent pride amid autumn’s fading colors was charming. I learned how in ten years the countess had re‑organized the estate—crop rotation, dividing it into four farms on share leases; she had induced the count to create a model farm and to rebuild two farmhouses for money rents later. She feared dying before her husband and sought to secure their income; fruit trees planted in the last decade were bearing, hedges and elms had grown. With new purchases and her farming system, Clochegourde, divided into four farms, might bring in forty thousand francs a year, not counting vineyards and woodlands. The new road to Chinon would attract tenants. She wished to invest fifteen thousand francs in each new estate to improve houses for better rents—thirty thousand francs total—which she and her husband argued about. Her fear—“If I die tomorrow, what will become of them?”—drove her. Common sense prevailed. She went to the Cassine and the Rhetorière to plan buildings; carriers and honest tenants were ready. In two years Clochegourde might yield twenty‑four thousand francs. Gravelotte in Maine was rented for seven thousand a year; his pension was four thousand. Later additions might let her go to Paris and arrange Jacques’ education.

When she said “Paris!” I knew she did not wish to be separated from her friend. I told her I would finish my education and work to be Jacques’ tutor. She looked grave. “No, Félix,” she said. That could not be, any more than my priesthood. She thanked me as a mother but, as a woman who loved me, could never allow me to be victim to my attachment: such a position would socially disgrace me—her father would never forgive such humility. “You must accept society as it is; never fly in its face,” she said. I whispered “Love.” She answered, “No, charity,” fighting tears. “This wild idea enlightens me as to your character; your heart will be your bane. Let my woman’s eye see for you. From Clochegourde’s solitudes I will share your successes. As to a tutor, do not fear; we shall find a good old abbé; my father will devote sums to the boy’s education. Jacques is my pride; he is eleven.”

At the Cassine Jacques, Madeleine and I followed her like children. I spoke with Martineau and learned how beloved Henriette was among the people. I gave a laborer all the money I had.

A pony arrived for Jacques; his father, an excellent horseman, would accustom him. On the first lesson, with the household clustered round, the mother burst into tears and leaned on my arm: “I think I have never suffered. Do not leave us today.” The child leapt into her arms; we arranged bouquets for dinner. She said, “The fifteenth of October is a great day for me. Jacques has taken his first riding lesson, and I have just set the last stitch in my furniture cover.”

“Then, Blanche,” the count joked, “I must pay you for it.” Returning, she whispered anxiously, “I am too happy; happiness is like illness—it overwhelms me; I fear it may vanish.” I loved her so passionately I felt jealous. She asked what my eyes had said; I told her. She was more touched than by any gift, then poured comfort into my heart. “Love me as my aunt loved me,” she said. “If I take your life, must I not be grateful?” She added that stitching had been her resource: while sewing she confided her secrets to every stitch, as I did with my bouquets.

Dinner that evening was lovely; Jacques offered his garland to his mother with grace; we played backgammon; the count was charming. It was a day of days for the poor woman.

Soon the riding lessons became contentious. The countess feared the father’s harsh reprimands; Jacques grew thin with worry and dark circles. I counselled him to tell his father when tired, but the old huntsman took over. Angry reproaches resumed. Then the cost of the Cassine and Rhetorière buildings exceeded estimates by half. The news, given first to Monsieur de Mortsauf rather than his wife, sparked a quarrel that escalated until the count’s malady reappeared.

That afternoon I had been searching for flowers with Madeleine. Returning, I could not find her and heard cries. Madeleine ran out: “The general,” she cried, “is scolding mamma; go and defend her.” I entered the salon unseen. Monsieur de Mortsauf was in a violent tirade.

“Never marry, Félix,” he shouted. “A woman is led by the devil; the most virtuous would invent evil if it did not exist; they are all vile.” He blamed her for imaginary faults, cursed the new system, and declared he could have been twice as rich had he managed differently. He sprang about, displacing furniture, then moaned of pain and accused his wife of ruining him. “Yes, Blanche,” he cried, “you are my executioner; you are killing me; you are a monster of hypocrisy. She is smiling! Do you know why she smiles, Félix?” I kept silent.

“That woman denies me all happiness; she is no more to me than to you, and yet she pretends to be my wife! She fulfills none of the duties of laws, human and divine; she lies to God and man. She puts all her art into keeping me away; she has made me mad through privations; she is killing me by degrees, and she thinks herself a saint and takes the sacrament every month!”

Henriette wept, answering only, “Monsieur! monsieur! monsieur!” The words burned me with shame for him and anger because they struck at chastity and delicacy—the warp of first love. “She is virgin at my expense,” he cried. The countess cried out “Monsieur!” Again enraged, he bellowed that he was her master. He advanced; Henriette slid from her chair to avoid a blow, fell full‑length and lost consciousness. The count stopped bewildered. I took her in my arms; the count opened her bedroom door. We laid her, still dressed, on the bed. When she revived she motioned to unfasten her belt; the count found scissors; I gave her salts and she opened her eyes. Ashamed, the count left the room. Two hours passed in silence. Henriette’s hand lay in mine; she pressed it but could not speak. Once she rose on her elbow and whispered, “Unhappy man!—ah! if you did but know—” then sank back. The memory of past sufferings and the present shock threw her into convulsions I calmed by an instinctive magnetism. I smoothed her hair—the first time I touched it—and sat looking about her simple room: chintz curtains, a crucifix, her aunt’s portrait, children’s drawings with locks of hair. What renunciations of luxury for such a woman—the chamber of a married nun, filled with holy resignation. The painful sight exposed a hidden, irreparable woe.

When the children and Manette came in I left. The count waited for me; he seemed to seek me as mediator. “Stay, stay with us, Félix!” he implored. I replied that Monsieur de Chessel had a party and my absence would cause remark, but I promised to return after dinner. He left the house with me and took me to the lower gate.

Reading the letter, I felt a maternal warmth under my fingers that sharply contrasted with my own mother's coldness; no wonder the countess had forbidden me to open it in Touraine, fearing I might break down. Soon after I met my brother Charles again — haughty, distant, lecturing on social matters — and only my deep love kept me from feeling foolish. Childhood sorrows and solitude had sharpened my perception of affection, and since returning from Clochegourde I judged genuine attachment more clearly.

I often visited the Duchesse de Lenoncourt, where Henriette's name was seldom spoken; only the old duke, whose welcome eased my shyness, seemed to understand our bond. Just as I was enjoying society again, the events of March 20 forced a change. At Henriette's advice I followed my brother to Ghent, accompanying the Duc de Lenoncourt. The duke's amiability protected me when he perceived my loyalty to the Bourbons and he introduced me to the king, who valued my steadfastness. Then a letter from Madame de Mortsauf told of Jacques' illness; her distress compelled me to help. Setting aside rivalry with my brother, I left the court to serve the sovereign as a reliable messenger, eager to return to Clochegourde.

After carrying out the king's orders and evading Bonapartist authorities, I travelled through the Vendée to Clochegourde. Jacques was better; the count welcomed me, and in the salon I found Henriette, surprised and joyful. She prepared my room, the count saw to my food, and at dinner we learned of Waterloo's disasters and Napoleon's flight — news that did not dim my concern for Henriette. She had even had an ice-house built for me; that simple, thoughtful effort revealed a tenderness that moved me. Over the week she regained health and spirit; absence had only deepened my love.

Henriette spoke plainly of long nights at Jacques' bedside, prayers and exhausted vigilance, and of the count's harshness that left her wondering why her words never pleased him. Still she clung to duty, urging her children to respect their father despite the strain. She described their agricultural success with practical wisdom and a peculiar knack for foreseeing matters about her children, which she attributed to divine intervention. I admired her counsel and felt gratitude.

She encouraged me to pursue opportunities in Paris, stressing availability to the king. Our conversations deepened our bond, yet the tension in her marriage was constant: she feared the count's behaviour threatened the children and hesitated to assert herself. When his health declined we alternated caring for him, a shared duty that drew us closer; his slow recovery brought back erratic behaviour, testing her patience while she kept putting her children's needs first.

Eventually I returned to Paris, absorbed in responsibilities but faithful to Henriette. Our correspondence sustained us; her letters revealed both love and the heavy burden she bore. News of Jacques' relapse later brought palpable pain; I felt powerless yet determined to remain devoted. Throughout, Henriette's resilience and maternal devotion shone — she navigated obligations with courage, often sacrificing personal happiness for her family — and my loyalty grew with her suffering. Our love remained a rare, guiding light I cherished and hoped would endure.

During a deeply emotional period I met a remarkable woman at the Élysée-Bourbon salons: immensely wealthy, of noble lineage and married to a distinguished British peer. Her beauty, grace, and wit made her the idol of Parisian society; she presided with a firm, charming manner.

The English, distant and reserved, live by strict laws and rigid virtues, producing an Englishwoman who, though delicate and charming, is often trapped by hypocrisy and compelled to maintain appearances; her passion is intense but constrained. You may envy these women, but they are complex and convinced that love alone suffices.

My liaison with the Marchioness of Dudley became notorious. At a time when my better feelings were subdued, I was drawn to her; my fidelity to Madame de Mortsauf only increased her desire. Lady Dudley craved the chase, and my resistance inflamed her passion. Her mocking remark about my relationship with Madame de Mortsauf—“I am wearied to death of these turtle-dove sighings”—should have warned me.

Men have fewer means to resist women than women have to escape men. Social codes keep men from appearing harsh; women are expected to be coy. Though I loved Madame de Mortsauf, I was not immune to Lady Dudley’s beauty and rank. Her attention in society stirred me; she often hinted she would be my mistress. After one beguiling evening she made her intentions clear, provoking scandal in the British aristocracy.

Seemingly fragile, Lady Dudley possessed a strong will. Her physical courage was remarkable—she could outpace any horse—and she pursued her desires relentlessly. By contrast, Madame de Mortsauf was nurturing and expansive, drawing others into her warmth; Lady Dudley was intense and consuming. English customs reflected a materialist bent, valuing comfort and order often at the expense of spiritual depth. Lady Dudley embodied that materialism: compelling physically but leaving an emptiness I did not feel with Madame de Mortsauf, whose love was spiritual and boundless.

My letters to Madame de Mortsauf went unanswered, and when I returned to Clochegourde her demeanor had altered—distant, sorrowful, her beauty dimmed by grief. The count, unaware of the cause, welcomed me warmly; she remained aloof. I realized the depth of my wrongdoing. The count confessed his fears; Madame de Mortsauf, devoted to her children, seemed fragile. I sought her forgiveness but was rebuffed, told I had caused too much pain. Left to grapple with guilt, torn between Lady Dudley’s material allure and Madame de Mortsauf’s nurturing devotion, I confronted the true nature of love and sacrifice: my desires carried duties, and the harm I’d caused weighed heavily on me.

I know nothing more destructive than the wit of an Englishwoman; it carries the pompous certainty with which the British mask their prejudices. French wit is lace, adorning joys and quarrels; English wit is corrosive, stripping flesh until only bone remains. Arabella wielded her power like a sultan, cutting down rivals with her words.

She addressed me in a torrent of flattery and moral sophistry. She declared her love both natural and pious: God made beauty to inspire adoration, she said, and the true crime would be not to love. Where another woman measured love by duty, Arabella exalted it into something divine, insisting my being “above” ordinary morality made her passion a weakness rather than a sin. She contrasted herself favorably with Henriette, whom she portrayed as practical and worldly; while Henriette cultivated estates, Arabella cultivated my heart. Her speech sought to erase the dignified impression of Henriette’s love by overwhelming me with impetuous affection.

The next morning she was tearful, confessing that love might ruin her; she had given everything and feared being abandoned for that other woman. She forced vows from me, and once reassured she dried her eyes: if I returned to Henriette it must be by my own will, otherwise I should be hers forever. Under her influence I went back to Clochegourde. Then, as now, the falsity of my conduct did not strike me; had I refused Arabella, Lady Dudley would have found in Henriette a triumph; if I had gone to Paris with Arabella, I would at least have been honest in deserting the countess. Instead, my return to Clochegourde insulted Madame de Mortsauf and, worse, betrayed my true love. I convinced myself a loving woman could forgive nothing: better to see a lover dead than happy with another. Lady Dudley knew this and had schemed well.

When I met the countess she was pale, dejected, and like one who had ceased to sleep. I saw actions that the world shrugs at but which lofty spirits call criminal. Henriette’s illusions were broken; her heart, crushed. She would not look at me. Where before there had been light, now there was a chilly distance. She refused to admit what she felt, calling me her brother and hiding her suffering under ostentatious attentions that humiliated me—tending me as one tends the convalescent. We had nothing to say; after five years of intimacy we were strangers. I was ashamed; I cursed the pleasure Arabella had given; I wished Henriette would demand my blood. I could not, in her presence, tear Arabella apart, for speaking of her would have made Henriette despise me. So we pretended.

Henriette put on cheerfulness, but it was a mask. She declared “I suffer more than you,” and when I answered that I suffered because I was to blame she turned away, and her coldness made me resolve to leave Clochegourde. That evening we walked together for the last time. At the grove marking the boundary she embraced me with the certainty of one who has seen the future: she foresaw our parting and the desolation it would bring. Her words were fierce and tender—she told me to take the place of their mother for Jacques and Madeleine; she begged me to save myself and swear I would be faithful. Alarmed by the look in her eyes, I commanded her to live, to obey Origet, to trust me; jealousy and pain drove her to promise obedience, and I kissed her eyelids. A momentary reconciliation erased the memory of Arabella. For a time I was the toy of two irreconcilable passions: an angel and a demon, two women equally beautiful but opposite—one a repository of virtues I often despised, the other the idol of my selfishness. Seeing Henriette surrounded by her children, I felt a guilty pride in inspiring such passion that the abandonment of either must be deadly; that conceit punished me.

I do not know why I remained with Arabella, expecting some eventuality—perhaps the count’s death—to reopen the way to Henriette. Back in Paris we grew intimate quickly and abandoned the restraints I had tried to keep. Arabella wanted me shown to all Paris as her “sposo,” and she used her gifts to keep me in her house. Once immersed in our illegal union, despair seized me. I saw my life bound to a course that offended Henriette’s ideals. From then on I lived in a rage, as consumptives live in agitation: no corner of my heart was free from pain. Letters I wrote to Henriette described this moral malady and compelled her to answer with sorrowful reproofs. “At the cost of so many treasures lost, I wished you to be at least happy,” she wrote. But I was not happy. Happiness is absolute; comparisons after the first ardor revealed the faults and incompatibilities I had ignored. I began to examine Lady Dudley critically and found many flaws.

She lacked the transforming capacity of a Frenchwoman in love. A Frenchwoman, when in love, takes a new shape: coquetry and surrender refine her, she espouses her lover’s interests, becomes mother, adviser, doctor, and never halves her soul between two spheres. An Englishwoman, shaped by icy manners and public expectation, can be passionate in private but cold in the world; she puts love on like a garment and later discards it. Lady Dudley could not live without the world; the English system of two lives was natural to her. She loved madly at times, but publicly she kept an impenetrable mask: reticent, composed, ambassador-like. Where Henriette’s smallest movements—gown’s rustle, lowered eyelids, musical voice—were a continual caress, Arabella’s love existed for spectacle and sarcasm; she loved to rend and bite, to make sport of my feelings. She wanted to exhibit me, to be amused by cutting me, to knead my vanity. She was insatiable for excitement and fame; everything concerned herself, not me. She was prodigal for herself without generosity; to save me a pang, Henriette would have sought for me what she would never seek for herself. The contrast extended to outward things: French luxury expressed the man and cultivated intimacy; English luxury, at first alluring, was mechanical and served the self. Arabella treated domestic affections as servants’ business; a servant’s death would have affected her less than money. Gradually the stony rock beneath her beauty revealed itself: Arabella’s love became intolerable.

Weariness took me when memories of Clochegourde—roses, terrace, nightingales—returned. While working in the king’s cabinet, the Duc de Lenoncourt shockingly told me the countess was dying. I begged leave to go; the king’s glance reproached me for my divided life. On the road I met Origet, who told me she was indeed dying—of grief manifesting as inanition. Her disease, he said, was beyond medicine: the stomach had rejected nourishment for forty days. “Her sufferings are dreadful,” he said; “young, beautiful, and rich, she dies emaciated—grief has done the work of a dagger.” The news pierced me: she was dying through me. I felt compassed by a conscience that called me murderer of the soul, by a human justice that forgives open crimes but overlooks slow, hidden ones. I arrived at Clochegourde in remorse.

The scene was somber: Jacques, Madeleine, and the Abbe Dominis kneeling at a wooden cross; servants dumb with grief. The count, white-headed, burst between despair and blame; the house seemed uninhabited. The abbe told me the countess’s illness had alternations of rage and repentance: “To heal the body the heart must first be healed,” the doctor had said, but the heart was ravaged. The countess had gone from bitter invocations for life to saintly resignation—each fall made her rise higher toward heaven. I begged to see her, but was told she must be prepared lest the meeting increase her suffering.

When at last I entered, Henriette sat in white before the fireplace, flowers about her. Her face, shrunken and waxen yet with inner fire, was greenish like a magnolia; her smile was the strained smile of one near death. She knew she was altered and met me with bitterness and some irony. She greeted me with: “Ah, my poor Felix, this is death—and you do not like death.” She accused me gently: my coming had brought the spectacle of her end and would not please Lady Dudley. Then, changing, she clung to life, declaring she would recover—to ride, to go to Paris, to know fêtes and pleasures. Those exclamations bespoke the anguish of true love lost. She flung her arms about my neck and kissed me violently before fainting; the doctor revealed it had been forty-two days since she had eaten. The abbe begged to leave her to the doctor, to opium, for the last sacraments. But I felt the meeting was a penance—my life was a failure.

I sat in a punt to be alone, and thought of the life I had rejected: had I not been called to the simple life she offered, to greatness given to be saved from shame? I saw Arabella’s version of love as abhorrent. Outside, Madeleine walked with averted head and entered the house without comfort; she would not forgive me, believing me her mother’s murderer. Jacques’s face was sullen and resolved; he showed me a bloodstained handkerchief and spoke of following his mother. Everything at Clochegourde was ruined.

That evening, opium wrapped Henriette in a sleep that could be deathless. At sunset her face cleared; suffering’s green gave way to a pure pallor, and she seemed to embrace the divine. Madeleine knelt, adoring; Jacques, smiling, seemed resolved to follow her. We knelt. The doctor signaled it was time for the last sacraments. At nine she awoke with gentle eyes and a returning beauty. “Mother! you are too beautiful to die,” cried Madeleine; Henriette answered: “Dear daughter, I shall live—in thee.” She smiled and blessed us with a serenity that transfigured her.

Later, after confession, the countess sat up dressed in the long white garment of penitence. She had burned her letters—an act she delayed until this hour, then performed willingly. In calm lucidity she spoke to me, pressing my hand: she asked me to stay and not be absent from even the least painful thing that concerned me. She knelt and, with a dying woman’s frankness, confessed that though she had been a faithful wife, she had sometimes failed in duty: she had given a friendship outside her family a care more affectionate than she gave her husband. “Perhaps I have irritated you by comparisons,” she said; “I have had a deep friendship which no one—not even he who has been its object—has fully known. Though virtuous by human laws and an irreproachable wife, other thoughts, voluntary or involuntary, have crossed my mind, and in this hour I fear I welcomed them too warmly. But having loved you and continued submissive, I now pray for your blessing with a clean heart.” She asked the count to forgive her, and then—turning to me—she made a last, large-hearted request: she begged that if I would not marry Madeleine, I would at least seek the soul’s repose of her husband by doing what I could to make Monsieur de Mortsauf as happy as possible. She wished to leave to me the pledge of her affection as a kind of adopted filial tie; she implored me to be kind to his future happiness and to return often to the valley where she would be buried.

Those words broke my self-absorption. Her last tenderness—thinking of my welfare and her husband’s peace—tore away the veils of my egotism. She was blind to her daughter’s hatred; she bestowed upon me Clochegourde and her daughter’s future in a final act of grace. I fell into abyssal reflection as the light from her life died: my clouds of selfishness rolled away; she had suffered as much as I—perhaps more, for she was dying. From that dread day the world lost warmth for me; a part of her has remained inside me ever since. All that is good in me now issues from that grave; my sarcasm and bitterness remain my faults.

In the days after, Henriette’s letter—her clear foresight and magnanimity—set before me the only star of hope: to live at Clochegourde with Madeleine and consecrate my life to that care. But it was necessary to know Madeleine’s true feelings. On the terrace, as the count spoke of his fear of being left alone and of the struggle between father and daughter that might end his days in bitterness, I begged him to fetch her. When alone with her, I pleaded for charity in her mother’s name: if her mother still had influence, I asked that she not banish me from Clochegourde; the sight of me, I said, caused me a pain I could not express.

Madeleine stopped me: with a trembling voice she returned my appeal in mirror image. She said she knew all my thoughts and would not change her feelings; in her mother’s name she begged me never to return while she was there. “The sight of you causes repugnance I cannot overcome,” she said, and left without looking back. The young girl’s acute gaze had discovered her mother’s secret friendship and judged me guilty. Her hatred was implacable. The edifice of my life fell in ruins: Madeleine condemned me without probing cause or victim; I alone had known the inner life of that unknown woman, and no one would measure her depths. Many families, I reflected, have their Henriette—noble feelings that leave earth with no historian.

Broken, I left with a heavy heart and took the road to Paris, following the Indre. The house of Clochegourde had given me the purest and the most destructive of lessons: the cruel power of wit and fashion, the serene sanctity of patient virtue, the horror of self-deceit, and the slow vengeance of grief. The rest of my life would be shadowed by that valley, the smell of roses and nightingales, and by the memory of a woman whose last words were pardon and benediction toward the man she had loved and toward the enemy who had caused her end.
