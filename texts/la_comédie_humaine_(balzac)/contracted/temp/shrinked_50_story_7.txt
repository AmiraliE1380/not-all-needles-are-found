The incident occurred in late November 1809, at the height of Napoleon’s empire. Wagram’s shadow still haunted Austria as peace talks with the Coalition went on. Kings and princes orbited Napoleon, who staged Parisian entertainments around his marriage to an Austrian archduchess. The French aristocracy’s new opulence—diamonds and gold on women and uniforms—contrasted with the Republic’s earlier austerity; military leaders flaunted wealth gained through the sacrifices of countless soldiers.

Many adopted a carefree, Louis XV–style hedonism. Both sexes embraced pleasures that seemed to foreshadow doom. A craze for military men suited the Emperor, since frequent calls to arms made every peace temporary; women’s affections shifted rapidly, often from mistress to widow in weeks.

Soldiers’ appeal rested on prospects of early widowhood, claims to historical glory, or the romance of courage; women imagined passions buried on the battlefield. This bred a culture that prized bravery and glitter—diamonds, fireworks, and jeweled men like Murat.

Comte de Gondreville (formerly Citizen Malin) delayed a grand entertainment to outshine rivals and please Napoleon. Ambassadors and princes filled his drawing-rooms expecting the Emperor, but a private quarrel with Josephine, hinting at divorce, kept Napoleon away. Paris’s most beautiful women and the financial elite—eager to rival military splendor and trade wealth for favor—crowded the event, where smiles masked rivalry and mistrust.

These remarks are necessary to explain the incidents surrounding the little imbroglio that is the subject of this study, reflecting the tone prevalent in Paris drawing-rooms at the time.

At a brilliant ball in Madame de Gondreville’s salon a small group of men debate the identity of a pale, delicate young woman seated by a candelabrum, her chestnut hair arranged a la Chinoise and bluebells at her breast. Hidden in a shadowy corner yet unmistakably the fairest complexion in the room, she leans forward, her almond-shaped blue eyes seeming made for tears. The men—Montcornet, a Colonel of Cuirassiers; Martial de la Roche-Hugon, a young Maitre des Requetes; and other acquaintances—exchange the usual teasing hypotheses: prude or coquette, an aristocrat or a humble companion, a widow, the daughter of a German prince. Each theory reveals more about the gossips than about the woman: one insists she must be married, another that she is simply too youthful to be so; one proposes she’s the widow of a gambler—a common occurrence since the peace; another detects in her expression an intention to charm this very evening and wagers she is already in love.

Montcornet delights in provoking Martial, arguing that Parisians jealously keep unknown beauties to themselves and that the ladies surrounding the stranger would conspire, by glances and silences, to keep men from taking her out. The group’s banter is punctuated with bursts of comic gallantry and professional rivalry. Martial, favored by Napoleon and of winning manners, is mocked as a lover of conquest despite his promising diplomatic career; Montcornet, a tall, vigorous soldier with an officer’s air of superiority, is admired for his shape and bearing in the fashionable white kerseymere breeches and the cuirassier’s uniform. Their friendly raillery ranges over social ranks and motives: is she an aristocrat or a companion in fine lace and sapphires? Montcornet even halts a portly, sharp-eyed man—Comte de Gondreville—asking if he knows the lady; Gondreville only suggests she is some friend of his wife’s, or at most one of the Comtesse’s whimsical invitations.

Montcornet then warns Martial that Madame de Vaudremont has been watching him with ominous attention and might divine their whispered scheming. Martial, conscious of his role as the fiancé-designate of the young, wealthy Countess de Vaudremont, teases and defies the Colonel’s warning. He will not surrender his liberty, he laughs, though the Colonel reminds him he risks losing a secure match if he allows himself to be a seducer. The lawyer’s complacent insouciance—his polished thumb-nail clicked under a tooth—reveals his ease with such juggling of attachments; he is careful to hide his ambition beneath a cultivated mediocrity. Montcornet taunts him with the absurdity of playing the Lovelace while betrothed to a widow of twenty-two with four thousand napoleons a year and a glittering diamond on her finger.

This little drama in the foreground of the party is soon overshadowed by the grand entrance of the most admired woman in Paris: Madame de Vaudremont, who makes it a rule to arrive only when a party has reached its highest pitch—never showing herself when flowers are drooping or faces are tired. She appears at about eleven with the Comte de Soulanges, a celebrated officer of artillery and a favorite of the Emperor. Her arrival halts conversation and draws a pressing circle of curiosity. Women whisper, men hurry in from inner rooms; the salon brightens as everyone looks to see how her dress and bearing outshine the rest. Madame de Vaudremont is tall, dignified, with dazzlingly white skin and a gracious manner that promises beauty fulfilled. Soulanges, in his thirties, is slender and pale, with black eyes full of vivacity but taciturn in society—a man whose oratorical gifts would later appear in political life.

Martial watches them approach with anxious energy. Though the Countess acknowledges Soulanges with a coldly polite smile and sits near Madame de Gondreville, Martial feels a prick of jealousy at the Count’s nearness and the thought of being displaced. He posts himself near the fireplace, where he can observe the Countess and Soulanges without drawing notice. Yet his composure is strained; when Soulanges remains standing after Vaudremont sits, an air of awkwardness passes between them, and the Comte’s pale face suddenly flushes scarlet. Without a word, Soulanges moves away and, melancholy and embarrassed, passes Martial to shelter himself in a cardroom. The company interprets this as the Count’s retreat before a possible public ridicule; Martial, emboldened, raises his head and looks toward the pale stranger by the candelabrum.

As Martial takes a seat near Madame de Vaudremont, he is so absent-minded that he misses her low-spoken plea about his ring—she asks him, behind her fan, not to wear the ring he took from her, saying she will explain later and requesting his arm to go to another hostess’s salon. He answers distractedly, and Vaudremont’s vexation shows; but the conversation between the men and the whisper of rivalry continues. The little blue lady becomes the magnet of renewed curiosity, attracting the eyes of the Colonel, Soulanges, Martial, and the Countess.

While Martial dances a brilliant quadrille with the Countess, half-lost in the turmoil and scents and trappings of the ball, Colonel Montcornet crosses the room, from group to group, in pursuit of information about the unknown woman. Finding a temporary opening of empty chairs near the candelabrum, he presses forward through the palisade of shawls and wraps, making his way close to her. Tactful and deliberate, he begins with a vapid conversation to the left-hand neighbor to provoke some response; the plan is to coax the stranger into speech. But the lady receives his bland compliments in silence. At last he addresses her directly, gently asking if she is married. She answers simply, “Yes, monsieur,” and says her husband is present. When asked why she remains in the shadow if she aims at attracting attention, she smiles sadly and declines to dance: “I do not intend to dance, monsieur.”

Her curt refusal discourages the Colonel enough to retire, though Martial, who has glimpsed the exchange, smiles and lets his diamond glitter. Vaudremont, noticing Martial’s distraction, scolds him for his inattentiveness; but the Baron is inwardly amused at the Colonel’s rebuff. The stranger, watching the Countess, comments on Martial’s diamond and asks the Colonel if the Baron is a fickle man and if he is on good terms with Madame de Vaudremont. When the Colonel replies that Martial is indeed close to the Countess, the unknown woman turns pale and admits she had thought Vaudremont long devoted to Soulanges. At mention of Soulanges, she says that she saw him earlier; in that moment the Colonel decides she must be in love with that “lucky devil” Martial.

This confession, or the look that prompted it, quickens the wagerings of men. Montcornet withdraws to the window-bay with Martial. Montcornet, a man of honor, refuses to court a woman who will not be his; he prides himself on decency and tells Martial he will not pursue one he cannot marry. Martial, with the coxcomb’s audacity, bets a hundred napoleons to Montcornet’s gray horse that he will dance with her that evening. The Colonel accepts for sport, and, still curious, says he will look for Soulanges, whom the stranger seemed interested in and who might therefore know her. Martial laughs, declaring Montcornet has lost already because his eyes have met hers and he knows her meaning if anyone does.

Montcornet indeed follows the instinct to find Soulanges. In the cardroom he finds the Count at a bouillotte table, amid a circle of men. There is little intimacy between the two soldiers beyond professional comradeship, but Montcornet is alarmed: Soulanges, prudent by nature, is playing with a frenzy that threatens ruin. The table bears heavy piles of gold and banknotes; men rise, cleaned out by the game. Others watch silently, speaking only in terse bets. As Montcornet approaches he sees Soulanges sweeping in a quantity of gold and notes—winning—but his face is more melancholy and scornful than joyful. He collects his stakes with a lip that curls in bitter defiance of fortune rather than with gratitude. The scene leaves Montcornet uneasy: the Count appears to gamble not for gain but to satisfy some inward tumult.

Thus the evening’s scenes intertwine: a pale, sorrowful unknown under the candelabrum who refuses to dance; a famous beauty, Madame de Vaudremont, whose late entrance compels and judges the assembly; Martial, the vain but ambitious diplomat torn between conquest and a prosperous engagement; Montcornet, the honorable cuirassier who resents seduction in absentia; and Soulanges, the taciturn, brilliant soldier whose sudden agitation and retreat to the cardroom mark him with a mystery that may link him to the stranger. The salon’s playful conjectures, wagers, glances, and jealousies create the invisible bonds that will knot these characters together, making the night more than a round of quadrilles and triumphs of dress—a small drama of passion, reputation, and social ambition played out against the blaze of chandeliers and the murmurs of fashionable Paris.

“Courage,” said the Colonel. “Courage, Soulanges!” He tried to pull him from the gaming table. “Come with me. I have good news, but on one condition.”

“What is that?” asked Soulanges.

“That you will answer a question.”

The Comte de Soulanges rose, gathering his winnings into his handkerchief; his face was so fierce no one stopped him. The players seemed relieved at his departure.

“Those soldiers are always as thick as thieves at a fair!” a diplomat remarked, and a tired face returned, “When we say military men, we do not mean civil, Monsieur le Ministre.”

“My dear fellow,” Montcornet said, leading Soulanges aside, “the Emperor praised you this morning, and your promotion to field-marshal is certain.”

“The Master does not love the Artillery.”

“No, but he adores the nobility, and you are an aristocrat. He said that men who married in Paris during the campaign are not in disgrace. Well?”

Soulanges looked confused.

“And now I hope you will tell me if you know a charming woman sitting under a huge candelabrum----”

At the mention of the mysterious lady the Count’s face lit up; he seized the Colonel’s hand. “My dear General,” he said, voice transformed, “if anyone else had asked me that, I would have cracked his skull with this gold. Leave me. I feel more like blowing out my brains tonight than----I hate everything I see. This gaiety, this music, these stupid faces, all laughing, are killing me!”

“My poor friend!” Montcornet replied gently. “You are too vehement. What would you say if I told you that Martial is thinking so little of Madame de Vaudremont that he is quite smitten with that little lady?”

“If he speaks to her,” Soulanges shouted, trembling with rage, “I will thrash him flat, even if he’s in the Emperor’s lap!”

He sank into a chair; Montcornet, seeing him inconsolable, withdrew.

Back in the ballroom Montcornet noticed Madame de Vaudremont, agitated, and sat beside her.

“I wager something vexes you?” he said.

“A mere trifle, General. I want to go to the Grand Duchess of Berg’s ball, but first to the Princesse de Wagram’s. Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon amuses himself by flirting with the dowagers.”

“That’s not the whole secret. I bet a hundred louis you will stay here the whole evening.”

“Impertinent man!”

“You are afraid of seeing Martial at the feet—”

“Of whom?” she exclaimed, feigning surprise.

“Of that candelabrum,” he replied, glancing at the pale stranger and studying the Countess.

“You have guessed it,” she said, hiding her face. “Old Madame de Lansac, malicious as an old monkey, told me Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon is flirting with that stranger, who sits there like a skeleton at a feast. I would rather see a death’s head than that face, so cruelly beautiful and pale. She is my evil genius. Madame de Lansac, who comes only to watch while pretending to sleep, has made me miserable. Martial shall pay dearly for this trick. Urge him, since he is your friend, not to make me unhappy.”

“I just left a man who promises to blow his brains out if he speaks to that lady. And he is a man, madame, to keep his word. But I know Martial; such threats encourage him. Besides, we have wagered—” The Colonel lowered his voice.

“Can it be true?” asked the Countess.

“On my word of honor.”

“Thank you, my dear Colonel,” she said, invitingly. “Will you do me the honor of dancing with me?”

“Yes; but the next quadrille. During this one, I want to find out what will come of this little intrigue and ascertain who the little blue lady is; she looks intelligent.”

Pleased, Montcornet retired to watch. At such entertainments the older women like Madame de Lansac gather to witness young people’s affairs. The Duchess of Lansac, a shrewd, mischievous dowager from an earlier generation, read hearts as if reading a book: a frown, a quiver, the slightest curl of the lips revealed the soul’s impressions. From her large armchair she admired the young coquette who disguised annoyance and grief; Madame de Vaudremont felt as much sorrow as she feigned cheerfulness. She had believed Martial a man of talent who could adorn her life with power; now she saw her mistake. Her sudden passion caused acute pain, like the amputation of a healthy limb.

The old Duchess seized the opportunity to interfere. Her sardonic glance, like a torch in a tragedy’s climax, terrified Madame de Vaudremont, who suspected the dowager’s hand in the stranger’s presence. Tall, tastefully dressed, diamonds and rouge worn but eyes promising epigrams, Madame de Lansac’s politeness softened a mischievous mind. Her triumphant look across the room brightened the pale stranger under the chandelier; the alliance between the Duchess and the unknown did not escape Madame de Vaudremont’s practiced eye, and she resolved to uncover the mystery.

The Baron de la Roche-Hugon, having questioned the dowagers without learning the blue lady’s name, turned to the Comtesse de Gondreville, who said the lady had been introduced by the “ancient” Duchesse de Lansac. By chance he caught the intelligence in the Duchess’s look and, despite previous coldness between them, approached. The Duchess’s smile and ironic air revived Martial’s hopes.

“If the old witch pretends to be friendly,” thought the Baron, “she will play me a spiteful trick.” He addressed her: “Madame, you have, I am told, undertaken the charge of a very precious treasure.”

“Do you take me for a dragon?” said the old lady. “But of whom are you speaking?” she asked sweetly.

“Of that little lady, unknown to all, whom the jealousy of these coquettes has imprisoned in that corner. You, no doubt, know her family?”

“Yes,” said the Duchess. “But what concern have you with a provincial heiress, married some time ago, a woman of good birth, whom you men do not know; she goes nowhere.”

“Why does she not dance? She is such a pretty creature. May we conclude a treaty of peace? If you will tell me all I want to know, I promise to urge a petition for the restitution of the woods of Navarreins by the Commissioners of Crown Lands on the Emperor.”

The Baron’s shameless suggestion to petition for an estate—an appeal to the Duchess’s past influence—was trickery revealing his ambition. The younger branch of Navarreins bears the arms of Lansac; the duchess’s past favors had once secured her husband a title. With deceptive gravity she answered, “Monsieur, bring the Comtesse de Vaudremont to me. I promise to reveal the mystery of the interesting unknown. You see, every man in the room has reached the same curiosity as yours. All eyes are turned towards the corner where my protégée has modestly placed herself; she reaps all the homage the women wished to deprive her of. Happy the man she chooses for her partner!” Fixing her eyes on Madame de Vaudremont she added, with a look that plainly said, “We are talking of you. I imagine you would rather learn the stranger’s name from the lips of your handsome Countess than from mine.”

There was such provocation in the Duchess’s tone that Madame de Vaudremont rose, approached and took the chair Martial had placed for her. Without acknowledging him she said, “I can guess, madame, that you are talking of me; but I admit my lack of perspicacity; I do not know whether it is for good or evil.”

Madame de Lansac pressed the young woman’s hand and replied low, “Poor child!”

The women looked at each other. Madame de Vaudremont understood that Martial was in the way and dismissed him with an imperious “Leave us.” The Baron, displeased to see the Countess under the spell of the dangerous sibyl, gave one of those decisive looks men use—potent over a blinded heart but ridiculous to a woman beginning to judge the man who attracted her.

Martial, a man of the world with wit and acumen, would not risk breaking with a woman favored at court whom the Emperor wished to see married. He counted on jealousy to discover the secret of her coolness and withdrew willingly as a new quadrille began.

The Baron leaned against a marble console, watching the two ladies talk. Comparing the Countess with the new beauty—made attractive by mystery—he fell prey to the common self-interest of adventurous lovers, hesitating between a fortune within reach and indulging his caprice. The light threw his anxious face against white silk hangings, making him resemble an evil genius; observers might have said, “There is another poor wretch who seems to be enjoying himself!”

Meanwhile the Colonel, in the doorway between ballroom and cardroom, laughed under his moustache. He enjoyed watching the dance’s turmoil, reading the Countess’s and Martial’s agitation, and wondering about the connection between Soulanges’ gloomy looks and the fair unknown’s face, which shifted between hope and dread. Montcornet, like a king of the feast, received inviting smiles; as Colonel of the Imperial Guard he was a fine match for society.

It was nearly midnight. Conversation, gambling, dancing, flirtations, rivalries and scheming reached a fervor that made a young man exclaim, “A fine ball!”

“My sweet little angel,” Madame de Lansac said to the Countess, “you are now at an age when I made many mistakes. Seeing you endure a thousand deaths, I thought I might give you some charity. To go wrong at two-and-twenty means spoiling your future; it is like tearing the gown you must wear. My dear, it is not much later that we learn to go about in it without crumpling it. Go on, making clever enemies and friends without sense of conduct, and you will see what a pleasant life you will lead!”

“Oh, madame, it is very hard for a woman to be happy, do you not think?” the Countess eagerly exclaimed.

“My child, at your age, you must learn to choose between pleasure and happiness. You want to marry Martial, who is not wise enough to be a good husband, nor passionate enough to remain a lover. He is in debt, my dear; he is the man to squander your fortune; still, that would be nothing if he could make you happy. Do you not see how aged he is? He must have been ill; he is making the most of what is left him. In three years, he will be a wreck. Then he will be ambitious; perhaps he may succeed. I do not think so. What is he? A man of intrigue, who may have business acumen and be able to gossip agreeably, but he is too presumptuous to have any real merit; he will not go far. Besides—look at him. Is it not written on his brow that, at this very moment, what he sees in you is not a young and pretty woman, but the two million francs you possess? He does not love you, my dear; he is calculating you as if you were an investment. If you are bent on marrying, find an older man with an assured position who is halfway in his career. A widow’s marriage should not be a trivial love affair. Is a mouse to be caught a second time in the same trap? A new alliance should be a good speculation on your part, and in marrying again, you ought at least to hope to be addressed as Madame la Maréchale!”

Her advice was practical and pitying: seek rank and security rather than a romantic match with Martial, whom she painted as indebted, aging, and opportunistic. The Countess listened, torn between feeling and worldly counsel. Around them the salon’s intrigues continued—wagers, furtive glances, jealous men and calculating women—while the mystery of the pale stranger and the designs of the Duchess, the Colonel, the Baron, and Martial rotated like the dance itself, each player seeking advantage among love, ambition, and social maneuvering.

As she spoke, both women turned to Colonel Montcornet’s handsome face.

The Duchess bluntly warned the Countess against flirting instead of marrying. She argued that brief vanities can destroy virtuous women and wreck families, urging genuine affection over fleeting passions. Citing her own regret and the Duc d’Albe’s remark that “one salmon is worth a thousand frogs,” she urged restraint and discretion: if one must toy with men, choose those with unsettled lives who have no duties. Then, pointing to a specific case, she accused the Countess of having ruined Soulanges’s happiness. For fifteen months the Countess had made him lovesick and neglectful of his wife; now the wife lived in tears and Soulanges felt remorse greater than any pleasure the affair had brought. “You’ve affected his whole life,” the Duchess said, and invited the Countess to witness the consequence.

Leading Madame de Vaudremont to the card-room door, the Duchess revealed a pale, trembling figure under the candlelight—her grandniece, the Comtesse de Soulanges—who had at last come at the Duchess’s persuasion, leaving the sorrowful room where even her child offered little consolation. The Countess looked away, struck by what happiness once was and what it had become. The sight of Soulanges himself—pale, rigid in an easy chair, his brow tense—confirmed the Duchess’s indictment. Players passed him as if he were dead; the image of husband and weeping wife separated amid festivity made the Countess fear for her own future. She pressed the Duchess’s hand in a childlike thanks.

Madame de Lansac, who had brought the niece, insisted that women could repel a man’s attentions as easily as they attracted them. She suggested the Countess could help restore the couple: choose a good husband and shut your door to her nephew. “A wife cannot accept her husband’s heart as a gift from another woman; she is far happier believing she has won it back,” the old lady lectured. By bringing her niece here she hoped to give the Comtesse a chance to regain Soulanges’s affection; all she asked of the Countess was to “play the Colonel.” She pointed to Montcornet; the Countess smiled at the implied role.

Left alone, Martial (the Baron) asked whether the Countess knew the unknown woman’s name; she confirmed it playfully. Believing she loved him, Martial affected charm and asked if she would be annoyed if he seemed to care about that name. She warned him that her lingering affection might make her refuse to tell him, and begged him not to approach the young lady—“It could be dangerous.” Martial insisted losing her favor would be worse than losing his life and pressed to dance with the Comtesse de Soulanges himself, taunting Montcornet with the paradox that the Colonel would leave alone the man who had taken another’s love yet would fight for his wife. The Countess retorted that Soulanges’s wife loved her husband, and Martial took the obstacle as a challenge. Their banter mixed jest and bitterness—he promised to desist and then, joking, to go win the other woman and return more devoted than ever.

Unaware of Madame de Vaudremont’s deeper designs and the ironic alignment of desires, the men were drawn together by mutual attraction. Meanwhile the lawyer approached the pale Comtesse de Soulanges; her husband entered the ballroom in a flash of anger. The Duchess, ever watchful, intercepted her nephew, took his arm, and pretended boredom to avoid a scene, giving her niece a knowing glance that seemed to signal revenge. Madame de Vaudremont, sensing duplicity in the Duchess’s moralizing, felt a surge of pride and curiosity that unsettled her; in her distraction the Colonel misread her embarrassment and became more ardent. Old diplomats watched, amused by so many concurrent intrigues—the passions of the two couples showed plainly in the crowded rooms and on surrounding faces.

Finally the Baron seated himself beside the Comtesse de Soulanges and admired her beauty—the fresh neck, the graceful figure emphasized by fashion. She replied coldly that she never danced at parties and was little known, unaware her aunt had signaled the Baron to engage her. At the same moment Martial, adjusting a diamond on his hand, caught the Countess’s eye and felt hope. She blushed and, when he asked if she enjoyed dancing, confessed she did. He offered to be her partner for the next quadrille; despite a shy protest that she had already refused a tall cavalry colonel, she accepted. Her tone hinted at a deeper emotion that touched Martial’s weary heart. Standing beside her as the quadrille formed, he intended to show all his charms; for him, as for many men, dance was a way to express and win a woman’s heart.

He led his conquest to the quadrille, where the most brilliant women prized dancing above all. As the orchestra played, the Baron watched the ladies form the square. Madame de Soulanges’ dress rivaled Madame de Vaudremont’s; men murmured their admiration and the blush that followed heightened her charm. When she raised her eyes she sought her partner’s opinion, blending innocence, vanity, and the budding affection of youth. Modest and inexperienced, she nevertheless knew when to glance at him.

A new figure by the dancer Trenis brought Martial face to face with the Colonel. The latter joked, “I have won your horse.” Montcornet retorted, “Yes, but you have lost eighty thousand francs a year!” Martial waved it off: “What do I care? Madame de Soulanges is worth millions!”

Whispers spread. Less attractive women moralized about Martial and the Comtesse; the more beautiful admired her ease. The men, puzzled by Martial’s luck, did not find him especially attractive. A few sympathetic women warned that a single glance or graceful dance could compromise a young wife. Only Martial grasped his happiness. During the last figure, as the ladies formed the moulinet, he clasped the Countess’s hand and felt her response through the thin glove. “Madame,” he said as the dance ended, “do not return to that corner where you’ve been hiding. Is admiration all you get from your jewels? Come and enjoy the scene — and yourself.”

Yielding to him, Madame de Soulanges moved through the rooms with Martial, hesitating at each doorway until he assured her, “He is not here.” They reached a grand picture gallery and later an oval boudoir overlooking the garden, draped with flowers and blue hangings. The noise faded. Startled at first, the Countess resisted, then sat on an ottoman and, reassured by a mirror, relaxed.

“This room is charming,” she said. “All here is love and delight!” Martial, moved, watched her: unease, modesty, eagerness—an expression that captivated him. She smiled and, flirtatious, took his left hand and slipped the ring from it: “What a fine diamond!”

Intoxicated by her touch, Martial urged, “Wear it, in memory of this hour, and for the love of—” He broke off, caught by her rapture. “You give it to me?” she asked. “I wish I had the whole world to give you!” he replied. “Will you accept only my diamond?” She pressed, “You will never take it back?” “Never,” he assured her. She placed the ring on her finger. As he moved to embrace her, she suddenly rose and said clearly, “I accept the diamond, monsieur, with less scruple because it belongs to me.”

Martial was taken aback. “Monsieur de Soulanges took it from my dressing-table and told me he had lost it,” she said. Martial protested that it had been given to him by Madame de Vaudremont. “Precisely,” she smiled. “My husband borrowed it, gave it to her, and she gifted it to you. This ring has made a little journey.” She touched a spring; a lock of M. de Soulanges’ hair appeared. Stunned, she fled into the crowded rooms, leaving Martial confounded.

He saw the Colonel and Madame de Vaudremont laughing nearby; the Colonel teased, “Will you have my horse to chase after your prize?” Martial took it in stride, grateful they kept silent about the evening’s events, feeling he had exchanged his horse for a young wife.

Driving home across Paris to the Faubourg Saint‑Germain, the Comtesse was consumed by anxiety. At the Hotel Gondreville she had failed to find her aunt and husband, who had left without her. She had long witnessed her husband’s torment since Madame de Vaudremont ensnared him and had hoped for his repentance; now she feared she had been wrong to follow her aunt’s scheme.

Crossing the Pont Royal, she discarded the hair from the diamond—the only token of pure affection—and wept, pondering how women sustain devotion when they do not love. At home she found her husband by the fire. He scolded her for attending balls alone; she softened him, saying only that her aunt had taken her and she had been happy. His smile and her tenderness reconciled them. He kissed her hand gratefully; gratitude, she thought, often accompanies love.

General Montcornet did not marry Madame de Vaudremont; she later perished in the fire that marred the ball hosted by the Austrian ambassador for Napoleon’s marriage to Joseph II’s daughter.
