Early in the autumn of 1826 the Abbé Birotteau, the chief figure of this tale, was caught in a downpour returning from an evening at a friend’s. Small, apoplectic and about sixty, he hurried across the deserted Cloister behind Saint-Gatien at Tours, anxious to keep his shoes dry because damp always brought gout. He bore the rain resignedly: he had won three francs ten sous at rubber with Madame de Listomère, wrapped himself in a canon’s fur cape, and was elated by talk that he might be named canon — a post he had long coveted.

The Cloister contained, on the Grand’Rue side, a group of cathedral houses that formed the narrow Rue de la Psalette. Opposite, a single house, its walls overshadowed by Saint-Gatien’s buttresses, had always been occupied by abbés and belonged to an old maid, Mademoiselle Gamard. Time had covered the cathedral in dingy mantles and the house in shadow; only those of absolute nullity or uncommon fortitude would choose such solitude. Devout and careful, Mademoiselle Gamard took priests to board and kept the dwelling—bought by her father in the Terror—without objection from the restored Church.

Birotteau had lived two years in that house and longed for its rooms as he longed for a canonry. His two great desires—being Mademoiselle Gamard’s boarder and becoming a canon—were the ambitions of a provincial priest who sought decent lodging, good food, clean garments and the self-esteem of a title. The apartment he inhabited had been the object of a passion begun when he first admired the rooms of his friend, the Abbé Chapeloud, and which grew into an obsession.

For years Chapeloud, who became canon when worship was restored under Napoleon, had occupied the south-facing apartment, furnishing and renovating it with modest legacies and gifts: an oak bookcase from a demolished château, waxed floors, painted woodwork, a long ebony table, Boulle cabinets, folios and Fathers of the Church. The rooms were transformed from shabby to dignified; an oratory and a salon with red damask and mahogany completed them. Birotteau, poor and the son of peasants like Chapeloud, admired these changes and secretly yearned to possess them. His desire increased until, when Chapeloud fell ill, he could not help thinking, “If Chapeloud dies I can have this apartment.” He never contemplated means to influence his friend’s will.

Chapeloud, indulgent and understanding his friend, forgave his covetousness, and on his deathbed, as Birotteau read the Quotidienne, said, “This time you will certainly get the apartment.” He bequeathed his library and furniture to Birotteau, who, though saddened by his friend’s death, found consolation in the inheritance and in Mademoiselle Gamard’s cheerful agreement to take him as a lodger. He moved into the apartment and enjoyed comforts he had long imagined: meticulous linen, fires, well-chosen wood, fragrant orris-root, and the small attentions for which Chapeloud had praised the spinster. Mademoiselle Gamard’s household had been famed, Chapeloud had often declared, for perfect order and attentive service.

Yet having obtained his long-desired comforts, Birotteau soon replaced the earlier obsession with a new hunger: the canonry. Hopes fed at Madame de Listomère’s conversations persuaded him promotion was near. Absorbed in these hopes, he did not notice until his return that he had left his umbrella and that some domestic attentions were missing. He found the outer door locked; Marianne, the servant, answered awkwardly that Mademoiselle had thought he was out; his candlestick had been moved to an antechamber; the fire in his room had not been burning; his slippers were not where they usually were. These trivial changes struck him as deliberate signs of displeasure. He concluded, by a sort of clerical logic, that Mademoiselle Gamard intended to chill and humiliate him. The small humiliations mortified him deeply.

Birotteau, kindly but simple-minded and ignorant of worldly calculations, lacked frank self-knowledge about what he had done to provoke dislike. He was a good man whose goodness verged on stupidity; his learning was shallowly acquired, and his life centered on mass and the confessional. He could not perceive how unconscious selfishness and provincial narrowness had made him difficult. In effect he was a clumsy egoist, unlike Chapeloud, who was a shrewd one.

Chapeloud had understood Mademoiselle Gamard: an old maid of about thirty-eight whose pretensions had hardened into strong self-esteem. The canon judged she needed constant, precise attentions if one wished to live comfortably under her roof. So he kept limited contact, avoided breakfast and supper, and came to dinner a few minutes early to pay polite compliments about the food and service. By these attentions, and by never creating points of contact beyond politeness, Chapeloud secured a peaceful coexistence and was praised as easy to live with.

Troubert, the other lodger, was entirely under her rule: obedient, patient and judged by some to have designs on her property because of their domestic intimacy. When Chapeloud died the landlady at first thought to offer the rooms to Troubert, but seeing Birotteau’s attachment she accepted him as lodger for her own advantage and even improved the rooms: replacing brick with wooden floors and rebuilding the chimney.

Yet Birotteau, dazzled by his new happiness, did not adopt Chapeloud’s circumspection. He fell into Mademoiselle Gamard’s life with the embarrassment and timidity of a man fearing to seem rude and who stays too long in conversation. He began to spend his evenings there, and the spinster, whose real desire had been to make her salon a weekly center of company, welcomed the chance to display more prominent visitors. Taking pride in entertaining, she boasted that thanks to Birotteau she could gather respectable company.

But the vicar soon deserted her salon for Madame de Listomère’s and Mademoiselle Salomon’s, preferring aristocratic circles to which the landlady did not belong. His withdrawal gravely wounded Mademoiselle Gamard; she had expected to use him to attract society. Her disappointment, joined to an old-maid’s craving for return on social investment and wounded pride, turned into resentment. The vicar, however, never understood this. He perceived, belatedly and clumsily, signs of slights—small omissions and unkindnesses—and concluded that ill will was directed at him though he could not see the reasons.

Mademoiselle Gamard, like many who never adapted their habits to changing lives, indulged a petty despotism over small things—where the basket of counters lay, the arrangement of cards, the order of household routine. Such trifles she magnified into tests of obedience. The vicar, unlike Chapeloud, could not accept being persistently cinched by such crochets without protest; he withdrew rather than submitting, irritating her more by his absence than by any direct opposition.

Her friends, defending her, called Chapeloud amiable and blamed Birotteau for turning away from the old maid; society at large often sided, covertly, with those who escaped a nightly round in the Cloister. Mademoiselle Gamard enjoyed the consolation she received, and Troubert explained the departure by saying the abbé sought fashion, luxury and witty talk—thus flattering the spinster by implying he had higher tastes.

In truth, Birotteau’s abandonment was a clumsy desertion, and his naïveté and lack of social tact made him gradually intolerable to Mademoiselle Gamard. Eighteen months after she had taken him in, when he mistook her silence for contentment, she had in fact begun a deliberate, underhand persecution. The locked door, the forgotten slippers, the unlit fire and the moved candlestick were the first signs. Birotteau, too innocent to see his own faults toward his landlady, could not understand why she had turned against him.

This prologue, recounting the small domestic developments that precede the greater drama, requires particulars. The little meannesses, jealousies and petty ambitions of bourgeois life inflict injuries as keen as those produced by grander interests, and their slow, minute growths needed this account to explain the coming catastrophe.

The next morning Birotteau woke thinking so much of his prospective canonry that he forgot the four circumstances of the previous night’s gloomy omens. He did not rise without a fire. He rang for Marianne and waited, sunk in his usual half-sleep musings. Marianne’s custom was to make the fire and rouse him by the gentle murmur of her movements—a sound he loved. Twenty minutes passed and she did not appear. Birotteau, now halfway to being a canon in his mind, was about to ring again when he heard steps on the staircase. After a discreet knock, the Abbe Troubert obeyed his invitation and entered. The two abbés, who visited one another monthly, were not strangers.

The canon immediately noticed the lack of fire and called harshly for Marianne. Then, turning to his ecclesiastical brother, he remarked, “If Mademoiselle knew you had no fire she would scold Marianne.” He inquired about Birotteau’s health and, gently, whether he had news encouraging hopes of his canonry. The vicar naïvely told of the people through whom Madame de Listomere was exerting influence, unaware that Troubert bore her a grudge for not admitting him—the same Troubert the bishop had twice proposed as vicar-general.

Troubert and Birotteau were contrasts in every line. Troubert was tall, lean, and yellow; Birotteau plump and ruddy. The canon’s long, deeply wrinkled face could assume sarcasm or contempt, though he rarely spoke and seldom laughed; a feeble smile would sometimes flit across his stern features. His habitual air of ambition, his severe aspect, and monumental cassock lent him an authoritative, imposing presence that made people cautious. Birotteau, by contrast, was expansive, frank, fond of good things, amused by trifles with the simple good nature of one without malice. He trotted through Saint-Gatien with a kindly smile; his manners marked him always as vicar and nothing higher.

Though Troubert’s aspect suggested ambition, he had by circumspect conduct, apparent lack of pressing desire for advancement, and a praiseworthy life removed the fears his presence had once stirred. His health had failed during the last year, and many thought he might soon be made vicar-general—his competitors hoping the chronic illness would delay any change long enough for their plans to mature. Birotteau, with a triple chin and robust appearance, seemed to others the very image of health; even his gout was, by proverb, a sign of longevity.

The Abbe Chapeloud—a sensible man whose amiability made him sought in Tours—had secretly opposed Troubert’s elevation, keeping him away from the best salons. Still, while Chapeloud lived, Troubert treated him with deferential respect. Chapeloud had warned Birotteau: “Distrust that lean stick of a Troubert,—Sixtus the Fifth reduced to the limits of a bishopric!”

This was the friend and habitual guest of Mademoiselle Gamard who, the morning after the old maid had, as it were, declared war on the poor vicar, came to visit and show friendship. “You must excuse Marianne,” said the canon when she entered. “I suppose she went first to my rooms. They are very damp, and I coughed all night. You are most healthily situated here,” he added, looking at the cornice.

“Yes; I am lodged like a canon,” replied Birotteau.

“And I like a vicar,” Troubert said humbly.

“But you will soon be settled in the archbishop’s palace,” Birotteau volunteered, wanting everyone to be happy.

“Yes, or in the cemetery, but God’s will be done!” Troubert raised his eyes to heaven with resignation. “I came,” he said, “to ask you to lend me the ‘Register of Bishops.’ You are the only man in Tours I know who has a copy.”

“Take it out of my library,” replied Birotteau, delighted to be useful and remembering this as the greatest happiness of his life.

The two abbés passed into the library while Birotteau dressed. When the breakfast bell rang the gouty vicar reflected that if Troubert had not come he would have had no fire to dress by. “He’s a kind man,” he thought.

They went downstairs together, each carrying a huge folio, which they set on the side table in the dining-room.

“What’s all that?” snapped Mademoiselle Gamard, addressing Birotteau sharply. “I hope you are not going to litter up my dining-room with your old books!”

“They are books I wanted,” replied Troubert. “Monsieur Birotteau has been kind enough to lend them to me.”

“I might have guessed it,” she returned with a contemptuous smile. “Monsieur Birotteau doesn’t often read books of that size.”

“How are you, mademoiselle?” Birotteau asked with his usual mild cordiality.

“Not very well,” she replied curtly. “You woke me up last night out of my first sleep, and I was wakeful for the rest of the night.” Then, sitting, she added, “Gentlemen, the milk is getting cold.”

Stupefied at such ill-nature from his landlady—whom he half expected to apologize—Birotteau, timid and alarmed at the prospect of a discussion, took his seat in silence. Observing Mademoiselle Gamard’s ill-humor, he felt a struggle between reason—telling him not to submit to discourtesy—and his instinctive desire to avoid a quarrel.

Inwardly miserable, he watched the familiar green lines painted on the oilcloth that eternally covered the table, and sat opposite Troubert in cane-seated armchairs, Mademoiselle Gamard occupying the head of the table from her high, cushioned chair near the stove. When his cup of sugared coffee arrived, the vicar felt chilled by the grim silence surrounding the usually social function of breakfast. He dared not look at Troubert’s dried features nor at the old maid’s threatening face, so he turned to the plethoric pug on a cushion near the stove—the household’s important though quiet personage.

“Well, my pretty,” he said, “are you waiting for your coffee?”

The pug turned his small fat eyes upon him and went back to sleep. To explain the poor vicar’s misery: he was one of those who believed talk aided digestion, and Mademoiselle Gamard, who shared that belief, had not refrained from talking at meals despite their coolness. For mornings past Birotteau had strained his mind to provoke her tongue. Their conversations were a perfect picture of provincial talk: gullible conjectures about Napoleon, the Dauphin, taxes, scandalous numbers of victims in the Revolution, and other absurd tales. He accepted Mademoiselle Gamard’s odd beliefs—eggs causing death in a year, bread curing sciatica, workmen dying after demolishing the Abbey Saint-Martin—because he liked her and was not skeptical.

On this occasion, however, Birotteau felt tongue-tied and ate in silence, thinking that silence might harm his digestion; he ventured, “This coffee is excellent.” The remark was wasted. Then, peering at a scrap of sky above the garden between the cathedral’s buttresses, he summoned courage to add, “It will be finer today than yesterday.” Mademoiselle Gamard threw a gracious look at Troubert and immediately turned with terrible severity upon Birotteau, who luckily was looking at his plate.

No one was a better exemplification of the elegiac nature of an old maid than Mademoiselle Sophie Gamard. To understand the influence she would exert on Birotteau’s last years, one must grasp what an old maid’s life and character often become: a person marked by social rejection and internal bitterness, producing rigidity and jealousy. Society condemns the woman with whom no man has chosen to share life; the old maid  bears a stigma that wounds her soul. If she is plain, people expect compensatory goodness of character; if she is handsome, people suspect some grave flaw. If her celibacy is deliberate, she is judged unfaithful to womanly devotion. In any case, never exercising the generous, attractive feelings of womanhood, she withers inwardly. Jealousy, resentment, and bitterness take root. Deprived of the practice of pleasing and being pleased, she grows selfish, narrow, and peevish; reproach and sometimes revenge replace gentleness. An old maid’s glance becomes oblique from fear and shame, and she can never forgive herself nor society.

Mademoiselle Gamard’s very appearance announced these truths. Dull gray eyes ringed with darkness, straight-lined wrinkles, a rigid forehead and prominent cheeks, scattered chin hairs she tolerantly allowed, thin lips over teeth too long though still white—her whole face bore the marks of inward conflict. Her hair had turned gray from frightful headaches and she wore a false front she did not fully conceal. Her gown, always brown, was tight for her angular figure; her collar lay limp, exposing red skin and a neck ribbed like oak-leaf veins. Her peasant origin, daughter of a wood-merchant who had risen, partly explained her roughness. Her features promised despotism rather than charm; her abrupt, constrained movements and a gait that seemed to advance “in a single block” announced her character. On occasion she would tell of chances to marry and the fortunate discovery of lovers’ lack of means—proving, unwittingly, that worldly judgment, not heart, guided her. In short, she was fatefully unsuited to the life of graceful womanhood.

Her room and the dining-room reflected her taste: grotesque Turkish landscapes on varnished paper, yellow curtains and furnishings in the salon, a mantel with crystal candlesticks and clock, and a private apartment rumored to be full of oddments dear to old maids. Habits replacing natural feminine activity, she had turned to petty intrigues, provincial cabals, and self-seeking schemes to give her life occupation. Birotteau, unhappily, had developed in Sophie Gamard the only sentiment possible for her—hatred—latent and now growing because spent on petty things in a narrow sphere. Birotteau, a man predestined to suffer because he could not see the causes of his suffering, became an object of her malice.

“Yes, it will be a fine day,” Troubert said after a pause, apparently returning to politeness.

Birotteau, frightened at how long silence had lasted during breakfast—the first time he had taken his coffee without speaking—left the dining-room with his heart squeezed as if in a vise. Feeling the coffee heavy, he walked among the narrow, box-edged garden paths that outlined a star. Turning after a round, he saw Mademoiselle Gamard and Troubert standing motionless on the threshold—Troubert with folded arms like a statue, she leaning against the blind door. Both seemed to be watching him and counting his steps. Nothing is so embarrassing to a timid person as being the focus of such close, hostile examination.

Fancied that he was obstructing their path, an idea born of fear and kindness, grew so strong that he left the garden and went to the church, abandoning thought of his canonry. Fortune provided distraction: several funerals, a marriage, and two baptisms at Saint-Gatien occupied him and eased his spirits. When his stomach reminded him of dinner, he glanced at his watch with alarm: it was minutes after four. Knowing Mademoiselle Gamard’s punctuality, he hurried home.

Passing the kitchen door he saw the first course removed. In the dining-room the old maid, in a tone mixing sour rebuke and joy at being able to blame him, said, “It is half-past four, Monsieur Birotteau. You know we are not to wait for you.” He looked at the clock and, by the way the gauze protecting it had been moved, saw she had opened the face and set the hands ahead of the cathedral clock. He could make no remark: any such observation would only provoke one of those fierce expositions to which she knew how to give vent. Mademoiselle Gamard instinctively divined the thousand petty annoyances a servant will sometimes force upon a master, or a woman upon a husband, and used them against Birotteau with malicious delight. Yet she so managed situations that, as far as appearances went, she was never in the wrong.

Thus, the little household dynamics—Birotteau’s timid goodness, Troubert’s reserved ambition and dry sarcasm, and Mademoiselle Gamard’s narrow vindictiveness—set the stage for the troubles that would follow.

Eight days after the events that opened this history, changes in the household and in the relations between the Abbé Birotteau and Mademoiselle Gamard made the former perceive a plot that had been growing for six months.

While the old maid’s vengeance had been covert and the vicar could shut his eyes to it, the harm was slight. But after the candlestick and the altered clock, Birotteau could no longer doubt that hatred watched him. From then on he fell into despair, seeing Mademoiselle Gamard’s skinny, clawlike fingers everywhere. She, delighting in a sentiment as pungent as revenge, hovered above him like a bird of prey preparing to pounce. She had long devised a plan the simple priest could not imagine, and now unfolded it with the petty ingenuity often shown by solitary people who, barred from large feelings, expend themselves on the details of outward devotion.

These trifling torments prevented Birotteau, effusive and seeking pity, from confiding in friends—another cruel aggravation. His timidity made him dread seeming ridiculous by speaking of such pettiness. Yet these petty things composed his life—busy about nothing, finding happiness in the absence of strong feeling. His paradise altered to hell; his terror of confronting Mademoiselle Gamard increased, and the secret distress blighted his health. One morning, while putting on his mottled blue stockings, he noticed his calves had shrunk. Terrified by such a symptom, he resolved to ask the Abbé Troubert to intervene officially between him and the landlady.

In Troubert’s bare, cheerless room the vicar, ashamed to bother a man so gravely occupied, after many internal agonies, explained his case. The canon listened coldly, suppressing an occasional smile. He blamed Birotteau’s failure to notice the trouble on his preoccupation with elevated thoughts, remarked that in former days recluses thought little of food and lodging, and suggested that modern priests can retire into the solitude of their hearts. He told Birotteau that he might act as arbitrator because his friendship with Mademoiselle Gamard had never exceeded the limits imposed by the Church, but justice required hearing both sides. Troubert professed to see no change in Gamard, attributing slight temper fluctuations to pulmonary suffering and recommending patience; if Birotteau stayed with her longer he would come to understand and esteem her better.

Birotteau left confounded. Unable to consult anyone, he judged Mademoiselle Gamard alone and fancied that by leaving her house for a few days he might extinguish her dislike. He resolved to spend a week at the country house of Madame de Listomère, a practice he had often followed. Ironically, this was exactly what Gamard wished; only patient endurance could have foiled her plan. The vicar, incapable of divining this, was doomed to fall.

Madame de Listomère’s country-place, the Alouette, near Tours, combined town and country charms. About ten days after his arrival there, while breakfasting, Birotteau was told Monsieur Caron wished to speak with him. Caron was Mademoiselle Gamard’s lawyer. Birotteau, agitated and surprised, learned Caron had been sent to ask how long he intended to remain in the country. The original agreement had not foreseen a long absence and might lead to contest. Gamard, anxious to avoid dispute, had sent Caron to understand his plans. The abbé asked for time to take advice.

Back at table, Birotteau described his trouble. The company, bored with country-house monotony, eagerly took sides against the old maid. Madame de Listomère surmised Troubert wanted Birotteau’s apartment; Monsieur de Listomère urged the vicar to resist Gamard. An old landowner, Monsieur de Bourbonne, suggested something more serious lay behind the moves: that Gamard intended to turn Birotteau out, and those like her risked nothing in such games. De Bourbonne, thin, shrewd, a sort of provincial Fabius, was practised in weighing words and measuring actions; his reputation for cunning and decency made him a man to be feared in local affairs.

Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix arrived from Tours with news that changed everything. She reported that the vicar-general was very ill and the archbishop had delegated his powers to Abbé Troubert provisionally, so the canonry would depend on him. At a recent gathering the Abbé Poirel had spoken of Birotteau’s alleged annoyances to Mademoiselle Gamard in a way that cast blame on the vicar. Madame de Listomère asked Birotteau whether he preferred to be made canon or to continue living with Gamard; in chorus they said canon. She suggested that by leaving Gamard’s house he might secure the canonry—“one good turn deserves another.” All applauded, except her nephew, the Baron, who joked about wishing to see a fight.

Despite their support, forces were unequal: the city’s best society had less power than Gamard backed by Troubert. By Madame de Listomère’s advice, a servant fetched Caron back. De Bourbonne counselled caution and delay, but passion prevailed. In a short conference Caron urged Birotteau to sign a paper declaring his relinquishment of domicile. Confused but overwhelmed, Birotteau read and mechanically signed it: he declared he left Gamard’s house of his own wish, that he had been properly fed and lodged. Caron asked where to send the abbé’s belongings; Birotteau said Madame de Listomère. De Bourbonne, suspicious, requested to see the paper and noted that there must be prior documents between Birotteau and Gamard. The fatal deed, the vicar said, was in his library.

The helplessness of the poor man, innocent of worldly craft, touched Madame de Listomère and Mademoiselle Salomon, who soothed him like mothers consoling a child. They promised him lodging and counsel, urged play to distract him, and spoke of asking Troubert for help. Birotteau, dazzled by the prospect of living with Madame de Listomère, temporarily forgot his ruined happiness. But that night the reality returned: the thought of moving, of his books and furniture scattered, of the end of long habits, overwhelmed him. He could not reconcile one sweet first year in Gamard’s house with the cruel second; the canonry seemed poor compensation. He clung to Mademoiselle Salomon’s friendship, but dared not fully trust new attachments.

The historian digresses to note that among spinsters there are heroic kinds—those who sacrifice themselves nobly. Mademoiselle Salomon belonged to this class: once beloved and young, she had tended a lover who went mad, refusing to call him insane; she had devoted herself for five years to his mechanical well-being, and after his death settled in Tours to do good. Simple, frank, and of regular features, she attached herself to the feeble, which made her warmly interested in Birotteau.

Mademoiselle de Villenoix returned Birotteau to Tours the next morning and left him at the quay of the cathedral to go to the Cloister, bent on preserving the canonry and overseeing removal of his furniture. At the house he found his old apartment occupied: Marianne told him Troubert was in his old rooms. The sight of Troubert, comfortably seated in Chapeloud’s library and armchair, confirmed the depth of the conspiracy. Birotteau grasped, with a sinking vision, that Troubert intended to possess Chapeloud’s and his spoils. Facing Troubert’s sardonic glance, the abbé felt as if struck dumb.

Troubert coolly told him that Mademoiselle Gamard had informed him of Birotteau’s departure and that he had been installed out of necessity; if the furniture belonged to Birotteau, Gamard’s scrupulous honesty guaranteed its return; if Birotteau wished to return, Troubert would cede the apartment. The words were terrible. Birotteau ran to find Gamard and met her on the staircase. Bowing calmly in spite of her derisive smile, he protested that she should have waited to let him pack his books and furniture. She interrupted, asking if he had not read his deed, and then, with the triumphant clarity of hatred, reminded him of the clause: by agreement, if he left her house his furniture would indemnify her for the difference in price of board between what he paid and what the late Abbé Chapeloud had paid. With Poirel’s appointment as canon just made, Gamard exulted in the arrangement. At these words Birotteau faintly bowed and fled, fearing a complete breakdown should he stay.

He found at Madame de Listomère’s house his linen, clothing, and papers packed in a trunk, and sank, hiding his face in his hands. Poirel had been made canon; Birotteau had no home, means, or furniture. Mademoiselle Salomon drove by, the porter noticed the priest’s despair, and persuaded her to take him in her carriage. She, ignorant of the deed’s terms, believed his derangement due to the shock of Poirel’s nomination. Back at the Alouette, friends cared for him until evening, when he recounted the morning’s events.

De Bourbonne asked to see the deed. Birotteau produced it. De Bourbonne read the clause providing that, because of an eight-hundred-franc yearly difference between Chapeloud’s board and what Gamard charged, and because Birotteau was unable to pay full price, he engaged in consideration of sums lent by Gamard to leave her, as indemnity, all household property he might possess at death, or to transfer the same should he voluntarily give up the apartment. De Bourbonne cursed the agreement and declared Gamard armed with claws.

Birotteau had never imagined anything would separate him from the house where he hoped to live and die. He had not remembered the clause, which he had not discussed; in his eagerness to enter the house he would have signed any document. Madame de Listomère, in hot indignation, exclaimed she had made him sign and must restore his lost happiness. De Bourbonne advised law: the deed might constitute fraud. The Baron and others urged litigation. De Bourbonne privately advised a different path: that most of those now friendly would desert him soon, and if Birotteau wished peace he should resign the vicariate and leave Tours to a distant parish where Troubert could not pursue him. This prospect horrified the vicar—leaving Tours for him was like death; celibates replace feelings with habits, and Birotteau needed his routine: his walks, salons, and games.

Word quickly spread that Madame la Baronne de Listomère had invited Birotteau to stay. The baroness’s reception of him implicitly denied Gamard’s assertions and censured her conduct, so Gamard was deeply wounded. To understand how Gamard had resources, it’s necessary to see the network that served Troubert. Accompanied by Troubert, Gamard made evening rounds to several houses where circles of old maids, a few men swayed by servants’ gossip, and other busybodies met. These women, ever sifting words and scrutinizing neighbors, kept exact accounts of fortunes and actions, retailing scandal and controlling local opinion. Spread through town like capillary fibres, they absorbed household secrets and transmitted them mechanically to Troubert. They formed a kind of police—dumb but perpetually talking—whose gossip could have terrible effects when it touched important interests. The quarrel between Birotteau and Gamard became the absorbing passion of these salons; a class sentiment underlay the conflict—an old Roman people-vs-senate struggle reduced to a provincial molehill.

Yet these small passions engaged their participants with an intensity equal to the great affairs of life. Troubert’s hours, like those of a gambler or statesman, were heavy with anxious thought. The narrator asks, in passing, to consider how certain passions prompt virtues that widen a life beyond its selfish center.

Madame de Listomère returned to Tours and took Birotteau to her lawyer. He did not think the case easy. Friends who had believed justice certain had delayed bringing the suit until returning to town, and Gamard’s allies had taken the initiative, spreading the affair to Birotteau’s damage. The lawyer—whose practice lay among the devout—cautioned against litigation. According to the deed, Gamard had the law on her side; in equity, judges and respectable laymen might view Birotteau as having derogated from his formerly peaceable and mild character. Gamard had lent him money to pay Chapeloud’s legacy duties without a receipt; Birotteau, not of age or character to sign without understanding, left Gamard’s house after two years while Chapeloud and Troubert had been there twelve and fifteen years respectively; the suit would appear like ingratitude. The lawyer declined to undertake it and besought Madame de Listomère to avoid embroiling herself.

That evening Birotteau could not refrain from telling friends the lawyer’s refusal. De Bourbonne advised caution and mentioned only a radical lawyer might take the case. The Baron, heatedly, insisted: Troubert’s involvement made it a public matter; valuable pictures and furniture had been involved, and Birotteau could not have intended to give away such wealth for two years of board. He proposed getting Monsieur Salmon, an expert from the Museum, to appraise the paintings and furniture.

Two days later the suit began. Choosing the radical lawyer harmed Birotteau’s cause: opponents of government and critics of religion seized on the case and the town buzzed. The Museum expert appraised the Virgin of Valentin and the Christ of Lebrun at eleven thousand francs; the bookshelves and Gothic furniture, increasingly fashionable in Paris, had immediate value of at least twelve thousand. The full appraisal exceeded thirty-six thousand francs. Clearly, Birotteau could not have intended to give Gamard such a sum under the deed; legally, he had equitable grounds to amend the agreement; if refused, Gamard’s conduct might constitute intentional fraud. The radical lawyer served a harsh writ on Gamard, a document of skillful legal argument supported by precedent and code citations; it so clearly condemned the old maid that many copies were maliciously distributed through the town.

A few days after hostilities began between Birotteau and the old maid, the Baron de Listomere, who expected promotion to corvette captain, received a letter warning he might be put on the retired list. He went to Paris and saw the minister, who laughed at his fears; yet the next day the baron, uneasy, made inquiries. By indiscretion a secretary showed him a document confirming the news, awaiting only the director’s signature.

The baron hurried to an uncle, a deputy who could see the minister at the Chamber, and begged him to learn the minister’s intentions in a matter that threatened his future. Waiting anxiously in his uncle’s carriage, he was told on the way home: the minister had first accused him of leading Radicals in Tours and of objectionable politics; but the real trouble, his uncle learned, was ecclesiastical. The baron had spoken slightingly of Abbe Troubert, the vicar-general, an influential man representing the Jesuits in the province. His uncle warned him to make peace with Troubert at once, saying such priests were men with whom one must live in harmony; Troubert was likely to be raised to a bishopric and could even harm the uncle’s prospects.

These words explained to the baron Troubert’s secret activities that Birotteau had often wondered about: “I can’t think what he does with himself—sitting up all night.” Troubert’s position, skill, and female network in Tours had made him the Congregation’s proconsul there; archbishop, general, prefect—all were under his covert influence. The Baron de Listomere decided on his course. “I shall take care not to get another round shot below my water-line,” he told his uncle.

Three days later the baron, returning hurriedly, told his aunt the family must abandon support of “that fool of a Birotteau.” He detained Monsieur de Bourbonne as the old man was leaving a rubber of whist; Bourbonne’s clear-sightedness seemed essential to navigate the reefs the Listomeres faced. The baron’s return and disquiet hinted he had met a check in his campaign against Gamard and Troubert. He revealed Troubert’s hidden power.

“I knew that,” said Bourbonne.

“Then why did you not warn us?” the baroness cried.

“Forget that I knew it, and I will forget that you did,” Bourbonne replied. “If we do not keep this secret now we shall be thought his accomplices. Pretend to be duped; but watch your steps. I warned you, but you would not understand me.”

Abandoning Birotteau was taken for granted by the three. “To beat a retreat with the honors of war has always been the triumph of the ablest generals,” said Bourbonne. “Bow to Troubert; if his hatred is less than his vanity you may win him; if you bow too low he will walk over you. Make believe you intend to leave the service, and you’ll escape him. Send away Birotteau, madame, and you will set things right with Mademoiselle Gamard. Ask Troubert, when you meet him at the archbishop’s, if he plays whist; he will say yes. Invite him to your salon; you are a woman and can win a priest. When the baron is promoted and his uncle made a peer, and Troubert a bishop, you can make Birotteau a canon if you choose. Meantime yield—but yield gracefully, with a slight menace. Your family can give him as much as he can give you. As for you, sailor, keep your deep-sea line about you.”

“Poor Birotteau?” said the baroness.

“Get rid of him at once,” Bourbonne replied. “If some clever Radical gets that empty head, he may cause trouble. The court might give a verdict in his favor, and Troubert must fear that. I have said my say.”

The next morning the baroness took Birotteau aside and, not without embarrassment, asked him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and to leave her house. As she spoke he turned pale. She explained, briefly and without disguising the gravity, the wide ramifications of the affair, making him see the net woven by revenge and the capacity and power of Troubert, whose hatred of Chapeloud now vented itself in seizing Chapeloud’s property and persecuting his friend. Birotteau clasped his hands and wept, listening as one at the edge of a precipice. The baroness ended: family duties must be considered before friendship; yield, and she would ensure he lacked nothing, arranging matters through Monsieur de Bourbonne to save appearances. “Grant me the right to abandon you,” she begged.

The bewildered abbe cried, “Chapeloud was right when he said that if Troubert could drag him by the feet out of his grave he would do it!” Yet he yielded. “I trust myself to you—I am but the stubble of the streets,” he said, using a local word for a pathetic bit of straw. He asked only for Chapeloud’s portrait, painted for him, and the baroness promised to see Mademoiselle Gamard herself and to consult the archbishop.

Birotteau left dismayed; Troubert now seemed to him a pyramid whose hands were in Paris and elbows in the cloister. The news of Birotteau’s removal from the Listomere house seemed mysterious: the baroness told people the nephew meant to marry and leave the navy, and wanted the vicar’s apartment. The Listomeres’ apparent neutrality implied recognition of the Congregation’s occult power. But the lawsuit still awaited judgment; their opponents yielded while threatening.

Then Mademoiselle Gamard took cold leaving the cathedral and was soon dangerously ill. The town burst into commiseration: “Mademoiselle Gamard’s sensitive nature could not bear the scandal; in spite of the justice of her cause she was likely to die of grief—Birotteau has killed his benefactress.” Such talk spread through the female conclave and the town.

Madame de Listomere went to pay her promised visit but the old maid was too ill to see her. She asked to speak to the vicar-general. Troubert, gratified to receive in Chapeloud’s library the woman who had hitherto ignored him, kept her waiting before admitting her. No courtier could have put more dissimulation into negotiating personal interests than the baroness and the priest did now.

Bourbonne had coached the baroness: “Don’t forget your cue. You are a mediator, not an interested party. Troubert is a mediator. Weigh your words; if he strokes his chin you have him.” Their conversation was a duel of implied meanings. Madame de Listomere began by expressing regret at Birotteau’s lawsuit and her desire to settle it.

“The harm is done, madame,” Troubert said gravely. “The pious Mademoiselle Gamard is dying.” (He intended to place the guilt on the baroness.)

“On hearing of her illness I entreated Monsieur Birotteau to relinquish his claims; I have brought the document intending to give it to that excellent woman,” she replied. (She meant to avoid compromising herself.) After a tense interchange of civilities and veiled thrusts about religion, politics, and the paintings, she laid the deed of relinquishment on the table and said she had come to reconcile two Christians.

“Your nephew has been to Paris, I believe,” Troubert said. “He returns tonight; the minister does not want him to leave the service.” “He has done us the service of getting a proper estimate on the value of those paintings,” Troubert added, eyeing the pictures. Madame de Listomere offered frames for Saint-Gatien if they were to be given to the chapel. He replied they did not belong to him. She handed him the relinquishment, saying it ended all discussion and that she had some compassion for Birotteau; he had asked, as the price of renunciation, for Chapeloud’s portrait. Troubert looked fixedly at her and went down to ask Mademoiselle Gamard.

He soon returned. “Madame,” he said, “I bring you the words of a dying woman. ‘The Abbe Chapeloud was so true a friend...that I cannot consent to part with his picture.’ As for me, if it were mine I would not yield it. My feelings to my late friend were so faithful that I should feel my right above others’.”

“That’s no need to quarrel over a bad picture,” the baroness said, offering to have a copy made and courteously inviting him to play whist at her house. Troubert, stroked his chin—caught by vanity—and promised he would oblige her for her sake.

Madame de Listomere went home hoping the archbishop would complete the peace. But Mademoiselle Gamard died the next day. Her will left everything to Abbe Troubert; the fortune was appraised at three hundred thousand francs. He sent the Listomeres invitations to the services and funeral; the baroness insisted they go. “We must go,” she said. Bourbonne added that the baron must go to the cemetery; the lieutenant, unluckily, had not left Tours.

The services were performed with unusual ecclesiastical magnificence. Only Birotteau wept, kneeling in a side chapel; he believed himself guilty and sincerely prayed for the deceased, lamenting that he had not obtained her forgiveness. Troubert followed the body to the grave and delivered a discourse that, by eloquence, enlarged the old maid’s life to monumental proportions. In the peroration he spoke of her life devoted to God and of Providence withdrawing her from trials; his tone suggested she rested innocent. After the sermon, in a gesture that betrayed more joy than grief, he sprinkled the coffin with holy water.

The next morning Mademoiselle Salomon came to tell Madame de Listomere that “our poor Abbe Birotteau has just received a frightful blow: he is appointed curate of Saint-Symphorien.” Saint-Symphorien lay beyond the bridge, a suburb three miles away—an exile in plain sight of the town. The parsonage was cold and damp, the parish too poor, and the removal seemed an infamous plot.

To end the history: five months later Troubert was made Bishop of Troyes; Madame de Listomere died, leaving an annuity of fifteen hundred francs to Abbe Birotteau. The day her will’s dispositions were announced, Monseigneur Hyacinthe, Bishop of Troyes, about to leave Tours for his diocese, delayed when he received the news. Furious at being foiled by the baroness—whom he had lately received while she had secretly aided a man he counted as an enemy—Troubert again threatened the baron’s career and jeopardized his uncle’s peerage. He made one of those soft, vengeful priestly speeches in the archbishop’s salon. The Baron de Listomere went to him the next day and must have submitted to hard conditions, for his later conduct showed entire submission to the Jesuit’s will.

The new bishop deeded Mademoiselle Gamard’s house to the cathedral chapter, gave Chapeloud’s books and bookcases to the seminary, and presented the two disputed pictures to the Chapel of the Virgin—but he kept Chapeloud’s portrait. No one could explain this near-total renunciation of Gamard’s bequest; Bourbonne supposed the bishop had secretly kept invested monies to support his rank in Paris. Only the night before Troubert’s departure did Bourbonne unearth the hidden reason: the baron had contested Madame de Listomere’s legacy to Birotteau on grounds of undue influence. The deathblow to the feeblest victim was complete.

A few days after the case was brought the baron was promoted to captain. As ecclesiastical discipline, the curate of Saint-Symphorien—Birotteau—was suspended; his superiors judged him guilty. The murderer of Sophie Gamard was also a swindler; had Troubert kept the property openly, it would have been harder to censure Birotteau.

When Monseigneur Hyacinthe drove along the quay Saint-Symphorien in a post-chaise on his way to Paris, poor Birotteau was placed in an armchair in the sun on a terrace above the road. Pale and haggard, smitten by the archbishop, he was the skeleton of the man who less than a year earlier had rolled contentedly along the cloister. The bishop cast one look of pity and contempt, then went his way.

There is no doubt Troubert might once have been a Hildebrand or an Alexander VI. In our era the Church is no longer the political power it once was; celibacy concentrates a man’s faculties on a single passion—egotism—which makes celibates either useless or mischievous. We live when governments tend to make Man for Society rather than Society for Man. There is a perpetual struggle between the Individual and a social system that uses him while he tries to use it. In earlier times man was first father, then lived for clan or city; later, for caste or religion, and his range of interests widened. Today his life attaches to a vast country; perhaps someday his family may be the whole universe.

Will such moral cosmopolitanism—Christian Rome’s hope of Brotherhood—prove only a sublime error? It is natural to believe in such a noble vision, but human nature lacks such divine proportions. Souls vast enough to hold a world of feeling belong to great exceptions. Physiologists who say as the brain enlarges the heart narrows are mistaken. The apparent egotism of those who bear a science, a nation, or a code is a highest passion; it is the maternity of the masses. To create peoples and ideas they must unite in their mighty brains the tenderness of woman and the force of God. The histories of Innocent III, Peter the Great, and other great leaders show the same vast thought of which Troubert was a provincial representative.
