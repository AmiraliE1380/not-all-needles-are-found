“HULLO! There is that old Box-coat again!” exclaimed a lawyer’s clerk, Simonnin, as he leaned out of a window, munching on dry bread. He playfully shot a crumb at a stranger crossing the courtyard of Maitre Derville’s office in the Rue Vivienne.

“Come, Simonnin, don’t play tricks on people, or I will turn you out of doors. However poor a client may be, he is still a man!” admonished the head clerk, who was busy with a bill of costs.

Simonnin, a typical messenger boy of about thirteen or fourteen, was known for his impudence and mischief. He and his fellow clerks, including Godeschal, were engaged in a light-hearted discussion about the old man in the courtyard, who they referred to as “old Box-coat.” Godeschal suggested they play a trick on him, claiming that M. Derville only saw clients at odd hours.

As the clerks continued their banter, the atmosphere in the office was lively, filled with laughter and camaraderie. The office itself was cluttered and dimly lit, with the smell of food mingling with the musty odor of old papers. The clerks were accustomed to the disarray, which reflected the chaotic nature of their work.

Suddenly, the old man entered the office, looking weary and out of place. He politely inquired if M. Derville was available, but the clerks, preoccupied with their own tasks, dismissed him. Boucard, the head clerk, suggested the man return at one in the morning, leaving the old man bewildered.

The clerks resumed their mockery, speculating about the old man’s past. They debated whether he was a nobleman or a former porter, each making humorous claims about his shabby appearance. When the old man returned to confirm his identity as Colonel Chabert, who had supposedly died at Eylau, the clerks erupted in laughter, dismissing his claim as a joke.

As the clerks continued their banter, they discussed their plans for the evening, debating which theater to attend. The conversation was filled with playful arguments and laughter, showcasing their camaraderie amidst the drudgery of their work.

Boucard eventually refocused the clerks on their tasks, emphasizing the importance of completing their work efficiently. The scene encapsulated the youthful exuberance and lightheartedness of the clerks, contrasting sharply with the somber reality of the old man’s situation.

At about one in the morning, Colonel Chabert knocked at the door of Maitre Derville, a prominent attorney in Paris. The porter informed him that Derville had not yet returned, but the Colonel insisted on his appointment and was shown upstairs. He found the head clerk organizing papers for the next day's cases. The clerk explained that Derville preferred to work at night when he could think clearly, and that the Colonel was only the third person to seek him out at such an hour.

When Derville arrived, he was taken aback by the Colonel's strange appearance. Chabert was thin and pale, with a scarred head that suggested a traumatic past. Upon introducing himself, Chabert claimed to be the Colonel who had been killed at Eylau, which led Derville and his clerk to suspect he was mad. Chabert insisted on sharing the details of his situation.

Derville, accustomed to dealing with various clients, urged Chabert to be concise. The Colonel recounted his military service at Eylau, where he was gravely injured and presumed dead. He explained that after being buried among the dead, he miraculously survived, thanks to the body of his horse shielding him. He described his harrowing experience of awakening in a grave, struggling to escape, and eventually being rescued by a woman who took him to a hospital.

After months of recovery, Chabert realized he had lost everything, including his identity. He had no documents to prove who he was, and his attempts to reclaim his life were met with disbelief and ridicule. He had wandered as a beggar, often treated as a madman, and had been confined in a mental institution for two years. Eventually, he was released but remained haunted by his past and the loss of his wife, the Countess Ferraud, who had remarried and had children.

Derville, realizing the gravity of Chabert's claims, expressed his disbelief but also his willingness to help. Chabert revealed that he sought to reclaim his rights and fortune, which he believed were unjustly taken from him. He lamented that no lawyer had taken him seriously enough to assist him in proving his identity and reclaiming his estate.

Moved by Chabert's plight, Derville offered to help him obtain the necessary documents from Germany and provided him with a small sum of money to support him in the meantime. Chabert was overwhelmed with gratitude, feeling a flicker of hope for the first time in years. He shared his longing for vengeance against his wife, who had moved on without him, and expressed his deep emotional turmoil.

Derville acknowledged the complexity of Chabert's case, noting that it would require careful consideration and could face significant legal challenges. Chabert, however, was resolute, stating that if he failed, he would rather die than continue living in obscurity. Derville assured him that he would treat the case with the seriousness it deserved and provided him with a letter to a notary for further assistance.

As Chabert left, he felt a renewed sense of purpose and determination. He had not only received financial help but also the promise of legal support in his quest to reclaim his identity and rights. The encounter marked a turning point for the Colonel, who had spent years in despair, and he left with a glimmer of hope for the future.

About three months after the interview, in Derville’s room, the notary Crottat, tasked with advancing half-pay to Derville’s eccentric client, sought the attorney's advice on a serious matter, beginning with a request to refund the six hundred francs the old soldier had received.

“Are you amusing yourself with pensioning the old army?” Crottat laughed, a young man who had recently taken over the office after his chief fled due to bankruptcy.

“Thank you for reminding me of that affair,” Derville replied. “My philanthropy will not extend beyond twenty-five louis; I fear I have already been duped by my patriotism.”

As Derville spoke, he noticed papers on his desk, including a letter with stamps from various post-offices. 

“Ah ha! Here is the last act of the comedy; now we shall see if I have been taken in!” he exclaimed, opening the letter, but finding it written in German.

“Boucard, go and have this letter translated immediately,” Derville instructed his head clerk.

The notary in Berlin informed Derville that the requested documents would arrive shortly and were all regular and legally stamped. He noted that most witnesses to the facts recorded were still in Eylau, Prussia, and the woman who saved M. le Comte Chabert was alive in Heilsberg.

“This looks like business,” Derville said after Boucard translated the letter. He then asked Crottat for information from his office regarding the settlement of Chabert’s estate, recalling the old rascal Roguin.

“Let’s call him the unfortunate Roguin,” Crottat interjected with a laugh.

“Wasn’t it that ill-used man who absconded with eight hundred thousand francs of his clients’ money?” Derville continued. “I believe he handled Chabert’s estate.”

“Yes,” Crottat confirmed. “I copied the papers when I was third clerk. Rose Chapotel, wife of Hyacinthe, called Chabert, Count of the Empire, married without settlement, so they held all property in common. The personalty was about six hundred thousand francs. Before his marriage, Colonel Chabert made a will leaving one-quarter to the hospitals and the State to take the other quarter. The will was contested, leading to a forced sale, and the widow received the portion bequeathed to the treasury.”

“So Comte Chabert’s personal fortune was no more than three hundred thousand francs?” Derville asked.

“Exactly!” Crottat replied. “You lawyers can be quite clear-headed despite accusations of false practices.”

Colonel Chabert was lodging in the Faubourg Saint-Marceau with an old quartermaster named Vergniaud. Derville had to walk to find his client, as his coachman refused to navigate the unpaved street. He eventually found shabby stone gate-posts with a sign reading “Vergniaud, dairyman.” The yard was a dismal sight, with a dilapidated house that seemed on the verge of collapse.

The house was poorly constructed, with mismatched windows and a floor of trodden earth. Derville noted the squalor but also the calm demeanor of the Colonel, who emerged from the house with a pipe in his mouth, displaying military coolness.

“Why did you not write to me?” Chabert asked, directing Derville to a better path to avoid the manure.

Derville entered the Colonel’s room, which contained only a chair and a bed of straw. The walls were damp, and the atmosphere was grim, yet Chabert seemed content. Derville expressed concern over the Colonel’s living conditions.

“Here, we are not distinguished for luxury,” Chabert replied. “But I have done no one wrong, and I sleep in peace.”

Derville hesitated to ask about the money he had advanced, instead inquiring why Chabert had not moved to Paris, where he could live better.

“The good folks here have taken me in and fed me for a year,” Chabert explained. “I cannot leave them now that I have a little money.”

Derville informed Chabert that he would soon receive papers from Heilsberg, and the woman who saved him was still alive.

“Curse the money! To think I haven’t got any!” Chabert exclaimed, throwing his pipe down in frustration.

Derville explained the complexities of the legal situation, emphasizing that proving Chabert’s identity would involve lengthy and costly legal battles. He warned that the case could be prolonged by the bigamy issue with Comtesse Ferraud, who had remarried.

“Things are not done so in the legal world,” Derville cautioned. “You will face many challenges, and your opponents will demand inquiries that could take years.”

Chabert, overwhelmed by the prospect of a lengthy lawsuit, expressed despair. Derville reassured him that the outcome could be favorable, suggesting a friendly suit to annul both the death certificate and the marriage.

“I will send you a power of attorney to sign,” Derville concluded, offering to help with finances.

Chabert, feeling hopeless, agreed to trust Derville completely. As Derville left, he encountered Louis Vergniaud, who expressed concern for Chabert’s well-being and requested a loan to help improve the Colonel’s living conditions.

Derville agreed to provide the funds but insisted that Chabert should be the one to help his friends. He then planned to visit Comtesse Ferraud, considering her position and the potential leverage he could gain.

Monsieur le Comte Ferraud, a former Councillor’s son, had returned to France after the Revolution and married Madame Chabert, who had managed to turn her share of her husband’s fortune into a substantial income. Their marriage was socially accepted, and Ferraud was politically ambitious, but he had begun to regret his union with Madame Ferraud.

Derville, reflecting on the situation, recognized the Countess’s precarious position. He arrived at the Hotel Ferraud, where he found her in a luxurious setting, contrasting sharply with Chabert’s squalor.

“Good-morning, Monsieur Derville,” she greeted him, but he quickly shifted to serious matters.

“I have come to speak with you on a very serious matter,” he stated, revealing that Colonel Chabert was alive.

The Countess laughed dismissively, but Derville’s serious demeanor silenced her. He warned her of the danger she faced if Chabert’s identity was proven, hinting at the potential legal ramifications.

She insisted that her second marriage was secure due to her children, but Derville countered that the law might not favor her if Chabert proved his identity. He suggested that Ferraud might not support her if he learned of the situation.

Derville’s words struck a nerve, and the Countess’s demeanor shifted as she considered the implications. He proposed a compromise, sensing her vulnerability.

“Does he still love me?” she asked, contemplating how to use Chabert’s affection to her advantage.

Derville left her with the suggestion to consider a compromise, confident that he had gained the upper hand in the situation.

A week after Derville's visits, Colonel Chabert and his wife, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud, set out from opposite ends of Paris to meet at their lawyer's office. Thanks to Derville's financial support, Chabert arrived looking dignified in a blue suit and the red ribbon of the Legion of Honor, embodying the soldierly style he had lost. He was transformed from the shabby figure he once was into a man who reflected the nobility of his past.

As Chabert entered Derville's office, Madame Ferraud arrived shortly after, dressed elegantly to appear youthful. The office remained unchanged, with clerks joking about Chabert's transformation. Derville, aware of the tension between the couple, separated them to discuss the terms of their agreement. He presented a memorandum outlining the conditions of their relationship, including Chabert's acknowledgment of his identity and the financial support he would receive from his wife.

The Countess was taken aback by the terms, particularly the financial obligations, and expressed her reluctance to engage in a lawsuit. When Chabert entered the room, he confronted her about her past, revealing the pain of their separation. The Countess, feigning surprise, attempted to leave, but Derville urged her to stay. Chabert, filled with anger and betrayal, expressed his desire for justice, but Derville cautioned him against rash actions.

After a heated exchange, the Countess led Chabert to her carriage, and they traveled to her country house in Groslay. During the ride, the Countess's tone shifted, revealing her emotional turmoil. She acknowledged her love for another man, Comte Ferraud, and expressed her desire to maintain dignity without public scandal. Chabert, though hurt, offered his understanding and support, recognizing the complexities of their situation.

Derville, moved by Chabert's plight, sought to help him, but Chabert's disdain for societal norms led him to reject material concerns.

In the end, Chabert's story became one of loss and resilience, illustrating the complexities of love, betrayal, and the struggle for identity in a world that often overlooks the individual.
