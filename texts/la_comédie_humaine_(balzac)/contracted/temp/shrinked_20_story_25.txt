“HULLO! There is that old Box-coat again!” cried the lawyer’s clerk Simonnin, leaning from a window and munching dry bread as he shot a crumb at a stranger crossing Maître Derville’s courtyard in the Rue Vivienne.

“Come, Simonnin, don’t play tricks on people, or I will turn you out of doors. However poor a client may be, he is still a man!” admonished Boucard, the head clerk, busy with a bill of costs.

Simonnin, a typical thirteen- or fourteen-year-old messenger, was known for impudence and mischief. He and the other clerks, including Godeschal, joked about the old man, dubbing him “old Box-coat.” Godeschal proposed a prank, claiming M. Derville received clients only at odd hours.

The office buzzed with laughter and camaraderie. It was cluttered and dimly lit, the smell of food mingling with the musty odor of papers; the clerks accepted the disarray as part of their chaotic work.

When the old man entered, looking weary and out of place, he politely asked if M. Derville was available. Preoccupied, the clerks dismissed him. Boucard brusquely suggested he return at one in the morning, leaving the visitor bewildered.

The clerks resumed their mockery, speculating about the old man’s past—nobleman, porter, or worse—trading extravagant guesses to explain his shabby appearance. When the old man insisted he was Colonel Chabert, supposedly dead at Eylau, they erupted in laughter and treated the claim as a joke.

Amid the jeering, they debated evening plans and which theatre to attend; their playful disputes underscored the youthful spirit that pervaded the office despite monotonous tasks. Finally Boucard quelled the chatter and refocused them on finishing work efficiently. The scene captured their rowdy, irreverent camaraderie in stark contrast to the old man’s somber, unsettling presence.

At about one in the morning, Colonel Chabert knocked at Maître Derville’s door. The porter said Derville had not returned, but the head clerk, preparing papers for the next day, explained that Derville preferred to work at night. The Colonel was only the third person to seek him so late.

When Derville arrived he was struck by Chabert’s appearance: thin, pale, his head scarred as if by a harsh past. The Colonel introduced himself as the man presumed killed at Eylau, which led Derville and the clerk to suspect madness. Chabert, however, insisted on telling his story.

Derville, used to varied clients, urged brevity. Chabert recounted serving at Eylau, where he was gravely wounded and buried among the dead. Miraculously he survived because his horse’s body had shielded him; he described awakening in a grave, struggling free, and being rescued by a woman who took him to a hospital.

After months of recovery he discovered he had lost everything, including proof of his identity. With no documents, attempts to reclaim his life were met with disbelief and ridicule. He wandered as a beggar and was confined in a mental institution for two years. Released but haunted by his past, he found his wife, the Countess Ferraud, had remarried and borne children.

Derville, sensing the seriousness of the claims, expressed skepticism but a willingness to help. Chabert said he sought to reclaim his rights and fortune, which he believed had been unjustly taken, lamenting that no lawyer had taken him seriously enough to prove his identity and recover his estate.

Moved by the Colonel’s plight, Derville offered to obtain necessary documents from Germany and gave him a small sum to subsist on. Overwhelmed with gratitude, Chabert felt hope for the first time in years. He also voiced a longing for vengeance against his wife and revealed the depth of his emotional turmoil.

Derville acknowledged the case’s complexity and likely legal obstacles. Chabert declared that if he failed he would rather die than continue living in obscurity. Derville promised to treat the matter seriously and provided a letter to a notary for further assistance.

As Chabert left, he experienced a renewed sense of purpose and determination: immediate aid and the promise of legal support gave him, after years of despair, a glimmer of hope.

About three months after the interview, in Derville’s room, the notary Crottat — a young man who had recently taken over an office after his chief fled in bankruptcy — raised a serious matter and asked Derville to refund the six hundred francs the old soldier had received.

“My philanthropy will not extend beyond twenty-five louis; I fear I have already been duped by my patriotism,” Derville replied. He noticed papers on his desk, including a letter with stamps from various post offices. The letter was in German; he ordered his head clerk Boucard to translate it.

The notary in Berlin informed Derville that the requested documents would arrive soon and were legally stamped. Most witnesses to the recorded facts were still in Eylau, and the woman who had saved Comte Chabert was alive in Heilsberg. After Boucard translated, Derville treated the matter as serious business and asked Crottat about Chabert’s estate, calling to mind the rogue Roguin.

“Let’s call him the unfortunate Roguin,” Crottat mocked. He explained that, when he was third clerk, he had copied papers showing Rose Chapotel, wife of Hyacinthe, had married Chabert without settlement, so their property was common. The personalty had been about six hundred thousand francs. Before marrying, Colonel Chabert had made a will leaving one-quarter to hospitals and another quarter to the State; the will was contested, triggering a forced sale, and the widow received the portion meant for the treasury. Roguin had absconded with clients’ funds — some said eight hundred thousand francs — and had handled Chabert’s estate.

“So Comte Chabert’s personal fortune was no more than three hundred thousand francs?” Derville asked.

“Exactly,” Crottat replied.

Derville then went to find Colonel Chabert, lodging in the Faubourg Saint-Marceau with an old quartermaster named Vergniaud. His coachman refused to drive in the unpaved street, so Derville walked. He found dilapidated gate-posts and a sign, “Vergniaud, dairyman,” and entered a dismal yard and a falling house. Inside, mismatched windows, a dirt floor, and a trodden atmosphere greeted him. Chabert emerged calmly, smoking a pipe, still bearing the soldier’s coolness.

“Why did you not write to me?” Chabert asked, guiding Derville away from the manure. His room contained only a chair and a straw bed; the walls were damp. Despite the squalor, the Colonel seemed content.

“Here, we are not distinguished for luxury,” he said. “But I have done no one wrong, and I sleep in peace.”

Derville hesitated over the six hundred francs he had advanced and questioned why Chabert hadn’t moved to Paris. “The good folks here have taken me in and fed me for a year,” Chabert explained. “I cannot leave them now that I have a little money.”

Derville told him the papers from Heilsberg were expected and that the woman who saved him was alive. Chabert, frustrated at still lacking money, threw down his pipe. Derville then explained the legal complexities: proving Chabert’s identity would require lengthy, costly proceedings in which opponents could use the bigamy issue — Comtesse Ferraud’s remarriage — to obstruct him.

“Things are not done so in the legal world,” Derville warned. “You will face many challenges, and your opponents will demand inquiries that could take years.” Overwhelmed, Chabert despaired at the prospect of a drawn-out lawsuit. Derville reassured him the outcome could be favorable and proposed a friendly suit to annul both the death certificate and the later marriage, offering to send a power of attorney and financial help.

Chabert, feeling hopeless, agreed to trust Derville completely. As Derville left, he encountered Louis Vergniaud, who expressed concern for Chabert’s well-being and asked for a loan to improve the Colonel’s living conditions. Derville agreed to provide funds but insisted that Chabert should be the one to help his friends.

Derville then planned to visit Comtesse Ferraud, considering her position and the leverage potential if Chabert’s identity were proved. Monsieur le Comte Ferraud, the son of a former Councillor, had returned to France after the Revolution and married Madame Chabert, who had turned her share of her husband’s fortune into a substantial income. Their marriage was socially accepted; Ferraud was politically ambitious but had begun to regret his union, leaving the Countess in a precarious, though affluent, position.

At the Hotel Ferraud, Derville found the Countess surrounded by luxury — a stark contrast to Chabert’s squalor. She greeted him lightly, but his seriousness silenced her. He bluntly revealed that Colonel Chabert was alive and warned of the danger she faced if his identity were proven, hinting at legal consequences. She laughed initially, confident that her second marriage was secure because of her children, but Derville countered that the law might not favor her; Ferraud might not support her if his name and fortune were at stake.

Derville’s words unnerved her; she began to consider the implications. Sensing her vulnerability, he proposed a compromise. The Countess, now calculating, asked, “Does he still love me?” — a question that revealed her intent to use Chabert’s affection to her advantage. Derville left her to ponder a settlement, confident he had gained the upper hand.

A week after Derville's visits, Colonel Chabert and his wife, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud, set out from opposite ends of Paris to meet at their lawyer's office. Thanks to Derville's support, Chabert arrived in a dignified blue suit with the red ribbon of the Legion of Honor, recovering the soldierly bearing that had been lost and transforming from a shabby figure into one reflecting his former rank.

When Chabert entered Derville's office, the Countess arrived shortly after, elegantly dressed to appear youthful. The office was unchanged; clerks made jesting remarks about the transformation. Sensing the couple's tension, Derville separated them to discuss terms. He presented a memorandum: Chabert's formal acknowledgment of his restored identity in exchange for financial arrangements from his wife.

The Countess was taken aback by the terms, especially the financial obligations, and insisted she did not want the matter dragged into court. When Chabert returned he confronted her about their past, exposing the pain of their separation. She feigned surprise and attempted to leave, but Derville urged her to stay. Chabert, bitter and wounded, pressed for justice even as Derville warned against rash actions.

After a heated exchange the Countess put Chabert into her carriage and they went to her country house in Groslay. During the ride her tone shifted, revealing emotional turmoil: she admitted attachment to another man, Comte Ferraud, and wanted to preserve her dignity without public scandal. Chabert, though hurt, offered a measure of understanding, aware of the social consequences she feared.

At the country house she treated him kindly, trying to erase past pain. They reminisced, and Chabert found temporary solace in her presence even as resentment and the sense of betrayal lingered. The Countess, meanwhile, worked discreetly to secure her position and undermine Chabert's claim. As days passed her unease grew; she wrote to Delbecq to manipulate the situation further. When Chabert discovered this, he felt deeply betrayed and resolved to confront her. Their relationship became a battleground of emotion, with Chabert torn between lingering affection and mounting anger.

Ultimately he decided to sacrifice his legal identity for the sake of the Countess and their children, accepting anonymity to avoid scandal. Her continued manipulations revealed the depth of her betrayal; he confronted her, expressed his disdain, and chose to live without reclaiming his name. Chabert then vanished from society. Months later Derville found him in a courtroom, reduced to a vagabond. Despite his circumstances he retained a measure of dignity and reflected on the world's indifference. Derville, moved, tried to help, but Chabert rejected material concerns. His story remains one of loss and resilience, illustrating the complexities of love, betrayal, and the struggle for identity in an indifferent world.

In June 1840 Godeschal, an attorney, accompanied Derville to Ris. Near Bicêtre they noticed an old pauper beneath an elm drying his handkerchief. In a shabby wrapper-coat with a striking look, the man prompted Godeschal to compare him to grotesque German carvings; Derville recognized him as Comte Chabert, the husband of the pious Comtesse Ferraud, who had likely sent him to the workhouse because he reminded her of their past.

Intrigued, Godeschal urged Derville to tell Chabert’s story. Two days later they returned and found the old man on a tree trunk, drawing in the sand. When addressed, he insisted he was no longer Chabert but “Hyacinthe,” a mere number of the institution, and envied a condemned man, thinking him fortunate for not being married.

Godeschal offered money for snuff; Chabert eagerly accepted, thanking them as “brave troopers,” playfully brandishing his stick and crying “Vive Napoleon!” Another pauper observed that Chabert, though sometimes childish, could be philosophical and imaginative, recalling how he once impressed a Prussian officer with his military reminiscences.

Derville reflected on Chabert’s tragic arc: from foundling to soldier under Napoleon, only to end in a workhouse. He lamented the disillusionment of three professions—the priest, the doctor, and especially the lawyer—each bearing witness to humanity’s darker side. Derville sketched the lawyer’s grim view, full of betrayal, greed, and moral decay, citing cases of familial cruelty: fathers abandoned by daughters and mothers who exploit children for gain.

Godeschal conceded he had seen similar horrors and expressed a desire to flee Paris’s corruption for a quiet country life with his wife. The narrative closes with an addendum listing characters from Balzac’s “Human Comedy” and their appearances elsewhere, including figures like Napoleon and Comtesse Ferraud.
