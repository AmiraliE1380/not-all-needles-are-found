DEDICATION

To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy, etc.

Dear Nodier, this book reveals actions hidden behind domestic doors, where divine intervention often supplants human justice. Its moral lessons concern families and motherhood. We may yet see the consequences of weakening paternal authority, historically the only tribunal for domestic wrongs. A mother, however loving, cannot replace patriarchal authority. This narrative stresses the indissoluble marriage bond and the dangers of unchecked selfishness. May a society built on wealth recognize the law's impotence in a system that glorifies success at any cost. I hope it returns to Catholicism for moral uplift through a different education.

In “Scenes from Military Life,” I will show noble self-sacrifice while exposing war’s corrupting influence on those who live domestically as if on a battlefield.

You have observed our times keenly, and your reflections on political chaos have been insightful. I entrust this story to your esteemed name, which may shelter it from inevitable criticism.

Your sincere admirer,

De Balzac  
Paris, November 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792 the townspeople of Issoudun regarded Dr. Rouget with dislike. Rumor held he made his beautiful wife unhappy, though she may have been somewhat foolish. Despite gossip, the household's true life remained hidden. Rouget was ill-liked but treated civilly. His wife, a demoiselle Descoings, had been frail in youth, which some said explained his choice. They had a son, Jean-Jacques, and a daughter, Agathe, born unexpectedly ten years later.

These details matter for understanding Rouget. He held the damaging belief that men must show strength, a notion responsible for much female suffering. The Descoings, his in-laws, were wealthy wool merchants who grew rich and miserly. Descoings, Rouget's brother-in-law, left Issoudun for Paris, failed as a grocer, and was driven by love for a beautiful widow.

By 1792 Descoings was thought prosperous, while his parents had moved from wool to invest in forfeited estates. Expecting his wife's death, Dr. Rouget sent Agathe to Paris to stay with Descoings, hoping she would win their affection. Agathe, the most beautiful girl in Issoudun, resembled her mother, which caused a rift between Rouget and his friend Monsieur Lousteau, who left town after their quarrel.

Madame Rouget, learning of her daughter's departure, predicted she would never see Agathe again, a sentiment that proved prophetic. Her health then declined, worsened by her son's neglect. Jean-Jacques, like his father, offered little comfort.

Agathe's arrival in Paris coincided with Descoings' imprisonment after political missteps. He had imprudently commented on the famine, angering influential men. His wife's attempts to secure his release were ill-advised; he was executed alongside the poet André Chénier. The incident made local grocers wary of politics for years.

Descoings' death affected the local community more than Chénier's, though history would later show the poet's loss was greater. After this, Agathe married Bridau, a ministry secretary, with Dr. Rouget's approval; Rouget sought to profit from the Descoings' inheritance. Bridau, enchanted by Agathe's beauty, married her unaware of Rouget's manipulations.

Once the Descoings' wealth passed on, Dr. Rouget fell into debauchery until his death in 1805, leaving Jean-Jacques, who remained a bachelor for reasons tied to his father's influence. Agathe, resembling her mother, became a devoted wife and mother, yet her ignorance of the world led to misfortunes.

Bridau, an ardent supporter of Napoleon, was promoted in 1804. He worked relentlessly, neglected his health, and in 1808 died from overwork just as a further promotion loomed. The Emperor, who recognized Bridau's dedication, intended to reward him but was too late.

After Bridau's death, no one in Issoudun wrote to Agathe. She lost her mother shortly before giving birth to her second son; her father's death coincided with the Emperor's coronation, which kept Bridau occupied. Her brother had not contacted her since she left home, and she gradually accepted the family's indifference.

Madame Hochon, Agathe's godmother, warned that Agathe would inherit nothing unless she granted power of attorney to her brother. Agathe hesitated to burden him; Bridau, either unaware or indifferent, did not contest the arrangement. Their tacit acceptance brought financial fragility.

Concerned for Agathe, Bridau invested their remaining funds prudently to secure a modest income. After his death, Agathe and Madame Descoings combined resources and lived on a joint income of twelve thousand francs a year. This practical arrangement concealed underlying strains.

By 1809 Madame Descoings, now sixty-five, retained youthful looks and charm, enjoyed good food and the theater, and secretly gambled on lotteries. She had a significant secret about her son, Bixiou, a major in the army, who had a son being educated alongside Agathe's children.

Agathe, devoted to her children, chose not to remarry, believing it her duty to be both mother and authority. Her lack of intelligence and blind trust in Madame Descoings, however, led to ruin. Madame Descoings, hoping to enrich the family by lotteries, ran up heavy debts and finally confessed.

Agathe, without reproach, took charge: she sold possessions and liquidated investments to settle the debts, a decisive turning point in their lives.

CHAPTER II

One of the bleakest areas in Paris is the stretch of rue Mazarin between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, overshadowed by the high gray walls of the college and library given by Cardinal Mazarin. Here a poor widow, Madame Bridau, lived on the third floor of a damp, cold building. Opposite were the Institute buildings, where aspiring artists, known as rapins, strove amid the chaos of examinations to become prize-winning talents; that artistic hub has since moved to the Palais des Beaux-Arts.

From her windows Madame Bridau could see the students’ cages below. To brighten her space she placed boxes of earth outside the windows to grow little gardens, despite police regulations. The house was shallow, with a winding staircase to three rooms: a dining room, a small salon, and a bedroom, plus a kitchen and two single rooms. She had chosen this apartment because it was affordable—four hundred francs a year—near her sons’ school, and in a neighborhood she knew.

The interior matched the house’s general dreariness. The dining room, with yellow wallpaper and unglazed tile flooring, held only essential furniture. The salon had an Aubusson carpet given to her and a mahogany sofa bearing a pastel portrait of her late husband, Bridau. The portrait captured his noble essence; a vivid painting of Napoleon hung above a card table.

Madame Bridau filled her home with two large birdcages, one with canaries and the other with Java sparrows, a hobby she took up after her husband’s death. Her room became cluttered with cat hair, millet, and bits of chickweed—signs of her grief and neglect of personal care. She preserved everything that had belonged to Bridau, even wrapping his last pen with a note. After his death she seemed to lose her sense of womanhood, consumed by sorrow and focused entirely on her children.

Agathe now lived for her children and worried over their financial struggles. Since moving to rue Mazarin a melancholy had settled on her face. She looked for support from the Emperor, who provided three hundred francs a year for each child, along with scholarships.

Her aunt, Madame Descoings, lived on the second floor and had assigned three thousand francs from her annuity to help Agathe. The Descoings now lived modestly on twelve hundred francs a year, employed a woman for morning chores, and shared dinners with the two widows. Madame Descoings doted on Philippe and Joseph, indulging them with treats and small sums.

Driven by maternal love, Madame Bridau kept her expenses low and cut off small pleasures. Distrustful of the future, she saved three thousand francs annually from her pension, hoping to secure a modest inheritance for her children. Thus both widows moved from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty, each for different reasons.

The view from her windows, the students’ tumult, and her husband’s portrait all contributed to the environment that would shape her son Joseph Bridau into a notable painter of the modern French school.

Philippe, the elder son, resembled his mother but had a bold demeanor often mistaken for courage. Agathe believed this physical likeness indicated moral similarity, hoping he would develop her sensitivity. At fifteen Philippe’s charm reinforced her hopes, while Joseph, three years younger, took after his father in less favorable ways. Joseph was untidy and often scolded compared to Philippe, and Agathe’s maternal preference for Philippe was plain.

Joseph’s artistic inclination began during a visit to the Tuileries where he was captivated by a caricature drawn on an Institute wall. This sparked his desire to draw, leading him to sneak into the Institute courtyard where older students teased him. His persistence, however, earned their respect and encouragement to pursue art.

Despite initial resistance, Joseph began visiting the atelier of the sculptor Chaudet, who recognized and encouraged his talent. Agathe feared for her son’s future and tried to dissuade him from art, believing it would mean hardship. Chaudet passionately defended the vocation, insisting true talent is a divine calling.

As Joseph pursued art, Agathe’s worries grew, especially when Philippe wanted to join the military. Enchanted by military life, Philippe secretly petitioned the Emperor to enlist. Within days he was sent to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr and rose rapidly, becoming a captain by nineteen.

Meanwhile Joseph’s scholarship was withdrawn and he sought to earn a living through art, encouraged by Madame Descoings. Despite Agathe’s concerns about his health, Joseph thrived in the artistic environment, forming close bonds with fellow students and mentors.

As politics shifted, Philippe returned home a decorated officer while Joseph faced uncertainty in his career. Agathe’s preference for Philippe became more apparent as she admired his military achievements and sacrifices for the Emperor. Joseph, less celebrated, remained devoted to his mother, who struggled to understand his artistic aims.

Despite their different paths, both brothers navigated the challenges of their careers, with Agathe’s love evident in her constant support and concern for their futures.

In 1816 Joseph won his mother’s consent to convert the adjoining garret into an atelier, partly funded by Madame Descoings. The two widows treated painting as a trade, but Joseph embraced it passionately, installing a skylight and chocolate-colored walls while continuing to work under Gros and Schinner.

Political unrest simmered in France: conspiracies and riots surrounded the Chamber of Deputies. Philippe, implicated in plots, was arrested and released for lack of evidence, but his military pay was cut and Agathe feared for him. The widows’ friends urged Agathe to send Philippe abroad for safety; she resisted the idea of him serving a foreign power.

Philippe ultimately joined General Lallemand in the United States to help found the Champ d’Asile, an enterprise that failed. Agathe equipped him with ten thousand francs, and by late 1817 she had adjusted to reduced means. Joseph lived frugally to support the family while his artistic talent developed.

In 1818 Roguin, the notary, absconded with funds intended for Madame Descoings. Soon after, Philippe sent a bill for a thousand francs, having lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. Agathe, distraught, blamed herself for urging his departure. Despite their own difficulties, Madame Descoings continued to support her.

Desperate for funds, Joseph turned to Gerard, who arranged for him to paint copies of a portrait of Louis XVIII. Joseph worked tirelessly, selling panels to cover Philippe’s debt. Philippe then returned from America; Agathe welcomed him with open arms, not perceiving how much he had changed.

Time abroad had hardened him. He became self-centered, indulged in drinking and gambling, and adopted a swaggering arrogance. Agathe, blinded by maternal love, excused his faults, viewing him as a victim of circumstance. The family’s finances deteriorated, and friends discussed selling possessions to help Philippe.

Philippe’s conduct alarmed acquaintances: he rejected serving the Bourbons, which hampered employment prospects, yet he lived as if supported by others. He frequently disappeared for days, maintained a lavish lifestyle, and grew distant from family responsibilities. He favored cafes and gambling halls, deepened friendships with former comrades, and showed increasing disregard for others.

Still, Philippe sought to impress Mariette, a young dancer. Giroudeau encouraged the pursuit and promised to help Philippe enter journalism, proposing he exploit his experiences for advantage. Philippe’s ambitions clashed with reality; he struggled to adapt to civilian life and grew more hostile to the Liberal party, blaming it for his misfortunes and nursing a desire for revenge against those he felt had wronged him.

When a potential job materialized, Agathe and Madame Descoings breathed easier and agreed to secure Philippe’s position, overlooking the sacrifices already made. The role—as cashier at a newspaper—brought temporary relief but did little to reform him. His arrogance continued to grow, and Joseph felt increasingly overshadowed and unappreciated despite his sacrifices and rising talent.

By the end, family dynamics had shifted markedly. Philippe’s return and changed behavior strained relationships and exposed fault lines in loyalty, ambition, and judgment. The once close-knit household faced tests that revealed the consequences of choices made in pursuit of dreams, the limits of maternal devotion, and the fragile balance between personal aspiration and familial duty.

Three months later the colonel, who consumed enough for four men, still criticized the food while the widows, reluctant to press him for payment, spent lavishly on meals though he had not contributed a penny. His mother and Madame Descoings hesitated to remind him of his promise. A year passed without any of the “tigers with five claws” Leon Gozlan referred to finding their way from Philippe’s pocket into the household purse. The colonel soothed his conscience by rarely dining at home. “Well, he is happy,” his mother remarked, “he has a place.”

Mariette, influenced by a feuilleton edited by Vernou, made her debut at the Porte-Saint-Martin, triumphing alongside the famous Begrand. One of the theatre's directors, a wealthy general enamored with an actress, became her impresario and, knowing Philippe and Giroudeau, arranged Mariette’s first appearance—an example of the solidarity among those captivated by folly.

Bixiou soon revealed to his grandmother and Agathe that Philippe, the cashier, was in love with Mariette, the celebrated ballet-dancer. This shocked the two widows; Agathe’s religion led her to view all stage women as morally corrupt, and both believed such women lived extravagantly.

“Do you think my brother is foolish enough to spend money on Mariette? Such women ruin rich men,” Joseph remarked. Bixiou added, “They’re considering engaging Mariette at the Opera. Don’t worry, Madame Bridau; the diplomatic body often visits the Porte-Saint-Martin, and that beautiful girl won’t stay long with your son. I heard an ambassador is madly in love with her.”

Philippe, motivated by financial interest, proposed marriage to Mariette, but she, anticipating an engagement at the Grand Opera, declined—either sensing his motives or valuing her independence. For the rest of the year Philippe visited his mother only twice a month, spending his time at the office, the theatre, or with Mariette. His friends saw him enjoying pleasure, mingling with Tullia, a leading singer, and other entertainers.

This carnival lasted eighteen months but was not without trouble. After her Opera debut in January 1821 Mariette quickly attracted a distinguished duke. Philippe, driven by passion, began dipping into the funds he managed, taking eleven hundred francs by May. Mariette then left for London, seeking lords to support her while the temporary opera house was prepared. Philippe, despite her infidelities, remained deeply in love; she viewed him as a stepping stone.

Forced to let Mariette go, Philippe returned to his mother’s apartment, reflecting on his inability to live without the pleasures of the past year. Suicide crossed his mind—not from the deficit but because he could not bear life without Mariette’s company. He entered his brother Joseph’s studio, where Joseph was painting.

“Is that how pictures are made?” Philippe asked, starting a conversation. Joseph explained he was copying a picture for a dealer and detailed his earnings. Philippe, pale and subdued, inquired how long it would take to paint his portrait, revealing he wished to leave his mother a likeness. When Joseph asked if he was going away again, Philippe replied, “I am going never to return.”

Pressed by Joseph, Philippe confessed he had taken money from the funds and could not live with the dishonor. Joseph reassured him he would not be dishonored if he repaid the money, but Philippe, feeling hopeless, rushed downstairs, leaving Joseph anxious.

Joseph informed Madame Descoings, who was horrified. Agathe, learning the truth, was devastated, fearing for Philippe’s life. “He! to fail in honor! the son of Bridau to take the money that was trusted to him!” she cried. Madame Descoings urged hope, suggesting Philippe’s misfortune stemmed from a bad woman; Agathe, however, lived in terror for her son.

That day Philippe calmly submitted his accounts, fabricating a story that he had retained the money for safekeeping. He then went to a gambling house, where he first won but ultimately lost everything. Returning home he found concern and affection from his family, which comforted him.

“Promise not to kill yourself, and all shall be forgotten,” his mother pleaded. Philippe, realizing the threat had worked, feigned gratitude and affection, embracing his mother. They shared dinner, and Philippe, despite earlier despair, felt relieved.

The next day Agathe appeared older and more anxious, aware their financial situation was dire. Madame Descoings, concerned for the family's honor, suggested consulting Desroches, who could help manage the affair. Agathe agreed, hoping to protect Philippe’s reputation.

Desroches, fulfilling his diplomatic role, advised Agathe to sign the transfer of funds and petition for Philippe’s reinstatement in the army. He assured them Philippe would soon regain his rank, relieving some worry. Still, Agathe’s faith was shaken and she lived in constant fear of his potential downfall.

Philippe, back to a life of leisure, resumed gambling and drinking, appearing disheveled and indifferent to his family’s plight. His love for Mariette had hardened his heart; he treated women with contempt. Meanwhile Joseph focused on painting, gaining recognition and support from Madame Descoings, who believed in his future success.

As Madame Descoings pursued her lottery dreams she remained oblivious to growing tensions. Joseph, noticing discrepancies in his finances, suspected Philippe. He confided in Madame Descoings, who shared his concerns. They devised a plan to catch Philippe in the act, hoping to confront him.

When Joseph discovered Philippe had indeed taken money from him, he felt betrayed. Despite his anger, he recognized Philippe as his brother and struggled with confronting him. Madame Descoings urged sympathy for Philippe’s struggles, but Joseph remained wary of his brother’s influence on the family’s future.

Despite Philippe's careful approach to his nightly gambling, he sometimes found himself "cleaned out." Needing his evening stake of ten francs, he began stealing from his family—taking from his brother and whatever Madame Descoings or Agathe left about. After a distressing dream in which Philippe robbed her, Agathe realized the depth of his decline. She begged him to ask for money instead of stealing, but within two weeks her savings were gone and she considered work. She took worsted work for a meager twenty sous a day. The strain of her hidden anguish showed in her appearance; she spent sleepless nights waiting for Philippe, noting his drunkenness and mood swings tied to gambling.

Madame Descoings suggested Philippe get new clothes; he retorted that his mother had no money and that he had mortgaged his pension. He had borrowed from Giroudeau, calling it a "debt of honor." Agathe defended him, believing he was out of his element, while Joseph criticized his neglect of appearance and duty. Philippe's gambling worsened and he became more debauched, losing his former good looks.

One day Philippe asked Joseph for money to pay a debt to the tobacconist; Joseph refused. Philippe then schemed to find where Madame Descoings kept her savings, guessing she would be out buying Christmas treats. He broke into her room and discovered twenty napoleons hidden in the mattress. He took the money, intending to gamble it.

At the gambling house Philippe won substantial sums over several sessions. By the end of the night he had amassed over one hundred fifty thousand francs. Intoxicated by success, he pressed on and lost everything. The next morning he woke in despair, having ruined his family.

Agathe and Madame Descoings waited for Philippe to return for Christmas dinner. When he failed to show, they worried. Madame Descoings had pinned hopes on her lottery stake, but when she checked her savings she found the money gone and fainted. Agathe, horrified, sought confirmation from the concierge, who revealed Philippe had been in the apartment that day.

Agathe returned pale and shaken; the family confronted Philippe. He dismissed their accusations, claiming he had taken what was owed him. The confrontation escalated into a physical altercation between Joseph and Philippe. As tensions peaked, Madame Descoings suffered a stroke and the family rushed to her side.

Afterward Joseph tried to help his mother with money, but she refused, clinging to the lottery hope. When the winning numbers were announced it became clear she had missed her chance, and she declined further. She died shortly after, leaving Agathe and Joseph to deal with the aftermath of Philippe's betrayal.

As Philippe recovered from his own illness, Agathe told him their financial situation had drastically changed and urged him to rejoin the army and support himself. He responded with indifference, feeling abandoned. Despite her tears she offered him money; he took it and left, seeking the company of those he called true friends. Agathe, heartbroken, prayed for her son and resigned herself to the loss of the child she once loved.

By February 1822, Madame Bridau had moved into the attic above the kitchen of her former apartment, recently occupied by her son Philippe. Seeing his mother’s reduced circumstances, Joseph resolved to make her comfortable: he rearranged the garret, added tasteful decor and a double door, and took Agathe to dine daily at a table-d’hôte that cost him nearly a hundred francs a month. Alarmed by the expense, Agathe, with her confessor’s help, accepted a seven-hundred-franc-a-year post at a lottery office that demanded long hours.

Agathe adapted to the routine, preparing Joseph’s breakfast and managing his household despite her lack of art knowledge. She mended his clothes, kept the studio tidy, and aided his work; their bond deepened through small acts of tenderness. They discussed Philippe, whose reckless life worried Agathe, but Joseph dismissed her fears, convinced Philippe was prospering in New York.

One afternoon Joseph visited Philippe, who worked at a newspaper. Philippe invited Joseph to dine, but Joseph declined to prioritize his mother. When he later told Agathe about Philippe, she felt renewed hope. She decorated Joseph’s studio and prepared a special breakfast for Philippe’s portrait sitting. During the session she hid, overwhelmed as she watched the two sons together.

After posing, Philippe invited them to dinner, which made Joseph uneasy because of Philippe’s apparent wealth; Agathe eagerly accepted. The lavish meal heightened Joseph’s suspicions about Philippe’s finances. At the next sitting, Agathe learned more about Philippe’s social circle—journalists and actresses—and heard his boastful ambitions, though he remained evasive about money.

Joseph had to postpone another sitting because of a pressing commission. When Philippe visited the studio he mistakenly took a valuable copy of a Rubens, believing it the original, and sold it for a fraction of its worth. Discovering the theft devastated Joseph; Agathe was heartbroken, feeling she had lost her son to vice.

Two months later an old officer told Agathe of Philippe’s dire condition: ill and needing money for care. Working at the lottery office, she had little to spare and refused to help the son she had banished from her heart. Joseph agreed, insisting Philippe should go to the hospital.

Some time after, Agathe encountered Philippe on the street—destitute and recently discharged from the hospital. Maternal instinct overcame her; she gave him money despite knowing it would likely be squandered, then fell into despair over his future.

Agathe later learned Philippe had been involved in a conspiracy that led to his arrest; she fainted when she heard his name. Joseph consulted Desroches, who advised them to secure the family inheritance. Desperate, Agathe wrote to her godmother for help but received a discouraging reply about her brother’s unwillingness to assist.

Desroches urged quick action to protect the children’s rights, suggesting they gather evidence of undue influence over Philippe and announcing plans to visit Issoudun during the holidays to help. Agathe and Joseph set off for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to face the consequences. As they traveled, Agathe reflected on the changes in Issoudun and the long estrangement from her brother, determined to confront the situation on her return.

Issoudun, one of France's oldest cities, is noted by Caesar and Rigord for its population and commerce. Recent excavations by Monsieur Armand Peremet uncovered a fifth-century basilica, probably unique in France, built from stones of a Roman temple to Isis. The town’s name, derived from "Isis" with "dun" indicating a religious and military settlement, points to ancient Celtic origins.

In the Middle Ages Issoudun was a center of power: Richard Coeur-de-lion built a tower above the basilica as part of fortifications, and the town later served as a stronghold for Routiers and Cottereaux mercenaries sent by Henry II against his son Richard. Yet its prominence has faded. After the 1830 insurrection vine-growers in Issoudun resisted government intervention, burned tax offices, and asserted autonomy, showing the force of a community of about eight thousand vine-growers.

In the fourteenth century Issoudun housed sixteen to seventeen thousand people, prospering on the wool trade and producing blankets and gloves. By Louis XIV’s time it enjoyed some elegance and social standing. Today the population has shrunk to about twelve thousand, including vine-growers from nearby suburbs. The local economy, especially wine, suffers from outdated practices; vine-growers oppose modernization, producing poor-quality wine that undermines potential prosperity.

This decline stems from a pervasive spirit of stagnation. When a proposal for a new road linking Paris and Toulouse through Issoudun arose, local leaders opposed it, fearing higher food prices. Their resistance exemplifies a broader provincial tendency to favor tradition over progress.

Issoudun’s social fabric has also deteriorated. The town lacks the old class distinctions: the nobility’s absence after historical upheavals removed a source of social competition and dynamism. The bourgeoisie, maintaining routine social forms, have become complacent, contributing to stagnation in governance and community life. Administration is dominated by intertwined local figures whose familial ties produce ineffectual leadership and weak accountability. The clergy exert little influence, further diminishing civic vitality.

Against this background the town’s youth, feeling trapped, formed the "Knights of Idleness," led by Maxence Gilet. Max, rumored to be the illegitimate son of a local official, was charismatic and adventurous. Raised in poverty and directionless, he enlisted in the military and distinguished himself in campaigns. Returning to Issoudun after the war, he clashed with the bourgeoisie, whose disapproval of his lifestyle and pranks only boosted his notoriety.

Max became a local hero after a violent confrontation with royalist officers, and his leadership of the Knights invigorated the town with mischief. The group staged pranks that both entertained and alarmed townspeople, executing elaborate hoaxes and frightful stunts that revealed their creativity and camaraderie. While disruptive, these antics brought excitement to an otherwise stagnant community.

The local tavern, run by Père and La Cognette, served as the Knights' secret meeting place where they planned escapades and shared meals. The couple, unaware of the full extent of the Knights’ activities, supported them out of self-interest, ensuring their silence and cooperation.

Issoudun’s ancient grandeur and archaeological significance contrast sharply with its modern decline marked by economic backwardness, social complacency, and weak institutions. The Knights of Idleness, under the magnetic Maxence Gilet, embody the youth’s frustration and thirst for excitement in a town that has lost much of its former vibrancy.

As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a clever prank. An old Spaniard, a former prisoner of war, left his empty cart at the foot of the Issoudun tower one morning. Maxence, arriving at a Knights' rendezvous, was asked, “What are we to do tonight?”

“Here’s Père Fario’s cart,” he replied. “Let’s get it up on the embankment first, and then decide.”

The tower of Issoudun, built by Richard Coeur-de-Lion, stands on the ruins of a basilica, which itself rests above a Roman temple and a Celtic dun. This layered history forms a mound rich with archaeological significance. The tower, comparable in height to the obelisk of Luxor, has an eighty-foot-high pedestal on the town side. In an hour the cart was disassembled and hoisted piece by piece to the top of the embankment, like soldiers moving artillery over the Grand Saint-Bernard. Once reassembled, the Knights, now hungry and thirsty, returned to Mère Cognette’s to laugh at the old Spaniard’s reaction when he came for his cart the next morning.

They did not engage in such antics every night; circumstances varied, and sometimes members refused because of personal ties. But they gathered regularly for daytime hunts, autumn vintages, and winter skating. Among the twenty youths, some were particularly close to Max, who had become their idol. The grandsons of Madame Hochon, François Hochon and Baruch Borniche, regarded Max as a cousin and relied on him for money and adventures their grandfather denied them.

At that moment François and Baruch sat beside Max at a dimly lit table after twelve to fifteen bottles of wine. Baruch, emboldened, warned Max, “You are threatened in your stronghold.”

“What do you mean?” Max asked.

“My grandmother received a letter from Madame Bridau, her goddaughter, saying she and her son are coming here. She’s preparing two rooms for them.”

“What’s that to me?” Max replied, downing his glass.

At thirty-four, Max’s soldierly appearance was striking: a well-defined face, clear skin, expressive eyes, slightly curly black hair, and a winning smile. He was well-proportioned and might have been a successful general.

“Don’t you know, Max,” called a young man from the other end of the table, “that Madame Hochon’s goddaughter is Rouget’s sister? If she’s coming with her son, she likely wants to secure the property when he dies, and then—goodbye to your harvest!”

Max frowned, gauging the reaction around the table. “What’s that to me?” he repeated.

“But,” François continued, “if old Rouget revokes his will—”

Max interrupted, “I’ve silenced those who meddle with you, François. Is this how you repay me? By using a contemptuous nickname for a woman I care about?”

Max had never before spoken so openly about his relationship with Flore Brazier, Rouget’s servant-mistress, whom society disparaged. He had always avoided discussing her, knowing it was sensitive. His friends had learned to tread carefully around it.

François hesitated; Max encouraged him. “Go on.”

“Faith, no!” François replied.

“Don’t be angry, Max,” said Goddet. “Didn’t we agree to speak freely here? If François slipped up, is that a crime against the Order of Idleness?”

“No,” Max conceded, “but it is against our friendship. I remembered we were in session, which is why I said, ‘Go on.’”

An awkward silence followed, broken by Max’s declaration: “I’ll continue for him—and for all of you—and tell you what you’re thinking. You believe Flore, the Rabouilleuse, has been providing for me since I returned to Issoudun. If I can throw away three hundred francs a month and treat you to dinners, you think it comes from her purse? Yes, ten thousand times yes! Flore aims for Rouget’s property.”

“She gets it from father to son,” Goddet remarked.

“You think I plan to marry Flore when Rouget dies, and that the arrival of these Parisians threatens my future?” Max continued.

“That’s it,” François affirmed.

“Don’t worry, friends,” Max reassured them. “If I need help to deal with these Parisians, will you assist me? Within the limits of our fooleries, of course. I’m not asking you to kill or poison them. If the Bridaus succeed and Flore has nothing but what she stands in, I’d be satisfied. I love her enough to prefer her to Mademoiselle Fichet, if she would have me.”

Mademoiselle Fichet was the wealthiest heiress in Issoudun, courted by Goddet. The Knights, moved by Max’s frankness, rose in unison.

“You’re a fine fellow, Max!”

“We’ll stand by you!”

“A fig for the Bridaus!”

“After all, it’s only three swains to a shepherdess.”

“Père Lousteau loved Madame Rouget; isn’t it better to love a housekeeper who isn’t tied down?”

“If Rouget is Max’s father, the affair is in the family.”

As the town buzzed with gossip about the impending duel, Philippe remained focused on his plan. He assured Rouget that Flore would soon be back and they would be happy together.

When Flore returned, Philippe confronted her, asserting control. He warned that if she did not comply with his wishes, he had ways to remove her from Rouget's life. Shaken by Philippe's demeanor, Flore realized how precarious her position was. Rouget, emotionally vulnerable, clung to her, believing she would return to him.

The next morning the town speculated about the duel. Philippe and Gilet's confrontation loomed; many hoped it would resolve the tensions. Philippe, confident in his strategy, prepared for the banquet, knowing the outcome would determine the future of Rouget's fortune and his own ambitions.

Around four o’clock the next day, officers of the old army gathered in the place du Marché in Issoudun, waiting for Colonel Philippe Bridau for a banquet celebrating the coronation. Conversation turned to Max’s recent dismissal from old Rouget’s house; only Potel and Renard defended him. An old sub-lieutenant said that if Max had taken up residence in Rouget’s house he should not have been dismissed without a fight. When Max arrived he received a cold welcome, sharply contrasting with the warm reception for Philippe and strengthening Max’s resolve to confront him.

At dinner Philippe proposed a toast to the Grand Army and was enthusiastically echoed by eleven soldiers, including Benjamin and Kouski. Mignonnet then toasted those who had tried to restore the Emperor’s son, and Max rose to make an ironic wish that everyone meet again next year. Tension spread as Max’s reputation made his words heavy with meaning. Philippe and Max exchanged barbs; Philippe remained calm while Max struggled to keep his composure. The meal continued tensely until dessert, and the duel was set for the next morning with sabres; Goddet, a former army surgeon, was to attend.

That night Monsieur and Madame Hochon visited Philippe to urge him to pray before the duel. Madame Hochon offered a relic for protection, which he lightly declined, though she insisted on praying for him. The next morning Max arrived at the duel site with his seconds and prepared for combat. Both men tried to appear composed, but Max felt intimidated by Philippe’s superior stance.

When the duel began Max launched an aggressive move but was quickly disarmed by Philippe, who paused to let Max recover his weapon. This courtesy increased the tension. When fighting resumed they exchanged fierce blows; Philippe was wounded but landed a fatal stroke. Max died, ending his ambitions and the Order of Idleness, much to the town’s relief.

Flore Brazier, devastated by Max’s death, fell ill with fever and inflammation. Philippe, recovering from his wounds, wrote letters outlining plans to secure his inheritance and manage Flore’s situation. He observed that Flore’s illness prevented her from fleeing and intended to use her to his advantage.

By February 1823 Flore, urged by those around her and by Philippe’s suggestions that marriage would secure her future, began to consider marrying Rouget. Philippe manipulated the situation, presenting himself as a supportive figure while secretly arranging to benefit from the match. Flore, recovering, was persuaded to accept Philippe’s proposal, which he framed as a path to respectability.

The marriage contract gave Flore a substantial dower and a life annuity. Agathe, Philippe’s mother, delighted in her son’s apparent reform and the town’s admiration. But Desroches, the family lawyer, warned Agathe that Philippe’s cunning would likely prevent her and Joseph from inheriting Rouget’s property.

Once married to Flore, Philippe asserted control over her and the household, demanding a power of attorney from Rouget. He planned to use Flore to manage his uncle’s affairs while keeping a close watch on her. His ambitions extended beyond Issoudun: he sought to reestablish himself in the army and secure a future in Paris.

In a letter to the minister of war Philippe professed loyalty to the new regime and requested reinstatement in the royal army, enclosing endorsements from local officials praising his conduct. These references ultimately led to his reinstatement as lieutenant-colonel.

Lieutenant-Colonel Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, whom he took to the Treasury to transfer his uncle's income to himself. The old man and the Rabouilleuse were soon immersed in the extravagant Parisian lifestyle; Rouget succumbed to excess and died after a lavish supper. Lolotte, a ballet dancer, was implicated in his demise, which was attributed to indigestion.

Madame Rouget prospered in this new environment, but Philippe had Mariette keep her under watch to prevent serious missteps. In October 1823 Philippe went to Issoudun to settle his uncle's estate, quickly amassing a fortune of sixteen hundred thousand francs, excluding valuable paintings held by Monsieur Hochon. He entrusted the money to Mongenod and Sons for investment.

Philippe later married, securing his wife's fortune in case she died childless. He bought a furnished apartment for her and secretly acquired a grand hotel in the rue de Clichy, investing heavily in renovations and furnishings. The rise of Charles X favored the Duc de Chaulieu's family, and because Philippe supplied information about past conspiracies he was appointed lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse's regiment. His court connections led to promotion and ennoblement as Comte de Brambourg.

Ambitious and insatiable, Philippe sought further honors, aiming for a post as aide-de-camp to the Dauphin. He distanced himself from his past, refusing to associate with old friends who might jeopardize his status. Philippe planned to marry Mademoiselle Amelie de Soulanges and aspired to become a general, acquiring military distinctions to bolster his ambitions.

Meanwhile Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist, supported by his mother Agathe, who managed a lottery office. Despite talent, Joseph faced financial hardship and criticism while Philippe's success overshadowed him. Agathe, misled by appearances, believed Philippe's rise justified his neglect of them.

In desperation Agathe wrote to Philippe asking for financial help for Joseph. Philippe's cold reply devastated her and hastened her decline. As she neared death she sought consolation from her confessor, who revealed the painful truth about her sons' contrasts. Agathe’s realization that she had misplaced her affections deepened her sorrow; she died shortly after.

Joseph, heartbroken, buried his mother alone. Philippe, indifferent to her death, was repelled by Joseph's scathing letter. Bereft, Joseph found solace among friends who supported him through his grief.

Later Joseph received a letter from Flore, Philippe's wife, pleading for help as she faced destitution. Moved, Joseph and Bixiou visited her and found a shadow of her former self. They arranged for medical care, but her condition was grave. Their discovery revealed the depths of Philippe's cruelty and neglect.

As Flore’s health deteriorated, Joseph and his friends plotted to expose Philippe's abandonment and ensure Flore received justice, while Philippe continued to rise socially, blind to the consequences of his actions. His fortunes, however, collapsed during the July Revolution of 1830, precipitating his downfall.

In the end Joseph inherited Philippe's estate and found success in his art, becoming Comte de Brambourg. Despite his achievements he remained humble, often reflecting on his family's tragic fate and the lessons learned from their tumultuous lives.
