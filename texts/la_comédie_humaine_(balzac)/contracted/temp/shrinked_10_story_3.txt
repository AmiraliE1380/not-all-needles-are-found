For effusive souls, twilight — that soft hour between day and night — invites reflection on art. During such an hour a young painter, absorbed in finishing a large canvas, falls from his ladder, strikes his head, and loses consciousness.

He wakes to the gentle voice of a young woman and her mother, who had rushed to his aid. As he regains his senses he is struck by the girl's beauty, matching the feminine ideal he has long imagined. The older woman, resembling a refined marquise, tends him kindly and advises on his recovery. The painter thanks them, but the two women, naturally reserved, depart without pressing for further acquaintance.

After they leave, the porter’s wife tells him the visitors are Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, long-time residents of the building. The painter, Hippolyte Schinner, has recently moved into the attic studio, having won some recognition. Determined to provide better for his mother, who sacrificed much for him, he remains reserved and reluctant to share his private life despite growing success.

During convalescence Hippolyte keeps returning in thought to the two women, especially the young girl's image, which lingers. When he is well enough to return to his studio he is eager to visit them, feeling a bond with their situation; he imagines they share a misfortune that draws him to them.

At their modest home he notes signs of poverty but feels no contempt, only empathy. Mademoiselle Leseigneur greets him politely, and he is struck by the contrast between their humble surroundings and the elegance they display. The room contains remnants of past luxury — faded and worn — yet everything is meticulously kept.

The conversation begins stiffly but soon turns to art, allowing Hippolyte to speak freely about his thoughts. Madame Leseigneur reveals how dear a portrait of her late husband, a naval officer, is to her, though it is deteriorating. Hippolyte offers to paint a new portrait, hoping to forge a closer connection; the offer is accepted with gracious gratitude, and he senses the emotional weight behind it.

As they talk, Adelaide steps out briefly and returns with candles to light the room. The atmosphere shifts when an unexpected visitor arrives — a man who seems acquainted with Adelaide. His presence stirs jealousy in Hippolyte, who wants to know the nature of their relationship.

The newcomer is an older gentleman in a coat whose style recalls a bygone era; he carries an air of confidence mixing nobility and libertinism, suggesting a complicated past. The exchange between him and the women reveals layers of their lives, leaving Hippolyte to ponder the dynamics at play and the histories hinted at by their manners and circumstances.

Throughout the evening Hippolyte wrestles with admiration for Adelaide and uncertainty about their differing social standings. He is drawn to her spirit and grace, increasingly aware of the delicate balance between their worlds and of the slender hope for connection amid the shadows of their circumstances.

A remarkable figure stood beside the “Louis XIV’s light infantry” specimen — the Bonapartists’ nickname for those remnants of the Monarchy. A lean, aged man, he was a faded echo of the first: a worn coat, dulled powder, a feebler intellect, the living reflection of Rivarol’s quip on Champcenetz.

This silent old man puzzled the painter, the Chevalier. He neither spoke nor was spoken to; his relation to the gallant man was unclear—friend, relative, or mere companion. He invited curiosity but offered no answers, especially about any past tie to the gallant’s deceased wife.

The least dilapidated of the group approached Madame de Rouville, kissed her hand, and sat beside her. Another bowed and took a seat nearby. Adelaide leaned over the old gentleman’s chair, echoing a famous painting’s pose, though his familiarity seemed to irritate her.

“Are you sulky with me?” he asked, casting a shrewd glance at Schinner, the painter, as if gauging his standing.

“This is our neighbor,” Madame de Rouville introduced Hippolyte, the celebrated painter. The old man acknowledged Hippolyte, noting the artist’s red ribbon against his own hard-earned honors.

Hippolyte, captivated by Adelaide’s beauty, grew lost in admiration, momentarily forgetting their squalor. Absorbed, he answered briefly; the old gentleman lamented card losses. Adelaide offered help, but he insisted she stay where he could see her.

As the game ended, the gentleman complained about his bad luck. The silent figure echoed the hour, prompting Hippolyte to leave, still entranced by the first stirrings of love.

The next day Hippolyte felt an overwhelming urge to see Adelaide. He hesitated until afternoon before visiting Madame de Rouville and awkwardly requested the Baron’s portrait. Adelaide, alone, asked him to fetch it, revealing her modesty. Their shared glance conveyed more than words, and Hippolyte took the portrait, promising a swift return.

In the following days their relationship blossomed with laughter and shared thought. Schinner, eager to learn piquet, often lost, while an unspoken bond between Hippolyte and Adelaide deepened. They became inseparable; Adelaide’s affection grew, and Hippolyte’s jealousy flared when the old Count showed undue familiarity.

Adelaide worried that Hippolyte’s social life harmed his health. Their love matured without grand gestures, built on trust and delicate exchanges. Within two months their lives were intertwined: Hippolyte visited daily, and their connection deepened.

The old gentleman’s visits waned as Hippolyte took his place at the card table, sharing in the losses. Yet Hippolyte couldn’t shake concern over Madame de Rouville’s finances. He spent two months on the Baron’s portrait, calling it one of his best, but the Baroness’s silence puzzled him.

One day Adelaide visited his studio and was awestruck by the portrait; their charged moment overflowed with unspoken feeling. Hippolyte, overwhelmed, declared his desire to marry her, but the old vice-admiral arrived, praised the work, and hinted at funds to pay for both portraits.

As the evening went on, Hippolyte noticed Madame de Rouville’s unusual excitement during cards and suspected a gambling habit. He began to question the relationship’s nature, wondering if deception hid beneath the intimacy. After a night of turmoil he discovered his purse missing and suspected Adelaide and her mother of theft.

Despite heartbreak, he clung to hope of misunderstanding. The next day a friend mentioned rumors about Adelaide and her mother, unsettling him further. Torn between love and doubt, he resolved to confront his feelings and visit Madame de Rouville.

Seeing Adelaide, pale and sad, he felt ashamed of his earlier suspicions. Their talk revealed mutual longing, and he realized his mistake. The evening grew sweet as they played cards, but his fears returned when he noticed Adelaide’s secretive glances at her mother.

During the game, Adelaide discreetly placed a new purse before Hippolyte, revealing her thoughtful attempt to replace the lost one. Overwhelmed, he grasped the depth of their bond and impulsively declared his intention to marry her.

Just then the old vice-admiral arrived, announcing his promotion and expressing support for the young couple. The evening ended with unity as the families came together, dispelling lingering doubts.

This tale of love, trust, and misunderstanding shows characters navigating complex relations and ultimately finding solace in shared bonds and the promise of a brighter future.
