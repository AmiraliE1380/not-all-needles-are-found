**THE PURSE**

By Honore De Balzac  
Translated by Clara Bell  

To Sofka

“Have you observed, mademoiselle, that the painters and sculptors of the Middle Ages, when they placed two figures in adoration, one on each side of a fair Saint, never failed to give them a family likeness? When you see your name among those dear to me, remember that touching harmony, and you will see this not so much as an act of homage but as an expression of brotherly affection from your devoted servant,  
“DE BALZAC.”

**THE PURSE**

There exists a delightful hour for those who easily express their emotions, a time when day transitions into night. The twilight casts soft lights and playful shadows, creating a dreamy atmosphere that inspires artists to reflect on their work. In this serene silence, the material aspects of art fade away, allowing the imagination to flourish. A young painter, lost in contemplation while working on a nearly finished painting, suddenly fell from his ladder, striking his head and losing consciousness.

He was awakened by a sweet voice and the gentle touch of two women. As he regained his senses, he saw a beautiful girl, embodying the ideal beauty he had always envisioned. Her delicate features and graceful form captivated him. The older woman, resembling a refined marquise, held a lamp and advised the girl. They had entered the studio after hearing his fall, and the younger woman explained how they had come to his aid.

The painter, Hippolyte Schinner, expressed his gratitude, but the older woman insisted he take care of his injury. The two women left, their concern for him evident but without any intrusive familiarity. Their noble manners left a lasting impression on Hippolyte.

Hippolyte had recently moved into the attic of a house in the Rue de Suresnes, where he could work without the long commute that had previously burdened him. His talent had begun to earn him recognition, and he was determined to provide a better life for his mother, who had sacrificed much for him. Mademoiselle Schinner, his mother, had raised him alone after being betrayed by a wealthy man. She devoted her life to him, and now that he was gaining success, he hoped to restore her happiness.

Despite his growing fame, Hippolyte remained reserved and did not easily share his life with others. He was deeply affected by the kindness of the two women who had helped him, and he felt a connection to them, particularly to the young girl, Adelaide. He imagined they shared a similar background of hardship.

After a few days of recovery, Hippolyte returned to his studio, eager to see Adelaide again. He found himself thinking of her constantly, and he made noise in his studio to attract her attention. Eventually, he decided to visit her, feeling a mix of excitement and nervousness.

Upon entering their modest home, he was struck by the signs of poverty that surrounded them. Mademoiselle Leseigneur welcomed him, and he thanked her for her care. The room was filled with remnants of past elegance, but it was clear that they were struggling. Despite this, Hippolyte felt no disdain; instead, he empathized with their situation.

As they conversed, the old lady and Adelaide engaged him in discussions about art, which eased the initial awkwardness. Hippolyte admired the old pastel portrait of a naval officer, which the mother cherished. He offered to paint a new portrait, believing it would preserve the likeness more effectively than the fading pastel.

Madame Leseigneur accepted his offer, and as they spoke, she revealed that her husband had died in battle, leaving her without support. The conversation deepened their connection, and Hippolyte felt a growing affection for Adelaide.

Their interactions continued over the following days, with Hippolyte visiting regularly. He found joy in their company, and Adelaide’s demeanor became more open and affectionate. They shared laughter and stories, and their bond strengthened without the need for overt declarations of love.

However, Hippolyte’s happiness was tinged with concern for their financial struggles. He noticed the old lady’s increasing eagerness to win at cards, which made him uneasy. He began to suspect that they might be living off gambling, and this thought troubled him.

One evening, after a game, he realized his purse was missing. The theft shattered his illusions about Adelaide and her mother. He felt betrayed and confused, struggling to reconcile his feelings for her with the reality of their situation. Despite his suspicions, he could not bring himself to confront them.

Days passed, and Hippolyte’s heartache grew. He longed to see Adelaide again but was torn by his doubts. When he finally visited, he found her looking pale and distressed. Their reunion was bittersweet, filled with unspoken emotions and lingering tension.

As they played cards, Adelaide discreetly slipped a new purse in front of him, revealing that she had replaced the one he lost. Overwhelmed with gratitude and love, Hippolyte embraced her, asking for her hand in marriage. Their joy was interrupted by the arrival of the vice-admiral, who had come to support Madame de Rouville.

In the end, the bonds of love and friendship triumphed over suspicion and misunderstanding. The evening concluded with a sense of unity and hope for the future, as the characters navigated the complexities of their lives and relationships.
