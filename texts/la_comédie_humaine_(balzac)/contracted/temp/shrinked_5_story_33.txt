THE ILLUSTRIOUS GAUDISSART

By Honore De Balzac

Translated By Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

THE ILLUSTRIOUS GAUDISSART

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller, a modern figure, symbolizes the shift from material enterprise to intellectual force. This century links isolated power with universal might, equalizing products and spreading them among the masses. The commercial traveller carries ideas, bringing light from the center to the provinces. He is a self-taught scholar and a juggler who knows the world superficially, a link between village and capital. A jester adept at mimicking political opinions, he must swiftly judge people's habits and solvency for his trade.

He knows haunts of vice and virtue across France and persuades with eloquence. Quick to seize opportunities and outmaneuver rivals, many such agents exist abroad, negotiating goods and often showing truer diplomacy than ambassadors. The commercial traveller embodies civilization’s spirit, bringing Parisian innovations to provincial life, often by persuasive language.

A shrewd economist observed that provincial policies are often collected by agents who coax the errant to renew. Conversation is a form of seduction; a nation with two Chambers and a woman who listens is easily swayed.

Consider this traveller closely. His attire—an olive green overcoat and a striped shirt—reflects his character. He is an acrobat in commerce, extracting wealth from the provinces through intellectual persuasion, using a flood of phrases to engage the populace.

In Paris lives an exceptional commercial traveller, Gaudissart, who embodies qualities for success. His speech is persuasive and disarming; he made his fortune in the hat trade, attracting vendors’ attention. His life is festive; he is celebrated wherever he goes, earning the title "Illustrious."

Gaudissart's reputation precedes him; he is welcomed in salons and taverns. Jovial and free-spirited, yet capable of serious business, he has mastered diplomacy, adapting his persona to his audiences. Until 1830 he remained loyal to hats, but that year’s upheaval led him to abandon them for ideas and speculation.

The events of 1830 birthed an era where ideas became commodities. Writers and thinkers sold thoughts, and speculators sought profit. Gaudissart recognized this shift and accepted an offer from a life-insurance company promising a lucrative commission. He trained to grasp the business and was soon celebrated for his talents.

Company heads boasted of Gaudissart, prompting newspaper offers. He agreed to sell both life insurance and subscriptions, confident of success.

CHAPTER II

For a week Gaudissart immersed himself in Saint-Simonism and financial diplomacy. By mid-April he was ready to travel. Yet two commercial houses persuaded him back to the hat trade with substantial commissions.

In a hackney-coach with a florist named Jenny, Gaudissart spoke of refurnishing her room from his travel profits. Jenny doubted his promises, prompting playful banter about his business.

Gaudissart assured her he would earn far more than before, aiming for a significant income from his ventures. He expressed ambition, comparing himself to successful figures in politics and commerce.

Next morning he set off for towns assigned to him, spent time in Blois, and wrote Jenny of his progress. He boasted of selling life insurance and newspaper subscriptions, especially the "Children's Journal," which he found profitable.

CHAPTER III

Gaudissart arrived in Vouvray, confident his persuasive skills would prevail. He soon encountered a local mindset resistant to new ideas. The Tourangians, witty and satirical, despised Saint-Simonism.

At the Soleil d’Or inn he met Monsieur Vernier, a local skeptic of commercial travellers. Aware of Gaudissart's fame, Vernier decided to trick him by sending him to Margaritis, a local lunatic famed for eccentricity.

Margaritis, obsessed with his wine and newspapers, engaged Gaudissart in conversation that quickly turned absurd. Despite Gaudissart's attempts to sell life insurance, Margaritis's madness produced nonsensical exchanges.

CHAPTER IV

Seated in his home, Margaritis spoke with delusions, while two women listened amused and Vernier plotted to make a fool of Gaudissart. Margaritis's erratic behavior and wine obsession thwarted Gaudissart's composure.

As the talk went on, Margaritis's madness grew clearer, yielding misunderstandings. Gaudissart explained life insurance benefits, but Margaritis replied incoherently, frustrating both.

Despite the absurdity, Gaudissart persisted in selling insurance. The lunatic's unpredictability, however, made success increasingly unlikely.

CHAPTER V

After humorous exchanges, the innkeeper revealed Margaritis was indeed a lunatic. Enraged at the trick, Gaudissart confronted Vernier, demanding satisfaction for the insult. Townspeople amused themselves with the drama; Vernier's refusal to apologize inflamed Gaudissart.

The quarrel escalated toward a duel. The innkeeper, fearing consequences, proposed mediation over breakfast.

Ultimately Gaudissart and Vernier reconciled, recognizing the absurdity of their dispute. Gaudissart left Vouvray, vowing to return to Paris and resume his work, with a renewed appreciation for provincial peculiarities.

On his journey back he reflected on challenges and lessons from Vouvray, especially the importance of reading local mindsets when navigating commerce and human interactions.
