DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller, a modern figure, marks the shift from material enterprise to intellectual force: his profession connects isolated, local power with universal influence, equalizing products and spreading ideas from the center to the provinces. He brings the capital’s light to the countryside, a self-taught scholar and juggler who knows the world superficially, a link between village and metropolis. Adept at mimicry, quick to gauge habits and solvency, he must judge people fast for his trade.

He learns the haunts of vice and virtue across France, persuading with eloquence, seizing opportunities, and outmaneuvering rivals. Abroad such agents often exhibit truer diplomacy than ambassadors, negotiating commercial treaties in taverns rather than salons. The commercial traveller embodies civilization’s spirit, importing Parisian innovations into provincial life by persuasive language.

Observe one such traveller. His olive-green overcoat and striped shirt announce his profession. An acrobat in commerce, he extracts wealth from the provinces by engaging them with a flood of phrases. In Paris, Gaudissart is an exceptional representative of this type: persuasive, disarming, and adept at courting attention. He made his fortune in the hat trade, enchanting vendors and winning social favor. Jovial and festive, yet businesslike when needed, he masters diplomacy by adapting to his audiences.

Until 1830 Gaudissart stuck to hats; the upheaval that year made ideas into commodities. Writers and thinkers began selling thought, and speculators followed. Gaudissart seized this change, accepting a life-insurance commission and quickly learning the business. His employers lauded him; newspapers offered him roles selling their subscriptions too. Confident in his persuasive talents, he agreed to sell both life insurance and periodicals.

CHAPTER II

After a week steeped in Saint-Simonism and financial talk, Gaudissart prepared to travel by mid-April. Yet two commercial houses tempted him back into hats with good commissions. Riding in a hackney with Jenny, a florist, he joked about refurnishing her room from his travels; she doubted such promises, prompting playful banter about his business prospects.

Gaudissart assured her he would earn much more than before, aiming for a substantial income from insurance and subscriptions. He expressed ambition, measuring himself against successful figures in politics and commerce. The next morning he left for the towns assigned him, stopped in Blois, and wrote to Jenny boasting of selling life insurance and particularly the profitable "Children’s Journal."

CHAPTER III

Arriving in Vouvray, Gaudissart expected his persuasive skills to prevail but met a provincial mindset resistant to new ideas. The Tourangians were witty, satirical, and hostile to Saint-Simonism. At the Soleil d’Or inn he encountered Monsieur Vernier, a local skeptical of commercial travellers and aware of Gaudissart’s reputation. Vernier decided to play a trick: he sent Gaudissart to Margaritis, a local reputed lunatic.

Margaritis, obsessed with his wine and newspapers, began a conversation that quickly turned absurd. Despite Gaudissart’s efforts to explain life insurance, the man’s eccentricities produced nonsensical replies. Gaudissart maintained his composure at first, trying to make sense of the madman’s chatter and to sell his product.

CHAPTER IV

Seated at home and drinking, Margaritis spoke with delusions while two women listened amused and Vernier watched, intent on humiliating Gaudissart. Margaritis’s erratic behavior—his obsession with wine, newspapers, and peculiar views—thwarted Gaudissart’s attempts to reason. The talk devolved into misunderstandings and incoherent exchanges. Gaudissart explained the benefits of life insurance, but Margaritis replied in unrelated or absurd terms, frustrating the salesman and entertaining the onlookers.

Despite the absurdity, Gaudissart persisted in his sales pitch, but the lunatic’s unpredictability made success unlikely. The situation exposed the limits of persuasive rhetoric when confronted with deliberate mockery and provincial antipathy.

CHAPTER V

After prolonged banter, the innkeeper disclosed that Margaritis was indeed a lunatic and that Vernier had engineered the encounter to trick Gaudissart. Enraged at the insult, Gaudissart confronted Vernier, demanding satisfaction. The townspeople enjoyed the spectacle; Vernier’s refusal to apologize escalated the tension into a duel. The innkeeper, fearing consequences, proposed mediation over breakfast.

Ultimately the dispute was patched up: Gaudissart and Vernier reconciled, recognizing the absurdity of their quarrel. Gaudissart left Vouvray resolved to return to Paris and his usual work, with a renewed appreciation for provincial peculiarities and the necessity of reading local mindsets. On the journey back he reflected on the challenges and lessons of Vouvray—how commerce and human interaction require not only eloquence but discernment about whom one bargains with and when persuasion becomes folly.
