Bianchon, a physician who had won early repute in Paris by contributing to theoretical physiology, began as a surgeon before taking up medicine. His earliest studies were guided by one of France’s greatest surgeons, the illustrious Desplein, who shone across science like a meteor. By common consent Desplein had an incommunicable method. Like many geniuses he left no heirs; his talent made no permanent trace. The glory of surgeons resembles that of actors: both are heroes of a moment, living only while they live.

Desplein’s fame shows how such transient genius fades outside its field. His name, once famous, will survive chiefly within his specialty. To reach broader historical stature requires circumstances that lift a professor from scientific history into the general human narrative. Did Desplein possess the universal command that makes a man the living word, the great figure of an age? He had a godlike eye; by intuition he discerned the sufferer and the malady, determining the precise time to operate and accounting for atmospheric conditions and temperament. Whether he deduced this from studying the assimilation of atmospheric elements or by analogy like Cuvier, he seemed to grasp the human frame in past and future while emphasizing the present.

But he did not epitomize all science as Hippocrates, Galen, or Aristotle did, nor did he guide a whole school to new worlds. Though a persistent observer of human chemistry, acquainted with elements in fusion and causes of life, everything in him was personal. Isolated by egoism in life, that trait now mars his glory; on his tomb there is no proclaiming statue to imprint his discoveries on posterity.

Perhaps Desplein’s genius accounted for his beliefs, and thus his mortality. He saw the atmosphere as a generative envelope and the earth as an egg; unable to decide which came first, he rejected both the antecedent animal and a surviving spirit. He had no doubts; he was positive. His bold atheism resembled that of many scientific men—good men yet invincible atheists, the kind religious people deem impossible. Accustomed from youth to dissect humans before, during, and after life, he hunted through organs without finding the individual soul essential to religious theory. Finding cerebral and nervous centres and a centre for aerating the blood—the first two so complementary that he later thought hearing and sight could be supplied by the solar plexus—he inferred two "souls" in man and embraced atheism, though that does not disprove God. He died impenitent, as many noble geniuses do.

Desplein’s greatness was marred by many smaller contradictions, which enemies labeled meannesses. Envious fools, ignorant of the motives of superior spirits, seize superficial inconsistencies to condemn. If actions succeed, showing correlation between preliminaries and results, some charges still cling. In our day, Napoleon was condemned for rashness; only later events explained earlier moves.

Because Desplein’s glory and science were invulnerable, critics attacked his odd moods and temper; in truth he was eccentric. Sometimes handsomely dressed, sometimes indifferent; sometimes in a carriage, sometimes on foot; by turns rough and kind—harsh and covetous outwardly yet capable of offering his whole fortune to exiled patrons—he provoked contradictory judgments. Though he might drop a prayer-book at Court to win a ribbon, in his heart he mocked everything; after studying humanity from every angle, he held a deep contempt for men, seeing their genuine expression in both solemn and mean acts.

The qualities of a great man are often federative. Even if one has more talent than wit, his wit surpasses that of someone merely called "witty." Genius presupposes moral insight; he who can see a flower can see the sun. A man who, on hearing a diplomat he saved ask, "How is the Emperor?" replies, "The courtier is alive; the man will follow!" shows not only medical skill but prodigious wit. Hence a student of human nature will accept Desplein’s exorbitant pretensions and believe—as Desplein believed—that he might have been as great a minister as a surgeon.

Among the riddles of Desplein’s life we choose one whose answer appears at the end and will vindicate him against foolish charges.

Of all Desplein’s students, Horace Bianchon was among those the surgeon most warmly attached to. Before becoming a house surgeon at the Hôtel-Dieu, Bianchon had been a medical student lodging in the squalid Maison Vauquer in the Quartier Latin. He knew the gnawing of poverty, a crucible from which great talents emerge pure and able to endure shocks. In the fire of unbridled passions they acquire impeccable honesty and the habit of constant work to meet life’s battles.

Horace was upright, incapable of tergiversation on honor, direct, ready to pledge his cloak for a friend and give his time or nights. He expected nothing in return yet trusted that in time he would receive more than he gave. His friends respected him for unostentatious virtue and feared his censure. He made no pedantic display; neither puritan nor preacher, he could swear with grace while giving advice, and was always ready for jollification. A jolly companion, frank and open as an honest man with nothing to hide, he walked with head erect and a content mind. In a word, Horace was the Pylades of more than one Orestes—creditors being the modern Furies.

He bore poverty cheerfully, an attribute of courage, and because he had nothing he incurred few debts. Sober as a camel, active as a stag, he was steadfast in ideas and conduct.

His fortunate turn began when Desplein noticed both his qualities and faults, which made Bianchon dear to friends. When a leading clinician takes a young man to his bosom, the young man gets his foot in the stirrup. Desplein did not fail to take Bianchon as assistant to wealthy houses where complimentary fees reached the student’s pocket and where Parisian life’s mysteries unfolded. He kept him at consultations, gave him occupation, and sometimes sent him with rich patients to watering-places; he was effectively building a practice for him. Over time the tyrant of surgery had a devoted ally. The two men—one at the summit of honor and fortune, the other a humble Omega without fortune or fame—became intimate friends.

Desplein confided in his house surgeon. Bianchon knew who had sat near the master or on the famous couch in Desplein’s surgery, where he sometimes slept. He knew the temperament, a mix of lion and bull, that swelled beyond measure and eventually caused the man’s death by heart degeneration. He studied the eccentricities of that busy life, the schemes of sordid avarice, and the politician who lurked behind the man of science; he could foresee mortifications awaiting the solitary sentiment hidden within a steeled heart.

Once Bianchon spoke to Desplein of a poor water-carrier of Saint-Jacques who had a horrible disease from fatigue and want; the man had eaten only potatoes during the winter of 1821. Desplein left his rounds and, at the risk of killing his horse, rushed off followed by Bianchon to remove the man to a sick house founded by Dubois in the Faubourg Saint-Denis. After curing him, Desplein gave him money to buy a horse and a water-barrel. The Auvergnat later took a sick friend to Desplein, saying, "I could not have borne to let him go to any one else!" Desplein grasped the man’s hand and said, "Bring them all to me."

Bianchon had noticed his chief’s predilection for Auvergnats and water-carriers, but because Desplein prided himself on cures at the Hôtel-Dieu, the pupil found nothing strange.

One morning as he crossed the Place Saint-Sulpice, Bianchon saw his master enter the church at about nine, slipping in by the door in Rue du Petit-Lion as if into a suspect house. Curious and a devoted Cabaniste himself, he stole into the church and was astonished to see the great Desplein—the atheist who had no mercy on angels—kneeling humbly in the Lady Chapel, staying through mass, giving alms toward the service and the poor, looking as serious as a man supervising an operation.

"He has certainly not come here to clear up the question of the Virgin's delivery," Bianchon thought. "If he'd been holding a canopy rope on Corpus Christi it would be laughable; but alone at this hour—it's a marvel!"

Not wanting to seem a spy, he left. Desplein asked him that day to dine at a restaurant. At dessert Bianchon steered conversation to the mass, calling it a farce.

"A farce," Desplein said, "which has cost Christendom more blood than all Napoleon’s battles and all Broussais’ leeches. The mass is a papal invention, not older than the sixth century, based on the Hoc est corpus. What floods of blood were shed to establish the Fête-Dieu, the Festival of Corpus Christi—Rome’s triumph in the question of the Real Presence, a schism which rent the Church for three centuries! The wars of the Count of Toulouse against the Albigenses were the tail end of that dispute. The Vaudois and the Albigenses refused this innovation."

Desplein thus delighted in atheistical vein, pouring out Voltairean satire or its poor imitation.

"Hallo! where is my worshiper of this morning?" Bianchon thought.

He said nothing; he doubted whether he had really seen Desplein at Saint-Sulpice. Desplein would not have lied; they had discussed systems de natura rerum before, probing them with the knife and scalpel of incredulity.

Three months passed. Bianchon did not pursue the matter though it remained in his memory. One day a physician of the Hôtel-Dieu, in Bianchon’s presence, asked Desplein bluntly, "What were you doing at Saint-Sulpice, my dear master?"

"I went to see a priest who has a diseased knee-bone, and to whom the Duchesse d'Angoulême did me the honor to recommend me," Desplein answered.

The questioner took this for an answer; not so Bianchon. "Oh, he goes to see damaged knees in church!—He went to mass," he thought.

Bianchon resolved to watch Desplein. He remembered the day and hour he had seen him entering Saint-Sulpice and resolved to be there the following year, same day and hour, to see if the phenomenon recurred. In such a man there should be no direct antagonism of thought and action.

Next year, at that hour, Bianchon—by then no longer Desplein’s house surgeon—saw the surgeon’s cab at the corner of Rue de Tournon and Rue du Petit-Lion; Desplein crept along the wall of Saint-Sulpice and once more attended mass at the Virgin’s altar. The regularity complicated the mystery. After Desplein left, Bianchon went to the sacristan and asked whether the gentleman was a regular worshiper.

"For twenty years that I have been here," the man replied, "M. Desplein has come four times a year to attend this mass. He founded it."

"A mass founded by him!" Bianchon said as he left. "This is as great a mystery as the Immaculate Conception—an article enough to make a physician an unbeliever."

Some time passed before Doctor Bianchon, though an intimate friend, found an opportunity to speak to Desplein about this incident of his life. Though they met often in consultation or society, it was difficult to find a confidential hour. At last, seven years after the Revolution of 1830, when the mob invaded the Archbishop's residence, Bianchon once more detected Desplein going into Saint-Sulpice. The doctor followed him, knelt beside him without remark, and they attended the mass he had founded.

"Will you tell me, my dear fellow," said Bianchon as they left the church, "the reason for your fit of monkishness? I have caught you three times going to mass—You! You must account for this mystery; explain such a flagrant disagreement between your opinions and your conduct. You do not believe in God, and yet you attend mass?"

"I am like many devout people—men who on the surface are deeply religious, but quite as much atheists as you or I."

He then launched into a torrent of epigrams about certain political personages, one of which turned contemporary politicians into a new Tartufe.

"All that has nothing to do with my question," retorted Bianchon. "I want to know why you have just been doing that, and why you founded this mass."

"Faith! my dear boy," said Desplein, "I am on the verge of the tomb; I may safely tell you about the beginning of my life."

They were in the Rue des Quatre-Vents, one of the worst streets in Paris. Desplein pointed to the sixth floor of a narrow house—its door opening into a passage with a winding staircase and 'borrowed light' windows. Throwing up his arm with a vehement gesture, he exclaimed: "I lived up there for two years."

"I know; Arthez lived there; we called it the pickle-jar of great men!" said Bianchon.

"The mass I have just attended is connected with events from my time in that garret—the garret with a clothes-line and a pot of flowers. My early life was so hard I may dispute the palm of Paris suffering with any man living. I endured hunger and thirst, want of clothes, shoes, and linen—every cruelty penury can inflict. I have blown on my frozen fingers in that pickle-jar, worked through a whole winter seeing my breath steam like that of horses on a frosty day. I do not know where a man finds the fulcrum that enables him to hold out.

"I was alone, with no money for books or medical expenses; no friend. My irascible temper was against me. No one understood that this irritability was the distress and toil of a man at the bottom of the social scale struggling to reach the surface. Still, I had a ground-bed of good feeling and sensitiveness—birthrights of any man strong enough to climb after long tramping in the bogs of poverty. My family gave an inadequate allowance. I breakfasted on a cheap leftover roll crumbled into milk—two sous. I dined only every other day in a boarding-house costing sixteen sous. I took such care of my clothes and shoes; few griefs equal the mocked seam in a shoe or the split armhole of a coat. I drank only water; a café seemed a promised land where only the Lucullus of the Latin Quarter had a right of entry. 'Shall I ever take coffee with milk?' I asked myself, 'or play dominoes?'

"I threw into my work the fury I felt at my misery. I tried to master positive knowledge so as to acquire personal value and merit the position I should hold when I escaped nothingness. I consumed more oil than bread; the light I burned cost me more than food. It was a long obstinate duel with no consolation. I found no sympathy anywhere. To have friends, must we not form connections with young men, have a few sous to go tippling, meet where students congregate? And I had nothing! Nothing means nothing in Paris. When I even thought of revealing my beggary, I had that nervous contraction of the throat which makes a sick man believe a ball rises from the oesophagus into the larynx.

"Later in life I have met the rich who, never having wanted anything, never heard the rule-of-three problem: A young man is to crime as a five-franc piece is to X. These gilded idiots ask me, 'Why did you get into debt?' They remind me of the princess who, hearing the people lacked bread, said, 'Why do not they buy cakes?' I should like to see one of these rich men alone in Paris without a sou, without a friend, without credit, forced to work to live. What would he do? Where go?

"Bianchon, if you have sometimes seen me hard and bitter, it was because I added my early sufferings to the insensibility and selfishness I have seen in the highest circles; or perhaps I thought of the obstacles hatred, envy, jealousy, and calumny raised between me and success. In Paris, when people see you ready to set your foot in the stirrup, some pull your coat-tails, others loosen your strap so you may fall and crack your skull; one wrenches off your horse's shoes, another steals your whip; and the least treacherous stands to fire his pistol at you point-blank.

"You will know the odious and incessant warfare mediocrity wages against the superior man. If you drop twenty-five louis one day, you'll be accused of gambling next; your best friends will report you lost twenty-five thousand. If you have a headache, you're mad. If you're a little hasty, no one can live with you. If you make a stand against this armament of pigmies, collect your powers, your best friends will cry you want everything, you aim at domineering. Your good points become faults; your faults vices; your virtues crimes.

"If you save a man, you will be said to have killed him; if he reappears it will be said you secured the present at the cost of the future. Stumble, and you fall! Invent anything and claim your rights, and you'll be called crotchety, cunning, ill-disposed to rising younger men.

"So, if I do not believe in God, I believe still less in man. But do you not know another Desplein in me, altogether different from the Desplein everyone abuses?—however, we will not stir that mud-heap.

"Well, I lived in that house, working hard to pass my first exam, with no money. I had come to one of those moments of extremity when a man says, 'I will enlist.' One hope remained: a box of linen from home, a present from an aunt who thinks of your shirts. The box arrived while I was at the schools; it had cost forty francs for carriage. The porter, a German shoemaker living in a loft, had paid the money and kept the box. I walked up and down the Rue des Fosses-Saint-Germain-des-Pres and the Rue de l'Ecole de Medecine without finding a plan to release my trunk without paying the forty francs, which of course I could pay as soon as I sold the linen. My stupidity proved to me that surgery was my vocation. Refined souls whose powers move in a lofty atmosphere have none of that spirit of intrigue fertile in device; their good genius is chance; they don't invent—things come to them.

"At night I went home just as my fellow lodger came in—a water-carrier named Bourgeat from Saint-Flour. We knew each other as two lodgers do who share the same landing. He told me the landlord, to whom I owed three quarters' rent, had turned me out; I must clear out next morning. He himself was also turned out on account of his occupation. I spent the most miserable night of my life. Where get a messenger to carry my few chattels and books? How pay him and the porter? Where go? Poverty has for its friends heavenly slumbers full of beautiful dreams.

"Next morning, as I swallowed my little bowl of bread soaked in milk, Bourgeat came in and said in his rough Auvergne accent:

"'Mouchieur l'Etudiant, I am a poor man, a foundling from the hospital at Saint-Flour, without parents, and not rich enough to marry. You are not fertile in relations either, nor well supplied with ready money. Listen, I have a hand-cart downstairs I hire for two sous an hour; it will hold all our goods; if you like, we'll try to find lodgings together, since we're both turned out. It is not the earthly paradise, when all is said and done.'

"'I know that, my good Bourgeat,' I said. 'But I'm in a great fix. I have a trunk downstairs with a hundred francs' worth of linen in it, out of which I could pay the landlord and porter, and I have not a hundred sous.'

"'Pooh! I have a few dibs,' replied Bourgeat, pulling out a greasy old leather purse. 'Keep your linen.'

"Bourgeat paid our arrears and settled with the porter. Then he put our furniture and my box of linen in his cart and pulled it along the street, stopping before every house where there was a notice. I went up to see whether rooms to let would suit us. At midday we were still wandering without finding anything. The price was the difficulty. Bourgeat proposed we eat at a wine-shop, leaving the cart at the door. Towards evening I discovered in the Cour de Rohan, Passage du Commerce, at the very top of a house next the roof, two rooms with a staircase between them. Each of us was to pay sixty francs a year. So there we were—housed, my humble friend and I. We dined together. Bourgeat, who earned about fifty sous a day, had saved a hundred crowns or so; he would soon buy a barrel and a horse. On learning my situation—for he extracted my secrets with quiet craftiness and good nature—he gave up, for a time, the ambition of his life; for twenty-two years he had carried water, and he devoted his hundred crowns to my prospects."

Desplein at these words clutched Bianchon's arm. "He gave me the money for my examination fees! That man understood I had a mission, that the needs of my intellect were greater than his. He looked after me, called me his boy, lent me money to buy books, would come in softly to watch me at work, and took a mother's care in seeing I had wholesome and abundant food instead of the bad and insufficient nourishment I had been condemned to. Bourgeat, about forty, had a homely, medieval face, a prominent forehead, a head a painter might have chosen as Lycurgus'. The poor man's heart was big with affections seeking an object; he had never been loved but by a poodle that had died, of which he would talk to me, asking whether the Church would allow masses for its soul. His dog, he said, had been a good Christian, accompanying him to church, never barking, listening to the organ as though praying.

"This man centered all his affections in me; he looked upon me as a forlorn creature, and became to me the most thoughtful mother and considerate benefactor—the ideal of the virtue that rejoices in its own work. When I met him in the street, he would throw me a glance full of unutterable dignity; he would affect to walk as though he carried no weight and seemed happy to see me well dressed. It was the devoted affection of the lower classes, the love of a girl of the people transferred to a loftier level. Bourgeat did all my errands, woke me at a fixed hour, trimmed my lamp, cleaned our landing; as good a servant as he was a father, and as clean as an English girl. He did all the housework. Like Philopoemen he sawed our wood, giving all he did the grace of simplicity while preserving his dignity, seeming to understand that the end ennobles every act.

"When I left this good fellow to be house surgeon at the Hôtel-Dieu, I felt an indescribable dull pain, knowing he could no longer live with me; but he comforted himself with the prospect of saving enough for me to take my degree, and made me promise to go see him when I had a day out: Bourgeat was proud of me. He loved me for my own sake and for his own. If you look up my thesis, you will see I dedicated it to him.

"During my last year as house surgeon I earned enough to repay him: I bought him a barrel and a horse. He was furious at my depriving myself of spending the money, yet delighted. He laughed and scolded, looked at his barrel and horse, wiped a tear, saying, 'It is too bad. What a splendid barrel! You ought not. Why, that horse is as strong as an Auvergnat!'

"I never saw a more touching scene. Bourgeat insisted on buying for me the case of instruments mounted in silver which you have seen in my room and which is my most precious thing. Though enchanted with my first success, never did a sign escape him implying, 'This man owes all to me!' Yet but for him I should have died of want; he had eaten bread rubbed with garlic that I might have coffee to sit up at night.

"He fell ill. I passed my nights by his bedside, and the first time I pulled him through; but two years after he relapsed and, despite all care and exertions of science, he succumbed. No king was ever nursed as he was. Yes, Bianchon, to snatch that man from death I tried unheard-of things. I wanted him to live long enough to see his work accomplished, to realize his hopes, to receive the only gratitude that ever filled my heart.

"Bourgeat, my second father, died in my arms," Desplein continued, visibly moved. "He left me everything he possessed by a will he had made with a scrivener, dating from the year when we had gone to live in the Cour de Rohan.

"This man's faith was perfect; he loved the Holy Virgin as he might have loved a wife. He was an ardent Catholic, but never said a word to me about my lack of religion. When dying he entreated me to spare no expense for every benefit of clergy. I had a mass said for him every day. Often at night he told me his fears about his future; he feared his life had not been saintly enough. Poor man! he labored from morning till night. For whom, then, is Paradise—if there be a Paradise? He received the last sacrament like the saint he was, and his death was worthy of his life.

"I alone followed him to the grave. When I had laid my only benefactor to rest, I looked around to see how I could pay my debt; I found he had neither family nor friends. But he believed. He had a religious conviction; had I any right to dispute it? He had spoken to me timidly of masses for the repose of the dead; he thought it a form of repayment for his services. As soon as I had money enough I paid Saint-Sulpice the requisite sum for four masses every year. As the only thing I can do for Bourgeat is to satisfy his pious wishes, on the days when that mass is said at the beginning of each season I go for his sake and say the required prayers; and I say with the good faith of a sceptic—'Great God, if there is a sphere Thou hast appointed after death for those who have been perfect, remember good Bourgeat; and if he should have anything to suffer, let me suffer it for him, that he may enter sooner into what is called Paradise.'

"That, my dear fellow, is as much as a man who holds my opinions can allow himself. But God must be a good fellow; He cannot owe me any grudge. I swear I would give my whole fortune if faith such as Bourgeat's could enter my brain."

Bianchon, who was with Desplein through his last illness, dares not affirm to this day that the great surgeon died an atheist. Those who believe may like to fancy that the humble Auvergnat came to open the gate of Heaven to his friend, as he did the gate of the earthly temple bearing the words—"A grateful country to its great men."

PARIS, January 1836.
