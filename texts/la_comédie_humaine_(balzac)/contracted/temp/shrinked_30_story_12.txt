During the Restoration the Archbishop of Besançon regularly attended Baronne de Watteville’s drawing‑room because of her devoutness.

Monsieur de Watteville, a gentle, unassuming descendant of the infamous Watteville, had lived quietly in La Franche‑Comté. He married Mademoiselle de Rupt, an heiress; the match was arranged in 1802 and formalized in 1815 after the Second Restoration. After her grandparents died, Madame de Watteville grew more pious and influential in Besançon. Thin and plain, he worked as a turner and collected geological specimens. Many admired his noble soul: he accepted that he would not dominate his wife and devoted himself to work and hobbies, often acting as his own butler. The Rupt residence showed both families’ standing—grand rooms, elegant furnishings and fine cuisine—Madame’s fortune being considerable while his estate was modest. Their intimacy with the Archbishop drew several notable abbés.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834 Abbé de Grancey announced a favorable lawsuit verdict. The Vicar‑General, calling it unexpected in the political climate, credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron, whose obscure origins intrigued the guests. Amedée de Soulas, present that evening, would later prove significant.

France and England long exchanged cultural influences, especially in language and fashion: what is “English” in Paris often becomes “French” in London. Examples ranged from the English anthem “God Save the King” being linked to Lulli to hoop skirts that, after ridicule, swept Europe; fashions mocked in 1815 later returned. More recently England supplied French with terms like “dandy” and “lion.” The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset, sparked a vogue for stylish young men that reached the provinces.

In 1834 Besançon had its own “lion” in Amedée‑Sylvain de Soulas, of Spanish descent and ill at ease in the dull military town. He kept a spare apartment, lived modestly yet cultivated an ostentatious appearance, and kept a young servant, Babylas, as his “tiger,” dressing him smartly despite scarce means. Seen as a spendthrift, Amedée was clever at economizing—often dining out to avoid household costs. His grooming and travel stories were a façade to impress the local aristocracy and win Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a modest but resolute eighteen‑year‑old whose mother fiercely guarded her honor. His life thus illustrates social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraint as he strives to win Rosalie’s heart.

“Madame,” Monsieur de Soulas began as he waited for his soup to cool, “a gentleman from Paris arrived one morning at the Hotel National and took rooms on the first floor of Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron. He registered at the Mairie as Albert Savaron and left his card with the local officials.”

“The Savaron name is well known,” Mademoiselle de Watteville observed.

“He’s a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amédée de Soulas replied. “If he claims the Savaron arms he must add a bar-sinister.” Rosalie noted that a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble. The Baroness stopped the genealogy debate, and the Vicar-General was urged to continue.

“In a town like Besançon everything is catalogued,” Monsieur de Soulas said. “The local lawyers quickly accepted Albert. They wondered who had sent him—he left cards with judges instead of calling. He hired Jérôme, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who can cook; otherwise no one saw him much.”

“Does he go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He gets up between one and two, works until eight, breakfasts, works again, strolls in his garden, dines, and goes to bed about six or seven,” Soulas replied. He explained his knowledge came from overlooking Savaron’s house; his servant spoke with Jérôme. Babylas and riding supplied the rest of the gossip.

“How did you come to hire a stranger for your defense?” the Baroness asked the Vicar-General.

“The President assigned him to defend a simple peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man innocent, exposed the real culprits, and won their arrest. His defense astonished the Court. A merchant later gave him a difficult case, which he won. Monsieur Berryer advised we employ Savaron for our matter. I felt confident upon meeting him—and was right.”

“Is he extraordinary?” asked Madame de Chavoncourt.

“Certainly,” the Vicar-General affirmed, and the company urged more detail. The Abbé de Grancey described Savaron’s rooms and appearance: an outer room lined with law books and simply furnished, and a younger man than expected, dressed in a black dressing gown with red trimmings. Madame de Watteville exclaimed at the devil’s colors, but the Abbé praised Savaron’s features—black hair streaked with gray, a broad forehead furrowed by deep lines of thought, an olive complexion, a square nose, fiery, passionate eyes, a voice both sweet and capable of thunder, average height, and hands like a prelate’s. He had a terrible yet gentle face and seemed to bear a heavy burden.

The Abbé recounted subsequent visits—one in a shabby bedroom, another at a frugal breakfast in his library—confirming the impression of a complex man with something significant behind his aspect. Having studied his head and heard him argue against a prominent Parisian lawyer, the Abbé believed Savaron capable of great things, and his portrait enthralled Rosalie.

For Rosalie the Abbé’s words were revelation. She had met suitors like the ordinary Count; now she heard of someone exceptional. She dwelt on the description—“what a fine forehead!”—and realized the window she’d glimpsed from her bed belonged to him. “I must see him!” she resolved. That night she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse his windows; she felt a thrill watching candlelight and imagined his furniture. “While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought.

The question of how much to educate girls framed Rosalie’s state: too much enlightenment forces premature maturity; too little yields stunted minds. Her sudden inward stir from the Abbé’s portrait made the ordinary extraordinary and launched a passionate desire to see and know Savaron.

The next morning she saw Albert walking in the garden beside the Hôtel de Rupt. Having seen him, Rosalie resolved to enter his house, uncover his mystery, hear his voice, and glimpse his eyes. She embroidered with the concentration of someone outwardly idle but deeply reflective, and arranged to go to confession at half-past seven, hoping to encounter him.

She visited her father in his workshop and suggested he build a grotto belvedere in the garden—ostensibly to display his work, really to give her a vantage point into Savaron’s rooms. Her father delighted in the idea; she warned him not to tell her mother. Young girls will contrive much for the sake of a man, even if he remains unaware.

Sunday arrived. Rosalie dressed carefully; her heart quickened as she hurried across the cross-court. She reached church before Mass, prayed, and watched to see if Abbé Giroud would be in his confessional, positioning herself to see Albert. In her heightened state no man seemed unattractive; Albert Savaron, striking and solemn, made a profound impression. His burdened look, eagle-like eyes, and deep gaze suggested intense thought and hidden ardor. Rosalie boldly stood in his path to meet his glance, and a rush of warmth overwhelmed her.

After Albert sat, she found a place to observe him during confession. Time in the dark felt fleeting; by the time she emerged, Mass had ended and he had gone. “The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besançon? I must know everything! But how?” She returned to embroidery, masking her excitement with an innocent demeanor to deceive her mother. From that Sunday, after their glance, she eagerly supported the belvedere plan.

“Mamma,” she said, “my father has a unique idea for a belvedere in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of everything your father does,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly. Rosalie suggested a cool room in the grotto for coffee; her mother approved and inspected the columns. They chose a site in the garden not visible from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows but commanding a view into Savaron’s rooms. A builder was commissioned, the path to be lined with periwinkles and vines, and the interior to be rustic with a mirror and a modest table. Monsieur de Soulas suggested an asphalt floor; Rosalie proposed a rustic chandelier. Rumors spread in Besançon about the Wattevilles’ new garden feature.

“They’re wealthy and can afford such whims,” people said; “A thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed. Young Monsieur de Soulas confirmed the cost and noted a man from Paris would rusticate the interior. No one suspected the true motive: the girl’s design to peer into Albert Savaron’s private study.

Meanwhile, Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter soon faded from memory amid lawyers’ envy. He remained secluded, avoiding social gatherings and thus appearing a quiet stranger, but he continued to prove his legal skill, arguing three complex cases at the Commercial Tribunal and gaining four prominent merchants as clients who recognized his talent.

On the day the Watteville family inaugurated the belvedere, Savaron was launching another enterprise: a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review. Quietly financed by his first ten clients—forty shares of five hundred francs each—this project aimed to promote Besançon as a commercial and intellectual center to rival Strasbourg and Dijon. Savaron’s vision was to challenge regional literary dominance and bring enlightenment to Eastern France, a pitch that appealed to merchants eager to promote trade.

Savaron prudently kept his name out of the spotlight. He let his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, handle finances while he retained editorial control and a share of the profits. To manage the literary side without offending local pride, he enlisted the eager Alfred Boucher to run contributions, subtly guiding him while allowing Alfred to believe he was gaining experience. The Review, priced low, quickly gained five hundred subscribers. Two hundred more would begin to generate dividends for shareholders; the editor, Savaron, remained unpaid but in control.

After the third issue, the Review gained recognition among French publications. The third number contained a tale attributed to Savaron, which stirred comment in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room; conservative circles in Besançon dismissed it for liberal views. Rosalie, hearing about the Review, persuaded her father to subscribe.

“Papa,” she said, “there’s a Review published in Besançon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room, as mamma wouldn’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.”

Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please the affectionate daughter who had shown him such regard, subscribed and lent her the four issues already published. That night Rosalie devoured the tale—the first story she had ever read. With her limited experience, the impact was profound. She hoped the narrative contained the sentiments and possibly the life of Albert. From the first pages she became convinced it reflected him; others noted that Albert often drew from his own life in such confessions.

In 1823 two young men left Lucerne to tour the lake of the Four Cantons. Passing Gersau, Rodolphe was captivated by a newly built house and a woman at the window and stayed while his friend Leopold continued the trip. The two had studied law together; Leopold was heading to a notary’s post in Paris, while Rodolphe—the illegitimate son of a nobleman—had a harder past, his mother having sacrificed everything to secure his education. Passionate and impulsive, he contrasted with Leopold’s steadier, pragmatic temperament.

Rodolphe lodged with a local couple and learned the woman was Fanny Lovelace, who had brought her elderly father for his health. Of modest means, Fanny supported them by translating and writing; Rodolphe was struck by her beauty, voice, musical talent and quiet self-reliance. The next day he obtained permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens—the family who rented to the Lovelaces—and, after initial reluctance, was allowed in. There he noticed Gina, a deaf-mute girl whose gestures suggested an unusual backstory and protective loyalties among the household; the Bergmanns were wary of outsiders.

That evening he heard Fanny singing and was enchanted. He later met Fanny and Gina, but their conversation was cut short when an unknown assailant attacked and wounded Rodolphe; Fanny and Gina aided him and got him to safety, deepening their bond. While convalescing, Rodolphe learned his mother had died, plunging him into depression. Seeking comfort in Fanny, he discovered shared literary and musical interests and confessed his feelings—only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite the marriage, Fanny’s affection for Rodolphe grew; she revealed she had married under duress and that Emilio was not as old as he had seemed, and she encouraged Rodolphe’s ambitions.

Their relationship deepened but was strained by Rodolphe’s jealousy, especially when Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past, reappeared. Though they dreamed of freedom together, Rodolphe’s restlessness, Fanny’s ties to her husband and social constraint forced them to weigh love against duty and sacrifice, the surrounding landscape echoing their intense emotions.

For twenty days Rodolphe stayed with the Bergmanns, watching Francesca with fascinated puzzlement. He noted youthful imprudence alongside a stubborn reserve. The old man treated her like a father; her gratitude suggested an innate nobility that deepened the mystery she presented.

Those days brought secret joys and melancholy, quarrels and harmony. Rodolphe became captivated by Francesca’s constant tenderness and grew jealous over trifles. One evening, when she said she wished to leave Gersau for its lack of luxuries, he asked if she prized luxury. She answered passionately, equating a love of luxury with an appreciation for art and beauty, while insisting she had borne hardship without complaint. Her playful claim that ordinary people aspired to greatness displayed her charm.

Francesca then forbade Rodolphe to accompany them to Geneva, citing town gossip and the need to protect her husband’s reputation. She professed love for Rodolphe but demanded secrecy, kissed him, and left him astonished.

The next day, after the Bergmanns’ lodgers departed, Rodolphe went to Vevay and Geneva. To avoid discomfort he rented a room in Eaux-Vives and sought Italian refugees, hoping to find a bookseller named Lamporani, but learned only that the Colonna and Gandolphini couples had arrived. On his first walk to Villa Diodati he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage with an old lady and felt dizzy. Discovering she was with the Colonnas shattered the intimacy he had imagined: her noble rank suggested he might have been a mere whim.

He left his card with Prince Gandolphini and met a false Lamporani who invited him to a concert; though curious, Rodolphe did not impose. That night his passion intensified; he felt driven to raise his social standing. At the concert Francesca was radiant; their eyes met, and her beauty convinced him she had remained true despite her rank.

Afterward he was introduced to the Colonnas and Francesca, who teasingly called herself “Princess Rodolphini,” reviving Gersau memories. She acknowledged his deep, faithful affection; he confessed an ambition to achieve greatness so she could be proud.

Francesca’s life was full of contradiction: born for the cloister, family tragedy forced her to marry Prince Gandolphini to preserve status, yet she preferred independence, liberal ideas, and sympathized with revolutionary sentiments. Winter in Geneva passed quickly in her intelligent, beautiful company, but with spring Rodolphe grew restless; her calm clashed with his need for more demonstrative feeling. On a terrace he bitterly lamented others’ happiness, particularly a noblewoman’s. Hurt, Francesca withdrew; when he found her in tears and kissed her, she rebuffed him, asserted her dignity, and ordered him to leave—declaring they would not meet again until Naples. He, recognizing the strength of her will, complied.

In Paris Rodolphe found a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner. Although he thought himself unmoved, Francesca persuaded him to commission originals for himself and Emilio and announced their correspondence.

Driven by love and ambition, Rodolphe poured himself into a deceitful venture that consumed three years. When Villele’s ministry fell he was ruined; after three months at Villa Gandolphini in Naples, where they recovered, he turned to politics. His talents attracted notice and a high post was promised, but the July 1830 upheaval thwarted him. Only Francesca and God witnessed his brave efforts—a noble heart unfavored by fortune. Sustained by love, he faced trials. Lovers, pray for him.

When Mademoiselle de Watteville finished, Rosalie flushed and wept. The story—a rare instance of a man narrating his own love—was her first encounter with that voice and convinced her Francesca Colonna was Albert’s beloved.

Curiosity hardened into resolve. Doubting her looks and lacking musical talent, Rosalie nevertheless determined either to win Albert or uncover the truth. Her sheltered imagination produced schemes: sneaking into his garden, writing to him, visiting her father’s land to force a meeting.

From Albert’s window she saw Jerome, his servant, helped over the wall by Mariette, the maid. Mariette’s plainness and relative freedom explained Jerome’s interest, and the scene taught Rosalie a dangerous lesson—servants could bypass her mother’s restrictions.

The next morning Rosalie accompanied Mariette to Mass and seized the chance to question her about Jerome. Confronting Mariette about secret meetings, Rosalie offered help in exchange for access to letters Albert gave Jerome. Relieved by Rosalie’s discretion, Mariette agreed.

Rosalie’s obsession deepened when she read a letter Albert had written to his friend Leopold. It revealed his noble lineage, past struggles, ambition, and steadfast devotion to Francesca. Though she felt the sting of her inadequacies and the wrong of invading his privacy, she kept the letters. One evening Mariette brought a letter meant for Francesca; torn, Rosalie read it and, struck by their fidelity, resolved to step back—yearning for the selfless love she had witnessed.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil; by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. That private struggle, kept to herself, fueled her passion and dark plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist; her story is presented as a cautionary tale.

That winter Albert de Savarus advanced his standing in Besançon while awaiting the Chamber’s dissolution. Backed by a wealthy contractor allied with moderates, he revived the old Arcier aqueduct question. Restoring Arcier—when the Doubs could supply water—seemed absurd, yet Savarus declared the river water unfit and promoted “Roman” water; the local Review echoed him and townspeople across parties accepted the idea. The controversy, driven by hidden interests, dismissed opponents as old-fashioned while Savarus’s projects dominated debate. After eighteen months he had stirred the previously stagnant town, gaining influence without broad popularity. He also won lawsuits for local priests; ambition consumed him. His apparent disinterest in personal gain masked another kind of moral usury: he expected a larger reward. In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with Léopold Hannequin’s funds, a move that also qualified him for election. The Abbé de Grancey, who discerned Savarus’s designs, called him remarkable and suggested he had taken a wrong path.

Meanwhile Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hôtel de Rupt, at first content merely to observe him. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, yielded ten thousand francs a year but was poorly managed by the inattentive Baron and an old steward, Modinier. The family often retreated there; the late Watteville had improved the picturesque spot by building a dam and lake. His heirs nonetheless pressed a dubious claim over the village of Riceys, whose inhabitants had long-held rights to the lands; the Baron’s death left a legacy of usurpation his descendants continued to assert.

Rosalie’s maneuvers were subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey, enchanted by the lake and associating it with love. On a boat trip her father confided legal troubles over Riceys; Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus as lawyer, stressing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for their estate. She argued a lawsuit would add excitement and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey, clearly aiming to fix her future there and create gardens.

Four days later the Abbé de Grancey visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing alignment would compromise his neutrality; the Abbé pressed political advantages, especially potential votes. Rosalie, determined to keep Savarus in Besançon and prevent his election, wrote to the préfet revealing Savarus’s ambitions, but was ignored. She understood the political landscape and sought to manipulate it.

As the election approached, influential men unexpectedly nominated Savarus—he had been unaware. The Abbé warned him of potential betrayals and advised transparency about his past. Savarus’s passionate speech captivated his audience, yet the préfet worked to undermine him, eroding support. The first vote disappointed Savarus; he returned home in despair, reflecting on his love for Francesca and an uncertain future. Then the Abbé brought hope: he had secured behind-the-scenes support, promised the Rouxey case would be resolved in time, and suggested marriage to Sidonie de Chavoncourt might consolidate votes. Savarus, initially resistant, recognized the advantage and prepared to navigate the coming choices of love and politics.

By nine the next evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were full of Besançon’s aristocracy to discuss the surprise nomination of the Rupts’ daughter, whose father had been Master of Appeals and secretary to a loyal Elder Branch minister. Madame de Chavoncourt attended with her younger daughter Sidonie; her elder sister stayed away. The Abbé de Grancey circulated, steering conversation and probing an elderly statesman about whom the Elder Branch might field; guests feared Berryer, noted the Duc de Fitz-James’s Toulouse nomination, and worried that backing Monsieur Savarus could drive Republicans toward them instead of the Moderates.

By half past nine Albert had not arrived; the Baroness took his lateness as an affront, while Madame de Chavoncourt counseled patience. Rosalie, watching hostess and mother, noted the leniency shown Albert and hinted at a possible match between him and Sidonie. By ten impatience grew and some gentlemen began to play cards. The Abbé, uneasy, went to the window where Rosalie had withdrawn and suddenly cried, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville, and Rosalie went to Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent; the servant Jérôme said Albert had left in a post-chaise. The Abbé cried ambiguously, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!”—and Rosalie’s face betrayed a joy the Vicar-General pretended not to notice.

Back in the drawing-room Monsieur de Watteville announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By half-past eleven about fifteen guests remained, among them Madame de Chavoncourt and her daughters, the Abbés de Godenars and de Grancey, Monsieur de Vauchelles, Rosalie, Amédée de Soulas, and a retired magistrate who had supported Albert. Grancey stationed himself near the Baroness to watch Rosalie, whose pallor had given way to agitation.

When Madame de Chavoncourt asked what had occurred, a servant brought a letter on a silver tray for the Abbé. He read it; Rosalie went pale and he recognized the handwriting. After a meaningful glance he pocketed the letter and declared, “She is in love with Albert Savarus!” As he moved to leave, Rosalie stopped him: the letter, she confessed passionately, was from Albert, and she begged him to remain silent about Albert’s withdrawal—knowing such an announcement would destroy any hope of her sister’s marriage to him.

On her way to Mass Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette that Albert had vanished precipitously. An elderly Parisian had visited Monsieur Savaron; Savaron turned pale at the card, met him for over an hour, left with a large package, then hurried to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage with post horses, quit his post, and obtained a passport. His abrupt departure inflamed local prejudice against outsiders. Most soon forgot Albert—except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness pressed Rosalie to marry; the Baron promised the family estate, les Rouxey, though his wife fretted over finances. A match with Monsieur de Soulas was arranged; Rosalie refused. Tensions rose during a fraught summer at les Rouxey. After Mariette and Jérôme married, the Baron invested in the estate—fuel for the Baroness’s resentment. The Vicar-General tried to mediate.

Alone with him, Rosalie confessed she had sabotaged Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo, by intercepting their letters, causing their separation. Horrified, the Vicar-General urged her to seek forgiveness. Her guilt deepened when a Parisian clerk acting for Albert came to sell his belongings and revealed Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received Albert’s letter: he forgave Rosalie and wished to remain in solitude.

The Vicar-General urged Rosalie to reconcile with her mother and accept de Soulas; she refused. After his death, Madame de Watteville married de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support. Now of age and financially independent, Rosalie went to Paris to confront the Duchess, publicly admitting at a ball her part in Albert’s downfall. She returned to Besançon having exposed the truth, but at heavy emotional cost—leaving a tale of love, betrayal, and the lasting consequences of ambition.

Mademoiselle de Watteville, known in the Eastern provinces, lived alone at Les Rouxey—riding, hunting, and improving her estate. Madame de Soulas, mother of two who looked younger than her years, was married to an elderly Monsieur de Soulas, who told Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, one must, unfortunately, marry her!” Eccentric, she visited the Grande Chartreuse annually, perhaps remembering a grand‑uncle who once forced his way through monastery walls to gain freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon ostensibly to marry but returned after a Loire steamboat boiler explosion cost her right arm and left leg and scarred her face. In failing health and pain, she devoted herself to religion at the Chartreuse of Les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
