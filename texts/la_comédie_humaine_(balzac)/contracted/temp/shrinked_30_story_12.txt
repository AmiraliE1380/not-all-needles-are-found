During the Restoration the Archbishop of Besançon regularly attended Baronne de Watteville’s drawing-room because of her devoutness.

Monsieur de Watteville, a gentle, unassuming descendant of the infamous Watteville, lived quietly in La Franche‑Comté. He married Mademoiselle de Rupt, an heiress, in a match arranged in 1802 and formalized in 1815 after the Second Restoration. After her grandparents’ deaths Madame de Watteville grew more pious and influential in Besançon’s circles. Thin and plain, he worked as a turner and collected geological specimens. Admirers praised his noble soul: he accepted that he would not dominate his wife and devoted himself to work and hobbies. The Rupt residence reflected both families’ standing—grand rooms, elegant furnishings, fine cuisine—and he often acted as his own butler. Madame’s fortune was considerable; his estate modest. Their intimacy with the Archbishop attracted several notable abbés.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834 Abbé de Grancey announced a favorable lawsuit verdict. The Vicar‑General, calling it unexpected in the political climate, credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron, whose obscure origins intrigued the guests. Amedée de Soulas, present that evening, would later prove significant.

France and England long traded cultural influences—language and fashion crossing the Channel so what is “English” in Paris can become “French” in London. Examples include the English anthem “God Save the King,” sometimes linked to Lulli, and hoop skirts that, after ridicule, spread across Europe. By 1815 English long waists were mocked in Paris, only for fashions to shift again.

More recently England supplied terms like “dandy” and “lion.” The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset, sparked a vogue for stylish young men that reached the provinces. In 1834 Besançon had its own “lion” in Amedée‑Sylvain de Soulas, of Spanish descent and ill at ease in the dull military town. He kept a spare apartment, lived modestly yet cultivated an ostentatious appearance and kept a young servant, Babylas, as his “tiger,” dressing him fashionably while subsisting on scant income.

Seen as a spendthrift, Amedée was clever at economizing—often dining out to avoid household costs. His grooming and clothes were a façade to impress the local aristocracy and win Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a wealthy heiress. Besançon’s conservative “Colony” kept strict hierarchies; Rosalie, strictly raised and modest at eighteen, was resolute, her mother fiercely guarding her honor. Amedée’s life thus illustrates social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraint as he strives to win Rosalie’s heart.

“Madame,” Monsieur de Soulas began as he waited for his soup to cool, “one morning a gentleman from Paris arrived at the Hotel National and took rooms on the first floor of Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron. He registered at the Mairie as ‘Albert Savaron’ and left his card with the local officials.”

“The Savaron name is well known,” Mademoiselle de Watteville observed.

“He’s a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amedee de Soulas replied. “If he claims the Savaron arms he must add a bar-sinister. The only surviving Brabant is Mademoiselle de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister denotes a bastard,” Rosalie chimed in, “but a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble.”

“Enough,” the Baroness cut in.

“You insisted she learn heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville said, and the Baroness urged Monsieur de Soulas to continue.

“In a town like Besançon, where everything is catalogued, Albert Savaron was quickly accepted by the local lawyers. They wondered who had sent him and what he intended—he sent his card to judges rather than calling. Three days later he was forgotten. He hired Jérôme, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who could cook a little. No one saw him much.”

“Does he go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He rises between one and two in the morning, works until eight, breakfasts, works again, walks a little in his garden, dines, and goes to bed between six and seven,” Monsieur de Soulas said.

“How do you know all this?” she asked.

“I live on Rue Neuve and overlook his house; my servant speaks with Jérôme.” Babylas and riding provided the rest of the gossip.

“How did you come to hire a stranger for your defense?” the Baroness asked the Vicar-General.

“The President assigned him to defend a half-witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man’s innocence, exposed the real culprits, and won their arrest. His defense astonished the Court. A merchant later gave him a difficult case, which he won. Monsieur Berryer advised we employ Savaron for our matter. Upon meeting him, I felt confident—and was right.”

“Is he extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” the Vicar-General replied.

“Tell us more,” urged Madame de Watteville.

“The first time I met him,” Abbe de Grancey recounted, “he received me in an outer room lined with law books, simply furnished: an old writing table, six armchairs, gray curtains, a green carpet. He was younger than I expected. He wore a black dressing gown with a red cord, red slippers, a red waistcoat, and a red smoking cap.”

“The devil’s colors!” exclaimed Madame de Watteville.

“Yes,” the Abbe continued, “but he has a magnificent head. Black hair streaked with gray, thick and curly; a round white throat; a splendid forehead furrowed by deep lines of thought; an olive complexion; a square nose; fiery eyes. He has passion but calm, and a sweet voice that can thunder when needed. Monsieur Albert Savaron is of average height, neither stout nor thin, with hands like a prelate.”

“The second time I visited, he received me in his shabby bedroom. The third time, I found him having a frugal breakfast in his library. He is no ordinary man; there’s much behind that terrible yet gentle face. He seems to carry a heavy burden.”

“Why leave Paris for Besançon?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. The Abbe, having studied his head and heard him argue against a prominent Parisian lawyer, thought Savaron would make a significant impact.

The Vicar-General’s portrait enthralled Rosalie. For the first time she encountered someone exceptional, unlike the mundane Count who had courted her. She replayed the Abbe’s words and fixated on his description—“What a fine forehead!”—comparing him to the men at the table. When her mother asked why she wasn’t eating, Rosalie answered she had no appetite, lost in thought about the Abbe’s prelate-like hands and the window she had glimpsed from her bed, now realizing it belonged to him. “I must see him!” she decided.

At dinner she asked whether the Chapter’s lawsuit was settled; the Abbe answered. Her mother laughed; Rosalie admitted the Abbe’s talk had engrossed her. That night she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse his windows. She felt a thrill watching candlelight through the trees and saw him writing, imagining the color of his furniture. “While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought.

The education of girls poses delicate questions: how much should they know? Too much enlightenment forces premature maturity; too little produces stunted minds. Rosalie’s crisis was triggered by the Abbe’s portrait of Savaron—a sudden inward stirring that made the ordinary extraordinary.

The next morning she saw Albert walking in the garden next to the Hotel de Rupt. Having seen him, Rosalie resolved to enter his house, uncover his mystery, hear his voice, and glimpse his eyes. Throughout the day she embroidered with the concentration of someone who appears idle but is deeply reflective. She decided to go to confession—arranging to confess at half-past seven, just before the eight o’clock service—hoping to encounter Albert.

She visited her father in his workshop and suggested he turn larger pieces and build a grotto in the garden to showcase his work. He delighted in the idea but she cautioned him not to tell her mother, fearing a scolding. Rosalie imagined a charming observatory from which she could see into Albert’s private room. Young girls often go to great lengths for the sake of a man, even if he remains unaware.

Sunday arrived. Rosalie dressed with such care that her maid Mariette smiled; the cross-court walk quickened her heart. She arrived at church before Mass, prayed briefly, and checked if Abbe Giroud was in his confessional, positioning herself to see Albert as he entered. No man could seem unattractive to Rosalie in her curious state. Albert Savaron, strikingly handsome, made a profound impression: solemn, burdened, his deep gaze conveying intense thought—those eagle-like eyes revealed a hidden ardor. In a bold moment she stood in his path to exchange glances, igniting a rush of warmth within her.

After Albert took a seat, Rosalie found a place to observe him during her confession. Time in the confessional seemed fleeting; by the time she emerged, Mass had ended and Albert had gone. “The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besançon? I must know everything! But how?”

With this new desire, Rosalie concentrated on her embroidery, masking her thoughts with an innocent demeanor to deceive her mother. From that Sunday, after their glance, she eagerly supported the plan for the belvedere.

“Mamma,” she said one day, “my father has a unique idea for a belvedere in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of everything your father does,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly. Rosalie mentioned a cool room in the grotto where they could take coffee; her mother approved and inspected the columns.

She approved a site in the garden not visible from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows but commanding a view into Albert Savaron’s rooms. A builder was commissioned to construct a grotto with a path lined with periwinkles, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper. The interior would be rustic, with a mirror, an ottoman, and a table of inlaid bark. Monsieur de Soulas suggested an asphalt floor; Rosalie proposed a rustic chandelier. Rumors spread in Besançon about the Wattevilles’ new garden feature.

“They’re wealthy and can afford such whims—” people said.

“A thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed.

“Yes, a thousand crowns,” young Monsieur de Soulas replied. “A man from Paris is coming to rusticate the interior, and it will be very pretty. Monsieur de Watteville is carving the chandelier himself.”

“Berquet is making a cellar beneath it,” an Abbe added; the young man corrected him—“No, he’s raising the kiosk on a concrete foundation to prevent dampness.” No one suspected that the girl thought a witless goose had devised the plan to get a closer look at Albert Savaron’s private study.

Albert Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon forgotten amid envy from other lawyers. He remained secluded, avoiding social gatherings and thereby increasing the likelihood of being overlooked as a stranger. Nevertheless, he successfully argued three complex cases at the Commercial Tribunal, gaining four prominent merchants as clients who recognized his legal skill.

On the day the Watteville family inaugurated the belvedere, Savaron was launching another enterprise. Through quiet connections among local merchants he was starting a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review, funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each from his first ten clients, who believed in promoting Besançon as a trade hub. To compete with Strasbourg, Besançon needed to become a center of enlightenment and commerce. Albert’s vision was to challenge the literary dominance of Strasbourg and Dijon and bring light to Eastern France. His ideas appealed to merchants eager to promote their own enterprise.

Savaron wisely kept his name out of the spotlight, letting his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, handle finances while he retained editorial control and a share of the profits. The Review appealed across regions, and with a low subscription price it quickly garnered five hundred subscribers. To avoid offending local pride, Albert invited the Boucher family’s eager son, Alfred, to manage the literary side. Albert maintained control, subtly guiding Alfred’s contributions while letting him believe he was gaining. The commercial community, pleased with the Review’s success, only had to pay a fraction of their shares. With two hundred more subscribers, the publication would yield dividends for shareholders, while the editor remained unpaid.

After the third issue, the Review gained recognition among French publications, which Albert began reading at home. The third number featured a tale attributed to him, stirring comment in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room, though higher circles of Besançon dismissed it for liberal views. Rosalie, hearing about the Review, persuaded her father to subscribe.

“Papa,” she said, “there’s a Review published in Besançon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room, as mamma wouldn’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.”

Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please Rosalie who had shown him such affection, subscribed and lent her the four issues already published. That night Rosalie devoured the tale—the first story she had ever read. Given her limited experience, the impact on her was profound. She hoped to find in it the sentiments and perhaps the life of Albert. From the first pages she became convinced it reflected him; others noted that Albert drew from his own life in this confession.

In 1823 two young men left Lucerne to tour the lake of the Four Cantons. Passing Gersau, Rodolphe was arrested by a newly built house and a woman at the window and stayed behind while his friend Leopold continued the tour.

They had studied law together: Leopold was headed for a notary’s post in Paris; Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a harder past—his mother had sacrificed everything for his education. Passionate and impulsive, Rodolphe contrasted with Leopold’s calm temperament.

Lodging in Gersau with a couple, Rodolphe learned the woman was Fanny Lovelace, who had come with her elderly father for his health. Of modest means, she supported him by translating and writing; Rodolphe was taken by her beauty and musical gift. Visiting the Bergmanns’ gardens—the family renting to the Lovelaces—he noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, who hinted at an unusual backstory. The Bergmanns guarded the household, reluctant to let him intrude.

That evening he heard Fanny sing and was enchanted. He later met Fanny and Gina, but an unknown assailant attacked and wounded him; Fanny and Gina cared for him and brought him to safety. While he recovered, Rodolphe received news of his mother’s death, which plunged him into grief; Fanny’s company became his solace. They bonded over shared interests, and he confessed his feelings, only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite this, their affection deepened and they shared intimate moments; Fanny later revealed she had married under duress and that Emilio was not as old as he’d seemed. She encouraged Rodolphe’s ambitions.

Their relationship intensified, but Rodolphe’s jealousy—especially when Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past, appeared—along with social ties and obligations, forced them to weigh love against duty and sacrifice, the landscape echoing their turbulent emotions.

For twenty days Rodolphe stayed with the Bergmanns, watching Francesca with a mix of fascination and puzzlement. He perceived in her both youthful imprudence and a resolute, sometimes resistant character. The old man treated her like a father; her gratitude revealed a native nobility that deepened the enigma she presented.

Those days mixed secret joys and melancholy, quarrels with harmony. Rodolphe grew captivated by Francesca’s constant tenderness and jealous of trifles. One evening, when she said she wished to leave Gersau for its lack of luxuries, he asked if she valued luxury. She answered passionately, equating a love of luxury with an appreciation for art and beauty, while insisting she had borne hardship without complaint. Her playful claim that ordinary people aspired to greatness revealed her charm.

Francesca then insisted Rodolphe not accompany them to Geneva, citing town gossip and the need to protect her husband’s reputation. She professed love for him but demanded secrecy, kissed him, and left him astonished.

The next day, after the Bergmanns’ lodgers departed, Rodolphe went to Vevay and then Geneva. To avoid local discomfort he rented a room in Eaux-Vives and inquired after Italian refugees—hoping for a bookseller named Lamporani—but learned the only recent arrivals were Prince and Princess Colonna and Prince and Princess Gandolphini. His first walk took him to Villa Diodati; there he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage with an old lady and felt dizzy. Learning she was at the Colonnas’ residence lifted a veil: the intimacy he’d felt now collided with the reality of her noble rank, and he feared he had been a mere whim.

He left his card with Prince Gandolphini and encountered a false Lamporani, who invited him to a concert that evening. Though curious, Rodolphe refrained from imposing. That night his passion intensified and he felt driven to raise his social standing. At the concert Francesca was radiant; their eyes met in a charged recognition, and her beauty convinced him she had remained true despite her rank.

Afterward he was introduced to the Colonnas and Francesca, who teasingly called herself “Princess Rodolphini,” reviving memories of Gersau. She acknowledged his faithful affection; he confessed ambition—he wanted to achieve greatness so she could be proud.

Francesca’s life was full of contradiction: born for the cloister, family tragedies forced a marriage to Prince Gandolphini to preserve status, yet she preferred independence, liberal ideas, and sympathized with revolutionary sentiments. Winter in Geneva flew by in her intelligent, beautiful company, but with spring Rodolphe grew restless; her calm contrasted with his need for more demonstrative feeling.

One day on a terrace he bitterly lamented others’ happiness, especially that of a noblewoman. Francesca, wounded, withdrew; he found her in tears and, in a moment of passion, kissed her. She rebuffed him, asserted her dignity, and ordered him to leave—declaring they would not meet again until Naples. He, recognizing the strength of her will, complied.

In Paris Rodolphe discovered a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner. Although he professed to be unmoved, Francesca persuaded Schinner to paint originals for Rodolphe and Emilio, and their correspondence began.

Driven by love and ambition, Rodolphe poured everything into a fraudulent affair that consumed three years. When Villele’s ministry fell he was ruined; after three months of recovery at Villa Gandolphini in Naples he turned to politics. His talents were acknowledged and a high post promised, but the July 1830 upheaval dashed those hopes. Only Francesca and God witnessed his brave efforts—a noble heart ill-favored by fortune. Sustained by love, he endured trials. Lovers! Pray for him!

When Mademoiselle de Watteville finished her tale, Rosalie flushed and wept. The novel—notable for a man narrating his own love—was her first encounter with that voice and convinced her Francesca Colonna was Albert’s beloved.

Curiosity hardened into resolve. Despite insecurity about her looks and lack of musical talent, Rosalie determined either to win Albert or learn the truth. Her sheltered imagination produced schemes: sneaking into his garden, writing, visiting her father’s land to force a meeting.

From Albert’s window she saw Jerome, his servant, helped over the wall by Mariette. Mariette’s plainness and relative freedom explained Jerome’s interest and taught Rosalie a dangerous lesson—servants could evade her mother’s restrictions. The next morning Rosalie accompanied Mariette to Mass and pressed her about Jerome. Offering help in exchange for access to letters Albert gave Jerome, Rosalie won Mariette’s trust.

Reading a letter Albert wrote to his friend Leopold, Rosalie learned his noble lineage, past struggles, ambition, and steadfast devotion to Francesca. Absorbed by his character, she read more and felt her own inadequacies and the wrong of invading privacy, yet clung to the letters as proof of love, punishing herself with penances. One evening Mariette brought a letter meant for Francesca. Torn, Rosalie read it; it confirmed Albert’s ongoing love and hopes. Moved by their fidelity and her unrequited longing, she resolved to step back—recognizing their bond’s nobility and yearning for the selfless love she had witnessed.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil; by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. Alone, that struggle fueled her passion and dark plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist; her story is presented as a cautionary tale.

That winter Albert de Savarus advanced his position in Besançon, eager for the Chamber’s dissolution. Backed by a wealthy contractor tied to moderates, he revived the old Arcier aqueduct issue. Though drawing water from the Doubs made restoring Arcier seem unnecessary, Savarus argued the river water was unfit and promoted “Roman” water; the local Review echoed him and townspeople across parties accepted the idea. The controversy, driven by hidden interests, dismissed opponents as old-fashioned while Savarus’s projects dominated discussion. After eighteen months he had stirred the previously stagnant town, gaining influence without broad popularity.

He also won lawsuits for local priests; ambition consumed him. His apparent indifference to personal gain masked another moral usury: he expected larger rewards. In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with Léopold Hannequin’s funds, a move that also qualified him for election. The Abbé de Grancey, who discerned Savarus’s designs, called him remarkable and suggested he had taken a wrong path.

Meanwhile Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hôtel de Rupt, at first content merely to observe him. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, yielded ten thousand francs a year but was poorly managed by the inattentive Baron and an old steward, Modinier. The family often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque spot improved by the famous Watteville, who had built a dam and lake. Watteville’s heirs nonetheless pressed a dubious claim over the village of Riceys, whose inhabitants had long-held rights to the lands. The Baron's death left a legacy of usurpation his descendants continued to assert.

Rosalie’s maneuvers were subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey, enchanted by the lake and associating it with love. On a boat trip her father confided legal troubles with Riceys over land rights; Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus as lawyer, stressing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for their estate. She argued a lawsuit would add excitement and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey, clearly aiming to fix her future there and create gardens.

Four days later the Abbé visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing alignment would compromise his neutrality; the Abbé pressed political advantages, especially potential votes. Rosalie, determined to keep Savarus in Besançon and prevent his election, wrote to the préfet revealing Savarus’s ambitions but was ignored. She understood the political landscape and sought to manipulate it.

As the election approached, influential men unexpectedly nominated Savarus; he had been unaware. The Abbé warned him of betrayals and urged transparency about his past. Savarus’s passionate speech captivated many, yet the préfet worked to undermine him, eroding support. The first vote disappointed him; he returned home in despair, reflecting on his love for Francesca and an uncertain future. Then the Abbé brought hope: he had secured behind-the-scenes support, promised the Rouxey case would be resolved in time, and suggested marriage to Sidonie de Chavoncourt might consolidate votes. Savarus, initially resistant, recognized the advantage and prepared to navigate the coming choices of love and politics.

By nine the next evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were filled with Besançon’s aristocracy to discuss the surprise nomination of a candidate—the daughter of the Rupts, kin of a former Master of Appeals and secretary to a minister of the Elder Branch. Madame de Chavoncourt arrived with her younger daughter Sidonie; her elder sister, secure in her relationship, stayed away. Abbé de Grancey circulated, steering talk and probing an elderly statesman about whom the Elder Branch might field; they feared Berryer, noted the Duc de Fitz-James’s Toulouse nomination, and worried that backing Monsieur Savarus might push Republicans toward them rather than the Moderates.

By nine-thirty Albert had not come; Madame de Watteville took his lateness as an insult, while Madame de Chavoncourt urged patience. Rosalie, watching hostess and mother, noted the leniency shown Albert and hinted at a possible match between him and Sidonie. By ten some guests began playing cards. The Abbé, uneasy, went to the window where Rosalie had withdrawn and suddenly cried, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville, and Rosalie went to Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent; the servant Jérôme said Albert had left in a post-chaise. The Abbé exclaimed ambiguously, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!”—and Rosalie’s face betrayed a joy the Vicar-General affected not to notice.

Back in the drawing-room Monsieur de Watteville announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By half-past eleven about fifteen guests remained, including Madame de Chavoncourt and her daughters, the Abbes de Godenars and de Grancey, Monsieur de Vauchelles, Rosalie, Amédée de Soulas, and a retired magistrate who had supported Albert. Grancey stationed himself near the Baroness to watch Rosalie, whose pallor had turned to agitation.

When Madame de Chavoncourt inquired, a servant brought a letter for the Abbé on a silver tray. He read it; Rosalie grew pale and he recognized the handwriting. After a meaning glance he folded the letter into his pocket and declared, “She is in love with Albert Savarus!” As he moved to leave, Rosalie stopped him: the letter, she confessed passionately, was from Albert, and she begged him to remain silent when he announced Albert’s withdrawal—knowing such a withdrawal would doom any hope of her sister’s marriage to him.

On her way to Mass Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette that Albert had vanished. An elderly Parisian had visited Monsieur Savaron; Savaron turned pale at the card, met him over an hour, left with a large package, then hurried to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage with post horses, quit his post, and obtained a passport. His abrupt departure inflamed local prejudice against outsiders. Most soon forgot Albert—except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness pressed Rosalie to marry; the Baron promised the family estate, les Rouxey, though his wife fretted over finances. A match with Monsieur de Soulas was arranged; Rosalie refused, insisting she was content. Tensions rose during a fraught summer at les Rouxey. After Mariette and Jérôme married, the Baron invested in the estate—fuel for the Baroness’s resentment. The Vicar-General tried to mediate.

Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General that she had sabotaged Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo, by intercepting their letters, causing their separation. Horrified by the moral weight, the Vicar-General urged her to seek forgiveness. Her guilt deepened when a Parisian clerk, acting for Albert, came to sell his belongings and revealed that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received Albert’s letter: he forgave Rosalie and wished to remain in solitude.

The Vicar-General urged Rosalie to reconcile with her mother and accept de Soulas; she refused. After his death, Madame de Watteville married de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support. Now of age and financially independent, Rosalie went to Paris to confront the Duchess, publicly admitting at a ball her part in Albert’s downfall. She returned to Besançon having exposed the truth, but at heavy emotional cost—leaving a tale of love, betrayal, and the lasting consequences of ambition.

################################################
Mademoiselle de Watteville, known in the Eastern provinces, lived alone at Les Rouxey—riding, hunting, and improving her estate. Madame de Soulas, mother of two and looking younger than her years, contrasted with her husband, who told Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!” Given to eccentricities, she visited the Grande Chartreuse each year, perhaps remembering a grand‑uncle who once broke monastery walls to gain his freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon ostensibly to marry; she returned after a Loire steamboat boiler explosion that cost her a right arm and left leg and scarred her face. With failing health and constant pain, she devoted herself to religion at the Chartreuse of Les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
