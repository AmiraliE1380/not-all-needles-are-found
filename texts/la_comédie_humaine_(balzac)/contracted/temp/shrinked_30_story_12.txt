During the Restoration the Archbishop of Besançon frequently attended Baronne de Watteville’s drawing‑room because of her devoutness.

Monsieur de Watteville, a quiet, unassuming descendant of the notorious Watteville, lived in La Franche‑Comté. He married Mademoiselle de Rupt, an heiress, in a match arranged in 1802 and formalized in 1815 after the Second Restoration. After her grandparents’ deaths Madame de Watteville became more pious and influential in Besançon. Thin and plain, he worked as a turner and collected geological specimens. Admirers praised his noble soul: he accepted that he would not dominate his wife and devoted himself to work and hobbies. The Rupt residence reflected both families’ standing—spacious rooms, elegant furnishings, and fine cuisine—while his own estate remained modest. Their intimacy with the Archbishop attracted several notable abbés.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834 Abbé de Grancey announced a favorable lawsuit verdict; the Vicar‑General, surprised by the outcome in the present political climate, credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron. Amedée de Soulas, present that evening, would later prove significant.

France and England long exchanged cultural influences—language, fashion, and customs crossed the Channel. The English anthem “God Save the King” was linked to Lulli; dresses shifted from hoops to altered waistlines; and English terms like “dandy” and “lion,” popularized by Alfred de Musset, launched a vogue for stylish young men that reached the provinces.

In 1834 Besançon’s “lion” was Amedée‑Sylvain de Soulas, of Spanish descent. Ill at ease in the dull military town, he kept a spare apartment, lived frugally while cultivating an ostentatious appearance, and maintained a fashionably dressed servant, Babylas, as his “tiger.” Clever at economizing—often dining out—he used grooming and clothes to impress the local aristocracy and to court Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a modest eighteen‑year‑old zealously guarded by her mother. Amedée’s life thus illustrates social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraint.

“Madame,” Monsieur de Soulas began while his soup cooled, “one morning a gentleman from Paris took rooms in Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron and registered at the Mairie as ‘Albert Savaron.’ He left his card with the local officials.”

“The Savaron name is well known,” Mademoiselle de Watteville observed.

“He's a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amedee de Soulas replied. “If he claims the Savaron arms he must add a bar-sinister.” Rosalie noted that a bastard of a Comte de Savarus would still be noble, but the Baroness cut the discussion as Monsieur de Soulas continued.

“In a town like Besançon everything is catalogued,” he said. “Albert Savaron was quickly accepted by local lawyers, who wondered who sent him and why—he sent cards to judges instead of calling. Three days later he was mostly forgotten. He hired Jérôme, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant; no one saw him much.”

“He goes to mass?” asked Madame de Chavoncourt.

“He attends early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He rises between one and two, works until eight, breakfasts, works again, walks in his garden, dines, and goes to bed between six and seven,” Monsieur de Soulas said. Living across the way, he had learned details from Jérôme and village gossip.

“How did you come to hire a stranger for your defense?” the Baroness asked the Vicar-General. The President had assigned Savaron to defend a half-witted peasant accused of forgery; Savaron not only proved the man’s innocence but exposed the true culprits. A merchant later entrusted him with a difficult case, which he also won. Monsieur Berryer advised employing him for the Chapter’s matter; the Vicar-General met Savaron and felt reassured.

“Is he extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” replied the Vicar-General, and Abbe de Grancey, who had also met Savaron, elaborated. He described a plain outer room lined with law books: an old writing table, six armchairs, gray curtains, a green carpet. Savaron was younger than expected, dressed in a black dressing gown edged with red and wearing a red smoking cap—the “devil’s colors,” joked Madame de Watteville. But the Abbe praised his head: black hair streaked with gray, thick and curly; a round white throat; a splendid forehead furrowed by thought; an olive complexion; a square nose; fiery, eagle-like eyes. He had passion with calm, a sweet voice that could thunder, average height, with hands like a prelate. Subsequent visits found him in a shabby bedroom or taking a frugal breakfast in his library. He seemed to carry a heavy burden.

“Why leave Paris for Besançon?” someone asked. The Abbe had watched him at the bar and in court and thought Savaron would make an impact. His portrait of Savaron enthralled Rosalie. For the first time she encountered someone exceptional, unlike the ordinary Count who had courted her. She replayed the Abbe’s words—“What a fine forehead!”—and, recalling the prelate-like hands and the window she had seen from her bed, felt an urgent desire: she must see him.

That night she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse his windows. “While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought, watching candlelight through the trees and imagining his furniture. The household lectured that girls’ education should be limited lest they mature too soon; Rosalie’s sudden inward stirring showed how an Abbe’s portrait could transform the ordinary into the extraordinary for a young mind.

The next morning she saw Albert walking in the garden beside the Hotel de Rupt and resolved to enter his house, uncover his mystery, hear his voice, and glimpse his eyes. All day she embroidered with the concentration of someone who appears idle but is deeply reflective. She contrived to go to confession at half-past seven that Sunday, planning to see him at early service.

She visited her father in his workshop and proposed a grotto or belvedere in the garden from which she might look into Albert’s windows. Her father delighted; she begged him not to tell her mother. Sunday came: she dressed carefully, quickened her pace across the cross-court, checked the confessional, and positioned herself to see Albert as he entered church. No man could seem unattractive to Rosalie in that state. Albert, strikingly handsome, made a profound impression—solemn, burdened, his gaze conveying intense thought. In boldness she stood in his path to exchange glances; heat rushed through her. After he sat, she found a place to observe him during confession; time in the confessional seemed fleeting and, by the time she emerged, Mass had ended and he had gone. “The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besançon? I must know everything!” From then on she masked her preoccupation with an innocent air and supported her father’s belvedere plan.

“Mamma,” she said, “Father has a unique idea for a belvedere in the garden.”

“I approve of everything your father does,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly. Rosalie described a cool grotto with a place to take coffee; her mother inspected the proposed columns and approved a site not visible from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows but commanding a view into Savaron’s rooms. A builder was hired to construct a grotto with a path of periwinkles, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper. The interior would be rustic, with a mirror, an ottoman, a table of inlaid bark, and a chandelier carved by Monsieur de Watteville. Rumor spread through Besançon about the Wattevilles’ new garden feature.

“They’re wealthy and can afford such whims,” people said. “A thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed. The cost and the Parisian craftsman were gossiped about—no one suspected that Rosalie’s motive was romantic.

Meanwhile Savaron’s legal brilliance continued to rouse envy. He avoided social gatherings, which helped him remain a modest stranger even as he won cases. He had argued three complex matters at the Commercial Tribunal and gained four prominent merchants as clients who recognized his skill.

On the day the Wattevilles inaugurated their belvedere, Savaron was launching another enterprise: a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review. Quietly, through merchant connections, he raised capital—forty shares of five hundred francs each from his first ten clients—who hoped to promote Besançon as a trade and enlightenment center to rival Strasbourg and Dijon. Savaron’s vision appealed: to make Eastern France a hub of commerce and letters. He kept his name out of the spotlight; his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, handled finances while Savaron retained editorial control and a share of profits.

To manage the literary side he invited Alfred Boucher, the eager son of his main backer, to oversee contributions, while Savaron subtly guided content. The Review, with a low subscription price, quickly drew five hundred subscribers. The merchant-shareholders, pleased with the Review’s success, had paid only a fraction of their shares; with two hundred more subscribers, the publication would yield dividends for shareholders while the unpaid editor retained influence.

After the third issue the Review gained recognition among French publications. The third number included a tale attributed to Savaron that sparked comment in drawing rooms for its liberal tone; higher circles dismissed it as too forward. Rosalie, hearing of the Review, persuaded her father to subscribe.

“Papa,” she said, “subscribe and keep it in your room—mamma wouldn’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.” Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please his daughter, subscribed and lent Rosalie the four issues already published. That night she devoured the tale—the first story she had ever read. Its confession-like quality made her certain it reflected Savaron himself; others noted that his work drew on his life. The story’s intimacy deepened Rosalie’s fascination, confirming that the man she had glimpsed and the author on the page were one and the same.

In 1823 two young men left Lucerne to tour the lake of the Four Cantons. Passing Gersau, Rodolphe was arrested by a newly built house and a woman at the window and stayed behind while his friend Leopold continued. They had studied law together: Leopold was bound for a notary’s post in Paris; Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a harder past—his mother had sacrificed everything for his education. Passionate and impulsive, he contrasted with Leopold’s calm temperament.

Lodging in Gersau with a couple, Rodolphe learned the woman was Fanny Lovelace, who had come with her elderly father for his health. Of modest means, she supported him by translating and writing; Rodolphe was taken by her beauty and musical gift. Visiting the Bergmanns’ gardens—the family renting to the Lovelaces—he noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, who hinted at an unusual backstory, and found the Bergmanns protective of the household.

That evening he heard Fanny sing and was enchanted. After an unknown assailant wounded him, Fanny and Gina nursed him. While he recovered he learned of his mother’s death and found solace in Fanny. They bonded and confessed love, though Fanny had been forced into marriage with Emilio, who was not as old as first thought. Their passion grew, but Rodolphe’s jealousy—especially when Tito, a youth from Fanny’s past, reappeared—along with social ties and obligations, forced them to weigh love against duty and sacrifice.

For twenty days Rodolphe stayed with the Bergmanns, studying Francesca with a mix of fascination and puzzlement. He saw both youthful imprudence and a resolute, sometimes resistant character. The old man treated her like a father; her gratitude suggested an innate nobility that deepened the mystery.

Those days blended secret joys and melancholy, quarrels and harmony. Rodolphe was captivated by Francesca’s constant tenderness and jealous over trifles. When she complained of Gersau’s lack of luxuries, he asked if she valued such things. She answered passionately, linking a love of luxury to an appreciation of art and beauty while insisting she had borne hardship without complaint; her playful claim that ordinary people aspired to greatness added to her charm.

Francesca then insisted Rodolphe not accompany them to Geneva, citing gossip and the need to protect her husband’s reputation. She professed love but demanded secrecy, kissed him, and left him astonished.

The next day Rodolphe went to Vevay and then Geneva, renting a room in Eaux-Vives and inquiring after Italian refugees—hoping for a bookseller named Lamporani—but found only the arrivals of Prince and Princess Colonna and Prince and Princess Gandolphini. On his first walk he saw Francesca at Villa Diodati in an elegant carriage; learning she lodged with the Colonnas revealed her noble rank and made him fear he had been a passing whim.

He left his card with Prince Gandolphini and met a bogus Lamporani who invited him to a concert. Though curious, Rodolphe did not intrude. That evening his passion intensified and he felt driven to raise his social standing. At the concert Francesca was radiant; their meeting of eyes convinced him she had remained true despite her rank.

Afterwards he was introduced to the Colonnas and Francesca, who teasingly called herself “Princess Rodolphini,” reviving memories of Gersau. She acknowledged his faithful affection; he admitted his ambition—to achieve greatness so she could be proud.

Francesca was full of contradiction: meant for the cloister but forced by family tragedy into marriage with Prince Gandolphini to preserve status, she prized independence, liberal ideas, and sympathized with revolutionary sentiments. Winter in Geneva passed in her intelligent, beautiful company, but by spring Rodolphe grew restless; her calm clashed with his need for more demonstrative feeling. On a terrace he bitterly lamented others’ happiness; she, wounded, withdrew. Finding her in tears, he kissed her; she rebuffed him, asserted her dignity, ordered him to leave—declaring they would not meet again until Naples—and he, recognizing her will, complied.

In Paris Rodolphe found a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner. Though claiming indifference, Francesca persuaded Schinner to paint originals for Rodolphe and Emilio, and correspondence began.

Driven by love and ambition, Rodolphe threw himself into a fraudulent affair that consumed three years. When Villele’s ministry fell he was ruined; after three months’ recovery at Villa Gandolphini in Naples he turned to politics. His talents were praised and a high post promised, but the July 1830 upheaval destroyed those hopes. Only Francesca and God witnessed his courageous efforts—a noble heart ill-favored by fortune. Sustained by love, he endured trials. Lovers! Pray for him!

When Mademoiselle de Watteville finished, Rosalie flushed and wept. The novel—notable for a man narrating his own love—was her first encounter with that voice and convinced her Francesca Colonna was Albert’s beloved.

Curiosity hardened into resolve. Insecure about her looks and lack of musical talent, Rosalie resolved either to win Albert or learn the truth, dreaming schemes: sneaking into his garden, writing, visiting her father’s land to force a meeting.

From Albert’s window she saw Jerome helped over the wall by Mariette. Mariette’s plainness and relative freedom explained Jerome’s interest and showed servants could evade her mother’s rules. Rosalie escorted Mariette to Mass, coaxed access to letters Albert gave Jerome, and won her trust. Reading them, she learned Albert’s lineage, struggles, ambition, and steadfast devotion to Francesca. Though ashamed of the invasion, she clung to the letters as proof. When Mariette later brought a letter meant for Francesca, its fidelity moved Rosalie to step back—recognizing their noble bond and yearning for selfless love.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil; by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. Alone, that struggle fueled her passion and dark plans; her story is presented as a cautionary example.

That winter Albert de Savarus advanced his position in Besançon, eager for the Chamber’s dissolution. Backed by a wealthy contractor allied with moderates, he revived the old Arcier aqueduct issue. Though the Doubs supplied water, Savarus attacked its quality and promoted “Roman” water; the local Review echoed him and townspeople across parties accepted the idea. The controversy, driven by hidden interests, dismissed opponents as old-fashioned while Savarus’s projects dominated discussion. After eighteen months he had stirred the previously stagnant town, gaining influence without broad popularity.

He also won lawsuits for local priests; ambition consumed him. His apparent indifference to personal gain concealed the expectation of larger rewards. In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with Léopold Hannequin’s funds, a move that also qualified him for election. The Abbé de Grancey, who discerned Savarus’s designs, called him remarkable and suggested he had taken a wrong path.

Meanwhile Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hôtel de Rupt, initially to observe him. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, produced ten thousand francs yearly but was poorly managed by the inattentive Baron and an old steward, Modinier. The family often retired there, a picturesque spot improved by the famous Watteville, who built a dam and lake. Watteville’s heirs nonetheless pressed a dubious claim over the village of Riceys, whose inhabitants had long-held rights; the Baron's death left a legacy of usurpation his descendants continued.

Rosalie’s maneuvers were subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey, enchanted by the lake and linking it with love. On a boat trip her father confessed legal troubles with Riceys; Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus as lawyer, stressing the need to secure the Dent de Vilard and insisting Savarus be brought to les Rouxey—clearly aiming to fix her future there.

Four days later the Abbé visited Savarus to win the Watteville case; Savarus hesitated, fearing loss of neutrality, but the Abbé promised political advantages. Rosalie’s letter to the préfet exposing Savarus’s ambitions was ignored. As the election approached, influential men unexpectedly nominated Savarus; he had been unaware. The Abbé warned of betrayals and urged honesty about his past. Savarus’s passionate speech impressed many, yet the préfet undermined him and the first vote disappointed him. In despair he reflected on his love for Francesca and an uncertain future—until the Abbé reported behind-the-scenes support, the Rouxey case’s likely resolution, and proposed marriage to Sidonie de Chavoncourt to consolidate votes. Savarus, reluctantly appreciative, prepared to navigate the choices of love and politics.

By nine the next evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were full of Besançon’s aristocracy, debating the surprise nomination of a candidate connected to the Rupts, kin of a former Master of Appeals. Madame de Chavoncourt arrived with her younger daughter Sidonie; her elder sister stayed away. The Abbé de Grancey moved among the guests, probing an elderly statesman about what the Elder Branch might field; they feared Berryer, noted the Duc de Fitz‑James’s Toulouse nomination, and worried that supporting Monsieur Savarus might alienate Republicans rather than attract Moderates.

Albert had not appeared by nine‑thirty; Madame de Watteville took it as an insult, while Madame de Chavoncourt counseled patience. Rosalie, observing hostess and mother, noted the indulgence toward Albert and hinted at a possible match between him and Sidonie. By ten some guests played cards. The Abbé, uneasy, went to the window where Rosalie had withdrawn and suddenly cried, “He must be dead!” The Vicar‑General, Monsieur de Watteville, and Rosalie went to Albert’s dark rooms; the servant Jérôme said Albert had left in a post‑chaise. The Abbé exclaimed ambiguously, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!”—and Rosalie’s face betrayed a joy the Vicar‑General pretended not to see.

Back in the drawing‑room Monsieur de Watteville announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By half‑past eleven about fifteen guests remained, including Madame de Chavoncourt and her daughters, the Abbés de Godenars and de Grancey, Monsieur de Vauchelles, Rosalie, Amédée de Soulas, and a retired magistrate who had supported Albert. Grancey stationed himself near the Baroness to watch Rosalie, now pale and agitated. A servant brought a letter on a silver tray; the Abbé read it, recognized the handwriting, folded it into his pocket, and declared, “She is in love with Albert Savarus!” Rosalie then confessed the letter was Albert’s and begged him to remain silent when he announced Albert’s withdrawal—knowing that withdrawal would doom any hope of her sister’s marriage to him.

On her way to Mass Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette that Albert had vanished after a Parisian visitor called on Monsieur Savaron; Savaron left with a large package, hurried to the Prefecture, bought a carriage with post horses, quit his post, and obtained a passport. His abrupt departure inflamed local prejudice against outsiders. Most soon forgot Albert—except Girardet, the Vicar‑General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news. The Baroness pressed Rosalie to marry; the Baron promised the estate les Rouxey, though his wife fretted about finances. A match with Monsieur de Soulas was arranged; Rosalie refused. After a tense summer at les Rouxey the servants Mariette and Jérôme married and the Baron invested in the estate, increasing the Baroness’s resentment. Rosalie confessed to the Vicar‑General that she had intercepted letters between Albert and the Duchess Francesca d’Argaiolo, causing their separation and the tragic sequence tied to the Duc’s death. A Parisian clerk later sold Albert’s belongings and revealed he had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar‑General obtained Albert’s letter: he forgave Rosalie and wished to remain in solitude. Urged to reconcile with her mother and accept de Soulas, she refused. After the Vicar‑General’s death the Baroness married de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support. Now of age and financially independent, Rosalie went to Paris, publicly confessed at a ball her part in Albert’s downfall, and returned to Besançon having exposed the truth—at heavy emotional cost, leaving a tale of love, betrayal, and the lasting consequences of ambition.

Mademoiselle de Watteville lived at Les Rouxey, managing the estate. Madame de Soulas, mother of two, contrasted with her husband, who told Chavoncourt that marrying a bigot had cost him dearly. Eccentric, she visited the Grande Chartreuse annually, recalling a grand‑uncle who broke monastery walls to escape. In 1841 she left Besançon to marry but returned after a Loire steamboat boiler explosion that cost her right arm and left leg and scarred her face. Failing health and constant pain led her to devote herself to religion at Les Rouxey's Chartreuse.

PARIS, May 1842.
