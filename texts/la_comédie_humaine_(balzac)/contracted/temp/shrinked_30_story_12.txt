In Baronne de Watteville's drawing-room, a hub of Besançon society, the Archbishop often visited due to her devout nature. Monsieur de Watteville, from a notorious lineage, married wealthy heiress Mademoiselle de Rupt in 1815, which led Madame de Watteville to become increasingly pious. Their home, filled with fine furnishings, showcased their social status and attracted notable clergy.

During a dinner in September 1834, guests welcomed Monsieur l’Abbe de Grancey, who announced a legal victory attributed to young lawyer Monsieur Savaron. This revelation intrigued the guests, particularly Madame de Watteville, who inquired about Savaron’s background. The Vicar-General noted Savaron lived nearby, but his origins were unknown, raising curiosity about his character.

Historically, France and England have influenced each other culturally, especially in language and fashion. Styles considered "English" in Paris are seen as "French" in London, highlighting a mutual fascination. Recently, England introduced new terms to French, such as "dandy" and "lion," the latter describing a flamboyant man and influencing towns like Besançon, where Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas emerged as a local "lion." Amedee, of Spanish descent, sought to elevate his status in a conservative town.

Living modestly, Amedee maintained a lavish lifestyle with the help of his stylish servant, Babylas. Despite his extravagant appearance, he struggled financially, relying on a limited income. His social life revolved around impressing the local aristocracy, particularly Rosalie de Watteville, a sheltered young woman from a wealthy family.

The dynamic between Amedee and Rosalie was complicated by social standings and familial expectations. Amedee aspired to marry Rosalie for her dowry, while her mother scrutinized his intentions. Despite Amedee's charm, he faced challenges in gaining their approval, as Rosalie remained indifferent. Ultimately, Amedee's pursuit of Rosalie was a strategic move to elevate his social standing and secure a prosperous future, reflecting broader themes of aspiration and societal expectations.

Monsieur de Soulas shared the story of Albert Savaron, who moved from Paris to Besançon, rented an apartment, and registered as a barrister. Mademoiselle de Watteville recognized Savaron's noble lineage, but Amedee clarified that he was a commoner who had added a bar-sinister to his name to suggest nobility.

Initially ignored by the local legal community, Savaron worked hard and kept to himself, leading to his obscurity. He attended early mass but remained enigmatic. His reputation shifted when he successfully defended a mentally challenged man accused of forgery, impressing the court and attracting clients. The Vicar-General noted Savaron's striking appearance and eloquence, emphasizing his calm demeanor despite a passionate nature.

Madame de Chavoncourt questioned Savaron's motivations for coming to Besançon and his lack of community integration. The Abbe speculated that Savaron would eventually make a significant impact.

Rosalie de Watteville, intrigued by Savaron, found him more appealing than her superficial suitors. She planned to meet him at church and felt an intense connection upon seeing him during mass. As her fascination grew, she encouraged her father to build a Belvedere in their garden to observe Savaron's private life. Meanwhile, Savaron launched a fortnightly publication, the _Eastern Review_, promoting Besançon's interests, which Rosalie's father subscribed to, allowing her to read its contents and deepening her infatuation.

In July 1823, friends Rodolphe and Leopold traveled to Switzerland. Rodolphe became captivated by Fanny Lovelace in Gersau and decided to stay while Leopold continued. Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, was passionate and sensitive, while Leopold was set to become a notary.

Rodolphe found lodging with the Stopfer couple and learned Fanny lived modestly as a translator and writer. Eager to meet her, he gained access to her gardens, where he suspected her mute servant, Gina, had a hidden background. 

One evening, while listening to Fanny sing, Rodolphe overheard her speaking Italian with Gina, suspecting they were refugees. After a misunderstanding led to Gina attacking him, Fanny and Gina helped him recover, deepening Rodolphe's attraction.

As Rodolphe healed, he learned of his mother’s death, which plunged him into despair. Leopold returned to France, leaving Rodolphe in Gersau, where he found solace in Fanny's company. Their bond deepened as they shared sorrows and dreams. Fanny revealed she was married to an older man, Emilio, but expressed hope for a future together.

Rodolphe's feelings for Fanny intensified, admiring her strength and independence. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential. Their conversations revealed a shared passion for freedom and liberal ideals. When Fanny received news that her family was safe and would return to Italy, she felt joy, but Rodolphe struggled with jealousy regarding her past. Despite these challenges, Rodolphe remained devoted to Fanny, vowing to wait for her. Their love blossomed against the stunning Swiss landscape, symbolizing their hopes amid political turmoil and personal sacrifice.

For twenty days, Rodolphe stayed with the Bergmanns, captivated by Francesca, who represented youthful imprudence and the complexities of womanhood. He was intrigued by her affectionate relationship with her husband, Emilio, marked by secret joys and playful quarrels. One evening, Francesca expressed her longing for luxury, likening it to her love for art, and declined to accompany Rodolphe to Geneva, fearing gossip that could harm Emilio’s reputation. While professing her love for Rodolphe, she stressed the need for discretion to protect their bond.

The next day, after learning the Bergmanns had left, Rodolphe traveled to Geneva, settling in Eaux-Vives and inquiring about Italian refugees, particularly a bookseller named Lamporani. His curiosity led him to Villa Diodati, where he encountered Francesca in an elegant carriage, revealing her connection to the aristocratic Colonna family.

Rodolphe grappled with feelings of inadequacy for loving a princess disguised as a commoner. He sought an audience with Prince Gandolphini, Francesca’s husband, who welcomed him warmly. Despite his desire to see Francesca, Rodolphe returned to his lodgings, consumed by thoughts of their future.

When Rodolphe finally saw Francesca at a gathering, her beauty captivated him. Their eyes met, igniting a profound connection. Francesca’s subtle acknowledgment of their bond amidst the social elite deepened Rodolphe’s feelings, solidifying his love for her. As they spent time together, Francesca encouraged Rodolphe to pursue his ambitions while remaining youthful, reflecting their shared commitment to love faithfully despite challenges.

Francesca’s background as a well-educated heiress, initially destined for a cloistered life, added depth to her character. Her strategic marriage to Prince Gandolphini contrasted with her revolutionary spirit, aligning with Rodolphe’s aspirations.

As spring approached, Rodolphe struggled with longing and anxiety. Francesca’s calm demeanor contrasted with his emotional turmoil, leading him to question her feelings. Tension arose when Rodolphe compared Francesca to a French noblewoman, inadvertently hurting her. Sensing her distress, he sought to comfort her, but she instructed him to leave until they could meet again in Naples, marking a pivotal moment in their relationship.

Upon returning to Paris, Rodolphe discovered a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner, inspired by Francesca. Driven by ambition and love, he invested in a venture that failed, costing him three years. After the Villele ministry fell, he sought solace at Villa Gandolphini in Naples. Just as he was poised for success, the July 1830 revolution struck, but Rodolphe persevered, supported by love.

Meanwhile, Mademoiselle de Watteville was consumed by jealousy over Francesca, believing she loved Albert. This ignited a fierce desire to win his affection, yet she doubted her worthiness. Rosalie's imagination ran wild with schemes to connect with Albert, leading her to confront Mariette about her relationship with Jerome, offering silence in exchange for access to letters from Albert.

Rosalie learned from her father that a copy of the _Eastern Review_ was sent to the Duchesse d’Argaiolo in Italy, fueling her jealousy. She intercepted a letter from Albert revealing his noble lineage and ambitions, intensifying her infatuation. As Albert's legal career flourished, Rosalie's feelings morphed into obsession, compelling her to read another letter detailing his hopes for the Duchesse. Ultimately, Rosalie resolved to step back, believing she should sacrifice her desires for Albert and the Duchesse's happiness, grappling with unrequited love and questioning when she would find someone who truly loved her.

In the spring of 1835, nineteen-year-old Rosalie aspired to overcome the Duchesse d’Argaiolo, driven by her solitude and desires. Albert de Savarus, meanwhile, was making political strides in Besançon, gaining support from influential figures, including a wealthy contractor, to restore a vital aqueduct.

Savarus, who had represented local priests in legal matters, masked his true ambitions with a facade of indifference. In October 1834, he arranged financing for a merchant to position himself for election. The Abbe de Grancey recognized Savarus's potential but noted his reluctance to align with the Wattevilles, fearing it would compromise his neutrality.

Rosalie visited her family estate, les Rouxey, where she learned of her father's legal troubles and suggested hiring Savarus as their lawyer to safeguard her inheritance. While trying to keep Savarus in Besançon, she attempted to undermine his political ambitions by informing the Prefect of his Royalist leanings. Despite her efforts, Savarus was nominated, and his eloquence won the townsfolk's support.

As the election approached, the Prefect and his allies sought to weaken Savarus's backing, revealing the complexities of local politics. In despair over his unrequited love for Francesca, Savarus received encouragement from the Abbe, who urged him to connect with the Wattevilles, particularly Madame de Chavoncourt, whose daughter Sidonie could provide crucial support. This led Savarus to consider a marriage alliance with Sidonie, recognizing the sacrifices he would need to make for his ambitions.

The following evening, Madame la Baronne de Watteville hosted a gathering of Besançon’s aristocracy to discuss the election, particularly regarding the Rupts' daughter. The former Master of Appeals was to be introduced, with attendees including Madame de Chavoncourt and her daughters. The Abbe mingled among the guests, offering sharp political insights.

By nine o’clock, Albert de Savarus had not arrived, raising concern. While Madame de Chavoncourt dismissed his delay, Rosalie suspected otherwise. Frustration grew, and the Abbe speculated that Albert might be dead. They learned from a servant that he had left in a post-chaise, prompting mixed reactions of shock and relief.

As the evening continued, only a few guests remained. The Abbe received a letter that visibly affected Rosalie, revealing her feelings for Albert. He deduced she was in love with him and discovered that Albert had withdrawn from the election, leaving Rosalie desperate to keep this news secret to protect her sister Sidonie's prospects.

The next morning, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette about Albert Savaron's sudden disappearance after a visit from an old gentleman from Paris left him pale and agitated. His departure stirred Besançon, leading to the election of Monsieur de Chavoncourt.

As time passed, most forgot Albert, except for a few affected by his absence. Rosalie, troubled by the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo's death, refused her mother’s suggestion to marry Monsieur de Soulas.

Tension escalated during a summer at les Rouxey, where Rosalie's refusal to marry became public. Tragedy struck when the Baron drowned while retrieving a shell from the lake, deepening the rift between mother and daughter. Grief-stricken, Rosalie contemplated confessing her role in Albert's fate, having intercepted letters between him and the Duchess.

The Vicar-General urged her to seek redemption. Hannequin revealed that Albert had entered a monastery, seeking peace after heartbreak. Years later, Rosalie's mother married Monsieur de Soulas, but Rosalie remained resolute in her feelings for Albert. She confronted the Duchess at a ball, revealing the truth about Albert's letters, leaving the Duchess shaken. Rosalie returned to Besançon, feeling a sense of closure marked by loss and a quest for redemption.

Mademoiselle de Watteville, now living alone at les Rouxey, is known for her solitary pursuits. After leaving Besançon in 1841 to marry, she returned severely injured from a steamboat accident, losing her right arm and left leg, and now leads a life devoted to religious practices at les Rouxey.
