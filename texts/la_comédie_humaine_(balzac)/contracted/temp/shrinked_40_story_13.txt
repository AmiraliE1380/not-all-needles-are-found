Sweetheart, I too am free! I write first, unless you have already written to Blois.

Do not be alarmed; leaving the convent was simple. The conscience that tormented me gave way to gentler reasons: my aunt feared I would waste away, and my mother relented. I am in Paris thanks to you. If you had seen me when I left you, you would have been proud of your influence on my young heart. We had been so intimate at the convent that when you left I sank into melancholy. I felt like a turtle‑dove widowed of her mate; death seemed sweet, and I nearly succumbed. To be at Blois, frightened of taking vows and without you, made me ill.

My aunt could not understand our private life or why the convent routine ill suited girls of our temperament. She had sacrificed for a brother; to expect the same of us was too much. For a fortnight I suppressed everything, but letter‑writing became my lifeline. I began a journal to mirror yours and dreamed of Gemenos as you will dream of Paris.

One morning a lady companion and Philippe, my grandmother’s last valet, arrived to take me away. My aunt kissed me and warned that pride might lead me to heaven or hell. I kissed her hands and promised not to return unless my heart were broken.

At Beaugency I still felt stunned. No one met me—my mother at Bois de Boulogne, my father at council, my brother late to dress. Philippe led me to my grandmother’s suite, the Princess de Vauremont, whose fortune had been left to me. Entering the rooms where she died brought a wave of grief; though she died in 1817, the place seemed recently occupied. It pained me that so little had been done: panels empty, marbles broken. Philippe said the Princess’s furniture was left to me, and my father awaited a law restoring lost property before renovating—an estimate of three hundred thousand livres had been set.

At first despair gripped me: had my father meant to send me to the convent while sparing no expense on my household? But memories of the Princess softened me; the rooms preserved her spirit. I set Philippe to polishing and rearranging, giving the old furniture a youthful air.

The bedroom and drawing‑room, hung in yellow damask and scarlet and filled with family relics—Savonnerie tapestry, a timepiece from Marshal de Saxe, china from Marshal de Richelieu, my grandmother’s portrait—breathed another century and her coquetries. I remembered how she tended her appearance to the end: traces of nocturnal visitors, disarranged furniture, card‑tables, snuff remained. When I was sent to the convent in 1816 she bade me go without display, promising I should remain independent and marry whom I pleased. Six months later she died; her will, entrusted to the Prince de Talleyrand, hinted she disapproved of the vows. I hope to meet the Prince and learn more.

Thus, though no one in flesh met me at first, the Princess’s presence comforted me, and I resolved to keep our pact: to tell each other the smallest domestic details. Send me every minute picture of your life.

October 19.
At half‑past five Rose announced my mother’s return. Her suite corresponds to mine and we share a secret staircase. I descended anxious about our reunion, wondering if I should feign affection. Her tact surprised me: neither distant nor effusive, she greeted me like a friend and kissed my forehead. She said she preferred my living with family to dying at the convent and wished to be more sister than mother. I found her charming at thirty‑eight—beautiful, with dark‑blue eyes and elegant hands—and responded kindly. She advised I not appear in society before visiting the dressmaker.

My father arrived and greeted me affectionately, calling me his rebel daughter and praising my looks; he predicted future pride. My brother, the Duc de Rhetore, arrived leisurely and kissed me; I was conscious of his indifference to my convent absence.

Thus ended the return of an eighteen‑year‑old after nine years at a convent, to a noble household where the old ways linger. Tired, I kept convent hours and slept early.

November 25.
The next morning the rooms were ready; Philippe had put flowers in the vases. Breakfast was troublesome—convent habits leave one hungry early. About one, my father found me writing to you and gave me a purse containing twelve thousand francs as the annual allowance he intended for me. He also told me my grandmother left me five hundred thousand francs in government stock, yielding about forty thousand francs a year. He had planned to use this to set up my younger brother but now left the decision to me, trusting my good sense. He warned that a Chaulieu daughter must comport herself with pride; any slander might have dire consequences. His frankness pleased me and spared my pride.

Philippe spent the morning arranging my transformation: dressmaker Victorine, corset‑maker, shoemaker, glove‑maker—all to fashion me out of convent attire. I was impatient to be made presentable; even a glovemaker was called. At lunch my mother proposed we go to the milliner; I felt dazzled by this independence—like a blind man who had recovered sight. I began to sense how far I had been from society.

My father often seems absent: deeply engaged in state affairs, he refused a Minister of State appointment and prefers an embassy. Family life is fragmentary at home: my brother and father are often away, my mother devotes mornings to dressing and keeps a busy social calendar of drives, receptions, and theatre evenings. We meet mainly at lunch and dinner; otherwise each pursues his own life. I see why family affection is so seldom; in Paris people find no time for private intimacy.

Miss Griffith, my governess—recommended by the English ambassador—is a Scotch clergyman’s daughter, thirty‑six, well‑bred and meant to teach me English and chaperon me. She is cautious but accommodating; I quickly see she will be governed by me. She advises against being dazzled by society and against being seduced by what attracts me most—her moral lectures are brief and repetitive.

I have not yet seen the streets; I must first be made presentable. The Italian opera opens soon, and my mother has a box. I read avidly; modern literature often dwells on love—the theme that once obsessed us at the convent. Corinne and Adolphe delighted me. When I asked if I might meet Mme. de Staël, my parents laughed and informed me she was dead.

December.
My Darling—tomorrow I make my debut. I have rehearsed every folly: stays, shoes, curls, dress, ornaments—all inspected in private with Griffith as witness. In short, I have left the convent and been reborn as a frivolous society girl. I may be raw in some ways—shoulder lines not yet rounded—but there is strength, health, and a clear, vivid complexion. My features are correct, my hair pale gold with a darker center, my eyes bright and insolent, the mouth expressive. I have grace of gait and voice; my grandmother’s manner and voice are in me. If I wish, I can be innocent, witty, or melancholy enough to command admiration.

My father has provided me a trousseau—ribbons, shoes, gloves, dressing‑case, scent‑box, fan, prayer‑book, cashmere shawl—and promised riding lessons. I can dance; tomorrow evening I will appear in white muslin with a Greek garland of white roses and intend to play the simpleton to win the other women. My mother underestimates me and sees me as a simpleton; my brother treats me with polite contempt. I suspect Alphonse harbors secret discontent and jealousy toward our father, which none of the household understands.

We are so unlike my parents: my father absorbed in public life and pleasures, my mother devoted to her toilette and society. They both know how to wear their parts; the show of unity conceals indifference. It amazes me how Paris life can consume people from morning until the small hours, draining energy and money. I am newly curious about this spell of society that keeps people enslaved and separate. For now I have sixteen hours a day to think, while those around me barely have minutes for reflection. There is much to learn in this world I sought; I am eager and anxious at once.

Yesterday at two o’clock I drove along the Champs-Élysées and through the Bois de Boulogne on a beautiful autumn day that reminded me of the Loire. At last I felt I had experienced Paris; the Place Louis XV seemed a testament to human artistry.

I was impeccably dressed, pensive, and poised, yet no one smiled. The carriage moved slowly, matching my demeanor. Only my father, a duke, acknowledged me with satisfaction; my mother blew a butterfly kiss. Griffith scanned indiscriminately. A young woman should know where to look.

I was furious. A man inspected my carriage without seeing me—probably a carriage-maker. I had misjudged my appeal; beauty, it seems, is common in Paris. Hats were tipped to the less deserving while my mother drew admiration. I resolved to learn why.

The men generally disappointed: unattractive, mere echoes of former elegance. Their fashion lacked the color and comfort of earlier days; hats perched like truncated columns. It seems easier to incite a revolution than to make a stylish hat, yet the French cling to ridiculous styles. Most faces looked worn, betraying thwarted ambition and bruised vanity; a fine forehead was rare. “And these are the products of Paris!” I said to Miss Griffith. “Most cultivated and pleasant men,” she replied. I was silent; a thirty-six-year-old spinster’s heart practices tolerance.

That evening I went to a ball, staying close to my mother, who received all attention. I was merely a pretext for compliments aimed at her. She found me partners who all praised the heat and beauty of the ball as though I were freezing and blind; each man pretended it was the first time he saw me.

My dazzling dress paled beside the extravagant attire of married women, each surrounded by admirers and watching one another closely. A few shone with genuine beauty—my mother among them. At a ball a girl is merely a dancing machine—insignificant.

The men impressed me no more there than on the avenue: exhausted, bland, uniform. The proud, vigorous men of our ancestors seemed vanished. One strikingly handsome man failed to stir me; he was not noble. No matter his genius, a commoner could not move me. He seemed absorbed in himself—an intellectual who views women as objects. I learned he is a professor and author, driven by ambition.

I resolved not to quarrel with society for failing to recognize my merits and instead embraced the simple joy of dancing, which I thoroughly enjoyed. I overheard trivial gossip about strangers; perhaps my ignorance spared me. Society is a web of intrigue, and I am perceptive.

Returning home pleasantly tired, I began sharing my experiences with my mother. She interrupted: “My dear child, it requires as much tact to know when to be silent as when to speak.” Her words revealed feelings that should remain concealed even from a mother. I realized the vast realm of feminine duplicity. In our innocence we might seem astute gossipers; a mere gesture or glance can teach profound lessons. Suddenly I felt overwhelming shyness. Must I never again express my joy in dancing? What of deeper emotions?

I went to bed sorrowful, reeling from the clash between my candid nature and society’s harsh rules. Already the hedges of my life are flecked with white wool! Farewell, beloved.

How deeply your letter moved me, especially as our paths diverge: you to Parisian brightness, I to a quiet retreat.

At the Castle of Maucombe my room remains the same, now opening on the Gemenos valley I once saw without understanding. Soon after my arrival my parents took me to dine with M. de l'Estorade, a wealthy, frugal old gentleman who lost his only son to Bonaparte’s conscription in 1813. The youth evaded the draft, joined the Emperor’s bodyguard, and vanished after Leipzig. The Baron clung to hope and hoarded his income for him. Only later I learned my future husband had been slowly returning from Russia, Poland, and Germany; his fortunes turned in Berlin where the French Minister helped him home. The Baron, a small Provençal landowner with about ten thousand livres income, remains obscure enough in Provence despite these adventures.

The late Mme. de l'Estorade’s estate combined with the father’s savings amounts to roughly two hundred and fifty thousand livres—excluding property—a considerable fortune here. The Baron has just bought a poorly run estate he plans to plant with ten thousand mulberry trees. Now that his son is found, his one desire is to marry him to a girl of good family.

My parents approved once they learned Renée de Maucombe would be accepted without a dowry and that her prospective inheritance would be acknowledged in the marriage contract. My younger brother Jean signed a document recognizing an advance on his inheritance. This is how the nobles of Provence work within Bonaparte’s Civil Code, which otherwise forces many girls into convents rather than marriage.

That dinner was the first meeting of your sweetheart and the exile. My father-in-law-to-be has no castle, only a simple country house at the entrance of a beautiful valley: unpretentious, four pebble walls and a red-tiled roof, a typically Provençal garden enclosed by low walls with a touch of neglect. Still, the house did its best for the dinner, serving us on old silver.

The exile is emaciated and pale, marked by suffering. At thirty-seven he looks much older, with white streaks in once-beautiful hair; he is a little deaf, like the Knight of the Sorrowful Countenance. Despite his ravaged appearance, he seems pleased to be marrying the fair Renée de Maucombe.

I have graciously agreed to become Mme. de l'Estorade, bringing the two hundred and fifty thousand livres, on condition that I may transform the grange into a park. I have asked my father for a water grant to help this. In a month I will be Mme. de l'Estorade, having made a good impression.

While you prepare to enjoy Paris’s vibrant life, I descend from our shared dreams into the humble realities of country existence. I am determined to bring joy to this young man, who knew little youth, going straight from his mother’s arms to war and Siberia. I will take pleasure in the quiet, improving the little oasis around our home with fine trees and greenery: a hillside park with perhaps a kiosk from which I can glimpse the Mediterranean, orange and lemon trees, and the poetry of the surrounding nature. My father-in-law and the Chevalier share my religious sentiments.

My life will run like a smooth highway, free from the turmoil of another Bonaparte. If I have children they will be mine to nurture; if you remain true to your destiny, your children will have in me a devoted protector. I bade farewell to the romances and adventures we once imagined: my milestones will be the care of the young Masters de l'Estorade, and the mischief they bring. Perhaps one day I may visit Marseilles; otherwise provincial dangers are minimal. We will interest ourselves in the silkworms whose mulberry leaves we sell and share in Provence’s ordinary ups and downs. Quarrels seem unlikely: M. de l'Estorade has promised to leave household matters to me, and I shall see that he does.

You, dear Louise, will provide the romance in my life. Tell me of your balls, your dresses, and the men you meet; your other self will always be with you, listening and feeling the excitement. If only I could enjoy some Parisian fun while you managed La Crampade—the name of our grange—poor M. de l'Estorade thinks he is marrying one woman; will he discover two?

I must conclude—I am foolish only through you. One kiss on each cheek; my lips remain untouched, he has only taken my hand. P.S. I have just opened your third letter. I have about one thousand livres to spend; buy me pretty things we cannot find here or in Marseilles. Think of the recluse at La Crampade; neither family has anyone in Paris with taste to buy what we need. I will answer your letter later. Goodbye, dear.

VI. DON FELIPE HENAREZ TO DON FERNAND PARIS, September.

The heading reassures you that our house survives. The slaughter of our ancestors in the Court of Lions forced us into a double life, teaching an Arab cunning that likely saved me. Ferdinand's fear made his acting convincing enough to deceive Valdez; had I not stepped in, the Admiral would have died. The Liberals misunderstand kingship; I know this Bourbon well—his repeated pledges of protection only confirm my mistrust. A true Spaniard need not repeat promises; too many words betray falsehood.

Valdez sailed on an English ship. Watching our beloved Spain's cause collapse in Andalusia, I set my escape in motion. I dispatched my steward, sent coral fishers to meet me on the coast, and while Ferdinand tried to seize me, I was already on my barony of Macumer among brigands who defy authority. The last Hispano‑Moorish family of Granada has found refuge in an African desert—there is even a Saracen horse on an estate inherited from Saracens. Those brigands who once dreaded my rule now shield me from the King's vendetta, proud to defend the Duc de Soria, a Henarez—the first to visit them since the Moors ruled.

I had imagined living off the estate's income, but Quevedo was right: my possessions are nominal—vast prairies without houses, virgin forests without furniture. It would take a million piastres and a resident master for fifty years to make them productive. That problem must be faced.

Seeing my noble country left a carcass for monks to exploit moved me to tears; it foretells Spain's grim future. In Marseilles I learned of Riego's fall. It struck me that my life would be prolonged as unnoticed martyrdom. Is existence worth living when one can neither die for his country nor live for a woman? To love and to conquer—this was our law. Now my sabre is broken, the palace in ruins, the spring dried to sand.

Here is my last will.

Fernand, this explains why I checked your enthusiasm and urged loyalty to rey netto. As brother and friend I implore you, as master I command: approach the King and claim my dignities, property, office, and titles. He may hesitate; tell him you are loved by Marie Heredia, and she will marry only a Duc de Soria. This will please the King: the Heredia fortune has long blocked my ruin. Your proposal will seem to deprive me of a final resource, so he will gladly hand over my spoils.

You will marry Marie. Her secret love for you was no secret to me; I have prepared the old Count for your succession. Marie and I fulfilled our family duties; everything favors you. You are handsome and possess Marie's heart. I am an ill‑favored grandee for whom she harbors an unconfessed aversion; any reluctance due to my misfortunes you will soon overcome.

As Duc de Soria, you will not regret my predecessor nor lose a maravedi. I will keep my mother's diamonds for independence; the gap they leave can be filled by Marie's jewels. Send them with my nurse Urraca—the only servant I shall keep. No one makes my chocolate like her.

During our short revolution my wants shrank to necessities, covered by my salary. You will find two years' income with your steward. This sum is mine, but a Duc de Soria cannot marry without expense, so we will share it. You will not refuse this wedding gift from your brigand brother; I intend it so.

The barony of Macumer, not Spanish territory, remains mine. Thus I still have a country and a name should I ever re‑enter the world.

Thank Heaven, this concludes our business, and the house of Soria is saved!

Just as I resigned myself to being merely Baron de Macumer, the French cannon announced the arrival of the Duc d'Angouleme. You will understand why I must stop here.

October.

When I arrived I had not ten doubloons. A poor leader finds means to live within calamities he cannot avert: for the vanquished Moor, a horse and the desert; for the frustrated Christian, the cloister and a few coins. My resignation is weariness, not surrender. I am not prepared to abandon all thought of life. Ozalga gave letters of introduction, including one to a bookseller who serves our compatriots as Galignani serves the English. He found me eight pupils at three francs a lesson. I teach every other day, earning twelve francs—more than I need. When Urraca arrives I will pass my connection to another Spanish exile.

I lodge in Rue Hillerin‑Bertin with a poor widow who takes boarders. My room faces south, with a small garden. It is quiet; I have green trees to gaze on, and I spend one piastre a day. The calm comforts me; from sunrise until ten I smoke and enjoy chocolate, contemplating two Spanish plants—a broom rising from jasmine—colors that thrill any scion of the Moors. At ten I give lessons until four, return for a frugal dinner, then read and smoke until bed.

I can endure this life of work, meditation, solitude, and modest society for a long time. Be happy, Fernand; my abdication brings no regrets. Five days have passed since I wrote my will; they feel like five centuries. Honor, titles, wealth—as if they never existed.

Now that conventional barriers of respect are down, I open my heart to you, dear boy. Though outwardly grave, my heart is full of tenderness and devotion—never granted to any object, not even she who was to be my bride since childhood. Spain became my mistress and received my homage. Now Spain is lost. Beggared of all, I look on the ruins of my former self and ponder the mystery of my existence.

Why was life given to this carcass, and when will it depart? Why did a race famed for chivalry infuse all its virtues into this last offshoot only for the seed not to sprout—no stem, no flower to scatter its Eastern perfumes? What crime did I commit before birth to inspire no love? Was I destined to be stranded, a useless hulk on a barren shore? In my soul I see deserts where my ancestors roamed under a scorching sun. Proud remnant of a fallen race, vain force, love wasted—an old man in the prime of youth—I shall await death's last grace. Under this murky sky no spark will rekindle these ashes. Thus my last words may echo Christ's: "My God, Thou hast forsaken me!"—a cry of agony and terror whose depth few mortals have explored.

You can now understand, Fernand, the joy I find in living through you and Marie. I will watch you with the pride of a creator satisfied with his work. Love one another well and continue to do so; any discord between you would pain me more than it would you.

Our mother had a premonition that events would one day fulfill her wishes. Perhaps a mother's longing is a pact with God. She often revealed a heart that rightly belonged to both her sons, yet was given to you alone by an irresistible passion. Her spirit will hover above you as you bow at the altar. What I have done pleases our womenfolk, the dead, and the King; it is God's will. Do not resist, Fernand; obey and remain silent.

P.S. Tell Urraca to call me nothing but M. Henarez. Do not mention me to Marie. You must be the sole living soul to know the secrets of the last Christianized Moor, whose veins carry the blood of a great family that began in the desert and is now about to die out in solitary exile.

Farewell.

VII. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENEE DE MAUCOMBE

What! To be married so soon? After a month you become engaged to a stranger about whom you know nothing. He may be deaf, sickly, tiresome—anything. Don't you see, Renée, what they want from you? You're needed to continue the l’Estorades line. You'll be buried in the provinces. Were these the promises we made? Were I you, I'd rather risk a voyage to Hyères, capture by Algerian corsairs, and a life as a Sultana—at least an adventure!

You're merely leaving one convent for another. I know you; you are timid, and you'll submit to family with docility. But I will guide you; come to Paris. There we will make men frantic and hold court like queens. Your husband may join the Chamber in three years. I know that world; you will work it well and live as a woman of fashion. I won't leave you to rot in your grange.

Monday.

For a fortnight I've been living society's life: evenings at the Italiens, the Grand Opera, always a ball. Opera music enchants me; I am the focus of admiration. Yet no young man has stirred me; none has evoked the feeling García rouses in Otello. I am jealous—jealousy pure as Rossini made it. What a wretched dramatist Shakespeare is! Othello wins battles and leaves Desdemona meekly neglected. Such a sheep deserves slaughter. Let the man I love beware of thinking of anything but loving me!

Give me the long trials of chivalry: the lover sent among lions for a glove—what exquisite prizes are lost by such impertinence! But I ramble. My father is likely to represent our master the King in Madrid, and I shall accompany him. My mother wishes to remain; my father may take M. de Canalis as secretary—if the King doesn't appoint another. Canalis, the poet, cultivates my mother and studies diplomacy with her. Diplomacy must be a fine art; he pursues it like a gambler at the Stock Exchange.

Our elder brother, the Duc de Rhetore, will stay in Paris. Miss Griffith has discovered Alphonse is in love with a ballet girl at the Opera—how can one love legs and pirouettes? Two ballet girls in a family are ruinous. My second brother is with his regiment; I have not seen him. Thus I must act as Antigone of His Majesty's ambassador. Perhaps I may marry in Spain; perhaps my father imagines a marriage without a dowry. He teased me, asking my opinion of young men; I dissimulated with trivial remarks about hair and faces. He seemed inwardly disappointed.

Saturday.

One thing I left out: the love we imagined is extremely well hidden; I have seen no trace. Quick glances occur in drawing‑rooms, but how colorless it all is! Love as we pictured it—wonders, dreams, sorrows that awaken sympathy—does not exist. The bewitching words, constant interchange of happiness, the misery of absence—where are they? What soil produces these radiant flowers of the soul? Which is wrong: us or the world?

I have seen hundreds of men; not one has moved me. No proof of admiration or devotion on their part would touch me. Love is so rare one might live a lifetime without meeting the being who can make one happy. The thought chills; if found too late, what then?

For days I've trembled at women's destinies. Marriage is mere chance. Look at yours. I dreamed a storm of wild thoughts: to be loved as much after ten years as on the first day—such love demands years. The lover must languish; curiosity must be piqued and satisfied. Is there a law for the heart's inner fruits, as for nature's? Can joy be made lasting? In what measure should love mix tears with pleasure? Only God can solve this.

I begin to think I'll return to the convent. If I stay in society I may do foolish things, for I cannot reconcile what I see with delicacy and principle.

My mother is the happiest, adored by Canalis, that "great little man." I sometimes crave to know what passes between them. Griffith says virtue is burying savage instincts, but then what becomes of the heart? It becomes a sink for the worst in us.

It's humiliating that no admirer has yet appeared for me. I am marriageable, but family honor may keep men away. If that's why, they are cowards.

The parts of Chimène in the Cid and the Cid himself delight me. What a marvelous play! Farewell.

VIII. THE SAME TO THE SAME, January.

Our master is a poor refugee, hiding for his role in the revolution quashed by the Duc d'Angouleme—a triumph that has led to splendid fêtes. Though Liberal and perhaps a man of the people, he has piqued my interest; I suspect he was condemned to death. I make him talk to uncover his secret, but he has a Castilian taciturnity—prideful yet angelic in patience and gentleness. His pride is not the petulant sort; it belongs to his nature, imposing civility through perfect manners and keeping us at a distance with the respect he shows.

My father jokes there is much nobility in Señor Henarez, whom he calls Don Henarez. I took the liberty of addressing him so. He raised his eyes—usually cast down—and flashed a look that abashed me; he certainly has the most beautiful eyes. I asked if I had offended; he replied in grand Spanish: "I am here only to teach you Spanish." I blushed and felt snubbed. I nearly retorted sharply, but remembering our mother's counsel, I instead begged his pardon.

A tremor passed through him; his olive cheeks flushed. He said religion must teach respect for the unhappy better than I could; had he been a don in Spain who lost everything in Ferdinand VII.'s triumph, my witticism would be unkind. If he were but a poor language teacher, would satire be heartless? Neither description suits a young lady of rank.

He bowed, opened my Don Quixote, and began. That little incident disturbed me more than any compliment. During the lesson I watched him, safe because he rarely looks at me.

From appearances, the tutor, whom we assumed forty, is under thirty. My governess admired his black hair and pearly teeth. His eyes are velvet and fire, but his face is plain and singular—smallpox scars give his ugliness piquancy; a prominent forehead, shaggy brows, a stoop, and an odd large head give him a harsh, plaintive look, like a sickly child needing superhuman care. As my father noted, his features recall a shrunken Cardinal Ximenes. The natural dignity of his manners unsettles the Duke, who dislikes him and never feels at ease before such superiority.

Once my father knows enough Spanish we'll go to Madrid. When Henarez returned two days after his reprimand, I said, out of gratitude: "I have no doubt you left Spain because of political events. If my father is sent there, as seems likely, we might help you obtain a pardon, if you are under sentence."

"It is impossible for anyone to help me," he replied.

"But," I asked, "is that because you refuse help or because the situation is impossible?"

"Both," he answered, bowing, closing the subject.

My father's blood boiled. I was offended by his haughty posture and promptly dropped Señor Henarez. Yet something admirable lay in his refusal of aid. "He would not accept even our friendship," I thought as I conjugated a verb. Suddenly I spoke to him in Spanish. Henarez said equality of sentiment is necessary between friends, which did not exist here, making the matter pointless.

"Do you mean equality of feeling, or equality in rank?" I demanded, eager to provoke.

He raised those awe‑inspiring eyes, and mine fell. Dear, he is a hopeless enigma. The glance asked whether my words implied love; the mingled joy, pride, and doubt in it moved me. It was clear that advances, taken lightly in France, might complicate with a Spaniard, and I retreated, feeling foolish.

The lesson ended; he bowed, and his eyes expressed a humble prayer: "Don't trifle with a poor wretch." This sudden variance from his usual grave dignity made a deep impression. It seems horrible to say, but I can't help believing there are treasures of affection in that man.

All is over, my dear child; Mme. de l'Estorade writes. Between us nothing has changed; it is only one girl the less.

Do not be troubled; I did not consent rashly. My life is planned, and certainty suits me. A moral resolve has removed chance. We have property to develop and a home to beautify; I must manage a household and reconcile my husband to life. I shall likely have children to educate.

Everyday life cannot be cast in a heroic mould, yet I can still explore the infinite within familiar tasks. My humble duties allow passion; restoring faith in happiness to the suffering is a noble way to relieve life's monotony. I see little room for suffering and much good to be done. My love for Louis de l'Estorade does not make my heart race, yet nothing in him offends me.

My instincts for greatness and my mental energies remain mine, concealed and devoted to the family's welfare. The time for a woman to seek admiration is short; if my life is not grand, it will at least be calm and free from shocks. Nature has given us the choice of love or motherhood. I have chosen motherhood: my children will be my gods, this spot of earth my Eldorado.

Thank you for what you have sent; see the enclosed list of my needs. I am determined to live with refinement and some luxury, taking from provincial life only what adds charm. Solitude never vulgarizes a woman—she remains herself. My father-in-law is delighted and is sending workmen from Paris to renovate the house.

X. MLLE. DE CHAULIEU TO MME. DE L'ESTORADE
January.

Oh Renée, you have made me miserable. Your proud beauty and fine heart will be wasted in a dull marriage; your children will be monsters. You seem resigned—neither hopeful nor afraid—but what if one morning a man awakens you? The thought chills me. At least you will have a friend and become the guardian of your valley; I will remain a heart untouched by petty society, all yours.

Monday.

My Spaniard bewilders me with calm, severe dignity. His solemn silence and reserve provoke curiosity; how strange that a language teacher should fascinate me more than any nobleman. His coldness, his distance, wrapped in pride and mystery, both amuses and irritates me.

Yesterday I said something that struck M. Henarez; he did not answer, and the lesson ended with a bow implying he might not return—an outcome I prefer, for I dread sentimental catastrophe. Tell me every detail of your first days with that strange creature called a husband; I promise to do the same if I ever find love.

XI. MME. DE L'ESTORADE TO MLLE. DE CHAULIEU
La Crampade.

Your Spaniard and you make me shudder. Dismiss him. Everything you describe fits a dangerous adventurer who risks all. He cannot be your husband or lover. I will write more about my married life when my heart is less anxious from your letter.

XII. MLLE. DE CHAULIEU TO MME. DE L'ESTORADE
February.

This morning my father visited, more thoughtful than usual. After flattering me and admitting he’d misjudged my intelligence, he launched into politics: France remains shaken—its Revolution persists in law and thought; the King rules without force, nobles are weak before the elected, and the family has been undermined by individualism and state finance. He argued a state cannot survive without paternal authority and half-jokingly asked me to persuade him to sacrifice my inheritance for my younger brother; then reminded me a husband will expect a dowry. We parted in good humor.

Your warning about Henarez made me cautious. I hesitated to end my lessons, yet I’m drawn to him. His voice thrills me; his manners are simple and genuine. After a lesson he almost kissed my hand, stopped, and looked terrified; my smile encouraged him, his eyes were wet, and I felt a strange happiness. He fascinates me.

Friday.

He is handsome and eloquent, revealing intellectual power and a new world. He told of a valet who died for a queen’s glance and I was captivated. At a ball M. de Vandenesse hinted Henarez might be of noble descent—the Duke de Soria, his titles lost under Ferdinand—raising suspense.

In our next lesson I suspected him; he denied it, calling himself a servant of the King of Spain. His reticence provoked me; I devised a test: I had him translate a letter I wrote describing my ideal lover. He was visibly moved and asked to keep the letter as a remembrance. He left asking to speak with my father; from my father I learned Henarez is now the Baron de Macumer, with property in Sardinia.

Two weeks have passed since I saw him; I wonder what he is doing. He has become necessary to me, and I am torn between love and the realities of my situation. Sunday at the Italian Opera I felt his fiery gaze. He has a genius for love.

XIII. MME. DE L'ESTORADE TO MLLE. DE CHAULIEU
La Crampade, February.

My dear Louise, I waited to write until I had learned enough for your happiness. The difference between girl and married woman is vast; one cannot fully understand the other. I chose to marry Louis de l'Estorade rather than return to the convent. The seriousness of marriage terrified me, yet I realized friendship might be the key to a happy marriage. I accepted his love believing it would not be slavery but a reign.

We agreed to separate marriage from certain marital rights to preserve independence: a compact to keep my freedom while being his wife. I watched Louis to see if suffering had softened or hardened him. His love proved a passion, and I judged I could risk much. I proposed a friendship in which we would share life’s burdens; I asked to be left free, promising to attend to his feelings. He was overjoyed, and we married soon after.

I am nearly happy now, though I must conceal my full contentment from him, for he is sensitive. For three months we lived much as before; I studied our relationship and saw how my presence changed him. I believed I could bring new life to my husband before bearing him children. His happiness brightened our home; even my father became generous. I manage the household with economy—seeking pleasure without excess.

I harbor ambitious plans for Louis, hoping to see him in politics; he has embraced my designs, showing the depth of his affection. To conclude, here is a letter he wrote expressing his love: he sees me as a treasure, and I feel proud of our relationship.

XIV. THE DUC DE SORIA TO THE BARON DE MACUMER
Madrid.

My dear brother, I did not make you Duc de Soria to betray the name. I cannot tolerate your exile while we enjoy happiness. Marie and I will delay marrying until you accept the money Urraca offers. We have prayed for your contentment; I believe Heaven will send you the love you seek. Marie and I will always be grateful to you. Write through the Nuncio. Farewell; take pride in the happiness you have brought us.

XV. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO MME. DE L'ESTORADE
March.

Ah, my friend, marriage has made a philosopher of you! Your prudent calculations trouble me. Do you think you can convert me to matrimony by cautious arithmetic? We left Blois innocent, armed with meditation, and now your weapons have turned against you. If I did not know you were pure, I would suspect vice.

You have made a holocaust of your youth—becoming a miser before your time. If Louis loves you, he will never realize your actions spring from necessity. Love is the first principle of all virtues; I cannot accept justifying marriage by calculation. You act a man's part; Louis must play the woman!

Your letter plunged me into thought; I see now the convent cannot replace a mother. Please do not turn away from my cries!

Monday.

Last night I looked at the stars and pictured my Spaniard in a tree by my window. He blew a letter to me through the window, but I burned it unread—it felt improper to read such an offering. At the opera he seemed sad; I learned he had renounced a rich heiress for another man’s sake. I was wretched for burning his letter—what might he have confessed? To be loved in secret, to know a man is devoted—how can I resist?

When I returned home I reopened my window, hoping he might be there. He was, and I received another letter, which I read and cherished.

XVI. THE SAME TO THE SAME
March.

I am dressed in white, with camellias in my hair. I feel an odd desire to delay my decision and make him wait. What a vision—driving my barouche, seeing him, the thrill. I complimented him on his horse; he said it was a gift from his steward. Henarez is no longer just a simple man; he grows more distinguished. Do my love affairs amuse you? I am a little off my head and must stop.

XVII. MME. DE L'ESTORADE TO LOUISE DE CHAULIEU

My angel, you have grieved me. You do not realize the force of the word indissoluble in marriage. Each marriage has laws shaped by place and character: a wife of a leader must resign to his guidance; a wife of a fool must take control. You speak of vice, but passion can be admirable when it springs from the heart. We are answerable to God and ourselves for our happiness.

You will find a wife’s calculation necessary to maintain happiness. I persist in my principles, convinced virtue varies with surroundings. If I wished to make Louis unhappy, I would leave him the helm entirely.

I may not have married a man of rank, but I hope to further him toward it. Five years from now we will see if I was mistaken. You taught me what a woman sacrifices in marriage; regret is not remorse, but I feel the loss of freedom. You say marriage has made you a philosopher; I feel far from it. Farewell, my dear friend.

How can one fathom the human heart? My father should introduce me to M. Bonald; I would ask him. I envy God, who can read the heart’s undercurrents. Does Felipe still worship me? That is the question.

No, he is not lost. The constitutional minister is perfect as a lover. Here is his letter, in essence:

My dear, reading this, he rose before me—pale, humble, but full of a tenderness that was not the ordinary flourish of lovers. It was the breath of happiness.

The weather has been atrocious; impossible to go to the Bois without arousing suspicion. Even my mother, who often goes out regardless of rain, remains at home.

Wednesday evening.

I have just seen him at the Opera; he was another man. He came to our box, introduced by the Sardinian ambassador.

Having read in my eyes that his audacity was well taken, he seemed awkward and addressed the Marquise d’Espard as “mademoiselle.” A light brighter than the chandeliers flashed from his eyes. He left with the uncertain air of a man unsure of his next move.

“The Baron de Macumer is in love!” exclaimed Mme. de Maufrigneuse.

“Strange, isn’t it, for a fallen minister?” replied my mother.

Outwardly I kept my composure, regarding Mmes. de Maufrigneuse and d’Espard and my mother as if they spoke a foreign language; inwardly my soul rose in waves of intoxicating joy. There is only one word for what I felt: rapture. Such love as Felipe’s makes him worthy of mine. I am the very breath of his life; my hands guide his thoughts. I have a mad longing to see him clear every obstacle and come boldly for my hand. Then I would know whether this storm would calm at a glance from me.

Ah, my dear, I stopped here, and I am still trembling. As I wrote, I heard a noise outside and rose to look. From my window I saw him coming along the wall at the risk of his life. I went to the bedroom window and made him a sign; it was enough. He leaped from the wall—ten feet—and then ran along the road, showing me he was not hurt. That he thought of my fear moved me so much that I am still crying; I don’t know why. Poor ungainly man! What was he coming for? What had he to say to me?

I dare not write my thoughts and will go to bed joyful, imagining all we would say if together. Farewell, fair silent one. If love is life’s essence, why is it excluded from marriage? Society demands women sacrifice for family, breeding marital discord. Anticipating this, men built defenses against women; without them, theories that subordinate women could be overturned by love or aversion. Marriage now embodies opposing forces the law should reconcile. Your letter challenges the foundations laid by the sage of Aveyron, under which I once found comfort. Laws made by men accept conjugal love without passion, allowing women to yield without desire, reflecting Nature’s emphasis on procreation.

Once a person, I now feel like property. I have swallowed many tears alone, aching for a comforting smile. Why are our fates so different? You thrive in lawful love where virtue aligns with pleasure, while I face an uncertain future.

You are loved and adored. Embrace this noble romance we long dreamed of. Your beauty, refined and spiritual, was made to enchant. Guard your heart’s secret and test Felipe’s love—and, more importantly, test your own. Novelty and the illusion of happiness easily mislead.

You remain independent, dear friend; I urge you not to rush into marriage without assurance. In intimate conversation, a gesture or word can reveal hidden depths. You have the courage to explore where others falter.

I watch you anxiously from afar; my heart is in sync with yours. Write to me about everything; your letters ignite passion in my otherwise mundane life. I oscillate between despair and hope—perhaps expecting too much. In youth we often believe ideals and reality will align.

Seated beneath a rock in my park, I concluded that love in marriage is a fortunate accident, not a universal rule. The philosopher rightly views the family as society’s unit, placing women in subservience as history shows. The solution to our dilemma lies in motherhood, which I yearn for to channel my energy and heart.

Louis remains cheerful; his love is active while mine is passive. He enjoys the flowers of my affection without acknowledging the toil behind them. I play the devoted mother, sacrificing for his happiness, and that sacrifice gives me joy. I await the rewards of my efforts, hoping motherhood will fulfill my aspirations and compensate for what I surrender.

I have braved bulls and battles, scaled redoubts, staked life on dice. Yet before your sweet eye my courage deserts me; I tremble, shrink, and lose my nerve as a child with ghosts.

SECOND SONNET (essence)
I sought sleep to dream of thee, but jealous wakefulness kept me on the balcony. Heaven itself seemed robbed of color and light by thy beauty: the moon’s silver and the stars’ sparkle appear pale beside you.

What a gracious way to tell a young girl she fills a man’s life! Tell me what you think of this love, which lavishes earth and soul. Only in these ten days have I understood Spanish gallantry.

Ah, dear, what is happening at La Crampade? How often I stroll there, inspecting our crops! Do you have news of our mulberry trees and last winter's plantations? Does everything prosper as you wish? While buds open on our shrubs, have they also blossomed in the bosom of the wife? Does Louis continue his madrigals? Do you enter each other's thoughts? I wonder if your wedding peace is better than the raging torrent of my love! Has my sweet lady professor taken offense? If so, I would send Felipe post-haste to plead for my pardon—or her head. Sweet love, my life here is a splendid success, and I want to know how it fares with life in Provence. We have just added a Spaniard with the complexion of a Havana cigar to our family, and your congratulations still await.

Seriously, my sweet Renée, I am anxious. I fear you may be eating your heart out in silence, afraid to cast a gloom over my sunshine. Write to me at once, naughty child! Tell me your life in every detail; tell me whether you still hold back, whether your "independence" stands erect, or has fallen on its knees, or is sitting comfortably, which would indeed be serious. Can you suppose the incidents of your married life are without interest for me? I muse over all you have said. Often, at the Opera, while absorbed in watching the dancers, I think, "It is half-past nine; perhaps she is in bed. What is she doing? Is she happy? Is she alone with her independence, or has it gone the way of other dead and cast-off independences?"

A thousand loves.

---

Saucy girl! Why should I write? What could I say? While your life is varied by social festivities and the passions of love, mine is as monotonous as a convent.

We go to bed at nine and rise with daybreak. Meals are served with maddening punctuality. Nothing ever happens. I have adjusted to this routine; perhaps such fixed laws give the world stability.

I have set rules of dress, which occupy my mornings. I feel it is my duty as a wife to look charming, and it pleases both Louis and the good old man. After lunch we walk; when newspapers arrive I disappear to manage household affairs or read—often writing to you. I return before dinner; after, we play cards or receive visits. Thus my days pass between a contented old man, who has done with passions, and the man who owes his happiness to me. Louis’ joy warms my heart.

For women, happiness cannot consist solely in satisfying desire. In the evenings, when I can relax, I imagine your life, its riches and passions, and wonder what such a stormy preface can lead to. May I not swallow the book itself? For you, my darling, love's illusions are possible; for me, only the facts of homely life remain. Your love seems a dream!

I find it hard to understand why you insist on romanticizing it. Your ideal man must have more soul than fire, more nobility and self-command than passion. You demand sacrifices to be rewarded by them, testing Felipe to see if desire, hope, and curiosity endure. But behind your stage scenery lies the altar where everlasting bonds are forged. The day after your marriage, the graceful structure raised by your strategy may fall before the reality that transforms a girl into a woman, a gallant into a husband. There is no exemption for lovers; for them as for ordinary folk like Louis and me, the great "Perhaps" of Rabelais lurks beneath wedding rejoicings.

I do not blame you for your rashness in speaking with Felipe in the garden or spending a night with him; but you play with life, and I fear it may turn the tables. I dare not give you the counsel my experience suggests, but let me repeat from my valley that the viaticum of married life lies in resignation and self-sacrifice. Despite your tests, I see that marriage will mean to you what it has meant to me. The greater the passion, the steeper the precipice we have hewn for our fall—that is the only difference.

Oh, how I wish to see the Baron de Macumer and talk with him! Your happiness lies so near my heart.

---

As Felipe has acknowledged my fortune, my mother has become friendlier, calling me sly-boots and a woman of the world.

"But, dear mama," I said before the contract was signed, "you attribute cunning to what is truly the outcome of the most unselfish love! I assure you I am not the 'woman of the world' you think."

"Come, Armande," she said, drawing me close, "you did not want to return to the convent or die an old maid. Like a noble Chaulieu, you recognized the need to build up your father's family."

I glanced at my father and said, "I love Felipe de Soria with all my soul. Though this love sprang up without my knowledge, I swear I never yielded until I recognized in him a character worthy of mine."

"But, my dear," she interrupted, "he is as ugly as..."

"As anything you like," I retorted, "but I love his ugliness."

"If you love him, Armande," my father said, "and can master your love, do not risk your happiness. Happiness in marriage depends largely on the first days—"

"Days only?" my mother interrupted. "You had better leave us to talk."

"You are to be married in three days," the Duchess began, "so I must give you the serious advice every mother owes her daughter. You are marrying a man you love, and there is no reason for pity. I have only known you for a year, but it is long enough to love you. You have gratified maternal pride and shown yourself a sweet daughter. I promise you will always find a staunch friend in me."

"You smile? Alas! it often happens that a mother who has lived well with her daughter as a girl comes to cross purposes with her as women."

"I want your happiness, so listen. The love you feel is that of a young girl, natural to us all, for it is a woman's destiny to cling to a man. Unfortunately, there is but one man for a woman! Sometimes fate’s choice is not ours. If we cannot love the man family chooses, the fault may lie in both or circumstances. Yet there is no reason the man chosen by family should not be the one we can love. The barriers that arise between husband and wife often stem from a lack of perseverance on both sides. Transforming a husband into a lover is as delicate as making a lover into a husband, which you have just proved marvelously successful.

"Your happiness is my object. Never forget that the first three months of marriage may bring misery if you do not submit to the yoke with the same forbearance, tenderness, and intelligence you showed during courtship. You have indulged in innocent pleasures of clandestine love. If the culmination of your love begins with disappointment, come and tell me. Do not hope for too much from marriage; it may bring more discomfort than joy. The happiness of your life requires as much patient cherishing as the early shoots of love.

"If you lose the lover, you will find in his place the father of your children. This is the secret of social life. Sacrifice everything to the man whose name you bear, whose honor cannot suffer without involving you in frightful consequences. Such sacrifice is not only a duty for women of our rank; it is also wise policy. This is the mark of great moral principles—they hold good from every aspect. But I need say no more.

"I fancy you are jealous, and if you knew how jealous I am! But do not be foolish about it. To publish your jealousy is like playing politics with your cards on the table; those who let their game be seen learn nothing of their opponents. Whatever happens, we must know how to suffer in silence."

She added that she intended to have a serious talk with Macumer the evening before the wedding.

I kissed her hand, dropping a tear, moved by her words. In her advice I saw high principles and tenderness, and she understood my character. Her words summed up the lessons life had taught her.

"Dear little girl, you've got a nasty crossing before you. Most women, in their ignorance or disenchantment, are as wise as the Earl of Westmoreland!" We laughed at the joke about a gentleman who feared crossing the Alps.

You will understand, Renée, that your gloomy philosophy and my mother's lecture revived fears that had disturbed us at Blois. As marriage approached, I needed all my strength and affection to face the passage from maidenhood to womanhood. All our conversations returned, and I re-read your letters, feeling a vague sadness.

This anxiety had one advantage: it helped me adopt the proper expression for a bride. Consequently, on the day of signing the contract everyone said I looked charming and quite right. This morning at the Mairie it was informal, with only witnesses present.

I write this tail to my letter while they prepare my dress for dinner. We shall be married at midnight at the Church of Sainte-Valere, after a gay evening. I confess my fears give me a martyr-like air to which I have no right, but which will be admired—I cannot conceive why. I am delighted to see that poor Felipe is as timid as I; society grates on him, and he feels like a bat in a glass shop.

"Thank Heaven, the day won't last forever!" he whispered innocently.

In his bashfulness he wished for no one there.

The Sardinian ambassador presented me with a pearl necklace linked with six splendid diamonds—a gift from my sister-in-law, the Duchess de Soria. Along with it was a sapphire bracelet engraved with the words, "Though unknown, beloved." I could not accept these without consulting Felipe.

"For," I said, "I would not want you to wear ornaments from anyone but me."

He kissed my hand, moved, and replied, "Wear them for the inscription and the sincere feeling."

Here, then, my poor Renée, are the last words of your girl friend. After the midnight Mass we set off for an estate Felipe bought in Nivernais, on the way to Provence. My name is now Louise de Macumer, but I leave Paris in a few hours as Louise de Chaulieu. However I am called, there will always be but one Louise for you.

I have not written to you, dear, since our marriage nearly eight months ago, and not a line from you! You are inexcusable.

We set off in a post-chaise for the Castle of Chantepleurs, which Macumer has bought in Nivernais on the Loire, sixty leagues from Paris. Our servants, except my maid, were there before us; we arrived the next evening after a rapid journey. I slept from Paris to beyond Montargis, my head on Felipe's shoulder. His tenderness moved me; he kissed my forehead twice and looked at me with unchanging passion.

At Briare we breakfasted in the carriage, talking like in our old Blois days and admiring the Loire. By half-past seven we entered the beautiful avenue to Chantepleurs, dined at eight, and retired to a charming Gothic bedroom made comfortable with modern luxuries. Felipe, often thought ugly, seemed beautiful to me in his kindness. He felt like an old friend, though he later confessed to storms of passion beneath a calm exterior.

I told him there was nothing terrible in marriage; he asked why fear it, reminding me he had never been false to his promise. His words and glances mastered me. I thought of duty and the unfair constraint placed on a woman's delicate feelings: what force seeks to crush a woman's sensitivity under obligation? Love should guard its rights and bloom freely under its warmth.

Oh, my noble Renée! I understand you now. The woman who has not used marriage to legalize her heart's choice has only motherhood left to turn to. When earthly ties fail, the soul seeks the divine.

A hard truth: only truly great men know how to love. They balance sensual and spiritual forces; weaker men confuse them. Great souls express instinct with grace, tempering passion with reverence. The depth of feeling follows the caliber of intellect; hence only men of genius possess a woman's delicacy. When mind, heart, and senses unite in rapture, we briefly soar to heaven.

This was the philosophy of my first three months of marriage. Felipe is angelic, another self; I can think aloud with him. His greatness surpasses my understanding, and our bond deepens with possession. I foresee years of marriage will only strengthen his affection and our connection. What joy!

Pleasure invigorates me; its sunshine becomes part of my being. The intervals between ecstasies are like short nights between long summer days. What happy chance has given me such a destiny? My mother's fears proved unfounded; your fears and mine dissolved.

We spent seven and a half months at Chantepleurs like runaway lovers in blissful solitude. One morning, in particular happiness, I reflected on my life and thought of your prosaic marriage. I grasped the meaning of your life. Oh, my sweet, why do we speak different languages? Your marriage of convenience and my love match are worlds apart. You walk the earth, while I soar in the heavens. Love crowned me queen; you reign by duty. My heights are so lofty that a fall would shatter me.

But I will not dwell on that. I hesitate to describe the vibrant joy of love's springtime as we know it.

For ten days we have been in Paris, staying in a lovely house on the Rue du Bac, prepared by the architect who decorated Chantepleurs. I have been listening to Rossini's divine music, which once soothed my restless heart. They say I am more beautiful now, and I take childish pleasure in being called "Madame."

Friday morning.

Renée, my dear, my happiness draws me back to you. I feel I can be more to you than ever. I have studied your married life alongside my own, and you emerge as great, noble, and splendid in your goodness. I declare myself your humble admirer and friend. My marriage has been a joy; I would have perished had it been otherwise. And you live! What feeds your heart? I will never mock you again. Mockery comes from ignorance; we jest at what we do not understand. "Recruits laugh where veterans are grave," as the Comte de Chaulieu said.

I suspect you have not told me everything. You hide wounds; I believe you suffer. From afar I wove romantic theories: perhaps you merely experimented with marriage, and what brought me happiness brought you pain. Your sacrifice seems unrewarded, wrapped in social axioms. If self-denial is noble, what of my joy, sanctified by church and mayor? Is it unnatural?

For the honor of the law and to complete my happiness, I long to see you content. Oh, tell me you see a dawn of love for Louis, who adores you! I return to this midday blaze, fearing it may consume me.

Dear Renée, do you remember how you once said, "I love you so, Louise, that if God appeared to me, I would pray for all your sorrows to be mine"? I take up your prayer, imploring Heaven to grant you a share in my happiness.

I have an idea. I suspect you are nurturing plans for Louis de l'Estorade. Very well; help him get elected deputy in the upcoming election. He will be nearly forty then, and the Chamber won't meet for six months, so he will qualify for a seat. You will come to Paris—wouldn't that be enough? My father and my friends will know and admire you. If your father-in-law consents to a family, we could secure the title of Comte for Louis. That would be something! And we shall be together.

My dear Louise, your letter left me dizzy with joy and a little envy. I read it at sunset beneath a barren rock overlooking the Mediterranean—Louise’s seat—a symbol of my solitude that still reminds me I am not alone.

It stirred the child within me: a strange blend of warning, delight, pain, promise and fulfillment that convinces me I shall one day know laughter and love. I feel a new purpose: to bring forth life. The certainty of motherhood has turned my sacrifices into a deep joy surpassing any personal passion.

For you, love reflects Felipe’s ardor; my love will return in my children. Your golden harvest may fade, but my delayed motherhood feels enduring and renewing. Love may be Nature’s gem, but motherhood is Nature’s purest joy. A single smile dried my tears; while love makes Louis happy, marriage made me a mother, and in that I find happiness.

I returned to my white grange to sit with these thoughts. For five months the miracle within me has unfolded, yet I remain emotionally detached. My family is excited; I feel cold and hesitate to confess that motherhood is still abstract. Louis’s surprise shows how little the father’s role seems to matter. My doctor says childbirth is governed by chance; children of passionate love may be luckier. Perhaps you will feel joys I never will, since your love for Felipe differs from my sense of duty.

These reflections come with nausea and odd cravings: while Louis brings fine oranges, I crave the half-rotten ones and find them strengthening. I will stay on the grounds to avoid being seen. I wonder when the true sense of motherhood will begin and hope it won’t arrive with suffering. Farewell, my fortunate friend—your love story inspires me; pray and do good. Religion has grown dearer since my marriage. God bless you.

XXX. LOUISE DE MACUMER TO RENEE DE L'ESTORADE
January 1826.

Macumer woke me with your husband's letter. Yes, we’ll come to Chantepleurs at the end of April. I look forward to the journey, to seeing you, and to being godmother to your first child. I insist on Macumer as godfather; sharing the role would be unbearable—if you had seen his look, you’d know how dearly he cherishes me.

I long to visit La Crampade; I may have a child there too. Can you picture me torn between you and an infant? If anything ever supplanted my place in your heart, I hardly know how I’d bear it—Medea’s anger seems less extreme now!

So: you have the fruit without the flowers, I the flowers without the fruit. Our lives contrast, yet we have the philosophy to endure. Ten months married is too soon to give up hope.

We live happily, as happy people do. Society now calls the Baronne de Macumer prettier than Louise de Chaulieu—love is a wonderful cosmetic. Driving along the Champs-Élysées this January I reflected on how much has changed since last year.

If I lack a mother’s joys, you can share them with me and I will learn by sympathy. Still, I cannot imagine anything surpassing the rapture of love; often I think it would be better to die at thirty, preserved from later disappointment, than to carry a young heart in an aged body among cold faces. Felipe and I quarreled about this: I wanted him to have the strength to let me slip away at thirty so I might pass from one dream to another; he refused and turned pale. This distinguished statesman can be surprisingly childlike. Now that we share such confidences, we often surprise each other.

Felipe and I would like to send you a gift, dear mother—something to bring you joy and remind you of us. Tell me what you would like. I thought of a luncheon service with baby figures, since lunch is our happiest meal. Tell me soon; we must order it.

Farewell, dear nursing mother. May you enjoy all the delights of motherhood. I eagerly await your first letter describing your experiences. Some parts of your husband’s letter moved me; I will tell my godson how dearly he must love you. Much love, my sweet one.

XXXI. RENEE DE L'ESTORADE TO LOUISE DE MACUMER

It has been nearly five months since the baby was born, and I have found no moment to write. When you become a mother you will understand. Please write more often! Share your joys; I am happier than you can imagine.

I went to the parish church for the Mass of recovery from childbirth, supported by both grandfathers. Kneeling before God, overwhelmed with gratitude, the image that stays with me is the Virgin on the altar seeming to bend and point to the infant Christ, who smiled at me. With a heart full of love, I held little Armand for the priest to bless, looking forward to his baptism.

How sweet the word "child" is! In the last two months of pregnancy I wandered through the gardens, scarcely conscious of the burden I carried. Gloomy forebodings haunted me; my heart did not respond to the thought of the little one, though he reminded me with lively kicks. When labor came, I summoned all my strength and bore the agony; I sank into a stupor, feeling like two beings: my body in pain and my soul at peace. Pain formed a halo; at its climax I screamed, and then the newborn's cries silenced my suffering.

They laid me back, and it felt like paradise despite exhaustion. Happy faces pointed to the child, and I cried out in terror, "It looks like a little monkey! Are you sure it's a child?" My mother, now my nurse, reassured me: "You've got the finest baby in the world. Don't worry; be a mother." I slept and left things to nature.

Waking from those first days' pain was heavenly. A ray of light pierced the darkness, and my heart opened like a flower. The moment the little one took my breast was pure joy—this felt like the dawn of motherhood. Nursing is a miracle of transformation. The cries go straight to your heart; the child's eyes and lips express everything. What else matters? The father could be forgiven nothing that disturbed the baby. The bond between mother and child is profound; the joy of nurturing is unparalleled.

At five months my little one has become the prettiest darling: he cries little and is content in our love. I strive to envelop him in affection.

My feelings for Louis have changed into a tender fondness, perhaps surpassing romantic love: unselfish gratitude that lights the future and recalls fond memories. The old L'Estorade and his son have redoubled their devotion; each look from them brings me joy. Louis is ecstatic at having a son—he watches him sleep for hours—and the fear of childlessness has given way to renewed purpose; he works harder, driven by fatherhood.

I grow happier by the hour. The bond between mother and child strengthens continually. This love is natural and enduring, unlike romantic passion, which can wane; a mother's love grows with the child's needs and combines passion, duty, and joy. Here a woman's true sphere unfolds, where self-sacrifice flourishes without jealousy.

Society reinforces nature's instincts: a mother cherishes the child who opens these delights and teaches her the art of motherhood. A mother learns from her child, and her character deepens through these duties and pleasures; a woman without children is incomplete.

Hasten, my sweetest, to fulfill your mission. Your happiness will multiply with mine.

23rd.

I had to leave you because my godson's cry reached me from the garden. I wanted to say farewell. Rereading this letter, I am surprised at how ordinary my feelings sound; yet every mother must have felt this way. I am as happy now as I once was miserable. This place is my land of promise—the desert is past. A thousand loves, darling. Write to me, for I can now read about your happy love without tears. Farewell.

Do you know it’s been over three months since I last wrote? I failed to answer your last letter—will you forgive me? Macumer and I took your silence as consent about the baby-wreathed luncheon service; it’s now bound for Marseilles after six months’ work. When Felipe asked me to inspect it before packing, I realized we hadn’t exchanged a word since the letter that revealed the heart of a mother.

I blame dreadful Paris—what is your excuse? Society here is a whirlpool. Didn’t I tell you one must conform? It drives out sentiment, monopolizes time, and can consume the heart. Moliere’s Celimene is the epitome of the society woman—timeless and apt. Without my breastplate—my love for Felipe—I should be lost. I told him this morning he is my salvation. Evenings are full of parties; nights my heart heals in passionate love.

I dine at home only with friends; I spend one afternoon a week in the house—Wednesday. I have entered the social fray, competing with Mmes. d’Espard and de Maufrigneuse; my house is lively. I became fashionable for Felipe’s pleasure. My mornings are his; from four in the afternoon until two in the morning, I belong to Paris. Macumer is an admirable host—witty, dignified, courteous. No woman could help loving such a husband.

My parents have gone to Madrid. With Louis XVIII gone, the Duchess obtained my father’s appointment as attaché. My brother, the Duc de Rhetore, now acknowledges me. My younger brother, the Comte de Chaulieu, owes me: I spent my fortune to secure him an estate and a marriage with Mlle. de Mortsauf, an heiress expected to inherit over a hundred thousand livres a year. The King will confer the de Lenoncourt and de Givry titles on him to preserve their legacy; my father insisted the de Chaulieu arms be placed on the de Lenoncourt escutcheon, making my brother Duc de Lenoncourt. The young de Mortsauf is gravely ill, so the marriage waits until next winter, after mourning.

My father’s plan has justified itself, earning me compliments and softening explanations of my marriage to society. The Prince de Talleyrand, kindly to my grandmother, is now warm to Macumer. Society that once criticized me now admires me. I now reign where I was once insignificant. Macumer is envied as the husband of “the most charming woman in Paris.” Many women vie for that title; men murmur and gaze. This adulation flatters vanity—I see why women pay dearly for such fleeting privileges. This triumph is intoxicating; I understand how women become selfish and frivolous under admiration. Society’s demands consume your spirit; you give yourself for smiles and jealousy and receive only flattery. You know the cost, yet can’t escape the vortex.

Ah, how I crave a true friend! Felipe’s love and devotion are my treasure, and my heart longs for you. We are joyfully preparing to move to Chantepleurs, where we can escape the farce of Paris. After rereading your letter, I realize no fashionable woman in Paris can be a good mother.

Goodbye for now, dear. We will be at Chantepleurs for only a week and hope to see you around May 10th. We will meet again after more than two years! How we have changed—we are both matrons now, you in motherhood, I in love.

If I haven’t written, it isn’t because I’ve forgotten you. How is my little godson? He must be over nine months now; I long to see his first steps, though Macumer says even precocious infants don’t walk at ten months. We will have delightful gossip and see if a child truly spoils a pattern. P.S. If you reply, address it to Chantepleurs. I’m just leaving.

My child, if you become a mother you’ll find it impossible to write during the first two months of nursing. Mary, my English nurse, and I are exhausted. I resolved to do everything myself—sewing baby clothes, embroidering caps. I am a slave, day and night.

Master Armand-Louis eats whenever he pleases, and I must constantly change, wash, and dress him. I love watching him sleep, singing to him, and carrying him about, leaving little time for myself. Society has been to you what motherhood is to me!

I cannot express how rich my life has become, and I long for your visit to see for yourself. I fear he will soon be teething, and you may find a fussy baby. So far he hasn’t cried much because I’m always attentive. Babies cry when their needs aren’t met, and I’m vigilant. My heart has expanded while yours shrinks under society’s demands. I eagerly await your arrival, wanting your thoughts on l’Estorade just as you’re curious about Macumer.

Write to me from your last stop. The gentlemen want to meet our distinguished guests. Come, Queen of Paris, to our humble home, where love will greet you!

The name on this letter shows my petition has been granted: your father-in-law is now Comte de l’Estorade. I wouldn’t leave Paris until I secured this for you; I’m writing in the Keeper of the Seals’ presence—he has just told me the patent is signed. Goodbye for now.

I am ashamed that my sudden departure will surprise you. But I must be honest: I am horribly jealous.

Felipe’s eyes rested too often on you. Your little talks at the foot of your rock tortured me, and I became irritable. Your beauty reminded him of his homeland and Marie Heredia; I can be jealous of the past too. Your magnificent features stabbed me in the heart. I tried to enhance my appearance, but everything paled beside you.

Felipe doted on the child, whom I began to resent. Yes, I confess—I wanted that exuberance of life for myself. I saw regret in Macumer’s eyes, and I wept for two nights. I was miserable in your home. You are too beautiful and triumphant for me to endure.

You complained of your lot, but L’Estorade is presentable, charming, and likely to become a deputy. His exile has given him a calm, dignified air, useful for a politician. Macumer, too, should achieve high place. Having satisfied myself of your happiness, I went to Chantepleurs, where Felipe must pursue his aspirations. I won’t receive you there without a fine baby to match yours.

I know I deserve your scorn. I am a fool, a wretch—jealousy is a poor virtue. I am not angry with you, but I was miserable; you will forgive my escape. Two more days and I might have made a scene. Despite my jealousy, I am glad to have seen you in the pride of motherhood. Even in Marseilles I feel proud of you as a splendid mother.

You are born for motherhood; I am not. A good mother and a passionate wife both require intelligence and grace. I watched you and admired you. Your children will be happy, not spoiled, by your tenderness and reason.

Tell Louis the truth about my departure, but find a decent excuse for your father-in-law and family. Felipe doesn’t know why I left, and he never will. If he asks, I’ll fabricate a pretext—perhaps that you were jealous of me. Forgive my little fib. Kisses to my dear godson. Come to Chantepleurs in October: I’ll be alone while Macumer is in Sardinia planning estate improvements. That seems to calm him; sharing plans with me unsettles him. Goodbye!

Dear sister, we were astonished at lunch to learn you had left, especially after your letter. You were happy; there was no reason for such objections. My sentence: you return at my first summons. Your letter didn’t say your next stop, so I address this to Chantepleurs.

Listen, dear sister of my heart. My focus is your happiness. Your husband commands respect—not only a grave exterior but a revealed power and a fire in his eyes. He has been Prime Minister and adores you, which makes him a profound dissembler. Extracting secrets from such a diplomatic soul requires skill.

You know my role is reason while yours is imagination. I am a simple country vicountess, ambitious for family prosperity. Macumer, the Duc de Soria, has a name, and you, a duchess by right, reign in Paris, where it’s hard to maintain power. You have a considerable fortune, which may double if Macumer develops his Sardinian estates. If anyone has the right to be jealous, it’s not you. Thankfully, we both have generous hearts that place our friendship above pettiness.

I know you must feel ashamed of fleeing, but your flight won’t spare you from my words. Read carefully; this concerns you more than Macumer.

Firstly, you do not love him as a wife should. In two years you may tire of adoration. You will never see Felipe as a husband; he will always be a lover to you. You do not revere him as a woman should her god. I have studied love, and this is not love.

You cannot escape the fate of all queens. One day you will long to be treated as a light-o’-love—to be swept off your feet by a strong man who won’t worship you. Macumer loves you too much to resist or criticize. Sooner or later you will scorn this excessive devotion. He spoils you, as I spoiled you at the convent; you are enchanting, and there’s no escaping your charms.

Worse, you are candid; our happiness often relies on social hypocrisies you won’t embrace. Society won’t tolerate a wife’s power over her husband. Convention demands that a man not show himself as lover to his wife, nor may a married woman act as a mistress. You both disregard this.

The unpardonable sin in society is to be happy; if happiness exists, it must remain hidden. More importantly, equality between lovers shouldn't appear in marriage, or it undermines social order. It is painful to see a man become a nonentity, and worse to see a capable man reduced to that. You will reduce Macumer to a shadow; your household will lack balance. You will regret it, but by then it may be too late. Women often lack the qualities essential to family life. Macumer senses this; his Sardinia visit suggests he hopes to regain himself.

You use the power his love gives you. Your position shows in your gestures and tone. You are the wanton your mother called you! Have you ever heard me contradict Louis? I respect his public authority. If I want to advise him, I wait for private moments; I never assume the role of mentor. If I didn’t present myself as submissive publicly, he would lose confidence. The good we do for others is spoiled unless we erase ourselves so they feel no inferiority. There is sweetness in these hidden sacrifices; it is a triumph when you praise Louis.

This is my counsel. Right now, you love Felipe not as a husband but as a reflection of yourself. Your father’s words ring true: beneath your passion lies a selfishness typical of a great lady. How I must love you to speak such bitter truths!

If you promise not to tell the Baron, I’ll share the end of our talk. We praised you, and he soon sensed my sisterly love. I ventured, “Louise has never struggled with life; fortune has spoiled her, and she might pay for this if you weren’t there to be father and lover.”

“Ah! but is it possible?” he broke off—enough for me. If you had stayed, he would have spoken more freely.

Think of the day when your husband’s strength wanes, when pleasure turns to satiety, and he feels diminished before you. The stings of conscience will awaken remorse, painful for you, as you’ll feel responsible and end up despising the man you failed to respect. Remember, scorn can become hatred. You are too noble to forget Felipe’s sacrifices, but what more can he give when he has already given himself fully? Woe to those who leave no desire unsatisfied—women can be insatiable in love.

Oh, Louise, change while there’s still time. If you treated Macumer as I treat l’Estorade, you could awaken the hero within him. Wouldn’t you feel pride in using your power to elevate a gifted man, as I do with one of lesser stature?

Had you stayed I would have told you this in person, but I know you will read and weigh my warnings. You have everything to be happy; don’t spoil your chances. Return to Paris as soon as Macumer does. The demands of society, which I complain about, are necessary; otherwise you’ll be lost in self-absorption. A married woman shouldn't be too generous with herself; a mother should reserve sacred hours for herself—children should not take her presence for granted.

Farewell, my dear jealous soul! Many women would be flattered by your pangs, but I can only mourn, for what is not mother in me is your dear friend. A thousand loves. Make whatever excuse you must for leaving; if you doubt Macumer, I am sure of Louis.

Your dreadful letter reached me, forwarded by my steward as I asked. Oh, Renée — I will spare you unnecessary complaint and tell you only what it produced.

We had just returned from an ambassador’s reception where I shone and Macumer was utterly captivated. Then I read your harsh reply to my letter and sobbed. My dear Arab flung himself at my feet, calling you mad. He led me to our balcony; speaking Italian, his declarations were like poetry. He swore that even if your predictions were true, he would not trade one moment of our love for a lifetime of convention—he would choose our love and death over a dull existence with children.

While he whispered, a bat’s cry startled us and I fainted. I am better this morning, though that sound haunts me. Upon waking I knelt before Felipe, looked into his eyes, and confessed: “My love, I may be naive, and Renée might be right. Perhaps I love only your love, but this is the only feeling in my heart. If anything about me disturbs you, tell me. I want to follow your wishes.” Macumer was too choked with emotion to answer.

I thank you, Renée; your letter revealed the depths of Macumer’s love. Rome is the city of love, where passion should flourish alongside art and faith.

In Venice we shall meet the Duc and Duchesse de Soria. If you write, address Paris; we leave Rome in three days after the ambassador’s farewell.

P.S. Dear, naive child, your letter shows you know love only in theory. Love is a force that yields diverse fruits beyond any theory’s grasp.

My father has been elected to the Chamber, my father-in-law has died, and I am near my second confinement; these mark the year’s end. I mention them first to spare you the fright of the black seal.

Your letter from Rome chilled me. You and Felipe behave like children. Either he is a deceitful diplomat, or his love is like a man’s for a courtesan—lavishing affection while knowing she is unfaithful. Enough. You say I rave, so I will hold my tongue: from our differing fates I draw a harsh lesson—do not love if you wish to be loved.

When Louis joined the provincial Council he received the Legion of Honor cross. Nearly three years on, my father asks the rank of Officer for his son-in-law. I would value your influence with the right person, but do not entangle yourself in my father’s affairs. The Comte de Maucombe seeks a marquisate for himself; reserve efforts for me. When Louis becomes a deputy next winter, we will be in Paris to secure him a government post so we may live on his salary. My father sits between center and right; a title will satisfy him. Our family was noble even under King René, and Charles X will likely grant a Maucombe title. My fear is my father using this to favor my younger brother—if a marquisate is dangled, he will focus on himself.

January 15th.

Ah, Louise, I have been through hell. I tell you because you are like a sister; still, I doubt I shall ever relive those five ghastly days. The word “convulsions” sickens me. Five days felt like five centuries. A mother who has not suffered this cannot comprehend it.

The night before, the weather was close; I thought Armand affected by teething. Usually sweet, he grew peevish, crying and breaking his toys. I put him to bed beside me. Toward morning he cried “Mamma!” in a strange voice and twitched. I rose for a drink; as I handed him the cup he was motionless, repeating “Mamma!” in a voice hardly a voice. His hand was stiff. He gulped water convulsively, clung to me, eyes rolling back, limbs rigid. I screamed for Louis.

“A doctor! Quick!... he is dying,” I cried.

Louis vanished. Armand gasped “Mamma!” then lost consciousness. Veins on his forehead swelled, and convulsions began. For an hour before doctors arrived I held my lifeless baby—my pride and joy. His eyes! I cannot think of them without horror. My pretty Armand was a mere shell—black, shriveled.

Two doctors from Marseilles hovered like birds of ill omen. One blamed brain fever, the other called them ordinary convulsions. Our country doctor offered no opinion. “Teething,” said one; “fever,” said the other. They agreed on leeches to the neck and ice to the head. To see a child like that—purple, silent—was horrible. They even cut his curls for the ice. The convulsions returned every ten minutes; the child writhed, white then violet, limbs clattering like wood. This unconscious flesh had smiled and said “Mamma!”—the thought pierced my soul.

After four days and nights, the doctors recommended a dreadful ointment to create open sores. Sores on my Armand! Five days earlier he had been playing and laughing. I refused, trusting nature. Louis, who believes doctors, scolded me. But in those moments when the disease mimicked death, I felt that remedy might save him. His skin was dry and rough; the ointment would not work. I wept; the doctors were at dinner!

Alone with him, I stripped off the medical appliances, pressed him to my bosom, and laid my forehead against his, begging God to spare him. For minutes I held him, longing to die with him. Then his limbs relaxed; the writhings ceased; the ghastly colors faded. I screamed; the doctors rushed in.

“He is saved!” cried the oldest doctor.

What music those words were! Two hours later Armand returned to life. I was crushed, saved only by the joy of his recovery. What tortures bind a mother to her child! Must the nails be driven to fasten him to my heart? Was I not mother enough? I who wept at his first words and steps—why these horrors?

Now Armand plays and laughs. What causes this dreadful disease in children? I ponder it, especially as I am again with child. Is it teething? A brain disorder? A nervous susceptibility? Our country doctor blames nervous trouble from teething. I would give every tooth in my head to see Armand’s through. One white pearl peeking from swollen gums brings a cold sweat. The heroism with which my angel bore suffering proves he is his mother’s son. A look from him pierces me.

Medical science offers no clear explanation for this tetanus that appears and vanishes swiftly, unpreventable and uncured. One thing is certain: it is hell for a mother to see her child in convulsions. How passionately I hold him now! I could walk forever with him in my arms!

To have endured this six weeks before my confinement made it worse; I feared for the coming child. Farewell, my beloved. Do not wish for a child—this is the essence of my letter!

Poor dear, Macumer and I forgave you upon hearing of your terrible trouble. I felt pain reading of your double agony; there seem compensations in being childless.

I write to tell you Louis has been promoted; he may now wear the ribbon of an Officer of the Legion. You are fortunate, Renée—likely to have a little girl, as you wished!

My brother’s marriage to Mlle. de Mortsauf was celebrated on our return. Our gracious King, who is extraordinarily kind, has granted my brother the reversion of the post of first gentleman of the chamber, presently held by his father-in-law, on condition the Mortsauf scutcheon be placed alongside the Lenoncourt’s.

“The office ought to go with the title,” he told the Duc de Lenoncourt-Givry.

My father is justified a hundredfold. Without my fortune none of this would have happened. My parents came from Madrid for the wedding and will return after the reception I am hosting for the newlyweds.

The carnival will be lively. The Duc and Duchesse de Soria are in Paris, which unnerves me. Marie Heredia is one of Europe’s most beautiful women, and I dislike the way Felipe looks at her. Therefore, I am extra sweet and affectionate. Every look and gesture says what my lips do not: “She could not love like this!” Heaven knows how lovely I am! Yesterday Mme. de Maufrigneuse said, “Dear child, who can compete with you?”

I keep Felipe entertained, making his sister-in-law seem dull by comparison. I am less sorry that a little Abencerrage is not on the way, since the Duchess will likely remain in Paris for her confinement and lose her beauty. If the baby is a boy, he will be named Felipe in honor of the exile. Fate decrees I shall be godmother again.

Goodbye, dear. I plan to go to Chantepleurs early this year: our Italian tour was shockingly expensive. I will leave around the end of March to economize in Nivenais. Besides, I am tired of Paris. Felipe and I long for the tranquility of the park, cool meadows, and the sparkling Loire. Chantepleurs will be delightful after Italy; splendor becomes tiresome, and a lover’s gaze is more beautiful than any opera or painting!

We expect you there. Do not fear my jealousy. You are free to test Macumer’s heart and expose his doubts. I am utterly indifferent. Since that day in Rome Felipe’s love for me has deepened. He just told me (looking over my shoulder) that his sister-in-law, Princess Heredia, his childhood dream, has no brains. Oh, my dear, I am worse than a ballet dancer! If you knew the joy that remark brought me! I pointed out her faulty French—she says “esemple” for “exemple,” “sain” for “cinq,” “cheu” for “je.” She is beautiful but lacks charm and wit. When praised she seems puzzled. Felipe could not have lived two months with Marie after marriage. Don Fernand, the Duc de Soria, suits her perfectly: generous but a spoiled child. I am tempted to be mischievous and make you laugh, but I will refrain. Much love, darling.

My dear Renée,

For the first time I am truly alone and in tears. I sat beneath a willow by the Chantepleurs marsh—pretty yet desolate without the sound of children. Nearly three years of marriage and still no child; your household full of little ones makes me examine my heart. I would brave any suffering, even what you endured at my godson’s birth, to hold a cherub of my own—an Athenais to call mine! I picture her looks and sweetness, yet you tell me nothing—just like you, Renée. I am sure you share my distress.

Each disappointment plunges me into melancholy, from which I compose mournful elegies. When will I embroider caps and sew lace for a tiny head? Will I never hear “Mamma” or have a little despot tug at my dress? My gravel may stay unmarked by carriage wheels, my courtyard free of broken toys. I long to watch a life unfold—another Felipe, only dearer; a son to be my second self. My park and château seem cold and empty. A woman without children feels unnatural; we exist to be mothers. Barrenness is a bleak fate. If I cannot be a mother, I will at least indulge in romantic sorrow.

I told my Moor of this, and he wept; he cannot bear jokes on his love. Sometimes I dream of pilgrimage to carry my longings to some shrine. Next winter I shall seek a physician. I am too angry with myself to write more. Farewell.

Your silence and seclusion in the country while your husband gains Parliamentary honors alarm me. His eloquence has secured favor, and he will likely receive a fine post when the session ends. Why did you not come to Paris? I could have enjoyed your company for four months. Louis told me you would fetch him and have your third confinement in Paris—what a monstrous Gigogne of a mother! My dear friend, have you forgotten my two little brown heads, my subjects and tyrants? I could not drag a four‑year‑old and an almost three‑year‑old to Paris while expecting again. I would not trouble you or disturb your husband with such tumult. I detest hotel life. When I come to Paris for the session it will be to my own house: Louis’s uncle, pleased with his grand‑nephew’s prospects, gave me two hundred thousand francs to buy a house near you, and my mother sent thirty thousand for furnishings. Louis is charged with finding a place; I will do my best not to disgrace you.

I appreciate all you have done for Louis at court; yet I do not want him placed too conspicuously. I prefer the Audit Office for steadiness rather than a post that would commit him too much. Our affairs are in good hands; once the steward understands the details, I will support Louis.

As for writing long letters—I cannot. One day is like another here: the children are either well or ill, and that sets the tone. In this solitary grange, minutes become hours depending on their health. My delightful hours come only when they sleep and I am no longer needed to rock or soothe them. When they lie beside me, I think, “Nothing can go wrong now.” Yet every mother is haunted by imagined dangers: razors, fires, ponds, the demon of convulsions. We spend our days on a stage of small dramas, tender and terrible.

At night I dream of their future, comforted by the watch of their guardian angel. Sometimes Armand calls in his sleep; I kiss his forehead and his sister’s feet and watch their beauty. Once an angel roused me to Athenais’ cradle—her head too low, Armand’s feet purple with cold. How vital it is to have one’s children at night! A nurse cannot soothe them as a mother after a nightmare. I have learned to sleep lightly, my ear tuned to every sigh or rustle by their bed.

Morning brings their chatter. Nais strains to reach me while Armand climbs up like a little monkey; they transform my bed into a playground and I am at their mercy. The baby tugs my hair and Armand guards my breast like a jealous sentinel. Their antics replace sleep; their laughter repays all care.

Then the dressing begins: dressing gown, apron, sleeves rolled up, and Mary’s help in baths and scrubbings. Bath time is a peril—so much crying from misjudged temperature or a dropped brush. Amusement is necessary—paper boats and glass ducks—to get them into the tub. I contrive diversions: prayers, scoldings, promises; motherhood becomes a game of cleverness matched to a child’s cunning. Our triumphs are private: Mary and I rejoice when, after a struggle, the children are at last clean and content. Only God, His angels, and perhaps you can grasp the satisfaction of such small victories.

English women excel at arranging comforts; their nurses and systems are admirable. My children will be bare‑legged in summer and in woolen socks in winter; no swaddling or tight bandages for them. A true mother, though, is never free; the helplessness of many French infants gives nurses a freedom their charge’s mothers would not allow. That is why I do not write: besides managing the estate, I am raising two children, with a third on the way.

Motherhood demands silent self‑denial and attention to minute details—a mother blows on the spoon for her child’s soup, minces the chop, endures annoyances. No servant or foreign nurse can replace a mother in those daily contests where gentleness must soothe every little grief. Louise, caring for these darlings, engages my whole soul. A crying child condemns the nurse or mother unless pain is natural. With two to tend (and soon three), they occupy all my thoughts—sometimes even you, whom I adore, recede to a memory.

Often my dressing is not finished until mid‑afternoon. I distrust mothers whose rooms are immaculate and who always look perfect. Yesterday, in a lovely April, I planned an outing with the children—an epic for a mother. I had laid out Armand’s little black velvet jacket, a new collar, a Scotch cap; Nais in white and pink with a baby cap. The little heads were to be brushed; Armand’s curls and Nais’ soft hair arranged; my heart was in my mouth. Once outside, proud of my work, I admired Armand’s princely air as he helped Nais along the path. Then a carriage approached and in our scramble the children tumbled into a puddle, wrecking my preparations. We had to return and change: a crying baby and a soaked child left the mother with no thought for herself.

At dinner I have oft achieved nothing. Feeding two, securing bibs, turning up sleeves, cajoling food—these recurring cares, joys, and catastrophes make me neglect my own tidiness. To write you I have let them cut pictures from novels and arrange my silks; they are absorbed and content.

Yet I have nothing to complain of. My children are robust and inventive; they amuse themselves more readily than you might think. A little freedom and a few pebbles, shells, and sand do more for them than many toys. Armand talks to flowers, flies, and chickens; he imitates and admires insects and asks “why” about everything. It is touching to see his curiosity—he even thinks of you, his godmother, as a fairy.

Let me not sadden you with all my joys. A poor man once followed us begging—beggars know a mother with a child cannot resist. Armand, innocent of hunger or money, offered the beggar the trumpet I had bought him: “Here, take this!” That moment of spontaneous charity was incomparable. The poor man, moved, kept it, saying, “I too, madame, have had children.” I wept at the sweetness.

I shudder at the thought of Armand going to school: I have only three and a half years before public institutions tear him away. The bright flowers of his sunny childhood will be cut by the scythe of education; his gracious candor may be lost. What will they do to the thinking being he is, to the curls I brushed and kissed so often? And you—are you still in love with Felipe? I have no fear of the Saracen. Goodbye; Nais has tumbled, and this letter threatens to become a volume.

My sweet, tender Renée, you will have learned from the papers of the terrible calamity that has crushed me. I could not write for twenty days; I did not leave his bedside. I received his last breath, closed his eyes, kept vigil with the priests, and said the prayers for the dead. The pangs I suffered felt like a just punishment; yet when I saw the calm smile on his lips, his final farewell to me, how could I think I had caused his death?

He is gone and I am bereft. To you, who knew us both, what more need be said? I would give my share of Heaven to bring him back even for a second. Will you not come soon and soothe me with such impossible promises? You told me beforehand that he would suffer through me. Was it so? Yes; I had no right to all his love. Like a thief I took what belonged to him and in my frenzied grasp crushed the life out of my bliss. The madness is over, but I feel alone and crushed. Merciful God! what torture can surpass the misery in that single word: alone?

When they took him away I lay on the same bed hoping to die. There was but a door between us and I thought I could force it. I was too young for death; after forty days of cruel care and attention, I was slowly brought back to life. Now I sit in the country by a window, surrounded by the flowers he had loved and made flourish, looking at the view he admired. Ah, Renée, how new surroundings wound when the heart is dead. His flowers torment me; to walk in my garden is to walk upon him. When I first ventured out I trembled and could not move.

My parents are in Spain, my brothers far off, and you detained in the country. But two angels of mercy comfort me: the Duc and Duchesse de Soria hurried to their brother in his illness and have been everything I could wish. The last nights found us gathered in mute grief around this great man—rare in any age, distinguished by mind and heart. Felipe’s calm resignation was angelic. His last words to me were simple and frank about his will and his love; he blessed me on his lips and spoke of my memory more fondly than any other. His brother forgave him, knowing what love is.

I owe my life to my brother‑in‑law and his wife; they wish to take me to Spain. To no one but you can I freely pour out my grief. A sense of my own faults sits heavy: my demands, jealousy, and anxious unrest may have worn him down. My love was dangerous because we were so alike in sensitivity; my careless words pierced him to the heart. You cannot imagine how submissive he was: I only had to tell him to leave me alone, and he obeyed without a murmur. His last breath blessed me, saying that a single morning with me was worth more than a lifetime with another. My tears fall as I write.

This is my life now: rising at midday, going to bed early, lingering over meals, standing motionless before a plant, gazing into the trees, finding significance in trifles. I seek shade, silence, and night. I drag through each hour, taking a gloomy pride in surviving them. My park gives me company enough; everything there echoes my vanished joy, visible only to me.

If I still live it is because Heaven tempers grief to the strength of those who bear it. Only a woman can know what it is to lose a love that was genuine—one that satisfied spirit and flesh. How rare a man so gifted that worship does not feel debasing! If such fortune befalls us once, we cannot hope to find it again. Men of true greatness, veiled by poetic grace, charm with a high spiritual power—beware of love: it may ruin you and the woman you love. That is my cry as I walk the paths of my wood.

And he has left me no child! That love, rich in smiles and tenderness, left no fruit. I feel cursed. Is it that intense, single‑hearted passion bears no children? Is only a marriage of reason blessed with progeny? Does Heaven envy our passions? Such wild questions haunt me.

You are the one person whose company I could bear. Come to me; none but you should help Louise in her sorrow. The day I first donned my widow’s bonnet—when I first saw myself in black—I fell back and wept until night. I weep again at the memory. Bring your children; you can nurse your baby here without making me jealous; what is gone is gone, and I will be glad to see my godson. Felipe wished for a child like little Armand. Come, then—come and help me bear this woe.

My darling—I'll be near when you read this; Louis is in Provence seeking re-election. Weep if you must—tears will turn to joy when you reunite. He journeys to Heaven; duties loosen their chains. My former life was tedious: six months in the country managing leases and impatient farmers, the rest in Paris plagued by delays and legal troubles. For a twenty-seven-year-old widow it was intolerable. Now my fortune is secured with the State. Instead of paying taxes, I receive 30,000 francs every six months from the Treasury, handed to me by a pleasant clerk. If the nation failed I should still be as well off as before, and I have time to save a little capital. Besides, the Comte de l’Estorade is a peer of this semi-republic and a friend at Court, so I have no cause for anxiety.

What drove me to these calculations? Love.

I must tell you plainly why I acted as I did. I am to be married in a village near Paris. I love and am loved. Forgive me for keeping this from you and de l’Estorade; I wished to spare you both anxiety. You know my jealousy; I thought secrecy kinder.

The man I love had 30,000 francs of debts, which I have paid. You may suspect an adventurer, but I investigated him. He has courted me nearly two years. I am twenty-seven and he twenty-three — yes, a wide gap. He is a poet who has lived on very little and spent more time dreaming than making a living. Practical people think artists unreliable; I understand that prejudice.

But my love is deliberate, not a caprice. He waited patiently; his love is noble and tender. My beloved is Gaston, the illegitimate son of the beautiful Lady Brandon, who died tragically. He was raised by his brother Louis, a sailor, whose letters once encouraged him; now Louis has vanished and Gaston is often despairing.

Gaston believed genius would bring fortune, yet his life has been hardship: failure to establish himself in literature, mounting debts, humiliation. I met him at Mme. d’Espard’s and felt an immediate sympathy that has grown into passion. He has talent and pride—qualities that can intimidate a woman—but his love for me seems genuine. He knows nothing of the depth of my feeling.

I am confident of our happiness, though I fear losing him or my charms as he remains young. I mean to nourish our love, to be loved for myself. If his affection should wane, I would blame myself rather than him. My nature inclines to be a lover rather than a mother; should disaster come, Renée, I beg you to care for my children as my legacy. Your tenderness would make my death less bitter.

I have resolved to marry quietly. I will retain control of my property; Gaston must not know everything. I propose to assign him stock yielding 12,000 francs a year, which I shall place where he will find it on the eve of our wedding. If he refuses it I will break the engagement.

This confession has fatigued me; I will continue later. I must spend tomorrow in the country.

October 20th.

I have taken steps for secrecy. I bought a small property near Ville-d’Avray: twenty acres, a little lake, and a charming garden. It is secluded, surrounded by woods, and beautifully laid out. My chalet, inspired by Swiss designs, is simple and solid, well-equipped and hidden from view. There is a separate house for servants and a concealed entrance; I have chosen discreet, loyal staff. All my cherished belongings from Paris are here, creating a simple but elegant home. The valley’s tranquility moved me to tears on my first visit; I paid the architect without hesitation, insisting he tell no one I was the owner.

Now you understand my actions and the purpose of my investments. I have cleared financial burdens to focus wholly on him. Gaston knows nothing of my plans; our life at the chalet will be modest, though I shall spare no care in preserving my appearance for him. I want him to love me at the end as at the beginning.

In two days I shall be Mme. Gaston. Is it fitting for a Christian to love so deeply?

Address future letters to Mme. Gaston, Poste Restante, Versailles. We shall keep our names and our retreat secret from the locals and obtain provisions from Paris. Farewell. Writing this I feel grief and joy together — proof that my love for Gaston is as deep as the sorrow of leaving you.

My Dear Daniel,

I need two witnesses for my marriage; please come tomorrow evening with our friend Joseph Bridau. My future wife wishes to live in complete retirement, which explains our infrequent meetings this past year. After the wedding we will be apart for a long time, but I trust you understand. Friendship can endure absence; I shall miss you, yet I shall not see you in my own home. She has sacrificed the closeness of a childhood friend for me, and I must do the same.

Our love is deep and genuine, founded on mutual understanding, and gives me immense joy. Yet I carry a painful secret. You know how desperate my finances have been; only through your support have I managed. My wife is wealthy; I am not. I once joked about wanting a rich heiress, but now that it is real I feel a bitter humiliation I cannot shake. That shame, however, only confirms my love for her—I do not recoil from it, though it overwhelms me.

Otherwise, my dreams have come true. She is the most beautiful and noble woman, with a charming personality and a keen mind. At twenty-seven she is a widow without children, lively yet sometimes melancholy. Despite her aristocratic background, she loves me despite my humble origins. Our affection has grown over time; it is chiefly emotional and respectful of physical restraint. Once she confessed that her marriage was her regret, revealing the depth of her feeling. You, Daniel, are a giant among men, and you love a remarkable woman, just as I love my Armande.

Louise, can it be that you intend a life of wedded solitude after sacrificing your first husband? You seem to court future misery. I see your resolve, and the man who won your heart must have rare charm. But have you forgotten your earlier criticisms of young men for tarnishing purity? You are lucky to believe in happiness, and while I cannot blame you, I must warn you: nature and society conspire against absolute happiness, and I foresee some disaster arising from boundless joy. I have no objection to your fiancé, but I hope you will describe him to me one day.

I have not told Louis about your marriage; it would only invite his ridicule. I will simply say you wish to keep it secret. You are brave to face winter first, and I will be your discreet friend. Your journey into this emotional wilderness is like an explorer’s perilous expedition, though fortunately only a short distance from Paris. I wish you a safe journey and a swift return.

What has happened to you, my dear? After two years of silence, Renee has a right to worry about Louise. Is this what love does, scattering friendships like ours? You must admit that, while I am devoted to my children, a mother’s love is expansive and does not diminish other affections. I miss your letters and long to see your face.

Regarding our situation, I will be brief. Your last letter contained sharp comments about our political position. You mocked us for keeping our posts in the Audit Department, which Louis owes to Charles X’s favor. But how could we provide for Athenais and Rene on an income of forty thousand livres, of which thirty thousand is entailed? We must live on our salary and save the estate’s income for their future. In twenty years we will have saved enough to provide for both children.

Louis has been raised to the Peerage and is now a grand officer of the Legion of Honor. He has made himself useful in the Chamber and was offered an embassy, which I declined for the sake of our children’s education. I will not leave Paris until Rene has finished his schooling. We could not remain strictly loyal to the elder branch of the dynasty without jeopardizing our children’s prospects. A mother is not called to be a Decius, especially now. In fifteen years, Louis will retire on a good pension, and I hope to secure a post for Armand in the Audit Department.

I am a happy mother; my children give me endless joy. Armand is now a day scholar at Henry IV’s school—I could not bear to part with him, so he attends by day and returns home each evening. He is the same charming boy you loved, and his tutor praises his progress. Home education has drawbacks: children raised at home may be exposed too early to the world’s passions and may lack the social skills gained with peers. A mother must keep her children from society’s distractions, as I have done. Now that I am thirty, I feel my duty fulfilled. Armand has lately excelled, winning prizes for translation and essays; Rene, inspired, wishes to attend school too. The three children can be noisy, but I cherish every moment. I must study their characters closely, for a mother alone discerns aptitudes. I believe Armand will become an admirable public servant, while Rene will be a bold sailor.

Nais is so dear to me I can hardly see her as separate from myself. I want her to be happy and will only give her to a man who truly loves her. Yet I worry about her future and the risk of a poor marriage. Your silence has reminded me of the anguish marriage can bring a mother. Farewell for now, dear Louise, and please write back.

My dear Renée, after two years of silence you ask why I have written so little. Words cannot convey my happiness. We bear everything with ease; we are in perfect harmony. There has been no discord in our feelings, and each day brings fresh joy. We do not fear monotony; we regret that our time together may not suffice to express all our love. We take more pleasure in each other's company than on the first day, and every hour reveals new reasons to love one another.

Each evening we talk of visiting Paris, the new boulevards, the finished Madeleine, but in the morning we linger over breakfast and indulge in a siesta. Gaston looks at me as if I were a painting; we shed tears of joy reflecting on our love. I still play the mistress, pretending to give less than I receive, delighting in the small coquetries of our life. There is something sweet about passion tempered by tenderness; Gaston, my master, remains a timid suitor at my command.

You asked me to describe him, yet it is impossible to portray the man we love without emotion. Society alters men so the public man may differ from the man of deep feeling. The charming suitor may lack the qualities a woman values, while an unrefined man may possess a profound capacity for love. Our ideal is a man whose outward appearance aligns with his true nature—a grace that cannot be acquired. In Gaston I have found that ideal.

I never knew love could so entwine youth, talent, and beauty. He is unpretentious, moving with effortless grace. When we walk together our bodies seem to fit, a single figure gliding through the woods. Our thoughts, desires, and words harmonize. Sometimes we walk in silence, listening to rain or admiring the sunset, feeling as if in silent prayer for our happiness. A timid kiss exchanged in nature feels like an act of worship. We return home each time more in love than before. Such passion between settled people would scandalize Paris; only in the woods can we express it freely.

Gaston is of average height, well-proportioned, and agile. He has a fair complexion, dark hair, and a striking resemblance to Louis XIII. His honorable poverty has spared him the vices that ruin many young men. His teeth are magnificent, and he has a robust constitution. His blue eyes, full of tenderness for me, can flash with intensity when inspired.

He has a remarkably even temperament. I have heard many tales of women’s troubles with men dissatisfied with themselves, but Gaston is free from such burdens. I have learned that marrying an older man can lead to unequal unions; do not let Athénaïs marry one.

His smile is a treasure—always fresh, full of unspoken love and gratitude. Our shared experiences are woven into our joy; the smallest details of nature recall our time together. We share a deep connection, as if we are two editions of the same book. Our ability to view things broadly brings constant pleasure, and this unity of thought feels like a pledge of love.

Despite these pleasures, life here is laborious. I do my own room, refusing to let a hired hand intrude on my personal space; it is arranged so Gaston finds it pleasing. I rise early, bathe in cold water to refresh myself before he wakes, and emerge like a newly opened flower. My maid manages daily grooming, and I dress several times a day for Gaston’s delight. We tend our flowers and horticulture with great interest, surrounding our chalet with beauty. Our garden is a source of pride; we cultivate fruits and vegetables and sell the surplus—an abundance hard to find in Paris.

To keep Gaston's mind engaged I encourage him to finish plays begun during harder times. Literary work needs reflection, and I assist him so our winter evenings are never dull. Our servants have an easy time; they see us as a contented traveling couple.

I strive to be society for Gaston, ensuring his solitude does not become tedious. I have learned true jealousy—the kind born of love; the mere thought of him ceasing to love me sends shivers through me.

Alas, I am still without a child. I long for the laughter of children running through our garden and envy your happiness. As I approach thirty, I feel the limits of my beauty closing in and worry about aging while Gaston remains young. I often ask him to promise he will tell me if his love ever fades; he swears it will not.

Goodbye, dear friend. Shall we let years pass without writing again? Happiness can be monotonous—perhaps why Dante seems greater in the Paradiso than in the Inferno. I am not Dante, only your friend, and I do not wish to bore you. Write to me—your children supply endless stories—while I... No more of this. A thousand loves.

My dear Louise, I have read your letter repeatedly; its tone is childish. You forget that pure love is natural but is distorted by civilization. Society built the family as the foundation of organized life at the cost of individual desires. A married man must dedicate himself to his wife, who rightly benefits from his efforts.

You have taken a mistaken path: the dream of being adored suits the young, not a mature woman. Vanity may enjoy adoration, but true fulfillment lies in the life of wife and mother. Return to Paris; fear the loss of ordinary happiness more than hardship. Life’s simple pleasures—bread, air, silence—sustain us. Passionate love exhausts; motherhood’s affections endure and reward. I chose self‑sacrifice and would choose it again. Your life, masked by romance, is rooted in selfishness; I plead with you as I watch your happiness tested.

Life seeks balance; excess brings pain or fleeting pleasure. You are ill because you cling to a passion a marriage should steady. The glory of home is calmness and shared life, which you overlook. More passionate than tender, you try to be both wife and mistress, undermining marriage’s sanctity. If you could understand a mother’s sweet self‑discipline: my proud nature softened into gentle melancholy, rewarded by motherhood’s joys. I fear your life may darken. I had hoped time would restore reason, but your appetite for love seems insatiable. Farewell; I despair that my letter, meant to reconcile you with society, only revealed what I see as selfishness.

Renee, calamity has struck—no thunderbolt like this for poor Louise. Doubt is my calamity; certainty would be death. The day before yesterday, after dressing, I looked for Gaston to walk and found him gone. In the stable his mare Fedelta was in a lather; it implied he’d been to Paris without telling me. Suspicion seized me. When Gaston returned he carried me to the parlor; I would not speak and fled to my room to weep. He waited patiently. I asked about mud on Fedelta’s legs; he teased, but I saw no disloyalty in his eyes, though I remained unconvinced.

We spent the day together; my heart clung to him. I hated that mare for my distress. I asked if I bored him; he reassured me. I suggested selling Fedelta; his response made me feel inadequate. Alone, I was consumed by despair. Next morning I told him to take Fedelta to Paris; my tone betrayed turmoil. He urged trust; I tried to suppress suspicion. He left, and I watched him go with a sense of doom. When he returned late with a whip as a gift I felt it only distracted from something deeper. I later learned he bought it from Verdier, which confirmed my fears. I resolved to keep watch while staying silent.

Days passed; Gaston changed, working harder—seeming to need money. His drawer was empty; he received letters he quickly tore up. Jealousy consumed me; I followed him to the post office and learned from a letter Nathan sent about a play’s acceptance. Despite his success, I couldn’t shake that he hid something. The conviction of a rival drove me to find the truth. After investigation I learned Gaston visited a woman in Paris; I followed and found she was his brother’s widow, with children. The revelation shattered me: he had been providing for her and her family. I returned tormented by thoughts of betrayal and jealousy, yet my love for Gaston remained. I feared confronting him, and I even pondered my own end, unable to bear the suffering.

My dear Louise, I write urgently; your last letter alarmed me. I confided in Louis to save you. Though our methods may seem extreme, the outcome is satisfactory. I even involved the police, kept secret among us. Gaston is not as you feared. His brother Louis died in Calcutta, leaving a widow and two children. Gaston has secretly supported them, using his earnings for his sister‑in‑law and her children. The woman you mistook for a rival is not a threat but a mother in need; Gaston acted from kindness and duty.

You should love him more; he has shown sensitivity and care. Welcome the widow and her children into your life—perhaps adopt the boys. This revelation should give you peace, not despair. Ah, my dear friend, what can I say? It is too late for me. My actions have sealed my fate. Tormented by love for Gaston, I cannot bear losing him. I am filled with despair and long for relief. Come, dear friend, and witness my decline.

My love,—Take the children to Provence without me; I must stay with Louise, who has only a few days left. I cannot leave her or her husband, for whom I worry.

You know the letter that sent me rushing to Ville d'Avray, gathering doctors on the way. Since then I have barely left my dear friend, sitting up nights for two weeks. When I arrived Louise and Gaston seemed charming and happy, but it was a façade. She whispered, "He must not know; I am dying." Her hand was burning, her cheeks flushed. I left the doctors in the woods until needed.

Alone with Gaston, she collapsed into my arms and wept. She begged me to summon help at once if it could save her. When I asked what she had done, she confessed simply that, driven by passion, she had forced profuse night sweats and lain in the dew by the lake until she brought herself to the last stage of consumption. Gaston thought it a cold; she knew she was dying.

I fetched physicians. After their consultation none gave hope; her strong constitution had only delayed the result—an outbreak of folly had made her case desperate. I was stunned. I have lived in Louise as much as in my own life. Crushed, I could not escort the doctors out. She, calm and clear, accepted the verdict. "So there is no hope for me!" she said, and sent me to her drawing-room.

"Stay with me to the end; I won't have sad faces around me. I must keep the truth from him. I know I have the strength to do it. I am full of youth and spirit, and can die standing! I have no regrets. I am dying as I wished, still young and beautiful, in the perfection of my womanhood." She reflected on love and life—how her jealousy had perhaps made life hard for Gaston—and felt serene about having lived intensely. "There are people who have lived sixty years without two years of real life, while my thirty years are doubled by the intensity of my love."

She insisted I alone had the right to mourn her, then drew a grave lesson: her death showed marriage cannot rest on passion alone. She praised my noble life and growing affection for my husband while confessing her own eager passion had waned. She spoke of two noble men she had known—one in mind, the other in form—and of dying adored. Perhaps she should have drawn nearer to God; her spirit, she hoped, would fly to Him and to those who had made her life heaven.

She warned that her example might tempt others to expect impossible felicities, and, with her usual disdain for the modern clamour about women's emancipation, dismissed that fuss as childish, insisting women should yield their wills to their husbands.

She spoke gently until Gaston returned with his sister-in-law, the children, and the English nurse he had fetched from Paris. "Here are the pretty instruments of my torture," she said of the boys, adding, "What a wonderful likeness to their uncle!" She played hostess to Mme. Gaston and insisted the chalet be hers. I wrote to friends and family—Madeleine, the Chaulieus, the Duc de Rhetore, and the Lenoncourts. It was time.

The next day she was too exhausted to go out; she rose only for dinner. Madeleine de Lenoncourt, her brothers, and her mother arrived. The coolness from Louise's second marriage faded as father and brothers visited daily and duchesses spent evenings at the chalet. Death unites as well as separates; it silenced petty feelings.

Louise remained charming, graceful, witty, and tender—one of Paris's queens to the last. "I want to look well even in my coffin," she said with that matchless smile, lying on the bed where she would linger for a fortnight. Her room lacked the usual sick-chamber air; medicines and nursing supplies were hidden. "Is not my deathbed pretty?" she asked the priest who came to confess her. We watched her like misers, treasuring each jest, each grace.

Gaston, prepared by our anxiety and the harsh truths, bore the blow with bravery, though deeply struck. I would not be surprised if he followed her soon. Yesterday, walking by the lake, he said simply, "I must be a father to those two children," and asked me to be a second mother to them and to persuade my husband to accept the guardianship. He wore smiles for Louise; his courage matched hers.

Louise wished to see her godson, but I preferred he remain in Provence lest she promise too much. Yesterday evening she was briefly delirious, yet even her babble was beautiful—proof that the madness of the gifted is not folly. In a faint voice she sang Italian airs from I Puritani, La Sonnambula, Moïse. We stood around her bed in silence; not one of us, not even the Duc de Rhetore, had dry eyes, as we felt her soul departing. Though she could no longer see us, the charm lingered in the melody.

During the night the death agony began. At seven in the morning I raised her from bed; a flicker of strength returned and she wished to sit by the window, taking Gaston's hand. Then, my love, the sweetest spirit we shall ever see on this earth departed, leaving us with an empty shell.

The last sacrament had been administered the evening before, without Gaston's knowledge—he had been sleeping during the ceremony. After she sat at the window, she asked me to read the De Profundis in French, repeating the words and pressing her husband's hands.

August 26th.

My heart is broken. I have just seen her in her winding-sheet; her face is pale with purple shadows. Oh! I want my children! Bring me my children!
