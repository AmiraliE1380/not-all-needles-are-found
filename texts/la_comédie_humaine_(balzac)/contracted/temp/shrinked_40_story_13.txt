Monsieur de Manerville was a respectable Norman gentleman whom the Maréchal de Richelieu married to a wealthy Bordeaux heiress when the old duke administered Guienne. The Norman sold his Bessin estate, adopted a Gascon life at his wife’s château de Lanstrac, and bought the post of major of the Gate Guards toward the end of Louis XV’s reign. He escaped the Revolution and lived on until 1813.

In late 1790 he went to Martinique, leaving his Guienne property under the care of Mathias, a notary’s clerk who sympathized with revolutionary ideas. On his return he found his estates intact and well managed—the Norman steadiness united to the Gascon energy. Madame de Manerville died in 1810. The old man, hardened by youthful dissipation, became penurious and stingy, giving almost nothing to his only son.

Paul de Manerville returned from the college of Vendôme in 1810 and spent three formative years under his seventy-year-old father’s strict rule. That despotism stunted the development of resistance in a naturally bold Gascon; Paul learned to suppress feeling and to yield outwardly. His suppressed impulses hardened inwardly, and later, when they met social norms, he could reason correctly but act wrongly: impulsive in crises yet timid in sustained opposition, simple and credulous, a dupe and voluntary victim of circumstances. Confined in his father’s mansion and without the means to live among equals, he envied their pleasures without sharing them.

Each evening the old man took him in a shabby carriage to royalist salons where the remnants of parliamentary and military nobility gathered—landed men humbled by mercantile wealth, who took pride in scorning commerce. Too young to grasp those social distinctions, Paul was bored, unaware that such connections would later secure a man’s preeminence. He found some solace in the manual exercises his father insisted on—fencing, riding, pistol practice, tennis, and courtly manners—believing these accomplishments a gentleman’s essentials. So Paul fenced, rode, fired at a pistol gallery, and read novels when not occupied by the enforced frivolities of nobility. Such monotony would have crushed him had not his father’s death set him free. He then became possessor of the considerable capital amassed by paternal avarice and of well-kept estates. Yet Bordeaux displeased him, and Lanstrac did not charm.

Once his inheritance was settled, the young heir, eager for enjoyment, invested, entrusted estate administration to Mathias, and spent six years abroad: attached to the French embassy at Naples, secretary at Madrid, then in London—making a European tour. After seeing the world and dissipating his father’s savings, Paul returned to draw on the territorial revenues Mathias had been conserving. He resolved, in a seemingly virtuous impulse, to leave Paris, return to Bordeaux, regulate his affairs, live as a country gentleman at Lanstrac, improve his property, marry, and seek a deputy’s seat.

Paul was a count; nobility again had matrimonial value. For seven hundred thousand francs squandered in six years he had bought what money cannot: the social experience of life—the knowledge drawn from love affairs, duels, lost bets, disillusions, and pleasures. He had become what is called elegant. He was not a mere man of fashion; in that burlesque army of worldlings, the man of elegance ranks above the mere fop. Paul maintained that reputation: his servants were well dressed, his carriages noted, his suppers in vogue; his bachelor establishment rivaled the best Paris houses. He had not seduced any woman permanently; he gambled openly, was honest in manner, left no incriminating billets-doux, and kept no chest of love-letters. Not wishing to touch his Guienne estates, he practiced no reckless extravagance; he did not borrow, though he foolishly lent to friends who deserted him. His dissipations had a method, the secret of a character molded by a tyrannical father into a kind of social mongrel.

One morning he told his friend Henri de Marsay, already destined for celebrity, “My dear fellow, life has a meaning.”

“You must be twenty-seven to find it,” de Marsay laughed.

“Well, I am twenty-seven; and because I am twenty-seven I mean to live the country-gentleman life at Lanstrac. I’ll move my belongings to Bordeaux, and spend three months a year in Paris in this house I shall keep.”

“Will you marry?”

“I will marry.”

De Marsay, after a pause, gave a devastating counternarrative. He warned that settling into the respectable roles of father and husband would make Paul ridiculous and miserable. Marriage, he said, shoves a man into a narrow social destiny: merchants marry for capital, peasants for labor, brokers and notaries for wives’ dots, kings for dynasties. To join this pack was to forgo the freedoms and chances of bachelor life. A bachelor with even modest income preserves an elegant reputation and many opportunities; marriage is social immolation, limiting ambition and exposing one to the petty cares and compromises that make men small. Even if Paul married a wealthy Englishwoman to preserve some independence, he would still be hampered by domestic obligations and the inevitable compromise of his habits.

De Marsay outlined a compromise—marry “en grand seigneur”: entail the estate, have two legitimate children, give the wife a separate household, meet her only in society, keep distinct spheres. Two hundred thousand francs a year could support such aristocratic life, which preserves dignity and social advantage while retaining bachelor privileges. Yet he doubted Paul could manage even that: he questioned whether Paul, shaped by a childhood under a tyrant and by his own gentleness, could become the necessary head of a household. Children, de Marsay argued, love a weak or prodigal father whom they later despise; to be a good paterfamilias requires not mere will but inherent strength. Marriage, he insisted, produces many men who dishonor their names.

Paul defended himself with a different logic. Tired of living for appearances—keeping horses and carriages that exist only to be shown—he longed for sincere domestic feeling. He did not envy de Marsay’s cool detachment from society’s prejudices; he belonged to the great majority of men made of emotions and needed the peaceful chances and changes of life. Often a man courts many women and wins none; Paul wanted a woman always at his side, a confidante to share interests and secrets. He wanted a close union so affection would not hinge on the turns of passion and would be steady against disillusion. He believed he had the courage to be a worthy husband and father, to submit to society’s conditions, to accept family joys.

De Marsay ridiculed the sentiment as naive. He argued that the problem of marriage is not merely a matter of love but of human psychology and the social framework that courtship disguises. Youthful conquest thrives on obstacles and the thrill of the pursuit; marriage removes those obstacles and often produces repulsion instead of intimacy. A lover can sometimes convert a reluctant woman, but that success recollects an earlier defeat—like Napoleon winning but unable to erase his retreat. The ardor a woman admires in a lover appears brutal in a husband. Moreover, the Civil Code treated women as wards and minors; to govern such a person effectively requires the rod of fear. Do you, de Marsay asked, have the strength to govern by fear? Paul’s gentleness and confiding nature made him ill-suited to play the necessary tyrant. Marriage’s daily realities—calculating dowries, discoursing on morality, becoming a domestic man—would turn him into what he most feared: ordinary and ridiculous.

He offered final counsel: remain a bachelor for thirteen years, enjoy life, and at forty, when gout and the tedium of youth set in, marry a widow of thirty-six; then you might be happy. If one insisted on youth, one would risk becoming a madman. De Marsay confessed amusement at life’s follies but hinted that he might have written a book on modern marriages’ paradoxes had he not chosen to spend his energies on immediate pleasures and on ensnaring hearts rather than theorizing.

Paul was not swayed. He laughed at de Marsay’s statecraft and declared his desire for domestic consolation sincere: “I shall feel happy when my valet says in the morning, ‘Madame is awaiting monsieur for breakfast’; happier still at night, to return to find a heart—” De Marsay mocked the sentiment as “indecorous,” but pledged to continue treating Paul politely, proposing, as if in a final jest, to meet the Comtesse de Manerville when she appeared.

They agreed to remain friends—Paul steadfast in his modest destiny of becoming a worthy father and husband, and de Marsay still convinced that marriage was a social trap requiring strength Paul might lack. In their banter and counsel Balzac lays out two competing philosophies: the romantic longing for domestic union and the cynical, pragmatic calculation that marriage confines and corrupts, with society’s laws and human nature conspiring to make husbands either tyrants or fools. The scene closes with the pair poised between practical counsel and sentimental resolve, the future introduction of Paul’s intended hinted at as the next movement in this moral and social drama.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Though the foregoing conversation affected the Comte de Manerville, he carried out his intentions and returned to Bordeaux in the winter of 1821.

Restoring and furnishing his family mansion sustained the elegant reputation that preceded him. Reintroduced by old connections to Bordeaux’s royalist society—which his opinions, name, and fortune bound him to—he quickly took a fashionable pre‑eminence. The Faubourg Saint‑Germain of Bordeaux admired his knowledge of life and Parisian accomplishments. An old marquise revived a courtly phrase, calling him “the pink of fashion”; the liberal clique used it satirically, the royalists sincerely.

Paul de Manerville fulfilled his flowery title. In public he seemed superior, and at ease displayed the fine qualities that accompanied his defects. His wit was mild; his manners not supercilious; his intercourse with women expressed a temperate respect they liked. His foppery was merely a carefulness of appearance that pleased. He respected rank, granted youth a certain freedom, and though adept with sword and pistol, was noted for a feminine gentleness. Medium height and plumpness did not prevent his playing the part of a Bordelais Brummell: fair complexion, handsome hands and feet, blue eyes fringed by long lashes, black hair, graceful motions, and a chest voice that vibrated in listeners’ hearts harmonized with his sobriquet. Paul was a delicate flower needing careful culture—one more to receive than to give happiness, with something of the woman in his nature—one to whom conjugal love should come as a providence.

Such a character creates private difficulties but commands public attraction. Consequently Paul succeeded greatly in the narrow provincial circle, where his half‑tinted mind was better appreciated than it would have been in Paris.

Restoring his town house and the château de Lanstrac—into which he introduced English country‑house comfort—consumed the capital the notary had saved over six years. Reduced now to his strict income of about forty thousand a year, he believed himself prudent in regulating his household accordingly.

After flaunting his equipages, entertaining the town’s most distinguished young men, and giving hunting parties at Lanstrac, Paul saw that provincial life would not do without marriage. He was too young for stinginess or for speculative improvements provincials undertake to secure children; he needed the variety of Parisian life. A name to preserve, property to transmit, a household to create social relations, and a weariness of irregular connections were motives, but the chief was his secret love for Bordeaux’s queen, the great beauty Mademoiselle Evangelista.

Around the century’s beginning a rich Spaniard named Evangelista had settled in Bordeaux; letters of recommendation and large fortune opened noble salons to him. His wife, a Creole from the Casa‑Reale, lived as a great lady, ignorant of money’s value and indulged by an adoring husband. From 1800 to 1812 the Evangelistas were objects of interest. The Spaniard died in 1813, leaving Madame Evangelista a thirty‑two‑year‑old widow with an immense fortune and a prettiest little girl of eleven who became an accomplished young woman. The Restoration shifted social positions; royalist ranks cleared and old families left Bordeaux. Lacking her husband’s direction, Madame Evangelista resolved not to change her mode of living: by squandering large sums on brilliant fêtes and a quasi‑regal life she maintained the public belief in her wealth while her capital steadily diminished.

Natalie, now nineteen, seemed the richest match in Bordeaux, but proposals had not come. Accustomed to indulgence, she wore cashmeres and jewels and lived in a luxury that alarmed the calculating provincials. A fatal remark circulated: “None but a prince can afford to marry Mademoiselle Evangelista.” Mothers jealous of Natalie’s beauty and tyrannical elegance spread treacherous warnings—her costly toilette, horses, maid, Mechlin laces—deterring suitors. Pampered as she was, Natalie knew nothing of the value of money or household maintenance; she bounded through life unbridled and unshod.

Six months after Paul’s arrival the Pink of Fashion and the Queen of Balls met in Bordeaux’s highest society. They exchanged apparently cold glances, each thinking the other charming. Madame Evangelista muttered, “He will be my son‑in‑law.” Paul thought, “She will be my wife.”

The Evangelistas’ reputed wealth lingered in Paul’s memory, so their pecuniary condition was known to him. When society’s flattering remarks about Natalie ended with warnings about extravagance, Paul replied with disdain that shut down provincial pettiness. He brought to Bordeaux a certain Britannic reserve and Byronic pessimism, together with English plate, boot‑polish, ponies, yellow gloves, cigars, and a habit of galloping—thus avoiding discouragements that deterred other suitors. Madame Evangelista asked him to formal dinners; he accepted, pleased to breathe the grandeur and luxury he loved.

When he rode by in his tilbury or on his English horse, young men said, “There’s a lucky man. He is rich and handsome, and is to marry, so they say, Mademoiselle Evangelista.” Society, which never does good without doing evil, credited Natalie with a dowry of a million and bestowed her, almost before consent, upon Paul. Their fortunes were thought to match: Paul’s habits of luxury and his newly arranged house made him seem uniquely fitted to meet the pecuniary difficulties of marrying such a daughter of a Creole great lady. Royalist friends flattered him: “Everyone gives you Mademoiselle Evangelista. You could not find, even in Paris, a more delightful girl. Her mother is intelligent and adroit; she will be a great help in public life. You must soon bring the matter to a close.”

“That is all very well,” replied Paul, anxious to keep freedom, “but I must be sure that the conclusion shall be a happy one.”

He therefore went often to the Hôtel Evangelista, partly to occupy idle hours harder for him than for most men. Madame Evangelista, at forty, had the beauty of a glorious summer sunset. Her spotless reputation was much discussed; her temperament combined the force and seductiveness of Spanish and Creole women. She had black eyes and hair, a swaying form and a complexion like muslin over crimson—white suffused with color. Full and rounded, she possessed the nonchalance of strength united with vivacity, sometimes the carriage of a queen. Men were taken by her conversation; she had the genius for schemes necessity bestows, advancing from concession to concession and retreating when asked for too much. Though ignorant of facts, her familiarity with courts and illustrious families gave a superficial but impressive education. She received society with natural grace and dignity, and her reputed virtue lent authority to her actions.

Mother and daughter had a true friendship that went beyond maternal and filial bonds; perpetual contact produced no jar. Madame Evangelista’s perpetual widowhood was not explained solely by maternal devotion: she had once loved a man who recovered titles and property under the Restoration, courted her to marry in 1814, and severed the connection by 1816. She nursed a resentment—“Odiate e aspettate”—and relished a revengeful triumph when he later ruined himself. This misadventure and pride kept her from remarrying and turned her motherly devotion into an ambition to place her daughter at the heart of Parisian society.

Madame Evangelista divined Paul’s nature and concealed her own. He was precisely the man she wanted for a son‑in‑law, an instrument for future influence. Through his mother he belonged to the Maulincour family; his connection to the Baronne de Maulincour and her grandson Auguste’s army position made Paul an excellent introducer for the Evangelistas into Parisian society. The widow, who disliked residing in Bordeaux, longed to shine in Restoration Paris, where political fortune lay. She looked to Paul as a means to that end; many men serve as screens for secret feminine ambitions, and Madame Evangelista had more than one interest in holding her daughter’s husband.

Paul was captivated. The mother charmed him by seeming to seek no influence while subtly magnifying herself and her daughter in his eyes. He valued himself more for their appreciation; he fancied himself clever when his remarks pleased Natalie and flattered when the mother’s praise appeared involuntary. So kind and welcoming were they that he spent most of his time at the Hôtel Evangelista.

A year after his return, though Paul had not declared himself, his attentions made the world consider him a suitor. Neither mother nor daughter pressed marriage. Natalie preserved the reserve of a great lady who can charm without allowing intimacy—a reserve that pleased Paul, timid and disliking sudden proposals. He therefore committed himself inwardly all the more. The mother’s remark that superior women sometimes place ambition above earlier emotions seduced him: “That woman is fitted,” he thought, “to advance me in diplomacy before I am even made a deputy.”

Paul was an optimist; he saw everything to advantage and did not suspect that an ambitious mother‑in‑law might become a tyrant. He pictured himself married each evening and embraced wedlock cheerfully—bachelor life seemed dull. “Marriage,” he mused, “is disagreeable for people without means, but half its troubles disappear before wealth.” Every day some favorable consideration swelled his sense of advantage.

“No matter what position I attain, Natalie will always be on the level of her part,” he told himself. “How many Empire men I’ve seen who suffered horribly through their wives! It is a great condition of happiness not to feel one’s pride or vanity wounded by the companion we have chosen. A man can never be really unhappy with a well‑bred wife; she will never make him ridiculous.” He taxed his memory among Faubourg Saint‑Germain women to see Natalie as their equal; all comparisons favored her, resting on his imagination, which followed his desires. Paris would have shown other types to balance his mind; in Bordeaux Natalie had no rivals and appeared when Paul was under an age‑old tyrant: the domination of an idea.

Thus propinquity, self‑love, and a real passion whose outlet was marriage led Paul into an irrational love he prudently kept to himself. He tried to study Natalie, mindful of de Marsay’s warnings. But luxury breeds indifference; Paul never imagined the gulf of ruin beneath the ladies’ habits. Moreover, there are no rules to predict conjugal harmony; young people display favorable weather only, oblivious to life’s common gray days. Each shows a favorable mask; later they blame marriage for evils inherent in life.

To detect in Natalie any sign of future imperfections would have required not only physiognomy but a kind of personal science few possess. Her face, like most young girls’, was impenetrable. The sculptural calm of virgin faces—Justice, Innocence—marked purity; such placid expression had not been clouded by passion or betrayal. If that expression were assumed, then no young woman remained behind it.

Yet any clever analyst might find in Natalie indications of future difficulties. Her beauty arose from extreme regularity of feature and proportion—perfection that often foreshadows coldness of organization. Superior nature usually shows slight imperfections that become luminous attractions; perfect harmony often indicates a mixed, colder nature. Her round waist signaled strength and a will liable to obstinacy; statue‑like hands suggested an inclination for illogical domination; meeting eyebrows hinted at jealousy that in inferior minds becomes hatred. The “hate and wait” of her mother lived in her. Her eyes were blackish brown with orange streaks, her hair a ruddy auburn—an external contrast giving charm but not necessarily soul. Where outlines lack soft roundness, beauty is not of the soul. Such roses of youth will drop their leaves, revealing hardness and dryness beneath admired nobility.

Though her face had some august outlines, her slightly full chin suggested passions that might grow violent; her astringent mouth expressed haughty pride consistent with hand, chin, brows, and figure. Her pure voice, though seductive, had metallic tones hinting at Casa‑Reale temperament. These marks suggested violent passions without tenderness,—sudden devotions, irreconcilable dislikes, a mind not deeply intelligent, and a desire to rule natural in those who feel their station inferior to their pretensions.

These defects lay in Natalie like ore in a mine and would only surface under life’s shocks. Meantime grace, freshness, distinction, sacred ignorance, and youthful sweetness gave a glamour that misled unthinking minds. Her mother taught her the art of agreeable talk, replying to arguments with clever jests and concealing the subsoil of her mind with volubility. Natalie had the charm of those who never knew suffering: frank, gay, natural, expecting pleasure from marriage and answering objections with jest.

How could Paul, dazzled by such beauty, foresee the woman she might be at thirty? Besides, happiness in such a marriage was not impossible. Embryo defects could be stifled by proper training, especially in a young girl who loved him. But to render ductile an intractable woman the iron wrist de Marsay recommended was necessary. Fear inspired by love is an instrument to manage women; he who loves fears, and thus is nearer affection than hatred.

Had Paul the coolness, firmness, and judgment required for this struggle? Did Natalie love him? Like most young girls, she mistook instinct and the pleasure of his external appearance for love; she knew nothing of marriage or its demands. Would she have the tact to distinguish his noble qualities among lesser defects, or would she magnify the latter and forget the former, as inexperienced wives often do? There comes a time when wives pardon defects in a husband who spares them annoyances; what conciliating power or wise experience would uphold this home? Paul, weak where even a strong man runs risks, might yield to tyranny instead of establishing his rule. Could he say “No”?

The subject here is not merely the change from bachelor to married man—a picture that would itself offer rich drama—but the great comedy that precedes all conjugal pairing in France: the discussion before a notary about the marriage contract, played in families noble and bourgeois, where human passions are excited by small interests as by large ones. This scene, neglected by dramatists though full of wit, controlled Paul’s future life and was awaited by Madame Evangelista with terror. It is to this comedy—the notarial negotiation—that we now turn.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At the start of the winter of 1822 Paul de Manerville, urged by his great-aunt the Baronne de Maulincour, formally asked for Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista’s hand. The baroness, back from Médoc with encouragement, insisted he marry: Natalie’s dowry was her patrimony and a man of name and estate must marry to continue the family. She advised consulting Maitre Mathias to draw the marriage contract.

Paul, uneasy about hints that Madame Evangelista was a “sly cat,” followed his aunt’s counsel and engaged Mathias. He approached Madame Evangelista with customary courtesy. She accepted notarial oversight but concealed a crucial fact: legally she owed Natalie one-third of Monsieur Evangelista’s fortune—about 1,156,000 francs—while having little ready cash. If Paul learned this through his notary he might withdraw and foil her plans; confident she could secure Natalie’s future or find an escape, she resolved to hide the difficulty.

Madame Evangelista called Maitre Solonet, a young, ambitious royalist notary noted for gallantry and zeal. Solonet, flattered by her reception, heard the situation and advised theatricality: deportment and appearance at the marriage-contract signing mattered more than law. He urged that Natalie be presented at her best that evening. Madame Evangelista dressed her daughter in white tulle and pink ribbons; Natalie, coiffed à la Sévigné, appeared charming. When pressed about love and finances, Natalie declared she loved Paul and promised to shoulder blame alone if property problems forced them apart.

Paul arrived with a bouquet. He and Natalie spoke fondly of a future at Lanstrac, Paris, and children while awaiting the notaries. But the marriage’s legal and financial complexities soon intruded. The two notaries embodied different eras and temperaments. Maitre Mathias, Paul’s notary, was an elderly, painstaking professional of forty years’ practice, respected for uprightness and caution. Solonet was young, fashionable, politically engaged, eager for profit and advancement. At Madame Evangelista’s salon they represented “former times and the present day.”

Solonet had prepared a contract favorable to the widow; Mathias insisted on detail and clarity. Mathias catalogued Paul’s assets—Lanstrac (rent 23,000 francs), farms, vineyards, houses in Bordeaux and Paris, furniture—emphasizing substantial landed wealth. Solonet countered that the Evangelista inheritance had been invested in Five-per-cent funds and effectively amounted to about 800,000 francs, that the Paris house added value, and that the mother could convey titles while reserving a life interest. Mathias challenged vague claims and demanded inventories. Solonet explained that, being Spanish and Creole, the family had not followed French formalities at the father’s death and had reconverted English securities in Paris, complicating strict accounting.

As arguments grew heated, Natalie privately told Paul she would accept blame if property difficulties separated them. Mathias, alarmed, warned Paul aside that there was little immediate dowry and recommended postponement. Solonet quickly proposed a remedy: sell the Paris house, value the Five-per-cent funds nearly at par, and convey titles into Natalie’s name while reserving Madame Evangelista a life interest. This would present an immediate, large patrimony and close the guardianship account.

Mathias saw a trap. Such an arrangement would acknowledge receipt of roughly 1,156,000 francs, obliging Paul to account to his heirs for that sum later while the family might not possess that secure capital—risking impoverishing Paul and his children. Madame Evangelista, provoked, threatened to break off the marriage and leave Bordeaux, appealing to Spanish sensibilities and the sentimental worth of her name. Paul, torn between love and prudence, nearly yielded to emotion. Madame Evangelista also harbored ambition: she wanted Paul to become a peer of France.

Suddenly Mathias conceived a legal compromise. He proposed to secure Paul’s public prospects and protect his estate by entailing Lanstrac and the Paris house and including eight hundred thousand francs of Natalie’s patrimony in that entail. Practically, this required purchasing adjoining domains and invoking the royal ordinance needed to create an entail, converting liquid patrimony into inalienable landed capital that would strengthen the family estate. This would effectively use the convertible part of Natalie’s fortune to create inalienable capital, satisfying Madame Evangelista’s wish to release Natalie’s patrimony while assuaging fears of immediate impoverishment and allowing Mathias to accept the guardianship account’s shortfall.

Solonet, surprised, recognized that an entail would allay distrust: making property inalienable reassured Paul and his advisers that the capital could not be dissipated. Madame Evangelista, unfamiliar with the technicalities, accepted Mathias’s plan because it appeared to secure Natalie’s patrimony and her own safety. Both she and Paul agreed that, if the entail were created, the marriage should proceed.

They arranged that Mathias would draw the marriage contract and Solonet the guardianship account and its release, to be signed days before the wedding. The notaries left together, each satisfied professionally: Solonet for guiding the match under a plausible appearance of security, Mathias for devising a means to protect Manerville.

Left alone, Paul and Madame Evangelista reconciled. Paul dreamed of a combined household yielding near one hundred thousand francs and of a peerage; Madame Evangelista, relieved, promised Natalie her diamonds and to sell her house to secure herself without injuring her daughter’s patrimony. Natalie, generous and emotional, accepted the possible sacrifices. Madame Evangelista fell asleep convinced she had achieved her ends: Natalie married into influence, the household assured, and Paul’s advancement plausible. Yet beneath the harmony remained seeds of distrust and competing designs—Madame Evangelista assured of triumph, Mathias guarding his client’s legacy, Solonet enjoying his victory, and Paul dreaming of love and ambition.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The next day Elie Magus, summoned by Madame Evangelista to Bordeaux, found that the talk of Comte Paul’s marriage had prompted expectations of jewels for the bride; instead she wanted Magus to value the mother-in-law’s diamonds, thinking their worth might appear in the marriage contract. She asked him to appraise them as if for a private buyer, though she did not plan to sell. Magus explained how experts tell Asiatic (Golconda, Visapur) from Brazilian stones—Asiatics have superior whiteness and brilliance, Brazilian stones a yellow tinge—and valued the necklace and earrings (entirely Asiatic) at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” diamond at 100,000. He warned that dealers’ buying prices are much lower because rare gems tie up capital; a dealer would pay about 75,000 for the Discreto and 160,000 for the necklace and earrings. He valued the rest—Brazilian diamonds, pearls, rubies—at roughly 100,000 to a dealer (resalable at 150,000). Madame Evangelista declared she would keep them; Magus noted that investing the income could replace similar diamonds within five years while preserving the principal.

News of the appraisal leaked. Servants overheard raised voices; gossip amplified the practical commerce into scandal: had Madame Evangelista sold her house? Was the marriage off? Notaries denied collapse, citing only delay on the entail’s constitution, but society persisted. Envious provincial mothers and officious neighbors speculated and embroidered the story; the prefecture even hinted that the notaries’ language implied trouble. Bets were laid; yet Paul and Madame Evangelista were not told. Paul rode twelve miles to Lanstrac and gave a hunting-party, which society took as proof of domestic strife.

Madame de Gyas, hoping to marry her daughter to Paul, approached the Evangelistas with a worried face as if to disclose misfortune. Natalie and her mother laughed off the gossip but recognized its motives: provincial mothers thwarted by lack of dowry often invent such plots. Madame Evangelista, preparing to leave the city, mocked both friends and enemies, enjoying barbed remarks at public judgment.

When Paul arrived—he had ridden to spend an hour with Natalie—he dismissed the rumors laughingly. He suggested a public ball on the day the contract was signed to refute the gossip. Madame Evangelista took him at his word and invited all Bordeaux to witness the signing; she intended the fête to silence rumor.

Preparations for the camellia fête took a month: camellias banked staircases and supper-rooms, partitions were removed, estates near Lanstrac were bought, banns published, doubts eased. The earlier stormy conference seemed only a notarial affair—until the morning of signing, when a memory returned to Madame Evangelista: Mathias’s earlier remark, made when he had accepted Solonet’s conditions—“Questa coda non e di questo gatto”—had hinted that someone else would bear the cost. If Mathias had seen compensation in the terms, it could not be Paul; might her daughter’s fortune be pressed to pay the price? She decided to seek explanations; the day would profoundly affect Paul’s conjugal life.

Despite winter, the house was adorned and crowded. About eight, as the final contract was discussed, a dense crowd assembled to see the ladies in full dress. The notaries had dined with the bridal pair and their mother; Mathias’s head-clerk was there to receive signatures and prevent surreptitious readings. Natalie was superb in her bridal toilette; Madame Evangelista, in cherry velvet, wore her pearl necklace clasped with the Discreto—an ostentatious contradiction to the ruin stories. Paul and Natalie sat close, whispering and touching; Natalie toyed with a peacock-feather fan Paul had given her.

The reading began. Solonet read the guardianship account: Natalie’s share of Monsieur Evangelista’s estate, of more than three million francs, amounted to 1,156,000 francs. The ordinary clerical formalities followed—property statements, deeds of gift in case of death without issue, life-interest gifts permitted by law, constitution of a common fund, settlement of the diamonds on the wife, library and horses on the husband—and finally the constitution of the entail. When all but signatures remained, Madame Evangelista asked what an entail would mean. Solonet offered a general explanation, but Mathias bluntly stated the entail’s practical effect: if the wife died first leaving several children including a son, the children would inherit only 360,000 francs, from which the husband would deduct his fourth in life-interest and his fourth in capital, leaving a net debt of about 160,000 francs—excluding savings and profits from the common fund. Conversely, if the husband died leaving a male heir, the wife’s right would be 360,000 francs plus any deeds of gift not in the entail, the diamonds settled on her, and profits and savings from the common fund.

The consequence struck Madame Evangelista: she whispered, “My daughter is ruined.” Both notaries heard. Mathias, with gentle irony, replied that constituting an indestructible fortune for the family could hardly be called ruin. The junior notary, reading her face, bluntly said they had attempted to deprive the family of 300,000 francs but that the children had actually secured 800,000—arguing that any supposed loss was only 400,000 from their interests for the children’s benefit—and advised her either to break the match immediately or proceed with it.

Silence followed. Natalie, unaware she had lost half her fortune, and Paul, ignorant that the Manerville house had gained, chatted lightly. Solonet and Madame Evangelista exchanged glances—one masking indifference, the other suppressing bitter feeling. Madame Evangelista’s remorse at compromising her honor for Natalie’s sake turned to fury when she realized she would be the loser and that Mathias must have foreseen this and informed Paul. The thought that Mathias might be lauded for his sagacity and turn Paul against her was intolerable. She faced a dilemma: three months earlier she might have broken the match; now banns were published, friends had come for the fête, and postponing would cause scandal. She felt trapped and knew she must yield.

Her anger surfaced—her chin trembled; Solonet observed the beginning of mortal hatred. She accused Mathias of incapacity or worse, of seeing the entail’s effect and not warning her sooner. The younger notary drew her aside, thinking of fees but speaking coolly: she had overreached legal craft in her own interest; if she did not want Comte Paul she could refuse him—the contract was not yet signed and could be postponed under some pretext. He argued that Natalie’s happiness mattered above titles, that as wife she could dominate the home and guide Paul; if she played her cards well, her daughter’s influence would secure the marriage’s benefits. This mollified Madame Evangelista; she agreed to proceed.

Mathias and Solonet then amended the contract to protect the wife’s interests further. The younger notary proposed—and Mathias concurred—clauses ensuring that if Manerville died without children or left only daughters, the entailed property should pass under the general deed of gift between husband and wife or that the entail be void. Solonet added the clause in the margin; Mathias read the added sentence aloud. Madame Evangelista, her eyes dropped, consented.

“We will now sign,” she said, voice strained. She resolved she would not let her daughter be ruined; she intended that Natalie should keep name, title, and fortune. If Natalie later loved another, Paul would be driven out of France. Her mind set on means to preserve Natalie’s wealth.

Mathias took Paul aside and explained the saving stipulation and the rescript for the diamonds: Paul must ask for them and could use them toward payments. He spoke practically: the first payment of 200,000 francs would be due after formalities; use the diamonds; the house could be sold to pay the remainder; plant vineyards to raise income. Paul gratefully pressed Mathias’s hand; Madame Evangelista noticed and suspected collusion. Rage overwhelmed her.

After signatures and initials, Mathias looked at the mother-in-law. Seeing Paul say nothing of the diamonds, Mathias remarked there could be no doubt about their transfer now they were family. Solonet suggested it was regular for Madame Evangelista to make them over immediately since Monsieur de Manerville had become responsible for guardianship funds and one never knew who might live or die. Paul protested immediate transfer would be insulting; Madame Evangelista, angered she interpreted as an insult and collusion, cried she would tear up the contract if he did not take the diamonds.

She left in fury, nearly mad with impotence. Natalie whispered to Paul to accept them: her mother was angry and would learn why that night; they must pacify her. Calmed, Madame Evangelista kept the necklace and earrings she wore and handed over the other jewels Magus had valued at 150,000 francs. Mathias and Solonet inspected them and found they would meet the first payments. Solonet assured Paul he would lose nothing; Mathias said the jewels would cover the first payment on the estate purchase and contract costs.

Madame Evangelista, however, took Paul’s hesitation as hypocrisy; his reluctance to accept the jewels seemed to her a masked insult, and a look she flung appeared to say, “Take your property.” Paul, eager to end the dispute, told Natalie to lock the jewels away as her gift. Natalie stowed them in a console drawer. At that moment carriages arrived, salons filled, and the fête began.

During the ball guests whispered and judged: some speculated on future domestic friction, others doubted whether the merchant’s daughter could hold her place socially. Card-players settled to tables, young people danced, supper was served, and the ball lasted till morning.

After Paul left, Madame Evangelista and Natalie retired. Though overtired, they exchanged a few words. Madame Evangelista confessed she had acted out of tender devotion and unschooled business ignorance to secure Natalie’s happiness; she had mortified her pride. Natalie, half-asleep, praised Paul’s innocence; she had no idea of her mother’s fears or the contract’s implications. In the dark, Madame Evangelista felt the first stirrings of avarice born of passion and hate: a resolve to secure Natalie’s entire fortune by reclaiming the husband’s property for her. She told herself honor required it; devotion to Natalie made her shrewd and calculating. She planned to invest capital and manage affairs closely. Passion and hatred had turned the once-prodigal woman into a miser and a plotter. She fell asleep muttering, “To-morrow!”—her mind already arranging measures to control Paul’s life, which she began to put into action the next day.

Though the banquet had eased Paul’s anxieties, they returned in bed. He turned over Mathias’s warning about the marriage contract and Madame Evangelista’s motives. He wondered whether, with Mathias’s help, he had been spared deceit—or whether suspicion was needless. In this second struggle with the contract his fate had already shifted unknowingly: of the two people he was marrying, the sharper—Madame Evangelista—had already become his adversary, resolved to protect her own interests from the common fund. Paul, unacquainted with Creole temperament, did not perceive her craft.

Madame Evangelista’s character had been dormant for years of social ease; but the impending loss of Natalie set her passions aflame. In three days her daughter would leave; this last day together offered a decisive chance to influence Natalie, who trusted her implicitly. Words could shape two lives; social law lacks the power to stop crimes that begin with suggestion. The mother understood that subtle influence could accomplish what force could not.

Midday found Madame Evangelista on Natalie’s bed, affectionate and mournful, rehearsing intimate counsel. She warned that conjugal love evaporates unless a wife carefully establishes influence at the start. Natalie, playful and naive, protested that Paul loved her; her mother countered that to rule the home she must appear to obey him while actually making him yield. If Madame Evangelista remained with them, the subtle acts that should form a wife’s authority might be ascribed to the mother, making Natalie's household into "a hell"; better, she advised, that the daughter be sole mistress and sovereign.

Natalie pleaded to keep her mother near, but Madame Evangelista insisted on separating: she would not expose Natalie to the risk of maternal interference, and she claimed practical reasons—poverty, the need to manage their rural property at Lanstrac and to restore her fortune by economy. She promised to write often, visit yearly, and return when her presence no longer threatened the marriage. Natalie, alarmed at the prospect of being alone with her husband, begged for concrete instruction. The mother had been waiting for this moment.

Her counsel was a compressed manual on feminine power in marriage. She blamed modern perpetual intimacy—born of revolutionary bourgeois manners and Rousseau’s ideas—for the failure of many marriages: constant contact breeds satiety, contempt, and "spleen." To maintain value, a wife must preserve distance and novelty. Go out, entertain, be ever new and amusing; let social life keep you desirable. If happiness is made to depend entirely on a man, the woman loses authority; conversely, a woman who amuses and keeps a man happy will retain him.

Madame Evangelista cautioned Natalie not to yield her whole will. Use the power of the first hours—when desire is still fresh—to establish habits of obedience by asking concessions, even on unreasonable points. A husband who has yielded once is more likely to yield again; making him do a foolish thing for you is the first step to ruling him. Keep reserve: coldness can be a maintained posture; the phrase "It is not becoming in your wife to do thus" is a talisman. The words "I will not" are feminine power when used sparingly. Inspire him to believe you understand him; if he believes in you, you can govern. In short: harmonize two wills by arranging from the outset that there shall be but one—the wife’s.

Her advice went broader: women are born to play their part in society as men are born to be generals or poets. Natalie, Madame Evangelista insisted, was made to charm salons and represent luxury, not to be a housekeeper or immediate mother. If children come only after a few years, governesses and tutors will preserve the wife’s figure and social role; display of vanity, carefully managed, flatters a man and keeps him pleased. Practical decisions would be learned from experience, the mother said, but a woman must cultivate the instinct to act on these principles.

When Natalie asked how to begin, the mother gave specific strategic precepts: a woman ardently desired before marriage must remain so afterward; do not give yourself wholly to his power; use reserve in speech and action; never demonstrate complete dependence—pride and calculation are tools of mastery. The mother’s final counsel emphasized that a wife should control events rather than only bear them.

Natalie received the lesson with gratitude and tears and, still childlike, asked why the mother had once claimed she alone could promote Paul’s advancement yet now planned to leave. Madame Evangelista lied about debts and obligations—an explanation that hid her real motive. She promised to manage matters from Lanstrac and to resume promoting Paul later if circumstances permitted. They rose to prepare for Paul’s arrival, conscious that they must salvage every asset—Mon Dieu, the "Discreto," the cherished jewel.

Paul arrived about four, attempting cordiality but showing signs of the earlier confrontation. Madame Evangelista seized the opportunity to turn matters to advantage: she declared that she would have to sell her pearl necklace and ear-rings to meet immediate payments and proposed exchanging the "Discreto"—a historic diamond said to have belonged to Philip II—for the jewels she had already given to Natalie. She framed this as generosity: the young wife would have modern diamonds she could enjoy, while the family would keep the historic stone. Paul protested, but the mother pressed the sentimental angle; Paul’s benevolent nature and desire for freedom inclined him to accept.

At that moment Solonet, a friend and speculator, arrived with news about contractors offering to buy three acres of grounds. He could raise the price if they wished. Natalie, forced into business by circumstance, agreed to sell part of the estate while reserving furniture and mirrors. Solonet suggested many were expected at the midnight mass; Madame Evangelista used the exchange with him to mention privately that she expected two hundred and fifty thousand francs of personal property and might raise more by selling her share of the house—funds she would invest at Lanstrac. The notary Mathias kissed her hand, misled into thinking the alliance might still serve familial and business ties.

When she returned to the bridal couple, Madame Evangelista reconciled her earlier claim to leave with her insistence that she would be useful as bailiff at Lanstrac. She suggested therefore it was proper she not go to Paris, reasoning that living with the young couple would oblige them and obstruct their independence, and that a mother’s presence sometimes breeds jealousy. Paul’s secret relief at being rid of his mother—he neither admitted nor fully understood it—showed itself in his smile at Natalie's sadness; he expected Paris to cure whatever misgivings she had. For Madame Evangelista this meant success: she had gotten the diamonds for Natalie and won separation, the prerequisites for her private revenge. She could leave the couple alone with their fragile love and watch events unfold.

The wedding itself was a nocturnal affair. After a small dinner for the witnesses—hosted by Madame Evangelista—the bridal couple and friends attended a torchlit mass before a crowded, curious audience. A midnight wedding suggested ill omen to some; as guests whispered, the couple’s separation and the mother’s decision to live elsewhere prompted gossip that the choice to shun the mother would open the marriage to lovers and other dangers. Elegant observers found the scene bare and indecent compared to more ostentatious nuptials.

After the ceremony Madame Evangelista accompanied Natalie to Paul’s house. Her parting counsel was the same: "Be his wife, not his mistress." She made an ostentatious display of grief to gain concessions from Paul—a theatrical maternal appeal that elicited typical concessions from men. The next day she accompanied them to the ferry for Paris; with a look and a word she signaled to Natalie that, though Paul believed he had won the contract and his freedom, the mother’s revenge had quietly begun. By obtaining the jewels and ensuring her absence, she had set the conditions for Natalie to begin reducing her husband to obedience by the very principles she had recommended—reserve, social preeminence, strategic concessions—turning the naive bride into an instrument of her design.

Paul’s future now depended on his own ability to make his wife truly love him; if he could arouse devotion rather than mere desire, he might escape. But Madame Evangelista’s craft, born of Creole temperament—a cultured, passionate, and calculating blend—was already at work: she had positioned herself to manage the estates at Lanstrac, to rebuild a fortune, and to influence events from afar. Natalie, instructed but inexperienced and trusting, left for Paris troubled and determined to follow her mother’s precepts. The mother believed she had preserved the family’s interests and secured her revenge; the couple, unmoored from maternal guidance, entered marriage under the shadow of the mother's doctrines—an alliance in which subtle domination and social strategy would reshape their lives.

Five years later, on an autumn afternoon, Comte Paul de Manerville arrived at the house of his old friend Maître Mathias in Bordeaux, cloaked and bearing a bowed, mysterious air.

Mathias, retired and too old to practice, was away on urgent business; his housekeeper Josette showed the count to Madame Mathias’s room. Fatigued by his journey, Paul slept until evening. When Mathias returned he climbed to the room and watched him sleep, as a mother watches a child; Josette followed, hands on hips.

“It is a year today, Josette, since I heard my dear wife’s last sigh; I little thought then I should see the count half dead,” Mathias murmured. Observing Paul’s moans and aged appearance, he counted the consequences of their mutual acquaintance and the passage of years. Leaning on his cane and pacing the garden, the gouty old notary reflected on the time since Paul’s aunt had proposed a match that had contributed to the count’s ruin.

At supper Mathias, struck by Paul’s outward calm but noting signs of mental strain that had aged him, urged him to sit. Paul replied that Mathias had his own troubles and proposed they speak over the meal. Mathias, aghast at placards notifying seizure of Paul’s estates—Grassol, Guadet, the vineyard of Belle-Rose, and the family mansion—read aloud the public notice naming Dame Natalie Evangelista, wife of the count, as separated from him as to worldly goods and chattels by the court. Paul answered that he and his wife were now separated in body as well.

Paul admitted he had left Paris without his wife’s knowledge. He had taken passage to Calcutta on the Belle-Amélie, sailing in two days, intending to earn a fortune in seven years and return to rebuild his wealth. To pursue commerce he would sail under the name Camille. He expected a friend, Henri de Marsay, to send a bill of exchange for 150,000 francs to Maître Mathias the next day to finance his voyage.

Mathias, dismayed, said he had never known a ruined man whose friend would lend him money. Paul insisted de Marsay would, claiming the man had already sold investments to oblige him. Mathias brightened: if true, might the friend settle matters and allow Paul to remain quietly at Lanstrac until his affairs recovered? Paul answered that no assignment or economy could pay off the approximately fifteen hundred thousand francs of debt that ruined him. He explained how events had compounded: he had given his wife diamonds, spent 150,000 francs from selling Madame Evangelista’s house on their Paris residence, used other money to meet marriage-contract payments, and sold twenty-seven thousand francs’ worth in the Funds at eighty-seven (a calculation that had left him two hundred thousand francs in debt within a month of marriage). Their income of sixty-seven thousand francs a year had been spent at three times that rate; usurers’ interest and other payments totaled a million. Then he had purchased a coronet of diamond wheat-ears for a hundred thousand, bringing the total to eleven hundred thousand, and discovered he also owed his wife’s fortune—three hundred and sixty-six thousand francs of her dot. Notes and protests added further pressure.

Mathias proposed selling properties, pressing Paul to pledge his income or sell Belle-Rose, worth perhaps six hundred thousand francs, possibly seven hundred under negotiation, and to consider the Bordeaux house, which might fetch two hundred thousand or more from buyers like Solonet. But Paul explained that such schemes were impracticable when his property was mortgaged and his wife’s claims took precedence; the multitude of notes and the seniority of his wife’s rights prevented easy settlement. Natalie’s mother, Madame Evangelista, had already improved Lanstrac and repaid Paul for its use; she had bought Grainrouge between Lanstrac and Grassol, paying one hundred thousand francs down. Paul spoke of both women who loved him—his wife and her mother—and of his unwillingness to force Natalie into a life stripped of her charms and pleasures; he preferred exile in India and honest labor to sacrifice her happiness. Mathias, moralistic, urged that a wife should share good and evil and that children might have consolidated the entail and prevented dissipation; Paul defended his indulgent view of love and begged Mathias not to preach.

The next day Mathias received the bill of exchange for 150,000 francs from de Marsay. Paul seized on this as proof his friend would help. Mathias tried to deter him from leaving but found Paul resolute after long arguments. Fortune favored them: the wind was fair and the Belle-Amélie prepared to sail. The quay filled with relations, friends, and curious onlookers; Paul’s ruin made him as celebrated as his former wealth. Mathias accompanied Paul to the quay and suffered the cruel comments of those who recalled the former dandy now heading for India.

Paul went aboard and leaned against the shrouds, defying the crowd with a contemptuous glance. As sailors weighed anchor, Mathias signaled with his handkerchief and Josette hurried to him. Paul asked the captain to wait for a boat; too feeble to go himself, Mathias gave two letters—one important packet just arrived by courier from Paris in thirty-five hours—into the sailor’s hands, with the imprudent suggestion that Paul might come ashore. The Breton sailor, unwilling to lose time, preferred not to return and handed the letters to Paul without stating their urgency. Recognizing the handwriting of his wife and de Marsay, Paul suspected their contents and, anxious not to be influenced, put the letters unread into his pocket.

At sea, consumed by sad thoughts and doubts, Paul reflected on the uncertain future and on Natalie alone. Sea-sickness then dissolved his will: he was confined to his cabin three days, vomiting and sleeping. On the first morning he felt better, went on deck, and found the letters in his pocket. He opened his wife’s letter first.

Paul’s parting letter to Natalie, written as he left Paris, had been ardent and confiding: he admitted he lacked the courage to explain his departure and had deceived her for the best; he could not accept the sacrifice of her fortune and vowed to go to India to repair his shattered fortune, promising to return rich. He asked her to trust de Marsay with the liquidation of his affairs and to keep his memory alive. He called himself her “poor Pink of Fashion,” a man blown away by mischance, but vowed to rebuild for her.

The Comtesse de Manerville’s reply poured out tenderness, grief, and resolution. She reproached him for leaving without consulting her, asserting that he belonged to her and she should have shared his decision. Yet she praised his courage, called his enterprise sublime, and vowed to support him. Her mother had already brought thirty thousand francs; Augustine had shared the distress; she and her mother would economize, pledge diamonds if needed, live at Lanstrac, and await him. She believed herself pregnant and hoped for a son. She promised to obey his wish to trust de Marsay, to be prudent and devout during his absence, and to send daily prayers, closing with passionate assurances of fidelity and love. Paul read and re-read Natalie’s ardent words: they alternately assailed him with memories and reassured him; her exclusive tenderness provoked jealousy and unease even as it revived his faith.

To understand what followed, one must know the contents of the letter Paul had written to de Marsay and de Marsay’s reply, which Mathias had received.

Paul’s letter to Henri de Marsay had been practical and desperate. He explained his ruin, begged de Marsay to sign a lease of his Paris house on nominal terms and to lend him 150,000 francs by bill of exchange to be sent to Maître Mathias—funds necessary to go to India and which Paul expected in Bordeaux the night before sailing. He confessed having hidden his ruin and having gambled in a last attempt, then failed. He admitted a weakness for lavishness in marriage but justified his departure as a manly enterprise: he would return rich or never return at all. He entrusted Natalie to de Marsay’s guardianship, requesting that Henri be her adviser, banker, and protector, given the danger of rivals while a husband was abroad.

De Marsay’s reply struck like a blow. He told Paul bluntly that his ruin was total: notes had been protested at Lecuyer’s instigation; Solonet and others had maneuvered to secure Natalie’s and her mother’s hold on the property. Belle-Rose and other estates were effectively in the hands of Madame Evangelista via intermediaries. De Marsay called it a domestic conspiracy and blamed Paul for secrecy: had Paul confided in him at marriage he could have defended him. He blamed Paul’s yielding temperament and hinted that Félix Vandenesse had touched Natalie in ways Paul never did; Paul’s indulgence and dissipation, de Marsay said, invited the wife and mother-in-law to “get rid of” him by ruining him financially.

But de Marsay did not merely upbraid; he offered a course of action and a future promise. He outlined his own political ambitions, aimed at forming an oligarchy to direct the nation, and said he was near to a successful marriage to an English heiress, Miss Dinah Stevens, whose fortune and prudence would secure him. He spoke of investments and an eventual large landed income. He assured Paul that, if Paul renounced his plan to go to India and returned at once to Paris, de Marsay would arrange a duel with Vandenesse in which Paul could wound or kill his rival—thereby restoring his standing. In France, a husband who shoots his rival regains respectability. De Marsay claimed to hold 100,000 francs to apply to Paul’s principal debts and promised to help Paul obtain offices and funds that would make redemption possible. He proposed that the ship sail without Paul: stay, come back, and they would “play the game again” in the political arena, with de Marsay’s support to control Natalie and make her mother pliable. In short, de Marsay urged Paul to renounce exile, return, and let politics and a staged vindication restore him.

Paul, sailing beyond the Azores, read de Marsay’s letter and felt as if struck by a pickaxe. It demolished his hopes, illusions, and love. Where Natalie’s letter had aroused tenderness and faith, de Marsay’s brutal realism and proposal for a political and personal struggle invoked anger and despair. He cried aloud the name “Henri!” Many men would have been driven mad; Paul went to bed and slept that heavy sleep which follows immense disasters—the stupefied, overfull sleep that comes after utter collapse.
