Balzac, famed for detailed character studies and everyday life, rarely portrayed real people. In “The Great Man of the Provinces in Paris” some figures recall actual persons; in Beatrix he borrows recognizably: Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin) suggests George Sand, while Beatrix, Conti, and Claude Vignon evoke the Comtesse d’Agoult, Liszt, and critic Gustave Planche. The opening scene captures the customs of an old Breton family—a social order largely vanished—marking a shift to modernity and standing out in Balzac’s “Comedy of Human Life.”

I. A BRETON TOWN AND MANSION

Some Breton towns remain little touched by nineteenth‑century progress because poor links to Paris keep them insulated; new civilization is spectacle, older customs preserved. Across the provinces a traveler finds traces of different eras; many feudal towns declined yet retain their memory and character.

Guerande exemplifies preserved feudal life: walled and moated with intact battlements and gates. The mayor’s planting of poplars along the moat provoked outcry—the promenade had long been public. Unmodernized houses and quiet streets create an atmosphere of ancient customs where even a gendarme seems out of place. The public square displays traditional Breton costumes, sharply contrasting bourgeois and noble styles; social divisions remain rigid and fervently Catholic, resisting modern ideas.

Overlooking famed salt‑marshes—whose salt locals boast improves butter and sardines—Guerande is joined to France by two slow roads from Savenay and Vannes, its isolation reinforced by indifferent governance and residents’ acceptance.

Near the church stands the du Guaisnic mansion: crested archway, stables, grand wood‑worked hall, massive fireplace, dining room hung with fourteenth‑century tapestries, simple family rooms, and a half‑acre garden with sundial and spiral column—an emblem of old Brittany’s enduring spirit.

Early in May 1836 the du Guenic household consisted of Baron and Baroness du Guenic, the baron’s elder sister Mademoiselle Zéphirine, and their only son Calyste, twenty-one. The baron, Gaudebert-Calyste-Charles, was a seventy-three-year-old veteran of the Vendée and Brittany insurrections who had fought with Charette and La Rochejaquelein. He had prudently transferred his property to his sister before the war, returned to Guérande in 1813, married Fanny O’Brien (an Irish noblewoman), and Calyste was born the day Louis XVIII landed.

Marked by his turbulent life, tall, wiry, deeply wrinkled, with large scarred hands, the baron was illiterate except for his prayer book and dressed plainly—sturdy shoes, ribbed stockings, a coat bearing the cross of Saint-Louis. Serenity tempered a somnolent gravity; household talk centered on the exiled Bourbons, the Catholic faith, and Brittany’s future, with Calyste as their sole hope. The baron rigorously trained him and even took him to join the Duchesse de Berry’s restoration attempt—leaving his wife and sister uninformed—and upon returning observed, “The barons did not all do their duty,” a comment mixing pride and lingering obligation.

That evening the baron slept in his armchair while his wife read the newspaper. Fanny O’Brien, forty-two, retained delicate Irish beauty and warmth—refined features, elegant dress, ringleted hair, and a tender manner that softened her husband’s austerity. Nearby, blind Mademoiselle Zéphirine knitted; proud and cheerful, she refused cataract surgery to avoid burdening her brother and shared a close bond with the baroness. As the room warmed at sunset, Mariotte lit the lamp and Gasselin tended the horses and dogs—longtime retainers whose steady loyalty, quietly managed by Zéphirine, exemplified the household’s affectionate, duty-bound cohesion.

When night fell Gasselin asked if he was needed; the baron said he could go after prayers unless the ladies required him. They knelt while Mademoiselle du Guenic led the prayer. A knock admitted Monsieur Grimont, the middle-aged, plump, genial curé who acknowledged the du Guenics’ feudal status, asked about Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel’s tardiness, and mentioned a young chevalier seen at Les Touches. Mariotte cautioned against speaking of such visits before Pen-Hoel, who loved gossip.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel arrived thin and elderly, dressed in her youth’s fashion, carrying keys and a silver snuff-box. Of high Breton nobility whose influence had waned, she ran her estate frugally, admired for avarice, and sought to marry a niece to Calyste to link houses. Conservative and proprietary toward Calyste, she mistrusted new influences. As they prepared for an evening game, the rector fetched a basket and counters with childlike eagerness. A firm knock announced Chevalier du Halga—frail, dressed for his health, once a competent naval officer, now gentle and preoccupied with his little dog; emigration hardships had broken his health, though he remained respected. The rector’s cough during the prayer for the king quietly hinted at the town’s lingering political tensions.

Mouche is a simple five-card trick game with one card turned up as trump; the dealer owns that trump and may swap it for any card in hand, and players may exchange cards from the deck only in order. The highest card is Mistigris, the knave of clubs. Players may play or drop out (dropping costs only the stake); winners of tricks share the pot, while losers who fail to win a trick become a mouche, paying the full stake and enlarging the pot. Though straightforward, mouche engrosses, revealing greed and cunning.

At the Hôtel du Guénic the regulars begin with twenty counters each—five sous apiece—so the stake is five farthings, a not insignificant amount. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël and Mademoiselle Zéphirine treat the game as a kind of speculation. A local convention of September 1825 allows a player who loses ten sous to quit, but the chevalier and the baron undercut this by lending counters to losers so play continues. The arrival of Demoiselle de Kergarouët and her aunt raises stakes further: these young women, besotted with Calyste, care little for such sums. Evenings at mouche are punctuated by gossip about local and public events; players sometimes forget stakes, prompting accusations of cheating—often aimed at the rector. Nights can end abruptly, with departures empty-handed and muttered complaints. Attempts to introduce games like whist and Boston fail; the circle prefers mouche.

One night, while the rector deals, talk moves from the chevalier’s joked ailments to the Duc de Lenoncourt’s death and general mortality. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël announces the Comtesse de Kergarouët’s marriage and provokes talk of Mademoiselle des Touches, an actress of doubtful repute. The baroness and rector exchange anxious looks—muted when Pen-Hoël holds Mistigris. After the game the chevalier escorts Pen-Hoël home; she confides that Calyste seems infatuated with Mademoiselle des Touches. The baroness fears Calyste will be led astray; the rector warns Pen-Hoël that she intends to leave her fortune to the niece who marries Calyste and cautions that the actress is a dangerous, scandal-prone influence. The baroness accompanies him to the gate and hears only his footsteps fade into the night.

At home she finds the town already aware that her son Calyste frequently visits Mademoiselle des Touches—visits she had assumed private. Her attempts at maternal diversion fail; Calyste comes home later and later, once as late as midnight. Educated by the rector, raised noble and devout, he nonetheless seems drawn to modern influences and to des Touches’s charms. The baroness’s modest plans—a prudent marriage aided by an expected inheritance—seem threatened. The next day Calyste sleeps late; at dinner his dress suggests he will dine again at Les Touches. His sister and mother fret; he defends Mademoiselle des Touches, saying she rejected his love and offered friendship, and that she is a talented, unconventional artist deserving respect. The baron dismisses his wife’s fears; Guerande buzzes that des Touches is infatuated with Calyste and holds a mysterious sway, much to the rector’s disapproval.

Mademoiselle Félicité des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, is a celebrated writer whose life provokes both curiosity and scandal. Orphaned in the Revolution and raised by an aunt, she acquired a broad intellect through voracious reading. Rejecting traditional marriage to preserve her independence, she made her salon a hub for artists and thinkers. Drawn to Calyste’s innocence, her relationship with him awakens private longings that conflict with her public ambition and hard-won autonomy; she must weigh love against the life she has built.

A few hundred yards from Guérande, Brittany’s soil yields to salt marshes, dunes, and a narrow arm of sea separating the mainland from the Croisic peninsula. At the crossroads to Croisic stands Les Touches, a slaty stone and granite country house set among wind-sculpted pines. Its plain gray exterior—regular windows and a high roof with gables and dormers—faces the road on one side and the desert toward Croisic on the other; a brook runs from the park, crosses the road, and disappears into the sands.

Though the Revolution stripped the estate of feudal rights, locals still call it “the château.” Félicité restored the house but preserved its desolate aspect, marking the entrance only with brick gate columns. The ground floor follows an old country plan: an entrance hall converted into a billiard room opening onto a large salon and dining room; the kitchen communicates with the dining room through an office. Décor is restrained and elegant—gray-painted walls, mahogany furniture draped in green silk, buffets and horsehair chairs, and Audran engravings.

Upstairs, Félicité occupies a suite with a salon, a large bedroom, and two cabinets; another suite receives guests. Their rooms are modest; her own apartment displays luxury—Gobelin tapestries, heavy silk drapes, fine Louis XV pieces—while her study, furnished in modern mahogany and filled with intimate objects, contrasts with the bedroom’s opulence and hints at her complexity.

Beyond the house, the marshes, dunes, and distant ocean lend a melancholic, elevating air. Camille prefers Les Touches’ desolation to Guérande’s richness. Calyste, seeking refuge from Guérande’s constraints, clings to his attachment to Félicité—rooted in admiration and longing—despite earlier rejection. At Les Touches he encounters nineteenth-century music and literature that awaken him; Félicité nurtures his intellect and acts as a maternal mentor, giving music lessons and filling the house with creativity.

On one visit he hears Félicité improvise at the piano; she stops, then resumes as he enters, found in tears over Claude Vignon’s absence. He offers to search for Claude, but she asks him to stay, calling her tears a natural overflow and preparing to speak of her fraught relationship with Claude—their age difference, inevitable separation, and why she once rejected Calyste. He sits at her feet, eager to support her yet unaware of the full depth she will reveal.

“I promised you this tale of the past, and here it is,” begins Camille: a letter from the Marquise de Rochefide about Béatrix de Casteran. The Rochefide family sought prestige by marrying into an old house; the Comtesse de Montcornet recommended Béatrix, the youngest of the dowry-less Casterans, so their male line’s estate would be preserved. Béatrix, raised at Château de Casteran, married in 1828 at twenty. Camille warns that Béatrix’s originality, independence, ardor, and love of art ennoble her but expose a woman to risks men meet with less danger.

The match pleased the Rochefides and briefly won a peerage under Charles X, later lost in the July Revolution. Early marriage proved perilous: Rochefide mistook Béatrix’s reserve for frigidity, resumed bachelor habits, and society praised him for a quiet wife who adorned his salon. His vanity turned to jealous pettiness when she appeared indifferent to his affairs. Camille uses this to explain Béatrix’s later behavior: admiration curdling to suspicion, and a failed attempt to found a salon that produced only shallow company because she lacked time and experience. Beneath the missteps lay a grand soul—regal pride, metaphysical intellect, and musical sensibility.

Béatrix was striking—slender, fair, with a noble forehead and pale sea-green eyes whose severe lines could seem almost divine when well dressed. Camille teases young Calyste not to be taken in; by the time he notices, it will be too late.

She spent early married life quietly appraising men and life. Revolutionary disturbances bored her at her husband’s country estate. Back in Paris after the July Revolution she explored new doctrines but found the regime left no place for individual superiority; she chose personal happiness over abstract ideals. Then she met Gennaro Conti, a Neapolitan composer whose voice, manner, and appearance suggested genius but masked duplicitous, self-serving character. Camille, devoted to art yet clear-eyed about character, warned Béatrix; blinded by passion, she was undeterred. Conti rejoiced; Camille even lent him money for a journey to Italy. Béatrix left a note for her husband, departed with Conti, spent two years abroad, and wrote enchanting, adoring letters to Camille—the only woman she felt understood her.

Claude Vignon then appears: imposing yet haggard, with a high forehead and sarcastic mouth. Capable of deep original thought but often paralyzed by it, Claude’s destructive criticism and dry brusqueness unsettle the company. His sardonic asides offend Calyste, who leaves angry and pitying Félicité.

Calyste brings Béatrix’s letter home, alarming his mother. In it Béatrix describes Italy’s weary enjoyment, the strain of living with Conti, diminished social equality, sacrifices made for love, a longing for solitude, admiration for Italy’s beauty, and financial worries likely to force their return to Paris. Camille presents the correspondence as equal parts exaltation and disillusionment.

Melancholy, Calyste confesses that Félicité has opened his eyes to the insufficiency of his education and shown him a nobler life. His aunt suggests a prudent Breton match; he rejects such safety, yearning instead for the freedom and passion he associates with love. His mother, understanding, counsels pursuit of happiness tempered by prudence and moral aim.

After dinner Calyste returns to Les Touches, where Claude’s cynicism and Félicité’s generous defense of Béatrix—her endurance and nobility—captivate him. Camille’s speculation about Béatrix and Conti’s return stirs sympathy in Calyste. The scene closes on a tender family moment: encouragement mixed with grounding wisdom, and a young man’s yearning for passion and purpose that will shape his future.

What young man, full of restrained feeling, would not be eager to see Madame de Rochefide land at Croisic? Calyste surprised his parents by leaving early, moved by an unfamiliar vigor as he hurried along Les Touches’ walls to avoid being seen, ashamed that Félicité or Vignon might notice. At the shore he hid in a travelers’ shelter and watched two boats arrive from Croisic, laden with luggage marking distinguished passengers. One trunk stamped "MME. LA MARQUISE DE ROCHEFIDE" caught his eye, and he felt an immediate, inexplicable attraction, ready to transfer the affection he had once held for Camille.

While the luggage was unloaded he overheard servants expecting someone from Les Touches and learned the marquise had already been collected. Agitated, he hurried toward Guérande, skirting the servants. Consumed by thoughts of the marquise, he compared this fresh ardor with his earlier feeling for Camille. Returning home late, he found a letter from Mademoiselle des Touches inviting him to celebrate the marquise’s arrival; it mentioned the Casteran family and reassured his mother of the woman’s noble lineage.

At the gathering Calyste arrived in a tumult of feeling. In Camille’s suite he was introduced to Gennaro Conti, a man with a Byron-like presence. The marquise, reclining on a divan, surpassed his imagination: more beautiful than he had pictured, her hair braided with bluebells, elegantly dressed. Assigned to escort her at dinner, he fumbled and confessed he had never seen anyone so beautiful except his mother. Mademoiselle des Touches counseled restraint, warning the marquise could not return such fondness. As Conti and Camille performed, their music moved him to tears; the marquise turned away. Overwhelmed by admiration and despair, he slipped to a nearby sofa, painfully aware of his own inadequacy.

Calyste returned to find Claude Vignon waiting in his room. Claude, who had seen more than he admitted, told Calyste the situation was not what it seemed: he believed Camille loved Calyste. When Calyste asked about Claude’s own feelings, Claude confessed a weary bitterness—his marsh wanderings had sprung from wounded pride at thinking Calyste preferred Camille. He planned to leave Les Touches, perhaps for Paris with Conti, and warned Calyste that passions there would soon break into tumult. Beatrix was relentless, Camille noble and abstruse, and Calyste’s excess of feeling for one might prevent prudent use of the other. Disgusted with women yet clear-eyed, Claude advised caution: between those reefs Calyste might be wrecked.

Stunned to learn Camille loved him while he loved Beatrix, Calyste felt crushed, replaying earlier rejections, Claude’s return, and Beatrix’s laughter. At midnight Claude confronted Camille (Félicité). He accused her of arriving in Paris already in love with Calyste and of using Calyste as a screen—bringing Claude to Les Touches to hide her secret devotion. He called her scheme grand and ignoble, saying she had misjudged him as simple when he had divined her, and declared she had never been loved in the ordinary way because her mind was too deep and overpowering for strong men. She might please youth like Calyste briefly, he said, but adults grew fatigued; she was an anomaly. He would leave to spare her the shame of his continued presence as a screen, insisting they could part without the pain of betrayed hope because they had had no mutual happiness to mourn.

Calyste then revealed himself. Claude told him plainly that Camille loved him, not in the ordinary way but for the exquisite pain of longing—an ascetic, almost masochistic affection. Camille’s tears and agitation proved the truth. Claude accused her of driving Calyste toward Madame de Rochefide, saying Camille had dug her own grave by concealing her suffering; had she confided in him, some hurt might have been avoided. Moved, Camille kissed Calyste’s hair and begged him to forget what he had heard, insisting she “count[ed] for nothing” and commanding him not to love her while vowing, “I will love you as no woman will!” Claude knelt and kissed her hand; Camille dismissed Calyste to spare his mother concern.

Distraught, Calyste returned to Guerande, glancing at Beatrix’s window. That night he told his mother, tearful and abrupt, that Camille loved him but he no longer loved Camille. He then placed a watcher on the road and rode out with two horses. A carriage arrived with Parisian visitors: Beatrix, Camille, Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel, the Vicomtesse de Kergarouet, and Charlotte. Camille invited the new ladies to ride in her carriage; the viscountess, delighted to meet Camille Maupin, accepted. In the crowd Beatrix, elegant, leaned on a parasol; Camille took Calyste’s arm. The viscountess expressed pity for Madame de Rochefide’s misfortunes; Camille explained her separation from Conti was mutual. Calyste kissed Camille’s hand, shedding a tear; Beatrix, wiping anger from her eyes, looked away. Camille’s calm and grace soothed Calyste despite the earlier revelations.

At home the visitors dined and played games. Calyste’s momentary gaiety made others assume the worst was over and that prospects with Charlotte might succeed, but he remained inwardly troubled, comparing Parisians to childhood acquaintances. Only his mother perceived his unease and urged him to offer the viscountess a ride in Camille’s carriage; Calyste ran back to Les Touches to arrange it.

Later, after dinner, Camille teasingly accused him of abandoning the viscountess and her daughter. When Beatrix suggested the viscountess’s niece might suit Calyste, Camille promptly demurred: the marriage would never take place. Mortified by the marquise’s indifference toward the Breton girl, Calyste was silent. Camille, seeing his discomfort and her own flushed face, yielded him her hand briefly and then played melancholy pieces at the piano.

Beatrix watched him closely. In private, Camille took Calyste into her chamber and delivered candid counsel: if he wanted Beatrix, he must seem to love Camille still; otherwise Beatrix would be lost. Loving and making oneself beloved were different arts; candor would not serve him. She proposed a deliberate deception: promise blind obedience, spend every day with Camille until four, avoid private encounters with Beatrix, and allow Camille to work on Beatrix’s feelings. Camille trusted her ability to fashion the marquise’s heart; in a week Beatrix would love him, she boldly claimed. Calyste, naive and desperate, agreed, embraced Camille, and left gaily, convinced of success.

After he departed, Camille confessed to Beatrix the depth of her feeling: at forty she was in love, had mourned youth, feared being loved only from pity, and might fling herself into the sea if Calyste asked it—an image of her despair. Foreseeing how Beatrix’s coldness might secure Calyste, she pleaded for the marquise’s generosity and warned that she, too, feared losing him. Beatrix, piqued and curious, left with mixed feelings; Camille sank into tears and smoked opium-laced tobacco, brooding on the young lover’s head and the pain of her position.

The next morning Camille set the plan in motion. She told Calyste to stay to dinner but to show Beatrix a look of regret when she passed—a signal to conceal his devotion. For six days Camille employed every conversational art and subtle deception—the feints and false magnanimities of a duel of hearts—aiming to conceal her love while revealing enough to inflame Beatrix’s pride and curiosity. Her remarks, sometimes treacherous, often wounded Beatrix, who, though offended, was secretly amused to find such a great writer caught in pettiness and resolved to show where Camille’s greatness ended.

Camille’s tactics included assigning Calyste to study, keeping him constantly in her company, and hinting at Beatrix’s supposed indifference. Calyste lived for the daily moments before Beatrix and read late into the night, bringing home armfuls of books. His mother implored him to leave Les Touches, but he refused; he resolved to approach Beatrix privately in the garden to read her eyes.

After a marsh outing, Calyste met Beatrix on a garden path. He trembled and asked if he had displeased her. She replied faintly that he had neither pleased nor displeased her. When he implored whether he was indifferent to her, she asked if they should not be indifferent given their “binding attachment” elsewhere. Calyste, stunned, declared that he had once loved Camille but loved her no longer. Beatrix, hurt yet composed, noted that Calyste and Camille spent every morning together and suggested Camille did not prefer a cigar to his company. Camille then appeared and rebuked Calyste: was this what he had promised? She took his arm and drew him away—forcing him into a reciprocity of appearances that could be read as noble or ruinous.

At breakfast Beatrix determined to stifle any rising love and behaved with gentle indifference, following Camille’s advice. Félicité proposed excursions, but Beatrix refused, saying her position was delicate and she would not jeopardize her happiness because Conti was coming to fetch her. Calyste took the news with pallor, left Les Touches depressed and near madness at the thought of losing Beatrix, and spent his time pondering a decisive explanation to win her.

Throughout, strategy, pride, and genuine feeling intertwine. Claude, preparing to leave, remains the cynical observer who recognizes motives and predicts calamities. Camille, inwardly torn and capable of self-effacement, chooses to guide events through subtle deception, convinced she can shape Beatrix’s heart and preserve her claim to Calyste. Beatrix, proud and resolute, answers with politeness that may hide deeper sentiment but insists on autonomy. Calyste, immature and ardent, is trapped between youthful passion and artful manipulation, a pawn in the hands of stronger wills whose consequences he cannot yet foresee.

When Calyste returned home he locked himself away and wrote feverishly. His mother found him amid crumpled pages composing a letter to Béatrix, prompted by renewed distrust of Camille after the marquise’s recent conduct.

His first love letter poured out intense feeling: meeting Béatrix had awakened a love beyond any dream; he likened himself—youthful and unworthy—to an insect drawn to her light. He contrasted this profound passion with a former, lesser attachment to Félicité, cited Camille’s teaching that true love must grow and endure, and begged for kindness, recognition, or even the simple right to accompany her as a friend. Much of the letter repeated these themes; his mother was moved to tears and alarmed by his ardor.

Unsure how to deliver it, Calyste consulted the Chevalier du Halga, who suggested using Béatrix’s maid for discretion. Calyste instead presented it himself. At Les Touches he found Béatrix and Camille together and impulsively handed her the letter; she accepted without hesitation.

After a restless night he waited in the garden for her reply. Béatrix acknowledged his noble feelings but reminded him he was still a child and affirmed her commitment to Camille. She refused him—accepting his love would insult Camille—and warned that her jealous, capricious nature would likely make him unhappy, urging him to seek a more fitting girl.

The baroness, moved, read Béatrix’s reply aloud, admired its nobility but fretted for Calyste’s future and urged him toward Charlotte de Kergarouet to secure the estate. Calyste remained steadfast, wrote again imagining elopement and convinced love could defy constraints. The baroness, distressed, consulted the amused Chevalier, discovered another revealing letter, and prayed for her son. When he returned she confessed reading his letters; he reassured her and they planned a trip to Croisic to see Béatrix. The correspondence showed surprising maturity and clarity in Calyste yet underscored the intensity and likely consequences of his infatuation.

One of Beatrix’s chief pleasures was outwitting those she felt superior to, though she truly admired Camille Maupin’s talent and passion. While Calyste rushed to Les Touches intoxicated with first love, Beatrix enjoyed being the object of his devotion without planning to return it. She prided herself on repressing feeling, imagining a noble sacrifice like Camille’s; French vanities flattered her and she felt morally upright resisting Calyste.

Reclining in the salon with the calm garden outside, Camille and Beatrix nursed inner unrest. Camille, aware of the upheaval she’d caused, guarded her secret love and saw Beatrix as a shrewd rival, seeking refuge in nature and higher reflection—Calyste having become almost a guardian angel. Beatrix’s seeming triumph, however, disturbed her.

When Calyste arrived with a letter, Camille watched an exchange that suggested an unspoken understanding: Beatrix now treated Calyste as hers while he looked guilty. He announced an excursion; Camille, suspecting a conspiracy, grew anxious. After he left to arrange the boat and later returned, neither woman met his eye; he departed for home heavy with despair. Beatrix, meanwhile, found the letter stirring unexpected feelings and, suspecting a plot with Camille, felt jealousy and anger.

Dinner was tense. Veiled insults and hostile looks escalated: Beatrix accused Camille of trickery and cruelty; Camille exposed Beatrix’s motives. Beatrix attacked Camille’s lack of conventional femininity; Camille declared her deep love for Calyste. Beatrix answered she would not be his mistress—only marriage would do. Wounded, Camille broke off the friendship and threatened to write to Conti. Beatrix refused to yield. The night left both women reflective, their conflict unresolved between desire and social constraint.

Late August brought clear skies and shimmering sea; paludiers harvested salt while the baron and baroness admired the marshes. A party from Les Touches arrived: the marquise, Calyste, and Camille. The baroness felt aversion to Beatrix, who seemed heartless. They boarded a boat for Croisic; the passage was tense and silent. On the wind-sculpted peninsula, Calyste led Beatrix to a viewpoint while Camille sought solitude among the rocks.

Beatrix confessed inner desolation and insisted she could never be with Calyste despite loving him—afraid of abandoning her life and the pain of unfulfilled love. Calyste offered to sacrifice everything, but she rejected sacrifice and reaffirmed her commitment to her situation. Passion then overwhelmed Calyste; during an intimate moment Beatrix slipped and fell. He saved her; Gasselin and Camille helped, and the injured Beatrix was carried to a farmhouse.

Calyste stayed by her, despairing and hopeful as the doctor assured recovery. Moved by his devotion, Beatrix softened and, playing the invalid, relished his adoration while keeping distance, reminding him of an obligation to another man. Yet she accepted his vow of exclusive love and their bond deepened—only to be shattered on return to Les Touches by the sudden appearance of Conti, leaving love, loyalty, and consequences tangled and uncertain.

The marquise’s turmoil was evident when Conti arrived unexpectedly, took Beatrix’s arm, and forced her to leave Calyste, who felt humiliated and stunned. Convinced of his love, he was struck by her distress and fell silent.

Camille explained the tangled power dynamics: Beatrix could not refuse Conti because of lingering ties, and Conti was manipulative, using insinuations to regain control. His return after a failed opera appeared opportunistic—feigning indifference to Beatrix’s new attachment while subtly undermining her confidence. At dinner his jovial manner masked intent; veiled remarks about women’s loyalty wounded both Beatrix and Camille. Beatrix later told Camille she felt helpless and even blamed her for Conti’s return, exposing a deep rivalry. Beatrix insisted she would always hold a place in Calyste’s heart; Camille retorted that her reign was fleeting.

Conti and Calyste exchanged tense words. Conti posed as the injured party, urged Calyste to pursue Beatrix to provoke her pride, and concealed his true scheme. Unaware, Calyste confessed details of his intimacy with Beatrix, inflaming Conti’s jealousy.

That evening Conti staged a salon performance to provoke Beatrix and assert dominance. Entranced by Conti’s singing, Calyste approached Beatrix with renewed confidence—walking into Conti’s trap. The next morning Beatrix left with Conti; Calyste was devastated. Camille informed him and tried to expose Beatrix’s true nature, but he clung to hope.

Weeks passed and his sorrow deepened. He wandered aimlessly; his family mistook melancholy for a phase and worried as his health and social life declined. Pressured to marry, he refused Charlotte, confessed his love for Beatrix, and begged his family to release her. After the baron’s death Calyste sank further until Camille proposed helping him find Beatrix in Paris, and he, renewed, agreed.

Felicité’s love had set the stage for Calyste’s future. She wrote to the Duchesse de Grandlieu about him, sold her house for 2,500,000 francs, bought another for 700,000, and planned to use one million to recover the du Guenic estates, settling the remainder on Sabine de Grandlieu. Aware the duke and duchess sought advantageous matches for their daughters—only the youngest, Sabine, was marriageable—Felicité aimed to turn Calyste’s affections toward Sabine.

On the trip to Paris, Mademoiselle des Touches confided in the baroness, who prepared a house for Sabine and Calyste. Welcomed at the hôtel de Grandlieu, they received proper respect. Mademoiselle des Touches urged Calyste to enjoy Paris while she searched for Beatrix. The city’s bustle eased his obsession; noticing a resemblance between Beatrix and the lively Sabine, he grew drawn to her. By winter 1837 he seemed refreshed and listened to his mother’s proposal, though he concealed reluctance.

On the day the marriage contract was to be signed, Calyste surprised everyone by accepting the benefits offered by Mademoiselle des Touches but then reading a letter she had sent through Sabine. Camille professed love, declared her intention to enter a convent, urged him to accept her gifts for his welfare, and asked him to remember her kindly while placing duty above passion.

After reading the letter, Calyste rejoined the others and the contract was signed. The wedding at Saint Thomas d’Aquin followed the next week; Calyste and Sabine left for Brittany amid tears and congratulations. The duchess worried for Sabine, knowing Calyste had not wholly chosen the match. On the journey the newlyweds felt awkward; Calyste suggested they be like friends or siblings, which hurt Sabine. Gradually, however, she found comfort in his promise to forget Beatrix and, as châtelaine in Brittany, embraced estate duties and local life. A convent visit from Mademoiselle des Touches yielded blunt advice—be capricious when necessary and never let Calyste see Beatrix again—leaving Sabine conflicted about how to secure love, reputation, and identity in an uneasy marriage.

Guerande, July 1838.

To Madame la Duchesse de Grandlieu:

Sabine opens to her mother with the agony of jealousy after three months of marriage. A single admonition—“Here’s the key to your palace; go everywhere, but avoid Les Touches!”—has poisoned her peace. Everyone warns her: Les Touches is dangerous; stay away. Yet curiosity burns. Calyste never suggests visiting the hermitage-turned-property, but consenting when she asks, he leads her there. Les Touches charms and torments Sabine; its memories of Mademoiselle des Touches—Madame de Rochefide—stir a jealousy she cannot master. Calyste pleads that she stop reviving old passions; this plea inflames her further. She vows never to return to Les Touches, convinced that Béatrix (Madame de Rochefide) has sown ruin between them. Sabine alternates between despair and clinging joy, deciding even to live in the old château’s ruins rather than face a rival. She is sure her hatred of Béatrix proves her love for Calyste; yet the suspicion haunts her dreams. “If my terrors are not delusions,” she writes, “Camille Maupin has sold me her fortune dearly.”

These letters reveal the private breach: Sabine imagined mutual, consuming passion; Calyste accepted a conventional, social match. Their honeymoon’s early joys mask deep mismatch.

Back in Brittany, the hôtel du Guénic is restored under Grindot, supervised by Clotilde and the duchesse. The young couple plan a December return to Paris. Sabine delights in presiding over a great household and in Parisian life; Calyste, indifferent, lets others shape his social steps. He owes his place to name, fortune, and alliance. Celebrated for her charm, Sabine finds the season’s diversions stimulating. Her pregnancy and the birth of a son in October 1839 confirm a respectable domestic union; Sabine, idealizing motherly devotion, erroneously nurses the child herself.

By August 1840, when Sabine is nearly done nursing, Calyste has shed the innocence of early passion. He now keeps company with Duc Georges de Maufrigneuse, Vicomte Savinien de Portenduère, and other idle young men who trade domestic virtues for cigars, whist, and clubs. Paris life teaches him different rules; wealth offers amusements. A proud wife may tolerate such dissipation as the cost of success, but the scene is set for rupture.

One October evening, to escape their newly weaned child’s cries, Calyste goes to the Variétés at Sabine’s suggestion. Seated near the avant-scène during the interlude, he sees Béatrix in a proscenium box—Béatrix in Paris. The sight strikes him like an arrow. He had sometimes—vainly and irrationally—substituted Béatrix for his wife in thought: she stands for light, life, the Unknown, while Sabine embodies duty and the expected. The proscenium door is ajar; he approaches. Béatrix, now thin and faded but still theatrically charming, greets him with a mix of affected grief and coquettish nostalgia. She complains at his marriage and teases him about “such a little fool.” She revives old intimacy—pressing his hand and turning tears into performance—talking of modest living and literature.

Leaving the theatre with Béatrix’s perfume in his nostrils, Calyste is haunted all evening. The splendor of his apartments now feels obscene—wealth cannot belong to such a woman. Sabine’s pure love means she can detect every quiver; he delights in possessing a night to relive past emotions and fears the simple light with which she reads him.

The next morning Sabine, who watched the child through the night, is joyful at the baby’s recovery. Calyste avoids breakfast, preferring a walk to a bachelor café. Vanity, the thrill of triumph, and the desire to revive a conquering youth draw him to Béatrix despite morals. He goes to rue de Chartres, to the house that seems familiar though he has never been there. Béatrix, reduced from fortune but still tastefully living, greets him in studied attire—gown scarcely modest, lace cap, and the perfume of Les Touches. Her rooms hold elegant mementos: garnet velvet, celadon vases, a silver cup attributed to Benvenuto Cellini, trinkets, and a paper-knife fashioned from a dagger handle—tokens of a wandering life.

She behaves like a lover: calling him, teasing him about his wife’s simplicity, and turning their meeting into a test. Three hours pass with her urging him to resolve his conjugal faith and treating Sabine as a “stiff, dry, brown woman” unfit for a man like Calyste. Pressed, he rises to leave; she tempts him to dine with her. When he starts to write a note to Sabine, Béatrix scolds his “My dear Sabine”—mocking his evasion—and forces him to write that he dined out, to be sent by her messenger. He returns home at two in the morning. Sabine, who had waited until half-past twelve, sleeps exhausted, then wakes worried by the terse note but consoles herself with the charity that love explains all.

The next morning the child is well and Sabine brings the baby before breakfast; Calyste overplays affection. In the salon she catches his face in a mirror and sees weariness. She asks, “What did you do yesterday?” He lies: Savinien kept him to dinner and they played whist. She reproaches the coldness of his note; small suspicions accumulate. She remembers throwing a paper into the letter-box—perhaps coroneted paper—and her unease grows.

Calyste, planning concealment, rushes back to rue de Chartres to see Béatrix again. She appears fresh after a bath, vivacious over eggs and conversation. He stays until late; his horse returns sweating, which Sabine notices from a window. Meeting him on the stair she says sharply: “Abd-el-Kader is nearly foundered. You said you’d be gone a moment. I waited three hours.” Calyste, attempting recovery, presents a dressing-table finished for her with contributions from his mother and aunt. Sabine, at first joyful, suddenly recoils—she smells a perfume on his cravat, a scent of another woman’s note-paper. Panic grips her. “Fetch the doctor and my nurse!” Calyste runs to call them.

Sabine, seized by chills and convulsions, cries, “I am dying!” Her maid fetches Madame de Portenduère; in desperation Sabine writes to her mother—an ordinary letter of thanks that cannot still her racing mind: “My God! my God! my God!” She fears betrayal after three years and twenty-two years of age; her teeth chatter, her face pales. She rummages the letter-box, seizes the thrown-away paper with a coronet—could it belong to a marquise? She begs Ursula to find out if Madame de Rochefide has returned to Paris.

Ursula, stunned, goes to the duchess, pledging secrecy. The duchess, experienced in women’s crises, advises a beneficial deception: “Sabine loves her husband; she might become insane.” They arrive at Sabine’s before the accoucheur Dommanget and Calyste. The duchess declares Béatrix absent from Paris and invents a lie to protect Sabine: Calyste has lost a great deal of money at cards and is guilty of reckless association, not adultery. She scoffs at the coronet—“Everybody writes on coroneted paper now”—and burns the paper.

When Calyste and Dommanget arrive, the duchess confronts them: “Sabine’s life is at stake; you have betrayed her for Madame de Rochefide.” Calyste, embarrassed, allows the invented explanation—that he lost thirty thousand francs at cards—to stand; Dommanget uses it to label Sabine’s fainting as hysterical symptoms from nursing and anxiety. He prescribes rest and comforts. Calyste sits at the foot of the bed trying to look tender; Sabine, hearing the explanation, retorts that her husband could lose any sum without being lectured. Calyste embraces her and calls her an angel. Two days later, apparently safe, Sabine improves.

Calyste then visits Béatrix, making a boast of his infamy. He tells her that Sabine nearly died because of the discovery: the paper, the perfume—Sabine’s milk was affected; erysipelas set in; she may bear marks for life. Béatrix’s face freezes; she answers with icy cruelty: “So much the better… perhaps it will whiten her for you.” Her sarcasms cut deep. She forces him to beg forgiveness for two hours, then scorns his shame, asking why he had the insolence to speak to her of his virtuous wife the day after her fall. She declares her love for his soul while sneering at his looks.

This cruelty is part of Béatrix’s system. Having fallen from fortune by Conti’s desertion and now thankful for scraps from friends, she resolves to win a terrible notoriety as revenge: fame and a sort of triumph over the young and splendid. To outshine a radiant wife like Sabine, she uses the arts of courtesans—contempt mixed with rare affection, humiliating comparisons, and cultivated disdain—to stimulate conquest. She aims to make Calyste feel himself ugly and unwelcome, thereby reviving his desire to conquer. She contrasts her poetical modest home with the hôtel du Guénic’s Faubourg Saint-Germain luxury, painting the latter stupid and empty. In a bower of flowers and rare plants, amid frugal sumptuousness, she molds the scene of reconciliation in which he must swear hatred for his wife and frame her as a mere actress in domestic virtue. Reduced but determined, Béatrix seeks at least the triumph of notoriety; Sabine’s misfortune becomes her pedestal.

When Sabine returns to ordinary life after nursing her first child she seems refreshed but finds her husband Calyste changed. A letter from her mother hints at his discontent and names another woman, Beatrix, confirming Sabine’s worst fears. The vague note offers no explanation; in despair Sabine scribbles a remark on it and leaves it where Calyste will see it. He finds the comment, ignores her, and burns the paper, humiliating her and deepening her distress.

Beatrix’s presence becomes unmistakable when the perfume on a later letter betrays her. Recognizing it, Sabine feels the rival’s hold. In a febrile moment she dances with her child, clinging to the innocence of motherhood, then weeps as Calyste departs to read Beatrix’s message. Her attempts to win him back—decorating the house with flowers, improving her cooking and appearance—meet only indifference, which intensifies her jealousy and self-doubt.

Despite her suffering, Sabine’s love persists; she tries to be the devoted wife but finds these virtues ineffective. After another failed attempt to move Calyste she collapses, tears off a beautiful dress, and her health falters. The duchess, her mother, notices and tries to distract her. Sabine, fearing interference, at first keeps silent but eventually confides in her sister Athenais, speaks of her anguish, and asks that her child be cared for should she die. Warned by experience, Sabine counsels Athenais to be cautious in love, lamenting that her straightforwardness and passion now obstruct happiness and push her toward duplicity.

Alarmed, the duchess consults Abbé Brossette and reveals a plan to break Calyste’s attachment and restore Sabine. The abbé cautions against mixing spiritual authority with worldly intrigue, but the duchess, determined to spare her daughter further suffering, resolves to act even if it means morally questionable measures.

A parallel story shows how society treats men and women differently. Monsieur de Rochefide, unaffected by Beatrix’s departure, continues to relish Parisian life. Arthur de Rochefide, now sole owner of the hôtel de Rochefide after his sister’s death, inherited a large fortune and the comfortable habit of living for pleasure: vanity, wit, clubs, fashionable causes, and costly racehorses kept by an English groom. He claims a natural luck—“born with a caul”—and lacks much taste or intellectual rigor.

After several affairs he marries Madame Schontz, La Petite-Aurélie, whom he met at Valentino’s. Their relationship evolves in four phases: courtship, when he provides allowance and lodgings while she displays charm and ambition; consolidation, as she upgrades the home and runs the household; motherhood and financial stewardship, where she oversees their son’s education and investments; and social management, building their circle and reputation while quietly amassing a modest fortune through prudent economy and discreet ventures. In each phase Madame Schontz shows tactical superiority, turning the match into mutual benefit while skillfully navigating society.

Such conduct implied a plan, and Madame Schontz had one. Jealous of Madame du Bruel for two years, she was resolved to remarry in both church and civil ceremonies — in short, to secure a second Arthur. Her secret rival was unknown: Bixiou suspected Léon de Lora, others named Victor de Vernisset or Stidmann. No one truly understood Madame Schontz, whose single aim was a good marriage.

The only man of dubious repute in her salon was Couture, a fallen stock-gambler but loyal. When he introduced Fabien du Ronceret, a provincial, Madame Schontz took notice. Fabien, son of a chief justice, had left a judgeship in Alençon for Paris with a modest income and a knack for exploiting social mores. He prized pride, vanity, and notoriety, staging a conspicuous Paris debut with a lavish apartment and greenhouse to be talked about.

Seeking entrée to Madame Schontz’s circle, he relied on Couture, who recommended him. Madame Schontz judged Fabien suitable for her ambitions, flattered him, and led him to believe he was the object of her affection. Bewitched, he dressed his best. A month before a key meeting she revealed her identity and aims; Fabien misunderstood. She then took him on a carriage ride, declaring she wanted a husband with ambition who would value her character. Despite her wealth and encouragement to rise socially, Fabien hesitated; when Couture learned of her fortune, he promptly proposed.

Meanwhile the Duchesse de Grandlieu enlisted Comte Maxime de Trailles for a delicate family matter. Maxime agreed on the condition that the duchess accept his future wife into her circle and demanded her obedience as he planned to eliminate a rival. He noted Calyste’s infatuation with Béatrix and the moral slipperiness of manipulating it. Rochefide told Madame Schontz of Maxime’s impending visit; she readied house and appearance, aware his reputation promised both privilege and challenge.

Maxime then summoned Finot to arrange a breakfast at the Café Anglais with Couture and Lousteau to discuss Madame Schontz. Feigning business with du Tillet, he waited for the Comte de la Palferine, probed his debts, admired his spirit, and offered a loan of twenty thousand francs to bankroll a courtship. Targeting a well-bred marquise, he urged La Palferine to woo sincerely; they agreed to meet later at Madame Schontz. He reassured the duchess the campaign would be discreet.

At a dinner Madame Schontz hosted Claude Vignon and La Palferine; she insisted she was an artist seeking a respectable marriage, not a courtesan. Maxime praised her success with Rochefide, privately funded La Palferine, and gave exacting advice on making a memorable impression. He and Madame Schontz schemed to sway Rochefide; Maxime vowed a post for Couture in return.

Meanwhile Calyste, feeling neglected, confronted Béatrix, who was torn between genuine feeling for him and a growing attraction to La Palferine. As Maxime arranged encounters that deepened Béatrix’s entanglement, Calyste’s attempts to reclaim her failed, culminating in her public humiliation at the opera. Loyalties shift and desires are manipulated, exposing how intrigue, vanity, and ambition breed disillusionment and force characters to reassess their pasts and futures.
