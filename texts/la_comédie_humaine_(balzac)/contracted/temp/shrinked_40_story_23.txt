There is a particular hour between day and night that artists adore: twilight blurs edges, softens contrasts, and invites a dreamy mood in which light and shade converse. Silence deepens contemplation; the artist, stepping back from a nearly finished canvas, sees meanings flash before the inner eye. In that hour, material art seems to lose its matter—figures speak and walk, shadow is shadow, light is day; flesh breathes, eyes move, and fabrics change sheen. The imagination magnifies every realistic detail and finds beauty alone. Illusion rules, summoning a mind’s night filled with fancies that lift the soul away from the mundane.

One such evening a young, gifted painter—who saw art as Art itself—worked on a large painting from a step-ladder, rapt with self-criticism and admiration. His reverie lasted until night had fallen. Whether he meant to step down or misjudged his footing, the ladder tipped; he fell, struck his head on a footstool, and lay unconscious.

A sweet voice roused him. When he opened his eyes a bright light hurt them; through a mist he heard two women whisper and felt two timid hands supporting his head. He regained consciousness to an old Argand lamp’s light and saw the most charming face he had ever seen: a young woman epitomizing the ideal beauty his imagination had fashioned—delicate as Prudhon, poetically sentimental like Girodet, with fresh temples, regular brows, virginal purity. She was slight, graceful, simply dressed.

Gratitude and admiration came fumbling to his lips. A handkerchief pressed his forehead; the ether used to revive him left a pungent odor. An older woman, bearing a marquise’s air, held the lamp and instructed the younger. The girl explained they had heard the crash and, alarmed by an ensuing silence, entered the studio through a latched door to find him lying motionless. The mother had fetched what was needed to bathe and revive him; he had cut his forehead but felt better. The older woman immediately advised leeches or bloodletting the next day—falls could be dangerous.

The girl's glances at him and at the pictures were discreet, proper, almost absent-minded; her eyes showed the natural interest women take in suffering. The two strangers were more occupied with his safety than his work, and they left without pressing questions or brazenness, looking back with anxious kindness. Their noble, simple manners made a deep impression on him later when memory pulled at details.

On the landing, the old lady gently chided her daughter for leaving the door open; the painter joked that she had left it “to hear me fall.” The girl blushed. They offered to accompany him downstairs because the stairs were dark; he declined, and they stayed by to light his way.

To appreciate the scene’s effect on the painter, one must know his circumstances. A few days earlier Hippolyte Schinner had set up a studio in the attics of that very house, in a dim, muddy quarter near the Madeleine and his rooms on the Champs-Élysées. Fame had recently showered him with success: he had become one of the country’s most acclaimed artists and was beginning to feel free from want—“enjoying his last privations,” as he put it. No longer willing to waste time or endure long walks to cheaper studios, he had taken the attic.

Hippolyte Schinner was a man who inspired fascination without revealing his secrets. He was the idol of a mother who had sacrificed all for him. Mademoiselle Schinner, an Alsatian farmer’s daughter, had never married. Once betrayed by a wealthy suitor who treated her without delicacy, she renounced the world, refused alms from him, and made a nobility of her shame. She devoted herself to maternal love, saved a treasure for her son, and her sacrifices were rewarded: at the last exhibition Hippolyte received the Legion of Honor; newspapers hailed a genius, artists acknowledged a master, and dealers festooned his canvases with gold.

At twenty-five he understood his position. He lived to restore to his mother the pleasures she had renounced, hoping fame and fortune would make her happy and respected. He chose his friends among the honorable and distinguished, fastidious in selection because he wanted to elevate the status his talent had given him. Early solitude had left him with beliefs and an adolescent soul open to shy, poetic emotions that worldliness calls foolish but which were sincere in their singleness.

Schinner’s manners were gentle and polite; his voice, from the heart, stirred noble sentiments and bespoke modesty. Those who met him felt a moral attraction they could not name. These traits explain why, when the porter’s wife returned with a coach, he did not press her for gossip about the two women who had tended him. He replied to her chatter but could not stanch her curiosity.

She said they were likely Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, who had lived in the building four years. No one knew their means: in the mornings an old half-deaf woman came to help; in the evenings two or three elderly gentlemen with ribbon loops—visitors—stayed late. One man came in a carriage with servants and reportedly had sixty thousand francs a year. They lived quietly and economically, paying promptly. Their names did not match: the mother and daughter bore different surnames. The girl dressed smartly for walks in the Tuileries and attracted many suitors whom she turned away; yet gossip flourished.

After the coach came and went, and after his mother tended his wound and forbade his studio return for a few days, Hippolyte’s idle hours drew his memory back to the fainting scene. Adelaide’s profile returned vividly, the mother’s faded features, the gentle touch—gestures, tones, and attitudes rose from deep waters. Once he could work again, he went early to the studio but truly pressed to visit his neighbors. Passion makes strange promises; he mounted to the fourth floor, heart quick, and stood before Mademoiselle Leseigneur’s humble brown door, imagining shared misfortunes of birth and feeling a kinship.

No painter of manners has sufficiently portrayed certain Parisian privacies: elegant outward toilettes and brilliant women whose homes betray ambiguous comfort. The house belonged to a proprietor who hated repairs and decoration, regarding property purely as business. Chimneys might fall and he would forgo theatre nights for “repairs.” Hippolyte, having seen similar poverty in his mother’s home, was not shocked by dark greasy paint, oily stains, and other signs of neglect—these marks of scarcity held a kind of poetry for an artist.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur opened the door, bowed with Parisian tact, and, with discreet pride, turned to shut a glass partition that screened a cluttered back room—linen over irons, an old camp bed, charcoal, a filter, crockery—domestic utensils. Muslin curtains hid the disorder; windows faced a neighboring yard. With a quick eye he took in uses, furniture, and the general condition. The front half of the main room, an ante-room and dining-room, wore old salmon-rose paper with a patched flock border. Prints of Alexander’s battles, gilding rubbed, adorned the walls. A heavy, timeworn mahogany table stood amid chairs whose carved mahogany and red morocco seats, gilt nails and all, were scarred like veterans.

The room presented a museum of inconsistency: luxury and penury mingled. A finely finished telescope hung near discolored glass; a painted sideboard pretended to be mahogany. Shabby rugs and worn furniture had a hard cleanliness that accentuated their oldness. An indescribable odor mingled with stairwell smells despite a partly open window. Dust stirred through curtains carefully drawn to conceal a window bay where former tenants had left attempted ornaments—a domestic fresco of faded taste.

Adelaide opened the inner room and announced the painter with evident pleasure. Hippolyte, who had known similar poverty at home, entered with sympathy. Madame Leseigneur rose from an antique armchair to offer him a seat. He thanked them for their care and especially the young girl who had heard him fall. Adelaide lit an Argand lamp and placed it on the chimney shelf, sitting slightly behind him to view him while appearing to adjust the lamp; the tactic embarrassed both.

While speaking with Madame Leseigneur, Hippolyte scanned the room. Egyptian iron fire-dogs were scarcely visible under cinders before a sham log; an old Aubusson carpet, faded and mended, left cold tiles exposed. Walls imitated figured silk in reddish paper; opposite the windows a crack and the paper’s outline suggested double doors closing a recess where Madame Leseigneur likely slept, disguised by a sofa. Above a mahogany chest hung a dim portrait of an officer, painted abroad perhaps; window curtains of red silk matched the faded worsted work of chairs. On the chest’s marble sat a malachite tray with a dozen magnificently painted coffee cups—perhaps Sèvres. On the chimney shelf stood an Empire clock: a warrior in a quadriga whose wheel marked the hours. Tall candlesticks held smoke-yellowed tapers; porcelain vases crowned with dusty artificial flowers stood in corners.

In the middle of the room a card-table with new packs posed a dilemma: either these women were impeccably honest, or they earned a living through intrigue and gambling. Yet Adelaide’s pure manner and noble bearing inclined Schinner to believe in their innocence, to attribute the furniture’s incoherence to legitimate, if strained, means.

“Make a little fire and bring me my shawl,” the mother asked. Adelaide wrapped her in a cashmere shawl, faded and darned, and revived the fire with wood from the lumber-room.

The subsequent conversation is hard to reproduce exactly. With tact born of early hardship, Hippolyte avoided indiscreet pity. The two women began by talking about painting; women readily dissolve first-call awkwardness by questioning an artist. Light talk gave Hippolyte room to speak, reveal his habits and mind. The mother’s face bore the traces of trouble—prominent features, an outline suggesting shrewdness softened by grace—an expression that might be artifice or delicacy. Female faces conceal mysteries: frankness can border on duplicity. The line between intrigue and heart is inscrutable; only intuition discerns.

At Schinner’s age the heart’s first impulse is to believe in goodness. Studying Adelaide’s noble brow and soulful eyes, he breathed the modest fragrance of virtue. He seized the moment to discuss portraits and examine a faded pastel whose chalk had flaked away.

“You are attached to that picture for the likeness,” he said. “The drawing is poor.”

“It was done at Calcutta, in great haste,” the mother answered, her voice agitated. She stared at the formless sketch with the absorption of someone remembering past happiness; regret left its mark on her face. Schinner read in her gaze the trace of a once-happy life and sat beside her.

“Madame,” he said, “the pastel’s colors will soon disappear; the portrait will only survive in your memory. Allow me to reproduce the likeness on canvas. It would be more permanent—and a pleasure to me. Artists sometimes welcome a simpler task as a respite from larger works.”

The old lady flushed; Adelaide shot a glance of deep feeling. Schinner sought a tie with his neighbors that would allow him into their life. His offer appealed to affection and his pride as an artist, and seemed inoffensive. Madame Leseigneur accepted with composed dignity, aware of the delicate obligations such favors create.

“You are right,” he added. “The uniform looks naval.”

“Yes,” she replied. “That was the uniform of a captain in command—Monsieur de Rouville—my husband. He died at Batavia from a wound received while fighting an English ship off the Asiatic coast. He commanded a frigate of fifty-six guns; the Revenge carried ninety-six. The struggle was unequal, but he defended his ship bravely, held out till nightfall and escaped. When I returned to France, Bonaparte was not yet in power, and I was refused a pension. When I applied again, lately, I was told that if the Baron de Rouville had emigrated I should not have lost him; that by now he would have been a rear-admiral; his Excellency quoted degrees of forfeiture. I dislike holding out my hand as a beggar for a grief that silences a woman. I resent this monetary valuation of irreparable blood—”

“Dear mother, this subject always harms you,” Adelaide murmured. The Baronne bowed and fell silent.

At that moment Adelaide observed, with a touch of reproach and innocence, that she had supposed a painter’s work to be quiet—reminding Schinner of his earlier crash. A carriage arriving at the door interrupted them. Adelaide went to fetch tall gilt candlesticks with partly burnt candles, lit them, and without waiting for the bell opened the outer door, setting down the lamp. A kiss given and taken reached Hippolyte’s ear and touched him. He was impatient to see the man who had kissed Adelaide; conversation in undertones delayed the reveal.

At last Mademoiselle de Rouville returned, followed by two men whose dress and countenances demanded description. The first, about sixty, wore a coat styled for Louis XVIII—neither purely civil nor military—with fleurs-de-lis embroidered on the lapels and gilt buttons. His powdered hair showed no hat’s pressure; he might be taken for fifty, robust, with a frank, loyal émigré expression tinged with libertine gallantry—the reckless air of a Musketeer. His gestures and manner betrayed no intention of renouncing royalism, religion, or love affairs; he suggested the unrepentant world of the émigré aristocrat.

A striking figure stood behind the portrait of “Louis XIV’s light infantry”—the Bonapartists’ nickname for those old Monarchy survivors. He should have been the picture’s principal subject, yet he was merely an accessory: a lean, dry man dressed like the first but seeming his reflection—an older coat, less white powder, dimmer fleurs-de-lis, feebler intellect, life nearer its end. Rivarol had called him “the moonlight” of the other. The painter found this speechless Chevalier mysterious. He spoke to no one and was spoken to by none. Friend, relation, servant, protector—his role was obscure. At the Baronne de Rouville’s he provoked curiosity without satisfying it. Who, after the Restoration, could recall an attachment that before the Revolution had bound him to the dead woman who had been his friend’s wife?

The leader of the group, least dilapidated, gallantly kissed Madame de Rouville’s hand and sat by her. The Chevalier bowed and stood a couple of chairs away. Adélaïde stood behind the old gentleman’s armchair, leaning her elbows on the back. Though the old gentleman’s familiarity was fatherly, it seemed to annoy the girl. “What, are you sulky with me?” he asked, then shot Schinner a shrewd glance asking, “Is he one of us?”

“This is our neighbor,” said the Baronne, indicating Hippolyte. “Monsieur is a celebrated painter; despite your indifference to the arts, you must have heard his name.”

The old man saw the suppressed name and bowed. “Certainly. I heard much about his pictures at the last Salon. Talent has immense privileges,” he added, noting the artist’s red ribbon. “That distinction, which we must earn at the cost of blood and long service, you win in youth; but all glory is of the same kind,” he said, touching his Cross of Saint-Louis.

Hippolyte murmured thanks and fell silent, content to admire Adélaïde. Her face gleamed in his mind; he forgot the misery of the room. He answered briefly, inwardly divided between what he heard and what he felt—hope pouring out wild visions of happiness. He refused to analyze the evening’s small incidents.

After a while the Baroness and daughter began to play cards with the old gentleman. The Chevalier stood behind, watching the game and responding with nods and repeated gestures. The gentleman complained of bad luck; Adélaïde sweetly offered advice. At last the game ended. The old man flung two louis on the table in temper: “Forty francs—the exact sum. Deuce take it! It is eleven o’clock.”

“It is eleven o’clock,” repeated the silent figure, looking at the painter.

Hippolyte thought it time to go. He found a few commonplace remarks, took leave, and left wholly possessed by the first raptures of true love, without analyzing the evening.

The next day he longed to see Adélaïde again. He waited until the afternoon. Blushing, he asked Mademoiselle Leseigneur for the Baron’s portrait. “But come in,” Adélaïde said; she had heard him. Tongue-tied, he felt happiness dull his wit. To see Adélaïde, to hear her skirt’s rustle after longing all morning, made trifles precious. Adélaïde brought a chair to reach the picture; finding she could not reach without stepping on a chest, she blushed and asked, “Will you get it down?”

He read her modesty, returned a look of mutual intelligence—love’s sweetest language. Adélaïde cast down her eyes. Hippolyte, almost timid, took down the picture, examined it, and left saying only, “I will return it soon.” The brief contact set long ripples in both hearts.

Back in his studio he prepared a fresh canvas and, with ardor, worked until dinner. That evening he returned, stayed from nine till eleven, and, a little bolder, chatted with Adélaïde. A week passed and their feelings slowly deepened into mutual understanding. Each day Adélaïde’s greeting grew more intimate; they laughed and talked with the innocence of newly made friends. Schinner learned piquet poorly and lost often. Without a word of love they knew they were everything to each other: Hippolyte enjoyed his power over his gentle friend; Adélaïde, timid and devoted, permitted many concessions. Familiarity with the old Count waned: Adélaïde perceived the painter’s melancholy when the old man unceremoniously kissed her hand or throat.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur began to expect accounts of the painter’s visits and scolded him for absences; he ceased other friendships and lived chiefly in the small apartment that enchanted him. Adélaïde’s jealousy flared when she heard he still visited Paris salons at eleven; she declared such life bad for his health and argued that a man who spent charms on several women could not love warmly. Driven by passion and Adélaïde’s demands, the painter lived almost exclusively with them.

Never was love purer. Both were trusting and delicate; their passion grew without sacrifice. Two months after meeting, their lives were nearly one: Adélaïde could tell by his footsteps that he was home; he always looked in when he returned to his mother and flew back at evening. He thought the ideal she dreamed of was realized.

The old gentleman came less often; Hippolyte, once jealous, had taken his place at the green table and shared his constant ill-luck. Yet sometimes, in happiness, Madame de Rouville’s desperate poverty haunted him. “Strange—twenty francs every evening?” he wondered, and dared not vocalize his odious suspicions.

He spent two months on Adélaïde’s portrait; when finished, varnished, and framed, he considered it one of his best. Madame de Rouville had not mentioned it again. Was it indifference or pride? He would not analyze her silence. Joyfully plotting with Adélaïde, he planned to hang it while the Baroness was out. One day, while her mother walked in the Tuileries, Adélaïde came to his studio to see the portrait in its light. She stood speechless in rapt admiration. He leaned forward uneasily; she held out her hand, two tears fell, and he kissed it. They stood mute, longing to confess love but not daring. Overcome, they went downstairs and hung the picture. Hippolyte dined with the Baroness, who, in tears, embraced him.

That evening the old émigré, the Baron de Rouville’s comrade, visited to announce a promotion to vice-admiral; his land travels through Germany and Russia had been counted as naval campaigns. Seeing the portrait, he shook Hippolyte’s hand and exclaimed he would give five hundred pistoles to see himself as well depicted. The Baroness, grateful, looked at Hippolyte. He suspected the admiral meant to buy both portraits; his pride and jealousy were offended, and he replied sharply that if he were a portrait-painter he would not have done this one. The admiral bit his lip and sat down to cards.

Hippolyte stayed near Adélaïde, who proposed a dozen hands of piquet. As he played he noticed an unexpected eagerness in Madame de Rouville to win and finger the old man’s gold. Evil suspicions troubled him: could the Baroness live by gambling? Was she losing to pay debt or rent? Why would so rich a man frequent a fourth-story room where he lost some forty francs each evening? Why had he been familiar with Adélaïde and then given up any claim?

These reflections led him to watch the old man and the Baroness more closely. Their sidelong glances at Adélaïde displeased him. “Am I being duped?” became his last, horrible thought. He resolved to stay after the two old men left to confirm or dispel his fears. He drew out his purse to pay and, carried away by thought, laid it on the table and fell into reverie; then, ashamed, he rose, answered a commonplace question, and went close to the Baroness to look at her as she talked.

He stepped out, racked by doubts. A few steps down, he turned back to fetch the forgotten purse. “I left my purse here!” he cried to Adélaïde.

“No,” she answered, reddening.

“I thought it was there,” he said, pointing to the card-table. Not finding it, shamed for Adélaïde and the Baroness, he felt in his waistcoat and said, “I must have made a mistake.”

The purse held fifteen louis d’or and change. The theft seemed flagrant and was denied; Hippolyte no longer doubted his neighbors’ morals. He stood on the stairs, legs shaking, dizzy, unable to walk, struggling with his destroyed hopes. He recalled trifling observations that now confirmed his suspicions. Had they waited until the portrait was given them? Perhaps the Baroness had used gambling to hide her need. He even remembered Adélaïde’s curiosity about a worn silk netting—had she been watching his purse? He briefly fantasized other motives—maybe the admiral hoped to marry Adélaïde—but rejected them: if she intended to marry him, they would not have robbed Hippolyte.

Clinging to illusion, he told himself the purse must have fallen, then searched his memory and saw it distinctly on the green cloth. Yet he excused Adélaïde, thinking misfortune might explain a degrading action; perhaps some secret misfortune forced them. He refused to accept the proud face as a lie.

The next morning he had not slept. The loss of the future they might have shared made deep inroads. To lose a dreamed future is keener than losing real happiness. Thus Hippolyte wandered under the Tuileries’ shade, absorbed and forgetful.

There he met François Souchet, a schoolmate about to go to Italy. “Why, Hippolyte, what ails you?” the sculptor asked.

“I am most unhappy,” Hippolyte admitted.

“Only a love affair can make you so,” Souchet replied. Led into confidences, Hippolyte told him of the Rue de Suresnes and the young girl on the fourth floor. Souchet laughed and recalled what others knew: the mother’s appearance gave away her station. Jokes and mockery followed; Hippolyte’s reticence made him suffer as his secret was handled lightly. He pretended contradiction, but the jesting continued. “Come at four and study their walk,” Souchet advised.

Torn, Hippolyte left his friends, convinced Adélaïde must be superior to such accusations and filled with remorse for suspecting her. His love was such that, despite the theft, he still worshiped her. He thought of playing the rescuer of a woman deserted by others—romantic and grand to his sensitive soul.

He sat in his studio, looking at the portrait without working, tears in his eyes. Night came; roused, he went downstairs, met the admiral, looked darkly, and fled. He had planned to see the ladies, but the admiral’s presence froze him. For the hundredth time he wondered what interested this prodigal, who had eighty thousand francs a year, in a fourth-story room where he lost forty francs nightly; motives multiplied in his mind.

In the next days Hippolyte threw himself into work to distract himself; study consoled him though memories of tender hours persisted. One evening, leaving his studio, he saw the ladies’ door half open and Adélaïde standing in the window recess. The door and staircase made it impossible not to be seen. He bowed coldly with a look of supreme indifference; yet thinking of the pain this coldness would cause, he shuddered. The week of disdain would have been terrible; perhaps the purse had been found and she had been seeking him. This hope filled him with remorse: perhaps Adélaïde deserved an inquiry and at least justification. Ashamed of resisting his heart, he called that evening on Madame de Rouville.

All his suspicions vanished at the sight of Adélaïde, pale and thin. “Good heavens! what is the matter?” he asked.

Adélaïde gave him a look of deep melancholy that pained him. “You must have thought us indifferent?” said the Baroness.

“I was wrong,” he replied. “There are forms of pain we know not how to confide.”

“The sincerity and strength of friendship are not to be measured by time,” the Baroness observed sadly.

Hippolyte, blushing with shame, ascribed the loss to an accident. The evening was delicious; Adélaïde could read his remorse, and though he could not confess his doubts, he came back more in love and seeking tacit forgiveness. Yet when the Baroness suggested a game—“Let us have our little game; my old friend Kergarouet will not let me off”—his fears revived. He sat at the table; Adélaïde said he needed a partner.

Throughout the game the Baroness and her daughter exchanged looks that alarmed Hippolyte more because he was winning. At last a final hand left the lovers in the old lady’s debt. Reaching for money, he saw a purse Adélaïde had slipped before him without his noticing; the poor child held the old one in her hand and, to keep her countenance, was looking into it for the money to pay her mother. The sight made blood rush to his heart; he was near fainting.

The substituted purse contained his fifteen gold louis. It was worked with gilt beads; rings and tassels showed Adélaïde’s taste and the small hoard she had spent on it. It was an impossible, delicate way to show him that his gift could only be repaid by proof of affection.

Overcome, Hippolyte clasped Adélaïde, pressed her to his heart, and kissed her; then, with an artist’s simple heartiness, he exclaimed, “I ask for her for my wife!” looking to the Baroness.

Adélaïde looked half-wrothful; Madame de Rouville, somewhat astonished, considered her reply when a bell rang. The old vice-admiral entered, followed by his shadow and Madame Schinner. Alarmed by calumnies against Adélaïde, Hippolyte’s mother had inquired and reported them to the admiral, who swore to punish the scoundrels.

Prompted by his wrath, the admiral explained his intentional losing at cards as his method to help the Baroness. After Madame Schinner paid her respects, the Baroness looked at Comte de Kergarouet, the Chevalier du Halga, Hippolyte, and Adélaïde, and said warmly, “So we are a family party this evening.”

PARIS, May 1832
