In Baronne de Watteville's drawing-room, a center of Besançon society, the Archbishop frequently visited due to her piety. In 1815, Monsieur de Watteville married wealthy heiress Mademoiselle de Rupt, enhancing their social standing. During a dinner in September 1834, guests discussed young lawyer Monsieur Savaron's legal victory, piquing Madame de Watteville's interest. Rosalie de Watteville, indifferent to suitor Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, found Savaron more appealing. Meanwhile, Rodolphe fell for Fanny Lovelace in Switzerland, vowing to wait for her despite her marriage to an older man.

For twenty days, Rodolphe was enchanted by Francesca, who embodied youthful imprudence and womanhood. She declined to join him in Geneva to protect her husband Emilio's reputation, expressing love but emphasizing discretion. After the Bergmanns left, Rodolphe sought information about Italian refugees and encountered Francesca in an elegant carriage, highlighting her aristocratic background. Their bond deepened at a gathering where she encouraged his ambitions, contrasting her education with her revolutionary spirit.

As spring approached, Rodolphe's longing grew, leading to tension over Francesca's comparison to a noblewoman. After she asked him to leave until Naples, he returned to Paris, where he faced setbacks in his ambitions. Meanwhile, Rosalie aimed to thwart the Duchesse d’Argaiolo while Albert de Savarus gained political traction. Despite her attempts to sabotage him, Savarus was nominated and gained support. After her father's drowning, Rosalie sought closure with the Duchess regarding Savarus, who had entered a monastery.
