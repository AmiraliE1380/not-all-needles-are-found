To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy.

Dear Nodier, this book uncovers actions hidden behind domestic doors where supposed divine intervention often replaces human justice. Its moral is about family and motherhood and the harms that follow when paternal authority—the traditional tribunal for domestic wrongs—is weakened. A loving mother cannot take the place of patriarchal power. The story defends indissoluble marriage and warns against unchecked selfishness in a society that exalts wealth and success while the law is impotent. I hope such a society will return to Catholic moral guidance through a different education.

In “Scenes from Military Life” I will portray noble self-sacrifice and expose war’s corrupting influence on those at home living as if on a battlefield. You have observed our times keenly; I entrust this tale to your name as a shield from criticism.

Your sincere admirer,
De Balzac  
Paris, November 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792 the townspeople of Issoudun disliked Dr. Rouget; gossip blamed him for his beautiful wife’s unhappiness, though their household concealed its true nature. Madame Rouget, née Descoings, had been frail; she bore a son, Jean-Jacques, and ten years later a daughter, Agathe.

Rouget’s conviction that men must display strength caused much female suffering. The Descoings—former wool merchants turned investors in forfeited estates—were rich and miserly. Descoings had gone to Paris, failed as a grocer, and been driven by love for a widow; by 1792 he seemed prosperous. Expecting his wife’s death, Rouget sent Agathe to Paris to secure Descoings’ favor and a share of the inheritance.

Agathe, beautiful like her mother, provoked a quarrel between Rouget and their friend Lousteau, who left town. Madame Rouget, learning her daughter had gone, foretold she would never see Agathe again; her health then declined, worsened by her son’s neglect.

Agathe’s arrival in Paris coincided with Descoings’ imprisonment for imprudent political remarks during the famine and his execution with the poet André Chénier—an event that struck the community. Agathe later married Bridau, a ministry secretary, with Rouget’s approval; Rouget hoped for profit. Bridau, devoted to Napoleon, rose by work and promotion but died of overwork in 1808 just as another advancement was imminent.

After Bridau’s death Agathe received no letters from Issoudun. Her mother died shortly before the birth of a second son; her father died around the Emperor’s coronation; Jean-Jacques remained absent. Madame Hochon warned Agathe she would inherit nothing unless she granted power of attorney to her brother; Agathe hesitated. Bridau’s prudence left only a modest income, and by 1809 Madame Descoings—still fond of theater and fine food—had secretly gambled on lotteries and concealed debts. Devoted to her children but limited in judgment, Agathe sold possessions and liquidated investments to settle the debts—a decisive turning point in their lives.

One of the bleakest stretches of Paris was rue Mazarin between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, shaded by the high gray walls of the college and library given by Cardinal Mazarin. There, on the third floor of a damp, cold house, lived a poor widow, Madame Bridau (Agathe). Opposite were the Institute buildings where aspiring artists, the rapins, worked amid the tumult of examinations; from her windows Agathe could see their cages and the students’ uproar. To brighten her room she defied police regulations and set boxes of earth on the sill to make little gardens.

The shallow house, reached by a winding staircase, held three rooms: a dining room, a small salon, and a bedroom, plus a kitchen and two single rooms. Agathe had chosen the flat because it was cheap—four hundred francs a year—near her sons’ school and in a neighborhood she knew. The interior matched the building’s dreariness: yellowed wallpaper, unglazed tiles, and only essential furniture. The salon contained an Aubusson carpet given to her and a mahogany sofa bearing a pastel portrait of her late husband, Bridau, which captured his noble essence; a vivid Napoleon hung above a card table.

After Bridau’s death Agathe’s life narrowed. She filled the room with two large birdcages—canaries and Java sparrows—and her space was littered with millet, chickweed, and cat hair, signs of grief and neglected care. She preserved everything belonging to her husband, even wrapping his last pen with a note. Seized by sorrow, she seemed to lose her sense of womanhood and devoted herself entirely to her children.

Agathe lived for her sons and worried ceaselessly over their finances. The Emperor provided three hundred francs a year for each child, scholarships supplemented this, and her aunt Madame Descoings assigned three thousand francs from her annuity to help; the Descoings themselves lived modestly on twelve hundred francs a year. Distrustful of the future, Agathe scrimped and saved—she set aside three thousand francs from her pension—hoping to secure some inheritance for her boys.

The setting shaped her sons’ destinies. Philippe, the elder, resembled his mother and possessed a bold demeanor; enchanted by military life, he petitioned the Emperor, entered Saint-Cyr, and rose rapidly to be a captain by nineteen. Joseph, three years younger and more like his father in untidiness, took to drawing after seeing a caricature in the Tuileries. He sneaked into the Institute, won the students’ respect, and found encouragement in the sculptor Chaudet, who defended art as a calling despite Agathe’s fear of hardship. As politics shifted, Philippe returned home a decorated officer while Joseph, his scholarship withdrawn, sought to earn a living by art with Madame Descoings’ encouragement. Agathe’s preference for Philippe became more apparent, yet Joseph remained devoted; both pursued their callings under the constant love and anxiety of their mother.

In 1816 Joseph persuaded his mother to let him convert the adjoining garret into an atelier, partly financed by Madame Descoings. Unlike the two widows, who treated painting as a trade, Joseph embraced it passionately—installing a skylight and chocolate-colored walls and continuing studies under Gros and Schinner—while living frugally to support the family.

Political unrest in France implicated Philippe: he was arrested, released for lack of evidence, and lost his military pay. Though friends urged exile, Agathe resisted his serving a foreign power; nonetheless Philippe joined General Lallemand in the United States to help found the ill-fated Champ d’Asile. Agathe equipped him with ten thousand francs; by late 1817 she had adjusted to reduced means. In 1818 the notary Roguin absconded with funds meant for Madame Descoings, and Philippe soon sent a bill for a thousand francs, having lost everything abroad. Agathe blamed herself, while Madame Descoings continued support.

When Philippe returned he was hardened—self-centered, drinking and gambling, swaggering and neglectful. He refused Bourbon service, sought to impress Mariette, and lived off others. Gerard and Giroudeau proposed journalism; a cashier’s post at a newspaper, obtained through Agathe and Madame Descoings, brought only temporary relief. Joseph, increasingly overshadowed and unappreciated, watched family finances and relationships fray under the weight of Philippe’s choices and maternal indulgence.

################################################
The colonel, a voracious guest who never paid his way, continued to criticize the food while the widows—reluctant to press him—covered the household expenses. His mother and Madame Descoings hesitated to remind him of obligations; a year passed with no enforcement. “Well, he is happy,” his mother said; “he has a place.”

Mariette, encouraged by Vernou’s feuilleton, made a sensational debut at the Porte-Saint-Martin alongside Begrand. A wealthy general, acquainted with Philippe and Giroudeau, became her impresario—a sign of the protective solidarity around fashionable folly. Bixiou soon told the household that Philippe, the cashier, was in love with Mariette, the rising ballet-dancer. The widows were appalled—Agathe’s piety condemned all stage women, and both feared the ruin such attachments bring. Bixiou tried to reassure them that Mariette might be taken by higher-ranking admirers.

Philippe, partly motivated by financial hopes, proposed; Mariette refused, perhaps suspecting his motive or valuing her freedom, and aimed for the Grand Opera. Philippe began to spend his days at the office, the theatre, and with Mariette. After her Opera debut in January 1821 she attracted a distinguished duke. Passion drove Philippe to dip into the funds he managed—by May he had taken eleven hundred francs. Mariette left for London to seek patrons while the new opera house was readied; to her Philippe was a stepping stone.

Compelled to give her up, Philippe returned to his mother’s apartment unable to imagine life without the pleasures he had tasted. Suicide crossed his mind—not merely because of the deficit but because he could not bear the loss of Mariette’s company. He went to Joseph’s studio and, pale and subdued, asked how long a portrait would take, saying he wanted to leave his mother a likeness. Pressed, he confessed he had taken money and could not live with the dishonor. Joseph urged repayment to save his honor; Philippe, despairing, fled. Joseph informed Madame Descoings and Agathe, who were distraught.

That day Philippe calmly submitted accounts, inventing a story that he had retained the money for safekeeping, then went gambling—winning at first, then losing everything. Returning home, he accepted his mother’s pleading—“Promise not to kill yourself, and all shall be forgotten”—and feigned gratitude. The next day Agathe, shaken, entertained Desroches’s counsel: sign a transfer of funds and petition for Philippe’s reinstatement in the army to preserve honor. Desroches’s tact eased some fears, but Agathe’s faith was broken.

Philippe resumed a life of leisure—gambling, drinking, growing careless and contemptuous toward women. Joseph, gaining recognition as a painter and receiving Madame Descoings’s hopes, began noticing financial discrepancies and suspected Philippe. He and Madame Descoings planned to catch him; when Joseph found that Philippe had indeed taken money from him, he felt betrayed. Despite Madame Descoings’s plea for sympathy, Joseph remained wary of Philippe’s corrosive influence on the family’s future.

Despite careful nightly gambling, Philippe was sometimes “cleaned out” and began stealing from his family—pilfering from his brother Joseph and from Madame Descoings and Agathe. After a distressing dream in which he robbed her, Agathe pleaded that he ask for money instead of stealing. Within two weeks her savings were gone; she took worsted work for twenty sous a day, spent sleepless nights waiting for Philippe, and endured his drunkenness and mood swings.

When Madame Descoings suggested new clothes, Philippe said his mother had no money, that he’d mortgaged his pension, and admitted borrowing from Giroudeau as a “debt of honor.” Agathe excused him while Joseph criticized his neglect. Philippe sank further into debauchery and gambling.

Denied money by Joseph, Philippe guessed where Madame hid her savings, and, expecting her to be out buying Christmas treats, broke into her room and found twenty napoleons in the mattress. He gambled the sum, winning heavily at first—amassing over 150,000 francs by night—but then lost everything. The next morning he awoke in despair, having ruined his family.

At Christmas dinner Philippe was absent; Madame, who had pinned hopes on a lottery stake, discovered her savings gone and fainted. The concierge confirmed Philippe had been in the apartment. Confronted, he claimed he’d taken what was owed him; a fight with Joseph ensued and Madame Descoings suffered a stroke. She later refused Joseph’s money, missed the lottery, and died. Philippe, indifferent and urged by Agathe to rejoin the army, accepted her reluctant help and left. Agathe, heartbroken, prayed and resigned herself to the loss of the son she once loved.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

By February 1822 Madame Bridau had been relegated to the attic above the kitchen of her former apartment, recently occupied by her son Philippe. Seeing his mother reduced, Joseph refurbished the garret, furnished it better, and began taking Agathe daily to a table-d’hôte that cost nearly a hundred francs a month. Alarmed by the expense and urged by her confessor, Agathe accepted a seven-hundred-franc-a-year post at a lottery office that demanded long hours.

She adjusted to the routine—preparing Joseph’s breakfasts, mending his clothes, keeping the studio tidy, and assisting his work despite no artistic training—and their bond deepened through these small cares. They often spoke of Philippe: Agathe feared his recklessness, while Joseph, convinced Philippe prospered in New York, dismissed her worries.

Joseph visited Philippe at a newspaper; Philippe invited him to dine, but Joseph stayed with his mother. Agathe, hopeful, decorated the studio and prepared an elaborate breakfast for a sitting; she hid and watched the brothers together. Philippe later treated them to a sumptuous dinner and bragged of plans and bohemian friends, vague about money. He then mistook a valuable Rubens copy for an original and sold it cheaply—devastating Joseph and breaking Agathe’s heart.

Two months later Philippe fell ill and destitute; Agathe at first refused help, then gave what she could. He was implicated in a conspiracy and arrested; Agathe fainted. Joseph consulted Desroches, who urged securing the family inheritance and collecting evidence of undue influence, planning a holiday visit to Issoudun. A discouraging letter from Agathe’s godmother about an unhelpful brother arrived; Agathe and Joseph set off for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to face the consequences and their estrangement unresolved.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Issoudun, one of France’s oldest cities noted by Caesar and Rigord, yielded in recent excavations by M. Armand Peremet a likely unique fifth‑century basilica built from stones of a Roman temple to Isis. Its name—Isis plus “dun,” a religious‑military site—signals ancient Celtic origins.

In the Middle Ages Issoudun was powerful: Richard Coeur‑de‑lion built a tower above the basilica, and the town later sheltered Routiers and Cottereaux mercenaries used by Henry II against his son Richard. Its prominence faded. In the 1830 insurrection some eight thousand vine‑growers resisted government measures, burned tax offices, and asserted autonomy.

In the fourteenth century the town held 16–17,000 people, prospering on wool and making blankets and gloves; by Louis XIV it enjoyed some elegance. Today it numbers about twelve thousand, including suburban vine‑growers, but the economy—especially wine—suffers from outdated practices: producers resist modernization, make poor wine, and squander potential prosperity. This stagnation shows in civic attitudes: local leaders opposed a proposed road linking Paris and Toulouse, fearing higher food prices and preferring tradition to progress.

Socially, the loss of a competitive nobility and a complacent bourgeoisie left governance in the hands of interrelated families and a weak clergy. Disaffected youth formed the “Knights of Idleness,” led by the charismatic Maxence Gilet—rumored an official’s illegitimate son—who staged mischievous hoaxes from a tavern run by Père and La Cognette. Issoudun’s archaeological importance and past grandeur stand in sharp contrast to its modern decline; the Knights express the youth’s frustration and thirst for life.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness staged a prank. An old Spaniard, a former prisoner of war, left his empty cart at the foot of the Issoudun tower. Maxence, arriving at the Knights’ rendezvous, proposed, “Here’s Père Fario’s cart. Let’s get it up on the embankment first, and then decide.” In an hour the cart was taken apart and hoisted piece by piece to the embankment like artillery; once reassembled, the Knights, hungry and thirsty, returned to Mère Cognette’s to await the Spaniard’s astonishment.

Their amusements varied; they did not prank nightly but met for hunts, the autumn vintage, and winter skating. The twenty youths made Max their idol. François Hochon and Baruch Borniche, grandsons of Madame Hochon, treated Max as a cousin and depended on him for money and adventures their grandfather denied them.

That night, after twelve to fifteen bottles of wine, François and Baruch sat beside Max at a dim table. Baruch warned, “You are threatened in your stronghold.”

“My grandmother got a letter from Madame Bridau—her goddaughter—saying she and her son are coming here. She’s preparing two rooms for them,” he said.

Max, thirty-four and soldierly, was outwardly confident but unsettled. Someone remarked that Rouget’s sister would secure the property when he died, threatening Max’s future. Max bristled at the nickname given to Flore Brazier, Rouget’s servant-mistress, and for the first time spoke openly of her. His friends knew to tread carefully. He explained his position plainly: he spent money and treated the others because of Flore’s influence and her hoped-for claim on Rouget’s estate.

“You think I plan to marry Flore when Rouget dies, and that these Parisians threaten my future?” François asked. Max admitted the worry but asked for limited help—“within the limits of our fooleries”—not murder or poisoning. If the Bridaus succeeded and Flore had nothing, he would be satisfied; he loved her enough to prefer her to Mademoiselle Fichet, the richest heiress in Issoudun whom Goddet courted. Moved by his frankness, the Knights cheered their loyalty. Max toasted, half in jest, to each having a mistress like Flore and told them to be ready when Père Fario returned for his cart.

At half-past three the Knights slipped away. Max returned to place Saint-Jean, masking his anxiety with the concealment learned in Cabrera. Rouget’s property yielded forty thousand francs a year; Rouget’s savings—accumulated over seventeen years and lodged with notaries—amounted to vast sums that could not be liquidated quietly in gossipy Issoudun. News of legitimate heirs unnerved him. “That fool of a girl never mentioned her sister and nephews in all these seven years!” he muttered. His priority: get rid of the relatives, then secure the property. Exhausted, he let himself in with a pass-key and went to bed, hoping clarity in the morning.

The narrative then traces how Flore became known as the “Rabouilleuse” and the quasi-mistress of Jean-Jacques Rouget’s household. One September day in 1799 the doctor found a captivating little girl by a brook, tattered but spirited; she claimed to be from Vatan and to be catching crabs for her uncle Brazier. Struck by her looks, the doctor took her in, fed and clothed her, and at first encouraged study. Town gossip colored perceptions of impropriety, though for five years Rouget enjoyed educating her without scandal. Born in 1787 amid revolutionary upheaval, Flore grew pragmatic rather than traditionally virtuous. As Rouget neared death, his refusal—prompted by cynicism and wounded pride—to leave her anything set the stage for the inheritance struggle now threatening Max’s position and Flore’s hopes.

Jean-Jacques Rouget did not mourn his father’s death, though Flore Brazier did. The old doctor had made his son miserable after Jean-Jacques came of age, yet he had given Flore the material comforts valued in the country. After the funeral, when Fanchette asked Flore what would become of her, Jean-Jacques showed animation for the first time.

At seventeen Flore retained the beauty that had drawn the doctor, though she was growing plump like other countrywomen. Jean-Jacques hesitated to speak; the memory of his father’s death and benefactions weighed on him, and Flore was puzzled by his silence. When the doctor died Jean-Jacques was thirty-seven: timid, submissive, and childlike. His torpor left him passionate but lacking confidence, awkward and unattractive, preferring solitude. Too late the father recognized his son’s temperament, wanted him to marry but feared losing control of property, and tried to instill avarice in him.

Secretly in love with Flore, Jean-Jacques took pride in the instruction his father had given her, hoping it would make her easier to win. Flore, unaware, waited for him to declare himself, but he remained silent. After three weeks of uncertainty, a night when Flore found him crouched outside her door seemed proof of his love; their conversations, halting, revealed his growing affection. Ten months later he brightened, cared for his appearance, and townspeople speculated. Fanchette, the disapproving cook, left; Flore learned to cook and manage the house, surpassing her.

Over nine years Flore gained control: familiar yet commanding, she became dominant while the childlike Jean-Jacques grew dependent. By 1815 she was at the height of her beauty; in 1816 she fell for Maxence Gilet, a penniless officer. Pressing Jean-Jacques about his wealth and empty rooms, she had Max invited. Jean-Jacques, fearing loss, consented. With Max in the house Jean-Jacques found conversation and companionship, while Flore shirked duties, hired staff, refined her appearance, bought Max a horse, and brought in a servant. The household shifted: Flore and Max became central and Jean-Jacques receded, increasingly dependent.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to claim her threatened inheritance, Jean-Jacques Rouget had become semi-vegetative. After Max’s financial aid, Flore raised Rouget’s standard of living, feeding him from Vedie’s kitchen; yet he grew weaker and utterly submissive, his devotion to Flore the only force in his life.

Max’s apartment displayed his taste and devotion: provincial comforts, colored tiles, elegant papering and mahogany furniture. A rush matting muffled footsteps so Max could return late without waking anyone — not even Rouget, who remained ignorant of his nocturnal activities.

One morning Flore discovered Max just returned and learned Rouget’s family were coming to Issoudun, likely to evict them. Flore vowed confrontation; Max urged caution. Before they could plan, Baruch announced that Fario’s cart had been stolen. Max led the crowd to the tower embankment where, to everyone’s surprise, the cart was on top. Amid laughter Max theatrically tossed it down, humiliating Fario and turning the episode into town gossip.

That evening François warned Max the Bridaus would arrive. Max decided they should not appear hostile and proposed letting the Hochons handle driving them off. Flore, furious at the news, punished Rouget by withdrawing affection; Vedie urged him to placate her. Max advised Rouget to be polite while retaining his will, and he and Flore plotted to secure Rouget’s wealth with the Hochons’ help.

The Hochons were modest and frugal; Madame Hochon would receive her goddaughter Agathe while granddaughter Adolphine eyed Joseph Bridau. News of the Bridaus split Issoudun: cautious bourgeoisie on one side, Max’s zealous Knights of Idleness on the other. The Fario incident and the town’s division showed how fragile reputations and power were, and how a few calculated acts could determine the coming inheritance struggle.

Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales in Issoudun at three. Though tired, Madame Bridau felt revived by familiar sights. News spread fast; Madame Hochon, bereft for years, embraced Agathe like a daughter. Agathe met the cold Monsieur Hochon and the household: the young men Baruch and François, and shy Adolphine, who noticed Joseph. The stingy Hochon remarked on Joseph’s recent hospital stay and hunger; the youths were struck by his looks. Agathe proudly introduced him as “my son, the painter.” Baruch rushed off to spread word, stressing Joseph’s striking appearance.

The town gossiped, likening Joseph to a brigand and sneering at artists; many planned to swarm the Hochons that evening. In modest attic rooms Joseph contrasted his Parisian artistic life with the provincial drabness. Dinner revealed the family’s frugality—plain bouilli, hard eggs and scant accompaniments—which Joseph, used to better fare, found disappointing but tried to accept. Agathe learned her childhood home had been sold to the Borniches; Madame Hochon braced for visitors.

That evening the women gathered in the salon. Agathe and Madame Hochon exchanged life stories and hard-won counsel, repeatedly interrupted by callers eager to meet the Parisians. Joseph stayed aloof, studying visitors with a critical eye that made them uneasy; when the house quieted, the two women resumed their heartfelt talk.

Elsewhere the Knights of Idleness plotted mischief: Max proposed using rats to sabotage merchant Fario and drive the Bridaus away—without harming the Hochons. Next morning at breakfast Joseph glimpsed his brother Jean-Jacques Rouget, dishevelled and accompanied by the conspicuous Flore Brazier. Joseph admired Flore; Agathe feared her influence. A letter from Jean-Jacques invited Joseph to dine; the Hochons, suspecting Flore, were uneasy. Gritte reported the Rouget household’s ostentation; Monsieur Hochon urged Joseph to observe and speak frankly about his nephews. With rival schemes and simmering tensions, a fraught family reunion loomed.

At four o’clock Joseph crossed from the Rouget to the Hochon house, where Kouski announced him. At the large table he exchanged light banter with Rouget, Flore, and Maxence Gilet, admired the house and Maxence’s looks, and suspected a triangular household. Baruch and Francois arrived; Flore suggested a pre-dinner walk. While she prepared, Joseph eagerly examined paintings Rouget said came from the Descoings, bought during the Revolution. Ecstatic, Joseph was shown more by Maxence upstairs. Flore urged Rouget to give the paintings to Joseph, arguing Rouget didn’t care for them.

Rouget offered the pictures—without frames—and Flore proposed paying Joseph four thousand francs to make copies. Overwhelmed, Joseph accepted. Word spread through Issoudun and boosted Flore’s standing. Dinner was splendid; Joseph, somewhat tipsy, left at eleven. Rouget, heavily drunk, was carried to bed. Max and Flore, left alone, agreed it was better to befriend the Bridaus than antagonize them and planned to send the pictures to Joseph the next day.

The next morning the paintings leaned against Rouget’s wall. Agathe, Joseph’s mother, visited to thank Rouget after Hochon warned her the Bridaus were influencing him and urged her to cultivate Flore. Agathe was warmly received; Rouget was ill. Flore feigned deference while hinting Rouget would remember her in his will.

Meanwhile the local troublemakers, the Knights of Idleness, released rodents into Fario’s grain store, causing havoc. The Bridaus tightened their hold: Flore claimed Rouget was too weak to walk and bought a shabby carriage that drew attention. Rouget signed a power of attorney giving Max control of his investments, while Flore handled transactions. Fario suspected Max and began gathering evidence.

Convinced the paintings were valuable, Joseph sent them to Desroches in Paris for appraisal. Agathe warned him to stay quiet, but the news leaked and searches for old artworks spread through town. Desroches and Schinner advised keeping Agathe in Issoudun, warned about Max, and suggested Rouget might already have a will—making the inheritance a struggle between Flore and the Church. Disturbed, Joseph decided to return to Paris. He bade Rouget farewell, resolved to return the pictures later and rely on his talent. The Hochon family suspected Max and Flore had manipulated Joseph; Agathe defended him, but Hochon warned that returning the paintings might jeopardize any chance of inheritance.

Between midnight and one the Knights of Idleness fed the town’s dogs; their expedition lasted until three. After supper at Cognette’s, Max, turning from rue l’Avenier into Grande rue at half-past four, was stabbed by Fario, who had lurked in a recess, fled toward Vilatte, and wiped the knife on his handkerchief. Fario then washed the handkerchief in the Rivière forcée and returned to his lodgings at Saint-Paterne, where his new watchman later found him in bed.

Max fell, crying out and alarming Lousteau-Prangin and young Goddet, who ran up the street calling for help; the townspeople, used to false alarms, stayed in bed. Max had fainted. He recognized Fario but, on regaining consciousness and seeing the wound was not fatal, decided to exploit the incident: “I think I recognized that cursed painter!”

Lousteau-Prangin hurried to his father, the judge. Max was carried home by Cognette, Goddet, and others. Goddet, suspicious of Max’s words, asked Gritte if Joseph Bridau was in bed; she replied that Joseph had left at half-past four, restless all night. The revelation shocked the group, and they entered Rouget’s house to find Max in a bloody shirt.

Joseph had spent the night anxious about how the townspeople would view him, fearing they might think he had robbed his uncle. He longed to flee to Paris and confront Max; at dawn he walked to the poplar avenue toward Tivoli to steady his nerves.

Max plotted revenge. After Goddet examined the wound and found it serious but not fatal, news spread and a crowd gathered at the place Saint-Jean. Max whispered to Flore that they could use the incident to rid themselves of the Parisians: pretend he was dying and have Joseph arrested. Flore, feigning tears, spread the story that Joseph had attacked Max out of jealousy over paintings. Incited by her, the crowd demanded Joseph’s capture; the mob grew hostile.

Joseph was spotted returning home and nearly seized by the mob. Gendarmes intervened and, despite his protests of innocence and his alibi, took him to the Palais for safety. Magistrates kept him in custody while verifying claims. Max then recalled the attacker had worn list shoes while Joseph had left in boots and wrote urging Joseph’s release. Witnesses confirmed Joseph’s alibi and he was freed. He returned to his mother’s relief; the ordeal strengthened their bond.

The townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians; Max and Flore were pleased. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris; Joseph’s spirits improved, while Agathe fretted about her son Philippe, whose punishment followed: five years’ surveillance and exile to Autun, later modified by Desroches to residence in Issoudun. Urged to reclaim his inheritance from Uncle Rouget, Philippe, resolved to restore the family fortunes and confront Max and the Rabouilleuse, prepared to return to Issoudun and departed after a brief dinner.

On All-Souls’ Day, November 2, Philippe Bridau registered at the Issoudun police station and was told to stay on rue l’Avenier. The town was uneasy after news that a banished officer linked to the painter falsely accused in the affair had arrived. Maxence Gilet, recently recovered, had converted Père Rouget’s mortgages into cash, alarming many. After consulting notary Héron—who felt Rouget’s heirs should thank him for preventing Gilet’s profit—Monsieur Hochon decided to protect the Bridaus, long resentful of Gilet and afraid of losing property.

Gilet and Flore Brazier welcomed Philippe, who arrived in ragged clothes but declared he would not beg from Rouget; his blunt pride impressed them and put Gilet on guard. Philippe visited the Hochons, candid about his poverty, and won some over with charm. He asked Hochon for secrecy; Hochon supplied names of officers hostile to Gilet—Mignonnet and Carpentier. Befriending them by revealing knowledge of the conspiracy against the Bourbons, Philippe integrated into the local military circle while hiding his true aims.

He cultivated a respectable image—dining with officers, taking fencing and shooting—while probing Gilet’s plans. Fario told him about the Knights of Idleness; Philippe suspected Gilet and Flore intended to secure Rouget’s income and flee. He urged Rouget on walks to break Flore’s hold, alerted Hochon that Gilet sought power of attorney, and together they agreed to watch Rouget. Gilet proposed going to Vatan to secure the money and marry; Flore, wary of Philippe’s influence, reluctantly agreed. Tensions rose as Philippe’s determination to protect his family and restore their honor hardened, setting the stage for a town showdown.

As Philippe walked arm in arm with his uncle Rouget along the boulevard Baron, he outlined a ruthless plan to settle the struggle over Rouget’s fortune: Colonel Bridau must confront Captain Maxence Gilet, and if necessary Philippe would remove Maxence to make Flore obedient again. Philippe told Rouget bluntly that Flore loved Maxence and wanted Rouget out of the way; he urged Rouget to refuse to sign the power of attorney demanded before December third, warning that document would allow the others to sell Rouget’s assets and flee to Paris. Rouget recoiled at Philippe’s proposal to take Maxence’s place; Philippe replied that fear and force were the only means to handle women.

Philippe instructed Mignonnet and Carpentier to arrange banquet seating so he could force a public confrontation with Gilet, despite warnings about Gilet’s dueling skill. At home Rouget found Kouski in tears: Flore had left with Vedie, and Rouget’s search failed. Gilet, urged by Captain Potel to face Bridau, felt pressure and aimed first to secure Rouget’s fortune by transferring assets to Flore to block Philippe. Philippe planned to counter, using Baruch to secure the power of attorney for Rouget while distracting Gilet.

Rouget began a threatening letter to revoke his will in Flore’s favor unless she returned; Philippe encouraged it. Town gossip swelled. When Flore returned, Philippe asserted control and warned her; Rouget clung to hope. By morning the town speculated on the duel, and Philippe, confident in his banquet plan, believed it would decide Rouget’s fate and his own.

Around four the next afternoon officers of the old army gathered at the place du Marché in Issoudun for a coronation banquet, waiting for Colonel Philippe Bridau. Conversation turned to Max’s recent dismissal from old Rouget’s house; only Potel and Renard defended him. Max arrived to a cold reception—contrasting with Philippe’s warm welcome—and resolved to confront him.

At dinner Philippe toasted the Grand Army and others followed. Mignonnet toasted those who had tried to restore the Emperor’s son; Max, whose reputation carried weight, rose with an ironic wish to meet again next year. Tension mounted as Philippe and Max exchanged barbs; Philippe stayed composed while Max grew angrier. The duel was set for the next morning with sabres and Goddet, a former army surgeon, to attend.

That night Monsieur and Madame Hochon urged Philippe to pray; he declined their relic. The next morning both men appeared composed, but Philippe’s superior stance intimidated Max. Max attacked, was quickly disarmed; Philippe paused to let him recover his weapon. They resumed, exchanged fierce blows; Philippe was wounded but delivered a fatal stroke. Max died, ending his ambitions and the Order of Idleness, much to the town’s relief.

Flore Brazier, devastated, fell ill. Philippe, recovering, wrote plans to secure his inheritance and manage Flore, intending to use her. By February 1823, persuaded that marriage would secure her future, Flore accepted a match he had arranged, with a substantial dower and life annuity. Agathe rejoiced, but family lawyer Desroches warned Philippe’s cunning would block Flore and Joseph from Rouget’s estate. Once married, Philippe asserted control—demanding Rouget’s power of attorney, using Flore to run her uncle’s affairs while watching her—and, backed by local endorsements, was reinstated as lieutenant-colonel.

Lieutenant-Colonel Bridau (Philippe) returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, whom he took to the Treasury to transfer his uncle’s income to himself. The old man and the Rabouilleuse were soon absorbed into extravagant Parisian life; Rouget overindulged after a supper with the dancer Lolotte and died, said to be of indigestion.

Madame Rouget prospered in Paris, watched by Mariette at Philippe’s behest. In October 1823 Philippe settled his uncle’s estate at Issoudun, amassing 1,600,000 francs (aside from valuable paintings held by Monsieur Hochon) and entrusting the money to Mongenod and Sons. He married, legally securing his wife’s fortune if she died childless, bought her a furnished apartment and secretly acquired a grand hôtel on rue de Clichy, lavishing money on renovations.

The rise of Charles X helped the Duc de Chaulieu’s circle; Philippe supplied information about past conspiracies and was made lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse’s regiment and ennobled Comte de Brambourg. Ambitious, he sought further honors—aidede-camp to the Dauphin and a general’s rank—distancing himself from old friends and planning a marriage to Mademoiselle Amélie de Soulanges.

Meanwhile Joseph Bridau, an impoverished artist supported by his mother Agathe, suffered criticism while Philippe flourished. Agathe, deceived by appearances, begged Philippe for help; his cold reply hastened her decline and death, buried by Joseph alone. Joseph found solace among friends; when Flore, Philippe’s wife, later fell into destitution, Joseph and Bixiou aided her and plotted to expose Philippe’s abandonment. Philippe’s fortunes collapsed in the July Revolution of 1830; Joseph ultimately inherited his estate, succeeded in art, became Comte de Brambourg, and remained humble, reflecting on the family’s tragedy.
