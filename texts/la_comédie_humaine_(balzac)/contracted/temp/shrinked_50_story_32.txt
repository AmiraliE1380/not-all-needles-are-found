**The Two Brothers**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Monsieur Charles Nodier, a member of the French Academy. This book examines the hidden injustices of domestic life, where divine chance often replaces human justice, reflecting on the decline of paternal authority and feminine weakness, urging a return to moral foundations in marriage and family.

---

**Chapter I**  
In 1792, the townspeople of Issoudun viewed Dr. Rouget as malicious, believing he made his wife, Madame Rouget, unhappy. Their son, Jean-Jacques, was born first, followed by their daughter Agathe, whose arrival strained family dynamics. The Rougets were connected to the wealthy Descoings family, who profited from the wool trade. Descoings’ brother sought fortune in Paris but failed.

Descoings was imprisoned and executed during the Revolution, shocking the town. After his death, Madame Rouget’s health declined due to her husband’s neglect. Agathe was sent to Paris to live with her uncle, but his imprisonment left him unable to care for her.

Madame Rouget fell ill, fearing she would never see Agathe again. Agathe’s beauty attracted Bridau, who married her with Dr. Rouget’s approval. After inheriting the Descoings’ wealth, Dr. Rouget led a debauched life, leaving Jean-Jacques unprepared for adulthood.

Agathe, devoted but naive, faced misfortunes. Bridau, loyal to Napoleon, worked tirelessly until his death in 1808. Agathe received a pension but remained estranged from her family. Despite warnings, she did not contest her father’s will, losing her inheritance.

Madame Descoings, now sixty-five, struggled with gambling addiction, worsening their financial situation. Agathe took drastic measures to pay off debts, selling possessions and cutting expenses.

The chapter highlights familial relationships, financial struggles, and the consequences of choices within societal expectations, showcasing the complexities of love and loyalty. In Paris, Madame Bridau lives modestly, consumed by grief and financial worries for her children, Philippe and Joseph. Philippe, bold and active, contrasts with introverted Joseph, who aspires to be an artist. Despite her objections, Joseph pursues art, while Philippe secretly petitions to join the army, illustrating their differing personalities and their mother's affections.

In 1816, Joseph Bridau set up an art studio in his attic with support from his mother, Agathe, and financial backing from Madame Descoings. Both widows viewed painting as a respectable profession. While Joseph studied under Gros and Schinner, political unrest in France led to conspiracies and riots. His brother Philippe became involved, was arrested, and later released, but his military career was jeopardized when he was placed back on active duty.

Agathe worried for Philippe's safety as he frequented disreputable cafes, while the Grecian sages advised sending him abroad. Despite their concerns, Philippe joined General Lallemand in the U.S., leading to the failed Champ d’Asile venture. Agathe invested ten thousand francs to support him, but upon his return in 1819, Philippe was changed, hardened by his experiences and struggling with alcoholism.

Eager to help, Agathe bought him new clothes and indulged his drinking, despite her suspicions about his character. Philippe's tales of woe masked his selfishness, which had developed during his time away. Back in Paris, his behavior alarmed Agathe and Madame Descoings, who faced financial struggles. Agathe suggested selling furniture to support him, but Philippe continued gambling and drinking, worsening their situation.

Philippe's social life revolved around old army comrades and gambling, becoming reckless. He formed a bond with Giroudeau, who introduced him to Paris nightlife. Philippe's ambitions shifted towards pursuing Mariette, a young dancer, while still dreaming of political influence. Despite his failures, he sought to leverage his past for personal gain, planning to demand accountability for the funds raised for Champ d’Asile. His mother and Madame Descoings secured him a cashier position at a new newspaper, believing it would help financially. However, Philippe lived extravagantly without supporting them, while Mariette gained fame through his connections.

Bixiou informed Agathe and Madame Descoings of Philippe's infatuation with Mariette, shocking them as they viewed stage women as morally corrupt. Philippe proposed marriage, but she declined, seeking wealthier prospects. As Philippe distanced himself from his family, he indulged in a hedonistic lifestyle, leading to embezzlement from his job.

After Mariette left for London, Philippe returned home, grappling with despair over his financial situation. He confided in Joseph about his suicidal thoughts stemming from his embezzlement. Alarmed, Joseph urged him to reconsider, suggesting repayment could avert disgrace. Philippe fled, leaving his family distressed. Agathe and Madame Descoings were devastated, fearing for his life.

Philippe visited a gambling house, initially winning but ultimately losing everything. He returned home, feigning distress, and Agathe begged him not to harm himself. In the following days, Agathe aged visibly from worry, realizing their dire financial situation. Madame Descoings suggested consulting a lawyer, Desroches, to manage the fallout from Philippe's actions. Desroches devised a plan to protect Philippe’s reputation, framing his theft as a result of misplaced trust.

Philippe resumed a life of leisure, while Joseph focused on his art, gaining recognition. Agathe remained preoccupied with Philippe, oscillating between hope and fear. Desroches assured them Philippe would soon be reinstated in the army, but warned that his gambling habits could jeopardize this opportunity. As Philippe continued his reckless lifestyle, Joseph suspected him of stealing, and they resolved to confront him. Despite their efforts, Philippe's charm allowed him to evade accountability, deepening their struggles.

Desperate for money, Philippe resorted to stealing from his family, including Agathe and Madame Descoings. Agathe, heartbroken, recognized his thefts indicated a deeper moral failing. After a troubling dream where Philippe robbed her, she urged him to ask for money instead of stealing. However, within two weeks, Agathe was left with nothing and considered finding work to support herself.

As Agathe's health deteriorated from worry, Philippe continued his reckless lifestyle, neglecting his appearance. On Christmas Eve, he planned to steal from Madame Descoings, breaking into her apartment and taking twenty napoleons. He gambled and experienced a lucky streak, but ultimately lost everything.

When Philippe arrived home drunk for Christmas dinner, tensions escalated as Agathe confronted him. The next day, Madame Descoings learned her lottery ticket had won three million francs, but was devastated to discover Philippe had stolen her money before she could pay for her stake, leading to her stroke.

As Madame Descoings lay ill, Philippe returned home, indifferent to the chaos he had caused. Agathe confronted him about his theft, but he remained unrepentant. The confrontation escalated into violence, leading to Philippe being restrained due to his delirium.

In the aftermath, Madame Descoings passed away, leaving Agathe with a meager annuity. Philippe, now estranged from his family, continued his reckless lifestyle, showing no remorse. Agathe, heartbroken, prayed for her son, realizing her motherhood had become a source of deep sorrow.

By February 1822, Agathe had moved into a modest attic room above her former apartment, where Joseph aimed to make her comfortable. Concerned about costs, Agathe secured a job as an assistant in a lottery office, earning seven hundred francs a year. Despite her new routine, Agathe found solace in its stability, preparing breakfast for Joseph and keeping his studio tidy.

One day, Agathe expressed concern for Philippe, prompting Joseph to visit him. Philippe invited Joseph to dinner, but Joseph declined, prioritizing his mother. Philippe's behavior raised Joseph's suspicions, particularly regarding his finances. One evening, Philippe stole a valuable painting from Joseph's studio, believing it to be a copy. When Joseph discovered the theft, he was devastated, and Agathe was heartbroken.

Months later, Agathe encountered Philippe on the street, now a shadow of his former self. Overcome with maternal instinct, she gave him money, despite knowing it would likely be squandered. Her health declined as she grappled with the pain of her son's choices.

When news broke of Philippe's involvement in a conspiracy, Agathe fainted, fearing for his life. Joseph sought legal advice from Desroches, who urged them to secure their family's inheritance from Philippe's influence over their wealthy uncle, Jean-Jacques Rouget. Agathe wrote to her godmother, seeking help, but learned Rouget was unlikely to assist. Desroches advised Agathe to travel to Issoudun to protect her family's interests, prompting her to reflect on her estrangement from her brother.

As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orleans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a prank on Pere Fario, hoisting his cart onto an embankment. They returned to Mere Cognette’s for food and laughter, eager to see Fario's reaction.

The Knights enjoyed leisure activities, with Max particularly admired by Madame Hochon's grandsons, Francois and Baruch, who relied on him for financial support. Baruch warned Max about the arrival of Madame Bridau and her son, which could threaten Max's interests in the property of Jean-Jacques Rouget, an old bachelor connected to Flore Brazier, Rouget's servant-mistress. Although Max initially dismissed the concern, he felt uneasy, knowing Rouget's wealth was tied to Flore, and the legitimate heirs could jeopardize his plans.

Francois speculated that if Rouget revoked his will in favor of Flore, it could impact Max's future. Max expressed confidence in his relationship with Flore and proposed that the Knights assist him in dealing with the Bridaus, clarifying he did not intend to harm them but wanted to secure his position. The Knights pledged their loyalty, toasting to Flore and dismissing the Bridaus, reflecting their relaxed morals. Max aimed to cultivate their loyalty for future support in reclaiming his status.

As the night ended, Max contemplated the implications of the Bridaus' arrival. He realized Flore had not informed him about her family, which could threaten their financial future. His affection for Flore was intertwined with her potential inheritance from Rouget, prompting him to act before the Bridaus could secure their claim.

Flore's backstory reveals she was taken in by Doctor Rouget as a child due to her beauty and innocence, receiving a better life than she would have had with her uncle. Under Rouget's care, Flore enjoyed education and luxuries, though townspeople misinterpreted their relationship. Rouget, nearing death, refused to leave her any inheritance, believing her beauty would ensure her future wealth.

Jean-Jacques Rouget, socially awkward and timid, secretly loved Flore, the only woman in his life. Their relationship evolved as Flore began to see him differently, and she took charge of the household, surpassing him in management. However, her beauty attracted attention, and she eventually fell for Maxence Gilet, straining her relationship with Jean-Jacques.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to safeguard her inheritance, Jean-Jacques had lapsed into a semi-vegetative state, motivated solely by his affection for Flore. A commotion erupted when Fario reported his cart missing, and Max feigned indifference while gathering a crowd, humorously suggesting the cart had magically ascended the tower.

As tensions rose, Francois informed Max of the Bridaus' imminent arrival. Max opted for manipulation rather than open opposition. Meanwhile, Flore, upset by the news, took her frustration out on Rouget. Max advised Rouget to welcome his family to avoid scandal, emphasizing the importance of appearances.

After breakfast, Max and Flore discussed financial strategies to secure Rouget's wealth, plotting to ensure the Bridaus would not interfere. The Hochon household prepared for the Bridaus' arrival, with Madame Hochon urging her husband to treat the visitors kindly, while Hochon remained skeptical.

Agathe and Joseph arrived in Issoudun, where Agathe felt nostalgic. Madame Hochon welcomed her warmly, having developed a maternal affection for Agathe. The family scrutinized Joseph, whose appearance led to unflattering comments about his health and character. Dinner revealed the Hochons' frugality, disheartening Joseph, though Madame Hochon expressed hopes for his success.

The next morning, Joseph saw his uncle, now unrecognizable under Flore's influence. Agathe was distressed, while Joseph noted Flore's beauty despite her role in their family's troubles. The Hochons advised Joseph to visit Rouget alone to discuss family matters without Flore's interference.

A letter from Rouget invited Joseph to dinner, raising suspicions among the Hochons about Flore's manipulations. They crafted a response emphasizing Agathe's struggles and Joseph's artistic aspirations. As dinner preparations unfolded, Gritte, the servant, reported on Flore's extravagant display.

At four o’clock, Joseph arrived at Rouget's house, meeting Kouski, Flore, and Gilet. Joseph admired Flore, which pleased Rouget. Flore suggested a walk to see the tower of Issoudun, and Joseph eagerly agreed. He explored Rouget's home, discovering significant paintings. Flore encouraged Rouget to pay Joseph four thousand francs for copies, much to his surprise.

News of Joseph's acquisition spread quickly, enhancing Flore's status. Dinner was lavish, attended by local dignitaries, and Joseph left feeling satisfied. Meanwhile, Rouget indulged heavily in wine.

Max and Flore strategized to keep the Bridaus at bay while benefiting from Rouget's wealth. The next day, Joseph found the paintings had been moved, and Agathe visited Rouget to express gratitude for Flore's care. Old Hochon advised Agathe to cultivate a relationship with Flore, but she struggled to speak privately with her brother.

As the Bridaus struggled for Rouget's favor, Joseph received letters from painter Schinner and lawyer Desroches. Frustrated, he decided to return to Paris, bidding farewell to Rouget, who seemed oblivious to the value of his paintings. Insulted by Flore and Max's manipulation, Joseph returned the paintings, asserting his independence. The incident became gossip in Issoudun, with exaggerated tales about Joseph's departure, leaving the Bridaus' future uncertain amid growing tension with Maxence and Flore.

Between midnight and one o’clock, the Knights of Idleness distributed food to the town's dogs, concluding their escapade around three in the morning. Max was attacked by Fario, who stabbed him before fleeing. Max cried out, prompting Lousteau-Prangin and Goddet to rush to his aid, but the townspeople, accustomed to false alarms, remained indifferent. After fainting, Max claimed to recognize Fario, leading Lousteau-Prangin to inform his father, the judge.

Max was taken home, where Goddet found the knife wound serious but not fatal. Seizing the moment, Max plotted to frame Joseph Bridau, the painter, for the attack. He instructed Flore to spread rumors of his impending death, hoping this would lead to Joseph's arrest and prompt his mother to flee to Paris.

Flore, feigning tears, accused Joseph of wanting Max dead to inherit from his uncle. The townspeople, stirred by her claims, began searching for Joseph, who had left Hochon’s house at dawn after a restless night. Alarmed by the crowd's anger, Hochon bolted his door, fearing for his gold. Meanwhile, Agathe, Joseph's mother, fainted upon hearing the news.

As the crowd grew, the police struggled to maintain order. Gendarmes arrived just in time to protect Joseph from the mob, who were convinced of his guilt. Despite his protests of innocence, Joseph was taken to the Palais for his safety. He recounted his whereabouts that morning, providing an alibi that could be verified by witnesses.

Initially skeptical, the magistrates agreed to investigate further but decided to detain Joseph for protection. He wrote a reassuring letter to his mother, calming her fears. Meanwhile, Max, recovering from his wound, plotted to keep Joseph imprisoned, believing it would rid them of the Parisians.

As the investigation progressed, Max's deception unraveled. Witnesses confirmed Joseph's alibi, and Max's own letter to the judge, detailing the assailant's shoes, further implicated him. Eventually, Joseph was released, warmly greeted by his relieved mother.

In the aftermath, the townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians as a victory. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris, where Joseph's spirits lifted despite his brother Philippe's ongoing trial for actions during the political upheaval. Desroches, a lawyer, sought to help Philippe by arranging for his release and advising caution with Max, who was clever and dangerous. Ultimately, Philippe was released and prepared to confront Max and reclaim his family's legacy.

On November 2nd, Philippe Bridau returned to Issoudun, registering with the local police and lodging on rue l’Avenier. His return as a banished officer linked to a military conspiracy stirred excitement, especially since he was the brother of a wrongfully accused painter. Maxence Gilet, now recovered, had converted Pere Rouget’s mortgages into cash, raising concerns in town. Monsieur Hochon sought information from notary Monsieur Heron about Gilet's actions, worried for the Bridau family.

Gilet and Flore felt secure with Philippe's arrival, knowing they could manipulate old Rouget for his wealth. Philippe visited his uncle in worn clothing, reflecting his hardships, yet he maintained dignity, refusing financial help from Rouget due to past grievances. Flore, sensing Philippe's hostility, advised Rouget to avoid him.

Philippe later visited the Hochons, introducing himself and expressing his intent to live modestly on his limited income. He engaged in polite conversation, winning over Madame Hochon by discussing her nephew, journalist Lousteau. Aware of the town's gossip, Philippe suggested a private discussion with Hochon to avoid suspicion, aiming to connect with other officers of the old army who opposed Gilet.

Philippe soon acquired a new wardrobe on credit and befriended Mignonnet and Carpentier, fellow officers disapproving of Gilet. He shared insights about the failed conspiracy to overthrow the Bourbons, earning respect among the officers while plotting to undermine Gilet and secure his uncle's fortune.

As Philippe ingratiated himself with the local elite, he maintained a facade of respectability, contrasting with Gilet's dubious reputation. He frequently dined with Mignonnet and Carpentier, solidifying his community position. Meanwhile, Gilet viewed Philippe as a rival.

Philippe discovered that Gilet's associates, including Rouget's grandsons, were involved in dubious activities, which he reported to Hochon. This revelation heightened tensions, as Gilet and Flore plotted to secure Rouget's wealth. Philippe confronted his uncle about Flore's influence, insisting on a private discussion.

After a tense exchange, Philippe met Hochon, who informed him that Rouget had not signed any power of attorney, preventing Gilet from accessing his funds. Philippe planned to monitor his uncle and Gilet closely, determined to protect his family's interests. Alarmed by Philippe's boldness, Flore relayed the details to Gilet, who proposed a risky plan to manipulate Rouget into signing a power of attorney.

As Philippe walked with Rouget along the boulevard Baron, he contemplated the impending clash between Colonel Bridau and Captain Gilet, both vying for Rouget's affections and fortune. Philippe expressed disdain for Gilet, revealing his plan to eliminate him to ensure Rouget's happiness with Flore. Despite Rouget's infatuation with Flore, Philippe urged him not to sign a power of attorney that would grant Gilet access to his wealth.

Rouget, easily swayed by Flore's charms, proved difficult for Philippe to convince. Philippe insisted he would deal with Gilet if necessary, promising to make Flore obedient to Rouget. Their conversation shifted when they encountered Mignonnet and Carpentier, who were aware of Rouget's precarious situation. Philippe concluded that either Gilet must die or he would have to kill Gilet himself to save his uncle.

Meanwhile, Gilet recognized the growing animosity against him in Issoudun due to his reckless behavior. He met with Captain Potel, who urged him to confront Bridau to restore his honor. However, Gilet was preoccupied with securing Rouget's wealth for himself and Flore.

At Rouget's home, Kouski, under Gilet's orders, feigned sorrow to inform Rouget that Flore had left with Vedie. This devastated Rouget, who searched for Flore in vain. Gilet attempted to maintain his reputation while plotting to keep Rouget under his control.

As rumors of Gilet's misdeeds spread, the townspeople began to turn against him. Potel expressed dismay over the division within the Imperial Guard, now pitted against itself due to Gilet's actions. Feeling cornered, Gilet resolved to confront Bridau, believing he could outmaneuver him in a duel.

Philippe, anticipating Gilet's next move, prepared to counteract his plans, suspecting Gilet would further manipulate Flore and Rouget. When Rouget returned home, he began drafting a letter to Flore, threatening to revoke his will if she did not return immediately.

Seeing an opportunity, Philippe convinced Rouget to send the letter with Carpentier for personal delivery to Flore, believing this would bring her back and restore Rouget's authority.

As evening approached, Philippe strategized to ensure Flore's compliance and Rouget's happiness. He planned to take Gilet's place in Rouget's life, promising to keep Flore in line. When Flore returned, Philippe confronted her, asserting his dominance and warning her of the consequences if she did not comply with Rouget's needs.

The next day, gossip about the impending duel between Philippe and Gilet spread throughout Issoudun, with many speculating on the outcome. Flore, realizing the stakes, began to act more affectionately towards Rouget to secure her position. However, Gilet remained defiant, unwilling to back down from the challenge posed by Philippe. As tensions escalated, the townspeople rallied around Philippe, hoping for a resolution that would restore order and honor to the Imperial Guard, setting the stage for a dramatic confrontation that would determine their fates.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The day after the coronation banquet, officers at Lacroix's restaurant discussed Max Gilet's dismissal. Tensions escalated when Max arrived, leading to a duel with Colonel Philippe Bridau the next morning. Philippe disarmed Max, fatally wounding him and ending the influence of the Order of Idleness.

As Philippe recovered, he plotted to marry Max's former lover, Flore Brazier, to secure his inheritance after she fell ill. He convinced his mother, Agathe, to support the marriage, which was signed with a substantial dowry.

Philippe returned to Paris with Flore, indulging her until her husband Rouget's death from indigestion. By October 1823, Philippe settled Rouget's estate, netting 1.6 million francs, which he invested. By March 1824, he married Madame Rouget, becoming Comte de Brambourg.

By January 1827, Philippe sought further honors, distancing himself from his past, while Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist. After Agathe's death, Joseph learned of Philippe's indifference and Flore's suffering. Philippe faced scrutiny during the July Revolution of 1830, returning to military service, where he tragically died in battle in 1839. Joseph inherited Philippe's estate, finding solace in art and friendships, ultimately becoming Comte de Brambourg, an ironic title given his brother's legacy.
