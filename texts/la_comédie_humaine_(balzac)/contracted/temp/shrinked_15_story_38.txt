The Marquise de Listomere is one of those young women raised in the spirit of the Restoration. She has principles, fasts, takes the sacrament, and goes to balls and operas elegantly dressed; her confessor allows her to combine the worldly with the sanctified. Always conforming to Church and society, she embodies the present day, which seems to have adopted "legality" for its motto. Her conduct shows enough religious devotion to recall the gloomy piety of Louis XIV.’s last days and enough worldliness to revive the gallantries of his earlier reign, should that time return. At present she is strictly virtuous by policy, possibly by inclination. Married seven years to the Marquis de Listomere, a deputy who hopes for a peerage, she may regard such conduct as promoting her family's ambitions. Some women await the moment when Monsieur de Listomere becomes a peer—when she will be thirty-six—often the age when many realize they have been duped by social laws.

The marquis is an insignificant man who stands well at court; his good qualities are as negative as his defects. As deputy he never speaks but votes RIGHT. He behaves at home as in the Chamber; thus he is deemed one of the best husbands in France. Though not lively, he never scolds unless kept waiting. His friends call him "dull weather"—neither bright nor dark. He resembles the ministers who have succeeded one another since the Charter. A woman with principles could not have chosen better; it is fortunate for a virtuous wife to have married a man incapable of follies.

Occasionally some fops have impertinently pressed the marquise’s hand while dancing; the attempt earned nothing but contemptuous glances, a frost that killed any flattering hopes. Beaux, wits, and fops—men nourished on their canes, a great name, or great fame—blanch before her. She has earned the right to converse freely and often with men she finds agreeable without becoming fodder for gossip. Some coquettish women might reserve such freedoms for later pleasure, but to suppose any such design in the Marquise would be calumny.

I have had the happiness of knowing this phoenix. She talks well; I know how to listen; consequently I please her, and I go to her parties. That, in fact, was the aim of my ambition.

Neither plain nor pretty, Madame de Listomere has white teeth, dazzling skin, and very red lips; she is tall and well made; her foot is small and slender, though she does not display it. Her eyes, unlike those dulled in so many Parisian women, have a gentle glow that becomes magical when she is animated; a soul is then perceived behind her reserved manner. If she takes interest in conversation she reveals a grace otherwise hidden by cold demeanor, and then she is charming. She does not seek success, but she obtains it. We often find what we do not seek; that truth is the moral of this adventure, which I should not tell but for its echo in Parisian salons.

A month ago the Marquise danced with a young man modest yet lively, full of good qualities but chiefly showing his defects. He is ardent but scoffs at ardor; talented yet conceals it; plays the learned man with aristocrats and the aristocrat with learned men. Eugène de Rastignac is one of those extremely clever young men who try everything, sounding others to discover what the future holds. Awaiting the age of ambition, he mocks much; he has grace and originality, two rare qualities because one often excludes the other. On this occasion he talked with Madame de Listomere for nearly half an hour without intent to please her. As their capricious conversation, which began with the opera "Guillaume Tell," reached the duties of women, he looked at the marquise more than once in a way that embarrassed her; then he left her and did not speak to her again that evening. He danced, played écarta, lost some money, and went to bed. I assure you the affair happened precisely thus. I add nothing, I suppress nothing.

The next morning Rastignac woke late, lingering in one of those morning reveries in which a young man glides mentally under silk, cashmere, or cotton draperies. The heavier the body with sleep, the more active the mind. Rastignac finally rose without the yawns of the ill-bred. He rang for his valet, ordered tea, and drank immoderately; to explain this oddity to any who might wonder, he was by then writing letters. He sat comfortably, feet more often on the andirons than on the rug. Ah, to have one's feet on the polished bar connecting the two griffins of a fender and to think of our love in a dressing-gown is delightful—I regret having neither mistress, nor fender, nor dressing-gown.

The first letter he wrote was soon finished; he folded and sealed it and laid it before him unaddressed. The second, begun at eleven, was not finished till midday; four pages filled closely.

"That woman keeps running in my head," he muttered as he folded this second epistle and meant to direct it once his reverie ended.

He crossed the flaps of his flowered dressing-gown, put his feet on a stool, slipped his hands into the pockets of his red cashmere trousers, and lay back in a delightful chair with side wings, the seat and back forming a wide angle. He stopped drinking tea and remained motionless, eyes fixed on the gilded hand of his shovel, without seeing either. He ceased even to poke the fire—a vast mistake! Isn't playing with the fire when thinking of a woman one of our greatest pleasures? Our minds find speeches in those tiny blue flames that dart up and chatter on the hearth. We interpret the strong tones of a "burgundian" at will.

Here I must pause to explain that word, from a distinguished etymologist who wishes his name kept secret. "Burgundian" names since Charles VI. those noisy detonations that fling a little coal or ember onto the carpet—the trivial nucleus of conflagration. Heat releases a bubble of air left in the wood by a gnawing worm. "Inde amor, inde burgundus." We tremble when the structure we had carefully erected between logs rolls down like an avalanche. Oh, to build and play with fire when in love is the material development of our thoughts.

At that moment I entered the room. Rastignac jumped and said, "Ah! there you are, dear Horace; how long have you been here?"

"Just come."

"Ah!"

He took up the two letters, directed them, and rang for his servant.

"Take these," he said, "and deliver them."

Joseph departed without a word—an admirable servant!

We began to talk of the expedition to Morea, to which I was eager to be appointed as physician. Eugene remarked that I would lose much time if I left Paris. We then conversed on various matters; I think you will be glad if I suppress the conversation.

When the Marquise de Listomere rose about half-past two that day, her waiting-maid Caroline handed her a letter which she read while Caroline did her hair—an imprudence many young women commit.

"Dear angel of love," began the letter, "treasure of my life and happiness—"

At those words the marquise was about to throw the letter into the fire; but a fancy common to virtuous women—curiosity to see how a man who began thus could possibly end—made her continue. When she had reached the fourth page she let her arms drop like one much fatigued.

"Caroline, go and ask who left this letter."

"Madame, I received it myself from the valet of Monsieur le Baron de Rastignac."

After that there was silence for some time.

"Does Madame intend to dress?" asked Caroline at last.

"No—He is certainly a most impertinent man," reflected the marquise.

I request all women to imagine for themselves the reflections of which this was the first.

Madame de Listomere ended hers by deciding to forbid her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac and to show him more than disdain in society; his insolence had far surpassed what the marquise had tolerated. At first she thought of keeping the letter, but on second thought she burned it.

"Madame had just received such a fine love-letter; she read it," said Caroline to the housemaid.

"I should never have thought that of madame," replied the other, surprised.

That evening Madame de Listomere went to a party at the Marquis de Beauseant's, where Rastignac would likely appear. By two in the morning, having gone solely to crush Eugene by coldness, she found she was waiting in vain. A brilliant man—Stendhal—gave the fantastic name of "crystallization" to the process through which Madame de Listomere's thoughts passed before, during, and after this evening.

Four days later Eugene was scolding his valet.

"Ah ca! Joseph; I shall soon have to send you away, my lad."

"What is it, monsieur?"

"You do nothing but make mistakes. Where did you carry those letters I gave you Saturday?"

Joseph became stolid, like a statue in a cathedral porch, motionless, absorbed in imagination. Suddenly he smiled idiotically and said:-

"Monsieur, one was for the Marquise de Listomere, the other was for Monsieur's lawyer."

"You are certain of what you say?"

Joseph was speechless. I saw I must interfere, as I happened again to be in Eugene's apartment.

"Joseph is right," I said.

Eugene turned and looked at me.

"I read the addresses quite involuntarily, and—"

"And," interrupted Eugene, "one of them was not for Madame de Nucingen?"

"No, by all the devils, it was not. Consequently, I supposed, my dear fellow, that your heart was wandering from the rue Saint-Lazare to the rue Saint-Dominique."

Eugene struck his forehead with the flat of the hand and began to laugh; by which Joseph perceived that the blame was not his.

There are morals young men should reflect on. First mistake: Eugene thought it would be amusing to make Madame de Listomere laugh at the blunder that made her the recipient of a love-letter not meant for her. Second mistake: he did not call on Madame de Listomere for several days after the affair, allowing that virtuous young woman's thoughts to crystallize. There were other mistakes which I will pass over, to give the ladies the pleasure of deducing them.

Eugene at last went to call on the marquise; but, as he attempted to pass into the house, the porter stopped him, saying Madame la marquise was out. As he was getting back into his carriage the Marquis de Listomere came home.

"Come in, Eugene," he said. "My wife is at home."

A husband, however good, never attains perfection. As they went up the staircase Rastignac perceived at least a dozen blunders in worldly wisdom that had, unaccountably, slipped into this page of his life.

When Madame de Listomere saw her husband ushering in Eugene she could not help blushing. The young baron saw that sudden color. If the most humble-minded man retains some conceit, any more than a woman ever rids herself of coquetry, who shall blame Eugene if he thought: "What! that fortress, too?" So thinking, he posed a little in his cravat. Young men may not be grasping but they like to acquire a new coin.

Monsieur de Listomere seized the "Gazette de France" from the mantelpiece and carried it to a window, to obtain, by journalistic help, an opinion on the state of France.

A woman, even a prude, is not long embarrassed; she always seems to have the fig-leaf Eve bequeathed her. Consequently, when Eugene, interpreting in favor of his vanity the refusal to admit him, bowed to Madame de Listomere in a tolerably intentional manner, she veiled her thoughts behind one of those feminine smiles more impenetrable than a king's words.

"Are you unwell, madame? You denied yourself to visitors."

"I am well, monsieur."

"Perhaps you were going out?"

"Not at all."

"You expected someone?"

"No one."

"If my visit is indiscreet you must blame Monsieur le marquis. I had already accepted your mysterious denial, when he himself came up and introduced me into the sanctuary."

"Monsieur de Listomere is not in my confidence on this point. It is not always prudent to put a husband in possession of certain secrets."

The firm and gentle tones in which the marquise said these words, and the imposing glance she cast upon Rastignac, made him aware that he had posed prematurely.

"Madame, I understand you," he said, laughing. "I ought, therefore, to be doubly thankful that Monsieur le marquis met me; he affords me an opportunity to offer you excuses which might be full of danger were you not kindness itself."

The marquise looked at the young man with surprise, but answered with dignity:-

"Monsieur, silence on your part will be the best excuse. As for me, I promise you entire forgetfulness, and the pardon which you scarcely deserve."

"Madame," said Rastignac, hastily, "pardon is not needed where there was no offence. The letter," he added in a low voice, "which you received, and which you must have thought extremely unbecoming, was not intended for you."

The marquise could not help smiling, though she wished to seem offended.

"Why deceive?" she said, with disdainful air, though her voice was gentle. "Now that I have duly scolded you, I am willing to laugh at a subterfuge not without cleverness. I know many women who would be taken in: 'Heavens! how he loves me!' they would say."

Here the marquise gave a forced laugh, then added, indulgently:-

"If we desire to continue friends let there be no more mistakes, of which it is impossible that I should be the dupe."

"Upon my honor, madame, you are so—far more than you think," replied Eugene.

"What are you talking about?" asked Monsieur de Listomere, who for the last minute had been listening to the conversation, the meaning of which he could not penetrate.

"Oh! nothing that would interest you," replied his wife.

Monsieur de Listomere tranquilly returned to his paper, and presently said:-

"Ah! Madame de Mortsauf is dead; your poor brother has, no doubt, gone to Clochegourde."

"Are you aware, monsieur," resumed the marquise, turning to Eugene, "that what you have just said is a great impertinence?"

"If I did not know the strictness of your principles," he answered naively, "I should think that you wished either to give me ideas I deny myself, or to tear a secret from me. But perhaps you are only amusing yourself with me."

The marquise smiled. That smile annoyed Eugene.

"Madame," he said, "can you still believe in an offence I have not committed? I hope chance may not enable you to discover the name of the person who ought to have read that letter."

"What! can it be still Madame de Nucingen?" cried Madame de Listomere, more eager to penetrate that secret than to revenge herself for the impertinence of the young man's speeches.

Eugene colored. A man must be more than twenty-five not to blush at being taxed with a fidelity that women laugh at—perhaps to hide envy. However, he replied with tolerable self-possession:-

"Why not, madame?"

Such are the blunders we all make at twenty-five.

This speech caused a violent commotion in Madame de Listomere's bosom; but Rastignac did not yet know how to analyze a woman's face by a rapid or sidelong glance. The marquise's lips paled, but that was all. She rang for wood, forcing Rastignac to rise and take his leave.

"If that be so," said the marquise, stopping Eugene with a cold, rigid manner, "you will find it difficult to explain, monsieur, why your pen should, by accident, write my name. A name, written on a letter, is not a friend's opera-hat, which you might have taken carelessly on leaving a ball."

Eugene, discomfited, looked at the marquise with an air both stupid and conceited. He felt ridiculous; after stammering a few juvenile phrases he left the room.

A few days later the marquise acquired undeniable proofs that Eugene had told the truth. For the last fortnight she has not been seen in society.

The marquis tells all who ask the reason of this seclusion:-

"My wife has an inflammation of the stomach."

But I, her physician, who am now attending her, know it is really nothing more than a slight nervous attack, which she is making the most of to stay quietly at home.
