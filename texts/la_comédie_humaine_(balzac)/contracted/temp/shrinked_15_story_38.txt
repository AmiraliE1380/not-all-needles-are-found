STUDY OF A WOMAN

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

To the Marquis Jean-Charles di Negro.

STUDY OF A WOMAN

The Marquise de Listomere is one of those young women raised in the spirit of the Restoration. She keeps principles, fasts, takes the sacrament, and goes to balls and operas in elegant attire; her confessor allows her to combine the worldly with the devout. Always conforming to Church and society, she embodies the present day, which seems to have adopted "legality" for its motto. The marquise's devotion hints at the gloomy piety of Louis XIV.'s last days and enough worldliness to borrow the gallantry of his early years, if revived. At present her virtue is at least partly strategic: married seven years to the Marquis de Listomere, a deputy who expects a peerage, she likely believes such conduct will aid her family's ambitions. Some await her true colors the day Monsieur de Listomere becomes a peer — when she will be thirty-six, that age when many women discover they have been dupes of social laws.

The marquis is an insignificant man. He stands well at court; his virtues are as unremarkable as his defects. As deputy he never speaks but votes RIGHT. He behaves at home as in the Chamber; for that he is considered one of the best husbands in France. He rarely scolds and has been nicknamed "dull weather" — neither bright nor dark. A woman with principles could not have fallen into better hands; it is a great thing for a virtuous woman to have married a man incapable of follies.

Fops have occasionally presumed to press the marquise's hand while dancing; they met contemptuous glances that chilled flattering hopes. Beaux, wits, men of various ranks — they all blanch before her. She has won the right to converse at length with agreeable men without being entered on the tablets of gossip. Certain coquettish women maintain such reserves for years to gratify later fancies; to suppose any such scheming in the Marquise de Listomere would be calumny.

I have had the happiness of knowing this bird of paradise. She talks well; I know how to listen; consequently I please her, and I go to her parties. That, in fact, was the object of my ambition.

Neither plain nor pretty, Madame de Listomere has white teeth, dazzling skin, very red lips; she is tall and well-made, with a small, slender foot she does not show. Her eyes, unlike those dulled in many Parisian women, have a gentle glow that becomes magical when she is animated; a soul is then divined behind that vague form. If she takes interest in conversation, a grace otherwise buried by cold demeanor emerges, and she becomes charming. She does not seek success, yet she obtains it. We often find what we do not seek; that saying is near the moral of this adventure, which I should not tell if it were not echoing now through Parisian salons.

A month ago the Marquise de Listomere danced with a young man as modest as he was lively, full of fine qualities and conspicuous defects. He is ardent yet mocks ardor, talented yet hides it; he plays the learned man with aristocrats and the aristocrat with learned men. Eugene de Rastignac is one of those very clever young men who try everything to divine the future. While awaiting the age of ambition he scoffs at much; he has grace and originality, two rare coexisting gifts. That evening he talked with Madame de Listomere for nearly half an hour with no deliberate plan to please. Conversation wandered from the opera of Guillaume Tell to the duties of women; he looked at the marquise at times in ways that embarrassed her, then left her and did not speak to her again that night. He danced, played écarté, lost some money, and went to bed. I state the facts: I add nothing and suppress nothing.

The next morning Rastignac woke late and lingered in one of those reveries in which a young man glides beneath many draperies. The heavier the body, the more active the mind. Rastignac finally rose without the ill-bred yawns of others. He rang for his valet, ordered tea, and drank immoderately; to explain, Eugene was, by this time, writing letters. He sat with his feet often on the andirons rather than the rug. Ah! to have one's feet on the polished bar between the griffins and to think of our love in our dressing-gown is a delight.

The first letter Eugene wrote was soon finished; he folded and sealed it and laid it before him unaddressed. The second, begun at eleven, was not finished till midday; four densely written pages.

"That woman keeps running in my head," he muttered as he folded the second and meant to direct it after his reverie.

He crossed the two flaps of his dressing-gown, put his feet on a stool, slipped his hands into his trousers' pockets, and lay back in a comfortable wing-chair, eyes fixed on the gilded hand of his shovel without seeing it. He ceased even to poke the fire — a great mistake, for playing with the fire when thinking of women often brings words and fantasies. Our minds find speeches in the tiny blue flames; even the crackling "burgundian" sparks speak to lovers.

Here I must pause to explain that "burgundian" is the name given to those noisy detonations which fling a little coal or ember upon the carpet — the trifling nucleus of a conflagration. Heat releases a bubble of air left by a worm in the wood. "Inde amor, inde burgundus." We tremble when the hearth we have built rolls down like an avalanche. Oh! to build and stir and play with fire when we love is the material development of our thoughts.

It was at this moment I entered the room. Rastignac jumped and said, "Ah! there you are, dear Horace; how long have you been here?"

"Just come."

"Ah!"

He took up the two letters, directed them, and rang for his servant.

"Take these," he said, "and deliver them."

Joseph departed without a word; admirable servant!

We began to talk of the expedition to Morea, to which I was anxious to be appointed as physician. Eugene remarked that I should lose much time if I left Paris. We then conversed on various subjects; I think you will be glad if I suppress the conversation.

When the Marquise de Listomere rose about half-past two that day, her waiting-maid Caroline gave her a letter which she read while Caroline did her hair — an imprudence many young women commit.

"Dear angel of love," began the letter, "treasure of my life and happiness—"

At those words the marquise was about to fling the letter into the fire; but she had the fancy — which virtuous women will readily understand — of seeing how a man who began thus could possibly end. When she had turned the fourth page and read it, she let her arms drop like a person fatigued.

"Caroline, go and ask who left this letter."

"Madame, I received it myself from the valet of Monsieur le Baron de Rastignac."

After that there was silence.

"Does Madame intend to dress?" asked Caroline at last.

"No—He is certainly a most impertinent man," reflected the marquise.

I request all women to imagine for themselves the reflections of which this was the first.

Madame de Listomere ended hers by deciding to forbid her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac and to show him more than disdain in society; his insolence far surpassed what the marquise had tolerated. At first she thought of keeping the letter, but on second thought she burned it.

"Madame had just received such a fine love-letter; she read it," said Caroline to the housemaid.

"I should never have thought that of madame," replied the other, surprised.

That evening Madame de Listomere went to a party at the Marquis de Beauseant's, where Rastignac would likely appear. By two in the morning Madame de Listomere, who had gone solely to crush Eugène by coldness, found she was waiting in vain. A brilliant man—Stendhal—gave the fantastic name of "crystallization" to the process through which Madame de Listomere's thoughts passed before, during, and after this evening.

Four days later Eugène was scolding his valet.

"Ah ça! Joseph; I shall soon have to send you away, my lad."

"What is it, monsieur?"

"You do nothing but make mistakes. Where did you carry those letters I gave you Saturday?"

Joseph became stolid, like a statue in a cathedral porch, motionless, absorbed in imagination. Suddenly he smiled idiotically and said:

"Monsieur, one was for the Marquise de Listomere, the other was for Monsieur's lawyer."

"You are certain of what you say?"

Joseph was speechless. I saw I must interfere, as I happened again to be in Eugène's apartment.

"Joseph is right," I said.

Eugène turned and looked at me.

"I read the addresses quite involuntarily, and—"

"And," interrupted Eugène, "one of them was not for Madame de Nucingen?"

"No, by all the devils, it was not. Consequently, I supposed, my dear fellow, that your heart was wandering from the rue Saint-Lazare to the rue Saint-Dominique."

Eugène struck his forehead with the flat of his hand and began to laugh; by which Joseph perceived that the blame was not his.

There are morals young men should reflect on. First mistake: Eugène thought it would be amusing to make Madame de Listomere laugh at the blunder that made her the recipient of a love-letter not meant for her. Second mistake: he did not call on Madame de Listomere for several days after the affair, allowing that virtuous young woman's thoughts to crystallize. There were other mistakes which I will pass over, to give the ladies the pleasure of deducing them.

Eugène at last went to call on the marquise; but, as he attempted to pass into the house, the porter stopped him, saying Madame la marquise was out. As he was getting back into his carriage the Marquis de Listomere came home.

"Come in, Eugène," he said. "My wife is at home."

A husband, however good, never attains perfection. As they went up the staircase Rastignac perceived at least a dozen blunders in worldly wisdom that had, unaccountably, slipped into this page of his life.

When Madame de Listomere saw her husband ushering in Eugène she could not help blushing. The young baron saw that sudden color, and, with the vanity of youth, thought: "What! that fortress, too?" He straightened his cravat. Men may not be grasping but they like to acquire a new coin.

Monsieur de Listomere seized the Gazette de France from the mantelpiece and carried it to a window, to obtain, by journalistic help, an opinion on the state of France.

A woman, even a prude, is not long embarrassed; she always seems to have the fig-leaf Eve bequeathed her. Consequently, when Eugène, interpreting in favor of his vanity the refusal to admit him, bowed to Madame de Listomere in a tolerably intentional manner, she veiled her thoughts behind one of those feminine smiles more impenetrable than a king's words.

"Are you unwell, madame? You denied yourself to visitors."

"I am well, monsieur."

"Perhaps you were going out?"

"Not at all."

"You expected someone?"

"No one."

"If my visit is indiscreet you must blame Monsieur le marquis. I had already accepted your mysterious denial, when he himself came up and introduced me into the sanctuary."

"Monsieur de Listomere is not in my confidence on this point. It is not always prudent to put a husband in possession of certain secrets."

The firm and gentle tones in which the marquise said these words, and the imposing glance she cast upon Rastignac, made him aware that he had posed prematurely.

"Madame, I understand you," he said, laughing. "I ought, therefore, to be doubly thankful that Monsieur le marquis met me; he affords me an opportunity to offer you excuses which might be full of danger were you not kindness itself."

The marquise looked at the young man with surprise, but answered with dignity:

"Monsieur, silence on your part will be the best excuse. As for me, I promise you entire forgetfulness, and the pardon which you scarcely deserve."

"Madame," said Rastignac, hastily, "pardon is not needed where there was no offence. The letter," he added in a low voice, "which you received, and which you must have thought extremely unbecoming, was not intended for you."

The marquise could not help smiling, though she wished to seem offended.

"Why deceive?" she said, with disdainful air, though her voice was gentle. "Now that I have duly scolded you, I am willing to laugh at a subterfuge not without cleverness. I know many women who would be taken in: 'Heavens! how he loves me!' they would say."

Here the marquise gave a forced laugh, then added, indulgently:

"If we desire to continue friends let there be no more mistakes, of which it is impossible that I should be the dupe."

"Upon my honor, madame, you are so—far more than you think," replied Eugène.

"What are you talking about?" asked Monsieur de Listomere, who for the last minute had been listening to the conversation, the meaning of which he could not penetrate.

"Oh! nothing that would interest you," replied his wife.

Monsieur de Listomere tranquilly returned to his paper, and presently said:

"Ah! Madame de Mortsauf is dead; your poor brother has, no doubt, gone to Clochegourde."

"Are you aware, monsieur," resumed the marquise, turning to Eugène, "that what you have just said is a great impertinence?"

"If I did not know the strictness of your principles," he answered naively, "I should think that you wished either to give me ideas I deny myself, or to tear a secret from me. But perhaps you are only amusing yourself with me."

The marquise smiled. That smile annoyed Eugène.

"Madame," he said, "can you still believe in an offence I have not committed? I hope chance may not enable you to discover the name of the person who ought to have read that letter."

"What! can it be still Madame de Nucingen?" cried Madame de Listomere, more eager to penetrate that secret than to revenge herself for the impertinence of the young man's speeches.

Eugène colored. A man must be more than twenty-five not to blush at being taxed with a fidelity that women laugh at—perhaps to hide envy. However, he replied with tolerable self-possession:

"Why not, madame?"

Such are the blunders we all make at twenty-five.

This speech caused a violent commotion in Madame de Listomere's bosom; but Rastignac did not yet know how to analyze a woman's face by a rapid or sidelong glance. The marquise's lips paled, but that was all. She rang for wood, forcing Rastignac to rise and take his leave.

"If that be so," said the marquise, stopping Eugène with a cold, rigid manner, "you will find it difficult to explain, monsieur, why your pen should, by accident, write my name. A name, written on a letter, is not a friend's opera-hat, which you might have taken carelessly on leaving a ball."

Eugène, discomfited, looked at the marquise with an air both stupid and conceited. He felt ridiculous; after stammering a few juvenile phrases he left the room.

A few days later the marquise acquired undeniable proofs that Eugène had told the truth. For the last fortnight she has not been seen in society.

The marquis tells all who ask the reason of this seclusion:

"My wife has an inflammation of the stomach."

But I, her physician, who am now attending her, know it is really nothing more than a slight nervous attack, which she is making the most of to stay quietly at home.
