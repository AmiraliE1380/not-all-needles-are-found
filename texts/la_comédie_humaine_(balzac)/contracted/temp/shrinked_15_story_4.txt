In October 1800, a foreigner named Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and young daughter. In his sixties and grave, he was deeply troubled; his wife kept a calm face for their child. Passersby noticed their despair, but his fierce gaze deterred sympathy.

After a moment’s thought he handed a dagger to his wife and declared he would confront the Bonapartes. He approached the palace but was stopped by a soldier who refused entry without a written request. Bartolomeo persisted in a tense standoff until Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, arrived, recognized him, and took him inside to meet his brother, Napoleon.

Inside, Napoleon dismissed his aides to speak privately with Bartolomeo. The Corsican sought asylum and confessed he had killed the Portas—who had burned his vineyard and killed his son—and had avenged his son's death by killing all seven members of the family. The revelation shocked Napoleon. Bartolomeo complained of the Bonaparte brothers' indifference, reminding them he had once protected their family.

Napoleon, now leader of a nation, felt bound by law and could not openly endorse private vengeance. Still, he acknowledged the Corsican tradition of vendetta and offered Bartolomeo a chance to live in Paris with promised financial assistance, warning that the law would not tolerate private justice in the city.

Bartolomeo accepted, pledged loyalty to the Bonapartes, and expressed gratitude for their recognition of his misfortune, appearing hopeful about life in Paris. Napoleon, seeing a possible ally, hinted he might need trustworthy friends in the future.

After the meeting, Lucien learned the family had arrived in Paris with nothing. He gave them money and promised to help secure their comfort, noting that Bartolomeo’s Corsican property would not suffice to support them in Paris.

Fifteen years later, events unfolded that required the context of these earlier struggles and decisions.

Servin, a distinguished artist, was the first to establish a studio for young girls wishing to paint. About forty devoted pupils attended; he was married to a general’s dowerless daughter. At first mothers accompanied their daughters, but they soon trusted Servin’s principles enough to send them alone. He accepted only young ladies from wealthy families to avoid class complaints and refused aspiring professionals who would demand more thorough instruction. His carefulness and the quality of his teaching won him a stellar reputation among the aristocracy, so that mothers recommended him without hesitation.

Servin became synonymous with feminine art, as Herbault was with bonnets and Leroy with gowns. A young woman trained by him was presumed able to critique museum paintings, paint striking portraits, or produce genre scenes. Despite his Parisian connections, Servin maintained an independent, patriotic stance, often expressed with a witty tone.

The studio itself was arranged for the pupils. The attic entrance above his apartment was sealed and reached by a small spiral staircase. The studio was large, lit by panes veiled with green linen shades; dark-gray walls were hung with caricatures and plaster models, a blend of ordered instruction and artistic chaos.

On a bright July day the studio buzzed with young girls at easels, each with distinct expressions and styles. Among them sat Ginevra Piombo, modestly dressed and apart, anxious yet quietly intent. The group was split into two cliques: daughters of wealthy merchants and aristocrats led by Amelie Thirion, vain and pretentious. The room was lively with work, laughter, and chatter, but under that surface simmered rivalry. Ginevra, the most talented and beautiful, drew jealousy from the aristocratic circle. Amelie, intent on ostracizing her, moved Ginevra’s easel away from the others, provoking whispers about the slight.

The political climate after the Bourbons’ second return intensified tensions. Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, faced disdain from royalist peers; Amelie’s action was thus both personal and political. When Ginevra arrived she encountered cold silence where she had expected warmth. Her beauty and grace still commanded attention, but her politics made her a target. She remained focused on her work, apparently unaware of the growing hostility.

During the session a mysterious sound behind a partition drew Ginevra’s attention. She discovered Servin was hiding a political fugitive: an officer of the Imperial Guard. The revelation brought excitement and concern for the man’s safety. Ginevra resolved to protect him, preferring to be near him rather than risk his exposure. Her indifference to Amelie’s insult only intensified the other girls’ jealousy, who were baffled by her calm.

Madame Servin’s arrival interrupted the brewing conflict. She announced her husband could not attend that day, and her manner suggested she knew of the hidden man. Sensing the tension, Ginevra began humming a Corsican song to mask the sound of the fugitive’s breathing. Throughout the session she stayed absorbed in her work, sketching the officer’s head with a fervor that surprised even herself. Servin praised her talent; others, including Amelie, were drawn to her sheet. Ginevra’s determination to keep the man’s presence secret became evident as she deftly deflected attention from her drawing.

Amelie, suspicious, tried to uncover the mystery but was foiled by Ginevra’s quick thinking. Servin, aware of the undercurrent, engaged each pupil while keeping an eye on Ginevra. Their shared understanding deepened a tacit bond; Ginevra felt empowered to help protect the fugitive. As the day ended, her resolve to keep the secret and aid the officer’s escape grew. Political turmoil loomed outside, but within the studio the girls navigated rivalries and friendships, united by their art.

When the painter Servin and Ginevra believed they were alone, Servin knocked and admitted a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, his arm in a sling and his face pale with pain. He recoiled at seeing Ginevra, but Servin calmed him, introducing her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo. Recognizing her patriotism, the soldier relaxed.

"You are wounded," Ginevra observed.

"It's nothing, mademoiselle," he replied. "The wound is healing."

Cries from the street announced a death sentence: "Condemned to death!" The officer went pale at the name Labedoyere and collapsed onto a stool, anguished that while Labedoyere faced execution for their cause, he was hiding. He felt abandoned after losing his protector and spoke of dying rather than betraying his ideals, even contemplating selling his life to the executioner.

Ginevra blocked his path when he tried to leave. "Can you restore the Emperor?" she asked. "Do you expect to raise that giant who could not maintain himself?"

Alarmed, she offered him her savings, explaining her father was wealthy and would not blame her for helping a soldier. She urged him to wait until the storm passed, suggesting he could escape and serve elsewhere. Her words, filled with maternal compassion and hope, moved him. He admired her goodness and beauty, then his thoughts returned to Labedoyere. In silence they shared a moment of understanding; their acquaintance deepened into a bond. Servin asked if he could save Labedoyere; the officer said he could only avenge him. Ginevra felt a surge at the thought of vengeance and recognized the officer's devotion to Napoleon.

As evening fell, Ginevra felt a strange connection, recalling childhood memories. Captivated, she smiled at him as she left, igniting his hope.

The next day she returned to the studio. The officer, now called Luigi, told stories of the retreat from Moscow and the disaster at Waterloo. Ginevra listened, admiration growing; speaking Corsican together deepened their bond.

Days passed and Ginevra's classmates noticed her absence from social gatherings, blaming her relationship with Luigi. Mademoiselle Thirion, worried by the rumors, withdrew from class, and attendance declined. Ginevra, lost in thoughts of Luigi, remained oblivious.

One day Laure, a fellow student, confided that her mother insisted she leave the studio because of gossip about Ginevra and Luigi. Shocked, Ginevra reassured Laure they would meet again. Laure, sad to go, left, and Ginevra felt a pang of loss.

As Ginevra and Luigi's bond deepened, they shared an unspoken love and mutual recognition of worth. One evening Luigi signaled to Ginevra but, seeing Laure, retreated. Unaware of the tension, Ginevra spoke with Laure and learned the others had left because of scandal.

Ginevra confronted Servin, revealing that the young ladies had spread falsehoods about her relationship with Luigi. Servin dismissed the rumors, focused on his exhibition. Luigi entered, jokingly claiming responsibility for Servin's lost students; the painter playfully suggested marriage. Their silence then became the first acknowledgment of their love. Servin encouraged their union and celebrated the joy of love. Ginevra proposed to help Luigi secure a pardon, confident in her father's influence.

Meanwhile her parents grew anxious about her late returns. Bartolomeo paced, concerned for his daughter. Though used to her late hours, he and his wife felt unease. Bartolomeo, a man of integrity who prioritized his family's well-being over appearances, reflected on their close-knit dynamic built on love and mutual respect. Ginevra's upbringing had instilled loyalty and passion, shared with her father and their political commitment to the Emperor.

When Ginevra finally arrived home, her father embraced her, relieved. The family shared a warm moment; their love was evident. Ginevra's heart swelled with affection for her parents, knowing they were her greatest supporters.

In the studio, Ginevra and Luigi's relationship blossomed amid challenges. Their love, forged in adversity, became a source of strength that united them against the world. As they navigated complexities, they remained steadfast in their commitment to one another, embodying the resilience of their shared ideals.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!” Ginevra exclaimed, but he set her down gently. She reassured her mother it had been a trick to escape. The baroness recovered her composure, and Piombo, rubbing his hands, showed courtly pleasure. The two old parents seemed revived.

“Now, to dinner!” Piombo called, offering his hand; Ginevra smiled.

“Your mother says you’ve been spending more time at the studio. Painting seems more important to you than us,” he remarked.

“Oh, father!” she protested.

“Ginevra is preparing a surprise for us,” her mother suggested.

“A picture of your own! Will you show us?” Piombo asked.

“Yes, I am busy at the studio,” Ginevra answered slowly.

“You look pale!” her mother exclaimed.

“No! It shall never be said that Ginevra Piombo acted a lie,” she declared.

Her parents exchanged astonished looks. “I love a young man,” she confessed, lowering her eyelids.

“Is he a prince?” Piombo asked ironically, making his wife tremble.

“No, father, he is a young man without fortune,” she replied gently.

“Is he handsome?” he pressed.

“He is very unfortunate,” she said.

“What is he?” Piombo inquired.

“Labedoyere’s comrade; he was proscribed and hidden by Servin,” she explained.

“Servin is a good fellow,” Piombo acknowledged, “but you wrong to love any man but your father.”

“It does not depend on me to love or not love,” Ginevra replied softly.

“I thought my Ginevra would remain faithful to me until I die,” Piombo lamented. “I did not expect our love to find a rival.”

“Did I ever reproach you for your devotion to Napoleon?” Ginevra countered. “You left me for months on missions. I endured your absence. Life has necessities.”

“Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed.

“You don’t love me for myself; your reproaches show your egotism,” she said.

“Dare you blame your father’s love?” he shouted.

“Father, I don’t blame you,” she replied gently. “You have grounds for your egotism, as I have for my love. I have never left your side for fifteen years; it has been my pleasure to charm your life. Is it ingratitude to desire a husband who will protect me?”

“What! You reckon benefits with your father?” Piombo asked dangerously.

A dreadful silence followed. Bartolomeo cried out, “Oh! stay with us! I cannot bear to have you love another man. Ginevra, you will not have long to await your liberty.”

“But, father, I need not leave you; we will both love you. You will come to know the man to whom you bequeath me,” she said.

“Oh! Ginevra, why did you not marry when Napoleon offered?” Piombo lamented.

“They loved me to order,” she replied. “I did not wish to leave you alone.”

“You don’t wish to leave me alone, yet you marry! I know you, my daughter; you would cease to love us,” he said.

Elisa, the baroness, remained motionless, stunned. “We have no longer a daughter; she wishes to marry,” Piombo declared, then bowed, hands raised, bowed by sorrow. Ginevra, touched by his restraint, expected a violent outburst but met gentleness.

“Father,” she said tenderly, “you shall never be abandoned by your Ginevra. Love me for my own sake. If you knew how he loves me! He would never make me unhappy!”

“Comparisons already!” Piombo shouted. “I cannot endure the thought of your marriage. If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me; if he does not love you, I would kill him.”

Ginevra alone could meet his gaze, her eyes aflame. “Oh! to love you! What man is worthy of such a life?” Piombo continued. “Who is worthy to be your husband?”

“He,” Ginevra replied, “he of whom I am not worthy.”

“He?” Piombo repeated. “Who is he?”

“He whom I love,” she said.

“How can he know you enough to love you?” he asked.

“Father,” she said impatiently, “whether he loves me or not, if I love him—”

“You love him?” Piombo interrupted. Ginevra nodded.

“You love him more than you love us?” he pressed.

“The two feelings cannot be compared,” she replied.

“Is one stronger than the other?” he asked.

“I think it is,” she admitted.

“You shall not marry him,” Piombo declared, shaking the window panes with his voice.

“I shall marry him,” Ginevra replied calmly.

“Oh, God!” cried her mother. “How will this quarrel end?”

Piombo, now seated, wore an icy sternness. He looked at Ginevra and said gently, “No! You will not marry him. Say nothing more tonight—let me think otherwise. Do you wish to see your father on his knees, his white hairs prostrate before you? I supplicate you—”

“Ginevra Piombo does not break her word,” she replied. “I am your daughter.”

“She is right,” the baroness said. “We are sent into the world to marry.”

“Do you encourage her in disobedience?” Piombo asked his petrified wife.

“Refusing to obey an unjust order is not disobedience,” Ginevra asserted.

“No order can be unjust from your father’s lips,” Piombo replied. “Why do you judge my actions? My repugnance is counsel from on high, perhaps to protect you from great evil.”

“The only evil would be if he did not love me.”

“Always he!” Piombo exclaimed.

“Yes, always,” she affirmed. “He is my life, my good, my thought. Even if I obeyed you, he would remain in my soul. To forbid me to marry him is to make me hate you.”

“You love us not!” Piombo shouted.

“Oh!” Ginevra shook her head.

“Then forget him; be faithful to us. After we are gone—you understand?”

“Father, do you wish me to long for your death?” she asked.

“I shall outlive you. Children who do not honor their parents die early,” he warned.

“All the more reason I should marry and be happy,” she replied.

This cool reasoning troubled Piombo. Ginevra shuddered, sprang onto his knee, and embraced him. “Oh, yes, yes, let me die first! I could never survive you, my father!”

“Oh! my Ginevra, my own Ginevra!” Piombo replied, his anger melting under her affection.

“It was time you ceased,” the baroness said, trembling.

“Poor mother!” Ginevra said.

“Ah! Ginevretta! mia bella Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed, playing with her as if she were a child. They had moments of joy, but Ginevra’s attempts to introduce Luigi met with playful refusals from her father.

The next day Ginevra was more affectionate, as if to thank him for apparent consent. That evening she sang and played for him, claiming they needed a man’s voice for the nocturne.

A week later, her mother signaled her to come closer. “I have persuaded your father to receive him,” Elisa whispered.

“Oh! mother, how happy you have made me!” Ginevra replied.

That day Ginevra returned home on Luigi’s arm. The officer emerged from hiding for the second time. Ginevra’s appeals to the Duc de Feltre had succeeded; Luigi’s name was restored on the officer’s list. Warned of her father’s potential disapproval, Luigi felt anxious; Ginevra’s awareness of his fear deepened her love.

“How pale you are!” she remarked as they reached home.

“Oh! Ginevra, if it concerned my life only—”

Though Bartolomeo had been informed of the formal introduction, he remained seated, his stern expression intimidating.

“Father,” Ginevra said, “I bring you someone you will be pleased to see—a soldier who fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean.”

Piombo rose, casting a sidelong glance at Luigi. “Monsieur is not decorated,” he remarked sarcastically.

“I no longer wear the Legion of Honor,” Luigi replied timidly.

Embarrassed by her father’s rudeness, Ginevra pulled out a chair for Luigi. That simple civility seemed to satisfy Piombo. Madame Piombo, noticing her husband soften, tried to engage him.

“Monsieur’s resemblance to someone we knew in Corsica, Nina Porta, is surprising,” she noted.

“Nothing could be more natural,” Luigi replied. “Nina was my sister.”

“Are you Luigi Porta?” Piombo asked.

“Yes,” Luigi confirmed.

Bartolomeo staggered, needing support from his wife. The two old parents left the room in silence, abandoning their daughter in horror.

Luigi, bewildered, looked at Ginevra, who had turned pale, staring at the door through which her parents had exited. The solemnity of the moment filled her with fear. “What misery in a word!” she exclaimed.

“In the name of our love, what have I said?” Luigi asked.

“My father never spoke of our tragic history, and I was too young to know,” Ginevra explained.

“Are we in vendetta?” Luigi asked, trembling.

“Yes. My mother said the Portas killed my brother and burned our house. My father then massacred their family. How did you survive? You were tied to the bed before they set fire to the house.”

“I do not know,” Luigi replied. “I was taken to Genoa at six and raised by an old man named Colonna. I was told I had enemies and advised to take Luigi as my surname to evade them.”

“Go, go, Luigi!” Ginevra urged. “Stay here; you are safe in my father’s house, but once you leave, danger awaits! My father has two Corsicans in his service, and they will hunt you.”

“Ginevra,” he said, “does this vendetta exist between us?”

She smiled sadly and nodded. “Oh, Luigi, our love must be pure and sincere to give me strength for the path ahead. But it promises happiness for our lives, does it not?”

Luigi smiled and squeezed her hand. Ginevra realized true love transcends superficial expressions. Their destiny was sealed; she foresaw a difficult struggle but felt no inclination to abandon him. With resolve, she helped him reach his modest lodging.

Returning home, Ginevra felt a calm resolve. She greeted her parents, who were preparing dinner, with gentle eyes, noticing her mother’s tear-stained face. They rose from the table in silence, unable to speak.

In the gloomy salon, Piombo attempted to speak but faltered, his strength failing him. He rang for a servant. “Pietro, light the fire; I am cold,” he ordered.

Ginevra trembled, watching her father’s internal struggle. Both suffered in silence, nursing pride and anger. The love that once brought them joy now felt like a burden. Ginevra’s gaze at her father was full of reproach, while Piombo’s eyes reflected deep pain.

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother, desperate to mend the rift, gathered them in Bartolomeo’s study. Ginevra sensed her mother’s intentions and smiled sadly.

At that moment, two notaries arrived with witnesses. Bartolomeo eyed them warily. The older notary introduced himself and began to discuss Ginevra’s marriage to Luigi Porta.

“Monsieur le baron, we have come to fulfill the law regarding your daughter’s marriage,” he stated.

“Are there laws in France that destroy paternal authority?” Piombo demanded.

“Monsieur—” the notary began.

“Which tear a daughter from her father?” Piombo pressed.

“Monsieur—”

“Which deprive an old man of his last consolation?” he continued.

“Monsieur, your daughter only belongs to you if—”

“And kill him?” Piombo shouted.

“Monsieur, permit me—”

The notary’s calm only fueled Piombo’s rage. In a sudden movement he grabbed a dagger from the wall and rushed toward Ginevra. Witnesses intervened, but Piombo tossed them aside, fury raw.

When Ginevra saw him approach, she knelt before him. “No, no! I cannot!” he cried, throwing the dagger away. “Have mercy! Give me Luigi! I ask for your consent upon my knees,” she pleaded, voice trembling.

Bartolomeo pushed her away. “Go. The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here,” he said, pressing his heart. “Leave my house, unhappy girl,” he added, ushering her out.

“Luigi!” Ginevra cried, entering her lover’s humble abode. “My Luigi, we have no fortune but our love.”

“Then I am richer than kings!” he exclaimed.

“My father and mother have cast me off,” she said sadly.

“I will love you in their place,” he promised.

“Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!” she declared, voice full of mixed joy and dread.

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father's house, she sought refuge with Madame Servin until her marriage to Luigi. Madame Servin received her coldly, resentful of the trouble Ginevra had caused her husband. Proud yet stunned by this selfishness, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her, painting a bright future that lifted her spirits despite her father's harshness.

One morning Ginevra received trunks of essentials from her mother, accompanied by a letter urging her to reconsider the marriage. The letter, full of love and sorrow, deeply moved Ginevra. Just as she felt compelled to return home, Luigi arrived and her resolve to abandon him faded. She chose to bury her grief for the sake of their love.

On the wedding day Ginevra was alone; Luigi brought witnesses to sign the marriage certificate. They were simple men in ordinary dress, their presence contrasting with the usual joy of a wedding. Ginevra's beauty silenced them, creating a somber atmosphere. As they walked to the mayor's office, they passed other wedding parties full of celebration, which only highlighted the difference in their situation.

Inside, the festive air around them made their own love feel overshadowed. The mayor's clerk noted the absence of Ginevra's parents, and when the mayor inquired about their protest, the clerk confirmed it. The bureaucratic formality felt oppressive, a reminder of her family's disapproval, and the ceremony was reduced to a legal act without celebration.

After signing the registers, Ginevra and Luigi left the office with mixed relief and sadness. Luigi expressed gratitude, and Ginevra felt a fragile happiness despite everything. They hurried to the church, where an old priest married them in a modest chapel amid grander weddings. The priest's remarks about their parents' absence stung, but a sergeant's blessing comforted Ginevra.

Once married they moved into a modest apartment, where Luigi embraced Ginevra, calling it their true wedding. They explored the space, which had been thoughtfully arranged with artistic supplies for her. Delightful details made them revel in their shared home, feeling like they had found paradise despite its limitations.

In the early days of marriage they indulged in their love, often losing themselves in each other's presence. Reality soon set in and they recognized the need to work. Ginevra began copying paintings and gained clients, while Luigi, after initial struggle, used his handwriting skills to offer copying services. His efforts paid off; he eventually secured enough work to hire assistants, providing a stable income for both.

This period marked the happiest time of their lives, filled with love and labor. They enjoyed evenings together, with Ginevra always smiling and never complaining. Each worked for the other, finding joy in shared effort, though Ginevra sometimes felt remorse for the sorrow she had caused her family. To mark an anniversary, she painted a self-portrait for Luigi that captured their love. Another year passed in contentment; no significant events disrupted their happiness.

At the beginning of 1819, picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce original works instead of copies as competition grew. Madame Porta regretted not taking up genre painting sooner, which might have elevated her reputation. Ginevra tried portraiture but faced stiff competition from more established artists. Despite modest savings, she and Luigi grew anxious about their future.

Through the winter Luigi labored relentlessly as the market for writing shrank. Unable to afford help, he worked longer hours for the same pay. Ginevra painted several commendable pieces, but dealers avoided lesser-known artists, and low prices failed to sell her work.

Love and hope in their household were overshadowed by looming poverty. They concealed their worries—Ginevra lavished affection while Luigi hid his fears—clinging to one another and to the belief that love would sustain them.

One night Ginevra woke to find Luigi gone and discovered him working late to avoid burdening her. Moved, she began coloring lithographs to help, working through the night while Luigi toiled in his garret. Both pretended to sleep to spare the other their anxieties.

On another exhausted night, Luigi opened his window and saw Ginevra’s lamp across the courtyard, realizing she was also at work. When he confronted her, she replied that she could not be idle while he suffered. They shared a tender moment watching the dawn together and felt briefly hopeful.

Their struggles deepened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. The joy of parenthood briefly masked their financial burdens, but soon they were selling possessions and jewels. Poverty eroded their comfort and pride, escalating their desperation.

Months later, frail and worn, Ginevra struggled to care for a sickly child. Luigi, feeling helpless, sought aid but found none; he sold paintings and remaining furniture for a meager sum that only prolonged their hardship. Ginevra showed remarkable strength, managing the household and caring for the boy while often starving herself to provide for Luigi.

One evening she offered Luigi her last piece of bread and urged him to eat. Realizing her sacrifice, he was consumed by despair and left to find help. In desperation he decided to sell himself as a military substitute, hoping the money would save his family.

After securing an advance, he hurried home, only to find Ginevra asleep with their child. When he roused her, she smiled, then saw Bartolomeo cold and lifeless. Luigi, horrified, sought aid, but it was too late. In delirium Ginevra spoke of her love and sorrow; she died in his arms, leaving him devastated.

As Luigi mourned, Ginevra’s parents sat at home reflecting on their daughter, whom they had not seen in years. Elisa Piombo spoke of hoping to rekindle Bartolomeo’s love for Ginevra. Just as he seemed to soften, a man arrived and revealed Ginevra and Luigi’s tragic fate. The old couple was shattered; Bartolomeo’s heart was broken. The cycle of suffering had claimed another victim, leaving only memories and grief.
